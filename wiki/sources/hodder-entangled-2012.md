# Entangled (Hodder 2012)

Full title: *Entangled: An Archaeology of the Relationships Between Humans and Things* (Wiley-Blackwell, 2012). [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]'s major statement of **[[entanglement-theory|entanglement theory]]**: humans and things form open-ended webs of enabling **dependence** and constraining **dependency** (HT + TT + TH + HH). Empirical anchors include [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (clay/plaster/brick, cattle, pottery), Mesolithic Lepenski Vir, plant and animal domestication, and modern analogies (cars, electricity grids).

Written while Hodder was on leave at Magdalen College Oxford, Fondation Maison des Sciences de l’Homme Paris, and Koç University Research Center for Anatolian Civilizations. Readers of the MS included Matthew Johnson, Lynn Meskell, Bjørnar Olsen, Bob Preucel, Steve Shennan, James Skibo, and others.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Entangled* is Hodder’s major **object-side** program statement after the 1999 method charter. The double argument is conceptual and ethical. Conceptually, the “return to things” across materiality studies, ANT, cognitive archaeology, and related strands still under-engages thing–thing and thing–human dependence, material chemistry, decay, and archaeometry; entanglement (HT+TT+TH+HH) as the dialectic of enabling dependence and constraining dependency retools that field for archaeology. Fittingness reworks evolutionary selection without reducing to reproductive fitness; tanglegrams and operational/behavioral chains operationalize thread-tracing at sites like Çatalhöyük. Ethically, long-term intensification of entanglement and the “fixing that further entraps” raise species-level stakes Hodder states as position, not as policy fact.
