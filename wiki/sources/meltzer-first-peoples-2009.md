# First Peoples in a New World (David J. Meltzer, 2009)

Scholarly synthesis of the peopling of Ice Age America: origins, antiquity, routes, adaptations, megafaunal extinctions, and the modern politics of the deep past. Meltzer frames the problem as both a scientific research program and a century-and-a-half controversy with cycles of orthodoxy, extraordinary claims, and resolution by high-bar evidence (Folsom 1927; Monte Verde 1997). The book is the wiki’s **primary specialist densification** of North American Paleoindian archaeology and of the Clovis-first vs pre-Clovis debate from the archaeological side, complementing genetics-forward layers already on [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] (Reich 2018) and earlier narrative syntheses ([[fagan-great-journey-1987]], [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]]).

## Volume Synthesis Note

*First Peoples* is the wiki’s best single-author **archaeological** account of how the Clovis-first consensus formed, cracked, and reorganized around pre-Clovis acceptance without dissolving into “any early claim goes.” Meltzer’s distinctive contributions relative to already-ingested synthesists:

- vs [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]]: more specialist depth on kill-site epistemology, overkill statistics, and colonization theory; same Folsom→Clovis spine.
- vs [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen]]: North America–centered; participant testimony on Monte Verde 1997; sustained landscape-learning/settling-in model; explicit ¹⁴C-BP dating convention.
- vs genetics-forward [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] (Reich 2018 layer): Meltzer supplies the material-culture, site-quality, and extinctions arguments genetics cannot replace; his 2009 molecular chapter must not overwrite later aDNA.

Tensions to keep visible: (1) ¹⁴C BP vs cal BP reporting across sources; (2) Clovis as *people* vs *technology diffusion*; (3) overkill still open despite Grayson–Meltzer critique; (4) ethics of peopling research under NAGPRA after Kennewick.
