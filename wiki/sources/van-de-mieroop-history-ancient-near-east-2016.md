# A History of the Ancient Near East, ca. 3000–323 BC

Marc Van De Mieroop, *A History of the Ancient Near East, ca. 3000–323 BC* (3rd ed.,
Wiley-Blackwell, 2016; Blackwell History of the Ancient World). A current-consensus
university survey of the whole ancient Near East from the Uruk period to Alexander,
organized as three parts — **City-States**, **Territorial States**, and **Empires**.

The wiki already holds a dense Mesopotamian layer ([[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]
narrative; [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]] society-and-economy;
[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Trigger]]/[[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|Adams]]
comparative). Van De Mieroop's role is therefore **current-consensus framing plus
coverage of the under-served topics**: the Late Bronze Age international system (the
"Club of the Great Powers"), the culminating **Persian/Achaemenid world empire**,
**Elam**, the **Medes**, and the historiography of "the ancient Near East" as a construct.

## Volume Synthesis Note

As a current-consensus survey landing on top of a wiki already dense with Mesopotamia,
Van De Mieroop's job was less to build than to **update, correct, and extend**. Three
kinds of contribution stand out.

**Corrections to the older layer.** Where [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] narrates
in mid-20th-century terms, Van De Mieroop supplies the current consensus, and several are
genuine [CONTRADICTION]-class updates now recorded on the pages: the [[akkadians|Akkad]]
"empire" was probably not a territorial state but a raiding/trade-route hegemony;
[[ur|Ur III]] was a bureaucratic but **not** totalitarian state; the
[[code-of-hammurabi|"Code" of Hammurabi]] is a royal-justice monument, not a statute
(Kraus 1960); the linear "token → writing" evolution is rejected (parallel systems); and,
most concretely, the [[ur-royal-cemetery-debate|Ur Royal Cemetery]] attendants died by
**violence** (Baadsgaard/Vidale 2011 forensic reanalysis), not Woolley's peaceful poison —
filling the exact gap the debate page had flagged. He also corroborates the revisionist
side of the [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate|temple-state debate]].

**The under-covered later empires.** The survey's largest net addition is the wiki's
first real coverage of the first-millennium and Iranian world: a major
[[achaemenid-empire|Persian/Achaemenid empire]] page (the "first world empire," with Van
De Mieroop's caution against the Cyrus-Cylinder-as-human-rights-charter reading), a
gap-filling [[elamites|Elam]] page (a recurrent ANE power finally given its own entry),
and a deliberately thin [[medes|Medes]] page whose point is the **sources problem** — a
unified "Median empire" may be largely a Herodotean construct. The
[[late-bronze-age-international-system|"Club of the Great Powers"]] page gains its fullest
treatment (Amarna diplomacy, Uluburun, Liverani's model), and a new
[[ancient-near-east-chronology|ANE chronology timeline]] anchors the whole subject to the
Middle-Chronology convention while foregrounding the long/middle/short/ultra-short problem.

**Reflexivity and method.** Van De Mieroop is explicit that "the ancient Near East" is a
modern construct, that royal inscriptions are ideology, and that absolute dates carry a
"false impression of certainty" — cautions now distributed across the discipline-adjacent
pages. A historiographic item worth flagging: his Debate 12.1 records a 1937 (von Soden)
Nazi-era "Aryan" explanation of Assyrian imperialism, kept on the [[assyrians]] page
strictly as a discredited episode in the history of the discipline, never as a live
position. No `studies/` page or hub is warranted — this is a textbook survey, scaffolding
by design.
