## Overview

Anthony argues that Proto-Indo-European was spoken on the Pontic-Caspian steppes after roughly 4000–3500 BCE (wool and wheel vocabularies fix a *terminus post quem*), that late PIE thrived among wagon-using steppe pastoralists of the Yamnaya horizon (~3300–2500 BCE), and that subsequent expansions carried IE dialects into Europe, the steppe belt, and eventually South Asia — via franchising-like elite recruitment and institutional adoption more than pure military conquest. The book is the archaeological counterpart to the steppe hypothesis later reinforced (on demography, not language) by aDNA.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Anthony's 2007 book is a single-author archaeological-linguistic program rather than a handbook or excavation report. Its load-bearing move is chronological: late Proto-Indo-European cannot predate wheeled vehicles (and is only weakly constrained by wool), so a 7000–6500 BCE Anatolian first-farming dispersal of the full IE family is incompatible with the reconstructed wagon vocabulary. That *terminus post quem* (~4000–3500 BCE), combined with a *terminus ante quem* near 2500 BCE from early daughter languages, forces the homeland search into a narrow Chalcolithic–Early Bronze window. Within that window Anthony places PIE on the Pontic-Caspian steppes — the Kurgan/steppe hypothesis of Gimbutas and Mallory — and treats the Yamnaya horizon as the material world of late PIE speakers: wagons, horses, animal sacrifice, tribal pastoralism, and persistent frontiers.

Methodologically the book is a hybrid. Part One teaches comparative reconstruction and linguistic palaeontology with worked examples (hundred, wheel, wool) and then builds a migration-and-frontier toolkit: pots are not people, yet robust multi-domain material frontiers can mark language borders; migration has structure (scouts, charter groups, leapfrogging); language shift often follows open elite hierarchies rather than pure conquest. Anthony renames Renfrew's "élite dominance" as **elite recruitment** and later likens western IE expansion to **franchising** — initial migration and conflict, then reproducing patron-client and guest-host institutions so that genetic links to steppe founders can fade while myths, rituals, and poetic language persist. Part Two supplies the archaeological narrative: Neolithic farmer–forager frontiers on the western steppe edge; cattle, copper, and chiefs after ~5200–5000 BCE; horse keeping from ~4800 and riding (via bit-wear) by the mid-fourth millennium; the crisis of Old Europe and Suvorovo penetration; Maikop as southern filter; Yamnaya wagon pastoralism; western contacts (Usatovo, Danube Yamnaya, Corded Ware pathways); Sintashta chariots and the LBA opening of the Eurasian steppe corridor.

What the volume adds to the wiki is a full, attributed Position A architecture for [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] and dense primary content for Pontic-Caspian cultures, horse-riding methods, and IE institutional vocabulary. What it cannot supply is nuclear aDNA demography (post-2015) or a settled chariot-priority consensus. Four-way non-identity is Anthony's own stance and remains mandatory: Yamnaya is a horizon, speech associations are arguments, and genes (when later layered from Reich and others) are a third evidence class — never silent synonyms for language or people. Read with Renfrew 1987 for the rival homeland logic, and with post-2015 genetic syntheses for demography that Anthony's 2007 text could only anticipate.
