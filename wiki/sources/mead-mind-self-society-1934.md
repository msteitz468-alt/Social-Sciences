# Mind, Self, and Society (Mead, 1934)

George Herbert Mead's *Mind, Self, and Society from the Standpoint of a Social Behaviorist* (Chicago: University of Chicago Press, 1934) — the foundational text of American [[social-psychology|social psychology]] and the principal source of [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]. Ingested from the 2015 "Definitive Edition" (Morris ed.; annotated by Daniel R. Huebner and Hans Joas).

## What the book is

A posthumously compiled record of [[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]]'s lecture course in social psychology at the University of Chicago, presenting a **[[social-behaviorism|social-behaviorist]]** account of how [[the-social-self|mind and self]] arise out of the social process of communication. Mead's central move: mind, meaning, and selfhood are not presupposed by society but are *products* of it — generated through the [[gesture|conversation of gestures]], the [[significant-symbol|significant symbol]], and the individual's capacity for [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|taking the role of the other]]. The book belongs jointly to [[disciplines/sociology|sociology]], social psychology, and the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophical movement.

## Provenance and structure (editor-imposed)

The four Parts and 42 chapters are **Morris's editorial architecture**, not Mead's design — see `reliability_notes` and the [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate|authorship debate]].

## Provenance note (integration)

The extraction confirmed and sharpened the central reliability caveat: "social behaviorism" and the sentence "Our behaviorism is a social behaviorism" are Morris's, not Mead's; the "generalized other" and the triadic theory of meaning enter the text specifically through Robert Page's 1930 notes. All quotation from the main text is treated as reconstructed lecture material in attributed voice; the Supplementary Essays (Mead's own manuscripts) are the higher-fidelity authorial witness.
