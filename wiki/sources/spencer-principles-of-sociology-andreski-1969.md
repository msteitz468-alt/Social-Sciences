# Principles of Sociology (Spencer; Andreski abridgement, 1969)

## Volume Synthesis Note

Spencer’s *Principles*, in Andreski’s selection, is the major English founding statement of **[[social-evolutionism]]**: evolution as [[differentiation-and-integration]], society as [[social-organism]] with [[sustaining-distributing-regulating-systems|sustaining, distributing, and regulating systems]], growth by [[social-compounding]], and the polar [[militant-type-of-society|militant]] / [[industrial-type-of-society|industrial]] typology (compulsory cooperation, status, positive regulation vs. voluntary cooperation, contract, mainly negative regulation). Parts II–IV then fill the architecture with comparative reconstructions of political, ecclesiastical ([[priesthood]]), and industrial institutions — from ceremonial regulation and headship through law, property, and revenue to slavery, serfdom, and free contract.

For this wiki the work is scaffolding for classical sociology’s structure/function and type vocabulary, set **beside** (not under) Comtean [[positivism]]: shared demand for a science of society, different master key (differentiation–integration vs. [[law-of-three-stages]]). Andreski’s introduction is frank reception — biased toward what he thinks still lives (structural-functional and systems anticipations; group/institution selection) and silent on cut “errors.” All doctrinal content is **attributed voice**. Ethnographic cases are 19th-c. illustrations, never society pages or wiki-voice ethnography.
