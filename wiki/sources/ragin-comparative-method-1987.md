# The Comparative Method: Moving Beyond Qualitative and Quantitative Strategies

Charles C. Ragin, *The Comparative Method: Moving Beyond Qualitative and Quantitative Strategies* (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1987; paperback 1989; new edition with a 2014 introduction, 2014). The founding statement of **Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA)** — a set-theoretic, Boolean-algebraic method for the systematic comparison of a moderate number of cases treated as configurations of conditions. ## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Comparative Method* is best read as a single sustained argument in three movements. The first (Chs. 1–2) establishes that comparative social science is distinctive not in its data but in its goals, and that its central obstacle is **causal complexity** — outcomes produced by combinations of conditions, reachable by multiple paths, with effects that flip by context. The second (Chs. 3–5) diagnoses the two available research strategies, **case-oriented** and **variable-oriented**, as each strong exactly where the other is weak, and argues that merely *combining* them (as three well-known studies do) is not a genuine synthesis. The third (Chs. 6–9) supplies the synthesis: a **Boolean, set-theoretic method** — later named QCA — that treats cases as configurations, uses truth-table minimization to find the combinations of conditions associated with an outcome, and reads necessity and sufficiency directly off the result.

For this wiki the book is a keystone methods source: it anchors the new [[qualitative-comparative-analysis]] page, supplies the [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]] and [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]] concepts, and gives the [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]] its clearest programmatic statement. It sits in productive tension with the concurrently-ingested [[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Lieberson]], who mounts an *internal* critique of quantitative practice rather than Ragin's *synthetic* alternative — the two together map the late-20th-century methodological ferment from opposite entry points. Everything the book asserts is attributed to Ragin; its empirical examples are demonstrations, never promoted to substantive facts. QCA's own contested standing (Lieberson 2004; Seawright 2004–05) is recorded, not adjudicated.
