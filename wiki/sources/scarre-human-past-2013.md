# The Human Past: World Prehistory and the Development of Human Societies

Chris Scarre (ed.), 3rd edition, Thames & Hudson, 2013. The standard
English-language university survey of world prehistory from hominin origins to
the threshold of the early historic world, organized as a global tour: human
evolution in Africa and the peopling of the Old World, then Holocene
developments region by region — the origins of food production and the rise of
complex societies across Southwest Asia, East and Southeast Asia, South Asia,
Africa, Europe, the Mediterranean, Mesoamerica, South America, and North
America. Written for undergraduates by a team of regional specialists under
Scarre's editorship; each chapter closes with "Key Site," "Key Discovery,"
"Key Method," and "Key Controversy" boxes and a chapter bibliography.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Human Past* is, in this wiki's terms, a **multi-author survey in the post-Clark
[[world-prehistory]] textbook genre** (alongside Wenke & Olszewski, Fagan, Mithen, and
Bogucki), distinguished by its region-specialist authorship and its "Key Site / Key
Discovery / Key Method / Key Controversy" apparatus, which keeps live disputes visible
rather than smoothing them into a single narrative voice — a structure congenial to this
wiki's Voice and Attribution Protocol.

Its overall argument (attributed, Scarre ed. 2013, ch. 19) is that the deep human past
resolves into four long-run processes: **demographic increase**; **agricultural
intensification and the environmental degradation that shadows it**; **biological
exchange** among previously separated biota and populations, culminating in the Columbian
exchange and catastrophic post-contact disease mortality; and **climate change** as a
recurrent backdrop. Consistent with the wiki's Evidence-Class rule 8, the volume treats
climate as a *contributor* to social change, rarely a prime mover — a stance it applies
explicitly to Andean collapses (Moche, Wari/Tiwanaku), the Sahara's desiccation, and the
end of the Bronze Age.

Two tensions with material already in the wiki are worth noting. First, as a textbook it
is **downstream of the primary studies** it summarizes and lags the archaeogenetic
research front: its human-origins, peopling, and population-history claims (2013 horizon)
should stay attributed and date-stamped, not promoted to current aDNA consensus. Second,
it repeatedly stages the wiki's own **four-way non-identity** problem — Austronesian
language vs. Lapita pottery vs. genes; "Celts" as ethnonym vs. La Tène art vs. Celtic
languages; Indus script/language vs. Harappan material culture; Yayoi migration vs.
Japonic vs. peninsular gene flow — and its region specialists generally handle these with
appropriate caution, which the extracted pages preserve as `associated_with`-style
attributions rather than identities.
