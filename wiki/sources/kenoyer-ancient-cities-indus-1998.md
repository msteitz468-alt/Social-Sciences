# Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization (Kenoyer 1998)

Jonathan Mark Kenoyer, *Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization* (Karachi / Oxford: Oxford University Press; American Institute of Pakistan Studies, 1998). An illustrated introductory synthesis and exhibition catalogue on the urban Indus (Harappan) civilization of Pakistan and northwestern India (Mature phase ~2600–1900 BCE), grounded in HARP fieldwork at [[harappa|Harappa]] and comparative material from [[mohenjo-daro]], [[mehrgarh]], Chanhu-daro, Nausharo, Allahdino, and Miri Qalat.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Kenoyer 1998 is an exhibition catalogue and introductory synthesis whose research spine is the **Harappa Archaeological Research Project** (HARP, from 1986) plus comparative work at Mohenjo-daro, Chanhu-daro, Nausharo, Allahdino, Miri Qalat, and Mehrgarh. Relative to [[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl 2002]] (already ingested), the two books are complementary specialist syntheses of the same civilization:

| Emphasis | Kenoyer 1998 | Possehl 2002 |
|---|---|---|
| Political model | Multi-elite **city-states**; trade + religion as control | Heterarchy / **civilization without the state** |
| Periodization | Shaffer **[[indus-valley-tradition]]** (four eras) | **[[indus-age]]** (food production → iron) |
| Geography | ~1,500 sites / ~680,000 km² | Domains scheme; >1,000,000 km² extent claim |
| Method signature | Craft technology, ethnoarchaeology, seal use-contexts | Settlement survey, Domains, thematic synthesis |
| Decline | Localization Era; no invasion; craft/weight legacy | Transformation not collapse; Rojdi expansion |

Both reject Wheeler–Piggott priest-king despotism and Aryan-conquest collapse; both keep four-way non-identity (culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people). Kenoyer’s distinctive contributions to the wiki are: (1) the full Shaffer tradition terminology as an operational periodization; (2) a four-category craft typology and the claim that seals, stoneware bangles, and cubical chert weights were elite-controlled status technologies; (3) script research as **contextual use analysis** rather than rebus decipherment races; (4) site densification of Harappa under HARP and new site pages for Chanhu-daro, Nausharo, Allahdino, and Miri Qalat; (5) object-level catalogue evidence (priest-king, horned masks, Allahdino gold recycling, Ernestite drills). Interpretive theses remain attributed; the book is pre-Rakhigarhi aDNA and pre-Farmer–Sproat–Witzel (2004) non-script debate.
