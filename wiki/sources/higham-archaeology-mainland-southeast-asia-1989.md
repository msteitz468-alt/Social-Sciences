## Overview

Charles Higham's *The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia* (Cambridge World
Archaeology, 1989) is the first comprehensive English-language synthesis of the
prehistory and protohistory of mainland Southeast Asia — Thailand, Cambodia, Laos,
Viet Nam, and adjacent areas — from the late Pleistocene hunter-gatherers to the fall
of Angkor in 1431 CE. It integrates the author's own excavations (Ban Na Di, Khok
Phanom Di) with the regional record to argue a broadly **indigenist** thesis: that
Southeast Asian social complexity developed largely through local processes —
sedentism, rice and animal domestication, exchange, and competition over ranked
status — rather than by external colonisation, with Indian and Chinese contact
shaping but not creating the early states (*mandalas*).

Load-bearing claims (attributed to Higham 1989 unless noted):

- **Hunter-gatherers.** The [[hoabinhian]] is a stone-tool technocomplex of Bac Bo (Son Vi
  → Hoabinhian → Bacsonian, c. 18,000–3,000 BCE); Higham himself criticises broad use of
  the term as implying spurious cross-regional affinity. Early-cultivation claims (Spirit
  Cave, Banyan Valley Cave rice husks) are treated skeptically — Yen's analysis favoured
  wild rice.
- **Sedentism before agriculture.** [[khok-phanom-di]] (c. 2000–1400 BCE) anchors Higham's
  "domestication of people" thesis — sedentism, not confirmed plant/animal domestication,
  as the socially decisive change; the site's rice wild/domestic status is left unresolved.
- **Emergent ranking without centralization.** [[non-nok-tha]] and [[ban-na-di]] show
  mortuary wealth-ranking among kin groups ("General Periods A–B") but no regional centre;
  centralised chiefdoms and moated central places appear only later with iron.
- **Bronze chronology.** Higham revises the 1960s–70s "very early metallurgy" claims
  downward to initial bronze c. 2000–1500 BCE — a contested dating question
  ([[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]).
- **Mandalas.** Early states (Funan, Zhenla, Dvaravati, Champa, [[angkor|Angkor]]) are
  best understood as [[mandala|mandalas]] — ritual polities of shifting allegiance, not
  bounded bureaucratic states — developing largely indigenously with Indian contact as one
  variable ([[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]]).
- **Non-identity.** Throughout, Higham keeps archaeological culture, language
  ([[austroasiatic-languages]] vs Cham/[[austronesian-expansion|Austronesian]]), and people
  distinct; the [[sa-huynh-culture|Sa Huynh]]↔Cham association is flagged as his contested
  inference.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia* is a single-author regional synthesis, and its
argumentative spine is an **indigenist, processual** reading of the region's deep past.
Higham's throughline — stated in the introduction and reaffirmed in the concluding chapter —
is that beneath the surface diversity of climate, language, and polity there is a coherence
in human adaptation to the Red, Mekong, and Chao Phraya river systems: a continuity of
competitive status display (exotic-goods exchange, ritual, monumental building) running from
the Khok Phanom Di burials of c. 2000 BCE through to Angkorian god-kingship. Against both the
European three-age system and the Heine-Geldern diffusionist / "Indianised states" tradition,
he argues for multi-variable causation (Renfrew's "multiplier effect") in which iron and
Indian/Chinese contact stimulate, rather than create, indigenous trajectories toward ranked
society and the [[mandala]].

For this wiki the volume is the founding source for the **mainland Southeast Asia** region,
previously absent from the collection. Its chief tensions with already-ingested material are
chronological and evidential: the book pre-dates the archaeogenetic "two-layer" model of
Southeast Asian population history and the resolution of the Ban Chiang dating controversy,
so its migration and chronology claims are date-stamped "(as of Higham 1989)" and several are
now superseded. It sits alongside the wiki's broader [[state-formation]] and
[[chiefdom]] material as a distinctive regional pathway, and its use of the mandala concept
converges with the galactic-polity (Tambiah) and segmentary-state (Southall) models.
Radiocarbon dates are reported as Higham gives them; he does not consistently state
calibration status, so lab dates from this source should not be merged with calibrated dates
elsewhere without checking.
