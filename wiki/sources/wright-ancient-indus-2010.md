# The Ancient Indus: Urbanism, Economy, and Society (Wright 2010)

Cambridge University Press case-study synthesis of the Indus civilization by
[[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]] (NYU), emphasizing urbanism, agropastoral and
craft-producing economies, social networks between cities and rural communities,
interregional trade ([[meluhha|Meluhha]], Dilmun, Magan), landscape and water
management, gender, religious ideologies, and the decline/transformation debate.
Third major Indus synthesis in the collection after Possehl 2002
([[possehl-indus-civilization-2002]]) and Kenoyer 1998
([[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]]); distinctive for its comparative,
Mesopotamia-informed and explicitly social/economic-organization framing.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Wright's *The Ancient Indus* is the third single-author Indus synthesis in the
collection, and the one most explicitly built around **political economy** and
**social difference**. Where Possehl (2002) argues "civilization without the
state" (heterarchy/corporate) and Kenoyer (1998) frames multi-elite city-states
through craft and the Indus Valley Tradition, Wright integrates agropastoral and
craft economies, landscape construction, and urban form into a single argument:
that Indus cities and crafts alike express a **"transformative technological
style,"** and that the civilization's apparent homogeneity masked a genuinely
**differentiated society** whose status distinctions were visible in the value
of artifacts, amenities, and architecture (contra D. Miller 1985). She adopts
Kenoyer's city-state framing but couples it to a **"vertical integration"**
(council-based) political model drawn from [[trigger-bruce|Trigger]] (2003) and
Charlton & Nichols, and reads political cohesion across great inter-city
distances as maintained by standardized material culture and trade rather than
coercion (no hydraulic despotism). On the end of the civilization she argues for
**uneven regional transformation** — collapse and reorganization in Sindh, but
continuity in the Sorath zone — and opposes Shaffer & Lichtenstein's strong
Post-urban→Ganges continuity thesis, since every defining Indus trait (planned
cities, weights, seals, script, narrative imagery) ends in the Post-urban
period.

The volume is broadly comparative throughout — Wright reads the Indus against
Mesopotamia, Iran (Jiroft), and Vijayanagara, and situates it within Philip
Kohl's "interconnected" third-millennium world via the [[meluhha|Meluhha]]
trade. It enforces the four-way non-identity rule on both the Aryan question
(reaffirming Kennedy 2000 against the massacre/invasion reading) and BMAC
contact (small-scale object exchange, not migration). Its horizon is ~2009 —
pre-Rakhigarhi aDNA — so its population-history statements are date-stamped and
subject to update via the Contradiction and Replication Protocol.
