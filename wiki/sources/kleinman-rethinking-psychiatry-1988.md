cross-cultural psychiatry"** — Kleinman's argument that culture is not marginal but central to
psychiatric disorder, diagnosis, and healing, and his critique of the [[category-fallacy|category
fallacy]] in cross-cultural psychiatric research.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Rethinking Psychiatry* is the programmatic statement of the **[[cross-cultural-psychiatry|new
cross-cultural psychiatry]]** and the canonical source for the **[[category-fallacy]]**. Where
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* turns the interpretive program on chronic
illness and the clinic, *Rethinking Psychiatry* turns it on psychiatry itself — diagnosis, the profession,
and healing — asking a sequence of questions (what is a psychiatric diagnosis; do disorders differ across
cultures; do social relations and meanings shape onset and course; how do professional values shape
psychiatry; how do psychiatrists heal). Its throughline is that psychiatry is itself a cultural system,
studied here by reflexive ethnography of psychiatrists as informants.

Three moves anchor it. First, the **category fallacy** (imposing an unvalidated category cross-culturally)
and the paired **reliability ≠ validity** critique of the WHO comparative studies. Second, a **"cultural
interactionist / modified relativist"** middle position — schizophrenia and mania are real everywhere, but
symptom content, course, stigma, and even the *existence* of a label (e.g., for depression) are culturally
particular — staking out the ground of [[psychiatric-universalism-relativism-debate|the universalism–
relativism debate]]. Third, a comparative theory of **[[symbolic-healing]]**: psychiatry and
psychotherapy are one instance of a pan-human structure of meaning-transformation, not its measure.

Within the vault the book pulls Kleinman toward [[critical-medical-anthropology]] (the Warner political
economy of schizophrenia; the medicalization and psychiatric-abuse material) without abandoning the
interpretive [[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology|meaning-centered]] core — consistent with the drift
his 2020 reflections in *The Illness Narratives* describe. It is the necessary companion to
*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* (1980), which supplies the health-systems
model and the Taiwan fieldwork the argument rests on.
