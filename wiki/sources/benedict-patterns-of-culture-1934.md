# Patterns of Culture (Ruth Benedict, 1934)

Benedict's *Patterns of Culture* (Houghton Mifflin, 1934) is the canonical popular and programmatic statement of **[[configurationism]]** within American [[anthropology]] — the thesis that each culture selects, from "the great arc" of human potentialities, a limited set of traits and elaborates them into an integrated whole, "personality writ large." The book pairs three theoretical chapters (I–III), three ethnographic case chapters (IV Zuni, V Dobu, VI Kwakiutl), and two synthetic chapters (VII–VIII) on the nature of society and the culturally relative status of the deviant individual.

Analyzed as a research act on its [[benedict-patterns-of-culture-study|study page]]; the theoretical program it founded is treated in depth at [[hubs/theory/configurationism|the Configurationism theory hub]].
