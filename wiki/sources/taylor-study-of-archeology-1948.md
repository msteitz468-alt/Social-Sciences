# A Study of Archeology (Taylor, 1948)

[[taylor-walter-w|Walter W. Taylor]]'s programmatic critique of mid-century **Americanist** archaeology and the charter text for the **[[conjunctive-approach|conjunctive approach]]**. Written against the grain of trait-list, presence/absence, time–space culture-history, Taylor argues that archaeology is a **method** for producing cultural information, not itself history or anthropology; that historiography constructs cultural contexts while anthropology compares culture; and that Americanist practice has claimed to "be" anthropology while "doing" thin chronicle. Part II develops a culture concept for archaeology, a typology/classification scheme distinguishing empirical from cultural categories, and a procedure outline for building multi-dimensional **[[cultural-context-archaeology|cultural contexts]]** at the site level.

## Claims Extracted

### Reception (Watson 1983; Taylor 1968)
- Watson: book as powerful early statement of what later became New Archaeology practical concerns; under-implemented because hard, and because it criticized establishment leaders; Taylor anticipated typology debates and context demands.
- Taylor 1968: basics of the argument still stand; refuses to soften the "polemic" chapter; hopes for more culture-oriented practice.

### Program frame (Part I Introduction)
- Americanist archaeologist as Jekyll–Hyde: claims to "do" history while "being" an anthropologist without relating the two.
- Part I destructive critique; Part II constructive procedures; one conceptual whole.
- **Conjunctive approach** named against comparative/taxonomic goals: first concern is description of cultures via associations, not time–space placement alone.

### Ch. 1–2 — Discipline and the history/anthropology split
- Sharp institutional split: Classical/Near Eastern vs anthropological archaeology (esp. United States).
- Four senses of "history"; **historiography** = projection of contemporary thought about past actuality in cultural terms with time sequences.
- Historical disciplines share four procedural levels ending in integration/synthesis/context.
- **Cultural anthropology** (for Taylor) = comparative study of the nature and workings of culture; **ethnography** = construction of cultural contexts = a branch of historiography, not of anthropology proper.
- **Archaeology *per se*** = method and specialized techniques for gathering cultural information; at the fourth level (contexts) it is historiography; when contexts support comparative study of culture it is anthropology. **"Archeology is neither history nor anthropology"** as autonomous discipline.

### Ch. 3 — Americanist practice critique (publications, not persons)
- Detailed critique of leaders including **Kidder** (most influential; anthropological aims, comparative/chronicle practice), **Haury**, **Roberts**, **Webb** (most cultural pictures among recent Americanists yet diverted by Midwestern trait lists), **Ritchie**, **Griffin** (trait lists as "cultures"; laboratory taxonomist).
- Trait-list presence/absence as insufficient representation of culture; time–space coordinates without multi-dimensional life picture; Carnegie Maya work as hierarchical/descriptive/chronological with neglect of common/domestic life (Taylor's reading).

### Ch. 4–5 — Culture concept and typology
- Culture concept is necessary first step of the conjunctive approach; culture as mental/ideational (not "material culture" as culture itself — fallacious conflation).
- Archaeological data = material results of cultural behavior **and** affinities among them (and environment).
- **Cultural context** ≠ aggregate of empirical categories (Stone, Bone…); requires cultural headings (Food, Dress, Hunting…).
- **"More interpretation is called for, not less"** — labeled inference and hypothesis-testing mandatory if history or culture is the aim.
- Type vs class; empirical vs cultural types; adaptive use of Linton (form/use/function/meaning), Kluckhohn (configuration), Rouse; quantification can reverse similarity judgments (textile industries example).

### Ch. 6 — Conjunctive procedures
- Distinctive features lie in **mental attitude and broad objectives**, not a rigid technique kit.
- Primacy of **constructing cultural context**; extra-local comparison is means, not end, until context is built.
- Comparative work must use cultural (not merely empirical) categories and **quantification**; presence/absence trait lists have limited function.
- Mechanical stratigraphy alone as "deus ex machina" fails fullest context (associations by units of deposit formation preferred).
- **Report outline:** (1) problem; (2) description and interpretation; (3) local chronology; (4) **cultural context (most concern)**; (5) extra-local relationships as cultural entity; (6) optional special problems (culture, art, architecture, ceramics…).
- Minimum for social science vs antiquarianism: construct fullest possible cultural contexts.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*A Study of Archeology* is the vault's primary charter for a **proto-processual critique internal to Americanist culture-history**: not a European Childe-style culture map, but an attack on reducing culture to trait inventory and time–space placement. Relative to already-ingested material it supplies the missing American voice that [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Willey & Phillips]] partly answered (archaeology as anthropology; process language) without adopting Taylor's full conjunctive report hierarchy, and that later processualism radicalized. It densifies [[culture-historical-archaeology]] with an internal critique; pairs with [[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Wheeler]] on excavation standards while warning that stratigraphy alone does not equal cultural context; and clarifies the history/anthropology boundary that the [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]] and four-field identity debates keep reopening.

Limits: polemical case readings of named monographs; feasibility of data demands; 1948 absolute chronology and ethnological analogies not updated here. All program and critique claims stay attributed (Taylor 1948; Watson 1983 for reception).
