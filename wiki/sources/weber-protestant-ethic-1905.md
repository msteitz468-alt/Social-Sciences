# The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism (Weber, 1904–5/1920)

Max Weber's *Die protestantische Ethik und der Geist des Kapitalismus* — first published as a two-part article in the *Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik* (1904–5), revised for the *Gesammelte Aufsätze zur Religionssoziologie* (1920–1) — the founding statement of the **[[protestant-ethic-thesis|Protestant-ethic thesis]]** and a central classical-era work of historical and economic sociology. Ingested from the Routledge Classics 2001 reprint of Talcott Parsons's 1930 English translation, with Anthony Giddens's 1976 introduction.

## What the book is

A historical-sociological argument that a distinctive **[[spirit-of-capitalism|spirit of modern capitalism]]** — methodical acquisition as ethical duty, not mere greed — found an elective affinity with the **[[inner-worldly-asceticism|inner-worldly asceticism]]** of the Reformed Protestant traditions (above all Calvinism), mediated by the ethic of the **[[calling-beruf|calling (Beruf)]]**. Weber presents this as one causal strand among others, not a monocausal "religion caused capitalism" story; the closing pages refuse to substitute a one-sided spiritualistic interpretation for a one-sided materialistic one. The study sits inside Weber's larger programme on the **[[rationalization|rationalization]]** of Western culture and is explicitly provisional relative to the comparative sociology of the world religions.

## Reliability and voice

Every substantive thesis is **attributed** (Weber 1904–5/1920, or Giddens 1976 as reception). Standing cautions:

1. **Not monocausal.** Weber's own closing caveat forbids reading the essay as pure idealist inversion of Marxism.
2. **Ideal type, not average.** The "spirit of capitalism" and "traditionalism" are constructed types; empirical mixtures are expected.
3. **Parsons translation.** Standard English carrier but contested on key terms (*Geist*, *stahlhartes Gehäuse* → "iron cage"); note translation issues where they matter for concepts.
4. **Giddens introduction** is secondary historiography — file under debate/reception, not as Weber's claims.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Protestant Ethic* is the wiki's first Weber primary source and the classical statement of culture–economy **elective affinity**. It triangulates founders already ingested: against [[historical-materialism|Marx]] it refuses both crude economic reflection *and* pure spiritual monocausality; toward [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] it shares the modernity problem of calculability and [[money-economy]] while distinguishing money economy from capitalism and free-labour organization; toward [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] it offers historical-ethical rather than moral-statistical explanation of modern economic discipline; toward [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]] it offers a religious-ethical rather than evolutionary-institutional account of bourgeois accumulation norms. The standing [[protestant-ethic-debate]] is open. *Economy and Society* and the world-religion volumes remain the chief missing Weberian depth.
