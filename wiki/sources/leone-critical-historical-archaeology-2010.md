# Critical Historical Archaeology (Leone, 2010)

[[leone-mark-p|Mark P. Leone]]'s *Critical Historical Archaeology* (Left Coast / Routledge, 2010) is a **career retrospective and program statement** for critical historical archaeology in the United States: fourteen shortened reprints of Leone essays, each preceded by autobiographical framing that ties outdoor history museums, Annapolis, Mormon material culture, public tours, and African American archaeology to Marxist and Althusserian theory of [[ideology]]. The book's declared aim is to show how historical archaeologists can find problems from **feelings** about living U.S. history and connect material remains to present conditions — without abandoning scientific method.

## Core Argument (attributed)

Leone argued that historical archaeology should articulate **problems adequate to its data and to living society** (segregation, poverty, partial democracy, destruction, waste) by starting from the archaeologist's emotional reaction to material remains and outdoor history, then connecting those problems to critical theory — especially Althusserian [[ideology]] as naturalization of contradiction under capitalism. Classic demonstration: the **[[william-paca-garden|William Paca Garden]]** in Annapolis read as perspective-rules that naturalize order while masking slave wealth vs. liberty ideology. Programmatic essay **"Toward a Critical Archaeology"** (with Parker Potter) operationalizes Althusser for public-facing excavation and interpretation. The book also records **failures and partial successes** of critical public archaeology (tours, exhibits), not only program triumphs.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Leone 2010 is the vault's **primary critical-HA charter**, complementary to Orser 1996's **global** program charter and Deetz 1996's **Georgian worldview** synthesis. Where Orser organizes the modern world by four haunts and mutualist nets, Leone organizes U.S. historic material culture by **Althusserian ideology** and **public unmasking**. Where Deetz reads multi-domain order as cognitive re-Anglicization, Leone keeps the pattern and rewrites the mechanism as naturalization of hierarchy under merchant capitalism (Paca Garden; forks/clocks; museum galleries). The book's distinctive methodological claim — **feelings as the preexisting condition** of research problems — is autobiographical and pedagogical, not a substitute for excavation quality. Part 3's honest failure register (slide shows, HPC conflict, limited ideology uptake) is load-bearing: critical archaeology is a research program with an empirical record of public experiments, not only a theoretical stance. Gaps remaining: full monographic Annapolis reports; complete *Current Anthropology* discussion of 1987; Handler & Gable / Wallace museum studies as primary; Deagan and other HA peers still outstanding on the roadmap.
