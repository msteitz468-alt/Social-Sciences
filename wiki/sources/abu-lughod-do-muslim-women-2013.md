# Do Muslim Women Need Saving? (Abu-Lughod 2013)

Lila [[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]]'s Harvard UP book expanding her 2002 *American Anthropologist* article (itself from a 2001 Columbia lecture, prompted by PBS NewsHour solicitations framing Afghanistan in religio-cultural rather than political terms), a critique of the post-9/11 discourse that framed the U.S. war in Afghanistan and Western feminist/humanitarian campaigns as rescuing Muslim women.

## Argument

Her four summary claims (all attributed — theses, not findings): (1) Muslim women's suffering has multiple causes, not reducible to religion or culture; (2) gender-patterned rights violations are real but configured differently by context and era; (3) no society is immune, and selective attention to distant suffering disavows complicity; (4) always interrogate who has the standing to define others' deficits and prescribe remedies. Chapter arc: the Afghanistan war-for-women rationale and the burqa (Hanna Papanek's "portable seclusion"); the "new common sense" of *Half the Sky* and *The Honor Code* and the "IslamLand" fantasy geography; the "pulp nonfiction" abuse-memoir genre as [[gendered-orientalism]]; the [[honor-crime]] as constructed category; the "social life" of Muslim women's rights (Egyptian NGO field, governance feminism, Musawah/WISE); ethnography of rights in an Upper Egyptian village; "registers of humanity" as the alternative to rescue and to relativism.

## Evidence base

Mixed: discourse/media analysis (speeches, covers, memoirs, NGO documents) plus the author's long-term ethnography — [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] Bedouin (late 1970s–1980s, revisits into the 2010s) and an Upper Egyptian village (from 1993; key visits 2008–2012; interlocutors Zaynab, Amal, ʿAysha, Khadija — all pseudonyms). Draws on her 2009 Radcliffe-Brown Lecture (per acknowledgments). Ethnographic vignettes are dated; the argumentative superstructure is attributed voice throughout.
