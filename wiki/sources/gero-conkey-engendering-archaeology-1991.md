# Engendering Archaeology: Women and Prehistory (Gero & Conkey, eds., 1991)

First major Anglophone multi-author collection to put **[[feminist-archaeology|feminist / gender archaeology]]** on empirical footing: dual object of **gender systems in prehistory** and **in archaeological practice**. Series editor [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]; co-editor [[conkey-margaret-w|Margaret Conkey]] also advisory editor. Production: Wedge Plantation conference, April 1988 (discussants [[moore-henrietta-l|Henrietta Moore]], Irene Silverblatt, Peter White); 1989 SAA symposium follow-up. Agenda precursor: Conkey & [[spector-janet|Spector]] 1984.

## Claims Extracted

### Preface + Program (Conkey & Gero; Wylie)

- Mid-1980s archaeology lagged sister disciplines on women as sociocultural subjects; feminist social-theory volume “long overdue” (Gero & Conkey preface).
- Conkey & Spector 1984 agenda still met “no archaeological literature to cite” ~3 years later; androcentric assumptions still structured hypothesis-making.
- **Wedge 1988** conference: deliberately prehistoric cases (hardest androcentrism challenge); Wenner-Gren + NSF; discussants Moore, Silverblatt, White.
- Volume dual aim: gender systems in **prehistory** and in **practice of archaeology**; women as entry point (both sexes have gender).
- Three aims: (1) expose gender bias in all inquiry phases; (2) “find” women / gender relations-roles-ideologies; (3) problematize assumptions about gender and difference.
- Anti-“add women and stir”; reject biological determinism; gender as historically contingent social construction.
- **Gender attribution not required** as prerequisite for gender research (Conkey & Gero; developed across cases).
- Gender no less “real” than population pressure / subsistence-settlement systems (parity vs abstract processual constructs).
- Aligns with [[post-processual-archaeology]] openings (social/symbolic theory) while developing own frameworks (not static borrow).
- **Wylie:** rejects “women/gender inaccessible archaeologically” as unsatisfactory explanation of the gap; stages of feminist inquiry (critique of androcentrism → reclaim subjects → rework categories); prospects good early 1990s; engages [[processual-archaeology|Binford]] eco-system framing of “ethnographic” variables; pluralism for gender archaeology; feminist philosophy of science (empiricism–standpoint–postmodern).

### Part II — Space and Gender

- **Conkey (Magdalenian):** not “reveal Magdalenian women” but model contexts of action/power where gender is “at work”; problem is scarcity of people as agents, not only of women; UP monolith/simulacrum critique; cordage and connectedness undervalued; Cueto de la Mina Level B as case entry.
- **Tringham:** South Carolina “Aha” — from “gender invisible in architecture” to **households with faces**; faceless economic household blobs; Opovo (SE Europe, ~4400–4200 BCE) architectural program; house-burning; dialogue with Gimbutas/Hodder/Sherratt; remedial vs transformative feminism.
- **Hastorf:** gender = socially constructed M/F; food systems express political/social/gender relations; spatial paleoethnobotany + stable isotopes on sexed burials; **Sausa (Wanka)** Mantaro Valley — Wanka II vs III (Inka): patio crop patterns; Wanka III males more maize/meat (*chicha*, *mit'a*); state circumscribes women’s activities while domestic production retained (attributed).

### Part III — Material Production

- **Gero ([[genderlithics]]):** challenges stone-tools-as-male; experimental flintknapping literature ~100% male samples; microwear ~1:1 F:M vs AAA archaeology ~1:5; constraints (scheduling, raw material, strength, social value); minimal criteria: habitation contexts, local materials, expedient flakes (~90% of record); **Huaricoto** (Peru) EIP: bifaces drop, local flake tools dominate — women’s household lithics rise as male prestige shifts off stone (attributed model).
- **Wright:** women’s labor and pottery, South Asia / Harappan sequence; feminist technology history (McGaw); women not automatically excluded by commercialization/urban craft quarters; challenges universal state-formation models of women’s subordination (attributed).
- **Brumfiel:** Aztec weaving and cooking as core women’s production (Sahagún); cloth as state medium; spindle-whorl patterns Valley of Mexico vs Morelos under Aztec rule; pot:griddle shift to portable tortilla foods; ethnohistory as “model for” not “model of”; elite vs popular ideologies of women’s work (attributed).

### Part IV — Food Systems

- **Watson & Kennedy:** textbook sex-linked DoL schemes project men-hunt/women-gather onto Eastern Woodlands domestication; three domestication episodes; women as chief protagonists of plant domestication (Smith domestilocalities); critique of Prentice “shaman cultivator” (innovation-to-men); Northern Flint as deliberate women’s work; preferred position: women intentionally domesticated plants.
- **Claassen:** ethnographic women shellfishers vs archaeological **de-gendering** of shellfishing; Shell Mound Archaic definition/chronology; reject simple overexploitation/OFT demise stories; alternatives (starchy seeds, fishing, dispersal, ceremonial shell ideology); gender testability vs sexism-of-imagination.
- **Jackson:** “Woman the Gatherer” banner; Western Mono / Sierra acorn production; bedrock mortars as women’s fixed property; K-sites; matrilocal Northfork Mono; women’s surplus negotiated into men’s inter-group exchange; devaluation of ground stone vs flaked stone (androcentric technology hierarchy).

### Part V + Epilogue — Images and Method

- **Handsman ([[lepenski-vir]]):** Southampton exhibit politics; “whose art?” answered as Males’ Art (Srejović architect-magician narrative; sculpture names Adam/Sorcerer/Old Chief); women as Lady/Mermaid/First Mother/Vulva only; discourse productive of gender inequality (Foucault/Said); gender-as-relations; communal surplus and dual art traditions (attributed re-reading).
- **Pollock:** representations of Sumerian women cannot equal “reality” but structure experience of power/prestige; literary texts rarely feature human women (auxiliary/nurturing roles); economic texts show queens/estates; Royal Cemetery of Ur burial patterns; iconography (seals, plaques); Early Dynastic–Sargonic state contexts (**OCR: chapter opening truncated**).
- **Spector:** parameters of explicitly feminist archaeology since early 1970s; white Western middle-class male domination of academic knowledge; **task differentiation** approach; Little Rapids / Wahpeton Dakota case; narrative “What This Awl Means” as feminist interpretive form (POSITION, not wiki-voice ethnography); critique of prior awl stories (Stone/Mason).
- **Moore:** no single narrative of women and production; “women” not unproblematic category; feminist archaeology = theorize gender as structuring principle, not merely identify women/activities; gender and division of labor, hypothesis generation, inscription of difference.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Engendering Archaeology* is this vault’s **charter collection for [[feminist-archaeology]]**, analogous to *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* for [[processual-archaeology]]: multi-author demonstration that a theoretically stigmatized problem (here gender; there social process) is researchable. Relative to ingested sources: densifies the feminist strand named inside [[post-processual-archaeology]]; connects archaeology to [[feminist-anthropology]] and [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|Woman-the-Gatherer]] debates; operationalizes gender across lithics, ceramics, architecture, food, and images. Outstanding next: Conkey & Spector 1984 primary article; Spector *What This Awl Means* (1993 book); later gender/queer archaeology handbooks.
