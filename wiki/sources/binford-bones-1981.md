# Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths (Binford 1981)

Academic Press, Studies in Archaeology series; dedicated to the memory of David L. Clarke. Binford's programmatic demonstration that inferences about ancient hominid behavior drawn from faunal ("bone") assemblages — Dart's osteodontokeratic culture, "man the hunter," early-hominid home bases and food-sharing — are **modern myths** unless secured by independent **middle-range research**: actualistic studies of how carnivores, other natural agents, and living humans (Nunamiut) produce and modify bone assemblages. The book builds diagnostic methods (bone modification, breakage, cut marks, assemblage composition indices) and applies them to reanalyze the Olduvai Gorge fauna, concluding for hominid **scavenging** rather than hunting.

## Structure

- **Part I — Documenting a long-term archaeological problem** (Ch 1 conceptual frameworks: relics→assemblages, Dart/Breuil/Weidenreich; Ch 2 middle-range research and actualistic studies)
- **Part II — Middle-range research, in search of methodology** (Ch 3 nonhuman bone modification; Ch 4 human bone modification/butchery; Ch 5 assemblage composition, man vs. beast)
- **Part III — Putting our knowledge to work** (Ch 6 Olduvai Gorge reanalysis; Ch 7 general conclusions)

## Claims Extracted

**Program / method** — the archaeological record is contemporary statics; converting it to statements about past dynamics requires [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range research]] that is actualistic and *intellectually independent* of the theories it tests (else one commits "confirming the consequent"). Binford separates the **paradigm** (guide to describing, after Kuhn) from **theory** (guide to explaining, after Hempel), and introduces *grain*, *historical integrity*, and *resolution* for evaluating deposits. Critiques Schiffer's "behavioral archaeology" and R.A. Gould's minimize-uniformitarianism claim.

**Taphonomy / actualistic control** — 12,716 dog-yard bones + 416 wolf-den bones + 122,000+ Nunamiut bones; tooth-mark typology (punctures, pits, scores, furrows), "channeling," "chipping back"; density-mediated destruction by anatomical part; spiral fracture, chipped-back edges, and pseudo-tools shown to be produced by carnivore gnawing and trampling (Myers et al. 1980 on pre-human assemblages) — not diagnostic of human agency (contra Dart, Bonnichsen, Frison, Breuil).

**Human butchery** — Nunamiut cut-mark form and placement (skinning / disarticulation / filleting classes), marrow-bone percussion breakage; comparison with Combe Grenal Mousterian ([[bordes-francois|Bordes]]); disputes Frison's "muscle-stripping" chopper model and Spiess on inferring tool type from marks.

**Assemblage composition** — MNI, utility indices, bone-density → survival-percentage regression to reconstruct "pristine" from ravaged assemblages; wolf den vs. kill vs. lair/palimpsest; hyena dens (Sutcliffe, Kruuk) and [[brain-ck|Brain]]'s Hottentot goat study reframing [[makapansgat|Makapansgat]].

**Olduvai reanalysis (Ch. 6–7)** — factor analysis of Mary Leakey's (1971) part-frequency data; Factor 1 = survivorship (destruction); conclusion (attributed) that the sites fit hominid **scavenging** of low-utility marrow bones, percussion tools dominant, cutting tools near-absent, **no home bases and no food sharing** — the "man the hunter" / home-base picture (Isaac; R. Leakey and Lewin) is a "modern myth." Choukoutien cannibalism/fire myths and cave-bear cults similarly deflated. Correlation (association) ≠ behavioral integration.
