# The Workers' Opposition (1921)

The only programmatic text of the Workers' Opposition, written by Kollontai for the Tenth Party Congress and banned by it — the second counter-source of the re-balance program, giving the [[soviet-trade-union-debate]] its fourth position in its author's own words. Single-agent extraction, ~22k words complete.

## Kollontai's argument (attributed)

- **The stakes:** not a technical union question but "cardinal economic and political questions" — who shall build the communist economy: the workers organized in their class unions, or state functionaries "over their heads." The Theses' demand: "organisation of control over the social economy is a prerogative of the All-Russian Congress of Producers."
- **Against one-man management:** the collective-vs-individual management dispute (from the Ninth Congress, 1920) as a class-principled divide — "one-man management is a product of the individualist conception of the bourgeois class"; the leadership's position a "tactical compromise" and "deviation from class policy."
- **Against the vanguard substitution:** "Only the vanguard of the class can create revolution, but only the whole class can develop through its everyday experience the practical work of the basic class collectives" — a direct rebuttal of Lenin's unions-as-schools position and of reliance on bourgeois specialists (with her feudal-to-capitalist analogy: the rising class builds its own production forms).
- **Theory of bureaucracy:** "a direct negation of mass self-activity," rooted not in misery (contra Zinoviev) or wartime habit but in the penetration of Soviet institutions by elements hostile to working-class self-activity; against Trotsky's "good sides of bureaucracy," outright rejection ([[bureaucracy]]).
- **Program:** the six-point union-management transition; Party democracy demands (purge of non-proletarian elements, election over appointment, recallability, ban on dual Party/state office-holding).
- **Loyalty frame:** no split intended; the Manifesto invoked ("the building of Communism can and must be the work of the toiling masses themselves"); confidence that "Ilyich will listen."

## The Solidarity apparatus (1961/68 — secondary commentary, attributed)

The editors reprint the text while rejecting its framework: Kollontai's class-origin theory of bureaucratization (blaming peasant/bourgeois infiltration) prevented her from concluding that the party's own ideology, structure, and monopoly of decision were bureaucracy-generating regardless of staffing; citing Brinton, they note the unions she would have empowered were already party-controlled by 1921; they record Lenin's charge of "petty-bourgeois anarchism" and that Kollontai and most Oppositionists denounced Kronstadt and stayed Bolshevik. The 1968 self-revision questions the "homogeneous working class" premise itself. All recorded as positions of a partisan editorial collective, useful precisely as a second, differently-partisan lens.

## Reliability

A banned faction's manifesto surviving through activist transmission: the translation is anonymous and period-polemical, the pamphlet apparatus doubly partisan (Bolshevik-era author, anti-Leninist editors). Its factual reports of 1920–21 conditions are a participant's claims; its account of Lenin's, Trotsky's, and Bukharin's positions can now be triangulated against their own ingested texts.
