# Early Mesopotamia: Society and Economy at the Dawn of History

J. N. Postgate, *Early Mesopotamia: Society and Economy at the Dawn of History*
(Routledge; first ed. 1992, revised paperback 1994). A thematic reconstruction of
early Mesopotamian society and economy, c. 3000–1500 BCE, built primarily from the
cuneiform documentary record read together with the archaeology. Where
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] narrates the dynastic-political sequence and
[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Trigger 2003]] / [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|Adams]]
set the comparative frame, Postgate supplies the **institutional and economic
anatomy**: the household, the temple and palace estates, land tenure, labour and
rationing, exchange and silver, law and order.

Its value to this wiki is depth on the society/economy layer — the pages
[[temple-economy]], [[city-state]], [[sumerians]], and the economic life of
[[ur]]/[[uruk]]/[[lagash]] — plus a small number of institution/concept pages the
narrative sources did not warrant.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Postgate supplies the **society-and-economy anatomy** that the narrative sources leave
thin. Read against [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] (dynastic-political narrative) and
[[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|Adams]]/[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Trigger]]
(comparative frame), it fills the institutional middle: the **household/oikos** as the
basic unit (scaled up into the temple and palace "great households"), **land tenure**,
the **agricultural and irrigation regime**, **labour and rationing**, **weighed-silver
money** and **long-distance trade**, and **law** as practice rather than code.

Its single most consequential move for this wiki is its **calibrated rejection of the old
"temple-state" model**. Where a strong reading (Deimel, on the Presargonic Girsu–Bau
archive) made the temple the owner of essentially all land and people, Postgate — with
Diakonoff — argues the temple was one major sector among several (private, communal/
household, and palace land coexisting), and that the Girsu archive is an unrepresentative,
looted, partial record. This is promoted to a standing debate page,
[[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]], and it retroactively sharpens the temple-economy
correction already noted from Roux. A second through-line is Postgate's **source-critical
discipline**: he is unusually explicit that the cuneiform record is a survivorship sample
(clay durability, destruction-by-fire as accidental preservation, excavation-quality
variance), so the economy's documented sector is not the whole economy — a caution now
recorded on [[cuneiform]] and [[temple-economy]].

Postgate also restates, in his own voice, the **four-way non-identity** principle
(criticizing inference of ethnic intrusion from pottery-style change), reinforcing the
cautions on [[sumerians]]. Several disputes are recorded as open positions rather than
adjudicated: the Diakonoff–Leemans extended-family land question (on [[household]]), the
Gelb-vs-revisionist Ur III labour-status question (on [[craft-specialization]]), the Ur
III trade-monopoly question (on [[mesopotamian-trade]]), and the Gulf-shoreline and
Jacobsen–Adams salinization disputes (on [[mesopotamia-geography]]). No `studies/` page or
hub is warranted — this is an analytical synthesis, not a primary research act.
