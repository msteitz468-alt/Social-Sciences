# The Russian Revolution: From Lenin to Stalin, 1917–1929 (Carr 1979)

## Bibliographic note

E. H. Carr, *The Russian Revolution: From Lenin to Stalin, 1917–1929* (New York: The Free Press / Macmillan, 1979; first American edition; ISBN 0-02-905140-1). Distills thirty years of research into Carr's four-instalment *History of Soviet Russia* for the general reader and introductory student. Period covered: October 1917 through the collectivization turn and Stalin's fiftieth birthday (1929).

## Key claims inventory


All interpretive claims **Carr-attributed** unless noted as brute chronology convergent with prior ingests.

1. **1917 as world-historical turning-point** — first open challenge to capitalism; consequence and cause of capitalist decline; rooted in Russian conditions (Ch. 1).
2. **Dual power as total dispersal of authority** — not only Provisional Government vs Soviet (Ch. 1).
3. **War Communism two elements** — centralization (partly durable) vs anti-market flight (rejected by NEP) (Ch. 3–4).
4. **NEP as breathing-space** — dual interpretations (return vs retreat-for-attack); Kronstadt may have aided acceptance + faction ban (Ch. 4).
5. **Scissors crisis 1923** — industrial:agricultural prices 3× 1913 at peak; overcome by anti-NEP price controls (Ch. 6).
6. **Lenin Testament** — Stalin power without caution; Trotsky ablest but administrative; proposal to remove Stalin as gen-sec (Ch. 7).
7. **Stalin gen-sec April 1922** — appointment underrated at the time (Ch. 7–8).
8. **Socialism in one country (Dec 1924)** — national/psychological impact; Lenin attribution spurious (Carr) (Ch. 8, 19).
9. **Comintern** — Russian-harnessed international; united front / China 1927 nadir; third period (Chs. 2, 9–10, 18).
10. **Defeat of Left Opposition 1926–27** — machine vs platforms; expulsions (Ch. 12).
11. **Planning vs NEP market** — incompatibility revealed gradually; First FYP landmark (Chs. 11, 15).
12. **Collectivization** — revolution from above; famine 1–5 million (Carr's reported range); Dizzy from Success (Ch. 16).
13. **Stalin as Peter** — ruthless despot and westernizer (Ch. 17).
14. **Closing verdict** — hybrid, flawed, ambiguous; material half of socialism; not proletarian self-emancipation; deeper impact in backward non-capitalist world; most lasting repercussions of any modern event (Ch. 19).
15. **Rejects "state capitalism" label** for Soviet planned economy (Ch. 19).

## Volume Synthesis Note

Carr's 1979 short history is the wiki's first continuous **secondary narrative** of 1917–1929 as a single arc, filling the gap between primary Lenin/Trotsky texts, Avrich's Kronstadt monograph, Service biographies, and Mawdsley–White elite prosopography. Its load-bearing contribution is structural: dualisms (two worlds; dual power as dispersal; War Communism's two elements; market vs plan) and the claim that isolation plus backwardness pushed the regime toward **revolution from above** — industrialization and collectivization — while still producing what Carr insists is the "material, economic half" of socialism rather than mere state capitalism. For the wiki, this enables proper concept pages ([[socialism-in-one-country]], [[scissors-crisis]], [[five-year-plan]], [[collectivization]], [[left-opposition]], [[united-front]]) and a subject-matter spine ([[russian-revolution]], [[comintern]]) that prior Bolshevik-primary ingests lacked. Reliability limits are explicit in Carr's own foreword: no footnotes; distillation for general readers; "view from the regime" institutional focus. Counter-weights already on disk: Avrich (Kronstadt from below), Service (contingency and personality), Kautsky/Trotsky polemics. Outstanding next: Figes *A People's Tragedy* or Carr's full multi-volume set for depth and apparatus.
