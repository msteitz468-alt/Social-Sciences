## Volume Synthesis Note

*Sweetness and Power* is the wiki's charter text for the historical anthropology of food and for commodity-centered joining of colony and metropolis. Mintz's "homecoming" method — follow production into consumption — complements [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]]'s relational world-history without duplicating mode-of-production apparatus: the book specializes the [[anthropological-political-economy]] neighborhood into edible circuits, [[plantation]] labor regimes, and everyday meaning.

Three analytic products carry the argument. First, the **[[sugar-plantation-complex]]** and **[[factory-in-the-field]]** redescribe sugar estates as industrial organization from the early modern Atlantic through 20th-century Puerto Rican cane proletarians. Second, the British consumption series (five early uses; tea–sugar coupling; free-trade acceleration; jam after ~1870; per-capita rise toward ~90 lb by 1900 as Mintz reconstructs) document massification without treating demand as natural. Third, the conceptual pairs **[[inside-meaning-and-outside-meaning]]** and **[[extensification-and-intensification]]**, plus the **[[proletarian-hunger-and-drug-foods]]** hypothesis, bind lived use to power and to the cheap reproduction of industrial labor.

What the volume adds relative to already-ingested sources: a worked Atlantic commodity where [[commodity]] and [[commodity-fetishism]] pages had thinner edible cases; densification of Steward-project genealogy via Mintz; a Studies Hub exemplar for mixed historical-anthropological design (explicit about thin contemporary fieldwork in the book itself). Tensions to watch in later ingests: specialist revision of particular consumption series; gender densification; non-British sugar paths; relation of "drug foods" metaphor to medical and addiction literatures.
