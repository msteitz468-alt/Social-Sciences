# Peasants (Eric R. Wolf, 1966)

Prentice-Hall Foundations of Modern Anthropology Series primer on the peasantry as a structural type of rural cultivator situated between "primitive" and industrial society. Series editor [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]. Wolf's double aim: evolutionary phase in the human career, and practical primer for development economists, political scientists, and area specialists who treat the "underdeveloped" world as empty of ordered forms.

## Core Argument

[[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] defines **[[peasant|peasants]]** as rural cultivators whose **surpluses are transferred to a dominant group of rulers** — not isolation vs involvement, but the *character* of involvement ([[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] on primitives as foil). The peasant runs a **household**, not a business enterprise (vs American farmer). Four fund categories structure the household budget: caloric minimum, [[replacement-fund]], [[ceremonial-fund]], [[fund-of-rent]]. The **[[peasant-dilemma]]** balances external demands against household provisioning via two strategies: raise production (when rent/ceremonial liens weaken) or curtail consumption. **[[types-of-domain|Domain types]]** (patrimonial / prebendary / mercantile + administrative extensions) organize surplus extraction; **[[peasant-ecotype|ecotypes]]** (paleotechnic five-forms / neotechnic) organize production; **[[peasant-coalition|coalitions]]** (three-criteria matrix) and ideology mediate adaptation; **[[peasant-movements]]** express latent opposition but require external professional staff for long-range revolutionary strategy under war-induced state breakdown.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Peasants* is a short synthetic charter for mid-century anthropological peasant studies. Its lasting vocabulary — fund of rent, ceremonial fund, replacement fund, types of domain, closed corporate community (from Wolf's earlier papers), paleotechnic/neotechnic ecotypes, coalition matrix — organizes later agrarian anthropology even where the 1966 development-era frame is dated. The book sits at the intersection of [[cultural-ecology]] (ecotypes as energy systems) and political economy (domain and surplus), substantivist-compatible without adjudicating the [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]. It deliberately refuses both the empty-void development model and the "tradition-bound" label: peasantry is ordered, varied, and dynamic between production-increase and consumption-curtailment poles. Political payload of Ch. 4 — jacquerie, social banditry (Hobsbawm), millenarianism as uniting-not-organizing, Communist staff as professional revolutionaries, revolution *with* not *for* peasantry — is first-class content for [[peasant-movements]] and [[revolution]], not a side note. Limits of the primer: second-hand cases, typological risk, gendered under-specification, and the absence of Wolf's later *Peasant Wars* (1969) and *Europe and the People Without History* (1982) densification in this ingest.
