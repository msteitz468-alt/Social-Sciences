# Middletown: A Study in Modern American Culture (Lynd & Lynd, 1929)

## Overview

[[lynd-robert-s|Robert S. Lynd]] and [[lynd-helen-merrell|Helen Merrell Lynd]]'s *Middletown* (1929) is the classic American **community study**: a synchronous, multi-domain portrait of a mid-sized Midwestern city approached "after the manner of social anthropology" (Clark Wissler, Foreword). Fieldwork was conducted chiefly 1924–25 (staff office opened January 1924; field work completed 1925); the baseline comparison horizon is **1890**. The book organizes community life under six activity areas (getting a living, making a home, training the young, using leisure, religious practices, community activities) and a master social cleavage between the **business class** and the **working class**.

Deep analysis of the research act: [[lynd-middletown-study]]; Studies Hub: [[hubs/studies/lynd-middletown-study|hub analysis]]. Society as documented: [[middletown]].

## Volume Synthesis

*Middletown* is the charter American **[[community-study|community study]]**: a mid-sized East-North-Central industrial city (pseudonym; later identified as Muncie, Indiana), approached after the manner of social anthropology, with **1890** as baseline and **1924–25** as fieldwork present. The Lynds refuse a single reform thesis and a better/worse verdict; they organize the life of ~35–38,000 people under six activity trunks and a master occupational cleavage between the [[business-class-and-working-class|business class and working class]] (~71% / 29% of the gainfully employed in their 1920 frame).

Empirically the book is richest on **getting a living** (machine industry, long arm of the job, open shop, advertising-driven wants), **housing and family** (classed domestic standards; romance ideology vs thin companionship; youth autonomy), **schooling** (mass high school as status/leisure cosmos), and **leisure inventions** (auto one per 6.1 persons; movies; radio). Religion and civic chapters document belief persistence with practice erosion, weak technical government, ad-dependent press, and solidarity cross-cut by class, Jewish "belonging" limits, Negro exclusion, and the early-1920s Klan episode. The Conclusion's **uneven rates of change** thesis (material living and leisure outrunning home, school, civic forms, and especially formal religion) is the book's portable theoretical residue — a cultural-lag style claim without a formal Ogburn law.

Against the wiki's other urban/community material: *The City* (1925) is a Chicago **ecological program** for a metropolis; *The Polish Peasant* is **documentary primary-group** depth; Whyte's Cornerville is **subcommunity** participant observation. *Middletown* is whole-city, six-domain, Institute-sponsored, deliberately low-minority for design isolation of "cultural change" — a choice that makes race appear as cleavage (segregation, Klan) while remaining off the master analytic drumbeat. Sample limits (native-born white family schedules; Negro HS answers excluded from tabulations) must travel with every generalization.
