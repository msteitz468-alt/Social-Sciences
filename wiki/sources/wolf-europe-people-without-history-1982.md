# Europe and the People Without History (Eric R. Wolf, 1982)

Landmark work of anthropological [[anthropological-political-economy|political economy]] by [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]]. Argues that the world of humankind is a totality of interconnected processes; that names like "nation," "society," and "culture" become false models when reified as billiard-ball entities; and that peoples anthropology studied as isolated "people without history" were always already enmeshed in wider fields of force — empire, long-distance trade, and, after European expansion, the capitalist [[mode-of-production|mode of production]]. Uses a three-mode typology (capitalist mode, [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]]) to organize global ethnohistory from c. 1400 through industrial capitalism, commodity circuits, and the making of new laborers.

Edition ingested: University of California Press 2010 (© 1982, 1997, 2010), with foreword by Thomas Hylland Eriksen and cartographic illustrations by Noël L. Diaz. Dedicated to Sydel Silverman.

## Core Argument

[[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]]'s central assertion: the world of humankind constitutes a manifold of interconnected processes; disassembling it into bounded nations/societies/cultures without reassembly falsifies reality. The ironic title challenges the premise that non-Europeans (and "primitives") lack history. From a 1400 baseline of interlocking hemispheric networks, European expansion reorganized the globe under mercantile then industrial capitalism. The analytical engine is the Marxian [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] — especially the triad of capitalist, [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], and [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] modes — used as research-orienting suppositions, not Systems-Marxist universal laws (1997 preface). Commodity histories (silver, fur, slaves, cotton, sugar, tea/opium, etc.) and the formation of working classes show how incorporation into capitalist circuits remade peoples anthropology had treated as timeless tribes.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Europe and the People Without History* is the synthetic charter of Anglophone [[anthropological-political-economy]]. Its load-bearing moves are (1) the interconnection / billiard-ball critique of social-science units; (2) the ironic [[people-without-history]] challenge to Eurocentric and ethnographic-present historiography; (3) the three-mode toolkit ([[mode-of-production|capitalist]], [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]]) as research guides for how [[social-labor|social labor]] is mobilized; (4) the sharp boundary between mercantile wealth and capitalism-in-production, grounding the open [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]] with [[world-systems-analysis]]; (5) commodity and labor ethnohistories (silver, fur, slaves, cotton, indenture, ethnic segmentation) that show articulation and transformation without isolation myths. The 1997 preface and Afterword answer economism charges by relocating culture as process and naming as power inside modes. Limits: second-hand regional chapters age with specialists; gender and subaltern voice thinner than later literatures; title irony can fail. Relative to *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]*, this volume scales household surplus politics to the world-historical field of force.
