## Claims Extracted

All claims are Mills's argued positions, recorded in **attributed voice** (the Appendix is likewise
Mills; the Afterword is Todd Gitlin, 2000, attributed to Gitlin). Core theses:

- **The sociological imagination** — the quality of mind grasping biography × history × structure;
  the three questions (structure / historical place / human variety); the mark of the "classic
  social analyst."
- **Personal troubles of milieu vs. public issues of social structure** — with the canonical worked
  examples (unemployment, war, marriage, the city); the analyst's task of translating one into the
  other.
- **Grand theory** — Parsons's *The Social System* as high-abstraction formalism ("drunk on syntax,
  blind to semantics"; "the fetishism of the Concept"); the value-order frame excludes power and
  conflict; Mills's plain-English "translations."
- **Abstracted empiricism** — Lazarsfeld/Bureau survey research and "the methodological inhibition";
  thinness of results; drift toward psychologism and administrative service.
- **The bureaucratic ethos** — the bureaucratization of social research itself; liberal vs. illiberal
  practicality; "prediction and control" as the bureaucrat's perspective; autonomy as the condition
  of publicly responsible social science.
- **Philosophies of science / method** — "Let every man be his own methodologist"; the "classic"
  (macroscopic) middle path between grand theory and abstracted empiricism; verification and the
  ethics of scholarship.
- **The human variety** and the **uses of history** — the nation-state as generic unit; the unity of
  the social sciences by problem not department; "all sociology worthy of the name is 'historical
  sociology'"; the principle of historical specificity; no trans-historical social law.
- **Reason and freedom** — the "Fourth Epoch"; "rationality without reason"; the [[cheerful-robot]];
  freedom as the chance to formulate and argue the available choices.
- **On politics** — the three political roles (philosopher-king / adviser / independent educator of
  publics); the politics of truth; democracy as effective voice for those affected; the social
  scientist's task of building publics against a mass society.
- **On intellectual craftsmanship** (Appendix) — scholarship as a way of life; the "file"; the
  techniques for stimulating the imagination; craft vs. methodology; against "socspeak"; the eight
  closing precepts.
- **Gitlin's Afterword (2000)** — Mills as "the most inspiring sociologist of the second half of the
  twentieth century"; the New Left adoption (Port Huron, Hayden); the durability of the twin critique;
  the fault that Mills "did not sufficiently apply his sociological imagination to... race."

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Sociological Imagination* is not, like the other two legs of Mills's trilogy, a study of a
stratum; it is a study of **the discipline itself** — a work of reflexive sociology avant la lettre
that supplies the wiki's sharpest statement of what sociology is *for*. It completes the arc the
collection already held: where [[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]] and
[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]] deployed the sociological imagination on the middle and
upper reaches of American power, this book names and defends the instrument. Its two polemical
targets are both already present in the wiki as their own charters — [[parsons-social-system-1951|
Parsons's *Social System*]] ([[structural-functionalism]]) and the survey tradition — so the book
sits at a genuine intersection: it is the wiki's first primary text to attack another primary text
in its holdings by name, and the [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]] page records that
collision without adjudicating it.

Two tensions with existing content are worth flagging. First, Mills's "bureaucratic ethos" chapter
reads [[bureaucracy|Weberian bureaucracy]] against the grain — as politically corrosive rather than
a neutral ideal type — a `challenges` relation now noted on that page. Second, the book converges
with the wiki's [[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt-School]] holdings ([[instrumental-reason]],
[[critical-theory]]) on the reason–freedom problem while staying more Weberian and less totalizing;
Mills cites Horkheimer directly but frames the "cheerful robot" as an American, empirical diagnosis
rather than a philosophy of history. The most consequential open item is editorial, not
substantive: with the trilogy complete, a **Mills Thinkers-Hub biography** is now warranted by the
selection criteria and its stated precondition is met — flagged on [[mills-c-wright]] for the
curator's go-ahead rather than built unilaterally in this ingest.
