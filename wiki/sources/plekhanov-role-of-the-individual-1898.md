# On the Role of the Individual in History (1898)

Plekhanov's classic essay on what later social theory names the structure/agency problem — the great-man question answered from within historical materialism. ~15k words, 1-agent extraction.

## The argument (attributed)

- **The core thesis:** influential individuals "can change the individual features of events and some of their particular consequences, but they cannot change their general trend" — established through the Mirabeau/Robespierre/Napoleon counterfactuals (had each died early, events' *features* differ, the Revolution's direction does not) and the Louis XV/Pompadour case studies on the other side (where weak structural constraint let personal accident matter more).
- **Against the "optical illusion" of great-man causation:** a talent that succeeds occludes the field of equivalent talents that social conditions would have promoted in its place — Napoleon's "personal power" appears in place of the general causes it instantiates.
- **"Talent is socially produced":** two conditions govern an individual's historical influence — talent matching the *social needs of the epoch*, and the social order not barring the person bearing it. Great men are "beginners" (his correction of Carlyle): they begin what deeper relations have prepared.
- **Freedom and necessity again:** consciousness of necessity as the ground of energetic action, with the "arithmetic of inaction" argument against fatalist quietism (the essay's answer to the charge that determinism paralyzes).
- **Historiographic frame:** the French Restoration historians against 18th-century individualist history; the Bismarck/Lamprecht/Monod controversy; Sainte-Beuve's Cleopatra's-nose challenge taken seriously and answered.
