# Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition

Ronald S. Burt, *Structural Holes: The Social Structure of Competition* (Harvard
University Press, 1992; paperback 1995). A landmark of network sociology and
economic sociology that recasts **competition as a matter of social structure**:
the competitive advantage of a person or an organization is a function of the
**structural holes** — gaps between non-redundant contacts — that its network
spans. The book is both a theoretical program (the *[[structural-holes-theory|
structural-holes theory]]* of social capital and the *[[tertius-gaudens|tertius
gaudens]]*) and two original empirical tests (see the study page
[[burt-structural-holes-study]]).

Most of the manuscript was drafted May–June 1990 while Burt was a Fellow at the
Netherlands Institute for Advanced Study, and revised in 1991 at Columbia
University's Center for the Social Sciences; the research was supported by the
National Science Foundation. At the time of writing Burt directed a research
program at Columbia. See [[burt-ronald-s]].

## What the Book Argues

*(Attributed to Burt throughout — this is a theoretical program, not established
fact.)* A **structural hole** is a relationship of non-redundancy between two
contacts; a network rich in holes gives its holder non-overlapping streams of
information (access, timing, referrals) and **control** advantages as the
*tertius gaudens*, "the third who benefits" (from [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]).
Players with networks optimized for holes — high **structural autonomy** — earn
higher rates of return because they know about, participate in, and control more
rewarding opportunities. Burt formalizes this as the **[[network-constraint|
network-constraint]]** measure and tests it at two levels: the market (Ch. 3) and
the manager (Ch. 4). Later chapters extend the argument to player–structure
duality and "escape from attributes" (Ch. 5), organizational survival and
population ecology (Ch. 6), and a structural-hole rereading of the theory of the
firm and of personality (Ch. 7).

## Reliability and Coverage Notes

The two empirical studies are Burt's own; their findings are **single-study**
(replication status recorded as unknown on the study page) and stated in
attributed voice. The market study uses population-level input–output data (all
markets in the economy), which makes routine statistical inference "awkward" —
Burt uses jackknife estimation. The manager study rests on one firm, a 52%
response rate on a stratified sample, and cross-sectional network data whose
causal order Burt himself flags as unsettled (Appendix B). The book engages, but
does not settle, its neighbours: [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s weak-ties
argument (which Burt reinterprets — the causal agent is the hole, not the
tie's weakness), [[coleman-james|Coleman]]'s closure account of social capital
(the opposite structural prescription — see [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]]),
Williamson's transaction-cost economics, and Hannan and Freeman's population
ecology. The last two, along with Lin's social-resources theory, are **coverage
gaps** in this wiki flagged by this ingest.

## Volume Note

Burt's book supplies the network-sociology strand of social-capital theory that
was previously a stub in this wiki: alongside [[social-capital|Bourdieu's]]
classed capital, [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman's]] closure-and-public-good
account, and [[social-capital-theory|Putnam's]] civic tradition, Burt provides
the **brokerage** strand — social capital as an ego-network *structural
position* rather than a group property. Its sharp disagreement with Coleman over
whether **closure or brokerage** produces returns is now filed as
[[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]], one of the defining debates of contemporary
network sociology.
