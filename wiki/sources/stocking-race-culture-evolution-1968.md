# Race, Culture, and Evolution: Essays in the History of Anthropology

George W. Stocking Jr.'s 1968 collection of eleven essays is a founding work of the
professional historiography of anthropology. It traces the interrelated ideas of race,
culture, and evolution from about 1800 — when race first became a matter of serious
scientific concern — through the later-nineteenth-century fusion of race and culture in
a single evolutionary hierarchy, into the first three decades of the twentieth century,
when [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] and his students developed the modern anthropological
approach to race and culture. It supplies the vault's standard account of how the plural,
relativist [[culture]] concept emerged and how the [[race]] concept was severed from it.

## Contents (essays)

1. On the Limits of "Presentism" and "Historicism" in the Historiography of the Behavioral Sciences
2. French Anthropology in 1800
3. The Persistence of Polygenist Thought in Post-Darwinian Anthropology
4. Matthew Arnold, E. B. Tylor, and the Uses of Invention
5. "Cultural Darwinism" and "Philosophical Idealism" in E. B. Tylor
6. The Dark-Skinned Savage: The Image of Primitive Man in Evolutionary Anthropology
7. From Physics to Ethnology
8. The Critique of Racial Formalism
9. Franz Boas and the Culture Concept in Historical Perspective
10. Lamarckianism in American Social Science, 1890–1915
11. The Scientific Reaction Against Cultural Anthropology, 1917–1920

## Key Claims and Contributions

All interpretive claims below are **attributed to Stocking**; the race-science doctrines
he documents are recorded as period positions.

- **Presentism vs. historicism** (essay 1). Stocking's methodological manifesto (originally
  a 1965 editorial in the *Journal of the History of the Behavioral Sciences*) distinguishes
  understanding "the past for the sake of the past" (historicism) from "the past for the sake
  of the present" (presentism), drawing on Butterfield's critique of whig history and
  Levenson's "thinking vs. thought." He argues the historiography of the behavioral sciences,
  being largely "pre-paradigmatic" (Kuhn), is especially prone to presentist vices, and
  defends an "enlightened presentism" that nonetheless requires a historicist method. See
  [[presentism-and-historicism|presentism]].
- **French anthropology in 1800** (essay 2). The Société des Observateurs de l'Homme (founded
  1799 by L. F. Jauffret, "the world's first anthropological society") and the Baudin
  expedition to Australia (1800–03) frame a contrast between two memoirs written to guide
  observation: Joseph-Marie de Gérando's culturally/environmentally
  focused, philanthropic *Considerations on the Methods to Follow in the Observation of Savage
  Peoples* (which uses "race" only once, of animals) and Georges Cuvier's race-focused,
  comparative-anatomical *Instructive Note* on obtaining and preserving the skulls of
  "savages." Stocking uses the pair to date the shift from Enlightenment "civilization" talk
  toward hereditarian "race" talk across the nineteenth century. See [[comparative-method]].
- **Persistence of polygenism** (essay 3). [[polygenism|Polygenist]] thinking (separate,
  fixed racial origins) did not die with Darwin's *Origin* (1859) but survived under new
  evolutionary vocabulary (Wallace, Broca, Topinard, Ripley, Closson), culminating in Madison
  Grant's *Passing of the Great Race* (1916).
- **Arnold, Tylor, and the "uses of invention"** (essay 4). Stocking's most-cited argument:
  [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1871 [[culture]] definition did not found the modern plural,
  relativist concept; Tylor used "culture" only in the singular, gradable, evaluative sense
  ("Culture or Civilization"), closer to [[arnold-matthew|Matthew Arnold]]'s humanist
  "culture" than to modern anthropology. Tylor "made evolutionary" the contemporary humanist
  idea rather than inventing the anthropological one.
- **"Cultural Darwinism" / "Philosophical Idealism"** (essay 5). Against Morris Opler,
  Stocking argues Tylor was neither a natural-selection theorist (his method rested on the
  "survival of the *unfit*" — the doctrine of [[survivals]]) nor a philosophical idealist, but
  an associationist/positivist/agnostic; the two labels reflect a 1960s disciplinary polemic
  read back onto Tylor.
- **The dark-skinned savage** (essay 6). Darwinian evolution, evolutionary ethnology, and
  polygenist race fused into a single "raciocultural hierarchy" in which the highest products
  of social evolution were "large-brained white men"; the [[comparative-method]] arrayed
  living peoples on one scale topped by western Europe. See [[social-evolutionism]].
- **From physics to ethnology** (essay 7). Boas's gradual (not "conversion"-like) move from
  a physics dissertation on light-absorption in water, through psychophysics and geography, to
  ethnology — the Baffinland year (1883–84) and "On Alternating Sounds" (1888/89) as the hinge.
- **The critique of racial formalism** (essay 8). Boas's empirical demolition of the "fixity
  of racial type" — the 1908–10 U.S. Immigration Commission head-form study (~18,000 subjects)
  showing the cephalic index changes with time since immigration — as part of the broader
  early-20th-century "revolt against formalism."
- **Boas and the culture concept** (essay 9). The book's crown essay: Boas did *not* arrive
  with the modern culture concept but developed it gradually (c. 1887–1911) out of his critique
  of the comparative method; the singular→plural inflection of "culture" is its datable marker.
  See [[culture]].
- **Lamarckianism in American social science** (essay 10). The doctrine of the inheritance of
  acquired characteristics let 1890–1915 social scientists blur "race" and "civilization";
  once Lamarckianism collapsed biologically (Weismann → Mendel → T. H. Morgan), "for 'race'
  read 'culture'" became possible. See [[lamarckian-inheritance]].
- **The scientific reaction against cultural anthropology** (essay 11). The 1917–1920
  hereditarian/eugenic pushback (the Galton Society, 1918; the NRC anthropology committee
  under Davenport and Grant; the 1919 AAA censure of Boas) — read by Stocking as an
  institutional episode through which Boasian anthropology nonetheless secured professional
  authority as "the voice of science" on race.

## Reflexivity

The volume is itself a reflexive document: Stocking repeatedly exposes his own method
(sampling and quasi-quantitative philology of word usage; the retrospective reconstruction
of an author's not-fully-conscious thought; institutional "microcosm" analysis of the 1919
AAA episode). He writes as a professional historian positioning himself as a partial
"outsider" to the "anthropological tribe," and flags the limits of his competence in
physical anthropology and statistics. His central historiographical warning — against
presentism — is applied to his own subjects (e.g., resisting the temptation to credit Tylor
or Boas with "inventing" the culture concept) and remains the standard caution in the
history of the human sciences.
