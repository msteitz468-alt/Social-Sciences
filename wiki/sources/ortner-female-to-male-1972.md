# Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?

Sherry B. Ortner, "Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?", *Feminist Studies* 1, no. 2 (Autumn 1972): 5–31; reprinted in Michelle Z. Rosaldo & Louise Lamphere, eds., *Woman, Culture, and Society* (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 1974): 67–87. Author: [[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]]. Dedicated to Simone de Beauvoir.

The founding theoretical statement of the [[feminist-anthropology|feminist anthropology]] that crystallized around the *Woman, Culture, and Society* volume: an attempt to explain **the universal secondary status of women** ([[female-subordination]]) not by biology and not by any local variable, but by a single pan-cultural symbolic logic — woman is everywhere seen as **closer to [[nature-culture-distinction|nature]]** and man as more fully identified with **culture**, and since culture's project is to transcend and subordinate nature, woman is devalued.

## Argument (attributed voice throughout)

Ortner distinguishes **three levels** of "the woman problem" and addresses only the first: (1) the *universal* cultural devaluation of women; (2) the *cross-cultural variation* in conceptions of women (deferred to later work); (3) the *on-the-ground* details of women's actual powers, "often at variance with cultural ideology." She argues level 3 cannot be understood without first grasping the overarching ideology at level 1.

- **The universal is a fact of cultural evaluation.** Ortner takes the "secondary status of woman in society" as "one of the true universals, a pan-cultural fact," evidenced by any of: explicit devaluing ideology, symbolic devices (e.g. [[ritual-pollution|defilement]]), or exclusion from the highest (sacred/political) powers. She rejects the search for a matriarchal or egalitarian counter-case and shifts the burden to those who would deny the universal, citing the matrilineal **Crow** (per Lowie): women held honorific ritual offices, yet the most sacred medicine bundle was tabooed to their touch and menstruation was a threat to warfare.
- **Not biology.** She refuses biological determinism: biological differences "only take on significance of superior/inferior within the framework of culturally defined value systems." The explanation must lie in *other universals of the human condition*.
- **Culture subordinates nature.** Every culture, through ritual and notions of [[ritual-pollution|purity/pollution]], asserts the human power to regulate and transcend natural givens; culture "asserts itself to be not only distinct from, but superior in power to, nature."
- **Woman as *intermediate*, not identical to nature.** Ortner's key revision (crediting critical readers) is that woman is seen not *as* nature but as **closer to nature** — an intermediate, mediating position between nature and culture, "lower on the scale of transcendence than men."
- **Three grounds for the "closer to nature" view.** (i) **Body**: following [[beauvoir-simone-de|de Beauvoir]] (*The Second Sex*), woman's physiology is "more enslaved to the species" (menstruation, pregnancy, lactation, childbirth), where man, "lacking natural creative functions," must create "artificially" through technology and symbols — hence killing (hunting, warfare) outranks giving birth. (ii) **Social role**: lactation and child care confine woman to the **domestic** sphere, which — via [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo's]] [[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic/public opposition]] and Lévi-Strauss's family/society (nature/culture) opposition — is subsumed by the higher, universalistic social order men monopolize. (iii) **Psyche**: drawing on [[chodorow-nancy|Chodorow]], a (non-innate) "feminine personality" of concreteness, personalism, and less-mediated relating, produced by women's near-universal responsibility for early child care, reads as "more like nature."
- **Intermediacy has three analytic payoffs.** Read as *middle status* it explains devaluation; read as *mediation* (converting nature→culture, e.g. socializing children, cooking) it explains the extra restrictions placed on women (culture must control its conversion mechanisms); read as *marginality/ambiguity* it explains why female symbolism swings between polar extremes — witches and menstrual pollution at one end, mother-goddesses and savioresses at the other ("the whore… can be redeemed to sainthood more easily than the faithful housewife").
- **Anti-determinist coda.** "The whole scheme is a construct of culture rather than a given of nature." Woman is "not 'in reality' any closer to… nature than man." The result is a "vicious circle" broken only by women's full participation "in projects of creativity and transcendence."

## Reliability and Reflexivity Note

The essay is a period document of the **Critical Turn**: second-wave feminist anthropology's opening move, written to give the emerging women's movement an analysis of a stubbornly universal fact. Its universalism (a single symbolic mechanism behind all female subordination) is exactly what its critics — [[nature-culture-gender-debate|MacCormack, Strathern, and others]] — rejected within the decade, arguing that "nature," "culture," and their opposition are Western folk categories illegitimately projected onto societies that do not organize thought that way. Ortner's own 1996 reassessment conceded much of this while defending a weaker, historicized version. Treat the "woman = closer to nature" thesis as a **famous, generative, and heavily contested position**, never as a wiki-voice finding.
