# Peddlers and Princes (Geertz, 1963)

Comparative economic ethnography of **[[javanese-modjokuto|Modjokuto]]** (east-central Java market town) and **[[balinese-tabanan|Tabanan]]** (southwest Bali former court town). [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] contrasts a **[[bazaar-type-economy|bazaar-type]]** path toward firm organization (Modjokuto *pasar* → *toko* / *perusahaan*, reformist Muslim peddler-entrepreneurs) with an **aristocratic firm-type** path (Tabanan *seka* / caste hierarchy → noble-sponsored enterprises). Frames both as pretake-off, transitional entrepreneurship — Janus-faced adaptation of customary means to novel ends — against [[modernization-theory|Rostowian take-off]] and national planning. Landmark of [[economic-anthropology]] and Indonesian development studies. Study analysis: [[geertz-peddlers-princes-study]]. Comparison page: [[modjokuto-tabanan-economic-modernization]].

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Peddlers and Princes* is Geertz’s major **urban commercial** contribution to the early-1960s Indonesian development literature, matching *Agricultural Involution*’s rural ecological diagnosis with a two-case ethnography of transitional entrepreneurship. The book’s enduring analytic product is the **bazaar-type / firm-type** organizational contrast and the paired entrepreneurial figures — **peddler** (bazaar-bred, reformist Muslim, *homo economicus*) and **prince** (court-bred, restorationist aristocrat, *homo politicus*) — both Janus-faced adapters of customary means to novel firm ends, both eventually needing to become professional managers. Methodologically it models what anthropology can add to development economics: microscopic analysis of social processes that pretake-off income series miss. Policy stance is neither Rostowian unilinearism nor anti-planning localism — Geertz argues for **pluralistic, growing-point** national planning sensitive to Indonesia’s compound traditions. Together with *Religion of Java* and *Agricultural Involution*, it completes the primary Modjokuto-era trilogy of religion, agrarian ecology, and town economy (with *Social History of an Indonesian Town* still outstanding for the full town chronicle).
