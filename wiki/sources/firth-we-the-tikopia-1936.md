# We, the Tikopia (Firth 1936) — Source

> Study analysis: [[firth-we-the-tikopia-study]]. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/firth-we-the-tikopia-study|Studies Hub — We, the Tikopia]]. Author: [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]]. Society: [[tikopia]].

## Bibliographic Record

Raymond Firth, *We, the Tikopia: A Sociological Study of Kinship in Primitive Polynesia*
(London: George Allen & Unwin, 1936; 2nd printing 1957, with a new introduction and a
preface by [[malinowski-bronislaw|Bronislaw Malinowski]]). An intensive ethnography of the
[[tikopia|Tikopia]] of the Solomon Islands, based on twelve months of continuous fieldwork,
July 1928–July 1929. The first and most celebrated of Firth's Tikopia monographs, followed by
*Primitive Polynesian Economy* (1939), *The Work and Wealth of a Primitive Community* (1939),
*The Work of the Gods in Tikopia* (1940), and *Social Change in Tikopia* (1959).

## Structure

Sixteen chapters plus an autobiographical Introduction. **I–II** In Primitive Polynesia /
Adjustment to Civilization (island, people, resources; contact history and missionization) ·
**III–IV** Village Life / Household and Family (local grouping; the *paito*; the elementary
family as kinship nucleus; sample-household census) · **V–VI** Personal Relations in the
Family Circle / The Kin of Father and Mother (parent–child, sibling, and the mother's-brother
*tuatina* / father's-sister *masikitaŋa* relationships; the *tama fakapiki* "adhering child")
· **VII–VIII** The Language of Kinship / Dirges for Dead Kin (classificatory terminology; the
critique of Morgan; mourning song as kinship evidence) · **IX–XI** Co-operation and Constraint
in Marriage Relationships / "House" and Clan / Principles of Land Tenure (affinal avoidance;
the *paito*/*kainaŋa* patrilineal descent structure; graded land tenure under chiefly
overlordship) · **XII–XIII** A Modern Population Problem / Firing the Ovens of Youth
(demography and population control; initiation as a maturity rite) · **XIV–XVI** Sociology of
Sex / Marriage by Capture / Kinship and Social Stability (procreation belief and contraception;
abduction marriage; the functionalist synthesis and comparative-Polynesian survey).

## Claims Extracted

Extracted across all 16 chapters (attributed to Firth / his 1928–29 fieldwork throughout):
**method** (a year of vernacular participant observation; trade-goods payment; naturalistic
recording of overheard talk; the genealogical record of ~20 major houses; explicit critique of
[[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]'s single-day Tikopia account as unreliable); **the family and kinship
extension** (the elementary family as the structural nucleus; kinship terminology, sentiment,
and obligation extending outward from it by graded genealogical propinquity — Firth's central
mechanism, set against Morgan's evolutionary reading of the
[[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory system]]); **descent structure** (the
*paito*/"house" as a patrilineal [[ramage]]; the four *kainaŋa*/clans — Kafika, Tafua, Taumako,
Faŋarere — each under an *ariki*; ~30 major houses; strictly patrilineal succession with
important matrilateral ties supplementing it); **the mother's brother and father's sister**
(the *tuatina*→*tama tapu* "diagonal" gift flow; the *masikitaŋa* as sacred quasi-paternal
figure — feeding [[avunculate]]); **land tenure** (graded primary/secondary rights; chiefly
overlordship via *tapu*; women's non-transmissible usufruct); **sex, procreation and population**
(recognition of physiological paternity, contra Malinowski's Trobriands; deliberate population
control by *coitus interruptus* and the ritual *fono*; the 1929 census, pop. 1,281); **marriage**
(abduction/*tuku pouri* marriage and its atonement-exchange sequence; polygyny; Firth's own naming
of coerced consummation as "marital rape"); and the concluding **functionalist synthesis** (kinship
as "a system of primary integration" and "a factor of stability" — a qualified, friction-acknowledging
functionalism, distanced from Malinowski's needs-functionalism). The comparative-Polynesian survey in
Ch. XVI (Samoa, Tonga, Maori, etc.) is a Galton's-problem case, routed to comparison material rather
than the society page.
