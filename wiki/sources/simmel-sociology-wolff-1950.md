# The Sociology of Georg Simmel (Wolff, ed., 1950)

Kurt H. Wolff's *The Sociology of Georg Simmel* (Glencoe, Ill.: The Free Press, 1950) — the volume that carried Simmel's mature sociology into English and anchored his American reception. It translates the bulk of Simmel's *Soziologie* (*Untersuchungen über die Formen der Vergesellschaftung*, 1908) together with the essays "Sociability" (1910) and "The Metropolis and Mental Life" (1903), behind a long editorial Introduction in which Wolff reconstructs Simmel's biography, his standing in America, and the internal tensions of his "field of sociology." Ingested as the second major Simmel source in the wiki (after *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]*); it supplies the concrete *forms of sociation* that the theory of [[formal-sociology|formal sociology]] had earlier been able only to name.

## What the book is

Not a monograph but a **reader**: Wolff's selection and translation of Simmel's sociological essays, arranged into five Parts, with the editor's Introduction supplying context and a critical map. Its unifying claim is Simmel's — that sociology's object is the recurrent **forms** of association ([[sociation]]) abstracted from their shifting contents — and its contribution to the wiki is to fill that program with worked cases: the dyad and triad, superordination and subordination, secrecy and the secret society, faithfulness and gratitude, the stranger, the metropolis, sociability. Where *The Philosophy of Money* argued the relational ontology, this volume exhibits Simmel's **social geometry** in operation.

## Reliability and voice

Every substantive claim is Simmel's theoretical position, in **attributed voice**; Wolff's framing claims are attributed to Wolff. Three standing cautions travel with the material:

1. **Geometry, not data.** Simmel's historical and cross-cultural vignettes (Roman consuls, Venetian councils, Quaker meetings, "primitive" secret societies) are illustrations chosen to exhibit a *form*, never systematic evidence, and are never promoted to wiki-voice ethnographic or historical fact.
2. **Dated evolutionism.** Period framings ("primitive peoples," savagery-to-civilization gradients) are recorded as attributed and dated.
3. **OCR fidelity.** The 1950 print's scan corrupts diacritics and occasionally intrudes page numbers into sentences; quotations were cleaned against sense.

## Volume Synthesis Note

If *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* argued Simmel's relational ontology — society as *Wechselwirkung*, value and money as reified relation — this volume is where that ontology is **cashed out as a catalogue of forms**. Its unifying achievement is to show [[formal-sociology]] doing actual work: the same analytic move (abstract the form, bracket the content) applied across a dozen essays yields the dyad's fragility, the triad's *tertius gaudens*, domination as reciprocal interaction, secrecy as a "second world," the stranger's nearness-and-remoteness, the metropolitan blasé psyche. The volume thereby converts what the wiki previously held as a *named but thinly-evidenced program* into a set of first-class analytical concepts.

Against the wiki's other classical founders the contrast is now fully drawn. Where [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] builds sociology on the [[social-fact]] and moral statistics and Weber on comparative-historical apparatus and ideal types, Simmel builds it on the *form displayed by illustration* — a method whose brilliance and whose vulnerability are the same fact, and which the [[simmel-scientific-status-debate|Sorokin–Park dispute]] made explicit. The volume also supplies, through Wolff's editorial reconstruction, the documented record of Simmel's marginalisation (the blocked chair, the antisemitism he faced) that the [[hubs/thinkers/simmel-georg|Thinkers-Hub analysis]] treats at length: the theorist of the stranger was himself the academy's stranger. Its chief tension with existing content is the one it shares with *The Philosophy of Money* — it is avowedly essayistic rather than data-driven, and its historical vignettes (Inca administration, the Frankfurt Jewish head-tax, Roman *lex*) are attributed illustration, never wiki-voice fact.
