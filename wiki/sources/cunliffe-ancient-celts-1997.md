# The Ancient Celts (Cunliffe, 1997)

## Bibliographic Record

Barry Cunliffe, *The Ancient Celts* (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1997). Preface January 1996. ~360 illustrated pages. Author: [[cunliffe-barry]].

A single-author archaeological synthesis of “the Celts” from the late Bronze Age through Roman conquest and into medieval vernacular survivals. Cunliffe intertwines three evidence streams that the wiki must keep **analytically separate**: (1) Greco-Roman ethnonyms and ethnography; (2) the Celtic branch of Indo-European languages; (3) Hallstatt and La Tène material-culture packages and their regional variants. Core methodological stance (attributed): classical sources are partial and manipulative but too rich to discard; archaeology without them is defeatist if sources are read critically. Rejects both New Celtomania and total terminological nihilism (R1-046, R1-087).

`source_type: mixed` — theoretical synthesis + secondary presentation of primary classical and archaeological evidence; not itself a landmark single excavation or ethnography, so no `studies/` page.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*The Ancient Celts* is a late-twentieth-century single-author synthesis that simultaneously (a) narrates Iron Age temperate Europe in contact with the Mediterranean and (b) historicizes the idea of the Celt from classical ethnography through Celtomania, culture-historical equation of La Tène with historic Celts, and contemporary pan-European and sceptical uses. Its enduring methodological contribution for this wiki is the **partial-overlap model**: classical ethnonyms, Celtic languages, and Hallstatt–La Tène (plus Celtiberian) material packages are real threads that **do not map one-to-one**. Iberia and the Insular ethnonym gap are decisive illustrations. Migrations of the fourth–third centuries BCE are accepted as historical; invasionism as a universal explanation for language and culture change is not.

Structurally the book moves from vision and “reality” (Ch. 1–2), through deep prehistoric Mediterranean–barbarian systems and Hallstatt aristocracies (Ch. 3), migration-period expansion (Ch. 4), social forms and art (Ch. 5–6), regional Iberian/Atlantic/eastern chapters (Ch. 7–9), religion (Ch. 10), late oppida and Roman economic catalysis (Ch. 11), conquest and survival (Ch. 12–13), to a four-phase retrospect (Ch. 14). Economic and world-systems language (prestige goods, periphery, “Trade preceded the flag”) sits alongside traditional culture-history periodization — a hybrid typical of senior British Iron Age synthesis in the 1990s.

**Limits for the wiki:** pre-aDNA (1997); secondary on linguistics; OCR-noisy scan for extraction; not a substitute for regional monographs or Chapman-style construct critiques (which Cunliffe cites rather than fully answers). **Value:** first major Celtic package in the collection; scaffolds [[celticity-debate]] as a standing position record; enforces four-way non-identity on all Celtic pages; feeds Hallstatt/La Tène/Celtiberian, oppida, druids, Celtic art, and migration phenomenon pages.

**Relation to already-ingested IE/steppe material:** Cunliffe assumes a west-central European crystallization of Celtic within IE and is not a homeland monograph; do not collapse his Atlantic lingua-franca hypothesis into [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] wiki-voice outcomes without further sources.
