# Social Systems (Luhmann, 1984/1995)

Niklas Luhmann's *Social Systems* (*Soziale Systeme*, 1984; English 1995) is the
programmatic statement of his general theory of self-referential, autopoietic social
systems — the "supertheory" he intended as a comprehensive alternative to both
[[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s action-theoretic [[structural-functionalism]] and
[[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s theory of communicative action. Its founding move is to
locate social systems not in acting human subjects but in **[[communication]]**: society
consists of communications, not of people, who belong to its environment. The book works
out the consequences across twelve chapters, from the system/environment distinction and
[[meaning]] through [[double-contingency]], [[interpenetration]], [[psychic-system|psychic
systems]], structure and time, conflict, and societal differentiation, to a
radical-constructivist epistemology.

## Reliability and Reading Note

*Social Systems* is a theoretical monograph of extreme abstraction: it programmatically
refuses to ground sociology in the human subject, intention, or action, and offers no
empirical study. All claims drawn from it are Luhmann's — attributed throughout. Its
illustrations (love, law, money, organizations) are gestures toward his later
domain-specific studies, not evidence, and must not be filed as subject-matter findings.
The book's difficulty is partly deliberate (Luhmann's "theoretical abstinence" from
easy readability); Knodt's foreword and Luhmann's own prefaces are the best internal
guides to its ambitions and its quarrel with the humanist tradition.
