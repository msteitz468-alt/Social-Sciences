# Cultural Landscapes in the Ancient Andes (Moore 2005)

Jerry D. Moore's comparative-theoretical monograph (University Press of Florida, 2005) arguing for an archaeology of **place** — as distinct from abstract space — in the prehispanic Andes. Moore combines cross-cultural ethnographic analysis with archaeological cases spanning the Archaic to the Late Horizon, organized around four experiential themes: soundscapes, ceremonial architecture and religious authority, processions, and architecture as social memory.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Moore's central move is methodological: he accepts the post-processual insistence that landscapes are culturally constituted experience, but rejects the purely intuitive phenomenology of [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]]-style landscape archaeology as unverifiable when applied to prehistory without ethnographic controls. His alternative is a comparative, middle-range program: identify cross-culturally documented relationships between social forms and spatial/experiential variables (speech intelligibility distances, sightlines, ritual movement patterns, architectural permanence), then apply them to Andean archaeological cases as constrained analogy rather than empathetic projection (Moore 2005). The book is thereby simultaneously an empirical synthesis of Andean ceremonial architecture and an argued position in the debate over [[phenomenological-archaeology]].

Its evidence classes are ethnographic (published ethnography, re-analyzed), material (published excavation data), and textual/archival (colonial ethnohistory, e.g. Guaman Poma, the Naymlap legend); Moore is explicit that each analogy is a hypothesis-generator. Tension with already-ingested sources: where [[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994]] treats bodily experience as directly recoverable, Moore argues experience is recoverable only through cross-cultural regularities — recorded as positions on [[phenomenological-archaeology]]'s critiques section rather than adjudicated.
