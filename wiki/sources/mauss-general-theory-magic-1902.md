# A General Theory of Magic (Hubert & Mauss, 1902–03)

Classic Durkheimian essay arguing (attributed voice throughout — this is a theoretical program) that magic is a **total social phenomenon**: not a mistaken individual science (against [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] and [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s intellectualism) but a social institution whose efficacy rests on collective belief. Its centerpiece is the elevation of the Melanesian notion of **[[mana]]** into a general analytic category — the impersonal force presupposed by all magical rites, prior to Frazer's laws of sympathy.

## Structure

1. Sources and historical background — the comparative corpus and prior theories
2. A definition of magic — magic vs. religion by rite: private, secret, tending toward the prohibited vs. public, obligatory cult
3. The elements of magic — the magician; the actions (rites); the representations (laws of sympathy, impersonal and personal representations, demonology)
4. An analysis and explanation of magic — belief; critique of ideological explanations of ritual efficacy; mana; collective states and collective forces
5. Conclusion — magic as a social phenomenon, kin to sacrifice and akin to the origins of science and technique
