# Edmund Leach: A Bibliography (RAI Occasional Paper 42, 1990)

A **near-complete chronological bibliography** of Sir [[leach-edmund|Edmund Leach]]’s writings (1935–1988), with posthumous and then-forthcoming items, published by the Royal Anthropological Institute as Occasional Paper no. 42 (1990). Prefaced by Eric Sunderland (RAI President); introduced by Stephen Gudeman and Jean La Fontaine; compiled from Leach’s own list and completed by Heather Weston after Leach’s death.

## Framing

### Acknowledgments (Jonathan Benthall, Director RAI)

- Base list: Leach with Martha Macintyre and Judy Stirrat; RAI Council asked (1982) to publish after his death — permission granted in principle.
- After death (6 January 1989): Weston commissioned to check and update through latest and posthumous/in-press works; Louisa Brown assisted.
- **Not complete:** few foreign-language translations; **excluded:** reviews *of* Leach’s books and commentary *on* him by others.

### Preface (Eric Sunderland)

Sunderland records Leach’s service as Council member, Vice-President, and President of the RAI; endowment and involvement of the **Esperanza Trust for Anthropological Research** (premises purchase, *Anthropology Today*, other RAI projects); broad support for all aspects of anthropology (including biosocial links); and the remark, after his second presidential term gift, that “The Institute is very close to my heart.”

### Introduction (Stephen Gudeman & Jean La Fontaine) — attributed

Gudeman and La Fontaine treat the bibliography as revealing the man, colleague, teacher, and **polemical opponent**: wide interests with persistent cores — **kinship, ritual and myth, art, economics and technology**; controversy as a professional engine that forced examination of uncritical assumptions.

**Three rough publication categories:** books; essays/articles; shorter pieces (reviews, correspondence, comments).

**Two major ethnographies, contrasting theories (their framing):**

| Work | Year | Theoretical stance (Leach’s own labels, per G&LF) |
|---|---|---|
| *Political Systems of Highland Burma* ([[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954]]) | 1954 | “Idealist”; Kachin Hills; “probably his most important intellectual contribution” |
| *Pul Eliya: a village in Ceylon* | 1961 | “Materialist” reading of dense kinship/caste/land-tenure data |

They report Leach professed puzzlement that colleagues charged inconsistency; cite Parry & Fuller (*Anthropology Today* 1989) on deeper resemblances and the bridging role of **verbal categories** as material expressions of linguistic phenomena developed already in the earlier work.

**Essay classics:** 1951 Curl Prize piece on structural implications of matrilateral cross-cousin marriage as landmark; admiration for [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] with a more **empiricist structuralism** of Leach’s own; 1970 *Lévi-Strauss* “pure Leach” as introduction. Essay form as home: devastating critique + fresh approach + ethnographic sketch.

**Ethnography vs theory:** declared boredom with ethnography yet heavy reviewing (esp. Sri Lanka → Asia); insisted fieldwork is foundation of social anthropology while distinguishing description that “does something” theoretically from mere description. Comparison and cross-cultural generalization at abstract/structuralist level; rejection of universal definitions (e.g. marriage, caste reserved for Hindu structural counterpart). Trobriand reanalyses via Malinowski corpus; African descent via alliance model in *Rethinking Anthropology* title essay (G&LF note less secure grasp of African issues).

**Later career themes:** epistemology (Malinowski empiricism; language; Vico); founding fathers (Malinowski vs Radcliffe-Brown vs Lévi-Strauss; lasting fascination with Bateson); public anthropology (1967 Reith Lectures *A Runaway World?*; war, violence, law, evolution, apartheid, family, education); biblical structuralism (*Genesis as Myth*; *Structuralist Interpretations of Biblical Myth* with D. A. Aycock) as ethnographic-style provocation of religious authorities; teaching texts (*Social Anthropology* for sixth-form/first-year).

## Publication-pattern findings (wiki voice for brute inventory facts)

As listed in this 1990 apparatus (not a statistical reanalysis):

- **Span:** first listed item **1935** (*Times* correspondence on China/Szechwan reforms); coverage through **1988** living publications; **1989** posthumous and “to be published” sections.
- **Genre mix:** monographs and edited volumes; journal articles; very large **review** output (hundreds of “Review of…” lines in the OCR text); frequent **correspondence** in *Man*, *TLS*, *Listener*, newspapers.
- **Major books appearing in the list (selected):** *Political Systems of Highland Burma* (1954); *Pul Eliya* (1961); *Rethinking Anthropology* (1961); *Genesis as Myth and Other Essays*; *A Runaway World?* (Reith Lectures 1967); *Lévi-Strauss* (1970); *Culture and Communication* (1976); *Social Anthropology* (1982); *Structuralist Interpretations of Biblical Myth* (with Aycock, 1983).
- **Edited series/volumes:** e.g. *Aspects of Caste…* (Cambridge Papers); *Dialectic in Practical Religion*.
- **Institutional sites of publication:** LSE Monographs, Cambridge Papers/Studies, *JRAI*/*Man*, *American Anthropologist*, *LRB*, *TLS*, *Anthropology Today*, etc.

## Volume Synthesis Note

This ingest closes a **historiographic gap** on an already well-ingested thinker: the wiki held deep analyses of *Highland Burma* and *Rethinking Anthropology* but only a thin publication spine. The RAI bibliography supplies (1) year-by-year scope of the corpus; (2) Gudeman–La Fontaine’s attributed framing of the 1954/1961 “idealist/materialist” contrast and essay-form intellectual style; (3) RAI/Esperanza Trust institutional context; (4) a queue map for remaining monographs (*Pul Eliya*, *Culture and Communication*, Reith lectures, biblical structuralism, *Social Anthropology*). Translations and reception literature remain outside this source by design. Tambiah’s biography remains the outstanding narrative life ([Outstanding sources] hub note).
