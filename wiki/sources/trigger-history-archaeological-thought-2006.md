# A History of Archaeological Thought (2nd Edition)

Bruce G. Trigger's *A History of Archaeological Thought* (Cambridge, 2nd ed. 2006) is the principal single-author intellectual history of archaeology from medieval and Renaissance antiquarianism through processual and postprocessual debates into early-2000s pragmatic synthesis. First published 1989; this edition rewrites the book for fifteen years of theoretical change and broader geographic/topical coverage while keeping length comparable by condensation.

Trigger frames the discipline's development as successive but interacting trends in archaeological thought, relating (in the West) personal and collective interests of archaeologists to middle-class fortunes, yet arguing that the gradual accumulation of archaeological data has exercised a growing constraint on interpretation — increasing objectivity and the field's value for understanding the human condition.

All historiographic and theoretical claims drawn from this book are **Trigger's** (attributed voice). Primary programs already ingested in this vault (Childe, Binford, Hodder, Willey & Phillips, Clark, Taylor, Lubbock, etc.) keep their own voices; *HAT* supplies the Trigger counter-reading, the long-run periodization, and the comparative worldwide frame.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*A History of Archaeological Thought* is the **standard intellectual history** of the discipline and the naming charter for several of this wiki's long-run periodization labels (antiquarianism → evolutionary → culture-historical → early functional-processual → processual/postprocessual → pragmatic synthesis). Its architecture is deliberate: affirm that social context shapes questions and preferred answers **and** that accumulating data constrain interpretation toward greater objectivity; survey classical, colonial, Soviet, East Asian, and Americanist streams within one comparative frame; refuse both 1970s processual anti-history and hyper-relativist "anything goes." Against this vault's denser primary holdings on Childe, Binford, Hodder, Willey & Phillips, Clark, Lubbock, and others, *HAT* must never overwrite those programs' own voices; it supplies the Trigger counter-reading, the worldwide scaffold, the Kossinna/Montelius/Thomsen founder network, and the epistemology package ([[data-constraint-on-interpretation]], middle-ranging vs middle-range, high-level theory). Outstanding for densification: Trigger *Understanding Early Civilizations* (2003); *Gordon Childe: Revolutions in Archaeology* (1980); primary Montelius/Kossinna/Thomsen texts.
