# In Small Things Forgotten (Deetz, 1996)

[[deetz-james|James Deetz]]'s *In Small Things Forgotten* (first ed. 1977; expanded and revised Anchor Books ed. 1996) is a landmark synthesis of **[[historical-archaeology]]** as the study of ordinary material culture — the "small things forgotten" of probate inventories — in early Anglo-America and African America. The 1996 edition retains the New England core (ceramics, gravestones, vernacular housing, [[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]) and adds sustained Chesapeake and **African American archaeology** material (shotgun houses, slave quarters, [[colono-ware]], clay pipes, [[creolization]]). Intellectual center of gravity: Henry [[glassie-henry|Glassie]] and the **[[georgian-worldview|Georgian worldview]]** as a cognitive/structural shift readable across artifact classes.

## Core Argument (attributed)

Deetz argued that early American culture is best recovered not from elite texts alone but from the material world of ordinary people — ceramics, houses, graves, refuse — read as patterned expressions of **worldview**. He periodized Anglo-American culture into three phases culminating in a **Georgian** mentalité (order, symmetry, segmentation, individuation) that reorganized multiple artifact domains after ~1760. He held that African American material culture is not a failed copy of Anglo forms but evidence of **creolization** and African cultural survival under slavery and freedom; [[parting-ways|Parting Ways]] shows that Anglo-derived interpretive rules break outside that tradition.

## Volume Synthesis Note

ISTF is the vault's primary popular charter for North American **[[historical-archaeology]]** as ordinary **[[material-culture]]** study. Relative to [[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996|Orser 1996]], Deetz is less a global-theory manifesto and more a multi-domain empirical pedagogy: New England seriations and houses prove a cognitive periodization; Parting Ways and the African American chapters refuse to let that periodization swallow non-Anglo lives. The Glassie debt makes the book a bridge between folklore/vernacular architecture and archaeology; the dating toolkit (South MCD, pipestems, TPQ) bridges New Archaeology quantification and interpretive reading. Limits: 1996 evidence base; New England gravity even after Chesapeake expansion; ethnicity and creolization claims that later specialists refine; *Flowerdew Hundred* (1993) still outstanding as site monograph.

## Reliability and Reflexivity Notes

Pedagogical synthesis with heavy secondary citation (Kelso, Ferguson, Vlach, Sobel, Carson, Noël Hume, Otto, Emerson). Case archaeology quality varies by sub-project (Parting Ways under Deetz; Kingsmill under Kelso; SC under Ferguson). OCR of the z-library PDF is continuous but hyphen-noisy; pipestem fraction tables partly illegible — claims use narrative dates, not garbled fractions. Separating African American chapters is an authorial presentational choice Deetz defends; integrated treatments exist elsewhere in the field.
