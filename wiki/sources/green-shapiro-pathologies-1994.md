# Pathologies of Rational Choice Theory (Green & Shapiro 1994)

A book-length appraisal of the empirical track record of **rational choice theory**
in political science, by [[green-donald|Donald P. Green]] and [[shapiro-ian|Ian
Shapiro]] (Yale University Press, 1994). The authors accept rational choice theorists'
aspiration to a scientific study of politics and do not object to the rationality
postulate itself; they ask instead why, after decades of theoretical sophistication,
successful empirical applications remain "few and far between." Their answer is that
the shortcomings are **method-driven rather than theory-driven** — a syndrome of
recurring [[method-driven-research|methodological pathologies]] that insulate the
theory from empirical refutation.

The book examines the literatures where rational choice is reputed to have succeeded:
the [[paradox-of-voter-turnout|paradox of voter turnout]], collective action and
[[free-rider-problem|free-riding]], legislative behavior and the
[[social-choice|paradox of voting]] (cycling), and [[spatial-model-of-voting|spatial
theories of electoral competition]]. In each it argues the empirical support is far
weaker than practitioners claim. A final chapter answers likely counterarguments.

The book launched the [[rational-choice-controversy-debate|rational choice
controversy]] — a sustained methodological dispute across political science, gathered
in the 1995 *Critical Review* symposium.

## Claims Extracted

All claims are **attributed** to Green and Shapiro (a single-source methodological
critique of a still-active research program; nothing here is promoted to wiki voice).

- **The disjunction.** Rational choice scholarship in political science grew from a
  1950s subfield to disciplinary prominence, but "the stature of rational choice
  scholarship does not rest on a readily identifiable set of empirical successes";
  theoretical sophistication has far outrun tested empirical achievement.
- **The pathologies.** The failures are method-driven, not theory-driven — a syndrome
  of recurring [[method-driven-research|methodological pathologies]]: post hoc theory
  development (retroduction), slippery predictions, vaguely operationalized predictions,
  searching for confirming evidence, projecting evidence from theory, and arbitrary
  domain restriction. Root cause: the universalist aspiration to a general theory of
  politics.
- **Voter turnout** (ch. 4): the [[paradox-of-voter-turnout|paradox of turnout]] and the
  serial rational-choice rescues (civic-duty D-term as tautology; collapsed
  game-theoretic models; Aldrich's domain restriction) illustrate the pathologies.
- **Collective action** (ch. 5): Olson's [[free-rider-problem|free-rider]] prediction is
  thinly supported; observed cooperation outruns the models, which survive only via
  "revisionist embellishments" that drain them of testable content.
- **Legislative behavior** (ch. 6): the [[social-choice|cycling/chaos]] results and the
  "structure-induced equilibrium" response; Riker's historical cycling cases contain
  factual errors, and the whole literature substitutes stylized models for tested
  causal claims. Headline null result: coreless experimental games are not markedly more
  unstable than games with cores.
- **Spatial competition** (ch. 7): [[spatial-model-of-voting|spatial models]] are
  "almost devoid of empirical content"; convergence and divergence "stylized facts" are
  each rescued post hoc.
- **Responses** (ch. 8): ten anticipated counterarguments answered; the constructive
  program is **problem-driven** rather than method-driven research, relinquishing pure
  universalism and stating falsification conditions. Closing reframing: shift from
  "whether or not rational choice theory?" to "how does rationality interact with other
  facets of human nature and organization to produce the politics we seek to
  understand?"
