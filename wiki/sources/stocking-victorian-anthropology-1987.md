# Victorian Anthropology (Stocking, 1987)

George W. Stocking, Jr.'s intellectual history of British anthropology from the Enlightenment idea of civilization to the institutionalization of evolutionary anthropology in the 1880s. Its central problem is how the mid-Victorian **[[social-evolutionism|sociocultural evolutionism]]** of [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]], [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], and [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] emerged around 1860 out of, and against, the earlier **[[ethnology|ethnological]]** tradition of [[prichard-james-cowles|James Cowles Prichard]] — and how that emergence was conditioned by the collapse of the Biblical chronology (the "[[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]]"), the [[darwin-charles|Darwinian]] revolution, the Victorian idea of [[civilization]], and the racial and colonial ideologies of the period. Written as an avowedly **[[presentism-and-historicism|historicist]]** history (understanding Victorian anthropology in its own terms rather than as a flawed anticipation of the present), it is the standard account of the discipline's founding era and a landmark of the historiography of the human sciences.

## Principal Theses (attributed to Stocking)

1. **The Prichardian transition.** Victorian sociocultural evolutionism (c. 1860) emerged out of and against the earlier [[ethnology|ethnological]] tradition of [[prichard-james-cowles|James Cowles Prichard]] — a shift from the governing metaphor of the racial **tree** (movement in space, human unity) to the evolutionary **ladder** (development in time, origin of civilization).
2. **Against "social Darwinism."** The 1860s efflorescence was "neither a transfer of biological evolutionism to the social sphere… nor simply the reemergence of a [Scottish/Enlightenment] tradition," but a synthesis assembled to answer new questions posed by the [[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]] and the Darwinian debate (the "Darwinian" vs "Darwinistic" distinction). See [[social-darwinism]], [[social-evolutionism]].
3. **The [[comparative-method]].** Classical evolutionism traced *up* (present "savages" as living representatives of past stages), not *back* through documented connection — lacking the controls of comparative philology or anatomy; it privileged independent invention over diffusion to keep divine intervention out of the developmental story ([[survivals]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]).
4. **The culture concept.** [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1871 definition equated [[culture]] with [[civilization]] and was singular and hierarchical — *not* the modern plural, relativist concept, which came later from Boasians closer to the German tradition ([[boas-franz]]).
5. **The image of savagery.** Victorian writing on religion ([[animism]]) and marriage ([[primitive-promiscuity]]) carried a "double image of savagery" (philosopher-savage / bestial-savage) reflecting middle-class experience; "savagery" was equated with domestic subordinate categories (women, the poor, the Irish) — similitude for them, asserted identity for "true savages" ([[savagery-barbarism-civilization]], [[race]]).
6. **The institutional compromise.** The 1860s [[victorian-ethnology-anthropology-schism-debate|ethnological–anthropological schism]] ([[hunt-james|Hunt]]'s polygenist society vs the monogenist Ethnological Society) resolved in the 1871 Anthropological Institute — a synthesis absorbing ethnology and physical anthropology while rejecting both active humanitarianism and overt political racism.
7. **[[presentism-and-historicism|Historicism vs presentism]].** The book is an avowedly historicist history; its epilogue reads classical evolutionism's "ambiguous heritage" — advancing the naturalization of humankind while regrounding hierarchies of [[race]] and [[culture]] on a cosmic time-scale.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Victorian Anthropology* is the vault's charter source for the **founding era** of British anthropology, and it reorganizes material the wiki previously held only in fragments (Tylor, Morgan, Maine, Spencer, Frazer). Its distinctive contribution is to make the *pre-history* of evolutionism legible: by recovering [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]]'s [[ethnology]] and the [[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]], Stocking shows that the discipline did not begin with Darwin, and that its own origin-myth ("anthropology began with Darwin") is a presentist distortion. This directly complements two sources already ingested. It is the upstream companion to [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988|Kuper's *Invention of Primitive Society*]] (1988), which extends Stocking's critical treatment of the [[primitive-society]] construct and kinship theory; and it is the sober historicist counterpart to [[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|Harris's partisan *Rise of Anthropological Theory*]] (1968) — where Harris grinds a [[cultural-materialism|cultural-materialist]] axe, Stocking reconstructs the Victorians in their own terms.

Three tensions with existing content are worth flagging. First, Stocking's insistence that Tylor's culture concept is *not* the modern one qualifies the discipline page's framing of Tylor as the man who "constituted culture as a single lawful phenomenon" — true in wiki voice as a claim about the 1871 definition's form, but Stocking shows the plural/relativist content arrived only with [[boas-franz|Boas]]. Second, Stocking's demonstration that functionalism's founders ([[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]], [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]) remained indebted to evolutionary and Spencerian assumptions complicates the tidy "overthrow" narrative in [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]. Third, the book supplies the missing nineteenth-century prehistory of the [[race-and-culture-debate]]: the Boasian critique of race science is a reaction against precisely the evolutionary racialism Stocking anatomizes.

**Reliability.** A work of historiography, not primary ethnography: every substantive claim about the Victorians is attributed, and Stocking's own interpretive theses are flagged as his. Empirical illustrations inside the book (Grey on Australia, Williams on Fiji, Galton on the Damara, Wallace on the Uaupés) are treated here as *data-sources Stocking analyzes*, kept in prose rather than promoted to society pages, since the book uses them to illuminate the observers' racial ideology rather than to document the peoples in their own right.
