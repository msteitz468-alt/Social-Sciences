# The Philadelphia Negro: A Social Study (1899)

W. E. B. [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]]'s *The Philadelphia Negro* (University of
Pennsylvania Press / Ginn, 1899) is widely regarded as the first sociological
case study of a Black community in the United States and one of the earliest
examples of American sociology conducted as a statistically grounded empirical
social science. It is the founding work of Du Bois's urban sociology and the
principal subject of the analytic [[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|study page]].

## Ingestion Note

This is a **web ingest**: the wiki's record of the work was built from secondary
and reference sources online (see `reliability_notes`), not from a reading of the
full 1899 text. Claims below are stated in **attributed voice** and flagged where
the underlying figures could not be verified against the primary text. The book
itself was commissioned by the University of Pennsylvania together with
Philadelphia's College Settlement in spring 1896; Susan P. Wharton is credited in
the secondary literature with suggesting that Du Bois be invited to conduct it,
and Du Bois held the deliberately modest title of "Assistant Instructor."

## What the Work Is

A single-community empirical study of the roughly 9,700 African Americans then
living in Philadelphia's Seventh Ward (Spruce to South Street, Seventh Street to
the Schuylkill), embedded within a broader account of the ~40,000 Black
Philadelphians. Du Bois combined a **door-to-door canvass** (yielding, per the
secondary sources, more than 5,000 personal interviews), household schedules on
occupation, health, education, family, and religion, **descriptive statistics**,
**cartographic mapping** of residences, churches, and businesses, and archival
and historical research. Isabel Eaton contributed a special report on Black
domestic service.
