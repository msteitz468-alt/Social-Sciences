# Fear of Small Numbers (2006) — partial ingest

Arjun Appadurai's essay on globalization and large-scale violence against minorities, the sequel to *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]* (1996). **Only the title chapter (ch. 3, pp. 49–85) is on disk and ingested** — see `reliability_notes`.

## The chapter's argument (attributed voice throughout)

Appadurai poses a puzzle: why do minorities — small, politically and militarily weak — become objects of fear and genocidal rage, especially since the late 1980s? His answer, in outline:

- **Majority and minority are recent, co-constitutive inventions.** Both categories are products of the modern nation-state's techniques of counting, classification, and political representation — "majorities are as much the product of enumeration and political nomination as are minorities," and majorities need minorities to exist "even more than the reverse."
- **[[predatory-identity|Predatory identities]]** are identities whose mobilization requires the extinction of proximate social categories. They are almost always majoritarian, mobilized through claims about a threatened cultural majority, and often deploy pseudo-demographic arguments about minority birthrates. They emerge where majorities and minorities can plausibly be seen as in danger of trading places.
- **The [[anxiety-of-incompleteness]]** explains why *small* numbers excite rage: a tiny minority is the obstacle between a numerical majority and the fantasy of a whole, pure national ethnos. "The smaller the number and the weaker the minority, the deeper the rage." Appadurai extends [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] on purity here, and reworks Freud's [[narcissism-of-minor-differences]] (via Ignatieff 1998) from psychodynamics into public ideologies of group identity.
- **Nazi Germany** is treated as the paradigm case of a majoritarian identity turned predatory — but Appadurai argues all majoritarianisms, including liberal ones (Hindutva in India, "sons of the soil" in Malaysia, European citizenship ideologies), "have in them the seeds of genocide," because all are tied to ideas of the singularity and completeness of the national ethnos.
- **Number in the liberal imagination.** Liberal social theory privileges the number one (the individual) and fears large numbers (the masses); small numbers are suspect as elites, conspiracies, and "special interests" — except minorities, valued because they echo the individual. The liberal regard for minorities originated as *procedural* (dissenting opinion in deliberation), and was transferred — poorly theorized, largely after the birth of the UN and the human-rights regime — onto *substantive* (permanent social and cultural) minorities. This displacement, Appadurai argues, is a key source of the modern ambivalence toward minorities.
- **The Indian case.** The rise of Hindutva (c. 1985–2004) illustrates the mechanics: the specter of global Islam swelling India's "small" Muslim numbers; the Shah Bano case (Das 1990); Masjid-vs-Mandal politics; the "vote bank" discourse descending from colonial census categories and separate electorates ("enumerated communities," Kaviraj 1992); and the discourse of "appeasement" linking domestic minority claims to the enemy state of Pakistan. Appadurai calls the Hindu majority "a double fiction" — a colonial-census construct riven by caste — "demonstrably a project, not a fact."
- **Globalization's contribution.** Migration, mass-mediated hybridity, census manipulation, traveling constitutionalism, and rapid illicit financial flows all intensify the "volatile morphing" by which today's minority is feared as tomorrow's majority, exacerbating social uncertainty and creating conditions for "crossing the line from majoritarian anxiety to full-scale predation, even to genocide."
- The chapter closes on the **suicide bomber** (born, he notes, in the Tamil–Sinhala struggles of 1970s Sri Lanka) as the "darkest possible version" of the liberal number one, and on the U.S. occupation of Iraq as a live case of building a "long-distance democracy" out of nothing but large, globally connected minorities.
