# In Search of Respect: Selling Crack in El Barrio (Bourgois, 1995/2003)

Landmark U.S. urban ethnography of Nuyorican crack dealers in East Harlem (El Barrio). Bourgois lived in a tenement apartment for years from 1985, befriended street-level dealers (Primo, Caesar, and their network under Ray), and analyzed the underground economy, legal-labor humiliation, school failure, gender violence, and family crisis as products of structural apartheid, cultural capital barriers, and a contradictory **street culture** of resistance and self-destruction. Not a book “about crack” per se — drugs as visible personal-level expression of deeper class and ethnic exclusion.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*In Search of Respect* is the charter U.S. urban ethnography of the crack era: multi-year residential [[participant-observation]] among Nuyorican street-level dealers in [[el-barrio|East Harlem]], treating the Game Room crackhouse as a **workplace** and the drug trade as epiphenomenon of class/ethnic **apartheid**. Bourgois’s load-bearing construct is [[street-culture]] — oppositional practices seeking dignity that also destroy participants and community — explicitly set against Oscar Lewis’s [[culture-of-poverty]] while refusing pure structuralism that erases agency, gender, and meaning. Legal service work and schools are sites of [[cultural-capital]] humiliation; gender chapters document sexual violence and patriarchy-in-crisis without sanitizing; family chapters reject simple fatherlessness. The 2003 edition adds post-1995 dynamics (boom/recession labor, Mexican succession, carceral/quality-of-life policing, one-strike housing, life-course updates) without overturning the thesis.
