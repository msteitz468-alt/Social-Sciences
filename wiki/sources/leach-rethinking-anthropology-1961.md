# Rethinking Anthropology (Leach, 1961)

Essay collection by [[leach-edmund|E. R. Leach]] (LSE Monographs No. 22). The title properly belongs to Chapter 1 (first Malinowski Memorial Lecture, LSE, 3 December 1959); the volume reprints five earlier papers with new introductory notes. Continuity of theme: reassess known ethnographic facts under unorthodox assumptions; preference for generalized structural patterns over typological "butterfly collecting"; sustained engagement with [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] and with British [[descent-theory]] (especially [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] on [[complementary-filiation]]).

## Core Moves (attributed — Leach)

1. Comparative anthropology should seek **generalized patterns** (topology of structures), not accumulate ethnographic "types" (butterfly collecting).
2. **Affinity as alliance** between corporate groups must be distinguished from individual husband–wife affinal ties; Fortes's "complementary filiation" can disguise the former.
3. Jinghpaw terminology is **algebraically generable** from a small set of ideal-rule hypotheses (asymmetrical marriage; mayu–dama).
4. Matrilateral cross-cousin marriage systems (Kachin type) have structural implications for **rank, bridewealth, and political hierarchy** that Lévi-Strauss under-specified ethnographically but rightly treated as generalized exchange.
5. **Marriage** definitions that start from Western monogamy miss jural/alliance criteria illustrated by polyandry and Sinhalese law.
6. Mythic and ritual representations of **time** (repetition vs irreversible succession) are amenable to structural analysis.

## Volume Synthesis Note

*Rethinking Anthropology* is Leach’s mid-career method-and-kinship portfolio. The 1959 Malinowski Lecture supplies the program: British functionalism’s comparative aim is exhausted if it means typological “butterfly collecting”; keep generalization by treating organizational ideas as mathematical patterns (topology, ratios, ideal models) in the spirit of Lévi-Strauss rather than Radcliffe-Brown natural kinds. The reprinted essays enact that program on kinship: the 1945 Jinghpaw paper (with 1961 self-critique of elementary-family assumptions) invents “ethnographic algebra”; the 1951 Curl Prize essay is an early English structural commentary on *ESK* that corrects Chin/Kachin ethnography and derives rank/bridewealth implications of matrilateral systems among local descent groups; the polyandry paper dissolves universal marriage definitions into Maine’s bundle of rights; the Kachin–Lakher bridewealth paper separates descent form from marriage institution and refuses complementary-filiation labels; the time essays apply structural myth analysis to Cronus and festival behaviour.

For the wiki, the volume densifies the **alliance vs descent** fight (affinity vs complementary filiation), the **Leach–Lévi-Strauss** critical friendship, and Leach’s method brand (models, algebra, anti-typology) beyond *Highland Burma* alone. Gaps remaining: *Pul Eliya* (1961); Tambiah biography; Goody/Gough primary replies as full sources.
