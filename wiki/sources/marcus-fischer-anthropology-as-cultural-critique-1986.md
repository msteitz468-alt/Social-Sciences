# Anthropology as Cultural Critique (Marcus & Fischer, 1986)

Joint programmatic essay by [[marcus-george-e|George E. Marcus]] and [[fischer-michael-m-j|Michael M. J. Fischer]] mapping anthropology's **experimental moment** in the 1970s–80s human sciences. Companion volume to *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986): where WC diagnoses ethnography as writing under a crisis of authority, ACC surveys experimental ethnographic writing and argues that anthropology's dual modern promise — documenting cultural diversity and serving as **[[anthropology-as-cultural-critique|cultural critique]]** of the ethnographer's own society — can be renewed through [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]] and two techniques of [[defamiliarization]] ([[epistemological-critique]] and [[cross-cultural-juxtaposition]]).

Edition: University of Chicago Press, Chicago/London, 1986. © 1986 University of Chicago. ISBN 0-226-50449-2 (pbk).

## Core Argument

*(Attributed to Marcus & Fischer 1986; not wiki-voice findings.)*

Marcus and Fischer hold that anthropology, like other human sciences, faces a **[[crisis-of-representation]]**: uncertainty about how to describe a changing, interdependent world after the erosion of confidence in totalizing social theory. Ethnography becomes the live arena of theoretical innovation. They restate anthropology's two modern promises — (1) salvaging/documenting cultural difference against homogenization, and (2) using other cultural patterns as **cultural critique** of "our own" common sense — and argue both are in predicament (Said; Mead–Freeman as controversies about representation). Renewal lies in the **experimental moment** of ethnographic writing (person/self/emotions experiments; political-economy embedding of "knowable communities") and in the **repatriation** of anthropology as domestic cultural critique, using two defamiliarization techniques: epistemological critique and cross-cultural juxtaposition.

## Volume Synthesis Note

Marcus and Fischer's 1986 essay is the dual-promise companion to *Writing Culture*: where Clifford et al. diagnose ethnography as writing under a crisis of authority, ACC argues that anthropology's two modern tasks — documenting cultural diversity and serving as cultural critique of home common sense — can be renewed through an experimental moment in ethnographic form and content, the repatriation of research to domestic subjects, and two techniques of defamiliarization (epistemological critique and cross-cultural juxtaposition). The book surveys two experimental trends (person/self/emotions; political-economy "knowable communities") and insists the experimental era should not be closed prematurely: practice is the engine of innovation when macrotheory is in disarray. All program claims remain attributed; third-party ethnographies are secondary readings. Together with the WC ingest, the vault now holds both charter volumes of the mid-1980s experimental/textual moment.
