# Timewalkers: The Prehistory of Global Colonization (Gamble, 1994)

[[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]]'s major mid-1990s synthesis of **world prehistory organized around global colonization** — the neglected question *why were people everywhere?* — rather than around the rise of civilization or the hero-narrative of human origins. Complements earlier global textbook frames ([[clark-world-prehistory-1961|Clark, *World Prehistory*]]) by making **range expansion** the primary analytic object and by arguing that colonization is often an **[[exaptation]]** (unforeseen consequence of social/behavioral change) rather than an **adaptation** designed to fill empty lands.

## Core Argument (attributed, Gamble 1994)

1. European explorers and later prehistorians **expected people everywhere** and therefore never treated near-global distribution as a research problem (Wallace's "universally distributed" as non-information).
2. Prehistory's default agenda became **why civilization was not universal**, not **why humans were** — progress narratives (simple→complex) and Landau-style hero myths structure origins research.
3. Global colonization is better analyzed with **adaptation vs. exaptation** (Gould & Vrba's terminology applied to behavior and range): filling the world is often an unforeseen consequence of social negotiation and world-construction, not a designed adaptive goal.
4. A three-phase colonization guide — **Hominids / Ancients / Moderns** (with a brief **Pioneer** interval) — organizes ~3 Myr of range history without equating any phase with "full humanity" as a teleological prize.
5. Returning **purpose** (deliberate action in ecological time) to deep prehistory does not require returning **progress**; purpose without teleology is the book's methodological wager.
