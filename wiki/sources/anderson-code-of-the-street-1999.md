# Code of the Street (Anderson 1999)

## Bibliographic note

Elijah Anderson, *Code of the Street: Decency, Violence, and the Moral Life of the Inner City*. New York: W. W. Norton, 1999. Ingested from the Norton epub (full text through Notes and Bibliography).

## What this source is

Landmark urban ethnography of interpersonal violence and public social organization in black inner-city Philadelphia. Anderson argues that where civil law and police protection are felt to fail, an informal **[[code-of-the-street|code of the street]]** — rules organized around a desperate search for **respect** and the credible threat of vengeance — governs public relations, especially among youth. The book maps the continuum from “decent” to “street” orientations, family socialization into the code, drug markets and street crime, mating and gender scripts, the “decent daddy” and the transitioning grandmother, and the extended case of John Turner; it closes on the “conversion of a role model.”

Evidence class: **ethnographic** (participant observation, direct observation, in-depth interviews across settings from Chestnut Hill to “ground zero” ghetto pockets along Germantown Avenue and related Philadelphia sites). Theoretical interlocutors include [[wilson-william-julius|W. J. Wilson]] (thanked; structural joblessness frame), Wolfgang & Ferracuti’s subculture-of-violence thesis (distinguished in notes), Whyte, Thrasher, and related street ethnography. ## Volume Synthesis Note

Anderson’s monograph supplies the wiki’s principal **ethnographic specification** of public order and youth violence in the late-20th-century U.S. black inner city. Read against already-ingested [[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|Wilson]] and [[massey-denton-american-apartheid-1993|Massey & Denton]], it neither replaces structural labor-market and segregation accounts nor reduces to [[culture-of-poverty]]: the [[code-of-the-street]] is framed as adaptation under joblessness, discrimination, and criminal-justice distrust, with **[[decent-and-street]]** plurality and code-switching as anti-essentialist guards. The Germantown Avenue continuum is both method and argument; John Turner and the Robert/Rob conversion cases turn structural timing into moral careers. Policy hierarchy is explicit (living-wage jobs, enfranchisement) and recorded as POSITION. Collection gaps that still bound interpretation: *Streetwise* (1990), *When Work Disappears*, quantitative code-scale literature, and peer ethnographies of the same era (e.g. Duneier *Sidewalk* — queued).
