# Ben Finney

## Overview

Ben Finney was an anthropologist who founded the modern experimental and replicative voyaging movement in Pacific studies, per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017). He established the Polynesian Voyaging Society and, from 1976, the Hōkūle'a program of experimental double-hulled canoe voyages, beginning with the first successful round-trip voyage between Hawai'i and Tahiti that year.

## Contributions

- **[[experimental-voyaging|Experimental voyaging]] as method** — founded the Polynesian Voyaging Society and directed the Hōkūle'a program, providing practical/experimental confirmation of the feasibility of purposive two-way sailing across the Pacific using traditional navigation techniques (star navigation/latitude sailing), without reliance on instruments for longitude.
- Contributed empirically to resolving the drift-vs-deliberate voyaging debate (see below): the Hōkūle'a voyages, alongside Geoff Irwin's "survival sailing strategy" model and earlier computer simulations (Levison, Ward, and Webb 1973, which found drift alone could not reach Hawai'i in 16,000 simulated trials), are presented by Kirch as jointly establishing deliberate, purposive two-way voyaging as the scholarly consensus account of Polynesian expansion.
- Quoted (Finney 1996b:372, per Kirch) on the dual scholarly and Indigenous-revival significance of the Hōkūle'a voyages — attributed claim linking the scientific program to a broader Pacific Islander cultural renaissance.
- Catalyzed what Kirch treats as a "voyaging renaissance" among Pacific Island communities, tying the scientific debate over settlement method to contemporary Indigenous cultural revival.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Finney's experimental program is presented by Kirch as part of the evidentiary basis (per the drift/deliberate-voyaging debate) that resolved in favor of purposive two-way voyaging (resolution_status: resolved-by-consensus per Kirch), against the earlier minority positions of [[heyerdahl-thor|Thor Heyerdahl]] (American-origins/drift) and Andrew Sharp (1956, Asian-derived but incapable of purposive long-distance navigation).
