# David H. Kelley

## Overview

David Humiston Kelley was a North American Mayanist whom Michael Coe presents as [[knorosov-yuri|Yuri Knorosov]]’s most effective Western spokesman and as a principal bridge from Cold War phonetic heresy to the post-Thompson decipherment (as reported by Coe 2012). Coe’s Harvard undergraduate contemporary, Kelley was a Tozzer advisee (with William Sanders), saw much of [[thompson-j-eric-s|Thompson]] and [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]] at Carnegie next to the Peabody, and is portrayed as a nonconformist interested in UFOs, Irish/Armenian genealogy, trans-Pacific diffusion, and lost continents. Set on archaeology at fifteen by Ann Axtell Morris’s *Digging in Yucatan*, he held views Thompson disliked: historical nature of Classic monuments, non-acceptance of the GMT (Thompson) correlation, a trans-Pacific calendar dissertation, and interest in phoneticism ([[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]; [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]).

## Contributions

Chance brought Kelley and Knorosov together at the 1956 International Congress of Americanists in Copenhagen; Kelley, previously unaware of him, was “transfixed,” chatted in bad Spanish, and became the Russian’s most effective Western advocate (Coe’s account). Kelley presented Knorosov at the student “Mesa Cuadrada” seminar at Harvard (1956–57), for which Proskouriakoff had drafted a discussion order and Thompson declined to attend.

Kelley’s 1962 paper “Phoneticism in the Maya Script” (*Estudios de Cultura Maya*) defended Knorosov and refuted Thompson. Coe credits it with first extending Knorosovian method to monumental inscriptions: at Chichén Itzá Kelley read Hermann Beyer’s recurring clause cluster as *k’a-k’u-pa-ka-l(a)* = K’ak’upakal (“Fiery Shield”), the Itzá captain of post-Conquest chronicles—a personal name on stone under Knorosov’s rules ([[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]; [[epigraphy]]; [[classic-maya]]). His massive *Deciphering the Maya Script* appeared in 1976, the year after Thompson’s death; Coe holds that even loyal Thompsonians then faced “irrefutable evidence that Knorosov had been right and Eric very, very wrong.”

Kelley later figures in the Palenque breakthrough network. On sabbatical he sent Calgary undergraduate Peter Mathews to the Primera Mesa Redonda (December 1973) with a blue notebook of every Palenque date, associated glyphs, and prior commentary—transcribed into Thompson catalog numbers under Kelley. The Inscriptions tomb occupant’s name “Shield” was phonetically spelled *pa-ka-l(a)* (Pakal) by Kelley (and independently Coe) using Knorosovian signs; Vienna Yucatec *pakal* = *escudo* “shield” ([[palenque]]). At the April 1974 Dumbarton Oaks Mini-Conference, Kelley worked with Schele, Mathews, Lounsbury, and Merle Greene Robertson on Maudslay plates, extending Palenque dynasty to its first two centuries. Coe links a leaf-shaped lineage glyph to dictionary *le* “leaf”/“lineage” in that circle; Lounsbury’s “Lady Beastie” mythic chronology and parentage breakthroughs (Mathews and Kelley on Bird-Jaguar as son of Shield-Jaguar) continued the same collaborative stream ([[yaxchilan]]; [[long-count]]).

At the 1979 Albany “Phoneticism” conference—Coe’s watershed after Palenque—Lyle Campbell affirmed Knorosov as elaborated by Kelley, Lounsbury, and others. Early in the meeting Kelley pinned a wall chart of largely confirmed CV syllabic signs (consonants × vowels)—a grid form long used for Linear B and Hieroglyphic Hittite but new to Maya studies: “We’ll start with this,” Dave told them ([[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Coe places Kelley with himself as early Western dissenters who found allies among linguists remembering Thompson’s treatment of Whorf and Hill; [[lounsbury-floyd|Floyd Lounsbury]] thought highly of Knorosov. Kelley praised Cyrus Thomas’s clearer late-nineteenth-century view of the script (phonetic-syllabic, logographic, polyvalence) than any other man of Thomas’s period. Proskouriakoff’s Mesa Cuadrada agenda explicitly assigned Kelley the question of Chol/Choloid evidence for the language of the glyphs. Thompson’s 1957 letter to Coe mocked a “witches’ coven” including Kelley, Burland, and “sex-starved Tania,” claimed hundreds of affixes disproved Knorosov, and predicted vindication of *Maya Hieroglyphic Writing* by AD 2000—documentary evidence of the opposition Kelley faced ([[thompson-j-eric-s]]; [[proskouriakoff-tatiana]]).

Downstream, Mathews, the Palenque Mesas, Albany syllabic work, and the post-1975 “take-off” of decipherment all run through channels Kelley helped open. Coe’s acknowledgments list interviews with Kelley among sources for *Breaking the Maya Code*.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Thompson’s hostility is the central critique Coe records: historical inscriptions, non-GMT correlation, phoneticism, and open Knorosov advocacy made Kelley a marked man in the Age of Thompson. Coe treats Kelley’s nonconformist extracurricular interests as color, not disqualification. Coe’s narrative presents *Deciphering the Maya Script* as decisive for the Western field after 1975 rather than as a contested minority tract. Where Coe notes slow uptake of Knorosov into the mid-1970s (including Demarest’s 1976 attack on Knorosov), Kelley stands as the patient monographic answer. Coe does not detail later specialist revisions of individual Kelley readings; the historiographic role is champion and consolidator of logosyllabic method ([[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]).

## Historiography

Primary filing source: [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]. Coe’s Kelley is the American hinge between Leningrad and the Young Turks: Copenhagen conversion, 1962 monument phonetics, 1976 summa, Palenque/Mathews pipeline, and Albany syllabic grid. Without claiming Kelley as sole Champollion (Coe reserves that analogy for Knorosov), Coe makes him indispensable to the Western institutionalization of Knorosovian [[epigraphy]].
