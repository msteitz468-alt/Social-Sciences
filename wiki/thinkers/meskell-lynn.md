# Lynn Meskell

Lynn Meskell is an archaeologist and anthropologist of heritage, a leading figure in
[[critical-heritage-studies]] whose work analyzes how the past is mobilized — and subordinated — in
contemporary politics. This page summarizes her contribution as it enters the vault through
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|*The Nature of Heritage: The New South Africa*]] (2012); it is a
concise network summary, not a full biography.

## Overview

Meskell's long-standing concern is how specific heritages are constructed and deployed at expedient
political moments, and how archaeology is drawn into ideological struggle. Trained originally as a
prehistoric archaeologist (with earlier work in Egypt), she retrained in socio-cultural anthropology
under an Andrew W. Mellon Foundation "New Directions" fellowship, and developed a method she calls
**archaeological ethnography** — long-term participant observation combined with formal videoed
interviews, archival research, heritage survey, and site monitoring. She is also known for founding
work on the journal and field infrastructure of critical heritage studies, and for her later
critical history of UNESCO World Heritage (*A Future in Ruins*, 2018 — noted here as a key work, not
yet ingested).

## Contributions

- **[[negative-heritage]]** — heritage that memorializes atrocity, repression, or a painful past;
  Meskell developed the concept after 9/11 (the Bamiyan Buddhas / World Trade Center parallel) and
  argues the very "processing of history into heritage" involves dealing with a negative legacy.
- **[[past-mastering]]** — the ongoing, unfinished struggle to come to terms with a contested past
  (echoing the German *Vergangenheitsbewältigung*), which in her South African case study extends
  beyond archaeology to territory, conservation, and "private pains."
- **Archaeological ethnography** — her signature method, treating heritage practice and institutions
  as ethnographic objects.
- **The "naturalization" thesis** — that cultural heritage has borrowed its management models from
  natural-resource conservation, so that "biodiversity trumps culture" (see [[kruger-national-park]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Meskell works within [[critical-heritage-studies]] alongside [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]] and
draws on post-processual archaeology, science-studies and governmentality theory (Escobar, Agrawal,
Latour, Appadurai), and post-colonial critique (Gilroy). Her teacher/student genealogy is not
documented in the ingested source and is left open.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Meskell's approach is itself an academic critical discourse with its own authority (a general
reflexive charge against critical heritage studies); her ethnographic claims rest on
participant-observation and interview data whose positionality she foregrounds — she reports, for
example, being excluded from a SANParks project after raising evidence of a peopled precolonial past.
Assessment of her specific readings (e.g., the Makuleke settlement) sits within a divided literature
(see [[makuleke]]).

## Historiography

Meskell's South African work draws on fieldwork c. 2003–2009 and Skukuza-archive research; it should
be read as documenting a specific decade of post-apartheid heritage politics, not a timeless account.
