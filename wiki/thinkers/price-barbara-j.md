# Barbara J. Price

## Overview

**Barbara J. Price** (1928–2006) was a Columbia-trained anthropologist and a theorist of comparative [[cultural-materialism|cultural evolution]]. She is best known as co-author, with [[sanders-william-t|William T. Sanders]], of *[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]* (1968), and for later work sharpening the materialist and comparative logic of state-formation theory.

## Contributions

- ***[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica]]*** (1968, with Sanders): the theoretical and comparative framing — the [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear-evolutionary]] reading of the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom–state]] typology, the anti-diffusionist argument, and the general model of pristine [[state-formation|state formation]] — bears her cultural-materialist stamp especially strongly.
- The **[[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]** concept, jointly developed and generalized in the 1968 synthesis.
- Later comparative work on secondary states and on cultural evolution as a materialist research program (e.g. her "cluster-interaction" model of secondary-state formation, developed after the 1968 book).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained at Columbia in the orbit of [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]]'s [[cultural-materialism|cultural materialism]] and [[fried-morton-h|Morton Fried]]'s political anthropology (both consulting editors of the 1968 book). Her long collaboration with [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]] paired his field-survey empiricism with her comparative-theoretical framing.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Shares the reception of the 1968 synthesis: praised for methodological rigor and comparative reach, criticized for ecological determinism and for the confidence that civilization was a near-inevitable ecological outcome. Her insistence on materialist, etic explanation aligns her with the [[cultural-materialism]] program and against the interpretive and agency-centered turns that followed.

## Historiography

Price is comparatively under-memorialized relative to her co-author despite carrying much of the book's theoretical load — a pattern the [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|source page]] notes in the book's consistent joint "we" voice. Read alongside [[harris-marvin|Harris]] and the Columbia cultural-materialist school.
