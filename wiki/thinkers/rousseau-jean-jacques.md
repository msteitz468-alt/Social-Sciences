# Jean-Jacques Rousseau

## Overview

Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan-French philosopher of the Enlightenment
whose *Discourse on the Origin of Inequality among Men* (1755) is treated by many as a
founding text for the social-scientific question of how inequality arose. He is included
here as a **precursor** (before the disciplines existed) because his framing of the
problem still structures debate — most recently in Flannery and Marcus's
*[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]* (2012), whose
closing chapter is explicitly an "updating" of Rousseau.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — as reconstructed by Flannery & Marcus 2012 and standard history of ideas.)*
Rousseau's *Discourse* was submitted to a 1753 essay competition of the Academy of Dijon
on the origin of inequality; it did not win but became the competition's only remembered
entry. Rousseau argued that in a hypothetical **"state of nature"** people differed only
in natural endowments (strength, agility, intelligence), that self-respect prevailed over
self-love, and that inequality grew as society developed — self-love displacing
self-respect, love of property displacing generosity — until wealthy families imposed a
**"social contract"** that institutionalized inequality and gave it moral justification.
He held that agriculture could exacerbate natural differences by letting the abler
generate a surplus outstripping their neighbors.

## Intellectual Genealogy

No teacher/student relationships are recorded here. Rousseau's importance for this wiki is
downstream: he set the terms — natural vs. artificial inequality, the social contract as
the moment inequality is legitimated — that later social scientists (from the
[[social-evolutionism|nineteenth-century evolutionists]] to Flannery & Marcus) inherited
and revised.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed — Flannery & Marcus 2012.)* Flannery and Marcus note that Rousseau wrote
before anthropology, sociology, or archaeology existed, so his "natural man" rested on
anecdotal travelers' accounts. They credit him with the enduring insight — which they say
their comparative evidence supports — that **inequality results from people's efforts to
be thought of and treated as superior**, while faulting the single-primordial-condition
premise: egalitarian foraging societies were plural (clanless and clan-based) rather than
one uniform "state of nature," and hereditary inequality was created and reversed
repeatedly, not once. See [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]].
