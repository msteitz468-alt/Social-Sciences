# James Hunt

English speech therapist and racial theorist who founded the **Anthropological Society of London** (1863) as a polygenist, anti-Darwinian rival to the [[ethnology|Ethnological Society]], precipitating the [[victorian-ethnology-anthropology-schism-debate|Victorian ethnological–anthropological schism]]. Hunt is significant in this wiki (as of the 1987 Stocking ingest) less for any lasting scientific contribution than as the polemical figure whose racial polygenism forced the institutional crisis out of which the modern discipline's name — "anthropology" — emerged. This page records Hunt's racial doctrines as historical positions, not as findings; they are scientifically and ethically defunct.

## Overview

Hunt made his living treating stammerers by his father's method and came to "the great question of races and languages" through work on human speech. He joined the Ethnological Society in 1856 and became joint secretary in 1860, but — amid friction between the Society's Quaker-humanitarian element and a racialist current — resigned in 1863, having already founded his competing Anthropological Society the same year. He died in 1869, at odds even in his last months with his own society's provocative "Cannibal Club."

## Contributions

- **Founded the Anthropological Society of London (1863).** Following [[unknown|Paul Broca]]'s Paris society, Hunt defined "anthropology" as "the science of the whole nature of man," posed against a narrower "ethnology" he derided as a "science of races" hamstrung by biblical dogma over the unity of mankind. His organization emphasized racial classification, was [[polygenism|polygenist]], and rejected Darwinism (Hunt held there was little difference between "a disciple of Darwin and a disciple of Moses").
- **"On the Negro's Place in Nature" (1863).** Hunt's presidential address argued that Negroes were a separate species, closer to apes, incapable of civilization — the hard hereditarian racism he acknowledged he had "imbibed from the late Dr. Knox." (See [[race]]; the claim is recorded as a repudiated historical position.)

## Intellectual Genealogy

Hunt's racial doctrine descended directly from [[unknown|Robert Knox]] and from the Continental polygenist anatomy of [[unknown|Paul Broca]]. He stood opposed to the Darwinian evolutionists — [[huxley-thomas-henry|Huxley]] (who called his society "that nest of imposters"), [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], and the young [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], whose humanitarian Quaker principles were offended by the "pugnacious" racism of the Anthropological Society's president. He was succeeded after his death by the milder [[unknown|John Beddoe]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

[[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]]'s monogenist works, which Hunt derided as "still the text-books of the day, to the disgrace of science," outlasted Hunt's own program. [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking]] treats Hunt (attributed) as the "antithesis" in the dialectic that produced the 1871 Anthropological Institute: his polygenist physical anthropology was partly absorbed into the post-Darwinian "compromise," even as his overt political racism was rejected by the reunified science. Hunt's historical importance is thus institutional and cautionary rather than doctrinal.

## Historiography

Hunt figures in histories of anthropology chiefly through the schism he caused; Stocking's *Victorian Anthropology* (ch. 7) is the standard analytic account. He is a central exhibit in the discipline's reckoning with its entanglement in scientific racism (see [[race-and-culture-debate]], [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]).
