# James Cuno

Museum director and leading public advocate of **cultural internationalism** and
the **[[encyclopedic-museum|encyclopedic museum]]** in the early-twenty-first-century
debate over antiquities acquisition, [[restitution]], and
[[unprovenanced-antiquities|unprovenanced antiquities]].

## Overview

James Cuno (b. 1951) directed the Harvard University Art Museums (from 1991),
the Courtauld Institute of Art, the Art Institute of Chicago (from 2004; context
of *Who Owns Antiquity?*), and later the J. Paul Getty Trust. His 2008 book
*[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns Antiquity?]]* is the vault's primary
statement of the cultural-internationalist / encyclopedic-museum position on
[[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] — the pole opposite
[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s demand-side anti-looting advocacy in
[[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Loot, Legitimacy and Ownership (2000)]].

## Contributions

- **Programmatic defense of encyclopedic museums** as cosmopolitan institutions
  presenting the world's cultures without nationalist prejudice (attributed, Cuno
  2008).
- **Critique of "nationalist retentionist cultural property laws"** as political
  instruments that nationalize a pre-national past and divert attention from what
  he treats as the real ownership question (attributed, Cuno 2008).
- **Advocacy of restored [[partage]]** and of Merryman's preservation–knowledge–access
  triad over pure ownership claims (attributed, Cuno 2008, following
  John Henry Merryman).
- Editorial volume *Whose Culture?* (2009) collected further museum-side essays
  (cited in notes of the 2011 paperback).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Cuno writes as a **museum director**, not as a field archaeologist. His argument
draws heavily on legal scholar **John Henry Merryman** (cultural property
internationalism), British Museum director **Neil MacGregor** (the museum as a
"world under one roof"), and theorists of nationalism and hybridity (Benedict
Anderson, Anthony D. Smith, Edward Said, Amin Maalouf, and others as he reads
them). He is the named target of archaeological critics who treat encyclopedic-
museum acquisition of unprovenanced material as demand-side complicity in looting
(see Position A on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Archaeologists in the Renfrew/IARC tradition reject Cuno's claim that the
unprovenanced-acquisition dispute is a "surrogate" diversion: they hold that
demand for unprovenanced objects **does** finance site destruction and that
encyclopedic ideals do not license post-1970 acquisition without provenance
(attributed counter-position; see [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]]). Source-nation
governments and cultural-nationalist advocates treat his internationalism as a
rationalization of historical power imbalances in collecting. These are live
positions, not wiki-voice verdicts.

## Historiography

Primary self-statement: *Who Owns Antiquity?* (2008; afterword 2011). Broader
museum debate literature (Merryman essays; MacGregor's BM program; responses in
archaeological ethics) is not yet fully ingested; this page rests on the 2008/2011
book.
