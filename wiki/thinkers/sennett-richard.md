# Richard Sennett

American sociologist of labor, cities, and public culture (b. 1943), known for a humanistic, essayistic study of how economic arrangements shape the moral and emotional texture of everyday lives. Summary page; primary treatment in this wiki is from *[[sennett-corrosion-of-character-1998|The Corrosion of Character]]* (1998). **Thinkers Hub deferred** — a single volume is ingested; a full intellectual biography would need *The Hidden Injuries of Class*, *The Fall of Public Man*, and *The Craftsman* plus secondary sources.

## Overview

Sennett works at the intersection of [[economic-sociology]] and the sociology of culture, using long-form interviews and participant observation to reach what survey and labor-market data cannot: the lived experience of work. His recurring question is how large-scale economic change registers in individual [[character]] — the capacity to sustain loyalty, purpose, and a coherent life narrative. He writes in an avowedly literary register, braiding fieldwork vignettes with intellectual history (Diderot, Smith, Weber, classical and Renaissance sources).

## Contributions

- **[[character]]** — the long-term ethical/emotional dimension of the self, set against short-run "personality"; the object whose "corrosion" under flexible capitalism titles his 1998 essay.
- **[[flexibility]]** — analyzed not as freedom but as a three-element regime of power (discontinuous reinvention of institutions; flexible specialization; concentration without centralization).
- The critique of the **new "teamwork" work ethic** (attributed): a "power without authority" in which managers hold power while denying they wield it — the ethical opposite of [[weber-max|Weber]]'s driven, self-disciplining [[spirit-of-capitalism|Protestant ethic]] (see [[protestant-ethic-debate]]).
- A running argument that flexible capitalism generates **indifference** and undermines the character question "Who needs me?", threatening a regime's legitimacy.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Sennett was trained at Harvard (his early collaborator on *The Hidden Injuries of Class* was Jonathan Cobb; his teachers included David Riesman — neither yet ingested). *The Corrosion of Character* is in explicit dialogue with [[weber-max|Weber]] (the work ethic), [[marx-karl|Marx]] (alienation vs. the bakery workers' "indifference"), [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (habit), [[beck-ulrich|Beck]]'s risk society, [[bauman-zygmunt|Bauman]] and Fredric Jameson (the postmodern self, which Sennett resists), [[castells-manuel|Castells]] and [[sassen-saskia|Sassen]] (the informational/global economy), and the economists Piore & Sabel and Bennett Harrison. He stakes explicit positions *against* communitarianism and against straightforward nostalgia for the old work ethic.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Sennett's method is his most contested feature: critics read *The Corrosion of Character* as a brilliant but impressionistic essay whose sweeping claims rest on a handful of unrepresentative vignettes and on nostalgia for a historically brief, unionized male-breadwinner order; his admirers value exactly the qualitative depth that quantitative labor sociology misses. The dispute is recorded at [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]]. Sennett is candid about his own positionality (a family radical past; an "outsider, just left of center" at Davos), which he folds into the text as reflexive material rather than concealing.

## Historiography

Sennett's reputation rests on a long sequence of books from *The Uses of Disorder* (1970) through *The Craftsman* (2008) and beyond; *The Corrosion of Character* is his most widely read and most frequently assigned, treated across the social sciences as a touchstone statement of the human costs of post-Fordist flexibility.
