# Nancy Chodorow

## Overview

Nancy J. Chodorow (b. 1944) is an American feminist sociologist and psychoanalyst whose object-relations account of gender has been widely influential. Her paper "Family Structure and Feminine Personality" in *[[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Woman, Culture, and Society]]* (1974) supplies the "feminine psyche" strand of [[ortner-female-to-male-1972|Ortner's]] nature/culture argument and is now primary-ingested. *The Reproduction of Mothering* (1978) is not yet ingested.

## Contributions

- **Feminine personality as socially produced, not innate** (1974): a relational, connected feminine personality and a more anti-relational masculine personality arise from the near-universal fact that **women raise children of both sexes**, not from biology. Mothers respond differently to infant sons and daughters; boys learn manhood as abstract rights/duties via emotional rejection of the mother's world; girls become "little women" through continuous personal identification with the mother.
- **Personal vs positional identification**: both sexes form a *personal* early identification with the mother; the boy later shifts to *positional* identification with a remote father-role (public/universalistic), while the girl's personal identification continues into mothering — a mechanism linking family structure to [[female-subordination|sexual asymmetry]] and the [[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic/public]] split.
- **Reform implication** (1974): more men in caregiving and valued non-maternal roles for women would interrupt the cycle.
- **The reproduction of mothering** (1978 monograph, not yet primary-ingested): generational reproduction of the gender division of labor via the same psychoanalytic mechanism.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Chodorow's model has been criticized for universalizing a Western, middle-class, nuclear-family childrearing arrangement and for essentializing the mother–daughter bond; Ortner herself flags that Chodorow's confirming studies are "primarily taken from Western society." These are the same generalization worries at stake in the [[nature-culture-gender-debate]].

## Historiography

*The Reproduction of Mothering* remains a touchstone of feminist theory and sociology of the family; Chodorow's later work turned toward clinical psychoanalysis. The 1974 essay is the primary source for this page's contributions section.
