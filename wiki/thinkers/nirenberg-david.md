# David Nirenberg

David Nirenberg (b. 1964), American historian of medieval and early-modern Europe and of the intellectual history of [[anti-judaism]]; author of *[[nirenberg-anti-judaism-2013|Anti-Judaism: The Western Tradition]]* (2013). Long associated with Johns Hopkins and later the University of Chicago. Acknowledgments in the 2013 book also note institutional support across those years.

## Overview

Nirenberg’s program (as stated in the 2013 book) treats “Judaism” as a category Western (and Islamic) thought has used to make sense of the world — a history of **thinking with Judaism**, not only a history of hatred toward living Jews. *Anti-Judaism* is the major long-span statement of that program; earlier work (*Communities of Violence*, 1996) focused on medieval inter-religious violence in the Crown of Aragon (not yet ingested).

## Contributions

From *Anti-Judaism* (2013, attributed throughout):

- **[[anti-judaism]] as habit of thought / basic tool of Western construction** — not an archaic closet; systems generate “Judaism” from their own entrails.
- **Mask/genealogy method:** continuity of form with transformation of content (Nietzschean “terrifying masks”; Foucault’s genealogy cited as somewhat confusing derivative).
- **Three methodological deviations:** (1) focus on non-Jews’ thought; (2) ideas granted power against pure interest models (Weber switchmen); (3) continuity + change against pure cutting.
- **Causal formula on the Holocaust:** inconceivable without the deep history of Jewish questions; **not caused by** that history, not inevitable, not German *Sonderweg* alone.
- **Cross-tradition scope:** Egypt → early Christianity → patristics → Islam → medieval Europe → Spain/Inquisition → Reformation → Shakespeare → confessional politics → Enlightenment → Revolution → German philosophy → modernity → Arendt/Horkheimer–Adorno/Auerbach.
- **Critical cost of critique itself:** concepts produced by criticizing Judaism can hamper critique of anti-Jewish critique (Arendt epilogue case).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

External review literature not yet ingested. Flashpoints visible from the book’s own self-positioning and from standard field tensions:

- Span vs. depth: millennia in one monograph.
- Whether “thinking with Judaism” under-weights material persecution and living Jewish agency (Nirenberg’s own acknowledged “deviation”).
- Whether mask-continuity still smuggles teleology despite disclaimers.
- Political reception in Israel/Palestine discourse (Nirenberg anticipates “special pleading” charges).
