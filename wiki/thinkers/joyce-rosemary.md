# Rosemary A. Joyce

Network summary — enriched from [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010]].

## Overview

Rosemary A. Joyce is an American archaeologist of Mesoamerica and a leading figure in the
archaeology of gender, sexuality, and the body. Her work brings Judith Butler's theory of
**performativity** to bear on the archaeological record, treating gendered identity as something
produced through repeated bodily practice rather than expressed by a fixed prior category.

## Contributions

In "Girling the Girl and Boying the Boy: The Production of Adulthood in Ancient Mesoamerica"
(*World Archaeology*, 2000), Joyce reads Aztec (Postclassic, via 16th-century ethnohistoric
sources) and Formative-period Mesoamerican evidence over a ~2,000-year *longue durée* to argue that
gendered adulthood was **produced** through lifecycle ritual and bodily modification — "girling"
and "boying" as ongoing performative work on the body, not the disclosure of a natural sex
(attributed, Joyce 2000). She flags a source-reliability caveat on the ethnohistoric codices
(Sahagún, Codex Mendoza). Her work is a key application of [[embodiment]] and
[[personhood]] to gender in [[feminist-archaeology]]. (See "Formative" as a regional
period-system term for Mesoamerica.)

## Intellectual Genealogy

Builds on Judith Butler's performativity (*Gender Trouble*, 1990) and the feminist-archaeology
program launched by Conkey and Spector (1984); part of the sex/sexuality retheorization (with
Meskell, Voss, Perry) noted in the reader. Teachers and students not recorded in the ingested
source (`[[unknown]]`).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Performative readings of ancient gender are powerful but depend on how far ritual/iconographic
evidence can bear the interpretive weight; the ethnohistoric sources for Aztec practice are
Spanish-mediated. Recorded here from a single source; to be enriched as her monographs are ingested.
