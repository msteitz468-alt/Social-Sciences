# David Reich

## Overview

David Reich (b. 1974) is a geneticist at Harvard Medical School and one of the
principal architects of the genome-wide **ancient-DNA revolution**. By his own
account (Reich 2018), his laboratory had by the end of 2015 published more than
half of all the world's genome-wide human ancient DNA. His central contribution
was to industrialize aDNA analysis — scaling the sample-by-sample techniques
developed in [[paabo-svante|Svante Pääbo]]'s Leipzig laboratory into a
high-throughput "genomics factory" capable of processing many hundreds of
ancient samples per study.

## Contributions

- **Industrial-scale ancient DNA.** With Nadin Rohland, adapted the
  in-solution hybridization capture method (developed by Matthias Meyer and
  Qiaomei Fu) to target >1 million informative genome positions, dropping the
  cost of genome-wide data to under ~$500/sample (as of Reich 2018). See
  [[ancient-dna]].
- **Admixture statistics.** Reich's group (with the mathematician Nick
  Patterson) developed and popularized the family of *f*- and *D*-statistics and
  admixture-graph methods used to detect and date ancient population mixture.
  See [[admixture-statistics]].
- **Findings (attributed, as of Reich 2018):** the near-replacement of northern
  European ancestry by a steppe migration after ~5,000 years ago; the mixed
  origins of South Asians ([[ancestral-north-indians]] / [[ancestral-south-indians]]);
  multiple Asian migration pulses into the Americas; the recurrent-mixture
  thesis. These belong to the study pages / subject pages, not to wiki voice.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Reich frames himself as part of [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]'s
school, having been trained by David Goldstein (a Cavalli-Sforza student) for
his Ph.D. He began a seven-year "apprenticeship" with [[paabo-svante|Svante Pääbo]]
in 2007 on the Neanderthal and Denisova genome projects, and established the
first U.S. whole-genome ancient-DNA laboratory in 2013 with Pääbo's help. His
long-term analytic partner is the applied mathematician Nick Patterson.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reich's Chapter 11, "The Genomics of Race and Identity," and an adapted 2018
*New York Times* op-ed drew substantial public dispute — including open letters
from groups of geneticists and social scientists — over his argument that
average biological differences among populations should be expected and openly
studied. Positions are recorded on [[race-and-genomics-debate]]; this wiki does
not adjudicate. Critics also note the book's foregrounding of Reich's own
laboratory (a caveat Reich states himself).
