# David Wengrow

British archaeologist (UCL Institute of Archaeology), specialist in comparative early civilizations and the archaeology of the Near East and Egypt. Co-author with [[graeber-david|David Graeber]] of *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* (2021). Primary wiki coverage so far is from *Dawn* only.

## Overview

Wengrow's solo books treat early Egypt, the idea of "civilization," and monster imagery in the first age of mechanical reproduction. *Dawn* is the major public statement of a joint project with Graeber: re-reading global prehistory against stage-evolutionary and "origins of inequality" narratives. After Graeber's death (2020), Wengrow wrote the acknowledgements and carried the book through publication.

## Contributions (from *Dawn*, attributed joint G&W)

Archaeological synthesis supporting (as G&W argue):

- Upper Palaeolithic and later evidence of seasonal hierarchy and grand burials that do not imply permanent ranked societies.
- Neolithic "play farming" and delayed/partial uptake of agriculture ([[play-farming]]).
- Early urbanism without kings (selected Mesopotamian, Indus, Trypillia, Chinese, and American cases) — **G&W's reading of specialist literature**, not wiki-voice site conclusions.
- Decomposition of "the state" into [[three-elementary-forms-of-domination]] without a single origin moment.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. **Keep separate from *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Dawn of Everything]]*:** this is Scott’s own deep-history of the earliest states citing Wengrow as Mesopotamian specialist and trade synthesizer, not the joint G&W program.)*

- **Guide and lecture host.** Scott names Wengrow among top Mesopotamian alluvium guides (with Pournelle, [[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]], Richardson) and thanks Wengrow (with Sue Hamilton) for the opportunity to give the 2016 Gordon Childe Lecture at the Institute of Archaeology, London.
- **“Uruk world system.”** Scott cites Guillermo Algaze and Wengrow’s framing of an integrated Uruk-period trade sphere (~3500–3200 BCE: Caucasus to Persian Gulf; Iranian Plateau to Eastern Mediterranean). Scott’s use: state demand for non-alluvium resources exploded long-distance trade and **enlarged disease-contact spheres**—a disease-pool reading of early urban trade, not a restatement of G&W’s *Dawn* political theses.
- Thematic anti-teleology (reversible sedentism, modular collapse, rejection of one-way primitivism→civilization) resonates with broader contemporary challenges to stage evolutionism; Scott does **not** engage Graeber/Wengrow by name in the extracted ranges—do not collapse this page’s *Dawn* material into Scott’s argument.
