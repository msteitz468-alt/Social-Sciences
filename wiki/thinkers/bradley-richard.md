# Richard Bradley

British prehistorian (Professor of Archaeology, University of Reading, as of the 1998 blurb), specializing in **landscape archaeology**, **monumentality**, and Atlantic European prehistory (Neolithic–Bronze Age; rock art).

## Overview

Bradley is a leading voice in British/European prehistoric interpretation at the interface of [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] and landscape approaches: monuments as active shapers of experience (time, place, social memory), not passive reflections of surplus economy. *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998) is the vault’s first full ingest of his program.

## Contributions

*(Attributed unless marked as shared field consensus; primarily from *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* 1998.)*

- **Surplus inversion:** Monument building need not wait on agricultural surplus; monumental architecture can create the temporal-spatial conditions that make farming acceptable (*Altering the Earth* lecture lineage; 1998 Part I).
- **[[death-of-the-house]]:** Long mounds and early enclosures as transformations of LBK house symbolism and household life-cycles.
- **Monument sequences over types:** Surface "types" (variant circles, etc.) often stages in construction/reconstruction sequences ending at different points on different sites (1998 Ch. 9).
- **Circular archetype:** Later Neolithic–Bronze Age British/Irish ceremonial architecture reworks a shared circular form through changing access, audience, and elite appropriation.
- **Landscape integration:** Henges and stone circles related to continuous horizons and experienced landscape (Orkney cases with Colin Richards; dialogue with [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]]/[[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]]).
- **Mesolithic ontology reconstruction:** Pan-European burial symbolism as evidence of reciprocal culture–nature relations; [[lepenski-vir]] counter-reading vs Hodder’s domestic/wild antagonism; Dragsholm side-by-side graves as staged worlds.
- **Memory theory for archaeology:** Bloch formal ritual time + Rowlands inscribed/incorporated memory applied to multi-phase monuments ([[stonehenge]] “Theatre of Memory”).
- **Domestication of ritual / second agricultural revolution:** Mid–2nd millennium BC shift of ceremonial focus into domestic sphere and field systems; Urnfield parallel; Cranborne Chase house–barrow isomorphism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Interlocutors named in the 1998 preface include Barbara Bender, John Chapman, Mark Edmonds, Colin Richards, Julian Thomas, Alasdair Whittle, Marek Zvelebil; excavation dialogue at Clava with colleagues including Gordon Barclay. Positions relative to [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] (absolute dating / social archaeology), [[sherratt-andrew|Sherratt]] (farming/ideology metaphors), and [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]/post-processual meaning programs are developed in the book rather than as a single school label.

## Whittle 1996 — manuscript reader and monument dialogue

*(Attributed bookkeeping + intellectual network — *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Whittle, Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996; *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* 1998.)*

- **Manuscript reader:** [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]] lists **Richard Bradley** among readers of the *Europe in the Neolithic* manuscript (Acknowledgements — names-only; not a co-author claim). The 1996 book and Bradley’s 1998 monument program are near-contemporary Critical Turn / early Contemporary syntheses of European Neolithic–BA landscape and belief.
- **Reciprocal citation climate:** Bradley’s 1998 preface names Whittle among interlocutors; Bradley links Sherratt’s “religious conversion” metaphor, **Whittle’s “creation of new worlds,”** and Thomas’s conceptual appropriation of nature when reframing later prehistoric farming intensification ([[agricultural-revolution]] Bradley densification).
- **Shared / parallel themes (do not collapse programs):** both stress monuments and places as active shapers of time, memory, and community rather than mere surplus reflections; both engage mobility, enclosure gatherings, and Mesolithic–Neolithic conceptual change. Whittle’s Ch. 10 critique that material-resource competition alone is “too limited” because beliefs and ideals are excluded sits in the same theoretical climate as Bradley’s landscape/memory operationalizations of [[ritual]] and [[social-memory]] — dialogue of milieu, not identity of models.
