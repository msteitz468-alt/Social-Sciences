## Overview

Francis Galton (1822–1911), a grandson of Erasmus Darwin and cousin of Charles Darwin,
led an approximately sixteen-month expedition (1850–51) west of Lake Ngami in
southwest Africa among the Damara and Ovampo peoples. George W. Stocking, Jr.
(*Victorian Anthropology*, 1987) treats this expedition as one of four case studies
(alongside George Grey, Thomas Williams, and Alfred Russel Wallace) illustrating how
Victorian ethnographic fieldwork, colonial encounter, and racial ideology jointly
shaped the transition from Prichardian ethnology to social evolutionism (attributed,
Stocking 1987). Stocking's central thesis about Galton is that this African field
experience "fixed a set of racial attitudes and preoccupations that shaped his
scientific work for the rest of his life" (attributed, Stocking 1987) — a claim about
Galton's own psychology and later doctrine, not a wiki-voice endorsement of the
doctrine itself. Galton later became a founder of eugenics and anthropometry and, in
a methodological aside during a discussion of [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1888
paper, posed what later scholarship calls "Galton's Problem" for the [[comparative-
method]].

## Contributions

Galton's 1853 travel volume, *Tropical South Africa*, combined quantitative physical
anthropology (sextant-based body measurement of a Khoikhoi woman), compiled but
unpublished vocabularies, and ethnographic notes on Damara religion, kinship
("eandas"/descent groups), and taboos; Stocking reports it became an important
reference for 1850s ethnologists, cited by Robert Latham in the Crystal Palace
guidebook and in *Descriptive Ethnology* (finding, Stocking 1987). As documented by
Galton during the expedition, the nomadic, "nganda"-organized Damara practiced
cattle-raiding and, per Galton's report, sometimes left dying kin unattended, while
the agricultural, monarchically organized Ovampo were reported by Galton to observe
elder-respect norms and to lack poverty (attributed, Galton per Stocking 1987 —
these are Galton's own field claims, not verified wiki-voice ethnographic facts).
Galton drew sharply contrasting racial characterizations from this contrast — judging
the Damara mentally inferior even to his dog "Dinah," and the Ovampo more moral but
"uglier" — a comparison Stocking says was later cited by many writers on "the savage
mind" (attributed, Galton per Stocking 1987). [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]] later
cited Galton's account of the Damara's limited numerical conception ("whose
conceptions of number scarcely transcend those of a dog") as evidence for mental
continuity from lower creatures to the highest human reasoning (attributed, Spencer
per Stocking 1987).

Following the 1859 publication of his cousin Charles Darwin's *On the Origin of
Species*, Galton underwent an intellectual reorientation: he lost his religious
belief, suffered a mental breakdown in 1866, and in 1865 published the two-part
article "Hereditary Talent and Character," which Stocking describes as containing
"the germs of all his later polymathic labors in anthropometry, experimental
psychology, statistical method and theory, the biometric study of heredity, and
eugenics" (finding, Stocking 1987). Stocking argues that Galton's resulting social
Darwinist theory of race and civilization directly transmuted his personal African
field experience: a generalized, homogenized "Negro race," modeled on the Damara, was
counterposed to the American Indian to argue for hereditary racial character, while a
generalized "savage" was counterposed to "civilized man" to argue that natural
selection had bred traits such as steady labor into Europeans (attributed, Stocking
1987 reading Galton). Galton held a pessimistic view of civilization's stability,
arguing that modern social conditions — inherited wealth and differential fertility
between "improvident" and "ambitious" classes — actively reversed eugenic selection,
and that unchecked this would cause civilizations to "lapse back into barbarism"
(attributed, Galton per Stocking 1987). **These claims about hereditary racial and
class hierarchy are recorded here as historical positions Galton held and Stocking
analyzes, not as findings this wiki endorses; twentieth- and twenty-first-century
genetics, psychology, and anthropology have comprehensively repudiated the
race-ranking and eugenic-selection claims Galton built his later program on.**
Stocking classes Galton, alongside Walter Bagehot, as an English writer of the 1860s
who might well be called a "social Darwinist," distinguishing this more pessimistic
strand — which saw modern civilization as enabling "unnatural selection" via
differential class fertility — from the optimistic, Lamarckian-tinged evolutionism of
[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson|
McLennan]], and [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] (attributed, Stocking 1987). See
[[social-darwinism]].

Institutionally, Galton replaced [[hunt-james|James Hunt]] as Ethnological Society
secretary in 1863; despite holding racial views in some respects as extreme as the
rival Anthropologicals', he argued anthropology's "political value" lay in teaching
sympathy toward other races "as our kinsmen, rather than as aliens" (attributed,
Galton per Stocking 1987). He became the dominant figure in the physical-anthropology-
led revival of the Anthropological Institute in the mid-1880s (finding, Stocking
1987).

Methodologically, Galton raised the objection — in the discussion following
[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1888 paper, where he was the Institute's president —
that cross-cultural comparisons used to generalize about the co-occurrence of
sociocultural traits needed first to establish that the compared cases were
historically independent rather than diffused from a common source, since related
cases amount to "duplicate copies" rather than independent data points (attributed,
Galton per Stocking 1987). This objection entered later scholarship as "Galton's
Problem." Stocking states that Galton's critique provoked [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] to
pursue diffusion studies leading to his general critique, "The Limitations of the
Comparative Method of Anthropology," with Edward Westermarck and Fritz Graebner
following similarly skeptical lines — so that Tylor's 1888 comparative-method paper
paradoxically catalyzed the antievolutionary reaction (attributed, Stocking 1987). See
[[comparative-method]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

Galton was Charles Darwin's cousin and a grandson of Erasmus Darwin; he was also a
friend of [[maine-henry-sumner|Henry Sumner Maine]] from Cambridge and recalled Maine
as one of the few outsiders "who became thoroughly at home in Trinity itself"
(attributed, Galton per Stocking 1987). Stocking groups Galton with
[[huxley-thomas-henry|Huxley]] and Wallace among evolutionists whose formative
overseas experience substantially shaped their anthropological viewpoints, while
noting that Galton, more systematically than [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], rejected
Lamarckian assumptions and treated race and culture as far less interactive
(attributed, Stocking 1987).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking's overall reading of Galton is that his scientific racism was not abstract
doctrine but a direct transmutation of a specific, brief episode of colonial field
contact into a totalizing theory of race and civilization — a case study, in Stocking's
account, of how thin ethnographic exposure could harden into durable and consequential
racial ideology. Modern biological and social science has since repudiated the
race-hierarchy and eugenic-intervention claims central to Galton's later program; the
eugenics movement his 1865 and later work founded caused substantial documented harm
in the twentieth century, a legacy this page notes but does not detail further (outside
Stocking's scope in the ingested material). Galton's methodological insight regarding
non-independence of compared cultural cases ("Galton's Problem") remains a live
concern in the [[comparative-method]] and is treated separately from his racial
theorizing.
