# Harold Garfinkel (1917–2011)

American sociologist, founder of **[[ethnomethodology]]**. Trained at Harvard under
[[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] and decisively shaped by the phenomenology of
[[schutz-alfred|Alfred Schutz]], Garfinkel took over Parsons's problem of social order but
inverted its solution: instead of asking how shared norms are internalized to produce
order, he asked how members *themselves* produce and recognize orderly, "accountable"
activity as an ongoing practical accomplishment. His *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]*
(1967) named the field and became its founding text.

*Deep treatment: [[hubs/thinkers/garfinkel-harold|Thinkers Hub — Garfinkel]]. This page is
the concise network summary.*

## Overview

Garfinkel was a professor at the University of California, Los Angeles, where he built the
research community — [[bittner-egon|Egon Bittner]], Aaron Cicourel, Harvey Sacks, Emanuel
Schegloff, David Sudnow, Don Zimmerman, and others — from which ethnomethodology and, later,
conversation analysis grew. The eight studies collected in the 1967 volume were written over
the preceding twelve years and draw on empirical materials from a suicide prevention center,
a university psychiatric clinic, and a jury project. The book is notorious for its
deliberately estranging prose, which Garfinkel used to block readers' common-sense reception
of the very taken-for-granted world he made his topic.

## Contributions

All theoretical contributions below are in **attributed voice**.

- **Reframed the problem of order.** Garfinkel argued that "the objective reality of social
  facts as an ongoing accomplishment of the concerted activities of daily life… is, for
  members doing sociology, a fundamental phenomenon" — see [[practical-action]].
- **[[accountability|Accountability]].** He held that producing a social setting and making
  it "observable-and-reportable… account-able" are one and the same activity.
- **[[indexicality|Indexicality]] and [[reflexivity-of-accounts|the reflexivity of accounts]].**
  He argued that accounts are irremediably tied to the occasions of their use, and that this
  reflexivity is an essential condition of practical action, not a removable defect.
- **[[breaching-experiment|Breaching demonstrations]].** He devised disruptions of everyday
  scenes to make [[background-expectancies]] visible.
- **[[documentary-method-of-interpretation|The documentary method of interpretation]].**
  He adapted Karl Mannheim's concept to describe the circular, mutually-elaborating way
  members read appearances as documents of an underlying pattern.
- **The [[etcetera-principle|etcetera principle]] and "ad hocing".** He argued that applying
  any rule or coding scheme requires unavoidable ad hoc work, so that coded results are "a
  persuasive version" of order rather than a neutral description of it.
- **The critique of the "cultural dope".** He rejected the model of the actor as a passive
  enactor of internalized norms, insisting members are practical methodologists.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under:** [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] at Harvard. Garfinkel's stance
  toward Parsons is admiring *and* oppositional — he builds on Parsons's rigor about the
  order problem while rejecting the internalized-norm solution.
- **Decisive influence:** [[schutz-alfred|Alfred Schutz]]'s phenomenology of the natural
  attitude and background expectancies; through Schutz, Husserl and Gurwitsch.
- **Trained / shaped:** [[bittner-egon|Egon Bittner]] (research assistant and co-author) and
  the wider UCLA circle. Harvey Sacks — singled out by Garfinkel as an "extraordinary"
  resource — carried the program toward conversation analysis.
- **Relation to programs:** defined ethnomethodology against Parsonian
  [[structural-functionalism]]; adjacent to but distinct from [[symbolic-interactionism]],
  [[interpretive-sociology]], and the [[social-construction-of-reality|phenomenological sociology of knowledge]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Garfinkel was charged with obscurantism (the prose) and with dissolving explanation into
description. The most consequential episode in his reception is the afterlife of the **Agnes
case** ([[garfinkel-agnes-study]]): the subject on whom his analysis of sex status as a
managed achievement rested was later found to have deceived the researchers about being
intersexed. Garfinkel treated this reflexively — the disclosure "turned the article into a
feature of the same circumstances it reported" — but critics have read it as raising sharp
questions about the evidential base of the study. The dispute over whether ethnomethodology
supplements or subverts conventional sociology is recorded at
[[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate|the ethnomethodology debate]].

## Historiography

Garfinkel's reputation rose from marginal provocation in the 1960s to that of a founder
whose program spawned conversation analysis and the sociology of scientific work. The
standard origin story — that "ethnomethodology" was coined in connection with his mid-1950s
jury project — circulates in the secondary literature; the 1967 volume itself reports the
jury study (Ch. 4) without dating the term's coinage, so the wiki records the coinage claim
as attributed to Garfinkel's later accounts rather than as established here.

### Directions in Sociolinguistics (1972)

Garfinkel's chapter "Remarks on Ethnomethodology" appears as ch. 10 of Gumperz and Hymes's
edited volume *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (1972, pp. 301–324), drawing on material
later incorporated into ch. 1 of *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in
Ethnomethodology]]* (1967) and explicitly framing the foundational assumptions underlying
[[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]]'s and [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]]'s chapters in
the same volume (see [[conversation-analysis]]). Garfinkel offered his own working
definition there (quoted verbatim, p. 301): "I use the term ethnomethodology to refer to
various policies, methods, results, risks, and lunacies with which to locate and
accomplish the study of the rational properties of practical actions as contingent
ongoing accomplishments of organized artful practices of everyday life."

The chapter presents two empirical cases in addition to the material already summarized
above: (1) a coding study of roughly 1,500 folders at the UCLA Outpatient Psychiatric
Clinic (with Michael Mend and Patricia Allen), in which coders following elaborately
specified instructions could not avoid invoking unstated "ad hoc" considerations —
glossed by Garfinkel as "et cetera," "unless," "let it pass," and the medieval legal term
"factum valet" — a practice he termed **"ad hoeing"**; the clinic itself objected to this
finding and would not permit publication of the coding-procedure specification without a
disclaiming cover note, a documented instance of institutional resistance to reflexive
research findings. (2) A "common understanding" study in which students recorded, in two
columns, what a husband and wife actually said in a commonplace conversation and what each
understood the other to mean; as students were pressed toward greater literalness, the
task became "impossible," illustrating Garfinkel's argument that common understanding is
a **process**, not a substantive shared "product" — the **documentary method of
interpretation** by which an utterance's sense is read as evidence-of an underlying
pattern, and the pattern is reciprocally used to interpret the evidence.

The chapter also states Garfinkel's **five closing policies** of ethnomethodology
(attributed): (1) any occasion whatsoever — "from witchcraft to topology" — may be
examined for how members accomplish sense, facticity, and explanation; (2) the "rational"
properties of inquiry are organizational phenomena, not products of rule-compliance; (3)
practical activity's rational properties must be examined as members produce and
recognize them in situ, not assessed against an external standard; (4) any setting is
"self-organizing" with respect to making its own order accountable; (5) the demonstrable
rationality of proverbs and elliptical expressions is itself an achievement of everyday
activity, not a defect. The editorial introduction to the volume (attributed to Gumperz
and Hymes) frames ethnomethodology's concept of **indexicality** as an alternative account
to Basil Bernstein's restricted/elaborated code framework, and draws an explicit — though
qualified — parallel between Garfinkel's "member" (competence in situated language use)
and Chomskyan linguistic competence, while noting Garfinkel treats practical reasoning's
underlying rules as open-ended rather than formalizable into a finite generative system
(citing Cicourel 1970 for the parallel). Garfinkel's cross-disciplinary genealogy is
widened in this chapter to include, beyond Schutz, later Wittgenstein, ordinary-language
philosophy, and existentialist Marxism (Sartre).
