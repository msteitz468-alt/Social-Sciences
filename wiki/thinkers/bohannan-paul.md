# Paul Bohannan

American cultural anthropologist (1920–2007), trained in the British
structural-functionalist tradition at Oxford under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E.
Evans-Pritchard]]. With his then-wife Laura Bohannan he conducted fieldwork among the
[[tiv|Tiv]] of central Nigeria around 1949–1953, producing a cluster of monographs — *The
Tiv of Central Nigeria* (1953), *Tiv Farm and Settlement* (1954), and the work ingested
here, *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (1957).
This page is dense on the legal-anthropology contribution; the economic-anthropology work
(spheres of exchange, the impact of money) is noted but not yet ingested.

## Overview

Bohannan is best known in legal anthropology for the methodological programme of
*Justice and Judgment* — the insistence that a people's own **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk system]]** of jural categories (for the Tiv, the concept **[[jir|jir]]**) be rendered in its own terms before any analytical or comparative frame is applied, and that the Western folk system of "law" must not be passed off as a neutral analytical vocabulary. He explicitly modelled the folk/analytical distinction on Evans-Pritchard's treatment of the [[nuer|Nuer]] lineage system (the Nuer agnatic genealogy as folk system; "lineage system based on the principle of segmental opposition" as analytical system).

## Contributions

- **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|Folk system vs. analytical system]]** — his central methodological move, generalizable well beyond law.
- **[[jir]]** — the elicited Tiv organizing concept for dispute-settlement institutions (court, case, moot), analyzed as "counteraction" rather than "rules."
- Founding contributions to modern **[[legal-anthropology]]** as a self-conscious subfield.
- In economic anthropology (not yet ingested here), a leading **[[substantivism|substantivist]]** through his analysis of Tiv multicentric exchange and the impact of general-purpose money.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] at Oxford, and thus a
second-generation heir of the British [[structural-functionalism|structural-functionalist]]
and segmentary-lineage tradition (Radcliffe-Brown, Fortes, Evans-Pritchard). His principal
antagonist in legal anthropology was [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]], whose insistence on
translating other legal systems into Western jurisprudential categories Bohannan opposed
(see [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Critics — Gluckman, E. A. Hoebel, Laura Nader, and others — charged that *Justice and
Judgment* was so committed to Tiv categories that its material could not be used
comparatively; Gluckman coined the barbed phrase "cultural solipsism." Bohannan replied
at length (notably in his review of Gluckman's *Ideas in Barotse Jurisprudence* and in the
1968 preface), defending the folk system and denying that he had ever claimed Tiv culture
was unique. He also, in 1968, offered his own self-criticism of the marriage-*jir*
chapters as having lapsed into describing substantive law after arguing that only
institutions of judgment are properly comparable.

## Historiography

The Bohannan–Gluckman exchange is a standard set-piece in histories of legal anthropology
and in the wider methodological debate over emic vs. etic description and the
translatability of legal concepts; see [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]].
