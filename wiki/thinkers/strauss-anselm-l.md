# Anselm L. Strauss

## Overview

Anselm L. Strauss (1916–1996) was an American sociologist in the [[chicago-school|Chicago]] / qualitative field tradition, co-author with [[glaser-barney-g|Barney G. Glaser]] of *[[glaser-strauss-discovery-grounded-theory-1967|The Discovery of Grounded Theory]]* (1967). The preface assigns him the Chicago pole of the partnership (down-to-earth qualitative research) against Glaser's Columbia quantitative/middle-range pole.

## Contributions

Joint 1967 program (attributed to Glaser & Strauss):

- [[grounded-theory]] as generation from data via comparative analysis.
- [[theoretical-sampling]] and [[constant-comparative-method]].
- Substantive vs formal grounded theory; application properties (fit, work, understandability, control).
- Dying research as empirical matrix (*Awareness of Dying*, etc. — not yet ingested); status-passage formalization path from dying as nonscheduled passage (Ch. IV).

Affiliations with [[symbolic-interactionism]] and Chicago craft are **contextual** (preface; later reception) — *Discovery* itself is a joint method charter, not a Blumerian SI manifesto.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Post-1967 GT manuals and the Glaser–Strauss methodological split are partially visible via Tavory & Timmermans (*[[tavory-timmermans-abductive-analysis-2014|Abductive Analysis]]*, 2014): they credit late-1980s Strauss and Strauss & Corbin manuals with GT’s textbook dominance, place Strauss/Corbin on an “interactionist” pole, and note Strauss mentioned abduction only once in a footnote — insufficient, they argue, for an abductive redesign of the program.
