# Theda Skocpol

## Overview

Theda Skocpol (b. 1947) is an American historical and political sociologist, a central figure in the "state-centered" turn in comparative-historical sociology from the late 1970s onward. Ingested through one source: her first book and best-known work, *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions: A Comparative Analysis of France, Russia, and China]]* (1979), the founding statement of the **structural, state-centered theory of [[social-revolution]]**. Deep analyses: study summary [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study]]; Studies Hub [[hubs/studies/skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study]].

The book grew out of Skocpol's Harvard doctoral work in the comparative-historical milieu of [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]], whose *Social Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (1966) she names as the most notable contemporary model for her method. *States and Social Revolutions* both extended Moore's agrarian-class comparative program and broke from it by insisting on the **potential autonomy of the state** as an administrative-coercive organization irreducible to class structure.

## Contributions

- **[[structural-theory-of-revolution|Structural theory of revolution]].** Skocpol argues (attributed) that social revolutions are not "made" by revolutionary movements acting on ideology but "come" from the conjunction of old-regime state breakdown under international/military pressure and autonomous peasant insurrection — a non-voluntarist, structural account. Her epigraphic touchstone (via Wendell Phillips): "Revolutions are not made; they come."
- **[[state-autonomy|Potential autonomy of the state]].** Against both Marxist (state as instrument/relation of class rule) and pluralist views, Skocpol argues the state is a set of administrative, policing, and military organizations headed by an executive, with interests of its own and potential autonomy from dominant-class control — a "Janus-faced" structure anchored in both class-divided society and an international state system. This concept launched the "bring the state back in" agenda of 1980s comparative sociology.
- **[[social-revolution|Definition of social revolution]].** The "rapid, basic transformation of a society's state and class structures... accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below" — defined by the coincidence of societal-structural with class upheaval and of political with social transformation.
- **[[comparative-method|Comparative historical analysis]].** A rigorous macro-comparison combining J. S. Mill's Method of Agreement (France, Russia, China as positive cases) and Method of Difference (England, Japan, Prussia/Germany, 1905 Russia as negative/contrast cases), defended against the demand that valid generalization requires large-n statistics.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under** [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]] at Harvard — the comparative-historical, agrarian-class program that *States and Social Revolutions* both continues and revises.
- **Builds critically on** [[marx-karl|Marx]] (adopts class analysis of peasant–landlord relations; rejects the reduction of the state to class) and on the Weberian–Hintzean tradition of the state ([[weber-max|Weber]], Otto Hintze, Tocqueville) for the autonomy argument.
- **Defines herself against** the four dominant theory families of the 1960s–70s: Marxist, aggregate-psychological (Ted Gurr), systems/value-consensus (Chalmers Johnson), and [[political-conflict-theory|political-conflict]] ([[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]], *[[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978|FMR]]* 1978) theories of revolution — see [[structural-theory-of-revolution]].
- **Sister comparativists** she situates alongside: Moore, Eric Wolf (*Peasant Wars of the Twentieth Century*), John Dunn (*Modern Revolutions*); Tilly is both foil and partial ally on organization/resources.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*States and Social Revolutions* became one of the most debated works in late-20th-century sociology. The principal lines of critique — the near-elimination of ideology and agency (William Sewell Jr., Michael Burawoy), the reliance on secondary historical sources rather than archival research (historians), and the challenge from rational-choice accounts of collective action — are recorded on the study page and in [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]. Skocpol's own later collaborative program (*Bringing the State Back In*, 1985) generalized the state-autonomy argument into a research agenda; her subsequent work turned to American social policy and civic life (beyond this wiki's current ingest).
