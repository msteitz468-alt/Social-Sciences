# Douglas E. Yen

## Overview

Douglas E. Yen was an ethnobotanist whose collaborative work with archaeologists shaped Pacific prehistory, per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017). Kirch's dedication describes Yen as, over three decades, "mentor, co-fieldworker, and colleague, as well as friend and confidant," crediting Yen's "ceaseless adherence to the sound scientific principle of always being alert to the unsuspected alternative hypothesis" as a direct methodological influence on Kirch's own work. As of the 2017 revised-edition preface, Yen was reported still living, "in the ninth decade of his life."

## Contributions

- **Makaha Valley irrigation study** (Yen et al. 1972) — collaborated with archaeologists on the first stratified evidence for agricultural change in Polynesia, at Makaha Valley, O'ahu.
- **Sweet-potato (*Ipomoea batatas*) dispersal research** — proposed a tripartite hypothesis (Yen 1974) for the plant's prehistoric spread, a foundational contribution to the evidence base bearing on the question of pre-Columbian Polynesia–Americas contact.
- Co-obtained NSF funding with [[green-roger|Roger Green]] in 1970 for the Southeast Solomons/Santa Cruz interdisciplinary project (the Southeast Solomons Culture History Program), with fieldwork phases in 1971–72 and 1977–78 across Kolombangara, Ulawa, Uki, San Cristobal, Santa Ana, Santa Cruz/Nendö, the Reef Islands, Anuta, Vanikoro, Tikopia, and Taumako.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained (mentored):** [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Vinton Kirch]] — direct mentor-mentee and co-fieldworker relationship over three decades, per Kirch's own dedication and biographical account, spanning Anuta, Tikopia, Makaha Valley, and the Southeast Solomons project.
- Close scholarly collaborator of [[green-roger|Roger Green]] on the Southeast Solomons Culture History Program.
