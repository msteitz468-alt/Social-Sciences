# Lynne G. Zucker

## Overview

Lynne G. Zucker is a sociologist whose 1977 laboratory experiments on institutionalization
and cultural persistence, republished with a new postscript as ch. 4 of *The New
Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis* (1991), are the volume's principal
micro-level, experimental contribution. Zucker also convened a 1985 UCLA conference
(cited by DiMaggio and Powell as Zucker 1987) credited by the volume's editors with
bringing the program's contributors together for the first time as a self-conscious
school.

## Contributions

Zucker defined institutionalization not as a binary state but "as a variable, with
different degrees of institutionalization altering the cultural persistence which can be
expected" — a definitional move DiMaggio and Powell (ch. 1) single out as characteristic
of the new institutionalism's cognitive orientation. Using the autokinetic-effect
illusion (adapted from Sherif 1935 and Jacobs and Campbell 1961), she ran three linked
experiments with 180 female subjects manipulating degree of institutionalization purely
through verbal framing (personal-influence dyad vs. "organizational context" vs. formal
"office"). Across transmission (three generations of judgment-passing), maintenance
(one week later), and resistance-to-change (a confederate's attempt to redefine the
judgment standard) experiments, results consistently ordered personal influence < organizational
context < office in degree of cultural persistence — e.g., resistance-to-change scores
of 1.59 (personal influence), 1.03 (organizational context), and 0.05 (office), F=16.27,
p<.001. Zucker concluded: "the greater the degree of institutionalization, the greater
the generational uniformity of cultural understandings, the greater the maintenance
without direct social control, and the greater the resistance to change through personal
influence."

In her 1991 postscript, "Microfoundations of Institutional Thought," Zucker positioned
her micro/cognitive approach as complementary to, but analytically independent of, the
macro-institutionalist accounts of [[dimaggio-paul]] and [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 3) and
[[scott-w-richard]] and [[meyer-john-w]] (ch. 5). She argued institutionalization should
be measured independently of resource dependence — critiquing macro studies (Dobbin et
al. 1988; Tolbert 1985) for confounding institutionalization with strategic,
resource-driven adaptation — and coined the phrase "contagion of legitimacy" (Zucker
1987) for the tendency of institutionalized systems to accrete interdependent
obligations that make deinstitutionalization "seldom accidental."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Zucker's chapter opens from ethnomethodology, drawing on Garfinkel (1967), Zimmerman
and Pollner (1970), Schutz (1962), and Berger and Luckmann (1967) for its account of
institutionalized acts as "objective" (repeatable without altering common understanding)
and "exterior" (reconstructed as intersubjective rather than merely subjective
understanding). She explicitly reacted against two prior approaches: the "subsystem"
approach (functional necessity/reward, citing Angell 1936, Parsons 1939/1940, Sumner
1906) and the "normative framework" approach (internalized norms, citing
[[parsons-talcott]] 1951 and Berger and Luckmann 1967) — critiquing both for lacking
independent criteria to identify which norms are actually internalized.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Zucker's predicted ordering diverged explicitly from [[meyer-john-w]] and
[[rowan-brian]]'s (1977) claim that organizations lacking well-defined technologies
import institutionalized rules while remaining loosely coupled internally: she expected
"substantive internal changes in tandem with more ceremonial practices, thus greater
homogeneity and less variation" rather than decoupling. [[dimaggio-paul]] and
[[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 3) register the same divergence from Meyer and Rowan, noting
their own view "diverges somewhat" from the loose-coupling account — situating Zucker's
prediction as part of a live disagreement over how deeply institutionalization
penetrates organizational structure versus buffering it.

## Historiography

DiMaggio and Powell (ch. 1) credit a 1985 UCLA conference, convened by Zucker (cited as
Zucker 1987), with bringing together scholars "intrigued by the effects of culture,
ritual, ceremony, and higher-level structures on organizations" in numbers sufficient
for "neoinstitutional theory to be named and reified" as a self-conscious research
program — placing Zucker at the organizational, not only intellectual, founding of the
school.
