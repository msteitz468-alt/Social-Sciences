# Jean-François Jarrige

## Overview

Jean-François Jarrige directed the French Archaeological Mission to Pakistan’s long campaign at [[mehrgarh]] (1974–1986; resumed 1996–97 as of Kenoyer’s writing) and related work at [[nausharo]]. [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] (1998) treats the Mehrgarh–Nausharo sequence as the principal deep-time foundation for the [[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]] — clarifying “settling down” and the establishment of domestic plants and animals on the Bolan piedmont long before the Harappan Phase cities.

Catherine Jarrige’s figurine studies (cited separately in Kenoyer 1998) form a related analytical stream for [[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]] and [[indus-religion]]; this page centers Jean-François’s site-sequence leadership as presented by Kenoyer.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]].)*

- **Mehrgarh as paradigm site.** Excavations under Jarrige revealed an aceramic Neolithic (Period IA ca. 6500–6000 BCE) of mud-brick houses (hand-formed plano-convex bricks with finger impressions; multi-compartment storage), domestic wheat and barley, early hunting then domestic sheep/goat, and the start of humped zebu management — overturning the pre-1970s assumption that South Asian domesticates were simply Near Eastern imports via Iran/Baluchistan.
- **Indigenous food production.** Kenoyer, following the Jarrige/Costantini line, treats the transition to food production as an **indigenous event** that probably occurred **simultaneously** in highland and piedmont Baluchistan, with early trade from western highlands to Indus plains and Makran coast.
- **Seasonal highland–piedmont model.** Kenoyer cites Jarrige’s reconstruction of seasonal movement with herds and grain; Mehrgarh at the Bolan Pass base became permanently reoccupied without requiring a major climate/rainfall shift.
- **Nausharo and urban transition.** Jarrige’s Nausharo work documents burned rooms and rebuilding around the Early Harappan–Harappan transition (Kenoyer notes ash/burning that need not be warfare); painted storage jars (e.g. bull tied to pipal tree), terracotta figurine groups, and craft contexts feed Integration-Era narratives. Kenoyer cites Catherine Jarrige on Nausharo figurine assemblages linked to ritual/iconography.
- **Regionalization without warfare package.** Kenoyer uses Jarrige’s Nausharo burning evidence among cases where destruction need not imply enemy slaughter — part of his wider argument that Regionalization war technology did not “flourish” as a city-making package.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **French Archaeological Mission to Pakistan** — multi-decade institutional vehicle.
- **Dialogue partners in Kenoyer’s frame:** [[meadow-richard|Richard Meadow]] (fauna); Costantini (plants); Lechevallier & Quivron (architecture); [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]] (urban synthesis); C. Jarrige (figurines).
- **Historiographic effect:** shifts Indus research’s chronological baseline from “cities and immediately preceding period” to a multi-millennial tradition rooted in farming/pastoral communities.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The “indigenous simultaneous highland–piedmont” model is an attributed research program; absolute dating refinements and debates over Near Eastern crop/animal packages continue beyond Kenoyer 1998. Figurine-to-gender and figurine-to-religion inferences (often via C. Jarrige) remain heavily interpretive (see [[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]]).
