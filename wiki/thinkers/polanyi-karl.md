# Karl Polanyi

## Overview

Karl Polanyi (1886–1964) was a Hungarian-Austrian-British economic historian and social theorist, a founder of **economic sociology** and of the **[[substantivism|substantivist]]** school of economic anthropology. Born in Vienna and raised in Budapest, he served as an officer in the Austro-Hungarian army in the First World War, became a journalist and editor at the Viennese financial weekly *Der Österreichische Volkswirt*, and fled the rise of fascism in 1934, settling in England (where he lectured for the Workers' Educational Association and the Oxford and London extramural delegacies) and later the United States (Bennington College, then Columbia University). His masterwork, *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]* (1944), was written in America during the Second World War.

Polanyi's central argument is that the nineteenth-century **[[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]]** was a historically unprecedented and utopian attempt to *disembed* the economy from society — to subordinate human beings, nature, and money (the "[[fictitious-commodities|fictitious commodities]]") to market law — and that its inevitable failure, through a spontaneous protective **[[double-movement|countermovement]]**, produced the catastrophes of the twentieth century: world war, depression, and fascism.

## Contributions

- **[[embeddedness|Embeddedness]].** Polanyi's thesis that "man's economy, as a rule, is submerged in his social relationships" — that in all societies before nineteenth-century market society, the economy was an instituted process embedded in kinship, religion, and politics, not a separate, self-regulating sphere. The market economy is the historical exception, in which "social relations are embedded in the economic system." The concept was revived for contemporary economic sociology by Mark Granovetter (1985).
- **[[forms-of-integration|Forms of integration]].** A comparative typology of the ways economies are instituted — **reciprocity** (supported by symmetry), **redistribution** (supported by centricity), **householding** (supported by autarchy), and **exchange** (supported by the market pattern) — drawn from the economic anthropology of Malinowski, Thurnwald, and Firth.
- **[[fictitious-commodities|Fictitious commodities]].** The argument that labor, land, and money are not genuine commodities (none is "produced for sale"), so that organizing them through markets rests on a "commodity fiction" which, unchecked, would annihilate the human and natural substance of society (the "satanic mill").
- **The [[double-movement|double movement]].** The thesis that the market's expansion was met by a spontaneous, cross-class countermovement for social protection — and the paradox that "laissez-faire was planned; planning was not."
- **The [[substantivism|substantive]] critique of economics.** Against the "economistic fallacy" that identifies the economy with market-price-directed choice under scarcity, Polanyi insisted on a *substantive* meaning of "economic" — provisioning through instituted process — fully developed in his later *Trade and Market in the Early Empires* (1957). This founded the **formalist–substantivist debate** in economic anthropology (see [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]).
- **A structural theory of fascism.** Fascism as a world-wide structural response to the deadlock of market society (see [[fascism-and-market-society]]), not a national or racial accident.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Formative influences:** Marxian and Weberian traditions (Polanyi credits [[weber-max|Weber]] as "the first among modern economic historians to protest against the brushing aside of primitive economics"); Aristotle (the distinction between *oeconomia* and money-making); Robert Owen (the "discovery of society"); guild socialism and Viennese debates over the socialist calculation problem (his interlocutor and opponent was Ludwig von Mises); and the economic anthropology of Malinowski, Thurnwald, and Firth.
- **Polemical targets:** economic liberalism — Adam Smith's "propensity to barter, truck and exchange," [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]], Ludwig von Mises, and Walter Lippmann (see [[economic-liberalism]]); the "materialistic fallacy" of revisionist economic historians; and the narrow class-interest theory of history shared, in his view, by liberals and "crude" Marxists alike.
- **Relation to [[marx-karl|Marx]]:** Polanyi drew on Marx's critique of market society but explicitly distinguished his [[fictitious-commodities]] from Marx's [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]], and faulted Marx for following Ricardo in defining classes in narrowly economic terms.
- **Students / vehicles:** the substantivist program he founded was carried forward by George Dalton, Conrad Arensberg, Harry Pearson, Marshall Sahlins (early), and the "Columbia group." His brother was the chemist-philosopher Michael Polanyi. *(Specific student links to be filled from later sources.)*

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The Speenhamland thesis** — central to his account of the labor market's birth — was sharply contested by economic historians, most influentially **Mark Blaug** ("The Myth of the Old Poor Law," 1963), who argued Polanyi (following the 1834 Poor Law Commissioners) overstated Speenhamland's prevalence and its demoralizing effects. See [[speenhamland]].
- His **historical cases** (the "hundred years' peace," the potlatch/Kula readings, the Indian famines) have been variously qualified by later scholarship; his anthropology relied on the mid-century ethnographic authorities available to him.
- The **formalist–substantivist debate** (economic anthropologists Herskovits, Firth, and later Scott Cook against the substantivists) contested whether marginalist economics applies to non-market societies — see [[formalist-substantivist-debate]].
- His reputation was revived from the 1980s by the **new economic sociology** (Granovetter's "embeddedness"), by critics of neoliberal globalization (who read the double movement as a template), and by the "always-embedded" reading (Fred Block) that treats the self-regulating market as necessarily and permanently a utopian project.

## Historiography

*The Great Transformation* was written 1941–43 on a Rockefeller fellowship at Bennington College; its main thesis was developed 1939–40 in Polanyi's WEA tutorial classes in England. The Beacon Press edition ingested here carries a foreword by Robert M. MacIver; a later (2001) edition adds a Joseph Stiglitz foreword and a Fred Block introduction that frames the "always-embedded" reading. Polanyi's substantive economics is elaborated in the posthumously influential *Trade and Market in the Early Empires* (1957) and *The Livelihood of Man* (1977).

### Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — the redistribution-vs-market critique

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022.)*

Blanton et al. treat Polanyi's substantivist claim (via Dalton 1968; cf. Sahlins 1972:195, 300–301) — that the major economic system of early state society is government-as-redistributor rather than commercial market exchange, with markets as a late historical development that brings destructive rationalism and individualism to traditional life — as a position their own [[collective-action-theory|collective-action]] account argues against, alongside [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s related "economic functionalism." The authors characterize the claim that markets had little importance before roughly AD 1800 economic modernity as "thoroughly discredited," citing market-importance research in Late Imperial China (Skinner 1964), Mesoamerica, and the Mediterranean (Manning and Morris 2005; Shaw 1981). They record a related live dispute over premodern state economies more broadly: [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s redistribution model, as followed by Service, was critiqued by Timothy Earle (2002, ch. 4), who showed Polynesian chiefs had little role integrating complex economies, with further critiques by Blanton and Fargher (2010) and Feinman and Neitzel (1984); the Inca empire's status as a "redistributive economy" is likewise contested — Murra (1980) documents storage/redistribution, while LeVine (1992) and Mayer (2013) challenge a singular-redistributive characterization. The authors state they will elaborate their critique of the Service/Polanyi position later in the book.
