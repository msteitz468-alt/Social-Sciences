# Sidney Tarrow

American political scientist and political sociologist; specialist in European politics, social movements, and (with [[mcadam-doug|Doug McAdam]] and [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]]) the comparative study of **[[contentious-politics|contentious politics]]**.

## Overview

Tarrow co-founded the Mellon/CASBS “Invisible College of Contentious Politics” that produced *[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* (2001) and companion volumes. His earlier monographs include *Peasant Communism in Southern Italy* (1967) and *Power in Movement* (1994; 2nd ed. 1998) on modular protest, cycles of contention, and the social-movement form. In *Dynamics of Contention* he is one of three co-equal authors of the mechanism–process program.

## Contributions

- **Protest cycles / cycles of contention** — temporal clustering of mobilization (elaborated in *Power in Movement*; carried into DOC trajectory analyses).
- **[[contentious-politics-theory|Dynamics of contention program]]** — with McAdam and Tilly: relational mechanisms (e.g. [[brokerage]], [[certification]], [[scale-shift]]) across wars, revolutions, nationalism, democratization, and social movements.
- **European contentious politics** — Italian postwar conflict; later work on transnational and European contention (*Contentious Europeans* with Imig, noted in DOC author blurb).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Berkeley Ph.D. (1965); taught at Yale and Cornell (Maxwell Upson Professor of Government, later also Sociology). Long collaboration with Tilly and McAdam; dialogue with structuralist, culturalist, and rationalist social-movement camps while advocating a **relational** alternative (*DOC* Preface).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

DOC-level critiques (static boxes vs dynamic arrows; Western/modern bias of cases; mechanism inventory openness) belong on [[contentious-politics-theory]] and the Theory Hub. Primary monographs (*Power in Movement*; Italian work) not yet ingested as full sources.

## Historiography

DOC Preface narrates the failed 1995 Amsterdam “retirement” for Tilly, the CASBS Special Project, and the Mellon Sawyer Seminar that built the Invisible College (Aminzade, Goldstone, Perry, Sewell, graduate cohorts).
