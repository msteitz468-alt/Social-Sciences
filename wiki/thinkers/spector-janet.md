# Janet D. Spector (d. 2011)

American archaeologist; with [[conkey-margaret-w|Margaret Conkey]] (1984), co-author of the program call for an archaeology of gender. Her *Engendering Archaeology* chapter **"What This Awl Means"** models an explicitly **feminist archaeological narrative** centered on a bone awl and women’s production among Wahpeton Dakota contexts, and elaborates a **task differentiation** approach.

## Overview

Spector frames feminist archaeology since the early 1970s as exposing the domination of Western academic knowledge production by white, middle-class men socialized in sexist, racist, and class-hierarchical cultures — and as recovering women’s perceptions and labor in the archaeological past. Critics across disciplines, she notes, showed women’s experiences ignored, trivialized, peripheralized, or stereotyped; studies about “man” claimed gender-neutrality while centering male experience. Her chapter moves from that critique through task differentiation to an inclusive feminist archaeology and a narrative reconstruction of “what this awl means” — filed in this wiki as interpretive **POSITION**, not wiki-voice ethnography. She also critiques prior archaeological stories about awls (e.g. Stone/Mason) as androcentric or thin.

## Contributions

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborator of Conkey; feminist scholarship across disciplines; volume closing case before [[moore-henrietta-l|Henrietta Moore]]’s epilogue. Cited by Jackson and others in the volume on the precariousness of gender attribution from artifacts.

## Densified — "What This Awl Means" per Johnson (2011)

*(Attributed; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson's secondary account of the same 1991 chapter quotes Spector's stated motives — "these motives are empathetic – a longing to discover essences, images, and feelings of the past" (Spector 1993: 1) — and her critique of prior awl typologies as ethnically and gender-biased: "An important but hidden assumption... is that European-produced metal awl tips are more important than Indian-produced awl handles" (Spector 1993: 31–2). Johnson treats the study as an exemplary case of feminist narrative method within [[feminist-archaeology]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Narrative form criticized by some as unscientific; Spector’s point is precisely that form is part of feminist method. Later reception and 1993 book outstanding.
