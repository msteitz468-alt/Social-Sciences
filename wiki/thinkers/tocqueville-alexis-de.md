# Alexis de Tocqueville

## Overview

Alexis-Charles-Henri Clérel de Tocqueville (1805–1859) was a French liberal political
thinker, magistrate, and politician whose *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*
(1835, 1840) is a founding text of comparative political sociology. Born into a Norman
Catholic aristocratic family that had suffered under the Terror (his great-grandfather
Malesherbes was guillotined), Tocqueville treated the advancing **[[equality-of-conditions|equality of social conditions]]**
as a "providential fact" — universal and irreversible — and asked, from the vantage of a
reluctant aristocrat addressing an anxious France, on what terms equality could be
reconciled with liberty. His nine-month 1831–32 tour of the United States (nominally to
study its penitentiaries, with [[unknown]] Gustave de Beaumont) supplied the case. He
wrote as a sociologist before the discipline's name: laws, **[[mores|mores (mœurs)]]**,
and circumstances jointly produce a people's political character, and of the three, mores
matter most.

Volume 1 (1835) analyzes American political institutions — [[sovereignty-of-the-people|popular sovereignty]],
[[township-democracy|township self-government]], the federal system, the jury, and the
danger of the **[[tyranny-of-the-majority]]**. Volume 2 (1840), more abstract, deduces from
equality its effects on ideas, sentiments, and mores: the coining of **[[individualism]]**,
the American reliance on **[[voluntary-association]]** and **[[self-interest-properly-understood]]**
as remedies for it, and the book's celebrated closing warning against a mild, tutelary
**[[democratic-despotism]]**. Later works — *L'Ancien Régime et la Révolution* (1856) and
the posthumous *Souvenirs* — extended his analysis of centralization and revolution to
France directly. This page is drawn from a single ingested source (the Bevan/Penguin
edition of *Democracy in America* with Kramnick's apparatus); the pre-America intellectual
formation below rests on that edition's editorial introduction and is attributed as such.

## Contributions

- **[[equality-of-conditions]]** — Tocqueville made the equalization of social conditions
  the master variable of a whole social science: "I had no difficulty in discovering the
  extraordinary influence this fundamental fact exerts upon the progress of society"
  (Author's Introduction). Its political consequences are not fixed — equality can incline
  toward freedom or toward servitude — which he framed as democracy's central problem.
- **[[individualism]]** — In Vol. 2 he gave the word its modern sociological sense: a
  "considered and peaceful" withdrawal into the small circle of family and friends,
  distinct from selfish *egoism*, that equality generates and that threatens public life
  by isolating citizens.
- **[[tyranny-of-the-majority]]** — He argued the American majority faces no external
  check and rules opinion more completely than any European despot rules action: "I know
  of no country where there is generally less independence of thought and real freedom of
  debate than in America."
- **[[democratic-despotism]]** — The book's climax: a warning that democratic equality
  could birth an unprecedented "immense and protective power," mild and all-providing,
  that reduces citizens to "a flock of timid and hardworking animals with the government
  as shepherd."
- **[[self-interest-properly-understood]]** and **[[voluntary-association]]** — the
  remedies he found Americans practising: an ethic that grounds virtue in enlightened
  self-interest, and the "art of association" by which isolated democratic citizens act
  together and school themselves in liberty.
- **[[mores]]** and the circumstances–laws–mores thesis — his ranked causal claim that
  physical circumstances matter less than laws, and laws less than mores, in sustaining a
  democratic republic.
- **[[township-democracy]]** and administrative vs. governmental centralization — his
  argument that American liberty is built upward from local self-government, and that
  decentralized *administration* (not weak central *government*) is what restrains the
  majority.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Tocqueville acknowledged no formal teacher-master in the disciplinary sense but named his
lineage clearly. Kramnick's introduction records that he immersed himself in Machiavelli,
Montesquieu, Pascal, Rousseau, and Burke, attended Guizot's lectures on European
civilization, and absorbed from the French *doctrinaire* liberals Guizot, Benjamin
Constant, and Royer-Collard the theses that democratization is inevitable, that
Christianity itself carries an egalitarian charge, and that centralized state power is
the chief modern threat to liberty. Royer-Collard reportedly judged the 1835 volumes
"since Montesquieu there has been nothing like *Democracy in America*" — the Montesquieu
comparison being the one Tocqueville's contemporaries reached for. His lifelong
collaborator was Gustave de Beaumont, whose novel *Marie* treated American slavery.
Tocqueville founded no school and trained no students in the academic sense; his influence
runs instead through reception (below).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- His claim of near-total **intellectual conformism** under majority opinion is widely
  contested as an over-generalization from a short visit and elite/genteel informants,
  against the vigorous dissenting print culture (abolitionist, religious, labor) of the
  same antebellum decades — recorded on [[tocqueville-tyranny-of-opinion-debate]].
- **Method:** Vol. 1 is grounded in observation; Vol. 2 proceeds by deduction from the
  single premise of "equality," a shift critics have flagged as producing elegant but
  under-evidenced generalizations. His fieldwork relied on ~9 months, seventeen states,
  and non-systematic elite informants (governors, judges, clergy) — a sampling frame that
  colors, e.g., his account of religion (drawn from clergy he sought out).
- **On race and slavery:** he condemned slavery as an evil and witnessed Indian removal
  firsthand, yet asserted a racial hierarchy and doubted the races could ever live equal.
  His anti-slavery witness and his racial framing sit together and are recorded factually
  on the study page's Historiography rather than adjudicated here.
- **On women:** his separate-but-equal-spheres account of American women is his own
  polemical position and a narrow, period-bound, elite-male observation, not a finding.

## Historiography

Tocqueville's posthumous reputation has moved more than most. Kramnick's introduction
documents his elevation, especially since the 1950s, into "America's public philosopher"
— quoted by every U.S. president since Eisenhower, invoked across the Cold War as the
liberal answer to Marx, and read into both communitarian and conservative programs. The
mid-20th-century mass-society and pluralist theorists received the soft-despotism chapter
as prophecy; the "social capital" literature later revived his associationalism. His own
notebooks and the two travel essays (*A Fortnight in the Wilds*, *Excursion to Lake
Oneida*) preserve the raw observational base — including the melancholy and the eye for
the vanishing frontier — behind the treatise's cool analytic surface. Deep treatment of
his formation, primary texts, and reception is a candidate for a future Thinkers Hub
biography (not yet created).
