# Eric Klinenberg

## Overview

Eric Klinenberg is an American sociologist (NYU) known for urban inequality, isolation, and climate/disaster vulnerability. This page is grounded primarily in *[[klinenberg-heat-wave-2002|Heat Wave]]* (2002; 2nd ed. 2015), his social autopsy of the July 1995 Chicago heat disaster. Study: [[klinenberg-heat-wave-study]].

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Klinenberg, *Heat Wave*.)*

- **[[social-autopsy|Social autopsy]]** of disaster: treat excess deaths as products of social organization (isolation, neighborhood ecology, city services, media), not only weather or individual frailty.
- Empirical demonstration that **[[social-isolation]]**, place-based vulnerability (North Lawndale vs Little Village), and institutional response shaped who died in the heat.
- Analysis of city **public relations** and news production in framing catastrophe.
- **Bodies that Don’t Matter** (*[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]*, 2002) — cautionary tale when the body becomes fetish: (1) journalistic commodity/spectacle selling news; (2) scientific/political object displacing social matters. On the [[chicago-heat-wave-1995|1995 Chicago heat wave]] (excess deaths over 700 in a week in the chapter’s framing; most proportionately deadly heat wave in recorded US history), Klinenberg argues that a **[[social-autopsy]]** of the deaths can reveal structure of urban marginality always present but hard to perceive — yet in real politics, focus on dead bodies (refrigerated meat-packing trucks at the morgue; TV/tabloid parade of corpses; Mayor Daley vs Medical Examiner attribution fight) **obfuscated** the social and political forces that determined who died. Dead bodies as triply abstracted commodities (sell stories → sell news products → sell ads); “heat-related” official status naturalized deaths as products of a necessary natural disaster. Title thesis: bodies that don’t matter — isolated residents became visible only as discovered corpses in “urban burrows,” but commodified spectacle distracted from the social conditions that threatened them most. *(Attributed — Klinenberg, 2001/02 chapter; related 1999 *Theory and Society* “Denaturalizing Disaster.”)*

## Intellectual Genealogy

Disaster sociology and urban ecology lineages; engagement with social capital debates (Putnam et al. as interlocutors in conclusion). Durkheimian suicide analogy for social facts of death rates (preface/notes apparatus).
