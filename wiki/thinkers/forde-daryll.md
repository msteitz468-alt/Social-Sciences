# Daryll Forde

British anthropologist (1902–1973); long-time director of the International African Institute. Co-editor with [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] of *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950) and author of the Yakö double-descent chapter—the volume’s principal full dual-lineage case.

## Overview

In ASKAM Forde appears in two capacities: (1) **co-editor** of the IAI symposium volume that became a charter comparative text of African kinship; (2) **field ethnographer** of the [[yako|Yakö]] of the middle Cross river (SE Nigeria), especially Umor (~11,000 in 1935). His chapter synthesizes prior monographs (1937–1941) into a structural analysis of simultaneous corporate patrilineal and matrilineal organization—“a man eats in his *kepun* and inherits in his *lejima*”—and of culture-contact pressures sapping matriliny in the “present generation.”

## Contributions

### Co-editor of *African Systems of Kinship and Marriage* (1950)

With Radcliffe-Brown, Forde organized the volume of regional kinship essays (Kuper Swazi; Wilson Nyakyusa; Schapera Tswana; Gluckman Lozi–Zulu; Richards Bemba/Central Bantu; Fortes Ashanti; Forde Yakö; Nadel Nuba; et al.). The Introduction (R-B) sets four ideal types including double lineage systems, for which Forde’s Yakö paper is the type case.

### Yakö double descent (ASKAM chapter)

Major analytic moves from the 1950 chapter (attributed):

- **Double unilineal organization as concurrent spheres:** Patrilineal and matrilineal reckoning are not mutually exclusive; both may operate as principles of affiliation and group organization in distinct social fields so succession and inheritance fall into distinct categories. Against older assumption of exclusive unilineal choice.
- **Corporate patricians (*kepun*) and patrilineages (*eponama*):** Territorially compact, exogamous, shrine-centred (*epundet*); land, house sites, oil-palm, co-operative farm labour; plastic under fission/accretion.
- **Corporate matriclans (*lejima*) and matrilineages:** Dispersed under patrilocal residence; currency, livestock, debts, bodily-injury obligations, marriage-payment trusteeship, homicide compensation (woman-transfer to victim’s *lejima*); fertility spirits (*ase*) and *Yabot* sacerdotal village council overriding patrician authority.
- **Adoption as structural process:** Genealogies often majority adopted; sister’s-son adoption into MB’s patrician; *yafoli* purchased children (esp. girls) enabling polygyny demography and matrilineal numerical prestige.
- **Marriage payment (*libeman*):** Multi-interest instrument—husband’s rights/social fatherhood; father’s renunciation of parental authority; matrilineal kin’s responsibility for wife’s marital performance. No levirate/widow inheritance.
- **Dual sentiments:** Matrilineal strength despite lack of co-residence explained via mystical fertility/peace ideology and *ase* cult (priests ritually feminized); not a native “justification” of dual descent but inherent qualities of paternity vs maternity as Yakö experience them.
- **Culture contact:** Palm-oil trade, warrant chiefs/Native Authority, and mission ethics cumulatively weaken matrilineal economic, political, and ritual bonds; patriliny reinforced only in narrow father–son span; kinship outside family more territorial and uncertain.

Prior monographs cited but not yet fully ingested: *Marriage and the Family among the Yako* (1941); “Land and Labour…” (1937); “Kinship in Umor” (1939); “Government in Umor” (1939).

### Comparative uptake

[[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] used Forde’s adoption findings to argue a Yakö trend toward unilateral reduction (Nadel’s position). R-B’s Introduction cites Yakö as the empirical base for the **double lineage** ideal type alongside father-right, mother-right, and cognatic systems.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Structural-functional complementarity** of land-vs-wealth spheres later read against colonial legal change that actively shifted inheritance toward sons—Forde himself documents the shift as underway, not only as external corruption of a timeless system.
- **Nadel’s unilateral-reduction reading** of sister’s-son adoption is a comparative hypothesis about dual-descent “difficulty,” not Forde’s own terminal judgment; keep attributed.
- **IAI / colonial applied anthropology** context: volume addresses administrators; Forde’s culture-contact close (“Custom is an ageing king”) is both ethnography and policy-relevant diagnosis.
- Full intellectual biography and non-Yakö corpus (IAI directorship, later West African work) **not yet ingested**.
