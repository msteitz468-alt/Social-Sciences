# Susan Ervin-Tripp

## Overview

American psycholinguist (1927–2018); professor of Rhetoric and Psychology at UC Berkeley (Ph.D. in social psychology, University of Michigan). Her contribution to *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972), "On Sociolinguistic Rules: Alternation and Co-occurrence," is a major statement of the rule-based framework for sociolinguistic competence. She also conducted fieldwork on bilingualism among French residents of the US, the Navaho, and Japanese-Americans in San Francisco, and worked extensively on child language development.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Ervin-Tripp, 1972, unless noted.)*

- Central theoretical distinction between two classes of rule governing sociolinguistic competence: **[[sociolinguistic-rule|alternation rules]]** (governing choice among alternative ways of speaking — the paradigmatic axis) and **co-occurrence rules** (governing what forms must or may accompany one another once a choice is made — the syntagmatic axis), the latter subdivided into **horizontal co-occurrence** (predictability within the same structural level, e.g., lexical register consistency) and **vertical co-occurrence** (predictability across levels — phonology, lexicon, syntax).
- An address-rule flow-chart analysis of American address terms (following Geoghegan's 1971 componential/information-processing formalism), diagramming binary selectors (child/adult addressee, status-marked settings, colleague rank equality, occupational/courtesy titles, dispensation) leading to terminal address-form outcomes.
- Analysis of the "Poussaint incident" (a policeman's "What's your name, boy?" exchange with Dr. Alvin Poussaint, quoted from Poussaint 1967) as a precise, rule-governed racial insult in which the address term "boy" neutralizes adult/occupational-rank status.
- Cross-cultural comparison of address systems — American, English university/boarding-school, Bisayan (via Geoghegan), Korean (via Howell), 19th-century Russian gentry (via Friedrich, ch. 9 of the same volume), Yiddish (via Slobin), and Soviet-era Russian — used to argue that Brown and Gilman's (1960) **power and solidarity** dimensions likely generalize as universal axes of address, while showing (via Korean data) that "solidarity" may itself decompose into separable intimacy and status components.
- Distinction between manifest/surface content and latent/functional content of an utterance, and a methodological caution that a theory of speech-act function can be pursued without settling ultimate speaker intent.
- Adoption of Hymes's (1964) claim that most societies distinguish at least three style levels, and of Halliday, McIntosh & Strevens's notion of **register**; distinguishes ordinary situational alternation from **metaphorical alternation** (code-switching within a single situation to convey meaning).
- Methodological caution distinguishing three referents of "rule" (generative/interpretive structure; reported beliefs about practice; normative standards of correctness): behavioral rules and speakers' own reports of their rules are systematically different — self-report is not behavior (an explicit instance of the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework rule 6).

Note: pp. 238–248 of the chapter (a stretch of additional style/socialization material bridging into the "Rules for Switching" section) were not read in full by the extracting subagent; the summary above is grounded in the material actually read and should not be treated as an exhaustive account of the full chapter.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Builds directly on Brown and Ford's address research (in Hymes, ed., 1964) and Geoghegan's (1971) componential/flow-chart method; generalizes Brown and Gilman's (1960) power-and-solidarity framework; cites Labov's (1966) New York City social-stratification data and index of linguistic insecurity. Her chapter anticipates and cross-references Sacks (ch. 11) and Mitchell-Kernan (ch. 5) on manifest/latent speech-act function, and forward-links to Blom and Gumperz (ch. 14) on metaphorical code-switching.
