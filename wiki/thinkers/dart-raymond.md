## Overview

Raymond Dart (1893–1988), Professor of Anatomy at the University of the Witwatersrand, South Africa, received the Taung skull in 1924 and published it in *Nature* in 1925 as a fossil hominid he believed occupied an important place in human evolutionary stages — the type specimen of *Australopithecus africanus* (see [[australopithecus]]). Per *Origins*, the paper was initially "treated with contempt and scorn" by the scientific establishment.

## Contributions

Dart's Taung identification later gained wide acceptance as evidence for an African, bipedal hominid ancestor. At Makapansgat, Dart proposed the "osteodontokeratic culture" hypothesis — that early hominids fashioned tools from bone, tooth, and horn — an interpretation later challenged and largely refuted by Bob Brain's taphonomic experiments, which showed many of the purported "tools" were the products of carnivore and other natural bone accumulation rather than hominid manufacture.

Dart was also, per *Origins*, one of the figures Leakey & Lewin credit (or blame) for giving early scientific credence to the idea that humans possess an innate aggressive instinct. Dart argued (attributed voice) that human history's "blood-bespattered, slaughter-gutted archives" and a "mark of Cain that separates man dietetically from his anthropoid relatives" reflected an innate hominid killing drive, reading early fossil and osteodontokeratic evidence as signs that hominid ancestors' era "was rife with predation and cannibalism." This reading anchors one pole of the [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]], alongside [[ardrey-robert]] and [[lorenz-konrad]]; Leakey & Lewin argue this reading of the fossil evidence is not supported.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The osteodontokeratic-culture hypothesis was substantially refuted by Bob Brain's later taphonomic work at Makapansgat and elsewhere, showing the "tool" assemblage largely reflected natural bone accumulation and carnivore activity rather than hominid tool manufacture. Dart's "killer ape" reading of early hominid behavior is likewise contested — see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]] for the fuller dispute, including Leakey & Lewin's counter-position.
