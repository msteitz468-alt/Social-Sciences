## Overview

Nineteenth-century American historian of France and England in North America; principal target of [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s critique of the "Heroic Age" Indian image in *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985). Parkman's multi-volume narrative made Jesuit missionaries and French explorers the historical protagonists and cast Indigenous peoples in racial-evolutionary terms that shaped Canadian as well as American popular history.

*(This page is filled from Trigger’s presentation of Parkman, not from a full primary reading of Parkman’s corpus. All reconstructions of Parkman’s views below are attributed via Trigger 1985 unless noted.)*

## Contributions

*(Attributed via Trigger 1985 and Parkman's own works as Trigger cites them.)*

**Narrative method and Canadian reception.** Trigger states Parkman’s books on the England–France struggle for North America greatly influenced Canada: while French-Canadian historians debated their merits, many English Canadians first became aware of their country’s history through them. Painstaking research was presented as dramatic narrative with dialogue and character sketches; talent for memorable phrases and authoritative presentation made extravagant conclusions widely accepted as facts (Wade 1942; Doughty 1962 as Trigger cites them).

**Institutional dualism of the Anglo–French contest.** Trigger reconstructs Parkman’s framework: the contest for North America as advanced society triumphing over more primitive ones. New England loyalties and Anglo-Saxon pride cast England vs. France as democracy vs. feudalism/absolutism, free enterprise vs. state control, religious freedom vs. Catholic tyranny, peaceful commerce vs. militarism — yet as a romantic Parkman admired French loyalty, zeal, and bravery defending “erroneous” principles, finding colourful French rivals more interesting than sombre New Englanders.

**Tribal essentialism.** Trigger states Parkman carefully treated each tribe separately and studied leaders like Pontiac as individuals, yet assumed each tribe possessed innate social, intellectual, and moral characteristics explaining behaviour — as with European nations. Pride of place went to the [[iroquois|Iroquois]]: boldest, fiercest, most politically astute and ambitious; success from inherent energy, superior mental/moral capacity, ruthlessness; “thorough savages” but “finished and developed” to the highest level without leaving the “primitive condition,” yet Parkman doubted they could evolve civilization alone (Parkman 1927:36–7 as Trigger cites). Other Iroquoians (e.g. [[huron|Hurons]]) shared Iroquois vitality; their subordination was circumstantial, not inherent.

**Ranked Algonkian stereotypes.** Trigger inventories Parkman’s rankings: Shawnees bold/roving; northern Algonkians inferior in intellectual vigour and moral stability; Ojibwas a ferocious horde; Illinois corrupt/degenerate; Montagnais lowest Algonkian type for whom cannibalism was not abhorrent — echoing source biases and social prejudices. Economic reliance on hunting betokened “visible descent in the scale of humanity” (Parkman 1927:7 as Trigger cites).

**Pan-Indian “tenants of the wilderness” psychology.** Trigger summarizes Parkman’s claim that because all Indians hunted to some degree, their mentality was shaped by that common experience (ignoring subsistence variability Parkman himself documented): as “tenants of the wilderness” they lacked settled peoples’ persistence, reason, and foresight; hunter-gatherer life encouraged pride, independence, fortitude but also mental lethargy and contentment with squalor; tribal loyalty coexisted with each man being his own master, preventing effective long-term war or politics; high sensory perception was stigmatized as animal-like; Indians less sensitive to pain and refined feelings than “higher races.”

**Childlike, rigid, doomed.** Trigger reconstructs Parkman’s further claims: inferior reason; vague religion; contradictory character; community order a thin veil over savage passions; guns sped but did not modify prehistoric meaningless intertribal slaughter; Indians basically childlike — lying, treacherous, capricious, predisposed to violence; fixed/rigid, unable to alter customs; minds closed to improvement; greatest leaders opposed civilization; independence made enslavement impossible hence bound to become extinct; “the savage prologue of the American drama” closing in his lifetime (Parkman 1927:8 as Trigger cites).

**Popularizer of contemporary American anthropological thought.** Trigger argues Parkman was a careful student of American anthropology and admirer of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]]; his opinions of Indians cannot be condemned independently of nineteenth-century anthropological opinion or broader White society. As a good scholar he used extraordinary literary talent to express contemporary scientific and popular views, becoming the most enduring expression of America’s mid/late-nineteenth-century consciousness of native peoples and shaping subsequent historians.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Influences Trigger emphasizes:** mid/late-nineteenth-century American anthropology (esp. Morgan); Enlightenment cultural-evolution doctrines popularized via Robertson’s *History of America* as received in American ethnology; romantic narrative history; New England institutional self-understanding.
- **Abandoned project:** Trigger notes Parkman as a Harvard student planned a history of the Iroquois and neighbouring tribes but abandoned it after concluding it would not interest civilized readers (Doughty 1962:92 as Trigger cites). Howard Doughty thinks romantic preconceptions shattered during three weeks among Oglala Sioux (1846); Trigger judges that long before that encounter Parkman already shared countrymen’s negative stereotypes (Wade 1942 as Trigger cites).
- **Canadian transmission:** Parkman impacted Ontario by the 1870s (romantic style; congenial anti-French prejudices; ties with Quebec historians; Faillon used extensively — as Trigger presents). His work fed the post-Confederation “Heroic Age” framing of New France (explorations to royal government 1663) that Trigger’s book reconsiders — see [[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]].
- **Land-right ideology:** Trigger links Parkman’s “tenants of the wilderness” phrase to colonizing powers’ claim that natives do not make adequate use of land as excuse for seizure — continuous with Puritan New Israel / Canaanite models.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Trigger (1985) treats Parkman as the most influential English-language diffuser of a static, inferior, and vanishing Indian, combined with a heroic French colonial narrative. Specific challenges Trigger records:

- **Scholars criticized Parkman’s negative attitudes** (Shulman 1971 as Trigger cites).
- **Francis Jennings (1963)** showed Parkman misrepresented sources to avoid mentioning Indians’ important role concluding a major peace treaty — yet the portrayal seemed fair to a wide audience.
- **Cooper parallel:** Cooper’s *Last of the Mohicans* (one Indian group exterminating another) made essentially the same point as Parkman’s claim that before Europeans the Iroquois had embarked on a conquest war that would probably have obliterated every other eastern North American tribe (Parkman 1927:4 as Trigger cites).
- **Ethnohistory as antidote:** Only ethnohistory, Trigger argues, reassesses the Jesuit *Relations* and related sources for native meanings and impacts; New France’s development is unintelligible without native goals. Popular histories still make 1603–29 the “era of Champlain” and 1634–50 the Jesuit Huron mission, with even Parkman praising “fanatical” Jesuits as civilizers and Indians as passive recipients.
- **Bibliographic pointers Trigger flags (range 6 notes):** Wade (1942), Doughty (1962), Jennings (1963), Shulman (1971) for Parkman’s views of Indians; Levin (1959) on Parkman among historians; Jennings (1975) as fundamental for English colonial Indian policy context.

Trigger’s larger point is structural, not ad hominem: Parkman expressed the scientific and popular consensus of his era with unmatched literary power, so critiquing Parkman in isolation from nineteenth-century anthropology (polygenesis debates, Darwinian racial hierarchies via Lubbock, salvage ethnology’s ethnographic-present trap) is incomplete. See [[ethnographic-present]], [[ethnohistory]], [[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]].

## Historiography

Parkman’s reputation as literary master of colonial North America long outran critical scrutiny of his Indian psychology. Mid-twentieth-century ethnohistory and colonial revisionism (Jennings and others) dismantled source-use and stereotype problems Trigger synthesizes. In Canadian historiography, Parkman remained a gateway text for English-Canadian readers of New France even as French-Canadian professional history debated him; Trigger’s *Natives and Newcomers* (1985) is a sustained effort to replace the Parkman-shaped Heroic Age with an interest-group ethnohistory of French–Iroquoian relations in which native peoples are historical agents.
