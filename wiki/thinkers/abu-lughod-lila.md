# Lila Abu-Lughod

Lila Abu-Lughod (b. 1952) is an American cultural anthropologist known for ethnography of [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] Bedouin society in Egypt and for critical work on gender, representation, and the politics of "saving" Muslim women. Daughter of urban/historical sociologist [[abu-lughod-janet|Janet L. Abu-Lughod]] (not to be conflated). Landmark ethnographies: *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-1986|Veiled Sentiments]]* (1986) — study [[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study|Studies Hub]] — and *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* (1993) — study [[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study|Studies Hub]].

## Overview

Abu-Lughod's early career ethnography, based on ~19 months of fieldwork (October 1978–May 1980) as a "guest and daughter" in a Western Desert Bedouin household, analyzed how [[honor-and-modesty|honor and modesty]] ideology shapes ordinary talk and behavior while a second, culturally valued discourse of vulnerability and attachment is carried by short lyric poems ([[ghinnawa|ghinnāwa]]). Return fieldwork (1986–89, bulk 1987) produced the companion narrative ethnography *Writing Women's Worlds*, which enacts [[writing-against-culture]] and [[tactical-humanism]]. The pair became touchstones for Middle Eastern ethnography, [[feminist-anthropology]], and the anthropology of emotion/sentiment. Later work (not yet fully ingested) includes media and nationhood studies and public critique of the "need to save" Muslim women frame (*Do Muslim Women Need Saving?*, 2013).

## Contributions

- **Dual discourse of sentiment.** Ordinary honor/modesty discourse vs. poetic discourse of loss, love, and vulnerability among Awlad ʿAli (attributed; [[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]).
- **[[hasham|Ḥasham]]** as voluntary deference — "honor of the weak" — linking modesty, sexuality, and hierarchical respect (attributed).
- **Contextual analysis of oral poetry** as social action, not free-standing folklore text; ~450-poem corpus (*Veiled Sentiments*).
- **[[writing-against-culture]]** (1991 essay; 1993 book) — refuse typifying cultural generalization; tell particular, historically timed stories ([[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]).
- **[[tactical-humanism]]** — limited use of humanist narrative against othering, without naive universalism (attributed, 1993).
- **Halfie / positionality ethnography** — Arab-American, Muslim-recognized, daughter role structuring access; intermediate reflexivity (neither pure solipsism nor omniscient objectivity).
- **Ethnographic reserve** (2016 afterword) — adapting Audra Simpson's ethnographic refusal to reluctance to disclose more when hosts become readers and tapes remain socially live.
- Later (not fully ingested): critique of civilizational "saving" narratives.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Credits **Paul Riesman** with introducing her to anthropology (dedication). Engages [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], Catherine Lutz, [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]], Mediterranean honor literature (Pitt-Rivers et al.), and critiques of mask/real dualism (vs. Goffman-style readings). Field support: Ahmed Abou-Zeid (Alexandria), Hind Khattab (AUC). Mother: [[abu-lughod-janet|Janet Abu-Lughod]]. Academic mentors/students to be filled as sources accumulate.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reception as exemplary feminist ethnography of Middle Eastern gender. Arabic-magazine criticism focused partly on political portrayal (tax avoidance, firearms, distance from state law — which the Haj reportedly affirmed). Later family members objected to published hardship details as reputation risk for a more schooled generation. 2016 afterword repositions the project under collapsing distance (phones, Internet, Arabic translation, university library copies). Specific secondary critiques expand as literature is ingested.
