# Robert L. Carneiro

## Overview

[[carneiro-robert-l|Robert Leonard Carneiro]] (b. 1927) is an American cultural anthropologist long associated with the American Museum of Natural History and a leading late-twentieth-century defender and historian of cultural evolutionism. He is best known for [[circumscription|circumscription theory]] of state origins (population pressure + environmental/social circumscription + warfare → political consolidation) and for *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology: A Critical History]]* (2003), a systematic rehabilitation of classical and neo-evolutionist programs against Boasian and functionalist critiques. Intellectual lineage runs through [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]] at Michigan.

## Contributions

1. **[[circumscription|Circumscription theory]]** (esp. 1970 *Science* article; restated in 2003): population pressure + environmental/social circumscription + warfare → political consolidation for pristine chiefdoms/states — conflict side of [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. Ranks population pressure first among determinants while rejecting equal “democracy of causes.”
2. **Critical history of cultural evolutionism** (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*, 2003): quote-heavy recovery of Spencer, Tylor, and Morgan against Boasian caricature; maps anti-evolution ascendancy and neo-evolutionist revival (White, Steward, Childe, Sahlins–Service, processual archaeology); synthesizes uni- and multilinear as complementary; defends cultural laws and the comparative method.
3. **Spencerian restriction of “evolution”** in the [[general-and-specific-evolution]] debate: reserve the term for increasing complexity; treat decline as devolution, not evolution (vs Sahlins/White’s broader Darwinian usage).
4. **Political unilinearity thesis:** common autochthonous line band → autonomous village → chiefdom → state; no known village→state skip; unilinear ≠ rectilinear (“saw-toothed” history).
5. **Scale analysis / objective complexity rating:** Guttman-style scalograms and trait-order regularities as empirical checks on evolutionary sequences (discussed at length in the 2003 book).
6. **South American ethnology** (Amazonia fieldwork; Kuikuru and related) and chiefdom comparative work — densified as primary ethnographies are ingested.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[white-leslie-a|Leslie A. White]] (Michigan). Carneiro 2003 preface: White’s influence “readily apparent”; emulates White by “never paraphrasing when I could quote,” against Boas-school practice of imputing errors without quotation.
- **Peers/allies (attributed network):** [[service-elman-r|Elman Service]], [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (early general/specific program), [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]; Joyce Marcus read every chapter of the 2003 book in draft.
- **Debates with:** [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]] (cultural materialism vs determinant pluralism — Carneiro reports the Service–Harris *Natural History* / *SWJA* exchange); integrative state theorists; post-processual anti-evolutionism.
- **reacted_against:** Boasian [[historical-particularism]]; functionalist ban on conjectural history; caricature unilinearity charges; Wittfogel hydraulic prime-mover (re-read as false correlation for circumscription).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Circumscription theory is a standing target: Service-style integrative theories contest warfare-as-engine accounts; multi-factor processual models (Flannery and others) reject single prime-movers; archaeologists contest how often pristine states meet circumscription conditions; ideology/agency programs challenge material-determinant ranking. The 2003 history is **partisan for evolutionism** — strongest as recovery of classical quotations and as a map of the neo-evolutionist revival, not as a neutral consensus handbook. White rejected the label “neo-evolutionism” (“neo-whiskey”); Carneiro’s generation often avoided it while practicing evolutionary anthropology.

## Historiography

Primary programmatic statements: "A Theory of the Origin of the State" (*Science*, 1970); *The Muse of History and the Science of Culture* (2000); *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]* (2003). The 2003 volume grew from a "Classical Evolutionism" chapter for Raoul Naroll's largely stillborn *Main Currents in Cultural Anthropology* (Appleton-Century-Crofts; poorly distributed after corporate takeover — “fell dead off the press”); Westview required a twentieth-century section that outgrew the classical core. Book thesis (Carneiro): evolutionism founded the discipline → 50+ years of hostility → ~1959 revival → later erosion to “islands”; still “too valid and fruitful” to abandon; core task = band→state transformation and its causes.
