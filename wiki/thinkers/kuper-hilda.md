# Hilda Kuper

South African–born anthropologist (1911–1992). Contributed the Swazi kinship essay to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Broader Swazi monographs (*An African Aristocracy*, 1947; marriage ritual in *Africa* 1945) are cited in the chapter but **not yet fully ingested**.

## Overview

In ASKAM Kuper provides the volume’s principal **Nguni father-right** case ([[swazi]]): centralized kingship subordinating clans; *ukulobola* / *emabeka* cattle as legal marriage and rights over children; social vs physical paternity; clan exogamy with royal exceptions; domestic kinship nested under national “fictional” kinship through king and queen mother. Radcliffe-Brown’s Introduction groups Swazi with Zulu as father-right—“Cattle beget children” / “The children are where the cattle are not”—while infancy still belongs to the mother.

## Contributions

### Swazi kinship (ASKAM chapter, attributed findings)

- **Political–kinship weld:** Swazi as Nguni–Sotho amalgamation under conquering Nguni aristocracy (late 18th c.); by military conquest allegiance to the king overrode clan loyalty; fictional national kinship through royal family (king and mother as symbolic parents).
- **Clan and local unit:** Patrilineal clan with *isidongo* (clan name); 70+ clan names; *isinanatelo* marks related sub-clans; fission most frequent in royal Nkosi—enables king to marry women otherwise prohibited and reaffirm hold through polygynous ties. *Isifunza* local/political unit diverged from scattered *isiBongo*. No distinctive interlinked lineage structure; *lusendvo* as effective family council ~lineage; commoner depth rarely >5 generations.
- **Exogamy:** Extends to clan; not enforced for the king. Unlike other Nguni, man might marry into clans of mother, paternal grandmother, and maternal grandmother, though certain relatives forbidden.
- **Marriage and paternity:** Legal marriage evidenced by acceptance of cattle *emabeka* under *ukulobola*—specifically connected with woman’s fertility and rights over children; if sterile, substitute “to bear for her.” All children (with first-daughter exception when chief/king contributed cattle) regarded as husband’s even if he was not genitor. Markedly: man might *lobola* children alone, leaving woman free to marry elsewhere. Genitor–pater tension (“call of blood”). Ghost marriage; co-wife substitution “to put into the womb of another.”
- **No adoption of non-related children** (war orphans as *tigcili* nearest practice).
- Essay concentrates on **domestic** kinship; national aspect deferred to *An African Aristocracy* (1947).

Exact fieldwork years **not stated** in the ASKAM essay; documentation moment date-stamped as Kuper’s Swazi ethnography ~1940s–1950 (king Sobhuza II; colonial labour migration and Commissioner’s court present).
