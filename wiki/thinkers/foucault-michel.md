# Michel Foucault

French philosopher and historian of systems of thought (1926–1984); one of the most consequential figures of the Critical Turn (1968–1990) across the social sciences and humanities. In this vault the primary ingested base is *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality, Volume I: An Introduction]]* (French *La Volonté de savoir*, 1976; Eng. trans. 1978) — the founding statement of his [[analytics-of-power|analytics of power]], his critique of [[the-repressive-hypothesis|the repressive hypothesis]], and the concepts [[power-knowledge]], [[biopower]], and [[deployment-of-sexuality|the deployment of sexuality]]. His earlier works (*Madness and Civilization*, *The Order of Things*, *The Archaeology of Knowledge*, *Discipline and Punish*) are referenced from this volume's own cross-references and from secondary mentions only, not yet ingested as primary sources.

## Overview

Foucault reframes the study of modern society around the mutual production of **knowledge** and **power**. Against both liberal and Marxist models that treat power as fundamentally repressive — a force that says "no," prohibits, and censors — he argues (in attributed voice throughout, since these are his theses) that modern power is **productive**: it incites discourse, produces categories of person, multiplies pleasures, and constitutes subjects. His method, [[genealogy]], traces how objects of knowledge (madness, the clinic, the delinquent, "sexuality") were historically fabricated by [[power-knowledge|power-knowledge]] apparatuses rather than progressively discovered. *The History of Sexuality* applies this to sex: the modern West, Foucault argues, did not silence sex but subjected it to an "incitement to discourse" and built a [[scientia-sexualis|science of sexuality]] on the ritual of [[confession]], culminating in [[biopower]] — the administration of life at the level of both the individual body and the population.

## Contributions

- **[[power-knowledge]]** — the thesis that the production of truth is "thoroughly imbued with relations of power," and that power and knowledge directly imply one another; "it is in discourse that power and knowledge are joined together" (attributed).
- **[[analytics-of-power]]** — his relational, non-juridical account of power: power as exercised not possessed, immanent and productive, coming "from below," "intentional and nonsubjective"; "power is everywhere ... because it comes from everywhere" (attributed).
- **[[the-repressive-hypothesis]]** — his named target: the story that modern (Victorian, bourgeois) society repressed sex. Foucault argues instead for a "discursive explosion" and multiplication of sexualities (attributed).
- **[[biopower]]** and **biopolitics** — the modern transformation of sovereign power ("take life or let live") into a power to "foster life," with two poles: an anatomo-politics of the body and a biopolitics of the population (attributed).
- **[[deployment-of-sexuality]]** — sexuality as a historically produced *dispositif*, distinct from the older "deployment of alliance" (kinship, marriage, law); the four strategic figures (the hysterical woman, the masturbating child, the Malthusian couple, the perverse adult) (attributed).
- **[[scientia-sexualis]]** vs. *ars erotica*, and **[[confession]]** as the West's central truth-technique (attributed).
- **[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]]** and **[[genealogy]]** as method — the "history of the present" written as a history of discourses and their tactical polyvalence.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The "no exit" / agency problem** (attributed to critics): if power is everywhere and even resistance is internal to it, critics ask whether Foucault leaves room for emancipatory politics; Foucault himself anticipates the charge that retaining "there is no escaping from power" smuggles the repressive model back in.
- **Feminist reception** (attributed): the "hysterization of women's bodies" and the account of the body have been both drawn on and criticized by feminist scholars (the body as passively produced; the absence of a standpoint for critique).
- **Historical selectivity** (attributed): as [[genealogy|genealogy]] by design, the work reads its archive tactically; specific historical assertions (dating "the homosexual" to Westphal's 1870 article, the periodization of the confession) are Foucault's readings, contested by social historians. Recorded here as his claims, not as settled history.

## Reception in adjacent fields

[[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]] names Foucault as major inspiration for *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995), applying discourse/power analytics to postwar development as a three-axis discursive formation ([[development-discourse]], [[post-development]]).

## Historiography

Foucault's reputation has moved from *enfant terrible* of the 1960s French scene to canonical status across sociology, history, anthropology, and cultural studies; the biographies (Eribon, Macey, Miller) and the posthumous publication of his Collège de France lectures (which develop biopower, governmentality, and disciplinary power at length) reshaped the reading of the single volume ingested here. Those lecture courses and the later volumes of *The History of Sexuality* (1984, on Greco-Roman antiquity) are not yet ingested; treat this page as anchored to Volume I only.
