# Michel Foucault

French philosopher and historian of systems of thought (1926–1984); one of the most consequential figures of the Critical Turn (1968–1990) across the social sciences and humanities. In this vault the ingested primary base is now three works spanning two phases of his oeuvre. The mid-1970s **power** phase: *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* (French *Surveiller et punir*, 1975; Eng. 1977) — genealogy of the modern [[institutions/prison|prison]] and of [[disciplinary-power|disciplinary power]] — and *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality, Volume I]]* (1976/1978) — critique of [[the-repressive-hypothesis|the repressive hypothesis]], [[biopower]], and the programmatic [[analytics-of-power]]. The early-1980s **ethics / subject** phase: *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (History of Sexuality Vol. II, French *L'Usage des plaisirs*, 1984; Eng. 1985) — the turn to classical Greek [[arts-of-existence|arts of existence]] and to [[ethics-foucauldian|ethics as the relation of the self to itself]] — and *[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, French *Le Souci de soi*, 1984; Eng. 1986) — the same problematic extended into the Roman imperial period, organized by the [[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]] and the precept of [[care-of-the-self|epimeleia heautou]]. Foucault himself periodizes his work into three phases — games of truth in **knowledge**, in **power** relations, and in the **relation of self to self** — and Vols. II–III constitute the third. The *History of Sexuality* trilogy (Vols. I–III) is now ingested complete; the projected fourth volume on the Christian flesh (*Les Aveux de la chair*) was unfinished at his death. Earlier works (*Madness and Civilization*, *The Order of Things*, *The Archaeology of Knowledge*) and the Collège de France lectures remain referenced but uningested. ## Overview

Foucault reframes the study of modern society around the mutual production of **knowledge** and **power**. Against both liberal and Marxist models that treat power as fundamentally repressive — a force that says "no," prohibits, and censors — he argues (in attributed voice throughout) that modern power is **productive**: it trains bodies, produces categories of person, incites discourse, multiplies pleasures, and constitutes subjects. His method, [[genealogy]], traces how objects of knowledge (madness, the clinic, the delinquent, "sexuality") were historically fabricated by [[power-knowledge|power-knowledge]] apparatuses rather than progressively discovered.

*Discipline and Punish* applies this to penality: the shift from public torture to the prison is not simple humanization but a redistribution of the power to punish through [[disciplinary-power|discipline]], [[panopticism]], and a [[carceral-archipelago|carceral continuum]] that produces the delinquent. *The History of Sexuality* Vol. I applies it to sex: the modern West, Foucault argues, did not silence sex but subjected it to an "incitement to discourse" and built a [[scientia-sexualis|science of sexuality]] on [[confession]], culminating in [[biopower]] — administration of life at the level of both the individual body (anatomo-politics, continuous with D&P's disciplines) and the population.

## Contributions

- **[[disciplinary-power]]**, **[[docile-bodies]]**, **[[panopticism]]**, **[[carceral-archipelago]]**, **[[examination-foucauldian|examination]]**, **[[normalizing-judgement]]**, **[[hierarchical-observation]]**, **[[microphysics-of-power]]** — the technical architecture of modern training and surveillance from *Discipline and Punish* (attributed).
- **[[power-knowledge]]** — production of truth thoroughly imbued with relations of power; power and knowledge directly imply one another (attributed; both D&P and HoS).
- **[[analytics-of-power]]** — relational, non-juridical account of power: exercised not possessed, immanent and productive, coming "from below," "intentional and nonsubjective" (attributed; programmatic in HoS; operationalized in D&P).
- **[[the-repressive-hypothesis]]** — named target in HoS: the story that modern society repressed sex (attributed).
- **[[biopower]]** and **biopolitics** — from sovereign "take life" to power to "foster life"; anatomo-politics of the body + biopolitics of the population (attributed; HoS Part Five, pairing with D&P's disciplines).
- **[[deployment-of-sexuality]]**, **[[scientia-sexualis]]**, **[[confession]]** — HoS concepts (attributed).
- **[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]]** and **[[genealogy]]** as method — "history of the present" (attributed).
- **[[problematization]]**, **[[ethics-foucauldian|moral subjectivation]]** (the four aspects: ethical substance, mode of subjection, self-forming activity, telos; code- vs. ethics-oriented moralities), **[[arts-of-existence]]** ("techniques of the self"), and the Greek cluster **[[aphrodisia]]** / **[[chresis]]** / **[[enkrateia]]** / **[[dietetics]]** — the apparatus of the late ethical turn from *The Use of Pleasure* (attributed). Where the mid-1970s work analyzed the subject as *produced by* power-knowledge, the late work analyzes how the subject *constitutes itself* — the two faces of one account.
- **[[care-of-the-self]]** (*epimeleia heautou*) and the **[[cultivation-of-the-self]]** — from *The Care of the Self* (Vol. III): the Roman imperial intensification of the self-relation, the "conjugalization of the aphrodisia," and the Conclusion's thesis that pagan austerity was a **continuity of themes** with, but a **discontinuity of subjectivation** from, Christian ethics (attributed).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The "no exit" / agency problem** (attributed to critics): if power is everywhere and even resistance is internal to it, critics ask whether Foucault leaves room for emancipatory politics; Foucault himself anticipates the charge that retaining "there is no escaping from power" smuggles the repressive model back in.
- **Feminist reception** (attributed): the "hysterization of women's bodies" and the account of the body have been both drawn on and criticized by feminist scholars (the body as passively produced; the absence of a standpoint for critique).
- **Historical selectivity** (attributed): as [[genealogy|genealogy]] by design, the work reads its archive tactically; specific historical assertions (dating "the homosexual" to Westphal's 1870 article, the periodization of the confession) are Foucault's readings, contested by social historians. Recorded here as his claims, not as settled history.

- **Asad's monastic corrective** (attributed): [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] (*[[asad-genealogies-of-religion-1993|Genealogies of Religion]]*, 1993, chs. 3–4) builds on Foucault's Cassian analysis and the D&P framework but corrects it on three counts: Foucault's assimilation of judicial torture, duel, and ordeal to one category of "ritual" obscures the differences that explain what each enables; his "combat of chastity" reading underweights humility as a *social* achievement — the Rule of St. Benedict's steps of humility "are precisely enmeshed in social relationships"; and the monastery had "no single point of surveillance" — a network of mutual observation, not a panopticon. Against the late Foucault's "renunciation of the self," Asad argues the rhetoric of renunciation constructs a self-policing function rather than rejecting a presocialized real self. See [[discipline]].

## Reception in adjacent fields

[[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]] names Foucault as major inspiration for *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995), applying discourse/power analytics to postwar development as a three-axis discursive formation ([[development-discourse]], [[post-development]]).

[[butler-judith|Judith Butler]] built *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]* (1990) on Foucault's [[genealogy]] and his critique of the repressive hypothesis, extending both to the incest taboo and to the category of "sex" (see [[gender-performativity]], [[heterosexual-matrix]]). Butler also charged Foucault with contradicting himself: his introduction to the memoirs of the intersex diarist Herculine Barbin, she argued, "romanticize[s] h/er world of pleasures as the 'happy limbo of a non-identity'," positing a pre-legal libidinal innocence that his own power-is-coextensive-with-sexuality position forecloses — "a radical misreading of the way in which those pleasures are always already embedded in the… law" (attributed to Butler).

## Historiography

Foucault's reputation has moved from *enfant terrible* of the 1960s French scene to canonical status across sociology, history, anthropology, criminology, and cultural studies; the biographies (Eribon, Macey, Miller) and the posthumous Collège de France lectures (biopower, governmentality, punitive society) reshaped readings of both mid-1970s books. This page is now anchored to **four** primary ingests (D&P + HoS Vols. I–III), spanning the power and the ethics phases; the *History of Sexuality* trilogy is ingested complete. The earlier archaeological works and the lecture courses remain uningested. A Thinkers Hub biography is warranted and grows more so with the full trilogy now in the vault; it remains an open densification task (flagged, not claimed) — deferred consistent with building hub depth across a fuller corpus (adding the earlier works and lectures) rather than committing it now.
