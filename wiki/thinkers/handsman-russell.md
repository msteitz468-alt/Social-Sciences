# Russell G. Handsman

Archaeologist; *Engendering Archaeology* chapter on **[[lepenski-vir|Lepenski Vir]]** examines gender relations and power in how archaeological art and settlement narratives are written and exhibited.

## Overview

Handsman argues that the absence of prehistoric women from archaeological “shelves” reflects relations of writing and power that enforce silences — not neutral scientific limits. Archaeology is not sexless: discrimination against women in the profession (Gero; Kramer & Stark) and sexist interpretive biases (Conkey & Spector) are documented. Using the 1986 Southampton exhibit “The Art of Lepenski Vir” (91 sculptures; World Archaeological Congress context) and Srejović’s publications, he argues the exhibit answers “whose art?” as **Males’ Art**: men cast as creative artists and leaders (architect-magician; sculpture names Adam, Sorcerer, Old Chief, Founder of the Tribe), while women appear only as Lady/Mermaid/First Mother/Vulva — art *about* women under a male gaze that cannot empower them as makers. Drawing on Foucault and Said, he treats archaeological discourse as **productive** of gender inequality, not a passive mirror of the past.

## Contributions

- Gender/power critique of Lepenski Vir interpretation and exhibit practice (1991).
- Linking professional sociopolitics to prehistoric narrative form.
