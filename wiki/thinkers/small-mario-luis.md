# Mario Luis Small

American sociologist of urban inequality, social networks, and multi-method research. Primary ingested work: [[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|*Unanticipated Gains: Origins of Network Inequality in Everyday Life*]] (Oxford, 2009), which develops an **[[organizational-embeddedness|organizational-embeddedness]]** account of [[network-inequality|network inequality]] through a multi-method study of mothers in New York City [[childcare-center|childcare centers]] ([[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]).

## Overview

Small argues that personal networks — and the resources available through them — are shaped by the institutional rules, norms, and practices of the everyday organizations in which people routinely participate. Organizations act as intentional and unintentional [[organizational-broker|brokers]] of social and organizational ties; differences across organizations help explain inequality in well-being. Gains are often **unanticipated** relative to the motives that bring people into organizations (e.g. securing childcare). *(Attributed — Small 2009.)*

Institutional context for the book (acknowledgments): research and writing support from Princeton and the University of Chicago; leaves at Columbia and NYU; NICHD funding (Paxson PI) and Fragile Families collaboration (McLanahan); research team including Jacobs and Massengill (*Social Forces* 2008 paper feeding Ch. 7).

## Contributions

- **[[organizational-embeddedness]]** — social capital as responding to organizational conditions; actors as organizationally embedded.
- **[[organizational-broker]]** — organization-level brokerage of person–person and person–organization resource access; mechanisms of validation, storage, referral, collaboration.
- **Empirical program** on childcare centers: Fragile Families (~3,500 urban mothers analyses), NYC centers survey (n=293, 2004), 23 center cases, 67 parent interviews — see [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]].
- **Neighborhood effects engagement** — centers in poor neighborhoods better organizationally connected; buffering thesis; organizational isolate ([[neighborhood-effects-debate]]).
- **Critique of investment models** of tie formation (Lin and instrumental readings of Bourdieu); engagement with [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman]] on trust/obligations and with [[strength-of-weak-ties|Granovetter]] on weak/strong ties via compartmental intimacy and support among nonintimates.

## Intellectual Genealogy

*(As engaged in *Unanticipated Gains*, not a full biography.)* Dialogue partners include Bourdieu, Coleman, Lin, Burt, Granovetter, Putnam, Wilson, Sampson (collective efficacy), Feld (foci of activity), DiMaggio & Powell (institutional isomorphism), and Chicago School / political-economy urban traditions. Appendix A traces the research process from Boston ethnography hunch through pilot to multi-method design.
