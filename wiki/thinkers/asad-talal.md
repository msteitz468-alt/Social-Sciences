# Talal Asad

Anthropologist (b. 1932), editor of the Critical-Turn collection *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (1973) and author of its Introduction and of "Two European Images of Non-European Rule." Contributor to *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986) with "The Concept of [[cultural-translation|Cultural Translation]] in British Social Anthropology." Later major works on religion and secularism (*Genealogies of Religion*, *Formations of the Secular*) are **not yet ingested**.

## Overview

Asad's 1973 intervention made the anthropology–colonialism problem an explicit multi-essay object for British social anthropology rather than a marginal New Left complaint. He diagnoses **ideological disintegration** of classical functional anthropology after decolonisation while the profession's **organisational base expands** — and theorises that the colonial (then postcolonial) world **determines how anthropology apprehends** its objects.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Asad 1973.)*

### Introduction — crisis, continuity, determination

- **Professional timeline:** British functional anthropology emerges after WWI via [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]; assured university status only after WWII; prestige by ~1961 (Macrae; Runciman) then internal misgivings (Leach 1961; Worsley "End of Anthropology?"; Needham 1970; Ardener 1971).
- **Crisis without organisational collapse:** ASA flourishes, textbooks multiply, school options appear — leaders talk "specialisation" and enduring functionalist viability; Asad reads this as continuity myth over **disintegration of the Old Anthropology**.
- **Object definition:** once unambiguously "primitive" / "simple" / "preliterate" societies as wholes ([[nadel-s-f|Nadel]]; Evans-Pritchard 1951); by ~1973 style-coherence gone — simple and complex, mixed methods, cognate-discipline affiliations.
- **Determination thesis:** "anthropology does not merely apprehend the world in which it is located… the world also determines how anthropology will apprehend it" — decolonisation, nationalist recovery of indigenous history, American anti-functionalist sociology, collapse of ethnographic holism into micro-sociology reshape object and ideology; ASA (1946–) and funding networks **save** organisational identity without restoring doctrine.
- **Power encounter:** anthropology rooted in unequal West–Third World encounter that made the object "accessible and safe," enabled one-sided intimacy, and structured theoretical choice (politics most affected, cosmology least); neutrality claims merge with status-quo; expertise that stops at the colonial system is **malformed**.
- **Against pure handmaiden *and* pure innocence:** wild handmaiden claims and "professional standards" dismissals are both naive; structure of bourgeois knowledge and patronage matters without reducing every ethnographer to a clerk of empire. ASA had never made colonialism a conference topic in over a quarter-century.
- **Seminar origin:** Hull, September 1972 (Ian Cunnison); all but Owen's paper first presented there; introduction dated March 1973.

### "Two European Images of Non-European Rule"

- Anthropology among bourgeois colonial knowledges (with orientalism, indology, sinology).
- **Functionalist image of African rule:** emphasis on **consent** and representative tribal authority (e.g. Fortes & Evans-Pritchard; Lloyd) — facilitated by *Pax Britannica* fieldwork under routine colonialism that underwrote "traditional" continuity.
- **Orientalist image of Islamic rule:** emphasis on **repression/despotism**, ulama religious integration vs secular political fragmentation (Gibb, von Grunebaum, Snouck Hurgronje) — rooted in precolonial European experience of Islam, essentialist synchrony.
- Shared theoretical question ("what holds society together?") with divergent selections/omissions; both images can **reassure colonial ruling classes** (unwittingly). Africanist texts omit European coercive power; orientalist texts omit accommodation, populist revolt, class/commerce.

### Writing Culture — cultural translation (1986)

*(Attributed to Asad in Clifford & Marcus eds., Writing Culture.)*

- Maps rise of “translation of cultures” metaphor in British social anthropology (Lienhardt, Beattie, Leach, Gluckman); notes Malinowski did *not* use the frame.
- Extended critique of Gellner’s “Concepts and Society” (excessive charity; sentence-matching; Nuer twin/bird; Leach Kachin; “bobility”; Berber *baraka*).
- Contesting a culture ≠ translating it; responsible criticism requires contestable addressee.
- **Inequality of languages** (Benjamin/Pannwitz): strong languages shape weak ones in translation (Arabic example).
- Individual experiments in ethnographic writing cannot overcome “stiffness” produced by power — brake on pure textual experiment within [[writing-culture]].
- Social authority of ethnography; reading the “implicit” as analyst power (Douglas, Pocock).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Interlocutors 1973:** Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown, Evans-Pritchard, Nadel, Firth, Gluckman, Leach, Fortes; New Left and French critique (Banaji, Goddard, Leclerc, Copans); Hull seminar contributors including [[james-wendy|Wendy James]].
- **Writing Culture network (1986):** SAR seminar systematic-critic roster with Clifford, Marcus, Pratt, Tyler; internal pole against free textual experiment; concept page [[cultural-translation]].
