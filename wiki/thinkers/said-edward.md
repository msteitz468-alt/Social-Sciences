# Edward W. Said

Edward Wadie Said (1935–2003) was a Palestinian-American literary critic and public
intellectual, University Professor of English and Comparative Literature at Columbia. His
*[[said-orientalism-1978|Orientalism]]* (1978) founded postcolonial studies as a field
and reshaped how anthropology, area studies, history, and literary criticism understand
the relation between knowledge and imperial power.

## Overview

Said built a **worldly**, secular-humanist criticism that insisted texts are embedded in
history and power ("the world, the text, and the critic"). Drawing on Michel
[[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s concept of [[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] and Antonio
[[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s [[hegemony]], he analyzed the Western representation of the
non-Western world as a form of domination — a project continued in *Culture and
Imperialism* (1993). He was also a prominent advocate for Palestinian rights.

## Contributions

- **[[orientalism]]** — the master concept: Western representation of "the Orient" as a
  discourse of power/knowledge (attributed to Said 1978).
- **[[imaginative-geography]]** — the arbitrary, self-serving division of space into
  "our" familiar land and "their" alien land.
- **[[othering]]** — constituting the European self through its contrast with an
  essentialized Oriental Other.
- The distinction between **latent** Orientalism (a stable unconscious structure of
  dogma) and **manifest** Orientalism (its shifting stated content).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Said drew on [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] (discourse, power/knowledge) and
[[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]] (hegemony, the "inventory of traces"), against a backdrop of
Auerbach's philology and Vichian humanism. His work seeded a generation of postcolonial
theorists (Homi Bhabha, [[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty|Gayatri Spivak]] among the "holy trinity" of the field). Spivak's "[[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study|Can the Subaltern Speak?]]" shifts from Orientalist discourse production toward the limits of recovering the gendered [[subaltern]] — complementary, not identical, to Said's project. See [[postcolonialism]], [[can-the-subaltern-speak-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

[[clifford-james|James Clifford]]'s review essay "On Orientalism" (1980; repr. in
[[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]], 1988) is the
canonical anthropological critique: Said's three definitions are inconsistent, his
Foucauldian method and residual humanism undercut each other, his genealogy omits the
Maghreb and German traditions, and the Massignon case shows the humanist rescue
escaping his own critique — yet Clifford credits the book with making "the Occident"
problematic and with unsettling the anthropological culture concept. Clifford also
reads Said biographically-sympathetically as exemplary of a "generalized condition of
homelessness," identity negotiated rather than given (the Conrad parallel). See
[[orientalism]] for the full recorded critique.

Said's central critic was the Orientalist historian Bernard Lewis, who charged him with
factual errors, a polemically selective canon, and hostility to scholarship itself; Said
replied in the 1994 Afterword. See [[said-lewis-orientalism-debate]]. Other critics
(Aijaz Ahmad, Sadik Jalal al-'Azm, Robert Irwin) faulted the book's internal tension
between Foucauldian anti-humanism and Said's own humanism, its essentializing of "the
West," its thin treatment of German Orientalism, and its silence on the Orient's own
agency. The book nonetheless remains a foundational reference.

## Historiography

*Orientalism* has generated an enormous secondary literature across disciplines and
several editions with new prefaces (1994 Afterword, 2003 Preface) in which Said addressed
his reception. His memoir *Out of Place* (1999) and numerous biographies document the
career. In development studies, [[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]] explicitly models
[[development-discourse]] on Said's Orientalism in *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995).
