# Leonard Dinnerstein

## Overview

American historian (Columbia PhD; long career at the University of Arizona) of ethnicity, immigration, and American Jewish history. He entered the field, as he recounts, via the Civil Rights–era turn to minority histories: *The Leo Frank Case* (1968) became the standard account of the 1913–1915 Frank affair, followed by work on southern Jewry, American ethnic groups, and the American reception of Holocaust survivors. *[[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-1994|Antisemitism in America]]* (1994) was the first comprehensive scholarly survey of its subject — see [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-study]].

## Contributions

His synthesis established the "Christian heritage" account of American [[antisemitism]] — theological inheritance, weakened in America's non-established religious order, cresting 1919–1945 and declining after — against purely economic, nativist, or Populist-origin accounts (see [[populism-antisemitism-debate]]). He treated the Leo Frank case as the paradigmatic episode of southern antisemitism and documented the [[blood-libel]]'s American appearances.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Built on and against Korn, Handlin, Hofstadter, and Higham (1950s), the Populism revisionists of the 1960s, and Dobkowski's *The Tarnished Dream* (1978), whose maximalist reading of Gilded Age antisemitism he largely endorsed.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

His Chapter 10 claims about rising African-American antisemitism are his most contested legacy (positions recorded at [[black-jewish-relations]]). His confident single-author verdicts (Frank's innocence; Adorno's *Authoritarian Personality* conclusion as "erroneous") are influential but are historian's judgments, not consensus findings.
