## Overview

Gregory L. Possehl was an American archaeologist specializing in the Indus (Harappan)
Civilization, best known for *The Indus Civilization: A Contemporary Perspective*
(2002), a thematic synthesis of the field organized around the idea that the Indus
represents "archaic sociocultural complexity... without the state." Biographical
detail (birth/death dates, formal training) is not given in the source ingested here
and is left `[[unknown]]` pending a source that supplies it.

## Contributions

Possehl coined the **"[[indus-age|Indus Age]]"** periodization — his own analytic
term for the roughly 6,000–7,000-year span running from the beginnings of agriculture
and pastoralism in South Asia (~9,000–10,000 years ago) through the Indus Civilization
proper to the widespread adoption of iron technology (~1000 BCE), which he treats as
coinciding with the emergence of Vedic ideology. This periodization structures the
whole of his 2002 synthesis.

He proposed a subregional geographic scheme he termed **"Domains"** for the Mature
Harappan phase, explicitly framed as a challenge to the earlier Wheeler/Piggott
"sameness"/"monolith" reading of Indus civilizational uniformity (see
[[indus-polity-debate]]). Possehl notes that G. Smith's independent statistical
(gravity-model and cluster-analysis) study of Mature Harappan sites over 10 ha
produced units closely matching his Domains — which Possehl treats as independent
corroboration of the scheme — and that Smith termed the resulting units "Harappan
polities." Possehl is also credited with the term "Sorath Harappan" for the regional
Gujarat/Saurashtra Harappan variant.

Possehl's central theoretical position, argued at length in his 2002 synthesis, is
that the Indus Civilization exhibits complexity **without a state**: he lays out
diagnostic criteria for the "archaic state" (kingship, bureaucracy, class hierarchy,
a monopoly on force, state religion) and argues the Indus archaeological record shows
none of them clearly — no unambiguous kingship in sculpture or palatial architecture,
no visible state bureaucracy, no state religion or monumental temple architecture —
calling the resulting polity "faceless" and without clear ethnographic or
archaeological parallel. This is Possehl's own theoretical position, not an
established consensus, and is recorded in `[[indus-polity-debate]]` as a position
rather than a wiki-voice fact.

He also took explicit stances rejecting two older interpretive traditions: he argued
against the "higher rainfall"/wetter-climate hypothesis for the Mature Harappan
period (endorsed earlier by [[marshall-john]], [[wheeler-mortimer]], and
[[childe-v-gordon]]), holding instead that regional lake salinity used as evidence
for Bronze Age aridification is better explained by tectonically controlled
subsurface drainage than by rainfall change; and he judged that the Wheeler/Piggott
"citadel-as-fortress"/"priest-king" reading of the Harappa and [[mohenjo-daro]]
citadels "has been dropped by most modern interpreters" by the time of his writing
(2002).

Possehl conducted his own fieldwork at [[rojdi]] and in the Ghelo and Kalubhar
valleys of Gujarat, work that fed directly into his Domains/Sorath Harappan regional
scheme.

## Historiography

Possehl's 2002 synthesis explicitly positions itself against two earlier interpretive
paradigms in Indus studies — the Marshall/Mackay/Childe reading of the Indus as
peaceful and mercantile, and the Wheeler/Piggott reading of Harappan cities as
theocratic "priest-king" states modeled on Sumer (see [[indus-polity-debate]] and
[[wheeler-mortimer]]) — and offers his "complexity without the state" and "Domains"
frameworks as his own alternative synthesis of the accumulated evidence as of 2002.

## Wright, *The Ancient Indus* (2010)

Wright 2010 characterizes several further Possehl positions, commented on by Wright:
his **"gateway communities"** concept (Possehl 1980) for settlements sited to
facilitate transport at coastlines, mountain passes, and cultural junctures; his
figure (Possehl 1990) of an increase from 463 recorded sites in the Pre-urban period
to 976 in the Urban period; and his **"Middle Asian Interaction Sphere"** framework
(Possehl 2002), which groups non-Indus complex polities of central/southern Iran,
southern Afghanistan, and Central Asia as a loosely contemporary regional system
(ca. 2500–1800 BCE). Wright also records a position dispute: Possehl (2003) reads
the Kulli material-culture zone of Las Bela as a local Indus variant, against
Wright's own reading of Kulli as a potentially separate, politically independent
culture — recorded as a live disagreement, not adjudicated here.
