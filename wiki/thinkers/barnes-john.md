# John A. Barnes

Rhodes-Livingstone Institute and [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] anthropologist, later credited with introducing "social network" as an analytic term. Enters the wiki as co-author, with [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] and [[mitchell-j-clyde|J. Clyde Mitchell]], of "The Village Headman in British Central Africa" (1949).

## Overview

Rhodes-Livingstone Institute researcher (Fort Jameson Ngoni, 1940s), later professor at Sydney, Canberra, and Cambridge; documented here through Gluckman's *Order and Rebellion* (1963).

## Contributions

- Co-author of the 1949 village-headman analysis (see [[intercalary-role]]).
- **Fort Jameson Ngoni village headmanship (Essay V, section 3, published 1949).** Studying the Fort Jameson Ngoni (Eastern Province, Northern Rhodesia, under Chief Mpezeni, roughly 60,000 people), Barnes described patrilineal succession to commoner village headmanship (eldest son by the chief wife, with regency and bypass provisions) and gave a detailed eyewitness account, dated **July 1947** at the village of Tsong'oto, of a ceremony installing a headman's deputy — used to illustrate the ritualized expression of structural hostility toward office-holders via the idiom **"You have killed me"** for accession to headmanship or deputyship, an idiom Gluckman echoes for the Lozi Paramount Chief in his introduction to the same essay.
- ***Politics in a Changing Society*** (1954) and **"Seven Types of Segmentation"** (*Rhodes-Livingstone Journal* 17, 1955) analyze the migratory Ngoni state's segmentation process — the Ngoni's flight from Shaka's Zulu conquest, and later splitting into five segments after the death of Zwangendaba — arguing that only one of several reported Ngoni secessions actually occurred, and that it was "relatively unimportant."
- **Independent formulation of the extended-case shift.** [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] credits Barnes with independently noting, in Barnes's Sydney Inaugural Lecture (1958), the same generational shift among younger British anthropologists from "apt illustration" toward analysis of a "series of cases" that Gluckman describes in his Foreword to [[turner-victor|Turner]]'s *Schism and Continuity in an African Society* (1957); see [[extended-case-method]].
