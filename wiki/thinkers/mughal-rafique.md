# M. Rafique Mughal

## Overview

M. Rafique Mughal is a Pakistani archaeologist whose **Cholistan / Hakra–Ghaggar** survey work and regional periodizations are key secondary anchors in [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]]’s *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* (1998). Kenoyer cites Mughal especially for river-system change, settlement density in the eastern plain, and Localization Era cultural geography.

## Contributions

*(As attributed via Kenoyer 1998.)*

- **Saraswati / Ghaggar-Hakra system.** Kenoyer cites Mughal (with older hydrographic literature: Oldham, Wilhelmy, etc.) on the eastern alluvial plain’s parallel river system — proposed outlets to the Greater Rann of Kutch or a join with the Indus near the delta head — as part of the environmental frame for Indus settlement geography.
- **Cholistan settlement and river change.** Endnotes mobilize Mughal 1992 on **Cholistan river changes** as a factor in settlement history (paired with Possehl 1990 on the urban revolution and other regional syntheses in Ch. 3 notes).
- **Major urban centers.** Kenoyer’s discussion of the five largest Integration Era cities ([[mohenjo-daro]], [[harappa]], [[ganweriwala]], [[rakhigarhi]], [[dholavira]]) draws on Possehl 1990 and **Mughal 1992** among sources that established the scale and spacing of primary centers (attributed citation chain).
- **Localization Era map key.** Kenoyer’s Figure 9.2 maps Localization Era cultural areas with a key **after Mughal 1990**, distinguishing **Cemetery H**, **Jhukar / Late Kulli**, and **Rangpur IIb–IIc** distributions across the greater Indus region — the standard Pakistani survey frame for post-urban regionalization.
- **Hakra phase in Regionalization.** Within the [[indus-valley-tradition]] / Shaffer phase scheme Kenoyer adopts, Hakra is one of the four important Regionalization cultural phases of the Indus plain (Balakot, Amri, Hakra, Kot Diji); Mughal’s survey work is part of the empirical base for Hakra settlement distributions (as relayed by Kenoyer).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Pakistani archaeological survey tradition; dialogue with [[possehl-gregory-l|Possehl]], [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]], and the American HARP generation on chronology and urban scale.
- Provides the **eastern-plain / Cholistan** empirical counterweight to Sindh- and Punjab-centered excavation narratives (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

River-identification and paleochannel reconstructions (Saraswati/Ghaggar-Hakra) remain contested in the wider literature; Kenoyer uses Mughal as an authoritative survey source without adjudicating every hydrographic detail. Settlement-count and size estimates depend on surface survey visibility and periodization choices.

## Historiography

Access here is via Kenoyer 1998 citations (esp. Mughal 1990, 1992). Mughal’s own survey monographs are the primary texts for a fuller page.

## Wright, *The Ancient Indus* (2010)

Wright 2010 gives fuller detail on Mughal's Cholistan settlement survey (as director):
site distribution shows Pre-urban settlements sited to avoid flood danger, and Mughal
(1997) reconstructs hydrographic shifts — around 2500 BCE the Yamuna River captured
the Chautang tributary, reducing flow to the Hakra's northern reach, and around 1500
BCE the Sutlej (which had fed the Hakra) was captured by the Beas, leaving the Hakra
channel "totally dry." Wright also relays Mughal's Hakra- and Kot Diji-phase site-size
tabulations for Cholistan and his interpretation of declining camp-site counts there
as evidence of increasing pastoral sedentarization. Wright records a terminology
dispute: Mughal (1990) prefers "Late Harappan" to her own "Post-urban" label, citing
the comparatively large (38-hectare) Kudwala settlement in Cholistan during this
period, while Wright states she remains "persuaded" that urbanism was severely
curtailed even where occupation continued — a live disagreement, not adjudicated
here.
