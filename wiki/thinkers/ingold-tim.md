# Tim Ingold

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/ingold-tim|Thinkers Hub — Tim Ingold]].

British social anthropologist (b. 1948); long associated with the University of Manchester and later Professor of Social Anthropology at the University of Aberdeen. Primary-ingested: *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* (Routledge, 2000).

## Overview

Ingold's program rejects the dualism that, he argues, cleaves Western thought and anthropology into "two worlds" of humanity and nature. He proposes a [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]]: organism-persons inhabit environments through skilled, attentive engagement; what is called "culture" is first of all variation in [[enskilment|skill]] grown in developmental systems. Autobiographically, he frames anthropology (from Cambridge, c. 1967) as the discipline that might bridge arts and sciences after disillusionment with military-industrial natural science (*Perception*, General introduction).

## Contributions

From *Perception of the Environment* (2000), attributed:

- **[[dwelling-perspective]]** — against the building perspective ("worlds are made before they are lived in"); Heideggerian founding gloss that we build because we dwell (Ch. 10).
- **[[taskscape]]** — temporal ensemble of interlocking tasks; landscape as congealed taskscape (Ch. 11); major archaeological uptake.
- **[[enskilment]]** — skills grown through practice; education of attention (Gibson); not transmitted content (intro; Part III).
- **[[wayfinding]]** vs navigation/map-use — knowing as movement along paths in a region (Ch. 13).
- **Gibsonian [[affordance]]** imported as perceptual ground of dwelling.
- **Trust → domination** rewrite of domestication and human–animal relations (Ch. 4).
- **Making as growth** — weaving/basket paradigm against hylomorphic design-first models (Chs. 5, 18–19).
- Critiques of optimal foraging / economic man (Ch. 2); cultural constructionism's paradox (Ch. 3); "anatomically modern human" and language-origins packages (Chs. 21–22); technology as modern disembedded concept (Chs. 15–16).
- Reformulation of [[animism]] / [[totemism]] and depiction (Ch. 7); Hallowell/Ojibwa other-than-human persons (Ch. 6).

From *[[ingold-lines-2007|Lines: A Brief History]]* (2007), attributed — a "comparative
anthropology of the line":

- **A taxonomy of lines** — threads vs traces (and the surfaces between them); the
  thesis that threads and traces are mutually transformable.
- **[[wayfaring]] vs. transport** — moving *along* a path and "knowing as one goes" vs.
  being carried *across* between fixed points; place as a knot, not a node.
- **[[meshwork]]** (from Lefebvre) — the world as interwoven lines of life, against the
  "network" of connected points (argued in this book against Chatwin/Semper, not ANT).
- **The genealogical-line critique** — the kinship chart "cuts up" the flowing
  pedigree-thread into points joined by connectors, "compressing lives into points"; a
  Bergsonian "braid of life" is offered instead.
- **"Writing is a species of drawing"** — engraving/print broke the bond between gesture
  and trace; Chinese calligraphy as "an art of rhythmic movement"; modernity as the
  "straightening" of the line.

From *[[ingold-making-2013|Making: Anthropology, Archaeology, Art and Architecture]]*
(2013), attributed:

- **[[knowing-from-the-inside]]** — the "4 A's" as ways of studying *with* and learning
  *from*, not making studies *of*; "anthropology is not ethnography."
- **Critique of [[hylomorphism]]** — making is not the imposition of form on matter but
  **morphogenesis**: form grows from the maker's [[correspondence]] with the material
  (the handaxe and the house as cases); "material" vs. "materiality."
- **[[correspondence]] vs. interaction** — lives and materials "answer to" one another
  along their paths (the "dance of animacy"); the tool as transducer.
- **Building as growth** and the round mound as gathering/"becoming earth" rather than a
  finished monument (the weather-world).

From *[[ingold-being-alive-2011|Being Alive]]* (2011), attributed:

- **The ANT critique ("When ANT meets SPIDER")** — against Actor-Network Theory's network
  of actants, [[meshwork|SPIDER]] ("Skilled Practice Involves Developmentally Embodied
  Responsiveness"): the web is spun *lines* of a living being, not a network of connectors.
- **The [[weather-world]]** — the world as a medium (earth, sky, wind, weather) one is
  immersed in and breathes, against the picture of a solid surface bearing objects;
  "landscape or weather-world?"; the critique of "soundscape."
- **Self-revision** — Ingold states he now regrets emphasizing "dwelling" and prefers
  **[[wayfaring]]**; "materials against materiality"; "anthropology is not ethnography."
  Deep treatment: [[hubs/thinkers/ingold-tim]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Standing discussions: whether dwelling soft-pedals power and history; relation to practice theory; ethnographic second-handedness of the essay collection; operationalization limits. Later corpus now being ingested (*[[ingold-lines-2007|Lines]]* 2007 ✅; *Making* 2013 and *Being Alive* 2011 in progress) — the meshwork/network and wayfaring/transport oppositions have drawn the criticism that they are evocative ideal-types more than operational analytics.

## Historiography

Career arc: Cambridge natural-science start → anthropology c. 1967 → hunter-gatherer / circumpolar and *The Appropriation of Nature* (1986, dualist phase he later repudiates) → dwelling synthesis of the 1990s → *Perception* (2000) → later lines/making/education books. Reputation as bridge figure among anthropology, archaeology, geography, and ecological psychology. Deep treatment: [[hubs/thinkers/ingold-tim]].

## "The Temporality of the Landscape" (2010 reprint)

*(Attributed — Tim Ingold, in [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* Ingold's landmark essay argues that human life "is also
the process of formation of the landscapes in which people have lived," and that "the practice of
archaeology is itself a form of dwelling" — the excavator's knowledge and the inhabitant's are of
"fundamentally the same kind." He introduces the **[[taskscape]]** ("to labour what landscape is to
land"): the array of interlocking activities whose intrinsic, rhythmic temporality (Gell's A-series;
his orchestra analogy of "resonance") he opposes to metronomic chronology. Landscape is "the
congealed form of the taskscape." He rejects Yi-Fu Tuan's environment/landscape split and Daniels &
Cosgrove's "landscape as cultural image" as smuggling back the nature/culture dualism his
[[dwelling-perspective]] dissolves.

### Gamble 1999 — Palaeolithic taskscape uptake

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]; as of Gamble 1999. Archaeological application of Ingold 1993 taskscape; Ingold’s own *Perception* (2000) is the primary vault base above.)*

Gamble adopts **[[taskscape]]** as one of three Palaeolithic **rhythms** (with *chaîne opératoire* and paths/tracks): an array of related activities carrying forward social life; landscape = what we see, taskscape = what we hear; temporality of the taskscape is social because people attend to one another in performance (Ingold 1993 via Gamble). Social occasions are only identifiable within regional social-landscape context (citing Ingold 1993). He develops taskscape to break Binford-style dichotomous “games against the environment” that relegate the social; planning is social as sharing of memories (Clark/Brody via Gamble). Place (vs mere gathering) draws on Ingold’s definition via experiences and ambience afforded by activities. See [[neanderthals]], [[palaeolithic-rhythms]], [[locale]].
