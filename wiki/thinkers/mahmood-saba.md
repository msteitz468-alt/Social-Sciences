# Saba Mahmood

## Overview

Saba Mahmood (1961–2018) was a Pakistani-American anthropologist (UC Berkeley) known for her ethnography of the women's mosque movement in Cairo and for a critique of liberal-feminist assumptions about agency and freedom. This page is currently sourced only through Lila [[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]]'s extensive approving engagement in *[[abu-lughod-do-muslim-women-2013|Do Muslim Women Need Saving?]]* (2013); expand from primary sources when ingested.

## Contributions

- **Piety as embodied self-cultivation.** Mahmood's study of the Cairo piety movement (fieldwork 1990s; *Politics of Piety*, 2005) argued that veiling and modest dress can be embodied practices cultivating virtue and closeness to God — not merely political symbols or marks of subordination (attributed; as relayed in Abu-Lughod 2013).
- **Agency beyond resistance.** Her most-quoted claim, cited verbatim by Abu-Lughod: "The desire for freedom and liberation is a historically situated desire whose motivational force cannot be assumed a priori, but needs to be reconsidered in light of other desires, aspirations, and capacities that inhere in a culturally and historically located subject" (Mahmood, as quoted in Abu-Lughod 2013). A direct challenge to treating resistance/liberation as the universal measure of women's [[agency]].
- **Secularization of the "saving" project.** Mahmood argued (per Abu-Lughod 2013) that today's overlap between liberal feminism and secular-democracy discourse continues the missionary-era rescue project in secularized language — see [[gendered-orientalism]] and [[saving-muslim-women-debate]].
- **Cold War genealogy of Afghan misogyny.** With Charles Hirschkind, analyzed the long-term impact of CIA Cold War funding of the Afghan mujahideen on warlords' treatment of women (attributed; cited in Abu-Lughod 2013).
- Critiqued Azar Nafisi's *Reading Lolita in Tehran* as "ruthless in its omissions" (attributed).
