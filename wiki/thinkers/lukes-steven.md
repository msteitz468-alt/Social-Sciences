# Steven Lukes

British political and social theorist; best known for *[[lukes-power-radical-view-2005|Power: A Radical View]]* (1974; 2nd ed. 2005), which argues that power has **three dimensions** and is most effective when least observable. This page is the concise network-oriented summary; coverage rests primarily on the 2005 second edition.

## Overview

Lukes entered the mid-century US community-power and elite–pluralism debates by mapping three “conceptual maps” of power: pluralist **one-dimensional** (overt decision-making), Bachrach–Baratz **two-dimensional** (agenda control and nondecisions), and a radical **three-dimensional** view that includes shaping wants and securing willing compliance. He treats the concept of power as **essentially contested** (Gallie) yet operational for empirical research (e.g. Crenson on air pollution).

## Contributions

- **[[three-dimensional-power|Three-dimensional power]]** — decision-making, nondecision-making, preference-shaping.
- **Power as capacity** (2005 correction): power is not only its exercise; domination is one species of power.
- **[[real-interests|Real interests]]** and counterfactual analysis of preference formation under power.
- Engagement with **Foucault** (Ch. 2, 2005): stimulated research on subtle compliance; rejects ultra-radical “constituted subject” implications for freedom and rationality.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Debates with Dahl-style pluralism (via literature; no wiki thinker page yet), Bachrach & Baratz, Mills/Hunter elite theses; later interlocutors include Foucault, Bourdieu, Clarissa Hayward, and critics of false consciousness. (Full genealogy pending denser biographical sources.)

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Accused of smuggling paternalism and discredited [[false-consciousness]]; of unworkable counterfactuals for real interests; of underplaying legitimate hierarchy. Lukes 2005 defends operational use of real interests as purpose-relative and retains a Spinozist space for freedom from others’ power.
