# Marshall Sahlins (1930–2021)

American cultural anthropologist, across a long career a leading theorist of economic anthropology (the substantivist [[original-affluent-society]]), of the **symbolic/culturalist critique of utilitarianism**, and of **historical anthropology** — how cultures change through **events**. Three of his major works are now primary-ingested: *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972), *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976), and *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985); the page also draws secondhand on [[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Sewell's *Logics of History*]] (ch. 7) and, for the evolutionary work, on [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s *[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*, which credits Sahlins's delineation of **General and Specific Evolution** (Sahlins & Service, *Evolution and Culture*, 1960).

## Overview

Sahlins (attributed, via [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]]) proposed a "possible theory of history" resting on two propositions: "the transformation of a culture is a mode of its reproduction," and "in action in the world … the cultural categories acquire new functional values." A structuralist of the Lévi-Straussian line ([[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]) given "an American pragmatist inflection," he argued that people necessarily interpret novel circumstances through inherited **cultural categories**, and that this application is at once reproductive and risky: because "the world is under no obligation to conform to the logic by which some people conceive of it," categories are revalued in use. His paradigm case is Captain Cook's arrival and death in Hawaii (1778–79), where Hawaiians assimilated Cook to the returning god Lono during the Makahiki festival, and the ensuing events both reproduced and transformed the Hawaiian cultural order. He named the situationally specific dynamics of such episodes the **[[structure-of-the-conjuncture|structure of the conjuncture]]**.

## Contributions

- **[[general-and-specific-evolution|General vs specific evolution]]** (lead essay in Sahlins & Service, *Evolution and Culture*, 1960) — dual evolutionary theory: **specific** = adaptive diversification of culture types; **general** = successive higher levels of all-round development. Service takes it as the spine of the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]. Carneiro 2003 records White’s acceptance of both as evolution and Carneiro’s own Spencerian preference to reserve “evolution” for complexity increase (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] Position 5).
- **[[structure-of-the-conjuncture|Structure of the conjuncture]]** — the emergent, situational regularities of practice through which cultural categories, mobilized by actors' interests, are put at risk and revalued in an event.
- **A theory of the event** — events as the working-out of cultural structures in circumstances that exceed them; the rehabilitation of *histoire événementielle* against the Annales/Braudelian dismissal of events as "froth."
- **Semiotic theory of "interest"** — a sign's *meaning* is its differential relation to other signs (Saussure), but its *interest* is the difference it makes to a subject's life schemes; "objective risk" and "subjective risk" of cultural categories.
- **Substantivist economic anthropology** — see the dedicated section below, now primary via *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972).
- **The critique of "practical reason"** — the two-paradigm argument that [[cultural-reason]] encompasses [[practical-reason]], and that even Western capitalism is a symbolic order ([[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]); now primary via *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976). See the Cultural Theory section below.
- **Historical anthropology** — [[mytho-praxis]], [[heroic-history]], the [[stranger-king]], and [[performative-and-prescriptive-structures]]; now primary via *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985). See the Historical Anthropology section below.
- **Polynesian stratification (1958 baseline; later historicized)** — *Social Stratification in Polynesia* (1958) remains the classic **synchronic** comparative baseline for ramage vs descent-line organization and degrees of stratification under ecological adaptation; not yet primary-ingested here. Via Kirch 1984: see dedicated subsection below.
- **Anahulu collaboration** — ethnohistorical–archaeological work with [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick V. Kirch]] on Anahulu Valley, Oʻahu; Kirch credits it with shaping his emphasis on structures of social reproduction in Polynesian chiefdom evolution (*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984).

## Economic Anthropology (*Stone Age Economics*, 1972)

*(Attributed — Sahlins, *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, now primary-ingested.)* Sahlins' major statement in [[economic-anthropology]], a leading work of the [[substantivism|substantivist]] tradition, treating "economy as a category of culture rather than behavior." Its principal contributions:

- **[[original-affluent-society|The original affluent society]]** — hunter-gatherers as affluent by wanting little (the "Zen road"), reanalyzing [[lee-richard-b|Lee]]'s !Kung and the Arnhem Land time-allocation data; scarcity as a market-instituted relation, not a universal. **First delivered as his discussion paper "Notes on the Original Affluent Society" at the 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium** (published 1968; also 1968 in *Les Temps Modernes*), then elaborated in *Stone Age Economics* — the 1968 volume is the concept's primary origin venue. At the symposium Sahlins himself flagged the [[netsilik|Netsilik]] as an exception and conceded (via Marshall) that "affluent society" was "a bon mot." Contested: [[original-affluent-society-debate]], [[kalahari-debate]].
- **[[domestic-mode-of-production|The domestic mode of production]]** and its structural [[underproduction]] — the household as dominant productive unit, oriented to livelihood not surplus; formalized through [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov's rule]].
- **Intensification and the [[big-man]]** — how kinship, chiefship, and the Melanesian big-man override the household's anti-surplus tendency. Here Sahlins **revises his own 1958 claim** that surplus production *causes* chieftainship, now arguing that leadership generates surplus, the two developing *pari passu*.
- **The [[reciprocity]] typology** — the generalized / balanced / negative continuum mapped onto kinship distance ([[generalized-reciprocity]], [[balanced-reciprocity]], [[negative-reciprocity]]), and [[redistribution]] as centralized reciprocity.
- **The [[gift-exchange|gift]] and the *[[hau]]*** — in "The Spirit of the Gift," a re-reading of [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] and of the Maori *hau* as the "yield" of a gift, and of the gift as the primitive analogue of the social contract.
- **Primitive trade** ([[exchange]]) — exchange rates set by the "diplomacy of economic good measure" among trade partners, not by market price competition.

## Cultural Theory (*Culture and Practical Reason*, 1976)

*(Attributed — Sahlins, *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]*, now primary-ingested.)* Sahlins's manifesto for a symbolic anthropology against utilitarianism in all its forms. He opposes two "paradigms of anthropological theory": [[practical-reason]] (culture as the codification or adaptive effect of material/practical activity — subjective utilitarianism, ecological naturalism, and Marxist praxis alike) and [[cultural-reason]] (the meaningful order that constitutes utility). The relation "can only be an encompassment," and cultural reason encompasses practical reason: "it is culture which constitutes utility." He builds a genealogy of the opposition ([[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] vs. [[boas-franz|Boas]]; [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s biological functionalism and its neofunctionalist heirs on the practical side; [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] and [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] on the cultural), critiques [[structural-marxism]] for reducing production relations to "an abstract shadow structure of productive requirements," and demonstrates in "[[la-pensee-bourgeoise|La Pensée Bourgeoise]]" that even Western capitalism is a symbolic/totemic order — "money is to the West what kinship is to the Rest." (Note: the ingested PDF is missing the bodies of chapters 3–4; see the source page.)

## Historical Anthropology (*Islands of History*, 1985)

*(Attributed — Sahlins, *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, now primary-ingested.)* Five essays arguing that "structure — the symbolic relations of cultural order — is an historical object," and that "the transformation of a culture is a mode of its reproduction." Because inherited categories are put at risk when applied to novel circumstances (objective risk: the world may not conform; subjective risk: interested actors use categories unpredictably), reproduction is also transformation — worked through the [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]. The essays develop [[mytho-praxis]] (the Maori mode of explicit mythic action; see [[maori]]), [[heroic-history]] and its "Principle of the Significant One," the [[stranger-king]] (Fijian installation and [[dumezil-georges|Dumézil]]'s three functions; see [[fijians]]), and the distinction of [[performative-and-prescriptive-structures|performative and prescriptive structures]]. The running case is Captain Cook's reception and death in Hawaii ([[hawaiians]]), against world-system theory's denial of indigenous cultural autonomy and against structural-Marxists Hindess and Hirst.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Sahlins worked in the structuralist tradition of [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] while breaking with its static synchrony by adopting [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s "duality of structure" — structure as both the medium and the outcome of conduct, transformable through practice. [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] treats him as the "necessary starting point for any theorization of events" and as convergent with his own account of change, while dissenting on two points (below). Formal training relations are not documented in the ingested source; `trained_under`/`trained` left as `[[unknown]]`.


## Reception in Graeber & Wengrow (*Dawn of Everything*, 2021)

*(Attributed — G&W.)* *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* thanks Sahlins in acknowledgements and deploys: [[original-affluent-society]] (with their caveats on multiple affluences); culture-as-antithesis / Athens–Sparta framing of [[schismogenesis]]; and broader substantivist inheritance. G&W's program is not a Sahlins school extension but a deep-time political synthesis that uses Sahlins as ally against stage evolutionism and utilitarian scarcity. See [[dawn-of-everything-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] endorses Sahlins's core mechanism of change but criticizes (a) his conception of structure as **singular** for a whole society — problematic for complex, mobile, multi-cultural populations — arguing structures are multiple and intersecting; and (b) his conception of structure as **exclusively cultural/symbolic**, excluding material resources, when Sahlins's own Hawaiian evidence (Cook's bones, sandalwood, firearms) presupposes a schemas-and-resources dialectic (see [[transposable-schemas]]); Sewell also "reverses" Sahlins's term, arguing a structure of the conjuncture presupposes a prior *conjuncture of structures*.

Sahlins's Cook interpretation was sharply contested by Gananath Obeyesekere, who argued the "Cook-as-god" story was a European colonial myth and that Hawaiians treated Cook pragmatically as a chief; Sahlins replied in *How "Natives" Think* (1995). Sewell judges that Sahlins "effectively refuted Obeyesekere's major claims," while noting the duel's outcome does not affect his own argument. See [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]].

## Historiography

Sahlins is a standard reference in debates over structure and event, the anthropology of history, and the interpretation of first-contact encounters. The historical-anthropology summary is now grounded in *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985) itself, supplemented by Sewell's engagement; *Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities* (1981) and the Cook controversy texts (*The Apotheosis of Captain Cook*, 1992; *How "Natives" Think*, 1995) remain outstanding — see [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]]. A graduate-level treatment of the whole career is filed at [[hubs/thinkers/sahlins-marshall|the Thinkers Hub]].

**Series editor note (*[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* 1966):** Sahlins edited the Prentice-Hall Foundations of Modern Anthropology Series in which [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]]'s *Peasants* appeared. Wolf quotes Sahlins's characterization of primitive economies (production for use/kinship; decentralized familial control of means of production; absence of exploitation in the production system itself) as the foil against which peasantry is defined by surplus transfer to rulers. Primary Sahlins texts still outstanding.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; engagement with Sahlins’s economic anthropology.)*

- **[[domestic-mode-of-production|Domestic mode]] and surplus under coercion.** Scott cites the pre-state domestic mode of production (Sahlins) together with [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]] and Boserup-type pressure arguments: where means of production are not monopolized, a peasantry that can meet basic needs will not automatically produce an elite-appropriable surplus and must be *compelled*; surplus “exists” for the embryonic state only when extracted—otherwise it is “consumed” in leisure or cultural elaboration. Open access via group membership and Chayanov’s work-effort response (more workers than dependents → reduced effort) underwrite Scott’s claim that early states required unfree labor forms (corvée, forced deliveries, debt bondage, serfdom, slavery) rather than voluntary social-contract surplus.
- **Forager leisure / anti-drudgery affinity.** Elsewhere Scott’s reappraisal of foragers vs farmers (diet, health, leisure; reluctance to take on staple cultivation without force or population pressure) occupies the same debate space as the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis even where *Stone Age Economics* is not named in a given chapter slice—file as conceptual kinship, not as a full citation network.

## Kirch 1984: *Social Stratification in Polynesia* as synchronic baseline (attributed)

*(Attributed via [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984 — Sahlins’s 1958 monograph itself not primary-ingested.)* Kirch frames Sahlins’s *Social Stratification in Polynesia* (1958; also 1957) as the leading **synchronic** thesis that Polynesian cultural differentiation was produced by **adaptation under varying technological and environmental conditions** — a single culture filling ecological niches; descent-line vs ramage systems linked to compacted vs dispersed resources; stratification explained via production and distribution of strategic goods. Kirch records the classic critiques (Freeman on Samoan organization; Finney on Tahitian resource concentration) and treats Irving Goldman’s status-rivalry program as a complementary aristocratic counterpoint. Kirch’s own project is to **historicize** that ethnographic spectrum archaeologically (Ancestral Polynesian baseline + multi-process sequences) rather than replace Sahlins’s typology wholesale. Kirch also cites later Sahlins (1968, 1972) on Hawaiian society at the “boundary of primitive society,” usurpation as a principle of legitimacy, and the incomplete break between chiefs and commoners under kinship morality — positions Kirch deploys for the Hawaiʻi chiefdom–state interface. **Anahulu:** Kirch credits collaboration with Sahlins on Anahulu Valley ethnohistory–archaeology for his views on structures of social reproduction in chiefdom evolution.
