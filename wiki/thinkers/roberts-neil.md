# Neil Roberts

Neil Roberts (b. 1953) is a British physical geographer and Quaternary scientist, best known as author of *The Holocene: An Environmental History* — a standard synthesis of [[holocene|Holocene]] [[environmental-archaeology|environmental]] change. He works at the intersection of palaeoecology, archaeology, and historical geography, and has conducted primary lake-catchment research in the Near East and Mediterranean.

## Overview

Roberts frames the study of the Holocene as "above all a work of geography," synthesizing biology, archaeology, geomorphology, and climatology to reconstruct human–environment relations across the last ~11 500 years. His central argument (attributed) is that across the epoch the human role in environmental change grew from negligible to dominant, so that the "natural" and "cultural" components of later-Holocene change become inseparable — an intellectual precursor to the [[anthropocene|Anthropocene]] framing.

## Contributions

- **Holocene environmental history** — his textbook ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) consolidated the field and, in its second edition, moved entirely to a calibrated calendar timescale (Cal. yr BP), dating the Holocene's onset to 11 500 Cal. yr BP (see [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[holocene]]).
- **"Geological opportunism"** — his interpretation (with Vita-Finzi, Limbrey) that early Near Eastern farmers exploited newly formed alluvial soil-water resources, developed through his [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] Konya-plain catchment work (Roberts 1983; Roberts et al. 1997).
- **The natural/cultural boundary problem** — his methodological insistence that whether an episode of [[deforestation]], [[soil-erosion]], [[desertification]], [[eutrophication]], or [[freshwater-acidification|acidification]] is "natural or cultural" is the defining question of later-Holocene environmental history, answerable only through dated multi-proxy evidence (see [[environmental-determinism]] for his critique of climatic determinism).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Roberts works within the British Quaternary and historical-ecology tradition (Frank Oldfield, whose "metastable equilibrium" and continuous-record arguments he adopts; Grahame Clark's economic-prehistory legacy) and in dialogue with palaeoclimate synthesists (Alayne Street-Perrott, with whom he published on African lake levels). His conservation argument draws on Paul Richards' work on indigenous ecological knowledge.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

As a synthesist rather than a primary theorist, Roberts is valued for judicious integration; his cautious, contribution-not-determination stance on climate–society links is broadly endorsed. His late-1990s framing (e.g. "Third World" terminology, pre-aDNA and pre-high-resolution-speleothem syntheses) is dated by subsequent advances — the ingested edition predates the ancient-DNA revolution and much high-resolution palaeoclimatology.

## Historiography

*The Holocene* ran to three editions (1989, 1998, 2014), each substantially expanded — the 2nd edition's move to calibrated chronology being the most consequential revision. Its longevity marks it as the field's standard student synthesis over three decades.
