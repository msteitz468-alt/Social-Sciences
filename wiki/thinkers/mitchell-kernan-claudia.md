# Claudia Mitchell-Kernan

## Overview

American linguistic anthropologist (1941–2016); at the time of *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972), affiliated with Harvard (Ph.D., UC Berkeley). Her chapter "Signifying and Marking: Two Afro-American Speech Acts," adapted from her dissertation, analyzed naturalistic tape-recorded conversation from a northern California urban Black community, with examples drawn from recordings made in Oakland.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Mitchell-Kernan, 1972.)*

- Distinguished two senses of **[[signifying]]**: (1) a tactic in game-activity/verbal dueling, the sense most treated in prior folklore literature (Abrahams, Kochman); and (2) "a way of encoding messages or meanings in natural conversations which involves, in most cases, an element of indirection" — an alternative message form chosen for artistic merit, which she argued was neglected in the existing literature and made the focus of her analysis.
- Defined the core feature of signifying as **indirection**: correct semantic interpretation cannot be reached from the dictionary meaning of the lexical items alone; recognizing and correctly reinterpreting a signifying utterance requires shared cultural/background knowledge operating on two levels.
- Analyzed **marking** as a distinct but related device: "essentially a mode of characterization" — a narrative technique reproducing not just what was said but *how* it was said (phonology, paralinguistic mimicry), offering implicit comment on a quoted speaker's background, personality, or intent (illustrated by an "Uncle Tom" mimicry example of a company spokesman's dialect).
- Explicitly built on Hymes's notion of the **[[speech-event]]** as a structured whole, and situated her work against traditional dialectology (Labov, Stewart, Wolfram) by treating linguistic code choice as signaling culturally specific communicative strategy rather than only grammatical variation.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Draws on Hymes's speech-event framework and on prior folklore scholarship on verbal dueling (Abrahams, Kochman); her attention to the discrepancy between surface meaning and communicative function parallels concerns raised elsewhere in the same volume by Ervin-Tripp (ch. 7) and Sacks (ch. 11).
