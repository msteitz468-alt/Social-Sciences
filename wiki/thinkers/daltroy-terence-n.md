# Terence N. D'Altroy

## Overview

Andean archaeologist specializing in the Inca empire and provincial political economy. Best known for integrating ethnohistory with regional survey/excavation (notably Upper Mantaro) and for placing Tawantinsuyu in a comparative empire framework rather than as an isolated New World anomaly ([[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]]).

## Contributions

- **Comparative empire model** for the Incas: hegemonic ↔ territorial continuum of rule intensity; critique of simple core–periphery complexity rankings (Incas as counter-example to “complex core / simple periphery”).
- **Provincial archaeology** as corrective to Cuzco-centric chronicle narratives; household and community scales alongside elite politics.
- Synthesis of **split inheritance**, *panaqa* politics, staple finance (labor and storehouses), and military logistics as interlocking institutions (attributed; densified from *The Incas*).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Works in dialogue with John Murra (verticality, staple finance tradition), John Rowe (horizon chronology, king lists), R. Tom Zuidema and Brian Bauer (zeq'e / Cuzco ritual landscape), and comparative empire theorists (Mann; Luttwak/Hassig hegemonic–territorial vocabulary). Field program in Mantaro provincial centers complements heartland work by Bauer, Covey, and others.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Chronicle-dependent chronology and king lists remain contested against archaeological early-Inca sequences (Covey and others, as of D'Altroy 2002's source base). First-edition (2002) synthesis predates substantial post-2002 Cuzco-basin and aDNA work — date-stamp claims; second edition (2014) not yet ingested.

## Historiography

*The Incas* (Blackwell Peoples of America, 2002/2003; 2nd ed. 2014) is a standard graduate/undergraduate synthesis alongside Moseley and ethnohistoric monographs. Wiki coverage: [[daltroy-the-incas-2002]]; densifies [[inca-empire]] beyond [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001]].
