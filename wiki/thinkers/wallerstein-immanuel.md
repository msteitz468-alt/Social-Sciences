# Immanuel Wallerstein

Immanuel Maurice Wallerstein (1930–2019) was an American sociologist and historical social scientist, founder of [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]], and long-time director of the Fernand Braudel Center for the Study of Economies, Historical Systems, and Civilizations (Binghamton). This page is now grounded in his primary texts — the founding monograph *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]* (1974) and his compact restatement *[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction]]* (2004) — with the edited 2004 anniversary volume *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-longue-duree-2004|The Modern World-System in the Longue Durée]]* as supplement. MWS Vols. II–IV remain uningested.

## Overview

Wallerstein's program treats the modern world as a single [[capitalist-world-economy|capitalist world-economy]] existing for roughly five centuries, structured by [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] relations, studied in the Braudelian [[longue-duree|longue durée]], with knowledge production itself a structure of the system. He held that the system is historical and mortal, and in later work argued it was entering a terminal crisis and chaotic transition.

## Contributions

- **[[world-systems-analysis|World-systems analysis]]** — unit of analysis = world-system; historical-system ontology; polarization; hegemonic cycles.
- **Conceptual synthesis** (self-described, 2004): Prebischian [[core-periphery]] + Braudel's *longue durée* / *économie-monde* + Sweezy-side transition-from-feudalism argument (*MWS* I, 1974).
- **[[structures-of-knowledge|Structures of knowledge]]** — historicizing social-science vocabularies (development, globalization, totalitarianism, etc.) as time-bound system products.
- **[[antisystemic-movements|Antisystemic movements]]** — analytic category for oppositional politics at system scale (developed with Arrighi and others).
- **Fernand Braudel Center** (1976–) — institutional program for historical systems research; 25th-anniversary conference (2001) produced the 2004 volume.
- **Vocabulary critique (2004 ch. 13).** Impressionistic history of post-1945 social-science keywords — authoritarian personality, totalitarianism, Third World, development/modernization, dependency, Kuhnian paradigm, core–periphery, longue durée, structuralism, complexity sciences, culture wars, class/race/gender, [[globalization]]/TINA/governance, human rights, clash of civilizations — argued as fickle and headline-following; prefers thinking the present as chaotic **transition** from a disintegrating capitalist world-economy (Wallerstein 2000b/2004, attributed).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Intellectual sources:** [[marx-karl|Marx]] / [[historical-materialism]]; Fernand Braudel; dependency/ECLA (Prebisch); Paul Sweezy side of transition debate.
- **reacted_against:** [[modernization-theory]]; national-state-as-unit sociology; two-cultures nomothetic/idiographic split.
- **Collaborators/network:** [[arrighi-giovanni|Giovanni Arrighi]]; Terence K. Hopkins; contributors to the 2004 volume including [[amin-samir|Samir Amin]], [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]], Mahmood Mamdani, Boaventura de Sousa Santos, Michel-Rolph Trouillot, Janet Abu-Lughod.
