# Bruce Trigger

## Overview

Canadian archaeologist (McGill University) known for comparative and
historiographic work on early civilizations and for *A History of
Archaeological Thought*. His *Understanding Early Civilizations* (2003) is used
by [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]] as the principal comparative framework in
*[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* — both for reading Indus
religion and for the comparative city-state analysis. Documented here only as
cited by Wright (2010); densify from his own works when ingested.

## Contributions

- **Comparative early civilizations.** Trigger (2003) compared a sample of
  intensively documented early civilizations — Old/Middle Kingdom Egypt,
  southern Mesopotamia, late Shang/early Western Zhou China, the Aztec Valley of
  Mexico, the Classic Maya, the Inka, and the Yoruba/Benin of West Africa —
  explicitly **excluding the Indus** for lack of readable texts (Trigger
  2003:33). Wright imports the framework to the Indus as, in her words, a
  strategy "not previously undertaken by Indus scholars."
- **Findings Wright draws on (attributed to Trigger 2003):** all sampled early
  civilizations were agrarian/craft-producing; none monotheistic; each built
  rich pantheons personifying natural forces, often with part-human/part-animal
  deities, reflecting a worldview that did not sharply divide human, natural,
  and supernatural realms; nearly all had monarchs (with Athens and possibly
  Teotihuacan as exceptions), but councils of lineage, merchant, and temple
  representatives frequently shared power. Trigger (2003:47) rejects the view
  that "class displaced kinship and ethnicity as the main organizing principle
  of society" — the revisionist counterweight to top-down state models that
  frames Wright's Indus reading.
