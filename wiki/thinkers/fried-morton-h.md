# Morton H. Fried

## Overview

[[fried-morton-h|Morton Herbert Fried]] (1923–1986) was an American cultural anthropologist at Columbia University, series co-editor (with Marvin Harris) of Random House Studies in Anthropology, and author of *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* (1967) — the principal ingested statement of the **[[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model|egalitarian / rank / stratified / state]]** sequence. He defined political-evolutionary levels by **status scarcity** and **differential access to basic resources**, treated stratified nonstate society as **unstable**, and coined the load-bearing **[[pristine-state|pristine]] / [[secondary-state|secondary]] state** distinction for origin theory.

## Contributions

From *The Evolution of Political Society* (attributed):

1. **Egalitarian–rank–stratified–state sequence** as a unified theory of ranking, stratification, and the state ([[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]; Theory Hub: [[hubs/theory/egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]).
2. **[[egalitarian-society]]** — as many prestige positions per age-sex grade as capable persons; reciprocal economy; no law; leadership situational and non-hierarchical.
3. **[[rank-society]]** — limited valued statuses without necessary resource stratification; redistribution; kinship ideology peak; relatively stable.
4. **[[stratified-society]]** — differential access to basic resources; requires sanctions beyond kinship; ethnographically nearly invisible as nonstate form.
5. **State** as organization of power **superior to kinship**, maintaining stratification ([[the-state]]; [[state-formation]]).
6. **[[pristine-state]] / [[secondary-state]]** — autochthonous vs stimulus/model-driven formation.
7. **Definitional toolkit** for political anthropology: culture (symbolic learning), society, social control, sanctions vs reinforcements, power vs authority, custom vs law (Austinian working definitions), political organization vs government, legitimacy as ideology blended with power.
8. **Critique of tribe as evolutionary stage** — praises Service–Sahlins sodality analysis but treats most tribes as secondary; ranking and stratification as the real evolutionary questions ([[service-fried-political-typology-debate]]).

Earlier China work (*Fabric of Chinese Society*, 1953) is not primary-ingested here; EPS is comparative synthesis, not a China ethnography.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Service rivalry:** alternate cut of political evolution; tribe stage; military vs feast external polity ([[service-fried-political-typology-debate]]).
- **Observability:** stratified nonstate form is a theoretical bridge, not an ethnographic inventory — vulnerability to “stage invented to fit the ladder” critique.
- **Later archaeology:** primary/secondary state language survives; rigid sequences and population-pressure monocausality contested (Adams-type ecological cautions Fried himself engages).
- **Self-positioning:** Fried presents the theory as crude, special, under-documented — a target for fire.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O pair [[fried-morton-h|Morton Fried]] with [[service-elman-r|Service]] as foundational political-evolution references: *The Evolution of Political Society* (1967) is listed among core sources for the typologies used in their complexity chapter. They note that ethnographic categories of Service and Fried were devised principally to classify extant and recent cultures but have been **widely applied by archaeologists** to prehistoric societies—useful abstractions that fit no case precisely and can mislead when treated as developmental stages rather than loose descriptive types.
