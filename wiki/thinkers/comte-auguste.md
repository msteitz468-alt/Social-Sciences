# Auguste Comte

## Overview

Isidore Auguste Marie François Xavier Comte (1798–1857), usually Auguste Comte, was a French philosopher who proposed a systematic **positive philosophy** and named and outlined **social physics** (later **sociology**) as the last science in a hierarchical encyclopedic order. His six-volume *Cours de philosophie positive* (1830–1842) is the foundational statement; Harriet Martineau's 1853 English condensation made that program available to a wide British readership with Comte's approval. proposed_by: [[positivism]] · proposed: [[social-physics]] · proposed: [[law-of-three-stages]]

## Contributions

- **[[law-of-three-stages]]** — Comte argued that each leading conception of knowledge passes through theological (fictitious), metaphysical (abstract), and positive (scientific) stages; the positive stage studies invariable laws of succession and resemblance rather than first or final causes (*Positive Philosophy*, Introduction ch. I, Martineau).
- **[[hierarchy-of-the-sciences]]** — He ordered the fundamental sciences by decreasing generality and increasing complexity and dependence: mathematics, astronomy, physics, chemistry, physiology/biology, and social physics/sociology (Introduction ch. II).
- **[[social-physics]] / [[sociology]]** — He treated social phenomena as the last domain still trapped in theologico-metaphysical methods and set the principal aim of the *Cours* as establishing social science on a positive footing (Introduction ch. I; Book VI).
- **[[social-statics]] and [[social-dynamics]]** — He split social science into the theory of spontaneous order (consensus, co-existence of institutions) and the theory of natural progress (historical filiation of social states) (Book VI, ch. V–VI).
- **[[positive-method]]** — He insisted on unity of method across sciences, observation guided by theory, historical filiation as sociology's distinctive procedure, and refusal of absolute or cause-seeking questions beyond laws (Introduction; Book VI ch. III–IV, XIII).
- **Historical schema of [[theological-military-system]] → critical period → positive reorganization** — Book VI ch. VII–XV stages European development as application and "proof" of the developmental law (attributed philosophy of history, not wiki-voice chronology).
- **Family and sex hierarchy in statics** — In Book VI ch. V he treated the [[family]] as the true social unit and argued the natural subordination of woman as fundamental to marriage and social existence (fully attributed; see institution page).

The later *Système de politique positive* (Religion of Humanity, sociocracy) is outside Martineau's condensation and is not treated as primary evidence on this page from the present source.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under / early association:** Henri de Saint-Simon (relations ~1817–1822/24). Comte's 1822 prospectus already stated classification of the sciences, the three-stage law, and sociology; later positivists and Comte himself treated this as independent of Saint-Simon's mysticism (Harrison intro to Martineau; reliability: secondary).
- **reacted_against:** theological and metaphysical politics (divine right; sovereignty of the people, liberty of conscience, equality, and national independence as absolute transitional dogmas); what he called the critical/negative philosophy of the revolutionary period; pure empiricism without systematic theory; introspective psychology as last phase of theology.
- **Engaged as incomplete precursors (Book VI ch. II):** Montesquieu, Condorcet, Turgot, and others as partial anticipations of social science — recorded as Comte's historiography, not as this wiki's verdicts on those figures.
- **Reception line:** English positivists and reformers (Martineau, later Congreve, Harrison, and others); Mill's mixed appraisal; the classical sociological lineage (especially French) reworked rather than simply continued Comtean system-building — see [[sociology]] and [[positivism]].
- **Durkheim (ingested):** [[durkheim-emile]] kept the demand for social science as science and for social order after crisis; rejected vague generalities and much of Comtean philosophy of history (*[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* Preface and design). Not listed under `trained` — intellectual reworking, not a student lineage.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Attributed critiques in the later literature (not adjudicated here) include: excessive system-building and encyclopedic rigidity; Eurocentric "most advanced peoples" / "white race" framing of the abstract historical series; authoritarian implications of the later Positive Polity; underestimation of economics and individual agency; the gap between programmatic sociology and actual research practice; sex hierarchy and organismic consensus models. Mill praised the historical analysis and philosophy of the physical sciences while criticizing the Positive Polity. Durkheim and later sociologists kept the idea of social facts and scientific sociology while rejecting much of Comte's philosophy of history and political program.
