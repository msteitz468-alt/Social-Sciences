# Nancy Scheper-Hughes

## Overview

Nancy Scheper-Hughes is a US anthropologist (long at UC Berkeley) and a central figure
in **[[critical-medical-anthropology]]** and the anthropology of violence, suffering, and
the body. She is best known for the ethnography *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death
Without Weeping]]* (1992; deep treatment
[[hubs/studies/scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study|Studies Hub]]), for the "three
bodies" framework of **[[the-mindful-body]]** (with Margaret Lock, 1987), and for
programmatically defending an engaged, **[[militant-anthropology|"militant" anthropology]]**.
Her career began not in the academy but in the field: she first lived on the
[[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do Cruzeiro]] in Northeast Brazil in 1964–66 as a twenty-year-old
Peace Corps community-health worker (*visitadora*), an experience that, in her own account,
"left its mark on me, indelibly so" and to which she returned as an anthropologist across
1982, 1987, and 1988–89.

## Contributions

- **The mindful body / three bodies.** With Margaret Lock ("The Mindful Body," 1987),
  Scheper-Hughes proposed analyzing the body simultaneously as three: the individual
  **body-self** (existential/phenomenological), the **social body** (the body as symbol),
  and the **body politic** (the body as regulated by power). *[[the-mindful-body]]* became a
  charter statement for critical medical anthropology.
- **Nervoso and the medicalization of hunger.** In *Death Without Weeping* she traced how
  the older Nordestino idiom **[[delirio-de-fome|delírio de fome]]** ("the madness of hunger")
  is transformed, through the expansion of clinics and pharmacies, into the medicalized folk
  syndrome **[[nervoso]]** — so that "an individualized discourse on sickness comes to replace
  a more radical and socialized discourse on hunger" (attributed).
- **Everyday violence.** She defined **[[everyday-violence]]** — the routinized, normalized
  violence of hunger, sickness, bureaucratic indifference, and death — and argued it is
  continuous with, and the precondition of, "extraordinary" state violence (death squads,
  the "disappeared").
- **The political economy of the emotions and mortal selective neglect.** Her most contested
  claims: that **[[maternal-thinking|mother love]]** is culturally and materially produced
  rather than natural or universal, and that under chronic scarcity and routine infant death
  Alto mothers practice a scarcity-shaped **[[mortal-selective-neglect|selective neglect]]** of
  infants judged unlikely to survive — a **[[political-economy-of-the-emotions|political economy
  of the emotions]]** rather than deficient maternal feeling.
- **Organs Watch and [[body-commodification]].** From November 1999 she co-founded **Berkeley Organs Watch** with [[cohen-lawrence|Lawrence Cohen]] (Soros/Open Society and UC Berkeley support), out of the Bellagio Task Force on Transplantation, Bodily Integrity and the International Traffic in Organs (Rothman et al. 1997). Method dictum: “follow the bodies” — hybrid of multi-sited ethnography, documentation/surveillance, and medical human-rights work, with a “hermeneutics of suspicion” toward gift/altruism/scarcity rhetoric (*[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]*, 2002). She defines body commodification broadly as capitalized exchanges in which human bodies are tokens often **masked** as love or kindness; commercialized transplant medicine, she argues, divides the world into invisible **organ givers** and cherished **organ receivers**. Chapter “Commodity Fetishism in Organs Trafficking” applies Marxian fetishism to the kidney as organ of opportunity and last resort, documents prices and circuits (e.g. Chennai ~$1,200 seller fees; Israeli packages $180–200k), and treats organ-theft *rumors*, documented sales, and medically substantiated theft allegations as distinct evidence classes. Continuity with Bom Jesus organ-theft rumor analysis in *Death Without Weeping*.
- **Volume editing.** Co-edited *Commodifying Bodies* (with [[wacquant-loic|Wacquant]]; *Body & Society* 7(2–3) 2001 / Sage 2002), gathering Cohen, [[lock-margaret|Lock]], Weiss, Brandes, [[klinenberg-eric|Klinenberg]], Tober, Epele, and Wacquant on body markets under late capitalism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Scheper-Hughes's work fuses critical theory (Marx, Gramsci's hegemony, Sartrean "bad faith"),
phenomenology (Merleau-Ponty; Emmanuel Levinas's ethics of "useless suffering"), and a
politicized reading of [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] (*méconnaissance*, the "bad-faith economy").
Her closest theoretical interlocutor is [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]], on whose "terror
as usual" and reading of rumor she draws heavily; she both aligns with and pointedly
complicates [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] (arguing that in "semi-feudal" Northeast Brazil the
state still governs through direct bodily violence rather than Foucault's "softer" disciplinary
apparatus). Her method is explicitly indebted to Paulo Freire's *conscientização* and to Franco
Basaglia's Democratic Psychiatry (with which she had worked in Italy). Her writing partnership
with [[lock-margaret|Margaret Lock]] produced her single most-cited concept ([[the-mindful-body]]). Organs Watch interlocutors include Cohen, the Bellagio network (David and Sheila Rothman et al.), and critical bioethics opponents of libertarian “win-win” paid-donation frames.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Death Without Weeping*'s thesis on maternal detachment drew both major recognition (the 1993
Staley Prize) and sustained challenge — most directly from Marilyn Nations and Linda-Anne Rebhun,
who argued that the flat maternal affect Scheper-Hughes reports is "the blankness of the
shell-shocked" *masking* real grief, not its absence (see [[mother-love-scarcity-debate]]).
Her subsequent manifesto "The Primacy of the Ethical: Propositions for a Militant Anthropology"
(*Current Anthropology*, 1995) provoked a defining disciplinary exchange with Roy D'Andrade over
whether anthropology should be a moral-political or an objective-scientific enterprise (see
[[militant-anthropology-debate]]). Critics charge that her partisan stance risks subordinating
analysis to advocacy and that attributing agency to neglectful mothers courts "victim-blaming";
she anticipates the latter charge directly, distinguishing causality from blame.

## Historiography

Scheper-Hughes is a self-documenting ethnographer: her books foreground their own conditions of
production, positionality, and funding (she notes wryly that "'development' and 'tobacco,' two
social ills, put me in the field and in their debt"). *Death Without Weeping* is thus also a
primary document for the reflexive, post–*Writing Culture* moment in anthropology and for the
consolidation of critical medical anthropology as a program. A fuller intellectual biography
(Thinkers Hub) is deferred pending ingest of her other major works (*Saints, Scholars, and
Schizophrenics*; fuller Organs Watch corpus). Partial Organs Watch primary material is now in
*[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (intro + “Commodity Fetishism in Organs Trafficking”).
