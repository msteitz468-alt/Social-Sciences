# James Woodburn (b. 1934)

British social anthropologist (LSE), the principal ethnographer of the [[hadza|Hadza]] and a
central theorist of forager egalitarianism. His two chapters in *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man
the Hunter]]* (1968) supplied the volume with both an "affluent society" data point and a key
exhibit against the patrilocal band model.

## Overview

*(Attributed — Woodburn, 1958–60 fieldwork.)* Woodburn documented the Eastern
[[hadza|Hadza]] as nomadic foragers who obtained food on "less than two hours a day" over the
year, were well-nourished (better than their agricultural neighbours), and practised no
resource conservation or storage. He insisted their residential units be called **"camps," not
"bands,"** because they lacked territory, leadership, corporate identity, and fixed membership
— demonstrable only through **longitudinal/diachronic** census data, not synchronic snapshots
or (unreliable) informant statements.

## Contributions

- **Hadza ethnography** — one of the best-documented African foraging cases (see [[hadza]]),
  feeding both the [[original-affluent-society|affluent-society]] thesis and the
  [[patrilocal-band-model-debate|band-model debate]].
- **Residential flexibility** — the demonstration, via diachronic census, that Hadza camp
  membership is radically fluid (67 different adults co-resident with one widow over three
  years).
- **Immediate-return vs. delayed-return systems** — Woodburn's later, influential distinction
  (1980/1982) grew from this fieldwork; his account of Hadza egalitarianism (mobility, sharing,
  refusal to accumulate) became a standard reference (noted here for context; those texts are
  not yet primary-ingested).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Woodburn worked in the British social-anthropology tradition but turned its tools to the
forager case, becoming (with [[lee-richard-b|Lee]] and Turnbull) a founder of modern
hunter-gatherer studies. His methodological insistence on numerical, longitudinal data
distinguished his contribution at the symposium. Documented training relations not in the
ingested source.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* Woodburn's report of weak Hadza interpersonal obligation (including an
instance of a paralysed boy abandoned by kin) complicated romantic readings of forager
sociality; his "less than two hours a day" figure, like Lee's, has been scrutinized in the
[[original-affluent-society|work-effort debate]]. His egalitarianism thesis is widely cited and
debated in later hunter-gatherer studies.

## Historiography

Woodburn is a standard reference for the Hadza, forager egalitarianism, and the immediate-
return concept. A fuller treatment awaits ingestion of his 1980s papers.
