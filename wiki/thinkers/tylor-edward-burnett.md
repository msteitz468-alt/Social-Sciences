# Edward Burnett Tylor

## Overview

Edward Burnett Tylor (1832–1917) was the founding figure of academic anthropology in Britain and is conventionally called "the father of cultural anthropology." A self-taught Quaker with no university degree, he became the first person to hold a post in anthropology at Oxford (Reader, 1884; Professor, 1896). His *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871) gave the discipline its first analytic definition of [[culture]], codified the [[comparative-method]] and the [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]], and advanced the [[animism]] theory of religion. Tylor's anthropology was thoroughly **evolutionist**: he held that human culture develops through broadly uniform stages from savagery to civilization, driven by the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|essential similarity of the human mind]] everywhere.

## Contributions

- **The concept of [[culture]]** — Tylor's opening sentence ("that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society") is the discipline's founding etic definition, treating culture as a single graduated phenomenon studiable by natural-science method. George Stocking argues Tylor's oft-repeated credit for "inventing the culture concept" is historically overstated: Tylor used "culture" always in the singular and in a developmental sense (civilization graded on a single ascending scale, with contemporary societies — Australian, Tahitian, Aztec, Chinese, Italian — ranked along it using Europe as the implicit standard), not in the plural, relativistic sense the term acquired in twentieth-century anthropology. Tylor himself declined the chance, when consulted by Leslie Stephen on the *Oxford English Dictionary*, to formally legitimate an anthropological definition of "culture" in his lifetime; the *Primitive Culture* opening line was not added to the OED until fifteen years after his 1917 death, and even then to illustrate the older evolutionary usage rather than a relativistic one (Stocking, *Victorian Anthropology*, 1987).
- **[[social-evolutionism|Cultural evolutionism]]** — a unilinear developmental scheme (savagery → barbarism → civilization) held together by the uniform operation of the human mind; see [[social-evolutionism]].
- **The [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]]** — "processes, customs, opinions, and so forth, which have been carried on by force of habit into a new state of society different from that in which they had their original home" — Tylor's chief evidential device for reconstructing earlier culture-stages from present-day residues.
- **[[animism]]** — his "minimum definition of religion" as "the belief in Spiritual Beings," and the theory that religion originates in the primitive philosopher's inference of a soul (anima) from dreams and death, elaborated into a doctrine of spirits and gods. George Stocking argues Tylor grounded this "ghost-soul" inference in a rationalist psychology: "ancient savage philosophers," reasoning from the observed difference between living and dead bodies and from dream/vision experience, drew "the obvious inference" of a soul — making primitive religious reasoning itself a form of applied logic, not superstition. Tylor extended the same rationalism to sacrifice, reading gift-offerings to deities as a rational analogy from human gift-giving to superiors, with irrationality entering only secondarily as ritual practices survived past the point their original meaning lapsed (Stocking, *Victorian Anthropology*, 1987).
- **The [[comparative-method]]** in ethnography, and (in later work) an early statistical/cross-cultural correlational method ("On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions," 1889) — the origin of the Galton's-problem critique of case non-independence. Reviewing the Maine–McLennan debate in 1885, Tylor concluded that "converging research" had established the early general prevalence of female-line kinship reckoning; by 1896, after failing (in correspondence with Lorimer Fison) to locate any actual society exemplifying [[primitive-promiscuity]], he himself noted a growing "reaction" against the theory (Stocking 1987).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Tylor was largely self-taught and had no formal teacher-pupil lineage. His decisive early encounter was with the ethnologist Henry Christy, whom he met in 1856 and accompanied through Mexico. He worked alongside and against contemporaries in the Victorian evolutionist circle: [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]], [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]], John Lubbock, and John Ferguson McLennan. [[frazer-james-george|James George Frazer]] regarded himself as Tylor's disciple and extended the animism-and-[[magic]] program in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* ([[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]; [[frazer-golden-bough-study]]). Tylor's evolutionism was later sharply repudiated by Franz Boas and the historical-particularist school (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). He also carried on a long-running epistemological dispute with [[muller-friedrich-max|Friedrich Max Müller]]: against Müller's view that language constitutively shapes and even precedes thought, Tylor held that mythology rested "on a basis of real and sensible analogy," taking material myth as primary and verbal myth as secondary — a materialist, associationist reading of the mythopoeic mind (Stocking, *Victorian Anthropology*, 1987).

Institutionally, Tylor was central to anthropology's professionalization in Britain: he chaired (with Lane Fox/[[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]) the 1874 committee that produced the first edition of *Notes and Queries on Anthropology*, was appointed Keeper of the Oxford University Museum and Reader in Anthropology in the mid-1880s after a petition by twenty Oxford professors, and published *Anthropology* (1881) — in effect the field's first textbook. Stocking judges Tylor's reputation the most durable of the classical evolutionists (unlike Lubbock, Maine, McLennan, and, in Britain, Spencer): "Tylor's name has never faded from the disciplinary memory of anthropology," an durability Stocking attributes partly to this institutional centrality and to Tylor's longevity (he lived until 1917).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Tylor's unilinear evolutionism, his "comparative method" that lifted customs out of context to array them on a developmental ladder, and his "intellectualist" theory of religion (religion as mistaken primitive science) were the principal targets of twentieth-century anthropology. Boas attacked the comparative method's assumption that similar traits imply parallel independent development; functionalists (Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown) rejected survivals and the armchair reconstruction of origins; Evans-Pritchard criticized the intellectualist "If I were a horse" reasoning imputed to the "primitive philosopher." His racial-developmental hierarchy is now read as a period artefact of colonial-era anthropology. Reappraisals (George Stocking) treat him as the key institution-builder who made anthropology a science even as his substantive theories were superseded.

## Historiography

George W. Stocking Jr.'s *[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Victorian Anthropology]]* (1987) is the standard modern treatment of Tylor and his milieu; it also documents Tylor's 1872 personal investigation of spiritualist séances and mediums, where he privately entertained a "prima facie case" for some unexplained psychic force while publicly using the inquiry only to document fraud — a heterodoxy toward Christianity that Stocking suggests may have functioned psychologically as a substitute for the religious certainty of Tylor's Quaker youth.
