# Kathryn Edin

American sociologist of poverty, family formation, and the lived economy of low-income households. Best known in this wiki (as of first ingest) for *[[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Promises I Can Keep]]* with [[kefalas-maria|Maria Kefalas]] — multi-neighborhood ethnography of unmarried motherhood in Philadelphia and Camden.

## Overview

Edin’s program combines sustained fieldwork with low-income women (and later men) and engagement with demographic trends (nonmarital childbearing, multipartner fertility, relationship instability). *Promises* argues that poor women revere marriage and hold it to a high economic and relational standard, while treating early nonmarital childbearing as a source of meaning and order under constrained opportunity.

## Contributions

- With Kefalas: the **“promises I can keep”** account of delayed marriage amid early motherhood ([[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]).
- Related (not yet ingested here): *Making Ends Meet* (welfare/budget survival with Laura Lein); fatherhood ethnography with Timothy Nelson (*Doing the Best I Can*).
- Concepts: [[being-there-mothering]] as emic mothering standard; class-similar family logics across African American, white, and Puerto Rican poor neighborhoods in their sample.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Engages [[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]] (joblessness, marriageability, [[male-marriageable-pool-index|MMPI]]), [[culture-of-poverty]] debates (rejects simple cultural-deficit readings while documenting local cultural logics), and family demography (Fragile Families / McLanahan; Cherlin on marriage-go-round). Network with Laura Lein, Timothy Nelson, Sara McLanahan noted in *Promises* apparatus.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Sample is purposive and city-specific — not a national probability sample of unmarried mothers.
- Partner behavior largely via women’s narratives; male agency under-specified in *Promises* alone.
- Policy uptake can flatten ethnographic nuance into marriage-promotion scripts the authors themselves complicate.

## Historiography

Primary sources: monographs and articles above. Reception in family sociology, poverty research, and policy debates on TANF-era family formation.
