# William L. Rathje

American archaeologist; co-developer of [[behavioral-archaeology|behavioral archaeology]] with [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]] and [[reid-j-jefferson|J. Jefferson Reid]]; founder of the Tucson **Garbage Project** as the flagship of Strategy 4 (present material culture to describe and explain present human behavior).

## Overview

Arrived at the University of Arizona in spring 1971 (as Schiffer narrates). Schiffer reports that Rathje immediately grasped the importance of [[formation-processes]] and shared the belief that archaeology’s material-culture focus gives a unique perspective among the social and behavioral sciences (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Later, Arizona’s traditional definition of archaeology excluding Projet du Garbage helped spur the behavioral redefinition of the discipline (attributed, same).

## Contributions

- **Four strategies matrix** — coauthor with Reid and Schiffer of the program that defines archaeology as relationships between human behavior and material culture in all times and places, organized as four strategies (Reid 1973 insight; Reid, Rathje, and Schiffer 1974; Reid, Schiffer, and Rathje 1975; Schiffer 1976/1995 Ch. 6) (attributed).
- **Strategy 4 / Garbage Project** — studies present material objects in ongoing cultural systems to describe and explain present behavior (e.g., Tucson ethnic consumption, waste streams); Schiffer cites the project as the Strategy 4 exemplar (Rathje 1974; Harrison, Rathje, and Hughes 1975; attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 6).
- **Fundamental correlates** — with Schiffer (1982 excerpt as 1995 Ch. 15): four dimensions of artifact variability (form, spatial, frequency, relational); techno-/socio-/ideo-functions; behavioral components hierarchy (household → community → region → empire; task groups; institutions) (attributed, Rathje and Schiffer 1982 / Schiffer 1995).
- Ethnoarchaeological caution on informant accuracy: Garbage Project findings figure in Schiffer’s argument that ethnoarchaeologists should prefer observed behavior over emic interview alone (attributed, Schiffer 1978b/1995 Ch. 9).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Arizona behavioral-archaeology circle with Reid and Schiffer. Schiffer’s Tucson Reuse Project (Schiffer, Downing, and McCarthy 1981) was sometimes seen as a derivative of Projet du Garbage — a perception that contributed to Schiffer’s decision to abandon reuse scale-up while remaining intellectually allied (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).
