# Sándor Bökönyi (1926–1994)

Hungarian archaeozoologist (Magyar Nemzeti Múzeum, Budapest); contributed “Archaeological problems and methods of recognizing animal domestication” to *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969|Ucko & Dimbleby 1969]]*.

## Overview

Bökönyi’s seminar paper is a methodological statement on how archaeologists recognize [[animal-domestication|animal domestication]] from bone assemblages — part of the late-1960s consolidation of [[zooarchaeology]] as more than species lists. Details of his criteria and case material are filled from extraction (attributed Bökönyi 1969).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Bökönyi 1969 in Ucko & Dimbleby.)*

- **Definition:** capture/taming of behaviourally suitable animals, removal from natural range and breeding community, maintenance under controlled breeding for profit; intention to breed under artificial conditions; “living food conserves.”
- **Keeping vs breeding:** primitive keeping (no purposeful selection; size decrease) vs true breeding (selection + feeding control; multi-breed; size increase/dwarfs; productivity).
- **Recognition criteria:** age/sex ≠ wild; outside wild range; morphology; art; husbandry objects — last four most reliable; first two most interesting early.
- **Local domestication:** transitional individuals decisive; wild+domestic co-occurrence; capture scenes.
- **Caution:** high immature kill alone ≠ domestication (Hopkins on Shanidar/Mousterian); combine age, sex, element spectrum (Arcikhowski).
- **Tempo:** hornless sheep / twisted-horn goats by 8th millennium B.C. SW Asia; stock into SE Europe by 7th millennium (as of Bökönyi 1969); late Neolithic European “fever of domestication” with milk/wool/draught.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Central European archaeozoology; 1968 London seminar cohort (Berry, Chaplin, Ducos, Grigson, Payne, Clutton-Brock, Reed, et al.).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Morphological and metrical domestication criteria of the 1960s have been refined by demography, isotopes, aDNA (as of later literature post-1969), and geometric morphometrics — treat as foundational method history, not final operationalization.
