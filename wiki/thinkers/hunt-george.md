# George Hunt

## Overview

George Hunt (born c. 1854 per Jacobsen's estimate; death date not given in the ingested source), of Fort Rupert, British Columbia, was [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]'s Kwakiutl collaborator for over forty years and the largest single producer of the Boas–Hunt [[kwakiutl|Kwakiutl]] corpus. [[codere-helen|Helen Codere]] (1966) called the partnership "one of the most productive and enduring ever to exist between an anthropologist and a member of another culture." Hunt first appears in print in Adrian Jacobsen's account of his 1881–83 North Pacific Coast expedition, in which Hunt served as boatman, guide, and interpreter; his association with Boas began in 1886, on Boas's first trip to Kwakiutl territory.

## Contributions

- **Co-author of the corpus.** Hunt is credited co-author of *Kwakiutl Texts* (1905), *Kwakiutl Texts, Second Series* (1906), and *Ethnology of the Kwakiutl* (1921), and acknowledged as major contributor in Boas 1897, 1909, 1920, 1925, and 1930. He is sole author of "The Rival Chiefs, a Kwakiutl Story" (ed. Edward Sapir, Boas Anniversary Volume, 1906). Boas ultimately published over three thousand pages of Kwakiutl texts and translations, most transcribed by Hunt.
- **The [[text-collection]] engine.** Boas taught Hunt to write Kwak'wala at the 1893 Chicago World's Columbian Exposition; from that year Hunt produced written native-language texts continuously for decades, paid forty cents per hour. He also worked with Boas in New York (1903, AMNH exhibits) and Victoria (1922, 1927), and by correspondence from 1893 on.
- **Field researcher in his own right.** Codere stresses Hunt was not a scribe: he recorded food-preparation knowledge through his wife and other women (~300 pages of "recipe" texts, Boas 1921), traveled through Kwakwaka'wakw territory collecting the crest story of every local group, and reportedly became a shaman himself in order to investigate whether shamanism was "true" or "made up" (Boas 1930).
- **Participant standing.** Hunt's crests, potlatches, and Winter Dance performances were authentic and recognized by the Kwakiutl; he appears in the Boas texts as first-person initiate (e.g., his own account of being "made a Sparrow" and his K·ēngalaLEla initiation) and as dated informant (e.g., the Dzawada'enuxw closing ceremonies of March 14, 1895; the 1856 Bella Bella war narrative recorded 1895).
- **Historical testimony supporting Boas's diffusion argument.** Hunt's 1921 family-history texts state that certain winter-ceremonial features arose "only lately, when they became mixed with the Rivers Inlet tribe" — Native corroboration of Boas's argument that the hamatsa complex diffused into Kwakiutl ceremonial from Bella Bella / Rivers Inlet (see [[kwakiutl]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Taught Kwak'wala literacy by [[boas-franz|Boas]] (1893); interpreter for F. W. Putnam's Kwakiutl exhibit at the 1893 World's Fair; earlier field service under Jacobsen (1881–83). His granddaughter Agnes Cranmer was among the elderly Kwakiutl informants who described Boas to Codere in 1951.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The authorship question is live wherever the Boas corpus is used: how much of "Boas's Kwakiutl ethnography" is Hunt's selection, phrasing, and standing in the community? Codere's edition keeps the layer visible (unnumbered source notes identify Hunt manuscripts, e.g. "The Order of a Chief's Potlatches"). Codere also speculates (editorial footnote, 1966) that an eyewitness witchcraft account in Boas 1930 is "presumably George Hunt" — speculation, not asserted identity; the separate shaman-skeptic narrator Qa'sElid (see [[boas-kwakiutl-ethnography-study]]) is not equated with Hunt in the ingested text. Fuller reappraisals of Hunt's authorship (recent Boasiana scholarship) are not yet ingested.

## Historiography

Primary trace: Jacobsen via Woldt (1884); the Boas–Hunt prefaces and co-authored volumes (1897–1930); Codere's 1966 editorial introduction, which assembles the collaboration's history from those sources and 1951 informant testimony.
