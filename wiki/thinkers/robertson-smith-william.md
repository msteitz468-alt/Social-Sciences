## Overview

**William Robertson Smith** (1846–1894) was a Scottish biblical critic and Semiticist whose account of **communal [[sacrifice]]** as a totemic sacrament recast religion as social and ritual in origin — a pivotal influence on both [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]].

## Contributions

- Scottish biblical scholar and Semiticist whose theory of communal sacrifice as a totemic sacrament — the ritual bond of the social group — reframed religion as primarily social and ritual rather than intellectual, deeply influencing Frazer and Durkheim.
- (Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) Born 1846, son of a Free Church of Scotland minister; died 1894 of tuberculosis while Professor of Arabic at Cambridge. Following [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]], he argued [[totemism]] supplied the "necessary machinery" for exogamy and blood-tie recognition in early, heterogeneous kin groups formed partly through marriage by capture; he held the pre-Islamic Arabs had once been "divided into totem-stocks," with survivals in animal/plant stock-names, claimed descent from a totem-animal/plant, and taboos on eating the totem species.
- Smith guided [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] toward writing the Britannica "Taboo" and "Totem" articles (1886) — Frazer's Britannica treatment of taboo was written "under [Smith's] guidance" — but the two later diverged sharply: Frazer disputed Smith's sociological reading of sacrifice-as-communion, insisting instead on "the old gift theory" (Tylor's *do ut des* — sacrifice as propitiation/averting anger rather than communion).
- Reviewing [[westermarck-edward|Westermarck]]'s *History of Human Marriage* (1891), Smith charged it was really "natural history" not "history," resting on the "tacit assumption" that social laws are "formulated instincts," and accused Westermarck's account of exogamy of circularity — assuming the very custom of avoiding in-group marriage that natural selection was meant to explain (position recorded, attributed to Smith).
- Durkheim credited his 1895 course on the sociology of religion, informed by "the reading of the works of Robertson Smith and his school," as marking a "dividing line" in his thought; Stocking attributes to Smith's influence Durkheim's social (not individual) definition of religion, priority of ritual over belief, priority of emotion over intellect, and focus on totemism/sacrifice as the sacred's social ground. [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] (*Sacrifice: Its Nature and Function*) later criticized Smith's residual ties to British evolutionism even while building on him.
- Freud's *Totem and Taboo* "just-so story" (the primal-horde slaying of the father) derived from Smith's totem-meal concept, transmitted via the wider McLennan-to-Frazer totemism literature — Stocking judges this speculative-historical element in Freud contributed to later anthropologists' alienation from psychoanalytic theory.
- Fundamental differences between Smith and Frazer on method, the magic/religion relation, sacrifice, and the idea of progress are documented by Stocking via "careful comparison" of the two figures' work.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Tried for heresy and removed from his Aberdeen chair; mentor to [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]; a decisive influence on [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s sociology of religion. Read by Stocking, alongside [[lang-andrew|Lang]], as one of the two "second generation" evolutionists whose internal critiques (Smith's social/ritual theory of sacrifice; Lang's primitive-monotheism thesis) destabilized core assumptions of Victorian evolutionism from within, in the run-up to the field's post-1896 fracturing (*[[stocking-after-tylor-1995|After Tylor]]*).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Assessed at length in [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]] (attributed); see that source for the historicist reconstruction of this figure's place in the transition from evolutionism to social anthropology. R. R. Marett later disputed with Frazer (by correspondence, 1911) over which of the two was the true heir of Robertson Smith on the priority of ritual to dogma — a mark of Smith's continuing authority a generation after his death.
