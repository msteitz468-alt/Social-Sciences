# Loïc Wacquant

## Overview

Loïc Wacquant is a French-American sociologist known for **[[carnal-sociology]]** — theoretically armed ethnography that takes the body as tool of inquiry — and for structural analyses of the post-Fordist ghetto, advanced marginality, and the carceral state. *[[wacquant-body-and-soul-2004|Body & Soul]]* (2004; French *Corps et âme*) is his apprentice-boxer ethnography of the [[woodlawn-boys-club|Woodlawn Boys Club]] (Chicago South Side, Aug 1988–Feb 1992), conducted while collaborating with [[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]] at the University of Chicago. He is a principal collaborator and interpreter of [[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]] in the Anglophone world (*An Invitation to Reflexive Sociology*, 1992). Acknowledgments in *Body & Soul* also credit UChicago teachers (Wilson, Coleman, Comaroffs, Przeworski, Steinmetz), Harvard Society of Fellows, and the MacArthur Fellows Program. Born trajectory noted in acknowledgments: Century Fellowship brought him from Nouméa, New Caledonia, to Chicago.

## Contributions

From *Body & Soul* (attributed):

- **[[carnal-sociology]]** / observant participation — sociology *from* the body; initiatory immersion; triple challenge (craft, ghetto, corporeal practice).
- **[[pugilistic-habitus]]** and gym as island of order/virtue, school of morality, sanctuary vs street.
- **[[bodily-capital]]** — body as tool+target; corporeal thrift; career-time management.
- Ghetto reconstruction against disorganization and underclass frames as ethnoracial prison / exploitation (bridge to later urban monographs).
- Empirical demonstration of Bourdieu’s learning-by-body thesis in a U.S. urban craft world.
- **Whores, Slaves and Stallions** (*[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]*, 2002; co-editor of the volume) — languages of exploitation and accommodation among professional boxers. Opening anti-dupe thesis: fighters are if anything **hyperconscious** of entering a universe of no-holds-barred exploitation (deception, manipulation, mistreatment; bodily damage customary), not naïve victims of “show business with blood.” Consciousness of exploitation expressed in **three kindred idioms**: (1) **prostitution** — fighter-manager as whore-pimp; “fleshpeddlers” will “fight King Kong for a dime”; (2) **slavery** — ring as plantation, promoters as slave masters (Ashante after underpaid brutal fight: “this is slavery all over again”); (3) **animal husbandry** — dogs, pigs, stallions, livestock (Luke: “How that black stallion doin’?” / keep him in the stable). Illinois 1991 survey: 88% of pros hold services grossly underpaid; 86% consider fair purse ≥ $100/round (twice then-Chicago going rate); actual purses $200–$300. Despite perfervid sense of exploitation, boxers rarely rise in collective revolt; three **vocabularies of motive** (Mills) enable compromise: (a) inescapability of exploitation as *datum brutum* of nether-region American life; (b) entrepreneurship / corporeal self-production of **[[bodily-capital]]** sold on the pugilistic market; (c) individual exceptionalism (“I won’t get hurt”). Culminating thesis: doxic belief in normalcy of exploitation, agency of corporeal entrepreneurship, and individual exceptionalism produces **collective misrecognition** that leads boxers to **collude in their own commercialization** — sinking “to the level of a commodity, and to a most miserable commodity” (Marx). Methods note in the essay: 35 months ethnographic fieldwork and observant participation (1988–91) in a boxing gym in Chicago’s black ghetto; **Stoneland Boys Club (pseudonym)** = the same field site otherwise known as the [[woodlawn-boys-club|Woodlawn Boys Club]]; 2,200 pages of diary; in-depth interviews with all 50 professional boxers active in Illinois summer 1991. Related program pieces: “Pugs at Work” (1995); “A Fleshpeddler at Work” (1998); French *Corps et âme* (2000). *(Attributed — Wacquant, 2001/02 chapter.)*

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reception of carnal sociology and the apprentice-boxer project (gender/masculinity of the site; generalizability; romanticization; narrative vs science; analyst racial position) — record as secondary literature is ingested. *Body & Soul* presents itself as partial, provisional answer to the third challenge.
