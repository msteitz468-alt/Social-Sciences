# Jean Comaroff

> Documented from *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution, Vol. 1]]* (1991). South African–born anthropologist; with [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] a leading practitioner of the [[historical-anthropology|historical anthropology]] of colonialism.

## Overview

Jean Comaroff is an anthropologist whose work fused the ethnography of southern Africa with a critical-theoretical concern for power, signs, and consciousness. Her *Body of Power, Spirit of Resistance* (1985) analyzed the Tshidi ([[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]]) Zionist churches as embodied resistance to colonial and postcolonial domination. With [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] she wrote the two-volume *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991, 1997), the definitive statement of their program.

## Contributions

- Co-developer of **[[colonization-of-consciousness]]** — the argument that colonialism worked most durably through the reshaping of everyday signs, practice, and the body rather than through force alone (see *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*).
- Co-developer of **[[the-long-conversation]]** as a model of the colonial encounter as protracted, mutual, contested exchange of meaning.
- Reworked **[[hegemony]]** and **[[ideology]]** (after [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]] and Raymond Williams) into a distinctive analytic triad with **culture** and **consciousness**.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained in the British social-anthropological tradition (University of Cape Town; PhD London School of Economics), inheriting the southern-African ethnographic lineage of [[schapera-isaac|Isaac Schapera]] and [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] while turning it toward Marxist and post-structuralist theory. Long associated with the University of Chicago; later Harvard. Her career is intertwined with that of her collaborator and spouse [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Critics (see the study page) have questioned the evidentiary asymmetry of the mission-archive method — that Tswana consciousness is largely inferred from European records — and the determinacy implied by "colonization of consciousness." The Comaroffs' reflexive treatment of the archive is itself a much-cited methodological intervention.
