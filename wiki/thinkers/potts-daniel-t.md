# Daniel T. Potts

## Overview

Archaeologist of Mesopotamia, the Arabian Gulf, and Iran, combining excavation, cuneiform philology, and natural-science evidence. Author of *Mesopotamian Civilization: The Material Foundations* (1997), the wiki's ingested source ([[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997]]), a deliberately material-culture-first counterweight to text-dominated Assyriology. Career spanning Harvard training, a chair at the University of Sydney, and later New York University's ISAW.

## Contributions

- **The "material foundations" program.** Potts argued (1997) that classic syntheses (Kramer's *The Sumerians*, Oppenheim's *Ancient Mesopotamia*) privileged religion, literature, and political ideology while neglecting soil, water, subsistence, and production — and that the cuneiform record and archaeological science together can reconstruct these "peripheral" domains. He explicitly framed all scholarship as selective: "there are 'many' Mesopotamias."
- **Against the resource-poverty topos.** Against the standard framing of southern Mesopotamia as devoid of resources beyond water, clay and reeds, Potts documented substantial local exploitation of limestone, glassy quartz, salt, timber, reeds, and bitumen (1997, Ch. IV) — undercutting explanations of Mesopotamian bureaucracy as primarily an import-organizing response to resource poverty.
- **Reflexive centre–periphery critique.** Potts argued both that Mesopotamia's assumed "centrality" among ancient polities is flawed (Potts 1993) and that the discipline itself has a centre–periphery structure — Oppenheim's dictum that texts are "far more valuable, far more relevant, than the monuments" marking archaeology as the field's periphery (see [[centre-periphery]]).
- **"Orientalization of the Occidentals."** For Seleucid Babylonia, Potts argued against Droysen-style "Hellenization": the evidence (Antiochus I's traditional temple dedications, Greek individuals fully integrated into Babylonian legal life at [[uruk|Uruk]]) attests continuity of Asiatic social and religious forms and the absorption of Greeks into them (see [[seleucia-on-the-tigris]]).
- **Methodological "lateral thinking."** His closing program (1997, Ch. XIV) rejects both narrow hypothesis-testing (a critique of processual archaeology's style of explanation) and disciplinary silos, advocating a "broad spectrum approach" combining philology, environmental science, ethnohistory, and laboratory analysis — with Robert McC. Adams's settlement-pattern work cited as the exemplar of cuneiform-informed anthropological archaeology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Potts positioned himself against Oppenheim's text-primacy and drew on Benno Landsberger's *Eigenbegrifflichkeit* ("conceptual autonomy" of the Mesopotamian world) as the strongest rejection of classical/Biblical interpretive lenses. His critique of anthropological archaeologists (Johnson's decision-hierarchy comparativism, Forest's Meillassoux-inspired marriage models, central-place theory imports) is that they lavished zeal on the "tribal" ethnographic literature while ignoring the cuneiform "native informants."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The 1997 volume's deliberately selective coverage (its author's own admission) omits political history and literature almost entirely; its stance toward world-systems framings of the Uruk expansion (contrast Algaze) makes it a party to, not an arbiter of, the centre–periphery debate. No major ethics or misconduct record is noted in the ingested source.
