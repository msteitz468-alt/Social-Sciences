# Isaac Schapera

South African social anthropologist (1905–2003). Contributed “The Political Organization of the Ngwato of Bechuanaland Protectorate” to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) and “Kinship and Marriage among the Tswana” to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Major Tswana ethnographer and legal anthropologist; broader monographs (*Handbook of Tswana Law and Custom*, *Married Life in an African Tribe*) cited but **not yet fully ingested**.

## Overview

Schapera’s APS essay analyzes the [[ngwato|Ngwato]] (BaMangwato), largest and historically best-known of the Tswana (*merafe*) of Bechuanaland Protectorate, as a **Group A** centralized chiefship under British Protectorate overrule. He stressed how under Kgama III (c. 1837–1923) the Ngwato underwent major legal/administrative change and partial Westernization (Christianity, liquor prohibition, education, support of British imperialism), so that by his account they had “departed considerably from their traditional system of government.” Documentation frame includes a May 1936 census (literacy 28% among Ngwato as a factor affecting chief–subject relations) and the 1934 Native Administration / Native Tribunals Proclamations.

## Contributions

From the APS Ngwato claims extract:

- **Tribal constitution:** Eight separate Tswana tribes each with name, reserve, and chief (*kgosi* / *morena*) subject to British Administration and independent of other tribes. Ngwato reserve ~39,000 sq. miles; 1936 population 101,481—only about one-fifth nuclear Ngwato; rest *bafaladi* (“foreigners, refugees”) absorbed by conquest, submission, flight, or secession, often retaining corporate life. Capital Serowe (~25,000; built 1902) concentrated Ngwato proper; foreigners mostly in ~170 outlying villages grouped into administrative districts.
- **Ward (*motse*) hierarchy:** ~300 wards; hereditary headmen; family-groups (*kgotlana*); hereditary principle “runs right through” the system—authority vested in leading families, not only individuals. Foreign community hereditary chieftains continued under the Ngwato chief.
- **Administrative machine:** Sections (*dikgotla*); district governors introduced by Kgama; graded courts with public discussion; chief as “at once its ruler and judge, maker and guardian of its law, and director of its economic life,” and formerly war leader and principal priest/magician.
- **Chief’s powers and reforms:** Installation respect, tribute, free labour, land precedence, legislation (Kgama’s law distinguished from “traditional Native law”); expanded administrative duties under European control—“office chief” vs. “*kgotla* chief” complaint as index of shift.
- **Colonial legal enclosure:** Early curtailments of war, murder jurisdiction, etc.; 1934 Proclamations defining chief’s powers, succession subject to Administration approval, formal Tribal Council, restricted tribunal competence; Tshekedi (Regent from 1926) opposed and sued; Special Court 1936 upheld High Commissioner; net effect—chief and councils still administer, but supreme authority is the Administration via District Commissioner.
- **Checks and public opinion:** Assemblies of headmen and of all men; regiments; deposition machinery; Schapera’s detailed constitutional ethnography of chiefship under dual Native/European authority.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- APS Ngwato account is explicitly **post-Kgama / Protectorate** political organization—not a timeless “traditional Tswana state.” Using it as pure precolonial type without Schapera’s own periodization is a misreading.
- Dual authority (chief + Administration) and 1930s Proclamation struggles are part of the ethnographic object; “Native authority” is co-produced.
- Heterogeneous *bafaladi* majority under a nuclear Ngwato dynasty complicates any simple ethnonym = polity equation.

## Historiography

Schapera is a foundational figure in Tswana ethnography and legal anthropology; this wiki holds APS political organization plus the ASKAM Tswana kinship chapter. Summary page only; no Thinkers Hub entry yet.

His mid-century Tswana corpus is the principal ethnographic backstop for [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff's]] *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991): the Comaroffs read Schapera-era data (on chiefship *bogosi*/*kgosi*, the *kgotla*, rainmaking, and the ancestor cult) back onto the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]] world of ca. 1800–1830 as [[historical-ethnography|controlled ethnographic analogy]] — a retrospective use they flag themselves, and which is the chief methodological caution on their precolonial reconstruction.

## Tswana kinship and marriage (ASKAM, 1950)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, Schapera contributed “Kinship and Marriage among the Tswana”—a comparative field study across Bechuanaland Protectorate tribes, building on *Handbook of Tswana Law and Custom* (1938) and *Married Life in an African Tribe* (1940). Attributed load-bearing claims (range 5; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Terminology:** classificatory pattern within the family; father’s side rooted in *rre*, mother’s in *mme*—FB as *rre-mogolo* / *rrangwane*, FZ as *rrakgadi* (“female father”), MB as *malome* (“male mother”); affine terminology in flux (Afrikaans loans *sebara*, *ausi* especially in eastern tribes).
- **Marriage validity (old law):** betrothal agreement between family-groups + *bogadi* cattle (typically 4–10 head, decided by groom’s people). Temporary matrilocal *go ralala* still in most tribes except Ngwato and Tawana; *bogadi* often delayed. Colonial/Christian change: *bogadi* abolished among Ngwato and Tawana (late 19th c.) and by many Christians elsewhere—consent of family-groups stressed for court recognition.
- **Preferential mating (African anomaly for R-B):** strong encouragement of kinship marriage, especially cross-cousin (*Ntsala wa motho ke mogatse*); preference order MBD → FZD; **agnatic cousin including FBD also approved** (*Ngwana rrangwane, nnyale…* so *bogadi* cattle return to the kraal)—the feature Radcliffe-Brown called “decidedly exceptional” vs Nguni disgust at marrying “sisters.”
- **Secondary unions:** *seantlo* (woman provided for barren/dead wife’s house); leviratic *go tsena mo tlung* (seed to deceased)—no *bogadi*; children jural of original husband.
- **Against terminology-determinism (Rivers line):** among Tswana, little causal connexion among terminology, mating regulations, and other kinship-behaviour rules—none necessarily “determines” the others; explain marriage choices from further features of the social system (parental arrangement; residential association favouring uncles’ daughters; *bogadi* claims for MBD; noble FBD preference as **status/politics**, not cattle retention). Wider Sotho–Nguni comparison deferred to a later publication.
- **Frequency evidence:** genealogical Tables I–III (not re-tabulated here) underwrite the preference order and noble/commoner contrast.
