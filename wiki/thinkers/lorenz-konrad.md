## Overview

Konrad Lorenz (1903–1989), a Nobel laureate and a founder of modern ethology, argued in *On Aggression* that humans carry an innate, biologically-programmed instinct for aggression and territoriality requiring periodic release, a position that fed the popularized "killer ape" thesis of [[ardrey-robert]] and drew on the fossil-interpretive claims of [[dart-raymond]].

## Contributions

Per *Origins* (1977), Lorenz argued (attributed voice) that humans carry "an inescapable legacy of territoriality and aggression, instincts which must be ventilated lest they spill over," and is quoted claiming that early tool-using australopithecines "promptly used their weapons not only to kill game, but fellow members of their species as well." Lorenz proposed competitive sport as a "safety valve" for built-up aggression, and attributed early-human territorial stress partly to "the counter-pressures of hostile neighboring hordes." This forms one pole of the [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]. Leakey & Lewin (see [[leakey-richard]], [[lewin-roger]]) counter each claim directly: they argue research shows sport correlates with more, not less, violence; that Lorenz's image of hostile neighboring bands is "more dramatically evocative than it is based in fact," since neighboring hunter-gatherer bands were more likely linked by kinship than hostility; and more broadly that human aggression is not a fixed instinct requiring release but a flexible, culturally-triggered behavior.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A professional ethologist (unlike the popularizer [[ardrey-robert]]), whose instinct theory of aggression was drawn on by Ardrey and paired in *Origins*' account with [[dart-raymond]]'s fossil-based "killer ape" reading to form the composite position Leakey & Lewin argue against.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Lorenz's specific claims (the "safety valve" function of sport; hostile-neighbor territorial stress) are directly disputed in *Origins* — see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]] for Leakey & Lewin's point-by-point counter-argument.
