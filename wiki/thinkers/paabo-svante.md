# Svante Pääbo

## Overview

Svante Pääbo (b. 1955) is an evolutionary geneticist and director at the Max
Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology in Leipzig, Germany. As
represented in Reich (2018), Pääbo and his colleagues invented much of the core
technology of the genome-wide ancient-DNA revolution, developing it to extract
DNA from extremely old and degraded samples such as archaic
[[neanderthals|Neanderthals]] and [[denisovans|Denisovans]].

## Contributions

- **Archaic genomes.** Led the sequencing of the first Neanderthal draft genome
  and the discovery/sequencing of the [[denisovans|Denisova]] genome (both
  published 2010), which demonstrated interbreeding between archaic and modern
  humans (see [[admixture]]).
- **Method development.** His laboratory (notably Matthias Meyer and Qiaomei Fu)
  developed the in-solution hybridization "capture" technique — using synthetic
  bait sequences to fish target human DNA out of samples overwhelmingly
  dominated by microbial DNA — that made industrial-scale [[ancient-dna]]
  analysis possible.
- **Contamination controls.** Established the stringent laboratory and
  analytical protocols for authenticating genuine ancient sequences against
  modern contamination.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Pääbo took on [[reich-david|David Reich]] for a seven-year "apprenticeship"
beginning in 2007 (Neanderthal and Denisova projects) and helped Reich establish
his own aDNA laboratory in 2013. Nadin Rohland, Reich's laboratory partner,
likewise trained under Pääbo. The Leipzig laboratory is thus the training hub
from which the U.S. genome-wide aDNA program descends.
