# João Biehl

## Overview

João Guilherme Biehl is a Brazilian-born medical anthropologist (Princeton) whose ethnography
*[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita: Life in a Zone of Social Abandonment]]* (2005) made a single life —
Catarina's — the entry point into the political economy of care, psychiatry, and abandonment in
post-democratization southern Brazil. He grew up in Novo Hamburgo near Porto Alegre (family rural-to-urban migration; classmates often left school for shoe factories); his path to Vita began in
AIDS and public-health fieldwork (from March 1995, introduced by human-rights activist Gerson Winkler) and turned into a multi-year reconstruction
of one woman's trajectory through family, clinics, pharmaceuticals, genetics, and a dump-site asylum. Last saw Catarina in August 2003; she died at Vita on 15 September 2003.

## Contributions

- **Zones of social abandonment.** Named and analyzed **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones
  of social abandonment]]** — Vita and hundreds of similar "geriatric houses" — as places where living beings go when no longer counted as people, while remaining entangled with family pensions, public psychiatry, charity politics, and the state.
- **Pharmaceutical being.** Argued (attributed) that people like Catarina become
  **[[pharmaceutical-being|pharmaceutical beings]]**: subjects whose identities, dependencies,
  and trajectories are organized by medication regimens rather than by sustained clinical or
  familial care; drug names enter self-naming (Catkini).
- **Ex-human and social psychosis.** Working concepts: the **[[ex-human]]** (from Catarina's "I am an ex") for residual personhood with language and desire; **[[social-psychosis]]** for the materials and relations that effect a "normal" order of which the labeled-mad person is a leftover.
- **Life history as method and ethics.** *Vita* is a single-case **[[life-history-method|life
  history]]** reconstructing medical archives ("file self"), family interviews, genetic diagnosis
  (Machado-Joseph / SCA3 with Laura Bannach Jardim), and Catarina's 21-notebook "dictionary" — treating her writing as theory of her own condition. Dialogic ethnography; oral and written consent (1999).
- **Biology and ethics together.** Genetic confirmation of ataxia reopened "madness" as contingent categorization without reducing abandonment to a missed diagnosis; engaged human-rights reporting, transfer attempts, children's visit, and long-term care support as part of the research act.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Single-case intensity raises classic generalizability questions; Biehl treats Catarina as a
  pathway into structures, not as a statistical type.
- Ethical questions of engagement, intervention, and representation in zones of abandonment
  (arranging care, genetic testing, public visibility) belong to the study's afterlife — see [[biehl-vita-study]] and [[hubs/studies/biehl-vita-study]].
- Biehl's own worry that "ex-human" might generate philosophical distance is recorded as self-critique.
