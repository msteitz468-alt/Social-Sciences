# Basil Bernstein

British sociologist of education and language (1924–2000), principal intellectual debt acknowledged by [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] in *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]*. Concepts: [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]. Douglas's adaptation: [[grid-group-analysis]], [[grid-and-group]]. Primary sociolinguistic statement: [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]; contested in [[deficit-difference-debate]].

## Overview

Bernstein's research on **speech codes** and later on **curriculum as boundary system** supplied Douglas with the two-variable control model she adapted from London families to comparative cosmology. Douglas called *Natural Symbols* "the other side" of his thesis once *Class, Codes and Control* (1971) appeared: he reads power under curriculum and speech; she reads power under overt cosmology and ritual form.

## Contributions

- **Restricted vs elaborated codes** — condensed shared-context speech vs explicit individualized elaboration.
- **Positional vs personal family control** — diagrams Douglas adapts when defining grid and group.
- **Curriculum boundaries** — strong vs weak classification/framing; Douglas analogizes to ritual vs anti-ritual.

### Sociolinguistic primary statement (1972 chapter)

In "A Sociolinguistic Approach to Socialization; with Some Reference to Educability"
(in Gumperz & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972), Bernstein gives
his own primary statement of the code distinction, independent of Douglas's later
adaptation:

- **Elaborated codes**: syntactic alternatives hard to predict across a
  representative range, flexibly organized, wide vocabulary differentiation.
  **Restricted codes**: syntactic alternatives easy to predict, drawn from a narrow
  range, rigid organization. Codes are defined by predictability of syntactic
  choice, not by vocabulary or "jargon" as such, and are explicitly **not
  evaluative** — each is functionally fitted to its social matrix, with its own
  "vast potential... considerable metaphoric range and unique esthetic capacity."
- Codes are cultural, not genetic, controls on performance (he distances his use
  from Chomsky's competence/performance distinction: "the codes refer to cultural
  not genetic controls upon the options speakers take up... codes refer to
  performance not to competence").
- Ties codes to **family type**: **positional families** (decision-making by
  formal status — age, sex, generational rank; role-segregated; "closed"
  communication) vs. **person-oriented families** (decisions on individual
  psychological qualities; "open" communication, greater role discretion), and to
  three modes of social control — imperative, positional appeal, personal appeal
  (the latter two tending toward shame vs. guilt respectively).
- Links restricted code, only as a hedged broad tendency, to positional
  working-class families (high population density, low mobility, occupational
  solidarity via collective rather than individual assertion) — he states this
  patterning may be weakening in postwar England and explicitly declines to extend
  the account to the US case of Black Americans' speech, citing his own lack of
  expertise there.
- **Explicitly denies that restricted code = linguistic or cognitive deficit**,
  citing Labov (1969a) approvingly: educability problems for restricted-code
  children have their "source... not in the genetic code but in the culturally
  determined communication code," and the restricted code "should not be
  disvalued" — he places the burden of adaptation on the school, not the child.
  This chapter and its reception are the anchor for [[deficit-difference-debate]];
  see that page for the difference/interactional counter-positions (Labov, Gumperz).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Speech-code work is contested in educational sociology (deficit readings, class essentialism charges). Douglas's ritual analogy inherits those risks; she flags the adaptation's crudeness.
