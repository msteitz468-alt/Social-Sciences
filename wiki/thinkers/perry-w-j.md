## Overview

**William James Perry** (1887–1949) was a British diffusionist whose *The Children of the Sun* (1923) systematized [[elliot-smith-grafton|Elliot Smith]]'s claim that a single 'Archaic Civilization' of sun-worshipping voyagers carried culture out of Egypt across the globe.

The son of an Anglican clergyman/headmaster, Perry won a mathematics scholarship to Cambridge in 1906, attended lectures by Haddon and [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], and by 1910 had become closely acquainted with Rivers — by 1915 the two addressed each other in correspondence as "My Dear Uncle" and "My Dear Nephew" *(source: [[stocking-after-tylor-1995]])*. Perry took a Yorkshire grammar-school teaching post in 1911 while remaining in close correspondence with Rivers. In autumn 1913, Rivers directed Perry to collect evidence for megalithic monuments and sun-cult in Indonesia; the resulting study, dedicated to Rivers and published by Manchester University Press at [[elliot-smith-grafton|Elliot Smith]]'s instigation, argued Indonesian stoneworks were built by migrants seeking gold and wealth who also introduced terraced irrigation, metalworking, rice-growing, sun-cult, and phallic/fertility symbolism. Elliot Smith obtained Perry a Manchester appointment within a year. As Reader in Comparative Religion at Manchester, Perry elaborated Elliot Smith's ideas into a fuller theory of the origin of magic and religion: a Paleolithic "Great Mother" belief giving way to Egyptian food-producers' irrigation god-kings and mummification-derived immortality belief, generating a search for "givers of life" (polished stone, crystals, gold, pearl shells) that then spread worldwide. He published *The Children of the Sun* in 1923 and moved that year from Manchester to University College London as Reader in Cultural Anthropology in Elliot Smith's Anatomy Department, where his office became a discussion hub attended by V. Gordon Childe, Daryll Forde, and A. P. Elkin. His final years were affected by Parkinson's disease, with onset following Rivers's death in 1922.

## Contributions

- British diffusionist who, with Elliot Smith, elaborated the 'Children of the Sun' thesis tracing a single 'Archaic Civilization' of sun-worshipping culture-bearers spreading from Egypt.
- **The "Children of the Sun" / "Archaic Civilization" thesis.** *(Attributed.)* Perry traced a uniform "culture-sequence" among "food-producers" worldwide (he held little interest in "food-gatherers") leading everywhere back to a single "archaic civilization" carried by "the children of the sun," evidenced by culture-hero myths present across regions. He proposed methodologically to "accept the tradition that culture was introduced, until it is disproved by irrefutable evidence" — explicitly echoing Andrew Lang's stance on primitive monotheism. Unlike Elliot Smith, Perry showed little interest in "race" as such, focusing on cultural diffusion and, reflecting his training under Rivers, on social-structural correlations between political/economic organization and religion. He expanded the "archaic civilization" trait inventory to include human sacrifice, mother-goddess worship, mother-right, totemic clans, dual organization, exogamy, and a two-part ruling class ("children of the sun," born of the sky-world and practicing incestuous unions, versus underworld-associated war chiefs).
- **Diffusion as an act of will.** *(Attributed.)* Perry argued diffusion was driven by "the mind of man" — "intelligence, deliberation, and will" — rather than geographic or climatic determinism, explicitly citing Frazer against Westermarck's biological-causal account.
- **Degeneration and Spenglerian pessimism.** *(Attributed — Stocking's characterization.)* Unlike Frazer's upward-progress narrative in *The Golden Bough*, Perry held that every culture-sequence moving away from the Egyptian source was one of degeneration: the archaic civilization's own internal dual-organization hostility bred warrior classes, subjection of women, and a "class system" that eventually "smashed" it, producing "fresh communities of a warlike nature." Stocking notes overtones of Spenglerian pessimism and links the imaginative structure of the book to the experience of the First World War. Perry nonetheless held — reflecting Rivers's "mildly socialist" politics — that this cycle was not inevitable: true food-gathering "primitives" lived in "perfect peace and perfect equality," the "great democratic movement" of the modern era was tending toward less violent behavior, and social psychology might make it "possible to order society on the right lines."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Reader in comparative religion at University College London under Elliot Smith; trained directly under [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], who directed his 1913 Indonesian megalith survey; leading popularizer of hyper-diffusionism alongside Elliot Smith. His UCL office (from 1923) was a gathering point for V. Gordon Childe, Daryll Forde, and A. P. Elkin.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed — contemporaries quoted by Stocking.)* Haddon attacked Perry's evidentiary standards directly: "careful students always bear in mind that the historical, as well as the scientific, method demands that one should quote correctly, consult the original sources at first-hand..." — and faulted his failure to address "racial and linguistic problems." Marett accepted the diffusion of a megalithic people into Oceania but found Perry's lumping of diverse peoples as "food-gatherers" absurd. Meyer Fortes later judged Perry's book fundamentally flawed by its assumption of "the historicity of myth," charging that "by pressing analogies... almost anything can be proved." Stocking situates Perry within the British diffusionist current (Rivers → Elliot Smith/Perry → [[hocart-a-m|Hocart]]) that he argues has been unfairly reduced, in disciplinary memory, to a mere aberration rather than recognized as a serious paradigmatic rival to evolutionism — see [[diffusionism]].
