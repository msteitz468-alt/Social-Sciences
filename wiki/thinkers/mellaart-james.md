# James Mellaart

British archaeologist of Anatolian prehistory; director of the 1961–1965 excavations at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] that made the site a global Neolithic landmark. His 1967 monograph *[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|Çatal Hüyük: A Neolithic Town in Anatolia]]* remains the founding narrative of the site’s art, architecture, and “shrine” religion.

## Overview

Mellaart surveyed the Konya Plain from 1951 (British Institute of Archaeology at Ankara) and formally “discovered” the double mound of Çatalhöyük in November 1958 with Alan Hall and David French. Four seasons of excavation (1961–65) produced the dense plans, wall-paintings, plaster reliefs, figurines, and burial series published in the 1967 book and specialist papers. He also excavated Hacılar and wrote broader Near Eastern Neolithic syntheses.

## Contributions

- **Site monograph as public archaeology:** *[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-study|Çatal Hüyük]]* (1967) framed the settlement as a seventh-millennium “town,” with roof-access cellular plan, ~40 of 139 excavated rooms as shrines, goddess/bull cult imagery, secondary burial with vulture excarnation, and early mixed farming with long-distance raw materials (obsidian, metals, pigments).
- **Anatolian Neolithic on the map:** Challenged Fertile-Crescent monocentrism by placing Anatolian plateau sites (Çatalhöyük, Hacılar, Mersin deep levels) in multi-foci Neolithic development (attributed, 1967 Introduction).
- **Iconographic type-series:** Goddess figurines, enthroned mistress of animals, bucrania, and vulture paintings became the default visual vocabulary for Neolithic religion debates — later re-read by [[cauvin-jacques|Cauvin]], goddess-spirituality publics, and [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]-project contextualists.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Postwar Anatolian field archaeology under British Institute auspices; general-editor framing by Mortimer Wheeler in the 1967 volume. Specialist collaborators: Hans Helbaek (palaeobotany), Dexter Perkins Jr and Pierre Ducos (fauna), Mlle Ferembach (physical anthropology). Afterlife interlocutors include [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] (1993– project renegotiating shrine/house and reflexive method) and textbook synthesists ([[mithen-steven|Mithen]], Wenke & Olszewski).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Shrine vs house:** Mellaart’s cult-room criteria (elaborate painting, reliefs, bucrania, cult statues, ochre burials) were challenged by microstratigraphy and contextual analysis under Hodder — Wendy Matthews and others argued routine domestic practice in “shrines”; ritual and domestic may not have been emically separate (as reported in later syntheses).
- **Goddess/matriarchy reception:** Popular and some scholarly readings extrapolated mother-goddess/matriarchal society from the art; feminist and post-processual critiques reject universal fertility-goddess models — see [[mother-goddess]].
- **Chronology:** Mid-1960s C-14 framework (Levels X–II ≈ 6500–5700 BC uncalibrated/legacy) superseded by calibrated project sequences; keep Mellaart dates attributed.
- **Career controversies:** Later authenticity disputes (Dorak treasure and related affairs) are part of disciplinary memory; they do not erase co-documented 1960s field finds but require care with claims resting solely on Mellaart’s uncorroborated personal testimony. State factually; do not prosecute or hagiographize.
