# James Clifford

Historian of anthropology and cultural critic (b. 1945); co-editor with [[marcus-george-e|George E. Marcus]] of *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986) and author of its Introduction ("[[partial-truths|Partial Truths]]") and of "On [[ethnographic-allegory|Ethnographic Allegory]]." Central figure of the [[writing-culture]] program and [[writing-culture-debate]].

> **Deep analysis (program):** [[hubs/theory/writing-culture|Writing Culture Theory Hub]]

## Overview

Clifford’s contribution in the ingested volume frames ethnography as **writing** under political and epistemological crisis after decolonisation, and analyzes ethnographies as **allegories**. He co-organized (with Marcus) the 1984 SAR Santa Fe Advanced Seminar. Later monographs (*The Predicament of Culture* 1988, etc.) are not yet ingested.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Clifford in Writing Culture 1986.)*

### Introduction: Partial Truths

- **Writing is central** — not occulted method. Ideology of transparent representation and immediate experience has crumbled; poetic and political are inseparable; science is in, not above, historical and linguistic processes.
- **Invention vs representation** — ethnography “always caught up in the invention, not the representation, of cultures” (citing Wagner 1975).
- **[[partial-truths]] / true fictions** — best ethnographies are systems or economies of truth; committed and incomplete; power and history work through them beyond authors’ control. Cree hunter anecdote: “I can only tell what I know.”
- **Six determinations** of ethnographic writing: contextual, rhetorical, institutional, generic, political, historical.
- **Colonial reckoning map** — Leiris 1950, Balandier, Asad 1973, Hymes; shift from Mead-as-authority to Deloria’s unflattering figure; ethnography enacts power but may be counter-hegemonic.
- **Feminism gap** — no primarily feminist essays in the seminar; exclusions left blatant.
- **Not only literature** — always writing; loss of Archimedean point is not simple relativism.

### On Ethnographic Allegory

- Ethnographies always function as **[[ethnographic-allegory|allegories]]**.
- *Nisa* as multi-register allegory; Mead/Freeman as diptych allegories of plasticity vs constraint ([[mead-freeman-debate]]); salvage and pastoral as temporal allegories.
- Ethics: allegories must be owned, not denied under realism’s mask.

### Editorial role

- Co-editor with Marcus; dual aim of textual critique and analysis of its political limits; non-unity of essays preserved.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborator: [[marcus-george-e]]. Engages [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] (interpretive textualism radicalized and critiqued), [[asad-talal]], literary theory (Hayden White, Bakhtin), colonial critique lineage. Prior essay “On Ethnographic Authority” (1983a) feeds [[ethnographic-authority]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Feminism gap (Clifford acknowledges); later charges of textualism and “invention” slogan misread as free fictionalizing. See [[writing-culture-debate]]; internal volume poles in Asad and Rabinow.
