# Byron J. Good

American medical anthropologist (Harvard Medical School), a principal architect — with
[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]] and Leon Eisenberg — of
[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology|meaning-centered medical anthropology]], and the
program's most philosophically explicit theorist.

## Overview

Good's career-long project, culminating in the 1990 Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures
(*[[good-medicine-rationality-1994|Medicine, Rationality, and Experience]]*, 1994), is a
critique of the "problem of belief": the empiricist habit of describing others' medicine as
"belief" and biomedicine as "knowledge." He traces this to a historically recent narrowing of
the English word "belief" (following his teacher Wilfred Cantwell Smith) and to the
rationality-debate tradition anchored in [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]. His
constructive alternative treats all medicine — biomedicine included — as a Cassirerian symbolic
form whose formative practices constitute its objects.

## Contributions

- **[[semantic-illness-network|Semantic network analysis]]** (1977, from the "heart distress"
  study in Maragheh, Iran): illness as a "syndrome of meaning and experience," a set of words,
  situations, and feelings that run together — an early alternative to referential/cognitivist
  accounts of illness terms; self-critiqued in 1994 for neglecting power and dialogue.
- **Four-approach taxonomy of medical anthropology** (empiricist, cognitive, meaning-centered,
  critical) — the field-defining reading in ch. 2 of the 1994 book, now a standard map of the
  subfield (attributed).
- **Medicine as symbolic form**: the Harvard Medical School ethnography showing how "seeing,
  writing, speaking" reconstruct the person as body/case/patient.
- **Narrative analytics of illness**: emplotment, subjunctivizing tactics, and narrative
  authority, developed through the 1988 Turkish epilepsy study — extending
  [[illness-narrative]] theory beyond Kleinman's clinical framing.
- Co-author of the founding meaning-centered manifesto, Kleinman, Eisenberg & Good, "Culture,
  Illness, and Care" (1978), and of [[explanatory-models]]-era clinical anthropology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Undergraduate year at the University of Nigeria (1964–65) among Igbo and Yoruba classmates,
which Good credits as the intuitive ground of his symbolic-anthropology commitments; trained
under Wilfred Cantwell Smith (comparative religion — the source of the "belief" critique), and
at the University of Chicago under [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]],
[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]], and Lloyd Fallers. Long-term collaborator and spouse of
Mary-Jo DelVecchio Good, with whom nearly all his fieldwork was conducted. Career partnership
with [[kleinman-arthur|Kleinman]] at Harvard defines the interpretive program's institutional
core.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

[[critical-medical-anthropology]] charges the interpretive program (Good included) with
underweighting political economy; Good's counter-critique — that reading others' illness
meanings as "mystification" repeats the rationalist "superstition" move — is recorded on that
page. Good is unusual among the program's principals in publishing a sustained self-critique of
his own method (the 1994 reappraisal of semantic networks).
