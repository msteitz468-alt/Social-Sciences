# Ernest Gellner

Philosopher and social anthropologist (1925–1995); interdisciplinary figure in late [[british-social-anthropology|British social anthropology]] whom [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] lists among the tradition’s most influential late exponents and personal friends among the “elders” (*[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]*, 2015). Densified here from Kuper’s historiography; full program (nationalism, Islam, philosophy of history) requires primary-text ingest.

## Overview

Kuper (preface, 4th ed.) groups Gellner with Bourdieu, [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]], and Latour as **interdisciplinary** figures in the transformed, post-classical discourse — anthropology no longer a specifically British or Franco-British guild project. Personal friend of Kuper’s. Fieldwork-based anthropology (*Saints of the Atlas*, 1969) sat beside philosophy-of-social-science critique and late world-historical synthesis (*Plough, Sword and Book*). John Hall’s *Ernest Gellner* (2012) is among the biographies Kuper recommends; Kuper also conducted a *Current Anthropology* interview (1991).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Kuper 2015 claims; not a substitute for Gellner’s own books.)*

- **Idealism charge against Leach (1958).** In a long appreciation in *Mind*, Gellner accused [[leach-edmund|Edmund Leach]] of Idealism — reality known only through a filter of ideas. Leach did not contest; the 1964 *Highland Burma* preface conceded that systemic ordering via verbal categories is “in the final analysis, illusory.” Kuper reads the charge as marking Leach’s “radical break with British empiricism,” and the sting of “empiricist puritanism” as part of what drove Leach to the detail-rich *Pul Eliya* polemic. See [[leach-edmund]].
- **Functionalism as doctrine of good ethnography.** Via Kuper (citing Gellner *Cause and Meaning in the Social Sciences*, 1973): Malinowskians insist anthropology differs from “mere” ethnography by having theory; on investigation, Functionalism often turns out largely “the doctrine that anthropology should be nothing but ethnography. Or rather — good ethnography.”
- **Malinowski as profession-builder.** Gellner (*Language and Solitude*, 1998, via Kuper): Malinowski “in effect created a new subject and profession, dominated it, and turned its practitioners into an astonishingly cohesive and effective guild.” Also: early functionalist studies sometimes most interesting when exploding established theories.
- **Exception to equilibrium models.** Kuper lists Gellner’s Berber Atlas work (*Saints of the Atlas* 1969) with Evans-Pritchard’s Sanusi Cyrenaica and Leach’s Kachin as rare classical-era exceptions that already treated societies without assuming rigid boundaries and equilibrium — material for the sixties crisis of [[structural-functionalism]].
- **Comparative world history.** In Kuper’s Ch. 7 map of post-imperial identity answers, Gellner’s *Plough, Sword and Book* stands with [[goody-jack|Goody]]’s comparative books as the British comparative-wing reply — testing large historical generalizations beyond WEIRD cases.

### Target of Asad's cultural-translation critique

*(Attributed to Asad, Genealogies of Religion 1993, ch. 5 — see [[cultural-translation]] and [[asad-genealogies-of-religion-1993]].)*

Asad's most sustained polemic against a contemporary is directed at Gellner's "Concepts and Society," chosen "because of its wide use in British undergraduate anthropology courses." Gellner's positions there (recorded as positions): functionalist anthropologists extend "excessive charity" in contextual interpretation of alien beliefs; the fictitious concept "bobility" shows how charitable interpretation obscures the socially functional incoherence of concepts; his Berber *baraka*/*agurram* material shows "what appears to be vox Dei is in reality vox populi." Asad's rebuttals: assuming coherence is an essential step in explanation, not a defense; Gellner never demonstrates incoherence, only political functionality; his "stigmata"/"consider-the-lilies" vocabulary smuggles Christian overtones into the Berber material with "a knowing Gibbonian smile"; and Gellner's criticism of "savages... some distance away" who cannot contest the account violates the condition that "for criticism to be responsible, it must always be addressed to someone who can contest it." Asad also speculates that the essay's diagrammatic style endures because it "promises visible results that can readily be graded." Gellner's reply tradition (his 1990s exchanges with relativism critics) is not yet ingested; record both sides when it is.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Late British social anthropology / LSE–Cambridge orbit; engages Malinowskian and structuralist inheritances critically (per Kuper). Interlocutor of Leach on epistemology of models; friend of Kuper; reader acknowledged by Douglas (*Purity and Danger* acknowledgements). Full teacher/student chains not yet documented from primary sources.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Gellner’s Idealism diagnosis of Leach is itself a position in the epistemology of models vs empiricist ethnography — record both sides; do not adjudicate in wiki voice. Broader reception of his nationalism and Islamic modernity theses is outside this source’s scope.
