# Richard B. Lee (b. 1937)

Canadian anthropologist whose fieldwork on the **Dobe !Kung** (Ju/'hoansi) of the Kalahari produced the quantitative work-effort and caloric data that became the empirical anchor of the **[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]** thesis. With [[devore-irven|Irven DeVore]] he convened the 1966 symposium and edited the volume *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968), a landmark restatement of hunter-gatherer studies, in which his own chapter **"[[lee-what-hunters-do-study|What Hunters Do for a Living]]"** appears. That volume is now primary-ingested, so the Dobe figures below are attributed to Lee's own chapter (and were also mobilized by Sahlins); Lee's monographs (*The !Kung San*, 1979) remain outstanding.

## Overview

*(Attributed — Lee, as reported by Sahlins.)* Lee's day-by-day study of a Dobe !Kung dry-season camp (population 41, observed four weeks in July–August 1964) found that adult foragers spent only about **two hours nine minutes per day** obtaining food — roughly a 15-hour work week — while achieving an average **2,140 calories per capita per day** against an estimated requirement near 1,975. He found that only about 65% of the population were "effective" food producers, who worked about 36% of the time, and that a substantial fraction (the young and the old) did not work at all. These figures anchored Sahlins' argument that foragers meet their wants with modest, intermittent labor.

## Contributions

- **Hunter-gatherer time-allocation and work-effort measurement.** *(Attributed.)* Lee's Dobe day-diaries quantified subsistence labor input against caloric output, converting an impressionistic picture of forager life into numbers that Sahlins mobilized for the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis. See also the parallel McCarthy & McArthur Arnhem Land study.
- **The Dobe !Kung / Ju/'hoansi as a reference case** for foraging economics, sharing, and mobility — including the observation that non-accumulation follows from a nomadic mode of life (portability limits), and that storage among foragers is often "economically undesirable and socially unachievable." Feeds [[underproduction]].
- **[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]] (1968, ed. with DeVore)** — the field-defining collection that reframed hunter-gatherers as the "[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]" (a phrase from Sahlins' contribution) rather than as populations on the edge of starvation. Lee's chapter also refrained the diet: gathering (women's work), not hunting, supplies most calories in most foraging societies (see [[hunter-gatherer]], [[sexual-division-of-labor]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Lee worked in the ecological/materialist wing of postwar American anthropology; his Kalahari research program (the "Harvard Kalahari Project") made the Dobe !Kung one of the most intensively documented foraging populations. Sahlins drew on Lee's and Lorna Marshall's !Kung material as the substantivist counter-evidence to the "subsistence economy" image of foragers. ## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* The !Kung/Kalahari evidence became the terrain of two major disputes. First, the durability of Lee's affluence figures: later re-examinations questioned whether his work-time accounting excluded food processing, tool maintenance, and childcare, and whether !Kung health, mortality, and seasonal hunger were consistent with "affluence" (see [[original-affluent-society-debate]]). Second, the **Kalahari debate** (Wilmsen and others vs. Lee) over whether the Dobe !Kung were a pristine foraging society or a marginalized people long enmeshed in trade and herding economies — bearing directly on how representative the Dobe are of the Paleolithic. Sahlins himself flagged the underlying worry that surviving foragers are "displaced persons" in marginal refuges.

## Historiography

Lee is a standard reference in hunter-gatherer studies and in debates over foraging affluence and the Kalahari. A fuller treatment awaits ingestion of *Man the Hunter* (1968) and *The !Kung San* (1979).
