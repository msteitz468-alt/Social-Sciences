# Richard B. Lee (b. 1937)

Canadian anthropologist whose fieldwork on the **Dobe !Kung** (Ju/'hoansi) of the Kalahari produced the quantitative work-effort and caloric data that became the empirical anchor of the **[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]** thesis. With [[devore-irven|Irven DeVore]] he convened the 1966 symposium and edited the volume *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968), a landmark restatement of hunter-gatherer studies, in which his own chapter **"[[lee-what-hunters-do-study|What Hunters Do for a Living]]"** appears. That volume and the monograph **[[lee-kung-san-1979|*The !Kung San: Men, Women, and Work in a Foraging Society*]]** (1979) are now primary-ingested; the full study object is [[lee-kung-san-study]]. *The Dobe Ju/'hoansi* (1984 and later editions) remains outstanding as a separate source.

## Overview

*(Attributed — Lee 1968 / Sahlins' use of Lee.)* Lee's day-by-day study of a Dobe !Kung dry-season camp (observed four weeks in July–August 1964) first appeared in *Man the Hunter* with roughly a **~2.5-day subsistence week**, **~2,140 Cal** and **93.1 g protein** per capita/day against an estimated requirement near 1,975 Cal — figures Sahlins mobilized for the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis.

*(Attributed — Lee 1979, Chs. 9–10; recalculated vs 1968a/1969a.)* In *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]*, Lee **recalculated** the same 28-day diary (6 July–2 August 1964) with later information: after correcting ethnographer truck-assisted mongongo trips, adults averaged **~2.4 subsistence workdays per week**; per-capita diet **2,355 Cal** and **96.3 g protein**/day; food output exceeded estimated requirements by ~**380 Cal**/person/day. Adding manufacture/maintenance and housework raised total work to about **44.5 hrs/week for men**, **40.1 for women**, **~42 hrs** both-sex average (Table 9.12) — far above pure "subsistence days" but still the load-bearing quantitative case for modest forager work effort. A separate 20-month anthropometric program (n=641, 1967–9) found only modest seasonal adult weight loss (**~1–2%** July high to January low), far short of African agricultural "hungry season" losses of **4–6.5%**. Gathering supplied ~**70%** of calories; hunting ~**30%**. See [[lee-what-hunters-do-study]] for how 1979 supersedes 1968 figures; [[original-affluent-society-debate]] for reception.

## Contributions

- **Hunter-gatherer time-allocation and work-effort measurement.** *(Attributed.)* Lee's Dobe day-diaries quantified subsistence labor input against caloric output, converting an impressionistic picture of forager life into numbers that Sahlins mobilized for the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis. *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* (1979) expanded the work definition to three categories (subsistence; manufacture/maintenance; housework), recalculated 1964 inputs, and added multi-season weight monitoring. See also the parallel McCarthy & McArthur Arnhem Land study.
- **The Dobe !Kung / Ju/'hoansi as a reference case** for foraging economics, sharing, mobility, land (*n!ore*), conflict, and transition under herding/cash/state incorporation — including the argument that non-accumulation follows from a nomadic mode of life and that the environment itself functions as storehouse. Feeds [[underproduction]], [[egalitarian-society]], [[hunter-gatherer]].
- **[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]] (1968, ed. with DeVore)** — the field-defining collection that reframed hunter-gatherers as the "[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]" (a phrase from Sahlins' contribution) rather than as populations on the edge of starvation. Lee's chapter also reframed the diet: gathering (women's work), not hunting, supplies most calories in most foraging societies (see [[hunter-gatherer]], [[sexual-division-of-labor]]).
- ***[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]] (1979)*** — synthesis of Lee's ecology and society research from fieldwork seasons **1963–64, 1967–69, and 1973**. Theoretical stack *(attributed, Introduction)*: Stewardian **cultural ecology**; Marxist **mode of production** (esp. French school / Godelier); ecological **systems** theory ("systems history"). Method *(attributed)*: long-term [[participant-observation]], quantitative core (population, botany, economics, nutrition, demography), internal variation by sex/age/region, multi-year ecological variation, historical placement ~1870–1980. Closing lessons *(attributed, Ch.15)*: dual reconstructive tools — **uniformitarian** ecology/demography plus **Marxist** analysis of consciousness, gender, and social relations of foraging production; foraging as a distinct mode with sharing as its ethical core ("primitive communism" with the proviso that sexual rights are not communal). Study page: [[lee-kung-san-study]].
- **Leveling and egalitarian politics.** *(Attributed — Lee 1979, Ch.1 Christmas ox; Ch.15.)* !Kung "humility-enforcing devices" (insulting the meat / kill; charges of stinginess and arrogance) maintain egalitarian norms; reported political ethos: "Of course we have headmen … each one of us is headman over himself." See [[egalitarian-society]].
- **Energy studies as baseline.** *(Attributed — Lee 1979, Ch.9.)* Work and nutrition studies cannot fully account for social life but provide a comparative baseline and anchor "ephemera of social life" on natural-science foundations after qualitative "anxiety" ethnographies proved inadequate.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Lee worked in the ecological/materialist wing of postwar American anthropology. Project origins *(attributed — Lee 1979 Preface)*: 1961 Berkeley discussions among Lee (then a student), Desmond Clark, Sherwood Washburn, and Irven DeVore about studying a contemporary hunting-gathering society from an evolutionary/ecological perspective. The Harvard Kalahari Research Group (with DeVore) made the Dobe !Kung one of the most intensively documented foraging populations; multidisciplinary results appeared in *Kalahari Hunter-Gatherers* (Lee & DeVore 1976). In the 1979 Introduction Lee situates himself between Steward (culture core) and French Marxist anthropology (mode of production, contradiction, Godelier on regulation/feedback), rejecting both Boasian anti-evolutionism and White's untestable generalism, and insisting contemporary foragers are not "living fossils." Sahlins drew on Lee's and Lorna Marshall's !Kung material as the substantivist counter-evidence to the "subsistence economy" image of foragers. Formal teacher/student relations are not fully documented in the ingested sources (`trained_under`/`trained` left `[[unknown]]`).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* The !Kung/Kalahari evidence became the terrain of two major disputes. First, the durability of Lee's affluence figures: later re-examinations questioned whether his work-time accounting excluded food processing, tool maintenance, and childcare, and whether !Kung health, mortality, and seasonal hunger were consistent with "affluence" (see [[original-affluent-society-debate]]). *Note:* Lee's own 1979 Table 9.12 already folds manufacture and housework into a ~**42-hour** total week; childcare is treated separately as mostly maternal (60–80%) and as redressing apparent male subsistence surplus. Second, the **[[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]** ([[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]] and others vs. Lee) over whether the Dobe !Kung were a pristine foraging society or a people long enmeshed in trade and herding economies — bearing directly on how representative the Dobe are of the Paleolithic. Lee 1979 already revises his own early "pristine" impression (most men had herded; many planted in good years; mafisa cattle clientage widespread) while arguing the foraging adaptation remained viable and that greater seasonal weight loss in Wilmsen's mid-1970s data reflected **cash-economy penetration and sedentarization**, not failure of hunting-gathering *(attributed, Chs. 10, 14–15)*. Wilmsen's primary counter-monograph *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* (1989) is now ingested — underclass history, CaeCae class stratification, year-round calorie range, and physiology-as-class claims densify Position B without wiki-voice adjudication. Sahlins himself flagged the underlying worry that surviving foragers are "displaced persons" in marginal refuges.

## Historiography

Lee is a standard reference in hunter-gatherer studies and in debates over foraging affluence and the Kalahari. Primary-ingested works as of 2026-07-10: *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968, ed. with DeVore — including [[lee-what-hunters-do-study]]), *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* (1979; study page [[lee-kung-san-study]]), and *[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|The Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* (4th ed. 2013) where present.
