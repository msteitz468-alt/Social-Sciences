# Richard Wrangham (b. 1948)

British-American biological anthropologist and primatologist; principal public advocate of the [[cooking-hypothesis|cooking hypothesis]] — the claim that control of fire and cooked food drove the *Homo erectus* transition and made humans biologically dependent on cooked diets. Primary ingested source: *[[wrangham-catching-fire-2009|Catching Fire: How Cooking Made Us Human]]* (Basic Books, 2009).

## Overview

*(Attributed — Wrangham 2009.)* Wrangham synthesizes comparative anatomy, nutrition science, forager ethnography, and paleoanthropological secondary literature into a single research program: humans are "the cooking apes, the creatures of the flame," adapted to cooked food as other species are adapted to signature diets. Cooking, he argues, raised net energy, shrunk guts and jaws, freed time from chewing, and underwrote a [[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labor]] and pair-bonds organized around camp meals. The book is a trade synthesis, not a peer-reviewed monograph; evolutionary and social claims stay in attributed voice.

## Contributions

- **[[cooking-hypothesis]]** — cooking and [[control-of-fire|fire control]], not meat alone, as the driver of the habiline → *Homo erectus* step (~1.9–1.8 Ma as Wrangham dates it), with meat retained for the earlier habiline jump.
- **[[energy-theory-of-cooking]]** — cooking raises usable energy via starch gelatinization, protein denaturation, and softening / reduced digestive cost (Secor python experiments; ileal digestibility data as he reports them).
- **[[obligate-cooked-diet]]** — modern humans do not thrive long-term on raw wild foods; cooking is biological necessity, not luxury (raw-foodist studies; absence of fireless societies).
- **[[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]** — social half of the program: stealable cooked meals, female domestic cooking near-universals, and pair-bonds as economic protection around the hearth.
- **Revision of [[expensive-tissue-hypothesis|Aiello & Wheeler]]** — gut–brain trade-off accepted; cooking assigned to the *erectus* encephalization step rather than meat alone.
- **Critique of [[hunting-hypothesis]]** — Man-the-Hunter cannot explain the two-step fossil sequence or small *erectus* jaws/teeth; raw-food time budgets make hunting–gathering division unworkable without cooking.

## Intellectual Genealogy

*(As recoverable from *Catching Fire* alone.)* Dialogue partners and foils: Washburn & Lancaster's hunting thesis; Aiello & Wheeler (1995) expensive tissue; Loring Brace on late fire/tooth-size; Darwin on fire as late cultural invention; Lévi-Strauss (*The Raw and the Cooked*) as symbolic rather than biological account; Brillat-Savarin, Michael Symons, Felipe Fernández-Armesto, Catherine Perlès, Joop Goudsblom as cooking-history precursors he says lacked a nutritional mechanism. Primatological comparative base draws on his own chimpanzee work (Gombe plant-feeding intervals; Ngogo hunt durations as he reports). ## Critiques and Reappraisals

The program is actively contested on archaeological dating of fire, on the strength of biological-vs-archaeological inference, and on the gender/patriarchy thesis in Chs. 6–7. See [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]]. Ethnographic illustrations are secondary citations (Hadza, !Kung via Marshall, Inuit via Stefansson, Tiwi, Mbuti, Vanatinai, etc.) — not Wrangham's fieldwork; treat as his program's evidence structure, not as primary ethnography.

## Historiography

*Catching Fire* (2009) is the public charter of Wrangham's cooking program. Academic paper trail (journal articles with collaborators on energy and fire) is not primary-ingested here. Thinkers Hub **deferred** — single popular synthesis; fuller biography awaits more of the primary research corpus.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017, Ch. 1 fire section; secondary citation of Wrangham, not a reanalysis of Wrangham’s evidence base.)*

Scott reports Wrangham’s claim that nutritional-efficiency gains from cooking largely account for brains ~three times expected mammalian size, and notes the coincidence of archaeological brain-size surge with hearths/meal remains. Scott then extends the argument in his own register: humans are so fire-adapted that, by the domestication litmus of non-independence, “we have adapted so massively to fire that our species would have no future without it”—fire has “in a real sense domesticated us” (raw-foodists lose weight as illustration). Scott embeds this within a longer *Homo erectus* fire-as-niche-construction and “radius of a meal” landscape narrative; the cooking–brain mechanism is Wrangham’s claim as Scott reports it.
