# Henrietta L. Moore (b. 1957)

British social anthropologist; Wedge 1988 conference discussant and author of the **Epilogue** to *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (1991). Warns that feminist archaeology is not simply “finding women” but theorizing **gender as a structuring principle** — and that “women” is not an unproblematic category.

## Overview

Moore refuses any single narrative that would encompass all understandings of women and production in prehistory. One success of feminist critique in the social sciences, she argues, is that researchers can no longer treat women as a self-evident object: as soon as feminists made women’s activities visible, the object slipped toward the whole theoretical basis of the disciplines. Feminist scholarship is “not in any straightforward way simply about women, and neither is this volume.” Doing a feminist archaeology means recognizing how gender structures the archaeological record and acknowledging the politics of knowledge — not merely identifying women or their activities. Sections develop gender and the division of labour, gender and hypothesis generation, and gender and the inscription of difference.
