# William T. Sanders

## Overview

**William T. Sanders** (1926–2008) was an American archaeologist of Mesoamerica at Pennsylvania State University, the leading exponent of a **[[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecological]] / [[cultural-materialism|materialist]]** approach to Mesoamerican civilization and the pioneer of **regional settlement-pattern survey** in central Mexico. He directed the Teotihuacan Valley Project and the Basin of Mexico survey, whose systematic mapping of sites, populations, and agricultural systems supplied the empirical backbone for his theoretical claims about [[state-formation|state formation]].

## Contributions

- **The [[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]]** (Sanders 1956): ecological complementarity between contrasting highland/lowland zones as the driver of exchange, competition, and centralization — his signature analytical construct.
- **Settlement-pattern method:** the distinction between "community" and "zonal" settlement patterns (Sanders 1956), and full-coverage regional survey as the way to read demography and social organization off the landscape.
- ***[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]*** (1968, with [[price-barbara-j|Barbara J. Price]]): the programmatic synthesis applying [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s ecology, [[multilinear-evolution|multilinear evolution]], and [[service-elman-r|Service]]/[[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s typologies to the Mesoamerican sequence, defending a revised irrigation–centralization thesis ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]).
- ***The Basin of Mexico*** (1979, with Jeffrey Parsons and Robert Santley): the mature statement of the Basin survey.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Worked within the [[cultural-ecology|Stewardian]] and Columbia [[cultural-materialism|cultural-materialist]] tradition (his 1968 book was consulting-edited by [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] and [[harris-marvin|Harris]]). His settlement archaeology parallels [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]]'s Mesopotamian surveys; his students and collaborators (Parsons, Santley, and others) extended the Basin of Mexico program. His symbiotic-region concept was taken up secondhand by [[service-elman-r|Service]] (1975).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Sanders's ecological determinism and confidence that civilization was "inevitable" given the right ecology drew criticism from post-processual and agency-oriented archaeologists; his highland-urban framing has been faulted for treating the lowland Maya as a deviation. Later Mesoamerican chronology (post-AMS Tehuacán maize dates, revised contact-period demography) superseded many 1968 particulars. The settlement-survey method itself, however, is broadly regarded as a durable methodological contribution.

## Historiography

Sanders's work is read as the high-water mark of Mesoamerican cultural ecology; his surveys are foundational data sets still mined by specialists. Assess against [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|Adams 1966]] (the Mesopotamian counterpart) and against the later multi-pathway, agency-sensitive state-formation literature recorded on [[state-formation]].
