# R. R. Marett

**Robert Ranulph Marett** (1866–1943) was an Oxford anthropologist who revised [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s [[animism]] with the notion of **pre-animism** (animatism), locating the roots of religion in emotional awe rather than rational inference.

## Overview

**Robert Ranulph Marett** (1866–1943) was an Oxford anthropologist who revised [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s [[animism]] with the notion of **pre-animism** (animatism), locating the roots of religion in emotional awe rather than rational inference.

## Contributions

- Oxford anthropologist who proposed 'pre-animism' or animatism — an emotional, awe-based stratum of religion prior to Tylorian animism — reframing the origins of religion in less intellectualist terms.
- (Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) Born 1866 in Jersey (Channel Islands), son of a legal scholar/antiquarian; educated Balliol College, Oxford, from 1884, fellow of Exeter College from 1891, Rector of Exeter College from 1929. His unpublished T. H. Green Moral Philosophy Prize essay "The Ethics of Savage Races" brought him into contact with examiner [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], whom he later succeeded (Reader in Social Anthropology at Oxford, 1910, two years after Tylor's 1908 retirement) and whose biographer he became.
- At the British Association meeting, autumn 1899 (drafted on Jersey from a "small bundle of extracts" his wife Nora Kirk had compiled), Marett argued primitive religion was both "wider" and "vaguer" than Tylor's [[animism]] ("belief in spiritual beings"), proposing the term **[[animatism]]** for a prior, more diffuse "basic feeling of awe" preceding articulate belief — savage religion was "something not so much thought out as danced out."
- His 1904 *Folk-Lore* critique of [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s magic→religion→science sequence charged that Frazer's "purely analytical method" had smuggled classificatory categories (magic, religion) into a "pseudo-genetic guise," reifying them as historical stages — an early methodological critique of unilinear stage sequences. He rejected Frazer's "laws of association" (similarity/contiguity) as an adequate explanation of magic, and called Frazer's rationalism an instance of "the psychologist's fallacy" — confusing the observer's standpoint with that of "the mind under observation." His own account held that "theory grew out of practice, rather than the other way about": his paradigm case was the emotional/cathartic act of a rejected suitor burning a former lover's portrait, magic as "imperative willing" via the spell as a "spiritual projectile," rooted in emotion and the Jamesian "will to believe" (explicitly invoking William James's essay of that title).
- Drawing on R. H. Codrington's account of Melanesian *mana*, Marett proposed "**negative mana**" as the concept underlying taboo, and generalized a combined "tabu-mana formula" naming both aspects of supernatural power under the composite category "magico-religious."
- From roughly 1905, under the growing influence of the Durkheimian school (initially difficult to access via *L'Année sociologique* in the Oxford library), Marett argued for a "sociological view of Comparative Religion": religious meaning exists "for the society as a whole" rather than for the "individual tribesman," and ritual — not belief — is the proper starting point of analysis; social psychology must work with "Social Morphology ever at its elbow." Stocking credits Marett as an important early mediator of Durkheimian sociology into British anthropology, predating [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] in this role.
- His methodological essay "The Study of Magico-Religious Facts" (1912, *Notes and Queries*) urged ethnographers to ask "what," not "why," and to collect data "uncoloured by" the observer's own theological or anthropological concepts — an early emic/etic methodological caution.
- Marett's own fieldwork was minimal — five hours observing pygmies in London and one afternoon watching Aboriginal dances during the 1914 British Association meeting in Australia, plus some Jersey archaeological digs — an "armchair anthropologist" in Stocking's phrase, like Frazer. He nonetheless made Exeter College a "nursery of anthropologists": pupils included R. S. Rattray, A. M. Hocart, and E. E. Evans-Pritchard. He organized a 1907 festschrift for Tylor's 75th birthday (contributing an essay again critiquing Frazer, on taboo and [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]]'s legacy), and corresponded and quarreled politely with Frazer (1911) over which of them was the true heir of Robertson Smith on the priority of ritual to dogma.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Reader in Social Anthropology at Oxford; taught within the Tylorian tradition while revising it. Stocking pairs Marett with [[westermarck-edward|Westermarck]] as the two lines of internal critique of the intellectualist tradition after Frazer: Marett's sociologically oriented critique leading toward Radcliffe-Brownian structural-functionalism, Westermarck's biologically oriented critique leading toward Malinowskian functionalism (Stocking's own historiographical framing).
