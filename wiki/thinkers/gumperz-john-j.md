# John J. Gumperz

John Joseph Gumperz (1922–2013) was a linguistic anthropologist, co-founder — with
[[hymes-dell|Dell Hymes]] — of the [[ethnography-of-speaking|ethnography of communication]]
and the principal architect of **[[interactional-sociolinguistics|interactional
sociolinguistics]]**. He edited, with Hymes, the two field-defining anthologies *The
Ethnography of Communication* (1964) and *[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions
in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972), and with the anthropologist Jan-Petter Blom authored the
Hemnesberget study ([[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]) that introduced the distinction
between **situational** and **metaphorical** [[code-switching|code-switching]].

## Overview

Gumperz was born in Hattingen, Germany, and emigrated as a refugee from Nazi persecution,
completing his doctorate in Germanic philology at the University of Michigan (1954). After
early dialect-geographic work he taught at Cornell and, from the late 1950s, at the
University of California, Berkeley, where he spent the rest of his career. His fieldwork
moved from village multilingualism in north India, through the Norwegian dialect community
of Hemnesberget, to interethnic miscommunication in urban Britain and the United States.

Across these settings Gumperz pursued one question: how speakers use the socially meaningful
alternatives in their **linguistic repertoire** to signal, and to interpret, social
relationships in the course of interaction. In his Introduction to *Directions* he named the
opposition that organizes his program — between a **correlational** sociolinguistics
(matching independent linguistic and social facts, as in [[variationist-sociolinguistics|Labov's]]
variable analysis) and an **interactional** approach that treats linguistic and social
categories as mutually constitutive and knowable only through situated language use.

## Contributions

- **[[interactional-sociolinguistics]]** — a research program treating social constraints
  and linguistic rules as parts of a single communicative system; its charter monograph is
  *[[gumperz-discourse-strategies-1982|Discourse Strategies]]* (1982, now ingested), built on
  **[[contextualization-cue|contextualization cues]]** and **[[conversational-inference]]**,
  with the "crosstalk" interethnic-communication program (job interviews, counseling,
  classrooms, the "kill Richard Nixon" prosecution analysis) as its applied edge and a
  network-based account of language shift ([[gail-valley-slovenes|Gail Valley]]) as its
  sociolinguistic base.
- **Situational vs. metaphorical [[code-switching]]** (with Blom, 1972) — the finding, from
  Hemnesberget, that alternation between a local dialect (Ranamål) and a standard (Bokmål)
  could either redefine a situation or, without changing it, allude to additional social
  meanings. This distinction founded the sociolinguistic study of code-switching.
- **[[speech-community]] and linguistic repertoire** — Gumperz recast the speech community
  as defined by shared knowledge of the communicative constraints and options governing
  social situations (Gumperz 1964; refined in his 1972 Introduction), not by a shared
  language; the repertoire is the totality of a community's meaningful linguistic resources.
- **Editorial architecture of a field** — the 1964 and 1972 anthologies assembled the
  ethnography of communication, ethnomethodology and [[conversation-analysis|conversation
  analysis]], variationist work, and [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes|Bernstein's code
  theory]] as coordinate "directions" out of a shared interactional turn.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Gumperz's interactional program drew explicitly on [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman's]]
interaction order, [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel's]] ethnomethodology, and
[[barth-fredrik|Fredrik Barth's]] transactionalism (Barth 1966) and Leach's political
anthropology — all cited in the Hemnesberget study as conceptual foundations. His lifelong
editorial and intellectual partnership with [[hymes-dell|Dell Hymes]] defined the
ethnography of speaking; his collaboration with Jan-Petter Blom produced the code-switching
work, and his later research with Jenny Cook-Gumperz extended interactional analysis to
schooling and interethnic communication. His Berkeley students and collaborators carried
interactional sociolinguistics into discourse analysis and the study of intercultural
(mis)communication.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The situational/metaphorical distinction has been criticized as difficult to apply
consistently and as under-specifying the boundary between the two types; later
conversation-analytic work (Auer and others) reworked code-switching as a sequentially
achieved practice rather than a mapping onto pre-given situations. The interpretive method
of *Discourse Strategies* — inferring speakers' intended framings from contextualization
cues — has been questioned for its reliance on the analyst's interpretation. These are
recorded as live methodological positions, not verdicts.

## Historiography

Gumperz is treated as a founder in histories of sociolinguistics and linguistic
anthropology; his Introduction to *Directions* is itself a much-cited historiographic
statement of the field's emergence from dialectology, descriptive linguistics, generative
grammar, and correlational sociolinguistics. Full analysis of his career, the Hemnesberget
fieldwork, and the reception of interactional sociolinguistics is in the
[[hubs/thinkers/gumperz-john-j|Thinkers Hub biography]].
