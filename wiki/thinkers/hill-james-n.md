# James N. Hill

American archaeologist; director of the Broken K Pueblo excavation (1963–1964) and author of the influential New Archaeology monograph *Broken K Pueblo* (1970), which sought to read room function and uxorilocal residence from ceramic spatial patterns. Entered this vault primarily via [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s formation-process reanalysis (*[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987 Ch. 12); see [[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]].

## Overview

Hill’s Broken K study was a flagship application of [[processual-archaeology|processual]] social inference: probability sampling of rooms, factor analysis of ceramic design elements and types, and claims about activity patterning and marital residence (attributed via Schiffer 1987).

## Contributions

- Early probability sampling and published multivariate analysis of pueblo ceramics for social organization.
- Exemplar — positive for New Archaeology ambition; critical for later formation-process scrutiny — of direct pattern-to-organization inference.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Processual / “new archaeology” circle of the 1960s (Chicago Field Museum / Southwest research context of the Broken K project as reported). Dialogues with later critics of the factor analyses (Lischka, Dumond, Plog — as listed by Schiffer) and with Schiffer’s deposit-formation critique.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Schiffer (1987) argued that neither Hill nor most statistical critics examined ceramic **formation processes**; floor assemblages and “trash” rooms are mixed de facto, secondary, and other pathways — undermining direct uxorilocal and room-function readings (attributed). See study page for detail.

## Historiography

Reputation as classic New Archaeology case study; afterlife largely methodological (what the case shows about inference warrants), not as a settled ethnography of Broken K social structure.
