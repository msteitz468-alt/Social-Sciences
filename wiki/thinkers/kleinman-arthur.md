# Arthur Kleinman

Psychiatrist and anthropologist (b. 1941), a principal architect of interpretive
[[medical-anthropology]] and of cross-cultural psychiatry. Kleinman built much of the field's
core vocabulary — [[explanatory-models]], the [[illness-disease-distinction|illness/disease
distinction]], [[illness-narrative|illness narratives]], and a medical-anthropological account of
[[somatization]] — and has spent his career at Harvard, jointly in the Department of Social
Medicine and the Department of Anthropology.

## Overview

Kleinman trained in medicine in the early 1960s and holds a dual academic identity "not only a
clinician but also an academic in both anthropology and psychiatry." His program,
[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology]], treats illness as inescapably meaningful and clinical
work as an interpretive, quasi-ethnographic act. Three books anchor it: *[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients
and Healers in the Context of Culture]]* (1980) founded the framework (the [[health-care-system]],
[[clinical-reality]], [[explanatory-models]]) on his Taiwan fieldwork; *[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking
Psychiatry]]* (1988) turned it on psychiatry (the [[category-fallacy]], [[symbolic-healing]]); and
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (1988) turned it on chronic illness and
the clinic. His fieldwork in Taiwan (1969–70, 1975–78) and China (Hunan, 1980, 1983 — studying
[[neurasthenia]] and the somatization of depression) anchored his comparative work.

## Contributions

- **The [[health-care-system]] as a cultural system** (*Patients and Healers*, 1980): the popular,
  folk, and professional sectors, plus [[clinical-reality]] and the [[core-clinical-tasks]].
- **[[explanatory-models]]** (with Eisenberg and Good, 1978): the notions patients, families, and
  practitioners hold about an illness episode — his most widely adopted concept.
- **The [[illness-disease-distinction|illness / disease / sickness]] distinction**: lived
  experience vs. biomedical reconstruction vs. macrosocial pattern.
- **The [[category-fallacy]]** and the **[[cross-cultural-psychiatry|new cross-cultural psychiatry]]**
  (*Rethinking Psychiatry*, 1988): the critique of imposing unvalidated diagnostic categories across
  cultures, and a comparative theory of [[symbolic-healing]] grounded in his Taiwan [[tang-ki]] study.
- **[[illness-narrative]]** and **empathic witnessing**: illness as story, and the clinician's task
  of interpreting and facilitating it.
- **[[somatization]] and [[neurasthenia]]**: a medical-anthropological reframing of bodily distress
  idioms, grounded in his Hunan fieldwork.
- **The [[explanatory-models-interview|mini-ethnography]]**: a clinical method importing ethnographic
  craft into chronic-illness care.
- Later concepts: "local moral worlds," "social suffering," and a sustained turn to caregiving
  (*The Soul of Care*, 2019).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Kleinman's closest collaborators were the psychiatrist **Leon Eisenberg** (his chairman in Social
Medicine at Harvard and co-author of the 1978 explanatory-models paper) and the anthropologist
**Byron Good**; his China fieldwork was conducted with his wife and research collaborator **Joan
Kleinman**, a China scholar. His interpretive framing is indebted to Clifford Geertz's semiotic
anthropology. He worked within, and against, both biomedical reductionism and the behavioral
models dominant in 1970s–80s pain medicine. His teachers/advisors are not documented in the
ingested source. Among his students at Harvard Social Medicine was [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]], who
named Kleinman "the best teacher I had" before pushing the interpretive program toward the political
economy of [[structural-violence]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **[[critical-medical-anthropology]]** (Baer, Singer, Frankenberg, Taussig) charges that Kleinman's
  meaning-centered program underweights political economy — the *sickness* register of poverty and
  structural violence. Kleinman's later "social suffering" work partly answers this.
- The [[explanatory-models]] concept has been criticized for a residual cognitivism (culture as
  belief); the embodiment turn (the [[the-mindful-body|mindful body]] of [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]
  and Lock) and Kleinman's own move to lived moral experience respond to it.
- Kleinman writes reflexively about his own clinical failures and countertransference (the 1973
  group-therapy episode with paralyzed adolescents; his rushed dismissal of the brain-injured
  patient "Paul Sensabaugh," after which "it was me… who should have felt shame") — modeling
  reflexive rather than heroic practitioner writing.

## Historiography

*The Illness Narratives* had an unusual reception history that Kleinman documents in its 2020
edition: initially read mainly by academic physicians and social scientists, it gradually reached
a wide audience of ordinary clinicians, patients, and family caregivers, and became a foundational
teaching text (Kleinman used it for seventeen years in clinical rounds). Writing in 2020 near the
end of his career, he reflects that were he to write it afresh he would give greater emphasis to
poverty, racial inequality, and family-caregiver perspectives — the concerns of his later work.
