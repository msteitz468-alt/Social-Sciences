# Georg Simmel

## Overview

Georg Simmel (1858–1918) was a German sociologist and philosopher, one of the founders of the discipline in its [[disciplines/sociology|classical era]] and the principal architect of **[[formal-sociology|formal sociology]]** — the program that took the recurrent *forms* of human association ([[sociation]]) as sociology's proper object, abstracted from their concrete contents. Born in Berlin, he spent most of his career there as an unpaid or honorary lecturer, celebrated as a dazzling teacher and essayist yet blocked from a regular chair until 1914; he is often described (after Lewis Coser) as a "stranger in the academy."

Where *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* argued the relational ontology, his *Soziologie* (*Untersuchungen über die Formen der Vergesellschaftung*, 1908) — carried into English in [[simmel-sociology-wolff-1950|Wolff's 1950 anthology]] — supplies the worked *forms of sociation*: [[dyad-and-triad|the dyad and triad]], [[superordination-and-subordination|super- and subordination]], [[secrecy]] and [[secret-society|the secret society]], [[sociability]], [[faithfulness-and-gratitude|faithfulness and gratitude]], [[the-stranger|the stranger]], and [[metropolis-and-mental-life|the metropolis]].

His masterwork, *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* (*Philosophie des Geldes*, 1900; enlarged 1907), uses money as a lens onto the whole of modern culture. Its premise is that a phenomenon like [[exchange]] "is by no means simply an economic fact": Simmel opens a philosophical "storey beneath historical materialism," treating economic forms as themselves the product of deeper valuations while preserving their power to shape intellectual and cultural life (as of Simmel's 1900 Preface). Around money he assembles theories of [[value]], [[exchange]], individual freedom, [[objective-culture|objective and subjective culture]], and the [[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]] that anticipate much twentieth-century social theory.

## Contributions

- **Formal sociology and [[sociation]].** Simmel argued that "society" is not a substance but "the synthesis or the general term for the totality of these specific interactions" (*Wechselwirkung*, reciprocal effect); sociology should extract the enduring *forms* of association (exchange, conflict, subordination, the stranger, the dyad) from their shifting contents — see [[formal-sociology]].
- **Relational theory of [[value]].** Value is "never a quality of the objects, but a judgment upon them which remains inherent in the subject," arising from the **distance** between a desiring subject and an object that resists possession; economic value is value objectified through [[exchange]].
- **Money as reified relation.** In *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]*, money is "the reified function of being exchanged," "the autonomous expression" of the exchange relationship — see [[money-economy]]. Its historical trajectory runs "from substance to function."
- **[[objective-culture|Objective vs. subjective culture]] and the [[tragedy-of-culture|tragedy of culture]].** The accumulated wealth of "objectified spirit" (technology, science, institutions) outgrows the individual's capacity to assimilate it — a divergence Simmel traced to the division of labour and later named the tragedy of culture (1911).
- **The sociology of modern inner life.** The [[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]], cynicism, calculability, the intensified pace of life, and psychic "distance" as reserve — diagnoses developed in *The Philosophy of Money* and the essay "The Metropolis and Mental Life" (1903).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Simmel was formed in the Berlin milieu of neo-Kantian philosophy and *Völkerpsychologie* — his teachers included the folk-psychologists **Moritz Lazarus** and **Heymann Steinthal** and the philosopher-historian **Wilhelm Dilthey**; his early dissertation engaged Kant. His relation to **Karl Marx** is central and contested: *The Philosophy of Money* is read (by Goldscheid and others) as "a very interesting correlate to Marx's *Capital*," extending the analysis of commodity fetishism and alienation into a general philosophy of culture, while Simmel's own subjective-relativist theory of [[value]] stands closer to marginal-utility economics (Menger, Böhm-Bawerk) than to Marx's labour theory. (Frisby notes that Simmel wrongly described Marx's theory of money as a "labour-money" theory, which Marx expressly rejected — a documented misreading; see [[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900]].)

Though Frisby records that Simmel "founded no school," his students and auditors carried his influence widely: **Georg Lukács** (who called him "the true philosopher of impressionism, a philosophical Monet") and **Ernst Bloch** studied under him in Berlin; **Siegfried Kracauer** wrote an early monograph on him; and the American sociologist **[[park-robert-e|Robert E. Park]]** attended his 1899–1900 lectures and became, more than Albion Small's translations, the chief conduit of Simmel's reception into the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] (urban program: *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]*). **Max Weber** — who tried and failed to secure Simmel a chair — absorbed and criticized his account of rationalization and calculability. Simmel foresaw his own diffuse legacy: "I know that I shall die without spiritual heirs… The estate I leave is like cash distributed among many heirs."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Sociology or social philosophy?** Otto Spann attacked Simmel's sociology as resting on a "psychologistic concept of society" (interaction as the interaction of *psychic* entities), a critique Weber said he largely shared. Hans Lieber and Hans Freyer read his form/content distinction as leaving him "an ahistorical social philosophy or social ontology" rather than an empirical science.
- **Ahistorical reification.** Lukács (*History and Class Consciousness*) praised *The Philosophy of Money* as "a very interesting and perceptive work in matters of detail" but faulted it for treating reification as "the timeless model of human relations in general" rather than a historically specific product of capitalism. Karl Mannheim and Andrew Arato similarly charged that Simmel abstracted "money as such" from its capitalist background and made alienation "independent of social relations."
- **Contemporary reception.** Durkheim reviewed the book in *L'Année Sociologique* (1901) as "a treatise on social philosophy," praising its account of money's "moral depreciation" of objects but judging its method "laborieuse" and bordering on "illegitimate speculation." Gustav Schmoller compared its significance to Durkheim's *Division of Labour* while noting "a strong strain of pessimism."
- **Zelizer's challenge (attributed).** [[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]] (*[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]*, 1994) reconstructs Simmel's "colorless" money and "frightful leveler" as the classic utilitarian model of homogeneous money and argues that modern monies are instead [[multiple-monies|multiple]] and [[earmarking|earmarked]] — social relations transform money as money transforms social life. Study: [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]. This is Zelizer's reading of Simmel as research foil, not a replacement of the wiki's Simmel pages.
- **Antisemitism and marginalization.** Simmel's exclusion from a regular chair until 1914 is attributed by Frisby (via Weber and Baumgarten) partly to pervasive academic antisemitism — Weber "never forgave Dilthey, Rickert and Windelband for concertedly blocking the call to a full professorship for Georg Simmel… because he was a Jew" — and partly to his genuinely unclassifiable, boundary-crossing style. This is recorded as documented history, not endorsement.

## Historiography

Reception has swung between reading Simmel as a rigorous formal sociologist and as an impressionistic essayist ("a philosophical Monet"). His American afterlife came through Park and Small; his German afterlife through Lukács, Bloch, Kracauer, and later the Frankfurt School (Adorno's "grudging" acknowledgment that Simmel "was… the first to accomplish the return of philosophy to concrete subjects"). Talcott Parsons largely marginalized him in *The Structure of Social Action* (1937). A major revival since the 1980s (Frisby, Poggi, Lichtblau, Köhnke's *Der junge Simmel*) has restored *The Philosophy of Money* to the classical canon; Fredric Jameson reads Simmel as a "sociological predecessor" of Walter Benjamin, and the cultural sociology of money (Zelizer, Dodd) works in his wake — including Zelizer's program of [[multiple-monies]] as both debt to and rebuttal of Simmelian colorless money. The early American dispute over whether his formal sociology counted as *science* (Sorokin vs. Park–Spykman) is recorded on [[simmel-scientific-status-debate]]. A graduate-level intellectual biography lives in the [[hubs/thinkers/simmel-georg|Thinkers Hub]]; this page is the network summary.

Among mid-century sociologists, [[blau-peter-m|Peter Blau]] is a principal heir of Simmel's *formal* sociology: *[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power in Social Life]]* (1964) titles its program a "free translation of Simmel's basic concept, *Die Formen der Vergesellschaftung*," and builds directly on Simmelian materials — [[exchange]] and [[faithfulness-and-gratitude|gratitude]], [[dyad-and-triad|the dyad and triad]], [[superordination-and-subordination]], and [[cross-cutting-conflicts|intersecting social circles]] (which Blau later operationalized quantitatively in *Crosscutting Social Circles*, 1984). See [[social-exchange-theory]].
