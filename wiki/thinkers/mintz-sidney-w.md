# Sidney W. Mintz (1922–2015)

American anthropologist; central figure in Caribbean anthropology and in the historical anthropology of food and commodities. This page is densified from *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985); fuller biography awaits primary ingest of *Worker in the Cane*, *Caribbean Transformations*, and secondary literature. Landmark study: [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]]; deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]].

## Overview

Mintz spent nearly his whole professional life studying the Caribbean and the tropical products — especially sugar — that bound the region to European and North American metropolises after 1492. In *Sweetness and Power* he "comes home" by following production into consumption: how Europeans became mass consumers of sucrose, and what changing uses and meanings of sugar reveal about power, class, and modern everyday life. Fieldwork among Puerto Rican cane workers (from January 1948), and observation of traditional brown sugars in Jamaica and Haiti, supplied ethnographic ground for a book that is mostly historical and theoretical in method.

## Contributions

- **Historical anthropology of a commodity** — *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study|Sweetness and Power]]* as charter for studying food as a production–consumption circuit across colony and metropolis; method commitment that either vantage alone is incomplete.
- **[[plantation]] as industrial form / [[factory-in-the-field]]** — sugar estate and mill as early industrial organization of land, labor, capital, and time; 20th-c. cane proletarians living like factory workers.
- **[[inside-meaning-and-outside-meaning]]** — lived ritual/meal meanings vs structural significations for rulers and system reproduction ("pacific domination").
- **[[extensification-and-intensification]]** — dual process of mass diffusion: emulative ceremonial continuity vs working-class innovation of new uses under factory rhythms.
- **[[proletarian-hunger-and-drug-foods]]** — tea, sugar, tobacco, and related tropical stimulants as cheap calories, habit, and time-savers in industrial diets; sugar as "one of the people's opiates."
- **[[sweetness]] as analytic object** — species predisposition to sweet taste + culturally variable sources, meanings, and uses; sucrose's English path exotic → luxury → necessity.
- **[[sugar-plantation-complex]]** — Atlantic production geography from Mediterranean/Arab and Atlantic-island precursors through New World slavery, indenture, and metropolitan refining.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under:** [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]] (Puerto Rico / *The People of Puerto Rico* project).
- **Adjacent peers / co-travellers:** [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] (shared Steward-project generation; [[anthropological-political-economy]]); Caribbeanists and historians of slavery and sugar engaged as secondary literature (Williams, Pares, Hobsbawm, etc., as cited in 1985).
- **Engages:** classical political economy and Marxian commodity/labor analysis; English social historians of diet and budgets; nutrition and disappearance-data series; Ortiz on sugar and capitalism (closing resonance).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Sweetness and Power* is widely treated as founding the contemporary anthropology of food. Subsequent commodity biographies and food studies both extend and critique its British-consumption emphasis, its handling of gender and domestic labor, and the balance of meaning vs structure. Full reception map awaits secondary ingest; nothing in the 1985 text is treated here as overturned finding.
