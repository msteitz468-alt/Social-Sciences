# Mark S. Granovetter

American sociologist; principal architect of the **[[strength-of-weak-ties]]** argument (1973), of the labor-market network study *[[granovetter-getting-a-job-1974|Getting a Job]]* (1974), and of the network reading of **[[embeddedness]]** that chartered the [[new-economic-sociology|new economic sociology]] (1985). Early work grew from discussions with [[harrison-white|Harrison White]] at Harvard; at publication of the 1973 AJS article he was at Johns Hopkins.

## Overview

Granovetter's early program used [[network-analysis|interpersonal network analysis]] to bridge micro interaction and macro patterns — diffusion, mobility, community organization, cohesion, and (from 1985) markets and firms. *[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973|The Strength of Weak Ties]]* (1973) is the portable theory statement; *[[granovetter-getting-a-job-study|Getting a Job]]* (1974) is the full empirical monograph on PTM job matching in Newton, Massachusetts; *[[granovetter-embeddedness-1985|Embeddedness]]* (1985) generalizes the relational critique of under- and over-socialized action. Collection now holds all three as primary sources (SWT 1973; GAJ 2nd ed. 1995 text including Embeddedness as Appendix D).

## Contributions

- **[[tie-strength]]** as a multidimensional construct (time, emotional intensity, intimacy, reciprocal services); frequency proxy in the job study.
- **Forbidden triad / bridge theorem** (attributed): under the overlap hypothesis, strong ties are almost never bridges; bridges are weak.
- **[[personal-contacts]] and labor markets:** *Getting a Job* — contacts ~56% of Newton PTM finds; better jobs; weak-tie skew; short chains; nonsearch; work vs family-social (see [[granovetter-getting-a-job-study]] · [[hubs/studies/granovetter-getting-a-job-study]]).
- **Community organization corollary:** bridging weak ties as necessary infrastructure for mobilization (West End vs Charlestown; [[west-end-network-fragmentation-debate]]).
- **[[embeddedness]] (network sense):** economic action constrained by ongoing social relations — against atomized under-socialized *and* over-socialized models ([[granovetter-embeddedness-1985]]; [[new-economic-sociology]]).
- **Programmatic network agenda** beyond small-group sociometry (weak ties, quasi-groups; contrast DHL transitivity models).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[harrison-white|Harrison White]] (dissertation advisor; acknowledged origin of SWT discussions; White vacancy chains engaged in GAJ Ch. 4).
- **Dialogue partners / early readers (SWT acks):** Ivan Chase, James Davis, William Michelson, Nancy Lee, Peter Rossi, Charles Tilly.
- **Intellectual debts:** Homans; Heider/Newcomb balance; Rapoport; Davis clustering; Milgram small-world; Bott, Barnes, Mitchell network anthropology; Gans; Jacobs; labor economists on informal placement (Reynolds, Brown, etc.); Polanyi lineage reconceived for modern markets.
- **Later lineage:** [[structural-holes-theory|structural holes]] ([[burt-ronald-s|Burt]] 1992 — another White student; hole not weakness as causal agent); Lin social-resources theory; social-capital network measures; new economic sociology field (Swedberg co-edited reader 1992).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Gans 1974:** weak ties overestimated for the West End; networks necessary not sufficient ([[west-end-network-fragmentation-debate]]).
- **Measurement:** frequency-only operationalization of strength; positive/symmetric assumption; weak ties under-sampled in sociometry; Light base-rate objection to weak-tie job findings.
- **Later research (GAJ Afterword 1994 and reception):** contact prevalence sustained; simple "weak ties always better wages" reading conditional or null under controls; strong ties dominate in some institutional settings (Japan, pre-reform China as summarized 1994); Burt structural-holes reframing; network inequality agenda (race/gender returns to contacts).
- Do not promote 1973/1974 point estimates to wiki voice.

## Historiography

No full biography ingested. Disciplinary memory places SWT among the most-cited late-20th-century sociology articles and GAJ among founding economic-sociology monographs. Collection coverage: **three sources** (SWT 1973 + Gans exchange; GAJ 1974/1995; Embeddedness 1985 via GAJ App. D).
