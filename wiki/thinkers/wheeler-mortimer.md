# Mortimer Wheeler (1890–1976)

Sir Robert Eric Mortimer Wheeler, British archaeologist and the most influential codifier
of stratigraphic excavation method in the mid-twentieth century. His
*[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from the Earth]]* (1954) became the
standard field-methods treatise for a generation and is the primary source for this page.

## Overview

By his own account Wheeler had been excavating "for thirty years" as of 1954 — a career
beginning c.1924 — "in a variety of countries and circumstances" (finding, Wheeler 1954).
He directed the Verulamium (Roman St Albans) excavations of the 1930s as a staged tactical
campaign, dug the Iron Age hillfort of Maiden Castle (Dorset), and served as Director-
General of Archaeology in India, 1944–48, where he ran an explicitly region-wide research
program — using datable Roman imports at Arikamedu (1945) as a fixed chronological peg for
otherwise-undated South Indian sequences, then tracing rouletted ware inland to Brahmagiri
(1947) and beyond (attributed/finding, Wheeler 1954). He visited [[petrie-flinders|Flinders
Petrie]] on his deathbed in Jerusalem "at the beginning of 1942" (finding, Wheeler 1954).

## Contributions

**The box-grid excavation system.** Wheeler is the originator and chief proponent of
**[[box-grid-excavation]]** — excavating an area as a grid of squares separated by standing
**baulks** that preserve continuous vertical **sections** until the final phase (attributed,
Wheeler 1954). He derived it from six stated requirements an area layout must satisfy
(subdivisibility, expansibility, preserved sections, eventual integration, access, light)
and held it the only layout meeting all six.

**Stratigraphic method and recording.** Wheeler insisted that "the basis of scientific
excavation is the accurately observed and adequately recorded section," likening strata to
"the successive pages of a book" ([[law-of-superposition]]), and made **three-dimensional
recording** of every find "the essence of modern excavation," crediting the principle to
[[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]] (attributed, Wheeler 1954). His governing rule was
"vertical digging first, horizontal digging afterwards" — control stratification from the
side, never predict it from the top.

**Tactics and strategy.** Wheeler distinguished **tactical** planning (a staged sequence of
excavations solving one bounded problem, e.g. Verulamium) from **strategic** planning
(region-wide research programs, e.g. the South Indian chronology); his maxim was "have a
plan, and make the opportunity," and the archaeologist without a policy "an archaeological
food-gatherer" (attributed, Wheeler 1954).

**The aims of archaeology.** Against W. W. Taylor's view that "the archaeologist, as
archaeologist, is really nothing but a technician," Wheeler argued that archaeology is a
**fact-finding discipline whose facts are the material records of human achievement**, so
that the excavator "is also, by that token, a humanist" — "the archaeological excavator is
not digging up things, he is digging up people" (attributed, Wheeler 1954). He held that
archaeology "very properly adapts and adopts the methods of natural science" without itself
being a natural science, endorsing O. G. S. Crawford's formula, "archaeology is an art which
employs a scientific technique." This position is recorded on
[[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

Wheeler places himself in direct descent from [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]], whose
Cranborne Chase standards of total recording, trained staff, and "super-accuracy" he treats
as the foundation "half a century of subsequent work has supplemented rather than
superseded" (attributed, Wheeler 1954). He read [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]] critically —
admiring his "untiring search for truth" while judging a "gulf" to separate Petrie's field
standards from Pitt Rivers's. Wheeler reported working with "500 students or more" across
England, Wales, France, and India, pairing senior and junior students on his signature
teaching model. (His own teachers are not documented in the ingested source.)

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Wheeler's box-grid, dominant into the 1960s, was subsequently challenged by **open-area /
single-context excavation**, which objects that standing baulks fragment horizontal
relationships and obscure the plan; the grid is today one option among several rather than
the universal method Wheeler presented. His writing on excavation labor is a first-person
primary source for archaeology's **colonial labor politics** — as Director-General in India
he described local workmen in period colonial terms (e.g. "the Oriental is a born
chatterer") that are attributed colonial characterizations, not wiki-voice fact, and belong
to the discipline's reflexive record rather than its findings. His explicit argument that
research should concentrate on "the riper achievements of Man as a social animal" — major
civilizational centers over "little domestic secrets" — is a value-laden research-priority
claim that later processual and household archaeology reversed.

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]
(*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches to Fieldwork]]*, 2002)
adds a critical-historical reading (attributed). For Wheeler and Kenyon "stratigraphy *was*
the section" — a conception Lucas, following Edward Harris, calls **biased toward "layers"**
and "overly geological," and one that did *not* treat the interface/cut as a numbered
stratigraphic unit in its own right (Harris's later contribution). Lucas notes that J. P.
Droop had preached the importance of stratigraphy in 1915, years before Wheeler's 1927
"key principle" address, complicating Wheeler's originator status; that Wheeler's own
"decades after Pitt Rivers were retrograde" narrative is factually wrong (Bulleid and Gray
at Glastonbury; Bushe-Fox at Wroxeter); and that the sharp Wheeler-vs-**Bersu** "grid vs.
open area" opposition (dramatized by the Hawkeses) is a caricature — the box system was
"an attempt to find the optimum compromise between section and plan." Lucas also reads
Wheeler's insistence on *absolute* chronology (against [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]]'s purely
relative sequence) as expressing the culture-group conception's need to fix cultures in
time and space simultaneously. See [[stratigraphic-recording-debate]].

## Historiography

This page rests on a single primary source, Wheeler's own
*[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from the Earth]]* (1954), which is
programmatic and autobiographical in register; most claims above are Wheeler's own
prescriptions and self-account, attributed accordingly. A fuller intellectual biography —
formation and training, the full career, the reception history, and the public-figure and
ethics record — awaits biographical and secondary sources; a Thinkers-Hub treatment is
warranted in principle (Wheeler as a method-inventor) but deferred pending that corpus.
