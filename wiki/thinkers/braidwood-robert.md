# Robert J. Braidwood (1907–2003)

American archaeologist — Research Associate in Old World Prehistory at the Chicago Natural History Museum; Professor in the Oriental Institute and Department of Anthropology, University of Chicago. Mid-century architect of the **[[hilly-flanks|hilly-flanks / natural-habitat]]** account of Near Eastern food production and of multidisciplinary village-farming fieldwork centered on **[[jarmo|Jarmo]]**.

## Overview

Braidwood’s popular synthesis *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* (enlarged 3rd ed., preface 1 March 1959; second-edition preface 15 June 1950) presents human prehistory as economic **stages** subdivided into **eras**: a long **food-gathering / food-collecting** stage ends in a terminal microlithic phase; a **food-producing revolution** opens the **food-producing stage** (later replaced by the industrial stage); within food production he separates an **[[incipient-cultivation|era of incipient cultivation and animal domestication]]** from an **era of the primary village-farming community**. The primary hearth he defends is a **[[nuclear-area|nuclear area]]** along the **[[hilly-flanks|hilly flanks]]** of the Fertile Crescent — rain-watered foothills, not Breasted’s irrigated alluvium and not Childe’s **oasis** propinquity model. He co-opts Childe’s “revolution” metaphor while stretching its duration and relocating its geography.

## Contributions

*(All attributed — *Prehistoric Men*, 3rd ed. 1959, unless noted.)*

### Method and popular pedagogy

- **Interpretation as the job:** archaeology makes finds “tell a story” (spark-plug / dump-yard analogy); pretty objects are secondary to historical inference.
- **[[archaeological-assemblage|Assemblage]]:** co-occurring sets of antiquities compared like successive mail-order catalogues; cross-site type correlation plus [[stratigraphic-excavation|stratigraphy]] (Schliemann/Troy cake-layer model) yields a “sort of history of human activity.”
- **[[radiocarbon-dating]]:** postwar shortening of chronologies welcomed with statistical caution (2/3 chance within ±; contamination; prefer multi-site fabrics — Jarmo as worked example).
- **Allied sciences:** geology, paleontology, paleobotany, paleoclimatology, and (in the field program) zoology — domestication cannot be read from flints alone (as of Braidwood 1959 program statement).
- **Tool-making traditions ≠ cultures:** pebble, core-biface, flake, and chopper traditions are persistent *habits* of manufacture, not peoples or total cultures; rejects “Mousterian man” and Acheulean/Levalloisian/Mousterian-as-cultures labels; industries may mix traditions (Levalloiso-Mousterian, etc.).

### Fossil men and race (deflected)

Survey of australopithecines through early moderns with operational criteria (anatomy + intentional tools). Piltdown and Galley Hill rejected. Classic European Neanderthals treated as specialized end-forms (Howell model cited). Modern race taxonomy deflected: quotes Boyd and Washburn biological definitions; denies pure races from skeletons; geographic breeding populations over simple continental stocks. All mid-century fossil names and race language are period positions (as of 1959).

### Food-producing revolution program

- **Label:** prefers **food-producing revolution** (credits [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] with good reason to use “revolution”) while rejecting overnight change; **[[incipient-cultivation|incipient era]]** “probably an affair of some duration,” then acceleration once village-farming is established.
- **[[hilly-flanks]] / natural habitat:** wild wheats + barley co-available; potential animal domesticates; climate similar to modern; oases and pure river valleys *not* where earliest traces cluster.
- **Oasis theory stated and rejected:** rain-belt retreat after glacial melt forcing people/plants/animals into oases — “too simple for the facts.”
- **[[nuclear-area]]:** environment with domesticable suite + cultural “receptiveness” / atmosphere of experimentation; western Asia nuclear for western cultural heritage; other nuclear areas noted (Middle America, Andes, possibly China/SE Asia).
- **[[jarmo]] type-case:** primary village-farming community in Iraqi Kurdistan; multidisciplinary team; goat sure, other animals provisional (Reed); pottery late; early radiocarbon cluster ~6750 ± 200 B.C. (as of 1959).
- **Civilization:** impossible without food production but not entailed by it; river control in Egypt and southern Mesopotamia as collective problem after farming spreads from the flanks — germ of urban order (Ubaid → Warka/Proto-Literate sequence).

### Stage language vs three ages

Explicitly prefers technological-economic **stages/eras** over Paleolithic/Mesolithic/Neolithic age labels for the food-production story; materials limit prehistoric deduction mainly to technology and economics.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Interlocutors in *Prehistoric Men* 1959:** [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]] (revolution vocabulary; foil for tempo and for oasis geography); James Henry Breasted (fertile crescent reworked as flanks-above-alluvium); Dorothy Garrod (Natufian; Mount Carmel); Kathleen Kenyon (Jericho sequence); Hallam Movius (Far Eastern chopper synthesis); natural-science collaborators (Washburn on second-edition fossil chapter; Reed on Jarmo fauna; Libby-era radiocarbon); Ralph Solecki (Zawi Chemi Shanidar). Downstream critics of nuclear-zone settling-in include [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]] (1968). Braidwood is quoted (by Binford) as calling the New Archaeology “a religion” — position recording only.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Processual:** nuclear-zone / natural-habitat models challenged as insufficient causal theory (Binford packing/demographic-ecological alternatives; later feasting, climate, and multi-proxy models).
- **Chronology:** early uncalibrated radiocarbon (Jarmo, Jericho, Fayum, Hassuna, Zawi Chemi) are (as of 1959) positions — calibration and later series revise absolute dates.
- **Multi-regional farming:** independent centers weaken exclusive “western heritage began here” framing while retaining the piedmont-vs-oasis *problem*.
- **Abundance paradox:** later work (e.g. Harlan-type harvest experiments, as surveyed in textbooks) questions why rich natural habitats would invent farming — a tension latent in Braidwood’s own “receptiveness” residual.

## Cauvin engagement (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]].)* Cauvin treats Braidwood’s nuclear-zone / hilly-flanks program as the major mid-century alternative to Childe’s oasis environmentalism, yet still within a materialist search for natural stimuli to farming. He **reinterprets** Braidwood’s maxim that “Culture was not ready”: technical capacity for cultivation existed earlier; what was missing was the **idea and desire** released by the [[revolution-of-symbols|revolution of symbols]]. Postscript: if early SE Anatolian cultivation were confirmed, that region could join the Levantine corridor in a Braidwood-style nuclear zone — as secondary neolithisation tied to Mureybetian/early PPNB expansion, not as independent PPNA peer of the corridor (as of Cauvin’s evidence).
