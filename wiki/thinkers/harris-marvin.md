# Marvin Harris

## Overview

Marvin Harris (1927–2001) was a Columbia-trained American anthropologist who formulated **[[cultural-materialism|cultural materialism]]** as a research strategy: priority to techno-environmental and techno-economic conditions in explaining sociocultural similarities and differences, by analogy with natural selection in biology. *[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|The Rise of Anthropological Theory]]* (1968) is his major historiographic and programmatic statement — a critical survey of two centuries of culture theory written to reinstate anthropology as a nomothetic science of history.

## Contributions

- **[[cultural-materialism]]** as named research strategy (not a closed set of laws): study material conditions of production and reproduction first; treat ideational and structural domains as dependent for purposes of causal priority (*Rise*, Introduction; chs 22–23).
- Separation of cultural materialism from **philosophical materialism** and from **Marxist dialectical materialism** — Harris retains Marx/Engels's materialist emphasis while rejecting Hegelian dialectic as scientifically unconfirmed (*Rise*, Introduction; ch. 8).
- Systematic critique of Boasian [[historical-particularism]], [[culture-and-personality]], [[diffusionism]], French [[structuralism]], and British social anthropology as idiographic or idealist detours from causal science (*Rise*, chs 9–19).
- Defense and reworking of [[emic-and-etic|emic/etic]] distinction (ch. 20) against pure emic "new ethnography."
- Engagement with [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s [[universal-evolution]] and [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s [[cultural-ecology]] as incomplete carriers of the materialist strategy (chs 22–23).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Columbia / mid-century American anthropology** milieu; acknowledgments in *Rise* include [[service-elman-r|Elman Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Morton Fried]], [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]], Carneiro, and others.
- **Intellectual descent with modification** from Marx/Engels (minus dialectic), nineteenth-century evolutionism (critically recovered), White, and Steward.
- **Reacted against** Boasian particularism and the "idiographic interlude" of early twentieth-century anthropology as Harris periodizes it.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- *Rise* is widely read as a **partisan** history: admirers treat it as a necessary corrective to anti-scientific particularism; critics charge selective reading of Boas, functionalism, and structuralism, and over-strong techno-economic priority claims.
- Later cultural-materialist empirical program (sacred cow, protein/warfare theses, etc.) is outside this ingest's primary source and remains to be audited from those monographs.
- Interpretive and practice-theoretic anthropology after 1968 largely bypassed Harris's strategy; ecological and archaeological materialism partially absorbed it under other labels.

### Carneiro 2003 on the Service–Harris exchange (attributed)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*, Ch. 9) uses the Service–Harris dispute as the opening frame for the determinants problem. As Carneiro reports: Service’s *Natural History* review (1968) called Harris’s treatment of evolutionism “unsympathetic and inept” and scholarship on Morgan, White, and Sahlins “shockingly bad”; Harris replied that Service had recommended the manuscript for publication and objected mainly to unaltered treatments of those figures. Parallel *SWJA* debate: Service rejected monistic prime-movers as inhibiting empirical location of causality; Harris rejected Service’s framing as “regressive and obscurantist,” insisted cultural materialism is **not** monistic (techno-environmental + techno-economic, including population), and defended priority of material conditions. Carneiro’s own hierarchy (population pressure first for chiefdoms/states) is a third position in that determinant debate, not an endorsement of either pole.
