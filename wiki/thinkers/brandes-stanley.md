# Stanley Brandes

## Overview

Stanley Brandes is a cultural anthropologist (Professor of Anthropology, UC Berkeley, as of the volume bio), known for work on popular ritual and religion in Latin America and the Mediterranean. His short piece in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* — “The Cremated Catholic: The Ends of a Deceased Guatemalan” — is a precise case study of how a mishandled corpse becomes an object of spiritual damage claims and financial redress: [[body-commodification]] at the end of life, not only in transplant markets. Method note: researched for attorney Scott H.Z. Sumner; fieldwork help Katharine Young; versions at AAA 1997 “The Dead Body” symposium and UC Berkeley Anthropology Monday dinner 1998.

## Contributions

### The Axel Flores case *(attributed — Brandes)*

- **Axel Flores** (Brandes’s pseudonym): 31-year-old Guatemalan Latino migrant; killed night of **11 December 1994** walking onto a busy highway in Brisbane, California; body to San Mateo County Morgue; identified via fingerprints (prior police record). Had come to the USA partly to escape Guatemalan civil-war dangers.
- Sister in San Francisco notified; parish priest said cremation was least expensive. Father in **“Nahualtenango”** (pseudonym, SW-coast Guatemala village) rejected cremation as unthinkable; insisted intact corpse return for proper mortuary ceremonies (traditional and believed Church-sanctioned). Father mortgaged house and borrowed at high interest.
- **Coroner’s error:** sister presented with wrong cadavers; office explained both men were “heavy and dark-skinned”; Axel’s body released under wrong name and **accidentally cremated** before error detected — “thoroughly irreversible mistake.”

### Legal compensation *(attributed — Brandes)*

Suit vs San Mateo County and funeral parlor; funeral parlor settled out of court; county case unresolved because US embassy refused visas for family deposition. Lawyers sought **$300,000** total; mediation brief cites California law on family sole authority over remains disposition; sole damages for **emotional distress** (relatively rare in practice).

### Catholic body, cremation, afterlife *(attributed — Brandes; family and clergy positions)*

- Family concerns: (1) Axel’s afterlife destiny; (2) family status in Nahualtenango. Cremation repulsive: “It’s the way you treat a dog” (brother Genaro); “a sin”; soul barred from heaven — “Está sufriendo el alm’” / “His soul is suffering”; soul cannot find release without intact body.
- Family belief **controverts** post–Vatican II Catholic permission of cremation (never encouraged). Local parish priest, when confidentially told, refused Mass of the Present Body (*misa del cuerpo presente*) without intact body; later celebrated 40-day and first-anniversary Masses. Wake unconventional: weeks after death, no corpse.
- Brandes consulted two parish priests of Santo Tomás, Chichicastenango: cremation would not automatically bar heaven; neither had heard of a single cremation instance in Guatemala despite crematoriums advertising against popular anti-cremation claims. Even learned clergy uncertain on dating of legalization and procedure — Guatemalan clergy “as ignorant about procedure as… poorly educated parishioners,” undermining orthodoxy vs popular-belief dualism.
- Bodily wholeness for resurrection: Genaro cites Creed — “How can Axel be resurrected if there is no body?” Brandes links to Bynum (1992) on Christian anxiety about part–whole reintegration and medieval opposition to partition/cremation.

### Secrecy, cemetery, Hertz *(attributed — Brandes)*

- Family struggled to keep cremation secret for status; Brandes himself implicated in partial cover. Community asked “Where is the body?”; excuse: could not afford to bring him home (itself shameful). Ashes transport rejected: disintegrated body unworthy of Christian burial, unidentifiable — “How would we know that those ashes are Axel’s?”
- Even without cremation, family admits Axel might never have entered heaven (unmarried cohabitation, children by two women). Real crime of the morgue: depriving survivors of bodily presence for wake, Mass of Present Body, burial. Mother (cancer) died months later — demise attributed to never viewing corpse, not merely to son’s death. Greatest agony: absence from village cemetery (visits at 40 days, one year, All Souls/All Saints).
- Cemetery ethnography (20 July 1997): Nahualtenango cemetery as miniature village of brightly colored crypts; mother’s sky-blue grave with poles for upper crypt meant for Axel. Brother: “Esta panteón es alegría” — “This cemetery is happiness.” Via Hertz (1960): death not at heartbeat stop; deceased retains presence among living; **“Societal integrity depends upon the integrity of the cadaver.”** Cremation = drastic fragmentation → solitude of dead and survivors.

### Cremated body as commodity *(attributed — Brandes)*

Economic impact: Axel’s remittances supporting common-law son stopped; father mortgaged house for body return while wife mortally ill. Brandes’s thesis: accidental cremation caused terrible suffering; yet the cremated body may more than compensate for lost remittances via US liability — ashes converted into commodity/bargaining chip for family financial security, “and a source of income for lawyers and anthropologist alike.” From sinful aberration to extravagant monetary value in the US legal system. Context: Bay Area ash-mixing awards, class-action crematorium cases, aircraft ash-scattering fraud settlements — “It seems that burnt bodies are big business in California.” In Axel’s case the family had explicitly ordered **not** to cremate.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Death studies and Catholic popular religion (Bynum on body and resurrection; Hertz secondary burial/presence of the dead). UC Berkeley medical-anthropology/death network acknowledgments (Scheper-Hughes, Sharon Kaufman). Attorney-commissioned ethnographic mediation as method.
