# Colin M. Turnbull

## Overview

Colin Macmillan Turnbull (1924–1994) was a British-born anthropologist best known for *[[turnbull-forest-people-1961|The Forest People]]* (1961), a widely-read popular ethnography of the [[mbuti|Mbuti]] (BaMbuti) net-hunters of the [[mbuti|Ituri Forest]], and for the far bleaker *[[turnbull-mountain-people-1972|The Mountain People]]* (1972), his account of the [[ik|Ik]] of northern Uganda under mid-1960s famine. He wrote deliberately in a humanised, personal register — presenting the people he studied as named individuals rather than as instances of abstract structure — which made his books exceptionally popular but drew professional suspicion that he had traded analytic rigour for literary sympathy. His academic monograph on the Mbuti, *Wayward Servants* (1965), was the technical counterpart to the popular *Forest People*; *The Mountain People* has no equivalent technical twin.

Turnbull was educated at Westminster School and Magdalen College, Oxford, and spent two years (c. 1949–1951) studying Indian religion and philosophy at Banaras (Varanasi), an experience he cited as formative for his sensibility. He came to the Ituri through the hospitality of Patrick Putnam, who ran a camp at Epulu, and he took his doctorate in anthropology at Oxford under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]]. He was Curator of African Ethnology at the American Museum of Natural History (where Harry L. Shapiro wrote the Foreword to *The Forest People* and later supported the Ik project) and later taught at several U.S. universities. His lifelong partner and collaborator was Joseph Towles, who shared the Ik fieldwork but is written out of *The Mountain People* by design.

## Contributions

- **Mbuti ethnography.** Turnbull documented the net-hunting Mbuti of the Epulu region in the late 1950s, giving a detailed account of camp life, the sexual division of the [[participant-observation|net hunt]], the near-absence of formal authority, dispute settlement by ridicule and consensus, and the great religious festivals — the [[molimo]] (following a death) and the [[elima]] (girls' puberty).
- **The forest as a benevolent moral order.** His central interpretive claim (attributed) was that the Mbuti conceived the forest as a conscious, benevolent parent — "Mother and Father, Lover and Friend" — a good world that supplied their needs, and that this forest-centred religion was fundamentally distinct from, and inwardly resistant to, the ritual life of the neighbouring [[mbuti|villagers]].
- **Mbuti autonomy from villagers.** Against Paul Schebesta, Turnbull argued the Mbuti were not hereditary serfs bound to Bantu and Sudanic villagers but were economically and spiritually independent forest people who manipulated the village relationship to their own advantage ([[mbuti-village-relations-debate]]).
- **Band flux.** In his later academic work (*Wayward Servants*, 1965; and *Man the Hunter*, 1968) Turnbull generalised the observation that Mbuti hunting bands had continuously shifting membership — "flux" — into an argument that fluid, non-lineal band composition was itself adaptive, a position that fed mid-century debates over the structure of hunter-gatherer bands ([[composite-hunting-band]]).
- **Ik famine ethnography and human-nature thesis.** In *[[turnbull-mountain-people-1972|The Mountain People]]*, Turnbull argued (attributed) that loss of hunting grounds to Kidepo National Park, forced settlement, drought, and starvation had dissolved Ik mutual care — family, love, law, and ritual — into individual survival competition. He generalised that love and "goodness" are luxuries of society, not inherent in humanity, and recommended forced random dispersal of the Ik ([[turnbull-mountain-people-study]]; [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Turnbull trained under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] at Oxford, and acknowledged Evans-Pritchard in *The Forest People* as the teacher who insisted the study of man be approached "with careful, scientific impartiality." His most important predecessor in the Ituri was the missionary-ethnographer Paul Schebesta. His entry to the field was mediated by Patrick Putnam's camp at Epulu. The Ik project was a third choice after blocked Ituri villager work and refused Andaman permission; Elizabeth Marshall Thomas had pointed him toward the mountain group while working with the Dodos.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*The Forest People* has been criticised as romanticised — an idealised portrait of forest harmony that underplayed conflict, coercion, and the depth of Mbuti entanglement with the village economy. The dependence question became a standing dispute: later ethnographers (notably Roy Richard Grinker) argued that Turnbull understated the Mbuti's structural embeddedness in village society ([[mbuti-village-relations-debate]]). Turnbull's reputation was more gravely tested by *The Mountain People* (1972): critics challenged empirical accuracy, fieldwork under duress, concentration-camp analogies, and his recommendation that the Ik be forcibly dispersed in random units until language and peoplehood ended. Positions are recorded on [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]] — not adjudicated here.

## Historiography

Turnbull's papers and the record of his partnership with Joseph Towles have been the subject of biographical scholarship (e.g., Roy Richard Grinker's *In the Arms of Africa: The Life of Colin M. Turnbull*, 2000 — not ingested), which situates the emotional intensity of both ethnographies within his biography. The standard account treats him as a gifted, unconventional writer whose humane popular books and contested field claims sit in permanent tension — the Mbuti light and Ik dark of that reputation.
