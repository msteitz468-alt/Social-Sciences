## Overview

Matthew Stirling, working for the Smithsonian Institution, is credited by Coe & Koontz (2013) with discovering Olmec civilization. In the 1930s and 1940s he explored and excavated [[sites/tres-zapotes]], [[sites/la-venta]], and [[sites/san-lorenzo]], discovering the last of these in 1945 — with his wife Marion — after being led to a colossal stone head reported as an "eye" visible along a trail.

## Contributions

At San Lorenzo, Stirling hypothesized that trough-shaped basalt stones he encountered formed part of an aqueduct system; this was confirmed in 1967 by Michael D. Coe's excavation of an actual drain system at the site (see [[sites/san-lorenzo]]). At La Venta, Stirling directed the first excavations for the Smithsonian Institution, with work later continued by the University of California (see [[sites/la-venta]]). At Tres Zapotes, Stirling reconstructed the missing numeral coefficient on Stela C as "seven," proposing a Long Count reading of 3 September 32 BCE; this reconstruction was vindicated in 1969 when a farmer discovered the stela's missing fragment, confirming the coefficient (see [[sites/tres-zapotes]], [[concepts/long-count]]).

Stirling consistently held that Olmec civilization predated the Classic Maya, a position vehemently opposed by Mayanist Sir Eric Thompson but backed by Alfonso Caso and Miguel Covarrubias. Coe & Koontz state, in their own attributed voice, that "time has fully borne out Stirling and the Mexican school" (2013) — the founding position in what is now the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stirling's dating priority claim, initially contested by Thompson, is now treated by Coe & Koontz as vindicated, though the underlying "mother culture" thesis it supports remains a live position in the debate literature rather than settled consensus (see [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]]).
