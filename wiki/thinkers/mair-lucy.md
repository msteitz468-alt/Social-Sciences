# Lucy Mair

British social anthropologist (1901–1986). Malinowski student; major LSE figure in [[applied-anthropology|applied anthropology]] and colonial-administration studies. Densified here primarily from [[kuper-adam|Kuper]], *[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]* (4th ed. 2015) — primary monographs not yet ingested.

## Overview

LSE-centered Africanist of the Malinowski generation. Unlike peers dispatched for “tribal” monographs, she was sent specifically to study **culture change** because (Kuper reports) she was thought still too new to anthropology for a conventional tribal study. She later collaborated with Lord Hailey on *An African Survey* (1938) and after WWII taught applied anthropology at the LSE, remaining with [[richards-audrey|Audrey Richards]] one of the principal London keepers of the applied tradition while Oxford (under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]) campaigned against it.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via Kuper 2015 unless otherwise noted; monographic claims await primary ingest.)*

- **Culture-change / applied programme:** IAI research fellow (1931 cohort with Fortes, Nadel, Richards, the Wilsons, Hilda Kuper, et al.). None of the IAI fellows made a career in colonial service; Mair’s path was academic applied teaching and policy collaboration rather than district officer employment.
- **Hailey *African Survey* (1938):** Team with Margery Perham and Richards under Sir Malcolm Hailey; report dismissive of Indirect Rule and a blueprint for administrative reform, economic welfare, and research funding that fed the 1940 Colonial Development and Welfare Act and the postwar CSSRC grant regime.
- **Postwar LSE applied anthropology:** With Richards, continued to foster applied work at LSE in the 1950s when [[firth-raymond|Firth]] led the department (joined at times by Nadel, Schapera, Leach, Freedman); Oxford disapproved. Kuper: Richards and Mair ruefully acknowledged that applied research was regarded as less serious/prestigious than fundamental [[social-structure]] studies.
- **Malinowski’s charisma (testimony):** Mair told Kuper that Malinowski’s functionalism had the charisma of “an entirely new revelation that was known only to the favoured few.” Women students more enduringly loyal; ambitious men found the personality oppressive.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[malinowski-bronislaw|Bronisław Malinowski]] (LSE seminar; enduring loyalty pattern Kuper associates with women students).
- **Peer / applied axis:** [[richards-audrey]] (IAI, Hailey survey, LSE colonial/applied teaching, CSSRC era); [[firth-raymond]] (postwar LSE leadership).
- **Institutional contrast:** Oxford structuralist line (Evans-Pritchard, Fortes, then Gluckman’s temporary Oxford stop) vs LSE neo-Malinowskian and applied line.
- **Class/historiography interlocutor:** Named by [[leach-edmund|Leach]] (1984) among upper-middle-class British recruits (with Richards, Evans-Pritchard, Bateson, Wedgwood) — rebels against class attracted to ethnography; Kuper rejects simple class explanations and asks whether Richards and Mair count as insiders (class) or outsiders (women in 1930s–40s academia).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Applied anthropology’s colonial location** is the standing critique on [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] and on the concept page [[applied-anthropology]] — Mair’s career is central evidence for the LSE applied pole, not a verdict on individual complicity.
- **Prestige hierarchy:** Kuper’s historiography treats the applied/fundamental split as structural within British social anthropology (administrators’ frustration with “minutiae”; Hailey’s limited applied niche; Oxford’s campaign against applied work), not as Mair’s personal failure.
- **Reception of Kuper 1973:** On first publication of *Anthropologists and Anthropology*, Mair **refused to read it** (Kuper preface; book provoked furious Gluckman correspondence and Oxford “samizdat” status for ~two decades).
