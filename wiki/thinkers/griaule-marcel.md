# Marcel Griaule

## Overview

Marcel Griaule (1898–1956) began his career as a WWI-era aviator before turning to ethnology under [[mauss-marcel]]'s encouragement; in 1928 he spent a year in Ethiopia with linguist Marcel Cohen. He organized and led the Mission Dakar-Djibouti (1931–33), a twenty-one-month, largely museum-collecting expedition crossing Africa from the Atlantic to the Red Sea, which first brought him into contact with the Dogon of Sanga (French Sudan, now Mali) and included five months in the Gondar region of Ethiopia. Nine members took part, including André Schaeffner, Deborah Lifchitz, and [[leiris-michel]]. In 1946 he held the first chair in ethnology at the Sorbonne, reportedly lecturing in his air force officer's uniform. He died in 1956. (Source: [[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]].)

## Contributions

The Dakar-Djibouti mission, patronized by Parisian high society and authorized by a special law of the Chamber of Deputies, brought back roughly 3,500 objects, 6,000 photographs, Abyssinian paintings, 300 manuscripts/amulets, and recordings for the Trocadéro Ethnographic Museum (renamed the Musée de l'Homme) — a scale of museum collecting Clifford contrasts with the emerging Anglo-American, Rockefeller-funded model of intensive field study. From 1935–39 Griaule led further group expeditions to French Sudan, Cameroun, and Chad, working increasingly among the Bambara and Dogon and developing a model of three African "culture areas" each bearing a traditional epistemology ("sophie").

In 1947 the elderly, blind Dogon sage Ogotemmêli instructed Griaule over thirty-three days of conversation in Dogon cosmology, becoming *Dieu d'eau* (1948); fuller compilations of Dogon "deep knowledge" appeared posthumously (*Le renard pâle*, 1965; Calame-Griaule's *Ethnologie et langage*, 1965). Griaule's informants were, by his own acknowledgment, "almost entirely men," with roughly 5% of the Sanga population "completely instructed" and 15% possessing partial secret knowledge.

Clifford argues Griaule's career moved through two linked but distinct paradigms: a "documentary" phase (1930s), governed by metaphors of collection, forced interrogation, and the ethnographer as detective or examining magistrate extracting hidden truth ("The role of the person sniffing out social facts is often comparable to that of a detective or examining magistrate"); and, after Ogotemmêli, an "initiatory" phase, governed by metaphors of pedagogical revelation in which the ethnographer is progressively admitted to deeper Dogon esoteric knowledge. Clifford reads Griaule's fascination with aerial photography (from his aviator background) as a "panoptic" epistemology of ethnographic power, and his advocacy of team-based, multi-observer documentation as a Maussian "documentary" ideal opposed to the Anglo-American solitary participant-observer model.

Griaule also played a political role in late-colonial Sanga, mediating between traditional Dogon authorities and government-installed chiefs, and served as president of the Commission on Cultural Affairs of the Assembly of the Union Française, arguing for preserving select African traditions under continued French guidance.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained under [[mauss-marcel]]; his Dakar-Djibouti team included [[leiris-michel]] and André Schaeffner. Germaine Dieterlen was his central long-term collaborator, co-authoring *Le renard pâle*. His ethnographic portrayal of Dogon/Bambara metaphysics resonated with the négritude movement after 1950, particularly Senghor's essentialist evocation of African cultural essence — an alignment Clifford notes came to seem increasingly "ahistorical" as négritude shifted toward [[cesaire-aime]]'s more syncretic model.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

A sustained dispute concerns the epistemic status of Griaule's Dogon "revelations." See [[griaule-dogon-debate]] for the full positions: Griaule and Dieterlen held the uniformity of the esoteric knowledge across Dogon culture indicated genuine collective tradition; critics including Goody (1967), I.M. Lewis (1973), and Copans (1973) argued it more plausibly reflected individual theological speculation by particular informants (notably Ogotemmêli); British social anthropologists (Richards 1967, Douglas 1967, Goody 1967) additionally criticized Griaule's reliance on a small circle of privileged informants and translators, and his neglect of daily and political life — Douglas called the resulting picture "curiously skewed." Lettens (1971) went further, suggesting the entire initiation narrative was a post hoc invention, a position Clifford calls "largely unsubstantiated and unconvincing." Griaule's 173 field-note cards for the Ogotemmêli interviews show evidence of rewriting that eliminated traces of linguistic difficulty and the interpreter Kogem's presence.

Griaule dismissed African intellectual (évolué) critics of his traditionalist portrayals as products of colonial "miseducation" ("You can't be simultaneously at school and in the sacred grove"); Clifford notes such critiques (including Hountondji 1977) targeted his essentializing of "tradition" and marginalization of African intellectual agency, and states this paternalist framing "no longer carr[ies] the authority" it once had.

## Historiography

Clifford's own interpretive move, developed in *The Predicament of Culture* (Ch. 2, "Power and Dialogue in Ethnography: Marcel Griaule's Initiation"), is to read Griaule's texts as neither simply true nor false representations of "the Dogon" but as historically situated, dialogically negotiated productions: "it does not follow that Griaule's version of the Dogon is false. His writings, and those of his associates, express a Dogon truth." Clifford treats the "originality vs. cultural typicality" framing of the reliability debate (per Hountondji 1977) as "probably fruitless," given how little is independently known about the key informants. As of this ingest, Clifford (1988) is the wiki's sole source on Griaule; the debate literature he surveys (Douglas, Goody, Lewis, Copans, Lettens, Hountondji) is known only at second hand.
