# Carol B. Stack

## Overview

American cultural anthropologist best known for *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]* (1974), a landmark ethnography of kinship, exchange, and domestic organization among the Black urban poor in "[[the-flats|The Flats]]" (pseudonymous Midwestern community). The book became a standard counter-text to [[culture-of-poverty]] and deficit models of the Black family. Fieldwork co-analyzed with John R. Lombardi (co-author of the Conclusion).

## Contributions

- **[[stack-all-our-kin-study|All Our Kin]]:** demonstrated that poor Black families in The Flats were embedded in resilient, multi-household **[[domestic-network|domestic networks]]** of cooperative exchange, not "disorganized" nuclear households.
- Concepts coined or reworked for this setting: **[[swapping]]** (trading that obligates), **[[personal-kindred]]** / essential kin vs relatives, **[[child-keeping]]** (temporary and lasting fosterage within networks).
- Method stance: white outsider ethnography with explicit Introduction on entrée, reliability, and resident co-researchers; rejected sponsorship through the black professional establishment.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Training / support (from Acknowledgments):** Edward Bruner; F. K. Lehman; engagement with Nancie Gonzalez, Eva Hunt, Dimitri Shimkin, Charles and Betty Lou Valentine, Norman Whitten, Oscar Lewis (encouragement/criticism).
- **Interlocutors / foils in the book:** Oscar Lewis ([[culture-of-poverty]]); Hylan Lewis; Ulf Hannerz; Elliot Liebow (*Tally's Corner*); Charles Valentine; William Ryan; Piven & Cloward; Joyce Ladner; R. T. Smith; David Schneider (American kinship).
- **Later reception:** durable reference point for network approaches to poverty; foil for Desmond's [[disposable-ties]] (durable Stackian kin vs short-lived crisis ties).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Positionality (white woman / Black community) is a standing reflexivity topic; Stack addresses it in the Introduction. Later poverty ethnography has asked whether 1960s–70s Flats-style kin webs still operate under eviction, mass incarceration, and welfare reform (see [[disposable-ties]] contrast). Policy misreadings sometimes flatten Stack into "culture of resilience" without the structural unemployment/AFDC analysis she insists on.
