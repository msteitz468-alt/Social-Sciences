# Herbert Spencer

## Overview

Herbert Spencer (1820–1903), English philosopher and systematizer, developed a general theory of **evolution** as increasing [[differentiation-and-integration|differentiation and integration]] and applied it across biology, psychology, and society in the *System of Synthetic Philosophy*. His *Principles of Sociology* (1876–1896; ingested via [[spencer-principles-of-sociology-andreski-1969|Andreski’s 1969 abridgement]]) is the main sociological statement: society as [[social-organism]], institutional evolution by [[social-compounding|compounding]] and functional specialization, the triple [[sustaining-distributing-regulating-systems|sustaining / distributing / regulating]] architecture, and the polar [[militant-type-of-society|militant]] / [[industrial-type-of-society|industrial]] typology. proposed: [[social-evolutionism]] · proposed: [[social-organism]]

He did not do ethnographic fieldwork; the *Principles* rest on comparative use of historiography and 19th-century travellers’ and missionaries’ reports (Andreski 1969).

## Contributions

Drawn from the *Principles* (Andreski abridgement) unless noted:

- **[[social-organism]]** — Spencer argued a society is an entity because permanence of relations among discrete units gives the aggregate individuality; society and organism share growth, structural differentiation, and functional interdependence, but he also stressed **unlikenesses** (society’s discreteness; no social sensorium; the aggregate exists for its members, not members for the aggregate) (*Principles* Part I, “What is a Society?”).
- **[[differentiation-and-integration]]** — Evolution as joint increase of specialized functions and of mutual dependence/coordination; applied to social growth, industry, politics, and church.
- **[[social-compounding]]** — Social growth by compounding and re-compounding of groups (simple → compound → doubly/trebly compound), often under war, with sequential stages of composition (*Principles* Part I, Social Growth; Social Types).
- **[[sustaining-distributing-regulating-systems]]** — Threefold “systems of organs”: industrial sustentation, commercial/communicational distribution, and governmental-military regulation (with later partial autonomy of industrial regulation).
- **[[militant-type-of-society]] / [[industrial-type-of-society]]** — Polar organizational constitutions: compulsory cooperation, status, positive regulation vs. voluntary cooperation, contract, mainly negative regulation (*Principles* Part II, dedicated chapters).
- **Institutional genesis** — Comparative reconstructions of political headship, councils and representation, ministries, military and judicial systems, law, property, revenue; [[priesthood|ecclesiastical]] organization from ghost-propitiation and undifferentiated chief-priest roles; industrial institutions from division of labour through communal/gild regulation to slavery, serfdom, free labour and contract (*Principles* Parts II–IV).
- **[[comparative-method]]** — Inductive establishment of associated traits across societies of different kinds and stages before deductive interpretation (*Principles* Part I, Scope).

Andreski (1969) additionally credits Spencer with anticipations of structural-functional analysis and cybernetic/self-regulation ideas — a **reception claim**, not Spencer’s self-description.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Independent English systematizer**; no formal university training path in the academic sense; engaged political economy (Andreski traces the evolution formula’s seed to Adam Smith on division of labour generalized) and the Comtean demand for a science of society without adopting Comte’s three-stage philosophy of history as master key.
- **Relation to [[comte-auguste]] / [[positivism]]:** Andreski held that on pure theory of the social system Comte had already formulated much, while Spencer’s superiority was comparative factual range; Spencer’s own essay “Reasons for Dissenting from the Philosophy of M. Comte” (not in this abridgement body) marks explicit distance. - **reacted_against (in the *Principles* framing):** intentionalist accounts of institutions as mere products of design; ethnocentric assumption that modern arrangements are uniquely natural; pure isolation of “ideas” from social-evolutionary phases.
- **Reception line:** Victorian fame; later eclipse (“Who now reads Spencer?” — Andreski citing mid-20th-c. rhetoric); claimed forerunner of structural-functionalism and systems theory (Andreski); association with **social Darwinism** is contested — Andreski argued Spencer did not escape its pitfalls in some later writings (*Study of Sociology*) but that his valuable innovation was selection at the level of **groups and institutional arrangements**, not merely ideology for the rich.
- **Huxley, Lester Ward (via Andreski):** noted disharmony between organismic theory (suggesting centralization) and Spencer’s extreme laissez-faire politics.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Organicism:** Drawing society–organism analogies proves nothing by itself (standard critique Andreski restates); Spencer himself reduced the load-bearing likeness to mutual dependence of parts and treated analogy as scaffolding.
- **Ethnographic base:** Travellers’ and missionaries’ reports; Victorian evolutionary vocabulary (“savage,” “barbarian,” race-mixture constitutional claims) — attributed historical discourse, not modern ethnography or wiki-voice fact about peoples.
- **Social Darwinism association:** Contested; separate from the group/institution selection insight Andreski prizes.
- **Laissez-faire vs. organism:** Logical tension between organic centralization and anti-statist politics (Huxley, Ward, Andreski).
- **Industrial present:** Andreski: accounts of institutional evolution **peter out** approaching industrial civilization; weak as observer of his own day’s society compared with Tocqueville or Marx–Engels, except on bureaucracy and war-driven reversion to absolutism.
- **Policy polemic:** Andreski omitted final policy sections as unoriginal relative to Mill; not evaluated here from primary text.
- **Durkheim's critique (1893):** In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor]]*, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] made Spencer his chief polemical target — rejecting Spencer's contractualist [[industrial-type-of-society|industrial-type]] thesis that social solidarity is the spontaneous accord of self-interested contractors and that positive/state control recedes under industrialism. Durkheim countered that a society of pure interest would be "a state of war," that restitutive and administrative law have *grown* not shrunk, and that contract rests on [[non-contractual-elements-of-contract|non-contractual social conditions]]; he also reversed Spencer on the origin of individualism (early homogeneity, not coercion, effaced the individual). See [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]].

- **Polanyi's use of Spencer against himself:** [[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]] (*[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*, 1944) turned Spencer's *The Man versus the State* (1884) — a catalogue of "collectivist" legislation Spencer denounced as an antiliberal conspiracy — into evidence *against* the conspiracy thesis: the sheer diversity of the measures (food inspection, mine safety, vaccination, public libraries) proved they were pragmatic, dispersed responses to industrial conditions, not a concerted campaign. Polanyi also named Spencer among the economic liberals (with Mises and Lippmann) who repeated Adam Smith's "bartering savage" fallacy. See [[double-movement]], [[economic-liberalism]].

## From Stocking, *Victorian Anthropology* (1987)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1987; source [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987]].)* Stocking treats Spencer as an evolutionist who developed his position **before and independently of Darwin**: his 1857 essay "Progress: Its Law and Cause" formulated a universal law of evolution — movement from the homogeneous to the heterogeneous via "multiplication of effects" — applied alike to the inorganic, organic, and "superorganic" (social) realms. Spencer's mechanism was Lamarckian throughout (use-inheritance of acquired characteristics), and although he coined the phrase "survival of the fittest," he fitted it into this Lamarckian scheme rather than a Darwinian selectionist one; Darwin himself credited Spencer with laying evolutionary psychology's foundation. Stocking distinguishes Spencer's comparative method — resting on structural analogy between "low types of organisms and low types of societies" and an embryological model of development — from Tylor's, Lubbock's, and McLennan's systematic use of contemporary "savagery" data as evolutionary evidence; Tylor later found it "most strange" that Spencer never placed the newly discovered antiquity of man in its proper place in his scheme.

Spencer's evolutionary associationism gave the Victorian premise of [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]] a hereditarian cast: mental and "racial" differences between savage and civilized humanity became organic, inheritable, and hierarchically ranked (he held Europeans had "from ten to thirty cubic inches more brain than the Papuan"), and he portrayed "primitive man" as impulsive, improvident, and mentally inflexible — traits he explicitly analogized to those of "women of the inferior ranks" of British society, using the analogy to argue against extending women the franchise, a marked retreat from the radicalism of his own early *Social Statics* chapter "The Rights of Women" (which Harriet Taylor sought to reprint, and which Spencer later declined to support in Mill's 1867 suffrage petition).

Stocking traces Spencer's reputation from Darwin's 1870 prediction that he would be remembered as England's greatest living philosopher, through a mid-twentieth-century "intellectual historical purgatory" (Crane Brinton's 1933 "Who now reads Spencer?"; Talcott Parsons's "Spencer is dead"), to a partial late-twentieth-century revival among neo-evolutionists. He argues Spencer's influence on British social anthropology, though substantial, was indirect — transmitted through Durkheim (via Radcliffe-Brown and, to a lesser extent, Malinowski) into structural-functionalism, forming a plausible line "from Spencer to Durkheim to British...functional anthropology to structural-functional sociology in the United States." Stocking also credits Spencer's tabular ethnographic compilations in *Descriptive Sociology* as the ultimate ancestor of the later Human Relations Area Files, distinguishing this data-compilation contribution from Tylor's systematic statistical cross-cultural correlation method.
