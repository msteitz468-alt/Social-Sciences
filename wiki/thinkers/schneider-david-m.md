# David M. Schneider (1918–1995)

American cultural anthropologist whose critique of kinship studies is a central interlocutor for [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]'s *[[sahlins-what-kinship-is-2013|What Kinship Is—And Is Not]]* (2013). This page is densified from Sahlins's reading (and standard bibliographic facts); Schneider's monographs are **not yet primary-ingested** — claims about his positions remain attributed through Sahlins unless independently confirmed later.

## Overview

Schneider is best known for *American Kinship: A Cultural Account* (1968), which treated U.S. kinship as a cultural system organized by the contrast between **biogenetic substance** ("blood") and **code for conduct** (law, marriage), and for the later conclusion that **kinship as a cross-cultural analytic category does not exist** — anthropology had projected a Western folk model onto others (*A Critique of the Study of Kinship*, 1984). Fieldwork included Yap (Micronesia). Institutionally associated with the University of Chicago's symbolic/culturalist anthropology.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — primarily as reconstructed in Sahlins 2013.)*

- **American kinship as cultural system:** "blood" ties presented in native folklore as natural and irrevocable are as conventionally made as relatives by marriage; "**Substance** is as constructed as **code**" (Sahlins's gloss of Schneider).
- **Dissolution of kinship:** long study convinced him there is no distinct cultural domain of kinship cross-culturally; the same *physis*/*nomos* opposition structures kinship, nationalism, and religion.
- **Paradoxical reception:** Sahlins notes that announcing the end of kinship spurred "numerous and enlightening analyses" by scholars indebted to him (Carsten's relatedness literature and the post-Schneider constructivist turn) — "the logical force of the famous observation of the Cretan that 'All Cretans are liars.'"

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Parsonian systems theory** — Sahlins roots Schneider's culture vs social/normative split in Parsons's differentiation of pure cultural symbols from normative action systems; Sahlins critiques this dualism as making kinship practices look non-symbolic.
- **Chicago / symbolic anthropology** context — contemporaneous with [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]-style cultural analysis (Sahlins pairs Schneider with the Geertzes as applying ethnocentric *physis*/*nomos* to non-things).
- **Successors who reopened kinship** — Janet Carsten and the "relatedness" literature; [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]]; Bamford & Leach; Franklin & McKinnon — attending to "symbols and meanings" while refusing Schneider's closure of the topic.
- **[[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]** — Sahlins's key contrast: Viveiros found one animistic regime across kinship, gift, and magic and treated that as revealing kinship's cosmic place; Schneider found cross-domain ontological similarity and concluded kinship did not exist. See [[kinship-biology-vs-culture-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed — Sahlins 2013 unless noted.)*

- Sahlins: Schneider's theory **recapitulates** the American blood vs in-law dualism it analyzes; his culture concept (≈ ontology) wrongly implies that non-exclusive symbols dissolve specific kinship properties.
- Sahlins recovers **[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]** against Schneider's reading of him as covert biologism — Durkheim as radical constructivist of mutual participation (totemic kinship as mutual participation in one another's existence).
- Viveiros inversion (via Sahlins): same style of cross-register analysis, opposite research program — open kinship studies, do not close them.
- Constructivist orthodoxy Sahlins endorses is "happily" the fruit of Schneider's failed closure.
