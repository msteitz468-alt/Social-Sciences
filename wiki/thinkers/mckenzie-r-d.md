# R. D. McKenzie

## Overview

Roderick Duncan McKenzie (1885–1940), American sociologist associated with the Chicago program of [[human-ecology|human ecology]]. His chapter in *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925), "The Ecological Approach to the Study of the Human Community," is a founding definitional statement.

## Contributions

- **Definition:** human ecology as spatial and temporal relations under selective, distributive, accommodative forces; effect of position on institutions and behavior.
- **Plant/animal analogy with human additions** (mobility + purpose); aggregate outcomes undesigned.
- **Four community types:** primary service, commercial, industrial, non-economic-base (resorts, capitals, etc.).
- **Cyclic growth** to climax/culmination; invasion restarting cycles.
- **Invasion stages** and use vs occupant-type change; climax resistance.
- **Natural areas** as Chicago departmental term; land-value centers/peripheries; mobility gradient center↔periphery; cultural areas and mores (Seattle voting/sex/age illustrations).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Program ally of [[park-robert-e|Park]] and [[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] within the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Biotic metaphor and "undesirable invader" language later criticized as underplaying culture, power, and institutions.

## Historiography

Often cited as co-founder of human ecology with Park; fuller biography awaits dedicated sources.
