# Robert S. Wistrich

## Overview

Robert Solomon Wistrich (1945–2015) was a historian of antisemitism and modern Jewish history, professor at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and, from 2002, head of its Vidal Sassoon International Center for the Study of Antisemitism (SICSA). Born in Soviet Kazakhstan to Polish-Jewish refugees (the family was repatriated to Poland in 1946, experienced the postwar antisemitic atmosphere there firsthand, and left for France within a year — his own account in the 2010 book), educated at Cambridge and London, he became the most prolific late-twentieth-century chronicler of antisemitism's history and its leading academic proponent of the [[new-antisemitism]] thesis.

## Contributions

- **Antisemitism as "the longest hatred"**: Wistrich argued (attributed) for deep continuity from pagan and Christian anti-Judaism through racial antisemitism to contemporary anti-Zionism — a single evolving demonological tradition rather than disconnected episodes. *[[wistrich-lethal-obsession-2010|A Lethal Obsession]]* (2010) is the fullest statement.
- **The [[new-antisemitism]] thesis**: contemporary anti-Zionism, where it demonizes Israel as a collective Jew, is (Wistrich argued) the current mutation of the older obsession — a claim contested by other scholars; see [[new-antisemitism-debate]].
- Historical work on socialism and Jews, Habsburg Vienna, and Soviet anti-Zionism connecting left-wing traditions to anti-Jewish politics.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Wistrich built explicitly on [[cohn-norman|Norman Cohn]]'s demonological-myth framework (*[[cohn-warrant-for-genocide-1967|Warrant for Genocide]]*), extending Cohn's analysis of the [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]] and the *[[protocols-of-the-elders-of-zion|Protocols]]* into the post-1945 Middle East and the contemporary left.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Critics (Brian Klug, Antony Lerman, and others — positions on [[new-antisemitism-debate]]) argued that Wistrich's continuity thesis conflates criticism of Israeli policy with antisemitism and flattens distinct phenomena into a single transhistorical hatred. Admirers credit him with documenting, earlier than most, the migration of European conspiracist antisemitism into Islamist discourse.

## Historiography

Died 2015 in Rome. His SICSA-era works (2002–2015) frame much of the current scholarly and policy debate over antisemitism's definition (e.g., IHRA working-definition disputes postdate him but inherit his framing).
