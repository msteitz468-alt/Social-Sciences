# Mitchell Duneier

## Overview

Mitchell Duneier (b. 1961) is an American urban sociologist and ethnographer, best known for *[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]]* (1999), a study of the informal book- and magazine-vending economy of poor, mostly Black men in Greenwich Village. Chicago-trained and mentored in fieldwork by Howard S. Becker, he works self-consciously in the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] urban-ethnographic tradition while pushing its methods toward unusual transparency — real names, taped verbatim data, member-checking, and a published informant afterword. He has taught at the University of California–Santa Barbara, the University of Wisconsin–Madison, and Princeton, where he chaired the sociology department.

## Contributions

- **[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]] (1999)** — his central work; see the [[duneier-sidewalk-study|study page]] and [[hubs/studies/duneier-sidewalk-study|Studies Hub]]. Coined the **[[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]]** and **[[interactional-vandalism|interactional vandalism]]** concepts, named the **[[extended-place-method|extended place method]]**, and proposed a mirror-image "fixed windows" thesis against **[[broken-windows|broken-windows]]** policing.
- **Slim's Table (1992)** — ethnography of Black working-class men at a Chicago cafeteria, arguing against underclass caricature; his first book (a collection gap here).
- **Ghetto (2016)** — an intellectual history of the ghetto as concept and place, from Venice through Chicago (collection gap).
- **Method as content.** Duneier's "Statement on Method" appendix in *Sidewalk* is a widely taught argument for verbatim tape-recording over reconstructed quotation, for "checking stuff out" (investigative verification of informants' accounts), and for treating the ethnographer's race and class position as data (attributed).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Duneier names his forebears explicitly in *Sidewalk*: [[du-bois-souls-study|W. E. B. Du Bois]], Robert E. Park, W. I. Thomas, Caroline Ware, Charles S. Johnson, Everett Hughes, St. Clair Drake, [[whyte-william-foote|William Foote Whyte]], Horace Cayton, Robert and Helen Lynd ([[lynd-middletown-study|Middletown]]), Howard S. Becker, [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]], Elliot Liebow (*Tally's Corner*), Gerald Suttles, Herbert Gans, and [[anderson-elijah|Elijah Anderson]]. He learned fieldwork from **Becker** (the "Becker principle" — situational structure outweighs the observer's presence) and takes Liebow's *Tally's Corner* appendix as a direct precedent for handling intervention and reciprocity. His **[[extended-place-method|extended place method]]** is positioned against Michael Burawoy's extended case method and alongside George Marcus's multi-sited ethnography; Erik Olin Wright dubbed his approach "diagnostic ethnography." He draws his affirmative frame from urbanist [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Sidewalk*'s reflexive apparatus is widely praised as raising the accountability bar for ethnography, while critics note that a white ethnographer's rapport still shapes what can be seen — a limit Duneier foregrounds himself (the taped antisemitic aside about him; his admission that trust was sometimes "nothing more than an illusion"). Structural critics invoke Stephen Steinberg's "ethnographic fallacy" — that close observation can obscure macro-structures — which Duneier answers by reading widely in the urban-poverty literature ([[wilson-william-julius|W. J. Wilson]], Massey, Jencks, Sassen). No restudy of the Sixth Avenue fieldwork exists.

## Historiography

Primary: *Sidewalk* (FSG, 1999), with photographs by Ovie Carter and the afterword by informant Hakim Hasan; the "Statement on Method" appendix is the key document for his approach. *Slim's Table* (1992) and *Ghetto* (2016) frame the career on either side and remain ingestion gaps here.
