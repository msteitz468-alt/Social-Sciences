# Joan M. Gero (1944–2016)

American archaeologist; co-editor with [[conkey-margaret-w|Margaret W. Conkey]] of *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology: Women and Prehistory]]* (1991), the multi-author charter collection for **[[feminist-archaeology]]** in this vault. Known for critique of the **woman-at-home ideology** in archaeological practice (1985) and for **[[genderlithics|Genderlithics]]** — women’s roles in stone-tool production.

## Overview

Gero co-organized the 1988 Wedge Plantation conference (“Women and Production in Prehistory”) that produced the 1991 volume, with deliberate emphasis on prehistoric cases as the hardest challenge to androcentric thinking. Her own chapter rejects the equation of flintknapping with maleness (Thomas 1983; NSF-reviewer common sense she quotes) and builds a theoretical-plus-material strategy for modeling female participation in lithic production — not a “technological breakthrough” that sexes each artifact.

## Contributions

*(Attributed from *Engendering* 1991 and works cited therein.)*

- **Co-editing *Engendering Archaeology*** with Conkey — program aims, dual object (prehistory + practice), anti-add-women-and-stir.
- **[[genderlithics]]:** critique of “tool” vs “utilized flake”; male monopoly of experimental knapping literature (~100% male published replication samples vs more balanced microwear); constraints of scheduling, raw-material access/control, strength, and social value; **minimum female-participation criteria** — habitation contexts, local raw materials, expedient flakes (~90% of lithic record); Huaricoto (Peru) EIP pattern: bifaces drop to ~4%, local metashale flake tools dominate — model of women’s household lithics rising as male prestige shifts off formal stone.
- **Woman-at-home ideology** (1985): sociopolitics of who does field vs lab work in the profession — cited throughout the volume as evidence of androcentric practice.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborator and co-editor with Conkey; conference community around Wedge 1988; series context under [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] Social Archaeology. Engages [[feminist-anthropology]] and feminist philosophy of science via volume contributors ([[wylie-alison|Wylie]]). Dialogue with lithic experimental tradition and ethnoarchaeology (Gould, Hayden, et al.) as sources of male-observer bias.

## Later Contributions, per Johnson (2011)

*(Attributed; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson's secondary account reports Gero's **grant-funding study**: she found men were statistically more likely to receive fieldwork/excavation grants, while women were more likely funded for lab-based "archaeological housework" (pottery, small-finds, and environmental analysis) — a funding pattern Gero argued shaped which interpretations, including stereotypical gender images, got produced. With Conkey (1997), Gero also distinguished the theoretically diverse "archaeology of gender" from specifically **feminist archaeology**, defined via four criteria: inseparability of politics and knowledge-products; rationality as a "mythical conflation"; an "intimate," nuanced cognitive style; and a challenge to disciplinary arrangements. See [[feminist-archaeology]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Gender attribution of individual tools remains underdetermined (Gero’s own caveat); reverse stereotyping risk; Huaricoto model is interpretive. Later gender archaeology extends genderlithics beyond the 1991 case without treating it as settled law.
