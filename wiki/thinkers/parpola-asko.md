# Asko Parpola

## Overview

Asko Parpola is a Finnish Indologist and Indus-script specialist whose *Deciphering the Indus Script* (1994) and seal-corpus collaborations (Joshi & Parpola; Shah & Parpola) are among the most frequently cited scholarly authorities in [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]]’s *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* (1998). Kenoyer draws on Parpola for **script structure**, **proposed Dravidian readings**, **seal typology**, **royal-title hypotheses**, and **astronomical orientation** of city planning — while keeping full decipherment claims outside demonstrated academic consensus.

## Contributions

*(As presented and delimited by Kenoyer 1998 — not a full digest of Parpola’s oeuvre.)*

### Script and language

- **Independence of the writing system.** On computer-aided comparison of symbol sequences, Kenoyer reports Parpola’s conclusion that the Indus script is **not directly related** to any known writing system; Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Indus each solved local needs differently rather than by simple borrowing.
- **Corpus baseline.** Kenoyer’s counts (~4,200 inscribed objects; average ~5 signs; longest series 26; majority from Mohenjo-daro and Harappa; ~60 sites represented) rest on corpus work associated with Parpola and collaborators.
- **Direction and media.** Fairservis and Parpola are cited on right-to-left (and occasional boustrophedon) practice and on the wide range of media (steatite, terracotta, copper, gold, bone, etc.).
- **Dravidian / rebus program.** Kenoyer summarizes the majority scholarly view that the language is **Dravidian**-related (with Brahui noted in Baluchistan) and presents Parpola’s method: isolate shared proto-Dravidian roots; pictographic concrete signs; abstract meanings via **rebus**. Kenoyer flags Parpola’s most convincing example as the **fish sign** = Dravidian *min* “fish” / “glitter, star” — still a **proposed** reading, not a completed decipherment. Kenoyer’s own standing position: all published full decipherments (50+) fail academic standards of proof.
- **Royal titles and seal hierarchy.** Kenoyer cites Parpola on possible “leader/king” signs and on short inscriptions with majestic zebu seals concentrated at the largest cities.

### Religion, astronomy, and iconography

- **City orientation.** Kenoyer links cardinal orientation of Indus cities to religious belief and precise astronomical observation, citing Parpola 1994: early directional reckoning from rising/setting sun, moon, or bright stars (Polaris not yet the pole star ca. 2300 BCE); east–west lines via stellar sightings or solar gnomon; possible use of Aldebaran (*rohini*) or the Pleiades (*krittikah*).
- **Horned-deity and “Pashupati” corpus.** Joshi & Parpola catalogue numbers (e.g. M-304) underwrite Kenoyer’s description of the multi-animal yogic seal; Kenoyer **refuses** confirmed identification with later Hindu Śiva even while recording the “proto-Shiva” scholarly habit (see [[pashupati-seal]]).
- **Gilgamesh-type combat and Mesopotamian parallels.** Kenoyer notes Parpola among scholars linking two-animal hero combat motifs to Mesopotamian Gilgamesh seals, while allowing independent invention.
- **Tokens/passes and geometric seals.** Parpola’s discussions of tokens and geometric seals feed Kenoyer’s account of non-impression seals as amulets/passes.

### External trade

- Kenoyer cites Parpola & Brunswig (among others) on **Meluhha** trade contexts, including the possibility that some long carnelian beads in Mesopotamian styles were made with Indus drilling technology by a “Meluhhan minority” of migrant artisans (attributed hypothesis).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Finnish Indus-script project; corpus partnership with Indian/Pakistani catalogue editors (Joshi, Shah).
- In Kenoyer’s notes, Parpola sits beside [[fairservis-walter|Fairservis]] (graffiti, “language X”), Marshall/Mackay/Wheeler baselines, and Gulf archaeologists (Cleuziou, Tosi, Potts).
- Intellectual conversation partner for Kenoyer on script **use** (administrative, identity, ritual) even where Kenoyer will not endorse a full reading.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kenoyer repeatedly treats full Dravidian rebus decipherments — including Parpola’s — as **unproven** against strict standards, while using Parpola’s corpus, statistical, and comparative work as the field’s best technical infrastructure. Rival language hypotheses and rival iconographic readings (Sullivan, Srinivasan, Hiltebeitel on “Pashupati,” etc.) remain open; this page does not adjudicate the [[indus-script]] debate.
