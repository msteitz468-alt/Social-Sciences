# Kwang-chih Chang (K. C. Chang)

## Overview

Kwang-chih Chang (張光直, 1931–2001) was the archaeologist who introduced Chinese
prehistory to anglophone anthropology and repeatedly re-synthesized it as data
accumulated. Trained at National Taiwan University under [[li-chi|Li Chi]] (excavator of
Anyang) and then at Harvard, he wrote *The Archaeology of Ancient China*
([[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]]; revised through a 4th edition in 1986),
for decades the standard English-language framework for the field. He taught at Yale
and later Harvard.

## Contributions

- **A cultural-historical framework for Chinese prehistory.** Chang organized a
  scattered, fast-growing PRC excavation literature into an ordered sequence —
  Palaeolithic, Mesolithic, [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]], [[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]],
  [[shang-civilization|Shang]], [[zhou-civilization|Chou]] — legible to prehistorians
  comparing China with other world civilizations.
- **The Lungshanoid expansion (1963, attributed).** In the first edition he argued that
  a single North China [[nuclear-area|nuclear area]] generated the Lungshanoid horizon,
  which then spread outward and underlay the regional Neolithic cultures and the Shang
  state — a **monocentric, diffusionist** model of Chinese origins.
- **Revision to the "interaction sphere" (later work, attributed).** As regional
  discoveries multiplied, Chang abandoned strict monocentrism, recasting early China as
  a set of regional cultures bound in a mutually stimulating **interaction sphere** — a
  reversal recorded on [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]].
- **Shamanism and political authority (attributed).** In his later synthesis Chang read
  Shang bronze art, writing, and ritual as instruments by which elites monopolized
  communication with ancestors and spirits, making religious/shamanic access — not
  control of production or technology — the lever of political power in early China.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Chang's teacher [[li-chi|Li Chi]] directed the Academia Sinica excavations at Anyang in
the late 1920s–1930s and supervised Chang's Taiwan bachelor's thesis on the Lungshan
culture, from which the 1963 book grew (Li Chi, foreword, 1962). At Harvard, Chang
worked in the Americanist settlement-archaeology tradition of [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon
Willey]], which shaped his attention to settlement pattern and regional context rather
than art-object typology.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The 1963 edition's monocentric nuclear-area thesis was superseded — first by Chang
himself, then by the polycentric consensus of later Chinese archaeology (regional
Neolithic centers such as Hongshan, Liangzhu, and the Yangtze rice cultures were shown
to be early and partly independent). His shamanism thesis for Shang religion is
influential but contested, criticized for reading a single ethnographic category
(shamanism) across a very different institutional world. His stature as the bridge
between Chinese and Western archaeology is uncontested.

## Historiography

Chang's successive editions are themselves a record of the field's transformation
between 1963 and 1986 — from a data-poor, pre-radiocarbon, largely relative chronology
to a radiocarbon-anchored, multi-regional picture. Read the 1963 first edition as a
historiographic document of Cold War–era anglophone sinological archaeology (Chang could
not excavate on the mainland and worked from published reports), not as current fact.
See [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963]].

### Liu & Chen (2012) assessment of the four editions

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]], citing Falkenhausen
1995.)* Liu and Chen describe Chang's four editions of *Archaeology of Ancient China*
(1963, 1968, 1977, 1986) as the most comprehensive and authoritative English-language
reference on Chinese archaeology across that period, and read the sequence itself as
tracking the field's shift from a monocentric to a multicentric model of Chinese origins:
the first three editions treated the [[nuclear-area|Central Plain]] as civilization's
nucleus, while the fourth edition (1986) replaced that framing with the "Chinese
interaction sphere" concept, covering a much broader geographic scope. This corroborates,
from a second source, the trajectory already recorded above (Chang's own revision from
nuclear-area diffusionism to the interaction-sphere model) and situates it within the
field's wider 1970s–80s move away from Central-Plain-only narratives (see
[[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]).
