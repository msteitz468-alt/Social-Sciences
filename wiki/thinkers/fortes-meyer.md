# Meyer Fortes

South African–born British social anthropologist (1906–1983). Co-editor of *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) with [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] and author of the [[tallensi|Tallensi]] chapter; author of *[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]* (1949; study [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]); author of the Ashanti kinship chapter in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Major figure of British [[structural-functionalism|structural-functional]] Africanist anthropology and kinship theory. *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi* (1945) and *Kinship and the Social Order* (1969) are **not yet ingested**.

## Overview

In APS Fortes appears in two capacities: (1) **co-editor**, framing with Evans-Pritchard the comparative programme and the Group A / Group B typology of African political systems; (2) **field ethnographer** of the Tallensi of the Northern Territories of the Gold Coast, using Taleland as a Group B case—segmentary clanship, fundamental Namoos/Talis cleavage, dual *na’am* (chiefship) and *tɛndaan* (Earth-priest) offices, and political order without a centralized coercive state. He confined the APS Tallensi account to the period preceding his first visit (1934), deferring Indirect Rule reforms begun in 1933, and grounded population figures in the 1931 Census. *The Web of Kinship* (1949) completes the monographic Tallensi project for domestic and cognatic structure (companion *Dynamics of Clanship*, 1945, still outstanding), introducing the ethnographic base of [[complementary-filiation]] and the [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]].

## Contributions

### Co-editor of *African Political Systems* (1940)

With Evans-Pritchard, Fortes organized the IAI symposium volume that became a charter text of British Africanist political anthropology. The Introduction (with the editors) sets the comparative frame of [[group-a-group-b-political-systems]], the role of [[mystical-values-of-office]], and the problem of political structure relative to the whole social structure—claims extracted and filed primarily under the study and source pages. Fortes’s Tallensi chapter is one of the volume’s principal Group B exemplars alongside the Nuer and Kavirondo cases.

### Tallensi political ethnography (APS chapter)

From the APS Tallensi claims, Fortes’s major analytic moves include:

- **No tribal state, yet political system:** He reported that twenty-five years earlier no one had authority over all Tallensi, no “tribal” government or citizenship, no centralized state for the whole society; yet a social structure fixed status, rights, and territorial/associational units, with formal leadership within units, institutions of mutual dependence, and explicit sanctions—an indigenous system of a “different character” from centralized kingdoms.
- **Segmentary clanship and equilibrium:** Maximal lineages and composite clans; criss-crossing clanship fields so that no clan was a closed unit; every lineage as a system of mutually balancing segments—centrifugal autonomy of segments vs. centripetal forces of clanship, cult, and political interdependence. studied: [[tallensi]]; concept links: [[segmentary-lineage-system]], [[complementary-opposition]].
- **Fundamental cleavage:** Autochthonous Talis (“owners of the land” / Earth-priests) vs. immigrant-origin Namoos (chiefship); myths of Mosuor and primordial *tindaanas* as complementary sanctifications of dual offices.
- **Polar offices *na’am* / *tɛndaan*:** Central factor of Tale political organization—homologous yet opposed, indissolubly coupled; community defined by complementary functions of chief and *tendaana*; Great Festivals as functional synthesis of the widest community without a fixed political entity.
- **Ritual as political medium:** Ancestor cult as ideology of lineage; Earth cult as ideology of locality; mystical values defining what chiefs and *tindaanas* could and must do “to prosper the community”; religion as instrument of social control.
- **Economy and political form:** Equalitarian fixed-cultivation peasantry without economic classes cutting across lineage/clan—utmost importance for political organization; chiefs/*tindaanas* without over-rights to farm-land rent or tribute.
- **Colonial transformation:** Administration monopoly of force; chiefs as administrative agents; *tindaanas* stripped of political status; new wealth and private-interest orientation of chiefs—documented as change of the system, not omitted.

### *The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi* (1949)

Ingested monograph (source [[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949]]; study [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]). Second part of the Tale social-structure analysis (after *Dynamics of Clanship*, 1945). Major moves (attributed):

- **Warp / woof:** lineage-clanship as corporate warp; kinship/family as personal woof — interpenetrating “like blood and tissue.”
- **[[complementary-filiation]]:** matrilateral/uterine kinship (*soog*) and the mother’s-brother complex (*ahab*/*ahay*) as centrifugal forces balancing centripetal patriliny — not residual bilaterality and not [[double-descent]].
- **[[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]]:** joint family and homestead (*yir*) as one entity whose composition is a cycle of growth, fission, and reintegration (1934 N=61 sample).
- **Parenthood and generations:** morally absolute parent–child bond under structural tension; ancestors as parent images; sibling grades (*soog* / *sunzo* / *mabiirat*).
- **Functions of kinship:** kinship as irreducible articulating principle and primarily a **moral mechanism** translating interdependence into concrete social life — affirming Radcliffe-Brown’s “organized whole,” distancing one-sided Malinowskian needs or pure sex/procreation explanations (also a gentle correction of Firth’s biological emphasis).

Foreword names Malinowski and Radcliffe-Brown as chief teachers on kinship and Firth’s *We, the Tikopia* as field stimulus. Dedicated to Sonia L. Fortes as co-fieldworker.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Structural-functional equilibrium language** in the Tallensi portrait (mutually balancing segments; centripetal/centrifugal kinship balance; long-run stability of festival cycle) later attracted the broader critique of British functionalism for underplaying history, conflict, and colonial co-production—even though Fortes himself detailed colonial transformation of chiefly secular power in APS §XI and noted impending postwar change in the *Web* Foreword.
- **Segmentary lineage model** as a general Africanist type was later contested in comparative and historical work (file under theory/debate pages as those sources are ingested); the Tallensi case remains a classic *primary* ethnographic construction, not automatically a universal model.
- **Complementary filiation** afterlife: contested by alliance theorists and critics who saw “smuggled bilaterality”; still standard teaching vocabulary for non-corporate other-parent ties in unilineal systems.
- **Companion gap:** *Dynamics of Clanship* (1945) not yet ingested — do not treat APS + *Web* alone as Fortes’s full Tallensi corpus.

## Historiography

Standard histories of British social anthropology place Fortes at Cambridge and among the core Africanists of the R-B / Malinowski generation. APS (1940) is the early public comparative statement; Tallensi monographs of the later 1940s elaborate clanship and kinship beyond the APS political sketch — of which *Web of Kinship* (1949) is now ingested. ASKAM Ashanti chapter (1950) adds his postwar Gold Coast kinship analysis. ## Ashanti kinship and marriage (ASKAM, 1950)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, Fortes contributed “Kinship and Marriage among the Ashanti” (pp. 252–284), based on Ashanti Social Survey inquiries 1945–6 (confirming Rattray’s accuracy with Busia and Kyei) while correcting misreadings. Attributed core (range 8; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Political frame:** Ashanti as most numerous Akan of the southern Gold Coast (~750,000); eighteenth-century confederation around Golden Stool; British wars ending 1901 annexation; political history disperses kin groups and shapes kinship institutions.
- **Matriliny as key:** following Rattray, matrilineal descent as basis of localized lineage organization generalized through dispersed clans; every free person (*odehye*) member of mother’s *abusua* and citizen of its chiefdom; lineage = all descendants of both sexes from a known ancestress in unbroken female line; segmentary structure with attached alien segments barred from office but protected from public origin-insult (*Obi nkyere obi ase*). Lineage head (*abusua panin*) holds male ancestral stools; “one lineage is one blood” / “one person” as corporate unity (exogamy, *mogya fra* incest extension)—not physiological theory. Only eight clans; same term *abusua* for clan and lineage.
- **Correction of equal dual descent:** specially rejects the view (e.g. Murdock) that Ashanti equally recognize matri-sibs *abusua* and patri-sibs *ntoro*—matriliny alone provides corporate jural-political organization; *ntoro* expresses ritual/value of paternity without corporate group, inheritance rights, or (contra Rattray) marriage bar by shared *ntoro* alone. Herskovits’s Asokore re-labelling rejected as non-standard. (R-B Introduction treated Ashanti as nearest African approach to pure mother-right via Fortes.)
- **Dual pull (domestic thesis):** matrilineal descent governs legal/moral relations and political groups, but households reconcile **conflicting claims of matriliny (incl. motherhood) vs marriage and paternity**—an old Ashanti problem aggravated by missionary teaching and modern fixed property (cocoa farms, buildings). Maximal-lineage solidarity declined in personal/domestic matters; matrilineal strength within domestic groups remained; by law inheritance still confined to matrilineage (three of four cocoa farms passed matrilineally despite denunciation and a dead-letter Confederacy thirds rule).
- **Mother–child bond:** keystone; disrespect to mother ≈ sacrilege; *Wo na awu a, wo abusua asa*—if your mother dies you have no lineage-kin left; birth customarily in mother’s natal home (fixes lineage/citizenship).
- **Father / *ntoro*:** conception ideology (mother’s blood + male *ntoro*); *sunsum*/*kra*/*ntoro* as spiritual bond without organized *ntoro* groups; father chooses name; limited legal authority; polar relationships father–child vs mother–child / MB.
- **Mother’s brother (*wofa*):** sole legal authority of parental generation within segment; accepts *nsa* for legal marriage; sister’s son as prospective heir (ambivalence; *Wofase eye dom*—“sister’s son is enemy”); potential levirate (*kuna aware*) obsolescing; segregation of conflicting kinship fields as practical solution.
- **Brother–sister pivot:** jural system pivots on uterine sibling bond—brother’s legal power over sister’s children; sister as continuity of his descent line; men hard-pressed between own children and sisters’ children.
- **Marriage:** *tiri nsa* payment; high divorce; preferred cross-cousin (*wofa ba* MBD); ≥75% spouses same/adjacent villages (1945); contemporary strains between matriliny and conjugal family under cocoa/missionary change.

## Gluckman's review of *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi* (1945)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], "An Advance in African Sociology," Essay I of *[[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963|Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa]]*, originally published 1945/46.)* Fortes' thirty-month [[tallensi|Tallensi]] fieldwork (Northern Territories, Gold Coast) is contrasted by Gluckman with [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s one year among the Nuer; Fortes explicitly frames his own work as following Evans-Pritchard's lead. Gluckman praises the book's central thesis — that Tallensi lineage structure is "far more than any of its functions," an organizing principle with its own autonomy rather than a reducible instrumental mechanism, such that "the lineage structure at a given time incapsulates all that is structurally relevant of its past phases and at the same time continually thrusts its growing-points forward": the "**dynamic equilibrium** of a lineage is an **equilibrium in time**." Gluckman also credits Fortes with adding "a new term to our vocabulary" by distinguishing **clanship** — a category/field of relations between lineages defined by critical norms (exogamy, leviratic rights, non-seduction of fellow clansmen's wives) — from **clan**, a group linked by putative common descent; and with coining **intercalary lineages** for the lineages that link two clans across a structural cleavage and can act as traditional peacemakers, belonging to both sides and wholly to neither (see [[intercalary-role]]).

**[POSITION — Gluckman vs. Fortes, patrilateral/matrilateral principles.]** Gluckman argues Fortes mis-stated the relation between patrilateral and matrilateral principles in Tallensi lineage segmentation. Fortes held that "patrilateral origin and matrilateral origin work in polar opposition to each other," the former uniting segments and the latter dividing them. Gluckman contends this is "contradicted by his own facts": maternal-origin linkage is only an *additional* bond and point of fission between already-coordinate lineages founded by different sons of one polygynist, not itself the primary source of differentiation — which follows simply from each son founding a new lineage, regardless of maternal origin. Gluckman proposes recasting the point via Evans-Pritchard's Nuer formulation that "the social principle of agnatic descent is, by a kind of paradox, traced through the mother." Gluckman separately endorses (via Kaberry's review) a broader charge that Fortes and other lineage-theory-focused Africanists (with Southall, Fallers, and to a lesser extent Barnes) were distracted by the dominance of "lineage theory" in 1950s British anthropology from examining state-power struggles directly — while crediting Evans-Pritchard's and Fortes' own influence as the cause of the preoccupation and praising its "great creative value."

## From Leach, *Rethinking Anthropology* (1961) — as interlocutor

*(Attributed — Leach’s report of Fortes 1959b and the dispute.)* Fortes’s *Man* “rejoinder to Leach” and related pieces defend [[complementary-filiation]] against Leach’s charge that it disguises [[affinity]]; Fortes holds complementary filiation is a function of affinal relations and the kinship reciprocal of the marriage tie. Leach’s Ch. 1 is framed as rejoinder; Ch. 5 bans applying the concept to Kachin–Lakher materials. Record both positions; do not adjudicate. Source: [[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961]].

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015 historiography; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].)*

- **Formation and “Mandarins.”** South African psychology doctorate and East London child-guidance work after Cape Town R-B lectures; LSE under Malinowski, where Malinowski derisively dubbed Fortes, [[nadel-s-f|Nadel]], and Hofstra the “Mandarins.” Fortes later complained Malinowski “had no sense for social organization… no real understanding of kinship or political organization,” and that large African societies could not run on the pattern of an Australian horde or Oceanic community.
- **Oxford trio and [[descent-theory]].** With [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] at Oxford (Fortes acting research lecturer 1939; reader postwar), co-architect of *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) and the [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage]] model for Nuer/Tallensi-type “stateless” polities. Kuper reports Fortes’s recollection of R-B on Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer observations: “My dear Evans-Pritchard, it’s perfectly simple, that’s a segmentary lineage system….” Fortes’s 1953 *American Anthropologist* formalization became the authoritative statement of the Oxford paradigm “descent theory.” Leading 1940s kinship theorist: gently disengaged from R-B naturalism; split corporate clan/lineage volumes from interpersonal kinship; coined [[complementary-filiation]] as moral/personal (not corporate) other-parent ties that cross-cut descent groups.
- **Cambridge chair and Fortes–Leach rift.** Moved to Cambridge 1950; Kuper: cleared ethnologists and remade the department “in the image of Radcliffe-Brown’s Oxford,” restating [[social-structure]] and descent dogmas while privately deploring Evans-Pritchard’s postwar “lapse.” Recruited [[leach-edmund|Leach]] (1953); by the early 1960s the small department was deeply divided—Fortes (with [[goody-jack|Goody]]) directing Africanists expected to work in the R-B tradition; Leach camp Malinowskian and increasingly Lévi-Straussian; students sorted into “Fortesian Africanist or structuralist South Asianist.” Kuper (research student, King’s, from 1962) records lunch characterizations: Leach that Fortes “never really understood” lower-middle-class Cambridge; Fortes “never forget that they don’t like Jews.” Personal friend among the elders Kuper names.
- **Colonial applied side-work.** IAI fellow; limited applied chores (Tallensi marriage law for courts; Northern Territories advice)—Kuper: dutiful diversions, not career applied anthropology. With [[schapera-isaac|Schapera]] and [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], challenged Malinowski’s “culture contact” for a single social-relations field including colonial agents.
