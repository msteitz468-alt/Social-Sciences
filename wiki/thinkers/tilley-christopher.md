# Christopher Tilley

British archaeologist and a principal architect of Anglophone [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] / interpretive archaeology, known for collaborative critique with [[shanks-michael|Michael Shanks]] and for **landscape phenomenology**. Primary-ingested: *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* (with Shanks, 1987), *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]* (with Shanks, 1987/1992), and *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (Berg, 1994).

## Overview

Tilley is listed among originators of the post-processual program (with [[hodder-ian|Hodder]], [[shanks-michael|Shanks]], and others). The 1987 co-authored charters densify **metatheory, politics, and critical practice** (theory-as-practice; [[logic-of-contingency]]; [[fourfold-hermeneutic]]; [[redemptive-aesthetic]]; material culture as signifying practice / ideology; anti-evolutionism; archaeology as critique). *A Phenomenology of Landscape* (1994) shifts to **embodied experience of place**: walking, looking, relating monuments to rock outcrops, coasts, rivers, and ridges. Named on [[post-processual-archaeology]] as co-originator.

### From *A Phenomenology of Landscape* (1994)

*(Attributed; densified from *Phenomenology of Landscape* 1994.)*

- **Phenomenological landscape archaeology** as method and theory ([[phenomenological-archaeology]]; [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]]).
- Critique of functionalist site location and bird's-eye distribution maps; "middle-range" gap between site plan and region map filled by **lived landscape relations**.
- Three-region British case study: Pembrokeshire, Black Mountains, Cranborne Chase — Mesolithic locales → Neolithic monument siting as enculturation of ancestral paths and landmarks ([[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]]).
- **Nämforsen rock-art self-deconstruction (attributed, Johnson 2011; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]).** In *Material Culture and Text: The Art of Ambiguity* (1991), Tilley analyzed rock carvings at Nämforsen, Sweden (3rd millennium BC, originally recorded by Hallström around the turn of the twentieth century), developing a structural-logic reading of the carvings and then self-consciously **deconstructing his own interpretation**, refusing to offer a final "pat" reading: "the point I am really trying to make is that these rock carvings invite a response from us... I do not present a proper conclusion because this is an impossibility" (Tilley 1991: 172, as quoted by Johnson). Johnson cites this as an exemplary case of postprocessual material-culture-as-text analysis (see [[post-processual-archaeology]]).
- **Phenomenology's philosophical roots (attributed, Tilley 2005 as reported by Johnson 2011).** Johnson reports that Tilley's phenomenology of landscape (2005 restatement) draws on Husserl via Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, with roots also in German Romanticism and Alfred Schutz's phenomenological sociology: "Phenomenology involves the study and description of phenomena... Processes of ordinary human description are of central significance" (Tilley 2005: 201–2, as quoted by Johnson). See [[phenomenological-archaeology]] for the fuller program and its critics.

## Intellectual Network

- **Associated with:** Michael Shanks (co-author); [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]'s post-processual cluster; Julian Thomas, Barbara Bender, Richard Bradley (acknowledged interlocutors).
- **Draws on:** phenomenological and humanistic geography traditions (as reconstructed in Ch. 1 of *Phenomenology of Landscape*); ethnographic landscape literature (Ch. 2); Critical Theory and social theory shared with Shanks in the 1987 volumes.
- **Positioned against:** processual environmental determinism and site-catchment functionalism ([[processual-archaeology]]; [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]); positivism and value-freedom in archaeology (1987 volumes).

## Critiques and Reception

Recorded on study/debate pages; phenomenological archaeology has been criticized for subjectivity, under-specification of method, and projection of modern walking experience onto prehistory — positions to be attributed, not adjudicated here. The 1987 co-authored volumes share the broader S&T reception (relativism charges; 1992 Appendix replies) — see [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].
