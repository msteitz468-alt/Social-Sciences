# Raewyn Connell

Australian sociologist (born 1944), the leading theorist of the sociology of masculinities and one of the most influential contemporary gender theorists. Connell published as **R. W. Connell** through the 1990s and, after gender transition in the 2000s, as **Raewyn Connell**; the two names denote the same author. Ingested sources: *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987) and *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995; 2nd ed. 2005).

> Deep intellectual biography: [[hubs/thinkers/connell-raewyn|Thinkers Hub — Raewyn Connell]].

## Overview

Connell built the dominant framework in the critical study of men and masculinities: the [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]] (gender as a structure of social practice ordered by labour, power, and [[cathexis]]) and the concepts of [[hegemonic-masculinity]] and [[emphasized-femininity]]. *Gender and Power* (1987) is the systematic structural statement; *Masculinities* (1995) fills in the diversity of masculinities the structural theory "demanded." Earlier work on class, education, and Australian social structure (1970s schools research) fed a lifelong concern with inequality; later work (*Southern Theory*, 2007 — not yet ingested) turns to the geopolitics of social theory.

## Contributions

- **[[hegemonic-masculinity]]** — culturally ascended configuration of masculinity that legitimates patriarchy; relational counterparts (subordinated, complicit, marginalized).
- **[[emphasized-femininity]]** — culturally supported femininity of compliance with subordination, oriented to men's interests (*Gender and Power* Ch. 8).
- **[[patriarchal-dividend]]** — collective advantage men draw from women's subordination (named 1995; mechanism already in 1987).
- **[[gender-order]]** / gender regime — whole-society and institution-level structural inventories; crisis tendencies.
- **[[cathexis]]** — structure of desire/emotional attachment as one of three main gender structures.
- **[[body-reflexive-practice]]** — body in gender without biological determinism or pure constructionism (argument 1987; name 1995).
- Systematic critique of [[social-role|sex-role theory]], categorical universalism, dual systems, and sociobiology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Connell works at the intersection of Marxist class analysis, second-wave feminism, and gay-liberation theory. The framework draws on Gramscian hegemony, the feminist structural theory of Juliet Mitchell and Gayle Rubin, [[practice-theory|practice theory]], and psychoanalysis (used critically). Connell credits feminism "especially in the life and work of my wife and partner Pam Benton" as formative — "issues about gender were never just contemplative, but always had to do with social action." The early theoretical work was done collaboratively with Tim Carrigan and John Lee. Connell's own students and the wider field of masculinity studies (Michael Messner, James Messerschmidt, and others) extend and contest the framework.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Connell's central concept has been challenged from discursive, poststructuralist, and psychosocial directions (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]); Connell responded with the reformulation in *Gender* (2002) and Connell and Messerschmidt (2005). The evidentiary base of *Masculinities* — four small purposive groups of Australian men — is a recurring point of critique, as is the theory's reliance on "men" as a category with a shared interest.

## Historiography

*Masculinities* was voted by members of the Australian Sociological Association (2003) one of the ten most influential books in Australian sociology. *Gender and Power* is the structural panel of the diptych — less often read whole than mined for gender regime, emphasized femininity, and the three structures. See [[hubs/thinkers/connell-raewyn|Thinkers Hub]] for career phases, verbatim analysis, and counterfactual significance. *Southern Theory* remains outstanding.
