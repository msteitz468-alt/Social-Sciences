# Raewyn Connell

Australian sociologist (born 1944), the leading theorist of the sociology of masculinities and a major interventionist in the geopolitics of social knowledge. Connell published as **R. W. Connell** through the 1990s and, after gender transition in the 2000s, as **Raewyn Connell**; the two names denote the same author. Ingested sources: *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987), *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995; 2nd ed. 2005), and *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]* (2007).

> Deep intellectual biography: [[hubs/thinkers/connell-raewyn|Thinkers Hub — Raewyn Connell]].

## Overview

Connell built the dominant framework in the critical study of men and masculinities: the [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]] (gender as a structure of social practice ordered by labour, power, and [[cathexis]]) and the concepts of [[hegemonic-masculinity]] and [[emphasized-femininity]]. *Gender and Power* (1987) is the systematic structural statement; *Masculinities* (1995) fills in the diversity of masculinities the structural theory "demanded." Earlier work on class, education, and Australian social structure (1970s schools research) fed a lifelong concern with inequality. *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]* (2007) extends the same anti-categorical, historically specific method to the **[[southern-theory|geopolitics of social theory]]**: critique of the unmarked [[metropole|metropolitan]] standpoint, reconstruction of sociology's imperial founding under [[global-difference]], and recovery of African, Iranian, Latin American, and Indian social thought as theory.

## Contributions

- **[[hegemonic-masculinity]]** — culturally ascended configuration of masculinity that legitimates patriarchy; relational counterparts (subordinated, complicit, marginalized).
- **[[emphasized-femininity]]** — culturally supported femininity of compliance with subordination, oriented to men's interests (*Gender and Power* Ch. 8).
- **[[patriarchal-dividend]]** — collective advantage men draw from women's subordination (named 1995; mechanism already in 1987).
- **[[gender-order]]** / gender regime — whole-society and institution-level structural inventories; crisis tendencies.
- **[[cathexis]]** — structure of desire/emotional attachment as one of three main gender structures.
- **[[body-reflexive-practice]]** — body in gender without biological determinism or pure constructionism (argument 1987; name 1995).
- **[[southern-theory]]** — relational framing of periphery–centre knowledge relations; periphery produces theory; multi-centred democratic social science.
- **[[global-difference]]**, **[[metropole]]**, **[[dirty-theory]]** — analytic tools of the 2007 program.
- Systematic critique of [[social-role|sex-role theory]], categorical universalism, dual systems, sociobiology, and metropolitan general theory (Coleman, Giddens, Bourdieu as cases).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Connell works at the intersection of Marxist class analysis, second-wave feminism, and gay-liberation theory. The gender framework draws on Gramscian hegemony, the feminist structural theory of Juliet Mitchell and Gayle Rubin, [[practice-theory|practice theory]], and psychoanalysis (used critically). *Southern Theory* draws on [[postcolonialism]], Latin American dependency economics ([[prebisch-raul]]; [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]]), Hountondji's critique of scientific extraversion, Subaltern Studies, and [[nandy-ashis|Nandy]]'s intimate-enemy reading of colonialism. Connell credits feminism "especially in the life and work of my wife and partner Pam Benton" as formative. Early theoretical work was collaborative with Tim Carrigan and John Lee; masculinity studies (Messner, Messerschmidt, and others) extend and contest the gender vocabulary.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Connell's central gender concept has been challenged from discursive, poststructuralist, and psychosocial directions (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]); Connell responded with the reformulation in *Gender* (2002) and Connell and Messerschmidt (2005). The evidentiary base of *Masculinities* — four small purposive groups of Australian men — is a recurring point of critique. *Southern Theory* can be read as English-language and Antipodean in standpoint (a limit Connell partly addresses by reading translations); it does not replace Northern theory with a single Southern grand system, which some readers want and which Connell refuses as another monologue.

## Historiography

*Masculinities* was voted by members of the Australian Sociological Association (2003) one of the ten most influential books in Australian sociology. *Gender and Power* is the structural panel of the gender diptych. *Southern Theory* is the geopolitics-of-knowledge panel — widely cited in global and decolonial sociology.
