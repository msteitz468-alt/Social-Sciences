# Emanuel Schegloff (1937–living)

American sociologist, co-founder with [[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]] of
[[conversation-analysis|conversation analysis]]. His chapter "Sequencing in
Conversational Openings," originally published in *American Anthropologist* 70(6) (1968)
and based on his 1967 University of California, Berkeley PhD dissertation, was reprinted
as chapter 12 of Gumperz and Hymes's 1972 volume *Directions in Sociolinguistics*,
immediately following Sacks's founding chapter. Schegloff explicitly credits
[[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]] and [[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]] — "and especially
... David Sudnow and Harvey Sacks" — as the intellectual sources of his general approach
(fn. 1, p. 346).

## Overview

Schegloff's data were roughly 500 tape-recorded phone calls to and from a Midwestern
police department complaint desk. From this corpus he derived a formal analysis of how
conversational openings are sequentially organized, generalizing a single "distribution
rule" violation into the broader apparatus of summons-answer sequences and conditional
relevance — an analytic strategy the volume's editors called "unusual in sociology" for
its resemblance to linguistic argumentation.

## Contributions

All contributions below are attributed to Schegloff.

- **The abab formula.** Two-party conversation sequencing alternates, "one party at a
  time" — credited by Schegloff to Sacks ("n.d., ms."); in settings of four or more
  parties, simultaneous talk is read as multiple conversations rather than a rule
  violation.
- **The distribution rule for first utterances.** "The answerer speaks first" upon
  picking up a ringing phone, regardless of the specific lexical item used ("hello,"
  "yeah," "Macy's," "Police Desk," etc.); Schegloff noted the answerer's informational
  disadvantage relative to the caller, who alone knows who is calling.
- **The deviant-case generalization.** One call among roughly 500 (call #9: a police
  officer calling the American Red Cross) violated the distribution rule. Rather than
  treat this as an exception to be explained away, Schegloff argued — crediting Michael
  Moerman's suggestion — that "not number of occurrences but common subsumption under a
  more general formulation is what matters."
- **Summons-Answer (SA) sequences.** The generalization subsuming the distribution rule:
  a telephone ring is analyzed as a summons (an attention-getting device, alongside terms
  of address, courtesy phrases, and physical devices such as taps or waves), the first
  part of a two-part SA sequence.
- **Nonterminality and nonrepeatability of SA sequences.** A completed SA sequence cannot
  properly be a conversation's final exchange — it obligates the summoner to talk again
  ("What is it?" being the prototypical next-summons answer); once answered, a summoner
  may not re-summon the same party.
- **Conditional relevance** (terms and elements credited to Sacks, 1969). Given a first
  sequence item, a second is expectable; its occurrence is heard as answering the first;
  its nonoccurrence is "officially absent," not merely absent — licensing rigorous
  sociological treatment of both sequenced pairs and structured absences. A subsidiary
  property, **immediate juxtaposition** (the "nextness" reformulation, credited to Sacks),
  holds that an answer not immediately following a summons will not be heard as answering
  it, unlike ordinary question-answer sequences where intervening talk can still connect
  the pair.
- **The Durkheimian framing of conditional relevance.** Schegloff explicitly analogized
  conditional relevance to Durkheim's "social fact" — "both 'external' and 'constraining'"
  — arguing a member "may not 'naively choose'" not to answer a summons without licensing
  an inference (e.g., "no answer–no person," or being read as cold-shouldering if
  physically present).
- **Availability and coordinated entry.** Schegloff analyzed conversation as a "minimally
  two-party" activity requiring established mutual availability, with SA sequences
  solving both the availability problem and the "coordinated entry" problem
  simultaneously; he credited David Sudnow with the observation that answers to summonses
  routinely borrow the properties of questions (e.g., "what?", "yeah?"), which further
  obligates the summoner to keep talking.

Schegloff explicitly flagged his own cross-linguistic asides (French "j'écoute," British
"Are you there?") as illustrative anecdotes only, with no systematic method or sample
behind them — not evidenced cross-cultural comparison.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The editors' framing note to the 1972 chapter (attributed to Gumperz and Hymes, not
Schegloff) positions the cross-cultural universality of his opening-sequence claims as an
open question, citing Michael Moerman's Thai conversational data as a future test not yet
resolved within the chapter. No further contemporaneous critique is recorded in the
sources ingested so far.

## Historiography

Schegloff went on to become one of conversation analysis's principal expositors and, with
Gail Jefferson, editor of Sacks's posthumous lectures. The wiki's coverage of Schegloff
currently rests on this single 1972/1968 chapter; a fuller historiographic account awaits
ingestion of his later methodological writings.
