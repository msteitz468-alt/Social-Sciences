# François Quesnay

## Overview

**This page is currently built entirely from Karl Marx's treatment of Quesnay in *[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume II]]* (1885), plus Ernest Mandel's 1978 introduction to the Fernbach translation — not from independent primary or secondary scholarship on Quesnay or Physiocracy.** All assessments below are Marx's and Mandel's attributed readings, filtered through their own theoretical projects, not disinterested intellectual history. A future ingest of primary Physiocratic texts or independent historiography of Physiocracy should substantially expand and check this page.

François Quesnay (1694–1774) was the leading theorist of French Physiocracy, court physician to Louis XV, and author of the *Tableau économique* (1758) — read by Marx as the first systematic analysis of the circulation and reproduction of the annual social product between economic classes.

## Contributions

- **The Tableau économique (1758).** Per Mandel, Quesnay's Tableau is a genuine circuit analysis of commodities and income flowing between the classes of a nation (productive/agricultural class, landowning class, "sterile"/manufacturing class) — credited by Mandel as Marx's acknowledged predecessor for the concept of [[reproduction-schemas]]. Mandel traces a lineage of aggregate economic modeling running "from Quesnay through Marx, Walras, Leontiev and Keynes," treating neo-classical and vulgar macroeconomics as, in key respects, a regression from this line.
- **Avances primitives and avances annuelles.** In *Capital, Volume II*, Chapter 10, Marx credits Quesnay with the origin of the fixed/circulating capital distinction, applied specifically to the farmer's capital (Quesnay held agricultural capital to be the only truly productive capital): *avances primitives* ("original advances," the fund for commencing cultivation) versus *avances annuelles* ("annual advances," yearly expenditures on cultivation). Quesnay is quoted directly: "The annual advances consist of the expenditures annually made for the work of cultivation; these advances must be distinguished from the original advances which form the fund for the commencement of cultivation" (*Analyse du tableau économique*).
- **Marx's assessment: correctly located within productive capital.** Marx praises Quesnay for correctly reducing the avances distinction to "the different ways in which they enter the value of the finished product... the different ways in which they are replaced or reproduced" — i.e., locating the distinction entirely within productive capital (not conflating it with the later categories of commodity capital or money capital), and for grasping that constant capital reappears in kind in the finished product, a point Marx argues Adam Smith later lost. Marx judges the Physiocratic system, despite its "feudal signboard," to be "the first systematic conception of capitalist production."
- **The doctrine's limit.** Per Marx, the Physiocrats' theory of wages — that labour's value-contribution equals only the value of workers' necessary means of subsistence — barred them from discovering the [[constant-and-variable-capital|constant/variable capital]] distinction, because they ascribed surplus-value production solely to agriculture, via nature's "collaboration" with labour, rather than to labour as such; only agricultural labour was deemed "productive." Marx notes, however, that the avances distinction itself is untouched by the later Smith/Ricardo confusion of fixed/circulating with constant/variable capital, since it was framed solely around differing reproduction periods within productive capital.
- **Contemporary critics.** Marx's Volume 2 material (via the claims extraction) notes Linguet and Mably as contemporary critics of Physiocratic doctrine, without extended detail.
- **Turgot's terminological shift.** Later Physiocrats progressively identified the avances more directly with "capital": Dupont de Nemours used "capital or avances"; Le Trosne wrote of a nation's capital "accumulated over a long period" independent of yearly reproduction; Turgot (*Réflexions sur la formation et la distribution des richesses*, 1766) used "capital" regularly for the avances and drew manufacturers' avances closer to farmers'.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Quesnay founded the Physiocratic school (the *économistes*) in mid-18th-century France; his circle included Dupont de Nemours, Le Trosne, and (in a looser, terminologically transitional relation per Marx) Turgot. No training lineage is established from the claims extracted here; mark `trained_under` as [[unknown]].

Per Mandel, Quesnay stands as the acknowledged forerunner of Marx's own [[reproduction-schemas]] in *Capital, Volume II* — a lineage Mandel extends forward to Walras, Leontiev, and Keynes. Comparative literature cited by Mandel: Shigeto Tsuru, "On Reproduction Schemes" (in Paul Sweezy, *The Theory of Capitalist Development*, 1942), and Jacques Nagels (1970).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Marx's own critique of Physiocracy is double-edged: analytically generous (Quesnay's avances distinction "correctly" located within productive capital, the Tableau as a real advance in circuit analysis) but doctrinally critical (the Physiocratic ascription of surplus-value to agriculture/nature is, per Marx, what blocked the discovery of the constant/variable capital distinction — the discovery Marx claims for himself). Adam Smith, per Marx, generalized Quesnay's fixed/circulating distinction from farmer's capital to all productive capital, but "in the details of his presentation... falls far behind Quesnay" by conflating it with the distinct opposition between productive capital and capital-in-circulation (commodity/money capital) — an error Marx holds the Physiocrats did not make.
