# Kathryn Walker Tubb

Lecturer in cultural heritage studies and conservation, Institute of Archaeology, University College London (as of 2006 bio); coordinator of the M.A. program in Cultural Heritage Studies. Co-editor of [[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Archaeology, Cultural Heritage, and the Antiquities Trade]] (2006); author of chapter 16, "Artifacts and Emotion." Long associated with anti-illicit-trade advocacy alongside [[brodie-neil|Neil Brodie]] and the IARC circle; named among demand-side voices in [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Renfrew 2000]]'s orbit on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Overview

Tubb brings **conservation and affective dimensions** to the illicit-trade literature: not only law and markets but how emotional attachments to objects structure collecting, stewardship, and resistance to restitution or acquisition restraint.

## Contributions

- **Artifacts and emotion** (attributed, Tubb 2006 ch. 16) — emotional bonds as part of the collecting and heritage field.
- **Co-editorship** of the 2006 volume; prior co-editorship with Brodie of *Illicit Antiquities* (referenced, not separately ingested).

## Intellectual Genealogy

UCL Institute of Archaeology heritage/conservation; IARC-adjacent network with Brodie, Renfrew, [[kersel-morag-m|Kersel]], [[luke-christina|Luke]].

## Contributions (densified)

### Artifacts and Emotion (ch. 16, attributed)
Tubb treats **emotion** as common ground between collectors, conservators, and archaeologists — passion for objects (Formanek; collectors such as Ortiz, Fleischman as discussed) is not only a market pathology but a force that can be redirected toward stewardship. Draws on reason–emotion literature (Damasio, Goleman). Uses Iraq 2003 as a case where media, market, and moratorium debates fractured professional response. Distinguishes commodity value from **evidential** value of objects in context.
