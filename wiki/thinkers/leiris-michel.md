# Michel Leiris

## Overview

Michel Leiris (1901–1990) trained under [[mauss-marcel]] and moved from the Documents surrealist milieu into a career as ethnographer, writer, and autobiographer. He served as secretary-archivist on the Mission Dakar-Djibouti (1931–33) alongside [[griaule-marcel]], making original ethnographic contributions on Dogon and Ethiopian material, and conducted a study of Gondar zar spirit-possession (1932; monograph 1958). He was a close friend of [[cesaire-aime]] in the postwar Paris milieu. (Source: [[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]].)

## Contributions

Leiris's field journal from the Dakar-Djibouti mission, *L'Afrique fantôme* (1934), was, per Clifford, "a clear breach of discipline" relative to Griaule's team ideal — an anti-narrative, self-critical surrealist ethnography — and Leiris never found an enduring place within Griaule's collaborative research "mechanism." Clifford contrasts Leiris's stance with Griaule's directly: Leiris "never underwent any 'initiation' into an exotic form of life or belief," and his ongoing self-critical autobiography (*L'Age d'homme*, *La règle du jeu*) constitutes, in Clifford's reading, "a relentless critique of the paradigm of initiation," reflecting persistent doubt about the legitimacy of representing another culture — Clifford asks rhetorically, "How could Leiris presume to represent another culture when he had trouble enough representing himself?"

In a 1953 UNESCO essay collection ("The African Negroes and the Arts of Carving and Sculpture"), Leiris grounded his generalizations about "African art" in a contingent, Western viewer's perspective rather than an essentialist claim about a shared African cultural substrate — a stance Clifford contrasts with Griaule's companion essay ("The Problem of Negro Culture"), which asserted a single, rigid "metaphysical substratum" for "the Negro races." A related 1953 footnote by Leiris warns that apparent resemblances among unfamiliar African objects often reflect only "common differentness" from Western sculpture rather than real affinity — used by Clifford as a more rigorous formulation than the MoMA "Primitivism" exhibition's "affinity" thesis: "we tend to see a certain resemblance between them, which lies, in point of fact, merely in their common differentness."

Clifford credits Leiris's 1950 essay "L'ethnographe devant le colonialisme" as "perhaps the first" extended analysis by a professional ethnographer naming and analyzing colonialism as an inescapable ideological ground for fieldwork, positioning the ethnographer as natural advocate for exploited peoples and warning against exclusionary definitions of cultural "authenticity" that write off évolués and cultural syncretism.

Leiris co-founded, with Georges Bataille and Roger Caillois, the Collège de Sociologie (met c. 1937–39 in a Latin Quarter café), which revived Durkheimian "sacred"/"collective effervescence" themes (via Mauss, Georges Dumézil, Marcel Granet) while rejecting surrealism's subjectivist excess; Leiris resigned over qualms about loose evidentiary standards, contributing the essay "The Sacred in Everyday Life" (1938).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained under [[mauss-marcel]]; collaborator and eventual critical counterpoint to [[griaule-marcel]] on the Dakar-Djibouti mission; co-founder of the Collège de Sociologie with Bataille and Caillois; close friend of [[cesaire-aime]]. Part of the Documents journal circle alongside Mauss, Griaule, and André Schaeffner.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Clifford reads an unfinished novel-sketch late in *L'Afrique fantôme* — centered on an alter ego modeled on Conrad's Axel Heyst — as prefiguring Leiris's later literary method of staged, theatrical "sincerity." No sustained scholarly rebuttal of Leiris's ethnographic reliability is recorded in this source, in contrast to the Griaule reliability dispute (see [[griaule-dogon-debate]]); Clifford's overall assessment of Leiris is favorable, treating him as an early, self-aware critic of ethnographic authority rather than a target of it.

## Historiography

As of this ingest, [[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]] (Ch. 4 "On Ethnographic Surrealism" and Ch. 6 "Tell about Your Trip: Michel Leiris") is the wiki's sole source on Leiris. Clifford's account is itself an interpretive reading — Leiris as exemplar of "ethnographic surrealism" and as a self-critical counter-figure to Griaule — attributed throughout to Clifford rather than stated as settled fact.
