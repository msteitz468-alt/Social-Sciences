# Alvin W. Gouldner (1920–1980)

American sociologist; Max Weber Research Professor of Social Theory at Washington University, St. Louis (as of the 1970 book). Principal architect of a historically informed **sociology of sociology** and of the program of [[reflexive-sociology|reflexive sociology]]. Best known for *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|The Coming Crisis of Western Sociology]]* (1970), the major critical-turn diagnosis of [[structural-functionalism|Parsonian structural-functionalism]] and of Academic Sociology's entanglement with the welfare state.

*Seeded from Gouldner 1970. Earlier industrial sociology (*Patterns of Industrial Bureaucracy*, *Wildcat Strike*) and *Enter Plato* not yet ingested.*

## Overview

Gouldner presents *Coming Crisis* as part of a series on "The Social Origins of Social Theory" (with *Enter Plato*). He argues that theories rest on an [[theoretical-infrastructure|infrastructure]] of [[domain-assumptions|domain assumptions]], sentiments, and personal reality; that Western sociology fissioned into Academic Sociology and Marxism; and that both faces of that binary are entering crisis. His constructive exit is [[reflexive-sociology]] — a work ethic that includes the theorist and the discipline among the objects of analysis. The book opens under late-1960s "sound of guns" (urban and campus conflict) and treats theory as the idiom in which those conflicts appear.

## Contributions

### Meta-theory and sociology of sociology

- **[[domain-assumptions]]** — background beliefs about man and society, more general than formal postulates; recoverable (he reports) by analysis and by the 1964 Sprehe–Gouldner ASA survey's domain-assumptions factor.
- **[[theoretical-infrastructure]]** — sentiments, personal reality, and sponsoring social settings that carry a theory's career; fit with audience sentiments explains acceptance better than logical refutation alone.
- **[[methodological-dualism]]** — one epistemic standard for subjects, another for the theorist; fear of the scholar's own self; dualism "boggles at a gnat but swallows a camel" of career and funding contamination (developed with William Yancey).
- **[[reflexive-sociology]]** — not a technical specialty on the profession's foibles but a historical mission "to transform the sociologist"; knowledge as *awareness* vs Positivist *information*; "not merely how to work but how to live."

### Historical model of Western sociology (Ch. 4)

Four periods defined by dominant theoretical syntheses and infrastructures: (I) Sociological [[positivism]]; (II) [[historical-materialism|Marxism]] after binary fission; (III) Classical sociology (decline of evolutionism; functionalism in anthropology; university integration); (IV) Parsonsian [[structural-functionalism]]. Emerging fifth period: polycentrism, entropy of Parsonsianism, Welfare-State methodological empiricism, and rival micro/critical infrastructures.

### Critique of Parsons and Functionalism (Chs. 5–10)

Extended reading of [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] as systems metaphysician of wholeness, moralist of order and piety, under-theorist of force/property, and late accommodator of the Welfare State with partial evolutionist convergence toward Marx. Functionalism's success breeds **entropy** (boundary loss; Kingsley Davis 1959 as symptom; generational disaffection). Rival infrastructures: [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] dramaturgy, ethnomethodology (Garfinkel), Homans/Blau exchange, New Left.

### Comparative and self-critical

- Soviet Academic Sociology and the crisis of official Marxism (Ch. 12) — mirror-image demand for integration theory; East–West convergence thesis.
- Epilogue self-application: admits possible **projection** of his own twenty-year Functionalism–Marxism fusion project onto the "objective" crisis narrative — and builds that admission into the reflexive program.

Industrial sociology (*Patterns of Industrial Bureaucracy*, *Wildcat Strike*) and "The Norm of Reciprocity" (1960) predate *Coming Crisis* and are not yet fully ingested here.

- **"The Norm of Reciprocity" (1960) and its use by Blau.** Gouldner argued a generalized norm of reciprocity functions as a "starting mechanism" of social interaction and group structure. [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] adopted the "starting mechanism" language in *[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power]]* (1964) but pointedly disagreed on priority: for Blau "the norm of reciprocity merely reinforces and stabilizes tendencies inherent in the character of [[social-exchange|social exchange]] itself," so the fundamental mechanism lies "in the existential conditions of exchange, not in the norm of reciprocity." See [[reciprocal-obligation]], [[social-exchange-theory]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Dialogue with / debt:** [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]] (prototype of sociological self-critique; jacket comparison to *Sociological Imagination*); Robert K. Merton (acknowledged critique of the "What Happened in Sociology" chapter; middle-range functional analysis); [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]], [[marx-karl|Marx]], [[weber-max|Weber]], [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]] as classical foils; Raymond Williams ("structure of sentiments"); Dennis Wrong (full-manuscript critique); Russell Sage support for 1965–66 European travel that informed the Soviet-bloc chapter.
- **reacted_against:** Parsonian [[structural-functionalism]] as dominant Academic synthesis; value-free technocratic professionalism / "liberal technologues" of the Welfare-Warfare State; [[methodological-dualism]]; New Left anti-theory currents he also criticizes.
- **Students (as of 1970 acknowledgment):** Barry Thompson, Robert Wicke (Washington University seminars); dissertation work with William Yancey on methodological dualism.
- **Adjacent:** New Left and Frankfurt critical sociology ([[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]], [[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]], [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]], Habermas) as context, not school membership.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Gouldner's "coming crisis" thesis was itself contested: some treated it as partisan New Left sociology-of-sociology; others as the definitive internal critique of Functionalism. His reading of Parsons is second-order and polemical — check against primary Parsons ([[parsons-social-system-1951]]; [[hubs/theory/structural-functionalism]]). Later reception (post-1980) folded "reflexivity" into feminist, postcolonial, Bourdieusian, and ethnomethodological programs that only partly track his infrastructure apparatus. East–West convergence and Functionalism's "death" were only partly borne out; much Functionalist vocabulary was **absorbed**. See [[coming-crisis-of-western-sociology-debate]].
