# Paul J. DiMaggio

## Overview

Paul DiMaggio is a sociologist of organizations, culture, and the arts. With Walter W.
Powell he co-authored "The Iron Cage Revisited: Institutional Isomorphism and Collective
Rationality in Organizational Fields" (originally *American Sociological Review* 48,
1983; reprinted as ch. 3 of *The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis*,
1991), the paper most credited with naming and systematizing the isomorphism strand of
[[new-institutionalism]] in organization theory. He also authored ch. 11 of the same
volume, "Constructing an Organizational Field as a Professional Project: U.S. Art
Museums, 1920–1940," an archival case study of field formation.

## Contributions

With Powell, DiMaggio reframed Weber's account of bureaucratization: where Weber traced
the "iron cage" of rationalization to market competition, state competition, and
demands for legal equality, DiMaggio and Powell argued (attributed) that by the late
twentieth century "bureaucratization and other forms of organizational change occur as
the result of processes that make organizations more similar without necessarily making
them more efficient." They proposed the tripartite typology of coercive, mimetic, and
normative [[institutional-isomorphism]] — mechanisms respectively rooted in political
dependence and legitimacy, uncertainty-driven modeling, and professionalization — and a
paired set of twelve field-level and organization-level hypotheses predicting the pace
and extent of isomorphic change. The chapter also developed the [[organizational-field]]
concept, defined as "those organizations that, in the aggregate, constitute a recognized
area of institutional life," and DiMaggio's own account (1983) of field "structuration"
as a four-part process (rising interaction, emergent domination/coalition structures,
rising information load, and mutual awareness of common enterprise).

In ch. 11, DiMaggio extended the field-formation argument empirically: through archival
research in the Carnegie Corporation of New York and the Pennsylvania Museum of Art
records, he traced the professionalization of American art museums (1920–1940) and
argued (attributed) that "institutional theory has neglected the contradictory tendency
of successful institutionalization projects to legitimize not just new organizational
forms, but also new categories of authorized actors whose interests diverge from those
of the groups controlling the organizations." Structuration, he argued, is "historically
and logically prior" to isomorphism within an already-formed field — a distinction
between field formation and field convergence. He documented Carnegie's role in the
first U.S. branch art museum (Philadelphia, 1931) and argued that professional
activists such as museum director Fiske Kimball exhibited "compartmentalized roles"
in which public reformist rhetoric coexisted with pragmatic, conventional institutional
practice — a mechanism he traced (attributed, drawing on Bernstein 1971 and Gouldner
1978) to different "codes" of rationality operating inside versus outside the
organization.

By 1991, DiMaggio and Powell revised parts of their own 1983 argument: they argued that
treating ceremonial/legitimating spending as "pure costs from the point of view of
efficiency" was "questionable," proposing instead that the effects of institutionally
driven change be separated analytically from the initial choice to adopt it, and that
legitimacy's income-producing effects (not just its costs) be weighed.

## Intellectual Genealogy

DiMaggio and Powell developed the isomorphism argument out of Max Weber (bureaucratization,
the "iron cage"), Amos Hawley (1968, population-level isomorphism), [[meyer-john-w]] and
[[rowan-brian]] (rationalized myths and ceremony), Kanter (1972, institutional
accommodation), and [[giddens-anthony]] (structuration). They positioned the argument
explicitly against Hannan and Freeman's competitive/population-ecology isomorphism, and
against both functionalist/Marxist "societies are smart" theory and elite-control
theory. DiMaggio's ch. 11 argument on field structuration builds on Meyer, Boli, and
Thomas's (1987) "Western cultural account" and cross-links to [[jepperson-ronald]]'s
chapters in the same volume. Within the volume's wider genealogy discussion (ch. 1),
DiMaggio and Powell trace new-institutionalism's cognitive turn through Talcott
[[parsons-talcott]], [[garfinkel-harold]] and ethnomethodology, Berger and Luckmann, and
[[bourdieu-pierre]]'s theory of habitus — crediting Bourdieu with the closest available
alternative to Parsonsian role theory, and noting (1991) that Bourdieu's influence on
organization theory was "just beginning," citing DiMaggio's own ch. 11 as an example.
DiMaggio's chapter also documents extensive colleague/discussant exchange with
[[zucker-lynne]], [[scott-w-richard]], and [[jepperson-ronald]] (acknowledged as
discussants on Scott & Meyer's ch. 5), evidencing a dense collegial rather than
training network among the volume's contributors.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Fligstein (ch. 13, same volume) explicitly distinguished his own power-based account of
organizational fields from DiMaggio and Powell's more normative isomorphism account,
arguing fields are "a construction of powerful organizations that is based on the
interests of those organizations" rather than primarily normative structures. [[scott-w-richard]]
(ch. 7) registered a self-flagged divergence from the DiMaggio-Powell "master
hypothesis" that structuration produces homogenization, arguing that under some
conditions more highly structured environments produce increased diversity of form
through deliberate, authority-driven specialization. [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) later
revised his and DiMaggio's own 1983 two-stage (efficiency-then-isomorphism) life-cycle
model of institutional change, arguing it understated the costs of conformity and
treated initially adopted practices as natural technical solutions rather than
contested, path-dependent choices. Brint and Karabel (ch. 14) criticized the
new-institutionalist program broadly — DiMaggio included — for "silences" on
organizational self-interest and group struggle in shaping field outcomes, a critique
DiMaggio and Powell themselves registered (1991) as a live, unresolved problem for the
volume's program.

## Historiography

DiMaggio and Powell's chapter carries an internal publication-date inconsistency in the
volume's acknowledgments (one block cites *American Journal of Sociology* 83(2), another
*American Sociological Review* 48); the standard citation is DiMaggio and Powell 1983,
*American Sociological Review* 48(2):147–160. The paper's first version was presented by
Powell at the 1981 American Sociological Association meetings in Toronto. DiMaggio and
Powell themselves narrate 1977 (the year of [[meyer-john-w]]'s two seminal papers) as
new-institutionalism's conventional "birth date," with the 1985 UCLA conference convened
by [[zucker-lynne]] (cited as Zucker 1987) marking the point at which the program was
"named and reified" as a self-conscious field.
