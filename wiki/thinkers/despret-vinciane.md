## Overview

Vinciane Despret is a Belgian philosopher and ethologist. This page is derived entirely from her presence as a cited interlocutor in Donna Haraway's *Staying with the Trouble* (2016) — specifically Chapter 7, "A Curious Practice" — and does not draw on any of Despret's own works directly; all claims below are Haraway's relay of Despret's positions, attributed accordingly *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Contributions

Haraway credits Despret with the concept of "the virtue of politeness" as an epistemic stance in research: Despret describes herself (quoted by Haraway) as committed to "a particular epistemological position ... one that I call a virtue: the virtue of politeness," glossed as training oneself — following Hannah Arendt's notion of "going visiting" — to ask questions that make interlocutors, human or animal, "interesting," and to remain open to being changed by the encounter rather than assuming fixed, pre-given natures or capacities *(Haraway 2016)*.

Central to this is Despret's concept of "rendering capable": in careful research encounters, humans and animals "render each other capable" of behaviors and feats not written into any prior script — ability is co-produced in the interaction, not simply revealed. Haraway relays Despret's discussion of ethologist Amotz Zahavi's research on Arabian babblers in the Negev desert, where observers and birds "rendered each other capable in ways not written into preexisting scripts, but invented or provoked" *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway summarizes: "Becoming-with is how partners are, in Vinciane Despret's terms, rendered capable" *(Haraway 2016)* — feeding [[companion-species]] and [[response-ability]].

Haraway also relays Despret's collaborative fieldwork with sociologist Jocelyne Porcher on nonindustrial French cattle and pig farms, where breeders reported continuous mutual attentiveness with their animals; the finding, attributed to Despret and Porcher, is that good farming depends on humans learning to attend to animals' wants — "their animals 'know what we want, but we, we don't know what they want'" *(Haraway 2016)*. Despret's work on human mourning and commemoration practices is treated by Haraway as continuous with this ethological method; Haraway cites Despret's discussion of the pigeon-loft memorial "Capsule" (built 2003 in Caudry, France, by designer Matali Crasset) as "a potent practice of commemoration" holding space for both pigeons and their human "colombophiles," past, present, and future *(Haraway 2016)*.

With [[stengers-isabelle]], Despret co-wrote *Women Who Make a Fuss: The Unfaithful Daughters of Virginia Woolf*, which Haraway discusses as arguing for a mode of intellectual inheritance — "thinking from" a heritage rather than "thinking that" doctrine — that demands transformation and reinvention rather than fidelity, linked by the authors to Virginia Woolf's "Think we must!" *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway also relays, via Despret (received in turn from Stengers), the parable of the twelve camels — a folk story about an impossible inheritance solved by borrowing a twelfth camel — used to theorize what it means to "start from" an inheritance rather than simply apply it *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Intellectual Genealogy

This source does not document Despret's formal training or teachers. Her closest documented intellectual partnership here is with [[stengers-isabelle]] (co-authorship, shared vocabulary of "thinking from") and with [[haraway-donna]], who credits Despret (along with Thom van Dooren, not on this ingest's canonical list) with teaching her that living and dying well in the Chthulucene requires "joining forces to reconstitute refuges," including mourning irreversible losses *(Haraway 2016)*.
