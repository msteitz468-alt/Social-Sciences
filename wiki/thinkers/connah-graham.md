# Graham Connah

Emeritus Professor of Archaeology, University of New England (Australia); Visiting Fellow, Australian National University. Africanist and Australian historical archaeologist; Order of Australia (2000). Best known for the multi-edition synthesis *[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* (1st 1987; 3rd 2016) and for fieldwork at Benin and Kibiro. Amaury Talbot Prize for *Three Thousand Years in Africa* (1981).

## Overview

In *[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* (3rd ed. 2016), Connah assesses how much archaeology can tell about two linked aspects of African social complexity: the growth of cities and the appearance of states. He reviews cases across the continent’s major environmental zones—lower and middle Nile, North Africa, Ethiopian–Eritrean highlands, West African savanna and forest, East African coast, Zambezia, Central Africa, South African Highveld—using a repeated local-conditions checklist (subsistence, technology, social system, population pressures, ideology, external trade) adopted from [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]. The book’s comparative thesis is that elite power and complexity typically originated from control of differentially productive land (and the people to work it) plus internal exchange networks; long-distance external trade intensified rather than originated those processes. The title word “civilization” is used deliberately provocatively, against Eurocentric definitional histories that equated civilization with writing and excluded most of Africa.

## Contributions

### Field archaeology: Benin City and Kibiro

Connah’s 1960s excavations at [[benin-city|Benin City]] (*The Archaeology of Benin*, 1975; synthesized in Connah 2016 Ch. 7) demonstrated occupation by about the thirteenth century, edge-laid potsherd pavements during or prior to the fourteenth century, artistic use of copper-base alloys from the thirteenth century, and a radiocarbon-dated mass burial of at least forty-one young women interpreted as ritual sacrifice indicating centralized authority. The innermost city walls (possibly mid-fifteenth century) represent an enormous labour investment; outer interlocking earthworks (>145 km surveyed by Connah) suggested possible synoecism of villages into a capital, later shown by Darling to form only a peripheral part of a vast rural enclosure system (~6,500 km²) reflecting power struggles for agricultural land—evidence Connah reads as speaking more to [[state-formation]] than urban origins alone.

At Kibiro (Uganda; Interlacustrine salt production), Connah’s work underpins Ch. 10 arguments that specialized manufacturing towns and internal salt/iron/copper networks matter more than sparse early extra-continental imports for Central African complexity; a roughly fourteenth-century elite female burial with iron, copper, glass, and shell beads is cited as evidence that women as well as men belonged among Interlacustrine elites.

### *African Civilizations* comparative program

The book’s chapter geography follows historically and archaeologically documented zones of urban/state development (Maghreb, lower Nile, middle Nile, Sahel edge, West African rainforest west of the lower Niger, Ethiopian Plateau, East African coast, Zimbabwe Plateau, lower Congo, Lake Victoria, South African Highveld). Connah rejects beheading the continent as “sub-Saharan”: the third edition extends coverage to Egypt, North Africa, and South Africa under a continent-wide frame. He prefers less loaded terms—“large settlement” and “polity”—when character is uncertain, while still using city/state language for clearer cases.

**Method.** Evidence-first over grand theory: “how we know what we think we know.” Archaeological visibility and uneven research systematically bias African distribution maps (stone/mud cities vs grass/wood settlements; savanna vs rainforest). Historical sources indicate more cities and states than archaeology documents (e.g. Bugandan organic capital; Mbanza Kongo poorly excavated). Oral tradition is valuable but rarely illuminates more than ~500 years (Vansina often ~250). Ethnohistory and ethnography are needed yet must not “tyrannize” the archaeological past (Fletcher).

**Trade as intensifier, not originator.** Across regions Connah finds indigenous or internal origins of complexity that pre-date large-scale long-distance external trade: West African savanna urbanism (e.g. [[jenne-jeno]]) before large-scale trans-Saharan commerce; East African coastal complexity with African origins and contributory foreign elements ([[swahili-culture]]); Central African hierarchy before substantial outside-world trade; limited early large-scale long-distance trade evidence in Zambezia and the West African forest; middle Nile and Ethiopian highland origins seeming to pre-date trade takeoff. External gold/ivory/slave trades later intensified elite power. Internal networks in copper, iron, salt, foodstuffs, and prestige goods were the first circuitry; external trade “plugged into” them.

**[[productive-land-hypothesis]].** Named in Ch. 12: elite power was first acquired by control of land with unusually high production potential—complementary to internal trade, not alternative to it. Critics who stress control of people alone (Edwards 1994; Peel 2000) ignore extensive low-utility land; Connah answers with a people-plus-land formula. The hypothesis is offered as field-testable. Regional applications recur: Nile flood silts; Aksumite Plain; Inland Niger Delta recessional rice; forest interfluves and ecotones; coastal soils and water; Zimbabwe Plateau fertile soils amid poor granite soils; Upemba alluvium and fishing; Interlacustrine bananas/cattle; Highveld arable/pasture.

**Cities and states as related but not identical.** Not all cities were capitals of significant states; not all states had large static centres. Both associate with centralization, stratification, specialization, and population pressure, but they are not a single process. African urban forms vary: dense long occupation, brief occupation, mobile capitals, seasonal residence, dispersed settlement over huge areas.

### Regional positions (selected)

- **Forest West Africa (Ch. 7):** Pre-European states and cities; rejects external-stimulus origin stories keyed only to Saharan or Atlantic trade; multifactor indigenous model (surplus, iron, specialization, land competition, divine kingship); European coastal trade reversed northward economic orientation and later accelerated complexity.
- **Swahili coast (Ch. 8):** Rejects “remote outpost of Islam” / colonial-origins interpretations; African origins with contributory foreign elements; Islam crucial binder later, not primary cause of urbanism; settlements as foci of complex African networks that later incorporated Indian Ocean elements.
- **Zambezia (Ch. 9):** Great Zimbabwe as classic case of colonial denial of African cultural heritage; African builders never in serious archaeological doubt; gold trade facilitated rather than originated hierarchy.
- **Central Africa (Ch. 10):** Relative isolation makes indigenous causes especially likely; low archaeological visibility of organic cities is a research bias, not absence of complexity.
- **South Africa (Ch. 11):** Recent Highveld/Tswana/Zulu trajectories as laboratory for how complexity develops and fails under ecological, political, and colonial stress.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Sparse formal teacher/student chain in the 2016 synthesis. Theoretical interlocutors Connah engages explicitly include:

- **[[renfrew-colin]]:** Local-conditions framework for each chapter; rejection of pristine/secondary civilization divide as “unacceptably diffusionist” / “meaningless exercise” when state formation is slow local politics.
- **Jonathan Haas:** Power theory of the state (control of basic resources → coercive power); Connah judges it has explanatory value for Africa despite Haas’s exclusion of African (even Egyptian) cases as “secondary.”
- **John Lonsdale:** Six African state-formation hypotheses; state formation as slow, often botched, coercive centralization from local politics, not external ideas.
- **Akin Mabogunje:** Urbanization as congregation in relatively large numbers at one spot; three limiting conditions (food surplus, power-holding group, traders).
- **[[childe-v-gordon]]:** Ten city criteria and writing-linked civilization definitions critiqued as Eurocentric and SW-Asia-bound.
- **Susan Keech McIntosh / Roderick McIntosh:** Heterarchy (horizontal organization) as alternative to hierarchical surplus control—important idea, but Connah holds current cases often rest on lack of hierarchical evidence rather than positive heterarchy evidence; more excavation needed (especially Middle Niger; also Igbo non-urban path).
- **Peter Garlake:** Indigenous evolution of African states; external trade as monopoly of outlets converting resources to surplus; Great Zimbabwe historiography.
- **[[diamond-jared-m]]:** Autocatalysis among subsistence, technology, social system, population, ideology, and trade—factors stimulate one another without any one alone “causing” cities/states; large groups require centralization for conflict resolution and decision-making.
- **[[yoffee-norman]], Pauketat:** Broader treatments of archaeological conditions of complexity that Connah pairs with Renfrew’s localism.

Field and regional specialists densely cited (e.g. McIntoshes on Jenné-jeno; D.W. Phillipson on Aksum; Horton, Chittick, Garlake on the coast and Zimbabwe; de Maret on Upemba; Darling on Benin earthworks) function as data interlocutors rather than a single school lineage.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reception of Connah 2016 as a whole is not yet densified from secondary reviews in this ingest; the book itself already stages major challenges:

- **People-over-land challenge (Edwards 1994; Peel 2000):** Control of people primary given alleged land surplus in underpopulated Africa—Connah’s main counter is limited-utility land plus people–land complementarity (see [[productive-land-hypothesis]]).
- **Heterarchy (R.J. McIntosh):** Horizontal complexity without despotic hierarchy (Jenné-jeno; possibly Igbo)—Connah accepts reexamination of assumptions but calls premature any general absence-of-stratification conclusion from limited excavation.
- **Trade-origin models (e.g. Levtzion Sahel “ports”; colonial Swahili foundation myths; gold-trade-only Great Zimbabwe):** Explicitly rejected as origin stories; retained as later intensifiers.
- **Religion as prime mover (e.g. Garlake on Zimbabwe):** Connah allows ideology as legitimizer/reinforcer and sometimes active power technology (fertility/rain cults; priest-kings), but rejects unproven claims that religion was the single most important first step toward stratification.
- **Pristine/secondary taxonomy:** Endorses Renfrew’s dissolution of the divide for African cases.
- **Visibility bias:** Connah’s own methodological self-critique—case selection tracks research intensity and permanent architecture, omitting ethnohistorically known organic cities.

Treat the productive-land hypothesis and trade-as-intensifier package as attributed Connah positions, not wiki-voice laws.

## Historiography

*African Civilizations* is a multi-edition textbook-handbook (1st ed. 1987; 3rd ed. 2016). Earlier editions focused more tightly on tropical Africa; the third edition’s continental scope and Ch. 12 “common denominators” chapter name and systematize arguments developed across editions (notably the productive land hypothesis and the intensifier/originator distinction). Connah’s Benin monograph (1975) and *Three Thousand Years in Africa* (1981) supply primary fieldwork depth for forest and Lake Chad margins; *Forgotten Africa* (2004) is a broader popular synthesis. Within Africanist archaeology, the book functions as a standard comparative survey against which regional specialists test local chronologies and origin debates (Swahili African agency; Middle Niger heterarchy; Zimbabwe gold-trade facilitation; forest-state pre-Atlantic origins).
