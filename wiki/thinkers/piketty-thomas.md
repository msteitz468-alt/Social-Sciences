# Thomas Piketty

## Overview

French economist and public intellectual (b. 1971) whose collaborative historical work on top incomes and wealth, culminating in *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* (French 2013; English 2014), recentered long-run inequality, the capital/income ratio β, and **r > g** in social-science and public debate. Positions economics as a subdiscipline of the social sciences / **political economy** rather than a detached “economic science”—preferring historical sources and open democratic debate to mathematical certainties. Generation turning 18 in 1989: rejects Soviet nostalgia and lazy anticapitalism; inequalities problematic when not “founded only upon common utility” (1789 Declaration art. 1).

## Contributions

### Data infrastructure and historical reconstruction

- Long-run series on **income distribution** (tax-based top shares linked to national accounts) and **wealth** (estate taxes, inheritance/savings decomposition, national balance-sheet capital/income ratios), covering three centuries and more than twenty countries where data permit; primary historical experience US, Japan, Germany, France, Britain—British and French cases especially complete (national wealth to early eighteenth century).
- Collaboration with Anthony Atkinson, Emmanuel Saez, Facundo Alvaredo, and multi-country teams on the World Top Incomes Database (WTID); with Gilles Postel-Vinay and Jean-Laurent Rosenthal on Parisian estates; with Gabriel Zucman on historical wealth and tax havens.
- Earlier *Les hauts revenus en France au XXe siècle* (2001) as French foundation of the top-incomes program.

### Analytical apparatus (*Capital*, 2013/2014)

- [[capital-income-ratio]] β; [[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]] α = r × β (accounting identity); [[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]] β = s/g (long-run asymptotic law under stated assumptions).
- Central tendency [[r-greater-than-g]]: when return on capital exceeds growth of output/income, inherited wealth grows faster than wages—powerful force for concentration, not a market imperfection.
- Diagnosis of returning [[patrimonial-capitalism]] and the mid-century rise of a [[patrimonial-middle-class]] (middle 40% of the wealth distribution).
- Dual top-income structure: hyperpatrimonial rentiers vs hypermeritocratic [[supermanagers]] (especially US after ~1980); within top decile, “the 9 percent” (labor) vs “the 1 percent” (capital increasingly).
- Inheritance accounting: annual flow $b_y = \mu \times m \times \beta$; French V-curve of inheritance; critique of Modigliani life-cycle “end of inheritance.”
- Literary-social evidence (Balzac, Austen) for rentier thresholds when statistics thin; “meritocratic extremism” as modern justification culture.

### Policy program (POSITION claims—not findings)

- Modernize the [[social-state]] (education, health, PAYGO pensions) without a second 1930–80-scale fiscal leap.
- Rethink [[progressive-taxation|progressive income tax]], including confiscatory top rates on economically “useless” extreme labor incomes (US interwar innovation).
- Propose a [[progressive-global-wealth-tax]] (illustrative annual net-wealth schedule; regional/European stepping stone) plus automatic international banking transparency—as regulation of capitalism more than revenue instrument.
- Prefer taxation to public debt; exceptional progressive capital tax over inflation or austerity for debt reduction; European debt pooling and budgetary democracy for the eurozone.
- Research program page: [[piketty-capital-dynamics]]; study object: [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]].

### Selected quantitative anchors from the book (attributed)

- European private wealth ~6–7 years NI late nineteenth century → 2–3 mid-twentieth → ~5–6 early twenty-first.
- US top decile of NI ~45–50% (1910s–20s and 2000s) vs 30–35% mid-century trough.
- France: top centile of NI from >20% (1900–10) to 8–9% (2000–10) via fall of rentiers; wage top centile stable ~6–7%.
- Wealth Europe 1910: top 10% ~90%, top 1% ≥50%; today top 10% ~60%, patrimonial middle class ~⅓ of wealth; bottom half ~5% throughout.
- Pure return on capital long-run ~4–5% (interval 3–6%); capital share rich countries ~25–30% by 2000–2010 after mid-century low.
- Social-state tax take: <10% of NI before WWI → 30–55% by 2000–2010.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained in the French/US economics circuit (École Normale Supérieure / MIT lineage—detail thin until further sources; `trained_under` left [[unknown]]). Collaborative genealogy: Atkinson and Saez as co-builders of top-incomes database; Postel-Vinay and Rosenthal on estates; Zucman on offshore wealth. Dialogues with classical political economy (Malthus, Ricardo, Marx, Kuznets) as historical interlocutors rather than school-membership. Reacts against data-poor distribution debate and against optimistic postwar [[kuznets-curve]] narrative. Methodologically claims continuity with French “serial history” (Bouvier–Furet et al.) and political arithmetic / social tables, updated with centiles. Critiques postwar representative-agent public-debt models (Barro) and pure mathematical/experimental economics that neglect history.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Major public and academic debate followed the 2013–14 book: measurement of capital and β; elasticity of substitution; r vs g as mechanism; policy feasibility of global wealth tax; spreadsheet and series controversies. Full critique map requires secondary sources—flag on [[piketty-capital-debate]] and study Critiques section. Book’s own self-limits: imperfect incomplete data; conclusions “tenuous” and open to democratic debate; global capital tax admitted as utopian reference point. Reception context: post–financial-crisis Europe/US; public intellectual role beyond academic economics.

## Historiography

*Capital* is both a research product and a public event. Production context: roughly fifteen years of collaborative inequality research (1998–2013), post-2008 reception. Later World Inequality Report / WID.world infrastructure continues the program beyond this single volume—date-stamp any post-2014 figures separately. Intellectual self-positioning in the Conclusion: social scientists must not abandon numbers to economists; those with wealth never fail to defend their interests.
