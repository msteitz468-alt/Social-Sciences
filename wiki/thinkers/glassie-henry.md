# Henry Glassie

American folklorist and material-culture scholar. [[deetz-james|James Deetz]] names Glassie as his intellectual mentor after their 1969 meeting (Toledo American Studies conference); Deetz dedicated the 1996 *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* in part to Glassie and states that much of the book's treatment of cognition and material culture "owes its form" to him (notes).

## Overview

Glassie's folk-life and vernacular-architecture program (including *Folk Housing in Middle Virginia*, 1975) supplied Deetz with a structural account of how house form expresses **worldview** — the contrast between additive medieval/folk composition and Georgian order, symmetry, and segmentation. Vault coverage is currently secondary, via Deetz 1996; primary Glassie monographs not yet ingested.

## Contributions

*(Attributed as Deetz engages them.)*

- Folk housing as cognitive/structural system, not mere style catalogue.
- Georgian vs pre-Georgian design as change in basic tradition (with art-historical parallels Deetz quotes, e.g. Alan Gowans).
- Material folk culture of the eastern United States as a research domain linking folklore and historical archaeology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Reciprocal influence with Deetz (Deetz: Glassie used *Invitation to Archaeology*; Deetz remade his American Material Culture course after Glassie). Broader folklore and vernacular-architecture networks not yet mapped from primary Glassie sources.

## Deetz 1996 — how Glassie is used

*(Attributed as Deetz engages.)* Bilateral three-part symmetry as Georgian material format; folk vs popular culture contrast; *Folk Housing in Middle Virginia* as cognition/worldview warrant (notes: much of ISTF "owes its form" to Glassie); porch/"veranda in Yorubaland" comparison; reasons for cultural change (fashion, social distance, anonymity). Primary Glassie monographs still outstanding.

## Historiography

In historical archaeology, Glassie is often cited as the folk-architecture half of the "Glassie–Deetz" Georgian-order lineage later taken up by Mark Leone and others (textbook map in Johnson 2011 via [[historical-archaeology]]).
