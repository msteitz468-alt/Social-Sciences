# Max Weber

> Graduate-level intellectual biography: [[hubs/thinkers/weber-max|Thinkers Hub — Max Weber]].

## Overview

Max Weber (1864–1920) was a German historical economist, jurist, and sociologist, one of the canonical founders of classical [[disciplines/sociology|sociology]] and of [[political-sociology|political sociology]]. Ingested through three sources: *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism]]* (1904–5; revised 1920; Parsons English 1930); the lecture *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (*Politik als Beruf*, 1919; Gerth & Mills English 1946); and *[[weber-economy-society-1921|Economy and Society]]* Part I — the February 1921 instalment in Keith Tribe's 2019 translation (the four chapters Weber himself drafted 1919–20). Together they cover the **[[rationalization]]** of Western culture, elective affinities between religious ethics and economic conduct, the conceptual apparatus of [[interpretive-sociology|verstehende Soziologie]], economic action and [[formal-and-substantive-rationality|formal/substantive rationality]], and the [[types-of-legitimate-rule|sociology of legitimate rule]].

Giddens (1976, in the ingested PE edition) places the Protestant Ethic at a pivotal moment after Weber's recovery from depressive illness: earlier work had been technical economic history and jurisprudence in the tradition of the German historical school of economics; the PE opens the broader second-phase programme. Tribe's introduction stresses that the 1921 instalment is the only portion of *Economy and Society* Weber prepared for publication; the fuller 1922 book is Marianne Weber's reconstruction from older manuscripts.

## Contributions

- **[[protestant-ethic-thesis|Protestant-ethic thesis]].** Weber argued that the rational ethics of ascetic Protestantism — especially Calvinism — stood in elective affinity with the **[[spirit-of-capitalism|spirit of modern capitalism]]**, mediated by the ethic of the **[[calling-beruf|calling (*Beruf*)]]** and **[[inner-worldly-asceticism|inner-worldly asceticism]]**. He presented this as *one side of the causal chain*, not a monocausal idealist inversion of Marxism (Author's Introduction; Ch. 5 close).
- **Definition of modern capitalism.** Against the identification of capitalism with greed, Weber defined capitalism as continuous rational profit-seeking enterprise and singled out the Occidental form: rational organization of (formally) free labour, business/household separation, and rational book-keeping (Author's Introduction).
- **[[ideal-type|Ideal type]].** The "spirit of capitalism" is constructed as an *historical individual* / ideal-typical complex of cultural significance, not an average empirical description (Ch. 2; notes).
- **[[iron-cage|Iron cage]]** (Parsons's rendering of *stahlhartes Gehäuse*). Once asceticism helped build the modern economic cosmos, machine production forces calling-discipline without religious root — "The Puritan wanted to work in a calling; we are forced to do so" (Ch. 5).
- **Comparative religions programme.** PE is the Western case study; companion studies (China, India, ancient Judaism; unfinished Islam, etc.) were meant to compare both directions of causality between economic life and religious ethics (Author's Introduction; Parsons preface).

### Political sociology (*Politics as a Vocation*, 1919)

- **Definition of [[the-state|the state]].** Sociologically the state is defined by its means, not its ends: the human community that "successfully claims the **[[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force]]** within a given territory" — Weber's single most-cited sentence (*Politics as a Vocation*); restated in ES Ch.1 §17.
- **Three pure types of [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]].** Traditional, [[charismatic-authority|charismatic]], and legal-rational — the grounds on which the ruled hold authority legitimate. The lecture develops charisma as "the root of the idea of a calling." Full morphology: [[types-of-legitimate-rule]] and [[hubs/theory/types-of-legitimate-rule|Theory Hub]].
- **[[bureaucracy|Bureaucracy]] and the ethic of office.** Expert officialdom, separated from the means of administration (Weber's structural analogy to Marx); the official's honor is impartial execution *sine ira et studio*, the leader's is personal responsibility and the will to fight. ES Ch.3 supplies the ten-feature ideal type and "rule through knowledge."
- **[[plebiscitary-leader-democracy|Plebiscitary leader-democracy]].** A comparative sociology of party machines (caucus, boss, spoils) and the plebiscitarian leader — a realist, leader-selection account of mass democracy anticipating Schumpeter.
- **The two political ethics.** The [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends|ethic of ultimate ends]] (*Gesinnungsethik*) vs. the [[ethic-of-responsibility|ethic of responsibility]] (*Verantwortungsethik*) — reconciled only in the mature vocation ("Here I stand; I can do no other"). Closing image: "politics is a strong and slow boring of hard boards."

### Conceptual system (*Economy and Society* Part I, 1919–20 / 1921)

- **[[interpretive-sociology|Sociology of social action]].** Sociology as construing/understanding [[social-action|social action]] for causal explanation; [[verstehen]]; meaning-adequacy + causal-adequacy; four [[types-of-social-action]]; [[communalization-and-sociation]]; [[legitimate-order]]; Macht vs [[domination-herrschaft|Herrschaft]].
- **Economic sociology (Ch.2).** [[economic-action]]; [[market]]; money and calculation; [[formal-and-substantive-rationality]]; appropriation and occupation (*Beruf*); conditions of formally rational capitalist calculation; economy and political/hierocratic organisations.
- **Types of rule (Ch.3).** Full [[types-of-legitimate-rule|tripartite Herrschaft typology]]; [[legal-rational-authority]] and [[bureaucracy]]; [[traditional-authority]] (patriarchal/patrimonial/feudal); [[charisma]] and routinization.
- **Class and rank fragment (Ch.4).** [[social-class-weberian|Class position]] vs [[status-group|Stand]] — unfinished; the famous "Class, Status, Party" of the 1922 composite is **not** this fragment.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Formed in the German historical school of economics and in legal-historical training (Giddens; Tribe). Intellectual neighbours and foils named in the PE apparatus and ES Ch.1 preamble include **Werner Sombart**, **Lujo Brentano**, **Ernst Troeltsch**, **[[simmel-georg|Georg Simmel]]** (money economy; intended vs valid meaning), **Ferdinand Tönnies**, **Heinrich Rickert**, **Karl Jaspers**, **Rudolf Stammler** (critically), and PE critics **Felix Rachfahl**, later **R. H. Tawney** (see [[protestant-ethic-debate]]). Weber tried and failed to secure a chair for Simmel (as recorded on Simmel's page). `trained_under` / `trained` remain `[[unknown]]` until secondary biography is ingested; denser network treatment: [[hubs/thinkers/weber-max|hub]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The PE provoked continuous controversy from 1905 onward; Weber answered in the 1910 *Antikritisches Schlusswort* and in 1920 footnotes (Giddens; notes). Standing lines of criticism (as mapped by Giddens and PE notes): mischaracterization of Protestantism/Catholicism; thin empirics (Baden statistics, Anglo-Puritan sources); reverse causality (Tawney); Marxist epiphenomenal re-reading; ideal-type "violence to historical reality." See [[protestant-ethic-debate]] and [[weber-protestant-ethic-study]]. No misconduct record is in the ingested source; later ethical debates about Weber's politics lie outside this ingest.

## Historiography

[[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]] credited Weber as "the first among modern economic historians to protest against the brushing aside of primitive economics as irrelevant to the question of the motives and mechanisms of civilized societies" — a lineage claim for Weber as a forerunner of the [[substantivism|substantivist]] economic anthropology Polanyi founded (see [[embeddedness]]).

[[swidler-ann|Ann Swidler]]'s [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]] reworks the *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|Protestant Ethic]]* as the paradigm case of her [[settled-unsettled-lives|settled/unsettled]] distinction: ascetic Protestantism is ideology directly building new [[strategies-of-action|strategies of action]] in an *unsettled* period, an influence "strong but not deep." She simultaneously rejects the "switchmen" reading of Weber that [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] hardened into a values-determine-ends model (attributed to Swidler).

Reception history is itself a field (Fischoff 1944 survey cited by Giddens; Eisenstadt 1968 collection; Tribe 2019 on the ES translation/reception chain). Parsons carried Weber into American sociology (*Structure of Social Action*, 1937) while translating PE and TSEO; Giddens's 1976 PE introduction remains a standard English framing of that essay and its debate. Whether Weber is best labelled founder of "[[interpretive-sociology|interpretive sociology]]" or a *Sozialökonom* of *Lebensführung* is a live dispute: [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]. Graduate-level intellectual biography: [[hubs/thinkers/weber-max|Thinkers Hub]]. This page is the network summary from the three ingested sources.

[[collins-randall|Randall Collins]]'s *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975) is a major later reworking: Collins reads Weber as the "nominalist" conflict theorist of stratification, organizations, legitimacy, and the state, and charges that [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] "emasculated" this conflict Weber into a functionalist social system. His [[conflict-sociology|conflict sociology]] and [[geopolitical-theory-of-the-state|geopolitics]] build directly on Weber's military-organizational and comparative-historical sociology.

[[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]]'s *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989) turns Weber's account of [[bureaucracy]] and [[formal-and-substantive-rationality|instrumental rationality]] against modernity's self-image: Bauman argues (attributed) that nothing in Weber's ideal types of value-free, calculating administration excludes the possibility of the *Endlösung* — reviving Richard Rubenstein's 1978 "unanticipated consequences" reading of Weber against Guenther Roth's defense. See [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]].
