## Overview

**Lorimer Fison** (1832–1907) was a Methodist missionary-ethnographer whose Australian and Fijian kinship data, gathered with [[howitt-a-w|A. W. Howitt]] and supplied to [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]], made group marriage and classificatory kinship central to evolutionary anthropology.

## Contributions

- Methodist missionary and ethnographer whose Australian kinship data — collected with A. W. Howitt and channelled to Lewis Henry Morgan — became central evidence in evolutionary debates over group marriage and classificatory kinship.
- Born 1832 at Barningham, Suffolk, to a Quaker-descended, Evangelical Anglican family; attended Caius College, Cambridge (1855) but was sent down after two terms; emigrated to the Australian goldfields (1856); underwent a religious conversion after his father's death, became a Wesleyan Methodist minister, and sailed for Fiji as a missionary in 1863.
- From roughly 1866 Fison collected Fijian folktales and, via family connections, began corresponding with [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]], supplying Fijian and Tongan kinship schedules for Morgan's *Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family*. At Morgan's request he returned to Australia (1871–1875) to collect Aboriginal kinship, class, and totem data, working with [[howitt-a-w|A. W. Howitt]]; the two published *Kamilaroi and Kurnai: Group Marriage and Relationship, and Marriage by Elopement* (1880, published in Australia rather than via the Smithsonian).
- Served in Fiji again 1875–1884 (principal of a native-teacher training school), returned to Australia in 1884, edited a Methodist newspaper after 1888, visited England in 1894 at [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s invitation, and died in 1907. His decades of correspondence with Tylor and [[codrington-robert-henry|Codrington]] form a major documentary source for Stocking's account of the decline of evolutionary theorizing.

## Contributions — Kamilaroi and Kurnai, and its retraction

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995]].)* In *Kamilaroi and Kurnai* Fison argued Australian Aboriginal marriage-class systems demonstrated [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s evolutionary sequence of family forms (Communal → Barbarian → Patriarchal → Civilized), and that Kamilaroi class-marriage rules logically generated the "Turanian" classificatory kinship terminology. He held that "savages" reasoned by deduction from a governing social principle (the exogamous class structure) rather than by induction from individual kinship experience — Stocking notes this "anticipates the later Durkheimian tradition," particularly Fison's claim that "to the savage the group is the individual," with individual rights emerging only via a historical process of "segmentation" from an original corporate/tribal unity.

Fison developed a theory that the Kurnai (Gippsland) marriage-by-elopement system was a transitional or degraded survival of the two-class Kamilaroi system, adapted under conditions of geographic isolation — evidence, he argued, for a prior state of "primitive promiscuity" and a progression from maternal to paternal descent. He later explicitly repudiated this theory as elegant but wrong: **"it accounts for all the facts, but it is wrong in spite of that."** [CONTRADICTION-flag: Fison's own late-career reversal on his central Kurnai theory — see also [[social-evolutionism]] on the wider abandonment of "primitive promiscuity."] Over his career Fison grew increasingly skeptical of theoretical speculation, telling Tylor "It is quite enough to find out what they are" rather than pursue origins-speculation — reflecting, per Stocking, a broader disillusionment paralleling the decline of classical evolutionism.

## Methodological cautions (attributed)

Fison articulated explicit cautions about ethnographic reliability decades before these became disciplinary orthodoxy: native informants would tell missionaries less than they would tell other whites; leading questions elicit "the answer the questioner wants"; he deliberately withheld his own hypotheses from his field intermediary (Heffernan) to avoid contaminating responses, and cross-checked reports against multiple sources. Stocking nonetheless notes that Fison's late-life published introduction to *Tales from Old Fiji* reverted to an ethnocentric, degenerationist tone about Fijian "horror of cruelty and filth," which Stocking reads as a "regression" from the more relativistic understanding evident in his private correspondence — a caution against treating Fison's methodological sophistication as uniform across his career or between his private and published voice.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Correspondent of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]] and [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]; collaborator with [[howitt-a-w|A. W. Howitt]]; a key missionary informant of armchair evolutionism whose data Morgan used to argue for the reality of group marriage.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Assessed at length in [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]] (attributed); Stocking treats Fison's late self-repudiation of his own Kurnai theory, and his growing distrust of theoretical speculation, as emblematic of the internal erosion of confidence within classical [[social-evolutionism]] well before Boas's external critique. See that source for the fuller historicist reconstruction of this figure's place in the transition from evolutionism to social anthropology.
