# James C. Scott

## Overview

James C. Scott (1936–2024) was an American political scientist and anthropologist known for peasant resistance, state [[legibility]], and nonstate highland societies. In *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017) he extends that statecraft program into deep prehistory: early Mesopotamian states as fragile grain-and-manpower capture modules rather than inevitable civilizational progress. He identifies as “a card-carrying political scientist and an anthropologist and environmentalist by courtesy,” self-described as an amateur trespasser at the junction of prehistory, archaeology, ancient history, and anthropology.

### Deep-history statecraft (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

Scott frames the book as asking how *Homo sapiens* came so recently to live in crowded sedentary communities packed with livestock and a handful of cereals, governed by ancestors of states — the agrarian ecological complex that became the template for most recorded history until fossil fuels. He treats the work as self-consciously derivative: it creates no new primary knowledge but aims to “connect the dots” of existing specialist findings in illuminating ways (Tanner Lectures origin, Harvard 2011; multi-year rewrite).

Major moves Scott advances (all attributed, 2017):

- **[[grain-hypothesis]]:** Cereal grains (wheat, barley, millets, rice, maize) have unique characteristics that make them, virtually everywhere, the major tax commodity essential to early state building. Scott argues history records “no cassava states, no sago, yam, taro, plantain, breadfruit, or sweet potato states”; only grains suit concentrated production, assessment, appropriation, cadastral surveys, storage, and rationing. Premier “political crops” grow above ground and ripen roughly simultaneously.
- **Inversion of the Neolithic–state sequence:** Scott argues sedentism long preceded plant and animal domestication; both were in place millennia before agricultural villages and before tax-collecting walled states (~3100 BCE in the Tigris–Euphrates valley by his chronology). States “pop up only long after fixed-field agriculture appears.” The Neolithic agro-complex is necessary but not sufficient — Weberian “elective affinity,” not cause and effect.
- **Wetlands before irrigation states:** Sedentism and the first towns, Scott argues (following Jennifer Pournelle’s southern-alluvium reconstruction), were typically products of wetland abundance, not effects of irrigation organized by states. “Making the desert bloom” as foundation of first sedentary communities is, he holds, mistaken in nearly every particular.
- **[[domus-complex]] and self-domestication:** Scott treats the late-Neolithic multi-species module (fields, stores, people, livestock, commensals) as an unprecedented evolutionary camp that domesticates humans as much as plants and animals — through labor routines, crowding, diet, and disease. Guillermo Algaze’s line that “Uruk urban institutions, in turn, domesticated humans” is mobilized; expansive domestication may extend to slaves, state subjects, and women in the patriarchal family (provocation).
- **Zoonoses of crowding:** Scott argues infectious diseases of crowding have been grossly underestimated in the demographic fragility of early states; the domus assembles pathogens across species barriers; epidemics leave weak bone signatures but may explain sudden abandonments.
- **Bondage and the population machine:** Early states, Scott argues, were not magnets of luxury drawing free subjects by charisma; they captured and held population by forms of bondage, slave raids, forced resettlement, and walls that confine as well as protect. “No slavery, no state” is a temptation he voices as thesis, not wiki fact. Acquisition of people, not only territory, is at the center of early statecraft.
- **Collapse as disassembly / possible welfare improvement:** Following Yoffee, Cowgill, and Simon’s modularity, Scott treats much “collapse” as disassembly of fragile large units into smaller, often more stable components; well-being of a population must never be confounded with power of a court. He aims to “normalize” collapse.
- **Golden age of the barbarians / [[nonstate-spaces]]:** Scott argues that until roughly ~1600 CE the vast majority of world population remained nonstate peoples; life outside the state may often have been freer and healthier for nonelites; “barbarian” is a political category (not-yet-taxed), not a culture or evolutionary stage. Trade with states mattered more than pure raiding; secondary primitivism (flight from the state) is rational, not failure. Two melancholy notes: barbarian slave trade and mercenary service replenished the state core and helped dig the golden age’s grave.
- **[[legibility]] deep-timed:** The early state strives to create a legible, measured, uniform landscape of taxable grain and hold a large population for corvée, conscription, and grain production — continuous with *Seeing Like a State*, applied to Uruk lists, beveled ration bowls, and surveyor-kings (“rod and line”).

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Self-positioning (2017):** Scott brings “two decades trying to understand the logic of modern state power (*Seeing Like a State*) as well as the practices of nonstate peoples, especially in Southeast Asia… (*The Art of Not Being Governed*).” *Against the Grain* is the deep-time hinge between those books.

**Specialist guides he names:** Jennifer Pournelle, Norman Yoffee, David Wengrow, Seth Richardson (Mesopotamian alluvium); Joe Manning (cereal–state argument anticipation; title *Against the Grain*); Gordon Childe Lecture context (Institute of Archaeology, London, 2016 — situating the book against Childe’s long shadow over Neolithic/urban-revolution narratives).

**Interlocutors and lineages mobilized in 2017 (as Scott uses them):** [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]-adjacent forager affluence / domestic mode of production; Ester Boserup (backs-to-the-wall agriculture — accepted for drudgery, rejected as Fertile Crescent origin story); Melinda Zeder (multi-millennial low-level food production); Richard Wrangham (cooking/brain); Helen Leach (domestication syndrome); Guillermo Algaze (Uruk expansion; human domestication); Hans Nissen (aridity/concentration); Robert McC. Adams (salinity; nomad–sedentary continuum); Owen Lattimore (Great Wall as confinement of taxpayers; Han–steppe frontier); Ibn Khaldun (flat land vs mountains); Weber (elective affinity; booty capitalism); Chayanov (labor–consumer balance); Finley and Hochschild on slavery scale; Barfield “shadow empire”; Clastres-adjacent secondary primitivism; Beckwith on steppe trade. Interlocutor adjacency with [[graeber-david|Graeber]]/[[wengrow-david|Wengrow]]–style civilizational revisionism though an independent program (Graeber listed among intellectual guides; Scott 2017 is not a *Dawn of Everything* text).

**Progress narrative he reacts against:** Hobbes, Locke, Vico, Morgan, Engels, Spencer, Spengler, and social-Darwinist evolutionary schemes that settled hunting/gathering → nomadism → agriculture and band → village → town → city as progressive sequence.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Self-described amateur synthesis.** Scott marks the book as derivative “connect the dots”; specialist pushback is expected on Mesopotamian chronology, disease paleopathology, slavery quantification, and universality of the grain thesis. He repeatedly flags epistemic limits: few sites with convincing “first state” evidence; tablet–ground gap; disease role often a “hunch”; golden-age dating a “fool’s errand.”
- **Provocations are positions.** Collapse as boon, barbarian golden age, underestimated epidemics, and “no slavery, no state” are Scott’s interpretive positions (2017), not wiki-voice findings — route disputes to debate pages rather than silent overwrite.
- **Romanticization risk.** Nonstate welfare claims must not erase predation, raiding, and the slave trade among nonstate peoples — Scott himself marks the last two as deeply melancholy aspects of the “golden age.”
- **Four-way non-identity.** Where archaeological cultures, ethnonyms, or genetic labels appear in the secondary literature Scott cites, this wiki must not equate pots, languages, genes, and peoples — Scott’s “barbarian” is explicitly a *political* category, which helps, but comparative cases (China, Egypt, Maya, Rome) still need evidence-class discipline.
- **Un-ingested corpus.** *Seeing Like a State* and *The Art of Not Being Governed* are not yet vault-primary; [[legibility]] and [[high-modernism]] densification from 2017 is the deep-time extension only, not a substitute for the 1998 text.
