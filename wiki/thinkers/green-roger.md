# Roger C. Green

## Overview

Roger C. Green was an archaeologist central to the development of Pacific prehistory, per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017). Trained in settlement-pattern archaeology at Harvard under [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon Willey]], Green introduced the approach to Polynesia — first at 'Opunohu Valley, Mo'orea (Green 1961; Green et al. 1967), then in a major multiyear Western Samoa project (Green and Davidson 1969, 1974). He was a close, long-term collaborator (not a formal teacher) of [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Vinton Kirch]], reading the full manuscript of the first edition of Kirch's *On the Road of the Winds*.

## Contributions

- **[[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]** — introduced to Polynesia after training under Willey; programmatic statement (Green 1967b:102, quoted by Kirch, attributed): "the day has passed when such monuments or their structural features can afford to be treated only as contexts for portable artifacts and not as artifacts in their own right."
- **[[near-oceania-remote-oceania|Near Oceania / Remote Oceania distinction]]** (Green 1991b) — proposed explicitly as a replacement for the historically "sterile" Melanesia category (attributed, theoretical claim). Green's key summary formulation (1967a:237, quoted): "There never was a Polynesian migration from elsewhere; becoming Polynesian took place in Polynesia itself" — the core statement of the in-situ origins model for Polynesian culture.
- **[[triple-i-model]]** — co-developed model of intrusion, innovation, and integration for tracing Pacific cultural sequences (attributed to Green).
- **Ancestral Polynesian Society reconstruction** — developed with Kirch (Kirch and Green 1987, 2001), including the joint phylogenetic-vs-reticulate models debate for Oceanic cultural history.
- Co-directed, with Douglas Yen, the Southeast Solomons Culture History Program (NSF-funded from 1970; fieldwork 1971–72 and 1977–78) across Kolombangara, Ulawa, Uki, San Cristobal, Santa Ana, Santa Cruz/Nendö, the Reef Islands, Anuta, Vanikoro, Tikopia, and Taumako — an interdisciplinary project on which Kirch participated as a fieldworker and photographer.
- Was "deeply engaged with the question of Polynesian origins" (Green 1966, 1967a, 1968, per Kirch) and became convinced [[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita]] held the key to both pre-Polynesian and Melanesian origins.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Co-principal investigator with [[unknown]] on the Southeast Solomons Culture History Program alongside Douglas Yen.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kirch presents Green's Near Oceania/Remote Oceania distinction and in-situ origins model as the analytical framework that superseded earlier diffusionist and racially inflected schemes (including Te Rangi Hiroa's "Micronesian route," see [[te-rangi-hiroa]]) — recorded here as Kirch's historiographical assessment, attributed, not adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Historiography
