# Gerhard E. Lenski

## Overview

American sociologist (1924–2015), long associated with the University of North Carolina (and earlier Michigan), best known for the comparative-historical theory of stratification in *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (1966) and for later ecological-evolutionary macrosociology (*Human Societies*). He redefines social stratification as the **[[distributive-process|distributive process]]** — “who gets what and why?” — and organizes human societies by subsistence technology and surplus size under two **[[laws-of-distribution|laws of distribution]]** (need vs. power over surplus).

*Seeded from Lenski 1966; biographical depth, training genealogy, and later works not yet ingested. Thinkers Hub deferred (single theoretical source this session).*

## Contributions

- **[[ecological-evolutionary-theory|Ecological-evolutionary theory of stratification]]** — technology and surplus as master variables across five society types; agrarian peak of inequality; industrial **reversal**.
- **[[laws-of-distribution|Two laws of distribution]]** and the power–privilege–prestige triad ([[power-and-privilege]]).
- **Multidimensional [[class-system-lenski|class systems]]** within one distributive system; industrial occupational system as chief distributor for most people; citizenship as equalizing resource reviving redistribution.
- **[[status-consistency|Status consistency / inconsistency]]** (1950s research; restated 1966) — mixed ranks and political leanings.
- Explicit **synthesis scorecard** of conservative vs. radical traditions (Ch. 13): three issues conservative, two radical, three mixed by societal type — not eclecticism, he argues.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Dialogue partners in 1966:** Marx, Engels, Weber, Mosca, Pareto, Michels, Spencer, Sumner, Veblen, Sorokin, Parsons, Kingsley Davis (Davis–Moore), Dahrendorf, C. Wright Mills, Ossowski, van den Berghe; Hegelian dialectic as organizing device for the history of thought.
- **Reception:** [[collins-randall|Collins]] (*[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]*, 1975) treats Lenski’s technology-and-stratification model as “the only empirically validated” conflict theory of wealth while noting limits for industrial variation — see [[conflict-sociology]], [[social-mobility]], [[hubs/theory/conflict-sociology]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Comparative data quality and evolutionary typology criticized in later neo-evolutionism debates; industrial “reversal” and wealth vs. income measures remain live issues; sex/race as “class systems” is contested vocabulary. Detail from secondary afterlife to be filled as literature is ingested.
