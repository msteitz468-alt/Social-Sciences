# Elinor Ostrom

American political scientist; co-founder of the Workshop in Political Theory and Policy Analysis (Indiana University); 2009 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences (with Oliver Williamson) for analysis of economic governance, especially the commons. This summary rests primarily on *[[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990|Governing the Commons]]* (1990).

## Overview

Ostrom challenged the policy orthodoxy that common-pool resources must be either privatized or state-controlled. She showed, through institutional analysis of enduring and failed self-governing systems, that appropriators can craft rules solving supply, commitment, and monitoring problems under specified conditions.

## Contributions

- **[[common-pool-resource|CPR]] framework** — resource systems vs resource units; subtractability + difficulty of exclusion.
- **[[design-principles-cpr|Design principles]]** for long-enduring CPR institutions.
- **[[polycentricity|Polycentric]]** public-enterprise arrangements (e.g. California groundwater).
- Empirical program linking theory to comparative cases ([[ostrom-governing-the-commons-study]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Engages Hardin, Olson ([[collective-action-theory]]), prisoner’s-dilemma models; institutional economics and workshop tradition (Vincent Ostrom on polycentricity). Reacts against metaphor-driven Leviathan/privatization prescriptions.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Scope limited to small-to-medium CPRs within one country; design principles not a checklist guarantee; OCR- and secondary-case dependence in the 1990 book; later IAD framework elaborates multi-level rules (not fully ingested here).

## Historiography

Central to commons scholarship and development policy. Wiki coverage: one primary (1990). Thinkers Hub candidate after further primaries.

## Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — CPR/public-goods distinction applied to archaic states

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022.)*

Blanton et al. draw on Ostrom (1990; also Acheson 2011) to distinguish two forms of collective action relevant to early-state formation: **common-pool-resource** extraction (sustainable extraction from a shared, degradable resource such as a grazing or fishing commons, whose main risk is selfish over-extraction) versus a **public-goods** system (joint production of a shared benefit, such as roads or water infrastructure, not privately controlled). They apply Ostrom's point that social dilemmas occur even in small, culturally homogeneous groups to counter the common anthropological objection that rational-actor [[collective-action-theory|collective-action theory]] cannot apply where "the norms of traditional folk society" (Service 1975:301) or evolved "social instincts" (Boyd, Richerson, and Henrich 2005) already govern behavior. Following political scientist Margaret Levi (1988; applied to archaeology in Blanton and Fargher 2008:112–32), the authors also use an Ostrom-informed public-goods model of state variation: autocratic ("Oriental"-type) regimes form where governing elites can rely on "external revenues" not dependent on broad taxation, giving little incentive to solve cooperator problems or provide public goods (citing Ostrom 1998:7–9); states dependent on "internal revenues" are predicted to develop more "relational," less top-down governance, since members — including leadership — are expected to behave responsibly regarding shared resources (Levi 1988; Ostrom 1990).
