# Lewis R. Binford (1931–2011)

> Graduate-level intellectual biography: **[[hubs/thinkers/binford-lewis|Thinkers Hub — Lewis R. Binford]]**.

American archaeologist; principal architect of **[[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]]** (the New Archaeology). Co-editor with [[binford-sally-r|Sally R. Binford]] of *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* (1968). Distinct from Sally R. Binford — do not merge pages.

## Overview

Binford reoriented Anglophone archaeology toward **explanation of cultural process** via explicit theory and testable propositions. In *New Perspectives* (1968) he argued that traditional methodology cannot "read" the archaeological record with a priori interpretive principles; remains must serve as data for testing propositions about past systems. Culture is treated as a non-additive system; social organization is in principle recoverable. His *Man the Hunter* chapter (1968) rejects dependence on ethnology for verification of analogies. *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983) synthesizes [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]] and [[ethnoarchaeology]] from [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] fieldwork (1969–1973); the primary *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* (1978) monograph remains outstanding.

## Contributions

*(From *New Perspectives* 1968 — attributed.)*

- **[[processual-archaeology]] charter:** three aims (history, lifeways, process); overhaul method for process explanation; "arguments of relevance" linking observations to past cultural systems.
- **Archeological Perspectives:** critique of culture-historical affinity/taxonomy practice; systems view of culture; social inference as methodological challenge not ontological ban.
- **Post-Pleistocene Adaptations:** critique of Mesolithic systematics and oasis/nuclear-zone agricultural-origin stories; demographic–ecological adaptive model of multi-origin food production (attributed 1968).

*(From *Man the Hunter* 1968 — attributed, prior ingest.)*

- Analogy inspires hypotheses but does not verify them; shared task with ethnology is explanation of cultural differences and similarities.

*(From *In Pursuit of the Past* 1983 — attributed.)*

- **Statics–dynamics epistemology:** the [[archaeological-record]] is contemporary material; decoding requires bridging knowledge of how activities produce residues — a “science of the archaeological record.”
- **[[middle-range-theory-archaeology]]:** general evolutionary/functional theories do not substitute for independently tested inference methods; post hoc accommodative pattern-matching yields endless debate.
- **[[ethnoarchaeology]] among the [[nunamiut]]:** land-use scale, site complexes, drop/toss and house structure models; lessons for assemblage comparison without treating Nunamiut as living Paleolithic replicas.
- **[[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]:** functional/activity reading of Mousterian facies vs Bordes’ culture-historical types (book dedicated to Bordes).
- **Agricultural origins packing model** and critique of “Garden of Eden” / “Slug Principle” sedentism assumptions; later self-critique of residual 1968 Garden-of-Eden assumptions.
- **Paths to complexity:** skepticism of redistribution→power and trade→complexity without stress specification; interest in big-man organizations as origin loci.

*(From *[[binford-bones-1981|Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths]]* 1981 — attributed.)*

- **[[taphonomy]] and the scavenging argument:** built [[actualistic-studies|actualistic]] wolf-den and [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]]-butchery diagnostics for bone modification and assemblage composition, then reanalyzed the [[olduvai-gorge|Olduvai]] fauna to argue for hominid **scavenging** of marrow bones — against [[isaac-glynn|Isaac]]'s [[home-base|home-base]]/[[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing]] model and the [[hunting-hypothesis|man-the-hunter]] reading (see [[hunting-scavenging-debate]], [[binford-bones-study]]). Deflated [[dart-raymond|Dart]]'s [[osteodontokeratic-culture|osteodontokeratic]] claim and the Choukoutien cannibalism/bone-tool "myths." Core maxim: association is not behavioral integration.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Post-processual critique of positivism and meaning-blindness ([[processual-postprocessual-debate]]); Bordes–Binford Mousterian debate remains partially open; middle-range ethnoarchaeology widely adopted even where program branding is contested. Charter and *Pursuit* claims stay attributed and dated.

**Analogy as the central issue (Johnson 2011, attributed).**
[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson]] reports Binford argued in the 1970s that
archaeology's claim to scientific status rests on the problem of analogy — linking
present "statics" (the archaeological record) to past "dynamics" (past cultural systems)
— and that this was more fundamental than the era's grand epistemological/positivism
debates. Binford's own account of his Nunamiut motivation (attributed, direct quote):
"My aim was to study the relation between statics and dynamics in a modern setting. If
understood in great detail, it would give us a kind of Rosetta Stone…" (Binford 1983a:
24). Johnson reports Binford's **footprint/bear analogy** for actualistic studies:
seeing a footprint shape, one conjectures the animal; observing a bear later making that
exact print lets one retroactively identify the earlier print's maker with more
confidence — the logic underlying "actualistic" ethnoarchaeological studies. Binford also
held ethnoarchaeological fieldwork suited to building middle-range theory had to be
conducted by archaeologists themselves rather than left to ethnographers, whose research
aims are not specifically oriented to the link between activity and material residue
(attributed, Johnson 2011).

**Rejection of Schiffer's terminology.** Johnson (2011, attributed) reports Binford
rejected the term "behavioural archaeology" and attacked
[[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]'s philosophy in outspoken terms, despite substantial
overlap between behavioural archaeology, [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range
theory]], and [[taphonomy]].

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O present [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]] as the central figure of the post-~1960 New Archaeology / [[processual-archaeology]]: influenced by [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]], he urged problem orientation, hypothesis testing, and general cultural processes over culture-history accumulation. Empirical/theoretical cases they report (as of 2007):

- **Hunting vs scavenging:** cut-mark and assemblage debates at Olduvai/Olorgesailie and early *Homo* sites; Binford vs Bunn & Kroll and others on whether early hominins primarily hunted or scavenged.
- **Mousterian variability:** with Sally Binford, functional task-kit interpretation (tool prep, woodworking, butchery) via factor analysis across Syrian/Israeli/French sites—against Bordes’s four long-lived cultural “tribes.”
- **Mortuary complexity proxy:** Binford tradition assumption that treatment of the dead correlates with social complexity (juvenile rich burials ≈ ascribed status), with Carr caveats.
- **Agricultural origins:** Binford/Flannery broad-spectrum revolution; Binford post-Pleistocene demographic/sedentism models in the farming-origins menu.

### Gamble 1999 reciprocal note

*(Attributed — [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]], [[gamble-clive]]; as of Gamble 1999.)* Gamble treats the Binfords’ critique of Bordes’s five Mousterian tribes as a deliberate move away from culture history **and** social explanation, then reopens social analysis via networks rather than ethnic facies. He also revises Binford’s **planning-depth** contrast (greater among Cro-Magnons for environmental coping): planning/anticipation depend on detachment; following Clark and Brody, planning is social as sharing of memories — decision taken in the doing; [[taskscape]] (Ingold) breaks dichotomous “games against the environment.” See [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]], [[mousterian-industry]], [[neanderthals]].

## Bellwood *First Farmers* densification (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005.)*

Bellwood restates the **post-Pleistocene packing** model associated with Binford (1968) and [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] (1969): high population densities in favorable coastal zones (sedentary fisher-foragers) produce outflow into marginal zones, where cereal cultivation becomes a strategy to increase food supply — cultivation likeliest at **edges** of wild plant ranges rather than cores. The packing model is one strand in Bellwood’s multi-causal menu for agricultural origins; his own program stresses demography of farmers and demic [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis|dispersal]] beyond homeland origins rather than packing alone. See [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]], [[broad-spectrum-revolution]].

## Barker 2006 densification (*The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006; Binford as processual agricultural-origins theorist and mobility ethnographer.)*

- **New Archaeology frame:** Barker situates Binford (1962, 1964; with Clarke 1968) in the 1960s [[processual-archaeology|Processual / New Archaeology]]: cultures as adaptive systems; change explained by environment/technology or internal stress rather than mainly migration/diffusion; multi-period regional survey + excavation; Tehuacán, Jarmo, Deh Luran as exemplary “pathways to agriculture.”
- **1968 packing / marginal-zone model (SW Asia):** Pleistocene population–resources equilibrium; postglacial sea-level rise → coastal food collectors rely more on fish/birds/shellfish → sedentism and population growth → migration into less-populated adjacent zones → packing above carrying capacity → intensification; upland wild cereals/caprines already exploited → husbandry developed to produce more food. Barker notes weak evidence for the proposed hillward migration but lasting influence of the environment–foraging–sedentism–population nexus (citing Layton 1999).
- **Shared premise with Flannery:** Foragers needed a **strong reason** to abandon foraging — contrast with earlier views that farming’s advantages were obvious (Harris review papers 1969, 1973 in the same stream). Classed among Stark’s (1986) “push” models (stress forces farming).
- **Forager vs collector mobility (Binford 1980):** Residential **foragers** (daily foraging radius; move whole group when patches diminish — e.g. !Kung-type plant-rich environments) vs **collectors** (base camps + logistic field camps/stations, cache meat — e.g. Nunamiut extreme latitudes); collectors need complex tech and storage. Paired in Barker’s Ch. 2 with Woodburn’s immediate- vs delayed-return contrast.
- **Nunamiut range scale (via Barker):** Band (~5 families) residential core ~5,400 km², annual range ~25,000 km²; drive lines, caches, hunting blinds (Binford 1983) — ethnographic spine for logistic mobility, not “proto-herding” of reindeer (European late-glacial herding claims remain discounted in Barker’s Europe chapter).
- **Demography revisited (as of Barker 2006 citing Binford 1998):** Binford modelled Europe such that Holocene foragers were forced into organizational change (complex foragers or forager-farmers) above a packing threshold of ~9 people per 100 km² — one late entry in Barker’s table of demographic-causation tools, amid general scholarly shyness about demography for lack of fine-scale reconstruction.
