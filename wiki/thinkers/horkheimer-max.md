# Max Horkheimer

## Overview

Max Horkheimer (1895–1973) was a German philosopher and social theorist, director
of the Institut für Sozialforschung (the [[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt School]])
from 1931 to 1959, and the chief architect of the program he named "Critical
Theory." As institutional leader he defined the Institut's interdisciplinary
research agenda and, with [[adorno-theodor-w|Theodor Adorno]], co-wrote its
foundational text. This page is the concise network summary; holdings so far
derive from the single co-authored source
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*
(1947).

## Contributions

- **[[critical-theory|Critical Theory]]** — the program named and defined in his
  1937 essay *Traditional and Critical Theory*: social theory should be
  emancipatory and reflexive about its own social conditions, not the
  value-neutral description "traditional" (positivist) theory claims to be.
- **[[instrumental-reason|The critique of instrumental reason]]** — the thesis,
  central to *Eclipse of Reason* (1947) and *Critique of Instrumental Reason*
  (1967), that reason has been reduced to a calculating instrument ("subjective
  reason") indifferent to ends, so that Enlightenment reason turns against
  itself. Horkheimer is the figure most associated with the phrase.
- **[[dialectic-of-enlightenment]]** and **[[culture-industry]]** — co-authored
  with Adorno; the joint diagnosis of Enlightenment's self-destruction and of
  mass culture as instrumental reason industrialized.
- **The Institut's empirical program** — as director he steered the collective
  studies on authority and the family (*Studien über Autorität und Familie*,
  1936) and, in American exile, the *Studies in Prejudice* series including
  *[[unknown|The Authoritarian Personality]]* (1950).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Horkheimer took over the Institut's directorship in 1931, reorienting it from
orthodox Marxist economics toward an interdisciplinary "materialist" social
philosophy. He founded and edited the *Zeitschrift für Sozialforschung*, the
Institut's journal, which gathered the first generation — [[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]],
[[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]], Erich Fromm, Leo Löwenthal, Friedrich Pollock (his
lifelong friend and the book's dedicatee), Walter Benjamin. He organized the
Institut's emigration (Geneva, then Columbia University in New York, then Los
Angeles) after 1933, and its return to Frankfurt in 1949–50. His debts run to
Hegel, [[marx-karl|Marx]], Schopenhauer, [[weber-max|Weber]], and Freud. *(The
`trained_under`/`trained` fields are `[[unknown]]` pending biographical sources.)*

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Recorded in attributed voice: like Adorno, Horkheimer is charged with a totalizing
pessimism that leaves no rational standpoint for critique — the objection Habermas
systematized in distinguishing communicative from instrumental reason (see
[[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]]). His postwar political conservatism (a
turn toward a resigned, quasi-theological "longing for the wholly other," and
public caution during the 1960s) is read by some as a retreat from the radicalism
of the 1930s program, and set the terms of a generational break with the students
Adorno faced in 1969.

## Historiography

Long overshadowed by Adorno as a stylist and by Marcuse as a public figure,
Horkheimer has been reassessed as the strategic and institutional author of
Critical Theory — the essays of the 1930s (collected as *Critical Theory*, 1968)
and his role as director now read as decisive for the school's shape. The
[[hubs/theory/critical-theory|Theory Hub analysis of Critical Theory]] treats his
programmatic 1937 distinction and the *Dialectic*'s argument in depth. A Thinkers
Hub biography is warranted but deferred pending ingestion of his own essays.
