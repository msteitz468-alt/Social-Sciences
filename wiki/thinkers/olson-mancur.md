# Mancur Olson

## Overview

Mancur Olson (1932–1998) was an American economist whose *The Logic of
Collective Action* (1965) founded the modern economic theory of interest groups
and collective action. Trained as an economist, he applied the tools of
price theory to problems conventionally left to political science and sociology,
arguing that the failure of large groups to organize around shared interests is
the *rational* outcome of individual self-interest, not a deviation from it.
The book is the primary source ingested here; his later
macro-historical work (*The Rise and Decline of Nations*, 1982) extends the
argument to national economic growth and decline.

## Contributions

Olson's core contribution is the [[free-rider-problem]] as a general theory of
organization: because a [[public-good|collective good]] cannot be withheld from
non-contributors, rational members of a [[latent-group|large "latent" group]]
have no incentive to contribute, so the good is under-provided absent
[[selective-incentives|selective incentives]] or coercion. From this single
move he derived: the privileged / intermediate / latent [[latent-group|group
typology]]; "the exploitation of the great by the small" (small members
free-riding on large members' provision in privileged groups); the necessity of
compulsory membership for labor unions; a reinterpretation of Marxian class
theory as a latent-group problem (see [[marx-karl]]); a critique of pluralist
"group theory" as committing the "anarchistic fallacy"; and the
[[by-product-theory|by-product theory]] of pressure groups. These launched the
[[collective-action-theory|collective action research programme]] within
[[rational-choice-theory]] and public-choice economics.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Olson wrote *The Logic of Collective Action* in dialogue with the emerging
public-choice and constitutional-economics literature — he reviewed Buchanan and
Tullock's *The Calculus of Consent* (1962) as the book was taking shape — and
drew on the public-finance tradition of collective goods (Samuelson, Musgrave,
Head, and, as the clearest early statement, Knut Wicksell). He acknowledged
Richard Zeckhauser (with whom he co-authored the alliances papers), John Rawls,
and Richard Musgrave for specific ideas. His work in turn framed the problem that
Elinor Ostrom's commons-governance research and much social-movement theory set
out to solve, and James S. Coleman's second-order [[free-rider-problem]] builds
on Olson's first-order statement. [[hirschman-albert|Albert O. Hirschman]]
(*[[hirschman-exit-voice-loyalty-1970|Exit, Voice, and Loyalty]]*, 1970)
explicitly cites Olson on few-buyer markets favoring [[voice-option|voice]] as
collective action; EVL's recuperation-after-decline problem is adjacent to, not
absorbed by, [[collective-action-theory]] (see [[exit-voice-loyalty]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Olson's central empirical prediction — that collective benefits do not motivate
participation, only selective incentives do — is among the most contested claims
in rational-choice social science. Green and Shapiro (*Pathologies of Rational
Choice Theory*, 1994) argue the evidence generally runs against it and that
later "revisionist" expansions of "selective incentives" (to include reputation,
solidarity, expressive benefit) drained the theory of falsifiable content — while
crediting Olson himself for resisting such ad hoc rescues as "unnecessary and
unscientific" (see [[free-rider-problem]] and [[rational-choice-theory]]). Olson
himself hedged the theory's scope, conceding it is "not at all sufficient" for
philanthropic, religious, and ideological "mass movement" organizations.

[[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] (*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How
Institutions Think]]*, 1986) argued from the anthropological record that the
evidence "supports the widest extension of Mancur Olson's main thesis that
individuals are easily deterred from contributing to the collective good," but
not his scale argument — smallness of scale does not itself produce trust — and
that his exemptions of religious organizations and small-scale societies were
mistakes. Her chapter "How Latent Groups Survive" then supplied what she saw as
the missing mechanism: a functionalist account (weak leadership, strong
boundary via an equality/100%-participation rule, shared belief in an outside
conspiracy) by which a [[latent-group]] persists without coercion or
[[selective-incentives|selective incentives]].

## Historiography

*The Logic of Collective Action* was reissued in 1971 with a new preface and a
bibliographic Appendix; Olson declined a substantive revision, stating his views
were unchanged. His reputation rests on the durability of the free-rider problem
as an analytic tool across economics, political science, and sociology, and on
the macro-historical *Rise and Decline of Nations*. His final book, *Power and
Prosperity*, appeared posthumously in 2000. [[ostrom-elinor|Ostrom]] (1990)
engages him as a third major CPR metaphor alongside Hardin and the prisoner’s
dilemma—see [[commons-governance-theory]], [[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990]].
