# Edwin N. Wilmsen

American anthropologist and archaeologist whose *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* (1989) became the central monographic statement of **Kalahari revisionism** — the argument that San-speaking peoples were long integrated into regional herding, trade, and colonial economies as a **rural underclass**, not isolated evolutionary remnants usable as a direct window onto the Pleistocene. At publication he was research fellow in anthropology at the University of Texas, Austin, with prior teaching and consulting (including for the government of Botswana).

## Overview

*(Attributed — Wilmsen 1989; densified at full ingest.)* Wilmsen's program is explicitly **not a classic ethnography**: he rejects the "ethnographic era" that excises societies from historical context and reconstructs the Kalahari as an integrated political-economic field linking San-speakers with Batswana, Ovaherero/Mbanderu, and Nama-speaking neighbors. Primary empirical base includes multi-year work with **Zhu** and **Mbanderu** at **CaeCae** (Onyaiyna) and surrounding Ngamiland, plus extensive archival and archaeological synthesis. Study object: [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]].

## Contributions

- **Revisionist Kalahari political economy** — densifies Position B of the [[kalahari-debate]]: San foraging of the mid-twentieth century as product of dispossession and class formation, not pristine autonomy.
- **Critique of forager models and *Man the Hunter* legacy** — challenges isolationist and evolutionary uses of San ethnography (against [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], Marshall, and related ecological-forager programs).
- **Multi-evidence reconstruction** — archaeology, archives, ethnography, kinship/tenure, labor, and nutritional/physiological data mobilized under one political-economy frame ([[anthropological-political-economy]]; [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] is an explicit epigraph interlocutor).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Draws on Marxist and political-economy anthropology (Wolf, Meillassoux, and related French/Anglophone traditions), southern African historiography, and Kalahari archaeology (including work associated with James Denbow and others). Positions himself against ecological-equilibrium and "foraging mode of production" framings associated with Lee and Silberbauer.
