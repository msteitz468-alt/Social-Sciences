# Friedrich Max Müller

## Overview

Friedrich Max Müller (1823–1900) was a German-émigré scholar who became Victorian Britain's most prominent popularizer of comparative philology. Trained in a German intellectual milieu marked by Lutheran pietist and Romantic influences, Müller settled at Oxford from 1847, edited the Rig-Veda, and in 1868 became the first holder of Oxford's chair in comparative philology — founded, per George W. Stocking, Jr., "with the express proviso that it be offered first to Müller." Stocking treats Müller as a pivotal and structurally ambiguous figure in the Victorian human sciences: a scholar whose linguistic methods anticipated aspects of Franz [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s later critique of evolutionary anthropology, even as his substantive conclusions echoed the older tradition of Christian degenerationism he might otherwise seem to have superseded.

## Contributions

- **Comparative philology and the classification of language.** In an 1847 "Letter on the Turanian Languages," contributed to Christian Bunsen's *Universal History*, Müller adapted Wilhelm von Humboldt's morphological classification of languages to hypothesize a series of migrations outward from "the common centre of mankind," producing an arrested Chinese language-stage, a rapidly differentiating agglutinative "Turanian" stage, and a grammatically settled Semitic/"Arian" stage.
- **Popularizing Aryanism.** Müller's 1856 essay on "Comparative Mythology" and his 1861 and 1864 *Lectures on the Science of Language* propagated the idea of "the Aryans" as the primitive linguistic and cultural ancestors of the civilized populations of modern Europe. Stocking records that Müller was apparently the first to give the older term "Arian" the spelling — "Aryan" — subsequently associated with modern racialism, and that for some years he was Britain's most vocal proponent of Aryanist ideas.
- **Linguistic paleontology.** Müller used comparison of common versus divergent root-words across the Indo-European languages to reconstruct a picture of the primitive, undivided Aryans as "agricultural nomads" — people who could plow, weave, and build roads, count to one hundred, recognized kinship and marriage law, followed leaders and kings, and held a belief in a divine being. This method — inferring a lost culture's material and social life from the vocabulary shared by its linguistic descendants — was among Müller's most influential contributions to Victorian ethnological method, independent of the mythological theory built on top of it.
- **The "mythopoeic age" and "disease of language."** Müller theorized that Aryan mythology arose as an unintended, metaphorical byproduct of purely linguistic processes — gendered grammatical terminations, the absence of auxiliary verbs, synonymy, and polyonymy — a process he later termed a "disease of language": "we have mythology, or if I may say so, we have diseased language." On this theory much Aryan mythology (Baldr, Sigurd, Sifrit, Achilles as dying young heroes) encoded, via a solar-mythology framework, a "whole solar drama" of the sun's daily or seasonal death and rebirth.
- **A rival epistemology of language and mind.** Müller held, against the naturalistic and Darwinian view, that language was constitutive of and even prior to thought, and he rejected the possibility of language arising naturalistically from animal communication, maintaining that "language is our Rubicon, and no brute will dare to cross it." Despite this broadly antievolutionary stance, Stocking notes that Müller nonetheless adapted the Darwinian idea of natural selection to explain how, among many candidate forms, particular words came to be preserved over time within a language — an instance of selective borrowing from evolutionary biology by an otherwise anti-Darwinian thinker.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Müller's Aryanism carried, in Stocking's reading, a genuine ambiguity: it implied on one hand a universalist "brotherhood" uniting light- and dark-skinned Indo-European speakers as "children of the same father," while on the other hand Müller described the "Aryan mind" — via his admiration for Kant — as representing "the perfect manhood," language resonant with the explicitly racial ideology given systematic form in the same period by Arthur de Gobineau. Late in life, after decades of controversy over Aryan origins, Müller repudiated racial uses of the term "Aryan" and insisted he had always maintained the independence of ethnological (racial) and linguistic classification — a retrospective claim that Stocking notes sits uneasily against Müller's own 1850s usage, in which "Aryan" implied a shared community of both language and race.

Müller stood in sustained methodological opposition to the leading sociocultural evolutionists of the 1860s. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] held, against Müller, that mythology rested "on a basis of real and sensible analogy" — that material myth was primary and verbal myth secondary — reflecting Tylor's materialist, associationist psychology against Müller's view of language as constitutive of and prior to thought; Tylor's 1866 review nonetheless credited Müller with applying a Lyellian uniformitarian method to mythology, reasoning from observable present-day linguistic processes back to their ancient causes. [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]] disputed Müller's account of the origin of language, holding that "as ideas came gradually, and therefore words... came after ideas" — aligning McLennan with Tylor, [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], and [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] as empiricist critics of Müller's more Kantian, language-constitutive epistemology. Lubbock, for his part, quoted at length "my friend Professor Max Müller" on the divine origin of language's "original roots," while himself preferring onomatopoeia (Müller's derisive "bow-wow theory") as a "satisfactory" naturalistic alternative. Müller and Tylor's followers eventually fell into a bitter dispute over the interpretation of Greek mythology, after which, Stocking records, Müller came to recognize "profound differences" between his own methodological assumptions and those of sociocultural evolutionism, and the two intellectual circles drew apart.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking characterizes Müller's critique of evolutionary linguistic and mythological assumptions as having an "ambiguous double thrust — on the one hand, anticipating issues that were to be raised by his intellectual countryman, Franz Boas; on the other, echoing themes of traditional Christian degenerationism." As early as 1856, Müller argued that "the idea of humanity emerging slowly from the depths of an animal brutality can never be maintained again" — a stance Stocking records as a contested theoretical position within Victorian debate rather than a settled finding, and one that placed Müller closer to degenerationist than to developmentalist assumptions about human history, despite his simultaneous embrace of a genuinely historical, comparative method for language itself.

## Historiography

Stocking's treatment of Müller in *Victorian Anthropology* (1987) is part of a broader argument that "classical evolutionism" should be read not as a single unified paradigm but as a synthesis of several distinct pre-existing intellectual currents — Prichardian ethnology, physical anthropology, and progressivist developmentalism — some of whose components (Müller's comparative and uniformitarian method) were absorbed into evolutionary anthropology even as others (his degenerationist and language-constitutive commitments) were rejected by it. On Stocking's account, Müller's reputation and methodological legacy accordingly bifurcated: his empirical technique of linguistic paleontology and his uniformitarian method for reading myth carried forward into anthropological and philological practice, while his substantive doctrines of Aryanism, solar mythology, and linguistic degeneration became, within a generation, the discipline's exemplary case of a superseded and largely discredited research program.
