# Jack Golson

## Overview

Jack Golson was a Cambridge-trained archaeologist, per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017), who brought a materialist tradition inherited from his mentors V. Gordon Childe and Grahame Clark to Pacific prehistory. He was appointed the University of Auckland's first academic archaeologist in 1954, professionalized New Zealand excavation standards, founded and organized the New Zealand Archaeological Association, and published key papers on the New Zealand prehistoric sequence (Golson 1959). In 1961 he moved to the Australian National University to expand Melanesian archaeology, where he directed a substantial cohort of students toward Melanesian and New Guinea sites.

## Contributions

- Professionalized New Zealand archaeological excavation standards and founded the New Zealand Archaeological Association.
- Wrote key early papers establishing the [[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita cultural complex]] as an object of study (Golson 1963, 1971, 1972a, 1972b).
- Excavated [[kuk-swamp|Kuk Swamp]] in the New Guinea Highlands, providing evidence for independent agricultural origins in New Guinea.
- Publicly refuted [[heyerdahl-thor|Thor Heyerdahl]]'s South American-origins claims for Rapa Nui, asserting (Golson 1965:78, quoted by Kirch) that the archaeological evidence supported settlement "by ancestral Polynesians from the west, with subsequent internal cultural developments" — a position, attributed.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]] and Grahame Clark (materialist archaeological tradition, Cambridge). Clark does not yet have a dedicated wiki page; recorded here in prose per naming-convention rules.
- **trained (directed as students at ANU, per Kirch):** J. Peter White, Peter Lauer, Brian Egloff, Ron Vanderwal, Geoff Irwin, and Jim Allen — a training network distinct from, and complementary to, the Bishop Museum/Polynesia lineage associated with [[te-rangi-hiroa]] and [[emory-kenneth]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kirch presents Golson's rebuttal of Heyerdahl, alongside Robert Suggs's, as part of the discipline's decisive rejection of the American-origins hypothesis (see [[heyerdahl-polynesian-origins-debate]]). No documented reversal or reappraisal of Golson's own findings is recorded in this source.

## Historiography

Primary source in this vault: Kirch, *On the Road of the Winds* (2017), which credits Golson with founding rigorous stratigraphic archaeology in both New Zealand and, later, the wider Melanesian and New Guinea region.
