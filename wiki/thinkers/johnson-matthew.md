# Matthew Johnson

## Overview

Matthew Johnson (b. 1962) is a British archaeologist known for landscape and
historical archaeology of late-medieval and early-modern England and, above all,
as author of the widely used textbook **[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|*Archaeological Theory: An Introduction*]]**
(1999; 2nd ed. 2010). He works within the broad **[[post-processual-archaeology|postprocessual]]**
and interpretive tradition while writing pedagogical surveys that aim to be
even-handed across schools.

## Contributions

Johnson's principal contribution to this wiki is synthetic and pedagogical: his
textbook's historical framing of archaeological theory — the New Archaeology and
its postprocessual reaction — and his insistence that "common sense is not enough"
(that all archaeological practice is theory-laden). His substantive research applies
interpretive and landscape approaches to English vernacular architecture, enclosure,
and the archaeology of capitalism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A product of the British theoretical milieu associated with the Theoretical
Archaeology Group; sympathetic to but not uncritical of the interpretive
archaeology of [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] and colleagues.

**An Archaeology of Capitalism (1996), as reported by Hall (2000).** [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000) summarized Johnson's *Archaeology of Capitalism* as interpreting British landscape enclosure and the transition from open medieval hall-houses to compartmentalized Georgian houses as materializing an emergent capitalist ideology of privatized, "de-spiritualized" space, in parallel across Britain and the eastern American seaboard: "The material presence of that landscape... thus changed its nature: from being a carrier of meaning in an embedded way, to one which might be drained of meaning" (Johnson 1996: 77–8); "If open fields mapped complex social relations on the ground, the enclosed landscape masked such relations... So it was with traditional houses" (Johnson 1996: 82). Hall grouped this work, with [[leone-mark-p|Leone]]'s and Shackel's, under **critical materialism** ([[critical-archaeology]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reviewers of the textbook have divided over whether Johnson achieves neutrality;
he concedes the book sits between "neutral survey" and "committed polemic." Critics
from the processual and evolutionary camps have argued the text underweights
scientific approaches relative to the British interpretive tradition.

**Hall (2000) on silencing subaltern voices.** [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*) critiqued Johnson's reading of probate inventories and 16th-century English architecture as silencing women, servants, and children, arguing this elides the historian-archaeologist's own narrative with the representations made by those already holding power — rendering subaltern voices invisible a second time. Hall placed this alongside his broader critique of critical-materialist "dominant ideology" approaches (Leone, Shackel) for missing the [[scott-james-c|hidden transcript]] of everyday resistance. See [[critical-archaeology]], [[theory-in-historical-archaeology-debate]]; study [[hall-archaeology-modern-world-study]].

## Historiography

Reputation rests primarily on the textbook's ubiquity in Anglophone theory teaching
rather than on a single landmark study.
