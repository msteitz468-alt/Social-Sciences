# James George Frazer

## Overview

Sir James George Frazer (1854–1941) was the central British figure of late Victorian and Edwardian **armchair comparative anthropology** and comparative religion. A classicist by training (Trinity College, Cambridge), he extended [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s intellectualist program into a vast comparative synthesis of magic, kingship, and vegetation ritual, culminating in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (1890; third edition 12 vols. 1911–15; author's abridgement 1922). He never conducted ethnographic fieldwork; his evidence base was travellers', missionaries', classicists', and folklorists' reports. Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/frazer-james-george|Thinkers Hub]].

## Contributions

- **[[sympathetic-magic]]** — analysis of magic into the Law of Similarity (homoeopathic/imitative) and the Law of Contact/Contagion (contagious), both as misapplications of the association of ideas; magic as "false science" and "abortive art."
- **Magic → religion → science** — the evolutionary sequence of human attempts to control the world (see [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]).
- **[[divine-king|The killing of the divine king]]** — the Nemi priesthood as type-case of sacred kings who must be slain when their strength fails, to transfer the embodied divine/vegetative life.
- **[[dying-and-rising-god]]** and **[[vegetation-spirit]]** — Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus, Demeter, and European corn-spirits as personifications of the yearly death and revival of plant life (building on W. Mannhardt).
- **[[taboo]]**, **[[scapegoat]]**, **[[external-soul]]**, **[[sacred-marriage]]** — comparative catalogues that shaped twentieth-century ritual theory, literature, and (later) critique.

## Intellectual Genealogy

trained_under (intellectual debt): [[tylor-edward-burnett]] — Frazer regarded himself as working in Tylor's evolutionist and intellectualist line ([[animism]], [[survivals]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], [[comparative-method]]). Key influence from Wilhelm Mannhardt on vegetation spirits. Contemporaries/rivals in Victorian evolutionism: [[spencer-herbert]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]], Lubbock, McLennan. Later fieldwork anthropology (Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown) and Boasian particularism reacted against his method; see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Twentieth-century anthropology rejected Frazer's unilinear evolutionism, his decontextualized comparison, his "If I were a horse" intellectualism, and his colonial-era ranking language. Functionalists denied [[survivals]] and conjectural history; Evans-Pritchard and others attacked the rationalist reconstruction of "primitive philosophy." Literary modernism (Eliot, Yeats, etc.) absorbed his imagery even as anthropology discarded his theories. Stocking and others reappraise him as a central institution of comparative religion and as a case of how evidence-class and production context shape theory.

George Stocking (*[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Victorian Anthropology]]*, 1987) notes that *The Golden Bough*, first published at the end of the 1880s, carried a "relatively pristine Tylorian" intellectualist viewpoint on magic and religion well into the twentieth century — long after the broader anti-evolutionary reaction had begun in British anthropology. Frazer's engagement with [[totemism]] began when his mentor William Robertson Smith asked him to write the totemism article for the ninth edition of the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*, building on [[mclennan-john-ferguson|John Ferguson McLennan]]'s original 1869 formulation; Frazer's subsequent "theories" of totemism became a focus of major anthropological debate in the early 1900s.

## Additional biographical and theoretical detail (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995)

(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995, Ch. 4 "The Frazerian Moment.") Born 1854, Glasgow, to a Free Church chemist family; educated University of Glasgow (matric. 1869) then Trinity College, Cambridge, fellow from 1879; married 1896; Professor of Social Anthropology, University of Liverpool, 1908, for five months only, on condition of no teaching or examining duties; knighted before WWI. The third (12-volume) edition of *The Golden Bough* sold roughly 36,000 copies in the decade after 1911; the one-volume abridgment sold roughly 33,000 copies in the following decade.

Frazer offered **three successive theories of totemism's origin**: (1) in the first edition of *The Golden Bough* (~1890), totemism as originating in belief in an "external soul" (drawing on folklorist Edward Clodd and ethnologist G. A. Wilken), which Frazer later called "at most only a partial solution"; (2) stimulated by Spencer and Gillen's Arunta ethnography (via a 1896 letter from Baldwin Spencer to Lorimer Fison), totemism as a system for the magical multiplication of food species by division of labor among totem groups — the theory that led Frazer to conclude "magic has everywhere preceded religion" and to formulate the magic-religion-science sequence explicitly; (3) by 1905, the "conceptional theory" — totemism originating in "savage ignorance of the physical process by which men and animals reproduce," based on Arunta beliefs that a person's totem is determined by where the mother first felt quickening rather than by biological descent. He synthesized the world ethnographic data on totemism in the four-volume *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910).

Per Stocking, Frazer held method/theory should be strictly separated from ethnography — theory was "left to the comparative ethnologist" — and he actively encouraged ethnographic fieldwork (Seligman, Roscoe, Radcliffe-Brown) while treating his own theories as "convenient pegs on which to hang my collections of facts." His later work *Psyche's Task* (1909) reversed his earlier optimistic Tylorian view: he argued superstition had historically been a necessary support of social institutions (government, property, marriage), warning in a 1908 lecture of "the ground beneath our feet... honeycombed by unseen forces" and describing contemporary society as a "volcano" threatened by resurgent irrationalism — a sociological pessimism contrasted by Stocking with Tylor's mid-Victorian optimism. Frazer disputed [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]]'s sociological reading of sacrifice-as-communion, insisting instead on "the old gift theory" (Tylor's *do ut des* — sacrifice as propitiation rather than communion).

**Reception (per Stocking).** William James (Dec. 1900, Rome) found it "amusing" that Frazer, "after Tylor... the greatest authority... on the religious ideas and superstitions of primitive peoples," dismissed psychical research and thought trance phenomena "feigned." Biographer Robert Ackerman opened his study "Frazer is an embarrassment," reflecting later British social anthropologists' view of Frazer as the "very model of an 'armchair anthropologist.'" [[lang-andrew|Andrew Lang]] was a sustained critic ("apostate" to Tylor), calling *The Golden Bough* "the most inconceivably silly book of recent times" and dubbing Frazer's approach the "vegetable school" of mythology. Jane Harrison (Cambridge Ritualist) recalled *The Golden Bough* as the text that converted classical scholars to anthropological interpretation of Greek religion, though Stocking notes recent scholarship suggests her recollection "overstated an influence that was in reality more Smithian than Frazerian" [CONTRADICTION-flag: retrospective account vs. later reassessment, both stated]. [[marett-r-r|Marett]] (Frazer Lecture review) noted "official anthropology was at first inclined to stand aloof" from *The Golden Bough* as "literary rather than truly scientific," though the 2nd edition was "backed by anthropologists almost to a man." C. G. Seligman went to the Nile in 1910 after reading Frazer's *Early History of the Kingship* and found the Shilluk practiced ceremonial regicide of an aging/ailing divine king, which fifteen years later fed Isaac Schapera's LSE doctoral dissertation on divine kingship. Tylor and Frazer had a documented rift in 1898 over totemism theory and evidentiary standards — relations were "for some time very cool." Frazer proposed "mental anthropology" (1921) as an alternative to "physical anthropology," reflecting Durkheimian influence mediated by Marett.

## Note — Preface to Malinowski *Argonauts* (1922)

Frazer wrote the preface to [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts of the Western Pacific]]*, framing the Kula as demolishing "Primitive Economic Man" and stressing magic's role in industrial enterprise — an endorsement of Malinowski's discovery from the leading armchair anthropologist of the previous generation.
