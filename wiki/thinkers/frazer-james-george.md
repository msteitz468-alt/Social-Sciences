# James George Frazer

## Overview

Sir James George Frazer (1854–1941) was the central British figure of late Victorian and Edwardian **armchair comparative anthropology** and comparative religion. A classicist by training (Trinity College, Cambridge), he extended [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s intellectualist program into a vast comparative synthesis of magic, kingship, and vegetation ritual, culminating in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (1890; third edition 12 vols. 1911–15; author's abridgement 1922). He never conducted ethnographic fieldwork; his evidence base was travellers', missionaries', classicists', and folklorists' reports. Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/frazer-james-george|Thinkers Hub]].

## Contributions

- **[[sympathetic-magic]]** — analysis of magic into the Law of Similarity (homoeopathic/imitative) and the Law of Contact/Contagion (contagious), both as misapplications of the association of ideas; magic as "false science" and "abortive art."
- **Magic → religion → science** — the evolutionary sequence of human attempts to control the world (see [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]).
- **[[divine-king|The killing of the divine king]]** — the Nemi priesthood as type-case of sacred kings who must be slain when their strength fails, to transfer the embodied divine/vegetative life.
- **[[dying-and-rising-god]]** and **[[vegetation-spirit]]** — Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus, Demeter, and European corn-spirits as personifications of the yearly death and revival of plant life (building on W. Mannhardt).
- **[[taboo]]**, **[[scapegoat]]**, **[[external-soul]]**, **[[sacred-marriage]]** — comparative catalogues that shaped twentieth-century ritual theory, literature, and (later) critique.

## Intellectual Genealogy

trained_under (intellectual debt): [[tylor-edward-burnett]] — Frazer regarded himself as working in Tylor's evolutionist and intellectualist line ([[animism]], [[survivals]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], [[comparative-method]]). Key influence from Wilhelm Mannhardt on vegetation spirits. Contemporaries/rivals in Victorian evolutionism: [[spencer-herbert]], [[morgan-lewis-henry]], Lubbock, McLennan. Later fieldwork anthropology (Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown) and Boasian particularism reacted against his method; see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Twentieth-century anthropology rejected Frazer's unilinear evolutionism, his decontextualized comparison, his "If I were a horse" intellectualism, and his colonial-era ranking language. Functionalists denied [[survivals]] and conjectural history; Evans-Pritchard and others attacked the rationalist reconstruction of "primitive philosophy." Literary modernism (Eliot, Yeats, etc.) absorbed his imagery even as anthropology discarded his theories. Stocking and others reappraise him as a central institution of comparative religion and as a case of how evidence-class and production context shape theory.

George Stocking (*[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Victorian Anthropology]]*, 1987) notes that *The Golden Bough*, first published at the end of the 1880s, carried a "relatively pristine Tylorian" intellectualist viewpoint on magic and religion well into the twentieth century — long after the broader anti-evolutionary reaction had begun in British anthropology. Frazer's engagement with [[totemism]] began when his mentor William Robertson Smith asked him to write the totemism article for the ninth edition of the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*, building on [[mclennan-john-ferguson|John Ferguson McLennan]]'s original 1869 formulation; Frazer's subsequent "theories" of totemism became a focus of major anthropological debate in the early 1900s.

## Note — Preface to Malinowski *Argonauts* (1922)

Frazer wrote the preface to [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts of the Western Pacific]]*, framing the Kula as demolishing "Primitive Economic Man" and stressing magic's role in industrial enterprise — an endorsement of Malinowski's discovery from the leading armchair anthropologist of the previous generation.
