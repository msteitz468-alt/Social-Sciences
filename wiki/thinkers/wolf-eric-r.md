# Eric R. Wolf (1923–1999)

Austrian-born American anthropologist; central figure in mid-century peasant studies and the charter voice of late-twentieth-century [[anthropological-political-economy|anthropological political economy]]. Career arc from comparative peasantry (*[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]*, 1966; *Peasant Wars*, 1969) to global historical synthesis (*[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]*, 1982).

Deep biography: [[hubs/thinkers/wolf-eric-r]].

## Overview

Wolf relocated anthropological subjects inside historically evolving systems of power over [[social-labor|social labor]]. *Peasants* defined peasantry as surplus transfer under domain; *EPWH* generalized the program: the world is a totality of interconnected processes; "nation," "society," and "culture" are false models when reified as billiard balls; peoples studied as [[people-without-history]] were already enmeshed in empire, trade, and later capitalism. Analytical engine: three [[mode-of-production|modes of production]] (capitalist, [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]]) as research guides, not universal laws (1997 preface).

## Contributions

### From *Peasants* (1966)

- **[[peasant|Peasant]] as structural type** — character of involvement with a surplus-extracting center.
- **Fund analysis** — [[replacement-fund]], [[ceremonial-fund]], [[fund-of-rent]]; [[peasant-dilemma]].
- **[[types-of-domain]]**, **[[peasant-ecotype]]**, **[[closed-corporate-peasant-community]]**, **[[peasant-coalition]]**, **[[peasant-movements]]**.

### From *Europe and the People Without History* (1982)

- **Interconnection thesis** — manifold of processes; critique of bounded culture/society units and Cold War modernization triads.
- **[[people-without-history]]** — ironic title challenging Eurocentric historiography and the ethnographic present.
- **Three-mode typology** — capitalist (wealth buys labor-power; capitalism-in-production, not mercantile wealth); tributary (political extraction while producers keep means); kin-ordered (labor via kinship).
- **Mode vs market** — capitalist mode late 18th c.; world market can articulate multiple modes ([[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]; vs Frank/Wallerstein).
- **Commodity ethnohistories** — silver, [[north-american-fur-trade|fur]], [[atlantic-slave-trade|slaves]], cotton, sugar, tea/opium, etc., as mechanisms remaking cores and "peripheries."
- **Culture as process** — fluid construction/dismantling of cultural materials under economic/political force; naming as power; hegemony as redundancy (Afterword).
- **[[historical-political-economy]]** as method for anthropology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Peasant phase:** Foundations series ([[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] ed.); [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]], Weber, Chayanov, Hobsbawm, Redfield/Foster, Fried.
- **EPWH phase:** Marxian tools (mode, social labor, mercantile vs industrial capital, long waves) as "intellectual tools, not ultimate verities"; critical engagement with [[wallerstein-immanuel|Wallerstein]], Frank, Mandel, Amin; Service/Fried on secondary tribes/chiefdoms; Lowie on matter-of-fact usage vs secondary interpretation; Lesser on weblike connections.
- **Students:** [[chagnon-napoleon]] among others (`trained_under` still thin from primary sources alone).
- **Life partner / critic:** Sydel Silverman (dedication of *EPWH*).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- *Peasants*: typological risk; development-era language; second-hand cases.
- *EPWH*: charges of Systems Marxism and thin culture/agency (Wolf 1997 reply: modes as suppositions; culture relational and power-laden); regional specialists revise chapter detail; concurrent Writing Culture moment offered different restorations of historicity (voice/textuality vs political economy).
- *Peasant Wars* and full biographical literature not yet primary-ingested.

## Historiography

Densified from *Peasants* 1966 and *EPWH* 1982/1997/2010 (Eriksen foreword). Secondary biographies and archives still thin in the collection. Reception tracked in Eriksen 2010 and Wolf's own 1997 preface.
