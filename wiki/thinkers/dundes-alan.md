# Alan Dundes

## Overview

American folklorist (1934–2005); professor of anthropology and folklore at UC Berkeley (Ph.D. in folklore, Indiana University; earlier taught at the University of Kansas). Widely credited as a leading figure in the revitalization of folklore as an academic discipline in the United States, known for structural definitional analysis of folkloristic genres (folktales, superstitions, riddles) and for reading folkloristic features into contemporary popular culture. With Jerry W. Leach and Bora Özkök, he co-authored "The Strategy of Turkish Boys' Verbal Dueling Rhymes" in *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972). Özkök, a native Turk and graduate student at Berkeley, was himself a former participant in the dueling tradition and served as the paper's primary informant; the authors report spot-checking his material against more than twenty other Turkish students from several regions, plus corroboration from Professor Ilhan Basgöz (Indiana University).

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Dundes, Leach & Özkök, 1972; analytic content only — verbal content of the dueling rhymes themselves is graphic and is treated here descriptively rather than quoted, per the wiki's sensitive-content handling protocol.)*

- Analyzed a Turkish boys' peer-culture verbal-dueling genre (roughly ages 8–14) as a rhymed challenge-and-obligatory-reply exchange with two joint requirements: a content rule (forcing the opponent into a passive role in a homosexual encounter, directly or by threatening his female relatives) and a form rule (the retort must end-rhyme with the preceding insult); failure to retort in rhyme, or at all, was read within the tradition as an admission of the passive/feminine role.
- Identified a traditional Nasreddin Hodja folktale functioning as **metafolklore** — a story about the dueling-rhyme tradition itself — read by the authors as evidence that the content requirement takes interpretive priority over the rhyme-form requirement when the two conflict.
- Reported that the tradition was essentially undocumented in prior Turkish scholarly literature, attributing the gap to the genre's obscenity and an associated taboo restricting it from both women's and scholars' purview.
- Advanced an explicitly flagged interpretive argument (their own words: entering "the ever-treacherous area of interpretation") that the tradition dramatizes a culturally specific "dependent-aggressive" conflict in Turkish male development, linked to the status transition from the women's world to the segregated adult male world and to circumcision experienced as an aggressive/castrating event (drawing on Stirling, Öztürk, Cansever, and comparisons to Miner and De Vos on Algerian male psychology and Bettelheim on initiation rites generally). This interpretive argument is theory, not wiki-voice finding, and should never be asserted as fact.
- Offered a supplementary etymological argument that the Turkish word for "friend" (*arkadaş*, literally "back-participant") and related idioms evidence a long-standing active/passive schema in Turkish cultural psychology.
- The volume's editors (Gumperz and Hymes) offered an explicit alternative, more sociolinguistic/structural-functional reading in their headnote: that the duel tests performers' ability to manipulate the community's deeply ingrained taboos within the constraints of a speech event, rather than expressing individual psychological motivation — a live interpretive disagreement between editors and chapter authors over the same data.
- The chapter is presented as an instance of "ethnography of speaking folklore" (citing Arewa & Dundes 1964), and is compared in the editorial framing to African-American "playing the dozens" (Abrahams, Labov) and Mexican *albures* (Ingham) as cross-culturally recurring verbal-dueling genres.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Associated with the folklore-as-discipline revitalization movement in the U.S.; his structural approach to genre definition parallels the ethnoscience/componential method used elsewhere in the same volume (Frake, ch. 3). The Turkish dueling chapter is explicitly framed by the editors alongside Labov's work on African-American ritual insult ("the dozens") as a comparative instance of verbal dueling as genre.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The volume's own editors record a direct interpretive counter-reading to the chapter's psychoanalytic-developmental thesis (see above) — an in-print instance of adjudicable scholarly disagreement over the same ethnographic data, recorded here as attributed positions rather than resolved in wiki voice.
