# Peter M. Blau

## Overview

Peter Michael Blau (1918–2002) was an Austrian-born American sociologist whose work spans three programs: the **empirical study of bureaucracy and informal organization** (*The Dynamics of Bureaucracy*, 1955); **social-exchange theory** (*[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power in Social Life]]*, 1964), the source treated here; and, later, a **quantitative macrostructural theory** of differentiation, heterogeneity, and inequality (*Inequality and Heterogeneity*, 1977; *Crosscutting Social Circles*, 1984). He also co-authored, with Otis Dudley Duncan, *The American Occupational Structure* (1967), the founding statement of the **status-attainment** research tradition. Trained at Columbia under [[unknown|Robert K. Merton]], he taught at Cornell, the University of Chicago (junior faculty in the 1950s), and Columbia.

*Exchange and Power in Social Life* is Blau's attempt to derive complex social structure from the elementary process of [[social-exchange|social exchange]], and to link the microsociology of Simmel and Goffman to the macrosociology of Weber and Parsons.

## Contributions

- **An emergentist social-exchange theory.** Blau treats [[social-exchange|social exchange]] — reciprocal transactions of rewards under [[reciprocal-obligation|unspecified obligations]] — as "the prototype of a social phenomenon," "an elemental process or particle of social life." Against Homans, he insists exchange "cannot be reduced to or derived from psychological principles that govern the motives of individuals": "In sociology, not only the explicandum… but also the explicans… is social." Social structures "have emergent properties that cannot be understood on the basis of the properties of the subunits."
- **The derivation of macrostructure from exchange.** From social attraction Blau derives, in sequence, [[social-exchange]], the [[differentiation-of-power|differentiation of power]] (through [[imbalance-of-exchange|imbalanced exchange]] and [[unilateral-dependence]]), [[legitimation]] and organization, and [[opposition]] — the "four facets of social structure" (integration, differentiation, organization, opposition), extended across complex structures by [[mediating-values|mediating values]] and [[institutionalization]].
- **A dialectical account of change.** Blau closes not with Parsonsian equilibrium but with [[dialectic-of-social-change|dialectical forces]]: "the very processes that restore social equilibrium are usually disequilibrating forces in other respects," so structural change is intermittent and dialectical.
- **Later macrostructural theory** (outside this source). Blau reconceived social structure as a multidimensional space of positions defined by **heterogeneity** and **inequality**, deriving testable theorems on intergroup relations (e.g., group size and intermarriage across 125 US metropolitan areas), operationalizing Simmel's [[cross-cutting-conflicts|crosscutting social circles]].
- **Status attainment** (with [[duncan-otis-dudley|O. D. Duncan]]). *[[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-1967|The American Occupational Structure]]* (1967) founded the quantitative [[status-attainment]] program via the OCG path model of origins → education → first job → occupational status; study: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Teachers / formative influences:** [[unknown|Robert K. Merton]] at Columbia (whose lectures introduced Blau to [[weber-max|Weber]]'s theory of bureaucracy and inspired his dissertation study); [[unknown|Conrad Arensberg]]'s course on industrial sociology (the method Blau applied to government officials). Blau also drew heavily on [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] (dyad/triad, super- and subordination, exchange, crosscutting circles) and on economic theory (marginal utility).
- **Central interlocutor:** [[homans-george-c|George C. Homans]]. The relationship is documented and reciprocal (see Reflexivity below): Homans's 1958 "Social Behavior as Exchange" built on Blau's consultation data; Homans's *Social Behavior* (1961) then rekindled Blau's interest; Blau's Preface names it the pervasive influence on the book's first half "despite some fundamental differences in approach." Their disagreement over reductionism is a standing methodological divide.
- **Bracketing figures:** [[weber-max|Weber]] and [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] (the macrosociology Blau reaches toward); [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (the microsociology of impression management Blau incorporates; Goffman read the manuscript). [[marx-karl|Marx]] appears as the model of "structural" analysis Blau later invokes (positions and relations, not myths or rules).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The reductionism dispute** with Homans is the central methodological controversy (see [[social-exchange-theory]] and the debate page). Later exchange theorists (Emerson's network exchange) reframed the field around exchange relations and power-dependence, partly bypassing the micro/macro framing Blau used.
- **Blau's own reappraisal (1986).** In the Transaction introduction he retracts the book's foundational premise — that macrosociology could be *built on* microsociology — concluding that micro and macro "require different approaches and conceptual schemes" that "enrich each other" (citing Merton on the value of multiple theories) rather than one grounding the other. This is a rare case of an author formally revising the load-bearing assumption of a classic.
- The macro-to-micro transition in the book is widely judged its weakest joint — an assessment Blau shares ("I have been more successful in the microsociological analysis of exchange than in making the transition to a macrosociological theory").

## Historiography

The primary self-documentation is Blau's 1986 "Introduction to the Transaction Edition," which narrates the theory's origin in his federal-agency fieldwork, the Homans exchange, and his subsequent macrostructural turn. The Preface (1964) records the manuscript's readers (Homans, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]], Stinchcombe, Becker, Katz, and others) and its composition during a 1962–63 fellowship year at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences.
