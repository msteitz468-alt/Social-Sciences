# W. E. B. Du Bois

## Overview

William Edward Burghardt Du Bois (1868–1963) was an American sociologist, historian, and public intellectual whose *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903) fixed the vocabulary of race as a social and spiritual structure for both American sociology and African American letters. Writing from Atlanta University and as “bone of the bone and flesh of the flesh of them that live within the [[the-veil|Veil]],” he treated the “problem of the Twentieth Century” as the problem of [[the-color-line|the color-line]] — not only in the United States but as “the relation of the darker to the lighter races of men in Asia and Africa, in America and the islands of the sea.”

This page is the network summary from *Souls*, the *[[du-bois-essays|Essays]]* collection, and a web-derived ingest of *[[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|The Philadelphia Negro]]* (1899) — his founding urban-sociological study. ## Contributions

- **[[double-consciousness]]** — the sense of always looking at oneself through the eyes of others; “twoness” as American and Negro (*Strivings of the Negro People*, 1897; *Souls* ch. I).
- **[[the-veil]]** — the barrier that shuts Black Americans out from the “other world” while granting “second-sight”; origin vignette of the New England visiting-card refusal.
- **[[the-color-line]]** — the organizing problem of the century; physical, economic, political, and spiritual forms (*Souls*; extended in Economic Revolution / Black North as national, not Southern-only).
- **[[talented-tenth]]** — higher training of the exceptional as indispensable to mass uplift; full program essay 1903 (culture top-down; teachers first; occupation and enrollment tables; Washington/Tuskegee staffing paradox).
- **[[conservation-of-races]]** — 1897 American Negro Academy race ideal: race as historical-spiritual group; duty to conserve identity and deliver the Negro “message”; Academy creed and race organizations.
- **[[sorrow-songs]]** — Negro folk-song as “the sole American music” and “greatest gift of the Negro people” (*Souls* ch. XIV; prefigured in *Strivings*).
- **Sociology of the [[black-church]]** — Negro church as social centre (*Souls* ch. X); ethics of slavery/emancipation as essence of Southern religion (*Religion in the South*, c. 1906).
- **Leadership critique** — opposition to political and educational half-truths of Booker T. Washington’s program while accepting thrift, patience, and industrial training for the masses (→ [[washington-du-bois-debate]]).
- **Empirical urban sociology** — *[[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|The Philadelphia Negro]]* (1899, fieldwork 1896–97): the first empirical, statistically grounded community study in American sociology, coining the [[submerged-tenth|submerged tenth]] and arguing racialized poverty structurally rather than culturally (→ [[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]]).
- **Other empirical programs** — Black Belt monography (*Souls* ch. VII–VIII); [[black-urban-north|Black North]] urban social observation (NY/Philadelphia comparisons, early 1900s); Atlanta University Conference graduate/common-school studies; Southern economic history of peonage, group economy, disfranchisement, and the Atlanta Massacre (c. 1906 lecture).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Du Bois was born in Great Barrington, Massachusetts; the *Souls*/*Strivings* childhood vignette places his first revelation of the Veil in a New England schoolhouse on the Housatonic. He studied at Fisk University (where he taught rural Tennessee schools in summer — “A Negro Schoolmaster in the New South”), Harvard (B.A., M.A., Ph.D. 1895 — first African American Harvard doctorate), and the University of Berlin under historical economists of the German Historical School (Schmoller, Wagner). He taught at Wilberforce and Atlanta University, organized the Atlanta University Conferences on the Negro problem, delivered the American Negro Academy paper on conservation of races (1897), and later co-founded the NAACP and edited *The Crisis*. Within *Souls*, Alexander Crummell is presented as a moral-vanguard type who refused Hate, Despair, and Doubt; Booker T. Washington is the contemporaneous mass leader with whom Du Bois both cooperates and contends.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Elitism of the Talented Tenth.** The higher-education program has been read as class-hierarchical relative to mass industrial education; Du Bois’s own later politics moved left of the 1903 formulation — to be thickened from later works.
- **Gender.** Classical-era essays center male leadership types; women’s labor and family structure appear mainly as social problem (sharecropping households, “red stain of bastardy,” widow percentages in Black North) rather than as equal analytical subjects — attributed, date-stamped.
- **Genre hybridity.** Essay, monography, polemic, and literary forms mix; critics debate science vs. literature — both are load-bearing for influence.
- **Race concept (1897).** *Conservation of Races* redefines race historically/spiritually while retaining race as real historical unit — contested in later race theory.
- **Representation.** Observational claims rest on travel, local knowledge, conference returns, and census-informed description — date-stamp; not timeless ethnography. *Black North* is truncated in the ingested collection before homes/public institutions.
