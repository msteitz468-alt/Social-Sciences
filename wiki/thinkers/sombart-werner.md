## Overview

Werner Sombart (1863–1941) was a German economist and theorist of the historical
school whose life work, on [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]]'s reading in
*[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure of Social Action]]* (1937, Ch.
XIII), was the study of modern capitalism as a single, historically unique economic
system — documented mainly in *Der moderne Kapitalismus* (2nd ed.), with *Der
Bourgeois* (trans. *The Quintessence of Capitalism*) and *Der proletarische
Sozialismus* as companion works. This page reflects Sombart only as reconstructed by
Parsons; it is not drawn from Sombart's own texts. All substantive content below is
attributed to Parsons's reading unless marked otherwise.

## Contributions

On Parsons's reading, Sombart treated the economic system not as a directly-described
historical phenomenon but as an "[[ideal-type|ideal type]]" used to understand the
concrete historical process, while holding — against positivist economics — that no
general economic theory applies across all times and places, only a plurality of
discrete, historically bounded economic systems. Parsons reports that Sombart analyzed
any economic system into three aspects: organization, *Geist* (spirit), and technique,
and that for Sombart the Geist "has created the form of organization for itself" —
giving causal priority to spirit over organization, contra [[marx-karl|Marx]].
Sombart's Geist of capitalism, on this account, had two aspects: the "spirit of
enterprise" (individuality, initiative, energy, struggle for power, linked to the
Renaissance and credited with creating the modern state, science, and exploration) and
the "bourgeois spirit." Parsons further reports that Sombart agreed with Marx that the
capitalist system is compulsive — that competitiveness and acquisitiveness stem from
situational conditions rather than individual motives, and that this compulsiveness is
historically specific, absent under the handicraft system — but held that the
compulsion only fully applies once the Geist has become "objectified" or
"institutionalized," a genetic account Sombart derived from spirit rather than from
prior material forms as Marx did. Sombart extended this discontinuity thesis to
technology itself: capitalist technique is rational and scientific, while precapitalist
(handicraft) technique was traditional and empirical — a claim Parsons flags as radical
since technology is normally treated as the stronghold of positivistic linear
evolutionism. Parsons also reports that class struggle is far less prominent in
Sombart than in Marx, not because Sombart denies its organizational basis but because
he frames capitalism's development as the "progressive objectification of the Geist"
rather than as the emergence of material contradictions; unlike Marx, Sombart's
orientation is not forward-looking toward capitalism's successors.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Parsons situates Sombart within German historicist-idealist methodology, in direct
dialogue with Marx: Sombart's theory, on Parsons's assessment, takes its concrete
descriptive content from Marx but "goes far beyond him" in eliminating everything but
the historical, unique character of economic systems — relocating the interpretation
of capitalism's genesis from Marx's materialism back into the mainstream of German
historicist-idealist method. Parsons frames a "Marx-Sombart conflict" over the causal
analysis of capitalism: Marx locating the driving factor in the material/utilitarian
conditions of production, Sombart locating it in an autonomous spirit (Geist) that
precedes and creates economic organization. Parsons states that his own subsequent
internal critique of the utilitarian tradition tends to favor Sombart's criticism of
Marx on this specific point. Parsons frames [[weber-max|Max Weber]] (treated in the
following chapters) as a thinker who was steeped both in the idealistic tradition and
in the particular empirical problems of Marx and Sombart, and who "transcended" the
Marx-Sombart dilemma.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Parsons's overall assessment is that Sombart eliminates everything from Marx's
framework except historical uniqueness, and that Parsons himself partly sides with
Sombart's critique of Marx regarding the causal role of "spirit" in capitalism's
genesis. This Marx-Sombart dispute over the causal genesis of capitalism is recorded
as context on the debate page [[parsons-convergence-thesis-debate]], where it appears
alongside Parsons's broader convergence-thesis argument.

## Historiography

Parsons's engagement with Sombart predates *The Structure of Social Action*: Parsons
cites his own earlier article, "Recent German Literature on Capitalism, I, Werner
Sombart," *Journal of Political Economy* (December 1928), as a prior treatment feeding
the 1937 chapter. The present page is built entirely from Parsons's 1937
reconstruction; no independent Sombart scholarship has yet been ingested into this
wiki.
