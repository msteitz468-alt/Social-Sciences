# Eric H. Cline

## Overview

Eric H. Cline is an American archaeologist and ancient historian (George Washington University) known for synthesizing Eastern Mediterranean and Aegean Late Bronze Age evidence for a general readership and for framing the end of the Bronze Age as a multi-causal, systems-level collapse. Principal work ingested here: *[[cline-1177-bc-2014|1177 B.C.: The Year Civilization Collapsed]]* (Princeton, 2014). Study page: [[cline-1177-bc-study]].

## Contributions

- Narrative reconstruction of the LBA "globalized" international system (Egypt, Hittites, Mitanni, Assyria, Babylon, Canaanite city-states, Cyprus, Minoans/Mycenaeans) from textual archives (Amarna, Hattusa, Ugarit) and archaeology.
- Popularization of the **1177 BCE** benchmark (Ramses III year 8 / Sea Peoples) as scholarly shorthand for a multi-decade process (~1225–1130 BCE).
- Multi-causal **"perfect storm"** model for [[late-bronze-age-collapse]]: earthquakes, drought/famine, migrations/invasions (Sea Peoples and others), internal revolts, cut trade networks — not monocausal Sea Peoples invasion.
- Engagement with complexity/systems metaphors and modern analogies (interdependence, strategic tin ≈ oil) — attributed rhetorical framing, not primary evidence.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Cline draws on and synthesizes a wide secondary literature (excavators of Ugarit, Hattusa, Mycenaean palaces; Egyptologists; archaeoseismologists; climate proxy studies). Explicitly positions against single-cause collapse narratives and against treating the Sea Peoples as a fully known people with a single origin. Relates comparatively to broader collapse literature ([[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]], [[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]]/Tainter tradition) while remaining case-specific to the LBA Eastern Mediterranean.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Accessible series format and modern analogies risk over-smoothing regional variation and chronological uncertainty.
- Sea Peoples origins remain unresolved; Cline reports multiple hypotheses without claiming resolution.
- Climate and earthquake "storm" arguments depend on dating precision and correlation-to-causation discipline (see [[late-bronze-age-collapse-causes-debate]]).

## Historiography

Reputation primarily as a synthesizer and public intellectual of Bronze Age archaeology rather than as the originator of a named school. *1177 B.C.* became a reference point in both scholarly and popular discussions of systemic collapse after 2014.
