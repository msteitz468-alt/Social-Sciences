# A. V. Chayanov (1888–1937)

Aleksandr Vasilyevich Chayanov, Russian **agricultural economist** and theorist of the peasant family farm. His analysis of prerevolutionary Russian peasant agriculture supplied the mechanism that [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] later imported into economic anthropology as **[[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov's rule]]** and the theoretical spine of the **[[domestic-mode-of-production]]**. This page began as a scaffold for the ingest of *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972); Chayanov's own texts are not yet primary-ingested, so claims below are attributed as Sahlins reports and uses them.

## Overview

*(Attributed — Chayanov via Sahlins.)* Chayanov argued that the **family-labor farm** operates on a logic different from the capitalist firm: lacking wage labor, it balances the drudgery of work against the satisfaction of household consumption needs, producing to a **subjectively acceptable livelihood** rather than to maximize profit. Because the farm has no category of wages or profit, its members engage in **"self-exploitation"** of family labor, working harder or less hard according to the household's composition. From his co-workers' district statistics he derived the empirical regularity Sahlins named **Chayanov's rule**.

## Contributions

- **Chayanov's rule (the labor–consumer balance).** *(Attributed.)* In a system of domestic production for use, the **intensity of labor per worker varies with the household's consumer/worker (dependency) ratio**: the more consumers a household must support per able worker, the harder each worker labors; households with many workers relative to consumers work less intensively. Sahlins quotes Chayanov (1966): "farm families possess considerable stocks of unused time." See [[domestic-mode-of-production]].
- **The consumer/worker (dependency) ratio** as the key variable of household economy, and the **developmental cycle** of the household (its ratio shifting as children are born and mature) as the source of variation in production. Links to [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]].
- **Self-exploitation of family labor** — the family farm intensifies its own labor without a wage relation, a concept later taken up in peasant studies and by substantivist economic anthropology.
- **Empirical basis:** Chayanov and co-workers' statistics on prerevolutionary Russian peasant households (e.g. the Volokolamsk study of 25 farm families, where working-days per worker per year ranged roughly threefold and rose with the dependency ratio) — reworked by Sahlins into the "true" vs "real" Chayanov-slope method.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Chayanov led the "Organization and Production School" of Russian agrarian economics. His reception in Anglophone social science was mediated by the 1966 English translation (*The Theory of Peasant Economy*, eds. Thorner, Kerblay, Smith) and, in anthropology, decisively by [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], who relocated Chayanov's rule inside a Marxian frame as the law of motion of the [[domestic-mode-of-production]]. ## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* Sahlins himself notes the rule holds "in general" but not "in detail": real communities deviate from the pure Chayanov slope in patterned ways produced by their political and kinship structure (the [[big-man]] system, chiefship). The rule presupposes a household oriented to livelihood rather than accumulation, and so applies within — not against — the substantivist premise. Critics of peasant-economy theory have questioned how far a model built on Russian family farms generalizes to tribal horticulture; Sahlins treats the fit as approximate and structural, not exact.

## Historiography

Chayanov was arrested in the Stalinist repression of agrarian specialists and executed in 1937; his work was suppressed in the USSR and rehabilitated only decades later. In the West his rediscovery drove the "peasant studies" field of the 1960s–70s (Thorner, Shanin) and, through Sahlins, a lasting place in economic anthropology.
