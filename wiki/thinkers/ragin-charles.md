# Charles C. Ragin

## Overview

Charles C. Ragin is an American comparative sociologist and methodologist, best known as the originator of **[[qualitative-comparative-analysis|Qualitative Comparative Analysis (QCA)]]**, the set-theoretic, Boolean method for systematically comparing an intermediate number of cases. Ingested through one source: his foundational statement *[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method: Moving Beyond Qualitative and Quantitative Strategies]]* (1987; new edition 2014). At the time of writing he was in the Department of Sociology at **Northwestern University**, having moved there from **Indiana University** (attributed, from the book's acknowledgments).

## Contributions

- **[[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]].** Ragin (attributed) formalized the comparison of cases as **configurations** of conditions using Boolean algebra and truth-table minimization — a "synthetic strategy" meant to "bridge and transcend" the qualitative–quantitative divide. He later extended it to fuzzy sets (*Fuzzy-Set Social Science*, 2000; *Redesigning Social Inquiry*, 2008) and built the fsQCA software; the underlying QCA package was implemented as Drass and Ragin (1986).
- **[[multiple-conjunctural-causation|Multiple conjunctural causation]].** His signature causal model: outcomes arise from combinations of conditions, several different combinations can produce the same outcome (equifinality), and a condition's effect depends on context.
- **[[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented|The case-oriented/variable-oriented distinction]].** His reframing of the qualitative/quantitative split as a metatheoretical divide between two research strategies, developed at length in *The Comparative Method*.
- **Legibility of [[necessary-and-sufficient-conditions|necessity and sufficiency]].** He showed that Boolean results, unlike statistical ones, can be read directly in terms of necessary and sufficient conditions.

## Intellectual Genealogy

The ingested source documents the formation of Ragin's method but not a full academic pedigree (`trained_under`/`trained` left `[[unknown]]` pending a biographical source). By his own account (attributed): early graduate reading of [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore]]'s *Social Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (1966) was "highly influential" for its combinatorial, non-independent-variable argumentation; he collaborated with Daniel Chirot on a study of the 1907 Romanian peasant revolt (Chirot and Ragin 1975); and, after a "five-year journey" of mounting frustration with the fragility of statistical interaction-effect analysis in small cross-national datasets, he returned to books on Boolean algebra, set theory, and switching circuits he had read as an adolescent — giving his first lectures on the emerging method after moving to the more qualitatively-open Northwestern. His work sits downstream of J.S. Mill's canons of comparative inquiry (method of agreement and indirect method of difference) and of the Weberian "historically interpretive" tradition, both of which he reworks. He positions himself against Neil Smelser's (1976) treatment of the comparative method as an inferior variant of statistical method.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

QCA became "the subject of intense methodological debate" (Ragin's own phrasing, 2014). Critics — notably Stanley Lieberson (2004) and Jason Seawright (2004, 2005) — have questioned the robustness of Boolean solutions to case selection and measurement error and the status of simplifying assumptions about non-existent combinations (logical remainders); Ragin and Rihoux replied (2004). Against this, Ragin reported (2014) that the book had been cited nearly 4,000 times and QCA used in 750+ published studies across 220+ journals, and cited John Gerring's description of QCA as "one of the few genuine methodological innovations of the past several decades."
