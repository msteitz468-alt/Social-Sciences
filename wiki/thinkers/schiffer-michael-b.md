# Michael B. Schiffer (b. 1947)

American archaeologist; principal architect of **[[behavioral-archaeology|behavioral archaeology]]** and of the book-length synthesis of **[[formation-processes|formation processes]]** (*[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]*, 1987). *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|Behavioral Archaeology: First Principles]]* (1995) reprints core 1972–1987 papers with a new personal history and principles inventory.

## Overview

Schiffer’s program holds that the cultural past is knowable only when the nature of archaeological evidence — including how people and nature transform materials — is understood (attributed, Schiffer 1987 preface; 1995 Preface). He distinguishes [[systemic-context|systemic]] from [[archaeological-context|archaeological]] context, classifies cultural formation processes (reuse, deposition, reclamation, disturbance), develops refuse typologies and discard equations, and treats the archaeologist’s own procedures as formation processes. In the 1995 charter he frames behavioral archaeology as a University of Arizona program of the early 1970s that redefines archaeology as describing and explaining relationships between human behavior and material culture in all times and places (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Preface).

## Contributions

- **Transformation theory / behavioral archaeology** — with [[reid-j-jefferson|J. Jefferson Reid]], [[rathje-william|William L. Rathje]], and others; four-strategies matrix (Reid’s classroom insight, 1972; Reid, Schiffer, and Rathje 1975); nomothetic c-transforms and n-transforms; three tenets: formal/spatial/quantitative/relational transforms; creation of non-behavioral patterning; regularities as laws (attributed, Schiffer 1987 Ch. 1; 1995 Ch. 1, 6).
- **[[refuse-types]]** — primary, secondary, residual primary (McKellar Principle), de facto refuse, curate behavior interface with Binford (Schiffer 1972a; 1976; 1995 Ch. 2, 20).
- **[[old-wood-problem]]** — systematic antiquity bias in wood radiocarbon (1982/1986/1987 Ch. 11 Hohokam; 1995 Ch. 16).
- **Broken K reanalysis** — ceramic formation processes undermine direct social factor interpretations ([[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]; Schiffer 1987/1989; 1995 Ch. 21).
- **Identification checklist** (1983 AmAnt → 1987 Ch. 10; 1995 Ch. 18) — multi-trace deposit analysis; four dimensions of variability.
- **[[behavioral-chain]] analysis** (Schiffer 1975d; 1995 Ch. 5) — relating past room activities to present archaeological traces.
- Later people-and-things / performance-characteristics work with [[skibo-james|James M. Skibo]] (Schiffer and Skibo 1987; 1995 Ch. 22; Skibo and Schiffer 2008).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **UCLA (1965–69):** After switching from chemistry, ethnographer Philip Newman directed him to [[hill-james-n|James N. Hill]], who made Binford intelligible and treated student work as potentially publishable science. Courses with Sally R. Binford (physical anthropology) and [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]] (“Technology and Invention”; audited “Strategy of Archaeology”) supplied Hempelian philosophy of science, research design, multi-causal variability, and systems frameworks (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Schiffer later listed Binford among formative influences while distinguishing formal Arizona graduate training from UCLA coursework.
- **Vernon / Martin Southwest Expedition (1968–69):** Sally Binford steered him to Paul S. Martin’s NSF expedition at Vernon, Arizona (staff including Mark Leone and Fred Plog); Gurley site and Hay Hollow Valley work, Leone sherd-color challenge, empty pit-house floor, and Chris White’s erosion caution seeded early formation-process awareness (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).
- **Arizona graduate years (from 1969):** Entered expecting to be a New Archaeologist; instead grappled with processualism’s internal contradictions. The term “behavioral archaeology” first appears in a January 1971 Culbert term paper. Synthetic model of inference (drafts 1971; *Behavioral Archeology* 1976) sought to reconcile Binford’s and Raymond Thompson’s views by focusing on law-like principles underpinning inferences rather than pure deduction vs induction; Binford liked c-/n-transforms as “archaeological theory” but disliked being “put to bed” with Thompson (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Quiet break with Longacre–Hill social-organization method (1971–72) over ignored [[formation-processes]]. Dissertation defended 14 August 1973 (Thompson chair; caution against becoming “generation’s Walter Taylor”).
- **Arizona network:** Co-crafted the program with [[reid-j-jefferson|Reid]] and [[rathje-william|Rathje]] (four strategies; “coconspirators” fantasy with Reid during dissertation year). Reid later urged the 1987 formation-processes book (1982). Acknowledgments include Wilcox, Sullivan, Dean, Wilk, South, Shipman; [[skibo-james|Skibo]] among full-manuscript readers (1987 preface) and coauthor of technological-change / performance-matrix work (1995 Ch. 22).
- **Tucson ethnoarchaeology:** Reuse Project (with Theodore Downing and McCarthy, 1976–77 household sample; Schiffer, Downing, and McCarthy 1981; 1995 Ch. 10) studied recycling, secondary use, lateral cycling, and conservatory processes; Schiffer later abandoned scale-up partly to keep a separate identity from Rathje’s Projet du Garbage (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).
- **Dialogue/dispute with Binford** — Alpine Summit 1978 (Binford disowns him as reference); 1979 SAA charge that Schiffer “retarded” formation-process study; Binford 1981a [[pompeii-premise]] critique; Schiffer responses in *Working at Archaeology* review (1985b; 1995 Ch. 19), “Is There a Pompeii Premise?” (1985a; 1995 Ch. 20), and the 1987 synthesis (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Shared ground: systemic vs archaeological/static distinction; need for correlates + formation-process principles; ethnoarchaeology and experiment as law sources (attributed, Schiffer 1985b/1995 Ch. 19).
- **Reacted against** correlates-only New Archaeology social inference (Hill 1970 as exemplar) and the fossilized-society metaphor he attributed to early processual inference (attributed, Schiffer 1976 introduction / 1995 Ch. 1).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Binford rejected the “behavioral archaeology” label and attacked distortion-of-a-living-system framing (attributed secondary and via Schiffer’s report of Binford 1981a/1983). Critical fieldwork writers (Lucas et al.) place formation-process theory still inside representational models of the record. Vocabulary of refuse types and formation processes is now standard even where the program label is less used.

## Historiography

Primary ingests of *Formation Processes* (1987) and *Behavioral Archaeology: First Principles* (1995) densify this page beyond Johnson textbook reportage. The 1995 volume reprints the 1972–1987 “first principles” (Ch. 1 personal history and Ch. 23 principles list are new). Full standalone reading of *Behavioral Archeology* (1976 monograph as originally issued) and Skibo & Schiffer (2008) remain useful for program history beyond the reprinted chapters.
