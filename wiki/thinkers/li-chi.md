# Li Chi (Li Ji)

## Overview

Li Chi (李濟, 1896–1979) is often called the father of modern Chinese archaeology. Harvard-
trained, he directed the Academia Sinica excavations at [[anyang|Anyang (Yinxu)]], the
late Shang capital, from 1928 to 1937 — the first large scientific excavations conducted
by Chinese archaeologists. After 1949 he continued at Academia Sinica in Taiwan, where he
taught [[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] and supervised Chang's undergraduate thesis on the
Lungshan culture.

## Contributions

Li Chi's Anyang campaigns recovered the oracle-bone archives, royal tombs, and palace-
temple foundations that established the historical reality of the Shang Dynasty and
anchored the archaeology of early Chinese civilization. His work moved the study of
ancient China from antiquarian and epigraphic traditions toward stratigraphic,
anthropological archaeology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

As Chang's teacher, Li Chi is the senior link in the genealogy that carried Anyang-based
Shang archaeology into anglophone scholarship. He wrote the foreword to Chang's
[[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|*The Archaeology of Ancient China*]] (1962),
framing it as necessarily interpretive work done from published data because fieldwork on
the mainland was closed to scholars outside the PRC.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Li Chi worked under acute wartime and political constraint (the Sino-Japanese War, then
the evacuation of collections to Taiwan). The interruption of Chinese field archaeology in
1937 and the Cold War division of the discipline shaped what could and could not be
studied for a generation — a production-context fact rather than a scholarly failing.

## Chang 1980 densification (attributed)

[[chang-kwang-chih|Chang]] dedicated *[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*
(1980) to Li Chi and treats the IHP Anyang program as Door 4 of Shang knowledge. Chang
records the Institute's **dataism** (*shih-liao-hsüeh*) philosophy (Fu Ssu-nien): modern
historiography as procurement and classification of data with natural-science tools; planned
An-yang report organized by artifact categories; division of labor (Tung written records,
Li other artifacts, Woo human remains). Chang's critique — dataism is itself a theory —
does not diminish Li's excavation achievement; it reframes how the archive should be
synthesized. See [[anyang]], [[oracle-bone-inscription]].

### Liu & Chen (2012) densification

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]].)* Liu and Chen
record that Li Chi held a PhD in physical anthropology from Harvard and organized/launched
a series of Chinese archaeological research projects beginning in 1926, then directed the
[[anyang|Anyang]] excavations from 1928 through 1937 as part of the Institute of History
and Philology, Academia Sinica — the first state-sponsored archaeological project in
China. His Anyang associates (Tung Tso-pin, Liang Siyong, Kao Ch'ü-hsun, Shih Chang-ju,
Guo Baojun, Yin Da, and Xia Nai) became the first generation of Chinese archaeologists,
dominating the field for decades on both sides of the Taiwan Strait. Li Chi framed the
discovery of Longshan black pottery at Chengziya (Li, C. 1990 [orig. 1934]) as key to
identifying a homeland for part of Shang culture and to resolving "most problems in the
formative period of Chinese history." After the 1949 Communist takeover, Li Chi and
several colleagues moved to Taiwan while Xia Nai and Liang Siyong remained on the
mainland, splitting the Institute of History and Philology's archaeologists between the
two successor traditions.
