## Overview

Richard E. Leakey (b. 1944, Kenya) is a paleoanthropologist and son of [[leakey-louis]] and [[leakey-mary]]. He led the East Turkana Research Group's fieldwork at [[koobi-fora]], whose finds included skull KNM-ER 1470, and co-authored *Origins* (1977) with science journalist [[lewin-roger]].

## Contributions

At Koobi Fora, Richard Leakey's team recovered skull 1470 (found late 1972), used to argue for a "multiple-lineage" reading of early hominid evolution — that more than one hominid lineage coexisted rather than a single linear sequence. With Glynn Isaac, he is associated with early formulations of the [[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing hypothesis]]. In *Origins*, Leakey & Lewin argued (attributed voice) that human ancestors were "strongly cooperative creatures" and that sharing of food and labor, not meat-eating or aggression alone, drove the transition from ape to cultural animal. They explicitly rejected the "killer ape" thesis of [[ardrey-robert]] and [[lorenz-konrad]] (see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]) and argued that the biological machinery for language and cooperative intelligence evolved gradually rather than in a recent rapid "explosion."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Son of [[leakey-louis]] and [[leakey-mary]]; formed by growing up amid their Olduvai excavations rather than through a conventional academic apprenticeship. Co-author relationship with [[lewin-roger]] on *Origins*.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Origins* predates Donald Johanson's 1978 formal naming of *Australopithecus afarensis* and reflects mid-1970s uncertainty about hominid phylogeny later revised as more fossil evidence accumulated.
