# Elliot Liebow

## Overview

American anthropologist (1925–1994), author of *[[liebow-tallys-corner-1967|Tally's Corner]]* (1967), a charter work of urban [[participant-observation]] ethnography and an early structural rejoinder to [[culture-of-poverty]] explanations. Born and raised in Washington, D.C., to Jewish immigrants from Eastern Europe (mother Latvian, father Russian); his father's grocery stores in predominantly Black neighborhoods gave him childhood contact across the color line. Career at NIMH, where he became chief of the Center for the Study of Work and Mental Health; his last book, *Tell Them Who I Am* (1993), studied homeless women in Washington-area shelters.

## Contributions

- *Tally's Corner* ([[liebow-tallys-corner-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/liebow-tallys-corner-study]]): the situational reading of "present-time orientation," the father-affection paradox, the theory of manly flaws, and [[shadow-values]] — behavior as "direct response to the conditions of lower-class Negro life," not subcultural imperative.
- The Appendix, "A Field Experience in Retrospect," is a standard methods text on race, positionality, and rapport in fieldwork (the "chain-link fence" metaphor) — see [[participant-observation]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

Ph.D. in anthropology, Catholic University of America (advisor Gottfried Lang; readers Regina Flannery Herzfeld, Michael Kenny, Jasper Ingersoll). Fieldwork done under Hylan Lewis on the NIMH Child Rearing Study; Liebow credited Lewis's non-directive brief ("Go out there and make like an anthropologist"). Drew on Merton (self-fulfilling prophecy, post factum interpretation), Rodman (value stretch), Bott, Cloward and Ohlin, Jules Henry; aligned himself with Frazier, Myrdal, Clark, and Cloward on joblessness as "the central fact of lower-class Negro life." Rebutted Walter Miller's lower-class-culture thesis. Lemert (2003) places Stack's *All Our Kin* as "in many ways the feminist sequel" and hears an unacknowledged Du Bois resonance.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Liebow's own text carries the sharpest design critique (no hypotheses, accidental selection, streetcorner vantage bias). In the 2002 Wacquant–Anderson–Duneier exchange over urban ethnography of Black men, Lemert argued all sides neglected Liebow's both-decent-and-street precedent ([[wacquant-urban-ethnography-debate]]). [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]] (2003) treated the book as foundational for structural accounts of Black male joblessness and argued its conditions had worsened.

## Historiography

*Tally's Corner* sold over a million copies — Lemert: among "only a handful" of social-science books to do so — and was republished in 2003 (Rowman & Littlefield, Legacies of Social Thought) with the Lemert foreword and Wilson introduction that frame its reception history.
