# Brian Hayden

Canadian archaeologist associated with **aggrandizer** models of early inequality and competitive-feasting explanations of domestication and social differentiation. Known in the wiki primarily via secondary synthesis in [[bogucki-peter|Peter Bogucki]]'s *[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|The Origins of Human Society]]* (1999); primary Hayden monographs not yet ingested.

## Overview

Hayden's mid-1990s work (especially “Pathways to power,” in Price & Feinman eds., and related papers) supplies two terms Bogucki adopts as load-bearing: **[[transegalitarian]]** societies (neither egalitarian nor fully stratified) and **[[aggrandizer|aggrandizers]]** — ambitious individuals who compete for labor and loyalty through feasts and prestige when sharing norms relax in resource-rich settings.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via Bogucki 1999 unless primary works ingested.)*

- **Competitive feasting model of domestication:** in fluctuating environments, foragers share food and have little incentive to produce surplus; in richer, more stable settings, collective sharing relaxes and ambitious individuals compete via feasts, stimulating demand for high-productivity foods and thus cultivation (Hayden 1992/1995a as summarized by Bogucki).
- **Aggrandizers as key element** of transegalitarian dynamics in communities of roughly 50–100+ people (Hayden 1995b via Bogucki).
- Explicit rejection (as of Hayden 1995a, via Bogucki) of deteriorating climate or extreme population pressure as *convincing* sole causes of domestication — favors social models while noting their wider inferential gap.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Engages Service/Fried evolutionary typologies as background against which transegalitarian fills a middle gap.
- Close conceptual neighbors: Clark & Blake (1994) on transegalitarian Formative Mesoamerica; [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] / Marcus agency traditions; [[bogucki-peter]] household + drop-down residual-elite elaboration.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Feasting-only accounts of hereditary rank are cautioned against by Flannery & Marcus (if feasting alone sufficed, no achievement-based societies would remain for ethnography) — see [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]].
- Primary texts and ethnographic bases not yet primary-ingested here; treat all Hayden claims as **(as of Bogucki 1999 secondary)** until direct sources land.
