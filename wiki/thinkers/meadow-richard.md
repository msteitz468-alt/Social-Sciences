# Richard H. Meadow

## Overview

Richard H. Meadow is an American archaeologist and zooarchaeologist whose work on South Asian animal domestication and on the Harappa excavations is extensively mobilized in [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Jonathan Mark Kenoyer]]’s *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* (1998). As of that book, Meadow is co-director of the Harappa Archaeological Research Project (HARP) with Kenoyer, succeeding the program’s founding generation around [[dales-george-f|George F. Dales, Jr.]].

## Contributions

*(As attributed via Kenoyer 1998; Meadow’s own monographs not yet ingested as primary sources.)*

- **Local domestication of humped zebu.** Kenoyer cites Meadow’s argument that humped cattle (*Bos indicus*) were **locally domesticated** in the greater Indus area, based on **decreasing cattle body size** through Mehrgarh Periods I–II concurrent with increasing representation of cattle bones among faunal remains. This undercuts a simple Near Eastern diffusion model for all Indus-margin domesticates.
- **Mehrgarh faunal sequence.** Through Kenoyer’s synthesis of Meadow (with Jarrige, Costantini, and others): early Mehrgarh communities hunted widely (gazelle, deer, pig, wild sheep/goat/cattle, nilgai, water buffalo, onager; ivory suggesting elephant hunting/trapping) while only a small proportion of early bones are domestic goats; domestic sheep/goat increase later; domestic cereals include wheat and multiple barley varieties. Neolithic burials with animal sacrifice (including five young goats in two earliest burials ca. 6500 BCE) are among the documented ritual–animal associations.
- **Transport and later fauna.** Kenoyer cites Meadow on pack and draft animals: two-wheel ox carts on the plains; Bactrian camel and horse domesticated in Central Asian highlands by ca. 3000 BCE but **no evidence of their use** in the Indus plains during the urban Harappan Phase (their later plains integration is a Localization/post-urban theme in Kenoyer’s Ch. 9).
- **HARP co-direction.** Meadow & Kenoyer co-publications (e.g. 1993, 1997) underwrite Harappa stratigraphy, craft contexts, molded tablets with ritual scenes, and cemetery finds reported throughout Kenoyer 1998. Figure captions and notes attribute 1986–1990 Berkeley and 1993–1997 HARP operations across Mounds AB, E, F and intervening areas.
- **Craft and burial co-authorship.** Kenoyer 1998 cites Meadow & Kenoyer for Harappa craft-area finds and for cemetery ornament inventories that feed [[bioarchaeology-of-the-indus]] and [[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]] reconstructions (attributed).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Collaborators:** [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]] (HARP co-director); French mission circle around [[jarrige-jean-francois|Jean-François Jarrige]] at Mehrgarh/Nausharo; earlier citation networks include Dupree/Perkins on Afghan Palaeolithic fauna (via Kenoyer).
- **Successor to Dales’s Harappa design:** program continuity from [[dales-george-f]] through Kenoyer–Meadow field leadership.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Zooarchaeological body-size and relative-abundance arguments for local domestication are method-dependent (sample size, wild/domestic morphological criteria, taphonomy). Kenoyer presents Meadow’s zebu thesis as a research position within the indigenous-food-production narrative opened by post-1976 Mehrgarh work, not as closed consensus. Further aDNA and morphometric reanalyses (post-1998; as of later literature beyond Kenoyer 1998) are outside this source’s horizon.

## Historiography

Primary access here is **second-hand via Kenoyer 1998** (text, figure captions, endnotes). Meadow’s specialized fauna reports and HARP interim reports remain the base literature for fuller ingest.

## Wright, *The Ancient Indus* (2010)

Wright 2010 reports Meadow and Patel's (2003) genetic evidence that the predominant
Mehrgarh domestic cattle, zebu (*Bos indicus*), is indigenous to South Asia and
genetically distinct from Near Eastern *Bos taurus* — read as strong evidence for
**local** zebu domestication by ca. 4800 BCE. Wright also notes a genetics-versus-
morphology tension in Meadow and Patel's sheep evidence: size diminution suggests
local domestication, but genetic data indicate modern sheep descend from a single
Near Eastern ancestor, a tension the source flags without resolving. Wright further
credits Meadow (1996) with framing pastoralism as a **"continuum of lifestyles"**
(nomadic, semisedentary/transhumant, sedentary), all three forms attested in Harappan
contexts, and reports his and Patel's (2003) finding that camel, horse, and donkey
appear in South Asia only around 2000 BCE and are not widespread until the second
millennium BCE.
