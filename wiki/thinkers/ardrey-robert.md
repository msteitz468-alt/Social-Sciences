## Overview

Robert Ardrey (1908–1980) was a playwright turned popular-science writer who, alongside Desmond Morris (*The Naked Ape*, not otherwise covered in this wiki), helped popularize the "killer ape"/innate-aggression thesis of human origins in works including *African Genesis*, *The Territorial Imperative*, *Social Contract*, and *The Hunting Hypothesis*.

## Contributions

Per *Origins* (1977), Ardrey argued (attributed voice) that humans carry an innate, biologically-programmed drive toward killing, aggression, and territoriality inherited from a "killer ape" evolutionary past — a position he drew on and popularized from [[dart-raymond]]'s reading of early hominid fossil evidence and [[lorenz-konrad]]'s ethological theory of instinct. This is one pole of the [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]; Leakey & Lewin (see [[leakey-richard]], [[lewin-roger]]) explicitly and emphatically reject Ardrey's thesis as a "dangerously persuasive myth," arguing instead that cooperation, not innate aggression, was the trait most strongly selected in human evolutionary history.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Not a research scientist; a popularizer synthesizing and disseminating the fossil-based "killer ape" claims of [[dart-raymond]] and the ethological instinct theory of [[lorenz-konrad]] for a general readership, alongside Desmond Morris.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Ardrey's thesis is directly and extensively contested in *Origins* (1977) — see [[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]] for Leakey & Lewin's counter-position (ritualized, non-innate territoriality; ethnographic counter-cases; the argument that war stems from agricultural surplus and ideology, not genetics).

## Historiography

Known in this wiki's collection through *Origins* (1977), which treats Ardrey primarily as the popularizing target of its counter-argument; further biography is unaudited.
