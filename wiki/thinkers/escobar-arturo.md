# Arturo Escobar

Arturo Escobar (b. 1952) is a Colombian-American anthropologist whose *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995) made development itself an object of [[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] analysis and helped define the [[post-development]] research program. He has held appointments including Smith College and (later career, beyond this source) the University of North Carolina; the ingested book was written from training and exchange at Berkeley, UC Santa Cruz, and Smith, with Colombian food/nutrition fieldwork funded by the United Nations University.

## Overview

Escobar treats **development** not as a neutral technical project of raising living standards but as a historically specific cultural and political formation of the early post–World War II period — a [[regime-of-representation|regime of representation]] that produced "the Third World" as underdeveloped and made systematic intervention thinkable. His approach is explicitly poststructuralist and anthropological: Foucauldian discourse and power, in dialogue with [[said-edward|Said]]'s [[orientalism]], Africanist critique (Mudimbe), and Latin American social-movement and dependency conversations.

## Contributions

- **[[development-discourse]]** — development analyzed as an encompassing cultural space that colonizes social reality (attributed to Escobar 1995).
- **[[anthropology-of-modernity]]** — proposed research program: ethnographic and historical analysis of modern institutions (economy, planning, expertise) as cultural constructs.
- **[[post-development]]** — call to imagine alternatives beyond reforming development (attributed; Ch. 6).
- **[[regime-of-representation]]** — development as a system relating forms of knowledge, techniques of power, and forms of subjectivity.
- Case analyses of how **peasants, women, and the environment** became clients of the development apparatus in the 1970s–80s.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained and influenced in a network that includes Paul Rabinow and Berkeley STS/anthropology circles; conversations with Michael Taussig, James Clifford, Stephen Gudeman, and development critics around Wolfgang Sachs, Ivan Illich, Majid Rahnema, Ashis Nandy, Vandana Shiva; fellow anthropologists of development [[ferguson-james|James Ferguson]] ([[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]) and Stacy Leigh Pigg. Draws heavily on Michel [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] ([[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]], [[power-knowledge]]) and Edward [[said-edward|Said]] ([[orientalism]] as model for developmentalism). Colombian interlocutors include Orlando Fals Borda and researchers on food and rural development. See [[post-development]] and [[post-development-debate]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Post-development and Escobar's discourse analysis have been charged (by various development theorists and some anthropologists) with understating material poverty, romanticizing the local, and offering thin alternatives — positions recorded on [[post-development-debate]]. The book itself is a polemic as well as analysis; its theses stay in attributed voice.

## Historiography

*Encountering Development* is a standard citation in anthropology of development, critical development studies, and political ecology. A 2012 edition with a new preface (not ingested here) extends the argument into later debates. Deep study analysis: [[hubs/studies/escobar-encountering-development-study]].
