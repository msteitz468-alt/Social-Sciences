# Ronald L. Jepperson

## Overview

Ronald L. Jepperson is a sociologist. In *The New Institutionalism in Organizational
Analysis* (1991) he authored ch. 6, "Institutions, Institutional Effects, and
Institutionalism," and co-authored ch. 9, "The Public Order and the Construction of
Formal Organizations," with [[meyer-john-w]].

## Contributions

Ch. 6 offers the volume's most systematic definitional treatment of "institution."
Jepperson defined an institution as "a social order or pattern that has attained a
certain state or property," where "institutionalization denotes the process of such
attainment," and specified that a pattern is institutionalized when "departures from
the pattern are counteracted in a regulated fashion, by repetitively activated,
socially constructed, controls." His central claim is that institutions "owe their
survival to relatively self-activating social processes," not to "recurrent collective
mobilization" — distinguishing institutional reproduction sharply from reproduction
through "action," which requires repeated remobilization. He argued institution-hood is
*relative* along four dimensions (analytical context, level of organization, dimension
of a relationship, and centrality), and that institutions are not merely constraining:
"all institutions simultaneously empower and control." He distinguished
taken-for-grantedness from comprehension, awareness, and evaluation, and set out three
carriers of institutionalization — formal organization, regimes (codified, centrally
sanctioned rules), and culture (customary rules without central monitoring) — plus a
four-part taxonomy of institutional change: formation, development/elaboration,
deinstitutionalization, and reinstitutionalization (his example: secularization as
reinstitutionalization of religion in Western societies).

With [[meyer-john-w]] (ch. 9), Jepperson developed a **polity typology** crossing degree
of actor-authorization with degree of institutionalization of collective functions,
yielding four polity types — liberal/individualist (United States), statist society
(France), segmental/"state outside society" (Latin American states), and corporatist
(Germany) — and three formal propositions linking each combination to the extent and
character of formal organizing. See [[meyer-john-w]] for the chapter's content in
fuller detail.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Jepperson's ch. 6 typology of "lines of theory in organization analysis" (crossing
degree of social construction with level of analysis) places institutionalism in the
high-construction/high-order cell, alongside [[meyer-john-w]] and [[rowan-brian]]'s
rationalized-myth account. He explicitly resists treating institutionalism and
[[rational-choice-theory]] as simple opposites, arguing both often invoke the other's
core mechanisms. His chapter's acknowledgments cite discussion with Elaine Backman,
Randall Collins, Carol Conell, [[powell-walter-w]], [[meyer-john-w]], David Strang, and
Morris Zelditch Jr., and written comments from Carol Conell, [[dimaggio-paul]], and
Francisco Ramirez — a colleague/discussant network. DiMaggio and Powell (ch. 1)
attribute to Jepperson (drawing on his ch. 6) the view of institutions as "stable
designs for chronically repeated activity sequences."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

[[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) credited Jepperson's chapter for establishing that
institutional reproduction need not depend on active agency, and for cautioning that
"not every cultural element or environmental influence is an institutional effect."
Jepperson himself flagged, but explicitly declined to adjudicate, a genuine scholarly
disagreement between Mary Douglas and Lévi-Strauss (who associate institutions with
"absence of thought") and Stinchcombe (1968), who argues modern societies show both
greater reflexivity *and* greater institutional self-replicating capacity — noted as a
possible future `debates/` thread on modernity and institutionalization. His and
Meyer's ch. 9 polity typology was self-qualified as raising a "sampling on the dependent
variable" concern, addressed only with the claim that "world history has done the
sampling."
