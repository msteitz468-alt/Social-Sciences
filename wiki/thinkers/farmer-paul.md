# Paul Farmer

## Overview

Paul Farmer (1959–2022) was an American physician and anthropologist, co-founder of **Partners
in Health**, and the figure most associated with the concept of **[[structural-violence|structural
violence]]** in [[medical-anthropology|medical anthropology]] and global health. Trained
simultaneously as an infectious-disease physician and a cultural anthropologist (M.D. and Ph.D.,
Harvard), he built his life's work around a single argument, developed across four decades of
clinical practice in [[do-kay|rural Haiti]]: that the distribution and outcomes of infectious
disease among the poor — [[tuberculosis]], [[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]] — are patterned by poverty and
inequality, and that social scientists and epidemiologists routinely mistake those structural
effects for cultural difference or individual failure (his **[[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest
claims of causality]]**). He decided at twenty, before first going to Haiti, that he wanted to be a
"physician-anthropologist," and pursued the two vocations as one.

*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999) is his central
programmatic statement; it followed his dissertation ethnography *AIDS and Accusation* (1992, on the
"geography of blame") and preceded *Pathologies of Power* (2003), where structural violence is joined
to a human-rights framework.

## Contributions

- **[[structural-violence]]** — Farmer's signature analytic, reworked from Johan Galtung (1969): the
  social, economic, and political arrangements (poverty, racism, gender inequality) that constrain
  agency and become **[[embodiment|embodied]]** as differential risk of disease and premature death.
  "Neither culture nor pure individual will is at fault; rather, historically given (and often
  economically driven) processes and forces conspire to constrain individual agency" (attributed).
- **[[immodest-claims-of-causality]]** — his critique of overreaching mono-causal, mono-disciplinary
  explanations of disease (culturalist accounts of AIDS, "noncompliance" accounts of TB failure)
  that inflate a discipline's favored variable into an "omnibus explanation" and shift blame onto the
  sick poor.
- **[[biosocial-analysis]]** — his methodological demand for an analysis "linking molecular
  epidemiology to history, ethnography, and political economy," treating disease as a *biosocial*
  phenomenon rather than analyzing biology and society on separate tracks.
- **Pragmatic solidarity** — the ethic and practice he and Jim Yong Kim named for their transnational
  work: a service-delivery commitment rooted in liberation theology's "preferential option for the
  poor," insisting the destitute sick receive the best available care regardless of cost-effectiveness
  arithmetic. Farmer's slogan for the physician's role, following Rudolf Virchow, was "the natural
  attorney of the poor."
- **Partners in Health / Zanmi Lasante / Socios en Salud** — the linked organizations (Cambridge MA;
  Haiti's Central Plateau; northern Lima, Peru) through which Farmer's arguments were operationalized:
  community-health-worker–supported treatment that achieved TB cure rates in destitute rural Haiti
  "as good as anywhere else," and that demonstrated multidrug-resistant TB could be cured in poor
  settings against prevailing "cost-effectiveness" objections (see [[tuberculosis]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Farmer's institutional home was Harvard Medical School's **Department of Social Medicine**, his
"intellectual roost" from his first year of medical school. He named [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur
Kleinman]] as his key teacher ("the best teacher I had"), placing him downstream of Kleinman's
interpretive/clinical [[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology|meaning-centered]] program even as he
pushed toward [[critical-medical-anthropology|political economy]]. He read [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]],
Sidney Mintz, and Immanuel Wallerstein ([[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]]), which he
called "less a theory than a call for analytic rigor." He named as fellow travelers the physicians
Leon Eisenberg, Howard Hiatt, and Julius Richmond, and — unusually — the comparatist Haun Saussy as a
lifelong influence on his cross-disciplinary reading. His closest collaborator was Jim Yong Kim; other
Partners in Health co-founders included Ophelia Dahl and Tom White, working alongside the Haitian
Episcopal priest called Père Aléxis in his texts.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Farmer's work has been read both as a landmark re-politicization of global health and as a committed,
even homiletic, program that states its own side. His writing is openly a "protest"; he does not claim
neutrality, so his interpretive theses are recorded here in attributed voice. Critics within
anthropology have questioned whether the structural-violence frame flattens local meaning; Farmer's
reply, staged in his "ethnographic interlude," is a self-critique: he shows how his *own* early
writing on [[do-kay|Do Kay]] inserted "local color" and sorcery exotica while erasing his own
presence and the inequality of access at issue — the very "conflation of structural violence and
cultural difference" he later named. The self-implicating quality of that reflexivity is part of his
method, not incidental to it.

## Historiography

This summary rests on a single ingested source (*Infections and Inequalities*, 1999), which is heavily
autobiographical (Chapter 1, "The Vitality of Practice," and the interlude) and thus a primary source
for Farmer's self-understanding — to be read as such, not as independent biography. His later career
(Partners in Health's global expansion, Rwanda, the 2014 Ebola response, his 2022 death in Rwanda) and
its major biographical treatment (Tracy Kidder's *Mountains Beyond Mountains*, 2003) postdate this book
and are not yet covered. A **Thinkers Hub** biography is warranted on the selection criteria
(program-builder, rich primary material, consequential figure) but is flagged, not built, from this
single early source — see the log note.
