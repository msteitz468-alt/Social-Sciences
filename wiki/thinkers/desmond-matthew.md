# Matthew Desmond

## Overview

American sociologist of poverty, housing, and urban inequality. Principal ingested work: *[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted: Poverty and Profit in the American City]]* (2016) — multi-sited ethnography of landlords and tenants in Milwaukee plus the Milwaukee Area Renters Study and Eviction Court Study, arguing that **[[eviction]]** and the **[[private-rental-housing|private rental market]]** are central mechanisms producing and deepening poverty. Related concept paper: **[[disposable-ties]]** (AJS 2012). Study: [[desmond-evicted-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/desmond-evicted-study]].

## Contributions

- **Housing as poverty mechanism:** “housing is implicated in the creation of poverty”; “Eviction is a cause, not just a condition, of poverty” (*Evicted* Prologue/Epilogue, attributed).
- **Measurement of forced mobility:** formal vs [[informal-eviction]]; MARS design capturing folk undercount of eviction.
- **[[disposable-ties]]:** crisis networks under deep poverty and displacement.
- **[[relational-ethnography]]:** study processes binding unequal actors (landlords–tenants), not the poor in quarantine.
- **[[nuisance-property-ordinance]] analysis:** police–landlord–tenant triangle; DV and racial disparity.
- **Policy positions (public sociology):** right to counsel in housing court; universal housing vouchers; critique of homeowner tax expenditures vs aid to poor renters (*Evicted* Epilogue — positions).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Graduate training at University of Wisconsin–Madison (field site choice of Milwaukee as “reality” relative to Madison — About This Project). Engages Wilsonian structural poverty, culture-of-poverty debates (Lewis; Stack as foil and resource), urban ethnography traditions, and relational sociology (Emirbayer co-work on racial order / relational ethnography). Foil: poverty research focused on jobs, welfare, parenting, and incarceration without landlords.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reception not yet systematically ingested. Desmond’s own About This Project records positionality limits, intervention effects, and gender suspicions in the field. Later *Poverty, by America* (2023) extends public-sociology poverty critique — not yet ingested.

## Historiography

*Evicted* won the 2017 Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction and became a public-sociology landmark; academic afterlife includes the Eviction Lab and national eviction measurement (follow-on corpus not yet ingested).
