## Overview

William Labov (1927–2006) was professor of linguistics at the University of Pennsylvania and a member of the Social Science Research Council's Committee on Sociolinguistics. He is credited as the founder of variationist (quantitative) sociolinguistics. His chapter "On the Mechanism of Linguistic Change," reprinted in Gumperz & Hymes's *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (1972), was originally published in a 1965 Georgetown University Monograph — per this wiki's dating-by-key-text convention, Labov's underlying research is dated to the mid-1960s: Martha's Vineyard fieldwork data were collected in 1961, and New York City data were collected and analyzed in 1963.

## Contributions

Labov argued (attributed) that sound change in progress can be directly observed — against the position, which he attributes to Bloomfield (1933) and Hockett (1958), that ongoing sound change is in principle unobservable. He did so via "apparent time" comparison across age cohorts within a socially-defined community, checked against earlier real-time linguistic-atlas records.

His central analytic unit is the **[[sociolinguistic-variable]]** — a linguistic feature whose variants correlate with social factors, defined empirically through covariation between linguistic and social data rather than as a purely descriptive category. He specified three linked analytic problems for a change in progress: the *transition problem* (tracing the route via apparent-time distributions), the *embedding problem* (locating the linguistic and social matrix carrying the change), and the *evaluation problem* (finding subjective/latent correlates — informants' attitudes toward the variable).

On Martha's Vineyard, Labov (1961 fieldwork, reported 1963/1972) documented rising centralization of the (aw) and (ay) vowels across four age cohorts (centralization index 0.22 → 0.37 → 0.44 → 0.88), against 1933 Linguistic Atlas records (Guy Lowman) showing little centralization — he attributed the change's evaluative correlate to speakers' claim to "native status as a Vineyarder," with returnees showing higher centralization than fathers who never left.

In New York City, Labov (1963 fieldwork) documented a curvilinear stylistic-stratification pattern for the (oh) variable (vowel in *law, talk, off*): lower-middle-class speakers showed the sharpest self-correction toward prestige forms across four elicited speech styles — the empirical basis for his "hypercorrection from above" finding — with a matching subjective-reaction test showing lower-middle-class speakers aged 20–39 were the most severe in condemning the stigmatized form in others' speech even though they used it most themselves. He proposed a hypercorrection mechanism rooted in the vowel-distinction loss among first-generation Yiddish-substrate speakers, later hypercorrected by their children (extended to Italian-American speakers per a suggestion credited to Marvin Herzog).

From twelve documented sound changes (three on Martha's Vineyard, nine in New York City), Labov proposed a thirteen-stage mechanism of sound change, divided into **change from below** (stages 1–8, below social awareness — origination in a marginal subgroup, generalization to an indicator, spread, consolidation as a community-wide marker with stylistic stratification, and structural readjustment triggering re-cycling) and **change from above** (stages 9–13, overt social awareness — stigmatization by the highest-status group, correction toward the prestige model, possible hypercorrection from above exceeding the prestige target, and, in extreme cases, divorce of the stereotype from actual usage). He used this model to reconstruct a documented re-cycling chain in New York City vowels: (eh) raising (from Babbitt 1896) triggering (oh) raising, triggering shifts in (oy) and (ah), triggering further re-cycling in (ay) and then (aw).

Labov explicitly rejected the position, which he attributes to Bloomfield, Hockett, and Postal (1968), that sound change is either unobservable or socially arbitrary ("There is no more reason for languages to change than there is for automobiles to add fins one year and remove them the next" — Postal, quoted by Labov to be refuted).

Separately, per Gumperz's introduction to the same volume, Labov's 1969 paper "The Logic of Nonstandard English" argued that children using nonstandard/"restricted" varieties in formal interview settings are highly creative and effective communicators in culturally realistic settings — a position cited (including by Bernstein himself, approvingly) as challenging readings of Basil Bernstein's code theory that treat restricted-code use as indicating a class-based verbal-skill deficit. This anchors Labov's position on the [[deficit-difference-debate]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

Labov's NYC dialect study (1966) is credited by Gumperz (1972) with making "a radical break" from traditional dialect-questionnaire methods, building on but superseding the Linguistic Atlas of the United States (Kurath 1941). Labov's work spawned a tradition of urban social dialectology (Black, Puerto Rican, and Chicano speech varieties), including curriculum-reform-oriented studies (Wolfram 1969; Shuy, Wolfram & Riley 1969).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Within the same volume, Labov's variationist/"correlational" program is framed by Gumperz as one pole of a disciplinary split, with Gumperz's own "interactional" approach ([[interactional-sociolinguistics]]) positioned as a complementary but methodologically distinct alternative that treats social and linguistic categories as mutually constitutive rather than independently correlated systems. No adjudication between the two is recorded in this source; both are documented as positions.
