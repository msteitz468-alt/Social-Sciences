# Te Rangi Hiroa (Sir Peter Buck)

## Overview

Te Rangi Hiroa, also known as Sir Peter Buck (1877–1951), was a Māori-Irish physician, ethnologist, and museum administrator. Per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017), he was raised bicultural, educated at Te Aute College, and earned a medical degree from Otago Medical School (1910). He served as a government medical officer to Māori communities and in the New Zealand parliament, then enlisted with the Māori regiment and survived Gallipoli. He joined the Bishop Museum staff in 1927 for fieldwork across tropical Polynesia and became the Museum's director in 1936, succeeding Herbert Gregory, while also holding a Yale professorship ex officio. He died of cancer in 1951.

## Contributions

- ***Vikings of the Sunrise*** (1938) — Hiroa's "mature theory," synthesizing the Bishop Museum's ethnographic survey of Polynesia (per Kirch).
- **"Micronesian route" theory of Polynesian origins** (1938; attributed, theoretical claim) — posited that ancestral Polynesians migrated from island Southeast Asia through the Caroline and Kiribati atolls, explicitly bypassing any route through Melanesia. The theory required an ad hoc secondary "reacquisition" of horticultural crops (breadfruit, banana, yam, taro) from Melanesia via Fiji, Samoa, and Tonga, patching the theory's chief empirical hole — that Polynesian horticulture is demonstrably of Melanesian origin, a fact Hiroa himself acknowledged. Kirch's own critical judgment (attributed): "It was hardly a parsimonious explanation."
- Public rejection of Melanesian ancestral admixture in Polynesian social organization, expressed in a 1930 letter to Sir Apirana Ngata hoping to "cut out any survivals of Melanesian influence" — a primary-source statement evidencing motivated reasoning behind the Micronesian-route theory.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Mentored and collaborated with [[unknown]] figures at the Bishop Museum; directly collaborated with Kenneth P. Emory on the joint 1934 Mangarevan Expedition (per Kirch's account, fig. 1.7 caption). Succeeded as Bishop Museum director by Alexander Spoehr upon his death (not by Emory — do not conflate).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

**Reflexivity and production context (per Kirch's explicit historiographical analysis, attributed to Kirch, not wiki voice):** Hiroa's Micronesian-route theory was shaped by the sociopolitical context of interwar racial science. Contemporary physical anthropologists classified Polynesians as "Negroid"/"Mongoloid" mixtures; Hiroa, himself part-Polynesian, was reportedly denied a UK knighthood despite WWI service and academic distinction, and encountered continued racial prejudice in seeking US citizenship (Polynesians then viewed by some as part of a "Yellow Peril" category). Kirch's stated thesis: "his work demonstrates how any scholar's work can be influenced by his or her sociopolitical context." The Micronesian-route theory is presented by Kirch as having "fall[en] completely once stratigraphic archaeology and modern comparative linguistics were brought to bear on the problem after World War II" — superseded and abandoned, not merely contested. See [[heyerdahl-polynesian-origins-debate]] for the adjacent, later Heyerdahl controversy over Polynesian origins, and [[near-oceania-remote-oceania]] for the analytical distinction (Roger Green, 1991) that replaced the older "Melanesia" category Hiroa's theory was implicitly reacting against.
