## Overview

Mary Leakey (1913–1996) was an archaeologist who worked alongside her husband [[leakey-louis]] at [[olduvai-gorge]], Tanzania, and was the mother of [[leakey-richard]]. Per *Origins*, she was a descendant (great-great-great-granddaughter) of John Frere, who described worked flint tools at Hoxne, Suffolk, in a 1797 paper to the Royal Society — an early antiquarian antecedent to scientific archaeology.

## Contributions

Mary Leakey analyzed Olduvai Gorge tool industries from the mid-1930s across more than twenty sites. On 17 July 1959, while Louis Leakey was in camp with flu, she made the first major Olduvai hominid fossil find, later named *Zinjanthropus boisei* (now classified within [[australopithecus]]). She and her helpers also recovered primitive *Homo* fossils, per *Origins* nearly four million years old, from deposits near Olduvai Gorge, and found an early *Homo* jaw at Laetolil, Tanzania.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Wife of [[leakey-louis]]; mother of [[leakey-richard]]. Her Olduvai tool-industry analyses fed directly into the family's broader hominin research program continued by her son at [[koobi-fora]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Taxonomic labels from her 1959 find (*Zinjanthropus*) were later folded into the genus *Australopithecus* by subsequent scholarship; *Origins* (1977) presents the phylogenetic picture as understood at the time of writing.
