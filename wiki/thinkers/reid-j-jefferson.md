# J. Jefferson Reid

American archaeologist; co-developer of [[behavioral-archaeology|behavioral archaeology]] with [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]] and [[rathje-william|William L. Rathje]]. Schiffer’s preface to *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987) credits Reid with first urging him to write the book (1982) and lists Reid among general influences on the theoretical framework.

## Overview

Southwest-focused archaeologist associated with the University of Arizona behavioral archaeology circle. In *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* (1995 Ch. 1), Schiffer attributes the four-strategies definition of the program to Reid’s classroom insight while they were TAs for Rathje in spring 1972.

## Contributions

- **Four strategies of behavioral archaeology** — Schiffer reports that Reid wrote a radical definition — archaeology as the study of relationships between human behavior and material culture in all times and places — with a simple matrix of four interrelated strategies (past/present × particular questions/general laws). Reid, Schiffer, and Rathje fleshed the matrix for dissertations and published it (*American Anthropologist*; Schiffer 1976 Ch. 6; 1995 Ch. 1, 6) (attributed, Schiffer 1995).
- Co-articulation of formation-process / transformation concerns (Reid, Schiffer, and Neff 1975; equivalence transformations; deposit-specific limits of inference) cited throughout Schiffer 1983c/1987/1995 Ch. 18–20 (attributed).
- Grasshopper Pueblo relative room-abandonment measure and abandonment classes (Reid 1973, 1978, 1985; Reid and Shimada 1982) used by Schiffer as comparative baseline for Broken K fill–floor analysis (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 20–21).
- “Coconspirators” partnership with Schiffer during dissertation year developing behavioral archaeology as a reintegrating disciplinary program (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Close colleague of Schiffer and Rathje in the Arizona network; part of the circle that also intersects CRM and Southwest chronological debates (Hohokam) treated in Schiffer 1987/1995. Schiffer frames Reid as originator of the four-strategies matrix insight that became the program’s organizational charter (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).
