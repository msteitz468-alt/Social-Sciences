# Stephen A. Tyler

American anthropologist (b. 1932); contributor to *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* with "Post-Modern Ethnography: From Document of the Occult to Occult Document." The volume’s frontispiece shows Tyler writing in the field in India (1963). Named among seminar systematic critics. Argues for ethnography as **[[ethnographic-evocation|evocation]]** rather than representation — the strongest “post-modern” position in the collection.

## Overview

Tyler’s essay is the volume’s most radical critique of realist documentary ethnography and of scientific rhetoric. Editors note it draws on pre-modern sources for the post-modern and is “generally unclassifiable”; they refused to place it as utopian close to the volume.

## Strathern engagement (*Partial Connections*, 1991)

*(Attributed — [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]].)* *[[strathern-partial-connections-1991|Partial Connections]]* takes Tyler’s [[ethnographic-evocation]] as starting point for Part I: ethnography as connection rather than representation of an independent object. Strathern argues the program **does not go far enough** for comparative anthropology — Tyler’s therapeutic departure-and-return still centers an integrating traveller/reader (hidden journey aesthetic); classical comparison’s scaffold is dismantled into text-referencing, but [[partial-connections]] / [[postplural]] comparison remains possible without holist units. PC acknowledgements list Tyler among commentators.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Tyler in Writing Culture 1986.)*

- **[[post-modern-ethnography]]** program: cooperative fragmentary discourse → evocation → therapeutic aesthetic integration.
- **[[ethnographic-evocation|Evocation]] vs representation** — abandon correspondence/documentary claims.
- Critique of scientific rhetoric: generalization, verification, experiment, truth as correspondence, natural-history [[ethnographic-realism]].
- Discourse over text; dialogue/polyphony; reject observer–observed split.
- “From document of the occult to occult document.”
- Supplement honesty: **no completed instance** of post-modern ethnography yet; Crapanzano, Tedlock, Majnep–Bulmer in the “right spirit.”

### Earlier work: Koya kinship-term alternation (1972, revised from Tyler 1966)

*(Attributed to Tyler, 1972; Ch. 8 of Gumperz & Hymes, eds.,* Directions in Sociolinguistics*.)* Well before the post-modern turn reflected in his *Writing Culture* essay, Tyler conducted fieldwork among the **Koya** (a Dravidian-speaking tribal population of the Godavari River region, central India, near Bhadrachallam-Nugur, 1962–1963, under a Ford Foundation Foreign Area Training Fellowship), producing "Context and Alternation in Koya Kinship Terminology" — a chapter revised from his 1966 *American Anthropologist* article. He argued that kinship terminologies cannot be adequately analyzed as a single unitary genealogical structure: alternation among kin terms is systematic and rule-governed, driven by social and linguistic context rather than genealogy alone. He extended **componential analysis** to alternation by adapting the linguistic device of context-sensitive ("slant-line") environment rules from generative phonology (notation credited to Romney 1964), using components of sex, generation, cross- vs. parallel-relation, and relative age. Koya kin terms alternated along at least three axes: code alternation (Koya vs. Telugu lexical stems, conditioned by the ethnic/religious identity of speaker and hearer); situational lexical alternation within one code (e.g., Telugu terms for "father" varying by formality/respect); and morphological alternation (choice of possessive pronoun and derivative suffixes tracking audience composition, relative status, occasion formality, joking relationships, and speaker age/sex). He found the highest frequency of alternation among kin categories most likely to co-reside in the same household, read as evidence that maximal terminological differentiation tracks areas of high social interest and role inconsistency. The chapter explicitly positions itself against the "genealogical method" as a sufficient field method (critiquing Hammel 1965; citing critiques by Schneider 1968, 1969), arguing the speech event, not the genealogical grid, must be the unit of analysis — a methodological stance continuous with his later, more radical critique of documentary ethnography in *Writing Culture*.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Seminar critic roster; engages Habermas/Lyotard post-modern framing; cites experimental ethnographers as partial precursors. In the earlier Koya work, credits Hymes with formulation suggestions and draws on Romney's (1964) componential notation; positions himself against Hammel's genealogical method and alongside Schneider's critiques of componential kinship analysis.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Asad (power/stiffness) and Rabinow (institutions; post-modern as new canon) are in-volume brakes. Full Tylerian program did not become disciplinary center; [[ethnographic-evocation]] survives more as experimental option than orthodoxy. See [[writing-culture-debate]].
