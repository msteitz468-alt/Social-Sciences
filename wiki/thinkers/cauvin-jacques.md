# Jacques Cauvin (1930–2001)

French prehistorian of the Near Eastern Neolithic; long-term excavator of **[[mureybet|Mureybet]]** and architect of the **[[revolution-of-symbols|revolution of symbols]]** thesis that a new religious ideology among late hunter-gatherers **preceded** the farming economy. Mature English synthesis: *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|The Birth of the Gods and the Origins of Agriculture]]* (2000; French *Naissance des divinités…* 1994/1997), translated by Trevor Watkins.

## Overview

Cauvin worked for decades on the beginnings of the Neolithic in the Levant and SE Anatolia, combining site-based fieldwork with a structuralist-influenced cultural reading of symbolism (Annales and Leroi-Gourhan interlocutors in the English edition framing). He defined the [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]] as essentially a **restructuring of human mentality** expressed in new gods and symbols, then traced the diffusion of the Neolithic package from a Levantine cradle.

## Contributions

*(Attributed primarily from Cauvin 2000 unless noted.)*

- **[[revolution-of-symbols]]:** ~9500 BC Khiamian appearance of woman/Goddess and bull as dominant symbols; new religion among still-foraging groups; farming as second step (~9000 BC).
- **PPNA cultural triad** along the Levantine corridor: Sultanian, Aswadian, Mureybetian — regional cultures, not a single undifferentiated PPNA “culture.”
- **PPNB as dominant expanding culture:** rectangular architecture, perfected cultivation, herding, skull cult; stages of expansion into Anatolia, southern Levant, Mediterranean, Cyprus, Jezirah.
- **Critique of materialist origins models:** rejects deriving the symbolic change from ecological or techno-economic infrastructure; reinterprets Braidwood’s “culture was not ready” as mental/religious readiness.
- **Diffusion as behaviour:** insists diffusion requires specified modalities and motivations, not a residual label.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Influences / interlocutors (as framed in Cauvin 2000):** André Leroi-Gourhan (structuralist archaeology of symbols); French Annales school (mentalités); [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]] (Neolithic Revolution vocabulary, reworked); [[braidwood-robert|Robert Braidwood]] (hilly-flanks / “culture not ready”); Kathleen Kenyon (PPNA/PPNB Jericho sequence); Ofer Bar-Yosef (Levantine corridor); Marie-Claire Cauvin; Danielle Stordeur; Harald Hauptmann (Nevalı Çori); James Mellaart ([[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] iconography as later explicit theology).

**Translator and Anglophone bridge:** Trevor Watkins (cognitive/symbolic interest in recent Near Eastern prehistory).

**Downstream engagement:** widely cited as the ideology-before-economy pole in agricultural-origins debates; [[mithen-steven|Mithen]] accepts broader ideology-before-economy while rejecting mother-goddess cult label; [[gobekli-tepe|Göbekli Tepe]] often read as support for ideology-first without Cauvin’s full Goddess/Bull package.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Mother-goddess / fertility reading of early Neolithic female figurines and bull imagery is contested (e.g. Göbekli wild/dangerous male animals; minimalist readings of figurines).
- Absolute chronologies and site inventories (as of Cauvin 1997–2000) need date-stamping against later radiocarbon, aDNA, and excavation programs.
- Causal leap from iconography to “divinities” and then to agricultural motivation is interpretive — record as position, not wiki-voice fact.
- Diffusion narratives risk underplaying multi-regional domestication outside SW Asia (Cauvin’s scope is Near East radiation, not global monogenesis of all farming).

## Historiography

English edition presents the 1997 French text with Postscript updates rather than full rewrite; first synthesis to stress calibrated dates for the Near Eastern Neolithic (Cauvin’s claim). Died 2001; dedicatee among the dead in Mithen’s *After the Ice* preface.
