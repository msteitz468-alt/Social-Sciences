# Roy Wagner

## Overview

Roy Wagner (1938–2018) was an American anthropologist of Melanesia (fieldwork among the **Daribi** of the Karimui area, Papua New Guinea) and a symbolic/semiotic theorist. *[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]* (1975; rev. 1981) — now primary-ingested — reframed "culture" as an act of invention performed reciprocally by ethnographer and studied alike, generalizing the argument of his Daribi monograph *Habu* (1972); with *Lethal Speech* (1978) the three form what Wagner called an "unintended trilogy." All claims here are attributed.

## Contributions

- **[[invention-and-convention|Invention and convention]]** (*The Invention of Culture*): two reciprocal modes of symbolization — collectivizing/conventional and differentiating/inventive; cultures differ in which they treat as innately *given* and which as humanly *made*, and Western and tribal/religious traditions invert the assignment. [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]] folds this into [[perspectivism]] and [[multinaturalism]].
- **[[reverse-anthropology|Reverse anthropology]]** — colonized peoples' objectifying comprehension of the West, paradigmatically Melanesian cargo cults: "we look at the natives' cargo… and call it 'culture,' whereas they look at our culture and call it 'cargo'" ("Road belong culture," Ch. 2).
- **[[obviation|Obviation]]**: the effect whereby differentiating symbols encompass what they symbolize; formalized in *Lethal Speech* as "the extended, or processual form of trope," and applied in Ch. 6 as the master pattern by which anthropological paradigms supersede themselves.
- **Relative objectivity**: "'Absolute' objectivity would require that the anthropologist have no biases, and hence no culture at all"; the reflexive "acid test" of any anthropology is whether it applies relativity to the anthropologist's own reality ([[cultural-relativism]]).
- **The wax museum critique**: early anthropology's museum context modeled culture as finite, discrete assemblage — a "wax museum of curiosities" (see [[culture]]).
- **[[fractal-person|The fractal person]]** — person as never a unit standing in relation to an aggregate but always an entity with relationship integrally implied; recast from hearing early [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] partial-connections material (PC acknowledgements; essay later in *Big Men and Great Men*). Strathern appropriates as imagery (not math); Viveiros de Castro reads through Deleuzian multiplicity and [[partial-connections]]. Barok figure–ground / tree materials in PC exemplify self-scaling imagery Wagner’s program supplies.
- His Daribi fieldwork dictum, "their misunderstanding of me was not the same as my misunderstanding them," which Viveiros de Castro calls "the best definition of culture ever proposed" and ties to [[controlled-equivocation]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Key encourager:** David M. Schneider — Wagner: "without the strong and interested encouragement of David M. Schneider this book would not have been written"; Schneider's "What Is Kinship All About?" (1972) supplies Wagner's paradigm case of anthropology's "controlling nature" mode.
- **Declared debts** (rev. ed. introduction): Lévi-Strauss (universalization/particularization in *The Savage Mind*), Geertz ("Ethos, World View…"), [[bateson-gregory|Bateson]] (*Naven*'s schismogenesis), Dumont (*homo hierarchicus/aequalis*), Schneider & Smith, Nancy Munn ("objectivate," *Walbiri Iconography*).
- **Fieldwork:** the Daribi, Karimui area, Papua New Guinea (underlying *Habu*, 1972 — fieldwork therefore pre-1972; no exact dates given in the ingested edition).
- **Affinities / reception:** cited Marilyn Strathern's then-forthcoming "No Nature, No Culture: The Hagen Case" as extending his theme; grouped by [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]] with [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] and [[latour-bruno|Latour]] as anthropology's genuine "post-structuralists," his convention/invention duplex read as a [[deleuze-gilles|Deleuzian]] reciprocal-presupposition structure. Wagner positioned himself against contemporaries Sperber ("subjective objectivism") and Barth ("objective subjectivism"), claiming a dialectical middle path.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Wagner conceded that opposing Western conventionalism to tribal differentiation risks Sahlins's "the West and the Rest" dichotomy; the binary contrast remains the framework's most-criticized feature.
- The book is explicitly programmatic, not evidential ("not written to prove… any set of precepts"); its ethnographic and American materials are illustrations.
- Some generalizations are asserted without citation (e.g., ethnolinguistic claims about American Indian, cockney, and Black American speech styles as "expressive" language use) — flagged here as claims, not findings.
- His rejection of deprivation/injustice explanations of cargo cults is a contested position within the [[millenarianism]] literature.
