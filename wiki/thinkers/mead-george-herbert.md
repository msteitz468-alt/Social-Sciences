# George Herbert Mead

## Overview

George Herbert Mead (1863–1931) was an American philosopher, social psychologist, and pragmatist, affiliated for most of his career (from 1894) with the University of Chicago, where his lecture course "Social Psychology" became one of the most influential in the discipline's history. He is regarded as a founder of **[[social-psychology|social psychology]]** and the principal intellectual source of **[[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]**, the tradition of American sociology that his student **[[blumer-herbert|Herbert Blumer]]** later named (1937) and led — see Blumer's reconstruction and method program in *[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]* (1969) and [[hubs/theory/symbolic-interactionism]].

Mead's central argument — reconstructed in *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* — reverses the individualist starting point of psychology: **[[the-social-self|mind and self are not presupposed by society but are products of it]]**, generated within the social process of communication. Out of the [[gesture|conversation of gestures]], the emergence of the [[significant-symbol|significant symbol]], and the individual's capacity for [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|taking the role of the other]], Mead derives reflective intelligence, the self, and the moral community. He belongs jointly to the [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophical movement (with Peirce, James, and his lifelong friend John Dewey) and to classical sociological theory — though, as Hans Joas notes, his twentieth-century influence "has been stronger in the social sciences than in philosophy proper" (attributed, Joas 2015).

**Provenance caution.** Mead never wrote the book on which his reputation chiefly rests. He published many papers but no monograph in his lifetime; every book bearing his name is posthumous. *Mind, Self, and Society* (1934) was compiled by Charles W. Morris from student lecture notes and stenographic transcripts of Mead's Chicago course. Claims about "Mead's" text must therefore be read as doubly mediated — spoken lecture, transcribed by students, arranged by an editor (see the [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate|authorship/reconstruction debate]]).

## Contributions

All below are attributed to Mead's position (as reconstructed) — this is a theoretical program, not established fact.

- **[[the-social-self|The social genesis of the self]].** "The self is something which has a development; it is not initially there, at birth, but arises in the process of social experience and activity" (attributed). Its defining property is reflexivity — it "is an object to itself." The self is "essentially a social structure, and it arises in social experience"; a self cannot arise outside it.
- **[[significant-symbol|The significant symbol]].** A gesture (paradigmatically the vocal gesture) that "has the same effect on the individual making it that it has on the individual to whom it is addressed" — arousing in the speaker the response it calls out in the hearer. Mind is "the internalized or implicit conversation of the individual with himself" by means of such symbols.
- **[[taking-the-role-of-the-other|Taking the role of the other]].** The capacity to arouse in oneself the response one calls out in another — "the very essence" of intelligence's social character, and the mechanism by which meaning, self-consciousness, and reflective intelligence arise.
- **[[generalized-other|The generalized other]].** "The organized community or social group which gives to the individual his unity of self" — the attitude of the whole community, internalized. It is the basis of abstract thought, moral character, and institutions.
- **[[the-i-and-the-me|The "I" and the "me"]].** The self has two phases: the "me" (the organized set of others' attitudes one assumes) and the "I" (the organism's novel, unpredictable response to them — "the sense of freedom, of initiative"). Their dialectic reconciles social control with creativity: "Thinking is simply... the carrying-on of a conversation between... the 'I' and the 'me.'"
- **[[play-and-the-game|Play and the game]].** Two developmental stages: in play the child takes particular roles serially; in the organized game it must take the attitude of every player at once, under rules — the mechanism by which the generalized other is internalized.
- **The biologic individual and the theory of the act.** In the Supplementary Essays (his own manuscripts, not lecture notes), Mead distinguishes the "biologic individual" — the impulse-driven organism living in "an undifferentiated now" — from the "socially self-conscious individual" whose conduct involves reflection over a differentiated past and future. He preferred "impulse" to "instinct" for human conduct, and argued that reflective intelligence "appears where impulsive conduct breaks down."
- **Social ethics.** Mead's "Fragments on Ethics" give a social-pragmatist reworking of Kant: "Man is a rational being because he is a social being"; the universality of moral judgment arises "from the fact that we take the attitude of the entire community." Against Kant's formalism he held that "we do universalize the end itself," offering his own imperative — that "all of the interests that are involved should be taken into account" in reconstructing a social situation.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Mead was formed in the milieu of Harvard idealism and German philosophy and psychology. At **Harvard** (1887–88) he studied under **Josiah Royce** (whose idealism and social theory of the self left a deep mark) and **William James**, in whose household he briefly tutored; through James he absorbed the emerging pragmatism and an interest theory of value. In **Germany** he studied with **Wilhelm Wundt** at Leipzig in the winter semester of 1888–89 — registering, per Hans Joas's research, only for Wundt's metaphysics course, not the psychology laboratory — before transferring to **Berlin**, where he worked under **Wilhelm Dilthey** and the physiological psychologists. (Morris's original claim that Mead did *not* study "at Leipzig with Wundt" is a documented error, corrected by Joas 1985.) Wundt's concept of the **[[gesture]]** became the pivot of Mead's own theory, though Mead sharply criticized Wundt's psychophysical parallelism for "presuppos[ing] selves as antecedent to the social process."

His decisive lifelong relationship was with **John Dewey**, met at the University of Michigan (1891–94) and rejoined at Chicago; Morris judged their work "complementary" and never in significant opposition, and Mead drew his standard of the moral impulse explicitly from Dewey. Mead engaged and contested **John B. Watson** (whose behaviorism he sought to broaden — see [[social-behaviorism]]), **[[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]]** and **James** (whose grounding of the self in reflexive "self-feeling" he rejected as unable to explain the self's *origin* — see [[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate]] and Cooley's *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]*), **Gabriel Tarde** (on imitation), and **Bertrand Russell** (on the location of experience).

His students carried the influence into sociology: **Herbert Blumer**, who coined "symbolic interactionism" in 1937, who taught the remainder of Mead's final (1931) course after Mead fell fatally ill, and who became the tradition's chief theorist and gatekeeper; and **Charles W. Morris**, the editor whose compilation made Mead famous while, on Huebner's account, silently reshaping the text (see below). Through Blumer and the Chicago tradition Mead's reception ran to Erving Goffman and beyond; Talcott Parsons omitted Mead from his sociological canon (later expressing regret), and Jürgen Habermas, in *The Theory of Communicative Action* (1981), declared Mead the originator of the paradigm shift "from an instrumentalistic to a communicative understanding of action" (attributed).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The reconstruction problem.** Mead's best-known "work" is an editorial artifact. Daniel Huebner's source-critical appendix to the 2015 edition shows that *Mind, Self, and Society* interleaves transcripts from several course-years without notice, that Morris composed sentences in Mead's voice with no source (including "Our behaviorism is a social behaviorism"), and that key vocabulary — the "triadic" account of meaning, the first appearance of "generalized other" — traces specifically to Robert Page's rewritten 1930 notes. This unsettles any confident quotation of "Mead's own words" from the main text; it is the substance of the [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate|authorship debate]].
- **"Social behaviorism" is not Mead's term.** Both Joas and Morris state plainly that the label characterizing Mead's approach was introduced by Morris and "should not be attributed to Mead himself" (attributed, Joas 2015).
- **Period-bound framing.** Mead's contrasts of human with insect and vertebrate societies rest on 1920s biology; his analogies between child development and "primitive man" carry the recapitulationist, evolutionist assumptions of his era. These are attributed and dated, not repeated in wiki voice.
- **The writer of no system.** Morris's own assessment — "That he was not the writer of a system is due to the fact that he was always engaged in building one... His genius expressed itself best in the lecture room" — is both tribute and diagnosis of why the corpus is what it is.

## Historiography

The recovery of Mead as a systematic thinker (rather than a name attached to a textbook) is a scholarly project of the last half-century. **Hans Joas**'s biographical and philological research (1970s; *G. H. Mead: A Contemporary Re-examination of His Thought*, German 1980) reopened the corpus; **Daniel R. Huebner** (*Becoming Mead*, 2014; the 2015 source appendix) reconstructed how the canonical text was actually made. International reception has compared Mead to Bakhtin and Vygotsky (Eastern Europe) and drawn him into hermeneutics and philosophical anthropology (Germany). The standard origin story — Blumer's Mead, transmitted through *Mind, Self, and Society* — is now read against the archival record of Mead's courses, and the two do not perfectly coincide.
