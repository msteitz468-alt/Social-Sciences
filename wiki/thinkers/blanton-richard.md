# Richard E. Blanton

## Overview
American archaeologist (Purdue University) who directed the intensive surface survey of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] — mapping some 2,000 residential terraces — and later became a principal architect of [[collective-action-theory|collective action theory]] in archaeology. In [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983) he wrote the topics on Monte Albán's founding, urban history, decline, and comparison with [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacán]].

## Contributions
- Blanton argued that Monte Albán was founded ca. 500 BC as a "disembedded capital" — a new neutral seat established by a confederacy of valley polities rather than growing out of any one of them — a claim debated with [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] and [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]], who favored San José Mogote's chiefly elite as the founders.
- Systematic urban settlement-pattern method: full-coverage terrace mapping at [[monte-alban]] ([[archaeological-survey]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy
Part of the Valley of Oaxaca Settlement Pattern Project network with [[kowalewski-stephen|Stephen Kowalewski]]; sustained productive disagreement with [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] and [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] over Monte Albán's founding and the drivers of Zapotec state formation ([[state-formation]]).

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Monte Albán survey densification

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, citing Blanton’s urban and regional survey; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

**Monte Albán city survey (1971–73).** Marcus & Flannery report that between 1971 and 1973 the Settlement Pattern Project surveyed the **6 km²** city of Monte Albán under Blanton’s direction — the urban spine of later valley-wide work. For Monte Albán IIIa they cite his estimate of **~16,500 persons on 1196 occupational terraces**, still the largest valley concentration but with narrowed margin as Valle Grande and Tlacolula centers approached it.

**Residence counts and elite fraction.** Blanton’s Late Period III urban survey estimates **57 “elaborate residences”** and **2899 “non-elaborate residences”**; at 5–10 persons per non-elaborate *yoho* and 10–20 per elaborate *quehui*, Marcus & Flannery calculate **less than 5%** of city residents in the ruling stratum.

**Three-cluster founding (as used in 1996).** The book’s synoikism chapter continues to engage Blanton’s reconstruction of early Monte Albán as founded in **three residential clusters** (disembedded-capital / confederacy framing) even while Marcus & Flannery favor San José Mogote’s chiefly elite as key actors in the relocation — a standing productive disagreement within the same empirical project.

**Regional SPP.** Blanton is credited with launching the valley survey Flannery urged: full ~2150 km² coverage 1974–80 with Kowalewski, Feinman, Finsten, and Nicholas — backbone of 1996 demography and [[settlement-hierarchy]] arguments.

## Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — survey history and disembedded-capital restatement

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022.)*

In the 1970s Blanton — trained under [[flannery-kent-v|Kent Flannery]] — initiated systematic surface survey and mapping of Valley of Oaxaca sites, beginning with the first complete plan of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] itself: Blanton (1978) located, mapped, and surface-collected roughly 2,000 terraces plus reservoirs, canals, roads, and defensive walls. Subsequent regional survey supervised by Blanton and [[kowalewski-stephen|Kowalewski]], completed in 1980, covered 2,150 km² and recorded 2,700+ sites; follow-up surveys directed by Feinman and Nicholas in the Ejutla Valley and Albarradas area extended coverage to 2,750+ km² and 3,150+ settlements — together, per the authors, one of the largest "macroregional" archaeological datasets in the world.

The 2022 edition restates the **disembedded-capital thesis** Blanton first proposed in 1978: Monte Albán as a neutral, newly built regional capital, disembedded from any single participating local polity's territory, established to unite previously autonomous or partly autonomous Valley of Oaxaca communities. The authors compare this pattern to later disembedded capitals (Washington DC, Ottawa, Canberra, New Delhi, The Hague, ancient Jerusalem, Brasília) and note the thesis has met mixed scholarly reception since 1978. In the 2022 book the thesis is reframed through [[collective-action-theory|collective action theory]] (developed with Feinman and Kowalewski from 1996 dual-processual theory onward): Monte Albán's founding and growth are presented as cooperative labor investment (terracing, retaining walls, shared construction) sustained by defense incentives and a "universalizing" Cocijo-cult ideology, with the authors arguing "it is hard to see early community foundation processes as having been driven simply by force or coercion since the earliest residents came to the site from different places, opted to invest their labor... and were able to enhance their standard of living."

## Basin of Mexico survey work (1966–69)

Before the Oaxaca project, Blanton worked as a field assistant and independent surveyor on the Basin of Mexico project directed by [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]], [[parsons-jeffrey-r|Parsons]], and [[santley-robert-s|Santley]] (*[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|The Basin of Mexico]]*, 1979; study [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]): field assistant on the 1966 and 1967 Texcoco-region surveys, then director of his own 4-month Ixtapalapa survey in 1969 — data later folded into the volume's Late Horizon settlement-hierarchy and Middle Horizon "primate system" analysis, which the 1979 authors explicitly compare (and partly contrast) with Blanton's own 1976a primate-system model.
