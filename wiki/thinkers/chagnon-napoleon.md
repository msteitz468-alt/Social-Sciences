# Napoleon A. Chagnon

> Deep biography: [[hubs/thinkers/chagnon-napoleon|Thinkers Hub]]. Landmark study: [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-study]]. Ethics afterlife: [[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]. Career memoir/defense: [[chagnon-noble-savages-2013]].

## Overview

Napoleon Alphonseau Chagnon (1938–2019) was an American cultural anthropologist whose first teaching ethnography, *[[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-1968|Yanomamö: The Fierce People]]* (1968), made the [[yanomamo|Yanomamö]] of Amazonia a staple of undergraduate anthropology and made **chronic warfare and valued fierceness ([[waiteri]])** the public image of that research. Born in Port Austin, Michigan (second of twelve children), he trained at the University of Michigan (B.A. 1961, M.A. 1963, Ph.D. 1966), held a joint appointment in Anthropology and as Research Associate in Human Genetics at Michigan Medical School, and served as the ethnological member of a long-term medical-genetics team project on the Yanomamö. Later appointments he records include Penn State (from 1972), Northwestern (from 1981), UCSB (retired ~1999), and University of Missouri / National Academy of Sciences membership (jacket bio of *Noble Savages*). This summary page is grounded primarily in the **1968 first edition** plus the **2013 memoir** as a Position-A artifact; Darwinian reframing and the Tierney controversy are recorded as network relations, not adjudicated here.

## Contributions

- **Amazonian political ethnography.** Graded violence scale; alliance developmental sequence (trade → feast → women); fission dynamics; village-level warfare narrative (Monou-teri / Patanowa-teri).
- **Field method document.** Chapter 1 as a classic (and self-dramatizing) account of culture shock, genealogical deception, and name taboos under [[participant-observation]] and the [[genealogical-method]].
- **Concepts in circulation.** [[waiteri]], [[nomohoni]], [[noreshi]] (emic terms as analytic anchors in his text).
- **Public anthropology.** Case-study genre reach; *Natural History* articles; ethnographic film with Timothy Asch.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Committee / Michigan formation (as named in 1968 acknowledgments):** [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]], Mervyn Meggitt, James Neel (human genetics collaborator), [[service-elman-r|Elman Service]], [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]]. **Draft readers of chapters:** [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]], Wolf, [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]. The configuration sits between Michigan neo-evolutionism (White, Service), political economy (Wolf), and structuralist kinship audiences (Lévi-Strauss as reader) while the published 1968 text remains a student case study rather than a grand-theory monograph.

`trained_under` lists the principal anthropology mentors on the committee (White, Service, Wolf); Neel was the genetics collaborator/committee member; Meggitt is not yet a wiki thinker page. Sahlins is recorded as draft reader, not as dissertation chair.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

From the first edition onward, the **title framing** invited the charge of essentializing violence. From the 1980s–2000s, reproductive-success analyses of unokai (more developed in later publications than in 1968 — restated in *Science* March 1988 and again in *[[chagnon-noble-savages-2013|Noble Savages]]*) and then Patrick Tierney's *[[tierney-darkness-el-dorado-2000|Darkness in El Dorado]]* (2000; ingested as polemic/artifact) made Chagnon a central figure in anthropological ethics debates — representation, consent, genetic research, film, and harm ([[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]). Chagnon's own strongest-form public defense is the 2013 memoir *Noble Savages* (also polemic/artifact mode): two-tribe framing, ABA and Salesian prehistories, point-by-point *Darkness* counters, and critique of the AAA El Dorado process. Defenders treat him as a careful fieldworker punished for unpopular findings about aggression; critics treat the research program as extractive and sensationalist. This wiki records both as positions, not as verdicts.
