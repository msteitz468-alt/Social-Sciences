# Glynn Isaac (1937–1985)

South African–born archaeologist of the earliest hominids, a leader of Plio-Pleistocene
"living-floor" archaeology in East Africa. His chapter in *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man
the Hunter]]* (1968) reports the [[olorgesailie|Olorgesailie]] Acheulean site.

## Overview

*(Attributed — Isaac 1968.)* Isaac excavated discrete Acheulean occupation floors at
[[olorgesailie|Olorgesailie]], including a floor dominated by the bones of at least 50+ extinct
giant baboons. He treated ethnography and primate ethology as sources of hypotheses and
"calibration" for archaeology — not as verification — dividing their contribution into three
components (terms of reference, interpretive inspiration, and testing archaeological inference
against ethnographic material). He linked the baboon assemblage to Woodburn's report of
[[hadza|Hadza]] baboon-drives as a "plausible" but untestable hypothesis.

## Contributions

- **Living-floor / home-base archaeology** — meticulous excavation and plotting of early-hominid
  occupation surfaces ([[olorgesailie|Olorgesailie]]; later Koobi Fora).
- **Actualistic taphonomy** — experiments (with M. Posnansky) on bone weathering and scavenger
  dispersal, used to interpret low bone densities without denying hunting/scavenging.
- **The [[food-sharing-hypothesis]]** (developed after 1968) — the argument that food transport to a
  home base and sharing were pivotal early-hominid behaviours. Its highest-profile popularization
  came in [[leakey-richard|Richard Leakey]] & Roger Lewin's *[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]*
  (1977), which made [[food-sharing]] — not hunting — the human-making adaptation and named it the
  alternative to the [[hunting-hypothesis|Man-the-Hunter thesis]]; Isaac collaborated with the
  Leakeys at [[koobi-fora|Koobi Fora / East Turkana]].
- A measured position in the [[ethnographic-analogy|analogy debate]]: analogy is indispensable
  for generating hypotheses but cannot substitute for archaeological verification.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Isaac worked in the orbit of the Leakeys in East African prehistory and became, with
[[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]], a leading methodologist of early-hominid behavioural
reconstruction — though more sympathetic than Binford to disciplined analogy. Documented
training relations not in the ingested source.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* Isaac's own "home base / food-sharing" model was later challenged (notably by
Binford's scavenging critique) as reading too much social behaviour into ambiguous bone
scatters; his insistence on actualistic testing, however, helped set the standard by which such
claims are now judged.

## Historiography

Isaac is a standard reference for Plio-Pleistocene archaeology, taphonomy, and the
home-base/food-sharing debate. A fuller treatment awaits ingestion of his Koobi Fora and
food-sharing work.

## Reception in Mithen 1996

[[mithen-steven|Steven Mithen]] (*[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]*) cites Isaac on million-year toolkit monotony (“shuffling of the same essential ingredients… minor, directionless changes”) as diagnostic of Early Human Phase-2 minds, and critically reassesses the home-base/food-sharing model for early *Homo* (Ch.6).
