# Robert J. Sampson

## Overview

American sociologist of crime, community, and urban inequality; scientific director and synthesizer of the Project on Human Development in Chicago Neighborhoods (PHDCN / Chicago Project). Best known for **[[collective-efficacy]]** (with [[raudenbush-stephen|Stephen Raudenbush]], Felton Earls, and others), **[[ecometrics]]** as the measurement logic of ecological settings, multi-indicator **[[concentrated-disadvantage]]**, and a multi-level **theory of context** for **[[neighborhood-effects]]** culminating in *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (2012). Henry Ford II Professor of the Social Sciences at Harvard (from 2003); previously University of Chicago faculty. Foreword by [[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]], who calls GAC among the most comprehensive empirical studies in social science and defends Chicago as a theoretical laboratory.

## Contributions

- **Collective efficacy** — social cohesion + shared expectations for informal control; elevates activation of resources over stocks of local ties or civic memberships; mediates structure→violence (*Science* 1997; GAC ch. 7). Dense ties neither necessary nor always protective (negative social capital / negotiated coexistence).
- **Ecometrics** (with Raudenbush) — ecological constructs require their own measurement logic, not stand-ins for individual traits; community survey, SSO, archival, and multi-method triangulation.
- **Durable [[concentrated-disadvantage]]** — six-indicator PCA (welfare, poverty, unemployment, female-headed households, percent black, density of children); 1990→2000 **r>0.9** locally and nationally; community-area poverty **1960–2000 r=0.78**; race–class ecological non-overlap (black vs white neighborhood income); incarceration ecology (“Punishment’s Place,” with Loeffler: disadvantage–incarceration **r≈0.80–0.82**).
- **Spatial interdependence** — “neighbors of neighborhoods”; Moran’s I for disadvantage, crime, immigration, CE; off-diagonal spatial advantage/disadvantage.
- **Structural sorting** — selection is itself a neighborhood effect; strong version: **“neighborhoods choose people rather than … people choose neighborhoods”** (mobility as inter-neighborhood network).
- **MTO critique** — mixed experimental results; ~5% extreme-tail population; destinations remain hypersegregated; social processes unmoved; included-variable bias on pathways (GAC ch. 11).
- **Broken-windows engagement** (with Raudenbush) — observed vs perceived disorder; disorder and crime share structural antecedents (disadvantage, low CE) rather than disorder uniquely causing crime.
- **PHDCN design** — community survey, systematic social observation, key-informant/leadership networks, longitudinal child cohorts, crime/incarceration archives (GAC ch. 4).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Chicago School ecological lineage ([[park-robert-e|Park]], [[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]], Shaw & McKay [[social-disorganization-theory|social disorganization]]) reworked with modern multi-level methods; dialogue with Wilson’s underclass / [[concentration-effects]] / [[social-isolation]] program (including Sampson–Wilson racial-invariance); Albert J. Reiss Jr. as PHDCN contextual advocate who enlisted Sampson; collaborative core with Raudenbush (ecometrics, CE, SSO); further coauthors Morenoff, Sharkey, Earls, Loeffler, Graif. Positions Chicago as “truth spot” / quintessential American city for theoretically driven hypothesis tests, not pure representativeness sampling.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Neighborhood-effects magnitudes, MTO experimental implications, generalizability beyond Chicago (answered partly by national tract parallels), and the causal status of collective efficacy remain live in [[neighborhood-effects-debate]]. Experimentalists stress selection; Sampson inverts the critique via structural sorting and higher-order city structure. Broken-windows advocates contest the disorder findings ([[broken-windows-debate]]).

## Historiography

Primary self-account of the PHDCN’s making in *Great American City* ch. 4 (origins in developmental/criminal-behavior framing; Reiss/Sampson push for community; Earls PI at Harvard SPH). Wilson’s foreword positions the book as a standard-setting empirical program elevating collective context over individualist models.
