# Charles Horton Cooley

## Overview

Charles Horton Cooley (1864–1929) was an American sociologist at the **University of Michigan**, a founding figure of **[[social-psychology|sociological social psychology]]**, and a principal precursor of **[[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]**. In *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]* (1902) he developed the **[[looking-glass-self|looking-glass (reflected) self]]** — the thesis that self-feeling arises from imagining how one appears in others' minds and what judgment they pass — and argued that **society and the individual are not separable phenomena but collective and distributive aspects of the same human life**. He defined ordinary "human nature" as a social nature formed in intimate association ([[primary-group|primary groups]]: family, neighborhood, play-group). Later work (*Social Organization*, 1909; *Social Process*, 1918) extended the primary-group analysis and the study of public opinion; only the 1902 book is ingested here.

## Contributions

All claims below are **attributed** to Cooley (*Human Nature*, text used = revised edition with post-1902 interpolations; first published 1902) — theoretical program, not established fact.

- **Two channels of life.** Heredity (germ-plasm "stream") vs. communication/education (social "road"); specific culture is social heritage; human heredity is plastic and teachable ("piano" vs animal "hand-organ"); long infancy is the biological condition of culture.
- **Three senses of "human nature."** (1) hereditary endowment; (2) social nature formed in intimate association / [[primary-group|primary groups]]; (3) particular behavior types of an age — the last two alterable with institutions.
- **Organic unity of society and individual.** "A separate individual is an abstraction unknown to experience, and so likewise is society when regarded as something apart from individuals." Society and individuals are "simply collective and distributive aspects of the same thing."
- **Personal ideas as immediate social reality.** "The immediate social reality is the personal idea"; "Society… is a relation among personal ideas"; "the imaginations which people have of one another are the solid facts of society"; sociology must "imagine imaginations." Incorporeal persons (dead, fictional, gods) are socially real if imagined; unimagined corporeal persons are not.
- **Sympathy as understanding.** Not pity but sharing others' mental states; range of sympathy measures personality, power, moral rank, and sanity; love enlarges rather than abolishes the self.
- **[[the-social-self|Social self]].** "Any idea, or system of ideas, drawn from the communicative life, that the mind cherishes as its own"; empirical self as **my-feeling**; group self / "we."
- **[[looking-glass-self|Looking-glass / reflected self]].** Three elements: imagined appearance; imagined judgment; self-feeling (pride or mortification). Illustrated on his children M. and R. and in adolescence as "self-effectuation."
- **[[primary-group|Primary groups]] (definitional use).** Family and neighborhood as universal intimate associations forming primary social sentiments. Full doctrine: *Social Organization* (1909), not yet ingested.
- **Suggestion and choice.** Conduct shaped by milieu and class atmospheres more than deliberative choice admits; suggestibility varies with social state; against pure imitation theories (Tarde/Baldwin).
- **Hostility, emulation, leadership.** Hostility socialized from animal anger into hostile sympathy and ethical indignation; conformity / rivalry / hero-worship as three emulations; leadership organizes undirected social energy — fame as symbol can exceed the private man; "does the leader lead?" answered by organic view (individual and society not opposing entities).
- **Conscience as social.** Right as rational (mental harmonization), often "onward," also habit; not the social *against* the individual but, in a sense, the social *against* the sensual; conscience always reflects a group and ideal persons; some functional universals (loyalty, in-group kindness, custom) despite Sumner-style variation of mores.
- **Freedom as opportunity for right development** through the social order, not absence of restraint; increases of freedom bring incidental moral strain and some degeneracy.
- **Period social pathology (Ch. XI).** "Personal degeneracy" as lack of higher sympathetic organization (heredity × environment); organic responsibility spreads blame to gang, parents, school, and influential classes; punishment retained but depreciated vs education. **Period discourse** (crime, insanity, race-caste asides) — attributed only; see source `reliability_notes`.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Cooley worked at Michigan in the orbit of **John Dewey** and American [[pragmatism|pragmatism]]; he drew on **William James** (empirical self, self-feeling) while developing a distinctly sociological account of selfhood. He is conventionally paired with [[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]] as a founder of the Chicago/Michigan tradition of social psychology, though Cooley remained at Michigan and never held a Chicago appointment. **Mead** later criticized Cooley (with James) for grounding the self in "self-feeling" rather than in the cognitive structure of role-taking — see [[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate]]. Reception runs through early American sociology textbooks, the Chicago interactionist tradition, and [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]] (Blumer and successors cite Cooley as precursor).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Mead's origin critique.** Mead argued that self-feeling experiences "do not account for the origin of the self," because the self must already exist for feelings to be referred to it; he insisted the self is "essentially a cognitive rather than an emotional phenomenon" (attributed, *Mind, Self, and Society*). Cooley's looking-glass process remains widely taught; the dispute is about *genesis*, not about the descriptive force of reflected appraisal — see [[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate]].
- **Idealism / mentalism charge.** Treating society as a relation among personal ideas is open to the charge of dissolving structure into imagination; Cooley replies that imaginations are "solid facts" of social life and does not deny corporeal persons, only their *immediate social* reality when unimagined.
- **Period science and politics.** Introduction eugenics (selection, "feeble-minded" non-propagation, race-suicide / "Yellow Peril" alarms), race typology in thought experiments, sex-difference essentialism in Ch. V–VI, and Ch. XI degeneracy/crime/insanity vocabulary are early-twentieth-century discourses. Cooley often softens hard heredity claims (progress is social process; prefer changing environment first) but still endorses limited eugenic measures — **attributed period position, not wiki fact**.
- **Method.** Introspective, literary, and small-n child observation (children M. and R.; related *Psychological Review* note). Strength: phenomenological depth. Limitation: not systematic ethnography or survey.

## Historiography

Cooley is standardly presented as co-founder of American social psychology with Mead, though the two programs differ (affective reflected self vs. cognitive role-taking self). *Human Nature* (1902) is the looking-glass classic; *Social Organization* (1909) is the primary-group classic — this wiki currently holds only the former.
