# Grahame Clark (1907–1995)

Sir John Grahame Douglas Clark, British prehistorian; Disney Professor of Archaeology at the University of Cambridge (1952–1974) and Master of Peterhouse. He was the principal architect of the **economic and ecological approach to prehistory** — the reconstruction of past subsistence, environment, and economy as the central aim of archaeology — which he set out programmatically in *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* (first edition 1939; substantially revised 1957) and demonstrated in his excavation of the Mesolithic site of Star Carr. He then scaled that program to a single-frame **[[world-prehistory|world prehistory]]** in *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961; later revised as *A New Outline* 1969 and *in New Perspective* 1977).

## Overview

Clark argued that the object of archaeology is not the typological ordering of artifacts for its own sake but the reconstruction of the whole life of prehistoric communities — above all their **economic life**, understood as the relationship between human groups and their **ecological setting** (attributed: Clark 1957). This reoriented British prehistory away from the culture-historical cataloguing of *[[archaeological-culture|cultures]]* toward the study of how people made a living, a shift often read as a bridge between [[culture-historical-archaeology]] and the later processualism of [[binford-lewis|Binford]] and [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]].

In *World Prehistory* (1961) he extended the same ecological–economic frame across **all inhabited continents**, treating regional sequences as successive adaptations (foraging → farming → urban civilization) within a shared human story rather than as national antiquities (attributed, Clark 1961 preface, Introduction, Retrospect). The book is a teaching outline: conclusions are stated without full argument, and Clark himself flags uneven research intensity and the rapid obsolescence of any such synthesis.

## Contributions

- **[[economic-prehistory]]** — the research program treating subsistence economy and environmental relations as the primary object of prehistoric reconstruction.
- **[[world-prehistory]]** — the first widely used modern global prehistoric textbook frame (*World Prehistory: An Outline*, 1961), with full chapters on the New World and Australasia/Pacific alongside the Old World.
- Systematic use of **[[ethnographic-analogy]]** and of natural-science evidence (pollen, faunal and floral remains, early [[radiocarbon-dating]]) to reconstruct past economies and to align regional chronologies.
- *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* as an early general statement of archaeological method and its social purpose for a wide readership.
- Culture-as-humanity criterion: a hominid qualifies as human by “works” (tool-making tradition), not by zoology alone (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 2).
- **Five lithic technological modes (1969)** — adopted as the organising metalanguage of [[phillipson-african-archaeology-2005|Phillipson, *African Archaeology* (2005)]]; see [[lithic-technological-modes]]. Modes describe technology without equating each stage to a fixed time period or culture-historical compartment (as of Phillipson 2005 citing Clark 1969).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Clark worked at Cambridge across the fieldwork-revolution and postwar eras; his students and the Cambridge "palaeoeconomy" milieu carried the economic approach forward. *World Prehistory* is dedicated in memoriam to **V. Gordon Childe** and **O. G. S. Crawford**, signalling debt to culture-history and air photography even as Clark reframes the field economically (attributed front matter, 1961). ## Critiques and Reappraisals

The economic approach has been criticized for **environmental determinism** and for underweighting social relations, meaning, and ideology — the charge later pressed by post-processual archaeologists. Clark's mid-century **racial and progressivist language** (adaptive pigmentation and nasal aperture in *World Prehistory* ch. 1; progress language in *Archaeology and Society* chs. VII–VIII and the 1961 Retrospect) reflects period assumptions and is kept attributed and historicized. Geographic coverage in the 1961 outline is self-admittedly distorted; later critics (e.g. Trigger on sub-Saharan Africa) note residual slighting of some regions even in a “global” book. Absolute chronology is **pre-calibration** radiocarbon.

## Historiography

Two ingested sources so far: *Archaeology and Society* (1957) for method and *World Prehistory: An Outline* (1961) for the global synthesis. Career phases, training, reception, and the full genealogy of the Cambridge economic-prehistory school still await a history-of-discipline or biographical source; a Thinkers-Hub biography is not yet warranted without those.

**Reception in Gamble *Timewalkers* (1994).** [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] opens *[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* by acknowledging Clark as the example that a world prehistory is "both possible and profitable," and closes by quoting Clark on prehistory as a more valid perspective on the contemporary situation than parochial histories (attributed). Gamble then reorients the world-prehistory project from ecological outline toward [[global-colonization]] as the central research question — a critical-turn sequel to Clark's postwar global textbook frame.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Star Carr:** Mithen presents Clark’s 1949–51 excavation as the moment British Mesolithic studies shifted from tool catalogues to lifestyle and environment; Star Carr among Europe’s earliest-known Mesolithic settlements, with exceptional waterlogged preservation of bone, antler, and wood, and the first European site of any period radiocarbon-dated (attributed, ch. 15).
- **Nea Nikomedeia:** 1961 Cambridge–Greek excavation led by Robert Rodden with Clark assisting after 1953 road-work pottery finds (attributed, European farming chapters).
- **World-prehistory genre:** Mithen’s global 20,000–5000 BC narrative is a post-Clark popular successor frame (ecological–economic outline + multi-continental scope); Clark is not a continuous interlocutor in the text but Star Carr remains the British Mesolithic type-case.
