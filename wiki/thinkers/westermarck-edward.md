# Edward Westermarck

**Edward Westermarck** (1862–1939) was a Finnish-British sociologist and moral philosopher whose *History of Human Marriage* (1891) challenged the evolutionary orthodoxy of primitive promiscuity, and whose long fieldwork in Morocco made him an early practitioner of extended empirical research.

## Overview

**Edward Westermarck** (1862–1939) was a Finnish-British sociologist and moral philosopher whose *History of Human Marriage* (1891) challenged the evolutionary orthodoxy of primitive promiscuity, and whose long fieldwork in Morocco made him an early practitioner of extended empirical research.

## Contributions

- Finnish-British sociologist and moral philosopher; critic of primitive-promiscuity theory who argued for the antiquity of marriage and a biological basis for exogamy (the 'Westermarck effect'), and a founder of empirical fieldwork in Morocco.
- (Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) Born 1862 in Helsingfors (Helsinki), then Grand Duchy of Russia; a native Swedish speaker. His doctoral dissertation formed the first six chapters of *The History of Human Marriage* (1891, Macmillan; introductory note by Alfred Russel Wallace). Already by an 1889 Helsingfors lecture he was arguing against the then-standard evolutionary assumption of "primitive promiscuity" and the priority of matriarchal/matrilineal marriage forms.
- Westermarck's central, most controversial thesis (later termed the "Westermarck effect" in modern kin-recognition research, though Stocking's text does not use that term): exogamy originated in an innate, naturally-selected "instinctive horror of incest" — an aversion to marriage between persons raised in close proximity from early childhood, not an aversion grounded in actual blood relationship. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] disputed this, holding "the reason of exogamy is not moral but political" — a recorded Tylor–Westermarck debate over the causal account of exogamy. [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]] and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] both reviewed the thesis critically (see [[robertson-smith-william]] for Smith's charge of circularity; Durkheim criticized Westermarck's attempt to "rest sociology on Darwinism" as "contrary to proper method" in sociology).
- Westermarck styled himself a "neo-Darwinian social evolutionist": naturally selected instinct, not misguided reason, explained "savage" behavior in his account, and he criticized the Tylorian doctrine of survivals as implying "the survival of the unfit." Methodologically he held that interpreting customs as social survivals without investigating their existing environment/function was "apt to lead to the most arbitrary conclusions" — a functionalist-leaning critique of the survivals method, though he never entirely abandoned survivals-based reasoning himself.
- In ethics, Westermarck held that "moral judgments are ultimately based, not on the intellect, but on emotions," and that there are no absolute moral truths — a moral relativism grounded in the James-Lange theory of emotion (an influence he traced to William James's 1884 theory, encountered via Alexander Shand, alongside William McDougall). Despite this relativism he sought comparative moral universals — a near-universal in-group homicide taboo, and a universal basis for altruism in maternal/paternal instinct extending outward through marriage, kinship, and common worship. His account of moral development nonetheless remained broadly evolutionary/stage-based, roughly parallel to [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s magic-religion-science sequence, though he rejected any necessary linkage between morality and religion, agreeing with Tylor (against [[lang-andrew|Lang]]) that Australian "high gods"/monotheism was not evidence of primal moral monotheism.
- **Morocco fieldwork.** From 1898 Westermarck made 21 trips to Morocco totaling roughly seven years of fieldwork, including two-plus continuous years 1900–1902 (his first trip's notes were stolen in Marrakesh). His method was extended residence with tribal groups — up to several months at a stretch (half a year in Fez, six months in Andjra, three months among the Shloh) — relying on local "teachers" (a village schoolmaster, a scribe, a divorced Berber-Arabic informant) for instruction in local custom rather than formal ethnographic questionnaires, and securing safe passage via the *baraka*/religious authority of his guide Sidi Abdsslam rather than a Sultan-mandated military escort. [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] is quoted (undated) judging "no better field-work exists than that of Westermarck in Morocco." He simultaneously held professorships at Helsingfors (docent, then Professor of Practical Philosophy), the founding rectorship of Åbo Academy, and a five-year personal chair at the London School of Economics from 1907 (funded by Martin White), teaching there at least one term a year until retiring in 1930; he later co-taught fieldworkers there alongside Charles Seligman (from 1910), and remained an unmarried bachelor throughout his life despite writing the standard history of marriage.
- His Moroccan trilogy — *The Origin and Development of the Moral Ideas* (largely written during the Morocco years before 1906) and *Ritual and Belief in Morocco* (dedicated to Frazer) — used the ethnographic evidence polemically: against Frazer's hypothesis that human sacrificial victims killed to ensure good crops represent the "corn-spirit," and to emend Frazer's divine-kingship theory, suggesting new kings inherited the predecessor's "divinity or holiness" (*baraka*) rather than his soul.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Held chairs at the London School of Economics and Åbo/Helsinki; a bridge between social evolutionism and empirical fieldwork. Stocking frames Westermarck and [[marett-r-r|Marett]] as the two lines of internal critique of the intellectualist tradition after Frazer: Westermarck's biologically oriented critique leading toward Malinowskian functionalism, Marett's sociologically oriented critique leading toward Radcliffe-Brownian structural-functionalism (Stocking's own historiographical framing).
