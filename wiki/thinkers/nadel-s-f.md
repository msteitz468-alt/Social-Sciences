# S. F. Nadel

Austrian-born British social anthropologist (1903–1956). Contributed “The Kede: A Riverain State in Northern Nigeria” to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) and “Dual Descent in the Nuba Hills” to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Later *A Black Byzantium* (Nupe) and *The Foundations of Social Anthropology* **not yet ingested**. Colonial applied career and Nuba/Sudan work are critically reconstructed by James Faris in *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (1973) — see below.

## Overview

Nadel identifies the Kyedye or [[kede|Kede]] as a section of the large Nupe tribe of Northern Nigeria (proper name Kyedye; he adopts Hausa/Government “Kede” for simplicity). He chose the case for its **unusual political organization**—a small-scale [[the-state|State]]—rather than for administrative utility. The Kede share much culture with Nupe proper (kinship; dialect; Islam as religion of the Nupe kingdom) yet are the only purely riverain Nupe sub-tribe, with economic life centred on fishing and canoe traffic and a political system of minority rule over *kintsoji* (“owners of the land”). Beyond APS, his ASKAM Nuba dual-descent essay (1950) is the wiki’s second primary Nadel source (linked *The Nuba*, 1947).

## Contributions

From the APS Kede claims extract:

- **Demography and dual Kede:** Main body on Niger and Kaduna; in Kede District of Bida Emirate a Kede minority (~2,225 of ~12,066) ruled the territory. Upper-stream Kede Tifin vs. down-stream Kede Takoy—distinctive political supremacy and traits apply only to the down-stream group; comparative inequality used to isolate factors moulding structure.
- **State definition (theoretical):** Dominion territorial and non-tribal (or inter-tribal); administration centralized (Muregi as centre); machinery of government monopolized by a privileged specialized body. Nadel: “The political system of the Kede corresponds in all important points to the concept of the State.” Cites Lowie and Oppenheimer; maps to APS **Group A**.
- **Kuta (chief):** Ultimate decision on war and founding colonies; judicial head; major share of taxes/revenues; state canoe display; authority rests on (1) hereditary moral sanction from mythical first Kuta invested by **Tsoede**, (2) economic power (canoe fleet, followers), (3) key offices allotted to blood-relations.
- **Offices:** *tici* councillors in capital; *egbaji* delegates over settlements/colonies; household ranks; Egba as heir-apparent rank; almost absolute authority via promotion dependence—more absolute than any other Nupe chief; weakness: no legitimate check, only feuds and factions.
- **Tax, law, autonomy:** Pre-British income tax and *albarka* trade duties; remittances to Etsu of Nupe; graded jurisdiction and “crimes of the king”; semi-autonomous vassal status under Nupe; sacred trust of “Rule over the Water.”
- **Stratification and evolution:** Integrative mechanisms economic, mythic, and religious; evolutionary sketch of how river monopoly and minority rule produced the small state (chapter §IX).
- **Mystical sanction:** Less continuous personal royal magic than some APS kingdoms; moral sanction in hereditary descent from Tsoede-invested first Kuta and river rites.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- “State” application to a small riverain minority polity is Nadel’s explicit theoretical move—record as attributed; later political anthropology may contest scale and criteria.
- Colonial realignment placed Kede country across multiple provinces/Emirates; only Bida left-bank Kede retained separate unit—administrative present ≠ pre-British vassal map.
- Rank-list chart in the source is OCR-garbled in the claims cache; do not invent office names beyond prose claims.

## Dual descent in the Nuba Hills (ASKAM, 1950)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, Nadel contributed “Dual Descent in the Nuba Hills” (pp. 333–359) on systems of **double unilateral descent**—every individual in both a patrilineal and a matrilineal exogamous descent group (gens and clan). Cross-ref Forde’s Yakö essay and *The Nuba* (1947). Attributed core (range 10; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Rarity / oversight:** African double-descent instances long recorded but only recently systematically analysed; still seem unusual though possibly overlooked. Combination of paternal and maternal affiliation is widespread; fully fledged double unilateral descent rarer (following R-B).
- **“Transitional”:** used **morphologically** (a form between other forms); whether evolutionary phase “will emerge from the discussion”—not assumed.
- **Nyaro (Kao-Nyaro):** extreme east Nuba near Nile/Kaka; with Kao/Fungur as local sections of one society via shared clan/gens list; double descent with differentiated segmentary rights—Nadel’s **syncretic** (internal solidarity / collaboration) vs **diacritical** / **symbiotic** (differentiated tasks making segments necessary to each other) framework (*Man* 1938 “social symbiosis”). Patrilineal factors heavily weighted (residence, land, supernatural possessions) yet dual identity retained; *kamerge* witchcraft as just retribution **within** matrilineal clan. Dual descent as “efficacious mechanism of social control”—only wider comparison can justify.
- **Tullishi:** western Nuba fringes; culturally/ethnically unrelated to Nyaro but closely resembling dual-descent organization; villages of patrilineal kin with **social identity** primarily from matrilineal clan; east–west dual sections of extreme social importance; conflict between a man and sister’s children over maternal rights left unresolved; witchcraft/hostility **between** dual sections.
- **Evaluative contrast (Nadel’s position):** “The Nyaro social system would thus appear to be a success where the Tullishi system is a failure”—even recognition of both descent factors vs incomplete dual recognition that leaves sex polarity as ill-regulated force. Sex-antagonism and outward hostility independently variable across twelve non-dual Nuba tribes studied.
- **Efficacy conditions (hypothesis):** combined small population + social isolation / little outside marriage + symbiotic clan/gens structure; none alone decisive.
- **Yakö comparative note:** Nadel read Forde’s sister’s-son adoption into MB’s **patrician** as a genealogical device indicating a **trend toward reduction of double to simple unilateral** organization—Nadel’s comparative hypothesis, not Forde’s terminal judgment.

## Colonial applied career (Faris in Asad ed. 1973)

James Faris, "Pax Britannica and the Sudan: S. F. Nadel," in *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]*, reconstructs Nadel as an explicit **applied colonial anthropologist**: IAI/Rockefeller Nigeria work (from 1932); Sudan appointment (1938) on Nuba control, Closed Districts, and Islam-buffer policy; Eritrea (1941); government-oriented reports; interview techniques Faris reads as bullying under police-backed access; functionalism as ideology of control under *pax Britannica* assumptions of "maladjustment." ## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].)*

- **Formation.** Studied psychology and philosophy at Vienna (musician: Busoni biography; opera company) before LSE; Fortes memoir of Nadel cited. With Fortes and Hofstra, Malinowski’s derided “Mandarins” in the seminar. IAI research fellow from 1931 (with Fortes among the first “Mandarins”).
- **Applied chores and EP’s suspicion.** Kuper: IAI fellows did little real applied work as career path; Nadel’s duties included “pagan courts” alongside Islamic courts in northern Nigeria, economic data for tax policy, and Sudan suggestions to make Nuba more amenable to colonial overrule — leading [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] to suspect Nadel might be angling for a colonial-service job. Such studies generally done as temporary diversions from “real work.”
- **1955 lecture attack.** After Nadel lectured on [[applied-anthropology]] in London in 1955, West African students in the audience violently attacked him, fellow workers, and anthropology generally, charging that they played into reactionary administrators’ hands and lent scientific sanction to a policy meant to “keep the African down” (Kuper cites Nadel, *Anthropology and Modern Life*, 1953). Emblematic opening of Kuper’s colonialism chapter for the 1960s African-intellectual critique of the discipline.
- **Theory 1951.** *The Foundations of Social Anthropology* — Weberian sociology themes (largely ignored by British social anthropologists); social-psychology background; Malinowskian message of institutions mutually adjusted to basic needs / “innate action potentials”; purpose, utility, and personal networks over structural constraints — part of LSE’s neo-Malinowskian challenge to Oxford [[structural-functionalism]] alongside Firth’s *Elements of Social Organization*. Postwar LSE lineup with Firth, Richards, Schapera, Mair, Leach.
