# Ernst Förstemann

## Overview

Ernst Förstemann (born Danzig 1822; died 1906, aged eighty-four) was a German librarian and armchair Maya calendricist who, in Michael Coe’s account, elucidated the whole framework of the Maya calendar from the Dresden Codex (as reported by Coe 2012, endorsing [[thompson-j-eric-s|J. Eric S. Thompson]]’s judgment). Son of a mathematics teacher, he studied linguistics under scholars including Jakob Grimm, took a Ph.D. in 1844, served as librarian at Wernigrode, and was attached to the Dresden Library in 1867. Coe (via Thompson) reports that Förstemann began Dresden studies at fifty-eight and published on Maya until his death. Coe frames him as the central figure of the nineteenth-century *calendrical-astronomical* thread of Maya decipherment, intertwined with—and for long dominant over—the abortive *phonetic-linguistic* thread ([[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]; [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]).

## Contributions

Förstemann’s first major task was an accurate chromophotographic facsimile of the Dresden Codex (1880; sixty sets). After World War II water damage to the original, Coe notes, the 1880 publication remains a unique record for [[epigraphy|epigraphers]]. He began great studies on the codex the same year.

Aided by [[landa-diego-de|Landa]]’s day and month names and by a mathematical bent, by 1887 Förstemann had discovered, as Coe summarizes: (1) the [[long-count|Long Count]] from the base date 4 Ajaw 8 Cumku; (2) vigesimal (base-20) calculation; (3) how 260-day (*tzolk’in*) almanacs work in the Dresden; and (4) the Venus tables (584-day cycle). In 1893 he recognized lunar tables now known as eclipse-warning tables. Coe endorses Thompson’s line that from this document “the whole framework of the Maya calendar was elucidated by” Förstemann. Paul Schellhas, an associate of Förstemann, published (1897) a classification of deities in Maya manuscripts (Gods A, B, K, N, etc.) that Coe reports is still in universal use as a basis for gods/god-complexes and associated glyphs ([[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]; [[classic-maya]]).

Coe contrasts Förstemann’s armchair problem-solving with Brasseur de Bourbourg’s romanticism: the calendrical-astronomical program triumphed as the nineteenth century wore on and was mainly German. Three surviving codices, Coe later argues, helped Förstemann’s calendrics but were insufficient for full linguistic reading without a monumental corpus ([[maudslay-alfred-p]]; [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]). Surviving Postclassic codices remain, in Coe’s summary, largely religious-astronomical in content (Dresden: 260-day almanacs, New Year, Venus/eclipse tables, multiplications)—the domain Förstemann opened.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Coe places Förstemann on the German calendrical line that succeeded where pure alphabetic phoneticists (Brasseur and others) failed. He used Landa’s calendrical nomenclature without adopting Brasseur’s phonetic program. Downstream, Joseph T. Goodman claimed independent discovery of the Long Count and 4 Ajaw 8 Cumku start; Thompson found internal evidence Goodman knew Förstemann’s Dresden work, and Coe treats Goodman’s independence claim as doubtful—while still crediting Goodman’s lasting tables, head variants, and GMT correlation proposal. John E. Teeple’s Supplementary (Lunar) Series work and Robert Willson’s pinning of Dresden eclipse tables continued the calendrical program Förstemann founded. Thompson’s own massive calendrical and astronomical synthesis stood on that German foundation even as Thompson rejected phonetic decipherment ([[thompson-j-eric-s]]; [[long-count]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Coe does not present Förstemann as a villain of the [[maya-script-decipherment-debate|decipherment debate]]; the critique falls on the *predominance* of calendrics at the expense of language. Late-nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century consensus, as Coe reports it, held that numbers and dates had “conquered” and phoneticists had fallen—so that even Goodman told Tozzer (1916) texts treated arithmetic and the science of numbers, not history. That consensus delayed historical and phonetic breakthroughs until [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]], [[berlin-heinrich|Berlin]], and [[knorosov-yuri|Knorosov]]. Coe’s broader historiography also notes that without a Maudslay-scale monumental corpus, Förstemann-style codex work alone could not yield a Champollion-like full reading ([[epigraphy]]).

Joseph T. Goodman’s priority dispute over the Long Count is the main personal contention Coe records around Förstemann’s discoveries; Thompson and Coe side against Goodman’s full independence claim while affirming the calendrical results.

## Historiography

Primary filing source: [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]. Coe’s portrait is short, laudatory on calendrics, and structural: Förstemann embodies successful German mathematical-astronomical Mayanism and the first half of a two-thread history that only reunited when logosyllabic phonetic method met historical interpretation of monuments. The 1880 Dresden facsimile’s post-war archival status is Coe’s concrete reminder that Förstemann’s library work remains infrastructural for modern [[epigraphy]].
