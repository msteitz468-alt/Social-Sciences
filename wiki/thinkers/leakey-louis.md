## Overview

Louis S. B. Leakey (1903–1972) spent nearly four decades searching for prehistoric human life in East Africa, centered on [[olduvai-gorge]], Tanzania, where he first found stone tools around 1931. He was married to [[leakey-mary]] and was the father of [[leakey-richard]].

## Contributions

Leakey's decades-long Olduvai program established the site as a major hominin fossil locality; per *Origins*, on 17 July 1959 — while Louis was in camp with flu — Mary Leakey made the first major Olduvai hominid find (later named *Zinjanthropus boisei*, now classified as *Australopithecus boisei*, see [[australopithecus]]). Olduvai fossils attributed to the Leakeys' program also include remains later assigned to [[homo-habilis]]. Beyond fossil hominins, Louis Leakey mentored field primatologists including Jane Goodall (chimpanzees) and Dian Fossey (gorillas), figures not otherwise covered in this wiki.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Husband of [[leakey-mary]]; father of [[leakey-richard]], whose own East Turkana fieldwork (see [[koobi-fora]]) continued the family's paleoanthropological program. Mentored Goodall and Fossey in primate fieldwork, extending his influence beyond fossil hominin research into primatology.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Taxonomic assignments from the Leakeys' Olduvai finds (e.g., *Zinjanthropus*) were revised by later scholarship into the broader *Australopithecus* genus; *Origins* (1977) reflects the phylogenetic understanding current at the time of writing, since substantially revised.
