# Flinders Petrie (1853–1942)

Sir William Matthew Flinders Petrie, British Egyptologist and excavator, credited by
[[wheeler-mortimer|Mortimer Wheeler]] with introducing systematic **[[typological-method|
typology]]** and **[[seriation|sequence-dating]]** into excavation and with the first
programmatic statement of excavation method, *Methods and Aims in Archaeology* (1904) —
the work whose title Wheeler's *[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from
the Earth]]* echoes. This page is drawn from Wheeler's account (attributed throughout) and
should be densified from Petrie's own works when ingested.

## Overview

Petrie excavated extensively in Egypt from the 1880s and, late in life, in Palestine, dying
in Jerusalem in 1942 — where Wheeler visited him on his deathbed (finding, Wheeler 1954).
Wheeler treats him as a figure of genius and "untiring search for truth" whose technical
field standards nonetheless fell short of [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s, opening a
"gulf" into which, in Wheeler's polemical judgment, "two generations of Near Eastern
archaeologists have in fact plunged to destruction" (attributed, Wheeler 1954).

## Contributions

**Sequence-dating and typology.** In *Diospolis Parva* (1901) Petrie ordered a
predynastic Egyptian cemetery into a relative chronology by the typological development of
its grave-goods — the founding application of **[[seriation]]**; Wheeler states that
typology "scarcely entered into our discipline until Petrie" did this (attributed, Wheeler
1954). See also **[[typological-method]]**.

**Methods and Aims in Archaeology (1904).** Four years after Pitt Rivers's death Petrie
published his own treatise on excavation aims and methods. In it he argued that "the main
structure of a book on any descriptive science is its plates," championed the **corpus /
index-series** method (standardizing recurrent artifact types into referenceable catalogue
numbers to compress publication of high-volume material), and held that an engineer's
"training of mind and sense" fits an excavator "better for excavating than book-work can
alone" (attributed, Petrie per Wheeler 1954).

**Field-practice positions.** Petrie advocated piece-rate payment of laborers to reduce
supervision needs, condemned the multiplication of trial-pits ("sondages") on town-sites as
destroying the connection between finds, and offered practical field techniques (e.g.
distinguishing mud-brick from fill by straw-impression orientation under magnification)
(attributed, Petrie per Wheeler 1954).

**Per Lucas (2002).** [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]
(*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches to Fieldwork]]*, 2002)
offers a less Wheeler-inflected reading (attributed): *Methods and Aims* was "perhaps the
first archaeological field manual in English"; Petrie's lettered grid-square recording of
finds anticipated the systems later associated with Kenyon and the
[[single-context-recording|single-context]] tradition; and, against
[[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s "total record" ideal, Petrie held recording to be
**inherently selective** ("one always selects what to record"). Lucas reads Petrie's purely
*relative*/sequence view of chronology — against [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s insistence
on absolute dates — as expressing an older unilinear-progress conception of prehistory,
before the culture-group concept made fixing cultures in absolute time and space essential.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Wheeler positions Petrie as the other great late-Victorian excavator alongside
[[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]], but on a divergent trajectory: where Pitt Rivers's
insistence on measured sections and total recording set the standard Wheeler inherited,
Petrie's reports show "almost complete absence of measured sections," and Wheeler attributes
much subsequent poor Near Eastern excavation to Petrie's influence outlasting its usefulness
(attributed, Wheeler 1954). Petrie's teachers and students are not documented in the
ingested source.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Wheeler's assessment is deliberately double-edged: profound admiration for Petrie's
intellect and industry, sharp criticism of his section-recording and his piece-work labor
regime. The corpus method Petrie pioneered Wheeler judged efficient but carrying "a very
serious danger" of "loose usage and the overlooking of those subtle variations of form."
Petrie's own controversial views (including his eugenic commitments) lie outside the
ingested source and should be added from biographical treatments.

## Historiography

This page rests entirely on Wheeler's 1954 characterization; every claim is attributed to
