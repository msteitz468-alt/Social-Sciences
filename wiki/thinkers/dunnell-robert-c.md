# Robert C. Dunnell

## Overview

Robert C. Dunnell (1942–2010) was an American archaeologist who founded
**[[evolutionary-archaeology|Darwinian (selectionist) archaeology]]**, arguing that
the archaeological record should be explained by natural selection and drift rather
than by progressive cultural-evolutionary stages.

## Contributions

Dunnell's 1980 essay "Evolutionary theory and archaeology" (attributed) reframed
change as descent with modification and proposed treating artefacts as phenotypic
traits; his distinction between selectively neutral **stylistic** traits and
selected **functional** traits reinterpreted seriation as a signature of drift.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A critic of both culture history and processualism from a rigorously selectionist
position; teacher of later evolutionary archaeologists.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Faulted for treating artefacts as literal phenotypes and for marginalizing human
intention; defended as bringing a real Darwinian mechanism to a field prone to
evolutionary metaphor.

**The Lamarckian-affinity critique (attributed — Johnson 2011).** Johnson reports that
Dunnell himself acknowledged a significant internal tension: selectionist
archaeology's treatment of artefacts/phenotypes as the transmitted "hard part" of
culture reverts to a pre-Darwinian, **Lamarckian** model (conscious or unconscious
striving, on the model of Lamarck's account of giraffe necks) rather than a strictly
Darwinian one. The approach proved popular in North America but was viewed elsewhere
as resting on a misunderstanding of Darwinism (Shennan, for instance, barely
references it). See [[evolutionary-archaeology]] for the fuller program-level
treatment, including Bettinger and Eerkens's (1997: 177) related quip that evolutionary
archaeology suffered "an unfortunate parallel... with the weather: everyone talks
about it but no one does anything about it."

## Historiography

Recognized as the intellectual origin of the selectionist program in American
archaeology.
