# W. Richard Scott

## Overview

W. Richard Scott is a sociologist of organizations. In *The New Institutionalism in
Organizational Analysis* (1991) he co-authored ch. 5, "The Organization of Societal
Sectors," with [[meyer-john-w]] (a revision of their 1983 paper, first presented "about
seven years" before this volume), and authored ch. 7, "Unpacking Institutional
Arguments," alone.

## Contributions

With Meyer, Scott defined the [[societal-sector]] as the volume's unit of comparative
analysis, distinguishing technical environments (where organizations are "rewarded for
effective and efficient control of their production systems") from institutional
environments (where organizations must conform to elaborated rules "to receive support
and legitimacy"). Their nineteen hypotheses connected a sector's technical/institutional
development and centralization/fragmentation to organizational size, administrative
complexity, internal conflict, and coupling — see [[meyer-john-w]] for the joint chapter
in full.

Ch. 7 is Scott's independently authored synthesis of the institutionalist program to
1991. He stated that "perhaps the single most important contribution of institutional
theorists" was the reconceptualization of organizational environments away from purely
technical/resource models, crediting [[meyer-john-w]] and [[rowan-brian]]'s 1977 paper
with introducing "symbolic elements capable of affecting organizational forms
independent of resource flows and technical requirements." He proposed the
[[organizational-field]] (bounded functionally rather than geographically, encompassing
exchange partners, funders, regulators, and professional/trade associations) as the
preferred unit of institutional analysis, citing parallel formulations — "industry
system" (Hirsch), "societal sector" (Scott and Meyer), "organizational field" (DiMaggio
and Powell) — as converging on the same underlying unit.

Scott's central contribution in ch. 7 is a **verbatim seven-mechanism typology** of how
institutional environments affect organizational structure: (1) imposition (by authority
or coercive power), (2) authorization (voluntary approval-seeking, e.g. hospital
accreditation), (3) inducement (market-like incentives from weak-authority agents), (4)
acquisition (deliberate, mimetic or normative adoption of structural models), (5)
imprinting (structural features fixed at organizational founding, following Stinchcombe
1965), (6) incorporation of environmental structure (unplanned mirroring of environmental
differentiation), and (7) bypassing of organizational structure (shared institutional
belief substituting for organizational-level coordination — "cultural controls can
substitute for structural controls"). Scott explicitly flagged the typology as
provisional: "The seven specific mechanisms I have detected in the empirical literature
may or may not hold up under further scrutiny as distinct types of institutional
forces."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Scott and Meyer's ch. 5 builds directly on Meyer, Scott, and Deal (1981) and cites
[[dimaggio-paul]] and [[powell-walter-w]]'s "organizational field" and Alford and
Friedland's (1985) "managerial worldview" as adjacent framings; the chapter's
acknowledgments name [[jepperson-ronald]], David Strang, [[zucker-lynne]],
[[dimaggio-paul]], Frank Dobbin, and [[powell-walter-w]] as discussants. Ch. 7 was
prepared for a 1987 Conference on Institutional Change at the Center for Advanced Study
in the Behavioral Sciences, Stanford, with revisions benefiting from comments by
[[jepperson-ronald]], [[meyer-john-w]], and [[powell-walter-w]] — a colleague/discussant
network, not a training relation. Scott's typology draws heavily on Berger, Berger, and
Kellner (1973) on the institutions of modern consciousness, and on [[zucker-lynne]]
(1983) for the claim that organizational form itself "has largely redefined modern
society."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Scott explicitly registered his own divergence from [[dimaggio-paul]] and
[[powell-walter-w]]'s isomorphism "master hypothesis" — that structuration produces
homogenization via coercive, mimetic, and normative mechanisms. Scott and Meyer argued
instead that under some conditions more highly structured environments produce
*increased* diversity of form: in low-centralized-authority environments convergent/
mimetic processes dominate, but as authority centralizes, decision-makers create
specialized forms by design, increasing diversity through coercion. Scott states this
directly: "Meyer and I argue that under some conditions, more highly structured
organizational environments may create increased diversity of form" — a live,
self-flagged disagreement within the new-institutionalist program. [[powell-walter-w]]
(ch. 8) endorsed Scott's reframing of the technical/institutional distinction as
continuous dimensions rather than a dichotomy, treating it as a corrective to his and
DiMaggio's earlier (1983) formulation.

## Historiography

Ch. 5 is explicitly framed by its authors as a revised version of Scott and Meyer's
paper "first published about seven years" before the 1991 volume — i.e., the 1983
original, with 1991 revisions. No further biographical or reception history is recorded
in the ingested source.
