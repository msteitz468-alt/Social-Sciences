# Irawati Karve

## Overview

Irawati Karve (1905–1970) was an Indian anthropologist and sociologist, a
foundational figure in Indian anthropology and the study of kinship and
[[caste]] in South Asia. She is cited in [[reich-who-we-are-2018|Reich (2018)]]
as the exponent of the view that Indian jati (endogamous caste groups) descend
from formerly independent endogamous tribal groups incorporated into a
stratified order — a position now bearing on the genetic evidence for the deep
antiquity of caste endogamy (see [[caste-origins-debate]]).

## Contributions

- **Kinship and caste.** Her *Kinship Organization in India* (1953) mapped
  regional kinship systems; she read jati as long-persisting endogamous units of
  tribal origin, a stance Reich's admixture-dating findings are presented as
  supporting (attributed, as of Reich 2018).
- **Reflexive breadth.** Also known for *Yuganta* (1967), an anthropological
  reading of the Mahābhārata.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained in anthropology in Germany in the 1920s–30s (her doctoral context
intersected the race-science milieu of the period — a reflexivity point worth
enriching from a dedicated source). Her students and the Deccan College
(Pune) tradition carried her kinship-and-caste program forward.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Her tribal-continuity account of caste stands opposite the colonial-construction
thesis of [[dirks-nicholas|Nicholas Dirks]] (see [[caste-origins-debate]]).
