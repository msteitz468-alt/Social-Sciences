# Charles O. Frake

American cognitive/ethnoscience anthropologist; at the time of *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972), professor of anthropology at Stanford University (doctorate from Yale; earlier taught at Harvard). Best known for pioneering componential, "ethnoscientific" analysis of native semantic domains — the "new ethnography" associated with Frake (1964c), Conklin (1962), and Sturtevant (1964).

## Overview

Frake's chapter in the volume, "'Struck by Speech': The Yakan Concept of Litigation" (reprinted from Nader, ed., *Law in Culture and Society*, Aldine, 1969), analyzes Yakan (Basilan Island, Philippines) litigation as a native speech-event category defined by contrastive componential features rather than by an imposed Western legal template. His earlier fieldwork was among the Eastern Subanun of the Zamboanga Peninsula (1953–1954, 1957–1958); the Yakan chapter draws on later fieldwork (1962, 1963–1964, 1965–1966), supported by a U.S. Public Health Service/NIMH grant and a Social Science Research Council award.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Frake, 1969/1972.)*

- **[[ethnoscience]]** / componential analysis of a native semantic domain: Frake elicits four successively-inclusive Yakan speech-event categories — *mitiŋ* 'discussion' ⊃ *qisun* 'conference' ⊃ *nawpakkat* 'negotiation' ⊃ *hukum* 'litigation' — via the query "What are they doing?" (*magqine siyeh*), distinguished along the dimensions Focus, Purpose, Roles, and Integrity.
- Analysis of the Yakan legal-economic concept **dapuq** (a status of legitimate economic interest/responsibility broader than ownership) as the core native criterion for what counts as an "offense" (*salaq*).
- Description of Yakan courts (*saraq*) at parish, community, and tribal jurisdictional levels, assigned by a rule of minimal social distance consistent with neutrality.
- Comparative analysis contrasting Yakan litigation (integral, serious, coercive-sanction-backed) with Eastern Subanun litigation (more often trivial, embedded in festive occasions, lacking comparable coercive apparatus) — explicitly framed as demonstrating the comparative payoff of componential definition in establishing genuinely comparable cross-cultural units, not as a causal explanation of the difference.
- Contribution to the **[[ethnography-of-speaking]]** applied to legal anthropology (an "ethnography of law" focused on recognition-conditions for a speech genre).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Associated with the "new ethnography"/ethnoscience program alongside Conklin, Sturtevant, and Goodenough (1964); his componential method treats culture as a "code for talking," with rules of interpretation analogous to linguistic deep structure. His Yakan/Subanun comparative work is cited alongside Abrahams & Bauman (1970), Faris (1968), and Agar (1971) in the volume's editorial framing.
