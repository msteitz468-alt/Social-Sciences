# James Cowles Prichard

## Overview

James Cowles Prichard (1786–1849) was a Bristol physician who, according to George W. Stocking, Jr., is the figure "primarily associated with" the founding and development of British [[ethnology]] — the discipline of tracing "the history of the tribes and races of men" to determine their affinity or diversity of origin. Stocking traces the growth of ethnology through the evolution of Prichard's thought, from his 1808 Edinburgh medical dissertation to the third, five-volume edition of his *Researches into the Physical History of Mankind*, completed the year before his death in 1849. Stocking argues that Prichard's career "goes a long way toward bridging the historiographical gap that has separated the developmentalism of the eighteenth century from the social evolutionism of the mid-nineteenth" — making him the pivotal transitional figure between the precursors era and the founding-era anthropological tradition.

## Contributions

- **Founding British ethnology as a program.** Prichard's "ethnological problem," as Stocking reconstructs it, was to demonstrate the unity of the human species against the polygenist challenge then emerging from comparative anatomy; the problem had both a biological aspect (explaining physical racial difference within one species) and a historical aspect (tracing racial distribution back to a single root). Stocking treats Prichardian ethnology as functioning like a scientific paradigm, with the *Researches* serving as its concrete exemplar and generating a characteristic set of ethnological puzzles.
- **[[polygenism|Monogenism]] as motivating commitment.** Stocking argues that Prichard's lifelong commitment to human unity originated in his Quaker upbringing and was sharpened in direct reaction against Lord Kames's polygenist reading of American Indian origins, which Prichard encountered in Dugald Stewart's lectures. Prichard moved from Quakerism to Anglicanism, touched by the Evangelical Revival, and held (per Stocking) that just as all men were originally one, God had originally revealed one true religion to all — a doctrine of primitive revelation that made human unity the foundation of a single ethical dispensation. Over four decades, Stocking argues, Prichard's explicit religious framing attenuated even as the commitment to unity itself became "the functionally autonomous motivation of his ethnology."
- **Method: language as the chief index of race.** Prichard used language, alongside physical type, religion, political institutions, and custom, as evidence of racial affinity, and Stocking documents that over his career Prichard "systematically subordinated" physical-anthropological criteria to cultural and especially linguistic ones. His historical method was fundamentally diffusionist rather than developmentalist — oriented to derivation and origin, not progress — and in his early work he linked independent invention to polygenism, treating any unexplained parallel as a threat to the unity thesis.
- **A proto-concept of [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]].** Late in his career Prichard used the observed uniformity of religious belief and practice across otherwise unrelated peoples as evidence for the underlying psychic unity of mankind. Stocking is careful to distinguish Prichard's use of the idea from Edward Burnett [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s later one: for Prichard psychic unity was the *end point* of an argument proving human unity, not — as for Tylor — a *starting premise* for tracing a uniform developmental sequence of culture.
- **Reversal on independent invention.** Stocking records that Prichard, confronted with cross-cultural similarities among geographically and racially distant peoples, reversed his earlier hostility to independent invention and began treating such parallels as further evidence for psychic unity — a methodological anomaly inside an otherwise diffusionist framework that Stocking reads as symptomatic of strain within the ethnological program.
- **Institution-building.** In 1839 Prichard promoted an ethnographic questionnaire through the British Association for the Advancement of Science, and he rated missionaries above "naturalists" as ethnographic informants because long residence gave missionaries genuine command of native languages. In 1848 he objected to ethnology's classification under natural history at the BAAS, arguing it was "more nearly allied to history than to natural science" — a boundary dispute over whether ethnology belonged with the biological or the historical sciences.
- **Chronology.** Late in life Prichard abandoned the traditional biblical chronology of roughly 6,000 years of human history in favor of the longer, 20,000-year chronology proposed by the Egyptologist Baron Bunsen, because the extended timespan gave more room for all races to have diverged from a single root — Stocking treats this as evidence of Prichard adjusting his chronological commitments to protect, not abandon, monogenism.

Stocking summarizes the structure of Prichard's entire body of work with the image of a tree: contemporary "tribal" twigs linked by racial branches back to the trunk of a single human species, rooted near where Noah's ark was traditionally supposed to have landed in southwest Asia. Stocking contrasts this governing metaphor with the "evolutionary ladder" (development in time) that later social evolutionists — including Tylor — superimposed on Prichard's spatial, diffusionist tree.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Prichard's 1808 Edinburgh medical dissertation drew on two influences that Stocking treats as running through his whole career: Johann Friedrich Blumenbach's comparative anatomy, and Sir William Jones's comparative philology — with the philological line, per Stocking, proving the more consequential over time. Prichard attended Dugald Stewart's lectures on moral philosophy, where he first encountered (and reacted against) Lord Kames's polygenist argument. His 1819 *Analysis of the Egyptian Mythology* attempted to harmonize Egyptian historical records with scripture, situating him within the earlier tradition of biblical chronology and biblical anthropology.

Prichard's chief contemporary opponent was the polygenist Robert Knox, who accused him of "misdirecting the English mind" on questions of race — a dispute Stocking records rather than adjudicating. After Prichard's 1849 death, Robert Gordon Latham became the most prominent carrier of the Prichardian tradition, publishing an explicitly Prichardian *Natural History of the Varieties of Man* (1850) and *Descriptive Ethnology* (1859), and mounting the ethnological exhibits at the Crystal Palace at Sydenham; Stocking notes Latham nonetheless retreated by the late 1850s into purely descriptive "natural history" ethnology as accumulating anomalous data overwhelmed the Prichardian classificatory scheme. Prichard's *Researches* also directly informed [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1865 *Researches into the Early History of Mankind*, which Stocking reads as an attempt "to carry on Prichard's Researches in a new temporal context," sorting the "mostly unclassified" ethnographic data Prichard's five volumes had accumulated between historically-connected (ethnological) and independently-parallel (evolutionary) categories.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking's central historiographical argument is that Prichard's work, viewed on its own terms rather than retrospectively through evolutionist anthropology, "should be regarded as an outgrowth of biblical anthropology, not only in its commitment to monogenism, but in its tendency to maintain the separation between man and the rest of animal creation." On this reading Prichard was never a proto-Darwinian: his biology remained explicitly antievolutionary, assuming an unbridgeable gap between apes and humans, and what he studied was the "evolution of varieties" within a single, divinely created human species — never the evolution of species themselves. Stocking notes that some earlier scholars had read Prichard's monogenetic explanations of physical racial difference as "foreshadowing Darwin," a reading he treats as anachronistic.

Stocking also documents the limits of Prichard's humanitarianism: though a committed monogenist and environmentalist opposed to slavery, Prichard nonetheless reproduced conventional racial-character stereotypes of his period — describing Germanic peoples as morally superior, Celts as unchaste and lacking self-command, and attributing skull and facial features of Irish and African populations to environmental "brutalization." He was not wholly opposed to physical anthropology either: although consistently skeptical of phrenology, he used craniological evidence on questions of racial affinity, and much of his general reasoning proceeded by analogy from variation within animal species generally.

Stocking argues Prichardian ethnology entered a state of crisis in the years following Prichard's 1849 death, as growing bodies of ethnographic evidence — especially "residual" material on religion, kinship, and social organization that did not bear directly on the racial-taxonomic ethnological problem — accumulated faster than the Prichardian paradigm could absorb them. On Stocking's account, social evolutionism's rise in the 1860s consisted substantially in reclassifying this same residual Prichardian data for a new evidentiary purpose: no longer establishing the unity of the species, but reconstructing a uniform developmental sequence of culture.

## Historiography

Stocking records that Prichard's work "quickly lost current anthropological significance" after the rise of classical evolutionism in the 1860s, surviving chiefly as "an encyclopedic compendium of ethnographic data" rather than as a living theoretical program. He further argues that the discipline's later institutional self-memory came to falsely credit its own founding to Darwin, "whose actual influence, in turn, was largely misconstrued" — effectively erasing Prichard's founding role from anthropology's standard origin story. Stocking's *Victorian Anthropology* (1987) is itself part of the revisionist recovery of Prichard's centrality, arguing that ethnology's Prichardian phase must be understood on its own terms — as an outgrowth of biblical anthropology answering a religiously and politically charged "ethnological problem" — rather than read backward as an imperfect anticipation of Darwinian social evolutionism.
