# Jim G. Shaffer

## Overview

Jim G. Shaffer is an American archaeologist whose **[[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]]** terminology — adapted from the Americanist Willey & Phillips “tradition” concept — is the chronological and theoretical scaffold of [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]]’s *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* (1998). Kenoyer uses “Indus Valley civilization” for the Integration Era urban expansion, and **Shaffer’s Tradition** for the long developmental process from food production through localization.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via Kenoyer 1998.)*

- **Tradition vs. civilization terminology.** Shaffer (and others) supply the **Tradition** concept covering human adaptations from plant/animal domestication through integration and later localization — so Mature Harappan cities are a **phase within a tradition**, not an isolated spike.
- **Parallel cultural traditions.** Shaffer identifies **Baluchistan** and **Helmand basin** cultural traditions evolving **alongside** the Indus Valley Tradition; Kenoyer notes further eastern traditions should appear with more research in peninsular India. Highland-related Mehrgarh phases are grouped with the Baluchistan tradition, not forced into the Indus plain sequence.
- **Four-era framework (Kenoyer following Shaffer).** Early Food-producing Era → Regionalization Era → Integration Era (Harappan Phase) → Localization Era — each subdivided into artifact/pottery/technology **phases** that may overlap via exchange networks (see [[indus-valley-tradition]] for dates and definitions as Kenoyer tables them).
- **Regionalization phases of the Indus plain.** Within the valley, Shaffer identifies four important cultural phases: **Balakot, Amri, Hakra, and Kot Diji**; Amri and Kot Diji especially contribute styles later incorporated in the Integration Era. Amri Phase: southern Pakistan possibly to Rann of Kutch; Kot Diji Phase: larger northern Indus and Saraswati plains plus parts of Baluchistan (late Regionalization ca. 3300–2600 BCE).
- **Non-uniform chronology.** Kenoyer cites Shaffer with Possehl and his own HARP dates for **regional time-lags** in origin and spread of urbanism — civilization was not a single simultaneous package.
- **Localization / Late Harappan.** Kenoyer’s Ch. 9 account of breakup into localized cultures (new ceramics, geometric seals, burial customs; ~600 years to ca. 1300 BCE) cites Shaffer’s chronology/tradition framing alongside [[mughal-rafique|Mughal]]’s regional maps.
- **Shortughai function (via Possehl/wiki MAIS page).** Wider literature (including on this wiki’s [[middle-asian-interaction-sphere]] page) records Shaffer’s proposal that the Shortughai outpost existed for **Bactrian camel procurement** rather than solely lapis access — a position distinct from French excavators’ lapis-mine hypothesis; not detailed in Kenoyer’s main 1998 narrative but part of Shaffer’s comparative profile.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Americanist tradition/phase system (Willey & Phillips) applied to South Asia.
- Dialogue partners in Kenoyer’s frame: [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]], [[possehl-gregory-l|Possehl]], [[mughal-rafique|Mughal]], French Mehrgarh team.
- Provides Kenoyer with an alternative to Egypt/Mesopotamia-centric “civilization checklist” narratives that erase pre-urban depth.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Competing periodizations ([[indus-age]], Early/Mature/Late Harappan, domain schemes) remain in use; “tradition” is an **etic** construct and must not be equated with a people, language, or genetic population. Phase boundaries and which highland sequences belong to which tradition are scholarly choices subject to revision with new 14C and survey data.
