# Henry Thomas Buckle

## Overview

Henry Thomas Buckle (1821–1862) was a largely self-taught London historian, the son of a prosperous shipowning merchant, whose sickly childhood led doctors to fear that formal schooling might injure him and who was accordingly educated mainly through independent reading, financed after 1839–40 by a £20,000 inheritance. George W. Stocking, Jr. treats Buckle, alongside Henry Sumner [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]] and the pre-1858 Herbert [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], as one of three mid-Victorian writers who formulated influential conceptions of [[civilization]] *before* the impact of Darwinism and the archaeological revolution in human antiquity — and thus as usefully distinct from, rather than identical to, the sociocultural evolutionism of John [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], and [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]] that followed in the 1860s.

## Contributions

- **A lawlike history of civilization.** Buckle argued that all human behavior was governed by law, and that historical events arose from the reciprocal modification of man by external nature and of nature by the mind of man. He held that the "laws of nature" (climate, food, and soil governing the accumulation of wealth; the "general aspect of nature" governing habits of thought) were gradually superseded, over historical time, by the "laws of mind." Stocking quotes Buckle's own summary: progress consisted of "an encroachment of the mind of man" upon the "organic and inorganic forces of nature" — the "triumph of the laws of intellect over the laws of nature."
- **Moral vs. intellectual laws.** Buckle distinguished invariable moral laws, which he held contributed nothing to progress, from cumulative intellectual laws, which he held drove it — a position that anticipated later Victorian debates over whether social change was moral or purely material/intellectual in character.
- **A Europe/non-Europe civilizational split.** Buckle held that the history of civilization divided into a European branch, in which man had subordinated nature, and a non-European branch, in which nature continued to subordinate man; he considered everything "worthy of the name of civilization" to have originated in Europe, with England — by virtue of its geographic isolation and mild government — as the closest thing to a self-made "experiment ready-made."
- **An "excursion into Prichardian ethnology."** Stocking documents that Buckle's reading of François Guizot's history of French and European civilization (in its 1846 Paris edition) and of the Göttingen historian A. H. L. Heeren's history of Asia and Africa drew him into the framework of James Cowles [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]]'s ethnology, into geographical determinism, and back to Montesquieu — producing, in Stocking's words, "a very negative (although monogenetic) view of savagery." Buckle held that "savages," dominated entirely by the physical world, possessed no "national character" and were doomed to extinction — a fate he took, per Stocking, as a model for the eventual "triumph of intellectual over physical laws" he believed characterized civilized history generally.
- **Conception and reception of the project.** Stocking records that Buckle did not definitely conceive his enterprise as a "history of civilization" until May 1851, and suggests the Great Exhibition at the Crystal Palace — which Buckle attended closely enough to judge an associated chess tournament — was a likely catalyst. The *History of Civilization in England* was received by contemporaries, including Leslie Stephen and John Stuart Mill, as a major intellectual shock and a spur toward viewing "collective series of social phenomena" as subject to general laws — an early and influential statement of what would become a standing ambition of Victorian social science.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Stocking characterizes Buckle's intellectual formation as haphazard rather than systematically transmitted: his idiosyncratic, self-directed reading had the effect of linking the England of the Crystal Palace directly to the French Enlightenment, without passing through any organized national tradition. Buckle briefly entertained racial explanation in the early 1850s but, per Stocking, ultimately joined Mill in rejecting race as "the most vulgar" of the ways of evading genuine inquiry into the social and moral influences that shape the mind — aligning him with the associationist, environmentalist psychology of the utilitarian tradition rather than with hereditarian racial theory.

Stocking places Buckle in explicit comparison with Maine and Spencer: each shaped mid-Victorian conceptions of civilization independently, related to the emerging Darwinian revolution in markedly different ways, and met correspondingly different intellectual fates — a divergence Stocking attributes partly to how each figure's thought could or could not be accommodated to Darwinism. Buckle's *History* directly influenced Tylor's 1865 *Researches into the Early History of Mankind*: Stocking records that Tylor was "very much influenced by Buckle's attempt to look 'through the details of history to the great laws of Human Development which lie behind,'" although Tylor sought to qualify what he took to be Buckle's "rash generalizations" by grounding them in ethnological evidence — antiquities, language, and mythology — rather than the historical record of advanced nations alone. Leslie Stephen later argued that Darwinism resolved a dilemma latent in the Buckle–Mill utilitarian tradition's treatment of human racial difference, allowing differences to be understood as "properties of a type, fixed for the moment, although slowly changing," rather than as either absolutely fixed types or superficial additions to a uniform human stock.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking treats Buckle as pre-Darwinian in a substantive, not merely chronological, sense: his conception of human time remained essentially biblical, and "savage races" carried negligible evolutionary weight in his historical argument. Buckle's *History* remained incomplete at his death — from typhoid, in Damascus, in 1862 — having reached only Scottish history of a projected sixteen volumes; he never wrote the volume on English civilization that was to have been the work's centerpiece. Stocking records that Buckle's reputation, extraordinarily high at first, then "declined so much in so short a time" that Henry Maine later said he knew of no comparable case — a collapse Stocking attributes to the impact of Darwinism on the associationist psychological assumptions underlying Buckle's entire project. Unlike the sociocultural evolutionists who followed him, Stocking notes, Buckle — writing before Darwin — was not centrally concerned to argue for the reality of specifically *moral* (as opposed to merely material) progress, a distinction that separates his project from the developmental anthropology of the 1860s even where the two shared a lawlike, developmentalist ambition.

## Historiography

Stocking's discussion of Buckle forms part of a broader argument in *Victorian Anthropology* (1987) that "classical evolutionism" is best understood not as a single unified paradigm but as a synthesis of several pre-existing intellectual orientations — Prichardian ethnology, emerging physical anthropology, and progressivist cultural developmentalism of the kind Buckle himself represents — assembled by Tylor, Lubbock, and McLennan to answer questions posed by the post-1858 biological and archaeological revolutions that Buckle did not live to confront. On this reading, Buckle is neither an evolutionist avant la lettre nor a mere curiosity: he is evidence that a lawlike, developmentalist conception of civilizational history existed in Britain independently of Darwinism, and that Darwinism's chief effect on such projects was to determine which of their assumptions survived contact with deep time and natural selection.
