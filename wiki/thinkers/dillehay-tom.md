# Tom D. Dillehay

American archaeologist best known for excavating [[monte-verde|Monte Verde]] (Chile), the pivotal pre-Clovis site that, in [[meltzer-david|Meltzer]]’s 2009 narrative, met the field’s standards of proof and ended Clovis-first as viable orthodoxy. Layers: secondary citation in [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] / [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]]; primary densification from [[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|Meltzer 2009]].

## Overview

Dillehay’s Monte Verde project (excavations 1977–1985 with ~80 collaborators; monographs 1989 and 1997) recovered a multi-material assemblage under peat with radiocarbon ages Meltzer puts at ~**12,500 BP** (range 11,790 ± 200 to 13,565 ± 250 BP) for MVII — non-Clovis in character, ~16,000 km from Beringia, and only ~1,000 years older than Clovis in radiocarbon time (as of Meltzer 2009; ages in **radiocarbon BP**, not cal BP). The January 1997 expert site visit (including Meltzer, Haynes, Dincauze, and others) is the landmark reception event in the [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]].

## Contributions

### Monte Verde as research act (Meltzer 2009)

- **Discovery and preservation.** Site on Chinchihuapi Creek, southern Chile (~50 km from Pacific via Río Maullín); woodsmen finds reached Dillehay; after occupation, damming created water-saturated peat over ~600 m² MVII surface stalling decay of wood, plants, hide, coprolites — excavation required non-metal tools and saturation protocols (C-3-069).
- **Economy and architecture.** ~75 plant species on MVII (more than off-site controls); ~1/3 charred; ~1/4 exotic from coast (~30 km, seaweed on tools), high Andes, or grasslands to ~600 km; >half still used by Mapuche as food/medicine/construction (ethnobotanic parallel, not identity of peoples); wild potato skins; medicinal “chaw” (C-3-070). Wishbone-shaped work hut (~3.9×3 m) and rectangular “row house” (~20 m) with foundation timbers, juncus string on stakes, hide drapes; child’s footprint; mastodon, paleo-llama, fish, shellfish, birds (C-3-071).
- **Artifacts.** Assemblage dominated by wood (digging sticks, mortars, lances) plus bone/ivory and ~715 stone pieces (bolas, cores, basalt perforator, grinding slabs, knives, unifaces, spear points); >40 distant stone sources; coastal bitumen hafting; non-Clovis character emphasized (C-3-072).
- **Publication strategy.** 1989 Vol. 1 proved natural vs cultural plant/animal patterning before publishing artifacts (novel burden). 1997 Vol. 2 (~1,000 pp, 32 co-authors): *Nature* hailed as among most impressive interdisciplinary archaeology volumes of a generation — “analytical overkill” required under Jackie Robinson-level skepticism (C-3-075–076).
- **Older MVI.** Separate area with stone specimens and charcoal basins >33,000 BP; Dillehay not fully committed — focus remained MVII (C-3-074).
- **2008 affirmation.** Dillehay et al. half-dozen previously unrecorded algae/seaweed species (seaweed on stone tool) in hearth sediment with ¹⁴C affirming age; long-time skeptic Daniel Sandweiss: results “remove any lingering doubts” (C-3-082).

### Role in peopling debate

Principal excavator and synthesizer of the evidence that Meltzer treats as having met pre-Clovis standards of proof in 1997 (unmistakable artifacts, secure context, reliable ages) (C-1-042, C-1-039). Monte Verde forces earlier departure from NE Asia/Beringia and coastal-route rethink because the ice-free corridor was closed until after ~12,000 BP (C-3-083–084). Also site-visited Pedra Furada with Adovasio and Meltzer (Dec 1993) (C-3-067).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Lynch:** doubted MVII archaeological surface; thought genuine artifacts might be Archaic mix-ins; dismissed MVI; complained visitors not free to observe during digs — Dillehay/Collins rebutted (C-3-075).
- **Haynes later second thoughts:** claimed only six unequivocal artifacts (those resembling N. American Paleoindian points); Meltzer calls red herring ignoring wood, bone, ivory, meat, strings, exotic plants (C-3-080).
- **Fiedel:** mapping/count/provenience discrepancies published in popular *Discovering Archaeology* with short response window before Clovis and Beyond 1999 — “ambush” dynamics; Dillehay clarified; standing ovation (C-3-081).
- Early critics (Lynch reworked-artefacts; Dincauze radiocarbon interpretation) also on [[monte-verde]] and the peopling debate page; later acceptance by former skeptics is reception history, not wiki-voice proof.

## Historiography

Secondary layers: Mithen 2003 chapter-reader acknowledgements; Fagan (1987/2004) singles out Monte Verde as the one pre-Clovis site that “has stood the test of time” and credits Dillehay’s two-volume Smithsonian monograph with rigor that foregrounded plant foods in the earliest American diet (attributed, Fagan; source [[fagan-great-journey-1987]]). Meltzer 2009 provides the densest participant-historian account of the 1997 visit, publication strategy, Fiedel episode, and 2008 seaweed affirmation.
