# Brian M. Fagan

## Overview

Brian M. Fagan (b. 1936) is a British-born American archaeologist, emeritus
professor at the University of California, Santa Barbara, best known as a
prolific author of archaeology textbooks and trade-scholarly syntheses that
carried the field to wide audiences. He trained at Cambridge and did early
fieldwork on the Iron Age of central Africa before turning to writing and
teaching. Within this wiki he appears as the author-synthesist of
*[[fagan-great-journey-1987|The Great Journey]]* (1987; updated 2004), a
narrative account of the peopling-of-the-Americas controversy.

## Contributions

Fagan's contribution is primarily **synthetic and communicative** rather than as
an originator of theory: he is a leading exemplar of the archaeologist-as-
narrator, translating specialist debates into readable prose for students and
general readers. *The Great Journey* set the first settlement of the Americas
in the wider frame of world prehistory and gave an outsider's even-handed survey
of the [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] as it stood in the 1980s and (in the
updated edition) c. 2003.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Product of the Cambridge world-prehistory tradition associated with
[[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] (world-prehistory frame; see
[[global-colonization]]). Fagan positions himself in *The Great Journey* as an
outsider to North American Paleo-Indian archaeology, a stance he presents as an
asset for surveying a polarized field.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

As a synthesist and popularizer, Fagan is downstream of the primary researchers
he reports; his books are valued for accessibility and balance rather than for
original data, and (like all pre-2010 treatments of American peopling) his
genetic and linguistic discussion is superseded by the later ancient-DNA
literature. *(Fagan-grounded detail added during integration.)*
