# Arlie Russell Hochschild

## Overview

Arlie Russell Hochschild (b. 1940) is an American sociologist whose program on the management of feeling under commercial and domestic regimes founded major research fields in the sociology of emotions, service work, and work–family life. She long taught at the University of California, Berkeley. This page is built from *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]* (1983) and *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]* (1989; with Anne Machung). *The Time Bind*, *The Outsourced Self*, and *Global Woman* remain queued.

## Contributions

### From *The Managed Heart* (1983)

- **[[emotional-labor]]** — management of feeling to create a publicly observable display sold for a wage; three job criteria; ~1/3 of U.S. jobs (1970 estimate).
- **[[emotion-work]]** / emotion management — private use-value form of the same acts.
- **[[surface-and-deep-acting]]** — outer mask vs inner transformation (Stanislavski).
- **[[feeling-rules]]** — rights and duties of feeling; pinches and misfeeling.
- **[[commercialization-of-feeling]]** — transmutation of private emotional systems into commodities under corporate engineering.
- **[[status-shield]]** — status as protection from others' displaced anger; gendered costs of public-contact work.
- Empirical poles: flight attendants vs bill collectors; student illustrations of private emotion work.

Study: [[hochschild-managed-heart-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/hochschild-managed-heart-study]].

### From *The Second Shift* (1989)

- **[[second-shift]]** / **[[leisure-gap]]** — unpaid domestic labor after paid work; ~15 extra hours/week for women in 1960s–70s time-use synthesis ("extra month a year").
- **[[stalled-revolution]]** — women changed; workplaces and men lag.
- **[[gender-strategy]]** — plan of action + emotional preparation; traditional / transitional / egalitarian ideologies.
- **[[family-myth]]** — shared narratives that obscure second-shift tension (e.g. upstairs/downstairs).
- **[[economy-of-gratitude]]** — conflict over recognition of gifts, not only task counts.
- Design: 50 dual-earner couples with young children; 12 home observations; interviews 1976–88 with Machung.

Study: [[hochschild-second-shift-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/hochschild-second-shift-study]]. Phenomenon: [[work-family-conflict]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Emotional labor **popularized and stretched** — Hochschild notes pleasure and risk of dilution.
- Second-shift time figures **period-bound**; sample middle-class skew (author-acknowledged).
- Gender analysis foundational; sometimes charged with residual essentialism (reception pattern).
- "Strategy" and "balancing" readings contested for intentionality and power essentialism.
