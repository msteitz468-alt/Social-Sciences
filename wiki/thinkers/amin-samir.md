# Samir Amin

Samir Amin (1931–2018) was an Egyptian-French political economist, a major theorist of unequal development, imperialism, and delinking, working in dialogue with [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]] and dependency theory. Seeded from his chapter in *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-longue-duree-2004|The Modern World-System in the Longue Durée]]* (2004).

## Overview

Amin distinguishes market from capitalism; argues really existing global capitalism is necessarily polarizing; treats imperialism as inherent in capitalist expansion (not a late "stage" only); and frames neoliberal globalization as "apartheid on a global scale." All claims attributed.

## Contributions

- Permanent [[core-periphery|center–periphery]] polarization as logic of accumulation.
- Three-phase sketch of imperialist expansion (mercantilist Americas; industrial colonial Asia/Africa; post-Soviet third wave).
- Critique of "marginalization" as false concept — regions are integrated differently, not less.
- Bandung-period autocentric development and its uneven outcomes (active vs passive peripheries).

### From "Globalism or Apartheid on a Global Scale?" (2004; attributed)

- **Market ≠ capitalism:** capitalism limits competition via private monopoly/oligopoly; really existing global capitalism expands product and financial markets while a true world labor market is blocked by national borders — hence permanent polarization.
- **Inequality numbers** (Amin's cited ratios): peoples' inequality from max ~2:1 around 1800 to ~60:1 "today," with ~20% of world population in centers; passive reserve majorities in peripheries (examples: Russia ~40%, India/China ~80%, Africa ≥90%).
- **Five monopolies** of imperialist centers: technology (incl. military R&D); control of global financial flows; access to natural resources; communications/media; weapons of mass destruction — redefining the global law of value so industrialization alone no longer equals catch-up.
- **Global apartheid** as logic of neoliberal globalization; **de-linking** as strategic alternative (national/regional control of accumulation, not autarky slogan alone).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Marxist political economy; Third World / Bandung intellectual milieu; dialogue with Wallersteinian world-systems and Latin American dependency. `reacted_against:` liberal market–capitalism conflation; catch-up developmentalism under TINA globalization.
