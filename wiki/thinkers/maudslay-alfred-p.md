# Alfred P. Maudslay

## Overview

Alfred Percival Maudslay (born 1850) was a British explorer and archaeological recorder who, in Michael Coe’s account, produced the first large-scale, accurate corpus of Classic Maya monumental texts and sculpture—the indispensable visual foundation for modern [[epigraphy]] (as reported by Coe 2012). Educated at Harrow and Cambridge, he served as a colonial official in Queensland, Fiji, Samoa (Consul 1878), and Tonga before being drawn by [[stephens-john-lloyd|John Lloyd Stephens]] to Maya ruins. From 1881 he made seven self-funded Central American expeditions, recording architecture, art, and inscriptions at Quiriguá, [[copan|Copán]], Chichén Itzá, [[palenque|Palenque]], and the recently discovered [[yaxchilan|Yaxchilán]] with wet-plate camera and casts ([[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]; [[classic-maya]]).

## Contributions

Beginning in 1889, Maudslay published *Archaeology* as an appendix to Godman and Salvin’s *Biologia Centrali-Americana*—one text volume and four plate volumes, complete by 1902. Annie Hunter drew accurate lithographic plates from his casts and photographs. Coe states that this work gave epigraphers large-scale, incredibly accurate illustrations of complete Classic texts for the first time. Coe ranks the lack of such a corpus as the first major stumbling block to early Maya decipherment: no major decipherment anywhere, he argues, proceeds without a major body of texts; codices helped [[forstemann-ernst|Förstemann]]’s calendrics but were insufficient for full reading until a monumental standard existed ([[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]; [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]).

At Yaxchilán Maudslay unexpectedly met Désiré Charnay, who had thought himself first at the ruins and intended to name them “Lorillard City.” Charnay’s account quotes Maudslay offering him priority and declining self-serving publication claims; they worked “like two brothers” (as reported by Coe). Maudslay also published Joseph T. Goodman’s hieroglyph work (1897) as an “appendix to an appendix,” despite later doubts about Goodman’s full independence from Förstemann on the [[long-count|Long Count]].

Later generations treated Maudslay’s plates as the baseline against which lesser corpora were judged. Coe criticizes Sylvanus Morley’s *Inscriptions of Copán* and *Inscriptions of Petén* for poor photographs versus Maudslay, crude drawings, and near-exclusive focus on dates. Neither Morley nor Thompson, Coe argues, mounted a Maudslay-standard corpus of all monumental texts. When Alberto Ruz’s finds and others fed structural analysis at Palenque, [[berlin-heinrich|Heinrich Berlin]] still worked from Maudslay (among other sources). Merle Greene Robertson’s mid-twentieth-century Palenque documentation, Coe notes, surpassed Maudslay in detail for stucco and limestone before petrochemical damage. The 1974 Dumbarton Oaks Mini-Conference extended Palenque dynastic history working “on Maudslay around the floor” ([[palenque]]; [[schele-linda]]; [[kelley-david-h]]). Coe’s 1969 advice that a Corpus of Maya Inscriptions was holding up progress explicitly measured the field against the Maudslay standard; Ian Graham’s Peabody Corpus later filled that gap.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Coe presents Maudslay as Stephens’s heir in field recording rather than as a phonetic decipherer. Teobert Maler’s large-format Peabody *Memoirs* plates (1901–1911) for Yaxchilán, [[piedras-negras|Piedras Negras]], Ceibal, [[tikal|Tikal]], and Naranjo complement Maudslay photographically, though Coe says Maler was a terrible draftsman and produced no good drawings. [[thompson-j-eric-s|Thompson]], who saw Maudslay receive an honorary Cambridge degree during Thompson’s brief anthropology year, later expressed chagrin (in a letter of thanks for his own knighthood) that Maudslay never received such an honor—Coe cherishes the anecdote as recognition of Maudslay’s standing ([[thompson-j-eric-s]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Coe’s question after Maudslay is not a critique of Maudslay but of the field: with Maudslay’s corpus and good codex facsimiles by 1902, why no Champollion-like breakthrough? Coe’s answer is that no one then engaged in Maya research had Champollion’s linguistic training and clarity of vision. Maudslay is thus cast as provider of the first of Coe’s “five pillars” of successful decipherment (large database of long texts), while language training, bilingual use of Landa, and rejection of ideographic fallacy lagged ([[epigraphy]]; [[landa-diego-de]]).

Coe does not charge Maudslay with theoretical error; the failure of Carnegie-era epigraphers to extend his corpus standard is laid on Morley, Thompson, and institutional priorities, which Coe links partly to inability to decipher and reluctance to equip rivals such as Kelley and Knorosov (Coe’s position).

## Historiography

Primary filing source: [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]. In Coe’s history Maudslay is the unglamorous hero of infrastructure—casts, wet plates, and Hunter’s lithographs—without whom historical and phonetic breakthroughs of the mid-to-late twentieth century would have lacked readable texts. Thompson’s deathbed-era regret that Maudslay was never knighted is Coe’s emblem of delayed recognition. Modern Corpus work and every Palenque/Yaxchilán/Copán reanalysis that still begins from Maudslay plates continue that lineage ([[classic-maya]]; [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]).
