# Peter J. Ucko (1938–2007)

British archaeologist and anthropologist; co-editor (with [[dimbleby-g-w|G. W. Dimbleby]]) of *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969|The Domestication and Exploitation of Plants and Animals]]* (1969), the published proceedings of the 1968 Research Seminar in Archaeology and Related Subjects at the Institute of Archaeology, London. Later associated with the World Archaeological Congress and debates over indigenous rights and the politics of archaeology (see [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]] for WAC context in later sources).

## Overview

In the 1969 volume’s introduction (with Dimbleby), Ucko frames plant and animal [[domestication]] as a multi-millennial, multi-regional **process** rather than a single event; criticizes the assumptions that hunters lived near starvation and that “civilized” traits require settled farming; situates Childe’s [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]] as historiographically central yet in need of continuous revision under radiocarbon and environmental evidence; and argues that desiccation-based oasis and simple hilly-flanks stimulus stories lack adequate direct paleoenvironmental support (as of Ucko & Dimbleby 1969).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Ucko & Dimbleby 1969 introduction, unless noted.)*

- **Interdisciplinary seminar model:** domestication research requires botanists, zoologists, environmental scientists, and archaeologists together (Grimes’s “On co-operation” in the same volume reinforces institutional cooperation).
- **Process and multi-origin:** domestication extended over several thousand years with special characteristics in different areas; “did not… happen only once but has recurred time and time again.”
- **Critique of leisure/starvation myth:** cites Hole & Flannery and ethnographic cases (Hadza, Bushmen, NW Coast, Australian groups) against the claim that only farming frees time for “luxury” culture.
- **Environmental revision:** questions Würm pluvial → post-glacial desiccation sequence for the Near East; cites pollen (Artemisia steppe in Würm II; increasing humidity into early post-glacial); notes Çatalhöyük shifting attention to Anatolia beyond the classic Fertile Crescent; Raikes on human-induced near-desert extension without climate change.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The 1969 introduction is a period position on absolute dates (9000–7000 B.C. for first settled farming-related events — uncalibrated-era framing as of Ucko & Dimbleby 1969) and on which environmental models were then collapsing. Later aDNA, microbotany, and multi-regional farming sequences (as of post-1969 literature) supersede many empirical claims in the volume; keep date-stamped.
