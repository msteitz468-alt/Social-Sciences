# Judith Butler

American philosopher (b. 1956), among the most cited theorists in the contemporary humanities and social sciences and a founding figure of **queer theory**. In this vault the primary-ingested base is *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]* (1990/1999); later works are referenced but not yet ingested. Butler's core intervention is the theory of [[gender-performativity|gender performativity]].

## Overview

In *Gender Trouble* Butler argued (attributed throughout) that gender is [[performativity|performative]] — produced by the reiterated acts said to express it — and mounted a genealogical critique of feminism's appeal to "women" as a stable subject. Drawing on [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s [[genealogy]], Beauvoir, Wittig, and psychoanalysis, she recast sex, gender, and desire as effects of the [[heterosexual-matrix|heterosexual matrix]] rather than its natural ground. The book reorganized gender theory and helped found queer theory.

## Contributions

- **[[gender-performativity|Performativity]]** — gender as "a doing… not a doing by a subject who might be said to preexist the deed"; "there is no gender identity behind the expressions of gender."
- **The [[heterosexual-matrix|heterosexual matrix]]** — the grid of intelligibility binding sex→gender→desire into compulsory heterosexual coherence.
- **Gender melancholia** — gender identity as the melancholic incorporation of the prohibited same-sex object, the same-sex love disavowed rather than mourned.
- **Critique of the feminist subject** — "the task of a feminist genealogy of the category of women," and a non-foundationalist politics: "the deconstruction of identity is not the deconstruction of politics."
- **Later work** — *Bodies That Matter* (materiality of sex), *Excitable Speech* (speech acts, injury), *The Psychic Life of Power* (Foucault + psychoanalysis), and post-2000 work on precarity, mourning, and ethics.

Butler reports a social-movement formation — "produced not merely from the academy, but from convergent social movements of which I have been a part," including fourteen years in an East Coast lesbian and gay community — and an autobiographical motivation in family experiences of gender violence.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Voluntarism.** The drag material in *Gender Trouble* was widely read as making gender a free choice; Butler rejected this in the 1999 preface and in *Bodies That Matter*.
- **Prose style.** Butler received the journal *Philosophy and Literature*'s "Bad Writing" award (1998), prompting a debate over difficulty and politics in theory.
- **Materialism and trans studies.** Materialist feminists and, from several directions, trans theorists have both drawn on and contested performativity.
- **Empirical distance.** Social scientists fault the reliance on textual reading over systematic observation (the standing contrast with [[doing-gender]]).

## Historiography

*Gender Trouble* is among the most-cited works in the humanities; its reception — canonization within gender/queer studies alongside persistent methodological and political critique — is itself a subject of study. Butler's turn after 2000 to ethics, war, and precarity broadened the reception beyond gender theory.
