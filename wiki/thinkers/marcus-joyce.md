# Joyce Marcus

## Overview

Joyce Marcus (b. 1948) is an American archaeologist and epigrapher (University of
Michigan), a specialist in Mesoamerican — especially **Zapotec** and **Maya** —
civilizations and a long-term collaborator of **[[flannery-kent-v|Kent V. Flannery]]**.
Her work joins settlement-pattern archaeology, epigraphy, and ethnohistory, and — with
Flannery — helped establish **[[cognitive-archaeology]]**, the study of ancient belief
and cosmology by processual means.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Flannery and Marcus 2012 and their earlier Oaxaca work.)* With Flannery,
Marcus reconstructed **Zapotec cosmology** from 16th-century Spanish eyewitness accounts
and combined it with subsistence and settlement data in Formative Oaxaca, demonstrating
that ancient religion could be studied with the explanatory ambitions of processual
archaeology. In *[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]*
(2012) she and Flannery advance a general comparative argument that hereditary
inequality is **created** by human agents manipulating pre-existing social logic —
kinship, ancestor ritual, debt, and prestige competition — rather than being an
automatic consequence of agriculture or surplus, and that societies repeatedly resist
and reverse it.

### Village-size survey (EMV 1976)

*(Attributed — Joyce Marcus, “The Size of the Early Mesoamerican Village,” in
[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|Flannery ed. 1976]]; study
[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]].)* Early Michigan Oaxaca contribution
(Marcus joined the project 1972). Flannery assigned the literature scan because neither
the team nor the field knew average Early Mesoamerican village size, yet some sampling
designs require site size beforehand.

Marcus argues most Mesoamerican archaeologists had not published **reliable,
period-specific** site areas for multiperiod sites. Notable exceptions she uses:
Sanders 1965, Parsons 1971, Blanton 1972 (eastern Valley of Mexico — paced surface
sherd scatters by period with large-scale air photos). On population estimation she
sides with Tolstoy & Fish (1973) and Winter’s Oaxaca practice: estimate **number of
households** (not surface-refuse intensity alone), convert at ~5 persons/household from
Colonial censuses, and distinguish **momentary population** from cumulative houses
built over a multi-century ceramic phase (e.g. Winter’s San José-phase house totals at
[[tierras-largas]] roughly halved when split into Early/Late subphases).

**Comparative findings (attributed):** roughly **~90%** of carefully measured 1500–500
B.C. sites are small **hamlets** (single household cluster to ~10–12 households /
~50–60 persons). Two size-distribution types appear:

1. **Normal / unimodal** — medium-sized sites without extremes (e.g. Blanton’s
   Ixtapalapa Early/Middle Formative: villages and hamlets in the ~2.5–12.5 ha range;
   no 1-ha extremes or giant nucleations).
2. **Strongly discontinuous** — one 20–50 ha “monster” surrounded by 1–2 ha hamlets,
   without restriction to a single environmental zone → Marcus treats social/political
   factors (and possible preconditions for later state formation) as candidate
   explanations, not pure agricultural ecology.

Regional illustrations she tabulates include: Texcoco Plain Middle Formative
**Chimalhuacan** at 45 ha (~60% of regional MF occupied area); Valley of Oaxaca late
San José phase — nine sample hamlets 0.5–2.0 ha vs [[san-jose-mogote]] at **20 ha**
(~10× next largest; ~80–120 households estimated by Winter densities); Rosario phase
SJM ~**40 ha**; southern Gulf Coast Early Formative San Lorenzo complex (San Lorenzo
~52.9 ha, Tenochtitlán ~23 ha?, Potrero Nuevo ~4.6 ha); Ocós Pacific Guatemala —
all hamlets ≤3 ha, some 200–300 m² possibly single [[household-cluster]]s. Upper
prestate nucleations she places near ~1000–1200 inhabitants (~45–55 ha at Winter/Coe
densities); what requires explanation is how some villages reached 20–50 ha, stayed
nucleated, and overcame fission (Carneiro 1961 cited).

## Intellectual Genealogy

No teacher/student relationships are documented in the material ingested here. Marcus is
part of the Michigan processual milieu and is most closely associated intellectually
with her collaborator [[flannery-kent-v|Kent Flannery]]; their joint work bridges
subsistence-settlement processualism and [[cognitive-archaeology|cognitive archaeology]].
The EMV village-size chapter places her early in the Flannery Oaxaca survey/settlement
program that later feeds cognitive and inequality syntheses.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Not documented in the material ingested here. The comparative argument of *The Creation
of Inequality* is contested where it bears on the origins-of-inequality and
origin-of-the-state debates (see [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]]); those
disputes concern the joint Flannery–Marcus program rather than Marcus specifically.
EMV size figures inherit survey-method limits (tropical-forest visibility; public-
building area confounding residential density; shaky continuous-occupation assumptions
for multiperiod maps such as Chiapa de Corzo) that Marcus herself flags.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Joyce Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Kent V. Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

Joint synthesis of ~25 years of Valley of Oaxaca research. Marcus co-directed the Human Ecology Project from 1973 and directed the **Zapotec Monuments Project** (recording 600+ monuments, 1972–82), one of three coordinated empirical pillars of the book alongside Flannery’s Human Ecology excavations and Blanton et al.’s Settlement Pattern Project.

**Programmatic contributions (attributed):**

- **[[action-theory-archaeology|Action theory]]:** With Flannery, she frames social evolution as changing relations between self-interested actors and “the system” (environment + culture + prior history), reacting against ecological-functionalist models that left humans as “cogs in a machine.” The closing chapter offers an “evolution without stages” narrative of the Oaxaca sequence while retaining stages as comparative shorthand.
- **[[synoikism]]:** She and Flannery treat the ~500 BC founding of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] as a synoikism-like urban relocation (Greek analogy flagged as such), with dual strain- and interest-related motives; later float [[jalieza|Jalieza]]’s IIIa growth as a possible second valley-internal example.
- **Oaxaca state formation path:** Rosario-phase maximal/complex [[chiefdom]] → Monte Albán synoikism → Monte Albán I transitional consolidation → unambiguous archaic state by Period II (four-tier hierarchy, colonization/conquest, full-time priesthood) — states form by takeover of neighboring chiefdoms, not smooth evolution of one chiefdom into a state.
- **Ethnogenesis and cognitive program:** Occupants of the Valley become recognizably “Zapotec” (their term) between ~400 BC and AD 100 via writing tied to archaic Zapotec language conventions, Cociyo/Lightning iconography, and two-class ideology; Ch.1’s reconstruction of 16th-c. Zapotec cosmology supplies the ethnohistoric bridge for later [[cognitive-archaeology]] of urns, temples, and “noble speech.”
- **Methods:** Direct historical approach for societies <~2000 years old; ethnographic analogy for earlier, dissimilar societies; monuments + settlement + excavation as mutually correcting evidence classes (projects agree “90 percent of the time”).

## Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — Structure 19/Monument 3 and a collective-action counter-reading

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022, drawing on Marcus and Flannery 1996.)*

Marcus and Flannery's excavation of the Rosario-phase platform Structure 19 at [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]] is reported in this later synthesis as the source of Monument 3 — a carved stone slab depicting a sacrificed, naked captive with an accompanying hieroglyph read "1 Earthquake" — from which Marcus and Flannery (1996:130) drew five inferences: the 260-day calendar already existed by the Rosario phase; the day-name "Xòo" links the glyphs to archaic Zapotec; sacrifice at this period included heart-removal rather than only bloodletting; the victim's nakedness anticipates later war-captive imagery; and the horizontal placement of the stone mirrors the later Mesoamerican practice of symbolically "treading upon" defeated-enemy carvings.

Blanton et al. (2022) also position Flannery's earlier work (Flannery 1999) as an example of the "prevailing notion that early social complexity arises only when leaders take control of their domains through the pure exercise of power" — the power-centered, neoevolutionist reading their own [[collective-action-theory|collective-action]] account argues against. They further contend, against a Marcus-and-Flannery-style descent-group/chiefdom reading of San José Mogote's dual earth/sky ("fire-serpent"/"earthquake") iconography, that unilineal descent-group systems ethnographically documented elsewhere (Hopi, Northwest Coast, Iroquois, Huron) typically show more than two symbolic groupings, whereas the Valley of Oaxaca shows a strict duality shared across a linguistically diverse area — and that excavated elite households at San José Mogote engaged directly in ordinary subsistence and craft labor rather than being exempt from it, unlike many ethnographically known chiefdoms. This is recorded here as a **live interpretive disagreement** between the Marcus–Flannery program and the Blanton et al. collective-action program over how to read the same San José Mogote evidence, not a settled finding.
