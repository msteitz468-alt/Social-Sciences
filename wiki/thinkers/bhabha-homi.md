# Homi Bhabha

Postcolonial theorist (b. 1949) whose concepts of colonial **ambivalence**, **mimicry**, and the **[[third-space|third space]]** are engaged in this wiki through [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]]'s application of them in historical archaeology (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000). The wiki's coverage of Bhabha is currently entirely secondary — via Hall's use of Bhabha 1985 and 1994 — not from direct ingestion of Bhabha's own texts.

## Overview

Bhabha argues that colonial authority is never simply imposed but is structurally **ambivalent**: it must be continually, performatively reasserted, and this repetition itself destabilizes the claimed naturalness of colonial power. His key illustrative case (1985), as reported by Hall, concerns a British missionary circulating an English Bible among assembled Indians outside Delhi — the colonized subjects neither comply nor resist outright, forcing repeated performative reassertions of colonial authority that undermine the symbol's claimed authority. Quote (via Hall): "the colonial presence is always ambivalent, split between its appearance as original and authoritative and its articulation as repetition and difference" (Bhabha 1985: 93); "the meaning and symbols of culture have no primordial unity or fixity" (Bhabha 1994: 37).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — via [[hall-martin|Hall]] 2000, drawing on Bhabha 1985/1994.)*

- **[[third-space|Third space]]** — the zone of ambivalence and uncertainty produced when colonizers simultaneously assert and undermine difference from the colonized.
- **Mimicry and racial stereotype** — Bhabha described the stereotype as "a form of knowledge and identification that vacillates between what is always 'in place', already known, and something that must be anxiously repeated" (Bhabha 1994: 66); repetition of the stereotype is itself a site of instability rather than simple domination.
- **Colonial ambivalence as method** — Hall extended Bhabha's framework to material culture generally, arguing that repeated material symbols (e.g., architectural motifs) can be "overdetermined" — laden with multiple, sometimes contradictory meanings — because they are not verbally fixed by their users, and so can index both domination and its own anxiety.
