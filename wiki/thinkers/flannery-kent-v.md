# Kent V. Flannery

## Overview

Kent V. Flannery (b. 1934) is an American archaeologist associated with the systems/cultural-ecology wing of **[[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]]** and, later, with **[[cognitive-archaeology]]**. On Matthew Johnson's account in *[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]]* (2011), Flannery is presented as a leading exponent of systems thinking applied to the origins of agriculture, and — with Joyce Marcus — as a pioneer of cognitive-archaeological analysis of prehistoric belief systems using rich ethnohistoric documentation.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Johnson 2011, summarizing Flannery 1973a.)* Flannery modeled the origins of agriculture in the Mexican highlands as a **systemic process**: he wrote of treating "man and the southern highlands of Mexico... as a single complex system, composed of many subsystems, which mutually caused each other," attributing the emergence of cultivation to gradual systemic change regulated by seasonal scheduling of resources. Johnson cites this study as processual archaeology's classic early attempt to explain long-term culture change functionally — and also, per Johnson's synthesis of critiques of functionalist/processual thinking, as an illustration of that approach's characteristic difficulty explaining change: Flannery's model needed an external environmental "kick" to set the systemic transition to agriculture in motion, since a self-regulating, homeostatic system does not, on its own logic, explain why it would shift to a qualitatively new state.

Flannery's seven-point summary of New Archaeology themes (as reported by Johnson) credits Flannery, alongside others, with the stress on **cultural ecology and cultural materialism** — the external environment as an explanatory driver of cultural systems — and with the "Jeep in first gear" parable illustrating concerns about statistical variability and sampling theory in survey design.

*(Attributed — Johnson 2011, summarizing Flannery and Marcus 1976, 1993.)* With **Joyce Marcus**, Flannery later contributed to **[[cognitive-archaeology]]**, combining ethnohistorically documented **Zapotec** cosmological beliefs (lightning, rain, blood sacrifice, and a "satisfizing ethic") with subsistence and settlement-pattern analysis in Formative-period Oaxaca. Johnson frames this work — made possible by rich 16th-century Spanish eyewitness accounts of Zapotec belief — as one of two main foci of cognitive archaeology (the other being cognitive development in hominid evolution), demonstrating that questions of ancient religion and cosmology could be pursued with the same explanatory ambitions as subsistence-settlement systems.

## The Creation of Inequality (2012, with Joyce Marcus)

With his long-term collaborator [[marcus-joyce|Joyce Marcus]], Flannery co-authored
*[[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|The Creation of Inequality]]* (2012), a
comparative synthesis arguing — in attributed voice throughout — that hereditary
inequality was **created** by human agents manipulating pre-existing social logic
(kinship, ancestor ritual, debt, prestige competition) rather than being an automatic
consequence of agriculture or surplus, and that societies repeatedly resisted and
reversed it. The book advances Flannery and Marcus's model of achievement-based →
[[rank-society|rank]] → kingdom → empire transitions and their concept of
[[chiefly-cycling]]. See [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

No teacher/student relationships are documented in the material ingested from Johnson's textbook. Flannery is presented as part of the same generation and intellectual milieu as [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]] within processual archaeology's case-study literature (*New Perspectives in Archeology*, 1968), and as a bridge figure toward the later cognitive-archaeological program associated with [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Johnson's synthesis of standard critiques of functionalist/processual thinking uses Flannery's agricultural-origins model as the illustrative case for the objection that functionalist/systemic explanations struggle to account for change and increasing complexity from within their own logic — hence the need for an externally-introduced "kick." This is recorded here as part of Johnson's general critique of processual functionalism, not as a critique of Flannery's empirical findings specifically.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

W&O cite [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] at multiple load-bearing points in agricultural-origins and complexity chapters (as of 2007 textbook synthesis, not Flannery primary ingest):

- **“Third-choice” seed crops:** major domesticates (wheat, barley, millet, rice) derive from wild ancestors that were harder to process but storable, plentiful, and genetically malleable—first eaten heavily under necessity.
- **Broad-spectrum revolution (with [[binford-lewis|Binford]]):** ~20–10 kya diets broadened to small game, fish, wild cereals, with new grinding/composite tools.
- **Compound vs village model:** circular-hut compounds share open storage and group economy (like forager bands); rectangular room villages facilitate expansion, defense, and family-level private storage—incentives for production intensification; SW Asia circular→rectangular shift ~11–9 kya (Beidha tracked by Byrd).
- **Deh Luran / Ali Kosh** (Hole, Flannery & Neely): early Neolithic plant/animal packages on the Iranian steppe.
- **Guilá Naquitz systems view** (Reynolds simulation in Flannery volume): cultivation densifies reliable plants under wet/dry and population pressures within a foraging calculus.
- **Tribe ceremonial/prestige elaborations** cited among sources for the tribe type in the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]; “Cultural Evolution of Civilizations” noted in their bibliography.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Acknowledgements:** Mithen lists Flannery among key readers/advisors of the manuscript (preface) — reception node for Near Eastern and Mesoamerican agricultural archaeology in the book’s network.
- **Circular→rectangular / family surplus:** Flannery argues the shift from small circular PPNA huts to large multi-room rectangular PPNB buildings reflects move from group-oriented surplus pooling to families as key social units owning/storing surplus (via Mithen, ch. 9; Beidha as illustration).
- **Reliability motive (Guilá Naquitz → New Guinea parallel):** lacking population-pressure evidence for Kuk Swamp wetland investment, Mithen favors Flannery-style reliability — clearing/ditching secured high, seasonally guaranteed plant yields enabling social aggregation (social as much as nutritional motives; parallel to Göbekli wheat feeding) (attributed, ch. 37).

Does not replace primary Flannery monographs still outstanding. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## Ucko & Dimbleby 1969 — Iran / Near East ecological program paper

*(Attributed — Kent V. Flannery, in *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969|The Domestication and Exploitation of Plants and Animals]]* 1969. Dates uncalibrated-era as of Flannery 1969.)*

Landmark English-language statement of Flannery’s **ecological / density-equilibrium** account of early Near Eastern [[domestication]], written from the Deh Luran (Ali Kosh) program with Frank Hole and others.

### Setting and adaptive eras

- Near East as a **“vertical economy”** altitude mosaic (high plateau, intermontane valleys, piedmont-steppe, alluvial desert) enabling zone exchanges — analogous to Mesoamerica and the Central Andes.
- Three **farming-adaptation** eras (not polity stages): (1) semi-nomadic hunting-gathering to ~10,000–8000 B.C.; (2) early dry-farming and caprine domestication to ~5500 B.C. (emmer, two-row hulled barley, goats, sheep; mixed wild/domestic cereal traits; early hornless sheep); (3) expanded cultivars (bread wheat, six-row barley, pulses, linseed), cattle, pigs, dogs, irrigation where feasible — lowland walled towns ~3000 B.C. Stages can be simultaneous (tiny Kurdistan villages vs 32-acre [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]]).

### Core model

- Rejects late/post-Pleistocene **environmental forcing** as main driver for much of SW Asia; adopts **population pressure / density equilibrium** (with [[binford-lewis|Binford]]): pressures for new foods strongest on **margins** of growth centres, not in optima.
- Coins / defines the **[[broad-spectrum-revolution]]** (before ~20,000 B.C.): fish, invertebrates, waterfowl, plants added while ungulates may still dominate meat weight; only context in which first domestication could occur.
- **Harlan–Zohary logic:** why farm where wild cereal stands are already field-dense? Cultivation as artificial density **adjacent to** the optimum zone; earliest morphologically domestic emmer often in marginal habitats (Wadi Araba, Khuzistan steppe).
- Sedentary **wild-cereal** collectors (Natufian; Tell Mureybat ~8000 B.C. wild barley/einkorn as of 1969) weaken domestication pressure in the habitat core.
- Early dry-farming **not** a clear dietary improvement or stability gain under erratic rainfall; caprine herding as **“banking”** unpredictable surpluses in live storage (with storage and exchange).
- **Irrigation** as third peripheral innovation (earliest evidence on Khuzistan steppe, not well-watered uplands); flax seed size and settlement alignments as proxies; salinity → barley strategy.
- Real consequences of domestication (his list): change means of production; enable atypical divisions of labour; foundations for stratification by shrinking optimum land while population expands; increased environmental destruction so return to former subsistence is nearly impossible. “Second cybernetics” (Maruyama): minor ~8000 B.C. deviation amplified by ~5000 B.C.

### Ali Kosh empirical spine

Bus Mordeh / Ali Kosh / Mohammad Jaffar phases (~7500–5600 B.C.): ~45,000 carbonized seeds / ~40 plant spp. (Helbaek); >10,000 bones / ~35 animal spp. Bus Mordeh: cereals ~3% of seeds but ~1/3 plant-food weight; wild legumes dominate seed counts; wild ungulates dominate meat weight — hunting and wild plants are **major strategies**, not “supplements.” Cultivation weeds out native annual legumes/grasses, introduces mountain crop weeds, increases *Prosopis*, shifts legumes toward caprine fodder by ~6000 B.C. — anthropogenic vegetation change can block return to pure gathering.
