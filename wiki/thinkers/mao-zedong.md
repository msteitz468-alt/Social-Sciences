# Mao Zedong

Mao Zedong (Mao Tse-tung; 1893–1976), principal leader of the Chinese Communist Party and of the People's Republic of China from 1949 until his death. He appears in this wiki **not as a social scientist** but as a **revolutionary theorist and state ideologist** whose formulations — especially on peasantry, contradiction, mass political work, and protracted people's war — entered global political and academic vocabularies, and whose mass-circulated catechism *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations from Chairman Mao]]* (1964/1966) is a primary artifact of twentieth-century political education and personality cult.

Primary source so far ingested: the Little Red Book anthology only. Claims below from that anthology are **attributed to Mao as packaged 1964–66**, not validated against full critical editions or historiography.

## Overview

Mao's writings (as anthologized) present Marxism-Leninism adapted to a semi-colonial, semi-feudal, overwhelmingly peasant China: industrial proletariat as "leading force," peasantry as principal mass base, Party-army-united-front as "three main weapons," and continuous class and ideological struggle after the transfer of ownership. The Little Red Book compresses decades of texts into maxims for workers, peasants, soldiers, and cadres.

## Contributions (from the ingested anthology — attributed)

- **Party and leadership.** Mao argues that revolution requires a Marxist-Leninist party; that the CCP is the "core of leadership"; and that Party, people's army under the Party, and united front are the three main weapons of victory (*On the People's Democratic Dictatorship*, 1949, as quoted in ch. 1).
- **Class analysis and permanent struggle.** Friends/enemies analysis by economic status (*Analysis of the Classes in Chinese Society*, 1926); revolution as violent class overthrow (Hunan Report, 1927); claim that after socialist ownership transformation, class and ideological struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie "will continue to be long and tortuous" (1957) — see [[class-struggle-under-socialism-debate]].
- **Peasantry.** Peasant uprisings as "real motive force" of Chinese feudal history; co-operative transformation and poor/lower-middle peasant dominance in rural socialism (1955 notes) — links [[peasantry-as-class]].
- **[[contradictions-among-the-people]].** Two types of social contradiction (enemy vs people); different methods (dictatorship vs democracy/persuasion); national bourgeoisie as dual-character case (1957).
- **[[mass-line]].** "From the masses, to the masses" as basic leadership method and as "Marxist theory of knowledge" (1943).
- **[[peoples-war]] and paper tigers.** War of the masses; dual strategic/tactical appraisal of imperialism and reactionaries.
- **Epistemology of practice.** Knowledge from production struggle, class struggle, and scientific experiment; theory tested and completed only in practice (*On Practice*; *Where Do Correct Ideas Come From?*, 1963).
- **Culture as weapon.** Literature and art to "fit well into the whole revolutionary machine" (Yenan Forum, 1942).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Self-positioned as inheritor of [[marx-karl|Marx]], [[engels-friedrich|Engels]], [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]], and [[stalin-joseph|Stalin]] ("Marxism-Leninism"), applied to Chinese conditions ("shooting the arrow at the target"). Official Chinese framing called the result **Mao Zedong Thought**; Western and third-world revolutionary usage often labels it **[[maoism]]**. Lin Biao's 1966 foreword claims Mao brought Marxism-Leninism to a "completely new stage" — a **cult-era elevation**, not a scholarly genealogy.

No academic *trained_under* / *trained* chain in the social-science sense; transmission is party schooling, rectification campaigns, and mass study of quotations.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Doctrinal:** Continuity vs rupture with classical Marxism and with Soviet "revisionism" (Mao's own term for opponents) remains contested; this anthology is one side's catechism.
- **Historical:** Social-scientific and historiographical literatures dispute Mao's claims about rural class structure, famine and campaign outcomes, Cultural Revolution violence, and the success of mass-line practice vs commandism — none of that evidence is *in* this source; do not launder it from the maxims.
- **Cult and source reliability:** The Little Red Book is an instrument of personality cult and political mobilization; Lin Biao's foreword was later discarded after 1971. Use as evidence of **what the regime taught**, not of Mao's full corpus unedited.
- **Ethics and violence:** Maxims valorizing struggle, dictatorship over enemies, and cultural subordination to politics are political instructions with historical human costs documented outside this text; the wiki records the instructions as attributed doctrine, not as endorsement.
