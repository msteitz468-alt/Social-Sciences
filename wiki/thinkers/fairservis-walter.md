# Walter A. Fairservis, Jr.

## Overview

Walter A. Fairservis, Jr. (1924–1994) was an American archaeologist of South Asia and the Indus region. [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]] is dedicated in part to his memory and cites him on highland early villages, urban population scale, script/graffiti, Indus origins and decline, and the Allahdino jewelry hoard.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] 1998.)*

- **Kili Gul Mohammed.** Kenoyer cites Fairservis for the only known highland Quetta-valley site with possible early cereal exploitation: mud houses, sheep/goat/cattle bones, sickle-gloss blades, ground stone; aceramic lowest levels; early highland villages otherwise largely unexcavated as of Kenoyer’s writing.
- **Mohenjo-daro population estimate.** Fairservis proposed **~41,250** people in Mohenjo-daro’s **lower town**, calculated as **76.6 ha** of the total site from house density in excavated areas (published via Possehl ed. 1979). Kenoyer **refines** this: inhabited area actually **>250 ha** and may have held considerably more people, but continuous full occupancy is unlikely; population probably fluctuated with festivals and trading seasons.
- **Allahdino hoard (1975–76).** Fairservis’s small-site excavations near Karachi recovered one of only five major Indus-region jewelry hoards: folded belt/necklace of **36 long carnelian beads** with bronze spacers (Kenoyer estimates ~480 person-days for the belt alone), multistrand silver bead necklaces, silver wire coils/rings, agate beads, gold-foil copper bead, and gold scraps prepared for remelting. Long carnelian of this type also appears in Ur royal burials — a [[meluhha]] / Gulf–Mesopotamia link. Kenoyer argues Allahdino is too small to support a resident jeweler; more plausible are hereditary family ornaments hidden for safekeeping and never reclaimed.
- **Script and language.** Endnotes and text cite Fairservis on **Allahdino graffiti**, Scientific American script overview, writing direction, and decipherment attempts (including “Language X”). Kenoyer places Fairservis among serious scholarly contributors while holding that **no** published full decipherment meets academic standards. Fairservis is also cited alongside Parpola on script direction/media.
- **Origins, character, decline.** Chapter notes list Fairservis among core secondary authorities on Indus origins and decline (with Possehl, Mughal, Wheeler, etc.).
- **Polity / temples.** Possehl’s synthesis (on this wiki’s [[indus-religion]] page) already records Fairservis 1986 on absence of temples not equating to mere chiefdom-level organization; Kenoyer’s multi-elite city-state model is in the same broad postwar American reaction against Wheeler–Piggott despotism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Postwar American Indus field; dialogue with [[dales-george-f|George F. Dales, Jr.]], [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]], [[possehl-gregory-l|Possehl]], and script specialists including [[parpola-asko|Asko Parpola]].
- Field program at small sites (Allahdino) complementary to metropolitan digs at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kenoyer explicitly revises Fairservis’s Mohenjo-daro area/population figures upward while rejecting continuous maximum occupancy. Fairservis’s script readings are included among the 50+ unmet decipherment claims. Hoard-as-family-treasure is Kenoyer’s interpretation of Fairservis’s find, not Fairservis’s alone.

## Historiography

Cited throughout Kenoyer 1998 endnotes and case studies; own monographs not yet ingested as primary sources. Dual memorialization with Dales in Kenoyer’s dedication marks him as a founding figure of the American research generation Kenoyer inherits.
