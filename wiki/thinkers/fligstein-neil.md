# Neil Fligstein

American economic and organizational sociologist; co-author (with [[mcadam-doug|Doug McAdam]]) of *[[fligstein-mcadam-theory-of-fields-2012|A Theory of Fields]]* (2012), the programmatic statement of [[strategic-action-fields-theory]]. Earlier work on the multidivisional firm and *The Transformation of Corporate Control* (1990) and markets-as-fields (*Architecture of Markets*, 2001) supplied much of the economic-sociology side of the SAF synthesis. Articulated the theory of [[social-skill]] in pieces that fed the joint book (notably *Sociological Theory* 2001).

## Overview

Fligstein's career links firm strategy, market formation, and European political economy to a general theory of mesolevel order. With McAdam, he treats markets, industries, and policy domains as [[strategic-action-field|strategic action fields]] structured by incumbents, challengers, [[social-skill|skilled]] framing, and ties to proximate state and nonstate fields.

## Contributions

- **Markets as fields** — firms and states co-construct market order; control projects stabilize competition (pre-2012 monographs; integrated into SAF).
- **[[social-skill]]** — capacity to induce cooperation by fashioning shared meanings and identities (core microfoundation of SAF theory).
- **[[strategic-action-fields-theory]]** — with McAdam: unified account of field emergence, settlement, crisis, and re-settlement across movements, organizations, and markets.
- **A power-based theory of [[organizational-field|organizational fields]] (1991).** In "The Structural Transformation of American Industry" ([[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]], ch. 13), a study of diversification among the 100 largest US firms 1919–1979, Fligstein defines the organizational field as "a construction of powerful organizations that is based on the interests of those organizations," explicitly distinguishing this from "recent institutional theorists, which stress the normative aspects of organizational fields" (attributed). He argues institutionalists have "overestimated the role of norms in the construction of organizational fields and underestimated the relative power of actors in organizations" — an internal critique of [[new-institutionalism]] aimed in part at [[meyer-john-w|Meyer and Rowan]] and [[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio and Powell]]'s own 1983 isomorphism argument. The chapter treats organizations as nested within three institutional spheres — internal authority structure, the organizational field, and the state — and finds CEOs' marketing/finance backgrounds shaped which diversification strategies ("conceptions of control") came to dominate their industries.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborative genesis with McAdam from late-1980s Arizona conversations (*Freedom Summer* / corporate control projects). Engages [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] field theory, DiMaggio–Powell institutionalism, White/network economic sociology, and social-movement opportunity/framing literature without identity to any one. Students and Arizona/Berkeley teaching networks noted in the 2012 acknowledgments (names not yet fully mapped in this vault).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

SAF theory's reception (boundary criteria for a field; distinctiveness vs Bourdieu and new institutionalism; scope of "social skill") belongs primarily on [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] and the Theory Hub. Fligstein-specific firm/market findings are not yet restudied in this collection beyond the 2012 synthesis.

## Historiography

Primary self-account: 2012 Acknowledgments and Ch. 7 "So What Is New Here?" on the long path from a 1990 draft manuscript through circulated political-cultural papers to the 2011 *Sociological Theory* summary and 2012 book.
