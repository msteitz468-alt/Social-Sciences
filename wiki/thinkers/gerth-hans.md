# Hans Gerth

Hans Heinrich Gerth (1908–1978) was a German-born sociologist, a refugee from
Nazi Germany who taught at the University of Wisconsin. A student of Karl
Mannheim, Gerth was the conduit through which the German classical tradition of
[[weber-max|Weber]] and [[marx-karl|Marx]] reached his student [[mills-c-wright|C.
Wright Mills]]. Their collaboration produced the standard English anthology *From
Max Weber: Essays in Sociology* (1946) and *Character and Social Structure* (1953).

## Overview

Gerth combined Weberian interpretive sociology with the sociology of knowledge he
absorbed from Mannheim. Russell Jacoby records that despite his learning he "found
little success in the United States"; when he returned to Germany in 1971 he was
received without enthusiasm. His lasting influence in the Anglophone world runs
chiefly through his translations of Weber and through Mills.

## Contributions

- Co-translated and edited *From Max Weber: Essays in Sociology* (1946) with
  [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]], the standard English Weber anthology for a generation —
  the principal channel through which Weber's political and comparative sociology entered
  postwar American social science.
- Co-authored *Character and Social Structure* (1953) with Mills, a synthesis of Weberian
  social structure with social psychology.
- Transmitted the German classical tradition (Marx, Weber, and Mannheim's sociology of
  knowledge) into American sociology, decisively shaping [[mills-white-collar-1951|White
  Collar]] and [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s wider program.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Gerth's own scholarly reputation remained modest; he is remembered chiefly as a translator
and collaborator rather than an independent theorist. The Gerth–Mills partnership itself has
drawn scholarly attention over the division of credit and the strains between the "unworldly
German" and his "go-getter Texan" student (Jacoby 2002). A fuller reappraisal awaits ingestion
of Gerth's own works.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained under **Karl Mannheim** in Germany; carried the Marx–Weber–Mannheim line
into American sociology; taught and collaborated with [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright
Mills]] at Wisconsin, decisively shaping the theoretical framework of *White
Collar* and *The Power Elite*.

## Historiography

Gerth is remembered largely in his collaborative and translational capacity; the
account here rests on Russell Jacoby's afterword to the Fiftieth Anniversary
