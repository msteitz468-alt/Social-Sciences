# William Graham Sumner

## Overview

**William Graham Sumner** (1840–1910) was the leading American exponent of
[[social-darwinism|social Darwinism]] and, through his long Yale professorship (Political
and Social Science, from 1872), a founder of academic sociology in the United States —
credited with teaching one of the first American courses to use the name "sociology," on
the basis of [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]'s *The Study of Sociology*. Originally ordained
in the Episcopal Church, he left the ministry for the academy and became the era's most
combative public advocate of laissez-faire economics, hard money, free trade, and
anti-imperialism (his 1899 essay "The Conquest of the United States by Spain"). This wiki
holds one of his works, the late masterwork *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* (1906).

## Contributions

Sumner's durable contribution is a **vocabulary for the sociology of custom**, coined or
fixed in *Folkways*:

- **[[folkways]]** — habitual, unconsciously-generated group usages ("use and wont").
- **[[mores]]** — folkways carrying a judgment of societal welfare; Sumner revived the
  Latin word as an English technical term (distinct from, and building on,
  [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]]'s *mœurs*), and is the reason "mores" is standard
  sociological English.
- **[[ethnocentrism]]** — his coinage, now central across sociology and social psychology.
- **[[in-group-out-group|in-group / out-group]]** ("we-group / others-group") — his
  coinage, with the (contested) thesis that internal peace and external hostility are
  correlative.
- **[[antagonistic-cooperation]]** and **[[societal-selection]]** — his terms for
  cooperation-within-conflict and selection among mores and groups.

His central thesis: [[folkways]] and [[mores]], not reason, philosophy, or legislation, are
the fundamental societal force — *"the folkways are the widest, most fundamental, and most
important operation by which the interests of men in groups are served"* (Folkways §40) —
with the corollary that *"the mores can make anything right"* and that reform which runs
against the mores fails.

## On Education, History, and Life Policy

*Folkways*' closing chapters (XIX–XX) extend the mores thesis reflexively. Sumner attacked
the *"superstition of education"* (§699): formal schooling addresses the intellect, but
character and the mores are *"borne on the mores... taken in from the habits and atmosphere
of a school, not from the school text-books"* — so schooling cannot directly reform custom.
He anticipated the politicization of curricula: any group with power over schools imposes an
*"orthodoxy,"* and the true aim of education is the *"critical faculty"* — *"all our
knowledge is subject to unlimited verification and revision"* (§§701–702). His
**historiographical relativism** is sharp: *"No historian ever gets out of the mores of his
own society of origin"*; *"a complete and unbiased history hardly exists. It may be a moral
impossibility"* (§704) — and every nation writes itself as *"the torchbearer of
civilization."* He concluded that what social science needs is a *"history of the mores"*
(§711) — which *Folkways* is meant to supply.

The book's summation (Ch. XX) poses the **virtue policy vs. success policy** antithesis,
using Renaissance Italy — *virtù*, *onore*, Machiavelli read as *codifying* observed
practice — as the type-case of a society openly choosing success over virtue, and drawing
an explicit 1906 parallel to *"the man-who-can-do-things"* in American finance and politics.
Its final paragraph (§728) is not relativist nihilism but a conservative, functional
convergence claim: *"any morality is better than moral anarchy... If all try the policy of
dishonesty, the result will be the firmest conviction that honesty is the best policy...
In so far as [the mores] reach correct results the virtue policy proves to be the only
success policy."* This qualifier — matched by Ch. XV's closing *"the mores sometimes becloud
the judgment, but they more often guide it"* — is essential to reading Sumner's relativism
accurately: the mores are a slow, self-correcting, quasi-selective system, not a warrant for
"anything goes."

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Formative influence:** [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]], whose *Study of Sociology*
  Sumner adopted as a text (provoking a famous academic-freedom clash with Yale president
  Noah Porter, who objected to Spencer's irreligion). Sumner's system is the American,
  custom-centered branch of [[social-evolutionism]].
- **Student:** Albert Galloway Keller, who systematized Sumner's
  approach and completed the posthumous four-volume *The Science of Society* (1927),
  carrying "the science of the mores" into the next generation at Yale.
- **Contemporaries and rivals:** the reform sociologists (Lester Frank Ward, Sumner's great
  antagonist on the possibility and desirability of planned social change) and, in Europe,
  the roughly parallel anti-voluntarism of [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s
  [[social-fact|social fact]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **The relativism/hierarchy tension.** Sumner coined the anti-bias concept
  [[ethnocentrism]] and argued a thoroughgoing relativism of the mores (Folkways §65: "for
  the people of a time and place, their own mores are always good"), yet framed his global
  material through a confident evolutionary ranking of "nature peoples" and endorsed a
  Galtonian grading of human "societal value" (§§47–51). The coiner of the concept exhibits
  the bias — the central critique of *Folkways* (see Reflexivity).
- **Anti-reform politics.** His social Darwinism and the "stateways cannot change folkways"
  thesis were contested in his own day by Ward and later refuted in practice by the welfare
  state and legislated changes in custom.
- **Reappraisal.** The descriptive vocabulary was cleanly separated from the discredited
  social Darwinism by mid-20th-century sociology, which kept folkways, mores, ethnocentrism,
  and in-group/out-group while discarding the evolutionary politics.

## Reflexivity

*Folkways* was produced by a Gilded-Age Yale professor defending laissez-faire against
Progressive reform, working entirely from second-hand, salvage-era ethnography and the
racial-evolutionary common sense of 1900s scholarship. Its illustrations of slavery,
infanticide, cannibalism, and sexual custom are marshaled to demonstrate the relativity of
the mores, but are compiled uncritically and voiced through the period's hierarchy of
"civilized" over "savage" peoples (see [[sumner-folkways-1906]] reliability notes). The
work's ethics record is thus double: methodologically it advanced a genuine relativist
insight; substantively it reproduced the scientific racism and eugenic register of its
moment — the call for a *"sound population policy"* against *"imbecility, insanity"* (§318),
the approving account of antebellum racial "mores" and hostility to Reconstruction (§81),
the block-quoted claims of the cognitive inferiority of *"nature peoples"* (§§137–138), and
the paternalist "brain strain"/"missionary-made men" case against educating colonized
peoples and women (§700). Chapter XVI is a live internal contradiction: Sumner insists such
customs *"never furnish grounds for an ethical judgment on the group which practices
them"* (§600) while calling them *"abominable"* and *"monstrous"* on the same pages. Sumner's reputation has moved accordingly — celebrated for the concepts, criticized
for the politics and framing, and treated in the history of ideas as the American test case
for what "[[social-darwinism|Social Darwinism]]" did and did not mean.

## Historiography

Richard Hofstadter's *Social Darwinism in American Thought* (1944) made Sumner the type-case
of the doctrine and shaped his 20th-century reception; subsequent intellectual historians
have complicated Hofstadter's portrait, debating how far Sumner's mature sociology of the
mores can be separated from his earlier laissez-faire polemics. Keller's posthumous
editing of Sumner's manuscripts into *The Science of Society* (1927) is itself a
historiographic filter on what survived of his program.
