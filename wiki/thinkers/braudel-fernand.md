# Fernand Braudel

## Overview

French historian, leading figure of the second-generation
[[annales-school|Annales School]] and originator of the [[longue-duree]]
framework: history layered into geographic time (near-immobile structural
constraints), social/conjunctural time, and event time. *The Mediterranean*
(1949) treated the sea itself as historical protagonist — the model Cunliffe
explicitly transposed to the Atlantic in
[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]] (2001).

## Contributions

Braudel argued that enduring geographic and environmental structures constrain
social life more powerfully than events; his three-tier temporality reshaped
economic and social history and, through *Civilization and Capitalism*, fed
directly into [[world-systems-analysis]] — Wallerstein's world-economy concept
is developed from Braudel's économie-monde.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Mentored by Lucien Febvre; led the Annales journal and the VIe Section (later
EHESS), institutionalizing the school. Influenced Wallerstein and, in
archaeology, maritime longue-durée syntheses (Cunliffe; Horden and Purcell's
*The Corrupting Sea*, 2000, in partial critique).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Critics argued the structural emphasis renders human agency epiphenomenal and
that geographic determinism shadows the longue durée; microhistory and the
cultural turn developed partly in reaction. (Positions attributed; see
[[annales-school]].)
