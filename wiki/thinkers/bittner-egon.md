## Overview

Egon Bittner was an early collaborator in the ethnomethodological research circle organized around [[garfinkel-harold]], affiliated with the Langley Porter Neuropsychiatric Institute. He is credited as a co-contributor to Chapter 7 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967), "Methodological Adequacy in the Quantitative Study of Selection Criteria and Selection Practices," and was part of the working group behind the UCLA/Colorado "Conferences on Ethnomethodology" (begun February 1962), alongside Garfinkel, Craig MacAndrew, Edward Rose, and Harvey Sacks. Beyond this source, Bittner is also known in the broader sociological literature for later work on the sociology of policing, though that body of work falls outside the material reviewed for this ingest.

## Contributions

In Chapter 7 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology*, Garfinkel and Bittner argued that any adequately framed quantitative study of clinic selection criteria must specify five interrelated parameters: sequence of successive population steps, the selection operations transforming one population into the next, an initial demand population taken at first clinic contact (not a pre-filtered "eligible" population), the composition of compared populations at each step, and a theory relating selection work to clinic caseload. They further argued that "predictive value" (statistical discriminability) is not identical to the actual selection criteria used by clinic staff, and that treating the two as identical risks an ecological-correlation-type fallacy that forecloses research into how predictive statistics are actually assembled from individual selection decisions. Bittner is also named as part of the working group responsible for developing the [[etcetera-principle|"et cetera" clause]] as an object of ongoing ethnomethodological study.
