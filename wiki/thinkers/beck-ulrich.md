# Ulrich Beck

German sociologist (1944–2015); originator of the **[[risk-society]]** thesis and, with [[giddens-anthony|Anthony Giddens]] and Scott Lash, a principal architect of the theory of **[[reflexive-modernization]]**. In this vault the primary ingested base is *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]* (1986). Later works (*World Risk Society*, *The Cosmopolitan Vision*) are referenced only from within that text or its editorial apparatus until ingested as primary sources.

## Overview

Beck's career origins were, per his English-language editors Scott Lash and Brian Wynne, as "a hard-working and — until recently — a not particularly celebrated sociologist specializing in research on industry and the family." *Risikogesellschaft* (1986) transformed his standing: it sold some 60,000 copies in five years, reshaped German ecological-political debate, and gave its name to the 1990 German Sociological Association conference ("The Modernization of Modernization?"). Beck was also a public intellectual in the continental *Schriftsteller* mould, writing regularly in the *Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung*. His work reorganizes the theory of modernity around a single claim: that modernization has become **reflexive** — it now dissolves the industrial society it built and confronts the hazards it manufactures.

## Contributions

- **[[risk-society]]** — the diagnosis that advanced modernity is organized increasingly around the social production and distribution of hazards ("bads") rather than only wealth; risk positions are not class positions.
- **[[reflexive-modernization]]** — the master concept: modernization confronting and undermining its own foundations and side effects (not to be confused with deliberate "reflection").
- **[[individualization]]** and **[[detraditionalization]]** — the release of individuals from industrial-society class, status, gender, and family forms into institutionally-dependent, self-constructed **[[do-it-yourself-biography|do-it-yourself biographies]]**; individualization is explicitly *not* emancipation.
- **[[relations-of-definition]]** — Beck's analogue to Marx's relations of production: the institutions and competences that decide how risk is identified, measured, and adjudicated.
- **[[boomerang-effect]]** — hazards eventually return to strike their producers; the partial equalization and globalization of risk ("poverty is hierarchic, smog is democratic").
- **[[reflexive-scientization]]** — science turning its skepticism on its own products and foundations; the demonopolization of scientific truth-claims and the "feudalization" of cognitive practice.
- **[[subpolitics]]** — the migration of society-shaping decisions out of the formal political system into science, technology, business, and private life ("the political becomes non-political and the non-political political").

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under / trained:** not yet established from primary ingest (`[[unknown]]` until biographical sources enter).
- **Reworks:** [[weber-max|Weber]] ([[rationalization]] recast as generating incalculability); [[marx-karl|Marx]] (the base–superstructure formula inverted for risk positions; class-formation theory made the "mirror image" of the individualization thesis); [[modernization-theory|modernization theory]] (linear modernization recast as *reflexive*).
- **Parallel and collaborative development:** [[giddens-anthony|Anthony Giddens]] — Lash and Wynne stress that Beck's and Giddens's theories of reflexive modernity, risk, trust, and biographical self-construction developed "quite independently" before the two came to appreciate one another, from divergent bases (Giddens from abstract social theory and the [[double-hermeneutic]]/[[ontological-security]]; Beck from the sociology of institutions). Giddens’s *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|Consequences of Modernity]]* (1990) engages Beck on global risk and "no others" while developing a distinct trust/disembedding apparatus. Later co-authorship of *Reflexive Modernization* (1994).
- **Frankfurt-School horizon:** Beck, like Habermas, treats social change as a learning process and positions himself as "the friend, not the foe of modernization," against the rationalization-pessimism of Weber, Adorno, and Foucault.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The risk-society and individualization theses are contested. The claim of a "capitalism without classes" drew sustained criticism from class-analysis sociologists who point to persistent structured inequality (see [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]]). The threshold periodization and "world risk society" globalization have been criticized as generalizing from a West German case. Lash and Wynne, sympathetically, question whether Beck over-credits the emancipatory automaticity of lay and scientific reflexivity. Beck's own labor-market predictions he framed as falsifiable hypotheses rather than settled claims.

## Historiography

The standard English-language framing derives substantially from Scott Lash and Brian Wynne's 1992 introduction to the Sage translation, which situates Beck between Habermas's *Structural Transformation of the Public Sphere* (as a comparably field-defining German work) and Giddens's contemporaneous modernity writings. Beck's origins in industrial and family sociology are routinely under-emphasized in accounts that read *Risk Society* as a purely environmental-theoretical intervention; the book's Part II (individualization, gender, labor) is as central to it as its Part I on hazard.

[[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]], in the 2000 Afterword to *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]*, takes up Beck's "risk society" and "modernization of modernity," linking incalculable modern risk to a diffuse anxiety that states displace onto "law and order" politics targeting migrants and "undesirables."
