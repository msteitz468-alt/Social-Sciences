# Diane M. Tober

## Overview

Diane M. Tober is a medical anthropologist (joint PhD, UC Berkeley / UCSF Medical Anthropology; SSRC Sexuality Research Fellowship Program postdoc hosted at UC Berkeley Anthropology, as of the volume bio). She has researched infertility and donor insemination since 1991. Her chapter in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* — “Semen as Gift, Semen as Goods: Reproductive Workers and the Market in Altruism” — analyzes how cultural interpretations of genetic inheritance shape sperm-donor screening and the market for donor sperm, and how gift/altruism rhetoric operates inside a price system. Book project (then): *Romancing the Sperm: The Screening and Making of Alternative American Families*.

## Contributions

### Fieldwork design *(descriptive — Tober)*

Two phases: **1991–1998** — interviews with sperm-repository representatives, sperm-bank file data, interviews with **15 single women** and **15 lesbian couples** attempting motherhood via donor insemination (DI); **1999–2001** — interviews with **15 donors** on motivations/experiences, plus re-visits to previous informants. Comparative focus on three repositories:

| Repository | Notes (as of Tober) |
|---|---|
| The Sperm Bank of California (SBC) | Founded 1982 by Barbara Raboy; first to cater to single women/lesbians and offer identity release; sliding-scale pay up to ~$40–$50/specimen |
| Rainbow Flag Health Services (RF) | Oakland, founded 1995; lesbian/gay community; open identity release at offspring age 3 months; minimal “stipend” max ~$200 at program end (Leland) |
| Repository for Germinal Choice (RGC) | Hermann J. Mueller / Robert Graham; opened 1980; positive-eugenics “best and brightest”; no donor pay as altruism evidence; ceased operation after Graham’s 1997 death |

### Grass-roots eugenics and market construction of desirability *(attributed — Tober)*

Tober labels women’s selection of donors by interpretations of “valuable genetic traits” as **“grass roots eugenics”** (Tober 1998) — affecting emotional and commodity quality of donor sperm. Good physical health is primary screen; beyond that, height/weight/hair-eye color, education, “personality,” motivation, identity-release willingness, ethnicity, intelligence, and altruism take varying values by repository and consumer demand. Informant “Sandy” rejected a high-count Mexican donor over gun/car narrative (“little member of the NRA”) while acknowledging non-genetic linkage; lesbian couple prioritized health, brightness, height, profession-as-proxy for smarts. Following Emily Martin (1991), Tober argues repository employees and recipients assign donor sperm a **personality** presumed to appear in offspring regardless of maternal biology or environment.

### Gift / commodity and the market in altruism *(attributed — Tober)*

Semen transactions **conflate gift and commodity**: semen is purchased, marketed, and screened, yet cannot escape social relations of “fatherhood” via fantasy/fetish of the donor, possible later contact (delayed gift/counter-gift), and recipients’ experience of paid semen as precious “gift of life.” Payment continuum: some repositories pay nothing; some reimburse “time and trouble”; some pay ~$40–$50 per sample — financial compensation is never morally neutral. Industry inconsistently links free semen with good and purchased with bad; emphasis on altruism is treated as connected to quality of person and, by extension, quality of semen.

**Titmuss parallel:** Tober maps Richard Titmuss’s *The Gift Relationship* (US vs UK blood systems — commercial = bad / voluntary = good) onto semen’s paid vs free tension, but argues that in semen donation **there is always some form of self-interest** even when free and anonymous. Blood–semen parallels: regenerative; donatable/sellable; “gift of life”; bankable; disease-transmissible; forge donor–recipient relationships without meeting.

**Market in altruism thesis:** repositories that pay little or nothing are also “suspect” — lower donor pay increases profit margin on resale; search for “altruistic” donors becomes search for men for whom **genetic incentive** outweighs financial incentive. Conclusion: in sperm donation, altruism/gifting becomes a **selling point, a secondary commodity**; “true altruism cannot exist” in semen transactions given complex genetic/social rewards (Tober’s position).

### Identity release, ethnicity, price differentiation *(attributed — Tober)*

Identity-release donors higher demand; one San Francisco repository charges ~$25 more per vial for identity-release semen — identity as secondary commodity under supply/demand. Bay Area banks (SBC, RF) seek ethnically diverse donors, often relaxing standards (e.g. height minima for non-Caucasian donors); unmet demand for African-American and Jewish sperm among lesbian clients (Leland). RGC less concerned — primarily Caucasian upper-middle-class scientist reproduction, married clients only (woman under 38, husband consent).

### Reproductive work ‖ sex work *(attributed — Tober)*

Tober proposes **“reproductive work”** as conceptual category paralleling sex work: body-involving labor with market value for body/products; paid “donors” sell semen (term “donor” inaccurate when paid); masturbation labor links male sexuality to the genetic-material market. Unlike sex workers’ self-labeling, semen donors do not call themselves “reproductive workers”; egg donors/surrogates face deeper medical intrusion. Class structure of gamete labor (contra Scheper-Hughes’s poor-selling-organs-to-wealthy pattern): intellectually privileged, often temporarily economically disadvantaged students for paid gametes; non-compensated donors more privileged (RGC scientists); surrogates typically working class (Roberts 1998). Extreme price poles: ronsangels.com beauty auctions $15k–$150k; Ivy League egg ads up to $50k+.

### Donor motives *(attributed — Tober fieldwork)*

Threefold rewards for compensated donors: money, gene-passing, desire to help. Intake forms couple need for money with eugenic “spread excellent genetics.” Most interviewed men said they would still donate unpaid. Semi-professional donors (regular donations, year-long commitments) build partnership/trust with banks — critical for STD/genetic disease risk control.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(To expand with secondary literature.)* Tober’s own critical moves: against pure-gift claims for unpaid banks; against Leland’s “better controls” claim for non-paying RF (donors may still lie for genetic/social motives; non-payment also raises repository profits); qualified engagement with Dawkins (selfish-gene may fit sperm donation better than most of his program). Chapter scope excludes known-donor unpaid arrangements common among single women and lesbians (n.1).
