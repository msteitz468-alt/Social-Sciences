# John W. Meyer

## Overview

John W. Meyer is a sociologist whose 1977 papers — "The Effects of Education as an
Institution" and, with Brian Rowan, "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure
as Myth and Ceremony" — are treated by DiMaggio and Powell as new-institutionalism's
conventional "birth date." In *The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis*
(1991) Meyer co-authored three chapters: ch. 2 (with [[rowan-brian]], reprinting the
1977 argument), ch. 5 (with [[scott-w-richard]], "The Organization of Societal
Sectors"), and ch. 9 (with [[jepperson-ronald]], "The Public Order and the Construction
of Formal Organizations").

## Contributions

**Ch. 2 (with Rowan).** Meyer and Rowan argued that "in modern societies, formal
organizational structures arise in highly institutionalized contexts" and that
organizations "incorporate the practices and procedures defined by prevailing
rationalized concepts of organizational work" to increase legitimacy and survival
prospects "independent of the immediate efficacy" of those practices. They defined
[[rationalized-myth]]s as institutionalized elements with "two key properties": they are
"rationalized and impersonal prescriptions that identify various social purposes as
technical ones," and they are "highly institutionalized and thus in some measure beyond
the discretion of any individual participant or organization." Their six formal
propositions established: that rationalized institutional rules generate formal
organization even holding technical/relational complexity constant; that more
modernized societies produce more elaborated formal organization; that organizations
incorporating legitimated rationalized elements maximize legitimacy and survival
capability independent of technical efficiency; and — the chapter's most influential
claim — that because control efforts in institutionalized organizations generate
conflict and legitimacy loss, "elements of structure are decoupled from activities and
from each other" (the origin of [[decoupling]] as a concept). They paired decoupling
with the "logic of confidence and good faith," arguing decoupled organizations sustain
day-to-day order not through anarchy but through participants' and constituents'
maintained confidence, drawing on Goffman's account of "face."

**Ch. 5 (with Scott).** Meyer and Scott defined a [[societal-sector]] as "a collection of
organizations operating in the same domain... together with those organizations that
critically influence the performance of the focal organizations," and distinguished
technical environments (rewarding efficient production) from institutional environments
(rewarding conformity to legitimating rules). They proposed nineteen numbered hypotheses
relating a sector's technical/institutional development, and its centralization/
fragmentation of decision-making, to organizational size, administrative complexity,
internal conflict, and coupling — building on Meyer, Scott, and Deal (1981).

**Ch. 9 (with Jepperson).** Meyer and Jepperson asked why formal organizing arises and
expands, and why organizing forms show marked commonalities across Western systems — an
"embarrassment," they argued, to functional and conflict theories alike, which tie
organizing to local interests or power. They developed a **polity typology** crossing
degree of actor-authorization (high/low) with degree of institutionalization of
collective functions (low/high), yielding four polity types: liberal/individualist
(United States, United Kingdom, Australia), statist society (France, Belgium, Italy),
segmental/"state outside society" (Latin American states), and corporatist (Germany,
Austria). See [[jepperson-ronald]] for the typology's full detail and the pair's three
formal propositions on how each polity type shapes formal organizing.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Meyer and Rowan's 1977 argument draws on Berger and Luckmann's *The Social Construction
of Reality* (1967) for its definition of institutionalized rules as "reciprocated
typifications," on Weber for the conventional bureaucratization account they revise, and
positions itself against the "informal organization" literature (Dalton 1959; Downs
1967; Homans 1950) that had documented gaps between formal and actual organizational
behavior without theorizing formal structure itself as symbolic. DiMaggio and Powell
(ch. 1) credit Meyer's work as the volume's originating strand, and note his concept of
the "Western cultural account" (tracing the diffusion of individualism, rationalism, and
evolutionism) as feeding directly into [[jepperson-ronald]] and Meyer's ch. 9 and into
[[friedland-roger]] and [[alford-robert-r]]'s ch. 10. Meyer, Scott, and Deal (1981) is
repeatedly cited across the volume as foundational to the technical/institutional
environments distinction elaborated in chs. 5 and 7. [[zucker-lynne]]'s ch. 4 postscript
explicitly positions her micro/cognitive approach as complementary to, but distinct
from, the macro-level accounts of Meyer's chapters.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

[[zucker-lynne]] noted a direct point of divergence: her experimental prediction of
greater homogeneity and less variation under institutionalization contrasted with Meyer
and Rowan's (1977) prediction that organizations lacking well-defined technologies
import institutionalized rules while remaining loosely coupled internally — DiMaggio and
Powell's ch. 3 makes the same contrast explicit, noting their own view diverges "somewhat"
from Meyer and Rowan's loose-coupling account. [[scott-w-richard]] (ch. 7) registered a
self-flagged disagreement with DiMaggio and Powell's isomorphism "master hypothesis," a
divergence bearing indirectly on Meyer and Scott's own ch. 5 argument that increasing
structuration can, under some conditions, produce increased organizational diversity
rather than homogeneity. [[jepperson-ronald]] and Meyer's own polity typology was
qualified by the authors as raising a "sampling on the dependent variable" concern, which
they addressed only by noting "world history has done the sampling."

## Historiography

DiMaggio and Powell (ch. 1) date new-institutionalism's conventional origin to 1977, "the
year in which John Meyer published two seminal papers," and trace precursor work to
Meyer and Hannan's world-system studies (1979) and Meyer's earlier work on school
"charter effects" (from a 1970 collection) and contextual effects in organizations
(1968). The Meyer–Scott collaboration (1983b) is credited with developing institutional
principles for formal organizations ahead of the 1985 UCLA conference, convened by
[[zucker-lynne]], that brought the program's contributors together as a self-conscious
school.
