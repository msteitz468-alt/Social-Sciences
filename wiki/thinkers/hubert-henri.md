# Henri Hubert (1872–1927)

## Overview

French sociologist of religion and archaeologist; [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s collaborator on the *Année Sociologique* and [[mauss-marcel|Marcel Mauss]]'s closest working partner. Hubert co-authored the two founding essays of the Durkheimian sociology of ritual — *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice: Its Nature and Functions]]* (1898; see [[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study]]) and *[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|A General Theory of Magic]]* (1902–03) — while also holding a curatorship at the Musée des Antiquités Nationales (Saint-Germain-en-Laye) and working as a prehistorian and Celticist.

## Contributions

- With Mauss, the **sociological theory of [[sacrifice]]** as a scheme of consecration mediating sacred and profane (1898).
- With Mauss, the **social theory of [[magic]]** and the promotion of **[[mana]]** to a general category of collective thought (1902–03), positioned against [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s intellectualism.
- An essay on the collective representation of **time** in religion and magic (1905), an early Durkheimian treatment of the social categories of understanding.
- Archaeological/Celtic synthesis published posthumously.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Core member of the first *Année Sociologique* circle around Durkheim; the Hubert–Mauss partnership was so close that contemporaries treated their joint essays as products of a single mind. English translations of *A General Theory of Magic* dropped Hubert's name from the title page — a documented attribution distortion; the original *Année* publication is Hubert & Mauss.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Hubert's independent stature was long eclipsed by Mauss's; recent history of the discipline (e.g., work on the Durkheimians and on Hubert's Celtic archaeology) has partially restored him as a figure bridging sociology and archaeology.

## Historiography

Known chiefly through the *Année Sociologique* corpus and through Mauss's own memorializing after Hubert's early death in 1927. Collection coverage: two co-authored sources primary-ingested (*Sacrifice* 1898; *Magic* 1902–03); Celtic/archaeological work still thin.
