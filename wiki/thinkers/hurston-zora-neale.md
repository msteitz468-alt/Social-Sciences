## Overview

Zora Neale Hurston (1891–1960) was an American **folklorist, anthropologist, and
novelist**, and one of the notable students [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] trained at
Columbia/Barnard. She pursued graduate anthropology under Boas's tutelage and applied
Boasian [[cultural-relativism|cultural relativism]] and salvage ethnography to African
American and Afro-Caribbean folklore, collecting in the US South, Jamaica, and Haiti in
the 1930s (*Mules and Men*, 1935; *Tell My Horse*, 1938). She is most widely known outside
anthropology for the novel *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937). A figure of the Harlem
Renaissance, she brought Boasian method to Black vernacular culture at a moment when that
culture was rarely treated as a serious ethnographic subject.

## Contributions

- **Ethnography of Black vernacular culture** — folklore, hoodoo/Vodou, and speech, treated
  as coherent cultural systems in their own terms (Boasian [[cultural-relativism]]).
- **Bridging anthropology and literature** — her ethnographic collecting and her fiction
  drew on the same field material, an early instance of the porous boundary between
  ethnographic writing and literary form.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Long marginalized in both anthropology and the literary canon, Hurston was substantially
reappraised from the 1970s onward (notably through Alice Walker's recovery of her work),
and is now read as a major figure in African American letters and in the history of
American anthropology. Her positionality — a Black woman ethnographer studying her own and
adjacent communities under white-dominated disciplinary structures — is itself a subject
of reflexive scholarship. *(Reappraisal noted in general terms; expand with dedicated

## Historiography

Hurston's disciplinary reputation has moved sharply. Neglected at her death in 1960, she
was recovered from the 1970s onward — first through literary scholarship and Alice
Walker's essay locating her grave — and only later reintegrated into the history of
anthropology as a Boasian fieldworker rather than solely a Harlem Renaissance novelist.
