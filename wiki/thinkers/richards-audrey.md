# Audrey Richards

British social anthropologist (1899–1984). Contributed “The Political System of the Bemba Tribe, North-Eastern Rhodesia” to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940) and “Some Types of Family Structure amongst the Central Bantu” to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950). Malinowski student; major figure in Africanist economic and political ethnography (*Land, Labour and Diet in Northern Rhodesia* and other works **not yet ingested**).

## Overview

Richards’s APS essay places the [[bemba|Bemba]] among Bantu political systems organized around a ruling lineage and territorial [[chieftainship]], with matrilineal kinship and a paramount (Citimukulu) whose person and ancestors were ritually bound to land prosperity. The chapter is a **Group A** case: centralized hierarchy of chiefs and headmen, judicial and military machinery, and integration through rank and supernatural sanction rather than age-regiments. She frames Bemba organization against “some general features” of Bantu political organization (tribe as lineage outgrowth; concentration of power in ruling lineage; variation by size, homogeneity, environment, conquest history, and white administration).

## Contributions

From the APS Bemba claims extract:

- **Composition and economy:** North-Eastern Rhodesia Bemba as relatively homogeneous political unit with Luba-origin tradition and eighteenth-century arrival; expansion and empire collapse; matrilineal/matrilocal kinship; clan (*umukoa*) and house (*ijanda*); *ulupwa* bilateral active kin with political effects; shifting cultivation without cattle; land power and village impermanence; migrant labour pressure; white administration as conditioning factor.
- **Dogma of descent and office:** Hereditary offices and *ukupyanika* succession identification; chief as owner of land via ancestral spirits (*imipashi*); person of the chief affects land prosperity; installation ritual’s political importance; divine-king rumours (throttling of declining kings) as part of the ideology of office ([[mystical-values-of-office]]); multi-heir succession rules and 1920s succession disputes (e.g. Mwamba–Citimukulu tradition).
- **Functions of leadership:** Headman *ukuteka bantu*; chief’s political, judicial, economic, ritual, and military functions; prerogatives and sanctions.
- **Machinery of government:** Personal vs. hereditary officials; administrative, military, judicial machinery; *bakabilo* (hereditary councillors) as ritual specialists, regency actors, and check on the paramount; other royal-relative advisers; Richards’s judgment that the system was not democratic in a Western sense.
- **Integration:** Tribal integration depends on loyalty to the paramount; no regiment or public ceremonial integration of the Zulu type; supernatural and rank as integrators; *ifyalo* (districts) linked by paramount and kinship; ritual unifies despite royal quarrels.
- **Post-European changes:** Ordinances and administrative remaking of chieftainship (chapter treats through start of 1929 Ordinances and related changes)—chiefship under colonial overrule as ethnographic present of the 1930s.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Matrilineal Bemba political succession and “dogma of descent” later generated specialist debate in Central African kinship studies—not yet filed from primary reanalyses here.
- Colonial remaking of paramountcy and district chieftainship is part of Richards’s own account; precolonial empire and 1930s Indirect Rule chiefship must be kept distinct by date.
- General “Bantu political organization” framing in the chapter opening is comparative typology of its era—attribute, do not promote to wiki-voice universalism.

## Historiography

Richards is a canonical Malinowski-school Africanist; APS Bemba chapter is a condensed political ethnography, not her full economic monographs. Summary page only; no Thinkers Hub entry yet.

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].)*

- **Formation.** Natural Sciences Cambridge; WWI relief work Frankfurt (Quaker; lifelong nutrition concern); four years League of Nations pressure group; 1928 LSE under [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (directed by Graham Wallas). Doctoral thesis *Hunger and Work in a Savage Tribe* (1932): “Nutrition as a biological process is more fundamental than sex,” yet nutrition is a whole “nutritional system” of institutions—orthodox functionalism. Bemba field study from 1929–30 (Indirect Rule introduced the year she entered the field); research proposal already addressed railway/copper development, administration, and white–black conflict. Personal friend of Kuper among the elders; recalled Malinowski seminar as a game discovering the necessity of custom; reported Malinowski’s extreme expressions under opposition.
- **Applied monographs and the prestige split.** Chaired IAI Diet Committee; *Land, Labour and Diet in Northern Rhodesia* (1939) modelled on Malinowski’s *Coral Gardens* but set in colonial context; RLI *Bemba Marriage and Present Economic Conditions* (1940) for administrators on migrant labour and family change. Drafted much of the IAI five-year research plan (Kuper: “trite enough” as social-science prose). **Kuper’s assessment:** applied studies carry colonial realism; academic chapters in *African Political Systems* and *African Systems of Kinship and Marriage* largely ignore overrule and family-change problems—practical problems filed as [[applied-anthropology]] in less prestigious outlets. Richards and [[mair-lucy|Mair]] ruefully acknowledged applied work as less serious than fundamental [[social-structure]] studies. *Chisungu* (1956) functionalist girls’ initiation in ethnographic present, ignoring change.
- **Institutions.** WWII temporary principal, Colonial Office; 1944 LSE special lecturer in colonial studies; Colonial Social Science Research Council board (with [[firth-raymond|Firth]], steered postwar social-anthropology fellowships); urged Uganda institute of social research, first director 1950 (rural development, migrant labour, Indirect Rule chiefs). Postwar LSE lineup with Mair still fostering applied work while Oxford disapproved. 1946 pleaded against LSE–Oxford camps becoming “two sets of psychoanalysts.” Commonwealth institute directorships among few senior posts women held while none headed a UK anthropology department.

## Bemba succession source and later work (via Gluckman, *Order and Rebellion*, 1963)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], Essay II, "Succession and Civil War among the Bemba," written 1946/published 1954.)* Richards's APS Bemba chapter supplied one of the two conflicting accounts of [[bemba|Bemba]] succession rules (the other from District Officer V. W. Brelsford) that Gluckman used to develop his succession-cycle analysis; her stated rules of succession conflicted with her own reported evidence, a discrepancy Gluckman attributes to fieldwork location within one of two branches of the royal family contesting the paramountcy. Richards's observation that ritually "the whole Bemba country can be said to act as a whole" despite "quarrels and jealousies between different lines of the royal family" is quoted by Gluckman as evidence for the rebellion-not-secession thesis for the Bemba-type polity.

Richards's earlier article "A Modern Movement of Witchfinders" (*Africa*, viii, 1935) is cited by Gluckman as a direct influence on his analysis of colonial-era anti-witchcraft movements in "The Magic of Despair" (1954; see [[witchcraft]]).

**[POSITION — Gluckman's critique.]** Gluckman criticizes Richards's 1960 Presidential Address to the Royal Anthropological Institute, "African Kings and their Royal Relatives," for focusing on "the problem of the African king with too many royal relatives" rather than relating royal-family structure to the wider political-economic system — part of a broader trend Gluckman identifies (with Fallers, Southall, M. G. Smith, and Colson) of anthropologists in the later 1950s shifting attention toward bureaucracy and administration rather than rebellion and civil-war process.

## Central Bantu family-structure types (ASKAM, 1950)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, Richards contributed “Some Types of Family Structure amongst the Central Bantu” (pp. 207–251)—a comparative typology synthesizing her Bemba/Bisa fieldwork with secondary sources (Van Reeth, de Cleene, Van Wing, Doke, Smith & Dale, Mitchell, Marwick, etc.). Attributed programme (range 7; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Thesis:** under matriliny the balance of interest between the two sides of the family is **uneasy**—hence inter-society variation and multiple marriage/family types within tribes.
- **Terminology:** prefers “marriage with immediate / delayed / without right of bride removal” over crude viri-/uxorilocal labels; classifies extended families by nuclear pivots (father–son, mother–son, parent–daughter / father–daughter grand-family, matrilineal fraternal, etc.).
- **Four types (crude comparative map):**
  - **A Mayombe–Kongo:** matrilineal descent/succession/inheritance; high marriage payments; immediate bride removal; children return to MB’s village at puberty; denser economic resources, slavery historically affecting kinship balance.
  - **B Bemba–Bisa–Lamba (and related):** service + token payments; delayed removal; **father–daughter grand-family** under shifting cultivation and plentiful land; no corporate property-owning matrilineage of Congo type; dispersed totemic clans + shallow local *yanda* houses; Yao–Cewa variants with stronger sororal (*mbumba*) cores and more pronounced eldest-brother avunculate.
  - **C Ila type:** cattle *chiko* enabling stronger paternal rights and co-residence patterns mixed with matrilineal features.
  - **(Fourth axis / residual contrasts)** as tabled in the essay (lineage structure, identification, rights)—full type labels and secondary peoples as in claims.
- **Determinants she stressed:** residence at marriage and **type of marriage payment** (goods/money → viri-local; service/token → not); strong avunculate can still pull women/children; land density and village permanence; political paths to headmanship (Bemba: stable marriage as step to grand-family authority). Divorce quantification lacking; payment size “by no means the only” stability factor.
