# R. G. Collingwood (1889–1943)

British philosopher and archaeologist of Roman Britain; major figure in idealist philosophy of history. [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]]* 2006) opens his case for studying the history of archaeological thought with Collingwood's dictum that **no historical problem should be studied without studying the history of historical thought about it** (*An Autobiography*, 1939, as quoted by Trigger), and expands Collingwood coverage in the 2nd edition as a previously neglected archaeological theorist.

## Overview

Collingwood practiced field archaeology while developing a philosophy in which historical knowledge is the re-thinking of past thought, structured by question-and-answer. Later postprocessual archaeologists drew on related idealist/hermeneutic themes; Trigger cites him for **historiographic self-consciousness**, not as a postprocessual avant la lettre in full.

## Contributions

*(Attributed via Trigger 2006.)*

- Methodological mandate for history of archaeology as necessary to research, not ornamental.
- Idealist account of historical understanding (re-enactment; logic of question and answer) as a standing alternative to positivist covering-law models.
- Roman Britain empirical work combining philosophy and fieldwork (primary not yet ingested).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Oxford idealism; Roman provincial archaeology. Reception: Trigger (historiography); postprocessual theorists (meaning, intentionality); philosophers of history (separate literature).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Positivist and processual archaeologists largely ignored Collingwood; postprocessual selective recovery. Trigger uses him without adopting full idealist metaphysics — Trigger remains materialist-realist.

## Method densified — Hodder & Hutson, *Reading the Past* (ch. 7)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 7 "Archaeology and history," ingested 2026-07-10. Collingwood is the chapter's central methodological source and a founding figure for archaeological [[hermeneutics]].)*

Hodder & Hutson build their interpretive method on Collingwood's claim that "what is miscalled an 'event' is really an **action**, and expresses some thought (intention, purpose) of its agent" — so history recovers the "inside" of events, not just their outside. Historical knowledge is "the **re-doing** of past acts in the present" by immersing oneself in contextual data and re-enacting past thought — which, they stress (contra V. G. [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s misreading), is *not* emptying one's mind to "commune" with the past, but critically thinking oneself into it. The method proceeds by **question and answer**: data "do not exist" except as perceived within a theory, so they must be interrogated with *definite* questions ("are these loose stones a ruined wall?") not vague ones ("let us see what is here"). Validation is by **continuity** (working back through Hawkes's "onion skins") and **coherence + correspondence** — the same criteria [[contextual-archaeology|contextual archaeology]] adopts. The worked exemplar is Collingwood's reasoning that the Antonine Wall was built cheaply (turf ramparts two-thirds the volume of Hadrian's, timber gates, poor siting) as "a deliberate effort after cheapness" reflecting a policy judgment that a strong frontier there was not needed. Hodder rejects Collingwood's occasional over-claim that historical knowledge has *no* rigor, keeping the criteria by which interpretations are judged.
