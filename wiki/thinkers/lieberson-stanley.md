# Stanley Lieberson

American sociologist and methodologist (1933–2018), known for a rigorous **internal critique** of nonexperimental quantitative research — especially the practice of treating observational data as a mock experiment. Principal methodological statement in this vault: *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count: The Improvement of Social Research and Theory]]* (University of California Press, 1985; paperback 1987).

## Overview

Lieberson wrote as a committed empiricist sympathetic to American sociology's goals of rigor and quantification who concluded that many standard procedures are "of no more merit than a quest for a perpetual-motion machine" and are often **counterproductive** — rejecting good ideas and accepting false ones. The remedy, he argued (attributed), is not abandonment of rigorous empirical research (a "plague on both houses": anti-empirical retreat *and* hard-science ritualism) but reconsideration of the **natural-science experimental analogy** that underwrites mock-experiment analysis of nonexperimental data.

Intellectual markers from the 1985 preface: early formation via Chicago empirical sociology (*Chicago: An Experiment in Social Science Research*, Smith and White 1929); graduate reservations at Chicago mid-1950s (Slotkin; Cohen and Nagel); later concentrated writing as Claude Bissell Distinguished Visiting Professor at the University of Toronto, with ties to Arizona and UC Berkeley; NSF SES-8207346; detailed comments from Allen D. Grimshaw and Donald Treiman; signed from Grand Lake District, Oakland.

Doctrine of the **undoable** (ch. 1): some questions are inherently impossible, premature, overly complicated, or nullified by joint theory and evidence — empirical research can fail by being *too ambitious*.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Training lineage and students are not yet filled from primary sources in this vault. Interlocutors and materials heavily used in *Making It Count* include Cook and Campbell on quasi-experiments; Coleman et al. on schools and segregation (as design examples); Duncan on structural equations; Mill and Burgess on comparative/experimental ideals; Merton on theory–research disjunction; Kaplan on scientific ritualism. Later methodological debates cite Lieberson among critics of [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]] (see method page reception) — a distinct 2000s exchange, not the 1985 monograph's focus.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Lieberson's 1985 program is reformist quantitative methodology, not a qualitative manifesto. Defenders of modern identification strategies argue post-1985 tools address some of his control-variable abuses; his structural claims about selectivity, contamination, and asymmetry remain standing challenges whenever observational research claims experimental analogues.
