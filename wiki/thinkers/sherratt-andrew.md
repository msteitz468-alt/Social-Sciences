# Andrew Sherratt (1946–2006)

British archaeologist and anthropologist at the University of Oxford; editor of *The Cambridge Encyclopedia of Archaeology* (1980); co-editor of *Archaeological Theory: Who Sets the Agenda?* (1993) and *Consuming Habits: Drugs in History and Anthropology* (1995). Principal architect of the **[[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]]** model for later Neolithic and Copper Age Eurasia and of a long program linking animal exploitation, traction, wool, dairying, exchange, monumentality, intoxicants, and language expansions.

## Overview

*[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe: Changing Perspectives]]* (Edinburgh University Press, 1997) gathers papers written roughly one a year across two decades (c.1972–1995). The volume’s hinge is the **fourth millennium BC** in the western Old World: changes Sherratt treats as comparable in magnitude to the initial adoption of farming and as largely a consequence of elaborating the farming package through **secondary products**, consumption, and core–periphery interaction with Near Eastern urbanism. The subtitle *Changing Perspectives* marks his self-historicizing arc from 1970s demographic–ecological models (Section II) toward consumption, value, symbols, and a critical combination of economic and post-processual insights (Introduction; Section V).

## Contributions

*(All attributed to Sherratt unless co-authors noted; essay years as in the 1997 collection.)*

### Secondary Products Revolution

- Coined and developed the **[[secondary-products-revolution|SPR]]** (1981+): milk, wool, and **[[animal-traction|traction]]** (plough/cart) as a second economic burst after cereal cultivation.
- Near Eastern multilinear origins → package integration → European impact; plough “most important development… after… cereal cultivation itself” (1981).
- 1997 self-corrections: TRB origins not plough-driven; Anatolian traction route; alcohol vs milk vessel split; horse chapter rewritten (scepticism of simple Dereivka/Botai stories; mid-fourth-millennium Pit-Grave association preferred as of his 1995 revision).

### Settlement, exchange, and the Hungarian Plain

- **Mobile resources** model (1982): livestock as exchange medium; multi-focal cattle domestication; regional alliance and cattle-export ↔ highland-import circuits.
- **Great Hungarian Plain** survey synthesis (1983–84): phase cycles (Körös/AVK → aggregation → Copper Age dispersal); salination and settlement drift; Baden cart/plough horizon; spatial exclusiveness of steppe tumuli vs Baden (associated populations, not equated peoples).
- **Neolithic exchange systems** (1987): pioneer frugality → regional bulk climax → Copper Age exotic flint; hierarchy without elite graves in some phases.

### Megaliths and “conversion”

- Tombs as **village/longhouse surrogates** (1990); mutual exclusivity of substantial settlements and substantial burial monuments.
- **Instruments of Conversion** (1995): megalithic monumentality as outward rhetoric recruiting Mesolithic communities; full vs sub-Neolithic strategies; later Corded Ware/Bell Beaker replacement horizons.

### Invisible flows: alcohol, narcotics, metals, languages

- **[[prehistoric-intoxicants]]:** alcohol hospitality (1987); opium/cannabis/henbane sacred–profane regimes (1991).
- **Metal vessels** with T. Taylor (1989): symposium kit; Vulchetrun context; west Pontic sheet-metal stages.
- **Troy–Maikop–Altyn Depe** (1991): land bridges; Maikop as Late Uruk “first barbarian society” type periphery.
- **Indo-European** with E.S. Sherratt (1988): rejects simple Renfrew farming=IE and simple Gimbutas equations; **languages ≠ archaeological cultures**; Circumpontic contact axes (see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]).

### Childe and intellectual history

- Ch.1 (1989) and Ch.20 (1995): Childe as ancestor and problem; Neolithic/Urban Revolutions as world thresholds; SPR as Urban Revolution spin-off on outer Eurasia; chronology choices and free-metalsmith models assessed as right/wrong in parts — **position recording**, not wiki-voice verdict.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Timing of dairying:** post-1997 lipid-residue work (recorded on [[residue-analysis]]) pulls milk use earlier than the classic mid/late fourth-millennium SPR package — often reframes SPR as multi-tempo intensification rather than a single “switch-on.”
- **Bundle coherence:** Chapman and others challenged simultaneous arrival of wool, wheels, and ploughmarks; Sherratt’s 1987 reply defends introduction models with staggered elements.
- **“Revolution” rhetoric:** Horwitz & Tchernov (1989) deny a circumscribed revolution; Sherratt retains the contrast even in “minimalist” form.
- **Genetics and IE:** steppe and Corded Ware genetic results (as of Reich 2018 layer on [[yamnaya-culture]] / [[corded-ware-culture]]) post-date his essays; his 1988 non-identity of languages and cultures remains methodologically useful even where migration evidence strengthened.

## Historiography

Sherratt is a central **Critical Turn / Contemporary** European prehistorian whose SPR program sits between Childean Near East–Europe diffusion concerns and Renfrew-era autonomy debates. The 1997 collection is self-consciously a document of theoretical fashion (processual demography → consumption and symbols) and of Oxford teaching synthesis. Reception tracks zooarchaeology, residue chemistry, and ancient DNA (as of post-2015 genomic papers; Reich 2018 layer on related culture pages) more than pure theory wars: SPR remains a teaching and research foil even when components are re-dated.

## Reflexivity

Oxford teaching post; English-language European prehistory across the processual → post-processual decades. Preface treats theory succession as partly *Zeitgeist* (Enlightenment/Romantic cycles). Absolute dates mix uncalibrated bc and calibrated BC by essay; genetic and linguistic arguments are **as of Sherratt 1988–95** and subordinate to later aDNA (as of Reich 2018) where they conflict. Four-way non-identity (culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people) is especially load-bearing on Corded Ware / steppe / IE chapters.

## As neo-diffusionism's ally (Kristiansen & Larsson 2005)

[[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005|Kristiansen & Larsson]] (2005) treat Sherratt as a key ally in reviving interaction-based explanation after the autonomist turn: his work on the [[secondary-products-revolution]], the diffusion of alcohol/narcotics as socialising media, and his Mediterranean–Carpathian–Baltic trade reconstructions model diffusion as *economic and ritual practice locally transformed*, not mere typological spread. They cite Sherratt (1995) and Sherratt & Sherratt (1998) as delivering the critique of extreme autonomism they build on (see [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]).

## Bradley 1998 engagement (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* cites Sherratt on the ~4500 BC agricultural frontier and the appearance of first major monuments where agricultural resources are newly employed (1990), and quotes Sherratt 1995 on monumentality as cultural rhetoric of Neolithic conversion. Bradley accepts the force of the conversion metaphor but develops an independent account of monument *form* via [[death-of-the-house]] and Mesolithic ontology, and extends the "religious conversion" analogy to Urnfield-era farming intensification (Ch. 10).

## Whittle 1996 — secondary products as over-stated revolution (position on Sherratt)

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]* 1996, Ch. 5 and Ch. 10; engagement with the [[secondary-products-revolution]] model.)*

Whittle finds Sherratt’s secondary-products model **attractive** (milk, wool, plough/traction, wheeled transport not early Neolithic essentials) but argues the **initial formulation over-states** a single later-4th-millennium Near Eastern package as a revolution:

- Wheeled vehicle remains the most convincing mid/late 4th-mill. innovation (Uruk pictograms; Baden cart models ~3500–3300 BC — rapid diffusion).
- Plough: later representations, but earlier ard-share claims and castrated-oxen metrics claimed into later 6th-mill. LBK; mid-4th-mill. NW European ard marks partly preservation bias under barrows.
- Earlier age patterns allow some milk/wool use; mature cattle/sheep at Ovčarovo, Golyamo Delčevo, Targovishte, Poljanica may already show secondary uses **before** the 4th millennium.
- Preferred gloss: secondary-products horizon = **acceleration/accentuation within changing residence**, not sole cause of SE European / wider change. Ch. 10 flags “secondary products revolution” among language traps that reify package completeness.
- Related: Baden double cattle burials signal new human–animal formality around secondary products and hospitality drinking sets — still within older traditions, not automatic ranking.

Recorded as **Whittle’s mid-1990s position** on Sherratt’s model (pre-lipid-residue re-dating of dairying); complements the Critiques section’s post-1997 milk chronology above. See [[agricultural-revolution]], [[animal-domestication]], [[baden-culture]].
