# Gavin Lucas (b. 1965)

British archaeological theorist and field archaeologist, associated with the reflexive,
practice-oriented current of post-processual archaeology and with sustained conceptual
work on **the archaeological record** and **archaeological time**. Trained at Cambridge
under [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]; worked for the Cambridge Archaeological Unit directing
excavation and post-excavation programmes, took part in the Çatalhöyük project (1996–97),
and later worked extensively in Iceland.

## Overview

Lucas works at the intersection of archaeological theory and everyday field practice,
arguing that method is theory in material form. His first book,
*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches to Fieldwork]]* (2002), is a
critical history of how excavation, recording, finds processing and classification came to
be done as they are, and of the conceptions of "the archaeological record" they encode. He
extended this into sustained work on **archaeological time** (*The Archaeology of Time*,
2005) and on the concept of the record itself (*Understanding the Archaeological Record*,
2012).

## Contributions

- **Temporalising the stratigraphic unit** — the argument that the
  [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] and geoarchaeological recording alike treat units as
  spatial *objects* without duration, and should instead foreground their event-character
  (see [[archaeological-record]], [[eventful-temporality]]).
- **Archaeology as material praxis** — archaeology is the discipline that must actively
  *materialize* its own data; its facts are the textual/graphic materialisations that stand
  in for the un-revisitable site, and validity rests on their **iterability**.
- **A critical history of field method** — revising progressive/Whig accounts of
  [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]], [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]], and the rise of
  [[single-context-recording|single-context recording]] and [[open-area-excavation|open-area
  excavation]].
- **The "splitting of objects"** — the historical co-production of finds specialisms and the
  separation of finds from context (see [[typological-method]], [[object-biography]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trained under [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] at Cambridge; his work extends Hodder's
interpretive and reflexive programme into the analysis of field method and the concept of
the record itself, while critiquing both processual ([[binford-lewis|Binford]], Schiffer)
and post-processual formulations for retaining an object-based, non-temporal model of the
record.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Critical Approaches to Fieldwork* was received as an important opening of a neglected topic
(the theory latent in routine practice). Its central provocation — that post-processualism
changed archaeological theory but barely touched everyday field practice — is a contestable
thesis, recorded here as a position rather than a settled finding. Its coverage is
explicitly limited to Britain and North America.
