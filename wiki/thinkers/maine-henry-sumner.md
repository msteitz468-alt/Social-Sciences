# Henry Sumner Maine

Sir Henry James Sumner Maine (1822–1888), English jurist and comparative legal historian; Cambridge professor of civil law; later member of the Indian Council. Primary ingested work: *[[maine-ancient-law-1861|Ancient Law]]* (1861), which formulated the **[[patriarchal-theory|patriarchal theory]]** of primitive society and the formula that progressive societies move **[[status-to-contract|from Status to Contract]]**.

## Overview

Maine used **comparative jurisprudence** — chiefly Roman law, with Hindu, Teutonic, Greek, and other archaic materials — to reconstruct the legal history of the family, property, wills, contract, and delict. He argued that archaic society was an aggregation of **families** under paternal power, not of individuals, and that progressive legal development emancipated the individual from status fixed by family membership toward capacity for free agreement. *(Attributed — Maine 1861; dates from Everyman intro by J. H. Morgan.)*

He is repeatedly identified (by the Everyman introducer and later reception) as a master of the **[[comparative-method|comparative method]]** applied to legal institutions — “first sower” of comparative legal history in Pollock’s formulation as reported in the intro.

## Contributions

- **[[status-to-contract]]** — exact formula (Ch. V): movement of progressive societies from family-derived personal Status to Contract.
- **[[patriarchal-theory]]** — primeval condition as patriarchal household; unit of ancient society = Family, of modern = Individual; Family→Gens→Tribe→Commonwealth.
- **[[legal-fictions]]** — with equity and legislation, the three agencies of legal change in progressive systems (historical order).
- Comparative history of **testamentary succession** (universal succession / *hereditas*), **property** (joint ownership archaic; Indian village community), **contract** (late, ceremonial), **delict and crime** (torts and compositions before true criminal law).
- Progressive vs **stationary** societies binary (attributed — Ch. I–II): progressive societies are the rare exception where law deliberately keeps pace with social necessity.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Victorian historical/comparative jurisprudence. Interlocutors and later rivals: M’Lennan and [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]] as matriarchal/kinship critics of patriarchal theory (noted in J. H. Morgan’s intro); [[bachofen-johann-jakob|Bachofen]] mother-right on adjacent terrain; [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]/Morgan unilinear schemes on [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]. Affinity of **status/contract** language with Spencer’s militant/industrial types — do not merge ([[industrial-type-of-society]]). Dialogue with Bentham/Austin command theory of law (rejected for primitive “habit” of law) and with Grotian natural-law / social-contract traditions (Ch. III–IV).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Patriarchal priority and progressive/stationary framing contested by particularist anthropology and alternative Victorian kinship schemes. Village-community claims entered later land-tenure controversies. Record positions; do not adjudicate.

## From Stocking, *Victorian Anthropology* (1987)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1987; source [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987]].)* Stocking argues Maine's inquiry differed fundamentally from the sociocultural evolutionism of [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], Lubbock, and McLennan: Maine traced civilization's roots *backward* from surviving written records (Roman law, Sanskrit and Slavonic village-community evidence) rather than forward from an assumed prehistoric state, rejected any universal parallelism of social development, and worked within a biblical timescale essentially unaffected by the archaeological revolution of deep time. His comparative method rested on the **Aryan-race hypothesis** — that Indian and Roman institutions could illuminate one another because both descended from a common Aryan stock and had once shared a culture — not on any general premise of the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], which Maine regarded as dubious. His concept of [[survivals]], derived from Savigny's historical jurisprudence, likewise differed from Tylor's folklore-and-geology-derived survivals: Maine's rested on a demonstrable historical connection between ancestor and descendant groups, Tylor's on psychic unity licensing inference from irrational custom to a once-rational origin. Leslie Stephen later folded Maine into the evolutionary canon, but Stocking argues this obscures Maine's own insistence that his method differed fundamentally from that of the Darwinian-conditioned evolutionists — though the two traditions moved closer over time: in correspondence with [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]] on the Roman *gens* and Iroquois material, Maine acknowledged the two lines of inquiry "promise more and more to connect themselves together."

Stocking judges Maine's reputation the most genuinely open-ended of the classical Victorian evolutionists (Maine was excluded from the later Classics of Anthropology series). His distinction between kinship-based and territory-based social organization anticipated the core subject matter of social anthropology, and his influence persisted through Durkheim, Radcliffe-Brown's work on corporate groups, and Indian village-community studies (the latter now criticized as empirically flawed and ideologically constituted). It has even been argued that lineage theory, the dominant paradigm of modern British social anthropology, is a "transformation" of Maine's model — though that argument was made in the course of calling for lineage theory's rejection.

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 1988, Ch. 1–2.)* Kuper places Maine’s *[[maine-ancient-law-1861|Ancient Law]]* (1861) at the head of the anthropological series on [[primitive-society]] more appropriately than Darwin’s *Origin*: Maine set the agenda (origins of family, property, state) by embellishing a classic original condition rather than by a new scientific theory. Maine took little notice of Darwin or Spencer. Kuper reads *Ancient Law* as political inversion of Rousseau/Bentham Utilitarianism (especially regarding Indian Empire policy): synthesis of German Roman-legal history and philology (Cohen-style transformation) in service of standing radical contract theory on its head (Lévi-Strauss-style inversion). Late-century orthodoxy (Tylor/Frazer adjudication) settled matriarchal exogamous descent groups *against* Maine’s continued patriarchal dissent. Maine’s reputation was already past its peak when he published his final denunciation of rivals; the German *mark* model eroded under Vinogradoff and revisionists stressing private property, serfdom, and women’s property.
