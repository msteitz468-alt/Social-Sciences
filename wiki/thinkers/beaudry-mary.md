## Overview

Mary C. Beaudry is a historical archaeologist (affiliated with Boston University as of the 2006 publication of this Companion) whose work spans documentary archaeology, household archaeology, material culture studies, and narrative experimentation in archaeological writing. She co-edited *The Cambridge Companion to Historical Archaeology* ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]) with Dan Hicks and co-authored its Chapter 11 on material culture studies with Matthew D. Cochran.

## Contributions

Beaudry introduced the term "documentary archaeology" to the literature in the introduction to her edited volume *Documentary Archaeology in the New World* (Beaudry 1988a: 1), arguing that "historical archaeologists must develop an approach towards documentary analysis that is uniquely their own" ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). This work anchors the [[documentary-archaeology]] method.

Beaudry has been identified as a leading voice for interpretive historical archaeology, rejecting "totalising" approaches drawn from processualism, structuralism, and structural Marxism (Beaudry 1995, 1996), and arguing that interpretive historical archaeologists should attend to the close relationships between people and things, revealing "the intimate and unheralded details of day-to-day life" (Beaudry 1996: 496, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

With Stephen Mrozowski and others, Beaudry conducted household-archaeology fieldwork at the Boott Cotton Mills workers' housing in Lowell, Massachusetts (1987–89), examining intersections of industrial capitalism and corporate-paternalism ideology in company housing and sanitary conditions; the study emphasized workers' personal agency — hygiene practices, boardinghouse landscape shaping, appropriation of "out-of-sight" spaces — over a simple top-down victimization narrative (Beaudry 1989b; Mrozowski et al. 1989, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Beaudry (1989a) is credited with an influential critique of normative household models that reduce households to statistical abstractions identified only through a household head — the "faceless blobs" critique — later developed in Beaudry 1996, 2004 arguing household conflicts are not reducible to class struggle and household materialities negotiate more than domination/resistance ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). She has been a proponent of applying practice theory (Bourdieu, Giddens) to household archaeology (Beaudry 2004).

With Matthew Cochran, Beaudry (2006) contributed studies of needlework and sewing implements, examining the task of sewing, women's embodied self-presentation, and reformers' use of sewing to promote industry, cleanliness, and spirituality (Loren and Beaudry 2006, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Beaudry has also experimented with narrative form in archaeological writing: her 1998 four-part dialogue combined real and imagined women's journal entries, addressing the absence of women's voices from the documentary record — "I've often thought how marvellous it would be if other people ... had left us journals recording their observations" (Beaudry 1998: 20, quoted in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Her co-authored study of urban archaeology at the New York [[five-points-new-york|Five Points / Courthouse Block]] site (Beaudry et al. 1991) argued that urban archaeological narratives should be told "from the inside out" (Beaudry et al. 1991: 284), a position contrasted with outsider mythologizing of neighborhoods like Five Points.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Beaudry's critique of Mark Leone's Althusserian-ideology reading of the Paca garden (with others including Hodder 1986) has itself been read as part of a broader interpretive-vs-critical-archaeology tension over how ideology operates in historical landscapes ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]); see [[processual-archaeology]] / [[post-processual-archaeology]] for the wider debate context.

## Historiography

Beaudry's household-archaeology and documentary-archaeology programs are widely cited touchstones within the field as represented in this Companion; her co-editorship (with Hicks) of the volume itself is a historiographic marker of her standing in the discipline as of 2006.
