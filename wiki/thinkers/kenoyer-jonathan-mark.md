# Jonathan Mark Kenoyer

## Overview

Jonathan Mark Kenoyer is an American archaeologist specializing in the [[harappan-civilization|Indus Valley (Harappan) Civilization]], ancient technology, and craft production. As of the 1998 book *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]*, he was Associate Professor of Anthropology at the University of Wisconsin–Madison and Co-director / Field Director of the Harappa Archaeological Research Project (HARP, from 1986). Born in India, he holds a BA, MA, and Ph.D. (1983) in South Asian Archaeology from the University of California, Berkeley; his mentor was [[dales-george-f|George F. Dales, Jr.]]. The 1998 volume is the principal source for the theses summarized below.

## Contributions

All interpretive claims below are **Kenoyer’s positions as of 1998**, attributed throughout.

### Chronology and tradition

Kenoyer adopts [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]]’s **[[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]]** (Willey & Phillips “tradition” concept) for the long developmental process, reserving “Indus Valley civilization” for Integration Era urban expansion and cultural integration. He tables Early Food-producing (ca. 6500–5000 BCE) → Regionalization / Early Harappan (ca. 5000–2600) → Integration / Harappan Phase (2600–1900) → Localization / Late Harappan (1900–1300), with an **Indo-Gangetic tradition** linking Localization to later Ganga–Yamuna trajectories. Distinctive Indus features, he argues, evolve from local farming/pastoral communities, not from an isolated brief urban phase. Origins were **non-uniform** with regional time-lags (with Possehl, Shaffer, HARP’s 70+ new Harappa 14C dates). Cities grew from earlier villages over centuries, not by short-term visionary foundation.

### Polity and urban form

Kenoyer models **multiple large urban centers** as relatively independent political arenas (explicitly “four major cities” / city-state scale competition) with **competing elite groups** — merchants, ritual specialists, landowners — rather than a single priest-king despotism. Rulers are unknown by name; long-distance trade, craft control, and ideological symbols maintain order more than standing armies. He rejects collapsing archaeological culture, Mesopotamian **[[meluhha]]** label, Dravidian-language hypothesis, and seal-animal “clans” into one people/state/language. Population at Mohenjo-daro is revised above [[fairservis-walter|Fairservis]]’s lower-town estimate toward >250 ha inhabited area with seasonal fluctuation. Walls and gateways order traffic and taxation; warfare package is downplayed for Regionalization/Integration relative to Egypt/Mesopotamia stereotypes.

### Craft specialization (core program)

With cities ca. 2600 BCE, specialized crafts produce **unique objects that distinguish high officials and rulers**. Kenoyer’s **four-category craft typology** crosses local vs imported materials with simple vs complex technologies:

1. Local + simple (wood, basketry, simple weaving, terracotta, house-building) — most regionally varied  
2. Imported + simple  
3. Local + complex (e.g. stoneware bangles, painted pottery) — considerable standardization  
4. Imported + complex (long carnelian, seals, some metallurgy) — most standardized  

He argues **stoneware banglemaking, seal production, and chert weight manufacture** may have been **directly controlled** in segregated workshops by rulers/state to limit access to power symbols requiring standardized form; pottery and most ornaments were standardized more loosely (market, tradition, or indirect control). High-fired stoneware bangles and glazed faience were more tightly controlled than low-fired ceramics; stoneware bangles as power symbols were inscribed and used by elites mainly in the largest cities. Metal production was likely monitored by dividing labor stages under merchant or head-artisan control. Beadmaking (constricted cylindrical “Ernestite” drills; Chanhudaro long-carnelian workshop) is a signature complex craft feeding [[meluhha]] trade. Ethnoarchaeology (Khambhat agate, shell workers, Harappa potters) and experimental reconstruction support technological claims. See [[craft-specialization]] and [[standardized-weights]].

### Script and communication

Kenoyer emphasizes **uses** of the undeciphered script: seals for literate + animal-motif dual messaging to loaders; tablets; personal ornaments; workshop molds. Literate users, he argues, were those who controlled cities — ritual specialists, landowners, traders — not a mass literate public. After urban decline, seal carvers lost work and inscription on tools/pottery ceased even where many crafts continued — separating craft continuity from elite communication technologies. He reports majority scholarly Dravidian leanings and [[parpola-asko|Parpola]]’s rebus examples while holding **no full decipherment** proven.

### Religion, gender, and burial (selected)

Kenoyer reconstructs fertility/reproduction cults, horned yogic deities, pipal and water symbolism, and Great Bath ritual without equating seals to later Hindu deities as confirmed identities ([[indus-religion]], [[pashupati-seal]]). Ornament systems (shell chevron bangles, bangles as unity-with-rank symbols) integrate and rank society ([[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]]). Harappan burials show a practical attitude to wealth: precious metals kept among the living; essential personal symbols buried ([[bioarchaeology-of-the-indus]]).

### Localization and continuity

Ch. 9 frames urban fade as a pipal-sapling metaphor: political center shifts to middle Ganga over >1000 years; Localization (~1900–1300 BCE) breaks Integration into regional cultures (Cemetery H, Jhukar, Rangpur — maps after [[mughal-rafique|Mughal]]); seals, writing, and chert weights drop out as taxation/integration tools. Continuities in subsistence, craft knowledge (faience → glass; copper → iron trajectories), and ideology are argued carefully against invasion-only models.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kenoyer’s multi-elite / city-state reading of Indus polity is one pole of the [[indus-polity-debate]]; Possehl’s heterarchy / “civilization without the state” is a related but distinct formulation. Both challenge Wheeler–Piggott centralized despotism. Direct “state control” of seals/weights/stoneware is an **inference** from standardization and workshop segregation, not from deciphered administrative texts. Craft ethnoarchaeology is a methodological strength and a potential over-bridge if ethnographic analogy is smuggled as evidence rather than hypothesis-generator. Script-use models remain underdetermined without decipherment.
