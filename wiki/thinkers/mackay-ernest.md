## Overview

Ernest John Henry Mackay was a British archaeologist who succeeded
[[marshall-john|John Marshall]] as excavation director at [[mohenjo-daro]] from 1926
to 1931, following an earlier career trained in Egyptian field method under Flinders
Petrie and directing Mesopotamian excavations. He died in 1943. Birth date is not
given in the source ingested here and is left `[[unknown]]`.

## Contributions

Mackay was field-trained by Flinders Petrie in Egypt (working there until 1916), then
served in Palestine during the First World War as Custodian of Antiquities
(1919–22). He subsequently directed the Field Museum–Oxford Expedition to
Mesopotamia (1922), excavating Jamdat Nasr and Kish. Possehl records that Mackay
joined the Archaeological Survey of India in 1926 specifically for this Mesopotamian
expertise, succeeding Marshall as director at [[mohenjo-daro]] for the final five
major excavation seasons (1926–31), during which the Assembly Hall, the "Seated Man"
and "Stern Man" sculptures, the "Lady of L Area" sculpture, and the "dancing girl"
bronze were recovered. Excavation at Mohenjo-daro halted in 1931 amid an ASI budget
crisis — a telegram halted the dig mid-season on 6 November 1931.

Mackay later directed the excavation at Chanhu-daro (1935–36), funded via W. Norman
Brown and the Museum of Fine Arts, Boston — one of the first Indus excavations
permitted to an "outsider" (non-British-Indian) archaeologist, a status only allowed
from 1932 onward under colonial-era ASI administration. He is credited, alongside
Marshall and [[childe-v-gordon]], with co-developing the early Marshall–Mackay–Childe
synthesis of the Indus Civilization as a peaceful, mercantile, non-theocratic urban
culture (see [[indus-polity-debate]]).

## Historiography

Mackay's Mesopotamian background directly shaped the comparative frame within which
early Indus archaeology was read (the "Indo-Sumerian" hypothesis Marshall himself
later rejected in 1926, and the broader Marshall–Mackay–Childe synthesis of a
peaceful trading civilization). His Chanhu-daro dig marks the first relaxation of the
colonial-era restriction on non-British-Indian excavators in the Indus field.
