# Philip Phillips

American archaeologist (1900–1994); co-author with [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon R. Willey]] of *[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory in American Archaeology]]* (1958). Lead author order on the 1953 culture-historical-integration paper; co-author on the 1955 historical-developmental paper; introduction to the 1958 book draws on Phillips 1955.

## Overview

Phillips’s Midwestern/Southeastern field experience (with Ford, Griffin, and others) supplies many of *Method and Theory*’s cautionary examples: formal cultural equation without independent dating in the Southeast; neglect of radiocarbon on charts; fiber-tempered pottery “synchrony” assumptions; Hopewell and Mississippian as Formative-threshold cases. The book is dedicated (with Willey) to James A. Ford.

## Contributions

*(From primary ingest of *Method and Theory* 1958.)*

- Co-architect of the **unit system** ([[archaeological-component]], [[archaeological-phase]], horizon, tradition) and of **[[culture-historical-integration]]** as the name of descriptive-level work.
- Co-architect of **[[processual-interpretation]]** as explanatory-level aim and of the five-stage New World scheme.
- Sustained critique of **area chronologies** built on cultural similarity alone — especially southeastern practice — while endorsing Rouse’s extracultural ideal ([[radiocarbon-dating]], dendrochronology, geological events).
- Typology stance (with Ford–Griffin literature): types combine design and discovery; aim is increasing behavioral correspondence — not pure arbitrary design or pure natural discovery.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Collaborators:** [[willey-gordon-r]]; James A. Ford; James B. Griffin (cited extensively, sometimes critically on Eastern U.S. “culture periods”).
- **Method dialogue:** McKern, Kidder, Rouse, Spaulding, Taylor, Kroeber, Childe, Steward (as in Willey page).
