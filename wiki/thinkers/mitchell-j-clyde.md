# J. Clyde Mitchell

Rhodes-Livingstone Institute and [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] anthropologist and later pioneer of social network analysis. Enters the wiki as co-author, with [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] and [[barnes-john|John Barnes]], of "The Village Headman in British Central Africa" (1949), the essay that framed the headman as an [[intercalary-role|intercalary position]] between the village and the administrative hierarchy.

## Overview

Rhodes-Livingstone Institute researcher (Yao fieldwork, Nyasaland, 1940s), later RLI director and Manchester professor; documented here through Gluckman's *Order and Rebellion* (1963).

## Contributions

- Co-author of the 1949 village-headman analysis (see [[intercalary-role]]); Gluckman's 1963 introduction to *Order and Rebellion* credits the essay's argument to joint Rhodes-Livingstone work.
- **Yao village leadership (Essay V, section 2, published 1949; fieldwork Nyasaland).** Mitchell described Yao village organization as built around matrilineal "sorority-groups" (*mbumba*), each headed by a male **"warden of the sorority-group"** (*asyene mbumba*) — typically the eldest brother of a set of adult sisters — responsible for domestic-quarrel mediation, marriage surety, health and sorcery matters, and court representation for his dependents. A headman's status ("a big man... has many people/huts") was measured by number of dependents, producing structural instability as younger brothers or a headman's own children's lineages competed for followers, and villages periodically fissioned along genealogical lines. Mitchell gives a dated **1946 case example** illustrating the pattern. He notes the Yao's historical prominence as domestic slavers/slave-traders (per Livingstone's accounts and colonial suppression efforts) and argues the pre-colonial status-through-dependents value system persisted into the 1949 present despite the abolition of slavery.
- *The Yao Village* (1956) is cited by Gluckman as exemplary of analysis in **"structural time"** — following a succession of witchcraft-accusation cases and segment oppositions over years, ending in village fission, treating quarrelling as inherent in the village developmental cycle rather than as pathology — and as a direct precedent for the [[extended-case-method]]. *The Kalela Dance* (1956), with A. L. Epstein's *Politics in an Urban African Community* (1958), is cited by Gluckman as part of the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute's coordinated urban/rural research programme documenting the emergence of trade unions and urban associations among still tribally-networked Africans.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Director of the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute after Gluckman and Elizabeth Colson; part of the Gluckman-trained Manchester cohort (see [[manchester-school]]).
