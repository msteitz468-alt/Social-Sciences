## Overview

Augustus Henry Lane Fox Pitt Rivers (1827–1900), known for most of his career as
Colonel (later General) Lane Fox before inheriting the Rivers estate and the Pitt
Rivers name in 1880, was a British archaeologist and museum founder whom George W.
Stocking, Jr. (*Victorian Anthropology*, 1987) treats as, in Stocking's words, "rather
more consistently Darwinian than his sociocultural evolutionary confreres" (attributed,
Stocking 1987).

## Contributions

Pitt Rivers applied Spencerian evolutionary psychology and natural-selection reasoning
to classify "products of human industry" into genera, species, and varieties (attributed,
Stocking 1987). Stocking's central claim about his method is that, unlike peers such as
[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] and [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], Pitt Rivers insisted on
tracing a given material form back to a single geographical source rather than
accepting independent invention — a position for which his friends Tylor and Lubbock
criticized him (attributed, Stocking 1987). By 1875 he declared it "sheer moonshine" to
study anthropology on any basis other than that of development (attributed, Pitt Rivers
per Stocking 1987), and his 1875 presidential address to the Anthropological Institute
referred casually to "the progress of anthropological science," a phrase Stocking reads
as signaling the acceptance of the name "anthropology" after the 1871 society merger
(finding, Stocking 1987).

His ethnological collection was, from its beginning, organized on typological-
developmental (evolutionary) principles, distinct from the geographically organized
collection of Henry Christy (finding, Stocking 1987); Pitt Rivers and Christy's two
collections are described by Stocking as the two major ethnological collections of the
evolutionary period, both apparently stimulated by the 1851 Great Exhibition, with Pitt
Rivers's arrangement of spears, bows, and clubs from simple to complex resembling the
Exhibition's own judging system (finding, Stocking 1987). A parliamentary committee
rejected his 1878 offer of the collection to the British Museum; in 1882 he offered it
to Oxford University instead, resulting in the Pitt Rivers Museum, with a stipulated
lectureship in anthropology (deed amended 1884) (finding, Stocking 1987). Pitt Rivers
participated in Huxley's 1866–1871 negotiations that formed the Anthropological
Institute, and in 1874 co-chaired, with [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], the committee
producing *Notes and Queries on Anthropology* (finding, Stocking 1987). In 1881 he
coined the term "applied anthropology" in connection with Sir Bartle Frere's paper on
savage-cultured race relations, though Stocking notes this did not spur real
institutional interest in colonial application (attributed, Stocking 1987).

Stocking also documents a distinctive political and religious cast to Pitt Rivers's
evolutionism: unlike most sociocultural evolutionists, he did not "banish God from
history" and did not write about the evolution of religion, reflecting a large personal
stake in the existing social order (attributed, Stocking 1987). He explicitly framed
his displayed sequences of artifacts as political instruction for the working classes
in gradualism, arguing that impressing them with the "slow growth" and continuity of
evolutionary development would make them less receptive to revolutionary agitation —
he wrote that the "law that nature makes no jumps" could be taught through the history
of mechanical contrivances "in such a way as at least to make men cautious how they
listen to scatter-brained revolutionary suggestions" (attributed, Pitt Rivers per
Stocking 1987). He also suggested that the very timing of the discovery of evolutionary
theory — arriving just before what he saw as the imminent extermination of "savage
races" that made the comparative method possible — was itself evidence of "an
overruling power" (attributed, Pitt Rivers per Stocking 1987).

## Archaeological Method (per Wheeler 1954)

Where Stocking treats Pitt Rivers as an evolutionist collector, [[wheeler-mortimer|Mortimer
Wheeler]] treats him as the founder of scientific excavation — "the first substantive advance
in the technique of excavation and recording" (attributed, Wheeler 1954). On his Cranborne
Chase estate he excavated for some twenty years with, in Wheeler's judgment, "a science and
scholarship that half a century of subsequent work has supplemented rather than superseded."
His methodological principles, as Wheeler transmits them, became the foundation of modern
field practice: **total recording** — every object plotted so it could be "replaced
accurately in its findspot on the recorded plan and section" (the origin of
[[stratigraphic-excavation|three-dimensional recording]], "the essence of modern
excavation"); the **full-removal** excavation of barrows (the whole mound removed and, where
possible, restored); a **trained, supervised staff** under the rule that "no excavation ought
ever to be permitted except under the immediate eye of a responsible and trustworthy
superintendent"; and the publication dictum "a discovery dates only from the time of the
record of it, and not from the time of its being found in the soil" (attributed, Pitt Rivers
per Wheeler 1954). Wheeler called him "a scholar not merely because he dug well, but above
all because his powerful imagination enabled him to visualize and create new standards of
scientific accuracy" — a "super-accuracy" he carried over from his earlier study of firearms,
illustrating (in Wheeler's reading) that rigorous method transfers across subject matter.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Pitt Rivers worked within the same Ethnological/Anthropological Institute circle as
[[huxley-thomas-henry|T. H. Huxley]], [[tylor-edward-burnett|Edward Tylor]], and
[[lubbock-john|John Lubbock]], the latter two serving as friendly critics of his
diffusionist insistence on single geographical origins for material-culture forms. His
typological method is presented by Stocking as an application of
[[social-evolutionism|social-evolutionist]] and Spencerian principles to material
culture rather than to society or belief directly.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking (1987) singles Pitt Rivers out as anomalous among the classical evolutionists
both methodologically (his stricter, more explicitly Darwinian insistence on tracing
single origins, against the diffuse "independent invention" assumptions common to
[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] and others) and politically (his explicit use of
evolutionary gradualism as an argument against working-class revolutionary sentiment).
Stocking treats this political motive as a documented fact about Pitt Rivers's public
rhetoric, not as representative of the movement as a whole.

Against the heroic Wheeler account above, [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]
(*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches to Fieldwork]]*, 2002),
drawing on Bowden (1991) and Barrett et al. (1991), argues (attributed) that Pitt Rivers's
reputation as the revolutionary founder of scientific excavation is overstated. His Wor
Barrow dig began conventionally (a single trench, as his teacher Canon Greenwell had done)
before he removed the whole mound; his actual digging methods were "in many ways no
different" from contemporaries', merely applied "on a more extensive scale." Bowden's verdict
is that "his recording was meticulous but his physical digging methods let him down… He dug
in spits and not by layers" — Pitt Rivers rarely addressed stratigraphic phasing, and toward
the end of Cranborne Chase he abandoned true measured sections for generalized "average
sections." Lucas's reading is that his real innovation was not technique but the
**totalisation** of recording and recovery — an extension of his interest in evolutionary
typology as "an exercise in totalising objects" — and that his reports show "very little real
interest in interpreting the site." Lucas correspondingly rejects Wheeler's claim that the
decades after Pitt Rivers were "retrograde," pointing to Bulleid and Gray's Glastonbury
excavation as "as good, if not even better" (see [[stratigraphic-excavation]]).
