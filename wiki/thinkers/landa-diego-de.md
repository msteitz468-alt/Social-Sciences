# Diego de Landa

## Overview

Diego de Landa (born 12 November 1524, Cifuentes, New Castile; died 1579 among the Maya of Yucatán) was a Spanish Franciscan missionary and, later, Bishop of Yucatán whose *Relación de las Cosas de Yucatán* became, in Michael Coe’s account, the principal ethnohistoric key to [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing|Maya hieroglyphic writing]] and the [[calendar-round|Calendar Round]] (as reported by Coe 2012). According to Coe, Landa went to Yucatán in 1547 with five other Franciscan priests and in 1549 was named assistant to the guardian of Izamal—a town that before the Conquest had venerated Itzamnaaj, inventor of writing in Yucatec belief. Coe presents Landa both as destroyer of almost all surviving lowland Maya books and as the colonial author who first matched day and month names to their hieroglyphs and recorded an “A,B,C” of Maya characters later crucial to phonetic decipherment ([[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]).

## Contributions

According to Coe (2012), the *Relación* (written in Spain around 1566 from notes and materials brought from Yucatán) was the first source to present names of days in the 260-day calendar and months of the ~365-day year with their appropriate hieroglyphs. From that material alone, Coe reports, any decipherer could interpret Maya hieroglyphic dates expressed in the 52-year [[calendar-round|Calendar Round]]. Landa also described Maya book culture: characters or letters used for ancient affairs and sciences; figures and signs in the figures; and Spanish destruction of books for “superstition and lies of the devil,” which the Maya greatly regretted (Landa quoted via Coe).

Coe highlights Landa’s “A,B,C” of Maya characters—with worked examples *le* (noose/to hunt), *ha* (water), and *ma in kati* (“I don’t want to”)—as the long-sought key to the hieroglyphs, the “Rosetta Stone” dreamed of since Rafinesque, [[stephens-john-lloyd|John Lloyd Stephens]], and Catherwood. Coe also notes oddities that should have cautioned pure alphabetic application: three signs for *a*, two for *b*, and CV signs such as *cu*/*ku*. Coe argues Landa was misled into treating Maya writing as alphabetic rather than logophonetic; Yuri Knorosov later held that Landa’s signs have the phonetic values attributed to them and that most are syllabic CV combinations, not alphabetic letters ([[knorosov-yuri]]; [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]).

Coe cites Landa for the fullest surviving account of Maya life on the eve of Conquest: a prosperous Yucatán of nobility, free farming commoners, and slaves of little economic importance (not Greco-Roman plantation slavery). Joseph T. Goodman’s Long Count–Christian calendar correlation, later refined into the GMT correlation, drew in part on Landa and colonial sources (as reported by Coe).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Landa’s principal informants, Coe reports, were Juan Nachi Kokom and Gaspar Antonio Chi—noble princes and probably trained scribes—not the “barefooted Indians” of Philipp Valentini’s later critique of the alphabet. Downstream users of the *Relación* form the main intellectual afterlife: Abbé Brasseur de Bourbourg recovered an abridged anonymous multi-hand copy (apparently 1661, not the original) at Madrid’s Royal Academy of History in 1862 and published it in 1864; [[forstemann-ernst|Ernst Förstemann]] used Landa’s days and months for Dresden calendrics; Cyrus Thomas linked Troano New Year pages to Landa’s year-end rituals; Benjamin Lee Whorf and later Knorosov treated the list as genuine phonetic evidence; [[thompson-j-eric-s|J. Eric S. Thompson]] long rejected or minimized that use. Coe ranks the document as more generous in sign readings than the Rosetta Stone was for Egyptology, yet spurned by most Mayanists for about a century after Brasseur’s recovery ([[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]]; [[epigraphy]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Coe states that Landa was a fanatic against native idolatry; in 1562 he began infamous, perhaps illegal, idolatry proceedings with enormous cruelties, and almost all surviving lowland Maya books perished in his auto-da-fé at Maní. Not yet a bishop, he was accused of exceeding authority and recalled to Spain in 1563; during those “black years” he wrote the *Relación*, was exonerated, returned in 1572 as Bishop, and died seven years later among the Maya.

Valentini’s 1880 pamphlet “The Landa Alphabet; a Spanish Fabrication” argued Landa elicited picture-writing responses by pronouncing Spanish letters; Coe holds Valentini wrong that the signs were mere pictures but right that they responded to Spanish letter names as pronounced—and notes Valentini’s approach became a club against phoneticists well into Coe’s time. Brasseur’s pure-alphabetic, backwards reading of the Troano Codex threw phonetic approaches into nearly a century of opprobrium (as reported by Coe). Eduard Seler and, later, Thompson treated the “Landa method” as missionary-instigated and original writing as largely ideographic. Knorosov and Western phoneticists reversed that verdict: Coe calls the Landa “A,B,C” the unrecognized Rosetta of Maya studies ([[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]).

## Historiography

The surviving *Relación* Brasseur found is not Landa’s original but an abridgment of a larger lost treatise; Coe still treats it as a gold mine on Maya life and as the true Rosetta Stone of Maya writing “in spite of the denial by generations of epigraphers.” Primary filing source for this page: [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012]] (Coe’s partisan history of decipherment). Landa appears throughout Coe’s narrative wherever calendar keys, phonetic method, book burning, and Conquest-era society are at issue—from Brasseur’s discovery through Knorosov’s Landa-based syllabary to late twentieth-century readings that still cite Landa day, month, and syllable values ([[maya-hieroglyphic-writing]]; [[epigraphy]]).
