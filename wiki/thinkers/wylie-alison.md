# Alison Wylie

Philosopher of science and archaeologist whose **[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]** chapter (1991) supplies the volume’s load-bearing **epistemology**: why archaeology lagged other social sciences on gender research, and what the prospects are for an archaeology of gender.

## Overview

Wylie poses two guiding questions from feminist commitments: (1) why is there no counterpart in archaeology to vibrant research traditions on women and gender elsewhere in the social sciences? (2) what are the prospects for developing an archaeology of gender at this juncture? She rejects as unsatisfactory the commonplace that women and gender are **methodologically inaccessible** in archaeological contexts — that archaeology is “too limited” and must await theory from fields with direct access to living subjects. Material constraints are real, she argues, but the research gap is better explained by androcentric frameworks, essentialism, and (on her reading) [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] priorities that sidelined internal social/“ethnographic” variables. Feminist philosophy of science (empiricist, standpoint, and postmodern strands) shows gender research can be epistemically productive. Prospects as of the early 1990s she judges good; **pluralism** is warranted.

## Contributions

- Diagnosis of the absence of an archaeology of gender (late 1980s).
- Critique of the inaccessibility alibi.
- Mapping of stages of feminist inquiry (critique → reclaim subjects → rework theory) onto archaeology.
- Bridge from feminist philosophy of science to archaeological method/theory; engagement with Binfordian and postprocessual epistemic claims.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Feminist philosophy of science; dialogue with [[processual-archaeology]] and [[post-processual-archaeology]]; volume network with [[gero-joan-m|Gero]] and [[conkey-margaret-w|Conkey]]. Parallel concerns with Hodder-style interpretive openings without equating feminism to post-processualism.

## Densified — "Chilly-Making Activities" and the Relativism Critique, per Johnson (2011)

*(Attributed; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson's secondary account attributes to Wylie the term **"chilly-making activities"** for the subtle, often non-overt exclusionary practices — language, career-trajectory assumptions, patterns of conversation — that reproduce sexism in archaeology without requiring conscious bias. Johnson also quotes Wylie's critique of strong constructivist/relativist positions as an "ideology of the powerful" that obscures the real powerlessness of the marginalized: "strong constructivist and relativist positions embody what seem to be patently an ideology of the powerful" (Wylie 1992b: 25). See [[feminist-archaeology]] and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Feminist critiques of science (2010 reprint)

*(Attributed — Alison Wylie, "Good Science, Bad Science, or Science as Usual?," in [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)*
Wylie distinguishes three feminist commitments — gender structures social life; women are
systematically disadvantaged *as women*; this is unjust (the first two empirical, the third
normative) — and offers a four-part typology of feminist science critique: **equity** (exclusion of
women from institutions), **content** (male-skewed research domains), **standpoint** (results
reflecting practitioners' gendered standpoint — "Man the Hunter," Kohlberg's all-male moral-stage
samples critiqued by Gilligan), and **reflexive** (feminists critiquing their own earlier work).
Drawing on Longino, she argues objectivity itself "requires that we increase the diversity of those
recruited to practice science." A foundational statement for [[feminist-archaeology]].
