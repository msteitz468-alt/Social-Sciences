# George C. Homans

## Overview

George Caspar Homans (1910–1990) was an American sociologist at Harvard, best known for *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]* (1950) — an inductive theory of small-group structure built from reanalysis of industrial, street-corner, Polynesian, and New England community cases — and later for social-exchange theory (*Social Behavior*, 1961; not yet ingested). Junior Fellow at Harvard from 1934; intellectual formation under L. J. Henderson's Pareto circle and contact with industrial-sociology and interaction-measurement work (Mayo, Roethlisberger, Chapple, Whyte, Arensberg).

## Contributions

- **Inductive small-group theory.** *The Human Group* defines the group by face-to-face interaction frequency, decomposes behavior into [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, and sentiment]], and analyzes mutual dependence within the [[external-system|external]] and [[internal-system|internal]] systems. Cases: Bank Wiring Observation Room (Hawthorne); Norton Street Gang ([[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte]]); Tikopia family (Firth); Hilltown (Hatch); Electrical Equipment Company (Arensberg et al.).
- **Bridge from industrial / anthropological field studies to general hypotheses.** Homans treated detailed case monographs as the raw material for cross-group generalization — clinical material feeding analytical science.
- **Later exchange theory** (outside this source): reduction of group processes to elementary social behavior and reinforcement; often contrasted with Parsonsian systems theory (see Gouldner's map of rival infrastructures). Keep *Human Group* apparatus distinct from 1961 exchange formalism until *Social Behavior* is ingested.

### Five cases in *The Human Group*

| Case | Role in Homans’s argument |
|---|---|
| Bank Wiring (Hawthorne) | Master illustration of AIS, cliques, external/internal cut, output norms |
| Norton Street Gang (Whyte) | Autonomous group; bowling–rank; leadership; reciprocity as control |
| Tikopia family (Firth) | Kinship as near-universal small group; interpersonal role matrix |
| Hilltown (Hatch) | Social *disintegration* → anomie path |
| Electrical Equipment Co. (Arensberg) | Social *conflict* under technical/organizational change; leadership practice |

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Homans himself later revised the claim that activity–interaction–sentiment are mutually dependent *in the external system* — external conditions are better treated as parameters/boundary conditions for the internal system (Hare/Polley intro citing Homans 1984).
- Mid-century small-group boom both confirmed and superseded his natural-group focus (lab experiments, group dynamics readers).
- Exchange-theory phase drew separate critiques (reductionism, behaviorism) not fully applicable to the 1950 book.
- **The reductionism dispute with Blau.** Homans's [[social-exchange-theory|exchange theory]] (*Social Behavior*, 1961) grounds exchange in behaviorist reinforcement and individual psychology; he called himself a "methodological individualist" and, in "Bringing Men Back In" (1964), argued sociology's general propositions reduce to psychology. [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] built on Homans (Homans's 1958 article had quoted Blau's federal-agency consultation data at length; Blau's 1964 *Exchange and Power* names *Social Behavior* the pervasive influence on its first half) but rejected the reductionism, holding that social structures "have emergent properties that cannot be understood on the basis of the properties of the subunits." See [[exchange-theory-reductionism-debate]].

## Historiography

Transaction 1992 introductions (Hare; Polley) place the book in Harvard interpersonal networks and subsequent small-group research; Strodtbeck & Hare (1954) bibliography rated it among the most highly rated contributions to small-group research. Reception as organization/management classic in the Transaction series is a second life beside the sociological one.
