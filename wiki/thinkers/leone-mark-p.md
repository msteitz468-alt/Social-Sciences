# Mark P. Leone

American historical archaeologist; principal architect of **[[critical-archaeology|critical historical archaeology]]** in the United States; long director of **Archaeology in Annapolis** (University of Maryland with Historic Annapolis, from 1981). Primary vault source: *[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Critical Historical Archaeology]]* (2010), a career retrospective of fourteen abbreviated essays plus autobiographical framing that ties outdoor history museums, Annapolis, Mormon landscapes, public tours, and African American archaeology to Marxist and Althusserian theories of [[ideology]].

Deep treatment: **[[hubs/thinkers/leone-mark-p|Thinkers Hub biography]]**.

## Overview

Leone's program asks how U.S. [[historical-archaeology]] can produce knowledge that illuminates **present** social conditions — class antagonism, race, the ideology of liberty under slavery and capitalism — rather than only reconstructing elite taste or serving patriotic outdoor-history narratives. He is best known for reading the **[[william-paca-garden|William Paca Garden]]** as ideology (not taste), for **"Toward a Critical Archaeology"** (Leone, Potter, and Shackel 1987), and for experiments in critical [[public-archaeology]] (Annapolis "Method as Message" tours; African American collaborative exhibits). The 2010 book insists research problems begin in **feelings** (anger, awe, annoyance at museums and remains), not in National Register criteria alone.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Leone 2010 frame and reprinted essays.)*

- **Critical archaeology** as named HA research program ([[critical-archaeology]]): illuminate how material culture and public history naturalize contradiction; design public interpretation that can unmask rather than re-mask inequality.
- **Ideology in things** — perspective gardens, tableware and clocks, museum galleries, Mormon temple and fences — as naturalization of hierarchy under developing capitalism (Althusserian masking; Marx on consciousness).
- **Rewriting [[georgian-worldview|Georgian patterning]]** (Deetz/Glassie): order, segmentation, and symmetry as **ideology of hierarchy** and cultural defense of new wealth, not only as cognitive "worldview."
- **Feelings as problem-finding method** (2010 Preface): questions about segregation, poverty, partial democracy, demolition, and waste arise from emotional encounter with living U.S. history and outdoor museums; psychoanalysis and Schemann on learned feeling shape the method narrative.
- **Public practice** — free site tours teaching "the past is thought up, not dug up"; African American HA as alternative voice (1995 multi-author essay); honest record of failures (slide shows, Historic Preservation Commission conflict, limited uptake of ideology message).
- **Site significance** — with Potter (1992): Habermasian dialogue and descendant/disfranchised questions against pseudo-objective National Register ranking that compromises ephemeral poor places.
- **First theoretical critique of time in archaeology.** Gavin Lucas ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 3) credits Leone (1978) with the first theoretical treatment of time in archaeology, raising both past people's perception of time and the archaeologist's own view of it; Leone's later work exposed the ideological nature of the archaeological presentation of the past at sites such as Colonial Williamsburg (Leone 1981a).
- **William Paca Garden, summarized.** Per Randall McGuire's account (Hicks & Beaudry 2006, ch. 8), Leone's reading of the Paca garden's optical illusions of perspective held that they were designed to hide the contradiction between Paca — signatory of the Declaration of Independence and enslaver of Africans — and the professed principle that "all men are created equal," and more broadly, American society's habitual capacity to ignore such contradictions.
- **Annapolis tour "failure" and the Habermas turn.** The Archaeology in Annapolis public program aimed to provoke critical reflection but, per Leone (1995) and Potter (1994), largely failed: tourists reinterpreted the alternative history in capitalist terms rather than shedding ideological preconceptions. Leone subsequently adopted Habermas's (1984) notion of "communicative action" and redirected Annapolis research toward the African-American working-class community (Leone 1995; Mullins 1999; Shackel et al. 1998).
- **"Possessive individualism"** (Leone 2005) — a concept naming new attitudes toward material culture and individual mastery-for-display that accompanied the shift from merchant to industrial capitalism, in which people were constantly obliged to remake themselves through goods.
- **Hoodoo research** (2005) — study of African hoodoo practices in the southern US, an attempted alternative history of lifeways at the edge of, or beyond, capitalism.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Leone records the standard charge that Marxists force data to theory and answers by starting from feelings and data cores with theory stripped from reprints. Broader field critiques of ideology theory (uniformly shared meaning; "duped" actors — see [[marxist-archaeology]]) apply to early critical HA; later African American collaboration and resistance-oriented work partially answer. Public critical tours produced measurable visitor categories of insight (Potter evaluations) but, Leone held, often failed to change Annapolis power or permanent museum messaging; Historic Annapolis blocked some leftist public interpretations. Relation to Orser: shared capitalism/modern-world concern; different scale (Chesapeake symbolic center vs. global mutualism). Leone's Althusserian reading of the Paca garden was itself criticized by Ian Hodder (1986) and Mary Beaudry and colleagues (1991), who favored more relational, situated readings of landscape ideology over a single naturalizing message ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. De Cunzo & Ernstein); see [[critical-archaeology]].

**Hall (2000) on the "hidden transcript" gap.** [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000) critiqued Leone's and Shackel's Chesapeake critical-materialist work for relying on a "blanketing 'dominant ideology'" — "a pervasive false consciousness that prevented the dominated from seeing their entrapment, and from resisting" (Hall 1992a) — which recovers only the elite "public transcript" and leaves the subaltern "hidden transcript" of everyday resistance ([[scott-james-c|Scott]]) unrecovered. See [[critical-archaeology]], [[theory-in-historical-archaeology-debate]]; study [[hall-archaeology-modern-world-study]].

## Historiography

Textbook maps (Johnson; Hodder & Hutson) already place Leone/Annapolis as the U.S. neo-Marxist HA landmark. *Critical Historical Archaeology* (2010) is the author's own framing — theory omitted from reprints, motive restored in prefaces — assembled with Collingwood's *Autobiography* as a model of intellectual self-account. Primary vault densification begins with that volume; monographic site reports and full *Current Anthropology* exchange remain outstanding.
