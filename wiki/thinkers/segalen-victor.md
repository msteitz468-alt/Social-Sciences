# Victor Segalen

## Overview

Victor Segalen (1878–1919), born in Brittany, was a French naval doctor, poet, novelist, and archaeologist. He traveled in Polynesia 1902–1905, arriving in the Marquesas just after Paul Gauguin's death and sending the first detailed account of Gauguin's last weeks to Paris, and spent nearly five years in China before his death in 1919 — of obscure cause, linked by contemporaries to a "spiritual crisis," with persistent rumors of suicide. (Source: [[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]], Ch. 5 "A Poetics of Displacement: Victor Segalen.")

## Contributions

Clifford presents Segalen as a key figure in a "postsymbolist poetics of displacement," alongside Rimbaud in Abyssinia, Blaise Cendrars, [[leiris-michel]] in Africa, and Antonin Artaud among the Tarahumara. Segalen's central concept, developed in the unfinished long essay *Essai sur l'exotisme*, is exoticism redefined as an "aesthetic of the diverse" ("esthétique du divers"), explicitly rejecting the conventional exoticism of Pierre Loti and Paul Claudel's "co-naissance" (participatory knowledge) of the East: "Segalen redefines exoticism as an 'aesthetic of the diverse.'"

Clifford argues Segalen's exoticist project draws on his friend Jules de Gaultier's "Law of Bovaryism" ("Every being in conceiving of itself conceives itself as necessarily other than it is") and reads it as an ultimately self-referential, failed quest: "By the end of his career the self, not the other, has become exotic" — which Clifford calls "both an epitome and a critique of the white man's relentless quest for himself."

Segalen's first novel, *Les Immémoriaux* (1907), is narrated from the standpoint of a Tahitian oral performer (Terii) whose forgetting of ritual genealogy allegorizes the arrival of European colonial power and the death of "Maori Civilization"; Clifford calls it Segalen's "best-known and least characteristic work," grounded in the best Polynesian ethnographic scholarship available at the time. His later China writings — *Stèles* (1912), *Peintures* (1916), *Briques et tuiles* (written 1910, published 1975), *Équipée* (1929, recording a pre-WWI expedition toward but not into Tibet), and the novel *René Leys* (1922, set during the fall of the Qing dynasty in Peking, 1912) — progressively shift, in Clifford's reading, from an early sonorous/oral evocative mode (Tahiti) to a visual, inscriptional, and self-referential mode (China) in which the search for "le Divers" collapses into personal projection. Clifford reads *René Leys* as an anti-Orientalist parable: the narrator's desire for total "inside" knowledge of the Forbidden City, echoing Loti's *Aziyadé*, is progressively destabilized, ending with "no more ultimate depths: the search for revelations is shown to be endless... an ethnography of signs without essential content."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Influenced by Jules de Gaultier's theory of Bovaryism; grouped by Clifford with Rimbaud, Cendrars, [[leiris-michel]], and Artaud as exemplars of a shared literary "poetics of displacement" rather than as a school with formal ties. No teacher/student relations to other wiki-linked thinkers are recorded in this source.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

No independent scholarly critique of Segalen's work is recorded in this source; Clifford's own reading treats Segalen's project as internally self-undermining (the collapse of "the diverse" into self-projection) rather than externally refuted.
