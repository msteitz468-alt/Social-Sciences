# David Stuart

## Overview

American Maya epigrapher (b. Washington 1965) whom [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012|Coe (2012)]] presents as unmatched in “epigraphic precocity”—comparing him favorably even to Champollion’s early start. Son of National Geographic archaeological editor George E. Stuart; MacArthur Fellow at eighteen (1984); core of Coe’s “Young Turks” with [[houston-stephen|Stephen Houston]], Mathews, Taube, MacLeod, and Grube, under [[schele-linda|Schele]] and [[lounsbury-floyd|Lounsbury]]. Primary densification here is Coe’s narrative through ~early 1990s career landmarks; later professional biography remains thin in this source. Coe lists Stuart among advisers who kept post-1992/1999 editions current.

## Contributions

### Formation (as reported by Coe 2012)

Early schooling in Chapel Hill, NC; 1968 (age three) first Mesoamerican trip (Monte Albán, Chichén Itzá, [[tikal]]). Summers 1974–75 at Cobá while George Stuart mapped: sketched new stelae, copied Thompson’s *Introduction* glyph drawings “just for the fun of it,” acquired Yucatec vocabulary without full speech. A 1975 cha-chahk rain ceremony in Cobá’s plaza (Chemax h-men; balche, cigarettes, Coca-Cola offerings) reinforced his resolve. That summer he met [[thompson-j-eric-s|Sir Eric Thompson]]—driven around Yucatán by the Stuarts—“the man who had written the book.”

In 1976 Schele, consulting on *The Mysterious Maya*, invited him to [[palenque]] after the eleven-year-old identified a “Fire glyph.” She later told Coe he read the Temple of the Sun tablet in eight hours with little consultation—progress the adult team had taken five years to reach. Stuart attributed early skill more to **drawing** glyphs than reading books. June 1978 Mesa Redonda: presented a Cross Group compound-glyph paper before turning thirteen. Winter 1980–81 expedition epigrapher at Naj Tunich (SE Petén): first phonetic-sign decipherment, month Pax as *pa-x(a)* via Landa’s netted *pa* + Knorosovian synharmony. 1983 Mesa Redonda: “count-of-captives” complex (*aj* + number + *bak* “bone/captive” rebus) as “he of *x* captives” (e.g. Bird-Jaguar of Yaxchilán as “he of the twenty captives”). 1983–84 Dumbarton Oaks fellowship before college; February 1984 MacArthur Fellowship ($128,000) just out of high school—Coe says publicity did not alter his self-effacing character. Two post-high-school years of full-time glyph work with Schele cemented the career.

### Phonetic and calendrical readings

Coe ties Stuart’s “Ten Phonetic Syllables” (Research Reports on Ancient Maya Writing no. 14, series edited by George Stuart) to the search for free **substitution** patterns—homophony/polyvalence methods advanced at Albany 1979 and by Houston’s four/snake/sky = *kan*/*chan* demonstration—filling blanks in Kelley’s syllabic grid with Lounsbury–Fox–Justeson methodology and Schele collaboration. At D.O. he re-read Thompson’s ADI/PDI fish-head not as *xok* “shark/count” but as *u*/*ut* “to happen/come to pass”; Justeson, Norman, Josserand, and Hopkins refined this with Ch’olan grammar (*ut-iy* / *ut-ix*; *iual ut*). Coe stresses the irony that even calendrics yield to phonetics.

Other Coe-cited readings: *tz’ib* “writing” / *aj tz’ib* “scribe”; *witz* “mountain” (and toponyms); *pitz* “play ball” / *aj pitz*; God K as *K’awiil* (*k’a-wi-l(a)*); *ka-ka-w(a)* cacao on the Primary Standard Sequence (chemically confirmed on a Río Azul lock-top jar residue by Hershey labs); *ul* “atole” on open bowls; *u bak* “his bone” on Tikal Temple I burial bone strips with king’s name and Emblem Glyph; *u tz’ib* artist signatures and Naranjo prince-scribe genealogy; “lu-Bat” carver-name introductions (Spinden had guessed personal names; Piedras Negras Stela 12 carries eight signatures). Senior thesis 1989 developed sculptor signatures. With Houston and Robertson (late 1990s), exceptions to Knorosov’s **synharmony** explained as short-vowel harmony vs long-vowel **disharmony** (*ba-k(i)* = *baak* “bone/prisoner”).

### Copán, toponyms, way

Summer 1986 with Schele at [[copan]]; met Grube; 1987 six months excavation under William Fash; with Grube, Houston, and Taube formed what Stuart called “a new school of thought.” 15 March 1987: dedicatory cache under the Hieroglyphic Stairway altar base. *Copán Notes* completed the ruler list from Yax K’uk’ Mo’ (5th c.) to Yax Pasaj (~AD 820). Stuart showed stelae named *lakam tun* “great stone,” “planted” with verb *tz’ap-*; incense burners *sak lak tun*; temples via *u k’aba y-otot*. With Houston: toponyms common, often introduced by *ut-i* “it happened (at),” many including *witz*; mythological places (e.g. *matawil* births on Palenque Cross Group). Houston and Stuart’s 1989 *way* work (with independent Grube letter): *wa-y(a)* “nagual / animal co-essence / dream”; Schele’s “Vision Serpent” as *way* of bloodletters or of K’awiil.

### Standing comments (Stuart via Coe)

Stuart told Coe that free substitutions made the script more repetitive than Lounsbury’s “terrible morass” impression from Thompson’s catalog; by the early 1990s he expected nearly **literal** reading of a far more phonetic system than anyone had predicted a dozen years earlier. Spinden’s bat-compound “here follows a name” was later identified by Stuart as introducing the carver—closing a nearly eighty-year loop (Coe Ch. 7).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Family / early:** George E. Stuart; Thompson’s handbooks as drawing models; early meeting with Thompson himself.
- **Mentors:** Schele; Lounsbury methods; Knorosov principles; Kelley grid.
- **Peers:** Houston, Grube, Taube, Mathews, MacLeod; Simon Martin (later readings, e.g. Piedras Negras Stela 3 narrative in Coe Ch. 11).
- **Debated_in:** [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]]; some early readings (e.g. a bloodletting verbal claim for Albany) he later disavowed while retaining the material blood-band identification (Joralemon instrument).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Coe’s hagiographic tone (Champollion comparison, “genius prize”) is **historiographic framing**, not independent evaluation.
- Individual sign values and early papers were revised by Stuart and others; the substitution methodology remains central to modern practice.
- Integrated archaeology (Copán, later projects) answers Coe’s own critique of epigraphy detached from dirt archaeology—Stuart as both desk and field epigrapher in Coe’s account.
