## Overview

Joshua A. Fishman (1926–2015) was professor of sociology at Yeshiva University. His chapter "Domains and the Relationship between Micro- and Macrosociolinguistics," in Gumperz & Hymes's *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (1972), extends and revises his own earlier work (Fishman 1965) on language loyalty in the United States and Spanish–English bilingualism among Puerto Ricans in New York. At the time of this volume's writing he was engaged in language-policy and language-development research.

## Contributions

Fishman proposed (attributed) the concept of the **[[domain-language-choice|domain]]** as "a sociocultural construct abstracted from topics of communication, relationships between communicators, and locales of communication, in accord with the institutions of a society and the spheres of activity of a speech community" — a higher-order abstraction meant to link face-to-face language choice to society-wide institutional patterning. He credits Schmidt-Rohr (1963) with the concept's first partial elaboration (a nine-domain scheme derived from studying pre-WWII German-speaking settler communities) and Frey (1945) with reducing it to three domains for Amish "triple talk."

Fishman's own empirical program (attributed, date-stamped to the late 1960s) used Greenfield's (1968) five-domain scheme (family, friendship, religion, education, employment) for Puerto Rican bilinguals in New York City, validated by a self-report instrument administered to high-school students; findings ranked Spanish-domain use family > friendship > religion > employment > education. A language census of 431 individuals in a Jersey City Puerto Rican neighborhood (Fishman 1968a), factor-analyzed with varimax rotation, yielded a five-factor solution matching the domains. Edelman's (1968) word-naming task with 34 Puerto Rican schoolchildren (ages 6–11) in Jersey City found family the most-Spanish domain and education the most-English domain, with significant age, language, and domain effects.

Fishman explicitly extended Charles Ferguson's H(igh)/L(ow) [[diglossia]] framework — originated by Ferguson for two varieties of one language — to cases involving two structurally distinct languages, associating H and L varieties with contrasting domain-linked value clusters ("high culture values" versus "intimacy and folksiness"). He stated that domains are not directly observable in talk but are "extrapolated from the data of 'talk'" via the investigator's "integrative intuition," validated iteratively against informant judgment and predictive success — an explicitly etic, investigator-constructed category rather than a native one.

Per the volume editors' framing, Fishman's domain analysis is methodologically distinct from Blom and Gumperz's situational/metaphorical [[code-switching]] framework: where Blom and Gumperz treat social and linguistic constraints as one communicative system input to a single set of rules, Fishman treats behavioral norms as jural rules — regularities standing apart from individual behavior — and employs quantitative survey techniques (self-report instruments, census data, factor analysis) rather than participant observation.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Fishman's domain concept descends from Schmidt-Rohr (1963) and Frey (1945); his diglossia extension descends from Ferguson. His work is explicitly cross-referenced against Blom and Gumperz's [[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study|Hemnesberget code-switching study]] as a contrasting method for the same general problem — relating micro-level language choice to macro-level social structure.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

None recorded in this source; the volume's editors present Fishman's approach as complementary to, not competing with, the interactional program, though it is aligned with what Gumperz elsewhere terms the "correlational" tradition.
