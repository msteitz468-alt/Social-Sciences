## Overview

Richard G. Klein (b. 1941) is a paleoanthropologist and zooarchaeologist, professor of
anthropology and human biology at Stanford University. He is best known as author of
[[klein-human-career-1999|*The Human Career*]], the leading graduate synthesis of human
paleontology and paleolithic archaeology, and as a forceful advocate of both the strong
**"Out of Africa" replacement model** and a **late, relatively abrupt threshold for
behavioral modernity** (~50–40 ka) that he attributes, tentatively, to a neural or
genetic change (see [[behavioral-modernity]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

## Contributions

Klein's empirical base is **faunal analysis** ([[zooarchaeology]] / [[faunal-analysis]]):
by comparing animal-bone assemblages from Middle and Later Stone Age sites in southern
Africa (notably Klasies River Mouth and Nelson Bay Cave), he argued that anatomically
modern people who behaved in fully modern ways were more effective hunters and foragers
than their Middle Stone Age predecessors — evidence he reads as marking a behavioral,
not merely anatomical, transition. In *The Human Career* he synthesizes the whole fossil
and archaeological record around the claim that anatomical modernity (by ~200–100 ka in
Africa) preceded behavioral modernity (by ~50–40 ka), the lag being the central puzzle
his neural-change hypothesis is meant to solve. All of these are attributed positions.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Klein took his PhD at the University of Chicago (1966) under **F. Clark Howell**, a
central figure in mid-twentieth-century American paleoanthropology; through Howell he
belongs to the lineage that professionalized the integrated study of hominin fossils and
their archaeological and faunal context. His long collaboration with zooarchaeologist
Kathryn Cruz-Uribe shaped the quantitative bone-analysis methods underpinning his
subsistence arguments. Students and further genealogy: [[unknown]] from this source.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Klein's late-and-abrupt behavioral-modernity threshold and its putative neural cause are
contested. Critics (e.g., advocates of a gradual, cumulative African Middle Stone Age
buildup of modern behavior — McBrearty & Brooks' "revolution that wasn't") argue the
archaeological signal of modernity appears piecemeal and much earlier in Africa, and
that a single genetic switch is neither necessary nor demonstrable. The 2nd edition's
positions predate the aDNA revolution: subsequent Neanderthal and Denisovan genomes
established low-level admixture (as of the post-2010 archaeogenetic literature, e.g. Reich
2018), complicating the *total*-replacement version of Out of Africa that Klein espoused in
1999 (see [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[denisovans]]).

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012) — Klein as foil

*Attributed via [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]] — opponent/colleague framing, not a primary Klein ingest.*

**Neural-mutation model as Stringer reports it.** [[klein-richard|Klein]] argues modern behaviour arose suddenly ~**50 ka** via genetic mutations enhancing brain function (cognition/language), catalyzing fully modern behavioural patterns and successful expansion beyond Africa with replacement of archaics including Neanderthals. Morphological and behavioural modernity are **decoupled** — morphology first (≥200 ka essentially modern brain size; early moderns and late Neanderthals actually larger-brained than today, though heavier-bodied). Pre-50 ka “modern” behavioural evidence Klein finds unconvincing; consistent art, tool diversity, symbolism, range expansion, diet breadth, and high densities appear only after that date. Fortuitous mutation may have “promoted the fully modern brain” and rapidly facilitated full language (as of Stringer 2012 summarizing Klein).

**Evidence base Stringer cites for Klein.** ~100 ka “modern” fossils from [[klasies-river]], [[skhul]], and [[qafzeh]] associated with Middle Paleolithic artifacts comparable to Neanderthal ones and (per Klein) lacking many modern behaviours. When African modern-origin data grew, many moved the “Human Revolution” to African LSA start ~45 ka — a minority view Stringer associates especially with Klein.

**Stringer’s critique (as of 2012).** Frontal heightening and parietal expansion already present ~100 ka earlier at [[omo-river|Omo Kibish]] and Herto — “no obvious physical evidence for such a change… 50,000 years ago.” Counterintuitive implication: if behaviour drove gracilization, behavioural change should precede morphology — yet Klein has morphology first. McBrearty & Brooks challenge Human Revolution models (Klein or Mellars) as Eurocentric, citing early MSA assembly of modern features ~100–250 ka. Saint-Césaire: Klein and Stringer shared the view that Châtelperronian makers would be Neanderthals capable of UP-style tools, not morphological intermediates (see [[neanderthals]], [[chatelperronian-industry]]).
