# Susan U. Philips

## Overview

American linguistic anthropologist (University of Arizona); in [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language*]] ch. 6 analyzes how language ideology in Tongan courtrooms participates in constructing the Tongan nation-state — extending kinship-based models of respectful speech (*lea kovi* ‘bad language’; sister–brother mutual respect) into a public of citizens.

## Contributions

- **Multisited** language ideology: primary production sites vs sites of metapragmatic commentary; courts as secondary/tertiary sites where magistrates harness local ideology to authorize the state.
- *Lea kovi* and related proscriptions modeled in sibling dyads, extended classifcatorily in court (cousins; behave as if relatives present); gendered construction of the Tongan state.
- Earlier Warm Springs / ethnography-of-speaking work (as cited in the Hymes program network — densify).
