# Ernest W. Burgess

## Overview

American sociologist (1886–1966), University of Chicago, co-architect with [[park-robert-e|Robert E. Park]] of the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] urban research program. In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925) he contributed the iconic **[[concentric-zones|concentric-zone]]** growth model and the essay "Can Neighborhood Work Have a Scientific Basis?" Trained in sociology at Chicago; deep interest in ecology framed his approach to the city (Sampson foreword).

## Contributions

- **[[concentric-zones|Concentric zones]]** as ideal construction of radial expansion (I–V); ideal-type caveat that no city fits perfectly.
- **Expansion processes:** succession (invasion of outer zones), concentration and decentralization, "centralized decentralization" and satellite loops.
- **Social metabolism:** organization/disorganization as anabolism/katabolism; disorganization may be normal prelude to reorganization.
- **[[urban-mobility|Mobility]]** as pulse of the community; land values, transit, mail, phones as measures.
- **Neighborhood science:** settlements as university extension; science concerns forces not factors (Park distinction); ecological / cultural / political aspects of community; "promiscuity" as primary intimacy on secondary contacts; delinquency triangles; research impulse of settlement movement (Woods).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborator of [[park-robert-e|Park]]; Chicago network with Thomas, McKenzie, Wirth; supervisor context for monographs listed in *The City* (Anderson, Thrasher, Zorbaugh, etc.).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Zones repeatedly caricatured; Burgess already treated them as ideal type. Later multi-nuclei and political-economy models revise geometry. Neighborhood-decay thesis debated by later community-development traditions.

## Historiography

Remembered chiefly for the zone diagram; *The City* essays and later family/disorganization work (not yet ingested) broaden the portrait.
