# Giovanni Arrighi

Giovanni Arrighi (1937–2009) was an Italian historical sociologist and political economist, a central figure in [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]], known for systemic cycles of accumulation and the analysis of [[world-system-hegemony|hegemony]]. Seeded here from his chapter "Hegemony and Antisystemic Movements" in *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-longue-duree-2004|The Modern World-System in the Longue Durée]]* (2004); *The Long Twentieth Century* is not yet ingested.

## Overview

Arrighi links successive world hegemonies (Dutch, British, US) to reorganizations of capitalism and to the opportunities and limits of [[antisystemic-movements|antisystemic movements]]. Claims on this page are attributed pending full monograph ingest.

## Contributions

- Hegemony as systemic leadership (not only state power) within the [[capitalist-world-economy]].
- Relationship between hegemonic cycles and antisystemic politics (2004 chapter).
- Systemic cycles of accumulation (primary statement in *Long Twentieth Century* — stub note only).

### From "Hegemony and Antisystemic Movements" (2004; attributed)

Arrighi reassesses the Arrighi–Hopkins–Wallerstein *Antisystemic Movements* propositions: classical movements (labor/social-democratic; national liberation) took state power as mid-term objective; success at state power often failed ultimate objectives; 1968 as reaction against US-hegemonic recuperation and Old Left failure; 1989 as continuation of 1968. Three self-critiques of the earlier book: underestimation of the 1979–82 neoliberal counterrevolution; missed 1990s US power inflation and pro/anti-system blur; open questions on East Asia as new economic epicenter. Amends with Gramsci: **Piedmontese function** (state substitutes for ruling class) and **passive revolution**; world hegemonies as successive passive revolutions. East Asia / China as arbiter of egalitarian vs non-egalitarian tendencies in the next hegemonic transition.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Close collaborator of [[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]] and Terence Hopkins in the world-systems network; dialogue with [[amin-samir|Samir Amin]] and others on polarization and imperialism.
