# Simone de Beauvoir

## Overview

Simone de Beauvoir (1908–1986) was a French existentialist philosopher and writer whose *The Second Sex* (1949) is the foundational philosophical text of twentieth-century feminism. She appears in this vault as the acknowledged source of the physiological argument in [[ortner-female-to-male-1972|Ortner's]] "Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?" (which is dedicated to her); no work of hers is yet primary-ingested.

## Contributions

- **Woman as the Other**: Beauvoir argued that man is posited as the absolute Subject and woman as the inessential Other, defined relative to him.
- **"One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman"**: the distinction between biological female and the cultural construction of "woman" — a seed of the later sex/gender distinction.
- **Immanence vs transcendence**: the argument, drawn on directly by [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]], that woman's body is "more enslaved to the species" (menstruation, pregnancy, lactation), tying her to the mere *repetition* of life (immanence), while man asserts *transcendence* through goal-oriented projects — hence, in Beauvoir's words, superiority is "accorded… not to the sex that brings forth but to that which kills." Ortner rebuilds this as woman's apparent closeness to [[nature-culture-distinction|nature]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Later feminists criticized *The Second Sex* for accepting a masculine model of transcendence as the human norm (devaluing maternity and "immanence" rather than revaluing them) — a critique that lands equally on Ortner's appropriation of the immanence/transcendence frame. The [[nature-culture-gender-debate]] targets exactly this inherited opposition.

## Historiography

Beauvoir is read across philosophy, literature, and gender studies; her standing as an independent philosopher (long overshadowed by Sartre) has risen sharply since the 1990s. A fuller page awaits ingestion of *The Second Sex* itself.
