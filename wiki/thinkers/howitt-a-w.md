# A. W. Howitt

**Alfred William Howitt** (1830–1908) was an Australian magistrate and ethnographer whose collaborative work with [[fison-lorimer|Lorimer Fison]] documented south-eastern Australian kinship and initiation for evolutionary anthropology.

## Overview

**Alfred William Howitt** (1830–1908) was an Australian magistrate and ethnographer whose collaborative work with [[fison-lorimer|Lorimer Fison]] documented south-eastern Australian kinship and initiation for evolutionary anthropology.

## Contributions

- Australian magistrate, naturalist, and ethnographer who, with Lorimer Fison, documented south-eastern Australian kinship and social organization for the evolutionary debate.
- Born in England, emigrated to Australia in 1852 with his father during the gold rush; became a bushman and explorer, and from 1863–1889 served as Police Magistrate and Warden of the Goldfields in Gippsland.
- Developed a method of collecting kinship terms using sticks arranged on the ground as a genealogical diagram; gathered ethnographic data on the Kurnai of Gippsland and came to be regarded there as an initiated tribal member. Co-authored *Kamilaroi and Kurnai* (1880) with [[fison-lorimer|Fison]]; later published *The Native Tribes of South-East Australia* (1904).
- Reported that the Gippsland Kurnai Aboriginal population fell from an estimated 1,500 (1839) to 140 (1877) as a result of colonization (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995 — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995]]).

## Interpretive claims and their limits (attributed)

*(Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995.)* Howitt interpreted a violent 1876 incident — a husband spearing his wife, followed by, in his account, "all the men had her in common" — as evidence of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s "Pairing System," using it to argue the Kurnai stood higher on the evolutionary scale than their marriage practices otherwise suggested. **[Flag: an ethically fraught use of an act of violence against a woman as evolutionary "evidence" — recorded here factually per the Reflexivity protocol, not endorsed.]** Howitt held that no Australian tribes currently practiced full "communal" marriage, but inferred a prior "theoretical" communal stage from [[survivals]], while explicitly acknowledging that "actual" observed practice diverged from this inferred "theoretical" state — a methodological tension Stocking treats as characteristic of the evidentiary strain classical [[social-evolutionism]] was placed under by increasingly detailed field data.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collaborator of [[fison-lorimer|Lorimer Fison]]; correspondent of [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] and later armchair theorists, including [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], who favorably cited the Fison–Howitt work in his 1880 Anthropological Institute presidential address.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Assessed at length in [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]] (attributed); Stocking reads Howitt's 1876 "Pairing System" interpretation and his theoretical/actual communal-marriage distinction as evidence of the interpretive strain placed on evolutionary theory by increasingly rich field reports even from sympathetic fieldworkers. See that source for the historicist reconstruction of this figure's place in the transition from evolutionism to social anthropology.
