# A. R. Radcliffe-Brown

> **Deep analysis:** [[hubs/thinkers/radcliffe-brown-a-r|Thinkers Hub]]

## Overview

Alfred Reginald Radcliffe-Brown (1881–1955; published the Andaman monograph as **A. R. Brown**) was a Cambridge-trained British social anthropologist and a principal architect of **[[structural-functionalism]]** in anthropology. His first major fieldwork (1906–08) and monograph *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]* (1922) combined intensive ethnographic description of the [[andamanese|Great Andamanese]] with an explicit program for interpreting customs by their **present meaning and systemic relations** rather than by conjectural origins — "social physiology" aimed at general laws of social life. He trained under A. C. Haddon, [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]], and W. L. H. Duckworth; wrote the Andaman interpretation chapters largely after leaving the field (c. 1910 draft); and completed the book from the University of Cape Town (preface January 1922). Later career densified via Kuper 2015: Australian synthesis; chairs at Cape Town, Sydney, Chicago, and Oxford; collected essays *Structure and Function in Primitive Society* (1952); see Institutional career section below.

## Contributions

- **Andaman ethnography (1906–08 / 1922).** Primary description of Great Andaman social organisation (local group, tribe, coast/forest division, family without clans), ceremonial (initiation, mourning, dance, peace-making), religious-magical beliefs, and myths — with heavy use of E. H. Man for South Andaman comparison. Study: [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]] · [[hubs/studies/radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].
- **Interpretive method (Chs V–VI).** Against historical reconstruction of origins and against isolating single customs for cross-cultural comparison, he argued (attributed) for discovering the **meaning** of customs by their place in the whole social system, and for comparing **social systems or types**. Ceremonial, on his working hypothesis, collectively expresses and **maintains/transmits** the **sentiments** on which social cohesion depends. Myths express **social values** of natural phenomena and tradition, parallel in function to ceremonial.
- **[[social-structure]] and function.** Early formulation of society as a system of interconnected customs and sentiments whose continuity is a scientific object; "extremely simple" Andaman structure as type-case.
- **The theoretical program (*[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*, 1952).** The collected essays give his mature doctrine (all attributed): social anthropology as "the theoretical natural science of human society" / comparative sociology, pursuing general laws by the [[comparative-method|comparative method]]; **[[social-structure]]** defined as "this network of actually existing relations" among persons (not "culture," "a vague abstraction"), distinguished from **structural form**; **[[social-function|function]]** as the contribution an activity makes to structural continuity (a testable hypothesis, not dogma); and applications to **[[social-sanctions]]**, **[[ritual-value|ritual value and taboo]]**, **[[joking-relationship|joking relationships]]**, **[[totemism]]**, religion, and unilineal descent. He traced the program to Montesquieu, Comte, Spencer, and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]], and defined it *against* conjectural history: "My objection to conjectural history is not that it is historical, but that it is conjectural."
- **Field training credo.** Despite thorough Cambridge preliminary training, he held that only "actually living with and working amongst a primitive people" gives the social anthropologist real training; the Andaman book is self-described "apprentice work."
- **Kinship programme (*[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|ASKAM]]* Introduction, 1950).** Co-edited with [[forde-daryll|Forde]]; the long Introduction (pp. 1–85) is his mature statement of structural principles of kinship and marriage (classificatory terminology; unilineal corporate groups; marriage as rights-transfer via [[marriage-payments]]; mother-right/father-right/double-descent/cognatic types; against conjectural history). Full section below; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Teachers:** [[haddon-a-c|A. C. Haddon]]; [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] (first postgraduate anthropology student, 1904); Duckworth (Cambridge). Haddon and Rivers read Andaman proofs.
- **Field and colonial setting:** Anthony Wilkin Studentship; Royal Society and Government of India grants; Port Blair commissioners Herbert and Browning.
- **Dialogue:** Continuity with and correction of E. H. Man; contrast with English **intellectualist** anthropology (beliefs as primitive explanations of nature) and with emotion/awe theories of religion; cites Durkheim *Formes élémentaires* critically on animism/naturism; parallel generation with [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (1922).
- **Disavowal of the "Functional School":** in "On Social Structure" (1940) he denied that any such school led by him existed, attributing the label to [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s "own sense of irresponsibility," and insisted "there is no place in natural science for 'schools'." He explicitly distinguished his sense of "function" from Malinowski's and Lowie's, and engaged Evans-Pritchard's narrower use of "social structure" (in *The Nuer*).
- **Students / institutional descendants:** `trained: [[unknown]]` until later sources name lineages (Fortes — credited in the 1949 joking-relationships note — Evans-Pritchard, Eggan, etc. await their primary ingests for reciprocal genealogy).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Field limits (self-noted):** Little Andaman and Jarawa thin; physical anthropology unpublished; linguistic series interrupted by war; interpretation drafted after departure.
- **Colonial documentation of a collapsing population:** measles 1877, syphilis, influenza, birth-rate collapse among "friendly" groups — reconstruction under Penal Settlement conditions.
- **Period racial framing:** Negrito classification and isolation hypothesis in the Introduction — historical scientific position, not to be restated as wiki-voice identity.
- **Later theory critique:** British SF charged with ahistoricism, underplaying conflict and colonial power, and over-integrating "function" — applies to Chs V–VI as program texts. Malinowski vs Radcliffe-Brown functionalisms often contrasted (needs vs structure/sentiments) in later historiography.

## Institutional career and rivalry (Kuper 2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]], *[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]* 4th ed.)*

- **Posts (brute sequence per Kuper).** After Andaman fieldwork and Trinity fellowship to 1914: schoolmaster Sydney (war years); director of education **Tonga 1916–19**; Johannesburg after 1919 influenza; **1921 Cape Town** — first established chair in social anthropology in Britain or the Empire (turning point; ~40, fieldwork behind him; double-barrelled name adopted then). **1926–31 Sydney** — first Australian social anthropology chair; founded *Oceania*; left with department near collapse (Depression; [[firth-raymond|Firth]] and A. P. Elkin “picked up the pieces”). **Chicago** (devoted circle; poor fit with broader American anthropology); **1937 first established Oxford chair** in social anthropology. WWII: RAI president; ~two years British Council São Paulo. Postwar teaching: Cambridge, London, Manchester, Grahamstown, Beijing, Alexandria; died London **1955**.
- **Durkheimian conversion.** Kuper: after Rivers approved the Andaman report, R-B’s Cambridge “conversion” to Durkheim/*Année Sociologique*; 1909–10 LSE and Cambridge lectures; Durkheim wrote approving of shared “general conception of our science.” Remained a critical Durkheimian while [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] moved toward agency and biological needs.
- **Structure vs function duel (1938) and agenda dominance.** Kuper: 1930s factional split (Malinowski vs R-B parties). R-B called himself an “anti-functionalist” against Malinowski’s culture/needs functionalism; insisted structural-functionalism concerns **social** not biological functions; denied a “Functional School” as Malinowski’s myth (*Structure and Function*, 1952 epigraph). June **1938** London duel: R-B on institutional functions for social order; Malinowski derided “hyphenated functionalism,” mission to add “flesh and blood” to a “purely social system.” Kuper’s periodization: roughly the **1940s–1950s**, R-B’s approach set the BSA agenda (Oxford concentration with Evans-Pritchard and Fortes; LSE declined after Malinowski left 1938); by 1955 R-B’s personal legacy was under threat even as the structural programme still organized “normal science.”
- **Method contrast (Kuper).** Unlike Malinowski’s village residence ideal, Kuper reports R-B in Andamans and Australia usually depended on interpreters/informants in settings far from traditional life; Andaman genealogies failed despite Rivers’s method (R-B’s own note). Analytic style: formal situations, rules, rituals — “none of Malinowski’s interest in the pragmatic strategies of traders, farmers and lovers” (Kuper).

## African Political Systems preface (1940)

Wrote the Preface to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]*, framing comparative study of political institutions as under-attended social anthropology; defining political organization via control/regulation of physical force; rejecting the State as philosophical fiction; urging theory-guided field description. Intellectual sponsorship of Fortes & Evans-Pritchard’s comparative African political program.

## African Systems of Kinship and Marriage Introduction (1950)

Co-edited with [[forde-daryll|Daryll Forde]] the IAI volume *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* and wrote the long theoretical Introduction (pp. 1–85)—the mature programme statement of his kinship analysis and a charter text of British structural-functional kinship ethnography. Attributed contributions (claims ranges 1–2; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Scientific programme:** scientific study of kinship against pseudo-history; pure and applied science; social function; synchronic analysis prior to diachronic speculation.
- **Definitions:** kinship via social recognition of descent/affinity (pater vs genitor); elementary and compound family; orders of relationship and **range**; descriptive vs [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory]] terminology; norms spanning affective, etiquette, and jural (rights *in personam* / *in rem*).
- **Structural principles:** abstractive comparison yields a small set of general principles—range of kinship recognition; unity of the sibling group (“male mother” / “female father” — [[avunculate]]); generation and rank; unity of the lineage (Omaha-type extensions); corporate [[unilineal-descent|unilineal]] groups vs cognatic systems (Teutonic sib; Masai agnatic illustration); marriage as rearrangement of rights (often via [[marriage-payments]] as legal instrument of transfer, not commercial wife-purchase; multi-aspect reading as indemnity, gift-alliance after Mauss, and determinant of children’s affiliation where dual forms exist); [[joking-relationship|joking]] and mother-in-law avoidance as structural complements; prohibited/preferential marriage, levirate/sororate, incest theory.
- **Typology:** mother-right / father-right (Nayar / Roman *patria potestas* type poles) with African approximations—qualified mother-right (Ashanti, Bemba, Congo), Nguni father-right (“Cattle beget children”), [[double-descent]] (Yakö), cognatic Lozi approximation; Tswana kinship marriage as “decidedly exceptional” vs Nguni.
- **Against conjectural history:** rejects nineteenth-century earliest-matriarchy / promiscuity reconstructions as imagination ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]); treats mother-right exemplars (Nayar, Menangkabau, Khasi) as advanced societies, not primitive stages.
- **Method close:** kinship analysis in terms of [[social-structure]] and [[social-function]] via systematic comparison; evolution as diversification, not unilinear stages; applied note on colonial culture contact and administration.
- **African illustrations drawn from the volume’s contributors** ([[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] Lozi–Zulu; [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] Ashanti; [[richards-audrey|Richards]] Bemba; [[schapera-isaac|Schapera]] Tswana; [[forde-daryll|Forde]] Yakö; [[kuper-hilda|Kuper]] Swazi; [[wilson-monica|Wilson]] Nyakyusa; [[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] Nuba; [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] Nuer local community; etc.) and from earlier literature (Masai, Kongo, Nguni, etc.).

## Stocking's historicist account (*After Tylor*, 1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], not wiki-voice.)*

- **Formation.** Born Alfred Reginald Brown, 1881, Sparkbrook, Birmingham (adopted the legally hyphenated "Radcliffe-Brown" surname c. 1926); father died 1886; early tuberculosis; educated at the Royal Commercial Travellers' School, Pinner, and King Edward's School, Birmingham. Read the Mental and Moral Science Tripos at Trinity College, Cambridge (entered 1902) under Rivers and Myers (psychology) and McTaggart (Part II); formal anthropology training (physical anthropology under Duckworth, archaeology under Ridgeway, ethnology under Haddon, kinship under Rivers) began in his final undergraduate year. Known at Trinity as "Anarchy Brown" for youthful anarchist/Kropotkin-influenced politics; later described himself as having moved to socialism.
- **Andaman fieldwork as actually conducted.** 1906–1908, but only **~10 months** of actual field time; worked initially at Fort Blair, briefly with the Onges of Little Andaman (abandoned for language barrier), then chiefly with North Andamanese via Hindustani. His stated goal, per Haddon and Sir Richard Temple's framing, was reconstructing the "primitive culture" of the Negrito race as presumed lowest of four population strata in Southeast Asia — an explicitly evolutionary/racial-hierarchy framing of the period, recorded here as documented period framing, not an endorsed classification. See [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].
- **From sentiment psychology to social structure.** Radcliffe-Brown's early interpretive chapters (drafted c. 1910–14, from *The Andaman Islanders*) relied heavily on the sentiment psychology of William McDougall and Alexander Shand. Correspondence with Rivers, 1912–1914, marks his shift from this diachronic/evolutionary and psychological framework toward a synchronic, Durkheimian **functional** analysis; Stocking dates the decisive break to a December 1913 exchange in which Radcliffe-Brown argued that judging whether a custom is a "survival" requires prior knowledge of its social function — an argument that helped dissolve the evolutionist "doctrine of survivals" (see [[concepts/survivals]]). "Social value" and "sentiment" language was later purged from his vocabulary as he moved, across the 1920s–30s, toward the strictly sociological/jural idiom of an explicitly anti-psychological **"natural science of society"** centered on **social structure**, crystallizing fully in his 1937 University of Chicago Dean's Seminar lectures (published posthumously, unedited, as *A Natural Science of Society*), where he argued a "theoretical science of human society was possible," distinct from psychology "as physiology is from chemistry."
- **The Bates priority dispute.** During his second fieldwork (Western Australia, 1910–12, joint Oxford/Cambridge sponsorship), Radcliffe-Brown had a contentious collaboration with ethnographer Daisy Bates over her unpublished manuscript on Western Australian Aborigines; he edited and cut her material, and she accused him of plagiarism and "mutilation." Whether Radcliffe-Brown "discovered" or merely "defined" the Kariera four-section kinship system is a live scholarly dispute per Stocking — candidate for a `source-reliability`/`priority` debate page.
- **Career chairs and their colonial funding.** Cape Town — chair in Social Anthropology, School of African Life and Languages, University of Cape Town, 1921–1926, funded by the Union of South Africa government, established amid the Bulhoek massacre (1921), the Rand miners' strike (1922), and the Urban Areas Act (1923). Sydney — chair in Anthropology, University of Sydney, 1926–1931, Rockefeller-funded and tied to League of Nations mandate administration needs in New Guinea/Papua; he directed *Oceania* from Sydney as an organ for his trained fieldworkers. Chicago — University of Chicago, 1931–1937 (recruited by Fay-Cooper Cole to succeed Edward Sapir; 1933 formal debate with Ralph Linton over a Boas quotation on history vs. general laws; disputes in print with Kroeber and Alexander Lesser). Oxford — Professor of Social Anthropology from 1937 (elected over Malinowski, who withdrew in his favor). He publicly criticized British imperial rule (questioning the right of empire to "exercise control over [the] destinies" of the peoples of India and Africa, 1930 address) while also arguing native warfare served an "integrative function," provoking the displeasure of colonial administrator Hubert Murray in Papua — recorded as an instance of the discipline's applied/administrative ambivalence, see [[applied-anthropology]] and [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].
- **Totemism and Australian kinship.** His mature (1920s) totemism theory reconceived totemism as a general, "practically universal" tendency to establish ritual relations between social segments and natural species, arising in societies dependent on natural production — explicitly inverting Durkheim's "projection of society onto nature" model. "The Social Organization of Australian Tribes" (1930/31) systematized "moieties," "sections," and "sub-sections," cataloguing 51 kinship types from his own and six students' fieldwork.
- **The 1938 "Bronio and Rex" debate and the "hyphenated functionalism" label.** See [[malinowski-bronislaw]] for the full Le Play House (17 June 1938) debate on the concept of "function"; Malinowski's 1939 term "hyphenated functionalism" for Radcliffe-Brown's structural program is recorded there.
- **Stocking's periodization caution.** Rivers died 1922, the same year *Argonauts* and *The Andaman Islanders* were both published — a date Stocking notes is sometimes chosen to mark a "revolution in anthropology," a periodization he treats as historiographical convenience rather than a sharp break, arguing the "delimitation of social anthropology" as a discipline was a gradual, institutionally contested project extending to Radcliffe-Brown's 1937 Oxford chair.
