# Paul Ricoeur

French philosopher (1913–2005) whose hermeneutics supplied the interpretive turn in the
human sciences with a philosophical charter. Enters this wiki chiefly as a **borrowed
authority** in reflexive and interpretive anthropology rather than as a social scientist
in his own right.

## Overview

Ricoeur's account of interpretation — that understanding a text (or a "meaningful
action" treated as a text) requires a **detour through the other** by which the self is
in turn re-understood — was taken up directly by [[rabinow-paul|Paul Rabinow]], whose
*[[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977|Reflections on Fieldwork in Morocco]]* (1977) opens
on Ricoeur's formula: fieldwork is "the comprehension of the self by the detour of the
comprehension of the other." [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]]'s treatment of culture
and action *as text* is likewise adjacent to Ricoeur's "The Model of the Text" (1971).

## Contributions

*(Attributed; as used by the anthropologists who cite him.)*

- **Meaningful action as a text.** Social action can be read like a text — fixed,
  autonomous of its author's intention, open to plural interpretation — a move that
  underwrites the textual metaphor in [[interpretive-anthropology]] and, radicalized,
  in [[writing-culture]].
- **The detour of the self through the other.** Self-understanding is achieved only
  through the interpretation of what is other — the hermeneutic circle that
  [[reflexive-fieldwork|reflexive fieldwork]] turns into a method.
- **Hermeneutics of suspicion / of recovery** — the double reading of symbols.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A philosopher rather than a fieldworker; his influence on the disciplines is by citation
and adaptation. In this wiki he is a node connecting hermeneutic philosophy to
[[rabinow-paul|Rabinow]], [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], and the interpretive/reflexive
programs.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Political-economy and practice-theory critics of the textual metaphor (see
[[writing-culture-debate]]) argue that reading action "as a text" brackets power and
production. This is a critique of the anthropological *use* of Ricoeur more than of his
own philosophy.
