# Alfonso Caso

## Overview

Alfonso Caso was a Mexican archaeologist whose excavations at [[sites/monte-alban|Monte Albán]], beginning in the 1930s, established the site's foundational phase sequence and the essential decipherment of its early writing and calendrical systems. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], Caso deduced from the Monte Albán I Danzantes and related carved slabs that day-glyphs of the 260-day Almanac and month glyphs were in use, establishing the Zapotec **Calendar Round**, while determining the Long Count "was definitely absent" from Zapotec practice. He correctly interpreted the more than 40 inscribed slabs on Building J (Monte Albán II) as records of town conquests, in a script confirmed to be the Zapotec language.

## Contributions

- Established the Monte Albán I–V phase sequence still used as the Valley of Oaxaca's chronological backbone (see [[sites/monte-alban]], [[cultures/zapotec-civilization]]).
- Deciphered the Building J conquest-record slabs as depictions of defeated rulers and place-glyphs, in the Zapotec language.
- Demonstrated the Zapotec Calendar Round (Year Bearer days falling in the 2nd, 7th, 12th, and 17th positions of the 20 named days), later refined by Javier Urcid.
- First interpreted the Tepantitla murals at [[sites/teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] as a depiction of the Rain God's paradise (Tlalocan) — a reading later revised by Esther Pasztory and Karl Taube, who reidentified the central deity as female.
- Proposed that the "9 Reptile Eye" glyph, found at [[sites/xochicalco]] and [[sites/cacaxtla]], was equivalent to "9 Wind," the later name of the culture hero Quetzalcoatl — a reading other epigraphers regard with less certainty (**[POSITION]**, contested).
- Excavated Mixtec-period Tomb 7 and worked extensively on Mixtec codex interpretation (per this source's brief references; full treatment not covered).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Caso's Tepantitla-mural (Tlalocan) reading was revised by Esther Pasztory and further developed by Karl Taube, who argued the central deity is female and possibly the presiding goddess of Teotihuacan religion rather than a male Rain God. His "9 Reptile Eye"/"9 Wind" equation at Xochicalco and Cacaxtla is a live, contested epigraphic **[POSITION]**, not adjudicated here.

## Historiography

Caso's Monte Albán phase sequence and his Building J and Calendar Round decipherments remain the field's baseline data as of Coe & Koontz (2013); later epigraphers, notably Javier Urcid, have refined but not overturned his core readings.

## Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — ceramic chronology and the founding-ideology reading

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022.)*

Blanton et al. credit Caso as first identifying the glyphs at Monte Albán and other Valley of Oaxaca sites as culturally Zapotec, and cite the ceramic phase sequence he co-developed with his student [[bernal-ignacio|Ignacio Bernal]] and Eduardo Acosta (Caso, Bernal & Acosta 1967) as the chronological backbone of the book's Monte Albán I–V periodization. The authors also credit Caso with excavating and reconstructing Monte Albán's Main Plaza by the 1950s, excavating over 100 pre-Hispanic tombs including Tomb 7 (found 1932, one of the richest tombs in the Americas), and excavating Monte Negro in the Mixteca Alta (1937–39), where local expressions of highland-Oaxaca and wider Mesoamerican styles appeared in architecture, ornamentation, pottery, braziers, and ritual practice in both domestic and public/temple contexts (Acosta and Romero 1992). Drawing on Caso and Bernal's earlier iconographic work (Caso and Bernal 1952: fig. 30), the authors argue that Monte Albán's founding (ca. 500 BC) coincided with an ideological shift: the dualistic were-jaguar/fire-serpent symbolism of the pre-Monte Albán Valley of Oaxaca diminished, replaced in Monte Albán I by imagery of Cocijo, a lightning-and-rain deity, whose representational prominence — a "more restrictively Oaxacan" and "universalizing" fertility/renewal ideology — persisted at the site for over a millennium and is argued to have helped promote collective action among a population drawn from formerly separate polities.
