# Viviana A. Zelizer

## Overview

Viviana A. Zelizer (b. 1946) is an Argentine-born American sociologist whose program on the cultural and relational differentiation of economic media — especially money — is a founding line of contemporary [[economic-sociology|economic sociology]]. Long associated with Princeton University. This page is built primarily from *[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]* (1994; 2017 PUP paperback with afterword). Earlier monographs (*Morals and Markets*; *Pricing the Priceless Child*) and later *The Purchase of Intimacy* remain only lightly referenced until ingested.

## Contributions

### From *The Social Meaning of Money* (1994)

- **[[multiple-monies]]** — rejection of a single homogeneous market money; people create and mark distinct currencies for distinct social relations.
- **[[earmarking]]** — practices that restrict, label, and moralize the uses of particular monies (domestic allowances, gift money, relief cash, tips, etc.).
- Empirical domains: domestic/household monies; gift monies; charitable and welfare cash in the United States roughly 1870–1930.
- Theoretical target: classical utilitarian / [[simmel-georg|Simmelian]] account of money as colorless leveler (also Marx, Weber as she reconstructs them).
- Historical paradox: state standardization of currency coincided with multiplication of earmarks.

Study: [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]. Theory: [[multiple-monies-approach]].

### Later program (from 2017 afterword; not fully ingested)

- **[[relational-work]]** — negotiating matches among ties, transactions, media, and meanings.
- *The Purchase of Intimacy* (2005); critique of **hostile worlds** accounts.
- Three components of money: accounting systems; media; practices.
- Policy-relevant earmarking (EITC form of payment; Financial Diaries).

### Earlier monographs (titles only until ingested)

*Morals and Markets* (life insurance); *Pricing the Priceless Child* (valuation of children) — Zelizer traces the 1994 book's origins to this valuation-of-lives program.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **"Special monies" as anomalies:** Zelizer disputes readings that confine her thesis to soft intimate exceptions.
- **Hostile worlds / nothing-but:** later clarifications that money has diverse moralities; not all monetization is corruption.
- **Gender and power:** earmarks can enforce women's dependency and welfare constraint even as recipients contest them — Zelizer states both.
- Reception: Hirschman, Bourdieu, Tilly among early readers (as she notes); 2015 ENS Paris conference; Dodd places the book with Simmel.
