# Jack Goody

British social anthropologist (1919–2015), Cambridge figure who inherited and then redirected the Fortesian strand of [[british-social-anthropology|British social anthropology]] toward large-scale comparative history (kinship, literacy, Eurasian social forms). Densified here from [[kuper-adam|Kuper]]’s *[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]* (2015); primary monographs not yet ingested.

## Overview

Associated with [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]]’s Cambridge Africanist stream in the classical postwar years, then with a post-1973 comparative turn after succeeding Fortes in the Cambridge chair. Kuper treats Goody as one answer to the discipline’s identity crisis once the “primitive/tribal” object and the specifically British project weakened: comparative wing of the social sciences, testing generalizations beyond Western liberal democracies — a path that “did not really suit” the bulk of the British school (fit poorly with Malinowski/Radcliffe-Brown ethnographic tradition; no impetus to further intensive fieldwork).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Kuper 2015.)*

- **Cambridge chair succession (1973).** After Fortes, Goody launched “a dozen ambitious comparative books” spanning kinship/marriage/inheritance in world-historical perspective; literacy; capitalism and modernity; Islam; cooking.
- **Comparative program as disciplinary answer.** In Kuper’s Ch. 7 typology of replies to “what are we doing?” once tribal specialism collapsed, Goody’s comparative oeuvre is the principal British exemplification of social anthropology as comparative social science (alongside [[gellner-ernest|Gellner]]’s *Plough, Sword and Book* world history and US neoevolutionist stages).
- **Institutional exit from BSA Academy section.** Kuper notes Goody later left the British Academy’s social-anthropology section for the sociologists — a symbolic relocation toward comparative sociology.
- **Memoir as historiography.** *The Expansive Moment* (1995) is listed by Kuper among recommended memoirs/biographical sketches on institutionalization and debates of the British school.

Primary ethnographic and theoretical arguments (West Africa, literacy thesis, Eurasia east–west contrasts, etc.) await monographic ingest — do not invent from outside knowledge.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under / early orbit:** [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] at Cambridge; Kuper’s student-eye Preface (early 1960s): Fortes directed Africanists “with Jack Goody” in the Radcliffe-Brown tradition, against [[leach-edmund|Leach]]’s Malinowskian/Lévi-Straussian camp. New students had to join one side; Africanists expected R-B-style work.
- **Target of Leach’s 1959 polemic:** Leach’s Malinowski Memorial Lecture mocked Goody (with Fortes and [[richards-audrey|Richards]]) as exemplifying “butterfly collecting” typology — situating Goody inside the descent/structure orthodoxy Leach attacked (*Rethinking Anthropology*; Kuper Ch. 6).
- **Fieldwork advice (as remembered by Kuper):** when pressed on participant-observation procedures, Goody said there were no set methods — stay healthy, on good terms with authorities, keep duplicate field notes.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Kuper’s own judgment is institutional more than technical: the comparative turn was a serious reply to the loss of a distinctive British object, but it sat awkwardly with the guild’s Malinowskian ethnographic identity. Full evaluation of Goody’s literacy and Eurasia theses requires primary sources not yet in the vault.
