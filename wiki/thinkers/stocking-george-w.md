# George W. Stocking, Jr.

American historian of anthropology, the discipline's pre-eminent historiographer. Trained as a historian (not an anthropologist), Stocking became the founding figure of the professional **history of anthropology**, best known in this wiki (as of the 1987 ingest) for *[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Victorian Anthropology]]* (1987) — the standard account of how British [[social-evolutionism|sociocultural evolutionism]] emerged out of Prichardian [[ethnology]] — and for the methodological distinction between **[[presentism-and-historicism|presentism and historicism]]** that reframed how the disciplines write their own past.

## Overview

Stocking argued that the history of a science should be written **historicist**ally — privileging the questions the past actors were themselves answering — rather than **presentist**ly, judging them as flawed anticipations of present knowledge (see [[presentism-and-historicism]]; the distinction was first stated in *Race, Culture, and Evolution*, 1968, and reaffirmed in the epilogue to *Victorian Anthropology*). He edited the long-running *History of Anthropology* series and taught at the University of Chicago. His work turned the disciplinary origin-myths of anthropology — "Malinowski invented fieldwork," "Tylor invented the culture concept" — into objects of critical historical analysis.

## Contributions

- **The history of anthropology as a scholarly field.** Stocking established the archival, contextual study of anthropology's past as a rigorous specialty, against both hagiographic textbook genealogies and crude "precursor-hunting."
- **[[presentism-and-historicism|Presentism vs. historicism]].** His signature methodological intervention; a standing reference point for how the human sciences narrate their own development.
- **The Prichardian thesis.** In *Victorian Anthropology* he argued (attributed) that mid-Victorian evolutionism arose not as "social Darwinism" nor as a simple Enlightenment revival, but as a synthesis assembled to answer new questions posed by the [[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]] and the Darwinian debate — displacing the [[ethnology|ethnological]] tradition of [[prichard-james-cowles|James Cowles Prichard]] and its "tree" of racial descent with the evolutionary "ladder."
- **The culture-concept argument.** Stocking argued (attributed) that [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1871 definition of [[culture]] ("that complex whole…") is *not* the modern anthropological concept: Tylor's culture was singular, hierarchical, and equated with [[civilization]], lacking plurality, relativity, and historicity — the plural/relativist concept came later, from Boasians closer to the German (Herderian) tradition (see [[boas-franz]]).
- **Evolutionism as "counterscience."** He read classical evolutionism as the counterscience to political economy — explaining, through deep time, behaviour that looked irrational by the standard of Victorian economic rationality — and stressed a "positivistic" continuity running from evolutionism through functionalism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Stocking wrote about, rather than within, a lineage: his subjects are [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]], [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], [[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]], [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]], [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]], and their successors down to British social anthropology (*After Tylor*, 1995). His historicism drew on the German idealist distinction between the *Natur-* and *Geisteswissenschaften*. His work is a principal influence on later historiography of the discipline, including [[kuper-adam|Adam Kuper]]'s *[[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988|The Invention of Primitive Society]]* (1988), which extends Stocking's critical treatment of the "[[primitive-society]]" construct.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Stocking himself conceded (attributed) that historicism is a relative, not absolute, position — even the choice of what to study reflects present concerns — and warned against imposing a "late-twentieth-century interest in ulterior motivation or ideological determination" on self-consciously rationalist Victorian writers, even as *Victorian Anthropology* itself reads their science against its class, colonial, and gender context. Critics have noted a tension between his historicist caution and the strongly contextual (near-ideological) readings the book actually performs. His framing of anthropology's colonial entanglement complements [[asad-talal|Asad]]'s determination thesis (see [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]).

## Historiography

Stocking's own corpus is the archive of the field he founded; *Victorian Anthropology* won the American Historical Association's prize and remains the standard treatment of anthropology's founding era. His reflexive stance — that the discipline's account of its own past is itself historically conditioned — is now a background assumption in histories of the human sciences. A fuller treatment of his career (the *History of Anthropology* series, *After Tylor*, the essays in *The Ethnographer's Magic*) would repay a Thinkers Hub biography; only the single 1987 volume is ingested here.
