# Jerry D. Moore

## Overview

Andeanist archaeologist at California State University, Dominguez Hills (as of 2005), specializing in the built environment, architecture, and power in prehispanic Andean societies. Excavated at Manchán and Quebrada Santa Cristina in the Casma Valley, Peru (Chimú-period sites), and conducted ethnoarchaeological fieldwork on house construction in Soconusco, Chiapas, on a project directed by his wife and colleague Janine Gasco (Moore and Gasco 1990).

## Contributions

- **An archaeology of place with comparative controls.** In [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Cultural Landscapes in the Ancient Andes]] (2005), Moore argued that landscape experience (sound, visibility, movement, memory) is archaeologically recoverable — but only through cross-culturally documented regularities, not intuitive phenomenology. His method rests on three propositions: the built environment spans multiple decision domains and requires "anthropological holism"; architecture both mirrors and shapes social interaction; and architecture–society relationships can be stated as falsifiable hypotheses (Moore 2005). This positions him within, but critically of, [[phenomenological-archaeology]] and [[landscape-archaeology]].
- **The archaeological [[soundscape]].** Proposed that in nonhierarchical societies dwelling spacing tracks social cohesion via the ambit of the human voice (whisper ≤~4 m, normal conversation ~8 m), tested against ethnoarchaeological camp plans and Andean Archaic sites ([[asana]], [[paloma]], early Valdivia dwellings at [[real-alto]]).
- **Religious authority and ceremonial architecture.** Applying Lawrence Sullivan's (1988) tripartite typology (medium, ecstatic shaman, canonist) to eight lowland South American ethnographic cases, Moore argued ecstatic shamanism is not associated with permanent, regional, public-scale ceremonial architecture, while canon-based authority is — reframing the [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]] over [[formative-stage]] monuments.
- **Design logics of [[procession|processions]]** and the [[house-society]] reading of Chimú ciudadelas at [[chan-chan]] as material devices of social memory.
- **Rejection of "retrodiction":** Moore explicitly opposes projecting Inca-period ethnohistoric patterns backward into deep prehistory; ethnography and ethnohistory generate hypotheses to be tested against archaeological data (Moore 2005).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Training documented as [[unknown]] in the ingested source. Moore self-reports an early theoretical debt to Marshall Sahlins's domestic-mode-of-production framework in his Casma household studies (1981–1991), which he later criticized as treating the house as "a passive backdrop for economic activities" (Moore 2005). Analytical debts to Amos Rapoport (environment-behavior studies), Edward T. Hall (proxemics), Don Handelman and Catherine Bell (ritual theory), and Lawrence Sullivan (comparative religion).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

No sustained critiques of Moore's program are yet documented in the collection; his own book records his self-revision on household archaeology. His comparative-analogical method occupies a middle position attacked from both sides of the processual/post-processual divide (see [[phenomenological-archaeology]]).
