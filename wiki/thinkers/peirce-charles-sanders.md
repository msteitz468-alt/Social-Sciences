# Charles Sanders Peirce (1839–1914)

American philosopher, logician, and founder of **pragmatism** and of a general theory of signs (**semiotics**). Peirce enters this social-sciences vault chiefly through his sign theory, which [[structuralist-archaeology|semiotic archaeology]] and *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]* (Hodder & Hutson) adopt in preference to [[saussure-ferdinand-de|Saussure]]'s as the model for [[reading-material-culture|reading material culture]].

## Overview

Against Saussure's **dyadic** sign (signifier/signified, wholly arbitrary), Peirce proposed a **triadic** sign relation — **sign, object, and interpretant** (the mediating actor/interpreter) — and a typology of three sign modes:

- **Icon** — resembles its object (a drawing of a pot);
- **Index** — has an existential or causal relation to its object (a grime line indexing a water level; a traded object indexing distance to its source);
- **Symbol** — arbitrary and conventional, equivalent to Saussure's sign (a bead standing for chiefship).

Because objects can be iconic and indexical, not only symbolic, Peirce's scheme accommodates the **materiality** and **non-arbitrariness** of things, and the **interpretant** builds in agency and situated use — the features that make it attractive to archaeology, where much of the evidence is precisely material and indexical.

## Contributions

- Founding pragmatist philosophy (later carried into [[archaeological-pragmatism]] and into sociology via the [[chicago-school]] lineage of James, Dewey, Mead).
- The icon/index/symbol typology and triadic sign, adopted by [[semiotic-practice|semiotic]] archaeology (Preucel & Bauer; Capone & Preucel) and by Hodder & Hutson.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A founder of American pragmatism alongside William James and John Dewey; his semiotics was recovered for the human sciences in the later 20th century as a materialist alternative to Saussurean structuralism.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Within archaeology, critics note the triad can reinstate an object/interpretation dichotomy (Thomas), and that not all material culture behaves as a sign at all (Bekaert). These are limits on the *application* of Peirce's semiotics, not on the sign theory itself.

## Historiography

Peirce's own work was fragmentary and largely posthumously assembled; his influence on the social sciences is indirect and mostly mediated — through pragmatism into the [[chicago-school]] and through semiotics into interpretive and [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] archaeology.
