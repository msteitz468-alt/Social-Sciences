## Overview

Eske Willerslev is a Danish evolutionary geneticist and one of the pioneers of ancient-DNA sequencing (see [[ancient-dna]]), responsible for several of the field-defining genomes discussed in [[reich-who-we-are-2018]]: the Mal'ta boy, the Anzick Clovis infant, Kennewick Man, the Spirit Cave remains, and the Saqqaq/Paleo-Eskimo genome. Reich also credits Willerslev with a distinctive and controversial practice of engaging indigenous and descendant communities directly around ancient-DNA research.

## Contributions

Willerslev and colleagues published genome-wide data in late 2013 from a boy who lived at the Mal'ta site in south-central Siberia roughly 24,000 years ago; the Mal'ta genome matched the population Reich's lab had inferred on statistical grounds and became the "type specimen" for the [[ancient-north-eurasians|Ancient North Eurasians]] ghost population (as of Reich 2018).

In 2014, Willerslev and colleagues published a whole-genome sequence from the Anzick Clovis-associated infant (Montana), radiocarbon-dated to just after 13,000 years ago. The genome showed that a deep split among Native American populations had already occurred by that time, disproving the idea that the Clovis culture represented the single founding population of all Native Americans (as of Reich 2018).

In 2015, Willerslev's team extracted ancient DNA from Kennewick Man (~8,500 years old, Washington State), overturning an earlier morphological analysis that had assigned the remains Pacific Rim/Pacific Islander affinities rather than Native American ancestry; the genetic result showed Kennewick Man derived from the same ancestral lineage as other Native Americans (as of Reich 2018). Reich separately credits ancient DNA generally with "trumping" morphological analysis for population assignment, given that morphology examines few variable traits against genetics' tens of thousands of independent positions, while also critiquing the specific published Kennewick Man genome paper for sampling only one of five claimant tribes and not sharing the Colville tribal data for independent analysis (as of Reich 2018).

Willerslev's team obtained ancient DNA from the roughly 11,000-year-old Spirit Cave, Utah remains (access granted October 2015), showing ancestry entirely within the same deep lineage as present-day Native Americans; the U.S. Bureau of Land Management subsequently returned the remains (2016) to the Fallon Paiute-Shoshone tribe (as of Reich 2018).

Willerslev and colleagues sequenced genome-wide data (2010) from a roughly 4,000-year-old hair sample of the Saqqaq culture, the first documented human culture of Greenland, finding a distinct ancestry blend from both "First American" and later Eskimo-Aleut sources; a 2014 expansion of this work argued the Paleo-Eskimo lineage represented a distinct migration that went extinct after Eskimo-Aleut arrival roughly 1,500 years ago (as of Reich 2018) — a conclusion Reich's own lab later revised (see Critiques below).

Willerslev, Rasmussen, and archaeologist Kristian Kristiansen also tested ancient DNA samples from Europe and the steppe for Yersinia pestis (plague bacterium), finding it in several individuals and establishing that the pathogen was endemic on the steppe during the period of Yamnaya-associated migration (as of Reich 2018).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Reich reports that his own lab's 2012 statistical study found no evidence for Willerslev's proposed distinct Paleo-Eskimo migration as a wholly separate lineage; instead Reich's lab proposed the Saqqaq/Paleo-Eskimo lineage derived from the same source contributing to the Na-Dene-speaking Chipewyan population — a revision later confirmed in a 2017 study (Reich, Flegontov, and Schiffels), which showed the Paleo-Eskimo lineage did not go extinct but survives within both Na-Dene and Eskimo-Aleut speakers today (as of Reich 2018).

Reich also reports that Willerslev's 2011 Four Population Test study — interpreted at the time as evidence for a "Southern Route" genetic contribution to Australian ancestry — failed to account for Denisovan admixture present in Australian populations; a reanalysis by Reich's lab correcting for Denisovan mixture found no such Southern Route signal (as of Reich 2018), a specific methodological disagreement recorded here as an attributed critique rather than an adjudicated verdict.

Reich characterizes Willerslev's practice of approaching indigenous and descendant communities directly, outside formal institutional (e.g. NAGPRA-style) processes, after obtaining ancient DNA — as with an Australian Aboriginal hair sample, the Clovis skeleton, and the Kennewick skeleton — as "innovative and brilliant," generating community goodwill and enabling further research, while also noting the approach is controversial among some archaeologists and museum professionals (as of Reich 2018). See [[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]] for the broader ethical debate over ancient human remains and community consent.
