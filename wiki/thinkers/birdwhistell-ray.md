# Ray L. Birdwhistell (1918–1994)

American anthropologist, credited by the editors of Gumperz and Hymes's 1972 *Directions
in Sociolinguistics* as the "inventor of the field of kinesics." At the time of writing he
was Professor of Communications at the Annenberg School, University of Pennsylvania (PhD,
University of Chicago). His chapter, "A Kinesic-Linguistic Exercise: The Cigarette
Scene," analyzes an 18-second filmed interactional sequence at microscopic resolution,
proposing that body motion is structured, patterned communication analogous to
phonology.

## Overview

Birdwhistell's data were "The Cigarette Scene," an 18-second segment from a larger
10-minute sound-filmed interview ("Doris and Gregory") collected in 1956 at the Center
for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences by Gregory Bateson, Henry Brosin, Charles
Hockett, Norman McQuown, and Frieda Fromm-Reichmann, as part of the unpublished
multi-author project *The Natural History of an Interview* (still unpublished as of
Birdwhistell's writing, June 1967). Birdwhistell analyzed the film at "every third frame
of a movie taken at 24 frames a second." The editors' headnote (pp. 382–384) describes
kinesics as institutionally thin — dependent on "a very few men" — and as contested even
within its own small research community, noting ongoing disagreement among Birdwhistell,
McQuown, and Brosin on how to treat the same corpus as of 1967.

## Contributions

All contributions below are attributed to Birdwhistell.

- **[[kinesics|Kinesics]].** Birdwhistell analyzed body motion as structured, patterned
  communication by explicit analogy to descriptive and structural linguistics, stating
  that "the theory and methodology of kinesics has been consistently influenced by that
  of descriptive and structural linguistics" — a commitment explicitly borrowed from the
  Trager-Smith linguistic model, which the volume's editors note had by 1967 already been
  superseded within linguistics itself by transformational-generative grammar, producing
  a mismatch between kinesics' founding model and the linguistics mainstream.
- **Core units of kinesic analysis.** By analogy to phone/phoneme, Birdwhistell proposed
  the **kine** (an isolable element of body motion), the **kineme** (a class of
  substitutable allokines), the **kinemorph**, and the **kinemorpheme**.
- **Instrumental, demonstrative, and interactional behavior.** A tripartite typology of
  non-kinesic-proper body behavior requiring separate analytic treatment: (1)
  **instrumental** behavior (task-oriented acts, e.g. lighting a cigarette, not thereby
  devoid of signal value); (2) **demonstratives** (patterned illustrative gestures
  resisting strict kinesic/linguistic analogy); (3) **interactional behavior** (spacing
  and orientation phenomena, citing Edward Hall's proxemics and Albert Scheflen's
  psychiatric-interview movement-pattern work). Birdwhistell framed instrumental and
  communicative readings as an "alternating context" — "Either can be the context for the
  other" — not a fixed hierarchy.
- **Kinesic junctures.** Six "tentative kinemes of juncture" (double cross, double bar,
  triple cross, hold, single bar, tie), segmenting stretches of body-motion behavior
  analogous to terminal junctures in phonology; Birdwhistell explicitly hedged that the
  relation of these junctures to content shifts is "often but not always" present and
  unresolved pending further research.
- **Stress kinemes.** Four variants (primary, secondary, unstressed, destressed) marking
  body-motion flow in parallel to, but not identical with, linguistic stress; Birdwhistell
  reported these track linguistic stress "in general" but diverge "in more than 20 per
  cent of the cases," an impressionistic count from "several thousands of exercises" run
  on sound-filmed data, not a controlled or formally sampled study.
- **Explicit non-reductionism.** Birdwhistell argued kinesic and linguistic
  suprasegmentals are "interdependent but not equivalent" systems, rejecting any claim
  that "the fuller channel carries the 'real' meaning" where kinesic and linguistic
  distinctions diverge.
- **Multimodal-analysis argument.** Birdwhistell concluded that "any discourse analysis,
  conversational analysis, communicational analysis, or interactional analysis which
  would attend to but one modality — lexical, linguistic, or kinesic — must suffer from...
  the assumption that the other modalities maintain a steady or non-influential state" —
  an explicit methodological argument for multimodal analysis.
- **Small informant-response sub-study.** Twelve women of comparable age and class to
  "Doris" were shown a typescript of a key analyzed utterance; eleven read it as standard
  "woman talk" (an apology-plus-pride pattern), one dissented. A separate control group of
  six, tested for recall after five minutes, produced systematic paraphrase
  substitutions. This is a thin-n, uncontrolled elicitation exercise embedded in the
  kinesic analysis, not a validated finding.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The volume's own editorial framing (attributed to Gumperz and Hymes) flags kinesics as
institutionally thin and internally contested — Birdwhistell, McQuown, and Brosin
disagreed on how to treat the same film corpus as of 1967 — and notes that kinesics'
founding linguistic model (Trager-Smith) had already been superseded within linguistics
itself. Birdwhistell's own stress-kineme finding (20% divergence from linguistic stress)
is an unreplicated, single-researcher impressionistic count.
