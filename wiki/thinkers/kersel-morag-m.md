# Morag M. Kersel

Archaeologist specializing in the **legal and illegal trade of antiquities**, especially in the Middle East. Co-editor of [[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Archaeology, Cultural Heritage, and the Antiquities Trade]] (2006); author of chapter 9, "From the Ground to the Buyer: A Market Analysis of the Trade in Illegal Antiquities." As of the volume's contributor note, she had recently completed her Ph.D. at the University of Cambridge.

## Overview

Kersel's 2006 contribution models the antiquities trade as a **chain from ground to buyer**, supplying the middle-market structure often missing from purely ethical or purely legal treatments. She sits in the IARC-adjacent empirical program with [[brodie-neil|Neil Brodie]] and co-editors [[luke-christina|Christina Luke]] and [[tubb-kathryn-walker|Kathryn Walker Tubb]].

## Contributions

- **Market-chain analysis** (attributed, Kersel 2006 ch. 9) — stages, actors, and price formation between looters, middlemen, dealers, and collectors/museums.
- **Co-editorship** of the 2006 Florida volume.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Cambridge archaeology Ph.D.; collaboration with Brodie / Luke / Tubb editorial team; dialogue with Renfrew heritage-ethics agenda and legal scholars ([[gerstenblith-patty]] and others in the same volume).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Market ethnographies and chain models are sometimes challenged by trade advocates as incomplete or as conflating legal and illegal circuits — record such challenges as positions on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] when sourced.

## Contributions (densified)

### Three-stage market model (ch. 9, attributed)
1. **Archaeologically rich / source** — subsistence diggers and looter bands; finders often receive **less than 1%** of retail.
2. **Transit** — free-ports and "no questions asked" jurisdictions (Switzerland, Hong Kong, Argentina as types) produce export papers and clean bills of sale.
3. **Destination** — dealers, auctions, collectors, museums; prefer not to ask origin after laundering.

Path variations: single-region chains (James ossuary path; Salisbury Hoard; U.S. Southwest) vs multi-country. Countermeasures: site protection funding, training at all stages, community engagement so excavation labor skills are not converted to looting technique.
