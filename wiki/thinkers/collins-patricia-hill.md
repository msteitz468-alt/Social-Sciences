# Patricia Hill Collins

## Overview

Patricia Hill Collins (b. 1948) is a U.S. sociologist and social theorist, Distinguished
University Professor Emerita of Sociology at the University of Maryland and former Charles
Phelps Taft Professor at the University of Cincinnati. In 2009 she became the first
African-American woman elected president of the American Sociological Association. Her
*[[collins-black-feminist-thought-2000|Black Feminist Thought]]* (1990; 2nd ed. 2000) is a
foundational statement of [[black-feminist-thought|Black feminism as critical social
theory]] and among the most-cited works in contemporary sociology. She writes, by
methodological choice, in the first person and treats scholarship and activism as a
"both/and" unity.

## Contributions

Collins developed or reframed several analytical tools now central to the study of
inequality: the [[matrix-of-domination]] and its four **domains of power**;
[[controlling-images]] of Black womanhood; the [[outsider-within]] location;
[[othermothers]] and women-centered kin networks; and a distinctive
[[black-feminist-epistemology]] (lived experience, dialogue, ethic of caring, ethic of
personal accountability). Her work is a principal channel through which
[[standpoint-theory]] and [[intersectionality]] entered mainstream sociology, and she gave
standpoint theory an influential **non-hierarchical, anti-essentialist** reformulation.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Collins draws on and synthesizes an African-American women's intellectual tradition — Maria
Stewart, Sojourner Truth, Anna Julia Cooper, Ida B. Wells-Barnett — and her contemporaries
Angela Davis, Audre Lorde, bell hooks, and Barbara Smith, alongside the term
"intersectionality" from Kimberlé Crenshaw. On the epistemology side she engages feminist
standpoint theorists (Hartsock, Dorothy Smith, Harding) and the sociology of knowledge
(Mannheim; Berger and Luckmann). Her frameworks are widely taken up across sociology, law,
education, and gender studies.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

(Attributed.) Collins's work has been challenged for the difficulties standpoint theory
faces — essentialism and relativism — objections she anticipates by insisting the Black
women's standpoint is collective, achieved, and internally diverse. Her most-documented
scholarly exchange is the [[doing-difference-debate]], where she argues that West and
Fenstermaker's interactional program offers "difference without power." She is herself a
critic within Black feminism of ranking oppressions and of romanticized authenticity
claims.

## Historiography

*Black Feminist Thought* itself narrates a fraught production history — the first edition's
publisher was taken over and its staff fired a month before release, and readers kept it
alive by circulating copies until it found its current publisher. The book's shift from a
1990 first edition to a substantially reorganized 2000 second edition (adding sexuality and
nation, developing the domains of power) is itself a documented episode in Collins's
intellectual development, which she reflects on in the Preface to the Second Edition.
