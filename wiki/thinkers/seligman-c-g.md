# C. G. Seligman

## Overview

Charles Gabriel Seligman (1873–1940), British anthropologist of the Cambridge school, teacher and patron of [[malinowski-bronislaw|Bronislaw Malinowski]] and teacher of [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]]. *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts of the Western Pacific]]* is dedicated to "my friend and teacher Professor C. G. Seligman, F.R.S." Author of *The Melanesians of British New Guinea* (1910), which Malinowski cites as baseline Massim ethnography. Evans-Pritchard (App. IV of the Azande abridgement) names him as teacher and recalls practical field counsel (quinine; keep off women).

## Contributions

- Cambridge-school Melanesian ethnography; *Melanesians of British New Guinea* (1910) as regional baseline for Malinowski's Massim work.
- Teacher/patron role for Malinowski (dedication; arranged Melanesia funding after a failed Sudan-Arab grant).
- Teacher of Evans-Pritchard (Azande fieldwork reminiscences).
- Torres Strait Expedition member (1898–9) with [[haddon-a-c|Haddon]] and [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] — Kuper’s pre-1922 field-career baseline.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained:** [[malinowski-bronislaw]], [[evans-pritchard-e-e]]
- **peers:** [[haddon-a-c]], [[rivers-w-h-r]] (Torres Strait circle)
- **LSE:** ethnology professor; held ethnology chair while Malinowski took the 1927 social anthropology chair

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]], *[[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015|Anthropology and Anthropologists]]* 4th ed.)*

- **Field path.** Kuper: Torres Strait with Haddon/Rivers → Melanesia 1904 → Veddas Sri Lanka 1907–8 → later Sudan. **1911** *The Veddas* (with B. Z. Seligman): Kuper quotes the admission that Veddas performed “professional primitive man” for tourists and had been “utterly spoilt” by frequent interviewing — a cautionary prehistory of [[participant-observation]] and informant reliability.
- **Malinowski patronage.** 1912: sought small grant for Malinowski on Arab tribes in Sudan (Seligman’s field) — turned down; then arranged two scholarships for Melanesia. Passage via Marett’s Australian conference secretaryship; WWI *libera custodia* narrative densified on Malinowski’s page.
- **LSE division of labour.** From 1925 Seligman taught “General Ethnology” (prehistory, races); Malinowski “Social Anthropology” via Trobriand material. Gentleman’s agreement that Africa students go initially to Seligman — Kuper: Malinowski “did not stick to the pact” and insisted all research students attend his seminars. When Rockefeller/IAI funded “applied anthropology” in Africa, Kuper reports Malinowski cornered the market, **shutting out Seligman** (the LSE African expert) and beating competing bids including [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]].
- **Sudan government non-use.** Kuper notes Seligman (and later Evans-Pritchard) as cases of anthropological work not absorbed into routine colonial administration in the Sudan.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Seligman's racial and diffusionist work of later career is outside *Argonauts* and not yet primary-ingested. Kuper’s institutional narrative frames him as Melanesian/Sudan ethnologist overtaken by Malinowski’s seminar monopoly and applied-Africa funding.

## Historiography

*Argonauts* dedication + E-P App. IV + Kuper 2015 densification (Torres Strait → Veddas → LSE patronage/competition). Primary monographs still thin in the collection.

## Stocking's account (*After Tylor*, 1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]].)*

- Part-time Professor of Ethnology at LSE from 1912; secured [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s travelling fellowship for the 1914 Australia trip and designed his fieldwork project as a follow-up to the Torres Straits Expedition, targeting the boundary region between two ethnic groups; continued private/independent financial support of Malinowski's career and helped place him at LSE.
- Seligman reportedly regarded Malinowski's "Baloma" essay as his best work and felt much of Malinowski's later 1920s output was "compromised by proselytizing purpose" (Seligman's private view, per Stocking).
- A 1930s "gentleman's agreement" divided LSE teaching — Malinowski's students to social anthropology, Seligman's to fieldwork method, regional ethnology, prehistory, and physical anthropology — but broke down within a year over whether Malinowski's students had to attend Seligman's lectures; the dispute was resolved by Seligman's declining health and 1934 retirement. In a testy 1930–31 exchange over interim chairmanship, Seligman accused Malinowski's public writing style of "semi-popular propaganda work... in the manner of the more strident kind of journalist"; Malinowski in turn felt Seligman was conflating "decided criticisms of theories" with "personal attacks."
- Seligman is described by Stocking as glorifying ethnographic fieldwork as "the blood of martyrs" to the church of anthropology, a framing echoed by later generations as fieldwork became the doctoral sine qua non.
- *African Political Systems* (1940) was dedicated to Seligman rather than to Malinowski, despite most contributors being Malinowski's students — recorded by Stocking as a marker of the discipline's shifting institutional loyalties in the transition from Malinowskian functionalism to Radcliffe-Brownian social structure. See [[radcliffe-brown-a-r]], [[structural-functionalism]].
