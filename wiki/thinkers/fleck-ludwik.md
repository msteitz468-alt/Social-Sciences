# Ludwik Fleck

## Overview

Polish physician, bacteriologist, and philosopher of science (1896–1961), author of *Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact* (1935), a study of how the identification of syphilis was stabilized as a scientific fact. Coined the [[thought-collective|thought collective]] (Denkkollektiv) and thought style (Denkstil). This page is currently built from Mary Douglas's extended engagement in *[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How Institutions Think]]* (1986), which pairs Fleck with [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] as co-anchor of a sociological theory of cognition; a direct ingest of *Genesis and Development* would deepen it.

## Contributions

- **[[thought-collective|Thought collective / thought style]]** — Fleck argued (1935) that "cognition is the most socially-conditioned activity of man, and knowledge is the paramount social creation," and that "every epistemological theory is trivial that does not take the sociological dependence of all cognition into account" (Fleck 1935, p. 42, as quoted in Douglas 1986). The thought style sets the preconditions of cognition — which questions are reasonable, which answers count as true — and is "essentially hidden" from members: "The individual within the collective is never, or hardly ever, conscious of the prevailing thought style which almost always exerts an absolutely compulsive force upon his thinking" (1935, p. 41).
- **Structure of collectives** — Fleck distinguished the thought collective (true believers) from the looser "thought community"; collectives vary from transient to highly stable (guilds, churches), with an inner elite at the center and "ossification at the rim" (as reported in Douglas 1986).
- **Science as a thought collective** — unlike Durkheim, Fleck applied the sovereign-thought-style analysis to modern science itself — a move Douglas remarked "would have horrified Durkheim." He accused Durkheimians of "an excessive respect, bordering on pious reverence, for scientific facts" (1935, pp. 46–47).
- **Demand-driven attention** — Fleck noted the syphilis cure was demanded more urgently than a tuberculosis cure though TB killed more; Douglas glosses this (citing Hacking 1983) as "thinking has more to do with intervening than with representing."

## Intellectual Genealogy

Douglas (1986) reads Fleck as extending — and in places exceeding — [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim's]] sociological epistemology from the history-of-science side, while judging him *more* methodologically consistent than Durkheim on one point: Fleck grounds the persistence of shared categories in communication and trust rather than in ritual-induced emotional effervescence, thus honoring Durkheim's own rule that social facts be explained by social facts. Thomas Kuhn (1962) was the first since 1937 to draw public attention to Fleck, but in his foreword to the 1979 English translation called the thought collective "intrinsically misleading" — "an individual mind writ large" (Kuhn 1979, p. x).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Fleck's 1935 book was initially rejected by reviewers — contrasted with the immediate success of Popper's *Logik der Forschung*, published about the same time. Editor-translator Trenn (1979) attributed the difference to relative thought-collective strength: Popper wrote from inside the prestigious Vienna philosophical milieu; Fleck was a working serologist and an outsider to philosophy. Reviewers faulted him for reductionist minimization of the individual scientist. Douglas (1986) treats this reception history as itself an instance of Fleck's theory. Kuhn's and Rawls's discomfort with collective-mind language (Rawls 1971 rejects treating society as "an organic whole with a life of its own") represents the standing liberal objection.

## Historiography

Rediscovered largely through Kuhn's citation (1962) and the 1979 English translation of *Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact*; Mary Douglas's *How Institutions Think* (1986) made him a founding figure for the sociology of institutional cognition.
