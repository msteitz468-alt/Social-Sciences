# René Millon

## Overview

René Millon, of the University of Rochester, directed the **Teotihuacan Mapping Project**, the systematic archaeological survey that established the full extent, grid layout, and orientation of [[sites/teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]]. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], the project demonstrated that the city "covered over 8 sq. miles (20 sq. km) and was fully urbanized," the largest Pre-Columbian New World city, laid out on a grid plan "oriented to 15 degrees 25 minutes east of true north" shortly after the time of Christ.

## Contributions

- Directed the **Teotihuacan Mapping Project**, producing the detailed map of the city's central core and establishing its overall size, grid orientation, and urban character (as opposed to a dispersed "ceremonial center" model).
- Estimated Teotihuacan's peak population, in the early fourth century AD, at approximately 125,000, possibly up to 200,000 (attributed, per Coe & Koontz 2013).
- The Mapping Project's finding that the city consisted primarily of walled residential apartment compounds (4,300–75,000 sq ft / 400–7,000 sq m) is a direct empirical product of Millon's survey.

## Reception in the Basin of Mexico synthesis

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (*[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|The Basin of Mexico]]*, 1979) relied heavily on Millon's Mapping Project data: his population estimate of ~40,000 for Teotihuacan underwrites their calculation that 94–97.4% of the Valley's population resided in the city by Phase Four ("extreme nucleation" — see [[urbanization]]), and unpublished stratigraphic-excavation results from Millon's project were made available to and used by the Sanders/Parsons/Santley survey. The authors also engage a **position attributed to Millon (1976)**, itself drawing on Jane Jacobs' *The Economy of Cities* (1969): that cities generate their own developmental dynamic and drive agricultural intensification in their hinterland independent of surrounding ecology. Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) accept this "city as independent driver" claim as consistent with their own Teotihuacan zone model, but explicitly reject the further Jacobs/Binford (1968) claim — which Millon's position is associated with in their discussion — that agriculture itself originated among nucleated urban hunter-gatherer settlements, calling it unsupported by Mesoamerican agricultural-origins data.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Millon's population and orientation figures remain the field's baseline data, cited without revision by Coe & Koontz (2013); no reappraisal or critique of the Mapping Project's methodology is recorded in this source.

## Historiography

The Teotihuacan Mapping Project is treated by Coe & Koontz as the foundational empirical basis for essentially all subsequent Teotihuacan scholarship discussed in their volume, including the apartment-compound residential model and the isotopic/enclave (Oaxaca barrio) studies that build on its spatial framework.
