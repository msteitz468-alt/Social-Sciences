# R. H. Codrington

## Overview

Robert Henry Codrington (1830–1922) was an Anglican missionary of the Melanesian Mission and
a pioneering missionary-ethnographer of island Melanesia. His *The Melanesians* (1891) and
*The Melanesian Languages* (1885) were the standard authorities on Banks Islands and
Melanesian society before [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], and introduced the concept of
**[[mana]]** (impersonal supernatural power) into anthropology, where it became central to
theories of religion and magic (Marett, Durkheim, Mauss).

## Contributions

- **Mana.** Codrington's report of the Melanesian concept of *mana* — a diffuse, transferable
  supernatural efficacy attaching to persons, objects, and words — became a foundational datum
  for early-20th-century theories of religion.
- **Documentation of the [[secret-societies|Sukwe and Tamate]].** Codrington gave the first
  detailed account of the Banks Islands graded club-house society and the bush "ghost"
  societies, and established the region as a type case of **[[dual-organisation]]**.
- **Melanesian linguistics.** *The Melanesian Languages* provided the phonetic transcriptions
  and vocabulary Rivers relied on for his Oceanic comparison.
- **Biography and formation** (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995 — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995]]): High Church Anglican, educated at Charterhouse and Wadham College, Oxford (Fellow from 1855); joined the Melanesian Mission in 1867 under Bishop John Coleridge Patteson, based first at Norfolk Island. His fieldwork was conducted largely at the Mission's Norfolk Island base among Melanesians brought there as students, supplemented by five voyages on the mission ship — his longest continuous stay in the islands proper was six weeks, at Mota in 1869. Patteson was killed in 1871 on Nukapu; Codrington and colleagues attributed the killing to prior abuses by "blackbirder" labor recruiters, and Codrington's subsequent report to the Society for the Propagation of the Gospel reached Prime Minister Gladstone and contributed to laws regulating (though not yet abolishing) the Pacific labor trade.
- Corresponded with [[fison-lorimer|Fison]] from 1872 to 1890; visited England in 1883 and attended [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s inaugural Oxford lectures as Reader in Anthropology. Published "Notes on the Customs of Mota, Banks Islands" (1879, arranged by Fison) and *The Melanesians* (1891); responded to James G. Frazer's totemism questionnaire in 1888.

## Codrington's dispute with Tylorian animism, and with totemism (attributed)

*(Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995.)* Codrington first described **mana** in an 1878 letter to Max Müller — "a supernatural power belonging to the region of the unseen," pursued through prayer and sacrifice, manifest "in physical force, or in any kind of power or excellence which a man possesses" — later republished in *The Melanesians* (1891). He presented mana as differing fundamentally from Tylorian [[animism]]: he explicitly disputed [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s animism thesis as applied to Melanesia, reporting he had found no Melanesian belief in "a spirit which animates any natural object" in the manner Tylor's theory required, and no evidence Melanesians failed to distinguish animate from inanimate. On Codrington's account Melanesian religion consisted in awe before extraordinary power, not — as Tylor's theory held — a proto-scientific "explanation" of nature. Max Müller in turn used Codrington's mana concept in his own Hibbert Lectures to argue against evolutionary (Tylorian) interpretations of the origin of religion, an early instance of mana being mobilized against animism.

Codrington similarly disputed the applicability of "totemism" to Melanesia, telling Frazer he did not believe genuine totemic descent-belief existed there, though a sufficiently motivated inquirer could probably elicit totem-like answers through leading questions ([[totemism]]).

## Codrington's fieldwork epistemology and critique of armchair anthropology (attributed)

Codrington criticized armchair anthropology by name (citing Lubbock specifically) for mistranslating and over-interpreting native statements — e.g., "savages" reported as unable to count past four, or taboo language ("plenty devil up there") taken literally rather than as adopted-English idiom. He held that adequate ethnographic understanding of religion required long residence, humility, and working directly through native testimony; he warned that missionaries often systematized native belief "too soon" and thereafter "made whatever they observe fit into their early scheme," and that all fieldworkers unconsciously project preconceptions ("he expects to see idols, and he sees them"). He cautioned Fison specifically that English kinship-term translations ("son," "daughter") imported false English connotations of "sonship" absent from Mota usage, and warned against imputing "promiscuous relationship" from mistranslated terms.

Stocking characterizes Codrington's underlying orientation as continuous with the pre-evolutionary "ethnological" (Prichardian/philological) paradigm associated with Max Müller, rather than a genuine convert to evolutionism, despite surface engagement with Tylor and Frazer. Stocking further argues that Codrington's ethnographic and translation skepticism anticipates concerns Franz Boas was raising independently in America at the same period, and that [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]]'s later "genealogical method" and post-1911 turn away from evolutionism toward migrationist "ethnological" analysis had roots traceable to Codrington's Melanesian tradition.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Codrington worked from Norfolk Island as the intellectual centre of the Melanesian Mission
(founded by Bishop Patteson, whose policy of preserving native institutions Rivers later cited
as a reason his own data were relatively uncontaminated). His work was the direct predecessor
and foil for [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]], who repeatedly supplemented and corrected him — most
notably arguing (against Codrington) that the Sukwe and Tamate societies are structurally
linked rather than separate institutions.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Codrington's data were collected under mission conditions, often from Christianized informants
and via the Mota lingua franca; his framing was that of a sympathetic Victorian evolutionist.
His *ethnographic record* has held up as a valuable early witness — Rivers treated it as
reliable — even as the evolutionist interpretive frame was superseded. The *mana* concept he
introduced has itself been much debated and partly deconstructed (as an over-generalized
category) in later anthropology.

## Historiography

*The Melanesians* remains a cited primary source for late-19th-century Banks Islands
ethnography. Codrington's role is treated in histories of the Melanesian Mission and in
reappraisals of [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]' Melanesian work (Herle & Rouse, Hviding), and in
[[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]], which uses Codrington's
decades-long correspondence with Fison and his dispute with Tylorian animism as evidence
for cracks within classical evolutionism predating the Boasian critique.
