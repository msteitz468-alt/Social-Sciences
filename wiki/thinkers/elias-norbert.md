# Norbert Elias

**Hub:** [[hubs/thinkers/elias-norbert|Thinkers Hub — Elias]]

## Overview

[[elias-norbert|Norbert Elias]] (1897–1990) was a German-born sociologist whose *[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]* (1939; English rev.) founded process-figurational sociology: long-run, unplanned interweaving of [[state-formation]], the [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of physical force]], and the transformation of manners and affect controls ([[threshold-of-shame]], [[self-constraint]]). Delayed reception (postwar English-language recognition from the 1960s–70s) made him a late-canon classical figure rather than a contemporaneous peer of the interwar Chicago and Frankfurt scenes.

## Contributions

*(To be densified from ingest claims.)* Core contributions: [[civilizing-process]] theory; [[figuration]] as unit of analysis (interdependence webs, not isolated individuals or reified systems); [[monopoly-mechanism]] and [[royal-mechanism]] in European [[state-formation]]; documentary series method on manners manuals; [[sociogenesis-psychogenesis|socio- and psychogenesis]] linked; early use of [[habitus]] for social moulding of drives (distinct from later Bourdieu consolidation).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

See [[civilizing-process-debate]]. Standing critiques (attributed in literature, not adjudicated here): alleged teleology and Eurocentrism; under-weighting of decivilizing bursts and 20th-century mass violence; gender and class scope of the manners series; relation of "civilization" to colonial expansion (Elias himself notes crusade/colonial echoes of the civilizing watchword).

## Historiography

German 1939 publication under exile conditions; English translation and 1968 Postscript reshaped reception. Dedicated hub biography: [[hubs/thinkers/elias-norbert]].

## Bauman's inversion of the civilizing process

[[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]]'s *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989) treats Elias's *civilizing process* as the leading scholarly support for an "etiological myth" of Western civilization as the movement from barbarism to restraint. Bauman argues (attributed) that the "civilizing process" did not eliminate violence but *redeployed* it — concentrating it in the state's monopoly and out of daily sight — so that the pacification of manners is bought with society's defencelessness before centralized coercion; "civilized manners showed an astounding ability to cohabit... with mass murder." The dispute is recorded on [[civilizing-process-debate]].
