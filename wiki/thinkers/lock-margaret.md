# Margaret Lock

## Overview

Margaret Lock is a Canadian medical anthropologist (McGill University — Social Studies of Medicine and Anthropology, as of the *Commodifying Bodies* bio). She co-authored with [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] the charter essay **[[the-mindful-body|"The Mindful Body"]]** (1987) and is a major analyst of brain death, organ transplantation, aging, and the alienation of body tissue. This page densifies her chapter “The Alienation of Body Tissue and the Biopolitics of Immortalized Cell Lines” in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (2002; first as *Body & Society* 2001). Related monographs named in the volume bio but not yet fully ingested: *Encounters with Aging*; *Twice Dead*; co-edited *Pragmatic Women and Body Politics* (with Patricia Kaufert); work in progress then titled “Tangled Memories, Alzheimer’s Disease, and the New Genetics.”

## Contributions

### Immortalized cell lines and gene prospecting *(attributed — Lock, 2001/02)*

Lock treats **immortalized cell lines** (genetically identical cultured cells that continue dividing under proper conditions) as prototypical techno/natural hybrids whose patenting converts genetic information into intellectual property. She cites mid-1990s market projections for tissue culture products (~US $428 million/year, growth forecasts toward ~$1 billion by 2002) and the mid-1990s legal infrastructure that globalized IP: the Convention on Biological Diversity (Rio 1992) and GATT TRIPs (1994), under which donors typically lose property rights and profit share unless special transfer agreements (as with the Hagahai) are written in.

Named patent controversies she narrates:

- **Guaymi** (Panama) — US Commerce Secretary patent claim on a cell line from blood of a 26-year-old Guaymi woman with leukemia; Guaymi General Congress opposition; Isidro Acosta’s 1993 Geneva effort to exclude human genetic material from TRIPs patenting failed; claim later dropped when the line proved unprofitable.
- **Hagahai** (New Guinea) and **Solomon Islands** — similar furor; Solomon Islanders opposed patenting from the outset; Hagahai reported as having agreed via anthropologist Carol Jenkins as mediator to blood donation, cell lines, patenting, and half-profit to claimants of Hagahai ethnicity if products emerged — Lock doubts full informed consent given 1980s “discovery” framing, while rejecting paternalist outsider veto of indigenous decision-making.
- Claims dropped, in Lock’s account, when lines proved unprofitable for pharma-business — **profit, not ethics**, as proximate cause of withdrawal.

She argues venture capital drives DNA procurement from indigenous peoples with features of mineral prospecting (uncertain futures); resistance is **not uniform**; commodity status of body parts is contestable, negotiable, and mutable.

### Gift critique and organ “gift of life” *(attributed — Lock)*

Lock mobilizes Mauss via Taussig, Sahlins’s caution that reciprocity is everywhere the indigenous category of exploitation, Strathern on power/gender in Melanesian exchange, Weiner on inalienable possessions and “keeping-while-giving,” and Thomas/Appadurai on the promiscuity and social lives of objects. Core thesis: body tissues, organs, and fluids are more often than not regarded as **inalienable** except in carefully circumscribed ritual or reproductive activity; to free them for exchange they must first be reified as thing-like, non-self, detachable without irreparable loss — the mystical/transcendental essence must be dissipated.

On transplant practice she argues (via Sharp and her own *Twice Dead* program) that the “gift of life” metaphor is deliberately fetishized to promote donation, while recipients who attribute animistic qualities to organs risk being reprimanded as pathological. Animation of body parts is **not** confined to the “underdeveloped” world — she reports a heart-transplant surgeon’s unease at receiving a “murderer’s heart.” **Japan**: brain death recognized only after a vituperative ~30-year debate, under delimited circumstances; relatively few transplants, virtually confined to living related donors (Lock 2001).

### History of corpse and body-part commodification *(attributed — Lock via Richardson, Linebaugh, Park)*

Long history of corpse and body-part value (trophies, relics, medicinals, anatomical specimens). Christian resurrection anxiety limited willing alienation except for saints’ relics. Public dissection of criminals/vagrants from 13th-c. Italy through early 19th-c. anatomy theatres. From the 17th century (Richardson; Linebaugh), the corpse becomes commodity with a price list. **Tyburn** hangings: surgeons and kin fought over half-dead bodies. **Anatomy Act (1831)** prohibited sale of dead bodies yet remains foundation for UK/North American scientific body-procurement law; post-Act workhouses and hospitals remained “lawfully in possession of the dead,” confiscating unclaimed poor — the poor effectively defined as socially dead for medicine’s interest. Bentham’s self-donation as pedagogy of voluntary gift; N/S Europe contrast on dissection as punishment vs expiation. North America: disproportionate Native and African-American bodies in medical institutions (Richardson). Post-WWII rise in body gifts to medical schools. Living biological materials (except blood trade, e.g. Haiti → US) could not enter global markets before late 20th-c. expertise.

### Immortalization technology and HeLa *(attributed — Lock via Landecker)*

Genealogy of immortalization from Alexis Carrel’s early 20th-c. tissue culture through thousands of contemporary human cell lines. **HeLa** (Henrietta Lacks): biomass after death exceeds living mass. Patenting requires showing the natural object was transformed into an “invention” — reification of cells as solely technological creation is integral to claims. NIH defender Amar Bhat’s claim that laboratories “cloned only the genes of the two viruses” (Hagahai/Solomon) is, for Lock, reification heaped upon reification.

### Human Genome Diversity Project (HGDP) *(attributed — Lock)*

Central case of the chapter’s second half. Conceived ~1991 by Allan Wilson and [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Luca Cavalli-Sforza]]; goal to survey human genetic variation via immortalized cell lines (400–500 populations; minimum 25 individuals per named group; plus cheek scrapings/hair roots). Remained virtually unfunded/unrealized. Scientists claimed anti-racism contribution; Lock argues the project stepped naively into the race–genetics minefield and committed a **category fallacy**: selecting groups who identify on shared culture and assuming shared genetic constitution — conflating time and space; gene-pool analysis tells little about ephemeral socio-political groupings.

Resistance and ethics:

- Onondaga Council / Native-L protest (Chief Leon Shenandoah, Dec 1993); “vampire project” label; blood immortalized while groups “continue on the road to extinction.”
- 722 groups selected without consultation as “genetic isolates” from a preliminary 7000.
- RAFI alerted World Council of Indigenous Peoples; urged veto powers and UN control — ignored for years; Montréal 1996 session excluded indigenous representatives → demonstration blocked by police.
- Named construct challenges: San peoples (colonial isolation, three language groups — Wilmsen); Japanese burakumin (“eta”) on list — discrimination product not genetic Other (Hatanaka); Yuchi contacted as “unique tribe” after BIA denied political recognition — turned scientist away.
- Fears (Montana 1999 conference) that genetic “purity” tests could damage land claims — Lock judges justified.
- North American Model Ethical Protocol (community + individual consent; compensation; sample withdrawal rights) — cannot bind private multinationals; no prior consultation before 1997; disease/therapy framing of design misleads (no matched phenotypic/environmental data).
- Aroha Te Pareake Mead (Maori Congress): genes as family/clan/nation heritage; patenting not humanitarian; Hagahai consent likely inadequate — Lock records position and cautions on who speaks for whom.
- NRC Committee on Human Diversity (1996–97): scientific merit for origins/evolution but numerous ethical/legal/human-rights challenges; *Nature* vs *Science* split reception; NIH instead launched $60M Environmental Genome Project.
- Existing US/Canadian tissue depositories poorly regulated (1995 counts).
- Ownership claims plural (group vs individual US property law after separation vs humankind vs patent necessity).
- Kennewick Man politics and Out-of-Africa vs time-immemorial narratives as creation-story sparring that blocks cooperation.
- Conclusion: biopolitics of DNA/tissue/organs circulation is here to stay; Gene Giants promote secrecy via patenting; UN/WHO/UNESCO bioethics silence on human DNA collection risks damaging affiliation across groups.

- Transplant/brain-death program in dialogue with Fox & Swazey, Sharp, Richardson; interlocutor for Organs Watch / Bellagio-era transplant ethics networks.
- Gift–commodity apparatus: Mauss, Marx via Taussig, Sahlins, Strathern, Weiner, Thomas, Appadurai, Haraway (corporeal fetishism), Bourdieu (symbolic capital).
- HGDP interlocutors: Cavalli-Sforza, Wilson, Lewontin, Duster, Rabinow (biosociality), RAFI, indigenous critics (Mead, Onondaga Council, Yuchi).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Secondary literature not yet ingested.)* Lock’s own meta-cautions: against uniform-resistance assumptions; against paternalist outsider veto of indigenous decisions; against essentializing gift vs commodity societies; against assuming medicalization is always unwilling (patients may welcome objectification to escape moralizing blame — Lock & Kaufert). On HGDP she records both indigenous rejection of “biodiversity resource” framing and the claim that evolution/archaeology research also undermined 19th-c. racist social-evolution discourse — benefit sometimes broader than local therapeutic gain.
