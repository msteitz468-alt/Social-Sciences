# Walter W. Powell

## Overview

Walter W. Powell is a sociologist of organizations. With Paul DiMaggio he co-edited *The
New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis* (1991) and co-authored ch. 3, "The Iron
Cage Revisited" (see [[dimaggio-paul]] for the joint 1983/1991 argument in full). He also
authored ch. 8 of the volume, "Expanding the Scope of Institutional Analysis," in which
he revisited and revised significant parts of his and DiMaggio's own earlier work.

## Contributions

In ch. 8, Powell argued that "the full power of the institutional perspective has yet to
be realized, due in part to ambiguities in some of the initial contributions to this
line of work" and stated explicitly, "I take issue with some of my previous work, done
collaboratively with Paul DiMaggio." He identified three needed corrections: (1) the
technical/institutional and competitive/institutional-isomorphism distinctions,
intended by Meyer, Scott, and Deal (1981) and DiMaggio and Powell (1983) as purely
analytical, had been misread as describing empirically distinct kinds of organizations,
wrongly ceding market processes an exclusive claim to rational-actor explanation; (2)
the "loosely coupled" insight had been misread to imply institutionalized organizations
are passive symbol-manipulators rather than substantively constrained actors; (3) the
program needed better accounts of heterogeneity within institutional environments and of
institutional change, since existing work presented "a static, constrained, and
oversocialized view of organizations."

Powell proposed **four avenues of institutional reproduction** — the exercise of power,
complex interdependencies, taken-for-granted assumptions, and path-dependent
development — the last drawing on Stinchcombe's (1965) imprinting findings and on
Brian Arthur and Paul David's economic-history work on increasing returns and
technological lock-in (QWERTY, VHS vs. Betamax). He also enumerated six **sources of
heterogeneity in institutional environments** (varying resource environments, varying
industry/state relations, fragmented and overlapping governance, non-coercive government
requirements, varying occupational/professional projects, and varying organizational
capacity to shape institutional expectations), and catalogued patterns of institutional
*change* — unsuccessful imitation across contexts, recombination under mixed pressures,
incomplete institutionalization, and the recomposition of field boundaries (citing
Reagan-era federal devolution and European Community integration as examples). He argued
innovation characteristically originates at the periphery of organizational fields,
since peripheral units face the least isomorphic pressure.

Powell directly revisited the DiMaggio-Powell (1983) two-stage life-cycle model, in which
early adoption of a practice is efficiency-driven and later adoption isomorphism-driven.
He judged the model empirically supported (citing Tolbert and Zucker 1983; M. Meyer,
Stevenson, and Webster 1985; Baron, Dobbin, and Jennings 1986) but flagged four
shortcomings: it implies conformity is cheap ("smoke and mirrors" adaptation); it treats
the initially adopted practice as a natural technical solution rather than one path among
several not taken; criteria for a "good technical solution" are themselves contested
among interested parties; and it obscures the institutional-sponsorship process by which
innovations come to be defined as useful in the first place.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Powell's ch. 8 credits [[jepperson-ronald]] (ch. 6) for the point that institutional
reproduction need not depend on active agency, and for the caution that "not every
cultural element or environmental influence is an institutional effect." It cites
[[scott-w-richard]] (ch. 7) approvingly for reframing technical and institutional
factors as continuous dimensions rather than a dichotomy, and draws on
[[friedland-roger]] and [[alford-robert-r]] (ch. 10) for the claim that "profit and risk
are culturally constructed categories whose definitions vary widely across
nation-states." The chapter was written at the Center for Advanced Study in the
Behavioral Sciences with NSF and Exxon Education Fund support, and Powell acknowledges
benefiting from comments by [[dimaggio-paul]], Granovetter, Jepperson, Keohane, Krasner,
White, and Zald — a colleague/discussant network rather than a training lineage.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Powell's chapter is itself an act of self-critique of DiMaggio and Powell (1983) rather
than a target of others' critique within this volume; see [[dimaggio-paul]] for the
parallel critiques (from Fligstein and [[scott-w-richard]]) of the isomorphism argument
the two co-authored.

## Historiography

Powell first presented the "Iron Cage Revisited" argument at the 1981 American
Sociological Association meetings in Toronto; the co-authored paper was published in
*American Sociological Review* 48(2):147–160 (1983) and reprinted, with the pair's 1991
retrospective qualifications, as ch. 3 of the edited volume. See [[dimaggio-paul]] for
further publication-history detail shared by the two co-authors.
