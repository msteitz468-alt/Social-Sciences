# Timothy Insoll

British archaeologist of **religion, identity, and material culture**, best known in this vault for the programmatic synthesis *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]* (1999, Blackwell Social Archaeology series, series editor [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]). Positions [[islamic-archaeology]] as a social archaeology of a living world religion rather than an art-historical adjunct.

## Overview

Insoll’s 1999 program argues that Islam constitutes a cross-cultural **superstructure** of belief and obligation (Qur’an, hadith, shari’ah) whose **substructure** of local practice and material culture is highly diverse; Muslim communities should still leave archaeologically recognizable signatures across domains beyond the mosque and the cemetery — domestic space, diet, dress, trade, magic, warfare, settlement form. He critiques “top-heavy” Islamic archaeology (palace, great mosque, prestige arts) and its institutional separation from mainstream theory, and proposes multi-disciplinary, multi-scalar (Annales-inspired) work attentive to ordinary material life.

Later editorial and monographic work (as listed on key_works; not yet primary-ingested) extends the program to ritual/religion and identity more generally.

## Contributions

- **Program for [[islamic-archaeology]]** — *The Archaeology of Islam* (1999): recognition criteria across mosque, domestic, “Muslim life,” art/trade, burial, and community scales; ideals vs practical diversity.
- **Archaeology of religion as first-class domain** — refuses to quarantine death, diet, and magic from “religion”; links to broader [[archaeology-of-religion]] agenda.
- **Critique of art-historical and top-heavy research designs** — everyday ceramics, fauna, botanical remains, landscape, and non-elite settlements as underused evidence classes (attributed, Insoll 1999).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Works in conversation with [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] / [[social-archaeology|social archaeology]] (series home under Hodder); cites Islamic anthropology (Geertz, Gellner, Gilsenan) and Said’s orientalism critique as adjacent self-examination traditions. Engages Grabar on Islamic art/archaeology’s cataloguing bias; draws Annales multi-scalar language as practical scaffolding rather than full Braudelian orthodoxy. ## Critiques and Reappraisals

The 1999 volume’s own conclusion stresses **ideals vs realities** (as of Insoll 1999): an “ideal suite” of Islamic material culture will rarely appear complete; exceptions (grave goods, alcohol, misaligned mosques, non-courtyard houses) are expected; creating a stereotypical “ideal Muslim” is analytically flawed.
