# James Deetz

American historical archaeologist; principal popularizer of **[[historical-archaeology]]** as the archaeology of everyday **[[material-culture]]** in early America. Author of *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* (1977; expanded 1996). Ph.D. Harvard; taught at Brown, UC Berkeley (Distinguished Teaching Award), University of Virginia; earlier appointments included Harvard, William and Mary, UC Santa Barbara, and the University of Cape Town (1996 author note).

## Overview

Deetz joined documentary history to patterned material culture — ceramics, gravestones, vernacular houses, refuse — to recover cognitive and social change among non-elite Anglo-Americans and African Americans. His **1969** meeting with [[glassie-henry|Henry Glassie]] (Toledo) re-centered his American Material Culture course; by **1972** the course form stabilized; **1975–77** produced the first book. Later career shifted research to the **Chesapeake** ([[flowerdew-hundred|Flowerdew Hundred]] monograph 1993); the **1996** ISTF revision incorporates that work and a full African American archaeology program. Landmark case: **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]** (1975–78).

## Contributions

*(Attributed — [[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996]] / [[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-study]].)*

- **Definitions of HA:** literacy-based contrast with prehistory; popular global definition (European expansion since the 15th c. and Indigenous interaction) — later cited in [[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s definitional lineage; insistence that African American, Asian American, and post-contact Native American archaeology are integral. Deetz's (1977) formula — historical archaeology as "the archaeology of the spread of European cultures throughout the world since the fifteenth century… and their impact on and interaction with the cultures of indigenous peoples" — became the field's dominant working definition, but has drawn Eurocentrism critiques (Courtney; Funari; Tarlow & West) for centering European expansion as the organizing axis rather than a genuinely global or multi-vocal framing; see [[defining-historical-archaeology-debate]].
- **[[georgian-worldview]]** and three-period Anglo-American model (to ~1660 / ~1680–1760 / ~1760+).
- Mortuary **[[seriation]]** (death's head → cherub → urn and willow) as cultural chronology.
- Ceramic foodways arc from dairy/corporate use to individualized matching sets after creamware mass production.
- **[[earthfast-construction]]** and tobacco-capital logic (Carson et al.).
- African American archaeology as **[[creolization]]** (not simple [[acculturation]]): Parting Ways, [[shotgun-house]], [[colono-ware]], creole pipes, foodways continuum.
- Pedagogical dating toolkit: TPQ/TAQ, South **[[mean-ceramic-date]]**, Harrington/Binford pipestems; [[probate-inventory-analysis]] as folk taxonomy.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Positions — reception monographs not fully ingested.)* Over-coherence of Georgian order; New England center of gravity; ethnicity inferences from architecture and ceramics. **Mechanism rewritten by critical HA:** [[leone-mark-p|Leone]] (*[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|2010]]*; Annapolis inventories; [[william-paca-garden]]) keeps multi-domain Georgian patterning but treats it as **[[ideology]]** naturalizing hierarchy under merchant capitalism rather than only as cognitive re-Anglicization — see [[critical-archaeology]], [[georgian-worldview]]. Deetz anticipates a-priori critique by stressing multi-domain convergence and Parting Ways as boundary.

**Hall (2000) on internal inconsistency.** [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000) argued Deetz's structuralism cannot explain historical change — "a structuralist world is a world without history" — and is internally inconsistent: to account for observed blending of "older" and "Georgianized" house styles, Deetz "grafts on" a contradictory diffusionist/"infection" metaphor of cultural transmission (citing Appadurai 1996) that is incompatible with a model of subliminal, generation-spanning cultural grammars. Hall also noted that *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* produces a sanitized history that omits violence, leading "the reader through an early-colonial America which is largely benign and civilized." See [[structuralism]], [[theory-in-historical-archaeology-debate]].

Julia King ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. King) argues that Deetz's structuralist household paradigm, and critical archaeology's ideological rewriting of it alike, still treated material culture as **reflective** rather than constitutive of social relations, and eliminated discussion of use and users; households in both approaches risk becoming statistical "faceless blobs" identifiable only via a household head (Beaudry 1989a; Tringham 1991). Deetz's structuralist framework also shaped **maritime particularism** in North American historical maritime archaeology — a fine-grained, artefact-assemblage-focused tradition distinct from the "shipwreck archaeology as anthropology" program of Richard Gould (Flatman & Staniforth, Hicks & Beaudry 2006, ch. 10).
