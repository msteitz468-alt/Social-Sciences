# François Bordes (1919–1981)

French prehistorian and professor at the University of Bordeaux; the leading mid-20th-century systematist of the Lower and Middle Palaeolithic and [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]]'s principal interlocutor in the **[[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate|Mousterian variability debate]]**. Bordes built the descriptive apparatus — a standardized type-list and the **cumulative-graph** method — that made rigorous quantitative comparison of stone-tool assemblages possible, then read the resulting patterning through a **[[culture-historical-archaeology|culture-historical]]** lens: the recurring assemblage types were distinct cultural traditions ("peoples"). Binford accepted the apparatus and contested the reading. *(Biographical frame per standard reference; substantive characterizations attributed to the ingested Binford sources.)*

## Overview

Bordes developed morphological and technological criteria applied consistently enough "that total assemblage differences can be expressed in precise quantitative terms" (attributed, S.R. Binford in *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* 1968) — a level of systematics that impressed Americanists accustomed to "relatively unsystematic North American projectile-point treatment." Using cumulative graphs of tool-type frequencies, he recognized **four basic Mousterian assemblage types** in the Dordogne: Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition, Typical Mousterian, Denticulate Mousterian, and the Charentian group (Quina and Ferrassie subtypes) (attributed, Binford *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* 1983). His excavation of **Combe-Grenal** produced the long, micro-stratigraphically controlled sequence on which the debate turned.

## Contributions

*(All attributed — Bordes' program as characterized by the Binford sources.)*

- **The Mousterian type-list (*typologie*)** and the **cumulative graph** — the descriptive-statistical foundation of Western European Palaeolithic classification (extended to the Upper Palaeolithic by his collaborator and wife **Denise de Sonneville-Bordes**, whose *Le Paléolithique supérieur en Périgord* (1960) is "the indispensable point of departure" for Dordogne research — attributed, Sackett in *New Perspectives*).
- **The four Mousterian types** and the demonstration, at Combe-Grenal and related sequences, of **alternating industries** (types keep their identity through time but alternate in stratigraphic succession) and **parallel phyla** (types co-exist as recognizable cultures over long spans).
- The **"tenacious cultures"** interpretation: assemblage types persist relatively unchanged over long periods, unaffected by contact with distinct cultures in the same area — Binford's label for Bordes' reading that these are ethnic/cultural entities.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Worked within the French culture-historical tradition of Breuil and Peyrony (parallel-phyla reasoning, which the Abbé Breuil had made orthodox in the 1930s — attributed, Binford 1983). Collaborated with **Denise de Sonneville-Bordes**. Hosted **[[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]]** for a year in Bordeaux (1968–69) to study fauna and horizontal distributions; **[[binford-sally-r|Sally Binford]]** drew on his method and assistance for her Near Eastern Mousterian analyses. The Binford–Bordes relationship was famously collegial across deep disagreement — Binford dedicated *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983) to Bordes' memory, "remembering the many hours of our joking, arguing, and enjoying each others' minds."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* Binford argued Bordes' refined methods were "integrated within an older view of culture and the past compatible with a biological model: the tree of life" — treating assemblages as cultural species (Binford 1983). The **[[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate|Mousterian debate]]** counterposed Bordes' ethnic/cultural reading against the Binfords' **functional** reading (assemblage types = different activity tool-kits within one adaptive system; Binford & Binford 1966 factor analysis). The dispute is a landmark case of how the *same* patterning can bear opposite meanings; later work (e.g. Dibble on scraper reduction) added a third axis — that some "types" are stages of tool resharpening — partly dissolving the original binary.

## Historiography

Bordes is the standard reference for Middle Palaeolithic systematics; his type-list remains in use even where his cultural interpretation is not accepted. Under the pen name **Francis Carsac** he was also a published science-fiction author. His work anchors the culture-historical pole of the discipline's central methodological debate about what stone-tool variability means.

### Gamble 1999 note

*(Attributed — [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]], [[gamble-clive]]; as of Gamble 1999.)* Gamble retains Bordian descriptive apparatus (type-list, cumulative curves, technical indices IL/ILam/IR) while rejecting the **five Neanderthal tribes** reading of SW French facies as ethnic cultures. He reports Turq’s conclusion that **Quina** is not a culture in Bordes’s sense (time-economizing + local raw material + specialized activities), and notes that Bordes’s long Perigordian tradition linking Chatelperronian to MAT is generally discarded. Boëda-style *chaîne opératoire* is contrasted with Bordes’s static finished-form classification. Reciprocal detail: [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]], [[mousterian-industry]].
