# Leon Trotsky (1879–1940)

## Overview

Lev Davidovich Bronstein — "Trotsky" from a forged 1902 escape passport — was born 26 October 1879 at Yanovka farm, Kherson province (southern Ukraine), to a prosperous, religiously lax Jewish farming family in the New Russia Jewish agricultural colonies, and died 21 August 1940 in Coyoacán, Mexico City, murdered with an ice-axe by NKVD agent Ramón Mercader. He enters this wiki not as a statesman but as a Marxist social theorist: author of [[permanent-revolution]] and the "law of combined development," an early critic of party "substitutionism," a practitioner-theorist of insurrection, an ambitious (if instrumentalist) cultural sociologist, and — in *The Revolution Betrayed* (1937) — author of the most influential Marxist sociology of the Stalinist [[bureaucracy]]. Coverage long rested primarily on Robert Service's revisionist biography ([[service-trotsky-2009]]); its interpretive judgments are attributed to Service throughout. *[[trotsky-terrorism-and-communism-1920|Terrorism and Communism]]* (1920) is now ingested as primary for his civil-war defence of [[red-terror]] and [[militarization-of-labour]].

## Contributions

**Permanent revolution and combined development.** Formed in dialogue with his mentor Alexander Parvus (Helphand) from 1904 and written up in prison as *Results and Prospects* (1906), the theory held that Russia's weak bourgeoisie meant the small working class would have to lead the anti-tsarist revolution and pass directly to socialist tasks, with survival depending on revolution spreading internationally — against the two-stage orthodoxy of Plekhanov and Menshevism (see [[permanent-revolution]]). In *The History of the Russian Revolution* (1932–33) he generalized this into the "law of combined development": "backward" countries borrow and skip developmental stages achieved elsewhere, so backwardness can paradoxically accelerate revolutionary change. Service argues the core ideas were less original than Trotskyist tradition claims — the workers'-government idea came from Parvus, the backwardness analysis "mimed" Russia's Westernizing liberal historians (he names Boris Chicherin) — and that "nobody felt" Trotsky was Lenin's theoretical equal before 1914 (Service 2009, his positions).

**The substitutionism critique.** In *Our Political Tasks* (1904), attacking [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]'s centralism after the 1903 split, Trotsky warned that the party would substitute itself for the working class, the central leadership for the party, and finally a single leader for the leadership: "A barracks regime cannot be the regime for our party just as the factory cannot be its model." Service calls the critique "prophetic in many basic respects" of one-party rule — an irony given Trotsky's own later role (see [[vanguard-party]]).

**Theory and practice of insurrection.** As Chairman of the Petrograd Soviet (September 1917) and of the Military-Revolutionary Committee, Trotsky coordinated the October seizure of power, articulating a tactical doctrine of insurrection under defensive cover: "The attacking side is almost always interested in seeming on the defensive. A revolutionary party is interested in legal coverings." He had chaired the St Petersburg Soviet in 1905 as well, though Service cautions that "only a few decisions were attributable to him personally" there, against later hagiography.

**Sociology of the Soviet bureaucracy.** *The Revolution Betrayed* (1937) analyzed the USSR as a "degenerated workers' state": state ownership kept it a workers' state, but the small proletariat's weakness and Russian backwardness let a "bureaucratic stratum" consolidate Bonapartist power under [[stalin-joseph|Stalin]], removable only by *political* (not social) revolution — "No devil ever yet voluntarily cut off its own claws." He had coined the Soviet "Thermidor" analogy in 1927 and conceded the descriptive usefulness of "totalitarian" for Stalin's USSR while refusing the "new class" conclusions of his dissident followers (see [[russian-question-debate]] and [[bureaucracy]]). Notably, his Left Opposition critique of party bureaucratization (*The New Course*, 1923; the Declaration of the Forty-Six) predates this by over a decade.

**Cultural sociology.** *Problems of Everyday Life* (1923) treated hygiene, drink, swearing, cinema, and women's position in the party as revolutionary problems; *Literature and Revolution* (1923) assessed Soviet writers by revolutionary utility, tolerating "fellow travellers" within limits. Service's verdict — that Trotsky thereby "laid down the philosophical foundations for cultural Stalinism" — is a strong single-author causal claim and is recorded here strictly as Service's position.

**Class-instrumental ethics.** *Their Morality and Ours* (1938) rejected transcendent moral standards: "The welfare of the revolution — that is the supreme law!" James Burnham's 1940 open letter attacked the underlying doctrine of "class truth" as "the road of Plato's Philosopher Kings, of prophets and Popes and Stalins."

**Terrorism and Communism (1920) — Red Terror and labour militarization.** The primary civil-war pamphlet *[[trotsky-terrorism-and-communism-1920|Terrorism and Communism]]* (reply to Kautsky; intro Moscow 29 May 1920, epilogue 17 June 1920) is now ingested in full. In it Trotsky defends [[red-terror]] as revolutionary self-defence and the continuation of armed insurrection: revolution, like war, breaks the foe's will; when resistance is ferocious, repression becomes a system of terror; hostages, executions, and press suppression are expediency measures of class war, not violations of an absolute "sacredness of human life" that would forbid revolution itself. He reclaims Marx's *Civil War in France* against Kautsky's contrast of a humane Paris Commune with Bolshevik severity, and argues that soviet power creates its majority *dynamically* rather than reflecting a pre-given parliamentary balance. Chapter 8 states the classic programme of [[militarization-of-labour]]: compulsory labour service ("He who works not, neither shall he eat"), labour armies, trade unions as production organizers under a single economic plan, piecework and repression for productivity, one-man management — with free-labour superiority treated as a liberal prejudice for the bourgeois→socialist transition. The same book affirms that "the dictatorship of the soviets became possible only by means of the dictatorship of the party" and that Mensheviks and SRs "have disappeared" after suppression (attributed). This 1920 doctrine is the positive programme later targeted in the [[soviet-trade-union-debate]] and the text Service and others use when arguing continuity between Trotsky and later Soviet compulsion — recorded as reception, not wiki-voice verdict. See also [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].

**In Lenin's primary texts (1917–1921, 1922–23).** Further primary documentation now spans the collaboration and the clashes. September 1917: "Trotsky was for the boycott. Bravo, Comrade Trotsky!" (on the Pre-parliament, [[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]); January 1918: the direct-line Brest telegrams (Lenin consulting Stalin, then jointly recalling Trotsky), and Lenin's record that about a quarter of the party leadership followed Trotsky's "neither war nor peace" position against his own separate-peace line. 1920–21: Trotsky's *The Role and Tasks of the Trade Unions* is the antagonist text of the [[soviet-trade-union-debate]] — Lenin charged it with "bureaucratic harassment of the trade unions" and, in "Once Again on the Trade Unions," diagnosed "one-track thinking, compulsiveness, exaggeration and obstinacy" ([[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]], all attributed).

 Two ingested Lenin sources now document the relationship from Lenin's side, unmediated by biography. In April 1917 Lenin named and rejected "Trotskyism" as the slogan "No tsar, but a workers' government" — "This is wrong... the petty bourgeoisie exists, and it cannot be dismissed" — and complained that Trotsky as a Paris editor "never made it clear whether he was for or against Chkheidze" ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964]], attributed). The Testament (Dec 1922) called him "personally perhaps the most capable man in the present C.C." while noting "excessive self-assurance" and over-preoccupation with "the purely administrative side of the work," identified the Stalin–Trotsky relation as the chief split risk, and recorded that the Gosplan legislative-powers idea "was suggested by Comrade Trotsky," which Lenin had come to accept "in substance" ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]], attributed).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Trotsky's Marxism came late and by conversion: as a teenager in the Nikolaev "Rassadnik" circle he was a narodnik-leaning anti-Marxist (following Mikhailovski), argued into Marxism partly by his first wife Alexandra Sokolovskaya and decisively, by his own account, by reading Antonio Labriola, then Plekhanov, then [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s *Capital* in Odessa prison. Formative relations: Lenin (recruiting him to *Iskra* in 1902 as "the Pen"; opponent after 1903, when Trotsky sided with Martov; convergent again from 1917, Service reading the April Theses as Lenin's substantive move toward permanent revolution); Parvus (intellectual mentor of the workers'-government idea); Plekhanov (persistent antagonist, finally broken with over war credits in 1914); [[luxemburg-rosa|Luxemburg]] (occasional ally who privately judged him "a rotten fellow" in the unity disputes). He built no school while in power — Lunacharski's memoir judged him "extremely bad at organizing... practically no wholehearted supporters at all" — and his exile movement, the Fourth International (founded Périgny, 1938, with 21 delegates from 11 countries and an NKVD agent on its secretariat), lost its ablest intellectuals (Burnham, Shachtman, Hook, Serge) in the 1939–40 splits.

### Service *Lenin* biography (2000) cross-check

Distinct from the Trotsky-focused [[service-trotsky-2009]]: Robert Service's *Lenin: A Biography* ([[service-lenin-2000]]) retells Trotsky's arc as it intersects Lenin's career. Interpretive emphases are Service's; dates and office titles are brute chronology.

**1902–1905.** Service reports Trotsky's autumn 1902 arrival in London after Siberian escape — Holford Square meeting with Lenin; friendship; Lenin proposing him as seventh *Iskra* editor (Plekhanov blocked the seat as an extra "Lenin's disciple" vote). The "Pen" nickname for the *Iskra*-era publicist is the conventional label this wiki already carries for that recruitment; Service's Lenin volume grounds the 1902 *Iskra* episode without restating the nickname as a load-bearing coinage. After the 1903 split Service places Trotsky with the Menshevik camp against Lenin's "hards"; in 1905 Service notes Trotsky's return to Russia and prominence as Petersburg Soviet deputy chair (September), via the workers'-government line associated with Helphand–Parvus.

**1917 convergence and Civil War.** Service reports Lenin courting Trotsky after his May 1917 return from North America and overcoming Bolshevik resistance to admitting a former enemy; Trotsky chaired the Petrograd Soviet / MRC side of the October transfer. In early Sovnarkom Trotsky held External Affairs, then — as Service stresses for the war years — People's Commissar for Military Affairs, with the "Trotski Train" for front travel and public plaudits. Service records naked Trotsky–Stalin rivalry that only Lenin could temporarily accommodate, and Lenin's backing of Trotsky (Imperial officers + commissars + discipline) against the Military Opposition inspired behind the scenes by Stalin.

**Brest-Litovsk.** Service's Brest narrative: Trotsky returned 7 Jan 1918 with the Central Powers' ultimatum; Lenin pressed immediate peace; Trotsky advanced "neither war nor peace." The Bolshevik fraction at the Third Congress of Soviets preferred Trotsky's line at first; after the German offensive Lenin forced the CC to sign (Trotsky eventually supporting the switch rather than war without a united party). Service treats this as one of Lenin's fiercest internal struggles after April and October.

**Trade-union debate and Polish war.** Winter 1920–21: Service reports Trotsky's programme of labour militarisation — ban strikes, unions as state organisations, Civil War commissariat methods carried into reconstruction — against Lenin's defence of unions under bureaucratic distortion; Lenin group victory by February 1921 (Bukharin buffer; Workers' Opposition condemning all three). On the 1920 Polish war Service has Trotsky not for invading Poland; coordination problems with Stalin on the southern prong; and at the Ninth Party Conference Trotsky denouncing Stalin for misleading the CC on victory prospects, with Lenin siding with Trotsky while Stalin demanded a reply.

**Late 1922 ally; Testament; New Course.** Despite earlier friction (Service's Lenin labels: Brest poseur, trade-union recklessness, vanity), Service has Lenin turn to Trotsky as ally on the foreign-trade monopoly (CC reaffirmation 18 Dec 1922; Lenin's note that the position was seized "without firing a shot") and on the Georgian case against Stalin. The Testament ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]]) calls Trotsky personally perhaps the most capable man on the CC while charging excessive self-confidence and preoccupation with pure administration (Service's paraphrase). Autumn 1923: Trotsky's *New Course* / Left Opposition — Service reports Stalin, Kamenev, Zinoviev, and Bukharin crushing it at the Thirteenth Party Conference (January 1924). At Lenin's funeral Trotsky was in Sukhumi; Service states Stalin "reassured" him he need not return — he was absent from the coffin-bearing and Red Square vault rites of 27 Jan 1924.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Service's biography is itself the leading hostile reappraisal: its stated thesis is that Trotsky and Stalin "shared more than they disagreed about," that Trotsky "revelled in terror" (Red Terror orders at Kazan 1918, the Panteleev executions, Kronstadt's suppression — of which Service calls him "the architect"; [[avrich-kronstadt-1921|Avrich 1970]] documents Trotsky's 5 March ultimatum and War Commissar role while recording "tragic necessity" framing ([[kronstadt-debate]])), and that he "did not go down to defeat at the hands of 'the bureaucracy': he lost to a man and a clique with a superior understanding of Soviet public life." *Terrorism and Communism* (1920) is the programmatic self-justification that makes Service's "revelled in terror" charge textually load-bearing: Trotsky there *argues for* intimidation, hostages, and labour compulsion as socialist necessity (see [[red-terror]], [[militarization-of-labour]]). Service documents self-serving memoir revision by draft-comparison (understated family wealth, omitted requests and overtures, *Works* articles silently rewritten — "Thief-taker Trotsky was also something of a felon in matters historical"). His explanation of the succession defeat is multi-factor: declined deputy-chairmanships, ill health at critical junctures, no factional instinct, and Stalin's superior cultivation of Lenin. Contemporaries supplied the character material: Ziv (instrumental attitude to comrades, "the kind that a peasant has for his horse"), Lunacharski ("colossal arrogance"), Eastman and Rosmer ("He has no humanity"). Against this, Service concedes he was "unmatched as propagandist," a brilliant organizer, orator, and stylist. On Jewishness: Trotsky identified as internationalist, opposed the Bund and Zionism as "despicable nationalism," repeatedly declined top posts fearing antisemitic backlash, and analyzed antisemitism as "cowardice about Jewry" (1919); only after Hitler's rise did he concede Jews needed a distinct programmatic response. Antisemitic "Judeo-Bolshevism" propaganda made him its central figure.

## Historiography

Three biographical traditions contend: Trotsky's own *My Life* (1930), "selective, evasive and self-aggrandizing" per Service; Isaac Deutscher's sympathetic trilogy (1954–63), which revived Trotsky's Western reputation and — diverging from its subject — held peaceful evolution of the USSR possible (Service calls Deutscher a worshipper "at Trotsky's shrine," Pierre Broué "an idolater"); and Service's 2009 revisionist demolition, itself contested (see `reliability_notes` on [[service-trotsky-2009]]). Reputation waves: the 1968 New Left revival (largely unread, per Service), Gorbachev-era judicial rehabilitation (1936 death sentence annulled), post-1991 fade. Nearly his whole family died by Stalinist repression.

## Carr 1979 — war, opposition, defeat

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*) narrates Trotsky's arc 1917–27 (attributed; pair with [[service-trotsky-2009]]):

- **1917–21:** Joined Bolsheviks after return; leading role in October via revolutionary military committee; first Foreign Affairs commissar ("revolutionary proclamations… shut up shop"); Brest "no peace, no war" then vote with Lenin; War Commissar — military specialists, Red Army to ~5 million; labour militarization and trade-union "shake-up" (Lenin parted company, 1920–21).
- **1923–24:** Scissors-crisis critique (diagram at 12th congress; 8 Oct letter; platform of the 46); defeated as opposition crystallized; *Lessons of October*; removed as War Commissar; Eastman disavowal episode.
- **United opposition (1926–27):** With Zinoviev–Kamenev; declaration of the 83; China and socialism-in-one-country critiques; expulsions; Alma-Ata exile.
- **1928:** Critique of Comintern draft programme attacking [[socialism-in-one-country]] as root of Comintern disasters (Carr).
- **Carr's structural note:** Policies on industrialization tempos partly adopted after the [[left-opposition]]'s political destruction — victory of the machine over the opposition, not simple rejection of all its economics.
