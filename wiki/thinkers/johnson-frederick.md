# Frederick Johnson

American archaeologist/anthropologist who chaired the **Committee on Carbon 14** of the American Anthropological Association and the Geological Society of America — selecting samples and priorities for early Chicago radiocarbon measurements (attributed, Libby 1952 preface) — and who wrote Chapter VII of *[[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Radiocarbon Dating]]*, "Reflections upon the Significance of Radiocarbon Dates" (revised and expanded for the second edition). He is the principal archaeological voice inside Libby's founding monograph.

## Overview

Johnson translates laboratory ages into mid-1950s archaeological and geological significance: why absolute dating matters against relative stratigraphic and stylistic chronologies; how to evaluate dates without letting process-rate opinions casually veto them; and what the early C-series lists implied for the Americas, the Near East, and late-glacial geology. The chapter is a **period document** (pre-calibration); treat its calendar conversions and culture-historical shocks as positions of the 1950s, not as current chronologies.

## Contributions

**Committee interface.** As chair with Donald Collier, Richard Foster Flint, and Froelich Rainey, Johnson structured which materials entered the early dating program (attributed, Libby preface).

**Absolute vs relative time.** He argued that geological, pollen, and archaeological time scales are almost always stratigraphic relative sequences; numbers of years attached to levels are often estimates with large implied error, and correlating one regional sequence with another is especially fragile (attributed, Johnson ch. VII). Radiocarbon offered world-wide, single-method ages directly comparable across fields — if critically evaluated.

**Error diagnosis.** Johnson held that the dominant archaeological failures were **selection and collection**, not the physics of counting: modern rootlets and fungal carbon in thin layers (his Denbigh Flint Complex example: C-792/C-793 charcoal from a folded, root-penetrated layer — dates rejected as context errors while geological estimates favored ~8500+ years); long-stored museum lots; samples that do not represent the described event; shell often too early. He warned against repudiating whole date series merely because they conflict with hypotheses about ice-sheet speed or rates of cultural change (attributed, Johnson ch. VII).

**American archaeological implications (via Willey and own synthesis — attributed positions, 1950s).** Using Gordon R. Willey's Middle/South American critique as a model, Johnson discussed: early entry to Latin America not long after North America (Magellan C-485 vs Lubbock Folsom C-558 order of magnitude); early maize in simple preceramic contexts; Formative pushed ~1500 years earlier than recent estimates, with Classic spans as the "greatest surprise"; Tikal lintel radiocarbon favoring Spinden over Goodman-Thompson in the contemporary debate; Southwest Cochise acceptance and non-Folsom contemporaries of Folsom-Yuma; Eastern sequences (Adena/Hopewell, Old Copper). **Glacial:** Two Creeks Forest Bed as among the most secure multi-lab pins (~11,400 years average in his summary), anchoring a ~10,000-year Mankato maximum discussion; European Allerød correlated with Two Creeks as Old/New World synchrony (attributed, Johnson).

**Near East.** He noted Jarmo as early village economy and Nippur results favoring younger Mesopotamian historical options (as discussed from Libby's catalogue).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Laboratory counterpart: **[[libby-willard-f]]**. Committee colleagues: Collier, Flint, Rainey. Archaeological interlocutors in the chapter include **Gordon R. Willey** (Middle/South America evaluation model) and, via the date list, Braidwood, Antevs, and many regional excavators. Downstream mid-century users of the same early lists include [[clark-grahame]] and [[childe-v-gordon]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Johnson's optimism about C-14 as a check on "absurd" process-rate criticism is a 1950s position; calibration later showed that some archaeological resistance to raw dates was partly about the production assumption Libby had treated as roughly constant. His Spinden-favoring reading of Tikal and many American calendar conversions are superseded as chronology but remain important as the first sustained archaeological reception of the Chicago list. Sample-context lessons (Denbigh-type root contamination) remain standard.
