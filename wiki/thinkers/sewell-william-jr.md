# William H. Sewell Jr. (b. 1940)

American historian and historical sociologist, a leading theorist of the relation between **history** and **social science** and of **social transformation**. Not to be confused with his father, **William Hamilton Sewell Sr.** (1909–2001), the quantitative sociologist who built the University of Wisconsin department and pioneered status-attainment research (the Wisconsin model) — a different person and a different intellectual project. This page summarizes Sewell Jr. as anchored by *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* (2005); his most cited theoretical statement is the 1992 essay "A Theory of Structure," reprinted there.

## Overview

Sewell (attributed) argues that history and the social sciences need each other: historians hold a subtle *implicit* theory of social temporality — that time is fateful, contingent, complex, eventful, and causally heterogeneous — while social scientists possess explicit "structural thinking" but impoverished, physics-envying conceptions of time. His program, an "[[eventful-sociology|eventful sociology]]," holds that social structures are built and transformed through **[[historical-event|events]]** and that social causality is **temporally heterogeneous** (see [[eventful-temporality]]). His signature theoretical intervention reworks structure as the interplay of **transposable [[transposable-schemas|schemas and resources]]**, making both agency and change internal to structure rather than exceptions to it. In cultural theory he argues for culture as a dialectic of system and practice possessing only a "[[thin-coherence-of-culture|thin coherence]]"; in his late "interpretivist manifesto" he refigures "the social" as an evolving web of **[[semiotic-practice|semiotic practices]]** materialized in a **[[built-environment|built environment]]**.

## Contributions

- **[[transposable-schemas|A theory of structure]]** — structure as mutually sustaining schemas (virtual) and resources (actual); five axioms (multiplicity of structures, transposability of schemas, unpredictability of resource accumulation, polysemy of resources, intersection of structures) that make structures inherently transformable. A direct rework of [[structuration-theory|Giddens]] and [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]].
- **[[agency|Agency]]** — defined as the capacity to transpose and extend schemas to new contexts and to remobilize resources under new schemas; universal but unevenly distributed and culturally formed.
- **[[historical-event|A theory of the historical event]]** — events as ramified sequences of occurrences, recognized as notable, that durably transform structures; demonstrated on the taking of the Bastille (1789) and the invention of the modern concept of "revolution."
- **[[eventful-temporality|Three temporalities]]** — the distinction of teleological, experimental, and eventful conceptions of social time, and the critique of the first two as fallacious.
- **[[thin-coherence-of-culture|The concept(s) of culture]]** — culture as a dialectic of system and practice; "thin coherence"; coherence as an achievement of power.
- **[[semiotic-practice|Refiguring the social]]** — a social ontology joining semiotic practices and the built environment; a reconciliation of interpretive and quantitative method ("hermeneutical quantification").

## Intellectual Genealogy

Sewell (attributed, *Logics of History* ch. 2) narrates his own trajectory as "confessions of a former quantitative historian." He entered graduate school at Berkeley in 1962, "raised a positivist" and trained in quantitative social history; his dissertation (1971) and *Structure and Mobility* (1985) were quantitative studies of nineteenth-century Marseille workers. He describes a "conversion" to cultural anthropology beginning 1972–74 at the University of Chicago under the influence of the historical anthropologist Bernard Cohn and the historian Ronald Inden (a student of Cohn, influenced by David Schneider), deepened in 1975–76 at the Institute for Advanced Study in a symbolic-anthropology seminar led by [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] (alongside Victor Turner, the Rosaldos, Orlando Patterson, and historians Robert Darnton, Thomas Kuhn, and William Reddy). He names E. P. Thompson, [[tilly-charles|Charles Tilly]], and Stephan Thernstrom as the works most formative for his dissertation, and Raymond Williams's "structures of feeling" as central to his interpretive method. His transitional book *Work and Revolution in France* (1980) drew theoretically on anthropologists (Geertz, Schneider, Turner, Gluckman, [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]) and Marx-inflected sources. Appointments: Chicago (History, 1968–75), IAS Princeton (1975–80), Arizona and Michigan (joint sociology–history, 1980s, directing Michigan's Center for the Study of Social Transformations), and Chicago (Political Science and History, from 1990). Formal doctoral teacher–student relations are not documented in the ingested source; `trained_under`/`trained` left as `[[unknown]]`.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(Attributed.)* Sewell's "A Theory of Structure" (1992) became one of the most cited theoretical statements in historical sociology and cultural sociology, widely used and widely debated as the standard rework of structuration/practice theory toward change. His most contestable historical thesis — that the shift from social to cultural history was causally entangled with the macroeconomic transition from Fordism to post-Fordism (the "political unconscious" of the cultural turn) — is explicitly offered as his own interpretation, not settled fact. His late-2000s reception centers on whether "eventful" analysis scales to macro-historical processes (his own postscript defends it via Michael Mann) and on the adequacy of his synthesis of the semiotic and the material. Full reception awaits ingestion of secondary literature.

## Historiography

*Logics of History* (2005) is a retrospective collection gathering essays written across roughly fifteen years, framed by a new theoretical introduction; it is the vault's anchor for Sewell. His empirical base is the social and labor history of nineteenth-century France (Marseille, Lyon, the Revolution); his theoretical interlocutors are chiefly [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]], [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]. A fuller treatment would ingest *Work and Revolution in France* (1980) and the AJS and *Representations* articles reprinted in *Logics of History*.
