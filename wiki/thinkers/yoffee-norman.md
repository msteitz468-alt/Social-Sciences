# Norman Yoffee

## Overview

Norman Yoffee is a Mesopotamianist archaeologist and anthropologist (Near Eastern Studies and Anthropology, University of Michigan; previously University of Arizona). His best-known single-author synthesis, *[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]* (2005), challenges neo-evolutionist models of the earliest cities and states. He co-edited the landmark collapse volume with George L. Cowgill (1988) and *Archaeological Theory: Who Sets the Agenda?* with Andrew Sherratt (1993); he has edited the *Journal of the Economic and Social History of the Orient* and the Cambridge World Archaeology series.

## Contributions

- **Critique of neo-evolutionist “myths”** about archaic states (large territorial despots; ethnographic-stage modeling; checklist correlate approaches) — see [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]]. **Yoffee’s Rule:** if you can argue whether a society is a state or isn’t, it isn’t.
- **Power and cities:** earliest states as recombinations of differentiated, stratified groups under new ideologies of centralization; **dimensions of power** (economic, social, political); cities as primary arenas; Mesopotamian [[city-state|city-states]] within a civilizational interaction sphere (state ≠ civilization).
- **[[evolution-of-simplicity|Evolution of simplicity]]:** states attempt to make society “legible” (after Scott) and to simplify competing authorities (law codes, bronze-ritual control, writing) — often intensifying complexity for subjects; Mesopotamian codes as political display, not practical monopolies of justice.
- **Identity and [[agency]]:** Old Babylonian *nadītu* and *kezertu* cases; everyday life remote from continuous state intervention without denying repressive rule.
- **[[societal-collapse|Collapse]] and [[social-memory]]:** collapse as restructuring of institutions and identity, not only system failure; regeneration of successor states; civilization can outlast particular states.
- **Non-state trajectories:** Chaco “rituality,” Cahokia “big bangs,” Australian cases — full evolutionary histories, not failed rungs on a state ladder.
- **Method:** BLT/MLT/HLT stack; ethnographic analogy (method page) appropriate mainly at basic level; [[comparative-method|comparison]] of developmental sequences; reject “contemporary ancestors.”

## Intellectual Genealogy

Mesopotamian text-and-archaeology training; long collaboration and conversation with John Baines, Piotr Michalowski, Peter Machinist, Carla Sinopoli, Stephen Lekson, and others (acknowledged in *Myths*). Engages critically with [[service-elman-r|Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]], [[white-leslie-a|White]], [[steward-julian-h|Steward]], [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], and processual operationalizations of neo-evolutionism; in dialogue with [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], and collapse literature (Joseph Tainter 1988 / Cowgill–Yoffee 1988 — see [[societal-collapse]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Myths* is itself a critique of mid-century archaeological theory; reception and counter-critiques to be filled as secondary literature is ingested. Comparative case detail is secondary and date-stamped to ~2003.

**Pauketat's critique (2007, attributed — [[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007]]).** Pauketat quotes Yoffee's Rule verbatim ("if you can argue whether a society is a state or isn't, then it isn't"; Yoffee 1993:69, 2005:41) and rejects it as "an arbitrary yardstick": the "you" is scholarly consensus, which reversed itself on [[cahokia]] twice (a state in the 1960s–70s "Ramey state" literature, not one after the chiefdom model took over). Lekson's counter-corollary (pers. comm. 2006): "If you can argue about whether a case is or is not a state, then it's really interesting." Pauketat also critiques Yoffee's **"rituality"** concept (proposed for Chaco/the Southeast; Yoffee coined "Chaco's 'little bangs'" at a 2003 SAR conference) as implying producer autonomy and suffering "chronic theoretical underdevelopment" — "all rituals are inherently political" — and charges Yoffee with not following his own cities-as-"generative" insight through (retreating to a peer-polity-style evolutionary frame to keep Cahokia and Chaco out of the city club). Recorded per Pauketat: Yoffee (pers. comm. 2005) "stands ready to accept that Cahokia was a city" while doubting it centered an archaic state, endorsing Monica Smith's uncoupling of cities from states. Pauketat conversely applies **Yoffee, Fish & Milner's (1999) four early-state criteria** (disembedding from kinship at new capitals; ruralization as urbanization's twin; interplay of competing power sources; ideological symbol manipulation) to argue Cahokia qualifies — see [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]].

## Historiography

Primary self-account in *Myths* Acknowledgments and Introduction: quarter-century of essays reworked into a connected narrative; School of American Research residencies; teaching at Arizona (1972–93) and Michigan (from 1993).

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; Yoffee as guide and modularity source, not a full restudy of *Myths*.)*

- **Acknowledged Mesopotamian guide.** Scott lists Yoffee among his top alluvium specialists (with Jennifer Pournelle, [[wengrow-david|David Wengrow]], Seth Richardson)—reception of Yoffee’s Mesopotamian/state-critical program into a political-anthropological deep history of the earliest states.
- **Fragility consensus.** Scott reports near-unanimous scholarly consensus that first archaic states were fragile and short-lived; among cited formulations is Yoffee (with others) that “concentrations of power in early civilizations were typically fragile and short-lived.”
- **Modularity / “nearly decomposable” units.** Following Yoffee and Cowgill’s use of Herbert Simon’s modularity insight (as Scott presents it), Scott treats much archaeological “[[societal-collapse|collapse]]” as **disassembly** of larger fragile polities into smaller, more stable, self-sufficient components—the apical center’s disappearance need not mean disorder or trauma for those units. Affinity with Yoffee’s collapse-as-restructuring and civilization-outlasting-states themes already on this page; Scott radicalizes the normative payoff (“normalize” collapse; welfare of subjects ≠ power of the court).
