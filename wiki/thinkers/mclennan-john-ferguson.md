## Overview

John Ferguson McLennan was a Scottish lawyer and self-taught evolutionary anthropologist whose *Primitive Marriage* (1865) was, according to George W. Stocking, Jr., the first British assertion of the evolutionary priority of "matriarchal" (matrilineal) marriage over patriarchal forms (Stocking 1987). McLennan practiced law and reform-minded legal writing in Edinburgh before turning to social theory; he died of malaria in 1881 after years of tubercular illness, having sacrificed much of his law practice to write *Primitive Marriage* (per Tylor's later recollection, as reported by Stocking 1987).

## Contributions

McLennan shifted, between 1863 and 1865, from a diffusionist/ethnological treatment of Indian hill-tribe marriage customs to a fully evolutionary theory of the origin of marriage (Stocking 1987). He coined the term "[[unknown|exogamy]]" for the rule prohibiting marriage within one's own tribe — a phenomenon Robert Latham had already documented descriptively among groups such as the Magar of Nepal, on whose data McLennan drew heavily (Stocking 1987). McLennan explained exogamy's origin as a consequence of female infanticide: tribes chronically short of women under conditions of perpetual primitive hostility resorted to capturing wives from other groups, and he held that "primitive marriage" was in effect "little more than 'rape'" (Stocking 1987, quoting McLennan). He posited an original condition of human sexual [[primitive-promiscuity]], succeeded by polyandry — kinship being traced through the female line because paternity could not be determined — and only later by patrilineal monogamy (Stocking 1987). He treated the levirate (marriage to a deceased brother's widow) as evidence of a prior stage of polyandry, a reading Tylor, despite extensive discussion with McLennan, could not accept (Stocking 1987).

McLennan coined the anthropological concept of "[[totemism]]" in 1869, defining it — following De Brosses's and Comte's terminology of "fetichism" — as fetishism combined with specific sociological features, notably exogamy and matrilineal descent, tribes in the "totem stage" believing themselves descended from an animal or plant "symbol and emblem," religiously regarded or taboo (Stocking 1987, quoting McLennan). He offered no systematic theory of totemism's origins; the concept was later elaborated by William Robertson Smith and James Frazer (whom Robertson Smith commissioned to write the *Encyclopedia Britannica* totemism article), and eventually critiqued by Boasians as ethnographically incoherent (Stocking 1987).

McLennan proposed a comparative-method heuristic — that within any "progressive community" different social sections advance unevenly, so that contemporary "lower" sections (he cited London's poor, rural Cornwall and Devon, and the Scottish Highlands) preserve earlier evolutionary phases usable for reconstructing unrecorded history (Stocking 1987, quoting McLennan). In a posthumously published essay he argued that female infanticide, undertaken to relieve population pressure on food, was "the most important step that was ever taken in the history of mankind" — an amoral, utilitarian account of civilization originating in an act of crime that Stocking notes carries "resonance of biblical [[degenerationism]]" and "premonition of Freud" (Stocking 1987, quoting McLennan). *Primitive Marriage* is judged by Stocking to be one of the clearest, most elaborate, and least apologetic expositions of the sociocultural-evolutionist [[comparative-method]] — methodologically distinct from, and premised on assumptions rejected by, [[maine-henry-sumner]]'s historical-comparative method, which compared genetically related, documented phenomena rather than globally dispersed, undocumented ones (Stocking 1987).

## Intellectual Genealogy

McLennan's marriage-evolution theory (promiscuity → polyandry → patriliny/monogamy, with exogamy explained via female infanticide) was independently paralleled by the Swiss jurist Johann [[bachofen-johann-jakob]], whose *Mutterrecht* (1861) McLennan did not read until 1866 and which was neither a response to nor a stimulus for his own work (Stocking 1987). His theory was disputed within his own generation: [[darwin-charles]] was skeptical, reasoning from the sexual jealousy observed in male quadrupeds and apes that promiscuity and polyandry were more plausibly later developments than an original condition, though McLennan defended his conclusions by insisting animal-behavior data could not be directly transposed to human inquiry (Stocking 1987). [[lubbock-john]] treated polyandry as merely an "exceptional phenomenon" rather than a necessary evolutionary stage, and Herbert [[spencer-herbert]] doubted the theory "taken as a whole," preferring an account of endogamy/exogamy as coexisting adaptations to tribal hostility; Spencer and McLennan also clashed publicly over Spencer's qualification to judge polyandry's universality (Stocking 1987). McLennan's most sustained rivalry was with [[maine-henry-sumner]], with whom he disputed the priority of patriarchal versus matrilineal kinship as the original human social form — a dispute recorded on [[morgan-mclennan-debate]] and [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]] — and his later career became bogged down, in Stocking's assessment, in a priority dispute with Maine over patriarchy, tested against ethnographic cases such as William Robertson Smith's *Kinship and Marriage in Early Arabia* (Stocking 1987). McLennan also disputed the priority of ideas over words in the origin of language with Friedrich [[muller-friedrich-max]], siding with the empiricist critics of Müller's Kantian, language-constitutive epistemology alongside [[tylor-edward-burnett]], Lubbock, and Spencer (Stocking 1987). His career involved hard-fought upward social mobility; in 1870 he asked Lubbock for help securing a Liberal party post from Gladstone, contrasting his own self-made efforts with a rival's inherited wealth, and an earlier attempt (with Maine's support) to secure him a teaching post at Cambridge had failed (Stocking 1987). In 1867 he attempted to organize a cooperative research venture with Lubbock, Thomas Henry [[huxley-thomas-henry]], and the Sanskritist S. T. Aufrecht to produce a "tentative scheme" charting stages of social and technological progress (Stocking 1987).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

McLennan's theories of universal evolutionary stages — [[primitive-promiscuity]], the priority of matrilineal kinship (the [[myth-of-matriarchy]]), "female infanticide," "marriage by capture," and polyandry-as-stage — were called into question almost as soon as they were formulated and were rejected by Edward Westermarck (1891); Stocking judges them, in retrospect, to have been largely "a fantasy of the Victorian male anthropological imagination" (Stocking 1987, attributed to Stocking's own assessment). Tylor's 1885 review of the Maine–McLennan debate initially concluded that "converging research" favored the early general prevalence of female-line kinship, but by 1896 Tylor himself acknowledged a growing "reaction" against the theory of "primitive matrimonial anarchy," and late-Victorian ethnographers (per correspondence with Lorimer Fison) could not locate any actual society exemplifying primitive promiscuity as McLennan's evidentiary footnote had implied (Stocking 1987). Stocking explicitly rejects a simple feminist-historiographical reading of Victorian marriage theory as a conscious ideological defense of patriarchy, noting the independence of Bachofen's work from the Maine–McLennan dispute and Maine's own comparatively progressive legal writing on women's status — a position recorded as a live historiographical dispute (Stocking 1987).

Despite being judged "almost entirely wrong" by modern lights, Stocking records that McLennan's anthropological reputation followed a trajectory opposite to Maine's: institutionally marginal in his lifetime, he nonetheless remained significant throughout the pre-functionalist period as a controversialist, though his kinship analysis lost out to Lewis Henry [[morgan-lewis-henry]]'s "classificatory system" approach, which — transmitted via Fison, Howitt, and [[rivers-w-h-r]] — became orthodoxy for British social anthropology in its Radcliffe-Brownian phase (Stocking 1987). He was nonetheless reclaimed in the Classics of Anthropology series as arguably more deserving than Tylor of the title "father of anthropology," on the grounds that the continuing topics of anthropological concern — marriage forms, incest, exogamy, and totemism — originated with him (Stocking 1987).

## Historiography

Stocking treats McLennan as a key figure of the originating 1860s generation of British sociocultural evolutionists (alongside Tylor, Lubbock, and Spencer), whose second generation (Andrew Lang, influenced by Tylor, and William Robertson Smith, influenced by McLennan) carried elements of his program forward from around 1880 (Stocking 1987).
