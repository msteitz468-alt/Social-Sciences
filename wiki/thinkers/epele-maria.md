# María E. Epele

## Overview

María E. Epele is an anthropologist (Visiting Scholar, Department of Anthropology, UC Berkeley; Postdoctoral Researcher, CONICET, Argentina, as of the *Commodifying Bodies* bio). Her chapter in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* — “Excess, Scarcity and Desire among Drug-Using Sex Workers” — examines the intersection of women’s drug addiction with street sex work in San Francisco’s **Mission District**, arguing that **scarcity**, not excess, organizes the economy of desire and need, and that **johns and male partners emerge as structural winners** in a gendered moral economy. Funding: CONICET postdoctoral grant; visiting scholar at UC Berkeley Anthropology and Center for Latin American Studies; thanks [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]], [[cohen-lawrence|Lawrence Cohen]], and Philippe Bourgois.

## Contributions

### Dual street images rejected as full explanations *(attributed — Epele)*

Street narratives oscillate between **sexual slavery** (excess, lack of control, victimization by men) and **prostitution as liberation** (re-appropriation of the body, breaking gender stereotypes). Epele argues both fail to explain complex everyday reality. Women use stereotypical slavery stories to differentiate themselves from “slave women” and to mark violence levels, while disguising real conditions of subjection — **scarcity, not excess/unconsciousness**, predominates.

### Artificial scarcity *(attributed — Epele)*

Core mechanism: women suffer a gendered **“artificial scarcity”** produced by the commodification of women in street sex work; men’s direct or indirect intervention in this scarcity (control of income with partners; continual progressive reduction of street sex-work prices; control of access to drugs as means of minimum well-being) is central to the dominant logic that subjects women. Subjection is dynamic, open to challenge — not frozen gender roles.

### Methods and sample *(descriptive — Epele)*

- Setting: Mission District concentration of drug dealing, consumption, sex work, and other illegal activities; research over **one year**.
- Recruitment: Needle Exchange Program (NEP) site, then snowball of network members who did not regularly use NEP; objective: risk conditions and care practices related to **HIV** in everyday life of female Chicana/Latina injector drug users.
- **In-depth interviews with 35 participants — 25 women and 10 men**; 88.5% taped and transcribed; most Spanish; half re-interviewed two or three times; five women’s life stories; ethnographic field notes on streets, hotels, hangouts.
- Women: age 22–54, mean 34; all used heroin IV or IM; ~52% injected speedball (heroin + cocaine); 84% also used other drugs (crack, cocaine, speed, marijuana).
- Hotels: mixed threat/refuge; small room ~$40–$50/day — “luxury refuge” unaffordable daily.

### Objectification → fragmentation → re-appropriation *(attributed — Epele)*

Epele focuses on commodification of female sex workers via three processes:

1. **Objectification** — most started drugs before prostitution; chain of losses leads women to realize (or be forced to realize) that their bodies were the only available form of “capital” for survival; body becomes property to trade through extreme objectification; estrangement reinforced by craving cycles and injection-related damage; body becomes alienated object and opponent. Differs from “real ownership through embodied sexual citizenship” (Scheper-Hughes 1994 cited).
2. **Fragmentation** — oral sex is the dominant practice (male long-term users’ erection difficulties; cheaper/faster; preventive control; resistance to price devaluation when injection marks make vaginal/anal performance risk “used and spoiled” recognition); progressive fragmentation into “good”/“bad” parts for men’s sight/touch, damaged vs preserved zones (some women save legs from injection for street attractiveness).
3. **Re-appropriation** — solo sex workers objectify bodies as owners of profit-objects, but re-appropriation comes “too late” — already trapped between progressive body objectification and objectification of women by the male-centered street economy.

Overlapping condensation of use-value and exchange-value on female bodies means commodification carries progressive bodily deterioration as hidden aspect of the same process.

### Johns win *(attributed — Epele)*

- “Having a man” for protection is double-sided (protection from other men + new threat); women provide most resources and pay for protection with drugs/money; “sharing drugs” means unequal distribution and secondary position in consumption sequence.
- Partners/pimps achieve subjection by controlling access to drugs — transforming women into “male-dependent subjects”; men’s power is not mainly ownership of bodies as whole objects but **intervention in the economy of female desires and needs**, interrupting the independent drug-consumption circuit of minimum well-being.
- Male re-appropriation works through repetition inside and outside the network; women understand the mechanism but can only simulate expected roles with men while remaining critical/ironic among women.
- Solo workers may present as liberated and keep profits, yet remain vulnerable to price reductions, non-payment, and lack of defenders — defying street rules has double-edged costs.
- When drug use or bodily damage is discovered, men often respond with violence or refusal to pay; inherent to prostitution is fantasy that women’s subjection produces a “plus” of female pleasure — when exchange nature is exposed, imaginary gender relation crumbles (Marxian fetishism inverted for sex work).
- **Structural claim:** in this androcentric moral economy, **johns (and male partners) emerge as the winners** — Scheper-Hughes’s volume introduction previews the same thesis from Epele’s chapter.

### Desire/need theory scaffolding *(attributed — Epele)*

Reads slavery stories against Hegel/Kojève (desire as struggle for recognition) and early Freud (experience of satisfaction); both construct desire as progressively de-naturalized. Via Marx and late-capitalist consumption theory (Friedman, Lowe): street drug addiction breaks flexibility of gratification — fixed satisfying object intensifies progressive embodiment of desires. Mainstream society projects excess/lack of control onto “different others” (Crawford) — drug consumers and sexual/ethnic minorities — while hotel sexual-slavery stories hide marginal everyday scarcity.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*(To expand.)* Epele’s intervention is against both abolitionist-slavery and liberationist self-presentation as total explanations; she retains both as fragmentary symbolic strategies that “domesticate” multidimensional daily violence. Female-to-female exploitation (two fieldwork cases of older women subjecting younger women under guise of “helping”) shows scarcity mechanism is not exclusively male-operated, though shorter-lived without imaginary gender cover.
