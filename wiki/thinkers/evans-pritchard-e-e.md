# E. E. Evans-Pritchard

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/evans-pritchard-e-e|Thinkers Hub — Evans-Pritchard]].

British social anthropologist (1902–1973). *[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937|Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande]]* (1937; fieldwork late 1920s) is the classic ethnography of a coherent African belief system and a founding text for the anthropology of rationality, witchcraft studies, and the analysis of how misfortune is socially explained. *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|The Nuer]]* (1940; fieldwork 1930–36) founded the anthropology of stateless ([[ordered-anarchy|ordered-anarchy]]) politics and the [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage model]]. *[[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-1951|Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer]]* (1951) supplies the domestic system ([[ghost-marriage]], bridewealth, family forms). The ASKAM (1950) local-community kinship chapter previews that mesh. This page is densest on the Azande phase; later career (Sanusi; the anthropology-as-history turn) remains thin.

## Overview

Evans-Pritchard trained under [[seligman-c-g|C. G. Seligman]] (named teacher; practical field counsel in App. IV) and conducted government-sponsored ethnographic survey work for the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan among the [[azande|Azande]] (first stay **about twenty months**; study material from c. 1927). He distinguished Zande **witches** (*mangu*) from **sorcerers**, reconstructed the interlocking use of **oracles** and **magic**, and showed (attributed) that witchcraft explains *why* misfortune strikes particular persons without denying empirical cause-and-effect ([[second-spear]]). He reported (App. IV) he had no prior interest in witchcraft — the Azande guided the research agenda — and that he could not have written the book as he did without reading Lévy-Bruhl.

## Contributions

- **Comparative political anthropology:** Co-editor with [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] of *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940); Group A/B typology; Nuer chapter as extreme Group B type — [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].




- **Witchcraft ethnography:** organic *mangu*; social distribution of accusation; unconscious agency; death and vengeance — [[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study]] · [[hubs/studies/evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study]].
- **Secondary causation:** [[second-spear|*umbaga* / second-spear]] and granary example.
- **Oracles:** [[poison-oracle|*benge*]] as daily decision technology; lesser oracles; secondary elaborations of failure.
- **Magic/medicines:** classification, witch-doctors, Mani association, leechcraft, vengeance-magic.
- **Method:** theory before field; language mastery; participant-observation as partial pretence / double marginal man; situational analysis; dual methods for public vs esoteric knowledge; preference for intimate informants over questionnaires — feed [[participant-observation]].
- **Terminology:** English words made to stand for Zande notions (*mangu*, *soroka*, *ngua*), not free-standing ideal types (App. I).
- **Structural amnesia:** *The Nuer* (1940) introduced the observation that Nuer genealogical memory holds a roughly constant 9–11 generations over time, dropping middle generations — selective public memory patterned by social structure. Extended comparatively by his students (Bohannan 1952; Barnes 1954); [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] (1986) paired it with [[merton-robert-k|Merton's]] forgotten-precursors work as convergent evidence that institutions do the remembering and forgetting — see [[collective-memory]].
- **Political anthropology (Nuer, 1940):** [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary opposition]] (fission/fusion), [[ordered-anarchy]], [[structural-time|structural time and distance]], the [[feud]] and [[leopard-skin-chief]] as the mechanism of stateless order, and a rigorous definition of social structure — while declining a Radcliffe-Brownian functionalism for his own results ("structural consistency... between abstractions"). See [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study]].
- **Nuer local-community kinship (ASKAM, 1950):** supplement to *The Nuer*’s political abstraction — village/camp as community of kin built around (not coextensive with) an agnatic lineage core; lineage vs kinship-category distinction (*buth* / *mar*); “whoever is kith must also be kin.” Full section below; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].
- **Nuer marriage and domestic kinship (1951):** *[[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-1951|Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer]]* — bridewealth, [[ghost-marriage]], true [[levirate]], family-form typology, kinship categories; study [[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]] · hub [[hubs/studies/evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Colonial employment and survey mandate shaped access and the vengeance system he observed under British rule.
- Ethnographic present of 1937 describes a late-1920s world already under colonial transformation (Gillies 1976).
- Gender access limited women's voices.
- Philosophical afterlife: type-case for the [[rationality-debate]] (Winch and successors — reception beyond this primary text).
- The Nuer's segmentary model was later contested as native ideology / idealization and challenged by ecological accounts of Nuer expansion — [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]].
- Later oeuvre (Sanusi; the anthropology-as-history turn) not yet ingested. *Nuer Religion* (1956) is now ingested — see below.

## Kinship and the local community among the Nuer (ASKAM, 1950)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, Evans-Pritchard contributed “Kinship and the Local Community among the Nuer”—explicitly a **supplement** to *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]* (1940), not a marriage essay (he deferred to his earlier Nuer marriage articles). Field base remains the 1930–36 visits (four visits, roughly a year total). Attributed core (claims range 11; study [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]):

- **Scope and method design:** In *The Nuer* he had omitted all but brief kinship references because political structure, he held (and still held), is better inquired into apart from other social relations; the village as smallest corporate political unit was stated while the kinship/affinal network inside it was “taken for granted.” This essay describes that intra-community network and aims to **correct the distortion** of lived Nuer social life to which the political abstractions intentionally led.
- **Village and camp:** *Cieng* (village) as corporate group with solidarity (affection for home, military/feud solidarity, economic corporation often spoken in lineage terms); seasonal scatter then shared dry-season cattle camp (*wee*) with greater spatial and moral solidarity and a larger effective kinship radius. Homestead (*gol*) and hamlet (*dhor*); joint vs extended family terminological caution.
- **Lineage vs kinship (analytic hinge):** Lineages = unilateral groups of agnates; kinship = categories of persons. Lineage relations are between groups within a system of groups; kin relations are between persons in categories within a system of categories. *Buth* (inter-lineage agnatic relation—“to share”) vs *mar* (personal kinship). *Cieng* always residential; exclusive descent group denoted *thok dwiel* / *thok mac* (doorway/hearth)—little used because abstracting lineage from its matrix is rare except ritual, feud, exogamy. Lineages are dispersed; political corporate form via dominant nuclei; small lineages exist beyond the dominant-clan systematics of *The Nuer*.
- **Community composition (negative conclusion):** A Nuer community is **not** composed exclusively of a single lineage and their wives; correspondingly, not all members of a lineage live in one community. Large villages/camps have an agnatic lineage core with female-line grafts and many affines; adoption (*buth* gift) only for Dinka and other foreigners, not true Nuer. “Whoever is kith must also be kin”—a village/camp is a **community of kin** (each member’s kindred including affines), not itself a specific kinship *group*; as a group it is residential and, politically, identified with a lineage.
- **Situational levels:** Daily life runs in kinship categories (*gwanlen*, *gatgwanlen*, *wac*, affinal address forms); lineages articulate as agnatic groups at ritual level and as conglomerates fused with attached elements at the political level (*cieng*/*wee* of the lineage as seen from outside). Cross-strands drawn into lineage structure give the local community its unitary tribal character; kinship clusters are fluid, not structurally differentiated kinship groups.
- **Case villages/camps (field piecing):** Konye (May 1935)—kinship-of-coresidence outweighs category/degree; Nyueny (1936)—lineage core of dominant Gaatbol/Keunyang with *gaatnyiet* attachment; female links treated as paternal for community purposes without becoming a “cognatic lineage”; Yakwac camp (1931) and wet-season restudy (1935)—“bull” (*tut*) leadership, Dinka absorption, settlement growth from kinship values; Mancom and Kurmayom cases of maternal/affine attachment and legal-vs-natural kinship complexity (incl. woman-marriage circles).
- **Field reflexivity:** Kinship hard to discover (complex domestic unions; “python-like” Dinka assimilation); Nuer would not cooperate in systematic Q&A (would fail and jeopardize friendships); only months of intimate co-residence and language learning made genealogies possible; early genealogies imperfect; study still incomplete at last departure 1935–36.

## Nuer Religion (1956) — trilogy capstone and anti-reductionist turn

*[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]* (1956) completes the Nuer trilogy begun with *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]* (1940) and *Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer* (1951), returning to material on Spirit (*kwoth*) and its social "refraction" that the 1940 volume had deliberately deferred (study [[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-study]]; concepts [[kwoth]], [[spiritual-refraction]]).

The book carries Evans-Pritchard's clearest statement of an **anti-reductionist position** against three prior traditions in the study of "primitive religion":

- **Against Émile Durkheim**, by name and directly: "Durkheim and his colleagues and pupils were not content to say that religion, being part of the social life, is strongly influenced by the social structure. They claimed that the religious conceptions of primitive peoples are nothing more than a symbolic representation of the social order... It was Durkheim and not the savage who made society into a god." He retained a qualified debt to the Année Sociologique tradition (Hubert and Mauss on sacrifice; Robert Hertz on death and on right/left symbolism) while rejecting the claim that Nuer religious ideas reduce to a coded representation of the social structure — a position that also marks his distance from the structural-functionalism of his own training.
- **Against Lucien Lévy-Bruhl's "prelogical mentality"** and "mystical participation": using the Nuer dogma that twins are birds, Evans-Pritchard argued the apparent contradiction dissolves once such statements are read as elliptical triadic claims of symbolic equivalence (twins–birds–God), not literal identity — "no contradiction is involved in the statement." He extended the same "mistaking ideal for real relations" critique to Tylor and Max Müller, and rejected Tylorian animism outright for the Nuer case, on the ground that Spirit (*kwoth*) and soul (*tie*) are ontologically distinct categories for Nuer, not the latter derived from the former.
- The book closes on an explicitly theological note that Evans-Pritchard, whose own Catholicism is documented as shaping his interpretive stance, states as a deliberate limit on anthropological method: "Though prayer and sacrifice are exterior actions, Nuer religion is ultimately an interior state... At this point the theologian takes over from the anthropologist." This interiority argument — that the sociological account of religion, however illuminating, cannot reach the phenomenon's core — is the book's final theoretical position and is documented in the text itself, not merely inferred from biography.

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].)*

- **LSE / Malinowski circle.** Public-school/Oxbridge background; felt LSE a come-down under [[seligman-c-g|Seligman]]; respected and feared in Malinowski’s seminar as the “Black Knight.” Anecdote (via Kuper): Seligman counselled keeping hands off local girls; Malinowski advised taking a native mistress—emblem of guidance by anecdote rather than method curriculum. Kenya/Sudan fieldwork 1926–1938.
- **Abstraction and political anthropology.** *Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic* (1937) as ambitious experiment in abstraction against Trobriand-style catalogue monographs. Co-editor with [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] of *African Political Systems* (1940); *The Nuer* (1940) as prototype [[segmentary-lineage-system]]; fission/fusion as one segmentary principle. Kuper restates the 1988 judgment that early essays recorded no native clan word and later monographs claim fluent lineage speech without further fieldwork—“Even the Nuer were not like *The Nuer*” (see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]).
- **Applied anthropology and colonial use.** Contracted Sudan government research (after Seligman): Evans-Pritchard recalled neither he nor Seligman was ever asked for advice in years of regional work (*Africa* 1946). From 1947, Kuper presents him as leading a campaign **against** [[applied-anthropology]]; [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], leaving the RLI for an Oxford lectureship, “signed on” to that campaign.
- **Postwar Oxford and the 1950 Marett reversal.** Postwar Oxford chair with Fortes as reader; concentration of R-B, Evans-Pritchard, and Fortes crucial as LSE declined after Malinowski left. 1950 Marett lecture U-turn (Kuper/Lienhardt reconstruction): social anthropology as a kind of historiography/philosophy/art—moral systems not natural systems; patterns not laws; interpret rather than explain—linked to wartime intellectual crisis, Catholic conversion (1944), and Oxford humanism. Later self-description as ethnographer; *Nuer Religion* (1956) almost exclusively theology; final *Azande* ethnohistory (1971) largely ignored. Fortes privately deplored the “lapse from true faith.”
