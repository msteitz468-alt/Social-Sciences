## Overview

Charles Higham (b. 1939), Professor of Anthropology at the University of Otago (New
Zealand) and Fellow of the British Academy, is the leading Western field archaeologist of
mainland Southeast Asian prehistory. Over more than three decades of collaboration with
Thai colleagues — especially **Rachanie Thosarat** and **Amphan Kijngam** — he has
excavated key sites including [[ban-na-di]], [[khok-phanom-di]], [[nong-nor]],
[[ban-lum-khao]], [[noen-u-loke]], and (from 2002) [[ban-non-wat]], and in 2001 worked at
Baksei Chamkrong within [[angkor]]. His two major English-language regional syntheses —
*The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia* (Cambridge World Archaeology, 1989) and its
fully rewritten successor *Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia* (River Books / Art
Media Resources, 2002) — established and then revised the standard framework for the
region's deep past, from late Pleistocene foragers to the fall of Angkor in 1431 CE.

His interpretive stance is broadly **indigenist and processual**: mainland Southeast Asian
social complexity develops chiefly through local processes — sedentism, exchange of
prestige valuables, competition over ranked status, iron-age chiefdoms — with Indian and
Chinese contact as stimulating variables rather than as the external cause of civilisation.
He frames change through Renfrew's **multiplier effect** rather than single prime movers.

## Contributions

- **A multi-variable, anti-monocausal model of social change.** Against single "prime
  mover" explanations (climate, population pressure, or diffusion alone), Higham
  decomposes culture and environment into interacting component variables. See
  [[southeast-asian-state-formation]].
- **The "domestication of people" thesis.** From [[khok-phanom-di]] he argues that the
  socially decisive transformation was **sedentism itself** — permanent co-residence,
  house-building, and ancestor cemeteries — rather than the biological domestication of
  plants and animals. In 2002 he **repositions** the site within a Yangzi-origin rice /
  Austroasiatic-expansion model: Khok Phanom Di is exposed to intrusive farmers rather
  than the initiator of inland Neolithic expansion (as of Higham 2002; see
  [[rice-domestication]]).
- **Emergent ranking without centralization.** Mortuary analyses at [[ban-na-di]],
  [[non-nok-tha]], [[ban-lum-khao]], and related Bronze Age cemeteries support, in his
  reading, internally ranked kin groups long before any regional political centre —
  feeding his account of [[chiefdom]] formation.
- **Chronological revision of early bronze.** Higham (1989) revised 1960s–70s "very early
  metallurgy" claims downward to c. 2000–1500 BCE; Higham (2002) further revises, via
  **AMS dating of rice-chaff ceramic temper**, to initial casting within **c. 1500–1000
  BCE**, and situates the technology in a steppe–China diffusion path (see
  [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]).
- **Iron Age chiefdom sequences as the eve of statehood.** The full-millennium sequence at
  [[noen-u-loke]] and related Mun Valley moated sites, together with [[dong-son-culture]]
  and [[ban-don-ta-phet]], document permanent ranking, craft specialisation, and maritime
  engagement before classical Sanskritized polities.
- **Applying the *mandala* model** to the archaeological record (after Wolters and
  Mabbett), rejecting the bounded-territory "state" as anachronistic for most of the
  region's premodern history.
- **Anti-hydraulic-city reading of Angkor (2002).** Higham moves from a 1989 intermediate
  lean toward Bronson to a 2002 sceptic position: barays as symbolic Meru oceans;
  decentralized rain-fed agriculture under grandee families; irrigation removed from
  rise/collapse explanations ([[angkor-hydraulic-city-debate]]).
- **Rejection of Heine-Geldern diffusionism** and of imposing the European three-age
  system uncritically on Southeast Asian prehistory — while accepting **technological**
  diffusion of bronze along documented Eurasian pathways.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Higham situates his approach as building on **Wilhelm Solheim's** call for a regional,
environment-based framework rather than pure diffusionism, while adding an explicit
theoretical apparatus (Renfrew multiplier effect; Goody on "means of destruction").
Comparative reading on contact-era Africa and discussions with **Henry Wright**,
**Peter Wilson**, and others shape his model of external trade acting on indigenous
complexity. Epigraphic and historical framing of early states leans on **George Cœdès**
(as historiographic target), **O. W. Wolters**, **Michael Vickery**, and **Ian Mabbett**.
Long collaboration with **Rachanie Thosarat** and **Amphan Kijngam** is central to his
Thai fieldwork programme. (Detailed teacher–student genealogy not established from the
present sources.)

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Chronology.** Both 1989 and 2002 rest on radiocarbon programmes that Higham himself
  treats as revisable. The 2002 AMS temper dates are a major step; later work at
  [[ban-non-wat]] and renewed Ban Chiang programmes (post-2002) is not yet fully
  represented here.
- **Pre-aDNA (as of Higham 2002).** Both volumes predate the archaeogenetic "two-layer" model of Southeast
  Asian population history; migration and language claims are date-stamped "(as of Higham
  2002)" and may be superseded.
- **Hydraulic city.** Higham's 2002 anti-irrigation stance confronts a debate later
  reshaped by airborne lidar of greater Angkor — evidence he did not have. Record both
  his position and the open status of the debate.
- **Methodological humility.** He is explicit that synthesis aims "to pose questions and
  propose explanatory structures" rather than supply settled answers (quoted from his
  1989 conclusion and reaffirmed in 2002 Ch. 8).

## Historiography

Higham writes within the discipline's post-colonial reorganisation: he documents (and
benefits from) the shift from the French École française d'Extrême-Orient (EFEO) monopoly
of the colonial era to the rise of Thai, Vietnamese, Cambodian, and Lao national
archaeologies. His primary sources are excavation monographs; his interpretive reach
extends across the whole region synthesised in:
