## Overview

Charles Higham (b. 1939), based at the University of Otago (New Zealand), is the
leading Western field archaeologist of mainland Southeast Asian prehistory. His
excavations at [[ban-na-di]] (with Amphan Kijngam, 1980–81) and [[khok-phanom-di]]
(1985) and his synthetic volume *The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia: From
10,000 B.C. to the Fall of Angkor* (Cambridge World Archaeology, 1989) established the
first comprehensive English-language framework for the region's deep past, from late
Pleistocene foragers to the fall of [[angkor]] in 1431 CE. His interpretive stance is
broadly **indigenist and processual**: he treats mainland Southeast Asian social
complexity as developing chiefly through local processes — sedentism, exchange of
prestige valuables, competition over ranked status — with Indian and Chinese contact
acting as stimulating variables rather than as the external cause of civilisation.

## Contributions

- **A multi-variable, anti-monocausal model of social change.** Higham argues against
  single "prime mover" explanations (climate, population pressure, or diffusion alone),
  instead decomposing culture and environment into interacting component variables — an
  explicitly processual method (drawing on Renfrew's "multiplier effect"). See
  [[southeast-asian-state-formation]].
- **The "domestication of people" thesis.** From [[khok-phanom-di]] he argues that the
  socially decisive transformation was **sedentism itself** — permanent co-residence,
  house-building, and ancestor cemeteries — rather than the biological domestication of
  plants and animals; for his argument it is "immaterial" whether the site's rice was
  wild or cultivated (Higham 1989). Relevant to [[domestication]] and
  [[rice-domestication]].
- **Emergent ranking without centralization.** His and Kijngam's mortuary analyses at
  [[ban-na-di]] (the Area A/B wealth disparity) and Bayard's at [[non-nok-tha]] support,
  in his reading, internally ranked kin groups long before any regional political
  centre — feeding his account of [[chiefdom]] formation.
- **Applying the *mandala* model to the archaeological record.** Following O. W. Wolters
  and Ian Mabbett, Higham adopted the Sanskrit term [[mandala]] for early Southeast
  Asian polities, rejecting the bounded-territory "state" as anachronistic.
- **Rejection of the Heine-Geldern diffusionist paradigm** and of imposing the European
  three-age system on Southeast Asian prehistory.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Higham situates his own approach as building on **Wilhelm Solheim's** (1972) call for a
regional, environment-based framework rather than diffusionism, while adding an explicit
theoretical apparatus. In *The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia* he credits
discussions with **Peter Wilson**, **Henry Wright**, and **Jack Goody**, and comparative
reading on contact-era Africa, as shaping his model of how external trade contact acts on
indigenous complexity. His epigraphic and historical framing of the early states leans on
**George Cœdès**, **O. W. Wolters**, **Michael Vickery**, and **Ian Mabbett**. His
long-running collaboration with the Thai archaeologist **Amphan Kijngam** is central to
his Khorat-plateau fieldwork. (Detailed teacher–student genealogy not established from the
present source.)

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Higham's 1989 syntheses rest on a radiocarbon chronology that he himself flags as
unstable — the [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate|early Southeast Asian bronze
dating]] was unresolved at the time of writing, and his revised "initial bronze c.
2000–1500 BCE" reconciliation of [[ban-chiang]] and [[ban-na-di]] has since been further
adjusted by continued dating work. The volume predates the archaeogenetic ("two-layer")
evidence on Southeast Asian population history of the 2010s, so its migration and
population claims are date-stamped and in several cases superseded. Higham is explicit
that his book aims "to pose questions and propose explanatory structures" rather than
supply settled answers, and he criticises earlier syntheses (Colani, Cœdès) for letting
authorial prejudice — e.g. Colani's framing of Hoabinhian foragers as "poor savages" —
substitute for evidence.

## Historiography

Higham writes within the discipline's post-colonial reorganisation: he documents (and
benefits from) the shift from the French École française d'Extrême-Orient (EFEO) monopoly of
the colonial era to the rise of Thai and Vietnamese national archaeologies, whose
practitioners' contribution, he notes, now "exceeds by far that of visiting foreign
workers." His own work at [[ban-chiang]]-adjacent sites was part of that
training-and-collaboration turn. His primary sources are his excavation monographs; his
interpretive reach extends across the whole region synthesised in the 1989 volume,
recorded here in [[higham-archaeology-mainland-southeast-asia-1989]].
