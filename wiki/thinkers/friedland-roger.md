# Roger Friedland

## Overview

Roger Friedland, with [[alford-robert-r]], authored ch. 10 of *The New Institutionalism
in Organizational Analysis* (1991), "Bringing Society Back In: Symbols, Practices, and
Institutional Contradictions" — the canonical source of the [[institutional-logics]]
concept. The pair's earlier joint work, "Powers of Theory" (Alford and Friedland 1985),
is cited within the chapter as the origin of its three-levels-of-analysis schema.

## Contributions

Friedland and Alford defined institutions as "both supraorganizational patterns of
activity through which humans conduct their material life in time and space, and
symbolic systems through which they categorize that activity and infuse it with
meaning." They argued the central institutions of the contemporary capitalist West —
capitalist market, bureaucratic state, democracy, nuclear family, and Christian religion
— each carry a distinct organizing logic: capitalism ("accumulation and the
commodification of human activity"), the state ("rationalization and the regulation of
human activity by legal and bureaucratic hierarchies"), democracy ("participation and
the extension of popular control"), the family ("community and... unconditional
loyalty"), and religion or science ("truth... and the symbolic construction of
reality"). Because these logics are "potentially contradictory," they argued,
"institutional contradictions are the bases of the most important political conflicts in
our society; it is through these politics that the institutional structure of society is
transformed" — citing, as illustration, U.S. feminists extending market logic into the
family, capitalists winning corporate juridical personhood, and workers extending
democratic logic into the capitalist firm.

Friedland and Alford also proposed that "an adequate social theory must work at three
levels of analysis — individuals competing and negotiating, organizations in conflict
and coordination, and institutions in contradiction and interdependency," mapping these
onto pluralist, managerial, and class traditions in political sociology respectively,
each with a "home domain" and blind spots elsewhere.

## Intellectual Genealogy

The chapter opens by declaring the social sciences "in the midst of a theoretical
retreat from society," criticizing both utilitarian/rational-choice approaches
([[rational-choice-theory]], public-choice theory, agency theory, transaction-cost
economics) and purely power-oriented organizational theory. Friedland and Alford engage
[[weber-max]], [[parsons-talcott]], Durkheim, and Marx as classical
base-superstructure theorists whose materialist-idealist dichotomy they seek to
reconstruct around "the centrality of the symbolic in the organization of social life."
They engage Clifford Geertz's interpretive anthropology (the Balinese cockfight essay)
approvingly, and critically engage Michel Foucault (credited with bridging power and
discourse but faulted for analyzing "power, but without agency, without conflict"),
Mary Douglas (a supporting but, they argue, undertheorized cognitive-institutionalist
source), and Mark Granovetter's [[embeddedness]] (engaged as necessary but insufficient
without specified institutional-logic content). They extend a sustained critique of
Theda Skocpol's state-autonomy theory as circular, and of [[dimaggio-paul]] (1988a) —
engaged positively as an interlocutor urging synthesis of political and institutional
approaches, but critiqued for retaining an "institution-free conception of interest and
power."

## Critiques and Reappraisals

DiMaggio and Powell (ch. 1) cite Friedland and Alford's institutional-logics framework
directly, applying it to [[dimaggio-paul]]'s (ch. 11) account of "democratic versus
elite" models of the American art museum and to Galaskiewicz's (ch. 12) study of
Minneapolis business leaders maintaining a "communitarian rather than a pure market
model" of the corporate role. [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) draws on Friedland and Alford's
claim that "profit and risk are culturally constructed categories whose definitions vary
widely across nation-states" to support his own critique of the technical/institutional
dichotomy.
