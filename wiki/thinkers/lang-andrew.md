## Overview

**Andrew Lang** (1844–1912) was a Scottish folklorist and critic who, though an evolutionist, used the 'high gods of low races' to challenge the [[animism]]-to-monotheism developmental sequence, and who developed a heterodox interest in psychical research.

## Contributions

- Scottish folklorist and man of letters who, from within evolutionism, argued that 'high gods' among 'low races' embarrassed the standard animism-to-monotheism sequence, and who publicly feuded with Frazer and Müller.
- (Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) Lang initially worked squarely within Tylor's [[animism]]/[[survivals]] framework: in *Custom and Myth* (1884) he attacked Max Müller's philological "disease of language" theory of mythology, arguing myth was "a disease of thought, far more than a disease of language," produced by savage minds reasoning from common human experience, and that myth-similarity across unrelated peoples (Greeks, Australians) arose "because they were originally framed to meet the same needs out of the same material" — the comparative/anthropological method against Müller's philological one.
- From the 1890s Lang broke with Tylor's own animism theory. In *The Making of Religion* (1898) he argued that "high gods" — supreme, moral, creator beings — occur "rudely, but recognisably, in the lowest-known grades of savagery," and so could not be a late development out of ghost-worship/[[animism]] as Tylor's sequence (ghost-soul belief → god-idea) required. Lang posited "two streams of religious thought": one from an "undifferentiated Being, eternal, moral, and creative," the other from ghost-doctrine. He held ghost-doctrine, being "best adapted to everyday needs," typically contaminated primitive monotheism with sacrifice/food-propitiation, and that only the Hebrew/Christian tradition preserved the original moral-monotheistic element alongside animism's care for the individual soul — a position Stocking notes placed Lang, ironically, close to Müller's degenerationism, the very position his early anthropological career had been built attacking.
- After 1900 Lang played "critical gadfly" to [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]: he called *The Golden Bough* "the most inconceivably silly book of recent times" and dubbed Frazer's approach the "Covent Garden" or "vegetable school" of mythology. He rejected Frazer's magic-before-religion sequence and the Morganian/Spencer-and-Fison theory of [[totemism]], arguing instead — building on J. J. Atkinson's Darwinian *Primal Law* and McLennan — for a rival account; he disputed the evidentiary use of classificatory kinship terms to infer group marriage, and described the Arunta as ethnographically anomalous rather than paradigmatically "primitive" — a position Stocking reads as anticipating both Boas's critique of "premature classification" and Lévi-Strauss's later skepticism about [[totemism]] as a coherent category.
- Lang's chief later influence ran to Fr. Wilhelm Schmidt's Central European Catholic school of primitive-monotheism theory; Stocking judges his relation to modern social theory (Durkheim et al.) as comparatively tangential, unlike [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]]'s.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A prolific literary intellectual rather than an academic anthropologist; ally then critic of Tylorian folklore. Lang and [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]] are read by Stocking as the two "second generation" evolutionists of the 1880s–90s whose internal critiques (the primitive-monotheism thesis for Lang; the social/ritual theory of sacrifice for Smith) each challenged core assumptions of the evolutionist program from within it, feeding the field's post-1896 fracturing documented in *After Tylor*.
