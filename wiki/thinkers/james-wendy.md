# Wendy James

British social anthropologist (B.Litt., M.A., D.Phil. Oxford; lecturer in social anthropology as of the 1973 volume contributors' note). This page is seeded from her essay **"The Anthropologist as Reluctant Imperialist"** in *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (Asad ed., 1973). Later monographs are **not yet ingested**.

## Overview

James supplies the volume's most carefully differentiated account of the anthropologist's structural location under colonial rule. Against both cosy-conspiracy and pure-handmaiden stories, she argues that the form of research itself **entailed a liberal stance**, while the anthropologist occupied a **third pole** — constrained by colonial authority on one side and by nationalist scrutiny on the other — and was typically a *reluctant* or *frustrated* participant rather than an eager agent of empire.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to James 1973.)*

- **Reluctant / frustrated radical thesis.** Anthropologists often opposed particular colonial policies and saw themselves as defenders of "their" people; structural location still lay inside the colonial field. Liberalism was not optional moral decoration but **entailed by the research form**.
- **Double ambivalence.** Relations with officials and with nationalists were both ambivalent; the field is not a conspiracy of cosy collaboration.
- **Sponsorship tactics.** Practitioners claimed scientific status *and* practical value; pure/applied dual claims and man/scientist splits managed contradictory audiences (sponsors, profession, subjects).
- **Case reconstructions:** [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] (witchcraft as moral critique; Sudan applied neglect; *Sanusi* access vs older-colony limits); [[firth-raymond|Firth]] (anthropology not handmaid); [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] arc 1929–45 from "Practical Anthropology" through Mitchell/Ruxton debates, land/labour politics, Kenyatta introduction, to *Dynamics* (mixing politics with science); Audrey Richards and IAI survey evidence of low official demand; Schapera and Mitchell (1951) on unfinished opposition.
- **Climax claim:** the colonial situation was not an integrated whole that anthropology simply served; anthropology was a constrained third pole whose main inter-war limit was colonial authority, yet it could also be a vehicle of criticism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Interlocutors in the essay: Malinowski, Evans-Pritchard, Firth, Richards, Schapera, Kenyatta (*Facing Mount Kenya* as nationalist use of anthropology). Sits inside the Hull 1972 seminar circle organized around [[asad-talal|Asad]]'s project.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

James's "reluctant imperialist" is one standing position on [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] — often cited against cruder handmaiden models and against establishment autonomy claims. Faris, Ahmed, and some New Left writers press harder structural-complicity lines that treat reluctance as insufficient.
