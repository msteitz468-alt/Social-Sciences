# Linda Schele

## Overview

American art historian and epigrapher (1942–1998) who, with [[lounsbury-floyd|Floyd Lounsbury]] and Peter Mathews, reconstructed the Late Classic dynastic history of [[palenque]] at the Primera Mesa Redonda (December 1973) and subsequent Dumbarton Oaks “Mini-Conferences.” [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012|Coe (2012)]] treats her as co-presider (with Lounsbury) of the post-Thompson “Young Turks” generation and as the chief popularizer of [[epigraphy|Maya hieroglyph workshops]]. She died the same year as Lounsbury and [[knorosov-yuri|Yuri Knorosov]]. Primary densification source for this page: Coe’s partisan historiography; treat interpretive praise and institutional conflict claims as **attributed to Coe**.

## Contributions

### Formation and method (as reported by Coe 2012)

Born Nashville 1942; Fine Arts degree, University of Cincinnati (1964); married David Schele; taught art at the University of South Alabama, Mobile (from 1968). Coe reports her self-description of method: cast a large “vacuum” over the data without strong predisposition and follow emergent patterns—what she called the “philosophy of the happy accident.” Christmas 1970, [[palenque]] “hit [her] in the gut”; she returned summers and in 1973 assisted Merle Greene Robertson lighting stucco for photography, including four days in the Temple of the Inscriptions burial vault studying sarcophagus figures and glyphs.

### Palenque dynasty breakthrough (1973–74)

At the Primera Mesa Redonda de Palenque (14–22 December 1973)—which Coe ranks among the most important Maya conferences ever held—Schele met Lounsbury and Mathews for the first time under Moisés Morales’s champa. Using Berlin’s royal-name prefix (later *k’inich*), date–verb–subject–[[emblem-glyph|Emblem Glyph]] structure, and Lounsbury’s Calendar Round → Long Count formulas, Schele and Mathews (with Lounsbury) reconstructed six successive Late Classic Palenque kings’ births, accessions, and deaths in roughly two and a half hours. Naming shifted under local pressure from Yucatec nicknames toward Ch’ol forms (e.g. Chan-Bahlum), with later complications noted by Coe. Architectural collaboration with Merle Greene Robertson attributed Pakal’s building program (from AD 647: Palace houses, Temple of the Inscriptions) and Chan-Bahlum’s Cross Group.

At Dumbarton Oaks in April 1974 (Betty Benson’s Mini-Conference), after tension with senior figures including [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]], Schele, Mathews, Lounsbury, Merle, and [[kelley-david-h|Kelley]] extended the king list from the last two centuries of history (1973) to the first two in about three and a half hours of floor work on Maudslay plates. Schele later told Coe the Mini-Conferences’ contribution was treating full inscriptions as Mayan sentences (verbs, adjectives, subjects by position), using Distance Numbers for temporal structure, and reaching high partial-translation levels even when verbs were incompletely known.

### Verbs, parentage, and institutionalization

Ruz’s challenge at the 1974 Segunda Mesa Redonda—how she knew a glyph was a verb—led her into UT Austin graduate school. Her 1980 dissertation established dynastic “event glyphs” (e.g. *chum* “to be seated” / enthroned) and syllabic verbal affixes; with Lounsbury’s T.130 *wa* and T.116 *ni*, the seating combination reads *chumwan(i)* in grammatical Ch’olan—the language Coe treats as accepted for Classic monumental inscriptions. Comparative parentage charts, building on Christopher Jones’s Tikal proposals, helped Mathews and Kelley confirm Bird-Jaguar as son of Shield-Jaguar at Yaxchilán and firm genealogies across cities.

Mesa Redonda attendance grew from ~35 (1973) to 142 from seven countries within five years. From 1978 Schele ran Maya Hieroglyph Workshops at UT Austin; by the mid-1980s Coe reports hundreds of participants, with discoveries sometimes outrunning publication and circulating by letter. With Mary Miller she curated the 1986 Kimbell *The Blood of Kings*, uniting epigraphy and iconography around royal bloodletting, descent, and warfare—explicitly not the “peaceful Maya” of Morley/Thompson (Coe’s framing; Paz praised the catalog).

### Mentorship of David Stuart

In 1976, consulting on National Geographic *The Mysterious Maya*, she invited eleven-year-old [[stuart-david|David Stuart]] to Palenque after he identified a “Fire glyph.” She later told Coe that Stuart, given the Temple of the Sun tablet, reached in eight hours where the adult team had needed five years; she urged his 1978 Mesa Redonda paper before he turned thirteen. Post-high-school MacArthur/Dumbarton Oaks years included intensive collaboration with her; Stuart told Coe those years made him feel “in this for good.” She also worked with him at [[copan]] (summer 1986).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Ruz / INAH conflict (Coe’s narrative):** Schele–Mathews–Lounsbury showed Pakal died at about eighty; Ruz’s osteological claim of ≤50 and *fantasistas* attacks (1975, 1977) made the age/history dispute public. Coe attributes Mexican professional absence from the 1973 Mesa Redonda partly to a Ruz-organized boycott—**Coe’s position**, not wiki verdict.
- **Proskouriakoff antipathy** at the 1974 D.O. meeting is reported by Coe as atmosphere, not adjudicated here.
- **Field-archaeology cold shoulder:** Coe reports dirt archaeologists felt overshadowed by elite epigraphy/iconography and often treated decipherment as unworthy or “propaganda”; Schele’s line (via Coe) was that the decipherment had occurred and must be embraced or deliberately dismissed. Media reach and lack of a traditional anthropology Ph.D. path fueled envy claims in Coe’s account.
- Individual readings and early nicknames were refined by later full phonetics; structural dynastic results at Palenque remain a founding episode of modern Maya [[epigraphy]].

### Martin & Grube 2008 dedication

[[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube, *Chronicle of the Maya Kings and Queens* (2nd ed. 2008)]] is dedicated to her memory (1942–1998). The volume’s reign-by-reign political narrative is a direct descendant of the historical-epigraphy program she helped institutionalize; [[martin-simon|Simon Martin]] and [[grube-nikolai|Nikolai Grube]] (named among Coe’s “Young Turks” with Schele and Lounsbury “presiding”) popularize the hierarchical [[overkingship]] model for eleven Classic kingdoms.

### *A Forest of Kings* (1990) — primary densification

With archaeologist [[freidel-david|David Freidel]], Schele co-authored [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|*A Forest of Kings*]] (William Morrow, 1990) — the major **popular-scholarly narrative** that presented Classic lowland politics as recoverable history for a general audience. Study page: [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-study]].

**Self-report in the Prologue (attributed):** first reached [[palenque]] December 1970; pattern-seeking method (“philosophy of the happy accident” also in Coe); 1973 Mesa Redonda dynasty work with Mathews; collaboration with Freidel from fall 1979 after Cerros Structure 5C mask consultation. Book anchors: Cerros, Palenque, Copan, Yaxuna.

**Program claims (all attributed, 1990):** Late Preclassic *ahau* already public at [[cerros]] (~50 BCE); Early Classic Tikal–[[uaxactun]] war (AD 378 horizon); seventh-century “star wars” ([[caracol]], [[naranjo]], [[calakmul]], [[tikal]]); [[palenque]] dynastic theology; [[yaxchilan]] *cahalob* and Lady Xoc bloodletting; [[copan]]–[[quirigua]] 738 crisis; [[chichen-itza]] *multepal*/cosmopolitan model; Terminal Classic as crisis of literate divine kingship ([[classic-maya-collapse-debate]]).

**Limits:** dramatized scenes are narrative devices; many 1990 nicknames (Great-Jaguar-Paw, Ah-Cacaw, 18-Rabbit) revised by later phonetics (Martin & Grube 2008 generation). Pair always with later dynastic consensus for reign detail.
