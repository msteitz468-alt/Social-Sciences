# Hans J. Nissen

## Overview

Hans Jörg Nissen (b. 1935), German ancient Near Eastern archaeologist, long professor
at the Free University of Berlin and associated with the Uruk excavations and with
settlement-oriented synthesis of early Mesopotamia. Best known in English for *The
Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.*
([[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|Nissen 1988]]) — an outline history of
political-organizational development from first settlement through the first territorial
states — and for collaboration with [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]] on the Uruk
region survey (*The Uruk Countryside*).

## Contributions

*(Key theses densified from *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|Nissen 1988]]* claims; attributed throughout; concise summary — not hub length.)*

- **Outline history as process.** Presents “a” history (not the only one) organized
  around **changes in early forms of political organization**, spanning preliterate and
  literate periods rather than treating writing as the start of history (Prefaces + Ch. 1).
- **Five-phase developmental periodization (ca. 9000–2000 BCE).** Settlement → Settlement
  to Town → Early High Civilization → Rival City-States → First Territorial States
  (chapter frames; see [[ancient-near-east-chronology]]). Subdivides early high civilization
  into Late Uruk / Jamdet Nasr / ED I; retains the older term “early high civilization”
  despite historiographic reservations.
- **Geographic over ethnic labels for early periods.** Prefers **Babylonia**,
  **Susiana**, and **Mesopotamia** over “Sumer” / “Akkad” as areal names when ethnic
  designation is insecure (English Preface).
- **Settlement systems, not early mega-sites, as the urban path.** Argues further
  political organization ran via multi-tier settlement systems, **not** via Neolithic
  extremes such as [[jericho|Jericho]] and [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (river-oasis /
  packed “towns” without dependent hinterlands). Degree of settlement-system organization
  scales with ecological-unit size (Zagros valleys → small plains → Susiana → lower
  Mesopotamian alluvium).
- **Uruk “early high civilization.”** Late Uruk four-tier hinterland hierarchy headed by
  [[uruk|Uruk]]; Eanna as multi-function central economic unit; seals, BRBs, stepwise
  invention of writing; dual Eanna/Anu–Kullaba cult layout unified at end Late Uruk /
  Jamdet Nasr; demotes excavators’ “temple”/“palace” labels to **public buildings** when
  function is unproven (Ch. 4; densified on [[uruk]], [[uruk-culture]],
  [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]]).
- **Temple-city as late particularist reaction.** Rejects projecting Girsu ED III
  temple-city ideology as deep-time prototype of the whole country; reads Urukagina’s
  “old order” as legitimizing fiction and rigid temple city as last-phase ED III reaction
  to centralization (Position C on [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]]).
- **Settlement and survey sensibility.** Co-author with [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] of
  *The Uruk Countryside* (1972); 1988 synthesis uses hinterland maps for size distributions,
  empty-settlement strips as political spheres of influence, and flags Akkad-period survey
  limits when mass pottery cannot date surface sites to ED III / Akkad / Ur III.
- **Irrigation and particularism.** Rejects early-irrigation-requires-large-organization
  for *initial* southern settlement; models later dendritic “irrigation oases” as drivers
  of city particularism; first territorial states (Akkad → Ur III) as the answer to that
  multipolar structure.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- German-edition reviewers (as Nissen reports) wanted either more archaeological detail
  or more of the Early Mesopotamian written record; he defends the outline’s selective
  developmental frame (English Preface).
- Post-1988 research (Uruk world-system debates, refined absolute chronology, aDNA,
  later restudies of Early Dynastic and Akkad political form) will often supersede
  specific causal claims — treat 1988 theses as **dated positions**, cross-checked
  against [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] and
  later work.
