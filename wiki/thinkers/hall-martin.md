# Martin Hall

## Overview

South African historical archaeologist (b. 1952), Professor of Historical Archaeology at the University of Cape Town and, at the time of publishing *Archaeology and the Modern World* (2000), President of the World Archaeological Congress. His earlier career was in southern African Iron Age archaeology (*The Changing Past*, 1987) before turning to the historical archaeology of the colonial Cape.

## Contributions

- **Colonial transcripts.** Hall's signature move ([[hall-archaeology-modern-world-study]]): extending James C. Scott's [[public-transcript]]/[[hidden-transcript]] distinction from speech to [[material-culture]], combined with Foucault's "statement," so that architecture, assemblages, and documents become one transdisciplinary body of evidence. The hidden transcript of resistance leaves a recoverable "impress" on the public transcript of domination.
- **Discordance as signal.** Against treating documentary and material records as parallel or hierarchical, Hall argued their systematic divergence ("taphonomic filters" — the Ziekedwars Street well against Anhuyzer's probate inventory) is the primary interpretive evidence for colonial societies ([[documentary-archaeology]]).
- **Comparative colonial archaeology.** The Cape–Chesapeake comparison grounded the "archaeology of the modern world" program (with [[orser-charles-e]]) in a paired-case design across two slave-based peripheries of European merchant capitalism.
- **Cape empirical corpus.** The 41-manor-house gable study and its "shadow lineages" of female property transmission ([[cape-dutch-architecture]]), the Paradise excavations, and the [[district-six]] archaeology and memory projects.
- **Postcolonial theory into archaeology.** Early sustained archaeological application of Bhabha's [[third-space]] and Spivak's subaltern voice ([[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty]]).
