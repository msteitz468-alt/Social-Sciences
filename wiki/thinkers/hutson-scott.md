# Scott Hutson

American archaeologist of the ancient Maya and interpretive theorist, co-author with [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] of the 3rd edition of *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]* (2003), the edition that added chapters on [[practice-theory|agency and practice]] and [[embodiment|embodied archaeology]] and new material on semiotics, [[feminist-archaeology|feminist archaeology]], and discourse. Affiliated at the time with the Archaeological Research Facility, University of California, Berkeley; later a Mesoamerican archaeologist working on Maya urbanism (Chunchucmil, Yucatán).

## Overview

Within *Reading the Past* Hutson's imprint is strongest on the semiotic argument (the adoption of [[peirce-charles-sanders|Peirce]]'s triadic sign model for [[reading-material-culture|reading material culture]]), on the reflexive/political chapters (the African Burial Ground and multivocality cases), and on the "third-wave" framing of feminist archaeology. His own cited work (Hutson 1998, 2001, 2002) develops [[semiotic-practice|semiotic]] and reflexive approaches to material culture and to archaeology's rhetoric.

## Contributions

- Co-authorship of the discipline's most widely used interpretive-theory textbook in its consolidated 3rd-edition form.
- Peircean semiotics as the sign model for [[contextual-archaeology|contextual]] interpretation of material culture.
- Maya urbanism and household/identity studies (Chunchucmil) — the empirical base of his later career.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Works within the [[post-processual-archaeology|interpretive/post-processual]] lineage descending from [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]; engages the Peircean semiotic-archaeology stream (Preucel, Bauer) and Mesoamerican material-culture scholarship (Joyce, Monaghan). ## Critiques and Reappraisals

As a co-author of a survey text his individual positions are not separately the object of controversy; the debates belong to the programs the book surveys ([[processual-postprocessual-debate]]).

## Historiography

The 3rd edition is the standard teaching text of archaeological theory; Hutson's addition marks the generational handover of the interpretive program from its 1980s founders to a cohort applying it in Mesoamerican field archaeology.
