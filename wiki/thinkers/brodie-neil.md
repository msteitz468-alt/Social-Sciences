# Neil Brodie

Research director of the **Illicit Antiquities Research Centre (IARC)** at the McDonald Institute for Archaeological Research, University of Cambridge (as of [[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Brodie et al. 2006]]). A principal empirical and policy voice on the [[illicit-antiquities-trade]], co-editor of the 2006 Florida volume and author of its introduction, Iraq case chapter (1991–2005 and the London trade), and (with [[luke-christina|Christina Luke]]) the conclusion on the social and cultural contexts of collecting. Named with [[tubb-kathryn-walker|Kathryn Walker Tubb]] and others among the demand-side cohort associated with [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s IARC program on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Overview

Brodie works at the intersection of archaeological ethics, market research, and conflict-heritage documentation. His contribution in the 2006 volume is less a new theoretical program than a **case-and-policy densification** of the claim that collector and dealer demand sustains looting: the introduction frames the global crisis; chapter 10 assembles the Iraq plunder record and its London market outlet; the conclusion (with Luke) situates collecting in social context.

## Contributions

- **IARC research program** — empirical and policy work on illicit antiquities alongside Renfrew's programmatic advocacy ([[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]]).
- **Iraq 1991–2005 documentation** (attributed, Brodie 2006 ch. 10) — post–Gulf War and 2003 invasion looting; National Museum Baghdad; site destruction; London trade connections.
- **Editorial synthesis** — co-editing *Archaeology, Cultural Heritage, and the Antiquities Trade* as a multi-genre record (law, ethnography, market analysis, conflict heritage).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Institutional:** McDonald Institute / IARC under Renfrew's Cambridge heritage-ethics agenda.

## Contributions (densified from 2006 volume)

### Introduction framing (attributed)
- Global antiquities trade converts destructively removed objects into legal commodities via jurisdictional gaps, forged papers, and corrupt officials.
- Distinguishes **provenience** (findspot) from **provenance** (ownership history); most market objects lack both.
- Three functional markets: archaeologically rich / transit / destination; auctions set prices and can launder via buy-back pedigrees.
- U.S. art museums as central demand engines for unprovenanced material as legitimate supply shrank under source-state controls.

### Iraq 1991–2005 in detail (ch. 10)
See [[iraq-national-museum]] for Bogdanos figures and institutional timeline. Brodie links museum theft, southern site destruction, and the **London** trade; discusses SI 1519 burden-of-proof shift and post-control auction effects.

### Conclusion with Luke (ch. 17)
Collecting motives (cultural capital, largesse), aestheticization of once-ethnographic categories, catalogue pseudo-scholarship, trade as social networks, opacity (e.g. Sevso), and the claim that archaeological publication can itself fuel market demand for comparable objects.
