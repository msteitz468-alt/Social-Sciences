# Roger Matthews

British Near Eastern archaeologist, field director, and historian of his own discipline.
Former Director of the British Institutes of Archaeology in Baghdad and Ankara; based at
the Institute of Archaeology, University College London (later University of Reading). He is
best known in this wiki as the author of *[[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|The
Archaeology of Mesopotamia: Theories and Approaches]]* (2003), the only book-length critical
account of [[mesopotamian-archaeology|Mesopotamian archaeology]] as a discipline — its
history, methods, politics, and future.

## Overview

Matthews's distinctive contribution is **reflexive**: rather than another culture-history
synthesis, he treats Mesopotamian archaeology itself as an object of analysis, foregrounding
its 19th-century imperial and museum origins, its entanglement with nation-building and
Orientalism, and its unresolved relationship with Assyriology. He writes in the first person
as "an institutional, male, white Westerner," using that positionality to argue both for the
achievements of the discipline's founders and for a decolonized future practice with
host-country scholars at every level. Methodologically he is a fieldworker who champions
adapting excavation technique to mud-brick sites (the hand-pick; micromorphology) and treats
survey and the text–object problem as first-class content.

## Contributions

- The **reflexive disciplinary history** of Mesopotamian archaeology (see
  [[mesopotamian-archaeology]]) — its colonial politics, the contested term "Mesopotamia,"
  and the postcolonial critique of Western-directed fieldwork.
- A **tripartite empire-identification framework** distinguishing anthropological
  characteristics, archaeological correlates, and textual correlates — his critique of
  Smith & Montiel for conflating the first two (see [[empire]]).
- An **arbiter position** in the [[uruk-world-system-debate]]: granting peripheral
  sophistication, he nonetheless sides with [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze's]] asymmetry reading
  against [[stein-gil|Stein's]] [[distance-parity|distance-parity]] model, arguing
  Uruk-Warka's sheer scale outweighs the periphery evidence and that no Uruk-heartland
  counter-colonies exist.
- Primary excavation/survey work at [[tell-brak|Tell Brak]] (Nagar), the Jemdet Nasr seal
  corpus, and Hittite-frontier survey in Turkey.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **allied with / draws on:** [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert Adams]] (survey; "texts as

  past — while resisting what he calls presentist over-corrections.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Matthews's book is valued as the field's standard reflexive guide, but it is downstream of
the primary studies it synthesizes and, by its own framing, a selective set of "theories and
approaches" rather than an exhaustive regional survey. His arbiter judgment in the
[[uruk-world-system-debate|Uruk debate]] — siding with Algaze on aggregate asymmetry — is one
attributed position among several, and shares the falsifiability problem common to that
dispute (aggregate asymmetry inferred largely from the core's own scale).

## Historiography

Written on the eve of the 2003 Iraq war and looting, *The Archaeology of Mesopotamia* is both
a disciplinary history and a prescient warning about heritage destruction (the Assur dam) — a
document of the field at the moment before its Iraqi heartland's catastrophe. Only this 2003
volume is ingested here; his later prehistory syntheses and Central Zagros fieldwork are not
yet represented.
