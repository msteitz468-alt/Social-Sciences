# Bruce G. Trigger (1937–2006)

Canadian archaeologist, ethnohistorian of Northern Iroquoians, and historian of archaeological thought; James McGill Professor in the Department of Anthropology at McGill University. Three interlocking careers: (1) **Huron / New France ethnohistory** (*The Children of Aataentsic*, 1976; *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]*, 1985); (2) **comparative early civilizations** (*Understanding Early Civilizations*, 2003); (3) **intellectual history of archaeology** (*[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|A History of Archaeological Thought]]*, 1989; 2nd ed. 2006). Early fieldwork in Egypt and the Sudan; undergraduate Toronto (encouraged by T. F. McIlwraith); long McGill teaching career.

## Overview

Trigger's historiographic program (as stated in *HAT* 2nd ed.) is an **intellectual history of the main ideas guiding archaeological thought**, written from **ontological materialism and epistemological realism**, with a materialist-Marxist understanding of relativism that still affirms cumulative, data-constrained knowledge of the past. He rejects cultural determinism, ecological determinism, and unilinear evolutionism while seeking to reconcile materialist explanation with cultural and historical diversity (Childe-inspired).

His North American ethnohistory applies the same anti-stereotyping, multi-evidence program to Canadian history: against the "Heroic Age" narrative that cast Europeans as sole historical agents and natives as static background, he reconstructs French–Iroquoian relations (Cartier–1663) as **creative symbiosis** in which traders and Indigenous partners co-founded a viable colony (Trigger 1985). See [[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]], [[huron]], [[ethnohistory]].

## Contributions

*(From *Natives and Newcomers*, 1985 — attributed; source [[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985]].)*

- **Critique of the Heroic Age / Indian image:** documents how Parkman, the Charlevoix tradition, nationalist Canadian histories, and Victorian anthropology produced stereotypes of static, inferior, vanishing natives and a history/anthropology split that denied Indigenous people historical agency.
- **Ethnohistory as interest-group method:** combine archaeology, ethnography, and documentary sources; analyse French traders, administrators, missionaries, and native polities as **interest groups**, not as a single "European" or "Indian" actor.
- **Creative symbiosis (to ~1632):** before catastrophic epidemics and Jesuit power, French–Huron relations were a nondirected mutual accommodation organized around the fur trade; natives were equals in self-assessment, not passive recipients of civilization.
- **Who founded New France?** Traders and native consent/alliances, not priests and officials alone — a direct inversion of Heroic Age founder pantheons.
- **St Lawrence Iroquoian disappearance and Huron destruction:** multi-causal ethnohistorical reconstructions (warfare, trade geography, epidemics, mission factionalism) against single-cause myths.
- **Iroquois ≠ Iroquoian:** terminological discipline — Five/Six Nations vs broader linguistic family including Huron, Petun, Neutral, St Lawrence groups.

*(From *A History of Archaeological Thought*, 2nd ed. 2006 — attributed.)*

- **Standard periodization of archaeological thought:** classical/text-based archaeologies → antiquarianism without texts → beginnings of prehistoric archaeology → nineteenth-century evolutionary archaeology → culture-historical archaeology → early functional-processual archaeology → processualism and postprocessualism → pragmatic synthesis.
- **Social-context thesis:** successive trends in archaeological thought reflect (in the West) personal and collective interests of archaeologists as related to fluctuating fortunes of the middle classes — without reducing interpretation to pure ideology.
- **Data-constraint / cumulative objectivity thesis:** gradual accumulation of archaeological data exercises growing constraint on interpretation, increasing objectivity and archaeology's value for understanding the full span of human history.
- **Levels of theory:** distinguishes high-level theory, middle-ranging theory, and archaeological data as nested concerns for how interpretation works (ch. 1; ch. 9).
- **Worldwide comparative frame:** treats classical, Egyptological, Assyriological, Chinese, Japanese, African, Soviet, and American traditions as part of one history of ideas, not an Anglophone-only narrative (expanded in 2nd ed.).
- **Childe scholarship:** major secondary interpreter of Childe as culture-historian and as Marxist archaeologist (also *Gordon Childe: Revolutions in Archaeology*, 1980 — not yet ingested).

*(From *The Children of Aataentsic*, 1976/1987 — attributed; source [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976]]; study [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-study]].)*

- **The founding monograph of Canadian ethnohistory:** a full-scale history of the [[huron|Huron]] to 1660 combining archaeology, 17th-century French documents, ethnography, and linguistics; "a history of the Huron, not of New France."
- **Rationalist-materialist method:** native behaviour explained by rational interest calculation within Huron culture, grounded in a Marxian theory of culture; the **interest-group method** (Homans/Sartre lineage) as the unit of ethnohistorical analysis.
- **Precontact dynamism thesis:** archaeological baseline change (confederacy formation c. 1440, trade-driven settlement concentration) as precondition for measuring European impact; the anti-equilibrium argument of ch. 13.
- **Anti-determinist fur-trade historiography:** systematic demolition of Hunt's *Wars of the Iroquois* middleman thesis ([[iroquois-wars-motivation-debate]]); epidemics weighted over violence in Huron demographic collapse; religion strengthened, not collapsed, under the 1634–40 epidemics ([[virgin-soil-epidemics]]).

*(From *Understanding Early Civilizations*, 2003 — attributed; study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]].)*

- **Seven-case comparative program:** Egypt, Mesopotamia, Shang China, Valley of Mexico, Classic Maya, Inka, Yoruba — controlled comparison of sociopolitical, economic, and cognitive institutions under Galton-aware independence assumptions.
- **[[early-civilization]] as class society:** earliest/simplest class-based form; writing neither necessary nor sufficient.
- **Two political forms:** [[city-state]] vs [[territorial-state]] as stable alternatives, not stages ([[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]).
- **Universal [[kingship]]** with sacred personal power; not priest-over-king theocracies.
- **Economic diversity thesis:** agriculture, tenure, and technology freer to vary than Stewardian neoevolution expected; polity type shapes exchange regimes.
- **Religious mutual-dependence package:** sacrifice projects taxation/class relations; cross-cultural symbolic regularities require more than energy ecology.
- **Theory of “human reason”:** materialist evolution + practical calculation + biological/psychological predispositions; rejects pure ecology, pure relativism, and short-term sociobiology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**North American ethnohistory (*Natives and Newcomers*):** dedicated to **T. F. McIlwraith** (founder of academic anthropology at Toronto); first Huron paper published 1960 through McIlwraith's offices. Interlocutors and targets include Francis [[parkman-francis|Parkman]], Charlevoix and his digests, Boasian salvage ethnography, ethnohistorians (Jennings and others as cited), and the Jesuit *Relations* as primary corpus. Students acknowledged for Ontario archaeological contributions (as listed in the 1985 preface).

**HAT / comparative:** Dialogue partners emphasized in *HAT*: R. G. Collingwood (history of historical thought); Gordon [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] (materialism + diversity); Soviet archaeology and its post-1989 reassessment; Anglophone processual ([[binford-lewis|Binford]]) and postprocessual ([[hodder-ian|Hodder]]) programs; Scandinavian founders (Thomsen, Worsaae, Montelius); Kossinna and nationalist archaeology. Formal teachers/students not fully documented (`[[unknown]]` where unstated). Acknowledgments in *HAT* 2nd ed. credit among others Alison [[wylie-alison|Wylie]], Randall McGuire, Kristian Kristiansen, Margarita Díaz-Andreu, Thomas Patterson, George Stocking.

**Comparative training (UEC preface, attributed):** undergraduate Toronto; graduate Yale under George Peter Murdock and Clellan Ford; Northwestern senior colleague Raoul Naroll. Long McGill teaching of comparative early civilizations (from 1970).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Matthew Johnson (*[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory]]*) treats Trigger 2006 as the place to start for historical perspective while disagreeing with Trigger's assessment of "where we are now." Postprocessual and hyper-relativist positions that Trigger criticizes will state their own case on [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] and related pages. Trigger's progressive narrative of increasing data constraint is itself a position in the objectivity/relativism dispute — not wiki-voice historiography.

**Ideology critique of North American prehistory (attributed — Johnson 2011, Ch.6).** Johnson cites Trigger as having "written extensively on how different interpretations of North American prehistory served to ideologically portray Native American cultures as backward and unchanging" — applying Trigger's own analytic concerns (production context, the politics of interpretation) not to a past society but to archaeology's own historical practice. Johnson presents this as a case study in [[ideology]] operating in present archaeological writing, alongside the more familiar case of ideology as a phenomenon of past societies themselves. See [[ideology]].

**Critical reviewer of Johnson's first edition (attributed — Johnson 2011).** Johnson's second edition acknowledges Trigger among critical readers who engaged with the first edition of *Archaeological Theory: An Introduction*, feeding into the second edition's revisions.

## Historiography

Standard reference as the field's preeminent historian of archaeological thought. *HAT* 1st ed. (1989) established the genre at monograph scale; 2nd ed. (2006) is the edition ingested here. *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003) is the principal comparative monograph on early class societies. *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985) is the major English-language ethnohistorical/historiographic intervention on New France and the "Heroic Age," continuous with *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]* (1976; ingested 2026-07-15; study page [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-study]]).
