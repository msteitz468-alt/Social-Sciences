# Igor Kopytoff

## Overview

Anthropologist at the University of Pennsylvania (at time of publication); co-editor with Suzanne Miers of *Slavery in Africa: Historical and Anthropological Perspectives* (1977). Author of "The cultural biography of things: commoditization as process" in *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* (1986) — the volume’s second theoretical charter, paired with [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]’s introduction.

## Contributions

### Cultural biography of things *(SLOT 1986)*

From a cultural perspective, commodities must be produced materially **and** culturally marked as a certain kind of thing. Only some things are appropriate for commodity marking; the same thing may be a commodity at one time and not another, or for one person and not another — shifts that reveal a **moral economy** behind visible transactions *(Kopytoff)*.

**Biographical questions** for things (parallel to persons): origin and maker; career so far and ideal career; recognized “ages”; how use changes with age; end of usefulness. Examples: Suku hut ten-year life cycle (housing → guest/widow → teenagers → kitchen → livestock → termites); Western judgments of a Renoir’s “proper” career; culturally divergent car biographies *(Kopytoff)*.

### Slavery as processual model

Western polarity of individualized persons vs commoditized things is recent and culturally exceptional *(Kopytoff)*. Slavery approached processually (not all-or-none property definition): **capture/sale** strips social identity → person becomes object/potential commodity → **reinsertion** into host group with new social identity (rehumanization, sometimes high status) → residual **potential recommoditization** by resale. Bridge: commoditization of other things is likewise cultural shaping of biographies *(Kopytoff)*.

### Commoditization and singularization

**[[commoditization]]** as process of becoming: (a) each thing exchangeable for more others; (b) more kinds of things more widely exchangeable. Drive toward optimum commoditization given exchange technology; money’s introduction expands internal commoditization and merges spheres *(Kopytoff, on Tiv and generally)*.

**[[singularization]]** as culture’s counterdrive: sacralization, royal monopolies, restricted spheres (Tiv prestige; kula closed sphere), terminal commoditization (no resale), individual attachment. Non-commodity = “priceless” from uniquely valuable to uniquely worthless *(Kopytoff)*.

### Spheres of exchange

Engages [[bohannan-paul|Bohannan]]’s Tiv multi-centric economy as cultural construction of separate commodity spheres where no single common standard can equate yams, ritual offices, and rights-in-persons *(Kopytoff)*.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Appadurai notes less comfort with a hard singularization vs commoditization opposition (middle-zone permanent commoditization of singularities) while accepting that the commodity is a **phase** in the life of some things *(Appadurai on Kopytoff)*. Broader reception not yet primary-ingested.
