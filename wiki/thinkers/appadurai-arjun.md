# Arjun Appadurai

## Overview

Indian-born anthropologist (b. 1949). At the publication of *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* (Cambridge, 1986) he was Associate Professor of Anthropology and South Asian Studies at the University of Pennsylvania; the preface is dated from the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences, Stanford. Editor of that volume and author of its programmatic introduction, "Commodities and the politics of value," which argues that **economic exchange creates value**, that value is embodied in commodities, and that **politics** (broadly construed) links exchange to value — so that commodities, like persons, have social lives *(attributed)*. Earlier: *Worship and Conflict Under Colonial Rule* (1981), on a South Indian temple. A Tamil Brahman raised in Bombay and "turned into *homo academicus* in the United States" (Brandeis 1967; Chicago from 1970, under "the still-dominant triumphal form of modernization theory"), he became a founder of the journal *Public Culture* and the field-defining voice of the **anthropology of globalization** with *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]* (1996).

## Contributions

### Politics of value (SLOT intro, 1986)

- **Charter claim:** focus on things exchanged (not only forms/functions of exchange) to show politics as the link between exchange and value; commodities have social lives *(Appadurai)*.
- **Simmel pivot:** economic value is never inherent in objects but arises from reciprocal sacrifice in exchange; Appadurai redirects this toward [[regimes-of-value]] across space and time *(via Simmel 1907/1978)*.
- **Methodological fetishism:** theoretically humans encode things with meaning; methodologically, **things-in-motion** illuminate human contexts — a minimum methodological fetishism corrects over-sociologizing of transactions associated with Mauss *(Appadurai, citing Firth 1983)*.
- **Commodity redefined comparatively:** a commodity is any thing **intended for exchange** / a thing in a **commodity situation** where exchangeability is the socially relevant feature — not a capitalism-only product. Disaggregates **phase**, **candidacy**, and **context**; types by destination, metamorphosis, diversion, and ex-commodities *(Appadurai)*.
- **Paths and diversions:** commodity flow as compromise between socially regulated paths and competitively inspired diversions (terms via Nancy Munn on kula). **[[tournaments-of-value]]** (kula paradigm; art auctions as analogue). **Enclaved commodities** and decommoditization from above (royal monopolies; Gluckman on Lozi “kingly things”).
- **Desire and demand:** demand as function of social practices and classifications (Baudrillard), not pure need or infinite desire; sumptuary control, fashion, knowledge politics.
- **Knowledge and commodities:** politics of value often a politics of knowledge (production knowledge vs consumption knowledge; authenticity; advertising as “fetishism of the consumer” / capitalist realism debates).
- **Conclusion:** politics links [[regimes-of-value]] to specific commodity flows; demystify the **demand side** as Marx demystified production *(Appadurai)*.

### The anthropology of globalization (*Modernity at Large*, 1996)

- **Theory of rupture.** The book proposes "a theory of rupture that takes media
  and migration as its two major, and interconnected, diacritics" and explores
  "their joint effect on the work of the imagination as a constitutive feature of
  modern subjectivity" *(Appadurai)* — an alternative to
  [[modernization-theory|modernization theory]]'s tradition/modernity break.
- **Imagination as social practice.** Imagination "has become an organized field
  of social practices… the key component of the new global order" — see
  [[imagination-as-social-practice]].
- **The five [[global-cultural-flows|-scapes]].** [[ethnoscape]], [[mediascape]],
  [[technoscape]], [[financescape]], [[ideoscape]] — perspectival landscapes whose
  relationships are "deeply disjunctive"; the source of a now-standard vocabulary.
- **[[deterritorialization]]** of persons, wealth, images, and political loyalty;
  "diasporic public spheres" and the "delocalized transnation."
- **[[culturalism]]** and the critique of primordialism (ch. 7): modern ethnicity
  as the conscious mobilization of difference, not ancient attachment — see the
  [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate|primordialism debate]].
- **The postnational** (ch. 8): the nation-state "on its last legs"; "trojan
  nationalisms"; issue-based patriotisms beyond the nation/state hyphen.
- **[[production-of-locality]]** (ch. 9): the locality/neighborhood distinction;
  locality as a fragile, produced "structure of feeling."
- **Empirical essays:** the [[vernacular-globalization|indigenization of Indian
  cricket]] (ch. 5) and [[colonial-enumeration|number in the colonial imagination]]
  (ch. 6).

### Volume editorship

Penn Ethnohistory Workshop (commodities theme proposed by Lee Cassanelli; Farriss program since 1975); May 1984 symposium (NEH + Penn SAS). Conversation with [[kopytoff-igor|Kopytoff]] and William Davenport that “the time was ripe for a revitalized anthropology of things” *(preface)*.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Appadurai’s deliberate blurring of gift and commodity, and his broad commodity definition, reopened economic anthropology’s dualisms; later material-culture and consumption studies absorbed the toolkit unevenly. Reception beyond SLOT not yet primary-ingested. Within the volume he notes limited coverage (no China/Latin America; luxury tilt).
