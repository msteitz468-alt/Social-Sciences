# Harvey Sacks (1935–1975)

American sociologist, co-founder with [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]] of
[[conversation-analysis|conversation analysis]], working out of the UCLA orbit built
around [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]]'s [[ethnomethodology]]. Sacks died in a car
accident in 1975. His chapter "On the Analyzability of Stories by Children," published in
Gumperz and Hymes's 1972 volume *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, is one of CA's founding
statements, introducing the apparatus of membership categorization devices alongside a
sequential analysis of children's story structure.

## Overview

Sacks's chapter analyzes two sentences from a 2-year-9-month-old girl's story, recorded
in Pitcher and Prelinger's *Children Tell Stories* (1963): "The baby cried. The mommy
picked it up." From this minimal data he built a formal apparatus — membership
categorization devices, rules of application, and hearer's and viewer's maxims — intended
to explain how competent hearers assign sense to person-referring terms without noticing
that an ambiguity existed. Sacks explicitly framed his five initial observations as "not
proposed as sociological findings, but rather do they pose some of the problems which
social science shall have to resolve" — a methodological self-description rather than a
claim to established results.

## Contributions

All contributions below are attributed to Sacks.

- **[[membership-categorization|Membership categorization devices (MCDs)]].** Sacks
  defined a device as "any collection of membership categories, containing at least a
  category, which may be applied to some population containing at least a member, so as
  to provide, by the use of some rules of application, for the pairing of at least a
  population member and a categorization device member. A device is then a collection
  plus rules of application." Example devices: 'sex' = {male, female}; 'family' = {baby,
  mommy, daddy, ...}.
- **The economy rule.** Sacks argued that "a single category from any membership
  categorization device can be referentially adequate" — one category suffices to refer
  to a person.
- **The consistency rule.** If a category from a device's collection has categorized one
  member of a population, other categories from that same collection may be used for
  further members of that population.
- **The hearer's maxim.** If two or more categories used for two or more members can be
  heard as belonging to the same collection, "hear them that way" — resolving lexical
  ambiguity (e.g., 'baby' as a member of both the 'family' device and the 'stage of life'
  device) without the hearer noticing that an ambiguity existed.
- **Duplicative organization and the hearer's maxim for duplicative organization.**
  Team-type devices like 'family' are counted by unit-case ("numbers of families"), not
  by individual category; members are "coincumbents" of a unit-case.
- **Category-bound activities.** Activities members take to be done by particular
  categories of members (e.g., 'cry' bound to 'baby' in the 'stage of life' device); Sacks
  used positioned-category devices (rankable categories, e.g. baby < adolescent < adult)
  to analyze departures from binding as "acting like a C" (degrading) or "acting like an
  A" (praising).
- **Viewer's maxims.** Parallel to hearer's maxims for perception of action: if a
  category-bound activity is seen being done by a member of the category to which it is
  bound, "see it that way." Sacks argued this reframes the sociological literature's usual
  focus on norms as social control, arguing instead that "our viewer's maxim suggests
  other importances of norms, for members" — norms as resources members use to see order,
  not merely constraints external to them.
- **Sequential story structure.** Sacks analyzed the small-children's stereotyped story
  opener "You know what?" via a **chaining rule** (a questioner has a reserved right to
  talk again after a reply) and the concept of a **"ticket"** — an item (e.g., "Your pants
  are on fire," an "announcement of trouble relevant to the other") that licenses a first
  utterance between parties otherwise lacking rights to talk. Sacks argued "The baby
  cried" functions as such a ticket, an announcement of trouble to the mother, making it a
  proper story-opening beginning.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under:** [[garfinkel-harold|Harold Garfinkel]] — Sacks's entire analytic
  apparatus is presented, per the editorial framing of the 1972 volume, as continuous with
  Garfinkel's ethnomethodology; Garfinkel's own thinker page names Sacks as an
  "extraordinary" resource within his UCLA circle. Sacks is also documented in CA
  historiography as having worked closely with [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]] in the
  formative period of his approach to interaction.
- **Co-founder:** with [[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]], jointly credited with
  founding [[conversation-analysis]]; Schegloff explicitly credits Sacks for the "abab"
  sequencing formula and the concept of "conditional relevance."
- **Relation to programs:** [[conversation-analysis]] and [[ethnomethodology]].

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Within the 1972 volume itself, Sacks's apparatus is presented without dissenting
commentary; his own methodological caution — that the observations "pose... problems"
rather than assert findings — is itself a form of built-in reflexive hedging about the
narrowness of a two-sentence data base. No contemporaneous critique or reappraisal is
recorded in the sources ingested so far; this section should be expanded as later CA
literature and historiography are ingested.

## Historiography

Sacks's death in 1975 left his program to be carried forward and consolidated by
[[schegloff-emanuel|Emanuel Schegloff]] and Gail Jefferson, who edited and published his
lectures posthumously. The wiki's coverage of Sacks currently rests solely on this single
1972 chapter; a fuller historiographic treatment awaits ingestion of the posthumous
*Lectures on Conversation* and secondary CA historiography.
