# Johannes Fabian

## Overview

**Johannes Fabian** (b. 1937) is a German-born, Chicago-trained anthropologist best known for the critical-theoretical treatise *[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other: How Anthropology Makes Its Object]]* (1983), one of the defining texts of anthropology's reflexive/critical turn. His ethnographic base is urban-industrial Central Africa — the Jamaa religious movement and popular culture, language, and performance in the Shaba (Katanga) region of the Congo/Zaire — but his widest influence is as a critic of the discipline's epistemology. Fabian argued (attributed) that anthropology systematically denies its subjects **[[coevalness]]** (shared present time), a device he named **[[allochronism]]**, reinforced by the **[[ethnographic-present]]** tense convention and by **[[visualism]]** (the bias toward knowledge-as-seeing).

## Contributions

- **[[coevalness]] and the denial of coevalness.** Fabian's central thesis: ethnographic fieldwork requires the sharing of Time between observer and observed, yet anthropological *writing* "construes the Other in terms of distance, spatial and temporal" — "The Other's empirical presence turns into his theoretical absence." He framed this as an *aporetic* split constituting a "cognitive complicity" with colonialism deeper than moral complicity. He also named the fieldwork/report split the **"schizogenic use of Time"** (field conceptions of Time vs. those informing published findings).
- **[[allochronism]].** The coinage for the systematic placing of the Other in another Time, distinguished from mere factual *anachronism*; diagnosed semantically (typological vocabulary), syntactically (the [[ethnographic-present]]), and stylistically ([[visualism]]).
- **[[visualism]]** and the "rhetoric of vision." A genealogy of anthropology's visual-spatial epistemology through the art of memory (Yates) and Ramist pedagogy (Ong), turning temporal relations into spatial distance.
- **Chronopolitics.** "Geopolitics has its ideological foundations in chronopolitics" — tying the discipline's temporal devices to the political economy of empire.
- **Positive program.** A processual, materialist, dialogical anthropology grounded in language-as-praxis (agreeing with Chomsky that language is not "characteristically informative"), in which "the anthropologist and his interlocutors only 'know' when they meet each other in one and the same cotemporality."
- **1971 intersubjective theses** (prefiguring the book; from "Language, History and Anthropology," attributed via Bunzl/Fabian postscript): (1) objectivity in anthropological investigation lies neither in logical consistency of theory nor givenness of data but in the foundation (*Begründung*) of human intersubjectivity; (2) objectivity is attained by entering a context of communicative interaction through language.
- **Alterity after the book (postscript).** Distinguishes allochronism from "creating" alterity — the failure is not *producing* otherness but failing to recognize its **epistemological** significance; *alius* vs. *alter*; alterity inside / outside / between / *tout court*; presence vs. re-presentation; ethnography as re-cognition/remembering; anti-inflation rule that "speaking about others needs to be backed up by speaking with others."
- **Africanist ethnography.** Jamaa movement monographs; Swahili and colonial language policy (*Language and Colonial Power*, 1986); popular theatre (*Power and Performance*, 1990); painter Tshibumba Kanda Matulu (*Remembering the Present*, 1996); African popular culture / contemporaneity (*Moments of Freedom*, 1998); exploration of Central Africa (*Out of Our Minds*, 2000) — all read by Bunzl (and Fabian himself) as putting coevalness commitments into practice rather than abandoning ethnography after critique.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Formation.** Theology and philosophy in Austria and Germany; Fabian later described (postscript, attributed) taking away a Marxist position from early Marx plus "a heavy dose of phenomenology and hermeneutics," with a vaguely Husserlian idea of *der Andere* already in hand before Chicago. PhD University of Chicago 1969 (*Charisma and Cultural Change* → *Jamaa*, 1971), initially under the influence of Parsonian systems theory tempered by Weber — which he quickly rejected after 1966–67 Jamaa fieldwork among ethnically diverse industrial workers in Shaba (movement founded by Belgian missionary Placide Tempels).

**1970s path to the book (as reconstructed by Bunzl and Fabian).** The German *Positivismusstreit* and **Jürgen Habermas**; **Wilhelm von Humboldt**'s hermeneutic philosophy of language; **Dell Hymes** on the ethnography of communication; phenomenology of **Alfred Schutz** (intersubjective shared Time); Marx on praxis. Prefigurative essays: "Language, History and Anthropology" (1971) against the "positivist-pragmatist" ethos; "How Others Die" (1972) already capitalizing "Others" and attacking survivals-logic in the anthropology of death. Ian Jarvie accused Fabian of leading a "phenomenological putsch." The book manuscript was finished **1978**, rejected thrice, published 1983 with support from Walter Ong, Edward Said, and editor Charles Webel.

**Wider interlocutors.** **[[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]** (*The Order of Things*; Bunzl notes the parallel with Said's *Orientalism*); **[[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]**'s critique of "objectivism"; **[[said-edward|Edward Said]]** (similarities of intent and method; later debate on power and interlocutors). Chief polemical targets: **[[levi-strauss-claude|Claude Lévi-Strauss]]** and [[structuralism]] ("preempting" coevalness), American [[cultural-relativism|cultural relativism]] ("circumventing" it), [[symbolic-anthropology|symbolic anthropology]] (**[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]**, **[[turner-victor|Turner]]**, **[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]**). African intellectuals (V. Y. Mudimbe, M. Owusu, Wamba-dia-Wamba). Explicitly **not** influenced by Levinas's *Le temps et l'autre* (same title, different project — see Critiques).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Time and the Other* is widely credited with naming a problem the discipline could not thereafter ignore; coevalness/allochronism entered standard critical vocabulary. Fabian was also his own sharpest critic (attributed, left unresolved in 1983): that his critique of anthropology's uses of Time might itself be *allochronic* for ignoring other cultures' time-concepts ("There is no simple way to counter that objection"); that a theory of coevalness could slide into "temporal absorption of the Other"; and — his most-cited concession — that the book has a "glaring absence of the issue of race," which he called a "lame excuse" while arguing that "a clear conception of allochronism is the prerequisite and frame for a critique of racism."

**Postscript responses (attributed).** Fabian accepts that the critique irritated colleagues who felt the discipline "unjustly maligned," and warns against **collateral damage**: terms like "savage" and "primitive" were also once critical of civilization-worship. **Marc Augé** argued Fabian's emphasis on denial of coevalness remains inside the frame it attacks and underplays modernity/contemporaneity; Fabian replies that if Augé is a further step there is little quarrel, but questions whether invalidating the first step (denouncing denial of coevalness) strengthens the second, and points to his popular-culture work as already addressing contemporaneity. On **Levinas**: shared Hegel/phenomenology background and a possible convergence that denial of coevalness is denial of otherness-as-other, but Fabian targets a discipline's research/representation contradiction, not "Western philosophy," and parts ways when Levinas moves to a transcendent Other (God). He also warns against equating allochronism with "creating alterity," against sociologizing others into strangers, and against re-philosophizing all otherness as identity work for the Self.

**Reception (Bunzl, attributed).** Bunzl places the book as both product of 1970s critical anthropology and bridge to the [[writing-culture-debate|Writing Culture]] representation crisis; notes early praise (Marcus 1984; Hanson 1984; Clifford 1986; Roche 1988) and the charge that the book is too abstract/"unethnographic" — a charge Bunzl rejects by embedding it in the Jamaa project. Bunzl claims (reception claim, not wiki-voice) that post-1980s ethnography largely abandoned the unreflected ethnographic present, historicized encounters, and made the authorial "I" the carrier of coevalness — with residual allochronism still alive outside anthropology (journalism, macroeconomics).

## Historiography

Fabian situated his own project within a lineage of critics — W. Lepenies's "End of Natural History," Ernst Bloch's *Gleichzeitigkeit*/*Ungleichzeitigkeit*, Justin Stagl's historiography of method — and read Malinowski's *Diary* as an "epistemological document" of struggle with coevalness. The book is routinely grouped with the 1986 *Writing Culture* moment as part of the crisis of ethnographic representation, though it precedes it and is theoretically distinct (political cosmology of Time more than textual experiment). Matti Bunzl's reprint foreword is the fullest single reception/history essay currently in the collection; Fabian's own 2001 reprint preface and 2006 postscript are the primary authorial afterlives.
