# Johannes Fabian

## Overview

**Johannes Fabian** (b. 1937) is a German-born, Chicago-trained anthropologist best known for the critical-theoretical treatise *[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other: How Anthropology Makes Its Object]]* (1983), one of the defining texts of anthropology's reflexive/critical turn. His ethnographic base is urban-industrial Central Africa — the Jamaa religious movement and popular culture, language, and performance in the Shaba (Katanga) region of the Congo/Zaire — but his widest influence is as a critic of the discipline's epistemology. Fabian argued (attributed) that anthropology systematically denies its subjects **[[coevalness]]** (shared present time), a device he named **[[allochronism]]**, reinforced by the **[[ethnographic-present]]** tense convention and by **[[visualism]]** (the bias toward knowledge-as-seeing).

## Contributions

- **[[coevalness]] and the denial of coevalness.** Fabian's central thesis: ethnographic fieldwork requires the sharing of Time between observer and observed, yet anthropological *writing* "construes the Other in terms of distance, spatial and temporal" — "The Other's empirical presence turns into his theoretical absence." He framed this as an *aporetic* split constituting a "cognitive complicity" with colonialism deeper than moral complicity.
- **[[allochronism]].** The coinage for the systematic placing of the Other in another Time, distinguished from mere factual *anachronism*; diagnosed semantically (typological vocabulary), syntactically (the [[ethnographic-present]]), and stylistically ([[visualism]]).
- **[[visualism]]** and the "rhetoric of vision." A genealogy of anthropology's visual-spatial epistemology through the art of memory (Yates) and Ramist pedagogy (Ong), turning temporal relations into spatial distance.
- **Chronopolitics.** "Geopolitics has its ideological foundations in chronopolitics" — tying the discipline's temporal devices to the political economy of empire.
- **Positive program.** A processual, materialist, dialogical anthropology grounded in language-as-praxis (agreeing with Chomsky that language is not "characteristically informative"), in which "the anthropologist and his interlocutors only 'know' when they meet each other in one and the same cotemporality."
- **Africanist ethnography.** Studies of the Jamaa movement, of Swahili and colonial language policy (*Language and Colonial Power*, 1986), and of popular theatre and painting (*Power and Performance*, 1990) that put his coevalness commitments into ethnographic practice.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Fabian trained at the University of Chicago (PhD 1969) and worked chiefly in the United States and, from the 1980s, at the University of Amsterdam. His argument draws teachers and interlocutors from outside anthropology as much as within it: the phenomenology of **Alfred Schutz** (intersubjective, shared Time), **[[foucault-michel|Michel Foucault]]** (the *episteme*, spatialized Time — Fabian credits *The Order of Things*), and Marx (praxis, the critique of the "contemplative stance"). He acknowledged convergence with **[[said-edward|Edward Said]]**'s *Orientalism* (discovered too late to incorporate at length), and with **[[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]**'s critique of "objectivism" and theory of practice. His chief polemical targets were **[[levi-strauss-claude|Claude Lévi-Strauss]]** and [[structuralism]] (charged with "preempting" coevalness through taxonomy), American [[cultural-relativism|cultural relativism]] and the culture-and-personality school (charged with "circumventing" it), and the [[symbolic-anthropology|symbolic anthropology]] of **[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]**, **[[turner-victor|Turner]]**, and **[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]**. He engaged African intellectuals (V. Y. Mudimbe, M. Owusu, Wamba-dia-Wamba) and discussed the work with Jürgen Habermas.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

*Time and the Other* is widely credited with naming a problem the discipline could not thereafter ignore, and coevalness/allochronism entered the standard critical vocabulary. Fabian was also his own sharpest critic, leaving several objections unresolved (attributed): that his critique of anthropology's uses of Time might itself be *allochronic* for ignoring other cultures' time-concepts ("There is no simple way to counter that objection"); that a theory of coevalness could slide into "temporal absorption of the Other"; and — his most-cited concession — that the book has a "glaring absence of the issue of race," which he attributed partly to his own training and called a "lame excuse," while arguing that "a clear conception of allochronism is the prerequisite and frame for a critique of racism." Critics note the difficulty of realizing coevalness in written ethnography and the polemical breadth of his school-by-school indictments.

## Historiography

Fabian situated his own project within a lineage of critics — W. Lepenies's "End of Natural History" (from which he differs on the cause of "temporalization"), Ernst Bloch's *Gleichzeitigkeit*/*Ungleichzeitigkeit*, and Justin Stagl's historiography of anthropological method — and read Malinowski's posthumously published *Diary* as an "epistemological document" recording a struggle with coevalness. His book is routinely grouped with the 1986 *Writing Culture* moment as part of the crisis of ethnographic representation, though it precedes and is theoretically distinct from it.
