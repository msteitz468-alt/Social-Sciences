# Arnold van Gennep

French (German-born) ethnographer and folklorist (1873–1957), known in this wiki
almost entirely through the reception of his 1909 *Les rites de passage* by
[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]], who revived and extended it half a century later.
Van Gennep worked outside the French academic establishment — a lifelong outsider to
the [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]ian school that dominated French sociology — and earned
his living substantially by translation and by his monumental folklore manual. **No
primary van Gennep source has yet been ingested**; the account below is as his scheme is
reported by Turner.

## Overview

Van Gennep's enduring contribution is the analysis of **[[rites-of-passage]]** — "rites
which accompany every change of place, state, social position and age" — as structured in
a universal three-phase sequence: **separation** (détachement from an earlier fixed point),
**margin** or **limen** (Latin for "threshold" — the ambiguous transitional phase), and
**aggregation** (reincorporation into a new stable state). His central observation was
that the *processual form* of transition rites recurs across cultures and across the very
different occasions (birth, initiation, marriage, death, seasonal change) that trigger them.

## Contributions

- **The tripartite scheme** (attributed to van Gennep): all transition rites move through
  separation → margin → aggregation; the relative weight of the three phases varies by
  occasion (e.g. funerals emphasize separation, initiations the margin, marriages
  aggregation).
- **The margin/limen** as the phase van Gennep's successors made central. Van Gennep
  identified and named it; [[turner-victor|Turner]] made **[[liminality]]** and its social
  correlate **[[communitas]]** into a general theory of social process (see
  *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]*).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Reacted against / marginal to:** the Durkheimian school ([[durkheim-emile]], Mauss),
  which largely ignored him in his lifetime; his reputation was recovered posthumously,
  above all through Anglophone anthropology's uptake of the rites-of-passage frame.
- **trained / trained_under:** not established from ingested sources — [[unknown]].
- **Revived by:** [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] (1960s–1970s); the concept of liminality
  became foundational to symbolic and performance anthropology, ritual studies, and beyond.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Turner (attributed) both adopts van Gennep's scheme wholesale and transforms it: where
  van Gennep described a *form*, Turner theorized the *content and experience* of the
  liminal phase (anti-structure, communitas), extending it from tribal ritual to complex
  societies — a move later critics charged with overgeneralization (see
  [[communitas-scale-debate]]).

## Historiography

Van Gennep's *Les rites de passage* is a rare case of a book whose influence arrived
decades after publication, carried by a later interpreter. His folklore corpus and
biography remain under-ingested here; the wiki's coverage is presently
**Turner-reception-anchored** and should be deepened when a primary source is ingested.
