# Thor Heyerdahl

## Overview

Thor Heyerdahl was a Norwegian zoologist and explorer whose 1947 Kon-Tiki balsa-raft voyage from South America was used to promote the hypothesis that Polynesia was settled from the Americas rather than from the west, per Kirch (*On the Road of the Winds*, 2017; Heyerdahl 1952). He subsequently led the 1955–56 Norwegian Archaeological Expedition to southeastern Polynesia, with Rapa Nui as its primary target and additional work at Rapa Iti and Ra'ivavae (Australs) and Hiva Oa (Marquesas). The expedition was staffed with four professional archaeologists — E. Ferdon, W. Mulloy, A. Skjølsvold, and C. Smith — who surveyed and excavated the Rano Raraku statue quarry and the Ahu Vinapu temple complex, premised on claimed architectural and statuary resemblances between Rapa Nui and South American monumental forms (Heyerdahl and Ferdon, eds., 1961, 1965).

## Contributions

- **American-origins hypothesis of Polynesian settlement** (theoretical claim, attributed to Heyerdahl) — held that Polynesians derived from American Indian populations of the Northwest Coast and/or Peru, using the Kon-Tiki voyage as a feasibility demonstration.
- **1955–56 Norwegian Archaeological Expedition** to Rapa Nui and neighboring islands — the empirical program mounted to support the hypothesis, resting on claimed parallels between Rapa Nui and South American (Tiahuanaco/Incan) architectural and statuary forms.

## Intellectual Genealogy

No teacher/student genealogy is documented in this source; Heyerdahl's argument is treated by Kirch primarily as a public and scientific controversy rather than a disciplinary lineage. See [[heyerdahl-polynesian-origins-debate]] for the dispute in full, including the opposing position (Robert Suggs, [[golson-jack|Jack Golson]], and the discipline's consensus) that Polynesia was settled from the west via island Southeast Asia and Melanesia.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Per Kirch's own historiographical assessment (attributed, not wiki voice): "What the Norwegian Expedition failed to do... was convince anyone of the veracity of Heyerdahl's South American origins hypothesis." Robert Suggs's 1960 parody (quoted by Kirch) pointed out chronological impossibilities in Heyerdahl's reconstruction (e.g., Peruvian voyagers arriving circa 380 CE while using post-750 CE Incan material culture), and [[golson-jack|Jack Golson]] (1965:78) publicly countered that the archaeological evidence instead supported settlement "by ancestral Polynesians from the west, with subsequent internal cultural developments." Kirch presents the hypothesis as decisively and durably rejected by the discipline. He explicitly cautions against conflating this rejection with a narrower, later (as-of-2016) finding noting some evidence for contact between Polynesia and the Americas — a distinct claim about limited contact, not a vindication of Heyerdahl's colonization-direction theory.
