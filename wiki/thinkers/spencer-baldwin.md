# Baldwin Spencer

**Sir Walter Baldwin Spencer** (1860–1929) was a Melbourne biologist whose fieldwork with [[gillen-frank|Frank Gillen]] among the Arrernte of Central Australia furnished the ethnographic evidence on [[totemism]] and ceremony that anchored the evolutionary debates of Frazer, Durkheim, and Freud.

## Overview

**Sir Walter Baldwin Spencer** (1860–1929) was a Melbourne biologist turned ethnographer whose fieldwork with [[gillen-frank|Frank Gillen]] among the Arrernte (Arunta) of Central Australia furnished the ethnographic evidence on [[totemism]] and the [[intichiuma]] increase ceremonies that anchored the evolutionary debates of Frazer, Durkheim, and Freud (attributed to [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking]]).

## Contributions

- Studied medicine at Owens College, then natural sciences at Oxford (Exeter College) under Henry Moseley, the Linacre Professor of Anatomy and former naturalist on HMS *Challenger*; attended [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s Oxford lectures as a student, including demonstrations of stone-tool making illustrating the comparative method and the psychic unity of man. Appointed to the new chair of biology at the University of Melbourne, 1886/87 (wiki voice; Stocking).
- Joined the Horn Scientific Expedition to central Australia (1894) as official zoologist — anthropological duties were formally assigned to E. C. Stirling, but Spencer began independent ethnographic observation. At Alice Springs he met [[gillen-frank|Frank Gillen]], and the two began a research partnership lasting until Gillen's death in 1912 (wiki voice).
- With Gillen, observed and documented the **Engwura** ceremony among the Arunta at Alice Springs, November 1896 — a multi-month initiation ceremony including [[intichiuma]] totemic-increase sequences, witnessed over roughly three months. Their findings were published as *The Native Tribes of Central Australia* (1899) and a second joint monograph following a 1901 south-to-north Australian expedition (wiki voice).
- Continued fieldwork after Gillen's death as Chief Protector of Aborigines for Australia's Northern Territory, with trips to the Macdonnell Ranges (1923) and Alice Springs (1926); died 1929 in a hut on an island off Cape Horn while doing fieldwork among the Fuegians, seeking to test Andrew Lang's high-gods/primitive-monotheism thesis against a people considered maximally "primitive" (wiki voice). He never established an academic anthropology program at Melbourne and left no direct ethnographic students — "no 'Spencerian' ethnographers" (attributed, Stocking).

### Interpretive framework and its reception (attributed)

- Spencer and Gillen interpreted [[intichiuma]] ceremonies as functionally aimed at "increasing the number of the animal or plant after which the totem is named" — increase rites for the total food supply. They explained the *churinga* (sacred totemic objects associated with the ancestral *alcheringa*/Dreaming past) via a Tylorian "external soul" framework, on the premise that "primitive man" imagines he can place his soul in a secure spot outside his body (attributed, Stocking summarizing Spencer and Gillen 1899).
- Disputing McLennan, Spencer and Gillen argued Arunta kinship terms reflected actual classificatory social organization rather than mere address-terms, concluding the observed facts "could not receive any satisfactory explanation except on the theory of the former existence of group marriage" — reinforcing the Morganian position within British anthropology (attributed).
- Among the Arunta, Spencer and Gillen found totemism had — contra the earlier assumption exemplified by the matrilineal, exogamous Urabunna totems — no connection to marriage regulation or descent: Arunta totem was assigned locally, based on where the mother believed conception had occurred (a spirit-child entering her body from the *alcheringa*). This finding was received in England as "revolutionary," overturning received principles linking totemism to marriage regulation (attributed).
- [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] used the Arunta material as a "crucial experiment" for *The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life*, treating totemism as constitutive of Aboriginal social organization and as evidence for religion's function of periodically reaffirming collective social sentiments (attributed). [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (1913) estimated that half of all anthropological theoretical work since 1899 had been based on Spencer and Gillen's book, and nine-tenths "affected or modified by it"; his own concern with reported "ignorance" of physiological paternity among "savages" was substantially sustained by their Arunta reports (attributed).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Professor of biology at Melbourne; scientific collaborator with [[gillen-frank|Frank Gillen]]; data supplier to armchair theorists including Frazer, Durkheim, and Freud.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Assessed at length in [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking's *After Tylor* (1995)]] (attributed); see that source for the historicist reconstruction of this figure's place in the transition from evolutionism to social anthropology.

T. G. H. Strehlow — son of missionary ethnographer Carl Strehlow and fluent in Western Aranda — later criticized Spencer and Gillen's ethnography as "defaced" by "many serious inaccuracies," attributing errors to their reliance on pidgin-speaking intermediaries, including on the widely cited claim of Aboriginal "nescience" of physiological paternity (attributed, Strehlow 1971 via Stocking; see also Wolfe 1994). Spencer had earlier dismissed the elder Strehlow's fieldwork as a product "entirely of the study," since Carl Strehlow, on missionary principle, never attended "pagan rites" (attributed). This reliability dispute is a candidate for a future `debates/` page on Spencer–Gillen ethnographic reliability and the Aboriginal-paternity-nescience question; it is not adjudicated here.

**Positionality note:** Gillen used racial slurs for Aboriginal people in his correspondence even while acting as a relatively interventionist Sub-Protector of Aborigines — protecting camping sites, opposing missions' "systematic degradation" of tradition, and personally regretting having encouraged looting of sacred *churinga* objects that led to an informant's killing (attributed, recorded per the Reflexivity protocol; see [[gillen-frank]]).
