# Andrew Abbott

## Overview

Andrew Delano Abbott is an American sociologist (University of Chicago; Gustavus F. and Ann M. Swift Distinguished Service Professor as of the *Time Matters* front matter), known for the historical sociology of professions (*The System of Professions*, 1988), for founding modern sociological **[[sequence-analysis]]** (with [[optimal-matching]]), and for a sustained critique of **[[general-linear-reality|general linear reality]]** collected in *[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]* (2001). Trained at Chicago under [[janowitz-morris|Morris Janowitz]]; Ph.D. dissertation on the historical transformation of American psychiatry (1982).

## Contributions

From *Time Matters* and the papers it collects (all attributed to Abbott):

- **[[general-linear-reality|GLR]]** — six-assumption ontology implicit in representational use of the general linear model (*Sociological Theory* 1988)
- **[[sequence-analysis]]** and **[[optimal-matching]]** — careers, institutionalization, cultural sequences (1983–; with Forrest, Barman, Tsay)
- **[[event-occurrence-distinction]]** and **[[colligation]]** — measurement hygiene for sequence data (1984)
- **[[seven-types-of-ambiguity]]** — multivocality of survey indicators (*Theory and Society* 1997)
- **Causal devolution** — history of sociological causalism; Durkheim *Suicide* as ANOVA-shaped paradigm; plea for description and simulation (*SMR* 1998)
- **Cases and plots** — what cases do in positivist articles; multicase narrative links; [[entity-processes]] (in Ragin & Becker 1992)
- **Temporality and process** — Bergson/Mead/Whitehead resources; stickiness of the past; structure as memory; encoding (1999)
- **[[turning-point]]** — narrative concept vs trajectory (*Comparative Social Research* 1997)
- **[[things-of-boundaries]]** — entities from boundaries (*Social Research* 1995)
- **[[narrative-positivism]]** — constructive program uniting formal methods with narrative ontology

Other major works not yet ingested: *The System of Professions* (1988), *Department and Discipline* (1999), *Chaos of Disciplines* (2001).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[janowitz-morris|Morris Janowitz]] (Chicago dissertation advisor; attributed, *Time Matters* prologue). Edward Shils also involved in dissertation acceptance (Abbott's report).
- **Formation:** Sputnik-era science aspiration; Dudley Fitts pushed him toward humanities; History and Literature major; entered sociology partly because his college lacked a department (attributed, prologue).
- **Sequence-methods contacts:** Joseph Kruskal, David Sankoff; software via David Bradley; Phipps Arabie as connector to Kruskal.
- **Interlocutors / audiences:** historical sociologists at SSHA (Dan Smith, Eric Monkkonen, Randy Roth); Susan Gal (linguistic anthropology) at Rutgers; [[ragin-charles|Ragin]] (cases conference → “What Do Cases Do?”); [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] (addressed in ch. 1 conclusion); [[harrison-white|Harrison White]] (network/structural alternative; vacancy chains); event-history community (including reported critique by Larry Wu); philosophers of history (Hempel vs Collingwood/Gallie/Mink/Danto).
- **Substantive parallel:** professions research (*System of Professions*) co-evolved with methods; flawed sequence chapters were cut from that book.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Abbott reports (attributed, prologue and epilogue) a durable nonconversation with both quantitative colleagues (“we already know / no alternative”) and qualitative colleagues (“quantification is useless in principle”). Sequence analysis's cost schemes and descriptive status remain debated. Event-history practitioners have contested aspects of the program; Abbott notes that some critiques overlook his 1984 measurement papers. *Chaos of Disciplines* (not ingested) extends his analysis of conflated dichotomies.

**Sibling program.** Abbott's temporal/processual critique of [[general-linear-reality|GLR]] runs closely parallel to [[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]]'s [[eventful-sociology|eventful sociology]] (*[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, 2005): both reject the variable-based "experimental" ontology and champion narrative, temporally heterogeneous causality. The division of labor is instructive — Abbott works from *method* (sequences, [[event-occurrence-distinction|events vs. occurrences]], [[turning-point|turning points]]), Sewell from *social ontology* (events as durable transformations of structures). Sewell cites Abbott's 1988 GLR critique approvingly.
