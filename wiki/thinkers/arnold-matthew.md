# Matthew Arnold

## Overview

Matthew Arnold (1822–1888) was a Victorian poet, school inspector, and cultural critic
whose *Culture and Anarchy* (serialized 1867–68 in *The Cornhill Magazine*, published as a
book 1869) gave "culture" its humanist, evaluative sense. He enters the history of
anthropology chiefly through [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]]'s argument that
[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s anthropological [[culture]] concept was, in its actual
usage, closer to Arnold's than to modern relativist anthropology.

## Contributions

*(Attributed — Stocking, "Matthew Arnold, E. B. Tylor, and the Uses of Invention," in
*[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race, Culture, and Evolution]]*, essay 4.)*

- **Culture as the pursuit of perfection.** Arnold's "culture" is normative, evaluative,
  and holistic — an "inward condition of mind and spirit," pursued through "getting to
  know... the best which has been thought and said in the world," and explicitly opposed to
  mechanical, material "civilization." He was a severe critic of urban industrial society
  and of the Liberal, Nonconformist "Philistine" middle class and its "fetish" of free
  trade.
- **Closer to the modern anthropological concept than Tylor's, in some respects.** Stocking
  argues the irony that Arnold's culture — despite its absolutist "pursuit of perfection" —
  carried features nearer to the modern anthropological concept than Tylor's singular,
  hierarchical, artifact-fragmenting one: it was holistic and organic (a "way of life"),
  inward and ideational rather than artifactual, and — via the Hellenism/Hebraism framework
  and Arnold's sense of critical distance from his own society — open to something like a
  culture-and-personality perspective. What full anthropological relativism additionally
  required, Stocking holds, was a later generation with closer contact to German
  transcendentalist thought and a deeper alienation from contemporary civilization than
  either Arnold or Tylor possessed.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Stocking treats Arnold's "culture" as one of two tributaries feeding the modern concept:
the German-transcendentalist / Romantic tradition (giving culture its integrative, organic,
"inner" character), against Tylor's French-Enlightenment / British-empiricist tradition
(giving the "scientific study of civilization"). He suggests, "conjecturally," that Tylor
may have chosen the title *Primitive Culture* (1871) partly as an implicit answer to
Arnold's *Culture and Anarchy*, echoing Arnold's Hellenism/Hebraism dichotomy in his own
passage on the "parting asunder" of intelligence and virtue.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Arnold's is a frankly hierarchical, absolutist concept of culture, and Stocking does not
present it as proto-relativist; the resemblance to modern anthropology is structural
(holism, ideation, way-of-life), not evaluative. Within the discipline Arnold figures as a
contrast case rather than an ancestor with a research programme.
