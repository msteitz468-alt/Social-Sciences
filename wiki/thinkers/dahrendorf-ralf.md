# Ralf Dahrendorf

## Overview

Ralf Gustav Dahrendorf (1929–2009), German-British sociologist and public intellectual, principal architect of the postwar sociological [[conflict-theory|conflict theory of class]]. His *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]* (German 1957; English author-revised edition, Stanford 1959) reconstructs class as authority-based conflict groups within [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated associations]], rejecting both Marx's property definition of class and the integration-only image of society associated with structural-functionalism. This page is built from that work; later political writings, the House of Lords career, and liberal essays are not yet ingested.

## Contributions

From *Class and Class Conflict* (attributed throughout):

- **Problem focus.** Social structures uniquely produce within themselves the elements of their supersession and change; certain conflict groups are paramount among those forces. He would replace the word "class" if he could find a better one — the concern is the problem, not the label.
- **Stratum vs class.** Stratum = descriptive hierarchical similarity (income, prestige, style of life); class = analytical interest groupings that effect structure change.
- **[[two-faces-of-society|Two faces of society]]** — integration/values ("Utopian") vs coercion/interests ("Rationalist") as co-equal heuristics; Parsons as eminent theorist of integration; this study develops the coercion face systematically ([[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]).
- **Authority basis of class.** Classes form from dichotomous authority roles in ICAs (Weber's *Herrschaftsverband*), not solely from property. Marx sustained on structure–conflict–change link; rejected on property determinant, universal history of class struggle, and revolutionary teleology.
- **[[latent-and-manifest-interests]] / [[quasi-group]]** — role interests → quasi-groups → interest groups under technical, political, and social conditions of organization.
- **[[intensity-and-violence-of-conflict]]** as independent variables; [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict|regulation]] (not resolution or suppression); pluralism vs superimposition; mobility.
- **Post-Marx structural changes:** [[decomposition-of-capital]], [[decomposition-of-labor]], [[new-middle-class]] (bureaucrats vs white-collar by authority criterion), mobility, Marshallian citizenship, institutionalized conflict.
- **Post-capitalist application:** industrial enterprise still an ICA; industrial democracy; **institutional isolation** of industry from political conflict; political ruling class as governmental elites plus interested parties; bureaucracy as service class *in* ruling side but never alone *the* ruling class; freedom vs totalitarianism as pluralism vs monism toward conflict.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Debts he records:** David Lockwood and the LSE Thursday Evening Seminar (1952–54) on Marx, Parsons, and interest groups; Center for Advanced Study Conflict Seminar (1957–58); Bendix and Lipset; continuous dialogue with [[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[weber-max|Weber]].
- **reacted_against:** Marxian property definition and revolutionary teleology; one-sided equilibrium/integration sociology; dilution of class into stratification prestige scales.
- **Adjacent dialogue partners in the book:** Geiger (institutionalization of class conflict), Renner (service class), Marshall (citizenship), Coser (functions of conflict — used critically), Burnham, Djilas, Schumpeter, Mills (power elite — zero-sum authority discussion).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Dahrendorf summarizes five early criticisms of the German edition (English preface): (1) retaining "class" without bourgeoisie/proletariat historical reference; (2) definitions of power and authority; (3) separation of industrial and political conflict; (4) application to contemporary ruling classes; (5) lack of large-scale practical inquiries. He treats these as "admittedly problematic points." Later reappraisals (not yet ingested as primary sources) often charge that authority dichotomies understate multi-dimensional stratification and that "post-capitalist" institutionalization was period-specific.
