# Annette B. Weiner

American cultural anthropologist (1933–1997) whose Trobriand restudy reopened Malinowski's most famous field site from the standpoint of women's exchange. Her first book, *[[weiner-women-of-value-1976|Women of Value, Men of Renown]]* (1976), became a reference point for [[feminist-anthropology|the anthropology of women]] and launched a career-long program on value, exchange, and what she later called **inalienable possessions**.

## Overview

Weiner did doctoral fieldwork in the village of Kwaibwaga, Kiriwina, in **1971–72** while a Ph.D. candidate at Bryn Mawr College, ~56 years after [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s 1915–18 fieldwork. Having gone to study wood carving for the tourist trade, she redirected the study on observing a women's [[sagali|mortuary distribution]] of banana-leaf bundles and skirts — [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]] that Malinowski and H. A. Powell had largely passed over. She later taught at the University of Texas at Austin and New York University, where she chaired the anthropology department.

## Contributions

- **[[womens-wealth|Women's wealth]] as a first-class economic and ritual domain** — Weiner argued that bundles of dried banana leaves and fibrous skirts, produced and controlled by women, carry explicit value and do decisive work in reproducing [[dala|matrilineal (dala)]] identity at death.
- **Exchange as [[reproduction-regeneration|reproduction of identity]]** — she distinguished exchanges that reproduce enduring, "cosmic" dala substance (a domain she associated with women) from those that generate ephemeral individual fame and renown (a domain she associated with men through [[kula]] and politics) — the thesis of the book's title.
- **A methodological critique of androcentric ethnography** — that classic fieldwork, working through male informants, rendered women's activities invisible and so misread the exchange system (feeding the reciprocity/tribute reinterpretation on [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]).
- **Inalienable possessions** (1992) — her later, widely cited generalization: certain objects are kept even while others are given, and "keeping-while-giving" anchors rank and identity; this developed out of the Trobriand women's-wealth work (attributed; post-dates the 1976 book).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Ph.D. at Bryn Mawr College; her Trobriand work sits explicitly in dialogue with [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (whom she reread page-by-page), with H. A. Powell's 1950s Kiriwina restudy, and with [[leach-edmund|Edmund Leach]] (whose hamlet-centered reading of Trobriand organization she endorsed against Malinowski/Powell). Her exchange theory engages [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] and Lévi-Strauss critically; her feminist framing aligns with the 1970s [[feminist-anthropology|anthropology-of-women]] program (Rosaldo, Lamphere, Ortner, Reiter). Her later work was a touchstone for Marilyn Strathern's *[[gender-of-the-gift|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Weiner's own restudy is the model case for how a later fieldworker can revise a canonical ethnography by attending to what the first observer omitted; it is also cited as showing the limits of the "ethnographic present," since her 1971–72 Kiriwina differed markedly from Malinowski's. Critics note her theoretical vocabulary (cosmic vs. historical time, regeneration) is interpretive and hard to falsify, and that her sharp woman/man = enduring/ephemeral mapping risks reinstating a dualism the anthropology of women elsewhere questioned (see [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]).

## Historiography

Her diaries, field materials, and the reception of *Women of Value* are treated in the study analysis at [[weiner-women-of-value-study]] and, in depth, at [[hubs/studies/weiner-women-of-value-study|the Studies Hub]]. Obituaries and disciplinary histories place her among the figures who reshaped economic anthropology and the anthropology of gender in the last quarter of the twentieth century.
