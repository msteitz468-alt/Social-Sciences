# Paul Friedrich

## Overview

American linguistic anthropologist and poet (1927–2016); professor of anthropology at the University of Chicago (M.A. in Russian, Harvard; Ph.D., Yale); taught structural linguistics and cultural anthropology at Harvard, Michigan, and Penn. Conducted two years of ethnographic fieldwork among the Tarascan Indians of Mexico; also worked on Malayalam (Malabar coast, India) and the reconstruction of Proto-Indo-European semantic systems. His contribution to *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972), "Social Context and Semantic Feature: The Russian Pronominal Usage," is a historical-philological study of the Russian *ty*/*vy* (T/V) address system.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Friedrich, 1972.)*

- Evidentiary base: a corpus of over eight thousand pages of nineteenth-century Russian realist novels (Tolstoy for high aristocracy, Dostoevsky for intermediate urban classes, Gorki for the proletariat/artisans, Leskov for clergy, Zlatovratsky and Sholokhov for peasants and Cossacks respectively), justified on the premise that Russian literary realism was ethnographically accurate by aesthetic convention. This is a **textual/archival** evidence-class study, not fieldwork — the corpus's survivorship and production-context caveats apply.
- Central analytic claim: **ten components** govern the choice between Russian second-person singular *ty* (T) and plural *vy* (V) — topic of discourse, social context of the speech event, age, generation, sex, genealogical/kinship distance, dialect, group membership, relative jural/political authority, and emotional solidarity — proposed (by analogy to Kroeber's 1909 nine kinship components) as possibly generalizing cross-linguistically to all two-pronoun address systems.
- Builds directly on and critiques **Brown and Gilman's (1960)** "The Pronouns of Power and Solidarity": argues the power/solidarity dichotomy is too coarse and offers his ten-component system as a refinement with greater predictive accuracy for the nineteenth-century Russian corpus specifically (see [[power-and-solidarity]]).
- Documents symmetrical *vy* (formal public occasions, strangers/equals, high aristocracy modeling French upper-class usage) and symmetrical *ty* (universal among young children, toward non-humans in folklore, among lower classes and same-class strangers, between siblings and most spouses, among ritual "brotherhoods").
- Identifies asymmetrical usage tracking generation, authority, and class, with a key qualitative finding: past a certain threshold of power differential (e.g., peasant to landlord, Tsar, or God), speakers reverted from *vy* to *ty* — marking either total subordination or unchallengeable intimacy, rather than the greater power that *vy* usually symbolizes.
- Analyzes eight extended literary cases of pronominal **"switching"** and **"breakthrough"** — sudden, motivated shifts in T/V usage mid-relationship (in Lermontov, Gogol, Gorki, Tolstoy, and Dostoevsky) — as diagnostic of which componential discriminations are most heavily weighted in a given relationship.
- Distinguishes overt/articulated pronoun choice from covert or **"latent" (latent-ty)** mental pronoun use, evidenced via novelistic access to interior monologue (e.g., in Tolstoy's *Resurrection*), framed as a critique of behaviorist approaches restricted to overtly articulated forms.
- Explicit methodological caveat: the inferred componential system is "a system that has been inferred by one analyst," largely subconscious to native speakers themselves — a first-person reflexivity note on the limits of textual-corpus inference.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Directly engages Brown and Gilman (1960) as the founding case of T/V address-rule study; analogizes his ten components to Kroeber's (1909) kinship-component analysis. His chapter is cross-referenced in the same volume by Ervin-Tripp (ch. 7) as a comparative case for address-rule analysis, and stands alongside Tyler's (ch. 8) componential analysis of Koya kinship-term alternation as a parallel application of context-sensitive alternation analysis to a different domain.
