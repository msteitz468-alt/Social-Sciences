# Joseph Stalin

## Overview

Iosif Vissarionovich Dzhugashvili (1878–1953), General Secretary of the Communist Party
of the Soviet Union (1922–1953). Stalin appears in this wiki not as a scholar but as the
**codifier and enforcer of official Marxist-Leninist doctrine**: texts issued under his
name defined what [[historical-materialism]] and [[dialectical-materialism]] meant for the
Soviet bloc and for much of the international communist movement, and Soviet-era social
science, linguistics, and archaeology operated under their authority.

## Contributions

- **[[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Dialectical and Historical
  Materialism]]** (1938, from the *Short Course*): the canonical schematization of
  Marxist philosophy — four features of the dialectical method, three of philosophical
  materialism, mode of production as the determining condition of social life, and the
  five-stage sequence of relations of production (primitive communal → slave → feudal →
  capitalist → Socialist). See [[dialectical-materialism]],
  [[base-and-superstructure]], [[mode-of-production]].
- **Marxism and the National Question** (1913, not yet ingested): the standard Bolshevik
  definition of a nation (common language, territory, economic life, psychological
  make-up) — long influential in Soviet nationality policy and ethnography.
- **Marxism and Problems of Linguistics** (1950, not yet ingested): repudiated N. Ya.
  Marr's "Japhetic" linguistics and removed language from the superstructure — a rare
  official *loosening* of the [[base-and-superstructure]] doctrine, consequential for
  Soviet linguistics and archaeology.
- **Personnel dictator of the party elite:** as documented in [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study|Mawdsley & White (2000)]], Stalin's consolidation used the Central Committee and apparatus (see [[circular-flow-of-power]]); the late-1930s Purges functioned as a second "elite revolution" installing the second generation ("class of '38") while the [[job-slot-system]] map of offices survived the destruction of incumbents. His 1937 "generalitet" metaphor (3–4k top leaders) is recorded as an actor's typology, not their operational N.


## Intellectual Genealogy

Self-positioned as the systematizer of [[marx-karl|Marx]], [[engels-friedrich|Engels]],
and [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] ("Marxism-Leninism" is substantially a Stalin-era
construction). No academic training or students; his "school" was enforced doctrine —
Soviet philosophers, historians, ethnographers, and archaeologists worked within the
*Short Course* framework, on pain of career or worse, from 1938 until de-Stalinization
after 1956. [[mao-zedong|Mao]]'s anthologized texts cite "Marx, Engels, Lenin and Stalin"
as universally applicable guides to action (*[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations]]*);
later Sino-Soviet split reframed that lineage (not in this Stalin source).

### Lenin's Testament (primary source, 1922–23)

The "Letter to the Congress" dictations, now ingested ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]]), carry Lenin's direct characterizations (attributed to Lenin, Dec 1922–Jan 1923): Stalin as General Secretary had "unlimited authority concentrated in his hands," and Lenin was "not sure whether he will always be capable of using that authority with sufficient caution"; the relationship between Stalin and [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]] was the chief risk of a party split. The January 4, 1923 addition recommended removing Stalin as General Secretary — "Stalin is too rude and this defect... becomes intolerable in a Secretary-General" — in favor of someone "more tolerant, more loyal, more polite." The nationalities dictation ("The Question of Nationalities or 'Autonomisation'," Dec 1922) assigned Stalin (with Dzerzhinsky) "political responsibility" for the "Great-Russian nationalist campaign" in the Georgian affair, tying his "haste and infatuation with pure administration" to the unreformed tsarist state apparatus (see [[bureaucracy]]). The dictations were suppressed in the USSR until 1956; their composition (a dying author, secretarial transmission) is part of the evidence situation.

### The succession struggle and Trotsky (as documented in Service 2009)

Robert Service ([[service-trotsky-2009]]) argues Stalin won the succession not as the impersonal instrument of "the bureaucracy" (Trotsky's own account) but through "a superior understanding of Soviet public life" — cultivating Lenin personally, controlling the apparatus, and exploiting [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]]'s tactical rigidity, ill health, and refusal to build a faction (Service's positions). Milestones: Tsaritsyn command clashes (1918), Lenin's testament codicil urging Stalin's removal (1923), defeat of the Left and United Oppositions (1923–27), Trotsky's expulsion (1927), deportation (1929), and assassination by NKVD agent Mercader in Mexico (1940), for which Service treats Stalin as instigator. Service's revisionist thesis that Trotsky and Stalin "shared more than they disagreed about" is his position, contested by Trotskyist historiography. "Socialism in one country" vs. [[permanent-revolution]] framed the doctrinal side; Trotsky's counter-analysis of the Stalinist state is at [[russian-question-debate]].

### Relation to Lenin (as documented in Service, *Lenin: A Biography* 2000)

Robert Service's Lenin biography ([[service-lenin-2000]]) narrates Stalin's rise relative to [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] as a sequence of organisational footholds, constitutional and nationalities clashes, medical-regime control, and posthumous cult construction. All interpretive framing below is Service's unless marked as brute chronology.

**Commissariats and Secretariat.** Service reports Stalin as People's Commissar for Nationalities in the early Sovnarkom, and as the leadership figure trusted for "any firm-hand organisation," alongside Sverdlov as Lenin's Secretariat/CEC right hand. After Sverdlov's death (16 March 1919), Service tracks Secretariat substitutes (Stasova; Krestinski/Serebryakov/Preobrazhenski; Molotov and others) and treats Stalin's appointment as General Secretary in 1922 as the "most fateful" of those choices — the only successor not more subordinate to Lenin than Sverdlov had been. Early Gorki visits as GenSec (red-wine hospitality) already looked, to Service, like a "good choice" for the apparatus role.

**Autonomisation, Georgia, "Great Russian chauvinism."** On the inter-republican constitution Service reports Stalin's "autonomisation" plan — fold Ukraine, Belorussia, Azerbaijan, Armenia, and Georgia into the RSFSR as autonomous units — against Lenin's equal-republic Union of Soviet Republics of Europe and Asia (RSFSR one among equals). After Orgburo ratification of Stalin's project (23 Sept 1922), Service has Lenin charge "Great Russian chauvinism"; a 26 Sept talk produced Stalin's climb-down on autonomisation and acceptance of a USSR name, while next-day Politburo notes still refused separate Union legislatures from the RSFSR and put Georgia into a Transcaucasian Federation rather than parity with the RSFSR. Stalin, per Service, still spoke of "firmness… against Ilich." The Georgian affair: Ordzhonikidze's "chauvinistic filth" toward the Georgian CC, mass resignation, a Dzerzhinsky commission that whitewashed Ordzhonikidze (including the beating of Kobachidze), and Lenin's conclusion that Stalin and Dzerzhinsky had concealed the truth and that Stalin was acting as a Russian imperialist despite being Georgian (Service's narrative of Lenin's reaction). Lenin's nationalities article in [[lenin-last-writings-1923]] and the later Georgia file are the documentary trail Service ties to these charges.

**Poison promise, Gorki, medical regime.** Service reports that Lenin extracted from Stalin a promise of poison if and when Lenin requested it (kept from doctors; Lafargue precedent), and that after the May 1922 stroke Lenin again asked Stalin for poison — Stalin, Bukharin, and Maria Ilinichna agreed to reassure him that the doctors were right and delayed suicide. Service presents Stalin as overseer of the medical regime around the dying Lenin: Gorki visits; co-control (with Kamenev and Bukharin) of the short daily dictation window after 24 Dec 1922; and the entourage politics that delayed or mediated letters.

**Krupskaya phone abuse and Testament.** Service reports that on 22 Dec 1922 Stalin discovered [[krupskaya-nadezhda|Krupskaya]]'s political assistance to the sick Lenin, phoned her, and abused her "in the language of the gutter"; her letter to Kamenev claimed thirty years without an obscene word from any comrade. When Lenin learned of the abuse (March 1923), Service has him demand apology or a break in relations; Stalin's line, per Service, was that "this isn't Lenin speaking, it's his illness." The Testament / "Letter to the Congress" ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]]) names Stalin as GenSec holding "unlimited" concentrated power, doubts whether he will always use it carefully, and in the 4 Jan 1923 addendum calls him "too crude" and proposes removal as General Secretary in favor of someone more tolerant, polite, and attentive (Service's paraphrase of Lenin's words).

**Twelfth Congress and retention of GenSec.** At the Twelfth Party Congress (1923), Service reports: Stalin delivered the national-question report; Mdivani's side was defeated; Testament characterizations reached delegation heads but not the floor; Trotsky failed to use the opening; Kamenev and Zinoviev supported Stalin against the Trotsky threat; Stalin kept the General Secretaryship.

**Mausoleum, cult, banned Testament.** Service presents Stalin as prime advocate of permanent embalming and the Red Square mausoleum (wooden, then marble 1930) as a unifying object for the USSR and world communism — against Krupskaya's objection — and as "chief priest" of the Lenin cult (writings as holy writ; "Marxism–Leninism"; manufactured image of Lenin as friend/adviser who recognised Stalin as successor). Under Stalin, Service states, the Testament was banned; the succession struggle rewrote associations around the dead leader.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- **Authorship**: scholars dispute how much of the doctrinal texts Stalin personally
  wrote versus commissioned; the codification is attributed to him canonically.
- **Doctrinal critique**: the 1938 codification is the paradigm case of "vulgar" or
  mechanical Marxism against which [[western-marxism]] (Lukács, Gramsci, the Frankfurt
  School) and [[structural-marxism]] defined themselves; critics note its omission of the
  negation of the negation and its conversion of a research program into a closed
  catechism.
- **Ethics record (factual)**: Stalin's rule encompassed the Great Terror, the Gulag
  system, mass deportations of nationalities, and the 1932–33 famines; the scholarly
  apparatus he enforced included the destruction and imprisonment of scholars (e.g., the
  purges of Soviet ethnographers and geneticists). Any use of Stalin-era Soviet
  scholarship must reckon with this production context.

## Historiography

Major biographies include Robert Service's *Stalin* and Stephen Kotkin's multi-volume
*Stalin*; on Soviet philosophy, the standard treatments distinguish the pre-1938 debates
(Deborinites vs. mechanists) from the post-1938 *Short Course* monopoly. Collection
coverage: one doctrinal pamphlet ingested; the biographical and history-of-science
literature is not yet ingested.

## Carr 1979 — rise and dictatorship pattern

[[carr-edward-hallett|E. H. Carr]], *[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979|The Russian Revolution: From Lenin to Stalin]]* (1979), is the wiki's principal secondary narrative of Stalin's rise 1922–29 (all Carr-attributed):

- **Early 1917:** With Kamenev, initially shared dual-power cooperation with the Provisional Government; Lenin's April Theses broke that line.
- **General secretary (4 April 1922):** Appointed with Molotov and Kuibyshev as secretaries after co-equal secretaries proved ineffective; "Nobody found the announcement particularly significant" — hard-working, efficient, loyal official.
- **Lenin's alarm:** After first stroke, Lenin mistrusted Stalin's concentration of power and personality; Testament (25 Dec 1922) + postscript (4 Jan 1923) proposed removing him as gen-sec; Georgia "great-power chauvinism" fight; break with Stalin.
- **Succession politics:** Triumvirate vs Trotsky; twelfth-congress modesty; *Foundations of Leninism* lectures; retention of Testament from full party circulation; fourteenth congress victory over Zinoviev–Kamenev; defeat of united [[left-opposition]] (1926–27).
- **[[socialism-in-one-country]]:** December 1924 formulation; national/psychological impact; Carr calls later Lenin-attribution **spurious**.
- **Great turn:** Siberia tour and grain crisis; "catch up and surpass" / life-and-death lag behind advanced countries; silent then "Year of the Great Break-Through"; mass [[collectivization]]; "Dizzy from Success" (March 1930).
- **Pattern of dictatorship (Ch. 17):** Post-1927 monopoly; defeat of the Right (Bukharin et al.); fiftieth-birthday adulation; skin-deep Marxism fused with Russian nationalism; **Peter the Great analogy** — "most ruthless despot Russia had known since Peter, and also a great westernizer." Carr: hold not only from astuteness/terror but from combining socialist modernization with national power; full horror of camps, show trials, orthodoxy still ahead of 1929 cutoff.
