# Karl Marx

## Overview

Karl Marx (1818–1883), German philosopher, political economist, and revolutionary, principal author of the [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848), *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte]]* (1852), and *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), and with [[engels-friedrich|Friedrich Engels]] founder of [[historical-materialism|historical materialism]]. He is a canonical founder of classical sociology’s **conflict tradition**. This page is built from the *Manifesto*, the *Brumaire*, and *Capital* I–III; the 1844 manuscripts, the *Grundrisse*, and *Theories of Surplus-Value* are not yet ingested. Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/marx-karl|Thinkers Hub biography]].

## Contributions

From the *Manifesto* (with Engels):

- History as [[class-struggle|class struggles]]; [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] as primary; [[bourgeoisie]] / [[proletariat]] dialectic; ideology thesis.
- **Asiatic / Oriental formations (via secondary historiography).** Marx and Engels wrote on Asiatic society and related categories; the full primary dossier is not yet ingested. [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]] (*[[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957|Oriental Despotism]]* Ch. 9) reconstructs Marx's [[asiatic-mode-of-production]] usage and later Soviet fights over it — treat as Wittfogel's attributed historiography until Marx's Asia writings are primary-ingested.

From the *Eighteenth Brumaire* (attributed; study [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]]):

- **[[men-make-their-own-history|Structure and agency]]** — men make history under unchosen conditions; tradition weighs on the living.
- **[[historical-costume]]** — revolutions borrow past names and costumes; 1848–51 as farce after 1789’s tragedy.
- **[[class-fraction|Class fractions]]** and the [[party-of-order|Party of Order]] — Legitimist land vs Orleanist capital; parliamentary republic as joint bourgeois rule.
- **[[bonapartism]]** and **[[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]]** — executive independence when the bourgeoisie abandons political rule; state machine perfected by successive overturns.
- **[[peasantry-as-class|Peasantry]]** (potato-bag; cannot represent themselves) and **[[lumpenproletariat]]** (Society of December 10) as bases of personal rule.
- **Smash reception (Lenin 1917).** [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] (*[[lenin-state-and-revolution-1917|State and Revolution]]*) elevates the *Brumaire* line that prior revolutions perfected the bureaucratic-military machine into the general programme that the proletariat must **[[smash-the-state-machine|smash]]** it — paired with Commune writings and the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]]. See [[marxist-theory-of-the-state]], [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].

From *Capital* I (attributed throughout):

- **Critique of political economy** — commodity as cell-form; force of abstraction; England as classic ground; aim to lay bare the economic law of motion of modern society.
- **[[labor-theory-of-value]]** — abstract labour; socially necessary labour-time; [[commodity-fetishism]].
- **[[labor-power]]** as commodity; **[[surplus-value]]** (absolute and relative); **[[constant-and-variable-capital]]**.
- Working-day struggle and Factory Acts as regulation of absolute surplus-value.
- Relative surplus-value via co-operation, manufacture, machinery.
- **[[organic-composition-of-capital]]**, **[[industrial-reserve-army]]**, **[[general-law-of-capitalist-accumulation]]**.
- **[[primitive-accumulation]]** as historical expropriation (not thrift); colonial system, public debt, protection; historical tendency that expropriators are expropriated (**position**).

From *Capital* II (posthumous, ed. Engels 1885; attributed throughout — see [[marx-capital-volume-2-1885]]):

- **[[circuit-of-capital|The circuits of capital]]** — money, productive, and commodity forms; capital "only as a movement, and not as a static thing"; the Mercantile System and classical economics each mistook one circuit form for the whole.
- **[[turnover-of-capital|Turnover]]** and **[[fixed-and-circulating-capital|fixed vs. circulating capital]]** — circulation time creates no value; the fixed/circulating distinction (mode of value-transfer) is not constant/variable, a conflation Marx charges to Smith and Ricardo; annual rate of surplus-value S′ = s′·n; fixed-capital renewal cycles as a material foundation of the business cycle.
- **[[reproduction-schemas|Reproduction schemas]]** — two-department model of the total social capital; I(v+s) = IIc under simple reproduction; critique of "Smith's dogma"; the monetary-realization problem; expanded reproduction left as an unfinished draft, seeding the [[reproduction-schemas-debate]].

From *Capital* III (posthumous, ed. Engels 1894; attributed throughout — see [[marx-capital-volume-3-1894]]):

- **[[price-of-production|Prices of production]]** — the transformation of values; total prices = total values, total profit = total surplus-value (Marx's claims); the "freemasonry" of the capitalist class; seed of the [[transformation-problem-debate]].
- **[[tendential-fall-in-the-rate-of-profit]]** — rising organic composition vs counteracting factors; "the real barrier of capitalist production is capital itself."
- **Surface forms of surplus-value** — [[merchant-capital|commercial profit]], interest and [[fictitious-capital|fictitious capital]] (credit as lever of overproduction and as abolition of private capital "within the confines" of the mode of production), [[ground-rent]] (differential and absolute; Ch. 47's genesis of capitalist rent with the "innermost secret … hidden basis of the entire social edifice" passage).
- **[[trinity-formula]]** — capital–interest, land–rent, labour–wages as "the religion of everyday life"; the realm-of-freedom/realm-of-necessity passage; the unfinished chapter on classes breaking off at "what makes a class?".

From Engels’s *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]* (posthumous use of Marx’s notes — attributed via Engels):

- **Morgan précis (1880–81).** After Marx’s death Engels found Marx’s extensive extracts from [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s *Ancient Society*; *Origin* is framed as executing Marx’s intended restatement of Morgan in materialist terms.
- **Quoted extensions.** Engels cites Marx: systems of consanguinity lag like political/juridical/religious systems; the modern family contains slavery and serfdom “in germ”; property differences within one gens transform unity of interest into antagonism among members.

Theoretical claims remain **attributed**, never wiki-voice laws of history.

### “On the Jewish Question” (1844) — via Nirenberg (attributed; primary not yet ingested)

[[nirenberg-david|Nirenberg]] (*[[nirenberg-anti-judaism-2013|Anti-Judaism]]*, 2013) reads Marx’s 1844 essay (reply to Bruno Bauer) as both insight and failure for the history of [[anti-judaism]]: (1) insight — the [[jewish-question]] is about basic tools of relation (money, property, mediation) that Christian-European culture has thought “Jewish” and that can produce “Jewishness” in their users; (2) failure — Marx exploits rather than historicizes the habit linking capitalism to Jewishness, elevating emancipation *from Judaism* into a revolutionary program and confusing figural with real Jews. Full primary ingest of the 1844 essay remains outstanding; treat the above as Nirenberg’s reading until then. Cross-links: [[jewish-question]], [[anti-judaism]], [[antisemitism]].

### Periodization as reception — Althusser’s *For Marx* (attributed)

*(Not Marx’s self-periodization; a 1960s theoretical thesis.)* [[althusser-louis|Althusser]] (*[[althusser-for-marx-1965|For Marx]]*) periodizes Marx as Early Works (1840–44; Kantian-Fichtean then Feuerbachian humanism; 1844 Manuscripts as extreme Feuerbach–Hegel synthesis), works of the [[epistemological-break]] (~1845: *Theses on Feuerbach*, *German Ideology*), transitional (1845–57), and mature (1857–83: *Capital*). He argues mature Marxism is [[theoretical-anti-humanism|theoretically anti-humanist]] and that continuous “young Marx is part of Marxism” readings confuse ideology with science ([[marxist-humanism-debate]]; [[structural-marxism]]). The 1844 Manuscripts themselves are not yet primary-ingested in this wiki.

### Reception in Lenin, *Materialism and Empirio-Criticism* (1908/1909 — attributed)

[[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]]'s *[[lenin-materialism-and-empirio-criticism-1909|Materialism and
Empirio-Criticism]]* mines Marx's early correspondence and the *Theses on Feuerbach* for
epistemological ground against the Russian Machists. Lenin quoted Marx's **Second Thesis on
Feuerbach** — "the question whether objective truth can be attributed to human thinking is
not a question of theory, but is a practical question" — as establishing the **criterion of
practice** at the basis of the materialist theory of knowledge (paired with Engels's
1888/1892 statements of the same thesis), and rebutted V. Chernov's reading of Plekhanov's
paraphrase as showing Marx diverged from Engels on the knowability of things-in-themselves,
insisting all materialists (Marx included) assert that knowability. He cited the non-Marxist
French scholar Albert Lévy's study of Feuerbach's influence on Marx as independent
corroboration that Marx's First and Second Theses presuppose things-in-themselves knowable
via practice.

Lenin also quoted Marx's **letter to Ludwig Kugelmann (December 5, 1868)**, in which Marx
praised Joseph Dietzgen's manuscript on "the faculty of thought" as containing "much that is
excellent... and admirable" despite "a certain confusion" — Lenin read that "confusion" as
Dietzgen's specific drift toward Mach/Berkeley/Hume, not toward vulgar materialism — and a
second Kugelmann letter (June 27, 1870) in which Marx attacked F. A. Lange for reducing all
of history to the biological "struggle for life" collapsed into Malthusian population
theory, calling the transfer of biological concepts into social science mere
"phrase-mongering"; Lenin applied this same critique to Bogdanov's "social energetics." A
third letter Lenin cited, **Marx to Feuerbach (October 20, 1843**, quoted via Karl Grün's
biography), shows Marx inviting Feuerbach to write against Schelling, described as a
"shallow braggart" claiming to unite/transcend philosophy-theology, materialism-idealism, and
dogmatism-scepticism — evidence, in Lenin's reading, that Marx rejected "conciliatory" middle
positions in philosophy as early as 1843. Lenin further noted that Marx's 1873 afterword to
*Capital* Vol. 1 contrasted his materialism sharply with Hegel's idealism and dismissed
Comtean positivism, concluding Marx's corpus consistently opposed materialism to idealism
without seeking a "non-partisan" third position. See [[dialectical-materialism]] for the
fuller epistemological reconstruction.

### Further Lenin reception, 1894–1918 (attributed)

Across five further Lenin texts ingested in the 2026-07-16 session, Marx recurs as authority, quoted interlocutor, and polemical target of misreading:

- **What the "Friends of the People" Are (1894, [[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894]]).** Lenin quotes the 1859 *Preface* at length as the canonical historical-materialist text (see [[historical-materialism]]) and stages the Marx–Darwin comparison — Marx as having done for society what Darwin did for species, ending both the view of society as a modifiable "mechanical aggregation of individuals" and of species as immutable. Lenin defends Marx against Krivenko's truncated quotation of Marx's 1877 letter (on Russia's possible path, published in *Yuridichesky Vestnik* 1888), citing Marx's actual words that Russia "will not succeed" avoiding proletarianization "without having first transformed a good part of her peasants into proletarians" if it takes the capitalist path — treating the question as empirical, not doctrinally predetermined. Lenin also quotes Engels's *Anti-Dühring* to show Marx grounds the "negation of the negation" reading of primitive accumulation in prior historical-factual analysis, not Hegelian deduction (see [[dialectical-materialism]]).
- **The Impending Catastrophe (1917) and "Marxism and Insurrection" (Sept. 1917, [[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]).** Lenin attributes to Marx himself the view that insurrection must be "treated as an art" — a formulation he uses to argue this is orthodox Marxism, not Blanquism, provided insurrection relies on the advanced class, a genuine revolutionary upsurge, and the enemy's maximal vacillation. Lenin also invokes Marx's description of a genuine "people's revolution" (via the *Civil War in France* material) to argue that no great revolution has occurred without civil war.
- **Can the Bolsheviks Retain State Power? (Oct. 1917, same volume).** Lenin restates Marx's Paris Commune thesis that the proletariat cannot simply seize the ready-made state machine but must smash it and build a new one (see [[smash-the-state-machine]]), and quotes Marx's maxim "Every step of real movement is more important than a dozen programmes" to argue practical accounting-and-control work under Soviet power outweighs theoretical debate.
- **Proletarian Revolution and the Renegade Kautsky (1918, [[lenin-proletarian-revolution-renegade-kautsky-1918]]).** Lenin quotes Marx's 1875 *Critique of the Gotha Programme* formulation as the decisive textual anchor of the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]]: "Between capitalist and communist society lies the period of the revolutionary transformation of the one into the other. Corresponding to this is also a political transition period in which the state can be nothing but the revolutionary dictatorship of the proletariat" — accusing Kautsky of trivializing the phrase as a mere "little word." Lenin also states that Marx and Engels reproached the Paris Commune for one cause of its downfall: that it "had not used its armed force with sufficient vigour to suppress the resistance of the exploiters," and quotes Marx's *Civil War in France* on the Commune's proposed transformation of universal suffrage — deciding administrative posts "as individual suffrage serves every other employer in the search for workers, foremen and accountants" rather than which member of the ruling class should "represent and suppress the people in Parliament." Elsewhere in the same pamphlet Lenin states Marx and Engels "repeatedly emphasised" that the first task of every victorious revolution is to smash the old army and build a new one.
- **The Tax in Kind (1921, [[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]]).** Lenin cites Marx's controversy with Rodbertus and passages in *Theories of Surplus Value* to argue that abolition of private land ownership under commodity-production conditions constitutes "nationalisation of the land" in the strict Marxist sense, distinct from "socialisation" (which he treats as only a tendency).

### The 1875 "dictatorship of the proletariat" phrase — the Lenin–Kautsky interpretive dispute (attributed both sides)

Marx's Gotha Programme transition passage became the textual battleground of the 1918 split. Lenin read it as licensing "rule based directly upon force and unrestricted by any laws" ([[lenin-proletarian-revolution-renegade-kautsky-1918]]); Kautsky read it as naming "a condition, and not a form of government," citing the Paris Commune's universal suffrage as Marx's own referent and Marx's 1872 Hague speech (peaceful attainment possible in England, America, and perhaps Holland) as showing Marx did not equate the dictatorship with the suspension of democracy ([[kautsky-dictatorship-of-the-proletariat-1918]]). Kautsky also turned the *Capital* preface ("neither clear by bold leaps, nor remove by legal enactments, the obstacles offered by the successive phases of normal development") against Bolshevik practice. Both readings are recorded as positions; the underdetermination is Marx's — as Kautsky himself noted, Marx "unfortunately omitted to specify more exactly what he conceived this dictatorship to be." See [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] and [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].

### Reception history: Lenin's marginalia on Plenge, and the "logic of Capital" aphorism (1914–16, per [[lenin-philosophical-notebooks-1933]])

Lenin's private notebooks preserve a specimen of pre-WWI German academic anti-Marx polemic. Annotating Johann Plenge's *Marx und Hegel* (Tübingen, 1911; not later than June 1916), Lenin quoted Plenge's hostile characterization of Marx as driven by "agitational motives," his historical materialism as "in its most profound basis an idealist examination of society," and — in an antisemitic framing **attributable to Plenge, not Lenin** — as "a Jewish radical healer" and "a revolutionary Jew of the nineteenth century who has re-tailored the garment borrowed from our great philosophy to suit his ends." Lenin's own marginal reaction was one of contempt for the source rather than engagement with its antisemitic framing specifically: "extremely vulgar!", "Theoretical aspect of dialectics has been overlooked!!", and, in summary, "This Plenge is an extreme vulgariser; the scientific value of his trashy book is zero" (marginal: "A pearl!!", sarcastic). Lenin quoted the material for refutation, not endorsement; the wiki reproduces it as a documented episode in Marx's reception history.

Separately, conspecting Hegel's *Science of Logic* (Bern, 1914–15), Lenin noted privately that although Marx "did not leave a 'Logic' (with a capital letter), he did leave the logic of *Capital*," which "ought to be utilised" for the theory of knowledge — adding the sharper aside: "none of the Marxists understood Marx!!" This "logic of Capital" aphorism is Lenin's own private methodological judgment, not a claim in his published work; see [[dialectical-materialism]].

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Collaborator:** [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] — *Manifesto* co-author; editor of English *Capital* and later volumes; *Origin* as fulfillment of Marx’s Morgan project.
- **Reacted against:** rival socialisms (*Manifesto* Ch. III); classical and vulgar political economy (*Capital* afterword 1873); Malthusian population dogma (reserve-army / Ireland discussion); mere “surprise” or great-man accounts of the 1851 coup (*Brumaire*).
- Continuity with 1859 *Zur Kritik*; illness delayed *Capital* (1867 preface).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- *Manifesto* predictions (immiseration, polarization, imminent revolution) did not unfold as forecast.
- *Capital* I: value theory contested; immiseration/general law contested by wage and welfare history; enclosure/colonial historiography revised.
- *Brumaire*: historiography of 1848–51 and peasant politics contested; state-autonomy readings diverge ([[relative-autonomy-of-the-state-debate]]).
- Weberian and other sociologies contest reduction of status/power to class.
- Political uses of Marx’s name after his death are reception, not entailments of 1848/1852/1867 texts.
- **Postwar amendment ([[mills-c-wright|Mills]]).** In *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]*
  (1951) C. Wright Mills extended Marxian [[alienation]] from the wage-worker to the salaried
  [[white-collar]] employee (the [[personality-market]]), but rejected the Marxian expectation of
  [[new-middle-class-politics-debate|proletarianization]], arguing propertylessness had coincided
  with rising, not falling, living standards, and amending the base/superstructure relation:
  "between consciousness and existence stand communications" ([[mass-society]]).
- **Western-Marxist revision ([[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]).** In *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional
  Man]]* (1964) Marcuse argued the working class had been *integrated* into advanced industrial
  society rather than pauperized into revolt, so it no longer functions as capitalism's
  "grave-digger" — leaving critical theory without a "demonstrable agent of change"
  ([[revolutionary-subject-debate]]). He read Marx's *Grundrisse* "Fragment on Machines" as
  anticipating that full automation would explode the value form, and held that emancipation
  requires transforming the [[technological-rationality|technological structure itself]], not
  only ownership ([[critical-theory]], [[one-dimensionality]]).
