## Overview

Sir John Marshall was a Cambridge-trained classical (Greek) archaeologist who served
as Director General of the Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) from 1902, appointed
at age 26 by Lord Curzon as part of Curzon's reorganization of the ASI. Marshall
announced the discovery of the [[harappan-civilization|Indus Civilization]] —
Mohenjo-daro and Harappa — in the *Illustrated London News* on 24 September 1924, and
directed the major 1925–26 excavation season at [[mohenjo-daro]] that recovered the
Great Bath and the "priest-king" sculpture. Birth and death dates are not given in
the source ingested here and are left `[[unknown]]`.

## Contributions

Marshall coined the term **"Indus Civilization,"** dropping the earlier working label
"Indo-Sumerian Civilization" in 1926 explicitly because that name implied a closer
connection to Sumer "than seems now justified" (Marshall's own stated reasoning, per
Possehl). Possehl notes Marshall never used the variant "Indus Valley Civilization."

Marshall authored the field's first major synthesis, *Mohenjo-daro and the Indus
Civilization* (1931), articulating what Possehl terms the "Marshall paradigm": a
reading of the civilization as strikingly uniform between Mohenjo-daro and Harappa
("to all intents and purposes identical" despite the roughly 400 miles separating
them), an assessment later endorsed by [[childe-v-gordon]] ("astonishingly
homogenous"). Marshall attributed the civilization's national/civilizational
character to naturalistic art, painted pottery, cotton use, and its script, while
treating other shared technologies (domesticated animals, wheat/barley cultivation,
wheeled vehicles, metalworking, faience and ivory work, writing itself) as "common
property of the then civilized world" rather than markers of a single polity.
Marshall's reading was of peaceful, mercantile cities without clear kingship,
bureaucracy, or theocratic rule — a position later challenged by the
Wheeler/Piggott "priest-king" paradigm of the 1940s–50s (see
[[indus-polity-debate]]).

Marshall explicitly denied any connection between Harappan language/culture and
Sanskrit or the Vedic Aryans, arguing the Indus Civilization predated the Vedic
period and represented "different peoples." He additionally speculated — an
attributed claim about linguistic identity, not a wiki-voice fact — that Harappan
speech was likely within the Dravidian group, reasoning that the area's size implied
more than one language was probably in use. Per this wiki's four-way non-identity
rule, this is recorded as an `associated_with` claim attributed to Marshall (1931),
never as an assertion that the Indus Civilization "spoke Dravidian."

Possehl praises Marshall's scholarly method as "a reasoned argument, backed by a
sophisticated use of other people's works... even when he is wrong... offers his
reasons" — an attributed, not wiki-voice, historiographic judgment.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Marshall's civilizational-uniformity thesis and his and [[wheeler-mortimer]]'s
shared "higher rainfall" hypothesis for the Mature Harappan climate were both later
rejected by [[possehl-gregory-l]] and others (Raikes and Dyson 1961; Possehl 1996b;
Misra 1984), who argue the Greater Indus region's climate was not markedly wetter in
the third millennium BCE than at present. See `[[indus-transformation-debate]]`.
Marshall's own reading of the Harappan cities as peaceful and non-state-organized was
challenged in turn by the Wheeler/Piggott "priest-king" paradigm before Possehl's
later synthesis moved back toward a stateless reading — see
`[[indus-polity-debate]]`.

## Historiography

Marshall's 1924 announcement created the first Bronze Age chronological anchor in
South Asian history predating the earliest previously secure date (326 BCE,
Alexander's raid into Gandhara); no Vedic or textual reference to a Bronze Age urban
civilization had existed. His 1931 synthesis remained the field's dominant
interpretive framework until challenged by the Wheeler/Piggott paradigm of the
1940s–50s, and was later revisited by [[possehl-gregory-l]]'s 2002 synthesis, which
partially vindicates Marshall's peaceful/non-state reading of the civilization while
firmly rejecting his climate hypothesis.
