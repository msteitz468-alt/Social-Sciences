# Margaret Mead

> Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/mead-margaret|Thinkers Hub — Margaret Mead]].

American anthropologist (1901–1978) whose *[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* (1928) and *[[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935|Sex and Temperament]]* (1935) made ethnographic comparison public arguments about adolescence, sex roles, and cultural plasticity, and helped define the [[culture-and-personality]] research program under [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]].

## Overview

Mead trained under Boas at Columbia. Her first fieldwork (nine months on Ta'ū, Manu'a Archipelago, American Samoa; return via Pago Pago 1926) produced *Coming of Age in Samoa* (1928), framed as a natural experiment testing whether adolescent [[storm-and-stress]] is biologically fixed or culturally variable. Her 1931–33 New Guinea expedition with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]] produced *Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies* (1935), a three-case comparison of how mountain [[arapesh]], river [[mundugumor]], and lake [[chambri|Tchambuli/Chambri]] standardized [[sex-temperament]]. *Growing Up in New Guinea*, wartime culture-at-a-distance studies, and later works remain for further ingestion; this summary is grounded in Samoa and *Sex and Temperament* (plus apparatus).

## Contributions

- **Culture and adolescence:** argued that the emotional turbulence of American adolescence is not a universal biological destiny but a product of specific civilizational pressures — see [[adolescence]], [[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-study]].
- **Sex and temperament:** argued that many so-called masculine and feminine traits are culturally standardized temperaments (Arapesh both mild; Mundugumor both aggressive; Tchambuli reversed relative to Western stereotypes); “human nature is almost unbelievably malleable” (Mead 1935) — see [[sex-temperament]], [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]].
- **Method as natural experiment:** Samoa one-society contrast with America; New Guinea three-society contrast design with partner division of labour.
- **Public anthropology:** wrote for parents, teachers, and general readers as well as specialists; Boas endorsed the Samoa project; *Sex and Temperament* Conclusion addresses planning, Fascism/Communism, and multi-temperament horizons.
- **[[culture-and-personality]]:** foundational cases for how culture shapes personality development, sexual mores, and sex-role ideals.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **trained_under:** [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] (Columbia; foreword to *Coming of Age*; dedication of *Sex and Temperament*); [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] (Boasian mentor/colleague; configurationism and culture-and-personality).
- **Field partner:** [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]] (1931–33 New Guinea).
- **Sepik orientation:** [[bateson-gregory|Gregory Bateson]] (Tchambuli background).
- **Boasian cohort:** [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] (senior colleague and mentor at Columbia, lifelong interlocutor; wrote the 1959 preface to *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*), Sapir, Lowie — expand as sources ingested.
- **Reacted against:** G. Stanley Hall's universal [[storm-and-stress]] account of adolescence; natural sex-temperament essentialism; Vaertings-style simple matriarchy reversal.
- **Public foil / later rival:** [[freeman-derek|Derek Freeman]]'s refutation of the Samoa work (*[[freeman-margaret-mead-and-samoa-1983|Margaret Mead and Samoa]]*, 1983, now ingested) — see [[mead-freeman-debate]]. Freeman charges her fieldwork was too brief, under-languaged, residence-compromised, and doctrinally pre-committed, and that Samoa is no "negative instance"; his own Western-Samoa, later-decade evidence base is itself contested. Record as positions, not verdict.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

`replication_status` of the Samoa findings is **reanalyzed-contested** (see study page and [[mead-freeman-debate]]). *Sex and Temperament* is likewise **reanalyzed-contested**: “too beautiful” three-case pattern (Mead 1950), uneven ethnographic depth, culture-and-personality homogenization, colonial pacification framing (especially Mundugumor). Treat all Samoa and Sepik claims as attributed and date-stamped. Do not collapse the two books’ distinct critique literatures into one verdict.

## Clifford's reading (*The Predicament of Culture*, 1988)

*(Attributed — [[clifford-james|James Clifford]], in [[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]], Chs. 1 and 10.)* Clifford records a 1939 methodological dispute: Mead argued ethnographers need not attain full vernacular fluency to "use" a language for valid research, against which Robert Lowie published a sharply critical 1940 rejoinder from the more philological Boasian tradition — Mead's looser standard prevailed in practice. A. L. Kroeber's 1931 review of *Growing Up in New Guinea* is cited praising her intuitive capacity to construct convincing cultural portraits from limited linguistic access. In the [[salvage-ethnography]] chapter, Clifford cites Mead's own account of selecting fieldwork populations for their relative lack of "civilized" contact — quoting her 1932 field report on "completing a culture" study design and her choice not to study "badly missionized" New Guinea groups — as an instance of the salvage paradigm's bias toward the seemingly "traditional." He also reads Mead's 1971 preface to *The Mountain Arapesh* (reproducing letters from Bernard Narakobi, an Arapesh law student in Sydney) as showing how her original framework had marginalized modern, hybrid Arapesh identity as merely "individual" rather than properly cultural — a critique of the salvage/wholeness bias built into the classic culture concept.

## Historiography

*Coming of Age* and *Sex and Temperament* are both discipline-internal classics and public bestsellers. Reputation history is inseparable from the Mead–Freeman controversy (Samoa-centered), from mid-century gender teaching uses of the three-tribe slogan, and from successive American readings of adolescence and sex roles.
