# Johan Gunnar Andersson

## Overview

Johan Gunnar Andersson (1874–1960) was a Swedish geologist employed by the Chinese
government from 1914 to conduct geological surveys. He participated in the early
expeditions to [[zhoukoudian|Zhoukoudian]] and, in 1921, discovered and excavated the
Yangshao Neolithic site in Henan, after which the "[[yangshao-culture|Yangshao culture]]"
was named. He was among the first-generation foreign fieldworkers who introduced modern
excavation methods to Chinese archaeology, though — per [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|
Liu & Chen 2012]] — he was not himself a trained archaeologist.

## Contributions

- **Discovery and naming of the Yangshao culture (1921).** Andersson's excavation at the
  Yangshao type-site in Henan established the culture that would anchor decades of debate
  over the origins of Chinese civilization.
- **Western-diffusion hypothesis for Chinese painted pottery (attributed, Andersson 1923).**
  Andersson argued the Yangshao material belonged to ancestors of the Han Chinese, but
  proposed that its painted pottery style was transmitted from the West, based on stylistic
  similarity to the Anau culture (Central Asia) and the Tripolje culture (southern Russia).
  This diffusion hypothesis "initiated a decades-long debate on the origins of Chinese
  culture and civilization" (Liu & Chen 2012, summarizing Andersson 1923) — recorded on
  [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]].
- **Qijia-before-Yangshao sequence (attributed, later refuted).** From Gansu-region
  findings, Andersson further proposed that Yangshao was preceded by an indigenous Qijia
  culture in the far west, implying an even more remote Western origin for Yangshao
  pottery. He first identified the Qijia culture itself at Qijiaping, Guanghe County,
  Gansu, in the 1920s, while searching for the Western origins of Yangshao painted pottery
  (Liu & Chen 2012, citing later Qijia scholarship).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Andersson worked within the Chinese government's early-20th-century program of foreign-
led geological and paleontological survey work, alongside other foreign fieldworkers
(E. Licent, P. Teilhard de Chardin, O. Zdansky, D. Black, J.F. Weidenreich) who introduced
modern field methods to China but were, per Liu & Chen, generally not trained
archaeologists themselves. No documented teacher/student links survive in the ingested
material; `trained_under` and `trained` are left empty pending further sourcing.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Andersson's Qijia-before-Yangshao sequence was decisively overturned: Xia Nai's fieldwork
in the northwest (an Academia Sinica expedition, published 1946) produced stratigraphic
evidence that the Qijia culture was in fact *later* than Yangshao, refuting Andersson's
proposed sequence — an episode Liu & Chen describe as having "become a legend, which has
inspired Chinese archaeologists for decades." More broadly, Andersson's Western-diffusion
hypothesis for Yangshao pottery was challenged by successive Chinese archaeologists,
notably in Fu Sinian's (1934) nationalist methodological critique of foreign-led,
"semi-Chinese" (*ban Han*) archaeological priorities, and was ultimately superseded by the
1930s "dual origins" model and later multiregional frameworks (Su Bingqi's *quxi leixing*;
K.C. Chang's "Chinese interaction sphere") — see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]
and [[nuclear-area]].
