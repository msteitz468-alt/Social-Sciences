# James Ferguson

American cultural/political anthropologist (b. 1959), best known for *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]* (1990), a founding case study in the **anthropology of development**. Later monographs (not yet ingested) address Zambian Copperbelt expectations of modernity, African "global shadows," and cash transfers / distributive politics.

## Overview

Ferguson analyzes **"development"** not as a policy package to be fixed, but as a discursive and institutional **apparatus** that constructs countries as objects of intervention and produces regular political effects independent of planners' stated goals. In the Lesotho case, he argues (attributed), rural-development projects depoliticize poverty while expanding bureaucratic state power — an [[anti-politics-machine|anti-politics machine]].

## Contributions

- **[[anti-politics-machine]]** — named constellation of depoliticizing representation + bureaucratic expansion as instrument-effect of development deployment (Lesotho / Thaba-Tseka).
- **[[instrument-effects]]** — Foucault-inspired reading of planned interventions' "useful" side effects (cf. prison / delinquency).
- **[[bovine-mystique]]** — ethnographic account of livestock as gendered property and power in rural Lesotho, explaining resistance to commercialization schemes.
- **[[depoliticization]]** of poverty and inequality via technical "development" framing.
- Discourse analysis of World Bank / planning constructions of Lesotho as isolated peasant economy vs. labor-reserve political economy.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Trained under:** Sally Falk Moore (Harvard thesis advisor); early guidance from Richard Huntington (suggested Lesotho); undergraduate encouragement from David Brokensha (anthropology of planned development / Africa). Ph.D. committee members Jane Guyer, Nur Yalman, David Maybury-Lewis (acknowledgments).
- **Draws on:** [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] (apparatus, [[genealogy]], [[instrument-effects]] via *Discipline and Punish*); critical political economy of southern Africa (Colin Murray, Andrew Spiegel, Charles van Onselen as interlocutors in acknowledgments); adjacent to [[post-development]] (Escobar cites Ferguson as fellow anthropologist of development) without being identical to Escobar's unmaking program.
- **Peers / network:** Liisa Malkki; readers including Colin Murray, Jean Lave, Gavin Williams, John and Jean Comaroff, Pauline Peters (acknowledgments).
