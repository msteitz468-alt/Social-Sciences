# Mary Louise Pratt

Literary critic and theorist of travel writing (b. 1948); contributor to *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* with "Fieldwork in Common Places." Analyzes ethnographic writing’s inheritance from travel genres and the personal/impersonal discursive split.

## Overview

Pratt’s essay treats ethnography’s self-definition against “mere travelers” as a rhetorical strategy that blinds the discipline to shared tropes with antecedent genres. *Imperial Eyes* (1992) not yet ingested.

## Contributions

*(Attributed to Pratt in Writing Culture 1986.)*

- Ethnography defines itself against travel, memoir, journalism, missionary accounts — and inherits their discursive practices.
- Neutral tropeless discourse is a “wild goose”; ethnographic writing is troped.
- **Arrival stories** as genre convention positioning ethnographer, native, and reader: Firth (utopian Tikopia), Malinowski (castaway), Evans-Pritchard/Maybury-Lewis (degraded), Shostak *Nisa* (double arrival).
- Personal narrative as recognized but subordinate subgenre recuperating face-to-face authority that objectifying science exorcises.
- **Donner *Shabono* controversy** as symptom: discipline rushed to fabrication on schematic true/false terms; issue is authority–experience–scientism–originality tangle, not mere accuracy.
- Travel-writing lineage: Hans Stade, Mungo Park, Mary Kingsley, Richard Burton.
- Closing program: invent new tropes, not only police old ones.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Seminar “systematic critic” roster (Clifford & Marcus Preface). Literary studies voice inside cultural-anthropology center of gravity.
