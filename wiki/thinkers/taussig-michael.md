# Michael T. Taussig

Australian-born anthropologist of capitalism, magic, and colonial violence; long associated with Columbia University. Best known for reading Latin American devil lore as a critical mediation of [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]] rather than as anxiety-relief magic or limited-good envy.

## Overview

Taussig arrived in Colombia in 1969 and conducted recurrent fieldwork among Afro-Colombian free peasants and cane workers of the southern Cauca Valley from about 1970. *[[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980|The Devil and Commodity Fetishism in South America]]* (1980; 30th anniversary ed. 2010) established his international reputation by yoking Marx's fetishism of commodities to ethnographic accounts of plantation devil contracts and (via June Nash and others) Bolivian tin-mine devil (Tio) rites. Later works (*Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man*; *Mimesis and Alterity*; fictocritical experiments) push further into terror, mimesis, and the poetics of ethnographic writing — material largely beyond the present ingest.

## Contributions

- **Devil contracts and baptism of money** as cultural critiques of wage labour and capital's barren fertility ([[devil-contract]], [[baptism-of-money]]; study [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]).
- **Use-value vs exchange-value orientations** as an ethnographic axis for reading peasant vs plantation metaphysics (attributed to Taussig's reading of Marx/Aristotle).
- **[[tio|Tio]] / mine devil** as spirit-owner of minerals under capitalist mining — synthesised especially from June Nash's mine ethnography (Nash not yet a wiki thinker page).
- Programmatic challenge to functionalist (Malinowskian anxiety; Foster limited good) reductions of magical belief.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Heavy secondary dependence for Bolivia; risk of over-generalising two regions as a single structural contrast.
- Marxist use-/exchange-value binary and "peasant mode of production" framing contested in later economic anthropology.
- 2009 self-critique of analytic distance and underdeveloped attention to narrative form and *dépense*.
- Later reception (not fully ingested here) debates romanticisation of use-value economies and the politics of reading "belief" as critique.
