# David L. Clarke

> Landmark work: **[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-study|Analytical Archaeology]]** · deep analysis [[hubs/studies/clarke-analytical-archaeology-study|Studies Hub]].

## Overview

David L. Clarke (1937–1976) was a British archaeologist at Cambridge and, alongside [[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]], a founder of the **[[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]]**. His *[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* (1968; 2nd ed. 1978, revised by Bob Chapman) imported systems theory, [[polythetic-classification|polythetic]] classification, and hierarchical entity models into archaeology and argued that archaeology should become an autonomous discipline with its own central theory rather than a servant of history or anthropology. He died in June 1976 while planning a revised edition; Chapman completed a reduced second edition that retained the theoretical spine.

## Contributions

*(Attributed unless noted as wiki-voice brute facts.)*

- **Program diagnosis.** Archaeology is an "undisciplined empirical discipline" lacking declared models, rules of procedure, and central theory synthesizing regularities (Preface, *Analytical Archaeology*).
- **Autonomy.** "Archaeology is archaeology is archaeology" — a discipline defined by archaeological data, entities, and processes, not reducible to history or anthropology.
- **Three models.** Hierarchical-polythetic entities; dynamic systems for processes; three-sphere feedback procedure (contextual / specific / hypothesis construction).
- **Entity hierarchy.** Attribute → artefact → [[artefact-type]] → [[archaeological-assemblage|assemblage]] → [[archaeological-culture|culture]] → [[culture-group]] → [[technocomplex]], each a polythetic population system.
- **Culture redefined.** Archaeological culture as polythetic set of artefact-type categories recurring in assemblages within a limited area — not a people, race, or language.
- **Technocomplex.** Named gross adaptive/technological packages that share type *families* rather than specific types.
- **Systems ontology.** Culture as "an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole"; material culture as subsystem of sociocultural information systems coupled to environment.
- **Aphorisms.** Scientific techniques "no more make archaeology into a science than a wooden leg turns a man into a tree"; questionless data accumulation valued no more than collecting engine numbers or cheese labels.
- **1973 essay.** "Archaeology: the loss of innocence" named the discipline's transition to self-conscious methodology (New Archaeology as "a set of questions rather than a set of answers" in later textbook use).
- **Philosophy of science.** Criticized wholesale imposition of Hempelian covering-law models extracted from natural sciences onto archaeology (Clarke 1972b).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Inheritance:** Cambridge archaeology; [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s hierarchical culture methodology (developed and reformed, not simply rejected); systems/cybernetics (Ashby, Wiener, Bertalanffy); numerical taxonomy (Sokal & Sneath).
- **Peers / parallels:** [[binford-lewis|Binford]] and American New Archaeology (*[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* 1968) — shared process/systems aims, different autonomy vs anthropology-as-frame emphases; [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]'s British "new prehistory."
- **Students / extension:** Influenced a generation of British quantitative, systems, and (indirectly) postprocessual archaeologists; early death curtailed direct lineage-building. Chapman as reviser of the second edition.
- **Distinguish from** [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] (Star Carr / economic prehistory) — different person.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Abstract systems vocabulary more programmatic than operational for many practitioners.
- Hierarchical culture theory later criticized for masking behavioural variability when mapped to social groups.
- Post-processual writers faulted law-like generalization and systems abstraction ([[post-processual-archaeology]]).
- Binford-side tensions over inductive classification vs hypothesis-testing priorities.
- Ch. 9 ethnology bridges (Urnfield–ethnic correlations) require four-way non-identity discipline even where Clarke used probability language.

## Johnson's textbook synthesis (2011)

*(Attributed — Matthew Johnson, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]], 2011.)* Johnson frames Clarke's 1973 article "Archaeology: the loss of innocence" as arguing that archaeology's naive equations between artefacts, cultures, and peoples needed to be questioned — Clarke's *Analytical Archaeology* opened with a Lewis Carroll epigraph ("it takes all the running you can do, to keep in the same place") to argue that accumulating more archaeological data without better theory produces no real advance in understanding: "the nebulous doubt arises in our minds that a modern empirical discipline ought to be able to aim at more rewarding results" (Clarke 1978: 3). Johnson also records Clarke's characterization of New Archaeology as **"a set of questions rather than a set of answers"** (1973) — used by Johnson to frame New Archaeology as a loosely unified school defined more by shared dissatisfaction with [[culture-historical-archaeology]] than by fixed doctrine. Elsewhere Johnson quotes Clarke's rejoinder to purely scientistic self-image: using scientific techniques "no more make archaeology into a science than a wooden leg turns a man into a tree" (Clarke 1978: 465), set against Jacquetta Hawkes's traditional/humanist position that "however scientific the methods employed, the final aims are historical" (Hawkes 1968: 257) — recorded as a position dispute, not adjudicated here. Johnson quotes Clarke (1978: 495) describing culture as "an intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole."

Note: David L. Clarke is a distinct person from Grahame Clark (of Star Carr); Johnson's text discusses both, and the two must not be conflated (see [[clark-grahame]]).
