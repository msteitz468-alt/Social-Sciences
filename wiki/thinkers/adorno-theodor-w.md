# Theodor W. Adorno

## Overview

Theodor W. Adorno (1903–1969) was a German philosopher, sociologist,
musicologist, and composer, and — with [[horkheimer-max|Max Horkheimer]] — the
central figure of the [[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt School]]'s first generation.
Trained in philosophy at Frankfurt and in composition with Alban Berg in Vienna,
he fused Hegelian-Marxist dialectics, Freudian psychology, and a modernist
aesthetics into the most uncompromising strand of [[critical-theory|Critical
Theory]]. This page is the concise network summary; the wiki's deep treatment is
the **[[hubs/thinkers/adorno-theodor-w|Thinkers Hub biography]]**. Holdings so
far derive from the single co-authored primary source
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*
(1947).

## Contributions

- **[[culture-industry|The culture industry]]** — coined with Horkheimer for the
  industrialized production of standardized mass culture (film, radio, jazz,
  magazines); the concept most identified with Adorno, extended across his music
  sociology.
- **[[dialectic-of-enlightenment|The dialectic of enlightenment]]** — the joint
  thesis that "myth is already enlightenment, and enlightenment reverts to
  mythology."
- **[[instrumental-reason]]**, **[[mimesis]]**, and **[[pseudo-individualization]]**
  — his readings of formalized reason, its repressed mimetic other, and the false
  individuality manufactured by mass culture.
- **Negative dialectics and identity-thinking** (from *Negative Dialectics*,
  1966, not yet ingested) — the critique of the "identity thinking" that
  subsumes the particular under the concept, and the demand to think "the
  nonidentical." Signalled here; developed beyond the ingested source.
- **The authoritarian personality** — his contribution (the F-scale, empirical
  social psychology) to the Berkeley study *[[unknown|The Authoritarian
  Personality]]* (1950), linking psychoanalysis and prejudice research to the
  Institut's fascism analysis.
- **Aesthetic theory** — the autonomy of the modernist artwork as the residual
  bearer of negation against the culture industry (*Aesthetic Theory*, 1970).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Adorno studied philosophy under Hans Cornelius at Frankfurt and composition under
Alban Berg in Vienna (1925), remaining a lifelong advocate of the Second Viennese
School (Schönberg, Berg, Webern). His decisive collaboration was with
[[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]], whom he joined at the Institut für
Sozialforschung, emigrating via Oxford to New York and then Los Angeles. His
closest intellectual bond was with Walter Benjamin, whose work on allegory,
aura, and mass reproduction shaped — and was sharply debated with — Adorno's
own. He drew on Hegel, [[marx-karl|Marx]], Freud, [[weber-max|Weber's]]
[[rationalization]], and (critically) Kant, Nietzsche, and Husserl. As director
of the reconstituted Institut after 1949 he trained the second generation,
including Jürgen Habermas, who would become his most consequential critic. *(The
`trained_under`/`trained` fields are marked `[[unknown]]` pending ingestion of
biographical sources; see the [[hubs/thinkers/adorno-theodor-w|hub biography]].)*

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Recorded in attributed voice: Adorno's cultural pessimism and his blanket verdict
on the culture industry (jazz especially) are charged with mandarin élitism, with
functionalist over-integration (a system depicted as seamlessly closed), and with
a performative contradiction — asserting the near-total closure of the reason his
own critique employs. Habermas's reconstruction of Critical Theory (distinguishing
instrumental from communicative rationality) is the major internal response to
the totalizing critique of reason (see
[[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]]). His 1969 confrontation with radical
students at Frankfurt — who occupied the Institut and disrupted his lectures
months before his death — became emblematic of the gap between his theory and the
activism it was said to inspire.

## Historiography

Adorno's stature has risen since the 1970s from "difficult" mandarin to a central
reference across philosophy, sociology, aesthetics, and cultural studies, aided by
the collected-works edition and by biographies (Müller-Doohm, Claussen) and the
Adorno–Benjamin and Adorno–Horkheimer correspondence. A fuller account of his
formation, the Los Angeles years, the *Authoritarian Personality* research, and
the late aesthetics is given in the
**[[hubs/thinkers/adorno-theodor-w|Thinkers Hub deep analysis]]**.
