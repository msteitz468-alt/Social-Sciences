## Overview

Ferdinand Tönnies (1855–1936) was a German sociologist best known for *Gemeinschaft
und Gesellschaft* (1887), which set out a paired-concept typology of social
relationships. This page draws entirely on Tönnies as expounded, at second hand, by
[[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] in the appended "Note on Gemeinschaft and
Gesellschaft" to Chapter XVII of [[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|*The
Structure of Social Action*]] (1937) — no primary Tönnies text has yet been ingested.
All substantive claims below are Parsons's reading of Tönnies and are recorded in
attributed voice throughout.

## Contributions

Tönnies's central dichotomy, per Parsons, distinguishes two ideal-typical modes of
social relationship. **Gesellschaft** is the rational pursuit of individual
self-interest within specifically bounded areas of a relationship — its paradigm
cases are exchange, contract, and bureaucratic authority ("Bureaucratic authority in
Weber's sense is a type case," per Parsons). Tönnies's Gesellschaft concept drew on
Hobbes, on Marx, and on Maine's notion of contract as the modern successor to status.
**Gemeinschaft**, by contrast, is broad, relatively unbounded solidarity — a
"community of fate" (Schicksal) carrying typically unspecified and unlimited mutual
obligation; its paradigm cases are marriage and family care in sickness. Tönnies
characterized Gesellschaft relations as "mechanistic" — resultants of ad hoc,
immediate action-elements within an institutional framework — and Gemeinschaft
relations as "organic," where specific acts are only intelligible within the wider,
transcending relationship.

The two types differ in how institutional control operates. Gesellschaft control
regulates specific acts, means, and ends — "formal legality." Gemeinschaft control
regulates *attitudes* rather than acts: acts are judged as expressions of attitudes
such as "love," "respect," or "filial piety," with gossip illustrating Gemeinschaft's
characteristic social-control mechanism. Gemeinschaft obligations, though unspecified
and unlimited relative to any single relation, are nonetheless bounded in practice by
the potentially conflicting claims of a person's other Gemeinschaft and ethical
relations, forming an implicit hierarchy of values — Parsons's example is a doctor
licitly subordinating a spouse's claims to patients' needs. Sexual relations
illustrate the boundary for Parsons: "prostitution" designates intercourse abstracted
from any wider Gemeinschaft context, while extramarital sex within friendship, though
condemned by mores, is never assimilated to prostitution because friendship is itself
a Gemeinschaft relation. Parsons also notes, citing Roethlisberger and Dickson and T.
N. Whitehead, that drudgery is more easily borne as an expression of a Gemeinschaft
attitude (housework for one's own family) than as an equivalent paid Gesellschaft
service — an empirical illustration Parsons supplies himself, not one drawn from
Tönnies. Gemeinschaft, per Parsons, is closely tied to traditionalism and to
religion, both marked by disinterested devotion over a broad area and a prominent
role for symbolism.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Parsons traces Tönnies's Gesellschaft concept to three sources: Hobbes (rational
self-interested action), Marx (though the specific line of debt is not detailed in
this range), and Maine's concept of contract as the culmination of the historical
shift from status to contract. Parsons further reports that [[weber-max|Max Weber]]
used a concept closely related to Gemeinschaft, but developed it "mainly on a
descriptive level," with its theoretically important implications left less explicit
in Weber than in Tönnies — which is why Parsons chose to expound the concept via
Tönnies rather than Weber directly. Parsons states that Weber's own treatment of
these problems was "substantially indebted" to Tönnies.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

Parsons deployed the Gemeinschaft/Gesellschaft dichotomy narrowly, to illustrate his
own analytical category of "modes of expression of attitudes" — closely related to,
but not identical with, [[value-orientation|norms of taste]] (Weber's Brauch), in
that Gemeinschaft carries an explicit moral/institutional element that mere taste
does not. Both, in Parsons's scheme, require what Parsons (following Sorokin) calls
the "jigsaw method" rather than direct means-end analysis. Parsons explicitly
declined to adopt the Gemeinschaft/Gesellschaft dichotomy — or Weber's related
threefold relationship typology — as an adequate general classification of social
relationships, flagging the task as beyond the scope of *The Structure of Social
Action* and reserved for future work. The dichotomy is used illustratively, not
adopted wholesale, and its status as a general sociological classification is not
adjudicated in this range.
