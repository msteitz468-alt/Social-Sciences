# Susan Gal

## Overview

American linguistic anthropologist (University of Chicago); co-author with [[irvine-judith-t|Judith Irvine]] of the load-bearing 2000 essay on language ideology and linguistic differentiation ([[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000]]). Eastern European multilingualism, language shift, and public/private language regimes feed the comparative side of the [[language-ideology]] program; the Macedonian/Balkan case in the 2000 essay draws heavily on this regional expertise.

## Contributions

- **With Irvine (2000):** [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]], [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]] as processes of ideological differentiation.
- **Macedonia / Balkan case.** Western recursive orientalism (“Near East,” “Balkan of the Balkans,” *macédoine* of peoples); Ottoman millet multilingualism vs Herderian monoglot nation; competing Serbian, Bulgarian, Greek, and Macedonian philological claims; codification as recursive purity work; practical erasure (Greek Macedonia language oaths). Linguistic scholarship as ground of political-economic contest.
- **Implications for colonial discourse and orientalism.** Linguistic ideologies help produce Europe’s self-image against an “East” that includes Asia, Africa, and Europe’s own eastern/southeastern peripheries (Implications section; links to [[orientalism]]).
- **Speech community / differentiation.** With Irvine, argues classic sociolinguistic [[speech-community]] work underplayed boundary conflict; homogeneous language is as much imagined as Anderson’s community.
- Broader program work on language shift, gender, and public/private regimes (densify when monographs ingested).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Chicago / US linguistic anthropology; SAR 1994 seminar; interlocutor with Silverstein and Kroskrity. Cites Barth (*Ethnic Groups and Boundaries*), Balkanists (Friedman, Lunt, Todorova), and orientalism historiography (Said, Wolff, Mudimbe, Olender).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

See [[language-ideology]] and triad concept pages. Macedonian case is a synthetic reading of contested nationalist scholarship — Irvine & Gal treat partisan maps and grammars as data on ideology, not as neutral dialectology.
