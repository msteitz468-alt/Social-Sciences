## Overview

Nick Patterson is an applied mathematician and statistician who became David Reich's long-term analytic collaborator in population genetics. Patterson's central contribution, as described throughout [[reich-who-we-are-2018]], was developing the statistical machinery that let Reich's lab detect and quantify ancient population mixture from genome-wide data — turning qualitative population-history hypotheses into testable statistical claims (as of Reich 2018).

## Contributions

Working with Reich's lab, Patterson developed the Three Population Test and Four Population Test, formal statistical tests for detecting deviations from a simple population-tree model — that is, for detecting admixture between lineages that a strict branching tree of populations could not otherwise explain (as of Reich 2018). These tests underpin many of the findings described in [[reich-who-we-are-2018]], including the detection of the [[ghost-population|Ancient North Eurasians]] and the finding that Europeans are more closely related to Native Americans than to East Asians at certain mutations — a result that could not be explained by a simple population tree and motivated the ghost-population hypothesis (as of Reich 2018).

Patterson is also credited by Reich with identifying a deep, roughly 50/50 mixture event between two highly differentiated hominin populations within Africa, potentially coincident with the appearance of anatomically modern human features after roughly 300,000 years ago — a finding grounded in a U-shaped mutation-frequency distribution observed in Yoruba genomic samples and corroborated by independent population-size reconstructions (Li and Durbin 2011) (as of Reich 2018). Patterson's statistical framework more broadly is described as the methodological foundation for [[admixture-statistics]] as practiced in Reich's lab.
