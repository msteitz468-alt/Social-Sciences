# Antonio Gramsci

> Encountered here as the principal source for the **[[hegemony]]** concept as reworked in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991). Italian Marxist theorist and politician; his *Prison Notebooks* are the fountainhead of the modern concept of cultural hegemony.

## Overview

Antonio Gramsci was an Italian Marxist, co-founder of the Italian Communist Party, imprisoned by the Fascist regime from 1926 until shortly before his death. In the fragmentary *Prison Notebooks* he developed an account of how ruling classes secure domination less through coercion than through **[[hegemony]]** — the organization of consent via the institutions of civil society, such that a class's worldview comes to appear as common sense.

## Contributions

- **[[hegemony]]** — the "cultural leadership" through which a dominant order makes its categories the taken-for-granted horizon of thought. Distinguished from **[[ideology]]** (articulate doctrine) and from direct domination.
- The **organic intellectual**, the **war of position**, and the analysis of "common sense" as sedimented ideology.

## Intellectual Genealogy

A founder of **Western Marxism**, moving Marxist analysis from economic determinism toward culture, consciousness, and the superstructure. His concepts were transmitted into Anglophone cultural studies (Stuart Hall, Raymond Williams) and, through them, into anthropology — where [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] made his hegemony/ideology distinction central to the analysis of colonialism (see [[colonization-of-consciousness]]).

## Critiques and Reappraisals

The notorious difficulty of establishing "what Gramsci may have meant by hegemony" — owing partly to the censored, fragmentary conditions of the *Notebooks*' composition — has licensed a wide range of later reworkings, the Comaroffs' among them.
