# Lawrence Cohen

## Overview

Lawrence Cohen is a US medical anthropologist (Associate Professor, Anthropology and South and Southeast Asian Studies, UC Berkeley, as of the *Commodifying Bodies* bio). He is known for *No Aging in India* (Victor Turner Prize 1998; AES First Book Prize 1999), for ethnography of Indian renal transplantation, and for coining and elaborating an **[[ethics-of-parts]]** — market ethics of the divisible body. With [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] he co-founded **Berkeley Organs Watch** (November 1999; Soros/Open Society funding plus UC Berkeley matching funds). Primary grounding here: “The Other Kidney: Biopolitics beyond Recognition” in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (2002), funded by Open Society Foundation grants and UC Berkeley support, developed in close conversation with Scheper-Hughes’s multi-sited Organs Watch work.

## Contributions

### Ethics of parts *(attributed — Cohen 1999; Scheper-Hughes citing Cohen; Cohen 2001/02)*

Scheper-Hughes’s volume introduction credits Cohen with a new **[[ethics-of-parts]]** in which the divisible body (and its owners) are presumed to respond rationally to market demands. Cohen’s own chapter situates Indian tissue recruitment as a relation between two moments of technique: **recognition** and **suppression**.

### Recognition → suppression; cyclosporine *(attributed — Cohen)*

- Mid-century **tissue-typing** (HLA) let transplantation escape “bleak infancy” (Fox & Swazey *The Courage to Fail*): monozygotic twins no longer necessary; recognition dispersed beyond skin/kin to cell membranes and screened populations (Haraway 1991 as interlocutor).
- Core thesis: **“Suppression, not recognition, turned transplantation into a major industry.”** The immunosuppressant **cyclosporine** (Sandoz → Novartis after Ciba-Geigy merger 1997) and combination therapies made transplants possible from far larger donor groups — shift from biopolitics of recognition (population screening for matches) to pragmatic biopolitics of **suppression** (disable the recognition apparatus; criterion becomes **operability**, not sameness/difference). Operability of the poor rises many times.
- By late 1990s, young nephrologists Cohen interviewed across India and elsewhere were abandoning rigorous HLA-matching; basic cross-match and blood-type remained standard of care. 1988 textbooks already noted cyclosporine overriding HLA matching, favoring local harvest over expensive organ-sharing.

### Multiple national biopolitics of suppression *(attributed — Cohen)*

As drugs and techniques migrate, “organ shortages” emerge with particular recruitment structures: quiet US cadaveric sales wrapped in gift/Christian rhetoric (with Scheper-Hughes); Indian state simultaneously outlawing and brokering sales (limiting foreign consumption of the poor’s organs while increasing wealthy domestic/diasporic consumption); China’s post-socialist military as transplant entrepreneur scheduling operations around festival-period public executions. Cyclosporine globalizes multiple biopolitical fields; subpopulations become chemically “same enough” for surgical disaggregation.

### India — markets, THOA, brokers, gender *(attributed — Cohen fieldwork)*

- Long-standing commercial blood donation and brokerage extended to kidneys; brokers cultivated neighborhoods, caste communities, local industries, villages.
- Chennai back-alley broker (former seller, domestic servant of a vascular surgeon) explained that only cross-matching matters under aggressive immunosuppression.
- **Transplantation of Human Organs Act (THOA), 1994**: illegal to buy/sell solid organs; related donation limited to parent, child, sibling, or spouse; exceptions vetted by state Authorization Committees. Passed amid kidney panics and reports of “kidney bazaars” in Madras and Bombay after 1980s clinic expansion. THOA linked to decline of non-South-Asian Malaysian patients seeking India operations.
- Indraprastha Apollo and “five-star” private hospital landscape (slum clearance; elite patient/guest blur); donors off-limits on Cohen’s 1998 Apollo visit; Noida scandal of alleged kidney theft/sales.
- **New York NRI** (2000): refused children’s kidneys; broker “Kidney Ravi” passed donor as family friend through Karnataka Authorization Committee; Malliyam Hospital — recipient framed as protecting family and funding donor’s children’s education (*in loco parentis*).
- Delhi teacher: “Why should I put a family member at risk when I can just buy a kidney?” — with immunosuppression, affordable “love” means never sacrificing family flesh.
- **Gendered sellers**: rural sellers primarily men (e.g. indebted cotton farmers from Rentachintala who left without telling wives); urban sellers often and in some cities primarily women (Chennai: male underemployment, alcoholism, exhausted credit). All sellers frame act as unavoidable sacrifice for those they care for. Money usually pays debts and is quickly exhausted; some ex-sellers become recruiters.
- Dual economy of sacrifice: buyers liquidate cash/jewelry for operation + organ + ongoing **cyclosporine** cost (“Suppression is expensive” — Dr Mani, Apollo Chennai: gold jewelry attenuated until sari and *mangalsutra* remain). Vertical money/organ link between class-restricted sacrifices; legible biomorality remains horizontal familial gift.
- Male fantasy of selling a kidney for sister’s wedding/dowry re-animates family form while erasing actual gender of sellers (urban Chennai majority women); fear of impotence after sale (Emergency sterilizations memory).

### Film as ethical publicity *(attributed — Cohen media analysis)*

Tissue transfer in Indian popular film as “ethical publicity” positing limits of human connectedness as a surgical problem:

- ***Sujata* (1959)** — progressive demand that caste not appear “under the microscope” in blood; transfusion recodes biomoral caste logic into national blood-group logic (Marriott dividual substance/code) — Nehruvian modernity as **recoding not decoding**.
- ***Amar Akbar Anthony* (1977)** — Hindu/Muslim/Christian brothers’ blood matches mother Bharati (“Indian”) figuring the nation.
- **1990s transplant films** — triple cost of operation, organ, cyclosporine; counter-transplant kidney sale to fund first operation (*Kareeb*; Tamil *Thullatha Manamum Thullum* — “the other kidney”).
- ***Saaheb* (1985)** — kidney sale for sister’s wedding; sacrifice re-animates joint family and traffic in women.
- Contrast: transfusion recodes nation; transplant reaffirms intrafamilial gift eroded by “Western modernity”; skin market (*Chachi 420*) gift “bears no trace of the other body.”

### Theory hinge — brain death, Agamben, zoe *(attributed — Cohen)*

Beside immunosuppression, **brain death** as tissue-recruitment invention is parallel suppression of life/death distinction (Agamben *zoe*/*bios*; *homo sacer*). For organ buying: “Zoe, the other kidney, carries no code.” Horizontal axis: sacrificial gift as *bios* (class-specific family protection); vertical axis: poor as organs bank (*zoe*). Stem-cell futurism (Dr R.R. Kishore: grow fetuses “like popcorn”) may shift high-end recruitment; present: “we selectively suppress.” Coda: “We can recruit bodies like popcorn: surgery is in your future.”

### Organs Watch co-founding *(descriptive — Scheper-Hughes 2001/02)*

With Scheper-Hughes, established Berkeley Organs Watch November 1999 (Soros Foundation + UC Berkeley Center for Human Rights, VCR, IIS matching funds) to coordinate worldwide research on transplantation practices and map organ/doctor/capital/donor routes — method dictum “follow the bodies.” Cohen is co-principal investigator on Open Society–funded field research.

## Intellectual Genealogy

- Close collaboration with [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] (Organs Watch; multi-sited transplant critical medical anthropology).
- Immuno-politics interlocutors: Haraway, Emily Martin; transplant sociology Fox & Swazey; Agamben, Foucault; Marriott (dividual substance); postcolonial science studies (Gupta, Prakash).
- Tober’s *Commodifying Bodies* chapter thanks Cohen (with Becker and Scheper-Hughes) for comments — Berkeley medical-anthropology network.
