# Robert McC. Adams

Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/adams-robert-mcc|Thinkers Hub — Robert McC. Adams]].

## Overview

Robert McCormick Adams (1926–2018), American anthropologist and archaeologist, long associated with the University of Chicago Oriental Institute (later Smithsonian leadership). Best known for **settlement archaeology** of the Mesopotamian alluvium (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*, 1981) and for **comparative urban origins**, especially the Mesopotamia–central Mexico comparison in *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966; Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures 1965).

## Contributions

- **Comparative pristine urbanism:** argued early Mesopotamia and prehispanic central Mexico share a single multilinear processual pattern — temple-centered theocracy → militaristic differentiation → conquest/trade-and-tribute states ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]; [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]).
- **Settlement survey tradition:** regional survey of watercourses, [[settlement-hierarchy|settlement hierarchies]], and land use (*[[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965|Land Behind Baghdad]]*, study [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study]]; *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*, study [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]). Diyala 1957–58: 867 sites over ~8,000 sq km in one season, methods charter in Appendices A–B; Euphrates campaigns: ~1,605 cataloged sites in the intensive zone; air-photo + ground-grid method; explicit self-stated survey limitations in both books.
- **Anti-Wittfogel irrigation thesis (1965, 1981):** already in *Land Behind Baghdad*, Early Dynastic towns arose without state-managed irrigation (flood/small-ditch irrigation within local capability), and maximal irrigation (Sasanian [[nahrawan-canal|Nahrawan]]) *followed* strong states; *Heartland* generalized it — large-scale irrigation **derivative of** prior urban/state organization; central government and irrigation system in only **limited, loose coupling**; Oriental despotism never Mesopotamian norm ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]; vs [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]]).
- **Three-epoch irrigation model (1965):** Diyala history as three successive configurations — natural-channel enclave cultivation; state-transformed maximal landscape (Seleucid-Parthian → Sasanian); post-Sasanian dissolution and artificial modern regime — urbanism "cumulative rather than merely cyclical," against Oriental-despotism stasis models.
- **Institutional focus on the Urban Revolution:** cities as containers for evolving kin, class, and polity institutions (dialogue with [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s [[urban-revolution]]); prefers cities over abstract "states" as primary analytic object (Heartland Conclusion).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Stewardian multilinear frame:** [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] on recurrent processes; against pure particularism and Morganian orthogenesis.
- **Dialogue with:** Morgan (1966 lecture frame); Childe (Urban Revolution, surplus); [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] (surplus as institutionally mobilized); Wittfogel (irrigation and power — critically); [[jacobsen-thorkild|Jacobsen]] (foreword; central-place reading of early cities); [[wright-henry-t|Wright]] (Eridu–Ur appendix); Diakonoff (rural community); Willey and surface-survey tradition.
- **Students / institutional orbit:** Chicago Near Eastern archaeology and social-science administration — detailed training chains incomplete.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Chronologies, ceramic periods, and demography superseded; absolute dates and settlement figures need specialist recalibration (Adams himself flags provisional dating and size≠population).
- Two-case 1966 comparison invites Galton and sampling critiques.
- Small-site undercount and pastoral under-detection bias the "land of cities" picture toward urban nuclei.
- Later primary-state literature multiplies pathways; theocratic→militaristic ladder is mid-century ideal type.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; Adams as cited authority on Ur III fragility, salinization, and nomad–sedentary continuum.)*

- **Ur III fragility.** Scott reports Adams’s note that Ur III’s century of five kings was an *unusual* stability record among archaic states before collapse—used to underwrite the broader consensus that first states were short-lived by default.
- **Progressive salinity.** Following Adams, Scott treats progressive salinization as a major factor in southern Mesopotamian ecological decline after ~2400 BCE: irrigation salts accumulate; barley substitution is only short-term adaptation; Ur III agricultural texts refer to brackish/salty fields to explain low yields; fallowing every second/third year required. Aligns with Adams’s long-run survey emphasis on agro-ecology under urban demand.
- **Nomad–sedentary continuum.** Scott cites Adams’s view that “the connection between nomads and sedentaries was a two-way street, with individuals and groups moving back and forth… as a response to environmental and social pressure”—parallel to Lattimore on the Han–Mongol frontier; “retrogression” to [[pastoralism]] may be prudent adaptation, not civilizational failure. Links: [[societal-collapse]] · [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] · [[pastoralism]].

### Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]], who leans heavily on Adams for the [[survey-archaeology]] method tradition.)* Matthews follows Adams (1988: 42) in arguing texts should be treated as archaeological artefacts in their own right — "texts are archaeological artefacts with meaningful contexts and associations well beyond the nature of their content" — and cites Adams's related call for better excavation-context publication of cuneiform finds. He also credits Adams (1981: 76–78) as an early critic of administrative-reductionism in state-origins research, arguing that administrative routines cannot represent the full complexity of a polity and that such models neglect the role of cult and religious organization; and cites Adams's caution against assuming ancient empires practiced "maximum milking of peripheries for minimum input" (Adams 1979: 395) given the paucity of data on actual tribute-exaction scale.


## Reception in McIntosh 2005

R.J. McIntosh (*[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]*) treats Adams (with Nissen) *Uruk Countryside* hinterland survey as industry-standard arid-floodplain method and reads early–middle Uruk clustering as a “lost opportunity” relative to Middle Niger polynucleation — before Early Dynastic nucleation. Comparative, not a reanalysis of Adams’s primary data.
