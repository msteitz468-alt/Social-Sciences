# Robert McC. Adams

Deep analysis: [[hubs/thinkers/adams-robert-mcc|Thinkers Hub — Robert McC. Adams]].

## Overview

Robert McCormick Adams (1926–2018), American anthropologist and archaeologist, long associated with the University of Chicago Oriental Institute (later Smithsonian leadership). Best known for **settlement archaeology** of the Mesopotamian alluvium (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*, 1981) and for **comparative urban origins**, especially the Mesopotamia–central Mexico comparison in *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966; Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures 1965).

## Contributions

- **Comparative pristine urbanism:** argued early Mesopotamia and prehispanic central Mexico share a single multilinear processual pattern — temple-centered theocracy → militaristic differentiation → conquest/trade-and-tribute states ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]; [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]).
- **Settlement survey tradition:** regional survey of watercourses, [[settlement-hierarchy|settlement hierarchies]], and land use (*Land behind Baghdad*; *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*; study [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]). ~1,605 cataloged sites in the intensive zone; air-photo + ground-grid method; seven explicit survey limitations (ch. 2).
- **Anti-Wittfogel irrigation thesis (1981):** large-scale irrigation **derivative of** prior urban/state organization; central government and irrigation system in only **limited, loose coupling**; Oriental despotism never Mesopotamian norm ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]; vs [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]]).
- **Institutional focus on the Urban Revolution:** cities as containers for evolving kin, class, and polity institutions (dialogue with [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s [[urban-revolution]]); prefers cities over abstract "states" as primary analytic object (Heartland Conclusion).

## Intellectual Genealogy

- **Stewardian multilinear frame:** [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] on recurrent processes; against pure particularism and Morganian orthogenesis.
- **Dialogue with:** Morgan (1966 lecture frame); Childe (Urban Revolution, surplus); [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] (surplus as institutionally mobilized); Wittfogel (irrigation and power — critically); [[jacobsen-thorkild|Jacobsen]] (foreword; central-place reading of early cities); [[wright-henry-t|Wright]] (Eridu–Ur appendix); Diakonoff (rural community); Willey and surface-survey tradition.
- **Students / institutional orbit:** Chicago Near Eastern archaeology and social-science administration — detailed training chains incomplete.

## Critiques and Reappraisals

- Chronologies, ceramic periods, and demography superseded; absolute dates and settlement figures need specialist recalibration (Adams himself flags provisional dating and size≠population).
- Two-case 1966 comparison invites Galton and sampling critiques.
- Small-site undercount and pastoral under-detection bias the "land of cities" picture toward urban nuclei.
- Later primary-state literature multiplies pathways; theocratic→militaristic ladder is mid-century ideal type.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; Adams as cited authority on Ur III fragility, salinization, and nomad–sedentary continuum.)*

- **Ur III fragility.** Scott reports Adams’s note that Ur III’s century of five kings was an *unusual* stability record among archaic states before collapse—used to underwrite the broader consensus that first states were short-lived by default.
- **Progressive salinity.** Following Adams, Scott treats progressive salinization as a major factor in southern Mesopotamian ecological decline after ~2400 BCE: irrigation salts accumulate; barley substitution is only short-term adaptation; Ur III agricultural texts refer to brackish/salty fields to explain low yields; fallowing every second/third year required. Aligns with Adams’s long-run survey emphasis on agro-ecology under urban demand.
- **Nomad–sedentary continuum.** Scott cites Adams’s view that “the connection between nomads and sedentaries was a two-way street, with individuals and groups moving back and forth… as a response to environmental and social pressure”—parallel to Lattimore on the Han–Mongol frontier; “retrogression” to [[pastoralism]] may be prudent adaptation, not civilizational failure.
