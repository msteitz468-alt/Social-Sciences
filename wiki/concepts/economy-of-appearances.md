# Economy of Appearances

## Definition as Coined

[[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] (*[[tsing-friction-2005|Friction]]*, Ch. 2; earlier *Public Culture*) names the **economy of appearances** the dependence of speculative resource and start-up capital on *performance* in a double sense: economic performance and theatrical spectacle. Profit must be **imagined before it is extracted**; firms, regions, and nations stage their potential to attract creative investment capital *(attributed)*.

Exemplary case: the **Bre-X** Busang gold scandal — Canadian junior mining, Indonesian frontier, spectacular accumulation — set against Freeport’s steadier “civilizing” corporate culture in West Papua as Tsing reads it (Americanization, company towns, Jim Bob Moffett’s rhetoric). Regional Kalimantan frontier culture (anti-local renaming of landscape, migrant optimism, elite and military partnerships, Contracts of Work / concession nepotism) supplies the landscape in which miracle-nation dreams and Canadian speculation become real enough to act on.

New Order “appearances” also include plantations and reforestation programs that may be as much staged products of funding regimes as durable production (palm oil, pulp/acacia clones, disease vulnerability as metaphor).

## Semantic History

Formulated for millennial speculative capitalism and the Indonesian miracle/crisis conjuncture. Related to but not identical with Debordian spectacle, Harvey on accumulation, or Appadurai’s tournaments of value. Articulation analysis (Hall) appears in Tsing’s apparatus for linking scales of appearance.

## Operationalizations

Case-based: Bre-X drama; national miracle staging; regional boosterism; frontier discovery narrative vs established corporate civilization narrative.

## Applications

Resource-frontier finance; startup culture analogies; critique of development as staged national performance; link from frontier resource-making ([[capitalist-frontier]]) to global capital markets.

## Critiques and Limitations

Case-heavy on spectacular fraud; generalizability to non-speculative sectors needs care. Freeport contrast is Tsing’s reading of corporate culture, not independent long-term corporate ethnography (corporate doors largely closed to her fieldwork).
