# Reflexive Modernization

Coined by [[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]] in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]* (1986) and developed jointly with [[giddens-anthony|Anthony Giddens]] and Scott Lash (*Reflexive Modernization*, 1994). The master concept beneath both the [[risk-society|risk-society]] and the [[individualization]] theses.

## Definition as Coined

Beck argued that "just as modernization dissolved the structure of feudal society in the nineteenth century and produced the industrial society, modernization today is dissolving industrial society and another modernity is coming into being" (Beck 1986: 12). Reflexive modernization is this second, self-confronting phase: "**Modernization is becoming reflexive; it is becoming its own theme**" (Beck 1986: 20). The clearest programmatic formulation: "Modernization within the horizon of experience of pre-modernity is being displaced by reflexive modernization."

**Crucial disambiguation (Beck's own):** "reflexive" here does *not* primarily mean conscious *reflection* on modernity. It names the way modernization *confronts and undermines its own effects and foundations* — often unintentionally, through side effects. Reflexivity in the sense of deliberate self-critique is a possible *consequence* of, not the definition of, reflexive modernization.

## Semantic History

The term has two registers that are easy to conflate. In Beck's usage the accent is on **self-confrontation** (modernity encountering its manufactured risks and dissolving its own class/family/gender structures). In [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s parallel usage the accent falls more on **institutionalized reflexivity** — the chronic revision of social practice in light of incoming knowledge (grounded in his prior concepts of the [[double-hermeneutic]] and [[ontological-security]]). Beck credited the subsequent development of the term to Giddens (1990, 1991) and Lash (1992) in a footnote to *Risk Society*. The two strands were consolidated, without being fully merged, in the co-authored *Reflexive Modernization* (1994).

### Giddens 1990 primary ingest (*[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|Consequences of Modernity]]*)

*(Attributed.)* Giddens listed the **reflexive ordering and reordering of social relations** in light of continual knowledge inputs as one of three sources of modernity’s dynamism (with time-space separation/recombination and [[disembedding]]). He argued that under modernity, tradition is increasingly filtered through knowledge claims rather than taken as binding formula; no knowledge is "certain" in the sense that classical Enlightenment confidence assumed; the [[double-hermeneutic]] ensures social-science and expert knowledge spiral back into the practices they describe, energising *and* destabilising the social world ([[juggernaut-of-modernity]]). His preferred label for the present is **[[radicalised-modernity]]** / high modernity, not "reflexive modernization" as Beck’s brand name — but the programs are siblings. He engages Beck explicitly on global risk ("Chernobyl is everywhere"; no "others").

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. It is indexed indirectly through the phenomena Beck treats as its symptoms: the emergence of manufactured, boundary-crossing risks; [[individualization]] and the [[detraditionalization|destandardization]] of class, gender, family, and labor; and [[reflexive-scientization]] (the demonopolization of scientific authority). New social movements (ecology, peace, feminism) are read by Beck not as anti-modern but as "an expression of reflexive modernization beyond the outlines of industrial society."

## Applications

Beck organized the whole of *Risk Society* as an application: Part I to hazard distribution, Part II to individualization, Part III to the "generalization of science and politics." The concept became foundational for the sociology of modernity, environmental sociology, and science-and-technology studies, and underwrote a three-stage periodization — pre-modernity → simple (industrial) modernity → reflexive (risk) modernity.

## Critiques and Limitations

The framework has been criticized for its optimistic reading of reflexivity's emancipatory potential (Lash and Wynne on the ambivalent, socially-constrained character of lay reflexivity); for a Eurocentric periodization; and, via the [[individualization-death-of-class-debate|individualization thesis]], for overstating the erosion of class structure. The ambiguity between Beck's "self-confrontation" and Giddens's "institutionalized reflexivity" senses is itself a standing source of confusion. Do **not** conflate with the distinct [[reflexive-sociology|reflexive sociology]] of [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], which concerns the researcher's reflexive objectivation of their own position, not an epochal transformation of modernity.
