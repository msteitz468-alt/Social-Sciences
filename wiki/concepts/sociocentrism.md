# Sociocentrism

## Definition as Coined

In the Conclusions of *[[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-1903|Primitive Classification]]* (1903), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] write that the center of the first schemes of nature is not the individual but the group — a pattern "better… called **sociocentrism**" than anthropocentrism (attributed). Things are classified by their relation to the social collectivity; the group is both model and center of the classificatory scheme.

Related to, but not identical with, [[ethnocentrism]] (Sumner) as in-group moral preference: here the stress is **cognitive/morphological** (classes modeled on social form and centered on the group), not only evaluation of outsiders.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| D&M 1903 Conclusions | Group-centered schemes of nature; counterpart to the social-origin thesis |
| Later Durkheimian usage | Rarely a standalone technical term; often absorbed into collective-representation talk |
| Contrast with ethnocentrism | Sumnerian ethnocentrism = we-group valuation; sociocentrism = group as cognitive center/model |
| Needham reception | Term noted within the broader affectivity/sociologistic package under critique |

## Operationalizations

- Spatial metaphors of the group as "navel" or center of the world (D&M note the idea is "perfectly natural everywhere" even when less intelligible for some place-names).
- Classification systems that assign all of nature to moieties/clans/regions of *this* society.
- Hard to measure independently of the full social-origin package — few later indices labeled "sociocentrism" in this sense.

## Applications

- Caps the 1903 argument: not only form of classes but *centering* of the cosmos on society.
- Useful contrast concept when teaching [[ethnocentrism]] vs cognitive group-centering.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Introduced briefly at the end of the essay; thin ethnographic development.
- Shares the contested causal architecture of [[primitive-classification]] ([[social-origin-of-categories-debate]]).
- Risk of conflation with ethnocentrism in casual use — keep the cognitive vs evaluative distinction.
