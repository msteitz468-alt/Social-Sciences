# High-Level Theory (Archaeology)

## Definition as Coined

**High-level theory** in [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]]'s scheme (*[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]]* 2006) names general frameworks about human behavior, culture, and history — materialism vs idealism, evolutionisms, structural Marxism, hermeneutic interpretation, etc. — that orient what archaeologists take data to mean. It sits above [[middle-ranging-theory-trigger|middle-ranging theory]] and archaeological data in Trigger's nested account of interpretation.

## Semantic History

Related to broader social-science talk of grand theory vs middle-range theory (Mertonian echo, not identity). Trigger insists archaeology cannot ignore high-level theory; debates between [[processual-archaeology|processual]] and [[post-processual-archaeology|postprocessual]] programs are largely high-level conflicts. He maps a materialist–idealist spectrum and resists Hodder-style rejections of theoretical unification as the only path (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Classification of schools (culture-history, evolutionary, functional-processual, processual, postprocessual) as high-level packages; assessment of convergence at middle-ranging levels while high-level differences persist; explicit epistemology (Trigger: ontological materialism + epistemological realism).

## Applications

Organizes *HAT*'s narrative of successive trends; frames ch. 9 "Pragmatic Synthesis" as partial accommodation among high-level programs constrained by data.

## Contested Issues

Whether high-level theory can be unified; whether it is epiphenomenal to method; relativist denial that high-level claims are accountable to data. All treated as positions under [[data-constraint-on-interpretation]] and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].
