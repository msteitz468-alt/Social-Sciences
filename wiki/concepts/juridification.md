# Juridification

## Definition as Coined

**Juridification** (*Verrechtlichung*) "refers quite generally to the tendency toward an increase in formal (or positive, written) law that can be observed in modern society" (attributed to [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]], [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|TCA Vol. 2]]). Habermas distinguishes the **expansion** of law (new domains brought under legal regulation) from the **increasing density** of law (finer regulation of domains already legalized). The term was introduced to academic discussion by the jurist Otto Kirchheimer during the Weimar Republic (originally regarding labor law and collective bargaining).

## Semantic History

Habermas gives juridification a developmental structure of **four epochal waves** (attributed):
1. the **bourgeois state** under Absolutism — institutionalizing the money and power media (theorized by Hobbes's *Leviathan*);
2. the **constitutional state** (*Rechtsstaat*, 19th-c. German constitutionalism) — civil rights against the sovereign;
3. the **democratic constitutional state** (French Revolution; Rousseau, Kant) — democratization of state power via suffrage;
4. the **democratic welfare state** (*sozialer Rechtsstaat*, 20th c.; the workers' movement, e.g. the Weimar Constitution).

The first three waves are, on Habermas's reading, "unambiguously freedom-guaranteeing." The fourth is **ambivalent**: "the very means of guaranteeing freedom endangers the freedom of the beneficiaries," and its negative effects "do not appear as side effects; they result from the form of juridification itself."

## Operationalizations

The pivotal analytic distinction is between:
- **law as a steering medium** — relieved of substantive justification, functionally bound to money and power, applied to already formally organized domains (commercial, administrative law); and
- **law as an institution** — requiring substantive justification, embedded in the lifeworld's legitimate orders (constitutional and criminal law near morality).

Habermas illustrates welfare-state juridification with empirical studies of **family law** (custody/wardship — drawing on Simitis) and **school law** (Frankenberg's study of teachers' perception of legal norms): in both, law-as-medium is superimposed on communicatively structured domains "by functional necessity," depersonalizing relations and turning clients and pupils into "objects" rather than "participants."

## Applications

Juridification is the **empirical test case** Habermas offers for the [[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]: the welfare state secures material well-being while its legal-bureaucratic form colonizes the communicative infrastructure of family, school, and socialization. It concretizes the [[system-and-lifeworld]] framework in the domain of law.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Anti-welfare-state reading** (attributed to critics): the ambivalence thesis has been read as lending support to deregulation; defenders stress Habermas's insistence that waves 1–3 are emancipatory and that the point is the *form*, not the fact, of welfare provision.
- **Neglect of rights' enabling role** (attributed): feminist and social-rights scholars argue legal intervention into the "lifeworld" (e.g. family) can protect the vulnerable, complicating the colonization framing.
