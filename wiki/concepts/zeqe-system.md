# Zeq'e (Ceque) System

## Definition as Coined

**Zeq'e** (also *zeq'e*, *ceque* in older Spanish orthography) means “line.” As of [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002), Andean geography was envisioned as sacred space; among the Incas this took form as a world of four parts converging at [[cuzco|Cuzco]]. Hundreds of holy places (*wak'a* / [[huaca|huaca]]) were joined to form “the most elaborate ritual complex in the native Americas — the **zeq'e (line) system** of Cuzco.” Shrines distributed across the landscape were linked by lines radiating out from central Cuzco; most lines originated in or near the Sun Temple (Qorikancha). Each line was assigned to a particular social group in Cuzco, which conducted rituals at the appropriate shrines following a ceremonial calendar.

Alias note: English and Spanish ethnohistory commonly write **ceque**; D'Altroy uses *zeq'e* for the Quechua form.

## Semantic History

Spanish awareness of Cuzco shrine scope grew under Polo de Ondegardo (magistrate of Cuzco, ~1558–60), who found Incas worshiped at more than 400 shrines in and around the city; Cuzco’s network was only one of many (Polo once ventured every Andean town had one). Cristóbal de Albornoz found more than two thousand *wak'a* in Chanka and Aymara lands of Chinchaysuyu. The most complete account of Cuzco’s shrines is Cobo’s list of **332 *wak'a*** (probably from Polo’s lost manuscript), with positions, social affiliations, mythical history, and sacrifices.

Modern scholarship (as D'Altroy summarizes): **Zuidema** (1964, 1983) proposed simultaneous reflection of Cuzco kinship and marital customs, cosmic space, and ceremonial calendrics (year-long clockwork with sighting lines)—some ideas accepted, others controversial. **Sherbondy** linked high-ranking kin to water and land rights via spring-shrines. **Van de Guchte** explored carved stone shrines mediating an animate landscape. **Rowe** (1979a, 1985a) traced historical development with royal genealogies, challenging Zuidema’s more static treatment. **Bauer** (1998) conducted the first comprehensive study integrating history and archaeological fieldwork. D'Altroy synthesizes: the system provided a conceptual map for Cuzco’s society, the empire, and the cosmos—grounding history in landscape, organizing annual ceremonies, and giving capital social groups a place in relations with ancestors and sacred powers—yet was **dynamic**, a “continuously invented ritual order” (Rowe; Bauer 1998:155–61).

## Operationalizations

**Line count and geometry.** Condensing Bauer 1998, D'Altroy reports Chinchaysuyu, Antisuyu, and Kollasuyu each contained nine lines; Cuntisuyu contained 14 or 15; total **41 or 42 lines**. Cobo’s enumeration runs clockwise for three parts and counterclockwise for Chinchaysuyu. Bauer’s fieldwork: most lines ran ~5–11 km with 3–15 shrines; some essentially straight, others jogged to adjust to *wak'a* positions. Near-Cuzco *suyu* borders were neither symmetrical nor cardinal (Espinoza 1977; Zuidema and Poole 1982)—asymmetric layout despite Inca capacity for cardinal planning.

**Social ranking.** Within each *suyu* part, lines were grouped into triads ranked *qollana–payan–kayaw* (descending) based on Inca kinship; individual lines in each triad also ranked. On Cobo’s list, 23 lines were assigned to social groups in or around Cuzco; in many cases one line of a triad went to a royal [[panaqa|panaqa]] and another to a non-royal [[ayllu|ayllu]]. Each group carried out sacrifices along its line. Year-long cycles of *panaqa* and affiliated *ayllu* venerated assigned shrines; Hanan Cuzco controlled upper-valley water and canals, Hurin the lower—integrating landscape, production, and social order (Sherbondy: over one-third of shrines water/hydraulic-related).

**Shrine typology (Bauer grouping of Cobo).** 96 springs/water sources; 95 stones; 32 hills/passes; 28 royal palaces and temples; 28 fields/flat places; 10 tombs; 7 ravines; 3 caves; 3 quarries; 3 stone seats; 3 sunset markers; 2 trees; 2 roads. Two most important shrines: Qorikancha and Huanacauri. Notable springs: Tocoripuquiu (source of Cuzco’s stream); Susurpuquio (Pachakuti’s vision of Creator/Sun on the eve of the Chanka battle); Tambo Machay/Quinoapuquio with dual channels and terraces.

**Offerings and specialists.** Common offering on Cobo’s list: *mullu* (sea shell), especially for rain; also llamas, guinea pigs, precious metals, textiles, coca. Human children rarely consecrated, mostly for Sun worship and important events in the Inca’s life ([[qhapaq-ucha|qhapaq ucha]]). Cobo: over one thousand record-keeping specialists needed in Cuzco to memorize lore, prayers, rituals, and sacrifices. Cobo also: 31 of 332 capital-area shrines owed human sacrifice.

**Dynamics.** At least 19 shrines associated with Pachakuti; others with Anawarki, Amaru Thupa Inka, Thupa Inka Yupanki; even Wayna Qhapaq’s and Waskhar’s palaces were *wak'a* on lines. Ranking reported to Spaniards may have worked fully only under Wayna Qhapaq; unfulfilled addition of his *panaqa* would have shifted rankings and shrines.

## Applications

- **Conceptual map** of society, empire, and cosmos at Cuzco ([[cuzco]], [[inca-empire]]).
- **Kinship–ritual calendar:** *Panaqa* and *ayllu* duties along ranked lines ([[panaqa]], [[ayllu]], [[dual-organization]]).
- **Empire-wide ritual logistics:** Straight-path processions of [[qhapaq-ucha|qhapaq ucha]] parties parallel zeq'e logic to provincial *wak'a* by rank.
- **Hydraulic and dual organization:** Hanan/Hurin control of valley water and canals.
- **Provincial analogy:** Shrine networks beyond Cuzco (Albornoz’s thousands; Polo on ubiquity).

## Critiques and Limitations

**Asymmetry and path shape.** Scholars remain uncertain whether layout asymmetries arose from historical change, oddities of Inca social structure, or recording error. Calendrical/astronomical models assume many straight sight lines; Bauer finds others crooked. Only a fraction of shrines have been located archaeologically.

**Structuralist vs historical models.** Zuidema’s simultaneous kinship–cosmos–calendar package remains partly controversial; Rowe/Bauer historicize a system that structural treatments risk freezing as timeless.

**Scale of human sacrifice.** Chronicle-scale victims at Cuzco *wak'a* lack archaeological support as of 2002: Bauer found surface human burial at only three Cuzco *wak'a*, nothing approaching thousands of victims; systematic shrine excavation was still open. High-peak finds better match Molina’s stress on exalted mountain locations.

**Wiraqocha underinvestment.** Despite the Creator’s mythic importance, D'Altroy (citing Demarest 1981) reports relatively little energy for Wiraqocha’s worship—no major ceremony, no endowment or elaborate priesthood, few Cuzco-area shrines—contrasting with Inti and the zeq'e apparatus.
