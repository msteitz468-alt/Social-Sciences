# Total institution

## Definition as Coined

In the introduction to *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]* (1961), [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] defines a **total institution** as:

> a place of residence and work where a large number of like-situated individuals, cut off from the wider society for an appreciable period of time, together lead an enclosed, formally administered round of life.

Prisons are the clear example, "providing we appreciate that what is prison-like about prisons is found in institutions whose members have broken no laws." The volume treats total institutions in general and mental hospitals in particular; main focus is the **inmate world**, not staff; a chief concern is "a sociological version of the structure of the self" (Goffman introduction, attributed).

Essay 1 develops the concept as a Weberian-style [[ideal-type|ideal type]]: none of the elements is peculiar to total institutions alone, and none is shared by every case; what is distinctive is that each exhibits **to an intense degree many items in this family of attributes** (attributed).

### Five rough groupings (denotative list)

Goffman lists (not neat, exhaustive, or of immediate analytical use — his caveat):

1. Care for persons felt **incapable and harmless** — homes for the blind, aged, orphaned, indigent.
2. Care for persons felt **incapable and an unintended threat** — TB sanitaria, mental hospitals, leprosaria.
3. **Protection of the community** against intentional dangers — jails, penitentiaries, P.O.W. camps, concentration camps (welfare of the sequestered not the immediate issue).
4. **Worklike instrumental** establishments — army barracks, ships, boarding schools, work camps, colonial compounds, large mansions from the servants' quarters' point of view.
5. **Religious retreats / training** — abbeys, monasteries, convents, cloisters.

### Central feature

A basic modern social arrangement, Goffman argues, is that the individual tends to sleep, play, and work in different places, with different co-participants, under different authorities, without an overall rational plan. The **central feature** of total institutions is the **breakdown of barriers** ordinarily separating these three spheres:

1. All aspects of life conducted in the **same place under the same single authority**.
2. Each phase of daily activity in the immediate company of a **large batch** of others, treated alike, doing the same thing together.
3. All phases **tightly scheduled**, one activity leading at a prearranged time into the next, imposed from above by formal rulings and officials.
4. Enforced activities brought together into a **single rational plan** purportedly designed to fulfill official aims.

The **key fact**, he writes, is "the handling of many human needs by the bureaucratic organization of whole blocks of people" — whether or not that is necessary or effective. From this follow surveillance (not mere guidance), a **basic split** between a large managed group (**inmates**) and a small supervisory **staff**, restricted mobility and information across the line, and work incentives that cannot have the structural significance of outside wage-and-domestic separation (attributed).

The total institution is "a social hybrid, part residential community, part formal organization" (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Goffman 1961 (*Asylums*) | Comparative ideal type; inmate-world focus; family-of-attributes method |
| Organizational / institutional sociology | Standard term for prisons, asylums, boarding schools, basic training, etc. |
| Critical / anti-psychiatry reception | Often read as indictment of mental hospitals specifically |
| Contemporary extensions | Nursing homes, camps, some digital analogies — often looser than Goffman's cluster |

## Operationalizations

Not a survey scale. Indicators Goffman clusters (attributed, Essay 1): barrier to outside intercourse; batch living; binary staff–inmate split (with qualifications for trusties, live-in staff, etc.); mortifying admission and daily processes ([[mortification-of-the-self]]); privilege system of house rules / rewards / punishments; institutional ceremonies that stage moral difference; modes of inmate adaptation (situational withdrawal, intransigence, colonization, conversion, playing it cool); permeability to outside standards; voluntary vs. involuntary recruitment; staff aim of self-regulating change vs. mere action compliance.

## Applications

- Principal empirical poles in *Asylums*: [[mental-hospital|mental hospitals]] (St. Elizabeths / "Central Hospital") and prisons, with comparative literature on monasteries, military establishments, ships, boarding schools, camps.
- Organizational sociology of closed residential settings; later policy talk of institutionalization and deinstitutionalization (reception — not yet separately ingested).
- Link to [[bureaucracy]] (batch management) and to [[symbolic-interactionism]] via the institutional production of self and moral career.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scope tension:** one ideal type across cure, custody, education, worship, and work charters risks homogenizing dissimilar places (Goffman himself marks the five-type list as rough).
- **Period base:** mid-century large custodial hospitals and prisons; post-asylum psychiatry and carceral change alter the empirical landscape.
- **Perspective bias:** inmate-world focus deliberately underweights staff experience and clinical outcomes (Goffman's stated design and partisanship warning in the Preface).
- Contested whether the concept describes *all* large residential organizations or only those with strong mortification and batch features.
