# Intergroup Mobility (big fish, little pond)

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] analyzes movement between substructures as governed by the internal distribution of rewards and the *character* of a substructure's status standards. High-status members have least incentive to leave (superior rewards), low-status members most — analogized to firms moving between industries by relative profitability (Boulding). But **direction** depends on whether internal status rests on **universalistic** or **particularistic** standards:
- If internal status is **universalistic** (valued throughout the macrostructure — instrumental achievement), high-status members are most likely to move voluntarily *upward*, and low-status members move only when pushed.
- If internal status is **particularistic** (religious/political devotion, valued only within), the *least* devoted / most alienated members are most likely to move to a substructure with contrasting values.

The signature is the **"big fish in a little pond / little fish in a big pond" dilemma**: vertical mobility under universalistic standards forces a choice between a *superordinate role* with rewarding daily interaction in a lower stratum, and a *subordinate daily role* with the greater structural privileges of a higher stratum.

## Semantic History

Blau's formulation predates and informs the "frog-pond effect" later studied in the sociology of education and organizations. It connects Simmel's formal analysis of relative position to a comparative theory of voluntary mobility.

## Operationalizations

Illustrated rather than measured: full professors who associate with junior colleagues (superordinate role) vs. those who associate with senior faculty (privilege, subordinate daily role); and [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte's]] corner boys — some used their resources to gain a superordinate position within the gang, others to raise their chances of moving up to a higher stratum.

## Applications

A component of Blau's [[macrostructure-and-substructure]] analysis and of how [[differentiation-of-power]] impedes adjustment: powerful groups restrict access to perpetuate their positions, so real societies sit between free mobility and rigid, caste-like closure. Where internal price-type adjustment fails, Blau argues (à la Keynes) an external stimulus is needed — supplied, for society, by [[opposition]] movements as "new investments."

## Critiques and Limitations

- The universalistic/particularistic determinant of mobility direction is an elegant deduction with thin direct evidence in the source (chiefly the Whyte illustration).
- Treats mobility as largely voluntary/choice-driven, underweighting the structural barriers its own closing pages emphasize.
