# Commodity Fetishism

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), Chapter 1, Section 4, [[marx-karl|Marx]] names the **fetishism of commodities**: under commodity production, the social character of producers’ labour appears to them as an objective character stamped upon the product of labour — as socio-natural properties of things — and the social relation of the producers to the sum of their own labour appears as a social relation between the products.

> “A commodity is therefore a mysterious thing, simply because in it the social character of men’s labour appears to them as an objective character stamped upon the product of that labour; because the relation of the producers to the sum total of their own labour is presented to them as a social relation, existing not between themselves, but between the products of their labour.”

Marx compares this to religious fetishism: products of the human brain (or hand) appear as independent beings endowed with life. The analogy is analytical, not theological.

**Attributed to Marx throughout.** The fetishism section was largely rewritten for the second German edition (1873 afterword).

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Markers Marx offers:

- Value appears as a property of things rather than a social relation of labour.
- Money appears magical — “the riddle of the money fetish is but the riddle of the commodity fetish.”
- Transparency returns only when production is consciously socialized (his counterfactual of free association of producers) or in non-commodity forms (e.g. patriarchal, feudal personal relations — illustrative contrasts).

## Applications

- Opens the critical sociology of “objective” market appearances.
- Links to [[labor-theory-of-value]] (abstract labour as social substance misrecognized).
- Wage-form later (Part 6) extends the mystification: price of labour-power appears as price of labour.
- **Taussig (1980):** In *[[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980|The Devil and Commodity Fetishism in South America]]*, [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]] treats Marx's category as the master trope for reading Cauca [[devil-contract|devil contracts]] and Bolivian [[tio|Tio]] rites. He argues that newly proletarianised workers' folk magic does **not** fully naturalise capital the way pure commodity fetishism would — it *supernaturalises* and moralises capital as evil, mediating the clash between precapitalist/use-value fetishisms and commodity fetishism. Baptism of money is read as folk M–C–M′; barren devil-wages as the unproductive underside of capital's fertility. Load-bearing (attributed): "the animate appearance of commodities provides testimony to the thing-like appearance of persons." See study [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]] · hub [[hubs/studies/taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]].
- **Scheper-Hughes — fetishized kidney / organs trafficking (2001/2002):** In "Commodity Fetishism in Organs Trafficking," published in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (originally *Body & Society* 7.2–3, 2001), [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] applies commodity fetishism to the global [[human-organs-trade|human organs trade]] and [[body-commodification]]. She argues (attributed) that the transplant kidney emerges as the "ultimate fetish," promising life, vitality, and *élan*, while naturalizing an extraordinary practice; that fetish energy attaches especially to the *purchased living* kidney (alive, young, "animate") over cadaveric organs imagined as nearly dead; and that the ultimate fetish, following Illich, is "life" itself as an object of endless manipulation — a fetishization that, she holds, erases a social ethic. Organs Watch's mandate, as she states it, includes forging a language "unencumbered by the medical rhetoric of scarcities, gifts, altruism and life-saving."
- **Lock — gene / cell-line commodity fetishism (2001/2002):** In the same volume, [[lock-margaret|Margaret Lock]] ("The Alienation of Body Tissue and the Biopolitics of Immortalized Cell Lines") argues that escalating procurement of human body materials constitutes a nearly uncontrolled **globalized commodity fetishism** in which Appadurai's "regimes of value" at production and consumption sites are far removed; human body parts, she holds, do not have universal value, and once convertible into circulating commodities their worth and alienability are open to dispute. She also channels Haraway's "corporeal fetishism": reification of cells, genes, or parts obscures heterogeneous human/non-human interactions, leaving "exotic" donors especially vulnerable to biocapitalist exploitation (attributed, Lock).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested as metaphysics by critics of Marxism; defended as social epistemology of markets.
- Risk of over-extension to any “false consciousness” without commodity production.
- Contrast [[money-economy]] (Simmel): metropolitan psychic effects of money — different mechanism and method.
- **Do not conflate with Polanyi's [[fictitious-commodities]].** [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] explicitly distinguished his fictitious commodities (labor, land, money — things treated *as* commodities though not produced for sale) from Marx's commodity fetishism, stating in a footnote that "Marx's assertion of the fetish character of the value of commodities refers to the exchange value of *genuine* commodities and has nothing in common with the fictitious commodities." Fetishism is the mystification of value in genuine commodities; fictitious commodities is the false commodity-status of non-produced things. Different problems.
- Taussig's ethnographic extension is itself contested: secondary Bolivian evidence; use-/exchange-value binary; risk of reading all devil lore as systematic anti-capitalist critique (see study Critiques).
- **Appadurai’s “methodological fetishism”** (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|SLOT]]* 1986) is a **method stance** (follow trajectories of things) and must not be confused with Marx’s critical account of fetishism as structural mystification under commodity production. Appadurai also sketches commodity-futures “meta-fetishization” and advertising’s fetishism of the consumer as advanced-capitalist cultural moves *(attributed)*.
