## Definition as Coined

Calpolli (plural calpoltin, literally "big house") is the Nahuatl term for a corporate
social and territorial unit in Aztec society. Rudolf van Zantwijk, as reported by Coe
and Koontz (2013), defines it as a group of families related by kinship or by long-term
proximity, and cautions explicitly against the common gloss "clan," which would wrongly
imply a shared belief in real or fictive common descent that the calpolli did not
require.

## Semantic History

The term is "migrated" in this wiki's sense: an emic Nahuatl social-organizational
category that has since become a standard etic classificatory term in Mesoamericanist
anthropology and archaeology for describing corporate residential wards more generally.
Its translation as "clan" in older literature is flagged by van Zantwijk (via Coe &
Koontz 2013) as a mistranslation that imports an unwarranted descent claim — an instance
of the wiki's four-way non-identity caution applied to kinship terminology rather than
ethnicity.

## Operationalizations

Each calpolli functioned as a localized, land-holding corporation with its own ritual
apparatus — its own temple, its own gods, and an association with a particular day in
the 260-day ritual count. Its chief, the calpollec, was elected for life by the
calpolli's inhabitants and confirmed by the ruler. Coe and Koontz (2013) report more
than 80 calpoltin in Tenochtitlan, organized into the city's four great quarters, which
were divided by imaginary north-south and east-west lines meeting at the Sacred
Precinct (see [[tenochtitlan]]).

Elite calpolli members provided commoners (macehualtin) with land and/or
non-agricultural occupations in exchange for services and tribute. Commoners worked
calpolli land and retained use-rights to it as long as it remained in use within a
two-year period; many households also held a calmil (house garden) managed at the
household level. Marriage was expected within one's own calpolli for commoners (Coe &
Koontz 2013).

## Applications

Several of Tenochtitlan's calpoltin survive as traditional barrios in modern Mexico
City. After the Conquest, calpoltin were converted into Colonial-era administrative
barrios, and calpolli temples were repurposed as parish churches (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979 — Late Horizon Corporate Structure and the Maquixco Analogue

*(Attributed — [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]], *The Basin of
Mexico*, 1979.)* The authors describe calpulli (calpultin) as the basic territorial
corporate unit of Late Horizon local society, translated by the Spanish as "barrio":
each had an elective hereditary headman, a tax-paying/economic function built on
common land ownership (in rural calpulli), an educational function (the telpochcalli
school), a military function (a unit within the tlatoani's army), and a religious
function centered on a small ceremonial complex. In Tenochtitlan the term was
sometimes replaced by tlaxilacalli, and calpulli could denote units of very different
scale — barrios pequeños of a few dozen to a few hundred people, larger calpultin of
500–1,000 people, and Tenochtitlan's four grouped major wards, also called calpulli.
Most rural calpultin were spatially discrete communities of commoner farmers; in
large centers such as Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tacuba, calpulli tended instead to be
functionally specialized (e.g., organized around particular crafts).

The authors also use excavated Middle Horizon and First Intermediate evidence to
argue the calpulli-type corporate structure has considerable time depth predating
its Late Horizon documentary description. At Maquixco Bajo, a Middle Horizon
apartment-compound village near Teotihuacan, they propose at least three territorial
levels (apartment / house-compound / site) corresponding to nuclear-family, lineage,
and village sociopolitical levels, fitting what they call a "ramage model" similar to
the Aztec calpulli — a segmentary-lineage-plus-ranking structure. Comparing Maquixco
Bajo to the First Intermediate Phase Two village of Loma Torremote, they find both
sites show a hierarchical status structure paralleling the calpulli ramage model,
with Maquixco near the lower end and Loma Torremote near the upper end of calpulli
population-size range; the sharpest contrast between the two is not structural but
economic and political — Loma Torremote's lineage head functioned as a senior kinsman
and source of redistribution who personally organized production for kin, whereas
Maquixco's lineage head produced no surplus goods for kin redistribution and instead
occupied an explicitly political role linking the local community to the higher
Teotihuacan organizational level. The authors treat this contrast as evidence that
calpulli-like corporate residential organization long predates the Late Horizon
documentary record, even as its economic and political functions varied considerably
by period and by a community's position relative to an urban center.

## Critiques and Limitations

The calpolli's precise legal and kinship character remains debated in the specialist
literature; van Zantwijk's non-clan reading, reported here via Coe and Koontz, is the
position recorded on this page, but it should be read as one scholar's corrective
rather than a wiki-voice settled fact, since the underlying documentary base (mainly
post-Conquest and Colonial-era records) is itself a lens shaped by Spanish
administrative categories.
