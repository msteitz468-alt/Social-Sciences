# Domain Assumptions

## Definition as Coined

In *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|The Coming Crisis of Western Sociology]]* (1970), [[gouldner-alvin-w|Alvin W. Gouldner]] uses **domain assumptions** for the background beliefs about man and society that a theorist (and a theoretical community) takes as given — more general than the theory's explicit postulates, often half-articulate, and decisive for which problems and solutions look "real."

Gouldner distinguishes them from formal theoretical propositions and from purely technical methodological rules. They include assumptions such as whether social structure is autonomous from individuals, whether man is essentially good or evil, whether order is precarious, whether society is fundamentally integrated or conflictual.

## Semantic History

Gouldner 1970 is the load-bearing coinage for this wiki's use. Related earlier vocabularies: [[sociology-of-knowledge]] (ideology, world-view); Polanyi-style tacit knowledge; later "ontological assumptions" in philosophy of social science. Post-1970 "domain assumptions" often appears as a loose synonym for background ontology — sometimes stripped of Gouldner's link to sentiments and personal reality.

## Operationalizations

Gouldner cites a research note using questionnaire / factor-analytic work (orthogonal Varimax; Sprehe dissertation material) to show that sociologists' domain assumptions cluster and relate to theoretical preferences — presented as illustration of recoverability, not as definitive measurement science. Treat those results as Gouldner's report.

## Applications

- Analytic key to Gouldner's reading of [[structural-functionalism]] (autonomy of social structure; problem of order; moral integration).
- Bridge concept between theory content and [[theoretical-infrastructure]] (sentiments, personal reality, career of theory).
- Programmatic for [[reflexive-sociology]]: making domain assumptions examinable.

## Critiques and Limitations

Concept can become a catch-all for "what I dislike in your theory." Empirical operationalization in *Coming Crisis* is thin and second-hand. Distinguishing domain assumptions from ideology, from metaphysics, and from testable claims remains contested.
