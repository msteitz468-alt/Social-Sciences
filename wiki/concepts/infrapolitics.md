# Infrapolitics

## Definition as Coined

[[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] ([[scott-domination-arts-resistance-1990|1990]]) coined "infrapolitics" for the wide variety of low-profile forms of resistance that "dare not speak in their own name" — the disguised, undeclared political action of subordinate groups that constitutes, in his phrase, the "cultural and structural underpinning" of visible politics. The prefix is deliberate: like infrared light, it lies just below the visible spectrum.

## Core Claims (attributed)

- **A three-domain typology.** Scott mapped each domain of domination onto an open and a disguised resistant counterpart: *material* domination (appropriation of grain, taxes, labor) countered openly by petitions and revolts, and infrapolitically by poaching, squatting, desertion, foot-dragging, pilfering; *status* domination (humiliation, forced deference) countered by hidden-transcript discourses of dignity — rituals of aggression, revenge tales, carnival symbolism, gossip, rumor; *ideological* domination countered by dissident subcultures — millennial religion, hush-arbor Christianity, myths of social banditry, world-upside-down imagery.
- **Infrapolitics is real politics.** Scott argued against confining "the political" to open, declared activity: doing so wrongly concludes that the historical majority of humankind — subjects rather than citizens — lacked political life, and misses "the immense political terrain that lies between quiescence and revolt." Infrapolitics is conducted "in more earnest, for higher stakes, and against greater odds" than liberal-democratic politics, and is the "elementary — in the sense of foundational — form of politics."
- **Guerrilla-war dynamics.** The balance of power is known only through constant probing: unpunished small breaches become new de facto limits (Thompson's poachers girdling trees; escalating symbolic breaches in caste dress codes, following Guha). Aggregated petty acts can produce macro effects — "rather like snowflakes on a steep mountainside, set off an avalanche" — restructuring de facto property rights, precipitating fiscal crises (French tithe evasion in Le Roy Ladurie's account), or hollowing out regimes.
- **Practice, not just talk.** Scott insisted the [[hidden-transcript]] is enacted in material stratagems, not merely griping: it is "a condition of practical resistance rather than a substitute for it," in dialectical relation with the offstage discourse of customary right that legitimates the practice.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified by Scott; identified through the same inferential strategies as [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] — systematic behavioral patterns (evasion rates, poaching prosecutions, desertion figures) read against offstage discourse, with intention inferred from pattern where speech is unavailable. Scott stressed the archive problem: successful infrapolitics is designed to leave no traces, so prosecution records (e.g., Prussian forest-crime counts) capture only the failures.

## Semantic History

Coined 1990 as the generalization of [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] (Scott 1985) from peasant class relations to all structures of domination, adding the discursive/symbolic register to the material one. Since taken up in African American studies (Robin D. G. Kelley's *Race Rebels*, 1994, is the most prominent adoption), labor history, and studies of authoritarian regimes; the term has drifted toward a catch-all for covert agency, thinner than Scott's typology.

## Applications

Slavery (pilfering redefined as reappropriation of one's own product), European forest struggles (Agulhon's Var; Marx's Rhineland wood-theft articles, where forest offenses were the majority of all Prussian prosecutions in 1836), tithe and tax evasion, desertion, and the coded public genres analyzed under the hidden transcript.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded at [[hegemony-resistance-debate]]: critics question whether aggregated petty acts are "resistance" absent intention or effect (the conceptual-stretching charge levied against the resistance literature generally), whether the framework can distinguish coping, crime, and politics, and whether it underplays the cases where quiescence reflects genuine consent. Scott's macro-effects claims rest on selective historical illustration rather than systematic comparison.
