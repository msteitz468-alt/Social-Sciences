# Meshwork

## Definition as Coined

**Meshwork** is the term [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]] adopts (in *[[ingold-lines-2007|Lines]]*,
2007) for the world understood as a **tangle of interwoven lines** — trails of growth
and movement laid down by organisms and inhabitants as they live along — as against
the **network**, which he takes to be a connection of pre-existing points or nodes by
links. In the meshwork, entities are not connected *points* but interpenetrating
*lines*: "it is in the entanglement of lines, not in the connecting of points, that the
mesh is constituted." Life is lived **along** the lines, not **across** the links
(attributed, Ingold 2007).

## Semantic History

Ingold explicitly **borrows "meshwork" from the philosopher Henri Lefebvre**, quoting
Lefebvre's description of "the reticular patterns left by animals… and by people…
whose movements weave an environment that is more 'archi-textural' than architectural"
(Lefebvre 1991: 117–18). In *Lines* the contrast is drawn not against Actor-Network
Theory (Ingold does not name Latour or ANT in this book) but against **Bruce Chatwin's**
description of Aboriginal songlines as an "interlocking network": Ingold objects that
the modern sense of "network," which he traces via a semantic shift from Gottfried
**Semper's** 1860 "invention of the network" (an open-work fabric of entwined threads)
to the information-technology/transport sense, now means "a complex of interconnected
points" rather than interwoven lines — "It is more a meshwork than a network"
(attributed, Ingold 2007). The concept generalizes the [[wayfaring|wayfarer's]] line to
a whole ontology of "lines of life," extending the [[dwelling-perspective]].

**The ANT critique (*[[ingold-being-alive-2011|Being Alive]]*, 2011).** Ingold's head-on
argument against **Actor-Network Theory** appears in the essay "When ANT meets SPIDER."
He objects that ANT's "network" — a web of connections among heterogeneous *actants*
(Latour) — reverts to the point-and-connector sense of network he rejects, and that the
original French *acteur-réseau* meant something closer to a spun web than a set of nodes.
Against ANT he poses **SPIDER = "Skilled Practice Involves Developmentally Embodied
Responsiveness":** the spider's web is not a network *linking* the spider to flies but
the very *lines* the living, skilled spider spins and lives along — trails of an
organism's activity, not connectors between pre-existing entities. The meshwork is the
world seen the spider's way, not the ant's (attributed, Ingold 2011).

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — an ontological/interpretive contrast used to redescribe
environments, social relations, and organisms as fields of interwoven lines rather
than assemblages of connected nodes.

## Applications

- **Place as a knot, not a node.** Ingold's signature spatial claim: a place is "a knot
  tied from multiple and interlaced strands of movement and growth," not a bounded
  container or a hub joined to others by transport connectors — "Every place… is a knot
  in the meshwork, and the threads from which it is traced are lines of wayfaring." He
  illustrates with Benjamin Orlove's Lake Titicaca trails (an "archi-textural meshwork")
  and Nancy Munn's Walbiri drawings of ancestral journeys as vortices rather than
  circles (attributed).
- **Lines of habitation vs. lines of occupation.** Ingold contrasts the meshwork of
  inhabitants' trails with the straight, pre-surveyed **"lines of occupation"** imposed
  by imperial/colonial power (roads, railways, frontiers) that "ride roughshod over the
  lines of habitation" and connect at "nodal points of power" (e.g. the Roman road
  network converging on Durobrivae).
- **Organisms as thoroughfares.** In the genealogical-line critique he glosses Henri
  Bergson's *Creative Evolution* (1911) — organisms as thoroughfares through which "the
  current of life" flows — as "a meshwork of intertwined thoroughfares," a vision he
  sets against the gene-as-information-particle model (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

The meshwork/network contrast has been criticized as resting on a narrow, IT-era
reading of "network" and as more evocative than analytically operational — a heuristic
ontology rather than a testable model. *(Enriched from later Ingold ingests.)*
