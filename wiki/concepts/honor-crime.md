# Honor Crime (as analytic category)

The "honor crime" — killing of a woman by relatives for violating a sexual code, to restore family honor — became a hyper-visible public category in the 1990s–2000s, disproportionately associated with Islam although honor-based violence is not confined to Muslim communities and is not sanctioned by Islamic law. This page treats the **category** as an object of analysis; the lived moral system it caricatures is at [[honor-and-modesty]].

## Definition as Coined

The definitional core (relative-perpetrated killing over a sexual code, family honor as motive) circulates through NGO fact sheets, journalism, and legislation. Amnesty International's "Culture of Discrimination" fact sheet roots it in a belief in "women as objects and commodities... property of male relatives" — a formulation Abu-Lughod (*[[abu-lughod-do-muslim-women-2013|Do Muslim Women Need Saving?]]*, 2013) criticizes as unrecognizable against ethnographies of honor systems, including her own [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] fieldwork (1970s–80s), where honor bound both sexes (valor, generosity, trustworthiness) and women asserted moral agency within the code (*[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-1986|Veiled Sentiments]]*).

## Semantic History

Abu-Lughod (2013) identifies four problems with the category (attributed): it distorts the moral relations of honor-based communities; it divides a "civilized" West from an "uncivilized" rest; it erases the modern institutions (police, courts, forensic medicine — including virginity testing traceable to 19th-century Egyptian court records — prisons, NGOs, media) entangled in both the violence and its labeling; and it works as an "antipolitics machine" obscuring real social transformation. Supporting scholarship she cites: Dicle Kogacioglu showing Turkish honor-crime discourse was produced by party programs, legal debates, and newspapers in the EU-accession context; Katherine Ewing (*Stolen Honor*) showing the 2005 Berlin Hatun Sürücü killing was retroactively bundled with five prior killings — most not fitting the definition — into a manufactured German "spate"; Leti Volpp's "blaming culture for bad behavior": structurally similar violence is read as individual pathology in the West but as "culture" in Muslim/immigrant communities.

## Operationalizations

Statistics are unreliable and non-comparative: the oft-repeated figure of "5,000 honor killings per year worldwide," vaguely attributed to the UN, is uncorroborated and has stayed suspiciously unchanged across a decade of publications — even appearing in Norma Khouri's fabricated memoir (Abu-Lughod 2013). The genre's veracity problem is real: Khouri's *Honor Lost*/*Forbidden Love* (2003) was exposed as a hoax by Jordanian journalist Rana Husseini (Khouri — real name Norma Bagain Toliopoulos — had left Jordan at age three; the publisher withdrew the book); *Burned Alive* (2003), written 25 years after the events from "recovered memories" with ghostwriter Marie-Thérèse Cuny and tied to the NGO SURGIR's fundraising, is treated by Abu-Lughod as reliability-compromised though not a proven hoax.

## Applications

Case analyses in Abu-Lughod (2013): the Fadime Sahindal killing (Sweden 2002) — where she charges anthropologist Unni Wikan with misreading racial harassment and lost dignity as timeless "culture" (a recorded scholarly dispute, see [[saving-muslim-women-debate]]); Human Rights Watch's Palestine reporting, where a Ramallah killing was entangled with Israeli checkpoint delays on police and aid workers (with Shalhoub-Kevorkian and Daher-Nashif's research). Counter-developments she records: fatwas condemning honor killing (e.g., the Palestinian Authority's Islamic Chief Justice, 2005, per Lynn Welchman); Jordan's "There Is No Honor in Honor Killing" campaign (nohonor.org, 2007; Abu-Lughod interview with Mohammad Al-Azraq, March 29, 2009); and the irony that Jordan's honor-defense penal provisions derive from the Napoleonic Code, Ottoman law, and British common law, while Islamic Party politicians blocked reform as a "Western plot."

## Critiques and Limitations

The critical literature above is itself a position: human-rights practitioners defend the category as naming a real, patterned harm requiring specific legal remedies. Both positions are recorded on [[saving-muslim-women-debate]]; no adjudication in wiki voice.
