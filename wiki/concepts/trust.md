Trust, in James S. Coleman's rational-choice sociology, is not a diffuse psychological disposition but a specific class of wager: a trustor places resources under the potential control of a trustee because the expected value of doing so is positive under the trustor's own probability estimate of the trustee's trustworthiness. Coleman develops the concept across *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990) — Chapter 5 ("Relations of Trust"), Chapter 8 ("Systems of Trust and Their Dynamic Properties"), and Chapter 28's formal treatment within the linear system of action — as one of the book's core apparatuses linking micro-level rational action to macro-level systemic phenomena (bubbles, panics, institutional confidence).

## Definition as Coined

Coleman defines a trust relation as involving at minimum two purposive parties, a trustor and a trustee, in which the trustor voluntarily places resources or outcomes under the trustee's control without a binding guarantee of return. He formalizes the trustor's decision as a bet: trust is rationally placed if and only if p/(1-p) > L/G, where p is the trustor's estimated probability that the trustee is trustworthy, L is the potential loss, and G is the potential gain. Coleman illustrates the concept with four cases: a Hambros merchant-banker's unsecured guarantee to a Norwegian shipowner (Wechsberg 1966); two farmers baling one another's hay on trust of future reciprocation; a high-school girl who trusted a boy walking her home through the woods and was sexually assaulted; and declining U.S. institutional confidence, 1966–1979 (Public Opinion 1979 survey data). Coleman explicitly positions his formulation against Deutsch's (1962) definition, which restricts trust to cases where potential loss exceeds potential gain (L > G); Coleman argues this excludes legitimate trust-placement in cases such as trusting a confidence man, where G is deliberately inflated relative to L, and cites a Raymond Chandler (1955) passage on an agent who fears failing to trust more than misplaced trust.

## Semantic History

Coleman's usage departs from the ordinary-language and psychological senses of "trust" (an affective disposition or interpersonal orientation) by treating it strictly as a bet governed by expected utility. He develops it in explicit contrast to Morton Deutsch's mid-century experimental social-psychological definition. Within Coleman's own text, the concept expands from the simple dyadic trustor/trustee bet (Chapter 5) to complex trust structures (Chapter 8): mutual trust (each party is both trustor and trustee of the other), intermediated trust (advisor, guarantor, entrepreneur roles), and third-party trust. Chapter 28 recasts trust formally as a discounting operation within the linear system of action — a promised delivery with trust-probability p < 1 is modeled as a devalued good.

## Operationalizations

Coleman operationalizes trust through the p/(1-p) > L/G inequality and its algebraic equivalent pG + (1-p)L > 0. He proposes a conjectural refinement to explain why people are slow to trust new acquaintances but quick to trust confidence men: for close relationships the effective threshold (L/G)* is high because potential loss is weighed against only the marginal gain over alternative friends, whereas confidence men deliberately inflate G, pushing even a low prior p above threshold — Coleman flags this himself as an unresolved conjecture with stated empirical implications, not an established finding. He distinguishes three trust intermediaries: the advisor (only reputation, not resources, at risk), the guarantor (directly liable, as with Hambros), and the entrepreneur (pools multiple trustors' resources, exemplified by political operators such as Sam Rayburn and Lyndon Johnson). Coleman also models trust dynamics through three information sources about a trustee — the trustee's own performance, others with similar interests (prone to reinforcing cascades), and others with different interests (more independent evidence) — arguing dense communication dominated by the second source produces the most extreme trust-expansion and trust-contraction cycles.

## Applications

Coleman applies the framework to legislative logrolling (attributing vote-trading stability to legislatures functioning as close, reputation-sensitive communities, against Schwartz's 1981 rational-choice prediction of majority-rule instability), trade-secrecy norms in industries such as diamond dealing, and the instability of expanding and contracting trust systems — the South Sea Bubble and Mississippi Scheme, declining U.S. institutional confidence after Vietnam, the rise of Solidarity in Poland, and historical mass movements of trust-placement in charismatic figures (Sabbatai Sevi, Peter the Hermit, Hitler). He treats trust as a component of what he elsewhere calls social capital, and extends the same discounting logic to the linear system of action in Chapter 28, where incomplete trust (p < 1) reduces a trusted party's effective power, lowers total exchange volume, and discounts promises below the value of goods themselves.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman himself flags limitations within the theory: he argues complete trustworthiness does not always produce a social optimum, citing suboptimal firm organization that fails to exploit innovations even absent conflicting interests, and the Mafia, where high internal trustworthiness enforced by threat of death coexists with net harm to the wider society — his own normative argument, stated in attributed voice. He also notes that sanctioning an untrustworthy trustee is itself a public good subject to free-riding, particularly where many alternative trustors exist, so one trustor's refusal to trust has negligible disciplining effect. Coleman explicitly leaves unexplained why speculative bubbles burst, flagging this as an incomplete part of the theory. Because Coleman's is a rational-choice conception of trust, it is distinct from later sociological and psychological trust literatures (e.g., generalized/institutional trust as a diffuse orientation) that do not require an explicit bet structure; readers should not conflate Coleman's formal p/L/G construct with those broader usages.
