# Secondary Sexual Characters

## Definition as Coined

Following John Hunter, [[darwin-charles|Darwin]] in *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|The Descent of Man]]* (1871, ch. VIII) distinguished **primary** sexual characters (organs of reproduction) from **secondary** sexual characters not directly connected with the act of reproduction: male sense and locomotive organs for finding the female, prehensile organs, greater size/strength/pugnacity, weapons of offence and defence, gaudy colouring and ornaments, organs of song, odoriferous glands. Prehensile organs graduate into primary organs, so the boundary is hard if “primary” is not restricted to the reproductive glands. Female organs for nourishing young (mammary glands, marsupial sacks) are related differences but not the main focus of [[sexual-selection]].

## Semantic History

Hunter’s anatomical term → Darwin’s evolutionary problem-space: how can selection produce characters that do not help in the ordinary struggle for life? Answer: sexual selection. Twentieth-century biology retained the primary/secondary distinction while refining genetic and hormonal accounts of sexual dimorphism.

## Operationalizations

Darwin’s comparative catalogue (Part II): insects (stridulation, horns, colour), fishes–reptiles, birds (display, song, ocelli), mammals (weapons, manes, colour), man (beard, hairiness, voice, stature dimorphism). Rules of thumb: male generally more modified; characters appear near maturity; lost after emasculation; young of both sexes often resemble the adult female.

## Applications

Empirical domain of sexual-selection theory; bridge from animal display to human sex differences and race-relative beauty standards in *Descent* ch. XIX–XX.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary with primary organs is fuzzy; some “secondary” traits also serve non-sexual functions (horns for defence against predators). Modern sex-difference research uses dimorphism, hormone profiles, and genomics rather than Hunter’s binary alone.
