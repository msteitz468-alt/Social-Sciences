# Legitimacy and Effectiveness

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]* Chapter III, [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] distinguishes two conditions of democratic (and regime) stability:

- **Effectiveness** — primarily *instrumental*: actual performance, the extent to which the system satisfies the basic functions of government as most of the population and powerful groups (e.g. business, armed forces) see them.
- **Legitimacy** — primarily *evaluative*: the capacity of the system to engender and maintain the belief that existing political institutions are the most appropriate ones for the society. Groups judge legitimacy by fit between institutional values and their own.

Lipset argued that stability depends on *both*; a system may be effective yet illegitimate for key strata (his Weimar example: army, civil service, aristocracy rejecting republican symbolism), or legitimate yet ineffective.

## Semantic History

The pair organizes mid-century comparative politics discussions of democratic breakdown and consolidation. It sits adjacent to [[weber-max|Weber]]'s [[legitimate-domination]] (belief in the validity of domination) but is more regime-performance oriented and explicitly two-dimensional. Later democratization literature (not yet ingested) reworks performance vs diffuse support in survey terms (e.g. Easton-style diffuse support).

## Operationalizations

Effectiveness via economic and administrative performance and elite/mass satisfaction; legitimacy via acceptance of institutions, absence of anti-system parties, historical resolution of key cleavages (religion, citizenship, class). Crises of legitimacy, for Lipset, are primarily crises of *change* — transitions that exclude major groups or destroy old symbolic order without integrating them into the new.

## Applications

- Conditions of the democratic order (*Political Man* Part I).
- Analysis of new states and rapid development (linked to [[economic-development-and-democracy]]).
- Comparison of systems facing communist and nationalist challenges (as Lipset framed them).

## Critiques and Limitations

The dichotomy can blur *whose* effectiveness criteria count (mass vs veto elites). "Appropriateness" belief is hard to measure independently of effectiveness over time. Normative loading: Lipset writes as a partisan of stable democracy. Related but not identical to Weberian legitimacy types — do not equate without attribution.
