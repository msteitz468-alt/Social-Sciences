# Colono Ware

A class of hand-built, low-fired colonial-period earthenware central to North American **[[historical-archaeology]]** debates over ethnicity, creolization, and multiethnic production. [[orser-charles-e|Orser]] (1996, Ch. 5, "The Strange Career of Colono Ware Pottery") treated that revision history as theoretically instructive for **[[global-historical-archaeology]]** and the **[[four-haunts]]**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed career as Orser 1996 narrates it — densify against primary ceramic literature when ingested.)* Pottery type/class on colonial southeastern US (and related) sites: typically hand-built, low-fired earthenware contrasting with imported European industrial ceramics. The name and ethnic attributions have shifted repeatedly.

## Semantic History (Orser's "strange career")

*(All steps attributed to Orser's 1996 synthesis of named specialists — not independent ceramic reanalysis.)*

**Descriptive baseline (as Orser states):** Coarse, unglazed pottery principally along South Carolina and Virginia coasts (also Caribbean), common on late 17th–18th-century slave plantations; largely gone by early 19th century, replaced by glazed European/American ceramics.

Interpretive career Orser narrates:

1. **Noël Hume (1962):** Named **"Colono-Indian Ware"** — European vessel forms, non-European manufacture; often found on African-American slave sites. Explained via Native potters making commodities for slaves (purchase/barter) — for Orser, already a mutualist link whether or not correct.
2. **Baker (1972):** Associated ware with the **Catawba**; broadened market to other Natives and many Europeans — Catawba-invented commodity for survival in a changing Southern economy (held into the 1970s in Orser's telling).
3. **Ferguson (1978–92):** Civil Rights-era reopening — hypothesized that "Afro-American slaves made much if not most of the Colono-Ware we see"; pots as political "struggle with pots," not mere documents; transatlantic skill networks.
4. **Hill (1987):** Nothing specifically "African" — resembles basic non-wheel, unglazed, clamp-fired pottery worldwide; stresses *non-European* character (Native and African-American both plausible against Eurocentrism).
5. **Creolization synthesis (Hall, Ferguson 1992, Mouer 1993, Vernon & Cordell on Apalachee, etc.):** Process of interaction — African, Native, European strands creating new worlds; Apalachee making Colono vessels for Spanish soldiers as network embedding. Classification similarity secondary to mutualism.
6. **Orser's four-haunt reading:** Even cottage-craft ware shows capitalist slavery for profit, Eurocentric condescension, colonial modernity — if so for coarse pottery, all the more for Staffordshire and mass trade goods.

Parallel career for decorated clay tobacco pipes (Emerson Chesapeake; Handler Barbados; Orser's Palmares surface pipes with palm-tree readings and small-n caveats).

## Operationalizations

Ware attributes, site associations (plantations, towns, Native sites), and distributional patterns have been used to argue identity, resistance, market participation, or network connection depending on theoretical frame. Orser preferred relationship-and-haunt readings over pure ethnic type fossils.

## Applications

- Southeastern US plantation and colonial town archaeology (primary literature).
- Orser 1996 Ch. 5 as theory case alongside Palmares pipes.
- Parallel lesson for any "ethnic ceramic" in colonial contexts worldwide.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ethnic attribution from ceramics is chronically underdetermined. **Four-way non-identity:** ware ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetics. Single-study or single-narrator (Orser 1996) summaries of the career are not substitutes for the ceramic primary literature. Contested: yes.
