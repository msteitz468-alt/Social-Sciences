# Archaeological Context

In [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s framework, artifacts that **interact only with the natural environment** as residues (example: materials in a dump), as opposed to [[systemic-context|systemic context]] (attributed, Schiffer 1972a; 1987:3–4; 1995 Ch. 2). Distinct from the broader concept [[archaeological-record]] (totality of surviving evidence and how it is theorized) and from field-recording “context” (stratigraphic unit / findspot).

## Definition as Coined

Cultural deposition transforms materials into archaeological context; environmental and further cultural processes (disturbance, reclamation, archaeological recovery) continue to act on them. Provenience = find-spot immediately prior to discovery; Schiffer criticizes ambiguous **in situ** usage (attributed, Ch. 2). In the 1995 charter restatement, generating archaeological test implications requires relating systemic properties to recovered remains via transformation procedures that model discard, disposal of the dead, loss (c-transforms) and erosion, decay, aeolian deposition (n-transforms) (attributed, Schiffer 1972a, 1975; 1995 Ch. 7).

## Semantic History

Paired with systemic context as the central cut of transformation theory. Reclamation returns items to systemic context; the archaeologist’s excavation is itself a reclamation/disturbance process (Ch. 5, 13). Principle 25 of the 1995 principles inventory treats the archaeologist’s own behaviors as a family of cultural [[formation-processes]] that create appreciable variability in the record (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 23, grounded in Broken K recovery/analysis inconsistencies, Ch. 21).

## Operationalizations

Deposit as analytical unit for identifying [[formation-processes]] (1983c / 1987 Ch. 10 / 1995 Ch. 18); refuse typology; multi-trace attributes (size, orientation, damage, Completeness/Fragmentation Indices, sediment fabric, refits).

## Applications

Inference design; CRM deposit evaluation; separation of behavioral from formational patterning; rejection of “equivalence transformations” that treat house-floor assemblages as if systemic inventories (Schiffer 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20).

## Critiques and Limitations

Mixed-bag deposits; single field “floor” may hold primary, secondary, and de facto refuse together (attributed, Schiffer 1987 Ch. 10, 12; 1995 Ch. 20–21).
