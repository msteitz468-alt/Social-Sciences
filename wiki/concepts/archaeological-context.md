# Archaeological Context

In [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s framework, artifacts that **interact only with the natural environment** as residues (example: materials in a dump), as opposed to [[systemic-context|systemic context]] (attributed, Schiffer 1987:3–4). Distinct from the broader concept [[archaeological-record]] (totality of surviving evidence and how it is theorized) and from field-recording “context” (stratigraphic unit / findspot).

## Definition as Coined

Cultural deposition transforms materials into archaeological context; environmental and further cultural processes (disturbance, reclamation, archaeological recovery) continue to act on them. Provenience = find-spot immediately prior to discovery; Schiffer criticizes ambiguous **in situ** usage (attributed, Ch. 2).

## Semantic History

Paired with systemic context as the central cut of transformation theory. Reclamation returns items to systemic context; the archaeologist’s excavation is itself a reclamation/disturbance process (Ch. 5, 13).

## Operationalizations

Deposit as analytical unit for identifying [[formation-processes]] (Ch. 10); refuse typology; multi-trace attributes (size, orientation, damage, sediment fabric, refits).

## Applications

Inference design; CRM deposit evaluation; separation of behavioral from formational patterning.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mixed-bag deposits; single field “floor” may hold primary, secondary, and de facto refuse together (attributed, Schiffer 1987 Ch. 10, 12).
