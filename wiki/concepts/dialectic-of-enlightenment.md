# Dialectic of Enlightenment

## Definition as Coined

The **dialectic of enlightenment** is the governing thesis of
[[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]] and [[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]]'s 1947 book of that
name. It is stated (attributed) as a pair of theses: **"Myth is already
enlightenment; and enlightenment reverts to mythology."** "Enlightenment" here is
not a historical epoch but the whole trajectory of demythologizing, calculating,
dominating reason — "the disenchantment of the world; the dissolution of myths and
the substitution of knowledge for fancy" — from Homer through Bacon to modern
positivism.

The thesis has two moving parts (all attributed):

- **Myth is already enlightenment.** Myth was itself an attempt to explain, order,
  and master a threatening nature; the archaic "primal powers" already "represent a
  phase of enlightenment." Myth and enlightenment share a common core: "domination
  and exploitation." Enlightenment therefore has no pure origin outside myth to
  return to.
- **Enlightenment reverts to mythology.** In its drive to eliminate every mythic
  residue, enlightenment turns totalitarian — "**Enlightenment is totalitarian**"
  — treating "whatever does not conform to the rule of computation and utility" as
  suspect. It liquidates the qualitative, dissolves the subject it claimed to
  liberate ("world domination over nature turns against the thinking subject
  himself"), and, "paralyzed by fear of the truth," lapses into a new mythology:
  the "myth of things as they actually are," positivism, and — in its extreme —
  fascism. "The curse of irresistible progress is irresistible regression."

The book demonstrates the thesis through four studies rather than a system: the
Odyssey (Excursus I) as the *Urgeschichte* of the reasoning subject; Kant, Sade,
and Nietzsche (Excursus II) as the honest exposure of formalized reason; the
[[culture-industry]] as enlightenment become mass deception; and anti-Semitism
(via [[false-projection]]) as "the limits of enlightenment."

## Semantic History

The phrase names both the book and its thesis. The authors insist the terms myth
and enlightenment are "not merely historico-cultural but real" — a claim about the
actual movement of civilization, not just intellectual history. Crucially, they
present the argument **not as a rejection of the Enlightenment** but as an
immanent, self-critical dialectic: "social freedom is inseparable from enlightened
thought," and "enlightenment must consider itself, if men are not to be wholly
betrayed." Their preferred method is Hegel's **determinate negation** — rejecting
false absolutes ("idols") without collapsing into dogma or skepticism — which they
trace back to the Jewish prohibition on naming God. Later readers, above all
Habermas, contest whether the book can consistently criticize reason using reason
(see [[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

None; the thesis is a philosophy of history, argued through allegorical and
textual readings (Homer, Bacon, Sade, Nietzsche, mass culture). Its empirical
materials are illustrations, not tests.

## Applications

The thesis is the frame that unifies the whole book and the wider Frankfurt-School
program: [[instrumental-reason]] and the [[domination-of-nature]] are its
mechanism, [[mimesis]] its repressed other, the [[culture-industry]] and
[[false-projection]] its two demonstrations. It underwrites
[[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse's]] [[one-dimensionality]] and stands behind later
critiques of technological modernity and (the authors claim to prefigure)
ecological critique.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded in attributed voice: the thesis is charged with performative
contradiction (using reason to indict reason wholesale), with a totalizing
pessimism that leaves no exit and no agent of change, and with historical
over-reach in linking Odysseus, positivism, and Auschwitz on a single line.
Habermas's communicative-reason reconstruction is the major response; defenders
read the book as a diagnosis of the self-undermining tendencies of a reason that
optimizes control while going silent on ends. The full debate is recorded on
[[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]] and analysed in the
[[hubs/theory/critical-theory|Theory Hub]].
