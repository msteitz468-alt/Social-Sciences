# Docile Bodies

## Definition as Coined

In *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* Part Three, chapter 1, [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] states (attributed): the classical age discovered the body as **object and target of power** — "the body that is manipulated, shaped, trained, which obeys, responds, becomes skilful and increases its forces." He defines: **"A body is docile that may be subjected, used, transformed and improved."**

Docility joins two registers of "Man-the-Machine": the anatomico-metaphysical (Descartes, physicians, philosophers — the intelligible body) and the technico-political (regulations of army, school, hospital — the useful body). La Mettrie's *L'Homme-machine*, Foucault argues, is both materialist reduction of the soul and a general theory of *dressage*. Discipline, as a political anatomy of detail, aims at the body as both productive force and subjected force — **utility and docility together**.

## Semantic History

- **1975:** technical term in Foucault's genealogy of [[disciplinary-power]].
- **Reception:** "docile bodies" entered sociology of the body, feminist theory, education studies, and sport ethnography (often looser than Foucault's classical-age apparatus analysis). Distinguish from [[biopower]]'s population pole: docile bodies are the **anatomo-political** pole (individual body), which *History of Sexuality* Vol. I later pairs with biopolitics of the species body.
- Not identical to [[capitalist-discipline]] (Ong 1987) — that concept dialogues with Foucault but is ethnographically specified to factory women in Malaysia.

## Operationalizations

Foucault operationalizes docility through four arts of discipline (Part Three ch.1):

1. **Art of distributions** — enclosure; partitioning; functional sites; rank (and the "table" that transforms the confused, useless, dangerous multitudes into ordered multiplicities).
2. **Control of activity** — timetable; temporal elaboration of the act; correlation of body and gesture; body–object articulation; exhaustive use ("non-idleness").
3. **Organization of geneses** — series of training; exercise; capitalizing time; evolutive individual series.
4. **Composition of forces** — body as segment of a mobile machine; four types of individuality (cellular, organic, genetic, combinatory); tactics.

## Applications

- Anchor concept of [[disciplinary-power]] and of the study [[foucault-discipline-and-punish-study]].
- Links to [[microphysics-of-power]] (scale of analysis) and to prison labour's production of "machine-men" / proletarians in Part Four.
- Contrast object: the sovereign scaffold's body as surface of torture vs. the disciplinary body's forces as target of training.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Passive body?** (attributed feminist and agency critiques): the formulation can be read as leaving little room for embodied resistance; later body sociology often reintroduces practice and agency.
- **Historical scope:** "classical age" discovery is Foucault's periodization; earlier and non-European body disciplines are outside the ingested source.
- **Scale:** micro techniques → macro "disciplinary society" is an interpretive leap; record as his synthesis, not as measured diffusion.
