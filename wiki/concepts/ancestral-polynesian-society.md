# Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS)

## Definition as Coined

**Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS)** is [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Kirch]]’s name for
the reconstructed common socio-cultural baseline from which the historically known
Polynesian societies diverged. In *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution
of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* (1984), Kirch treats APS as the necessary starting point for
any evolutionary account of Polynesian chiefdoms: without a shared ancestor, later
variation cannot be read as transformation. Contact-era societies are modelled as
ethnographic endpoints of internal development from APS via colonization of environmentally
contrastive islands and subsequent mostly isolated change. Kirch called APS the “zero-point”
from which the ethnographic spectrum — relatively egalitarian to elaborately stratified —
all reflect transformations.

Kirch (1984) dated APS as flourishing in West Polynesia roughly a millennium BC; archaeologically
distinguishable from Lapita by about **500 BC**, with separate Tongan and Samoan pathways
underway by about **AD 300** (1984 chronology). Reconstruction focuses on excavated materials
c. 500 BC–AD 300 plus Proto-Polynesian (PPN) lexical evidence.

## Semantic History

The idea that Polynesian societies share deep commonalities is older (comparative
ethnology; Proto-Polynesian linguistics). Kirch rejects a “migration mentality” that treats
ancestral Polynesians as a single ethnic unit entering the Pacific separately from other
Oceanic peoples: Polynesians **became Polynesians in Oceania** over millennia of local
adaptation (following White 1979). Ultimate origins leading back to island Southeast Asia
are explicitly set aside; the 1984 program begins after APS is already in place.

Kirch’s contribution is to fuse **Eastern Lapita archaeology**, **linguistic
reconstruction**, and **controlled ethnographic comparison** into an explicit baseline
society (technology, subsistence, settlement, ritual, social organization) against which
[[tonga]], [[hawaiians|Hawaiʻi]], [[rapanui|Easter Island]], and others can be measured.
Later Kirch and others refined APS/Ancestral Polynesian culture with further archaeology
and historical linguistics; the 1984 version is the primary-ingested statement here.

## Operationalizations

*(Attributed — Kirch 1984 Ch. 3 unless noted.)* APS is reconstructed from three
cross-checked evidence classes:

1. **Archaeology** — Lapita and immediately post-Lapita / Lapitoid sites in the
   Fiji–Tonga–Samoa region; Ancestral Polynesian technology distinguishable from Lapita
   by ~500 BC. Settlement sites ~1,000–7,000 m² with deposit depths suggesting continuous
   occupation perhaps 300–400 years; no large areal exposures excavated as of 1984, so
   internal layout unknown. Direct archaeology implies only dispersed multi-household
   hamlets; **no APS cemeteries** located; shell ornaments not unequivocally rank insignia;
   **no significant public architecture** of later chiefdom type.
2. **Historical linguistics** — PPN lexical reconstructions for social ranks, ritual,
   crops, technology, and landholding groups (Pawley and Green among sources Kirch uses).
   Cognates shared across Tongic and Nuclear Polynesian branches (e.g. Tongan *‘eiki*,
   Futunan *aliki*, Hawaiian *ali‘i* → PPN *‘ariki*) ground claims of ancestral chiefship.
3. **Controlled ethnographic comparison** — shared structural features across historically
   attested Polynesian societies more economically explained as inheritance than independent
   invention or late diffusion. Comparative ethnography alone is insufficient: it captures
   endpoints of millennia of local evolution; sequences require diachronic archaeology.

Kirch’s reconstruction is generalized from multi-site West Polynesian data and allows local
variation; he notes increasing evidence for inter-settlement variability but enough
consistency for a regional model.

### Material culture and production (as of Kirch 1984)

- Tool kit adapted to restricted oceanic raw materials: adzes (gradual development from
  Lapita prototypes; reduced Tridacna shell; more plano-convex forms), stone/shell chisels,
  coral/echinoid abraders, sparse fishing gear (one-piece shell hooks, net weights, Cypraea
  octopus-lure caps), ceramics (water jars, cooking pots, serving bowls) until pottery
  abandonment c. AD 300, earth ovens (PPN *umu*).
- **Subsistence** — broad-based generalist economy (contra Groube’s “oceanic strandloopers”):
  tropical vegeculture with taro–yam central; arboriculture (esp. breadfruit; *maa/*masi*
  fermentation pits); animal husbandry (pig *puaka, dog *kulii, fowl *moa — pig bone at ≥6
  sites as of 1984); marine resources as primary protein (netting, spearing, angling, plant
  poisons). Swidden basis for most cultivation; rudiments of drainage/irrigation may have
  been known (Spriggs 1982 cited).
- **Exchange** — exotic lithics in low frequencies mainly in earliest sites; Kirch
  hypothesized exchange was not a significant APS economic strategy and that growing
  self-sufficiency aided later long-range colonization (contrast wider Lapita exchange).

### Social structure — conical clan and key PPN lexemes

| Reconstructum | Kirch’s gloss (1984) |
|---|---|
| *kainanga | Land-holding descent group under chiefly authority (ramage/conical-clan segment); ancient Malayo-Polynesian structure (Goodenough 1955) |
| *kainga | Minimal descent group / extended household plus lands occupied and cultivated; residence likely a membership criterion (unlike larger *kainanga) |
| *‘ariki | Hereditary chief: first-born in senior line; strong personal *mana and *tapu; Pawley: from older POC title pair “Great One” / “Little One” to common-noun class via primogeniture |
| *fono | Political assembly (esp. adult males); decision-making by consensus within the *kainanga despite hereditary chieftainship |
| *mala‘e / *malae | Open ceremonial assembly ground — open yards for rites and feasts; West Polynesia retained open form; East Polynesia elaborated *marae* into megalithic religious structures |
| *mana, *tapu | Reconstructable with *‘ariki; legitimation via descent and sacred efficacy |
| *tufunga | Expert/specialist/craftsman — probably not hereditary; some reflexes mean “priest” (e.g. Hawaiian *kahuna*) |
| *toa | Warrior (achieved status); potential conflict with *‘ariki over claims to mana |
| *tautai / *tauuta | Seaman-navigator-fisherman / “landlubber” — possible land/sea specialization (Hocart–Sahlins Fijian analogy flagged as suggestion only) |
| *‘atua | Ancestral deities; clan deities as apotheosized chiefly ancestors |
| *kawa, *taano‘a | Kava ritual and bowl; Green speculated some elaborately decorated Lapita dishes as kava vessels |

Kirch (following Kirchhoff 1955; Sahlins 1968) treats the **conical clan** as the
organizing geometry: rank by genealogical distance from a founding ancestor; inherently
segmentary and expansionistic (suited to radiation into ecological zones) yet able to
amalgamate large political units under a ranking chief because branches retain genealogical
interrelations. Land controlled by corporate non-unilinear descent groups/ramages.

**Chiefship as sacerdotal.** Drawing on Williamson (1924) and Koskinen (1960), Kirch
hypothesizes ancestral chiefship concentrated sacred duties and secular rule, with the
oldest form “more clearly sacerdotal”; legitimation via direct male descent from the gods
and associated mana and tapu. The *fono* on the *malae* (where *‘ariki likely invoked
ancestral deities) is consistent with the chief as more sacred than secular ruler. Senior/
junior same-sex sibling terminology (*tuakana / *t(a,e)hina) is treated as fundamental to
rank ideology and retained by all more highly stratified chiefdoms.

**Structural contradictions already in APS** (Kirch’s theoretical claims): (1) junior/senior
opposition in kinship and ranking; (2) high/low mana correlating with rank but also efficacy
in practical action — chiefs as mana receptacles without monopoly, opening usurpation by
*toa*; (3) contradiction between centripetal household underproduction and centrifugal
demands of the larger polity. APS already exceeded pure [[domestic-mode-of-production]]
underproduction (Sahlins 1972): exchange, ritual obligations, and chiefly
maintenance/aggrandizement impelled production beyond household minima. Ancestral surplus
appropriation was likely sacrificial **first-fruits** to the chief as divine exemplar —
later transformed into regularized taxation (*makahiki*, *‘inasi*) in stratified endpoints
(see [[agricultural-intensification]], [[redistribution]]).

## Applications

- Baseline for Kirch’s process chapters (demography, environment, intensification, warfare)
  and for the Tonga / Hawaiʻi / Easter Island case studies.
- Comparative anchor for [[chiefdom]] evolution debates in Oceania; rejects functionalist
  invention of hierarchy as redistributors or irrigation managers — *ariki, mana, tapu, and
  surplus demand already present at colonization (Kirch 1984 synthesis).
- Foundation for the dual horticultural–marine production base transferred as
  [[transported-landscape]] package and later intensified ([[agricultural-intensification]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **1984 reconstruction limits.** Quality tracks the 1980s archaeological sample (no APS
  cemeteries; no large areal exposures; sparse status markers) and then-current linguistics.
  East Polynesian colonization dates and some social inferences have been revised in later
  literature. Absolute chronologies on this page are **as of Kirch 1984**.
- **Inference hierarchy.** Social structure leans heavily on PPN reconstruction and
  controlled comparison where archaeology is silent; treat *kainanga/*kainga/*fono/*malae
  social glosses as attributed reconstructions, not excavated facts.
- **Etic construct.** APS is not an ethnonym used by living communities.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ self-conscious people: APS is a
  cultural-historical construct tied to Polynesian languages and Lapita-derived material
  sequences, **associated with** Proto-Polynesian and Eastern Lapita, not identical to a
  genetic population or a single “people.”

## Update: *On the Road of the Winds* (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]], Ch. 7, drawing on Kirch and Green 2001,
*Hawaiki, Ancestral Polynesia*.)* The 2017 synthesis refines APS's dating and its place in a nested
phylogeny, and updates the 1984 chronology above:
- **Sequence and descent.** APS descends from — and is analytically **distinct from** — the
  earlier, broader **[[ancestral-oceanic-society|Ancestral Oceanic Society]]** (the Proto-Oceanic /
  Lapita-period reconstruction). APS is the later, geographically narrower Western Polynesian stage
  reached via a Proto Central Pacific → Proto Tongic / Proto Nuclear Polynesian split; the two are
  sequential, never to be merged. The homeland region is remembered in later Polynesian oral
  tradition as **[[hawaiki|Hawaiki]]**.
- **Dating (revised).** APS emerges in the second half of the first millennium BCE, following
  Eastern Lapita settlement of Tonga (founding site Nukuleka, ~896–880 BC) and Samoa (Mulifanua,
  ~900–850 BC) — later and tighter than the 1984 "c. 500 BC–AD 300" framing, consistent with the
  short chronology ([[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]]).
- **Method and anchors.** Recovered by the Kirch–Green **triangulation method** (linguistics +
  ethnography + archaeology + biological anthropology cross-checked within a phylogenetic model);
  key archaeological anchor is the Toʻaga site (Ofu, Manuʻa, American Samoa; Kirch's excavations
  1986–89, oldest Polynesian Plain Ware fineware).
- **Reconstructed content.** *\*kainanga* descent groups under *\*qariki* leaders; *\*mana* / *\*tapu*
  central; ritual in a *\*fare* and open *\*malaqe* (ancestor of *marae* / *malae* — the East/West
  divergence Burrows 1938 used to infer settlement direction); only Tangaloa reconstructed as a
  deity (the differentiated Tāne/Tū/Rongo pantheon is a later East Polynesian innovation). Kirch
  characterizes early APS communities as **heterarchical rather than hierarchical**. This is a model
  case of proper evidence-class non-conflation — linguistics, biology, and ethnography treated as
  three *separately converging* lines, not one identity.
