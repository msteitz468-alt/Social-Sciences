# Stigma

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-stigma-1963|Stigma]]* (1963), [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] uses **stigma** for an attribute that deeply discredits when evidence arises that a person differs, in a less desirable way, from others in the category available for them to be — reducing them "from a whole and usual person to a tainted, discounted one" (attributed). It is a special discrepancy between **[[virtual-and-actual-social-identity|virtual and actual social identity]]**.

Goffman insisted a **language of relationships, not attributes**, is needed: an attribute that stigmatizes one type of possessor can confirm the usualness of another, and is therefore neither creditable nor discreditable as a thing in itself (attributed). Stigma is "a special kind of relationship between attribute and stereotype" (attributed).

**Historical note (Goffman):** Greeks used *stigma* for bodily signs cut or burnt to mark slave, criminal, or traitor; Christian and medical metaphorical layers followed; modern use shifts toward the disgrace itself rather than the bodily evidence.

**Three types (attributed):**

1. **Abominations of the body** — physical deformities.
2. **Blemishes of individual character** — weak will, unnatural passions, rigid beliefs, dishonesty, inferred from records of mental disorder, imprisonment, addiction, alcoholism, homosexuality, unemployment, suicidal attempts, radical political behaviour (period list).
3. **Tribal stigma** of race, nation, and religion — transmitted through lineages and contaminating all members of a family. Footnote: in recent British history, low class status could function as tribal stigma.

**Normals:** those who do not depart negatively from the expectations at issue. Goffman wrote that by definition "we" normals believe the person with a stigma is not quite human and exercise discrimination that reduces life chances; normals construct a **stigma theory** — an ideology explaining inferiority and danger (attributed).

**Central feature:** the problem of **acceptance** — the stigmatized individual's receipt of partial and fragile acceptance from normals in mixed contacts (attributed). **Shame** is a central possibility because the stigmatized typically shares the identity beliefs of normals (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Goffman: conceptual typology and interaction analysis, not a survey scale. Later work operationalizes perceived, enacted, anticipated, and internalized stigma — record when those sources are ingested.

## Applications

- Mixed contacts as "primal scenes" of the sociology of stigma (*Stigma* Ch 1).
- Information control for the [[discredited-and-discreditable|discreditable]] ([[passing]], [[covering]], [[social-information]]).
- Continuity with [[moral-career]] (*[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]* and *Stigma* Ch 1).
- Boundary work with [[deviance]] (Ch 5) — stigma processes as general; "social deviants" as a possible core for a deviance field (attributed).
- [[courtesy-stigma]]; [[the-own-and-the-wise]].
- **Ethnic stigma (Eidheim 1969):** Harald Eidheim’s “When Ethnic Identity is a Social Stigma” in *[[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-1969|Ethnic Groups and Boundaries]]* applies Goffman’s apparatus to Coast Sámi / “Coast Lapp” identity in 1960 West-Finnmark — public Norwegian-coded sphere vs closed Lappish sphere; passing and impression management when ethnic ascription is discreditable (see [[coast-saami]], [[ethnic-boundary-theory]], [[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-study]]). Links tribal-stigma type to organizational ethnicity.

## Illness Stigma (Kleinman)

[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]], in *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]*
(1988), takes over Goffman's apparatus (etymology, discrediting/discreditable, spoiled identity,
[[moral-career]]) for chronic illness and distinguishes two pathways (attributed): stigma *brought
to* the patient by societal reaction to a culturally marked label (leprosy; AIDS; mental illness in
China, where taint extends to kin and marriage prospects), versus stigma beginning with the patient's
own acceptance of a stigmatized identity. He adds that *shame* can arise from the clinical encounter
itself, independent of a stigmatizing diagnosis — a bridge to the [[illness-disease-distinction|illness/disease]]
critique of biomedical practice.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Analytic "we normals" standpoint; later disability studies contest the normal/deviant binary.
- Lumping of heterogeneous categories under one scheme.
- Limited structural/political analysis relative to later "structural stigma" research.
- Period vocabulary and examples (character blemish list, racial examples) are historical artifacts of early-1960s sources.
- Ch 4 corrective: stigmatized and normal are **perspectives/roles**, not two piles of concrete persons (attributed) — often lost in textbook compression.
