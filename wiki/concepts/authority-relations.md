Authority relations, in James S. Coleman's rational-choice sociology, are a species of rights-transfer in which one actor (the superordinate) comes to hold the right to direct another actor's (the subordinate's) actions within a bounded domain. Coleman develops the concept in Chapter 4 ("Authority Relations") and Chapter 7 ("From Authority Relations to Authority Systems") of *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990), building it out of the rights-to-act apparatus of Chapter 3 and extending it, via Max Weber's account of bureaucracy, into a general theory of how authority is organized into stable, transferable structures.

## Definition as Coined

Coleman defines an authority relation as holding when actor 2 possesses the right to direct certain of actor 1's actions in some domain; that right must be "vested" in the superordinate — either voluntarily by the subordinate or by ascription (parental or state authority at birth) — before it can be exercised. He draws the central conjoint/disjoint distinction: a conjoint authority relation is one in which the subordinate unilaterally vests authority believing its exercise will serve the subordinate's own interests (Coleman's examples, following Zablocki 1980, include communes, trade unions, religious orders, and following a guide when lost); a disjoint authority relation is one in which authority is vested in exchange for extrinsic compensation, exemplified by employment contracts, Weber's ideal-type bureaucracy, and the common-law of agency. Coleman further distinguishes simple authority relations (two actors; the superordinate personally exercises the vested authority, which cannot be transferred) from complex authority relations (three or more actors; a delegated "lieutenant" exercises authority on the superordinate's behalf), noting that complex relations require the transfer of two distinct rights — the right to control the action, and the right to transfer that right.

## Semantic History

Coleman positions his account against, and as a reconstruction of, Weber's (1947 [1922]) classical treatment of bureaucratic authority, crediting Weber with the key historical shift from a structure of persons to a structure of positions — rights and resources attaching to an office rather than an occupant, exemplified by bureaucracy's hierarchy of offices, defined jurisdictions, merit appointment, salary, and non-ownership of office. Coleman notes that Weber's own perspective starts from an authority system already in place, whereas his own starts from actors holding pre-authority rights and asks how authority comes to be vested at all. Coleman treats Weber's account of charismatic authority as a form of conjoint authority arising under social or personal disorganization (following Zablocki 1980 and Bradley 1987), and flags Weber's own ambivalence — regarding whether charisma is a real quality of the leader or merely one followers perceive, illustrated by Weber's uncertainty over whether Joseph Smith was a "deliberate swindler."

## Operationalizations

Coleman operationalizes the limits of an authority relation along four dimensions — domain/scope, time, physical location, and prescriptiveness (the right to prescribe versus merely proscribe an action) — cross-tabulated into four authority-system types, with Hayek's (1973) distinction between "made order" (organization: prescriptive, narrow domain) and "spontaneous order" (proscriptive, broad domain) as the two classic poles. He operationalizes the "internal morality" of an authority system through three structural tests: (1) whether widespread withdrawal of legitimacy can remove incumbents through the system's own institutions; (2) whether institutions can limit an incumbent's power short of removal; (3) whether an individual can exit the system at low cost after withdrawing rights — illustrated with the Shah of Iran (failing test 1 despite nonviolent departure) and Charles I under the English Parliament (initially failing, later passing test 2). He models the structure of complex authority using the English/American common law of agency — distinguishing "servant" (principal has right of control over physical conduct, and is liable) from "independent contractor" (no such right, no such liability) — and formalizes the principal-agent problem as a non-zero-sum game in which the principal must choose between "attending to actions" (policing) and "attending to outcomes" (contingent compensation), each with characteristic defects.

## Applications

Coleman applies the conjoint/disjoint distinction to explain divergent pathologies: conjoint relations exhibit a systematic free-rider defect, since subordinates let the leader or others act on shared interests, producing a gap between public participatory norms (support for voting, union activity) and low private participation; disjoint relations exhibit shirking and Merton's (1968) "bureaucratic personality" (rule-focus as self-protection), since actions, unlike goods, are inalienable and remain under the subordinate's own control. He extends the analysis to "affine agency," where an agent internalizes the principal's interests as fully as if they were the principal's own, with degrees illustrated by family and national identification (citing kamikaze pilots), employer identification, and identification with a captor (citing Bettelheim 1953 on concentration-camp inmates and Korean-War POWs). Coleman treats authority relations as ongoing contracts, noting that continuing rights of control can be leveraged to extend authority beyond its original domain — sexual harassment on the job is given as an illustrative case, handled briefly and non-graphically. He also analyzes slavery as authority without limits, preferring Finley's (1983) emphasis on the slave's personhood becoming another's property over Patterson's (1977) "institutionalized alienation from labor and kinship," which he judges too weak.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman identifies "authority without intentional exercise" as a structural puzzle for his own framework: a person may cede control of actions or beliefs to another (a commentator, an author, a public figure) who never intends or knows they are exercising authority; Coleman treats this as an ordinary conjoint relation rather than inventing a separate category of "structures of influence," illustrated via Zablocki's (1980) account of a commune member unconsciously mirroring a charismatic leader's gestures. He also identifies the fundamental problem of simple authority structures as non-transferability of the vested right — structurally analogous to bureaucratic span-of-control limits (Simon 1957; Blau and Schoenherr 1971) — with complex authority structures solving this only partially, via delegation, at the cost of new principal-agent problems (loafing, false reporting, misuse of "usage rights" versus "benefit rights"). Coleman's theory is a formal, rational-choice reconstruction; it does not itself adjudicate the sociological debate over the reality versus perceived quality of charisma, which he explicitly notes Weber left unresolved.
