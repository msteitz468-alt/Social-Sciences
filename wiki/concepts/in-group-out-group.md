# In-group and Out-group

## Definition as Coined

[[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]] introduced the distinction in
*[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* (§13), initially in the paired vocabulary
**"we-group" (in-group)** and **"others-group" (out-group)**: *"a differentiation arises
between ourselves, the we-group, or in-group, and everybody else, or the others-groups,
out-groups."* The in-group is defined by internal *"peace, order, law, government, and
industry,"* the out-group by *"war and plunder"* except where agreements modify it (§13).

Sumner's central attributed claim is the **correlativity of internal peace and external
hostility** (§14): *"The relation of comradeship and peace in the we-group and that of
hostility and war towards others-groups are correlative to each other. The exigencies of
war with outsiders are what make peace inside."* War and peace *"have reacted on each other
and developed each other,"* and *"the closer the neighbors, and the stronger they are, the
intenser is the warfare, and then the intenser is the internal organization and discipline
of each."* Loyalty, sacrifice, in-group brotherhood, and *"hatred and contempt for
outsiders"* thus *"all grow together, common products of the same situation"* (§14). The
sentiment attaching to this structure is [[ethnocentrism]].

## Semantic History

Sumner's terms were adopted almost verbatim by 20th-century sociology and social
psychology, becoming among the most durable coinages in the social sciences. The
correlativity thesis — that in-group cohesion is generated by out-group threat — became a
research program: it underwrites the "realistic conflict theory" of intergroup relations
(Sherif) and the functionalist proposition (Coser, after [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]) that
external conflict integrates the group. Social identity theory (Tajfel) later **decoupled**
the two halves Sumner had welded: minimal-group experiments showed in-group favoritism
arising from mere categorization, without any out-group conflict or competition — a direct
empirical challenge to Sumner's claim that hostility is what *makes* the boundary. The
terms "in-group"/"out-group" now circulate far beyond their origin, stripped of Sumner's
war-and-peace mechanism.

## Operationalizations

Sumner offered none. Social psychology supplied several: the minimal-group paradigm
(assignment to arbitrary categories → measured in-group allocation bias); social-distance
scales; measures of perceived intergroup threat. These operationalizations tend to
disconfirm the strong correlativity thesis — in-group preference is measurable in the
absence of out-group antagonism — while confirming the reality and ease of the boundary
itself.

## Applications

- Explaining the co-production of internal solidarity and external enmity in small-scale
  societies (§14) and in modern nationalism (via [[ethnocentrism]] and patriotism).
- Grounding conflict-functionalist accounts of how external threat integrates groups.
- Anchoring the social-psychology of intergroup bias.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Correlativity overstated** — see Semantic History; categorization suffices for
  in-group bias, so out-group hostility is not the necessary source of internal peace.
- **Idealized "peace within."** Sumner's picture of the in-group as a zone of order
  understates internal stratification and conflict (a point his own §46 on the mores of
  subgroups partly concedes).
- **Period framing.** The illustrations (§§16–18) are drawn from the racial-evolutionary
  ethnography of 1906 (see [[sumner-folkways-1906]] reliability notes).
