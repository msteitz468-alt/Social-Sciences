# Exchange of Women

## Definition as Coined

The **exchange of women** is the central analytic premise of [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] [[alliance-theory]]: marriage systems are treated as the circulation of women between groups of men. "It is men who exchange women, and not vice versa"; the woman "figures only as one of the objects in the exchange, not as one of the partners." Extending [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]], women are "that most precious category of goods," and the [[incest-taboo|incest prohibition]] is "the supreme rule of the gift" (*[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]*, 1949).

## Semantic History

Coined as a structural-analytic device, the phrase became — via Gayle Rubin's essay **"The Traffic in Women: Notes on the 'Political Economy' of Sex" (1975)** — a keyword of feminist anthropology, now often used *critically* to name exactly the androcentrism it originally expressed. The concept's two lives (structural premise / object of feminist critique) must both be recorded.

## Operationalizations

In alliance theory, the direction and form of women's circulation defines the system: symmetric, reversible transfer is [[restricted-exchange]]; unidirectional, cyclical transfer is [[generalized-exchange]]. The "value" of the woman transferred, and the compensations attached (bride-price, sister-exchange), are read as expressions of the exchange structure.

## Applications

Grounds Lévi-Strauss's reading of cross-cousin marriage, moiety exogamy, the Kachin and Gilyak systems, and the culminating claim that "the emergence of symbolic thought must have required that women, like words, should be things that were exchanged" — kinship, economics, and language as three modalities of one communicative logic.

## Critiques and Limitations

Lévi-Strauss's own text partly anticipates the objection: "woman could never become just a sign and nothing more, since even in a man's world she is still a person... In contrast to words, which have wholly become signs, woman has remained at once a sign and a value." **Gayle Rubin (1975)** turned the frame against itself, arguing it describes a **sex/gender system** that produces male dominance rather than a neutral structure, and that women exchanged are subjects coerced into the position of objects. This became a founding move of feminist anthropology; the position is recorded here in attributed voice, not as a wiki-voice verdict. See [[alliance-theory]], [[structuralism]].
