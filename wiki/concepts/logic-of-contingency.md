# Logic of Contingency

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Shanks & Tilley, *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* 1987, Ch. 2, drawing on Laclau & Mouffe 1985.)* Against a **logic of necessity** that assumes (1) the social is an intelligible, definable totality and (2) history has a rational substratum (the rationale of evolutionary archaeology), Shanks and Tilley propose a **logic of contingency**: social order as open field of relations, historically particular articulations, primacy of the political (negotiation, strategy, power), without hierarchy of determination that reduces the particular to an abstract social logic.

## Semantic History

- **Laclau & Mouffe (1985):** contingency as challenge to essentialist totalities in socialist/political theory (as S&T cite and adapt).
- **Shanks & Tilley 1987:** imported into archaeology as critique of traditional culture-history narratives and processual systems/variable social archaeology — both treated as operating a logic of necessity (necessary categories, recognition research strategies, telos of totality).
- Later post-processual and practice-theory archaeologies absorb related themes (specificity, power, practice) without always retaining the Laclau–Mouffe label.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Operationalized as **negative heuristics** for research design: refuse a priori variable sets (subsistence, technology, ranking…) that reify before analysis; refuse cross-cultural stage templates that dissolve historical particularity; insist on structure of meaning and political practice in explaining why *this* form rather than another.

## Applications

Critical social archaeology programs of the mid-1980s onward; densifies [[post-processual-archaeology]]'s attack on systems functionalism and neo-evolutionism. Paired in S&T with positive concepts of practice, structure, power, ideology, and material culture as signifying practice (Chs. 3–4).

## Program points (Shanks & Tilley 1987, Ch. 2)

*(Attributed.)* Against social archaeologies that are reductionist or essentialist:

1. No hierarchy of determination (e.g. economy or population–environment as master determinants of institutional form).
2. Social order as relational whole / open field of relations — indeterminate articulation, not closed system.
3. Primacy of the political: practical negotiation, strategy, and power structure social reality; social order is constituted in the practice of individual social actors in historical context, not an abstract universal pattern.
4. Corollary: stress the practical constitution of the past in archaeology itself — archaeology as discursive event mediating past and present.
5. (With further points on refusing eternal recognition strategies and teleologies of totality — see source claims R2-C67–C70.)

**Paired concept — logic of necessity:** defines necessary categories and relations for "society to be society"; research strategy of *recognition* (finding patterns that emanate from pre-defined essentials); telos of totality ("everything in its place"); prioritizes Totality over Fragment, Necessity over Contingency, Essence over Appearance, etc.; occludes the individual as actor.

## Critiques and Limitations

Processual and other critics may treat contingency rhetoric as undercutting explanation and cross-cultural comparison; S&T present it as the condition for explaining **particularity** rather than redescribing it as system function. Reception folds into the [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] (relativism charge vs critical particularism). Not a measurement construct — a research heuristic and polemical diagnostic.
