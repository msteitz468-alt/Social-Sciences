## Definition as Coined

Encephalization refers to the increase in brain size — both in absolute terms and
relative to body size — across the hominin lineage, from early [[homo-habilis]]
(endocranial volumes in the low hundreds of cc) through successive taxa to
anatomically modern [[behavioral-modernity|Homo sapiens]] (roughly 1,300–1,500 cc).
[[klein-human-career-1999]] treats encephalization as one of the central variables
tracked across the whole of hominin evolution, alongside [[bipedalism]] and tool use.

## Semantic History

Klein argues (attributed) that [[homo-erectus|H. ergaster]]'s larger body size
accounts for most of its brain enlargement relative to [[homo-habilis]], so that it
was only slightly more encephalized once body size is controlled for. Klein states
that substantially greater encephalization — endocranial volumes generally exceeding
1,200 cc — occurred only after 500 thousand years ago, in populations resembling
H. ergaster in body size but carrying much larger brains (this population is
discussed under [[homo-heidelbergensis]]).

Klein's whole-career synthesis (Chapter 8 of [[klein-human-career-1999]]) states that
brain enlargement, artifact manufacture, and carnivory were "probably co-dependent" in
earliest Homo around 2.5 million years ago: increased reliance on tools and meat-eating
is offered as part of the explanation for reduced cheek-tooth size, since less
mechanical food processing was required of the dentition once artifacts and diet shift
took on that role.

A key attributed claim, load-bearing for interpreting the emergence of behavioral
modernity: Klein states that "humans virtually everywhere had achieved modern or
near-modern brain size by 200 ky ago" (as of Klein 1999) — brain size, whether measured
absolutely or relative to body size, had roughly approached the modern standard across
all three lineages then extant ([[homo-erectus]], [[neanderthals]], and early
[[behavioral-modernity|H. sapiens]]) well before the archaeological signal of
behavioral modernity appears around 50,000 years ago. Klein's inference: since gross
brain volume was already essentially modern by 200 ka, any neurological change
associated with the emergence of behavioral modernity around 50 ka must have been
organizational (internal reorganization of neural structure/connectivity) rather than
volumetric (a further increase in brain size) — a claim he presents as attributed
interpretation, not a directly observed finding, since brain organization is not
preserved in the fossil record.

## Operationalizations

Klein proposes (attributed) that the emergence of H. ergaster (with its larger brain)
and the appearance of the Acheulean industry were linked events, paralleling — roughly
800,000 years earlier — the appearance of genus Homo itself alongside the first stone
tools. He suggests both transitions may have been abrupt (punctuational; see
[[punctuated-equilibrium]]) rather than gradual, and possibly linked to simultaneous
episodes of global climate change (see `quaternary-glaciation`).

Klein also notes (attributed) that H. erectus, early H. sapiens, and early
H. neanderthalensis all retained robust, rugged postcranial skeletons, implying
reliance on bodily power over artifactual skill relative to anatomically modern
humans, and displayed marked long-term behavioral and artifactual conservatism up
until roughly 50,000–40,000 years ago — a pattern he reads as consistent with
essentially modern brain volume being present long before modern behavior appears in
the record.

## Applications

Encephalization is a key variable distinguishing taxa across the hominin lineage
([[homo-habilis]], [[homo-erectus]], [[homo-heidelbergensis]], [[neanderthals]]) and
is central to the debate over the origins and timing of [[behavioral-modernity]]: if
brain size was already modern by 200 ka, the "human revolution" around 50 ka
(associated with the Upper Paleolithic and its symbolic material culture) cannot be
explained by a further increase in raw neural volume and must instead be explained
organizationally, culturally, or via some other mechanism (see the
modern-human-origins-debate).

## Critiques and Limitations

Klein is explicit that the timing and tempo of the encephalization trend remain
uncertain, owing to a sparse and poorly dated fossil record — attributing a smooth,
gradual increase versus punctuated jumps to the data is itself an interpretive choice,
not a directly observed pattern. The "organizational, not volumetric" explanation for
the 50 ka transition is, on Klein's own framing, an inference from absence (brain
organization leaves no fossil trace) rather than a positive finding, and should be read
as attributed interpretation rather than settled wiki-voice fact.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Attributed; (as of Stringer 2012).*

### Comparative cranial capacity across taxa

*H. erectus*: brain size beyond any ape or australopithecine but small average volume vs later humans. *H. heidelbergensis*: brain volumes overlap smaller *erectus* and larger *sapiens*/Neanderthal values. Neanderthals: large brain volume “like ours” (as of Stringer 2012). Brain-size increase and dental reduction began in *erectus*, developed by heidelbergensis, and reached near-modern levels in Neanderthals (attributed).

### Encephalization quotient (EQ)

Stringer reports EQ ~**3.4–3.8** early humans including large-bodied heidelbergensis; ~**4.3–4.8** for pre-200 ka African ancestors and Neanderthals; ~**5.3–5.4** for Skhul/Qafzeh and Cro-Magnons (highest); thereafter leveling or slight decline (as of Stringer 2012). Brain volume and EQ took off ~**2 Ma** with meat-eating and toolmaking; cerebral hemispheres expanded more than cerebellum until recent humans, in whom cerebellum is proportionately larger; average brains ~**10% smaller** over the last 20 ka (attributed).

### Shape change and development

Modern brain/braincase shape became shorter/higher with upper parietal expansion; Gunz et al. suggest shape divergence of archaic vs modern skulls began soon after birth; CT work showed frontal lobe relative size changed less than external forehead (as of Stringer 2012). Birth-canal brain-size threshold of ~500 cc must have been reached in *H. erectus* time, implying extended post-birth immaturity vs apes; estimated molar eruption ages for erectus ~5, 9, 15 years vs modern ~6, 12, 18 and chimpanzee ~3, 6, 10; moderns insert childhood and juvenile phases between infancy and adolescence; Neanderthals with large brains must have had long childhoods, but some evidence (Scladina; synchrotron Engis/Scladina/Le Moustier) suggests they reached adulthood earlier than average modern humans (as of Stringer 2012). Devil’s Tower Neanderthal child CT: brain size 1,370–1,420 cc with more growth to come (attributed).

### Social Brain Hypothesis and memory

Social Brain Hypothesis (Humphrey, Dunbar, Byrne, Whiten via Stringer): large brains evolved not only for foraging/hunting/tools but for complex social life; primate neocortex size correlates better with social than environmental complexity; human neocortex ~85% of total brain weight; Dunbar’s number ~100–220 (avg ~148) (as of Stringer 2012). Wynn and Coolidge: key modern-mind change was enhanced episodic/autobiographical working memory; parietal lobes implicated but networked with frontal and temporal; Ambrose stressed integration of working, prospective, and constructive memory for large social networks — archaic humans like Neanderthals lacked full brain integration and hormonal systems for much larger modern social networks (via Stringer). Sarah Blaffer Hrdy: large-brained dependent infants by *H. erectus* already required alloparents; cooperative breeding enabled slow growth and still bigger brains (via Stringer).

### Against Klein’s ~50 ka volumetric/organizational switch

Klein holds brain size essentially modern by ≥200 ka so ~50 ka change must be organizational not size (via Stringer). Stringer argues against Klein’s ~50 ka cognitive event: frontal heightening and parietal expansion already present ~100 ka earlier at Omo Kibish and [[herto]]; no clear physical brain change at 50 ka (as of Stringer 2012). Weaver, Roseman, and Stringer: cranial differences between Neanderthals and moderns can be explained by genetic **drift** rather than special adaptive accounts after ~400 ka separation (attributed).
