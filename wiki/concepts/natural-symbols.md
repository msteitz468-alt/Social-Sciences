# Natural Symbols

Title concept of [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]'s *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]* (1970). Program: [[grid-group-analysis]]. Study: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]].

## Definition as Coined

Douglas's thesis is **negative and constructive**. Negative: there are no natural symbols in the sense of universal, pre-social meanings of the human body (blood, bones, breath, excrement, etc.). Constructive: every culture **naturalizes** a certain view of the body so that selected bodily media carry social meanings; body symbolism is always in service to social intentions. The book seeks regularities: which forms of social life bring forth which varieties of symbolic expression.

## Semantic History

- Pre-Douglas: "natural symbols" can mean symbols taken to be given by nature or body (a foil Douglas rejects).
- Douglas 1970: the phrase is the problem, not the solution — *Natural Symbols* argues against naturalness while explaining why naturalization happens.
- Rodney Needham's seminar paper on natural symbols (acknowledged) helped focus the interest; Needham is not the coiner of Douglas's thesis.
- Later body studies and embodiment theory may cite the book while shifting the research object; keep Douglas's claim attributed and dated.

## Operationalizations

Read body and ritual symbolism against [[grid-and-group]] position; compare hierarchy (body as hierarchy), sect (body as closed system), individualism (body as energy), etc., as Douglas proposes — always attributed.

## Applications

Chapters on the two bodies, trance, waste, witchcraft, and anti-ritualism in *Natural Symbols*; links back to body-as-society in *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* Ch. 7.

## Critiques and Limitations

Strong social determination of symbolism is contested by psychologies of universal body schema and by more agentive/practice theories of embodiment. Douglas's own later work simplifies diagrams without abandoning the anti-naturalness thesis.
