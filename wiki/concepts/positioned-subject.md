# Positioned Subject

Central concept of [[rosaldo-renato|Renato Rosaldo]]'s *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]* (1989).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Rosaldo 1989.)* Rosaldo defined the "positioned (and repositioned) subject": the analyst occupies a particular structural location (age, gender, outsider status, life experience, position in relations of domination) that enables some insights and precludes others, and — following hermeneutic method — **repositions** interpretively as inquiry proceeds. "All interpretations are provisional; they are made by positioned subjects who are prepared to know certain things and not others." Correspondingly, the people studied are themselves **analyzing subjects** whose perceptions must be taken nearly as seriously as the analyst's own: "Neither ethnographers nor their subjects hold a monopoly on the truth."

## Semantic History

*(Attributed.)* The concept condenses Rosaldo's critique, across *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]*, of the classic ideal of the detached, neutral, omniscient observer (Ch. "After Objectivism") and of the myth of the "Lone Ethnographer." It anticipates his later synthesizing notion of **relational knowledge** (Ch. "Border Crossings"), in which analyst and subject are mutually interpreting positioned subjects.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; a stance. In practice Rosaldo asked how age, gender, association with a neocolonial regime, and life experience shape what an ethnographer can learn — and applied the test symmetrically ("so-called natives are also positioned subjects who have a distinctive mix of insight and blindness": older vs. younger Ilongot men, chief mourners vs. distant kin).

## Applications

Rosaldo grounded the idea in his own experience of bereavement, which he said gave him analytical access to the **force** of the rage in [[ilongot|Ilongot]] grief that detached fieldwork had missed. In "Subjectivity in Social Analysis" he extended it to **subordinate knowledge** — "the dominated usually understand the dominant better than the reverse" — reading Geertz, Jean Briggs (*Never in Anger*), Fanon, Marx, and Zora Neale Hurston to argue that "unmanly" states (rage, feebleness, embarrassment) are analytical resources that the Weberian "masculine heroics" of detachment suppress. The concept underwrites his critiques of objectivism and of [[imperialist-nostalgia|imperialist nostalgia]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Rosaldo distinguished the positioned subject from what he saw as a self-absorbed reflexivity in which "the self-absorbed Self loses sight altogether of the culturally different Other" — situating his method between classic detachment (which slid "from the ideal of detachment to actual indifference") and confessional navel-gazing.
