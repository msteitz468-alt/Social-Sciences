# Reality of Everyday Life

## Definition as Coined

Berger and Luckmann, Part One of *[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|The Social Construction of Reality]]* (1966), following [[schutz-alfred|Schutz]]: the **reality of everyday life** is the **paramount reality** — reality *par excellence* — the taken-for-granted, intersubjective world of common-sense experience.

Attributed features:

- Appears already objectified and ordered independent of my apprehension; language continuously provides objectifications.
- Organized around the **here and now**; zone within reach / world of working dominated by the **pragmatic motive**.
- **Intersubjective**: shared with others; common-sense knowledge is knowledge shared in normal self-evident routines; natural attitude suspends doubt “until further notice.”
- Other realities (dreams, theory, religion, play) are **finite provinces of meaning** — enclaves entered by a shock or leap; language still often grounds them back in everyday life.
- Temporal structure is coercive (standard time, finitude); shapes biography and agenda.

Part One uses **phenomenological analysis** (descriptive; brackets causal, genetic, and ontological claims) as prolegomena to the genetic sociology of Parts Two and Three.

## Semantic History

Schutz’s *Lebenswelt* analysis made available for the sociology of knowledge; B&L make everyday reality the central object of the redefined discipline. Contrasts with classical sociology of knowledge’s focus on theoretical ideas and ideology.

## Operationalizations

Phenomenological description, not a variable. Later ethnomethodology studies the *methods* by which everyday reality is achieved (different program).

## Applications

Baseline for the [[social-construction-of-reality]] program; contrast cases (conversion, theory, night-side of reality); opening of institutional and socialization analysis.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Paramount reality” may be culturally weighted toward modern everyday pragmatism; B&L allow that other societies weight provinces differently. Phenomenological brackets mean Part One is not yet causal sociology — by design.
