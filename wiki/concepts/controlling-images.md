# Controlling Images

## Definition as Coined

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]].) **Controlling images** are the
stereotyped representations of Black womanhood that operate in the **hegemonic domain** of
the [[matrix-of-domination]] to make intersecting oppressions of race, class, gender, and
sexuality appear "natural, normal, and an inevitable part of everyday life." Following
Hazel Carby (1987), Collins argues the images function "not to reflect or represent a
reality but... as a disguise, or mystification, of objective social relations" — they
persist even after the material conditions that produced them change, because they serve an
ongoing ideological function rather than a descriptive one. They rest on **binary/oppositional
thinking** (white/black, male/female, reason/emotion, culture/nature) whose devalued halves
converge on Black women, objectifying them as the "Other."

## The Principal Images

Each image is cross-walked against all four axes of oppression (attributed to Collins):

- **Mammy** — "the faithful, obedient domestic servant," created to justify the economic
  exploitation of enslaved and domestic-service labor; the "normative yardstick" of
  deference; desexualized. Collins notes counter-evidence: Black sources depict the mammy
  as "cunning, prone to poisoning her master" (Barbara Christian 1985). A "modern
  mammy"/"mammification" (Omolade 1994) targets Black professional women.
- **Matriarch** — the "overly aggressive," emasculating mother, racialized via the 1965
  Moynihan Report, which reversed earlier accounts (DuBois; Frazier) that treated
  female-headed households as an *outcome* of oppression; functions to blame Black mothers
  for poverty and to discipline women's assertiveness.
- **Welfare mother / welfare queen** — an updated slavery-era "breeder woman" image; the
  "welfare queen" (Lubiano 1992) emerged under the 1980 Reagan administration to justify
  social-spending cuts.
- **Black lady** — the middle-class professional coded as too consumed by work to keep a
  man and as taking jobs "reserved" for others; a superficially positive variant.
- **Jezebel / hoochie** — the hypersexualized image originating under slavery to rationalize
  the sexual assault of enslaved women; marks the boundary of "deviant" female sexuality.
  Collins reads the contemporary "hoochie" through misogyny in some hip-hop.

The images form a system: mammy and Black lady are desexualized; matriarch and welfare
mother are stigmatized through fertility; jezebel/hoochie is hypersexualized.

## Semantic History

Coined in the 1st edition (1990); the 2nd edition (2000) adds the "welfare queen," "Black
lady," and "hoochie" as updated variants and situates the whole set explicitly within the
hegemonic domain of the [[matrix-of-domination]].

## Operationalizations

Analyzed through close reading of popular culture, media, social-science discourse, and
policy (e.g., coercive contraception; adolescent-pregnancy research), rather than by
content-count metrics — though Collins cites supporting content analyses (e.g., Cowan and
Campbell 1994 on racialized pornography).

## Applications

Central to Collins's account of Black women's oppression (Ch. 4) and its resistance through
[[self-definition]] (Ch. 5). Institutions — schools, media, government, and even Black
churches and families — are theorized as "complex sites where dominant ideologies are
simultaneously resisted and reproduced."

## Critiques and Limitations

Collins herself complicates the concept: because most Black women do **not** internalize the
images (Ch. 5), the hegemonic domain is "much less cohesive or uniform than imagined." She
also warns that replacing negative images with *positive* stereotypes solves nothing if the
underlying function of stereotyping-as-control is left intact.
