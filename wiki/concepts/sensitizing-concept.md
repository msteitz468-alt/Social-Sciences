# Sensitizing Concept

## Definition as Coined

[[blumer-herbert|Herbert Blumer]] contrasts **definitive concepts** (clear attributes, fixed benchmarks allowing clean identification of instances) with **sensitizing concepts**. A sensitizing concept, he argues, "lacks such specification of attributes or bench marks"; instead of enabling the user to move directly to the instance, it "merely suggests directions along which to look" (*[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]* — essay "What Is Wrong with Social Theory?", first published 1954).

Blumer's claim (attributed): most concepts of sociology and social psychology are fundamentally sensitizing instruments, not definitive ones. Treating them as if they were definitive produces pseudo-precision and detaches theory from the empirical world.

## Operationalizations

- **Definitive path:** specify attributes → identify instances by rule → measure.
- **Sensitizing path:** use the concept to orient observation → examine empirical instances carefully → refine the concept's content from the world (partner procedures: [[exploration-and-inspection]]).

## Applications

- Justifies flexible naturalistic research designs against premature operationalization.
- Explains chronic "vagueness" of social-science concepts as partly structural to the subject matter, not only as failure of rigor.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested: critics argue the sensitizing/definitive contrast excuses soft measurement or blocks cumulative science.
- Risk that "sensitizing" becomes a blank check for undefined terms.
- Blumer does **not** reject precision; he rejects false precision that ignores the nature of the empirical social world.
