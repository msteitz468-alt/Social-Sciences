# Serious games

## Definition as Coined

[[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]] uses **serious games** as a compact image for social life under [[practice-theory]]: people do not merely enact material necessity or cultural scripts but live with purpose and intention; they are defined by social and cultural contexts that frame resources *and* desires; social life is relational (cooperation and competition, solidarity and exploitation); **serious** marks the constant play of power and that much is at stake; the whole phrase refuses the split of “discourse” vs “acts,” treating experience as interwoven images and practices in which history constructs people and games, and people make history by enacting, reproducing, and transforming those games (*[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-1999|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]]*, 1999, “Serious Games” section; *proposed_by:* [[ortner-sherry]]).

In the Everest book, Himalayan mountaineering is the central serious game of the sahib–Sherpa encounter — a dream and a practice, solidarity and power — only intelligible through the different ways each party entered the reality and imagination of the other relative to games they brought and games that evolved on expeditions.

## Semantic History

Ortner had already developed practice vocabulary in “Theory in Anthropology since the Sixties” (1984) and *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* (1989: practice, structure, actor, history; [[cultural-schema]]). “Serious games” is her later condensation emphasizing **intention/desire**, **relationality**, and **stakes/power** against both pure constructionism (dominant representations remake the weak entirely) and pure resistance ethnography (the weak appear only as reverse of domination). The phrase appears as programmatic framing in *Life and Death* (1999) and is flagged as recently conceptualized in her own prior essays (Ortner cites her own earlier formulation in the book). Later secondary literature sometimes treats “serious games” as Ortner’s distinctive brand of practice theory; this vault currently primary-grounds it in the 1999 monograph.

## Operationalizations

No standard metric. Analytic checklist (as Ortner deploys it on mountaineering):

1. **Representations** — who writes the encounter; double reading of dominant texts (orientalism *and* ethnographic recovery).
2. **Who the players are** — class, gender, and internal stratification (not homogenized “West” vs “Sherpas”).
3. **Intentions/desires** — Geertzian thick description of purpose (sahib modernity/manhood; Sherpa money/modernity/respect with different meanings).
4. **The encounter** — Sahlins’s “structure of the conjuncture”: joint task under power asymmetry produces history that changes expeditions *and* the backgrounds both sides bring.

## Applications

- *[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]]* (1999) — primary book-length application. Mountaineering is the central serious game of the sahib–Sherpa encounter; **monastic reform** is the parallel early-twentieth-century game (*High Religion* foundings + post-founding campaign). Ortner elaborates (Ch.6): games include purposes, discursive categories, organization and relative power of players; they thrive on difference (Sherpa/sahib permutations). Ch.9: Sherpas always play **several** games at once; prefer **remaking/reconfiguration** language over corruption/spoilage narratives.
- Death and base-camp funerals (Ch.5): multivalent intentionality — grief, expanded religious practice, dramatization of cost, identity, internal monastic-vs-popular debate — as serious-game play under risk (*agency* densification).
- Gender (Ch.8): “liberation games” for memsahibs and Sherpa women climbers within male-dominated expedition games.
- Related earlier practice ethnography: *[[ortner-high-religion-study|High Religion]]* (1989) — monastery foundings as intentional historical action through [[cultural-schema|cultural schemas]] — without the “serious games” label as chapter program.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The image is deliberately broad; critics of practice theory more generally may see it as under-specified relative to Bourdieu’s habitus/field apparatus or Giddens’s duality of structure.
- Risk of treating “game” as metaphor that softens structural violence — Ortner’s “serious” adjective is meant to block that, but the metaphor still invites misreading as play.
- Intention-centered framing must stay attributed and dated; it is Ortner’s theoretical commitment, not a wiki-voice finding about all social life.
