# Sagali

The Trobriand (Kiriwina) mortuary distribution — the sequence of public giveaways following a death through which the deceased's social relationships are dismantled and settled. As a noun *sagali* is "a distribution of any kind of object of exchange, including food"; as a verb, "to divide among everyone present," and by extension "to settle accounts" and "reclaim ownership." The women's mortuary ceremony ([[womens-wealth|women's wealth]] distribution) is its climactic phase and the empirical heart of [[weiner-annette-b|Weiner]]'s *[[weiner-women-of-value-1976|Women of Value, Men of Renown]]*.

## Definition as Coined

Among the [[trobriand-islanders|Trobriand Islanders]], death sets off a graded series of distributions organized by the deceased's uterine kin — the **owners** (*toliuli*, "owners of the dead person's things") of the [[dala]] — who give, while **workers** (*toliyouwa*, blood kin of the deceased's father and spouse) prepare the body, mourn, and receive. Weiner reported (as observed 1971–72) the sequence:

- **Burial** within a day (three days' singing for a chief), with removal of decorations and valuables; the *kosi* spirit is guarded against while the *baloma* (ancestral spirit) prepares to depart for the island of Tuma.
- **Sigiliyawali** (first sagali, day after burial) — repays workers; a cluster of named sub-distributions of food, valuables, and bundles.
- **Tadabali** (second sagali) — women's-wealth payments releasing the secluded widow/widower by stages.
- The **women's mortuary ceremony** proper (*lisaladabu*), held four to eight months later, at which women distribute skirts and bundles across some sixteen named sub-exchanges (including *kaymelu*, "the most important," repaying the men who gave raw yams).
- Further annual *sagali*, each devoted to one produce (yams, taro, betel, fish, pork), and — traditionally — skull and bone rites concluding years later.

## Semantic History

Malinowski (1929) described the mortuary sequence but, Weiner argued (attributed), treated it as exotic and emphasized the men's side; her reanalysis foregrounds the women's ceremony he had noted only in passing ("women... conduct some parts of the ceremonial distributions themselves," 1929, p. 37). Vanoi, her informant, called the women's ceremony *sagali pela baloma* ("a distribution for the spirit of the deceased") and likened it to competitive kula and to *kayasa* (competitive harvest display) — a "game" (*mwasawa*) in which women strive to "be first."

## Operationalizations

Sagali are measured in objects distributed. Weiner's largest attended case — the ceremony for Obwelela (a man in his thirties, not high rank), April 1972 — totalled **30,000 bundles, 212 colorful skirts, 139 pieces of trade cloth, A$25, 13 clay pots, across 1,035 individual transactions**. Scale reflects the wealth of the deceased's affines and kin and is a political statement, not merely a ritual obligation.

## Applications

Weiner read the sagali (attributed) as the mechanism by which society negates the "antisocial event" of death: the deceased is "untied" from the reciprocal relationships built over a lifetime, and a pure ancestral *baloma* is returned to the [[dala]] — the cosmological payoff of [[reproduction-regeneration]]. Men reconstitute the deceased's social network by handling the physical remains; women, by giving away [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]], "restore that which is dala to their dala." The event is also a live arena of political competition — over who wears the long mourning skirt (*sepwana*), over burial-site rights, and over displays of affinal wealth.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with the wider argument of *Women of Value*, the interpretive claims rest on one fieldworker's 1971–72 observation; the practices are date-stamped to that moment (skull/bone rites were by then curtailed by government and mission prohibition and only reported, not witnessed). The cosmological reading is attributed, not established across independent evidence classes. See [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]] for the restudy's disputed points.
