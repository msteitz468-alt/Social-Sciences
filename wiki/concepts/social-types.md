# Social types (social species)

## Definition as Coined

In Chapter IV of *[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|The Rules of Sociological Method]]* (1895), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] requires a branch of sociology devoted to the **constitution and classification of social species (types)**. A social fact can be labelled [[normal-and-pathological|normal or abnormal]] only **relative to a given social species**; hence typology is methodologically mandatory (attributed).

He positions the concept as a **middle ground** between:

- **Historical nominalism** — each people is unique, incomparable; generalization almost impossible.
- **Philosophical extreme realism** — all societies are only temporary modes of a single human society progressing toward one ideal.

Social species allow **comparison and law-seeking** without dissolving historical diversity into a single teleological Humanity (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Moment | Usage |
|---|---|
| *Division of Labor* (1893) | Societies ordered by solidarity type ([[mechanical-solidarity]] / [[organic-solidarity]]) and legal index; evolutionary ranking of “lower”/“higher” societies (period language) |
| *Rules* Ch. IV (1895) | Formal classification program via [[social-morphology|morphology]] |
| *Suicide* (1897) | Rates compared across confessions, family statuses, nations as different structural conditions — type-relative interpretation of pathology |
| Later | “Social type” becomes ordinary comparative vocabulary; evolutionary species ladder largely abandoned or heavily revised |

## Operationalizations

Durkheim’s preferred basis (attributed, *Rules* Ch. IV):

1. Start from the **simplest** society — the **horde** (undivided segment).
2. Build higher types by **compounding** of segments (clans, tribes, polysegmentary societies, etc.).
3. Classify by **morphological structure** — number and nature of parts, degree of coalescence — not by dominant ideas or political labels alone.
4. Analogy with biological species is heuristic; he notes limits of the analogy.

Related causal variable for change within/between types: [[dynamic-density]] / volume of the inner social environment (*Rules* Ch. V; *Division of Labor*).

## Applications

- Normality judgments (*Rules* Ch. III–IV).
- Comparative legal morphology (*Division of Labor*).
- Comparative moral statistics (*Suicide*).
- Notes on civilization types among subsequent methodological writings (Lukes volume).

## Critiques and Limitations

Attributed critiques: evolutionary ranking and ethnographic base reflect 19th-c. colonial anthropology; morphological classification underweights culture and meaning; “horde” as origin is speculative; Galton’s problem affects cross-type comparison ([[comparative-method]]); later sociology often drops species-talk for ideal types ([[ideal-type]]) or historically specific formations.
