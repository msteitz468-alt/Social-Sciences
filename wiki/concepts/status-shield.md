# Status Shield

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]* Ch 8, [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] argues that **status** provides a **shield** against the displaced feelings of others — especially anger. Higher-status persons are freer to express aggression and less often its target; lower-status persons (and women more often than men in public-contact service) receive more free-floating frustration and must manage it as part of the job (attributed).

Closely tied to **status enhancement** as emotional labor's task for many service workers (making the customer feel important, cared for, right) versus **status degradation** in bill collecting (deflating the debtor).

## Semantic History

Builds on stratification and gender analysis of feeling rights: a "doctrine of feelings" that codes women's emotionality as expressive resource while denying equal protection from others' unmanaged feeling (attributed). Related to but distinct from [[status-panic|Mills's status panic]] (anxiety about middle-class standing) — the shield is about interactional insulation, not status anxiety.

## Operationalizations

Illustrative occupational ethnography (flight attendants absorbing passenger aggression; gender asymmetry in who is yelled at); not a quantitative index in the primary text.

## Applications

- Explains why emotional labor's human costs fall unevenly by gender and rank.
- Links private family training in niceness/"shadow labor" to public-contact vulnerability.
- Informs later care-work and nursing burnout literatures (afterword pointers).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Primary evidence is qualitative and airline-centered.
- Intersection with race and class is noted (e.g., racist passenger abuse) but not fully theorized as a multi-dimensional shield.
