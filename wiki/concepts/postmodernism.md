# Postmodernism

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Jean-François Lyotard, via [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson 2011]].)*
Lyotard defined "the postmodern condition" as **"incredulity toward metanarratives"** —
a loss of confidence in the large totalizing frameworks that structured Western
knowledge, replaced by local, plural, provisional accounts. Johnson glosses the
metanarratives postmodernists reject as including: the belief in scientific
progress; unilinear cultural-evolutionary schemes (band/tribe/chiefdom/state);
universal moral systems; and notions of historical "progress" as such.

Two further figures are central to the postmodern cluster as Johnson presents them
(attributed): **Jacques Derrida**, associated with the claim that there is "nothing
outside the text" and with **deconstruction** — reading texts against themselves to
expose their internal contradictions and unstated assumptions; and **Michel
[[foucault-michel|Foucault]]**, whose account of the modern "disciplinary society"
holds that norms of knowledge and power are epoch-specific rather than
timeless or universal.

## Semantic History

Lyotard's 1979 diagnosis (of the condition of knowledge in advanced capitalist
societies) was taken up across the humanities and social sciences through the
1980s–1990s, including archaeology. On [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson's]]
explicit account, **postprocessual archaeology "parallels the postmodern turn ...
rather than being derived from it"** — that is, postprocessualism arose from its own
disciplinary critique of processual/functionalist archaeology and only subsequently
came to be read alongside, and partly informed by, wider postmodern philosophy; the
relationship is one of intellectual affinity and convergence, not straightforward
derivation. This is Johnson's own argued position, not a settled consensus.

## Operationalizations

Postmodernism is not itself an empirical method; in Johnson's presentation it
functions as a philosophical backdrop that licenses methodological pluralism, a
suspicion of grand explanatory schemes, and attention to how archaeological
narratives are constructed rather than simply "read off" the evidence.

## Applications

- Cited (attributed, Johnson 2011) as parallel context for the rise of
  [[post-processual-archaeology|postprocessual archaeology]] and the broader
  [[archaeological-pragmatism|"new pragmatism"]] critique of positivist middle-range
  theory.
- Foucault's disciplinary-society thesis and Derrida's deconstruction are invoked as
  intellectual resources archaeologists have drawn on for critiquing the authority of
  "scientific" archaeological narrative.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed, per Johnson 2011.)* Common criticisms include: that postmodern
relativism, taken to its logical end, disables any grounds for judging between
accounts — the standard reductio offered by critics is that an unrestrained
relativism cannot in principle rule out Holocaust denial as "just another
narrative"; and, from the political Left, that treating inequality and violence as
matters of competing "language games" trivializes their reality and stakes.
Johnson notes archaeologists broadly reject unrestrained relativism of this kind
(see [[archaeological-pragmatism]]) while retaining postmodernism's critique of
totalizing metanarratives.

## Harvey's Political-Economic Account

*(Attributed to [[harvey-david|David Harvey]], *[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The
Condition of Postmodernity]]*, 1989 — a distinct sociological/geographic account of the same
term, alongside the archaeology-framed reading above.)*

Where Lyotard treats postmodernism as a condition of *knowledge*, Harvey treats it as a
**"historical-geographical condition"** — the cultural and experiential registration of a
shift in the political economy of capitalism from [[fordism|Fordism]] to
[[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]], mediated by an intensified round of
[[time-space-compression|time-space compression]] after the crisis of overaccumulation that
peaked in 1973. On this account postmodern culture — its ephemerality, pastiche, fixation on
surface and image (the "simulacrum"), and "depthlessness" (Jameson) — is not an autonomous
epochal break but the aesthetic expression of accelerated capital turnover and volatility.

Harvey's diagnostic phrases: the postmodern condition marks a **"triumph of aesthetics over
ethics,"** of image over narrative, and its cultural production is one in which "sheer
profit-seeking is determinant in the first instance." He reads modernism (c. 1848–1918) and
postmodernism (post-1972) as two cultural responses to successive waves of compression, and
argues postmodernism is therefore "much more continuity than difference" with modernism —
"a particular kind of crisis within" it. His response to postmodernism's rejection of
metanarrative is not to abandon but to *renew* [[historical-materialism|historical
materialism]] and the Enlightenment project. Whether this materialist reading, or the
epochal-break reading it opposes, is correct is a live dispute — see
[[postmodernity-epochal-break-debate]].
