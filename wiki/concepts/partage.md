# Partage

**Partage** is the historical practice — and, in recent museum advocacy, a
proposed policy revival — of **dividing excavated finds** between the host
government (or local museum) and the foreign archaeological expedition or its
sponsoring museum. [[cuno-james|James Cuno]] (*[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who
Owns Antiquity?]]*, 2008) treats restored partage as a cooperative alternative
to total nationalist retention of antiquities.

## Definition as Coined

Under classical partage arrangements (late nineteenth and early twentieth
centuries, as described by Cuno), foreign excavators worked with local
permission; after recording, a negotiated share of finds went to the local
authority and a share left the country for the excavating institution. Cuno
(attributed, 2008) cites examples such as the building of major Gandharan
holdings at the Musée Guimet via such arrangements.

## Semantic History

- **High period:** partage as standard diplomatic-scientific practice under
  Ottoman and other host regimes, before the mid-twentieth-century wave of
  national patrimony laws.
- **Decline:** Cuno attributes near-disappearance of partage to "national
  retentionist cultural property laws" in the second half of the twentieth
  century (attributed, 2008).
- **Revival proposal:** cultural-internationalist museum directors propose
  restoring partage to feed both local museums and encyclopedic collections
  while incentivizing scientific excavation over looting (Cuno's claim —
  position, not established finding).

## Operationalizations

| Element | Typical form |
|---|---|
| Host share | Objects retained in national/local museums |
| Expedition share | Objects exported to sponsoring museum/university |
| Legal basis | Concession / excavation permit / bilateral agreement |
| Recording | Cataloguing before division (ideal of scientific excavation) |

## Applications

- Historical formation path for parts of European and North American museum
  collections of ancient art (alongside purchase, gift, and colonial seizure —
  these paths must not be collapsed).
- Policy alternative, in Cuno's program, to both unregulated market acquisition
  and total source-state lock-down (position on
  [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Consent under empire:** critics note that historical partage often operated
  under colonial or semi-colonial power imbalances; "agreement" was not
  symmetric.
- **Does not solve post-1970 looting:** Renfrew-side arguments hold that market
  demand for unprovenanced objects, not the absence of partage, drives
  destruction of context.
- **Source-state sovereignty:** cultural-nationalist positions treat permanent
  export of newly excavated finds as illegitimate regardless of scientific
  benefit.
