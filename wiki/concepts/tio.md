# Tio (mine devil / uncle)

## Definition as Coined

In highland Bolivian tin mines, as synthesised by [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1980) mainly from **June Nash** and related sources, the **Tio** ("uncle") — also identified as the devil — is regarded as the **true owner of the mine and the mineral**. Miners propitiate him (coca, alcohol, cigarettes, and more elaborate *ch'alla* rites) to maintain production, find rich veins, and reduce accidents. He is simultaneously a **gluttonous** spirit bent on destruction if neglected or offended.

Iconography in reported accounts includes a blond, bearded, red-faced *gringo* apparition and sculpted mine images — conquest-coded male power in Taussig's reading.

## Operationalizations

Ritual frequency (e.g. *ch'alla* twice weekly before mid-1960s suppression — as Taussig cites); presence of Tio images in stopes; offering inventories. Primary measures live in Nash, not re-measured by Taussig.

## Applications

- Second pole of Taussig's South American comparison ([[bolivian-tin-miners]] vs [[cauca-valley-afro-colombians]]).
- Drama of Tio vs Pachamama / Virgin as salvation of fertility principles under class struggle (Taussig's interpretation).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Secondary for this wiki until Nash is ingested** — do not treat Taussig as underground ethnographer of the Tio.
- Continuity with precolonial *supay*/mountain spirits is historiographically contested.
- Not identical to Cauca [[devil-contract]] (collective propitiation vs individual production pact).
