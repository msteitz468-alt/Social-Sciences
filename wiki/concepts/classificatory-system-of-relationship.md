# Classificatory System of Relationship

## Definition as Coined

In [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems]]* (1871), the **classificatory system** is the second radical form of kinship terminology: it **classifies** multiple genealogical positions under shared terms (e.g. father and father’s brother under one term in many American Indian systems), following a plan Morgan traced across the **Ganowanian** (his American Indian construct), **Turanian**, and **Malayan** series. He treated the Seneca-Iroquois form as the American type case.

## Semantic History

- **Morgan 1871:** typology + evolutionary conjectural solution (classificatory terms as residues of prior marriage institutions).
- **Later anthropology:** retained the insight that terminologies have **structure**; renamed Morgan’s Turanian ≈ **Dravidian** type and Malayan ≈ **Hawaiian** type (Tooker 1997); largely rejected universal evolutionary marriage sequence ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]).
- Engels used classificatory evidence for [[group-marriage]] stages in *Origin of the Family*.

## Operationalizations

Schedule diagnostics for mergers and bifurcations; subtype contrasts Malayan (most “simple” in Morgan’s ranking) vs Turanian/Ganowanian.

## Applications

Parts II–III of *Systems*; Iroquois ethnography; Marxist kinship evolutionism.

## Critiques and Limitations

Sociological explanation of terms via ancient marriage forms contested; linguistic/cognitive alternatives proposed; colonial data quality; etic “Ganowanian/Turanian” labels obsolete as language-family science.

## Rivers' Oceanic morphological study

[[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]] took over Morgan's classificatory system and, against McLennan
and Westermarck, insisted it is not "merely a system of titles of address" but carries real
duties and privileges and is "of the utmost social importance" (attributed). In
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914) he separated a
system's **morphological structure** from its **linguistic content**, surveyed the great
diversity of Oceanic systems, and argued that terminological features track **forms of marriage**
(above all [[cross-cousin-marriage]]) and **social structure** rather than race — resemblance
between systems following social organisation, not geography or physical type. His companion
*Kinship and Social Organisation* (1914) made the determination of terminology by social
structure explicit. See [[oceanic-relationship-systems]] and the [[genealogical-method]].

## Radcliffe-Brown 1950 structural restatement (ASKAM Introduction)

In the Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950), [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] restated the classificatory system in **structural-functional** terms—retaining Morgan’s insight that terms group genealogical positions, but rejecting evolutionary marriage-stage explanations ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]). Claims below are attributed to R-B’s 1950 Introduction (claims ranges 1–2).

1. **Terminology within the whole system.** Part of any kinship system is a system of terms; the first study step is discovering terms and usage, but terminology must be read relative to the whole system of social relationships. “Descriptive” scientific compounds (e.g. “mother’s brother”) are necessary for analysis; some African systems (e.g. Yakö material in the volume; Dahomey, Edo, Ibo cited) use descriptive forms in that sense. Where a single term covers multiple genealogical kinds (classificatory categories), R-B held that English “uncle” (FB, MB, aunt’s husband) corresponds to absence of regular social distinction among those positions in English law and etiquette—i.e. terminology tracks standardized social distinctions.
2. **Wide-range facilitation (Maine).** R-B endorsed Maine’s point that classificatory terminology’s chief effect is to bring a much greater number of kindred “within your mental grasp”: classificatory terminology is primarily a **mechanism facilitating wide-range kinship systems**, not a residue of primitive group marriage.
3. **Unity of the sibling group.** General classificatory equations (FB = F; MZ = M; parallel cousins = siblings) express the principle that siblings of the same sex form a unit for social reckoning. For MB and FZ, two principles compete: **generation** (place first-ascending female cognates as “mother”) vs **sibling-group unity** (FZ as same kind as father/FB; MB as same kind as mother/MZ). Many African systems show the latter: Kongo “mothers” / “fathers” for mother’s and father’s siblings (van Wing); Bantu *umalume* = “male mother” for MB; “female father” for FZ among Kitara, Ndau, Yao, Huana and others. R-B argued “female father” / “male mother” confuse Europeans only because English merges physical parentage with jural relation—the African terms connote social/legal relationship with sex qualifications.
4. **Unity of the lineage (Omaha type).** Special Omaha-type extensions (mother/MB terms applied through mother’s brother’s line over generations; African illustrations Nandi, Bari, Kitara; partial Masai; Shona–Ndau–Shangana-Tonga “grandfather” for MB and his male descendants) express **unity and solidarity of the patrilineal lineage**: ego treats mother’s lineage, over generations, as one category. R-B claimed a single interpretive method: general classificatory terminology = sibling-group unity; Omaha form = lineage-group unity (*JRAI* 1941 “The Study of Kinship Systems”).
5. **Masai illustration.** Limited classificatory terms confined to agnatic kindred (Hollis) emphasize agnatic vs other kin; relationships via females or marriage continue in the male line only; *sotwa* fringe kin may marry under restrictions—kin arranged into few large categories.
6. **Incest and classificatory extension.** R-B defined incest narrowly as sexual intimacy within the parental family; classificatory “mother/sister/daughter” produces symbolic incest feelings; he reversed the common theory that marriage bans follow sexual disgust—sex is often felt wrong *because* marriage is forbidden, marriage rules maintaining structural continuity.

**Relation to Morgan:** R-B keeps classification-as-structure; drops Malayan/Turanian/Ganowanian evolutionary ladder and marriage-form residues as primary explanation. African volume applications: Zulu “female fathers” / “male mothers” (Gluckman); Tswana *rrakgadi* / *malome* (Schapera); Lozi generation-merged cognatic classes (Gluckman). Study: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

## Firth's Tikopia refinement of Morgan (1936)

In *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]* (Ch. VII), [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]] gave the
classic functionalist correction of Morgan while explicitly crediting his insight. Of a worked Tikopia
case Firth wrote that it "illustrates well the principle of the classificatory system which Morgan
expressed so many years ago—the manner in which... the collateral line is continually merged with the
'lineal' line." His correction (the load-bearing quote): "What he did not recognize clearly enough was
that this merging is **primarily terminological**, that there is a **grading in behaviour on the basis
of propinquity in kinship**, and that there is always a **system of secondary terminology** to indicate
the differences." Firth's evidence is the observed gradient: formal *tapu*/respect applies equally to
"true" and classificatory fathers, but informal intimacy, appeal-priority, old-age support, and mourning
intensity all scale with genealogical closeness — so a classificatory system is internally "inconsistent,"
merging kin in terminology while grading them in conduct. This severs Morgan's terminological observation
from his evolutionary inference (terms as survivals of group marriage) more cleanly than R-B's structural
restatement, by grounding the point in graded *behaviour* rather than in structural principle alone.
Firth also engaged the ontogeny of terms — recording how a Tikopia child first over-applies "mother" and
"father" to wide kin before learning the distinctions (contra [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s
Trobriand time-lag claim). Source: [[firth-we-the-tikopia-1936]].

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 3, Conclusion.)* Classificatory terminologies as fossils of extinct marriage forms (Morgan) became the most sophisticated and durable component of the [[primitive-society]] model — accidental byproduct of Morgan’s philological program that outlasted other elements. Kroeber 1909 (Boasian) reassigned causes to linguistic categories not sociology. Alliance theorists revived “relationship terminologies” after Malinowskian/descent-theory neglect. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].
