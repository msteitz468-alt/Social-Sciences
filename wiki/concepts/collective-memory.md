# Collective Memory

## Definition as Coined

Maurice Halbwachs (L'Année Sociologique school; *Les cadres sociaux de la mémoire*, 1925; *La mémoire collective*, posthumous 1950) argued that individual remembering depends on social frameworks: what is recalled, and in what shape, is structured by the groups a person belongs to. Mary Douglas (*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How Institutions Think]]*, 1986) restates the thesis as: public memory is "the storage system for the social order" — institutions create "shadowed places in which nothing can be seen" alongside areas of finely discriminated detail.

## Semantic History

- **1925/1950** — Halbwachs's framework concept; Douglas notes Marx and Hegel anticipated the theme but explicitly declines the priority dispute.
- **1940** — **structural amnesia** enters British social anthropology with [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s *The Nuer* (fieldwork early 1930s): [[nuer|Nuer]] informants recalled 9–11 generations of ancestors, but the count stayed roughly constant over time as middle generations were dropped — remembering is selective and structurally patterned. Students of Evans-Pritchard (Bohannan 1952; Barnes 1954) compared which social structures could sustain genealogical depth.
- **1957–65** — [[merton-robert-k|Merton]]'s sociology of science (priority disputes, "Singletons and Multiples," *On the Shoulders of Giants*) treated scientists' forgetting of precursors and resistance to multiple-discovery claims as functional for institutionalized competition (an early Freudian "motivated denial" framing he later dropped).
- **1986** — Douglas argued Evans-Pritchard and Merton independently converged on one mechanism: the social system explains what gets remembered and forgotten — "which scientists and which ancestors get to be remembered is the same general question." Her worked case: Condorcet's 1785 voting paradox (and Borda 1781) forgotten for ~160 years until Arrow and Black independently rediscovered it in 1948 — forgotten not for mathematical inadequacy but because the political climate had no institutional use for it (Arrow 1984 memoir quoted; Douglas 1986).

## Operationalizations

Genealogical depth and shape over time (structural-amnesia comparisons); documented multiple-discovery and citation-forgetting records in science; Douglas (1986), generalizing Halbwachs and Merton, predicted memory styles by social form: "The competitive society celebrates its heroes, the hierarchy celebrates its patriarchs, and the sect its martyrs."

## Applications

Douglas's chapter-length case of *institutional forgetting*: Frederic Bartlett, trained under W. H. R. Rivers in the Cambridge circle, set out to build a sociological theory of memory, but experimental psychology's disciplinary institutions diverted him — his serial-reproduction method could not capture institutional influences, and Bartlett himself forgot his founding intention (Douglas 1986). Later partial rediscoveries she lists (Festinger 1948; Coleman, Katz & Menzel 1957; Jaspars & Fraser 1981; Campbell 1975) were, she argued, each forgotten again for lack of interlock with prevailing validation procedures.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept risks reifying a group mind — the standing Kuhn/Rawls objection to [[thought-collective]]-style constructs applies. Halbwachs's own texts are engaged here only through Douglas (1986); a direct ingest would let this page state his framework independently. Claims about Nuer genealogies carry their fieldwork dates (early 1930s) and Evans-Pritchard's attribution.

Jan and Aleida Assmann's [[cultural-memory]] extends Halbwachs's concept beyond living-group ("communicative") memory to the institutionally maintained deep past (texts, monuments, rites, specialist carriers) — see that page and [[assmann-jan]].
