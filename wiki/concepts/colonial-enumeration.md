# Colonial Enumeration

[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s analysis (ch. 6, "Number in the Colonial
Imagination," of *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]*) of
counting and classification as **productive** colonial techniques. Attributed to
Appadurai, building on named predecessors.

## Definition as Coined

Central thesis: "exoticization and enumeration were complicated strands of a single
colonial project, and… in their interaction lies a crucial part of the explanation
of group violence and communal terror in contemporary India" *(Appadurai)*.
Colonial numbers were not merely utilitarian but combined "informational,
justificatory, and pedagogical techniques," producing "the illusion of bureaucratic
control and a key to a colonial imaginary in which countable abstractions… created
the sense of a controllable indigenous reality."

## Semantic History

Positions itself as completing [[said-edward|Edward Said]]'s [[orientalism]]
(which showed orientalist discourse to be "absolutely anatomical and enumerative"
but "does not specify how" knowledge and domination connected) via David Ludden's
"orientalist empiricism" and Bernard Cohn's *locus classicus* on the census, which
"creates by its practical logic and form a new sense of category identity in
India." Draws Ian Hacking's **"dynamic nominalism"** (labeling creates new kinds of
person) and Foucault's [[biopower|biopolitics]].

## Operationalizations

- **Cadastral survey** (the 1847 Joint Report): Appadurai coins **"the prose of
  cadastral domination"** (after Ranajit Guha's "prose of counterinsurgency") and
  the maxim "land is to teach" (inverting "land is to rule").
- **The human census (1870–1931):** caste "unyoked" from local agrarian context and
  set adrift in "a vast pan-Indian social encyclopedia," which "stimulate[d] the
  self-mobilization of these groups into… larger translocal political forms."
- **"Enumerated communities"** (term from Sudipta Kaviraj): Hindu/Muslim identities
  "transformed not just into imagined communities but also into enumerated
  communities."

## Applications

Contrast with the Mughals (who surveyed land but "conducted no known census of
persons") frames colonial "political arithmetic" as a distinctive fusion of
orientalist classification and fiscal-democratic enumeration. Links to contemporary
communal violence (Mandal Commission, Ayodhya) whose intensity Appadurai ties to
"contact with the techniques of the modern nation-state, especially those having to
do with number." Feeds [[the-state]] and [[culturalism]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Appadurai himself qualifies the thesis: the "essentializing and enumerating project
was by no means completely effective," resisted by peasant revolt, vernacular
writing, and Gandhian counter-mobilization. Critics note the difficulty of proving
that enumeration *caused* (rather than reinforced) reified communal categories.
