# Anthropocene

The Anthropocene is the proposal that human impact on the Earth system has become so pervasive as to define a new geological interval succeeding (or ending) the [[holocene|Holocene]]. Although the term was popularized after this source, [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) anticipates its core argument: that across the Holocene the human role in environmental change grew from negligible to dominant, so that the "natural" and "cultural" components of change become inseparable in the recent past.

## Definition as Coined

The term was used informally by the ecologist Eugene Stoermer in the 1980s and brought to prominence by the atmospheric chemist Paul Crutzen (with Stoermer) in 2000, who proposed that the influence of humans on the global environment warranted recognizing a human-dominated geological epoch. It remains a proposal: in 2024 the International Union of Geological Sciences declined to ratify a formal Anthropocene epoch with a mid-20th-century base, leaving it an informal but widely used concept.

## Semantic History

Roberts (1998) frames the whole later Holocene as a progressive "taming of nature," arguing (attributed) that questions such as whether an episode of erosion, [[desertification]], or lake eutrophication is "natural or cultural" become the defining problems of recent environmental history — precisely the boundary the Anthropocene concept later crystallized. Debate over the concept's *start date* recapitulates this: Ruddiman's "early Anthropocene hypothesis" locates significant human climate forcing in early-Holocene agriculture, whereas stratigraphers favour a sharp mid-20th-century signal.

## Operationalizations

Candidate stratigraphic markers include the ¹⁴C and ²³⁹Pu "bomb spike" of ~1950, fly-ash and plastic horizons, novel mineral and isotopic signatures, and — for earlier proposed starts — pollen and charcoal evidence of [[deforestation]] and agriculture (see [[pollen-analysis]]).

## Applications

The concept reframes the deep-time record studied in [[environmental-archaeology|environmental archaeology]] as the prehistory of anthropogenic global change, and is the horizon toward which Roberts' concluding chapter, "The Environmental Future: A Holocene Perspective," points.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note the tension between a *geological* definition (requiring a globally synchronous, preservable marker) and an *historical/ecological* one (a diachronous process beginning at different times in different regions). Roberts' own framing sidesteps a single boundary, treating growing human dominance as a Holocene-long trend rather than a datable epoch — a position closer to the "early Anthropocene" than to the stratigraphers' 1950 marker.
