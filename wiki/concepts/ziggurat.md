# Ziggurat

The ziggurat (also ziqqurat, zikkurat) is a Mesopotamian stepped temple platform, the best-preserved example being Ur-Nammu's ziqqurat at [[ur]], per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]]. Ur-Nammu built ziqqurats at Ur, Uruk, Eridu, Nippur, and other cities as part of his broader Ur III temple-building program.

## Definition as Coined

The term derives from the Akkadian verb *zaqaru*, "to build high" — Roux notes this etymology is philologically uninformative as to the structure's actual function. As an emic Mesopotamian architectural and religious term, it denotes a tiered stage-tower, most commonly a component of a temple complex dedicated to a city's patron deity.

## Semantic History

The Ur ziqqurat is Roux's descriptive benchmark: a mud-brick core given a baked-brick "skin" set in bitumen, with a base measuring 60.50 by 43 meters; at least three tiers were built, though only the first and part of the second survive, standing at a present height of roughly 20 meters. The walls were given a deliberate slight curvature for a "lightness" effect — a device Roux notes was long thought a Greek invention (the Parthenon), in fact anticipated roughly 2,000 years earlier at Ur. Three flights of stairs on the northeast side converge at a landing between the first and second platforms, with further stairs leading to upper stories and the summit shrine. The structure sat within the walled "sacred city" precinct, overlooking the courtyard of the moon-god Nanna and his consort Ningal, the royal palace, and the Euphrates.

Ziggurats derive, per Roux, from earlier low temple platforms under Ubaid, Uruk, and Jemdat Nasr-period temples — **not** from tombs, unlike Egyptian pyramids, despite a superficial resemblance; Roux allows that possible influence on Egyptian architecture has been suggested, without asserting it as established.

## Operationalizations

The ziggurat form is operationalized architecturally as a stepped mudbrick platform with staged tiers, exterior staircases, and a summit shrine, distinguished from ordinary temple platforms by its monumental multi-tiered scale.

## Applications

Applied across major Sumerian and later Mesopotamian cities as the characteristic monumental religious structure of the region from the Ur III period onward (Ur-Nammu's building program at Ur, Uruk, Eridu, and Nippur).

## Critiques and Limitations

Roux surveys, and treats as live, competing positions on ziggurat function, none definitively settled:

1. An early, now-discredited theory held ziggurats served as astronomical observatories or priest refuges — Roux states this "obviously does not make sense."
2. Ziggurats derive from earlier low platforms under Ubaid/Uruk/Jemdat Nasr temples, not from tombs (contra a superficial Egyptian-pyramid comparison); a possible, unproven influence on Egyptian architecture is noted but not asserted.
3. A "highlander-origin" theory holds that Sumerians, as former mountain-dwellers, built artificial peaks reproducing a remembered highland landscape.
4. The platform is read by some as elevating the city-god above other gods, or as protecting the god's precinct from lay "promiscuity."
5. Roux's own preferred reading treats the ziggurat as a staircase or bridge between the lower, routine-cult temples and an upper sanctuary "half-way between heaven and earth," where gods and humans could meet — he invokes the biblical Tower of Babel passage (Genesis xi.4, "to reach unto heaven") as an apt characterization, and draws an analogy to "prayers of bricks" comparable to a Gothic cathedral's "prayers of stone." **This fifth position is Roux's own stated preference, not a settled wiki-voice consensus among Mesopotamian archaeologists.**
