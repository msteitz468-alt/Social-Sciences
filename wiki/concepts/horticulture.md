# Horticulture

## Definition as Coined

**Horticulture** denotes plant cultivation at garden or swidden scale — clearing, planting, weeding, ditching — often combined with hunting, gathering, or herding, and not necessarily producing fully domestic morphological crops or permanent agrarian villages. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] horticulture is the right scale term for New Guinea highland systems, early Mexican squash/maize/bean management, Jōmon “wild gardening,” and Nea Nikomedeia-style garden plots, distinct from industrial or plough agriculture.

## Semantic History

Anthropology’s forager–horticulturalist–agriculturalist typology separated hand-tool garden production from intensive field systems. Archaeology showed long **pre-domestication cultivation** and mixed economies (Natufian “special gardeners” via Hastorf in Mithen; Flannery risk-buffering cultivation at Guilá Naquitz). Mithen treats independent horticultural origins (New Guinea, Mesoamerica, China rice, SW Asia cereals) as parallel historical experiments.

## Operationalizations

- **Earthworks and clearance:** Kuk Swamp ditches and tree clearance (Golson via Mithen); burning for garden and browse.
- **Morphology of plants:** domestic-size squash by ~8000 BC cultivation at [[guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]] (Bruce Smith restudy via Mithen); teosinte→maize trajectory; taro/yam/tree crops in Melanesia.
- **Settlement:** garden plots beside rectangular houses (Nea Nikomedeia); sedentary villages still on wild foods (Initial Jōmon) vs cultivation before permanent settlement (Mexico, New Guinea — Mithen’s comparative table).
- **Social drivers:** Flannery risk-buffering vs Hayden feasting (Mithen prefers Flannery for early Oaxaca); pig-fencing as catalyst tipping gardens toward bounded horticulture in New Guinea (attributed scenario).
- **Ethnographic baselines:** Torres Strait gradient from hunter-gatherers to swidden horticulture of yams, sweet potato, taro, tree crops (Harris via Mithen).

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **New Guinea:** independent highland horticulture (Kuk); pigs as later catalysts; not a Fertile Crescent transplant.
- **Mesoamerica:** squash cultivation and later maize/beans at Guilá Naquitz / Oaxaca; long delay before village farming.
- **East Asia:** Jōmon forest management and “wild gardeners” at apogee ~3000 BC still short of full rice agriculture until ~500 BC.
- **Europe:** early farming villages with garden plots of wheat/barley, peas/lentils.
- **Amazon:** path toward manioc and maize after riverine specialized economies and pottery.
- **Comparative principle:** cultivation can precede sedentism (Mexico, New Guinea) or follow it (West Asia, Japan, Ganges) — sequence is not universal.
- **Achuar domestic horticulture (Descola 1986/1994):** *[[descola-in-the-society-of-nature-1994|In the Society of Nature]]* — manioc-centered polycultural gardens, quantified labor and yields, under-exploitation relative to potential, diet ~3408 kcal/day with gardening as main calorie source ([[descola-society-of-nature-study|study]]; [[achuar]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundary with **[[complex-hunter-gatherers]]** plant management is fuzzy (“wild gardening”).
- Boundary with full **agriculture** is equally fuzzy (labor intensity, dependence share, morphology).
- Ethnohistoric crops (sweet potato in New Guinea) may postdate the early systems under discussion — chronological hygiene required.
- Bailey-type claims that pure rainforest foragers are impossible without horticulture or trade are **positions**, not findings.
