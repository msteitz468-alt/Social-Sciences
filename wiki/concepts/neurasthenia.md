# Neurasthenia

"Weakness of the nerves and nervous exhaustion" — a diagnostic category that flourished in the
nineteenth-century West, was eliminated from American psychiatry, and persisted as a leading
diagnosis in China, where it became the pivot of [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s
cross-cultural work in *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (1988) and
*Social Origins of Distress and Disease* (1986).

## Definition as Coined

A "portmanteau term" gathering chronic fatigue, weakness, and assorted bodily and emotional
complaints under a presumed neurological cause and long treated as "a 'real physical disease.'"
Coined by New York neurologist **George Beard** shortly after the U.S. Civil War and originally
called "the American disease," attributed by Beard to the "pressures" of modern civilization. By
around 1900 it was, in Kleinman's phrase (citing Drinka 1984), "the medical cynosure of the age."

## Semantic History

- **Gilded Age West** (attributed via Drinka 1984, Sicherman 1977, Feinstein 1984): Weir Mitchell
  linked it to elite women's constrained social roles; Sicherman read it through "the overloaded
  electric circuit and the overdrawn bank account" — a belief in a limited nervous-energy supply
  strained by capitalist modernity. Famous diagnosed patients included William James, Henry James,
  Freud, and Darwin. Feinstein: neurasthenic invalidism "coalesced… into a durable social role,"
  offering sanctioned leisure, protection from responsibility, and expression of inadmissible
  feelings.
- **Decline in the West**: as distress was psychologized and the "helping professions" gained
  authority (Lasch 1979; Rieff's "triumph of the therapeutic"), the category faded. **DSM-III**
  (1980) eliminated neurasthenia from U.S. psychiatric nosology, redistributing it into depressive,
  anxiety, somatization, and psychophysiological categories. WHO's **ICD-9 retained it**, and it
  stayed in active use in Eastern Europe, Japan, India, and China.
- **China (shenjing shuairuo)**: at the time of Kleinman's fieldwork, neurasthenia was the most
  common psychiatric diagnosis and among the ten most frequent diagnoses in general medical
  clinics, while depression was rarely diagnosed. Kleinman notes a chronic behavioral problem
  answering to it was "depicted in traditional Chinese medical texts" before the Western term
  arrived.

## Operationalizations

Kleinman's Hunan Medical College study (1980, 1983, with Joan Kleinman) applied DSM-III criteria
to neurasthenia patients and found most could be rediagnosed as depression or anxiety — a
symptom-level convergence between the etic (DSM) and emic (shenjing shuairuo) categories. Yet the
categories carried very different social meanings and legitimating functions, and antidepressant
medication did not resolve the complaints: "neurasthenia as a chronic illness is extremely
sensitive to influences from the local life world of the patient and from the wider social
system." (All attributed to Kleinman's own single-researcher fieldwork.)

## Applications

Kleinman's explanation (attributed) for neurasthenia's persistence in China is threefold: it
legitimizes bodily distress without the family-wide [[stigma]] of a mental-illness label; it
avoids the politically dangerous connotations of "depression" (which Maoist framing could read as
"wrong political thinking"); and it assimilates readily to traditional Chinese medicine's concern
with *qi* and yin–yang balance. He pairs a Chinese case (Yen Guangzhen, whose Cultural Revolution
losses present as headache and exhaustion) with a U.S. case (Eliza Hartman, a post-mononucleosis
"whole-body tiredness" reading as dysthymic disorder): a shared stress-physiology substrate,
divergently elaborated toward social situation (China) versus inner psychological self (U.S.).

## As an "Archetype of Distress"

In *[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking Psychiatry]]* (1988) Kleinman treats Chinese
neurasthenia as one of several culturally specific **[[idioms-of-distress|archetypes of distress]]**
(alongside *fatigué* in France, chronic pain in North America, [[nervoso|nervios]] in Latin America)
onto which a shared nonspecific psychobiological state of demoralization is mapped. He notes Japan's
*shinkeishitsu* — described as a type of neurasthenia, associated with obsessions and phobias,
predominantly affecting men (unlike agoraphobia's female predominance in North America). And he frames
the Western disappearance of the label as a *professional relabeling*: an "exhaustion-weakness-dizziness"
cluster once called neurasthenia "is now diagnosed as depression or a personality problem," reflecting
Western psychiatry's intrapsychic emphasis — a relabeling far less likely in China given sociocentric
values and mental-illness [[stigma]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Neurasthenia is a **contested** construct and a textbook case of the difficulty of applying a
diagnostic category across cultures: does DSM-rediagnosing Chinese neurasthenics as "depressed"
recover a real underlying disorder or impose an alien nosology? Kleinman holds both that a
symptom-level convergence exists *and* that the emic category cannot be reduced to it — the
tension that his later concept of the "category fallacy" (coined in his 1977 work, not in *The
Illness Narratives*) names. The historical Western material rests largely on secondary sources
(Drinka 1984); the Chinese findings are Kleinman's own and remain single-researcher until
independently replicated.
