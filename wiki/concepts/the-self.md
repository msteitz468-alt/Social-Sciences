## Definition as Coined

In Chapter 19 of *Foundations of Social Theory*, Coleman argues that the unitary actor assumed elsewhere in his theory is, on closer analysis, a structure of at least two functional components: the **object self** (receptor — the locus of satisfaction, akin to Mead's "me" and the legal category of principal) and the **acting self** (actuator — the component that acts in service of the object self, akin to Mead's "I" and the legal category of agent), with **interests** as the linkage between them. Coleman states the assumption of unproblematic transmission between the two as a simplifying "first approximation" that licenses treating natural persons as unitary actors elsewhere in the book; a unitary actor is, in his words, "a degenerate case of the corporate actor," one for which the complexities of internal structure can be disregarded.

## Semantic History

Coleman explicitly situates his object-self/acting-self split against three predecessor schemes. Mead's (1934) "me" (the socially formed object of self-reflection) and "I" (the spontaneous, responding agent) supply the closest analogue. Adam Smith's *Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759) offers a triple division, adding an evaluating "judge." Coleman argues Freud's ego/id/superego does **not** map cleanly onto his split: all three Freudian components, he contends, are varieties of the object self, leaving no clear analogue for the acting self. Cooley's (1902) "looking-glass self" is invoked, in a broadened reading, as an early precedent for treating the self as an internal mirror of the external system of action and its actors.

## Operationalizations

For natural persons, Coleman treats the object-self/acting-self connection as normally unproblematic and thus analytically negligible. For corporate actors, by contrast, the split corresponds to physically distinct parties — principals/owners (object self) versus agents/officers (acting self) — and the connection between them is "highly problematic," constituting the domain of organization theory and the principal-agent problem. He reinterprets Berle and Means's (1933) ownership-control separation thesis in exactly these terms, while noting the thesis is contested by theorists emphasizing stock-market efficiency and hostile takeovers. English common law's historical treatment of infants, married women, serfs, monks, and nuns (per Pollock and Maitland, 1898) as having object selves (recognized interests) but no independent legal acting-self capacity — requiring an external guardian, husband, master, or the Church — is offered as a further historical illustration of the split.

Coleman further theorizes **identification** as expansion of the object self to encompass other persons or causes as if their outcomes were one's own, distinguishing five generative circumstances: acting to benefit another (parent-child, servant-master); the other's success (fan-club identification); shared experience of consequential events (soldiers who "have gone through hell together"); dependency on another (children on parents; hostages on captors, citing Bettelheim 1953 and Lifton 1961); and vesting control or rights in another (charismatic leaders, romantic love). He models a "picoeconomics" (borrowing Ainslie's 1986 term) of the self as an internal system of quasi-actors — internalized others or appetites — holding partial, constitutionally allocated rights of control over the person's actions, shaped by socialization history.

## Applications

Coleman critically reconstructs Heider's (1958) **balance theory**, arguing Heider fails to specify what changes, or on what basis, when an actor's attachments to two conflicting social objects create tension. Reanalyzing Heider's Bob/Jim/poems experiment (Esch 1950, n=101) through his own rational, interest-based diagrammatic model, Coleman links disharmony to the need for a single joint action rather than separable ones, and extends the analysis to ancillary relationships that create continuing tension. He also applies the framework to corporate actors' self-change: Ford Motor Company's mid-1920s internal power shift from manufacturing to product engineers and stylists is read as the corporation changing its own operative interests via its internal constitution, realigning Ford with a shifting market and averting decline relative to General Motors — though Coleman leaves open whether pre- or post-change interests were more "objectively" Ford's own, given its multiple principals.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman records, without adjudicating, the **objective-versus-subjective-interests dispute**. The Marxist position (represented by Balbus 1971) holds that a person's objective interests are given by their structural position and may diverge from what they subjectively believe they want — grounding claims of false consciousness and justifying paternalist intervention. The liberal/neoclassical position holds that only subjectively expressed preferences are knowable or legitimate grounds for action, rejecting any externally imposed "true" interest as illegitimate. Coleman proposes a reconciling move via Arthur Bentley's (1908/1953) activity→position→interest→interest-group chain: objective interests are real, generated by a person's position in a social system, but only inferable indirectly — through longitudinal satisfaction outcomes or documented systematic distortions in the object-self/acting-self linkage — never directly observable as subjective interests are. He treats the dual linkage between the two selves as inherently imperfect, capable of producing action "against one's own interest," and cites Michels's iron-law-of-oligarchy divergence between officers and members, and Berle-Means divergence between owners and managers, as corporate-actor-level instances of the same structural gap. Coleman also treats documented "deviations from rationality" — framing effects (Tversky and Kahneman 1981), simultaneous gambling and insurance-buying, preference reversal near deadlines (Ainslie and Herrnstein 1981) — as evidence that the internal organization of the self is more complex than the unitary rational actor assumed elsewhere in his theory, though he argues these deviations leave most of the book's predictions intact.
