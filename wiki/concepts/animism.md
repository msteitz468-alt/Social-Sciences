# Animism

## Definition as Coined

[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] adopted "Animism" (from Latin *anima*, soul/breath) as the name for his theory of religion in *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, Vol. I ch. XI – Vol. II). He proposed it as the **"minimum definition of Religion"**: "the belief in Spiritual Beings." Animism has, in his scheme, two great doctrines: (1) the doctrine of **souls** of individual creatures, capable of continued existence after death or the destruction of the body; and (2) the doctrine of other **spirits**, up to the rank of powerful deities. (All claims in this section are Tylor's, in attributed voice — an intellectualist theory of religion's origin, not a wiki-voice account of what religion is.)

## Semantic History

Tylor's animism was a **developmental theory of the origin of religion**: the "primitive philosopher," reasoning from two puzzles — the difference between a living body and a dead one, and the human shapes seen in dreams and visions — inferred that "every man has two things belonging to him, namely, a life and a phantom," and combined them into the **ghost-soul**:

> "It is a thin unsubstantial human image, in its nature a sort of vapour, film, or shadow; the cause of life and thought in the individual it animates... capable of leaving the body far behind... continuing to exist and appear to men after the death of that body; able to enter into, possess, and act in the bodies of other men, of animals, and even of things." (Tylor 1871, Vol. I ch. XI)

This soul-concept was then extended to animals, plants, and objects, and elaborated upward into nature-spirits, ancestral ghosts, polytheistic gods, and finally monotheism — a single ascending chain. Tylor insisted the doctrine's cross-cultural uniformity is not evidence of borrowing but of independent rational inference — the beliefs "answering in the most forcible way to the plain evidence of men's senses, as interpreted by a fairly consistent and rational primitive philosophy" ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]). He held the deepest schism in world religion to be "that which divides Animism from Materialism." The term later migrated: (a) in the history of religions, "animism" became a (contested) label for a *type* or *stage* of religion attributed to small-scale societies; (b) in developmental psychology, Piaget borrowed "animism" for the child's attribution of life/intention to objects; (c) recent anthropology (the "new animism" — Bird-David, Descola, Viveiros de Castro's perspectivism) has revived and inverted the term to describe relational ontologies, explicitly rejecting Tylor's framing of it as mistaken primitive science.

## Operationalizations

As Tylor's "minimum definition," animism operationalizes *religion* itself as the **presence of belief in spiritual beings** — a deliberately minimal criterion chosen to include all peoples (against claims that "savages" had no religion) and to make religion a cross-culturally comparable variable for the [[comparative-method]]. This intellectualist operationalization competes with Durkheim's (religion as the sacred/profane distinction and collective effervescence; see [[collective-effervescence]]) and functionalist definitions (religion by social role).

## Applications

Animism organizes the entire second half of *Primitive Culture*: the doctrine of souls, the future life, transmigration, spirits as causes of disease and possession, nature-worship, [[magic]], fetishism, polytheism, the "great gods," and rites (sacrifice, prayer, fasting). Frazer's *The Golden Bough* extends the intellectualist-evolutionist program. The concept remains a fixture of the anthropology of religion, mostly as a foil.

## Critiques and Limitations

Tylor's animism was criticized as **intellectualist** (it makes religion a mistaken theory produced by lone reasoners — Evans-Pritchard's "If I were a horse" objection: it imputes Victorian rationalist reasoning to non-Western peoples) and **individualist** (Durkheim: religion is social in origin, not a private inference). Its evolutionary sequence (animism → polytheism → monotheism) was rejected with the rest of unilinear [[social-evolutionism|evolutionism]]. The "new animism" retains the word while discarding the deficit framing. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

## Codrington's Melanesian dissent and Marett's critique (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995)

(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) The missionary-ethnographer R. H. Codrington disputed Tylor's animism thesis as applied to Melanesia: he reported finding no Melanesian belief in "a spirit which animates any natural object" in the manner Tylor's theory required, and no failure of Melanesians to distinguish animate from inanimate. Stocking treats Codrington's *[[mana]]* as differing fundamentally from Tylorian animism — religion as awe before extraordinary power rather than a proto-scientific "explanation" of nature — and reads Codrington's underlying orientation as continuous with the pre-evolutionary "ethnological" (Prichardian/philological) paradigm associated with Max Müller, who himself used Codrington's *mana* concept in his Hibbert Lectures to argue against Tylorian evolutionary interpretations of religion's origin. [[marett-r-r|Marett]] drew directly on Codrington's *mana* data in formulating [[animatism]] against Tylor's animism (see that page).

## Coinage and rival: Marett's pre-animism (Stocking 1987)

Stocking ([[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|*Victorian Anthropology*]], 1987) reports (finding) that Tylor chose the term "animism" specifically because the more directly descriptive term "spiritualism" had already been claimed by the contemporary modern spiritualist sect (séances, mediums); Tylor personally investigated spiritualist phenomena in 1872 and concluded most of it was fraud, though he privately entertained a "prima facie case" for some unexplained force while publicly using the investigation only to document fraudulence. R. R. Marett, drawing on missionary-ethnographer R. H. Codrington's Melanesian data on *mana*, proposed in 1900 the alternative notion of "pre-animism" — locating the essence of religion in an **emotional** response at the point where thought "breaks down," rather than in Tylor's rationalist inferential chain (attributed; a rival, not a refinement, of the intellectualist program).

## Descola: Achuar ethnographic base (1986/1994) and animism restored (2005)

**Empirical base first.** In *[[descola-in-the-society-of-nature-1994|In the Society of Nature]]* ([[descola-society-of-nature-study|study]]; Fr. *La Nature domestique* 1986), Descola’s [[achuar]] ethnography documents a continuum of sociality among beings (*aents*, *wakan*), gardening as consanguineal relation to plant “children” under Nunkui, hunting as affinal negotiation with game mothers via [[anent]], and a Conclusion that treats nature as domestic/sociable rather than as a nature/culture dualism. That monograph does **not** yet name the four-ontology matrix; it is the fieldwork and monographic construction later re-described as animism.

**Comparative formalization.** In *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, [[descola-philippe|Descola]] **restores** animism as a [[modes-of-identification|mode of identification]]: humans attribute to nonhumans an **interiority identical to their own**, while discontinuity runs through **physicality** (form, ethogram, "outward apparel" of feathers, fur, scales, bark — more than substance). Shared interiority justifies extending "culture" to nonhumans (intersubjectivity, techniques, ritualized conduct). This is explicitly **not** Tylor's intellectualist stage-theory of mistaken soul-inference; it is a combinatorial cell of the [[four-ontologies|four ontologies]], the structural reverse of [[naturalism-ontology|naturalism]].

Descola distinguishes **standard animism** (nonhumans see themselves as humans) from [[perspectivism]] (optional additional clause: nonhumans see humans as nonhumans — crossed inversion of viewpoints), arguing the latter is not attested in all animist systems. Metamorphosis is re-read as **anamorphosis**: coinciding with the other's perspective. Exemplary cases in his synthesis include [[achuar]]/Jivaroan Amazonia, Makuna, Chewong, Cree/Ojibwa, and Siberian hunters. See Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]]; Studies Hub [[hubs/studies/descola-society-of-nature-study]].

## Ingold: animism, totemism, depiction (2000)

In *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* Chs. 6–7, [[ingold-tim|Ingold]] draws on Hallowell's Ojibwa ethnography (other-than-human persons; relational animacy — some stones alive) and contrasts **animic** and **totemic** modes of relating to animals and land (form generated in life vs form already in land; dialogical vs essential). He criticizes comparing animism to naturalism solely on naturalism's terms (Ch. 6). Depiction of animals is treated as non-representational revelation within dwelling, not encoding of a worldview onto a surface (Ch. 7). This is "new animism" lineage adjacent to Bird-David and Descola, routed through the [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]] rather than four-ontology combinatorics.

## Mithen / Boyer — religion as domain-mixing (1996)

*(Attributed — Mithen synthesizing Pascal Boyer and Tylor in *[[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996|The Prehistory of the Mind]]* Ch.9.)* Supernatural beings violate intuitive biology/physics while remaining intentional agents — concepts Early Humans with isolated [[specialized-intelligences]] could not form. First religious ideologies at Middle/Upper Palaeolithic transition as product of [[cognitive-fluidity]]; anthropomorphism and totemism as social × natural-history integration. Complements Tylor’s classic [[animism]] definition without equating UP ideology with ethnographic animism in wiki voice.

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Chs. 3, 5; thin but explicit mentions — not a monographic treatment of animism.)*

- **Iconography method (Ch. 3).** Barker argues archaeologists must not split prehistoric imagery into familiar “everyday life” vs unfamiliar “symbolic/ritual”: ethnographic art among Australian Aborigines and Kalahari San is largely produced in ritual/trance contexts. Saharan rock art mixes wild animals and herded stock during foraging→pastoralism; European Neolithic monuments especially developed where indigenous foragers became farmers (attributed). This is a methodological caution about reading non-Western nature/culture categories, adjacent to — not identical with — ethnographic [[animism]] as a relational ontology.
- **Vindhyan rock art (Ch. 5).** Barker reports Vindhyan rock art (Bhimbetka) imagery of hunting, plant/honey collection, and **animistic** imagery among early Holocene Indian foragers (Mathpal via Barker) — a source-language label for the imagery, not an ethnographic diagnosis that these foragers practiced Tylorean or Descolian animism as a complete system (attributed; keep thin).
- **Do not promote.** Barker’s uses are historiographic/methodological pointers within agricultural-origins synthesis, not a theory of animism. Cross-links: [[hunter-gatherer]], [[natufian-culture]], [[barker-graeme]].

## Evans-Pritchard's rejection of Tylorian animism for the Nuer (1956)

*(Attributed — [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]], *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]*, 1956; fieldwork among the Nuer of the southern Sudan, 1930–36.)*

Evans-Pritchard stated flatly: "the so-called animistic theory of religion, chiefly associated with the name of Tylor, cannot be sustained for the Nuer; since they are not animists and there is no evidence that they ever have been." His reasoning was structural, not merely empirical: Tylorian animism supposes the conception of Spirit *derives from* the conception of soul, but for the Nuer, [[kwoth|*kwoth*]] (Spirit) and *tie* (soul) are of "quite a different nature" — soul is "part of man and a created thing," while Spirit is "extrinsic to man, operating on him from outside him, and invasive." As evidence that soul and Spirit are ontologically distinct categories rather than the latter derived from the former, he pointed to the Nuer belief that the soul of a person killed by lightning is believed to be "changed into Spirit" (the *colwic* — see [[kwoth]], [[spiritual-refraction]]) — a conversion between categories, not a graded extension of one into the other.

Evans-Pritchard drew a deliberate terminological consequence from this distinction, framed as his own methodological prescription rather than a Nuer emic rule: "we should speak of the 'soul'... of man and of the 'ghosts'... and not of the 'spirits' of the departed" — reserving "Spirit" (and its cognate *kwoth*) exclusively for God and his refractions, and using "soul"/"ghost" (*tie*/*joagh*) for the human dead, precisely to avoid conflating the two conceptual categories that Tylor's derivational scheme runs together. This break sits alongside his broader rejection, stated in the book's Preface, of the inherited comparative-religion vocabulary — animism, fetishism, totemism, mana, tabu, shamanism — as a set of terms whose "meanings are still obscure," symptomatic of "the failure to build up an adequate and agreed-upon terminology in Comparative Religion." His stated aim was to treat Nuer religion "as a subject of study *sui generis*," not as an instance to be sorted into pre-existing evolutionary categories such as Tylor's.
