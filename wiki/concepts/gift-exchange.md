# Gift Exchange

## Definition as Coined

**Gift exchange** names institutionalized circulation of goods, services, and renown-objects through cycles of giving, receiving, and repaying under obligation. The anthropological charter is [[mauss-marcel|Marcel Mauss]]'s *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (1925; Cunnison English primary-ingested): gifts are "**in theory voluntary but in fact… given and repaid under obligation**"; the form is generous offer while "accompanying behaviour is formal pretence and social deception." Mauss's [[total-prestation]] carries **three obligations**: to give, to receive, and to repay. Type-cases include Malinowski's [[kula|Kula]] (*[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]*, 1922; fieldwork 1915–18) and Northwest Coast [[potlatch]].

## Operationalizations

Documented ethnographically through the described institutions: the Kula's timed counter-gifts; the potlatch's competitive distribution and destruction of valuables; the etiquette of "throwing" a valuable abruptly and receiving it with disdain (which Blau reads as institutionalized role distance, after [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]). Mauss operationalizes comparatively: simultaneous legal/religious/economic functions; group (not only individual) contracting; honour and credit stakes; renown-valuables vs coined money.

## Applications (Mauss — primary)

From *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (attributed):

- **Research question:** what force in the thing given compels return? Answer path: Maori [[hau]] of *taonga* — the gift remains part of the giver; "to give something is to give a part of oneself."
- **Morphology:** [[total-prestation]] (simple) vs [[potlatch]] (agonistic); gift regime as phase between clan total prestation and pure individual contract/money market — while markets themselves are treated as universal human phenomena in archaic as well as modern form.
- **Distribution:** Andaman moral exchange; Melanesian [[kula]] as extreme gift exchange vs [[gimwali]]; Northwest Coast potlatch as "monster child" of the gift system (war of wealth, credit, destruction, renown-money).
- **Survivals:** Roman *nexum*, Hindu *danadharma*, Germanic *wadium*/Gift as legal-historical traces of person-in-thing obligation (reconstructive — contested).
- **Normative program (POSITION):** mutuality, professional groups, social insurance; peace as precondition of trade ("lay down his spear"); Round Table allegory of redistributed common riches.

See [[mauss-the-gift-study]] and concept pages [[total-prestation]], [[hau]], [[potlatch]], [[total-social-phenomenon]].

## Applications (Malinowski — primary)

In *Argonauts* Ch. VI Malinowski ordered Trobriand transactions by increasing strictness of equivalence: pure gifts; customary irregular repayments (e.g. [[urigubu]]); payments for services; strictly equivalent returns; exchange of goods for titles/magic/dances; ceremonial barter with deferred payment ([[kula]], wasi); and [[gimwali]] ("trade pure and simple") with haggling. He refused a sharp trade-vs-gift line while recording that natives scorn improper Kula as "done like a gimwali." Ch. XXII: Kula exchange is not a bargain; equivalence follows the repayer's sense of custom and dignity; vaygu'a are not money. See [[malinowski-argonauts-study]].

In *[[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Coral Gardens]]* Vol. II (attributed) Malinowski densified the **garden** side of the continuum: no abstract "ownership" word; four possessive classes; *toli-* mastership vs *to-kabi* wieldership; distribution lexicon richer than production abstracta; [[urigubu]] and companion harvest terms (*takola*, *karibudaboda*, *taytupeta*…); *kayasa* as contractual ceremonial enterprise natives link to magic; *gala kam* poverty insult; basketful of *taytu* as vegetable value unit. See [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]].

## Applications (Strathern — Melanesia, primary)

In *[[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988; attributed), Strathern re-reads Melanesian exchange against Western commodity root metaphors: persons and things take the social form of **persons** in a gift economy; [[mediated-unmediated-exchange|mediated]] (partible objects) and **unmediated** (direct effects) modes structure sociality; gendering attaches to transactions, not object essences. Domination appears in particular excess acts, not only as structural appropriation of surplus. Study: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]. Concepts: [[dividual]], [[gender-of-the-gift]].

## Applications (Blau's use)

Blau enlists gift exchange as the comparative evidence that his [[social-exchange]] principles are not culture-bound. He extracts two general functions institutionalized giving serves — **establishing peer bonds** (Kula) and **establishing superordination** (potlatch) — and a single underlying rule: bestowing benefactions is a claim to superiority, "invalidated by reciprocation" but "validated by failure" to reciprocate. Hence unilateral generosity is a strategy of [[differentiation-of-power|power]]: "to crush [a rival] if possible under future obligations which it is hoped he cannot meet." **Tributes to chiefs** are the instructive exception — the chief's superiority survives receiving gifts because tribute repays a standing debt for his leadership. Blau parallels the potlatch with [[conspicuous-waste|Veblen's conspicuous waste]]: destroying wealth signals resources so abundant one need not husband them, meriting deference. Homans's epigram, endorsed by Blau: "Where we have institutionalized the market, they have institutionalized the gift."

## Applications (Zelizer — gifted money)

[[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]] (*[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]*, 1994) treats **gifts of money** as a distinct class of [[multiple-monies|multiple monies]]: tips, Christmas bonuses, remittances, courtship transfers, and ritual cash gifts are earmarked by relation type and etiquette, not merely payments for services. Transfer form (including intermediate sexual economies) marks whether a relation is read as gift, payment, or something else. Study: [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]. Concepts: [[earmarking]], [[relational-work]].

## Applications (Hochschild)

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] (*[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]* Ch 5) treats the **gift exchange of feeling** — paying respects with feeling under [[feeling-rules|feeling rules]] — as the third element of the private emotional system (with [[emotion-work]] and feeling rules). Straight vs improvisational exchanges of emotional "bows," nonpayment and guilt, and status-unequal feeling gifts prepare the Part Two argument: when that gift becomes a commodity sold as [[emotional-labor|emotional labor]], contribution thins and estrangement risk rises (attributed). This is a microsociology of emotional prestation, not a restatement of Mauss; it links to but is not identical with Blau's use of the anthropological gift literature.

## Applications (Bourdieu — gift, time, misrecognition)

*(Attributed — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]. The analysis **originates in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]* (1972), ch. 1**, and is reworked in *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]*, Book I Ch. 6 "The Work of Time," with Kabyle illustrations. Distinct from Blau's exchange-theoretic reading of Mauss and from Hochschild's emotional gift.)* In *Outline*, the argument opens as a critique of Lévi-Strauss's structuralist reading of Mauss — Bourdieu faults him for "making a complete break with native experience … positing that it is the exchange as a constructed object which 'constitutes the primary phenomenon'." Against this, "**the temporal structure of gift exchange, which objectivism ignores, is what makes possible the coexistence of two opposing truths, which defines the full truth of the gift**," and "**to abolish the interval is also to abolish strategy**."

### Irreversible sequence, not spectator's cycle

Bourdieu argues that gift exchange and the joust of honour are, **for agents**, irreversible oriented sequences of relatively unpredictable acts. "'Cycles of reciprocity', mechanical interlockings of obligatory practices, exist only for the absolute gaze of the omniscient, omnipresent spectator… In reality, the gift may remain unreciprocated… it may be rejected as an insult." He positions this as critique of Lévi-Strauss's reduction of exchange to automatic laws of the cycle of reciprocity, against Mauss's more phenomenological account.

### Deferred and different counter-gift

He holds that, in every society, if it is not to constitute an insult, the counter-gift must be **deferred and different**; immediate return of an exactly identical object amounts to a refusal. Gift exchange is thus opposed both to swapping (which telescopes gift and counter-gift into the same instant) and to lending (where legal contract already guarantees calculable return).

### Interval as instrument of [[misrecognition]]

The functioning of gift exchange, on this account, presupposes individual and collective misrecognition of the objective "mechanism" of exchange — a truth an immediate response brutally exposes. The interval between gift and counter-gift allows a relation always liable to appear as irreversible (forced, self-interested) to be seen as reversible. "To abolish the interval is to abolish strategy." Even if reciprocity is the "objective" truth of discrete gift acts, it is not the whole truth of a practice that could not exist if its subjective truth coincided perfectly with its objective truth.

Gift exchange is the paradigm of operations through which "symbolic alchemy produces the reality-denying reality that the collective consciousness aims at as a collectively produced, sustained and maintained misrecognition of the 'objective' truth." Players must refuse to acknowledge the objective truth of the game that objectivist analysis brings to light, and must contribute efforts, marks of care, and time to the production of collective misrecognition.

### Tempo as strategic resource

Agents' strategies, he argues, play on the tempo of action and the interval between actions: delaying revenge or the return of a gift keeps the partner-opponent in the dark; putting off response can exact deferential conduct. The interposed period must be neither too short (gift exchange) nor too long (especially revenge-murders). The temporal structure of practice functions as a screen preventing totalization; excellence is necessary improvisation, not a catalogue of recurrent situations.

### Habitus, not axiomatics or rational calculation

A generative model reducing exchange to successive choices can account for gifts, words, or challenges, but on paper it reproduces the functioning of [[habitus]]: series of irreversible choices under pressure, often with heavy stakes — not a file of prefabricated representations. "The motor of the whole dialectic of challenge and riposte, gift and counter-gift, is not an abstract axiomatics but the sense of honour, a disposition inculcated by all early education…" Teleological rational-actor models of gift sequences (A gives in order to produce B's response in order to step up the gift) are, he claims, as naive as mechanistic programmed sequences.

Objectivist truth of the gift (the cycle model) sets aside the collective official definition of subjective experience and ignores that agents practise as irreversible a sequence the observer constitutes as reversible. Correct use of models requires a theory of practice as practical participation in a game ([[illusio]]) and of theoretical separation.

### Kabyle registers (documentation moment)

Among the Kabyles as Bourdieu reported them: ordinary "little presents" that bind friendship were of modest value, frequent, continuous, functioning within the logic of surprise rather than ritual mechanism (often a dish of cooked food), opposed to extraordinary gifts (*elkhir*, *elahdya*, *lehna*) at major festivals and to obligatory gifts to holy men (*lwdada*). Daily participation in gift exchanges (small boys as messengers between women's and men's worlds) was among the structural exercises transmitting practical mastery. Agrarian and sacrifice rites also used gift-exchange / *do ut des* logic (life for a life; denied murder of ox or field). Ordinary marriages stood to extra-ordinary marriages as everyday exchanges to ceremonial exchanges involving official kin. Parallel-cousin vs distant marriage was lexically aligned with direct peasant exchange vs market transactions.

### Economy of practices

More generally, he treats the theory of strictly economic practices as a particular case of a general theory of the economy of practices: practices never cease to comply with an economic logic even when they appear disinterested; [[symbolic-capital]] is denied capital, recognized as legitimate — i.e. misrecognized as capital. The labour required to conceal the function of exchanges is as important as the labour needed to perform that function.

## Applications (Sahlins — "The Spirit of the Gift")

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 4, 1972.)* Sahlins re-reads [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]'s *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* on two fronts. First, the **[[hau]]**: against Mauss's mystical "spirit of the gift" (and against [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s "native rationalization" and Firth's secular-prestige readings), Sahlins argues the Maori *hau* of a taonga is its **"yield" or product** — the return generated by putting a gift to use, which by right flows back to the original giver ("one man's gift should not be another man's capital"). He grounds this in the three-party structure of the Ranapiri text and its parallel in the *whangai hau* forest-fertility rite, concluding *hau* is a **"total" category** (undifferentiated economic/religious) — so Mauss was wrong on the spiritual specifics but right that the gift is total.

Second, the **gift as social contract**: "The primitive analogue of social contract is not the State, but the gift." Where Hobbes's contract fuses individuals into a sovereign that monopolizes force, the gift organizes society only **segmentarily** — it correlates the opposition of armed groups and perpetuates it as alliance rather than dissolving it. Politically Sahlins reads Mauss as "akin to Hobbes" (peace through a reason overcoming a war of all groups), sentimentally as heir to Rousseau. See [[reciprocity]], [[hau]].

## Appadurai on gift vs commodity (1986)

In *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] argues anthropology has **exaggerated and reified** the gift/commodity contrast (romanticizing small-scale societies; underplaying calculation outside capitalism; oversimplifying Marx vs Mauss). Drawing on [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]’s temporal analysis of the gift, he treats gift exchange as sharing a calculative spirit with commodity circulation even when sociality intensity differs; kula shows private-treaty calculation and “indebtedness engineering” without money, seeking reputation rather than industrial profit *(Appadurai)*. Geary’s medieval relics further blur gift, theft, and commerce as modes of circulating sacred things *(Geary in SLOT)*. This is a **position** reframing Mauss–Marx dualism, not a wiki-voice dissolution of the gift concept.

## Tsing on matsutake gift–commodity toggle (2015)

In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]*, [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] argues matsutake is a capitalist commodity that **begins and ends its life as a gift**, spending only hours as fully alienated inventory (shipping crates / aircraft) — hours that structure exporter–importer accumulation ([[salvage-accumulation]]). Oregon pickers’ mushrooms are first non-alienated freedom trophies; Japanese markets re-embed them as seasonal gifts, perks, and bribes after wholesale re-sorting *(Tsing 2015)*. Complements Appadurai’s gift/commodity blur without dissolving either pole; see also [[commoditization]], [[alienation]], [[open-ticket-oregon]].

## Sahlins 2013 — gift in the kinship–magic regime

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-what-kinship-is-2013|What Kinship Is—And Is Not]]* 2013, via [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]].)* Gift exchange, kinship, and magic are modalities of one animistic regime of intersubjective participation: parties reciprocally appropriate things inalienably associated with the other, creating **fellowship** = hallmark of [[mutuality-of-being|kinship]]; intentional deployment of a thing-person for beneficial effects has the quality of a magical act. Failed exchange ruptures fellowship with effects analogous to sorcery — Johansen on Maori unrequited gift as *mate* (weakening) or *kaihau* of the *[[hau]]* (draining the giver's life). Politics of gift hierarchy deferred by Sahlins but flagged (gifts as poison/slavery/domination). Links: [[mutuality-of-being]] · [[hau]] · [[kinship-biology-vs-culture-debate]].

## Applications (body tissues, organs, semen — *Commodifying Bodies*, 2001/2002)

*(Attributed — chapters in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* / *Body & Society* 7.2–3. These applications treat gift ideology as rhetoric that can mask or reframe market relations in [[body-commodification]]; they do not dissolve Mauss's analytic.)*

- **Lock — "gift of life" and organ/tissue alienation.** [[lock-margaret|Margaret Lock]] ("The Alienation of Body Tissue…") re-reads Mauss via Taussig: non-capitalist gift bonds center reciprocity and a modicum of the giver's "essence," which modern markets deny in favor of Marxian [[commodity-fetishism]]. She argues transplant promotion deliberately mobilizes organ fetishism: coordinators bandy the **"gift of life"** metaphor, and donor families speak of the dead "living on," while recipients who animate organs with donor qualities are reprimanded as pathological — a contradictory suppression of Maussian person-in-thing once the organ is stitched in. Body tissues, she holds, are more often treated as inalienable except in carefully circumscribed activity; free exchange requires first reconceptualizing parts as thing-like and non-self. See also [[human-organs-trade]].
- **Tober — semen as gift and goods; Titmuss parallel; market in altruism.** Diane M. Tober ("Semen as Gift, Semen as Goods: Reproductive Workers and the Market in Altruism") argues semen giving/buying/selling **conflates** gift and commodity: semen is purchased, marketed, and screened yet cannot escape social relations of "fatherhood" (fantasy of the donor; possible later contact; recipients experiencing paid semen as a precious gift). She maps Titmuss's *The Gift Relationship* (blood: commercial = bad / voluntary = good) onto sperm banking, but holds that semen donation always involves some self-interest even when free and anonymous. Across repositories, she finds **altruism itself** treated as a quality of person (and thus of semen) with economic value — a **market in altruism** in which non- or low-pay banks may also increase profit margins on resale. Recipients and banks often reframe collection/sale as gift despite prices (attributed, Tober).
- **Scheper-Hughes — piercing gift/altruism rhetoric.** In her Organs Watch program statement ("Commodity Fetishism in Organs Trafficking"), [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] seeks to pierce secrecy around harvesting and distribution protected by transplant's moral aura, and to forge a language free of "scarcities, gifts, altruism and life-saving." She closes by wanting assurances that the so-called "gift of life" never deteriorates into a "theft of life." She also notes "compensated gifting" within families alongside "coerced gifting" by domestic workers and prisoners (attributed). Method/politics: [[militant-anthropology]], [[multi-sited-ethnography]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Mauss primary (now ingested):** hau mechanism rests heavily on one Maori text; Indo-European potlatch "traces" and phase model carry evolutionary and reconstructive risk; armchair synthesis from colonial ethnography — see [[mauss-the-gift-study]] Critiques. Do not promote universal hau or unilinear gift→market sequence to wiki voice.
- The equation of Kula/potlatch dynamics with modern exchange is Blau's analogical claim, flagged as such; Mauss frames the gift as [[total-social-phenomenon]], a tension Blau underplays when reducing to instrumental exchange principles.
- Blau's ethnographic content was at two removes before Malinowski/Mauss primary ingests; re-verify Blau's quotations against [[malinowski-argonauts-1922]] and [[mauss-the-gift-1925]] when adjudicating.
- Hochschild's gift-of-feeling layer is illustrated from student and occupational materials, not from classic gift ethnography.
- Bourdieu's gift analysis is theoretical reconstruction from Kabyle (and comparative) materials in *Outline*/*Logic*; it is not a primary re-study of Mauss's Melanesian/American cases. Who authorizes the sociologist's "objective" truth against agents' "subjective" truth is a standing charge against the misrecognition apparatus (see [[misrecognition]] Critiques).
- Economism charges cut both ways: Bourdieu attacks economistic ethnocentrism while generalizing an "economy of practices" — a tension he addresses by insisting on double truth (objective mechanism *and* necessary misrecognition), but critics may still read a residual reduction.
