# Anti-politics machine

**Anti-politics machine** is [[ferguson-james|James Ferguson]]'s name (1990, attributed) for what the **"development" apparatus** does in practice: it **depoliticizes** everything it touches — whisking political realities (inequality, labor migration, state power) out of sight by casting them as technical problems — while almost unnoticed performing its own political operation of **expanding bureaucratic state power**.

## Definition as Coined

In the Preface to *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]*, Ferguson states the short answer to what the development apparatus in Lesotho does: it is an "anti-politics machine," depoliticizing everything it touches, everywhere whisking political realities out of sight, while expanding bureaucratic state power.

Chapter 9 crystallizes the mechanism via [[instrument-effects]]: development projects "fail" to eliminate poverty / transform agriculture as advertised, yet (1) expand and entrench bureaucratic state power and (2) depoliticize poverty and the state by reducing them to technical problems. The apparatus is "not a machine for eliminating poverty that is incidentally involved with the state bureaucracy; it is a machine for reinforcing and expanding the exercise of bureaucratic state power, which incidentally takes 'poverty' as its point of entry" (attributed). Analogy: SF anti-gravity machine suspending gravity — here, suspending politics from sensitive political operations under a technical cover "to which no one can object."

The concept is not a claim that development is merely "false ideology"; Ferguson frames the book as **vivisection** of a conceptual apparatus — asking what ideas *do* — not as a refutation of development thinking's truth-value alone. He denies conspiracy: unintended effects "really do just happen to be the way things work out," yet their strategic usefulness helps explain replication of "failed" projects.

## Semantic History

Coined as book title and synthesizing chapter (Ch. 9) for the Lesotho case; rapidly taken up in anthropology of development and critical development studies. Adjacent to but not identical with [[post-development]] (Escobar): shared Foucauldian tools and object (development as apparatus), different political horizon (effects analysis vs. unmaking development as language). See [[post-development-debate]] and [[hubs/theory/post-development]].

## Operationalizations

- Discourse analysis of planning texts that construct a country as an LDC "national economy" amenable to technical rural development.
- Project ethnography of a specific IRDP (Thaba-Tseka) and its livestock, crop, and decentralization components.
- Tracing [[instrument-effects]] (Foucault) where "failure" on stated goals coincides with regular political side-effects.

## Applications

Organizes [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]; cited as charter case for anthropology of development; cross-linked to [[bureaucracy]], [[applied-anthropology]], and critiques of [[modernization-theory]]-style planning.

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-country / single-project base for a global-sounding name — Ferguson himself poses generalization as open (Epilogue). Materialist critics of discourse approaches may assimilate it to thin "ideas first" analysis (see position 3 on [[post-development-debate]]). Concept must stay in **attributed** voice when stating the machine's causal claim.
