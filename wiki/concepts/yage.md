# Yagé

## Definition as Coined

**Yagé** (also yajé; related to the wider ayahuasca complex) is the emic name, in much of southwest Colombia’s highland–lowland circuit, for the ritual brew and the nights of song, vomiting, vision (*pinta*), and curing through which shamans heal. [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1987) treats yagé nights as **montage**: alterations, cracks, displacements, and swerves; flashing between self and group; ordered disorder of song; a testing-ground where colonial images of the [[wild-man|wild man]] and devil are put to work against envy, sorcery, and misfortune (attributed).

Santiago Mutumbajoy (Ingano shaman, Mocoa river area) and patients such as José García (white colonist pupil) are central ethnographic figures in the book (names as published; some anonymity noted in Author’s Note).

## Semantic History

- **Lowland–highland circuit.** Highland Ingano and related curers; lowland Siona, Cofán, and other forest peoples as sources of plant knowledge and *auca*-typed power in highland eyes (as Taussig reports — association, not identity).
- **Colonist patients.** Poor white colonists and townspeople as major clientele; visions of shaman-as-devil; payment in money and goods; “Indian magic” as colonial creation purchased against fate (attributed).
- **Priests vs shamans.** Capuchin and parish competition/exorcism beside shamanic practice (Umbría and related cases).
- **Global ayahuasca boom.** Post-1987 literature (not this ingest) transforms yagé into transnational spirituality; do not read that industry back into Taussig’s 1969–85 Putumayo/Cauca circuit.

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic description of séances: preparation, song, *pinta*, diagnosis of sorcery vs *mal aires*/forest illness, purging, soil-and-farm curing songs, tiger transformation narratives (Casemiro), apprenticeship via self-healing (“the cure is to become a curer”). Not a pharmacological monograph in this source.

Hilberio’s teaching as Taussig records: “Yage is a great deceiver… not positive. You see a lot of false things” — yet a part that is not deception (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Circuit ≠ pan-Amazon.** Taussig’s yagé is SW Colombian foothill–lowland and linked Cauca circulation; not a general theory of all ayahuasca traditions.
- **Gender and race.** *Las tres potencias*, mulatto healers, and women patients/healers need separate tracking; Marlene chapter shows Putumayo power circulating in Afro-Colombian Cauca without travel ([[cauca-valley-afro-colombians]]).
- **Positionality.** Author as participant-observer/patient shapes the montage method and the data.
