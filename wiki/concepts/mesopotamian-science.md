# Mesopotamian Science

All evaluative claims on this page — about progress, stagnation, comparative quality, or "submission to the gods" as an explanation — are [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux's (1992)]] own attributed judgments, not wiki-voice verdicts. Roux frames his overall treatment as pushing back against a "pack of wolves" caricature of the Assyrians, arguing they were "less thirsty of blood than of knowledge and culture" — an explicit authorial thesis about how Assyriology's own historiography has swung between "warlike" and "learned" characterizations of Mesopotamia (attributed; a candidate Reflexivity note for a future `disciplines/archaeology` or `disciplines/assyriology` page).

## Definition as Coined

"Mesopotamian science" here denotes the mathematical, astronomical, divinatory, and medical scholarship documented in cuneiform tablets from the early second millennium BCE through the end of cuneiform literacy in the Parthian period. Roux characterizes the whole enterprise as a **"science of lists"** — a term he calls "somewhat derisive" while partially endorsing it: number-tables, geographical itinerary-lists (measured in *bêru*/"double-hours," roughly 10 km), a Nippur city plan matching modern archaeological surveys, and a sixth-century BCE clay "map of the world" (flat earth, circular "Bitter River," Euphrates central) are all cited as instances of this list-structured intellectual style. Historical texts followed the same logic — king-lists, eponym lists, synchronistic lists, and even chronicles are characterized by Roux as "developed lists of events" rather than continuous narrative; royal annals and building inscriptions are explicitly excluded by Roux as "not really historical but propaganda and votive texts" — a source-reliability point relevant to how the wiki treats royal inscriptions generally as an evidence class.

## Semantic History

The term "science" is an etic, anachronistic label whose fit is itself part of the
subject. Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], the boundary modern readers draw
between "science" and "pseudo-science" does not map onto the Mesopotamian material:
**astrology was the foundation of astronomy** (celestial observation grew out of
predictive divination), and divination was a formal, institutionally sanctioned "science."
Roux argues the later Classical Greek image of the "Chaldaeans" as mere
magicians/fortune-tellers is a distortion — the vulgarized popular astrology of the
near-Christian era, he holds, was a *late degeneration*, not representative of the earlier,
more genuinely empirical scholarship. That decline narrative is itself Roux's attributed
historiographic claim, and the modern reader should read "Mesopotamian science" as naming
an emic body of scribal-priestly practice, not as a claim that it satisfies a modern
demarcation of science. Roux's further verdicts — that the tradition "lacked synthesis"
(no cosmological theory-building comparable to Greek astronomy) owing to "total submission
to the gods," and that it nonetheless fed the later Hippocratic and Pythagorean traditions
— are contestable attributed positions, not settled findings.

## Mathematics and Astronomy

**Numeration.** Mesopotamian mathematics used a **sexagesimal** (base-60) system with true **positional/place-value notation** — a genuine mathematical innovation that Roux explicitly distinguishes from "juxtapositional" systems, including Roman numerals. Decimal sub-units persisted within the sexagesimal framework; a placeholder "zero" was unknown until the Seleucid period (a firm, datable claim per Roux).

**Algebra vs. geometry.** Babylonian mathematicians demonstrably used cube roots (attested on a tablet dated by Roux to the 17th–18th century BCE), square roots (√2 approximated as 1.414213 against a true value of 1.414214), and algebraic methods equivalent to solving quadratic equations (a worked example is given: 11x² + 7x = 6,15 in sexagesimal notation). Geometry, by contrast, was comparatively weak — no proofs survive, only approximations of polygonal areas. Roux characterizes the whole intellectual style as more interested "in the intricate calculations suggested by [lines, surfaces, and volumes'] mutual relations" than in their properties as such (attributed characterization).

**Astronomy's dual motivation.** Roux states this as an explicit epistemic claim: **"Astrology therefore was the foundation of astronomy."** Mesopotamian astronomy combined a "metaphysical" motive (astrology — celestial correspondence with earthly events, predictive divination) with a "chronological" one (regulating the lunar calendar). This is a historiographically important point: the ancient astrology/astronomy distinction should not be conflated with the modern pseudoscience/science boundary. Individual horoscopes (personal predetermination via celestial configuration) appear only in the Achaemenid period — a datable innovation, not a feature of the earlier tradition.

**Instruments and method.** Observational instruments — the gnomon (sundial), clepsydra (water clock), and *polos* (shadow-instrument) — are described by Roux as rudimentary; the accuracy of Babylonian astronomical results is attributed instead to sustained, disciplined observation combined with mathematical extrapolation, not to instrumental precision.

**Chronology of key advances:** Venus (Ishtar) observations were recorded under the First Dynasty of Babylon (early 2nd millennium BCE); star catalogues date to the 8th–7th centuries BCE; King Nabû-nasir's 747 BCE decree established a 19-year / 235-lunar-month intercalation cycle (the "Nabonassar calendar"), later standardized 388–367 BCE. The astronomer **Nabû-rimâni** ("Naburianus," per Strabo), early 4th century BCE, produced highly accurate lunar, solar, and eclipse tables. **Kidinnu (Cidenas)**, c. 375 BCE, calculated the length of the solar year with an error of only 4 minutes 32.65 seconds — a result Roux notes was more accurate than the corresponding 1887 calculation by the modern astronomer Oppolzer (attributed comparative claim).

**East–West comparison.** Roux quotes an unnamed "specialist" (source not further identified in the extraction): "The Greeks were philosophers as well as geometers, the Chaldaeans were empiricists and sophisticated calculators." Babylonian astronomy, on this reading, never produced synthetic cosmological theories comparable to Aristarchus's heliocentrism or Hipparchus's geocentrism. Roux attributes this absence, in his own causal/interpretive claim, to "total submission to the gods" — a strong theological-determinist explanation that should be recorded as an attributed interpretive position, not fact (candidate for a debates page on explanations for the perceived "lack of synthesis" in Mesopotamian science, should a source disputing this framing later be ingested).

### Late Babylonian astronomy (Achaemenid–Parthian)

Named astronomers Nabû-rimâni and Kidinnu (above) exemplify continued scientific activity in priestly/scribal circles into the Achaemenid and Hellenistic periods, even as the general population lost cuneiform literacy. In the 2nd–3rd centuries BCE the Babylonian **Sudinês** translated Kidinnu's and other astronomers' writings into Greek, and **Berossus**, a priest of Marduk, wrote a Greek-language work (*Babyloniaca*, dedicated to Antiochus I) mixing astrology with historical narrative. Roux argues Greek intellectual interest in Babylon focused chiefly on "the scientific and pseudo-scientific works of the 'Chaldaeans'" rather than on history or literature — limited contacts that "saved for posterity some of the most remarkable achievements of Mesopotamian scientists," while, in Roux's own negative value judgment, astrology "permeated and corrupted the religions of the West" (attributed position, not wiki voice).

The last known cuneiform text of any kind is an **astronomical almanac dated 74–75 CE**, under Parthian rule — Roux's chosen terminal marker for the end of cuneiform Mesopotamian civilization. Only about 200 astronomical/astrological texts, a handful of contracts, and two or three fragmentary chronicles and Babylonian-Greek vocabularies survive from the Parthian period as a whole.

## Divination

Divination is defined broadly by Roux as words or actions intended to influence supernatural forces, and treated in the Assyrian state as "a highly developed and official 'science'": methods included astral-movement observation, dream interpretation, reading of natural phenomena, extispicy (examination of a sacrificed sheep's liver), bird-flight observation, records of monstrous births, and oil-on-water or flame-shape patterns. Quoted omen-letters survive from Bêl-ushezib to Esarhaddon and from Zakir to Ashurbanipal (the latter reporting a lunar eclipse).

Roux is explicit that despite this elaborate apparatus, Assyrian royal policy remained fundamentally realist: omens supplied "a general set of circumstances" within which the king acted freely, and kings sometimes solicited repeated omens until receiving a favorable one — divination functioning, on this reading, as much as post-hoc legitimation as genuine guidance (attributed, methodologically important nuance). Roux separately argues that the Classical Greek image of "Chaldaeans" as mere magicians and fortune-tellers is a distortion: he characterizes vulgarized popular astrology and witchcraft as a late (near-Christian-era) degeneration, not representative of the earlier, more genuinely empirical scientific culture — an explicit periodization/decline narrative that is itself an attributed, potentially contestable historiographic claim.

## Medicine

Mesopotamian medicine combined two coexisting traditions, which Roux stresses were *not* mutually exclusive: **âshiputu** (magico-religious medicine, treating illness as divine, demonic, or sorcery-caused, administered by *âshipu*-exorcists and *bâru*-diviners) and **asûtu** (rational, pragmatic medicine, practiced by the *asû* physician). Roux's key interpretive point is that *asû* physicians recognized natural causation (dust, dirt, food and drink, even contagion) alongside supernatural etiology, rather than practicing a purely magical medicine.

**Professional status.** The *asû* physician held professional, upper-middle-class status after lengthy training (school plus apprenticeship under a senior physician). The Code of Hammurabi's nine medical/surgical laws (fixed prices, harsh malpractice penalties) are characterized by Roux as exceptional-case law never actually enforced in practice, not evidence of low professional status; physicians were, on his reading, "held in high esteem," and royal courts exchanged doctors internationally — Mitanni's king Tushratta sent physicians to Egypt's Amenophis III (14th century BCE), and Babylonian doctors were sent to the Hittite king Hattusilis III (1275–1250 BCE).

**Labat's diagnostic treatise.** Professor R. Labat reconstructed a diagnostic/prognostic treatise from tablets of the 8th–5th centuries BCE: 40 tablets in five "chapters," beginning with ominous signs observed en route to the patient (e.g., the color of a pig seen along the way as a prognostic sign) and organized thereafter by organ, syndrome, or order of symptom appearance, with the final six tablets devoted to gynecological and pediatric disease. The treatise emphasizes prognosis over diagnosis; treatments are rarely specified within it.

**Named conditions:** epilepsy (grand mal, attributed to the "hand of Sin"), urinary/bladder stone (treated with a beer-based folk remedy, water intake contraindicated), and severe jaundice/"icterus gravis" (*ahhazu*, deemed incurable) are among the specific diseases identified in the corpus; psychiatric conditions including states resembling depression are also documented, described by Roux as "not as modern as one may think" (attributed).

**Pharmacopoeia.** The oldest known Mesopotamian pharmacopoeia dates to the Third Dynasty of Ur, recording recipes for ointments, lotions, and mixtures compounded from minerals and plants, administered orally, by inhalation, fumigation, instillation, ointment, poultice, enema, or suppository — explicitly never by injection. Quoted examples include an opium-and-myrrh-oil urethral instillation for urinary retention, and a complex herbal poultice (sheep kidney, dates, resins, mustard, cantharis) for "stricture of the lungs," applied with a gazelle-skin dressing and a supporting dietary/warmth regimen.

**Practical competence.** A quoted letter from the royal physician Arad-Nanna to Ashurbanipal describes treating epistaxis (nosebleed) via nasal packing; Roux's own aside — "Modern physicians would not change a word of this procedure" — is an attributed editorializing endorsement of empirical validity, not an independent clinical-history finding, and should be recorded as such.

**Contagion.** A letter from Zimri-Lim, King of Mari (c. 1780 BCE), instructs his wife Shibtu to isolate a sick woman — no shared cups, seats, or bed, and restricted social contact — using a term, *mushtahhiz* ("contagious," from *ahâzu*, "to catch"), that constitutes an explicit early contagion concept, textually attested a full millennium or more before the Neo-Assyrian material that otherwise dominates this page. The chronological range of "Mesopotamian medicine" as reconstructed here therefore spans Old Babylonian to Neo-Assyrian periods, not a single synchronic snapshot.

**Roux's overall verdict** (explicitly attributed, not to be promoted to wiki voice): Mesopotamian medicine "made very little progress" over two millennia because it "founded its art upon metaphysical doctrines" and lacked "curiosity to ask themselves 'how' and 'why,'" but nonetheless "often surpassed... the other learned men of the ancient East" and was "transmitted in part to the Greeks... paving the way for the... Hippocratic reform." This is a strong comparative and evaluative claim; it should remain attributed to Roux specifically unless a source disputing Mesopotamian medicine's stagnation, or its influence on Greek medicine, is later ingested (candidate debates-page material).

## Operationalizations

- Numeration: sexagesimal, positional; no zero-placeholder before the Seleucid period.
- Astronomy: intercalation cycles, eclipse tables, solar-year calculation (Kidinnu, c. 375 BCE) — checkable against modern astronomical reconstruction.
- Medicine: diagnostic/prognostic treatise structure (Labat's reconstruction) organized by symptom/organ; pharmacological recipes with specified routes of administration.
- Divination: extispicy and omen-letter correspondence as an institutionalized, textually documented practice tied directly to royal decision-making (see also the "substitute king" ritual under [[cultures/assyrians]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Applied/practical knowledge (engineering implied by aqueduct and monolith transport; chemistry implied by dye, glass, and pigment recipes kept as craft-guild "secret recipes") is inferred from material culture rather than directly documented in surviving texts — a clear illustration of the wiki's evidence-class framework: material evidence can attest a capability that textual evidence does not explicitly theorize.
- Comparative and evaluative judgments throughout this page (astronomy's "lack of synthesis," medicine's "very little progress," the Greek/Chaldaean philosopher/calculator contrast) are Roux's own attributed historiographic verdicts and should not be treated as consensus assessments absent independent corroboration.
- Divination's status as "science" is itself a translation/framing choice; Roux's own qualification (post-hoc legitimation as much as guidance) should accompany any wiki-voice mention of Mesopotamian divinatory practice.
