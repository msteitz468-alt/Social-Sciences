# Pavula

> Principal analytic source in this wiki: [[leach-pul-eliya-1961]] / [[leach-pul-eliya-study]] ([[leach-edmund]], fieldwork 1954). Society: [[pul-eliya]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Leach 1961.)* In Dry Zone Sinhalese usage as analysed at Pul Eliya, **pavula** (“family” / kindred) names a practical grouping of effective kinsmen. Leach distinguishes:

- **Ideal pavula** — sibling solidarity and shared rights (what people say kin groups should be).
- **Effective pavula** — the set of people who actually act together for land work, faction, and ceremony at a given time; often structured through **affinal** (*massina*) alliance rather than full-brother unity.

Effective pavula is elastic but not arbitrary: choice is constrained by property, irrigation co-operation, and prior factional history. Poor persons may remain multi-aligned; leadership often tracks Gamarala / Vel Vidane office.

## Semantic History

- **Vernacular Sinhalese:** kin-group / family senses predating anthropological capture.
- **Ceylon sociology ideal types:** Ryan and others used *ge* / house language inviting lineage readings; Leach’s chapter IV on *gedera* and *pavula* is framed as correcting that terminology — compound group ≠ descent group.
- **Leach 1961:** pavula as political kindred in a kinship idiom, adapted to immutable irrigation terrain; three ideal *pavula* / three Gamarala of “old days” read as **projection of the three baga** of the Old Field, not historical demography known to living residents.

## Operationalizations

Leach operationalises via named individuals, compound residence, marriage tables, genealogical charts (e.g. “effective pavula” of V. Menikrala, summer 1954), puberty and labour cases, and plot co-operation. Membership is not established by genealogy alone: “two individuals can only be said to be of the same kinship group when they share some common interest… and justify that sharing by reference to a kinship nexus.”

## Applications

- Explains faction (e.g. compounds B vs C over Meda baga *gamvasama* succession; 1953 exchange-marriage crisis).
- Explains why **brothers** are often rivals for the same assets while **affines** maximise co-operation (*kayiya* harvest parties).
- Links to [[variga]] endogamy as the outer field within which pavula recruitment activates kinship.
- Core evidence for the thesis that kinship solidarity images landholding patterns.

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-village, short-fieldwork base; later Sinhalese kinship ethnography (not yet fully ingested here) may refine regional variation. Do not export “pavula = bilateral kindred” as pan-Sinhalese without attribution. Distinct from Fortes’s [[complementary-filiation]] (which assumes a unilineal corporate frame pavula is precisely not).
