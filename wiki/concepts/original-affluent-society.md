# The Original Affluent Society

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]; first delivered as "Notes on the Original Affluent Society," his discussion paper at the 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium (pub. 1968), and as "La première société d'abondance," *Les Temps Modernes*, 1968; elaborated in *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, 1972.)* The thesis that **hunter-gatherers were the "original affluent society"** — not despite producing little but *because* they wanted little. Affluence, Sahlins argues, is a relation between wants and means, and can be reached by two roads: the **"Galbraithean way"** of the market economy (producing much to narrow the gap between unlimited wants and limited means) and the **"Zen road"** of the forager, where "human material wants are finite and few, and technical means unchanging but on the whole adequate." Foragers take the Zen road: their wants are satisfied, their labor intermittent, their leisure abundant.

Corollary claims *(attributed)*: **scarcity is not a universal condition** but "a relation between means and ends" instituted uniquely and to an unparalleled degree by the market-industrial system; "Economic Man is a bourgeois construction." And **poverty is a social status**, "the invention of civilization," not a fixed quantity of goods.

## Semantic History

The phrase entered anthropology through Sahlins' contribution to the *Man the Hunter* milieu ([[lee-richard-b|Lee]] & DeVore, 1968) and was elaborated in *Stone Age Economics*. It inverted the mid-century textbook image (Herskovits; Steward & Faron) of foragers as barely meeting subsistence needs through ceaseless toil, and challenged the Neolithic-Revolution triumphalism (and [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]]'s energy-capture evolutionism) that treated agriculture as an unambiguous gain in leisure and security. Sahlins argued the reverse: "with the advent of agriculture, people had to work harder."

## Operationalizations

The thesis rests on **time-allocation and work-effort studies**:
- **Arnhem Land** (McCarthy & McArthur, 1948 expedition, pub. 1960): average subsistence work of 4–5 hours per person per day, highly intermittent, with adequate caloric intake and much leisure. *(Note: the Fish Creek and Hemple Bay study groups are not the same as the separately-discussed [[murngin|Murngin]].)*
- **Dobe !Kung** ([[lee-richard-b|Lee]], 1964 fieldwork as first published 1968/69): about 2 hr 9 min per adult per day (~15-hour week) in Sahlins' retelling; Lee's own *Man the Hunter* chapter reported ~2.5 subsistence days/week, **2,140 Cal** and **93.1 g protein**/capita/day vs ~1,975 required. See [[lee-what-hunters-do-study]].
- **Dobe !Kung recalculated** ([[lee-richard-b|Lee]] 1979, *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]*; same July 6–August 2 1964 diary, drought-year midwinter dry season): after truck-trip adjustment, **~2.4 subsistence workdays per adult per week**; camp diet **2,355 Cal** and **96.3 g protein**/person/day (meat 690 Cal / 34.5 g protein; mongongo 1,365 Cal / 58.8 g; other vegetables 300 Cal / 3.0 g); surplus ~**380 Cal**/person/day over weighted requirement ~1,975. Gathering ~**70%** of calories (~12,000 Cal/gathering day) vs hunting ~**30%** (~7,230 Cal/hunting day). **Full work week** including manufacture/maintenance and housework (Table 9.12): men **44.5 hrs**, women **40.1**, average **~42.3 hrs** — the figure Lee himself puts against industrial wage-work comparisons when all three work categories are counted. Anthropometry 1967–9 (n=641): adult seasonal weight loss only **~1–2%** (July high → January low), vs **4–6.5%** among West African agriculturalists in "hungry season" studies Lee cites. Draper /Du/da multi-season check: overall **2.3-day** subsistence week. Lee accepted that the 1964 diary alone is not representative of all !Kung groups or all seasons (Johnson 1975 critique). Study: [[lee-kung-san-study]].
- **Hadza** (Woodburn): "less than two hours a day" over the year; Hadza reportedly declined agriculture to preserve leisure.

Sahlins compares these to swidden cultivators (Conklin's Hanunoo at ~3 hr 20 min/day in cultivation alone) to argue foragers work no more, and probably less, than early farmers.

### Lee 1979 densification (primary monograph)

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, esp. Chs. 9–10, 15; fieldwork 1963–64 / 1967–69 / 1973.)* Lee frames the book as a "revisionist" account against the precarious-forager image: among the !Kung, food supply is sufficient to needs with simple tools; vegetable foods 60–70% of diet (mongongo up to half the vegetable share); game 30–40% of calories; hunting remains men's ritual/social focus. He argues energy studies give a comparative baseline rather than a complete social account. On body size vs "chronic semistarvation," he sides with Tobias-style adaptive/genetic readings over de Almeida and softens Truswell–Hansen's calorie-insufficiency line, citing adequate diet, high leisure, and higher lifetime meat contribution among shorter hunters. He attributes Wilmsen's larger (~6%) mid-1970s /Xai/xai weight loss to **cash-economy and sedentarization** stress, not wild-resource failure — a live clash later absorbed into the [[kalahari-debate]] / [[original-affluent-society-debate]]. Do **not** mix 1968/69 and 1979 numbers without noting the 1979 recalculation (mongongo yields raised; women's productivity ratio cut from 2.5× to ~1.67× relative to a hunting day; all four diary weeks retained with truck adjustment).

## Applications

The thesis reframed the economics of foraging (non-accumulation as rational under mobility, not as poverty of means — "the pure nomad is the poor nomad"), fed the concept of [[underproduction]] and the [[domestic-mode-of-production]], and supplied substantivist economic anthropology ([[substantivism]]) with its most striking counter-example to the universality of scarcity. It became a fixture of introductory anthropology and of ecological and primitivist critiques of industrial society (David Graeber's 2017 foreword calls for Sahlins to be read against economics itself).

## Critiques and Limitations

The thesis is contested — see **[[original-affluent-society-debate]]** and the **[[kalahari-debate|Kalahari debate]]**. At the *Man the Hunter* symposium where it was first aired, dissent was immediate: Balikci ([[netsilik|Netsilik]]) and Williams (Birhor) reported real hardship, so the editors concluded it "would not apply in all cases," and Lorna Marshall — reporting Nyae Nyae [[juhoansi|!Kung]] as "all thin" and anxious about food — called "affluent society" "a bon mot" that "does not add to the understanding." Sahlins himself flagged two vulnerabilities: (1) modern foragers are "displaced persons" in ecologically marginal refuges, possibly unrepresentative of the Paleolithic; and (2) some documented foragers (the industrious, ceremonially-driven [[murngin|Murngin]]) contradict the affluence-through-leisure pattern, raising the worry that forager ethnography records "incomplete cultures." Later critics questioned whether the work-hour figures excluded food processing, tool maintenance, and childcare, and whether !Kung health, seasonal hunger, and mortality are consistent with "affluence." The claim is a single-author interpretive construct built on others' data; it belongs in attributed voice, never as a finding.
