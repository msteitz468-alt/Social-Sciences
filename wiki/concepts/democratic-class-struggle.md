# Democratic Class Struggle

## Definition as Coined

[[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Chs VII–VIII) treats elections in modern democracies as the **expression of the democratic class struggle**: conflict among groups (especially classes) over government, channeled into party competition rather than civil war or revolution. Left voting is analyzed as a response to group needs (income insecurity, unsatisfying work, status) under social conditions that shape communication, mobility beliefs, and traditionalism.

## Semantic History

The phrase reframes [[class-struggle]] for stable electoral regimes — continuous with Marx's problem of class politics, but relocating struggle inside democratic norms (parallel in spirit to Dahrendorf's later [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict]], from a different theoretical program). It became a slogan for class-cleavage electoral sociology (Lipset and Rokkan's later cleavage model is adjacent but not this 1960 book).

## Operationalizations

Party choice by occupation, income, class identification; left/right scales; modifiers: religion, region, community type, generation, [[cross-pressures]]. Continuity of historical party patterns vs short-term change.

## Applications

- Comparative Western voting (*Political Man* Part II).
- American parties and sectional exceptions (Part III, including the one-party South).
- Contrast with extremist (anti-system) class politics in Chs IV–V.

## Critiques and Limitations

Assumes party systems that organize class; weak where ethnicity, religion, or clientelism dominate. Mid-century Western survey base. Class voting decline in later decades (not yet ingested) is often read as challenge or historical limit of the thesis — attribute later findings separately.
