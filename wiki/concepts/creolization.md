# Creolization

Analytical concept for cultural mixing that produces **new integrated forms** rather than one-way absorption of a subordinate group into a dominant culture. In [[deetz-james|James Deetz]]'s *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* (1996), creolization is the explicit alternative to simple **[[acculturation]]** for African American and Southern material culture.

## Definition as Coined (Deetz 1996)

*(Attributed.)* Deetz defines creolization as "the interaction between two or more cultures to produce an integrated mix which is different from its antecedents." He applies a **creole-language analogy** to material culture: Anglo-American artifacts may supply the "lexicon" while African American "rules" of combination govern use — as Haitian Creole has French lexicon with West African grammatical structure.

## Semantic History

Long career in linguistics and Caribbean cultural studies before HA uptake. Deetz 1996 uses the term to refuse the model of Africans simply adopting dominant Anglo-American lifeways. He quotes Mechal Sobel (*The World They Made Together*) on mutual Black–White influence in the South producing a new mixed culture of space, time, causality, and death. Parallel HA career for **[[colono-ware]]** (Hall, Ferguson, Mouer, Orser 1996 "strange career") treats creolization as multiethnic process, not pure ethnic type fossils.

## Operationalizations

| Domain (Deetz 1996) | How creolization is read |
|---|---|
| Architecture | Shotgun path (West Africa → Haiti → New Orleans); Parting Ways 12-ft modules; SC wattle/clay quarters; Chesapeake African *additions* (porches, root cellars) to English earthfast forms |
| Ceramics | [[colono-ware]] as largely African-made under plantation economies; vessel shapes tracking residential history |
| Pipes | English mold technology + African-derived decoration = "true creole form" (Emerson; Deetz synthesis) |
| Foodways | Stews/bowls/chopped bone vs individualized Georgian plate service; gumbo/two-part meal analogies |

## Applications

- Free Black **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]** as high-expression end of continuum (less slavery constraint).
- Regional contrast: South Carolina Black majority and Barbadian founding vs Chesapeake slower racial-slavery consolidation.
- Critique of isolated "ethnic markers" in favor of whole-site fabric (Deetz closing Ch. 8).

## Deagan's St Augustine research programme (1983–1996)

*(Attributed — Kathleen Deagan, as reported in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], chs.5 and 16, Lawrence & Shepherd; King.)* Deagan's long-running research programme on St Augustine, Florida — a Spanish garrison/mission town continuously inhabited since 1565 — began from the premise that the settlement was a **Creole community** from the outset, not a purely Spanish one: its population included Spanish-born colonists, Spanish-Americans, Guale Indians, Africans and African-Americans, and mestizo (Spanish/Guale/African) people, with experience further mediated by gender and social status. Across food remains, furnishings, dress, and architecture, Deagan traced how this diversity was negotiated differently by different households over time — a programme distinct from, but complementary to, Ferguson's colono-ware-centered creolization work (see [[colono-ware]]). King's household-archaeology chapter separately credits Deagan (1973) with the term "**mestizaje**" for the specific process by which Native Florida women who married colonizing Spanish men gained enhanced status (see [[household-archaeology]]).

## The Vlach–Upton shotgun-house debate

*(Attributed — [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch.15, Hicks & Horning.)* John Michael Vlach's classic study of the Southern shotgun house (Vlach 1986 [1976]) traced its architectural form along a Yoruba-via-Haiti route (after the Haitian Revolution) to New Orleans, and is credited with convincingly demonstrating that route — but is also criticized, attributed to Dell Upton (1996: 3–4), for emphasizing a single "Africanness" over other Caribbean and American influences, and for resting on a **static** conception of ethnic identity rather than a **dynamic, creolised** one. This exchange is presented as a live methodological caution rather than a resolved dispute: James Delle (2000a) separately warns that the term "creolisation" can itself mask the diversity of the new identities it is meant to describe, functioning as a smoothing label rather than an analytical one. Together, Vlach/Upton and Delle's cautions qualify the confidence with which any single creolization narrative (shotgun-house, colono-ware, or otherwise) should be read as settled.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested: degree of African vs Native vs European contribution; risk of romanticizing "survival"; analogy from language to things is itself a theory. **Four-way non-identity** still binds: house plan ≠ language ≠ genetics ≠ self-identified people. Orser and others may keep creolization while embedding it in capitalism/colonial haunts. Upton's and Delle's cautions (above) extend this: "creolisation" risks flattening internal diversity into a single hybrid narrative unless the specific strands and their weighting are argued, not assumed.
