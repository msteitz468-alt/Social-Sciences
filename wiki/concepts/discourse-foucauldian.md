# Discourse (Foucauldian)

## Definition as Coined

In [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s usage (attributed), **discourse** is not language or representation but a regulated practice — the system of statements, and the rules governing what can be said, by whom, and from which institutional position — through which objects of knowledge and speaking subjects are constituted. It is inseparable from power: "it is in discourse that power and knowledge are joined together." In *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I the question is reframed at the level of discourse: not "why are we repressed?" but "what is the over-all 'discursive fact,' the way in which sex is 'put into discourse.'" (This is distinct from the phenomenological sense of pre-discursive reality — see [[things-before-discourse]].)

## Semantic History

The concept is developed in *The Archaeology of Knowledge* (1969, not ingested) as the rules of "discursive formations"; in the mid-1970s [[genealogy|genealogical]] turn it is folded into [[power-knowledge]]. In *The History of Sexuality* Foucault's headline claim is the **"discursive explosion"**: from the eighteenth century, "around and apropos of sex, one sees a veritable discursive explosion" — a "regulated and polymorphous incitement to discourse," not a "massive censorship." Sex became "a multiplicity of discourses produced by a whole series of mechanisms operating in different institutions" (demography, biology, medicine, psychiatry, pedagogy, ethics, political criticism), displacing the medieval "unitary" discourse of the flesh.

## Operationalizations

- **The economy of discourses, not their content.** "The 'economy' of discourses... their intrinsic technology, the necessities of their operation, the tactics they employ... this, and not a system of representations, is what determines the essential features of what they have to say." The history of sexuality "must first be written from the viewpoint of a history of discourses."
- **Silence as part of discourse.** "There is not one but many silences, and they are an integral part of the strategies that underlie and permeate discourses" — e.g. eighteenth-century schools that never named children's sex yet organized their whole architecture and discipline around it.
- **Tactical polyvalence.** Discourses are "tactical elements or blocks operating in the field of force relations"; the same categories that pathologized homosexuality enabled a "reverse discourse" demanding legitimacy "in the same vocabulary" (see [[analytics-of-power]]).

## Applications

Discourse underpins [[scientia-sexualis]], [[deployment-of-sexuality]], and the whole [[analytics-of-power]]. As method it grounds Foucauldian **discourse analysis**, widely adopted across sociology, media studies, linguistic anthropology, and critical policy studies. [[escobar-arturo|Escobar]]'s *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (1995) applies the toolkit to postwar [[development-discourse]] (three-axis discursive formation; [[regime-of-representation]]); see [[post-development]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Idealism / the missing referent** (attributed): critics charge that privileging discourse slights extra-discursive material reality (bodies, economies). Foucault replies that his deployments of power are "directly connected to the body."
- **Method underspecification** (attributed): "discourse analysis" in Foucault's wake covers heterogeneous practices; the rigor of "discursive formation" from the *Archaeology* is often lost in application.
