# Desacralization (sacrificial)

## Definition as Coined

In Hubert & Mauss's *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice]]* (1898), **desacralization** names the general function of rites that **rid the [[sacrifier]] (or an object) of a sacred charge**. That charge may be:

- residual consecration after a high rite (exit so the person can re-enter the [[sacred-and-profane|profane]]);
- **impurity** from ritual breach or contact with impure things — which they treat as itself a kind of consecration ("the sinner, just like the criminal, is a sacred being");
- dangerous concentration of religious force that must be eliminated rather than absorbed.

When the aim is to remove impurity, the rite is an **expiation**. Force typically moves from sacrifier into victim **before** immolation; the victim is then destroyed, expelled, or abandoned (Azazel goat, leper's bird, holocaust, precipitation). Exit sequences are highly developed relative to sacralizing rites.

Paired opposite: [[sacralization]].

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Hubert & Mauss 1898 | General function: dispel sin / remove charge; exit phase of every scheme |
| Evans-Pritchard *Nuer Religion* 1956 | Maps **personal/piacular** sacrifice onto desacralization — "ridding man of Spirit"/danger; central to his account of Nuer religion |
| Douglas / pollution theory | Adjacent but distinct: [[ritual-pollution]] and matter-out-of-place need not be sacrificial desacralization |

## Operationalizations

Code by initial charged state, pre-immolation transfer of impurity onto victim, elimination vs communion disposition of remains, and weight of exit/purification sequences. [[scapegoat|Scapegoat]] and expulsion rites are desacralizing subtypes in this framework (Hubert & Mauss engage Frazer's line on this point without adopting his origin story).

## Applications

- Piacular and purification sacrifices; nazir-type release from vow-consecration; first-fruits as ridding the harvest of a dangerous religious character so mortals may eat.
- Every complete sacrifice's **exit** phase — even sacralizing rites require some desacralization so participants can leave the sacred zone.
- Agrarian and building rites that combine elimination of a dangerous spirit with consecration of a structure or group.

## Critiques and Limitations

Calling impurity a form of the sacred was already a Robertson Smith inheritance and remains contested where ethnography sharply separates holy and unclean (Douglas). Reducing all piacular logic to "desacralization" can underplay substitution/*vita pro vita* meanings that Evans-Pritchard centers for the Nuer. The term is easily confused with secularization (historical decline of religion) — different concept entirely.
