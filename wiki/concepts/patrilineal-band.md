# Patrilineal Band

## Definition as Coined

[[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s **patrilineal band** is a [[culture-type]] whose [[cultural-core]] comprises "certain inevitable relationships between a hunting economy, descent, marriage, and land tenure" (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, Ch. 1 illustration; full treatment Ch. 7, from the 1936 Festschrift essay). Typically: localized lineage/band, exogamy, patrilocality, territorial association. Slightly higher [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration|integration]] than Great Basin family-level organization.

## Semantic History

Stewardian type-concept in neo-evolutionary literature; later debated against alternative kinship/band reconstructions. Contrasted with **[[composite-hunting-band|composite hunting bands]]** (multi-family, often bilateral, northern Canadian type cases) and with post-contact **predatory bands** (form/function type after horses and settler pressure among some Shoshoneans).

**Service rename (1962/1971):** [[service-elman-r|Service]] (*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*) renames Steward’s type the **[[patrilocal-band]]**, arguing “patrilineal” misleads when the structural core is virilocal residence + exogamy rather than recognized unilineal descent. He ranks the patrilocal form expectable under aboriginal conditions while (2nd ed.) demoting that ranking from firm conviction to surmise. See [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]].

## Operationalizations

Steward argued similarity across cases rests not on total environment (desert Bushmen/Australians/S. Californians vs rainforest Negritos vs cold rainy Fuegians) but on comparable **subsistence problems** (game in small nonmigratory groups favoring collective localized hunting and male land inheritance). Ecological conditions he stressed include population density, game distribution, and hunting technology that makes co-operation productive. Comparative ethnonyms in Ch. 7 (as mid-century literature named them): Bushmen, Negritos, Australians, Ona, southern California groups, and others — filed here as type cases, not as separate society pages from this source alone.

He rejected pure diffusion and "archaic heritage" as sufficient explanations of the type's wide distribution.

## Applications

Charter illustration of [[multilinear-evolution]] and [[cultural-ecological-method]]; baseline for Southwest lineage→clan trajectories (Ch. 9: conditions under which patrilineal bands amalgamate into multiclan villages).

## Critiques and Limitations

Second-hand ethnography and ideal-type vs variation (Steward notes deviations); alternative accounts of band composition and gender; colonial ethnonyms; Galton's problem among related hunter-gatherer cases. Service challenges pure geographical determination of band *structure* (habitat useful for demography; Central Australians as counterexample) and treats many composite cases as contact products rather than Steward’s migratory-herd aboriginal type. The 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium found the patrilineal/patrilocal ideal not to be the modal empirical form: observed band composition is typically fluid and bilateral — see **[[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]**.
