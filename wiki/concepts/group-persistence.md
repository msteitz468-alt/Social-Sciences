# Group-persistence (Pareto Class II residues)

## Definition as Coined

Pareto’s **Class II residues** — Italian *persistenza degli aggregati* — rendered by Livingston as **group-persistence** / **persistence of aggregates** after rejecting several English alternatives (Livingston n. at §991). They are the relatively constant sentiments that give **stability** to combinations: “Were it not for the persistence of certain relations, the combinations in Class I would be ephemeral… Persistence of associations… is the cement that holds it together” (§§890–891).

Livingston: basic to Pareto’s social equilibrium, class-circulation, and conception of history; related to ordinary “habit” or “custom” but as a technical index of residue force.

## Operationalizations

Class II genera (overview from Ch VI; eleven group-persistences in Pareto’s table per Livingston):

- Persistence of relations between living and dead; of living and places; of families and social groups; of uniformities; personifications and abstractions (Roma, Annona, Progress, Democracy, Reason) acquiring independent existence (§§970–991 region).
- Passive side: given one element of a cemented combination, others are expected; active side: defend and restore the aggregate.
- Mutual reinforcement of residues and “facts” once a complex is stabilized (§987).

Exact genus list is dense in the treatise; wiki summary stresses the **cement / conservative / personification** functions rather than every species.

## Applications

- Explains durability of myths, cults, national personifications, and political abstractions after original combinations fade.
- **Elite theory:** Type-I (force/sentiment) governing classes show Class II over Class I; crystallization ideal (Sparta, late Empire, decadent Venice) (§§2274, 2550–2551).
- **Optimum power:** Class II in subordinates (obedience) + Class I in leaders ([[lions-and-foxes]], §§2427–2455).
- **Demagogic failure:** Speculator governments deficient in Class II and fail to exploit subjects’ group-persistences (§2454 n.).
- American reception: Livingston’s 1925 “Myth of Good English” referenced group-persistences (Editor’s Note).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Translation opacity (Livingston admits syntactic difficulty parallel to “non-logical actions”).
- Risk of labeling any continuity “Class II” without independent measure.
- Personification examples (Progress, Democracy) are polemically charged in Pareto’s prose — attributed THEORY, not wiki rejection of those ideals.
