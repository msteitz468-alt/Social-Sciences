# Social Sanctions

An analytical concept given its standard anthropological formulation by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] in his article "Social Sanctions" (*Encyclopaedia of the Social Sciences*, 1933, reprinted in *Structure and Function in Primitive Society*, 1952). A sanction is the mechanism by which a society enforces conformity to its recognized standards of conduct, and thereby maintains its [[social-structure|social structure]].

## Definition as Coined

Radcliffe-Brown defined a sanction as (attributed): "a reaction on the part of a society or of a considerable number of its members to a mode of behaviour which is thereby approved (positive sanctions) or disapproved (negative sanctions)." He crossed this positive/negative axis with a second: **diffuse** sanctions (spontaneous reactions of individual members) versus **organized** sanctions (traditional, recognized collective procedures). He held that in all human societies negative sanctions are more sharply developed than positive ones, and defined **social obligations** as rules whose breach entails a negative sanction.

## Semantic History

The term long predates Radcliffe-Brown (in law and in Bentham's utilitarian classification of sanctions), but his 1933 codification made it a standard analytic grid in social anthropology. He internalized it psychologically as well as structurally: sanctions operate as motives both through the individual's wish for approbation and through the internalization of communal standards — "what is called conscience is thus in the widest sense the reflex in the individual of the sanctions of the society." He treated religious sanctions as a distinct sub-type, defining **sin** as "behaviour falling under a negative religious sanction," and noted that in many societies an involuntary condition (ritual pollution) can be sanctioned where modern Western law requires voluntary fault.

## Operationalizations

Radcliffe-Brown built a working typology used in comparative ethnography:

- **Diffuse** — moral/ethical reprobation, satirical ridicule, and religious sanctions.
- **Organized negative** — public derision, exclusion, degradation, fines/seizure, corporal punishment, mutilation, exile, imprisonment, death; these he called **legal sanctions** when applied by a constituted authority.
- **Organized positive** ("premial") — rare: honours, decorations, titles.
- **Primary vs. secondary** — secondary sanctions (retaliation, the duel, indemnification, war-as-reprisal) are procedures set in motion when recognized rights have been infringed, aimed at proportionate satisfaction to the injured party. His "Primitive Law" article extends this to public and private delicts.

## Applications

Sanctions anchor Radcliffe-Brown's analyses of law, morals, [[taboo|ritual prohibition]] (enforced by "ritual sanctions" — the belief that misfortune follows non-observance), and religion. The concept feeds directly into the sociology of law and deviance and stands behind later work on social control. See [[structural-functionalism]] and [[social-function]].

### "Primitive Law" (1933; in *Structure and Function*)

Radcliffe-Brown's companion article (attributed throughout) narrows **law** to "social control through the systematic application of the force of politically organised society" (citing Pound): the field of law is "coterminous with that of organised legal sanctions." Societies without legal sanctions still have custom and convention backed by other sanctions; in that restricted sense some simple societies "have no law." He rejected applying the modern criminal/civil distinction to preliterate systems and substituted **public delicts** vs. **private delicts**:

- A **public delict** normally triggers organised procedure by the community or its representatives, fixing responsibility and inflicting a penal sanction. Immediate function (attributed): express collective moral indignation and restore "social euphoria"; ultimate function: maintain the offended moral sentiment. Typical public delicts in simpler societies: incest, sorcery against a community member, repeated breaches of tribal custom, sacrilege — often overlapping **ritual** pollution of the whole community. Disobedience of political authority and offences against chiefs may also be treated as public.
- A **private delict** is an injury to a person or group that leads to a **restitutive** sanction (indemnity, damages) via judicial authority or negotiated procedure. Typical cases: killing, wounding, theft, adultery, unpaid debts. In preliterate systems the sanction is often both restitutive and repressive (e.g. double restitution for theft).

A single deed may attract three concurrent reactions: diffuse moral reprobation, a ritual sanction (pollution / purification / fear of illness), and a penal sanction. Where no legal system yet exists, **retaliatory sanctions** (regulated vengeance, lex talionis, the duel, Australian spear-throwing as satisfaction) and customary **indemnities** do the work of private redress. Comparative illustrations he used as method-demonstrations (not primary ethnography for this vault): **Yurok** (indemnities without public delict procedure; no political organisation); **Ifugao** (kindred conflict, go-betweens, scales of indemnity by class; no judicial authority in the narrow sense); **A-Kamba** (and related Kenya Bantu) elders' arbitration courts, curse-enforcement, limited public procedure (*kingolle*); **Ashanti** (organised public delicts as "things hateful to the gods," chief's courts; private delicts as "household cases"). His closing claim: elementary law is "intimately bound up with magic and religion," and understanding its beginnings requires comparative study of whole systems of social sanctions.

## Critiques and Limitations

Radcliffe-Brown's functionalist gloss — that "the function of the sanction is to restore the social euphoria" after a deed produces "social dysphoria," making sanctions "reactions on the part of a community to events affecting its integration" — carries the same equilibrium bias critics charge against [[social-function|functionalism]] generally: it frames enforcement as system-maintenance and underweights the way sanctions serve *particular* interests and entrench power (the conflict-theory objection). The descriptive typology (diffuse/organized, positive/negative, primary/secondary) has proven more durable than the functionalist explanation attached to it.
