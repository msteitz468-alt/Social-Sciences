# Invention and Convention

The central conceptual pair of [[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]]'s *[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]* (1975; rev. 1981). All claims here are Wagner's, in attributed voice.

## Definition as Coined

Wagner distinguishes two reciprocal modes of symbolization:

- **Collectivizing (conventional) symbolization** — symbols that generalize and order by "standing for" something other than themselves (the classic denotative symbol); the domain of shared convention, morality, and the collective.
- **Differentiating (inventive) symbolization** — symbolization in which a new referent is introduced simultaneously with the symbol, so that "symbol and symbolized collapse" and the thing "stands for itself"; the domain of the particular, individual, and situational.

The two are dialectically interdependent: "The necessity of invention is given by cultural convention, and the necessity of cultural convention is given by invention. We invent so as to sustain and restore our conventional orientation; we adhere to this orientation so as to realize the power and gain that invention brings."

Wagner grounds his sense of "invention" not in the modern "bolt-from-the-blue" connotation but in older usages — musical invention, and Rudolphus Agricola's fifteenth-century *Dialectical Invention*, where invention is a part of dialectic (proposing an analogy subsequently "judged").

## Core Mechanisms (as argued by Wagner)

- **Context and relational meaning.** Meaning is "a function of the ways in which we create and otherwise experience contexts"; there are no primary meanings — "the definition and the extension of a word or other symbolic element are fundamentally the same operation," and "the good, solid feeling of 'absolute' denotation… is an illusion grounded in nonrelation, or tautology."
- **Control and masking.** Every act of symbolization ideologically discriminates the two modes: one is treated as the realm of deliberate human action (the "controlling context"), the other as innate or given. This discrimination "masks" the symbolic nature of both — necessarily, since "to see the whole field at once, in all its implications, is to suffer a 'relativization' of intention."
- **Counterinvention and motivation.** Every act of invention produces both the intended transformation and an unintended transformation of the control itself, which the actor externalizes as *motivation* — a felt, compulsive force assigned to psychology, spirits, or drives, but not in fact an internal "thing."
- **Relativization and inversion.** Because contexts continually exchange characteristics through use, conventional distinctions relativize over time; when a controlling context becomes too relativized to work, actors invert modes (Americans invert toward differentiation/"creativity" to recharge a relativized collective Culture; tribal/religious traditions invert toward deliberate conventionalizing — prophets, cult leaders, lawgivers).

## The Inversion Thesis

Wagner's pivotal comparative claim: *which* mode is treated as innate versus artificial is itself a cultural convention, and Western and tribal/religious traditions invert the assignment. Middle-class American culture treats the collective/conventional (institutions, "collective enterprise") as humanly made and the differentiating (individuality, impulse, "human nature") as innate; among the Daribi and other Melanesian societies the reverse holds — the conventional order (kinship, cosmology) is innate and motivating, while differentiating action is what people deliberately do. "In the first instance, cultural convention masks its own invention as motivation; in the second, its conscious articulation masks the invention of an innate and motivating nature." Compactly: the urban Westerner "'is' power… and 'does' morality," whereas the religious or tribal person "'does'… power… and 'is' moral."

Wagner extends the scheme to the self (ego/"personality" vs. soul; guilt vs. shame; neurosis vs. hysteria as failure modes), to language (Chomskyan performance vs. competence reread as invention vs. convention), to the rise of class society and civilization (the division of the dialectic between social segments), and to the history of anthropology itself (see [[obviation]]).

## Applications

- [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]] folds the inversion thesis into [[perspectivism]] and [[multinaturalism]]: Western ontology takes body/nature as given and soul/culture as constructed; many indigenous ontologies invert this.
- [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]]'s Melanesian work (e.g., "No Nature, No Culture: The Hagen Case," cited by Wagner as extending his theme) develops the same refusal to universalize the Western nature/culture assignment.
- The pair underwrites Wagner's account of fieldwork itself: every human being is "a 'fieldworker' of a sort, controlling the culture shock of daily experience" — see [[culture]] and [[reverse-anthropology]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Wagner himself acknowledges (rev. ed. introduction) that opposing "Western conventionalism" to tribal differentiation risks the "progressive-conservative" dichotomy Sahlins parodied as "the West and the Rest"; he argues his version is more nuanced, but the binary contrast remains the scheme's most-criticized feature.
- The framework is programmatic, not evidential: Wagner states the book "was not written to prove, by evidence, argument, or example, any set of precepts" but to present "a different viewpoint for anthropologists." Its empirical materials (Daribi ethnography, advertising, cargo cults) are illustrations, not tests.
- Some of Wagner's applications rest on uncited generalizations (e.g., ethnolinguistic claims about American Indian, cockney, and Black American speech styles as "expressive" language use) — asserted, not documented.
