# Price of Production

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume 3]]*, Part Two (1864–65 manuscript; published 1894), [[marx-karl|Marx]] defines the **price of production** as cost price plus the share of the annual average profit falling to a capital: price of production = k + k·p′. It answers the problem Marx states openly at the end of Chapter 8: at equal rates of exploitation, capitals of different [[organic-composition-of-capital|organic composition]] would produce different rates of profit if commodities sold at their values — yet real competition shows an equalized average rate, so "the theory of value thus appears incompatible with the actual movement."

Marx's resolution (Ch. 9, the five-capitals example): competition and capital migration redistribute the total social surplus-value among capitals in proportion to their size — capitalists stand to total surplus-value "in the position of shareholders in a joint-stock company." Commodities of above-average composition sell above their values, those below sell below, and Marx argues the divergences cancel: **the sum of prices of production equals the sum of values, and total profit equals total surplus-value**. All theoretical claims attributed to Marx.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Smith's "natural price", Ricardo's "cost of production", Physiocrats' "prix nécessaire" | Same extension, per Marx — but none explained the difference between price of production and value |
| Marx 1894 | Transformed form of value; profit as redistributed surplus-value |
| Bortkiewicz (1907) onward | "Transformation problem" — the charge that Marx transformed outputs but not inputs (→ [[transformation-problem-debate]]) |

## Key Claims (attributed to Marx)

- **Values prior to prices.** Values are "not only theoretically prior to the prices of production, but also historically prior to them" — exchange at values characterizes conditions where producers own their means of production (peasants, handicraft). An Engels footnote adds that Maurer-to-Morgan scholarship established this "opinion" as fact (Engels's judgment).
- **The input acknowledgment.** Marx explicitly notes that the elements of productive capital are themselves bought at prices of production, "so that the profit in one branch of industry goes into the cost price of another," and answers with an aggregation argument. This passage is the hinge of the later [[transformation-problem-debate]].
- **Mystification.** In the price of production, "surplus-value denies its own origin … and becomes unrecognizable": profit appears to spring from capital as such, obliterating the constant/variable distinction — "in competition, everything appears upside down."
- **Class freemasonry.** Because average profit depends on aggregate exploitation, every capitalist has a stake in the exploitation of the whole working class — "a real freemasonry vis-à-vis the working class as a whole."
- **Market value and supply-demand.** Demand and supply explain nothing when they coincide; sale at value is the law of equilibrium "on which divergences have to be explained, and not the converse".

## Operationalizations

The concept is value-theoretic, not statistical. Formalizations begin with Bortkiewicz's simultaneous equations and run through Winternitz, Seton, Sraffian systems, and Shaikh's iterative method — recorded as positions on [[transformation-problem-debate]], not as refinements in wiki voice.

## Applications

- Grounds Volume 3's whole account of the surface forms of surplus-value: commercial profit ([[merchant-capital]]), interest, and [[ground-rent]] are all analyzed as claims on the pool of surplus-value the equalization establishes.
- The framework for Marx's account of surplus profit, of which differential rent is the special case.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The transformation of input values is the most-litigated technical issue in Marxian economics ([[transformation-problem-debate]]): critics from Bortkiewicz to Steedman argue the two aggregate equalities cannot in general hold together; defenses range from Mandel's temporal reading (inputs are prior-period prices of production) to iterative and "new interpretation" solutions. Not adjudicated here.
- Marx himself flags the derivation's circularity risk ("how does this equalization lead to a general rate of profit, since this is evidently a result and cannot be a point of departure?").
