# Discipline (Foucauldian)

"Discipline" in [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s sense names techniques of power that *form* capacities and selves — timetable, drill, surveillance, examination — rather than merely repressing an already-constituted subject (*Discipline and Punish*, 1975). Its most substantial anthropological extension and revision is [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]]'s analysis of medieval Christian monasticism (*Genealogies of Religion*, 1993, chs. 3–4).

## Definition as Coined

Foucault analyzed discipline through the modern prison: punishment shifted, in Asad's summary, "from a ritual of political communication" (sovereign torture displayed on the body) "to a ritual of social production" — an economy of training molding body and soul toward the useful, reformed individual. In his late work Foucault extended the analysis to Christian asceticism as a "technology of the self," reading Cassian's "combat of chastity" as "an entire technique for analysing and diagnosing thought" rather than a code of permitted and forbidden acts.

## Semantic History

Asad shows the word itself carries a longer Christian history. Medieval *disciplina* merged classical Latin usages (military, political, domestic) with the biblical *paideia* — divine education, chastisement, correction — and served as the common term for legally prescribed flogging. In the Rule of St. Benedict, Asad decomposes discipline as (a) divinely oriented knowledge embodied in (b) physical and spiritual practice within (c) an organized community under (d) the absolute authority of an abbot applying (e) corrective measures for attaining virtue.

## Operationalizations

Not a metric. Foucauldian analyses identify discipline through its techniques (timetable, drill, hierarchical observation, normalizing judgment, examination); Asad's monastic application identifies it through programmatic texts (the Rule, custumals, treatises for novices) and the institutional apparatus of formation (the abbot's authority, the Chapter of faults, the *circas*). Both operationalize from prescriptive and institutional records rather than measured behavior — a documented limitation (see Critiques below).

## Asad's extension and critique of Foucault

Asad accepts the formative (non-repressive) conception of power but revises Foucault on three points, each attributed:

1. **The will to obey.** Monastic discipline "explicitly aims to create, through a program of communal living, the will to obey" — obedience experienced as the monk's own virtue, which distinguishes the monastery from Goffman's total institutions and from Weber's definition of power as overcoming resistance, which Asad says "obscures... the conditions within which obedient wills are created."
2. **Against "renunciation of the self."** Where the late Foucault characterized Christian asceticism as renunciation of self and reality, Asad argues the rhetoric of renunciation "is part of the construction of a self-policing function" — not the rejection of a presocialized "real" self. Bernard of Clairvaux's pedagogy *exploited* rather than repressed his noble novices' secular desires, redescribing them through biblical allegory as *materia exercendae virtutis* (material for exercising virtue); the pedagogic relation was "one of authority, not of domination."
3. **No panopticon.** The monastery had "no single point of surveillance" but "an entire network of functions through which watching, testing, learning, teaching, could take place" (the *circas* roundsmen, the daily Chapter of faults); "the Benedictine monk's truthful self was the continuous work of a structured community," not a microcosm of solitude.

Asad also argues penance was "a disciplinary technique for the self to create a desire for obedience to the law... intrinsic to what the self was," and therefore inaccurately described as manipulative "social control"; and that the central monastic principle was that "virtuous desire had first to be created before a virtuous choice could be made" — inverting the modern assumption that choices are self-justifying.

## Applications

Asad's account connects discipline to [[ritual]] (rite as apt performance within a disciplinary program), to [[habitus]] (Mauss's embodied aptitudes as the non-dualist frame for such formation), and to [[religion]] (medieval Christianity implanting dispositions through power — Augustine's *disciplina* — rather than through symbols alone). The Cistercian case supplies a cautionary complication: manual labor was valued as humiliation, not economic instrumentality, yet the lay brothers (*conversi*) who did most of the humble work were excluded from the disciplinary program and "rebelled most often" (Southern 1970, cited by Asad).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's critics (social historians disputing Foucault's datings and continuities; see [[genealogy]]) apply equally to its religious extension. Asad's own account rests on prescriptive sources — rules, custumals, treatises for novices — which document programs rather than compliance; he acknowledges the tension between virtue-formation and law-obedience *within* the medieval concept (Bernard's *De praecepto*: where interpretation of the Rule is disputed, obedience "suppresses a discordant will" rather than expressing a common one).
