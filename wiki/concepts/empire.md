# Empire

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — definitions from Taagepera 1978 and Larsen 1979 as presented by
[[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews]] 2003.)*

**Empire** is defined by Rein Taagepera as "any large sovereign political entity whose
components are not sovereign, irrespective of this entity's internal structure or official
designation," and by Mogens Trolle Larsen as "a supernational system of political control."
A notable fact frames the whole analytic: there was **no native Mesopotamian word for
"empire"** — only terms for countries, lands, and peoples. The Akkadian period (later 3rd
millennium BCE) is conventionally called the "first Mesopotamian empire," though
[[algaze-guillermo|Algaze]] extends the label back to the [[uruk-expansion|Uruk]] phenomenon
(as an [[informal-empire|informal empire]]) and Liverani argues empires existed before Akkad.

## Semantic History

The concept is a modern etic import; Mesopotamian archaeology inherited it from
comparative history and political science and has applied it, with varying caution, from the
Uruk period through the Achaemenid, Seleucid, Parthian, Sasanian, Abbasid, Ottoman, and
British empires that successively ruled the region. [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews]]
(attributed) warns that "the apparent wealth of texts surviving from empires of the
Mesopotamian past has perhaps encouraged … an over-hasty definition and detection of imperial
modes of power" — royal inscriptions are propaganda, and the archaeology of empire in the Old
World, he argues, is "in its infancy" compared with the more exploratory New World tradition
(Inka, Wari).

## Operationalizations

Matthews's central methodological move is a **tripartite table** distinguishing (1)
*anthropological characteristics* of empire (militarism, glorification of the king, tribute,
an integrating ideology), (2) genuine *archaeological correlates* (settlement reorganization,
provincial administrative architecture, production standardization, imperial-core cities),
and (3) *textual correlates*. He criticizes Smith & Montiel's "archaeological criteria" as
actually conflating the first two categories — "not archaeological criteria at all but
anthropological characteristics." The core difficulty, following Postgate, is that "we have
no agreed-on criteria for distinguishing archaeologically between conformity imposed by
external forces and that arising from the voluntary congruence of an integrated cultural
system." A standard sub-distinction (Feinman) separates **territorial** empires (direct,
intrusive control and tribute) from **hegemonic** empires (indirect control through local
elites) — with Steinkeller noting the Ur III empire's core–periphery economics may
exceptionally have been symmetrical.

## Applications

- **The imperial core:** monumental capitals as ideological statements — Sargon II's
  single-generation [[nineveh|Khorsabad]] (Dur-Šarrukin), with throne-room reliefs of the
  king presiding over the torture of rebels ("an unambiguous message"); Sennacherib's
  Nineveh. Matthews stresses (with Liverani) that 19th-century techniques could not excavate
  mud-brick, so the *non-monumental* fabric of imperial capitals — households, stratification,
  ethnic quarters — remains almost unknown.
- **Domination of peripheries:** provincial bureaucracies (Assyrian; Ur III royal-appointee
  governors), extracted commodities (metals, dyes, wine, lapis, horses, slaves), and
  "attached" vs. "independent" craft specialists (Stein & Blackman, Tell Leilan).
- **Frontiers and "shadow empires":** secondary polities stimulated by an adjacent empire —
  Urartu, of which Zimansky wrote "Without the Assyrian empire there would not have been an
  empire of Urartu."
- **Expansion and collapse:** Taagepera's finding that empires are fragile, "invariably
  collapsing after relatively brief periods at near-maximum extent," on a "slow-quick-slow"
  rhythm; Tainter's "declining productivity for investment in complexity"; Liverani's remark
  that Mesopotamians "seem to have been well accustomed to cohabiting with collapse."

## Critiques and Limitations

The category is hard to operationalize archaeologically without smuggling in the textual
(propagandistic) record — Liverani's provocation is that "if we didn't know from the texts
that the Akkad empire really existed, we would not be able to postulate it from the changes
in settlement patterns." Matthews's rejoinder is that the Akkadian empire may belong to
"prehistory, or better ahistory." Environmental-collapse arguments (Weiss on later-3rd-
millennium aridification) should, per the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework, license only
`concurrent_with` absent a stated mechanism. The relation of "empire" to the softer
[[informal-empire|informal-empire]] and [[world-systems-analysis|world-system]] framings for
the earliest cases is exactly the terrain of the [[uruk-world-system-debate]].
