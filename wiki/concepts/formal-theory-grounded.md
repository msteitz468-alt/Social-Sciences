# Formal Theory (Grounded)

## Definition as Coined

[[glaser-barney-g|Glaser]] and [[strauss-anselm-l|Strauss]] (1967) distinguish **formal theory** generated by grounded comparative analysis from both (a) [[substantive-theory|substantive theory]] of one area and (b) speculative/logico-deductive "grand" theory. Formal theory addresses a **conceptual area** of sociology and is preferably developed **from** grounded substantive theory, though they allow direct generation from multi-area data.

Slug `formal-theory-grounded` avoids collision with other uses of "formal theory" and with [[formal-sociology|Simmel's formal sociology]].

## Semantic History

1967 GT contrast. Not Simmelian form analysis or mathematical formalization. Mills's [[grand-theory]] is a polemical foil, not a synonym. G&S put "greater faith in grounded formal theory" than in logico-deductive grand theorizing to close the abstract-theory / miniscule-study gap (Ch. IV).

## Operationalizations

- Scope: conceptual domain vs substantive area.
- Path: usually substantive → formal via multi-area comparison; one-area rewriting alone is inadequate.
- Extension strategy: theoretically sample forgotten comparisons in others' formal theory (e.g., Goffman "cooling the mark out").
- For formal discovery, select dissimilar substantive groups that are conceptually comparable at the formal level (e.g., social isolation across seemingly non-comparable sites).

## Applications

Status-passage formalization from dying as nonscheduled passage; extending published formal theories via theoretical sampling (Ch. IV).

## Critiques and Limitations

How "formal" remains grounded is a standing methodological tension; later GT literature (not ingested) debates premature formalization. Contested relative to grand theory's defenders and relative to those who see all formalization as reification.
