# Network Society

Analytical concept for a social form in which **networks are the predominant morphology** of organization, communication, and power — as developed by [[castells-manuel|Manuel Castells]] in *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* (1996/2010).

## Definition as Coined

Castells contends that around the end of the second millennium, major transformations converged on a new form of society: *the network society*, “made of networks in all the key dimensions of social organization and social practice.” Networks are an old form of organization; what is new is that digital networking technologies enable their endless expansion and reconfiguration. Conclusion of Vol. I: dominant functions and processes in the Information Age are increasingly organized around networks; diffusion of networking logic modifies production, experience, power, and culture. Presence or absence in the network, and dynamics among networks, are critical sources of domination and change. The society is characterized by “the pre-eminence of social morphology over social action.” A network is “a set of interconnected nodes” (nodes vary by network: stock exchanges, media systems, political councils, criminal logistics, etc.). Distance within a network approaches zero; outside it, infinite — inclusion/exclusion becomes structural.

**Power thesis (attributed):** “the power of flows takes precedence over the flows of power.” *Switchers* connecting networks (e.g. finance controlling media that shape politics) are privileged power-holders.

## Semantic History

Distinct from slogan “information society” (Castells prefers *informational* to mark a specific mode of development — see [[informationalism]]). Related to but not identical with post-industrial society (Touraine, Bell — different analytical axis). Differs from purely technical “network analysis” methods. 2010 preface restates the concept after financial crisis (“global automaton”), Web 2.0, and mass self-communication without abandoning the morphology claim.

## Operationalizations

Historical-structural: IT paradigm diffusion; [[network-enterprise]]; global financial and media networks; G-7 occupational change; [[space-of-flows]] / [[space-of-places]]; [[timeless-time]]; [[real-virtuality]]. Not a single survey scale.

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; urban and media sociology; [[globalization]] studies; organizational analysis.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested as grand periodization; risk of morphological metaphor overdetermining agency (addressed in Vols II–III via identity and movements — not yet ingested); Northern empirical base in Vol. I; dual reception as analysis and policy slogan.
