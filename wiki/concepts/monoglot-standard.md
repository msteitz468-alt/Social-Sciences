# Monoglot Standard

The **monoglot standard** is [[silverstein-michael|Silverstein]]’s term for the language-ideological regime in which a single, denotationally standardized language is imagined as the transparent, natural vehicle of a national public and of “correct” thought — erasing or subordinating multilingual and multi-register realities.

## Definition as Coined

Developed in Silverstein’s language-ideology program and elaborated in “Whorfianism and the Linguistic Imagination of Nationality” ([[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000]], ch. 3): a **hegemonic standardized language community** imagined as a pyramid of dialects under one denotational standard — the natural medium of a national public and of “correct” thought. Not merely a code but a **regime** linking grammar, print-public address, and nationalist deixis (“we the people”).

## Semantic History

[[silverstein-michael|Silverstein]] (2000) reads Benedict Anderson’s *Imagined Communities* as itself a **Whorfian construction from within** the monoglot-standard condition: Anderson recognizes language’s centrality to nationalism yet (Silverstein argues) mistakes dialectically produced tropes of homogeneous language for social reality. Structural analogies are drawn between Whorf’s SAE categories of “time” and Anderson’s nationalist “we.” Kroskrity’s intro places the monoglot standard among Western folk theories that reify linguistic homogeneity and erase variation — continuous with Herderian language–nation–state alignments and with “homogenism” (Blommaert & Verschueren, as cited).

## Operationalizations

- Institutional histories of standardization (schools, print capitalism, law, language academies).
- Analysis of political oratory and media as monoglot public address; nationalist **we**-deixis / we-voicing.
- Contrastive cases of dual or plurilingual regimentation (Indonesian standardism + exemplarism; Tongan courtroom; Arizona Tewa purism; Kaluli mission literacy).

## Applications

Silverstein 2000; foil and comparator for Errington (Indonesian), Philips (Tongan), Kroskrity ([[arizona-tewa]]), Schieffelin ([[kaluli]]); Bauman & Briggs on Locke’s purified monoglot public sphere vs Herder’s national language of the *Volk*.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Monoglot” names an **ideological project**, not demographic description — elite bilingualism often thrives inside standard-language nations. Silverstein’s charge that Anderson “buys the trope” is a position within the language-ideology program, not a settled verdict on Anderson studies.
