## Definition as Coined

Charles Ferguson is credited (attributed; cited in this source as Ferguson 1964a, referencing his originating work) with coining "diglossia" to describe a speech community's use of a High (H) variety and a Low (L) variety of the same language in complementary distribution — H reserved for formal, literary, or institutional domains and L for everyday, intimate domains. Ferguson's originating text itself is not present in this ingest; his contribution is recorded here as reported within Gumperz & Hymes (1972).

## Semantic History

Joshua Fishman, in "Domains and the Relationship between Micro- and Macrosociolinguistics" (1972), explicitly extended Ferguson's H/L framework beyond its original scope of two varieties of one language to cases involving two structurally distinct languages — associating H and L varieties with contrasting domain-linked value clusters ("high culture values" versus "intimacy and folksiness"). This extension is Fishman's own theoretical move (attributed), distinct from Ferguson's original bidialectal formulation; a `developed_from` relation to Ferguson's diglossia concept should be recorded once a dedicated Ferguson source or page is ingested. `contested: yes` reflects the definitional stretch itself — whether "diglossia" properly names bilingual as well as bidialectal complementary-distribution cases is Fishman's extension, not a claim already settled in the source material to hand.

## Operationalizations

In this source, diglossia is operationalized indirectly through Fishman's domain analysis: language choice patterns by [[domain-language-choice|domain]] (family, friendship, religion, education, employment) among Puerto Rican bilinguals in New York and New Jersey are read as evidence of H/L-type complementary distribution between Spanish and English, validated via self-report instruments, census factor analysis, and word-naming tasks with schoolchildren (see [[fishman-joshua]]).

## Applications

Fishman's Puerto Rican bilingual studies (Greenfield 1968; Fishman 1968a Jersey City census; Edelman 1968 schoolchildren study) are the applications recorded in this source.

## Critiques and Limitations

No direct critique of Ferguson's original diglossia concept is recorded in this source; the only tension on record is the implicit stretch involved in Fishman's extension from bidialectal to bilingual cases, which the source presents as an extension rather than a disputed claim.
