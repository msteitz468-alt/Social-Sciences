# War of Position

> [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s strategic concept in the State and Civil Society notes of *[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]*. **Attributed voice** throughout. Paired with **war of manoeuvre** (frontal attack / war of movement).

## Definition as Coined

Gramsci transfers military metaphors into political strategy. Modern warfare, he notes, knows war of movement, war of position, and underground warfare; political struggle has analogous forms.

**Load-bearing East/West contrast** (attributed): In the East (Russia), the State was everything and [[civil-society]] was "gelatinous"; a war of manoeuvre (1917) could strike the State directly. In the West, States were only an "outer ditch," behind which stood a sturdy system of fortresses and earthworks — the private organisms of civil society. There, strategy must become a **war of position**: siege warfare over the trenches of consent, culture, parties, churches, unions, press.

Related formulations in the same selection:

- Transition "from the war of manoeuvre (frontal attack) to the war of position in the political field as well."
- Identification (with caveats) of war of position with [[hegemony|civil hegemony]] and with the United Front strategy; "permanent revolution" reworked as something like permanent war of position / civil hegemony after 1870–1917 changes.
- Link to [[passive-revolution]]: passive revolution can *be* a form of war of position from above; Gramsci asks when position might again become manoeuvre.

Gandhi's passive resistance is listed as a war of position; boycotts as position, strikes as (in one typology) movement — illustrations of the metaphor's range, not a finished taxonomy.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Use |
|---|---|
| Gramsci *Quaderni* | Military analogy → Western revolutionary strategy; civil-society trenches |
| Postwar PCI / Eurocommunism | Legitimation of long cultural-political struggle vs insurrectionism |
| Cultural studies | Struggle over meaning, media, common sense as "positional" |
| Neo-Gramscian IR | World-order hegemony as positional struggle among historic blocs |

Reception sometimes equates war of position with purely cultural politics and erases the State/coercion pole — against Gramsci's formula that State = political society + civil society, "[[hegemony]] protected by the armour of coercion."

## Operationalizations

- **Trench-system audit:** density and complexity of civil-society organisms as measure of how "positional" struggle must be.
- **East/West (historical):** Russia 1917 vs advanced capitalist West of interwar years — a historical contrast, not a timeless geography.
- **Relation to [[organic-crisis]]:** when consent collapses, coercive-only domination may open space; still not automatic manoeuvre victory.
- **Relation to [[passive-revolution]]:** molecular/transformist absorption as ruling-class positional strategy.

## Applications

- Gramsci's rethinking of Western socialist strategy after the failure of post-WWI revolutionary wave and under fascism (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971]]*).
- Later analyses of media, education, and cultural institutions as hegemonic "trenches."
- Cautionary use: not a warrant for indefinite postponement of structural conflict, nor for reducing politics to discourse alone.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Military metaphor can reify strategy and under-specify agency, organisation, and timing.
- Two somewhat different "wars of position" in the notebooks (editors flag internal tension: position as epochal form after 1870 vs position as civil-society siege) — do not force a single schematic.
- Eurocommunist reception accused (by rivals) of quietism; insurrectionist reception accused of ignoring civil society — both are positions about Gramsci, not notebook lemmas.
- Source: 1971 English selection.
