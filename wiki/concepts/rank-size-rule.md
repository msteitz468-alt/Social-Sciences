# Rank-Size Rule (settlement systems)

## Definition as Coined

A descriptive regularity from urban geography: in many national city systems, the population of the *n*th city approximates the largest city's population divided by *n* (Zipf). Archaeologists adapt the plot to **site areas** as population proxies to characterize regional settlement systems (primate capital vs more continuous hierarchy).

## Semantic History

Moved from modern urban geography into archaeological survey analysis as regional datasets grew. [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] deploys rank-size and size-class figures extensively in *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]* (1981) for Uruk through Sasanian landscapes.

## Operationalizations

- Log–log rank vs site area plots by ceramic period.
- Comparison across periods to track integration, fragmentation, or primacy.

## Applications

Mesopotamian alluvial surveys (Adams 1981 figures; study [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]):

- **Uruk period south:** primate Uruk throughout; more pronounced early.
- **Uruk period north:** Early–Middle "flattop"/convex (Skinner: imperfect integration) → Late approaches log-normal.
- **ED I:** combined plot shows Uruk primacy; may understate late primacy after hinterland collapse.
- **Sasanian / late periods:** rank-size often convex/"flattop" rather than single-primate classic Zipf.
- Crumley primate→rank-size urbanization sequence fits Uruk hinterlands poorly for the early northern flattop (Adams: possible early bifurcation of urban function).

## Critiques and Limitations

Site area is a noisy population proxy; incomplete survey and dating error warp ranks; Zipf's rule is empirical, not a law of political structure — interpretive leap from plot shape to polity type must stay attributed.
