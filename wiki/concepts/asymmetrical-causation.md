# Asymmetrical Causation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]* (1985, ch. 4; revisited ch. 9), [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] argued (attributed) that most empirical social research implicitly assumes **symmetrical (reversible)** causation: a given value of X has the same implication for Y regardless of whether X rose or fell to that value. **Asymmetrical (irreversible / direction-dependent)** causation violates that assumption — increasing X from 10 to 20 need not have the same consequence for Y as decreasing X from 30 to 20.

He insisted this distinction is **not** Duncan's recursive vs. nonrecursive distinction (whether causal arrows run one way or allow reciprocal feedback). Symmetry concerns the **history of how a variable reached its present level**, not the topology of a path diagram.

Chart 4.1 (attributed summary):

| Form | Pattern |
|---|---|
| **Reversible / symmetrical** | Shift X up, Y responds; return X to prior level, Y returns |
| **Irreversible / asymmetrical** | Shift X up, Y responds; return X, Y does **not** fully return (path dependence / ratchet) |
| **Partial irreversibility** | Asymmetry of magnitude even over the same X range |

## Semantic History

Lieberson presented the distinction as largely neglected in the quantitative toolkit of the early 1980s, which estimated associations "as if" direction of approach to X did not matter. Related ideas appear elsewhere under other labels — hysteresis, path dependence, ratchet effects, QCA's causal asymmetry via De Morgan (causes of presence ≠ causes of absence; see [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]]) — but Lieberson's framing is specifically a critique of **standard statistical practice's symmetry assumption**, not a set-theoretic program.

In Part II he extended the point: theories and data practices are biased toward detecting reversible relations because they are easier to experience and model; spectacular one-way historical causes (wars, institutional founding events) and uniform background processes both get mishandled when symmetry is assumed by default.

## Operationalizations

- **Process vs. event**: irreversible causation is often event-like (a threshold crossed, a stock accumulated) rather than a smooth reversible process.
- **Three types of irreversible causes** (ch. 4, attributed): (1) relatively constant social forces that push one way (analogues: gravity; selectivity as a standing force; illustrative citations to Simmel's *tertius gaudens*, Malthusian constraints, Treiman on prestige invariants, Marxist structural determination — listed as *types*, not endorsed findings); (2) causes that create conditions with "lives of their own" after the originating force recedes; (3) forms that produce **ceteris paribus error** when analysts hold "other things equal" across irreversible transitions.
- **English-language formalization** (Figs. 4.1–4.2 in the book): historical sequences of language shift and institutionalization as worked illustrations of irreversibility.
- **Multivariate implication**: control-variable and regression practice that estimates a single coefficient for X on Y silently averages over (or ignores) direction-of-change heterogeneity.

## Applications

Lieberson's applied warning (attributed): policy that tries to **reverse a cause** to reverse an effect fails when the original linkage was asymmetrical — undoing X does not undo Y. Desegregation, language policy, and status attainment illustrations in the book are design/logic examples. Cross-link: [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]]'s asymmetry (presence vs. absence equations) is a different formalization of a related intuition; do not equate the two without argument.

## Critiques and Limitations

The distinction is easy to over-apply: many social relations may be approximately symmetrical over short ranges; irreversible cases need independent historical or longitudinal evidence. Lieberson's 1985 toolkit did not supply a full estimation technology for asymmetry (later work on hysteresis, dynamic panels, and set-theoretic methods partly fills that gap). The concept is a **design and theory demand**, not a single statistical test.
