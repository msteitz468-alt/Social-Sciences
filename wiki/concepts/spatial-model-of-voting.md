# Spatial Model of Voting

## Definition as Coined

The **spatial model of voting** represents political competition geometrically:
voters and candidates occupy positions in an issue space, voters prefer the closer
candidate, and vote-maximizing candidates adjust position accordingly. In one
dimension with single-peaked preferences the **median voter theorem** (Black 1948;
Downs 1957, from Hotelling 1929) predicts two office-seeking candidates converge to
the median voter's position. Multidimensional and multi-candidate extensions
generally lack equilibria (see [[social-choice]]).

## Semantic History

Hotelling (1929) analogized political competition to two shops on a Main Street, each
edging toward the center. Downs (1957) built the fuller account — including uncertainty,
abstention, and ambiguous platforms — usually reduced by later authors to the bare
median-voter convergence result. From the 1970s the field moved to **probabilistic
voting** models (Hinich, Ledyard, and Ordeshook 1972; Enelow and Hinich 1982, 1984),
in which nonpolicy or random factors give even a less-proximate candidate a nonzero
win probability, and the equilibrium (when it exists) can sit at the *mean* rather than
median voter. A parallel strand introduced **policy-motivated candidates** (Wittman
1983, 1990; Calvert 1985; Cox 1984), for whom mixed office-and-policy motives predict
divergence proportional to the weight placed on policy.

## Operationalizations

Voters and candidates as points in a Euclidean issue space; the median voter theorem
requires (Ordeshook 1992) that candidates know the distribution of preferences, voters
know candidate strategies, preferences be single-peaked, and no constraints bind
candidate strategy. Multidimensional competition generically lacks an equilibrium (the
cycling result, see [[social-choice]]); the **uncovered set** (Miller 1980; McKelvey
1986) is one bounded-region successor prediction, though it is hard to identify
empirically even for small electorates.

## Applications

Critiqued at length in *[[green-shapiro-pathologies-1994|Pathologies of Rational Choice
Theory]]* (ch. 7). [[green-donald|Green]] and [[shapiro-ian|Shapiro]] argue (attributed)
that the spatial literature is "almost devoid of empirical content" (quoting the
rational choice theorist Wittman 1983): a theory is advanced with casually adduced
confirming evidence, a "stylized fact" (first convergence, later divergence) casts
doubt on it, and "a torrent of post hoc theorizing" absorbs the anomaly — the cycle
repeating as game-theoretic fashions change. They note U.S. presidential elections show
convergence (Nixon–Kennedy), divergence (Goldwater–Johnson, Reagan–Mondale), and
destabilizing new-issue moves, so no single equilibrium prediction fits. They read
Riker and Ordeshook's (1973) catalogue of "excuses" for non-convergence (imperfect
information, mobility constraints, primary commitments, ideological prejudice, or
simply that "candidates err") as paradigmatic [[method-driven-research|theory-rescue]].
Their constructive proposal is to test effects of *exogenous* shifts in the strategic
environment (e.g., changes in officeholder salary) with panel/control-group designs
rather than case-by-case narrative interpretation.

## Critiques and Limitations

The core objections in the ingested critique: multidimensional non-existence of
equilibria; the arbitrariness of what counts as a policy vs. a "nonpolicy" (stochastic)
determinant of the vote, when campaign strategy plausibly shapes candidate image; the
non-nested proliferation of divergence mechanisms with no empirical basis for choosing
among them; and the difficulty of ever statistically identifying models resting on
unobservables (risk aversion, discount rates, beliefs). Defenders hold these are the
normal idealizations of a maturing formal science (see
[[rational-choice-controversy-debate]]).
