# Cultural Schema (Ortner)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* (1989), [[ortner-sherry|Sherry Ortner]] treats **cultural schemas** as organized scenarios for enacting culturally typical relations and situations—transferable across contexts and, crucially, across historical time. She roots the term in her earlier concept of **“key scenarios”** (Ortner 1973a, from a 1970 dissertation): preorganized schemes of action, symbolic programs for staging and playing out standard social interactions. Cultures, she argued, contain not only symbols and ideologies but *schemas for enacting* typical relations and situations (*proposed_by:* [[ortner-sherry]]; *developed_from:* key-scenario work; *part_of:* [[practice-theory]]).

For *High Religion* specifically, Ortner defined cultural schemas as **plot structures that recur throughout many cultural stories and rituals**, depicting actors responding to cultural **contradictions** in appropriate, even “heroic,” ways. Structure, she insisted, comes with emotional and moral configurations, not just abstract ordering principles. Schemas and structural contradictions together constitute, in her formulation, a **hegemony**—a mutually sustaining universe of social experience and symbolic representation.

Related but **not identical**: [[transposable-schemas|Sewell’s schemas]] (virtual procedures paired with resources in a dual theory of structure). Ortner’s schemas are narrative/ritual plots that resolve contradictions for actors; Sewell’s are more abstract, virtual procedures enabling resource mobilization. Cross-link without equating (*contrasts_with* / *associated_with:* [[transposable-schemas]]).

## Semantic History

**Hospitality key scenario (Ortner 1970).** Ortner’s first Sherpa application showed a hospitality scenario structuring ordinary socializing, pressure transactions for favors, shamanistic seances, and offerings to gods—Ego as host, Alter as guest(s), food and drink as if at a party. She argued such schemas take on an ordering function with generality and **transferability** across disparate situations, endowing meanings and setting interactions along more or less predictable lines.

**Parallel formulations.** Ortner placed her idea alongside Edward Schieffelin’s “cultural scenario” of reciprocity and opposition among the Kaluli (*The Sorrow of the Lonely and the Burning of the Dancers*, 1976) and Victor Turner’s “root paradigms” (e.g. martyrdom in Christianity) in *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors* (1974).

**Historical turn — Geertz and Sahlins.** Ortner recorded that schema-like ideas resurfaced in historical anthropology: [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]’s *Negara* (1980) argued the Balinese state form reconstructed itself over nearly six centuries as “further transcriptions of a fixed ideal”; [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]’s *Historical Metaphors* (1981) argued a cultural script embodied in myth and ritual ordered Hawaiian dealings with Cook via the Makahiki sequence. Ortner claimed neither Geertz nor Sahlins adequately explained **why schemas endure**.

**Durability via contradiction.** Ortner’s distinctive theoretical move: cultural schemas endure because they depict actors responding to and resolving, from their own point of view, the **central contradictions of the culture**. In the Sherpa case, a schema portrays a hero playing out and “winning” the fraternal and political rivalries recurrently reproduced from the egalitarian–hierarchical contradiction (*supports:* [[practice-theory]]; *applies:* Sherpa temple and monastery history).

**Theoretical inspirations kept separate.** For schema “meaning,” Ortner cited Lévi-Strauss (myths mediate cultural contradictions) and structural Marxists (mediation legitimates/reproduces order) but insisted the two points should stay separate because mediations are **politically ambiguous**—they can legitimate [[big-people-small-people|big people]] or encourage challenge by the small, depending on reader and circumstance.

## Operationalizations

**The Zhung seven-step schema.** From the Zhung temple-founding tale (Dorje Zangbu vs. pembu Lama Gombu) and the Pangboche cycle (Lama Sangwa Dorje), Ortner extracted a canonical sequence:

1. Political and/or **fraternal rivalry** opens (stakes: tax rights, “name,” reputation).
2. Struggle; the rival gains the upper hand (often violently).
3. Hero **departs** for remote places and acquires a powerful **protector** (high lama, sacred idol, rich mother’s brother, tutelary deity).
4. Returns and, with the protector’s aid, **defeats** the rival.
5. **Acquires** the rival’s subjects/clients.
6. Rival is humiliated and **leaves** the area permanently (pembu as person not office—defeat undoes the whole self).
7. Hero **founds a temple**—act of great virtue that crystallizes hierarchical protector–follower relations and hereditary congregation support.

**Ritual mapping.** Ortner argued *kurim* offering rituals embody the same schema with minor transforms: people–demon rivalry; gods as protectors; violent defeat of demon effigies; demons forced to leave (cannot be annihilated). The altar is a temporary temple on mandala plan—each performance a symbolic temple founding. Temple founding and offering stand as mutual metaphors: founding as vast continuous offering; ritual as tiny temple founding.

**Grounding in practice.** Schema reproduction, she argued, is privileged in frequent, materially consequential, emotionally heightened **rituals** over mere storytelling; also grounded in fraternal inheritance practices (equal shares → fragmentation → departure of discontented brothers) and do-it-yourself pembu succession seeking protectors (later, the Nepal state).

**Historical reenactment readings.** Ortner mapped twentieth-century monastery sponsors onto the schema: Karma’s rivalry with Sangye → departure to meditation → Lama Gulu/Zatul as protectors → Tengboche as triumph; Sangye’s Darjeeling departure → Rana protectors → defeat of Karma → Chiwong founding (possibly intentional reenactment of Dorje Zangbu at the Chiwong cliff). She stressed “enacting the schema” is **not cultural programming** but actors finding pragmatic and/or emotionally meaningful fit between circumstances and cultural stories (*applies:* [[agency]]).

**Soft / hard / middle positions.** Ortner mapped three views of how schemas structure: soft/external (models/resources for rational interest); hard/internal (embedded identity—“do the cultural thing”); **middle** (her preference)—actors have varied motives, yet others and some participants interpret events *as if* following the schema, whereupon the schema enters and shapes the event.

## Applications

- **Sherpa temple foundings** (late 17th–early 18th c.): Pangboche, Zhung as *mythes de référence* and schema source texts (*studied:* [[ortner-high-religion-study]]).
- **State entanglement:** Ortner argued Sherpa connection to the Nepal state after early temples was partly generated by the protector schema—claimants calling the king as “protector,” then events escaping native intentions (*structure of the conjuncture* with Sahlins).
- **Celibate monastery foundings (1916–1925):** big-people legitimation (prestige *and* merit); small-people empowerment via Darjeeling wage labor read as schema of departure/return; “little big people” (monks/nuns) using Rumbu vows analogously (*see* [[big-people-small-people]]).
- **Merit and power mediation:** Schema constructs as noncontradictory that temple founding is both egotistical triumph and religious merit—one must be small as well as big, big as well as small; submission to a protector is the key mediation of the egalitarianism–hierarchy contradiction.
- **Practice-theory apparatus:** One of two “active” notions of structure in *High Religion* (the other: structural contradictions), mutually constituting a hegemony (*part_of:* [[practice-theory]]; *associated_with:* [[habitus]] as parallel dynamic structure image).

In *[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-1999|Life and Death on Mt. Everest]]* (1999), schema-like cultural material continues in founding **tales** (Appendix A: Nupki Gyelwu, Gelungma Palma, Dü Mother) and in expedition ritual programs (base-camp puja; non-killing “clean mountain” campaigns), now under the broader [[serious-games]] frame rather than as the book’s central named apparatus (*studied_by:* [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

1. **Emic vs. analyst’s extraction.** The seven-step sequence is Ortner’s analytic extraction from multi-version folklore; alternative versions (Oppitz; fraternal vs. political catalysts) show the tales are not a single fixed script. Pangboche chronology is fuzzy partly because Sangwa Dorje-as-political-loser conflicts with hero-as-temple-founder.
2. **Politically ambiguous hegemony.** Ortner herself insisted the schema can legitimate dominance *or* authorize challenge—tactical polyvalence of discourse (Foucault). Treating schema as only elite ideology would misread small-people participation.
3. **Not Sewell’s schemas.** Do not merge with [[transposable-schemas]]; different theoretical jobs (narrative contradiction-resolution vs. structure-as-schemas-plus-resources).
4. **Evidence uneven for “enactment.”** Small-people intentions for monastery foundings are, Ortner admitted, fragmentary and “very tentative”; schema readings of founders can overfit after the fact. She distinguished intentional reenactment (possible for Sangye) from looser cultural logic experienced piecemeal.
5. **Speculative early history.** Schemas drawn from undatable folklore used for both historical reconstruction and structural analysis; Ortner noted reconstructions may be wrong while structural analyses remain useful for more recent conditions.
6. **Durability thesis is contested terrain.** Explaining schema durability via contradiction-resolution is Ortner’s claim against Geertz/Sahlins’s relative silence—not a settled consensus in anthropology.
