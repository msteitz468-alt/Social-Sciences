# Multiple Sovereignty

## Definition as Coined

Tilly develops Trotsky's dual power and Amann's power-bloc definition, dropping Trotsky's single-class and two-bloc restrictions (with Moore on coalitions). **Multiple sovereignty** is then the identifying feature of revolutionary situations:

> "A revolutionary situation begins when a government previously under the control of a single, sovereign polity becomes the object of effective, competing, mutually exclusive claims on the part of two or more distinct polities. It ends when a single sovereign polity regains control over the government."

Four paths: annexation-like subordination of one polity by another; subordinate polity asserts sovereignty (rebellion/colonial revolution); nonmembers seize apparatus (rare pure form); **fragmentation** of an existing polity (usual, often via member–nonmember coalitions). Onset recognized when previously acquiescent subjects **obey** the alternative body (taxes, men, symbols) despite the old government's prohibitions. A **revolutionary outcome** is displacement of one set of polity members by another — situations without outcomes are common; medium-run significant transfer without multiple sovereignty is impossible or unlikely (attributed).

## Semantic History

Becomes standard in political-conflict theories of revolution; Skocpol adopts organization/resources from Tilly but rejects reducing revolution to contender mobilization alone without state breakdown structure.

## Operationalizations

Dual power, competing tax/military control, alternative governments recognized by parts of the population.

## Applications

Comparing revolutions, rebellions, and civil wars; linking mobilization models to regime crises.

## Critiques and Limitations

Threshold of "substantial" control is fuzzy; international war and state collapse (Skocpol) may produce multiple sovereignty without prior mass mobilization.
