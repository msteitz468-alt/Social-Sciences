# Cold and Hot Societies

## Definition as Coined

**Cold and hot societies** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] distinction (*[[levi-strauss-savage-mind-1962|The Savage Mind]]*, 1962; earlier in his conversations with Charbonnier) proposed to replace "the clumsy distinction between 'peoples without history' and others." **Cold** societies "seek, by the institutions they give themselves, to annul the possible effects of historical factors on their equilibrium and continuity in a quasi-automatic fashion"; **hot** societies "resolutely internalize the historical process and make it the moving power of their development."

## Semantic History

The contrast is a corrective, not a denial of history: cold societies "do not deny that all societies are in history and change" — "that is patent" — but they treat history "as a form without content," seeking to keep prior states "as permanent as possible" through ritual repetition ("there is indeed a before and an after, but their sole significance lies in reflecting each other"). The Northern Aranda (via T. G. H. Strehlow) illustrate the cold pole: present activities are held to replicate exactly what totemic ancestors did. The distinction is bound to Lévi-Strauss's claim that History is one classificatory code among others (like [[totemism]]), not a privileged medium — the pivot of his polemic against Sartre (see [[levi-strauss-sartre-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Marked by whether a society's central institutions absorb or resist change: myth, ritual, and totemic classification (which "incorporate history... as a form without content") mark the cold pole; historical consciousness and cumulative development mark the hot pole. The types are ideal-typical ends of a continuum, not a binary sorting of real societies.

## Applications

A widely cited frame for the anthropology of time, memory, and historicity, and for debates on whether "traditional" societies are static (they are not — they choose to *treat* change differently). It informs Lévi-Strauss's reading of [[totemism]] as a-historical classification and his critique of history's privileged status.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics (including historians and later anthropologists such as those in the historicity/temporality debates) argue no society is truly "cold": all change, and the appearance of stasis is itself a historical product; the contrast can romanticize or freeze the peoples it labels "cold" (the ethnographic-present trap). Record it as an analytic ideal type of [[structuralism]], attributed to Lévi-Strauss, useful as a contrast of *orientations to change*, not a factual typology of societies.
