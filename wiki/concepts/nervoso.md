# Nervoso (nerves) as idiom of distress

## Definition as Coined

*Nervos* / *nervoso* / *doença de nervos* ("nerves," "nervousness," "nervous sickness") is, in
[[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s analysis in *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death
Without Weeping]]* (Chapter 5), the master **idiom of distress** on the [[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do
Cruzeiro]] — "a rich folk conceptual scheme for describing relations among mind, body, and social
body." Its symptoms (trembling, weakness/*fraqueza*, dizziness, fainting, *ataques de nervos*) overlap,
she argues, with the physiological signs of chronic hunger; her key (attributed) thesis is that through
**[[medicalization]]**, *nervos* — the folk successor to the older **[[delirio-de-fome|delírio de fome]]**
("madness of hunger") — is "appropriated by medicine and transformed into… a biomedical disease that
alienates mind from body and that conceals the social relations of sickness."

## Semantic History

*Nervos* belongs to a wide Mediterranean and Latin American family of "nerves" complaints (*nervios*,
*nevra*, "bad nerves," "falling out"), plausibly rooted in Greek humoral pathology and usually analyzed
as somatized stress from domestic or work relations (D. Davis; Low; Lock & Dunk). Scheper-Hughes's
original move is to read the specifically *hungry* body beneath the nervous one, while insisting the two
are not simply identical — "*nervos* and *fome* are quite distinct in the minds of the people of the
Alto." She documents a historical shift over her fieldwork years: in the mid-1960s people spoke openly
of "fainting from hunger"; by the 1980s they described the same collapse as *fraqueza* or *nervos* —
hunger had become, like racism, a "disallowed discourse."

## Operationalizations

Residents distinguished several types — *nervos de trabalhar muito* (overwork nerves), *nervos de sofrer
muito* (sufferers' nerves, from shock or the killing of kin), "anger nerves," "fear nerves." Treatment
flowed through Bom Jesus's dozen-plus pharmacies and its expanding clinics: in one 1987 clinic morning
Scheper-Hughes counted 12 of 23 patients presenting *nervos*, 9 of them prescribed a tranquilizer or
sleeping pill. The folk phenomenology mapped *força*/*fraqueza* (strength/weakness), *corpo*/*cabeça*
(body/mind), and *ricos*/*pobres* onto a metaphor of "life as a *luta* [struggle]."

## Applications

- **Sickness as oblique protest.** Drawing on Marx (against Parsons), she reads *nervos* as "an act of
  refusal… the covert language of the organs" — men who "lie down" and cannot work enact "a body
  language… a form of surrender… also a drama of mockery and refusal." The **[[the-mindful-body|three
  bodies]]** framework links the nervous body-self, the social body, and a "nervous" body politic.
- **Political safety valve.** Given the crushed history of the Peasant Leagues, she argues *nervos*
  offers a "safe" idiom for anger — one endorsed by the health system, pharmaceutical industry, and
  local political machine, and so a "bad faith" defense of the social order.

## Critiques and Limitations

Scheper-Hughes explicitly breaks with the **somatization** literature (the Kleinmans), which treats
bodily-idiom distress as a "maladaptive… primitive defense mechanism"; she seeks instead to "politicize
the uses of the body." The reading is thus a committed one: whether *nervos* is best understood as
concealed hunger, as somatized social suffering, or as an autonomous folk-medical category with its own
logic remains a matter of interpretation, and her hunger-centred account is offered in attributed voice.
