# Authorized Heritage Discourse (AHD)

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* 2006.)* The **Authorized Heritage Discourse** is the dominant Western professional and institutional way of talking about heritage that:

1. Equates heritage with **old, monumental, aesthetically pleasing material fabric** (tangible "things" and sites).
2. Treats cultural value as **inherent** in that fabric (time-depth, monumentality, aesthetics, scientific significance).
3. Privileges **expert knowledge** (archaeology, architecture, art history, conservation science) as the legitimate voice that identifies, ranks, and manages heritage.
4. Promotes a **consensual, often national**, view of the past and present — downplaying conflict, subaltern experience, and living cultural process.

Core definitional quote (Smith): the AHD “privileges monumentality and grand scale, innate artefact/site significance tied to time depth, scientific/aesthetic expert judgement, social consensus and nation building.” Focus statement: aesthetically pleasing material objects/sites/landscapes that current generations “must” care for, protect, and revere so they may be passed to nebulous future generations for “education” and to forge common identity based on the past.

Smith's load-bearing gambit: **“There is, really, no such thing as heritage”** as a natural inventory of the usual monumental suspects — a hegemonic discourse constitutes how we think, talk, and write about heritage. The discourse naturalizes “rounding up the usual suspects” and promotes Western elite cultural values as universally applicable while obscuring the cultural work heritage actually does.

## Semantic History

- **Nineteenth-century roots (as Smith periodizes):** Ruskin, Morris, SPAB; pastoral expert care; European upper-middle/ruling-class aesthetic and national experience. Architecture and archaeology claim professional expertise and stewardship roles.
- **Twentieth-century institutionalization:** national monument legislation; National Trust and amenity societies; Venice Charter (1964) as “canonical text of modern” heritage practice; World Heritage Convention; national CRM / AHM / CHM regimes dominated by archaeologists and conservation architects.
- **Australia:** Ruskinian fabric philosophy imported into **Burra Charter** 1979 (1999 rewrite retains fabric/expert trusteeship while opening “cultural significance” language). 1970s green bans / NSW Heritage Act show aesthetic fabric victories that can still defeat community/housing values.
- Pre-2006 critical literature (Hewison, Wright, Lowenthal, museum studies) attacked the “heritage industry” for commodification and reactionary nostalgia; Smith argues that critique often **shares conceptual space** with the AHD (heritage still imagined as fabric + authenticity problem). English Heritage rebrands toward “historic environment.”
- **2006:** *Uses of Heritage* coins/stabilizes **AHD** as the analytic target of [[critical-heritage-studies]].
- Counter-discourses: **subaltern and dissenting** heritage discourses (labour, community, Indigenous) that redefine heritage as process, memory, performance, and **control**.

## Operationalizations

| Mechanism | What the AHD does (Smith, attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Things / “the past”** | Materializes heritage as fabric; “the past” as definite, expert domain; disconnects heritage from present values |
| **Innate value** | Aesthetic/scientific significance locked in fabric; multi-vocality obscured |
| **Expert speakers** | Identifies who may speak; experts not mere stakeholders — set agendas and epistemological frames |
| **Site-bounding** | Reduces wider social debates to bite-sized site management/ownership contests ([[heritage-management]] as depoliticizing technology) |
| **Consensus / nation** | Common identity, social stability; Billig-style banal nationalism via leisure tourism |
| **Passive public** | Visitors cast as recipients of education/care rather than active meaning-makers |
| **Inclusion-as-assimilation** | Excluded groups “invited” to learn authorized values rather than redefine heritage |

**Charter carriers (Ch. 3):** Venice Charter (conserve as found; civilization language; expert fellowship); World Heritage Convention (universality; natural/cultural hierarchy; List imbalance; property framing); Burra Charter (values/cultural significance openings + residual fabric/expert AHD); UNESCO intangible instruments (openings that still negotiate listing/universality logics).

**Visitor/case performances:** English country-house visiting as “knowing your place” / banality of grandiloquence; Australian landscape myths at [[riversleigh|Riversleigh]]; industrial museums and [[castleford|Castleford]] as subaltern counter-performances; Indigenous control struggles where AHD naturalizes non-Indigenous identity politics and marks Indigenous claims as “political.”

## Applications

- English country houses (Smith 2004 survey, n=454): majority middle-class identity work within AHD; ~21% want servants/slavery/workers interpretation; minority dissonance.
- Industrial museums (Beamish, NCMM, Tolpuddle; n=273): labour heritage as remembering/commemoration against AHD.
- Riversleigh WHS vs [[waanyi|Waanyi]] country; Castleford community heritage after deindustrialization.
- Indigenous repatriation and management control (Kennewick, Lake Mungo examples as Smith deploys them).

## Critiques and Limitations

Smith herself notes the AHD characterization is strong, historically situated (Western elite origins), and can underplay internal expert dissent and mutability over time and space. The concept is a critical analytic, not a census of all practitioners. Empirical reach in the 2006 book is multi-sited qualitative (UK, Australia, Indigenous politics US/Australia), not a global survey — treat generalizations as **program claims**, attributed. Later critical heritage studies both extends and debates the AHD formulation (secondary literature not yet ingested).
