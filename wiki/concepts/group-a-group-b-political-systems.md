# Group A / Group B Political Systems

> Introduced by [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] in the Introduction to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940). Study: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].

## Definition as Coined

Fortes and Evans-Pritchard argued that the eight political systems in the volume fall into **two main categories**:

| | **Group A** | **Group B** |
|---|---|---|
| **Institutions** | Centralized authority, administrative machinery, and judicial institutions — **a government** | Lack centralized authority, administrative machinery, and constituted judicial institutions — **lack government** |
| **Stratification** | Cleavages of wealth, privilege, and status correspond to the distribution of power and authority | No sharp divisions of rank, status, or wealth |
| **Membership in APS** | [[zulu]], [[ngwato]], [[bemba]], [[banyankole]], [[kede]] | Logoli (see [[logoli-and-vugusu]]), [[tallensi]], [[nuer]] |
| **State language** | Those who define the state by governmental institutions will call these **primitive states** | **Stateless societies** |

They treated the dichotomy as an organizing sample typology for comparative description, not as an exhaustive taxonomy of all African polities. They noted that those who studied Group A described governmental organization (kings, classes, officials, tax/tribute, checks on authority), while those who studied Group B had to ask what, in the **absence of explicit government**, constitutes political structure — simplest among the Nuer (clear territorial divisions), harder among Logoli and Tallensi (less clear spatial political units).

## Semantic History

- **1940 charter:** APS Introduction fixes Group A/B as the British structural-functional comparative frame for African politics.
- **Postwar textbookization:** The typology organized a generation of Africanist teaching and research; “primitive state” vs “stateless society” became standard labels (often looser than the editors’ criteria).
- **Later critique:** Segmentary-lineage and acephalous models were reanalyzed and contested (see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]); the binary was criticized for underplaying intermediate forms, colonial production of “chiefs,” conquest stratification complexity, and the editors’ functional equilibrium assumptions. Those critiques are largely **outside** the 1940 volume; record them as later positions, not as APS findings.

## Operationalizations

Editors operationalized by **institutional presence/absence** (centralized authority, administration, courts) plus **alignment of stratification with power** (A) or its absence (B). Supporting contrasts in the same Introduction:

- **Lineage vs administration:** In Group B the **lineage system** (permanent unilateral descent groups) supplies corporate political units; in Group A **administrative organization** primarily regulates political relations, with lineages secondary.
- **Force:** Group A — organized force concentrated at the apex; Group B — force balanced among homologous segments; jural order rests on **self-help**.
- **Colonial transformation:** Opposite directional effects under European rule (A: paramount becomes pivot/agent; B: balancing segments collapse toward bureaucratic centralization).

[[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]’s preface defined political organization more generally as the aspect of total organization concerned with **control and regulation of physical force** — a definition that covers both types without requiring the state fiction.

## Applications

- **Comparative charter** for [[political-anthropology]] in the British mid-century.
- Case anchors: Group A essays (Gluckman Zulu; Schapera Ngwato; Richards Bemba; Oberg Ankole; Nadel Kede); Group B essays (Wagner Logoli/Vugusu; Fortes Tallensi; Evans-Pritchard Nuer condensation).
- Concepts that travel with the typology: [[ordered-anarchy]], [[segmentary-lineage-system]], [[complementary-opposition]], [[mystical-values-of-office]], [[chieftainship]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Binary compression:** Intermediate and hybrid forms (ritual chiefship without monopoly of force; colonial “chiefs” imposed on segmentary systems) sit uneasily in A/B.
- **Sample, not census:** Editors themselves said not every African type is represented.
- **Colonial frame:** Produced under Indirect Rule interests; editors explicitly hoped the book would inform administration.
- **Later ethnographic and theoretical reanalyses** of segmentary models and African states — see study Critiques section and [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]].
