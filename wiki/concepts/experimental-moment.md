# Experimental Moment

Marcus and Fischer's periodizing label for a late-twentieth-century **experimental moment** in the human sciences, focused for anthropology on ethnographic writing (*[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique]]*, 1986 — subtitle: *An Experimental Moment in the Human Sciences*).

## Definition as Coined

They hold that hyperawareness of the limits of traditional theories of society, culture, and reality — analogous in some respects to experimentation in the 1920s–30s — makes ethnography a crucible for theoretical play, reconceptualization, and rebuilding social theory. The Concluding Note states that the acutely felt problem of description makes the present generally an **ethnographic moment in the human sciences**, while within anthropology what ethnography is/can/should be is explored self-consciously — an **experimental moment**. They take the positive view against those who see intellectual chaos: "In periods when fields are without secure foundations, practice becomes the engine of innovation." They argue against those who would **precipitously close** the experimental era.

## Semantic History

Overlaps with "experimental ethnography," [[post-modern-ethnography]], and the *[[writing-culture|Writing Culture]]* textual moment; ACC stresses both form *and* content experiments (person/self/emotions; political economy) plus cultural-critique repatriation, not textualism alone.

## Operationalizations

Catalogues of experimental monographs; formal devices (dialogism, polyphony, modernist framing); content shifts (self, emotion, world system); domestic critical projects.

## Applications

Organizes ACC's historical sweep and concluding caution; period tag for Critical-Turn anthropology pages.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether a coherent "moment" existed, whether it ended, and whether experimentation improved knowledge remain contested ([[writing-culture-debate]]).
