# Deference and Demeanor

**Deference** (the ceremonial regard one shows another) and **demeanor** (the bearing through which one presents oneself as a certain kind of person) are a paired concept coined by [[goffman-erving|Erving Goffman]] ("The Nature of Deference and Demeanor," 1956). In *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975), Chapter 4, [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]] and **Joan Annett** give the pair a **comparative-historical** treatment that Goffman's ahistorical analysis lacked.

## Definition as Coined

For Goffman, deference and demeanor are elements of the ceremonial "interaction order" — the small rituals of regard through which persons confirm one another's social selves. Collins and Annett (attributed) treat them as [[interaction-ritual|interaction rituals]] whose form varies systematically with social structure, specifically with the authority and social-density variables of the book's stratification theory.

## Semantic History

Goffman (1956, 1967) analyzed deference/demeanor as features of modern face-to-face life without asking how they change historically. Collins and Annett historicize them: drawing on Lenski's typology of society types, they argue that deference cultures are elaborate and rigid where status is stable, ascribed, and locally enforced, and that they have progressively **"evaporated"** under conditions of high mobility, cosmopolitan contact, and mass culture — tracing an American sequence from a gentry deference order through a "fashionable" status-competition elite to a mass culture in which fixed deference cultures dissolve.

## Operationalizations

The chapter states summarized causal principles (Appendix A, propositions 6.0–7.4) linking the intensity and rigidity of deference/demeanor rituals to copresence, surveillance, and the stability of authority — the same variables that generate [[class-cultures|class cultures]]. Status competition, in this account, intensifies where deference is no longer ascribed but must be continually won.

## Applications

Collins uses the concept to explain status competition, etiquette regimes, youth cultures (Appendix B reads the 1960s counterculture through a Weberian sociology of religion), and the general modern shift from ceremonial deference toward negotiated, situational self-presentation.

## Critiques and Limitations

The historical typology leans on secondary sources and an authority-and-density causal frame that Collins elsewhere concedes is loosely linked to its micro-foundations. Notably, Norbert Elias's closely parallel account of courtly deference (*The Civilizing Process*) is **not** cited, a gap in the chapter's comparative apparatus. Claims are **attributed** to Collins and Annett (1975).
