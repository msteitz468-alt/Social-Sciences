# Episode of Contention

*(Attributed — Fligstein & McAdam 2012, building on social-movement / contentious-politics usage.)* An **episode of contention** is a period in which a [[strategic-action-field]] is in open conflict: shared understandings of stakes, positions, rules, and frames break down or are actively challenged, and actors mobilize to restructure (or defend) the field. Episodes are typically triggered by **exogenous shocks** from proximate fields (legal rulings, economic crises, geopolitical shifts, state reorganization), though endogenous paths are possible.

## Definition as Coined

In *[[fligstein-mcadam-theory-of-fields-2012|A Theory of Fields]]*, field dynamics move between settlement and contention. During an episode of contention, "the very existence and structure" of a SAF can be up for grabs; outcomes include a new [[field-settlement|settlement]], restoration of the prior order, or disintegration into unorganized social space. Related to, but not identical with, Tillyan contentious performances and [[repertoire-of-contention|repertoires]] — SAF usage embeds contention inside a general field ontology shared with markets and organizations.

## Semantic History

Borrowed and generalized from social-movement studies (political opportunities, framing, protest cycles) into a language that also covers firm and market crises. *[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* (McAdam, Tarrow, Tilly 2001) is the primary sibling vocabulary: episodes are continuous streams of contention that always involve two or more processes; analysis scans episodes for mechanisms rather than matching whole episodes to general models. DOC ingested.

## Operationalizations

Historical periodization of shock → mobilization → conflict → settlement (civil rights and mortgage chapters). Distinction between **fundamental** change (reordering of major field aspects) and **piecemeal** change (authors note this may be better as a continuum).

## Applications

Civil rights movement and transformation of U.S. racial-politics fields (Ch. 5); mortgage-market destabilization and MBS industrial model crisis (Ch. 5).

## Critiques and Limitations

Episode boundaries can be researcher-imposed; "exogenous shock" risks redescription without mechanism unless inter-field linkages are specified (the theory's own demand).

## From *Dynamics of Contention* (2001)

*(Attributed — MTT.)* Episodes = continuous streams of contention including collective claims-making that bears on other parties' interests; they are unique sequences of alterations in relations among connected elements, always involving two or more processes (e.g. Paris July 1789: mobilization, identity shift, polarization). Beginnings/endings are observers' conventions. Fifteen major episodes organize the book's comparisons.
