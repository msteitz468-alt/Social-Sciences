# Patchiness

## Definition as Coined

In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]*, [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] describes the world as **patchy**: a mosaic of open-ended assemblages of entangled ways of life, with multiple temporal rhythms — visible only when progress and scalability assumptions are suspended. Related coinage in the same text: **third nature** — what manages to live despite capitalism — emerging within temporal polyphony *(Tsing 2015)*.

Capitalism without progress assumptions, she argues, also looks patchy: concentration of wealth is possible because value produced in unplanned patches is appropriated for capital ([[salvage-accumulation]]) *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Semantic History

Ecological "patch" dynamics long predate Tsing; she socializes and multi-species the term against capitalist **scalability**. Related older anthropological figure: [[shreds-and-patches]] (different genealogy — Boasian culture-history pejorative, not Tsing's affirmative use). Assemblage for Tsing is **polyphonic** — autonomous melodies intertwining — not a fixed ecological community *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Operationalizations

- Livelihood patches (Open Ticket foraging/buying without local capital accumulation).
- Forest disturbance mosaics (Japan, Oregon, Yunnan, Finland).
- Knowledge patches (national matsutake sciences translating vernacular and scientific frames differently).
- Gift and commercial nodes that do not nest into one scalable economy.

## Applications

- [[tsing-mushroom-study]] · [[open-ticket-oregon]]
- Contrast: [[scale-making]] · plantation model of smooth expansion *(Tsing 2015)*

## Critiques and Limitations

Patchiness can under-specify hierarchy if every difference is celebrated equally; Tsing pairs it with analysis of concentrated salvage wealth. Nonscalability is not automatically ethical — unregulated destruction can be nonscalable too *(Tsing 2015)*.
