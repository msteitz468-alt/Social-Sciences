# Invidious Distinction (Invidious Comparison)

## Definition as Coined

[[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]] gave **invidious comparison** a precise, deliberately value-neutral definition in *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|The Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899, ch. 2): "a comparison of persons with a view to rating and grading them... in respect of relative worth" — used "descriptively," without approval or condemnation. The paired noun, **invidious distinction**, names the honorific standing such comparison confers: "The possession of wealth confers honour; it is an invidious distinction."

Invidious comparison is the elementary mechanism beneath Veblen's entire system. It grades persons, then extends to *employments* (honorable exploit vs. base drudgery), to *goods* (honorific vs. base articles of consumption), and to *service* (noble vs. ignoble livery). In the primitive barbarian mind, Veblen argued, honor connotes nothing but assertion of superior force: "'Honourable' is 'formidable'; 'worthy' is 'prepotent'."

## Semantic History

Veblen's usage runs against the ordinary sense of "invidious" (giving offense, unjust). He needed a term for comparative status-grading stripped of moral tone, and repurposed "invidious" technically — a move readers persistently resist, hearing criticism where Veblen claims mere description. The concept is the ancestor of later "invidious comparison" usage in reference-group theory and status research, and of the general sociological interest in how societies rank persons. Its contrast class in Veblen's late chapters is the **non-invidious interests** — the [[instinct-of-workmanship|instinct of workmanship]], the parental bent, and idle curiosity — which orient toward the impersonal life-process rather than toward relative standing.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. It functions as a definitional primitive in Veblen's argument rather than a measured variable; downstream, status-attainment and social-comparison research operationalize related notions of ranking and relative position.

## Applications

Veblen used invidious comparison to unify phenomena that look unrelated: the exploit/drudgery split, the honorific vs. base grading of consumption goods and of livery, [[pecuniary-emulation|pecuniary emulation]], sport and wagering (heightening one's side's ascendancy at the loser's cost), the status structure of devout observance (worshipper-to-deity as a relation of status), and even the honorific vocabulary of the academy ("noble"/"base," "higher"/"lower" learning) as inherited status-language rather than neutral judgment.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The value-neutral claim is contested in practice: the analytic apparatus is inseparable from Veblen's satire, so "invidious" reads as indictment however he defines it.
- As a universal solvent that dissolves diverse practices into one mechanism, it buys breadth at the cost of discriminating power.
