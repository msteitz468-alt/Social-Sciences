## Definition as Coined

Alienation (*Entfremdung*) is [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s concept for the estrangement of workers
from their own activity and its products under capitalist relations of production. In the
*Economic and Philosophic Manuscripts* (1844) Marx distinguished the worker's estrangement
from (1) the product of labor, which confronts him as an alien power; (2) the act of production,
experienced as external and coerced; (3) his "species-being" (his human capacity for free,
creative activity); and (4) other human beings. Marx adapted the term from Hegel's philosophy
of spirit via Feuerbach's critique of religion, giving it a material, economic basis.

*This page records the concept as it bears on the sources so far ingested, above all
[[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s mid-twentieth-century extension of it; a fuller treatment awaits
ingestion of Marx's manuscripts themselves.*

## Semantic History

The concept's meaning has drifted considerably. In the 1844 *Manuscripts* (unpublished until
1932) it is philosophical and humanist; in *Capital* Marx's vocabulary shifts toward
[[commodity-fetishism]] and exploitation, leading to a long dispute over whether the "young"
and "mature" Marx hold one theory or two. In American sociology the term was often
de-radicalized into a set of survey dimensions (Melvin Seeman's powerlessness, meaninglessness,
normlessness, isolation, self-estrangement). [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]], in
*[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951), took a third path: he extended alienation
from the manual wage-worker to the salaried [[white-collar]] employee, arguing (attributed)
that the "property conditions of alienation" now govern white-collar work, and that
rationalization has "expropriated" not only ownership but "rationality itself" from work.

## Operationalizations

Marx did not quantify alienation; it is diagnosed structurally from the wage-relation.
Mills likewise treated it interpretively, but grounded it in the [[personality-market]]
(the salaried worker sells "one's self," so that personality traits "become part of the
means of production"), in deskilling, and in the [[the-big-split]] between work and leisure.
Later empirical sociology (Seeman, Blauner) built attitude scales, at the cost of severing the
concept from Marx's structural theory.

## Applications

Beyond Marx, the concept anchors Mills's account of [[white-collar]] work, the Frankfurt
School's culture critique, the sociology of work and deskilling, and studies of the labor
process. Its contrast-ideal in Mills is [[craftsmanship]].

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] (*[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*, 1983) extends the factory-boy arm-as-instrument image to service work: the flight attendant can become estranged from managed feeling — "the margins of the soul" — when [[emotional-labor]] is sold under corporate control (attributed). Estrangement, she argued, depends on context: surface and deep acting are not inherently alienating on stage or in private life, but become so when profit systems ignore their psychological costs (attributed).

**Tsing (*[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]*, 2015):** uses alienation for investors’ dream of making people and things stand alone as mobile assets removable from life-world entanglements — distinct from using others within a life world (e.g. eating and being eaten). Simplification for alienation produces ruins (single-asset landscapes abandoned when the asset is exhausted). In the matsutake chain, Oregon mushrooms are first non-alienated freedom trophies; they become fully alienated inventory only for hours on the tarmac before re-embedding as Japanese gifts — the conversion that enables [[salvage-accumulation]] *(Tsing)*. Keep this ecological-economic sense distinct from Marx’s wage-labor *Entfremdung* and Mills’s personality-market estrangement.

[[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]], in *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional
Man]]* (1964), gives the concept a paradoxical turn (attributed): under advanced industrial
society the concept of alienation "seems to become questionable" because individuals
genuinely identify with their commodities — "they find their soul in their automobile, hi-fi
set, split-level home." Marcuse holds that this identification is not the *end* of alienation
but "a more progressive stage" of it, in which "the subject which is alienated is swallowed up
by its alienated existence" (see [[one-dimensionality]]). He also distinguishes Marxian
alienation from an "artistic alienation" — the conscious, mediated transcendence of alienated
existence embodied in disruptive literary figures — which one-dimensional society liquidates by
absorbing higher culture as commodity ([[the-great-refusal]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Alienation is among sociology's most contested concepts: critics charge it is normatively
loaded (presupposing an un-alienated human essence), difficult to operationalize without
distorting it, and stretched to cover disparate phenomena. Mills's psychologized extension —
alienation as self-estrangement in the personality market — is powerful but interpretive, and
shares the romantic vulnerability of the [[craftsmanship]] ideal against which he measured it.
The concept is written in attributed voice throughout.
