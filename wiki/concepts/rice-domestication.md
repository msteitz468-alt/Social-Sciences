# Rice Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Rice domestication** is the coevolutionary process turning wild rice (*Oryza rufipogon*) into domestic Asian rice (*Oryza sativa*). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] this is an **independent Yangtze valley origin** — a global turning point feeding billions — parallel to Fertile Crescent cereals, Mesoamerican maize, and New Guinea horticulture, not a secondary diffusion from India or mainland Southeast Asia as older models held.

## Semantic History

Earlier research sought origins where wild stands were abundant (India, mainland SE Asia). Anping Pei’s 1988 **Pengtoushan** excavation found then-earliest domesticated rice ≥7500 BC; 1984 rediscovery of wild rice stands in the Yangtze and subsequent middle-Yangtze work (Bashidang, Diaotonghuan phytoliths, Hemudu) shifted consensus toward China (as of Mithen 2003). Southern “early” grains were re-dated younger. Japan’s [[jomon-culture|Jōmon]] kept wild-food economies with earliest pottery until rice farming at scale ~500 BC (incoming China/Korea-linked economy — as of Mithen).

## Operationalizations

As of Mithen 2003:

- **Domestication syndrome:** non-shattering grain-heads that “wait for the harvester”; synchronized germination/ripening; larger grains vs wild.
- **Phytoliths (Pearsall):** larger glume phytoliths mark domestic rice; Diaotonghuan sequence — wild surge ~12,000 BC; scarcity in Younger Dryas 10,800–9600 BC; equal wild/domestic by ~7500 BC; wild gone by ~6500 BC.
- **Macroremains:** Pengtoushan rice temper in pottery; Bashidang ~15,000 fully domestic grains (7000–5000 BC).
- **Pottery co-evolution:** Yangtze pottery by ~9500–10,000 BC (Diaotonghuan slab pots oldest in China in Mithen’s account); steaming/boiling and storage argued as essential as Fertile Crescent grinding stones.
- **Cultivation path (conjectural):** flood-recession broadcast sowing on annual silts without heavy tillage tools (Pei found no spades/hoes/knives at Pengtoushan).
- **Spread:** rice at Chopani Mando (India) most likely from southern China by ~5000 BC (indigenous domestication also possible — Mithen).

## Applications

- Independent agricultural origin center in global prehistory.
- Settlement: Pengtoushan rectangular and semi-subterranean houses **without** grinding stones ubiquitous in SW Asia.
- [[hemudu|Hemudu]] (~5000 BC) pile houses, paddy, water buffalo, pigs as mature rice economy snapshot.
- Contrast with Jōmon: complex sedentary foragers adjacent to rice world for millennia before adopting it.
- Environmental scaffold: late-glacial and Holocene Yangtze vegetation flip-flops ([[younger-dryas]], [[post-glacial-recolonization]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Origin-zone consensus** in 2003 may be refined by later genetics (japonica/indica, multiple origins debates) — date-stamp.
- Phytolith size criteria and species identification remain methodologically argued in specialist literature.
- “Independent of Southwest Asia” is well supported as a package claim; local multi-center detail is open.
- Rice farmers ≠ Jōmon people ≠ modern national populations as simple equations — Mithen’s remarks on modern Japanese ancestry and Ainu–Jōmon links are **positions as of 2003**, pre-aDNA.
