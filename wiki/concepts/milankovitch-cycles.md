# Milankovitch Cycles

The Milankovitch (or orbital-forcing) theory holds that changes in Earth's orbit and axial geometry, by redistributing solar radiation across seasons and latitudes, pace the growth and decay of ice sheets — the fundamental external driver of [[quaternary-glaciation|Quaternary glacial–interglacial cycles]] and thus of the environmental conditions in which the [[holocene|Holocene]] arose.

## Definition as Coined

The Serbian geophysicist Milutin Milanković (1879–1958) calculated, over the 1920s–1940s, how three orbital parameters vary through time and computed the resulting insolation curves:
- **Eccentricity** — the ellipticity of Earth's orbit, ~100 000-year (and ~400 000-year) cycles;
- **Obliquity** — the tilt of Earth's axis (21.8°–24.4°), ~41 000-year cycle;
- **Precession** — the wobble of the axis / movement of the equinoxes, ~23 000- and ~19 000-year cycles.

## Semantic History

The idea long predated confirmation. It was decisively supported when [[unknown|Hays, Imbrie and Shackleton (1976)]] found the predicted orbital periodicities in deep-sea oxygen-isotope records — the study that gave the theory its "pacemaker of the ice ages" formulation. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) presents orbital forcing as the accepted first-order explanation of long-term climatic change, while stressing (attributed) that it is a *pacemaker* amplified by internal feedbacks, not a complete mechanism: high-frequency and abrupt events such as the [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] fall outside the Milankovitch bands and require ocean-circulation explanations.

## Operationalizations

Orbital signals are read from the frequency spectra of long climate proxy series — deep-sea δ¹⁸O (see [[oxygen-isotope-stratigraphy]]), loess sequences, and long lake records. The match between predicted insolation and observed ice-volume periodicities is the operational test.

## Applications

The theory frames the [[quaternary-glaciation|glacial cycle]] within which the Holocene is the current interglacial, and underpins reconstructions of early-Holocene insolation maxima that drove the strengthened monsoons of the "green Sahara" (see [[desertification]] and [[holocene-climate-change]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The "100 kyr problem" — that the late-Quaternary glacial cycle is dominated by the ~100 kyr eccentricity band, whose direct insolation effect is weak — shows orbital forcing alone is insufficient and must be amplified by internal feedbacks (ice-albedo, greenhouse gases, ocean circulation). Roberts (1998) is careful to present orbital forcing as a boundary condition, not a determinant of Holocene sub-millennial change.
