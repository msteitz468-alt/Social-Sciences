# Inner-Worldly Asceticism

## Definition as Coined

**Inner-worldly** (or worldly) **asceticism** is Weber's term in *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]* for the rationalization of conduct *within* this world and its institutions — but for the sake of the world beyond — as developed especially in ascetic Protestantism. Christian asceticism had fled to monasteries and ruled the world from outside; the Reformation, on Weber's account, "strode into the market-place of life, slammed the door of the monastery behind it," and penetrated daily routine with methodical discipline (transition into Ch. 5).

Sebastian Franck's formula (as Weber uses it): in a sense **every Christian must be a monk all his life** — asceticism democratized into lay callings (Ch. 4).

## Semantic History

- **Contrast pairs in PE:** worldly vs monastic (other-worldly) asceticism; asceticism vs mysticism; active "tool" of God (Calvinism) vs vessel of the Holy Spirit (Lutheran-leaning types).
- **Four historical forms of ascetic Protestantism (Ch. 4):** Calvinism, Pietism, Methodism, Baptist sects — variations on the ascetic theme.
- **Calvinist peak:** Predestination + proof produces systematic ethical life-planning; elimination of magic; distrust of emotional/sacramental shortcuts (Ch. 4A).
- **Later secularization:** Religious asceticism "escapes the cage"; utilitarian and sport-like wealth-pursuit remain (Ch. 5).
- English secondary literature often says "this-worldly" or "inner-worldly" asceticism for *innerweltliche Askese*.

## Operationalizations

| Form (Weber) | Distinctive mechanism (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Calvinism | Predestination, proof, methodical sanctification of whole life |
| Pietism | More emotional, sometimes weaker "iron consistency" |
| Methodism | Emotional certainty as makeshift for Calvinist proof; no new calling idea |
| Baptists/Quakers | Sect of regenerate; inner light; honesty; elimination of magic |

## Applications

- Causal-psychological bridge in [[protestant-ethic-thesis]] between doctrine and [[spirit-of-capitalism]].
- Explains combination of intense piety with business acumen among Quakers, Mennonites, Pietists, Calvinists (Ch. 1).
- Foundation for later Weberian sociology of rational life-conduct (*Lebensführung*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Continuity with monastic asceticism: Weber treats it as fundamental postulate (reply to Brentano, notes); critics see rupture or exaggeration.
- Catholic and non-Protestant asceticisms underrated by design of the one-sided PE question.
- Ideal types flatten intra-confessional diversity.
