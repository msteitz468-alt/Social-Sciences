## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) define a research design as "a plan that shows, through
a discussion of our model and data, how we expect to use our evidence to make
inferences," adding that such a plan is "at least implicit in every piece of research"
even where it is never made explicit. Their book, KKV state, is explicitly not a
philosophy-of-social-science treatise nor a technique manual for surveys, fieldwork, or
statistics, but a treatment of "how to pose questions and fashion scholarly research to
make valid descriptive and causal inferences" ([[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994]]).

## Semantic History

KKV divide all research designs analytically into four components: the research
question, the theory, the data, and the use of the data ("For analytical purposes, we
divide all research designs into four components: the research question, the theory,
the data, and the use of the data"). This framework was constructed explicitly to unify
qualitative/small-n and quantitative/large-n research under a single logic of
[[causal-inference]] and [[descriptive-inference]], rejecting a categorical
"quasi-experiment" label: "Either a research design involves investigator control over
the observations and values of the key causal variables (in which case it is an
experiment) or it does not (in which case it is nonexperimental research)." The concept
was elaborated across the book's chapters, with chapter 4 devoted to the conditions
under which a design becomes indeterminate (see [[indeterminate-research-design]]) and
chapter 6 to case/observation selection strategy.

## Operationalizations

KKV's operational core is the distinction between **cases** and **observations**: a
single case can yield many observations at different levels of analysis, and
[[observable-implications]] of a theory are what a design must be built to generate and
test. They argue that qualitative researchers must be as self-conscious about design and
as explicit in reporting it as any other scientific researcher: "Readers should not have
to reformulate published qualitative studies to make them scientifically valid ... it
should not be the responsibility of readers or reviewers to explain that the author had
a better implicit than explicit research design." A design's adequacy is assessed
against a bias–efficiency (mean-squared-error) trade-off between design choices — e.g.,
case-study versus large-n approaches — rather than by genre convention alone.

Three general routes for increasing the number of observations available to a design
are identified: applying the same measures to new units, applying new measures to the
same units, and combining both. KKV argue this move can convert an
[[indeterminate-research-design]] into a tractable one even without new data
collection, because an apparently single-case study may contain many potential
observations at finer levels of analysis: "By increasing the number of observations,
even without more data collection, the researcher can often transform an intractable
problem that has an indeterminate research design into a tractable one."

## Applications

KKV's framework is applied throughout the book to worked examples of case and
observation selection, including Skocpol on revolutions, Porter on competitive
advantage, Tilly on state survival, Kohli on Indian antipoverty policy, Laitin on
Somalia/Yoruba, Achen and Snidal on deterrence, Inkeles and Rossi on industrialization,
and CDC cancer-cluster studies. They credit the "method of structured, focused
comparison" (associated with the [[comparative-method]] tradition) as useful for
hypothesis generation but insist it "does not provide a methodology for causal
inference" when the resulting design is indeterminate.

## Critiques and Limitations

The claim that qualitative and quantitative research share a single underlying logic of
inference is itself contested terrain: see [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]]
and the related [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]. Critics have argued that
KKV's regression-derived framework underweights case-specific, interpretive, and
mechanism-based modes of inference (see [[process-tracing]]) on their own terms rather
than as extensions of a variable-oriented logic.
