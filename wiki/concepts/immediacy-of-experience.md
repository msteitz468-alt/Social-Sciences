# Immediacy of Experience Principle

The **immediacy of experience principle (IEP)** is [[everett-daniel|Daniel Everett]]'s proposed cultural value governing [[piraha|Pirahã]] life and language: **declarative utterances assert only what is directly experienced by the speaker, or witnessed by someone alive during the speaker's lifetime.** It is an **etic analytical construct** — Everett's own interpretive framework, built inductively across his fieldwork, not an observed fact or an emic Pirahã term. Everything on this page is **attributed to Everett** and is contested.

## Definition as Coined

Everett's canonical statement: "formulaic language and actions (rituals) that involve reference to nonwitnessed events are avoided … the Pirahãs avoid formulaic encodings of values and instead transmit values and information via actions and words that are original in composition with the person acting or speaking, that have been witnessed by this person, or that have been told to this person by a witness." A second formulation ties it to grammar: "Declarative Pirahã utterances contain only assertions related directly to the moment of speech, either experienced by the speaker or witnessed by someone alive during the lifetime of the speaker."

## Semantic History

The principle emerged as Everett's attempt to unify a scattered set of otherwise-puzzling reported facts about the Pirahã under one explanatory value. It moved from a fieldworker's working hypothesis to the load-bearing premise of his broader **culture-constrains-grammar** program and his challenge to [[universal-grammar|Universal Grammar]].

## Operationalizations

The principle is operationalized (by Everett) as a testable restriction: **declaratives assert only the directly witnessed** (by the speaker, or by someone alive in the speaker's lifetime). Diagnostics he applies: search for constructions that require reference to non-witnessed states — perfect tense, embedded assertions, numerals, quantifiers, fiction — and predict their absence. Because it is a single-observer construct, there is no standardized cross-analyst measurement; its "operationalization" is internal to Everett's corpus.

## Applications — What It Is Invoked to Explain (attributed)

Everett derives from the IEP the reported absences of:

- **numbers and counting** (abstractions that generalize beyond immediate experience) — and Pirahã adults' failure to learn to count in an 8-month class;
- **grammatical number / plural**, **quantifiers** (*all, every*), and **colour lexemes** (only descriptive phrases: "it is blood" = red);
- the **perfect tense** and other constructions anchored to events other than the moment of speech;
- **embedded/subordinate assertive clauses** → hence, he argues, no sentential **[[recursion]]**, no coordination, no disjunction;
- **great-grandparent kinship terms** (beyond a witnessable lifespan);
- **creation myths, fiction, and folklore** — Pirahã "myths" retell witnessed events and require a living eyewitness at the telling;
- receptivity to **missionary religion** — Abrahamic scriptures recount events with no living eyewitness, which Everett offers as the reason no Pirahã is known to have converted in ~300 years.

Dreams (*xaipípai*) and **spirit encounters** are treated as *confirming* the principle: the Pirahã classify them as genuine direct experience, so talk of them is talk of real events, not fiction.

## Relation to Linguistic Relativity

Everett is explicit that the IEP is **not** [[linguistic-relativity|Sapir–Whorf]]. Whorf's arrow runs grammar→thought; Everett's runs **culture→grammar** — a cultural value shaping the grammar itself. He accepts only a weak Sapir–Whorf ("we don't normally perceive how language affects thought") and argues the strong version cannot unify the Pirahã facts; his counter-evidence is that the Pirahã, unlike the Warlpiri, refused to borrow numerals despite two centuries of trade.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Single-observer construct.** No independent replication of the IEP as a unified principle is presented; it rests on one fieldworker's corpus.
- **Circularity worry.** Everett himself concedes "the route from a cultural constraint to complex noun phrases can seem a bit circuitous," and that the underlying recursion question is "far from settled."
- **Contested downstream claims.** Because the IEP is the premise for the no-recursion argument, disputes over the [[piraha-recursion-debate|Pirahã data]] bear directly on it.
