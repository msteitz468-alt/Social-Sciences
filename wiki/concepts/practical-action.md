# Practical Action

## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel proposed that "practical action," "practical accomplishment," and the recurring formula "for all practical purposes" name the central, previously untheorized phenomenon of social order: the objective reality of social facts is not a fixed thing awaiting description but "an ongoing accomplishment of the concerted activities of daily life," produced through "the ordinary, artful ways of that accomplishment being by members known, used, and taken for granted." He framed this explicitly against Durkheimian sociology's treatment of "social facts as things," proposing instead that practical sociological reasoning and practical action — not compliance with rules imposed from outside a setting — are what render everyday activities "visibly-rational-and-reportable-for-all-practical-purposes," i.e., accountable.

Central to this concept is Garfinkel's rejection of the passive, rule-compliant actor found in mainstream sociological and psychological theory. He named this rejected model the "cultural dope": "By 'cultural dope' I refer to the man-in-the-sociologist's-society who produces the stable features of the society by acting in compliance with preestablished and legitimate alternatives of action that the common culture provides." Garfinkel argued that standard models — the sociological "cultural dope," the psychological "psychological dope" — treat members' common-sense rationalities as epiphenomenal, when in fact members actively judge, interpret, gloss, and improvise (relying on the "et cetera" clause, irony, euphemism) in producing recognizable social order. His breaching demonstrations were designed to force this ordinarily invisible judgmental work into view by presenting subjects with practical problems that could not be resolved by rule-following alone.

## Semantic History

The phrase "for all practical purposes" functions throughout *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]* as a near-technical term of art — Garfinkel used variants such as "rational-adequacy-for-all-practical-purposes" and "scientific-for-all-practical-purposes" to argue that the professional-scientific program of substituting objective, context-free expressions for indexical ones (see [[indexicality]]) is never actually completed; it remains "programmatic in every particular case" and is only ever achieved practically, on the occasion at hand. This reframed the distinction between "objective" and "indexical" language itself as a practical accomplishment rather than a solved methodological problem — licensing Garfinkel's broader claim that reflexivity is essential to, not a removable defect of, any account. The "cultural dope" critique, first stated in this volume, became one of ethnomethodology's most widely cited polemical formulations and was subsequently invoked across sociology as shorthand for over-socialized or rule-determinist models of action.

## Operationalizations

Garfinkel did not quantify practical action; he demonstrated it through paired empirical cases. The Los Angeles Suicide Prevention Center's "psychological autopsies" of equivocal deaths were presented as exemplary of practical sociological reasoning in common-sense situations of choice: investigators "make do" with whatever evidentiary remains are at hand, treating them as both precedent and goal rather than testing them step-by-step against a pre-given list of categories. The reanalysis of the UCLA clinic coding study similarly showed that graduate coders required unstated background knowledge to apply written instructions to actual case folders — undermining any claim that the coding produced disinterested, rule-derived description rather than a practical, situated accomplishment.

## Applications

Beyond the SPC and clinic-coding studies, Garfinkel used the concept to reanalyze mundane conversation (the "husband and wife" colloquy, showing common understanding to be a matter of methods of speaking rather than shared substantive content) and organizational selection studies (Chapters 6 and 7, with Egon Bittner, [[bittner-egon]], on clinic records and selection criteria). Across these cases, Garfinkel treated the "rational properties of practical actions as ongoing accomplishment" as ethnomethodology's central topic, closing Chapter 1 with the formula: "The demonstrably rational properties of indexical expressions and indexical actions is an ongoing achievement of the organized activities of everyday life."

## Critiques and Limitations

Because Garfinkel refused to formulate his studies as "correctives" to professional or lay sociological reasoning — writing that ethnomethodological studies "are useless when they are done as ironies" — critics have sometimes read the practical-action concept as methodologically agnostic to the point of offering no basis for evaluating competing accounts of a setting. Others have argued that the "cultural dope" critique, while an effective polemic against over-socialized models of action, risks understating the degree to which members' practical judgments are themselves patterned and constrained by durable institutional arrangements (a tension later worked out differently in structuration-style theorizing outside this volume). Within ethnomethodology, the concept remains foundational and is treated as continuous with [[membership]], [[accountability]], and [[background-expectancies]].
