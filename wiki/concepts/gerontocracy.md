# Gerontocracy (Melanesian)

## Definition as Coined

**Gerontocracy** — literally "rule of the old" — names a social condition in which the
older men of a community hold disproportionate authority. [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]
used the word for the wide powers of the elders among Australian Aboriginal peoples;
[[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] adopted it for a specific hypothesized Melanesian condition
(attributed): a stage in which old men **monopolized the young women** of the community,
taking wives two generations their junior and transferring surplus wives to designated
junior kin.

## Semantic History

- **Frazer 1905:** "gerontocracy" for the authority of Australian elders over ritual and
  marriage.
- **Rivers 1914:** built the term into a reconstruction. From anomalous terminological
  correspondences — a father's father addressed as elder brother (Fiji, Buin); a mother's
  mother classed with elder sister (Pentecost) — Rivers inferred (attributed) an ancient
  practice of old men marrying granddaughters and monopolising young women, generating a
  cluster of "anomalous" marriage forms (with a brother's granddaughter, with the wife of the
  mother's brother, with the wife of the father's father) and, secondarily,
  [[cross-cousin-marriage]]. He held the condition presupposed an *already* developed
  [[dual-organisation|dual-organisation]], matrilineal, classificatory society — hence a
  *late* stage, not a primitive one, since the terminologically richest systems bear its
  clearest marks.
- Rivers explicitly distinguished his gerontocracy from the Atkinson–Lang **primal-horde**
  hypothesis (a single male monopolising all women in an undifferentiated group): his
  presupposes two exogamous moieties and a full classificatory system.

## Operationalizations

Purely inferential: the "presence" of past gerontocracy is read off cross-generational
terminological equations and anomalous marriage forms via the [[genealogical-method]] and
the logic of [[survivals]]. Rivers corroborated it with a cross-cultural analogy to the Dieri
of central Australia (Howitt 1904), where a man may marry his elder brother's granddaughter
and the term *nadada* links potential spouses across two generations.

## Applications

The organising hypothesis of the theoretical volume of
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]*: from the gerontocracy
Rivers derived the anomalous marriages, the simulated marriage-by-capture and infant
betrothal of Pentecost (reread as young men's escape from the elders' monopoly, not
McLennan's capture-from-a-hostile-tribe survival), and the origin of individual marriage out
of an earlier sexual communism. He grounded the elders' power in a supposed general belief in
the magical potency of old men.

## Critiques and Limitations

The reconstruction is a chain of inference upon inference and did not survive the
functionalist critique of conjectural history (see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]). No
independent evidence established that such a gerontocratic stage ever existed; the anomalous
terminologies it was built to explain admit other readings. As a *documented ethnographic
pattern* — elders controlling marriage and ritual — gerontocracy remains a usable descriptive
term; as Rivers' *historical stage* it is obsolete.
