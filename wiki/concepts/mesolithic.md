# Mesolithic

## Definition as Coined

**Mesolithic** ("Middle Stone Age") labels the archaeological cultures of the early Holocene (and, in some older usages, late Pleistocene) that succeed Advanced / Upper Palaeolithic hunting traditions and precede — or locally coexist with — the spread of farming. In *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961), [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] treats Mesolithic communities as a major world-prehistoric theme: SW Asian groups at the threshold of domestication, European Maglemosian and coastal foragers, and expansions across northern Eurasia (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 3).

The term is **not** a universal stage: regions without a distinct microlithic/post-glacial forager horizon are not forced into it.

## Semantic History

- **European prehistory:** fills the chronological gap between Palaeolithic and Neolithic once the three-age system and glacial chronology matured.
- **Clark 1961:** embeds Mesolithic in an economic–ecological narrative (forest clearance thresholds, coastal resources, contact with Neolithic farmers) rather than pure typology.
- **Later:** absolute dating and "Epipalaeolithic" / "Archaic" regional vocabularies (Near East, Americas) often replace or parallel "Mesolithic"; the European Maglemosian remains a classic type-case.

## Operationalizations

- **Clark (attributed, 1961):** post-glacial / Neothermal forager economies; microlithic and specialized coastal toolkits; Maglemosian as northern European exemplar; SW Asian "Mesolithic" groups discussed in relation to early farming at Jericho and related sites.
- **Cunliffe (attributed, 2013, Britain/Ireland):** chronological bracket from post–Younger Dryas recolonization (~9600 BC continuous story) through forest adaptation (~9000 BC tool-kits) to the energetic debate over the Mesolithic–[[neolithic]] transition (c. 4200–3800 BC formative centuries in his narrative); operational markers include microlithic / blade industries, hide and log boat technologies, lithic exchange networks, and isotope/diet contrasts with early farmers. Ireland’s later Mesolithic distinguished by flint blades rather than microliths (c. 5500–c. 4000 BC).

## Applications

Period tag for early Holocene foragers in Europe and as a comparative label in Clark's global outline; links to economic prehistory via [[star-carr]] and Maglemosian subsistence reconstruction (Clark's own research tradition). In regional British/Irish synthesis ([[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]]), the label organizes recolonization via [[doggerland]] and Atlantic seaways, maritime connectivity, ritual practice, and the contested farming transition ([[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Europe-centric label exported unevenly; blurs into Epipalaeolithic in the Near East; can imply a necessary evolutionary stage between Palaeolithic and Neolithic rather than regionally contingent adaptations; absolute chronology in 1961 sources is pre-calibration. Genetic ancestry percentages and mutation-rate dates for Mesolithic Britain (as reported in popular and research genetics **as of Cunliffe 2013**) are provisional, method-dependent, and must not be equated with archaeological cultures or languages (four-way non-identity). Ethnographic analogies (Pacific Coast long journeys; Greenland umiaks) are hypothesis generators when used for boat and mobility models — flag as analogy, not evidence.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Post-YD Holocene foragers:** after 9600 BC warming, European early Holocene hunter-fisher-gatherers (Maglemosian-type woodlands; coastal catastrophes/Doggerland) occupy the Mesolithic slot in Mithen’s narrative; Near Eastern “Epipalaeolithic” (Kebaran → Natufian) is the SW Asian parallel, not forced under the European label (attributed).
- **Star Carr:** among Europe’s key early Mesolithic sites; first European site of any period radiocarbon-dated; [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] 1949–51 excavation shifted British Mesolithic studies from tool catalogues to lifestyle and environment (waterlogged bone/antler/wood) (attributed, ch. 15).
- **Early vs late cultural geography:** early Mesolithic virtual cultural identity across northern Europe (Star Carr horizon) fractures by late Mesolithic into marked identities (clothes, harpoons, axe forms) (attributed).
- **Complex Mesolithic cases:** [[lepenski-vir|Lepenski Vir]] sedentary Iron Gates fishermen ~6400 BC; Oleneostrovski, Skateholm, Ertebølle, Oronsay as complex HG with cemeteries/aggregation before Neolithic absorption (attributed, chs. 18–20).
- **Labour contrast:** Mesolithic groups “seemed to work only a few hours a day” because knowledge filled the stomach vs Nea Nikomedeia hard agricultural labour — original-affluent-society theme in Mithen’s voice (position).
- **Farming interface:** Balkan leap-frog farmers first stimulate then collapse Lepenski Vir culture; generational resistance vs seduction by farming (attributed).

Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## Britain Begins densification (Cunliffe 2013)

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* 2013, especially Chs. 2–5. British and Irish application of the period concept; not a redefinition of global Mesolithic.)*

### Chronology and recolonization

Cunliffe’s continuous narrative of the islands starts ~10,000 BC as Britain emerged from LGM conditions; rapid warming from ~9600 BC toward climates by ~8000 BC as warm or warmer than today. He reports intensifying Devensian ice left Britain abandoned ~25–15 ka, Younger Dryas cold (~10,900 BC onset) likely drove most people south again, then Holocene pioneers re-entered from refuges stretching from Atlantic France across to Germany — culturally close to Ahrensburgian groups of the North European Plain — as “the first Britons” and ancestors of a significant component of the present population (attributed voice; ancestry claim as setup for his genetics discussion, **as of 2013**).

By ~9000 BC, birch/pine then hazel forests forced adaptation: forests support only 20–30% of open-landscape animal biomass; smaller bands, limited territories, dietary diversification, and new tool-kits (microliths, axes/adzes, bone/antler gear) define the classic North European Plain Mesolithic package in his account (attributed).

**Earliest British sites on present evidence (ninth millennium BC):** Thatcham (Kennet) ~9000 BC; [[star-carr]] 8770–8460 BC; Cramond near Edinburgh ~8500 BC; Ireland not yet occupied at that horizon in his reading (attributed).

### Dual corridors: Doggerland and Atlantic

- **Eastern:** [[doggerland]] as huge low plain joining SE Britain to the continent (Pas de Calais to Denmark), gradually inundated until North Sea and Channel tidal currents joined (he gives both traditional islandization windows and later mollusc-based 5800–5400 BC breakthrough; full Channel–North Sea connection possibly not until ~3800 BC). For up to four millennia foragers could roam the forested plain; artefact types show broad similarity Pennines to Poland with regional styles and harpoon links eastern Britain–N France–Belgium–Netherlands (attributed).
- **Western:** settlers from Late Glacial Cantabrian and SW French refuges spreading north along the Atlantic coastal zone — a hypothesis he says was common in early twentieth-century literature then sidelined by east-focused discussions; SW French Azilian (tenth–ninth millennia) vs earliest Atlantic Scottish Mesolithic dates then in the seventh millennium make gradual littoral migration a distinct possibility (position + chronological finding).
- **Ireland:** separation from the mainland ~9000 BC; initial colonization early seventh millennium, roughly contemporary with west Scotland, densest early sites in the NE (Antrim/Down landfalls; Bann valley); later Mesolithic (c. 5500–c. 4000 BC) still NE-focused, with flint **blades** rather than microliths distinguishing Irish later Mesolithic from Britain’s, as if the widening Irish Sea became a barrier while North Channel still linked Ireland and the Inner Hebrides (attributed).

### Mobility, boats, and exchange

- **Boats:** earliest known log boats Mesolithic in France, Netherlands, Denmark; Britain’s earliest known log boat fourth millennium BC; Tybrind Vig log boats with hearths may imply extended time away from land; hide boats on light frames assumed from at least the Mesolithic though no physical remains (leather-working and fish traps show technologies available); Greenland umiaks offered as ethnographic analogy for prehistoric Atlantic skin boats (analogy flagged) (attributed).
- **Catchment ≠ territory:** ability to gather all necessary resources within ~10 km of Mount Sandel does not mean communities restricted themselves to that radius; longer journeys for rare resources, social intercourse, or exploration remain likely; Pacific Coast multi-day feasting journeys cited as analogy for long-distance social travel (theoretical + analogy) (attributed).
- **Lithic networks (Atlantic Scotland):** Rhum bloodstone at twenty-two other sites up to ~70 km from Bloodstone Hill; three overlapping networks — Rhum bloodstone, Arran pitchstone, Skye mudstone — showing the sea as connective tissue (finding).
- **Southern Britain:** Portland (Dorset) chert and Cornwall–Devon slate/pebbles on Mesolithic sites across central southern Britain including the Isle of Wight — social transmission between hunting bands, perhaps at annual gatherings (finding). Cunliffe later treats desire for rare commodities (Mesolithic Portland chert / Rhum bloodstone; later Alpine jadeite) as ancient and socially articulating (theoretical).

### Diet, landscape, and management

- **Isotopes:** C and N stable isotopes in bone collagen reflect diet over the last 5–15 years of life; early Danish study showed coastal Mesolithic high maritime plant percentages while later Neolithic preferred land plants despite largely unchanged environment — cultural preference (finding as reported).
- **Fire and prey:** ample fire clearance evidence at [[star-carr]] phases and in upland pollen; burning can raise ungulate biomass up to tenfold; feed piles as possible first stage toward red-deer domestication (position).
- **Footprints:** Severn (Goldcliff) and Mersey (Formby Point) estuary muds preserve human and animal tracks (deer, aurochs, horse, dog/wolf, wild boar, birds); Formby study suggested women/children near water collecting shellfish while men moved faster near forest edge after deer; stature estimates ~1.45–1.65 m (finding as reported).
- **Population:** density-based estimates of 3,000–20,000 he calls a considerable underestimate given resource richness; gradual colonization via Atlantic seaways and Doggerland, growth possibly to several hundred thousand by period’s end, with early warfare and local extinction risks (position).

### Belief, disposal of the dead, and monuments

Cunliffe holds Mesolithic communities would not have drawn sharp secular/religious or physical/spirit divides; supernatural demands were as pressing as hunger (theoretical). At [[star-carr]] he reads complex red-deer (and probably elk) related ritual, frontlet headdresses, exceptional barbed-point counts, and lake deposition as propitiation at a preferred spiritual place (position). **Excarnation** was probably widely practised and explains rarity of complete inhumations; complete burial may have been reserved for the dangerous or malign (position). Gough’s Cave (Cheddar) young adult male, eighth millennium bc, among few complete skeletons; lesion above the right eye may have marked him as unclean (speculation). Aveline’s Hole: remains of >50 individuals (mostly disarticulated), red ochre, perforated teeth, fossil ammonites; deaths concentrated 8400–8200 bc (finding). Stonehenge car-park row of three (possibly four) large pine post-holes treated as a true Mesolithic monument ~10,000 years ago (finding as reported; see [[stonehenge]]).

### Genetics and ancestry claims (date-stamped)

**As of Cunliffe 2013** (modern-DNA phylogeography and mutation-rate literature he summarizes — not a 2020s aDNA consensus):

- Broad geneticist agreement that a high percentage of modern British/Irish population traces ancestry to recolonization between end of LGM and start of Neolithic; archaeology holds islands largely empty in Younger Dryas then recolonized from ~9600 bc via Atlantic seaways and [[doggerland]] (reported synthesis).
- He states Mesolithic populations are the ancestors from which a majority of modern British and Irish are descended (bridging claim into DNA section — attributed, not wiki voice).
- Dominant maternal haplotype **H**: north Iberian refuge origin, post-LGM expansion largely along Atlantic seaboard; most British H genes likely via direct Atlantic route, some via North European Plain/Doggerland (as of literature he cites).
- Genetic prominence of the Atlantic route **challenges archaeological preconceptions** that emphasized inward migration across Doggerland (his position on the state of debate).
- Mutation-rate dating places some lines in Mesolithic Britain but some patriarchal/matriarchal lines earlier (e.g. male R1b-9 founding six clusters before Younger Dryas, ~27% of modern men in the account he reports) — implying significant Younger Dryas population if accepted; on archaeological evidence he prefers largely empty land with only summer-straying hunters and asks geneticists to reassess dating margins after 10,000 bc (position vs genetic early-dating).

These claims are **method- and date-stamped**; they do not identify archaeological cultures with gene trees, languages, or peoples.

### Mesolithic–Neolithic interface

Cunliffe frames the nature of the Mesolithic–Neolithic transition in Britain as multi-decade energetic debate swinging invasion → indigenous acculturation → renewed immigration with DNA — core content for [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]], not a settled fact (historiographic finding). Alternative hypothesis he reports: late Mesolithic hunter-gatherers in sea contact with continental neighbours adopted and adapted new lifeways, technologies, and belief systems through existing connectivity — indigenous populations “became Neolithic” by acculturation (position in the debate). Continental comparanda include Dutch coastal foragers gradual adoption 5000–3600 bc and Ertebølle remaining essentially Mesolithic until ~4000–3900 bc (finding as reported). Neolithic society more sedentary than Mesolithic: tilled plots, seed storage, flocks needing winter corralling and possible summer transhumance (theoretical contrast). Irish coastal Mesolithic sites (Ferriter’s Cove and others: Sutton, Dalkey Island, Kilgreany) with domestic animal bones mark pre-Neolithic or contact-edge domesticates in his narrative (finding as reported).

### Semantic note within Cunliffe’s usage

In *Britain Begins* “Mesolithic” is primarily a **British/Irish and NW European period/economy label** tied to post-glacial foragers, not a world-stage theory. Cunliffe’s contribution for this page is dense regional operationalization — corridors, boats, networks, ritual, genetics literature as of 2013, and transition historiography — sitting alongside Clark’s global outline and Mithen’s comparative narrative.

## Facing the Ocean densification (Cunliffe 2001) — Atlantic Mesolithic cemeteries and cemetery-based status

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]]* 2001, Ch. 4 "The Emergence of an Atlantic Identity: 8000–4000 BC." Earlier and independent of the 2013 *Britain Begins* synthesis above; treat as a distinct densification layer citing the earlier book.)*

**Earliest organized cemeteries in Europe.** Cunliffe (2001) states it is among Atlantic Mesolithic foraging groups that careful cemetery burial first becomes evident anywhere in Europe, with no precedent in any preceding period. Known cemeteries: the Ertebølle culture of Denmark/southern Sweden, the Armorican Mesolithic ([[coastal-adaptation|Téviec and Hoédic]]), and the Concheiros/Muge culture near Lisbon. Cemetery sizes vary widely — Cabeço da Arruda (Lisbon area) ≥175 individuals, Skateholm (Denmark) 86, Téviec (Morbihan) 23 — and burials were collective tombs reused over time rather than simultaneous interments, each new burial disturbing earlier ones (Cunliffe 2001).

**Hereditary status interpretation (attributed, contested-by-implication).** Cunliffe (2001) interprets variation in grave-good richness at Téviec (perforated shells, animal teeth, stone pendants, bone pins) as status differentiated by gender and age, and reads elaborately-furnished child burials as evidence that status could be **acquired through birth** — i.e., that these Mesolithic communities had developed a system of hereditary inequality. This is Cunliffe's interpretive reading of grave-good variation, not an independently-converged finding; record as attributed, not wiki voice. See [[hereditary-rank]].

**Violence and red-ochre/antler rites.** At Téviec, Grave K held six individuals around a repeatedly relit hearth; a young man killed by arrows (two embedded in his backbone) received special stone-lined, undisturbed treatment. Red ochre was spread on bodies at sites in Denmark, Morbihan, and Portugal; burials at Skateholm, Bogebakken, Téviec, and Hoédic were accompanied by red deer antler sets, which Cunliffe (2001) speculates may mark shamans as intermediaries with the spirit world (explicitly speculative).

**Stonehenge Mesolithic posts.** Cunliffe (2001) calls the Stonehenge car-park pits — three large pits (1.5–2 m diameter, up to 1.3 m deep) that held upright pine trunks ~0.75 m diameter, erected in the second half of the 8th millennium BCE — "the first truly monumental structure of the Mesolithic period known to us." See [[stonehenge]].

**Population-pressure/Atlantic-mind-set thesis.** Cunliffe's own interpretive thesis for the chapter: rich, reliable maritime/estuarine resources combined with being at the ocean's geographic edge encouraged sedentism, territoriality (midden-as-monument, cemetery practice), and — under population pressure against local holding capacity — both migration and resource intensification (deep-sea fishing, later adoption of agriculture). He cites Graham Clark's hypothesis that fishermen following migratory shoals (hake, cod, haddock, ling) first established Atlantic communication networks, calling it "a compelling argument" without adjudicating it as settled.

**Rejection of Azilian "colonization."** An older hypothesis holding that Azilian (Iberian) populations "colonized" along the Atlantic seaways to explain harpoon-point similarities with the Obanian (western Scotland) is, per Cunliffe (2001), no longer seriously entertained; local mobility and rapid diffusion of ideas among densely-settled coastal communities is preferred.

## Europe in the Neolithic densification (Whittle 1996) — forager sharing ethic and grounding of Neolithic values

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, esp. Preface, Chs. 1–3, 10. Parallel layer to Clark/Mithen/Cunliffe densifications; mid-1990s, pre-aDNA.)*

**Programme claim.** Whittle characterises Neolithic Europe as a way of life **rooted in the forager past** — especially social values of **sharing and integration** — yet distinguishable by material and economic changes *and* by new beliefs about descent, beginnings, and time. Neolithic beliefs and values are **grounded in Mesolithic** practice; old period labels inadequate; in a sense the Neolithic is not so much creation of new worlds as **prolongation of old ones** — yet fundamental conceptual differences need not coincide with 19th-century period labels (Preface; Ch.10). He doubts meaningful separation of Neolithic from Mesolithic by ground/polished stone tools and pottery alone: foragers already had effective cutting, hunting, digging, water/snow travel, nets, baskets, and containers (Ch.1). Prefers values, ideals, and social sanctions — sharing, hospitality, generosity, honour, with shame as sanction — over evolutionary band→tribe→chiefdom ladders (attributed).

**Ethic of sharing.** Cites the generalisation that what distinguishes herders from hunters is not technical knowledge but values — hunter life based on an ethic of **sharing**, herder life on an ethic of **acquisition** — while noting ideal and practice may diverge (Ch.2). Selected body parts of the dead carried by the living (Noyen, Uzzo, Franchthi, submerged Danish coasts), some with cut marks, suggest continuous presence of the dead; a practical ethic of sharing and cooperation may have been reinforced by a conceptual order uniting people and nature, living and dead (attributed). Speculative conceptual shift (Ch.10): many earlier foragers in an undivided timeless world shared with animals (“cosmic economy of sharing”); later foragers and Neolithic generally: categorisation/separation, sense of time and beginnings, sharing within human society + relationships with otherworld — possibly reinforced by **guilt** at breaking earlier bonds with nature / objectifying domesticates (attributed).

**Case vignettes (Ch.2, as of Whittle 1996).**
- **Duvensee 13** (N Germany, between Baltic and Elbe) ~7700 BC: short-stay autumn hazelnut camp — birch bark mat, pine-log fire, flint knapping spalls, smashed hazelnut shells; specialised short-stay gathering on sand elevations in a post-glacial lake landscape (possible few hours to a day or two).
- **Franchthi Cave** (Argolid): only substantial well-investigated post-glacial Mesolithic occupation in Greece in his account; late Palaeolithic from ~20,000 BC; Melian obsidian from ~10,000 BC implying boats (>100 km sea); Mesolithic ~2 m deposits; after ~9000 BC broad-spectrum use; after ~8000 BC more sea use, tunny, more obsidian; sparse other Greek Mesolithic sites. Contrasts with Levantine Natufian/PPN diversification and early permanence. Neolithic shift at Franchthi (wild cereals/deer/pig/tunny → emmer/einkorn/barley and sheep/goats) arguable as newcomers or rapid influence; lithic continuity, continued Melian obsidian, and continued *Cerithium vulgatum* collection cited against total population replacement (attributed).
- **Friesack** (NW of Berlin): lake-edge site used episodically over the Mesolithic — on average about once a generation (intervals ~10 years to a century); fine stratigraphy shows clustered occupation episodes (e.g. ~15 occupations 8700–8450 BC); later visits less frequent, shift spring → late summer/autumn; inland foragers documented to mid sixth millennium BC (attributed). Related mobility vocabulary: circulating vs radiating mobility under changing conditions (also Noyen; upper Danube late Mesolithic site decline possibly reflecting radiating mobility leaving fewer base sites).

**Indigenous foragers as agents of Neolithic change (position).** Third model for SE Europe: mobile flexible foragers with sharing ethic, learning settlement/subsistence changes via sea voyages (tunny, obsidian), adopting herded animals and cultivated plants initially **to support the existing social ethic** and broaden resources — transition as enhancement of existing social system, not radical break (Ch.3). Coastal Brittany’s first monument builders treated as forager descendants (Ch.1). Mediterranean: pots and sheep may have been adopted first as novelties complementing wide-ranging hunting/foraging and bolstering established food-sharing traditions (Ch.1; Ch.8). Synthesis: forager ethic of sharing/incorporation **continued**; cultivation, herding, material culture as new means to existing ends — facilitate sharing and gatherings; celebrate provision of food/drink and group identity display. Mesolithic–Neolithic transition less momentous than social-evolutionary narratives suggest; technological-economic factors not of major importance given forager choice (Ch.10) (attributed). Mid-third-millennium northern Poland/Baltic mosaic (e.g. Chwalim) may still include foragers of Mesolithic descent little affected by surrounding change; Pitted Ware as possible Mesolithic-descent or forager-like reversion (Ch.7) (attributed).
