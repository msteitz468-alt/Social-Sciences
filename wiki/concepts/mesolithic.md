# Mesolithic

## Definition as Coined

**Mesolithic** ("Middle Stone Age") labels the archaeological cultures of the early Holocene (and, in some older usages, late Pleistocene) that succeed Advanced / Upper Palaeolithic hunting traditions and precede — or locally coexist with — the spread of farming. In *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961), [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] treats Mesolithic communities as a major world-prehistoric theme: SW Asian groups at the threshold of domestication, European Maglemosian and coastal foragers, and expansions across northern Eurasia (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 3).

The term is **not** a universal stage: regions without a distinct microlithic/post-glacial forager horizon are not forced into it.

## Semantic History

- **European prehistory:** fills the chronological gap between Palaeolithic and Neolithic once the three-age system and glacial chronology matured.
- **Clark 1961:** embeds Mesolithic in an economic–ecological narrative (forest clearance thresholds, coastal resources, contact with Neolithic farmers) rather than pure typology.
- **Later:** absolute dating and "Epipalaeolithic" / "Archaic" regional vocabularies (Near East, Americas) often replace or parallel "Mesolithic"; the European Maglemosian remains a classic type-case.

## Operationalizations

Clark (attributed, 1961): post-glacial / Neothermal forager economies; microlithic and specialized coastal toolkits; Maglemosian as northern European exemplar; SW Asian "Mesolithic" groups discussed in relation to early farming at Jericho and related sites.

## Applications

Period tag for early Holocene foragers in Europe and as a comparative label in Clark's global outline; links to [[economic-prehistory]] via Star Carr and Maglemosian subsistence reconstruction (Clark's own research tradition).

## Critiques and Limitations

Europe-centric label exported unevenly; blurs into Epipalaeolithic in the Near East; can imply a necessary evolutionary stage between Palaeolithic and Neolithic rather than regionally contingent adaptations; absolute chronology in 1961 sources is pre-calibration.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Post-YD Holocene foragers:** after 9600 BC warming, European early Holocene hunter-fisher-gatherers (Maglemosian-type woodlands; coastal catastrophes/Doggerland) occupy the Mesolithic slot in Mithen’s narrative; Near Eastern “Epipalaeolithic” (Kebaran → Natufian) is the SW Asian parallel, not forced under the European label (attributed).
- **Star Carr:** among Europe’s key early Mesolithic sites; first European site of any period radiocarbon-dated; [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] 1949–51 excavation shifted British Mesolithic studies from tool catalogues to lifestyle and environment (waterlogged bone/antler/wood) (attributed, ch. 15).
- **Early vs late cultural geography:** early Mesolithic virtual cultural identity across northern Europe (Star Carr horizon) fractures by late Mesolithic into marked identities (clothes, harpoons, axe forms) (attributed).
- **Complex Mesolithic cases:** [[lepenski-vir|Lepenski Vir]] sedentary Iron Gates fishermen ~6400 BC; Oleneostrovski, Skateholm, Ertebølle, Oronsay as complex HG with cemeteries/aggregation before Neolithic absorption (attributed, chs. 18–20).
- **Labour contrast:** Mesolithic groups “seemed to work only a few hours a day” because knowledge filled the stomach vs Nea Nikomedeia hard agricultural labour — original-affluent-society theme in Mithen’s voice (position).
- **Farming interface:** Balkan leap-frog farmers first stimulate then collapse Lepenski Vir culture; generational resistance vs seduction by farming (attributed).
