# Artifact

An **artifact** is a **humanly made or modified portable object** — stone tools, pottery, metal weapons, and the ordinary refuse of daily life that constitutes most of the [[archaeological-record]]. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 open their evidence chapter by contrasting rare monumental constructions with this modest discarded debris, and by insisting that an artifact’s scientific value depends on **context**, not aesthetic isolation.

## Definition as Coined

Textbook definition (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 2, attributed):

> Artifacts are humanly made or modified **portable** objects, such as stone tools, pottery, and metal weapons.

**Portability** is the key differentiator from [[archaeological-feature|features]] (hearths, postholes, storage pits), which some researchers would fold into a broadened “artifact” category but which Renfrew & Bahn prefer to keep separate as non-portable artifacts. [[ecofact|Ecofacts]] are non-artifactual organic/environmental remains. Together with structures, they co-occur at [[archaeological-site|sites]].

## Semantic History

- **Antiquarian / early archaeology:** Artifacts as collectible curios and type-fossils for relative chronology; association with extinct fauna established high human antiquity in sealed deposits.
- **Culture history:** Artifacts (esp. ceramics and lithics) index [[archaeological-culture|archaeological cultures]] and horizons.
- **Behavioral / formation-process turn:** Artifacts modeled through a **life cycle** — (1) raw-material acquisition, (2) manufacture, (3) use and distribution, (4) disposal/discard, with possible recycle — and may enter the record at any stage (Renfrew & Bahn 2012). Stage identification is required for behavioral reconstruction.
- **Cognitive and material-culture approaches:** Artifacts as extensions of mind, value scales, and chaîne opératoire (treated elsewhere in the textbook’s cognition and technology chapters).
- **Market and ethics:** Decontextualized artifacts fuel the illicit antiquities trade; unlabeled provenience on display often signals illicit origin.

## Operationalizations

| Concern | Practice |
|---|---|
| Context | Record matrix, provenience (X,Y,Z), associations |
| Classification | Typology, attribute analysis, assemblage composition |
| Technology | Manufacture sequences, experimental replication, use-wear |
| Content | Residue chemistry, DNA, isotopes on tools/vessels |
| Source | Trace elements; Pb/Sr/O isotopes for metals, marble, etc. |
| Formation stage | Quarry waste vs finished tool vs curated heirloom vs secondary refuse |

Primary vs secondary context: looters and natural forces (river gravels for Paleolithic tools) routinely shift artifacts from original deposition.

## Applications

- Everyday refuse as evidence of diet, craft, and household practice.
- Hoards and grave-goods as deliberate deposition (with votive vs recover-later ambiguity).
- Cross-dating and seriation; association for contemporaneity in undisturbed primary context.
- Exchange and characterization studies.
- Cognitive inferences from design standardization and manufacturing complexity (textbook Ch. 10).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Looting destroys matrix, provenience, and association** — “all the contextual information is lost” (Renfrew & Bahn 2012); a collector’s vase is poor social evidence.
- Broadening “artifact” to all anthropogenic landscape modifications collapses the useful portable/non-portable distinction.
- Museum aesthetic display can divorce objects from historical context as “works of art,” enabling unethical acquisition.
- Stage misidentification biases economic inference (e.g. charred plants from threshing floor vs occupation floor).
- Most artifacts are fragmentary refuse, not intentional messages; interpretation requires formation-process control.
