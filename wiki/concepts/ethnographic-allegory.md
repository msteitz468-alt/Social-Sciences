# Ethnographic Allegory

Clifford’s claim in "On Ethnographic Allegory" (*[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]*, 1986) that ethnographies function as **allegories** — stories that say one thing and mean another, carrying moral, political, and theoretical messages beyond their descriptive surface.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Clifford 1986.)*

Allegory as presentation of ideas by means of stories of people, things, and happenings; ethnographies always allegorical. Related pastoral and salvage modes are temporal allegories of disappearing or redeemable worlds.

## Semantic History

Draws on literary theories of allegory; applied to Mead/Freeman, Shostak *Nisa*, and classical monographs. Rosaldo’s pastoral analysis of *Montaillou*/*Nuer* is a parallel rhetoric of idealization.

## Operationalizations

Reading ethnographies for second-order narrative structures (pastoral, tragic, comic, feminist recognition, initiation).

## Applications

- Mead Samoa / Freeman as **diptych allegories** of cultural plasticity vs biological constraint ([[mead-freeman-debate]]).
- Shostak *Nisa* as multi-register allegory (ethnographic, personal, feminist).
- Salvage as allegory of disappearing worlds; textualization as allegory of conquest and initiation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of reducing ethnography to pure literature; see [[writing-culture-debate]]. Clifford’s ethics: allegories must be owned, not denied under realism’s mask.
