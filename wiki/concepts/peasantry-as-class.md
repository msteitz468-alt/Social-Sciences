# Peasantry as Class

## Definition as Coined

In Ch. VII of *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852), [[marx-karl|Marx]] analyzes French **allotment farmers** (parcel/smallholding peasantry; De Leon English) as **both a class and not a class**:

- **A class** insofar as millions of families live under economic conditions that separate their mode of life, interests, and culture from other classes and place them in hostility toward the latter.
- **Not a class** insofar as only local connection exists among them; individuality of interests prevents “any unity of interest, national connections, and political organization.”

Image: the large mass of the French nation is “the simple addition of equal magnitudes—much as a bag with potatoes constitutes a potato-bag.” Consequence: “They can not represent one another, they must themselves be represented” — by a master-like Executive that “bestows rain and sunshine upon them.” Hence their political influence culminates in autocratic executive power; the Bonapartes are “the dynasty of the farmer.”

## Semantic History

Foundational for Marxist agrarian class theory and for debates on peasant politics (representability, clientelism, populism). The “potato-bag” / “sack of potatoes” image is among the most cited (and criticized) metaphors in peasant studies. Later agrarian sociology, anthropology of the state, and revisionist histories of 1848–51 France contest, refine, or reject the formula (secondary literature not yet ingested). Distinct from industrial [[proletariat]] and from [[class-fraction|bourgeois fractions]].

**Wolf 1966 (*[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]*).** [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]] cites Marx’s sack-of-potatoes formula in the coalitions chapter as one position on peasant class unity, while his own definition of [[peasant]] is structural-political-economic (surplus transfer under domain), not primarily about national representability. Wolf’s [[peasant-coalition]] typology and analysis of [[peasant-movements]] (organizational deficit; revolution *with* not *for* peasantry) form a mid-century anthropological counterpoint: local coalitions and external professional staff, not the potato-bag’s pure isolation.

## Operationalizations

- Isolation of production (little division of labor or science; exchange with nature over society).
- Mortgage/capital burden on small allotments; taxation as lifeblood of bureaucracy and army (Marx’s follow-on argument).
- Conservative vs revolutionary farmer: Bonaparte dynasty represents the former (confirm allotment; Vendée past) not the latter (press beyond allotment with the cities).
- Electoral base: 10 December 1848 election fulfilled, Marx claims, only by the 2 December 1851 coup.

## Applications

- [[bonapartism]] and [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]] (government “does not float in the air”).
- Comparative peasantry and state formation (to be linked when agrarian sources are ingested).
- Limits of two-class *Manifesto* schematic for predominantly agrarian societies.
- **Peasant insurrectionary capacity** ([[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]] 1979): where Marx's "potato-bag" peasantry cannot organize nationally, Skocpol argues (attributed) that *local* community solidarity and autonomy from landlords — the French village, the Russian obshchina — nonetheless give peasants the leverage to revolt autonomously when state coercion collapses, whereas the gentry-fragmented Chinese peasantry required outside (CCP) organization. The unit of peasant agency is the village, not the nation. See [[social-revolution]] and [[france-russia-china-revolutions]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Underestimates rural organization** and regional variation (Jacqueries, later peasant leagues — positions for future debate pages).
- **French smallholder specificity** — not a universal peasant model.
- **Representation thesis** can be read as denying peasant agency; Marx’s own text also records farmer uprisings and army repression after the coup.
