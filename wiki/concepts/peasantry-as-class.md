# Peasantry as Class

## Definition as Coined

In Ch. VII of *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852), [[marx-karl|Marx]] analyzes French **allotment farmers** (parcel/smallholding peasantry; De Leon English) as **both a class and not a class**:

- **A class** insofar as millions of families live under economic conditions that separate their mode of life, interests, and culture from other classes and place them in hostility toward the latter.
- **Not a class** insofar as only local connection exists among them; individuality of interests prevents “any unity of interest, national connections, and political organization.”

Image: the large mass of the French nation is “the simple addition of equal magnitudes—much as a bag with potatoes constitutes a potato-bag.” Consequence: “They can not represent one another, they must themselves be represented” — by a master-like Executive that “bestows rain and sunshine upon them.” Hence their political influence culminates in autocratic executive power; the Bonapartes are “the dynasty of the farmer.”

## Semantic History

Foundational for Marxist agrarian class theory and for debates on peasant politics (representability, clientelism, populism). The “potato-bag” / “sack of potatoes” image is among the most cited (and criticized) metaphors in peasant studies. Later agrarian sociology, anthropology of the state, and revisionist histories of 1848–51 France contest, refine, or reject the formula (secondary literature not yet ingested). Distinct from industrial [[proletariat]] and from [[class-fraction|bourgeois fractions]].

**Wolf 1966 (*[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]*).** [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]] cites Marx’s sack-of-potatoes formula in the coalitions chapter as one position on peasant class unity, while his own definition of [[peasant]] is structural-political-economic (surplus transfer under domain), not primarily about national representability. Wolf’s [[peasant-coalition]] typology and analysis of [[peasant-movements]] (organizational deficit; revolution *with* not *for* peasantry) form a mid-century anthropological counterpoint: local coalitions and external professional staff, not the potato-bag’s pure isolation.

## Operationalizations

- Isolation of production (little division of labor or science; exchange with nature over society).
- Mortgage/capital burden on small allotments; taxation as lifeblood of bureaucracy and army (Marx’s follow-on argument).
- Conservative vs revolutionary farmer: Bonaparte dynasty represents the former (confirm allotment; Vendée past) not the latter (press beyond allotment with the cities).
- Electoral base: 10 December 1848 election fulfilled, Marx claims, only by the 2 December 1851 coup.

## Applications

- [[bonapartism]] and [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]] (government “does not float in the air”).
- Comparative peasantry and state formation (to be linked when agrarian sources are ingested).
- Limits of two-class *Manifesto* schematic for predominantly agrarian societies.
- **Peasant insurrectionary capacity** ([[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]] 1979): where Marx's "potato-bag" peasantry cannot organize nationally, Skocpol argues (attributed) that *local* community solidarity and autonomy from landlords — the French village, the Russian obshchina — nonetheless give peasants the leverage to revolt autonomously when state coercion collapses, whereas the gentry-fragmented Chinese peasantry required outside (CCP) organization. The unit of peasant agency is the village, not the nation. See [[social-revolution]] and [[france-russia-china-revolutions]].
- **Mao (attributed, via *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations]]*):** peasant class struggles and peasant wars as "the real motive force of historical development in Chinese feudal society"; industrial proletariat as leading force, semi-proletariat and peasantry as closest friends; co-operative socialism relies on poor and lower-middle peasants, unites middle peasants; "the serious problem is the education of the peasantry." Program frame [[maoism]]; not ethnography.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Underestimates rural organization** and regional variation (Jacqueries, later peasant leagues — positions for future debate pages).
- **French smallholder specificity** — not a universal peasant model.
- **Representation thesis** can be read as denying peasant agency; Marx’s own text also records farmer uprisings and army repression after the coup.

## Wolf stratification (1969)

*[[wolf-peasant-wars-1969|Peasant Wars]]* does not resolve Marx’s representation problem by declaring peasantry *a* class subject. Wolf disaggregates **[[middle-peasant]]**, rich peasant, poor/landless, and free/peripheral poor inside power domains ([[tactical-mobility]]). Revolutionary capacity is stratum- and field-specific. See [[wolf-peasant-wars-study]] · [[peasant-movements]].

## Plekhanov 1885: the commune disintegrating (attributed)

The differentiation analysis a decade before Lenin's: [[plekhanov-our-differences-1885|Our Differences]] documented the village commune's dissolution under money economy and redemption payments into kulaks and "airy people" — against the Narodnik commune-to-socialism hope, with Herzen's and Chernyshevsky's positions quoted and answered. Plekhanov's 1905 footnote stratum retracts his own earlier commune-as-primitive-communism equation (dated self-correction, recorded as such).

## Lenin 1917: internal differentiation of the peasantry (attributed)

Where Marx's 1852 "potato-bag" treats the French smallholder peasantry as a largely undifferentiated mass, Lenin's 1917 agrarian writings (*[[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964|Collected Works, Vol. 24]]*) insisted on **internal class differentiation within the peasantry itself**. His "Political Parties in Russia" catechism, on the land question, called for separate organization of agricultural wage-workers and poor peasants apart from wealthier peasants, on the grounds that "the wealthy peasants are also capitalists" liable to "wrong or cheat" agricultural laborers. His agrarian programme for the April Conference proposed: immediate, uncompensated confiscation of landed, crown, and church estates; land use transferred to peasants organized in Soviets of Peasants' Deputies; nationalisation of land (state ownership, local democratic disposal); and — the differentiating measure — organizing agricultural laborers and poor peasants into **separate groups within every peasant committee**, plus converting large confiscated estates into "model farms" for common cultivation under **Soviets of Agricultural Labourers' Deputies**, using the landowners' own animals and implements and scientifically trained agronomists. He argued this differentiation was necessary because "voluntary agreements" for land rental in practice favored richer peasants who could pay, and rejected the Narodnik "labour standard"/"subsistence standard" land-tenure norms as a "bureaucrat's plan" that ignored how uneven distribution of capital, animals, and implements — not land area alone — structures exploitation of hired labor by rich peasants.

The formally adopted April Conference **Resolution on the Agrarian Question** predicted this reform would accelerate class struggle between the "**rural (agricultural) proletariat**" and "the well-to-do peasants (**the peasant bourgeoisie**)," and held that the outcome of the Russian revolution depended on whether the urban proletariat could rally the rural proletariat and semi-proletarians, or whether the peasant bourgeoisie instead allied with counter-revolution. The resolution explicitly characterized full agrarian nationalisation — sweeping away landed-estate ("semi-feudal") property relations — as **"a bourgeois measure"** that nonetheless dealt "a powerful blow" to private ownership of the means of production generally; Lenin argued elsewhere that Russian peasants' historic demand for nationalisation (via the Trudovik "Bill of the 104" in the First and Second Dumas) reflected not socialist "consciousness" (as the Socialist-Revolutionaries claimed) but a bourgeois-democratic urge of small proprietors to clear away pre-capitalist landownership, not an equalising impulse among proprietors. He cited U.S. post-1865 land distribution to freed slaves as evidence — "the socialist teaching," drawn from study of West European nations — that mere land distribution to a peasantry does not by itself prevent later capitalist domination, an argument for common/collective over individual free-soil cultivation.

Politically, Lenin held (1917) that "the dictatorship of the proletariat and the peasantry" was "**won, not proclaimed**" and represented the vast majority (workers and poor peasants), with a close urban-worker/poor-peasant alliance necessary because land transfer alone — without capital, credit, and implements — could not lift peasants out of poverty. He also argued (against a blanket "counter-revolutionary" characterization of the Provisional Government urged by a Moscow Bolshevik resolution) that it was premature to assume the peasantry would necessarily go further than the bourgeoisie in a proletarian/peasant revolution — the bourgeois revolution being, in his view, already substantially complete.

## Lenin 1920–1923: worker-peasant alliance under War Communism and NEP (attributed)

*[[lenin-left-wing-communism-1920|"Left-Wing" Communism]]* (1920) recorded, as historical fact per Lenin, that the Bolsheviks had systematically advocated a worker-peasant alliance against the liberal bourgeoisie and tsarism since 1905, and that at the moment of the October Revolution the Party adopted the Socialist-Revolutionary agrarian programme wholesale, as a deliberate compromise to win peasant trust — sealed by a formal governing bloc with the Left SRs that broke after Brest-Litovsk. Lenin listed among the historical conditions that made socialist revolution comparatively easy to *start* in Russia in 1917 a "profound bourgeois-democratic revolutionary movement" among the peasantry, which allowed the Bolsheviks to adopt SR peasant demands directly — conditions he said did not hold in Western Europe.

In the *[[lenin-last-writings-1923|Last Writings]]* (1922–23), Lenin identified the Soviet social order as resting on "the **collaboration of two classes**," workers and peasants, with the "Nepmen" bourgeoisie permitted limited participation under the [[new-economic-policy]]; he warned that a worker-peasant split — potentially engineered by Nepmen driving a wedge between the two classes — would be "fatal for the Soviet Republic." He argued Soviet internal work was shifting from political/revolutionary struggle toward "peaceful, organisational, 'cultural' work" — chiefly educational work among the peasantry aimed at organizing peasants into co-operatives — and reversed his earlier dismissal of Owenite co-operative socialism, arguing that under proletarian state power the mere growth of co-operation "is identical with the growth of socialism," which he explicitly called "a radical modification in our whole outlook on socialism." He held Russia's fate hinged on whether the "small and very small peasantry" remained under the political leadership of the working class, given low labor productivity and post-war/post-Civil-War international isolation. He also cited a c.1856 letter of Marx expressing hope of combining a German peasant war with the working-class movement, analogizing it to Russia's actual 1917 combination of peasant war and proletarian revolution.

## Lenin 1894: statistical reanalysis and the "break-up" of the peasantry (attributed)

In *[[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894|What the "Friends of the People" Are]]* (1894), Lenin's earliest sustained argument for the concept reworked official Zemstvo statistics on 24 peasant households in Ostrogozhsk Uyezd (Voronezh Gubernia), replacing Narodnik "legal categories" (former state vs. former landlords' peasants) with **economic categories** (prosperous/middle/poor) and finding income/expenditure ratios near 10:2:1 rather than the roughly 3:2 ratio the legal-category averages implied. The six "prosperous" households all employed hired labor and ran a net income; the eleven "middle" households mostly broke even or ran deficits; the seven "poor" households farmed at a loss and derived increasing shares of income from "industries" (sale of labor-power). He concluded this was "a process of the complete differentiation of the small producers, the upper groups of whom are being turned into a bourgeoisie, the lower into a proletariat," extending the same method to draught-animal ownership across the whole uyezd (33,581 households: roughly 10% prosperous/hiring labor, 57% poor and increasingly wage-dependent) to refute the Narodnik claim that land-poverty, not commodity-economy differentiation, was the cause. He extended the same "capitalist-relations-even-in-the-village" argument to Russian handicraft/"domestic system" industry via statistics on the Moscow-region brick, pottery, and Pavlovo cutlery trades.

## Lenin 1899: the full differentiation schema — "depeasantising" (attributed)

*[[lenin-development-of-capitalism-in-russia-1899|The Development of Capitalism in Russia]]* (1899) is Lenin's full-length statistical elaboration of the differentiation thesis sketched five years earlier in *What the "Friends of the People" Are* (1894), and substantially upgrades the wiki's earlier excerpts-based coverage of this concept. Working chiefly from Zemstvo house-to-house census data (Postnikov's Taurida material; Perm, Krasnoufimsk, and other district returns), Lenin argued the post-Reform peasantry was not merely becoming *unequal* but was undergoing a process the peasants themselves, he reported, called "**depeasantising**" (*raskrestyanivanie*) — the dissolution of the old undifferentiated patriarchal peasantry and its replacement by two structurally new class types, with the "middle" peasantry squeezed out between them ("the middle members are swept away and the extremes are reinforced"). He estimated a **rural bourgeoisie** of "probably not more than one-fifth" of peasant households (roughly three-tenths of the population) — independent commodity-farming households combining relatively large-scale crop farming with commercial and industrial enterprises, renting substantial land beyond their allotments, and employing hired labor; and an allotment-holding **rural proletariat** of "not less than half" of households (the horseless and, largely, one-horse households), whose main livelihood was sale of labor-power and whose standard of living he argued approximated that of a landless hired farm laborer. He argued the peasant bourgeoisie, though a numerical minority, was "undoubtedly predominant" in total means of production and produce — "the masters of the contemporary countryside" — citing figures such as the top stratum holding 92.8% of recorded reaping/mowing machines in the Taurida returns he cites. Lenin also described the peasant bourgeoisie as the point where "the threads both of merchant's/usurer's capital ... and of industrial capital ... merge," explicitly refuting the Narodnik separation of "kulak/usurer" from "enterprising muzhik" as two distinct types.

**Classification methodology (defended against critics).** Lenin insisted differentiation was visible only when households were classified by **economic strength** (draught animals, sown area) rather than by land-allotment size, because actual land use was disturbed by leasing, purchase, renting, and non-agricultural income; classifying by allotment, he argued, "lump[ed] together the rural proletarian and the members of the rural bourgeoisie," producing "purely fictitious" averages. In the 1900 polemic "Uncritical Criticism" (against P. Skvortsov), Lenin defended this methodological choice at length, citing his own finding that allotment land was distributed comparatively evenly (the top 20% of households held only 29–36% of allotment land in various uyezds) while draught animals, sown area, and implements were distributed far less evenly — i.e., that the allotment obscures exactly the differentiation that economic-strength classification reveals.

**The dual home-market mechanism.** Lenin argued the two poles of differentiation feed the home market in complementary ways: the transformation of poor peasants into a rural proletariat creates a market chiefly for **articles of consumption** (labor-power becoming a commodity), while the transformation of well-to-do peasants into a rural bourgeoisie creates a market chiefly for **means of production** (means of production becoming capital) — jointly instantiating, at the level of the Russian peasantry, the general home-market theory he laid out in Ch. I (see [[mode-of-production]]). He cited Engels's remark that the Russian famine of 1891 signified "the creation of a home market for capitalism" as illustrating a point he judged unintelligible to Narodnik economists.

**The 1907 preface: the "dual role" of the peasantry.** In the Second-Edition Preface (July 1907), written after the 1905 Revolution, Lenin reread this same differentiated peasantry politically as having a "**dual position and dual role**": (1) as a whole, survivals of corvée/serfdom and mass impoverishment made the peasant movement revolutionary; (2) internally, the same petty-bourgeois differentiation documented in 1899 — the antagonism of "proprietor" and "proletarian" trends within the peasantry — produced political vacillation of the "impoverished small master" between the counter-revolutionary bourgeoisie and the revolutionary proletariat. This reading anticipates the later "two-stage alliance" framework of 1918 (below) and should be read alongside the [[mode-of-production]] page's note on the Junker-vs-revolutionary agrarian paths Lenin set out in the same preface.

## Lenin 1918: two-stage alliance theory against Kautsky (attributed)

In *[[lenin-proletarian-revolution-renegade-kautsky-1918|The Proletarian Revolution and the Renegade Kautsky]]*, Lenin restated a **two-stage theory** of the peasant alliance against Kautsky's charge that the Bolsheviks had abandoned Marxism by favoring the peasantry: first, alliance with "the whole" of the peasantry against tsarism and the landlords (the bourgeois-democratic stage, 1905 and Feb.–Oct. 1917), then alliance with the poor peasants and semi-proletarians against capitalism and the rural kulaks (the socialist stage, from late 1918) — warning against erecting "an artificial Chinese Wall" between the two stages. He argued the small peasant producer "inevitably vacillates between the proletariat and the bourgeoisie," confirmed (he claimed) by "the whole modern history of Europe," and described the concrete mechanism of the socialist stage: armed worker detachments sent from cities to requisition surplus grain from kulaks under the grain monopoly, splitting proceeds between the urban population and the poor peasantry, organized increasingly through **Poor Peasants' Committees**, which he said grew alongside a wave of kulak revolts and a "proletarian" revolution spreading into the countryside itself during 1918.

## Lenin 1921: the tax in kind and accepted kulak revival under NEP (attributed)

In the Tenth Congress speeches and *[[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921|The Tax in Kind]]* (spring 1921), Lenin identified the peasantry, in its now-predominant "middle peasant" composition, as the decisive majority whose economic satisfaction was necessary to preserve Soviet power pending world revolution — stating that "a greater proportion of peasants in Russia have become middle peasants" and that laboring peasants made up "nine-tenths" of the population the Bolshevik government represented. Replacing surplus-grain appropriation (prodrazvyorstka) with a fixed tax roughly half the prior rate, announced in advance each spring, Lenin explicitly accepted that permitting free local exchange "means encouraging the kulaks" and "will mean more kulaks under the new system," arguing this should be countered not by prohibitive measures but by state-sponsored association, machinery, and electrification rather than administrative suppression. He restated his five-tier schema of coexisting "socio-economic structures" — patriarchal peasant economy, small commodity production, private capitalism, state capitalism, socialism — with peasant Russia's economy overwhelmingly weighted toward the first two (see [[mode-of-production]]), and, addressing the Kronstadt mutiny, characterized the crisis as "the most striking expression" of the "vacillation" rooted in "the very nature of the small producer" amid acute agricultural crisis (crop failure, cattle loss, war ruin).

## Kautsky 1918: the peasant-state prognosis (attributed)

The counter-position to Lenin's alliance strategy, from [[kautsky-dictatorship-of-the-proletariat-1918]]: because peasants form the great majority of Russia and "do not form a class able itself to govern," a Soviet-type "dictatorship of the proletariat" logically becomes a dictatorship *of the peasants* — or of a shrinking urban minority ruling against them. Kautsky held peasant–proletarian alliance durable only "so long as feudalism exists": once the revolution secures peasant private property, the peasant's market interest (high food prices) sets him against the urban worker, and the new antagonism — not socialism — is "the real legacy of the Russian Revolution." He predicted peasants persuadable to collective agriculture only by demonstration, never by theory, and noted (citing Masloff) that no partition of Russia's land could raise the general peasant standard without higher productive forms. Lenin's 1921–23 texts (tax in kind, "On Co-operation") wrestle with substantially the same structural problem from the other side — a convergence worth reading across [[new-economic-policy]].
