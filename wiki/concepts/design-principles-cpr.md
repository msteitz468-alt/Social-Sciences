# Design Principles for CPR Institutions

*(Attributed to [[ostrom-elinor|Ostrom]], *[[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990|Governing the Commons]]* 1990.)* Empirical-theoretical regularities characterizing **long-enduring, self-organized, self-governed** [[common-pool-resource|CPR]] institutions — not a panacea checklist but a framework for comparing successful and fragile cases.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Ostrom 1990 Ch. 3, Table 3.1 / synthesis of long-enduring cases.)* Principles associated with robust CPR institutions:

1. **Clearly defined boundaries** — of the CPR and of who has rights to withdraw resource units.
2. **Congruence** between appropriation/provision rules and local conditions.
3. **Collective-choice arrangements** — most individuals affected can participate in modifying operational rules.
4. **Monitoring** — monitors who audit CPR conditions and appropriator behavior are accountable to the appropriators, or are the appropriators.
5. **Graduated sanctions** — rule violators face sanctions starting modestly and escalating with seriousness/repeat offenses, by other appropriators or accountable officials.
6. **Conflict-resolution mechanisms** — rapid access to low-cost local arenas to resolve conflicts among appropriators or with officials.
7. **Minimal recognition of rights to organize** — external government authorities do not challenge appropriators’ rights to devise their own institutions.
8. **Nested enterprises** (for CPRs that are parts of larger systems) — appropriation, provision, monitoring, enforcement, conflict resolution, and governance activities organized in multiple layers.

Ostrom presents these as **empirical regularities / working hypotheses**, not necessary and sufficient conditions guaranteed to produce success.

## Semantic History

Induced from alpine, Japanese village, Spanish huerta, and Philippine zanjera cases; later literature tests, extends, and critiques the principles.

## Operationalizations

Presence/absence coding of principles across cases; relation to robustness and failure (Ch. 5).

## Applications

Commons governance design; irrigation reform; community forestry.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of post-hoc fitting; not sufficient conditions; scale and power asymmetries underplayed in early formulation.
