# Double-consciousness

## Definition as Coined

The magazine form **“Strivings of the Negro People”** (1897, *Atlantic Monthly*; reprinted in *[[du-bois-essays|Du Bois: Essays]]*) and *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903), chapter I (“Of Our Spiritual Strivings”), carry the same load-bearing definitions. [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]] named **double-consciousness** as:

> “this sense of always looking at one's self through the eyes of others, of measuring one's soul by the tape of a world that looks on in amused contempt and pity.”

He immediately specified its structure as **two-ness** / **twoness**:

> “One feels his two-ness,—an American, a Negro; two souls, two thoughts, two unreconciled strivings; two warring ideals in one dark body, whose dogged strength alone keeps it from being torn asunder.”

The Negro, he wrote, is a “sort of seventh son, born with a [[the-veil|veil]], and gifted with second-sight in this American world” — a world that yields no true self-consciousness, but only self-seeing through the other world.

**Edition note:** the *Essays* reprint of *Strivings* reads “eight thousand people” in the waste-of-double-aims passage — possible OCR/reprint corruption or Atlantic variant; do not silently “correct” to *Souls* population phrasing without a critical text.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Strivings* (1897) | Magazine form of the spiritual-strivings argument; same double-consciousness core |
| *Souls* ch. I (1903) | Book form; paired with full color-line program |
| *[[conservation-of-races|Conservation of Races]]* (1897) | Dual “Am I an American or a Negro?” dilemma without the double-consciousness label — related structure |
| Later sociology / Black studies | Expanded to identity, standpoint, and “second sight” as epistemic advantage; sometimes loosened to any dual identity |
| Popular use | Vague “divided self” — weaker than Du Bois’s race-structured account |

Du Bois tied the concept to a historical program: the history of the American Negro is the history of this strife — longing for “self-conscious manhood,” to merge into a “better and truer self” without Africanizing America or bleaching the Negro soul. The end of striving: “co-worker in the kingdom of culture.” Incomplete panaceas in sequence: liberty → ballot → book-learning; need **work, culture, and liberty** together under a unifying race ideal (*Strivings*).

## Operationalizations

*Souls* does not operationalize the concept statistically. Later researchers have used survey identity scales, experimental stereotype-threat designs, and qualitative life-history methods — related programs, not equivalent to the 1903 construct. Do not silently equate.

## Applications

- Waste of “double aims” in artisan, minister/doctor, savant, and artist roles under contradictory expectations (*Souls* ch. I)
- Linked to successive post-Emancipation ideals (liberty → ballot → book-learning) and the need to weld work, culture, and liberty
- Paired with [[the-veil]] (barrier and second-sight) and [[the-color-line]] (structural condition)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scope:** Formulated for the American Negro under Jim Crow-era conditions; extension to other groups is analogical and must be argued.
- **Gender:** The “manhood” framing is of its moment.
- **Psychologization risk:** Reducing a structural color-line effect to individual identity conflict loses Du Bois’s institutional chapters (Bureau, church, Black Belt, education).
