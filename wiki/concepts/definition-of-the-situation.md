# Definition of the Situation

## Definition as Coined

In the Methodological Note to *[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|The Polish Peasant]]* Vol I (1918), Thomas and Znaniecki define a **situation** as "the set of values and attitudes with which the individual or the group has to deal in a process of activity and with regard to which this activity is planned and its results appreciated." Every concrete activity is "the solution of a situation." The situation involves three kinds of data (attributed):

1. **Objective conditions** — the totality of values (economic, social, religious, intellectual, etc.) that at the given moment have actual influence on the person's conscious status.
2. **Pre-existing attitudes** — those that actually influence behavior at the moment.
3. **The definition of the situation** — "the more or less clear conception of the conditions and consciousness of the attitudes."

The definition of the situation is "a necessary preliminary to any act of the will," because given conditions and attitudes leave an indefinite plurality of actions possible; one definite action appears only when conditions are selected, interpreted, and combined and attitudes are systematized so that one predominates. Sometimes a value or attitude imposes itself unreflectively; usually there is reflection, after which either a ready **social definition** is applied or a new **personal definition** is worked out (attributed).

The later textbook formula often called the **Thomas theorem** — "If men define situations as real, they are real in their consequences" (Thomas & Thomas, *The Child in America*, 1928 — not yet ingested) — popularizes and hardens a claim already load-bearing in the 1918 Methodological Note. Keep the 1918 triad and the 1928 aphorism periodized.

## Operationalizations

Documentary: recover definitions from letters, life-histories, institutional records. Ethnographic: observe how participants name and frame situations. Survey items sometimes proxy "definitions" poorly; the concept resists pure variable reduction.

## Applications

- Interpreting peasant letters where the same objective event (remittance, marriage proposal, lawsuit) is defined differently by family members.
- Immigrant adjustment and delinquency research in the Chicago tradition.
- Later labeling theory and constructionist programs (not yet ingested).
- [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s *[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]* (1956): individuals influence others' definitions by [[impression-management|managing impressions]]; teams sustain a fostered definition; disruptions produce micro-anomie of face-to-face systems ([[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of **subjective idealism** if objective constraints are ignored — Thomas & Znaniecki themselves insisted on pairing attitudes with social values (objective cultural data).
- Aphoristic overuse of the 1928 theorem can erase the fuller 1918 methodological apparatus.
