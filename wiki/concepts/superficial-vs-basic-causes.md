# Superficial vs. Basic Causes

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]* (1985, esp. chs. 7, 9–10), [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] distinguished **superficial** from **basic** causes. Superficial causes are the covariates that appear in ordinary multivariate tables — attributes associated with Y, often manipulable in imagination, that **maintain or track** the outcome without generating the system that produces it. Basic causes are the deeper drivers that make the observed configuration of independent variables what it is; altering only superficial causes leaves the generative process intact.

He argued (attributed) that standard control-variable analysis **lumps both together**, so "net effects" and policy levers derived from them often address the wrong level. Analytic reorientation: first ask whether/how much Y occurs; then what drives the system that leads Y to occur — which may differ from which X's precede Y in particular contexts. Presence or absence of particular X's is itself generated by more basic causes.

## Semantic History

The vocabulary is Lieberson's methodological use, not a standard econometric pair. Related older ideas include standing causes vs. occasions, structure vs. precipitants, and (in other traditions) ultimate vs. proximate causes. Lieberson's target is specifically the **mock-experiment control table** that treats every partialed covariate as if it were a candidate intervention of the same causal depth. Pseudocausality — associations that look causal under controls but are not basic — is part of the same diagnosis (race–income illustrations in the book are design examples).

Physical analogies he offered (attributed): rivers flow generally downward — other factors operate under that constraint; evolutionary competition constrains directions of change — studying species change without the underlying principle misleads. Gravity vs. variance in falling speeds (ch. 5) is the companion point: factors that explain **variation** in an outcome are not necessarily the basic cause of the outcome's existence.

## Operationalizations

- **Policy test**: if reversing or equalizing a covariate fails to move Y as the net-effect model promised, suspect superficial status.
- **Hierarchy of causal forces** (ch. 10): theories should layer causes so that secondary forces operate within bounds set by more basic ones; revision incorporates earlier theories as special cases. (He used Marxism as a *formal* example of hierarchical ambition — both attractive as total explanation and dangerous when practitioners demote all other causes without the required analysis.)
- **Link to [[transformational-causal-principle]]**: transformational principles help distinguish superficial from basic by mapping many inputs onto one output — learning the transformation, not the input controls, is the basic task.
- **Association vs. presence**: asking what happens to Y when X varies is a different question from asking why the range/presence of X is what it is.

## Applications

Race and income, school-type effects, and mobility variation appear as worked illustrations of how variance-focused, control-heavy analysis can mistake superficial patterns for basic structure. Outcome analysis (why is Y at its level?) is proposed as the reoriented research question.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk Lieberson himself noted (attributed): "basic cause" language can become a vague escape from prosaic empirical variables — he required rigorous procedures (citing Hirschi and Selvin) rather than hand-waving. Critics may see the distinction as unfalsifiable without independent process evidence. The concept does not by itself identify *which* causes are basic in a given domain; it is a demand on theory construction and research design.
