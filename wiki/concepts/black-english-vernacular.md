# Black English Vernacular (BEV)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[labov-language-inner-city-1972|Language in the Inner City]]* (1972), [[labov-william|William Labov]] defines the **black English vernacular (BEV)** as the relatively uniform dialect spoken by the majority of black youth in most parts of the United States — especially northern inner-city centers (he lists New York, Boston, Detroit, Philadelphia, Washington, Cleveland, Chicago, St. Louis, San Francisco, Los Angeles, among others) — also present in many rural areas and in the casual intimate speech of many adults. Within the volume he narrows operational focus to the relatively uniform **grammar** found in its most consistent form in speech of black youth **ages 8–19** who **participate fully in the street culture** of the inner cities.

He rejects the label **"black English"** for this dialect because it implies a false dichotomy (SE vs. "black English"). "Black English," he argues, better names the whole range of forms used by black people in the United States — from Gullah Creole grammar on the South Carolina Sea Islands to formal literary style. "Black English" had itself replaced earlier **"Nonstandard Negro English"** when that phrase became less acceptable.

## Semantic History

| Label (approx.) | Era / use |
|---|---|
| Nonstandard Negro English (NNE) | Labov team and contemporaries, mid-1960s |
| Black English | Broader popular/academic label Labov finds ambiguous |
| Black English Vernacular (BEV) | Labov 1972 preferred structural term |
| AAVE / African American Vernacular English; AAL | Later field standard (not coined in this source) |

Meaning drift is substantial: later AAVE work often includes broader age, gender, and regional coverage than Labov's street-culture youth core. Mitchell-Kernan's Oakland work (1969) is cited by Labov as rare community-member research parallel.

## Operationalizations

Labov operationalizes BEV through quantitative [[sociolinguistic-variable|variables]] and [[variable-rule|variable rules]], not through a checklist of "errors":

- **Phonology:** r-lessness (including intervocalic); consonant-cluster simplification (t,d; s,z); other variables intersecting grammar (*-ed*, *are*).
- **Morphosyntax:** copula contraction and deletion (ordered rules); invariant habitual *be₂*; remote perfect *been*; negative concord; reduced surface *-s*/*-ed* under phonological constraints.
- **Social scope:** peak consistency among full peer-group members ~9–18; **lames** (isolates) show lower vernacular rates and more standard features.
- **Geographic claim:** relative uniformity across major U.S. urban centers (Labov 1972; cites parallel work e.g. Wolfram Detroit).

Creole-origin arguments (Stewart, Dillard) are treated as **historical hypotheses**; Labov insists on **synchronic** description and notes northern youth are not living on a Creole continuum. Some features (remote *been*) he places outside other dialects' competence; others he models as extensions of shared English machinery.

## Applications

- Structural analysis of urban African American speech ([[labov-language-inner-city-study]]).
- Educational linguistics and the [[deficit-difference-debate]] (BEV as full system vs. "verbal deprivation").
- Discourse: [[ritual-insult|sounding]], [[signifying]], and [[labov-narrative-model|narrative]] as displays of vernacular skill.
- Method: peer-group sampling to capture vernacular under the [[observers-paradox]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Etic construct:** BEV is an analytic dialect label, not a self-name of a people. **Four-way non-identity:** BEV ≠ genetic population ≠ "Black people" as a whole ≠ any single community's self-identification.
- **Sample bias of the classic studies:** boy-centered street groups in south-central Harlem (1965–68); adult, female, southern, and middle-class black speech are differently scoped.
- **Terminology politics:** "vernacular" and earlier "nonstandard" carry stigma risks even when Labov argues for systematic equality of dialects.
- **Contested boundaries:** how separate from Southern white vernaculars; how deep the Creole legacy; how to periodize shift to AAVE/AAL labels — live research questions beyond this 1972 source.
