# Dying-and-Rising God

## Definition as Coined

[[frazer-james-george|Frazer]], following Wilhelm Mannhardt, treated gods such as **Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus**, and goddesses such as **Demeter/Persephone**, together with European corn-spirits, as embodiments of the **yearly death and revival of vegetation**. Myths of dying and returning, and rites of mourning and rejoicing, were read as the vegetation cycle personified (*[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|Golden Bough]]* ch. 29–49 and related). (Attributed construct.)

## Semantic History

Enormously influential in early comparative religion and in literary modernism; later specialized scholarship (notably Jonathan Z. Smith and others) argued the "dying-and-rising god" is a **modern synthetic type** that homogenizes unlike Near Eastern and classical figures and over-reads Christian parallel. The label remains a historiographic object even where the type is rejected.

## Operationalizations

Frazer: shared pattern of death, disappearance, search, return/rebirth tied to planting and harvest. Competing readings: local cult histories; political myth; initiation; Christian-theological apologetic or anti-apologetic frames.

## Applications

Core of the middle third of *The Golden Bough*; template for interpreting [[vegetation-spirit]] and corn-mother/corn-spirit folklore.

## Critiques and Limitations

Category error risks (four-way non-identity of myth type, cult practice, and agricultural fact); selective parallel-hunting; contested equation of diverse deities. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].
