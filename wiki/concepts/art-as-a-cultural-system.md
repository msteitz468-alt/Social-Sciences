## Definition as Coined

In "Art as a Cultural System" (in *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983; first 1976), [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] argues (attributed) that "a theory of art is thus at the same time a theory of culture, not an autonomous enterprise." Art is a **semiotic** system — a matter of "the life of signs in society" — but Geertz explicitly opposes his semiotics to the structuralist/formalist kind that treats sign systems "in an invented world of dualities, transformations, parallels, and equivalences." The meaning and force of an art form are "never wholly intra-aesthetic"; they lie within "the other modes of social activity" and are "always a local matter."

## Semantic History

The essay is a double polemic. Against **formalism** (Geertz's target: "aesthetic formalism, best represented right now by structuralism, and by those varieties of semiotics which seek to follow its lead"), he denies that "the whole secret of aesthetic power is located in the formal relations among sounds, images, volumes, themes, or gestures," noting (via Kristeller) that Western "fine arts" aesthetics is only a mid-eighteenth-century invention. Against the **functionalist** theory of art (art works as "elaborate mechanisms for defining social relationships, sustaining social rules, and strengthening social values"), he insists "nothing very measurable would happen to Yoruba society if carvers no longer concerned themselves with the fineness of line... the central connection between art and collective life does not lie on such an instrumental plane, it lies on a semiotic one." The key positive term is **sensibility**: "to study an art form is to explore a sensibility, [and]... such a sensibility is essentially a collective formation."

## Operationalizations

Geertz proposes "a kind of natural history of signs and symbols, an ethnography of the vehicles of meaning" — redirecting the available semiotic traditions (Peirce, Saussure, [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]], Nelson Goodman) "toward an investigation of [signs] in their natural habitat." He works this through cross-cultural cases in which an art's power is legible only against its culture's wider preoccupations:

| Case | Source | The semiotic link |
|---|---|---|
| **Yoruba** line/carving/scarification | Robert Farris Thompson | vocabulary of line shared by sculpture and facial marking; *ilaju* ("civilized") = "lines upon the face/earth" — "the meanings of things are the scars that men leave on them" |
| **Abelam** painting (Sepik, New Guinea) | Anthony Forge | the pointed-oval "belly of a woman" motif signs an opposition of precultural female vs. ritual male creativity; color-naming restricted to "things of ritual concern" — "pigment signs power" |
| **Quattrocento Italian** painting | Michael Baxandall's **"period eye"** | learned, culturally variable skills of discrimination (drawn from preaching, the *bassa danza*, commercial "gauging") shape how a beholder experiences a picture: Piero (gauged), Fra Angelico (preached), Botticelli (danced) |
| **Moroccan** oral poetry (*qasida*) | Geertz / Hildred Geertz fieldwork | poetry as "a kind of para-Quran" poised between the Quran as literal divine speech (Hodgson) and the "agonistic" thrust-and-counterthrust of everyday Moroccan speech |

Baxandall's **period eye** — "the equipment that a fifteenth-century painter's public... brought to complex visual stimulations" — is Geertz's paradigm case: "much of what we call 'taste' lies in this, the conformity between discriminations demanded by a painting and skills of discrimination possessed by the beholder."

## Applications

The closing thesis: aesthetic response is "no less a cultural artifact than the objects and devices concocted to 'affect' it" — "the artist works with his audience's capacities... Art and the equipment to grasp it are made in the same shop." Innate elements (e.g. not being colorblind) are actualized only through cultural experience. A Bamileke chief's memorial statue (via Jacques Maquet), deliberately left to erode as the chief's memory fades, shows that "form" must include an object's social career, not just its physical shape. The essay is a keystone of [[interpretive-anthropology]]'s extension into aesthetics and a bridge to [[symbolic-anthropology]]'s concern with [[expressive-symbols]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Geertz criticizes naive Western appreciation of non-Western art as ethnocentric — seeing "African sculpture as bush Picasso" and hearing "Javanese music as noisy Debussy" — but the alternative he offers (reconstructing another culture's whole sensibility) is exactly the interpretive claim that later reflexive critique found presumptuous. As with [[common-sense]] and the wider program, the semiotic readings are demonstrations by illumination rather than by test; the leading cases (Thompson, Forge, Baxandall) are other scholars' ethnographies redeployed to carry Geertz's general thesis.
