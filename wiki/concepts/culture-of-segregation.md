# Culture of Segregation

## Definition as Coined

[[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Denton]] reject an autonomous "culture of poverty" as the root cause of black underclass outcomes. They argue instead that **residential segregation** creates a structural niche within which a deleterious set of attitudes and behaviors — a **culture of segregation** — arises and flourishes: an oppositional culture that devalues work, schooling, and marriage and stresses behaviors hostile to success in the larger economy (*American Apartheid* Ch 1, attributed). They stress that many residents still lead conventional lives; the growing concentration of poor, welfare-dependent families is an "inevitable result" of segregation concentrating poverty.

## Semantic History

Positioned against Oscar Lewis culture-of-poverty and against underclass theories that omit spatial structure; related to later neighborhood-effects and oppositional-culture literatures (not yet ingested).

## Operationalizations

Not a single metric; supported in the book via models linking segregation → poverty concentration → neighborhood social environment (Ch 5–6). Load-bearing contrast (attributed): "It is not a self-perpetuating 'culture of poverty' that retards black educational progress but a structurally created and sustained 'culture of segregation' that, however useful in adapting to the harsh realities of ghetto life, undermines socioeconomic progress in the wider society."

## Applications

- Explains intergenerational transmission of disadvantage without essentializing culture as independent of structure.
- Connects oppositional culture, linguistic divergence, and peer sanctions against "acting white"/integration to spatial isolation (Ch 6 synthesis of Clark, Ogbu/Fordham, Anderson, Labov as cited).
- Anchors the closing "How to Build an Underclass" primer: after confinement and concentrated poverty, culture of segregation becomes a transmission mechanism.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of re-inscribing cultural deficit language even while structuralizing it; contested relative weight of culture vs labor market vs discrimination (see [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]). Later neighborhood-effects work sometimes drops "culture" vocabulary while retaining spatial mechanisms.
