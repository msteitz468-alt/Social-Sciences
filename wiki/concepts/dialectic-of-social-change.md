# Dialectic of Social Change (Blau)

## Definition as Coined

The concluding thesis of *[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power]]*. [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] holds that social change is "neither evolutionary progress in a straight line nor recurring cycles but alternating patterns of intermittent social reorganization along different lines." The mechanism is the cross-level dynamic of [[imbalance-of-exchange|reciprocity and imbalance]]: "reciprocity on one level... entails imbalances on others," so "every social process restoring equilibrium engenders some new imbalances." Because equilibrating and disequilibrating are two faces of the same process, structures do not settle — they reorganize intermittently.

Blau specifies three sources of the contradictory forces:
1. **Cross-level trade-off.** The approval that legitimates a ruler's moderation simultaneously *reinforces* the underlying power imbalance; the disapproval that penalizes excess generates opposition that *disturbs* equilibrium — the same reactions balance one level and unbalance another.
2. **Success breeds its opposite.** Integration heightens differentiation, which revives the need for integration; the underprivileged organize leftist opposition, whose success produces *relative* deprivation for the lower-middle class, which spurs rightist opposition.
3. **Latency and resistance.** Vested powers and institutionalized values resist change, so pressure must accumulate and opposition must reach a threshold before readjustment — hence change by "intermittent reorganizations" rather than continuous adjustment.

## Semantic History

Blau borrows the *dialectical* framing from the Marx–Engels tradition (the Engels epigraph to Ch. 12) but empties it of teleology: there is no predetermined direction, only the perpetual generation of new imbalances. He explicitly contrasts his view with [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] and Smelser's distinction between "processes of change in a structure" and "processes of equilibrium within it," calling it "one of degree rather than a clear dichotomy, since hardly any adjustments merely restore a pre-existing equilibrium" — a [POSITION] against equilibrium functionalism. Blau notes the parallel with van den Berghe's near-contemporaneous "Dialectic and Functionalism."

## Operationalizations

Not measured; a pattern claim about the *form* of change (intermittent reorganization), evidenced by the phenomena the theory predicts — legitimation reinforcing power, opposition regenerating rigid structures, integration and differentiation alternating.

## Applications

Blau's alternative to the equilibrium imagery of [[structural-functionalism]]; the frame in which [[opposition]] is a "regenerative force"; and the book's answer to how a theory founded on reciprocity accounts for conflict and directional change.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The claim that *every* equilibrating process is disequilibrating elsewhere is difficult to falsify — an offsetting imbalance can always be located post hoc.
- Blau's own 1986 retrospective judges the macro-level argument (of which this is the capstone) the book's least secured; he later held macro analysis needs a different conceptual scheme than the micro exchange theory this dialectic extends.
