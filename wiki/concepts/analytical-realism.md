# Analytical Realism

**Analytical realism** is the epistemological position [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] set out in
the final chapter of *[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure of Social Action]]* (1937)
as the methodology implied by his [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic theory of action]].
The whole account is **attributed** to Parsons.

## Definition as Coined

Parsons argued that "at least some of the general concepts of science are not fictional but adequately
'grasp' aspects of the objective external world." The qualifier *analytical* marks the crucial limit:
these concepts correspond "not to concrete phenomena, but to elements in them which are analytically
separable from other elements." No single concept — nor even a whole logically coherent system of
concepts — is ever completely descriptive of a concrete thing. It is a **realism** (an external
empirical world exists independent of the mind) that is nonetheless **analytical** (its concepts name
abstracted elements, not concrete units).

## Semantic History

Parsons defined the position against four rejected alternatives:

- **Positivistic empiricism** — reifying general theoretical systems (e.g., treating classical
  economics as valid only under literal perfect competition), Whitehead's "fallacy of misplaced
  concreteness."
- **Particularistic empiricism** — only concrete particulars are knowable (a Humean skepticism about
  general concepts).
- **Intuitionist empiricism** — concepts may only individualize a unique phenomenon, never generalize.
- **Fictionalism** — Weber's account of the [[ideal-type|ideal type]] as a "useful fiction"; Parsons
  judged this correct for some concept-types but untenable as a general theory of scientific concepts.

The common error Parsons diagnosed beneath reification and fictionalism alike is an
"empiricist-monistic fallacy": the assumption that a concept must either directly correspond to a
concrete existing unit or else be an unreal fiction, with no third option — the analytical element
that is real but not a concrete part.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct but a stance governing how theory relates to data. In practice it licenses
generalized analytical theory (elements/variables that vary independently) over rigid type-atomism,
and treats scientific theories as standing in a "functional relation" to reality — adequate for
certain purposes, approaching but never reaching a limit of "humanly possible" knowledge.

## Applications

The methodological foundation of the [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]] and of Parsons's later systems
theory. Parsons used it to argue that the [[unit-act|unit act]]'s elements (ends, means, conditions)
are analytically abstracted, not concrete pieces, and to warn against the "mosaic theory" that
reifies analytical elements — a fallacy he likened to the discredited unit-character theory of
inheritance in genetics.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics have questioned whether "analytical realism" coherently occupies a middle ground between
realism and instrumentalism, or merely names the wish for one. The position is bound up with the
contested [[parsons-convergence-thesis-debate|convergence thesis]] and with Parsons's reading of
Weber's methodology as an "unstable halfway point" — itself an attributed, disputed interpretation.
