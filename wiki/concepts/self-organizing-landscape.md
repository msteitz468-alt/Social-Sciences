# Self-Organizing Landscape

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]* (2005), McIntosh treats Middle Niger urban origins as a **self-organizing landscape**: co-evolution of human–land relations through successive transformations, each a leap in complexity, culminating in heterarchical [[urban-cluster|Urban Clusters]]. Draws on complexity/self-organization language (e.g. Kauffman-style “edge of chaos” imagery) as analogy for archaeological process.

## Semantic History

Migrates concepts from evolutionary biology and complex adaptive systems into landscape archaeology and African urbanism. Related to [[historical-ecology]]’s non-equilibrium landscape view but specifically aimed at *urban* emergence without citadel.

## Operationalizations

Phased diagrams of specialist counts and interaction codes (self-organizing landscape I–IV in Ch. 3); survey/excavation tests of clustering and functional complementarity; resilience/sustainability discussion in hinterland chapter.

## Applications

Primary: Middle Niger. Comparative: moments in Mesopotamia, Nile, northern China “through a Middle Niger lens” (Ch. 6).

## Critiques and Limitations

Self-organization vocabulary can become metaphorical rather than predictive; risk of smuggling teleology (“leap in complexity”) while denying hierarchy; must keep attributed and tied to archaeological tests ([[pulse-model]]).


## McIntosh 2005 densification

*(Attributed — Chs. 1–3, 5–6.)*

Successive **phase transforms** of human–land relations (analogized to physical phase changes) produce leaps in complexity culminating in heterarchical [[urban-cluster|Urban Clusters]]. Co-evolution: new niches open for specialists as climate, landforms, and social rules change; the system sits “at the edge of chaos” (Kauffman language).

**Sustainability Paradox (Ch. 5):** the landscape can charter abrupt population-complexity change *and* long-run resilience if pluri-corporate canons block fused hierarchy / despotism. State-driven cities are framed as fundamentally fragile by contrast.

**Comparative export (Ch. 6):** Uruk “lost opportunity” for clustered urbanism; Nile proto-cities then aborted classic city under Pharaonic territorial state; northern China persistent clustering into Shang. McIntosh’s own restraint: Middle Niger models are not a universal explanation — prefer vibratory nesting of hierarchy and heterarchy.
