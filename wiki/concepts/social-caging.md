# Social Caging

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]*, 1986, esp. Chs. 2–4) uses **caging** / **social cage** for the process and condition in which populations become trapped in permanent, intensifiable power relations — civilization, stratification, and the state — because ecological and social boundaries block easy exit. Following Renfrew, civilization combines ceremonial center, writing, and city as a jump in "insulation"; Mann renames this insulation a social cage. Alluvial agriculture in river valleys is his paradigmatic early cage: high productivity *plus* confinement. Causal weight falls on **caging forces**, not alluvium or irrigation as such:

> "The decisive feature of these ecologies and of human reactions to them was the closing of the escape route. … These caging forces must occupy the causal role in our explanation, not the alluvium or irrigation itself, which was merely their usual form or indicator in this historical epoch."

## Semantic History

Developed inside Mann's anti-evolutionist account of why most prehistoric peoples "evaded power" while a few regions crossed into civilization. In noncivilized settings, Mann argues, escape from the cage remained possible — authority freely conferred and recoverable; permanent coercive power unattainable. Related metaphors exist in political anthropology (e.g. Carneiro/Webb environmental circumscription); Mann's usage is similar but without their emphasis on population pressure and militarism as primary, and is organizational-historical within [[iemp-model|IEMP]] and [[power-networks]].

## Operationalizations

Qualitative: fixed settlement; delayed-return investment in land/livestock; irrigation/alluvial dependence; fortification; tax/labor capture; blocked frontier exit; Renfrew's three insulators as a clustered complex. Not a single quantitative index in Vol. I. Military investment generally *reinforces* caging once fixity exists, but Mann denies the permanent coercive state was original among gatherer-hunters.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann throughout Vol. I:

- **Ch. 2 (prehistory)** — Fixed settlement traps people into cooperation ("least caged" gatherer-hunters retain flexible attachments); Neolithic delayed-return extremes on floodplains; rank societies and chiefdom cycles (Wessex/Stonehenge, Oceania) leave the cage unlocked via popular recovery of powers and competing elites; no general evolution beyond early settled Neolithic rank — permanent states require local caging resources.
- **Ch. 3 (Mesopotamia)** — Alluvium surplus as opportunity plus territorial/social cage; temple as first state via caging intensity more than ruler→ruled coercion; lateral alluvium–hinterland and vertical river relations intensify property and centralization while loose overlaps reduce cage force → Sumerian [[multi-power-actor-civilization]].
- **Ch. 4 (comparative pristine cases)** — Model tested as suggestive not mechanical: Indus (alluvial cage, "arrested"); Shang (alluvium + loess, earlier militarism); Egypt as extreme quasi-unitary cage ("as total as has ever been seen," not the dominant developmental path); Minoan Crete (vine–olive–cereal cage, less barred); Mesoamerica (less caging → looser polities, less permanent stratification — Mann); Andean/Inca admitted partial exception (more internal caging, precocious logistics). Anti-endogenous thesis: state and stratification did not arise from within systemic "societies" but from unusually caged alluvial networks interacting with peripheral ones.
- **Later re-caging** — Empires of domination intensify a more singular extensive cage via military organization and [[compulsory-cooperation]] (Ch. 5 onward); European states re-cage class-nations within multistate fields (Chs. 13–15).

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of ecological reduction if "cage" is read as environment alone; Mann explicitly ties cage to *organization* of [[power-networks]] and interaction with peripheries. Andean exception and Maya weak fit show the model is ideal-typical. Archaeological detail is mid-1980s secondary synthesis — date-stamp on subject-matter pages; do not promote Mann's case narratives as wiki-voice archaeology.
