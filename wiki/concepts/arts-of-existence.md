# Arts of Existence (Techniques of the Self)

## Definition as Coined

**Arts of existence** — also **"techniques of the self," "technologies of the self,"** and **"aesthetics of existence"** — is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s term in *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984) for "those intentional and voluntary actions by which men not only set themselves rules of conduct, but also seek to transform themselves, to change themselves in their singular being, and to make their life into an *oeuvre* that carries certain aesthetic values and meets certain stylistic criteria" (attributed). It names the third of the three axes constituting Foucault's late object — after knowledge and power, the **relation of the self to itself**.

## Semantic History

The concept marks the pivot of Foucault's late work. Where *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I analyzed sexuality as produced by an external [[deployment-of-sexuality|apparatus of power-knowledge]], the arts of existence turn to how individuals actively **constitute themselves** as subjects — not a retreat from the power analytic but its completion on the axis of subjectivity. Foucault credits **Jacob Burckhardt** with first marking the significance of the arts of existence for the Renaissance, and cites Walter Benjamin (on Baudelaire) and Stephen Greenblatt's *Renaissance Self-Fashioning* (1980) as partial continuations, positioning his own project as resuming "the long history of these aesthetics of existence and these technologies of the self." He notes these arts "lost some of their importance and autonomy" once assimilated into priestly power in early Christianity and, later, into educative, medical, and psychological practices — a supersession, not a disappearance.

## Operationalizations

There is no measurement; the arts of existence are reconstructed **genealogically**. Their content is specified by the four-aspect analysis of [[ethics-foucauldian|moral subjectivation]] (ethical substance, mode of subjection, self-forming activity, telos) applied to a given domain. In *The Use of Pleasure* the domain is the [[aphrodisia]] and the arts are the three "great arts of self-conduct": [[dietetics]] (the body), the economics of marriage (the household), and the erotics of the love of boys — each imposing a different temporal logic of moderation ([[chresis]]) achieved through self-mastery ([[enkrateia]]).

## Applications

Foucault frames the study of ancient sexual austerity as "one of the first chapters" of a general history of the techniques of the self — austerity understood "not as an expression of… deep and essential prohibitions, but as the elaboration and stylization of an activity in the exercise of its power and the practice of its liberty." The Greek relation to truth, he argues, opened onto an **aesthetics of existence** rather than a hermeneutics of desire: moral value grounded in formal principles of use, distribution, and hierarchy, not in code-conformity or self-decipherment. Part Five's reading of Platonic erotics shows the first turn from a stylistics of pleasure toward a relation to truth — "ground is broken for a future inquiry into desiring man" — the forward pointer to the Christian hermeneutics of desire and to Foucault's next volume, *The Care of the Self*.

## The imperial art of existence (*The Care of the Self*)

*[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, 1984) shows the arts of existence at their historical peak — a Roman imperial [[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]] organized by the [[care-of-the-self|care of the self]]. Its Conclusion supplies Foucault's fullest statement of what drives such an art: the era's heightened sexual austerity reflects "the development of **an art of existence that revolves around the question of the self**," not an accentuation of prohibitions. Crucially, Foucault uses the arts of existence to mark the **discontinuity** with Christianity: the later Christian ethics will differ not in its rules but in constituting the ethical subject through finitude and the Fall, obedience to a personal god's law, "a decipherment of the soul and a purificatory hermeneutics of the desires," and self-renunciation — where the ancient art aimed instead at self-mastery and "a pure enjoyment of oneself." This is the connective thesis across the *History of Sexuality* trilogy (see [[deployment-of-sexuality]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The "aesthetics of existence" language has been read both as an emancipatory late-Foucauldian ethics of self-fashioning and as politically thin or aestheticizing (attributed). Foucault himself qualifies it sharply: the Greek art of existence was for "the smallest minority… free, adult males" and "rested on a very harsh system of inequalities and constraints."
- Whether the late turn to the self **breaks with** or **completes** the earlier analytics of power is a standing question in the reception (attributed); this wiki records it as continuous on Foucault's own three-axis account (knowledge / power / subject).
- "Technologies of the self" received its fullest statement in Foucault's later lectures and seminars, not ingested here; the version on this page is that of *The Use of Pleasure* (1984).
