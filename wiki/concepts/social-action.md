## Definition as Coined

Weber opens *Economy and Society* by defining sociology as "a science that in construing and understanding social action seeks causal explanation of the course and effects of such action" (Ch. 1 §1). Weber defines "action" as human behaviour linked to a subjective meaning on the part of the actor or actors, whether overt or purely inward, and including refraining from acting or tolerating a situation. Weber further specifies that behaviour becomes "social" action specifically when the actor's intended meaning is related to, and oriented to, the behaviour of others (Ch. 1 §1).

## Semantic History

Tribe's Translation Appendix records that Weber's German term is *Handeln* (soziales Handeln), which Tribe renders "action" rather than "behaviour" or "conduct," because Weber deliberately contrasts *Handeln* with *menschliches Verhalten* ("human behaviour") to mark a stronger sense of intentionality; Tribe considered but rejected "conduct" as failing to preserve the conceptual link Weber draws between Chapters 1 and 2. Weber himself distinguishes "action" from mere reactive behaviour by an "entirely fluid border" — Weber notes that purely traditional action often sits directly on this border, meaning the category is not sharply bounded even in Weber's own usage (Ch. 1 §1).

## Operationalizations

Weber does not operationalize social action statistically as such; rather, he argues sociology's object is always the action of individual persons, never collectivities as agents — Weber states that a "state" exists sociologically only insofar as there is a Chance that particular meaningfully-oriented action will occur among individuals, not as an entity with its own action (Ch. 1 §9). Weber holds that other framings (an individual as a "sociation of cells," a society as an organism) may serve other purposes but cannot yield Verstehen.

## Applications

Weber's concept underlies his typology of [[types-of-social-action]], his account of the [[social-relationship]], and his definitions of organisation, rulership, and the state, all of which he builds up from individual oriented action rather than from collective concepts treated as agents (Ch. 1 §9, §12, §16–17).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber himself flags the limits of the construct: he notes that agents "for the most part" act in a "drearily half-aware condition," so that fully conscious, explicitly meaningful action is, in Weber's words, "always in reality quite a marginal case" (Ch. 1 §11). Tribe (per the editorial overview) argues that Weber deliberately builds the chapter from individual action toward institutional power specifically to reject a class-based sociology of power relationships — a framing choice, not a neutral one.
