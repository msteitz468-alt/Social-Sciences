# Practical Consciousness

## Definition as Coined

Giddens Glossary: what actors know (believe) about social conditions, including especially the conditions of their own action, **but cannot express discursively**; no bar of repression protects practical consciousness as is the case with the unconscious (*[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|Constitution]]*).

## Semantic History

Part of Giddens's **stratification model** of the agent: [[discursive-consciousness]], practical consciousness, unconscious. Replaces Freudian id/ego/super-ego as primary social-theoretic architecture (*Constitution* Ch. 2). Affinities with tacit knowledge / "knowing how to go on" (Wittgensteinian and ethnomethodological currents Giddens reworks); distinct from Bourdieu's [[practical-sense]] (embodied strategy in a field) though both target non-discursive mastery.

## Operationalizations

Not a scale. Empirical illustrations (Willis "lads"; Goffmanian tact) show knowledgeability enacted without full discursive formulation.

## Applications

- Bulk of day-to-day [[routinization]] and mutual knowledge.
- Explains how institutional reproduction can be skilled without being fully theorized by actors.
- [[double-hermeneutic]]: social science can move practical into discursive consciousness.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary with the unconscious and with discursive consciousness is analytic; critics question accessibility of "what actors know but cannot say."
