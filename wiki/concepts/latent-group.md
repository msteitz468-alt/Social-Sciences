# Latent Group

[[olson-mancur|Mancur Olson]]'s (1965) typology sorts groups by their capacity
to provide a [[public-good|collective good]] through the voluntary action of
rational members. A **latent group** is a large group whose members cannot
individually make a noticeable contribution and who therefore have no incentive
to contribute — the group has a "latent power or capacity for action" that can be
realized only with [[selective-incentives|selective incentives]]. The typology is
the classificatory backbone of Olson's [[collective-action-theory|collective
action theory]] and his account of the [[free-rider-problem]].

## Definition as Coined

Olson defined three types (1965):

- **Privileged group** — "a group such that each of its members, or at least some
  one of them, has an incentive to see that the collective good is provided, even
  if he has to bear the full burden of providing it himself." The good will
  presumably be obtained "without any group organization or coordination
  whatever."
- **Intermediate group** — a group in which no single member's benefit is large
  enough to make unilateral provision worthwhile, "but which does not have so
  many members that no one member will notice whether any other member is or is
  not helping." The good may or may not be provided, but only with some
  coordination.
- **Latent group** — "the very large group ... distinguished by the fact that, if
  one member does or does not help provide the collective good, no other one
  member will be significantly affected." No member can make a noticeable
  contribution and none reacts to another's non-contribution, so no one has an
  incentive to contribute. A latent group successfully mobilized (only via
  selective incentives) Olson called a "mobilized latent group."

Olson drew the typology as the non-market analog of market structures — privileged
and intermediate groups correspond to monopoly and oligopoly; the latent group
corresponds to atomistic competition.

## Semantic History

The decisive variable is not raw size but the individual's *fraction of the total
benefit* (Olson's F_i) and the "noticeability" of a member's action — so a group
of a few very unequal members can behave like a privileged group while a numerous
group of equals is latent. Olson distinguished his "mobilization" of a latent
group from Karl Deutsch's different use of the term. In later usage "latent
group" became shorthand for any large diffuse constituency (consumers, taxpayers)
predicted to remain unorganized — Olson's "forgotten groups."

## Operationalizations

Classification turns on two tests: (1) does any member's share of the benefit
exceed the total cost of provision (F_i > C/V_g)? If yes, the group is privileged.
(2) Are members few enough that one member's (non)contribution is noticed and
reacted to? If yes, intermediate; if no, latent. Olson's applied cases: dispersed
corporate stockholders and taxpayers as latent groups; oligopoly-sized industries
as privileged/intermediate "special interest" groups that organize easily (see
[[by-product-theory]]); Marxian social classes as large latent groups (see
[[marx-karl]]).

## Applications

The typology grounds Olson's counter-intuitive corollary — small concentrated
interests routinely defeat large diffuse ones ("the exploitation of the great by
the small") — and his critique of pluralism, which mistook latent groups for
groups that would automatically organize. It is standard in interest-group
theory, the analysis of diffuse-versus-concentrated interests in regulation, and
social-movement theory.

## Critiques and Limitations

The boundaries between the three types are analytic rather than sharply
measurable, and the prediction that latent groups will not organize is the
contested heart of the theory: much observed mobilization of large diffuse groups
(environmental, consumer, civil-rights movements) sits awkwardly with it, and
critics argue Olson under-weighted collective benefits, threat, and solidarity as
mobilizing forces (see [[free-rider-problem]], [[collective-action-theory]]).

## Douglas's extension: how latent groups survive

[[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] (*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How
Institutions Think]]*, 1986, ch. 3) treated the latent group as collective-action
theory's hardest case — neither coerced like a prison nor market-driven like a
firm — and argued it survives through three interlocking functionalist cycles,
each meeting Elster's own five conditions for valid functional explanation:
**weak leadership** (an effect of the credible threat to secede), a **stable
boundary** (an effect of insisting on equality and 100% participation, per Olson
1965, p. 41), and a **shared belief in an evil outside conspiracy** (an effect of
mutual accusations of betrayal, which splits or expels suspected traitors and
disciplines would-be leaders). Each element is unintended, its causal loop
unperceived, and each sustains the others — her rehabilitation of functional
explanation against Elster (1983), and her answer to the gap Olson's theory
leaves for groups without coercion or selective incentives.
