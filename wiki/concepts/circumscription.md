# Circumscription (theory of state origins)

## Definition as Coined

**Circumscription theory**, as formulated by [[carneiro-robert-l|Robert L. Carneiro]] (1970), is a conflict-theoretic account of **pristine** [[state-formation|state formation]]. Carneiro argued that autonomous villages under rising population pressure normally fission and expand into free land; where exit is blocked by **environmental circumscription** (deserts, mountains, seas around a fertile core) or **social circumscription** (hostile or dense neighbors), warfare among villages produces successive conquest and political consolidation — first multi-village polities and [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]], then the state — rather than dispersal.

## Semantic History

Carneiro's 1970 *Science* article became a standard citation in political anthropology and archaeology of complex societies. The concept enters *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]* (2003) as part of his broader defense of evolutionary explanation and of population pressure and warfare as determinants of political evolution (alongside critical discussion of White, Steward, Service, and Harris). Later literature distinguishes environmental from social circumscription and tests the model against non-circumscribed pristine cases and against integrative/managerial alternatives.

## Operationalizations

- **Environmental circumscription:** geographic enclosure of productive land (classic illustrations in the literature: coastal Peru, Nile valley, Mesopotamian alluvium — as *cited* cases, not wiki-voice proofs).
- **Social circumscription:** effective enclosure by human barriers (dense settlement, enemies) even without hard geographic walls.
- **Mechanism chain (attributed):** population growth → resource competition → warfare → defeat cannot be escaped by fission → losers incorporated → hierarchical consolidation.

## Applications

Linked to [[state-formation]], [[chiefdom]], [[complex-society]], and the conflict side of the [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. Archaeological and ethnographic applications (Amazonia, Andes, Mesoamerica, Old World river valleys) are attributed case by case as secondary sources are ingested.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Integrative/managerial counter-position** ([[service-elman-r|Service]] and others): early centralization may arise from organizational benefits (redistribution, mediation, public works) rather than conquest under enclosure — see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]].
- **Prime-mover critiques:** single-factor (or short-chain) models underplay ideology, agency, trade, and contingency (Flannery and later multi-factor generative models). Service’s “Down with prime-movers!” (1968) is the programmatic rival to monistic determinant theories, including population-warfare packages.
- **Scope:** designed for *pristine* state formation; secondary states and non-circumscribed pathways require other accounts.
- **Empirical contest:** whether specific "classic" pristine cases actually meet circumscription conditions is an open archaeological debate — record positions, do not adjudicate in wiki voice.
- **Measurement:** population *pressure* ≠ population *density* (Carneiro 2003): pressure exists only when numbers force action beyond conventional means; incipient pressure under shortening fallow can be hard to detect ethnographically or archaeologically.

## Carneiro 2003 restatement (attributed)

In *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]* Carneiro restates and situates the 1970 theory within the determinants chapter:

- **Wittfogel false correlation:** arid/semiarid locations of many autochthonous civilizations pointed not to large-scale irrigation as prime cause but to **environmentally circumscribed arable land** as the common catalytic feature (Carneiro 1970a, restated 2003).
- **Timing claim:** states first arose in circumscribed agricultural environments; some early states in non-circumscribed land arose later and more slowly.
- **Population pressure primacy:** among factors producing chiefdoms and states, Carneiro “wholeheartedly” ranks population pressure first (agreeing with Harner’s cross-cultural scarcity→stratification/centralization argument as he reports it), always interacting with others but not in a “democracy of causes.”
- **Warfare:** pressure proponents generally pair pressure with warfare as the consolidating mechanism (conquest and incorporation); Keeley and others are cited on prehistoric war’s under-recognition.
- **Sequence of pressure effects (attributed model):** shorter fallow → intensification → marginal land → forceful acquisition of neighbors’ land → aggregation of villages into multivillage polities → chiefdoms → states (Graber’s quantitative fission-until-circumscription model cited as allied).

Conflict-side position in [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]; complementary vocabulary with [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] stage sequence without equating them.
