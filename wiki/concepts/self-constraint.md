# Self-Constraint

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Elias.)* **Self-constraint** (*Selbstzwang*) is the relatively automatic regulation of impulses and conduct by inner controls, as distinct from **external constraint** (*Fremdzwang*) exercised by other people through immediate threat, command, or surveillance. Elias argues that as the state's [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of physical force]] pacifies social space and lengthens chains of interdependence, the balance of controls tilts toward self-constraint — experienced as self-control, foresight, and shame anxiety ([[threshold-of-shame]]).

## Semantic History

German pair Fremdzwang/Selbstzwang is technical in Elias; English "external constraint" / "self-constraint" (sometimes "self-compulsion"). Related to but not identical with Freudian super-ego (Elias engages psychoanalytic vocabulary while insisting on socio-historical formation) or Bourdieu's [[habitus]] (overlapping problem: durable dispositions — different theory architectures).

## Operationalizations

Historical correlation of pacification/monopoly with manners restraint; socialization demands on children; court observation as external constraint becoming second nature.

## Applications

Part Four theoretical synopsis of *[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*; links state monopolies to psychical habitus.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether modern societies actually show net increase in self-constraint; bureaucratic/external surveillance as counter-trend; decivilizing episodes (debate page).
