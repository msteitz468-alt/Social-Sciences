# Ethnographic Authority

The constructed right of the ethnographer to speak for and about a culture — achieved through fieldwork credentials, realist rhetoric, institutional position, and tropes of being there. Central problem of *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* and of Clifford’s earlier “On Ethnographic Authority” (1983a, cited in the volume).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Clifford and volume contributors.)*

Authority as literary-political achievement, not automatic product of observation. Clifford: authority to represent cultural realities is unequally shared and at times contested (fifth of six determinations of ethnographic writing).

## Semantic History

From unexamined professional credential to explicit object of critique in the Critical Turn. Pratt shows authority built via arrival stories and genre contrast with “mere travelers.” Rosaldo shows norms of authoritative description shared with inquisitorial historiography. Crapanzano shows authority masking subversion. Rabinow sociologizes authority modes and academic micropolitics.

## Operationalizations

Close reading of voice, pronoun use, presence claims, citation of “the native,” scientific rhetoric, introduction/body split (politics confined to intro).

## Applications

Analyses of Malinowski, Evans-Pritchard, Geertz, travel writing, Donner controversy, experimental ethnographies in *Writing Culture*.

## Critiques and Limitations

See [[writing-culture-debate]]; tension with scientific reliability norms; polyphony can become new authority mask (Rosaldo on false polyphony).
