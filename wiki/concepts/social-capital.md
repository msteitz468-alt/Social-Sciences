# Social Capital (Bourdieu)

Bourdieu's network-resources concept of social capital — **not** identical to later Putnam-style civic-trust usages or to Coleman's rational-choice network goods. *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]* treats durable relationships (kin, clientage, gift debt, affines) as accumulated resources convertible under conditions of recognition and honour. Distinguish carefully from community-level "social capital" literatures when those are ingested.

> **Collection note:** *Logic* develops social capital mainly through kinship, marriage, and clientele ethnography (especially [[kabyle]] *nesba* and practical vs official kinship), not as a survey construct. Systematic definition in the "forms of capital" essay and contrastive reception (Coleman, Putnam) await those sources. This page tracks the **Bourdieusian lineage** until other sources force split pages or a debate page.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu.)* Social capital is the sum of resources that accrue to an individual or group by virtue of possessing a durable network of more or less institutionalized relationships of mutual acquaintance and recognition. In *Logic* ch. 1, discussing the social history of the genealogy tool, Bourdieu defined social capital as effective possession of a network of kinship (or other) relations capable of being mobilized or at least manifested — concern to maintain such networks produced and reproduced the genealogical instrument.

> "the concern to maintain and preserve social capital, understood as effective possession of a network of kinship (or other) relations capable of being mobilized or at least manifested."

In ch. 7, symbolic capital procures *nesba* — the network of affines and relationships held through commitments and debts of honour accumulated over generations, mobilizable in extraordinary circumstances. Economic and symbolic capital are inextricably intertwined; good repute is the best if not only economic guarantee in a good-faith economy. Those with capital of social relations can, in Bourdieu's Kabyle market image, "go to market with only their faces, their names and their honour for money."

## Semantic History

### Bourdieu lineage (this page)

- **Genealogy and practical kinship (*Logic*).** Inclination to keep kinship working rises with useful material or symbolic functions; one is more or less "kith and kin" at equivalent genealogical distances depending on interest.
- **Official vs practical kinship (Book II ch. 2).** Official kinship: unambiguous genealogical protocol. Practical kinship: boundaries vary by users and occasions. **Practical kin make marriages; official kin celebrate them.** Hierarchy of utility nearly opposite hierarchy of genealogical legitimacy in ordinary marriage negotiations (least official persons handle least avowable economic talks; official kin replace them as negotiations solemnize).
- **Reproductive work on relationships.** Higher groups, richer in official relationships, devote more reproductive work to reproducing them; the poor settle for ordinary marriages practical kinship provides; distant marriages as long- and short-term investment to maintain or increase social capital (e.g. quality of "maternal uncles").
- **"Forms of capital" essays / later field work.** Fuller abstract definition and convertibility matrix — not yet ingested.

### Coleman lineage (contrast — not this page's object)

[[coleman-james|James Coleman]] treats social capital as aspects of social structure facilitating action (obligations, trustworthiness, information channels, norms) — often functional for collective goods, compatible with rational-choice microfoundations. Do not merge with Bourdieu's classed, competitive, convertible-capital framing. Now on its own page: [[social-capital-coleman]] (ingested from *[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|Foundations of Social Theory]]* ch. 12).

### Putnam lineage (separate page — [[civic-social-capital]])

Robert Putnam's civic-community "social capital" (trust, norms, networks enabling coordination; [[bonding-social-capital]] vs [[bridging-social-capital]]) is a **community-level and often civic-democratic** construct popularized in *[[putnam-bowling-alone-2000|Bowling Alone]]* (2000; study [[putnam-bowling-alone-study]]). **It is not Bourdieu's concept.** Full treatment: [[civic-social-capital]]; research program: [[social-capital-theory]]; decline debate: [[bowling-alone-debate]]. Putnam-style usage stresses public-goods effects of association; Bourdieu stresses unequally distributed resources of connection convertible into other capitals under [[misrecognition]]. Never silently equate.

### Granovetter / network-bridging lineage (contrast — not this page's object)

[[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s [[strength-of-weak-ties]] argument (1973) is a major precursor of later **bridging-network** talk, but he does **not** use "social capital" as his master term in the AJS article. His object is [[tie-strength]] and [[local-bridge|bridging weak ties]] as structural paths for information, mobility, and community integration — a relational-mechanics claim, not a Bourdieusian capital convertibility claim and not yet Putnam's civic stock. Do not rename SWT findings as "social capital" in wiki voice; cross-link instead ([[strength-of-weak-ties-theory]], [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]]).

### Organizational-embeddedness lineage (contrast — not this page's object)

[[small-mario-luis|Mario Luis Small]] (*[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*, 2009) treats what the literature calls social capital as substantially dependent on **[[organizational-embeddedness|organizational conditions]]** of routine participation — including **organizational (nonsocial) ties** brokered by institutions. He critiques investment models of tie formation and engages Bourdieu, Coleman, and Lin without collapsing them. Full treatment: [[organizational-embeddedness]], [[network-inequality]], [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]].

### Sampson / collective-efficacy lineage (contrast — not this page's object)

[[sampson-robert-j|Robert J. Sampson]] (*[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]*, 2012, attributed) treats community-level “social capital” talk as **insufficient** for informal social control. He argues: (1) **dense ties can impede control** — networks connect “not just do-gooders but the underbelly” (drug dealers, gangs); Pattillo, Venkatesh, and Browning show dense local cohesion can foster networks that block ridding neighborhoods of organized crime (**negative social capital** / dual face of ties); (2) drawing on Granovetter, **shared expectations for control can be fostered without thick neighbor ties** — friendship-based urban-village order is mathematically impossible at city scale; (3) **[[collective-efficacy]]** elevates an active view beyond accumulation of personal resources in local ties or civic memberships, consistent with Portes’s redefinition of social capital as expectations for action within a collectivity — **resource potential of ties ≠ shared expectations for action**, which is why dense networks are not sufficient for control. Wilson’s point that poor neighborhoods can be tightly interconnected yet isolated from public resources is cited as part of this rationale. Do not rename CE findings as Bourdieusian social capital; cross-link [[collective-efficacy]], [[social-disorganization-theory]], [[sampson-great-american-city-study]].

## Operationalizations

- **Network size, status of contacts, institutional memberships** — later survey tradition.
- **In *Logic* (ethnographic):** mobilizable kin and affines; guarantors in marriage negotiations (prestigious kinsmen strengthen bargaining and underwrite agreements); *nesba* debts of honour; clienteles produced by honour strategies; repudiation subterfuges to avoid loss of alliance capital; practical groups resting on community of dispositions ([[habitus]]) and interests as basis of undivided ownership of material and symbolic patrimony.
- **Domestic unit as competition site.** Homogenized dispositions may engender competition for the same rare goods; cohesion of monopolistic groupings continuously threatened by competition for control over capital (Book II).

## Applications

- **[[kabyle]] matrimonial strategies and political credit.** Distant marriage as social-capital investment; parallel-cousin marriage as cheap/forced option presented as ideal; guarantors and market reputation.
- **Béarn.** Manipulation of genealogical presentation to justify alliances serving lineage interests.
- **Gift and honour clienteles.** *Thiwizi* and honour accumulation as production of mobilizable followings (overlap with [[symbolic-capital]]).
- **Academic and cultural fields** — network resources within fields; thin in *Logic*, fuller elsewhere.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Overlap with [[symbolic-capital]].** In honour economies, social and symbolic capital are tightly fused (*nesba* procured by symbolic capital); analytical separation is clearer in modern differentiated fields.
- **Circularity of network value.** Value of ties depends on capital of alters, which depends on their ties — a known measurement problem.
- **Popularization strips class analysis.** Putnam-style and policy "social capital" often drop domination, [[misrecognition]], and convertibility into economic capital — a semantic drift this page refuses.
- **Source limits.** Full Bourdieusian definition and quantitative operationalizations await forms-of-capital / later works; *Logic* is strongest on kinship-as-resource ethnography.
- **Urban/community dual-face of ties.** Sampson (2012, attributed) and related Chicago ethnography stress that network density can **attenuate** collective efficacy’s regulatory effect on violence (Browning negotiated-coexistence model) — a limitation for any usage that treats “more social capital” as uniformly protective.
