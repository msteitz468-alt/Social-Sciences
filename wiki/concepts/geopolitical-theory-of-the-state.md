# Geopolitical Theory of the State (Collins)

The **geopolitical theory of the state** is [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]]'s causal model, in *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975), Chapter 7, of how state power is built, extended, and lost as a function of territorial position and the organization of violence. It is an early statement of the framework Collins later developed into explicit long-term geopolitical prediction.

## Definition as Coined

Collins begins from the claim (attributed) that "the state is, above all, the army and the police" — the state *is* the way violence is organized. Political outcomes are therefore to be predicted from the conditions of military organization and territorial position. A **heartland** is defined (after Stinchcombe) as "the territory that is militarily accessible from a self-sufficient economic center."

## Semantic History

The word "geopolitics" carries the baggage of early-20th-century organic-state theory (Ratzel, Kjellén, Mackinder's "heartland"). Collins strips the organicism and restates territorial advantage as testable causal propositions within conflict theory. A terminological caution: although the tradition (and Collins's own later work) speaks of "marchland" advantage, in the 1975 text the systematic technical terms are **heartland**, **peripheral state**, and **interior state**; "marchland" appears only as informal narrative synonym.

## Operationalizations

Collins states (in Chapter 7's appendices) propositions linking:

- **Resource base and reach** — economic surplus and population density set potential army size; weapon type (expensive individual → aristocratic monopoly; cheap group → interdependent equals; expensive group → central command) and supply/administration type govern centralization.
- **Front exposure (peripheral vs. interior advantage)** — a **peripheral state** threatened from fewer directions accumulates resources undisturbed, while **interior/heartland-adjacent states** facing multiple simultaneous enemies tend to stalemate. Collins uses this to explain why the German and Italian states, at Europe's contested center, unified late and were "battered apart" while more cohesive states grew on the periphery.
- **Natural barriers** — become actual borders only where military organization on each side is roughly equal; otherwise the stronger heartland's border extends beyond the barrier. Where no barrier separates equally powerful heartlands, the intervening zone (e.g., the Franco-German border) becomes a site of "constant tension."
- **Overextension / logistical limits** — empire durations range from a few years (tribal conquest coalitions) to centuries (insulated bureaucratic empires: England ~900 years, Byzantium ~1500). Conquest among already-bureaucratized rivals is the least stable case: Napoleon's and Hitler's empires each lasted **under 15 years** — Collins's paradigm of catastrophic overextension.

## Applications

The model feeds Collins's account of [[state-formation|state formation]], the rise and fall of empires, and the structural preconditions of democracy and fascism (extending [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore]]'s five-class model). After 1975 it grounds his best-known applied claim, an early forecast of Soviet imperial contraction from geopolitical overextension.

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes` — critics question whether propositions calibrated against the historical record can be predictive rather than post hoc, and whether "logistical reach" and "resource advantage" can be measured independently of the outcomes they explain. Collins himself notes that prediction is possible short-range but "compounds into unpredictability over long chains." All claims are **attributed** to Collins (1975).
