# Mechanical solidarity

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] named **mechanical solidarity** the social cohesion that arises from **resemblance** among members: "a solidarity *sui generis* which, born of resemblances, directly links the individual with society" (attributed). He called it "mechanical" only by analogy — "to the cohesion which unites the elements of an inanimate body, as opposed to that which makes a unity out of the elements of a living body." Under it the individual conscience "is a simple dependent upon the collective type and follows all of its movements, as the possessed object follows those of its owner."

Durkheim argued this solidarity is strong precisely when the shared **[[collective-conscience]]** is large in **volume**, high in **intensity**, and high in **determinateness** — at its maximum "our individuality is nil." Mechanical solidarity and individual personality therefore vary **inversely** (one centripetal, one centrifugal). Its external index is **[[repressive-law|repressive/penal law]]**: crime is an act that "offends strong and defined states of the collective conscience," and punishment expiates the shared sentiment it wounds. All claims here are attributed to Durkheim.

## Semantic History

| Work | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| *Division of Labor* (1893) | Solidarity by likeness; indexed by repressive law; tied to the **segmental** social type; recedes as the division of labor advances |
| Later usage | Retained as one pole of the classic solidarity dichotomy; often loosened into "traditional community" (cf. Tönnies's *Gemeinschaft*, not equated by Durkheim) |

Durkheim paired it with **[[organic-solidarity]]** (solidarity through interdependence of differentiated functions); the passage from the first to the second is the master transformation of his social evolutionism.

## Operationalizations

- **Legal index**: the ratio of repressive to total law — the larger the repressive share, the more mechanical the solidarity ([[repressive-law]]). Durkheim tallied ancient codes (Pentateuch, Laws of Manu, Twelve Tables, Salic/Burgundian/Visigothic law) to argue the repressive share shrinks as societies advance (attributed; single-scholar textual coding).
- **Social-structural correlate**: the **segmental society with a clan-base** — Durkheim's "horde" (undifferentiated "social protoplasm"), the clan as its element, and politico-familial segments bound by a shared name/putative common origin more than verified kinship. He posited a general law (attributed): "the more the segmental organization with a clan-base is manifest and strong among a people, the more inferior is their social type."

## Applications

- Anchors the [[repressive-law]] / crime / punishment analysis of *Division of Labor* Book I.
- Contrast case for [[organic-solidarity]]: as segmental structure dissolves (Iroquois → Salic-law Franks → Republican Rome), mechanical solidarity gives way to organic.
- Feeds the wiki's cross-cultural comparison of solidarity types and the [[differentiation-and-integration]] lineage from [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The empirical base for "resemblance in primitive societies" leans on 19th-c. **race science and craniometry** (Le Bon, Waitz) that is discredited — record as Durkheim's period apparatus in attributed voice, never wiki voice (see [[social-fact]] reflexivity; four-way non-identity of culture/language/genes/people).
- Simpson (1933) objected that a conscience grounded in "likenesses" is not properly *common* (shared) but merely *similar* across individuals — a semantic critique of the whole construct (attributed to Simpson).
- The strict mechanical/organic dichotomy is a classic target of later critique (real societies mix both; the evolutionary ranking of "social types" is dated).
