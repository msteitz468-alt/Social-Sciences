# Earthfast Construction

Post-in-ground (earthfast) framing characteristic of much seventeenth-century **Chesapeake** domestic architecture, central to [[deetz-james|Deetz]]'s account of early Virginia building and capital allocation in *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]*.

## Definition as Coined

Structures whose principal uprights are set in dug posts rather than on continuous footings/cellars of more permanent English vernacular types. Archaeological recovery is often limited to posthole patterns, hearth bases, and related below-ground features.

## Semantic History

Tied to the "impermanent architecture" thesis in southern colonial studies (Carson and colleagues, as Deetz cites). Contrasts with surviving New England framed houses and with later brick/Georgian permanence.

## Operationalizations

Posthole plans; absence of cellars; cheap capital plant vs tobacco investment (Deetz's economic reading).

## Applications

Chesapeake plantation archaeology; comparative Anglo-American housing; [[flowerdew-hundred|Flowerdew Hundred]] and related sites.

## Deetz 1996 densification

*(Attributed.)* Entire seventeenth-century Chesapeake earthfast tradition almost without surface trace; commonest construction through the seventeenth century and into the eighteenth in places; thin refuse, scarce cellars, plow-removed hearths → detection hard; excavation closer to prehistoric technique. Economic reading (Carson et al. as Deetz cites): tobacco monoculture favors cheap plant (posts in ground) over permanent English-style capital in buildings. Slave quarters at Kingsmill and elsewhere also earthfast, with African American–associated additions (porches, root cellars) on English-derived frames. Contrast Anglo **sixteen-foot** module with Parting Ways **twelve-foot** modules (free Black New England — not earthfast but modular comparison).

## Critiques and Limitations

"Impermanent" is etic and relative; some earthfast buildings were substantial. Preservation bias: earthfast stock under-represents in standing architecture surveys.
