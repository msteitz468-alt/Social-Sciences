# Social Capital (Coleman)

[[coleman-james|Coleman]]'s rational-choice formulation of social capital: **aspects
of social structure that function as resources for actors** pursuing their interests.
Where [[social-capital|Bourdieu's version]] treats social capital as classed,
convertible network resources, and [[social-capital-theory|Putnam's civic version]]
treats it as community-level norms and trust, Coleman's is the **pivot** between
them — defined functionally, at the intersection of purposive action and social
structure, and given a **public-good character** that explains why it is chronically
underproduced. Coleman credits Glenn Loury (1977, 1987) with the term.

> Attributed voice throughout — this is Coleman's construct. Distinguish sharply
> from the other two social-capital lineages; the three are cross-linked, not
> merged. See [[social-capital]] (Bourdieu) and [[social-capital-theory]] (civic).

## Definition as Coined

Coleman defines social capital **by its function**, verbatim: "Social capital is
defined by its function. It is not a single entity, but a variety of different
entities having two characteristics in common: They all consist of some aspect of a
social structure, and they facilitate certain actions of individuals who are within
the structure." Unlike physical or human capital, Coleman argues, social capital
"inheres in the structure of relations between persons and among persons. It is
lodged neither in individuals nor in physical implements of production." It is not
fully fungible: valuable for facilitating some actions, useless or harmful for
others.

Coleman's three-capital contrast: physical capital is created by transforming
materials into tools; **human capital** by imparting skills to persons; **social
capital** by changing relations among persons so as to facilitate action. In his
node-and-line image (Fig. 12.1), human capital resides in the nodes (individuals),
social capital in the lines (relations) connecting them — and the two are usually
complementary (a parent needs both human capital and a social-capital-bearing
relation with the child to advance the child's development).

## Semantic History

Coleman attributes the term to Loury and notes parallel uses by Bourdieu (1980),
Flap and De Graaf (1986), Ben-Porath's "F-connection" (families, friends, firms),
and Granovetter's (1985) embeddedness critique of the "undersocialized concept of
man." His formulation stands between Bourdieu's earlier classed conception and the
later civic-trust literature of Putnam, which popularized and communitarianized the
structural core Coleman supplied (see [[social-capital-theory]]). The subsequent
drift — from *a structural resource for action* to *a community virtue* — is the
concept's chief semantic instability.

## Operationalizations

Coleman identifies **three forms**:

| Form | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Obligations and expectations | "credit slips" A holds against B, contingent on trustworthiness of the environment and density of outstanding obligations (rotating credit associations; the U.S. Senate "Club") |
| Information channels | using relations maintained for other purposes as low-cost information (Katz and Lazarsfeld on opinion leaders) |
| Effective norms and sanctions | a norm subordinating self-interest to collective interest — "powerful, but sometimes fragile" social capital |

He measures **family and community social capital** indirectly. In a 1980–82 U.S.
survey (~28,000 students; Hoffer 1986; Coleman 1988b), higher high-school dropout
was associated with single-parent households, more siblings, mothers not expecting
college, and residential mobility — read as proxies for reduced parent–child
attention and network closure (a two-parent/one-sibling/college-expecting profile
showed 8.1% dropout vs. 30.6% for the opposite). **Intergenerational closure** —
closure among the adults who each relate to a child — functions as social capital
supporting norms and sanctions; Coleman formalizes relative quantities via the
[[linear-system-of-action]] (in an open triad the central actor holds ~1.41× the
power of unconnected peers).

## Applications

- **Creation of human capital.** Religious-school (e.g. Catholic) lower dropout
  rates (Coleman and Hoffer 1987) are attributed to community social capital, not to
  doctrine as such.
- **The public-good aspect** (core argument): most social capital is a by-product of
  activity undertaken for other reasons, and its benefits accrue to persons other
  than those whose effort sustains it — producing systematic **underinvestment**.
  "Most forms of social capital are created or destroyed as a by-product of other
  activities … [they arise] or disappear without anyone's willing it into or out of
  being." (Contrast: social capital inside a for-profit firm, whose benefits the
  investing actor captures, is *not* systematically underprovided.)
- **Creation, maintenance, destruction** turn on **closure** (needed for norms and
  reliable trust-transmission), **stability** (mobility is inherently destructive of
  relation-based social capital), **ideology** (religious ideology can generate it;
  radical self-sufficiency suppresses it), and **reduced mutual dependence**
  (affluence and state provision lower the generation of new social capital). Like
  other capital, it **depreciates** if not renewed.
- **The replacement of primordial social capital** (chs. 22, 24): Coleman's
  historical thesis that modern [[corporate-actor|purposively constructed corporate
  actors]] have displaced the family, kin, and community that once supplied social
  capital — an insurance-like system distributing "sustained attention" on a
  needs-basis that markets cannot readily supply. Constructed replacements (schools,
  insurers, welfare agencies) are only partial substitutes, because they "undermine
  that existing incentive structure (by encouraging free riding) and do not generate
  a comparable replacement." Children are, he argues, the chief uncompensated
  losers; he speculates about new "microsocial institutions" to refill the void.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Closure vs. brokerage.** Coleman's insistence that **closure** — dense,
interconnected networks — is what makes norms enforceable and trust reliable is
directly opposed by [[structural-holes-theory|Burt's]] argument that social capital
comes instead from *brokerage* across [[structural-holes|structural holes]] in
*open*, sparse networks. The two prescribe contrary network shapes and are filed
as [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]] (Burt's own later work treats them as
complementary). The **functional definition** ("defined by its function") is
separately criticized as circular or underspecified — it names a role, not a mechanism, and risks labelling
any helpful relation "capital" post hoc. A tension runs between the public-good/
underinvestment framing and the rational-actor foundation (if social capital is
mostly an unwilled by-product, the purposive-action apparatus does limited work in
explaining its supply). The primordial-replacement thesis (chs. 22, 24) leans on
contested policy claims (e.g. Murray 1984 on welfare and an "underclass") and states
Coleman's own diagnostic and normative position — recorded here as attributed
argument, not wiki-voice fact. Coleman's construct is also frequently conflated with
Bourdieu's and Putnam's despite denoting a different object; keeping the three
distinct is the standing editorial task.

**Organizational formalization of trust and obligations (Small 2009).** *(Attributed —
[[small-mario-luis|Small]], *[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*.)*
Coleman's model of trust as information and risk calculation is engaged and partly
displaced: mothers in childcare centers often trusted acquaintances with childcare
despite scant personal information, via **repeated organizational encounter** and
**formal** devices (emergency contact lists) rather than only informal network
products. Small also treats **organizational (nonsocial) ties** as an institutional
form of social capital Coleman-style interpersonal SC underplays. See
[[organizational-embeddedness]], [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]].
