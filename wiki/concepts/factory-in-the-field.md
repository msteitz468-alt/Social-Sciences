# Factory in the Field

## Definition as Coined

Analytic image for the sugar [[plantation]] and mill as an early **industrial** form: field and boiling-house as coordinated factory process, not peasant farm. In *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]*, [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] writes of Puerto Rican cane workers (fieldwork from January 1948): they were not farmers or peasants but agricultural laborers owning neither land nor productive property — "wage earners who lived like factory workers, who worked in **factories in the field**," buying nearly all necessities from stores.

The same industrial logic organizes historical slave plantations: Europe invents overseas experiments combining land, coerced labor, capital, and technical plant to produce what workers do not principally consume.

## Semantic History

U.S. "factories in the field" agrarian critique (e.g., industrial agriculture and migrant labor) is an adjacent phrase-history; Mintz's Caribbean application is plantation-specific and deep-time (sixteenth century onward). Related: agro-industry; "combination of field and factory" in sugar technical literature Mintz draws on.

## Operationalizations

Describe labor discipline, crop-to-mill scheduling (cane spoils), technical plant (mills, boiling-houses), capital intensity, and the proletarian consumption basket of cane workers. Contrast with small traditional sugar makers (Jamaican loaves; Haitian *rapadou*).

## Applications

Caribbean and Atlantic sugar; Mintz's bridge from 1948 ethnography to early modern plantation history; metropolitan factory rhythms that later remade sugar *consumption* (time-saving tea, jam, convenience eating).

## Critiques and Limitations

How early and how fully "industrial"; relation to free vs slave labor debates; whether the image overstates continuity between slave plantation and wage agro-industry.
