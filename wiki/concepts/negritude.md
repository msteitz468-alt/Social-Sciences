# Négritude

## Definition as Coined

Négritude was coined by [[cesaire-aime]] in *Cahier d'un retour au pays natal*
(1939), naming a self-conscious Black diasporic literary and political identity
asserted against colonial devaluation of African and African-diasporic culture. In
*[[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]]* (1988),
[[clifford-james]] treats négritude as a case of neologistic identity-invention — a
term with no prior referent, deliberately manufactured to name and claim a collective
identity — and situates it as part of the broader interwar Parisian ferment that also
produced [[ethnographic-surrealism]]. Clifford presents the movement as being in full
swing by the early 1950s.

## Semantic History

Clifford identifies two divergent strands within the movement from its origins.
Léopold Sédar Senghor's version emphasizes an essential, traditionalist African
identity — a fixed cultural essence recoverable through the concept. Césaire's own
version, by contrast, is syncretic, parodic, and historically self-aware rather than
essentialist, a distinction Clifford draws partly via René Ménil's readings of
Césaire. Clifford cites Césaire's own explicit anti-biological, anti-essentialist
statements (via a 1980 interview with René Depestre) as evidence that Césaire
resisted the racial-essence reading of négritude that became attached to the
Senghorian strand. Clifford's own use of the concept treats it as evidence that
diasporic and colonized intellectuals could, in the same interwar Parisian milieu
that produced ethnographic surrealism, radically question inherited European
categories of knowledge and identity — a context also visible in Michel Leiris's 1950
writing.

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized as a measured construct; it functions in Clifford's account as an
illustrative case of identity as an active, historically situated invention rather
than a recovered essence — paralleling his broader argument (via
[[salvage-ethnography]] and [[art-culture-system]]) against treating cultural
identity as something simply salvaged or preserved.

## Applications

Clifford uses négritude to argue for a model of culture and identity as constructed
and strategic rather than given, extending this reading to his broader account of how
colonized and diasporic subjects fashioned usable collective identities under
colonial conditions. He links the Senghorian strand's emphasis on African
metaphysical essence to [[griaule-marcel]]'s Dogon cosmological research, noting the
two projects' thematic resonance — Griaule's account of an elaborate, unified Dogon
metaphysical system was received by some as congenial to a Senghorian essentialist
reading of African thought, a connection Clifford records as a position rather than
endorsing it (see [[griaule-dogon-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The standard critique, which Clifford records without fully adjudicating, is that the
essentialism charge lands squarely on the Senghorian strand of négritude but not on
Césaire's own more syncretic and historically self-conscious version — a distinction
frequently collapsed in later commentary that treats "négritude" as a single,
uniformly essentialist position. Clifford's citation of Césaire's 1980 anti-biological
statements is presented as evidence for this internal split rather than as a
resolution of the wider dispute over the movement's politics.
