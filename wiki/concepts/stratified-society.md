# Stratified Society

## Definition as Coined

In [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* (1967), **stratification** is a condition in which members of the same sex and equivalent age status **do not have equal access to the basic resources that sustain life**. Basic resources are treated ecologically (relative to technology and perceived environment) and as **capital-like ultimate sources** (land, water rights, raw materials), not mere consumables or stockpiled tools.

**Two impairment categories (attributed):** (1) total exclusion of some from direct access without periodic full-population redistribution of usufruct; (2) need to obtain access via payments/labor exceeding those of direct-access holders — the juncture at which **exploitation** appears (share of labor/produce for access rights).

**Distinct from ranking:** ranking is prestige scarcity; stratification is resource-access inequality. Prestige without resource privilege remains rank, not stratified, society.

## Semantic History

Broader sociological “social stratification” (class, status, party; occupational prestige scales) is **not** identical to Fried’s resource-access definition. Fried’s term is a political-evolutionary bridge between [[rank-society]] and [[the-state]].

## Operationalizations

From EPS Ch. 5 (attributed):

| Claim | Content |
|---|---|
| Rarity | Stratified societies **lacking state institutions** are almost impossible to find ethnographically |
| Instability | Once stratification exists, cause of stateship is implicit; formal state appears relatively quickly |
| Sanctions | Maintenance demands power beyond kinship resources adequate for egalitarian and rank society |
| Pressures | Differential livelihood access + labor exploitation create pressures kinship/ideology alone cannot contain |
| Origins (positive list) | Population pressure; postmarital residence shifts; resource contraction/alteration; subsistence/market shifts; managerial roles from social/ceremonial maturation |
| Origins (rejected) | **War** and **slavery** as *initiating* conditions — war follows stratification; slavery *is* a form of stratification |
| Wittfogel | No necessary hydraulic–stratification link; China chronology may reverse manager→state story |
| Method | Reconstruct from earliest states + logic between complex rank and simple states; no pristine field site |

NW Coast “slavery,” on Fried’s reading, is captive status within ranking games, **not** generalized economic stratification of basic resources.

## Applications

Bridge form every [[pristine-state]] must traverse (attributed); secondary states need not. Kachin residence-rule breach / gumsa materials as secondary stratification path; Tikopia land-tenure narrowing under population pressure as density–tenure illustration.

### Kirch 1984 — Tonga and Hawaiʻi as stratified Polynesian endpoints

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984; framed through Sahlins’s and Goldman’s typologies as Kirch deploys them.)* Kirch maps contact-era Polynesia onto a **stratification continuum** rather than a single Fried stage. Extremes he illustrates: Pukapuka (two levels, limited *tabu* separation, economic decisions in male village meetings); Futuna (paramount *sau*, lineage *aliki*, kava rank validation, chiefly impingement on household production); **Hawaiʻi** as the extreme — “chiefs had become a conical clan superimposed upon a truncated class of commoners” — approximated also by [[tonga|Tonga]] and the Society Islands (seven or eight *aliʻi* grades; sibling marriage; *kapu moe*; *konohiki*-regulated production; corvée; land control by chiefs, not ramages). Region-wide, increased stratification was most significant in Tonga, the Societies, and Hawaiʻi.

**Goldman Traditional / Open / Stratified (as Kirch reproduces it).** Traditional: seniority, *mana*/sanctity, sacred chief, graded worth. Open: seniority modified by military/political effectiveness. Stratified: status differences are economic and political; high rank holds rule and land title; commoners are subject and landless — a synthesis of Traditional and Open; most stratified Polynesian societies border the true state (Sahlins on Hawaiʻi: threshold at “the boundary of primitive society itself”).

**Contact structural departures from APS.** By contact Hawaiʻi had two fundamental departures from Ancestral Polynesian Society: commoners **lost genealogies** and **lost direct control of land** — yielding the superposed chiefly conical clan over truncated commoners paying tribute. Tonga by contact: class stratification, formalized dual paramountship, multi-tiered decision-making, maritime empire binding outliers by *‘inasi* and prestige-goods outflow. Kirch correlates Sahlins stratification levels with maximal political-unit size and arable density but refuses single-direction demography→stratification primacy. Parallel Tonga–Hawaiʻi pathways (sacred/secular split; ritual annual tribute as elaborated first-fruits; administrative hierarchy) are attributed to shared APS base under isolation, not diffusion.

### Adams — kin and class (1966, attributed)

In *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* ch. III, [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] defines **class** for early states as **objectively differentiated degrees of access to the means of production**, without requiring sharply reduced mobility, class consciousness, or overt interclass struggle. Parallel sequence in Mesopotamia and central Mexico: corporate kin groups originally preponderating in land control are gradually supplemented by private estates of urban elites; by terminal points corporate kin remain active but **encapsulated** in a rigidly class-divided pattern. Elementary units (Mesopotamian corporate kin / Mexican *calpulli* and conical clans) take on specialized economic, military, and administrative functions. Mortuary and textual evidence (Early Dynastic gradients; Aztec *pilli*/*mayeque*/slaves) densify the sequence as of mid-1960s knowledge. Stratification and polity are analytically independent but intertwined; managerial (Mesopotamian temple/estate) vs more predatory (Aztec conquest-estate) elite styles are noted as variants within one fundamental course. See [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Unobservable pure type risks circular stage construction.
- Population-pressure causation contested (Adams; Fortes & Evans-Pritchard density remarks).
- Overlap with Marxist class and Engels origin-of-the-state narratives invites conflation; Fried uses exploitation language but is not simply Engels restated.
- Whether any archaeological phase cleanly matches “stratified nonstate” remains debated.

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): rank turned into stratification

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]].)* Flannery and Marcus describe stratification as **created** when a society draws "an invisible line" across a formerly continuous rank hierarchy, hardening it into endogamous strata (marriage restricted within a stratum). Consequences they trace include ever-lengthening noble genealogies, competition for maximally elite spouses, and the relaxation of incest taboos to permit royal sibling marriage (concentrating *mana*). Their model case is [[tonga|Tonga]] (and, more sharply, [[hawaiians|Hawai'i]], where the intermediate landed-gentry category was eliminated to leave two strata). They explicitly distinguish this genealogically-defined, endogamous "stratum/class" from the wealth-continuum "upper/middle/lower class" of modern industrial societies. See [[rank-society]], [[hereditary-rank]], [[the-state]].
