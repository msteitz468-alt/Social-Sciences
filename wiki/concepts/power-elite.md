# Power Elite

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]] defined the
**power elite** as those "whose positions enable them to transcend the ordinary environments
of ordinary men and women; they are in positions to make decisions having major consequences."
Whether they act or fail to act is secondary to occupying "the strategic command posts of the
social structure," in which are centered "the effective means of the power and the wealth and the
celebrity which they enjoy." They "rule the big corporations," "run the machinery of the state,"
and "direct the military establishment."

Mills insisted (attributed) that **non-decision** is part of the concept: failure to act from a
pivotal position is often more consequential than the decisions made. The elite is not a solitary
ruler or a secret cabal; advisers, consultants, and opinion-makers serve higher thought, while
professional celebrities distract mass attention. Immediately below sit the
[[middle-levels-of-power]] — Congress, pressure groups, local and regional upper classes.

## Semantic History

Mills coined the phrase as a characterizing term for mid-twentieth-century American higher
circles. He explicitly **rejected** several near-synonyms:

- **"Ruling class"** — too loaded: "'Class' is an economic term; 'rule' a political one." The
  phrase smuggles a simple theory that an economic class rules politically, understates
  political and military autonomy, and says nothing about the military as such.
- **"Military clique"** — too narrow; what the public called the Washington military clique was,
  for Mills, a **coalition** of generals as executives, politicians as admirals, executives as
  politicians — the three hierarchies in uneasy coincidence.
- **Omnipotent conspiracy / "great men"** — vulgar substitutes for institutional analysis.
- **Impotent elite / pure [[theory-of-balance]]** — confuses middle-level bargaining with the top.

He also distinguished the power elite from a hereditary **aristocracy**: the American elite has
no compact noble basis in a few great families, though members derive disproportionately from
upper strata of local society and the Metropolitan 400.

Later usage (Domhoff's governing class, "power structure" research, journalistic "power elite")
extends and sometimes dilutes Mills's institutional three-hierarchy specification. Record those
extensions as attributed positions when sources are ingested; do not equate them silently with
Mills 1956.

## Operationalizations

Mills did not offer a survey scale. He operationalized the elite **institutionally and
biographically**:

| Indicator | Mills's use (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Command posts | Top of giant corporations; political directorate (esp. executive); high military |
| Scale facts | Hundreds of giant firms dominate the economy; centralized executive state; military as largest/most expensive government feature |
| Social origins | Bulk of very rich, executives, political outsiders, high military from upper third of income/occupation |
| Interlock | Career interchange; clubs, schools, style of life; explicit coordination in crisis |
| Historical epochs | Five-phase sketch of US power structure culminating in "military capitalism" with a political vacuum at the top |

Evidence bases included elite directories, corporate and military lists, biographical careers,
secondary statistics, and institutional history — not participant observation of the elite
itself (Mills noted the top is "often inaccessible").

## Applications

- **Anatomy of US power** in the early Cold War: corporate, military, and political command
  posts as co-determinants of major decisions (war, economy, state direction).
- **Critique of pluralism**: the [[theory-of-balance]] fits the [[middle-levels-of-power]], not
  the top ([[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]).
- **Bridge from *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]***: the propertyless new middle class
  faces a concentrated apex it does not control.
- **Moral diagnosis**: [[organized-irresponsibility]] and [[higher-immorality]] as structural
  features of elite–mass architecture, not mere personal vice.
- **Later power-structure research** treated the book as a research program to refine (network
  interlocks, classwide property, state autonomy debates) — see Wolfe afterword summary on
  [[mills-power-elite-study]] and [[hubs/studies/mills-power-elite-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Central dispute: [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]. Pluralists contested elite unity and
decisiveness; later scholars (state autonomy, fragmented business, revised military sociology)
revised parts of the military-ascendancy and business–state picture. Mills's own cautions —
uneasy coincidence not monolithic will; historically specific shape; rejection of conspiracy —
are often lost in popular reception. All of Mills's theses remain **attributed**, not wiki-voice
findings.
