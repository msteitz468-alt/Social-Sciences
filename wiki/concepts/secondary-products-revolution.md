# Secondary Products Revolution

## Definition as Coined

[[sherratt-andrew|Andrew Sherratt]]’s **Secondary Products Revolution** (SPR; also **secondary products complex**) names a proposed second major transformation of Old World farming after the initial [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic]] package. Following Greenfield’s operational gloss (cited by Sherratt), **secondary products** are those for which animals may be utilised repeatedly over their lifetimes (milk, wool, traction), as against **primary products** (meat, bone, hide) that require slaughter. The classic paper is “Plough and Pastoralism: Aspects of the Secondary Products Revolution” (1981), with extensions in 1983 (revised to 1995), 1987, and the 1997 collection *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]*.

Sherratt (intro, 1997) frames the fourth millennium BC western Old World as undergoing changes “comparable in magnitude to the beginning of farming” some five millennia earlier and “in large part a consequence of it.” He treats the Neolithic beginning as the “original Consumer Revolution” and the SPR as “its second round” (invoking Runnels & van Andel 1988 on cash-crop-like farming).

## Semantic History

- **Coinage and reception:** Sherratt’s 1997 Section III headnote is frank about “inventing” the concept to stress the *otherness* of early farming (meat-focused, hoe horticulture) versus later plough–pastoral economies. Piggott and Renfrew receptions are registered there; Renfrew’s minimalist “intensification” reading is accepted as still serving that contrast.
- **Childe dialogue:** In Ch.20 (1995) and the Section III intro, Sherratt argues Europe’s SPR impact is the outer-Eurasian spin-off of Childe’s [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]] in Mesopotamia — parallel to how Europe’s earlier farming impact was a spin-off of the Near Eastern [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]]. The plough/wheeled-vehicle/horse/wool/alcohol complex recovers Childe’s Europe–Near East concern under a different causal packaging.
- **Self-corrections (1997):** Original Ch.6 error linking TRB *beginnings* to the plough abandoned (TRB start re-read as loess Neolithic × indigenous fusion); ox-traction routes redrawn through Anatolia as well as Pontic steppes; liquid vessels split (lower forms milk-processing, upper/metal models more likely alcohol).
- **Critiques already in the volume:** Horwitz & Tchernov (1989) deny a circumscribed “revolution” and object to combining data without urban context; Sherratt answers that Ch.7 embeds urban historical context. Chapman’s challenges on wool/wheels/ploughmarks are answered in the 1987 paper (cart introduced; plough earlier in Near East / mid-fourth millennium in Europe; drinking vessels for alcohol not milk; wool c.3000 BC).
- **Post-1997 residue dairying** (see [[residue-analysis]]) pulls milk use earlier than the classic mid/late fourth-millennium package — a **timing** challenge recorded on the method page; not present in Sherratt’s own essays as lipid chemistry.

## Applications

- **Settlement:** Plough spread restructures settlement — wider clearings, less long-lived sites, fallow supporting pastoral sectors; SE tell patterns modified (valley-edge/spur/upland; larger spaced/fortified tells); Hungarian Plain cycles of aggregation and dispersal co-vary with traction horizon (Ch.10–11).
- **Social structure:** Invokes Goody (1976) and HRAF patterns: hoe/female labour ↔ uxorilocal/matrilineal tendencies; plough + pastoralism ↔ male subsistence dominance, virilocal residence, patrilineal descent; diverging devolution of property.
- **Exchange and metals:** Secondary farming features travel with Uruk-periphery impress (vehicles, alcohol, textiles, metalwork); SE Europe Baden–Cernavodă–Ezero transformation; longer-distance trade enabled by equid transport.
- **Steppe / language (associated, not identity):** Cart + horse enable interfluve pastoralisation; population expansion onto steppes linked (as of Sherratt 1981) to conditions for Indo-European differentiation — see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]; **culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people**.
- **Intoxicants:** Alcoholic drinks travel with the secondary-products package (Ch.15–16); see [[prehistoric-intoxicants]].
- **Historiography:** Distinguishes mature Neolithic vs Copper Age pathways (Ch.5); plough as test of European indigenism vs external introduction.

## Critiques and Limitations

1. **Bundle timing:** Milk, wool, and traction need not switch on together; residue dairying later pulls milk earlier than classic package dates.
2. **“Revolution” rhetoric:** Long-process critics (Horwitz & Tchernov) reject the label; Sherratt retains it for analytical contrast even in “minimalist” form.
3. **Diffusion vs autonomy:** Renfrew-type autonomy readings of European plough/metallurgy remain live positions; Sherratt insists fourth-millennium change is a historical conjunction with Near Eastern urban periphery, not pure local trend.
4. **Evidence unevenness:** Direct wheels rare (waterlogged); plough-marks regional; equid claims revised within the volume itself.
5. **Horizon:** Pre-aDNA essays; any genetic population narrative must be date-stamped subordinate to later genomic layers (as of Reich 2018 and subsequent aDNA).

### Anthony densification (2007) — wool, wheels, and steppe timing

*(Attributed — [[anthony-david-w|David W. Anthony]], *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* 2007.)* Anthony uses Sherratt’s secondary-products framing as chronological and economic scaffolding for late PIE, especially **wool** and **wagons/traction**.

- **Partial survival of the SPR package:** Anthony treats Sherratt’s Secondary Products Revolution as a useful synthesis whose components (wool sheep, wheeled vehicles, secondary herd products) partially survive critique as dating anchors for when late PIE speakers lived (R1-080). Wheel origin is left open — not necessarily Mesopotamian (R1-081).
- **Wool termini for PIE:** Woven wool textiles require long fibers from genetic mutants; wild sheep undercoat is “structurally unspinnable” (Barber via Anthony). Conventional wool-sheep horizon ~4000–3500 BCE; Near Eastern butchering patterns shift after ~3400/3350 BCE; European wool sheep ~3300–3100 BCE with earlier hints; wool textiles widespread by ~2800 BCE (R1-064–067). PIE *HwlHn- ‘wool’ has cognates in almost all branches including Hittite (archaic depth); Anthony treats wool vocabulary as a weaker absolute terminus than the wheel package (R1-063, R1-068).
- **Early felt/plucking hypothesis:** For DDII steppe acceptance of sheep after ~5200 BCE, he suggests animals may already have been plucked for felt and that wool vocabulary “might have first appeared among Pre-Proto-Indo-European speakers at about this time” (R3-076) — speculative, attributed.
- **Post-3600 BCE eastern Europe:** Following Sherratt, Scheibenhenkel drinking cups mark intoxicating-beverage ritual; by Cernavoda III/Baden-Boleraz ~3600–3200 BCE horses appear widely with larger (possibly wool) sheep; 60–91% adult sheep-goat kill-off in middle Danube lowland sites read as secondary-products management (R4-031).
- **Link to pastoral mobility:** Wagons + riding + herd secondary products underwrite Yamnaya deep-steppe mobility (cross-link [[pastoralism]], [[animal-traction]], [[yamnaya-culture]]); do not equate SPR package with IE speech.
