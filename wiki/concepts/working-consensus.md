# Working Consensus

## Definition as Coined

[[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (*[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, 1956) argued that projected definitions of the situation in face-to-face encounters are usually sufficiently attuned to avoid open contradiction — but this is **not** candid consensus of real feelings. Each participant is expected to suppress immediate heartfelt feelings and convey a temporarily acceptable view; surface agreement is facilitated by concealing wants behind value-statements offered for lip-service. There is a **division of definitional labour**.

Together, participants contribute to a single overall definition involving **not so much real agreement as to what exists as real agreement as to whose claims concerning what issues will be temporarily honoured**, plus agreement to avoid open conflict of definitions. Goffman called this level of agreement a **working consensus** — an interactional modus vivendi — whose content varies by setting (friends at lunch vs specialist–client) (attributed).

The working consensus is typically **contradicted** by performers' attitude toward the audience in its absence and by carefully controlled **[[communication-out-of-character|communication out of character]]** while the audience is present. After crises that shift social distance, the previous working consensus is often re-established, "albeit bashfully" (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Observe what contradictions are *not* voiced; when a scene breaks the polite appearance of consensus; post-crisis restoration of form. Not a survey "agreement" item.

## Applications

Service encounters; professional–client relations; domestic hospitality; any two-team interaction under temporary truce "to get business done." Links: [[definition-of-the-situation]], [[impression-management]], [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Can be misread as claiming people never mean what they say — Goffman analysed the *level* of official projection, not total psychology.
- Thin on power asymmetries that force whose claims get honoured (though illustrations include super/subordinate teams).
