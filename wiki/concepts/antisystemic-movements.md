# Antisystemic Movements

## Definition as Coined

**Antisystemic movements** is the world-systems term for organized challenges to the [[capitalist-world-economy|capitalist world-economy]] — classically including social-democratic/labor movements, national-liberation movements, and later New Left / identity / alternative movements. The category unifies diverse oppositions at the scale of the historical system.

## Semantic History

Program language of the Arrighi–Hopkins–Wallerstein collaboration; used extensively in the 2004 volume (Arrighi; González Casanova; van der Linden on internationalism). Differs from purely national "social movement" sociology by system-level frame.

## Operationalizations

Historical typologies of movement families; success/failure relative to state power and hegemony; incorporation vs transformation. Not a survey instrument.

## Applications

Arrighi on hegemony and antisystemic opportunity structures; Casanova on present trends; van der Linden on [[proletarian-internationalism]]'s long view; Chase-Dunn on movement strategy through hegemonic obstacles.

### Arrighi–Hopkins–Wallerstein propositions (as restated by Arrighi 2004; attributed)

Classical antisystemic movements institutionalized opposition in two main varieties (labor/social-democratic; national liberation); state power was the mid-term objective; obtaining state power often failed ultimate transformation and produced new instruments of oppression; 1968-family movements reacted against US-hegemonic recuperation and Old Left failure; 1989 continued 1968 by ending Old Left illusions. Arrighi amends with Gramscian **passive revolution** and the **Piedmontese function** of hegemonic states.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of teleology (all opposition as "anti-system"); underplays movements that are anti-status-quo but pro-capitalist or xenophobic; gender and household-based politics may fall outside classical typologies (see von Werlhof's challenge).
