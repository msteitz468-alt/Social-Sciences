# Knowable Communities

Concept Marcus and Fischer take up (via Raymond Williams) in *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|ACC]]* Chapter 4 to name the problem of representing **local communities** that are only intelligibly "knowable" when ethnography takes account of **world-historical political economy** — larger systems in which those communities are embedded.

## Definition as Coined

In ACC's framing, experimental ethnography that stays only at intimate meaning (person/self/emotions) is incomplete without parallel experiments that situate knowable local worlds in political-economic structures (world system, class, colonial and neocolonial relations). Marcus and Fischer take Williams's suggestion that experiments should merge "knowable communities" with the "darkly unknowable" via **combinatory texts** linking intimate detail of language and manners to portraits of larger impersonal systems that both affect local communities and are internalized in subjects' lives. Related Williams concept they deploy: **structure of feeling** — articulation of everyday experience with larger systems and subtle ideology.

## Semantic History

Williams's literary-cultural usage concerns how novels make social worlds knowable; Marcus and Fischer relocate the problem to ethnography under global interdependence.

## Operationalizations

Political-economy ethnographies (e.g., works they discuss such as Willis, Taussig, and related world-system embeddings); multi-locale designs (cf. Marcus in *Writing Culture*).

## Applications

Balances ACC Ch. 3 (experiential ethnography) with Ch. 4 (system embedding); links [[anthropology-as-cultural-critique]] to [[world-systems-analysis]] without equating the programs.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of political-economy reduction of cultural difference (a danger Marcus & Fischer discuss in related interpretive debates); risk of world-system scale swamping ethnographic intimacy.
