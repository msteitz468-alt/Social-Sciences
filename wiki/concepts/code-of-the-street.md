# Code of the Street

## Definition as Coined

[[anderson-elijah|Elijah Anderson]] defines the **code of the street** as informal prescriptions and proscriptions — “people’s law” or street justice — organized around a desperate search for **respect** (and the credible threat of vengeance) that governs public social relations, especially violence, in the most economically depressed, drug- and crime-ridden inner-city pockets (*[[anderson-code-of-the-street-1999|Code of the Street]]*, 1999). The code emerges **where the influence of the police ends** and personal responsibility for safety is felt to begin. Displays of “nerve” and “heart” build a reputation for payback that deters aggression and disrespect. Respect functions as scarce **social capital** when other capital is denied (Anderson 1999 Ch 2).

Anderson distinguishes the code from Wolfgang & Ferracuti’s **subculture of violence**: the code applies predominantly to **public** behavior and is fully normative for only a **segment** of the community, while remaining defensive knowledge for others (attributed, 1999 notes / Ch 1).

## Semantic History

| Phase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Emic / local | Residents’ rules of watching one’s back, carrying protection, not being “dissed,” settling matters man-to-man (e.g. Don Moses formulation, Anderson 1999) |
| Anderson 1990s | Analytic construct: public order under institutional vacuum; tied to poverty, alienation, drug markets |
| Afterlife | Criminology/education scales; media shorthand sometimes collapses to “black street culture” (distortion risk) |

## Operationalizations

- **Ethnographic (Anderson):** rules, presentations of self, sanctioning, staging areas, school as street extension.
- **Survey afterlife:** attitude items on respect, toughness, distrust of police — may thin the concept; mixed predictive results → study `replication_status: partially-supported`.

## Applications

Youth interpersonal violence; school climate; drug-market regulation; gender/mating scripts under joblessness; code-switching by [[decent-and-street|decent]] youth; policy arguments that living-wage jobs weaken the code’s hold (Anderson 1999 Conclusion — POSITION).

**Adjacent (not identical):** [[bourgois-philippe|Bourgois]]’s [[street-culture]] (*[[bourgois-in-search-of-respect-study|In Search of Respect]]*, 1995/2003) also organizes **respect** under inner-city exclusion and drug markets, but as a wider resistance/self-destruction complex (work, school, gender, community ruin) rather than Anderson’s tighter public-order “people’s law” with decent/street family typology. Comparative teaching pair; do not equate the constructs.

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-city origin; risk of reification and racial pathologizing when majority-decent and code-switching claims are dropped; circularity risk (violence explained by rules defined around violence); culture-vs-structure disputes with Wilsonian and segregation accounts. See [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]] and [[hubs/studies/anderson-code-of-the-street-study]].
