Coleman's theory of the social norm treats norms as objects to be *explained* by rational actors' interests rather than assumed as primitive social facts. A norm, on this account, is a right of control over an action held not by the actor performing it but by others — and its existence depends on two independent conditions: a demand condition (why interested parties would want the right transferred) and a realization condition (whether that transfer can actually be brought about). The concept is developed across chapters 10–11 of *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990) and reformulated in a fully quantitative version in chapter 30.

## Definition as Coined

Coleman defines a norm as existing "when a socially defined right to control an actor's action is held not by the actor but by others" (ch.11). He explicitly rejects functional explanation of norms — the observation that a norm serves some function for its holders is, he argues, only the first of two necessary conditions for its emergence, not a sufficient explanation ("the functions a norm serves for those who hold it... are not sufficient as an explanation of its emergence"). The full explanatory task requires the demand condition and the realization condition together.

## Semantic History

Coleman's norm concept was deliberately built against the Durkheimian tradition in which norms figure as pre-given social facts constraining individuals from outside, and against Parsonsian normative theory in which shared values are posited as the integrative glue of social systems. Coleman instead derives norms endogenously from rational actors' interests in controlling externality-generating actions, following the demand/realization structure worked out chapter by chapter (ch.10 demand, ch.11 realization). By chapter 30 the same account is restated formally within Coleman's linear system of action: a norm favoring an action emerges when the aggregate value to affected actors of the action being taken exceeds the value, to the actor, of not taking it (eqs. 30.12–30.13). Coleman treats legal theory's case-by-case, micro-to-macro method as the appropriate model for studying systems of norms, rather than abstract analysis of norm content.

## Operationalizations

**Demand condition (ch.10)**: a norm's demand arises where an action produces externalities (positive or negative) for a set of others, a market in rights of control over the action cannot easily be established (due to transaction costs, illegality, or dispersion of affected parties), and no single actor can profitably exchange for control bilaterally. Where two actors can reach a Coasean bilateral solution, Coleman argues no norm is needed; norms are required only in *n*-person (n>2) situations.

**Realization condition (ch.11)**: even where demand exists, a norm is realized only if beneficiaries can overcome the [[free-rider-problem]] (specifically its second-order form) in sanctioning violators — see that page for the mechanics.

**Typologies**: Coleman distinguishes *proscriptive* norms (discouraging a focal action, negative feedback) from *prescriptive* norms (encouraging it, positive feedback); *disjoint* norms (beneficiaries and targets are different sets of actors, e.g., adult norms directed at children) from *conjoint* norms (beneficiaries and targets coincide, e.g., an anti-hoarding norm among coworkers); and *conventional* norms (the focal action's direction is arbitrary, e.g., driving on the right) from *essential* norms (direction is not arbitrary; targets' and beneficiaries' interests would exist independent of the norm). He draws partial parallels to Ullmann-Margalit's (1977) typology of PD norms, coordination norms, and norms of partiality, noting the mappings are not exact.

**Social efficiency**: for conjoint, homogeneous-group norms, social efficiency is a straightforward Pareto-style gain to all. For disjoint norms, Coleman argues "social efficiency" is relative to the existing distribution of power and resources — both the presence and absence of a disjoint norm can be Pareto-optimal, with only one "socially efficient" given the standing distribution. He states this explicitly to avoid naturalizing functionalist readings of norms governing subordinate groups (citing Gluckman 1955, 1963 on norms affecting women in traditional societies) as neutral rather than power-relative.

## Applications

Coleman predicts, from differential power, that both norm violation and sanctioning should correlate positively with power (citing Bailey 1971, Starr 1978); that low-status actors are less compliant despite being no less gossiped-about (Pitt-Rivers 1971); and that actors with outside social ties beyond the closed group of norm-holders are less compliant (Bott 1971, Merry 1981). He also argues norms can arise absent prior externalities, generating *positive externalities of group differentiation* — high-school dress codes, religious dress conventions, and norms of etiquette (drawing on Elias's historical work on table manners) that create and maintain status groups by making conformity an elaborated, exclusionary signal; Coleman flags this extension as "more conjectural" and in need of empirical study. Internalization of norms is treated as a parallel mechanism to external sanctioning, used both in individual socialization and in corporate actors' principal-agent problems (stock options, long-tenure expectations); Coleman predicts underinvestment in internalization structurally parallels firms' underinvestment in general human capital, and provocatively predicts that egalitarian, laissez-faire parenting produces *less* internalization than authoritarian parenting.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman himself acknowledges that internalization of norms — why an actor comes to accept a norm as legitimate rather than merely comply under sanction — pushes at the edge of rational-choice theory's explanatory reach, citing social-psychological findings (Asch 1956, Sherif 1936) not straightforwardly reducible to rational choice. He argues this weakness is "far less debilitating," however, than beginning theory from societal purposes or norms taken as given — the central methodological-individualist commitment of the book. His demand/realization framework stands in explicit contrast to Durkheim's treatment of norms as constraining social facts and to Parsons's normative-integration account; the comparison and its stakes for wiki-voice claims about norm origins remain attributed to Coleman's own theoretical program rather than settled fact.
