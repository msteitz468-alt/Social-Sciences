# Social Drama

> Analytical concept developed by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]], first in *Schism and
> Continuity in an African Society* (1957) and worked through the ritual analyses of
> *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]* (1967) and
> *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969). The concept is the
> processual, [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] core of Turner's method; its fullest
> formal statement comes in his later *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors* (1974, not yet
> ingested).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Turner.)* A **social drama** is a processual unit isolating an episode of
observable social conflict, through which the anthropologist reads the otherwise abstract
structure and dynamics of a society. In its mature form Turner describes four phases:
**breach** of a norm-governed relationship; **crisis** (widening cleavage that mobilizes
factions); **redressive action** (juridical or ritual mechanisms deployed to heal the
breach); and **reintegration** of the disturbed group or public recognition of an
irreparable **schism**. Ritual, in this frame, is not decoration but the redressive machinery
of social process — "a phase in broad social processes."

Within the two ingested books the term itself is used sparingly, but the *method* is
pervasive (attributed):

- In *The Forest of Symbols*, Turner analyzes a particular 1953 **Mukanda** (boys'
  circumcision) in the Nyaluhana vicinage as an unfolding of village succession politics
  between rival headmen — an [[extended-case-method|extended case]] rather than a normative
  account of "the" rite.
- In "A Ndembu Doctor in Practice," the healer Ihembi's treatment of Kamahasanyi's *ihamba*
  affliction is analyzed as ritual therapy that "remedies the ills of a corporate group"
  rather than an individual — the climactic tooth-extraction literally drawing out "the hidden
  animosities of the village."
- In *The Ritual Process*, the theoretical generalization: society is "a type of dialectical
  process" in which "maximization of communitas provokes maximization of structure, which in
  its turn produces revolutionary strivings for renewed communitas" (see [[communitas]]).

## Semantic History

- **1957:** *Schism and Continuity* — the social drama as a device for analyzing Ndembu
  village fission around figures like Sandombu; the empirical birth of the concept within
  Gluckman's [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] and the [[extended-case-method]].
- **1960s:** ritual read as the redressive phase of social process (the two ingested books).
- **1974 (*Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors*):** the four-phase model stated formally and
  extended cross-culturally and trans-temporally; the pivot toward performance and "processual
  symbology" (not yet ingested).
- **Afterlife:** the dramaturgical/performance turn — Turner's collaboration with theatre
  director Richard Schechner — carried the concept into performance studies. Compare, at a
  distance, Goffman's [[dramaturgical-analysis|dramaturgy]] (micro-interactional, not
  processual-structural).

## Operationalizations

The social drama is operationalized as a bounded observational unit: the analyst identifies a
**breach** of a norm-governed relationship, follows the widening **crisis** and the factional
alignments it mobilizes, documents the **redressive** mechanisms deployed (a divination, a court
case, a curative ritual), and records the outcome as **reintegration** or acknowledged
**schism**. In practice Turner reconstructs the sequence from participant-observation of a
particular performance plus genealogical and case data — treating the named actors, their
kinship positions, and their disputes as the data (e.g. the Nyaluhana-vs-Machamba succession
conflict driving the 1953 Mukanda). The four phases are the coding frame; the [[ndembu]]
village and its shifting alliances are the unit.

## Applications

- Ndembu village fission; the 1953 Mukanda; the Ihembi cure (see [[ndembu]]).
- The general structure/communitas oscillation as a macro-social drama.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The four-phase model risks imposing a narrative arc (breach→reintegration) on conflicts
  that do not resolve; Turner allows the schism outcome, but the frame can flatter tidy cases.
- Like the [[extended-case-method]] generally, it can move quickly from a single observed
  incident to large structural claims.
