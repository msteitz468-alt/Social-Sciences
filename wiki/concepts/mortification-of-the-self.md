# Mortification of the self

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]* Essay 1, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] analyzes how [[total-institution|total institutions]] effect a **mortification of the self**: processes that curtail the inmate's prior self and its supporting social arrangements. The recruit is shaped into an object that can be fed into the administrative machinery; the "presenting culture" brought from home is subjected to disculturation and role dispossession (attributed).

### Named components (attributed, Essay 1)

- **Role dispossession** — loss of civilian roles and the scheduling that sustained them; barrier to outside roles.
- **Admission procedures / trimming** — life history, photographing, weighing, fingerprinting, numbers, searching, listing possessions for storage, undressing, bathing, disinfecting, issue of institutional clothing, instruction in rules — a "leaving off and taking on."
- **Identity kit** — the personal possessions and cosmetic equipment through which the individual ordinarily presents a self; total institutions typically deprive inmates of it and substitute institutional issue.
- **Obedience tests and will-breaking** — deference requirements, forced activity, verbal or physical humiliation.
- **Contaminative exposure** — forced interpersonal contact, disclosure of private facts, mess and dirt, physical violation of personal territory; exposure of intimate secrets as staff tactic.
- **Looping** — defensive responses to mortifying conditions themselves become further evidence for staff judgment and further grounds for restriction; the inmate's reaction is fed back into the system that produced it (attributed).
- **Privilege system** — after baseline deprivations are established, house rules, rewards, and punishments reorganize the self around institutional incentives; messing up brings demotion through privilege grades.
- **Echelon authority** — any staff member may discipline any inmate across a wide domain of living, not only within a specialized work jurisdiction.

Mortification is not only "once at the door"; it continues in the daily round and in the inpatient phase of the [[moral-career]] (ward system, psychiatric self-attacks, case-record reconstruction of biography — Essay 2).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Goffman 1961 | Technical term for identity-stripping in total institutions |
| General sociology | Metaphor for identity loss under extreme institutions; sometimes diluted |
| Clinical critique | Read by some as denying disease process; Goffman separates social fate of patienthood from clinical pathology |

## Operationalizations

Observational inventory of admission and daily mortifying practices; comparative across institution types. Not a psychometric scale in the source. Essay 2 links mortification to the **ward system** as graded living arrangements expressing status without the person's final control.

## Applications

- Mental-hospital and prison admission as paradigm cases.
- Comparative notes on monasteries, military boot camp, boarding schools.
- Analytical bridge from institutional structure to [[moral-career]] phase changes and to the need for [[secondary-adjustment|secondary adjustments]] / [[underlife]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of totalizing all institutional discipline as mortification.
- Inmate-perspective emphasis can understate inmate-on-inmate mortification and staff occupational constraints.
- Empirical base is mid-century custodial practice.
