# Big-Man

## Definition as Coined

The **big-man** is a Melanesian political-economic type: an aspiring leader who acquires influence not by inherited office but by **personal achievement** — outworking others (self- or "auto-exploitation," often expanding his own household labor through polygyny), then building a following by giving generously and creating debt, and finally sponsoring large public feasts and exchanges to "build his name." *(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, 1972, ch. 3, developing his 1963 essay "Poor Man, Rich Man, Big-Man, Chief.")* The big-man is thus a **point of articulation** through which otherwise fragmented, acephalous segmentary societies achieve larger-scale cooperation without formal political offices.

## Semantic History

"Big-man" is long-standing Melanesianist ethnographic vocabulary (Hogbin, Oliver, Reay). Sahlins made it an analytic type within a continuum of primitive political-economic forms — from the minimal foraging **headman** ("autoexploitation," e.g. the !Kung leader who gains respect by giving everything away), through the **big-man** as a transitional achieved form, to full **chieftainship**, where authority is vested in a structural office and commands tribute rather than relying on personal industry. The Melanesian big-man is classically contrasted with the Polynesian chief (achieved vs ascribed authority).

## Operationalizations

Sahlins argues the big-man system leaves a measurable trace in household economics: a **bifurcate "fish-tail" distribution of labor intensity** — one set of households following a rising [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]] trend, another working at distinctly higher intensity — reflecting the polarization of households into ambitious producers/followers versus others. He documents this in Pospisil's Kapauku (Botukebo) data, where 69% of households worked at surplus intensity and surplus labor produced 35% of village output, and predicts the pattern will recur "generally in the Melanesian big-man systems."

## Applications

- **Intensification of the [[domestic-mode-of-production]]:** the big-man is the mechanism that overrides the household's anti-surplus tendency, converting [[generalized-reciprocity]] (generosity) into political dependency and mobilizing followers' production toward collective feasts and exchange.
- **Evolutionary/comparative political anthropology:** the autoexploitation → big-man → chieftainship series links economic leverage to political form; ethnographic cases include Busama (Hogbin), Lakalai (Chowning & Goodenough), and the Kuma (Reay), whose big-men seek "incremental reputation" — to be *known* to be wealthy, not merely to be wealthy.
- Contrast cases: Northwest Coast chiefs (authority from lineage headship) and the [[nuer|Nuer]] segmentary lineage system (inter-group relations fixed by descent, with no personal leadership differentiation — hence, Sahlins argues, "a lower coefficient of intensity").

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* The big-man/chief contrast has been criticized as too clean — many Melanesian and Polynesian polities blend achieved and ascribed authority, and later work (e.g. on the "great man" societies) multiplied the typology. The "fish-tail" prediction rests on a single reworked dataset. As a comparative type it is stated in attributed voice; its ethnographic base is mid-century Melanesian fieldwork.
