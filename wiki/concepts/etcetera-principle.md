## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel identified a cluster of practices — "et cetera," "unless," "let it pass," and "factum valet" (an action otherwise prohibited by a rule is counted correct once done) — as invariant, essential features of how members apply any set of instructions or agreement to actual, particular circumstances. In his analysis of a UCLA psychiatric-clinic coding study, Garfinkel found that coders needed unstated background knowledge of the clinic's organization simply to apply written coding instructions to real case folders; the instructions alone never determined their own application. He coined a name for this necessary supplementary work: "For convenience let me call these 'ad hoc' considerations, and call their practice 'ad hocing.'"

Garfinkel extended the same logic to agreements and contracts in Chapter 2, arguing that any agreement attains its practical status only via an unspoken "et cetera clause": specific stipulations are retrospectively reread under present circumstances to determine what the agreement "really" consisted of "all along" — "Specific stipulations are formulated under the rule of an agreement by being brought under the jurisdiction of the et cetera clause." Garfinkel attributed the sustained study of the et cetera clause to the working group active in the UCLA/Colorado Conferences on Ethnomethodology (begun February 1962), naming Egon Bittner, Craig MacAndrew, Edward Rose, and Harvey Sacks alongside himself as contributors to this line of analysis.

## Semantic History

The et cetera principle first appears in Garfinkel's Chapter 1 analysis of the clinic-coding study (originally published 1962) and is elaborated in Chapter 2's discussion of agreements and the "et cetera clause." Garfinkel's crucial theoretical move was to reject the "prevailing view" — common in survey and content-analysis methodology of the period — that ad hocing was a remediable flaw or nuisance to be minimized through better-written rules or instructions. Instead, he argued that ad hoc considerations are "required if the researcher is to grasp the relevance of the instructions to the particular and actual occasions they are intended to analyze": no set of instructions, however detailed, can specify its own application to every particular case without an indefinite regress of further instructions. The concept later became one of ethnomethodology's standard exhibits for the argument that rule-following in general — not only in coding but in law, bureaucratic procedure, and everyday agreement — depends on situated, unformalizable judgment.

## Operationalizations

Garfinkel did not operationalize the etcetera principle as a measurable variable. It was identified qualitatively, by tracing the actual reasoning coders or parties to an agreement employed when confronted with cases the explicit rule did not clearly cover, and by noting the gap between the formal instructions and what was actually done to make them applicable. A methodological consequence Garfinkel drew from this: coded results produced through ad hocing might be better described as "a persuasive version of the socially organized character" of a setting, and coding instructions themselves as furnishing "a 'social science' way of talking so as to persuade consensus" among members with differing interests, rather than as producing an independently existing, rule-derived description.

## Applications

The principle is exhibited in the UCLA outpatient psychiatric clinic coding study (Chapters 1, 6, and 7, the last two co-authored with Egon Bittner, [[bittner-egon]]), where returns were obtained on only a fraction of scheduled record items and coders required background knowledge of clinic operating procedure to interpret folder contents at all — a finding Garfinkel and Bittner reframed as "'good' organizational reasons for 'bad' records" rather than as coder or clinician failure. The et cetera clause is also central to Garfinkel's analysis of everyday agreements in Chapter 2, where the "hidden wire recorder" demonstration showed subjects treating an unstated privacy understanding as though it had been an explicit part of an agreement all along.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because the etcetera principle denies that any rule can be self-applying, it has been read as a challenge to survey and content-analysis methodology generally, implying that inter-coder reliability figures obscure rather than eliminate the ad hoc judgment coders actually perform. Critics working within quantitative traditions have viewed this as an overstatement that collapses the distinction between well- and poorly-specified coding schemes; Garfinkel's own response was that the distinction is one of degree, not kind, since ad hocing is constitutive of rule-application as such rather than a symptom of badly written rules. The concept is closely bound to [[documentary-method-of-interpretation]] and to the general ethnomethodological claim, discussed under [[indexicality]], that expressions and instructions never fully specify their own sense apart from the occasion of their use.
