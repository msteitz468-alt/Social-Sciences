# Language Regimentation

**Language regimentation** names the processes by which [[language-ideology|language ideologies]] and institutions **organize, constrain, and authorize** linguistic practice — producing “regimes of language” (standards, purist codes, courtroom discourse, mission literacy, national languages).

## Definition as Coined

Central framing device of [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|Kroskrity (ed.), *Regimes of Language* (2000)]]: languages are not only used but **regimented** by ideological and political projects. Related to Silversteinian accounts of how indexical orders and standards regiment denotation and interaction.

## Semantic History

“Regime” links linguistic anthropology to broader social theory of power and institutional order without reducing language to epiphenomenon. Kroskrity’s introduction maps multiple levels at which regimentation operates (from interactional to state).

## Operationalizations

Track sites of enforcement (schools, courts, missions, academies, media), sanctions and rewards, and the ideologies that naturalize them.

## Applications

All case chapters in *Regimes of Language*; densify per case on society/site pages as warranted.

## Critiques and Limitations

Metaphor of “regime” can over-systematize messy multilingual practice; volume authors generally treat regimentation as partial and contested.
