# Old Wood Problem

A **formation-process bias in radiocarbon dating**: dates on wood and wood charcoal often pre-date the cultural event of interest (building, hearth use) because tree death, dead-wood collection, structural reuse, and scavenging for fuel create age lags. Named and pressed by [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]] (1982; *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987 Ch. 11) in the Hohokam chronology debate.

## Definition as Coined

Schiffer argued that heavy reliance on wood dates has probably skewed many radiocarbon chronologies toward **excessive antiquity**. Variability is **systematic, not random**: bias is toward older ages. Pathways include: trees dead long before use; firewood collected as dead wood; structural members reused then burned as fuel (attributed, Schiffer 1987:309–311).

## Semantic History

Grew from Dean’s (1978) point that a radiocarbon date refers to a noncultural event (ring growth, organism death) and that relating dates to cultural events requires identifying formation processes of specimens and deposits — “disjunctions” (attributed via Schiffer). Became standard caution in New World and arid-lands chronology; annual plants and short-lived samples preferred where possible.

## Operationalizations

Schiffer’s standing methodological conclusions (attributed, 1987):

1. Avoid treating central tendencies of wood (especially firewood) date series as event ages.
2. Small numbers of firewood dates alone cannot give high-resolution chronology.
3. Weight annual plants (and appropriately treated bone collagen) more heavily — free of old-wood lag (with fractionation corrections as needed).

Sonoran Desert illustrations he cites: surface ironwood ~200–1500 B.P.; long-surviving surface mesquite; driftwood adding old carbon at riverine sites like Snaketown; problem most severe under low population (Archaic, Pioneer Hohokam) when dead-wood surplus is large (attributed).

## Applications

Hohokam Pioneer–Colonial chronological reanalysis (Schiffer 1987 Ch. 11): strong cases using maize and de facto-house samples vs weak trash-mound charcoal; critique of accept/reject dating and of pure statistical pooling. General CRM and research chronology building.

## Critiques and Limitations

Does not invalidate wood dates; requires pathway control. Construction wood may be less affected than opportunistic firewood in some settings (Schiffer’s own relative ranking). Other disjunctions (intrusive charcoal, bioturbation, laboratory issues) remain separate.
