# Old Wood Problem

A **formation-process bias in radiocarbon dating**: dates on wood and wood charcoal often pre-date the cultural event of interest (building, hearth use) because tree death, dead-wood collection, structural reuse, and scavenging for fuel create age lags. Named and pressed by [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]] (1982; *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987 Ch. 11; reprinted/condensed as *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* Ch. 16 from the 1987 treatment) in the Hohokam chronology debate.

## Definition as Coined

Schiffer argued that heavy reliance on wood dates has probably skewed many radiocarbon chronologies toward **excessive antiquity**. Variability is **systematic, not random**: bias is toward older ages. Pathways include: trees dead long before use; firewood collected as dead wood; structural members reused then burned as fuel (attributed, Schiffer 1982; 1987:309–311; 1995 Ch. 16).

## Semantic History

Grew from Dean’s (1978) point that a radiocarbon date refers to a noncultural event (ring growth, organism death) and that relating dates to cultural events requires identifying formation processes of specimens and deposits — “disjunctions” (attributed via Schiffer 1995 Ch. 16). Became standard caution in New World and arid-lands chronology; annual plants and short-lived samples preferred where possible. In the Binford *Working at Archaeology* review, Schiffer cites Hohokam “strong cases” — annual plants free of old wood on house floors with ceramic de facto refuse — as formation processes handled “up front” (attributed, Schiffer 1985b / 1995 Ch. 19).

## Operationalizations

Schiffer’s standing methodological conclusions (attributed, 1987 / 1995 Ch. 16):

1. Avoid treating central tendencies of wood (especially firewood) date series as event ages.
2. Small numbers of firewood dates alone cannot give high-resolution chronology.
3. Weight annual plants (and appropriately treated bone collagen) more heavily — free of old-wood lag (with fractionation corrections as needed).
4. Apply **tree-longevity corrections** when mixed rings are dated (century+ possible for Hohokam native trees; Schiffer 1986) (attributed).

Sonoran Desert illustrations he cites: surface ironwood ~200–1500 B.P.; long-surviving surface mesquite; driftwood adding old carbon at riverine sites like Snaketown; problem most severe under low population (Archaic, Pioneer Hohokam) when dead-wood surplus is large (attributed, 1995 Ch. 16).

## Applications

Hohokam Pioneer–Colonial chronological reanalysis (Schiffer 1982/1987 Ch. 11; 1995 Ch. 16): strong cases using maize and de facto-house samples vs weak trash-mound charcoal; phase-overlap claims rejected when associations come from artificial mound construction, rodent disturbance, or arbitrary levels rather than behavioral contemporaneity; caches, cremations, and pure-phase floor vessels cited as positive evidence of phase discreteness (attributed). General CRM and research chronology building; argument that many New World formative chronologies may share excessive-antiquity bias and that New Archaeologists downplayed chronological difficulty (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 16).

## Critiques and Limitations

Does not invalidate wood dates; requires pathway control. Construction wood may be less affected than opportunistic firewood in some settings (Schiffer’s own relative ranking). Other disjunctions (intrusive charcoal, bioturbation, laboratory issues) remain separate. Haury and others disputed Schiffer’s shortened Hohokam chronology on experiential and other grounds (attributed via Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1).
