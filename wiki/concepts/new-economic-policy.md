# New Economic Policy (NEP)

## Definition as Coined

The Soviet policy regime adopted from 1921, replacing War Communism: legalized market exchange and small private enterprise (the "Nepmen") while the state retained political power, large industry, and banking. This page's claims derive from Lenin's last writings of 1922–23, where NEP is not merely described but theoretically re-founded ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]]).

## Origin, 1921

Lenin substantiated the policy at its introduction in *The Tax in Kind (The Significance of the New Policy and Its Conditions)* (written late March–April 1921, published May 1921; [[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]]) and in reports to Moscow Party cells and trade-union bodies the same spring. The immediate cause, as Lenin stated it, was not any change in Bolshevik preference but "the extraordinarily acute crisis of peasant farming" by spring 1921 — wartime ruin, crop failure, fodder shortage, and cattle loss compounding three and a half years of War Communism. The tax in kind replaced the surplus-appropriation system (*prodrazvyorstka*): a fixed grain levy announced in advance each spring (roughly half the prior appropriation rate — 240 million poods versus 423 million previously), after which a free market in the remainder, at least locally, was licensed. Lenin called the tax "one of the forms of transition" from War Communism to "regular socialist exchange of products," itself one of the forms of transition from socialism (in a predominantly small-peasant country) toward communism, and stated plainly that its effect would be "the revival of the petty bourgeoisie and of capitalism on the basis of some freedom of trade (if only local)" — a consequence he called certain and not to be denied.

Lenin justified the policy by restating a "five economic structures" schema he had first used in 1918 against the Left Communists (*"Left-Wing" Childishness and the Petty-Bourgeois Mentality*): (1) patriarchal, largely natural peasant economy; (2) small commodity production; (3) private capitalism; (4) [[state-capitalism]]; (5) socialism — arguing all five coexisted in Russia's 1921 economy and that channelling the peasantry's inevitable turn toward trade into *state* capitalism (rather than trying to suppress exchange altogether, which he called "foolish and suicidal") was "the only sensible and the last possible policy." He treated [[finance-capital|foreign]] concessions (leasing state-owned oilfields and factories to foreign capitalists for a share of output — he cites the Baku/Grozny fields and an unconcluded Swedish ball-bearing proposal) as themselves a form of state capitalism, calling the tax in kind and concessions together "the two crutches we must now use to move on." He distinguished the tax in kind from a full retreat by insisting the proletariat retained political power and ownership of large-scale industry, banking, and transport throughout.

### Kronstadt and NEP chronology (Avrich 1970, attributed)

[[avrich-paul|Avrich]] argues NEP outlines were formulated **before** the March 1921 Kronstadt rising (Politburo tax-in-kind outline 8 February; detailed draft to CC 24 February) and that the revolt **hastened** rather than invented the turn; Lenin treated Kronstadt as lighting up the political crisis of [[war-communism]]. NEP, Avrich holds, met neither the rebels' free-soviets program nor industrial democracy demands — economic concession to consolidate political monopoly. See [[kronstadt-debate]], [[avrich-kronstadt-1921]].

## Semantic History

- **1921–22 (adoption).** Lenin later characterized the sequence with Napoleon's maxim "On s'engage et puis... on voit" — power was seized first, and adaptations like the Brest peace and NEP were worked out afterward ("Our Revolution," 1923).
- **1923 (re-founding).** In "On Co-operation" (January 1923) Lenin argued that under NEP, with political power and the means of production in proletarian hands, organizing the population into co-operative societies had become "all that is necessary and sufficient" to build a complete socialist society — explicitly reversing his earlier dismissal of Owenite co-operative socialism and announcing "a radical modification in our whole outlook on socialism." The emphasis of Soviet work, he wrote, was shifting to "peaceful, organisational, 'cultural' work" among the peasantry ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]], attributed).
- **After Lenin.** NEP's status became a central stake in the succession struggles of the 1920s and was ended under Stalin's collectivization and first Five-Year Plan; that later history is not yet documented from ingested sources (as of 2026-07-16).

## Operationalizations

As an emic policy category, NEP is identified by its institutional markers: the tax in kind replacing requisition, legal private trade, concessions, and the "collaboration of two classes" — workers and peasants — that Lenin in 1923 called the basis of the Soviet order, warning that a split between them, potentially engineered by the Nepmen, would be "fatal for the Soviet Republic" ("How We Should Reorganise the Workers' and Peasants' Inspection," 1923).

## Applications

Lenin's late articles tie NEP to a "cultural revolution": because Russia's political and social revolution preceded the cultural prerequisites classical Marxist expectation placed first, mass literacy and administrative competence had to be created *after* the seizure of power — a reversal he defended against Sukhanov's charge that Russia lacked the prerequisites for socialism ("Our Revolution"; see [[historical-materialism]]). The survival calculus of "Better Fewer, But Better" (March 1923) — economize, electrify, hold the worker-peasant alliance until revolution matures elsewhere — is NEP-era strategy stated at full length ([[lenin-last-writings-1923]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Within the ingested material the main tension is internal to Lenin's own last writings: the claim that co-operatives suffice for socialism sits alongside his diagnosis that the state apparatus remained a tsarist "survival of the past" and that cultural backwardness was immense — sufficiency, on his own account, was conditional on a cultural transformation not yet achieved. Positions in the post-Lenin dispute over NEP's meaning (Bukharin's prolongation vs. left critiques) are not yet documented from ingested sources.

## Service 2000 narrative and thesis

Robert Service, *[[service-lenin-2000|Lenin: A Biography]]* (2000), recounts the turn as follows (all Service-attributed):

- **Politburo 8 February 1921.** Replace forcible grain requisitioning with a **tax-in-kind**; after the fiscal contribution, trade produce in local markets. Draft basis: Lenin’s “Preliminary Rough Draft of Theses on the Peasants.”
- **Precedents Service notes.** NEP’s agricultural essentials had been advocated by Mensheviks and SRs since 1918 and by [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]] in February 1920 (Trotsky, Service reports, later reminded Lenin the change could have come a year earlier). Service ranks the political achievement as equal to Brest-Litovsk though understated by biographers because less raucous than Brest or the trade-union discussion.
- **Tenth Congress concurrent with Kronstadt.** Open mutiny **2 March 1921** (“all power to soviets and not to parties”); Congress from 8 March sanctioned NEP, foreign concessions, Lenin’s trade-union line, and condemnation of the Workers’ Opposition; ice assault retook the fortress; faction-ban resolution pushed through as economic retreat requiring internal unity (see [[kronstadt-debate]]).
- ***On the Food Tax*.** Service reports Lenin’s booklet framed NEP as restoration of 1918 recognition of space for capitalism interrupted by Civil War — “War Communism” as suspendable emergency; greater peasant trade freedom; **no** political/ideological concessions; retained terror for fraud, corruption, bureaucratic abuse, and profiteering (“Freedom of trade is capitalism, capitalism is speculation”).
- **Service thesis.** NEP = **bare minimum economic reform** + **deep repression** (one-party, one-ideology state kept and political terror intensified). Service: without Lenin, quite possibly **no NEP**; without NEP, the Soviet state overwhelmed by rebellions. Party ideology, stiffened by Civil War, treated state economic near-monopoly as an article of faith; Lenin seemed to abandon ownership/regulation while keeping political monopoly. Cross-link: [[kronstadt-debate]], [[red-terror]], [[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]].

## Carr 1979 on NEP trajectory and end

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 4–6, 11–16) extends the wiki's Lenin-primary NEP dossier through the 1920s (attributed):

- **Package (10th congress, March 1921):** Tax in kind; peasant market sales; artisan industry; cooperatives; path to stable currency; "commanding heights" and foreign-trade monopoly retained. Kronstadt concurrent — Carr: may have increased readiness for NEP **and** for faction ban.
- **Dual interpretations:** If War Communism was aberration, NEP was return to the pre-June 1918 path; if War Communism was over-rash dash into socialism, NEP was Lenin's "defeat" and "retreat—for a new attack," "seriously and for a long time."
- **Recovery costs:** 1921 famine; 1922–23 harvest recovery; kulak re-emergence (lease/hire labour under 1922 agricultural code); industry and planned economy lagged.
- **[[scissors-crisis]]:** NEP recovery produced price crises requiring **price controls that contravened NEP market principles** — market vs managed economy struggle "throughout the nineteen-twenties."
- **End:** Planning and forced industrialization made NEP market-feeding of cities impracticable; private sector share of national income from >50% (1926–27) to "insignificant" by end of First [[five-year-plan]]; mass [[collectivization]] as agrarian counterpart. Carr: the break "logically followed" from planning commitments even while leaders denied NEP was dead.

Bukharin's rearguard defence of the peasant market and the Right's defeat are in Carr Chs. 13–17. Cross-link [[left-opposition]], [[socialism-in-one-country]].
