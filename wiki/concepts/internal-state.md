# Internal State

## Definition as Coined

Burawoy (*[[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-1979|Manufacturing Consent]]*, 1979): "the set of institutions that organize, transform, or repress struggles over relations in production and relations of production at the level of the enterprise." Under competitive capitalism its form was "the despotic overseer"; under monopoly capitalism with trade unionism it takes hegemonic form in grievance procedures and collective bargaining, which "protects the managerial prerogative... by imposing constraints on managerial discretion and by endowing workers with rights as well as obligations."

## Semantic History

Built from Selznick (collective bargaining "creates a system of government"; prerogative → constitutive contract), Habermas ("repoliticization of the relations of production"), and Weinstein's Progressive-Era corporatism. Key derived notion: the **industrial citizen** — "everyday life under the internal state inserts the laborer into the political process as an industrial citizen with a set of contractually defined rights and obligations," the union acting as umpire. Collective bargaining becomes "another game — a game about rules and outcomes of other games, such as [[making-out]]": negotiating marginal changes makes capitalist relations of ownership "the object of consent." The internal state has **relative autonomy** — it binds management against its own consent-destroying arbitrariness, holds only while surplus value is secured, and expresses institutionalized managerial power in the context of an organized working class. Unlike the global state, it "explicitly recognizes classes and thereby becomes, at least potentially, more vulnerable to class struggle" (all attributed). Distinguish from political-science uses of "internal state."

## Operationalizations

Contract provisions (union shop, rationalized discipline with union notification, management-notice clauses), grievance-flow records, ratification votes (Allied 1974: 408–307 for a pension-weighted contract), meeting attendance (6–9% at Allied locals).

## Applications

[[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-study]]; comparative work regimes (Burawoy's despotic/hegemonic typology, extended in *The Politics of Production*, 1985 — not yet ingested); industrial-relations analyses of grievance systems.

## Critiques and Limitations

One-plant evidence; the union-as-umpire reading competes with more agency-affirming labor-history accounts (Burawoy himself rejects both "corrupt leadership" and concession-theory alternatives — positions on [[labor-process-debate]]); race gap between membership and executive at Allied flags the construct's silence on internal stratification.
