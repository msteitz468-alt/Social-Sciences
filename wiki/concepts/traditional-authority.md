## Definition as Coined

Traditional rule is one of Weber's three pure types of legitimate domination (Ch.3 §6): rule "will be called traditional if its legitimacy is based on, and believed in, by virtue of the sanctity of long-established orders and ruling powers that have existed 'time out of mind'" (Ch.3, line 613–627). Under this type the ruler is a *personal* ruler (not a "superior" in the legal-rational sense), the staff are personal "retainers" rather than officials, the ruled are "traditional associates" (Genossen) or "subjects," and obedience runs to the person appointed by tradition, not to enacted statutes.

Weber identifies a distinctive "dual character" of the traditional ruler's freedom of action: the ruler's commands are legitimate partly by virtue of traditional content and limits (violating them is dangerous to the ruler), and partly by virtue of a traditionally-conferred freedom of decision that is "in principle unlimited" — a sphere of personal favor and clemency, arbitrary and a potential source of income via gifts. Resistance to abuse of this sphere targets the person of the ruler, not the system as such — what Weber terms a "traditionalist revolution" (Ch.3, line 628–659).

The traditional administrative staff is recruited either "patrimonially" (kinsmen, slaves, domestic officials, clients, coloni, freed slaves) or "nonpatrimonially" (favourites, vassals bound by fealty, or officials who voluntarily entered personal loyalty) (Ch.3, line 668–683). Weber specifies the pure traditional staff by five absences relative to bureaucracy: it lacks (a) definite competence per objective regulations; (b) a fixed rational hierarchy; (c) regular contractual appointment with rule-governed promotion; (d) specialist training as a norm; (e) fixed salary or money payment, often (Ch.3, line 709–717).

## Semantic History

See [[domination-herrschaft]] for the genus concept and Tribe's rejection of "authority"/"domination" as renderings of Herrschaft in favor of "rule/rulership." Traditional rule's variant forms are further subdivided by Weber: gerontocracy (rule by elders held to know sanctified tradition best) and primary patriarchalism (rule within a domestic organisation by a person fixed by strict rules of heredity, exercised for the members' interest — the two primary types lacking a personal administrative staff, Ch.3 §7a, line 802–829); patrimonialism and its extreme form "sultanism" (where Genossen become "subjects" and the ruler acquires a personal, saleable, inheritable prerogative to rule — "despotic and unrestricted by tradition," line 835–860); Ständische Herrschaft or "hierarchical/status-group rule" (a form of patrimonial rule in which economic Chancen linked to ruling powers are appropriated to the administrative staff, line 861–931); and feudalism, which Weber treats as "quite distinct from patrimonialism" — enfeoffed feudalism (a fief: appropriated ruling powers granted personally, for the lifetime of ruler and vassal, by free contract with a free man of knightly rank, establishing a "brotherly relationship" of unequal but mutual loyalty) versus benefice feudalism (rents calculated by yield, personal and service-conditioned, but not grounded in a free fealty-contract — e.g. Turkish Sipahi benefices, Indian zamindars/jagirdars) (Ch.3, line 1800–2150).

## Operationalizations

Weber ties traditional rule's economic consequences to how the ruling organisation is financed: oikos/liturgical (in-kind) financing is highly traditional and blocks capitalism outright; financing through social-rank privilege limits the market less severely; monopolistic financing (profit, fees, tax) irrationally limits the market and diverts capitalist development into "politically oriented capitalism" (tax farming, sale/lease of office, capitalistic army provisioning) (Ch.3, line 1103–1141). Generally, Weber argues, patrimonial administration is hostile to *rational* capitalism specifically: traditionalism resists calculable long-term formal regulation, formally trained specialist staff are absent, corruption and arbitrary fees have scope, and there is an inherent tendency to substantively regulate the economy for subjects' welfare, undermining formal-legal rationality — though mercantile, tax-farming, war-financing, and plantation/colonial capitalism can flourish under normal patrimonial rule (Ch.3, line 1189–1242).

## Applications

Weber traces modern occidental capitalism's origin to relatively rationally-administered occidental urban organisations (16th–18th c. Dutch/English ständisch polities with bourgeois predominance), contrasting this with Continental patrimonial/feudalized fiscal imitation, which did not produce the same result (Ch.3, line 1251–1262) — presented as Weber's own comparative-historical illustration, not a settled finding. Weber also treats the long struggle between lords and enfeoffed vassals over control of the means of administration as resolving, in the Occident and then (via conquest) in Japan, India, and China, in the "victory of bureaucratic administration" (Ch.3, line 1935–2011).

## Critiques and Limitations

As with the other two types, Weber insists traditional rule never appears in historically pure form; real cases blend traditional, charismatic, and legal-rational elements (see [[domination-herrschaft]]). The internal variety Weber packs into "traditional rule" (gerontocracy, patriarchalism, patrimonialism, sultanism, Ständische Herrschaft, and two forms of feudalism) has itself been a source of definitional contestation about how unified the type really is.
