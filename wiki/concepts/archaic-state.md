## Definition as Coined

"Archaic state" as a general comparative category originates with Joyce Marcus and Gary Feinman (1998), defining archaic states cross-culturally (not Polynesia-specifically) as societies with, at minimum: two class-endogamous strata (a professional ruling class and a commoner class); a government that is both highly centralized and internally specialized; and greater capacity than preceding rank societies to wage war, exact tribute, control information, draft soldiers, and regulate labor. Where textual evidence survives, such polities are ruled by kings rather than chiefs, maintain standardized temples implying a state religion staffed by full-time priests, and can retain conquered territory (attributed, Marcus and Feinman 1998:4–5, as quoted by Kirch 2017).

Patrick Vinton Kirch (Kirch 2010, *How Chiefs Became Kings*; elaborated in Kirch 2017) operationalized six criteria specifically for adjudicating "archaic state" status in Hawai'i: (1) well-developed class endogamy; (2) kings tracing descent directly to gods and regarded as instantiations of deities on earth; (3) a political economy substantially centrally controlled by the king's bureaucracy; (4) king's status and power legitimated by state cults with a formalized temple system overseen by full-time priests; (5) power maintained by a monopoly of force via a full-time warrior cadre or standing army; (6) king and court occupying special residential quarters (palaces) supplied by full-time specialist/craft cadres.

**This is Kirch's own thesis, not a wiki-voice or field-wide consensus classification.** Kirch argues that, among Polynesian societies, only Tonga and Hawai'i crossed the threshold from complex chiefdom to archaic state in late prehistory — a judgment he presents as "cogent arguments... advanced [by archaeologists working in both Tonga and Hawai'i]," i.e., a position within Pacific archaeology rather than a field-wide settled fact (attributed, Kirch 2017, ch. 8).

## Semantic History

Kirch explicitly distinguishes his (and Marcus and Feinman's) comparative, typological "archaic state" from the general neoevolutionary stadial "state" stage of Elman Service's and Morton Fried's band→tribe→chiefdom→state ladder (see [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]). Kirch does not adopt a rigid unilineal sequence; "archaic state" here names a specific cross-cultural comparative category (tied to the Marcus and Feinman archaic-states research program) applied to particular Polynesian cases, not a universal evolutionary destination that all chiefdoms tend toward.

## Operationalizations

Applied by Kirch to:
- **Tonga** — the unified "Tongan maritime empire" under the Tu'i Tonga sacred paramountcy and the hau/Tu'i Ha'a Takalaua secular diarchy, sustained by "wealth finance" (prestige-goods flow) and hegemony over Ha'apai, Vava'u, Niuatoputapu, and 'Uvea.
- **Hawai'i** — a precontact polity Kirch estimates at possibly ≥500,000 people at the time of Cook's 1778–79 arrival, with a chiefly class (ali'i) ranked in 7–8 grades, sibling/half-sibling marriage at the highest ranks to concentrate bloodline, and a formalized luakini-heiau temple system overseen by full-time priests.

Causal accounts of Hawaiian state formation are debated even among proponents: Hommon (2013) advances a "hard times" hypothesis (dryland field-system intensification, population growth, soil-fertility decline, and land-limit pressure driving "escalated coercive development"), while Kirch (2010) distinguishes "ultimate" causes (exponential post-1200 CE population growth, resource/labor pressure, agro-ecosystem intensification) from "proximate" or agentive causes (status rivalry, marriage-alliance strategy, conquest warfare). These are complementary rather than opposed positions within Kirch's own account.

## Applications

Feeds directly into [[societies/tonga]] and [[hawaiians]] as the analytical framework for their late-prehistoric political transformation, and into [[state-formation]] as a comparative case of pristine, non-Old-World state emergence.

## Critiques and Limitations

The "archaic state" designation for Tonga and Hawai'i is a live analytical/classificatory judgment rather than a settled taxonomy — the threshold between "complex chiefdom" and "archaic state" is graded, not sharp, and rests on Kirch's own selection and weighting of the six Hawaiian criteria. Applying a comparative category built from Old World (Mesoamerican, Near Eastern) archaic-state cases to Oceanic polities also risks importing assumptions (e.g., about textual evidence, standardized state religion) that fit unevenly onto societies known primarily through archaeology and oral tradition rather than administrative texts.
