# Negative Heritage

## Definition as Coined

**Negative heritage** is [[meskell-lynn|Lynn Meskell's]] term for heritage that memorializes
atrocity, repression, or a painful past — the kind of legacy "typically jettisoned from the crafting
of new nations" — rather than a celebratory or restorative one. Meskell reports (attributed,
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|*The Nature of Heritage*]] 2012) that she developed the idea after
**9/11**, in debates comparing the Taliban's destruction of the **Bamiyan Buddhas** with the fall of
the **World Trade Center**, and she frames the very "processing of history into heritage" as
inherently involving "dealing with a negative legacy."

Where [[dissonant-heritage|dissonant heritage]] (Tunbridge & Ashworth; Smith) names the contested
quality of heritage in general, negative heritage picks out the specific class of heritage anchored in
harm — and asks what a society does with material remnants of its own repression.

## Semantic History

- Coined in the immediate post-9/11 moment around destruction and commemoration; carried into
  Meskell's South African work as an analytic for the material residue of apartheid and colonial
  dispossession.
- In Meskell 2012 it becomes tightly coupled to **[[past-mastering]]**: negative heritage is the
  material and testimonial substance that a post-conflict nation must "master."

## Operationalizations

Meskell's South African cases (attributed, 2012):

- The **dog cemetery at Skukuza** ("Little Heroes Acre," beside the Stevenson-Hamilton Museum in
  [[kruger-national-park|Kruger]]) — an uncomfortable colonial remnant the new black management chose
  **not** to erase, read by Meskell as a negotiated-settlement ethos.
- The **forced removals** from Kruger — evictions, cattle-killing, and denial of compensation —
  "recounted in almost every conversation outside the fence," constituting negative heritage as lived
  testimony rather than monument.

## Applications

Bridges [[heritage]], [[past-mastering]], [[social-memory]], and [[dissonant-heritage]]; useful for
atrocity sites, contested monuments, and the heritage of colonialism, slavery, and state violence.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's boundary with dissonant heritage can blur (Meskell's own point is that heritage is
rarely purely positive); its analytic force depends on distinguishing heritage that *commemorates*
harm from heritage that merely *provokes* discomfort. Recorded here as Meskell's analytical
construct, attributed.
