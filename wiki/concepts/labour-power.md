# Labour Power

*(Attributed. Origin: [[marx-karl|Marx]], *Capital* (1867). Principal ingested treatment: [[willis-paul|Willis]], [[willis-learning-to-labour-1977|*Learning to Labour*]] (1977).)* **Labour power** is the worker's capacity to labour, which under capitalism is sold as a commodity in exchange for a wage. In Marx's analysis its peculiarity is that it is "a commodity like no other": bought at its value (wages, the cost of its reproduction) but capable of producing value far in excess of that price, it is the source of surplus value and hence of exploitation — "an infinite capacity has been bought for a finite sum."

## Definition as Coined

For Marx, the wage bargain appears as a free and equal exchange in the "marketplace of exchange" ("Freedom, Equality, Property and Bentham"), but this apparent equivalence masks the extraction of surplus in production. Labour power is *variable*: what the worker actually delivers is not fixed by the contract but depends on the labour process and the balance of control within it.

## Semantic History

Marx distinguished **labour power** (the capacity to work, the commodity actually sold) from **labour** (its exercise) — a distinction central to his theory of surplus value. Twentieth-century labour-process theory (Braverman) re-centred the concept on the *control* of labour power at the point of production and its degradation through de-skilling. Willis's contribution was to give the category a **cultural and subjective** dimension it lacked: not only the capacity sold, but the historically and culturally formed *disposition* to give or withhold it. The concept thus spans a rigorous economic term and a looser cultural one — a stretch some critics resist.

## Willis's Cultural-Subjective Development

Willis takes Marx's category and gives it an ethnographic, subjective content: labour power is not only an economic magnitude but "the specific cultural and subjective self-preparation of labour power" — how workers *learn to give* their capacity, in a detached, manual-not-mental, masculinised way. Individual identity, he argues, is "importantly formed by the culturally learned sense, and subjective inhabitation, of labour power."

Several claims follow, developed through the [[counter-school-culture|counter-school culture]] and its [[penetration-willis|penetrations and limitations]]:

- **Variability grasped and defended.** The lads' culture "as if by instinct" limits the giving of labour power — the school analogue of shop-floor "limitation of output" — to protect the space for "sensuous physical and mental involvement."
- **The wage-packet fetishism.** The weekly wage (manual) versus monthly salary (professional) form makes it "much easier to overlook the real continuous, sensuous and variable quality of labour power" — Willis calls this "a fetishism of the wage packet" (cf. [[commodity-fetishism]]).
- **Abstract labour.** The lads' near-indifference to the particular job is read as an intuition that "what is common to all wage-labour work is more important than what divides it," since "capitalism is production for profit rather than use."
- **Masculine cross-valorisation.** The subjective preparation of manual labour power is bound up with masculinity, so that giving it becomes "a heroic exercise of manly confrontation" rather than submission to brutal conditions — the hinge of Willis's [[penetration-willis|cross-valorisation]] argument and of [[cultural-reproduction|reproduction]] through the cultural level.

## Applications

The concept underwrites the Marxist theory of exploitation and surplus value; the labour-process tradition's analysis of workplace control and de-skilling; and, in Willis, the account of how a [[counter-school-culture]] prepares working-class boys to give manual labour power "freely" — the subjective hinge of [[cultural-reproduction|social reproduction]]. It also grounds the analysis of the wage form ([[commodity-fetishism|fetishism]] of the wage packet) and of [[alienation]] in wage labour.

## Operationalizations

Labour power is not directly measured; in the Marxist tradition it is theorised through the value form, and in Willis reconstructed interpretively from cultural practice. Labour-process scholarship (Braverman's de-skilling thesis) operationalises its degradation through changes in the skill content and control of jobs.

## Critiques and Limitations

Willis's subjective reading has been criticised for stretching a rigorous economic category into a looser cultural one, and for reading class-conscious "penetration" into what may be ordinary occupational preference. The concept is central to Marxist sociology and remains contested where non-Marxist stratification theory prefers occupation, status, or human-capital framings. *Emic/etic note:* wholly etic and theory-derived.
