# Place (Lived Place)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (1994), [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] treats **place** as humanized, experienced location — contrasting abstract **space** of spatial science. Places gather meaning through body, path, narrative, and landmark relations.

## Semantic History

Philosophical/geographic lineage (phenomenology; humanistic geography) brought into British prehistoric archaeology as alternative to distributional and site-catchment location. Distinct from Giddens [[locale]] (interaction setting) and Castells [[space-of-places]] (network society).

## Operationalizations

Monument/locale relations to outcrops, coasts, ridges; chamber orientations; approach views; intervisibility; path sequences between places.

## Applications

Mesolithic flint-scatter locales and Neolithic monument siting in SW Wales, Black Mountains, Cranborne Chase ([[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether modern place-experience licenses prehistoric meaning claims; under-determination by sparse Mesolithic evidence.

## Moore 2005 — space vs. place, via Eugene Walter (attributed)

[[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore (2005)]] frames the same space-vs-place distinction independently of Tilley, drawing on Eugene Walter (1988): "The difference between space and place is human experience. Space is indifferent to humanity, but place requires the inscription of human acknowledgment." Moore traces the distinction to ancient Greek usage, per Walter: *chora/choros* (pre-470 BCE) denoted place with its emotional, subjective, qualitative dimension, while *topos* emerged afterward as an objective, metric, "stripped" descriptor of place-as-location (e.g., in Aristotle) — a bifurcation Walter argues persists in modern Western thought, splitting "feeling" from "thinking" ways of engaging place ("Preoccupations with the logic of space tend to suppress the feeling of place," Walter 1988:2). Moore treats place-marking acts — myths, oral histories, songs, toponyms, icons, grave wrappings, the destruction or preservation of ruins, desecration and consecration — as cultural acts whose material remains are legitimate archaeological subject matter.

## Tilley 2010 densification

*(Attributed — *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010]]*.)* Places remain basic units with paths; densified via geological specificity (tors, pebble ridges, chalk coombes, solution basins) and the Body–Place–Path triangle. "Natural" places without monuments are research objects equal to cultural monuments (affordances; super-natural places). Regional exemplars: [[bodmin-moor]] tors; [[west-penwith]] basins; [[east-devon-pebblebeds]] cairns.

## Smith 2006 — place and heritage (attributed)

*([[smith-laurajane]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006]]*.)* Sense of place is central to the heritage process toolkit: being **in country** (Waanyi); English country-house landscapes; Riversleigh’s competing place claims (scientific, tourist, local, Indigenous); Castleford after the physical “slate wiped clean.” Place is lived positioning and affect (Casey, Thrift, Escobar as Smith uses them), not only mapped site. Management of heritage places is also management of sense of place. Cross-link [[riversleigh]], [[waanyi]], [[castleford]], [[dissonant-heritage]].
