# Anxiety of Incompleteness

## Definition as Coined

[[appadurai-arjun|Arjun Appadurai]]'s answer, in *Fear of Small Numbers* (2006), to why *small* numbers excite genocidal rage: predatory majoritarianism "thrives in the gap between the sense of numerical majority and the fantasy of national purity and wholeness." A minority — precisely because it is small — is a "tiny obstacle between majority and totality or total purity," a reminder that the nation's peoplehood is not a whole and uncontested ethnos. "The smaller the number and the weaker the minority, the deeper the rage about its capacity to make a majority feel like a mere majority." Incompleteness here concerns "not only effective control or practical sovereignty but more importantly purity and its relationship to identity."

## Semantic History

Coined in the 2006 book (introduced in an earlier chapter not on disk; developed in ch. 3). Appadurai builds it from two lineages: [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] on purity and categorical identity, and Freud's [[narcissism-of-minor-differences]] as extended by Ignatieff (1998) to the ethnic wars of the 1990s — Appadurai's move is to relocate the narcissistic wound from individual psychodynamics to "public ideologies about group identity," where it becomes an incitement to [[predatory-identity|predatory identities]].

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized as a measured variable; used interpretively in discourse analysis of majoritarian rhetoric and nationalist mobilization.

## Applications

Appadurai's cases: Nazi Germany (Jews as "an intolerable deficit in the purity of the national whole"); Hindutva India (the "appeasement" discourse and the fear that Muslim small numbers mask global Islam's large ones); and, more generally, the globalization-era intensification of the fear that today's minority may become tomorrow's majority.

## Critiques and Limitations

An interpretive mechanism, not a measured variable; its explanatory work depends on accepting Appadurai's discourse-level reading of majoritarian rhetoric. Reception and critique not yet ingested (only the title chapter of the coining work is on disk).
