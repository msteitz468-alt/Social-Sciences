# Spiritual Refraction

**Spiritual refraction** is Evans-Pritchard's own analytical term, introduced in *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]* (1956), for the way the Nuer conception of [[kwoth|Spirit]] (*kwoth*) is differentiated into a hierarchy of particular spirits that tracks the segmentary structure of Nuer society — attributed to Evans-Pritchard's fieldwork among the Nuer of the southern Sudan, 1930–36.

## Definition as Coined

The load-bearing statement: "This refraction of Spirit by the social order was even more evident in our discussion... of the *colwic* spirits, which are God, or Spirit, in a particular and exclusive relationship to lineage groups. We find the same refraction of Spirit by the social order in the totemic spirits, which are Spirit in a more immanent and material form... Each of the totemic spirits is Spirit in a particular relationship to a lineage, which expresses its relation as an exclusive social group to God in the totemic refraction... The imprint of the social order on the conception of Spirit is very evident in the totemic beliefs and observances of the Nuer. And we see in the totemistic spirits the conception of Spirit refracted to the level of the individual..."

Evans-Pritchard stated the hierarchy explicitly, from most general/universal to most particular/material: God (universal, unrefracted) → spirits of the air (patrons of political leaders and prophets) → *colwic* (souls of persons killed by lightning) and totemic spirits (patrons of lineages and families) → nature sprites and fetishes (patrons of individuals). "God figured as common father and creator is patron of all men; figured in spirits of the air he is patron of political leaders; figured in *colwic* and totemic spirits... patron of lineages and families; and figured in nature sprites and fetishes... patron of individuals."

## Semantic History

Evans-Pritchard elaborated the hierarchy along several correlated dimensions, all in attributed voice:

- **Rank and moral quality decrease with particularity.** The refractions "decrease in the degree of universality, stability, and morality... the smaller the social space to which they refer." God is "everywhere... permanent and changeless... one... all-powerful, just, and compassionate"; spirits of the air are localized, dependent on unstable political/personal prestige, plural, and "unpredictable, and even capricious and ill-intentioned"; *colwic*/totemic spirits are restricted to lineages and families and are "numerous" and "unimportant" relative to air-spirits; nature sprites and fetishes are acknowledged only by owners and immediate kin, are very recent introductions, "potentially inexhaustible in number," and generally disapproved of — yet receive the *most* frequent and materially concrete ritual attention. Evans-Pritchard thus records an explicit inverse relationship between a refraction's rank/morality and the concreteness and frequency of its cult.
- **Genealogical metaphor.** "God is the father of the greater spirits of the air, and the lesser of them are said to be children of his sons, of his lineage. The totemic spirits are often said to be children of his daughters... The fetishes (and possibly also the nature sprites) come lowest of all in the representation of children of daughters of the air-spirit *deng*." A parallel political-status metaphor ranks spirits of the air as *diel* ("true or aristocratic spirits"), totemic spirits as *jaang* ("Dinka-like spirits"), and fetishes as *jur* ("despised foreigners").
- **Spatial and light symbolism.** Height and luminosity correspond to higher rank; earth and darkness to lower: "God is symbolized by the sky... The totemic spirits as spirits are above and as creatures are below... The fetishes are the most earthly of the spirits, some of them speaking from beneath the ground."
- **The segmentary-lineage analogy.** Evans-Pritchard mapped the logic of segmentary opposition directly onto cosmology: "Just as, for example, two lineages are distinct and opposed groups in relation to one another at one level of segmentation and are a single unit at a higher level of segmentation, so Spirit as conceived in relation to these segments must be divided at the lower level and undivided at the higher level." See [[segmentary-lineage-system]].
- **Totemism as a special case.** Against a Durkheimian reading of totemic emblems as representations of the clan itself, Evans-Pritchard argued: "It is not that the members of a group see themselves as a group through a totemic class of objects which are then further conceptualized collectively as a spirit of the class, but rather that the conception of Spirit, which is in itself quite independent of the social structure, is broken up along the lines of segmentation within the structure." (See [[totemism]] for his fuller argument against utilitarian and Durkheimian readings of Nuer totemism.)
- **Historical account of proliferation.** Evans-Pritchard linked the historical multiplication of refracted spirit-forms (spirits of the air, fetishes, most totemic spirits, probably also nature sprites) to nineteenth- and twentieth-century Nuer historical processes: large-scale absorption of Dinka people and lines of descent (bringing Dinka totems), the emergence of prophets directing raids against the Dinka and resistance to "the Turks" (Arabs and the British) (bringing new spirits of the air), and "the peace and administration imposed... by the government of the Anglo-Egyptian Sudan," which gave protection to private vengeance-seekers (spreading fetishes). He dated the appearance of spirits of the air, by informant testimony and prophet chronology, to "as late as the second half of last century" — a historical/etic inference from oral testimony, not independent chronometric evidence; treat as `approximate`/`disputed` precision. He also cited Dr. Lienhardt's post-fieldwork report that spirit-proliferation had continued, attributed to "further social disintegration," dated later than the 1930–36 fieldwork window but not given an exact date.
- **Reaffirmed for prophets.** Late in the book Evans-Pritchard extended the concept to prophets and their totemic representations: "It is much the same with the totemic representations of Spirit. They are not in themselves of a spiritual nature but only material symbols which serve to differentiate Spirit in its relation to some people from its relation to other people." He credited the concept's continuing analytic power for the historical spread of new spiritual forms: "We have seen in an earlier chapter how the Nuer notion of Spirit lends itself to refraction without limits... all the new spiritual conceptions which have arisen in the course of the last two generations could not have come into existence [otherwise] without entirely changing the character of Nuer religious thought. As it is, they have partly changed it."

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct. Evans-Pritchard's evidentiary method was primarily a single extended ethnographic case: a delayed-homicide (*nin diet*) settlement ceremony he witnessed in western Nuerland, in which successive speakers addressed Spirit by different titles depending on group affiliation — *kwoth* (unqualified), *kwoth a nhial* ("Spirit who is in the sky"), *kwoth wic(da)* ("spirit of our home/community"), *kwoth ngoap(na)* ("spirit of our fig-tree," the Lual lineage's totem), *kwoth rieng(da)* ("spirit of the flesh," the sacerdotal power of leopard-skin priests) — all addressed to, and sacrificed over, a single ox victim, which he read as proof that one and the same Spirit was being invoked in different refracted aspects rather than distinct deities: "may be compared to a war between European powers in which each prays for victory to the God of its fathers, Lord of its battle-line. Those engaged in the struggle do not believe that two distinct deities are being appealed to." He paired this with a counter-illustration: when no sectional interests were at stake — severe storms, drought, famine, serious illness, and a second case of a boy accidentally wounded by a spear where the two villages remained on good terms — Spirit was addressed as unqualified *kwoth*, without refracting titles. He also recorded further unnamed/generic refractions: *kwoth muonde* ("spirit of his [the priest's] earth"), *kwoth cuekni* ("spirit of twins," invoked at twin births), the nameless tutelary spirit of an age-set, and *kwoth rieka* ("God of the post/shrine," a domestic hearth spirit invoked at byre-building). Naming pattern tracked ownership: individually owned, competitive spirits (spirits of the air, fetishes) received proper/ox-names, while unqualified group refractions (of a lineage, age-set, etc.) remained nameless because ownership was collective and impersonal.

## Applications

Evans-Pritchard applied the refraction concept beyond the spirit hierarchy proper to explain clan spear-names (*mut gwara*): God invoked "in some refraction" as clan patron (*kwoth gwara*, "God of our fathers") paired with the clan spear-name exordium — offered as a further empirical instance of refraction operating at the level of clan invocation (see [[sacrifice]] for the fuller treatment of spear symbolism and the sacrificial act). The concept is also foundational to his account of [[totemism]] (totemic spirits as the refraction of Spirit to the lineage level) and of the priest/prophet distinction and the historical emergence of prophets (refraction proliferating under nineteenth-century political pressure).

## Critiques and Limitations

Evans-Pritchard explicitly flagged the refraction analysis as an external, sociological heuristic, not Nuer self-understanding, and paired it with a call for a complementary "inside" phenomenological account: "In relating the configuration of Nuer religious thought to the structural order of their society I am, of course, relating abstractions to one another by a method of sociological analysis. It is not suggested that the Nuer see their religion in this sort of way." Later: "The analysis made in this chapter is from the outside... But in a study of religion, if we wish to seize the essential nature of what we are inquiring into we have to try to examine the matter from the inside also, to see it as Nuer see it." He also admitted the historical (proliferation) leg of the argument is "not susceptible of proof."

Although the refraction thesis maps Nuer cosmology onto segmentary social structure in a manner continuous with [[structural-functionalism]] (Evans-Pritchard was trained under Radcliffe-Brown), he elsewhere in the same book explicitly subordinated the structural-functionalist reading of religion to an individual/theological one: "The role of this religion in the regulation of the social life, its structural role, is subsidiary to its role in the regulation of the individual's relations with God, its personal role." He also directly challenged Durkheim's reductive claim that religious conceptions are "nothing more than a symbolic representation of the social order": "This postulate of sociologistic metaphysic seems to me to be an assertion for which evidence is totally lacking. It was Durkheim and not the savage who made society into a god." The refraction concept should therefore be read as accepting that social structure *shapes* Nuer religious representation without accepting a fully reductive Durkheimian derivation of it — a `challenges`-type relation to Durkheimian sociology of religion, attributed to Evans-Pritchard.
