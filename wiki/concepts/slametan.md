# Slametan

## Definition as Coined

[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] (*[[geertz-religion-of-java-1960|The Religion of Java]]*, Ch. 1): “At the center of the whole Javanese religious system lies a simple, formal, undramatic, almost furtive, little ritual: the **slametan** (also sometimes called a *kendurèn*).” It is “the Javanese version of what is perhaps the world’s most common religious ritual, the communal feast,” symbolizing “mystic and social unity” of participants. In Modjokuto it forms a “social universal joint” fitting social life together to minimize uncertainty — “or at least it is supposed to do so.” Among [[abangan]] it “still retains much of its original force.”

## Semantic History

Emic ritual name. Geertz’s **core ritual** framing made it a type-case in ritual studies and Indonesian ethnography. Santri and prijaji participate selectively; Geertz’s emphasis is abangan structure.

## Operationalizations

**Pattern (Geertz):** evening, just after sunset; women prepare food *mburi*, men perform *ngarepan*; territorial invitation of all neighbors within a short radius (messenger 5–10 minutes before); host’s formal high-Javanese speech (thanks, specific intention, general aim of *slamet*); incense; Arabic Islamic chant (often by *modin*); distribution of food parcels.

**Cycles:** birth (*tingkeban*, *babaran*, *pasaran*, *pitonan*); circumcision (*sunatan*) and marriage (*kepanggihan*); death (*lajatan*) and commemorations; calendrical (*muludan*, *maleman*, etc.); village (*bersih désa*); intermittent (house-moving, name-changing, illness, political meetings).

**Economics:** costs vary by scale; Geertz reports observed prices during fieldwork and proposes a “comparative phenomenology of currency” for foreign readers.

## Applications

Ritual analysis; neighborhood integration; contrast with [[santri]] prayer/Friday/fast pattern.

## Critiques and Limitations

Urban/suburban change weakens efficiency (Geertz’s own caveat). “Core” is analytic emphasis, not exclusive ownership by one variant.
