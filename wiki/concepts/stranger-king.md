# Stranger-King

## Definition as Coined

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985, ch. 3 "The Stranger-King; or, Dumézil among the Fijians"; first as a 1981 lecture) names the **stranger-king** the recurrent structure in which "the kingship makes its appearance from outside the society. Initially a stranger and something of a terror, the king is absorbed and domesticated by the indigenous people, a process that passes by way of his symbolic death and consequent rebirth as a local god." The founding act is a marriage: the incoming prince weds a ranking woman of the "people of the land," and the ruling line descends as the *sister's son* (vasu) of the indigenous people — an exchange, in Sahlins's diagram, of "raw women" (given by the land people at the founding) for "cooked men" (human sacrifice victims distributed by the chief thereafter).

Sahlins frames the pattern, after Clastres, as "society against the state" — the indigenous people's ritual domestication of an alien sovereign — while noting the motif's wide distribution (Frazer and [[dumezil-georges|Dumézil]] on divine kingship across Polynesia, Africa, Rome, India, Ireland, Persia, and Mexico).

## Semantic History

The essay takes its subtitle from [[dumezil-georges|Georges Dumézil]], who suggested the Fijian chief's installation illuminates Indo-European sovereignty. Sahlins applies Dumézil's **tripartite ideology** (the three functions) and the paired dispositions **celeritas** (the youthful, violent, magical energy of conquering princes) and **gravitas** (the staid, priestly, productive character of the established people): "in the initial moment of their combination celeritas prevails over gravitas, as the invaders capture the reproductive powers of the land to found their kingdom." He distinguishes his reading from Frazer (the political as a reflex of nature) and from functionalism (the king's death as disguised political catharsis), and from ordinary [[totemism]] — because Polynesian cosmology posits genealogical *consubstantiality* among gods, nature, and humans, its relations are "synecdochic," "a universal system of differential homologies rather than of homologous differences."

## Operationalizations / Applications

The core evidence is **Fijian installation ritual** (see [[fijians]]; Sahlins's sources are A. M. Hocart's early-20th-century ethnography and the 1969 Tui Nayau investiture at Lakeba): the chief, styled a "stranger" (*vulagi*) and "different person" (*kai tani*), arrives from the sea, is led along a barkcloth path from foreign to domestic ground, and is **symbolically poisoned by kava** — the kava root, "child of the land," split by a native owner, is figured as a sacrificed child of the indigenous people, and the chief drinking it enters a state "dead from" (*mateni*) before being revived "to live" (*bula*). Land is never conveyed to him: he receives *lewa* ("rule") while the soil remains the indigenous owners'. The parallel Hawaiian case is the **Makahiki** and Cook's reception as Lono (see [[hawaiians]], [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]]). In *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors]]* (1981) the Hawaiian charter is the **Paao legend**: a foreign priest-chief from Kahiki deposes the indigenous ruler, installs a new line (ancestral to Kamehameha, ~20 generations back), and introduces human sacrifice, the Kū cult, and the prostration tabu — the deposed Kapawa type representing "an earlier, more indigenous type of ruling chief... who eschews human sacrifice." Hence "a chief is a shark that travels on land," and Europeans stood to Hawaiians generally as Hawaiian chiefs — themselves godlike beings from Kahiki — stood to commoners; usurpers customarily appropriated the ranking woman of the deposed line (Kamehameha marrying Keōpūolani to sire Liholiho), and per Hocart "the succession is the celebration of a victory." Sahlins even reads Kamehameha's reported challenge to Vancouver (that each jump off a cliff to test whose god was stronger) as poetic re-enactment of the Paao myth — "the entire Hawaiian theory of the European presence." Other comparative cases comparative cases include Rome (Aeneas marrying Lavinia; the polity taking the indigenous "Latin" name) and the pan-Polynesian sacred-marriage myths.

## Critiques and Limitations

The stranger-king is an interpretive structural type assembled from ritual and myth across many societies; like [[heroic-history]] it raises the Galton's-problem caution about treating diffusion-linked Polynesian cases as independent confirmations. Sahlins's Fijian and Hawaiian reconstructions rest on colonial-era ethnography (Hocart, missionary sources) and are attributed. The concept has had a long afterlife in the anthropology of kingship well beyond this wiki's ingested sources.
