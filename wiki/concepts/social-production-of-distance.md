# Social Production of Distance

## Definition as Coined

In *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989), especially Chapter 6 ("The Ethics of Obedience"), [[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]] argues (attributed voice) that **cruelty is a function of social distance, not of individual disposition**: readiness to inflict harm rises as the physical and psychical distance between actor and victim increases. Modern society *produces* that distance systematically — through the division of labour and long chains of mediation — thereby dissolving moral inhibition without any need for anti-moral indoctrination.

## Semantic History

Bauman builds the concept on his reading of **Stanley Milgram's** obedience experiments. Milgram reported (facts attributed to Milgram 1974) that obedience — willingness to administer the highest "shock" — rose with each stage of mediation: roughly 30% when the subject had to force the victim's hand onto the plate, ~40% manipulating levers, ~62.5% when the victim was audible behind a wall, ~65% with sound off; and that 37 of 40 subjects continued to the maximum when they performed only a *subsidiary* act. Bauman's interpretive move (his, not Milgram's) generalizes this — citing John Lachs — into a theory of mediation: "the distance we feel from our actions is proportionate to our ignorance of them." He also reads Philip Zimbardo's prison experiment and Gregory Bateson's *schismogenesis* as further evidence that cruelty is situational and structural, not characterological.

## Operationalizations

- **Milgram's proximity gradient** (attributed to Milgram) — the empirical anchor.
- **Bureaucratic mediation** — "minute division of labour, as well as the sheer length of the chain of acts that mediate between the initiative and its tangible effects, emancipates most... from moral significance." See [[bureaucracy]]; this shades directly into [[adiaphorization]].
- **The "agentic state"** (Milgram's term, adopted by Bauman) — once responsibility is shifted to a superior, the actor "sees himself as carrying out another person's wishes," the elementary unit of responsibility-diffusion.
- **Weapons technology** — Bauman (via Philip Caputo) reads the historical trend toward killing "at long range with sophisticated weapons" as the paradigm of distance-production removing the need to indoctrinate combatants.

## Applications

Bauman maps the Nazi "destruction process" (Raul Hilberg's stages: definition → dismissal/expropriation → concentration → exploitation → annihilation) as a sequence of *increasing distantiation* — physical and mental — culminating in the "eviction from the universe of obligation" (Helen Fein's phrase). The 2000 Afterword extends the argument to **abstraction**: "when applied to humans, that power means effacing the face," making categorial (genocidal) murder possible — see [[genocide]] and the "categorial murder" idea (Roberto Toscano). One protective inference Bauman draws from Milgram's multi-authority variant (obedience collapsed when authorities visibly disagreed) is that **pluralism is "the best preventive medicine"** against morally normal people doing morally abnormal things. → [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]]

## Critiques and Limitations

The reliance on Milgram and Zimbardo inherits their contested afterlives (methodological and ethical critiques of Milgram; the well-known later controversies over Zimbardo's prison study) — neither has a study page here yet, and both are used at second hand through Bauman. As with [[adiaphorization]], critics who stress ideology argue distance-and-mediation understates believed hatred; Bauman's rejoinder is that distance, not conviction, is what makes cruelty scalable.
