# Generalized Other

## Definition as Coined

For [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], **the generalized other** is "the organized community or social group which gives to the individual his unity of self"; "the attitude of the generalized other is the attitude of the whole community." Where [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|taking the role]] of a *particular* other organizes the self in a specific act, taking the attitude of the generalized other imports the organized attitude of the group *as a whole* — the decisive second stage in the self's development.

Per Huebner's source-critical work, the concept's first appearance in *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* comes specifically from Robert Page's rewritten 1930 lecture notes (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]); the term itself is Mead's, developed in his 1925 paper "The Genesis of the Self and Social Control."

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Mead, 1925/1934 | The organized attitude of the whole community, internalized as the standpoint of the self |
| Institutions | The generalized other in stabilized form — "an identical response on the part of the whole community" |
| Later usage | Generalized into "society in the individual," reference groups, internalized norms |

Mead extends it beyond persons: inanimate objects can enter the generalized other "in so far as [the individual] responds to such objects socially" (e.g., a community's cult relation to its physical environment).

## Operationalizations

Not measured. Its developmental emergence is operationalized through the [[play-and-the-game|game]]: taking the organized attitudes of *all* players at once, under rules, is the concrete achievement of the generalized-other standpoint. In "abstract thought the individual takes the attitude of the generalized other" without reference to particular persons — the basis of a "universe of discourse."

## Applications

- **Institutions.** "One of the greatest advances in the development of the community arises when this reaction of the community on the individual takes on... an institutional form" — an identical, impersonal response regardless of which particular person is involved.
- **Moral character** is the structure formed by internalizing the generalized other's organized principles, distinct from a bundle of unconscious habits.
- **Property** exemplifies it: "This is my property" calls out "an organized... common attitude" shared by all community members — unlike a dog's non-social possession of a bone.
- **Social reform and the higher community.** One can defy the whole community "only by setting up a higher sort of community which in a certain sense out-votes the one we find" — speaking "with the voice of reason," a wider generalized other of past, future, and rational beings. This grounds Mead's account of the [[the-i-and-the-me|"I"]]'s creative resistance to the given [[the-i-and-the-me|"me"]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The concept assumes a relatively unitary "community"; critics note the multiplicity and conflict of generalized others in stratified, plural societies (an issue Mead's own account of resisting the community partly anticipates).
- Its canonical wording is editorially reconstructed and traceable to Page's 1930 notes rather than a Mead manuscript.
