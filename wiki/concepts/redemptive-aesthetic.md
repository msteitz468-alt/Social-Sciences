# Redemptive Aesthetic

Programmatic proposal by [[shanks-michael|Shanks]] and [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] in *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]* (1987/1992, Ch. 4): an alternative to commodified, aestheticized, and heritage-myth presentations of the archaeological past in museums — a “redemptive” relation between past and present that retains heterogeneity, democratizes authorship, and treats display as open construction rather than delivery of a completed past.

## Definition as Coined

After interpretive studies of British museum modes, Shanks & Tilley (attributed) stated four implications:

1. **Retain heterogeneity and difference** — fragmentary, discontinuous past — to overcome ideological effects of a reified object world; resist homogeneous “always-the-same” history that conceals class-structured present and imposes present images on the past (drawing on Adorno/Jameson as they cite them).
2. **Oppose professional preservative History** — archaeologist-curator speaking for a monolithic past; democratize and personalize authorship; renounce expert-producer / consuming-public relation and elitist high culture.
3. **Embrace reflexivity of authorship** — presentations are specific present encounters with particular pasts; museum as open process in which visitor constructs pasts with the curator, not participation toward a pre-given end.
4. **Refuse one-dimensional objectivity of the artifact** — detach the artifact from “self-evident” meaning as scientific specimen; reveal non-identity with apparent meaning; allow gesture to material inscription in social practice (cross-linked to style analysis, Ch. 7).

**Techniques** they listed: introduce political content showing how pasts are manipulated; montage/juxtaposition of decontextualized past and contemporary artifacts; irony, humour, exaggeration against third-person narrative; non-permanent displays emphasizing authorship; community use of museum artifacts outside institutional monopoly.

## Semantic History

- **Critical Theory / Benjaminian aesthetics** (as S&T use them): montage, non-identity, critique of reification and homogeneous empty time — vocabularies migrated into archaeological museum critique.
- **1980s heritage boom** (UK): Beamish, Jorvik, period rooms, “living history” — S&T’s chapter is a contemporary critique of that boom as much as of traditional glass-case museums.
- Later public archaeology / critical heritage studies pursue related aims (multivocality, community archaeology, contested heritage) often without the phrase “redemptive aesthetic”; the page keeps their attributed term rather than equating it with all later public archaeology.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Operational content is **display critique + alternative techniques**:

| Museum mode (as S&T characterize) | Sites/examples in Ch. 4 | Alleged effect |
|---|---|---|
| Artifact as commodity in commodified time | Museum of Antiquities, Newcastle | Chronometric reification |
| Aesthetic artifact / *Homo Artifex* | British Museum | High-culture aestheticization |
| Narrative display / information | Museum of London | Artifact as information |
| Period room naturalism | Castle Museum, York | Simulacrum of presence |
| Shop-front commodification | Castle Museum, York | Retail aesthetics |
| Heritage mythical past | Beamish Open Air Museum | Visiting a constructed “heritage” |
| Archaeologist as hero / discovery labour | Jorvik Viking Centre | Spectacular rescue narrative |

These are **attributed interpretive analyses** of 1980s displays, not multi-site findings in wiki voice.

## Applications

- Feeds [[heritage]] / [[heritage-management]] critique and [[post-processual-archaeology]] politics-of-presentation strand.
- Complements *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* politics-of-theory chapter (same authors, same year).
- Related later programs: multivocal heritage, indigenous consultation, feminist archaeology’s stake in who authors the past (named in Ch. 9).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Museum readings are **single-volume interpretive case studies** of selected English sites in a specific decade — not a survey of world museums.
- “Redemptive” language invites charges of political program over scholarship — same reception cluster as the wider S&T project (relativism, ideology critique as “Stalinism,” etc.; see [[processual-postprocessual-debate]] and 1992 Appendix).
- Practical CRM and permanent-collection constraints often sit uneasily with non-permanent, montage-heavy ideals — a tension later public archaeology literature negotiates without necessarily endorsing S&T’s full aesthetic program.
