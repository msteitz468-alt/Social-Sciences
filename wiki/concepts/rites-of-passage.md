# Rites of Passage

> Analytical concept coined by [[van-gennep-arnold|Arnold van Gennep]] (*Les rites de
> passage*, 1909) and revived and extended by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] in
> *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]* (1967) and
> *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to van Gennep.)* **Rites of passage** are "rites which accompany every change
of place, state, social position and age." Van Gennep showed that all such rites share a
processual form of three phases:

1. **Separation** — symbolic detachment of the ritual subject from an earlier fixed point
   in the social structure or a cultural "state."
2. **Margin** (or **limen**, Latin for "threshold") — the transitional phase in which the
   subject's condition is ambiguous, passing through a cultural realm with few or none of
   the attributes of the past or coming state. This is the phase Turner made central (see
   [[liminality]]).
3. **Aggregation** (reincorporation) — the subject returns to a relatively stable state
   with newly defined structural rights and obligations.

Turner (attributed) elects the inclusive term **"state"** over "status" or "office," to
cover any culturally recognized stable or recurrent condition and sharpen the contrast
between *state* and *transition*. He distinguishes **ritual** (associated with transitions,
"transformative") from **ceremony** (associated with states, "confirmatory").

## Semantic History

- **van Gennep 1909:** the tripartite scheme, developed comparatively across birth,
  initiation, marriage, funerary, and calendrical rites; the relative emphasis on each
  phase varies with the occasion.
- **Neglect (1909–1950s):** the concept was largely ignored by the dominant Durkheimian
  school in van Gennep's lifetime.
- **Turner's revival (1960s–70s):** Turner recentered the scheme on its middle phase,
  theorizing the *content and experience* of the margin — **[[liminality]]** and its social
  correlate **[[communitas]]** — and extending the frame from tribal ritual to complex
  societies (monasticism, millenarian movements, the 1960s counterculture).

## Operationalizations

Turner applies the three-phase frame to Ndembu ritual (attributed, fieldwork 1950–1954):

| Phase | Ndembu terms (per Turner) | Marker |
|---|---|---|
| Separation | Ilembi (women's cults); the *mukoleku* gateway (Mukanda) | detachment from prior state, stripping of status |
| Margin/liminal | Kunkunka (seclusion hut); the lodge *ng'ula* (Mukanda) | ambiguity, seclusion, communitas among neophytes |
| Aggregation | Ku-tumbuka (festive reintegration dance) | return with new rights and identity |

## Applications

- Boys' circumcision (**Mukanda**) and girls' puberty (**Nkang'a**) among the [[ndembu]].
- Women's fertility cults (Isoma, Wubwang'u) analyzed as rites of passage in
  *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]*.
- Chief installation (the Kanongesha) as a rite of status elevation whose liminal phase
  humbles the aspirant.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The scheme is a description of *form*; it does not by itself explain the *symbolic
  content* of any given rite (which Turner supplies via [[dominant-symbol]] analysis).
- Turner's extension of the liminal phase to complex societies drew the charge of
  overgeneralization (see [[communitas-scale-debate]]).
