# Levallois

Prepared-core **flake production technique** central to Middle Palaeolithic / [[mousterian|Mousterian]] systematics. Treated in Stringer & Gamble (1993) as part of the technological repertoire of the Ancients (*[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]* boxes and ch. 7).

## Definition as Coined

Named from **Levallois** (Paris suburb) where archaeologists first recognized the knapping technique. As of Stringer & Gamble (1993): the shape of the flake is **predetermined** before it is struck off the core — nodule prepared with a domed surface and striking platform; flake/blade struck and the procedure repeated until the core is too small ("**tortoise core**"). Bruce Bradley's experiments: from tabular flint blocks measuring 4 × 15 cm, between **3 and 8** flakes of predetermined dimensions could be struck. Large Levallois blades also occur in the Middle Palaeolithic; triangular Levallois points may have been hafted (attributed, S&G 1993).

## Semantic History

### Index of Middle Palaeolithic skill

S&G adopt ~250–200 ka as MP start partly because after this hand axes decline while Levallois becomes much more common; La Cotte (Jersey, ~238 ka as of 1993) is dominated by flake tools/side scrapers with Levallois common and hand axes rare/absent — artifactually "Middle Palaeolithic" yet similar in age to coarser "Lower" assemblages elsewhere (attributed, 1993). Levallois appears on coarse Pontnewydd rocks and African volcanics as well as fine flints/cherts — not purely a fine-raw-material phenomenon (attributed, 1993).

### Facies marker

In Bordes' [[mousterian|Mousterian]] systematics, Levallois frequency helps distinguish facies (e.g. **Ferrassie** high Levallois / side scrapers vs **Quina** rare Levallois / transverse scrapers). Bordes treated flake-production technique as largely raw-material conditioned for culture-historical purposes (see [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).

### Disc cores and Upper Palaeolithic blades

S&G contrast **disc cores** as an alternative MP flake-production method: cores not prepared for predetermined shape but rotated for detachment angles; loss of predetermined size may be compensated by more serviceable flakes per lump. They stress that Levallois differs in approach and end product from later Upper Palaeolithic **blade cores**, and judge it **unlikely** that Levallois indicates the same degree of **forward planning** in tool manufacture as UP blade technology (attributed, S&G 1993) — a theoretical claim linking technology to [[behavioral-modernity]].

Prepared-core **blade** production is a novel raw-material use relative to Levallois/disc methods but is **not**, in their view, an Upper Palaeolithic innovation in the old sense — hence not a reliable Ancient/Modern flint-working marker. Howiesons Poort blades/microliths (Klasies River Mouth etc.), intermittent PUP at Haua Fteah (Libya) and Tabun (Mount Carmel), and Amudian blade episodes show so-called UP technologies in Middle Palaeolithic contexts; earlier blade appearances should not be read as behavioural continuity support (attributed, S&G 1993).

### Regional variants (as of S&G 1993)

- **Victoria West** cores (South Africa): large side-struck flakes for hand axes/cleavers.
- **Wadi Halfa** (Nubia; Marks): pebble preparation with bladelet removals.
- **Boker Tachtit** (Negev; Anthony Marks): four levels, earliest ~47 ka — within one sequence, Middle→Upper Palaeolithic flake-to-blade shift while Emireh projectile end-product stays constant; refitting shows knapper began with Middle Palaeolithic Levallois then shifted to blade technique as the nodule reduced — maker species unknown; further undermines technology = biology equations (attributed via S&G 1993).

## Operationalizations

- Presence/frequency of Levallois flakes, points, and tortoise cores in type lists and Bordes cumulative graphs.
- Experimental replication of predetermined-flake yields (Bradley).
- Refitting sequences documenting core-reduction pathways (e.g. Marks at Boker Tachtit).
- Contrast metrics vs disc-core and prismatic blade-core assemblages.

## Applications

- Hallmark of many [[mousterian]] and broader Middle Palaeolithic / MSA assemblages.
- Chronological marker (partial) for MP onset in European systematics (S&G 1993 framing).
- Test of planning-depth and cognitive-capacity arguments in Ancient vs Modern comparisons.
- Facies discrimination within SW French Mousterian.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Cognitive over-reading**: predetermined flakes do not automatically equal modern forward planning (S&G themselves downgrade Levallois vs UP blade cores).
- **Not uniquely Neanderthal** worldwide; not a species marker.
- Blade and PUP episodes show that prepared-core/blade methods are not exclusive Upper Palaeolithic Modern markers.
- Reductionist and raw-material models (Dibble, Barton, Kuhn) complicate reading any single production mode as a cultural tradition.

## Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein describes the Levallois technique as a prepared-core method: a core is shaped by peripheral
flaking, then flakes/blades of predetermined size and shape are struck from the prepared surface.
It appears sporadically in late Acheulean assemblages (~400–200 ka) but becomes common only in the
[[mousterian|Mousterian]]/Middle Stone Age after ~200 ka, its use heaviest where large,
high-quality nodules were abundant. Boëda's refitting studies show knappers commanded **several
distinct Levallois methods** (for single large flakes, recurrent flakes, points, or blades),
implying deliberate technical flexibility rather than one fixed procedure. Associated mainly with
[[neanderthals]] and their near-modern contemporaries.

## From Gamble, *Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]*; as of Gamble 1999.)*

Gamble stresses preparation/predetermination of flakes and blades (Levallois and blade core reduction) as major Middle Palaeolithic developments; soft-hammer prismatic blades dated to at least ~100 Kyr BP when once thought exclusively Upper Palaeolithic — major trend toward elaboration of **débitage** rather than façonnage (attributed). Widespread Levallois after the Saalian (~after 300 Kyr BP; Bridgland proposes first wide appearance in OIS 8); conceptual links to biface façonnage earlier (e.g. Cagny la Garenne OIS 11–12). White & Pettitt (via Gamble): Levallois is the only major technological innovation 500–200 Kyr BP justifying the Lower/Middle division.

**Not raw-material-determined alone:** used on quartzites and “inferior” materials (Reutersruh, Pontnewydd volcanic rocks) as well as high-quality flint (Bakers Hole) (attributed). Boëda’s **recurrent Levallois** at Biache-St-Vaast level IIA (late OIS 7/early OIS 6) as [[chaine-operatoire|chaîne opératoire]] dynamics — knappers master **actions of making**, not merely pre-determined shapes (attributed). Geneste: the most mobile Mousterian elements (distant raw materials) are Levallois products — transport promotes flexibility under less predictable environments; Gamble prefers mobility-tactics readings (Kuhn 1995) over linguistic/pre-planning readings of Levallois (attributed). Of eight exploitation modes, only Mode 8 (blanks/tools without primary waste — often Levallois) occurs >11 km from source (Féblot-Augustins via Gamble). Quina facies: IL <10%, small territories, artifacts **not** landscape-transported — contrast with Levallois assemblages (attributed). Association of Levallois with more distant raw materials tracks enhanced individual mobility and a new temporality of action within societies still built on co-presence and strong intimate/effective networks (attributed, ch. 5 summary).
