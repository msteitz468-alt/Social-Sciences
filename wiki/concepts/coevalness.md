# Coevalness

## Definition as Coined

**Coevalness** is the term [[fabian-johannes|Johannes Fabian]] introduced in *[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other]]* (1983) for **the sharing of the same Time between the anthropologist and the people studied** — "recognizing cotemporality as the condition for truly dialectical confrontation between persons as well as societies." Fabian argued (attributed) that ethnographic fieldwork *requires* coevalness — "for human communication to occur, coevalness has to be created," and "communication is, ultimately, about creating shared Time" — because social interaction presupposes intersubjectivity, which presupposes that participants share Time (an argument he drew from Alfred Schutz's phenomenology of the "mutual tuning-in relationship").

Fabian chose the word deliberately to steer between two narrower terms: *synchronous/simultaneous* (mere coincidence in physical time) and *contemporary* (co-occurrence in a typological scheme). "Coeval" — glossed from the pocket Oxford dictionary as "of same age, duration, or epoch" — spans both senses and additionally connotes "a common, active 'occupation,' or sharing, of Time." He pointed to the German *gleichzeitig* / *Gleichzeitigkeit* as cognate.

The concept's polemical charge lies in its negation. Fabian's central thesis is that anthropology exhibits a **denial of coevalness**: "a persistent and systematic tendency to place the referent(s) of anthropology in a Time other than the present of the producer of anthropological discourse." He named this device [[allochronism]] to distinguish a systematic rhetorical-political strategy from a mere factual error (*anachronism*). Crucially, Fabian did **not** intend coevalness as *identity* of Time: "If it meant the oneness of Time as identity, coevalness would indeed amount to a theory of appropriation" — a reading he explicitly rejected. Coevalness names the ground on which genuine, conflictual, dialectical difference can appear ("different societies facing each other at the same Time"), not the dissolution of difference.

## Semantic History

The ordinary English adjective "coeval" long predates Fabian; his contribution was to make **coevalness** a technical epistemological and political concept. Fabian himself flagged possible precursors he did not fully develop — notably Ernst Bloch's *Gleichzeitigkeit* / *Ungleichzeitigkeit* (contemporaneity / non-contemporaneity) and the phenomenological "intersubjective Time" of Schutz. After 1983 the term entered the critical-anthropology and postcolonial vocabulary as shorthand for the demand that the people anthropology studies be treated as contemporaries rather than as living survivals of an earlier stage. It travels together with its antonym-device [[allochronism]] and with the phrase "the denial of coevalness."

## Operationalizations

Coevalness is not measured; it is a condition whose *denial* Fabian showed can be located textually:

- **Tense.** The [[ethnographic-present]] (describing a studied society in the present tense) combined with the grammatical third person marks the Other as outside the dialogic situation — "another way to describe denial of coevalness."
- **Typological vocabulary.** Terms such as "primitive," "savage," "tribal," "traditional," "cold society" assign the Other to another Time ([[allochronism]]; [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]).
- **Visual-spatial reduction.** Presenting culture as something *seen* — maps, tables, taxonomies, diagrams — spatializes and distances ([[visualism]]).
- **Positive test.** Traces of coevalness surface where fieldwork breaks through the writing — Fabian read Malinowski's *Diary*, Turnbull's *The Forest People*, and Lévi-Strauss's *Tristes Tropiques* as such moments.

## Applications

The concept anchors Fabian's re-reading of the discipline's history and grounds his positive program: a **processual, materialist, dialogical** anthropology in which "the anthropologist and his interlocutors only 'know' when they meet each other in one and the same cotemporality," and in which the Knower cannot claim ascendancy over the Known. Fabian tied the denial of coevalness to the political economy of colonialism ("geopolitics has its ideological foundations in chronopolitics"), making coevalness a category of the critical/reflexive turn of the 1980s alongside the crisis of ethnographic representation. It underwrites arguments that the ethnographic present be abandoned and that fieldwork's shared time be carried into the written text.

**Reception and extension (attributed).** Matti Bunzl's reprint foreword (in [[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983]]) treats coevalness as the criterion by which post-1980s reflexive ethnography is judged: imperfect/past tense, authorial "I," historicized encounters, and performative/dialogical monographs (Fabian's own *Power and Performance*, *Remembering the Present*; Tsing's *Diamond Queen*). Fabian's postscript extends coevalness toward **recognition and memory** — "in order to be knowingly in each other's presence we must somehow share each other's past" — and pairs it with presence vs. re-presentation: co-presence is a condition of *inquiry*, not simply a literary requirement of *representation*. Global-anthropology programs (Appadurai's scapes; Hannerz's "global ecumene") and native anthropology (Narayan, Weston) are read by Bunzl as further pressure against allochronic relegation of a "really distant Other."

## Critiques and Limitations

Fabian himself raised the sharpest objections (attributed, and left unresolved in the book):

- **The reflexive objection.** His own critique might itself be allochronic — examining anthropology's uses of Time "while disregarding time-conceptions in other cultures." Fabian: "There is no simple way to counter that objection."
- **The absorption worry.** A theory of coevalness could become "a program for ultimate temporal absorption of the Other," folding all societies into a single Western-managed present — hence his insistence that coevalness is not identity.
- **Domination vs. liberation.** He asked whether there are "criteria by which to distinguish denial of coevalness as a condition of domination from refusal of coevalness as an act of liberation," and offered none.
- **Augé's contemporaneity critique.** Marc Augé argued (as Fabian reports) that emphasis on denial of coevalness remains inside the very frame it tries to break and underplays modernity and contemporaneity. Fabian replies that a further step toward contemporaneity need not invalidate the first step of denouncing allochronism, and points to his popular-culture work as already concerned with African contemporaneity.
- **Allochronism ≠ manufacturing alterity.** In the postscript Fabian insists a likely misunderstanding is that coevalness was meant to "overcome" otherness: without alterity there is no culture and no plural *Lebenswelt*; the failure is failing to *recognize* alterity's epistemological significance, not the existence of difference.
- **Presence paradox.** If anthropology succeeded fully in making others present, would that put ethnographers "out of business as their representers"? Fabian leaves the quandary open while insisting voice-giving remains representation.

Later readers note that coevalness is easier to state as a demand than to realize in writing, and that Fabian conceded a "glaring absence of the issue of race" from the analysis that frames it. See the [[ethnographic-present-debate]] for the dispute over the specific textual device.
