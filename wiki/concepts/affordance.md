# Affordance

## Definition as Coined

**Affordance** is James J. Gibson's term (*The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception*, 1979) for what the environment **offers** an animal, relative to that animal's powers of action and perception. Affordances are relational properties of the animal–environment system — neither purely subjective projections nor organism-independent physics alone *(Gibson, attributed)*.

## Semantic History

Gibson developed affordances against Cartesian and cognitivist theories that treat perception as constructing internal representations from raw sense data. [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]] makes Gibson the decisive psychological resource of *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* (2000): perception is "not the achievement of a mind in a body, but of the organism as a whole in its environment," "tantamount to the organism's own exploratory movement through the world" (General introduction). Affordances ground the [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]], [[enskilment]] (education of attention into fields of opportunity), and [[wayfinding]] (occlusion, path, region). Later HCI/design "affordance" (Norman) drifts toward perceived usability of objects — a more subjectivist, product-design usage Ingold's ecological line does not endorse.

[[hodder-ian|Hodder]] (*[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* 2012, via Knappett and others) takes Gibsonian affordance as the **functional side of [[fittingness]]**: properties of materials/things afford outcomes and “point both ways” to environment and actor (handles/hands; pot bottoms and tables). He also stresses that mutual affordance can tip into unequal **[[dependence-dependency|dependency]]** (e.g. deeper ploughing later depleting nutrients) and that affordances alone do not exhaust why traits are accepted — resonance and coherence complete fittingness inside [[entanglement-theory|entanglement]].

## Operationalizations

Ecological psychology; design research (Norman-influenced, distinct drift); anthropological description of skilled practice (hunting clues vs ciphers; craft materials as force-fields; Chs. 1, 18–19); archaeological material-connection analysis (Hodder 2012: pottery paste spaces, tool–body pairs, assembly constraints).

## Applications

In Ingold 2000: livelihood (what land and animals afford), dwelling (home-making), skill (tools/materials), vision and hearing as modes of exploratory engagement (Ch. 14), apprenticeship scaffolding (Ch. 19).

In Hodder 2012: thing–thing fit in fire, plaster, wheel, and car assemblies; nested technical factor spaces (van der Leeuw paste); organizational “fit” analogies; one component of trait selection within entanglements (with abstraction/resonance).

## Critiques and Limitations

Debate over ontological status of affordances; risk of underplaying culturally specific education of attention (which Ingold stresses) or unequal social access to environments. Anthropology of the senses debates (Stoller, Howes, etc.) partially parallel, partially conflict with Gibsonian naturalism about sensory systems (Ch. 14). Hodder’s use folds affordance into a larger entanglement apparatus — critics of that apparatus may accept Gibson while rejecting the dependence/dependency dialectic.
