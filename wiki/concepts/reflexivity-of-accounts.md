# Reflexivity of Accounts

In [[ethnomethodology]], the **reflexivity of accounts** names [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s
claim that the descriptions members give of a setting are *constituent features of the very
setting they describe* — accounts do not stand outside the activities they report but are
part of producing them.

## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel identified "the '**reflexive**,' or '**incarnate**' character of accounting
practices and accounts" as "the crux" of his recommendation about [[accountability]].
Members' accounts, he wrote, are "reflexively and essentially tied for their rational
features to the socially organized occasions of their use **for they are features of the
socially organized occasions of their use**." Describing a setting is itself an activity
within that setting, and helps make it the orderly setting it is taken to be.

## Semantic History

This technical sense must be **distinguished from "reflexivity" in the ordinary
methodological sense** — a researcher reflecting on their own social position and its effect
on their knowledge (the sense used in this wiki's Reflexivity sections and in the
post-*Writing Culture* anthropology of the [[symbolic-interactionism|interpretive]] turn).
Garfinkel's reflexivity is not a virtue researchers should cultivate but an *unavoidable
property* of all accounting, lay and professional alike. He notes that members are, in a
specific technical sense, "not interested" in this reflexivity — it is taken for granted as
"an unalterable and unavoidable condition of their inquiries," not something they study.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. It is exhibited analytically: the clinic-records studies (Ch. 6–7 of the
1967 volume) show that a case folder's entries help constitute the very organizational
reality they appear merely to record, so that the record cannot be read as an external,
"actuarial" description of an independently existing order.

## Applications

Reflexivity is why the [[documentary-method-of-interpretation|documentary method]] is
circular (evidence and underlying pattern elaborate each other), why coding produces "a
persuasive version" of order rather than a neutral description of it (see
[[etcetera-principle]]), and why Garfinkel could read the [[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes]]
disclosure as an account that "turned the article into a feature of the same circumstances
it reported."

## Critiques and Limitations

If accounts are always part of what they describe, critics ask, how can any sociological
account claim to be *about* an independent social world? Ethnomethodologists treat this not
as a paradox to escape but as the condition under which all description, including their own,
operates (see [[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]]).
