# Production of History

## Definition as Coined

[[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] frames the **production of history** as the object of analysis in *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]* (1995; topic essays from 1981; International Roundtables in History and Anthropology with David W. Cohen from 1985). The program asks **how history works** — where power enters the making of sources, archives, narratives, and public pasts — not only what a finished narrative says.

> “What history is matters less than how history works.” (Trouillot 1995, via Carby’s paraphrase and Ch.1)

Core thesis (attributed): production of historical narratives involves the **uneven contribution** of competing groups and individuals with **unequal access** to the means of such production. Forces at work are less visible than gunfire or class property but no less powerful. **Power is constitutive of the story**, not an external contaminant removable by better method. “In history, power begins at the source.”

## Semantic History

- **1981+:** Trouillot writes production of history as a distinct topic; family historiography (Ernst & Hénock Trouillot; Pressoir) as personal origin.
- **Roundtables 1986/1989:** Ch.1–2 evolve from Paris and Bellagio papers.
- **Interlocutors:** Sara Berry, Sidney Mintz, Katherine Verdery, Brackette Williams, Arjun Appadurai, Joan Dayan, among others (Acknowledgments).
- **Foucault:** cited for “how does it happen?” alongside “who exercises power?” — adjacency to [[power-knowledge]], not identity.
- **Relation to [[historical-anthropology]]:** overlaps Comaroff/Sahlins programs (culture-power over time; material as text) but centers **silence and narrative production** more than ethnography of colonial encounter.

Trouillot rejects: (a) prisoners-of-the-past fatalism; (b) history-as-whatever-we-make presentism; (c) rootless “end of history” postmodern claims; (d) guild monopoly on historical production.

## Operationalizations

1. **Expand the cast** — professional historians, politicians, students, fiction writers, filmmakers, peasant guides, commemorators; public continues reading after the last scholarly sentence.
2. **Expand chronology** — participants may narrate before the historian arrives; narrative frames can preexist events (Sahlins on Cook; Reagan’s prepackaged U.S. narrative — Trouillot’s illustrations).
3. **Four moments of [[historical-silences|silence]]** — sources → archives → narratives → retrospective significance.
4. **Case-matched strategies** — reposition sparse evidence; make unthinkable silences speak; track competing appropriations under abundance.
5. **Truth claim retained** — each historical narrative renews a claim to truth; fakes differ from fiction; Appadurai’s “debatability of the past”: nowhere is the past infinitely inventable.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Program is methodologically demanding (burden of the concrete); resists abstract blueprints.
- Risk of reducing all historiography to power critique; Trouillot still defends empirical exactitude as necessary (not sufficient) and authenticity as present ethical relation.
- One-author program with wide uptake; not a school with closed membership.
