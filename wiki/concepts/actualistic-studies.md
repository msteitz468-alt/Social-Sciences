## Definition as Coined

Binford used the term "actualistic studies" for research that investigates properties
of the contemporary world in order to make accurate diagnoses of past behavior —
explicitly naming the label a borrowed convenience ("what are sometimes called
'actualistic studies'") rather than claiming to have minted it (Binford 1983). The
concept names a *class* of research designs unified by a single move: because the
archaeological record is static and contemporary, and because what archaeologists want
to know about — past human activity — is dynamic and no longer observable directly,
any warranted inference from the one to the other must be grounded in cases where the
dynamic process and its material consequences can both be observed at once, and the
only place that simultaneous observation is possible is the present (Binford 1983).
Binford grouped three actualistic routes under this rationale: **ethnoarchaeology**
(living with and observing present-day peoples), **experimental archaeology**
(re-creating past processes — house-burning, tool-making, marrow-cracking — under
controlled conditions), and **historic-sites archaeology** (sites whose formation
process is independently documented in historical records) (Binford 1983).

## Semantic History

Binford's own account, given in *In Pursuit of the Past*, traces the concept to a
specific crisis: after several field seasons generating ever more correlations and
patterns from Mousterian assemblages in France, he concluded that "none of these new
facts spoke for themselves... none of the correlations carried sufficient information
to tell us why they were that way" (Binford 1983). On the return voyage from that
fieldwork he framed the problem in the terms that would organize his subsequent career:
"The archaeological record is static... what we are interested in about the past is
dynamics... how can we make inferences from statics to dynamics?" and "The
archaeological record is contemporary" (Binford 1983). He concluded there was "no way
to develop archaeological methods of inference, except via the study of contemporary
living peoples, or by controlled experiments under laboratory conditions, or by doing
archaeology in situations whose dynamic component is historically documented" and named
"'Ethnoarchaeology', 'experimental archaeology', and 'historic sites archaeology'... the
only chances we have for the development and perfection of methods of inference dealing
with humanly-generated artifactual material" — comparing the expected payoff to how
experimental physics yielded radiocarbon dating (Binford 1983).

Binford grounded the epistemic warrant for this program explicitly in geology's
uniformitarian principle, the working assumption that processes observable operating in
the present also operated, in the same lawlike way, in the past: "We must therefore
proceed along the research path forged quite eloquently by our sister discipline
geology in its adoption of the proposition of uniformitarianism. Is the formation of
archaeological remains as a by-product of adaptive behavior a process that is operative
in the contemporary world? Can we directly experience this process...?" (Binford 1978).
He argued that because many species exploited archaeologically are still extant and
still hunted or processed today, faunal remains offer a domain where the crucial
behavior-to-statics linkage can be directly observed rather than merely guessed at
(Binford 1978). This borrowing frames actualistic studies as archaeology's answer to a
problem geology and paleontology had already confronted and named — the field did not
invent uniformitarian reasoning but imported it as the justification for a new empirical
program.

Binford also distinguished actualistic work from mere analogy or "empathy": generating
plausible stories about the past is only a first step, and without a method for
evaluating those stories against real alternative processes that could produce the same
static pattern, their accuracy cannot be assessed (Binford 1983). This positions
actualistic studies as the disciplined alternative to unsystematic ethnographic analogy
— see `ethnographic-analogy` — precisely because the actualistic case supplies a
controlled or at least fully observed instance of the cause-effect linkage being
proposed, rather than an impressionistic resemblance.

## Operationalizations

An actualistic study builds a bridge from an observable present-day cause to its
material trace, then applies that bridge to the archaeological record. Binford's
Nunamiut fieldwork (1969–1973) supplied the paradigm case: he and his crew conducted
timed marrow-cracking experiments on named sheep and caribou skeletal elements
(mean processing time, cavity volume, recovery efficiency in ml/min), paired with a
colleague's chemical assay of bone-grease (oleic-acid) content, to construct a
quantitative Marrow Index scaling each bone's expected yield (Binford 1978). He then
checked that index against Nunamiut informants' actual butchering and processing
choices, finding a strong correlation (r = .82) between the anatomically-derived index
and the percentage of a given part actually selected for marrow processing, and used
divergences (a "thresholding phenomenon" in which very-low-yield bones such as
phalanges, mandible, scapula, and pelvis were ignored even though the index assigned
them nonzero value) as evidence of a labor-cost floor overriding pure caloric
maximizing (Binford 1978). Binford framed this whole construction — controlled
experimental measurement of a present-day process, checked against present-day human
behavior — as "a template instance of using directly observed present-day process to
calibrate interpretation," the actualistic logic underlying his broader ethnoarchaeological
and middle-range program (Binford 1978).

A second operationalization strategy is the "culture-free" control case: Binford cited
C. K. Brain's studies of Hottentot-village goat remains (a fully documented, non-trade
slaughter context) as isolating purely mechanical and natural causes of skeletal-part
underrepresentation — bone density and epiphyseal fusion timing — independent of any
cultural explanation, and Voorhies's (1969) pre-human Pliocene Nebraska bone deposit as
an even cleaner control, since any part-frequency skew there could not be attributed to
human behavior at all and so isolates the contribution of carnivore scavenging, natural
sorting, and differential bone strength (Binford 1978). The logic in both cases is to
first establish, actualistically, what a *non-cultural* process produces, so that a
cultural explanation is only invoked for the residual pattern that mechanical and
natural causes cannot account for — Binford described this residual-isolation tactic
as proceeding "from known to unknown conditions by isolating the residues" (Binford
1983).

## Applications

Beyond the marrow/grease/meat utility indices, Binford assembled comparative
actualistic faunal datasets — 36 wolf-kill sites (24 reinventoried), wolf dens, Andrew
Hill's lion and hyena open-kill sites in Uganda and southern Ethiopia, and Richard
Klein's excavated hyena den in South Africa — to characterize how non-human predators
and scavengers structure bone assemblages, as a baseline against which candidate
early-hominid sites could be evaluated (Binford 1983). He also cited experimental
archaeology more broadly (house-burning-and-erosion experiments, craft-skill
re-creation, pioneered heavily in Britain), historical documents used as experimental
control, and modern comparative studies of urban refuse disposal (a Tucson, Arizona
project tracking garbage collection) as parallel present-serving-the-past research
modes (Binford 1983). Binford treated these actualistic results explicitly as building
blocks of `middle-range-theory-archaeology` — instruments, analogous to a thermometer,
for giving meaning to archaeological observations, and he was careful to mark the
substantive behavioral claims they generate (e.g., a general economy of maximizing
strategies) as provisional and possibly not universal, distinct from the instrument
itself (Binford 1978).

## Critiques and Limitations

The program's warrant rests on the uniformitarian assumption that present-day
cause-to-trace relationships hold, without independent verification, for the past
case under interpretation — a premise Binford imported from geology rather than
demonstrated archaeologically, and one that is stronger for slow, physically
determined processes (bone density, mechanical breakage) than for culturally variable
behavior. Binford's own data illustrate the limitation: Nunamiut marrow-processing
decisions shifted by subsistence-security state (labor-cost minimization under
abundance versus use-everything under scarcity) and even generationally — three of
five informants under 50 no longer recognized low-yield bones (pelvis, scapula,
calcaneus) as marrow-bearing at all, while informants over 70 recalled processing them
in leaner years — meaning a single actualistic observation can capture only one point
in a range of culturally and historically contingent behavior, not a fixed law
transferable without qualification to other times and places (Binford 1978). The
representativeness of any one modern case for a given past context is therefore an
argument to be made, not an assumption to be smuggled in — the same caution the wiki's
Evidence-Class Framework applies to ethnographic analogy generally.
