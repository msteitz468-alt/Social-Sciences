# Replacement Fund

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 1), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] distinguishes the **replacement fund** from mere caloric minimum. Beyond the physiological intake needed to balance energy expenditure (~2000–3000 calories/person/day in the figures he cites), a cultivator must replace the instruments and materials of production and household maintenance — seed corn, tools, draft animals, buildings, clothing, etc. Failure of the replacement fund threatens not only biological existence but the capacity to farm again.

Wolf stresses thinking of replacement not only as physical stock but as a **claim on the product** that must be met before other claims (ceremonial, rent, accumulation).

## Semantic History

Part of Wolf's four-category household budget scheme for peasantry (caloric minimum, replacement, ceremonial, rent). Resonates with classical political economy's distinction between necessary reproduction and surplus, and with Chayanov's peasant farm accounting, but coined as explicit fund vocabulary for anthropological comparison in 1966.

## Operationalizations

- Seed and breeding stock reserved from harvest; tool and animal replacement; housing and storage repair.
- **Cultural, not merely technical:** once pottery cups, storage bins, or work animals enter cultural expectations, they become commitments the cultivator must meet; drought or locusts endangering the replacement fund threaten biological existence *and* capacity to meet cultural necessities.
- **Indirect replacement via exchange:** grain grown to obtain blankets still charges the replacement fund; under asymmetrical market ratios, "a considerable share of the peasant's replacement fund may become somebody else's fund of profit."
- Competes with [[ceremonial-fund]] and [[fund-of-rent]] under scarcity — [[peasant-dilemma]].

## Applications / Exemplars (Wolf 1966)

- **Mecklenburg 14th–15th c.** (via Abel): 40-acre farm produced 10,200 lb grain; 3,400 seed + 2,800 horse feed = more than half committed in advance to instruments of production — not absolute surplus.
- **Kuikuru (Amazon, via Carneiro):** caloric minimum + replacement met in ~3.5 hours/day; no technical or social reason to work more — illustrates that potential surplus does not automatically generate surplus for rulers.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundaries between replacement and ceremonial or rent outlays can blur in practice (e.g., feast that also redistributes seed labor).
- Quantitative calibration left to case studies; primer is categorical.
