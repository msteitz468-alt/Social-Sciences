# Opportunity Hoarding

## Definition as Coined

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (*[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]*, 1998): **opportunity hoarding** operates when members of a categorically bounded network acquire access to a resource that is valuable, renewable, subject to monopoly, supportive of network activities, and enhanced by the network's modus operandi. Second **installing** mechanism of durable inequality alongside [[exploitation]].

Four elements (Tilly Ch. 5, attributed): (1) a distinctive network; (2) valuable, renewable, monopolizable, network-supportive resources enhanced by network practice; (3) sequestering of those resources by network members; (4) beliefs and practices that sustain network control. Monopolizing work depends on monitoring/sanctioning against defection, many interdependent triads, or both.

Can produce secure niche advantage **without** major exploitation of outsiders (ethnic trade monopolies hiring co-ethnics; no surplus extracted from excluded outsiders' effort).

## Semantic History

Related to Weberian social closure and ethnic-enclave / niche literatures (Waldinger, Model, Anderson — used as illustrations). Tilly's coinage standardizes the mechanism for stratification theory; later widely used in sociology of inequality and education (reception beyond this source not fully ingested). Distinct from [[exploitation]]: hoarders need not command outsiders' labor.

## Operationalizations

Network closure around jobs, licenses, land, or trade niches; chain-migration labor monopolies; professional licensing as categorical gatekeeping; declining returns as niches saturate (Model: expanding niches advantage latecomers less; fixed/declining niches worse; advantages need similarly qualified excluded populations).

## Applications

- **Chain migration niches:** Roccasecca–Mamaroneck Italians in landscape gardening (near-monopoly; little non-Italian labor) vs Lyon factory cousins from same village — hoarding without major exploitation (Tilly's natural experiment)
- **Complementary niches:** Johnstown East European Jews (retail) vs Gentiles (manufacturing) under American capital dominance (Morawska via Tilly)
- **Professions:** state-backed licensing as quintessential hoarding (French veterinarians; NY cosmetology vs braiders)
- **Firm-internal:** employee-referral hiring (Juárez maquiladoras); soft-skills screening that maps exterior categories onto jobs (Moss–Tilly)
- **Politics:** state-backed property rights, professional licenses, veterans' niches; historical sale of French privileges

## Critiques and Limitations

Normative ambiguity (credentialing vs pernicious exclusion); hard to distinguish efficient trust networks from exclusion; Model qualifications on niche dynamics; risks to hoarders (invasion, saturation, state withdrawal of licenses — Tilly notes).

## From *Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties* Ch. 10 (2005)

*(Attributed — Tilly.)* Restates opportunity hoarding’s four elements within the categorical-inequality kit; develops [[chain-migration]] as network-mediated path into niches; immigrant-niche synthesis (Waldinger/Portes); policy close — intervene in destructive hoarding without stamping out all network migration.
