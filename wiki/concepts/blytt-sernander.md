# Blytt–Sernander Scheme

The Blytt–Sernander scheme is the classic climatostratigraphic subdivision of the European [[holocene|Holocene]], documented in [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]], Technical Box V). It names a sequence of postglacial climatic periods still used as shorthand in European palaeoecology.

## Definition as Coined

The Norwegian botanist Axel Blytt (1876) inferred alternating wetter and drier climates from the layering of raised-bog peat — dark, humified "recurrence surfaces" marking drier phases, fresh *Sphagnum* peat marking wetter ones. The Swedish botanist Rutger Sernander developed and named the periods. The resulting sequence is:

| Period | Approx. character |
|---|---|
| Preboreal | cool, early postglacial |
| Boreal | warm, dry |
| Atlantic | warm, wet — the Holocene "climatic optimum" |
| Subboreal | warm, drier |
| Subatlantic | cooler, wetter (to present) |

## Semantic History

Originally a *climatic* scheme, it was co-opted as a *chronological/pollen-zone* framework when Lennart von Post's [[pollen-analysis|pollen analysis]] provided a way to correlate bog and lake stratigraphies. The boundaries were later given approximate radiocarbon ages. Roberts (1998) notes the scheme's slippage between three distinct meanings — climatic phases, pollen assemblage zones, and chronozones — and the resulting confusion, since the "boundaries" are diachronous across Europe.

## Operationalizations

Periods are identified from peat humification (Blytt–Sernander's original signal), from regional pollen assemblage zones (Godwin's British zones I–VIII), and, in modern practice, from radiocarbon-dated chronozones. These do not everywhere coincide.

## Applications

The terms remain a convenient shorthand for phases of [[holocene-climate-change|Holocene climate]] and vegetation in Europe — e.g. the Atlantic-period maximum of temperate forest, the Subboreal elm decline, and the Subatlantic expansion of [[deforestation|cleared]] and blanket-mire landscapes.

## Critiques and Limitations

Roberts (1998, attributed) treats the scheme as obsolete as a *climatic* framework: its boundaries are time-transgressive, the peat-humification signal reflects local bog hydrology as much as regional climate, and modern high-resolution records show Holocene climate variability the five-fold scheme cannot capture. It survives as descriptive terminology, not as a climatic model.
