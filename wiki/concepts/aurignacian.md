# Aurignacian

Early **Upper Palaeolithic** industry complex of Europe (with Near Eastern extensions), treated by Stringer & Gamble (1993) as firmly associated with **[[anatomically-modern-humans|Moderns]]** and as the technological–symbolic package of incoming populations rather than local Neanderthal evolution (*[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]* chs. 8–9, attributed).

## Definition as Coined

Named after Aurignac (France). As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), the first "true" Upper Palaeolithic industry — "in the sense that it was made by anatomically modern humans and has all the technological features and artistic additions normally expected of the Moderns" — is widely recognized as the Aurignacian. Diagnostic content they emphasize: long slender **blade** tools; distinctive types (strangled blades, nosed scrapers, Dufour bladelets); **bone and ivory projectile points** rare or absent in earlier Middle Palaeolithic and worldwide pre-Upper Palaeolithic (PUP); jewellery, ornaments, and art; well-organized campsites (attributed, S&G 1993).

## Semantic History

### Modern package vs Neanderthal Mousterian (as of S&G 1993)

S&G use Aurignacian as the archaeological proxy for Cro-Magnon colonization of Europe. They contrast it with the European [[mousterian|Mousterian]] of the Ancients and with "transitional" industries ([[chatelperronian]], Uluzzian, Szeletian) that they read as **imitation** of, or response to, incoming Modern technology rather than independent invention of the Upper Palaeolithic package. One chronological argument: unlike African/Levantine PUP, all European transitional industries are either contemporary with or postdate the earliest Aurignacian, which they say is firmly associated with anatomically modern people (attributed, 1993).

### Colonization chronology (as of S&G 1993)

Accepting Aurignacian–Cro-Magnon association, S&G reconstruct spread:

| Horizon (as of 1993) | Region / sites |
|---|---|
| Earliest >40 ka | Centre and east of Europe; Bacho Kiro (Bulgaria) blade-dominated assemblage radiocarbon >43 ka (Kozlowski) |
| Levantine early | Ksar Akil (Lebanon) possibly ~44 ka; Boker Tachtit transitional ~47 ka — pointing to **eastern origin** for European UP |
| Northern Iberia | Next-oldest European cluster; El Castillo 37.7–40 ka; l'Arbreda ~38.5 ka (Cabrera Valdés and others) |
| Southern Germany | By ~36 ka (e.g. Geissenklösterle) |
| SW France | Well established by ~34 ka (e.g. Abri Pataud); French Neanderthal **refuge** / peninsular Europe marginal vs Middle Eastern sequences |
| Alternating levels | Roc de Combe, Piage (Châtelperronian/Aurignacian); southern Spain (e.g. Zafarraya) also a late Neanderthal refuge |

Many of these absolute dates have been revised since 1993; treat as the book's chronological apparatus, not current consensus.

### Art and style

S&G treat the appearance of art with Aurignacian tools as highly significant: small sculptures at Stadel, Geissenklösterle, Vogelherd (southern Germany); Stadel lion-headed anthropomorph reconstructed by Joachim Hahn (>32 ka per their plate caption); SW France limestone blocks with signs interpreted as vulvae (La Ferrassie, Castanet, Blanchard); pierced deer/fox canines, shells, ivory beads; Randall White (after Marcel Otte) on complex ivory-bead manufacturing and SW France obtaining ivory from elsewhere (attributed, S&G 1993). Early UP styles more uniform than late MP regional projectiles: Aurignacian **split-base bone points** essentially alike across their European distribution (attributed, 1993).

### Contested associations

**Vindija**: some remains claimed from a level with an Aurignacian bone point — which would be the only Neanderthal–Aurignacian association — but S&G judge excavation uncertainties too great to guarantee the association (attributed, 1993). Skhul/Qafzeh–Cro-Magnon derivation via early Aurignacian (e.g. Bacho Kiro) is complicated if TL/ESR make Levantine moderns ~60 ka older than Cro-Magnons (attributed, 1993).

## Operationalizations

- Typological markers: blade/bladelet technology; strangled blades, nosed scrapers, Dufour bladelets; split-base and other bone/ivory points.
- Symbolic package: personal ornaments, portable art, structured campsites.
- Chronological proxy for Modern entry into Europe in replacement models (S&G 1993 position).
- Long-distance raw materials: e.g. Bacho Kiro Aurignacian — 53% of flint for blades imported from >120 km (Kozlowski via S&G); chocolate flint transport up to ~400 km in early UP contexts they cite (attributed, 1993).

## Applications

- Archaeological side of the [[modern-human-origins-debate]] and [[behavioral-modernity]]: Aurignacian as European expression of the Modern behavioural package.
- Stratigraphic foil for late [[mousterian]] and [[chatelperronian]] in western Europe.
- Source for dating last Neanderthals relative to Modern arrival (imitation timing argument).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Whether earliest Aurignacian could be Neanderthal-made at some sites (Vindija-type claims) remains contested; S&G reject secure association as of 1993.
- Continuity readings (e.g. Clark & Lindly "dimmer-switch" build-up of symbolism) deny that Aurignacian art is a sharp switch; S&G reject that model (POSITIONS — see [[behavioral-modernity]]).
- Dates and site associations heavily revised post-1993 (AMS, Bayesian modelling, re-excavation).
- Blade technology alone is not a Modern marker (PUP, Howiesons Poort, Amudian) — S&G themselves use art, bone points, campsites, and colonization as stronger discriminators.

## Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein treats the Aurignacian as "the earliest undeniable Upper Paleolithic culture complex,"
spread across southeastern, central, and western Europe by ~40 ka and associated with intrusive
anatomically modern humans — the archaeological signature of the [[upper-paleolithic-revolution]]
(blades, split-base bone points, ornaments, art). He lists the SW-France succession Aurignacian
(~40–28 ka) → Gravettian/Perigordian (~28–21 ka) → Solutrean (~21–16.5 ka) → Magdalenian
(~16.5–11 ka). Its appearance beside, and interstratification with, the Neanderthal-made
[[chatelperronian]] frames Klein's replacement argument (see [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).
