# Contentious Politics

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly, *[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* 2001.)* Umbrella concept for **collective political struggle** studied as a single analytic field rather than as separate literatures on social movements, revolutions, nationalism, democratization, industrial conflict, and war.

## Definition as Coined

> "By contentious politics we mean: episodic, public, collective interaction among makers of claims and their objects when (a) at least one government is a claimant, an object of claims, or a party to the claims and (b) the claims would, if realized, affect the interests of at least one of the claimants."

**Episodic** excludes regularly scheduled votes, elections, and associational meetings unless they become springboards for contention. **Public** excludes claim-making wholly inside well-bounded organizations (though the book concentrates on struggles with political ramifications). Most politics, the authors stress, is *not* contentious — ceremony, consultation, bureaucracy, registration, voting, taxation without public struggle.

**Contained** contention: all parties previously established as constituted actors using well-established means. **Transgressive** contention: at least some parties newly self-identified and/or action innovative (unprecedented or forbidden in the regime). Nixon impeachment inquiry and Mau Mau both fall under the definition; they differ as contained vs transgressive poles, not as different sciences.

## Semantic History

Builds on Tillyan [[collective-action]] and [[repertoire-of-contention]]; McAdam political process; Tarrow cycles. DOC (2001) systematizes the umbrella and the mechanism–process program ([[contentious-politics-theory]]). Later [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] reuses “[[episode-of-contention]]” inside field ontology. Tilly *IBST* (2005) continues repertoires, identities, and democratization without abandoning the umbrella.

## Operationalizations

- Episode catalogs and paired comparison across regime space (state capacity × [[protected-consultation|protected consultation]]).
- Mechanism inventories (brokerage, certification, attribution of threat/opportunity, etc.) rather than necessary-and-sufficient condition lists for “revolution” or “the social movement.”

## Applications

DOC’s comparative episodes (Mau Mau; Philippines Yellow; Hindu–Muslim; South Africa; U.S. antislavery; Spain; Nicaragua; China 1989; Italian unification; Soviet collapse; Switzerland; Mexico) — illustrations of mechanisms, not complete case monographs. See [[contentious-politics-theory]] and Theory Hub.

## Critiques and Limitations

State-centered definition underweights purely nonstate targets; boundary with routine politics is researcher-imposed; mechanism lists risk redescription; case selection still modern-state heavy (authors partly acknowledge scope challenges in Ch. 10).
