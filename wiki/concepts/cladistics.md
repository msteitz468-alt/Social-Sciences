## Definition as Coined

Cladistics, following Willi Hennig, is a perspective on biological classification that
assigns different taxonomic weight to different homologous characters depending on
their evolutionary status. It distinguishes primitive (plesiomorphic, or when widely
shared, symplesiomorphic) characters from derived (apomorphic) characters.
[[klein-human-career-1999]] describes cladistics as "the increasingly popular
perspective on classification" built on this distinction.

## Semantic History

- **Plesiomorphies** (primitive/generalized characters) arose early in a group's
  history, are widespread across its members, and are therefore not useful for
  subdividing the group — Klein's example is the presence of five digits in mammals,
  a primitive feature shared so broadly it carries no information about finer
  relationships within Mammalia.
- **Apomorphies** (derived/advanced characters) arose relatively late and differ
  among members of a group, making them useful for inferring genealogical
  relationships. These are further divided into **synapomorphies** (shared derived
  characters, present in two or more taxa and indicating shared ancestry more recent
  than the group as a whole) and **autapomorphies** (unique derived characters/
  novelties confined to a single taxon).
- A **cladogram** organizes taxa by the number of shared derived features they hold in
  common. Klein stresses that a cladogram resembles a phylogeny in form but is not
  one: cladograms place all taxa at the tips of branches, ignore absolute time, and do
  not assert ancestor-descendant relationships — they represent perceived degrees of
  derived similarity, not lineage history as such.

## Operationalizations

Cladists, per Klein (attributed), treat all derived features as equally important
regardless of their functional or adaptive significance, which "can produce
classifications that radically revise traditional ones." Klein's example: biomolecular
data implying that chimpanzees, gorillas, and humans share a last common ancestor too
recent to justify keeping humans in a separate family (Hominidae) from the great apes
(traditionally Pongidae).

This produces a live methodological dispute (position) between strict cladists and a
traditionalist/gradistic counter-position, which argues that humans warrant a distinct
family because their unique derived features — habitual bipedalism and especially the
large, complex brain (see [[encephalization]]) — constitute a novel "grade" of
adaptation, even though rejecting this grade distinction is, on strictly cladistic
grounds, "less arbitrary." Klein notes that cladistic procedure does not exclude
recognizing such grades, but excludes them from determining classification level,
"because grades need not bear on the degree of evolutionary relationship" that
classification is supposed to track.

## Applications

Cladistic method works best, per Klein, on discrete (present/absent) characters, and
is more equivocal when applied to characters that vary by degree of expression — which
is the typical situation for closely related taxa within genus Homo. It is applied
throughout the hominin fossil record to organize taxa such as [[australopithecus]],
[[homo-habilis]], [[homo-erectus]], [[homo-heidelbergensis]], and [[neanderthals]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Cladistic analysis can be confounded in two documented ways: (1) shared derived
features may actually be analogies or parallelisms (independently evolved) mistaken
for true homologies; and (2) character reversals can mimic a false phylogenetic
signal — Klein's example is skull bone thickness in hominids, which changed from
relatively thin in the earliest hominids to relatively thick in [[homo-erectus]] and
early archaic *H. sapiens* ([[homo-heidelbergensis]]), then back to thin in later
*H. sapiens*, a
non-monotonic pattern that a naive character count could misread.
