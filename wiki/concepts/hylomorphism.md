# Hylomorphism

## Definition as Coined

**Hylomorphism** (Greek *hyle*, matter + *morphe*, form) is the Aristotelian doctrine
that a thing is a compound of matter and form. In the wiki it appears chiefly as the
target of [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s critique in *[[ingold-making-2013|Making]]*
(2013): the **hylomorphic model of making** — the assumption that to make something is
to impose a preconceived form, held in the mind of the maker, upon inert, passive
matter. Ingold argues this model, inherited from Aristotle and entrenched in Western
thought, systematically misrepresents what actually happens when people make things
(attributed).

## Semantic History

Ingold's critique explicitly extends **Gilbert Simondon's** analysis of brick-making
and **Deleuze & Guattari's** account of metallurgy (following the flow of metal), both
of which object that hylomorphism takes the standpoint of the one who *commands* the
making from outside and ignores the practitioner's engagement with the variable, active
properties of materials. He traces the model back to Aristotle and forward into
twentieth-century material-culture theory's talk of "materiality" (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — a model of the making process. Ingold's alternative is
making as **morphogenesis**: form is not imposed but **grows**, emerging from the
maker's [[correspondence]] with the forces and flows of materials (the "textility of
making"). The maker "joins with and follows" materials rather than commanding them.

## Applications

- **The handaxe** (Ingold's central case): against the "mental template" model of stone
  tools (form as a preconceived image realized in stone), Ingold argues the biface's
  symmetry emerges from the **knapper's skilled correspondence with the fracturing
  stone** — form as the outcome of a process, not its precondition. He stages this
  against both the template camp (Gowlett, Pelegrin) and the "leftover-core" hypothesis
  (Davidson & Noble), resolving it as emergent form.
- **The house / building** (Ch. 4): a building is never finished but **grows** and is
  continually maintained — against the design-first "building perspective."
- **Material vs. materiality**: Ingold rejects the abstraction "materiality" (the
  supposed general property of being material) in favour of attending to actual
  **materials** as "substances-in-becoming" — flowing, active, alchemical.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ingold's anti-hylomorphic account has itself been questioned — critics ask whether it
underplays the real role of design, planning, and imposed form in much modern
production, and whether "growth" can be generalized from craft to industrial making.
