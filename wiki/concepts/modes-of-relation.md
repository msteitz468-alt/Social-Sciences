# Modes of Relation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]* (Part V, "An Ecology of Relations"), [[descola-philippe|Descola]] distinguishes **modes of relation** (practical schemas of attachment between beings) from [[modes-of-identification]] (ontological assignment of interiority/physicality). He proposes a limited set of elementary relational schemas — **giving, taking, exchanging, producing, protecting, transmitting** — that organize how humans and nonhumans act on one another.

Groupings (as developed in Ch.13):

- **Reversible / potentially symmetrical:** exchange; gift (give); predation/taking
- **Asymmetric / generative or hierarchical:** production; protection; transmission

Identification and relation are **analytically independent**: the same animist identification can host predation (Jivaro/Achuar), balanced exchange (Tukanoan), or sharing/gift (Campa; northern Algonquin) as dominant moral horizons.

## Semantic History

Engages Mauss ([[gift-exchange]]), Lévi-Strauss ([[alliance-theory|exchange of women]]), Testart's critiques of the gift, Sahlins on reciprocity typology, Marx on production, Bird-David on sharing, Ingold on trust and dwelling. Descola argues production is **not** a universal human–world relation (contra naturalist common sense and some Marxist anthropologies); many animist regimes do not treat nonhumans as products of human labor.

## Operationalizations

Compatibility matrix (Ch.15 / program architecture): not every relation thrives under every ontology — e.g. gift and predation highly developed under animism; protection under pastoral and analogist hierarchies; production under naturalism; transmission under totemic actualization of Dreaming classes. Collectives are fields of dominant relational schemas with subordinate niches.

## Applications

Amazonian predation vs exchange vs sharing triad within one animism; pastoral protection of herds and ancestors; naturalist production of nature as resource; totemic transmission of Dreaming identities; analogist hierarchical protection and sacrifice.

## Critiques and Limitations

Six-fold set is heuristic; ethnographic systems hybridize. Risk of reading moral "ethos" as total system. Descola's independence of identification and relation is a strong program claim — still debated in ontological-turn literature.
