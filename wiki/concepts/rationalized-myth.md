# Rationalized Myth

## Definition as Coined

[[meyer-john-w]] and [[rowan-brian]] introduced rationalized myths in "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony" (1977; ch. 2 of [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]]), the paper D&P's introduction credits with the "birth date" of the sociological new institutionalism. Their opening claim: "in modern societies, formal organizational structures arise in highly institutionalized contexts," and organizations that incorporate "the practices and procedures defined by prevailing rationalized concepts of organizational work and institutionalized in society... increase their legitimacy and their survival prospects, independent of the immediate efficacy of the acquired practices and procedures." Such "institutionalized products, services, techniques, policies, and programs function as powerful myths, and many organizations adopt them ceremonially."

The canonical definition, given against a Weberian alternative account of formal structure, holds that these myths have "two key properties. First, they are rationalized and impersonal prescriptions that identify various social purposes as technical ones and specify in a rulelike way the appropriate means to pursue these technical purposes rationally. Second, they are highly institutionalized and thus in some measure beyond the discretion of any individual participant or organization." Meyer and Rowan located the underlying category of "institutionalized rules" more generally as "classifications built into society as reciprocated typifications or interpretations" (following Berger and Luckmann 1967), which "may be simply taken for granted or may be supported by public opinion or the force of law."

## Semantic History

Meyer and Rowan posed rationalized myths against the prevailing Weberian account, in which bureaucracies emerge to coordinate boundary-spanning technical exchanges as markets and states expand. They argued the classic "informal organization" literature (Dalton 1959; Homans 1950) had already shown a "great gap between the formal and the informal organization" and that formal structures are "often loosely coupled" from actual work — evidence the coordination account could not explain. Their alternative: formal structure is adopted for its legitimacy, not (only) its coordinating function, because "norms of rationality... exist in much more specific and powerful ways in the rules, understandings, and meanings attached to institutionalized social structures" than as diffuse general values.

The concept subsequently differentiated from a single generalized "rationality" into a plurality of sources: myths were identified as arising from multiple, sometimes-competing origins — public opinion, educational systems, laws, courts, professions, ideologies, technologies, regulatory structures, awards and prizes, and certification/accreditation bodies — a differentiation elaborated by [[scott-w-richard]] and [[meyer-john-w]]'s later distinction between "technical" and "institutional" environments (see [[societal-sector]]).

Three generative processes were proposed for how rationalized myths arise: elaboration of complex relational networks (universalism, contracts, expertise diffusing across occupations and becoming codified into "mythlike form" via law, education, and credentialing); the degree of collective organization of the environment (rational-legal state-building giving official legitimacy via legislative, judicial, and administrative authority); and the leadership efforts of powerful organizations that actively shape institutional environments to their own advantage (Meyer and Rowan's example: automobile producers shaping road, fuel, liability, and public-opinion standards). This last point led them explicitly to reject a "domesticated organizations" (Carlson 1962) or pure goal-displacement (Selznick 1949) reading of organization–environment relations as "misleading."

## Operationalizations

Meyer and Rowan illustrated rationalized myths with institutionalized categories functioning as myths independent of proven efficacy: professions (controlled via licensing, certification, and schooling, not work inspection); organizational programs (business functions like sales, accounting; university functions like instruction, research; hospital functions like surgery, described as "prefabricated formulas"); and technologies (production, accounting, personnel-selection, and data-processing procedures that establish an organization as "appropriate, rational, and modern" apart from actual efficiency). Cited historical examples: the professionalization of personnel work driving the spread of personnel departments; the professionalization of R&D creating pressure for dedicated R&D units; and the rationalization of prostitution along medical lines producing "sex-therapy clinics, massage parlors."

The chapter's formal propositions state the mechanism directly: "As rationalized institutional rules arise in given domains of work activity, formal organizations form and expand by incorporating these rules as structural elements" (Proposition 1); "the more modernized the society, the more extended the rationalized institutional structure" (Proposition 2); and "organizations that incorporate societally legitimated rationalized elements in their formal structures maximize their legitimacy and increase their resources and survival capabilities" (Proposition 3) — this last proposition decouples legitimacy and survival from technical efficiency.

## Applications

Meyer and Rowan proposed two research theses (of a planned three, the third cut off in the source's OCR at the chapter's close): environments and domains with more institutionalized rational myths generate more formal organization even holding economic and technical development constant, testable through diffusion studies of specific institutions (personnel professions, R&D departments); and organizations incorporating institutionalized myths are "more legitimate, successful, and likely to survive," testable through comparative studies of institutionalized practices (e.g., whether banks lend more readily to firms whose plans include econometric projections in societies where such projections are themselves institutionalized).

## Critiques and Limitations

The paper's own DiMaggio and Powell (introduction, this volume) revisited the assumption that ceremonial conformity to rationalized myths is "pure cost from the point of view of efficiency," arguing this claim is "questionable": adoption should be separated analytically from its effects, institutionally driven choices may carry no net efficiency cost, and legitimacy itself can be income-producing (their example: a school district's ceremonial spending may be efficient if it secures bond-issue approval). They concluded that "the key thrust of institutional analysis is neither to expose the inefficiency of organizational practices nor to celebrate the triumph of institutional arrangements," being skeptical instead of any assumption that surviving practices must be efficient, since "rates of environmental change frequently outpace rates of organizational adaptation." [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) separately warned that the rationalized-myth/loose-coupling framework had been misread as portraying institutionalized organizations as "relatively passive, inefficient manipulators of symbols rather than substance."
