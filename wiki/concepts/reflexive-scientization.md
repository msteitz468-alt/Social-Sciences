# Reflexive Scientization

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s term (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Ch. 7 "Science beyond Truth and Enlightenment?") for the phase in which the sciences turn their own methodological skepticism upon their own foundations, products, and unintended consequences — the scientific face of [[reflexive-modernization]].

## Definition as Coined

Beck distinguished two phases:

- **Primary (simple) scientization** — science applied to a "given" world of nature, people, and society, while "its own claims to knowledge and enlightenment are still spared from the application of scientific skepticism to themselves." This "bisection of scientific rationality" — methodical doubt turned outward but not on science's own foundations or applications — sustained the "unbroken faith in science and progress" of industrial modernity into the first half of the 20th century.
- **Reflexive scientization** — a "complete" scientization in which the sciences confront "their own products, defects, and secondary problems"; both their claim to truth and their claim to enlightenment are "demystified." "Demystification spreads to the demystifier and in so doing changes the conditions of demystification" (Beck 1986: 157).

## Semantic History

Beck traced the reflexive turn through the 20th-century sociology, history, and philosophy of science: Mannheim's cognitive sociology, Popper's fallibilism, Kuhn's historical critique, Feyerabend's "anything goes." His distinctive claim is causal and epochal: "**It is not their failure but their success that has dethroned the sciences**" — the more thoroughly science succeeded, the more its own validity claims were relativized.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. Beck worked it out through the **demonopolization of scientific truth-claims** (science "becomes more and more necessary, but at the same time less and less sufficient for the socially binding definition of truth"); the **"feudalization" of cognitive practice** (target groups grow more dependent on scientific argument in general but more independent of any particular finding, so selection criteria that "escape scientific scrutiny" — sponsor interests, political acceptability — gain decisive weight); and the resulting "battle of experts and counter-experts." He also proposed a **calculability–estimability** thesis: the *incalculability* of side effects and their *estimability* (their being knowable-in-principle, robbed of latency) grow together.

## Applications

Grounds Beck's account of the [[relations-of-definition|politics of knowledge]] and of [[subpolitics]] (contested expertise as a site of politics outside the formal system), and his case study of the environmental movement as the "scientization of protest against science" (Carson's *Silent Spring*, Commoner). It closes with a call for science to "specialize in the context" and to institutionalize its own self-criticism.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with Beck's program generally, the emancipatory reading of reflexivity is contested (Lash and Wynne on the ambivalence and social constraint of actual lay/scientific reflexivity). Do **not** conflate with [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s [[reflexive-sociology|reflexive sociology]], a distinct methodological program about the researcher's self-objectivation.
