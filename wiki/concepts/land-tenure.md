# Land Tenure (Mesopotamia)

**Land tenure** here names the general analytical problem — how agricultural land is held, allotted, worked, transferred, and inherited within a society — exemplified through [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate's]] (1994) reconstruction of early Mesopotamian (c. 3000–1500 BCE) practice from the cuneiform documentary record. Mesopotamia is used here as a worked case for a category that applies cross-culturally; the specifics below are Postgate's synthesis of the Mesopotamian evidence, not a general theory of tenure.

## Definition as Coined

Postgate frames Mesopotamian land tenure as a documentary "palimpsest": *(attributed)* written sources disproportionately record institutional and legal transactions (large-institution and legal matters are over-documented, while private and unwritten practice is under-documented); the ideal, legally stated forms of tenure and actual practice diverge, and old tenure categories persist formally in the record even as the real underlying proportions of land under each shift over time. Postgate states that detecting real structural change in land tenure would require quantitative data that "research has not yet progressed" to supply (as of [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]]).

Two broad tenure classes recur through the corpus: **institutional land** (held by temple or, later, palace, distributed to dependents in various forms) and **private/household land** (held by individuals or kin-groups, in principle freely disposable, in practice often restricted).

## Semantic History

**Deimel's three-way schema.** The canonical model of institutional landholding derives from Father Anton Deimel's early-twentieth-century analysis of the Bau (Baba) Temple archive at Girsu ([[lagash]]), endorsed by Postgate only with caveats. Deimel divided Sumerian temple land into three categories: (1) **"property of the en"** ("en-land") — cultivated directly by temple staff for the temple's own account; (2) **subsistence or prebend fields** — allocated to temple staff as part of their remuneration; (3) **surplus land rented out** to private individuals for a crop-share or fixed payment. At Girsu, Deimel estimated the total institutional land involved at 200–300 sq km at the end of the Pre-Sargonic period; Postgate notes the temples of Lagash are "no longer thought" to have controlled the entire territory, a revision of Deimel's stronger claim (as of Postgate 1994). This three-way division persisted roughly three centuries into the Ur III period at Girsu, with the institution's own land renamed "ox-fields" while prebend and rented land kept their names; the prebend category is attested as early as Early Dynastic III at Abu Salabikh (holdings assigned to gods, the *ensi*, and officials including scribes) and in archaic Early Dynastic I texts from Ur. In the Old Babylonian period, prebend land was held from palaces as well as temples.

**Prebend land.** Prebend land (Akkadian *isqum*, "lot") could not, in principle, be sold or automatically inherited, being tied to office-holding rather than person; by Ur III times it could nonetheless be leased to a third party, on terms indistinguishable from private-property leases (well attested also in the Old Babylonian period). In practice, some priestly-office prebends — and the land attached to them — came to be inherited father-to-son.

**Ilkum land.** A distinct category, **ilkum land**, was held from the state in exchange for a "temporary service" obligation (civilian and/or military), and Postgate treats it as likely a secularized descendant of the prebend system. Ilkum is attested from Ur III precursors through Hammurapi's Code and royal correspondence. Unlike prebend land, ilkum rights were inheritable by sons obligated for the corresponding service, and ilkum fields could likewise be rented out; Hammurapi's correspondence with Larsa governors documents active administration of, and disputes over, such land assignments. Under Ur III, a parallel but distinct arrangement existed in eastern territories, where soldiers received allotted plots in return for paying a "rental of the territory" (*gun mada*).

Hammurapi's Code permits certain office-holders (priestesses, merchants) to sell their ilkum plots (§§36–37), which Postgate reads as implying that illicit sale of such plots — forbidden to others — had likely already been occurring (as of Postgate 1994). Postgate's own view, stated explicitly as "In my view," is that ilkum liability applied specifically to holders of state-land allotments tied to a service duty, not to families holding property under traditional tenure — an interpretive, attributed claim rather than an established consensus.

## Operationalizations

**The private-land-sale question — a live position, not a settled fact.** Whether private land ownership (as opposed to institutional/prebend/ilkum land) existed at scale, and whether it could be freely sold, is disputed. It is "no longer thought" there was no private land at all in Pre-Sargonic Lagash, but the near-total absence of field-sale documents from the Ur III period has led some scholars to argue against private field ownership existing in that period. Postgate rejects the strong "nationalization" reading as improbable, favoring instead an **embargo-on-sale** reading (attributed to unnamed "others," and endorsed by Postgate): land ownership was private, but land *sale* specifically was restrained by social convention, not law — noting that land sales are also rare at Ur and Larsa in the Isin–Larsa period. Postgate flags evidentiary bias as a confound: only Nippur has a substantial Ur III private-document corpus, and Old Babylonian public-land documentation outside Hammurapi's Larsa correspondence is sparse (as of Postgate 1994).

This tracks a longer historiographic dispute recorded on [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]]: Diakonoff argued land sale was apparently prohibited throughout Ur III and the Larsa kingdom (citing the absence of surviving Ur III sale documents) and read the earliest sale documents — where relatives of a seller appear not just as witnesses but as recipients of payment, and sales are made jointly by groups of brothers — as evidence for strong joint/extended-family land ownership (e.g., the Obelisk of Maništušu, recording a purchase from a "brother-owner" group of up to seventeen "grandsons" of a common ancestor). Even where sale was restricted, workarounds existed: at Nuzi (later second millennium) land sale was technically forbidden but achieved via a legal fiction in which the purchaser was adopted by the seller. Land could also be held by kin/patrilineal descent groups rather than individuals — at Nippur no Old Babylonian document records a land sale outside the patrilineal descent group, and at Ur kinship groups jointly rented fields and date groves.

**Debt and land.** Old Babylonian *andurarum* edicts (royal debt-relief proclamations) could annul land sales resulting from indebtedness — the detailed mechanism belongs with the wider institutional debt-relief apparatus.

**Lease terms.** With land sale rare, lease was the primary mechanism correcting land/labor imbalances: a typical lease term ran 2–3 years, with rent paid as a crop share, a fixed absolute amount, a per-hectare rate, or pegged to the yield of "like neighbouring fields"; a supplementary payment (a kid, later silver) may have covered communal irrigation-maintenance costs. New or uncultivated land (e.g., date plantations) was leased on more favorable terms, running about three years — the Code of Hammurapi and the Code of Lipit-Ištar record a gardener growing an orchard for four years and dividing the produce with the landowner only in year five.

**Institutional advantage.** Postgate's synthesis (attributed): large institutions held systematic advantages over individual smallholders — economies of scale in irrigation and canal construction/maintenance, efficient long-furrow field layouts, superior equipment, greater resilience to environmental risk (able to diversify crops and locations, switch land in response to salinization or water shortage), and capital to exploit marginal or risky land and invest in reclamation. Postgate notes these advantages could in principle be replicated by close village or family collaboration, but "no village or extended family could compete with the massive irrigation schemes of the Ur III kings," nor cope with major environmental shifts such as the drying of the eastern Euphrates channel (as of Postgate 1994).

**Documentary form.** The earliest known legal/real-estate documents are Early Dynastic I "telegraphic" texts carved on stone recording land purchases (archaic *kudurru*, e.g. the "Blau Monuments"), though these lack secure archaeological context.

## Applications

Land tenure links directly to [[temple-economy]] (institutional landholding, the three-way Deimel division, prebend administration) and to the wider question of how [[redistribution]], private exchange, and household production coexisted in the same economy — Postgate's calibration against a pure-redistribution reading (see [[redistribution]]) rests in part on evidence that private and lease-based land tenure operated alongside institutional landholding rather than being absorbed by it. The [[sumerians]] and their successor Akkadian- and Amorite-period polities are the documentary source for all tenure categories described here.

## Critiques and Limitations

Every claim above is bounded by Postgate's own "palimpsest" caution: the documentary record over-represents institutional and legal transactions and under-represents private, unwritten practice, so apparent absences (e.g., of Ur III sale documents) are evidentially ambiguous between "the practice did not occur" and "the practice was not the kind of thing written down." The private-land-sale question is recorded here as a live, unresolved dispute — see [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]] for positions and holders — not adjudicated in wiki voice.
