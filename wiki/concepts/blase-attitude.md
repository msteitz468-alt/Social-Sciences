# The Blasé Attitude

## Definition as Coined

The **blasé attitude** (*Blasiertheit*) is [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]'s term for a modern psychological disposition in which "the feeling for value differences" is lost, so that the blasé person "experiences all things as being of an equally dull and grey hue." It appears in the 1889 "Psychology of Money" essay, is developed at length in *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* (1900, ch. 3), and receives its most famous statement in "The Metropolis and Mental Life" (1903).

It is the more complete of two "levelling" end-states of the [[money-economy|money economy]]. Its twin is **cynicism** — the pleasurable degradation of high values to low; both "are the results of the reduction of the concrete values of life to the mediating value of money." Where the cynic still triumphs in dragging honour, beauty, and salvation down to a "market price," the blasé person has passed beyond feeling any difference at all. Simmel distinguishes modern cynicism from the ancient Greek Cynics, whose downward levelling served a positive ideal of moral freedom now vanished.

## The mechanism

Simmel offers a two-part explanation. First, the standard account: over-stimulated, satiated nerves (of metropolitan life) blunt responsiveness. Second, his own addition — [[money-economy|money]] itself. Because money's function is to focus attention on what things *share* (their common exchange [[value]]) rather than their specific qualities, and because acquiring objects "by money" is uniform, mechanical, and indifferent, the objects lose the individual colour normally invested in them by the specific effort of obtaining them: "their acquisition by money… makes the objects necessarily more indifferent." The blasé attitude is thus money's levelling of qualitative difference internalized as a structure of feeling. Simmel links it to the modern craving "for excitement, for extreme impressions, for the greatest speed" — "one of those interesting cases in which the disease determines its own form of the cure," since the remedy (mere stimulation) uses the same indifferent currency that caused the illness.

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct. Simmel diagnoses the blasé attitude from observable settings and behaviours: the indifferent, "grey" affect of metropolitan and large-turnover milieus (the stock exchange as its "nursery"), the craving for ever more extreme stimulation, and the reserve/psychic distance of urban interaction. Its structural correlate is the degree to which a life is saturated by [[money-economy|money]]-mediated, qualitatively indifferent transactions.

## Applications

- Part of Simmel's diagnosis of modern inner life alongside calculability, psychic reserve/distance, and the intensified pace of life ([[money-economy]]).
- Connected to the [[objective-culture|preponderance of objective culture]] and the [[tragedy-of-culture|tragedy of culture]] — the levelling of qualitative values.
- A foundational text for urban sociology and the sociology of modern subjectivity; in *[[metropolis-and-mental-life|The Metropolis and Mental Life]]* (1903) the blasé attitude becomes the metropolitan psyche's chief defence against nervous over-stimulation.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The concept is an attributed psychological diagnosis, not a measured construct; it generalizes from Simmel's essayistic observation of metropolitan and financial milieus.
- Simmel treats the stock exchange and large-turnover settings as characteristic "nurseries" of cynicism and the blasé attitude — an illustrative, not sampled, claim.
