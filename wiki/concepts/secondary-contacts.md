# Secondary Contacts

## Definition as Coined

In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* Ch I, [[park-robert-e|Park]] argues that growth of cities is accompanied by substitution of "indirect, 'secondary,' for direct, face-to-face, 'primary' relations," quoting [[cooley-charles-horton|Cooley]] on [[primary-group|primary groups]] as intimate face-to-face association and co-operation forming social nature and the "we" (attributed).

In great cities with unstable population — parents and children employed outside the home, people living "side by side for years without so much as a bowing acquaintance" — primary relationships weaken and "the moral order which rested upon them is gradually dissolved" (attributed, Park).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Cooley primary groups | Intimate face-to-face nursery of human nature |
| Park 1925 | Urban substitution of secondary for primary; fashion vs custom; public opinion vs mores; advertising as control technique |
| Burgess 1925 (neighborhood essay) | "Promiscuity": primary and intimate behavior on the basis of secondary contacts (dance halls drawing city-wide patrons) |
| Simmel parallel | Metropolitan mental life / [[blase-attitude]] — not cited by name in Park Ch I but genealogically linked via Park's Simmel training |

## Operationalizations

Qualitative contrast in *The City*; political machine as primary-relation organization inside secondary-relation city; good-government leagues as secondary "public."

## Applications

- Explaining urban impersonality, publicity, and advertising.
- Linking primary-group breakdown to vice/crime statistics (Park).
- Dance-hall / bright-light leisure as secondary-contact intimacy (Burgess).

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary may overstate rural primary intimacy and understate structured inequality in who enjoys which contacts.
