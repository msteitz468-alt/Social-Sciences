# Adiaphorization

## Definition as Coined

In *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989), [[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]] adapts the theological term *adiaphoron* (in Church usage, "a thing declared indifferent") to name the social process by which action is **rendered "adiaphoric" — neither good nor evil, "measurable against technical (purpose-oriented or procedural) but not moral values"** (attributed voice). Adiaphorization does not corrupt morality by argument; it removes action from the field where moral judgement applies at all.

## Semantic History

Bauman generalizes the term across the book and the appended Amalfi Prize Lecture (1990), and reaffirms it in the 2000 Afterword. He formalizes it as **three complementary arrangements**: (1) *stretching the distance* between action and its consequences beyond the reach of moral impulse (see [[social-production-of-distance]]); (2) *"effacing the face"* — exempting some others from the class of potential objects of moral conduct; (3) *dissembling* persons into "aggregates of functionally specific traits" so that no unified moral "face" can be reassembled. The concept became central to his later moral sociology and to the "liquid modernity" writings.

## Operationalizations

Bauman locates adiaphorization above all in modern **[[bureaucracy]]**:

- **Moralization of technology.** Bureaucracy does not abolish moral vocabulary but redirects it: loyalty, duty, and discipline toward superiors become the operative moral terms, while the human target of the action drops out of moral concern — "the moralization of technology, coupled with the denial of the moral significance of non-technical issues."
- **Technical vs. moral responsibility.** Once hierarchy is supplemented by functional division of labour, the act is judged only by intrinsic technical criteria (efficiency, cost); "the bureaucrat's own act becomes an end in itself."
- **Expertise as a distancing device.** Where physical distance from victims is unavailable, responsibility is relocated from the person to the "state of the art" they represent (Bauman's case: Christopher Browning's Foreign-Ministry "Jewish Desk" officials).
- **"Rule by Nobody."** The dispersion of responsibility across mediated hierarchies produces what Bauman, following Hannah Arendt, calls "rule by Nobody," and what he elsewhere calls **free-floating responsibility** — everyone believes responsibility lies with someone else.

In the 2000 Afterword he corroborates the thesis with Robert Johnson's ethnography of American death-row execution teams (a warden: "We can honestly say that we did not do it") and Stephen Trombley's history of the electric chair, and notes that efficient killing organizations — like Nazi *Einsatzgruppen* recruitment — take care to *exclude* sadists and zealots, because bureaucratic killing depends on emotional neutrality, not passion.

## Applications

Adiaphorization underwrites Bauman's central "ordinary people" claim: "people who are neither morally corrupt nor prejudiced" can participate with dedication in destroying targeted categories precisely because the process **suspends** rather than mobilizes their moral convictions. Following Hans Jonas, Bauman argues modern technology gives adiaphorized action an unprecedented destructive *range*, calling for a new "ethics of distance." → [[social-production-of-distance]], [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]], [[genocide]]

## Critiques and Limitations

Because adiaphorization is diagnosed rather than measured, it shares the falsifiability worries of Bauman's larger thesis (see [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]]). Critics who emphasize ideology and antisemitism argue that "moral indifference" understates the active, believed hatred documented in some perpetrator populations (the Goldhagen position). Bauman's reply is that indifference and mediation, not conviction, are what scale — hatred cannot sustain industrial-scale killing; adiaphorized routine can.
