# Semiperiphery

## Definition as Coined

In [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]], the **semiperiphery** is an intermediate structural position between [[core-periphery|core and periphery]]: states/regions that mix core-like and peripheral activities, often important for political stability of the system and for upward/downward mobility.

**Wallerstein's primary definitions (attributed).** In *[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|WSA: An Introduction]]* (2004) the semiperiphery is a property of **states, not processes**: "There are no semiperipheral products... However, if one calculates what proportion of a country's production is core-like and what peripheral, one finds that some countries have a fairly even distribution. Hence we can talk of semiperipheral countries" — which "have a special kind of politics" and play "a particular role in the functioning of the world-system" (his early-21st-century examples: South Korea, Brazil, India). In *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]* (1974) he insists it is structural, not residual: "The semiperiphery... is not an artifice of statistical cutting points, nor is it a residual category. The semiperiphery is a necessary structural element in a world-economy" — functioning like the "middle trading groups" of an empire, collecting "vital but politically unpopular skills" and deflecting the political pressure that peripheral groups might otherwise aim at the core, while being itself excluded from core states' political coalitions. Semiperipheral states compete chiefly with one another to capture "leading industries" relocating during Kondratieff B-phases — what they call "economic development."

## Semantic History

Wallersteinian addition to the Prebischian binary. Contested as residual category vs necessary third pole. Adjacent "semi-industrialized" or "emerging market" languages are not identical.

## Operationalizations

Mixed export profiles; intermediate wage levels; sub-imperial roles. Assignment of particular countries is highly disputed and date-sensitive.

## Applications

System dynamics and mobility; Chase-Dunn and others on global socialism strategies (2004 volume) use intermediate zones as political terrain.

## Critiques and Limitations

Often criticized as under-specified; empirical assignment debates are endemic.
