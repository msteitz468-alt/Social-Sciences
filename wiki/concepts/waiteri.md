# Waiteri (fierceness)

## Definition as Coined

In [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-1968|Chagnon 1968]], **waiteri** means "fierce" / "fierce one"; **waiterimou** means "to behave ferociously." Chagnon used the valuation of fierceness as ethnographic warrant for the monograph's title: he argued that a high capacity for rage, a quick flash point, and willingness to use violence were desirable traits, and that "the fierce people" was how Yanomamö conceived themselves and wished to be seen (POSITION when totalized as ethnic essence; FINDING when restricted to reported prestige of toughness among men he knew).

## Semantic History

Within the 1968 text, waiteri labels both a personal reputation (Damowa as "the true waiteri" of Monou-teri; "fierce ones" of Hasabowa-teri) and a general cultural value linked mythically to Moon's blood. Later popular and critical discourse collapsed waiteri into a global brand for Yanomami identity — a semantic expansion that is part of the [[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate|representation debate]], not a neutral translation fact.

## Operationalizations

Chagnon did not scale waiteri numerically in 1968. He operationalized it narratively: leadership by demonstrated ferocity (Kaobawa's quiet authority after reputation established; pressure on reluctant men such as Hukoshikuwa to raid); wife-beating as low-risk ferocity display; chest-pounding and club scars as public indexes; raid participation and revenge obligation as tests.

## Critiques and Limitations

The move from observed conflict and emic prestige of toughness to a totalizing people-label is the core representational controversy. Reactivity concerns (ethnographer performing aggression; trade-good and mission contexts) affect how much "fierceness" is stable culture vs. situation. Later quantitative claims about unokai reproductive success belong mainly to post-1968 publications, not this edition's thin *unokaimou* (killer purification) mention.
