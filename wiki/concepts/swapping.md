# Swapping (Stack)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]* (fieldwork late 1960s, pub. 1974), [[stack-carol|Carol Stack]] elevates the Flats emic term **swapping** / **trading** to an analytic for the main mode of redistributing scarce goods among the Black urban poor. Stack (attributed): trading means any object or service offered **with the intent of obligating** — a possession, pledge, loan, trust, or "bank account" the giver can draw on later. Lily Jones (emic): "That's just everyday life, swapping. You not really getting ahead of nobody, you just get better things as they go back and forth."

## Semantic History

- **Emic (The Flats, late 1960s):** everyday trading of food stamps, rent money, clothes, furniture, cars, cigarettes, child care, meals, and children.
- **Stack 1974 analytic:** links Flats swapping to Maussian gift obligation and economic-anthropology exchange, stressing community sanctions and domestic-network entanglement (not one-shot barter).
- **Later contrast:** Desmond's [[disposable-ties]] describe short-lived crisis exchanges when durable kin webs fail — different temporal structure from Stackian continuous swapping.

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic observation of flows and reputations; not a survey scale in Stack. Distinct from market purchase and from pure altruism: obligation and expected return are constitutive.

## Applications

Core mechanism in [[stack-all-our-kin-study]]; feeds [[domestic-network]] stability analysis. Comparative link to [[reciprocal-obligation]] (Whyte) and [[gift-exchange]] theory without equating Cornerville rackets politics with Flats subsistence swapping.

## Critiques and Limitations

Bound to Stack's fieldwork moment and AFDC-era resource streams; may understate coercion/exploitation within networks (Stack notes people expect to be "exploited" in the game). Not a general theory of all poor people's exchange.
