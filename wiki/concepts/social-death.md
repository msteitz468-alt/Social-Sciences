# Social death

## Definition as Coined

**Social death** is a long-standing anthropological and sociological construct for the condition of being treated as dead to social life while still biologically alive. In *[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]* (2005), [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]] operationalizes it for contemporary Brazil: "Before biological death, there comes their social death"; **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of abandonment]]** "accelerate the death of the unwanted," and in that register "the human, the mental, and the chemical are complicit" (attributed, Biehl 2005).

This page tracks Biehl's use as the vault's primary ingested operationalization; earlier genealogies (including classical discussions of social death Biehl cites) remain to be densified from their primary sources.

## Semantic History

- **Classical / mid-century.** Uses in anthropology and sociology of slavery, total institutions, and ritual status (lineage not yet fully ingested here).
- **Biehl 2005.** Ties social death to pharmaceutical management, family deposition, and informal asylums under Brazil's democratic right-to-health regime — social death coexists with "incipient citizenship" for some (recovery-area AIDS pensions, antiretrovirals) while the infirmary waits with death.
- Adjacent: **[[ex-human]]** (the residual personhood form); **[[everyday-violence]]** (Scheper-Hughes) for ordinary authorized dying without the same institutional-pharmacy emphasis.

## Operationalizations

In *Vita*: institutional invisibility (not on maps; no remedial program); loss of ID, children, income, and continuous clinical care; family/police/hospital deposition; polypharmacy substituting for care; death without assistance as endpoint.

## Applications

- *[[biehl-vita-study|Vita]]* — Catarina's trajectory and the Vita infirmary (1995–2003 documentation).
- City network of "geriatric houses" in Porto Alegre as infrastructure of social death (Biehl, citing local human-rights and health reports).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Metaphor can blur biological dying with social exclusion; Biehl insists both are real and linked, not interchangeable.
- Risk of over-generalizing from one city and one life; keep multi-case claims attributed.

## Applications — Cape slavery (Hall 2000)

[[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] (*[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000), discussing [[cape-slavery|Cape Colony slavery]], invokes Orlando Patterson's concept of slavery as social death — quoted: "The essence of slavery is that the slave, in his social death, lives on the margin between community and chaos, life and death, the sacred and the secular. Already dead, he lives outside the mana of the gods and can cross the boundaries with social and supernatural impunity" (attributed, Patterson 1982: 51, quoted in Hall 2000) — as the theoretical frame for reading the public execution of slaves at the Cape (Robert Shell's 1994 finding of roughly one public execution per month under Dutch rule) as a display of sovereign power enacted specifically on bodies already denied social personhood.
