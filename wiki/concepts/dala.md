# Dala

The Trobriand (Kiriwina) matrilineal descent unit — the group of people who share the "same blood" transmitted through women from a common ancestral origin, together with the land and property attached to that identity. Glossed by [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] and H. A. Powell as "subclan" or "lineage"; [[weiner-annette-b|Weiner]] (1976) argued this gloss flattens several distinct elements the Trobrianders hold together.

## Definition as Coined

In the ethnographic record for the [[trobriand-islanders|Trobriand Islanders]], the *dala* is the corporate matrilineal unit below the level of the four exogamous *kumila* ("clans": Malasi, Lukuba, Lukwasisiga, Lukulabuta). Members of a dala trace descent through women to founder-ancestors (*tabu*) who emerged from a named place of origin — often as a brother–sister sibling pair — and claimed hamlet and garden land in the dala's name. The Trobriand expression **"same dala, same blood"** captures its core: dala blood identity cannot be taken away.

Weiner stressed (attributed) that the four *kumila* are, following [[leach-edmund|Leach]], **not** descent lines or property-holding corporations but vague symbolic categories (marked by birds, animals, plants); the dala is the genuinely corporate unit.

## Semantic History

Weiner argued that Malinowski treated *dala* as a single "gestalt" fusing at least five things — birth, matrilineal descent, women, place of emergence, and hamlet land — and that separating them is analytically necessary. Her key distinctions (attributed):

- **Dala blood vs. dala property.** Blood identity is transmitted at conception via *baloma* — an amorphous, un-remembered ancestral spirit that reincarnates as a spirit-child (*waiwaia*) entering a woman — and is pure and inalienable. Dala **property** (land, magic spells, body/house decorations, taboos) has an "impure" history: it circulates, is loaned, stolen, lost, or augmented, and is controlled chiefly by men, though women can reclaim loaned property.
- **Baloma vs. tabu.** The *baloma* carries blood substance and is not remembered as an individual; the *tabu* is the named, remembered founder-ancestor attached to land. Conflating them, Weiner held, obscures how identity and property descend differently.
- **Land as negotiated, not charter-fixed.** Against Malinowski's (1935) claim that the site of emergence fixed land rights, Weiner reported that land rights are "continually negotiated between living members" — land genealogies are deep and politically consequential (recited in dispute courts) even where personal genealogies are shallow (rarely beyond three generations).

## Operationalizations

Dala rank divides the units into **guyau** (dala of eminence, e.g. the Tabalu of Omarakana) and **tokai** (commoner dala); hamlets are correspondingly *viloguyau* or *vilotokai* (see [[rank-society]], [[chiefdom]]). Residence is often in the father's hamlet rather than strictly avunculocal — Weiner reported avunculocal residence applies mainly to the few men who are heirs to hamlet-manager status, nuancing the textbook image of the Trobriands as *the* avunculocal case.

The related exchange category **keyawa** ("others") cross-cuts dala and clan: generated through the "trick" of marriage within the father's clan, it organizes the networks of mortuary-exchange obligation through which [[womens-wealth|women's wealth]] flows at a [[sagali]].

## Applications

The dala is the pivot of Weiner's larger argument: because dala identity is carried and regenerated by women, "without women to reproduce, the dala ceases to exist" (a formulation she reports Kiriwinans state explicitly). Mortuary exchange is where dala identity is reasserted and the deceased "untied" from reciprocal claims, returning a pure ancestral *baloma* — the core of [[reproduction-regeneration]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The term sits inside a long dispute over Trobriand kinship categories ([[leach-edmund|Leach]] 1958; Lounsbury 1965; Powell) over whether hamlet or village, dala or clan, is the primary unit — see [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]. As an emic term, *dala* resists clean translation; "subclan," "lineage," and "matriclan" each import assumptions the Trobriand usage does not carry. Claims here rest on ethnographic report (Malinowski 1915–18; Weiner 1971–72) and should be read to those documentation moments, not as a timeless structure.
