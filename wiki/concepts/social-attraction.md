# Social Attraction

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] defines **social attraction** as the force that induces individuals to establish and expand voluntary associations: "a person is attracted to another if he expects associating with him to be in some way rewarding." He works with a broad and a narrow sense. In the **narrow** sense, attraction means liking another *intrinsically* (as an end in himself); in the **broad** sense, it means being drawn to another *for any reason*, including purely extrinsic benefit. The broad sense is the theoretically load-bearing one, because it drives Blau's opening derivation: "processes of social attraction... give rise to processes of social exchange." A person attracted to others is thereby moved to make himself attractive to them — to "impress" them — since his own benefit from the association depends on being found an attractive associate in turn.

Blau distinguishes **intrinsically rewarding associations** (ends in themselves — love, friendship — "right by fiat," not amenable to objective comparison) from **extrinsically rewarding associations** (means to further ends, where associates *can* be compared factually). He treats this as "a special case of Parsons's more general distinction between particularism and universalism."

## Semantic History

The term generalizes the social-psychology of "interpersonal attraction" (liking) into a structural driver. Blau's move is to argue that even apparently pure intrinsic attraction is built up: "It is not so much a specific kind of social reward as the fusion of a variety of rewards in a given association that makes these fused rewards inseparable from the associate who is their source and hence creates intrinsic attraction to him." Attraction, in other words, begins extrinsic and becomes intrinsic through fusion — a developmental, not primitive, category.

## Operationalizations

Not directly measured; inferred from association preferences and sociometric choices (whom one seeks out, names as a valued companion, wishes to associate with). Blau's welfare-agency studies use first-name informal acceptance among colleagues as an index of realized attraction.

## Applications

The first term in the derivation chain of [[social-exchange-theory]]: attraction → [[social-exchange|exchange]] → [[differentiation-of-power|power differentiation]]. It underlies the "paradox of integration" (impressive qualities attract but also threaten — see [[social-integration]]), the analysis of impression management (drawing on [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]), and the treatment of love as the polar case of intrinsic attraction (the "dilemma of love": freely granted expressions of affection stimulate another's love but, granted too freely, depreciate in value).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The intrinsic/extrinsic distinction is porous by Blau's own account (fusion converts one into the other), and many real associations (union membership, collegial ties) mix both — limiting its use as a clean classificatory device.
- As with the wider program, "reward" and "attraction" risk circularity absent independent measurement of expected reward.
