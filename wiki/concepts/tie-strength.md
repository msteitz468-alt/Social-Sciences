# Tie Strength

Analytic construct for the intensity of a positive interpersonal relationship, formalized by [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]] in *[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973|The Strength of Weak Ties]]* (1973) as the basis of the [[strength-of-weak-ties]] argument.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Granovetter 1973.)* The strength of a tie is a (probably linear) combination of:

1. the **amount of time** spent in the relationship;
2. **emotional intensity**;
3. **intimacy** (mutual confiding);
4. **reciprocal services** characterizing the tie.

Each element is somewhat independent though highly intracorrelated. Ties in the 1973 paper are assumed **positive and symmetric**; negative and asymmetric ties are deferred. "Absent" includes both no relationship and negligible ties (nodding acquaintance, habitual newspaper vendor) — with a note that disasters may make negligible ties consequential.

## Semantic History

- **Pre-1973:** Sociometry and small-group research scaled liking and choice without a portable multi-dimensional "strength" construct linking dyad properties to large-network structure. Anthropological "multiplexity" (Kapferer) was one alternative indicator of strength; Granovetter allowed multiplex ties to be strong but refused multiplexity as the sole criterion (citing [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] on single-content strong ties).
- **1973 formalization:** Discrete labels strong / weak / absent stand in for an underlying continuum "for convenience of exposition."
- **Post-1973 research program (partly ingested):** Survey batteries (closeness scales, discussion-network "important matters"), structural alternatives ([[structural-holes|Burt's structural holes]] and [[network-constraint|constraint]], 1992 — which relocate the causal variable from a tie's *strength* to the *non-redundancy* it spans), and debates over whether "weak" should mean infrequent contact, low intimacy, or structural non-redundancy. Putnam-style **bridging vs bonding** [[social-capital]] later popularized a related distinction at community scale — related but not identical to Granovetter's dyadic construct.

## Operationalizations

| Tradition | Measure | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Granovetter job study | Frequency of contact around time of job tip | Only first definitional dimension; author claimed interview anecdotes support full definition |
| Classic sociometry | Mutual choice, rank-order of friends | Tends to capture strong ties; fixed-choice designs suppress weak ties |
| Milgram small-world postcards | "Friend" vs "acquaintance" categories | Granovetter treats as strong vs weak for interracial chain analysis |
| Later surveys | Closeness, duration, multiplexity, role relation | Measurement literature extensive; not yet ingested |

## Applications

- Predicting **friendship-circle overlap** (stronger A–B → more shared third parties).
- Identifying candidate **[[local-bridge|bridges]]** (only weak ties can be bridges under the forbidden-triad logic).
- Explaining **job-information channels**, diffusion velocity, and community bridging capacity (see [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Single-dimension proxies** (frequency alone) underdetermine the four-part definition.
- **Positive/symmetric restriction** excludes rivalry, hierarchy, and one-way dependence.
- **Boundary of "absent":** continuous variable forced into discrete bins.
- **Content ignored:** strength without content (instrumental vs affective, hierarchical vs peer) was listed by Granovetter as unfinished business.
