# Law of Three Stages

## Definition as Coined

[[comte-auguste|Comte]] stated a "great fundamental law" of the development of human intelligence: each leading conception — each branch of knowledge — passes successively through three theoretical conditions (*Positive Philosophy*, Martineau, Introduction ch. I):

| Stage | Also called | What the mind does (Comte's account) |
|---|---|---|
| **Theological** | fictitious | Seeks essential natures and first/final causes; explains phenomena by supernatural beings; highest perfection substitutes providential action of a single Being for numerous divinities |
| **Metaphysical** | abstract | Replaces gods with personified abstractions / entities inherent in things; last stage substitutes one great entity ("Nature") |
| **Positive** | scientific | Gives up absolute causes; studies invariable laws of succession and resemblance by combined reasoning and observation |

Comte held that the theological is the necessary point of departure, the positive the definitive state, and the metaphysical only a transition. He also claimed an **individual parallel**: each person is "theologian in childhood, metaphysician in youth, natural philosopher in manhood" — and that individual education should reproduce the race's stages (Book VI ch. XIII).

Different sciences, he argued, reach positivity at different rates by generality, simplicity, and independence: astronomy earliest, social science latest — coupling the law to the [[hierarchy-of-the-sciences]].

## Semantic History

- **1822 prospectus** — early statement of the law (secondary: Harrison intro).
- ***Cours* / Martineau** — systematic statement and application to the encyclopedic scale and to social history (Book VI dynamics and historical chapters).
- **Later textbook use** — often reduced to a simple three-box philosophy of history; stripped of Comte's hierarchy-of-sciences coupling and of his political reorganization aim.
- **Do not equate** with later modernization or secularization theories without attribution — related problems, different programs.

## Operationalizations

Comte operationalized the law historically in Book VI:

| Phase (Comte) | Book VI locus | Content (attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| Fetichism | ch. VII | First theological phase; beginning of [[theological-military-system]] |
| Polytheism | ch. VIII | Development of theological-military system |
| Monotheism | ch. IX | Peak and modification of theological-military system (Catholic/medieval) |
| Metaphysical / critical period | ch. X | Five centuries from 14th c.; negative doctrine; transitional dogmas |
| Rise of positive elements | ch. XI | Scientific and industrial reorganization materials |
| Revolutionary crisis | ch. XII | Negative outruns positive; French Revolution as experiment |
| Positive reorganization | ch. XIII–XV | Method complete; doctrine and final social action |

He urged using the most general, familiar facts and the "most advanced" social series rather than exceptional events. No quantitative index exists in the source; the construct is qualitative-historical and programmatic.

## Applications

- Architecture of [[positivism]] and of [[social-dynamics]]
- Justification for why [[sociology]] arrives last ([[hierarchy-of-the-sciences]])
- Historical chapters of Book VI (fetichism through revolutionary crisis)
- Critique of pure critical philosophy as stuck in the metaphysical stage of politics

## Critiques and Limitations

Linear progress, Eurocentrism, teleology toward the positive polity, and stage-forcing of complex histories are the main later objections. Keep all stage claims in attributed voice. The law is a theoretical program, not a finding established by independent evidence classes.
