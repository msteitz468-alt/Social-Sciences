# Monumentality

## Definition as Coined

**Monumentality** names the social and experiential significance of large built works — not only their scale. In European prehistory, the term groups mounds, enclosures, [[megalith|megalithic tombs]], [[henge|henges]], stone circles, and related earthworks that stand apart from (or transform) ordinary domestic architecture. [[bradley-richard|Richard Bradley]]’s *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998) treats monuments as **shapers of human experience of time and place**, not mere indexes of surplus.

## Semantic History

- **Culture-history / diffusion:** "Megalithic monuments" as trait-packages and missionary religions (see [[megalith]] historiography).
- **Processual:** Monuments as energy sinks, territorial markers, or information systems (e.g. [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] on chiefdom territories) — function and social ranking first.
- **Ideological conversion frame:** [[sherratt-andrew|Sherratt]] (1995, via Bradley): "Monumentality was an essential element of the cultural rhetoric whereby… indigenous groups were converted to a Neolithic way of life." Bradley accepts the *force* of monuments as cultural rhetoric but rejects it as sufficient explanation of *form* or of farming links without independent subsistence proof (attributed, Bradley 1998).
- **Post-processual / landscape (1990s):** Meaning, memory, bodily experience, and sequence; Bradley 1998 as a major British synthesis; dialogue with [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology|Tilley’s phenomenology of landscape]] and with Thomas, Hodder, Whittle, Barrett.
- **Comparative:** American moundbuilding ([[moundbuilder-myth]], Poverty Point, Cahokia) and other non-European monumentalisms use related but not identical vocabularies — keep regional constructs separate.

## Bradley 1998 — load-bearing theses (attributed)

1. **Surplus inversion.** Monument building should not be treated as a by-product of agricultural surplus. Agricultural production required a different sense of **time and place** (and different attitudes to nature); monumental architecture often **created the conditions** in which farming first became acceptable (*Altering the Earth* lecture lineage; 1998 Preface / Ch. 1).
2. **Monuments as topic in their own right.** Among the earliest clear Neolithic signals in north and north-west Europe were enormous funerary constructions that played no part in everyday affairs; their landscape prominence contrasts with limited settlement, house, and intensive-agriculture evidence (beyond the loess longhouse zone).
3. **Mesolithic contrast.** Built monuments are rare among European Mesolithic contexts (slab-lined cists, tiny cairns, small ritual buildings); if Mesolithic ontology lacked a sharp culture/nature split and treated natural places as sacred without construction, freestanding monuments would have little place. Both **ownership of domesticates** and **monument building** reflect control over nature once traditional partnerships with the natural world lapsed (Ch. 2).
4. **Properties of earthworks.** Durability and landscape presence whether or not actively used; large labour and group identity; sites as ongoing *projects*; only *ideas* of monuments (not earthworks themselves) travel between cultural settings — unlike portable artefacts (Ch. 5–6 framework).
5. **Sequence over type.** Surface "types" (variant circles, successive henge styles) are often **stages** in construction/reconstruction sequences ending at different points on different sites (Ch. 9).
6. **Mnemonics and later prehistory.** Monuments act as mnemonics of a vanished past; by the mid–late second millennium BC open ceremonial monuments go out of use as land is reorganized into fields and settlements, yet domestic architecture reworks circular schemes already established in the ritual landscape (Ch. 10).

## Operationalizations

Scale (earth/stone volume), durability, labour estimates, non-domestic structured deposits, intervisibility, construction sequences, bank/ditch polarity, permeability (earthwork arena vs freestanding circle), and access relations living–dead (closed single burial vs open multi-deposit chambers).

## Applications

European Neolithic–Bronze Age ceremonial landscapes (Britain, Ireland, Atlantic façade, loess prototypes for [[long-mound|long mounds]] and enclosures); debates on farming–ideology coupling; [[ritual-landscape]] analysis; [[social-memory]] via durable architecture.

## Moore's Variables and the Scale-vs-Authority Debate (attributed)

[[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore (1996b, 2005)]] operationalizes monumentality along five variables: **permanence**, **centrality**, **ubiquity**, **scale**, and **visibility** — applied to Formative-period Peruvian ceremonial architecture to test whether construction scale by itself indicates centralized political authority.

The question is contested. Thomas Pozorski (1980:101–104), excavating Huaca de los Reyes (Caballo Muerto Complex, Moche Valley), estimated labor investment at roughly 350,000 person-days; assuming a single coherent architectural vision, this implied construction within under 25 years, achievable by a workforce of 240 people laboring about two months a year in agricultural off-seasons — Pozorski concluded "organizational efforts need not have been as substantial or as rigid as might be supposed" (1980:104), i.e., monumental scale need not imply a large workforce or a rigid centralized organization (attributed). Richard Burger and Lucy Salazar-Burger (1991:292–93), by contrast, argued that the roughly contemporary Cardal complex (Lurín Valley) was built cumulatively across four phases over 400–500 years, achievable by ~100 people working two months a year across four centuries — "a rather modest lifestyle," they argue, evidence of small-scale, noncentralized, religiously motivated construction rather than state power. Moore concedes "they may be correct" but counters that deliberate ritual entombment of earlier features (e.g., Cardal's central staircase carefully buried and replicated) reflects intentional continuity-emphasizing practice rather than mere incremental remodeling — evidence, in his reading, of continuity of canonical religious practice rather than proof against centralization either way (attributed). See [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]] for the fuller Pozorski-vs.-Burger/Salazar-Burger dispute.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Monument" can lump functionally distinct forms; labour estimates are model-dependent; experiential claims are interpretive (attributed voice). Chronologies and demography have moved since 1998 (Bayesian 14C, aDNA — as of later literature superseding Bradley 1998). Four-way non-identity: monumental culture ≠ ethnos ≠ language ≠ genes.

## Europe in the Neolithic densification (Whittle 1996) — abstract funerals and re-created community

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 1, 7, 10. Parallel interpretive layer to Bradley 1998 surplus-inversion and sequence theses above — complementary sacred-values programme, not a replacement.)*

**Core monumental programme.** Whittle characterises Neolithic Europe as a way of life rooted in the forager past (sharing and integration) yet distinguishable by new beliefs about descent, beginnings, and time. From ~4000 BC (or possibly earlier), monuments containing human remains mark west, north-west, and parts of central Europe; significance lies primarily in **sacred/religious realms** — origins, time, descent — against power-difference-first readings (Ch.7). Synthesis: monuments only partly about burial; more about ancestral cult and mythical as well as actual ancestors; human remains as offerings uniting living with beginnings; bone circulation as regeneration; living and dead linked in collectivity — belief that “may lie at the heart of what we choose to call the Neolithic way of life” (attributed). Brittany vignette: stone houses of the dead, “trees of stone,” freer-access shrines, menhir reuse as roofing — constructions as much about preserving a timeless past as about power of community segments (Ch.1).

**Settlement evidence and monument siting.** Ch.7 title frame “Unfair settlements and abstract funerals”: settlement evidence quality is uneven; Alpine-foreland waterlogged villages should **not** set the template for southern English chalk, Scandinavian coasts, or Brittany — richness of survival can blind us; Złota cattle-keepers’ communal values may be a better general guide than expecting nucleated villages everywhere (attributed). Scarcity of houses in Britain/Ireland/NW France treated as real, not only survival bias. Rather than reading large later monuments (Avebury, Mount Pleasant) as symptoms of ever more sedentary numerous populations, Whittle envisages renewed clearances as products of **monument construction by people from wide areas** — builders need not be confined to immediate vicinities (attributed).

**Architecture of place and late shift.** Longhouse for the living of past generations transformed into shrine for ancestors of a timeless past ([[long-mound]], [[death-of-the-house]]). Non-monumental human deposits are not a sideshow: early Neolithic rites less formal/dispersed into wider contexts — remains as talismans making present realms of the dead. Later phase: ancestors “at rest” — respected but taken for granted in an established landscape — while funerals deal with continuation of the living community; reuse of monuments often respects structure while disturbing previous contents, emphasising house of spirits not bones of human forebears (attributed). Ditched enclosures ([[causewayed-enclosure]]) as social arenas for gatherings, feasting, food/drink sharing, animal veneration, deposits, and gifts — celebrate domesticity, fertility, hospitality, generosity, shared origins; means by which the Neolithic world was consolidated, not symptoms of social differentiation (attributed). Cursus monuments may enhance pathways of ancestors and spirits; later henges/stone circles: unfinished multi-phase projects under **sacred obligation**, with shame as sanction for non-participation rather than political coercion (attributed).

**Ch.10 synthesis (monumentality within values).** Social landscape framed by enduring constructions on origins/identity; memory of longhouses transformed into shrines for spirits/ancestors (timeless sacred past; place fixed in mobile pattern). Continuing mobility throughout: before people “settled down,” sense of place (tells, monuments, cave deposits) created by routines and rites. Neolithic = slow histories over many generations; sense of history twofold — beginnings/time past **and** timelessness/repetition generation to generation. Against copper/megaliths/enclosures as straightforward hierarchy engines; for communal identity, place-making, fertility/regeneration metaphors, and continual re-creation of community (attributed).
