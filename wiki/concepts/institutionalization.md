# Institutionalization

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (1966):

> Institutionalization occurs whenever there is a reciprocal typification of habitualized actions by types of actors. Put differently, any such typification is an institution. … The institution posits that actions of type X will be performed by actors of type X.

Institutions imply **historicity** (built up in shared history) and **control**: they channel conduct along predefined patterns among theoretically many possibilities. Primary social control is the institution as such; sanctions are secondary/supplementary when institutionalization is incomplete.

## Semantic History

“Institution” is older sociological vocabulary (Spencer, Sumner, Durkheim, Parsons). Berger and Luckmann’s contribution is a **genetic** account: from biological world-openness → need for social order → [[habitualization]] → reciprocal [[typification]] → institutional facticity for the next generation. When institutions are transmitted to children, objectivity thickens: “There we go again” becomes “This is how these things are done”; the social world confronts as external and coercive fact (Durkheimian facticity idiom within a constructionist frame).

Related processes in their text: **sedimentation** and **tradition** (meanings deposited in a social stock, transmitted as tradition); **roles** as representations of the institutional order; **scope and modes** (degree of institutionalization varies; de-institutionalization possible; division of labour and sub-universes of meaning).

## Operationalizations

Theoretical typology of scope/modes rather than a scale. Empirical programs inspired by B&L track role systems, recipe knowledge specialization, and institutional transmission across generations.

## Applications

- Objective reality of society (Part Two of *Social Construction*)
- Basis for [[legitimation]] (second-order objectivation of the institutional order)
- Link to [[reification]] when institutions appear as nature or divine will
- Bridge to [[secondary-socialization]] into institutional sub-worlds

## A power-and-transmission account (Blau)

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]]'s *[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power]]* (1964) supplies exactly the power dimension the constructionist origin story is criticized (below) for underplaying. Blau analyzes institutionalization as resting on **three conditions**: (1) organizing principles become embodied in **formalized, historically-transmitted procedures** independent of the individuals who carry them out; (2) their **legitimating [[mediating-values|values]] are transmitted intergenerationally through socialization**; and (3) the society's **dominant groups are strongly identified with those values and lend their power to enforce them** (freedom of speech, he notes, stays institutionalized only as long as powerful groups defend it). Institutions thus have "double roots in the past — in historical tradition and in internalized cultural values — and roots in the power structure," quoting Durkheim on how written laws and dogmas acquire "a manner of action of their own." Blau adds that every cultural heritage also contains a **"counterinstitutional component"** — unrealized ideals whose gap from practice is the ultimate source of [[opposition]] and change.

## Critiques and Limitations

Functional integration is not assumed a priori (they reject automatic functionalist integration of the whole order). Critics argue the micro A–B origin story underplays conquest, law, and material power in institution-building; B&L treat power more explicitly at the level of universe-maintenance and monopoly of experts (Part Two §2). Blau's account (above) makes the power and enforcement dimension central rather than supplementary.
