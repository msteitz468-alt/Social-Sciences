## Definition as Coined

"Mass society" names a social order in which centralized mass communications and large-scale
hierarchies shape the self-images, standards, and political consciousness of an atomized
population, displacing a "community of publics." In *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]*
(1951) [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] argued (attributed) that the mass media "seep into our images of
self" and stand *between* individuals and the "eventful scene." His amendment to Marx: "If the
consciousness of men does not determine their existence, neither does their material existence
determine their consciousness. Between consciousness and existence stand communications."

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), chapter 13, he developed the concept
systematically as the **public vs mass** contrast and as the bottom tier of his three-level map
(unified [[power-elite]] / stalemated [[middle-levels-of-power]] / fragmented mass). The
transformation of public into mass, he argued (attributed), is "an important clue to the meaning
of the power elite": an elite connected to a community of publics means something different than
an elite facing a society of masses.

## Semantic History

The mass-society idea has a long, politically ambiguous European lineage (from conservative
crowd theory to the Frankfurt School's culture-industry critique). Mills's version is closest
to the latter — he absorbed Weimar and émigré sociology through [[gerth-hans|Hans Gerth]] — but
recast for postwar American conditions. He drew on Walter Lippmann's "pictures in our heads" /
pseudo-environment argument. He **rejected** the Le Bon–Ortega line that "the masses" are
triumphant; autonomous collectivities' influence, he held (attributed), is diminishing as masses
are manipulated and re-atomized. Classic democratic "Great American Public" images remain as
**legitimations masquerading as fact** — "images out of a fairy tale."

## Operationalizations

### Four dimensions (Power Elite, Ch. 13)

| Dimension | Public (ideal type) | Mass (ideal type) |
|---|---|---|
| I. Opinion ratio | Virtually as many express as receive | Far fewer express than receive; abstract media audience |
| II. Answer-back | Immediate, effective reply without reprisal | Difficult or impossible |
| III. Opinion → action | Opinion readily finds outlet even against authority | Realization controlled by authorities who organize channels |
| IV. Institutional penetration | Public more or less autonomous | Mass has no autonomy; agents of institutions penetrate it |

Dominant communication: in a public, discussion ascendant; in a mass society, formal media
dominate and publics become "mere media markets." Mills held the US had moved "a considerable
distance along the road to the mass society" whose end is totalitarianism, but "We are not yet
at that end."

### Media psychology formula (attributed)

Media tell the man in the mass (1) who he is, (2) what he wants to be, (3) how to get that way,
(4) how to feel that way when he is not — identity, aspirations, technique, escape.

*White Collar* reach figures (Louella Parsons, weekly moviegoers) and banalization of political
symbols remain as earlier operational hints.

## The Frankfurt-School variant (Marcuse)

The culture-industry lineage Mills absorbed reaches the wiki directly with [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964). Marcuse argues (attributed) that advanced industrial society "liquidates" the oppositional, "two-dimensional" character of higher culture "not through the denial and rejection of the 'cultural values,' but through their wholesale incorporation into the established order... on a massive scale" — mass reproduction (paperbacks, records, radio) that "establishes cultural equality while preserving domination." The formerly disruptive character-types (artist, rebel, vamp, gangster) survive "as affirmation rather than negation of the established order." This is the culture-side of [[one-dimensionality]] and connects mass society to [[repressive-desublimation]] and [[false-needs]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Mass-society theory has been widely criticized for overstating audience passivity and
media effects, understating active reception and interpretation, and carrying an implicit
nostalgia for a "public" that may never have existed. Mills himself insisted the US is a
**mixture** of public and mass features, not a pure type, and resisted crude owners'-interests
reductionism. The concept remains contested; all of Mills's theses stay in **attributed** voice.
