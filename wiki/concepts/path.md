# Path (Landscape Path)

## Definition as Coined

In Tilley's landscape phenomenology, a **path** is not only a track but a **structure of movement and narrative** linking places; herding, walking, and monumental alignments re-inscribe paths across Mesolithic–Neolithic change (*[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* 1994).

## Semantic History

Draws on de Certeau's art of practice and ethnographic path/track systems; archaeological use centers monument–landmark alignments and long-term route memory.

## Operationalizations

Monument alignments along ridges/escarpments; relations to cursus routes; sequences of approach to chambers; coastal/river corridor movement.

## Applications

Cranborne Chase Cursus–barrow complex; Welsh monument rows and approaches ([[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Archaeological recovery of actual prehistoric paths is often indirect; risk of reading modern access routes back.

## Tilley 2010 densification

*(Attributed — *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010]]*.)* Paths structure sequences of perception (directionality, posture, what enters/leaves sight). Regional applications: Ridgeway east→west processional barrow path; Bodmin stone-row perspectival sequences; Exmoor settings without such sequencing; Avenue approach at [[stonehenge]]. Paths alter the character of places (Body–Place–Path triangle, Ch. 10).
