# Practical Sense (*Sens pratique*)

Title concept of *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]*: the pre-reflective, temporally situated mastery of a social game — "feel for the game" — generated by [[habitus]] and systematically destroyed when theorists substitute timeless, reversible models for practice-in-process. Cross-linked throughout Book I (chs. 3–5 especially) and demonstrated in Book II on [[kabyle]] ritual, kinship, and honour and on Béarn succession.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu.)* Practical sense is quasi-bodily involvement in the world that presupposes no representation of body, world, or their relationship; it is social necessity turned into nature, converted into motor schemes and body automatisms. It orients "choices" that, though not deliberate, are systematic and charged with retrospective finality. In sporting language, Bourdieu called its clearest example a "feel for the game" — near-miraculous encounter of habitus and [[social-field|field]].

> "Practical sense is a quasi-bodily involvement in the world which presupposes no representation either of the body or of the world, still less of their relationship. ... A particularly clear example of practical sense as a proleptic adjustment to the demands of a field is what is called, in the language of sport, a 'feel for the game'."

The "feel" (*sens*) for the game is, he argued, the sense of the imminent future of the game — the direction (*sens*) of the game's history that gives the game its sense. Because agents never know completely what they are doing, what they do has more sense than they know. Practice has a logic which is not that of the logician; the principle of the **economy of logic** mobilizes no more logic than practice requires, generating approximate, fuzzy coherence that cannot withstand pure logical criticism.

## Semantic History

- **Substance predates the 1980 coinage.** While the term *sens pratique* titles the 1980 book, its content is already worked out in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline]]* (1972): "**learned ignorance** (*docta ignorantia*), a mode of practical knowledge not comprising knowledge of its own principles"; "agents are **possessed by their habitus more than they possess it**," the *modus operandi* revealing itself "only in the *opus operatum*"; and the **economy of logic** — the "fuzzy logic of practice" that mobilizes no more coherence than practice requires. The *Outline* critique of the "**synoptic illusion**" (calendar, genealogy, and map as detemporalizing theoretical artefacts) is the direct source of *Logic*'s treatment.
- **French *sens pratique*** pairs meaning and direction/sense; English translations split the load between "practical sense" and "logic of practice."
- **Against objectivism and phenomenology.** Objectivism, Bourdieu argued, cannot analyse production and functioning of the feel for the social game that makes meaning objectified in institutions taken for granted; phenomenology describes taken-for-grantedness without conditions of possibility (coincidence of objective and internalized structures).
- **Against structuralist pan-logicism and intuitionism.** Analogical practical sense produces practices "less logical than structuralist pan-logicism would have it and more logical than … intuitionism would suggest" (Book II, "Irresistible Analogy").
- **Learned ignorance (*docta ignorantia*).** Practical mastery does not contain knowledge of its own principles; native theories ("honour requires…") dangerously reinforce objectivist extraction of norms from *opus operatum*.
- Related to ordinary-language "feel for the game" and, via [[hexis]], to Maussian techniques of the body.

## Operationalizations

Demonstrated ethnographically and strategically, not scaled:

- **Timing and tempo.** Gift and honour exchanges: counter-gift must be deferred and different; strategies play on the interval; excellence is necessary improvisation under urgency that "brooks no delay."
- **Transfer of schemes.** Same schemes applied across homologous situations (loom assembly and opening of ploughing; funeral chants at harvest and when cloth is cut) without explicit homology (Preface).
- **Ritual as "the done thing."** Rites as practices justified by mere performance — ends in themselves, sensible yet devoid of sense intention that science projects onto them.
- **Calendar and synoptic illusion.** Informants' practical grasp of seasonal structure (e.g. *eliali* as scansion of time, not geometric "forty days") overrides calculating reason; synoptic calendars are theoretical artefacts (Book II).
- **Legalistic vs practical mastery (Béarn).** "Rules" of succession are strategic resources; reducing family strategy to formal rules is legalistic fallacy.
- **Sport/art pedagogy.** Teaching that falls back on extracted "moves" and rules risks academicism once excellence is no longer based on practical mastery.

## Applications

- **[[kabyle]] gift, honour, and marriage.** Sense of honour as disposition enabling instant appreciation of situations; generative model of challenge/riposte and gift/counter-gift powered by sense of honour, not abstract axiomatics; ordinary vs extra-ordinary marriages as regulated improvisation vs high-stakes ceremonial strategy.
- **Ritual generative schemes.** Reuniting separated contraries (marriage, ploughing) and separating reunited contraries (harvest as denied murder); multifunctional, over-determined ritual actions maximizing polysemy.
- **Béarn.** Practical mastery of succession and marriage markets without ethical/juridical reduction.
- **Theory of practice.** Correct use of models requires theory of practice as participation in a game ([[illusio]]) and of theoretical separation that detemporalizes practice.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Falsifiability.** Claims about unarticulated mastery risk post-hoc reconstruction: any successful practice can be redescribed as "practical sense."
- **Economy of logic vs science of logic.** How much systematicity the analyst may impose before producing forced coherence is a standing methodological tension Bourdieu names but does not eliminate.
- **Relation to rational choice.** "Strategies" without strategic intention can look either like a genuine third way or like smuggled teleology, depending on the critic.
- **Observer effect.** Mere description, Bourdieu argued, changes the status of gestures that make sense without signifying intention — so operationalization always risks transforming the object.
