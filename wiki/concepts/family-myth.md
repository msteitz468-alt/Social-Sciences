# Family myth

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]*, [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] described **family myths** as "versions of reality that obscure a core truth in order to manage a family tension" (Ch. 2, attributed). Term indebted to Antonio J. Ferreira (1967) on family myth in psychotherapy (notes). Not all couples need or have myths; when they arise they often manage key tensions linked "by degrees" to the [[stalled-revolution|stalled revolution]] (attributed).

**Canonical illustration — Nancy and Evan Holt:** after open conflict over the [[second-shift|second shift]], they adopted an upstairs/downstairs division (Nancy: living areas, kitchen, bedrooms, baths; Evan: garage, car, hobbies, the dog Max), presented as equitable "half and half." Hochschild called this a "modest delusional system" that denied Evan's refusal to share and Nancy's greater fear of divorce (Ch. 4, attributed). Suppressed struggle continued as "Joey's Problem" (bedtime/sex fatigue framed as the child's issue).

## Semantic History

Ferreira's clinical usage → Hochschild's sociological reuse for dual-earner marital accounting. Adjacent myths in the book: "we're just dreadfully busy with our careers" (avoidance of competition over care); careerism as cover for mutual withdrawal; public presentations of traditional harmony (e.g. Judsons). Stein case elaborates multiple layered myths around career and help. Distinct from ideology: a myth is a **shared story about what the couple is already doing**, not only what they believe men and women should do.

## Operationalizations

Analytic, not survey:

- Discrepancy between task inventories / observation and the couple's joint narrative of fairness
- "Rezoning" of anger (e.g. Nancy miniaturizing equality claims to the dog alone)
- Emotion work required to "feel the right feeling" under the myth (Holt; attributed link of national emotion work to stall vs divorce)

## Applications

| Couple (illustrative) | Myth / management form |
|---|---|
| Holts | Upstairs/downstairs equality |
| Livingstons | Overbusyness covering marital distance |
| Steins | Career demand / help as covering scarce gratitude |
| Judsons | Public presentation vs private instability |

Myths enable joint emotional life under unresolved tension "at a cost in authenticity" in the stalled revolution (Ch. 14, attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of pathologizing ordinary sense-making; Hochschild's "delusional" language is interpretive.
- Observer access to "core truth" depends on interview and observation privilege.
- Couples who separate emotionally or physically (Steins, Judsons) may manage without a joint equality myth — myths are one management form among others.
