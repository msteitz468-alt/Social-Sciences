# Ethnocentrism

## Definition as Coined

The term is [[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]]'s, introduced in *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]*
(§15): *"Ethnocentrism is the technical name for this view of things in which one's own
group is the center of everything, and all others are scaled and rated with reference to
it."* Each group *"nourishes its own pride and vanity, boasts itself superior, exalts its
own divinities, and looks with contempt on outsiders,"* and *"thinks its own folkways the
only right ones"* (§15).

Sumner tied the concept structurally to the [[in-group-out-group|in-group/out-group]]
distinction: ethnocentrism is the sentiment-side of the boundary between the "we-group" and
"others-groups" (§13–14). Its sociological payoff, for him, is a feedback effect on custom:
*"ethnocentrism leads a people to exaggerate and intensify everything in their own folkways
which is peculiar and which differentiates them from others. It therefore strengthens the
folkways"* (§15). He illustrated it with tribal autonyms meaning "men" or "real people"
(Caribs, Kiowa, Lapps, Tunguses), the Jew/Gentile and Greek/"barbarian" divisions, and —
pointedly turning the lens on his own society — the 1896 Chinese schoolbook on the
"middle kingdom" alongside the *"civilizing mission"* rhetoric of France, Germany, Russia,
and the United States (§§16–18).

Sumner distinguished related sentiments: **patriotism** as loyalty to the modern civic
group, and **chauvinism** as *"boastful and truculent group self-assertion"* — patriotism
*"degenerate[d] into a vice"* (§§19–20).

## Semantic History

Sumner's 1906 coinage moved from descriptive sociology into social psychology, where it was
reframed as a measurable attitude. Adorno et al.'s *The Authoritarian Personality* (1950)
built an "ethnocentrism scale," treating it as a generalized hostility to out-groups
correlated with authoritarianism — a narrower, pathologizing sense than Sumner's, for whom
ethnocentrism was a near-universal structural feature of grouping, not a personality
defect. Later intergroup research (realistic conflict theory; social identity theory)
retained Sumner's basic in-group/out-group architecture while decoupling in-group love from
out-group hate, which Sumner had treated as *"correlative to each other"* (§14). In
anthropology "ethnocentrism" became a methodological warning — the bias fieldwork and
[[comparative-method|comparison]] must guard against — and the near-antonym of cultural
relativism, itself partly descended from Sumner's own relativism of the [[mores]] (§65).

## Operationalizations

Sumner did not measure it. Social psychology later did: the Adorno E-scale (1950); measures
of in-group bias in minimal-group experiments; social-distance and out-group-attitude
scales. These operationalizations disagree on whether ethnocentrism is one thing (a
generalized syndrome) or two separable tendencies (in-group preference vs. out-group
derogation) — an empirical dispute Sumner's original correlative formulation prejudged.

## Applications

- Explaining the intensification of group markers under intergroup competition (§15).
- Grounding cultural relativism's caution in [[comparative-method|cross-cultural method]].
- Analyzing nationalism and the "civilizing mission" ideologies of imperial states (§§18–19).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Universality overstated.** Treating in-group love and out-group hostility as necessarily
  correlative is contradicted by evidence that the two vary independently.
- **Descriptive vs. evaluative slippage.** Sumner's own framing embeds the evolutionary
  hierarchy it purports to relativize (his "nature peoples"), so the coiner of the anti-bias
  concept exhibits the bias — see [[sumner-william-graham]] Reflexivity.
- **Level ambiguity.** "Group" ranges from village to nation to "race" without a fixed unit,
  carrying the ambiguity into the derived measures.
