# Scientia Sexualis

## Definition as Coined

**Scientia sexualis** — a "science of sexuality" — is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s term (attributed) in *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I (Part Three) for the distinctively Western procedure for producing the truth of sex. He opposes it to ***ars erotica*** (an erotic art in which truth is drawn from pleasure itself, "understood as a practice and accumulated as experience"), which he attributes to "China, Japan, India, Rome, the Arabo-Moslem societies." "Our civilization possesses no ars erotica. In return, it is undoubtedly the only civilization to practice a scientia sexualis... procedures for telling the truth of sex which are geared to a form of knowledge-power... I have in mind the confession."

## Semantic History

Foucault's claim is that the nineteenth-century science of sexuality was **not a break** with religious tradition but kept [[confession]] as its "nucleus": "the scientia sexualis that emerged in the nineteenth century kept as its nucleus the singular ritual of obligatory and exhaustive confession." Its most consequential product is **"sexuality" itself**, understood not as a natural given but as "the correlative of that slowly developed discursive practice which constitutes the scientia sexualis" (attributed) — a point that anchors [[deployment-of-sexuality]]. The *ars/scientia* dichotomy has been much criticized as an Orientalizing over-simplification (see Critiques).

## Operationalizations

Foucault lists **five mechanisms** by which confession was made scientific in the nineteenth century (attributed):
1. a **clinical codification of the inducement to speak** (interrogation, questionnaire, hypnosis, free association);
2. the postulate of a **general and diffuse causality** — sex as "a cause of any and everything";
3. the principle of a **latency** intrinsic to sexuality (a truth hidden even from the subject);
4. the **method of interpretation** — a "hermeneutic function" held by the listener; and
5. the **medicalization** of confession's effects, placing sex "under the rule of the normal and the pathological."

He argues this discourse was scientifically weak relative to the biology of reproduction, and functioned "not to state the truth but to prevent its very emergence," and that it "justified the racisms of the state... grounded them in 'truth'" (see [[biopower]]). Charcot's Salpêtrière is his example of "an immense apparatus for producing truth."

## Applications

Central to [[power-knowledge]] (the confessor as "master of truth") and to the account of how modern selfhood is organized around sex. Foucault suggests that, ironically, the Western science of sex generated its own "pleasure of analysis," so that scientia sexualis "has not functioned, at least to a certain extent, as an ars erotica."

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Orientalism** (attributed): the clean *ars erotica* / *scientia sexualis* split has been widely criticized (e.g. by later scholars of non-Western sexualities) for essentializing "the East" as the West's erotic other.
- **Historical accuracy** (attributed): the continuity thesis (confession → clinic) is contested by historians of medicine and psychiatry. Recorded as Foucault's argument.
