# Equality of Conditions

## Definition as Coined

For [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]], the **equality of social conditions**
(*l'égalité des conditions*) is the master social fact of the democratic age — the
progressive disappearance of hereditary rank and of large, fixed differences of standing,
producing a society of roughly comparable, mobile individuals. He opened
*[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]* by naming it the fact that
"struck me more forcibly" than any other in America and whose "extraordinary influence...
upon the progress of society" he took as the generative cause behind law, government,
opinion, and private habit alike (Author's Introduction). It is a condition of *society*,
prior to and larger than any form of government; democracy the political regime is, for
Tocqueville, its expression.

He presented equality's advance as a "providential fact" — universal, durable, and beyond
human power to arrest — tracing a 700-year genealogy of leveling in France (the church
open to all ranks, kings as levelers of nobles, commerce, literacy, printing,
Protestantism, the discovery of America). Crucially, its **political consequences are not
fixed**: equality can incline a people toward the "manly" love of freedom or toward a
"degenerate taste for equality" that prefers "equality in a state of slavery to inequality
in a state of freedom." That bifurcation is the problem the whole book addresses.

## Semantic History

Tocqueville extended the term beyond wealth to intelligence and manners — a "common level"
of knowledge with fewer at either extreme. In later social science the idea splits: into
*stratification* research (measuring inequality of income, wealth, status) and into the
study of *status equality* and citizenship (T. H. Marshall and after). Tocqueville's own
usage is closer to the second — a felt equality of standing and possibility — than to any
measured distribution, which is one reason his "equality" and modern quantified inequality
data do not map onto each other cleanly.

## Operationalizations

Tocqueville did not quantify it; he read it from the absence of hereditary aristocracy,
partible inheritance dissolving landed estates, near-universal white male suffrage (1830),
and social manners. This impressionistic operationalization — and its silence on slavery
and on women as boundaries of the "equal" society — is a standing criticism.

## Applications

The concept anchors the derivations across *Democracy in America*: from equality Tocqueville
deduces [[individualism]], the authority of mass opinion, the [[tyranny-of-the-majority]],
the reliance on [[voluntary-association]] and [[self-interest-properly-understood]], the
softening of [[mores]], and the risk of [[democratic-despotism]]. His closing thesis makes
the stakes explicit: nations "cannot stop social conditions from becoming equal... but they
can determine whether equality can lead to slavery or freedom."

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Boundary problem:** the "equal" society of 1830s America rested on the exclusion of
  the enslaved and of Native peoples (Tocqueville's own race chapter) and on women's
  confinement to a domestic sphere — equality of conditions *among white men*.
- **Unmeasured:** the construct is asserted from observation, not operationalized, making
  it hard to test against distributional data.
- **Single-cause reasoning:** critics note that deriving so much from one master variable,
  especially in Vol. 2, outruns the evidence (see [[tocqueville-democracy-study]]).
