# Communicative Competence

## Definition as Coined

[[hymes-dell|Hymes]] develops **communicative competence** against the Chomskyan ideal of **linguistic competence** (knowledge of an ideal speaker-hearer in a homogeneous community). The fundamental problem of sociolinguistics, he argues, is the competence that enables **conduct and interpretation of speech** in a [[speech-community|speech community]] — not only the ability to produce grammatical sentences (*[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations]]* Chs. 1–2, 4, 10).

Hymes's four-aspect sketch of competence (Ch. 4):

1. **Systemic potential** — what is possible in the formal system
2. **Appropriateness** — what is fitting in context (not a property of sentences alone)
3. **Occurrence** — what is done (probabilistic / distributional)
4. **Feasibility** — what is practicable given means and processing

Creativity, on this view, requires **rules of appropriateness**, not only recursive generative power. "Grammar mastery" is not freedom to speak; knowing when **not** to speak is part of competence. Performance, reconceived, is **accomplishment** (and responsibility, investiture, emergence) — not mere adulteration of ideal competence (Chs. 6–7, 10).

## Semantic History

| Phase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Chomsky *Aspects* (1965) | Competence = grammatical knowledge of ideal speaker-hearer; performance = imperfect use |
| Hymes 1967 ms. / 1970s | Communicative competence = knowledge of use, appropriateness, community norms |
| Applied linguistics & education (1970s–) | Often operationalized as teaching/testing of functional language skills (Canale & Swain and successors) — a pedagogical migration that can thin Hymes's ethnographic force |
| Linguistic anthropology | Remains tied to ethnography of speaking / interactional competence traditions |

Hymes also speaks of **symbolic competence** beyond technical linguistic competence (via Burke; Ch. 7) and of **unequal competence opportunities** as a social-justice stake (Ch. 10).

## Operationalizations

- Ethnographic: what members must know to participate in [[speech-event|speech events]] ([[speaking-model|SPEAKING]] components; norms of interaction and interpretation).
- Developmental: acquisition of communicative competence as socialization, not only grammar acquisition (Ch. 3).
- Educational: bilingual and classroom programs assessed by repertoire and rules of speaking, not only by standard-language grammar (Ch. 5).
- Contrast: [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes|Bernstein's codes]] — Hymes treats elaborated/restricted dimensions as potentially universal taxonomic dimensions, not only British class-linked (Ch. 2).

## The 1972 Volume as Approach

*[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972) builds toward communicative competence as the volume's implicit goal concept without yet fixing the term to a single technical definition: [[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]]'s Introduction frames the book's project as one that "extends the notion of linguistic competence to enquire into the nature of communicative competence," and Hymes's chapter 1 treats knowledge of "ways of speaking" and of speech-event components as what such competence comprises (attributed, both authors).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scope creep:** Once "everything social" is competence, the concept can lose precision (a risk Hymes accepts by making ethnography the method).
- **Idealization residual:** "Knowledge of rules" still idealizes; Hymes counters with unequal ability, participation vs membership, and occurrence/feasibility aspects.
- **Pedagogical capture:** Education research often reduces communicative competence to testable skills, detaching it from community ethnography.
- **Relation to performance:** Reclaiming "performance" for folklore/ethnography (positive competence) conflicts with Chomsky's residual use and with some psycholinguistic uses — productive tension, not settled terminology.


### Gumperz's interactional redefinition (1982)

*(Attributed — [[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]], [[gumperz-discourse-strategies-1982|Discourse Strategies]], Postscript.)* "Communicative competence can be defined in interactional terms as 'the knowledge of linguistic and related communicative conventions that speakers must have to create and sustain conversational cooperation,' and thus involves both grammar and contextualization." Producing grammatical sentences "is common to all who count as speakers of a language or dialect"; what varies is knowledge of [[contextualization-cue|contextualization conventions]] — learned only through prolonged face-to-face contact, hence unevenly distributed along network lines, with direct social-inequality consequences in gatekeeping encounters.

### Bauman & Sherzer 1974 densification

*(Attributed — [[bauman-sherzer-explorations-ethnography-speaking-1974|Bauman & Sherzer 1974]].)* Performance = exercise of competence with community resources in situations. Philips: knowing how/when to participate (including temporal indeterminacy) is part of competence. Sankoff: probabilistic competence from variation data. Hymes “Ways of Speaking” in §VI: competence spans means of speech and speech economy, not grammar alone.
