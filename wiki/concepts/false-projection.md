# False Projection

## Definition as Coined

**False projection** (*falsche Projektion*) is [[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]] and
[[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]]'s term, in the "Elements of Anti-Semitism" excursus of
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*,
for the paranoid structure they place at the root of anti-Semitism and, more
broadly, of the [[instrumental-reason|instrumentally rationalized]] mind. Their
thesis (attributed): "**Anti-Semitism is based on a false projection. It is the
counterpart of true mimesis... it is probably the morbid expression of repressed
mimesis. Mimesis imitates the environment, but false projection makes the
environment like itself**." Where healthy perception returns to the object more than
it receives ("in order to reflect the thing as it is, the subject must return to it
more than he receives from it"), false projection abolishes this reflective moment:
the subject "no longer reflects the object" and so "ceases to reflect upon himself,"
attributing its own forbidden impulses to a chosen victim.

Its key claims (all attributed):

- **Projection as such is normal; its pathology is the absence of reflection.**
  "The morbid aspect of anti-Semitism is not projective behavior as such, but the
  absence from it of reflection." Paranoia is "the dark side of cognition" — the
  same objectifying operation that makes science, arrested and stripped of
  self-correction.
- **The interchangeable victim.** The persecuted are "interchangeable according to
  circumstances—gypsies, Jews, Protestants, Catholics." "There is no genuine
  anti-Semitism, and certainly no such thing as a born anti-Semite"; the Jew is the
  screen onto which a "false social order" projects the destructiveness it cannot
  own.
- **Repressed [[mimesis]].** Civilization forbids the mimetic impulse; the
  anti-Semite both "cannot stand the Jews, yet imitates them" — fascist ritual is
  "the mimesis of mimesis," the organized, licensed release of what is otherwise
  tabooed.
- **"Ticket thinking."** In the seventh thesis (added 1947), Adorno and Horkheimer
  argue that anti-Semitism has become "a plank in the platform" of the "Fascist
  ticket": subjective conviction is replaced by "the predetermined and subjectless
  reflexes of a political party," and "the ticket mentality as such is as
  anti-Semitic as the anti-Semitic ticket." Anti-Semitism "has as much chance in
  areas where there are no Jews as it does... in Hollywood."

## Semantic History

The concept fuses a psychoanalytic account of projection (Freud on paranoia, the
super-ego, and repressed aggression) with the book's epistemology of perception and
its [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]] thesis: fascism is not a relapse *behind*
Enlightenment reason but its limit-case — "its 'irrationalism' is deduced from the
nature of the dominant *ratio* itself." The seventh thesis's "ticket thinking"
anticipates Adorno's later empirical work on prejudice (*The Authoritarian
Personality*, 1950). The account is a philosophical-psychological interpretation,
not a validated theory of prejudice.

## Operationalizations

None in the text; the analysis is philosophical and psychoanalytic. Adorno's later
Berkeley study operationalized authoritarianism via the F-scale, but that is a
separate, empirical project.

## Applications

False projection is the demonstration of the [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]] thesis
in the domain of anti-Semitism and mass violence; it is the negative counterpart of
[[mimesis]] and an application of the pathology of [[instrumental-reason]]. It feeds
the wiki's account of the Frankfurt School's theory of fascism and prejudice. Per
the wiki's source-type handling, this theoretical treatment is **not** used as
empirical evidence on subject-matter pages.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded in attributed voice: critics argue the account is speculative,
untestable, and universalizes a single psychoanalytic mechanism across a
historically specific phenomenon; historians of the Holocaust question the
philosophical deduction of anti-Semitism from "the dominant *ratio*." Defenders read
it as a still-productive account of scapegoating and projective hatred, and note the
seventh thesis's prescience about depersonalized, media-driven prejudice.
