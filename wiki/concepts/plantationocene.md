## Definition as Coined

"Plantationocene" is presented by Donna Haraway (*Staying with the Trouble*, 2016) as one of several proposed names for the current epoch, used alongside "Anthropocene" and "Capitalocene": "hence Anthropocene, Plantationocene, and Capitalocene" *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway attributes the term to a collective conversation — "Anthropologists Are Talking about the Anthropocene," recorded at Aarhus University, Denmark, in October 2014 — whose participants included [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt]], Haraway herself, biologist Scott F. Gilbert, and anthropologists/geographers Nils Bubandt, Noboru Ishikawa, and Kenneth Olwig *(Haraway 2016)*. The term is thus presented as collectively generated rather than the invention of any single author.

## Semantic History

As relayed by Haraway, the Plantationocene names the historical transformation of diverse, human-tended farms and forests into extractive, enclosed systems of forced and slave labor — the plantation form. Scholars in this conversation, per Haraway, note that the slave plantation supplied the organizational model and motor for the later industrial factory system, so that the Plantationocene is not superseded by industrialization but continues through it *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway extends this into the present, treating globalized factory meat production and monocrop agribusiness (she cites oil palm) as contemporary continuations of the plantation logic *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct; used descriptively and polemically, in the same register as [[capitalocene]] and [[anthropocene]], to name a historical-causal emphasis (here: coerced plantation labor and monocultural extraction) rather than to quantify a process.

## Applications

Haraway treats "Plantationocene" as feeding directly into [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]], alongside her own preferred [[chthulucene]] and the [[capitalocene]] framing she credits to Andreas Malm and Jason Moore. ## Critiques and Limitations

As with [[capitalocene]], the term competes for explanatory priority with rival namings of the epoch; Haraway does not adjudicate among them in wiki voice but proposes Chthulucene as a further, needed supplement. The plantation-as-origin-of-industrial-factory-system claim is presented by Haraway as the collective's shared analytic point, attributed to the Aarhus conversation rather than argued independently in this text.
