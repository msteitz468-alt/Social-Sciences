# Bandwidth and Echo

*(Attributed to [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]], *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]* 2005, Ch. 4.)* Two rival accounts of what [[network-closure|network closure]] does to information and reputation:

- **Bandwidth hypothesis** — closure improves information quality/quantity (more redundant channels; better monitoring).
- **Echo hypothesis** — closure amplifies **opinion tone** through etiquette and path-dependent conversation: speakers coordinate emotionally, avoid contradicting the conversation’s tone, and stories echo to extremes of **trust and distrust**.

## Definition as Coined

Burt argues (attributed) that polite conversation is **context-shaped and context-renewing**; in closed networks this produces **echo** — character assassination, groupthink-like rigidity, “ignorant certainty” — not only Coleman-style productive trust. Contradictory predictions of bandwidth vs echo make the pair testable.

## Semantic History

Extends Coleman [[social-capital-coleman|closure]] and reputation mechanisms while supplying Burt’s account of closure’s **failure mode** (organization “arthritis”) complementary to brokerage’s chaos risk (*Brokerage and Closure* Ch. 5).

## Operationalizations

Associations of prior-year closure with trust *and* distrust; network stability/decay patterns; qualitative conversation-analysis citations (Fine, Eder & Enke, Gibson, etc.) as mechanism illustrations.

## Applications

Manager networks; gossip and reputation; organizational inertia; why closed groups resist change even when brokerage would pay.

## Critiques and Limitations

Burt notes bandwidth and echo are often hard to separate empirically; much evidence is correlational within his samples.
