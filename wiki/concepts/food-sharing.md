# Food Sharing

The concept that carrying food — plant food and, especially, meat — back to a shared
**home base** and distributing it among group members was the pivotal behavioral
adaptation separating the genus *Homo* from the australopithecines and from other
primates, and the central mechanism proposed for early hominin sociality. The concept
is central to the [[food-sharing-hypothesis]] as developed by [[isaac-glynn|Glynn
Isaac]] and popularized by [[leakey-richard|Richard Leakey]] and Roger Lewin in
*[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]* (1977).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Leakey & Lewin 1977.)* Leakey & Lewin state the core of the behavioral
difference between australopithecines and early *Homo* is "simple but crucial: *Homo*
established home bases and they shared their food," and that meat as a protein source
is "secondary to the real prime mover... the practice of sharing food within an
organized social group." Elsewhere they write that the core of human social evolution
"was the social group focused on a food-sharing economy: plant foods and meat were
brought to a home base and shared," describing it as the single most important factor
in human emergence, contingent on cooperation. They summarize the claim in its
strongest form: "The key to the first mixed economy was sharing" — a pattern they argue
"shattered the basic primate social pattern."

## Semantic History

*(Attributed — Leakey & Lewin 1977, contrasted with vegetarian/primate baseline.)*
Vegetarian primates, per the authors, typically do not share food because plant items
are small and dispersed — "an individual belonging even to a very cohesive group will
simply pick and eat its own fill." A concentrated food source, above all a large
carcass, creates both the *opportunity* and the *necessity* for sharing. The authors
cite chimpanzees' passive, beggar-driven meat-sharing (after Jane Goodall's field
observations) — non-hunters must beg a share, unlike active provisioning among wild
dogs — as a plausible evolutionary precursor pattern, contrasted with the more limited
and passive sharing of plant food among chimpanzees and baboons. They date the
emergence of meat as a significant dietary component to roughly two to three million
years ago and propose meat-eating led to sharing, which in turn encouraged greater
social cohesion, explicitly flagging the timing of this shift as unresolved: "Precisely
when that cohesion began... remains a mystery."

Two further elaborations recur through the book:
- **Meat as social currency.** Meat distribution — unlike plant-food sharing, which the
  authors say stayed largely within the immediate family — functioned as a form of
  social and political currency, establishing status networks and reciprocal alliance
  in hunter-gatherer societies (attributed).
- **The "carrier bag."** The authors argue a container for transporting gathered plant
  food was a crucial, if archaeologically invisible, invention enabling a stable
  home-base economy, inferring hominins "more than probably" had some such device by
  roughly three million years ago — explicitly noted as unverifiable given the
  non-survival of organic containers.

## Operationalizations

The primary archaeological operationalization offered is the concentration of animal
bones and stone artifacts at "home-base" or "living-floor" sites (e.g., early Koobi
Fora and Olduvai localities), interpreted as evidence that carcass parts were
transported and consumed communally rather than at the kill site — a pattern the
authors contrast with most other carnivores, which do not cache prey near dens. Leakey
& Lewin also propose a **four-level typology of hunting-gathering societies** by degree
of dependence on meat/hunting (attributed, ethnographic-analogy based): Hadza/Paliyans
(low meat, individual foraging); !Kung (30–40% meat, shared, high status stakes);
BaMbuti-type net-hunters (cooperative); and Eskimo groups (near-total meat dependence,
exclusively male-hunted) — correlated with the degree of male social dominance: "the
status of women in these hunting societies is inversely proportional to the amount of
meat hunted."

The authors' own reconstruction of a *Homo habilis*-type camp — sex-differentiated
foraging, food-carrying containers, multi-day home-base occupation — is explicitly
flagged by them as speculative narrative rather than established fact: "All of this is,
of course, a complete fairytale, a fabric of more or less inspired guesses."

## Applications

Food sharing is the organizing concept of the [[food-sharing-hypothesis]] and underlies
the authors' account of the sexual division of labor, group cooperation, and the origin
of language (see [[bipedalism]] for the parallel account of hand-freeing). It is also
deployed as the counter-concept to the [[hunting-hypothesis|"Man the Hunter"]] emphasis
on hunting-as-master-adaptation, and to the "killer ape" reading of human origins (see
[[human-aggression-killer-ape-debate]]): the authors present cooperative sharing, not
predatory aggression, as the trait most strongly selected for in the hominin lineage.
Ethnographic analogy — !Kung, G/wi, Hadza, BaMbuti, Eskimo forager economies — is used
throughout to model plausible sharing patterns for the deep past; this is flagged
explicitly by the source as **analogy, not direct evidence** (see [[hunter-gatherer]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's evidentiary base — concentrated bone/artifact scatters read as
"home-base" living floors — rests on inference from the archaeological record that the
authors themselves characterize as substantially speculative ("a fairytale, a fabric of
more or less inspired guesses"). The timing of the shift from opportunistic to
systematic sharing is stated as unresolved. Post-1977 critiques of the
home-base/food-sharing model exist in the wider literature (notably Lewis Binford's
scavenging-based reanalysis of the same bone assemblages) but fall outside this source
and are not detailed here — see [[food-sharing-hypothesis]] for a brief note. The
concept also competes directly with the [[hunting-hypothesis]]'s emphasis on hunting
itself (rather than sharing) as the master adaptation.
