# Antagonistic Coöperation

## Definition as Coined

[[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]] coined **antagonistic coöperation** in
*[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* (§21): *"the combination of two persons or groups to
satisfy a great common interest while minor antagonisms of interest which exist between
them are suppressed."* He reached it by refusing the reduction of the
[[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]] to pure competition: *"Combination and
coöperation are so fundamentally necessary that even very low life forms are found in
symbiosis."* His illustrations run from desert commensalism (the saguaro and its
"commensals," McGee's "solidarity of life" in the Arizona desert) to the Saharan townsmen
and nomads who are *"enemies by caste and race, but allies in interest,"* to political
parties, which *"can suppress minor differences, and combine for what they think most
essential"* (§21).

Sumner rated it *"the most productive form of combination in high civilization... a high
action of the reason to overlook lesser antagonisms in order to work together for great
interests"* (§21). It is the mechanism by which the [[in-group-out-group|in-group]] holds
together despite internal rivalry, and by which civil
institutions replace warfare with "system" (§56).

## Semantic History

The phrase entered the sociological lexicon and anticipated later analyses of cooperation
under conflict: coalition theory, Coser's functions of social conflict, and — in spirit —
game-theoretic cooperation among self-interested parties. It corrects the caricature of
Sumner as a pure competitive Darwinist: he explicitly held that *"competition and
combination are two forms of life association which alternate"* (§21), and used the concept
against what he called *"many socialistic fallacies"* as well as against a naïve war-of-all
reading of nature.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Sumner identified antagonistic coöperation qualitatively, wherever parties
suppress lesser antagonisms to combine for a larger shared interest. Its nearest
formalized descendants are coalition theory and game-theoretic models of cooperation among
self-interested actors, which supply the payoff structures Sumner only described.

## Applications

- The cohesion of interest-based coalitions (parties, alliances, contract relations)
  despite residual antagonism.
- The internal solidarity of the [[in-group-out-group|we-group]] under external pressure.
- The formation of civil institutions out of the [[mores]] as "regulative processes... by
  which warfare is replaced by system" (§56).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Descriptive, not predictive.** The concept labels a pattern but specifies no conditions
  under which antagonism will be suppressed rather than break the coalition.
- **Elides power.** Which "minor" antagonisms get suppressed, and in whose favor, is a
  question of power the concept leaves untouched.
