# Ideal Worker Norm

The **ideal worker norm** is the cultural and organizational image of the preferred employee as fully available for work, free of primary daily care responsibilities — a figure that overlaps more with stereotypic men than with stereotypic women as primary caregivers. Central to gendered-organization theory (Acker) and work–family scholarship (Williams); synthesized into [[gender-as-primary-frame]] by Ridgeway (2011).

## Definition as Coined

Ridgeway (citing Acker 1990, 2006; Williams 2000; Gorman 2005) argues employers develop a shared image of the **preferred or ideal worker** that guides job design, advertising, and evaluation. When gender is effectively salient, that image is infused with cultural gender beliefs. Structural example: the traditional 40-hour Monday–Friday workweek embeds the assumption that ideal workers lack direct daily responsibility for dependent children (Acker via Ridgeway 2011).

## Semantic History

Acker’s “disembodied” worker and gendered organization; Williams’s ideal-worker critique of employment law and practice. Ridgeway nests both under the primary-frame account: ideal-worker images are how gender beliefs get **written into** procedures that then bias outcomes even when actors deny personal prejudice.

## Operationalizations

- Job descriptions and hiring criteria coded for agentic/male-typed vs communal/female-typed traits.
- Work-time structures (full-time continuous careers; promotion clocks during prime childbearing years in law/academy).
- Experimental preferred-worker trait ratings by job sex-type.

## Applications

Demand-side job matching (who is seen as fit); [[maternal-wall]]; devaluation of care-typed jobs; embedding gender into **new** jobs under uncertainty (e.g., early Internet-era roles) when preferred-worker images are still plastic (Ridgeway 2011 Ch. 4).

## Critiques and Limitations

Ideal-worker content varies by class and sector; some professional cultures valorize long hours more than others. Intersection with race and citizenship is under-specified in the 2011 synthesis.
