## Definition as Coined

Blom and Gumperz, in "Social Meaning in Linguistic Structures: Code-Switching in Norway" (1972), distinguished two functionally distinct types of alternation between the local Norwegian dialect (Ranamål, "R") and the national standard (Bokmål, "B") in the town of Hemnesberget: **situational switching**, alternation that redefines the situation — a change in governing norms, participants, and/or interactional strategies (e.g., a teacher shifting from B to R to invite open classroom discussion); and **metaphorical switching**, alternation that "enriches" an unchanged situation by alluding to more than one social relationship or topic, without redefining participants' rights or obligations (e.g., a clerk and a customer interjecting dialect greetings into an otherwise standard-language official transaction).

## Semantic History

The concept as stated in this 1972 chapter does not yet use the later Gumperz phrase "we-code/they-code," though the same value symbolism is functionally present: R is described as symbolizing membership in the local "team" (hræmnesværing, "Hemnes resident") and egalitarian, kin/friendship-based local identity, while B symbolizes non-local, official, national, or class-differentiated status (using B casually among locals is derided as å snakke fint, "to put on airs"). The wiki flags this distinction for future correction: the exact terms "we-code" and "they-code" belong to a later Gumperz publication and should not be dated to this 1972 chapter.

## Operationalizations

Blom and Gumperz operationalized the distinction through participant observation combined with structured linguistic elicitation and an experimental component: self-recruited friendship and kin groups were recorded in natural "friendly gatherings," yielding four groups along a gradient of local-team identification. Groups I and II (locally rooted craftsmen and kin) showed only situational switching, with strict adherence to co-occurrence rules. Group III (former locals now studying at university) showed both situational and metaphorical switching with breakdown of co-occurrence rules — a gradient mixing of standard and dialect features correlated with an acquired "intellectual" status identity — while these same speakers, interviewed conventionally, self-identified as "pure dialect speakers," unaware of their own recorded switching behavior. Group IV (a family that normally speaks B even in-group) showed the reverse pattern, inserting R forms metaphorically "to provide local color."

## Applications

The Hemnesberget study ([[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]) is the concept's founding application, explicitly framed as a test of Bernstein's (1961, 1964) hypothesis that social relationships act as intervening variables between linguistic structure and speech. The metaphorical/situational distinction is drawn in explicit parallel to T/V pronoun alternation (per Friedrich's chapter, elsewhere in the same volume, on Russian literature).

## Critiques and Limitations

The study itself records a validation problem: when recordings were played back to a local linguistic informant, he initially refused to believe the mixed-code conversations were recorded locally, then expressed disapproval on recognizing the switching, labeling it *knot* ("artificial speech") — participants promised to stop switching, but later recordings showed the same pattern recurring under topic pressure. This is recorded in the source as a reflexivity/validation episode, not resolved further.
