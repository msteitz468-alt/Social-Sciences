# Opposition (Blau)

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] defines **opposition** generically: "the distinctive characteristic of social opposition is organized collective dissent and action against an established order." It is not confined to revolutionary politics — new artistic styles, new scholarly schools, innovating firms, reform movements, stockholder revolts, and union-leadership challenges are all opposition in Blau's sense. Opposition arises when subordinates "experience shared feelings of exploitation and oppression," judged against norms of fairness: power derived from supplying needed benefits is tolerated while returns meet the fair rate, but demands that "far exceed what social norms define as fair" evoke the desire to retaliate and, collectively organized, opposition.

Blau's most distinctive claims:
- **Revolutionary ideology as "social surplus."** Severe deprivation, like an excess of reward, creates a social surplus; a revolutionary ideology converts it into opposition "by making exploited and oppressed groups less dependent on material rewards and thus freeing their energies to fight existing powers."
- **Opposition as a regenerative force.** Opposition "introjects new vitality into a social structure"; it is "a catalyst or starting mechanism of social change" against the rigidities (vested powers, institutionalized values) that otherwise block adjustment — a Keynesian "external investment" reactivating a stagnant structure.
- **Expressive, not merely instrumental.** Against a purely rational (Downsian) model of politics, Blau holds support for radical parties with no chance of victory "express[es] antagonism against existing powers" and gratifies the desire to retaliate — which is why rational-choice models "cannot account for the growth of opposition parties."

## Semantic History

Blau synthesizes Simmel and Coser (conflict as sociation; ideology stripping the guilt from aggression), Marx (revolutionary ideology; the world-wide reach of Marx's program as the exemplary opposition ideal — see [[mediating-values]]), and mid-century political sociology (Lipset, Downs, Duverger, Michels) into an exchange-theoretic account of dissent as the fourth facet of social structure.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by movement and party formation and by ideological incentives (Clark and Wilson's material/solidary/purposive typology — small opposition parties depend disproportionately on purposive/ideological incentives). Blau distinguishes **leftist extremism** (a reaction to absolute economic deprivation of the poorest strata) from **rightist extremism** (a reaction to *relative* [[relative-deprivation|status deprivation]] of threatened middle strata), drawing on Lipset's studies of Nazism, Poujadism, and McCarthyism — a [POSITION] attributed to Lipset and Blau.

## Applications

The fourth of Blau's facets of social structure; the retaliatory counterpart to [[legitimation]] in the [[imbalance-of-exchange|reciprocity/imbalance]] dynamic; the driver of [[dialectic-of-social-change|dialectical change]]; and, channeled through [[cross-cutting-conflicts|cross-cutting affiliations]], the force whose moderation or intensity determines the stability of democracy.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The generic definition (any organized dissent) is capacious to the point of blurring artistic innovation and armed revolution; its analytic payoff is the shared *form*, at the cost of specificity.
- The leftist/rightist deprivation mapping is a mid-century empirical generalization (Lipset) that later research has qualified.
