# Tactical Humanism

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to [[abu-lughod-lila|Lila Abu-Lughod]] in the introduction to *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* 1993.)* **Tactical humanism** is a deliberate, limited use of humanist narrative conventions — particular lives, recognizable emotions, dailiness, shared complexity — as a **political tactic** against anthropological othering, without reclaiming a naive universal human essence or abandoning feminist and poststructural critique.

Her definitional formulation: humanism remains "the language of human equality with the greatest moral force" in the West even after poststructural discrediting; what is required is "a tactical humanism, made politically useful but also limited in its effects by anthropology's location on the side of power in a world organized unequally along lines of 'cultural' difference."

## Semantic History

**Why humanism despite discredit.** Abu-Lughod listed (attributed) standard critiques: humanism masks systematic social difference behind an allegedly universal individual; centers "man" over nature; smuggles culturally specific traits as "essential human"; ignores subjects as constructed in power-laden discourses. Clifford's critique of Said for lapsing into humanism is noted as emblematic of how damning the association became.

**Why not abandon it.** Because experimental posthumanist forms (pastiche, dialogue, collage that break narrative and block identification) demand sophisticated readers; if experimental ethnographies are solipsistic or hard to read, political and human messages fail in a world still full of prejudice against those written about. Ethnographies of the particular supply a "discourse of familiarity" that professional social science withholds from "outsiders."

**Relation to [[writing-against-culture]].** Storytelling against generalization is the main tactic; tactical humanism names the ethical-political wager behind choosing familiar narrative over purely experimental forms. Both answer the [[writing-culture-debate]] and [[feminist-anthropology]]'s awkward relation to ethnography (Strathern).

**Limits she states.** No illusion of universal good; humanism is local post-Enlightenment Western language, useful because the anthropologist works as a Westerner contributing to Western discourse; positionality cannot be escaped (halfie dilemmas of speaking for, to, and from); Riesman's point that researchers use people for goals they would never imagine still holds.

## Operationalizations

Techniques Abu-Lughod adopted (attributed):

1. **Traces of the ethnographer** without total erasure (contra Atiya, Friedl, Munson style) or equal-participant imposition (contra Dwyer, Lavie dialogic extremes).
2. **Chapters loosely centered on one or two individual women** (Migdim, Gateefa, Kamla) to encourage familiarity.
3. **Everyday stories, conversations, songs, arguments** rather than only high-status "culture" texts.
4. Acknowledgment that this alternative tradition (often women's ethnography — Fernea, M. Wolf, E. Turner; pseudonymous professionals) was under-recognized by *Writing Culture* as textual innovation.

## Applications

## Critiques and Limitations

Self-limited by design: does not claim to fix global power; may still betray hosts by publishing personal talk; subjects may prefer formal images and memorable poems to candid stories; critics of humanism may read the move as theoretically soft. Abu-Lughod's own "For Whom?" section treats reception by Bedouin hosts as open and uneasy.
