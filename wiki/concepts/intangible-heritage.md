# Intangible Heritage

## Definition as Coined

**Intangible heritage** in international policy denotes living practices, representations, expressions, knowledge, and skills (and associated objects/spaces) that communities recognize as cultural heritage — formalized especially in UNESCO's **2003 Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage** and earlier folklore/masterpieces programs.

**[[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]]** (*[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* 2006) uses intangibility more radically: not a residual category beside "real" material heritage, but the claim that **heritage is cultural process** — acts of remembering, performing, and negotiating identity — so that **"all heritage is ultimately intangible"** even when material sites are important cultural tools *(attributed)*.

## Semantic History

- Western AHD historically privileged **tangible** monuments; folklore and ethnographic categories held "intangible" at the margins.
- UNESCO instruments (Recommendation on Traditional Culture and Folklore; Masterpieces proclamations; 2003 Convention; Living Human Treasures) institutionalize intangible heritage while, Smith argues, still negotiating AHD universals and expert listing logics.
- Smith's process definition aims to de-privilege fabric so that memory, performance, dissonance, and place come into view.

## Operationalizations

- UNESCO listing and safeguarding programs.
- Waanyi Women's History Project: oral history in country as heritage *act*, not only site recording.
- Castleford: festivals, networking, everyday community interaction as heritage practice.
- Industrial museums: remembering work experience and class identity.

## Applications

Bridge between museum/folklore policy and critical heritage theory; challenges [[authorized-heritage-discourse]] materialism; densifies [[heritage]], [[performance]], [[social-memory]], [[place-as-lived]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Policy "intangible heritage" can still reify listed practices and expert/state mediation. Smith's "all heritage is intangible" is a theoretical move — contested by conservation professions for whom fabric remains primary. Record both registers; do not collapse policy category into Smith's ontology in wiki voice.

## Meskell 2012 — intangible heritage as a road not taken (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[meskell-lynn|Lynn Meskell]], *[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|The Nature of Heritage]]* 2012.)* Meskell points to **indigenous knowledge and intangible heritage** as a potential alternative to the nature/culture binary that structures South African heritage management — implemented at some Southern African rock-art sites (Deacon and colleagues) but with negligible uptake across national parks and mainstream archaeology. In [[kruger-national-park|Kruger]] the opposite logic prevailed: San people were at points administratively classified as part of the "fauna" (1930s–40s Kalahari Gemsbok Park), the inverse of recognizing living intangible heritage.
