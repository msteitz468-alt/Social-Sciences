# Tertius Gaudens

*(Attributed.)* **"The third who benefits"** — the party who profits from being
positioned between two others who are divided or in competition. [[simmel-georg|
Georg Simmel]] named the figure; [[burt-ronald-s|Ronald Burt]] rebuilt it as the
active role at the heart of his [[structural-holes-theory|structural-holes
theory]]: the broker who turns [[structural-holes|structural holes]] into
information and control benefits.

## Definition as Coined

Burt takes the term directly from Simmel: "The tertius gaudens is 'the third who
benefits' (Simmel, 1923)." The idea survives in folk speech — the Italian proverb
*"Fra i due litiganti, il terzo gode"* and the Dutch *"de lachende derde,"* the
laughing third. Burt pairs it with **entrepreneur**: "entrepreneur refers to a
kind of behavior; the tertius is a *successful* entrepreneur" — a person "who
generates profit from being between others."

## Semantic History

A **migrated** term: Simmel's descriptive sociology of triads becomes, in Burt, a
formal network mechanism. Burt routes it through [[merton-robert-k|Merton]]'s
**role-set**: a person subject to conflicting demands can make those demands
explicit and so, quoting Merton, "originally at the focus of the conflict,
virtually becomes a more or less influential bystander whose function it is to
high-light the conflicting demands … and to make it a problem for them, rather
than for him, to resolve their contradictory demands." Burt also cites Simmel's
**"divide and rule"** (Incan and Venetian governments manufacturing conflict among
subjects). In Chapter 7 he adds "**the other tertius**" — the broker who does not
merely exploit existing holes but *manufactures* them or neutralizes the
constraint of their absence through strategic embedding.

## Operationalizations

Burt distinguishes **two tertius strategies**:

1. **Third between competitors** for the same relationship — a buyer playing two
   sellers, "a woman with multiple suitors or a professor with simultaneous
   offers."
2. **Third between conflicting demands** in separate relations — a subordinate
   under two superiors; a Mertonian role-set.

The strength of tertius control is governed by **secondary structural holes**: a
threat to take one's business elsewhere works only if (a) replacement contacts
exist and (b) those replacements are *not* connected to each other (a "market" of
disconnected alternatives gives leverage; a "clique" of colluding ones does not).
The formal leverage measure is [[network-constraint|network constraint]].

## Applications

- The mechanism behind the **control benefits** of holes in every application of
  [[structural-holes-theory]] — market negotiation, managerial advancement,
  political control (Burt's reading of Barkey's 17th-century France/Turkey
  comparison, where the Ottoman state co-opted rival provincial leaders while the
  French crown provoked alliances against itself).
- **"The essential tension."** Burt argues the tertius benefit rests not on
  hostility between the other parties but merely on *uncertainty* about whose
  preferences dominate a relationship: "No tension, no tertius."

## Critiques and Limitations

The tertius is the point where the [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate|closure–brokerage
debate]] bites: the same broker Burt casts as the beneficiary of disconnection is,
in [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman]]'s account, precisely the actor whom network
**closure** exists to constrain (closure enforces norms and trust by *removing*
the third party's room to manoeuvre). Whether the tertius role reliably pays is
also contingent on the broker's legitimacy — see
[[burt-structural-holes-study|the manager study]].
