# The Great Refusal

## Definition as Coined

**The Great Refusal** is [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s name for "the protest against that which is" — the negation of the established order that survives, in a [[one-dimensionality|one-dimensional society]], chiefly in aesthetic and existential rather than organized-political form. In *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964) he first locates it in art: "art contains the rationality of negation. In its advanced positions, it is the Great Refusal — the protest against that which is" — the "promesse de bonheur" that keeps unrealized possibilities visible.

At the book's close, facing the absorption of the working class (see [[revolutionary-subject-debate]]), Marcuse locates the *social* bearers of refusal not in the integrated majority but in a "substratum" outside the system (attributed): "underneath the conservative popular base is the substratum of the outcasts and outsiders, the exploited and persecuted of other races and other colors, the unemployed and the unemployable. They exist outside the democratic process... Thus their opposition is revolutionary even if their consciousness is not." He offers no guarantee — "It is nothing but a chance" — and ends on Walter Benjamin's line, "It is only for the sake of those without hope that hope is given to us."

## Semantic History

The phrase originates in Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization* (1955), where it named Eros's refusal of surplus-repression; *One-Dimensional Man* transfers it from a psychoanalytic to a political-aesthetic register. After 1968 the Great Refusal became a watchword of the New Left and of movements — student, Black liberation, anti-colonial, feminist — that seemed to embody Marcuse's "substratum"; his later *An Essay on Liberation* (1969) developed the theme with more hope than the 1964 ending allowed.

## Operationalizations

None; the concept is normative and diagnostic. Its "evidence," on Marcuse's account, is aesthetic (avant-garde art that names "the things that are absent") and social-structural (the standpoint of the excluded), not behavioural.

## Applications

The Great Refusal is the hinge between Marcuse's diagnosis ([[one-dimensionality]], [[technological-rationality]]) and his alternative ([[pacification-of-existence]]); it is the crux of the [[revolutionary-subject-debate|debate over the revolutionary subject]] and a foundational reference for theories of art-as-resistance and for New Left political thought.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue (attributed) that locating revolutionary hope in a marginal "substratum" is romantic and empirically weak — the outcasts lack the structural leverage Marx ascribed to the proletariat — and that an "absolute refusal" with "no concepts which could bridge the gap between the present and its future" is politically impotent by Marcuse's own admission. Orthodox Marxists read it as abandoning the working class; sympathizers read the post-1968 movements as partial vindication. Recorded in attributed voice.
