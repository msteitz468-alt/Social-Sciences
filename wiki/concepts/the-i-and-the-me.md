# The "I" and the "Me"

## Definition as Coined

For [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], the self has two distinguishable phases. The **"me"** is "the organized set of attitudes of others which one himself assumes" — the self as socially constituted object, the [[generalized-other|generalized other]] taken into the individual. The **"I"** is "the response of the organism to the attitudes of the others" — the acting subject's reply to the socially given "me." "Through taking [others'] attitudes we have introduced the 'me' and we react to it as an 'I'" (attributed).

The two are phases of a single process, not substances: "The 'I' both calls out the 'me' and responds to it. Taken together they constitute a personality as it appears in social experience... If it did not have these two phases there could not be conscious responsibility, and there would be nothing novel in experience."

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| William James, Principles (1890) | The "I" (self-as-knower) vs. the "Me" (self-as-known) — a distinction Mead adapts and transforms |
| Mead, 1913 / 1934 | The "I" as the organism's novel response; the "me" as the internalized social attitudes — a dialectic of freedom and social control |
| Mead's own manuscripts (Supplementary Essays) | Framed as "biologic individual" (subject) vs. "self" (object); the terms "I"/"me" appear only rarely there |

Mead's Supplementary Essays (his own prose) largely use "biologic individual" and "self" rather than "I"/"me"; the sharp "I"/"me" vocabulary is most developed in the edited main text, so the terminological consistency across Mead's corpus is itself qualified (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Not measured. The "I" is methodologically elusive by definition: it "never appears directly in experience" but only in memory, "as a historical figure" — "The 'I' of this moment is present in the 'me' of the next moment." Its response to the "me" is inherently unpredictable: "what that response will be he does not know and nobody else knows."

## Applications

- **Freedom, novelty, creativity.** "The 'I' gives the sense of freedom, of initiative." Mead grounds its novelty in the philosophical notion of **emergence** (water from hydrogen and oxygen): the "me" supplies common social form, the "I" brings "something that was not there before." Artistic and scientific creation, and reform figures (Mead names Jesus, Buddha, Socrates, Einstein), exemplify the "I" reorganizing the community.
- **Social control.** "If we use a Freudian expression, the 'me' is in a certain sense a censor"; "Social control is the expression of the 'me' over against the expression of the 'I.'" Where the "me"'s structure fails to control (impulsive or mob conduct), the "I" dominates unpredictably.
- **The capstone of the whole work.** Mead's final formula: "Thinking is simply the reasoning of the individual, the carrying-on of a conversation between... the 'I' and the 'me'."

## Critiques and Limitations

- The "I"'s methodological inaccessibility (knowable only retrospectively) has drawn the charge that it is a residual, unanalyzable posit rather than an explanatory term.
- The reliance on the edited main text for the "I"/"me" vocabulary means the concept's sharpest formulations are editorially mediated.
