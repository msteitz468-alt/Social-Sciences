# Empire of Domination

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]*, 1986, esp. Chs. 5, 8–9) uses **empire of domination** for imperial forms centered on military-political domination and [[compulsory-cooperation]] over multi-power or peripheral zones — pioneered by semiperipheral "marcher lords," with a mobile army more than a fixed territorial core. It is the pole opposite [[multi-power-actor-civilization]] and, in Mann's typology, distinct from the later **territorial empire** (Rome as first clear case of the latter under agrarian logistics). Through compulsory-cooperation techniques, he argues, the leading edge of power shifted from multi-power-actor civilizations to empires of domination.

Four strategies of rule (restated Ch. 8 from Ch. 5): rule through conquered elites; rule through the army; mixture of militarized-economy compulsory cooperation; beginnings of a diffused upper-class culture.

## Semantic History

Mann's narrative typology within the [[iemp-model]], not a single Assyriological taxon. Ch. 1 already names the geopolitical dual: hegemonic empire dominating marcher/neighboring clients vs multistate civilization. "Revitalized empires of domination" (Ch. 8: Assyria, Persia) continue the type after Iron Age diversification, polar opposite classical Greece. Comparative sociology of empires (Eisenstadt, Wittfogel, etc.) captures similarities but, Mann holds, misses the developmental dialectic of compulsory cooperation.

## Operationalizations

Conquest-extraction logistics; limited routine military radius (~80–90 km, Mann); ruling-class military core; client kings/vassals or satraps; deportation and militaristic colonization; high formal [[despotic-power]], typically weak [[infrastructural-power]]; fragile federal control generating private property and decentralizing forces.

Three vulnerability mechanisms (Mann, leading into Ch. 6): no safe resting place for army-made boundaries; delicacy of compulsory-cooperation integration (redistribution and continuous coercive energy); upgrading of outer marches raising "protection rent" and cutting trade profits.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ch. 5** — Akkadian and successor Near Eastern empires; Sargon as personal domination over clients more than territorial control; dialectic of centralization/decentralization as developmental (toward higher collective power), not merely cyclical.
- **Ch. 6–7 context** — Empires under strain meet northern/chariot challenges; Greece and revitalized empire of domination as polar responses to Iron Age conditions; Greek multi-power world lacks central compulsory cooperation.
- **Assyria (Ch. 8)** — Army-rule + compulsory cooperation + exclusive upper-class core "nationalism"; terror for "voluntary" submission; stretched by militaristic rule and undone by exclusive nationalism; two interaction levels (not unitary).
- **Persia** — Loose rule atop cosmopolitanism; high despotic pretensions, feeble infrastructure; international upper-class ideology; ecological diversity bars tight centralization.
- **Rome (Ch. 9)** — Begins as (small) empire of domination via allies and clients; develops into true territorial empire via legionary economy and elite absorption — "the first to become a territorial empire rather than an empire of domination" (Mann).
- **Athens** — Imperialism threatened Near Eastern hegemonic pattern; blocked by multistate resilience (Mann).

## Critiques and Limitations

Ideal types blur in practice; specialist empire studies may reject the label's lumping and mid-1980s secondary logistics. Keep historical case claims attributed.
