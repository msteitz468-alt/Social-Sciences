# Ritual

"Ritual" is anthropology's master category for formalized action, and one of its most heavily theorized. The concept's own history was made an object of analysis by [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] (*Genealogies of Religion*, 1993, chs. 2–4), whose genealogy argues that the modern anthropological concept — ritual as symbolic action to be *read* — displaced an older Christian sense of rite as disciplined practice to be *performed*.

## Definition as Coined

There is no single coinage. The major 20th-century positions, each attributed:

- **Radcliffe-Brown (1939):** ritual acts differ from technical acts "in having in all instances some expressive or symbolic element in them."
- **Leach (1954):** ritual momentarily "make[s] explicit what is otherwise a fiction" — the underlying social order — on a continuum rather than a sacred/profane dichotomy.
- **Douglas (1966, 1970):** ritual is "pre-eminently a form of communication," a "restricted code" correlated with social solidarity.
- **[[turner-victor|Turner]] (1969):** ritual symbols are "a set of evocative devices for rousing, channelling and domesticating powerful emotions."
- **[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] (1980) / Lévi-Strauss (1981):** ritual, like all cultural products, is a text to be read.
- **Durkheim (1915):** ritual periodically restores collective ideals against egoistic passions, on the basis of *homo duplex* (see [[sacred-and-profane]]).

## Semantic History

Asad's genealogy rests on the *Encyclopaedia Britannica*'s entries. The first edition (1771) defined "ritual" as a **book** "directing the order and manner to be observed in celebrating religious ceremonies... in a particular church" — a script regulating practice, complementary to "rite" (the particular manner of celebrating divine service in a given country). The entry persisted through the seventh edition (1852), then vanished; when a wholly new entry appeared in the eleventh edition (1910), citing Tylor, Frazer, Robertson Smith, Hubert and Mauss, ritual had become a universal type of symbolic behavior found in all religions and even in secular life — "no longer a script for regulating practice but a type of practice that is interpretable as standing for some further verbally definable, but tacit, event" (Asad 1993).

Asad argues this shift secularized theological materials: the "outward sign"/"inward meaning" distinction is ancient theological equipment, and the anthropologist's practice of authoritative symbolic decoding descends from Christian exegesis — "Anthropologists have... incorporated a theological preoccupation into an avowedly secular intellectual task." He further argues (via Bacon's essays on simulation and the Renaissance court's split between private essence and public representation) that behavioral signs became conceptually detachable from what they represent in early modernity, a precondition for reading behavior as a mask and life as "readable text."

The suppressed older sense — ritual as **apt performance** within a disciplinary program — is documented from the Rule of St. Benedict, where liturgy is "a practice among others essential to the acquisition of Christian virtues," involving "not symbols to be interpreted but abilities to be acquired" (see [[discipline]]).

## Operationalizations

Symbolist traditions operationalize ritual by identifying symbols and eliciting or constructing their meanings (see [[symbolic-exegesis]]); practice-oriented and Asadian approaches instead examine the disciplinary programs, authorizing discourses, and institutional conditions under which rites form dispositions. Asad cites Gell's Umeda fieldwork (1975) — no informant willing to discuss what symbols "mean," and no Umeda word for "stand for" — as a warning that the symbolic construal is often the ethnographer's, not the community's.

## Applications

Asad's own application (chs. 3–4) analyzes medieval judicial ordeal, torture, and penance as distinct rituals of truth: the ordeal regulated conflict between equals and inscribed truth directly on the body; inquisitorial torture "hunted" verbal truth under a doctrine that truth must be freely confessed; sacramental penance (mandatory private confession from the Fourth Lateran Council, 1215) created the truthful conscience. Against triumphalist histories of the ordeal's decline he argues "the reforming Church did not rediscover rationality, it redefined it," using [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s Azande oracle analysis as leverage. Against [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s assimilation of torture, duel, and ordeal into one "ritual" category, he insists the differences between rituals are what explain what each enables.

## Critiques and Limitations

Asad is "skeptical of ritual as the object of a general theory" and rejects the idea that ritual as such encodes special meaning; he closes by denying that ritual stands universally opposed to ordinary/pragmatic behavior — how domains of life are articulated is a historical question, not a definitional one. Symbolist and communication-theoretic accounts (Douglas, Turner, Tambiah) remain live positions; this page records them side by side without adjudication.

## Bradley 1998 — public ritual time and domestication of ritual (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* operationalizes **public ritual** for multi-phase monuments via Bloch (formalised communication; ritual time merging past/present; stability enabling political change) and Barrett (conversation vs reading a text). Monuments, art styles, and hoard deposition as long-lived ritual evidence on Braudel's *longue durée*. Later prehistory: **domestication of ritual** — ceremonial focus moves from specialised circular monuments into houses, settlement boundaries, and grain-storage pits (human remains with domestic deposits). See [[monumentality]], [[social-memory]], [[ritual-landscape]].

## Whittle 1996 — gatherings, sharing, sacred imperatives (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]* 1996; esp. Mediterranean Ch. 9 and Ch. 10 synthesis.)*

- **Ritual as community re-creation:** Joint ritual with feasting and food/drink sharing is load-bearing for continually re-created Neolithic community under mobility; aggregation arenas (e.g. Villeneuve-Tolosane / Saint-Michel-du-Touch heated stones + cattle bones; Ripoli pits and ditch segments with burials; Tavoliere and Languedoc enclosure traditions) as seasonal/year-to-year social interaction more than permanent villages.
- **Pottery and formal consumption:** Impressed/Cardial pottery initially for serving food and **drink** in special as much as mundane circumstances; bone spoons (Cova de l’Or) may hint formalised consumption; pots as containers of plenty for sharing ideals presumed in late foragers.
- **Sacred imperatives (closing program):** With new senses of beginnings, descent, and time came sacred sanctions from a world older than humans; ancestors/spirits underwrite cooperation, participation, integration, generosity, hospitality, prowess. Ideals mediate rivalry even where practice includes violence. Processual and Marxist/post-processual accounts that prioritise material-resource management alone are “too limited because beliefs and ideals are excluded” (his critique of the theoretical climate — dialogue partner to [[bradley-richard]]-style landscape/meaning programs).
- **Copper Age enclosures:** [[los-millares]] walls/tombs as place-taking and communal identity statements rather than pure military ritual of ranked elites (position).

See [[social-memory]], [[impresso-cardial-culture]], [[agricultural-revolution]].
