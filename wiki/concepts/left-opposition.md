# Left Opposition

## Definition as Coined

The **Left Opposition** designates the principal intra-party challenge to the emerging Stalin leadership in the Soviet Communist Party from the 1923 scissors crisis through expulsion after the fifteenth party congress (December 1927). Its core figure is [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]]; allies shifted over time (platform of the 46; later united opposition with Zinoviev and Kamenev). Documented here from [[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] 1979 (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*).

## Semantic History

- **October 1923:** Trotsky's CC letter denouncing "flagrant radical errors of economic policy"; platform of the 46; last open *Pravda* debate before the machine closed ranks (Carr, Ch. 6–8).
- **1924–25:** Defeat of Trotskyist wing; *Lessons of October*; removal as War Commissar; Eastman affair/disavowal; Zinoviev's Leningrad opposition later broke with Stalin and fused with Trotsky in the **united opposition** (1926).
- **1926–27:** Declaration of the 83; critiques of kulak-oriented agrarian policy and [[socialism-in-one-country]]; China policy after Chiang's anti-communist turn; OGPU action against illicit opposition press; expulsions; Trotsky to Alma-Ata (Carr, Ch. 12).
- **Ironic afterlife (Carr):** Policies the opposition had demanded on industrialization tempos were partly adopted **after** their expulsion — while the political monopoly that crushed them hardened.

## Operationalizations

Factional membership is not a survey variable; historical markers include signed platforms, congress votes, expulsions, and exile. Carr emphasizes the **secretarial appointment regime** and atrophy of open CC debate as structural advantages of the majority ([[bureaucracy]], [[soviet-central-committee]], [[circular-flow-of-power]]).

## Applications

- Organizational prehistory of international Trotskyism and of [[russian-question-debate|the Russian Question]].
- Case of how [[democratic-centralism]] and the 1921 faction ban (tenth congress) constrained legitimate dissent.
- Pair with Right opposition (Bukharin, Rykov, Tomsky) — defeated later, after the Left (Carr Ch. 17).

## Critiques and Limitations

Carr writes from institutional-political economy, not as a Trotskyist or Stalinist partisan; still a secondary narrative. For Trotsky's self-understanding see Service biography and primary Trotsky texts (partially ingested). Do not collapse Workers' Opposition (1920–21) or 1918 Left Communists into the mid-1920s Left Opposition without dating.
