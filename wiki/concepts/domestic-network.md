# Domestic Network

## Definition as Coined

In *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]*, [[stack-carol|Stack]] uses **domestic network** for the cooperating cluster of people who share the work of subsistence and child-rearing across multiple dwellings. Unlike an ego-centered [[personal-kindred]], a domestic network is **not** centered on one person: several participants organize exchanges. Unlike a "domestic group," it need not share a single residential boundary. Stack notes Valentines' related "inter-domicile" language for similar phenomena.

## Semantic History

Stack substitutes network analysis for household-type labels (including inaccurate [[matrifocality]] uses) when describing urban Black domestic organization. Later poverty research inherits "kin networks" language; Desmond's [[disposable-ties]] mark a different network form under eviction crisis.

## Operationalizations

Genealogical charts of who swaps with whom; residence histories; observation of daily/hourly exchanges; case studies (e.g. Magnolia and Calvin Waters network in The Flats).

## Applications

[[stack-all-our-kin-study]]; cross-link to [[the-flats]]. Comparative caution: not identical to middle-class kinship visiting or to corporate descent groups.

## Critiques and Limitations

Construct is etic-analytic even when grounded in emic "those you count on." Risk of over-stability: networks recombine; Stack documents conflict and exclusion as well as cooperation.
