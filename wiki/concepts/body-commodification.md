# Body Commodification

## Definition as Coined

In the framing of *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (eds. [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] and [[wacquant-loic|Wacquant]], 2002; orig. *Body & Society* 2001), **body commodification** names capitalized economic relations between humans in which **human bodies are the token of economic exchanges that are often masked as something else** — love, altruism, pleasure, kindness (Scheper-Hughes, introduction, attributed). The concept is deliberately **broad**: kidney sales, tissue and cell-line alienation, sperm banking’s “gift” rhetoric, mishandled corpses as objects of legal redress, forensic and media traffic in disaster dead, sex work, and prizefighting as sale of the body in parts or whole.

Scheper-Hughes insists the academic body-as-text must be returned to the body as tangible, alienable material, while retaining Marx/Taussig’s “phantom objectivity” of commodities — bodies as both objects and semi-magical fetishes of desire (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Not a single metric. Indicators used across the volume:

- Price schedules and brokerage (kidneys: e.g. Chennai ~32,500 rupees / ~$1,200 as of Cohen’s interviews reported by Scheper-Hughes; Israeli transplant packages $180–200k with donor fees ≤$5,000 — Scheper-Hughes, attributed)
- Gift/altruism rhetoric that conceals payment (organs; semen banks — Tober)
- Population split into organ **givers** vs **receivers** (Scheper-Hughes)
- Patenting of cell lines/genes; donors lose property rights unless special agreement (Lock: Guaymi, Hagahai, Solomon Islands cases)
- Compensation claims over mishandled corpses (Brandes)
- Media/forensic “value” of dead bodies (Klinenberg on 1995 Chicago heat wave)
- Gendered moral economies of sex work (Epele) and prizefighting vernacular of whores/slaves/stallions (Wacquant)

## Applications

- **[[human-organs-trade]]** — Organs Watch multi-sited project; [[ethics-of-parts]] (Cohen); commodity fetishism of the kidney (Scheper-Hughes)
- **Genetic resources** — Human Genome Diversity Project; immortalized cell lines; TRIPs/WTO IP regimes (Lock)
- **Reproductive markets** — semen as gift and goods; “grass roots eugenics” in purchaser choice; market in altruism (Tober)
- **Death and the body politic** — Yemenite Children Affair forensic DNA/exhumation (Weiss); cremated Guatemalan Catholic worker (Brandes); heat-wave dead as tabloid/forensic spectacle (Klinenberg)
- **Carnal markets** — drug-using sex workers, Mission District (Epele); pugilistic [[bodily-capital]] and collusion via misrecognition (Wacquant)

## Critiques and Limitations

- Neo-classical and some bioethical positions treat body markets as autonomy-enhancing “win-win”; Scheper-Hughes treats that framing as disciplined into consumer globalization (attributed characterization of bioethics, not wiki-voice endorsement of either side).
- Risk of over-extension: the volume itself flags commodification as a problematic, contested concept — not every body practice is market exchange.
- Distinguish **documented sales**, **medically substantiated kidney-theft allegations**, and **organ-theft rumors** as different evidence classes (Scheper-Hughes treats all three as data, for different analytic purposes).
- Do not equate archaeological culture / genes / language / people when reading Lock’s genetics chapters — cell lines are materials, not ethnic essences.
