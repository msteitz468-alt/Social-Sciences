## Definition as Coined

The "Pashupati seal" (also called "Proto-Śiva seal" or "seal 420") is a steatite stamp
seal excavated at Mohenjo-daro (DK-G Area, Southern Portion, Block 1, 1928–29 season,
approximately 3.9 m below datum; 3.56 × 3.53 × 0.76 cm; Mackay's catalogue number 420),
depicting a seated figure in a cross-legged, heels-together posture with bangled arms
resting on the knees, a horned headdress, and an entourage of animals (an elephant,
rhinoceros, water buffalo, and tiger, plus two smaller quadrupeds — antelope or ibex —
beneath a dais), possibly with three faces (contested), and seven Indus script signs.
The name derives from an identification proposed by the excavation's director,
[[marshall-john]] (1931), who read the figure as "Proto-Śiva" or "Paśupati" ("Lord of
Animals"), a term coined and defined by Marshall himself as an interpretive label, not
an Indus-period emic term — the wiki treats "Pashupati" here strictly as an etic,
attributed identification, one of several rival readings, never as an established
identity of the seal's subject.

## Semantic History

Marshall's identification (1931) rested on four stated points of comparison to the
later Hindu god Śiva: (1) the figure appeared three-faced, paralleling artistic
conventions depicting Śiva with three or five faces; (2) the horned headdress, in a
trisula-like ribbed form, echoed both the bull and the trisula as later emblems of
Śiva; (3) the seated posture resembled yogic asana, evoking Śiva's later epithet
Mahāyogi; (4) the surrounding wild animals evoked Śiva's epithet Paśupati, "Lord of
Animals" — Marshall noted that the Vedic term *pasu* originally meant "beast of the
forest," not domestic cattle, making the wild-animal entourage consistent with the
older sense of the term. Possehl (2002) reports Marshall's argument was, and remains,
an explicitly retrospective analogy: the name "Śiva" is not attested prominently in the
textual record before roughly 200 BCE, while the closer Vedic antecedent, Rudra
(associated with vigor, punishment, and asceticism, later merging with Śiva), still
leaves a documented gap of well over a millennium between the seal (c. third millennium
BCE) and any textual attestation of the identification Marshall proposed.

A rival identification was offered by I. Puskas (1984), who read the figure as
Proto-Brahmā, a creator god, rather than Proto-Śiva.

Three independent critiques substantially challenged Marshall's reading:

- **Sullivan (1964)** disputed nearly every element of Marshall's description, denying
  the presence of a phallus and of three faces, and argued the figure is female rather
  than male.
- **Srinivasan (1975–76, 1984)** likewise found no confirmed phallus or three faces (no
  other tricephalic image is known elsewhere in Harappan art), judged the posture
  "indeterminate" as evidence of proto-yoga, and, from her own review of Vedic
  references to Rudra, found Rudra's dominant trait toward animals to be wrath rather
  than protection — undercutting Marshall's "Lord of Beasts as protector" reading.
- **Hiltebeitel (1978)**, drawing on B. Volchok's Soviet-era decipherment work,
  emphasized that the headdress horns are specifically water-buffalo horns (not bull
  horns), connected the image to the rich later Indian mythology of Mahiṣa, the
  "Buffalo Demon" (Mahishasura), and linked the surrounding animals to the later Indian
  "vahana" (animal-vehicle) tradition of the four dik-pālas/loka-pālas ("World
  Regents") — a possible connection Marshall himself had raised only in a footnote and
  never developed. Possehl characterizes this "proto-vahana" extension as "interesting
  but not proved."

Rival decipherment-based readings of the seal's inscription (catalogued fully on
[[indus-script-decipherment-debate]]) further multiply competing identifications of the
same object: readings by Hrozny ("tribute to god Kueya"), Sankarananda ("aquatic birds
covering waterways"), Barua ("mountain-worshipped one," read in Sanskrit), the Finnish
decipherment team ("Man of the Star [Śiva]"), Chakravorty ("Satta Kosika"), Priyanka (a
Sanskrit phrase "tri Maha mahisa"), and Fairservis ("An-il the Ruler...") — Possehl
treats this diversity of mutually incompatible readings as evidence that no
decipherment-based interpretation of the seal is validated, not as a set of competing
valid readings among which to choose.

Possehl's own post-critique synthesis concedes the critiques' force against Marshall's
overreach, particularly the "proto-vahana"/sacrificial-cult extensions, but argues three
narrower points remain defensible: (1) that the central figure is a deity of some kind
— a judgment Possehl states in explicitly non-empirical terms ("it is a god because it
looks like a god!"), which the wiki flags as a bald assertion rather than an
evidence-based conclusion; (2) that the headdress derives specifically from
water-buffalo horns, based on ribbing detail and corroborating comparanda (a Kot Diji
pot from the Early–Mature Harappan Transition, a Kalibangan terra-cotta cake, and a
Padri storage jar showing a horned figure flanked by a plant motif); and (3) that the
posture represents a disciplined ritual posture, a possible precursor to later yoga, per
T. McEvilley's (1981) analysis matching the pose to historically attested yoga asanas —
offering, in this reading, early documentation of a physical-mental discipline tradition
later attributed to the sage Yajnavalkya and systematized by Patañjali (per B. Walker,
1968). Possehl notes, without resolving, that if yoga does trace to the Indus
Civilization this conflicts with Hindu tradition's own origin account for the practice —
an explicitly unresolved tension.

Comparable water-buffalo cult evidence is attested in India from at least the Purāṇas
(first millennium BCE), especially in Dravidian-speaking regions of south India
(Hiltebeitel, per Possehl). The wiki flags a four-way-non-identity caution here: linking
a buffalo-cult motif to "Dravidian-speaking" geography is an ethnolinguistic inference
drawn from later textual geography, and is not itself evidence that the Indus imagery
was produced by Dravidian-speaking people.

## Operationalizations

The seal has been "operationalized" almost entirely through iconographic and
comparative-textual analysis — comparison of its visual elements to later Hindu deity
iconography and to Vedic and Purāṇic textual epithets — rather than through any
internally Indus-period evidentiary chain; no contemporaneous text explains the image,
and the Indus script inscribed on the seal remains undeciphered (see [[indus-script]]).
Every "identification" of the figure is therefore an argued position, not a
measurement.

## Applications

The seal functions as the central iconographic anchor for the broader, heavily
attributed thread of proposed continuity between Indus religious imagery and later
Hindu tradition (see [[indus-religion]]), including the Great Goddess/Great God
framework, the proto-yoga hypothesis, and Shaktist dualism claims. It is repeatedly
invoked in debates over method — how much interpretive weight an isolated iconographic
object from an undeciphered-script civilization can bear when read against textual
traditions attested only much later.

## Critiques and Limitations

The seal illustrates, in acute form, the risk of projecting a later, textually
documented religious tradition (historical Hinduism) backward onto an undeciphered,
non-textual civilization separated from it by well over a millennium. Every specific
identification — Proto-Śiva, Proto-Brahmā, a female deity, a purely secular or ritual
figure — remains a contested attributed position; none commands independent
convergence across evidence classes, and the seal's own script inscription offers no
adjudicating evidence given the undeciphered state of [[indus-script]]. The wiki records
all readings in attributed voice and treats "Pashupati" as a conventional but explicitly
contested label, never as an established religious identity.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Description without confirmed Śiva identity

*(Attributed to [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] 1998, drawing on Joshi & Parpola corpus numbers and HARP tablets.)*

### The classic multi-animal yogic seal

Kenoyer describes the famous Mohenjo-daro seal: male deity seated yogically with erect phallus, heavy bangles, graduated necklaces, massive **water-buffalo-horn** headdress with central fan element, surrounded by rhinoceros, water buffalo, elephant, tiger, and two antelope on the dais, plus a long script inscription (Joshi & Parpola M-304 and related). He records that the seated figure has been called **“proto-Shiva”** due to later iconographic similarities but **insists specific connections to later Hindu deities cannot be confirmed**; meanings may have differed significantly even if some later deities had roots in Indus or indigenous traditions. Multi-faced readings are treated as a distinct early phenomenon, not as proof of later multi-faced Śiva.

### Related horned/yogic corpus (same interpretive family)

- Additional DK-G Mohenjo-daro seals: three-faced figure on bovine-hoofed throne; profile examples with stars in horns and five-leaf branch; nearly identical molded tablets from Mohenjo-daro and Harappa; Harappa tablets show deity with reed or branch headdress elements.
- Catalogue Cat. no. 23 (Mackay): nude male deity with three faces, yogic position on throne with bovine-hoof feet, elaborate two-projecting-horn headdress — core “Pashupati-type” imagery without Marshall’s label forced as fact.
- Cat. no. 24: horned, bangle-wearing deity standing in a pipal tree above a kneeling worshiper; human head on a stool; giant ram; seven plumed skirted figures — the “Divine Adoration” / head-offering complex Kenoyer links to open-air pipal cult (see [[indus-religion]]).
- Cat. no. 25 (Meadow & Kenoyer 1997, Harappa): deity under thirteen-pipal-leaf arch; Cat. no. 27: female deity battling two tigers / buffalo spearing before seated horned yogic deity with gharial — narrative cycle associated with the same horned-deity complex.
- Miniature terracotta masks of bearded horned deities (peaceful Mohenjo-daro vs ferocious tiger-toothed Harappa faces) treated as possibly the same deity’s aspects for puppet/amulet use (Dales; Meadow & Kenoyer).

### Methodological stance

Kenoyer’s standing rule: **precise meanings of Indus religious symbols await script decipherment**. Continuity of yogic **posture** (heels-to-groin pose correlated with *mulabandhasana*) is distinguished from continuity of **deity identity**. Buffalo-spearing scenes before seated horned figures receive later Durga analogies only as parallels, not identifications. This page therefore keeps “Pashupati” as a **conventional contested label** for a seal-and-tablet cluster, not as a wiki-voice Indus theonym.

## Wright (2010) — the rejection consolidated, and the chronological-gap critique

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Ch. 10.)* Wright surveys Marshall's (1931) four proto-Śiva criteria (yogic posture; three faces; a four-animal "Lord of Animals" entourage of elephant, tiger, rhinoceros, and water buffalo; a horned/pipal-branch headdress prefiguring Śiva's trident) and reports that Piggott (1950) and Wheeler (1968) broadly endorsed the identification, while later scholarship rejected it on several independent grounds. Doris Srinivasan (1984) found the seal's wild-animal attributes inconsistent with the historic Śiva, who is not associated with protecting wild animals. J. M. Koller (2006) added that Indus urbanism sits awkwardly against the pastoral/village character of the later tradition, that the Indus imagery emphasizes bulls and other male animals against later Hinduism's emphasis on cows, and that the seal depicts an image to be worshipped where later Hindu practice contains a strong aniconic strand — though Koller allows that yoga "may have been practiced by the Indus people." A. L. Basham (1989) judged "the evidence for any kind of continuity between this prehistoric god and Śiva... rather weak." Possehl (2003), cited by Wright, reads the seated posture more cautiously, as consistent with yoga understood as a first-millennium-BCE physical and mental discipline, and counts sixteen Indus seals depicting the same seated posture-type, of which only a few show Marshall's multi-face feature.

Wright's own critique adds an explicit chronological argument: the gap between the end of the Indus Civilization (she frames the civilization's span as roughly 2600–1800 BCE in this context) and the mid-to-late first-millennium-BCE emergence of Hinduism and Buddhism exceeds 800–1,000 years, making direct religious continuity implausible absent a demonstrated mechanism of transmission — and she notes, following Collon (1995), that borrowed imagery typically changes meaning when it moves into a new cultural context. She also floats, as an explicitly speculative aside, a possible parallel between the seal's three-faced figure and the double-faced Mesopotamian vizier-deity Usmu (attendant of Enki/Ea, able to look in two directions at once) as an iconographic device for simultaneous multi-directional observation, without asserting any historical connection.
