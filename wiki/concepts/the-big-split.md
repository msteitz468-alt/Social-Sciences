## Definition as Coined

"The Big Split" is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s name for the sharp segregation of work from
leisure in the society of employees. As the old middle-class "gospel of work" gave way to a
"leisure ethic," Mills argued (attributed), there emerged "a sharp, almost absolute split
between work and leisure. Now work itself is judged in terms of leisure values." Because work
no longer carries intrinsic meaning (see [[craftsmanship]]), it becomes purely instrumental —
"men sell little pieces of themselves in order to try to buy them back each night and week end
with the coin of 'fun.'" The result is two self-images: an "everyday image" rooted in work and
a "holiday image" rooted in leisure.

## Semantic History

The concept is titled "The Big Split" in the text (the OCR variant "big slipt" that circulates
is an artifact). It belongs with Mills's [[craftsmanship]] and [[alienation]] arguments and
anticipates later work on consumer society. Mills cited Leo Lowenthal's content-analysis of
magazine biographies showing a shift in popular "idols" from figures of *production* (business,
professional, political) to figures of *consumption* (movie stars, athletes) over the preceding
forty years — evidence, for Mills, of the ascendancy of the leisure image.

## Operationalizations

Mills did not quantify the split directly; he inferred it from the segregation of home and
workshop under the division of labor, from Lowenthal's idols-of-consumption finding, and from
the parallel structure of "status cycles" (weekends and vacations producing a temporary
"holiday image"; see [[status-panic]]). The concept is identified wherever leisure supplies the
standards by which work is judged, rather than the reverse.

## Applications

The Big Split ties together Mills's treatment of work, leisure, mass culture, and the
[[personality-market]]. It also frames his reading of the "literature of resignation" (the
"tarnished image" of success): popular culture internalizes success as a spiritual or leisure
state precisely because external, work-based success has soured. The concept remains a
reference in the sociology of leisure and consumption.

## Critiques and Limitations

"To modern man leisure is the way to spend money, work is the way to make it," Mills wrote;
"when the two compete, leisure wins hands down." The claim that pre-modern or old-middle-class
leisure genuinely "restored vitality" while modern leisure merely "diverts" is a value-laden
contrast, not an established finding, and shares the romantic-nostalgic vulnerability of the
[[craftsmanship]] ideal on which it depends.
