# Reading Material Culture (the Text Metaphor)

The proposal, associated with [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]], that [[material-culture]] can be "read" for meaning as a text is read — and the careful qualification, worked out across editions of *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]*, that **text is a metaphor, not an analogy**, for material culture.

## Definition as Coined

Earlier editions proposed treating the archaeological record "as a text to be read." *Reading the Past* (ch. 8) then catalogues why the comparison is imperfect (attributed to Hodder & Hutson): material culture shares no words or grammar with language; the surviving "text" is partial; its primary job is often to *do* a physical task, not to represent; its signifier–signified relation is frequently **not arbitrary**; and its experience is often semi- or unconscious. Bloch's Madagascar case sharpens the point — carvings "do not 'mean' anything; rather they 'honor the wood'."

## Semantic History

Hodder's own resolution: "**Text is only a metaphor, not an analogy, for material culture**." Material culture "is not a language at all — it is more clearly action and practice in the world" (Hodder 1989). But because a *text* is both sign and object (a book is a thing written to do something), and because [[ricoeur-paul|Ricoeur]] argued human **action** is best modelled on text rather than language, text is a better metaphor for material culture than language is. Correspondingly, the sign model shifts from Saussure's arbitrary dyad to [[peirce-charles-sanders|Peirce]]'s triad (icon, index, symbol), which accommodates the non-arbitrary, material, indexical character of objects. The turn of much later post-processual work toward **[[materiality]]**, "experience," and "understanding" — rather than textual "reading" — records a widely felt limit of even the metaphor: material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not.

## Operationalizations

Operationalised as [[contextual-archaeology|contextual analysis]]: meaning is read from significant similarities and differences along temporal, spatial, depositional, and typological dimensions of variation. Like a written text, meaning depends on the **context of reading** (use) as much as the context of production (the "author"), and, per Ricoeur, inscription fixes part of a meaning beyond the maker's intention — which is what makes the past readable at all.

## Applications

Hodder's reading of Neolithic tombs as "houses"; readings of "structured deposition" and decorated pottery; the treatment of British punk safety-pins as a "text" for which wearers supply multiple, non-authoritative verbal glosses.

## Critiques and Limitations

The text metaphor risks importing a linguistic model where objects resist it (Bloch; Thomas; Bekaert's "things just are as they are"); it can license over-reading; and it foregrounds representation over the sheer physical force and durability of things — the objection that drove the [[materiality]] turn and Hodder's own later [[entanglement-theory|entanglement]] program. Its defenders retain it because "misreading of the language does not imply that the objects must remain mute."
