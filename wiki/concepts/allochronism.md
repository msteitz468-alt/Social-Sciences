# Allochronism

## Definition as Coined

**Allochronism** (from Greek *allos*, "other," + *chronos*, "time") is the term [[fabian-johannes|Johannes Fabian]] coined in *[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other]]* (1983) for **the systematic placing of the anthropological Other in a Time other than the anthropologist's own present** — the concrete expression of what he called the [[coevalness|denial of coevalness]]. Fabian chose the word precisely to distinguish his target from *anachronism*: an anachronism is a factual mistake or accident, whereas allochronism denotes a systematic device — in his words "existential, rhetoric, political" — not a mistake. Anthropology, on this account, "emerged and established itself as an allochronic discourse; it is a science of other men... in another Time."

Fabian located allochronism at the level of texts and identified three registers of the device (attributed):

- **Semantic** — technical evolutionary and typological terms ("savagery," "kinship," "primitive," "tribal") that carry irreducible connotations of pastness. "Savagery is a marker of the past," so that even contemporary "savagery" gets relocated "in their Time, not ours."
- **Syntactic** — the [[ethnographic-present]]: present-tense predication combined with the grammatical third person, which (following Benveniste and Weinrich) marks the Other as the "non-person" outside the dialogic present.
- **Stylistic** — [[visualism]], the reduction of culture to something *seen* and spatially arranged (maps, tables, taxonomies), which turns temporal relations into spatial distance.

## Semantic History

The term is Fabian's coinage and has remained bound to his argument; unlike "coevalness," it did not acquire an independent ordinary-language life. Its analytic value is as the name for a **class of distancing devices** shared across otherwise-opposed schools. Fabian's historical claim (attributed) is that once Enlightenment sacred history was secularized and then *naturalized* (Lyell, Darwin) and finally **spatialized** by evolutionism, "anthropology's efforts to construct relations with its Other by means of temporal devices implied affirmation of difference as distance." Later paradigms did not escape it: functionalism "put Time on ice" via synchrony; American culturalism relativized Time into isolated "culture gardens"; and structuralism "preempted" coevalness by relocating Time in atemporal logical form — "its ostensibly achronic stance turns out to be a flagrant example of allochronic discourse."

## Operationalizations

Fabian's method was a critical reading for temporalizing markers rather than a measurement. Diagnostic questions the concept licenses:

- Does the text predicate a people in the timeless present tense while positioning the observer's "I" in the dialogic present? ([[ethnographic-present]])
- Does its classificatory vocabulary rank cases on a "temporal slope" (up/downstream, advanced/backward)? ([[social-evolutionism]]; [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]])
- Does it present its object primarily as *seen* and spatially tabulated? ([[visualism]])
- "Aristotelean political physics": where two peoples cannot occupy the same space, is the conflict resolved by assigning the conquered "a different Time"?

## Applications

Allochronism organizes Fabian's indictment of the discipline and connects it to the political economy of empire: "geopolitics has its ideological foundations in chronopolitics." It reframes anthropology's colonial entanglement as **epistemological, not merely moral** — a "cognitive complicity" built into how knowledge is produced and written, distinct from (and deeper than) bad intentions. The concept became a touchstone of the reflexive/critical turn and of postcolonial critiques of representation, and it supplies the rationale for abandoning the ethnographic present and for reflexive, dialogical ethnographic writing.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Self-implication.** Fabian conceded (attributed) that a critique of anthropology's uses of Time that ignores other cultures' own time-concepts may itself be allochronic — "There is no simple way to counter that objection."
- **Polemical breadth.** Fabian described his own procedure as deliberate *polemic* and warned that sweeping "ism"-labels risk their own allochronic dismissal (he noted "animism" was partly invented to place an opponent outside contemporary debate). His sorting of whole schools under one device is an argument, not a neutral survey.
- **Metaphor.** Fabian held that showing a distancing device to be metaphorical is "no excuse" — but the reliance on reading connotation and rhetoric makes the diagnosis contestable case by case.
- **Race.** By his own admission the framework was developed with a "glaring absence of the issue of race," which he argued a "clear conception of allochronism" should frame rather than replace.
