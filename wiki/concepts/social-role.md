# Social Role

## Definition as Coined

"Role" is older sociological and anthropological vocabulary (Mead's role-taking; Ralph Linton's *status* and *role*, 1936), but *The Social System* (1951) makes the **status-role** the load-bearing unit of [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s analysis. The elementary unit of a social system is the *act*, but for macroscopic analysis the operative unit is the status-role, which Parsons likened to "the particle of mechanics." He split it into two aspects:

- **status** — the *positional* aspect: an actor's location in the relationship structure;
- **role** — the *processual* aspect: "what the actor does in his relations with others seen in the context of its functional significance for the social system."

Crucially, statuses and roles are **not attributes of the actor** but units of the [[social-system|social system]]. Parsons defined a role as "a sector of the total orientation system of an individual actor which is organized about expectations in relation to a particular interaction context … integrated with a particular set of value-standards which govern interaction with one or more alters." Every actor is a "bundle" of roles, appearing in each relationship both as an *orienting actor* and as an *object* — but never in both capacities from the same point of reference at once.

An **institutionalized role** is one whose value-standards are [[institutionalization|institutionalized]]: conformity simultaneously fulfills the actor's [[need-disposition|need-dispositions]] and is a condition of optimizing others' reactions. Role-expectations (standards for ego's own conduct) and **sanctions** (ego's expectations of others' contingent reactions) are reciprocal — "what are sanctions to ego are role-expectations to alter and vice versa."

## Semantic History

Parsons defined the character of any role through the [[pattern-variables]] — a role is a specific combination of the five dichotomies (e.g. the physician's role is achievement-based, universalistic, specific, affectively neutral, collectivity-oriented). This "pattern-variable profile" conception of roles became standard in postwar role theory. The concept later fragmented: symbolic interactionists (via role-*making* and role-*taking*) reconceived roles as negotiated and emergent rather than prescribed, while Goffman's dramaturgy recast role as performance — moves defined partly against the Parsonian, prescriptive sense.

**Sex-role critique (Connell).** In *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987) and *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]*, [[connell-raewyn|Connell]] mounts a systematic critique of applying role theory to gender: role language is "logically vague," exaggerates prescription, has "a fundamental difficulty in grasping issues of power," and "cannot theorize change as a dialectic within gender relations." "'Sex role' is basically an inappropriate metaphor for gender interactions." [[hegemonic-masculinity]] is explicitly not a general "male sex role."

## Operationalizations

Roles are analyzed through their pattern-variable profiles and their reciprocal expectation–sanction structure, not through a measurement scale.

## Applications

- The unit through which [[functional-prerequisites|functional prerequisites]] are met: "Roles are, from the point of view of the functioning of the social system, the primary mechanisms through which the essential functional prerequisites of the system are met," analogous to the organ/function relation in an organism.
- The [[sick-role]], the physician role, occupational and kinship roles — all analyzed as institutionalized roles with distinct pattern-variable profiles.

## Critiques and Limitations

The prescriptive Parsonian role concept was criticized for treating actors as role-executors ("oversocialized"), understating improvisation, role-distance, and conflict between roles; interactionist and dramaturgical alternatives arose largely as correctives.

### The sex-role critique (Connell)

The extension of role theory to gender — the **"sex role"**, born when mid-century "sex difference" research met the 1930s concept of social role — is the target of a sustained critique by [[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]] in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995), foundational to his [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]]. Connell reads Parsons's *Family, Socialization and Interaction Process* (mid-1950s), with its instrumental/expressive division, as "the sophisticated statement of sex role theory," and (crediting Joseph Pleck's internal critique) charges it with, among other faults: being "logically vague"; exaggerating how far behaviour is "prescribed"; "categoricalism" (collapsing gender into two homogeneous poles, obscuring race, class, and sexuality); a "fundamental difficulty in grasping issues of power" (reciprocal-role assumptions "play down violence" and assume consent); and an inability to "theorize change as a dialectic within gender relations," leaving it "fundamentally reactive." His summary verdict: "'Sex role' is basically an inappropriate metaphor for gender interactions." The critique is one of the founding moves of the sociology of masculinities.
