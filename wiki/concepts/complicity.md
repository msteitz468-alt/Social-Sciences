# Complicity (fieldwork relation)

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[marcus-george-e|Marcus]], "The Uses of Complicity" (1997), in *[[marcus-ethnography-thick-and-thin-1998|Ethnography Through Thick and Thin]]*.)* Marcus proposed **complicity** as the "evil twin" of **rapport** — a device for tracing how the valorized ideal of fieldwork relationships loses force once fieldwork's classic mise-en-scène (the ethnographer inside a bounded community of Others) breaks down. His positive sense: an affinity between ethnographer and informant grounded in **shared curiosity and anxiety about a "third"** — the outside forces and sites affecting both — appropriate to [[multi-sited-ethnography|multi-sited]] fieldwork where inside/outside boundaries no longer hold. He expressly declined to promote it as a new disciplinary commonplace: it is "a device for tracing a certain critique," and a "positive, but morally ambiguous and difficult concept of fieldwork participation."

## Semantic History

Marcus's genealogy runs through three prior positions:

1. **Geertz** — the Balinese cockfight raid (fieldwork 1958; "Deep Play," 1972): being caught in the police raid created rapport; Geertz's "anthropological irony" acknowledges the mutually self-interested fiction underneath rapport, but Marcus argues Geertz "pulled back" from its radical reflexive implications out of commitment to the frame in which anthropology could be done.
2. **Clifford's collaborative ideal** — plural authorship and polyphony replacing rapport; Marcus's critique: it is "very much forged in the traditional mise-en-scène of fieldwork — and in fact reinforces that traditional setting."
3. **Rosaldo's [[imperialist-nostalgia]]** — complicity located in the fieldworker's kinship with the colonial "agents of change" ("herein resides the complicity of missionary, constabulary, officer, and ethnographer"); for Marcus a moral impasse with "little further response."

Marcus's own reconception then relocates complicity from the colonial frame into the multi-sited one, paired with the **circumstantial activist** persona.

## Operationalizations

Type case (per Marcus): Douglas Holmes's fieldwork among the European right — interviews with Bruno Gollnisch (European Right group, chaired by Le Pen) and Richard Edmonds (British National Party) — where Holmes's concept of "illicit discourse" required an affinity of **analytic imagination** with objectionable informants that is distinct from ethical endorsement. This cognitive complicity, not empathy or rapport, is what makes such fieldwork possible.

## Applications

Fieldwork among elites, experts, and political actors the ethnographer cannot or should not "side with" — Marcus's own dynastic-wealth research (*Lives in Trust*, 1992), Holmes's European right, and, prospectively, any multi-sited design that "lays across contested and conflicted social ground." Marcus explicitly distanced elite ethnography from Nader's (1969) "studying up" framing — anthropologists are not "temperamentally suited to be so clearly oppositional at the outset."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept inherits rapport's un-operationalized quality; its moral ambiguity is by design ("the uncertain moral economy of multi-sited strategies"). It is a knowledge-layer figure of speech about fieldwork relations, not a measured variable — treat all uses as attributed program-talk, and see [[writing-culture-debate]] for the wider dispute over reconceiving fieldwork.
