## Definition as Coined

The "personality market" is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s term for the commercialization of
personal traits under salaried employment. His definitional statement (attributed): "Whenever
there is a transfer of control over one individual's personal traits to another for a price,
a sale of those traits which affect one's impressions upon others, a personality market
arises." In a society of employees oriented to selling and servicing people rather than
handling things, Mills argued, "it is inevitable that a personality market should arise."

The concept extends Marxian [[alienation]] from the wage-worker to the salaried employee.
Where manual work sacrifices "time, power, and energy," salaried white-collar work — above
all salesmanship — additionally requires the employee to sell "one's self": personality
traits "become part of the means of production," and the worker "instrumentalizes and
externalizes intimate features of his person." Mills explicitly reframed Marx's thesis of
"increasing misery" in psychological rather than purely material terms.

## Semantic History

The idea condenses several sources: Marx on [[alienation]] and [[commodity-fetishism|the
commodity form]], [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]] on salesmanship-and-sabotage, and Weimar
sociology of the salaried employee. The phrase "the personality market" is Mills's. Its
principal sociological descendant in the collection is [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]]'s
[[emotional-labor]] and [[commercialization-of-feeling]] (*[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*, 1983): Hochschild explicitly took Mills's sold smile and psychological misery of white-collar work as a starting point, then specified the labor process of managing feeling (surface/deep acting, feeling rules, employer training) that Mills named more loosely as personality sale.

## Operationalizations

Mills specified three structural conditions for a stabilized personality market: the
employee is part of a bureaucratic enterprise; the role requires regular public contact
representing the firm; and that public is largely anonymous urban strangers. He documented
its apparatus — "personnel shoppers" covertly rating clerks' friendliness, a Schenley
Distillers personality course, a Milwaukee municipal "Charm School," and a Purdue study
finding personality "paid more than six times" the bonus value of higher intelligence. His
salesgirl typology (drawn from James B. Gale's store observations) — the Wolf, the Charmer,
the Social Pretender — catalogs commercialized self-presentation as sales technique.

## Applications

The personality market underpins Mills's accounts of salesmanship, office work, the salaried
intellectual ("selling the lies of others" is "selling himself"), the reframed success
ideology (from "ability" to "agility"), and the [[status-panic]]. Mills argued it "underlies
the all-pervasive distrust and self-alienation... characteristic of metropolitan people":
manipulation becomes mutually recognized and normalized, until "one makes an instrument of
himself, and is estranged from It also."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept rests on interpretive and secondary-source material (business-literature surveys,
one observer's salesgirl typology, planted-observer anecdotes) rather than systematic data,
and reflects Mills's diagnostic, sometimes moralizing register. Jacoby's charge that Mills's
work-and-alienation writing can sound "more Matthew Arnold than Max Weber" applies here.
Whether selling personality is genuinely more self-estranging than selling labor, or simply
different, remains an interpretive claim rather than an established finding.
