# Evolution of Simplicity

## Definition as Coined

In *[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]* (2005, Ch. 4), [[yoffee-norman|Norman Yoffee]] argues that a main trend in the evolution of the earliest states is not only increasing “complexity” but an **evolution of simplicity**: rulers and central institutions attempt to **refashion and simplify** social arrangements so that they become **legible** (after James C. Scott, *Seeing Like a State*, 1998) — permanent names, standard measures, writing, cadastral knowledge, law codes, controlled ritual technologies. Against neo-evolutionist images of the state as successful monopolist of “true law” and total information, Yoffee holds that ancient states **tried** to simplify competing authorities but often **failed**, leaving life more complex for subjects.

All claims here **attributed to Yoffee 2005** (and to Scott as he cites him).

## Semantic History

- **Scott 1998:** modern high-modernist states make society legible via standard grids; premodern states were “partially blind.”
- **Yoffee 2005:** accepts the legibility frame but **extends it to earliest states** — they were not blind by default; writing and codes appear with many first states precisely as simplification projects. “Complex society” remains archaeological shorthand for multi-group, centralized systems; Yoffee “complicates complexity” by treating simplification as the state’s own project.
- Not a general evolutionary stage label; a **process claim** about statecraft.

## Operationalizations

Yoffee’s exemplars (attributed):

| Domain | Simplifying move | Outcome Yoffee stresses |
|---|---|---|
| Early China | Control of bronze ritual metal, itinerant kingship, oracle/scribal specialists | Partial “Swiss cheese” territorial control (Keightley); not total tofu-solid rule |
| Mesopotamian law | Written codes (e.g. Hammurabi) as royal display | Codes do not replace local assemblies/elders; political instruments more than practical statutes |
| Writing | Accounts, professions lists, standardized official language | Legibility for institutions; not full social knowledge |

## Applications

- Critique of neo-evolutionist checklists that equate the state with successful law monopoly ([[fried-morton-h|Fried]], [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] as cited).
- Bridge from modern state studies (Scott) to archaeological political theory.
- Complements [[heterarchy]] and bottom-up [[agency]]: simplification is attempted from the center; resistance and parallel authorities persist.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Thesis is **programmatic** from secondary Mesopotamian and Chinese literature (~2003 horizon).
- “Simplicity” is from the ruler’s standpoint; Yoffee explicitly says the same projects make citizens’ lives more complex.
- Relation to Scott’s modernism: Yoffee italicizes Scott’s “premodern” to mark the contrast he challenges — ancient states were not merely partial-blind predecessors of modern grids.
