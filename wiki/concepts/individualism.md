# Individualism

## Definition as Coined

[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] gave *individualisme* its modern sociological sense
in Volume 2 of *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]* (1840). He
defined it carefully **against** egoism: egoism is an "ardent and excessive love of self"
— a vice as old as humanity; individualism is "a considered and peaceful feeling which
disposes each citizen to cut himself off from the mass of his fellows and withdraw into
the circle of family and friends," leaving "society at large to itself." It is not a sin
of the heart but "an erroneous judgment" — and, crucially, a product of **social
conditions**, not character. Tocqueville warned that individualism "at first only dams the
spring of public virtues" but "in the long run attacks and destroys all the others and
finally merges with egoism."

## Semantic History

The term's later career diverged sharply from Tocqueville's cautionary usage. In much
Anglo-American thought "individualism" became a value-word for self-reliance and liberty;
in Durkheim it splits again. From the *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* ingest:
[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] names **egoism** the excess of the individual ego over the social ego
and builds [[egoistic-suicide]] from **excessive individualism** / low [[social-integration]];
he also notes that modern “dignity of the person” (man a God to mankind) can enlarge
egoism even though individualism is “not necessarily egoism.” [[anomie]] is a related but
distinct regulation-side pathology. Tocqueville's specific claim — that equality
*produces* social atomization by dissolving the aristocratic chains that had bound each
person to those above and below — is the ancestor of a long sociological line on
mass society, privatism, and the erosion of community (cousin to Durkheimian egoism).

[[simmel-georg|Simmel]] (in *[[simmel-sociology-wolff-1950|Soziologie]]*, 1908) cut the
concept a third way, distinguishing two historically successive forms (attributed to
Simmel): an **eighteenth-century "quantitative" individualism** — the individual as a
free-and-equal instance of a universal "natural man," identical in every accidental
person, "sublimated" in Kant's transcendental ego, and projected economically as free
competition — versus a **nineteenth-century "qualitative" individualism** of *uniqueness*
(*Einzigkeit*) rather than mere *singleness* (*Einzelheit*), in which each person is
"called" to an irreplaceable, incomparable role. Simmel (attributed) traced the second
through Goethe, Schleiermacher, and Romanticism, and grounded it metaphysically in the
modern **division of labour**: "the doctrine of freedom and equality is the foundation of
free competition; while the doctrine of differentiated personality is the basis of the
division of labor." He also pressed an **antinomy between freedom and equality** — full
freedom, given unequal powers, breeds exploitation — quoting Goethe that legislators
promising both at once "are fantasts or charlatans."

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Tocqueville read it from manners and from the American propensity to
retreat into private life; later research operationalizes cognate ideas through measures of
civic participation, associational membership, and social isolation.

## Applications

Individualism is the hinge of Vol. 2's political argument. Because equality isolates and
weakens citizens, they become prey to a centralizing state — the mechanism that yields
[[democratic-despotism]]. Tocqueville's Americans **combat** individualism through
[[voluntary-association|free associations]], local self-government, a free press, and the
doctrine of [[self-interest-properly-understood]] — the "art of association" that turns
isolated individuals back into a public. The concept thus generates both the disease and
the remedy in his account.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The sharp egoism/individualism distinction is often flattened in later usage; Tocqueville's
  hedge (Americans may act from disinterested impulse but *attribute* it to self-interest)
  is load-bearing and easily lost.
- As with [[equality-of-conditions]], the derivation is deductive and impressionistic
  rather than measured.
