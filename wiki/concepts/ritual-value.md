# Ritual Value

An analytical concept coined by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] in "Taboo" (the Frazer Lecture, 1939; in *Structure and Function in Primitive Society*, 1952) to replace the loaded cross-cultural use of "[[taboo]]." Paired with the term **ritual status**, it is the pivot of his social theory of ritual.

## Definition as Coined

Radcliffe-Brown proposed discarding "taboo" for comparison because the Polynesian *tabu* means simply "forbidden" and is far wider than the anthropological sense — "in such a science as ours words are the instrument of analysis and we should always be prepared to discard inferior tools for superior when opportunity arises." In its place he defined a **ritual prohibition** as "a rule of behaviour which is associated with a belief that an infraction will result in an undesirable change in the ritual status of the person who fails to keep to the rule," and introduced **ritual value** as the correlative property: "Anything — a person, a material thing, a place, a word or name, an occasion or event, a day of the week or a period of the year — which is the object of a ritual avoidance or taboo can be said to have ritual value." Positive rites (consecration) confer it as much as avoidances.

Following [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s inclusive use of "sacred," he chose "ritual value" precisely because it is non-connotative — it does not smuggle in the Western **holy/unclean** opposition, which he argued is not universal (his Hawaiian *kapu* example: the sanctity of a chief and the uncleanness of a person executed for incest "have the same source and are the same thing").

## Semantic History

"Value," for Radcliffe-Brown, "always refers to a relation between a subject and an object... exhibited in and defined by the behaviour." He built up from individual value to **social value** (two or more subjects aware of a common interest in an object) and argued that most ritual values are also social values — objects of common concern, or symbols of them. The concept thus fuses ritual with [[social-structure|social structure]]: ritual value marks what the society collectively cares about. The idea originates in his *Andaman Islanders* theory of 1908/1922 (see [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]]); the 1939 lecture supplies its sharpest terminology.

## Operationalizations

Ritual value is read off behaviour, not informants' reasons (which Radcliffe-Brown treated as data, not explanation). His method for recovering a symbol's meaning: compare all the occasions within one society on which the same symbol (e.g. personal-name avoidance) appears, and infer the common element — a procedure he likened to analysing an unwritten language. He distinguished a rite's **psychological function** (its effects on participants) from its **[[social-function|social function]]** (its effects on the network of social relations), charging that "functional anthropology" too often stops at the former.

## Applications

Applied to birth and death avoidances (the personal name as "a symbol of the social personality"), to [[totemism]] (natural species as objects of a general "ritual attitude"), and to the general thesis that ritual regulates and transmits "the sentiments on whose existence the social order itself depends." See [[structural-functionalism]] and [[taboo]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's strength — reading ritual value from behaviour and social structure rather than belief — is also its main vulnerability: it brackets meaning and native exegesis in favour of the observer's functional inference, exactly the move [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (on meaning) and later interpretive/symbolic anthropology (Geertz, Turner) would push back against. Radcliffe-Brown's own reversal of Frazer — that the social function of ritual is "essential," not an accidental by-product of erroneous belief — is a strong programmatic claim that the interpretive turn treats as one option rather than a demonstrated result.
