# Social Archaeology

Label for archaeology that aims at **social** reconstruction beyond chronology and artifact typology. The term is shared across opposed programs: processual “social archaeology” (systems, variables, adaptive organization) and the critical reconception advanced by [[shanks-michael|Shanks]] and [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] in *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]* and *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]* (both 1987).

## Definition as Coined

No single coining. In practice:

- **Processual usage (as S&T and others characterize it):** recover social organization and cultural process from material remains via explicit models (roles, ranking, exchange, adaptation) — see [[processual-archaeology]], *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* (1968).
- **Shanks & Tilley 1987 (attributed):** the object of a **fully** social archaeology is “the relation of **material culture-patterning** to social practices, social strategies and social structures in determinate social totalities in the past and in the present” (*Re-Constructing Archaeology*, Ch. 6 conclusion).

## Semantic History

| Strand | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Culture-historical | Peoples, cultures, trait complexes — social content often implicit |
| Processual social archaeology | Systems, variables, middle-range prediction of organization |
| Critical / post-processual (S&T) | Contingency, ideology, agency, material culture as objectification and signifying practice; past–present mediation |
| Later diversification | Gender, indigenous, phenomenological, symmetrical, etc. under broader interpretive umbrellas |

Shanks & Tilley rejected equating “social archaeology” with more sophisticated systems boxes; they opposed what they called a **[[logic-of-contingency|logic of necessity]]** (social as intelligible totality with rational historical substratum) and proposed contingent, open social relations (developed more fully in *Social Theory and Archaeology*).

## Operationalizations

**Shanks & Tilley Ch. 6 toolkit (attributed):** against essentialist artifact/culture/system packages; social system as **relational whole**; **agency** and social practices; stratified model of agency (discursive / practical / unconscious levels as they present them); **structures** and structuring principles; **structuration** (drawing on [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] / habitus vocabularies as used); contradiction, power, ideology, change; material culture as **objectification** within social strategies; meaning in material culture as context-bound (anti–cross-cultural free-floating symbolism).

**Processual operationalizations** elsewhere: mortuary analysis, settlement hierarchies, exchange models — see program pages, not equated here with S&T’s object.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Ambiguous label: “social archaeology” can mean almost any non-purely-typological program — always specify which school.
- S&T’s reconception is **theoretical-program** content; critics charged relativism and under-specified method ([[processual-postprocessual-debate]]; RCA 1992 Appendix).
- Empirically, their two material-culture chapters illustrate the program; they explicitly denied those chapters are decisive “tests” of the rest of the book.

## Insoll 1999 — Social archaeology of a living world religion

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Timothy Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]*, Blackwell Social Archaeology series.)*

Insoll uses “social archaeology” as the missing theoretical register of [[islamic-archaeology]]: the subfield had lagged the self-examination of European medieval archaeology and of Islamic anthropology, remaining art-historical, top-heavy, and undertheorized. His program places Islamic/Muslim material culture in **social context** (not typology alone) across mosque, domestic space, diet, magic, warfare, trade, burial, and settlement — treating religion as a way of life rather than a compartmentalized ritual domain. Dual scales (structuring principles vs cultural diversity) and ideals-vs-realities testing are the operational toolkit. Series home under [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] situates the book in the broader post-processual/social-archaeology publishing environment without Insoll self-labeling as “post-processual” in the extracted ranges.
