# Collective Representations

## Definition as Coined

**Collective representations** are ways of thinking, feeling, and classifying that are shared by members of a society and that cannot be reduced to individual psychology: myths, religious beliefs, moral ideals, and the **categories of the understanding** (time, space, class, causality, force, etc.). In *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms]]* (1912), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] argues that such representations are **socially generated** — especially in religious life and ritual assembly — and that they are *sui generis* realities (attributed). They refine and partly supersede the earlier vocabulary of **[[collective-conscience|collective conscience]]** (*Division of Labor*, *Suicide*) by stressing cognitive and symbolic content, not only moral consensus.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Rules* 1901 Preface | Social life "made up entirely of representations"; consciousness not a substance but phenomena *sui generis* — defense against reification charges while keeping exteriority of [[social-fact|social facts]] |
| Durkheim "Représentations individuelles et représentations collectives" (1898) | Collective vs individual representations as distinct orders |
| Subsequent letters (Lukes *Rules* volume) | "All sociology is a psychology, but a psychology *sui generis*"; collective representations more complex than individual ones |
| *Primitive Classification* 1903 (Durkheim & Mauss) | Subtitle: contribution to the study of collective representations; classificatory systems as socially modeled representations |
| *Elementary Forms* 1912 | Religious beliefs and categories as collective representations; sacred as collective ideal |
| Later Durkheimian / cultural sociology | Shared symbols, codes, and classifications |
| Contrast with psychology | Not mere sum of individual ideas; imposed with authority of the group |

## Operationalizations

Content analysis of myths, ritual symbols, classification systems; survey measures of shared beliefs (downstream). Durkheim's method is comparative-ethnological interpretation.

## Applications

- Bridges religion and **theory of knowledge**: categories are collective representations modeled on social morphology.
- Links to [[sacred-and-profane|sacred]] ideals that society creates of itself.
- Continuity with [[social-fact|social facts]] as external and constraining.
- *[[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-1903|Primitive Classification]]* (1903): first systematic *Année* application of collective-representation analysis to hierarchical classifications of nature ([[primitive-classification]], [[symbolic-classification]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of reifying "society" as a mind.
- Relation between individual and collective representation remains philosophically disputed.
- Category-origin thesis contested — Needham (1963) on 1903 and broader Gehlke/Dennes circularity critique: see [[social-origin-of-categories-debate]].
