# Supermanagers

[[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s term for the explosion of very high **labor** incomes among senior managers—especially in the United States after about 1980—as a major driver of top-end [[income-inequality]], distinct from classical rentier capital income. Developed in *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* Chapter 9 and related [[top-income-shares]] research; object of analysis in [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]].

## Definition as Coined

**Supermanagers** (and related “supersalaries”) designate top corporate executives and highly paid managers whose compensation—wages, bonuses, exercised options, and other labor income—pushes the upper centile and thousandth of the income distribution. Piketty argues that the rise of the supermanager is **largely an Anglo-Saxon phenomenon**: since 1980 the upper centile’s share of national income rose significantly in the US, Britain, Canada, and Australia; in most cases the explosion of top **labor** incomes explains at least **two-thirds** of the top-centile share increase (the rest robust capital income). Primary reason for rising income inequality in English-speaking countries in recent decades is, on his reading, the supermanager in both financial and nonfinancial sectors.

Magnitude among Anglo-Saxon countries (early 2010s vs late 1970s baseline ~6–8%): US top centile nearly **20%** of national income; Britain/Canada **14–15%**; Australia **9–10%**—US increase roughly twice Britain/Canada and about three times Australia/New Zealand. Continental Europe and Japan: much smaller rises (e.g. France/Japan ~7% → ~9%+; Sweden ~4% → ~7%; Germany ~9% → nearly 11%). Macro transfer to “the 1%”: only **2–3 points** of national income in continental Europe/Japan vs **10–15 points** in the US. Top 0.1% US share: **2% → nearly 10%** (a 10% share means ~100× national average income).

## Semantic History

Contrasts with nineteenth-century **rentier** figures whose top incomes were almost entirely capital income. Related Piketty vocabulary: “society of managers” vs “society of rentiers”; “hypermeritocratic” inequality (US 2010-type total-income top shares driven by labor) vs “hyperpatrimonial” (Europe 1910-type driven by capital). “Meritocratic extremism”: modern (especially US) societies’ need to designate “winners” and reward them more generously when selection seems based on intrinsic merit rather than birth—invoked to justify pay 50–100× average so that only heirs would otherwise achieve true wealth; can ground a race between supermanagers and rentiers “to the detriment of those who are neither.”

## Operationalizations

- **Top shares and composition**: top 1% / 0.1% of income; labor vs capital split within the top. Within the top decile, “the 9 percent” progressed faster than average but far less than “the 1 percent”; pay $100k–$200k rose only slightly faster than average while >$500k (especially >$1m) exploded—a discontinuity hard to match with continuous skill measures.
- **Decomposition**: overperformance of the top centile explains nearly **three-quarters** of the increase in the top US decile’s share of national income since 1970; distinct from the rising college–high-school wage gap (Goldin–Katz).
- **Governance evidence**: firm-level data show executive pay hard to explain by firm performance; pay rises most when sales/profits rise for external reasons—Bertrand and Mullainathan’s “**pay for luck**.”
- **Tax interaction**: propensity to pay for luck varies with top marginal income tax rates; large post-1980 cuts in English-speaking top rates transformed pay-setting incentives and can amplify into political feedback (high earners funding parties and think tanks to keep rates low).

## Applications

Challenges pure capital-only or pure skill-biased technical change (SBTC) stories for late-twentieth-century US inequality: education–technology race offers clues for some historical evolutions but, Piketty holds, fails for the supermanager takeoff; IT and productivity growth were similar across rich countries while inequality outcomes diverged sharply—pointing to institutions and **social norms** of acceptable executive pay (US/UK firms more tolerant of generous packages after 1970; Europe/Japan later and less far; 1950s–60s US more egalitarian than France on wage hierarchy—reversed since 1980). Interacts with [[wealth-inequality]]: extremely generous top-manager rewards can be a force for wealth-distribution divergence if the best paid set their own salaries; top centile wage share could reach 15–20%, 25–30%, or higher in the US. Still, Piketty judges renewed capital-ownership concentration as growth slows and β rises “in some respects even more worrisome” than the geographically limited supermanager wage gap. ## Critiques and Limitations

Marginal-productivity and optimal-contract accounts of top pay contest Piketty’s power/norms reading; he treats individual marginal productivity for unique senior jobs as nearly undefinable under imperfect information—“close to a pure ideological construct”—with pay set by executives or compensation committees of similarly paid peers. Whether “hands in the till” is fairer than Smith’s invisible hand is polemical phrasing he half-endorses. Cross-country tax and norm explanations compete with firm-size, finance-share, and superstar theories not fully adjudicated in the 2014 book. Minimum-wage policy affects the bottom far more than the top—so supermanager dynamics are analytically separate from bottom-end wage compression debates.
