# Instinct of Workmanship

## Definition as Coined

In *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|The Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899), [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]] named the **instinct of workmanship** as a basic human propensity: man is "by force of his being" an agent possessed of "a taste for effective work, and a distaste for futile effort... This aptitude or propensity may be called the instinct of workmanship." It is the positive, industrious counter-pole to the predatory/pecuniary temperament that the rest of the book anatomizes.

Its role in the argument is double. First, it is the *source* that gets corrupted: applied under conditions of habitual comparison between persons, the instinct of workmanship is "perverted" into "an emulative or invidious comparison of persons" — so [[pecuniary-emulation|pecuniary emulation]] is a derivative of workmanship, not a separate drive. Second, it is the *check*: it persists as a countervailing force against the canon of [[conspicuous-waste|conspicuous waste]], producing the felt need for wasteful expenditure to carry "some colorable excuse in the way of an ostensible purpose," and it serves as "the court of final appeal in any question of economic truth or adequacy."

## Semantic History

Veblen made the concept the centerpiece of a later book, *The Instinct of Workmanship and the State of the Industrial Arts* (1914), which systematized it alongside the "parental bent" and "idle curiosity" as the non-invidious, industrially serviceable instincts. The "instinct" vocabulary is of its period (McDougall-era instinct psychology) and later fell out of favor; the underlying idea — an intrinsic orientation toward competent, purposeful making — survives in the sociology of work, craft, and the critique of alienated labor, and echoes in later work on intrinsic motivation and Richard Sennett's *The Craftsman*.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Veblen treated it as a deep dispositional constant read off from the human "sense of merit and demerit" attaching to serviceable vs. futile activity. In his late chapters it appears as the tendency that reasserts itself when pecuniary pressure relaxes — in "rustic"/"natural" taste, in leisure-class women's charitable and reform "meliorative" work, in the "new-woman" and kindergarten movements — each framed as an "ante-predatory" reversion.

## Applications

The instinct of workmanship anchors Veblen's contrast between two temperaments: the industrial/workmanlike (truthful, peaceable, causally-minded, non-invidious) versus the predatory/pecuniary (status-seeking, animistic, invidious). On this contrast he built his claims that the belief in luck and devout observance are archaic drags on industrial efficiency, that modern science is "a by-product of the industrial process," and that sport offers only a "colorable make-believe of purpose" that the deeper instinct of workmanship ultimately judges as falling short.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The instinct psychology of 1899–1914 is obsolete; positing a discrete "instinct" is not tenable in modern terms, and the concept must be read as a dispositional posit rather than a biological claim.
- Paired with the canon of conspicuous waste, the instinct of workmanship gives Veblen's system a two-principle structure flexible enough to rationalize almost any observed taste — the standard objection to its explanatory closure.
- Whether workmanship is genuinely prior to, and corrupted into, emulation (rather than co-equal or the reverse) is a bare assertion in Veblen, not a demonstrated sequence.
