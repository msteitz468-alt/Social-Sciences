## Definition as Coined

The "new middle class" is the salaried, propertyless middle stratum that replaced the
old propertied middle class of entrepreneurs, farmers, and free professionals over the
course of industrial capitalism's development. Where the [[old-middle-class]] owned the
property it worked, the new middle class owns none: it depends entirely on employment
within large bureaucratic enterprises. The term originates in early-twentieth-century
German sociology (Lederer, Speier) as the *neue Mittelstand*; [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright
Mills]]'s *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951) is its principal American
analysis.

Mills framed the transformation in a compressed formula (attributed): "Negatively, the
transformation of the middle class is a shift from property to no-property; positively,
it is a shift from property to a new axis of stratification, occupation."

## Semantic History

The concept arose as an anomaly for classical Marxism. Mills argued (attributed) that
the salaried stratum was "first discovered" as a problem by Marxist theoreticians who
expected society to polarize into bourgeoisie and proletariat, and who were then forced
to differentiate within the propertyless category once a large, propertyless, but
non-socialist salariat appeared. In the German context the salaried employee's supposed
susceptibility to Nazism (per Lederer, Speier, Kracauer) gave the concept a political
urgency Mills carried into the American case, while insisting the American new middle
class had "been from the beginning non-bourgeois in mentality" — it never held the old
work ethic it is often assumed to have lost.

## Operationalizations

Mills measured the new middle class as an occupational residual. His headline series
(reworked from census data): the new middle class rose from ~6% of the labor force
(1870) to ~25% (1940), a growth of roughly 1,600%, while the old middle class fell from
33% to 20%. Its internal composition shifted sharply toward office work: office workers
rose from 12% to 40% of the new middle class between 1870 and 1940, while salespeople
fell from 44% to 25%. Mills flagged the category as coherent "only in contradistinction
to the other classes."

## Applications

The concept organizes Mills's whole account: the [[managerial-demiurge]] (bureaucratization
of management and the professions), the [[status-panic]], the [[personality-market]], and
the [[new-middle-class-politics-debate|debate over the stratum's political destiny]]. It
also underpins comparative and successor work on middle-class formation, the sociology of
professions, and mobility studies.

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*,
1959, Ch. II) treats the *neuer Mittelstand* as a central post-Marx structural change
alongside [[decomposition-of-capital]] and [[decomposition-of-labor]]. From his
[[conflict-theory|conflict-theory]] viewpoint he argues (attributed) there can be no true
"middle class" of conflict groups — authority is dichotomous within associations — so the
new middle class is **born decomposed**: he splits **bureaucrats** (hierarchical authority
positions, on the domination side / service class) from **white-collar workers** (salaried
but non-bureaucratic, nearer the subjected side). The stratum extends older classes rather
than forming a third camp; class remains questionable as a label for these post-capitalist
aggregates. This is a structural answer to the [[new-middle-class-politics-debate]] parallel
to, but distinct from, Mills's property-class / status-split analysis.

## Applications — Wright *Class Counts* (1997)

[[wright-erik-olin|Erik Olin Wright]] (*[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]*) reconstructs the employee middle strata not as a free-floating third class but as **[[contradictory-class-locations]]** along authority and skill within exploitation relations — managers/supervisors and experts as privileged appropriation locations among employees. Distinct from Mills's residual salariat: Wright keeps them *inside* a Marxist class map. Empirically (project surveys), extended expert-manager shares and authority density vary by country (Sweden denser hierarchies); US structural change 1960–90 expanded contradictory locations among wage-earners. See [[wright-class-counts-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

As with [[white-collar]], the residual definition is the core problem: the "new middle
class" lumps higher managers and technicians with clerks and salespeople whose life-chances
differ greatly. Mills predicted (attributed) that a distinct "social class" would form
within a generation between lower white-collar and wage-worker positions, excluding
professionals and higher managers — a prediction whose fate later stratification research
has contested. Critics also note the concept's dependence on a property/no-property axis
that undervalues intra-white-collar hierarchy. Dahrendorf's authority split addresses that
hierarchy but (position) still forces a dichotomy that multi-dimensional stratification
research may reject.
