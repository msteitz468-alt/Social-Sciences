# Primitiveness

## Definition as Coined

The category of the "primitive" — societies imagined as representing an early, ancestral
condition of humankind. This page treats **[[levi-strauss-claude|Claude Lévi-Strauss]]'s
critical reworking** of the concept in "The Concept of Primitiveness," his closing essay to
*[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* (1968) — delivered as his Viking Fund
Medal acceptance speech, 8 April 1966.

*(Attributed — Lévi-Strauss 1968.)* Lévi-Strauss resisted framing hunter-gatherers as a
"semi-animal condition of mankind." However different from industrial civilization, they
"belong to such a recent past that there is a much greater difference between them and any
kind of animal society than between them and more complex human societies."

## Semantic History

Lévi-Strauss's central move is to **distinguish "primitive" from "regressive."** Living and
recent hunter-gatherers, he argued, are frequently "'leftovers' or devolved descendants of
richer contemporary cultures" — "regressive rather than primitive" — echoing his earlier
argument about the Nambikwara ("The Concept of Archaism in Anthropology," *Structural
Anthropology*, 1963). This directly undermines naive [[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic
analogy]] from living foragers to the Paleolithic. He separately rejected treating "primitive"
peoples as intellectually underdeveloped: "I see no reason why mankind should have waited
until recent times to produce minds of the caliber of a Plato or an Einstein... they were
probably more interested in kinship" — archaic humans likely had full cognitive capacity
directed at different problems.

## Operationalizations

The concept resists operationalization — that is Lévi-Strauss's point. He proposed instead
that indigenous myths encode their own "native theory" of social structure (distinguishing a
"paleolithic" model of cross-affines from a "neolithic" model of parallel affines), so that
the analyst should "get back to the older native theory at the origin of the facts" rather
than rank societies on a developmental scale.

## Applications

The regressive/primitive distinction reframes hunter-gatherer studies: it cautions against
reading the ethnographic present as a Pleistocene survival (feeding the
[[ethnographic-analogy]] and [[kalahari-debate]] debates) and against evolutionist rankings
of societies (contrast the dated developmental framing of some 1968 contributors, e.g.
Rose's characterization of Australians as "the least developed of any known people").

## Critiques and Limitations

Lévi-Strauss's own structuralist defence of models against statistics (he held a ~20%
cross-cousin-marriage rate "amply sufficient to imprint the society with a given structure")
was itself contested at the symposium by [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|Hiatt]]
and others. His broader claim that anthropological "truth" is irreducibly perspectival — "the
observer can never coincide with the observed" — is a philosophical position, not a settled
finding. The term "primitive" survives in scare-quotes as a flagged historical category, not
a wiki-voice descriptor.
