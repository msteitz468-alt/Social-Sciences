# Methodological Dualism

## Definition as Coined

[[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]] (*[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|Coming Crisis]]*, 1970) names **methodological dualism** the practice of applying one epistemic and moral standard to the people sociologists study and another — more protective, less demystifying — to sociologists themselves. Subjects are treated as objects of scrutiny; the theorist's own assumptions, interests, and social location are methodologically exempt.

He notes developing the idea while advising William Yancey's dissertation. Dualism, for Gouldner, is not a minor inconsistency but an ideology of method that sustains Academic Sociology's professional self-image and blocks [[reflexive-sociology]].

## Semantic History

Related critiques: Mills on abstracted empiricism and grand theory; later standpoint and positionality debates; ethnomethodology's symmetry principle (different lineage). Gouldner's term is specifically about the *asymmetry of scrutiny* between knower and known in professional sociology.

## Operationalizations

Diagnostic rather than metric: look for methods texts and research practices that demand candor about informants while treating the researcher's domain assumptions as non-issues.

## Applications

- Core foil for [[reflexive-sociology]].
- Linked to Gouldner's claim that methodology functions as ideology (Ch. 2).
- Connects to the book's treatment of value-freedom and nonpartisanship as moral claims of Functionalism.

## Critiques and Limitations

Opponents may redescribe the same practice as ordinary role differentiation (scientist vs citizen) rather than dualism. Boundary between legitimate analytic distance and protective exemption is itself contested.
