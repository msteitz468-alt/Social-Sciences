# Cross-Pressures

## Definition as Coined

**Cross-pressures** are conflicting social and political pulls on an individual — from class, religion, ethnicity, region, family, workplace, or media — that do not point toward the same party or participation decision. In *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]* Chapter VI (building on Columbia voting research with Lazarsfeld, Barton, and Linz), [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] treats cross-pressures as a major mechanism reducing turnout, increasing indecision, and shaping intermittent or deviant voting.

## Semantic History

The term crystallizes in the Columbia School voting studies (*The People's Choice*, *Voting*) and enters standard electoral sociology. Lipset's chapters VI–VIII adapt the collaborative "Psychology of Voting" framework for a comparative, class-structured synthesis. Later American Voter / Michigan and rational-choice traditions redescribe related phenomena (conflicted partisanship, ambivalence) without always using the Columbia label.

## Operationalizations

Indicators include: membership in groups with opposing political norms; exposure to conflicting media or discussants; status inconsistency; religious vs class pulls. Predicted outcomes: lower participation, later decision, higher rates of nonvoting or unstable preference.

## Applications

- Who votes and who does not (*Political Man* Ch VI).
- Continuity and change in the [[democratic-class-struggle]] (Chs VII–VIII).
- Mechanisms that moderate pure class voting.

## Critiques and Limitations

Survey measures of "pressure" are indirect; group membership ≠ felt conflict. Cross-national portability of Columbia US findings is limited. Still useful as a middle-range mechanism concept when attributed to the voting-research tradition.
