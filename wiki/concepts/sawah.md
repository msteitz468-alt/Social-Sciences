# Sawah

## Definition as Coined

**Sawah** is the Indonesian/Javanese term for irrigated wet-rice fields. In *[[geertz-agricultural-involution-1963|Agricultural Involution]]*, [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] elevates it to an **ecosystem type** characteristic of **Inner Indonesia** (esp. central/east Java, south Bali, west Lombok): permanent, irrigated, land-intensive wet-rice agriculture capable of extraordinary **labor absorption** and high yield per unit land — the ecological precondition of [[agricultural-involution]].

## Semantic History

Emic agricultural term → analytic ecosystem contrast with [[swidden]]. Also central to colonial sugar interlocking (cane planted on village sawah under lease cycles).

## Operationalizations

Geertz:

- Water as medium of nutrition and control (vs forest-fallow nutrient path of swidden)
- Technical complexity and stability of the terrace/irrigation system
- Population response: **concentrative** and **inflatable** — more labor can raise output on fixed land
- Interlock with colonial sugar: 21½-year leases, one-third/half land cycling in cane
- Classical geography: wet-rice heartland inland before Dutch coast trade shifted gravity

## Applications

Javanese agrarian history; *[[geertz-agricultural-involution-study|Agricultural Involution]]*; wet-rice society comparisons (incl. Japan).

## Critiques and Limitations

Not all Java is sawah; *tegal* dry fields and rainfed systems matter; elasticity thesis is the contested core of the involution debate.
