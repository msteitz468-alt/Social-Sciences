# Flexible Accumulation

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to [[harvey-david|David Harvey]], *[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The
Condition of Postmodernity]]*, 1989, p. 147.)* Harvey defines flexible accumulation as the
regime of capital accumulation succeeding [[fordism|Fordism]] after the crisis of 1973:
"It is marked by a direct confrontation with the rigidities of Fordism. It rests on
flexibility with respect to labour processes, labour markets, products, and patterns of
consumption. It is characterized by the emergence of entirely new sectors of production,
new ways of providing financial services, new markets, and, above all, greatly
intensified rates of commercial, technological, and organizational innovation."

Harvey ties it directly to a new round of [[time-space-compression|time-space
compression]]: shrinking decision-making time horizons alongside expanding geographic
reach via satellite communication and falling transport costs.

## Semantic History

*(Attributed to Harvey.)* Key features Harvey associates with the regime:

- **A dualized labour market** — a shrinking "core" of full-time employees with
  functional flexibility, and a growing "periphery" of part-time, casual, temporary, and
  sub-contracted workers supplying numerical flexibility. In Britain, "flexible workers"
  rose 16% (to 8.1 million) between 1981 and 1985 while permanent jobs fell 6%.
- **Revival of older labour forms** — domestic, artisanal, familial-patriarchal, and
  paternalistic "sweatshop" systems (the US–Mexico *maquiladora* programme; family labour
  in Hong Kong; the "Third Italy" co-operatives), which Marx had expected capitalism to
  eliminate. Harvey calls the effect on women's labour "a revolution (by no means
  progressive)."
- **"Economies of scope" over "economies of scale"** — small-batch, quick-changing
  production (*Fortune* reported 75% of machine parts made in batches of 50 or fewer);
  product half-lives collapsing.
- **Financialization** — an explosion of mergers (US M&A spending rose from $22 bn in
  1977 to ~$180 bn in 1985), the Eurodollar market's growth (from ~$50 bn in 1973 to ~$2
  trillion in 1987), flexible exchange rates after 1973, and "paper entrepreneurialism"
  (Robert Reich's phrase) — profit through arbitrage, raiding, and creative accounting
  rather than production.

Harvey pointedly **disputes** the rival "disorganized capitalism" thesis of Offe (1985)
and Lash and Urry (1987): capitalism, he argues, is becoming "ever more tightly organized
through dispersal, geographical mobility, and flexible responses," coordinated by
information as a commodity and by the reorganized global financial system.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by proxies: shares of contingent/part-time/sub-contracted employment; batch sizes
and product half-lives; merger volumes and cross-border financial flows; and the
geographic dispersal of production networks. Regulation theorists treat it as a candidate
new *regime of accumulation* (see [[regulation-theory]]); whether it has stabilized into
one, or is a series of "temporary fixes," is Harvey's own open question.

## Applications

- The political-economic engine of Harvey's account of [[postmodernism|postmodernism]]:
  flexible accumulation's volatility and ephemerality are, on his argument, registered
  culturally as the postmodern aesthetic (see [[postmodernity-epochal-break-debate]]).
- Harvey reads it as a recombination of Marx's classical strategies for extracting surplus
  value — *absolute* (longer hours, wage erosion, capital flight to low-wage "peripheral
  Fordism") and *relative* (technological/organizational innovation).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Harvey lays out three positions on the "flexibility debate" and places
himself between them: **(1)** Piore and Sabel (*The Second Industrial Divide*, 1984) — a
genuine, potentially democratizing "second industrial divide"; **(2)** Pollert (1988),
Gordon (1988), Sayer (1989) — flexibility is largely ideological, legitimizing political
practices without a firm empirical base, "nothing structurally new"; **(3)** Harvey's own
"in-between" view — flexible forms are real and widespread but coexist unevenly with
persistent Fordism, and the *underlying* logic of capitalist accumulation "remain[s] the
same." Critics on the "nothing new" side hold Harvey overstates the epochal significance
of a surface change; Harvey replies that "the evidence for increased flexibility … is
simply too overwhelming" to dismiss, while granting capitalism "has periodically taken
these sorts of paths before."
