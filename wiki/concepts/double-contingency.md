# Double Contingency

## Definition as Coined

**Double contingency** names the reciprocal uncertainty of interaction: unlike simple
stimulus–response, *alter*'s response is contingent on *ego*'s action and *ego*'s on *alter*'s,
so each makes their behaviour depend on the other's. The concept is [[parsons-talcott|Talcott
Parsons]]'s (with Shils, *Toward a General Theory of Action*, 1951): for Parsons the problem is
solved, and action made possible, only through a prior **value consensus** — a shared symbolic
system of norms already in place. "Without culture neither human personalities nor human social
systems would be possible."

## Semantic History

The concept's meaning turns decisively with [[luhmann-niklas|Luhmann]]'s reworking in
*[[luhmann-social-systems-1995|Social Systems]]* (1984). Luhmann accepts Parsons's problem — a
"pure circle of self-referential determination... leaves action indeterminate" — but rejects
Parsons's normative solution. His theses (attributed):

- **Double contingency is self-catalyzing.** It is not merely an obstacle social order must
  overcome but the very thing out of which social systems *emerge* — "the problem of double
  contingency is itself a component of the system that it forms." Order forms **because of**,
  not despite, mutual indeterminacy.
- **The probability of the improbable.** The doubling of improbability (both parties uncertain,
  each depending on the other) paradoxically *raises* the probability of order — "the emergence
  of a social system... made possible by a doubling of improbability."
- **Black boxes.** Ego and alter are mutually opaque self-referential systems that never
  calculate one another; by mutually assuming determinability they "create whiteness... enough
  transparency for dealing with each other" without understanding each other better. The system
  stabilizes **expectations, not behaviour**.
- **Functional equivalents to consensus.** Nothing forces the solution into the social
  dimension of shared values; a tentative gesture answered in the temporal dimension suffices
  ("order from noise") — "the system emerges *etsi non daretur Deus*." Luhmann needles Parsons
  that his own "reward/punishment" formula smuggled in a time-based mechanism Parsons then
  credited to normative culture.
- **Trust/distrust** appear as a downstream special case — strategies for the risk that the
  other, knowing what is expected, can act otherwise.

Luhmann faults [[symbolic-interactionism]] for treating "only half of double contingency"
(building a contingent alter into ego's perspective) rather than both partners' simultaneous
contingency.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; a foundational premise about the conditions of social order.

## Applications

- The generative mechanism of social order in [[social-systems-theory]] — the point where
  [[meaning]], [[complexity]], and [[self-reference]] converge to produce a [[social-system]].
- Explains why social systems control the uncertainty of one's *own* behaviour and work by
  stabilizing expectations (see [[communication]], structure-as-expectation).

## Critiques and Limitations

The two readings mark a genuine dispute: Parsons's normative solution versus Luhmann's
time-and-emergence solution reflects the larger quarrel between value-consensus sociology and
Luhmann's anti-normative systems theory (see [[luhmann-habermas-debate]]). Critics of Luhmann's
version note that "emergence from double contingency" describes rather than explains the
selection of *particular* orders.
