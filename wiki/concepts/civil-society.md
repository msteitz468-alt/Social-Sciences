# Civil Society (Gramsci)

> Distinctive [[gramsci-antonio|Gramscian]] sense of *società civile* in *[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]*. **Attributed voice** throughout. **Not** identical to Hegelian *bürgerliche Gesellschaft*, Tocquevillian voluntary association talk, or late-20th-century NGO/"third sector" usage without explicit bridging.

## Definition as Coined

In "The Intellectuals," Gramsci fixes two major superstructural levels:

1. **Civil society** — "the ensemble of organisms commonly called 'private'."
2. **Political society** or **the State** — the terrain of direct domination / juridical government.

These correspond to **[[hegemony]]** (consent organised throughout society) and **direct domination** (command). Intellectuals are deputies on both levels.

Canonical State formula (State and Civil Society notes):

> "State = political society + civil society, in other words hegemony protected by the armour of coercion."

Editors note internal variation in the notebooks: sometimes State is opposed to civil society; sometimes State = political society + civil society; sometimes balance between them; sometimes (with Hegelian echo) civil society as "private" versus political society. **Do not force one diagram** — record the load-bearing hegemony-armoured formula and the trench metaphor for [[war-of-position]].

In the East/West contrast, Western civil society is the "trenches and permanent fortifications" behind the State's outer ditch; Russian civil society in 1917 is described as gelatinous — hence different strategic forms.

## Semantic History

| Tradition | Civil society means roughly… |
|---|---|
| Hegel / Marx early | System of needs / bourgeois society vs State |
| Liberal | Sphere of voluntary association and market against State |
| **Gramsci** | Private hegemonic apparatus; consent trench-system |
| 1980s–90s democratisation | Anti-authoritarian "civil society vs State" (often de-Marxised) |
| Contemporary policy | NGOs, social capital — distant from prison notebooks |

Comaroff & Comaroff and cultural studies transmit the Gramscian sense into anthropology via [[hegemony]]; always check which lineage a text is using.

## Operationalizations

- Inventory of private organisms that organise consent (church, school, press, unions, parties, cultural associations).
- Density/complexity of that inventory ≈ how "positional" struggle must be.
- Crisis test: when masses detach from traditional ideologies, ruling class may remain only "dominant" (coercion) not "leading" (hegemony) — [[organic-crisis]].
- Economism warning (Modern Prince): identifying civil society with the State is at most a **methodological** reduction, not a historical identity that abolishes politics.

## Applications

- Intellectuals as hegemony deputies (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971]]*).
- War of position / Western strategy.
- Burawoy's factory-hegemony extension starts from a *different* Gramsci locus (Americanism) — plant-level consent — and should not erase civil-society-wide hegemony.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Notebook inconsistency on State/civil society boundaries is real (editors foreground it).
- "Private" can mislead: parties and unions are not market firms; churches are not clubs.
- Late "civil society" romanticism (pure resistance sphere) contradicts Gramsci's view that civil society is also the ruling class's trench system.
- Source: 1971 English selection.
