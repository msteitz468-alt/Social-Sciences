# Composite Hunting Band

## Definition as Coined

[[steward-julian-h|Steward]] (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, Ch. 8) defines the **composite hunting band** as a [[culture-type]] of multi-family hunting groups composed largely of **unrelated families** (composite rather than a single localized lineage), typically under conditions of scarce foods, low density, and poor transport—**or** where large migratory game allows aggregates of hundreds in which kinship knowledge is lost. Contrasted with the **[[patrilineal-band]]** (localized lineage, exogamy, territorial male inheritance).

## Semantic History

Stewardian ecological typology of foraging organization. Distinct from post-contact **predatory bands** among some Shoshoneans (horses + settler pressure)—a form/function type, not the aboriginal composite type. Later band literature reworked labels; treat Steward's term as his mid-century construct.

**Service (1962/1971):** In *[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*, [[service-elman-r|Service]] retains “composite” for the Stewardian pole opposite the **[[patrilocal-band]]**, but argues Algonkian, Athabaskan, Yahgan, and Andamanese composites (and often Steward’s “family-level” Shoshone/Eskimo) are largely products of **civilizational disruption, depopulation, and refugee amalgamation** rather than aboriginal migratory-herd ecology alone. Superorganic environment (contact) is his main explanatory ingredient ([[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]).

## Operationalizations

Steward's **causal formulation** (Ch. 8, attributed): composite rather than patrilineal when (1) chief food is game in **large herds** enabling large multi-family aggregates, **or** (2) **social practices** introduce unrelated families into what would otherwise tend toward patrilineal localization—e.g. inter-band adoption (Andamanese, as he read Radcliffe-Brown), parallel-cousin marriage endogamy (some Bushman/Negrito cases in his sources), matrilocal residence under family shortage or better subsistence in wife's territory (Algonkian as he cites Speck), remoteness limiting marriage pools. He held such social factors may make bands *temporarily* composite but ecological factors normally restore the typical pattern unless herd-game scale persists.

Comparative cases he tabled or discussed (secondary ethnography): northern Algonkian / Montagnais–Ojibway composite bands with later family trapping territories under fur trade; Andamanese composite small bands despite high density; selected Bushman and Negrito configurations. Cases are type illustrations, not separate society pages from this source alone (except where other sources warrant them).

## Applications

- Contrasts with [[patrilineal-band]] and with family-level [[great-basin-shoshoneans]] organization.
- Fur-trade shift toward individual territories among northern hunters (also [[carrier-indians]] latitude case in Ch. 10).
- Supports [[multilinear-evolution]]: "hunting and gathering" is not one stage.

## Critiques and Limitations

Second-hand comparative sample; ethnonyms and kinship labels mid-century; ecological dualism (herds vs social offsets) may underplay politics and colonial transformation. Steward himself notes Andamanese density as an anomaly relative to the scarce-food framing of many composite cases. Service’s contact-disruption thesis is itself contested by ethnographers who treat flexible bilateral bands as aboriginal; both positions remain attributed. At the 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium, Eggan and others argued the flexible bilateral form is the **baseline** and the patrilineal/patrilocal band a derived special case — reversing Service's assumption that composite bands are degraded patrilocal ones (see [[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]).
