# Maternal Wall

The **maternal wall** (Williams 2000, as used by [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Ridgeway]]) is the set of status biases and career obstacles faced by **mothers of dependent children** in the workplace. Ridgeway treats it as especially consequential for overall gender inequality because it affects most women workers at some career point — unlike the elite-focused [[glass-ceiling]].

## Definition as Coined

Ridgeway (2011) pairs the maternal wall with the glass ceiling as the two intensified obstacle sites where gender-frame biases become large barriers. The wall arises from clash between cultural schemas of **family devotion** (good mothers put family first, are "always there") and **devotion to work** / the [[ideal-worker-norm]] (workers ever available). Fathers face less of this contradiction because culture links their family role to **provider**, not primary daily caretaker (Blair-Loy 2003; Hays 1996; Acker 1990 — as cited by Ridgeway).

## Semantic History

Williams's legal/organizational formulation enters Ridgeway's status-frame synthesis: motherhood becomes a **status-relevant** cue that lowers performance expectations and raises doubts about commitment, net of individual productivity. Related wiki terms: [[second-shift]] (Hochschild — unpaid load), [[work-family-conflict]], [[stalled-revolution]].

## Operationalizations

Ridgeway reports (Correll, Benard, and Paik 2007 and related work, as cited): experimental and audit evidence of hiring and evaluation penalties for mothers; motherhood wage penalty on the order of roughly **5%** per child in U.S. studies she cites in the persistence discussion. Exact designs and magnitudes remain study-attributed, not wiki-voice law.

## Applications

- Explains why equal formal qualifications do not equalize careers once women become mothers.
- Links household [[household-division-of-labor|division of labor]] to workplace inequality (primary care responsibility feeds the wall).
- Policy relevance: leave, scheduling, ideal-worker redesign — change levers in Ridgeway Ch. 7.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Heterogeneity by class, race, partnership status, and job type.
- Fathers who are primary caregivers may face related but not identical penalties (not fully settled in Ridgeway 2011).
- Risk of reifying "mothers" as a homogeneous status category.
