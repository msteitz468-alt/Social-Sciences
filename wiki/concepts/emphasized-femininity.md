# Emphasized Femininity

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s companion concept to [[hegemonic-masculinity]], coined in *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987). It names the form of femininity organized around **compliance** with women's global subordination to men, oriented to accommodating men's interests and desires. The term does **not** appear in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995); that book presupposes the structural theory of *Gender and Power* without restating emphasized femininity.

## Definition as Coined

Connell argues that at the level of mass social relations there is no femininity that is *hegemonic* in the sense that the dominant form of masculinity is hegemonic among men — there is no form of femininity that holds cultural ascendancy over other women in the way hegemonic masculinity organizes relations among men. Forms of femininity are nonetheless clearly differentiated by the structural fact of men's global dominance:

- **Emphasized femininity** — "defined around compliance with this subordination and … oriented to accommodating the interests and desires of men."
- Other femininities — defined by **strategies of resistance** or **non-compliance**, or by **complex strategic combinations** of compliance, resistance, and co-operation.

The interplay among these forms is, on Connell's account, a major part of the dynamics of change in the [[gender-order]]. There is "likely to be a kind of 'fit'" between hegemonic masculinity and emphasized femininity: the former is a collective strategy institutionalizing men's dominance; the latter is the complementary cultural pattern that supports it.

## Semantic History

Coined in *Gender and Power* (1987), ch. 8 ("Sexual Character"), in the section "Hegemonic Masculinity and Emphasized Femininity." The concept travels with the wider [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]] into gender studies, education, and media analysis. Later literature sometimes loosely treats "emphasized femininity" as simply "traditional femininity" or as a mirror of hegemonic masculinity; Connell's own formulation is narrower — **compliance and accommodation**, not any culturally visible femininity, and **not** a claim that women hold a parallel hegemonic position. *Masculinities* (1995) develops the typology of masculinities (hegemonic / subordinated / complicit / marginalized) without re-theorizing emphasized femininity; the two books form a diptych.

## Operationalizations

Like gender order, emphasized femininity is analyzed rather than scored: media images, domestic division of labour, sexual display, and institutional gender regimes are read for the pattern of compliance/accommodation. It is not a trait scale and is not equivalent to high scores on masculinity/femininity psychometric inventories (which Connell critiques in the same chapter).

## Applications

- Completes the relational map of the [[gender-order]]: hegemonic masculinity is always constructed in relation to subordinated masculinities **and** to women; emphasized femininity is the primary cultural form of that relation on the women's side under patriarchy.
- Used in media and schooling studies of gender display, romance culture, and "emphasized" performances of femininity.
- Anchors critiques of unitary "female sex role" models — femininity is plural and ordered by strategy (compliance vs resistance), not a single role.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept has been less extensively reworked than hegemonic masculinity (which received the Connell & Messerschmidt 2005 reformulation). Critics of the broader framework sometimes treat emphasized femininity as under-specified relative to the multi-relational typology of masculinities — Connell himself notes that other femininities (resistance, strategic mixes) receive only brief treatment in the 1987 statement. The concept is also vulnerable to being collapsed into "traditional gender norms" without the structural link to men's dominance that is load-bearing in Connell's definition.
