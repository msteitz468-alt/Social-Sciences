# Despotic Power

## Definition as Coined

For [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (1984; *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]* 1986, esp. Chs. 1, 5, 8, 13–15), **despotic power** is the *elite autonomy* face of state power:

> "Despotic power refers to the range of actions that the ruler and his staff are empowered to attempt to implement without routine, institutionalized negotiation with civil society groups."

High formal despotism can coexist with low [[infrastructural-power|infrastructural power]] (spectacular but shallow rule) or, in modern states, with high infrastructure (deep plus autonomous). Divine claims (e.g. Naram-Sin, Mann's narrative) justify personal despotism in theory while practice remains feeble — contra Wittfogel-style "absolute" readings.

## Semantic History

Paired with infrastructural power as Mann's two-dimensional state map (also in the 1984 *AES* essay); widely cited in debates on [[state-autonomy]] and comparative empires vs. modern states. Introduced in Vol. I Ch. 1 alongside geopolitical dual types: hegemonic empire vs multistate civilization. "Despotic" here is technical, not only pejorative Orientalist residue — though reception sometimes re-imports that charge. Mann explicitly rejects equating eastern "abjectness" with real infrastructural control.

## Operationalizations

Qualitative range of unnegotiated elite decisions (life, property, war, religion, law-making monopoly) vs. constrained constitutional/corporate/estates regimes. Separate "strong state" into despotic vs infrastructural dimensions — a single "strong/weak" axis confounds them.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Empires of domination (Chs. 5, 8)** — Akkadian and later Near Eastern imperial form: high formal despotism, dependence on aristocrats for infrastructure; Persian Great King monopoly of kingship and prostration rituals alongside feeble provincial penetration; "constitutional rather than real" despotism relative to Greek intensive polis control.
- **Rome** — Formal imperial despotism contrasts with medieval Europe, where Mann argues despotic power over society was **not** a formal characteristic ("unlike Rome"); medieval coordination of estates could yield high collective power without unitary despotism.
- **Early modern Europe (Ch. 14)** — Absolutism as ideal type: sole human source of law + permanent bureaucracy/army. Mann holds absolutist states were not infrastructurally stronger than constitutional ones; remaining difference is mainly despotic power. Constitutional England/Holland more [[organic-national-state|organic]] (taxed propertied classes with consent); absolutism more divisions/exclusions, blocked organic capitalist class — war punished infrastructural weakness (Spain, France, as Mann narrates). European monarch no ancient emperor of [[compulsory-cooperation]]; private property deeply embedded.

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary risk; intermediate corporate, religious, and clientelist constraints need careful case work. "Despotic" reception can reimport Orientalist caricature Mann partly critiques. Keep attributed to Mann.
