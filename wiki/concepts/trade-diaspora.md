# Trade Diaspora

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Abner Cohen 1969, 1971; the archaeological adaptation is
[[stein-gil|Stein]] 1999.)*

The anthropologist **Abner Cohen** coined *trade diaspora* to describe the Hausa
merchant enclaves living among Yoruba hosts in the towns of Nigeria and West Africa.
Cohen's definition (1971): trade diasporas are "interregional exchange networks composed
of spatially dispersed specialized merchant groups that are culturally distinct,
organizationally cohesive, and socially independent from their host communities while
maintaining a high level of economic and social ties with related communities who define
themselves in terms of the same general cultural identity." Cohen stressed that such
diasporas emerge where communication and transport are difficult and no centralized state
guarantees the security of long-distance exchange: an ethnic group solves the problem by
monopolizing stages of the trade and organizing itself as a corporate entity, bound
together by "an integrated ideological scheme" — myths, norms, and values, often anchored
in a "universal" religion (Cohen 1971:276; Stein notes the recurrent tie to Confucianism,
Hinduism, Islam, and Judaism). Cohen's *trade diaspora* is distinct from the broader,
later usage of "diaspora" in globalization studies (Clifford 1994) — a distinction Stein
flags explicitly.

## Semantic History

The term migrated from the ethnography of West African complex societies into world
history through **Philip D. Curtin's** *Cross-Cultural Trade in World History* (1984),
and from there into archaeology chiefly through [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]], who adopted it as
a **"neutral construct that frees us from the conceptual baggage inherent in the modern
use of the term 'colony'"** — the assumption, inherited from 16th–19th-century European
overseas expansion, that a colony necessarily dominates its host. On Stein's cross-cultural
definition a colony is simply "an implanted settlement established by one society in the
territory of another society," and a trade colony is "a territorially grounded form of
trade-diaspora settlement"; whether the enclave dominates its host, coexists with it, or is
subordinate to it is left an **open empirical question** rather than assumed. This
re-opening of the power question is the concept's whole analytic point, and the hinge of
the [[uruk-world-system-debate]].

## The Power Continuum and the "Working Themselves Out of Business" Pattern

Cohen and Curtin locate trade-diaspora–host relations on a continuum of power (attributed):

- **Pariah / marginal status** — e.g. Jewish diasporas in medieval Europe; the Dyula
  under the Fulani Massina state.
- **Protected autonomy** — the Chinese trade diaspora in Southeast Asia; the Jahaanke
  (Diakhanke) of 17th–18th-century Senegambia, who traded gold and salt across ~1,000 km
  by a deliberate "strategy of vulnerability" — pacifism, neutrality, and Islamic devotion
  that kept them outside host factional politics (Curtin 1971, 1984).
- **Diaspora domination of the host** — the European trading-post empires in Africa and
  Asia (English in India, Dutch in Indonesia), which grew into territorial empires.

Curtin's rule links diaspora structure to host strength (attributed): "when the local
elites in the host community can dominate the alien enclave, there will be weak links
between diaspora nodes; when local rulers are weak, trade-diasporas can maintain strong
links between nodes and with their homeland." He also observed that trade diasporas tend to
**"work themselves out of business"** as their cross-cultural brokering gradually erases
the very cultural difference that made them necessary — dissolving into one of three
outcomes: withdrawal/expulsion (the Hanseatic League from London; Indians from Uganda),
transformation into a resident ethnic minority (Chinese in Southeast Asia), or assimilation
into the host. Richard White's *Middle Ground* (1991) supplies the limiting weak-diaspora
case: French fur traders in the *pays d'en haut*, too few and too far to coerce, had to
mediate and gift-give — an alliance-dependent, not dominant, diaspora.

## Operationalizations (archaeological)

[[stein-gil|Stein]] specifies how to recognize a trade-diaspora enclave in the material
record, insisting the identification rest on a **broad complex** of traits rather than one
artifact class (ceramics alone index style, not ethnicity — Kramer 1977; Santley et al.
1987): homeland-style architecture, ceramics, diet and butchery practice, craft technology,
and administrative apparatus, set off spatially and functionally from surrounding local
material. Santley, Yarborough, and Berg's (1987) Teotihuacan enclave at Matacapan supplies
the template — ethnic identity marked at both an enclave-wide public level (ceremonial
architecture, dress, burial) and a domestic level (homeland culinary practice, imported
ritual paraphernalia). Stein warns of the diagnostic floor: complete acculturation can make
an enclave archaeologically invisible — the Old Assyrian *karum* at Kanesh (Kültepe) used
entirely local architecture and pottery and was identifiable as a colony **only** through
its cuneiform business archives (Özgüç 1963).

## Applications

The concept's flagship archaeological application is Stein's reading of the
[[uruk-expansion|Uruk expansion]]: the segregated Uruk quarter at [[hacinebi|Hacınebi
Tepe]] is analyzed as an autonomous trade diaspora that "dealt with its host community as
an equal," coexisting for 3–5 centuries without dominating it — the empirical core of the
[[stein-hacinebi-study|Hacınebi study]]. Godin Tepe V in the Iranian Zagros (Young 1986,
read as "a small, tolerated trade-diaspora") is Stein's second Uruk case. Stein pairs the
trade-diaspora model (the "bottom-up," local-scale analytic of enclave–host–homeland power
balance) with the large-scale [[distance-parity|distance-parity model]]: the variables of
one feed directly into the other. Comparanda he invokes include the Oaxaca Barrio at
Teotihuacan (Spence 1976) and the 17th-century European trading enclave at Savi in the
kingdom of Hueda (Kelly 1997).

## Critiques and Limitations

Because a fully acculturated enclave can vanish materially, absence of a diagnosed trade
diaspora is weak evidence of absence. And because the model deliberately holds the
power relation open, applying it does not by itself settle whether a given enclave was
autonomous or dominated — that is the empirical work, and the site of dispute in the
[[uruk-world-system-debate]]. The concept nonetheless does real corrective work against the
default equation *colony = domination* that [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] and
[[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]] framings tend to smuggle in.
