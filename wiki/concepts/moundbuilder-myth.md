# Moundbuilder Myth

## Definition as Coined

The **Moundbuilder myth** was the widespread eighteenth- and nineteenth-century
belief among European-Americans that the thousands of earthen mounds and
earthworks from the Great Lakes and Nebraska through the Midwest to Florida
**could not have been built by American Indians**, and were instead the work of a
vanished non-Indian race — variously imagined as Phoenicians, Hebrews, Tartars,
or a lost white warrior civilization. As documented by
[[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]], the myth is a paradigm case of racialized
prehistory: it survives in the wiki as a **history-of-the-discipline** object, not
as a live claim. It is the New World twin of the "vanished race" reflex that also
denied Maya ruins to Native ancestry.

## Semantic History

Fagan frames the myth's persistence as "based on racist assumptions that American
Indians were incapable of much more than the simplest of tasks" (attributed).
Its documented promoters and refuters:

- **Josiah Priest**, *American Antiquities and Discoveries in the West* (early
  1830s), sold 20,000+ copies depicting the mounds as the work of a vanished race
  of white warrior-moundbuilders (attributed).
- **Caleb Atwater**, postmaster of Circleville, Ohio, surveyed and excavated
  mounds but refused to attribute them to Indian manufacture, proposing non-Indian
  migrants from Asia (attributed).
- **Ephraim Squier & Edwin Davis**, *Ancient Monuments of the Mississippi Valley*
  (Smithsonian, 1848), described Indians as "hunters averse to labor" and the
  moundbuilders as non-Indian "defensive experts" (attributed).
- Countervailing voices: **Samuel Haven**'s *Archaeology of the United States*
  (Smithsonian, 1856) — the first systematic scholarly critique, arguing Asian
  origin and Indian ancestry; **Samuel Morton** (1839), who on (now-obsolete,
  itself racially-typological) craniometric grounds assigned mounds and modern
  Indians to one "Mongolian race."

## Operationalizations

The myth was **empirically refuted**. In 1882 Congress funded the Smithsonian's
Bureau of Ethnology (under [[unknown]] — John Wesley Powell) to investigate; Powell
appointed **Cyrus Thomas**, whose seven years of fieldwork and hundreds of
excavations (Florida to Nebraska) established definitively that the moundbuilders
were **ancestral American Indians**, not a separate vanished race (a settled,
wiki-voice finding). This is treated on the wiki as the refutation event.

## Applications

The episode is a standing exhibit in the **reflexivity** of American archaeology
(see [[archaeology]]): how nineteenth-century racial ideology and settler-colonial
dispossession shaped what counted as a credible prehistory, and how disciplinary
professionalization (Powell, Thomas) overturned it. It parallels the
[[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]]–Holmes "no early man" orthodoxy as a case where
authority and assumption, not evidence, set the terms.

## Critiques and Limitations

The refutation is not in dispute; the interpretive caution is that the myth's
"vanished race" structure recurs (California-skull and other extreme-antiquity
claims) whenever prehistory is read to detach living peoples from an impressive
past. Do not treat the older racial-typological *counter*-arguments (Morton's
craniometry) as valid science merely because they reached the correct conclusion.
