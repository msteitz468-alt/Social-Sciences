# First Fundamental Law of Capitalism

Accounting identity that organizes the capital–labor split in [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* and [[piketty-capital-dynamics]].

## Definition as Coined

Piketty’s **first fundamental law**: the capital share of national income **α** equals the product of the average rate of return on capital **r** and the capital/income ratio **β**:

**α = r × β**

Example he repeats: if national wealth is six years of national income (β = 600% or 6) and r = 5% per year, capital’s share α = 30% (labor 70%). He presents the formula as a **pure accounting identity**, valid at all times and places by construction given the definitions—yet still names it the “first fundamental law” because it links the three most important concepts for analyzing capitalism. It does **not** determine β, α, or r independently (two degrees of freedom remain).

Around 2010 in wealthy countries, he reports capital income generally hovered around **30% of national income**; with β on the order of 600%, that implied r around **5%**. Traditional conversion of capital to rent in the eighteenth–nineteenth centuries for least-risky assets (land, public debt) was generally ~5%/year (capital ≈ 20 years’ income), sometimes 4% (25 years)—treated as common knowledge in Balzac and Austen.

## Semantic History

The relation is arithmetic once α, r, and β are defined from the same accounts; dressing it as a “law” is pedagogical and programmatic. The deeper historiographic fight it organizes is the mid-twentieth-century belief in a **stable capital–labor split** (Cobb–Douglas α fixed; Keynes 1939 calling stability “one of the best-established regularities” on thin British manufacturing data) versus Marxist expectations of a rising capital share. Piketty’s U-curve for α challenges both the peaceful fixed-split view and simple infinite-rising-α apocalypse. Contrasted with the [[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]: the first is identity; the second is a long-run dynamic equilibrium.

## Operationalizations

- **α**: capital incomes in national accounts (rents, profits, dividends, interest, royalties, etc., excluding interest on public debt, before taxes) ÷ national income.
- **r**: same capital-income aggregate ÷ national capital stock (β’s numerator).
- **β**: capital stock ÷ national income (years of income); see [[capital-income-ratio]].
- **Mixed-income uncertainty**: for the current period ≤1–2% of NI; for eighteenth–nineteenth centuries mixed incomes may have been over half of NI, so early capital-share estimates are intervals (Piketty prefers **35–40%** for that era); landlord rents alone ~20% of NI in Britain and France in the eighteenth/early nineteenth centuries.
- **Pretax vs after-tax**: figures for r in the book’s long series are pretax; average tax rate on capital income ~30% in most rich countries today, so after-tax return to owners fell more than pretax pure economic return.

Historical orders of magnitude Piketty reports for Britain and France: α ~**35–40%** late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries; **20–25%** mid-twentieth; **25–30%** late twentieth/early twenty-first—same U-shape as β but shallower. Across rich countries 1970–2010, capital income ~**15–25%** of NI in 1970 vs **25–30%** in 2000–2010. France detail: capital share of NI (net profits + rents) **15% (1982) → 27% (2010)**; housing rent **2% (1948) → 10% (2010)** of NI.

## Applications

Part Two (especially capital–labor split chapters) of *Capital* and [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]]: bridges stock (β) and flow (α) sides of distribution. When β rises, whether α rises depends on how fast r falls—the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor. Piketty argues history favors elasticity >1: α co-moves with β’s U-curve; postwar high r when capital was scarce reduced the amplitude of α’s fall but did not invert the U into an inverted-U. Links to [[r-greater-than-g]] and [[patrimonial-capitalism]]: a high β with durable r ~4–5% keeps capital income macroeconomically large even if slightly below nineteenth-century peaks. “Engels’ pause” (Allen): British capital share rose ~10 points of NI to ~45–50% by mid-nineteenth century when Marx wrote, then partly reversed—medium-term, not durable long-term, trend.

## Critiques and Limitations

As identity, true by construction; debate is over measured r and β, treatment of housing and mixed income, and causal stories hanging on α’s path ([[piketty-capital-debate]]). Cobb–Douglas (α fixed technology parameter) remains a rival modeling habit: if α is constant at 30%, then β = 6 implies r = 5%, β = 3 implies r = 10%, β = 10 implies r = 3%—stability of α does not, Piketty stresses, guarantee social harmony or a stable β. Whether modern technology is “favorable to labor” enough to prevent α returning toward early-nineteenth levels is a live interpretive dispute he records with caution.
