# Informationalism

Castells’s term for a **[[mode-of-development|mode of development]]** in which the source of productivity lies in the technology of knowledge generation, information processing, and symbol communication — *knowledge acting upon knowledge*. Distinct from industrialism as mode of development and from capitalism/statism as modes of production. See *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Prologue and Chs. 1–2.

## Definition as Coined

Modes of development (agrarian, industrial, informational) are technological arrangements through which labor works on matter to generate product and surplus. In the informational mode, information processing focuses on improving the technology of information processing itself in a virtuous circle. The emerging society is **both capitalist and informational**: IT originated and diffused during global capitalist restructuring, for which it was an essential tool. Informationalism *alters but does not replace* the dominant mode of production.

**IT paradigm (material foundation, Ch. 1, attributed):** (1) information as raw material — technologies to act on information, not only information to act on technology; (2) pervasive effects; (3) networking logic; (4) flexibility; (5) growing convergence of technologies into a highly integrated system. “For the first time in history, the human mind is a direct productive force.”

## Semantic History

Differentiated from Bell-type post-industrialism and from “information society” slogans. Castells insists on the dual axis: production (capitalism/statism) × development (industrialism/informationalism), following but reworking Touraine and Bell. Related organizational culture: [[spirit-of-informationalism]].

## Operationalizations

Techno-economic paradigm (Freeman-influenced); productivity debates (Solow residual / productivity paradox treated at length in Ch. 2); organizational forms ([[network-enterprise]]); employment restructuring under the informational paradigm (Ch. 4).

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; analysis of the informational/global/networked economy; East Asian and Western organizational trajectories.

## Critiques and Limitations

Periodization and measurement of “informational” productivity contested; risk of tech-centrism despite anti-determinist framing; G-7 and advanced-economy empirical weight in Vol. I.
