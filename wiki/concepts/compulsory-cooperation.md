# Compulsory Cooperation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]* (1986, esp. Ch. 5; continuing through Chs. 8–9), [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] adapts Herbert Spencer's "compulsory cooperation" (militant society) for early **[[empire-of-domination|empires of domination]]**: postconquest economic relationships increase surplus, give fragile economic unity, and extract the state's share — only by increasing coercion in the economy at large. Naked repression/exploitation and common benefit are inseparable:

> "The peculiarity of this is the inseparability of naked repression and exploitation from more or less common benefit."

Through techniques of compulsory cooperation, Mann argues, the "leading edge" of power shifted from [[multi-power-actor-civilization|multi-power-actor civilizations]] to empires of domination.

## Semantic History

Mann's label for a recurring imperial pattern rather than a single-case emic term. Adjacent to older "Oriental despotism" / hydraulic theses and to liberal/parasitic-empire theories (Smithian natural growth; Wesson; Kautsky; Ekholm & Friedman core/periphery parasitism), which Mann rejects or reorganizes under [[iemp-model|IEMP]] logistics: the imperial state *helps create* accumulation while generating decentralizing elites. Not identical to concentrated-coercive military power as such, though military organization is the pivot into the form.

## Operationalizations

Four strategies of imperial rule (Mann Ch. 5): (1) rule through clients; (2) direct army rule; (3) **compulsory cooperation**; (4) common ruling-class culture. Strategies 3–4 require complex infrastructures; only (3) flourishes in early Near Eastern empires; both massive by Rome.

Five economic processes of compulsory cooperation (Mann): (1) **military pacification** (trade protection); (2) **military multiplier** (army demand; roads/staging posts; later "military Keynesianism"); (3) **authoritative imposition of economic value** (weights/measures, contracts, law, quasi-coinage); (4) **intensification of labor through coercion** (corvée, slavery, serfdom); (5) **coerced diffusion of techniques/culture** through conquest. Deportation, fortresses, and militarized colonization appear as packages in his secondary synthesis.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ch. 5 dialectic** — Compulsory cooperation increases militaristic state power (thesis) and decentralized elites who can overthrow the state (antithesis); elites still need imposed order → reconstitution at higher collective power (synthesis). Infrastructure is double-edged (coinage, literacy diffuse into society). Core/periphery military contradictions: professionalization weakens communal cohesion; success raises marcher threats.
- **Assyria (Ch. 8)** — Army option plus some compulsory cooperation (palaces, state plows, grain reserves, deportation-as-colonization); Iron Age decentralization made pure compulsory cooperation less productive while cosmopolitan class culture rose.
- **Persia** — Less tight compulsory cooperation; sat loose on Middle Eastern cosmopolitanism with client vassals.
- **Rome (Ch. 9)** — Most developed preindustrial example of Spencerian compulsory cooperation; **legionary economy** plus ruling-class culture absorbing conquered elites; transition from empire of domination toward **territorial empire**.
- **Medieval Europe (Chs. 12–13)** — Compulsory cooperation "swept aside by Christendom’s [[normative-pacification]], and the European state never recovered it" (Mann) — states live off protection rent and judicial coordination rather than ancient-style militarized redistribution. Greek/Phoenician multi-power fields likewise lacked central production-redistribution cycles of compulsory cooperation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Depends on dated Assyriological, archaeological, and Roman secondary summaries; do not promote to wiki-voice site fact without later specialist sources. "Productive militarism" thesis is contested in empire studies — keep as Mann's position.
