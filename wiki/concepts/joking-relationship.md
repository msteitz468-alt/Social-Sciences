# Joking Relationship

An analytical concept named and theorized by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] in "On Joking Relationships" (*Africa*, 1940) and "A Further Note on Joking Relationships" (*Africa*, 1949), both collected in *Structure and Function in Primitive Society* (1952). It designates a standardized social relation of licensed mockery, and became a textbook demonstration of [[structural-functionalism|structural-functionalist]] method.

## Definition as Coined

Radcliffe-Brown defined it (attributed): "The joking relationship is a peculiar combination of friendliness and antagonism. The behaviour is such that in any other social context it would express and arouse hostility; but it is not meant seriously and must not be taken seriously... To put it in another way, the relationship is one of permitted disrespect." He distinguished **symmetrical** forms (both parties joke) from **asymmetrical** ones (one jokes, the other tolerates), and contrasted the whole class with its structural opposite, the **avoidance relationship** (extreme mutual respect, e.g. mother-in-law avoidance).

## Semantic History

The concept translates and generalizes the French *parenté à plaisanterie*. Radcliffe-Brown's originality was to fit it into a general theory of social structure: joking and avoidance are two alternative, stable solutions to one structural problem — a relation combining **social conjunction** (attachment, the need to stay connected) and **social disjunction** (divergence of interest, the possibility of conflict), as typically arises between a man and his wife's kin. "Both the joking relationship which constitutes an alliance between clans or tribes, and that between relatives by marriage, are modes of organising a definite and stable system of social behaviour in which conjunctive and disjunctive components... are maintained and combined." He placed joking among four "modes of alliance or consociation": intermarriage, exchange of goods/services, blood-brotherhood/name-exchange, and the joking relationship.

## Operationalizations

Discrimination between respected and joking kin is read off structural position — typically **generation** (first ascending generation = respect, to preserve the authority that transmits tradition; alternate generations = mild joking, grandparents "retiring" as grandchildren enter social life) — with recognized exceptions (the mother's brother in patrilineal systems). The relation is "in some ways the exact opposite of a contractual relation": no specific enforceable duty, only the obligation not to take offence within customary bounds. Radcliffe-Brown analysed Crow, Cherokee, Ojibwa, Dogon–Bozo, and Tallensi cases to show the pattern tracks indirect structural connection between separated groups.

## Applications

Beyond kinship, Radcliffe-Brown extended the idea to **clan and tribal** joking alliances (groups not linked by intermarriage) and to a still wider category of "friendship... in which there is an appearance of antagonism, controlled by conventional rules" (he cited sporting rivalry as a mild instance). See [[social-structure]], [[social-function]], [[structural-functionalism]].

### Gluckman political densification (1965)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual]]*, Ch. III; closing Eskimo song-contest hypothesis in Ch. VII.)* Among the [[plateau-tonga|Plateau Tonga]], **clan-joking** has political consequences: more friends for all, and **privileged moral arbiters** in a society without political authorities — “strangers” who can rebuke defaults on exogamy, property, and food rules. Court **jesters** / African praise-singers are treated as institutionalized jokers at the apex of a political unit (same structural bind: protest admits the hit). Gluckman’s concluding reanalysis of **Eskimo song-contests** (Keewatin; Ammassalik) reads derision duels partly through joking/cross-cutting lenses rather than as pure “courts” (vs Hoebel’s legal reading). Ethnonym retained as in sources; modern Inuit/Yupik identity cautions apply.

## Critiques and Limitations

The 1949 "Further Note" is itself a methodological defence: against Marcel Griaule's particularistic reading of the Dogon–Bozo "cathartic alliance," Radcliffe-Brown argued that adopting a society's own explanation does not preclude comparative-structural analysis of the institution as one instance of a widespread type. Later anthropology treated the concept as a paradigm case of structural-functionalism's strengths and limits: elegant at correlating custom with structural position, but weak on meaning, agency, and historical change, and prone to circularity (the function is inferred from the very relation it is meant to explain). The descriptive category endures; the equilibrium-functionalist explanation is contested.

## ASKAM Introduction densification (1950)

In the Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, R-B folded joking into the broader analysis of **affinal reverse kinship** and marriage as temporary disequilibrium (attributed; claims range 2, §IX). Key densifications beyond the 1940/1949 essays:

1. **Definition restated.** Joking relationship = conventionalized disrespect expected between equals (especially wife’s siblings): behaviour that would be insulting outside the institution, but is required and must not be taken seriously—paired structurally with **avoidance** of parents-in-law.
2. **Avoidance as friendship-preserving.** R-B reported an Aboriginal explanation that one avoids mother-in-law because she is “my best friend in the world: she has given me my wife”—limited contact prevents quarrels that would break friendship. Avoidance is thus not primarily hostility, but regulated distance.
3. **Marriage disequilibrium and Nguni bride integration.** Marriage removes a daughter and introduces a stranger; among Nguni the bride gives presents/services to husband’s women until accepted; her natal personal name may not be used in the husband’s family (new name)—she is a different person in the two groups; avoidance may relax as marriage develops.
4. **“Shame” (hloni / ntloni) as imposed constraint.** Against Westermarck’s sexual-shame reading (and Nadel’s sexual-shame reading of Nuba food avoidances), R-B argued Bantu *hloni* is felt social constraint/distance imposed by etiquette, not specifically sexual shame; food and name avoidances maintain distance; Freudian eating-as-sex symbolism is unnecessary. Explicit disagreement with [[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] on Nuba avoidances is recorded here as a **position**, not adjudicated.
5. **Privileged familiarity and the [[avunculate]].** Sister’s-son joking/taking of MB’s property (Winnebago; Fiji/Tonga *vasu*; South Africa per Junod) is treated as the same structural family of conventionalized privilege—generation and lineage position prescribe when respect, avoidance, or joking applies (Introduction §V–VI, range 1).

**Shared social function (ASKAM §IX):** R-B held avoidance and joking have the same general function after marriage — two families remain separate yet must maintain friendly relations; reciprocal joking is friendliness as sham hostility (not taken as offence); avoidance symbolically expresses social distance and respect toward parents-in-law (distinct from authority-respect to father/MB); maximum tension between wife’s mother and the son-in-law who received control and sexual rights over her daughter. Mother-in-law avoidance ranges from total face-to-face prohibition (Ganda) to name/food taboos; incest-prevention theories of avoidance rejected as unscientific speculation. Ashanti (Fortes): no conventionalized joking in kinship relations (nearest = privileged familiarity with some neighbouring Akan); contrast with systems where wife’s siblings joke by rule.
