# Significant Symbol

## Definition as Coined

For [[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]] (in "A Behavioristic Account of the Significant Symbol," 1922, and *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]*, 1934), a **significant symbol** is a [[gesture]] — paradigmatically a *vocal* gesture — that "has the same effect on the individual making it that it has on the individual to whom it is addressed." A gesture becomes significant when it "implicitly arouse[s] in an individual making [it] the same responses which [it] explicitly arouse[s]... in other individuals." Its **meaning** is "the act or response which it calls forth explicitly in the individual to whom it is addressed, and implicitly in the individual who makes it" (attributed).

The significant symbol is thus more than a substitute stimulus: "the symbol is... more than a mere stimulus for a conditioned response or reflex. For the conditioned reflex... does not or need not involve consciousness; whereas the response to a symbol does and must involve consciousness" (attributed). Only through significant symbols is mind possible, "for only in terms of gestures which are significant symbols can thinking—which is simply an internalized or implicit conversation of the individual with himself—take place."

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Mead, 1922/1934 | A gesture that calls out the same response in its maker as in its recipient; the unit of language, mind, and shared meaning |
| Later [[symbolic-interactionism]] (Blumer) | Generalized to all "symbolic" objects toward which actors act on the basis of interactionally-formed meaning |
| Communication theory (Habermas) | Read as the germ of "communicative" (vs. instrumental) action |

Because Part II of *Mind, Self, and Society* is largely editorial reconstruction, the crispest definitional sentences here are lecture-note text arranged by Morris (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]); Mead's own 1922 paper is the securer authorial witness.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Mead's criterion is behavioral: does the gesture arouse in the maker the response it arouses in the other? The vocal gesture qualifies uniquely because "it is only the vocal gesture to which one responds or tends to respond as another person tends to respond to it" — one hears oneself as others hear one, which a facial gesture (unseen by its maker) cannot achieve.

## Applications

- Foundation of Mead's account of [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]] and the [[the-social-self|social self]] — "the same procedure which is responsible for the genesis... of mind... also necessarily involves the genesis... of significant symbols."
- The "universe of discourse" — "a system of common or social meanings" — is the field of shared significant symbols, generalized in the [[generalized-other|generalized other]].
- Mead's account of meaning as **triadic** (gesture → adjustive response of the other → resultant of the social act) grounds the symbol's meaning objectively "within the field" of the act rather than in consciousness.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The privileging of the *vocal* gesture has been questioned by later work on gesture, sign language, and embodied interaction.
- The strongest formulations in the ingested text are editorially mediated (Morris/Page), so quotation should distinguish Mead's 1922 authorial prose from the 1934 reconstructed lectures.
- As an etic construct it treats "meaning" behaviorally; interpretive critics hold this underplays the hermeneutic dimension the concept was later used to recover.
