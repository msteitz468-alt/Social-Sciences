# Social Disorganization

## Definition as Coined

In the [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|Polish Peasant]] program, **disorganization** names the process by which primary-group rules lose influence over members under rapid change; **reorganization** is the counter-process (attributed; peaks in later volumes not fully ingested).

In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925), [[park-robert-e|Park]] makes the link explicit: what Thomas calls "**individualization**" from the individual side is, from the community side, **social disorganization** — the undermining of earlier culture and social-control systems. Present society appears a "congeries and constellation of social atoms" (attributed, Park Ch V).

Park also writes of dissolution of the moral order when primary restraints weaken — "largely responsible for the increase of vice and crime" — registered in divorce, truancy, and crime statistics (attributed, Ch I), without always using the technical phrase in Ch I.

[[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] treats urban growth as organization/disorganization "metabolism"; rapid expansion yields excess disease, crime, disorder, vice, insanity, suicide as "rough indexes of social disorganization"; disorganization pointing toward more efficient reorganization "must be conceived not as pathological, but as normal" when reciprocal with organization (attributed, Ch II).

## Semantic History

| Source | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Thomas & Znaniecki 1918–20 | Primary-group rule loss under migration/industry |
| Park *The City* 1925 | Community-side of individualization; delinquency as measure of community organizational failure |
| Burgess 1925 | Metabolic balance; mobility as cause-index; zone of deterioration concentration |
| Shaw & McKay (reception) | Delinquency mapped by area — not yet ingested |
| Later critique / Sampson | "Disorganized community" label contested; place-based mechanisms retained as neighborhood effects |

## Operationalizations

- Documentary case analysis (Thomas & Znaniecki).
- Ecological rates and area coincidence of pathology with mobility (Burgess).
- Delinquency as "measure of the failure of our community organizations to function" (Park).
- Immigrant "control organizations" (Japanese Association, Chinese Six Companies) as buffers (Park Ch VI).

## Applications

- [[homans-george-c|Homans]] (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*) splits “social disorganization” into **disintegration** (Hilltown: declining collaborative activities, interaction, and control → anomie) vs **social conflict** (Electrical Equipment: subgroup vs whole; differences of degree not kind).


Immigrant adjustment; urban delinquency and gangs; neighborhood work; critiques of individual-pathology crime models.

## Critiques and Limitations

Normative and ethnocentric load; confuses cultural difference with breakdown; underplays structural exclusion; reworked as collective efficacy / neighborhood effects (reception).
