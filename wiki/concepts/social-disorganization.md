# Social Disorganization

## Definition as Coined

In the [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|Polish Peasant]] program, **disorganization** names the process by which primary-group rules lose influence over members under rapid change; **reorganization** is the counter-process (attributed; peaks in later volumes not fully ingested).

In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925), [[park-robert-e|Park]] makes the link explicit: what Thomas calls "**individualization**" from the individual side is, from the community side, **social disorganization** — the undermining of earlier culture and social-control systems. Present society appears a "congeries and constellation of social atoms" (attributed, Park Ch V).

Park also writes of dissolution of the moral order when primary restraints weaken — "largely responsible for the increase of vice and crime" — registered in divorce, truancy, and crime statistics (attributed, Ch I), without always using the technical phrase in Ch I.

[[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] treats urban growth as organization/disorganization "metabolism"; rapid expansion yields excess disease, crime, disorder, vice, insanity, suicide as "rough indexes of social disorganization"; disorganization pointing toward more efficient reorganization "must be conceived not as pathological, but as normal" when reciprocal with organization (attributed, Ch II).

## Operationalizations

- Documentary case analysis (Thomas & Znaniecki).
- Ecological rates and area coincidence of pathology with mobility (Burgess).
- Delinquency as "measure of the failure of our community organizations to function" (Park).
- Immigrant "control organizations" (Japanese Association, Chinese Six Companies) as buffers (Park Ch VI).
- **Canonical later definition** (as stated by Sampson 2012): inability of a community to realize common values of residents and maintain effective social controls; systemic operationalization via network density (critiqued by Sampson as necessary but incomplete).
- **[[collective-efficacy]] / ecometric scales** (Sampson–Raudenbush): cohesion/trust + shared expectations for informal control, measured independently of crime outcomes.

## Applications

- [[homans-george-c|Homans]] (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*) splits “social disorganization” into **disintegration** (Hilltown: declining collaborative activities, interaction, and control → anomie) vs **social conflict** (Electrical Equipment: subgroup vs whole; differences of degree not kind).
- Immigrant adjustment; urban delinquency and gangs; neighborhood work; critiques of individual-pathology crime models.
- Late-20th/21st-c [[neighborhood-effects]] research and PHDCN (*[[sampson-great-american-city-study|Great American City]]*).

### Sampson / PHDCN (2012)

[[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] treats social disorganization as the "herpes of criminology" (Bursik–Grasmick anecdote he attributes likely to Reiss): repeatedly declared dead, it resurged with neighborhood-effects work. He accepts the premise that **community-level variation in social control** contributes to crime and wellbeing, but argues classical and systemic versions fail when:

1. **Tautology** — disorganization defined by crime/disorder itself (Bursik's point as Sampson restates it).
2. **Dense-ties ideal** — high-crime areas can be densely organized (Whyte; Reiss paradox; Pattillo, Venkatesh: dense ties can *impede* ridding neighborhoods of gangs/drugs); Wilson on poor neighborhoods tightly interconnected yet without collective resources.
3. **Urban-village myth** — shared expectations for control can form without thick neighbor ties (Granovetter weak ties; mathematical impossibility of friendship-based citywide order).

His reformulation is **[[collective-efficacy]]**: social cohesion + shared expectations for intervening in support of neighborhood social control — not dense intimate ties alone. See [[social-disorganization-theory]] · [[sampson-great-american-city-2012]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Normative and ethnocentric load; confuses cultural difference with breakdown; underplays structural exclusion; reworked as collective efficacy / neighborhood effects (Sampson 2012; reception). Early ethnic-homogeneity expectation for control not supported by Sampson's CE maps (mixed high-CE South Side communities). Contemporary immigration–crime link of founding SD fails on PHDCN facts as Sampson reports them (immigration often protective).
