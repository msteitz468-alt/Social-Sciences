# Ethics of Parts

## Definition as Coined

**Ethics of parts** is [[cohen-lawrence|Lawrence Cohen]]’s term (1999; developed in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]*) for a late-modern moral framing in which the **divisible body** (and its “owners”) are presumed to respond rationally to market demand — an ethics organized around **parts** rather than the whole person or embodied integrity. Scheper-Hughes cites it in the volume introduction as the ethical face of [[body-commodification]].

## Semantic History

Coined in Cohen’s “Where it Hurts: Indian Material for an Ethics of Organ Transplantation” (*Daedalus* 1999); elaborated in “The Other Kidney: Biopolitics beyond Recognition” (2001/02), which links tissue-typing, cyclosporine-enabled expansion of donor pools, and Indian kidney sales to a biopolitics of **recognition and suppression**.

## Operationalizations

Primarily interpretive: tracks how antirejection pharmacology and tissue-typing recode who can “match,” enabling quiet and overt markets that treat kidneys as alienable stock.

## Applications

Indian renal transplantation ethnography; critique of bioethical “win-win” paid donation narratives (paired with Scheper-Hughes Organs Watch work).

## Critiques and Limitations

The term is polemical-analytic rather than a measured construct; its force depends on ethnographic and media evidence of actual part-markets, not only on philosophical description.
