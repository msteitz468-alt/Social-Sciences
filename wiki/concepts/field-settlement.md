# Field Settlement

*(Attributed — Fligstein & McAdam 2012.)* A **settlement** is the relatively stable package of understandings and power relations that allows a [[strategic-action-field]] to be reproduced over time: agreement (or enforced acquiescence) on what is at stake, who holds which positions, what rules apply, and how moves are interpreted. Settlements vary along a continuum from rare high-consensus fields to conflictual but still structured orders.

## Definition as Coined

Reproduction is not automatic taken-for-grantedness: incumbents and challengers continue to jockey; [[social-skill]] maintains coalitions; [[internal-governance-unit|internal governance units]] and allies in proximate state/nonstate fields shore up order. Crisis and [[episode-of-contention|episodes of contention]] rupture settlements; skilled re-settlement or imposition of a new order follows (or fails).

## Semantic History

Related to institutionalist "institutionalization" and political "settlements," but specified as the four understanding-bundles of SAF theory rather than a single logic. Distinct from legal settlement in the narrow sense (though court decisions often *are* the exogenous shocks that unsettle or lock in field settlements).

## Operationalizations

Compare field structure before/after episodes; identify rules, dominant actors, and IGUs that mark a settlement (method Ch. 6; empirical Ch. 5).

## Applications

Jim Crow racial-politics settlement; New Deal–era mortgage market settlement; post–civil rights institutionalization; 1990s–2000s MBS industrial-model settlement and its crisis.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Settlement" can blur power asymmetry and genuine consensus; authors stress hierarchy vs coalition pathways at field emergence shape the character of order.
