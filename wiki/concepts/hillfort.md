# Hillfort

Classificatory term for enclosed, often elevated later prehistoric fortifications and settlements, especially of Iron Age Britain and Ireland. Densified from [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013). Not every “hillfort” is a permanent town or a pure military stronghold; chronology, rampart form, and function are regionally and historically diverse.

## Definition as Coined

In British archaeology a **hillfort** (also hill-fort) is a substantial earthwork enclosure — typically bank(s) and ditch(es), often on elevated ground — used as a settlement, refuge, assembly place, elite residence, stock corral, or multi-function community focus. Classical authors did not use a single technical equivalent; the modern class groups sites from Late Bronze Age multivallate enclosures through Early–Middle Iron Age “developed” forts to late multivallate complexes. [[oppida]] are a related but later/larger (and regionally distinct) late Iron Age class; British “oppida” and Caesar’s British *oppidum* are not identical to Gaulish large urban oppida (see that page).

## Semantic History

- **Antiquarian recognition.** John Leland (1503–52), under Henry VIII’s commission, distinguished Roman structures from Iron Age hillforts as pre-Roman while still working within Brutus-origin frameworks (attributed via Cunliffe 2013).
- **Culture-historical type.** Nineteenth–twentieth-century British archaeology made the hillfort a signature of the Iron Age “Celtic” landscape — often over-read as tribal capitals or invasion strongpoints.
- **Processual / social models.** Late-twentieth-century work (including Cunliffe’s long Danebury programme) shifted emphasis to internal organization, storage, territory, and social cohesion — not only defence.
- **Functional pluralism (Cunliffe 2013).** Cunliffe treats hillforts as products of multi-lineage communities expressing cohesion and territorial ownership; defence, assembly, procession, elite residence, and stock management can co-exist or dominate at different sites and phases (theoretical, attributed).

## Operationalizations

*(Indicators as Cunliffe synthesizes them for Britain and Ireland in *Britain Begins* 2013 — attributed; regional variation is the rule.)*

| Dimension | Indicators |
|---|---|
| Enclosure scale | From ≥1 ha larger hill-top enclosures; large “hill-top enclosures” ≥15 ha (e.g. Balksbury ~18 ha, Walbury 33 ha); classic Iron Age forts often smaller but more heavily ramparted |
| Ramparts | Early Iron Age: defensive-proportion rampart and ditch, often two opposed gates; early faces often timber- or stone-revetted. Later (from third century BC in central south): glacis ramparts, blocked gates, hornworks; multivallation (e.g. Maiden Castle) |
| Internal use | Streets, houses, storage, domestic activity at some sites ([[danebury]], Maiden Castle); others largely empty or short-lived |
| LBA precursors | Eastern England **ring forts** (10th–9th c. BC; Thwing, Mucking); Irish multivallate widely spaced forts (1200–900 BC; Rathgall, Mooghaun, Haughey’s Fort, etc.) |
| Landscape role | Early forts sited to dominate discrete territories (often river valleys); later aggregation — some forts abandoned, few enhanced as central places |

**Three-zone model of later first-millennium BC Britain (Cunliffe, attributed):** (1) **eastern** — open villages and enclosed homesteads (Thames to Forth patterns vary north/south of Humber); (2) **western** — strongly defended single/extended-family homesteads (later rounds, raths, brochs, duns); (3) **central hillfort-dominated** — south coast to north Wales and Solway–Tay belt, forts thick among smaller settlements. Simple reading: east and west as systems in relative equilibrium; hillfort zone as liminal marcher belt of inherent instability.

## Applications

### Late Bronze Age precursors

- **Central southern Britain:** larger hill-top enclosures (≥1 ha) with substantial banks/ditches — possible elite homesteads and/or seasonal assembly (functions not exclusive).
- **Large pastoral corrals (≥15 ha)** Wessex–Welsh borderland: Balksbury magnetometry/excavation shows largely empty interiors (hay-racks, light shelters), long-term flock/herd corralling, association with linear drove ditches; coarse dates from perhaps ninth into eighth/seventh centuries BC (finding, Cunliffe 2013).
- **Eastern ring forts** (Kent–Yorkshire, mainly 10th–9th c.): one or two concentric ditches with palisades/timber-framed earthworks; construction effort implies elite homesteads, possibly graded by number of circuits.
- **Ireland:** relatively few large multivallate forts with widely spaced walls (Mooghaun, Haughey’s Fort, Cashel, Rathgall) may represent powerful tribal leaders’ residences *and* refuges for people/animals; Rathgall’s inner enclosure held a large circular house (attributed).

### Early–Middle Iron Age “classic” hillforts

Drawing on southern excavations, Cunliffe generalizes that **most British hillforts were built in the sixth and fifth centuries BC**, with defensive rampart and ditch and usually two opposed gates; early ramparts maintained and repaired over long lives. [[danebury]] (eastern Hampshire chalk) and Maiden Castle (Dorset) exemplify permanent occupation with streets, houses, and storage; many other forts show little sustained internal use. Spatially, early forts dominate discrete territories; later **polity aggregation** leaves some enhanced (Danebury over four abandoned neighbours; Maiden Castle similarly) while others fall out of use — selection mechanisms (veneration, leadership, coercion) undecidable on present evidence (attributed).

### Middle–Late Iron Age stress and multivallation

From the third century BC, Cunliffe argues latent aggression became real stress in the central southern hillfort zone: glacis ramparts, blocked gates, hornworks; Maiden Castle multiplies circuits. He rejects pure aggrandizement in favour of response to social tensions that could flare into violence (position). Burning episodes and weapon-marked bones (e.g. Danebury) support deadly conflict without implying standing armies — endemic hostility, boasting, occasional skirmishes and raids (attributed). Warfare in densely settled Middle Iron Age areas of the Wessex–Marches belt is judged “endemic” (finding/position hybrid).

### Late horizons and relation to oppida

SE British late pre-Roman “oppida” / dyke-enclosed centres and Caesar’s British *oppidum* (wooded refuge with rampart and trench) are filed under [[oppida]] — **do not collapse** multivallate hillforts into the continental large-oppidum urban class. Post-Roman hillfort reuse (e.g. South Cadbury) is a separate early medieval phenomenon outside this concept’s core definition.

### Atlantic-façade round houses, rounds, raths, brochs, duns (Cunliffe 2001)

[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Cunliffe (2001)]] densifies the western/Atlantic zone of the three-zone model above with a distinct family of defended enclosures not built primarily as forts in the classic central-hillfort sense. Cornwall and south-west Wales share dense settlement of family-based defended enclosures — "rounds" in Cornwall, "raths" in Wales — many in continuous occupation for centuries (e.g. Trevisker, Cornwall, from the second millennium BCE) with settlement patterns largely unaffected by Roman occupation; underground stone "fogous" likely served ritual protection of stored surpluses (Cunliffe 2001). Northern Britain (Galloway to Orkney/Shetland) shows "duns" (irregular drystone enclosures, west coast/Western Isles) and "brochs" (circular structures with walls exceeding 3m and mural cells, extreme north-west/Northern Isles); Cunliffe treats both as varieties of "complex round houses," with early Orkney examples dated to 600 BCE and the broch's origin still "a hotly debated subject" (Cunliffe 2001).

Cunliffe explicitly argues, against a defensive/warfare reading, that the mid-first-millennium-BCE shift from inconspicuous cellular buildings to these prominent stone round houses reflects **social display rather than defense**: weapons are rare in the record, and settlement-pattern analysis (e.g. Shetland) shows the land could have supported larger populations without economic stress. He interprets the structures as symbols of a family's ability to mobilize labour and as identity markers, functionally paralleling earlier megalithic monuments (Cunliffe 2001) — a position, not a settled wiki-voice verdict, that should be read alongside this page's Critiques section on invasion/warfare over-reading of British hillforts generally.

Related to this same non-warfare reading, Cunliffe treats **cliff castles** (headland promontory enclosures, e.g. in Armorica, Cornwall, north-west Iberia, and — with associated chevaux-de-frise stone-field obstacles — Ireland's Dún Aonghasa) as more symbolic/liminal than purely defensive, marking "the interface between land and ocean," with possible continuity of promontory significance back into the Neolithic (Cunliffe 2001; summarized in his recurring formula "the sea joined and the land divided"). Post-Roman Britain saw renewed **hillfort reoccupation** in the Atlantic-facing west (e.g. South Cadbury, Cadbury-Congresbury) as surviving Britons reverted to small kingdoms under local war leaders resembling pre-Roman social forms (Cunliffe 2001) — a second-source layer alongside this page's existing South Cadbury note.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Not all hillforts are forts.** Empty large enclosures may be stock corrals; some “defences” may signal status or community labour more than continuous military threat.
- **Invasion narratives.** Older culture-historical readings equated multivallate forts with successive Celtic invasions; Cunliffe’s *Britain Begins* frame is continuity-dominant with limited continental inputs (e.g. Arras, Belgae) — hillfort density is social geography, not ethnic mapping (see [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]], [[celticity-debate]]).
- **Sampling bias.** Southern chalkland excavations (Danebury programme) dominate textbooks; western and northern defended settlements follow different trajectories.
- **Function over time.** A site can shift from assembly/pastoral focus to permanent occupation to abandoned earthwork; single-function labels fail.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Hillfort builders ≠ “Celts” as a people ≠ a language community ≠ a genetic population; material enclosure is an etic class.
