# Spheres of Exchange

## Definition as Coined

In a multicentric economy, goods circulate in **distinct spheres (or "closed circuits") of exchange**: categories of goods and services exchangeable within their own series but not directly convertible across series — there is no common measure of value spanning the whole economy. "Spheres of Exchange" is [[firth-raymond|Firth]]'s own section heading in *[[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study|Primitive Polynesian Economy]]* (1939), where he identified three, arguably four, Tikopia spheres *(attributed, fieldwork 1928–29)*:

1. **Food series** — payment for small objects, small services, non-specialist labour;
2. **Bark-cloth/sinnet/pandanus-mat series** — payment for timber, bowls, specialist craft skill, damages;
3. **Bonito-hook/turmeric-cylinder/canoe series** — rare, emotionally and ritually charged transfers;
4. (proposed) goods of "unique quality" — women in marriage transfer and land, given "in satisfaction of unique obligations."

Firth reported that expressing a bonito-hook's value in food "would be regarded by the Tikopia as fantastic," and analogized the system to a tri-metallic currency of gold, silver, and copper "but there was no accurate means of rendering them in terms of each other" *(his simile, not a native concept)*. Native terminology independently supported the scheme: *kai* (food) vs. *koroa* (non-food wealth, itself split into "weighty" *mafa* and "light" *mama*), with distinct, never cross-applied reciprocation terms (*toŋoi* for food returns, *fakapenu* for goods returns).

## Semantic History

The **canonical formulation** is Paul Bohannan's analysis of the Tiv economy (1955, 1959; not yet ingested here), with its three ranked spheres (subsistence goods; prestige goods — brass rods, slaves, cattle; rights in persons) and the key vocabulary of **conveyance** (within-sphere exchange, morally neutral) versus **conversion** (across spheres, morally charged — upward conversion praiseworthy, downward shameful), plus the argument that general-purpose money collapsed the spheres. Firth's 1939 Tikopia treatment is an independent, earlier statement of the same structure — non-fungible categories, conventionalized bridges (sinnet/bowls exchangeable against mats; a canoe substituting for a bowl at one funeral because of shape and honorific fit, where "no amount of bark-cloth could have acted as substitute") — without Bohannan's conveyance/conversion vocabulary. The concept was later generalized in discussions of "special-purpose money" (Polanyi's circle) and partly dissolved into [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s [[regimes-of-value]] and [[commoditization]] framework, which treats sphere boundaries as politically maintained and historically leaky rather than structural givens *(attributed)*.

## Operationalizations

Firth's operational tests *(1939)*: (a) which exchanges never occur (bonito-hook against food); (b) mobility and durability of goods per sphere (food least mobile; sinnet and bark-cloth "embryonic media of exchange"; top-sphere goods durable but transferred only in ritually significant contexts); (c) native categorical terminology; (d) the conventional bridges that do allow adjacent-sphere substitution and their conditions. Bohannan's test is moral valence of conversions. The frameworks disagree subtly: Firth stresses absence of a common measure; Bohannan stresses ranked convertibility and its ethics.

## Applications

The concept organizes analysis of non-monetary and colonial-contact economies: what money does to multicentric economies (Bohannan's Tiv argument), why bridewealth goods resist commodification, and how prestige economies ([[kula]], potlatch) stay insulated from subsistence exchange. Firth's Appendix III supplies an early culture-contact case: his own trade goods entered Tikopia exchange at rates he partly dictated as "monopolist," yet were slotted by the Tikopia into existing categories.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Later work argues spheres are ideal-typical and porous in practice — actors constantly engineer conversions, and the "collapse under money" narrative underestimates how special-purpose constraints persist in monetized economies (Parry & Bloch's *Money and the Morality of Exchange*, 1989, not ingested). Appadurai's regimes-of-value reading treats sphere maintenance as the exercise of power by those who benefit from the boundaries. Firth himself flagged his explanatory hypothesis for non-accumulation (a cultural "lack of stimulus to ultimate acquisition") as "a hypothesis rather than an empirical generalization."
