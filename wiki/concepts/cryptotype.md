# Cryptotype

## Definition as Coined

[[whorf-benjamin-lee|Whorf]]'s **cryptotype** is a **covert grammatical-semantic category**: a class of forms united by meaning and by selective formal behavior (what can combine with what) **without** a single consistent overt mark. He contrasts cryptotypes with **phenotypes** — categories marked by an overt formal signal (affix, particle, etc.). Developed in "Grammatical Categories" (1937) and related essays in *[[whorf-language-thought-reality-1956|Language, Thought, and Reality]]*. Attributed to Whorf; builds on [[sapir-edward|Sapir]]'s sense that languages are systems of relations ([[phonetic-pattern|pattern]]), not mere inventories of marks.

## Semantic History

Cryptotype/phenotype is Whorf's contribution to the analysis of **covert categories** in structural linguistics. Later generative and typological traditions discuss covert categories and selectional classes under other names; do not equate without attribution. The pair is load-bearing for [[linguistic-relativity]] because Whorf held that **backgrounded** (often cryptotypic) categories most powerfully shape [[habitual-thought|habitual thought]] — speakers are least aware of them.

## Operationalizations

In LTR analyses, a cryptotype is identified when a semantic class (e.g. a type of verb or noun) shows consistent combinatorial restrictions or aspectual behavior without a single dedicated morpheme. Phenotypes are the textbook "marked" categories. English examples of covert classes appear alongside Hopi illustrations.

## Applications

- Architecture of Whorf's Hopi and English grammatical analyses
- Mechanism linking grammar to thought in [[linguistic-relativity]] (background categories)
- Bridge to [[linguistic-typology]] and descriptive method

## Critiques and Limitations

Operational criteria for "covert" can be theory-dependent; risk of projecting analyst categories. Reception of cryptotype is less publicly contested than the relativity thesis itself but shares the structural-linguistic context of the 1930s–40s.
