# Modes of Incorporation

How receiving contexts — not only individual skills — shape immigrant and second-generation outcomes. In *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*, [[portes-alejandro|Portes]] and [[rumbaut-ruben|Rumbaut]] treat modes of incorporation as a core determinant of first-generation attainment and of children's segmented assimilation paths.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001.)* Immigrants do not meet a level playing field. Contextual factors shape whether human capital can be converted into occupational and economic success. Principal dimensions include: **government policy** (exclusion, passive acceptance, active encouragement), **attitudes of the native population**, and the **presence and institutional completeness of a co-ethnic community**, alongside family structure.

## Semantic History

Earlier Portes (and collaborators) used modes of incorporation in analyses of immigrant labor-market and enclave outcomes; *Legacies* extends the construct as the first-generation side of the second-generation [[segmented-assimilation]] model. Not identical to general "context of reception" slogans in journalism — here it is a three-level structural typology tied to measurable group differences.

## Operationalizations

In CILS/*Legacies*, nationality groups are arrayed by modal reception (e.g. refugee assistance vs undocumented-linked hostility vs neutral labor migration), community resources, and average human capital. Exact cell coding appears in ch. 3–4 tables (to be detailed on the study page).

## Applications

Explains residual nationality differences in earnings and family composition after individual education/experience controls; sets starting conditions for children's language, identity, and school paths.

## Critiques and Limitations

Nationality is a bundled proxy (selection, race, legal status, local history). Two-city CILS sites over-represent particular modes (notably Cuban Miami). Cross-national applications require re-specification.
