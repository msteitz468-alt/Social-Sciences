# External Soul

## Definition as Coined

In *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (ch. 66–68), [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] collected folk-tales and customs in which a person's **life or soul is kept outside the body** — in a stone, egg, tree, animal, etc. — so the person cannot die until that external seat is found and destroyed. He linked the type to **Balder and the mistletoe** and to the **Golden Bough** at Nemi: the King of the Wood's life in the mistletoe on the oak. (Attributed.)

## Semantic History

Popular in folklore studies and literary criticism; overlaps [[animism|soul]] concepts and later discussions of distributed personhood; less used as a formal analytic in contemporary anthropology.

## Operationalizations

Narrative motif (ATU-type invulnerability) + ritual claim that life resides in a separable deposit.

## Applications

Resolution of the Nemi Golden Bough riddle in Frazer's finale; Balder cycle; folk-tale catalogues.

## Critiques and Limitations

Motif-collecting without ethnographic context; Nemi–mistletoe identification is conjectural poetry-to-ritual inference. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]].
