# Epistemological Critique

One of two anthropological techniques of [[defamiliarization]] named by Marcus and Fischer (*[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|ACC]]* Ch. 6). Arises from traditional fieldwork on the Eurocentric "periphery," where the ethnographer must revise ordinary categories to grasp what European terms treat as exotica — then bring those revised insights back to unsettle conceptualization at the "center."

## Definition as Coined

Marcus and Fischer hold that **defamiliarization by epistemological critique** challenges settled differentiations of "us" (center) from "them" (periphery) by recognizing that we live in as culturally constructed a reality as they do; once that unity is recognized, substantive differences can be considered on a more valid basis. Strong versions also criticize the means of defamiliarization itself (e.g., not only comparing doctors to shamans to unsettle medicine, but also rethinking how we think about shamans).

## Semantic History

Programmatic label in ACC for a long-standing anthropological critical impulse; not identical to philosophy-of-science "epistemological critique" in general.

## Operationalizations

Fieldwork-driven conceptual revision; return of revised categories to domestic topics; risk of being received as satire or eccentricity rather than consequential critique.

## Applications

Pairs with [[cross-cultural-juxtaposition]] as the two ACC techniques of cultural critique; supports the dual-promise program of [[anthropology-as-cultural-critique]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Effectiveness depends on form and sustained inquiry; weak use remains "cute"; institutional reception may neutralize critical force.
