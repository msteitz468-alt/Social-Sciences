# Linguistic Typology

## Definition as Coined

**Linguistic typology** classifies languages by structural type rather than by genetic descent. The term postdates the practice; the 19th-century philological tradition (Friedrich Schlegel, Humboldt, Schleicher) established the familiar **isolating / agglutinative / inflective** scheme, with American Indigenous languages awkwardly appended as "polysynthetic." [[sapir-edward|Edward Sapir]], in *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]* (1921, Ch. VI), critiqued that scheme and proposed a **conceptual reformulation** — the version this page primarily records. (Sapir's scheme is attributed to him; the typological enterprise itself is older.)

Sapir levied four criticisms of prior classifications: the difficulty of choosing a classificatory viewpoint; the danger of generalizing from too few languages; the "strong craving for a simple formula"; and — most importantly — **"evolutionary prejudice"**, the unscientific ranking of Latin/Greek-type "inflective" languages as the highest development. His rejoinder is memorable: "A linguist that insists on talking about the Latin type of morphology as though it were necessarily the high-water mark of linguistic development is like the zoölogist that sees in the organic world a huge conspiracy to evolve the race-horse or the Jersey cow." And: "Language in its fundamental forms is the symbolic expression of human intuitions... regardless of the material advancement or backwardness of the people that handle the forms."

## Semantic History

Sapir first cleared away distinctions he judged invalid: **form vs. formless languages** ("every language is a form language," since all express fundamental syntactic relations) and the "inner form / outer form" ranking. He then proposed a **threefold classification** by three independent axes:

1. **Which concept-types a language expresses** — drawing on his Ch. V classification into basic/concrete (I), derivational (II), concrete-relational (III), and pure-relational (IV) concepts. This yields four conceptual types: **Simple Pure-relational** (I + IV; e.g. Chinese), **Complex Pure-relational** (I, II, IV; e.g. Polynesian), **Simple Mixed-relational** (I + III; e.g. Bantu, French), **Complex Mixed-relational** (I, II, III; most familiar "inflective" languages plus many agglutinative and polysynthetic ones — English, Latin, Nootka, Semitic).
2. **Prevailing technique** — isolating, agglutinative, fusional, or symbolic (internal modification). Sapir stressed these are *techniques*, not ranks, and that most languages mix them (Semitic is prefixing + suffixing + symbolic at once).
3. **Degree of synthesis** — analytic / synthetic / polysynthetic, described as "purely quantitative — and relative."

Sapir judged the conceptual axis the most fundamental, redefining **inflection** strictly (fusion that involves the syntactic relations themselves, not merely derivational fusion). He offered a **historical test**: across genetically related languages, degree of synthesis changes most readily, technique less readily, and conceptual type "tends to persist the longest of all."

> Note: the source's large cross-tabulation table (concept-type × technique × synthesis over ~20 languages) is partly OCR-garbled in the ingested edition; the scheme is recorded here descriptively, and specific concept-group codes for a few languages (e.g. Modern Tibetan, Salinan) should be checked against a clean edition before being asserted in a wiki table.

## Operationalizations

A language is placed on all three axes at once, e.g. Chinese = Simple Pure-relational, isolating, analytic; English = Complex Mixed-relational, fusional, analytic; Latin = Complex Mixed-relational, fusional (symbolic tinge), synthetic; Nootka/Yana = polysynthetic. The scheme is descriptive-qualitative; it was never turned into a standardized quantitative instrument.

## Applications

- A non-evaluative alternative to the isolating/agglutinative/inflective hierarchy.
- Sapir's claim that unrelated languages converge on similar types via "powerful drifts" (see [[linguistic-drift]]) — structural convergence without common descent.
- A precedent for later typology (Greenbergian word-order universals; morphological-index measures), which pursued Sapir's quantitative intuitions with explicit measurement.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Contested.** Sapir himself warned the categories are not "to be worshipped as fetiches." His fourfold conceptual classification, though admired as suggestive, was never operationalized into a stable, reproducible instrument and did not displace the older technique-based scheme in practice; the conceptual-group assignments are interpretive. Modern typology largely bypassed Sapir's concept-type axis in favor of feature-based and statistical approaches, while retaining his central methodological point — that structural type is independent of genetic affiliation and carries no developmental rank.
