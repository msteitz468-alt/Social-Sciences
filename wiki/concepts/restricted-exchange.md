# Restricted Exchange

## Definition as Coined

**Restricted exchange** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] term (*[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]*, 1949) for any marriage system that "effectively or functionally divides the group into a certain number of pairs of exchange-units so that, for any one pair X-Y there is a reciprocal exchange relationship" — if an X man marries a Y woman, a Y man can always marry an X woman. Its simplest form is the division into two exogamous moieties ([[dual-organisation]]); superimposing a second dichotomy produces a four-section system, a third produces eight subsections. It is realized in **sister exchange** and **bilateral [[cross-cousin-marriage|cross-cousin marriage]]** (with both the mother's brother's and father's sister's daughter).

## Semantic History

The term is one pole of the fundamental distinction Lévi-Strauss introduced (with [[generalized-exchange]]) to organize the world's positive marriage systems under [[alliance-theory]]. It reworked older material — Australian section systems, moiety exogamy, cross-cousin marriage — that earlier writers (Tylor, Rivers, Frazer, Morgan) had treated historically or economistically, recasting it as a synchronic structure of reciprocity (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by paired, reversible exchange: two moieties; four-section (Kariera-type) systems; eight-subsection (Aranda-type) systems; sister-exchange marriage. Lévi-Strauss's analytic point: moving from two moieties to four sections does *not* change which cousins may marry (still cross-cousins) — it adds a second (residence) axis; only the eight-subsection system narrows the pool by splitting cross-cousins into marriageable and excluded categories. Restricted exchange, he argued, "can never function as a whole both in time and in space" — it works only by splitting the group into sub-units.

## Applications

Australian Aboriginal systems are the privileged case (moieties, sections, subsections); moiety exogamy worldwide; the [[murngin|Murngin]] system, which Lévi-Strauss argued is an underlying four-class generalized-exchange system projected onto an eight-class restricted-exchange grid. Restricted exchange characteristically arises under a **disharmonic regime** (descent and residence rules opposed), which Lévi-Strauss held to be structurally stable and capable of section/subsection elaboration.

## Critiques and Limitations

Like all of [[alliance-theory]], the concept treats women as the objects circulated (see [[exchange-of-women]]) and rests on secondary ethnography. The neatness of the restricted/generalized dichotomy is a model: real systems are mixed and shade into one another (the Naga cases), and the "harmonic/disharmonic" derivation is a structural argument, not an observed historical sequence.
