# Deterritorialization

The concept originates philosophically with Deleuze and Guattari; this page
records [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s influential redeployment of it in the
anthropology of globalization (*[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at
Large]]*, 1996). All substantive claims attributed to Appadurai.

## Definition as Coined

For Appadurai, deterritorialization applies "not only to obvious examples such as
transnational corporations and money markets but also to ethnic groups, sectarian
movements, and political formations, which increasingly operate in ways that
transcend specific territorial boundaries and identities" *(Appadurai)*. "The
loosening of the holds between people, wealth, and territories fundamentally
alters the basis of cultural reproduction." It is "one of the central forces of
the modern world."

## Semantic History

Borrowed from Deleuze and Guattari (whom Appadurai cites) but recast as an
empirical feature of the [[ethnoscape]] and [[financescape]]. Related to (but not
identical with) diaspora: deterritorialization names the *process*; diaspora names
the resulting collectivity.

## Operationalizations

Traced through diasporic finance and religious mobilization (overseas Indians and
Hindu nationalism), "invented homelands" (the coinage of **Khalistan** among
diasporic Sikhs), gray-market consumer flows in Bombay, and the electronic
mobilization of the Indian diaspora around the 1992 Babri Masjid destruction.
Appadurai coins "**delocalized transnation**" for the diasporic collectivity that
"retains a special ideological link to a putative place of origin but is otherwise
a thoroughly diasporic collectivity."

## Applications

Deterritorialization creates new markets (homeland-contact industries) and
produces "invented homelands" that can grow "so fantastic and one-sided" as to
fuel ethnic conflict; it strains family reproduction, shifting culture from
[[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s tacit [[habitus]] "to more an arena for conscious
choice, justification, and representation." It underwrites deterritorialized and
"long-distance" nationalism — though Appadurai distinguishes his account from
Benedict Anderson's more critical treatment of "long-distance nationalism."

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note the term can overstate the "loosening" of territory when states,
borders, and citizenship remain powerfully binding; Appadurai himself pairs it
with the reassertion of territorial control in [[production-of-locality|locality
production]] and the nation-state's "context-producing drives."
