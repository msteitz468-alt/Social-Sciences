# People Without History

## Definition as Coined

[[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] took the phrase **people without history** as the ironic title of *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982). He did not invent it: he notes nineteenth-century usage (including Marx and Engels on some eastern European national movements). Wolf's intent was to **challenge** those who treat Europeans as sole history-makers and non-Europeans (especially anthropology's "primitives") as peoples outside history.

## Semantic History

- **19th c.:** Eurocentric dismissal or polemical category for populations deemed without political history.
- **Wolf 1982:** ironic inversion — the book shows deep pre-1400 histories and mutual interdigitation of European and non-European trajectories.
- **1997 preface:** Wolf records that some readers missed the irony; he clarifies the title was never a description of actual peoples but a critique of a historiographical and anthropological habit.
- **Pedagogy:** became a shorthand in historical anthropology for the ethnographic-present trap and billiard-ball culture models.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurable type. Functions as a **negative diagnostic**: any account that freezes a people outside time, outside trade, or outside power qualifies as producing "people without history."

## Applications

Charter critique for restoring historicity to ethnographic subjects; teaching tool against culture-area isolation; bridge to global history and [[anthropological-political-economy]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Irony can fail; title sometimes misread as Wolf denying non-European history (opposite of his claim).
- Risk that "giving people history" becomes only the history of European contact.
- Concurrent Writing Culture / interpretive critiques asked for different restorations (voice, textuality) than Wolf's political-economic one.
