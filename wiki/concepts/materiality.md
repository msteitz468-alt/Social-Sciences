# Materiality

## Definition as Coined

**Materiality** names the analytic focus, consolidated in archaeology and anthropology from the
1980s–2000s, on the **agentive quality of things** — the way the physical properties of objects
(durability, mobility, detachability, scarcity, vulnerability to accident) actively mediate and
sometimes transform social practices, rather than merely reflecting a prior social order.
Preucel & Mrozowski (*[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology
in Theory]]*, 2010) treat it as one of the concepts most "refining and reorienting archaeological
practice," and note it is "increasingly bringing together anthropological archaeology, social
anthropology, sociology, and museum studies." All claims here are attributed.

## Semantic History

Preucel & Mrozowski distinguish three successive (but coexisting) ways archaeology has
conceptualized things (attributed):

1. **Material culture** — artifacts equated with past cultures, the part metonymically standing
   for the whole (the [[culture-historical-archaeology|culture-historical]] horizon marker; the
   Bell Beaker or Hohokam "culture" defined by a pottery style). Radicalized by
   [[processual-archaeology|processualism]]: Binford's *technomic / sociotechnic / ideotechnic*
   artifact typology (1962), Schiffer's behavioral archaeology.
2. **Material language** — things as sign-vehicles: Wobst's (1977) information-exchange theory of
   style; Conkey on Palaeolithic art; then the post-processual "text" analogy —
   [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s *Reading the Past* ("artifacts are silent only when out of their
   texts"; meaning is relational, not inherent) and Tilley's *Material Culture and Text* (material
   culture as "solid metaphor," "written" through differentiation).
3. **Material network / engagement** — humans and things as entangled hybrids: Renfrew's
   "material engagement theory" (symbols and concepts are brought forward *together* through
   "substantialization," not mind-first), and the Latourian actor-network current.

The **materiality** heading proper foregrounds the *physical* over the purely semiotic: things
mean *because* they are material.

## Operationalizations

Webb Keane's "Money Is No Object" (Ch. 17) is the volume's paradigm case (attributed, Keane;
Sumba fieldwork **documented 1980s–1990s**, one episode 1986 — not the ethnographic present). Keane
argues objects are neither arbitrary Saussurean signs nor mere use-values: "their materiality
makes a difference both in the sources of their meanings and in their destinations." Because a
thing is durable, mobile, and detachable, it can slide between regimes of value (gift ↔
commodity) and is exposed simultaneously to "natural meaning" (physical accident — a ceremonial
textile torn unnoticed) and intentional social meaning (Grice's natural/non-natural distinction).
In an Anakalang marriage negotiation, a banknote placed in the offering dish functions as a formal
ceremonial token like a gold ornament, yet remains vulnerable to "slippage" back into mere cash —
a slippage rooted in its irreducible materiality. Keane ties money's alienability to the colonial
and Indonesian **state** (head tax from 1911; markets only from the 1920s–30s; 2–10% cash access
in the 1980s–90s).

## Applications

Materiality analysis anchors work on [[social-memory|memory]] (Mills on Chaco deposition; object
"renewal" as memory-practice), value and exchange, the body ([[embodiment]]), technology
([[chaine-operatoire|chaîne opératoire]]), and the commodification of the past (the "past as
commodity," advertising imagery). It is a methodological hinge of the
[[archaeological-pragmatism|new pragmatism]], whose Peircean semiotics treats material culture as
a distinct-but-complementary sign system alongside language.

## From Text Analogy to Materiality — Johnson (2011), attributed

*(Attributed; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]] and [[post-processual-archaeology]].)* Johnson reports that postprocessualists initially analogized material culture to **text** — polysemic, manipulable, meaning independent of "author" intention, admitting no single final reading — but that many later postprocessual archaeologists argued this textual analogy is fundamentally limited: material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not. This drove the shift in emphasis toward **materiality**, "understanding," and "experience" as against reading-as-text. Johnson also notes Peircean semiotics (sign–object–interpretant; icon/index/symbol) as a further alternative to the disputed structuralist/textual analogy for understanding archaeological meaning.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept risks two opposite failures: reinflating a naïve "pots = people" equation under new
vocabulary, or dissolving human agency into symmetrical "human–thing networks" with "somewhat
unclear implications" (the editors' own caution about extending [[agency]] to inanimate things).
Its strong-network (Latourian) versions are contested for flattening the distinction between
persons and objects. As with all fast-moving theory, specific formulations are date-stamped to
their authors.

## Reading the Past (2003) — from text to materiality, and objects gaining agency (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., chs. 5–6.)* Hodder & Hutson report a turn away from reading material culture purely as text and toward materiality and lived experience, on the grounds that "material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not." They connect this to a broader argument (Thomas, Gell, Weiner) that objects gain a kind of agency through their relation to human actors — accruing histories and social significance through use and exchange rather than functioning as arbitrary signs.

## Renfrew densification (2003)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-figuring-it-out-2003|Figuring It Out]]*.)* Renfrew's [[material-engagement]] program is a cognitive-archaeological statement of materiality: mind and social institutions (property, value, divinity, prestige) are co-produced with things. Pre-writing "analogue" [[external-symbolic-storage]] (monuments, valuables, alignments) and later writing/money/electronics mark changing regimes of materiality — including late **dematerialization** when electronic signs replace object-repositories of meaning. Neighbor contrast: Hodder [[entanglement]] stresses sticky dependency; Renfrew 2003 stresses constitutive cognitive-symbolic emergence.
