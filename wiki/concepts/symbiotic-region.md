# Symbiotic Region

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[sanders-william-t|Sanders]] (1956) and [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]] 1968.)*

A **symbiotic region** is a set of contrasting ecological zones — characteristically highland and lowland — bound together by **economic interdependence**: "the economic interdependence of social and physical population units in a given region to the advantage of all." Because Mesoamerica compresses "nearly all of the world's environments" into 60–80 km, adjacent micro-zones produce different goods (different crops, growing seasons, raw materials), and their **complementarity** generates specialization and exchange. Sanders & Price argue it is this diversity-over-short-distances, not resource abundance, that drives inter-zonal trade, competition, and — ultimately — political centralization and urbanism.

The region is explicitly **not rigidly bounded**: membership shifts over time (Highland Guatemala could pair with the Petén in one period and the Pacific Coast in another). The distinctive case is the **Central Mexican Symbiotic Region** ("Nuclear Mesoamerica," Sanders 1962) — the Central Plateau plus its southern escarpment (a ~20,000 km² demographic core, ~35,000 km² total; population 1.5–3 million in 1519) — singled out as the only Mesoamerican region combining highland *and* lowland components with intensive (hydraulic) agriculture and dense population in both, and the seat of Teotihuacan, Tula, and the Aztec empire in turn.

## Semantic History

- **Sanders 1956 / 1962:** coined for the Basin of Mexico and central highlands; a "Guatemalan Symbiotic Region" also invoked (to analyze Teotihuacan–Petén relations via Kaminaljuyú).
- **Sanders & Price 1968:** generalized into a driver of civilization — symbiosis as a second ecological engine alongside the [[state-formation|hydraulic]] process.
- **[[service-elman-r|Service]] 1975** ([[service-origins-state-1975]]) took the concept up secondhand, reading Teotihuacan's rise as symbiotic-region complementarity favoring centralized [[redistribution]] — but recast the mechanism as integrative *benefit* rather than [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]]'s competition-and-surplus (see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]). The term survives in Basin-of-Mexico settlement archaeology as a description of highland–lowland exchange structure.

## Operationalizations

Archaeologically inferred from: paired zones of contrasting subsistence potential (e.g. Tierra Fría lake-and-plain basins vs. Tierra Templada escarpment with longer growing seasons); market frequency (5-day/daily markets in Basin-of-Mexico towns vs. 20-day markets on the Gulf Coast); and the density and spacing of complementary hydraulic and non-hydraulic plots. Contrast [[nuclear-area|nuclear area]] (a region of outsized cultural influence) — a symbiotic region is defined by *internal economic complementarity*, not by external radiating influence.

## Applications

Used to explain why highland central Mexico, rather than the initially more productive Gulf lowlands, became the durable core of Mesoamerican political-economic development (the [[nuclear-area]] shifting via the Law of Evolutionary Potential); why the lowland Maya, lacking local symbiosis in a geographically uniform environment, developed states but not true cities ("[[state-formation|civilizations without cities]]"); and, comparatively, as the New World parallel to the ecological mosaics of other pristine-state regions.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept carries [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]]'s highland-urban bias — it reads the lowland Maya as a symbiosis-deficient deviation rather than an alternative pathway. It leans on 1968-vintage demographic and market reconstructions (contested even at the time — Berkeley high counts vs. their conservative estimates). As with all [[cultural-ecology|cultural-ecological]] constructs, whether ecological complementarity *causes* centralization or merely permits it is the live question — the same critique levelled at the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate|hydraulic hypothesis]]. And it treats "region" membership as analytically fluid, which makes the boundaries of any given symbiotic region partly a matter of the analyst's framing.
