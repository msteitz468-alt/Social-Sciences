# Locale

## Definition as Coined

Giddens Glossary: a physical region involved as part of the **setting of interaction**, having definite boundaries which help to concentrate interaction in one way or another (*[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|Constitution]]*).

## Semantic History

Preferred to geographic "place" as point in space. Settings are chronically drawn on by agents to sustain meaning (Garfinkel, Goffman). Ranges from room to nation-state territory; typically internally [[regionalization|regionalized]]. Distinct from Hägerstrand "station" (stopping-point along paths) — locale has institutional and meaning content.

## Operationalizations

Architectural and interactional description of settings; time-space paths between locales; power-container analysis (school, prison, workplace).

## Applications

- Home/workplace differentiation; school as disciplinary container; urban neighbourhoods.

## Critiques and Limitations

Physicalist misreading if meaning-use of settings is ignored.

## From Gamble, *Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe]]*.)*

Gamble imports Giddens’s **locale** into Palaeolithic analysis as the place where interaction took place and is preserved — deliberately preferred to “site.” Analysis starts from the individual moving through the environment with ambulatory perception (Gibson 1979) along paths and tracks. Table 3.1 ranks locales as a scale of performance:

| Locale type | Definition (Gamble 1999) | Residue |
|---|---|---|
| **Encounter** | Path-intersection node; perceptual interaction with animals, persons, plants, materials | No lasting archaeological residue (“information sound-bites”) |
| **Gathering** | Enduring locale from quality/quantity of residues of single or multiple visits; encounters that survived | Hearths, lithics, bone; portable resources structure interaction |
| **Social occasion / Place** | Performance context established by objects **disembodied** from the actor (architecture, non-portables, or named locales with associations) | Built environment, graves, ovens, middens; caves as natural *places* that structure privacy without symbolic architecture |

Gatherings use portable resources (food, stone, bodies); the hearth preserves interaction patterning robust enough to survive taphonomy (Stapert, Whallon via Gamble). Dunbar’s conversational limit (~4 people; ~1.7 m dyadic comfort) and Binford’s Mask Site (Nunamiut) seating/drop–toss zones supply middle-range anchors — but Gamble, following Whitelaw, argues body-size models alone remain mechanical if **performance content** is ignored.

Illustrative contrast (attributed): Lower Palaeolithic **Bilzingsleben** as gathering (lake, trees, stone and bone anvils) vs Upper Palaeolithic **Dolní Věstonice** as social occasion (huts, graves, ovens, middens). After ~33 Kyr bp (as of Gamble’s chronology), locales change structure as social occasions, not only as tool assemblages. Neanderthals, on his reading, moved gathering-to-gathering without regularly provisioning places for future action (Kuhn rephrased); modern humans move social-occasion-to-social-occasion. Attaching/detaching rituals (Goffman) structure entry and exit from gatherings; materials at gatherings are gestures — extensions of the actor (Leroi-Gourhan via Gamble).

Middle Palaeolithic preservation environments he treats: fluvial, lacustrine, loess, caves — all nine European regions occupied (as of Gamble 1999). High-density refitting locales may mark tool maintenance; artifact-poor locales specific tasks — possible spatial differentiation vs earlier Boxgrove-type locales.
