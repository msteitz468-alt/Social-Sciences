# Longhouse

In European Neolithic archaeology, the **timber longhouse** is the signature dwelling of the [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] and related central European early–middle Neolithic cultures: elongated, post-built, often three-aisled structures housing household groups. Whittle’s *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* (1996) Ch. 6 (“Longhouse lives”) treats longhouse settlement as the social frame for central and western European Neolithic histories c. 5500–before 4000 BC.

## Definition as Coined

Descriptive architectural category, not a single theoretical coinage. Archaeological longhouses are defined by plan (length >> width), post settings, and often loess-soil siting.

## Semantic History

From culture-historical LBK systematics through processual settlement archaeology (Bylany, Langweiler, Cuiry-lès-Chaudardes) to interpretive readings of household, gender, and house life-cycles ([[death-of-the-house]] in [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] 1998). Ethnographic “longhouse” (e.g. Iroquoian) is a **separate** comparative analogy — flag when used.

## Operationalizations

House length, internal compartments, rebuild sequences, house→tomb metaphors (long mounds as dead houses). Do not assume one family size from plan alone.

## Applications

LBK, Lengyel, Rössen and related settlement archaeology; monument origin debates (long mound from longhouse).

## Critiques and Limitations

Uniformity of house plans can mask social variation; plough truncation destroys floors; ethnographic analogy over-reads European Neolithic households.

## Whittle densification — architecture, settlement, social values (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 6–7 and synthesis Ch. 10. Mid-1990s chronology; **pre-aDNA**. Longhouse plans are architectural evidence, not household censuses or ethnic markers.)*

### Preservation and research conditions

Whittle stresses that **no LBK longhouse has been found with its floor intact** (erosion/cultivation): bones and plants survive mainly in postholes and pits, and plant assemblages are hard to stage into processing zones — research conditions that encourage rigid subsistence models. Animals-in-houses remain unproven from plans; phosphate analysis was sparse as of 1996; small herds would not be viable on-site, so he prefers herding stock away from settlements (few palisades/fences; short Aldenhoven stretches; Geleen–Janskamperveld palisade + yard fences as exceptions).

### Architecture (as of Whittle 1996)

LBK longhouses use substantial timbers; smallest ~10 m, many 20 m+, some nearly 30 m, occasional >30 m; often two- or three-part; usually treated as single-storey though upper floors are possible; doors on the short end; flanking daub pits; contemporary houses share orientation and stand tens of metres apart with recurrent outdoor working areas. NW three-type scheme (Elsloo, Langweiler 8; orientation NW–SE): (1) middle-only unit 8–12 m; (2) +NW part (sometimes plank walls) 15–25 m; (3) three-part with SE denser posts, sometimes all-plank walls, often 25–30 m — ideal scheme with variation. Popular functional hypothesis: middle living (charcoal hearths); NW animal stall (no further evidence); SE lofted granary (extra posts); Aldenhoven chaff/weeds relatively more in houses with SE part. Small single-unit buildings comparatively rare where less eroded (Geleen–Janskamperveld).

Post-LBK / Rössen longhouses (Whittle): more markedly trapezoidal; interiors less cluttered, roof on side walls with extra posts; sometimes boat-shaped bowed walls; clear cross-walls into 2–3 compartments; spectacular lengths — Deiringsen-Ruploh two >50 m trapezoidal continuous wall trenches; Inden-Lamersdorf three >40 m. Late [[lengyel-culture|Lengyel]] trapezoidal longhouses at Brześć Kujawski / Oslonki: broad front, narrow rear; walls of closely set posts/planks supporting the roof **without internal postholes**; up to ~30 m; N–S rows — different construction solution continuing the longhouse idea.

### Langweiler / Merzbachtal vignette

Whittle opens Ch. 6 with the Merzbachtal (NW Germany, Aldenhoven plateau, lignite-mining rescue): after c. 5500 BC a small timber longhouse settlement (as few as three houses, growing within three–four generations to six or seven); individual houses may last about a generation and be replaced close to predecessors; from the start some buildings ~25 m, tens of metres from neighbours. Sequence: Langweiler 8 first, then Langweiler 9, Langweiler 2, Laurenzberg and others; cemetery across the stream from Langweiler 8 is Niedermerz. After ~four generations, daughter longhouse groups a few hundred metres up-valley; about the sixth generation a burial ground of simple earth-cut graves begins, receiving over 100 people over seven+ generations, lapsing ~thirteenth generation (~5000 BC). Toward 5000 BC ditched enclosures appear on former daughter and parent sites as longhouse numbers dwindle; longhouse tradition continues regionally, often with closer grouping. Langweiler 8 pottery seriation ~15 phases ≈ generations; Graetheide total occupation ~350 years (as of Whittle).

### Bylany

Bylany complex begins in the LBK middle phase: ~7 ha excavated of ~30 ha; contemporary houses fluctuated around six; fresh analysis (as of Whittle) again suggests **several hiatuses** in the major complex, against unbroken-continuity readings of western megasites (Elsloo, Langweiler 8). >20 pottery phases currently (middle-phase dating hard; middle phase could begin ~5300 BC and last two+ centuries). SBK later occupation extent ≤ LBK; only three SBK house plans found (more likely originally present); SBK cemetery (cremations + inhumations); continued research produced a double-ditched four-entrance “roundel” (+ possible further outer ditch) and another enclosure with large outer palisade. No LBK cemetery located at Bylany — may serve the whole settlement area. Flint: first occupation Baltic erratics via Elbe ≥100 km N; middle phases Jurassic Kraków area ~300 km.

### Social values and household world (Whittle’s position)

Whittle rejects reading the longhouse as a mere technical response of sedentary “serious settled farmers from the outset.” Dominant post-1970 model: sedentary LBK, fixed intensive small plots/gardens, longhouse replacement continuity. Older shifting-settlement/shifting-cultivation model (Bylany breaks + ethnographic analogy) still contributes. Whittle’s third model: re-examine degree of sedentism, resource balance, and purpose of production; seasonal mobility compatible with fixed bases; if indigenous inland foragers transformed into LBK, longhouses **enhance aggregation/cooperation** and may **enhance rather than alter** values of cooperation, sharing, and aggregation — major shift = longhouse life in small groups.

Subsistence channelled through the longhouse: cereals+legumes with little meat **or** meat valued as medium for **sharing and exchange**; pots may hold porridge, beer, milk, blood; animals valued in their own right; production may satisfy hospitality/exchange as much as basic needs. Longhouses normally not alone — neighbours at shouting distance, shared orientation; basic unit the individual longhouse; possible “wards” (Elsloo up to 2–3 houses/phase); Langweiler 8 configurations varied by phase. House size need not map status: at Olszanica (S Poland) of two densest obsidian houses one was 41.5 m, the other only 12 m; adzes concentrated near large longhouse no. 6 — caution against generalising open access from NW sites alone. Larger/longer sites as **foci of an unstable world**, not apex of hierarchy — greater commitment to renewal/place where cooperation was practised most successfully; analytic scale may be the whole settlement cluster.

Burial orientation often left side, head E face S — Whittle links to longhouse alignment/entrances. Enclosures with vs without contemporary longhouses: Aldenhoven Langweiler 9 enclosure **succeeded** longhouse occupation; Langweiler 8 enclosure last phase, set ≥50 m from remaining dispersed longhouses. Ch. 7 / synthesis: most likely source for long-mound/monument idea was the longhouse tradition — “longhouse for the living of past generations was transformed into shrine for the ancestors of a timeless past” (attributed; cf. [[death-of-the-house]], [[long-mound]]); Scandinavian round-mound passage graves imply the longhouse idea “must therefore in some sense have faded.”
