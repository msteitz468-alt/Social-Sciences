# Multinaturalism

## Definition as Coined

**Multinaturalism** is [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Eduardo Viveiros de Castro]]'s name (given currency in his 1998 article "Cosmological Deixis and Amerindian Perspectivism" and elaborated in *[[viveiros-de-castro-cannibal-metaphysics-2014|Cannibal Metaphysics]]*, 2009/2014) for the ontological regime that is the corollary of Amerindian [[perspectivism]]. Viveiros de Castro argues that where modern Western "multiculturalism" rests on **one nature and many cultures** — a single objective world of bodies and matter, apprehended through the plural subjective representations of different cultures — Amerindian cosmology inverts the formula: **one culture (or soul/interiority) and many natures (or bodies)**. As he puts it, multiculturalism supposes "unicity of nature, multiplicity of cultures"; multinaturalism supposes "a unity of mind and a diversity of bodies," with "'culture' or subject as the form of the universal, and 'nature' or object as the particular" (attributed; *Cannibal Metaphysics*).

## Semantic History

The term is deliberately built as the mirror of "multiculturalism." Viveiros de Castro insists it does not describe a Kantian relativism in which one Thing-in-itself is apprehended differently by each species: "Multinaturalism does not suppose a Thing-in-Itself partially apprehended through categories of understanding proper to each species." There is no self-identical substrate (a single "blood" seen variously); instead there are "immediately relational multiplicities of the type blood/beer." Reading Deleuze, he glosses multinaturalism as affirming "not so much a variety of natures as the naturalness of variation — variation as nature" (attributed).

[[latour-bruno|Bruno Latour]] adopted "multinaturalism" (attributed) to name the non-viability, from a cosmopolitical standpoint, of the modern pairing of **multiculturalism / mononaturalism** — one of the routes by which the concept entered the broader [[ontological-turn]] literature.

## Operationalizations

In Viveiros de Castro's two-schema contrast: (1) Amerindian multinaturalism = **metaphysical continuity** (a generic, shared soul/interiority across species) combined with **physical discontinuity** (species-specific bodies); (2) modern multiculturalism = physical continuity (shared corporeal matter/biology) combined with metaphysical discontinuity (humans set apart by spirit, mind, or culture). The "body" that individuates each nature is not physiology but a "bundle of affects and capacities" — a habitus or *ethogram* (see [[perspectivism]]).

## Applications

Multinaturalism is inseparable from [[perspectivism]] (the doctrine that nonhumans see themselves as persons and differ from humans by body) and from Amerindian mythology, in which — on Viveiros de Castro's reading — the pre-cosmological condition is a common **humanity**, not a common animality: "Nonhumans are ex-humans — and not humans are ex-nonhumans" (attributed). It has become a reference point in Amazonian ethnography and in comparative-ontology debates, often juxtaposed with [[descola-philippe|Descola]]'s [[four-ontologies|animism]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Viveiros de Castro's own claim is that multinaturalism/perspectivism is "a concept, not a type" — explicitly resisting [[descola-philippe|Descola]]'s subsumption of perspectivism as a subtype of [[animism]] within the [[four-ontologies|four-ontologies matrix]] (see [[ontological-turn-debate]]). Critics of the wider program question whether a formula abstracted from Amazonian material generalizes, and whether "multinaturalism" states Amerindian ontology or Viveiros de Castro's structural-philosophical construction of it. As with all claims here, this is **attributed** theoretical argument, not established ethnographic finding.
