## Definition as Coined

The "old middle class" is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s term for the propertied middle
strata of nineteenth-century America — independent farmers, small businessmen, and free
professionals — who owned the productive property on which they worked. Its defining
condition, in Mills's account (attributed), was the fusion of "income, status, work, and
property" into a single unit: to own one's working property was to be self-directing, and
self-direction was the material basis of the classical liberal ideal of the free
individual. The concept exists chiefly as the historical contrast against which the
propertyless [[new-middle-class]] is defined.

## Semantic History

Mills reconstructed the old middle class as the social base of what he called (attributed)
"the self-balancing society" — a small-property order requiring "little or no authority at
the center," integrated by tradition and a few safeguards for property, in which Adam
Smith's economics coincided with Jeffersonian political morality. He distinguished
"democratic property" (property the owner works, a condition of working) from "class
property" (property others are hired to work, a condition of *not* working), and argued
the middle class was undone not by "a sharp red line between those with property and those
without" but by "the development of the split between small and large property" — an
intra-class polarization.

## Operationalizations

Measured as an occupational residual, the old middle class fell from 33% of the labor
force (1870) to 20% (1940), and from 85% to 44% of the total middle class. Within it,
farmers collapsed from 62% to 23% of the old middle class; the farm share of the labor
force fell from roughly three-quarters (1820) to one-eighth (1949). Mills documented the
"rural debacle" and the squeeze on small business (of ~16 million firms begun in the four
decades before WWII, ~14 million failed) as the mechanisms of decline.

## Applications

The concept anchors Mills's account of the [[private-property|property]]–freedom–security
nexus, the "rhetoric of competition" (the persistence of competitive ideology after its
economic base eroded), and the "lumpen-bourgeoisie" (the marginal, high-failure stratum of
small enterprise). Russell Jacoby (2002) judged Mills's "bedrock proposition" — that "the
decline of the free entrepreneur... has parallelled the decline of the independent
individual" — the book's most durable claim.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mills's picture of the old middle class romanticizes small-property independence: his own
data show large stretches of rural poverty, tenancy, and the "lumpen-bourgeoisie" of failed
small enterprise, sitting uneasily with the "self-balancing society" ideal. His framing of
slaveholding as incidental to the "small entrepreneur" thesis (noting that in 1850 only 30%
of white families in slave states held slaves) has drawn criticism for minimizing slavery's
structural place in the antebellum property order. The category is best read as an analytic
foil, not a neutral description.
