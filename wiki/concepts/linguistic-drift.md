# Linguistic Drift

## Definition as Coined

**Drift** is [[sapir-edward|Edward Sapir]]'s term (in *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]*, 1921) for the slow, **directional**, cumulative historical change that every language undergoes. Sapir's load-bearing formulation: "Language moves down time in a current of its own making. It has a drift" (Ch. VII). Crucially, drift is not random variation but selection: "The drift of a language is constituted by the unconscious selection on the part of its speakers of those individual variations that are cumulative in some special direction" (Ch. VII). (Attributed to Sapir throughout — this is a theoretical construct, not an established law.)

Sapir distinguished **individual variation** (idiosyncratic, canceled by the consensus of usage) from **dialectic variation** (shared by a subgroup, resistant to leveling); dialects arise when groups become "sufficiently disconnected to drift apart... instead of together." Each speech community is held to "an ideal linguistic entity... a tacitly directing norm" against which variation is measured. Ordinary speakers feel their language is a fixed system whose changes are as likely to go one way as another — a feeling Sapir called "fallacious," since a language has a "slope."

## Semantic History

The concept has two layers in Sapir's own usage. **Grammatical/morphological drift** (Ch. VII): Sapir traced three "major drifts" of English — the leveling of the subjective/objective case distinction (seven Indo-European cases → four Germanic → near-collapse in English), the drift toward **fixed word order** as inflection ceases to mark relations, and the drift toward the **invariable word**. His famous illustration is the decline of *whom*: "It is safe to prophesy that within a couple of hundred years from to-day not even the most learned jurist will be saying 'Whom did you see?'" **Phonetic drift** (Ch. VIII): parallel, independent sound changes in related-but-separated languages (English and German both developing umlaut plurals — *foot/feet*, *Fuss/Füsse* — though German lagged English by roughly three centuries and neither inherited the change), pointing to "some general tendency... at work in early Germanic" that drove both dialects along parallel paths.

In later linguistics "drift" was taken up (and contested) as **Sapir's drift** — the claim that change has inherent long-range directionality — and distinguished from the population-genetics-inspired notion of random drift. Sapir himself refused to explain the primary cause: rejecting "ease of articulation" and children's "faulty perception," he wrote, "It is much better to admit that we do not yet understand the primary cause or causes of the slow drift in phonetics."

## Operationalizations

Drift is diagnosed retrospectively, from the direction of accumulated change, not measured prospectively; Sapir offered a qualitative scale of "hesitation values" among competing constructions (the several forms of *who/whom* questions) as symptomatic evidence of the direction of drift. There is no standard quantitative index.

## Applications

- Explaining parallel developments in genetically related languages without common inheritance (Ch. VIII umlaut case) — a bridge to [[historical-linguistics|phonetic law]].
- The interaction of drift with the [[phonetic-pattern|phonetic pattern]]: sound change tends to preserve a language's overall pattern even as individual sounds shift.
- Grounding Sapir's claim that a language's line of variation "runs inexorably in the channel ordained for it by its historic antecedents" — independent of speakers' temperament (see [[race-and-culture-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The strong reading — that drift has an intrinsic, quasi-teleological direction — is **contested**: it risks unfalsifiability (direction is only known after the fact) and Sapir explicitly could name no mechanism. Critics have argued that what looks like unitary directional drift is the aggregate of many locally-caused changes, and that the population-genetics analogy (change as accumulated random variation) undercuts the "slope" metaphor. The concept nonetheless remains a standard reference point in historical linguistics for the observation that related languages change in parallel, patterned ways.
