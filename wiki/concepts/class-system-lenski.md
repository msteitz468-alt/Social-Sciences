# Class System (Lenski)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (Ch. 4), [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] defines a **class** as an aggregation of persons who stand in a similar position with respect to some form of power, privilege, or prestige; by default he means **power class**. A **class system** is the hierarchical ordering of such classes along one resource or power base. A society’s **distributive system** typically contains **several** class systems (property, political, occupational, educational, racial/ethnic/religious, sex, age, etc.). Individuals may belong to multiple classes at once when bases are imperfectly correlated.

## Semantic History

Broadens “class” beyond Marxian property dichotomies and beyond single prestige ladders; related to Weberian multidimensionality ([[social-class-weberian]], [[status-group]]) but systematized for comparative evolutionary types. “Class system based on sex/age” vocabulary is etic and dated; later gender and age stratification theories redescribe the same terrain.

## Operationalizations

Named class systems in industrial chapters (political, property, entrepreneurial, managerial, professional, working, farming, etc.); agrarian classes (governing, retainer, merchant, priestly, peasant, artisan, unclean, expendables).

## Applications

Structural backbone of Lenski’s type chapters:

| Society type | Illustrative class systems / classes (Lenski 1966) |
|---|---|
| Advanced horticultural | Ruler, nobility/kin, commoners; hereditary office and slavery |
| Agrarian | Governing, retainer, merchant, priestly, peasant, artisan, unclean/degraded, expendables |
| Industrial | Political; property; occupational (entrepreneurial, managerial, military, professional, clerical, sales, working, farming, unemployed/slave-labor); educational; racial/ethnic/religious; sex; age |

Class struggle multiplies across systems (property, status-group, sex, age) rather than collapsing to a single Marxist property dichotomy. Citizenship is an equalizing resource that revives redistributive politics ([[redistribution]], [[social-state]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Nominalist breadth of “class”; risk of proliferating systems without a theory of which dominate; sex/race as “class systems” contested by scholars who refuse to subsume them under class vocabulary.
