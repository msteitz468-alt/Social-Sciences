# Cosmotheism

## Definition as Coined

In [[assmann-jan|Jan Assmann]]'s usage, **cosmotheism** names the religious world-form in which the divine is immanent in the cosmos itself — the cosmic process (above all the sun's daily course) *is* the unfolding of the divine, and religion's task is participatory maintenance of that process through ritual. Assmann treats Egyptian religion as the paradigm case and cosmotheism as the antitype against which he defines exclusivist monotheism (the "Mosaic distinction") (Assmann 1990s; [[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|source]]).

## Semantic History

- **1782** — the word appears in early-modern deist/spinozist debates (deus sive natura tradition).
- **1990s** — Assmann repurposes it analytically; in *The Mind of Egypt* it appears as "cosmotheism as a form of knowledge": Egyptian cosmology as a knowledge-form sustained by priestly observation and rite.
- **1997–** — *Moses the Egyptian* makes cosmotheism one pole of the mnemohistorical opposition monotheism defined itself against.

## Operationalizations

Readings of solar hymns, the Amduat and related underworld books, temple ritual, and New Kingdom theological texts.

## Applications

History of Egyptian religion; Assmann's account of [[amarna|Akhenaten]]'s revolution as an intra-Egyptian rupture in cosmotheistic knowledge; early-modern intellectual history of "Egyptian" wisdom (Hermeticism, Freemasonry).

## Critiques and Limitations

Positions recorded: theologians and historians of religion have contested the cosmotheism/monotheism binary as too clean and as carrying normative weight; Egyptologists note that "Egyptian religion" so unified elides regional, temporal, and non-elite variation.
