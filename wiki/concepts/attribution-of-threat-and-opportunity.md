# Attribution of Threat and Opportunity

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* Cognitive-relational **mobilization mechanism**: contenders form shared definitions that environmental change constitutes **threat** or **opportunity**. Part of DOC’s critique of static “political opportunity structure” boxes — opportunities must be **attributed**, often through competition among interpreters.

## Definition as Coined

> "Collective attribution of threat or opportunity is a crucial mechanism in mobilization. It involves (a) invention or importation and (b) diffusion of a shared definition concerning alterations in the likely consequences of possible actions (or, for that matter, failures to act) undertaken by some political actor. Threat-opportunity attribution often emerges from competition among advocates of differing interpretations, one of which finally prevails."

## Semantic History

From McAdam political-process “opportunity structure” to DOC’s **attribution** emphasis. Related “opportunity/threat spirals” appear in polarization and nationalism chapters. Environmental shocks (assassinations, aid cuts, succession crises) matter as they are **read**.

## Operationalizations

Narrative and documentary evidence of how actors (and competing factions) interpret regime openings, threats, allies, and international models.

## Applications

*(DOC Ch. 4 attributed illustrations:)* Postwar Kenya — settlers and authorities attributing decolonization as threat (Malaya Emergency model; Kenya Plan); nationalists attributing opportunity (KAU, Kenyatta return, regional strikes). Philippines — Aquino assassination as suddenly imposed grievance reshaping opportunity/threat definitions across classes and Church.

## Critiques and Limitations

Post-hoc risk (successful mobilizations coded as “correct” attribution); perception measurement hard; co-occurs with [[social-appropriation]] and [[brokerage]] so isolation is analytical, not empirical purity.
