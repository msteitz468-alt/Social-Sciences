# Long Count

## Definition as Coined

The **Long Count** is the Mesoamerican absolute calendrical count of days from a mythic Creation base date written 4 Ajaw 8 Kumk'u. As synthesized by [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]], Classic Maya monuments typically open with an **Initial Series**: Long Count from that base, plus the 260-day day name, lunar calculations, and the 365-day month day. Numerals use bar-and-dot with a stylized zero in a vigesimal (base-20) place system (with a modified third place of 18×20 = 360 for the *tun*).

## Semantic History

- **Emic practice:** Classic and earlier scribes recorded Long Counts on stone, jade, and (later) codices; period endings (*k'atun*, half-*k'atun*, etc.) structured royal ritual.
- **Modern correlation:** Converting Long Count to Gregorian depends on correlation constants. Coe & Houston treat the **Thompson 11.16** correlation as overwhelmingly supported by Tikal sapodilla lintel radiocarbon and related work; Martin & Skidmore refine the constant (584286 in their study as cited). Spinden 12.9 (260 years earlier) is the main rejected alternative for dirt archaeology.
- **Origins:** Authors report oldest recorded Long Counts in Bak'tun 7 *outside* the Maya area proper (Chiapa de Corzo Stela 2 reconstructable to 9 December 36 BC; Tres Zapotes; La Mojarra AD 143/156), with debate over Isthmian/Mixe-Zoquean vs Maya roles. San Bartolo early writing (c. 300–200 BC) is said to upend simple “Isthmian → Maya” descent models.

## Operationalizations

- **Classic definition of period:** Coe & Houston define the Classic as the span when lowland Maya used the Long Count on monuments.
- **Terminal Classic last dates:** k'atun 10.3.0.0.0 (AD 889) at only five sites; 10.4.0.0.0 (AD 909) at Tonina and a jade; last Peten stela date at Itzimte = 16 January AD 910 (as of C&H 2015).
- **Distance Numbers** move forward/back sometimes millions of years (mythic dynastic time).

## Applications

Anchors [[classic-maya]] dynastic history; structures [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] chronology; pairs with [[calendar-round]] for full dates.

## Critiques and Limitations

Correlation remains a technical debate (though Thompson-class solutions dominate archaeology as of C&H 2015). Absolute “origin of the Long Count” (who invented it, where) is open. Glottochronology and some early 14C series for Preclassic are treated cautiously in the same book.

### From Sharer & Traxler 2006

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler, *The Ancient Maya*, 6th ed. 2006]] unless noted.

**Shared Mesoamerican cycles vs Maya absolute count.** They report three ancient cycles shared across Mesoamerica — 260-day almanac ([[tzolkin]]), 365-day year ([[haab]]), and 52-year [[calendar-round|Calendar Round]] — while treating the **Long Count** (and Period-Ending / Short Count derivatives) as the chronological system **unique to the Maya**, tracking days from a fixed zero independent of those cycles. They present the Maya as the only pre-Columbian society to use such a fixed start date in their account.

**Base date, great cycle, units.** Chronology reckoned from the end of the preceding great cycle of thirteen *bak’tuns* (~5,128 solar years): **13.0.0.0.0 4 Ajaw 8 Kumk’u** = a day in **3114 BCE**, creation of the current world in Maya cosmology; precedes the earliest contemporary Long Count (Chiapa de Corzo Stela 2) by over 3,000 years. Under the GMT correlation they adopt, that great cycle ends **21 December 2012** (Appendix Table A.1: 13.0.0.0.0 4 Ajaw 3 K’ank’in). Basic units: *k’in* (day); *winal* (20 *k’in*); calendrical *tun* = 18 *winals* = **360** days (not 400), approximating the solar year; 20 *tuns* = 1 *k’atun* (7,200 days); 20 *k’atuns* = 1 *bak’tun* (144,000 days); higher orders (*piktun*, *kalabtun*, etc.) continue vigesimally. Numeral systems vigesimal with **zero** and bar-and-dot notation (positional by Late Preclassic).

**Initial Series structure.** Long Count dates open most Classic inscriptions (“Initial Series,” named by Alfred Maudslay): oversized introductory glyph (variable element = patron of the Haab month); five blocks for *bak’tuns*–*k’ins*; almanac day; Glyphs G/F (Nine Lords of the Night); lunar series (E, D, C, X, B, A); Haab day closes. First appear on Late Preclassic southern monuments, then throughout Classic lowlands. **Distance numbers** count days forward/backward from a base Long Count to a new Calendar Round (often ascending *k’ins*–*winals*–*tuns*); used for history and deep mythic past (e.g., Quirigua Stela F ~91,683,930 *tuns* earlier than 9.16.10.0.0).

**Abbreviation path.** By Late Classic, full Long Counts often replaced by abbreviated **period-ending** dates unique within ~19,000 years; by Postclassic, *k’atun*-ending **Short Count** (*u k’ahlay k’atunob*) unique within ~256 years (thirteen differently designated *k’atuns*), as in the Books of Chilam Balam.

**GMT correlation and alternatives.** They follow **Goodman–Martínez–Thompson (GMT / 11.16)**: 11.16.0.0.0 13 Ajaw 8 Xul = Gregorian **12 November 1539** (constant **584,283**), as best matching archaeology, history, and radiocarbon. Spinden places dates 260 years earlier; Vaillant’s 11.3 adds 260 years (Classic end ~1150). Early Tikal lintel 14C seemed to favor Spinden; a larger Temple IV sample (10 of 12 dates consistent with GMT) supported GMT. No Spanish-era Long Count–European day correspondences survive to fix the correlation directly.

**Isthmian and early lowland anchors.** Isthmian Long Counts (single vertical bar-and-dot column), assuming Classic zero date: Tres Zapotes Stela C ≈ 31 BC; La Mojarra ≈ AD 143 and 156; Tuxtla Statuette ≈ AD 162; Chiapa de Corzo Stela 2 most plausible 7.16.3.2.13 = 36 BC. Early lowland: Tikal Stela 29 = 8.12.14.8.15 (**AD 292**). Last known stela with Long Count: [[tonina]] Monument 101 at **10.4.0.0.0 (909)**.

**Historiography — Morley’s barometer.** [[morley-sylvanus-g|Morley]]’s stelae catalog treated Long Count frequency as a barometer of the “Old Empire” (peak sites at 9.18.0.0.0 = AD 790; last dates shifted from 889 to 909). Sharer & Traxler argue end of Long Count monuments is only one symptom of multi-century decline of divine kings, not demise of civilization ([[el-mirador]] rose and fell without Long Count). Royal texts do **not** narrate Terminal Classic change; declining monument quantity and workmanship index diminished support for [[kuhul-ajaw|k’uhul ajaw]].

**Accuracy caveats.** They reject claims that Maya fixed the tropical year more accurately than the Gregorian calendar (no evidence); they do credit accurate lunation measurement (~29.53 days) and Dresden eclipse tables. Venus (*Noh Ek’* / *Xux Ek’*) synodic period ~584 days; five Venus revolutions = eight calendar years = 2,920 days as auspicious coincidence.
