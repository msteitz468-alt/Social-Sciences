# Affinity (Kinship)

> Relations created by **marriage**, as opposed to relations of **filiation/descent**. In mid-century British kinship theory the term is load-bearing and contested: [[leach-edmund|Leach]] argued that treating affinity only as the individual reciprocal of parent–child ties **disguises group alliance**; [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] treated complementary filiation as the kinship reciprocal of affinal relationship in the marriage tie. Primary source for Leach’s side of the fight: *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]* (1961), esp. Preface, Ch. 1, Ch. 5.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Leach 1961.)* Two levels must be kept distinct:

1. **Individual affinal ties** — the particular wife–husband bond and the personal in-law relations of named persons.
2. **Affinity as alliance** — enduring relations between **corporate kin groups** established and renewed by marriage (e.g. Jinghpaw *[[mayu-dama|mayu/dama]]*, Lakher *patong/ngazua*).

Leach’s polemical sentence (the one that provoked Fortes 1959b and Goody 1959): Fortes, while recognizing that ties of affinity have importance comparable to ties of descent, “disguises the former under his expression ‘[[complementary-filiation|complementary filiation]].’”

Marriage, on Leach’s formulation, creates an alliance between groups A and B; children may relate to either group by **incorporation** (common substance / we-group membership) and/or by the **alliance** itself (metaphysical influence).

## Semantic History

| Tradition | Affinity means |
|---|---|
| Ordinary English / legal | In-laws; relationship by marriage not blood |
| Fortesian descent theory | Reciprocal of the marriage tie, often absorbed analytically into filiation categories (complementary filiation as “kinship reciprocal of affinal relationship”) |
| Leach / alliance reading | Primarily **inter-group alliance**; individual marriage ties are not an adequate model for *mayu–dama*-type systems |
| Lévi-Straussian alliance theory | Marriage exchange as generative of social structure; affinity as structure of reciprocity between groups |

## Operationalizations

| Indicator | Group-alliance affinity | Individual affinity |
|---|---|---|
| Jinghpaw *mayu / dama* | Enduring wife-giver / wife-receiver between local descent groups | Particular husband–wife |
| Lakher *patong / ngazua* | Same structural pair; Leach stresses greater fragility and hire-like transfer of procreative powers | Same |
| Bridewealth / divorce rules | Express permanence or revocability of **group** transfer of rights | Personal cohabitation |

Leach (Ch. 5): societies may share the same **descent** form yet differ in the **nature of the marriage institution** — Ordinary Jinghpaw (near-indissoluble person-transfer) vs Lakher (expensive, complicated bridewealth; easy divorce; widow freer to leave). That difference is about affinity/marriage, not about inventing “complementary filiation” or double descent for the Lakher.

## Applications

- **Continuum argument (Ch. 1).** Trobriand extreme: father as non-relative / affine-like; Kachin–Lakher extreme: mother-side as source of mystical influence and political rank via affinal alliance; intermediate cases where Fortes’s concept may have utility without being universalized.
- **Witchcraft / metaphysics.** Leach maps uncontrolled mystical influence to **alliance**, controlled supernatural attack to **potential authority**; where sources coincide, affinity expresses political dominance.
- **Polyandry / definition of marriage (Ch. 4).** Bundle-of-rights analysis of marriage presupposes that affinity is a package of transferable rights, not a single legitimacy criterion.
- **Achuar “affinal nature” (Descola 1986/1994).** In *[[descola-in-the-society-of-nature-1994|In the Society of Nature]]* ([[descola-society-of-nature-study|study]]), [[descola-philippe|Descola]] extends affinity beyond human marriage into forest ecology: hunting [[anent]] address game as *sai* (brother-in-law); game mothers/*amana* are go-betweens; the forest is an “ideally affinal world,” structurally inverse to the garden’s **consanguineal** mothering of plant children under Nunkui (attributed — Achuar 1970s). Human Dravidian alliance and feud among the [[achuar]] remain the social template this extension presupposes.
- **Amazonian Dravidianate: actual vs. potential affinity (Viveiros de Castro 1992 [1986]).** Fieldwork among the [[arawete|Araweté]] (1981–83) documents a terminological system Viveiros de Castro describes as "Dravidian"-type, in which "actual affinity is strongly differentiated from potential affinity, a trait characteristic of the Amazonian 'Dravidian group' systems" (attributed). The nonrelative/cross-cousin category *tiwã* denotes potential spouses and potential affines, but the terms for actual affines (wife's father, mother, brother) are terminologically excluded from it; after marriage, formerly-kin categories convert to "ex-X" forms, which Viveiros de Castro reads as "a certain repulsion between consanguinity and affinity — as if marriage cancels out previous bonds" (attributed). He generalizes this actual/potential distinction into a full concept, [[potential-affinity]], including its cosmological extension (the Araweté gods as "our gigantic potential affines," death as a marriage converting *tiwã* into actual affines) and its historical extension to the 16th-century [[tupinamba|Tupinambá]] war captive, read as an "anti-affine" via H. Clastres's observation that Tupinambá enemy and brother-in-law share a term (*tovaja*). See *[[viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-1992|From the Enemy's Point of View]]* for the full argument.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Fortes’s position** (as Leach reports it): complementary filiation *is* a function of affinal relations; the charge of “disguise” is therefore misplaced. Record both sides; do not adjudicate in wiki voice.
- **Goody** denounced Leach’s argument as grounded in fundamental error (1959) — position, not settled fact.
- **Not a synonym for alliance theory.** Affinity is the relation; [[alliance-theory]] is the program that takes group marriage exchange as generative. Leach is a critical ally of Lévi-Strauss, not a pure school disciple.
- **Evidence class.** Leach’s affinity arguments rest on reanalysis of published ethnography (Parry on Lakher; his own Kachin work; Malinowski on Trobriands) plus short Ceylon fieldwork for the polyandry paper — date-stamp and attribute.
