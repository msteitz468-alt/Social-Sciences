# National State

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly, [[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]].)*
**National states** are "states governing multiple contiguous regions and their cities by means
of centralized, differentiated, and autonomous structures." Tilly's load-bearing distinction:
**national state ≠ nation-state**. "The term national state, regrettably, does not necessarily
mean nation-state, a state whose people share a strong linguistic, religious, and symbolic
identity" — "Great Britain, Germany, and France — quintessential national states — certainly
have never met the test," while China is the "extraordinary exception": nearly three millennia
of national states, "but never one year as a nation-state."

The national state is one of three recurrent European state forms, alongside **tribute-taking
empires** (large military/extractive apparatus, local administration left to autonomous
regional powerholders) and **systems of fragmented sovereignty** ([[city-state|city-states]],
urban federations). "The long survival and coexistence of all three types tells against any
notion of European state formation as a single, unilinear process, or of the national state...
as an inherently superior form of government."

## Semantic History

Tilly's usage sharpens loose talk of "the nation-state" into a form-classification: what
generalized worldwide is the *organizational* form (centralized, differentiated, autonomous,
contiguous), not national homogeneity. His historical claims (attributed): nothing like a
national state existed anywhere in Europe in 990; ~200 states in 1490 consolidated to ~25–30 by
1890; at Charles V's abdication (1555) "nothing then assured the ultimate victory of the
national state" — empires were still advancing. The form won because
[[capitalized-coercion]]-path states (France, England) "set the terms of war": "national states
won out in the world as a whole because they first won out in Europe, whose states then acted
to reproduce themselves" — via war settlements (Westphalia 1648 consolidating "the European
system of national states"; Vienna 1815 as near-"deliberate collective mapping"), colonization,
standardized institutional models, and post-1945 great-power tutelage, until the form became
"the template for state formation everywhere" (European political control of the earth's land:
7% in 1500, 35% in 1800, 84% in 1914, via Headrick).

Tilly's 1990-92 speculation that states "may soon lose their incredible hegemony" (blocs,
transnational firms, stateless-nation claims) is date-stamped and contested.

## Operationalizations

Membership rosters of the state system (Levy's and Modelski–Thompson's great-power lists —
which Tilly critiques as Eurocentric); UN membership as an imperfect proxy post-1945; formal
organizational convergence ("the present 160-odd recognized states cover a much narrower
organizational range than the 200-odd European states of 1500").

## Applications

- The dependent variable of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]] and the outcome of the
  [[capitalized-coercion]] trajectories.
- Grounds the analysis of nationalism: Tilly distinguishes state-led identification from
  state-seeking independence claims — two distinct phenomena that multiplied together in the
  19th century as direct rule homogenized populations.
- The frame for Tilly's post-1945 analysis: new states acquired the *form* while diverging in
  state–citizen relations.

## Critiques and Limitations

The national-state/nation-state distinction, though analytically valuable, cuts against
entrenched usage and is often ignored in reception. The form-classification inherits the
book's Eurocentrism: it defines the global norm from the European winner. Attributed throughout.
