# Concentration Effects (Wilson)

## Definition as Coined

[[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]] (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*, 1987) uses **concentration effects** for the **constraints and opportunities** that come from living in neighborhoods where the population is overwhelmingly socially disadvantaged — ecological niches of job access and job networks, availability of marriageable partners, quality schools, and exposure to conventional role models — effects that operate **with** [[social-isolation]] and are not reducible to individual-level poverty alone.

## Semantic History

Tied to the **class transformation** of the inner city: exit of black middle and working classes raises the proportion of the “truly disadvantaged,” producing extreme-poverty areas (Wilson’s Chicago maps). Feeds later **neighborhood effects** research (Wilson 2012 Afterword reviews this lineage). Overlaps but is not identical to Massey–Denton’s spatial **concentration** dimension of [[hypersegregation]] — different evidence class and causal story ([[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]).

**Sampson reception (*[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]*, 2012, attributed):** Wilson’s concentration/isolation thesis is a standing interlocutor. Sampson cites Wilson that residents of very poor neighborhoods may be **tightly interconnected through personal networks without producing collective resources or social regulation** — ties excessively personalistic and parochial, isolated from public resources and mainstream role models (rationale for [[collective-efficacy]] over dense-ties ideals). He operationalizes a multi-indicator **[[concentrated-disadvantage]]** PCA as the structural correlate of concentration effects and shows **inertial ecological ranking** (poverty r=0.78 community-area 1960–2000; disadvantage 1990→2000 r>0.9 nationally) that reframes concentration as durable relative position, not only 1970s–80s transformation. Against readings of Wilson that treat deindustrialization/globalization/migration as fully misspecifying neighborhood effects, Sampson holds macrosocial forces matter but **do not negate lower-order neighborhood mechanisms** when superimposed on a reproducing structure. Wilson’s foreword to GAC summarizes Sampson as showing powerful effects of ecologically concentrated disadvantage **magnified by racial segregation**.

## Operationalizations

1987: community-area poverty thresholds and associated social indicators. Later literature: multilevel models of neighborhood poverty share; Sampson/PHDCN **concentrated-disadvantage PCA** (six census indicators including poverty and percent black) and process mediators (CE, disorder) — date-stamp any claim (as of Author Year).

## Applications

Core of Wilson’s structural account of the [[urban-underclass]]; policy rationale for deconcentrating poverty and for universal labor-market strategies. Empirical afterlife in PHDCN / *Great American City* violence, disorder, incarceration-ecology, and developmental (verbal ability) analyses ([[sampson-great-american-city-study]]; [[hubs/studies/sampson-great-american-city-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Causal identification of neighborhood vs selection effects contested in later work ([[neighborhood-effects-debate]]; MTO literature); segregation theorists argue concentration of black poverty is produced by ongoing segregation, not only class exit. Sampson’s “structural sorting” inverts selection critiques without dissolving the concentration thesis.
