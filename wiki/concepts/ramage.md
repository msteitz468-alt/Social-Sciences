# Ramage

## Definition as Coined

**Ramage** is [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]]'s revival of an older, near-disused English word (from
Latin *ramus*, "branch") to serve as a general comparative label for a kind of kinship group: a named
descent group whose members trace connection to a common ancestor and which itself grows by *branching*
— segments splitting off and acquiring autonomy while retaining a graded relation to the parent stem.
In *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]* (1936) Firth applied it to the Tikopia *paito*
("house"), preferring "ramage" over "joint family," "extended family," "lineage," "sib," "clan," or the
Maori "hapu," because it foregrounds the fission-with-connection process rather than a fixed structure.

## Semantic History

Firth's own usage shifted. In 1936 he translated *paito* as "house" and offered "ramage" as the general
type-term. In his 1957 "Introduction to Second Printing" and the article "A Note on Descent Groups in
Polynesia" (*Man*, 1957) he revised course: he would now render *paito* as **"lineage,"** and reserve
**"ramage"** specifically for **non-unilineal (ambilineal/cognatic) descent groups with a structural
element of choice** — the type common across Polynesia, where an individual may affiliate to a descent
group through either parent. This narrowed, more technical sense — ramage = optative/cognatic descent
group — is the one that entered comparative anthropology, notably in the debates over Polynesian
descent (Goodenough, Sahlins, and others) about whether "unilineal descent" theory could handle the
Polynesian material at all.

## Operationalizations

A ramage is identified through recorded genealogies (the branching charter from a founding ancestor)
together with the group's material and ritual foci — an ancestral house serving as temple, associated
land, a name, and a place in a ranked order of segments. Tikopia's ~30 major *paito*, grouped into four
ranked *kainaŋa* (clans), are Firth's worked example; the Tikopia case is patrilineal, whereas the later
technical sense of "ramage" emphasizes the *ambilineal* Polynesian type.

## Applications

- The Tikopia *paito*/*kainaŋa* descent structure ([[tikopia]]).
- The comparative typology of Polynesian descent groups (Samoa, Tonga, Maori *hapu*, Tahiti, etc.),
  where Firth's Ch. XVI survey treats descent as "plastic" material shaped by residence and economy.
- More broadly, any analysis distinguishing branching descent groups from strictly [[unilineal-descent|unilineal]]
  lineages or [[double-descent|double-descent]] systems.

## Critiques and Limitations

The term's very flexibility is the objection: Firth's 1936 (general, unilineal-inclusive) and 1957
(narrow, non-unilineal) usages diverge, and later writers used "ramage" inconsistently — some as a
synonym for cognatic descent group, some for a ranked segmentary lineage — so the label can obscure as
much as it clarifies unless the intended sense is stated. It remains a useful pointer to the
fission-and-rank dynamics of Polynesian descent that classic [[unilineal-descent|unilineal-descent theory]]
handled poorly. See [[firth-raymond]], [[firth-we-the-tikopia-study]].
