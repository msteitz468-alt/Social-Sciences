# Labour Theory of Value (Marx)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), [[marx-karl|Marx]] argues that the **value** of a [[commodity]] has as its substance **abstract human labour** — labour in general, abstracted from particular useful forms — and as its magnitude **socially necessary labour-time**.

He presents this as the scientific analysis of the commodity’s exchangeability, not as a moral claim about just price. Exchange-value is the **form of appearance** of value; money is the finished form of the value relation.

**All claims attributed to Marx** unless otherwise marked. Do not overwrite the wiki’s [[value|Simmel value]] page with this framework.

## Operationalizations

From *Capital* I, Ch. 1 (and prefaces):

1. **Dual character of labour** — concrete useful labour produces use-values; abstract human labour creates value.
2. **Socially necessary labour-time** — average skill and intensity under normal social conditions of production.
3. **Simple average labour** as unit; complex labour as multiples of simple (social reduction).
4. **Value-form analysis** — how value appears as exchange-value and becomes money.
5. **Money functions** — measure of value, medium of circulation, money proper (hoard, payment, world money).

Method (1867 preface): analysis of economic forms requires the **force of abstraction**; the commodity is the cell-form of bourgeois society.

## Applications

- Foundation for [[surplus-value]] (unpaid labour beyond the value of [[labor-power]]).
- Critique of circulation-only explanations of profit (Part 2).
- Historical claim that scientific bourgeois political economy ends as [[class-struggle]] becomes open (1873 afterword).

### "Smith's Dogma" (per Capital Vol. 2, Ch. 19–20)

In *[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume II]]*, Marx extends the twofold-character-of-labour argument into a critique of Adam Smith's resolution of commodity price/value into wages + profit + rent (v + s, "Smith's dogma"). Marx argues Smith conflates "the value of the annual product" with "the annual value product": only the latter is newly created by the current year's labour, while the former also includes the value of means of production carried over from prior years and merely reappearing (transferred, not created). Per Marx, this conflation lets Smith's constant-capital component "vanish" from the social-level analysis of value, and it traces back to Smith's failure to distinguish concrete useful labour (which produces use-values, preserving old value) from abstract labour (which creates new value) — the same dual-character-of-labour distinction Marx develops in Vol. I, Ch. 1. Marx holds that revenues (wages, profit, rent) derive from value, not value from revenues. Smith (and later writers responding to his dogma — Ricardo, Say, Sismondi, J.S. Mill, among others, per Marx) is discussed in plain text, having no dedicated wiki page.

### Values, Prices of Production, and Essence-vs-Appearance (Vol. III, 1894)

In *[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume III]]* (ed. Engels, 1894), Marx develops a methodological distinction between **essence and appearance**: "the rate of profit is the historical starting-point. Surplus-value and the rate of surplus-value are, relatively, the invisible essence to be investigated, whereas the rate of profit... are visible surface phenomena." Ordinary economic consciousness moves from the visible (profit, [[price-of-production]]) backward, but scientific exposition must move from essence (value, surplus-value) to appearance. Marx argues values are **historically and logically prior** to prices of production: the direct exchange of commodities at (approximately) their values is characteristic of **simple commodity production**, where producers own their own means of production, before the capitalist transformation of value into cost price plus average profit supersedes value as the immediate regulator of exchange ratios.

**Engels's Supplement (1895).** In his posthumous "Supplement to Volume 3," Engels answers Werner Sombart and Conrad Schmidt's readings of the law of value as merely "ideal" or "fictional" with an explicitly historical argument: the law of value "applies universally, as far as economic laws are valid at all, for the entire period of simple commodity production" — from the earliest exchange of products as commodities (which Engels dates, citing Egyptian and Babylonian evidence, to roughly 3500–6000 BCE) down to the fifteenth century CE, when merchant and early industrial capital began to impose the price-of-production mechanism. On Engels's reading the transformation of values into prices of production is thus not merely a logical device internal to the three-volume exposition but a *historical* transition Marx's theory tracks. This is Engels's own 1895 argument, attributed to him; not independently corroborated by archaeological or historical-linguistic evidence within this source, and not to be promoted to wiki voice on any deep-time page.

### Lenin (1899): Smith's Dogma as the Root of the Narodnik Realization Error

In *[[lenin-development-of-capitalism-in-russia-1899|The Development of Capitalism in Russia]]* (1899), [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] credited Adam Smith with founding the erroneous doctrine, dominant in political economy until Marx, that a commodity's price — and the total social product — resolves into only two or three parts (wages, profit, rent), omitting constant capital. Quoting Smith's *Wealth of Nations* directly alongside Marx's critique (attributed to Marx via *Capital* I and II/III), Lenin held that Smith's "dogma" — rooted in confusing the value of the total product with the value newly created within a year — "prevented political economy from understanding even the most elementary mechanism... of social reproduction." Lenin deployed this diagnosis (attributed) as the doctrinal root of the Narodnik economists' [[surplus-value|realization]] error: because N.-on and V. V. followed Smith's two-part schema rather than Marx's three-part division of the product into constant capital + variable capital + surplus-value (c + v + s), together with the two-department division of production (Department I, means of production; Department II, articles of consumption), they mistook a genuine problem of realizing constant capital for an impossibility of realizing surplus-value on the home market alone.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested by subjective-value economics and by internal Marxist debates (prices of production, abstract labour).
- Marx’s own later volumes and unpublished manuscripts refine distribution of surplus-value — not fully in Vol. I.
- Empirical “test” of SNLT is not a modern econometric operationalization; the construct is theoretical-analytic.

## Bogdanov 1921: an organizational defense (attributed)

In [[tektology]] Bogdanov criticized marginal-utility theory as "anti-organizational" — grounding exchange in the isolated individual's psychology while exchange is an organizational relation within social production — and held the labor theory of value already stands on the organizational point of view, though "not quite completely carried out," citing his own "Exchange and Technology" and the Bogdanov–Stepanov Political Economy for the completion ([[bogdanov-essays-in-tektology-1980]]). Book 1 Addendum 1 ([[bogdanov-tektology-book1-1996]]) further applies organizational equilibrium and the law of the leasts to labour and needs: each worker must receive enough of the social product to perform their social function (a Bazarov-derived *norm* of distribution, not a description of market facts); qualified labour is treated as multiplied energy expenditure; mechanical wage-levelling is rejected as incompatible with organizational balance.
