# Vanguard Party

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lenin-what-is-to-be-done-1902|What Is to Be Done?]]* (1902), [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] did not use a fully scholastic label “vanguard party” as later Bolshevik lexicon would, but **programmatically defined** the role of a revolutionary Social-Democratic party as **vanguard**: “the role of vanguard can be fulfilled only by a party that is guided by an advanced theory.” Without revolutionary theory, he argued, there can be no revolutionary movement—especially when opportunism’s fashionable preaching combines with the narrowest practical activity.

He asked what Social-Democracy’s function is if not to be a “spirit” not only hovering over the spontaneous movement but **raising it to the level of “its program”**—not dragging at the tail (*khvostism*). Only a party that will organize **real, public exposures** can become the vanguard of revolutionary forces; sticking the label “vanguard” on rearguard theory and practice is not enough. If “we” desire to be the vanguard, it is “our duty” to guide activities of various opposition strata, organize universal political struggle under Party leadership, and train practical workers as political leaders.

Organizationally this vanguard is the **[[professional-revolutionary|organization of professional revolutionaries]]**—compact, secret under autocracy, led by real political leaders of the whole people—not the wide trade-union apparatus of economic struggle. *Iskra*, he claimed, desires to **elevate** working-class trade-union politics to Social-Democratic politics; *Rabocheye Dyelo* desires the reverse while assuring compatibility.

## Operationalizations

Lenin’s operational picture of vanguard function (attributed):

- **Ideological:** combat spontaneity; bring [[consciousness-from-without|consciousness from without]]; no “middle” ideology between bourgeois and socialist.
- **Political:** all-sided exposure of autocracy; go among all classes as theoreticians, propagandists, agitators, organizers; subordinate reforms to liberty and socialism as part to whole; preserve proletarian political independence while uniting pressure in the name of the whole people.
- **Organizational:** continuous organization of leaders; professional revolutionaries; specialization and centralization; national over purely local center of gravity; lead all-Russian trade unions from a single organization of revolutionaries.
- **Negative markers of failure:** lagging leaders; *kustar* methods; economic struggle as sole path; tactics-as-unbounded process without plan; broad internal “democracy” as toy under autocracy; workers reduced to rearguard of bourgeois democracy after spontaneous risings.

## Applications

- **Against Martynov / RD:** vanguard must guide opposition strata and dictate positive programs for students, Zemstvo figures, sects, teachers, etc., at the right time—not merely “expose” in a negative role.
- **Against Economist letter (*Iskra* No. 12):** allies among liberals/intelligentsia needed for political training of workers; “accumulating forces” means that training, not waiting in pure trade-union politics first.
- **German prestige model (as Lenin cites):** Social-Democracy always ahead on every protest and sphere of social-political life.
- **Force of millions:** Marxism, he argued against RD’s ban on tactics plans, gives gigantic impetus to initiative and places at SDs’ disposal the force of millions spontaneously rising—plans do not contradict Marxism’s spirit.
- **Mao / CCP packaging (attributed):** *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations]]* ch. 1 insists on a revolutionary party built on Marxist-Leninist theory and style; Party as "core of leadership"; three main weapons (Party, army under Party, united front). The [[mass-line]] claims to ground that leadership in a spiral "from the masses, to the masses" — a Chinese organizational complement (and tension) relative to Lenin's [[consciousness-from-without]]. See [[maoism]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Economist / *Rabocheye Dyelo* counter-position (strongest form):** revolutionary SD’s task is only to **accelerate** objective development, not substitute “subjective plans”; *Iskra* belittles the objective/spontaneous element; mass movement determines tasks; economic struggle is the widely applicable political method; broad democratic organizational principles; fear that centralized “conspirative” organization is Narodovolist or undemocratic.
- **Class-independence worry (Economist letter):** seeking liberal allies and foregrounding general democratic discontent risks obscuring class antagonisms—*Iskra* answers that class character is preserved in **how** SD organizes universal agitation and trains the proletariat.
- **Scope:** 1902 pamphlet under illegality; “vanguard” here is a **function and organizational design**, not yet the full later party-state doctrine. Link to [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] is conceptual adjacency in the wider Lenin corpus, not a full theory of state power in this text.
- **Editor vs author:** do not replace Lenin’s arguments with anthology headnotes about “authoritarian organization” alone.

## Trotsky's substitutionism critique (via Service 2009)

The earliest inside critique of the vanguard model: [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]]'s *Our Political Tasks* (1904), written after siding with Martov in the 1903 membership-clause split, charged that Lenin's centralism would produce "political substitutionism" — the party substituting itself for the working class, the central leadership for the party, and finally a single leader for the leadership: "A barracks regime cannot be the regime for our party just as the factory cannot be its model." Robert Service calls the critique "prophetic in many basic respects" of later one-party rule ([[service-trotsky-2009]], Service's position); Trotsky himself merged his group into the Bolshevik party in 1917 and co-founded the Comintern on vanguardist lines. Adjacent: Jan Machajski's pre-1905 thesis that the radical intelligentsia would dominate the workers in whose name it ruled — a lineage running to the [[russian-question-debate]].

## Elite formation (Mawdsley & White 2000)

Mawdsley and White argue that purely functional accounts of Soviet elite change omit the Leninist ideology of a [[vanguard-party]] of professional revolutionaries distinct from the masses by higher political consciousness — a factor shaping closed early recruitment of the CC elite (mostly pre-revolutionary Bolsheviks for ~two decades). See [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]], [[democratic-centralism]].

## 1917: "patient explanation," not seizure by a minority (attributed)

In the April 1917 agitational writings collected in *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964|Collected Works, Vol. 24]]*, Lenin repeatedly cast the vanguard's task as winning a **majority**, not substituting for one. Against calls to act on the slogan "Down with the Provisional Government!," he argued the slogan was, at that moment, "either an empty phrase, or, objectively, amounts to attempts of an adventurist character," because it lacked the backing of an organized, class-conscious majority; the Party's actual task among the still-wavering petty-bourgeois and semi-proletarian masses was one of **"patient explanation"** (comradely persuasion), not premature action. At the Petrograd City Conference he stated the operating principle directly: "So long as the Soviets have not seized power, we shall not take it" — explicitly rejecting Blanquism ("a striving to seize power with the backing of a minority"): "We are not Blancists, we do not stand for the seizure of power by a minority. We are Marxists, we stand for proletarian class struggle." When the Petrograd Committee raised "Down with the Provisional Government!" alongside "Long Live the Soviets!" during the April 20–21 demonstrations, Lenin conceded this was a serious organizational error — "disorganisation" — reflecting weakness in the Party apparatus, adding that had it been deliberate the responsible individuals "would not have remained in the Central Committee for one moment." His "Political Parties in Russia" catechism mapped Russian parties to represented classes, assigning the Bolsheviks ("which properly should be called the Communist Party") the class base of class-conscious proletarians, wage-workers, and the poor peasantry (semi-proletarians) — group D of four, against Cadets (bourgeoisie as a whole) and Mensheviks/SRs (petty bourgeoisie and workers still under bourgeois influence).

## 1920: iron discipline, the vanguard/masses distinction, and work inside reactionary institutions (attributed)

In *[[lenin-left-wing-communism-1920|"Left-Wing" Communism: An Infantile Disorder]]*, Lenin argued Bolshevik Party discipline rested on three conditions: (1) the class-consciousness and devotion of the proletarian vanguard; (2) the vanguard's ability to link up and merge with the broadest masses of working people; (3) the correctness of the vanguard's political leadership, strategy, and tactics as confirmed by the masses' own experience. He insisted that **winning the vanguard is not enough**: "to throw into the decisive battle... only the vanguard, before the entire class... has taken up a position" of at least sympathetic neutrality would be "not merely foolish but criminal" — the masses need their own political experience, not propaganda alone; the distinct task after ideologically winning the vanguard is leading the broad, still-apathetic masses forward, which requires "liquidation of Left doctrinairism." Against German "Left" Communists who rejected all parliamentary and trade-union work as compromise with reactionary institutions, Lenin argued a genuine vanguard party must work even in "the most reactionary" trade unions to win over the backward masses, "**that is where the masses are to be found**" (see [[trade-union-consciousness]]), and defended the Bolsheviks' own organizational structure — Party (611,000 members, April 1920 Congress), trade unions (over 4 million members, formally non-Party but Communist-led), and the Soviets — as the concrete vehicle for vanguard leadership without formal party control of the unions. He acknowledged this structure could appear as "a full-fledged 'oligarchy'" (a 19-member Central Committee, with a 5-member Political Bureau) but dismissed the objection as comparable to debating "whether a man's left leg or right arm is of greater use to him."

## 1917 insurrection: the vanguard's relation to conspiracy (attributed)

In "Marxism and Insurrection" (Sept. 1917, in *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]*), Lenin distinguished Marxist insurrection from Blanquist conspiracy by three conditions: it must rely on the advanced class rather than on a party or conspiracy alone, on a revolutionary upsurge of the whole people, and on a moment of maximal vacillation among enemies and "wavering" allies — treating insurrection itself, following Marx, as "an art." He judged the July 1917 days premature on this test but the post-Kornilov September conjuncture ripe, and pressed the Central Committee toward concrete insurrectionary preparation, including organizing an insurgent headquarters. He also criticized the vanguard's own errors of tactics at the Democratic Conference (Bolshevik participation he called a "Bonapartist fraud" staged by the SRs and Mensheviks) as evidence that vanguard leadership requires correct reading of "objective class relations," not just revolutionary will.

## Kollontai 1921: the anti-substitutionist counter-position (attributed; [[kollontai-workers-opposition-1921]])

From inside the party, the Workers' Opposition inverted the vanguard/masses relation: "Only the vanguard of the class can create revolution, but only the whole class can develop through its everyday experience the practical work of the basic class collectives." On Kollontai's argument, the party cannot build the new economy on the class's behalf — dispersing communist cadres into heterogeneous state institutions "decays" their influence, and reliance on bourgeois specialists violates the principle that a rising class builds its own production forms. Lenin's Tenth Congress answer ("you cannot lead the proletariat without a Party"; the "producer" category dissolves class lines) and the ban on the faction are on [[soviet-trade-union-debate]].

## Trotsky 1920: party dictatorship enables soviet dictatorship (attributed)

In *[[trotsky-terrorism-and-communism-1920|Terrorism and Communism]]* (Ch.7), [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]] argues that dictatorship of a class presupposes unity of will amid heterogeneous moods, so revolutionary supremacy of the proletariat presupposes political supremacy of a party with a clear programme and faultless internal discipline. Coalition with other "socialist" organizations contradicts revolutionary dictatorship. To the charge of substituting party for soviets he replies: "the dictatorship of the soviets became possible only by means of the dictatorship of the party" — not accidental substitution, because Communists express the fundamental interests of the working class. Party holds general control and the final word on departmental and personal conflicts without immediately administering; trade unions after victory become compulsory organizers of production under Communist leadership and apparatus of repression against "undisciplined" workers (see [[militarization-of-labour]], [[soviet-trade-union-debate]]). Criterion of line under civil war: "We have suppressed the Mensheviks and the SRs — and they have disappeared."

## Party as "school of communism" and the vanguard/trade-union relation (1920–21, attributed)

In the trade-union controversy debated at the Tenth Congress (in *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]* and reprised in *[[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]]*), Lenin elaborated the vanguard/masses relation through a "system of transmission belts": the dictatorship of the proletariat cannot be exercised by the whole class, which remains "divided," "degraded," and "corrupted in parts" under capitalism, but only by its vanguard, requiring belts running "from the vanguard to the mass of the advanced class, and from the latter to the mass of the working people" (in Russia, the peasantry) — with trade unions positioned as the link between vanguard and masses and as a "reservoir of the state power." He contrasted this with syndicalism, which "hands over to the mass of non-Party workers... the management of their industries," making "the Party superfluous," and with the Workers' Opposition's proposed "All-Russia Congress of Producers," which he judged a "radical departure from Marxism" because it erased the vanguard's distinct organizing role by conflating proletarians with small commodity producers. He stated flatly: "you cannot lead the proletariat without a Party," and described the state itself as "a sphere of coercion" for which the Party functions as "the leader, the vanguard of the proletariat, which rules directly."

## Kronstadt and the closing of opposition (1921, via Avrich)

At the Tenth Congress concurrent with Kronstadt, Lenin pushed an end to opposition, framed factions as exploitable by counter-revolution, and secured Party Unity dissolving factions (secret expulsion clause). Avrich: Kronstadt used as "cudgel"; ~¼ membership purged by summer 1921. Rebel slogan "All power to the soviets but not the parties" opposed vanguard monopoly. See [[kronstadt-debate]], [[avrich-kronstadt-1921]].

## Service 2000 on institutionalisation

Robert Service, in *[[service-lenin-2000|Lenin: A Biography]]* (2000), **controverts** the historiographic claim that the party as supreme state organ was a deeply laid scheme already fully specified in *[[lenin-what-is-to-be-done-1902|What Is to Be Done?]]* (1902). On Service’s reading of archives, letters, and memoirs, there is no evidence of a planned, specific institutional form in which the Bolshevik party would become the supreme organ of the Soviet state. Service argues that Lenin did found a one-party, mono-ideological state, but that the **sketches remained vague**: basic assumptions (leadership, infallible policies, [[vanguard-party|vanguard]]) were **elaborated under the pressures of 1918–19**, not already a 1902 prescription for party supremacy over Sovnarkom and the soviets. Service reports that by January 1919 the Central Committee set up the Politburo and Orgburo, which (with the CC) took highest state affairs with decisions mandatory for Sovnarkom, the Council of Labour and Defence, and commissariats — party bodies as supreme state agencies in practice. Service’s broader claim: centralised one-party rule grew more from off-the-cuff measures under long-held assumptions than from a grandiose organisational blueprint (see also his reading of WITBD as a “hymn to leadership” rather than a permanent technical manual).

## Carr 1979 on monopoly of power

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 1, 4, 12) traces one-party monopoly from Constituent Assembly dissolution (Jan 1918) through tenth-congress faction ban ("On the Unity of the Party"; secret CC expulsion clause) to the defeat of the [[left-opposition]] and 1928 orthodoxy. He quotes Lenin acclaiming "the dictatorship of the party" in the civil war: "the dictatorship of the working class is carried into effect by the party." Bolshevization of [[comintern]] sections extended the model abroad. Complements Service's improvisation thesis and Mawdsley–White elite structure with a continuous 1917–29 narrative.
