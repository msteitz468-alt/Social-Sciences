# Moral Sense (Darwin)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|The Descent of Man]]* (1871), [[darwin-charles|Darwin]] treated the **moral sense** or **conscience** as the most important difference between man and other animals, approached from natural history rather than pure philosophy. His **fundamental proposition**: any animal with well-marked social instincts would inevitably acquire a moral sense once its intellect approached the human level.

A **moral being**, in Darwin’s summary definition, is “one who is capable of comparing his past and future actions and motives, — of approving of some and disapproving of others.” Conscience arises when enduring social instincts (love, sympathy, community-limited aid) conflict with temporary desires; after the passion passes, vivid memory produces dissatisfaction — the animal (if reflective) “ought” to have obeyed the stronger enduring instinct. Language makes public opinion a guide; praise and blame rest on sympathy.

Ultimate multi-factor formula (ch. V): moral sense is “a highly complex sentiment, having its first origin in the social instincts, largely guided by the approbation of our fellow-men, ruled by reason, self-interest, and in later times by deep religious feelings, confirmed by instruction and habit.”

## Semantic History

| Phase | Sense |
|---|---|
| British moral-sense philosophy | Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume, Smith — conscience as sentiment |
| Darwin 1871 | Naturalized: social instincts + intellect + reputation + habit; tribe-level selection |
| Mill / Bain (engaged in notes) | Alternative associationist/utilitarian accounts — Darwin defends more innate/instinctive base |
| Later evolutionary ethics | Group selection, reciprocal altruism, cultural evolution reframe the same problem space |

## Operationalizations

Darwin did not measure the moral sense. He reconstructed it by:

1. Comparative animal sociality (sympathy, warning, mutual defence).
2. The within-tribe **altruism paradox**: self-sacrificers leave fewer offspring, so pure individual NS within the tribe can scarcely raise the standard.
3. **Tribe-level selection**: tribes richer in patriotism, fidelity, obedience, courage, and sympathy conquer others — “and this would be natural selection.”
4. Within-tribe aids: reciprocity habit (possibly inherited), love of praise / dread of blame.
5. Expansion of sympathy from tribe to nation to “all nations and races” as the highest stage (attributed progressive ideal).

## Applications

Central to *Descent* Part I (esp. ch. III and V) and the ch. XXI summary. Interfaces with [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]] (shared social-emotional base across races) and with Darwin’s reading of Tylor, Lubbock, and McLennan on “savage” custom. Distinct from Durkheim’s later [[collective-conscience]] (social fact, not evolved instinct) though both naturalize morality.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Relies on inheritance of acquired habit (Lamarckian residue).
- Victorian civilised/savage ranking frames the “expansion of sympathy” narrative — attributed period structure.
- Ch. V’s adjacent discussion of civilised checks on elimination of the weak was later misread as eugenic mandate; Darwin refused to abandon sympathy as policy.
- Modern evolutionary psychology and cultural evolution supply alternative mechanisms; treat 1871 as historical theory, not current consensus.
