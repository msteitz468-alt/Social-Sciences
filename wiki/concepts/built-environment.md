# Built Environment

## Definition as Coined

"Built environment" is a term from human geography for the humanly constructed physical and spatial fabric of social life. [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, "Refiguring the 'Social'," *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* ch. 10) adopts it as an **epistemic metaphor** for social ontology, arguing it improves on the "social construction" metaphor because it "emphasizes both the constructed quality of the entire social world and the importance of material instantiation," not just a single constructed feature (gender, race, nation).

In Sewell's ontology the social is constituted, simultaneously and dialectically, by an evolving web of **[[semiotic-practice|semiotic practices]]** and by a built environment that "provide[s] matrices for these practices and constrain[s] their consequences." He ties the pair to two aspects of human nature — *homo sapiens* and *homo faber* — as phylogenetically intertwined: symbol use and tool-making/environment-transformation co-evolved.

## Semantic History

Sewell reaches the concept by critique of the "social construction" metaphor (coined by Berger and Luckmann, 1966), whose sharp post-1970s rise he documents. He credits it for being inherently **diachronic** — implying temporally extended building and enduring results — where Saussurean/paradigmatic linguistic explanation is synchronic and can only model formal, reversible transformations. But "social construction" typically foregrounds one constructed category at a time; "built environment" foregrounds the durable, material, spatial instantiation of the whole social world.

## Operationalizations

The key claim is a **dialectic**: "semiotically generated practices necessarily give rise to social mediations based on the built environment. Semiotic practices are carried out by embodied persons in and by means of physical media … these media … constrain, thereby acting back upon the codes that exploit them." Analytically, one traces how discursive/semiotic processes give rise to structures governed by "built-environment logics (logics of spatial fixing, material instantiation, accretion, and duration)," which then condition subsequent semiotic processes.

## Applications

Sewell's illustrations (attributed analyses): [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s Hawaii, where acts of reference "burden" cultural categories with worldly instantiations that constrain them in turn; and American racial segregation, where the semiotic stigmatization of skin color produced housing discrimination whose durable built-environment effect (segregated neighborhoods) autonomously worsened outcomes as jobs suburbanized (citing William Julius Wilson; Massey and Denton). The concept also bears on archaeology and urban sociology, where material/spatial matrices are primary evidence.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is offered as a metaphor to hold the material and semiotic in dialectic, against both reductive materialism and pure discursivism; its precision as an analytic (versus a programmatic image) is left to be worked out in application. Recorded here as Sewell's attributed proposal.
