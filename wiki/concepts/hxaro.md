# Hxaro (Ju/'hoan delayed exchange partnership)

## Definition as Coined

*(Emic; analytic framing by ethnographers of the [[juhoansi|Ju/'hoansi / !Kung]], especially [[lee-richard-b|Lee]] 1979 and Polly Wiessner’s dedicated analyses.)* **Hxaro** is the !Kung term for their institution of **long-distance exchange networks**. Goods travel for hundreds of kilometers across the Kalahari from one hxaro partner to another. Individuals have dozens of partners, many seen less than once a year. The structural peculiarity Lee stressed is the **inordinate length of time** separating the two parts of a transaction: A gives B an item (spear, arrow, eggshell necklace, etc.); they part; months or years later B gives A a return item.

Hxaro is a traditional San exchange form, especially important for disseminating black and European manufactures into the Kalahari interior. Lee points to Wiessner (1977) as the detailed study. Distinct from everyday **[[food-sharing]]** (immediate generalized reciprocity within the camp) and from market trade.

## Semantic History

Became a classic case of forager exchange networks in economic anthropology; often contrasted with **immediate-return** food sharing (Sahlins’s generalized reciprocity within the band). In Lee’s *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* (1979), hxaro appears:

1. as a **public-life** activity drawing multi-group assemblies (with trance curing, choma initiation, marriage brokering);
2. as the network through which **arrows** circulate, blurring meat-ownership credit after a kill (Ch. 8);
3. as part of the regional system of reciprocal access that makes flexible use of [[nore|n!ores]] workable.

Later economic anthropology (especially Wiessner) densified partner inventories, gift-type flows, and risk-pooling functions; revisionist Kalahari historiography also mobilized long-distance exchange as evidence against “isolated forager” models.

*(Attributed — [[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]] 1989.)* Wilmsen writes **haro** (and related wage/purchase lexemes *xwua*, *tcao*, *xama*) and argues compensated labor is indigenous, not a late-20th-c. invention; employment contracts are negotiated compensation rather than haro reciprocity. He places haro inside stratified tenure and class relations at CaeCae, not as proof of classless isolation — see [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]], [[kalahari-debate]].

## Operationalizations

**Lee’s definitional features (Ch. 12):**

- Delayed dyadic exchange (not simultaneous barter).
- Non-food valuables as primary medium (arrows, ornaments, tools, clothing, trade goods)—food follows different rules ([[food-sharing]]).
- Network span: hundreds of km; many partners rarely co-present.
- Role in large reunions: especially important at multi-group gatherings when people from distant n!ores meet.

**Arrow ownership and meat distribution (Ch. 8).** Lee reports the !Kung rule: **“the owner of the arrow is the owner of the meat,”** even if another man shot it (first arrow to hit takes credit if multiple). Ownership means primarily the right to **distribute** meat formally. Men deliberately blur credits by circulating arrows in hxaro: “Give me an arrow and if I kill something with it I will give the meat to you.” Lee’s 1964 quiver inventory of four Dobe men showed wide sharing (two men had no arrows of their own; donors included wife’s brother, wife’s father, etc.). Women may own arrows, trade them, and become meat owners. Lee endorses Lorna Marshall’s comment that the society seems to want to “extinguish in every way possible the concept of the meat belonging to the hunter” (Marshall 1976:297, cited in Lee 1979).

**Public-life calendar.** Lee (following Mauss on Eskimo concentration/dispersion) estimates traditional ~3 months public life / ~9 months private life. Hxaro is a major pull into concentration alongside trance curing and choma. Informant /Tilkai on /Xai/xai as pre-Bantu meeting place: people came from !Kangwa, !Goshe, /Gam, /Gausha for hxaro, n!o fruit, mongongo, choma; “the main reason to meet was hxaro trade.”

**Related material culture of calibrated sharing (mongongo, Ch. 7).** Lee describes the small */tau* bag request system—requester hands rolled own bag so the collector fills a specified amount—avoiding embarrassment over whether 10%, 20%, or 50% of a load is expected. This is camp-level food sharing design, not hxaro proper, but belongs to the same ideological system of managing reciprocity without open haggling.

## Applications

- **Risk pooling and mobility:** networks support visiting rights and access to distant [[nore|n!ores]] when local resources fail—ecological complement to vague n!ore boundaries under extreme rainfall variability.
- **[[leveling-mechanisms]]:** circulating arrows and meat-distribution rights separate hunting skill from exclusive control of prestige meat; formal distribution brings prestige *and* risk of stinginess accusations, so diffusion of ownership is a social blessing as well as an economic one (Lee 1979, Ch. 8).
- **[[egalitarian-society]]:** delayed gift obligations drain exclusive accumulation of non-food goods and spread glory (and hostility) after kills.
- **Contact economy:** hxaro as conduit for iron, pots, European goods into the interior without converting the foraging base into market production (until wage work and cattle posts reorganize incentives).
- **[[kalahari-debate]]:** hxaro networks mean Dobe people were never “isolated atoms”; how that regional integration is read (autonomous forager world-system vs. clientage under pastoral/state power) remains disputed.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Lee’s 1979 treatment is **definitional and functional**, not a full network study—defer quantitative partner counts, gift inventories, and longitudinal flows to Wiessner (and later work). Date-stamp claims to Dobe-area 1960s–70s fieldwork.
- Do not collapse hxaro into food sharing or market trade; the delayed non-food structure is the defining feature Lee names.
- Arrow-sharing in hxaro is a **meat-distribution** device; it does not eliminate differentials in hunting skill (Lee documents large success inequalities—e.g. 34% of hunters accounting for ~79% of kudu kills among interviewees).
- Revisionist readings may cast exchange as evidence of deep commercial entanglement; Lee presents hxaro as traditional San exchange that *also* moved introduced manufactures. Keep each position attributed.
