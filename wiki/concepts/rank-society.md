# Rank Society

## Definition as Coined

In [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* (1967), a **rank society** is one in which **positions of valued status are limited** so that not all of sufficient talent achieve them. It **may or may not be stratified**: a society may sharply limit prestige without affecting access of the whole membership to **basic resources**. Sumptuary marks (plumage, dentalium, paraphernalia) are usually **not wealth** in a universal-exchange sense; prestige and subsistence often sit in separate **spheres of exchange** (Bohannan).

**Stability (attributed):** rank society without stratification is relatively **stable** unless unbalanced by pressures from more highly organized societies — contrast with unstable [[stratified-society]].

## Semantic History

- **Fried vs Service [[chiefdom]]:** Service defines chiefdom by permanent redistributional center and office of chief; Fried’s rank is status scarcity that often *includes* redistribution and chiefs/big-men but does not require Service’s full stage architecture. Fried argues that in Service–Sahlins schemes the **chiefdom** carries the evolutionary load while **tribe** is largely secondary.
- **British “chiefship”:** [[chieftainship]] in Africanist APS usage is not the same object as Fried’s evolutionary rank type.
- **Archaeology:** ranked society / middle-range complexity often merges Fried and Service labels.

## Operationalizations

From EPS Ch. 4 (attributed):

| Dimension | Fried’s content |
|---|---|
| Economy | **Redistribution** paramount; chiefs rich for what they **dispense**, not hoard; NW Coast administrator vs individual owner (Drucker); Fiji paramount as redistributor (Sahlins) |
| Kinship | Ideology peaks; conical clan / seniority (Kirchhoff); residence opportunism; coresidence formulas may shift toward rank attraction |
| Politics | Institutions **embedded**; authority regularized; often weak sanctions; ritual leadership coincides with rank; little transferable priestly power alone |
| Law | Still **no law** (vs Pospisil on Kapauku); repair processes, ridicule, sorcery, big-man personal power |
| External | Feasts/potlatch as intercommunity system; dispute with Service’s military-only external polity; war intensity hypothesized to track population pressure on resources |
| Demography | Higher density and community size than egalitarian; ranking rare without domestication (secondary exceptions) |

## Applications

NW Coast, Polynesia (Tikopia, Fiji), Melanesia (Siuai, Choiseul), Mandari, Tallensi, Camayura (egalitarian organization with ranked chief functions), Kachin *mayu-dama* / gumsa materials, etc.

**Emergence (attributed):** rank often emerges **unnoticed**; primary causal hypotheses ecology/demography + redistribution; rank holders typically work harder and are expected to be generous — not managerial necessity of hunting-gathering.

### Kirch 1984 — Ancestral Polynesian Society as ranked baseline (hereditary *‘ariki*)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch’s reconstruction of **Ancestral Polynesian Society (APS)** supplies a ranked ancestral baseline from which contact-era chiefdoms differentiated — rank already present at colonization, not invented by population pressure or redistributive function after settlement.

**Hereditary *‘ariki*.** Cognates for chief in both Tongic and Nuclear Polynesian branches (Tongan *‘eiki*, Futunan *aliki*, Hawaiian *aliʻi*) warrant reconstruction of Proto-Polynesian *‘ariki* and a concept of chiefship in APS. Pawley’s semantic agreement (as Kirch cites it): “chief, head of a lineage; first born in the senior line, who succeeds to the chieftainship of the lineage and has strong personal *mana* and *tapu*”; *mana* and *tapu* likewise reconstructable. Lexical data, on Kirch’s reading, show APS had already developed **hereditary chieftainship**. Ancestral chiefship is hypothesized as more clearly sacerdotal (Williamson; Koskinen): concentration of sacred duties and secular rule; legitimation via direct male descent from gods and associated *mana*/*tapu*. Parallel reconstructable statuses: *toa* (warrior — achieved prowess, germ of *ariki*–*toa* conflict over *mana*); *fono* (political assembly of adult males) implying consensus decision-making within the *kainanga* alongside hereditary office; *kainanga* as land-holding conical-clan ramage; *kainga* as minimal household descent group with land.

**Conical-clan geometry.** APS centred on pyramidal conical clan (Kirchhoff; Sahlins) with segmentary ramages and ambilateral affiliation; rank by proximity to founding ancestor with critical senior/junior opposition. Population growth after settlement led to fission into ramages (Firth; Earle), each headed by its senior male and ranked relative to the senior ramage. Rank rests on seniority of descent, mirrored in the household as “little chiefdom,” yet with a qualitative disjunction between chief and people (sumptuary access, prescribed behaviour, distinctive ritual). Flexible succession (maternal links; usurpation) coexists with ideological bias to patrilineage and primogeniture — junior–senior opposition as a “structure of significance” underlying status rivalry (Goldman complementary to Sahlins). Direct archaeology of APS yields only dispersed multi-household hamlets without clear stratification markers; the ranked baseline is reconstructed primarily from linguistics and comparative ethnography cross-checked against archaeology. Contact stratified endpoints (Tonga, Hawaiʻi) are transformations *of* this ranked baseline, not transitions from egalitarian society.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundary with stratified society (when does prestige convert into resource privilege?).
- “Tribe” repudiation may underplay pan-tribal integration Service stressed.
- Overlap with chiefdom archaeology creates terminological muddle (see [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]]).

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): dynamic reading

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]].)* Flannery and Marcus keep Fried's category but read it **dynamically**: the analytic question is not *which type* a society is but *how* achieved renown gets converted into [[hereditary-rank|hereditary rank]], and how often that conversion is blocked or reversed (see [[chiefly-cycling]]). They also draw a terminological distinction their cases require — a **[[chiefdom]]** names a *territory* controlled by a chief, not a *type of society*; a society like [[tikopia|Tikopia]] "should simply be referred to as a rank society." Their intermediate category, the **[[achievement-based-society|achievement-based society]]**, sits between egalitarian foragers and rank societies.
