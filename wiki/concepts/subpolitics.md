# Subpolitics

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s term (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Ch. 8 "Opening up the Political") for the exercise of society-shaping decision-making *outside and below* the formal parliamentary–governmental system — in science, technology, business, medicine, and private life — where consequential choices about society's future are actually taken, "outside democratic consent and voting."

## Definition as Coined

Industrial society, Beck argued, split the citizen in two: a *politico-economic* system subject to democratic legitimation, and a *techno-economic* system treated as "non-politics" and legitimated by the equation "technical progress = social progress" — "**Progress replaces voting**." In risk society this demarcation collapses. Techno-economic development acquires enormous society-changing (and society-endangering) power without corresponding legitimation, becoming "a third entity" — **sub-politics** — defined by the paradox that "the scope of the social changes... varies inversely with their legitimation" (Beck 1986: 186, 204). The result: "**The political becomes non-political and the non-political political.**"

## Semantic History

Subpolitics is one pole of Beck's broader **"unbinding of politics"** (*Entgrenzung der Politik*): (a) established democratic rights generate participatory demands (citizens' initiatives, new social movements) that outrun the formal system, while (b) techno-economic decision-making loses its non-political innocence. Beck read the apparent "standoff" of formal politics as an artifact of "limiting the political to what is labeled political" — society is meanwhile undergoing "revolutionary" change "in the form of the non-political." The concept became central to Beck's later political sociology and to debates on governance, expertise, and democracy.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. Beck located concrete "centers of sub-politics" in constitutionally protected domains: an independent **judiciary** (where proliferating expert/counter-expert testimony opens decision leeway for judges); the **mass media** (whose circulation-driven "power to define problems" can overturn policy overnight — "People in the political administration read *Der Spiegel*, not investigation reports"); and the **private sphere** (reproductive and household decisions with major political-economic effects — "the personal is political"). His "extreme case study" is **medicine**, which uniquely fuses controlled research, training, and clinical application, achieving "directness without implementation" — arriving with its social consequences already in force, bypassing any "parliamentary purgatory."

## Applications

Subpolitics grounds Beck's three future scenarios (reindustrialization; democratization of techno-economic development; and his own preferred **"differential politics,"** which accepts that "modern society has no control center" and proposes protecting subpolitical influence plus institutionalized rights of self-criticism *within* professions: "Only when medicine opposes medicine, nuclear physics opposes nuclear physics... can the future that is being brewed up in the test-tube become intelligible and evaluable for the outside world"). It connects directly to [[reflexive-scientization]] (the demonopolization of expertise) and [[relations-of-definition]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics question whether "subpolitics" romanticizes the democratic potential of movements, media, and lay contestation, and whether it under-weights the capacity of states and corporations to recapture subpolitical arenas. As a diagnostic frame it is difficult to falsify, and its optimism about self-limitation ("differential politics") is among the more contested parts of Beck's program.
