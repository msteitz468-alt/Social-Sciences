# Ritual Pollution

## Definition as Coined

**Ritual pollution** (purity/impurity, defilement, uncleanness) names rules and beliefs about dangerous unclean contact. In *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*, [[douglas-mary|Douglas]] treats pollution as powers **inhering in the structure of ideas**, by which a classification and social structure is expected to protect itself. A polluting person is "always in the wrong" — wrong condition or crossed line; displacement unleashes danger. Unlike witchcraft/sorcery, bringing pollution is a capacity humans share with animals; it can be intentional but **intention is irrelevant to effect** (Ch. 6; attributed: Douglas 1966).

Douglas distinguishes two levels of pollution ideas (2002 restatement of the book's program):

| Level | Function (Douglas) |
|---|---|
| **Instrumental** | Coerce good citizenship; back social rules with threat of automatic harm |
| **Expressive** | Body, sex, and contact symbolize the social order and its dangers |

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Robertson Smith / Frazer stream | Uncleanness as survivals; often confused with holiness as "primitive" mark |
| Early 20th-c. anthropology | Contagion separated from ethics → "magic" vs religion |
| Douglas 1966 | Pollution as systemic classification + social structure; reunites purity with comparative religion |
| Douglas 2002 / later biblical work | Revises application to Leviticus; pollution theory itself retained |
| Risk literature | Pollution fear as selective political accusation (*Risk and Culture*, 1982) |

Douglas argued (Ch. 1) against the evolutionary story that "advanced" religions distinguish holy from unclean while "primitive" religions confuse them: Hindu pollution idiom shows fine relative algebra (Havik Brahmins: cow-dung pure relative to mortals yet impure for a god). "So far from there being confusion between the idea of holiness and uncleanness, here there is nothing but distinction of the most hair-splitting finesse."

## Operationalizations

- Catalog contact, food, sex, death, and bodily-margin rules; map who endangers whom.
- Relate pollution rules to the moral code **without equating them**: Douglas argues (Ch. 8) the relation is patterned, not arbitrary — pollution can act as post-hoc detector, conflict simplifier, marshal of lagging disapproval, or deterrent when practical sanctions fail.
- Read body-boundary ritual as social-boundary symbolism (Ch. 7): body as model of any bounded system.
- Reject **medical materialism** as sole explanation (Ch. 2): hygiene side-benefits do not exhaust ritual meaning; nor is "our" dirt non-symbolic.

## Applications

- Caste purity and food (Havik; Coorgs; cooked vs uncooked food as pollution conductors).
- Levitical uncleanness (1966 anomaly reading **and** Douglas's later covenant/body–altar revision — keep both dated).
- Witchcraft at interstitial roles vs pollution as structural power (Ch. 6).
- Sexual pollution under contradictory sex-role systems (Ch. 9).
- Creative use of the unclean in framed ritual (Ch. 10): "pollution symbols are as necessary as the use of black in any depiction."

## Critiques and Limitations

- Over-systematizing local practice into one "total structure."
- Period language and residual "primitive/modern" contrast in Ch. 5 even while dismantling other evolutionary frames.
- Leviticus application revised by Douglas herself (2002 preface; *Leviticus as Literature* 1999) — do not cite 1966 Ch. 3 as her standing biblical view.
- Secondary-ethnography base: comparative illustrations, not equal primary depth for every case.
