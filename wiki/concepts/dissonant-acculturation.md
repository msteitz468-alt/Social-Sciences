# Dissonant Acculturation

Intergenerational gap in which children's learning of English and American ways — and loss of immigrant culture — **outstrips** their parents'. In *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*, Portes & Rumbaut treat this as the pattern that produces **role reversal** and raises risk of downward assimilation without guaranteeing it.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001.)* Dissonant acculturation: children's English and host-culture learning plus loss of home culture proceed faster than parents'. Key family decisions may depend on children's cultural brokerage; parental authority is undermined, especially when parents lack other means to navigate the host society.

## Semantic History

Contrasted with [[consonant-acculturation]] (shared pace) and [[selective-acculturation]] (community-buffered retention). Updates early-20th-century role-reversal stories by arguing today's opportunity structure makes premature freedom more dangerous than in the industrial-expansion era.

## Operationalizations

Parent-child language gaps; family conflict/cohesion scales; embarrassment about parents; role-reversal indicators in CILS (see ch. 8).

## Applications

Mechanism linking weak parental resources / isolated families to school and identity risks in the [[segmented-assimilation]] model.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk language can pathologize ordinary youth autonomy; measurement depends on self-report of conflict; not all dissonant cases "fail."
