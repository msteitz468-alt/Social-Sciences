# City-State

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003), [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] argues that among early civilizations only **two** basic political forms are found: **city-states** and [[territorial-state|territorial states]]. They are **stable alternatives, not evolutionary stages**. City-states (in his comparative usage) tend toward smaller scale, higher urban density with farmers often residing in urban centres, more [[heterarchy|heterarchical]] elements, broader market access and independent merchants, and kingship under closer urban observation (divinity claims often weaker than in remote territorial courts). All Trigger-specific claims **attributed**.

## Semantic History

"City-state" has classical (polis) and Near Eastern (Sumerian city-kingdom) roots; modern comparative archaeology and political anthropology extend it to Yoruba, Maya, and other cases. Distinct from modern nation-state usage. Trigger’s contribution is systematizing the type as half of a binary that organizes urban form, administration, and exchange across seven cases — not inventing the term.

## Operationalizations

Trigger’s seven-case typology (study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]; comparison: [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]). City-state systems in his sample include Mesopotamia, Classic Maya, Valley of Mexico (with Aztec tributary hegemony layered on city-states — not a territorial state), and Yoruba.

| Dimension | City-state pattern (Trigger) |
|---|---|
| Scale | Smaller polities / systems of peer polities |
| Settlement | Higher urban density; farmers often in cities |
| Administration | More heterarchical elements |
| Exchange | Broader markets; independent merchants more visible |
| Law | Detailed codes more associated with hegemonic city-state systems |
| Slavery | More prominent than in some territorial cases (comparative note) |

## Applications

Comparative early-civilization political typology; links [[state-formation]], [[urbanization]], [[heterarchy]], and case pages ([[aztec-empire]], [[classic-maya]], [[yoruba]], Mesopotamian urbanism).

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary typology may under-differentiate federated and intermediate forms; Aztec “empire” as tributary overlay shows hybridity Trigger himself records. Case assignment and ethnohistoric projection are contested in specialist literatures after 2001.

### Yoffee 2005 (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths]]*) makes the **city-state** central against neo-evolutionist large-territorial despots: most earliest states were relatively small peer polities embedded in larger **civilizations** (shared symbols/identity), not congruent state=civilization packages. Cities were transformative arenas of differentiation and struggle, not mere apex nodes of settlement hierarchies. Ideal-type checklists (full independence, autarky, ethnic homogeneity) fail for many cases; Egypt is the clearest early **non**-city-state territorial pattern; Teotihuacan is an oversized exception still treated as a kind of city-state. Mesopotamia: city-states within Mesopotamian civilization, rarely lastingly unified until late. Complements Trigger’s city-state / [[territorial-state]] dichotomy ([[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]) from a Mesopotamianist evolutionary critique rather than seven-case institutional comparison. Debate: [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) supplies a **Uruk peer-polity** layer on the city-state form: earliest Mesopotamian states as small grain cores competing for manpower, not embryonic territorial empires. All claims **attributed**. Links: [[pristine-state]], [[urbanization]], [[wealth-appropriation]], [[grain-hypothesis]].

**Uruk as pioneer peer polity.** By Scott’s criteria the state of Uruk is firmly in place by **3200 BCE**. Uruk pioneered a form replicated by roughly **twenty** other competing city-states or “peer polities” on the alluvium — small enough that one could walk center-to-boundary in a day. Southern Mesopotamia hosted several related state-making experiments ~3300–2350 BCE (Kish, Ur, especially Uruk), likened to China’s Warring States or later Greek city-states: priests, strong men, and local chiefs scaled kinship idioms; thousands of cultivators, artisans, traders, and laborers were “repurposed as subjects” — counted, taxed, conscripted, subordinated.

**Scale and walls.** Uruk estimates 25,000–50,000 inhabitants; population tripling over ~200 years unlikely from natural growth alone; non-Sumerian place-names (Ur, Uruk, Eridu) suggest in-migration; bas-reliefs of prisoners in neck shackles suggest capture as population augmentation; walls enclosed ~250 ha (twice classical Athens). By mid-third millennium most alluvium towns were walled — labor-intensive carapace and proxy for permanent cultivation and storage. Iconic founding act of a Sumerian polity: building a city wall (Uruk wall ~3300–3000 BCE).

**Peer-polity war as manpower politics.** Warfare among southern alluvium peer polities was abundant; given the preciousness of population, wars were “probably more destructive than bloody.” One account claims the population lived at subsistence except when victorious armies returned with loot and tribute; the early state was more often an added threat to subsistence than its benefactor. Following Seth Richardson, Scott holds the vast majority of alluvium wars were **petty hinterland wars** to conquer smaller independent communities for labor — “herd non-state clients into state orders” — not wars among large urban polities. Prize of war was more often captives than territory. States constantly lose populations to nonstate units and must coerce replacements.

**City-state vs territorial ambition.** Scott contrasts Sumerian peer city-states (day-walk radius, tribute from hinterland) with later **Qin** ambition for a true centralized [[territorial-state]] via unified script, measures, cadastre, and mobility controls — not a strong city-state content with occasional tribute from quasi-independent satellites. Superregimented Ur III lasted barely a century; ambitious political landscaping may undo ambitious states.

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992): the Sumerian city-state specifically

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] gives a narrative-historical definition
converging with, but independently derived from, the comparative typologies above: a
Sumerian/Akkadian **city-state** = a city + suburbs + satellite towns/villages + a
defined territory of gardens, palm-groves, and barley/wheat fields, with open steppe
between irrigated zones used as pasture. For the Early Dynastic period, Roux's sources
list no more than **eighteen major cities** across Sumer and Akkad, north to south:
Sippar, Kish, Akshak, Larak, Isin, Nippur, Adab, Zabalam, Shuruppak, Umma, Girsu,
Lagash, Nina, Badtibira, Uruk, Larsa, Ur, Eridu — plus many unlocated towns/villages.

Each city had multiple districts, each with its own local god and temple, and the city
as a whole had a "national" patron deity. Ruler titles varied: ***en*** ("lord,"
combining secular and religious roles, used at [[uruk]] and persisting as a title into
the mid-19th century BCE), ***ensi*** ("governor," PA.TE.SI, etymology uncertain), and
***lugal*** ("king," literally "great man") — Roux notes it is unclear why some rulers
used *ensi* versus *lugal*, and that in some cases a *lugal* appears to have ruled over
several city-states with subordinate *ensi*s as vassals. The ruler's wife held the
title ***nin*** ("lady"/"queen") and could play a significant public role — at Girsu,
for instance, managing the Baba-temple's affairs. Excavated Early Dynastic palaces
(Eridu, [[kish]], and two superimposed palaces at [[mari]]) share a common
architectural plan: a square central courtyard, chambers on three sides, and a long
rectangular audience hall on the fourth, surrounded by twin walls with a corridor
between — evidence of a shared institutional template across otherwise independent
city-states, alongside the **temple/palace duality** documented in detail at
[[temple-economy]].

The **Lagash–Umma border war over the Gu-edin field** is, per Roux, the
best-documented Early Dynastic conflict between Sumerian city-states: Mesalim of Kish
first arbitrated the dispute and set a boundary stele (see [[kish]]); Eannatum of
Lagash later defeated Umma and restored the boundary by force (commemorated on the
fragmentary Stele of the Vultures); his nephew Entemena renewed the war a generation
later, digging a permanent boundary ditch. Full narrative detail is filed on
[[lagash]] (not owned by this integration pass). Roux also records, without resolving,
Kish's and Nippur's distinctive supra-city-state symbolic roles — "King of Kish" as a
title uniting Sumerian and Semitic populations, and Nippur as Sumer's religious
capital contested but never ruled outright — see [[kish]] and [[nippur]].

### Postgate (*Early Mesopotamia*, 1994): city, territory, and civic institutions

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] adds a society/economy layer to the
Sumerian city-state form, emphasizing city–countryside symbiosis over the city as a
walled monument alone. City size varied enormously even within one period: Early
Dynastic [[uruk]]'s wall ran roughly 9 km, enclosing about 400 hectares, while Abu
Salabikh's Main Mound wall ran roughly 1.3 km, enclosing only about 10 hectares — "no
more than a village in many societies," yet both count as cities on functional and
political grounds. The wall itself, alongside the temple, functioned as an expression
of collective political identity; conquerors' inscriptions boast of building walls, and
a defeated city's fortifications are recorded as deliberately "demolished." Cities
frequently outgrew their original walled circuit, solved either by building unwalled
suburbs outside the old walls (Old Babylonian Sippar's suburb Sippar-Amnanum, apparently
originating as an Amorite-tribe encampment, seemingly never itself walled) or by
abandoning the old wall line for a much larger enclosure (post-Ur III Eshnunna).
Postgate cautions against imagining the "average" city as uniformly built up within its
walls — at Uruk, residential quarters apparently never filled the huge walled
precinct, reflected (he suggests) in Uruk's epithet "the sheep-fold"; the Gilgamesh
epic describes the Uruk wall enclosing date plantations and brick-pits alongside houses
and shrines (attributed).

**City and countryside.** The city's rural territory was organized around the village
(*ālum*) and the urban ward (*bābtum*), each with a degree of self-government under
elders and a "mayor" (*rabiānum*) linking to central authority — see [[mesopotamian-law]]
for their judicial role. Postgate argues there was structural tension between
municipal-government wards (spatially/administratively defined) and kin-based social
divisions, and that archaeologically detectable topographic divisions likely correspond
more closely to administrative wards than to kin groups; the *bābtum* is argued *not*
to have originated as merely the residence of a single extended family, since both the
social group and the physical enclosure it names would need to be considerably larger
(attributed).

**The Assembly and "primitive democracy."** Following Thorkild Jacobsen's
"primitive democracy" thesis — endorsed by Postgate as retaining conviction — the city
Assembly (Sumerian *ukkin*, Akkadian *puhrum*) is read as the Early Dynastic forum for
popular political and military decision-making, modeled on the depiction of the divine
Assembly reaching consensus in later myth; the Sumerian epic "Gilgamesh and Akka"
portrays Early Dynastic Uruk's decisions as reached through the advice of both elders
and young men. This dual elders/young-men structure persisted at least into Old
Babylonian times as "the city and the elders," but by then functioned only as a
law-court — political power had shifted to the palace — with Assur as the one
exception where the Assembly (via its elders) still took an active political role, not
merely a legal one (attributed; see [[mesopotamian-law]] for the Assembly's judicial
role in the Nippur homicide trial).

**Jacobsen's "league of city-states" (attributed, named theoretical model).** Jacobsen
(1957) interpreted a set of Early Dynastic I "city seals" from Ur — composite symbols
(altar plus city-god emblem) for multiple cities on a single sealing — as evidence of
inter-city cooperation, "used for sealing deliveries from a common fund of goods,
created for a common purpose by individual contributions from the cities collectively
sealing." Jacobsen built on this a broader theory of Early Dynastic Sumer as a "league
of city-states," collaborating economically and, to some extent, politically —
recorded here as Jacobsen's named theoretical model (endorsed approvingly by Postgate),
not as settled wiki-voice fact.

**The market-existence question (position, unresolved).** Whether Mesopotamian cities
had "markets" in the commercial-institution sense has been debated since Polanyi's
writings in the 1950s. No archaeological marketplace has been identified — Postgate
notes this would be hard to identify archaeologically in any case. Akkadian/Sumerian
terms for open spaces exist (*rebītum*/*sila dagala*, "wide street"), but no textual
evidence ties these spaces to commerce; assembly meetings may instead have occurred in
the main temple's courtyard. The city "gate" is better evidenced as a public space than
any open square. Evidence for retail or commercial streets (e.g., a "street of the
cloth-merchants") is absent, and the existence of that kind of retail trade "remains
controversial." The *kārum* ("quay") began as a harbor unloading point but by Old
Babylonian times had become an institutionalized merchants' guild or society; there is
no proof its office (*bīt kārim*) formed part of a demarcated "commercial quarter."
Postgate records both the Polanyi-adjacent skepticism and the possibility of informal
market activity as open positions, not a resolved question.
