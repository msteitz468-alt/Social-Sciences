# Lifeworld

## Definition as Coined

The **lifeworld** (*Lebenswelt*) is the taken-for-granted, pre-reflexive background horizon within which communicative action moves. In [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s communicative reworking (attributed) — in [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|TCA Vol. 2]] — it "is, so to speak, the transcendental site where speaker and hearer meet, where they can reciprocally raise claims that their utterances fit the world." Unlike facts, norms, or experiences, the lifeworld cannot itself be thematized as "something in the lifeworld"; a communicative situation is only "a segment of lifeworld contexts of relevance thrown into relief by a theme," with a movable horizon.

## Semantic History

The term is **Husserl**'s (phenomenology); Alfred Schütz and Thomas Luckmann developed a phenomenological sociology of it (*The Structures of the Life-World*). Habermas's decisive move is to **shift the lifeworld from the philosophy of consciousness to a communication-theoretic footing**: he criticizes Schütz and Luckmann for keeping "the experiencing subject" as "the court of last appeal," and grounds the lifeworld instead in linguistically generated intersubjectivity. He distinguishes his account from three one-sided reductions: Parsons reduces the lifeworld to social integration alone ("societal community"); the symbolic-interactionist tradition (Blumer et al.) reduces it to socialization/role-taking; and the phenomenological tradition, consistently followed to its end in [[social-construction-of-reality|Berger and Luckmann]], falls into what Habermas (crediting Albrecht Wellmer) calls **"hermeneutic idealism."**

## Operationalizations

Habermas analyzes the lifeworld through **three structural components** and their matching **reproduction processes** (attributed):

| Component | Reproduction process | Resource | Pathology when disturbed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Culture (stock of knowledge) | cultural reproduction | meaning | loss of meaning |
| Society (legitimate orders) | social integration | solidarity | anomie |
| Personality (competences) | socialization | ego strength | psychopathology / alienation |

Communicative action reproduces all three simultaneously. A sociology that dissolves society *entirely* into lifeworld relies, Habermas argues, on three fictions: the autonomy of actors, the independence of culture from external constraints, and the transparency of communication — which is why the lifeworld concept must be paired with the **[[system-and-lifeworld|system]]** concept.

## Applications

The lifeworld is one pole of Habermas's two-level theory of society (see [[system-and-lifeworld]]); its rationalization is the [[linguistification-of-the-sacred]]; its endangerment by system imperatives is the [[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]. (Distinct from the pre-discursive "reality" sense on [[things-before-discourse]].)

**In medical anthropology** ([[good-byron|Byron Good]],
*[[good-medicine-rationality-1994|Medicine, Rationality, and Experience]]*, 1994, attributed):
Good works the Schutzian, not Habermasian, lifeworld — proposing (after Elaine Scarry's pain as
"unmaking the world") that serious chronic illness systematically **unmakes the lifeworld**
across Schutz's six dimensions of common-sense reality (form of self-experience, sociality,
time perspective, consciousness, intentional projects, suspension of doubt about mortality),
illustrated by the "Brian" chronic-pain case (Boston, TMJ). He also applies Habermas's
colonization of the lifeworld to the hospital: routine technical procedures restore order after
moral/existential ruptures (an AIDS patient's psychotic crisis retold at rounds as "an acute
psychotic break and we just took care of that"), instantiating instrumental rationality at
clinical medicine's core.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Idealism vs. materialism** (attributed): Habermas's own worry — a purely culturalist lifeworld concept "blends out" material reproduction; hence the system concept.
- **Reification of the boundary** (attributed to critics): treating economy and state as norm-free "system" over against a communicative "lifeworld" is charged with naturalizing power (see [[system-and-lifeworld]], [[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]).
