## Definition as Coined

Labov defined the sociolinguistic variable as "by definition a feature subject to social evaluation, hence requiring data from the speech community for its description... making explanation possible through concomitant variation between the linguistic and social data." The definition is explicitly an empirical assertion of covariation, not a purely descriptive linguistic category: a feature counts as a sociolinguistic variable only insofar as its variants are shown to correlate with social factors.

In *Language in the Inner City* (1972), Labov extended the construct from class/style-stratified New York City phonology to the grammar and sound system of [[black-english-vernacular|Black English Vernacular (BEV)]]. There he insisted that variables be reported under the **principle of accountability** — each variant as a proportion of the environments in which it *could* have occurred — and formalized many of them as [[variable-rule|variable rules]] with probabilistic application, not as free variation or categorical dialect mixture.

## Semantic History

Within Labov's own 1965/1972 analysis, the concept was further differentiated into three types along a scale of social awareness: an **indicator** (a variable correlated with group membership alone, showing no stylistic variation across speech contexts), a **marker** (a variable that has become a community-wide norm, showing both social and stylistic stratification), and a **stereotype** (a variable that, under extreme stigmatization, becomes an overt object of social comment, potentially divorced from actual usage patterns). Labov's own examples move variables through this progression as part of his thirteen-stage mechanism of sound change (see [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]).

Blom and Gumperz, in the same 1972 *Directions in Sociolinguistics* volume, applied the underlying logic outside Labov's own dialect studies: they described Ranamål/Bokmål variation in Hemnesberget, Norway, as "a single linguistic system, differentiated by features similar to what Labov... calls variables" — phonological and morphophonemic variables along a scale — rather than as two categorically distinct grammatical codes, extending the construct from a single-community class/style analysis to a bidialectal contact setting (see [[code-switching]]).

In *Language in the Inner City*, the construct is pushed further into **grammatical** variables (copula contraction/deletion, 3sg -s, possessive -’s, negative concord, t,d as past vs monomorphemic) and into **membership-linked** social dimensions (peer-group core vs peripheral vs “lames”), not only SES and interview style. Labov also treated constraint *weights* themselves as diagnostic: the same t,d deletion rule is shared across dialects, but BEV peer groups vs white nonstandard speakers differ in whether phonological or grammatical constraints dominate in vernacular style.

## Operationalizations

**Founding NYC / Vineyard operationalization.** Labov operationalized the variable through sociolinguistic interviews eliciting four contextual styles (word-list reading as most self-conscious, casual/danger-of-death narrative as least self-conscious), quantitative indexing of phonetic variants across a stratified socioeconomic sample, and comparison against historical Linguistic Atlas records (Kurath 1941) and earlier scholarship (Babbitt 1896, Gauchat 1905, Hermann 1929). On Martha's Vineyard he used "apparent time" comparison across four age cohorts (centralization index 0.22 → 0.37 → 0.44 → 0.88 for the (aw) variable). In New York City he combined stylistic-stratification diagrams with a subjective-reaction test in which listeners rated recorded speakers' job suitability without realizing they were rating the same speakers twice.

**BEV / Harlem operationalization (*Language in the Inner City*).** Variables were measured in long-term peer-group sessions (multi-track lavaliere + central mic), individual interviews, adult interviews with channel-cue isolation of casual speech, and formal tests (repetition, subjective reaction, vernacular and classroom correction) under Office of Education projects 3091 and 3288 ([[labov-language-inner-city-study]], [[south-central-harlem-youth]]). Style continuum for (r): A vernacular/casual; B careful interview; C reading; D word lists. Key BEV variables quantified in the volume include:

| Variable | What is counted | Social/linguistic conditioning Labov emphasized |
|---|---|---|
| (r), (r##V), (VrV) | Consonantal vs vocalized/deleted r | All groups r-less in vernacular; formal styles raise (r); black groups vocalize pre-vocalic and sometimes intervocalic r unlike Inwood whites |
| t,d cluster simplification | Simplified vs retained final clusters | Shared rule; BEV vs WNS by relative phonological vs grammatical constraint weights; monomorphemic before C most simplified, past before V least |
| s,z clusters | Loss of first or second elements | Labov: much more characteristic of black than white speakers in his tables |
| Copula *is/are* | Full / contracted / deleted | Inherent variability for all speakers studied; following __NP < __PA-Loc < __Ving < __gon; preceding Pro favors C/D; ordered contraction then deletion as [[variable-rule]] |
| 3sg -s, possessive -’s | Presence vs zero | Inferred grammatical absence in core vernacular (prevocalic zeros not phonology alone); plural -s largely intact |
| Negative concord | Within-clause rates | Often categorical for peer-group members; weakened among lames |

Cedergren–Sankoff product-model fits to copula tables treated φ as community-level rule probability with independent constraint weights (see [[variable-rule]]).

## Applications

Labov's Martha's Vineyard (aw)/(ay) centralization study and New York City (oh) and (r) studies are the concept's founding applications (see [[labov-william]] and [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]). Blom and Gumperz's Hemnesberget study applies the underlying logic of graded, socially-indexed linguistic variation to a bidialectal code-choice setting (see [[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]).

In *Language in the Inner City*, the same construct underwrites:

- Diagnosis of **reading-relevant** homonym sets and inflectional weakness for BEV-speaking students (Ch. 1).
- The argument that BEV and white nonstandard English can be fitted into a **single system** with SE via constraint adjustments rather than wholly separate grammars (Ch. 2–3).
- Differentiation of **members vs lames**: core Jets show higher *is*-deletion (φ_d ≈ .63–.70) than peripheral members and lames (≈ .33–.36); categorical BEV rules weaken to variable among lames (Ch. 7).
- Educational application of copula analysis: *He wild* ≡ SE *He’s wild*; teach contraction without automatic deletion.

## Critiques and Limitations

No critique of the construct itself is recorded in the Gumperz 1972 volume source. Labov explicitly positions it against the view, attributed to Bloomfield (1933), Hockett (1958), and Postal (1968), that sound change is either unobservable in principle or asocial and arbitrary in its direction.

From the *Language in the Inner City* program:

- Variables require **vernacular data**; school and formal-elicitation samples systematically shift rates — the [[observers-paradox|Observer’s Paradox]] is a validity threat for the construct, not a side issue.
- **Adult rates** in interviews may not equal adult peer-group vernacular; Labov withheld firm adult-grammar claims pending participant observation of adult groups.
- Treating surface zeros as single variables without environmental accounting (Loflin-type “no -ed / no copula” stereotypes) is exactly what the accountability principle was meant to block.
- Interactional sociolinguistics (Gumperz’s pole of the 1972 divide) treats social and linguistic categories as mutually constitutive rather than as independently measured systems correlated after the fact — a standing alternative framing of what “variable” indexes (see [[variationist-sociolinguistics]], Critiques).
