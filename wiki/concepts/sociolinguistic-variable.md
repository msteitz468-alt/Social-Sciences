## Definition as Coined

Labov defined the sociolinguistic variable as "by definition a feature subject to social evaluation, hence requiring data from the speech community for its description... making explanation possible through concomitant variation between the linguistic and social data." The definition is explicitly an empirical assertion of covariation, not a purely descriptive linguistic category: a feature counts as a sociolinguistic variable only insofar as its variants are shown to correlate with social factors.

## Semantic History

Within Labov's own 1965/1972 analysis, the concept was further differentiated into three types along a scale of social awareness: an **indicator** (a variable correlated with group membership alone, showing no stylistic variation across speech contexts), a **marker** (a variable that has become a community-wide norm, showing both social and stylistic stratification), and a **stereotype** (a variable that, under extreme stigmatization, becomes an overt object of social comment, potentially divorced from actual usage patterns). Labov's own examples move variables through this progression as part of his thirteen-stage mechanism of sound change (see [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]).

Blom and Gumperz, in the same 1972 volume, applied the underlying logic outside Labov's own dialect studies: they described Ranamål/Bokmål variation in Hemnesberget, Norway, as "a single linguistic system, differentiated by features similar to what Labov... calls variables" — phonological and morphophonemic variables along a scale — rather than as two categorically distinct grammatical codes, extending the construct from a single-community class/style analysis to a bidialectal contact setting (see [[code-switching]]).

## Operationalizations

Labov operationalized the variable through sociolinguistic interviews eliciting four contextual styles (word-list reading as most self-conscious, casual/danger-of-death narrative as least self-conscious), quantitative indexing of phonetic variants across a stratified socioeconomic sample, and comparison against historical Linguistic Atlas records (Kurath 1941) and earlier scholarship (Babbitt 1896, Gauchat 1905, Hermann 1929). On Martha's Vineyard he used "apparent time" comparison across four age cohorts (centralization index 0.22 → 0.37 → 0.44 → 0.88 for the (aw) variable). In New York City he combined stylistic-stratification diagrams with a subjective-reaction test in which listeners rated recorded speakers' job suitability without realizing they were rating the same speakers twice.

## Applications

Labov's Martha's Vineyard (aw)/(ay) centralization study and New York City (oh) and (r) studies are the concept's founding applications (see [[labov-william]] and [[variationist-sociolinguistics]]). Blom and Gumperz's Hemnesberget study applies the underlying logic of graded, socially-indexed linguistic variation to a bidialectal code-choice setting (see [[blom-gumperz-hemnesberget-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

No critique of the construct itself is recorded in this source. Labov explicitly positions it against the view, attributed to Bloomfield (1933), Hockett (1958), and Postal (1968), that sound change is either unobservable in principle or asocial and arbitrary in its direction.
