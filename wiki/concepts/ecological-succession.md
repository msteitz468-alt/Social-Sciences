# Ecological Succession

## Definition as Coined

[[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]]: the tendency of each inner [[concentric-zones|zone]] to extend by **invasion** of the next outer zone "may be called **succession**, a process which has been studied in detail in plant ecology" (attributed, *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* Ch II).

[[mckenzie-r-d|McKenzie]]: structural growth of the human community proceeds in successional sequence "not unlike the successional stages in the development of the plant formation"; successions are products of **invasion** (citing Clements); invasions classified as change in **use** of land vs change in **type of occupant**; stages initial → developmental → **climax** (dominant organization temporarily resists further different-use invasion) (attributed, Ch III).

## Semantic History

Borrowed from plant ecology; applied to urban land use, population sifting, and community cycles (growth to culmination/climax; innovating element restarts cycle). "Invasion" language later criticized as naturalizing displacement.

## Operationalizations

Zone history on Chicago; Quaker Hill railroad transformation; Seattle service-sequence counts; land-value rise/building-value decline in use-change invasion; vices segregating in transitional obsolete buildings (McKenzie Seattle: >80% of recorded disorderly houses near downtown high-value transitional area).

## Applications

Urban expansion; immigrant first/second settlement; industrial and transport shocks to community structure.

## Critiques and Limitations

Analogy may hide covenants, redlining, violence, and state action; teleology of "climax"; period "undesirable invader" language (attributed only).
