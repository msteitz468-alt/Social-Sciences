# Utility Index (Faunal)

Ethnoarchaeologically grounded **measures of skeletal-part economic value** (meat, marrow, grease) used to model what hunters transport or abandon — load-bearing tools in Binfordian [[faunal-analysis]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Binford, *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* 1978.)* Binford built a family of indices from [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] caribou (and control-sheep) butchering experiments:
- **Meat Utility Index (MUI)** and its smoothed form **SMUI** — the meat value of each anatomical part.
- **Marrow Index** — the marrow yield of each bone.
- **Bone-grease Index** — the renderable grease value.
- **General Utility Index (GUI)** and the **Modified General Utility Index (MGUI)** — a composite that "rides" the value of a low-utility part up when it stays attached to a high-utility one during butchering.
The assemblage-side measure is **%MAU** (percentage of the Minimum Animal Units represented by each part). Binford reported high sheep–caribou index agreement (r ≈ .97) and a strong index-to-behaviour correlation (r ≈ .82) between predicted utility and observed transport/abandonment across **277+ caribou at 111 kill-butchering locations**.

## Semantic History

The indices operationalize [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]] inference: replace the narrative "they ate this" with a model-based *prediction* of part frequencies. The underlying behaviour is the **schlepp effect** — high-utility parts are carried away from a kill and low-utility parts abandoned, so kill sites show an inverse (or bell-shaped) utility curve while consumption sites show its mirror. A crucial qualification Binford himself drew from the Nunamiut is the **"cultural bias versus objective food preferences"** point: culturally patterned preferences (and non-utilitarian ritual treatment of parts) diverge from objective utility, so the indices predict the economic baseline, not the whole assemblage.

## Operationalizations

Part-frequency (%MAU) plots against utility ranks; comparison of kill vs cache vs residential vs special-purpose sites; layered indices for specific decisions (a drying utility index for rack placement, a marrow-dominant index for storage removal), used jointly because no single index captures a multi-stage decision.

## Applications

Became a standard [[faunal-analysis|zooarchaeology]] tool worldwide; applied to Paleolithic and African early-hominin assemblages (with [[taphonomy]] controls); the empirical backbone of Binford's functional reading in the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate|Mousterian debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed; post-dates the source.)* The central challenge is the **density–utility confound**: R. Lee **Lyman** and Donald **Grayson** showed that differential survival driven by **bone mineral density** can mimic or erase a utility signal, so an "inverse utility curve" may record [[taphonomy|taphonomic]] destruction rather than transport choice. **Metcalfe & Jones (1988)** revised the MGUI arithmetic. Indices are also taxon- and culture-specific, and bulk marrow/grease processing reshapes signatures after initial discard. The apparatus survives in corrected form; the naïve utility-curve reading does not — hence `contested: yes`.
