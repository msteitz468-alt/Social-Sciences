# Mores (Mœurs)

## Definition as Coined

[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] used **mores** (*mœurs*) as a technical term, glossing
it in Vol. 1 of *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*: he meant
it "in the sense the ancients attached to the word" — "not only... habits of the heart but
also the different concepts men adopt... the whole collection of ideas which shape mental
habits... the entire moral and intellectual state of a nation." Mores are thus broader than
manners: they include beliefs, dispositions, and habits of thought, not just etiquette.

His most influential use is causal. In the "circumstances, laws, and mores" thesis he ranked
the three forces that sustain a democratic republic: "physical causes contribute less than
legislation and legislation less than customs [mœurs]." He supported the ranking
comparatively — South America has better physical circumstances but no stable democracy;
Mexico copied U.S. federal laws without the result; the eastern and western United States
shared laws and circumstances but differed in mores — concluding that mores are the deepest
and most durable determinant of political life.

### The sociological sense: Sumner (1906)

The term entered *sociology* as standard technical vocabulary through
[[sumner-william-graham|William Graham Sumner]]'s *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]*
(1906). Sumner revived the Latin *mores* deliberately, and explicitly distinguished his
usage from Tocqueville's French *mœurs*: he complained that "the English language has no
derivative noun from 'mores'" and that "the French *mœurs* is trivial compared with
'mores'... the German *Sitte* renders 'mores' but very imperfectly" (§42). English-language
sociology's word "mores" is Sumner's, not a translation of Tocqueville.

Sumner's technical definition is developmental, not (like Tocqueville's) a synonym for a
people's whole moral state. Mores are **[[folkways]] raised to a higher plane**: *"When the
elements of truth and right are developed into doctrines of welfare, the folkways are raised
to another plane... Then we call them the mores. The mores are the folkways, including the
philosophical and ethical generalizations as to societal welfare which are suggested by
them, and inherent in them"* (§34; fuller definition at §66). Where a folkway is bare
custom, a more carries a judgment that the custom serves group welfare — and so acquires
coercive moral authority.

Two Sumnerian claims are much-cited:

- **The relativity of the mores.** *"For the people of a time and place, their own mores are
  always good... the standards of good and right are in the mores"* (§65). The corollary is
  the title thesis of his Chapter XV: **"the mores can make anything right"** — slavery,
  infanticide, and cannibalism were each "right" within their mores. This is an analytic
  relativism (a warning against judging past custom by present standards), not an
  endorsement — Sumner qualified it (*"the mores sometimes becloud the judgment, but they
  more often guide it,"* §584) and closed the book against "moral anarchy" (§728: *"any
  morality is better than moral anarchy... the virtue policy proves to be the only success
  policy"*). The mores are a slow, self-correcting system, not a warrant for "anything goes."
- **The primacy of custom over reason and law.** *"Philosophy and ethics are products of the
  folkways... never original and creative"* (§44); *"legislation, to be strong, must be
  consistent with the mores"* (§62). Hence the doctrine — sharpened by later writers into
  "stateways cannot change folkways" — that reform against the mores fails (see
  [[social-darwinism]]).

Sumner and Tocqueville thus share the anti-voluntarist intuition that custom is the deepest
social determinant, but differ in scope and mechanism: Tocqueville's *mœurs* is a holistic
national moral-intellectual state read causally against laws and circumstances; Sumner's
*mores* is a precise developmental stage of custom, generated by [[societal-selection|selection]]
and carried by "the masses."

## Semantic History

The term is a bridge between older moral philosophy (Montesquieu's *mœurs et manières*) and
modern social science. It anticipates the sociological and anthropological concern with
culture, values, and "habits of the heart" (a phrase later borrowed as a book title on
American individualism). In Vol. 2 Tocqueville used it to analyze how
[[equality-of-conditions|equality]] softens customs, reshapes the family, honor, and
manners. Compare later, more systematized cognates: Sumner's "folkways/mores," Durkheim's
collective representations, Bourdieu's habitus.

Near-contemporary English program: [[martineau-harriet|Martineau]] (1838) wrote of **morals and manners** as the joint object of trained observation — not using Tocqueville's technical *mœurs*, but the same Montesquieu-era problem-space. She insisted manners are inseparable from morals and must be read from general institutional indications ([[things-before-discourse]]), not generalized from individual etiquette.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Tocqueville read mores from observation, informant testimony, and literary
and documentary sources — a qualitative, comparative-historical method, not systematic
sampling.

## Applications

Mores do the heavy explanatory work throughout: the softening of manners and penal law
under equality, the democratic family ("democracy loosens social ties but tightens natural
ones"), the shift in honor codes from martial-aristocratic to commercial-democratic, and —
via the primacy thesis — the claim that American liberty rests less on its constitution than
on its habits. His internal "control case" is telling: the same American humane toward equals
"becomes insensitive to their afflictions the moment equality ceases" (the enslaved) — mores
track equality of condition specifically.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Breadth is a double edge: encompassing "the entire moral and intellectual state of a
  nation," mores risks becoming an unfalsifiable residual explanation.
- The primacy-of-mores ranking is an attributed causal thesis, argued by comparison, not
  measured.
- Sumner's relativism of the mores (§65) is analytically powerful but sits in tension with
  his own evolutionary ranking of peoples; and his "reform against the mores fails" corollary
  is empirically strained by legislated changes in custom (see [[sumner-william-graham]],
  [[social-darwinism]]).
