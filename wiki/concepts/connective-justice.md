# Connective Justice

## Definition as Coined

[[assmann-jan|Jan Assmann]]'s term (konnektive Gerechtigkeit) for the principle he reads in Egyptian [[maat|ma'at]]: the binding of act to consequence and person to community, such that good is requited and social solidarity is the condition of cosmic order. Justice "connects" — deeds return upon the doer through the community's memory and reciprocity, not through an impersonal mechanism (Assmann 1990, 1996; [[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|source]]).

## Semantic History

- **1990** — coined in *Ma'at. Gerechtigkeit und Unsterblichkeit im Alten Ägypten*.
- **1996** — *The Mind of Egypt* traces its historical career: embodied in Old Kingdom patronage and tomb autobiography, politicized and textualized in the Middle Kingdom state, transposed to the hereafter in the Judgment of the Dead, and displaced in the New Kingdom by personal piety's "theology of will" (all Assmann's argument).

## Operationalizations

Readings of wisdom instructions (Ptahhotep, Merikare, Amenemope), the Eloquent Peasant, tomb autobiographies, and Book of the Dead ch. 125.

## Applications

Egyptian ethics and theodicy; comparative work on justice-and-memory couplings in early states; taken up in biblical scholarship on act–consequence (*Tun-Ergehen-Zusammenhang*).

## Critiques and Limitations

Positions recorded: rests almost entirely on elite literary sources; Egyptologists dispute how far ma'at was a lived social norm versus a scribal ideology; the developmental sequence (solidarity → state → hereafter → piety) is Assmann's narrative construction, not an evidenced causal chain.
