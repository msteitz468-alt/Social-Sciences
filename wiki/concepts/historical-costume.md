# Historical Costume

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|The Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852), [[marx-karl|Marx]] argues that at revolutionary crises people “anxiously conjure up into their service the spirits of the past, assume their names, their battle cries, their costumes to enact a new historic scene in such time-honored disguise and with such borrowed language.” **Historical costume** (this wiki’s label for that mechanism) is the use of past political identities as disguise and language for present struggles.

## Semantic History

Marx distinguishes **functional** costume (1789–1814 Roman garb that helped emancipate modern [[bourgeoisie|bourgeois society]]; Cromwell’s Old Testament language) from **parodic** costume (1848–51 as “only the ghost of the old revolution” — Marrast as Bailly, Louis Bonaparte under Napoleon’s “iron death mask”). The opening epigram — great facts recur “Once as tragedy, and again as farce” — frames the nephew’s coup as farcical second edition of the uncle’s 18 Brumaire. Later cultural and political theory generalized “costume” and “farce” beyond French 1848 (reception not yet ingested).

## Operationalizations

In the *Brumaire*: mapping of doubles (Caussidière/Danton, Louis Blanc/Robespierre, Mountain 1848–51 / Mountain 1793–95, Nephew/Uncle); revival of old calendars, edicts, and names; peasant “superstition” that a man named Napoleon would restore glory ([[peasantry-as-class]]).

## Applications

- Analysis of symbolic politics and path-dependent revolutionary language.
- Diagnosis of [[bonapartism|Bonapartism]] as farcical imperial restoration.
- Link to [[men-make-their-own-history]]: tradition weighs on the living *as costume and memory*, not only as material constraint.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of over-reading all historical reference as inauthentic “parody.”
- Edition- and translation-dependent phrasing; the analytic distinction (glorifying new struggles vs. parodying old) is the load-bearing claim, not the metaphor alone.
