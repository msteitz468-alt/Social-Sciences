# Reactive Ethnicity

Ethnic identity strengthened or reasserted **in response to** perceived hostility, discrimination, or nativist politics, rather than as simple inherited continuity. In *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*, Portes & Rumbaut discuss reactive ethnicity as a possible aftermath of hostile modes of incorporation and of assimilationist attacks on immigrant culture (chs. 7, 10).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001.)* Under threat, second-generation youth may abandon hyphenated identities (e.g. Mexican-American) for more purely national-origin stances (e.g. Mexican), structuring self-identity and political mobilization against a mainstream seen as hostile. Distinguished from selective acculturation's partial cultural retention within a supportive community framework.

## Semantic History

Fits a longer social-science tradition of conflict-produced identity (threat → boundary hardening) specified here for the immigrant second generation and CILS identity-shift data.

## Operationalizations

Change in ethnic self-identification between 1992 and 1995–96 CILS waves (plain American IDs fall; national-origin and panethnic rise); associations with discrimination experiences and regional politics (e.g. California Prop 187). Case illustrations in *Legacies*: Mariel stigma, Koreatown/LA unrest, Mexican-origin identity shifts, West Indian strategic identity against anti-Black racism (Mary Patterson vignette).

## Applications

Mechanism linking hostile [[modes-of-incorporation]] and nativist policy to identity polarization and to self-fulfilling prophecies of ethnic conflict (*Legacies* chs. 7, 10). Distinguished from [[selective-acculturation]] (community-supported partial retention without necessarily oppositional framing).

## Critiques and Limitations

Identity labels are noisy; "reactive" inference is interpretive; adolescent reactivity may soften over the life course; panethnic vs national-origin shifts are not identical phenomena.
