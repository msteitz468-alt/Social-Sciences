# Domestic/Public Dichotomy

## Definition as Coined

Proposed by [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] in her theoretical overview to *[[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Woman, Culture, and Society]]* (1974): the **domestic** sphere is that of activities organized immediately around one or more mothers and their children; the **public** (or "social," or "political") sphere comprises the collective, extra-domestic institutions that link and rank domestic units. Rosaldo argued that women's near-universal location in the domestic sphere and men's in the public sphere both *reflects* and *produces* the [[female-subordination|universal asymmetry]] in the valuation of the sexes, because the public sphere is where cultural authority and value are concentrated.

**Power vs. authority (Rosaldo 1974, following Weber and M. G. Smith):** *authority* is the culturally legitimated *right* to command; *power* is the ability to act effectively and secure compliance even when that right is not allocated. Rosaldo held that men everywhere hold some authority over women, while women may still exercise systematic informal power (gossip, economic leverage, refusing to cook, mobilizing sons) — often coded as illegitimate or manipulative when noticed.

## Semantic History

The opposition has a double genealogy: Rosaldo's structural version, and [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] pairing of the biological family with nature and the wider society (built by the [[incest-taboo|incest prohibition]] and exogamy) with culture. [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]] fused the two — treating the domestic/social opposition as an instance of the [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]] opposition — to ground the "social role" leg of her "woman is closer to nature" thesis in [[ortner-female-to-male-1972|"Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?"]]. The term overlaps with, but predates and is broader than, the sociological "[[bourgeois-public-sphere|public sphere]]" of Habermas.

## Operationalizations

Used to code, across the ethnographic record, which activities and persons are confined to household contexts versus which have access to collective political and ritual authority; the ratio and the barriers between spheres serve as an index of gender stratification.

Rosaldo (1974) stated **conditions** (attributed): women's status is *lowest* where domestic and public are firmly differentiated and women are isolated under a single man's household authority; status *rises* when women enter men's roles or create ordered female worlds of their own; the *most egalitarian* societies are those where the two spheres are only weakly differentiated and men participate in domestic life. [[lamphere-louise|Lamphere]] (same volume) operationalized strategies by sphere integration: integrated spheres → cooperative "economic" strategies among women (e.g. Navajo); separated spheres + male hierarchy → "political" influence-through-men and conflict among co-wives/in-laws. Sanday (same volume) coded female *public-domain* status with multi-indicator scales independent of domestic respect.

## Applications

Organized much 1970s [[feminist-anthropology|feminist anthropology]]; applied across *Woman, Culture, and Society* to Chinese uterine family (Wolf), Mende female chiefship (Hoffer — who partly challenges a sharp domestic/juro-political split for West Africa), Ijaw associations (Leis), Balkan patricentrism (Denich), Maya work/sex domains (Paul), and urban Black domestic networks (Stack). Explains why women's real, "culturally unrecognized" powers rarely translate into public authority, and why initiation, politics, and religion tend to be male-controlled extra-domestic domains.

## Critiques and Limitations

Heavily criticized — including by [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo herself]] ("The Use and Abuse of Anthropology," 1980) — for projecting a Western, bourgeois public/private split onto societies where domestic and public are not separated that way, or where the household *is* the political unit. [[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] and others argued the dichotomy is an analyst's category, not a cross-cultural given (see [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]). Distinguish it sharply from claims that women are *actually* powerless: the concept is about the cultural *location* of authority, not a description of women's on-the-ground agency. Hoffer (1974) and Sacks (1974) supply internal volume friction: West African female office and Engelsian public labor cut against treating domestic confinement as a near-universal mechanism.
