# Ascribed Status

A social position assigned on the basis of birth or other criteria not chosen by the individual, as opposed to [[achieved-status|achieved status]]. In *[[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-1967|The American Occupational Structure]]*, Blau and Duncan organize the analysis around how ascribed statuses shape occupational achievement.

## Definition as Coined

In the Lintonian contrast, ascribed statuses are given; achieved statuses are acquired through performance. Blau and Duncan operationalize ascription via origin variables (parental SES, race, region, nativity, farm, kinship structure).

## Semantic History

Standard mid-century stratification vocabulary; the achievement–ascription polarity also appears in Parsonsian pattern variables. Status-attainment research made the contrast a measurement program.

## Operationalizations

Father's SEI and education; race; South/North birth and residence; immigrant generation; farm origin; family size and sibling position; broken family — as used in OCG analyses.

## Applications

[[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]]; [[status-attainment]] models generally.

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary ascription/achievement oversimplifies (education is "achieved" but heavily origin-conditioned); race is not a neutral "ascribed status" equivalent to father's job — discrimination is a structural process, which Blau and Duncan partly capture as residual net effects.
