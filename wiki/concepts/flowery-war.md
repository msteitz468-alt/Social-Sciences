## Definition as Coined

The Flowery War (xochiyaoyotl in Nahuatl) refers to a form of institutionalized warfare
among Aztec-era Mesoamerican states conducted primarily to capture enemy warriors for
sacrifice, rather than to seize territory. Coe and Koontz (2013) attribute its
institution as recurring practice to Tlacaelel, the influential Cihuacoatl ("Female
Snake," chief internal-affairs adviser) who served through three reigns in the
mid-fifteenth century; under Motecuhzoma Ilhuicamina (r. 1440–1469), Tlacaelel
instituted the Flowery War against Tlaxcala and Huexotzingo, fought via the Triple
Alliance (Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, Tlacopan) explicitly to obtain sacrificial captives for
the sun god Huitzilopochtli.

## Semantic History

The tzompantli (skull rack) in Tenochtitlan's Sacred Precinct, displaying thousands of
human heads, is described by Coe and Koontz as "a reminder of the purpose of the
never-ending 'Flowery War'" (see [[tenochtitlan]]). A carved wooden drum (huehuetl) from
Malinalco depicts the "'Flowery,' or Sacred, War of the Aztecs," symbolized by dancing
eagles and jaguars and the calendrical sign 4 Motion (the sign of the present cosmic
age), accompanied by a quoted Nahuatl war song.

Note on textual coverage: the passage of the source text covering the Malinalco drum
and the tzompantli reference does not itself use the term xochiyaoyotl or lay out the
institution's recurring, city-state-specific character in full; that fuller
institutional framing (the Tlacaelel origin, the named opposing states) is drawn from a
separate range of the same source and has been reconciled here.

## Operationalizations

War for the Aztecs was ideologically framed as sacred and honorable rather than merely
instrumental. Nahuatl war poetry, quoted by Coe and Koontz, describes "the flowery
death" as the most precious of ends: "There is nothing like death in war, nothing like
the flowery death so precious to Him who gives life." Inga Clendinnen, cited by Coe and
Koontz, characterizes Aztec battle ideally as "a sacred duel between matched warriors";
per this account, before waging war the Aztecs allegedly sent their enemies arms so
that combatants would be evenly matched — a claim recorded here as attributed and not
independently verified.

## Applications

The Flowery War supplied captives that fed the sacrificial economy analyzed on
[[sacrifice]], and is cited by Coe and Koontz as one of the ideological factors (Aztec
battle norms clashing with Spanish tactics regarded as "cowardly") in their four-factor
account of the Spanish Conquest's success.

## Critiques and Limitations

The scale and regularity of Flowery War combat, like the scale of sacrifice it fed, is
contested territory: some scholars read it as a genuinely routinized ritual institution,
while the underlying evidence is a mix of post-Conquest documentary sources and
iconography (the Malinalco drum) whose interpretive load is heavy. Treat claims about
its frequency and formal rules as attributed rather than settled. See [[sacrifice]] for
the parallel, unresolved debate over how many captives Flowery War combat and other
sacrificial occasions actually consumed.
