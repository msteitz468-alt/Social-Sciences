# Detraditionalization

The dissolution, in [[reflexive-modernization|reflexive modernity]], of the taken-for-granted traditions, class-cultural milieus, and ascribed roles of industrial society, as theorized by [[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]] in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]* (1986). Bound tightly to [[individualization]]: Beck argued that in the second modernity "the 'tradition' of industrial society itself replaces pre-modernity" — the reference points that are dissolved are no longer feudal or pre-modern traditions but the settled forms of industrial modernity itself (class, the nuclear family, the standard biography, the standard job).

## Definition as Coined

Detraditionalization is the "disenchantment" moment of Beck's triple model of individualization: the loss of traditional security in practical knowledge, faith, and guiding norms, leaving individuals to construct their own biographies without inherited scripts. It is not a return to some pre-modern condition but the erosion of *modern* industrial-society traditions.

## Semantic History

Beck's usage is closely paralleled by [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s writings on post-traditional order and by the shared program of *Reflexive Modernization* (1994). The concept risks over-generalization: Beck himself noted counter-forces ("counter-modernization") that re-embed people in ascribed roles — the "modern feudal" gender order being his central example.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by the decline of class-cultural milieus and class voting; loosening of inherited gender and family roles; and, most sharply in Beck's Ch. 6, the **destandardization of labor** — the transition "from the system of standardized full employment to the system of flexible and pluralized underemployment," dissolving the three pillars of the standard job (contract/law, fixed work site, fixed working hours) via flexitime, part-time, job-sharing, and decentralized "electronic cottage" work.

## Applications

The destandardization-of-labor argument is the most developed application: Beck predicted a bifurcation of the labor market into a shrinking "uniform standard industrial-society labor market" and an expanding "flexible, plural risk-society market for underemployment," and inverted the Marxist expectation — "the productive forces no longer break apart the relations of ownership... [they] break apart the relations of the labor contract and the labor market." He flagged this explicitly as a falsifiable hypothesis.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with [[individualization]], critics question how far ascribed statuses and class milieus have actually dissolved versus been recomposed, and note the West German specificity of the labor-market evidence. The concept's breadth — covering class, gender, family, and work at once — makes it powerful as diagnosis but hard to test as a single claim.
