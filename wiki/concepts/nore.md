# n!ore (land / resource locality)

## Definition as Coined

*(Emic term; analytic framing attributed to ethnographers of the [[juhoansi|!Kung / Ju/'hoansi]], especially [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]] in *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, Ch. 12.)* An **n!ore** (pl. n!ores) is the !Kung word for locality, land, or territory—the block of land that surrounds each water hole and provides the resources on which the people of that water hole depend. Each n!ore is associated with a core group of **owners** (*k”awsi*). Lee estimated sizes roughly at **300–600 km²**, noting that sizes cannot be usefully fixed and vary year to year. The water hole and its immediate surroundings are clearly owned and transmitted generationally if descendants remain; the surrounding belt is shared with adjacent groups.

Lee’s load-bearing formulation: the !Kung **do own** the land they occupy—“Although ownership is collective, not individual, and the boundaries are not well marked, the concept of ownership is there nonetheless” (Lee 1979, Ch. 12). This rejects both the freehold model and the claim that foragers have “no property in land.”

## Semantic History

Moved from ethnographic description (Marshall, Lee) into comparative hunter-gatherer debates on **territoriality without exclusive ownership of daily food**. Distinct from freehold property and from Service-style rigid “band territory” models challenged at *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*. Lee framed a century of scholarly dispute: Fourie on scrupulous territorial boundaries; Frere on “no shadow of land tenure”; early Marshall on clear ownership via patrilineal male headman (later revised); Yellen & Harpending on open circulating land use; Barnard on variation across !Kung, /Gwi, !Ko, Nharo (Lee 1979, Ch. 12).

In Lee’s system, n!ore is a **relation of production** within the [[foraging-mode-of-production|foraging mode of production]]—collective, nonexclusive ownership of land and resources—not residual ecology.

*(Attributed — [[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]] 1989.)* Writes **nqore** / *nqorekau* (possessors of country) and *xhaiha* leadership; treats tenure as stratified entitlement overlapping Herero and Tswana systems, with inherited rights privileging access to hunted meat and livestock accumulation at CaeCae — against readings that make San land relations structureless residuals of dispossession. Contested relative to Lee’s more egalitarian stewardship picture; see [[kalahari-debate]], [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]].

## Operationalizations

**Lee’s three-level resource-use rules (Dobe-area 1960s):**

1. **Within camp:** free access. The camp is a unit of sharing—“when sharing breaks down it ceases to be a camp.” Visitors and residents share the day’s take; reciprocal visits later. No matter where people are from, as long as they live in a single camp the n!ore’s food is theirs to share.
2. **Neighboring groups** camping separately in a n!ore must ask permission (often framed as invitation to share named plant resources). Inclusive/exclusive “our” grammar marks joint vs exclusive claims.
3. **Distant groups** must ask carefully and incur host obligation. Small/short visits freely allowed; large/long stays may prompt owner reassertion. Disputes over food between groups were rare compared with fights over sex, adultery, and betrothal.

**Boundaries.** Informants often could not specify where one n!ore ended and another began; even mongongo groves grade gradually between groups. Lee argued the ambiguity is functional: conscious maintenance of a “boundaryless universe” suits variable group size and resources. “Among the !Kung and other hunter-gatherers, good fences do not make good neighbors” (Lee 1979, Ch. 12).

**Inheritance (151 Dobe-area men, Lee’s survey):** inherited n!ore from father **39.7%**, mother **26.5%**, both **10.6%**, neither **13.9%**, unknown/none **9.3%**. Only **32 of 151 (21%)** were living in their natal n!ores at the time of study. Wiessner (cited by Lee) held that all inherit both parents’ n!ores and gravitate to one as adults. Core owners = longest association; immigrants (e.g. in-marrying siblings) are absorbed over time as *k”awsi*. Reasons for leaving natal n!ore include following parents, work for blacks, marry in, Boer displacement, death of old people, seeking water/food/tobacco.

**Case: Hxore tsin fields 1968–9.** #Toma/twe recognized senior owner of Hxore fields SW of /Xai/xai. April 1968: 40+ /Du/da southerners (plus few affines) ate out the season’s tsin. January 1969: an entirely different /Xai/xai-led group of 25–29 arrived ~2 months early to roast beans, reasserting rights; after 1969 southerners did not return in strength (small friendly visits continued). Soft territorial enforcement via use, not fortification.

**Ecology.** Extreme rainfall variability (Lee: year-to-year swing up to ~500%; Dobe measured 239 mm drought 1963–4 vs 597 mm 1967–8) and scarce permanent water made exclusive territorial claims “sheer folly.” In a ~6400 km² /Xai/xai–/Gam sample: only five permanent water points, three known to fail. Lee’s 1920s reconstruction: ≥11 landholding groups wintering on five permanent holes (only two really reliable)—reciprocal access supported higher density than if every n!ore needed its own permanent water.

## Applications

- **Spatial organization of the [[juhoansi]]** at Dobe, /Xai/xai, /Du/da: camps map onto water holes and seasonal ranges; leadership paths include n!ore ownership and marriage to an n!ore owner (most common path for men).
- **[[food-sharing]]:** co-residence in a camp converts n!ore food into shared take; camp and n!ore are linked but not identical.
- **[[hxaro]]:** long-distance delayed exchange networks and multi-group reunions (e.g. /Xai/xai as pre-Bantu entrepôt for hxaro, trance, choma) presuppose reciprocal access across n!ores.
- **[[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]:** Lee’s inheritance and mobility statistics (only 21% of men in natal n!ores; bilateral pathways) undercut strict patrilocal territorial bands; flexibility treated as primary forager principle, not mere post-contact breakdown.
- **[[kalahari-debate]]:** whether n!ore systems show long-standing independent land tenure vs. forms reshaped by cattle-post “magnets” and black settlement. Lee’s contact history: pre-settlement most of year in n!ores, few months at permanent water; post-Bantu reverse—most of year at /Xai/xai-type magnets, few months in n!ores—yet he argued flexible spatial arrangements continue pre-contact flexibility rather than replace a once-rigid system.
- **Emotional attachment:** a young woman of group 3 at /Xai/xai told Lee her true n!ore was /Dwia: “No, we are not of /Xai/xai. /Dwia is our earth. We just came here to drink the milk.”

**Named groups (!kusi).** Remnants of named, once-exogamous, semilocalized groups (animals, plants, attributes: Giraffes, Elands, Big Talkers, Carrying Yokes, etc.) were fading by the 1960s—of 151 men, 41% did not know their group. Lee suggested past named groups operated somewhat like n!ore groups but were exogamous, pointing to more corporate living groups than 1950s–60s ethnography alone shows. Locality ownership labels became the main spatial identity.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Do **not** equate n!ore with Western freehold or with Service-style patrilocal band territories. Rights are multiplex, kinship-mediated, and seasonal.
- Size estimates (300–600 km²) are Lee’s rough range, not cadastral survey.
- Inheritance percentages are for **men** interviewed in Lee’s Dobe-area questionnaire (1967–9 context); women own and transmit n!ores too (Wiessner; Lee’s own statements that land is owned by men and women).
- Boundary vagueness is Lee’s interpretive claim (“Though I cannot prove it…”); functional reading of ambiguity is theory, not a measured finding.
- Contact and cattle-post residence reshaped use patterns; reconstruct pre-contact land tenure carefully (Lee’s 1920s reconstruction is oral-historical, not direct observation).
- Leadership: n!ore ownership is a path to informal leadership, not a title of office; Lee rejected hereditary headmanship as a pre-contact !Kung institution (see [[egalitarian-society]]).
