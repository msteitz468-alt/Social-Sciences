# Intrinsic Penalties

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — primarily via [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual in Tribal Society]]* (1965), Ch. V section on “intrinsic penalties,” building on [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]’s *Crime and Custom in Savage Society* (1926).)* **Intrinsic penalties** are sanctions that flow from the **internal logic of reciprocal and status relations** rather than from an external court or police: the defaulter loses exchange partners, reputation, marriage prospects, or cooperative labour; the “penalty” is the collapse of the relationship-structure the person lives by. Malinowski’s civil-law formula — binding obligations regarded as a right by one party and a duty by the other, underwritten by reciprocity and publicity — is the classic source. Gluckman places the idea within a pluralist vocabulary of [[social-control]] (law, custom, mediation, adjudication, mystical sanctions) rather than treating intrinsic penalties as “the” definition of tribal law.

## Semantic History

- **Malinowski 1926:** reciprocity and publicity as sanctions of “civil law” among the [[trobriand-islanders|Trobrianders]] — against the stereotype of the custom-bound savage.
- **Mid-century legal anthropology:** absorbed into debates over whether societies without courts have “law” (Radcliffe-Brown force-of-political-society definitions vs Malinowski right–duty definitions). Gluckman (1965) treats that clash as largely **terminological**.
- **Gluckman 1965:** section on intrinsic penalties alongside law-and-custom, enforcement, and the notion of right/rightdoing; notes leeway in norms and extreme self-sanctions (e.g. suicide as sanctioning gesture in some systems). Complements mystical sanctions (witchcraft, ancestors) treated in Ch. VI.

## Operationalizations

Not a quantitative index. Ethnographically observed as: withdrawal of exchange; public shaming; loss of following; automatic status consequences of default. Distinct from (a) physical force by a privileged enforcer (Hoebel’s “legal” bite) and (b) mystical misfortune attributed to witches or ancestors — though Gluckman analyses all three as layers of control.

## Applications

- Trobriand reciprocity (Malinowski) as textbook case.
- Gluckman’s comparative discussion of tribal right-doing and of systems where self-help pressures work *through* group cohesion (e.g. seizing a kinsman’s stock so that kinsmen press the debtor — Fortes Tallensi / Tonga parallels in Ch. III).
- Bridge to [[prestige-goods-economy|prestige economies]] where hoarding is pointless and reputation is made by giving away.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Underplays coercion:** force-centered definitions (Radcliffe-Brown, Hoebel) treat reciprocity sanctions as “custom,” not law — the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate|terminological fight]] continues.
- **Romanticization risk:** reading all order as “intrinsic” can soft-pedal colonial courts, chiefs’ police, and inequality of sanction power.
- Single-source densification here is Gluckman 1965’s textbook restatement; Malinowski primary and Barotse monographs still pending for deeper operational detail.
