# On (obligation)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[benedict-chrysanthemum-and-sword-1946|The Chrysanthemum and the Sword]]*, [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] presents Japanese *on* as **passively incurred obligation** — a debt one “receives” or “wears.” *On* is a **load of indebtedness**, not a synonym for love or free loyalty. Major sources she lists include Imperial *on* (*ko on*), parental *on* (*oya on*), and *on* to teachers and masters. One “never returns one ten-thousandth” of the greatest *on*.

Benedict contrasts Oriental self-understanding as **debtors to the ages** with Western “heirs of the ages” confidence. Righteousness, she argues, depends on recognizing one’s place in a network of mutual indebtedness embracing forebears and contemporaries.

## Semantic History

Emic Japanese moral vocabulary given English analytic life by Benedict (1946) and subsequent Japan teaching. Later specialists often argue she over-systematized and over-moralized everyday usage; the term remains a teaching shorthand for hierarchical indebtedness.

## Operationalizations

Benedict’s operationalization is **typological**, not a survey scale:

| Category | Character |
|---|---|
| **I. On** | Obligations passively incurred |
| **II. Reciprocals** | Active repayment |
| **II.A Gimu** | Fullest repayment still partial; no time limit (*chu*, *ko*, *nimmu*) |
| **II.B Giri** | Mathematical equivalence; time limits (to the world; to one’s name) |

Virtue, in her account, begins at active gratitude/repayment, not at the mere receipt of *on*. Casual *on* is avoided (thank-you formulas express unease: *kino doku*, *sumimasen*, *katajikenai*). Love/kindness carry strings; making *on* too heavy is a social fault.

## Applications

Core of Benedict’s model of Japanese ethics, hierarchy, wartime self-sacrifice, and quick resentment where Westerners expect none. Links to [[taking-ones-proper-station]] and to post-surrender *chu*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of freezing wartime or samurai-inflected vocabulary as “the Japanese”; class and period variation; see [[national-character-debate]]. Always attribute the full chart to Benedict 1946 unless independent sources confirm.
