## Definition as Coined

Dentate stamping is a pottery-decoration technique using stamps carved with rows of fine teeth, pressed into leather-hard clay before firing. It is the diagnostic, defining decorative technique of the Lapita ceramic series — described by Patrick Vinton Kirch as the sine qua non of Lapita pottery. No dentate stamps themselves have been recovered archaeologically; their material is inferred, on the basis of the impressions left, to have been wood, bamboo, or turtle shell (attributed, Ambrose 1997, via Kirch 2017).

## Semantic History

The technique has been analyzed since the 1970s as a formal design "grammar," with motif catalogs compared structurally across sites, in a manner drawing on linguistic/structuralist methods of analysis, to assess degrees of cultural relatedness between Lapita communities (attributed, Mead et al. 1975; Sharp 1988, 1991). This grammar-based approach treats dentate-stamped design elements as a rule-governed system rather than a set of unrelated decorative choices, allowing regional and chronological variation within the Lapita cultural complex to be tracked formally.

## Operationalizations

Motif catalogs are built site-by-site and compared for shared design elements, layout conventions, and stylistic drift, functioning as a chronological and geographic marker within the [[lapita-cultural-complex]] and as supporting evidence for the regional "provinces" (Far Western, Western, Southern, Eastern) into which Lapita pottery has been subdivided.

## Applications

Dentate-stamped ceramics anchor major Lapita sites such as Talepakemalai (Mussau Islands) and Teouma (Vanuatu), where more than 70 reconstructed dentate-stamped vessels apparently marked mortuary contexts, interpreted (attributed) as evidence of ancestor-oriented ritual.

## Critiques and Limitations

The technique's origin is genuinely unresolved and treated by Kirch as a matter of competing, speculative hypotheses rather than established fact: it may derive from the transfer of a body-decoration (tattooing) aesthetic to the ceramic medium (attributed, Kirch 1997a:141–44), or from weaving patterns (attributed, Ambrose, personal communication 2016). Both explanations are presented as hypotheses, not findings, and neither should be promoted to wiki voice. Within the Triple-I model of Lapita ethnogenesis (see [[triple-i-model]]), Kirch specifically characterizes dentate stamping itself as an "innovation" arising within the Near Oceania contact zone, present in neither the intrusive Austronesian-speaking nor the resident Papuan-speaking source populations.
