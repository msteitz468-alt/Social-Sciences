# Circular Flow of Power

## Definition as Coined

[[daniels-robert-v|Robert V. Daniels]]'s mechanism for how a faction controlling the **party apparatus** could dominate the formal elective hierarchy of the CPSU: appointees in regions and key posts shaped congress delegations, which elected the Central Committee, which chose the executive organs (including those running the apparatus). Mawdsley and White (2000) present this as the standard account of how "apparatus men" displaced rivals in the 1920s, paired with the [[job-slot-system]].

## Semantic History

Classic Western Sovietology model of intra-party power; later archival work (incl. Mawdsley & White) can refine timing and limits without discarding the loop as an organising hypothesis. Related to but distinct from totalitarian "transmission belt" images — the circular flow is specifically about **leadership selection inside the party**.

## Operationalizations

Observed co-variation of apparatus control, congress slate construction, and CC composition; limited write-in success in CC ballots after the early 1920s (Mawdsley & White document high approval of printed lists).

## Applications

Explains Stalin's consolidation path as organisational rather than purely ideological; template for other single-party systems with formal congress–CC–secretariat chains. Paired with the [[job-slot-system]] in [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]]: apparatus favour fills slots; slots confer CC status; CC elects executives controlling the apparatus.

Mawdsley & White document high approval of printed CC lists and limited successful write-ins after the early 1920s — empirical texture for the loop's elective facade.

## Critiques and Limitations

Can overstate automaticity; underplays external shocks (war, economic crisis), police power, and personal networks; post-1953 collective leadership and Gorbachev-era contested plenums strain pure apparatus-loop accounts.
