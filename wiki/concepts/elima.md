# Elima

> Documented by [[turnbull-forest-people-study|Turnbull, *The Forest People* (1961)]]; source [[turnbull-forest-people-1961]]. A [[mbuti|Mbuti]] emic term. Single-band fieldwork account (1957–58), attributed voice.

## Definition as Coined

Among the [[mbuti|BaMbuti]], the *elima* was the festival celebrating a girl's **first menstruation** — "the Dance of Life," in [[turnbull-colin-m|Turnbull]]'s chapter title. A girl who reached menarche entered a specially built or extended hut (the **elima house**) together with her unmarried female friends, not in isolation, where an older woman taught them adult women's songs and crafts. A bachelor was appointed "father of the elima" and a widow "mother of the elima" to chaperone and protect the girls. The festival Turnbull observed lasted roughly a month or two. During it, the girls emerged armed with long *fito* whips to chase and whip the young men; being whipped placed an obligation on a youth to visit the elima house, where — with the girl's consent — courtship, flirtation, and sometimes sexual relationship followed (Turnbull reported that his informants held these relations to reliably avoid conception, as no elima pregnancy was reported to him — an attributed claim). For boys as well as girls the elima was a coming-of-age: a youth proved manhood by fighting his way into the elima house and by killing "a real animal."

## Semantic History

As with the [[molimo]], the villagers (BaBira and others) had a rite of the same name, but Turnbull reported that the two peoples gave first menstrual blood **opposite meanings**: the villagers treated it as a dangerous, shameful calamity requiring seclusion, purification, and interrogation of the girl; the Mbuti treated it as "a gift, received with gratitude and rejoicing." He read this reversal as emblematic of the wider forest/village opposition. Turnbull judged the Mbuti "not a ritualistically minded people": the substantive elima ended when the girls were publicly acknowledged in song, while the days-long "traditional," village-style close (bathing, oiling, new bark cloth, a feast) was staged mainly to placate villagers offended by the girls' unorthodox conduct.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; documented through its procedure (the elima house, the appointed elima parents, the whipping courtship, the closing song) as above. Turnbull noted the festival could be pulled between forest and village settings — younger men and the elder Tungana favoured holding it in the village, partly (Tungana) to "make money out of tourists" drawn by the Epulu animal park.

## Applications

The elima is, with the male-oriented [[molimo]] and the killing of "a real animal," one of the [[mbuti|Mbuti]] markers of adulthood, and a counterpoint to the villagers' coercive **nkumbi** initiation — evidence for Turnbull's claim that the Mbuti possessed their own celebratory rites and did not depend on the villagers for ritual adulthood ([[mbuti-village-relations-debate]]). As a joyful [[rites-of-passage|rite of passage]] around female puberty it contrasts sharply with the seclusion-and-danger model documented elsewhere in African ethnography.

## Critiques and Limitations

The account is Turnbull's alone, from one band, and shares in the general critique that *The Forest People* presents an idealised forest world (see [[turnbull-colin-m]]). The claim that elima sexual relations reliably avoided pregnancy rests on informant report, not observation. Fuller technical treatment is in *Wayward Servants* (1965), not ingested.
