# Formative Stage

Third New World historical-developmental stage in [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Willey & Phillips]] (1958) — the Americanist village-agriculture (or equivalent) threshold, deliberately **not** equated with Old World [[neolithic|Neolithic]] as a single historical package.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958, revised from 1955.)*

> We now define the New World Formative by the presence of agriculture, or any other subsistence economy of comparable effectiveness, and by the successful integration of such an economy into well-established, sedentary village life.

### What changed from 1955

- **1955** used maize and/or manioc agriculture as criteria — historically derived crops inside a developmental classification (ambiguity they retract).
- **Preformative** dropped: agriculture proved a “slow-footed” not explosive stimulus; early domestication appears in “lower” Archaic-type contexts; settlement patterns, not raw agriculture, are the practical criteria.
- Agriculture remains the **principal** formative agent but not the **indispensable** one — shift toward the developmental side of their hybrid historical-developmental scheme.
- Associated traits (pottery, weaving, stone-carving, specialized ceremonial architecture) usually co-occur but are **not** causally locked to agriculture.

### Origins model

Formative origins will be complex and diverse — gradual assemblage over time and wide areas — **not** a concurrent explosion in one locality (explicit contrast with sudden [[neolithic-revolution|revolution]] imagery).

## Semantic History

“Formative” / “Preclassic” / “Developmental” already organized Mesoamerican and Andean sequences. W&P made Formative the **hinge** of New World prehistory (with Archaic) and extended the label to Southwest, Eastern Woodlands, Intermediate area, Amazonia, etc., with later thresholds outside nuclear America. Still used in Mesoamerican periodization; contested as a pan-hemispheric stage.

## Operationalizations

| Region (as of W&P 1958) | Approx. Formative threshold |
|---|---|
| Middle America | 2nd millennium B.C. (e.g. El Arbolillo, Early Zacatenco) |
| Peru | Nearly as early (1st millennium B.C. range in their text) |
| SW & Eastern U.S. | First centuries A.D. (many authorities) |
| South Andes | Possibly as late as A.D. 1000 |

Early maize in New Mexico/Tamaulipas without stable agricultural villages stays **Archaic** by their criteria. Hopewell reassigned fully Formative by configuration under loosened standards. Local “classics” (Pueblo Bonito, Mississippian) remain Formative **ceilings** from a hemispheric view.

## Applications

- Nuclear American village sequences (Valley of Mexico through Cuicuilco; Andean Formative).
- Debates over California/NW Coast as non-agricultural Formative vs Archaic.
- Teaching contrast with Childe’s [[neolithic-revolution]] (multi-source gradualism vs revolution).

## Critiques and Limitations

Hybrid historical (crop diffusion) + developmental (village sedentism) criteria remain tense. Hemispheric Classic ceiling for non-nuclear areas is a value-laden evaluation, not a pure description. Absolute dates and many phase equations are 1958-state-of-knowledge.

## Moore 2005 — Formative ceremonial architecture and religious authority

*(Attributed — [[moore-jerry|Moore]], [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Cultural Landscapes in the Ancient Andes]] 2005.)* For the Andean Formative, Moore analyzed ceremonial architecture at nine principal sites ([[real-alto]], La Emerenciana, [[kotosh]], Piruru, Montegrande, [[huaca-de-los-reyes]], Purulén, San Luis, Cerro Ñañañique) plus fifteen more from Moore 1996b, on qualitative variables of **permanence, ubiquity, and visibility**. Sites cluster into three sets: impermanent/community-scale/intimate spaces; permanent, regional/interregional, public-distant centers (Purulén, Montegrande, Huaca de los Reyes, Sechín Alto, Las Aldas, Cardal…), many with axial [[procession|processional]] plans; and a "transitional" set of durable but small, consultative spaces (Kotosh, San Luis, Cerro Ñañañique) with no clear ethnographic analogue. Moore argues the permanent public centers reflect **canon-based religious authority**, not ecstatic shamanism and not necessarily state power — his intervention in the standing dispute over Formative monumentality: see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]].

## Flannery ed. 1976 — Early–Middle Formative village window

*(Attributed — [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] (ed.), *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* 1976.)* For operational Formative research in southern Mexico and western Guatemala, EMV focuses on **permanent village farming communities** roughly **1500–500 B.C.** — Early through Middle Formative — treating the **village** as the key settlement type from which specialized camps and, later, temple-centered (“temple Formative”) patterns develop. Flannery prefers restricting analysis to that window, using Late Formative only to track trends. At the start of this millennium, he argues, true permanent pole-and-thatch (wattle-and-daub) villages first became widespread in [[mesoamerica]], and with primary village farming communities Mesoamerica first became definable as a culture area distinct from desert food-gatherers to the north and tropical forest peoples to the south. This is a **research-periodization and culture-area claim for the village horizon**, not a replacement of Willey & Phillips’s hemispheric Formative definition above.
