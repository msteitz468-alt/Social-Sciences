# Achievement-Based Society

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]].)*
An **achievement-based society** is one in which prestige and leadership are **earned
within a lifetime** — through feasting, generosity, success in warfare or raiding,
ritual knowledge, or debt relationships — rather than **inherited**. Flannery and Marcus
place it as an intermediate condition between egalitarian foraging societies (which
actively level self-aggrandizement) and hereditary **[[rank-society|rank societies]]**
(in which status is ascribed at birth). It overlaps with the classic
**[[big-man]]** systems of Melanesia but is treated by the authors as a broader analytic
category, marked archaeologically by communal **men's houses / ritual houses** rather
than temples served by a hereditary priesthood.

## Semantic History

The idea descends from Morton [[fried-morton-h|Fried's]] distinction between
**egalitarian**, **rank**, and **stratified** societies and from Elman
[[service-elman-r|Service's]] band–tribe–chiefdom–state typology (see
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]). Flannery and Marcus reframe the achieved/ascribed
distinction dynamically: the central question is not *which type a society is* but *how*
achieved renown gets converted into heritable advantage — and how often that conversion
is blocked or reversed. The label "achievement-based society" is their 2012 usage; the
underlying achieved-vs-ascribed status contrast is much older (Ralph Linton, 1936).

## Operationalizations

Different traditions read the achieved/ascribed line differently. Ethnographically it is
identified through succession rules (is leadership competed for or inherited?).
Archaeologically Flannery and Marcus infer it from **ritual architecture** (communal
men's houses vs. temples), from the **absence** of hereditary status markers in burials
(no lavishly furnished infant/child graves that would imply ascribed rank), and from
settlement patterns lacking a fixed paramount center. These proxies are debated, since
achieved and ascribed systems can leave similar material traces.

## Applications

In *The Creation of Inequality* the category organizes the book's middle section:
Melanesian Big Man systems, the Mountain Ok men's-house societies of New Guinea, and the
Ancestral Pueblo of the American Southwest are read as achievement-based, and their
prehistoric ritual houses are traced as the architectural ancestor of the later temple.

## Critiques and Limitations

The scheme inherits the standard critiques of neoevolutionary typologies (see
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]]): real
societies rarely sit cleanly in one box; the material proxies for "achieved" vs.
"ascribed" are ambiguous; and using living ethnographic cases to interpret prehistoric
ones is flagged analogy, not direct evidence. Flannery and Marcus respond that the
value of the category is dynamic — tracking the *process* of conversion — not
pigeonholing societies into a stage.
