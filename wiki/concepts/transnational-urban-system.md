# Transnational Urban System

Sassen's construct for the (incipient) formation of cross-border networks among major cities whose economic ties to one another can rival or outpace their ties to national hinterlands. There is no single [[global-city|global city]]; importance is network-derived.

## Definition as Coined

From the fifth hypothesis in the 2001 preface: specialized service firms' need for global service delivery produces affiliate/partnership networks and strengthens city-to-city transactions — "the beginning of the formation of transnational urban systems."

## Semantic History

Extends world-city hierarchy ideas toward network topology; 2001 edition rewrites the hierarchies chapter to emphasize transactions among cities. Related later literatures: world city network (GaWC and others — not yet ingested).

## Operationalizations

Cross-border financial transactions; producer-service firm branch networks; international property markets; currency roles; comparative urban hierarchy measures within and across nations.

## Applications

New York–London–Tokyo relations; insertion of other cities into transnational circuits (*[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]* ch. 7).

## Critiques and Limitations

"System" may overstate integration; empirical measurement of inter-city transactions is hard; national regulatory and currency structures still matter. Degree of "disconnect" from national economies is debated.
