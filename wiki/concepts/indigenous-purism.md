# Indigenous Purism

**Indigenous purism** is Kroskrity’s term for a language-ideological preference — especially among the [[arizona-tewa|Arizona Tewa]] — for keeping the heritage language free of foreign loans and **strictly compartmentalized** from other codes in the repertoire, so that maximally distinctive languages can index differentiated social identities.

## Definition as Coined

In [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language*]] ch. 9 (and earlier Kroskrity 1993, 1998 as he cites them), purism is one of several preferences extending the **kiva speech** model (*te e hiili*) beyond the ceremonial: convention, **indigenous purism**, compartmentalization, and identity indexing. Purism is not treated as a timeless “Pueblo essence” but as ideology regimented in association with elite ceremonial speech.

## Semantic History

Kroskrity (intro and ch. 9) argues that earlier scholarship attributing linguistic conservatism as an essential feature of Pueblo culture **obscured** the association of purism with kiva discourse controlled by a ceremonial elite — an interest-laden construction, not cultural essence. Contrasts with [[kaluli|Kaluli]] mission-literacy settings where speakers could not retain comparable control over loan admission and written “the language.”

## Operationalizations

Track loanword admission, codeswitching norms, metalinguistic talk about “mixing,” and sites (kiva, school, home) where purism is enforced or relaxed.

## Applications

[[arizona-tewa]] case in *Regimes* ch. 9; comparative foil for Kaluli (Schieffelin) and monoglot-standard regimes.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Indigenous” can reify the boundary the ideology itself polices; purism coexists with long multilingualism (Tewa/Hopi/English) — it regulates contact, it does not eliminate it.
