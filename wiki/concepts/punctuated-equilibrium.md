## Definition as Coined

Punctuated equilibrium, proposed by Niles Eldredge and Stephen Jay Gould, holds that
speciation occurs mainly in rapid bursts during brief branching episodes
(cladogenesis), after which species change very slowly if at all — long periods of
morphological stasis. It stands opposed to the gradualist model descended from Darwin,
in which modal morphology shifts steadily and continuously along a lineage
(anagenesis) until the cumulative shift is extensive enough to be recognized as a new
species. As presented in [[klein-human-career-1999]], the punctuationalist model
postulates essentially random, noncumulative morphological change through time; new
species emerge only when innovations become fixed in a small peripheral population,
which then diverges rapidly before settling into stasis.

## Semantic History

Klein argues (attributed) that many paleontologists have abandoned gradualism in
favor of punctuated equilibrium because the fossil record repeatedly shows abrupt
appearance and disappearance of species with little morphological change in between —
a pattern Darwin attributed to the incompleteness of the fossil record, which Klein
states "is no longer the case" (as of Klein 1999). Advocates of punctuated equilibrium
argue that the innovations marking a new species become fixed in small peripheral
populations that are unlikely ever to be sampled by paleontologists, which is offered
as the explanation for the rarity of transitional "missing link" fossils.

Klein is explicit, however, that it is the nature of the fossil record itself — not
logic or controlled experiment — that supports the model, and that even its strongest
advocates do not claim it must characterize human evolution in the absence of fossil
evidence for it. Klein's own assessment (attributed): punctuated equilibrium is "more
a thoughtful generalizing of data than a theory."

## Operationalizations

Applied to the hominin fossil record, punctuated equilibrium is read off long stretches
of morphological stasis interrupted by comparatively rapid transitions. Klein notes
(attributed, citing further sources) that African antelopes and primates, including
hominins, apparently speciated in bursts tied to episodes of climatic and environmental
change around 5 million years ago and again around 2.5 million years ago — linking the
tempo of speciation to external (climatic) forcing rather than treating it as a purely
internal, gradual process.

The model also frames a live dispute (position, unresolved as of Klein 1999) over
whether the transition from [[homo-habilis]] to [[homo-erectus]] (H. ergaster) was
abrupt/punctuational or gradual; Klein leans toward an abrupt origin on the grounds of
the near-simultaneous emergence of H. ergaster and the Acheulean industry (see
[[encephalization]]).

## Applications

- Framing debates over hominin lineage transitions ([[homo-habilis]] to
  [[homo-erectus]]; the emergence of [[homo-heidelbergensis]]) as candidates for
  punctuational rather than gradual change.
- Reframing the "missing link" problem: rarity of transitional fossils is explained
  by the small-peripheral-population mechanism rather than by record incompleteness
  alone.
- Linking speciation tempo to episodes of climatic/environmental forcing (see
  [[quaternary-glaciation]]) rather than treating speciation as a steady background
  process.

## Critiques and Limitations

Klein cautions against overreading the model as an established mechanism: it rests
on pattern-matching to the fossil record's discontinuities rather than on independent
experimental or genetic demonstration, and its most careful proponents do not extend
it as a universal law to cases (such as human evolution) where the fossil evidence
does not itself display the punctuated pattern. The gradualist/punctuationalist
question remains, on Klein's own account, a live methodological dispute rather than a
settled finding — see [[species-concept]] for the related difficulty of defining
species boundaries in a discontinuous fossil record.
