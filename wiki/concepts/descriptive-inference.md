## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) define descriptive inference, in *Designing Social
Inquiry* (1994), as one of the two basic types of inference available to social
scientists, the other being causal inference. They write: "Descriptive inference is
the process of understanding an unobserved phenomenon on the basis of a set of
observations." More fully, KKV describe inference itself as "the additional step of
attempting to infer beyond the immediate data to something broader that is not
directly observed. That something may involve descriptive inference—using
observations from the world to learn about other unobserved facts. Or that something
may involve causal inference—learning about causal effects from the data observed."

KKV argue that a central goal of descriptive inference is to distinguish the
systematic component of a phenomenon from its nonsystematic component (see
[[systematic-vs-nonsystematic-variation]]): "one of the fundamental goals of inference
is to distinguish the systematic component from the nonsystematic component of the
phenomena we study."

## Semantic History

KKV's formalization is explicitly directed at qualitative political science and
sociology, where they argue description had often been treated as a separate,
lesser activity from causal explanation. They deny this separation is meaningful in
itself: "it is not description versus explanation that distinguishes scientific
research from other research; it is whether systematic inference is conducted
according to valid procedures." They further hold the two are mutually dependent:
"We cannot construct meaningful causal explanations without good description;
description, in turn, loses most of its interest unless linked to some causal
relationships."

The term does not originate with KKV — descriptive versus causal (or explanatory)
inference tracks a long-standing distinction in statistics between estimation of
population parameters and estimation of causal effects — but KKV's 1994 treatment is
the point at which the distinction was formalized as a paired concept pair for
qualitative political methodology, importing the statistical apparatus of
unbiasedness, efficiency, and consistency (introduced in their Chapter 2, alongside
causal-inference criteria in Chapter 3) as the shared evaluative standard for both
descriptive and causal claims regardless of quantitative or qualitative method.

## Operationalizations

KKV operationalize descriptive inference through formal statistical notation: a
"realized variable" y (the actually observed values) versus a "random variable" Y
(the hypothetical variable across repeated, hypothetical replications of the same
underlying conditions), with the sample mean as an estimator judged by three
criteria — unbiasedness, efficiency, and consistency — combined into mean square
error (MSE). They state: "we introduce three explicit criteria that are commonly
used in statistics for judging methods of making inferences—unbiasedness,
efficiency, and consistency," adding, "We also use these same criteria for judging
causal inferences in the next chapter."

KKV also give five guidelines for improving the quality of descriptive-inference
data: record and report the data-generation process; collect data on as many
observable implications of a theory as possible (see [[observable-implications]]);
maximize measurement validity; ensure reliable data-collection procedures; and make
data and analyses replicable where possible. They cite the Lynds' *Middletown*
studies (1929, replicated 1937, and later reanalyzed by Caplow et al. 1983) as "an
exemplar for what is possible" in qualitative replication.

## Applications

KKV apply the case-study bias/efficiency trade-off directly to descriptive
inference: a careful, corrected case-study estimate may be less biased but less
efficient than a large-n but lower-quality dataset (e.g., government-supplied
statistics), with mean square error offered as the formal criterion for choosing
between them — the same trade-off treated at length under [[measurement-error]].
Sub-national and single-case descriptive claims (attendance rates, vote shares,
typical household composition) are the paradigm cases KKV have in mind.

## Critiques and Limitations

KKV's attempt to unify qualitative and quantitative research under a single
statistical logic of inference — descriptive and causal alike — is itself a
contested methodological position, not a settled consensus; see
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] and the broader
[[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]. Critics associated with interpretivist
and case-oriented traditions (Geertz, Ferejohn, Psathas, Moon, among others cited by
KKV themselves) dispute whether a single logic of descriptive inference, borrowed
from quantitative statistics, can or should govern interpretive and case-study work
without distorting its distinctive aims. KKV's own framework anticipates and stages
this dispute rather than resolving it in wiki voice here.
