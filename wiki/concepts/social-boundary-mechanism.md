# Social Boundary Mechanism

> [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]’s preliminary inventory (*[[tilly-identities-boundaries-social-ties-2005|Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties]]* Ch. 9) of **robust causal mechanisms** that explain formation, transformation, activation, and suppression of [[social-boundary|social boundaries]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly 2005.)* Answers to puzzles of boundary salience (why peaceable difference becomes lethal; why unbridgeable lines fade; why enemies become friends) share a limited set of mechanisms:

1. **Precipitating mechanisms** — cause boundaries to change  
2. **Constitutive mechanisms** — *are* forms of boundary change and thus produce consequences of change  

Precipitating mechanisms are causally prior even when nearly simultaneous with constitutive ones (e.g. authoritative **imposition** before **activation**).

## Semantic History

Builds on identity, nationalism, and cross-boundary conversation literatures (Brubaker & Cooper, Lamont, Mamdani, Sahlins, Zelizer, etc. as Tilly cites) but claims **no one had systematically catalogued** boundary-change mechanisms. Explicitly mechanism-process explanation (see also Ch. 3 of the same volume on political mechanisms).

## Operationalizations

Inventory of candidate mechanisms (Tilly 2005); each is illustrated, not exhaustively verified.

### Precipitating mechanisms

| Mechanism | Brief sense (Tilly) |
|---|---|
| **Encounter** | Previously separate populations come into contact; new us–them organization becomes available |
| **Imposition** | Authorities (states, organizations) decree or enforce a categorical divide |
| **Borrowing** | Actors import a boundary template from elsewhere |
| **Conversation** | Interaction and discourse reshape which distinctions matter |
| **Incentive shift** | Changes in payoffs (rewards, risks, resources) make boundary organization more or less attractive |

### Constitutive mechanisms (boundary change itself)

| Mechanism | Brief sense (Tilly) |
|---|---|
| **Inscription** | Marking / writing the distinction into practice, law, space, bodies |
| **Erasure** | Removing or fading the marks of a distinction |
| **Activation** | Making a latent or weak boundary interactionally salient |
| **Deactivation** | Lowering salience of a previously organizing boundary |
| **Site transfer** | Moving which social sites a boundary organizes (e.g. rites of passage, “passing,” reclassification) |
| **Relocation** | Replacing one organizing boundary with another (e.g. soccer fans vs police after pitch invasion; war realignments) |

Tilly does **not** claim the lists are exhaustive; he claims varying combinations figure in most or all social boundary changes.

## Causal flow (Figure 9-1 sense)

Precipitating mechanisms → constitutive boundary change → effects (including attack–defense spirals, ethnic cleansing pathways when imposition + activation couple, occupational sex-typing, immigrant identity oscillation).

Non-boundary effects (ordinary within-group cooperation, etc.) remain distinct.

## Applications

- **Immigrant festivals / dual identities** (Buenos Aires Bolivians) — activation on festive occasions, deactivation or competing identities in daily work.  
- **Ethnic cleansing** — imposition + activation + attack–defense.  
- **Occupational sex-typing** — inscription of gender boundaries into jobs.  
- **Chain migration niches** — boundaries that support [[opportunity-hoarding]] (Ch. 10).  
- **Citizenship** — inscription of citizen/noncitizen with twin inclusion/exclusion (Ch. 11).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Preliminary** by author’s admission; not experimentally verified across settings.  
- Concatenation and interaction of mechanisms under-specified in the chapter.  
- Overlap with [[boundary-work]] (Lamont) and [[ethnic-boundary]] (Barth) — related but not identical: Tilly focuses on **change mechanisms**, not only content of evaluative standards or ethnic interaction alone.  
- Mechanism talk itself contested in social science methodology (Tilly engages the unease in-text).
