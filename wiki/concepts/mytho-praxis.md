# Mytho-Praxis

## Definition as Coined

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985, ch. 2 "Other Times, Other Customs," section "Mytho-Praxis") names **mytho-praxis** the cultural mode in which "historical/political action is explicitly and self-consciously conducted through the deployment... of myth." His paradigm case is the Maori: "Clearly, Maori are cunning mythologists, who are able to select from the supple body of traditions those most appropriate to the satisfaction of their current interests, as they conceive them. The distinctiveness of their mytho-praxis is not the existence (or the absence) of such interests, but exactly that they are so conceived" — in and through mythic categories.

Mytho-praxis sits inside Sahlins's larger contrast between "structures that are practiced primarily through the individual subconscious" (the unreflected [[habitus]], illustrated by the Ilongot and by Western market ideology) and structures "that explicitly organize historical action as the projection of mythical relations." The Maori exemplify the latter: for them "the final form of cosmic myth is current event," and happening and structure collapse into one another, mediated by the concept **tikanga** — "the distinctive action of beings and things that comes of their particular nature." Events are "hardly unique or new but are immediately perceived in the received order of structure, as identical with their original."

## Anticipation in *Historical Metaphors* (1981)

*(Attributed — *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities]]*, ch. 2, "History as Myth, Epic and Cosmic Travelogue.")* The 1981 monograph works out the substance before the 1985 label. Against Malinowski's charter theory — myth "cannot merely be a set of tricks the living play on the dead" — Sahlins argues the Maori "re-lives history": mythical incidents are archetypal situations re-experienced by the living. He grounds this in Johansen's **"kinship I"** (a Maori narrator recounting ancestral deeds centuries past in the first person — literal identification, not metaphor) and in the genus–species logic of Polynesian descent: "the ancestor stands to his descendants as a general class to its specific instances, a 'type' to its 'tokens'" — avengers "put on Whakatau," the dying "die the primordial death of Maui." A Dumézil-style contrast distinguishes Maori cosmology (mythic, philosophical, moral) from Hawaiian tradition (a humanized, historicized *epic* of legendary chiefs, per Beckwith), the "plane of dramatic action" rotating from vertical (heaven/earth) to horizontal (voyaging between distant lands) as genealogy approaches the present — and the same genus–species logic of divine classification is what let Cook be classified as Lono "even in the absence of demonstrable genealogical connection."

## Semantic History

Sahlins's term reworks the anthropology of myth against two foils: the Thucydidean historiographic ideal (logos triumphing over mythos, the elimination of "the marvelous") and the Annales-school treatment of the singular event as froth. In mytho-praxis myth is not the opposite of practical action but its very medium — the concept extends his "possible theory of history" (that inherited categories are put at risk and revalued in use; see [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]) to societies where the categories mobilized are avowedly mythic. It is close kin to [[heroic-history]] (the mode of divine-kingship polities) but names specifically the explicit, exegetical deployment of myth rather than the amplification of history through the royal person.

## Operationalizations / Applications

Sahlins's worked case is **Hone Heke's flagstaff war** (Ngapuhi, Bay of Islands, New Zealand, 1844–46; documented from contemporary British/missionary sources and Sahlins's 1980s inquiry — see [[maori]]). Heke cut down the British flagstaff at Kororāreka four times, stating repeatedly that the flagstaff itself — not plunder — was the "root cause of the war" (*putake o te riri*). Sahlins reads the flagstaff as homologous to the Maori *tūāhu* (the sacred pole/shrine marking a clan's *mana* over land, and re-enacting the god Tāne's primal separation of Heaven and Earth): a post-1840 trade depression supplied the "revelatory sign" of the conflict but not its determining cause, which was mythically framed. "One myth is thus decoded by another," he adds — "for the Treaty of Waitangi was a myth, even in European terms" (its English *sovereignty* and Maori *rangatiratanga* not mutually intelligible). He notes the schema's persistence: the same flagstaff was attacked again by Maori protesters in 1982–83.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mytho-praxis is an ideal-typical mode, not a claim that mythic peoples lack calculation — the point is precisely that interest is *conceived* mythically, not that it is absent. As with [[heroic-history]] and [[performative-and-prescriptive-structures]], the risk is a too-clean opposition between "mythic" and "modern" historicities; Sahlins guards against it by insisting Western market ideology is *also* a culturally specific historicity (the bourgeois "mode of appearance"). The Maori ethnohistory is Sahlins's reconstruction from colonial-era sources and is attributed, not wiki-voice.
