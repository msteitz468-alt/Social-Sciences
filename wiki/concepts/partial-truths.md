# Partial Truths

Clifford’s framing term in the Introduction to *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986): ethnographic accounts are **partial** — incomplete, positioned, interested — rather than total objective mirrors of culture. Core of the [[writing-culture]] program.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Clifford 1986.)*

Ethnographic writings are fictions in the sense of *something made* (*fingere*), not mere falsehood. Good ethnographies are “true fictions” — systems or economies of truth through which power and history work beyond authors’ full control. **Ethnographic truths are inherently partial — committed and incomplete.** Crapanzano’s Hermes figure (promising not to lie, never undertaking to tell the whole truth) sharpens the oxymoron. The Cree hunter who hesitates under oath — “I can only tell what I know” — models artful speech under power.

## Semantic History

Title and thesis of Clifford’s Introduction; absorbed into later reflexive anthropology vocabulary; parallel to feminist and decolonial standpoint language of positioned knowledge. Distinct from simple relativism: Clifford denies that loss of Archimedean point means “anything goes.”

## Operationalizations

Epistemic and rhetorical analysis of ethnographies, not a measurement construct. Paired with Clifford’s six determinations of ethnographic writing (contextual, rhetorical, institutional, generic, political, historical).

## Applications

Programmatic for experimental ethnography and critiques of monographic omniscience; teaching monographs as positioned texts.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics charge that “partiality” can license relativism or undercut empirical standards; see [[writing-culture-debate]]. Defenders stress “true fictions” keeps both poles.
