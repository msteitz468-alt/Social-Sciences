# Material Culture

## Definition as Coined

"Material culture" long named simply the artefacts and physical remains studied by
archaeology and museum anthropology — objects understood as passive reflections or
by-products of a society's culture and technology.

## Semantic History

The **material culture studies** current of the 1980s (associated with
[[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]], Daniel Miller, Christopher Tilley, and colleagues)
transformed the term (attributed): objects are not passive reflections of society
but **active** in constituting social relations — material culture is
"meaningfully constituted" and manipulated by people, and can be "read" as a kind of
text or engaged as a medium of practice. On Johnson's account this reconception is a
hinge between processual treatments of artefacts as functional/adaptive data and
postprocessual treatments of them as symbolically active.

## Operationalizations

Ranges from the processual measurement of artefacts as adaptive or economic
indicators, through Hodder's contextual "reading" of objects as symbols in action,
to phenomenological and **[[entanglement-theory|entanglement]]** approaches to the
human–thing relation.

## Applications

Ethnoarchaeology and **[[ethnoarchaeology|material culture studies]]**; contextual
archaeology; studies of style, exchange, consumption, and identity; the archaeology
of the recent and contemporary past.

### Deetz 1996 — historical archaeology as American material culture

*(Attributed — [[deetz-james|Deetz]], *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]*, 1996.)* Deetz defines material culture broadly as that sector of the physical environment modified by culturally determined behavior (including cut landscapes, not only portable artifacts). He links material form to **cognition / worldview** (challenge to purely adaptive processual tenets) and treats HA as American material culture in historical perspective. Multi-domain program: ceramics, houses, gravestones, refuse, furniture, music. African American material culture read via **[[creolization]]** (rules of combination may be African American even when artifacts are Anglo-made). Closing claim: material culture is the most objective/immediate historical source because it was not written to persuade posterity. Intellectual debt to [[glassie-henry|Glassie]] made explicit.

## Critiques and Limitations

Debates persist over whether objects can literally be "read" as text, over how far
material "agency" extends, and over the boundary between symbolic and functional
analysis. The status of material culture as evidence for meaning versus behaviour is
a standing methodological question.

## Shanks & Tilley 1987 (critical charter)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]*, Ch. 4 and Appendix §3.)*

Shanks & Tilley treat material culture as archaeology's primary object and as **signifying practice**, not a mute residue later infused with present consciousness. They critique:

- **Traditional archaeology:** types as mental templates / ethnicity-reflecting styles (Krieger, Rouse, Gifford, Chang as they cite) — meaning as direct reflection of culture and ideas.
- **New archaeology style/function packages:** Binford techno-socio-ideo classes; ceramic sociology; Dunnell style/function dichotomy; Wobst social signalling; Sackett isochrestism — in all of which function is privileged over style, or style is explained only by social function, while **specificity of form** (why *this* design) remains unexplained; they conclude "function is virtually redundant" as an explanatory concept for particularity.

**Positive program (attributed):** material culture forms a set of resources, a **symbolic order in practice**, drawn on in political relations and activated in ideological systems — **actively involved in the social world**. It signifies relationships between groups and relations of power more than people–nature; meaning is lateral and contextual, not one-to-one codes. Recovery of "original" prehistoric meaning is not the goal; the archaeologist **translates** the past into a fresh constellation; the past still speaks in signifying residues of its social texture. Cases they discuss include Braithwaite on Azande pots, late Neolithic Wessex beakers, Hodder *Symbols in Action*, and their own tomb studies — as illustrations of active mediation, not as findings to re-assert in wiki voice.

## Shanks & Tilley 1987 — *Re-Constructing Archaeology* (practice charter)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]*, Chs. 6–8; companion densification to STA above.)*

**Object of social archaeology (Ch. 6):** material culture-patterning in relation to social practices, strategies, and structures; MC as **objectification** within social strategies, not passive reflection of culture or system.

**Style (Ch. 7):** seven conventional accounts (normative type-fossil / pots=people; stylistic drift; regional adaptation; social interaction; motor habits; information exchange; isochrestic variation) rejected as inadequate for specificity of design. Alternative: style as social/signifying practice at three levels — (i) mediation of habituated social consciousness; (ii) restructuring of social reality in material form; (iii) insertion of **ideology** (imaginary resolution of social contradiction). **Case (attributed single analysis):** ~70 middle Neolithic TRB funnel-neck beakers (~5.6% of ~1,256 vessels; ~14k sherds) outside passage grave Fjälkinge No. 9, southern Sweden — generative design rules (bounded/unbounded), temporal phases, six spatial regions at the entrance, dramaturgical reading of tomb space; ideology of boundedness vs non-closure. Not multi-study findings in wiki voice.

**Modern material culture (Ch. 8):** British vs Swedish beer-can design (1980s sample; multi-variable PCA/PCO) under political economy of drink, disciplinary power, medicalization, advertising — design as ideological mediation of consumption vs social constraint. Context of the artifact is **dispersal** (production, advertising, law, media…), not a fixed site context. Authors deny Chs. 7–8 are decisive empirical "tests" of the theory chapters.

## Johnson's textbook synthesis (2011)

*(Attributed — Matthew Johnson, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological Theory: An Introduction]], 2011.)* Johnson describes "material culture studies" as an interdisciplinary field in which ethnoarchaeological findings pushed archaeology and anthropology toward convergence, exemplified by the interdisciplinary *Journal of Material Culture*, with deep roots in nineteenth-century ethnographic collecting (the Pitt Rivers Museum, the Smithsonian) and early-modern antiquarianism. Johnson reports two ethnoarchaeological studies illustrating why material culture cannot be read without cultural context: Henrietta Moore's study of the Marakwet (East Africa), which found domestic-compound spatial layout reflected gender ideology (specific areas culturally coded "male" or "female"); and [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]'s study of Nuba compounds, which found markedly different quantities of pig bones across compounds, attributed to differing local beliefs about female "pollution" — Hodder argued an archaeologist would need ethnographic knowledge of Nuba belief to correctly interpret the faunal remains. Johnson also records postprocessual archaeologists' analogy of material culture to text (polysemic, manipulable, no final reading, meaning independent of "author" intention), while noting that many later postprocessual archaeologists argued this textual analogy is fundamentally limited because material things are functional and non-arbitrary in ways words are not — shifting emphasis toward "materiality," "understanding," and "experience."

## The founding formulation — *Reading the Past* (Hodder & Hutson 2003)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]], chs. 1–2, 8, ingested 2026-07-10.)* Hodder & Hutson trace their thesis to three ideas from Hodder's *Symbols in Action* ethnoarchaeology: material culture is **meaningfully constituted**; **agency** must be built into any theory of material culture and social change; and archaeology's closest ties are to history. Because *all* material culture has a symbolic dimension, they argue the core problem is not "how do we study symbolism in the past?" but "**how do we do archaeology at all?**" — objects are "meaningfully constituted," so their meaning aspects are "irreducible" and cannot be predicted from an environmental or economic base. They hold that "there is no direct, universal cross-cultural relationship between behaviour and material culture" (even inferring a hunting economy from bone percentages assumes attitudes to animals, bones, and waste), and refine the text analogy into **[[reading-material-culture|text as metaphor, not analogy]]** (adopting [[peirce-charles-sanders|Peirce]]'s icon/index/symbol over Saussure's arbitrary sign).

## Insoll 1999 — Muslim material culture as structured diversity

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Timothy Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]*.)* Extends material-culture analysis into a living world religion: faith-specific material signatures should exist cross-culturally (city form to seal ring), yet **ideal suites** rarely appear complete and exceptions (alcohol, grave goods, non-courtyard houses, misaligned mosques) are expected to outweigh pure orthodoxy. Material culture is socially constituted and non-static — a contemporary Bradford mosque belongs with the Dome of the Rock inside Islamic material culture. “Art of Muslims” is preferred over essentialized “Islamic art”; style must not be privileged over fabric/scientific analysis; trade goods and technology transfer are part of the same package as ritual architecture. Developmental thesis: Islamic civilization’s material package was not laid out fully fledged at conquest (Spain/Córdoba lag; possible ninth-century consolidation).
