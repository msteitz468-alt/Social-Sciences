# The Production of Locality

The organizing argument of *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at
Large]]*'s final chapter. Attributed to [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] throughout.
Distinct from [[locale]] (Giddens): Appadurai's locality is a **relational,
phenomenological property**, not a setting.

## Definition as Coined

Appadurai's load-bearing distinction, verbatim: "I view **locality** as primarily
**relational and contextual rather than as scalar or spatial**. I see it as a
complex phenomenological quality, constituted by a series of links between the
sense of social immediacy, the technologies of interactivity, and the relativity
of contexts… In contrast, I use the term **neighborhood** to refer to the actually
existing social forms in which locality, as a dimension or value, is variably
realized. Neighborhoods… are situated communities characterized by their
actuality, whether spatial or virtual, and their potential for social
reproduction" *(Appadurai 1996)*.

## Semantic History

Informed by, but distinguished from, Henri Lefebvre's "production of space."
Appadurai rejects the "grand cliché of social theory (going back to Tönnies,
Weber, and Durkheim)" that locality merely comes *under siege* in modernity:
locality is "an inherently fragile social achievement" even in the most intimate
premodern settings — "ephemeral unless hard and regular work is undertaken to
produce and maintain its materiality."

## Operationalizations

- **Rites of passage** ([[van-gennep-arnold|van Gennep]]), house-building,
  path/garden-making, and territory-mapping reread as "complex social techniques
  for the inscription of locality onto bodies" — techniques that produce "local
  subjects."
- **"Context-generative"** (coinage): neighborhoods "both are contexts and at the
  same time require and produce contexts"; the ongoing action of local subjects
  "produces the context of neighborhoods," changing the conditions of locality
  production (converging with [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s conjunctural change).
- **"Virtual neighborhoods"** and **"translocalities"** (coinages): electronically
  mediated and tourism-produced neighborhoods that feed back into lived ones.

## Applications

The Yanomami under the Brazilian/Venezuelan states (a neighborhood becoming
"prisoners in the context-producing activities of the nation-state"); refugee camps
and urban warfare ([[global-cultural-flows|Karachi]], Sarajevo, Mogadishu) as
extreme, "context-produced rather than context-generative" sites; the electronic
production of locality in diaspora. Closing formula: **"Locality is thus fragile in
two senses"** — against material entropy ("the corrosion of context") and against
"the context-producing drives of… the modern nation-state," the two related through
[[state-formation]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The locality/neighborhood distinction is powerful but abstract; critics note the
difficulty of operationalizing "locality as a structure of feeling" empirically,
and that the framework's stress on production can understate the durability of
place.
