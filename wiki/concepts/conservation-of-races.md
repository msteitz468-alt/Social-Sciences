# Conservation of Races

## Definition as Coined

In the American Negro Academy paper **“The Conservation of Races”** (1897; reprinted in *[[du-bois-essays|Du Bois: Essays]]*), [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]] defines a **race** as:

> “a vast family of human beings, generally of common blood and language, always of common history, traditions and impulses, who are both voluntarily and involuntarily striving together for the accomplishment of certain more or less vividly conceived ideals of life.”

**Conservation** names the duty of Americans of Negro descent to maintain race identity, solidarity, and organizations until the Negro people have delivered their full “spiritual message” to civilization and human brotherhood becomes practical possibility — not absorption into white America or “servile imitation of Anglo-Saxon culture,” but “stalwart originality” following Negro ideals.

All claims below are **attributed** to Du Bois (1897 Academy paper) unless otherwise marked.

## Operationalizations

This is a **program concept**, not a metric:

- **Definitional move:** physical criteria (color, hair, cranial measures, language) are “exasperatingly intermingled”; physical science reduces at most to two or three great families with likenesses greater than unlikenesses (Du Bois cites Huxley, Ratzel, Darwin’s brotherhood doctrine as he reads them).
- **Historical taxonomy:** eight races “in the sense in which History tells us the word must be used” (Slavs, Teutons, English of Britain and America, Romance nations, Negroes of Africa and America, Semitic peoples, Hindoos, Mongolians) — historical-sociological, not Blumenbach’s five-race scheme.
- **Deeper differences:** “spiritual, psychical… based on the physical, but infinitely transcending them.”
- **Academy Creed (seven points, attributed):** unique Negro contribution; maintain race identity until mission done; two races can develop side by side in political/economic/religious harmony; “social equilibrium” (judge culture, ability, moral worth regardless of skin) rather than social equality that disregards likes/dislikes; first step on the Negro Problem is correcting immorality, crime, and laziness as slavery’s heritage among Negroes; second step is more impartial selection of ability and respect for liberty by whites; resolve for strong manhood, pure womanhood, and race ideal in America and Africa.
- **Institutions called for:** Negro colleges, newspapers, business organizations, school of literature and art, and an intellectual clearing house — the **Negro Academy**.

## Applications

- **Dual identity:** “Am I an American or am I a Negro? Can I be both?” — civic Americanism (birth, citizenship, political ideals, language, religion) stops short of full cultural absorption; at that point “we are Negroes.” Prefigures and feeds [[double-consciousness]].
- **Race prejudice** defined as friction of differing group ideals; under shared law, language, and religion with economic adjustment, plural national ideals may thrive in one country.
- **Economic sequel:** *[[du-bois-essays|Economic Revolution in the South]]* (c. 1906) develops **group economy** — cooperative closed circle of Black-serving industry and service — as a practical conservation of economic independence under the color-line.
- **Leadership:** Academy and [[talented-tenth]] programs both center educated race organization; not identical but mutually reinforcing in Du Bois’s classical-era writing.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Race as real historical unit** remains contested; Du Bois’s eight-race list and “spiritual message” can be read as reifying groups he elsewhere shows are internally indefinite (“Negro” the most indefinite term).
- **Moral-uplift / vice language** (disease, crime, sexual impurity as race dangers the elite must fight) is period self-reform rhetoric — record as attributed Academy program, not wiki-voice pathology of Black populations.
- **Gender:** “pure womanhood” / guarded purity framing is of its moment.
- **Production context:** Paper for an elite race Academy; program is deliberately advance-guard, not mass ethnography.
