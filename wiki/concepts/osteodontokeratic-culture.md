# Osteodontokeratic culture

An **osteodontokeratic** (Greek: bone–tooth–horn) culture is a proposed tool tradition made from animal skeletal material rather than stone. The term is [[dart-raymond|Raymond Dart]]'s (1949, elaborated in his 1957 monograph) for what he claimed was the tool kit of the [[australopithecus|australopithecines]] at [[makapansgat|Makapansgat]] and [[sterkfontein|Sterkfontein]], South Africa.

## Definition as Coined

Dart argued that the bone accumulations in the australopithecine deposits were a deliberately manufactured and selected assemblage: long-bone splinters produced by a "crack-and-twist" technique served as points and daggers, mandibles as saws/choppers, "skull bowls" as containers, horn cores as weapons. He combined two claims (per [[binford-bones-1981|Binford 1981]]): (a) morphological modifications on bones were referable to hominid action, and (b) the differential frequencies of anatomical parts reflected selective transport of useful parts to a hominid living place. Because stone tools were largely absent from his sites, the bone tools were made to carry the "tool criterion" for behavioral (protohuman) status — and, in Dart's reading, evidence of a predatory, "blood-bespattered" hominid nature (see [[hunting-hypothesis|the hunting/killer-ape hypothesis]]).

## Semantic History

The construct shifted from a claimed **culture** (a real tool tradition) to a standard **negative example** in method — a byword for reading behavior off a bone assemblage without independent warrant. This inversion is the concept's chief significance today: it is cited far more often as the paradigm of a discredited inference than as a real archaeological entity.

## Operationalizations

Dart operationalized it through part-frequency counts and a typology of bone modifications. The decisive counter-operationalization was [[actualistic-studies|actualistic]]: [[brain-ck|C.K. Brain]]'s study of goat bones scavenged by dogs around Hottentot villages showed that Makapansgat's part-frequency profile could be produced by carnivore ravaging and differential [[taphonomy|preservation]] alone, with no hominid selection required — and that bone density predicts which parts survive. [[binford-lewis|Binford]] (1981) independently argued that the modifications Dart read as tool manufacture (spiral fracture, chipped-back edges, pointed splinters) are routinely produced by carnivore gnawing and trampling.

## Applications

Beyond South Africa, comparable bone-tool-before-stone claims were made for [[zhoukoudian|Choukoutien]] (Breuil; Weidenreich's associated cannibalism claim) and various European and New World sites — all subject to the same taphonomic critique. The concept therefore anchors the broader argument that **material association is not behavioral integration**.

## Critiques and Limitations

The construct is regarded as **obsolete as a claim about the past** while remaining important as a case in the history of method. Brain and Binford's taphonomic reanalyses are the standard refutation; Dart's Taung/*Australopithecus africanus* identification (a separate claim about anatomy, not behavior) stands independently of the osteodontokeratic hypothesis. The episode is a founding case of [[taphonomy]] and of the [[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting-versus-scavenging debate]].
