# Clinical Reality

[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s term (coined in *[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and
Healers]]*, 1980) for the socially and symbolically constructed context that constitutes sickness and
clinical care — and which is, in turn, "clinically constructed."

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman states: "I have coined a new term, clinical reality, to designate the socially constituted
contexts that influence illness and clinical care." His formal (Figure 2) definition (verbatim):

> "'Clinical Reality' = The beliefs, expectations, norms, behaviors, and communicative transactions
> associated with sickness, health care seeking, practitioner-patient relationships, therapeutic
> activities, and evaluation of outcomes. The social reality that expresses and constitutes clinical
> phenomena and which itself is clinically constructed."

The mixed image is deliberate: "clinical phenomena are socially constituted and… the social world can be
clinically constructed."

## Semantic History

Built explicitly on Berger and Luckmann's *The Social Construction of Reality* (1967) — itself grounded
in Alfred Schutz — and translated to medicine via Eliot Freidson (1970); Foucault's *Birth of the Clinic*
is cited as a "historical reconstruction of clinical reality." Kleinman distinguishes social reality (the
locus of clinical reality) from psychological, biological, and physical realities, bridged by **symbolic
reality** ("formed by the individual's acquisition of language and systems of meaning").

## Operationalizations

Each sector of the [[health-care-system]] "creates its own clinical reality," as does each healing
profession within the professional sector. Because these frames are tacit ("personal knowledge," after
Polanyi), discrepancies between a patient's and practitioner's clinical realities "are usually hidden,"
producing non-compliance, dissatisfaction, and inadequate care that go unrecognized — "That is why it is
so difficult to see clinical reality the way others see it." Which clinical reality prevails "when
differing views… are in conflict" is decided by "the sources of legitimation and power impinging on the
health care system" (Giddens's triad of meaning, norms, power).

## Applications

- **Healing as transformation of clinical reality.** In Kleinman's Taiwan *[[tang-ki]]* study, the
  patient "Mr. Chen's illness was healed by the cultural construction of clinical reality" — not by the
  shaman as such but by the fit between a sanctioned illness form and its sanctioned care (see
  [[symbolic-healing]]).
- **The interpretive problem of care.** Divergent clinical realities across sectors are why the
  [[explanatory-models]] framework is needed to make cross-sector care intelligible.
- In the Epilogue Kleinman elevates the concept to a discipline-defining object: "the core clinical
  tasks… and the clinical construction of reality provide medical anthropology with an autonomous subject
  matter."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is a strong constructionist claim, and Kleinman guards it against being read as denying
biological reality — clinical reality "relat[es] as well to psychobiological and physical realities." Its
power-and-legitimation dimension gestures toward, but does not fully develop, the political economy that
[[critical-medical-anthropology]] foregrounds. As an etic analytical term it risks circularity (clinical
phenomena construct, and are constructed by, clinical reality) that Kleinman treats as a productive
dialectic rather than a defect.
