# Performance Idealization

## Definition as Coined

[[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (*[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, 1956) argued performances present through their front abstract claims and are **socialized** — moulded to fit societal understanding and expectations. Performers tend to offer an **idealized** impression: incorporating and exemplifying officially accredited values of the society more than the individual's behaviour as a whole (citing Cooley). To the degree a performance highlights official values, one may look upon it (in the manner of Durkheim and Radcliffe-Brown) as a **ceremony** — expressive rejuvenation of moral values. "The world, in truth, is a wedding" (attributed).

Idealization includes **upward** mobility via maintenance of front and **downward** idealization (underplaying wealth, strength, or self-respect for systematic reasons — e.g. forced racial performance, rent-avoiding under-improvement, junk dealers' "down and out" myth).

**Concealments** supporting idealization (attributed): secret consumption; profitable side-activities incompatible with official view; correction of errors before showtime; showing only finished products; "dirty work" (Hughes); sacrifice of some ideals to sustain more visible ones; rhetoric of training and ideal motives for role acquisition.

Related: **[[audience-segregation|audience segregation]]** fosters the illusion that the current routine is the only or most essential one; personalized "unique" treatment of the current audience obscures routine character.

## Semantic History

Idealization in Goffman ≠ psychoanalytic idealization; nearer to ceremonial accent of official values. Later "brand idealization" is a partial descendant.

## Operationalizations

Compare official values enacted in front regions with backstage practices; inventory concealed dirty work and secret consumption.

## Applications

Class mobility and status symbols; professional licensing fronts; domestic hospitality; colonial/service racial performances as historical illustrations (period language). Links: [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Examples involving race and gender are mid-century materials; analytical claim is about systematic under/over-playing of status, not endorsement of the performances described.
