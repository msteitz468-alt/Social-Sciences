# Ghinnāwa

## Definition as Coined

The **ghinnāwa** (pl. *ghinnāwāt*) is the short oral lyric form Abu-Lughod calls the **poetry of personal life** among [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]]: individuals recite poems in specific (often private) social contexts, articulating sentiments about personal situations and close relationships. Typical form is roughly **fifteen syllables in two hemistiches**; recited order can reverse when sung. Poems are formulaic and traditional — drawn from a cultural repertoire of themes, metaphors, and structures — yet creatively recombined; among ~450 poems she recorded, few were duplicates. Meaning, she argues, is inseparable from **context of recitation**, not only from text. Self and beloved are named through a specialized lexicon (*ʿēn*, *ʿagl*, *khāṭr*; *ʿazīz*, *ʿalam*, *awlāf*) with a dysphoric bias (tears, wounds, despair, separation).

## Semantic History

Related Libyan genre labels (e.g. *ṣōb khalīl*) and earlier collections (Falls 1908; Smart 1960s; Arabic Libyan folk-poetry studies) show continuity of form; several Falls poems match ones she heard ~75 years later. Performance occasions for competitive exchange (wells, weddings, sheep shearings, grain threshings, tattooing, *mijlās*) declined with sedentarization and sex segregation by the late 1970s — elders regretted that "there are no longer occasions for singing." Sister songs and *radd* (response) repartee remain compositional logics. Shearing (*jallāma*) poems substitute flock imagery for love terms while meaning love. Appendix: formula looser than Lord–Parry epic (little rhyme pressure; not rapid epic composition), yet conventional images and rare vocabulary function as formula.

## Operationalizations

- Recording in situ with attention to "Who said it?" and social company
- Pairing poem with life situation and ordinary-discourse statements about the same event
- Translation with poetic consultants (Mohammed B. Alwan et al.)
- Distinction of private intimate recitation vs. public or joking play

Load-bearing thesis: ghinnāwa sentiments systematically **disjoin** from ordinary honor talk — vulnerability vs. pride/anger/indifference — about loss, death, love, marriage, divorce, polygyny ([[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study]]).

## Applications

Core of *Veiled Sentiments*; anthropology of emotion; oral poetry as social action ([[linguistic-anthropology]]); feminist ethnography of women's expressive worlds. Abu-Lughod later worried that publishing context-bound poems and digitizing tapes violates privacy as family becomes audience (2016 afterword).

## Critiques and Limitations

Genre analysis depends on translator mediation and on the dual-discourse thesis. Not a general theory of all Arabic poetry. Authorship is weak (oral repertoire); "their" poems often recirculated.
