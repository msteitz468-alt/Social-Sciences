# Emotional Labor

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]* (1983), [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] coined **emotional labor** for labor that requires one "to induce or suppress feeling in order to sustain the outward countenance that produces the proper state of mind in others" — for the flight attendant, "the sense of being cared for in a convivial and safe place" (attributed). Footnote definition (attributed):

> I use the term *emotional labor* to mean the management of feeling to create a publicly observable facial and bodily display; emotional labor is sold for a wage and therefore has exchange value. I use the synonymous terms *emotion work* or *emotion management* to refer to these same acts done in a private context where they have use value.

Emotional labor calls for "a coordination of mind and feeling" and "sometimes draws on a source of self that we honor as deep and integral to our individuality" (attributed). Beneath the difference from physical labor, Hochschild argued a similarity of cost: the worker can become estranged from an aspect of self used to do the work — "either the body or the margins of the soul" (attributed).

**Three job criteria** (Ch 7; attributed): public-contact jobs most call for emotional labor when they (1) require face-to-face or voice-to-voice contact with the public; (2) require the worker to produce an emotional state in another person; (3) allow the employer to exercise a degree of control over the emotional activities of employees (via training and supervision). Professionals may meet two of three without full employer control of feeling.

**Polar cases:** the flight attendant (status enhancement, friendliness, care) and the bill collector (status degradation, distrust, anger) — "the toe and the heel of capitalism" (attributed).

Descends from [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s [[personality-market]] and great salesroom; from Marxian [[alienation]] of body-as-instrument; from [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] on [[impression-management]] — Hochschild argued Goffman under-theorizes deep acting and employer profit from display work (attributed).

## Operationalizations

- **Conceptual (Hochschild):** three criteria + Appendix C occupational tables on 1970 Census categories.
- **Estimates (attributed FINDING):** ~**38.1%** of all U.S. jobs (1970); ~**55.2%** of employed women; ~**27.7%** of employed men; "roughly one-third" of American workers and "roughly one-half" of working women in Ch 1 prose.
- **Later scales** (surface acting, deep acting, display-rule commitment) — post-1983 organizational literature; not in the primary text.

## Applications

- Airline service theater (Delta training; multi-carrier FA interviews) — primary empirical base of [[hochschild-managed-heart-study]].
- Debt collection as reverse emotional labor.
- Broader service: secretaries, wait staff, social workers, sales, funeral directors, ministers — "most of us have jobs that require some handling of other people's feelings" (attributed).
- Gender: women more often put private emotion-management skill on the market; status and [[status-shield|status shield]] asymmetries (Ch 8).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scope creep:** later uses of "emotional labor" for any unpaid care or any emotion at work dilute Hochschild's wage + employer-control criteria — she notes popularity with ambivalence (2012 preface, attributed).
- **Operationalization disputes:** organizational psychology often measures display rules and acting strategies without her three-criteria job filter.
- **Gender essentialism risk:** linking women to emotion management can be read as naturalizing rather than historicizing a division of labor — the book ties the pattern to subordination and market structure (attributed), but reception debates this.
- **Single-industry depth:** FA world is the deep case; bill collectors are a "mini project" (five interviews); student questionnaire is illustrative not probability-sample.
- `replication_status` of the founding study: [[hochschild-managed-heart-study|reanalyzed-contested]] — concept extended and remeasured, not laboratory-failed.
