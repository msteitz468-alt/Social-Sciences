# Kinshipping

[[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]]'s term (in *[[gamble-settling-the-earth-2013|Settling the Earth]]*, 2013) for the practice of **making kinship** beyond genetic relatedness — treating people as kin through shared concepts so that social networks can scale and persist when parties are not co-present (attributed, Gamble 2013 ch. 1; dialogue with anthropologist Alan Barnard). Paired with "keeping" as two concepts describing how people manage relationships and materials.

## Definition as Coined

Paired with **keeping**, kinshipping describes the human capacity to **[[going-beyond|go beyond]]** and create imaginary geographies: constructing relations **not based on genetic kinship** that nonetheless allow free movement between non-related groups (attributed, Gamble 2013 after Barnard 2011; Shryock, Trautmann & Gamble 2011). "Sometimes described as fictive kinship, the point is the ability to create relations outside the genetic pool by using shared concepts" (attributed footnote). Social life need not be face-to-face or genetic-only (attributed).

Gamble also links kinship to the **container** metaphor: nets, baskets, houses, and boats contain and constrain as kinship does; appearance of container-concept artefacts late in Terra 2 and dominating Terra 3 is offered as evidence for kinship’s archaeological visibility (attributed after Gamble 2008). Barnard’s universal kinship (original, then elaborated after agriculture) and Read’s shift from experiential interaction to relational roles/trust/obligation are cited supports (attributed, ch. 1).

## Semantic History

Draws on social-anthropological kinship theory (Barnard; Read; Hill et al. 2011 non-kin co-residence among FGH) and on Gamble's earlier network archaeology ([[intimate-effective-extended-networks]]). In 2013 it becomes a named hinge for Terra 3 global settlement — one of four breakthrough elements alongside storage, defence, and domestication (attributed, chs 6–7).

## Operationalizations

Not a single index; inferred from symbolic, exchange, residential, and container technologies of large-scale relatedness — always attributed when used. Ethnographic FGH universal kinship is a veneer to handle carefully for deep time (ch. 3).

## Applications

Supports the claim that global settlers needed social technologies of imagination, not only larger brains or better tools (attributed). Applied to Sahul (kinship for ocean and inland settlement), Pacific voyaging social reproduction, and Terra 5 reunions as re-kinshipping of a divided humanity (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

Term is authorial synthesis; classical kinship ethnography does not use "kinshipping" as a standard emic or etic category. Risk of projecting ethnographic kinship onto deep Palaeolithic contexts without bridging argument. Horizon: as of Gamble 2013.
