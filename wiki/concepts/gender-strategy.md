# Gender strategy

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]*, [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] defined a **gender strategy** as "a plan of action through which a person tries to solve problems at hand, given the cultural notions of gender at play" (Ch. 2, attributed). It adapts Ann Swidler's "strategies of action" to manhood/womanhood: connecting how one *thinks* about gender, what one *feels*, and what one *does*. "Strategy" includes **emotional preparation** — suppressing career ambition for children, hardening against a spouse's appeals, or allowing oneself to notice a child's need (attributed).

**Gender ideology** (two axes): preferred identity sphere (home vs work) and desired marital power (less, more, same). Three types:

| Type | Identity / power (ideal-typical) |
|---|---|
| **Traditional** | Woman identifies with home (even if employed); man with work; woman wants less power |
| **Egalitarian** | Same spheres and equal power (joint home, joint careers, or balanced) |
| **Transitional** | Woman wants both work and home; man supports her job but expects her lion's share at home — **most interviewees** |

Surface ideology can fracture from deep feelings (egalitarian "on top," traditional "underneath," or reverse). Ideology is fitted not only by upbringing but by matching personal assets to marriage and job markets in a [[stalled-revolution|stalled revolution]] (Ch. 2, attributed).

## Semantic History

Swidler (1986) on culture as toolkit for lines of action is the acknowledged source; Hochschild specializes it to gender and the domestic division of labor. The concept bridges cognitive gender-role attitudes and the microsociology of [[feeling-rules]] / [[emotion-work]] from *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*. Later family sociology often speaks of "gender strategies" without always citing the Swidler debt — keep the 1989 definition as the wiki anchor.

## Operationalizations

Not a survey scale in the book. Analytic indicators:

- Stated ideology vs observed task share
- Feeling-rule clashes (e.g., "you shall feel good about your wife working" vs anger at unavailability — Livingston case)
- Women's strategies: direct (showdown, crisis, prods, talks); indirect (playing helpless, illness signals, reframing, sex withdrawal); supermoming (~1/3 of mothers); cutting back work/house/marriage/self; seeking help; giving up leisure
- Men's resistance tactics: playing dumb / distracted tasks; waiting to be asked; substitute offerings; needs reduction; praising wife's organization
- Pathways to male sharing: **sharing showdown** (Shermans) vs **natural drift** (Art Winfield)

Hochschild argued Chodorow's mothering theory flattens within-gender variation; gender strategy explains differences among men and among women (Ch. 10, attributed).

## Applications

Every couple in *[[hochschild-second-shift-study|The Second Shift]]* is analyzed as an interplay of strategies. Actual sharing depends on both strategies plus outer circumstances and the [[economy-of-gratitude|economy of gratitude]] that "tips the balance" (Ch. 13, attributed). Over half of working mothers interviewed tried to change home roles (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Ideal types can reify; most people are transitional blends.
- "Strategy" risks implying fully conscious calculation; Hochschild stresses unconscious preparation and childhood cautionary tales.
- Heterosexual married sample with young children; other family forms under-specified in this source.

Where [[gender-strategy]] operates at the micro scale of the couple, [[connell-raewyn|Connell]]'s [[gender-order]] and [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]] analyze the macro-structure of gender relations within which such strategies are formed — the household division of labour is Connell's "production" structure seen up close.
