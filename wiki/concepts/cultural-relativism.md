# Cultural Relativism

## Definition as Coined

**Cultural relativism**, in the Boasian lineage, is the principle that cultures are to be understood in their own historical and meaningful terms, and that ranking them on a single civilizational scale (savage → civilized) is scientifically illegitimate. [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] established the **logic** in "The Limitations of the Comparative Method" (1896) and *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* (1911/1938) without making "cultural relativism" his trademark slogan; the **term** was popularized by students (notably Melville Herskovits). Do not equate the methodological rule with the stronger ethical claim that "anything goes" or that no cross-cultural judgment is ever possible — those are later extensions and polemical targets.

## Semantic History

Boasian usage was primarily **methodological and anti-hierarchical**: against Victorian evolutionism and against race–culture equations. Mid-century American anthropology taught relativism as professional ethics of fieldwork (suspend ethnocentrism). From the 1960s–present the term entered culture-war discourse as either a badge of anti-imperial tolerance or an accusation of moral nihilism; those polemical senses are **not** identical with Boas's scientific argument about mental equality and historical particularity. Related but distinct: **[[linguistic-relativity]]** (Sapir–Whorf), which *Mind* anticipates only partially in its language-and-thought discussion and which [[whorf-benjamin-lee|Whorf]] develops as a cognitive-structural thesis about grammar and [[habitual-thought|habitual thought]] (*[[whorf-language-thought-reality-1956|LTR]]*) — not as the ethical rule "anything goes." Do not collapse cultural and linguistic relativity; see [[linguistic-relativity-debate]].

## Operationalizations

- **Historical particularism:** reconstruct each culture's history before comparing forms ([[historical-particularism]]).
- **Ethnographic suspension of ranking:** describe first; withhold evolutionary grade labels.
- **Independence of race/language/culture:** refuse to read cultural "level" off of physical type ([[race-and-culture-debate]]).
- Later ethics codes (AAA) encode variants of non-harm and respect that draw on, but are not identical to, classical relativism.

## Applications

Underwrites Boasian ethnography, the pluralization of [[culture]], and the mainstream anthropological rejection of unilinear ladders ([[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). Used (and misused) in public debates on multiculturalism, human rights, and "primitive" vs. modern values.

[[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]]'s *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* (1934) was the most influential popular vehicle for relativism and pushed it furthest: from the incommensurability of whole [[cultural-configuration|configurations]] ("they are travelling along different roads… essentially they are incommensurable") to the [[cultural-relativity-of-normality|cultural relativity of the normal]] itself — the claim that even psychiatric categories of the abnormal are defined relative to a culture's selected [[the-great-arc-of-culture|arc]] of behaviour. Her closing plea framed relativity not as "a doctrine of despair" but as grounds for tolerance of "coexisting and equally valid patterns of life."

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue strong relativism blocks moral and political critique of harmful practices; that Boasian particularism under-produced general theory; that "understand first" was unevenly applied under colonial power. Defenders distinguish **methodological** relativism (context before judgment) from **normative** relativism (no judgment ever), and note Boas himself made strong scientific and political judgments against racism and for intellectual freedom (1938 Preface).

## Boas 1938 — Primary Logic (not the slogan)

In *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*, Boas never organizes the book under the heading "cultural relativism," but the methodological logic is pervasive:

1. **Do not rank cultures by proximity to Europe** — achievement gaps are historical (time, chance, contact, conquest), not proof of racial aptitude (Ch. 1).
2. **Interpret custom through tradition and association** — "primitive" belief is not failed logic (Chs. 11–12).
3. **Secondary explanations** — actors' reasons for customs are often post-hoc; the ethnographer reconstructs history of associations (Ch. 12).
4. **Equal opportunity caveat** — without equal conditions, hereditary comparisons of groups' cultural "level" are blocked (Ch. 8).

Herskovits and later textbooks named and ethicized this logic. Culture-war uses of "relativism" as moral nihilism are a further drift — record as POSITION when those sources are ingested.

The same posthumous-coinage point is reported for *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]* (1940): *(attributed — Seiferle-Valencia 2017)* Boas never organizes that collection under "cultural relativism" either — the term was coined by students after his 1942 death to synthesize the recurring methodological logic of the essays. The guide also stresses the standing distinction between **methodological** relativism (a scientific procedure for understanding difference) and **moral relativism** (the ethical claim that there is no objective right or wrong) — the latter a popular post-WWII misreading, not Boas's position.

## The Temporal-Distancing Critique (Fabian) — POSITION

[[fabian-johannes|Johannes Fabian]] (*[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other]]*, 1983 — attributed) argued that cultural relativism, whatever its anti-racist intentions, is one of the two main devices by which anthropology denies its subjects [[coevalness|shared present time]] — it "**circumvents**" coevalness by fencing each culture into its own Time. Borrowing Ernst Bloch's figure for Spengler's relativism, he called these bounded, mutually windowless wholes "**culture gardens**" — "culture souls without windows, with no links among each other, yet full of mirrors facing inside," giving the observer "the illusion of objectivity, coherence, and density." Studying a culture "from a distance" — normally a methodological vice against participant observation — thereby becomes a "theoretical virtue," and (in the WWII/Cold War "culture at a distance" work of Mead, Métraux, Benedict, and E. T. Hall) a tool of control. Fabian read even Benedict's incommensurable [[cultural-configuration|configurations]] ("travelling along different roads... essentially they are incommensurable") as a temporalizing move. On this view relativism does not escape the evolutionist "temporal slope" so much as break it into separated, coexisting-but-not-contemporary "Times." See [[allochronism]].
