# Accountability

**Accountability** is [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s term for the property whereby the
activities of everyday life are, as a matter of course, "observable-and-reportable" — visible
and describable to those who take part in them. In [[ethnomethodology]] the word does not mean
answerability or blame; it means that ordinary activities are organized so as to display their
own sense.

## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel defined ethnomethodological studies as analyses of "everyday activities as members'
methods for making those same activities **visibly-rational-and-reportable-for-all-practical-purposes, i.e., 'accountable**,'** as organizations of commonplace everyday activities." To be
accountable is to be "observable-and-reportable, i.e. available to members as situated
practices of looking-and-telling." He stressed that accountability is "an endless, ongoing,
contingent accomplishment," carried out by parties whose competence — their "skill with,
knowledge of, and entitlement to" the work — is presupposed and taken for granted.

## Semantic History

Garfinkel deliberately re-specified an ordinary English word. Where "accountable" normally
connotes moral or bureaucratic answerability, his usage is analytic and near-perceptual:
an activity is accountable when it is done in ways that make it recognizable *for what it is*.
The term is bound to [[reflexivity-of-accounts|the reflexivity of accounts]] and to
[[indexicality]] — accounts render activities observable while being, themselves, features of
those activities.

A major migration of the term is [[west-candace|West]] and [[zimmerman-don-h|Zimmerman]]'s
use in the [[doing-gender]] program (1987). Drawing on Heritage's reading of ethnomethodology,
they treat accountability as members' orientation to how activities might look and be
characterized — including actions made *unremarkable* because they accord with approved
standards. Grafted onto [[sex-category|sex category]], accountability becomes the claim that
to "do" gender is to engage in behavior *at the risk of gender assessment*, with the idiom of
assessment drawn from institutional arenas. West and Fenstermaker later extend the same hinge
to race and class ([[doing-difference]]; contested in [[doing-difference-debate]]). This
gender/race/class use reintroduces ordinary-language "answerability" tones that Garfinkel had
bracketed — a semantic drift that is productive for inequality analysis and a source of
misreadings when Garfinkel's and West–Zimmerman's senses are collapsed.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Its load-bearing claim is an identity: "the activities whereby members produce
and manage settings of organized everyday affairs are **identical with** members' procedures
for making those settings 'account-able.'" Producing social order and making it accountable
are, on Garfinkel's account, one activity, not two.

## Applications

Accountability organizes every study in the 1967 volume: the [[breaching-experiment|breaching demonstrations]]
work because breaches make ordinarily unnoticed accountable order suddenly and troublingly
visible; the [[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes]] analysis treats "normal" sexed conduct as made
"accountable — for all practical purposes" through witnessed talk and conduct.

In the [[doing-gender]] program (see [[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study]] and
[[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]]), accountability is the load-bearing
mechanism across empirical applications: household labor allocation (Fenstermaker, *Gender
Factory*); cosmetic-surgery candidate screening (Dull & West); UC Regents affirmative-action
deliberations (West & Fenstermaker); and California second-parent adoption practice (Dalton &
Fenstermaker).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's strength — dissolving the boundary between doing an activity and describing it —
is also the source of the charge that ethnomethodology cannot distinguish a warranted
sociological description from members' own talk (see
[[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]]). It has nonetheless become a durable
analytic term, especially in conversation analysis and studies of work.
