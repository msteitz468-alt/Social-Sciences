# Threshold of Shame

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Elias, *The Civilizing Process*.)* The **threshold of shame** (and related thresholds of repugnance/delicacy/embarrassment) marks what a social standard treats as embarrassing, disgusting, or unmentionable. Elias argues that in the Western civilizing process this threshold has advanced: acts once performed publicly or without comment (certain table practices, bodily functions, bedroom arrangements, open aggression) become privatized, medicalized, or heavily restrained, and children must learn the advanced standard in a short socialization span.

## Semantic History

Pairs shame (socially instilled fear of others' loss of respect) with repugnance (visceral disgust). Distinct from later psychological trait measures of "shame-proneness"; Elias's construct is socio-historical.

## Operationalizations

Comparative reading of manners books across centuries on the same occasions (table, nose-blowing, spitting, bedroom, sexual relations, aggression); noting what becomes unspeakable or relegated to "private" space.

## Applications

Part Two documentary series of *[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*; theory of how [[self-constraint]] and super-ego formation intensify as thresholds rise.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether the direction is universal or Western-specific; decivilizing reverse movements; class and gender skew of sources (see [[civilizing-process-debate]]).
