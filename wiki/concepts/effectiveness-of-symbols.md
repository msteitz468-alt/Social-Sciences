# The Effectiveness of Symbols

## Definition as Coined

**The effectiveness of symbols** (*l'efficacité symbolique*, "symbolic efficacy") is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] thesis (essay of 1949, collected in *[[levi-strauss-structural-anthropology-1958|Structural Anthropology]]*) that purely **symbolic means** — a song, a myth, a ritual — can produce real physiological and psychological effects. His case study is a **Cuna (Kuna, Panama) shamanic song for difficult childbirth**: the shaman never touches the woman or gives a remedy, yet effects a cure by leading protective spirits on a mythical journey through "Muu's way" (identified in the text with the birth canal) to recover the patient's captured soul.

## Semantic History

The mechanism, in Lévi-Strauss's words: "the shaman provides the sick woman with a language, by means of which unexpressed, and otherwise inexpressible, psychic states can be immediately expressed. And it is the transition to this verbal expression... which induces the release of the physiological process." The cure works because a **symbolic structure homologous** to the bodily disorder reorganizes it — an "inductive property" relating "formally homologous structures, built out of different materials at different levels of life — organic processes, unconscious mind, rational thought." He drew an explicit analogy to psychoanalytic abreaction, with an inversion: "in the schizophrenic cure the healer performs the actions and the patient produces his myth; in the shamanistic cure the healer supplies the myth and the patient performs the actions."

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed where cure proceeds through the symbol/meaning relation (internal to mind) rather than the germ/disease relation (biomedical). The companion essay "The Sorcerer and His Magic" adds the **shamanistic complex** — the interdependence of the shaman's own experience, the patient's, and the group's collective faith, "a sort of gravitational field": "Quesalid did not become a great shaman because he cured his patients; he cured his patients because he had become a great shaman."

## Applications

A founding text of the anthropology of healing, ritual, and the placebo/mind-body relation; it shaped medical anthropology, studies of shamanism and spirit-mediumship, and psychoanalytic anthropology. It complements Lévi-Strauss's theory of [[myth]]: the shaman deploys a socially given myth as a therapeutic language.

## Critiques and Limitations

The reading rests on a single text whose philology (Holmer & Wassén) Lévi-Strauss extends interpretively; later scholars questioned whether Cuna women actually understand the archaic song, undercutting the "provides a language" mechanism (Carlo Severi and others). Michel Leiris criticized the equation of sorcerer and neurotic. The homology between symbolic and organic structures is asserted more than demonstrated. Record as an influential but contested thesis of [[structuralism]], attributed to Lévi-Strauss.
