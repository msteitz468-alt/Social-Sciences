# Potlatch

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (1925), [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] defines **potlatch** analytically as the **agonistic type of [[total-prestation|total prestation]]**: the whole clan, through its chiefs, contracts involving all members and everything it possesses, under a spirit of rivalry and antagonism — "essentially usurious and extravagant… a struggle among nobles to determine their position in the hierarchy." Pure destruction of wealth (slaves, oil, coppers, houses) aims to eclipse a rival and also sacrifices to gods/spirits.

Ethnographic home base for the type: Northwest Coast (Tlingit, Haida, Kwakiutl/Kwakwaka'wakw, Tsimshian, etc., via Boas and others). Mauss notes Chinook **potlatch** means "gift." He calls NW American potlatch the "**monster child** of the gift system," parallel to Trobriand [[kula]] as extreme gift exchange.

As **[[total-social-phenomenon]]**: "more than a legal phenomenon… religious, mythological and shamanistic… economic… also a phenomenon of social morphology."

## Operationalizations

Mauss's indicators: competitive giving with interest (return making creditor into debtor); prestige bound to expenditure; destruction without equivalent return as ultimate display; rank, marriage, and seating settled through potlatch given and returned; debt sanction including loss of rank / debt slavery (compared to Roman *nexum*).

## Applications (as Mauss uses the type)

- **NW America:** honour and credit; war of wealth; *Renommiergeld* / money of renown (coppers); Property Woman and animate valuables.
- **Comparative stretch:** potlatch *elements* in Melanesia; "traces" in Indo-European materials (Thrace, Hindu epics as "tremendous potlatch," Germanic feasting) — these are **attributed reconstructions**, often contested by specialists.
- **Modern analogies (Conclusions):** aristocratic invitation-return; always return more than received — "traces of the traditional basis, the aristocratic potlatch."
- **Blau / exchange theory (secondhand on vault):** potlatch as establishing superordination through unilateral generosity (see [[gift-exchange]] Applications).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Type inflation:** reading Mahabharata or Lorraine hospitality as potlatch risks metaphoric overreach.
- **Secondary ethnography:** Boas-era sources; colonial violence and potlatch prohibition shape the record.
- **Destruction vs distribution:** popular image of pure waste underplays redistribution and credit functions Mauss himself stresses.
- Identity caution: potlatch is **not** a people; it is a practice/type associated with multiple Northwest Coast societies.

## The Boas primary record (1897/1966, attributed)

From *[[boas-kwakiutl-ethnography-1966|Kwakiutl Ethnography]]* (ed. Codere 1966), the Kwakiutl mechanics as [[boas-franz|Boas]] recorded them in the 1880s–90s: the **single blanket (50¢)** as unit of value; **term-scaled loan interest** (≈25% over months, 100% at a year); **name-pawning** for the credit-poor ("selling a slave": borrow 30, repay 100); a male career of names (birth name → boy's distribution name at ~10–12 → potlatch name), each validated by distribution, the father ceding his seat before a council of elders. **Coppers** functioned "the same function which bank notes of high denominations have with us" — named instruments whose value was the property last given for them (Fort Rupert 1893 list headed by Ma'xts'ólem, "all other coppers are ashamed to look at it," 7,500 blankets; its winter 1894–95 sale is transcribed in full, bid to 4,200 blankets); breaking a copper, or refusing the grease-feast spoon and breaking one's own, escalated rivalry (*p'a'sa*, "flattening" a rival's name). [[hunt-george|Hunt]]'s manuscript "The Order of a Chief's Potlatches" fixes a nine-step sequence "to be walked by true chiefs," including paying the marriage debt (a case witnessed by Boas at Fort Rupert). Boas's framing: wealth mattered as "the ability to give great festivals"; the famous rivalry idiom is an informant historicization — "nowadays... rivals fight with property only." Interpretations diverge from this shared record: see [[potlatch-interpretation-debate]] (Boas's credit system, Mauss's total prestation, Benedict's Dionysian ethos, [[codere-helen|Codere]]'s post-contact escalation thesis).

## The archaeological potlatch (Fagan 1991, attributed)

[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]] approaches the potlatch from the
**[[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition|Northwest Coast]] archaeological** side rather than
Mauss's comparative theory. Ceremonial feasts of controlled redistribution by wealthy
individuals in the Middle Period, he suggests, "may have been" the ancestors of the historic
potlatch — while noting such personages are very hard to identify archaeologically. In the
Croes & Hackenberger model, **ritualized redistribution at feasts (potlatches)** is read as an
*adaptive mechanism* for buffering food-supply fluctuation, emerging from territorial ownership
and differential resource access rather than from prior centralized authority — a functional-
ecological framing distinct from Mauss's honour-and-rank agonistics. Fagan also notes the
material record here is thin: potlatch mechanics are known ethnographically, not excavated.
