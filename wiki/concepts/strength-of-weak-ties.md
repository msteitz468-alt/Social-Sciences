# Strength of Weak Ties

Core concept and catchphrase of [[granovetter-mark-s|Mark Granovetter]]'s 1973 argument: **weak interpersonal ties**, especially those that function as [[local-bridge|local bridges]], are indispensable for individual opportunity and for the integration of communities — contrary to traditions that treat only strong, primary ties as sociologically central.

> For the theory page (core claims, origins, critiques, status), see [[strength-of-weak-ties-theory]]. This concept page tracks the **idea and its semantic career**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Granovetter 1973.)* Starting from the definition of [[tie-strength]] and the hypothesis that friendship-circle overlap rises with tie strength, Granovetter derived that the triad of two strong ties plus an absent third is rare ("forbidden"). Therefore strong ties are almost never bridges, and **all bridges are weak ties**. Whatever is to be diffused — information, influence, job tips, organizational enthusiasm — can reach more people and traverse greater social distance when passed through weak ties than through strong ones. Paradox: strong ties breed local cohesion but overall fragmentation; weak ties, often cast as alienating (Wirth 1938), enable opportunity and community integration.

## Operationalizations

- Job contact frequency (rarely/occasionally/often) as strength proxy.
- Small-world "acquaintance" vs "friend" chain completion.
- Sociometric reach by friend rank-order (Rapoport & Horvath).
- Community-level: count or density of local bridges (proposed; rarely measured in early tests).

## Applications

- Labor-market information and [[social-mobility|mobility opportunity]] — full design in [[granovetter-getting-a-job-study]] (*Getting a Job* 1974); frequency skew and short chains among Newton PTM men.
- Diffusion of innovations and rumors.
- Interracial and cross-group contact (Milgram/Korte chains).
- Neighborhood mobilization capacity ([[west-end-network-fragmentation-debate]]).
- Later: organizational knowledge transfer; Afterword 1994 conditionality (strong ties in some institutional settings; inequality of returns to contacts).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The hole, not the weakness.** [[structural-holes|Burt (1992)]] accepts the empirical pattern but relocates its cause: "the causal agent … is not the weakness of a tie but the structural hole it spans." Tie weakness is a correlate; see [[structural-holes-theory]].
- **Necessary ≠ sufficient** for community organization (Gans 1974; Granovetter conceded).
- **Not all weak ties bridge**; non-bridging weak ties may belong with the dense sector analytically.
- **Outcome-conditional:** information novelty vs social support pull different tie strengths.
- **Class and culture:** Gans insisted network structure interacts with subcultural trust, historical precedent, and political timing.
- **Measurement under-sampling** of weak ties in classical sociometry.
- **Organizational contexts and compartmental strong ties (Small 2009).** *(Attributed — [[small-mario-luis|Small]], *[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*.)* Among urban mothers in childcare centers, close (strong) friendships were common (~68% of friend-makers reported a close friend), often as **compartmental intimates** (domain-focused around children) rather than pure weak bridges; support also flowed among nonintimates via organizationally structured trust. Organizational practices can produce and maintain strong ties under time constraints — a finding that conditions weak-tie expectations for this domain. See [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]].
