# Primary Group

## Definition as Coined

In the Introduction to *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]* (1902), [[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]] uses **"primary groups"** while defining one sense of "human nature": a social nature "developed in man by simple forms of intimate association or 'primary groups,' especially the family and neighborhood, which are found everywhere and everywhere work upon the individual in somewhat the same way." That nature consists chiefly of "certain primary social sentiments and attitudes, such as consciousness of one's self in relation to others, love of approbation, resentment of censure, emulation, and a sense of social right and wrong" (attributed).

**Coverage caution.** The 1902 book introduces the term definitionally. Cooley's **full classic treatment** is *Social Organization* (1909) — not yet ingested — where primary groups (family, play-group, neighborhood) are developed as the nursery of human nature and of ideals of democracy and justice. This page currently rests on the 1902 definitional use plus standard reception; update substantially when *Social Organization* is ingested.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Cooley 1902 | Intimate association (family, neighborhood) producing primary social sentiments; one definition of "human nature" |
| Cooley 1909 (*Social Organization*) | Full doctrine: primary groups as face-to-face, cooperative, relatively permanent associations that form the individual's social nature and moral ideals (standard textbook source) |
| Thomas & Znaniecki 1918 | Polish peasant **family-group** and community as primary-group organization baseline; solidarity, marriage-group isolation, letters as solidarity under separation (*[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|Polish Peasant]]* Vol I) |
| Park, *The City* (1925) | Urban life substitutes secondary for primary relations; primary-group moral order dissolves; machine politics try to preserve primary control |
| Mid-century sociology | Paired with "secondary groups" (impersonal, formal, segmental); community–society and Gemeinschaft–Gesellschaft analogues |
| Homans 1950 (*The Human Group*) | Operational research definition: persons who communicate often over time, few enough for face-to-face (not secondhand) contact; chance meetings excluded; group further defined by relative interaction frequency (Ch. 4) |
| Contemporary | Endures as micro-sociology vocabulary; often detached from Cooley's organic whole and moral-formation claims |

## Operationalizations

Typological rather than metric in Cooley. Later research operationalizes intimacy, multiplex ties, face-to-face frequency, emotional investment, and whole-person vs role-segmental relation. Network analysis sometimes treats "primary" as high-density, high-multiplex ego networks.

## Applications

- [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|Thomas & Znaniecki]] treat peasant family and community as the primary-group baseline that emigration and industry partially transform (Vol I organization; later volumes disorganization).
- Explains how [[looking-glass-self|looking-glass]] self-feeling and early conscience are stocked with particular others.
- Comparative and developmental sociology: family and peer groups as sites of socialization.
- Political and moral theory (in the 1909 extension): primary-group ideals as seeds of democratic and humanitarian standards.

- [[homans-george-c|Homans]] (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*) takes Cooley’s primary group as the unit for inductive [[small-group-theory|small-group theory]], adding interaction-count definition and the external/internal system analysis. Ch. 18 argues civilizations must preserve primary-group characteristics at scale or generate loneliness, sects, and collapse risk.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Romanticization risk.** Later critics argue the concept can idealize family and neighborhood and underplay conflict, power, and exclusion within "intimate" groups.
- **Historical specificity.** "Neighborhood" as Cooley knew it is not universal; urban, digital, and diaspora intimacy reorganize primary association without abolishing the analytic contrast.
- **Wiki coverage gap.** Do not treat the 1902 one-paragraph use as the complete Cooley doctrine; mark 1909 material as not yet ingested.
