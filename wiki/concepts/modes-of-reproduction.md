# Modes of Reproduction

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, [[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996|The State Nobility]] Part IV.)* A **mode
of reproduction** is the system of strategies by which a class or class fraction perpetuates its
position across generations. Bourdieu's **strategies of reproduction** form a system — fertility,
inheritance, education, prophylactic, economic, social-investment, marriage, and
[[sociodicy]] strategies — "objectively organized... without having been explicitly designed
and instituted with this end in mind," founded in [[habitus]]. The book's load-bearing contrast
is between two modes:

- **The family mode**: direct transmission of property and position within the family —
  designated heirs, concerted homogamy ("Cousins should marry cousins so that the dowry remains
  in the family" — André Michelin), anti-division succession devices, exclusive private
  schooling, family spirit.
- **The school-mediated mode**: transmission routed through academic titles and the
  [[grandes-ecoles]] — the school and its *corps* replace the family; classmate cooptation
  replaces nepotism.

"The fundamental difference between the two modes... lies in the strictly **statistical logic**
of the school-mediated mode": unlike direct transfer to a designated heir, school-mediated
transfer "guarantees properties to the class as a whole that it withholds from one or another of
its elements taken separately" — the class reproduces only "by sacrificing certain members" (the
"excluded of the bourgeoisie").

## Semantic History

The distinction generalizes the reproduction analysis of *[[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|Reproduction]]*
and *[[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]* into a historical thesis (per Wacquant): the rise
of [[cultural-capital]] as "second capital" shifts advanced societies "from direct reproduction...
to school-mediated reproduction" — though **all ruling classes use both modes conjointly**, and
the two "coexist within the field of economic power where they represent the two poles of a
continuum." Key elaborations (attributed):

- **The misrecognition dividend.** School-mediated reproduction tolerates more distortion and
  costs more ("greater waste and a higher price tag"), but its mechanisms are "doubly hidden"
  (aggregate statistics + concealed domestic transfer of cultural capital): "academic transfer
  compensates for its lesser reproductive return through an increased effectiveness in its
  concealment of the work of reproduction." The school only *appears* to be an impartial
  redistribution machine; its effects are "infinitely closer... to those produced by the system
  of direct hereditary transfer than to chance redistribution."
- **Family uses of the school.** The contrast is not family *versus* school but "a strictly
  familial management of the problems of reproduction" versus "a type of familial management
  that makes use of the school" — families pool members' capital ("an unofficial circulation of
  capital that enables the networks of official circulation to function"; the Debré case), and
  the business bourgeoisie uses "sanctuary schools" and low-academic-cost consecration
  (Sciences-po/ENA) to legitimate inherited position.
- **Against human-capital economics.** Becker-style accounting "misses the best hidden and most
  socially important of educational investments — the domestic transfer of cultural capital,"
  since scholastic "ability" is itself the product of prior investment.
- **Numerus clausus.** All reproduction strategies imply an inclusion/exclusion mechanism
  holding group size constant; the school-mediated mode removes this from family control,
  producing overproduction of "bourgeois-rights" holders, "catch-up" strategies, and (Bourdieu's
  example of collective subversion) May 1968.

## Operationalizations

1972 French corporate data: 3% of family-controlled-company chief executives held 2+
higher-education diplomas versus 73–74% at technocratically/state-controlled companies;
family-firm heads disproportionately used provincial private schools (Collège des Roches);
fertility differences (large families common among family-firm heads, absent at technocratic
firms). Data are French, 1972 — date-stamp accordingly.

## Applications

- The explanatory core of [[state-nobility]] formation and of the [[grandes-ecoles]] field's
  bipolar structure ("two uses of the school").
- Frames [[reconversion-strategies]]: shifts between modes are experienced as "changes of taste
  or vocation."
- Comparative research on credentialism and elite formation outside France (by homology).

## Critiques and Limitations

The functional language ("strategies" without strategists) draws the standard teleology
objection; Bourdieu answers with habitus, not intention. The two-mode continuum is built from
one national case at one moment; the "managerial revolution" literature it attacks
(Berle–Means, Granick) reads the same data as genuine separation of ownership and control —
Bourdieu counters that owners retain real power behind the "manager" facade and that such
theories are themselves moves in the struggle. Attributed throughout.
