# Corporate Actor

[[coleman-james|Coleman]]'s term for a **constructed, non-natural actor** — a firm,
association, agency, church, union, or nation — that holds rights, controls
resources, and pursues interests as a unit, yet is built up from rights of control
that natural persons transfer to it through a **constitution**. The corporate actor
is the central macro-level object of Coleman's [[rational-choice-theory|rational-choice
sociology]]: it shows how purposive individual action composes into a durable
structure that then acts back on persons. Coleman treats it as an actor "viewed from
the outside" and an authority structure "viewed from the inside."

> Attributed to Coleman. Distinguish the corporate actor (an analytical construct)
> from the legal person and from any particular organization.

## Definition as Coined

A corporate actor arises when individuals transfer rights of control over certain of
their actions to a collectivity — through **conjoint** norms/constitutions
(beneficiaries = targets, social-contract type) or **disjoint** constitutions
(beneficiaries ≠ targets, imposed). In the informal case "the collectivity or the
corporate actor consists of nothing more than this set of norms and sanctions";
formal constitutions are the analogue for larger, less-closed systems. Crucially,
the modern corporate actor is "composed not of persons but of positions" — rights
and resources attach to offices, not occupants — so one natural person can occupy
several positions at once. Internally it splits [[the-self|the object self]]
(principals, benefit rights) from the acting self (agents, usage rights).

## Semantic History

Coleman traces (via Gierke, Pollock and Maitland) the legal emergence of the
corporate actor as a "juristic person" from Roman law's *universitas* through
medieval church and borough law to the 17th–19th-century recognition of corporations
as free legal persons not derivative of the state ("the law is beginning to know the
corporation"). His **historical thesis** (Part IV): purposively constructed
corporate actors have progressively displaced "primordial" social organization
(family, clan, community), freeing persons from fixed feudal positions while
transferring sovereignty and social capital away from them — a structural change he
contrasts with, rather than reduces to, Weber's rationalization thesis.

## Operationalizations

Coleman analyzes corporate actors through: the **principal–agent** structure (the
law of agency; servant vs. independent contractor); the **constitution** (explicit
or implicit), conjoint vs. disjoint; and viability criteria — **reciprocal
viability** (every pairwise relation must pay, n(n–1)/2 relations), **independent
viability** (the corporate actor as third party reduces this to n relations), and
the weaker, riskier **global viability** (a single aggregate profit/loss that
removes self-policing, as in central planning). Devices that restore self-policing
include divisionalization and transfer pricing, forward vs. **backward policing**
(each stage rejects defective inputs), and franchising.

## Applications

- **American high schools** as a disjoint constitution whose corporately held
  authority shrank 1960–1980 as parental authority and community consensus eroded.
- **Weberian bureaucracy** critiqued: Weber treats only the central authority as a
  purposive actor, ignoring that position-occupants have inalienable interests
  (Coleman's "fundamental flaw"); Michels's iron law describes the same phenomenon
  as a top-of-structure deviation rather than a pervasive property. The
  [[social-capital-coleman|ITU]] (Lipset, Trow, and Coleman 1956) is the documented
  exception — "organization within organization" sustaining an opposition party.
- **Constitutional innovations**: German codetermination (1976), Japanese quality
  circles, U.S. ownership-of-innovation arrangements — each a society's distinct
  route beyond classical bureaucracy.
- **Corporate responsibility** and its free-rider structure (Olson selective
  incentives; philanthropy concentrated in dominant local firms); **voice vs. exit**
  (Hirschman 1970) for governing agents; the nation-state as an "intermediate" form
  and the multinational as the "prototypically modern purposive" form.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman's own open problems: whether a corporate actor has "objective" interests
beyond those its constitution specifies (the Ford Motor Company case), and how to
assign responsibility once ownership and control split (Berle and Means) or once the
firm dissolves analytically into a market intersection of contending actors (Fama
1980). Critics note the construct's debt to an economic image of organization and
its thin account of culture and meaning inside organizations. The historical
displacement thesis carries Coleman's own diagnostic and normative charge and is
recorded as attributed argument, not established fact.
