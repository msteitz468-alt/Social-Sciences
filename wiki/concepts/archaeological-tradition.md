# Archaeological Tradition

Integrative unit associated with [[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] and fixed with [[phillips-philip|Phillips]] (1958): **long time, limited (or flexible) space** — the temporal counterpoise to [[archaeological-horizon|horizon]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958, amended after over-expansion.)*

> An archaeological tradition is a (primarily) temporal continuity represented by persistent configurations in single technologies or other systems of related forms.

Spatial scope is supplied by qualifiers (“regional tradition,” “areal tradition,” etc.).

## Semantic History

1. **Peruvian pottery tradition** — counterpoise to horizon style: ceramic unity with **reversed** space/time proportions (deep time, not necessarily wide space).
2. **Expansion** — McGregor (Southwest): deeply rooted “ways of doing things” / attitudes inferred from technology; Goggin (Florida): full **cultural** traditions as distinctive ways of life with persistent themes, strong environment link.
3. **Willey & Phillips cutback (1958):** after defining tradition almost as broadly as whole culture, they retract “full cultural tradition,” return that role to maximum units **culture/civilization**, and restore tradition as an **integrative means** focused on technologies or functionally interrelated form systems.
4. Bennett’s **co-tradition** (areal cultural whole over long time) is discussed; W&P do not adopt it as their core tool.
5. SAA Ann Arbor 1955 seminar on cultural stability further anatomized “tradition” as socially transmitted form units — contemporaneous methodological ferment.

## Operationalizations

| Concept | Primary axis | Typical content |
|---|---|---|
| Horizon | Space (rapid spread) | Style, trade goods, technologies |
| Tradition | Time (persistence) | Pottery series, house types, lithic industries |
| Phase | Local content package | Full assemblage at locality/region |

## Applications

- Peruvian ceramic traditions vs horizon styles (Willey’s earlier Andean work).
- Long Plains hunting continuity marked mainly by projectile-point change (W&P Lithic discussion — tradition-like framing).
- Eastern Woodland and Southwest technological continuities in Archaic–Formative chapters.

## Critiques and Limitations

Over-broad “cultural tradition” blunts the concept’s contrast with horizon and steals the job of “culture.” W&P’s own fix was narrowing. Later archaeology still uses “tradition” loosely (e.g. “Archaic tradition”) — sometimes as stage synonym, which W&P warned against (stage ≠ period ≠ tradition).
