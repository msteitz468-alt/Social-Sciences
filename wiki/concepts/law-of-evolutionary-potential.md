# Law of Evolutionary Potential

## Definition as Coined

A generalization from [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] & [[service-elman-r|Service]],
*Evolution and Culture* (1960), restated by Service in
*[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]* (1975): as a
society succeeds in adapting to and dominating its environment, it becomes more
specialized, committed, and conservative, and **less** able to adapt further; while
newer, less-committed "hinterland" societies gain **increasing** evolutionary
potential by borrowing advanced techniques without the accumulated institutional
rigidity. Service captured it with two borrowed phrases (attributed): Thorstein
Veblen's "**penalty of taking the lead**" for the ossifying center, and Leon Trotsky's
"**privilege of backwardness**" for the adaptable periphery.

## Semantic History

Formulated within the general-evolution program (the distinction between *general*
evolutionary advance and *specific* adaptive divergence). In *Origins of the State*
Service put it to a specific use: explaining why "**the first classic civilizations…
did not fall; they were pushed**." He read the falls of Old Kingdom Egypt, the Indus
civilization, Teotihuacán, and the successive Andean and Mesopotamian centers not as
internal decay but as competitive displacement by newer, less-committed frontier or
"marcher" societies — the same peripheral-advantage pattern he traced in dynastic
turnover (Sargon, the Chou and Ch'in, the Aztecs). The concept thus links to Service's
[[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative theory]] (a successful integrative
bureaucracy is also a rigid one) and to his cross-case
[[archaic-state-formation-pathways|comparison of state-formation pathways]].

## Operationalizations

The "law" resists measurement: it is stated as a tendency, "other things constant,"
with adaptive *specialization/commitment* the operative but unquantified variable.
Service explicitly declined to make it a metric or a single cause (see Critiques).

## Applications

Service (attributed) applied it to the fall of every archaic civilization in his
sample and, tentatively, to the modern case — floating (without asserting) that the
United States' heavy commitment to a fossil-fuel/automobile complex could leave it
vulnerable to later-industrializing, less-committed rivals (naming China as an
example). More broadly it underwrites neo-evolutionary readings of "why the pioneers
stagnate," and connects to core–periphery and marcher-state arguments across
archaeology and world history.

## Critiques and Limitations

Service himself entered the strongest caveat ("**Caveat Lector**"): the law is
**not** offered as *the* cause of any civilization's rise or fall — ancient or modern
— only as one generalizable factor among many specific historical causes (disease,
drought, war), holding "other things constant." He rejected metaphorical cyclical
theories ("growth and decay," youth/senescence, challenge/response) as tautologies and
proposed the displacement mechanism instead — but the displacement claim inherits the
same difficulty: near-untestability, risk of *post hoc* selection of cases, and
dependence on labeling one party "committed" and the other "backward" after the fact.
As with the [[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative theory]] it accompanies, it
is recorded here in attributed voice, not as an established finding.

### Carneiro 2003 (attributed restatement)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*) restates Service’s formulation: “The more specialized and adapted a form in a given evolutionary stage, the smaller is its potential for passing to the next stage,” with the corollary that the next major advance is likeliest from a society not currently in the cultural vanguard — linking Veblen’s “penalty of taking the lead” and Trotsky’s “privilege of historic backwardness.” He also notes Spencerian anticipations (leapfrogging / non-vanguard advance) in the classical corpus.
