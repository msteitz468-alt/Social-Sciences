# Problem of Order

The **problem of order** — the "Hobbesian problem of order" — is the theoretical question of how
stable social order is possible at all. [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]] made it the pivot of
*[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure of Social Action]]* (1937): his four writers'
convergent answer, he argued, is what constitutes the [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic
theory of action]]. Attributed to Parsons throughout.

## Definition as Coined

Parsons framed the problem as arising "as a result of [Hobbes's] exploration of the consequences of an
unlimited struggle for power." Order becomes a problem specifically once a **plurality of actors** is
admitted into an action system (see [[unit-act]]): this introduces the possibility of coercive power,
and hence the need for a "distributive order" — normative regulation — for the system to be stable.

## Semantic History

Parsons distinguished two families of solution:

- **The utilitarian / coercive answer.** "The element of coercive power received its classic
  formulation on a utilitarian basis with Hobbes and has since appeared… whenever the postulate of the
  natural identity of interests has been broken down." Order is secured by force, or by a postulated
  natural harmony of interests — the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] options Parsons judged unstable.
- **The normative answer.** Parsons argued Durkheim broke through by distinguishing social *constraint*
  from natural causation: institutional rules hold actors primarily through "the sense of moral
  obligation," not fear of sanction. Moral obligation toward a norm is itself a value attitude, so a
  shared social milieu implies a common system of ultimate values. Weber's [[legitimate-order|legitimate
  order]] is, on Parsons's reading, the structural equivalent — the same normative-order concept
  reached independently.

This normative solution is Parsons's "sociologistic theorem": a significantly common value system is
an essential condition of social-system equilibrium.

## Operationalizations

The standing threat to order is named by the forces that erode the common value system: Durkheim's
[[anomie]] and Pareto's "interests" (centrifugal tendencies escaping normative control) are treated by
Parsons as structurally parallel. Order is not a state but a continually maintained equilibrium.

## Applications

The problem became the organizing question of Parsons's [[structural-functionalism]] and of the
"sociology" branch in his classification of the sciences of action (defined around common-value
integration; politics is defined around power and the order problem).

## Critiques and Limitations

Parsons's normative solution is the most contested legacy of the book: conflict theorists argue it
resolves the Hobbesian problem by assumption — building consensus in as a premise and thereby
under-weighting coercion, domination, and change. The move is bound up with the disputed
[[parsons-convergence-thesis-debate|convergence thesis]].
