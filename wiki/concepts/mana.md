# Mana

## Definition as Coined

**Mana** is a Melanesian (and broadly Oceanic) term introduced into anthropology by the
missionary [[codrington-robert-henry|R. H. Codrington]] — in an 1878 letter to Max Müller and
at length in *The Melanesians* (1891) — for an **impersonal, transferable supernatural power**
that attaches to persons, objects, words, and acts and accounts for extraordinary efficacy or
success. In Codrington's gloss it is "a power or influence, not physical, and in a way
supernatural; but it shows itself in physical force, or in any kind of power or excellence which
a man possesses." A chief has mana; a stone that makes gardens flourish has mana; a rite works
because it channels mana.

## Semantic History

- **Codrington 1891:** reports mana as a Melanesian emic concept, distinguishing the beings and
  objects that hold it (ghosts, spirits, potent persons) from the power itself.
- **Codrington's own polemical use (attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995]]):** Codrington did not present mana as a neutral datum but explicitly against [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s [[animism]] thesis — he reported finding no Melanesian belief in "a spirit which animates any natural object" of the kind Tylor's theory required, framing Melanesian religion as awe before impersonal power rather than a proto-scientific animating "explanation" of nature. Max Müller used Codrington's mana data in his own Hibbert Lectures to the same anti-Tylorian end, making mana an early weapon in the [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate|dispute over the origin of religion]] before Marett's animatism formalized the move. See [[codrington-robert-henry]].
- **Marett (1909):** built the theory of **animatism** / pre-animism on mana, arguing an
  impersonal-power stage precedes animism in the evolution of religion.
- **Hubert & Mauss (1902–03):** promoted mana from Melanesian datum to **general analytic
  category** in *[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|A General Theory of Magic]]* (primary-ingested;
  see below).
- **Durkheim (1912):** assimilated mana to his notion of impersonal religious force (relating it
  to totemic principle and the [[sacred-and-profane|sacred]]), building on Hubert & Mauss's move.
- **Later critique:** anthropologists (notably Roger Keesing, 1984) argued that "mana" as a
  free-floating substance is partly an **analysts' artefact** — that in many Oceanic languages
  the term is stative/verbal ("be efficacious, be true") rather than a noun for a thing — a
  caution against the reified comparative category.

## Operationalizations

Inferred from efficacy (a person's or object's demonstrated potency) and from ritual practice
(objects treated as charged, formulae believed to work). Codrington and Rivers documented it in
the Banks Islands attached to stones, songs, and rites, and to the ghost-cult and
[[secret-societies|secret societies]].

## Applications

### Hubert & Mauss, *A General Theory of Magic* (1902–03; primary-ingested)

*(Attributed — [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] & [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]; see [[mauss-hubert-general-theory-magic-study]].)* The essay's culminating argument: the standard explanations of magical efficacy — sympathetic formulas, notions of property, demonology — each "leave a residue"; what fills it is mana. Following Codrington they stress mana's grammatical breadth ("a noun, an adjective and a verb" — force, quality, state, action) and its social reach ("It is really mana which gives things and people value, not only magical religious values, but social value as well"). They catalogue cross-cultural cognates — Iroquois *orenda* (Hewitt, whose gloss "magic potence" they adopt), Algonquin *manitou*, Sioux *wakan*, Malay *kramât*, Malagasy *hasina*, Mexican *naual*, Indian *brahman* — and argue the idea of impersonal spirit-power **precedes** the idea of spirit, reversing Tylorian animism. Their strongest claims: mana "functions as a kind of category, making magical ideas possible… a category of collective thinking," an unconscious category of the understanding (compared to Euclidean space); and "the sacred is a species of the genus mana." Magical value tracks **social position, not intrinsic properties** — "They are social facts not experimental facts." **Lévi-Strauss's canonical critique** (1950 "Introduction à l'œuvre de Marcel Mauss," relayed in Pocock's foreword to the English edition): mana-type notions are floating signifiers of "zero symbolic value," "empty of meaning and therefore susceptible to the reception of any meaning whatsoever," and deriving mana from social sentiments trades on non-scientific notions (sentiment, fatality, arbitrariness). The Keesing line below extends the deflation linguistically.

A foundational datum for early-20th-century theories of religion and magic (Marett, Durkheim,
Mauss). In [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]' Banks Islands material, mana underlies beneficent magic
(rain-making, garden increase), the potency of the *werewere* rite, and the authority of
elders (whose supposed magical power Rivers made the engine of [[gerontocracy]]).

### Malinowski, Trobriands (*Coral Gardens* Vol. II, 1935; attributed)

[[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] reported that in the Trobriands the magical force comparative anthropology calls Melanesian *mana* is **present in belief and behaviour** — each rite produces/generates force and conveys it by the magician's voice/breath — **but there is no local noun** for it. Force "not in word but in rite"; gaps in terminology do not entail absence of the concept/attitude. He still used *mana* as an analytic label for the "situation of magic." This is a primary case against assuming the Oceanic *word* is required for the *comparative category* — and a caution about the category itself. See [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]], [[magic]], [[trobriand-islanders]].

### Sahlins, Polynesia (*Islands of History*, 1985; attributed)

[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] deploys mana across his Polynesian ethnohistory, and his usage converges with the stative/verbal reading above. In **Fiji**, following Hocart, the word for "true" (*dina*) glosses mana as "a power of bringing-into-existence," while an action that fails for want of mana is a "lie" (*lasu*) — mana as efficacy/truth rather than a substance (see [[fijians]], [[stranger-king]]). In **Hawaii**, mana's effects are mediated by the gaze: high-tabu chiefs, "called divine (*akua*)," cannot be looked upon directly; the eye is "the symbolic site of subjection" (Sahlins drawing on Valerio Valeri) — see [[hawaiians]]. Among the **Māori**, mana in connection with land is (following Firth) "most nearly akin to the idea of sovereignty," its loss bound to conquest and dispossession, and central to Sahlins's reading of Hone Heke's flagstaff war (see [[maori]], [[mytho-praxis]]). Sahlins adds a further nuance: for the initial reception of Cook and other foreigners it was *akua* ("god"/"divine"), not mana, that served as the "zero semantic category" marking a remarkable experience.

### Sahlins 1981 — mana vs. tabu; foreign mana

*(Attributed — *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities]]*.)* The 1981 monograph adds two points. First, an explicit **mana ≠ tabu** distinction: "mana is not tabu. 'Tabu' is consecrated things, those set aside" — and, by Polynesian conceptions, to defy tabu and survive is itself "the sign of extraordinary mana" (which is why Hawaiian women's commensality with Europeans desacralized the foreigners without stripping their power). Second, **foreign mana as a historical force**: even after the Europeans were "humanized" and secularized in Hawaiian eyes, "European mana was still the decisive fact of Hawaiian life" — European goods retained a mana held superior to things Hawaiian, and Sahlins reads Kamehameha's pro-British policy as inheritance of "Cook's mana" through the killing and ritual appropriation of the god. He also connects mana as visibility/brilliance to the sun (Wakea, *awakea* "high noon") and the *kapu moe* prostration — commoners' "burnt eyes" (*makawela*). See [[hawaiians]], [[taboo]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]].

### Kirch 1984 — *ariki* vs *toa* mana, warfare, and usurpation

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch treats *mana* as reconstructable for Proto-Polynesian and as a structural principle of Ancestral Polynesian chiefship alongside genealogical rank, primogeniture, and *tapu*. Closest to the gods, the hereditary *‘ariki* is their earthly representative “by which supernatural efficacy, *mana*, is conveyed to the society at large”; chiefs may taboo crops, fish, activities, and land, and ritually regulate production (“Work of the Gods”). First-fruits elaborations (*‘inasi*, *makahiki*) channel that ritual mediation into tribute.

**Two routes to *mana*.** For *ariki*, *mana* “flowed” from the gods through sacred ancestors. But because *mana* was also “manifest in prowess, power, and especially, in an ability to challenge the established order and succeed,” warrior success could elevate *toa* (warriors — PPN *toa* reconstructable; achieved status by skill) to challenge *ariki* authority. Kirch quotes Goldman: prominence of war and adulation of sheer power “almost inevitably elevated the warriors to a level where they could challenge the *ariki*.” The germ of *‘ariki*–*toa* conflict over claims to *mana*/power is already posited for APS; historically it structures usurpation and political transformation (Mangaia Temporal Lordship of successful war leaders; Easter *matatoa* seizing secular power from the *ariki-mau*; Hawaiian junior-collateral usurpation with post-conquest marriages to high-ranking women to legitimize heirs). Warfare thus preserves chiefly *mana* and status, offers rivals a path to usurp that same *mana*/status, and lets *toa* achieve positions not open by birth. Kirch cites Sahlins: “usurpation itself is the principle of legitimacy.” Development of Polynesian warfare cannot, on this account, be explained solely demographically-ecologically — ancestral definitions of *mana* and status are equally significant.

### Kirch 2017 — mana and the limits of sanctity in the archaic state

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]], Ch. 8, citing Earle 1997.)* Kirch
reconstructs *mana* as attaching to the Ancestral Polynesian *\*qariki* (the hereditary head of a
*\*kainanga* descent group; see [[ancestral-polynesian-society]]), access derived from birth
order. His developmental point: as Polynesian polities grew larger and more hierarchical,
kinship-grounded *mana* alone became **insufficient** to command labour and tribute or wield
life-and-death authority — additional economic, military, and ideological sources of power
(Earle's three "power media") had to be mobilized. The Hawaiian and Tongan **[[archaic-state|
archaic states]]** are where sacred chiefship was reinforced by those other power sources into
divine kingship.

### Firth, Tikopia *manu* (*Primitive Polynesian Economy*, 1939; attributed)

In [[firth-raymond|Firth]]'s Tikopia material (fieldwork 1928–29) the operative ritual term is consistently **manu**, not *mana* — glossed by Firth as "efficacy," "the idea of success and a proper yield," appearing in net-ritual formulae ("that the net might be manu"). Firth did not equate the Tikopia term with the pan-Oceanic comparative category in that text (he addressed the relation directly in "The Analysis of Mana," 1940). The Tikopia case supports the later Keesing-style caution: the local concept is efficacy-in-outcome, and orthographic/semantic variation across Oceania resists a single reified substance. See [[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study]], [[tikopia]].

## Critiques and Limitations

"Mana" is a **migrated term** — an emic Oceanic word turned into an etic comparative category —
and its cross-cultural reification is contested (Keesing). Its early evolutionary use (as a
"pre-animist" stage) belongs to the abandoned developmental scheme of religion. Treated
carefully as a documented Oceanic concept it remains valuable; treated as a universal
substance it over-generalizes. Malinowski's Trobriand "force without the noun" supports the
anti-reification line while still deploying the comparative label — record both moves.

## Sahlins 2013 — mana as fellowship / unbalance of mutual being

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-what-kinship-is-2013|What Kinship Is—And Is Not]]* 2013, reading Johansen on Maori.)* Johansen triples kinship, fellowship, and *mana*: kinsfolk *manaki* (honour / create mana, fellowship) each other; *manaki* is "to give out of one's own life." The kinship I is "the fellowship in contrast to the individual life." Sahlins reads this as anticipatory of the Viveiros kinship–gift–magic synthesis: mana as politico-religious technique of fellowship; the chief has more fellowship/mana/"kinship I" via privileged ancestral connection. **Power is "a certain unbalance of mutual being."** Links: [[mutuality-of-being]] · [[gift-exchange]] · [[hau]].
