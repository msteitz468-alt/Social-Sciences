# Field of Power (*Champ du pouvoir*)

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, [[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996|The State Nobility]].)* The **field of
power** is "a field of forces structurally determined by the state of the relations of power
among forms of power, or different forms of capital. It is also, and inseparably, a field of
power struggles among the holders of different forms of power, a gaming space in which those
agents and institutions possessing enough specific capital (economic or cultural capital in
particular) to be able to occupy the dominant positions within their respective fields confront
each other using strategies aimed at preserving or transforming these relations of power."

Introduced in 1971 but "elaborated for the first time both theoretically and empirically" in
*The State Nobility* (Wacquant), it is **not** a field like the others and **not** a synonym for
the dominant class: where ordinary [[social-field|fields]] are arenas of struggle over a
specific capital, the field of power is the meta-arena in which holders of *different* capitals
struggle over "the 'exchange rate' (or 'conversion rate')... among the different forms of
capital" — over "the power to dictate the dominant principle of domination," and inseparably
"the legitimate principle of legitimation and... the legitimate mode of reproduction."

## Semantic History

Bourdieu explicitly distances the concept from two rivals: the Marxist **dominant class**
tradition and elite-studies "who governs?" prosopography — both of which study real, bounded,
interacting populations rather than a space of objective relations. Its structure is
**chiasmatic**: the distribution by economic capital (dominant principle) is "intersected" by
the inverse distribution by [[cultural-capital]], stretching from the economic pole to the
artistic pole, with the administrative and university fields intermediate. "Industrialist and
artist in the nineteenth century, manager and intellectual in the twentieth" personify the two
poles (Wacquant). Each subfield reproduces the same chiasmatic opposition internally — including
the economic field itself ("technocratic" vs "family" chief executives) and the space of the
[[grandes-ecoles]], whose homology with the field of power is the book's central empirical
claim. Bourdieu holds the chiasmatic organization "characteristic of all advanced societies,"
in nationally variable forms — a theoretical extrapolation from the French case, attributed.

## Operationalizations

- The **1972 correspondence analysis of the field of economic power**: 216 of 241 CEOs of the
  top French companies (90%), triangulated from yearbooks, business press, genealogical
  directories, and informants. First factor opposes everything public/state/academic to
  everything private/patrimonial; further factors oppose length of family tenure in the
  bourgeoisie and academic-ascent versus academic-legitimation uses of credentials.
- The grandes écoles surveys (1965–69, with Monique de Saint Martin; see
  [[correspondence-analysis]] and [[bourdieu-state-nobility-study]]).
- Bourdieu himself flags the field-of-power analysis as "only of limited value (principally
  owing to the discrepancies in the categories used for the different populations)."

## Applications

- Grounds the analysis of the [[grandes-ecoles]] as a mirror of the field of power, of
  [[modes-of-reproduction|reproduction modes]], and of the [[state-nobility]].
- "Palace wars": struggles *within* the field of power (HEC's rise, ENA's post-1945 conquest of
  higher public service) fought through schools and titles.
- The **lengthening of the circuits of legitimation**: as fields differentiate, power ceases to
  be incarnated in persons and becomes "coextensive with the structure of the field of power";
  symbolic efficacy rises while legitimation costs and crisis risks grow (see [[state-nobility]]).
- Later adopted widely in field-theoretic studies of national elites; Fligstein & McAdam's
  [[strategic-action-field]] apparatus treats the state differently — do not equate.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept inherits field theory's standing boundary problem (where does the field of power
end?) and adds one: because it is defined relationally over *other* fields, its empirical
delineation depends on prior constructions of those fields. Bourdieu's own data (1972 CEOs;
1960s students) are date-stamped and Francocentric; the universalizing claim is argued by
homology, not demonstrated cross-nationally. Attributed throughout.
