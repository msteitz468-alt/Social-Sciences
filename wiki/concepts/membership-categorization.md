# Membership Categorization

Membership categorization is the analytic apparatus introduced by
[[sacks-harvey|Harvey Sacks]] in "On the Analyzability of Stories by Children" (in Gumperz
& Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972) to explain how hearers assign
persons to categories, and infer relations among them, from minimal referring
expressions. It is one of the founding analytic devices of
[[conversation-analysis|conversation analysis]].

## Definition as Coined

Sacks defined a **membership categorization device (MCD)** as "any collection of
membership categories, containing at least a category, which may be applied to some
population containing at least a member, so as to provide, by the use of some rules of
application, for the pairing of at least a population member and a categorization device
member. A device is then a collection plus rules of application." Example devices: 'sex'
= {male, female}; 'family' = {baby, mommy, daddy, ...}. Sacks derived the apparatus from
analysis of a single two-sentence story told by a 2-year-9-month-old girl: "The baby
cried. The mommy picked it up," recorded in Pitcher and Prelinger's *Children Tell
Stories* (1963).

Sacks specified several **rules of application**, attributed to him throughout:

- **The economy rule** ("reference satisfactoriness rule"): a single category from any
  MCD can be referentially adequate to refer to a person.
- **The consistency rule**: if a category from a device's collection has categorized one
  member of a population, the same collection's categories may be used for further
  members of that population.
- **The hearer's maxim**: if two or more categories used to categorize two or more
  members can be heard as belonging to the same collection, "hear them that way" —
  resolving ambiguity (e.g., 'baby' belonging to both the 'family' device and the 'stage
  of life' device) without the hearer noticing an ambiguity existed.
- **Duplicative organization** and its associated hearer's maxim: team-type devices such
  as 'family' are counted by unit-case ("numbers of families"), not by individual
  category; members of a categorized population are "coincumbents" of a unit-case.
- **Category-bound activities**: activities members take to be done by particular
  categories of members (e.g., 'cry' bound to 'baby' in the 'stage of life' device), with
  departures from binding read via **positioned-category devices** as "acting like a C"
  (degrading) or "acting like an A" (praising).
- **Viewer's maxims**: parallel to hearer's maxims, governing how category-bound
  activities are seen (not just heard) as norm-conforming.

## Semantic History

The concept originates in this single 1972 chapter; no drift or later redefinition is
recorded in the sources ingested so far. Sacks explicitly presented his initial
observations as "not proposed as sociological findings, but rather do they pose some of
the problems which social science shall have to resolve" — signaling the apparatus's
provisional, foundational status rather than a settled analytic toolkit at time of
writing.

## Operationalizations

In Sacks's own analysis, membership categorization is operationalized by identifying,
for a stretch of talk or narrative, which MCDs are invoked, which rules of application
resolve apparent ambiguity, and which category-bound activities are implicated —
demonstrated on the single "baby cried" / "mommy picked it up" example. No alternative
operationalization is recorded in the sources ingested so far.

## Applications

Sacks used the apparatus to analyze sequential story structure more broadly: the
stereotyped children's story opener "You know what?" (via the **chaining rule**, a
questioner's reserved right to talk again after a reply) and the concept of a
**"ticket"** — an item licensing a first utterance between parties otherwise lacking
rights to talk (e.g., "Your pants are on fire," an announcement of trouble). Sacks
argued "The baby cried" functions as such a ticket — an announcement of trouble to the
mother — making it a proper story-opening beginning.

## Critiques and Limitations

No contemporaneous critique of membership categorization is recorded in the sources
ingested so far. The concept's evidentiary base in its founding statement is a single
child's two-sentence story — a narrow empirical foundation for what became a widely
applied analytic device in later conversation-analytic and ethnomethodological work; this
narrowness is noted here as a standing caution rather than as an established critique.
