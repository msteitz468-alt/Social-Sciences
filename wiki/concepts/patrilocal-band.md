# Patrilocal Band

## Definition as Coined

[[service-elman-r|Service]] (*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*, Ch. 3) renames Steward’s **[[patrilineal-band]]** the **patrilocal band**. Steward had codified hunting-gathering bands as **patrilineal** vs **[[composite-hunting-band|composite]]** (1955, Chs. 7–8). Service argues “patrilineal” is misleading: the key consequence of **virilocality** is that children grow up among the father’s relatives. Defining rules: **reciprocal band exogamy** + **virilocal marital residence**.

He ranks the patrilocal band as more expectable under aboriginal conditions than composite or “family-level” types — more numerous across extremely varied environments and, he argues, functionally necessary at low densities. He treats it as probably the earliest full form of social organization as well as the most adaptable. **Second-edition preface caveat (load-bearing):** what the standard aboriginal band was “will never be known for sure”; the likelihood of the patrilocal band was “only a surmise” so often read as firm conviction that he “willingly give[s] it up” while saying he has not really changed his mind. Main-text ranking and preface hedge must both be recorded.

## Semantic History

| Author | Term | Emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| Steward 1936/1955 | Patrilineal band | Localized lineage, exogamy, male land inheritance, hunting ecology |
| Service 1962/1971 | Patrilocal band | Residence + exogamy; descent secondary; superorganic (offense-defense) causes of virilocality |
| Later HG literature | Flexible / bilateral bands | Challenges both ideal types; post-contact disruption debates |

Service glossary: **virilocal/uxorilocal** = residence rules; **patrilocal/matrilocal** = group composition results; **patrilineal** requires people’s own recognition of common descent — not automatic from living with father’s group.

## Operationalizations

- **Rules:** reciprocal exogamy; virilocal residence; levirate/sororate as delayed reciprocal marriage pacts; cross-cousin marriage often resultant construct rather than folk rule.
- **Structure:** internal — members of father’s group, mother outsider early; nuclear family as most cohesive unit; no specialization above conjugal dyad. External — affinal dualism / moiety-like band alliances.
- **Demography:** Steward’s ~30–100+ persons; ≤1 person/sq. mile; working groups of 6–8 males ≈ ~25-member bands; “dialectical tribe” ~500 sharing language/culture but not corporate “true tribe.”
- **Statuses:** familistic/egocentric; bifurcate-merging terminology from reciprocal group marriage exchanges + unilocal residence (Service’s preferred cause vs Murdock’s tested alternatives).
- **Type-cases (Service’s inventory):** Australians, Tasmanians, Ona, Tehuelche (pre-horse), Southern Californians, Baja California desert groups, Philippine Negritos, Semang, Ituri, some Bushmen — as he reads the literature.

**Composite contrast:** Algonkians, Athabaskans, Yahgan, Andamanese — Service’s ethnohistorical thesis: near-destruction, depopulation, refugee amalgamation under civilization produce composite forms; not pure habitat products (vs Steward’s migratory-herd explanation). Western Shoshone and Eskimo (Steward family-level) reconsidered as possibly once more band-like.

## Applications

- Baseline of Service’s evolutionary sequence ([[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]).
- Dialogue and partial dissent from Steward’s [[cultural-ecology]] of band structure (geography useful for demography; social organization is cultural; Central Australians as counterexample of formal organization under variable food).
- Kinship theory: earliest widespread full system as bifurcate-merging.

## Critiques and Limitations

Universality challenged; composite/flexible bands may be aboriginal; male offense-defense model; second-hand ethnography; colonial ethnonyms; Service’s own 2nd-ed demotion of conviction. The 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium is where the challenge became decisive: Part III fieldwork (Hiatt, Woodburn, Turnbull, Damas, Helm) found band composition typically fluid and bilateral, and Eggan judged flux the baseline and patrilocality a derived special case — see **[[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]** and the [[hunter-gatherer-band-organization|band-organization comparison]].
