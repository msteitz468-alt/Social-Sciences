# Indexicality

In [[ethnomethodology]], **indexicality** is the property whereby the sense of an expression
or action cannot be decided independently of the occasion of its use. [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]
made it, in his own words, the first of the "constituent problematic phenomena" of the study
of [[practical-action|practical action]].

## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel defined ethnomethodology as "the investigation of the rational properties of
**indexical expressions** and other practical actions as contingent ongoing accomplishments
of organized artful practices of everyday life." An indexical expression is one, in the
logicians' sense he borrows, "whose sense cannot be decided by an auditor without his
necessarily knowing or assuming something about the biography and purposes of the user… the
circumstances of the utterance." Its truth or reference changes with the occasion even when
the words are the same.

## Semantic History

The term comes from logic and philosophy of language, not from sociology: Garfinkel takes it
from Husserl (via Marvin Farber's exposition), Bertrand Russell's *Inquiry into Meaning and
Truth* (1940), and Nelson Goodman's *Structure of Appearance* (1951), where "indexical" (or
"token-reflexive") expressions — *I*, *here*, *now*, *this* — are treated as a bounded class
to be, ideally, replaced by context-free "objective" expressions for the purposes of science.
Garfinkel's move is to **generalize** the property: he argues (attributed) that the
programmatic substitution of objective for indexical expressions "remains programmatic in
every particular case" and "is always accomplished only for all practical purposes." What
logic treats as a removable defect, Garfinkel treats as an ineliminable and constitutive
feature of all practical discourse — a decisive drift from the philosophical usage.

## Operationalizations

Indexicality is not measured; it is *demonstrated*. Garfinkel's "husband and wife" colloquy
exercise — asking students to write out everything two people "really" understood in an
ordinary conversation — was designed to show that the task becomes impossible, because the
sense of the talk is progressively realized through the occasion itself rather than fixed in
advance.

## Applications

The concept underwrites the whole program: the [[breaching-experiment|breaching demonstrations]]
show background sense being repaired when occasioned meaning is challenged; the
[[documentary-method-of-interpretation|documentary method]] describes how members read
occasioned particulars as evidence of an underlying pattern; the clinic-records studies argue
that coded data are indexical to the organizational occasions that produced them.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue that if every account is irremediably indexical, sociology loses any warrant
for context-free generalization — the charge that the concept dissolves explanatory method
(see [[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]]). Ethnomethodologists reply that
naming indexicality does not forbid generalization but specifies the practical work by which
it is achieved "for all practical purposes."
