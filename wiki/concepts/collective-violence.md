# Collective Violence

## Definition as Coined

For Tilly (1978), **collective violence** is not a separate irrational outburst but typically a **by-product of claim and counterclaim**: one group undertakes action stating a claim; another challenges it; they struggle. The counterclaimant is often a specialized repressive force (police, troops). Hyde Park 1866 is his textbook example. Most collective violence grows out of ordinary political processes; repressive agents are often the main killers. Violence tends to **cluster around entries into and exits from the polity**; when membership is stable, violence is less prevalent (attributed hypotheses). Operational coding (Appendix): mutual + collective coercion damaging persons/objects above scale thresholds, excluding pure war and closed-institution violence.

## Semantic History

Breaks with crowd-psychology and pure "riots as pathology" traditions. Related Snyder & Tilly work contested hardship→violence links.

## Operationalizations

Event series of violent interactions; participation rates; relation to strikes, elections, polity membership changes. French 1830–1960 series and British brawls as illustrations in *FMR*.

## Applications

Historical sociology of protest; policing and facilitation; revolutionary sequences.

## Critiques and Limitations

Tracer role of violence can miss nonviolent contention; coding boundaries (damage thresholds) are research-design choices.
