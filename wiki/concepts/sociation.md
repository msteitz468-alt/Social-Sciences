# Sociation (Vergesellschaftung)

## Definition as Coined

**Sociation** (German *Vergesellschaftung*) is [[simmel-georg|Georg Simmel]]'s term for the process by which individuals become a society through **reciprocal interaction** (*Wechselwirkung*). Against any view of society as a substance or collective being, Simmel argued that "society is not an absolute entity which must first exist so that all the individual relations of its members… can develop within its framework"; rather, "society" is "identical with the sum total of these relations" — "only the synthesis or the general term for the totality of these specific interactions."

Society, on this view, is not a thing but an *event* continually accomplished: a group "exists" so long as enough of its constituent interactions (super- and subordination, cohesion, imitation, division of labour, [[exchange]], common defence, religious community, party formation) remain intact, and it ceases only when all interaction stops. Sociation is thus the elementary object of [[formal-sociology|formal sociology]] — the raw process from which sociology abstracts recurrent *forms*.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Simmel, *Über sociale Differenzierung* (1890) | Society as differentiation and interaction rather than organism |
| Simmel, *Philosophy of Money* (1900) | Exchange as "a form of sociation"; money as reified sociation |
| Simmel, *Soziologie* (1908) | Systematic: "forms of sociation" (*Formen der Vergesellschaftung*) as sociology's object |
| American reception (Park, Chicago) | Rendered as "association" / social interaction; feeds social-distance and small-group theory |

The English "sociation" (sometimes "sociality," "association") is a coinage to preserve Simmel's active, processual sense; ordinary "socialization" (learning norms) is a different concept and should not be conflated.

## Operationalizations

Sociation is not measured but identified analytically. Its purest developed instance, for Simmel, is [[exchange]]: "interaction is the more comprehensive concept and exchange the narrower one; however, in human relationships the former appears predominantly in forms that may be interpreted as exchange." Because reciprocity is the universal constitutive mechanism, Simmel treats [[value|economic value]], law, truth, and personality as parallel products of sociation — each an objectivity arising "through the exchange of claims and restrictions" rather than from a prior substance. Larger structures (the state, [[money-economy|the money economy]]) are analyzed as **reified** or crystallized sociation: "money belongs to this category of reified social functions."

## Applications

- Foundation of [[formal-sociology]]: the forms of sociation (dyad, triad, conflict, the stranger, subordination) are its subject matter.
- In *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]*: [[exchange]] "brings about socialization: for exchange is a form of socialization," and money is its reified expression ([[money-economy]]).
- Downstream: social-network relationalism, exchange theory, and microsociology inherit the premise that structure is constituted by relations.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Psychologism.** Otto Spann and (with reservations) Max Weber charged that defining society as the interaction of *psychic* entities reduces sociology to psychology — Simmel as "the epistemologist of psychologistic sociology."
- **Ahistoricism.** Treating sociation as a set of timeless forms was criticized (Lieber, Freyer, Lukács) for detaching social analysis from historical specificity.
- Sociation is an analytical abstraction, not an operational variable; its identification rests on interpretive insight, a standing worry about the cumulativeness of [[formal-sociology|the program]].
