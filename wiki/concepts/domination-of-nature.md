# Domination of Nature

## Definition as Coined

The **domination of nature** (*Naturbeherrschung*) is, for
[[horkheimer-max|Horkheimer]] and [[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]] in
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*,
the project at the root of Enlightenment reason and, they argue, of world history —
and the claim that this project is self-undermining. Their thesis (attributed):
mastery of external nature is inseparable from the domination of other human beings
and from the repression of the dominator's own inner nature.

Its key claims (all attributed):

- **The price of mastery is alienation.** "Men pay for the increase of their power
  with alienation from that over which they exercise their power. Enlightenment
  behaves toward things as a dictator toward men. He knows them in so far as he can
  manipulate them."
- **Domination of nature = domination of men.** Bacon's "sovereignty of man"
  already fuses the two: knowledge "knows no obstacles: neither in the enslavement
  of men nor in compliance with the world's rulers." "Men have always had to
  choose between their subjection to nature or the subjection of nature to the
  Self."
- **Domination requires self-domination.** The formation of the unified, purposive
  self (paradigmatically Odysseus) demands renunciation and the repression of inner
  nature: "**the history of civilization is the history of the introversion of
  sacrifice**... the history of renunciation." "Man's domination over himself,
  which grounds his selfhood, is almost always the destruction of the subject in
  whose service it is undertaken." "The persistence of domination brings about a
  fixation of the instincts by means of heavier repression. Imagination atrophies."
- **The chiastic reversal.** "Animism spiritualized the object, whereas
  industrialism objectifies the spirits of men" — modernity disenchants nature but
  bewitches human relations (see [[reification]]).
- **A prefiguration of ecological critique.** The 1969 preface presents the book as
  anticipating the environmentalist critique of "the domination of nature"; the
  authors argue that unreconciled, dominated nature "rebounds" against society. Yet
  they insist environment is rarely a sole cause of social change; the concept is a
  critique of the *stance* of domination, not an environmental determinism.

## Semantic History

The concept generalizes and historicizes Marx's account of the human metabolism
with nature and [[weber-max|Weber's]] [[rationalization]]. Where earlier
progressive thought treated the mastery of nature as unambiguous emancipation,
Adorno and Horkheimer make it the site of the [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]]:
liberation from natural necessity produces new domination. The theme was taken up
by [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]] (a "pacified," non-dominative relation to nature; see
[[pacification-of-existence]]) and became a touchstone for ecological political
theory, which reads the concept both as prescient and as still too anthropocentric.

## Operationalizations

None; the concept is philosophical and historical, argued through the ethnology of
magic and mana, the Odyssey, Bacon, and the history of science.

## Applications

The domination of nature is the substantive content of [[instrumental-reason]]
within the [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]]; it is the counterpart of the repressed
[[mimesis]], and it links to [[reification]], [[alienation]], and Marcuse's
[[technological-rationality]] and [[pacification-of-existence]]. It is a foundational
reference for critical environmental thought.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded in attributed voice: critics argue the concept is too sweeping to
discriminate among forms of interaction with nature (subsistence, science,
industry, care), that it risks a romantic naturalism the authors elsewhere disavow,
and that treating self-preservation itself as domination leaves the critique no firm
ground. Defenders read it as an indispensable diagnosis of the technocratic stance
toward nature and persons.
