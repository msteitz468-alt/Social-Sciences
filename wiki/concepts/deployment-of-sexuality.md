# The Deployment of Sexuality

## Definition as Coined

**The deployment of sexuality** (French *dispositif de sexualité*) is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s term (attributed) in *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I (Part Four) for the historically specific apparatus — of discourses, institutions, techniques, and knowledges — through which, from the eighteenth century, modern societies produced and administered "sexuality." Its central thesis: **sexuality is not a natural given that power represses but is itself produced.** "Sexuality must not be thought of as a kind of natural given which power tries to hold in check... It is the name that can be given to a historical construct."

Foucault opposes it to an older **deployment of alliance** — "a system of marriage, of fixation and development of kinship ties, of transmission of names and possessions" governed by law, licit/illicit, and the reproduction of the social order. The deployment of sexuality "is superimposed" on alliance without replacing it; where alliance is concerned with the link between partners and with reproduction, the deployment of sexuality engages "the sensations of the body, the quality of pleasures, the nature of impressions."

## Semantic History

*Dispositif* ("deployment"/"apparatus") is one of Foucault's key methodological terms — a heterogeneous ensemble that responds to a strategic need. In this volume it is worked out specifically for sexuality; in reception it became a general Foucauldian analytic. The deployment of sexuality is the concrete arrangement (*agencement concret*) that, in Part Five, links the disciplinary and regulatory poles of [[biopower]] — "one of them, and one of the most important."

## Operationalizations — the four strategic figures

Foucault identifies **four "great strategic unities"** that formed knowledge-power around sex from the eighteenth–nineteenth centuries, each crystallized in a "privileged object of knowledge" (attributed):
1. a **hysterization of women's bodies** → *the hysterical woman* (the body saturated with sexuality, medicalized, tied to the family and children; the figure of "the Mother" / the "nervous woman");
2. a **pedagogization of children's sex** → *the masturbating child* (the two-century "war against onanism"; children's sex as at once natural and "contrary to nature," demanding surveillance by parents, doctors, educators);
3. a **socialization of procreative behavior** → *the Malthusian couple* (the couple "responsibilized" to the social body; the medicalization of birth control); and
4. a **psychiatrization of perverse pleasure** → *the perverse adult* (a distinct sexual "instinct," its anomalies clinically isolated, normalized, and corrected — "the homosexual was now a species," dated by Foucault to Westphal's 1870 article).

## Applications — class and the archaeology of psychoanalysis

Against the repression-of-the-poor narrative, Foucault argues the deployment was applied **first to the privileged classes themselves** ("they first tried it on themselves"), as an "autosexualization" and "intensification of the body," yielding a bourgeois "class body." The working classes were subject longer to the deployment of *alliance*; sexuality penetrated them in stages (birth-control panic, the "moralization of the poorer classes," control of perversions). Hence "there is a bourgeois sexuality, and there are class sexualities... sexuality is originally, historically bourgeois." Foucault proposes the history of the deployment "can serve as an **archaeology of psychoanalysis**," which he reads as simultaneously reattaching sexuality to alliance (law, the Sovereign-Father, tabooed incest), opposing degeneration theory, and differentiating classes. See [[the-repressive-hypothesis]] and [[repressive-hypothesis-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The strong constructionist claim (sexuality wholly produced) is contested (attributed) by biological and psychological accounts; Foucault insists he does not eliminate the body — his aim is "a 'history of bodies.'"
- The "bodies and pleasures" prescription — "The rallying point for the counterattack against the deployment of sexuality ought not to be sex-desire, but bodies and pleasures" — has been read variously as liberatory and as politically thin (attributed).
