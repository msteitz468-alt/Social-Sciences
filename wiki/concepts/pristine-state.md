# Pristine State

## Definition as Coined

In [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* (1967), a **pristine state** is one that took shape **in the absence of predecessors**: specialized formal instruments of control grow slowly out of a [[stratified-society|stratified society]]’s own needs, in a **political vacuum** with no more highly developed state present. Power may first be held by priest, warrior, manager, or charismatic genealogical leader; the quantum leap of power may outrun the handlers’ realization of its extent (“It takes time for a king to become a god”).

Fried held that **each pristine state** traversed stratification-without-stateship (secondary paths may skip that stage). All **contemporary** states are really secondary; pristine states of antiquity have long been dismantled or transformed (not organic “death”).

## Semantic History

Standard in later archaeological literature as **primary state formation** (often paired with secondary). Fried’s pristine/secondary vocabulary is among the more durable exports of EPS.

## Operationalizations

Old World candidates Fried discusses (attributed): Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, Yellow River China. He **leans China and Indus as pristine** despite stimulus-diffusion hypotheses for metallurgy, writing, chariot, sacrifice, etc., arguing institutional packages do not diffuse without receptive social preparation. Number of pristine cases uncertain; possible lost African cases noted.

Neighbors of pristine states may exist, but conquest fits **after** the state is virtually born, not as the origin story of pristine formation.

## Applications

[[state-formation]] theory; contrast with [[secondary-state]] under empire, peer polity, and colonial stimulus.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Identifying “first” states and independent invention vs diffusion.

### Connah 2016 / Renfrew — taxonomy emptied for Africa (attributed)

[[connah-graham|Connah]] (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* 2016), following Lonsdale’s view of African state formation as a **slow coercive centralization of power from local politics** (not imported blueprints), endorses [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]’s insistence that the **pristine/secondary division is a “meaningless exercise”** for understanding African (and, by Renfrew’s broader claim, civilizational) origins: local conditions, not checklist taxonomy, organize explanation (attributed, Connah 2016 Ch. 12). This is a position on the comparative utility of the pristine/secondary vocabulary, not a finding that no independent African state trajectories exist.
- Sample size of primary cases.
- Whether “political vacuum” is archaeologically realistic.

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

As of [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]], **early / primary ("pristine") states** formed essentially independently in at least six areas: Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus Valley, China, Mesoamerica, and Peru (Wright & Johnson operational definition: polity with ≥3 decision-making hierarchy levels) (attributed, ch. 7). Parallel transitions toward social complexity occurred largely independently in those centers; later similar changes occurred in societies in contact with the earliest civilizations ([[secondary-state|secondary]] path in other vocabularies) (attributed). Complex societies have existed only for the last five or six millennia yet have almost completely replaced simpler forms that dominated for a million years or more (attributed).

Causal model survey for pristine formation (all attributed via W&O): Steward/Childe arid–semi-arid intensive cereal settings; Athens/Boserup population–food imbalance driving intensification; Cowgill critique of "strictly from hunger" population determinism; Wittfogel hydraulic despotism and empirical problems (simple societies irrigate without despotism; complex irrigation often post-dates early monumental urbanism in SW Asia); Carneiro warfare + population growth + environmental circumscription (Peruvian coast type-case); Harris cultural-materialist grain "energy gates" in circumscribed farmlands (Egypt, Mesopotamia, N. India, Yellow River, highland Mexico, Peruvian coast, Andes); Marxist class-from-land-control sequences (hard to test archaeologically). Case chapters treat Egypt, Harappan Indus, North China, Mesoamerica, and Andes as variations on primary pre-industrial states — Egypt with slow population growth and non-indispensable urbanization; Harappan as largely self-contained evolutionary dynamics; Andes with Carneiro circumscription "particularly applicable" but warfare more important after monumentality and specialization already appeared, plus isolation and lack of Old World draft animals as constraints (attributed). Many early states competed as peer polities until competitive relationships broke down and one expanded (imperial scale first in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus toward end of 4th millennium BCE; Inka and Aztec just before 16th-c. European arrival — as of W&O 2007).

### Adams (*The Evolution of Urban Society*, 1966, attributed)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] cites [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s pristine/secondary distinction and selects **southern Mesopotamia** and **central Mexico** as the two best-documented independent urban trajectories (Old World / New World pair minimizing diffusion). He prefers Mesopotamia over Egypt, Indus, and China on evidence depth (texts + Early Dynastic excavations); prefers central Mexico over the Andes and over the Maya for the comparison. "Pristine" here labels independent internal sequences of cause and effect leading to statehood, not a claim of total isolation from external stimuli. See [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]], [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]].

### Service (*Origins of the State and Civilization*, 1975, attributed)

Service adopted Fried's pristine/secondary dichotomy but qualified it, preferring **"precocious"** — a relative, local term — over "pristine," an absolute term he found less useful for tracking evolutionary rise and fall: "local relative precocity, rather than absolute 'pristineness,' is what is significant." His archaic-civilization cases (Egypt, the Indus, Mesopotamia, China, Mesoamerica, Peru) are the pristine/precocious instances proper, developed via the redistributive, theocratic mechanism of his [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]] rather than through conquest or hydraulic despotism (which he rejected, citing Woodbury's Hohokam counter-case and Peruvian irrigation predating urbanizing empires by 1,500 years). He argued geographic circumscription (Egypt's Nile Valley, Peru's coastal valleys) and persistent external threat strengthened political integration in these cases, citing Carneiro's circumscription theory as an unrebuttable partial factor, while also proposing a "law of evolutionary potential" (after Veblen and Trotsky) by which precocious civilizations' very success bred institutional rigidity, leaving them vulnerable to less-committed "hinterland" successors — "the first classic civilizations... did not fall; they were pushed."

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) uses the field’s **“pristine”** label for the first small states of the **southern Mesopotamian alluvium** (Sumer, south of contemporary Basra) — not as Fried’s full stratified-society origin theory, but as geographic and chronological focus for a grain-capture model. All claims **attributed**.

**Where and when.** Scott centers the southern alluvium because the first small pristine states arose there, covering roughly Ubaid (~6500 BCE) through Old Babylonian (~1600 BCE), with most evidence **4000–2000 BCE**. By his standards (walls, tax collection, officials — “stateness” as continuum), the state of **Uruk** is firmly in place by **3200 BCE**; Nissen’s “era of high civilization” is 3200–2800 BCE. Southern Mesopotamia hosted several related state-making experiments ~3300–2350 BCE (Kish, Ur, especially Uruk) — rivalrous city-polities rather than a single mega-polity. Mesopotamian forms later influenced Egypt, northern Mesopotamia, and the Indus on Scott’s reading.

**What pristine does *not* mean here.** Scott stresses that southern alluvium was **not** the location of first sedentism, first domesticated crops, or even first proto-urban towns. Fixed settlements and domesticated grains appear earlier elsewhere (Jericho, Levant, hilly flanks east of the alluvium) but **did not give rise to states**. Permanent proto-urban wetland settlements near the Persian Gulf ~6500 BCE were latecomers for year-round settlement and cereals, yet Eridu, Ur, Umma, and Uruk later became the first “statelets.” The grain-and-manpower module is necessary scaffolding; ecological richness and early towns alone do not produce pristine states ([[grain-hypothesis]], [[state-formation]]).

**Writing and inventory.** Writing appears roughly with the pristine state; Scott resists crude functionalism that state makers simply “invented” notation, but holds systematic numerical record keeping virtually indispensable once subjects number beyond a few thousand — inventory of population, land, yields, livestock, stores; continuous records of deliveries, corvée, and requisitions. Earliest Uruk Level IV administrative tablets (~3300–3100 BCE) are lists (barley rations/taxes, war captives, male and female slaves by frequency). Pinpointing birth of the early state remains relatively arbitrary and constrained by few sites with convincing archaeological and historical evidence — Scott’s own caution.
