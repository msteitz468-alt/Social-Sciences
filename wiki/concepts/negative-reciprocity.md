# Negative Reciprocity

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 5, 1972; term credited to Gouldner.)* **Negative reciprocity** is the unsociable extreme of the [[reciprocity]] continuum: **"the attempt to get something for nothing with impunity"** — transactions "opened and conducted toward net utilitarian advantage." It ranges from haggling ("what the traffic will bear") through barter with strangers, gambling, chicanery, and guile to theft, seizure, and "the finesse of a well-conducted horse raid." It is the most **impersonal** exchange and, Sahlins notes, "from our own point of view the 'most economic.'"

## Semantic History

Alvin Gouldner coined "negative reciprocity" within his sociology of the norm of reciprocity (1960); Sahlins made it the third term of his anthropological typology, opposite the "pure gift" of [[generalized-reciprocity]]. The label registers that seizure and cheating are not the *absence* of exchange but a mode of it — the transactional face of enmity.

## Operationalizations

In Sahlins' **sectoral model**, negative reciprocity is characteristic of the **most peripheral** social sectors — the intertribal border, the stranger, the enemy — where "nonkin" shades into "enemy." Its diagnostic is the drive toward **net advantage** at the other's expense, unchecked by kinship morality. Where interdependence makes peace valuable, negative reciprocity at the periphery is deliberately checked — the Trobriand *wabuwabu* (sharp dealing) and impersonal [[gimwali]] give way to protected [[balanced-reciprocity|balanced exchange]] and *utu*.

## Applications

Negative reciprocity anchors the "unsociable" pole against which the whole moral geography of primitive exchange is measured; it clarifies why trade across group boundaries requires special peace-keeping institutions (trade-partnership, silent trade), and why, under subsistence crisis or external threat, "the whole sectoral scheme of reciprocities is altered, compressed" — solidarity may intensify inward while "chicanery becomes the order of the day" outward. It also frames the Deuteronomic double standard (usury forbidden to the brother, permitted to the stranger).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* As the "most economic" pole, negative reciprocity is where formalist and substantivist readings most nearly meet — yet Sahlins insists it remains a *social* relation graded by distance, not the operation of an impersonal market. The category is an ideal-type endpoint on a continuum; real exchanges grade between the three forms. Stated in attributed voice.
