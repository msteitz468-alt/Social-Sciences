# Rock Art

## Definition as Coined

**Rock art** denotes paintings and engravings on natural rock surfaces. In African archaeology it is both an evidence class and an interpretive minefield: images are mute on language and self-ascribed identity, and ethnographic analogy (especially San/Bushman analogy in southern Africa) is powerful but must be flagged (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Semantic History

Phillipson’s Ch. 4 survey treats southern and south-central African rock art as part of later mode-5 / LSA cultural landscapes, with interpretive traditions drawing on San ethnography (as of Phillipson 2005). North African and Saharan rock art sequences (pastoral and earlier) form a parallel literature often periodised separately.

## Operationalizations

- **Style and superimposition** sequences for relative chronology.
- **Direct dating** rare; many chronologies are relative or by associated deposits.
- **Interpretive frames:** hunting magic, shamanism/trance (southern Africa debates), territorial marking, historical narrative — all **attributed positions**, not wiki-voice meanings.

## Applications

- Southern African painted traditions in Phillipson’s regional MSA/LSA chapter (as of Phillipson 2005).
- Links to [[khoe-san]] ethnography as **analogy**, not identity of ancient painters with modern groups without argument.
- Sahara pastoral imagery in Holocene food-production discussions (cross-chapter).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Ethnographic-present and analogy traps.
- Colonial collection and aesthetic framing histories.
- Dating poverty relative to lithic sequences.
- Four-way non-identity: art style ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ “a people.”
