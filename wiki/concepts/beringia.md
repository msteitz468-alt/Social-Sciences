# Beringia

## Definition as Coined

**Beringia** is the analytical name for the land area — now partly submerged
beneath the Bering and Chukchi Seas — that joined northeastern Asia to Alaska
whenever Pleistocene glaciation locked up enough water to drop sea level below
the shallow Bering shelf. The term was introduced by the Swedish botanist Eric
Hultén (1937) for a biogeographic refugium; archaeologists adopted it for the
platform across which the first Americans reached the New World (see
[[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]], [[global-colonization]]). It is an **etic
geographic-analytical construct**, not a people or a culture.

## Semantic History

As [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] reports, the connection was inferred from
faunal similarities (Heilprin 1887; Dawson 1894) before Eric Hultén (1937) named
**Beringia**, reframing it from a narrow "land bridge" to a broad exposed
**subcontinent** — a treeless arctic-lowland refuge. Daly's eustatic/isostatic
sea-level theory (1934) and Hopkins's four-stage exposure chronology (Pelukian →
"Happy Interval" → Boutellier → Duvanny Yar) gave it temporal structure; the bridge
was exposed ~75,000–45,000 and again ~25,000–14,000 BP, flooding in "Termination 1"
(~15,000–14,000 BP) (attributed, Fagan; radiocarbon, calibration unspecified).

### Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|First Peoples in a New World]]* 2009; radiocarbon BP / geological ages as he states.)*

Meltzer treats Beringia as the land bridge **plus** linked Siberia and Alaska—not a narrow causeway. Modern Bering Strait is still ~100 km at its narrowest; seas need fall just over **50 m** for Bering/Chukchi shelves to dry. At LGM minimum a land bridge ~**1,000 km wide** linked Asia–America. Beringia emerged in late Wisconsin ~**27,000 years ago** and was passable until rising seas severed it ~**11,000 years ago**; until ~**10,000 BP** the breach could still freeze for winter walking; thereafter Pacific mollusks reappeared in the Chukchi Sea marking south–north Bering Strait restored.

**LGM environment:** vast, flat, ice-free, nearly featureless; absence of trees/lakes plus sand dunes, loess, permafrost, and ice-wedge casts indicate significantly drier and colder climate than present (precipitation estimated **40%–75% lower**; average air temperatures **7°F–11°F lower**).

**Mammoth Steppe / Productivity Paradox:** Dale Guthrie envisioned LGM Beringia as a cold, dry African-savanna analogue—the **Mammoth Steppe**—from abundant large grazers (mammoth, horse, bison, saiga, musk ox) and predators (Pleistocene lion, sabertooth, giant short-faced bear) in Alaskan fossil assemblages (often gold-sluice). Same mammals in Siberia and Alaska imply easy cross-Beringia travel except Siberia’s woolly rhinoceros and a few Alaskan endemics (including giant short-faced bear) that failed the commute. Palynologists challenged with sparse tundra-like pollen—the **Productivity Paradox** (many animals, little visible forage). Patricia Anderson and colleagues: LGM Beringia more tundra than steppe but a tundra unlike modern Arctic—higher productivity with grasses for grazers; humans may have found few edible plants but animals to hunt en route from Siberia (as of Meltzer 2009).

**Entry timing:** uninterrupted Siberia-to-Texas travel possible only after Beringia emerged but before full ice burial, or after ice retreat before Beringia drowned; between those windows, a **two-step** (to Alaska, later south after ice retreat). Meltzer’s synthesis: ancestors left Asia via Beringia (possible standstill); south via corridor or coast; [[monte-verde]] by ≥12,500 BP; Clovis widespread by 11,500 BP possibly a second pulse. He also frames Beringia as a **“germ filter”**: hunter-gatherers entered without Old World crowd-disease co-evolution—with long-run consequences for post-1492 disease asymmetry (thesis).

**Genetics as of Meltzer 2009 only:** molecular-clock estimates of Asia–America splits (some ~40 ka; later mtDNA/NRY clusters ~18–14 ka in his review) are **maximum ages** for departure (haplogroups arose in ancestral Asia) and cannot date archaeological arrival; archaeology gives **minimum** ages. Do not equate genetic population with archaeological culture or ethnonym.

## Operationalizations

Reconstructed from bathymetry and glacio-eustatic sea-level curves (when the
shelf was exposed), and from pollen, insect, and mammal records (what its
environment was). Fagan reports Hopkins's chronology of exposures and the
central **paleoenvironmental dispute** over whether central Beringia was a
productive "mammoth steppe" grassland or a bleak polar desert — the
[[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]]-reported Colinvaux vs. Guthrie/Young debate,
mediated by Matthews. Meltzer adds Anderson et al. reconciliation and fossil-mammal
interchange evidence (as of 2009).

## Applications

The environment and productivity of Beringia bear directly on **whether and when**
foragers could live in and cross it, and thus on the timing of first settlement.
Route choice couples Beringia to the [[ice-free-corridor]] and Pacific
[[coastal-adaptation|coastal route]]. Disease-filter thesis links Beringia entry
to [[global-colonization]] afterlives.

## Critiques and Limitations

The most consequential archaeological limitation is that the heart of Beringia is
now **under water**: any coastal or lowland sites that would test early crossing
and a coastal route are submerged, biasing the surviving record toward interior
uplands. Productivity Paradox remains cross-class evidence tension (bones vs pollen).
Four-way non-identity: Beringia ≠ a people ≠ a language ≠ a genetic cluster.
