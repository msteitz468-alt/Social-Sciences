# Decomposition of Capital

## Definition as Coined

In Ch. II of *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] labels the historical separation of **ownership** from **control** in the modern corporation the **decomposition of capital**: the classical capitalist who both owned and managed is replaced by a differentiated structure of shareholders and managers, so that "capital" no longer forms a single undifferentiated class actor of the Marxian model.

## Semantic History

Builds on Berle and Means, Burnham's managerial revolution, and related literature Dahrendorf surveys; he incorporates the finding into a broader reconstruction of class theory (authority, not property alone). Adjacent to Mills's account of managerial power and the [[new-middle-class]] (*[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]*).

## Operationalizations

Ownership–control split; rise of professional management; joint-stock company as typical enterprise form.

## Applications

One of the structural changes "since Marx" that, with [[decomposition-of-labor]], the new middle class, mobility, and institutionalized conflict, undercuts two-class revolutionary polarization while leaving authority conflict intact.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether managers constitute a distinct class, a service class, or agents of capital remains contested (managerial-revolution debates); Dahrendorf's authority frame relocates managers by their command positions rather than ownership.
