# Molimo

> Documented by [[turnbull-forest-people-study|Turnbull, *The Forest People* (1961)]]; source [[turnbull-forest-people-1961]]. A [[mbuti|Mbuti]] emic term. All description is Turnbull's single-band fieldwork account (1957–58), rendered in attributed voice.

## Definition as Coined

Among the [[mbuti|BaMbuti]] net-hunters of the Ituri Forest, *molimo* named both **a ritual and an instrument**: the festival of songs sung nightly by the men at times of great crisis — above all following a death — and the long trumpet (traditionally of wood; in Turnbull's camp, a length of metal drainpipe) sounded during it. The men gathered at the ***kumamolimo***, "the heart of the molimo," the central hearth of the men's religious organisation, where — after the women and children had shut themselves in their huts — they sang, ate, and sang again: "to eat and to sing... this is the real work of the molimo." The trumpet, moving unseen in the surrounding forest, echoed and transformed their song into animal sounds and back into music.

Turnbull reported that the great molimo he witnessed was occasioned by the death of the elderly Balekimito and ran roughly three months. Its songs did not petition for cures or good hunting; they simply woke and rejoiced the forest. The load-bearing molimo song, sung fully only after a death, ran: "There is darkness all around us; but if darkness is, and the darkness is of the forest, then the darkness must be good." The commonest refrain was "The Forest is Good."

## Semantic History

The villagers had an instrument of the same name — a clan-totem object that, in the elder Moke's account, "makes only animal sounds—it does not sing" — and Turnbull treated the shared name as a borrowing whose *direction* mattered less than the opposite meanings the two peoples gave it. On the Mbuti side he read the molimo as the expression of a **benevolent forest deity**, related to with filial affection rather than the fearful, compulsion-seeking "magic" he attributed to village religion — an explicit magic/religion contrast that is Turnbull's own interpretive framework, not a neutral finding. Turnbull's account of the molimo drainpipe is often cited for its deflation of the exotic: what mattered, the Mbuti insisted, "was the sound," not the material — "it does not rot like wood."

## Operationalizations

The molimo is not a measurement construct; it is documented ethnographically through its **procedure**: daily midday food-collection from every hut into a communal molimo basket; nightly men's singing at the kumamolimo, obligatory for adult males (the gravest traditional crime being to sleep while the molimo sang); the trumpet's nightly and dawn appearances, including the youths' morning "rampages" in which the "animal of the forest" beat on the huts of anyone who had given offence; and, at the death festival Turnbull saw, an extraordinary **women's intervention** — an old woman danced into the sacred fire and scattered it, then "tied up" the men, the hunt, and the molimo with a loop of nkusa vine until they paid a ransom to be freed. Turnbull linked this reversal to an origin legend that the molimo had once belonged to women before the men stole it. The festival closed in two stages: a final forest night "for singing," then a deliberately destructive re-enactment staged in the village for the villagers' benefit — which Moke resisted ("It is a thing of the forest, and it should end there").

## Applications

The molimo is Turnbull's central evidence for Mbuti religion as trusting rather than fearful, and for the forest as the Mbuti's moral and cosmological centre ([[mbuti]]). It also functions in his account as an instrument of **social control** — the dawn rampages punishing disruptive members (the [[egalitarian-society|egalitarian]] sanction of ridicule made ritual) — and, exceptionally, of incorporation: the Mbuti sang a molimo song of thanks for the recovery of the sick villager Kaweki, "even if he was a villager." As a nightly communal effervescence that binds the group and marks the sacred against the profane, it invites comparison with [[collective-effervescence]] and [[sacred-and-profane]], though Turnbull did not frame it in Durkheimian terms.

## Critiques and Limitations

Everything known here rests on one ethnographer's account of one band over one death-festival; Turnbull himself repeatedly flagged his interpretive uncertainty ("I did not understand it by any means, but somehow it seemed to make sense"). The forest-as-benevolent-deity reading is the most contested element — part of the broader charge that *The Forest People* romanticised the Mbuti (see [[mbuti-village-relations-debate]] and [[turnbull-colin-m]]). Turnbull's technical treatment of Mbuti ritual is in *Wayward Servants* (1965), not ingested.
