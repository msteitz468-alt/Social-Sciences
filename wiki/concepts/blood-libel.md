# Blood Libel

The **blood libel** (ritual-murder accusation) is the recurrent false charge that Jews kill Christian children to use their blood for ritual purposes. It is distinct from, though historically entangled with, the [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]]: the blood libel accuses a local community of a concrete atrocity; the conspiracy myth alleges coordinated global domination. Both feed the broader phenomenon of [[antisemitism]].

## Definition as Coined

Dinnerstein (1994, drawing on the medievalist literature) traces the accusation to 5th-century tales, with the paradigmatic medieval case in 1144: the death of William of Norwich, blamed on Jews by the convert Theobald of Canterbury. The most notorious case, Hugh of Lincoln (1255), produced a tortured false confession, 92 imprisonments, and 18 hangings, and entered English folklore (Chaucer's "Prioress's Tale," the "Sir Hugh" ballads).

## Semantic History

Dinnerstein tallies documented ritual-murder accusations by century: at least 6 in the 12th, 15 in the 13th, 10 in the 14th, 16 in the 15th, 13 in the 16th, 8 in the 17th, 15 in the 18th, and 39 in the 19th. The libel was repeatedly condemned as false by popes (Gregory X, Innocent IV, and others through John XXIII), yet persisted as folklore. Folklorist Alan Dundes (attributed) argued there is "not a shred of evidence" behind the legend, which arose from a "Christian need for a scapegoat," and that such stereotypes travel through folklore (proverbs, songs, jokes) rather than personal contact — "It is not easy to legislate folklore out of existence," he remarked of the Judenstein (Austria) pilgrimage that Pope John XXIII's edict failed to end.

## Operationalizations

Tracked via counts of documented ritual-murder accusations by century (Dinnerstein 1994 tallies, above), trial records, press accounts, papal condemnations, and — for the modern Middle East — state-media content monitoring (Wistrich 2010).

## Applications — the American record

Dinnerstein documents the libel's transatlantic migration: the Damascus Affair (1840), which prompted the first organized American Jewish political protest; a September 1850 New York City incident in which a mob of ~500 ransacked a synagogue on a false ritual-murder rumor; further accusations in New York and Clayton, PA (1913), Chicago, Pittsfield, and Fall River (1919); and the Massena, New York episode (September 22, 1928), in which the town's mayor speculated that a missing four-year-old had been ritually murdered by local Jews — the child was found unharmed, and Louis Marshall secured a state investigation and public apology.

## Applications — migration into the Muslim world (Wistrich 2010)

[[wistrich-robert|Wistrich]] (*[[wistrich-lethal-obsession-2010|A Lethal Obsession]]*, 2010, attributed) extends the case chronology beyond Europe and America: Middle Eastern accusations at Beirut (1824), Antioch (1826), and Hama (1829) preceding the Damascus Affair (1840), initially carried by Christian communities and European consuls rather than Islamic tradition; the rumor that triggered the Kielce pogrom (1946); and modern state-media reuse — Syrian defense minister Mustafa Tlass's *The Matzo of Zion* (1983) reviving the Damascus charges, Saudi and Egyptian press recycling the libel in the 2000s, and the Syrian TV series *Al-Shatat* (broadcast on Hezbollah's Al-Manar) staging a fictional ritual murder. He also records the Toaff affair as a source-reliability episode: Ariel Toaff's *Pasque di Sangue* (2007), arguing a possible kernel of truth in the 1475 Trent case from torture testimony, was sharply challenged (Esposito, Quaglioni) and revised by its author — and was promptly invoked by Egyptian writers as vindication of the libel. Hsia (cited by Wistrich) states the historiographical baseline: the violence against Jews, not any Jewish "blood ritual," is the actual historical fact of these episodes.

## Critiques and Limitations

As an analytic category the term is stable and uncontested; the scholarly work lies in explaining transmission (folkloric, per Dundes) and in distinguishing it from adjacent antisemitic myth-complexes (well-poisoning, host desecration, the conspiracy myth). Evidence base is textual/archival — trial records, press accounts, papal condemnations — documented via [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-study]] for the American cases.
