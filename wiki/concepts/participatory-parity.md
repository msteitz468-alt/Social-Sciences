# Participatory Parity

Fraser’s central normative standard of justice in *[[fraser-honneth-redistribution-or-recognition-2003|Redistribution or Recognition?]]* (2003) and related work.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Fraser 2003.)* Justice requires **participatory parity**: social arrangements that enable all adult members to participate as **peers** in social life. That requires overcoming both **maldistribution** (economic obstacles) and **misrecognition** as status subordination (cultural-value patterns that deny peer standing). Neither dimension alone secures parity. Fraser contrasts this with Honneth’s teleological ethics of intact identity and with pure resource equality.

## Semantic History

Developed across Fraser’s 1990s essays; crystallized in the exchange with Honneth. Distinct from formal legal equality and from pure resource equality.

## Operationalizations

Evaluate institutions by whether they support peer participation; diagnose parity obstacles as economic and/or status-based.

## Applications

Feminist justice theory; multiculturalism debates; two-dimensional critique of identity politics that ignore class and of economism that ignore status.

## Critiques and Limitations

Honneth: may underplay moral-psychological depth of recognition. Others: operational vagueness of “peer”; global/scale problems (later Fraser work).
