# Social Labor

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982, Ch. 3), [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] adopts Marx's distinction between **work** (individual energy expenditure) and **labor** as a **social** process: human beings bonded in organized pluralities mobilize, deploy, and allocate productive effort. **Social labor** is thus never Robinson Crusoe's effort; the laborer always stands as kinsman, serf, slave, or wage laborer, and controllers of labor are elders, chiefs, lords, or capitalists assigned by the system of deployment.

## Semantic History

Marx's labor-process analysis (*Capital*) and the discovery of "labor-in-general" under money economy (via Adam Smith, as Wolf notes) made abstract labor thinkable. Wolf lifts the concept out of capitalist specificity to compare how **any** organized society activates production — founding the three-mode typology of [[anthropological-political-economy]].

## Operationalizations

Ask: who can command whose effort? how are products shared? what institutional and ideational forms secure that command? Modes of production are answers to these questions.

## Applications

Analytical hinge of Wolf 1982: fur-trade reorganization of kin labor, slave-trade capture of persons as labor, hacienda and plantation command, factory wage labor, indenture and ethnic labor segmentation.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Abstract labor's capitalist origin can smuggle market assumptions into non-capitalist analysis if not handled carefully (Wolf flags the history of the concept).
- Care/domestic/gendered labor requires explicit extension (later feminist political economy).
