# Developmental Cycle of the Domestic Group

> Analytical concept rooted in [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]]’s analysis of the Tallensi joint family and homestead in *[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]* (1949, Ch. III), later generalized in the Cambridge papers edited by Jack Goody (1958). Study: [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]. Institution: [[family]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Fortes.)* Among the [[tallensi|Tallensi]], the physical homestead and the domestic family form **one social entity** (*yir* — “house” means primarily the joint family as a coherent unit in its dwelling). That unit is not a fixed household type (elementary vs extended as timeless alternatives) but a **cycle**: marriage and child-rearing found a domestic group; polygyny and coresident agnates expand it; sons build separate gateways and units (*bit u zanor* and related fission); death, levirate, and lineage obligation reintegrate or recompose groups. Cross-sectional census “types” are snapshots of phases.

Fortes tied the domestic cycle to lineage segmentation: fission in the joint family follows cleavages of the effective minimal lineage; lineage fission follows the pattern of cellular segmentation in the joint family — “simultaneous in time but complementary in function.”

## Semantic History

- **1949.** Tallensi Ch. III supplies the ethnographic demonstration (including a 1934 sample of 61 domestic families, mostly Tongo) and the architecture–structure mapping (*yir*/*dug*, gateway, granary, women’s apartments).
- **1958 Goody volume.** *The Developmental Cycle in Domestic Groups* generalizes the approach across societies; Fortes’s Tallensi analysis is the charter case.
- **Later demography and household studies.** The cycle idea became standard in Africanist and peasant studies; variants appear in family sociology’s life-course language (related but not identical).

## Operationalizations

| Phase (schematic) | Tallensi indicators (Fortes 1949) |
|---|---|
| Formation | Marriage; wife’s *dug*; young couple under senior head |
| Expansion | Children; co-wives; coresident married sons; joint labour and granaries |
| Fission | Married sons establish separate gateway/homestead units; property and ritual differentiation |
| Reintegration / succession | Death of head; levirate; recomposition under next *yirdaana*; ancestral *daboog* continuity |

Evidence classes: household census (N=61), homestead plans, genealogies, case histories (e.g. reintegration cases).

## Applications

- [[tallensi]] joint family (Fortes 1949) — type case.
- Comparative African domestic groups in later Cambridge/Goody tradition.
- Methodological caution for any society page: do not freeze “the” household form from a single cross-section without cycle awareness.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Ideal-typical phases.** Real cycles are interrupted by labour migration, colonial taxation, famine, and mission change — Fortes notes contact effects (e.g. male absenteeism) but the model is structural.
- **Patrilineal joint-family bias.** The Tallensi formulation assumes agnatic coresidence; matrilineal or neolocal systems need re-specification (Goody volume’s comparative point).
- **Not a full demographic model.** Fertility, mortality, and migration schedules are ethnographic, not formal demographic projection.
