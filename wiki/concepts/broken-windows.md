# Broken Windows

## Definition as Coined

**Broken windows** is the thesis, introduced by James Q. Wilson and George L. Kelling in a 1982 *Atlantic Monthly* article, that visible **disorder** — an unrepaired broken window, litter, panhandling, loitering — signals that "no one cares," which invites further disorder and, ultimately, serious crime; conversely, maintaining order prevents crime. The claim rested substantially on Philip Zimbardo's 1969 abandoned-car experiments (a car left in the Bronx was quickly stripped; one in Palo Alto only after Zimbardo himself broke a window). It became the intellectual rationale for 1990s "quality-of-life" / order-maintenance policing, most prominently under New York's Giuliani administration, and for anti-panhandling and anti-vending ordinances across many U.S. cities.

## Semantic History

The concept descends from [[chicago-school|Chicago-School]] [[social-disorganization|social-disorganization]] theory but inverts its policy valence: where disorganization theory diagnosed structural breakdown, broken windows prescribed order-maintenance as a lever on crime. Its meaning broadened over time — George Kelling and Catherine Coles's *Fixing Broken Windows* (1996) extended it toward removing "disreputable or obstreperous or unpredictable people: panhandlers, drunks, addicts… loiterers," and unlicensed vendors — shifting the referent from *physical* to *social* disorder.

## Operationalizations

Originally argued by demonstration (Zimbardo's cars) rather than measurement. Later criminology operationalized it through observed and survey-based **neighborhood disorder indices** correlated with crime. In the PHDCN / *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* program, [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] and [[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]] separate **systematically observed** disorder ([[systematic-social-observation|SSO]]) from **perceived** disorder ("how much of a problem…") and test both against predatory crime and structural predictors.

## Applications

- Justified order-maintenance policing, Business Improvement District enforcement, and anti-panhandling/anti-vending law in the 1990s (NYC best-known case; tactics exported, e.g. Paris; Blair-era UK "war on incivility" — as Sampson notes, 2012).
- In *[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]]*, [[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] treats it as the theory his fieldwork engages: BID counsel explicitly cited Wilson and Kelling in targeting the vendors.
- Health literature links *perceived* disorder to physical decline, depression, distress, and powerlessness (as reported by Sampson 2012).

## Critiques and Limitations

### Ethnographic critique (Duneier)

[[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]]'s ethnographic critique (attributed, *Sidewalk* 1999): Wilson and Kelling illegitimately extended Zimbardo's *physical*-disorder demonstration to *social* disorder, wrongly assuming the two have equivalent effects. Social "broken windows," he argues, "are human beings… capable of thinking and creating meanings that range from 'everyone cares' to… 'no one cares'" — unlike inanimate objects. Applied to the [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Sixth Avenue vendors]], the theory misreads a working informal economy as incipient crime; the vendors are better understood as [[public-character|public characters]] signaling that "someone cares." Duneier does not reject broken windows wholesale — he grants its community-policing prescriptions may have helped lower crime — but calls its application to people making "an honest living" its "intellectually weakest" part, and counters with a mirror-image **"fixed windows"** thesis: informal economic activity and mentorship reintegrate marginalized people. Whether New York's 1990s crime drop is attributable to broken-windows policing (crime fell in cities that did not adopt it) remains "a matter of fierce debate." See [[broken-windows-debate]].

### Sampson / PHDCN (2012)

In *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* ch. 6, [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] restates Wilson–Kelling: public incivilities attract predatory crime because offenders read visual cues as signals of resident indifference. He then mounts a multi-pronged challenge (attributed):

1. **Tautology risk.** Much measured "disorder" (solicitation, public drug/alcohol use, graffiti, smashed windows, drug vials) is itself crime or ordinance violation — so strong BW claims risk saying "crime causes crime" (with Raudenbush).
2. **Common-cause continuum.** Public disorder and predatory crime may be manifestations of the same process at different ends of a seriousness continuum, predicted by shared community processes ([[collective-efficacy]], concentrated disadvantage, density, land use) — so disorder need not be an essential causal link to crime.
3. **SSO evidence.** Concentrated poverty and mixed land use forecast observed physical and social disorder; [[collective-efficacy]] predicts *lower* observed disorder after sociodemographic/land-use, perceived-disorder, and prior-crime controls, and predicts lower violent crime after adjusting for violence feedback. Observed disorder is only a moderate correlate of burglary/homicide; once structure is controlled, the disorder–crime link shrinks in most tests (homicide included). **Partial exception:** direct association with police-recorded *robbery* (offenders appear to respond to visual cues), with possible cascading feedback (disorder → robbery → undermined CE → more disorder) — without endorsing the strong thesis that disorder is the essential pathway to predatory crime generally.
4. **Perceived ≠ observed.** Racial and immigrant concentration predict *perceived* disorder more powerfully than carefully observed (SSO) disorder; whites perceive more disorder than blacks/Latinos/Asians living in the same neighborhood; both whites and blacks perceive more disorder as percent black rises (~0.4 unit / >0.5 SD from lower to upper quartile of percent black, net of observed disorder). Shared 1995 perceptions (not concurrent SSO) strongly predict 2002 individual perceptions; perceived disorder r≈0.89 at community-area level over time; 1995 shared perceptions predict later poverty (r≈0.91) while SSO disorder has *no* independent association with later poverty once structure is controlled — black neighborhoods bear steeper slopes.
5. **Self-fulfilling / "perversely so."** Sampson argues that designating areas disordered reinforces stigma and durable concentrated inequality; if BW theory is "correct," it is "apparently perversely so, and not for the original reasons" — by popularizing the idea that signs of disorder lead to decline, it may promote outmigration and deepening poverty. Policy implication he draws: law-enforcement eradication of disorder leaves common origins untouched and treats perceptions as unproblematic.
