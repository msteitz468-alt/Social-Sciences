# Broken Windows

## Definition as Coined

**Broken windows** is the thesis, introduced by James Q. Wilson and George L. Kelling in a 1982 *Atlantic Monthly* article, that visible **disorder** — an unrepaired broken window, litter, panhandling, loitering — signals that "no one cares," which invites further disorder and, ultimately, serious crime; conversely, maintaining order prevents crime. The claim rested substantially on Philip Zimbardo's 1969 abandoned-car experiments (a car left in the Bronx was quickly stripped; one in Palo Alto only after Zimbardo himself broke a window). It became the intellectual rationale for 1990s "quality-of-life" / order-maintenance policing, most prominently under New York's Giuliani administration, and for anti-panhandling and anti-vending ordinances across many U.S. cities.

## Semantic History

The concept descends from [[chicago-school|Chicago-School]] [[social-disorganization|social-disorganization]] theory but inverts its policy valence: where disorganization theory diagnosed structural breakdown, broken windows prescribed order-maintenance as a lever on crime. Its meaning broadened over time — George Kelling and Catherine Coles's *Fixing Broken Windows* (1996) extended it toward removing "disreputable or obstreperous or unpredictable people: panhandlers, drunks, addicts… loiterers," and unlicensed vendors — shifting the referent from *physical* to *social* disorder.

## Operationalizations

Originally argued by demonstration (Zimbardo's cars) rather than measurement. Later criminology operationalized it through observed and survey-based **neighborhood disorder indices** correlated with crime; results are mixed, and the causal claim (disorder → crime) is contested, with Robert Sampson and Stephen Raudenbush's "collective efficacy" work among the influential challenges (secondary literature, not ingested here).

## Applications

- Justified order-maintenance policing, Business Improvement District enforcement, and anti-panhandling/anti-vending law in the 1990s.
- In *[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]]*, [[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] treats it as the theory his fieldwork engages: BID counsel explicitly cited Wilson and Kelling in targeting the vendors.

## Critiques and Limitations

[[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]]'s ethnographic critique (attributed, *Sidewalk* 1999): Wilson and Kelling illegitimately extended Zimbardo's *physical*-disorder demonstration to *social* disorder, wrongly assuming the two have equivalent effects. Social "broken windows," he argues, "are human beings… capable of thinking and creating meanings that range from 'everyone cares' to… 'no one cares'" — unlike inanimate objects. Applied to the [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Sixth Avenue vendors]], the theory misreads a working informal economy as incipient crime; the vendors are better understood as [[public-character|public characters]] signaling that "someone cares." Duneier does not reject broken windows wholesale — he grants its community-policing prescriptions may have helped lower crime — but calls its application to people making "an honest living" its "intellectually weakest" part, and counters with a mirror-image **"fixed windows"** thesis: informal economic activity and mentorship reintegrate marginalized people. Whether New York's 1990s crime drop is attributable to broken-windows policing (crime fell in cities that did not adopt it) remains "a matter of fierce debate." See [[broken-windows-debate]].
