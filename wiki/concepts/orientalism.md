# Orientalism

In Edward [[said-edward|Said]]'s analytic sense (1978), **Orientalism** is the Western
system of representing, studying, and dominating "the Orient" — a
[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] rather than a body of true knowledge. The word
**migrated**: before Said it named a scholarly field (the academic study of Oriental
languages and cultures); Said turned it into a critical concept for a structure of
power/knowledge.

## Definition as Coined

Said distinguished three interlocking senses (all attributed to Said 1978):
1. **Academic** — anyone who researches, teaches, or writes about the Orient is an
   Orientalist; the institutional discipline.
2. **Imaginative/ontological** — a style of thought based on an ontological and
   epistemological distinction between "the Orient" and "the Occident."
3. **Corporate/institutional** — "a Western style for dominating, restructuring, and
   having authority over the Orient": the historically concrete enterprise through which
   Europe managed and produced the East politically, sociologically, ideologically,
   scientifically, and imaginatively.

## Semantic History

The term's older meaning (a philological/area scholarly field, respectable and neutral in
self-image) survives, but after 1978 "Orientalism" became, for most humanists,
pejorative — the name of a distorting, power-laden discourse. This inversion is itself a
central effect of the book. Said further split the concept into **latent** and **manifest**
Orientalism (Ch. 3), in his own words (attributed): "The distinction I am making is really
between an almost unconscious (and certainly an untouchable) positivity, which I shall call
latent Orientalism, and the various stated views about Oriental society, languages,
literatures, history, sociology, and so forth, which I shall call manifest Orientalism.
Whatever change occurs in knowledge of the Orient is found almost exclusively in manifest
Orientalism; the unanimity, stability, and durability of latent Orientalism are more or less
constant." The latent structure comprises a recurring set of attributed traits — the
Orient's "eccentricity, its backwardness, its silent indifference, its feminine
penetrability, its supine malleability," its "sensuality, its tendency to despotism, its
aberrant mentality."

In "The Latest Phase" Said draws these together as Orientalism's **four principal dogmas**
(attributed): (1) the West is rational, developed, and superior, the Orient aberrant and
inferior; (2) classical textual abstractions about the Orient are preferable to direct
modern evidence; (3) the Orient is eternal, uniform, and incapable of defining itself, so a
Western vocabulary is needed to describe it "objectively"; (4) the Orient is at bottom
something either to be feared or to be controlled.

## Operationalizations

Not measured quantitatively. Identified through close reading of representational
texts — scholarship, literature, travel writing, political speech — for recurring tropes,
binary oppositions (rational/irrational, mature/childlike, normal/aberrant), and the
positional superiority of the Western observer.

## Applications

Extended by Said and others to the analysis of colonial and area-studies knowledge, media
representation of Islam and the Arab world, and the general logic of imperial
representation. Foundational to [[postcolonialism]]. [[escobar-arturo|Arturo Escobar]] (1995) explicitly models [[development-discourse|development as discourse]] / developmentalism on Orientalism — an attributed analogy, not identity of objects — in *[[escobar-encountering-development-1995|Encountering Development]]* (see [[post-development]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue the concept is over-totalizing (treating a heterogeneous scholarly tradition
as a single discourse), that it under-weights German Orientalism and the Orient's own
scholars, and that it risks reproducing the very East/West binary it criticizes. See
[[said-lewis-orientalism-debate]].

## Bahrani: Orientalism in Mesopotamian archaeology (2010)

*(Attributed — Zainab Bahrani, "Conjuring Mesopotamia," in [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* Bahrani extends Said's
*[[said-orientalism-1978|Orientalism]]* from literature/history to **archaeology**, arguing the
field enacted "a European mapping of the subjugated terrain of the Other." The category
"**Mesopotamia**" (traced to mid-19th-c. European scholarship, against the older local/Arabic "Iraq")
and the trope of oriental "**despotism**" (Montesquieu — "space without places, time without
duration") together produced an atemporal "dead civilisation" cut off from modern Iraq. Bahrani
documents live political effects (the 1992 Met "Royal City of Susa" exhibit omitting "Iraq"/"Iran"
from its maps; a *Houston Chronicle* op-ed linking ancient "despotism" to Saddam Hussein) and
rebuts Bernard Lewis's claim that only Greeks and Jews "survived with a continuing identity" — a
specific enactment of the [[said-lewis-orientalism-debate]].
