# Escape Agriculture

## Definition as Coined

**Escape agriculture** is Scott’s term in *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* for cultivation forms **designed to thwart state appropriation** — especially [[swidden]], diverse root and tuber crops, maize in highland niches, and dispersed fields that raise the **friction of appropriation** relative to irrigated rice cores that underwrite the padi state (attributed — Scott 2009). Paired with **escape social structure** (dispersal, autonomy, resistance to permanent hierarchy).

## Semantic History

Links to the [[grain-hypothesis]] of *Against the Grain* (grains as uniquely political/taxable crops) and to classic swidden ethnography in SE Asia. Inverts the narrative that swidden is evolutionary “backwardness” awaiting wet-rice progress.

## Operationalizations

Crop portfolios (visibility, storability, staggered ripening); field location and permanence; labor calendars; settlement mobility. Scott treats [[swidden]] as paradigmatic escape agriculture because it frustrates cadastral measurement and fixed surplus extraction.

## Applications

Zomia highland subsistence as **political technology** of [[state-evasion]], not residual primitivity. Contrasts with wet-rice state space (Ch.2–3): concentration of grain and manpower.

## Critiques and Limitations

Not all swidden is state-evasion; ecology, markets, and preference also drive hill agriculture. Some hill polities adopted wet rice where terrain allowed (Scott’s plateau-state exceptions).
