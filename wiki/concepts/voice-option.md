# Voice (option)

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[hirschman-albert|Hirschman]] 1970.)* **Voice** is "any attempt at all to change, rather than to escape from, an objectionable state of affairs, whether through individual or collective petition to the management directly in charge, through appeal to a higher authority with the intention of forcing a change in management, or through various types of actions and protests, including those that are meant to mobilize public opinion." It is "political action par excellence" — messy, graduated (grumbling to violent protest), articulated, direct — opposite of neat market [[exit-option|exit]]. Framework: [[exit-voice-loyalty]].

## Semantic History

Political science long studied interest articulation (Almond & Powell) and democratic control; Hirschman's move was to place voice **alongside exit as a recuperation mechanism for economic as well as political organizations**, and to analyze their interaction. He treated voice first as residual of exit (feeds on inelastic demand / locked-in customers), then as genuine **alternative/substitute** when customers postpone exit because they believe voice will work. "Voice is essentially an art constantly evolving in new directions"; exit's presence "can therefore tend to atrophy the development of the art of voice."

## Operationalizations

- Residual model: non-exiting customers are voice candidates; V-rectangle = non-exiters × deterioration (App. A).
- Choice model: voice as lottery over quality recovery vs certain exit quality (App. B); influence estimates and risk-bearing matter.
- Cost structure: opportunity cost of foregoing exit + direct time/money costs (extends Banfield's influence formula).
- Structural conditions favoring voice: few buyers or high sales concentration (explicit citation of [[olson-mancur|Olson]] *Logic of Collective Action*); durables; organizations with fewer "memberships" than product categories; institutional cheapening of voice (Nader-type entrepreneurship; regulatory revitalization).
- Captive power: consumers/voters with "nowhere else to go" have maximum incentive to cajole and threaten — revises Hotelling's powerless captive.
- Pathologies: overshoot (harassing protests; Goldwater 1964); domestication of dissent (devil's-advocate role stripping resignation threat); institutionalized voice as mere "blowing off steam."

## Applications

Consumer complaint and Nader-era consumerism; labor/union revitalization when raiding restricted; two-party systems and activist letter-writing; public enterprise soft budgets more sensitive to hell-raising than revenue loss; Black Power as collective voice against exit-mobility; Vietnam-era official dissent and McCarthy 1968 as clamorous exit complementing voice.

## Critiques and Limitations

Voice is costly relative to exit and subject to free-riding on others' protests (adjacent to [[free-rider-problem]] / [[collective-action-theory]]). Effectiveness is discovered in use and thus underestimated *ex ante* — creativity "always comes as a surprise." No guarantee voice returns organizations to a social optimum; captive voice can overshoot. Management and members jointly atrophy unfamiliar modes.
