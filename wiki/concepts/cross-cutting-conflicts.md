# Cross-Cutting Conflicts

## Definition as Coined

The idea originates with [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]'s analysis of intersecting social circles (*Die Kreuzung sozialer Kreise*): as social differentiation proceeds, individuals belong to multiple, overlapping groups, so that group boundaries intersect rather than nest. [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] develops the political consequence: **multigroup affiliation creates cross-cutting conflicts that prevent a society from splitting into two hostile, mutually reinforcing camps.**

When organized groups take opposing sides on an issue, interlocking memberships mean many individuals have sympathetic ties to the other side, generating internal **cross-pressure** that "dissipates... part of the conflict between the two sides and makes it less intense." Without cross-affiliation, "conflicts tend to be cumulative" — repeatedly falling along the same cleavage — and communication within each isolated camp escalates hostility toward the opponent's destruction rather than mere defeat.

## Semantic History

From Simmel's formal sociology of intersecting circles, through Blau's political application here, to Blau's own **later macrostructural theory** (*Inequality and Heterogeneity*, 1977; *Crosscutting Social Circles*, with Schwartz, 1984), where the concept is operationalized quantitatively: the more weakly a society's social differences (race, education, income, religion) are correlated, the more they intersect, and the more structural pressure toward intergroup relations. The concept also anchors the pluralist theory of stable democracy (Lipset, Lipset & Rokkan's cleavage theory).

## Operationalizations

In this source, qualitative (patterns of overlapping voluntary-association membership, following Coleman's *Community Conflict*). In Blau's later work, formalized as the degree of correlation among social dimensions — low correlation = strongly cross-cutting = more intergroup association.

## Applications

Blau's account of **stable democracy**: it requires both deeply internalized democratic values (institutions alone fail — the Weimar Republic as his example) *and* extensive cross-cutting conflicts that promote mutual recognition of legitimacy and "rules of fair play." Cross-cutting affiliations restrain [[opposition]] from hardening into civil war.

### Gluckman / Africanist densification (1955/56)

[[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]’s *[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict in Africa]]* gives the classic **anthropological** statement of the same family of ideas: men quarrel under some customary allegiances but are restrained by other, **cross-cutting** customary allegiances, so that conflict in one set of relationships re-establishes cohesion over a wider range or longer time (“the peace in the feud”). Scope condition: general need for peace and a shared moral order. Cases: Nuer feud and exogamy; Tonga homicide; frailty of authority (rebellion not revolution); family estrangement; witchcraft accusations; [[ritual-of-rebellion]]; South African colour-bar (“divide and cohere” until apartheid eliminates cross-links). Study: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study]]. Related sociological line: [[cross-pressures]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The distributional cost.** Blau stresses (attributing the pattern to Lipset) that cross-cutting conflicts stabilize democracy "at the expense of the most oppressed social strata, whose political influence they diminish": cross-pressures depress participation, and lower strata are more cross-pressured (exposed to both reformist class interest and conservative influence via press, church, school). Stability is bought partly by dampening the political voice of those who would most benefit from change. Blau records this as a structural trade-off, in attributed voice, not as a verdict.
- The mechanism assumes memberships genuinely cross-cut; where cleavages are reinforcing (segmented societies), the stabilizing effect reverses.
