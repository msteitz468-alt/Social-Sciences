# Legal Fictions

*(Attributed — [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]], *[[maine-ancient-law-1861|Ancient Law]]* Ch. II.)* Assumptions that **conceal (or affect to conceal)** that a rule of law has changed: the letter remains while the operation is modified.

## Definition as Coined

Maine places legal fictions first among **three agencies of legal change** in progressive societies, historically ordered:

1. **Legal Fictions**
2. **Equity**
3. **Legislation**

After early codes, spontaneous development ends; change becomes deliberate through these means. Fictions (e.g. adoptions extending the family, English corporation sole, collusive conveyances, blood-fiction of the [[gens]]/village community) allow old forms to govern new cases without open repeal.

## Semantic History

Roman and English practice long predate Maine; his contribution is the **comparative developmental sequence** of agencies and the link to progressive vs stationary societies (stationary systems, once coded, stagnate under priestly/oligarchic custody of custom).

## Operationalizations

Illustrations across *Ancient Law*: adoption preserving agnatic family; universal succession and corporation-sole analogies; village-community elasticity via fiction of common origin; *emptor familiae*; fines and recoveries; bonitarian ownership.

## Applications

Histories of private law; sociology of legal change; analytic debates on open reform vs fiction.

## Critiques and Limitations

Three-agency sequence is itself a progressive-society narrative. Technical “fiction” ≠ ordinary-language lie.
