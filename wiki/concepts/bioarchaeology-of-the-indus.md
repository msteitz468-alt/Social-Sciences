## Definition as Coined

This page collects the skeletal-biological evidence base for the [[harappan-civilization|Indus/Harappan Civilization]] as synthesized by Possehl (2002), drawing heavily
on the work of biological anthropologists K. A. R. Kennedy and John R. Lukacs. It is
an etic, analytical assemblage of findings — dental pathology, anemia indicators,
and population-affinity studies — not a single named framework.

## Semantic History

"Bioarchaeology" as a label for the study of human skeletal remains in archaeological context
emerged in the 1970s (Jane Buikstra). Applied to the Indus, it names the reconstruction of health,
diet, disease, and biological affinity of the Harappan population from cemetery skeletal series
(chiefly Harappa R-37, Kalibangan, Lothal, [[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-daro]]). [[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl 2002]]
synthesizes the work of Kenneth Kennedy, John Lukacs, and Brian Hemphill. The evidence base is
small and uneven — most Mature Harappan sites yield few or no human remains — so all
population-level claims are provisional and pre-date the aDNA era (as of 2002).

## Operationalizations

Indus bioarchaeology is operationalized through: dental pathology and microwear (caries, attrition
→ diet); cranial and post-cranial metrics for biodistance/affinity analysis (population
relationships); paleopathology (porotic hyperostosis, cribra orbitalia → anemia and nutritional or
parasitic stress); and demographic reconstruction from cemetery series. Each measure is attributed
and date-stamped to its study, and none licenses an inference from skeletal affinity to language or
ethnicity (see Four-Way Non-Identity below).

## Dental Pathology (Lukacs)

Lukacs (1982, 1985), studying 98 specimens from [[mohenjo-daro]] and Harappa, found
caries, abscesses, antemortem tooth loss, and gum disease, with incidence
consistently higher at Harappa than at Mohenjo-daro except for antemortem tooth loss
(roughly equal at both sites). Lukacs interprets the pattern as consistent with an
urban agricultural population subsisting on soft, starchy food. Mehrgarh remains show
high fluorine levels, tentatively linked to some protective effect against dental
disease there and at Mature Harappan sites generally. (Attributed to Lukacs 1982, 1985;
via Possehl 2002.)

## Porotic Hyperostosis, Anemia, and the Malaria Hypothesis

Kennedy (1984, 1990) reports cranial thinning (porotic hyperostosis) on approximately
25% of the Marshall-era Mohenjo-daro skeletal series, "(as of Kennedy 1984/1990)."
Kennedy links this pattern to hereditary hemoglobinopathies (thalassemia, sicklaemia)
maintained as a balanced polymorphism under endemic malaria selective pressure,
paralleling patterns documented in modern South Asia. Kennedy argues — via the near
absence of the marker in Mesolithic South Asian/Sri Lankan skeletal samples (with one
exception) — that malaria's genetic signature in the Subcontinent postdates the
Mesolithic and appears later than in the Mediterranean/Nilotic African archaeological
record. Only one case of porotic hyperostosis is reported from Harappa's 1987 season,
against a higher incidence at Mohenjo-daro/Sindh sites; Kennedy suggests differing
regional disease ecology between Sindh and Punjab as a possible explanation. This
entire thread is attributed to Kennedy and explicitly date-stamped, as bioarchaeological
disease-ecology inference of this kind is fast-moving and subject to revision.

## Regional Biological Differentiation and Independent Convergence

Hemphill, Lukacs, and Kennedy (1991), cited by Possehl, report evidence for a
north (Harappa-centered) versus south (Mohenjo-daro-centered) biological grouping
within the Indus population — a division that Possehl notes parallels an
*independently* observed north-south stylistic distinction in stamp-seal styles.
This is flagged here as a case of two **independent evidence classes** (skeletal
biology and material-culture style) converging on the same regional division, which
per the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework is a stronger basis for a claim than either
class alone — though Possehl himself cautions that the Mohenjo-daro sample is small
and some dating is uncertain "(as of Hemphill, Lukacs & Kennedy 1991)."

Hemphill et al. (1991) also report biological affinity linking Cemetery R-37 and the
lower stratum of Cemetery H at Harappa to the Iron Age population at Timargarha
(c. 800 BCE), read as "strong evidence for biological continuity from the Bronze Age
into the Iron Age" — but Possehl notes a possible biological discontinuity between
Bronze Age Indus-related populations and the Iron Age population at Sarai Khola
(c. 200 BCE), which complicates any simple continuity narrative. Both claims are
date-stamped "(as of Hemphill, Lukacs & Kennedy 1991)."

At Lothal, Kennedy (in Possehl and Kennedy 1979; Kennedy 2000) reports high phenotypic
heterogeneity relative to Mohenjo-daro/Harappa, with the Lothal population's closest
biological affinities (via principal-components analysis against 14 other South Asian
sites) lying with the Mesolithic hunter-gatherers of Langhnaj — interpreted as evidence
of ongoing gene flow between Harappan farming/urban populations and neighboring
hunter-gatherer groups (Langhnaj, Bagor), a reading Possehl notes is independently
corroborated by trade-goods evidence (raw-material/semiprecious-stone exchange) at
those hunter-gatherer sites — again, two independent evidence classes converging.

## Population-Continuity Claims — Handle with Care

Kennedy (1982, 2000), as endorsed by Possehl, argues that "whatever the racial origins
of the Harappans may have been, they were a relatively stable population inhabiting
the northern and northwestern sectors of the Subcontinent for several millennia prior
to their... urbanization," and that this biological continuum persists "in the modern
populations of Punjab and Sind" (Kennedy 1982: 290–291) — date-stamped explicitly
"(as of Kennedy 1982/2000)." This is a **population-continuity claim about skeletal
biology only**; it is not a claim about language, culture, or self-identified ethnicity
continuity, and must not be read as such. Possehl notes that Kennedy's own earlier
framework (Sewell and Guha 1931's racial typology of Indus skeletons into
"Proto-Australoid / Mediterranean / Alpine / Mongolian-Alpine" branches) is an obsolete
biological-race construct that Kennedy (1977) himself later discredited; this wiki
does not reproduce those racial-type labels in wiki voice.

Note also that this source predates the ancient-DNA (aDNA) / archaeogenetics era (as of Possehl 2002): Possehl's synthesis rests entirely on craniometric and biodistance methods, not genomic data, and any post-2010 aDNA findings (as of Reich 2018) on population history should be treated as a separate, later evidence stream requiring its own date-stamped citation, not silently merged with these earlier skeletal-biology conclusions.

## Four-Way Non-Identity — Critical

Every claim on this page is a claim about **skeletal/biological affinity**, evaluated
via biodistance and pathology data. None of it is a claim about language, about a
self-conscious ethnic identity, or about the archaeological-culture labels (Amri-Nal,
Kot Dijian, etc.) assigned to material assemblages. Skeletal affinity is
`associated_with`, never identical to, linguistic or ethnic categories. This
distinction is directly load-bearing for the [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]], where
Kennedy's skeletal reanalysis speaks only to cause-of-death and trauma dating for a
specific set of skeletons, and is not itself evidence for or against separate
questions of Indo-Aryan linguistic migration into South Asia.

## Applications and Critiques

This evidence base underlies interpretive claims about internal diversity within the
Indus population (feeding the "union of peoples" reading of the [[harappan-civilization|Mature Harappan]] developed on that culture page) and bears directly on the
[[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate|Mohenjo-daro "massacre" debate]], where Kennedy's direct
skeletal re-examination is the central line of evidence debunking the older
invasion-massacre narrative. As with all bioarchaeological synthesis of this kind,
sample sizes are frequently small (dozens of individuals per cemetery), excavation
quality and recovery bias affect what skeletal material survives for study, and
findings should be treated as provisional pending larger samples or independent
(including genetic) corroboration.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Cemetery practice, body ornaments, and demographic inferences

*(Attributed to [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] 1998, primarily from Harappa cemetery contexts excavated/published with [[dales-george-f|Dales]] and [[meadow-richard|Meadow]]; not a replacement for Kennedy/Lukacs pathology studies above.)*

### Burial form and sampling bias

At Harappa, deep rectangular graves sometimes mud-brick lined; pottery at grave bottom then soil then coffin (~0.50 × 1.7–1.9 m); Wheeler 1946 recovered rosewood coffin and cedar shroud wood; later coffins often visible only as dark stains; two mud-brick cemetery buildings noted. Scattered burials and small cemeteries in major regions **do not represent all inhabitants** — other disposal modes possible. Kenoyer proposes the Harappa cemetery was used by a **limited kinship/clan group** stacked vertically; later graves cut earlier ones; diggers (possibly different social class) unconcerned about disturbance and may have pilfered meager goods (e.g. missing left-arm shell bangles on disturbed female burials).

### Sex-patterned grave goods (body as social surface)

Adult women commonly buried with left-arm shell bangles and steatite disc-bead anklets; truncated cylindrical throat amulets almost exclusive to adult females — possible married/cult markers; copper/bronze mirrors with several adult females. One older male in coffin wore necklace of **340 graduated steatite beads** plus three pendant stone beads (including rare onyx eye design framed by gold) and three gold beads — stated as the only gold ornaments ever found in a Harappa burial (Cat. 65–68, burial 196a; Dales & Kenoyer 1989). Another coffin male had broken left shell bangle, carnelian bead, and microbead hair ornament. Seven *Turbinella pyrum* shell bangles on an elderly woman’s left arm (Burial 114) worn smooth where they touched. These patterns feed [[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]] more than pathology tables, but they are **bioarchaeological** in the broad sense: material left on sexed bodies.

### Health, diet, and status (Kenoyer’s reading of available data)

Lack of differential dietary stress at Harappa may indicate well-fed upper classes, with variation in offerings marking only **minor** status differences — an attributed inference, sample-limited. Mother–infant burial records childbirth mortality. Kenoyer does not re-run Kennedy’s porotic hyperostosis or Lukacs’s dental series; those remain the specialized pathology base (above).

### Burial ideology vs living wealth

Kenoyer concludes Harappan burials show a **practical attitude to material wealth**: precious metals and valuable beads generally kept in circulation among the living; only essential personal objects buried (shell bangles, eye beads, steatite discs, mirrors) — reinforcing ornaments as living-world status media and contrasting with Mesopotamian royal burial wealth.

### Matrilocal hypothesis (biodistance)

Initial genetic-trait / biodistance analysis (Hemphill, Lukacs, Kennedy) suggested many Harappa cemetery women related by descent while men were not — compatible with matrilocal burial (men buried near wife’s ancestors). Kenoyer stresses need for further samples and statistics before confirming powerful positions of certain women via residence rules. Four-way non-identity still applies: relatedness patterns are **biological affinity**, not language or ethnicity.

### Related craft/body evidence

Ornament manufacturing wear, bangle-size change over adult life, and sexed figurine programs (most infant figurines male) are adjacent lines of evidence Kenoyer pairs with cemetery data. Zooarchaeology of Mehrgarh (Meadow) is animal-bone bioarchaeology of the food-production transition, not human skeletal series, but underwrites long-run population history premises of the [[indus-valley-tradition]].
