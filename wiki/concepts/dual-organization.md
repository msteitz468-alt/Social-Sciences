# Dual Organization

## Definition as Coined

**Dual organization** names bipartite social structures—moieties, upper/lower halves (Andean *hanan*/*hurin*)—that structure marriage, ritual, and authority. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) treats gender duality and complementary halves as deep Andean organizational principles scaled from cosmology through [[ayllu]] charters to imperial capitals (Cuzco, Chan Chan) and iconography ([[staff-god]] as synthesizer of duals).

## Semantic History

Classic ethnological type (moieties worldwide); Andean studies specialize it into spatial (hanan/hurin), gendered, political dualism, and *tinku* (ritual joining of two to form one). Diarchy vs Spanish-style monarchy is a standing ethnohistoric debate for Inca succession.

## Operationalizations

**Cosmology and gender (as of Moseley 2001):** Male/female forces, balance, and equality; Pacha Mama and moon linked to women, sun to male sphere; gender-parallel descent (women often through mothers, men through fathers) in Pre-Hispanic times; marriage ideology of gender equality at folk level; postmarital residence to husband’s moiety/suyu with retained membership in origin lineage.

**Ayllu charter:** Duality as one of four charter principles (with unity, hierarchy, replication); intermarriageable moieties retain property; cargo hierarchies per moiety; dual kuraka (*kuraka principale* and *segunda persona*) per dual structure.

**Cuzco / Inca:** Moiety split of capital into upper (*hanan*) and lower (*hurin*); Huacaypata as *tinku* nexus uniting royal *panaka*; ceques grouped upper/lower. Spanish monarchical king-lists contested: diarchy (principal hanan / segunda persona hurin) or panaka office charter rather than father–son dynasty (as of Moseley 2001).

**Archaeology (interpretive):**
- Huaca del Sol / Huaca de la Luna as potentially dual power at Moche capital—Sol as *huaca sepultura*/palace-mausoleum, Luna as *huaca adoratorio*; “tempting to see dual organization at the apex of rule” (Moseley).
- Tiwanaku Gateway of the Sun and paired processions in *tinku* fashion; Staff God as dual synthesizer (continuity with Chavín black-and-white portal).
- Chan Chan *ciudadela* pairing and east/west moiety organization of royal kin-groups; western moiety elevation after Lambayeque victory.
- LIP altiplano dwelling clusters with dual organization (as of Moseley 2001).

## Applications

Inca statecraft model; interpretations of earlier ceremonial centers; Chimor royal architecture; iconographic analysis of Staff God and dual processions. Explains why “dynasty” lore for Taycanamu/Naymlap may mask dual rulerships.

## Critiques and Limitations

Dual schemes can be over-read into any paired architecture; require period-specific evidence. Sol/Luna dual-apex reading is explicitly difficult to prove in Moseley. Diarchy thesis for Inca emperors is a position among historians, not consensus. Gender-equality ideology in marriage ritual may not equal political equality of kuraka offices (mostly male).

### From D'Altroy 2002

[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy (2002)]] treats dual ranking as a structural principle that linked capital, empire, [[ayllu]], ritual, and military command — without promoting one government model to fact.

**Community scale.** *Ayllu* usually contained two or three opposing parts (*hanan*/*hurin*, or left-right-center); marriage preferably across sides of a flexibly defined *ayllu*; lineages within *ayllu* could hold different ranks.

**Capital and empire.** Origin myth (Pacariqtambo) groups ten *ayllu* into Hanan Cuzco and Hurin Cuzco. Dual ranking mapped empire halves: Upper (Hanan) Cuzco included Chinchaysuyu and Antisuyu; Lower (Hurin) included Kollasuyu and Cuntisuyu. At Spanish arrival each capital half included five royal [[panaqa]] and five non-royal kin groups; late Upper Cuzco *panaqa* dominated offices and estates. Urban layout: Hanan northwest of main plazas (socially and topographically elevated), Hurin southeast; twelve districts, three per *suyu*. Hanan controlled upper-valley water/canals and assigned [[zeqe-system]] shrine lines; Hurin the lower. Within each *suyu*, *zeq'e* lines grouped into triads ranked *qollana–payan–kayaw*; often one line of a triad to a royal *panaqa* and another to a non-royal *ayllu*. Qoya Raymi/Citua purification sent members of royal *panaqa* and non-royal *ayllu* out the four roads in dualized numbers.

**Provincial *saya*.** Each province was divided into two or three ranked *saya* parts corresponding in some way to ethnic divisions, aiming at decimal multiples (ideally 10,000 household heads per *saya*). Military call-ups and colonial levies often used *saya* fractions; decimal units themselves contained two halves with own leaders; campaigns often had two or four commanders.

**Succession politics.** Waskhar’s symbolic divorce from Hanan Cuzco, reaffiliation with Lower, and reliance on a Hurin guard (distrusting Upper as Atawallpa-allied) show dual structure as live factional resource. Competing formation models for *panaqa*/moiety balance: generative [[split-inheritance]] each generation (modal view D'Altroy reports); all ten groups pre-imperial (Betanzos tradition); Lower from first five rulers / Upper from later (Sarmiento); Pachakuti reorganization; Rowe’s suggestion of periodic Upper→Lower shifts to rebalance. **Diarchy** (Zuidema et al.: always two kings, one Upper and one Lower) remains a minority position in D'Altroy’s presentation because it elevates poorly attested figures and because one moiety leader usually ranked higher and spoke externally.

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — collective-action reading, Oaxaca and New Guinea

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The authors quote [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] (via Kuper 1977:65–6) defining moiety structure as "a unity of opposing groups, in the double sense that the two groups are friendly opponents, and they are represented as being in some sense opposites," and Fred Eggan (1950:302, on the Tewa/New Mexico Pueblos): "Dual organizations in a broad sense are devices to organize and regulate rivalry and opposition in order to serve the purposes of the group as a whole... Ceremonial dual divisions... reflect differences in seasonal activities, observations of solar phenomena, and the like; they control ceremonial rivalry by regulating competition and dividing responsibility in channelled areas."

Reading dual organization through [[collective-action-theory]], Blanton et al. treat moiety systems as specifying **obligated reciprocal actions** between opposing segments — cross-moiety marriage requirements, reciprocal gifting/feasting, organized inter-segment competition, mutual ceremonial-service obligations — rather than as symbolic classification alone. They apply this to Formative [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]] (Valley of Oaxaca), where San José-phase carved pottery shows a "fire-serpent"/sky motif and an "earthquake"/earth motif restricted to male burials, and the community itself was spatially partitioned into "sky" and "earth" halves (after Flannery and Marcus 1994; Marcus 1989; Pyne 1976): they argue **against** reading this duality as evidence of ranked unilineal descent groups (a Service-type [[chiefdom]]), since ethnographically known unilineal systems (Hopi, Northwest Coast, Iroquois, Huron) typically show more than two symbolic groupings, whereas the Valley of Oaxaca's strict duality spans a linguistically diverse area; and elite San José Mogote households engaged directly in ordinary subsistence/craft labor rather than being exempt as in many ethnographic chiefdoms. They propose instead dual "barrio" (moiety-like) power-sharing, drawing an **explicit ethnographic analogy** to Western Mixe communities (Beals 1945), where dual barrios rotate the mayor and judge offices annually and prevent power concentration — flagged by the authors themselves as not fully explanatory ("Applying a dual-barrio analogy to the Valley of Oaxaca of 1000 BC does not provide all the answers...").

As comparative ethnographic illustration of dual/collective governance at larger scale, they cite Donald Tuzin's (2001) study of **Ilahita**, an Arapesh coalescent village in highland New Guinea organized around **eight intersecting moiety systems** linked to a community-wide men's society (the Tambaran), with no official hierarchical governance — war/peace decisions were made communally. The moiety system mandated cross-segment marriage exchange and reciprocal initiation feasting; it lost influence following enforced colonial pacification beginning in the 1950s and was largely inoperative by the 1980s (fieldwork/documentation dates approximate — flagged here as ethnographic analogy, not evidence for Oaxaca).
