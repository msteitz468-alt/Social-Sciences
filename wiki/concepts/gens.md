# Gens

## Definition as Coined

In [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s usage as restated by [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] (*[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*), the **gens** is the fundamental kinship-political unit of “gentile” society: a unilineal descent group (originally, for Engels/Morgan, matrilineal) practicing exogamy, bearing a common name, and exercising mutual defense, inheritance, and religious functions. Phratries and tribes are successive compounds of gentes. Classical Latin *gens* and Greek *genos* are treated as historical instances of the same type as the [[iroquois|Iroquois]] clan.

## Semantic History

Victorian evolutionism equated clan/gens forms across continents as one stage of a universal sequence. Later anthropology retained **clan/lineage** as comparative concepts but rejected the claim that all gens-like groups are stages on one ladder toward the state; “gens” in Marxist and some classical historiography still carries the Morgan–Engels package. Distinguish: (1) ethnographic clan/lineage; (2) Roman legal *gens*; (3) Engels’s world-historical gentile constitution.

**Lowie’s terminological intervention:** *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* promotes **[[sib]]** (Anglo-Saxon) for the unilateral unit precisely to escape gens/clan nomenclature confusion — same comparative object-class for many cases, different packaging (family prior; sib restricted; not a universal stage). Do not silently equate gens = sib = people.

**Maine (1861) — Roman Gens / House.** *(Attributed — [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]], *[[maine-ancient-law-1861|Ancient Law]]*.)* Aggregation of Families into Gens/House under patriarchal model; common-descent assumption often fictitious (legal fiction of blood). Village community should be compared to the Roman **Gens**, not the Roman Family (Ch. VIII). This is an **agnatic patriarchal house**, not Morgan’s matrilineal-type gens — do not equate silently ([[patriarchal-theory]]; [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Morgan–Engels type features (attributed): descent rule; exogamy; eponymous ancestor or name; chief/sachem offices; common burial or cult; mutual inheritance rights; right to adopt; democratic council. Exact trait lists vary by chapter (Iroquois vs Greek vs Roman).

## Applications

- **Iroquois** as living type of gens democracy (*Origin* ch. III): matrilineal gentes, exogamy, sachems/chiefs, councils, phratries, tribes, confederacy; no special public power of the state type — **as of Morgan’s mid-19th-c. documentation**.
- **Greek gens** (ch. IV): father-right transition; family not the unit of gentile society; Homeric military democracy; dissolution toward the invented state.
- **Athens / Rome** (chs. V–VI): Solon–Cleisthenes and Servian reforms as pathways from gens to territorial/property-class state.
- **Celts and Germans** (chs. VII–VIII): survivals (mark community, Irish gens) and feudal synthesis after conquest of Rome — largely Engels’s own material.
- Ch. IX: gens flourishes at lower barbarism with no rulers/ruled and rights=duties; first pastoral division of labor, private herds, slavery, and monogamy split it. Marx: property differences within one gens transform unity into antagonism.

## Critiques and Limitations

Cross-cultural identity of “the gens,” mother-right as original universal, and gens→state as single pathway are contested ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]). Archaeological and ethnographic evidence classes do not support a single gentile stage of humanity.
