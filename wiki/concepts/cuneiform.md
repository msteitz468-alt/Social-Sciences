# Cuneiform

## Definition as Coined

"Cuneiform" (from Latin *cuneus*, "wedge") is a modern, etic label for the wedge-shaped writing system used across Mesopotamia for roughly three thousand years. [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] treats the name as describing the mature form of the script rather than its origin: the earliest inscriptions were pictographic, not wedge-shaped, and "cuneiform proper" — true wedge strokes produced by a prismatic stylus — was only completed by the mid-3rd millennium BCE (attributed).

## Semantic History

Roux traces the script's evolution in stages (attributed synthesis):

- **Precursors — token accounting (from the 7th millennium BCE):** small clay balls, cubes, and cones ("calculi") representing quantities of goods, found across Iraq and Iran. By c. 3500 BCE calculi were being sealed inside clay envelopes ("bullae") marked on the outside with impressions of their contents — a direct precursor to writing as record-keeping rather than as language notation.
- **Pictographic tablets (c. 3300 BCE):** the first true tablets appear in the archaic temples of E-Anna at [[uruk|Uruk]], at the end of the Uruk period. Simple numeral-only tablets (circles, short lines) are attested even earlier in nonliterate contexts at Khafaje, Tell Brak, and Habuba Kabira.
- **Procedure:** a scribe shaped a smoothed clay "cushion," incised guide-lines with an obliquely cut reed stylus to divide it into squares, and drew signs into each square; tablets were then baked or dried (unbaked tablets survive only if carefully hardened). Some archaic inscriptions were engraved in stone instead, using a bronze point and later a cold chisel (Roux, attributed).
- **Phoneticization:** because Sumerian is largely monosyllabic, the earliest script followed a rebus-like principle — one object or idea equalling one sound equalling one sign — starting from a repertoire of 2,000+ pictograms (attributed). Polysemy and homophony were pervasive: the "foot" sign (*du*) could also be read *gub* ("to stand"), *gin* ("to go"), or *tum* ("to come/bring"); the "bow" sign served for *ti* ("arrow") and *til/ti* ("to live"). These ambiguities were later resolved through context, phonetic complements, determinatives, and grammatical particles — all absent from the earliest texts, which are consequently readable only at the level of genre (economic documents: workmen lists, goods lists, receipts) rather than connected sense (Roux, attributed).
- **Abstraction and wedge form:** pictographic signs were progressively laid out in horizontal lines rather than squares or vertical columns, grew smaller and more abstract, lost pictorial resemblance, and had their curved strokes replaced first by straight lines and then by true wedge-shaped strokes (from the prismatic stylus) by the mid-3rd millennium BCE. Minor sign changes continued afterward, allowing specialists to date texts stylistically "as surely as archaeologists date a piece of pottery" (Roux's own analogy).
- **Later spread and specialization:** cuneiform was adapted to write not only Sumerian and Akkadian but also, in glossaries and multilingual dictionaries, Kassite, Hittite, and (very late) Greek terms. Archaic pictographic sign-forms, obsolete for millennia, continued to be copied by 1st-millennium scribes purely for scholarly and antiquarian interest, evidence of a deliberate philological tradition rather than a purely utilitarian one (Roux, attributed).

## Operationalizations

Modern Assyriology reads cuneiform via reconstructed sign-lists (syllabaries), bilingual/multilingual glossaries, and comparative stylistic sequencing of sign shapes across dated archives. Because Sumerian had become a dead, liturgical-scholarly language by the Neo-Assyrian period (Akkadian being the vernacular), scribal training relied on verbal instruction and memorization rather than surviving textbooks (Roux, attributed) — a methodological point relevant to how confidently any given cuneiform genre can be read.

### Decipherment history

- Pietro della Valle brought cuneiform-inscribed bricks from Ur/Babylon to Europe as early as 1625; the first scientific mission (sent by the King of Denmark, 1761, led by Karsten Niebuhr) copied the trilingual Persepolis inscriptions.
- **Grotefend** (a teacher of Greek at Göttingen) achieved the first partly successful reading of Old Persian cuneiform from Niebuhr's copies.
- **Henry Creswicke Rawlinson** (1835–1844) copied and began translating the trilingual Behistun inscription (Old Persian, Babylonian, Elamite) — called by Roux the "Rosetta stone of Assyriology."
- Rawlinson, **Edward Hincks** (Irish), and **Jules Oppert** (French) are termed by Roux the "holy triad" of cuneiform decipherment.
- Akkadian (Assyro-Babylonian) decipherment was "considered assured" by 1848; Sumerian was "broadly comprehended" by 1900, with "dark corners" remaining as of Roux's writing (1992).
- Roux estimates, "at a conservative estimate," some half a million cuneiform tablets available or eventually available to historians, many still unpublished as of 1992.

## Applications

Cuneiform underlies nearly every other page fed by this ingest: the [[sumerian-king-list|Sumerian King List]], the mythological and religious corpus synthesized on [[mesopotamian-religion]], and the mathematical/astronomical/medical corpus on [[mesopotamian-science]]. It is also the vehicle for royal inscriptions, letters, contracts, and administrative archives used throughout the wiki's Mesopotamian culture and site pages — always as a source class with its own biases (royal inscriptions are self-glorifying; administrative archives survive unevenly; a single unusually large archive, such as the Baba temple archive at Girsu, can distort historical reconstruction if treated as representative — see [[sumerians]] and [[temple-economy]]).

A striking case of scribal preservation across political rupture: under royal sponsorship (especially Ashurbanipal), Assyrian scribes copied and collected older Sumerian and Akkadian literary and scientific texts at Nineveh. Roux frames this as an explicit irony worth preserving in the wiki's own voice as an attributed judgment: "the Assyrians who caused so much destruction saved for posterity a great deal of the spiritual treasures of Sumer, Akkad and Babylon" (Roux 1992, attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The rebus/monosyllabic-Sumerian account of writing's origin is Roux's own synthesis of a broader scholarly consensus, not a settled fact beyond dispute; the earliest pictographic tablets remain largely unreadable beyond genre-level inference (attributed).
- Roux notes cuneiform was probably preceded by writing on wood, skins, or leaves that did not survive Iraq's humid subsoil — an argument from absence, explicitly speculative.
- Decipherment history is itself a historiographic construct: priority disputes among Rawlinson, Hincks, and Oppert are simplified in most textbook accounts; treat "the holy triad" framing as period historiography, not adjudicated fact.
- The claim that Sumerian pictograms may have inspired Egyptian hieroglyphs (via Jemdat Nasr–style cylinder seals adopted as amulets in late-prehistoric Egyptian graves) is presented by Roux as attributed to "some authorities" and explicitly uncertain in transmission mechanism — do not promote to wiki voice.

Last known cuneiform text: an astronomical almanac dated 74–75 CE, under Parthian rule — treated by Roux as the terminal marker of cuneiform Mesopotamian civilization's "death" (attributed periodization; see [[mesopotamian-science]]).

## The Documentary Record and Its Method (Postgate 1994)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] treats cuneiform primarily as an
**administrative technology**, and states his own method explicitly: to derive an
account of society and economy from written sources while framing it so that it
remains useful to archaeologists — "eschewing the traditional historical enumeration of
kings and dynasties." He stresses that the documentary record is richer in *quantity
and variety* for the third millennium BCE than for later periods (though literary and
religious texts are comparatively rarer that early), while the Old Babylonian record
offers more qualitative but less quantitative detail — a standing evidentiary asymmetry
that governs which questions the record can and cannot answer for any given period.

**Survival bias.** Postgate attributes the disproportionate survival of detailed
third-millennium (versus later) institutional accounts partly to the tablet medium
itself: clay tablets endure, while the wax-filled wooden boards that later supplemented
or replaced them (not attested before the end of the Old Babylonian period) do not
preserve at all. Tablets are also immutable once dry — correctable only while damp —
which suits legal and final-record documents but works against running or cumulative
accounts, a structural contrast with Egyptian papyri (addable at any time, rolled for
length). Archive preservation is itself frequently accidental: Ebla's palace archive
survived because the room burned and the tablets fell, still legible, from collapsed
wooden shelving — destruction-by-fire as unintentional preservation. Excavation quality
is a further reliability variable: Ur III archives at Tello/Girsu were recovered by de
Sarzec in the nineteenth century without any record of their original storage order, an
information loss attributable to the excavation itself rather than to antiquity.
Illicitly excavated and antiquities-market material (e.g., the Bau/Baba temple archive
from Girsu, looted and scattered across multiple modern collections) compounds this:
context is lost even where texts survive.

**Anachronism traps.** Postgate repeatedly cautions against retrojecting later
political titles onto earlier periods — for instance, Samsi-Addu (Shamshi-Adad), who
seized Assur and then Mari, is often anachronistically termed a "king of Assyria" by
modern writers, but his actual, contemporary title was "Governor of Assur"; at that
date, Postgate states, it is "highly misleading" to speak of "Assyria" as a polity at
all. He treats this class of error — reading a later administrative or political
vocabulary back into an earlier document — as a recurring hazard of documentary
history specifically, distinct from the archaeological record's own biases.

**Constitutive vs. evidentiary status of documents.** Postgate makes a load-bearing
legal-historical point: a written and even sealed document was not, in Mesopotamian
law, what *created* legal validity — "the law did not recognize the creation of the
document as the essence of the transaction, but accepted it as proof that the
transaction had taken place in a legally valid manner." Legal validity instead rested
historically on spoken formulae (*solemnia verba* — e.g., marriage and divorce
performed by set verbal formulae) and enacted/symbolic acts (the "crossing of the
pestle" for a slave sale, "throwing a lump into a canal" for a land sale, cutting a hem
for divorce), with written and sealed documents becoming dominant only gradually.
Postgate draws an explicit cross-cultural analogy — flagged as such, not identity — to
the fading of the Anglo-Saxon turf-on-the-altar land-conveyance ritual as written record
gained ground in medieval England. This documentary-vs-legal-act distinction is
Postgate's own methodological throughline for reading Mesopotamian legal texts: written
sources are frequently **evidentiary traces of an already-existing unwritten legal or
social practice**, not the origin of the practice itself. (As of
[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] makes an important **correction** to the token-precursor account given above under Semantic History. He explicitly **rejects the linear "evolution of writing" narrative** — the popular/textbook sequence running bullae-with-tokens → tablets-with-impressed-signs → proto-cuneiform — as historically inaccurate: "There was thus not an evolution of precursors to writing, from bullae with tokens to tablets with signs impressed on them, as is very often suggested. These presumed stages coincided and must be seen as different and competing attempts at conceptualizing the surroundings." On this corrected reading, tokens, bullae, numerical tablets, and proto-cuneiform were **parallel, competing recording systems**, not sequential evolutionary stages — the Semantic History section above, which presents these as ordered "stages," should be read alongside this correction rather than as a settled account.

Consistent with this, Van De Mieroop reports that two independent, contemporaneous, but non-identical writing systems emerged from the same shared precursor systems: proto-cuneiform at Uruk (south Mesopotamia) and Proto-Elamite at Susa/Susiana (western Iran) — "immediately afterwards they developed distinct and independent systems of true writing." Proto-Elamite remains undeciphered; proto-cuneiform is well understood owing to its continuity into later cuneiform. Quantitatively, roughly 90% of proto-cuneiform (Uruk IV) tablets are numerical/accounts and roughly 10% lexical lists; scribes used seven different, mutually incompatible numeral systems depending on what was being counted (sexagesimal for animals/humans/dried fish; bisexagesimal for processed grain/cheese/fresh fish; separate systems again for grain volume, field area, and time) — roughly 60 distinct number signs in total, read by Van De Mieroop as consistent with the "archaic bookkeeping" administrative-accounting model of writing's origin (following Nissen, Damerow, and Englund's 1993 *Archaic Bookkeeping*): writing arose to solve accounting problems — recording quantities together with the person or office responsible — not as a linguistic project.

**Script ≠ language.** Van De Mieroop states the non-identity point explicitly and quotably: "It is important to keep in mind that cuneiform is a script, not a language... Just as the Latin alphabet has the potential to record any language, cuneiform script could do the same." This extends the wiki's four-way non-identity principle: a script is not a language, just as culture is not language is not genes is not people. (as of [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]])
