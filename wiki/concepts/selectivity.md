# Selectivity

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]* (1985, ch. 2), [[lieberson-stanley|Stanley Lieberson]] treats **selectivity** as the central threat in nonexperimental social research. The experimental ideal requires **random assignment** of units (persons, groups, organizations, nations) to conditions, or at least assignment with no bearing on the outcome. Social life, he argued (attributed), continuously sorts units non-randomly into conditions that researchers then treat as "treatments." When assignment is selective on factors that also affect the **explanandum**, observed differences confound the force under study with pre-existing population differences — including **unmeasured selectivity** that residual controls cannot remove.

Lieberson aligned this with Cook and Campbell's definition of the **quasi-experiment**: treatments and outcomes without random assignment, hence non-equivalent groups. The researcher's task is to separate treatment effects from initial noncomparability; without random assignment, one cannot know how much of the observed outcome reflects the forces under study versus unmeasured initial differences (compounded by possible interactions between selective assignment and response).

He distinguished **three types of nonrandom process**:

1. **Self-selectivity** — units sort by choice (who volunteers for military service; who chooses private school).
2. **Selective assignment by the independent variable** — who is exposed to which levels of the force under study by the operation of that force or its institutional carriers (school assignment policies tied to past achievement).
3. **Selectivity due to exogenous forces** — sorting by factors outside the variables under consideration.

Selectivity is **not** a problem, he held (attributed), if the selective factors do not affect the dependent variable. As a general rule in social quasi-experiments, they do.

## Semantic History

"Selection bias" and related terms long predate Lieberson in statistics and econometrics (e.g. Heckman's sample-selection work, which Lieberson cites elsewhere in the book as limited for the sociological control problem). Lieberson's contribution (attributed) is to make selectivity the **organizing diagnosis** of mock-experiment sociology: not a technical nuisance to be "controlled away," but a structural feature of social processes — "social processes are selective processes" — that undermines the experimental analogue whenever it operates *within* the control categories themselves.

The chapter illustrations are drawn from mid-century U.S. quantitative sociology: military service and later earnings (Griliches and Mason 1973); elite colleges vs. state universities; racial composition of schools and the Coleman Report (Coleman et al. 1966; Smith 1972 in Mosteller and Moynihan); private vs. public schools (Coleman, Hoffer, and Kilgore 1982a). These are design examples, not findings promoted here as substantive results.

## Operationalizations

Lieberson operationalized the threat via a **control × selectivity** design (Charts 2.1–2.2). True school-type effect under random assignment is T+; SES is control C (with C+/C−/C° effects on the outcome); within-SES selectivity is S+/S−/S°. Nine C×S combinations show when applying controls:

- recovers the experimental analogue (S° cases);
- moves toward truth but still overestimates (common S+ pattern: wealth plus within-class concern for education → private schools);
- understates or **reverses** the true relationship when selectivity opposes the treatment effect (|S−| > |T+|).

Thus incomplete controls are not always "better than nothing": under some unmeasured-selectivity conditions, controls move results **farther** from truth than no controls. Pseudocontrols (e.g. North–South black education/income patterns that look like controls but do not isolate the force of interest) further corrupt inference.

## Applications

- **Institutional outputs**: distinguishing entrants' initial qualities from institutional impact (schools, colleges, military).
- **Policy quasi-experiments**: desegregation and "white flight" (ch. 3) face selectivity *and* contamination jointly.
- **Neutral-variable test** (ch. 11): if residual selectivity is fully accounted for, the joint association of the controls with a **neutral** variable (unaffected by the test condition) should be the same across levels of the test variable — a diagnostic editors might demand.

## Critiques and Limitations

Lieberson wrote as an internal reformer of quantitative sociology, not as an opponent of measurement. Later causal-inference literature (matching, selection models, design-based identification) develops tools he only partly engaged; his 1985 claim is that routine control-variable practice **as then (and often still) used** cannot secure the experimental analogue. The concept's load-bearing assumption — that social sorting is nearly ubiquitous on outcome-relevant attributes — is itself a theoretical claim requiring case-by-case defense.
