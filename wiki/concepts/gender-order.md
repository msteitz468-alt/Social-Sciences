# Gender Order

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s term for the whole-society structure of gender relations within which particular masculinities and femininities are configured. Coined (following Jill Matthews) in *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987); restated in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995) as the macro-frame for [[hegemonic-masculinity]].

## Definition as Coined

In *Gender and Power* Connell distinguishes **structural models** (labour, power, [[cathexis]]) from **structural inventories**:

- **Gender order** — "the structural inventory of an entire society"; Matthews's "historically constructed pattern of power relations between men and women and definitions of femininity and masculinity." Dynamically: "the current state of play in this macro-politics" of interest formation, institutional relations, and cultural definition.
- **Gender regime** — "the structural inventory of a particular institution" (school, workplace, family, state, street): "the state of play in gender relations in a given institution."

Labour, power, and cathexis are "the major elements of any gender order or gender regime." Complete inventory requires relationships *between* regimes (complementary, abrasive, parallel).

In the *Masculinities* 2nd-edition Introduction he adds a third, global level: "The world gender order can be defined as the structure of relationships that interconnect the gender regimes of institutions, and the gender orders of local societies, on a world scale." The modern order is **historically specific** — roughly the last five centuries of state, industrial-capitalist, and imperial reorganization (see [[hubs/theory/gender-relations-theory|Theory Hub]]).

## Semantic History

The concept is inseparable from Connell's claim that a gender order is a **system** and can therefore be subject to **"crisis tendencies"** — a term he borrows from Jürgen Habermas. This underwrites one of his sharpest terminological interventions: there can be no logical "crisis of masculinity," because masculinity is "a configuration of practice within a system of gender relations" and not a coherent system in itself — a masculinity can only be "disrupted or transformed"; only a gender *order* can be in crisis.

> "The scale of contemporary violence points to crisis tendencies … in the modern gender order."

Connell reads the collapse of the legitimacy of patriarchal power and the rise of a global women's movement as evidence of such crisis tendencies.

## Operationalizations

A gender order is analyzed, not scored: it is decomposed into the three structures of Connell's [[gender-relations-theory|model]] — relations of **power**, **production** (the gender division of labour and the [[patriarchal-dividend]]), and **cathexis** (the structuring of desire). Institutions are examined as gender regimes with their own recruitment, division of labour, and policy patterns.

## Applications

- Frames the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history study]]: the four groups of men were chosen to probe crisis tendencies located in different structures of the gender order (power, cathexis, production, authority/rationality).
- Grounds Connell's global argument that the European/American gender order has been exported through corporations, states, armies, and mass education, reshaping local gender arrangements worldwide.
- The gender-order / gender-regime distinction is standard in institutional analyses of gender.

## Critiques and Limitations

As a whole-society construct, the gender order shares the scale problems of any macro category: it is read off from heterogeneous evidence and cannot be directly measured, and its unit ("a society") is analytically bounded rather than observed. Its value, on Connell's account, is that it keeps the analysis of any single masculinity tethered to the historically specific structure that produces it.
