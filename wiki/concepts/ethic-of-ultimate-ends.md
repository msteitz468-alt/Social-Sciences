# Ethic of Ultimate Ends

## Definition as Coined

*Gesinnungsethik* — variously rendered "ethic of ultimate ends," "ethic of conviction," or "ethic of intention" — the counter-pole to the [[ethic-of-responsibility|ethic of responsibility]] in [[weber-max|Weber]]'s *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (1919). The actor guided by it acts on the intrinsic rightness of the maxim and leaves outcomes aside: "in religious terms, *'The Christian does rightly and leaves the results with the Lord.'*" Weber's exemplar is the **Sermon on the Mount** — "the absolute ethic of the gospel," which "is not a cab, which one can have stopped at one's pleasure; it is all or nothing." "Turn the other cheek," "resist not him that is evil," "give what thou hast — absolutely everything": these are *unconditional*, indifferent to social consequence, and (Weber says) coherent only for a saint who lives "like Jesus, the apostles, St. Francis." The believer "feels *responsible* only for seeing to it that the flame of pure intentions is not quenched." (Attributed voice — Weber's ideal type.)

## Semantic History

Coined in the 1919 lecture as the foil to *Verantwortungsethik*; the two terms have travelled together ever since. Weber's diagnosis is pointed and dated to the German Revolution around him: he aims it at revolutionary syndicalists, pacifists, and *Weltanschauungspolitiker* ("worldview politicians") who "pass the watchword, 'The world is stupid and base, not I.'" His key dynamic claim: the ethic of ultimate ends, unable to bear "the ethical irrationality of the world," tends to flip — "the adherent… suddenly turns into a chiliastic prophet," those who "preached love against violence now call for the use of force for the last violent deed" to end all violence. As with its partner, Weber's final move complicates the opposition: the two ethics "are not absolute contrasts but rather supplements" in the genuine political vocation.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Recognized by: (1) *all-or-nothing* commitment indifferent to foreseeable consequences; (2) displacement of responsibility for bad outcomes onto "the world… or God's will"; (3) exemplary/expressive acts meant to keep a "flame" alive rather than to succeed; (4) a latent tendency to chiliastic violence "for the last time."

## Applications

The analytic frame for studying conviction politics, principled pacifism and conscientious refusal, revolutionary and prophetic movements, and moral absolutism in public life. In this wiki it is the constitutive contrast term for [[ethic-of-responsibility]] and connects to the prophetic register of [[charismatic-authority|charismatic]] leadership.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Caricature risk.** Weber's framing is polemical; defenders of conviction ethics (and of principled non-violence) argue he stacks the deck by pairing consequence-indifference with self-righteous blame-shifting.
- **Not equal to irresponsibility.** Weber explicitly denies that *Gesinnungsethik* "is identical with irresponsibility," yet his examples make it look that way — a tension in the concept.
- **Convergence at the limit.** The "supplements, not contrasts" conclusion undercuts the sharp dichotomy the term seems to assert; whether the two ethics are opposed or entangled is the live interpretive question.
