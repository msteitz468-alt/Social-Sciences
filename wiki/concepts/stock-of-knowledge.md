# Stock of Knowledge

## Definition as Coined

Berger and Luckmann (following [[schutz-alfred|Schutz]], 1966): the **social stock of knowledge** is the accumulated, socially distributed body of what is known and taken for granted in a society. It supplies [[typification|typificatory schemes]], recipe knowledge, and the “what everybody knows” that integrates discrete items of knowledge.

Attributed features:

- **Recipe knowledge** — pragmatic competence in routine performances under the pragmatic motive.
- Differentiated by **degrees of familiarity** and by **relevance structures** (what matters for my projects).
- **Socially distributed**: not everyone knows everything; expertise and meta-expertise (knowing who knows) structure access.
- Language and semantic fields select what is retained in the stock.
- Institutional meanings are **sedimented** into the stock and transmitted as tradition.

## Semantic History

Schutzian life-world vocabulary brought into the center of the [[sociology-of-knowledge]] by Berger and Luckmann’s redefinition (everyday knowledge, not only ideology). Related: common sense; collective representations (Durkheim — different theoretical architecture); culture as shared knowledge.

## Operationalizations

Theoretical; distribution of knowledge is itself a research topic (who knows what, with what legitimacy).

## Applications

Common-sense reality; expertise vs lay knowledge; occupational knowledge in [[secondary-socialization]]; sedimentation of institutional order; relevance and “until further notice” validity of everyday knowledge.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Stock” can sound static; B&L also analyze change, pluralism, and competing sub-universes. Measurement of a society’s stock is always partial.
