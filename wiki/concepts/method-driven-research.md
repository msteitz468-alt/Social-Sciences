# Method-Driven Research (and the Methodological Pathologies)

## Definition as Coined

[[green-donald|Green]] and [[shapiro-ian|Shapiro]] (*[[green-shapiro-pathologies-1994|
Pathologies of Rational Choice Theory]]*, 1994) distinguish **method-driven** (or
theory-driven) research — in which a favored technique or theoretical apparatus
governs which problems are addressed and how evidence is read — from **problem-driven**
research, in which a substantive empirical question governs the choice of method. They
argue (attributed) that the thin empirical yield of applied rational choice theory in
political science is a symptom of method-driven inquiry: the theory's development has
been insulated from empirical refutation by a recurring set of **methodological
pathologies**.

Green and Shapiro distinguish "pedestrian methodological defects" (statistical
misapplication, measurement error, small-N overreach) — which "come with the
territory" — from "fundamental and recurrent methodological failings rooted in the
universalist aspirations" that drive rational choice theorizing. Only the latter are
what they call pathologies, and they trace all of them to a method-driven rather than
problem-driven approach: "empirical research becomes theory driven rather than problem
driven, designed more to save or vindicate some variant of rational choice theory
rather than to account for any specific set of political phenomena" (attributed).

Green and Shapiro stress (attributed) that the pathologies are **not** inextricable
features of rational choice theorizing and that they do not argue rational choice
models should be abandoned; their claim is that "the rational choice approach must be
rethought fundamentally."

## The Pathologies

Green and Shapiro's chapter 3 taxonomy (all attributed to them):

- **Post hoc theory development.** Explanations engineered after the fact to fit any
  known regularity. They cite Fiorina and Shepsle's (1982) term **retroduction** —
  starting from an observed regularity X and asking "how might the world be structured
  so that X is an anticipated feature of it?", which typically yields many models all
  implying X. Because the theory is not specific about what counts as rational, "it is
  not obvious what sorts of behaviors, in principle, could fail to be explained by some
  variant." They quote Ordeshook (1993) conceding: "we can establish nearly any
  reasonable outcome as an equilibrium to some model… little more than an exercise in
  curve fitting." Data that inspire a theory cannot properly test it.
- **Slippery predictions.** Theories load up on unobservable constructs (tastes,
  beliefs, decision rules, equilibria); as the ratio of latent constructs to observable
  measures grows, whether any data confirm or disconfirm the theory becomes
  undecidable (an analogy to statistical underidentification). Green and Shapiro
  compare this to Marxist rescues of the "declining rate of profit" via countervailing
  tendencies.
- **Vaguely operationalized predictions.** Point predictions "invariably false to some
  degree" are rescued by an "approximation" framing, and defenders slide between point
  predictions and comparative-statics (direction-of-change) predictions to claim
  support when the absolute prediction fails.
- **Searching for confirming evidence.** Selecting on the dependent variable —
  dwelling on confirming historical episodes and successful predictions while ignoring
  failures or rival theories' equal successes; supportive lab experiments run without
  control groups "illustrate rather than test."
- **Projecting evidence from theory.** Asserting, without independent checking, that a
  datum consistent with rational choice logic is empirically true, or that a model's
  feature "mirrors reality." (Example: the Powell Amendment "sophisticated voting"
  story, which Krehbiel and Rivers 1990 showed rested on factual distortions.)
- **Arbitrary domain restriction.** Conceding the theory fails in a domain and simply
  restricting its scope to where it works, without independently specified limiting
  conditions — "draining lakes that contain problematic data." Green and Shapiro
  distinguish this from legitimate ceteris-paribus clauses and interaction effects: the
  defect is retreat "when an empirically testable set of limiting conditions is
  lacking." If the domain is defined as wherever the theory works, "testing becomes
  pointless."

## Semantic History

The vocabulary was coined in the 1994 book and became a reference point in the
[[rational-choice-controversy-debate|rational choice controversy]]; "post hoc"/
retroduction and "selecting on the dependent variable" have older, broader lives in
philosophy of science and methodology, which Green and Shapiro draw on rather than
originate.

## Operationalizations

The pathologies function as a diagnostic checklist Green and Shapiro apply to four
literatures — the [[paradox-of-voter-turnout|paradox of voter turnout]] (ch. 4),
collective action and [[free-rider-problem|free-riding]] (ch. 5), legislative behavior
and the [[social-choice|paradox of voting]] (ch. 6), and [[spatial-model-of-voting|
spatial models of electoral competition]] (ch. 7) — arguing that in each the apparent
successes dissolve into one or more pathologies.

## Applications

Beyond the book, the problem-driven/method-driven contrast has been invoked widely in
debates over formal theory, the "empirical implications of theoretical models" (EITM)
movement, and the qualitative–quantitative divide (see
[[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Rational choice theorists replied (see [[rational-choice-controversy-debate]]) that the
diagnosis is itself under-specified — that "post hoc" theorizing and idealization are
normal, productive phases of any maturing science, and that Green and Shapiro apply a
double standard, demanding of rational choice an empirical rigor they do not require of
its competitors. Green and Shapiro's own reply is that they hold rational choice to the
standard its proponents claim for it.
