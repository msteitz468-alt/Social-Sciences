# Haab (365-Day Vague Year)

## Definition as Coined

[[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] define the **Haab** as composed of **nineteen months**—eighteen named *winals* of 20 days each and a closing month of **5 days (Wayeb)**—totaling **365 days** that approximated the solar year (actually slightly longer). New Year’s Day = **1 Pop**; the previous day is the seating of Pop (**0 Pop**); day count in each *winal* runs 0–19 (0–4 in Wayeb). Yukatek month names: Pop, Wo, Sip, Sotz’, Sek, Xul, Yaxk’in, Mol, Ch’en, Yax, Sak, Keh, Mak, K’ank’in, Muwan, Pax, K’ayab, Kumk’u, Wayeb.

## Semantic History

- **Shared Mesoamerican heritage:** One of three common cycles (with 260-day almanac and 52-year [[calendar-round|Calendar Round]]) ancient across [[mesoamerica]]; not Maya-exclusive.
- **Relation to Long Count arithmetic:** For calendrical reckoning the *tun* = 18 *winals* = **360 days** (not 400), approximating the solar year, then vigesimal higher orders (*k’atun*, *bak’tun*, etc.)—distinct from the Haab’s 365-day civil/ritual year but thematically related to solar approximation (Sharer & Traxler).
- **Year-bearers:** Only four [[tzolkin|almanac]] days could occupy first position of the Haab: Classic **Ak’bal, Lamat, Ben, Etz’nab** (52 = 4×13 possibilities); by Spanish Conquest shifted to **K’an, Muluk, Ix, Kawak**.
- **Calendar Round:** Combination of 260-day almanac and 365-day Haab produces a 52-year Calendar Round (LCM 18,980 days). Except for the Maya (who had the [[long-count|Long Count]]), the 52-year cycle was the largest calendrical unit of all Mesoamerican societies; Mexica (Aztec) *xiuhmolpilli* (“year bundle”) with New Fire renewal is the central Mexican analogue.
- **Cyclic history and power:** Postclassic sources indicate cyclic history (repeated destiny); supernatural forces ruled numbers, time, and cosmos; elite calendar knowledge was a source of power, though farmers needed basic calendrical knowledge for daily agricultural life.

## Operationalizations

| Unit | Definition (attributed S&T 2006) |
|---|---|
| *k’in* | Day |
| *winal* (Haab month) | 20 *k’in* (0–19 day numbers) |
| Wayeb | 5-day closing “month” (0–4) |
| Haab | 18 × 20 + 5 = 365 days |
| New Year | 1 Pop (seating 0 Pop previous) |
| Calendar Round | 52 Haab = 73 × 260-day cycles = 18,980 days |
| Year-bearer | Almanac day name seating 0/1 Pop |

**Accuracy claim rejected:** Sharer & Traxler reject the popular claim that the Maya fixed the year more accurately than the Gregorian calendar—**no evidence**. They do report accurate **lunation** measurement (~29.53059 days modern): alternating 29/30-day months and possibly a 4,400-day = 149 lunations formula (~29.53020 days); Dresden Codex pp. 51–58 present 405 consecutive lunations (~32¾ years) as a solar eclipse table with skillful interpolation so discrepancy never reaches a full day.

## Applications

- Civil and agricultural year framing for [[classic-maya]] and Conquest-era Yucatan.
- Pairing with [[tzolkin]] for [[calendar-round]] dates on monuments and in codices.
- Year-bearer and New Year rites; comparative Mesoamerican New Fire / year-bundle ceremonies.
- Distinguishing “Vague Year” drift from true tropical year in chronological work—feature of the system, not a failure of arithmetic.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **No leap-day intercalation** — Haab drifts relative to seasons over centuries; do not treat Haab dates as fixed tropical-year seasons without independent anchors.
- **Superior solar-year myth** — rejected by Sharer & Traxler; credit accurate lunar/eclipse work instead.
- **Wayeb** carries ethnohistoric associations of danger and liminality in some sources—handle as attributed ritual ethnography, not universal Classic psychology.
- Ethnographic and colonial month-name lists (Yukatek) are the usual teaching set; Classic glyphic month names and regional variants need period-specific reading.
