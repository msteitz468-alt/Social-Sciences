# Maya Hieroglyphic Writing

## Definition as Coined

[[classic-maya|Classic Maya]] **hieroglyphic writing** is a logosyllabic system mixing logograms and phonetic syllabic signs. As of [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]], if decipherment means matching a sign to a specific sound/word, roughly **80% of the code is broken**, with meaning of many remaining glyphs understood without full pronunciation. The language of the Classic inscriptions is identified with **Ch'olti'an** (Eastern Ch'olan), not Yukateko.

## Semantic History

1. **19th c.–c. 1900:** Brasseur + Landa alphabet → day glyphs, bar-and-dot; zero/twenty, directions, Venus, months, Long Count established.
2. **Mid-20th c. block:** Morley & Thompson long treated much of the script as non-phonetic/ideographic and non-historical.
3. **Breakthroughs:** Yuri Knorosov’s phonetic readings; Heinrich Berlin’s **Emblem Glyphs** (1958) as kingdom titles; Tatiana Proskouriakoff’s historical hypothesis (reign groups at Piedras Negras); subsequent Stuart–Houston–Martin generation.
4. **San Bartolo:** painted texts c. 300–200 BC among the earliest Maya writing — complicates older Isthmian→Maya descent models (as of C&H 2015).

## Operationalizations

- Phonetic complements on logograms; polyvalence; disharmony rules in spelling.
- Emblem Glyphs = *[[kuhul-ajaw|k'uhul ajaw]]* (“holy lord”) titles of named kingdoms (30+ secure centers).
- Dynastic event glyphs: birth, accession (*johyaj ti ajawlel*), death/burial, war (“star-shell” etc.), bloodletting, parentage, captive relations.
- Four surviving codices (Dresden, Madrid, Paris, Grolier) — religious/astronomical content; Classic “history graven in stone” plus painted ceramics with ownership texts.

## Applications

Converts mute monuments into dynastic narrative — foundation of modern [[classic-maya]] political history and the Martin–Grube “Great Game” model of hierarchical polities ([[tikal]]–[[calakmul]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Incomplete decipherment; royal self-presentation bias; sample survival (stone > books); contact ethnohistory is Yucatecan and must not be projected uncritically onto Classic Ch'olti'an courts. When writing “died” is debated (Colonial court records vs earlier truncation).

### From Sharer & Traxler 2006

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.

**Dual foundation of native Maya history.** Chapter 3 frames calendrical anchoring and hieroglyphic recording of people and events as the two foundations of native Maya history; combining archaeology with historical sources for the final ~1,500 years yields a more comprehensive view than either alone. As of 2006 they judge the Maya the Native American society with the most detailed contemporaneous records (names, dates, events) to pair with archaeology, though incomplete (Classic books mostly lost).

**Sources of history.** (1) Pre-Columbian hieroglyphic texts (mostly Classic AD 250–900; few Late Preclassic) on durable media and lost books; (2) Conquest-era Maya Chronicles in European script; (3) Spanish histories, dictionaries, ecclesiastical and administrative records. Surviving pre-Columbian codices date to ~1200 or later: **Dresden**, **Paris**, **Madrid** (plus Grolier in broader literature). Spanish burning of “pagan” books (Landa) is held partly responsible for loss of ~2,000 years of learning; public religion and writing vanished with Conquest-era repression.

**Structure of the script.** Reading order usually left-to-right in rows, top-to-bottom in columns; paired columns read in pairs. Glyphs = main signs + affixes; conflation and infixation possible. Thompson 1962 catalog: 861 glyphs; Macri & Looper Classic catalog: 673 distinct signs + 213 variants = 886 Classic signs. Mixed **logosyllabic** system: main signs often logograms, affixes often phonetic complements/grammatical affixes. About **80%** of the most commonly used glyphs deciphered as of 2006. Language of essentially all Classic lowland inscriptions: **Ch’olan** — Classic Mayan (Houston, Robertson, Stuart), ancestral to Ch’olti’/Ch’orti’; earlier Justeson & Fox north/south language split superseded for the bulk of the corpus.

**Pioneer decipherment sequence.** Landa’s *Relación* (~1566) calendar and “alphabet”; Förstemann (from 1880): zero/twenty, vigesimal notation, Dresden Venus, Long Count base, distance numbers; Goodman head-variant numerals and correlation work; mid-century Thompson–[[morley-sylvanus-g|Morley]] logographic/non-historical consensus; [[knorosov-yuri|Knorosov]] (1950s) CV syllabics + synharmony + Landa as Spanish letter-names; [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]] (1960) historical method at [[piedras-negras]] and [[yaxchilan]]; 1970s+ Lounsbury–Schele–Mathews Palenque dynasty; Stuart & Schele Copan founder; Martin & Grube (2000) eleven-kingdom *Chronicle*. Name-tagging chain: *u tup*, *u baak*, *u lak*, *kakaw* vessels; *way* (spirit companion) recognized by decipherment.

**Media and recording.** Earliest known texts Late Preclassic; media include stone monuments, portable objects, [[san-bartolo]] painted glyphs; Classic freestanding monuments, lintels, stucco, wooden lintels ([[tikal]]), signed sculptors (Usumacinta), bone/pottery, murals, Comalcalco bone strips. Recording pioneers: Maudslay, Morley, Ian Graham (*Corpus of Maya Hieroglyphic Inscriptions*).

**Origins (unresolved as of 2006).** Writing in Mesoamerica by Middle Preclassic (San José Mogote ca. 700 BC; Monte Albán stelae ca. 500–400 BC). Two southern Late Preclassic traditions: western **Isthmian** (likely early Mixe-Zoquean) vs eastern early **Mayan** (Pacific plain into southern highlands). Import via coastal trade vs local development both possible; earliest known Maya texts appear fully developed (perishable precursors hypothesized). Majority of Preclassic dated monuments southern; lowland public writing rarer until Classic (preservation vs practice debate). El Portón Monument 1 (~400 BC) glyphs may be earliest Maya writing if ancestral forms hold.

**Content bias and Terminal Classic silence.** Classic texts mostly politics and ritual for the elite; nonelite majority unmentioned. Royal records offer **no narrative** of Terminal Classic systemic change — only fewer monuments and declining workmanship. Deciphered texts uniquely recover elite kinship, dynastic succession, and alliances; settlement archaeology supplies the complementary nonelite spatial frame (see [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]).

**Colonial tools for decipherment.** Earliest Colonial Mayan dictionaries invaluable (language closer to hieroglyphic era); modern Ch’orti’ folklore structure parallels *Popol Vuh* poetic conventions (Fought); Late Classic Palenque texts show structural parallels to ethnohistoric/contemporary Mayan narratives.
