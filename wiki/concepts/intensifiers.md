# Intensifiers

## Definition as Coined

Wiki slug for the set of substances [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] groups as tropical **"drug foods"** in *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985): tea, coffee, chocolate, tobacco, rum, and sugar — commodities whose European consumption rose sharply from the seventeenth to twentieth centuries. They intensify effort, rest, palatability, or habit: alcohol/tobacco rest and deaden hunger; coffee/chocolate/tea stimulate without nutrition; sugar provides calories and makes the others attractive when combined.

**Naming note:** Mintz's primary label is **"drug foods,"** not "intensifiers." "Intensification" in the same book names a *process* of meaning-acquisition ([[extensification-and-intensification]]), not this substance class. This page holds the substance-class concept under a short slug; treat "drug foods" as the attributed term.

## Semantic History

Mintz stresses that of this set only sugar escaped religious proscription (Aquinas: sugared spices medicinal, do not break the fast). Sucrose's effects are less visibly behavioral than alcohol or caffeine, which he links to its "secular" virtue. The set later anchors food-studies and commodity-history work on stimulants and industrial diets.

## Operationalizations

Commodity histories of the set; coupling analysis (tea+sugar); working-class budget shares; duty and smuggling series (tea); caloric contribution of sucrose over time.

## Applications

British industrial diet; Caribbean and Asian colonial production (sugar islands; Indian tea after China); temperance and factory discipline (tea vs alcohol); modern processed-food ubiquity.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Drug" metaphor risks medicalizing culture; not all items are pharmacologically alike; gender and age differentials in intake need finer data than national averages Mintz often must use.
