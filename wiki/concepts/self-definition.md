# Self-Definition

## Definition as Coined

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]].) **Self-definition** is "the power to
name one's own reality"; **self-valuation** is the power to define the content and worth of
that reality. Collins draws the distinction sharply: self-definition rejects the *power
dynamics* of externally imposed [[controlling-images]] (challenging *who* has the authority
to define), while self-valuation asserts the positive *content* of Black womanhood. The key
political move, she argues, is not disputing the technical accuracy of an image (e.g.,
refuting the "matriarchy" thesis) but contesting the power to define at all — the act of
self-definition itself validates Black women as human subjects.

## Semantic History

The term draws on a longer African-American tradition of "coming to voice" and, per
Collins, on constructed knowledge (Belenky et al. 1986) — knowledge gained by "jumping
outside" externally authored frames. Collins turns it into a core analytic axis of
[[black-feminist-thought]], one of four recurring themes of Black women's consciousness
(alongside self-valuation, self-reliance/independence, and a changed self as the basis of
empowerment).

## Operationalizations

Collins locates self-definition in three historical **"safe spaces"** where it was
nurtured: (1) relationships among Black women (friendship, mother–daughter ties, church,
clubs); (2) the Black women's **blues tradition**; and (3) the community of Black women
**writers**. Safe spaces are exclusionary as a *strategy* (Black-and-female) yet oriented
to an inclusionary, just society; they are threatening to dominant groups precisely because
they are free of surveillance. Collins notes post-1970s desegregation made them harder to
sustain without being stigmatized as "separatist."

## Applications

Applied to literature (Mary Helen Washington's typology of "suspended," "assimilated," and
"emergent" women), to the blues, to motherhood and love relationships as sites of
self-definition (Chs. 7–8), and to "erotic autonomy" (drawing on M. Jacqui Alexander 1997
and Audre Lorde's "Uses of the Erotic"). The Black feminist self, Collins argues, is
**relational/communal** — achieved through accountability to family and community, not
through individualist separation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Collins flags a countervailing finding: Beth Richie's (1996) study of incarcerated women
found that a "strong Black woman" self-image could make battered women *less* likely to
seek help, experiencing it as personal failure — a caution that self-valuation content can
itself be turned into a controlling image (the "superstrong Black mother"; see
[[othermothers]]). Critics more broadly ask whether individual self-definition can bear the
political weight Collins assigns it absent structural change — which is why she insists
self-definition is necessary but not sufficient for empowerment.
