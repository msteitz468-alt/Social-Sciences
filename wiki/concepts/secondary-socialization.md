# Secondary Socialization

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (1966):

> Secondary socialization is the internalization of institutional or institution-based “sub-worlds.” … [It] is any subsequent process that inducts an already socialized individual into new sectors of the objective world of his society.

Attributed features:

- Internalization of role-specific knowledge and vocabularies (occupational, educational, etc.).
- Typically more **anonymous** and less emotionally charged than primary socialization; teachers need not be “significant others” in the primary sense.
- Subjective reality of secondary internalizations is usually **more brittle** unless intensifying techniques (novitiate, total immersion, high affective charge) are used.
- Must maintain consistency with the primary base — or manage inconsistency.
- Under the pragmatic motive, much secondary knowledge is recipe knowledge from the social [[stock-of-knowledge|stock of knowledge]].

## Semantic History

Technical contrast with [[primary-socialization]] is the contribution; related to role theory, professional socialization, and religious novitiate literatures. Connects objective institutional differentiation (division of labour, sub-universes of meaning) to subjective career.

## Operationalizations

Process/role analysis. Later education and occupations research operationalizes professional socialization without always citing B&L.

## Applications

Schooling, occupations, military/religious training, political indoctrination; [[plausibility-structure]] for specialized worlds; alternation when secondary processes aim at radical re-socialization.

## Critiques and Limitations

The primary/secondary binary can oversimplify lifelong identity work and peer socialization. “Brittleness” is a theoretical expectation, not a measured finding in the 1966 text.
