# Zapotec Writing

## Definition as Coined
The hieroglyphic and calendric inscription tradition of the Valley of Oaxaca — the earliest known writing system in [[mesoamerica|Mesoamerica]] on the argument of [[marcus-joyce|Joyce Marcus]] in [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983), beginning with Monument 3 at [[san-jose-mogote]] and the danzante corpus of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] I.

## Semantic History
[[caso-alfonso|Alfonso Caso]] established the corpus and calendric framework (1928–1947); Marcus reframed the monuments as political-propagandistic texts (conquest slabs, captive lists, genealogies). The script remains undeciphered beyond calendrics, names, and place signs — all readings are correspondingly provisional.

## Operationalizations
Epigraphic analysis of carved monuments (danzantes, Building J conquest slabs, genealogical lintels and murals), cross-read against [[ethnohistory|ethnohistoric]] sources on the Zapotec calendar.

## Applications
Anchors the chronology of Zapotec [[state-formation]] claims — writing and the 260-day calendar appear before the founding of Monte Albán.

## Critiques and Limitations
Undeciphered; interpretations (e.g., Building J slabs as conquest records) are attributed positions, not established readings.

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — dating, the Xòo glyph, and persistence

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The authors confirm [[caso-alfonso|Alfonso Caso]] as first to identify the glyphs at Monte Albán and other Valley of Oaxaca sites as culturally Zapotec, and report (citing Marcus 2020) that the earliest evidence for Mesoamerican writing overall dates to roughly **600 BC** — before Monte Albán's founding or Mesoamerican urbanism — first appearing on carved stones including [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]]'s Monument 3. The 260-day calendar is dated to existing by the Rosario phase (700–500 BC) on the strength of hieroglyphs accompanying Monument 3: the glyph reading "1 Earthquake" uses "Xòo," a known Zapotec day-name, linking the hieroglyphs to an archaic form of the Zapotec language (Marcus and Flannery 1996:130). Zapotec writing persisted — on stone monuments, human leg bones, bark paper, and deerskin — until the 16th-century Spanish conquest.
