# Atlantic Façade

The ocean-facing coastlands of western Europe — Morocco and Iberia through
Armorica, Britain, and Ireland to Scandinavia and Iceland — treated as a
coherent analytical zone bound by maritime connectivity. The concept's fullest
statement is [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]]'s *Facing the Ocean* (2001)
([[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|source]]), an explicitly Braudelian program:
as [[braudel-fernand|Braudel]] made the Mediterranean the protagonist of the
[[longue-duree]], Cunliffe makes the Atlantic the structuring constant of
western European prehistory and history, 8000 BCE–1500 CE.

## Definition as Coined

Cunliffe (2001) defines the façade physically — an arc of peninsulas across 40
degrees of latitude (Canaries to Iceland, ~4,000 km along 15°W), each
accessible within five to ten days' sailing of its neighbours — and
structurally, through two "core zones" of maritime articulation: a southern
core from the Tagus to the Souss (Morocco) commanding the Straits of Gibraltar
("the marine antechamber of the Mediterranean"), and a northern core from the
Shannon to the Loire commanding the Channel, with Galicia as the metal-rich
"stepping-stone" between them. His substantive thesis is that the communities
of this zone developed, from the Mesolithic onward, a distinctive "Atlantic
mind-set" or "oceanic mentality" — shaped by rich, reliable maritime resources
and by living at the perceived edge of the world — whose elements he lists as
"challenge, awe, a heightened awareness of time, and a deep restlessness"
(Cunliffe 2001). The recurring formula is that along the façade "the sea joined
and the land divided": adjacent maritime communities across the water were more
akin to one another than to their landward neighbours.

## Semantic History

The idea has a long historiography that Cunliffe himself traces: Mackinder's
*Britain and the British Seas* (1902) still treated the sea as barrier;
O. G. S. Crawford (1912, 1936) first read artifact distributions as sea-borne;
Fleure and Peake, Cyril Fox's *The Personality of Britain* (1932), and
[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s Neolithic "Argonauts" (1946) developed the
"western seaways" tradition; Bowen's *Britain and the Western Seaways* (1972)
gave the first full diachronic overview. Mid-century versions leaned on
diffusionist mechanisms ("megalithic missionaries") that radiocarbon dating
demolished; Cunliffe's 2001 restatement deliberately rebuilds the zone's
coherence on indigenous development plus exchange networks rather than
colonization. The concept has also been politically mobilized: the 1867
Inter-Celtic Congress in Brittany and the EU-era "Arc Atlantique" commission
(Faro, 1989) both invoked Atlantic-façade identity — Cunliffe closes by asking
whether the Arc Atlantique is "a timeless geopolitical reality" or "yet another
Atlantic myth" (Cunliffe 2001).

## Operationalizations

- **Geographic:** land within 50 km of the ocean, along the peninsular arc
  (Cunliffe 2001).
- **Chronological:** Cunliffe periodizes the façade's connectivity into three
  cycles — a first cycle of gift-exchange and shared megalithic/ritual koine
  (Mesolithic to c. 800 BCE), a second cycle of Mediterranean-driven
  articulation from the Phoenician foundation of Gadir (c. 800 BCE–c. 400 CE;
  see [[phoenician-expansion]]), and a third cycle of post-Roman and medieval
  commercial reintegration (c. 400–1500 CE; see [[medieval-atlantic-trade]]).
  He flags the model himself as "a broad generalization" (Cunliffe 2001).
- **Material proxies:** polished-axe petrology and exchange distributions,
  [[megalith]] and [[passage-grave]] distributions, [[atlantic-bronze-age]]
  metalwork and hoards, amphora typologies, wreck evidence.

## Applications

The concept organizes Cunliffe's readings across the whole sequence:
Mesolithic coastal sedentism and Europe's earliest cemeteries
([[coastal-adaptation]], [[mesolithic]]); the indigenous Atlantic
megalithic phenomenon; Beaker and Bronze Age maritime exchange
([[bell-beaker-culture]], [[atlantic-bronze-age]]); Iron Age parallels among
[[castro-culture|castros]], brochs, and cliff castles; the post-Roman inversion
in which, as landward states hardened their frontiers, the sea became "a core
to the peripheral lands" (an explicit reworking of [[core-periphery]]
vocabulary); and the fifteenth-century moment when the façade displaced the
Mediterranean as "the innovating interface" of a "true world system"
(Cunliffe 2001; the world-system usage extends
[[world-systems-analysis|Wallerstein]] far beyond its original early-modern
scope).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Identity leakage.** The construct is etic; the recurring risk is sliding
  from shared material forms to a shared people or language. Cunliffe's own
  "Atlantic Celtic" proposal — that a Celtic lingua franca crystallized along
  the seaways over the first cycle — is a contested position within the
  [[celticity-debate]], not an established finding, and he hedges it
  ("no doubt", "might have been") even while advancing it.
- **Environmental determinism.** The oceanic-mentality thesis (tidal rhythm →
  time-awareness → megalithic monumentalization; littoral iodine → Mesolithic
  fertility) is explicitly speculative in the source and should never be
  promoted beyond attribution.
- **Cycle-model smoothing.** The three-cycle periodization is a heuristic;
  Cunliffe's own chapters document long stretches (Roman period, 6th–4th
  centuries BCE for Ireland and Britain) when Atlantic connectivity thinned to
  local cabotage or lapsed — the façade's coherence is variable, not constant.
- **Pre-aDNA.** All population-movement claims date to 2001; the 2018-era
  archaeogenetic literature (e.g., on Beaker-associated population turnover in
  Britain) post-dates and in places challenges the book's mobility-minimizing
  readings — date-stamp accordingly.
