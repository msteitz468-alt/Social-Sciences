# Zone of social abandonment

## Definition as Coined

[[biehl-joao|João Biehl]] coined **zone of social abandonment** for places like
[[vita-porto-alegre|Vita]] — and the hundreds of informal asylums and "geriatric houses" around
Porto Alegre — where people cut off from family life and medical care are left
to wait with death. In *[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]* he argues such zones "accelerate the death of the unwanted" and that in this "bureaucratically and relationally sanctioned register of [[social-death]]" the human, mental, and chemical are complicit (attributed, Biehl 2005). They make regeneration of the *abandonados* impossible and dying imminent: "Before biological death, there comes their social death" (attributed).

## Semantic History

- **2001–2005.** Biehl developed the phrase in a 2001 *Social Text* article and fully in
  the 2005 monograph. It is an **etic** analytic construct, not a local legal category — Vita
  itself was not on city maps and was not the object of a dedicated remedial policy.
- **Relation to adjacent concepts.** Overlaps with Goffman's **[[total-institution]]** (batch
  living, control) but stresses *extramural* abandonment: Vita is often the *end* of a
  hospital and family career, not a total institution with a full professional staff. Resonates with
  **[[everyday-violence]]** (Scheper-Hughes) and social-death literatures without collapsing
  into them. Houses the **[[ex-human]]** and is organized in part by **[[pharmaceutical-being|pharmaceutical]]** control.
- **Citizenship paradox.** Biehl observed (March 1997) "incipient citizenship" generated *along with* social death: recovery-area men rehabilitated, some accessing AIDS disability pensions and antiretrovirals, while the infirmary continued in utter abandonment (attributed).
- **Later uptake.** Entered medical-anthropology and global-health writing as a name for dump-sites of care under democratization and pharmaceutical citizenship — track further as sources arrive.

## Operationalizations

Not a survey index. Operationalized ethnographically by: (1) the place-type (informal asylum /
geriatric house receiving welfare pensions and abandoned persons; Porto Alegre ~25 such houses c. 1976 → >200 by Biehl's period, ~70% clandestine); (2) the social process
(family, hospital, and police deposition; staff of resident-volunteers; absence of continuous
clinical care; political-philanthropic patronage); (3) the subjective condition of those left there (Catarina's "people forgot me").

## Applications

- *[[biehl-vita-study|Vita]]* — type case: Vita (founded 1987, Porto Alegre) and the city's
  network of similar institutions (documentation 1995–2003).
- Human-rights inspections after Santa Luisa (34 deaths, 1998): raids of 200+ services; few closed; judges often gave owners "one last chance" (attributed via city officials Oliveira/Godoy).
- Comparative potential: other post-asylum and informal long-term care dump-sites under
  neoliberal and post-authoritarian welfare regimes (not yet ingested).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Place-name metaphor risks reifying a spatial "zone" when the process is relational (family +
  clinic + pharmacy + state + charity politics).
- Single-city, single-life depth in *Vita* is the concept's empirical base in this vault —
  general claims stay attributed until independent cases converge.
