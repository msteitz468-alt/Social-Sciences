# Cultural Toolkit (Repertoire)

Core concept of [[swidler-ann|Ann Swidler]]'s [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]]. Introduced in her 1986 ASR article and elaborated in *[[swidler-talk-of-love-2001|Talk of Love]]* (2001). All claims here are **attributed** to Swidler.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Swidler, Talk of Love.)* Culture is to be understood "less as a great stream in which we are all immersed, and more as a bag of tricks or an oddly assorted tool kit… containing implements of varying shapes that fit the hand more or less well, are not always easy to use, and only sometimes do the job." A person's culture is the repertoire of symbols, stories, rituals, and worldviews available to them for building [[strategies-of-action|strategies of action]] — not a unified belief-system that dictates conduct.

> "We must think of culture less as a great stream in which we are all immersed, and more as a bag of tricks or an oddly assorted tool kit (see Swidler 1986) containing implements of varying shapes that fit the hand more or less well, are not always easy to use, and only sometimes do the job."

## Semantic History

- **Toolkit (1986).** The founding image, emphasizing selection: people pick up and put down cultural tools, including — Swidler adds — the kind of self they inhabit, which is itself one of the tools. This is how she reconciles a "constitutive" view of culture (culture makes the actor) with a "tool-using" one: cultures constitute *multiple* selves.
- **Repertoire (2001 refinement).** In *Talk of Love* Swidler prefers "repertoire," on the model of an actor's, musician's, or dancer's repertoire, because it adds that culture cultivates **skills and habits**: people can be more or less good at their cultural repertoire, and hold it with variable mastery (some orientations ingrained and effortless, others "insecurely learned"). A repertoire is organized not by logical coherence around one theory but around "core situations or problems," so mutually incompatible models (love as choice / as commitment / as organic growth) coexist and are each deployed as they fit.
- **Caution against voluntarism.** The tool/trick imagery risks implying deliberate rational manipulation, which Swidler rejects: "people are often 'used by' their culture as much as they use it."

## Operationalizations

Swidler does not measure the toolkit with an attitude scale; she infers it from interview talk, along three dimensions of cultural *use*: how much of the available repertoire a person draws on, how far they integrate culture with experience (vs. segregate the two, e.g. "policy statements" held apart from revision), and how coherent the resources they deploy are. Two people "floating in the same cultural soup" can share a love vocabulary yet use it in radically different ways.

## Applications

- **Incoherence as resource.** Holding multiple, even contradictory rationales gives a person more argumentative material to defend a way of life: "if one argument fails, there are plenty of others available" (Swidler, drawing on Bourdieu).
- **Bat-echolocation image.** People locate their own views by "bouncing" them off the range of alternatives visible in their environment — orienting by distance from, as much as agreement with, available positions; novel events prompt people to widen the repertoire.
- **Distanced culture.** People "know much more of their culture than they use"; materials from one's past can remain in memory yet be functionally inert — a reason culture is hard to "find," since "when culture fully takes, it so merges with life as to be nearly invisible" (Biernacki, cited).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Positions.)* The toolkit's chief theoretical neighbour and rival is [[habitus]]: both are practice-oriented, dispositional views of culture, but Bourdieu's habitus makes stronger claims about durable, class-structured, hard-to-shed disposition, whereas Swidler's repertoire is more plural, switchable, and lightly held. Critics ask whether "toolkit" underweights how deeply culture can constrain, and whether the selection metaphor smuggles in the very voluntarism Swidler disavows. Swidler's own reply routes through [[codes-contexts-institutions]] (culture's power without depth) and [[settled-unsettled-lives]] (when the toolkit constrains loosely vs. when ideology builds new strategies).
