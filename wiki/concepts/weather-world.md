# Weather-World

## Definition as Coined

The **weather-world** is [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s term (developed in
*[[ingold-being-alive-2011|Being Alive]]*, 2011) for the world reconceived not as a
solid **surface** dividing earth from air and studded with discrete objects, but as a
**medium** — a zone of earth, sky, wind, light, sound and weather in which inhabitants
are bodily immersed and through which they move and perceive. To be alive, Ingold
argues, is to be exposed to the weather: perception and life happen *in* the medium, not
*on* a surface (attributed, Ingold).

## Semantic History

*(Enriched from [[ingold-being-alive-2011|Being Alive]] during integration.)* The
weather-world sharpens the [[dwelling-perspective]] against the "landscape as surface /
cultural image" picture (Ingold's "Landscape or weather-world?"), and against the
"soundscape" as another bounded surface; it recasts the earth-sky as the very medium of
the [[meshwork]] along which [[wayfaring|wayfarers]] move.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — a phenomenological reframing of environment as medium
rather than surface, used to redescribe perception, mood/atmosphere, and inhabitation.

## Applications

- **The shape of the earth** (Ch. 8): Ingold contrasts the earth as a **globe** seen
  from outside (the "exhabitant's" view — a closed outer surface) with the earth as a
  **sphere** experienced from within by an inhabitant, open to the sky — using child-
  development studies (Vosniadou & Brewer) as a springboard through Augustine, Kant,
  Gibson and Heidegger.
- **Earth, sky, wind and weather** (Ch. 9): to be alive is to be immersed in and to
  **breathe** the medium — weather binds inhabitant and world; grounded ethnographically
  (Koyukon, Sámi, Khoisan).
- **Landscape or weather-world?** (Ch. 10): a critique of "landscape as surface / cultural
  image" (extending his exchange with Christopher Tilley), reworked through Deleuze &
  Guattari's smooth/striated and the haptic/optical (feeds [[dwelling-perspective]]).
- **Against "soundscape"** (Ch. 11): sound, like light, is not a bounded surface to be
  scanned but a **medium** we are immersed in — so "soundscape" repeats the surface error.

## Critiques and Limitations

Like Ingold's other reframings, the weather-world is a programmatic phenomenology whose
analytic and empirical purchase — beyond re-description — has been questioned.
