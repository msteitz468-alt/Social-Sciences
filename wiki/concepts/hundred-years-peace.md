# The Hundred Years' Peace

## Definition as Coined

The opening thesis of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]* (Part One). [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] observes that "the nineteenth century produced a phenomenon unheard of in the annals of Western civilization, namely, a **hundred years' peace — 1815–1914**": the European Great Powers were at war with one another for only about eighteen months across the century (the Crimean War aside), against an average of sixty to seventy years of major war per preceding century.

Polanyi's explanation is the crux: the peace was not produced by the balance of power alone (which normally produces war), but by a novel **economic "peace interest."** The nineteenth-century international order rested on **four institutions**: the balance of power, the international **gold standard**, the self-regulating market, and the liberal state — all, in his account, "shaped by one common matrix, the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]]." The crucial social instrumentality was **haute finance** — international high finance (the Rothschilds, later J. P. Morgan) — "an institution *sui generis*... [that] functioned as the main link between the political and the economic organization of the world." Because the international monetary and credit system required currency stability, finance had a functional interest in peace — though Polanyi insists its motive was gain, not pacifism, and that "power had precedence over profit... ultimately it was war that laid down the law to business."

## Semantic History

Polanyi dates the structural end of the balance-of-power system to 1904–07 (the Entente Cordiale and the Anglo-Russian entente), when the flexible multipolar Concert hardened into two rigid blocs. He treats the interwar "conservative twenties" as a doomed attempt to restore the pre-1914 gold-standard order, whose failure produced the "revolutionary thirties" (Britain off gold 1931, the Five-Year Plans, the New Deal, the Nazi seizure of power). The collapse of the gold standard is "the invisible link" between the disintegration of the world economy and the political transformation of the 1930s — though it "did hardly more than set the date of an event which was too big to have been caused by it."

## Operationalizations

Polanyi tabulates the war-years count for the period and tracks the sequence of currency stabilizations and one-party-government dates in interwar Europe (in his Notes on Sources) as evidence of the gold standard's disciplining pressure.

## Applications

Grounds Polanyi's claim that the nineteenth-century international system was an outgrowth of market society, so that its collapse — and the twentieth-century catastrophe — traces to the same [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] and its [[double-movement|failure]]. "In order to comprehend German fascism, we must revert to Ricardian England" (see [[fascism-and-market-society]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Diplomatic historians dispute the primacy Polanyi gives haute finance over dynastic, ideological, and strategic factors in explaining the long peace.
- The "one common matrix" claim (four institutions all reducible to the self-regulating market) is a strong reductive thesis, contested as overdetermined.
