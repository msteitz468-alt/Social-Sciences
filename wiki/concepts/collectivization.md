# Collectivization

## Definition as Coined

**Collectivization** names the Soviet drive to replace individual peasant farming with collective and state farms. Early kolkhozy and sovkhozy existed under [[war-communism]] and [[new-economic-policy|NEP]] as limited experiments; the **mass collectivization** of 1929–33, as narrated by [[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Ch. 16), was a forced "revolution from above" tied to grain procurement failure and First [[five-year-plan|Five-Year Plan]] food/labour needs.

## Semantic History

- **Early forms:** War-communism-era kolkhozy (communist idealists) and sovkhozy ("socialist grain factories") met peasant resistance; Lenin reportedly recorded peasants saying "We are Bolsheviks, but not communists" (Carr, Ch. 3).
- **1927–29 grain crisis:** Marketing shortfalls, extraordinary measures, Ural-Siberian methods, and failed attempts to wage rural "class war" against kulaks within a NEP framework (Carr, Ch. 13).
- **Great Break-Through:** Stalin silent on collectivization April–November 1929; then *Pravda* article claiming the middle peasant "has entered the Kolkhozy"; still "hovered on the brink" (Carr). January 1930 resolution and dekulakization drove mass amalgamation; brigades of party officials, workers, and trade unionists (Carr estimates 100,000–200,000 persons engaged in 1929 collection/supervision operations).
- **Dizzy from Success:** Stalin's 2 March 1930 article reined in excesses while consolidating the turn (Carr).
- **Costs:** Carr reports famine mortality estimates in the range of **one to five million**; livestock losses lasting longer than grain recovery (into the later 1930s). He frames the episode as revolution from above, placing heaviest burdens on the classes declared its beneficiaries.

## Operationalizations

| Instrument | Function (Carr) |
|---|---|
| Kolkhoz | Collective farm; peasant amalgamation under quotas |
| Sovkhoz | State farm; large "grain factories" |
| MTS (Machine-Tractor Stations) | State-controlled mechanization and political leverage over villages |
| Dekulakization | Expropriation/deportation of designated kulaks |
| Advance quotas / brigades | Predetermined regional delivery quotas; urban cadres in countryside |

## Applications

- Agrarian base of forced industrialization ([[five-year-plan]]).
- Endpoint of NEP's market-feeding model; Carr: break with NEP "logically followed" from planning/industrialization commitments.
- Comparative reference for peasant–state conflict and [[peasantry-as-class]] stratification politics.
- Ethical and historiographical centre of Stalin-era violence (Carr notes horrors of collectivization, camps, and later purges as blots not erased by wartime victory).

## Critiques and Limitations

Carr's 1979 short history is summary resolution — not a demographic monograph. Famine magnitude, regional variation (Ukraine, Kazakhstan, etc.), and intentionality debates require specialist sources not yet ingested. Keep death-toll figures **attributed to Carr's range**, not wiki voice. Do not equate collectivization with all Soviet agricultural policy after 1933 without dating.
