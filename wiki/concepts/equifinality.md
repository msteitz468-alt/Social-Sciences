# Equifinality

## Definition as Coined

**Equifinality** is the methodological problem that distinct causal processes can converge on the same observable outcome, so the outcome alone cannot discriminate among the processes that might have produced it. In archaeology the concept was given decisive force by [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] and Clive Orton's *Spatial Analysis in Archaeology* (1976), as reported by Matthew Johnson (attributed, Johnson 2011; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]): simulating different modes of prehistoric exchange (down-the-line trade, directional trade, and other mechanisms) generated fall-off curves — the decline in artifact frequency with distance from a source — that were **similar rather than distinguishable** from one another. "Different spatial processes can produce very similar fall-off curves" (Hodder and Orton 1976, as cited by Johnson 2011).

## Semantic History

Johnson (2011) frames Hodder and Orton's result as a direct challenge to the confidence of 1970s [[processual-archaeology|processual]] spatial science that computer simulation could "test" or "prove" a specific interpretation of an archaeological pattern. If several qualitatively different processes are equifinal with respect to the observable data, matching a simulation to the data is not sufficient grounds to claim that simulation identifies the actual past process. Johnson presents this result as one strand feeding Hodder's own move away from a purely processual, statistically driven mode of archaeological reasoning and toward the position later elaborated as [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual archaeology]].

## Operationalizations

Equifinality is demonstrated, not merely asserted, through simulation: multiple candidate generative processes are run under matching parameters and their output distributions compared against the observed archaeological pattern and against each other. Where outputs converge, the archaeological pattern underdetermines the choice among candidate processes.

## Applications

Cited (attributed, per Johnson 2011) as a foundational case for the limits of "testing" as a warrant for interpretation in archaeological spatial analysis; linked to [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s subsequent argument (developed via ethnoarchaeological fieldwork among the Nuba, see [[hodder-ian]]) that data alone, however rigorously modeled, cannot adjudicate between competing accounts without additional contextual or interpretive argument.

## Critiques and Limitations

Equifinality does not show that simulation or quantitative spatial analysis is worthless — only that a match between simulated and observed patterns is not, by itself, sufficient confirmation of an interpretation. The concept has been mobilized on both sides of the [[processual-postprocessual-debate|processual/post-processual debate]]: processualists as a technical problem to be managed with better models and independent tests ([[middle-range-theory-archaeology]]); post-processualists (following Hodder) as evidence that data-driven "testing" claims were overstated in principle. All claims here are attributed to Hodder and Orton (1976) as reported by Johnson (2011); the original monograph is not separately ingested in this vault.
