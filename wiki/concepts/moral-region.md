# Moral Region

## Definition as Coined

[[park-robert-e|Park]] (*[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* Ch I): population segregates not only by interests but by "tastes or… temperaments." Every neighborhood "may assume the character of a 'moral region.' Such, for example, are the vice districts… A moral region is not necessarily a place of abode. It may be a mere rendezvous, a place of resort" (attributed).

Park insists the term need not mean criminal or abnormal place: it applies where a "divergent moral code prevails" because people are dominated by a taste, passion, or interest rooted in "original nature" — art, music, horse-racing — producing "moral, rather than intellectual, isolation" (attributed). Such regions and their "eccentric and exceptional" inhabitants must be accepted "as part of the natural, if not the normal, life of a city" (attributed).

## Semantic History

Tied to Park's claim that the city is a "mosaic of little worlds which touch but do not interpenetrate" and a laboratory where human nature appears "in excess." Adjacent to but not identical with [[natural-area]] (ecological/economic grouping vs moral-code dominance).

## Operationalizations

Research agenda: Bohemia, half-world, red-light district — external facts, vocations, mental types, entry/exit, license vs restriction. Party politics should study appeals by moral region.

## Applications

Urban moral order; vice and leisure geography; temperament and "social contagion" among segregated exceptional types (period language — attributed only).

## Critiques and Limitations

Moral labeling from outsider/reform standpoint; "contagion" and genealogical rhetoric (Jukes/Ishmael) is period and must stay attributed; overlaps natural-area inventory.
