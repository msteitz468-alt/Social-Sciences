# Territorial State

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003), [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] pairs **territorial states** with [[city-state|city-states]] as the only two stable political forms among early civilizations. Territorial states (in his usage) tend toward larger unitary scale, dispersed rural majorities, elite/administrative urban enclaves rather than farmer-filled cities, more hierarchical bureaucratic or **delegational** administration, stronger central control of exotic goods and elite craft production (two-tier economies), and often stronger sacralization of remote kings. Attributed to Trigger 2003.

## Semantic History

Related to older "territorial kingdom" / "unitary state" vocabularies in Egyptology and Andean studies; Trigger systematizes the contrast as a comparative type rather than a culture-historical label. Distinct from modern territorial nation-state.

## Operationalizations

Trigger’s seven-case typology (study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]; comparison: [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]). Classic territorial examples in his treatment: Egypt and the Inka; Shang discussed with territorial leanings in the synthesis. Bonded free labour (corvée, drafted service) often stronger relative to chattel slavery than in city-state systems (comparative note).

| Dimension | Territorial-state pattern (Trigger) |
|---|---|
| Scale | Larger unitary territories |
| Settlement | Dispersed rural majority; elite/admin centres |
| Administration | Hierarchical bureaucratic and/or delegational |
| Exchange | Central monopoly of exotics; two-tier craft |
| Kingship | Remoter rulers; divinity claims often stronger |
| Afterlife optimism | Trigger notes more afterlife-optimistic elite ideologies than typical city-state patterns |

## Applications

Comparative [[state-formation]] and [[early-civilization]] analysis; contrasts with city-state exchange and urban density; links [[inka-empire]], Egypt as territorial cases, and [[wealth-appropriation]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary may compress variation (imperial vs non-imperial territorial forms; secondary expansions). Specialist chronologies revise case classifications after 2001. Trigger stresses neither form evolves into the other within early civilization — both are replaced by later, more complex preindustrial forms.
