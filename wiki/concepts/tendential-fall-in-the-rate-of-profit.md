# Tendential Fall in the Rate of Profit

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume 3]]*, Part Three, [[marx-karl|Marx]] argues that because only variable capital produces [[surplus-value]] while capitalist development continuously raises the [[organic-composition-of-capital|organic composition]] (dead labour relative to living), the general rate of profit s/(c+v) has an inbuilt tendency to fall — "the law of the tendential fall in the rate of profit". Marx presents it as a *tendency*: Chapter 14 enumerates counteracting factors — more intense exploitation, depression of wages below their value, cheapening of the elements of constant capital, relative surplus population, foreign trade, and the growth of share capital content with interest rather than profit. Stamatis (1977, per Mandel) showed Marx develops the law already under a *rising* rate of surplus-value. All theoretical claims attributed to Marx.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Classical economics | The falling profit rate as observed anxiety — Smith (competition), Ricardo (diminishing returns/rent). Marx reads "Ricardo's anxiety" as a sensed threat to the system's permanence |
| Marx 1894 | Endogenous tendency from the labour theory of value; the mass of profit can rise while the rate falls |
| 20th-century debates | Crisis theory battleground; Okishio theorem (1961) as the formal challenge; empirical disputes over the capital/output ratio |

## Key Claims (attributed to Marx)

- **Internal contradictions (Ch. 15).** Overaccumulation up to "absolute overproduction of capital" (added capital yields no added profit); crises as violent devaluations of capital that restore profitability; "the real barrier of capitalist production is capital itself"; plethora of capital alongside surplus population.
- **Cyclical anchor.** The multi-year renewal cycle of fixed capital is "one of the material foundations for the periodic cycle" of business (Part One, Ch. 9 of the volume); a crisis is "always the starting-point of a large volume of new investment."
- **Not underconsumption alone.** Marx's scattered crisis remarks resist a single-cause reading — production of surplus-value ("first act") and its realization ("second act") are distinct constraints.

## Operationalizations

Rate-of-profit measurement is theory-laden: disputes over whether national-accounts capital/output and wage-share proxies track c:v and s/v (Gillman 1957; Shaikh 1977 vs the "neutral technical progress" claim) belong to the receptions below.

## Applications

- Core of Marxist crisis and breakdown theories: Grossman's collapse extrapolation (1929), profit-squeeze and long-wave accounts (Mandel), against disproportionality and underconsumption alternatives — positions surveyed under [[reproduction-schemas-debate]] and in Mandel's introduction (as of Mandel 1981).
- The Okishio theorem (1961): if capitalists adopt only techniques raising their own profit rate at given wages, the equilibrium rate cannot fall — the principal formal challenge; Mandel's rebuttal (capitalists forced to mechanize; fixed capital bracketed out) is a position, not a verdict.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The law's validity, empirical standing, and relevance are all contested among serious scholars — neo-Ricardian critics reject it formally; defenders dispute the critics' abstractions. Recorded, not adjudicated.
- Engels's editorial hand: parts of Ch. 13–15's apparatus (a turnover digression, an elaborated manuscript note) are signed Engels interpolations.
