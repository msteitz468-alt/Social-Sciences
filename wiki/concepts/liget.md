# Liget

**Liget** — [[ilongot|Ilongot]] "anger," energy, passion — the emic term at the center of the Rosaldos' Ilongot corpus. Renato Rosaldo's *[[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-1980|Ilongot Headhunting]]* (1980) cross-references Michelle Rosaldo's *Knowledge and Passion* (1980, not yet ingested) "for an extended analysis of this key Ilongot term."

## Definition as Coined

*(Emic; documented at the 1967–74 fieldwork moment; attributed to the Rosaldos.)* Liget names the anger/energy that Ilongot men were expected to possess, moderate, and vent: the ideal adult man fused "knowledge" and "anger." Insults (*bengen*) — calling a man *depyang*, incompetent, "one who fails to bag game" — provoked liget and could become the retrospectively identified "source" (*rapu*) of a [[feud]]. Grief was said to intensify liget; taking a head in a [[headhunting]] raid was described as "throwing away" that burden.

## Semantic History

Within the 1883–1974 span Rosaldo reconstructed, liget's behavioral outlets were historically variable: raiding in violent epochs, foreclosed after the 1972 martial-law moratorium — after which some men turned to New Tribes Mission Christianity as an alternative means of coping with grief (Rosaldo's Afterword, Radu's case). In Rosaldo's later *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]* (1989), the "force" of bereaved rage became the pivot of his positioned-subject argument.

## Operationalizations

Never quantified; documented qualitatively through informant exegesis, narrative, and observed conduct (insult vocabularies, suitor ordeals, raid participation). The knowledge–anger fusion functions as an evaluative folk standard, not a measurement construct.

## Applications

- **Feud and raiding conduct** (Rosaldo 1980): Luku, whose "boundless anger" exceeded the knowledge–anger fusion, was regarded "more as a public menace than as a man admirable" — his 1952 beheading of a Butag man, violating an oath and the era's norm of raiding only lowlanders, set the Butag–Rumyad feud in motion.
- **Peer envy as historical motor**: Rosaldo argued that mutual envy among age-peers — a liget dynamic — drove the rapid chains of head-takings (1952) and marriages (1955–58) that give Ilongot history its episodic, cohort-structured shape.
- **Gender**: men's greater "anger" and lesser "fear," tied to distant hunting/raiding treks, were the Ilongot rationale for male courtship initiative; suitors' ordeals tested composure under provocation.

## Critiques and Limitations

An emic term of one society at one documented moment; not a general-purpose analytic of emotion. The deeper treatment (M. Rosaldo, *Knowledge and Passion*) awaits ingestion — claims here rest on R. Rosaldo 1980.
