# Pacification of Existence

## Definition as Coined

**Pacification of existence** is [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s name, in the closing chapters of *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964), for the positive alternative to [[one-dimensionality|one-dimensional society]]: the redirection of technology and reason toward *attenuating* rather than intensifying the struggle for existence. He insists the concept is not utopian in the pejorative sense (attributed): "'Pacification,' 'free development of human needs and faculties' — these concepts can be empirically defined in terms of the available intellectual and material resources and capabilities and their systematic use for attenuating the struggle for existence. This is the objective ground of historical rationality."

Marcuse specifies criteria for judging a "transcendent project" (an alternative to the established totality) rationally superior: it must preserve civilization's productive achievements, accurately diagnose the existing totality, and offer "a greater chance for the pacification of existence." Crucially he holds pacification requires *reducing* the "overdeveloped" standard of living, not merely redistributing it, and he issues "a warning against all technological fetishism": the shift "involves a political reversal," not automatic liberation through machines. He distinguishes a "liberating" mastery of nature (reducing misery, violence, and cruelty toward nature and animals) from a "repressive" one, and criticizes ideological appeals to "the natural" — including racial-inferiority arguments — as protecting an unjust society.

## Semantic History

The concept carries forward the utopian dimension of Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization* (1955) into the bleaker frame of 1964, supplying the affirmative pole the diagnosis otherwise lacks. It anticipates later ecological and "de-growth" critiques (in its insistence on reducing overdevelopment) and, in its call for "a new technology," the critical philosophy of technology. In the Conclusion Marcuse argues the institutions compatible with pacification "defy the traditional classification into authoritarian and democratic, centralized and liberal administration."

## Operationalizations

Marcuse claims (uniquely among his key concepts) that pacification *can* be empirically defined — by the gap between a society's technical capacity to reduce toil, scarcity, and violence and its actual deployment of that capacity. He offers no index, but grounds the criterion in "the available intellectual and material resources."

## Applications

Pacification of existence is the destination of Marcuse's argument: the goal that [[technological-rationality|redirected technology]] would serve, the content of [[the-great-refusal]], and the standard against which one-dimensional affluence is judged wanting. It is a reference point for critical theories of technology, ecology, and post-scarcity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note (attributed) the tension between Marcuse's claim that pacification is "empirically definable" and his admission that critical theory holds "no concepts which could bridge the gap between the present and its future"; the vagueness of "a new technology" and of who effects the required "political reversal"; and the paternalism latent in prescribing reduced consumption (see [[false-needs]]).
