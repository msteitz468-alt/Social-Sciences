# Foraging Mode of Production

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, especially Introduction and Ch. 5.)* The **foraging mode of production** is Lee’s Marxist-inflected characterization of hunter-gatherer economy among **band-organized** peoples. He distinguishes **mode of production** from **mode of subsistence**: foraging starts from wild game, plants, fish/shellfish but also includes ownership of the factors of production (land, labor, tools), organization around production, and allocation of products.

Lee’s core statement:

> “Central to the foraging economy are a sexual division of labor; a simple technology; a collective, nonexclusive ownership of land and resources; and widespread food sharing within and among local groups according to the principle of generalized reciprocity.” (Lee 1979, Ch. 5)

**Scope.** Applies to hunting-gathering peoples organized as bands (!Kung, Mbuti, Hadza, Great Basin Indians, Dene and Cree, Inuit, Australian aborigines, and similar)—**not** to groups such as Northwest Coast Indians who hunted/gathered/fished but were organized as stratified chieftainships with highly developed production systems. Lee cites Meillassoux (1973) and Jordaan (1975) for similar MOP definitions. Political-economic, not merely technical, definition.

**Three factors of production.** Ch. 5 sets out the first factor (tools/toolkit); labor is deferred to Ch. 9; land to Ch. 12 ([[nore]]). Explicit Marxist analytic frame fused with Stewardian cultural ecology and systems theory (Introduction: Steward, Godelier’s French school, ecological systems theory).

## Semantic History

Lee adopts Marx’s mode of production (means/forces of production vs social relations of production; base and superstructure—Marx 1859) as going beyond Steward’s “culture core.” He treats **contradiction** as constant even in pre-class societies; uses a modest dialectic close to systems-theory feedback, rejecting Hegelian metaphysical dialectics. Research goal: uncover Godelier’s “inner dynamic” of a foraging society on the threshold of triple transformation to agriculture, feudalism, and capitalism (Introduction).

Brings mode-of-production analysis into hunter-gatherer studies during the critical turn; competes with purely formalist and purely ecological framings. In dialogue with—but distinct from—Sahlins’s [[domestic-mode-of-production]] and with the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis (low work effort, plant-primary security).

## Operationalizations

### Structural features (Lee Ch. 5 theory)

Relative to agriculture, foraging leaves much reproduction of means of subsistence to nature, so foragers fit organization into niches nature affords, yielding five features Lee enumerates:

1. **Mobility** over a wide area (~2000–3500 km walked per year in the food quest for tropical foragers).
2. **Environmental upper limits** on group size.
3. **Flexible group structure** under annual/regional resource variability.
4. **Limits on material wealth** from the need to move.
5. **Elaborate rules for reciprocal resource access** despite varied landownership ideologies.

**Immediate return.** Among tropical foragers most food is distributed and consumed within the local group **within 48 hours** of collection—steady work/leisure without a single harvest-peak season (contrast husbanding/saving of agricultural-pastoral economies).

**Sexual division of labor.** Virtually universal among foragers: men hunt and gather; women primarily gather and very occasionally hunt; both sexes fish and gather shellfish. !Kung operationalization: men combine gathering with hunting (bring vegetables if no kill); sexes almost always segregated in food-getting; women typically go in groups of 3–5 for defined plant species and return same day—never overnight (Ch. 5).

**Camp as sharing unit.** Food is never consumed alone by a family but always (actually or potentially) shared with a living group/band of up to 30+. Even if only a fraction of able-bodied go out, meat and gathered foods are divided so every camp member gets an equitable share.

> “The hunting band or camp is a unit of sharing, and if sharing breaks down it ceases to be a camp.” (Lee 1979, Ch. 5)

Generalized reciprocity within camp reported for hunter-gatherers worldwide (Sahlins 1965). Dishes/plates unknown in 1960s Dobe: people at a single fire eat from the same bowl.

**Land: collective, nonexclusive—not absent.** Lee rejects “hunters own no land”: land divided into tracts/“countries” ([[nore|n!ores]]) used primarily but not exclusively by a band/camp; core owners are a kin-based collective (men and women) from whom permission is sought; groups regularly host and make reciprocal visits—Lee’s impression many hunter-gatherers spend more of life outside home country than in it.

**Tools private, food shared.** Lee rejects “no private property” as inaccurate: land and resources collectively owned/utilized, but tools and belongings are the owner’s—meat may be distributed throughout camp while the bow and arrow that killed the animal belong to the hunter (subject to arrow-sharing and distribution rules; see [[leveling-mechanisms]], [[hxaro]]). “The ‘worker’ owns the means of production.”

**Hierarchy provisional.** Privilege, status differences, and hierarchy appear “far less developed in foraging societies as a group than in any other kind of human society”—with open questions about male dominance and elder control (e.g. Australia) as possible “exploitation” (Hindess and Hirst 1975 cited).

**Storage vs sharing.** Food storage facilities minimally developed; mongongo stores could be built at Apr–May harvest but are not; large-kill biltong lasts ≤~2 months technically but is mostly redistributed within days.

> “The prime reason, in short, for the lack of food storage facilities lies not simply in the ecological circumstances but in the practice of food sharing, one of the core values of the ideological system that surrounds the foraging mode of production.” (Lee 1979, Ch. 5)

**Toolkit design criteria.** Tools few, lightweight, local materials, multipurpose—imposed by sharing, self-sufficiency, and mobility (from unimproved calcrete nut-cracking cobbles to complex hunting bows). Carrying devices indispensable: mobility plus diet of hundreds of small nuts/berries/roots; Lee argues a vegetable-food carrier was a prerequisite for Pleistocene human economic and social life (Appendix E).

### Empirical Dobe spectrum (still foragers, 1960s)

Despite experiments with agriculture and stock, few Dobe !Kung crossed the farming/herding threshold:

- **1963–4:** hunting-gathering supplied **80–95%** of subsistence at most camps (!Goshe up to ~30% domestic calories).
- **1967–9** good rains: domestic share rose (45% !Goshe; 20–25% other camps); **/Du/da remained 100%** hunting-gathering.

Vegetable foods primary; meat highly esteemed but secondary for basic security (Ch. 6–8). Preface synthesis: vegetable foods **60–70%** of diet; [[mongongo]] up to half the vegetable diet; game **30–40%** of total calories (Ch. 8 restates ~40% all game / ~60% vegetable). Women’s gathering ~**two-thirds** of camp food (Ch. 11). July 1964 work diary (and Lee 1968a/1969a): adequate diet via **2–3 subsistence workdays per week**.

**Short-run plant strategy (Ch. 6):** spatial (eat way out—mongongo: 1.5 km week 1, 3 km week 2, 4.5 km week 3) × hierarchical (desirable classes first). Younger members go farther for Class I–II; older collect nearer Class III–IV; day’s foods pooled—“a modest form of primitive communism” (Lee’s phrase).

**Worldwide organizational core (Ch. 12 comparative):** seasonal concentration–dispersion and rules for reciprocal/joint access to key resources—more basic to the foraging adaptation than the patrilocal band model.

## Applications

- Frames Dobe data as a **system** (relations of production, not only calories and hours)—backbone of *[[lee-kung-san-study]]*.
- Underpins comparative claims about forager egalitarianism and evolutionary baseline of human economy.
- Organizes links among [[food-sharing]], [[nore]], [[sexual-division-of-labor]], [[mongongo]], [[leveling-mechanisms]], [[original-affluent-society]].
- Used for contemporary transformation analysis (Ch. 14): contradictions of moving from foraging MOP toward farming/herding under capitalist development in Botswana and armed struggle in Namibia.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Mode-of-production language is **theory-laden**—keep in attributed voice; never promote Lee’s MOP claims to wiki-voice “facts about all foragers.”
- Revisionists argue “foraging MOP” can reify a moment of marginalization or cattle-post articulation as a pure type ([[kalahari-debate]]). *(Attributed — [[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]] 1989.)* *Land Filled with Flies* treats forager-mode constructions (Meillassoux/Lee/Sahlins lineage) as carrying a nineteenth-century authenticity pedigree that must be abandoned for class analysis of historically integrated Kalahari formations; July energy agreement with Lee coexists with rejection of year-round isolationist inference ([[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]]).
- Northwest Coast exclusion shows technical “hunting-gathering” ≠ foraging MOP—boundary is political-economic and contested.
- Hierarchy “less developed” claim is provisional/comparative (Lee’s own wording); Australian elder/male dominance cases are open questions he flags.
- Immediate-return and 48-hour consumption are tropical-forager generalizations in Lee’s text—not automatic for all Arctic/complex foragers.
- Storage-vs-sharing thesis combines ecology (rain spoilage) and ideology; do not drop either half.
- Date-stamp all percentages to fieldwork years and camp; multiyear hierarchies shift (85 edible spp. drought list → 105 multiyear; major foods 8 → 13).
