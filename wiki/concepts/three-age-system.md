# Three-Age System

## Definition as Coined

The **three-age system** classifies prehistoric material culture by dominant cutting-tool metal (or its absence): **Stone, Bronze, Iron**. Northern European archaeologists — especially **C. J. Thomsen** (Copenhagen museum) and **Sven Nilsson** — raised earlier antiquarian suggestions to scientific classification. [[lubbock-john|John Lubbock]] (*[[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872|Pre-Historic Times]]*, 1865/1872) credits them and **adds a four-epoch English framework** by splitting Stone into [[palaeolithic|Paleolithic]] and [[neolithic|Neolithic]], then Bronze and Iron (attributed, Ch. I). He cites Lucretius and Hesiod as ancient anticipations of metal sequence, and notes stone continues in use into metal ages so "mere presence of a few stone implements" does not date a find.

## Semantic History

- Antiquarian hints (Eccard, Goguet, Borlase, Colt Hoare).
- Thomsen museum ordering → Nilsson, Worsaae Danish school.
- Lubbock popularization + Paleolithic/Neolithic labels (1865).
- Later: Chalcolithic/Copper Age inserted; regional sequences and absolute dating relativize any single ladder; Morgan rejected three-age as insufficient for "ethnical periods" ([[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]).

## Operationalizations

Association finds, closed contexts, and (today) radiometric dates. Lubbock already stresses statistical/tabulated evidence and warns against single-object dating.

## Applications

Foundational teaching scheme; still used as coarse European shorthand. Wiki deep-time tags (`bronze-age`, `iron-age`, `neolithic`) descend from this vocabulary.

## Critiques and Limitations

Not universal (Lubbock applied primarily to Europe); overlaps of materials; independent invention of metallurgy; teleological reading when fused with social-evolutionist stages.
