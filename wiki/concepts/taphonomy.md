# Taphonomy

The study of **how organic remains become fossil or archaeological assemblages** — death assemblages, scavenging, weathering, transport, and burial — a cornerstone of middle-range bridging for bone-bearing sites.

## Definition as Coined

Efremov coined **taphonomy** for the laws of burial; archaeologists and paleoanthropologists (e.g. C.K. Brain; [[binford-lewis|Binford]] in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* 1983) use it to separate hominin-produced patterns from non-human accumulations and post-depositional overprinting.

## Semantic History

Moved from paleontology into archaeology with early-hominid debates. In *Pursuit* Part I, Binford recounts challenges to Raymond Dart’s reading of Makapansgat and related bone+tool associations as self-evident meals of bloodthirsty early hunters: **Brain’s** model of limestone solution chambers, fissures, roof fall, and multi-agent access (owls, bats, primates/hominids, leopards, hyenas) treats deposits as **ecosystem-level** products, not single-species behavior. Waterhole actualistic work similarly separates modern death assemblages from claims about ancient base camps and shared food.

## Operationalizations

Actualistic studies of carnivore dens, waterhole death assemblages, experimental fracture, weathering; comparison to archaeological bone frequencies and damage. Intersects [[formation-processes]] more generally (cultural discard vs natural accumulation). In *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* (1978) Binford supplied actualistic taphonomic baselines directly: modeling **dog-yard attrition** (deriving "survival percentages" to correct part frequencies before behavioural interpretation), documenting **bone-grease processing** destruction, and — against the assumption that **spiral (green-bone) fractures** diagnose human marrow-cracking — reporting a directly observed **bear** producing spiral fractures, i.e. an equifinality warning (attributed, Binford 1978).

Schiffer (*[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987) embeds vertebrate taphonomy inside **n-transforms** and site/region environmental chapters (Brain vs Dart cave accumulation; carnivore vs hominid bone signatures; size-sorting by water, wind, burrowers) while treating cultural discard and trampling as parallel c-transform pathways on bone (attributed).

## Applications

Early man hunting vs scavenging ([[hunting-hypothesis]]); waterhole sites; control samples for [[faunal-analysis]]; caution against treating bone–tool association as self-evident diet evidence.

## Critiques and Limitations

Equifinality of damage patterns; incomplete actualistic coverage of past environments. *Pursuit* develops ethnoarchaeological bone **site structure** (drop/toss) more fully than formal taphonomic nomenclature — densify further with specialist monographs.

## Further Worked Examples (Johnson 2011, attributed)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson's *Archaeological Theory*]] (2011) reports
further taphonomic worked examples:

- **Sebastian Payne — dog gnawing and bone survival.** Payne showed gnawed bones decay
  faster than intact bones, so sites where dogs were present may show fewer bones (and
  different bone-type proportions) than otherwise-similar sites without dogs — risking
  the misinterpretation of economically similar sites as different (attributed
  single-study finding).
- **The "Schlepp effect."** Archaeologists assume the ratio of meat to bone is roughly
  constant per body part, so hunters carry back meat-bearing parts and leave low-meat
  parts at kill sites — informing the distinction between "kill sites" and
  "residential/home-base sites" from differential bone-part representation. Johnson
  cautions this signature can be confounded by cultural taboos or ritual valuation of
  specific body parts unrelated to caloric value (attributed, with caveats; see
  [[formation-processes]]).
- **Grain processing and animal ageing.** Grain-processing by-products (chaff, charred
  remains) are traceable via ethnoarchaeological and experimental analogy, citing
  Hillman's (1984) model derived from Turkish ethnographic fieldwork; animal
  tooth-eruption sequences and seasonal breeding patterns are assumed uniform, allowing
  inference of age-at-death and season of killing, and thence meat versus dairy
  exploitation strategies (wiki-voice methodological description).

These worked examples depend on a graded **[[uniformitarianism|uniformitarian
assumption]]** — strongest for physical/chemical processes, weaker for ecological ones,
and weakest for human cultural behaviour specifically — that Johnson (2011) argues
underlies all taphonomic and middle-range inference.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook framing (2012)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012, esp. Ch. 2 and environmental/subsistence chapters. Brief addition where the textbook adds pedagogical value; does not replace Efremov/Brain/Binford/Schiffer layers above.)*

**Gloss.** Renfrew & Bahn equate awareness of [[formation-processes]] with **taphonomy** in the broad sense: processes affecting both burial and post-burial history of finds. Differential survival is the baseline rule: inorganic materials (stone, fired clay, some metals under favorable chemistry) generally outlast organics; organic survival is governed largely by **matrix** and **climate** (with occasional catastrophic burial — Pompeii, Ozette, Cerén, Skara Brae).

**Matrix and climate controls (textbook examples).** Chalk preserves bone well; acid soils destroy bones and wood within years but leave posthole discolorations; copper can favor organic preservation by inhibiting microorganisms; caves act as natural conservatories (alkaline limestone caves especially); tropical climates are most destructive of organics; wetlands/anoxic waterlogging preserve organics if permanently wet (acid peat kind to wood/plants, may destroy bone/iron/pottery; Alpine lake sites preserve more broadly). The sea can scatter remains but also coat metals protectively.

**Zooarchaeological first question.** In subsistence chapters, taphonomy — what happens to bones between deposition and excavation — is treated as the first question for animal-bone interpretation; Brain’s re-reading of australopithecine-bearing caves (prey, not “killer man-apes”) and Binford actualistic work are the standard textbook exemplars (aligned with this page’s primary densification).

**Cannibalism caution.** Better taphonomy and funerary knowledge make most prehistoric cannibalism claims ambiguous; cutmarks may indicate butchery or secondary burial cleaning; “custom cannibalism” is hard to prove (Ch. 11 synthesis).
