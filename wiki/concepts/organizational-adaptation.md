# Organizational Adaptation (Tilly)

## Definition as Coined

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (1998): **adaptation** is the elaboration of daily routines — mutual aid, political influence, courtship, information gathering — and accumulation of **local knowledge** that lock categorical distinctions into place on both sides of a boundary. With [[organizational-emulation|emulation]], a **reinforcing** mechanism of [[durable-inequality|durable inequality]].

## Semantic History

Everyday sense of "adapting to circumstances"; Tilly specifies it as mechanism that makes categorical inequality self-reproducing even when original exploiters leave — subordinates and superordinates both invest in routines that assume the categories.

## Operationalizations

Workplace scripts; mutual-aid practices within niches; local knowledge barriers to newcomers; gendered interaction routines that survive formal equality rules.

## Applications

Immigrant niche mutual aid; co-worker gender scripts; clientelist chains inside organizations; underlife and coping that inadvertently stabilize categories; citizens fashioning routines around state-backed inequalities and sometimes condemning those who escape common obligations (until extreme pressure, e.g. South Africa 1980s — Tilly Ch. 7); health-care local knowledge locking professional categories.

## Critiques and Limitations

Can shade into tautology (whatever stabilizes inequality is called adaptation); agency of subordinates in resisting vs reinforcing is under-specified in brief statements.
