# Social Organization

## Definition as Coined

[[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]] used **social organization** as the deliberate counterpart to
**social structure**. Structure names the persisting framework of social relations — the descent
rule, the role-set, the terminological grid. **Organization** names the *working* of that framework
in real time: "the systematic ordering of social relations by acts of choice and decision," the
variation, adjustment, and individual agency through which structural principles are actually put
into practice. Where structure is the anatomy, organization is the physiology — Firth's own metaphor
in *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]* (1936), later made programmatic in *Elements of
Social Organization* (1951).

## Semantic History

The distinction was Firth's response, from within the [[structural-functionalism|functionalist]]
tradition, to what he saw as [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s over-static conception of
social structure. In *We, the Tikopia* the move is already visible: Firth insists kinship be studied
as graded, choice-laden behaviour — a classificatory system that merges kin terminologically while
individuals continually grade and adjust their actual obligations by genealogical closeness, wealth,
and rank. By 1951 he had generalized it: structural form is the "expectation," organization the
"arrangement" that real actors improvise within and against it. The concept is now read as an early
statement of the **structure/agency** problem and a precursor to later practice- and decision-oriented
anthropology; the vocabulary of "social organization" survives widely even where Firth's precise
structure/organization opposition is not invoked.

## Operationalizations

Firth operationalized organization ethnographically: tracing, in concrete cases, how a Tikopia land
division, a marriage, or a ceremonial gift-distribution was actually settled — who chose, who
deferred, where the customary rule was bent — and reading the gap between rule and act as data rather
than noise. This is a qualitative, case-based operationalization; it does not reduce to a quantitative
index.

## Applications

- Firth's own Tikopia corpus (kinship, land tenure, economy, ritual) is the type-case.
- The distinction underwrites his founding work in **economic anthropology** (choice and allocation
  as the analytic core of non-market economies).
- It is invoked wherever analysts separate the formal model of a society from the negotiated,
  variable conduct that realizes it.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note the structure/organization line can blur in practice (much "organization" congeals into
structure over time, and vice versa), and that later agency-centred theory (Bourdieu's
[[practice-theory|practice]], Giddens's structuration) reframed the same problem more powerfully.
Firth's version nonetheless remains a clear, early, ethnographically grounded formulation. See
[[firth-raymond]] and [[firth-we-the-tikopia-study]].
