# Scientific Racism

## Definition as Coined

**Scientific racism** is the analytic label for the use of scientific or pseudoscientific
methods, theories, and data to construct racial categories, rank them hierarchically, and
naturalize social inequality as biological fact. It is not a self-description: practitioners
took themselves to be doing ordinary science. The term is applied **retrospectively and
critically** to a body of work — craniometry, anthropometry, polygenist comparative
anatomy, eugenics, and later hereditarian IQ argument — that presented racist conclusions
as empirical findings. It shades into **pseudoscience** where methods were invalid or
data misused, but part of the concept's force is that some of it used the *accepted*
science of its day. Distinguish from [[racialization]] (the social process of marking
groups as races) and from [[social-darwinism]] (the broader application of
"survival of the fittest" to society).

## Semantic History

The practice runs from Enlightenment natural-historical classification (Linnaeus, Cuvier)
through nineteenth-century [[polygenism]] (Morton's craniometry, Nott & Gliddon) and the
Gobineau–Chamberlain–Grant–Stoddard line of racial-hierarchy theory, into eugenics and
Nazi race science. The analytic *label* consolidated in the mid-twentieth century as
anthropology and genetics repudiated the underlying claims — the 1950 UNESCO *Statement on
Race* and later the American Anthropological Association's 1998 *Statement on "Race"* being
landmarks. "Scientific racism" is now used both descriptively (for that historical corpus)
and polemically (as an accusation against contemporary hereditarian argument), and the two
uses should be kept distinct.

## Operationalizations

Historically instantiated through: **craniometry** (skull measurement as a race index);
**anthropometry** more broadly (body measurement to define fixed "types");
**intelligence testing** read hereditarily and comparatively across groups; and, in the
genomic era, contested attempts to map social race onto population-genetic clusters.
Each method's validity as a race index is exactly what critics from [[boas-franz|Boas]]
onward have attacked.

## Applications

The concept organizes the wiki's treatment of the race-science tradition and its
refutation. Its central anthropological antagonist is **Boas**: in
*[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]* (1940) and
*[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* (1911/1938) he argued —
using the anthropometric tools of his opponents — that cranial form is environmentally
plastic (immigrant head-form change within a generation), that "racial type" is a
misleading construct, and that race, language, and culture vary independently. See
[[race-and-culture-debate]], [[historical-particularism]]. The concept also underwrites
[[racialization]], [[conservation-of-races]] (Du Bois's counter-formulation), and the
critique of [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] ranking.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because "scientific racism" is a retrospective and evaluative label, its boundary is
contested: applied narrowly it names a defunct historical corpus; applied broadly it can
be used to foreclose empirical questions about human variation before they are examined.
Careful usage separates (a) invalid method or misused data, (b) valid method with racist
framing, and (c) the political uses to which findings were put — collapsing the three is
itself a source of error. The AAA and modern population genetics hold that most human
genetic variation lies *within* conventional racial groupings, so that "race" is a social
construct with real social consequences rather than a biological natural kind — a position
Boas anticipated on anthropometric grounds nearly fifty years earlier
*(as reported in Seiferle-Valencia 2017)*.
