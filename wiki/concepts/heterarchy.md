# Heterarchy

## Definition as Coined

Carole Crumley (1987), as reported by Johnson (2011), coined **heterarchy**
to describe complex societies whose organization need not be rigidly hierarchical:
"structures are heterarchical when each element is either unranked relative to other
elements or possesses the potential for being ranked in a number of different ways"
(Crumley 1987: 158). The concept decouples social/political **complexity** from
**hierarchy** — a society can be materially and organizationally complex while lacking
a single overriding rank order, or while containing multiple, cross-cutting rank
orders (economic, ritual, military) that do not align into one pyramid.

## Semantic History

Proposed within the neo-evolutionary cultural-evolution literature as a corrective to
schemes (Service's [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]; Fried's
[[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]) that measure social complexity primarily
by degree of hierarchy. Johnson (2011) situates heterarchy within a broader current in
[[multilinear-evolution|multilinear-evolutionary]] and archaeological-complexity
thinking that treats hierarchy as one possible organizational solution among several,
not a necessary correlate of scale or complexity — a move also echoed in later
reassessments of sites such as Cahokia, where scholarship has been characterized as
moving from an emphasis on hierarchy toward heterarchical/horizontal readings of power
before further complicating both (Cobb 2003, attributed, describing the historiography
as a "Doppler shift" rather than a full reorientation).

## Operationalizations

No single quantitative measure; applied qualitatively to identify cases where multiple,
non-nested ranking systems (e.g. ritual authority, wealth, military leadership) coexist
without full alignment, or where archaeological correlates of hierarchy (settlement
size ranking, mortuary wealth) fail to converge on one ordering.

## Applications

Cited by Johnson (2011) as part of the "recent multilinear evolution" literature that
incorporates agency, practice, and factional competition rather than ranking societies
on a single evolutionary scale (see [[multilinear-evolution]]). Also invoked in debates
over how to interpret complex polities such as Cahokia (see
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]) that resist a clean chiefdom/state binary.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with other complexity vocabulary, "heterarchy" risks becoming a residual category
for "complexity that isn't hierarchy," without its own operational criteria; its value
is primarily as a corrective reminder that hierarchy is not conceptually entailed by
complexity, rather than as a fully specified alternative typology.


### Trigger — heterarchy in city-state systems (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) associates greater **heterarchical** elements with [[city-state]] systems among early civilizations, contrasting them with more hierarchical bureaucratic/delegational [[territorial-state|territorial states]]. Not a free-floating network model of the whole society — early civilizations remain aristocracies with class appropriation. See [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]].

### Yoffee 2005 (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] uses heterarchy (alongside Crumley and others) when discussing Chaco/Cahokia and non-ladder trajectories: multiple overlapping hierarchies and corporate groups rather than a single neo-evolutionist rank scale. Complements his “dimensions of power” and anti-checklist program in *[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths]]*.

### Connah 2016 — Middle Niger / McIntosh (attributed)

[[connah-graham|Connah]] (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]*, 3rd ed. 2016) treats heterarchy as an important alternative model for socio-political organization — “organized horizontally rather than vertically” (citing McIntosh 2005: 187) — especially in discussion of Middle Niger urbanism around [[jenne-jeno]]. In the synthesis chapter he **accepts the idea’s importance** but cautions that it often “depend[s] on a lack of evidence for hierarchy rather than on the presence of positive evidence for heterarchy,” and that resolving the question needs more extensive urban excavation (attributed, Connah 2016).

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]: Sinclair Ch. 47; Monroe Ch. 48; Davies Ch. 49; MacDonald Ch. 57 on Jenné-jeno; ranges 8–9.)*

### African urban and state literature (Sinclair / Monroe)

Sinclair reports that questioning linear evolutionism and top-down/bottom-up dichotomies led to sophisticated treatment of hierarchical **and** lateral (heterarchical) organization, especially Mali’s Inland Niger Delta (McIntosh 1999, 2005), drawing on Tainter, Crumley, resilience theory, and eco-dynamics. Cities must not be understood only hierarchically; lateral/heterarchical forms warrant equal consideration.

Monroe (after S.K. McIntosh) reconceptualizes complexity as degree of internal differentiation *horizontal as well as vertical*. Hierarchy and heterarchy are **independent sliding scales**, not mutually exclusive. In the face of a clear alternative to the state, archaeologists must account for what factors encouraged social hierarchy rather than heterarchy—answer lies in power and political centralization. At [[jenne-jeno]], lack of differential wealth access has led scholars to suggest communities **resisted social hierarchy for centuries**, depending on heterarchical integration, before Islam and trans-Saharan demand incorporated them into hierarchical militaristic empires (Monroe summarizing McIntosh).

### Clan- and lineage-based complexity (Davies Ch. 49)

Matthew Davies argues African archaeology over-focused on hierarchical state/chiefdom models, misreading records for remains fitting Western ideals. Fortes & Evans-Pritchard’s headed vs acephalous dichotomy is insufficient. Kin-based societies organizing via clans, lineages, age-sets, sodalities, title societies, and situational specialists were subsumed as “tribe.”

**Heterarchy definition in use:** decision loci variously ranked, co-level, or alternating by context (Crumley); Inland Niger “horizontal” civil institutions without monopoly (McIntosh 2005). Blanton dual-processual corporate vs exclusionary power; archaic states flux between hierarchical and heterarchical forces. Superficially simple clan/lineage societies are complex in specialized roles, institutions, and inter-society dealings.

**Sirikwa tradition** (~AD 1200–18th c., Kenya): >200 sites, no socioeconomic stratification yet long regional dominance with pastoral vs agricultural specialization—complex **without** intra-settlement stratification. **Engaruka:** densities rightly called “urban” yet little stratification—likely heterarchical kinship, local councils, situational leaders. Davies concludes specialization/stratification often operated at **ethnic/economic group and ideology** scale while community-level personal accumulation was checked by decentralizing institutions.

### Companion site applications

- [[jenne-jeno]] — Phase III heterarchical specialist power-sharing; no elite burials (MacDonald/McIntosh via handbook).
- Copperbelt short-lived chiefly settlements (Sinclair).
- Rainforest: slave trade also **decimated heterarchical polities** (e.g. Eguafo) while militaristic states expanded (Ogundiran).
- [[igbo-ukwu]] — hierarchy without urbanism (related comparative problem; Ogundiran/Connah).

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — heterarchy as precursor to collective-action/dual-processual theory

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The authors position [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew's]] (1974) identification of chiefdom-like societies lacking overt centralized power, and Carol Crumley's heterarchy concept (with Ehrenreich, Crumley, and Levy 1995) as an important early conceptual step questioning neoevolutionist assumptions that political power is necessarily centralized and hierarchical. They quote Elizabeth Brumfiel's (1995:130) call that these new approaches "will require 'substantial model-building and testing in the future'" and present their own subsequent [[collective-action-theory|collective-action]] and institutional-analysis program — including [[dual-processual-theory|dual-processual theory]] — as a direct response to that call, situating heterarchy as a precursor rather than a rival framework.


## McIntosh 2005 primary densification (*Ancient Middle Niger*)

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]]; primary statement of the Middle Niger application.)*

McIntosh operationalizes heterarchy as **horizontal social complexity**: multiple overlapping and competing agencies of resistance to centralization, with the [[urban-cluster|polynucleated Urban Cluster]] as spatial instrument. Contrasts *ex astra* (top-down, citadel/state-dependent) city models. Essential city function: reconcile **corporate diversity** without fused hierarchy. Gatekeepers in hierarchical systems are “scleroses” blocking information about climate and threat.

**Positive archaeological correlates he emphasizes:** functional complementarity among satellites; craft/corporate segregation; long-run urban occupation without palace architecture; reputation-driven ethnohistoric analogies (Mande).

**Negative evidence he treats as telling:** no king, court, palace, elite burial chamber in pre-Islamic excavated sample after ~30 years (program claim).

**Comparative:** hierarchy and heterarchy **nest** (Mesopotamian “tapestry of power”; harmonic oscillation model) — pure either/or overstated (Ch. 6).

**Standing critique (Connah 2016, already above):** importance accepted; often depends on lack of hierarchy evidence; needs more extensive urban excavation for positive resolution. This vault records both positions without wiki-voice adjudication.

### Wright (2010) — the Indus Civilization as a differentiated, non-monarchic polity

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Ch. 9.)* Without invoking the term "heterarchy" directly, Wright's synthesis fits the pattern the term describes: she argues explicitly against the older view (attributed to D. Miller 1985) that the Indus Civilization's apparent material homogeneity concealed unspoken sanctions against publicly displaying inequality between rulers and non-elites. Instead, she reads differential house size, amenities, and access to prestige goods at Mohenjo-daro as evidence of a socially differentiated population — farmers, pastoralists, traders, merchants, and artisans of unequal rank coexisting within shared neighborhoods — without any accompanying evidence of centralized ruler iconography, palace architecture, or a single dominant political office (see [[city-state]], [[priest-king-statue]]). She states plainly that self-glorifying ruler imagery, common elsewhere among early states, is "totally lacking" in the Indus material record, and that there is "no basis on which to proclaim kingship" for any known figure. This combination — documented social/material differentiation without a demonstrated apex ruler or unified state apparatus — is offered here as an Indus-specific case bearing on the same organizational question heterarchy addresses, though Wright does not use Crumley's term herself.
