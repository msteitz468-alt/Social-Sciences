# Visualism

## Definition as Coined

**Visualism** is the term [[fabian-johannes|Johannes Fabian]] coined in *[[fabian-time-and-the-other-1983|Time and the Other]]* (1983, ch. 4) for **anthropology's ideological bias toward vision "as the 'noblest sense'" and toward geometry / graphic-spatial conceptualization "as the most 'exact' way of communicating knowledge."** He gave the concept a role "comparable to that of denial of [[coevalness]] or temporalization" in his argument. Visualism names the tendency by which *being able to visualize* a culture — to map it, tabulate it, diagram it, "see" it — becomes near-synonymous with *understanding* it, resting on "a corpuscular, atomic theory of knowledge and information" that invites quantification and diagrammatic display.

Fabian traced the bias to rationalism (Descartes' *res cogitans* / *res extensa*), empiricism (Locke: perception "is most aptly explained by words relating to the sight"; Hobbes' geometry), and, further back, to the classical and Renaissance **art of memory** (Frances Yates) and the diagrammatic pedagogy of **Petrus Ramus** (W. J. Ong), whose dichotomizing "method" — Ong: "the Ramist arts of discourse are monologue arts" oriented "toward an object world... rather than toward a person world" — Fabian saw echoed in "generations of visual devices used by anthropologists," from evolutionary trees to structuralist opposition-diagrams. He named a further step, **iconism** (via Whitehead's *Symbolism*), for the projection of sense data onto a spatial "systematic scheme."

## Semantic History

The concept is Fabian's coinage; its currency is largely within critical, reflexive, and visual-anthropology literatures. Fabian was careful to say (attributed) that visualism is *not* a call to purge the visual from anthropology, nor a blanket condemnation of visual anthropology — which he thought might paradoxically work *against* visualism by affirming intersubjective time. It names "an ideological current" that becomes damaging "especially where entangled with allochronic tendencies," functioning as a cognitive style "likely to prejudice the study of all kinds of cultural expression."

## Operationalizations

Fabian read visualism off the discipline's own methodological vocabulary and apparatus:

- The standard fieldwork toolkit — "learn the language, map the settlement, count households, draw genealogies" — treats language as a mere tool and privileges maps/charts/tables (an atomistic theory of knowledge) and a researcher-centered "time-economy."
- Organizing metaphors are visual-spatial in origin: trait, pattern, configuration, structure, model, cognitive map; system, integration, network, exchange.
- "Observing reason" and the "**méthodologie du regard**" (Moravia): Durkheim's social facts as *choses* ("things"); Bastian's museum-driven view that once-known "primitive" societies are "doomed."
- Fabian read Victor Turner's dominant/instrumental symbol charts, James Boon's Bali as a visual *topos*, and Marshall Sahlins's symbolic/practical-reason split (*Culture and Practical Reason*) as further instances — the last, he argued, effectively "resurrects the doctrine of survivals."

## Applications

Visualism supplies the fourth strand of Fabian's argument: sign-theories of culture are "theories of representation, not of production; ... of meaning, not of praxis," aligning "the Here and Now of the signifier... with the Knower, and the There and Then of the signified... with the Known" — what he called the "**rhetoric of vision**." His conclusion (attributed): "As long as anthropology presents its object primarily as *seen*... it is likely to persist in denying coevalness to its Other." Fabian's positive counter (drawing on Marx's Theses on Feuerbach, and agreeing with Chomsky that language is not "characteristically informative") privileges *hearing*, dialogue, and language-as-praxis — "the temporality of speaking... implies cotemporality of producer and product, speaker and listener, Self and Other." His critique converges with Bourdieu's attack on "objectivism," which constitutes the social world "as a spectacle presented to an observer."

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Against nostalgia.** Fabian himself resisted W. J. Ong's equation of the oral/aural with the "personal" and "humane," warning it "comes dangerously close to a kind of antiscientism which feeds on moral indignation and nostalgia for 'dialogue'"; knowledge can be depersonalized orally (rote, formula) as easily as visually. He wanted the aural invoked for *epistemological*, not sentimental, reasons.
- **Breadth.** As with [[allochronism]], sorting whole schools under one "bias" is a polemical argument rather than a neutral description, and rests on reading root metaphors.
- **Not the whole story.** Fabian granted that visualism alone does not explain anthropology's "political cosmology"; it had to be "cast into spatial schemes" and joined to temporal distancing before acquiring its political force.
