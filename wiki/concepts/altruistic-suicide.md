# Altruistic suicide

## Definition as Coined

[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] (*[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]*, Book II ch. 4) argues that **insufficient individuation** — when [[social-integration|social integration]] is **too strong** — produces suicide as surely as excessive individuation. He names **altruism** the state opposite to egoism (ego not its own property, blended with the group) and **altruistic suicide** the suicide caused by intense altruism.

| Variety | Character (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Obligatory** | Society “speaks the sentence of death”; duty under social and religious sanctions (e.g. reported widow, servant, aged-warrior cases) |
| **Optional** | Not expressly imposed but favored by public opinion; prestige of renouncing life |
| **Acute / mystical** | Self-destruction “purely for the joy of sacrifice”; impersonality at highest pitch |

In egoistic suicide society forbids death or at most suggests it; in obligatory altruism it **compels**. Durkheim defended including “heroic” duty deaths under suicide: excluding them for admiration would make classification arbitrary; every sort of suicide is an exaggerated or deflected virtue.

## Semantic History

1897 technical type; later revived for military, terrorist, and cult self-destruction debates — often loosely. Keep the 1897 sense: **excessive integration**, not mere “selflessness.” Book III: the altruistic current is **not** the driver of the modern European rise; it loses ground except where (e.g. army) a moderate intensity is required for military spirit.

## Operationalizations

- **Ethnographic/historical reports** (Durkheim’s 1897 synthesis of secondary sources — *not* timeless ethnography): duty suicides of the aged/sick, widows on husbands’ death, followers on chiefs’ death; optional and acute religious forms. Date-stamp as his argument; apply identity cautions (culture ≠ people; colonial knowledge politics).
- **Military suicide** — modern chronic theatre: soldiers’ rates much above same-age civilians (coefficients of aggravation 1.25–10 in his Table XXIII); rises with length of service and among those best adapted to military career; interpreted as military *esprit* (impersonality, external principle of action), not merely bachelorhood or disgust with service.
- **Inverse** with civilian egoistic rates: high civilian suicide → lower military aggravation coefficient (traditionalism protects civilians when moderate, becomes cause in the army when exaggerated).

## Applications

- [[durkheim-suicide-study]]
- Contrast phenomenology: egoist’s melancholy (only individual real) vs altruist’s energy of faith (individual unreal; goal outside this life)
- Religious pantheism as *reflection* of social absorption of the individual, not independent cause of suicide (Durkheim)

## Critiques and Limitations

- “Lower societies” framing is ethnocentric 1897 vocabulary — attributed, not wiki voice.
- Ethnographic base is second-hand and often colonial-era reporting; not independent evidence-class convergence.
- Military statistics have been reanalyzed; keep his numbers as *his* evidence base.
- Boundary with martyrdom, combat death, and sacrifice remains contested in later ethics and classification.
