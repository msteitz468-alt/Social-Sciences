# Global Difference

Analytic label used by [[connell-raewyn|Raewyn Connell]] in *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]* (2007) for the organising contrast in late-nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century sociology between the civilisation of the [[metropole]] and other cultures cast as "primitive" — the intellectual form through which empire entered the new science of society.

## Definition as Coined

Connell: the enormous spectrum of human history early sociologists took as their domain "was organised by a central idea: difference between the civilisation of the metropole and other cultures whose main feature was their primitiveness. I will call this the idea of **global difference**." Presented in many forms, the contrast pervades the sociology of the period.

## Semantic History

- **Connell 2007** — names the organising idea of imperial-era sociology, distinct from later classical-canon origin stories
- Adjacent older vocabularies: [[social-evolutionism|social evolution]], civilisation/savagery scales, *Gemeinschaft*/*Gesellschaft*, grand ethnography
- Not the same as Cold War "First/Third World" or world-systems [[core-periphery]] (though historically related)

## Operationalizations

Connell's evidentiary illustration (not a formal index): content of *L'Année sociologique* (1898–1913) — roughly 28% of large-type reviews focused on recent Western Europe / modern North America; twice as many concerned ancient/medieval, colonial/remote societies, or global surveys. Comparative method and "origins" genres enact the contrast.

## Applications

- Reconstructing sociology's founding against the industrial-modernisation-only narrative
- Linking race, gender, and sexuality as core early topics under progress ideology
- Explaining why Spencerian evolutionism was popular in settler colonies
- Diagnosing residues of global difference in modern general theory (e.g. Coleman's modern/primordial; Giddens's tribal / class-divided / class society grid)

## Critiques and Limitations

"Global difference" is Connell's retrospective analytic, not a self-description of all early sociologists; it risks homogenising diverse metropolitan traditions. Use with attributed voice and with the four-way non-identity rule when material, linguistic, and genetic categories appear in the sources Connell cites.
