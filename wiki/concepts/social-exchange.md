# Social Exchange (Blau)

## Definition as Coined

In [[blau-peter-m|Blau]]'s usage (following [[homans-george-c|Homans]]'s lead), **social exchange** refers to "voluntary actions of individuals that are motivated by the returns they are expected to bring and typically do in fact bring from others." Blau restricts the term deliberately: it covers actions "contingent on rewarding reactions from others" that "cease when these expected reactions are not forthcoming." Two limiting cases are excluded — action compelled by physical coercion (a holdup at gunpoint) and action springing purely from internalized conscience (alms given with no thought of return).

The **crucial distinction from economic exchange** is that social exchange "entails unspecified obligations": where an economic contract "stipulates the exact quantities to be exchanged," social exchange "involves the principle that one person does another a favor, and while there is a general expectation of some future return, its exact nature is definitely not stipulated in advance." Because the return cannot be bargained or enforced, social exchange "requires trusting others to discharge their obligations" — and thereby generates that trust, since relations "begin with minor transactions in which little trust is required" and expand as parties prove reliable. Blau notes this indeterminacy "is a substantive fact, not simply a methodological problem": the actors themselves "cannot precisely specify the worth of approval or of help in the absence of a money price."

Blau situates social exchange as "an intermediate case between pure calculation of advantage and pure expression of love" — it carries intrinsic significance (unlike strictly economic dealing) yet turns on extrinsic benefit and implicit bargaining (unlike profound attachment).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| [[exchange]] (Simmel, *Philosophy of Money*, 1900) | Exchange as *sui generis* sociation, the purest form of interaction — Blau's ultimate ancestor |
| Homans, "Social Behavior as Exchange" (1958); *Social Behavior* (1961) | Exchange of activity between persons, grounded in behaviorist reinforcement — the reductionist sense |
| Blau, *Exchange and Power* (1964) | Reciprocal reward-transaction under unspecified obligation; an emergent social process, not reducible to psychology |
| Emerson (1972) onward | Exchange **relations** in networks; power-dependence — the concept migrates to network analysis |

The word "exchange" thus names three overlapping things across the tradition — a form of sociation (Simmel), a reinforcement process (Homans), and an obligation-and-trust relation (Blau). Blau's is distinguished by the emphasis on *unspecified* obligation and *emergence*: "the emergent properties of social exchange... cannot be accounted for by the psychological processes that motivate the behavior of the partners."

## Operationalizations

Blau does not assign exchange a cardinal price. He identifies it structurally — as reciprocal transactions of rewards distinguished from unilateral (power-generating) transactions and from intrinsic (love/friendship) ties — and measures particular exchanges sociometrically (who consults whom for advice; who is named as a valued companion) in work-group studies. Costs are typed as **investment cost**, **direct cost** (chiefly the cost of subordination in paying respect or compliance), and **opportunity cost**. Homans, by contrast, ties exchange to measurable frequency and reinforcement schedules.

## Applications

The generative concept of [[social-exchange-theory]]: from social exchange Blau derives [[differentiation-of-power]] (via [[imbalance-of-exchange]] and [[unilateral-dependence]]), [[legitimation]], [[opposition]], and — through [[mediating-values]] and [[indirect-exchange]] — the structure of complex societies. The [[gift-exchange|gift]] literature (Mauss, Malinowski) supplies its comparative illustration; [[fair-exchange]] and [[reciprocal-obligation]] its regulative norms.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Tautology.** If reward is whatever is pursued, exchange claims risk circularity; Blau's defense is the contingency restriction plus derived testable hypotheses (see [[social-exchange-theory]]).
- **Economism.** The charge that it reduces all relations to markets; Blau replies that coercion and moral norms fall outside exchange.
- **Reductionism dispute.** Whether exchange is grounded in individual psychology (Homans) or is an emergent social process (Blau) is unresolved — see [[exchange-theory-reductionism-debate]].
- Distinguish sharply from [[exchange|Simmel's "exchange"]], which this page does not duplicate: Simmel's is a theory of value and sociation; Blau's is a theory of obligation, trust, and power.
