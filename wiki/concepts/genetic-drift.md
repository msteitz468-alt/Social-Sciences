# Genetic Drift

## Definition as Coined

**Genetic drift** is the evolutionary change in gene frequencies caused by
**chance** sampling of gametes/individuals in finite populations — as opposed
to natural selection, migration, or mutation. [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]
(2000) calls the name "somewhat misleading" because in other sciences "drift"
can imply directional trend; here it means random fluctuation.

## Semantic History

Sewall Wright and others formalized drift; Kimura's neutral theory elevated it
in molecular evolution ("survival of the luckiest," as Cavalli-Sforza 2000
glosses Kimura). Cavalli-Sforza's Parma work made drift *empirically vivid* for
human village systems using parish demography.

## Operationalizations

- Effective population size ($N_e$) and variance in allele-frequency change.
- Isolation-by-distance and stepping-stone models (Kimura & Weiss).
- Founder effects and bottlenecks as extreme drift ([[founder-event]]).

## Applications

Parma valley: high plains density vs mountain isolates; predicted between-village
genetic variance from migration and size matched observed classical-marker
variance (attributed, Cavalli-Sforza 2000 acknowledgments/ch. context). Small
human groups (islands, forager bands) expected to show strong drift signatures.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Hard to separate from selection without design.
- Human long-range migration and admixture swamp simple isolate models at large
  scales.
- Name confuses non-geneticists.
