# Collective conscience

## Definition as Coined

*Conscience collective* — the shared beliefs and moral sentiments of a society (or group). In *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]*, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] also speaks of **collective representations** and **suicidogenetic currents** as collective forces that dominate the individual and fix the social suicide-rate. A definite moral constitution, he argued, corresponds to each type of suicide and is interconnected with it.

He refused to hypostasize the collective conscience as a substantial soul: “We do not recognize any more substantial a soul in society than in the individual.”

The **canonical definition** comes from *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor]]* (1893): "The totality of beliefs and sentiments common to average citizens of the same society forms a determinate system which has its own life; one may call it the collective or common conscience" (attributed). Durkheim specified that it has no specific organ (diffuse throughout society), is independent of particular individuals ("they pass on and it remains"), connects successive generations, is "the psychical type of society," and is **not** coextensive with the total social conscience (governmental, scientific, industrial functions lie outside it). He varied it along **four dimensions**: **volume** (how much of social life it embraces), **intensity** (how strongly its states are held), **determinateness** (how precisely defined its beliefs/practices are), and **content** (historically variable — religious/domestic formerly, altruistic and person-centered today). Strong, intense, determinate collective states are the root of [[repressive-law|penal law]] and index [[mechanical-solidarity]].

## Semantic History

| Work | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| *Division of Labor* (1893) | Canonical definition; the four variables (volume, intensity, determinateness, content); its progressive **weakening** in intensity and determinateness as [[organic-solidarity]] grows — leaving room for individual variation — with the [[moral-individualism|cult of the individual]] as the one region that intensifies |
| *Suicide* (1897) | Currents of egoism, altruism, anomy; collective melancholy; systems of thought as symbols of social distress |
| *Elementary Forms* (1912) | Thickened into [[collective-representations|collective representations]] and the [[sacred-and-profane|sacred]]; society becomes conscious of itself in religion; categories of understanding as social (see [[durkheim-elementary-forms-study]]) |

English “collective conscience” / “collective consciousness” both appear; French *conscience* covers moral consciousness as well as awareness.

## Operationalizations

Indirect: stability of suicide-rates; simultaneous multi-country movements; association of rates with confessional and family structures; proliferation of pessimist philosophies as symptom of abnormal intensity of collective melancholy (Book III).

## Applications

- Explanation of why private shocks kill some: the individual is already steeped in a collective mood
- Normality of a limited current of collective sadness; morbid when it occupies too much place or is wholly excluded
- Sociology of knowledge line (Simpson notes): relating systems of thought to states of the collective conscience involved in egoism, altruism, and anomy

## Critiques and Limitations

Reification charges; difficulty of independent measurement; risk of circular explanation (rate explained by current inferred from rate). Durkheim’s own non-hypostasis footnote is the built-in hedge.
