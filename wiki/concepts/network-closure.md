# Network Closure

*(Attributed — multi-author.)* **Network closure** is dense interconnection among a set of contacts such that third parties can monitor and sanction behavior. [[coleman-james|Coleman]] treats closure as the structural condition for productive [[social-capital-coleman|social capital]] (norms, trust, intergenerational closure). [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]] accepts the mechanism while arguing (2005) that closure **decreases variation**, amplifies trust *and* distrust, and **reinforces the status quo** — a complement to [[brokerage]] across [[structural-holes|holes]], not its simple opposite.

## Definition as Coined

Burt (attributed, *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]*): closure increases the odds of being caught and punished for belief or behavior inconsistent with closed-network preferences; it protects new relations between friends of friends from decay and amplifies strong relations to extremes (see [[bandwidth-echo]]).

## Semantic History

From Coleman’s closure thesis through Burt 1992’s opposition (holes over closure) to Burt 2005’s **complementarity**: social capital as closure *within* a group plus brokerage *beyond* the group ([[structural-autonomy]]).

## Operationalizations

Network density/redundancy; constraint high when contacts are connected; panel evidence on decay and trust (Burt 2005 Chs. 3–4).

## Applications

Schools and families (Coleman); corporate workgroups; reputation systems and 360 evaluations as closure devices when hierarchy is delayered.

## Critiques and Limitations

Live dispute: [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]]. Whether closure’s trust benefits outweigh information costs is program- and task-dependent.
