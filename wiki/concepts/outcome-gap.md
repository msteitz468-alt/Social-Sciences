# Outcome Gap

## Definition as Coined

[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s term (in *Pathologies of Power*,
[[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003]]) for the growing disparity in health outcomes between rich
and poor that appears *even as aggregate indicators improve*. Farmer argued that whenever an
effective intervention (combination antiretroviral therapy, second-line [[tuberculosis|TB]]
drugs) exists but is rationed away from the poor, faster medical progress can paradoxically
produce worse relative outcomes for the destitute sick. He treated the gap as both a human
rights violation in itself and the metric against which [[pragmatic-solidarity|pragmatic
solidarity]] interventions should be measured.

## Semantic History

Coined within Farmer's critique of "commodified medicine" and of a bioethics preoccupied with
the "quandary ethics of the individual" (see [[critical-medical-anthropology]]). Usage has
remained close to Farmer's, primarily in global-health and social-medicine writing.

## Operationalizations

Farmer illustrated the gap with the HIV example: withholding a *marginally* effective drug from
the poor produces marginal harm, but withholding *highly* effective combination therapy widens
class-based mortality differences over time. Reduction of the outcome gap, in his program, is
the explicit goal — the thing pragmatic solidarity is meant to shrink.

## Applications

- HIV/AIDS treatment access ([[hiv-aids]]): Farmer contrasted 1997 rationing of protease
  inhibitors in New York (reserved for the "compliant") with Partners In Health's delivery of
  antiretrovirals to poor Haitians.
- MDR-tuberculosis: the gap between curable-in-Boston and untreated-in-Tomsk outcomes.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with [[structural-violence]], critics note the concept is a normative-analytic hybrid — it
names a moral wrong and a measurement in one term; recorded here in attributed voice as
Farmer's framework claim, not a wiki-voice finding.
