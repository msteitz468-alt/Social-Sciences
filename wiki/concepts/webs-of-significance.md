# Webs of Significance

## Definition as Coined

[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] (*[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973|Interpretation of Cultures]]*, Ch. 1): “Believing, with Max Weber, that man is an animal suspended in webs of significance he himself has spun, I take culture to be those webs, and the analysis of it to be therefore not an experimental science in search of law but an interpretive one in search of meaning. It is explication I am after, construing social expressions on their surface enigmatical.”

## Semantic History

Signature sentence of late-20th-c. meaning-centered culture theory; teaching staple and foil for practice/toolkit alternatives.

## Operationalizations

Culture as public semiotic webs → method [[thick-description]]; culture as **context** within which events are intelligibly described, “not a power… to which social events… can be causally attributed” (Geertz, same chapter).

## Applications

IoC program; cultural sociology definition wars; Sewell’s materialist-friendly rereading of symbols as extrinsic information.

Reflexive extension: [[rabinow-paul|Rabinow]] (*[[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977|Reflections
on Fieldwork in Morocco]]*, 1977) turned the metaphor onto the fieldwork encounter — "both
the anthropologist and his informants live in a culturally mediated world, caught up in
'webs of significance' they themselves have spun. This is the ground of anthropology; there
is no privileged position, no absolute perspective" — and closed by arguing that, through
[[reflexive-fieldwork|fieldwork]], the parties' "different webs of signification … were now
at least partially intertwined."

## Critiques and Limitations

Partial, contradictory, or shallow culture use under-theorized (Swidler); power and distribution of webs under-specified; causalists reject “not a power” framing.
