# Restitutive law

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] defined **restitutive (restitutory) law** as rules whose sanction consists "only of the return of things as they were, in the reestablishment of troubled relations to their normal state" rather than expiatory suffering — civil, commercial, procedural, administrative, and constitutional law (minus any penal rules within them). It is the external index of **[[organic-solidarity]]**, in contrast to **[[repressive-law]]** (the index of [[mechanical-solidarity]]).

Durkheim placed restitutive law in "very ex-centric regions" of the [[collective-conscience]] — further from its center than penal law — because society tolerates debate over modifying inheritance, servitudes, or contract, but not over tolerating murder; restitutive rules "either do not totally derive from the collective conscience, or are only feeble states of it" (attributed).

## Semantic History

The **negative vs. positive** distinction Durkheim draws within restitutive law is his own analytic (attributed):

- **Negative / real-right solidarity** — property, succession, real rights linking persons to things, and delict/quasi-delict duties *not to harm*. Durkheim argued this yields no true integration on its own — "the negative side of every species of solidarity"; a society under pure negative solidarity would resemble "an immense constellation where each star moves in its orbit." It is "only an emanation from some other solidarity whose nature is positive."
- **Positive / cooperative solidarity** — domestic, contract, commercial, procedural, administrative, and constitutional law. This is restitutive law's substantive core and what indexes organic solidarity proper.

## Operationalizations

Durkheim traced restitutive law's *growth* concretely:

- **Domestic/family law** becomes more complex, with positive (non-contractual, status-based) reciprocal rights and duties; family history is "an uninterrupted movement of dissociation" of once-undivided functions (against [[comte-auguste|Comte]], who denied the family involves cooperation to a definite goal).
- **Marriage** shifts from a private contract toward a publicly (ecclesiastically, then civilly) regulated institution.
- **Adoption** grows steadily rarer, more restricted, and juridically weaker (Iroquois → pre-Islamic Arabs → Slavs/Germans → Greece/Rome → modern French law) as clan-based organization recedes.
- **Contract law** grows in volume and complexity, supplying the **[[non-contractual-elements-of-contract|non-contractual conditions]]** that make individual contracts possible.

## Applications

- The positive-law counterpart to [[repressive-law]] in *Division of Labor* Book I's method: the rising restitutive share indexes the rise of [[organic-solidarity]].
- Empirical lever against [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]: administrative and restitutive law have *grown*, not shrunk, under industrialism — contradicting Spencer's prediction of shrinking positive social control (see [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- That restitutive-law growth measures rising solidarity (rather than state expansion, market complexity, or bureaucratization) is the standing objection.
- The negative/positive split and the "distance from the collective conscience" metric are interpretive, not measurable.
