# Emotion Work

## Definition as Coined

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] uses **emotion work** and **emotion management** as synonyms for the act of trying to change in degree or quality an emotion or feeling — "trying to fall in love or putting a damper on love, of trying to feel grateful, of trying not to feel depressed, of checking their anger, of letting themselves feel sad" (attributed; student materials). Same acts done for a wage are **[[emotional-labor|emotional labor]]** (exchange value vs use value).

Emotion work is done under **[[feeling-rules|feeling rules]]** (what one ought to feel) by techniques of **[[surface-and-deep-acting|surface and deep acting]]**.

## Semantic History

Grew from Hochschild's 1974 Berkeley student questionnaire (261 students; open prompts on deep emotion and changing situation vs changing feelings). The concept is elaborated in Ch 3 of *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]* and forms the private face of the "emotional system" (emotion work + feeling rules + interpersonal exchange of feeling) before commercial **transmutation**.

Unpaid "emotion work" later entered popular and academic discourse as the counterpart to emotional labor (2012 preface).

## Operationalizations

- Qualitative: awareness of "will" in talk about feeling ("I psyched myself up," "I squashed my anger down").
- Response types coded in notes: instrumentalists, accommodators, adapters, "emotion workers."
- Sex difference in spontaneous mention: 32% F / 18% M (notes; attributed FINDING, exploratory).

## Applications

- Private life: bereavement, romance, gratitude, gender display, family gift exchange of feeling (Ch 4–5).
- Bridge to commercial life: when standardized and sold, emotion work becomes [[emotional-labor|emotional labor]] and is more susceptible to [[alienation|estrangement]] (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Exploratory questionnaire, not a probability sample of U.S. adults.
- Boundary with ordinary self-control and with clinical "defense" is analytic, not clinical.
- Distinguishing emotion work from later "emotional labor" misuse in popular speech requires holding the wage/employer-control line.
