# Pecuniary Emulation

## Definition as Coined

**Pecuniary emulation** is, for [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]], the master-motive of the whole system in *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|The Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899, ch. 2): the drive to gain esteem by comparative wealth. "The motive that lies at the root of ownership is emulation... The possession of wealth confers honour; it is an invidious distinction." Veblen located the motive *not* in the consumption of goods but in the comparative standing that wealth confers.

Its central mechanism is a **moving target**. Once wealth becomes the conventional basis of esteem, "it becomes also a requisite to the complacency which we call self-respect"; but any new acquisition merely resets the standard, so "the tendency in any case is constantly to make the present pecuniary standard the point of departure for a fresh increase of wealth." Because "the struggle is substantially a race for reputability on the basis of an invidious comparison, no approach to a definitive attainment is possible." Accumulation therefore can never be satiated — a direct challenge to the classical-economics assumption that the struggle for wealth aims at subsistence or comfort.

## Semantic History

Veblen presents pecuniary emulation as a *derivative* of the deeper [[instinct-of-workmanship|instinct of workmanship]]: under conditions of habitual comparison between persons, the taste for effective work is "perverted" into "an emulative or invidious comparison of persons." This genealogy — emulation as corrupted workmanship — is distinctive to Veblen and often lost when the idea is absorbed into later work on relative income, reference groups (Merton, the "relative deprivation" literature), the "keeping up with the Joneses" of consumer economics, and Hirsch's positional goods. Duesenberry's relative-income hypothesis and the modern economics of status are recognizable descendants.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified by Veblen. Later social science operationalizes the family of ideas via reference-group comparison, relative-income effects on satisfaction, and the ratcheting "standard of living" (ch. 5), where Veblen argued each class emulates the class *immediately* above it — not those far above or below — and that the standard, being "of the nature of habit," rises readily but resists reduction.

## Applications

Pecuniary emulation is the source from which Veblen derives [[conspicuous-leisure]], [[conspicuous-consumption]], and the canon of [[conspicuous-waste]]; the [[invidious-distinction|invidious]] grading of persons, employments, and goods; and the ratchet of the pecuniary standard of living, which he linked to a low birthrate among classes under heavy decency-pressure ("probably the most effectual of the Malthusian prudential checks").

## Critiques and Limitations

- As a single master-motive it is close to unfalsifiable: almost any acquisitive or display behavior can be redescribed as emulation.
- Veblen's derivation of emulation from a prior "instinct of workmanship" is a psychological posit, not an established finding; he conceded his argument rests on "everyday facts as lie ready to hand."
- The account treats desire for comfort and security as real but secondary and itself shaped by emulation — a strong claim that later consumption theory qualifies.
