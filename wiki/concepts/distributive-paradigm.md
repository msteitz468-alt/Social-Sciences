# Distributive Paradigm (Justice)

Young’s critical name for the dominant modern approach to justice that she seeks to **displace** in *[[young-justice-politics-difference-1990|Justice and the Politics of Difference]]* (Ch. 1).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Young 1990, Ch. 1.)* The **distributive paradigm** treats justice as coextensive with **morally proper distribution** of benefits and burdens among individuals — material goods first, then rights/opportunity/self-respect/power treated as if they were things. Young argues two failures: (1) it obscures **institutional context** and social relations; (2) it **overextends** distribution metaphors to non-thing-like goods (rights as relationships; power as process). She reorients justice toward **decision-making**, **division of labor**, and **culture**, and toward humans as doers (self-development / self-determination) not only as possessors.

**Disambiguation:** Not Lenski’s [[distributive-process]] (stratification theory of who gets what) — related theme, different apparatus.

## Semantic History

Critical reconstruction of Rawlsian and related liberal justice theory as Young reads it; used thereafter as a foil in feminist and critical justice theory.

## Operationalizations

Ask whether a justice claim only redistributes things among individuals or also restructures power, labor, and cultural norms.

## Applications

Opens space for [[five-faces-of-oppression]] and [[politics-of-difference]]; critiques of affirmative-action debate when trapped in distribution-only frames (Ch. 7).

## Critiques and Limitations

Defenders of distributive justice argue distribution can include primary goods broadly; Young’s “displacement” may overstate the paradigm’s narrowness.
