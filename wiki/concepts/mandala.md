## Definition as Coined

In Indian usage a *maṇḍala* is literally a circle — originally a circular tantric ritual
diagram, and in classical Indian political theory (Kauṭilya's *Arthaśāstra*, attributed
to c. 325 BCE) a doctrine of concentric relations of enemies and allies among rulers,
each ruler's sphere visualised as a circle. As adapted for Southeast Asia by O. W.
Wolters (1982), following Ian Mabbett (1978), **mandala** names a characteristic
political form: a polity centred on a ritual overlord whose authority radiated outward
through shading circles of allegiance, with no fixed frontier. Wolters likened it to a
"concertina," expanding and contracting as lesser centres sought the protection of
greater ones and overlords extended or lost their spheres; Mabbett stressed peripheries
that faded rather than ended — in Higham's gloss, "there was no Hadrian" (Higham 1989,
citing Mabbett 1978). This is a **migrated** term: an emic Sanskrit word turned into an
etic analytic category.

## Semantic History

The analytic use arose as a deliberate replacement for the older concept of the
"Indianised state." Georges Cœdès and Paul Wheatley had framed the early Southeast Asian
polities as states created or decisively shaped by Indian civilisation. Wolters (1982),
Michael Vickery (1986), and others argued instead that the Western/Chinese notion of a
bounded, bureaucratic **state** imposed a false fixity: as Higham puts it (following
Dupont, Wolters, and Vickery), "no states in the Western sense existed in Southeast Asia
until the 19th century," and hegemony over other centres "was transient at best." The
mandala concept thus carries an explicit anti-diffusionist, anti-bureaucratic-state
argument in its very adoption. It converges with two independently coined notions:
Stanley Tambiah's **galactic polity** and Aidan Southall's **segmentary state** —
all three describe polities where ritual centrality, not administrative control of
territory, is the organising principle.

## Operationalizations

Higham (1989) treats a mandala as archaeologically and textually recognisable through:

| Marker | Evidence class | Example |
|---|---|---|
| A dominant ritual centre (temple-mausoleum, sacred precinct) | material / art-historical | Isanapura (Sambor Prei Kuk); [[angkor]] |
| Reservoirs (*baray* / *travan*) and water management | material | Angkorian barays; Zhenla *travan* tied to the *pon* title |
| Divine or semi-divine kingship (linga cult; *devarāja* / *kamraten jagat ta raja*) | textual / epigraphic | Angkorian royal cult |
| Vassalage, tribute missions, dynastic marriage, and periodic warfare | textual | Champa–Zhenla dynastic links |
| Ranked titles for local lords (*pon* → *mratan* → *raja*) | epigraphic | Vickery's (1986) Khmer-inscription analysis |

Because centrality "waxed and waned even within the span of a human lifetime," Higham
compares the mandala explicitly to Edmund Leach's (1954) oscillating model of the Kachin
of highland Burma.

## Applications

The concept organises Higham's account of [[southeast-asian-state-formation]]: the
early mandalas of the lower Mekong (Funan), the middle Mekong and Tonle Sap (Zhenla), the
Chao Phraya plains (the Mon-speaking Dvaravati centres), and the central Vietnamese coast
(the Cham-speaking Champa/Linyi polities), culminating in the [[angkor|Angkorian
mandala]] (802–1431 CE) as the form's most durable and monumental realisation. Its
durability, following Hermann Kulke (1984), rested on Jayavarman II's conversion of
vassal mandalas into administrative provinces (*viṣaya*/*praman*), so that rivals sought
to seize Angkor itself rather than found competing centres.

### Associated case — Geertz’s exemplary center (not identity)

[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]’s *[[geertz-negara-1980|Negara]]* (1980) reconstructs the classical Balinese [[negara]] as a [[theatre-state]] organized around an [[exemplary-center]] — court as microcosm and model, lesser centers as diminished replicas, multi-court competition under Klungkung’s titular primacy, frontiers as zones rather than fixed lines. This is **associated with** mandala-type SE Asian polity talk (radiating allegiance, ritual overlordship, weak Westphalian borders) but is **not** the same construct: Geertz’s model is Balinese-specific (*dadia*, [[sinking-status]], *subak*, *puputan*) and interpretive-political rather than Wolters/Mabbett’s mainland epigraphic synthesis. ## Critiques and Limitations

A load-bearing caution, central to this wiki's four-way non-identity rule: **a mandala is
a political-ritual structure, not an ethnic or linguistic unit.** Structurally similar
mandalas arose among Khmer speakers (Zhenla), Mon speakers (Dvaravati; see
[[austroasiatic-languages]]), Cham (Austronesian) speakers (Champa; see
[[austronesian-expansion]]), and Vietnamese speakers (Bac Bo) alike — the form does not
track language or people. Higham concedes the term's use is a methodological choice: he
notes only "some evidence" that the polities' members were themselves familiar with the
mandala concept, and the label remains contested (Vickery would abandon even the Chinese
exonym "Zhenla" for actual inscriptional names such as Isanapura). The distinction from
[[chiefdom]] is one of degree, not kind — in Higham's phrase, "the overlord of a mandala
had wider horizons and used force, the magnetism of the gods and of himself, to attract
followers and workers from a wider orbit" than a chief. The mandala's economic base is
itself disputed, most sharply in the [[angkor-hydraulic-city-debate]].

### Higham 2002 densification

Higham’s 2002 synthesis retains the mandala frame while hardening two supporting
arguments: (1) Iron Age chiefdoms ([[noen-u-loke]], [[dong-son-culture]], Mun Valley
moated sites) already display permanent ranking and maritime agency before Sanskritized
courts; (2) Chinese unitary labels (Funan, Chenla, Linyi/Champa, Dvaravati) often overstate
political unity relative to inscriptions and archaeology — so the mandala’s concertina
form is preferred over the bounded kingdom. On Angkor specifically he removes large-scale
baray irrigation from the economic base of the mandala (symbolic waterscapes; decentralized
rain-fed rice under grandee families — as of Higham 2002). Sources:
