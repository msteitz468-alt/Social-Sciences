# National Character

## Definition as Coined

In mid-century American anthropology, **national character** named the project of describing the modal personality or cultural configuration of a modern nation as if it were a single patterned whole — “personality writ large” scaled from *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* cases to nation-states. Benedict’s object statement for *[[benedict-chrysanthemum-and-sword-1946|The Chrysanthemum and the Sword]]* is paradigmatic: not religion, economy, politics, or family alone, but “what makes Japan a nation of Japanese” — assumptions about the conduct of life thrown large on the national screen.

Nations, she wrote, wear **spectacles** they take for granted; the social scientist’s job is to write the formula for those lenses. Ideal evidence is the man-in-the-street’s expected habits, not only elite strategy or statistics.

## Semantic History

Wartime boom (OWI, related programs); postwar academic decline under charges of essentialism and thin method; survives as a historiographic label for that research program and as a folk concept outside anthropology. *Chrysanthemum* is the emblematic case.

## Operationalizations

Configurational synthesis from institutions, child-rearing, films, literature, and informant interview — Benedict’s Japan book is literary-configurational, not projective-test based (though she notes War Relocation Camp Rorschachs by Leighton/Holter in passing). Related Kardiner/basic-personality variants used different tools.

## Applications

[[benedict-chrysanthemum-study]]; other wartime enemy/ally studies (expand when ingested). Ethics of difference: Benedict’s “world made safe for differences” (U.S. American, France French, Japan Japanese) without forced uniformity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Homogenization; political misuse; weak sampling; see [[national-character-debate]].
