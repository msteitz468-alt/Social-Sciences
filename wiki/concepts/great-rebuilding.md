## Definition as Coined

W. G. Hoskins proposed a "Great Rebuilding" of England between 1570 and 1640: a "revolution in the housing of a considerable part of the population," combining physical rebuilding and modernisation of medieval houses with increased household furnishings (Hoskins 1953: 44, quoted in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). Hoskins evidenced the thesis with the buildings themselves — architectural and visual evidence — as well as probate inventories, describing the study of buildings as "the classical example of a marriage between fieldwork and documents" (Hoskins 1967: 94).

## Semantic History

Maurice Beresford (1971: 171) noted that one effect of Hoskins's paper was to establish "veteran houses of the Old English Village of the travel posters" as post-medieval rather than medieval in the popular and scholarly imagination. Hoskins's paper also shifted buildings archaeology's focus toward vernacular domestic housing, away from industrial buildings or elite houses ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Matthew Johnson (1993b: 121) coined the related term "vernacular threshold" to describe the relationship between differential survival rates of vernacular buildings (E. Mercer 1975) and genuine early-modern architectural change. Johnson's later work (1993a, 1996; with Williamson 1998) acknowledged historical change and regional variation more fully, introducing what Johnson (1997) termed buildings archaeology's "loss of innocence" and a "genealogical" perspective (Johnson 1996: 206–212): "we cannot and do not interpret vernacular architecture independently of the present" (Johnson 1997: 16, quoted in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Operationalizations

Hoskins's original method combined visual/architectural survey of surviving buildings with probate-inventory analysis (see [[probate-inventory-analysis]]) to date and characterize the rebuilding wave. Later scholars questioned whether apparent building-survival patterns actually tracked historical rebuilding activity, or instead reflected differential attrition of the housing stock over subsequent centuries.

## Applications

The Great Rebuilding thesis shaped post-war study of rural and urban vernacular houses in England and contributed to wider use of probate inventories in domestic-house studies within [[historical-archaeology]] and [[landscape-archaeology]] ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Robert Machin (1977) argued the Great Rebuilding thesis was overly simplistic, noting that "archaeological work ... far outstripped historical investigation" of traditional houses (Machin 1977: 34). Alan Dyer (1981) and Colin Platt (1994) argued that changes in the late 15th–16th centuries were distinct from a second, separate rebuilding wave in the late 17th century, complicating Hoskins's single-period model.

Chris Currie (1988) offered a methodological attrition critique: small variations in house survival across different areas "can mean dramatic differences in final survival ... apparent waves of rebuilding may be illusory" (Currie 1988: 6, quoted by Johnson 1993b: 119, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]) — i.e., what looks like a historical rebuilding wave may instead be an artifact of differential survival of building stock over time.

A later, more totalising version of the thesis — Johnson's own "Georgian Order" model (Johnson 1996) — was itself criticized as monolithic, obscuring regional complexities and the "intimate and powerful nature of buildings and things" (per Deetz 1977; Leone 1988b); the framing was also criticized for failing to accommodate the situational diversity of capitalism (McGuire, in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]), and by the mid-1990s the Georgian Order model had itself become "another normative model of change."
