# Oral Testimony (Vansina)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[vansina-oral-tradition-1965|Oral Tradition]]* (1961/1965), [[vansina-jan|Jan Vansina]] made **the testimony** — not the tradition as a whole — the working unit of [[oral-tradition|oral-tradition source-criticism]]. He defined it (attributed): **"A verbal testimony is the sum of the statements made by any one informant concerning a single series of events, so long as all the statements relate to the same referent."** An informant is "a person or a group of persons who gives an account of a referent"; the referent is "that of which an account is given." Crucially, "a testimony... is the tradition as interpreted through the personality of the informant, and is coloured by his personality" — so two informants reporting the same underlying tradition give two distinct testimonies.

Vansina distinguished **three types of oral testimony** (attributed): the **eyewitness account** (direct, not reported), **oral tradition** (reported/hearsay, about the past), and **rumour** (reported, but about the present). Only the second is [[oral-tradition|oral tradition]] proper.

## Semantic History

The concept is a deliberate transposition of documentary source-criticism onto spoken sources: a testimony is a "verbal document." Vansina layered several classifications onto the unit, all of which structure his method:

- **Position in the chain of transmission** — the **initial (proto-)testimony** (the first observer's report), the **chain/hearsay testimonies** (each transmitter a link), and the **final testimony** (given to the recorder). Distortion enters at every link, and the three positions carry different biases: chain testimonies are the most socially conditioned; initial and final testimonies the most personally motivated.
- **Form** — **fixed texts** (memorized verbatim; the words belong to the tradition) versus **free texts** (only the outline transmitted). "Formal texts" (bound by phonological/syntactic rule) are always fixed, but fixed texts need not be formal.
- **Significance** — **official** traditions (imposed and ratified from above — e.g., Rwanda's *ubwiiru*) versus **private** traditions (unratified — e.g., much of Burundi's past, carried in proverbs and songs). Official traditions are better preserved but more likely functionally distorted; private traditions are transmitted more haphazardly but with less institutional incentive to falsify.

Vansina's manner-of-testifying distinctions (single informant, **group testimony** — "always a minimum testimony," reduced to what all agree on; **dispute** testimony; **interrogation** testimony — jointly produced with the questioner, "generally unreliable" when leading) further specify how a testimony is co-authored by its social setting.

## Operationalizations

The unit is what gets *evaluated*: assess the initial informant's opportunity to know; trace the continuity of the chain; then compare testimonies of the same referent by the [[comparative-method|comparative method]]. For free texts Vansina built a narrative toolkit — **episode** ("the equivalent of a single move" in chess), **motif**, **plot**, **setting**, **theme** — to compare structure across versions and reconstruct an archetype; functional elements (episode, plot) resist alteration, while secondary elements (theme, setting, names) falsify most easily. "The more perfect the structure of a text, the more likely it is that it has been altered for artistic reasons."

## Applications

The testimony-as-unit governs the whole of Vansina's method and the practice of [[ethnohistory]]: fieldwork collects **all variant testimonies** of a referent (not a random sample of informants), and publication presents one version with a full variant apparatus and *stemma*. See [[oral-tradition]] for the typology of tradition types the testimonies fall into.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because a testimony is "a mirage of the reality it describes," the unit's value is always relative to the specific events being tested — a falsified testimony has zero value for its ostensible content but real value as a sociological fact about its period. The distortion typology (social function, cultural values, informant personality) is the standing caution; later skeptics argued Vansina's unit underweights **feedback** and present-interest reshaping (see [[oral-tradition-reliability-debate]]).
