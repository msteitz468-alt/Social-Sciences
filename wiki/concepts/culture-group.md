# Culture Group (archaeological)

Mid-to-high rank in [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]]'s hierarchy (*[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]*): a family of related archaeological cultures above the single culture and below the [[technocomplex]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Clarke, Definitions, 1978 ed.)*

> "A family of transform cultures; a group of affinally related, collateral cultures characterized by assemblages sharing a polythetic range but differing states of the same specific multistate artefact-types."

**Transform cultures** (related definition): successive and collateral culture states from a single multistate cultural assemblage system trajectory.

## Semantic History

Culture-history already spoke of culture groups / *Kulturgruppen*; Clarke systematized the rank with polythetic multistate-type criteria and placed it explicitly between culture and technocomplex. Childe's hierarchical methodology is an acknowledged ancestor (Chapman Foreword: Clarke develops Childe's hierarchical classification with culture as central unit). Later critiques (flagged in Chapman apparatus) question equating such hierarchical entities with social groups.

## Operationalizations

- Shared **specific multistate artefact-types** with differing **states** across member cultures (tighter than technocomplex type-family sharing).
- Collateral/affinal relatedness via transform trajectories — phenetic affinity plus transformation arguments, not automatic genealogy (Clarke warned against reading affinity as pure descent).
- Phase, time, and system pattern regularities parallel to lower ranks (ch. 7).

## Applications

- Organizing European later prehistory culture families (e.g. Urnfield-related groupings in ch. 9 illustrations).
- Rank hygiene: compare culture groups only with culture groups, not with single cultures or technocomplexes.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Identity cautions:** culture group ≠ self-conscious people, language family, or genetic population. Clarke's ch. 9 ethnology offers **probabilistic** bridges (e.g. Urnfield radial expansions and later Celtic/Slav/Italic historical distributions) that must remain attributed hypotheses with independent linguistic/genetic controls.
- Social-variability critiques: hierarchical entities may mask behavioural diversity within and across "groups."
- Contested operational thresholds for when cultures cohere as a group vs a technocomplex.
