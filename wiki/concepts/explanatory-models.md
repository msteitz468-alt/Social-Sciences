# Explanatory Models (of illness)

The notions that patients, families, and practitioners hold about a specific illness episode
and its treatment — [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s best-known analytical and clinical
concept, central to *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (1988).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman defines explanatory models (EMs) in attributed voice: "the notions that patients,
families, and practitioners have about a specific illness episode." EMs respond to a set of
core questions — in *The Illness Narratives* Kleinman gives the list as:

1. the cause of the disorder;
2. why it had its onset when it did;
3. what the illness does to the body (pathophysiology);
4. the course it is following and will follow;
5. the source of improvements and exacerbations;
6. how the illness can be controlled;
7. its principal effects on life;
8. what is most feared about it;
9. the treatment wished for;
10. what is expected of the treatment;
11. the effects of treatment that are feared.

(This is the book's granular list; Kleinman's canonical shorthand elsewhere reduces it to five
domains — etiology, onset, pathophysiology, course, and treatment.) He stresses that these
questions "are not asked simply to obtain information. They are deeply felt."

## Semantic History

The concept originates in Kleinman, Eisenberg & Good's 1978 *Annals of Internal Medicine* paper and
receives its first full statement in *[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]*
(1980): "Explanatory models are the notions about an episode of sickness and its treatment that are
employed by all those engaged in the clinical process." There Kleinman also (a) distinguishes EMs from
**general beliefs** ("health ideology" that pre-exists any episode) — EMs are "marshalled in response to
particular illness episodes"; (b) distinguishes tacit **clinical EMs** (used in practice) from articulated
**theoretical EMs** (elicited by researchers), which often diverge; (c) frames EMs as "the main vehicle
for the clinical construction of reality" (see [[clinical-reality]]), anchored in the sectors of the
[[health-care-system]]; and (d) models practitioner–patient interaction as a *transaction* between the
patient's EM and the practitioner's, measuring their "cognitive distance." *[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking
Psychiatry]]* (1988) then ties EM discrepancy to healing efficacy. Over time "explanatory model" migrated
into medical education and cross-cultural clinical practice as a standard tool, sometimes flattened into a
checklist — a use Kleinman's own cautions resist. The concrete elicitation questions are on the
[[explanatory-models-interview]] method page.

## Operationalizations

Kleinman characterizes EMs as **practical and justificatory rather than theoretical, usually
tacit, self-contradictory, and unstable** — they "congeal and unravel," are "inchoate," and are
"deeply rooted in the emotional turmoil that accompanies illness," so he recommends re-eliciting
them at different points in treatment. Crucially, "the explanatory model is an interpretation of
what the practitioner thinks the patient thinks, not just a direct rendering of the patient's
actual words." Practitioners hold EMs too, shaped by "personal (countertransference) and
professional (disease) interests." The clinical procedure for eliciting, presenting, and
negotiating EMs is detailed on the [[explanatory-models-interview]] method page.

## Applications

- **Clashing EMs** structure Kleinman's cases: in the William Steele asthma case, patient, wife,
  primary physician, two psychiatrists, in-laws (religious causation), and children each hold a
  distinct EM, and "the clashes among these models also contribute to the… problems."
- **Folk vs. biomedical EMs**: Melissa Flowers's "pressure" and "high blood" (folk hypertension
  categories; Nations et al. 1985; Blumhagen 1980) versus her physician's "noncompliance."
- **Culturally hybrid EMs**: Eliza Hartman draws eclectically on biomedical, alternative-medical,
  stress, hot/cold, psychoanalytic, and chronic-viral models.

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman warns that because EMs are inchoate and emotionally charged, patients "may minimize or
even dissimulate" in answering, so elicitation must attend to "nonverbal, metaphoric channels."
The concept has been criticized (in the wider literature) for a residually cognitivist framing —
treating culture as belief in the head — which later embodiment and [[critical-medical-anthropology|political-economy]]
approaches sought to correct. Kleinman's own later work moved toward "local moral worlds" and
lived experience, partly in response.

Within the interpretive program itself, [[good-byron|Byron Good]]
(*[[good-medicine-rationality-1994|Medicine, Rationality, and Experience]]*, 1994, attributed)
pairs EMs with his [[semantic-illness-network|semantic network]] method — EMs elicit
episode-specific causal accounts, networks map the standing cultural field a complaint draws on
— and adds, via Charles Taylor's invoking/depicting distinction, that diagnosis "is more like
invoking than depicting": symbolization calls forth a therapeutic mode of experience rather
than merely describing a disease state.
