# Double Hermeneutic

## Definition as Coined

Giddens Glossary: the intersection of **two frames of meaning** as a logically necessary part of social science — the meaningful social world as constituted by lay actors and the metalanguages invented by social scientists; constant "slippage" from one to the other in the practice of the social sciences (*[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|Constitution]]*).

In *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]* (1990), Giddens restated the sociology–modernity relation in double-hermeneutic terms: sociological knowledge is parasitical upon lay agents’ concepts; metalanguage notions routinely reenter the universe of actions they describe; knowledge spirals in and out of social life, reconstructing both itself and that universe — **without** yielding transparent instrumental control. Sociological concepts are thus constitutively involved in what modernity *is*. This underwrites his claim that modern institutional reflexivity subverts certitude: more knowledge does not equal more control.

## Semantic History

Present from *New Rules of Sociological Method* (1976). Distinguishes social from natural science: concepts can become first-order when appropriated in social life (e.g. "capitalism," "unemployment," psychiatric categories). *Consequences* ties the device to the **reflexivity of modernity** as a source of dynamism (and of the juggernaut’s instability).

## Operationalizations

Methodological awareness rather than metric; track concept migration between academy and lay discourse.

## Applications

- Practical connotations of social science and critique (*Constitution* Ch. 6).
- Mutual knowledge vs common sense; internal vs external critique.
- Institutional reflexivity of modernity; spiral of knowledge and social change (*Consequences*).

## Critiques and Limitations

Some realists and positivists deny or minimize slippage; some relativists over-extend it to dissolve validity criteria (Giddens retains validity/credibility criteria).
