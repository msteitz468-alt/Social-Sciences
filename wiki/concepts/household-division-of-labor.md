# Household Division of Labor

The **household division of labor** is the allocation of unpaid domestic work and care between household members. Ridgeway (2011, Ch. 5) treats the home as “gender’s home base”: a high-salience site where the primary frame and family-devotion schemas continually reproduce unequal care loads that feed workplace inequality.

## Definition as Coined

In *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]*, the household division of labor is both **outcome** of gender status beliefs and family schemas and **input** that sustains positional inequality (time, career continuity, maternal-wall exposure). Sex remains a top predictor of who does housework and child care in the U.S. evidence Ridgeway reviews (Bianchi and colleagues; Coltrane — as cited), despite women’s labor-force gains.

## Semantic History

Overlaps [[second-shift]] (Hochschild’s metaphor for employed women’s unpaid load) but is broader: includes non-employed households, men’s shares, and task-type specialization. Related: [[leisure-gap]], [[stalled-revolution]], [[gender-strategy]], [[family-myth]].

## Operationalizations

Time-use diaries; relative shares of core housework vs intermittent tasks; child-care hours; “responsibility” vs hours (who manages the care system). Ridgeway emphasizes that **even when hours move**, cultural schemas of mother-as-essential-caregiver can keep responsibility gendered (Blair-Loy; Hays — as cited).

## Applications

- Link from home to [[maternal-wall]] and wage penalties.
- Ch. 7 change lever: men taking major routine caregiving to revalue care and erode diffuse male status advantage.
- Comparison cases (e.g., same-sex households, Carrington as cited) used by Ridgeway to show gender framing still organizes domestic work even without male–female pairs — attributed, not wiki-voice law.

## Critiques and Limitations

Class, race, and immigration shape paid domestic labor outsourcing; Ridgeway’s synthesis is primarily U.S. heterosexual dual-earner patterns. Exact equality thresholds remain politically and methodologically contested.

**Evolutionary-cooking densification (Wrangham 2009).** *[[wrangham-catching-fire-2009|Catching Fire]]* treats domestic cooking as the ethnographic near-universal core of household labor (women cook in 97.8% of Murdock & Provost societies as he reports; family vs community cooking distinction for rare male-cook cases). Wrangham links the pattern to pair-bonds around stealable camp meals and to a patriarchy thesis (men’s communal power consigning women to domestic roles) — see [[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]. This is evolutionary program argument from secondary ethnography, not Ridgeway-style status-belief sociology; keep the two discourses attributed and separate.
