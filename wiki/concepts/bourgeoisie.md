# Bourgeoisie

## Definition as Coined

In the [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848),
[[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] use **bourgeoisie** for the class of
owners of the [[mode-of-production|means of social production]] who employ
[[proletariat|wage-labour]]. The *Manifesto* narrates its rise from the "chartered burghers"
and burgesses of the medieval towns, through the manufacturing "middle class," to the
"industrial millionaires" of modern industry who "conquered for itself, in the modern
representative State, exclusive political sway" — so that "the executive of the modern State is
but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie."

## Semantic History

Originally French for town-dweller, the term is here converted into a precise analytic
category — a class defined by ownership, not residence. The *Manifesto* treats the bourgeoisie
with notable ambivalence: it "has played a most revolutionary part," dissolving all "feudal,
patriarchal, idyllic relations" into "naked self-interest," reducing worth to "exchange
value," and revolutionizing the instruments of production so ceaselessly that "all that is
solid melts into air." It is credited with creating world-market interdependence, cities,
and "more massive and more colossal productive forces than have all preceding generations
together" — and simultaneously condemned as the class whose property relations have become
"fetters" on those forces. The *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852)
disaggregates the French bourgeoisie into [[class-fraction|fractions]] (landed Legitimists vs
finance/industrial Orleanists) fused as [[party-of-order|Party of Order]] under the
parliamentary republic; Marx argues the mass of the bourgeoisie eventually “longed to be rid
of its own political rule,” enabling [[bonapartism]]. In later usage the term narrowed and
diffused (haute vs. petty bourgeoisie; colloquial "bourgeois" for conventional
respectability), and Weberian sociology
recast such groups in terms of market situation and status rather than pure ownership.

## Operationalizations

Engels's 1888 note (outside the ingested body) fixes the working definition: the capitalists,
owners of the means of production and employers of wage labour. The *Manifesto* itself marks
the class empirically by its command of capital, its political capture of the modern state,
and its distinction from the "lower middle class" (petty bourgeois, shopkeepers, artisans,
peasants) whom it drives down into the [[proletariat|proletariat]].

## Applications

Half of the two-class model at the heart of [[historical-materialism|historical materialism]]
and [[class-struggle|class struggle]]; central to analyses of capitalism, the state, and
globalization.

## Critiques and Limitations

The prediction that society would polarize into bourgeoisie and proletariat, with intermediate
strata disappearing, is among the *Manifesto*'s least borne-out claims: the "new middle class"
of salaried professionals and managers grew rather than vanished. The purely ownership-based
definition is challenged by the twentieth-century separation of ownership from control in the
large corporation. As with all of the wiki's Marxian material, the class's attributes are
recorded as the authors' theoretical positions.
