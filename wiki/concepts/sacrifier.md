# Sacrifier (*sacrifiant*)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice: Its Nature and Functions]]* (1898), [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] use **sacrifier** (French *sacrifiant*) for the subject **to whom the benefits of sacrifice accrue, or who undergoes its effect**. W. D. Halls's English translation (1964) coins "sacrifier" because *sacrifiant* has no exact English equivalent; [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s foreword flags the term as load-bearing.

The sacrifier is **not** necessarily the killer or the ritual technician. That role is the **sacrificer** (priest/officiant). The third pole is the **victim** — the intermediary object consecrated and destroyed. The sacrifier may be an individual or a collectivity (household, city, secret society); when a collectivity acts, the rite still modifies a moral person or the objects with which that person is concerned (Hubert & Mauss's Ch. I definition of [[sacrifice]]).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Hubert & Mauss 1898 | Technical French *sacrifiant* vs sacrificer vs victim |
| Halls 1964 English | "Sacrifier" coined for Anglophone sociology of religion |
| Evans-Pritchard *Nuer Religion* 1956 | Uses Hubert–Mauss scheme; Nuer "any man can offer sacrifice" blurs sacrificer/sacrifier in practice while retaining beneficiary logic |
| Later anthropology | Standard role vocabulary in sacrificial studies; sometimes collapsed back into "offerer" in less technical prose |

## Operationalizations

Identify (1) who pays for / commissions the rite, (2) whose state the rite is said to change, (3) who may not touch the victim or enter the sacred zone. Personal sacrifices affect the sacrifier's person directly; objective sacrifices act on external objects (house, field, harvest) for the sacrifier's sake.

## Applications

- Anchors the entire [[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study|Hubert–Mauss scheme]]: entry rites consecrate the *sacrifier*; immolation and communion redirect force to or from him; exit desacralizes him.
- Clarifies why priestly mediation matters: when the sacrifier cannot himself penetrate the sacred, sacrificers communicate on his behalf (e.g. Hebrew *hattat* portions reserved to priests).
- Prevents equating "who kills" with "who benefits" — critical for building sacrifices, royal rites, and proxy offerings.

## Critiques and Limitations

The triad is etic and ideal-typical; many ethnographies show overlapping roles (Nuer lay sacrificer-sacrifier). Collectivity-as-sacrifier can smuggle group personality without specifying decision and cost. The term's French origin means non-specialist English readers often misread "sacrifier" as "one who sacrifices" in the everyday sense (the sacrificer).
