# Maize Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Maize domestication** is the process by which humans and *Zea* populations coevolved from wild **teosinte** into domestic **maize** (*Zea mays*), a staple that later underwrote Mesoamerican civilizations. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] the key archaeological locus is [[guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]] (Oaxaca), excavated by [[flannery-kent-v|Kent Flannery]], with genetic markers pointing to intense prehistoric cultivation in the Balsas River valley.

## Semantic History

Early Tehuacán (MacNeish) chronologies placed maize deep in the Archaic; **direct AMS dating** of cobs and beans collapsed many ages (Coxcatlán maize cobs ≤3500 BC — as of Mithen 2003). Concurrently, botanical genetics cemented teosinte ancestry and Balsas similarity. Theoretical frames split between Flannery’s **risk-buffering** mobile foragers and Hayden’s **competitive feasting** — Mithen reports being “entirely persuaded” by Flannery for 8000 BC Oaxaca.

## Operationalizations

As of Mithen 2003:

- **Ancestor:** maize from teosinte; Balsas valley populations biochemically especially similar to modern maize.
- **Archaeological oldest cobs:** Guilá Naquitz maize cobs at **4200 BC** then oldest known; genetic studies of modern maize indicated domestication by **~7000 BC** — gap implies missing early record.
- **Companion crops:** common bean non-shattering pods (“wait for the harvester”); squash cultivation by **~8000 BC** at Guilá Naquitz (Bruce Smith 1995 restudy — domestic-size seeds/stems, thick rinds).
- **Settlement context:** small seasonal family camps (four–five people, autumn visits 8500–6000 BC) rather than Hayden-style permanent feasting villages; Gheo-Shih open site without structures.
- **Later spread:** Amazonian path eventually includes maize; Plains dry soils/frost limit maize agriculture (Mithen).

## Applications

- Independent New World plant package contrasting with SW Asian cereals — long post-ice delay relative to West Asia (as of Mithen).
- Sequence: **cultivation before permanent settlement** (Mexico) vs sedentism-before-farming elsewhere in Mithen’s global comparison.
- Foundations, “unwitting,” for later Mesoamerican civilizations (Mithen’s teleology — attributed).
- Method lesson: associate-charcoal dates can be thousands of years too old; date the cob.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Genetic vs archaeological date gap (~7000 vs 4200 BC in Mithen’s report) means early stages are under-sampled.
- Post-2003 aDNA, macrobotanical, and microfossil work has revised centers and ages — **date-stamp as of Mithen 2003**.
- Domestication of maize ≠ simultaneous village farming or state formation.
- Do not equate maize farmers with any single language family or genetic population.

## Bellwood 2005 — *First Farmers* Americas synthesis

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter|Bellwood]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* ch. 8–10; botanical and archaeological dates **as of Bellwood 2005**.)*

Bellwood folds maize into his comparative [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] and language-family mapping for the Americas, while recording botanical disputes without adjudicating them in wiki voice.

**Botanical positions he reports.** Most botanists (as he summarizes) agree maize domesticated from one or more annual teosintes, most important *Zea mays* var. *parviglumis* (Galinat), today in the Balsas basin (Michoacán/Guerrero), with a related variety in Jalisco; common bean *Phaseolus vulgaris* also possibly from that western Mexico zone. **Iltis:** teosinte may have been domesticated into maize not in the lowland biological homeland (Balsas) but in a highland region (e.g. Tehuacán) after human translocation of ancestral forms. **Eubanks / MacNeish & Eubanks 2000:** maize from cross-pollination of perennial teosinte and *Tripsacum dactyloides*, probably highland Mesoamerica — not annual Balsas teosinte alone. **Matsuoka et al. 2002:** genetic analysis favors highland origin. **Piperno & Flannery 2001; Pohl et al. 1996; Buckler et al. 1998:** phytolith evidence suggests teosinte absent in Mesoamerican highlands until appearance as primitive domesticate ~6,000 years ago — at face value supporting lowland origin. **Iltis 2000; Smalley & Blake 2003:** early teosinte/maize use may have been for sugary stalk (like sugar cane); primitive maize may have spread widely in the early Holocene as a sugar source for alcoholic beverages, domesticated as grain later and possibly in more than one location — snack-food movement ≠ systematic agriculture.

**Direct dates and spread (as of Bellwood 2005).** Oldest AMS-dated domesticated maize *cobs* (vs teosinte) from Guilá Naquitz ~4250 BC (Piperno & Flannery 2001); San Marcos Cave Coxcatlán Phase Tehuacán ~3600 BC (Long et al. 1989; Benz & Iltis 1990); carbon isotopes suggest increasing cereal reliance Tehuacán ~3500 BC; rapid cob morphological development 3500–3000 BC (Benz & Long 2000). Directly dated domesticated maize in NE Mexico and Tabasco Gulf lowlands by 2500 BC; *Zea* pollen + agricultural deforestation in Maya lowlands around the same time. Geographic span eventually 47°N to 43°S, up to ~4,000 m altitude; short-day plant requiring adaptation to longer days/shorter summers northward. Entry southwestern USA ~2000 BC, perhaps only centuries after large productive Mexican varieties (Matson 2003); eastern USA only around the time of Christ, dietary mainstay after AD 500. Flannery (1972 as cited): by ~2000 BC maize size/productivity (cob ~6 cm; yields ~200–250 kg/ha) could underpin intensive production and Formative population efflorescences in Mesoamerica, Andes, Southwest. Niacin availability problem remedied by lime-water cooking (Mesoamerica/Southwest) and wood-ash pounding (eastern USA) (Heiser 1990).

**South America caution.** Piperno & Pearsall school: maize phytoliths/pollen in Panama and Ecuador early Holocene, back to ≥6000 BC by association. Smith (1995:159) accepts no South American maize dates until clear cob presence after 2000 BC at Valdivia, Ecuador — rejects older phytolith-in-uncertain-context dates. Bellwood’s synthesis: maize not convincingly a significant South American staple before ~2200 BC (La Emerenciana isotopes/dental calculus/sherd residues; chicha possibility); **completely absent** in several large Late Preceramic agricultural sites (~2500–2000 BC) northern Peru — “ominous” for early-dates scenarios. Hoopes (1996) on Panama: ambiguous evidence; calls for chronometric hygiene. Andes: no maize in Late Preceramic northern/central Peru subsistence (irrigation of squash, beans, sweet potato, potato, etc.); maize appears with pottery/weaving in Initial Period after 2000 BC; Early Horizon (900–200 BC) rapid production increase; cob length almost doubled 500 BC–AD 1 (Sevilla 1994); stable isotopes suggest maize still not a food staple comparable to potato/quinoa — perhaps mainly chicha (Burger & van der Merwe 1990). Amazonia/Orinoco: maize in Middle Orinoco by ~800 BC, possibly preceded by manioc (Roosevelt 1980); generally non-intensive in Amazonia until ~AD 1000 (Roosevelt 1999b).

**Eastern Woodlands and Southwest (as of Bellwood 2005).** Earliest eastern maize remains ~200 BC Holding site Illinois; possibly introduced across the Caribbean (Riley et al.); New England ~AD 650; common bean (Mesoamerican) only ~AD 1000 — see [[eastern-agricultural-complex]] for pre-maize native seed crops. Southwest: cave cobs widely pre-1000 BC; irrigation canals at Las Capas San Pedro from ≥1200 BC; oldest Las Capas maize 1500 BC, Los Pozos 1700 BC. Matson (2003 as cited): core maize cultivation into Arizona via agriculturalists from northern Mexico; possibly very rapid (~500 years) from Balsas Guerrero to Arizona; higher-altitude eastern Colorado Plateau Basketmaker II may show indigenous Archaic adoption.

**Identity caution.** Multiple independent domestications of wide-range plants (including possibly maize) need not inflate counts of independent *agricultural genesis* regions (Bellwood). Maize ≠ language family ≠ genetic population; Hill’s Uto-Aztecan maize vocabulary hypothesis is recorded on language-family pages as a **position**, not as proof that maize farmers = Uto-Aztecan speakers.

## Coe & Koontz (2013)

**The "Corn Wars," resolved.** Botanist Paul C. Mangelsdorf's "Tripartite Hypothesis" held that
a wild relative (teosinte) crossed with an extinct wild maize ancestor to produce annual
teosinte, which evolved into maize, later crossing with *Zea tripsacum*. Geneticist George W.
Beadle countered that positing an extinct, unknown ancestor was unwarranted, and that maize
arose solely through human selection of teosinte — teosinte being the sole ancestor. Coe &
Koontz (2013) state plainly that "Beadle has posthumously won the 'Corn Wars,'" citing molecular
biology studies pointing directly to teosinte as sole progenitor, including work by a University
of Wisconsin geneticist tracing all maize origin to a specific teosinte strain in the **Balsas
River drainage, Guerrero**. This is recorded as a resolved position: both sides of the historical
dispute are preserved with attribution, alongside the resolution and its date-stamp (Coe & Koontz
2013).

**Earliest domestication dates (as of Coe & Koontz 2013).** Combined stratigraphy, radiocarbon
dating, and phytolith/starch-grain microfossil analysis at **Xihuatoxtla rockshelter**, Balsas
basin (Piperno et al., as reported by Coe & Koontz 2013), indicate maize was first transformed
from wild teosinte into a domesticate "early in the seventh millennium BC," subsequently
spreading from Guerrero across Mesoamerica and south into the American tropics. Large maize
phytoliths (probable domesticates) are reported at **San Andrés, Tabasco**, near La Venta, dated
to 4800 BC (Mary Pohl & Kevin Pope, as reported by Coe & Koontz 2013); no wild *Zea* is native
to coastal Tabasco, so if correctly identified this would be an early record of maize
cultivation, accompanied by evidence of large-scale forest clearance — flagged by the source
itself as provisional ("if this Tabasco material is true maize cultivation"). The earliest
**firmly dated maize cob** remains that from [[sites/guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]], Oaxaca, at
4300 BC (Coe & Koontz 2013) — 700 years earlier than the figure reported elsewhere on this page
from Mithen (2003), reflecting continued refinement of the Guilá Naquitz chronology; both dates
are retained here with attribution rather than silently reconciled.

**Tehuacán dating revision.** The [[sites/tehuacan-valley|Tehuacán Valley]] sequence (MacNeish),
long treated as an anchor for early maize, has been substantially revised: MacNeish's original
association-based radiocarbon dates placed Tehuacán maize cobs at c. 5000 BC; direct AMS dating
of the cobs themselves has since moved this to c. 3500 BC and later (MacNeish contested the
revision, attributing it to sample contamination). See [[sites/tehuacan-valley]] for the full
account — **[CONTRADICTION]** flagged there and here per the Contradiction and Replication
Protocol.

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 7; molecular claims **(as of Barker 2006 / Matsuoka et al. 2002)**.)*

- **Genetics (as of Barker 2006).** Barker reports modern genetic studies make clear maize developed from teosinte (*Zea mays* ssp. *parviglumis*); most maize-like teosinte in the Balsas valley (tropical lowland western Mexico); molecular evidence then indicated a **single domestication** probably on western Mexican lowlands; separation teosinte–maize around **6000 BC** (Matsuoka et al. 2002; Wang et al. 1999). Marked N/C/S American variety differences once taken to imply multiple domestications — molecular evidence (as he reports) does not support that (attributed).
- **AMS revisions of highland sequences.** Classic Tehuacán (MacNeish) and Guilá Naquitz chronologies revised downward: Tehuacán domesticated maize cobs no earlier than ~3500 BC; Guilá Naquitz maize cobs AMS 4500–4000 BC (Piperno & Flannery 2001); squash earlier (8000–6000 BC). Highland foragers manipulated plants from early Holocene including maize but continued broad-based hunting/gathering for millennia (attributed).
- **South-West and eastern North America.** Bat Cave maize once thought ~5000 BC → AMS no older than ~1000 BC; maize/squash/beans in SW from ~1000 BC with little impact on forager way of life for almost two millennia (Wills: tactical storage/risk reduction, not subsistence transformation). Eastern Woodlands: maize present from at least start of first millennium AD (Icehouse Bottom, Edwin Harness) but not a staple for another thousand years; full triad (maize, beans, squash) from early second millennium AD; 8-row Northern Flint-type enabled cool-latitude spread; health costs unequal by sex (Dickson Mounds; Georgia study via Barker) (attributed).
- **Tropical lowlands and South America.** “Dooryard horticulture” from early Holocene; Panama/Gatun/Belize/Honduras maize phytoliths/pollen with swidden over 5th–1st millennia BC. Ecuador Vegas maize phytoliths ~6000–5000 BC disputed by Staller & Thompson (~2000 BC; fermentation emphasis); Barker leans maize in region nearer 5000 BC, cultivated but probably not first as food staple — Smalley & Blake (2003) alcoholic-beverage case (attributed). Peruvian coast: maize small-scale/possibly high-status into Chavín while marine foods remained isotopic staples.
- **Synthesis positions.** Barker: multi-center early Holocene plant manipulation across the Americas, not single highland maize-first model; cultivation long before full sedentism/staple farming; maize as staple often related to availability of high-ranked wild foods and social valuation (status/beer), not intrinsic yield superiority (Barlow 2002 via Barker). ## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Oaxaca / Tehuacán dates

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, Chs. 5–6; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]]. Date-stamp as of 1996 synthesis.)*

**Teosinte ancestry (as they summarize).** Most botanists then held wild ancestor of maize is teosinte (*teocentli*); some focus on *parviglumis* of west-central Mexico; *tga1* locus controls hard fruit case vs exposed kernel; Beadle estimated ~5 major gene differences converting spike to multi-row ear.

**Dating package and AMS caution.** They report 1960s charcoal dates placing bottle gourds by ~7000 BC; squash seeds 7000–6000 BC Oaxaca / 5000–3000 BC Tehuacán; wild runner beans Oaxaca 8000–7000 BC; domestic beans Tehuacán 4000–2000 BC; earliest maize in Coxcatlán phase charcoal-dated 5000–3500 BC. **1989 AMS** on 12 oldest Tehuacán cobs yielded dates ~**1500 years younger** (San Marcos / Coxcatlán into the third millennium BC range). Authors urge **patience**: neither charcoal-association nor AMS set is proven absolutely correct; even if Coxcatlán phase were 3500–2500 BC, context of Late Archaic semi-nomadic cultivators would remain. Oldest known domestic maize **cobs** they cite from Coxcatlán and San Marcos caves: tiny primitive cobs 19–25 mm, 4–8 kernel rows; early yield maybe only **60–80 kg/ha**.

**Oaxaca cave signals.** Teosinte-like pollen in Archaic refuse at [[guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]], Gheo-Shih, and Cueva Blanca (no Archaic carbonized teosinte seeds for Oaxaca). Bottle gourd present by Guilá occupation; early squash/beans as artificial densification.

**Yield threshold and villages.** Citing Anne Kirkby: Zapotec farmers need expected **200–250 kg/ha** shelled maize to justify clearance; early Tehuacán maize (~60–80 kg/ha) less than mesquite pods (160–180 kg/ha); by **1700–1500 BC** cob size implies 200–250 kg/ha — “remarkably close” to permanent village commitment near best farmland in Oaxaca. Natural co-occurrence of teosinte, wild runner beans, and squash in secondary growth may explain later maize–beans–squash intercropping. Early squash domestication claims remain “tentative” given primitive morphology.
