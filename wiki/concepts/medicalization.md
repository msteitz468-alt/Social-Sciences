# Medicalization

## Definition as Coined

**Medicalization** is the process by which non-medical problems — behaviors, conditions, or forms of
suffering — come to be defined and managed as medical problems, typically as illnesses or disorders
requiring diagnosis and treatment. The term was introduced by the medical sociologist Irving Kenneth Zola
(1972), who described medicine as "an institution of social control," and was systematized by Peter Conrad
(with the counter-process of *de*medicalization). It is a foundational concept of the sociology of health
and illness and of [[critical-medical-anthropology]].

## Semantic History

Zola's original framing (medicine displacing religion and law as the dominant institution of social
control) was elaborated by Conrad and Schneider into a general model of how deviance and ordinary life
events (childbirth, aging, unhappiness, hyperactivity, addiction) are redefined "from badness to sickness."
Where early accounts stressed medical-professional imperialism, later work (Conrad's "medicalization of
society") emphasized more diffuse drivers — biotechnology, consumers, and pharmaceutical markets
("pharmaceuticalization"). In anthropology the concept was radicalized: critical medical anthropologists
treat medicalization not merely as category-expansion but as a mechanism of *depoliticization*.

## Operationalizations

Studied through the tracking of diagnostic categories over time, prescription patterns, and the
reframing of a complaint in clinical encounters. [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s
*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* supplies a paradigmatic ethnographic
case: on the [[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do Cruzeiro]], the collective experience of hunger
(**[[delirio-de-fome|delírio de fome]]**) is transformed, through the expansion of clinics and pharmacies,
into the individualized folk-medical syndrome **[[nervoso]]**, treated with tranquilizers and tonics —
so that "a hungry body exists as a potent critique of the society in which it exists. A sick body implicates
no one." She counts, for instance, tranquilizer prescriptions in clinic sessions to trace the process.

## Applications

- **Depoliticizing suffering.** In critical medical anthropology, medicalization converts socially- and
  politically-caused distress into individual pathology, defusing its critical charge (Scheper-Hughes's
  "bad faith" argument; the substitution of medicine for food).
- **Expansion of the medical domain.** ADHD, menopause, obesity, addiction, shyness/social anxiety, and
  grief have all been analyzed as sites of contested medicalization.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note the concept can imply a lost "natural," non-medical baseline that may not exist, and that
"medicalization" is sometimes used pejoratively to dismiss conditions whose sufferers experience real relief
from medical treatment (the risk of denying legitimate care). Conrad himself cautions that medicalization is
not uniformly good or bad but a definitional process whose consequences must be assessed case by case. In its
critical-anthropological use (as here) the concept is deployed polemically and its claims are recorded in
attributed voice.
