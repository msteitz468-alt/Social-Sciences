# Ethnographic Surrealism

## Definition as Coined

[[clifford-james]] coined "ethnographic surrealism" in a 1981 essay, reworked as
Chapter 4 of *[[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]]*
(1988), to name a sensibility he identifies in interwar Paris in which ethnographic
and surrealist practice converged. Clifford argues that both projects treated reality
as artificial and assembled — a set of locally specific arrangements rather than a
natural given — and that collage, the juxtaposition of incongruous cultural fragments,
functioned as the master figure of this shared stance. He is explicit that the term
names a "utopian construct," a possibility he reconstructs retrospectively, not a
movement that organized itself under that label at the time.

## Semantic History

Clifford locates the sensibility in a specific institutional cluster: the journal
*Documents* (1929–1930, edited by Georges Bataille, with contributors including
[[mauss-marcel]], [[griaule-marcel]], [[leiris-michel]], Paul Rivet, and Georges Henri
Rivière), the Collège de Sociologie (1937–1939, run by Bataille, Leiris, and Roger
Caillois), and the parallel institutionalization of French ethnography through the
Institut d'Ethnologie (founded 1925) and the Musée de l'Homme (opened 1938). He
distinguishes "ethnographic surrealism" as a general sensibility from "surrealist
ethnography" as an actual practice, for which he treats [[leiris-michel]]'s
*L'Afrique fantôme* as close to the sole pure instance. He offers Gregory Bateson's
*Naven* as a text resistant to classification on either side of a surrealist/scientific
divide, using it to argue the boundary was porous rather than fixed.

## Operationalizations

The concept is not measured or coded; it functions as an interpretive, historiographic
category applied to reread institutional history — journals, salons, and museums — as
sites where avant-garde aesthetics and empirical fieldwork overlapped rather than
opposed one another.

## Applications

Clifford applies the concept to reread figures usually treated as purely scientific
ethnographers, notably [[griaule-marcel]] and the *Documents* circle around
[[mauss-marcel]], as also participants in a surrealist-inflected questioning of
Western assumptions about culture and reality. It links directly to Clifford's broader
argument, developed through [[art-culture-system]] and [[salvage-ethnography]], that
early twentieth-century ethnography and modernist art shared collecting and
juxtaposing strategies for representing other cultures.

## Critiques and Limitations

Clifford himself flags the category as a constructed, expanded usage rather than a
description of a self-identified historical movement. Historians of anthropology have
subsequently questioned how tightly Surrealism and professional ethnography were
actually linked outside the narrow *Documents*/Leiris circle, noting that most interwar
French ethnographers — including many affiliated with the Musée de l'Homme — worked
within positivist and colonial-administrative frameworks largely untouched by
Surrealist aesthetics. The concept's evidentiary base is a small Parisian coterie,
raising an open question about how far it generalizes as an account of the
discipline's formation.
