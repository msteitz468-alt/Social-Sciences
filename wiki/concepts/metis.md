# Mētis

## Definition as Coined

In *[[scott-seeing-like-a-state-1998|Seeing Like a State]]* (1998), [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] adopted the Greek term mētis — following Marcel Detienne and Jean-Pierre Vernant's classical study — for "a wide array of practical skills and acquired intelligence in responding to a constantly changing natural and human environment," broader than the usual English gloss "cunning intelligence." He contrasted it with *techne*/*episteme*: universal, codifiable, logically deducible knowledge teachable as formal discipline. "Where mētis is contextual and particular, techne is universal"; mētis is personal ("touch") and concerned with practical results rather than explanation and verification. Exemplars: the harbor pilot whose local knowledge of one port outranks general navigation; the Squanto rule (plant corn when oak leaves reach the size of a squirrel's ear); Aristotle's navigation and medicine as arts that cannot, even in principle, be fully captured in universal rules.

## Core Claims (attributed)

- **Formal order is parasitic on mētis.** Scott argued that formal schemes are "always and to some considerable degree parasitic on informal processes, which the formal scheme does not recognize, without which it could not exist, and which it alone cannot create or maintain" — evidenced by the work-to-rule strike (literal rule-following halts production), the unofficial improvisers who kept East German factories meeting quotas, and the informal economies sustaining collective farms and ujamaa villages.
- **Mētis resists codification** because its environments are complex and nonrepeatable; rules of a craft are, in Oakeshott's formulation Scott adopts, "mere abridgements of the activity itself." Scott's analogy: mētis is to rules of thumb as speech is to grammar.
- **Mētis is dynamic, not "tradition."** Against the high-modernist caricature of static custom, Scott argued mētis is plastic, local, divergent, and innovative — West African adoption of New World maize; smallpox variolation anticipating Jenner; indigenous cultivators as continuous on-farm experimenters (following Robert Chambers, Paul Richards).
- **Its destruction is a project, not a byproduct.** Scott argued that the replacement of mētis by centrally legible standardized formulas is "virtually inscribed in the activities of both the state and large-scale bureaucratic capitalism," since dispersed local knowledge resists appropriation and control — Taylor's scientific management as the deliberate transfer of workers' knowledge to management; contract farming standardizing the farm to fit the contract's grid (see [[legibility]]).
- **Not democratically distributed.** Scott flagged two ironies: mētis can be monopolized (guilds, castes, gender divisions), and the marginal, stable communities that best elaborate it are being eroded by the very forces that also destroy it deliberately. He wrote of "mētises," plural.
- **Mētis-friendly institutions.** The book's conclusion advocates multifunctional, plastic, adaptable institutions — democracy, common law, and language as exemplars — and four planning rules of thumb: take small steps, favor reversibility, plan on surprises, plan on human inventiveness.

## Semantic History

An emic classical-Greek term (the cunning of Odysseus; the goddess Mētis) turned etic analytic category — first by Detienne and Vernant (*Les ruses de l'intelligence*, 1974) for Greek culture, then generalized by Scott (1998) into a knowledge-politics concept. Kin concepts Scott acknowledged: Michael Polanyi's tacit knowledge, Oakeshott's practical knowledge, Hayek's dispersed knowledge (a convergence with Kropotkin that Scott noted crosses left–right lines). Post-1998 the term circulates in development studies, STS, and organizational theory, often interchangeably with "local/indigenous knowledge" — flattening Scott's emphasis on adaptiveness over locality.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Scott's indicators are inferential: performance gaps between formal scheme and lived practice (work-to-rule; the "two maps" of planned vs. actual use of space), restudies showing dismissed practices were adaptive (ridging resistance in colonial Malawi; West African intercropping per Paul Richards; Andean potato "craft" cultivation per van der Ploeg), and the "dark twin" informal economies formal institutions generate.

## Applications

Runs through every case in *Seeing Like a State* as the counter-term to [[legibility]] and [[high-modernism]]: Jane Jacobs's street-level urban order, Luxemburg's and Kollontai's revolution-as-learning against Lenin's blueprint, cultivator knowledge against agronomic monocropping doctrine.

## Critiques and Limitations

Scott himself disclaimed romanticism — local practice is "often bound up with domination and exclusion," and mētis has real limits (no microscopy, no aerial survey). Critics of the book (positions widely voiced in development economics and planning literatures after 1998) argue the mētis/techne dichotomy is overdrawn, that Scott underplays cases where codified science outperformed local practice, and that his own evidence base is a curated set of failure cases — critiques of selection, not usually of the concept itself.
