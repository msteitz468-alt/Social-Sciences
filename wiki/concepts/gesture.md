# Gesture (Conversation of Gestures)

## Definition as Coined

The concept is adopted by [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] from **Wilhelm Wundt** and revised. Mead credits Wundt with isolating "a very valuable conception of the gesture as that which becomes later a symbol, but which is to be found in its earlier stages as a part of a social act... that part of the social act which serves as a stimulus to other forms involved in the same social act." A **gesture** is thus the beginning-phase of one organism's act that operates as a specific stimulus calling out an adjustive response in another — "the mechanism of gesture... makes possible the appropriate responses to one another's behavior of the different individual organisms involved in the social process" (attributed).

A **conversation of gestures** is the reciprocal interplay in which each organism's gesture calls out a response in the other, which becomes a further stimulus. Mead's standing example: two hostile dogs, whose "attitude of one calls out a different attitude in the other" — "a process out of which language might arise," though not yet language, because the animals do not respond to their own gestures as the other does (that step yields the [[significant-symbol|significant symbol]]).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Wundt (Völkerpsychologie) | Gesture as expressive movement, analyzed under psychophysical parallelism |
| Darwin, Expression of the Emotions (1872) | Gesture as vestigial act expressing a prior inner emotion |
| Mead, 1934 | Gesture as a phase of the social act functioning as stimulus; consciousness/emotion emergent from it, not prior to it |

Mead explicitly reverses Darwin: animals "certainly do not undertake to express" emotions "for the benefit of other animals"; the gesture is not the outward sign of an antecedent conscious state but a stage of conduct from which meaning and consciousness emerge.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. Mead distinguishes the **non-significant** gesture (the dog's, effective without the animal responding to its own gesture) from the **significant** gesture (which calls out in the maker the response it calls out in the other). "Attitudes" — "the beginnings of acts" — are the incipient, internally-initiated phase of gesture.

## Applications

- The developmental pivot of Mead's whole system: the [[significant-symbol]] is a gesture that has become self-arousing; [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]], mind, and the [[the-social-self|self]] all build on it.
- Grounds Mead's critique of **imitation** as the origin of language: "imitation... is mechanically impossible" as an explanation; apparent imitation is really self-stimulation via the vocal gesture.
- Grounds the critique of Wundt: Wundt's parallelism "presupposes selves as antecedent to the social process," whereas gesture is meant to explain how selves arise *within* it.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The vocal gesture's privileged status (over facial, manual, and other gesture) is a contestable emphasis in light of later gesture and sign-language research.
- As with all of Part I–II, definitional wording is editorially reconstructed (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]).
- The dog-fight and other animal illustrations carry Mead's 1920s comparative-biological framing.
