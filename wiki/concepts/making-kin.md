# Making Kin (Oddkin)

## Definition as Coined

"Making kin" is [[haraway-donna|Donna Haraway]]'s call, in *[[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]]* (2016), to build relations of obligation and care that are not grounded in ancestry, genealogy, or biological reproduction. Haraway proposes "making kin as oddkin rather than, or at least in addition to, godkin and genealogical and biogenetic family," arguing this "troubles important matters, like to whom one is actually responsible" *(Haraway 2016)*. Kin, in her usage, is decoupled from descent: "Kin making is making persons, not necessarily as individuals or as humans" — and explicitly extended across species lines, tying the concept to [[companion-species]] and [[sympoiesis]].

Haraway condenses the program into a deliberately provocative slogan: **"Make Kin Not Babies!"**, which she calls the Chthulucene's needed slogan and "perhaps the hardest and most urgent part."

## Semantic History

Haraway builds on feminist and anthropological kinship theory rather than inventing the idea whole. She cites Marilyn Strathern's point that "relatives" in British English originally meant *logical* relations, only narrowing to "family members" in the seventeenth century, and credits feminist Melanesianist ethnography ("our debts here are due especially to Melanesians, in alliance with Marilyn Strathern and her ethnographer kin") for the resources to think kinship apart from biogenetic descent. She also invokes Shakespeare's pun on "kin" and "kind." The move continues a broader late-20th-century denaturalizing of kinship in anthropology, redirected by Haraway toward multispecies and environmental ends.

## Applications

"Making kin" anchors Haraway's [[chthulucene|Chthulucene]] ethic: in place of techno-fixes or despair, small-scale practices of "becoming-with" other species and other people. Her closing speculative fabulation, the Camille Stories (Chapter 8), dramatizes it — fictional "Communities of Compost" in which every child requires at least three co-parents and is bonded from birth to an endangered animal "symbiont," and in which deliberately limited reproduction lets a fabulated human population fall over centuries. These are avowed fiction, not policy proposals or forecasts; they illustrate the concept rather than evidence it.

## Operationalizations

The concept is not operationalized in any measurement sense — it is a normative and figurative call, not a construct traditions quantify. Its nearest "operational" content is prescriptive: kin relations built through commitment, care, and "becoming-with" rather than through descent or reproduction; in the Camille Stories this is dramatized as a rule that each child have at least three co-parents and a chosen cross-species symbiont. The absence of a measurable criterion is deliberate and is itself a point critics raise (below).

## Critiques and Limitations

The "Make Kin Not Babies" argument carries an explicit demographic claim that is genuinely contested. Haraway argues for reducing human numbers as part of addressing the [[anthropocene|Anthropocene]]/[[capitalocene|Capitalocene]] — imagining a decline from a projected ~11 billion toward "2 or 3 billion or so" over "a couple hundred years," achieved through kin-making rather than coercive reproductive control. Reproductive-justice, feminist, Indigenous, and postcolonial critics have argued this echoes population-control and eugenic discourses historically aimed at the Global South, whatever Haraway's stated anti-racist and non-coercive intent. Haraway maintains the argument is decolonial and feminist and rejects coercion. This dispute is recorded in its strongest forms, without adjudication, on [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]].

A second, milder critique targets the concept's operational vagueness: as a figurative and normative call it offers no measure or criterion for what counts as "kin," which is a strength for its ethical work and a weakness for empirical application.
