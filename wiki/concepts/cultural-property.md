# Cultural Property

**Cultural property** is the legal-analytical category through which states and
international conventions designate archaeological objects, sites, and other cultural
heritage as protected — subject to ownership rules, export controls, and restitution
claims. It is the juridical face of [[heritage]]: where "heritage" names the past curated
as inheritance, "cultural property" names the past constituted as **ownable and
regulable**. The concept is intrinsically contested because it forces the question
*whose* property — the source nation's, the finder's, the current holder's, or
humanity's.

## Definition as Coined

The term is codified in international law: the **1954 Hague Convention** (protection in
armed conflict) and, centrally for the antiquities trade, the **1970 UNESCO Convention on
the Means of Prohibiting and Preventing the Illicit Import, Export and Transfer of
Ownership of Cultural Property**. As documented in [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Renfrew
2000]], the instruments define a framework in which objects illegally excavated or exported
in violation of a source state's law become recoverable — the **1995 Unidroit Convention**
(Art. 3.2) sharpens this by deeming an unlawfully excavated cultural object "**stolen**."

## Semantic History

- **1954 Hague:** cultural property as protected in war.
- **1970 UNESCO:** the property frame extended to peacetime illicit traffic; states assert
  export control and restitution rights. Non-retroactive and dependent on ratification —
  91 states by 2000; the UK conspicuously **not** among them at the time Renfrew wrote.
- **1995 Unidroit:** supplements UNESCO with private-law rules on stolen/illegally-exported
  objects and due-diligence standards for purchasers.
- **National regimes:** state-ownership vesting laws (Greece 1833 onward; source-country
  patrimony laws) vs. permissive regimes (US private-land digging; pre-1996 English
  Treasure Trove).
- **Merryman's antinomy** — "cultural **nationalism**" (property belongs to the nation of
  origin) vs. "cultural **internationalism**" (shared heritage of humankind, best served
  by circulation) — is the fault line under most restitution disputes. [[cuno-james|James
  Cuno]] (*[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns Antiquity?]]*, 2008) is the vault's
  primary advocate of the internationalist pole: he treats cultural property as a
  **political construct** ("whatever one sovereign authority claims it to be") and
  endorses Merryman's regulatory triad of **preservation, truth (knowledge), and access**
  over pure ownership claims (attributed — Cuno 2008).

## Operationalizations

| Instrument | What it does |
|---|---|
| 1970 UNESCO Convention | Framework for import/export control and restitution; non-retroactive; ratification-dependent |
| 1995 Unidroit Convention | Deems unlawfully excavated objects stolen; private-law recovery; due-diligence test |
| National patrimony laws | Vest ownership of undiscovered antiquities in the state |
| Bilateral MOUs (e.g. US–Mali, US–Peru) | Import restrictions on specified at-risk material |
| Treasure Act 1996 (England & Wales) | Statutory "Treasure" definition; reward system for reported finds |

## Applications

- Grounds [[restitution]] litigation (Lydian Hoard; Aidonia Treasure) and the presumption
  attaching to [[unprovenanced-antiquities]].
- Structures museum acquisition ethics (ICOM Code; Philadelphia Declaration; British Museum
  policy).
- Frames the politics of ownership on [[nationalism-and-archaeology]] and
  [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Nationalism vs. internationalism** is unresolved: property framing can entrench state
  claims over communities or over a genuinely shared human past. Cuno (attributed, 2008)
  argues further that "nationalist retentionist" laws **fail as site-protection tools**,
  promote sectarian identity politics, and (via Merryman) condone "over-retention" /
  "hoarding" under the 1970 UNESCO Convention — a **position** on
  [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]], not a wiki-voice finding.
- The instruments are **non-retroactive** and **unevenly ratified**, so the "property"
  they define is only as strong as the weakest jurisdiction in a laundering chain (the
  Politis Jordan case: material intercepted in the UK proceeded to the US because Britain
  had not ratified).
- Treating antiquities as *property* can eclipse the archaeological point that their value
  is **contextual knowledge**, not ownership (Renfrew's position — see
  [[illicit-antiquities-trade]]). Cuno's counter-position holds that objects retain
  aesthetic, technological, iconographic, and epigraphic meaning without findspot.
