# Fetishism

## Definition as Coined

"Fetishism" was coined by Charles de Brosses (*Du culte des dieux fétiches*, 1760, from Portuguese *feitiço*, "charm," used by traders on the West African coast) for the worship of material objects. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], in *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, Vol. II), redefined it within his [[animism]] system as **"the doctrine of spirits embodied in, or attached to, or conveying influence through, certain material objects"** — a *subordinate department* of animism, not (as in Comte and de Brosses) the earliest independent stage of religion. (Attributed to Tylor.) He treated even the great Nature-gods (Heaven, Earth, Sea, Fire) as fetishes writ large — "huge... but still fetishes themselves."

## Semantic History

The concept had three prior lives before Tylor: de Brosses's object-worship, Comte's **first theological stage** (fetishism → polytheism → monotheism, in the law of three stages — see [[law-of-three-stages]]), and Hegel's and later Marx's metaphorical borrowings (Marx's "commodity fetishism" — the misattribution of social power to objects — is a distant descendant). Tylor demoted fetishism from a *stage* to a *mechanism* within animism, and built the developmental arc **animism → fetishism/nature-worship → polytheism → monotheism** as one continuous elaboration: souls model spirits, spirits model nature-gods, nature-gods are organized into a pantheon on the model of human chiefship, and a "doctrine of divine supremacy" (a chief god over lesser gods) tends toward — but, Tylor insisted, never among "savage" peoples strictly reaches — monotheism ("no savage tribe of monotheists has ever been known," in his strict criterion). Twentieth-century anthropology largely abandoned "fetishism" as an analytic (it bundles disparate practices under a colonial-era pejorative) while retaining the underlying questions about materiality and religion; recent material-culture and "material religion" scholarship has partly rehabilitated the topic without the evolutionary framing.

## Operationalizations

For Tylor, a practice is fetishistic when **power or spirit is located in a particular material object** (stone, charm, image, tree, or natural feature) rather than diffused or abstract. He distinguished this from generalized elemental worship (a Fire-god vs. worship of an actual flame), and used the distinction to stage the transition from particular fetish to generalized deity.

## Applications

Fetishism, nature-worship, polytheism, and the doctrine of divine supremacy structure the ascent of Tylor's [[animism]] in Volume II. The framework fed the comparative anthropology of religion and (via Frazer) the study of magic and cult objects.

**Marx → Taussig:** Marx's metaphorical "commodity fetishism" retools the colonial-era word for capitalist culture critique. [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1980) makes that critique ethnographic: Cauca [[devil-contract]] and Bolivian [[tio]] as mediations of precapitalist vs commodity fetishism under proletarianisation ([[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]). He distinguishes nature/gift fetishisms of peasant production from the evil-coded fetish of capitalist mine and plantation production.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Fetishism" is now treated warily: as a term it was forged in the colonial encounter and carries a pejorative, evolutionist charge (the "fetish-worshipping savage"); as an analytic it lumps together charms, idols, relics, and sacred objects with very different logics. Tylor's own developmental sequence fetishism→polytheism→monotheism falls with unilinear [[social-evolutionism|evolutionism]] (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). Tylor's methodological caution against equating deities across cultures merely by resemblance (his critique of loose "comparative theology" that identified Woden with Buddha, or Thoth with Mercury) is, however, an early precedent for this wiki's four-way non-identity discipline — legitimate cross-cultural identification, he argued, extends only to attributes actually shared.
