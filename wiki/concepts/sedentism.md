# Sedentism

## Definition as Coined

**Sedentism** is the residential pattern of staying put — typically **permanent year-round residence** (often with architecture) — as opposed to high residential mobility. Archaeology treats it as analytically separable from [[domestication]] and food production: some foragers are relatively sedentary; some farmers remain mobile (attributed frame: [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 6).

W&O’s comparative sequence claim (as of 2007): in SW Asia sedentary communities may appear ~**1,000 years before** domesticated wheat/barley, whereas in Mesoamerica several plants were domesticating **millennia before** sedentary/agricultural villages.

## Semantic History

Rose with settlement archaeology and Near Eastern Neolithic research; Natufian and other Epipaleolithic cases forced recognition of sedentary or semi-sedentary foragers before full farming. Testart’s storing-hunter-gatherer argument recast the critical variable as intensive **storage economy**, not wild vs domestic staple per se — storing HGs share sedentism, high density, and socioeconomic inequality with agriculturalists (Testart via W&O). Hodder’s symbolic *domus* (domestication of the “wild” via the household) links sedentism to social control of nature enabling storage, processing, exchange, and feasts. Southwest pithouse-to-pueblo transition (Whalen; McGuire & Schiffer via W&O) ties aggregation and architectural investment to population, cultivation reliance, and ceremonialism.

## Operationalizations

Architecture durability, storage facilities, site size/depth, seasonality of fauna/flora, cemetery investment — no single criterion is decisive. Demographic/behavioral correlates discussed in textbook literature:

- Village/sedentary life correlates with higher fertility, more epidemic disease, family-based (vs group) economic units, material accumulation, and strong “inertia” once established (e.g., Danish shore villages shift from marine to agricultural diet after 4000 BC despite nearby marine richness — Tauber via W&O).
- Sedentism + high-carbohydrate cereal diets may raise fertility (reduced maternal mobility; Frisch & McArthur body-fat thresholds; nursing costs under high-mobility HG diets — as presented by W&O).

## Applications

- **SW Asia / Natufian:** large sedentary or semi-sedentary communities before full farming; Donald Henry rejects gradual pre-Natufian broad-spectrum path as sole explanation — specialized intensive cereals/nuts plus specialized gazelle hunting (via W&O). McCorriston & Hole: hyper-arid summers favored storage and sedentism among people already with cereal-processing tools. **Densified from [[bar-yosef-valla-natufian-foragers-2013]] (as of 2013):** Bar-Yosef & Valla preface flag seasonality and house-occupation duration as open (Valla 1998); brush/hide superstructures hypothesized. Belfer-Cohen & Goring-Morris reject simple permanent-base vs hunting-station dichotomy for the [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] cultural complex. Core-area architecture (el-Wad, Eynan, Wadi Hammeh 27) vs higher mobility in steppic periphery (Hasa, Negev–Sinai). Yeshurun et al.: primary refuse *inside* el-Wad Structure II implies limited daily cleaning — not “house-proud” Neolithic-style maintenance. Weissbrod et al.: [[commensalism-archaeology|commensal]] *Mus* frequencies often 10–30%, below many ethnoarchaeological sedentary benchmarks — taphonomy and occupation intensity remain contested.
- **Hayden resource-reliability model:** late Pleistocene shift to r-selected small animals/plants/fish encouraged sedentism and ground-stone tech; agriculture as extension of Mesolithic/Archaic reliability strategies in stress-prone areas — not richest ranked HG coasts.
- **Mesoamerica:** coastal shell-mound communities after 3000 BC may have pre-adapted sedentism before incorporating maize (data thin as of W&O 2007); Fritz on maize among less-mobile foragers.
- **Eastern North America:** O’Brien coevolutionary model — interaction with native annuals by ~200 B.C. making sedentism viable despite flood/drought risk; Poverty Point earthworks with rich hunting-foraging base (agriculture not prerequisite).
- **Southwest:** pithouse-to-pueblo transition associated with higher population, larger communities, greater cultivation reliance, more elaborate ceremonialism; Ancestral Pueblo aggregation and [[chaco-canyon|Chaco]] Great Houses as investment peaks.
- **Andes / other:** textbook notes cases where complex culture may have evolved without primary agriculture (Peru — leisure-time explanation rejected partly on this ground).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Binary sedentary/mobile obscures logistical mobility, seasonal aggregation, and multi-site annual rounds.
- Ethnographic analogies over-constrain prehistoric possibilities; Trigger (via W&O) warns every HG/tribal society may have been influenced by more advanced societies before ethnography.
- “Inertia” of villages can mask continuing mobility of segments of the population.
- Fertility/disease claims are model-dependent and often under-specified archaeologically.
- Conflating sedentism with farming or with [[complex-society]] collapses variables that reverse regionally.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Early Natufian experiment:** planned year-round villages (Mallaha, Hayonim, etc.) as new lifestyle; Bar-Yosef “point of no turning back” (via Mithen); Lieberman gazelle cementum as multi-season kill signal for permanent occupation (position).
- **Not food-stress forced:** Mithen rejects overpopulation/starvation as prime mover of Natufian sedentism (healthy skeletons; site-number jump earlier in Kebaran).
- **Valla–Mauss model:** extend seasonal aggregation periods until year-round residence under improving climate (attributed).
- **YD failure:** by ~10,800 BC sedentary village life “failed” after ~2,000 years; Late Natufians mobile with ancestral reburial gatherings at former villages (attributed).
- **PPNA restart:** permanent farming village life at [[jericho|Jericho]] ~9600 BC after Holocene rains; round sunken mud-brick houses; population estimates >500 then ~1,000 (Lubbock-scene counts — interpretive).
- **PPNB town form:** Beidha circular→rectangular two-storey; indoor room differentiation (craft/storage vs living); Flannery: circular compounds = group surplus pooling → rectangular multi-room = family ownership of surplus (attributed).
- **Psychological reading:** Mithen — anonymity/distrust in thick-walled towns exceeding face-to-face knowledge (Lubbock-framed interpretation).
- **European Mesolithic sedentism:** [[lepenski-vir|Lepenski Vir]] Iron Gates fishermen’s village ~6400 BC; late Mesolithic complex HG cases (Oleneostrovski, Skateholm, Ertebølle, Oronsay) with aggregation/cemeteries before Neolithic absorption (attributed).
- **Çatalhöyük:** rooftop cellular plan as extreme residential packing (Mithen ch. 11).

Sedentism ≠ farming; reverse sequences regionally. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]].)*

- **Natufian first villages** without subsistence production = essential but not universal Natufian trait; social/cemetery framework for later agriculture.
- **PPNA regrouping:** sudden large settlements (e.g. [[jericho|Jericho]] >2 ha) with collective monumental architecture; site numbers can fall while sites enlarge — progress in sedentarisation + concentration in ecological band, not pan-regional population boom.
- **Definitional fight with Watkins:** sedentary pre-agricultural villages beyond corridor (Qermez Dere, M’lefaat, Hallan Çemi) acknowledged; Cauvin still withholds “Neolithic” until productive economy.
- **Jerf el Ahmar:** terrace-bonded multi-house planning = cooperative sedentism before rectangular standardisation of middle PPNB.

## Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; synthesis of specialist archaeology, not primary excavation. Do not promote to wiki voice.)*

- **Sequence inversion (position):** Scott reports as a key reversal of received civilizational wisdom that **sedentism long preceded plant and animal [[domestication]]**, and that both sedentism and domestication were in place **at least four millennia** before anything like agricultural villages / agrarian economies based on them; ancestors “did not rush headlong into the Neolithic revolution or into the arms of the earliest states” (Preface; Ch.1).
- **Wetland abundance, not irrigation/state (position):** Sedentism and the first towns were typically products of **wetland abundance**, not effects of irrigation and states as earlier assumed. Southern Mesopotamia: sedentary populations and towns up to ~5,000 with little or no agriculture; reverse anomaly also occurs (crop planting with mobility except harvest) (Introduction; Ch.1).
- **Pournelle wetlands thesis (attributed via Scott):** Jennifer Pournelle’s reconstruction of seventh–sixth millennia BCE southern alluvium as a foragers’ wetland paradise (marine transgression, remote sensing, hydrology) underwrites Ubaid multi-web subsistence (fish, birds, turtles, wetland plants) on “turtlebacks”; first villages sat at multi-ecotone seams so habitats moved across stationary communities — more stable, resilient, low-labor than agricultural risk (Ch.1).
- **Domus superiority assumption (theoretical):** Farming-superiority narratives rest on a deeper, rarely questioned assumption that **sedentary life is superior to and more attractive than mobile subsistence**; the place of the *[[domus-complex|domus]]* / fixed residence is “so deep as to be invisible.” Scott argues there is no warrant for reading modern sedentary “givens” back as universal aspiration; massive evidence of resistance to permanent settlement (pastoralists, foragers) (Introduction).
- **Prestate Neolithic package:** Settled grain-growing populations and small towns (~1,000+ inhabitants) facilitating commerce were, Scott states, autonomous Neolithic achievements nearly two millennia before the first states (~3,300 BCE); concentration/incipient urbanism ≠ state making without large-scale fixed-field grain (Ch.4–5 synthesis claims).
- **Sedentism as reversible / not once-and-for-all:** Five millennia of sporadic pre-state sedentism; abandonments (Younger Dryas; PPNB ~6000 BCE); as a subspecies of sedentary grain communities, early states shared general perils of sedentary dissolution (Ch.6 via Scott).
- **Pathogen “feedlots” (attributed):** Sedentism alone, before widespread domesticated cropping, created crowding conditions ideal for pathogens — Mesopotamian large villages/small towns a **ten- to twentyfold** density increase over prior human experience; sedentism transformed *Homo sapiens* into far more of a “herd animal,” generating multi-mammalian zoonoses in the “late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camp” (Ch.2–3). Cross-links: [[agricultural-revolution]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]].
- **Osteology of grain sedentism:** Women in grain villages ~9 kya show bent-under toes and deformed knees from long grinding hours — repetitive-stress signatures paralleling later work-animal pathologies (Ch.2).
