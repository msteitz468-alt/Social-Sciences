# Defamiliarization

Critical strategy of disrupting common sense by making the familiar strange. In *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique]]* (1986), Marcus and Fischer treat **defamiliarization** as the basic strategy of anthropological cultural critique and distinguish two techniques: [[epistemological-critique]] and [[cross-cultural-juxtaposition]].

## Definition as Coined

Marcus and Fischer hold that effective criticism depends on form as well as content; defamiliarization aims to make the reader conscious of difference — disruption of common sense, doing the unexpected, placing familiar subjects in unfamiliar or shocking contexts. In anthropology (unlike single-effect artistic surrealism), defamiliarization should open a **sustained inquiry**, and in strong versions should reflect critically on the means of defamiliarization itself.

## Semantic History

Borrowed from artistic/literary critical discourse (surrealism; related formalist "making strange"); applied programmatically to ethnography's dual home/abroad structure in ACC Ch. 6.

## Operationalizations

- **Epistemological critique:** revise center concepts through peripheral fieldwork, then bring revised concepts home.
- **Cross-cultural juxtaposition:** match serious ethnography abroad with serious ethnography at home as mutual probe (Mead's Samoa/America as classic but incomplete model).

## Applications

ACC's method chapter for cultural critique; links repatriation to technique; frames evaluation of Mead-style comparison vs. dual ethnographic projects.

## Critiques and Limitations

Marcus and Fischer argue fully realized juxtaposition is rare; epistemological critique is often received as cute or satirical rather than consequential; weak versions use exotic cases as attention-grabbers without revising home categories.
