# Megalith

## Definition as Coined

"Megalithic" derives from Greek *megas* ("big") + *lithos* ("stone"): a monument built of
large stones. The term groups a heterogeneous range of prehistoric constructions — burial
tombs, temples, standing stones (menhirs), alignments, and stone circles. Megalithic **tombs**
cluster along the Atlantic and North Sea coasts (Iberia, France, Britain, Ireland, the
Netherlands, north Germany, Denmark, Sweden) and are largely absent from central, eastern, and
southeastern Europe and the east Mediterranean (attributed, Renfrew 1973).

## Semantic History

The category was long read through a **diffusionist** frame: a single "megalithic province"
or "[[ex-oriente-lux|megalithic religion]]" carried by "megalithic missionaries" from the
Near East or Iberia along the Atlantic seaways ([[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]; Glyn Daniel's
"Mother Goddess" reading). [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew's *Before Civilization*]]
(1973) broke the category apart: calibrated radiocarbon dates make the earliest Breton chamber
tombs older than 4000 BCE (calendar years) — predating Egypt's earliest stone pyramids
(~2700 BCE) and the Aegean tholos tombs by roughly 2,500 years — so the tombs cannot be
derived from a single eastern source. A crucial internal distinction Renfrew insists on: burial
megaliths (tombs) must be discussed **separately** from ceremonial megaliths such as the
[[maltese-temples|Maltese temples]], the Breton alignments, and stone circles including
[[stonehenge]] — they differ fundamentally in form and function and are not one phenomenon.

## Operationalizations

Two chief tomb forms (per Glyn Daniel, via Renfrew): the **passage grave** (a central chamber
under a round mound entered by a stone passage, sometimes corbel-vaulted — Greek *tholos*) and
the **gallery grave** (a single rectangular chamber entered at full width under a rectangular
mound; e.g. Essé in Brittany, 20 × 5 × 2 m). Construction technique tracked local stone:
orthostat-and-lintel where large workable slabs existed, corbelled drystone vaulting elsewhere.
Atkinson's experimental figures (via Renfrew): ~600–700 people to move a 35-ton stone, ~200
for the 50-ton Tinkinswood capstone — "not beyond the competence of neolithic communities."

## Applications

**Renfrew's positive account** (attributed, replacing diffusion): megaliths were the product
of at least four (possibly five) **independent regional developments** — Denmark, south
Britain, Brittany, Iberia (perhaps Ireland's court cairns) — each with its own sequence from
simple to complex. Using the Scottish islands of **Arran** (18 tombs) and **Rousay**, Orkney
(13 tombs), he argued the tombs are spaced regularly relative to arable land, each marking the
territory of a small farming community/lineage (estimated ~35–70 persons per territory). Such
small populations imply "a straightforward and fairly egalitarian tribal organization," **not**
a [[chiefdom]] — Renfrew warns explicitly against imagining a "megalithic civilization." The
monument was "an enduring symbol of the continuity of [a community's] occupation of the land."

He tied the Atlantic distribution to the frontier ("Atlantic façade") where the Neolithic
farming "wave of advance" met resource-rich Mesolithic coastal populations (Téviec, Hoëdic),
whose rapid adoption of farming produced population growth, land pressure, and a need to mark
territory — megaliths as territorial/solidarity markers under those conditions. Renfrew flags
this as "very general and … untested," and his ethnographic analogies (Kelabit megalith-
building in Borneo; Kyaka feasting cycles in New Guinea) as analogy, not evidence: "impressive
funerary monuments are often designed for the living rather than the dead."

## Critiques and Limitations

The category's original diffusionist unity is abandoned: surface resemblance across regions
that "share almost nothing beyond being neolithic" is a circular trap. The term survives as a
loose **classificatory** label spanning tombs, temples, and circles that are not a single
historical phenomenon — its use requires specifying which kind of monument is meant. See
[[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

### Sherratt — genesis and conversion (1990/1995)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*, Ch.13–14. )* Megalithic and related monuments as **surrogates for village/longhouse presence** where substantial settlements and substantial tombs are mutually exclusive. Two strategies: long unchambered monuments (loess/Neolithic stimulus) vs chambered/round forms (native reaction); independent regional expansions. **“Instruments of Conversion”:** outward rhetoric recruiting Mesolithic communities into farming social worlds; monument bursts as demographic/social intake; later Corded Ware/Bell Beaker horizons as replacement packages. House–tomb continuum dialogue with [[hodder-ian|Hodder]].

## British and Irish densification — Cunliffe *Britain Begins* (2013)

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* 2013. Shared monument form ≠ single ethnicity, language, or genetic population.)*

### Antiquarian frame (brief)

Cunliffe reports John Aubrey’s view that Avebury/Stonehenge-class stone circles, also found in Ireland and Scotland beyond Roman/Phoenician/Danish reach, were British Druid temples; William Stukeley’s 1719–24 plans of [[avebury]] and [[stonehenge]] later gave way to Druidic fantasy in *Stonehenge* (1740) and *Avebury* (1743) (attributed historiography).

### Western introduction and tomb typology

Cunliffe holds that **megalithic tomb building** was introduced around the Irish Sea, probably from **Armorica**, at the end of the fifth / start of the fourth millennium BC — closed chambers, simple passage tombs, and timber mortuary structures beginning a western tradition (evidence he calls tenuous; simplest explanation: Armorican settlers on existing Atlantic networks). Early simple megaliths inspired later portal tombs, passage graves, and court tombs that gave the Atlantic zone distinctive character (attributed). End-fifth-millennium Armorican decorated menhirs pulled down for passage-grave capstones are reported as possible social-tension signals on the continent (attributed).

**Irish/western types** (counts and dates as of Cunliffe 2013):

- **Portal tombs** (~400 known): simple rectangular chambers under a single capstone with tall portal stones. Human bone at Poulnabrone (Burren, Clare) 3800–3200 BC; Dyffryn Ardudwy (Wales) also cited (attributed).
- **Court tombs** (~400; most common megalithic type in northern Ireland): oval/semicircular courtyards defined by wings giving access to chambers under mounds; plan variation including facing pairs. **Clyde cairns** of the west Scottish coast (esp. Firth of Clyde) are strikingly similar; North Channel proximity plus Antrim porcellanite axes (Tievebulliagh/Rathlin) evidence active contact (attributed). Court tombs (N Ireland) and Clyde tombs (W Scotland) mark a shared northern zone, yet portal tombs and passage graves also occur broadly contemporaneously in fourth-millennium Ireland — Cunliffe’s simplest reading: local lineages preferred different disposal practices within multi-lineage communities (attributed).
- **Passage graves** (~230 in Ireland): type extends along Atlantic seaways from Cornwall through Wales to NW Scotland and the Northern Isles, and beyond to Armorican and Iberian Atlantic coasts. Small simple passage graves first appear in the Irish Sea region 4200–3800 BC (early examples at Carrowmore among Ireland’s four large cemeteries — Carrowmore, Carrowkeel, Loughcrew, Boyne valley). Size increases over time; Knowth, Dowth, and [[newgrange]] average ~85 m diameter and ~11 m high; Knowth and Newgrange radiocarbon dates indicate construction around **3000 BC** (attributed).
- **Severn–Cotswold group** (~175 monuments either side of the Severn estuary, Cotswolds into south Wales): megalithic chambers within usually trapezoidal long mounds (terminal, transepted, or false-portal/lateral arrangements); sparse dates point to first half of the fourth millennium BC. Cunliffe’s simplest explanation: British **hybrid** of Atlantic/Channel megalithic tomb traditions and the eastern long-barrow concept of the Wessex uplands; multi-phase mounds (Ty Isaf, Notgrove) show successive encapsulation (attributed).

Eastern Britain’s long barrows and timber mortuary structures (Fussell’s Lodge, Nutbane, Wayland’s Smithy) are largely an **eastern-side** tradition contemporary with early southern Scottish examples but not the western megalithic suite (attributed) — keep the Renfrew tomb-vs-ceremonial distinction above; British long barrows and Atlantic chambered tombs are not one phenomenon.

### Culmination ~3000 BC and Atlantic connectivity

Huge tombs of the Boyne valley, Anglesey (Barclodiad-y-Gawres, Bryn Celli Ddu — form and decoration shared with Boyne tombs ~130 km across the Irish Sea), and Orkney (notably Maes Howe: central chamber 4.6 m square, corbelled roof once ~5 m high) all built around 3000 BC culminate a thousand-year tradition (attributed). They share architectural styles, stone-carving values, and solstice passage alignments. Cunliffe holds they result from **local developments** yet imply communities stayed in contact along seaways — ancestors’ end-fifth-millennium networks still honoured a millennium later. Frequency, reciprocity, and numbers of travellers are unknowable; robust connectivity systems were in place (attributed). Motif similarities with Morbihan and artefacts with Iberian (esp. Lisbon-area) parallels — carved stone mace-head from Knowth, carved bone pins from Four Knocks, bone pendants from Carrowmore, Carrowkeel, and Loughcrew — suggest long-distance Atlantic journeys among passage-grave builders (attributed).

After pioneer Neolithization, continental contact decreases while British/Irish regional culture develops; the **exception** is ~3000 BC Atlantic passage-grave communities (Ireland, north Wales, Orkney) sharing knowledge with Armorica and Atlantic Iberia (attributed).

### Henges, stone circles, and third-millennium ritual landscapes

Cunliffe separates **henge** earthworks (ditch with bank outside; circular; one or more entrances; internal posts/stones/pits/burials; Cornwall to Orkney and Ireland) from **stone circles** (especially western Britain/Ireland; some with ditch, some without). Earliest known henges currently **Orkney** (end fourth millennium); southern examples (Flagstones near Dorchester, [[stonehenge]] phase 1, [[avebury]] phase 1) ~3000 BC; origins left open for lack of dates (attributed, as of 2013). Stone circles may often be a later enhancement of henges after ~thirty-first century inception (attributed possibility).

Preferred ritual landscapes recur: five chalkland central-southern clusters each dominated by a mega-henge — Mount Pleasant, Knowlton, Durrington Walls, Marden, Avebury — each one element in a complex (Durrington–Woodhenge–Stonehenge–Avenue–Cursus group functionally linked through third-millennium remodelling) (attributed). National outliers include Priddy Circles, Stanton Drew, Thornborough triple henges, Millfield, Orkney Stenness–Harray (Maes Howe in henge-style enclosure — early henge, possible type origin), and Boyne valley monuments with standing stones, long mounds, and henges; Knowth timber circle near east passage dated twenty-eighth–twenty-fifth centuries (attributed). Ring of Brodgar: stone circle 104 m diameter on a circular ditched enclosure, one of several monuments between lochs Harray and Stenness north of Maes Howe (attributed).

**Grooved Ware** (flat-bottomed, line/stab decoration; fashion ~1000 years from thirty-second/thirty-first century; pan-British/Irish) is invariably associated with henges; elite-ritual ceramic hypothesis is possible but not statistically validated because few non-ritual third-millennium sites are excavated (attributed caution). Some writers, noting earliest dates for both henges and Grooved Ware in Orkney, suggest northern origin and southward/east-coast and Atlantic spread when passage graves were most elaborate; Cunliffe allows this but requires more precise chronology — at minimum, shared belief system and intense third-millennium interaction (attributed). Paired stone-circle vs Grooved Ware maps suggest an east–west divide in Britain at this horizon (attributed caption claim).

Preseli bluestones moved to Stonehenge (possibly as an existing circle) exemplify the interactive society’s capacity (attributed). Cunliffe attributes early communal monuments to surplus energies harnessed by coercive powers — social mechanism, not demonstrated chiefdom polity (attributed; compare Renfrew’s egalitarian-tomb vs chiefdom readings above).

### Identity cautions (Cunliffe layer)

- Atlantic façade connectivity is **association** of practices and motifs, not a pan-megalithic people or language.
- Tomb-type regions (court/Clyde, portal, passage, Severn–Cotswold, eastern long barrow) are etic architectural distributions.
- Henge/Grooved Ware homogeneity ≠ single ethnicity.

## Bradley 1998 — houses of the dead and access (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* densifies the Atlantic/northern [[megalith]] problem beyond dating alone:

- Megalithic tombs are **not unitary**: ≥2 architectural styles/sources/distributions; local plan styles cut across deeper distinctions of long vs round mound and closed vs open chamber access.
- Fundamental properties Bradley isolates: **outward form** (one system based on houses of the ancestral past — [[long-mound]], [[death-of-the-house]]); **chamber form** encoding living–dead access ([[passage-grave]]).
- Cross-distribution tendency: early single/intact/closed → later multi/unfleshed/open; bone reordering; relic circulation to enclosures and settlements.
- Megalith building runs **parallel** to beginnings of agriculture as shared product of new world-thinking — not because first tombs required surplus or defined farm territories; clear farming impact and full ancestor rites often lag the first monuments (Brittany, Denmark cases).
- Complementary geography with loess longhouses (Fig. 12 after Hodder 1984 modification).

## Facing the Ocean densification (Cunliffe 2001) — indigenous-origin thesis and Atlantic debates

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]]* 2001, Chs. 4–5; an earlier, independent Cunliffe synthesis, complementary to Renfrew's 1973 dating revolution above and to the 2013 *Britain Begins* layer.)*

### Rejection of "megalithic missionaries"

Cunliffe (2001) explicitly rejects older diffusionist "megalithic colonization"/"megalithic missionaries" models (associated with [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s later work and pre-radiocarbon literature): calibrated radiocarbon dating, by extending the chronological span of Atlantic megalith-building over two millennia, removed any need to derive it from eastern Mediterranean influence. He characterizes the "megalithic culture" of the Atlantic coasts as "an entirely indigenous phenomenon... a manifestation of local Atlantic genius," and raises as an open, unresolved question whether the megalithic package had a single point of origin (candidates: Portugal or Brittany, both with fifth-millennium monuments) or evolved independently across a broad front among interconnected maritime communities.

Radiocarbon dating similarly overturned the earlier view that Portuguese collective tombs and the bastioned defensive walls of the Tagus/Sado Chalcolithic settlements (Vila Nova de São Pedro, Zambujal) were introduced by east Mediterranean immigrants: passage graves in the Upper Alentejo date to at least 4500 BCE, and Galician chambered tombs likely began by the same date, with passage graves there not appearing before c. 4000 BCE — an indigenous, west-to-more-elaborate development (Cunliffe 2001).

### Armorican long-mound vs. passage-grave origins debate

Two burial traditions developed in Armorica/Vendée in the early 5th millennium BCE: **long mounds** (*tertres tumulaires*, concentrated in the Carnac/Morbihan region; associated pottery dated 4700–4400 BCE) and **passage graves** (initially in western Brittany; Barnenez chamber G radiocarbon-dated 4800–4500 BCE, chamber A 4500–4000 BCE, chamber F 4500–4200 BCE; Île Carn and Bougnon in similar ranges). Position 1 (diffusionist): the long-mound tradition was adopted from the Bandkeramik/Seine region via acculturation or small-scale immigration. Position 2 (indigenist): long mounds developed locally from the Mesolithic burial tradition already visible at Téviec/Hoédic half a millennium earlier (see [[mesolithic]]). Cunliffe (2001) states the debate is unresolved and that the two positions "need not be entirely at odds" — recorded here as live positions, not adjudicated.

**Barnenez** itself began as a 35 m cairn with two drystone revetment walls and five burial chambers (radiocarbon dated as above), later extended westward to add six more chambers.

### Menhir iconoclasm, c. 3800 BCE

Le Grand Menhir Brisé at Locmariaquer — originally >20 m tall, 348 tonnes, carved with an "axe-plough" motif, part of a row of menhirs of decreasing size, dragged 4 km (estimated to require mobilizing >2,000 people) — was, along with many other decorated menhirs in coastal Morbihan, deliberately toppled and broken up around 3800 BCE for reuse as capstones in new passage graves (La Table des Marchand, Gavrinis, Er Grah, Mané-Rutual, Mané-er-Hroëk, Le Petit Mont). Cunliffe (2001) interprets this destruction/reuse as marking a major socio-religious "dislocation" — possibly a break in the legitimacy of the local elite — but explicitly flags this reading as speculative. Grand Tumulus monuments (only seven known, all within 20 km of Carnac; the largest, Le Tumulus-St-Michel, 125 × 60 × 10 m) that incorporate fragments of destroyed menhirs must postdate the iconoclasm.

### Carrowmore early-dates dispute

Radiocarbon dates claimed for some tombs at Carrowmore, Co. Sligo, place them in the early 5th millennium BCE — contemporary with the earliest Breton passage graves and 500+ years earlier than the accepted start of the Irish Neolithic. Cunliffe (2001) reports that many archaeologists find these early dates difficult to accept, leaving the question unresolved, though he considers Atlantic maritime transmission (Brittany–Ireland links) a plausible mechanism if the dates hold. This bears directly on the debate over whether the Irish passage-grave tradition began at the Boyne and became progressively "impoverished" westward, or began with small Sligo tombs and grew more elaborate eastward — Cunliffe favors the latter model (Carrowmore early and small; Boyne giants at the fourth millennium's end, averaging 85 m diameter vs. 10–20 m for early mounds).

### Nordic megaliths — diffusion vs. local origin

In southern Scandinavia/northern Germany, the Funnel-necked Beaker culture (TRB) built earthen long mounds (Vistula to Elbe, into Jutland) and, from c. 3700 BCE on Zealand (eventually 5,000–6,000 tombs in Denmark alone), stone-built chamber tombs roughly coincident with the earlier Ertebølle culture zone. Cunliffe (2001) weighs two positions: (1) typological similarity to British/French/Iberian megaliths, despite being later, may reflect seaborne contact with the Atlantic tradition; (2) the northern collective-burial rite (with exposure before bone deposition) could be explained by entirely local development, "no outside inspiration need have been required." He leans toward treating collective burial as an ancestral-territorial practice that is the shared linking factor, rather than asserting direct diffusion.

### Orkney and the Boyne–Orkney axis

Orkney was settled by farming communities in the early fourth millennium BCE, likely from mainland Scotland via skin boats across the Pentland Firth; by c. 3100 BCE permanent stone settlements existed (e.g. Skara Brae). Of 53 surviving chambered tombs, five are the complex "Quanterness type" (circular cairns, corbel-vaulted elongated chambers); Cunliffe (2001) cites an estimate that building Quanterness required ~10,000 person-hours, achievable by a community of no more than twenty — evidence, he argues, of small, roughly-equal-status lineage groups each with its own territorial ancestral tomb. At the start of the third millennium BCE a monumental ritual landscape developed between the Loch of Harray and Loch of Stenness: Maes Howe (30–40 m tumulus, chamber 4.6 m square, corbelled roof ~5 m high, radiocarbon dated just before 2800 BCE), the Stones of Stenness (31 m stone circle), the Ring of Brodgar (~104 m, largest of the group, ditch cut from solid rock requiring an estimated 100 workers for three months), and the Ring of Bookan. Cunliffe cites estimates that Maes Howe needed roughly ten times, Brodgar eight times, and Stenness at least four times the labour of a "normal" tomb — implying, in his reading, mobilization of the entire Orkney population for a ritual complex broadly contemporary (within the same generation or two) with the Boyne valley monuments 700 km away. Callanish (Isle of Lewis) — a ~12 m stone circle with four radiating stone rows — has no direct dating evidence but Cunliffe suggests it may be broadly contemporary with the Orkney circles.

**Grooved Ware/henge "ritual package" diffusion.** Cunliffe (2001) argues Grooved Ware pottery (ritual, not domestic, contexts) may have developed locally in Orkney c. 3200–3100 BCE and then spread rapidly (~1,000 km) to eastern/southern Britain together with the henge tradition, forming a widely-adopted "ritual package" — suggesting belief systems, not just objects, could spread this way. See [[stonehenge]] and [[newgrange]] for the Wessex and Boyne ends of this network.

**Clava Cairns — a cautionary case against migration inference.** Clava Cairns (Moray Firth) were once thought, from their simple passage-grave form, to indicate an immigrant population linking Boyne, Orkney, and Clava; radiocarbon now places Clava construction at 2200–2000 BCE — a full eight centuries after the Boyne/Orkney monuments — reclassifying them as a late regional development preserving memory of earlier forms, not evidence of population movement (Cunliffe 2001).

### Exchange networks underpinning the megalithic centres

Cunliffe (2001) uses polished-stone-axe distributions (traced by rock-source petrology) as primary evidence for Atlantic exchange systems, drawing an explicit ethnographic analogy to Kula-ring-style exchange (New Guinea/Solomon Islands; also invoked directly for the Channel Islands dolerite trade) while cautioning against arguing directly from ethnographic analogy. Key production centres: the Sélédin/Plussulien dolerite quarry (Brittany), estimated to have produced ~6 million axes over 1,200 years (~5,000/year, ~20 workers annually); Jersey's Pinacle dolerite source, mostly exported (31% of Guernsey axes, 21% of Sark axes, but only 16% of Jersey's own); and Tievebulliagh/Rathlin porcellanite (Co. Antrim), supplying over half of all axes found in Ireland and tracking the passage-grave tradition's distribution into Scotland, the Isle of Man, and Orkney/Shetland. Er Lannic (Morbihan, partly-submerged stone hemicycles) shows evidence of axes gathered for final polishing alongside hearths and pottery, suggesting ritualized feasting tied to the exchange network.

Cunliffe identifies three regional "centres of power and innovation" across the Atlantic zone by the early third millennium BCE — the Tagus region (Portugal), the Morbihan (Brittany), and the diffuse Boyne–Orkney axis — each showing elites mobilizing labour for monumental construction (fortified settlements in the south-west; celestially-aligned tombs in the north-west).

## Europe in the Neolithic densification (Whittle 1996) — stone houses, Brittany sequence, sacred realms

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic: The Creation of New Worlds]]* 1996, esp. Chs. 1 and 7. Parallel layer to Bradley 1998 and Cunliffe densifications above; mid-1990s chronology, pre-aDNA. Do not overwrite earlier densifications.)*

**Brittany opening vignette (~4500 BC as Whittle frames it).** Whittle imaginatively reconstructs early Neolithic Brittany: ancestors invited into dark, inaccessible **stone houses** with food and stone gifts; large upright megaliths as “trees of stone”; later freer-access stone shrines; re-use of earlier standing stones as chamber roofing; human descent traced through union with spirits; shrines fostering regeneration and expiating guilt of beginning to domesticate the natural world (Ch.1). He follows recent scholars in treating coastal Brittany’s first monument builders as **descendants of foragers** who had kept the land earlier — not a large or fully isolated population. The idea of the stone house, he argues, must have come from timber [[longhouse|longhouses]] of central and western Europe (from ~5500 BC), documented to the Paris basin and east to Hungary. Brittany constructions were unlikely to be pursued as acquisition of a new subsistence form; people only very gradually became “farmers,” a label he finds inadequate through the Neolithic (attributed).

**Sacred programme vs sociopolitical reading.** From around 4000 BC (or possibly earlier), monuments containing human remains were built across west, north-west, and even central Europe. Whittle’s Ch.7 programme renews attention to structures themselves: significance of monuments and contents lay primarily in **sacred/religious realms** — ideas of origins, time, and descent — against assumptions that monuments mainly express power differences or that collective-burial ideology masked differentiated reality. Synthesis: monuments only partly about burial; more about ancestral cult and mythical as well as actual ancestors; human dead deposited as offerings to unite living with beginnings; regeneration implied in bone circulation; this kind of belief “may lie at the heart of what we choose to call the Neolithic way of life” (attributed). Treating non-monumental human deposits (settlements, enclosures, bogs, rockshelters) as a sideshow to built “tombs” is, for him, a fundamental misconception.

**Chronology and Brittany sequence (Whittle position).** Claims for very early (earlier–mid fifth millennium) monument beginnings in Brittany/central-west France are uncertain; he prefers a horizon beginning **around or a little before 4000 BC**, with Cerny-horizon constructions at Passy (Yonne) among possible beginnings of monumentalisation. Earliest Brittany monuments may have been **long mounds enclosing individual cists**, not trapezoidal passage graves; Les Fouaillages (Guernsey) first phase radiocarbon suggests before 4000 BC. Preferred Brittany development: early long cairns covering cists (perhaps contemporary with standing menhirs) → very large Morbihan long mounds covering multiple cists **and** the [[passage-grave]] tradition; grand tumulus monuments (Er Grah, Le Moustoir, Saint-Michel, 90–125 m) ended closed without external access, with axe/bead depositions as offerings; passage graves structured around accessibility (Dissignac, Gavrinis, Barnenez, La Hougue Bie); allées couvertes and great stone rows come at the end of the Breton tradition, more inland with more pollen evidence of clearance. Pollen in Brittany and northern coastal Normandy indicates very little early woodland impact and possibly very little early cultivation (attributed, as of Whittle 1996).

**Menhir reuse (Morbihan).** A large menhir was subsequently broken up to serve as capstone in at least three passage graves; fitting pieces identified at Gavrinis and La Table des Marchand; at Locmariaquer, Grand Menhir Brisé, other standing stones, and elongated mound Er Grah preceded the passage grave of La Table des Marchand, with a dismantled stone row of 18 stone-capped pits between Grand Menhir and Table des Marchand — parallel factual ground to Cunliffe’s “menhir iconoclasm” densification above, without adopting Cunliffe’s speculative elite-dislocation reading (Whittle 1996 after Le Roux, L’Helgouac’h).

**Multiple origins caution.** Whittle warns against single origins for monumental forms: if the dominant early form was long, standing stones and some round mounds were also early; menhirs could rework sacred trees; circular mounds could monumentalise round huts more characteristic of forager occupations; Ertebølle elongated shell middens with inserted burials (e.g. Bjørnsholm long barrow near a midden) another possible source for the [[long-mound]] idea. Transformations cannot be reduced to forager imitation of cultivator-herder neighbours (“early Neolithic cargo cult”); evidence for convergence suggests rather an idea of the past, time past re-presented, descent, and beginnings, with LBK/Danubian tradition a powerful common element (attributed).

**Ireland / Boyne.** Pattern of constructions similar to Brittany culminating in Boyne valley passage tombs; great Boyne monuments could have been **shrines to mythical ancestors** — houses of ancestral figures honoured by offerings including human remains — rather than primarily collective tombs of known individuals; passages may have been as much for exits of souls/spirits as entrances of the living; spiritual/religious dimension revived without “megalithic missionaries” (attributed). See also [[passage-grave]] for the “abstract funerals” thesis.
