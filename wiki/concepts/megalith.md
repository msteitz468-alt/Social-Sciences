# Megalith

## Definition as Coined

"Megalithic" derives from Greek *megas* ("big") + *lithos* ("stone"): a monument built of
large stones. The term groups a heterogeneous range of prehistoric constructions — burial
tombs, temples, standing stones (menhirs), alignments, and stone circles. Megalithic **tombs**
cluster along the Atlantic and North Sea coasts (Iberia, France, Britain, Ireland, the
Netherlands, north Germany, Denmark, Sweden) and are largely absent from central, eastern, and
southeastern Europe and the east Mediterranean (attributed, Renfrew 1973).

## Semantic History

The category was long read through a **diffusionist** frame: a single "megalithic province"
or "[[ex-oriente-lux|megalithic religion]]" carried by "megalithic missionaries" from the
Near East or Iberia along the Atlantic seaways ([[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]; Glyn Daniel's
"Mother Goddess" reading). [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew's *Before Civilization*]]
(1973) broke the category apart: calibrated radiocarbon dates make the earliest Breton chamber
tombs older than 4000 BCE (calendar years) — predating Egypt's earliest stone pyramids
(~2700 BCE) and the Aegean tholos tombs by roughly 2,500 years — so the tombs cannot be
derived from a single eastern source. A crucial internal distinction Renfrew insists on: burial
megaliths (tombs) must be discussed **separately** from ceremonial megaliths such as the
[[maltese-temples|Maltese temples]], the Breton alignments, and stone circles including
[[stonehenge]] — they differ fundamentally in form and function and are not one phenomenon.

## Operationalizations

Two chief tomb forms (per Glyn Daniel, via Renfrew): the **passage grave** (a central chamber
under a round mound entered by a stone passage, sometimes corbel-vaulted — Greek *tholos*) and
the **gallery grave** (a single rectangular chamber entered at full width under a rectangular
mound; e.g. Essé in Brittany, 20 × 5 × 2 m). Construction technique tracked local stone:
orthostat-and-lintel where large workable slabs existed, corbelled drystone vaulting elsewhere.
Atkinson's experimental figures (via Renfrew): ~600–700 people to move a 35-ton stone, ~200
for the 50-ton Tinkinswood capstone — "not beyond the competence of neolithic communities."

## Applications

**Renfrew's positive account** (attributed, replacing diffusion): megaliths were the product
of at least four (possibly five) **independent regional developments** — Denmark, south
Britain, Brittany, Iberia (perhaps Ireland's court cairns) — each with its own sequence from
simple to complex. Using the Scottish islands of **Arran** (18 tombs) and **Rousay**, Orkney
(13 tombs), he argued the tombs are spaced regularly relative to arable land, each marking the
territory of a small farming community/lineage (estimated ~35–70 persons per territory). Such
small populations imply "a straightforward and fairly egalitarian tribal organization," **not**
a [[chiefdom]] — Renfrew warns explicitly against imagining a "megalithic civilization." The
monument was "an enduring symbol of the continuity of [a community's] occupation of the land."

He tied the Atlantic distribution to the frontier ("Atlantic façade") where the Neolithic
farming "wave of advance" met resource-rich Mesolithic coastal populations (Téviec, Hoëdic),
whose rapid adoption of farming produced population growth, land pressure, and a need to mark
territory — megaliths as territorial/solidarity markers under those conditions. Renfrew flags
this as "very general and … untested," and his ethnographic analogies (Kelabit megalith-
building in Borneo; Kyaka feasting cycles in New Guinea) as analogy, not evidence: "impressive
funerary monuments are often designed for the living rather than the dead."

## Critiques and Limitations

The category's original diffusionist unity is abandoned: surface resemblance across regions
that "share almost nothing beyond being neolithic" is a circular trap. The term survives as a
loose **classificatory** label spanning tombs, temples, and circles that are not a single
historical phenomenon — its use requires specifying which kind of monument is meant. See
[[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

### Sherratt — genesis and conversion (1990/1995)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*, Ch.13–14. )* Megalithic and related monuments as **surrogates for village/longhouse presence** where substantial settlements and substantial tombs are mutually exclusive. Two strategies: long unchambered monuments (loess/Neolithic stimulus) vs chambered/round forms (native reaction); independent regional expansions. **“Instruments of Conversion”:** outward rhetoric recruiting Mesolithic communities into farming social worlds; monument bursts as demographic/social intake; later Corded Ware/Bell Beaker horizons as replacement packages.
