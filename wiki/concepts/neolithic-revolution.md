# Neolithic Revolution

## Definition as Coined

[[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]'s label for the economic and scientific transformation from food-gathering ([[palaeolithic|Palaeolithic]]/Mesolithic "savagery") to food production ("[[neolithic|Neolithic]] barbarism").

**Primary coining text** — *[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]* (1936; rev. 1951), ch. V:

> "The first revolution that transformed human economy gave man control over his own food supply. Man began to plant, cultivate, and improve by selection edible grasses, roots, and trees. And he succeeded in taming and firmly attaching to his person certain species of animal in return for the fodder he was able to offer, the protection he could afford, and the forethought he could exercise."

Childe treats the change as gradual and cumulative, yet archaeologically visible as a single result; he equates this first revolution with the **"neolithic" revolution** and later calls it "not a catastrophe, but a process." He operationalizes progress via population: food production breaks the hard limit of wild resources, making children economically useful, and burial counts (as of his evidence) show enormous post-revolution increase in Europe and the Near East (attributed).

In *[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* (1942; rev. 1954) he restates: the escape from savagery "was an economic and scientific revolution that made the participants active partners with nature instead of parasites on nature" — deliberate sowing of wheat and barley so society "was henceforth actively producing food" (attributed, ch. III).

Childe calls it a **revolution** because it enables a sharp population increase (a "kink in the population graph" / upward kink parallel to the Industrial Revolution in *MMH* ch. I) and a new economic base — not because it was instantaneous.

## Semantic History

- **Childe 1930s–50s (*MMH* primary coining; *WHIH* extension):** package of cultivation, herding, villages, pottery, ground stone, textiles; Near Eastern primary theatre; not initiated among Magdalenians; wheat/barley foundation for "historic civilizations"; self-sufficing communities; "no such thing as a neolithic civilization"; diffusion produces many distinct neolithic cultures, not one; ages as economic stages, not universal absolute periods (Britain lag ~3.5 millennia after Egypt/Mesopotamia, as of Childe 1951).
- **Radiocarbon / multi-regional turn:** absolute dates push origins earlier and multi-centric; single-package "event" contested; pathways (foraging–farming continua, [[play-farming]]) multiply.
- **Wiki usage:** historiographical concept for Childe's food-producing threshold; do not assert a single worldwide simultaneous event in wiki voice.

## Operationalizations

**Childe (attributed, *WHIH* ch. III–IV):**
- Subsistence: cultivation ± stock-breeding (animals as "living larders"; milk, selective wool sheep).
- Material package: pottery as chemical transformation of clay; spinning/weaving/loom; built huts; polished stone axe as archaeological hall-mark (not universal).
- Economy: neolithic self-sufficiency as both achievement and limit — competition for land, war, and import dependence push toward the [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]].
- Geography: primary theatre "Hither Asia" / Fertile Crescent belt; European loess Danubians, Fayum/Merimde, pre-pottery Jericho/Jarmo as illustrations (absolute dates as of Childe 1954).
- Stage mapping: Morgan [[savagery-barbarism-civilization|barbarism]] ≈ archaeological Neolithic economically — but "neolithic does not correspond to a period of time" (e.g. Maoris still neolithic in A.D. 1800).

**Modern:** multi-proxy domestication, settlement, demography; regional sequences over global stage.

## Applications

Organizes deep-time narrative on [[neolithic]]; pairs with [[urban-revolution]] as the two revolutions that kink the population graph (*WHIH* ch. I stage summary); anchors critiques of unilinear progress on [[social-evolutionism]]; densifies [[ex-oriente-lux]] farming-priority claims without requiring full Orientalist chronology.

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-package, Near-East-centric, and progress-teleological in Childe's popular form; absolute chronology largely pre-radiocarbon (Childe notes early radiocarbon on Jericho as experimental); equates economic form with Morgan-stage labels; ethnographic analogies for "contemporary savagery" carry hierarchical colonial vocabulary (attribute only). Later archaeology retains the *problem* (how and why food production emerged) while rejecting the *event* model.

**Clark 1961 parallel (not Childe's coinage).** *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* frames mixed farming as the necessary economic base of Old World civilization and treats the Near Eastern piedmont–Palestine–Iran zone as the primary invention heartland, with Proto-Neolithic [[jericho|Jericho]] and Jarmo as type-cases; farming is called “revolutionary” yet gradual and archaeologically subtle at first; rich non-farming foragers (e.g. Northwest Coast) are said not to have exited the Stone Age unaided (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 4). Same Near-East-centric structure as Childe’s Revolution vocabulary, cast in Clark’s ecological–economic voice; keep as POSITION.

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

As of [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]], the Childean **Neolithic Revolution** survives as a teaching problem — *why* multi-center food production after ~10 ka — while the single-package single-center *event* is rejected. Farming did not appear in one place and spread alone; it developed in many places at about the same time in Old and New Worlds (attributed, ch. 1). They reject popular myths: single-genius invention, extraterrestrial teaching, or farming-as-easier — farmers often have less leisure than hunter-gatherers; multi-millennial transitions make single-invention scenarios improbable (attributed, ch. 6).

Hypothesis survey (attributed): (1) Childe **oasis** hypothesis (end-Pleistocene drying forced people/plants/animals together) — weakened when wild cereal habitats and early domestication timing failed to match oasis geography; later climate models (McCorriston & Hole; Moore & Hillman) revive climate without the full oasis package; (2) **natural habitat** / Braidwood — cumulative interaction in wild-wheat/barley zones (Jarmo as type); Harlan's wild-emmer harvest experiment (~year's grain for a family in weeks) suggests natural-habitat dwellers had least incentive to farm; (3) packing/demographic-ecological and Hayden reliability and feasting models; (4) Richerson, Boyd & Bettinger — Pleistocene climate "hostile to agriculture," Holocene stability/moisture/CO₂ favoring plant-focused economies; (5) Hodder symbolic *domus* domestication of the wild. Three recurrent factors across models: post-Pleistocene climate change; long-term population growth; accumulating technology/knowledge. Agriculture defined as systematic purposeful modification of plant/animal environments; largest complex civilizations rested mainly on six plant genera (wheat, barley, millet, rice, maize, potatoes — as of W&O pattern claim). See also phenomenon page [[agricultural-revolution]].

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003; historiographical and multi-pathway update to Childe’s event model.)*

- **Package unbundled:** Kenyon’s Pre-Pottery Neolithic at Jericho shows “Neolithic” settlement/burial **without pottery** — Childe’s indivisible package does not hold (attributed, ch. 7).
- **Jericho demoted as sole origin:** largest known PPNA with unique tower/wall, but “we can no longer identify that site with the origin of the Neolithic itself”; West Bank densification and northern Fertile Crescent (Göbekli, Jerf el Ahmar, Mureybet) reframe centres (attributed, ch. 8).
- **Causal inventory Mithen entertains:** YD stress and failed first sedentism; sickle-selection domestication pathway (Hillman & Davies); Cauvin ideology-before-economy (mother-goddess rejected; broader ideology thesis supported by monumental gatherings); Göbekli/Karacadağ accidental by-product of ceremonial feeding (theoretical); exchange networks spreading seed-grain with obsidian.
- **Rubicon by 5000 BC:** farming + permanent towns + specialists as history’s foundational transition — multi-regional in the book’s later parts, not SW Asia only (attributed, ch. 1).
- **Europe:** leap-frog demic/cultural spread through Balkans; Mesolithic florescence then collapse at [[lepenski-vir|Lepenski Vir]] as contact case (attributed).

Keep as historiographical concept; do not assert a single simultaneous worldwide event in wiki voice. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

### Braidwood — *Prehistoric Men* (3rd ed. 1959)

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]*.)* Braidwood uses the label **food-producing revolution** more than “Neolithic Revolution,” but explicitly credits Childe with “good reason” to speak of a revolution as important as the Industrial Revolution: men domesticate plants and animals, produce food, and settle in village-farming communities when production becomes effective. He accepts the *contrast* (food-collectors as mobile bands taking nature as found vs food-producers modifying nature, storing, living in permanent villages) while **rejecting overnight change**: the [[incipient-cultivation|incipient era]] was “probably an affair of some duration”; once village-farming is established, acceleration is “truly revolutionary suddenness” relative to half a million years of food-gathering. Geography: primary evidence in the **[[hilly-flanks|hilly-flanks]]** **[[nuclear-area|nuclear area]]**, not oases — see [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]]. Type village: [[jarmo]]. Absolute dates as of 1959 (Jarmo preferred cluster ~6750 ± 200 B.C.) are early radiocarbon positions, not current consensus.

### Cauvin — *Birth of the Gods* (2000)

*(Attributed — [[cauvin-jacques]], *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000]]*. )* Cauvin keeps Childe’s **Neolithic Revolution** label and the productive-economy criterion (cultivation/herding as defining “Neolithic” **state**), but relocates the Revolution’s essence: a **[[revolution-of-symbols|restructuring of mentality]]** and birth of divinities **before** the farming economy. Period ~12,000–6300 BC calibrated in Levant + Anatolia; ideological changes *accompany and sometimes precede* technical ones. Secondary neolithisation (e.g. Europe) useful for diffusion mechanisms, not for origin explanation. Contrast: Watkins (translator) emphasises sedentism as process; Cauvin restricts “Neolithic” to subsistence production (including later mobile Neolithic nomadism). See [[agricultural-revolution]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]].

### Sherratt — second revolution after the Neolithic (1997)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]*.)* Sherratt keeps Childe’s Neolithic Revolution as the first Old World farming threshold but argues a **second** transformation — the **[[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]]** (milk, wool, [[animal-traction|traction]]) — reorganized later Neolithic/Copper Age economies, especially from the fourth millennium BC. He treats the Neolithic beginning as the “original Consumer Revolution” and the SPR as “its second round,” and (Ch.20 / Section III intro) reads Europe’s SPR impact as the outer-Eurasian spin-off of Childe’s [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]] rather than a mere intensification of local European trends. Early farming in his model is meat-focused hoe horticulture whose *otherness* from later plough–pastoral Eurasia the SPR concept was invented to emphasise.
