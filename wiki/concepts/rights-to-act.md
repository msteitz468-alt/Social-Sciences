Rights to act form the foundational apparatus of James S. Coleman's *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990): the theory that rights over actions and resources are not objectively fixed possessions but socially constituted through consensus among those with a stake in the action. Coleman develops the concept in Chapter 3 ("Rights to Act"), and it underlies the book's subsequent treatment of authority, trust, exchange, and norms — every transaction Coleman models is, at bottom, a transfer, exchange, or contestation of a right.

## Definition as Coined

Coleman's core definition holds that a right exists only via intersubjective consensus among "relevant others" — those with both power and interest in the action in question — and that there is no "objective" rights-structure independent of actors' subjective, consensual recognition. He illustrates this with a four-cell smoker/nonsmoker table (Table 3.1) showing how disputed rights move toward consensus over time as exogenous information (such as evidence on secondhand-smoke harm) shifts the equilibrium. Drawing on Hohfeld (1923), Coleman distinguishes "liberty-rights" (rights to act) from "claim-rights" (rights to control another's action): when a liberty-right is transferred, the transferor loses it and the recipient gains a claim-right. Coleman further argues that rights are inherently bundled or partitioned, drawing on Honoré's (1961) analysis of property into distinct rights of use, consumption, and disposal, and citing extensive examples — mineral rights versus surface rights, air rights, ground rent and leasehold, common stock versus preferred stock and stock options, Coase's (1960) social-cost examples of brewery odors and disturbing machinery, and ethnographic examples of partitioned camel rights among Saharan nomadic groups and partitioned carcass rights among Eskimo whale and bear hunters.

## Semantic History

Coleman's consensual theory of rights is explicitly framed as a response to Thomas Hobbes's (1960 [1651]) social-contract problem. Where Hobbes treats the surrender of individually held rights (such as the right to avenge wrongs) in the state of nature as acutely vulnerable to a free-rider problem, Coleman argues Hobbes overstated the difficulty: because rights are inherently consensual and collectively controlled, an actor who tentatively relinquishes a right does not lose it outright until others follow suit, and can reclaim it without sanction if wronged before consensus fully shifts. Within Coleman's own text, the concept is illustrated historically by the transfer of smoking rights from smokers to nonsmokers in the United States during the late 1970s and 1980s as new information about passive-smoking harm changed beliefs, and by *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) alongside the 1966 "Equality of Educational Opportunity" report — the latter Coleman's own earlier empirical and policy work, cited here as a historical illustration within the theoretical text rather than as independent wiki-voice material.

## Operationalizations

Coleman operationalizes the theory through the smoker/nonsmoker consensus table, which models rights as a function of the distribution of power and interest among relevant others and tracks how exogenous shifts in belief move the consensus point. The bundling/partitioning apparatus is operationalized through Honoré's use/consumption/disposal decomposition, applied across legal, economic, and ethnographic cases. Coleman also formalizes how the free-rider problem in relinquishing individually held rights is reduced by the inherently consensual, collectively controlled character of rights themselves: giving up a right is not a set of individual actions each subject to free-riding, but an implicit collective decision. He extends this to conjoint authority relations, where the same consensual-rights logic produces a gap between public norms favoring participation (voting, union activity) and low private participation, since each subordinate's interests are as fully satisfied by others' action as by their own.

## Applications

Coleman applies the theory of rights to the puzzle of why law voids self-enslavement contracts while permitting ordinary wage labor: a valid voluntary transaction requires an ongoing actor whose interests the transaction serves, and selling oneself into slavery eliminates the very self in whose interest the "sale" would need to be rational — except when the alternative is death, as with ancient war captives, which Coleman reframes as "buying one's life" rather than "selling oneself into slavery," drawing on Patterson (1977) and Finley (1983) for definitions of slavery. He extends the rights apparatus to corporate actors, analogizing a corporation "selling itself" (losing independent existence) to the puzzle of voluntary enslavement, and cites the 1712 chartering of the South Sea Company by the British Parliament as an exemplar of a corporate actor's rights being wholly created and held "at the pleasure" of a chartering body — illustrating that even valuable rights, later reflected in stock value, are consensually contingent rather than intrinsically possessed. The rights framework also underwrites Coleman's account of social exchange more broadly: he argues exchange need not be limited to conventional economic transactions, since threats and coercion (such as a parent's threat of spanking) can be modeled as exchange within his framework, because what is given up and gained is always a right to act.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman's theory is a deliberately etic, formal reconstruction of rights as social facts rather than a jurisprudential or moral theory of what rights ought to exist; he states directly that "the existing distribution of rights is right" only in the sense that a system of exchange has no vantage point outside itself from which to judge the distribution of rights that constituted it — a normative implication he draws explicitly rather than one implicit in the theory. This entails that Coleman's framework, on its own terms, cannot adjudicate whether a given consensual rights-distribution (his example: a patriarchal household's weighting of the husband's interests in its "maximum aggregate satisfaction") is just, only that it is the constitutional starting point for the system's exchanges. The theory's central innovation — reducing the free-rider problem in rights-relinquishment via the consensual, non-atomistic character of rights — is Coleman's own theoretical claim, positioned against Hobbes, and should not be read as a settled resolution of the social-contract problem in political philosophy generally.
