# Abstracted Empiricism

## Definition as Coined

**"Abstracted empiricism"** is [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]]'s polemical term, from *[[mills-sociological-imagination-1959|The Sociological Imagination]]* (1959), for a style of social research built around a technique rather than a problem — the large-scale statistical study of standardized interviews with sampled individuals, coded and run as correlations, exemplified for Mills by the survey-research tradition of Paul Lazarsfeld and Columbia's Bureau of Applied Social Research (with George Lundberg, Samuel Stouffer, and Stuart Dodd named as fellow-travelers). Its defining vice, Mills argued (attributed), is **"the methodological inhibition"**: "Methodology... seems to determine the problems" — practitioners adopt one philosophy of natural science as "The Scientific Method" and let it delimit which questions can even be posed. It is the exact twin of [[grand-theory]]'s "fetishism of the Concept": where grand theory is "an idea... too large for the content," abstracted empiricism is "the content [swallowing] the idea."

Mills's summary charge was a mismatch of thinness to cost: "thinness of results" matched only by "elaboration of methods." Of the 1940 Erie County voting study (*The People's Choice*, Lazarsfeld et al.) he wrote that "we learn that rich, rural, and Protestant persons tend to vote Republican... But we learn little about the dynamics of American politics," because the design omitted party machinery and political institutions entirely; of Stouffer's *The American Soldier* he judged that it proved "it is possible for social research to be of administrative use without being concerned with the problems of social science."

## Semantic History

The label is Mills's coinage and remains tied to his critique; it is used today chiefly as shorthand for method-led, theory-thin quantitative sociology, and as one pole of the recurring [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate|theory-versus-method]] and qualitative–quantitative disputes. Mills's own "abstracted" was double-edged: the style *abstracts* the individual respondent from social structure and history (confining studies to "the one national social structure of the United States" over "the last decade or so"), and it is *abstract* in the pejorative sense of empty. Todd Gitlin's 2000 afterword argues the term still bites but that its 1959 referent (Cold-War-funded government and corporate survey work) lost prestige as that funding contracted, and that a contemporary Mills would aim the *grand theory* charge at Foucault-era Theory rather than Parsons.

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct. Mills identified abstracted empiricism by symptoms: the study organized around a data-collection technique; problems chosen for their tractability to that technique rather than their importance; "spongy 'indices' of 'socio-economic status'" substituting for structural concepts like class or "class consciousness"; and the confusion (he cited Bernard Berelson's definition of public-opinion research) of the object of study with the methods proposed to study it.

## Applications

Mills's deeper objection (attributed) was that the style structurally tends toward **psychologism** — "the attempt to explain social phenomena in terms of facts and theories about the make-up of individuals," resting on "a metaphysical denial of the reality of social structure" — because its basic datum, the individual interview aggregated across a sample, cannot capture structural causation, which is often unknown to the interviewees themselves. He rebutted two standard defenses: that commercial/funding constraints (not the method) cause the thinness, and that many small studies will eventually "add up" (Lazarsfeld's "building-block" image) into an integrated science. Both fail, Mills held, because the thinness is inherent to the style's "metaphysical choice." He tied the critique to his account of the **bureaucratic ethos**: because abstracted-empirical research is expensive, it requires large research bureaus and well-funded clients (corporations, the military, the state, advertising), so practitioners "abdicate the choice of their specific problems to others" — the method's affinity for administrative clients is, for Mills, part of its epistemology, not an accident of funding. In a footnote he argued the style is incompatible with the democratic public role of social science, since it teaches publics to distrust their own capacity to reason and to locate valid social knowledge only in "a necessarily bureaucratic kind of research."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is one side of a polemic. Mills quotes Lazarsfeld, Berelson, and Stouffer to convict them but does not state the survey tradition's own rationale in its strongest form: that probability sampling and measurement deliver comparability, replicability, and cumulative trend data no ethnographic or "classic" study can match, and that Mills's "classic" exemplars (Neumann, Weber) are essays of interpretation whose claims are harder to check, not easier. Defenders note Lazarsfeld's own methodological sophistication (Mills conceded he was "among the more sophisticated spokesmen"). The dispute — whether social science advances through general theory, through disciplined measurement, or through Mills's problem-driven "classic" craft — is filed on [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]]. Mills's own verdict — "Social research of any kind is advanced by ideas; it is only disciplined by fact" — is his position, not a settled finding.
