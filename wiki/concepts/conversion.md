# Conversion (religious)

> Analyzed critically in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991) by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]].

## Definition as Coined

"Conversion" names a change of religious allegiance or belief. Its dominant social-science model derives, the Comaroffs argue, from the **Pauline** template absorbed into "the bourgeois ideal of spiritual individualism," and is deployed chiefly in the **Weberian** tradition (rationalization under crisis).

## Semantic History

A migrated term: an emic Christian category turned etic analytic tool. The Comaroffs mount a pointed critique of that migration — the wiki's clearest example of theory smuggled into description. They identify two dangers:

1. Conversion "conflate[s] changes in individual spiritual identity with cultural transformation," muddying the relation between subjective experience and collective existence.
2. Used as a noun, it **reifies "belief,"** abstracting it from the total symbolic order and importing "a Protestant, market-like model of personal conviction as a form of resource allocation."

Their conclusion, in attributed voice: to treat conversion as an analytic tool "is to dress up ideology as sociology … 'conversion' has always been part of [colonialism's] apparatus of cultural coercion."

## Operationalizations

The Comaroffs show why standard indices mislead among the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]]: formal church affiliation tracked poorly onto actual cultural change; the earliest baptism candidates were disproportionately outsiders, junior royals, serfs, and women seeking to reverse social disabilities; and most Tswana were selective **[[bricolage|bricoleurs]]** — "nominal Christians" engaging the church "with one ear and tradition with the other." What registered transformation was the uptake of *sekgoa* forms generally (see [[colonization-of-consciousness]]), not professions of faith.

## Applications

Central to the argument of *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*; the critique bears on the anthropology of religion, missions, and the [[protestant-ethic-thesis]] tradition.

## Critiques and Limitations

The debate the Comaroffs enter is live: **Robin Horton's** "intellectualist" thesis (conversion as a quest for meaning under an expanded macrocosm) is challenged by them and by **J. D. Y. Peel** on sociological grounds; defenders reply that abandoning "conversion" altogether loses a real phenomenon of religious change. The Comaroffs' own solution is to dissolve conversion into the [[the-long-conversation|long conversation]] and the graded transformation of consciousness.
