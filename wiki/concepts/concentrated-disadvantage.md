# Concentrated Disadvantage

## Definition as Coined

**Concentrated disadvantage** is an ecological construct summarizing the **co-occurrence** of poverty and correlated structural deficits in neighborhoods (joblessness, family disruption, welfare receipt, racial composition in U.S. cities where race and class are entangled). In the Sampson/PHDCN program it is a principal **structural predictor** of low [[collective-efficacy]] and elevated violence — related to but broader than [[concentrated-poverty]] alone and linked to Wilson’s [[concentration-effects]].

## Semantic History

Builds on [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]]’s concentration thesis and Chicago ecological tradition; operationalized as multi-item disadvantage indices in 1990s criminology and urban sociology. *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (2012) ch. 5 treats **legacies of inequality** and the **durability** of disadvantage across decades; later chapters treat disadvantage as a multi-outcome ecological force (violence, disorder, altruism, incarceration, verbal ability, collective efficacy) not reducible to individual composition (Sampson 2012, attributed).

## Operationalizations

[[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] and colleagues (GAC ch. 5, attributed) construct concentrated disadvantage from **six 1990/2000 census-tract indicators** that load on a **single principal component** in both decades for Chicago and for ~65,000 U.S. tracts: **welfare receipt, poverty, unemployment, female-headed households, percent black, and density of children**. Black-poor, high-unemployment, female-headed tracts are treated as **ecologically distinct**, not merely low-SES. Factor scores / standardized indices at tract or neighborhood-cluster level remain the usual form; inclusion of racial composition raises interpretation debates about race vs class (contested).

## Patterns from *Great American City* (Sampson 2012, attributed)

- **Racial non-overlap of income.** For 2000 Chicago tracts ≥75% black vs ≥75% white, mean per capita income was **$12,276** vs **$42,508**; Sampson reports the entire white-community income distribution sits to the right of the black mean — so a single pooled “effect of concentrated disadvantage” across racial groups is a “phantom reality.” Latino/mixed communities intermediate but below white means.
- **Race-contingent coupling.** Unemployment and economic dependence show **no significant relationship in white communities** (flat line) but a strong positive correlation in mixed and minority areas — synergistic intersection of segregation and disadvantage.
- **Inertial durability.** Concentrated-disadvantage 1990→2000 correlations **above 0.9** with identical patterns for Chicago and ~64,000 non-Chicago U.S. tracts; welfare concentration 1990–2000 stable at **r=0.85** in both Chicago and the nation despite federal welfare reform. Continuity is much higher in segregated black neighborhoods (**r=0.83**) than white (**r=0.24**).
- **Incarceration ecology (“Punishment’s Place,” with Loeffler).** Chicago incarceration rates 1990–95 correlate **0.82** with concentrated disadvantage and **0.75** with percent black (1990); 2000–2005 rates correlate **0.80** / **0.74** with 2000 disadvantage and percent black; tract-level incarceration persistence **r=0.86**. Disadvantage–incarceration ≈0 in white areas but **>0.6** in black, Latino, and mixed areas; large SW/NW swaths nearly untouched while near-west and south-central clusters concentrate punishment.
- **Spatial dependence.** Significant Moran’s I for concentrated disadvantage (**0.66**) alongside immigration and crime — disadvantage is spatially clustered (“neighbors of neighborhoods”).
- **Developmental outcome.** With Sharkey and [[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]], Sampson estimates severe concentrated disadvantage reduces later **verbal ability** among African American children by **>4 points** (~25% of a SD, ≈ missing a year of school), lingering after exit; ages 6–9 at baseline ≈6-point deficit when lag is modeled.
- **Mediation via collective efficacy.** Association of concentrated disadvantage (and residential instability) with violence **declines after [[collective-efficacy]] is controlled** — a community-level pathway (PHDCN program; *Science* 1997 lineage restated in GAC).

## Applications

Key independent variable across PHDCN papers and *[[sampson-great-american-city-study|Great American City]]*; used widely in [[neighborhood-effects]] and crime research; policy debates on mobility experiments (MTO) and community development. Deep study treatment: [[hubs/studies/sampson-great-american-city-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Index composition choices (especially race items); ecological fallacy risks; durability claims depend on period and city; relationship to segregation mechanisms ([[residential-segregation]], Massey & Denton) requires cross-theory linkage. Sampson himself argues pooled causal estimates ignore race–class ecological non-overlap. Live dispute: [[neighborhood-effects-debate]].
