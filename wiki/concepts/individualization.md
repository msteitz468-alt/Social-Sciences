# Individualization

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s term (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Part II) for the process by which, in [[reflexive-modernization|reflexive modernity]], individuals are "set free" from the social forms of industrial society — class, stratification, family, and the gender status of men and women — and thrown back on their own biographies. Distinct from the mass-culture sense of [[pseudo-individualization]] (Adorno), which concerns standardized cultural products masquerading as individual, and which Beck's structural concept in effect *contrasts with*.

## Definition as Coined

Beck argued that people are "set free from the social forms of industrial society... just as during the course of the Reformation people were 'released' from the secular rule of the Church into society" (Beck 1986: 87). Ascribed **class biographies** are transformed into **reflexive biographies** "dependent on the actor's own decisions"; the individual "becomes the reproduction unit of the social in the lifeworld" and "biography itself is acquiring a reflexive project" (Beck 1986: 90).

**Non-negotiable qualification (Beck's own):** individualization is **not** emancipation or autonomy. "This differentiation of individual conditions... must not be equated with successful emancipation" (Beck 1986: 90). It is accompanied by *new* institutional dependency — on the labor market, education, consumption, welfare regulation, and the medical/psychological/pedagogical apparatus: "The apparent outside of the institutions becomes the inside of the individual biography." The very media that individualize (market, money, law, mobility, education) also **standardize** — Beck credits [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] with already showing this of money.

## Semantic History

Beck treated individualization as historically recurrent, not new to the 20th century — locating earlier waves in the Renaissance (Burckhardt), courtly society (Elias), Protestant asceticism (Weber), and peasant emancipation (Marx). He formalized it as a **triple process**: (a) *disembedding* / removal from traditional forms (the "liberating dimension"); (b) *loss of traditional security* in knowledge, faith, norms (the "disenchantment dimension"); (c) *re-embedding* — a new type of social commitment (the "control or reintegration dimension"). His distinctive move was to insist on the *objective life-situation* column that prior "individuation = personalization = emancipation" accounts neglected.

## Operationalizations

Not directly measured; indexed by de-standardized life-courses (see [[do-it-yourself-biography]]), labor-market flexibilization, rising divorce and single-parenthood, and the decline of class-cultural milieus and class-based voting. Beck read a shift in the attribution of *fault* as a symptom: catastrophic life-events once seen as "blows of fate" (war, disaster) are increasingly experienced as **personal failure** (failed exams, unemployment, divorce) — "the immediacy of crisis and sickness."

## Applications

Beck applied the concept to the erosion of class identity ("a capitalism without classes, but with individualized social inequality" — see [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]]); to gender and the family (Ch. 4, the "'I am I'" argument — industrial society as a "modern feudal society" resting on ascribed gender roles; the emergence of the "negotiated family," "a more or less regulated exchange of emotional comfort, which is always cancellable"); and to the [[detraditionalization|destandardization of labor]]. It became one of the most productive and most disputed concepts in the sociology of contemporary modernity.

## Critiques and Limitations

The strong version — individualization as the eclipse of class — is contested by class-analysis sociologists who stress persistent structured inequality (see [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]]). Critics also note the West German, welfare-state specificity of Beck's evidence on family and labor. Beck himself distinguished individualization sharply from autonomy and warned against reading it as the "self-creation of the world by the resurrected individual."
