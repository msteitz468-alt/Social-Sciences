# Play and the Game

## Definition as Coined

[[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] distinguishes two developmental stages in the genesis of the [[the-social-self|self]]. In the **play** stage, "a child plays at being a mother, at being a teacher, at being a policeman; that is, it is taking different rôles" — but serially and without organized relation among them, passing "from one role to another just as a whim takes him." In the organized **game**, by contrast, "the child who plays in a game must be ready to take the attitude of everyone else involved in that game, and... these different roles must have a definite relationship to each other." Mead's standing example is baseball: the player "has to take all of these rôles" at once — "three or four individuals present in his own attitude."

The game's organization "is put in the form of the rules of the game": rules are "the set of responses which a particular attitude calls out... These responses are all in yourself as well." The game is therefore the mechanism of the [[generalized-other|generalized other]] — "the passage in the life of the child from taking the role of others in play to the organized part that is essential to self-consciousness in the full sense" (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Mead, 1925 / 1934 | Play (serial particular roles) vs. game (organized simultaneous roles under rules) as stages toward the self |
| Developmental social psychology | Adapted as a model of socialization and perspective-taking development |
| Mead's own manuscripts (Supplementary Essays) | Same transition marked via "imaginary companions" and the completion of the "circle of the social world" |

Mead extends the distinction to "primitive" ritual: adult role-taking of gods and heroes in religious pageant is analogized to children's play — a period-bound, evolutionist framing to be historicized, not repeated in wiki voice.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. The observable marker is structural: does the child organize taken roles into a rule-governed, simultaneous system (game) or hold them in loose succession (play)? In the Supplementary Essays Mead treats "imaginary companions" as evidence of the earlier, imperfectly-organized self, with the "passage from the earlier form of play into that of games" announcing an achieved, continuous self.

## Applications

- The concrete developmental route by which [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]] becomes organized into the [[generalized-other|generalized other]].
- A foundational model of **socialization** widely used in the [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic-interactionist]] and developmental literatures.
- Supplies the sense in which social **rules** are internalized organized responses rather than external constraints only.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The play→game sequence is a stylized developmental model; its universality and timing are empirical questions the ingested (theoretical) source does not test.
- The analogy between children's play and "primitive" ritual reflects early-twentieth-century recapitulationism.
- Editorially reconstructed source text (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]).
