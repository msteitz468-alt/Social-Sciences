# Regimes of Value

## Definition as Coined

In *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* (1986), [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] redirects [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]’s analysis of economic value (value as judgment and reciprocal sacrifice, not inherent property) toward the conditions under which economic objects circulate in different **regimes of value** in space and time. Desire and demand, reciprocal sacrifice, and power interact to create economic value in specific social situations *(Appadurai)*.

He prefers “regimes of value” over language that implies complete cultural sharing of assumptions among exchange parties:

> "I therefore prefer to use the term regimes of value, which does not imply that every act of commodity exchange presupposes a complete cultural sharing of assumptions… A regime of value, in this sense, is consistent with both very high and very low sharing of standards by the parties to a particular commodity exchange."

## Operationalizations

Tied to Appadurai’s three-part **commodity situation**:

1. **Phase** — temporal presence in commodity state (in/out, slow/fast, reversible/terminal).
2. **Candidacy** — symbolic, classificatory, moral standards of exchangeability (e.g. Bohannan Tiv spheres; attenuated in cross-cultural trade or famine/warfare exchanges).
3. **Context** — social arenas linking candidacy to phase (marriage exchanges, auctions, bazaars, strangers, silent trade).

Politics (broadly: privilege, control, contests over rules) is “what links value and exchange in the social life of commodities” and links regimes of value to specific flows *(Appadurai conclusion)*.

## Applications (in SLOT)

- Kula paths and [[tournaments-of-value]] as nonindustrial regime with fame as increment.
- Enclaved/royal and sacred regimes (Gluckman kingly things; Geary relics; Davenport sacred canoes).
- Knowledge regimes (Spooner carpet authenticity; Reddy cloth connoisseurship; Bayly cloth as political discourse).
- Qat’s shift from culturally restricted to more fully commoditized demand (Cassanelli).

## Critiques and Limitations

Programmatic 1986 term; later uses may loosen the exchange focus. Does not replace production analysis — Appadurai aims to demystify the **demand** side alongside Marxian production demystification *(attributed)*.
