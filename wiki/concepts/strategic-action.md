# Strategic Action

> **Disambiguation:** This page is Habermas's action-theoretic concept from *[[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v1-1984|TCA Vol. 1]]*. It is **not** Fligstein and McAdam's [[strategic-action-field]] / [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] (a different research program about fields of organized actors).

## Definition as Coined

**Strategic action** is [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s term (attributed) for **social action oriented to success** — action coordinated by egocentric calculation of success and by influencing the decisions of other rational opponents under rules of rational choice. In the TCA typology (Figure 14):

| | Oriented to success | Oriented to reaching understanding |
|---|---|---|
| **Nonsocial** | instrumental action | — |
| **Social** | **strategic action** | **[[communicative-action]]** |

Habermas (attributed): "We call an action oriented to success *strategic* when we consider it under the aspect of following rules of rational choice and assess the efficacy of influencing the decisions of a rational opponent." Strategic and communicative action are treated as **real types of social action** (identifiable attitudes of participants), not merely two analytic aspects of the same act.

A further distinction (Ch. III): **open** strategic action vs **linguistically mediated** strategic action that uses speech **perlocutionarily** — treating illocution as a means to success rather than as an offer of criticizable validity. Only the latter can *look* like communication while remaining strategic.

## Semantic History

- **Weberian background.** Builds on [[purposive-rationality|Zweckrationalität]] and the teleological model of the solitary actor; Habermas argues Weber never adequately left this model for interpersonal reaching-understanding.
- **TCA Vol. 1 (1981/1984).** Formal definition opposite [[communicative-action]]; linked to Austin's perlocution vs illocution.
- **TCA Vol. 2.** When money and power as [[steering-media]] replace language, system domains institutionalize success-oriented coordination "while bypassing processes of consensus-oriented communication" — strategic logic at subsystem scale.
- **Not to be confused with** post-2000s field theory of "strategic action fields."

## Operationalizations

Not measured. Diagnostic contrasts:

- Does the actor treat the other as an opponent to be influenced or as a participant in defining a shared situation?
- Is speech used for illocutionary binding or for perlocutionary effects?
- In systems contexts: is coordination media-steered (delinguistified strategic) or communicatively achieved?

## Applications

- Counterpart concept that makes [[communicative-action]] sharp rather than residual.
- Bridge from Weber's purposive rationality to Habermas's diagnosis that modern subsystems expand strategic coordination at the expense of communicative coordination ([[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]).
- Useful foil in deliberative-democracy debates (strategic vs communicative use of public reason).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Sharp dualism** (attributed): critics argue real interaction always mixes success and understanding motives; treating them as exclusive types over-purifies.
- **Strategic action inside "systems"** (attributed): by locating much strategic coordination in norm-free media subsystems, Habermas is charged with naturalizing economy and state as beyond communicative critique.
