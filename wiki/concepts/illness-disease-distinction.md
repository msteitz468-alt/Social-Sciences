# Illness / Disease / Sickness Distinction

An analytical distinction, systematized by [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]], between the
**lived experience of a disorder** and its **biomedical reconstruction** — the conceptual
core of interpretive [[medical-anthropology]] and of *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The
Illness Narratives]]* (1988).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman draws the distinction in attributed voice (his own terms, quoted):

- **Illness** — "the innately human experience of symptoms and suffering… how the sick
  person and the members of the family or wider social network perceive, live with, and
  respond to symptoms and disability." Illness is "the lived experience of monitoring bodily
  processes," the common-sense appraisal and explanation of distress, and the *illness
  behavior* (self-treatment, deciding when to seek care) that follows. Kleinman distinguishes
  **illness problems** ("the principal difficulties that symptoms and disability create in our
  lives") from **illness complaints** ("what patients and their families bring to the
  practitioner").
- **Disease** — "what the practitioner creates in the recasting of illness in terms of
  theories of disorder… what practitioners have been trained to see through the theoretical
  lenses of their particular form of practice." In the narrow biomedical model, disease is
  "an alteration in biological structure or functioning" (e.g., "an occluded coronary
  artery"); in the biopsychosocial model (citing Engel 1977) it is "a dynamic dialectic"
  among biological, psychological, and environmental processes.
- **Sickness** — a third term, "the understanding of a disorder in its generic sense across a
  population in relation to macrosocial (economic, political, institutional) forces" — e.g.,
  tuberculosis in relation to poverty, or lung cancer in relation to the tobacco industry.
  This register is the entry point for [[critical-medical-anthropology]].

Kleinman's summary formulation (attributed): "The difference between transcript and record,
interview and written medical notation, is the difference between illness as the patient's
problem and disease as the physician's problem."

## Semantic History

The illness/disease split crystallized across 1970s medical anthropology and the sociology of
health (the medical-sociological [[sick-role]] and "illness behavior" literatures are cognate).
Kleinman's contribution was to make the gap *clinically actionable* and to add the third,
macrosocial term, *sickness*. He gave it a compact formulation in
*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* (1980) — "Disease refers to a
malfunctioning of biological and/or psychological processes, while the term illness refers to the
psychosocial experience and meaning of perceived disease… illness is the shaping of disease into
behavior and experience" — and there, following Engelhardt (1974), stressed that "disease and illness
are explanatory concepts, not entities… representing relationships," anchored in the sectors of the
[[health-care-system]] and their [[clinical-reality|clinical realities]]. *The Illness Narratives*
(1988) then developed the third term, *sickness*, and the clinical program. He grounds the distinction in his own medical-school encounters
(a badly burned seven-year-old girl; an elderly woman with tertiary syphilis) — "a key
distinction I will develop in the course of this book." A recurring illustration of the
semantic gap: female "semen loss" (leucorrhea) is a real, feared illness category in South
Asian Ayurvedic theory but "impossible in biomedical theory."

## Operationalizations

In *The Illness Narratives* the distinction is operationalized clinically: illness is elicited
through the patient's [[explanatory-models|explanatory model]] and [[illness-narrative]], while
disease is the reconstruction recorded in the chart. Kleinman argues (attributed) that the very
act of **recording a case in the medical record** is "a profound, ritual act of transformation
through which illness is made over into disease, person becomes patient, and professional values
are transferred from the practitioner to the 'case.'" He reads doctor–patient transcripts (the
Jill Lawler psoriasis interview; the Melissa Flowers hypertension interview) as showing "the
voice of medicine" (Mishler 1984) drowning out "the voice of the life world" — the chart note on
Flowers ("Hypertension, poorly controlled; Noncompliance") is, in Kleinman's reading, "a
systematic distortion of the interview." **Hypochondriasis** and **factitious illness** are
treated as limit cases where the two come apart entirely: in hypochondriasis "the patient
complains of disease… and the doctor can confirm only illness."

## Applications

- The clinical program of *The Illness Narratives*: the [[explanatory-models-interview|mini-ethnography]]
  is designed to recover the illness that the disease reconstruction discards.
- Cross-cultural psychiatry: Chinese [[neurasthenia]] as an illness whose disease reconstruction
  (DSM depression/anxiety) does not exhaust its social meaning.
- A standing critique of biomedicine's "mind–body dichotomy, which assumes that only biological
  aspects of illness are 'real.'"

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman himself qualifies the distinction: a rigid, disease-focused biomedical approach is
"often appropriate for acute disease and acute exacerbations," and "inappropriate" chiefly in
the *long-term care of chronic illness*. The three-term scheme (illness / disease / sickness)
is an etic analytical grid, not a claim about how any patient or clinician actually partitions
experience; Kleinman warns against jettisoning the disease "copy" ("lest the disease go
untreated"). Later [[critical-medical-anthropology]] argues the *sickness* register — political
economy — deserves more weight than the interpretive program gives it.
