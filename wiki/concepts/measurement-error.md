## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) treat measurement error across two chapters of
*Designing Social Inquiry* (1994): Chapter 2 (statistical vs. substantive bias, and
sources of biased data) and Chapter 5 (the reliability/validity trade-off and its
consequences for causal inference).

KKV first distinguish "statistical bias," a property of an estimator (systematic
over- or under-estimation across hypothetical replications), from "substantive bias,"
a property of the underlying system or institution being measured — their example
being an electoral rule that structurally disadvantages one group. They write: "we
might wish to distinguish our definition of 'statistical bias' in an estimator from
'substantive bias' in an electoral system. An example of the latter are polling hours
that make it harder for working people to vote." An estimator can therefore be
statistically unbiased while measuring a substantively biased system.

KKV further insist bias is theory-relative, not a property of raw data alone: "bias
depends on the theory that is being investigated and does not just exist in the data
alone. It makes little sense to say that a particular data set is biased, even though
it may be filled with many individual errors."

## Semantic History

The reliability/validity distinction long predates KKV, with deep roots in
psychometrics and survey methodology; KKV's contribution is folding it into their
broader single-logic argument that both qualitative and quantitative measurement face
the same formal error structure. In Chapter 5, KKV state: "Reliability—different
measurements of the same phenomenon yield the same results—is sometimes purchased at
the expense of validity—the measurements reflect what the investigator is trying to
measure," identifying a trade-off in which quantitative measures often gain
consistency (reliability) at the cost of construct fidelity (validity).

## Operationalizations

KKV distinguish several operational cases:

- **Self-interested informants as a source of bias.** Officials or informants
  supplying raw data often have incentives to over- or under-report systematically.
  KKV illustrate with Weiner's (1991) account of Indian school-attendance statistics:
  "ninety-eight percent of school age children attend school. However, a closer look
  revealed that attendance was measured once, when children first entered school. They
  were then listed as attending for seven years, even if their only attendance was for
  one day!" KKV generalize: "people who provide the raw information that we use for
  descriptive inferences often have reasons for providing estimates that are
  systematically too high or low."
- **Constant vs. subgroup-correlated error and causal inference.** KKV show that
  measurement error's consequences differ for descriptive versus causal claims (see
  [[systematic-vs-nonsystematic-variation]]): "systematic measurement error which
  affects all units by the same constant amount causes no bias in causal inference,"
  because a constant additive error cancels out of a between-group difference; but
  when error is correlated with group membership (e.g., differential over-reporting
  by subgroup), "both the descriptive inference and our inference about the causal
  effect of education on income would be biased."
- **The bias-efficiency trade-off.** KKV argue researchers routinely choose between a
  less-biased-but-less-efficient measure (a carefully corrected case study) and a
  more-efficient-but-biased measure (large-n data of dubious quality, e.g.
  government-supplied statistics), with mean square error (variance plus squared bias)
  as the formal criterion for the choice.
- **Remedy for subjective/systematic qualitative coding error.** KKV recommend using
  codings or measures produced by others for unrelated purposes, or blind cross-coding
  by multiple observers unaware of the hypothesis under test, as a guard against
  hypothesis-driven coding bias.

## Applications

The Weiner India schooling case and the reliability/validity trade-off are KKV's
principal illustrations, chosen to show that measurement problems are not unique to
quantitative research: a qualitative case study relying on official records inherits
the same informant-incentive and coding-bias hazards as a large-n survey.

## Critiques and Limitations

KKV's insistence that bias is theory-relative and that qualitative and quantitative
measurement share a single error structure is part of their broader single-logic
thesis, which is itself contested; see
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] and
[[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]. Critics of the unified-logic program have
questioned whether treating qualitative coding error as formally equivalent to
quantitative sampling/measurement error understates distinctive interpretive
difficulties (meaning-construction, positionality) that do not reduce cleanly to a
bias/efficiency calculus. This page records that dispute rather than resolving it.
