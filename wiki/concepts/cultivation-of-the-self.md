# The Cultivation of the Self

## Definition as Coined

**The cultivation of the self** is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s periodizing name (attributed), in *[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (1984), for the general phenomenon of the first two centuries CE in which "the relations of oneself to oneself were intensified and valorized." Organized around but broader than the single precept of [[care-of-the-self|epimeleia heautou]], it is the historical movement — a "kind of golden age in the cultivation of the self" — through which the [[arts-of-existence|art of existence]] came to revolve around self-preoccupation. Foucault restricts it explicitly to the "very limited" literate and cultured groups for whom a *technē tou biou* had meaning.

## Semantic History

The concept is defined against a rival explanation Foucault rejects. He refuses to read the era's heightened sexual austerity and self-attention as a generic **"rise of individualism"**, separating three phenomena usually conflated under that label: (1) the *individualistic attitude* (value on singularity, independence from the group); (2) the *positive valuation of private life*; and (3) the *intensity of the relation to self*. These can combine but need not — he cites military aristocracies (individualism without intense self-relation), nineteenth-century bourgeois society (private-life valuation, weak self-relation), and early Christian asceticism (intense self-relation *rejecting* individualism and private life) as dissociating cases. The cultivation of the self is specifically the third — an intensification of the self-relation — and Foucault likewise rejects the older thesis that it followed from the **decline of the city-state** and a withdrawal from public life: imperial political life, he argues (with Veyne, Vatin, MacMullen, and others), was not a hollow shell but a "complex space" of dispersed power.

## Operationalizations

As a periodizing category the cultivation of the self is reconstructed textually. Its diagnostic claim (attributed) is Foucault's central methodological thesis for the whole volume: the added severity of imperial-era sexual morality is "not… a tightening of the code that defined prohibited acts, but… an intensification of the relation to oneself by which one constituted oneself as the subject of one's acts." The *content* of the norms (regimen, conjugal fidelity, restraint toward boys) is not measurably stricter than in the classical period; what changes is "the modality, scope, constancy, and exactitude of the required vigilance." Foucault also judges the political-legislative explanation (Augustus's marriage and adultery laws) too sporadic and limited to account for a sustained two-century trend addressed to individuals rather than enforced by coercion. He names the underlying difficulty **"a crisis of the subject, or rather a crisis of subjectivation"** — a new problem of how the individual could constitute himself as ethical subject once self-mastery was no longer automatically tied to household and civic power.

## Applications

The cultivation of the self is the master frame for the volume's substantive analyses: the medicalized [[dietetics|regimen of the body]], the [[ethics-foucauldian|new conjugal ethic]] ("the art of conjugality is an integral part of the cultivation of the self"), and the deproblematization of the love of boys. Foucault presents it as the immediate prehistory of Christian asceticism — a reservoir of practices and themes that Christianity would take up and recode (a continuity of techniques, a discontinuity of the mode of subjectivation). As an analytic it has traveled into the historical sociology of the self, the history of subjectivity, and studies of self-help and therapeutic culture.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The three-way separation of "individualism" and the rejection of the city-state-decline thesis are Foucault's arguments against specific historiographical conventions; both are attributed, and the periodization (a "golden age" of the self in the 1st–2nd c. CE) is his construction.
- Restricting the phenomenon to a literate elite is a stated sampling frame, not a claim about imperial society at large; the archive cannot speak to the self-relation of the non-elite.
- Whether the imperial intensification is best read as *continuous* with or *broken from* Christian asceticism is the live debate Foucault stages in the Conclusion and answers with his "continuity of themes, discontinuity of subjectivation" thesis — recorded here as his position.
