# Law of Superposition

## Definition as Coined

The law of superposition holds that, in an undisturbed sequence of deposits, **each layer
was laid down before the layer above it and after the layer below** — so that relative age
follows vertical position. It was formulated in **geology** (associated with William "Strata"
Smith, whose *Strata Identified by Organized Fossils* appeared in 1816) and borrowed into
archaeology as the foundation of **[[stratigraphic-excavation]]**.
[[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]] rendered it through the analogy of a book: "the successive
accumulations of construction and debris on a buried occupation-site have much the same
validity as the successive pages of a book," to be read in sequence (attributed, Wheeler
1954). Jacquetta Hawkes's "apples on Newton" image expresses the same principle.

## Semantic History

In geology the law concerns natural sedimentary deposition. Transferred to archaeology it
governs **anthropogenic** layers — floors, rebuildings, destruction debris, pit-fills,
alluvium — whose deposition is far more irregular than geological sedimentation. This
transfer is the concept's key drift: Wheeler stressed that human strata do not accumulate at
a uniform rate and that not all layering is time-significant, so the archaeological law is
hedged by his caution that "the first rule about stratification is that there is no
invariable rule" (attributed, Wheeler 1954). The concept remains foundational and
uncontested in principle, while its *application* — reading real, disturbed, intruded, and
residual deposits — is where archaeological method concentrates.

## Operationalizations

Superposition is read in the cut **vertical section** and recorded layer by layer (Wheeler's
labelled-peg method), then combined with **[[seriation]]** and absolute dating
([[radiocarbon-dating]]) to convert relative order into chronology. It licenses the
**[[stratigraphic-excavation|association]]** of finds and structures with the layer that
contains them.

## Applications

The principle underwrites every stratigraphic excavation; Wheeler's deep marginal sections
at Harappa and Verulamium are exemplary readings of superposed human deposits.

## Critiques and Limitations

The law gives only **relative** order and can be defeated by disturbance: intrusion (later
material cut down into earlier layers, e.g. a burial), residuality (earlier material
redeposited in later layers), animal and root action, and deliberate infilling all violate
simple superposition. Detecting these — via soil character at junctions and the statistical
overlap of finds across boundaries — is the interpretive core of
[[stratigraphic-excavation]]. Superposition says nothing about absolute date or about
whether a boundary marks a long occupation gap or none at all.
