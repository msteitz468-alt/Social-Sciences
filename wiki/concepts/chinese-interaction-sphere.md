# Chinese Interaction Sphere

## Definition as Coined

The "Chinese interaction sphere" is the model [[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] introduced in the
fourth edition of *The Archaeology of Ancient China* (1986) to describe how, around 4000 BCE, a set
of previously separate Late Neolithic regional cultures across the Yellow and Yangzi River basins
became linked into a single sphere of interaction — sharing prestige objects, ritual practices, and
stylistic conventions. Chang argued (attributed) that this network of contacts among regional
cultures, rather than radiation from a single center, was the mechanism that catalyzed the
development of what later became Chinese civilization.

## Semantic History

The model marked a deliberate break from Chang's own earlier [[nuclear-area|"nuclear area"]]
framework, in which north-China cultures were the monocentric source of civilization that then
spread outward (the "Lungshanoid expansion"). The interaction-sphere model was initially received as
heterodoxy but became widely accepted for the Late Neolithic — part of the broader "regionalist
paradigm" (Falkenhausen 1995) that reshaped Chinese archaeology from the mid-1980s, when a surge of
salvage-archaeology discoveries in previously "peripheral" regions made the monocentric story
untenable.

## Operationalizations

The interaction sphere is operationalized as a **distribution of shared prestige and ritual items
across otherwise distinct regional Late Neolithic assemblages** — jade forms (*cong*, *bi*),
comparable burial-ranking practices, and stylistic conventions recurring from the Yellow River to the
Yangzi and beyond around 4000 BCE — read as the material trace of contact among regional cultures
rather than of a single expanding culture. Because the same distribution can in principle reflect
migration, down-the-line exchange, or independent convergence, the concept's application turns on
specifying the interaction *mechanism*; critics (below) note that unspecified "interaction" is not
measurably distinct from the older "diffusion." It is operationally the regional instance of
[[peer-polity-interaction|peer-polity interaction]] and contrasts with the monocentric
[[nuclear-area|nuclear-area]] measure of a single hearth radiating traits outward.

## Applications

[[shelach-lavi-gideon|Gideon Shelach-Lavi]] (attributed, 2015) treats the Chinese interaction sphere
as a specific case of Renfrew's [[peer-polity-interaction|peer-polity interaction]] — it describes
the *effects* of interaction among comparable polities but not the *mechanisms* by which interaction
was carried out. The concept is central to the
[[chinese-civilization-origins-debate|debate over the origins of Chinese civilization]] (monocentric
"nuclear area" vs. multiregional/interaction models).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note (attributed) that few researchers have extended Chang's model beyond the Yellow–Yangzi
basins or beyond the Neolithic, and that "interaction" risks becoming a modern relabeling of the old
"diffusion" — explaining everything and therefore nothing — unless the specific mechanism, direction,
and social level of contact are specified. The model does not by itself distinguish interaction among
equals from asymmetric [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] domination.
