# Systemic Context

In [[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s framework, the state of artifacts **when they are participating in a behavioral system** (attributed, Schiffer 1972a; 1987:3–4; 1995 Ch. 2). Contrasted with [[archaeological-context|archaeological context]] (e.g. materials in a dump interacting only with the natural environment). An artifact may move back and forth many times between the two.

## Definition as Coined

“Systemic context refers to artifacts when they are participating in a behavioral system” (Schiffer 1987). The book the reader holds, and remaining artifacts in a dwelling or office, are in systemic context. Ethnoarchaeology, experimental archaeology, and modern material-culture studies treat items still interacting with people; past systemic contexts of recovered things are knowable only through **inference** — “a statement about the past supported by relevant principles and relevant evidence” (attributed). In *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* Schiffer restates the 1972 charter cut: materials participating in past behavioral systems vs materials recovered as remains, linked by transformation procedures (c-transforms / n-transforms) that model cultural and noncultural [[formation-processes]] (attributed, Schiffer 1972a / 1995 Ch. 2, 7).

## Semantic History

Core binary of [[behavioral-archaeology]] transformation theory. Related but not identical to the “historical record” (artifacts retained in living societies after change in form, function, or user — not discarded). Reuse processes primarily create historical-record survival within systemic context (attributed, Ch. 3). Critics of the Pompeii/distortion framing question whether the binary smuggles a living-system original (see [[pompeii-premise]]). In the Binford–Schiffer shared-ground list (Schiffer 1985b / 1995 Ch. 19), keeping systemic context (Binford’s “dynamics”) distinct from archaeological context (“statics”) is principle (1) of agreement even where transformation language is disputed (attributed).

## Operationalizations

- Life-history stages: procurement → manufacture → use (techno-, socio-, ideo-functions) → reuse or deposition; [[behavioral-chain]] analysis organizes individual activities along those trajectories (Schiffer 1975d; 1995 Ch. 5, 23).
- [[refuse-types]]: de facto refuse = usable systemic inventory left at abandonment; curate behavior moves items to new systemic inventories.
- Discard equations use **systemic number** S (average number of a type normally in use).
- Ethnoarchaeology defined as study of material culture **in systemic context** to acquire information useful for archaeological investigation (Schiffer 1978b / 1995 Ch. 9).

## Applications

Formation-process identification; ethnoarchaeology of discard and reuse; caution against labeling archaeological finds with use names without wear/context control; equivalence-transformation critique when analysts treat archaeological patterns as if systemic properties were preserved (Reid 1973; Schiffer 1976, 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20).

## Critiques and Limitations

Clean cut is analytical; reclamation, scavenging, and archaeological collection complicate trajectories. Field “context” jargon (stratigraphic unit) is a different sense of the word.
