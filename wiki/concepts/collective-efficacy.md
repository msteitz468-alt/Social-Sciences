# Collective Efficacy

## Definition as Coined

**Collective efficacy** (Sampson, Raudenbush & Earls, *Science* 1997; full program synthesis in *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* 2012, ch. 7) is the linkage of **social cohesion and mutual trust** with **shared expectations for informal social control** — neighbors’ willingness to intervene for the common good. [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] treats it as a **task-specific** capacity of the neighborhood as a social unit, not general friendliness, dense kinship, or Putnam-style stocks of [[social-capital]] alone. Emphasis is on **activation under challenge**: expectations for control increase interventions when needed; high efficacy may mean little visible intervention precisely because challenges are suppressed.

## Semantic History

Develops from [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] [[social-disorganization-theory|social-disorganization]] theory (Shaw & McKay) but shifts emphasis from the *absence* of organization or the urban-village dense-ties ideal to **shared expectations for control under structural constraint**. Sampson (2012) critiques dense personal ties as insufficient and potentially neutral or double-edged (ties can support crime as well as control; “negotiated coexistence”). Related to but distinct from Putnam-style social capital: collective efficacy stresses **agency and purpose** (control of children, disorder, violence). Differs from [[broken-windows]] disorder→crime pathways by locating the key mechanism in **shared expectations and intervention**, with disorder partly downstream of low efficacy and structural disadvantage (Sampson–Raudenbush program).

## Operationalizations

**Informal control vignettes** (likelihood neighbors would act if): children skipping school/hanging out on a corner; spray-painting graffiti; showing disrespect to an adult; a fight in front of the house; the closest fire station threatened with budget cuts.

**Cohesion/trust items** (agreement): people willing to help neighbors; people can be trusted; close-knit neighborhood; people generally get along; people share the same values.

Combined summary measure with aggregate-level reliability in the high 0.80s after multi-level adjustment for individual social position (age, race, sex, class, home ownership). Predictors in the PHDCN program include [[concentrated-disadvantage]], residential instability, and immigrant concentration. Outcomes: homicide, violent victimization, disorder, and other community processes. Sampson (2012) reports that a **two standard-deviation** elevation in collective efficacy was associated with a **~26% reduction** in the expected homicide rate after adjusting for prior violence; associations held with independent violence measures and extensive compositional controls. Panel models he reports link prior CE to later lower homicide (e.g. coeff. ≈ −0.87 with prior homicide controlled) alongside [[legal-cynicism]] effects in the opposite direction.

## Applications

- Core mechanism of *Great American City* and the PHDCN crime papers ([[sampson-great-american-city-study]]; [[hubs/studies/sampson-great-american-city-study]]).
- Linked to organizational density (survey-measured services → CE; IRS nonprofit density → collective events — dual pathways, Sampson 2012 ch. 8).
- Spatial clustering: high-high CE co-clusters with low homicide; neighbors’ violence conditions local risk (ch. 10).
- KeyNet: CE predicts internal leadership cohesion (ch. 14).
- 2010 revisit: CE still related to later violence (~1 SD ≈ −25% in Sampson’s 2010-framed associations); off-diagonal high-CE black middle-class communities (e.g. Avalon Park/Chatham) as theoretical anomalies.
- Cross-context: Stockholm comparison; UK and other replications of PHDCN items (as cited by Sampson 2012); Pratt–Cullen meta-analysis r≈−0.303 for social-disorganization/CE-type constructs (as Sampson reports).

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement relies on survey perceptions; reverse causation (crime→efficacy via fear/cynicism) is built into Sampson’s dynamic account rather than denied. Selection into neighborhoods remains a standing identification concern ([[neighborhood-effects-debate]]). Cultural and institutional depths beyond survey items (gangs, churches, politics) are thinner — Sampson notes church density’s complex/null patterns for events and trust. Generalizability beyond Chicago is an ongoing empirical question; Sampson answers with theory-driven comparison and cited replications, not multi-city original design. “Dark side” of efficacy (exclusionary control) is acknowledged.
