# Kurgan Hypothesis

## Definition as Coined

The **Kurgan hypothesis** (also **steppe hypothesis** in later literature) places the Proto-Indo-European homeland on the Pontic-Caspian steppes and associates early IE expansion with kurgan (barrow) burial pastoralists. [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]] gave the classic multi-wave "Kurganization" package; J.P. Mallory argued persuasively for the same region with different criteria. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]]'s *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* (2007) is the major archaeological refinement used as Position A on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] for the *homeland/chronology core*; Gimbutas’s full dual-structure narrative (goddess civilization vs patriarchal Kurgans) is treated separately as Position A on [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]].

Gimbutas states she introduced the blanket term **“Kurgan tradition”** in 1956 for the culture of seminomadic pastoralists who built round funeral mounds (Russian *kurgan*, from Turkish = barrow/tumulus). In *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* (1991, Ch. 10) she identifies Proto-/Early Indo-Europeans as “Kurgan” people who arrived from southern Russia on horseback; first contact with [[old-europe|Old Europe]]’s borderlands (Lower Dnieper, west of Black Sea) began mid-5th millennium B.C., with continuous flow lasting two millennia (her dates and framing).

Anthony states (with many others) that the PIE homeland was in the steppes north of the Black and Caspian Seas — southern Ukraine and Russia — and holds that recent discoveries (as of 2007) so strengthen the Pontic-Caspian case that one may proceed on that assumption. In the book's conclusion he writes that linguistic and archaeological discoveries converge on the probability that PIE was spoken in the Pontic-Caspian steppes between 4500 and 2500 BCE, with alternatives increasingly hard to square with new evidence; he credits Gimbutas and Mallory as predecessors.

### Gimbutas 1991 three-wave package (primary formulation)

Gimbutas 1991 (attributed, verbatim load-bearing frame): “The Kurgan tradition became manifest in Old European territories during three waves of infiltration: I at c. 4400–4300 B.C., II at c. 3500 B.C., and III soon after 3000 B.C. This chronology does not represent the evolution of a single group but of a number of various steppe peoples who shared a common tradition…”

| Wave | Date (cal B.C., her figure) | Source area (her claim) | Yamna mapping (her equation) | Principal impact (her claim) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-wave / formation | ~5000–4500 | Middle Volga forest-steppe → Lower Volga; Sredniy Stog II into Dnieper ~4500 | Horse cult, shell-tempered pottery, weapons, mobile pastoralism | Steppe N of Black Sea; first Dnieper contact mid-5th mill. |
| **Wave 1 (Kurgan I)** | **c. 4400–4300** | Lower Volga / Early Yamna via Lower Dnieper | **early Yamna = Kurgan I** | Moldavia, S Romania, E Hungary; catastrophic for Varna, Karanovo-Gumelniţa, Vinča, Lengyel; Cucuteni survives; LBK/Rössen hill forts; single graves → Ireland/England by mid-4th mill. |
| **Wave 2** | **c. 3500** | North Pontic Maikop / Mikhailovka I (not Volga) | **Mikhailovka I or Maikop = Kurgan II** | Cucuteni → Usatovo amalgamation; Baden–Vučedol–Ezero; Globular Amphora; hill-fort rule of central Europe |
| **Wave 3** | **soon after 3000** (~3000–2900) | Late Yamna (Lower Dnieper–Don–Volga) into E-central Europe | **late Yamna = Kurgan III** | Romania, Bulgaria, Yugoslavia (Banat), E Hungary; Corded Pottery expansion; Bell Beaker as Vučedol+Yamna amalgam (her reading) |

Diagnostic packages she inventories: Wave 1 — round barrows, pit graves, male elite, horse-head scepters, suttee, weapons usable from horseback; Wave 2 — elite mortuary houses, stone cupolas, orthostats/stelae, arsenical bronze, apsidal hill-fort houses, wagons/ox teams; Wave 3 — deep pit graves, timber huts in pits, ochre, low barrows + stone rings/ditches, poor inventory + elite pins/daggers.

**Dual OE vs Kurgan contrast (Gimbutas 1991, attributed):** Old Europe “in the main, peaceful, sedentary, matrifocal, matrilineal, and sex egalitarian”; Kurgans “warlike, patriarchal, and hierarchical” pastoralists with pit graves under cairn/mound, rudimentary agriculture, seasonal semi-subterranean settlements. She holds OE and IE belief systems “diametrically opposed” — collision of two ideologies, not evolution out of OE. Burial contrast: OE cyclic regeneration (“tomb was a womb”), symbolic goods, communal/two-stage burial vs IE lineal continuity of powerful males, mortuary houses, weapons, horses, wives/servants, lavish royal tombs. She claims no weapons except hunting implements among European grave goods until c. 4500–4300 B.C., and no hilltop fortification of OE settlements before Kurgan contact (her inventory — contested elsewhere). End-state transformation she lists: matrilineal→patrilineal; learned theacracy→militant patriarchy; sexually balanced→male-dominated hierarchy; chthonic goddess religion→IE sky-oriented pantheon. She rejects climate, soil exhaustion, and epidemic as explanations for late-5th-mill. discontinuities; treats trait-complex co-occurrence (hill forts + horses + pastoralism + violence + patriarchy markers + sun cult) as a non-independent Kurgan package.

She maps Russian archaeological terms: *yama* = pit under barrow; early Yamna = Kurgan I; Mikhailovka I/Maikop = Kurgan II; late Yamna = Kurgan III. Method: reconstruct PIE culture from comparative IE linguistics and mythology, support with early historic records, equate with Kurgan archaeology of 5th-mill. Volga forest-steppe/steppe and N Pontic. She rejects [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]’s 1987 equation of matristic Neolithic Europe with proto-IE as based on “a misconception of cultural structures” and as contrary to IE studies evidence.

## Semantic History

- **Gimbutas (classic and 1991):** successive Kurgan waves as above; Old Europe disruption narrative; goddess/matristic package (separate and more contested than the homeland chronology). Full dual-structure program in *Civilization of the Goddess* Ch. 10; earlier formulations from 1956 coinage through 1970s–80s wave papers. Anthony retains only the EBA Pit-grave ([[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]]) steppe core of her Kurgan concept, rejecting her later expansion of "Kurgan culture" to Maikop and many European Late Neolithic cultures as so broad that almost any mound-using (or even Baden-like non-mound) culture could be included. He prefers the Western term **horizon** — neutral about ethnic identity — for a style/fashion rapidly accepted across local cultures.
- **Mallory:** same Pontic-Caspian endpoint with criteria differing in details from Gimbutas.
- **Anthony 2007:** retains steppe homeland and Yamnaya-centered late PIE; replaces pure invasion with [[elite-recruitment]] / institutional franchising; anchors chronology with wool and wheel vocabularies ([[linguistic-palaeontology]]); uses [[bit-wear-analysis]] and horse economies for mobility; models persistent ethnolinguistic frontiers at steppe ecotones; sequences out-migrations to match branch order. **Later narrowing of Gimbutas:** Anthony’s Suvorovo ~4200–3900, Afanasievo, Yamnaya ~3300, Corded Ware contact, Sintashta sequence reworks Wave-1–3 chronology and mechanisms without endorsing the full OE goddess-civilization binary.
- **Post-2015 aDNA:** large-scale steppe-related ancestry movements into Europe (as of Reich 2018) reweight demography toward a steppe vector without equating genes with language (four-way non-identity).

## Operationalizations

Anthony's 2007 package combines independent constraints:

### Chronology (linguistic termini)

- **Terminus post quem ~4000–3500 BCE:** late PIE after wool and especially wheeled-vehicle vocabulary; wheeled vehicles not invented before 4000 BCE, evidence explosion ~3400–3000 BCE.
- **Terminus ante quem ~2500 BCE:** reconstructed PIE becomes anachronism once early daughters are historically dated (Greek/Indo-Iranian perspectives).
- **Maximum window ~4500–2500 BCE** for archaic-to-late PIE.
- **Phased sequence (Anthony):** archaic PIE (partly preserved in Anatolian) before 4000 BCE; early PIE (partly in Tocharian) 4000–3500 BCE; late PIE (Italic/Celtic source with full wagon vocabulary) 3500–3000 BCE; Pre-Germanic ~3300; Pre-Greek ~2500; Pre-Baltic from Pre-Slavic ~2500; Pre-Indo-Iranian 2500–2200 BCE. Heart of reconstructable PIE ~4000–3000 BCE.

### Geography (linguistic ecology + frontiers)

- Temperate flora/fauna; honeybees exclude Siberia/Kazakh steppes; *h₁éḱwos* horse points toward Eurasian steppes; farmers-stockbreeders package; Uralic and weaker Caucasian/Kartvelian contacts.
- Synthesis location: west of Urals, between Urals and Caucasus — Pontic-Caspian steppes of eastern Ukraine and Russia.
- Multi-millennial material frontiers at steppe edges (Tripolye/Dnieper west; Russian forest forager/steppe herder north; Volga-Ural herder/Kazakh forager east) treated as probable homeland borders.
- Steppe–farming ecotone as durable linguistic frontier through recorded history (China to eastern Europe).

### Archaeological match (attributed correlation, not identity)

Anthony claims Pontic-Caspian archaeology shows cultures fitting the vocabulary: domesticated horse/cattle/sheep sacrifice, at least occasional grain, wagons, institutionalized status in funeral ritual; sky-god ideology fits steppe landscape. [[yamnaya-culture]] meets late-PIE expectations "chronologically (the right time), geographically (the right place), materially (wagons, horses, animal sacrifices, tribal pastoralism), and linguistically (bounded by persistent frontiers)."

**Kurgan origins:** [[sredni-stog-culture|Sredni Stog]] abandoned communal DDII grave pits for individual graves; some with stone circles and low mounds — Anthony: probably the earliest kurgans in the steppes, reflecting more openly self-aggrandizing values.

### Migration sequence matching branch order (Anthony 2007)

1. West ~4200–3900 BCE (Suvorovo-type) — possible Pre-Anatolian detachment, before wheeled vehicles in steppes.
2. East ~3700–3300 BCE (Repin→[[afanasievo-culture|Afanasievo]]) — possible Pre-Tocharian.
3. Early Yamnaya spread ~3300 BCE scatters late PIE.
4. Western Yamnaya Danube/Carpathian migration ~3100–3000 BCE — possible Pre-Italic/Pre-Celtic incubation.
5. Contact with [[corded-ware-culture|Corded Ware]] ~2800–2600 BCE for northern IE.
6. Late Yamnaya/Poltavka→[[sintashta-culture|Sintashta]] ~2200–2000 BCE for Indo-Iranian precursors.

Expansion mechanisms: not primarily warfare for Yamnaya horizon spread within the steppes (minimal evidence); sharing mobility institutions made people potential allies. Outside the steppes: franchise-like [[elite-recruitment]] more than organized invasion — though initial penetration often involved migration and confrontation. Old Europe's end (4200–3900 BCE tell abandonments): Anthony's position is that immigrants from the steppes were somehow involved, with climate, erosion, and internecine warfare also possible primary causes.

### Evidence classes kept separate

Kurgan architecture, wagon burials, horse evidence, reconstructed PIE technical vocabulary, and (later) steppe genetic ancestry components are each **associated_with** — not identical to — IE speech. Anthony frames Yamnaya as a material horizon rapidly accepted by local cultures; speech associations (Anatolian with Suvorovo, Tocharian with Afanasievo, late PIE with Yamnaya) are theoretical correlations. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Culture≠language:** pots and kurgans are not speech; four-way non-identity is mandatory.
- **Gimbutas package:** invasion waves and goddess-patriarchy narrative often detached from the core chronological/homeland argument; Anthony himself narrows "Kurgan culture." Wave dates, ethnic-replacement scale, “peaceful OE,” suttee-as-IE-ideology, and linguistic=archaeological equation are **Gimbutas positions** (1991), not settled science — keep attributed. Source: [[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991]]. Dual-structure goddess narrative: [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]].
- **Pre-aDNA demography:** Anthony 2007 wrote before Haak/Allentoft/Reich-scale ancient DNA; genetic support for steppe migration into Europe largely post-dates the book and must be layered from later sources (e.g. [[reich-who-we-are-2018]]), not read back as Anthony's evidence.
- **Radiocarbon hygiene:** absolute cemetery chronologies depend on calibrated dates and Anthony's provisional freshwater-reservoir corrections for fish-heavy steppe diets. Gimbutas 1991 uses calibrated radiocarbon with tree-ring counting (her preface note) but Samara rests partly on typology without C-14 for key cemeteries (her own method note).
- **Branch-order archaeology:** migration-to-branch equations remain interpretive associations.
- **Violence vs franchise:** tension between raiding narratives (Old Europe, Männerbünde) and non-invasion expansion rhetoric.
- **Anatolian alternative:** Renfrew first-farming dispersal; Gray–Atkinson phylogenetic dates favoring Anatolia — Anthony rejects both as incompatible with wagon vocabulary and Neolithic diversity. Gimbutas 1991 independently rejects Renfrew’s 1987 proto-IE = Neolithic farmers equation.
