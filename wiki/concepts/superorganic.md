# Superorganic

An analytical concept central to [[kroeber-alfred-l|A. L. Kroeber]]'s culture theory: culture is **superorganic** and **superindividual** — carried only by human organisms, yet not explained from organic endowment alone; it has historical continuity, transmissibility, and conditioning force of its own. Primary vault formulation: *[[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963|Culture Patterns & Processes]]* (1948 *Anthropology* Chs. 1 and 7).

## Definition as Coined

In "What Culture Is" (§112), Kroeber insisted the term must not be misread as dualist metaphysics (attributed):

> "Superorganic" does not mean nonorganic, or free of organic influence and causation; nor does it mean that culture is an entity independent of organic life in the sense that some theologians might assert that there is a soul which is or can become independent of the living body. "Superorganic" means simply that when we consider culture we are dealing with something that is organic but which must also be viewed as something more than organic if it is to be fully intelligible to us.

Culture is superorganic and superindividual in that, though "carried, participated in, and produced by organic individuals, it is acquired; and it is acquired by learning." Properties he listed as hard to explain from organic composition of personalities alone include transmissibility, high variability, cumulativeness, value standards, and influence on individuals. Particular cultural manifestations, he argued, "find their primary significance in other cultural manifestations" and "cannot be specifically explained from the generic organic endowment of the human personality, even though cultural phenomena must always conform to the frame of this endowment."

Illustrations (attributed): **Roman Catholicism / Islam** — exist only through living adherents, yet hierarchy, beliefs, and ritual persist across generations of popes, bishops, and communicants and shape history; **English language** — faculty of speech is organic, but English as system is the "cumulative and joint product of millions of individuals for many centuries," not invented by any speaker, and not a spontaneous outflow of being human (else all would talk alike). A cultural fact is therefore "always a historical fact," and calling culture superorganic largely means it "yields more readily to historical interpretation than to organic or psychosomatic explanations."

He also used a **coral-reef** simile: polyps secrete the reef; the reef outlasts any generation and conditions where later polyps live — incomplete (culture is not only "dead precipitate"), but meant to convey cumulative social product plus ongoing conditioning.

In Ch. 1 (§5) he paired **super-hereditary / superorganic** language with the distinction between organic evolution and cultural change (the bird vs. the airplane): cultural "evolution" is not Darwinian heredity.

## Semantic History

- **Pre-Kroeber:** Spencerian and organic-analogy sociology treated society as organism; Kroeber redirected the "super-" prefix to **culture** rather than to society-as-body.
- **Kroeber 1917–1948:** essay "The Superorganic" (1917) and textbook codification make culture the level that anthropology owns against biology and individual psychology.
- **Later:** criticized as reifying culture, underplaying agency, power, and practice; partially absorbed into milder formulations (culture as system of meanings/practices not reducible to genes or individual motives). Related to, but not identical with, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s [[social-fact|social fact]] (constraint and exteriority) and later [[social-construction-of-reality|social construction]] programs.

## Operationalizations

Kroeber used the concept to **demarcate** sociocultural anthropology: the object is the interrelation of organic and historical-social factors, but the distinctive explanatory load for customs, languages, and institutions is historical-cultural, not race or individual psychology. Language≠race≠culture arguments (Ch. 6) rest on the same level distinction. Pattern analysis ([[culture-pattern]]), ethos/eidos ([[ethos-and-eidos]]), and process accounts (invention, diffusion, loss) are methods for studying the superorganic level.

## Applications

- Demarcation of anthropology among the sciences (Ch. 1).
- Culture definition against purely "social" labels (ants/bees have society without culture).
- Invention and simultaneous discovery as "superindividual" currents tied to pattern fruitfulness (Patterns / Processes chapters).
- Resistance to eugenic and racial explanations of cultural achievement.
- **The empirical demonstration.** *[[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944|Configurations of Culture Growth]]* (1944) is Kroeber's book-length case for the superorganic level: the [[genius-clustering|clustering of genius]] in time and space shows high achievement to be a property of [[growth-configuration|growth configurations]] of [[culture-pattern|culture patterns]], not of individual endowment ("Geniuses are the indicators of the realization of coherent pattern growths of cultural value").

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics charge **reification** (culture treated as an actor "above" persons), **ahistorical holism**, and neglect of **agency, inequality, and colonial power**. Kroeber's own hedges — culture always "conforms to the frame" of organic endowment; superorganic ≠ dualist soul — answer only part of the charge. Practice theory, political economy, and interpretive anthropology largely abandoned strong superorganicism while retaining the weaker claim that cultural forms are not reducible to individual organic properties. The concept remains a **historiography landmark** of Boasian culture theory more than a live research program under that name.
