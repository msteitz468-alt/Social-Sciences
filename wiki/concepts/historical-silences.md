# Historical Silences

## Definition as Coined

[[trouillot-michel-rolph|Michel-Rolph Trouillot]] (*[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]*, 1995) treats **silence** not as the simple absence of facts but as an **active product** of uneven power in historical production. Silences, he argues, enter at four overlapping moments:

1. **Fact creation** — the making of **sources**
2. **Fact assembly** — the making of **archives**
3. **Fact retrieval** — the making of **narratives**
4. **Retrospective significance** — the making of history “in the final instance”

> “Silences enter the process of historical production at four crucial moments: the moment of fact creation (the making of sources); the moment of fact assembly (the making of archives); the moment of fact retrieval (the making of narratives); and the moment of retrospective significance (the making of history in the final instance).” (Trouillot 1995)

These moments are **heuristic tools**, not a realistic pipeline for any single narrative. Each narrative is “a particular bundle of silences”; redress requires matching strategy to mixture. Silence is **transitive** — someone silences — and **constitutive**: absence is part of how history is produced, not a defect external to it.

## Semantic History

- **Trouillot 1981–95:** Essays and International Roundtable papers (History and Anthropology) feed the book-length formulation.
- **Carby 2015 foreword:** Popularizes the “forensic analysis of the four moments” for interdisciplinary graduate training (field / archive / text as relations of power).
- **Adjacent but non-identical vocabularies:** [[subaltern]] speech limits (Spivak/Guha); [[people-without-history]] (Wolf’s ironic title); archival absences in postcolonial studies. Trouillot’s specificity is the **processual four-moment map** plus case strategies that differ by source poverty, unthinkability, or abundance.

Do not equate “silence” with “missing documents only.” Trouillot’s Ch.3 case shows silences already inscribed when an event is [[unthinkable-history|unthinkable]]; Ch.4 shows silences amid **surfeit** of sources.

## Operationalizations

| Moment | What is made | Power question (Trouillot, attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| Sources | Traces, notes, bodies, buildings | Which occurrences are engraved? Facts are never meaningless or equal |
| Archives | Assemblies of sources (guild, museums, guides, state collections) | Who collects, classes, preserves? |
| Narratives | Stories retrieved and emplotted | Whose facts enter which plots? |
| Retrospective significance | “History” as public past | What gets commemorated, taught, packaged? |

**Three case strategies** in the book (attributed):

| Chapter | Problem type | Method |
|---|---|---|
| 2 Sans Souci | Sparse but available evidence; layered naming | Reposition evidence to generate alternative narrative |
| 3 Haitian Revolution | Unthinkable as it happened; no “new facts” | Make silences speak by juxtaposing climate of times, specialist histories, world histories |
| 4 Columbus | Abundance + forged consensus | Show silences in interstices of competing appropriations |

## Critiques and Limitations

- The four moments can be reified into a checklist; Trouillot stresses overlap in real time.
- “Silence” can inflate metaphorically; force of the concept is **uneven power in production**, not any gap.
- Reception sometimes reduces the book to “history is written by winners” — Trouillot rejects both naive winner-history and pure constructivism ([[historicity-1-and-2]]).
