# Bricolage

## Definition as Coined

**Bricolage** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] term (*[[levi-strauss-savage-mind-1962|The Savage Mind]]*, 1962) for a mode of thought and production that "makes do with 'whatever is at hand'." The French *bricoleur* — a handyman working with a closed, heterogeneous stock of leftover materials — is opposed to the **engineer**, who procures materials and tools designed for a specific project. "Mythical thought is... a kind of intellectual bricolage": it builds structures "by using the remains and debris of events," recombining a finite repertoire because "it has nothing else at its disposal."

## Semantic History

The verb *bricoler* once described a deviant, rebounding movement (a ball in billiards, a swerving horse). Lévi-Strauss made it a technical contrast with engineering to illuminate the [[science-of-the-concrete]]: "the engineer works by means of concepts and the bricoleur by means of signs." Concepts aim to be "wholly transparent with respect to reality"; signs (Peirce: they "address somebody") incorporate human culture into reality. The distinction is not absolute — even the scientist works from a historically bounded prior stock — but "the engineer is always trying to... go beyond the constraints imposed by a particular state of civilization while the bricoleur... always remains within them."

## Operationalizations

Identified by: a closed instrumental set of pre-constrained, "may-always-come-in-handy" elements; each element an "operator" usable across a range of tasks of the same type; every choice reorganizing the whole system (the elements form a system where altering one affects all); building structures out of events (where science creates events out of structures). **Art** lies half-way between science and bricolage; the small-scale model / miniature reverses the normal cognitive order (whole before parts).

## Applications

The master image for how [[myth]] and "savage" thought work — recombining a bequeathed stock of signs into ever-new structures ("mythological worlds have been built up, only to be shattered again, and... new worlds... built from the fragments," Boas, quoted). The concept escaped anthropology into cultural studies, art, design, education, and software ("bricolage programming"), usually naming improvisation from available materials.

## Critiques and Limitations

Bricolage is a suggestive analogy rather than a measured mechanism; its opposition to the "engineer" idealizes both poles (later science studies stress that engineers, too, improvise). Record it as a heuristic of [[structuralism]], attributed to Lévi-Strauss, whose wide later borrowing has diluted its original technical sense (thought that works by signs within a closed set, tied to the [[science-of-the-concrete]]).
