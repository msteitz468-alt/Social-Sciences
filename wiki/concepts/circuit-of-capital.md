## Definition as Coined

In [[marx-capital-volume-2-1885]], Marx argues that industrial capital passes through three successive stages in a repeating cycle: it is advanced as money (M) to buy commodities — means of production and labour-power (M-C, which itself breaks into M-L for labour-power and M-mp for means of production); those commodities are consumed productively in the production process (P), yielding a new commodity of greater value (C′); and that commodity is sold, reconverting it into money of greater value than was originally advanced (C′-M′). Marx writes the whole movement as M-C...P...C′-M′, where the dots mark that circulation is interrupted by production and the primes (C′, M′) denote value increased by surplus-value. He states that Volume 1 of *Capital* treated the buying and selling stages only insofar as needed to understand production, and that Volume 2 is devoted to investigating "the forms themselves."

Marx argues that the same total movement can equally be described starting from productive capital — P...C′-M′-C...P, "the circuit of productive capital" — or from commodity capital — C′-M′-C...P...C′, "the circuit of commodity capital." These are not three different processes but three ways of cutting into a single continuous movement, each foregrounding a different moment as the analytical starting point.

## Semantic History

Marx presents the circuit concept as his own theoretical apparatus, built by generalizing and correcting earlier partial analyses. He credits the Physiocrats — citing [[quesnay-francois]] and Turgot specifically — with being the first to analyze production, rather than mere circulation, as the ground of wealth, though restricted to agriculture (their "avances" and "reprises"). Marx argues Quesnay's *Tableau économique* is implicitly organized around the commodity-capital circuit form (C′...C′), and praises Quesnay's "great discernment" in choosing that form over the money-capital form (M...M′) fixed on by the Mercantile System, or the productive-capital form (P...P) later adopted by classical political economy.

Marx explicitly maps each of the three circuit forms onto a historical school's blind spot: the Mercantile System (and its precursor, the Monetary/Bullionist System), which he criticizes via a quoted passage from D'Avenant's 1697 *Essay on the East-India Trade*, mistook the money-form of the circuit (M-C...C′-M′) for capital's essence, generating what Marx calls an "illusory character" because it emphasizes the money-form of valorization rather than valorization itself. Classical political economy, by contrast, is said to have considered the circuit "in the form in which the classical economists have considered the circuit of industrial capital" — namely as P...C-M′.M-C{L,mp}...P(P′), the productive-capital form — which let it ignore the specifically capitalist character of production and treat production simply as producing-as-much-as-possible. Marx credits Quesnay's C′...C′ form with grounding the analysis of social reproduction, since consumption (both productive and individual) is built into it as a precondition — Marx identifies this as the conceptual bridge to the reproduction schemas developed in Part Three of the volume; see [[reproduction-schemas]].

Marx also frames the circuit-of-productive-capital form as an implicit critique of the money-capital form: because in Form II money appears merely as a transient shape the capital sheds, rather than as the palpable driving purpose it appeared to be in Form I, Marx's manuscript carries a note "Against Tooke" at this point, and he elsewhere explicitly criticizes the economist Thomas Tooke for failing to distinguish money as mere means of circulation from money as capital.

## Operationalizations

Marx works the three circuit forms through a running numerical example (a spinning mill: 50 workers, £50 weekly wages, £372 on means of production, 10,000 lb of yarn worth £500, of which £78 is surplus-value) to show that the c:v:s ratio holds at any level of division of the commodity mass, down to a single pound of yarn.

The central analytical move — "the unity of the three circuits" — is Marx's claim that in reality "each individual industrial capital is involved in all three circuits at the same time": different fractions of a given capital simultaneously occupy money-capital, productive-capital, and commodity-capital form, so the three circuit-forms are not sequential options but co-present aspects of a continuously reproducing capital. Marx states that the continuity of production (capital's simultaneous "coexistence" in all phases) is only possible as the result of, and simultaneously as the condition for, the successive movement of different capital-portions through the stages — coexistence and succession are mutually determining. From this Marx draws the methodological claim that capital "does not just comprise class relations... It is a movement, a circulatory process through different stages... Hence it can only be grasped as a movement, and not as a static thing." He criticizes those who treat the "autonomization" of value as "a mere abstraction," arguing that the movement of industrial capital literally enacts this abstraction in practice.

## Applications

Marx uses the circuit concept to give an early statement of crisis theory as circulation-interruption: "If capital comes to a standstill in the first phase, M-C, money capital forms into a hoard; if this happens in the production phase, the means of production cease to function, and labour-power remains unoccupied; if in the last phase, C′-M′, unsaleable stocks of commodities obstruct the flow of circulation." He extends this in the discussion of Form II, arguing that because an individual capitalist's circuit is formally complete as soon as C′ is sold (even to a merchant, not a final consumer), commodities can pile up unsold "in the hands of retail traders" while production and apparent demand continue expanding — "one stream of commodities now follows another... it finally emerges that the earlier stream had only seemed to be swallowed up by consumption... At this point the crisis breaks out." Marx states crisis first appears not as a direct fall in consumer demand but "in a decline in the number of exchanges of capital for capital, in the reproduction process of capital." He notes fixed capital (machinery, buildings) transfers value to the product only gradually across many repetitions of production, which modifies the simple circuit picture and is treated at length under [[turnover-of-capital]].

Marx also uses Circuit III (C′-M′-C...P...C′) to argue that because it presupposes the existence of other industrial capitals' output as its own input commodities, it "demands to be considered... at the same time as the form of motion of the sum of individual capitals, i.e. of the total social capital of the capitalist class" — the bridge into the [[reproduction-schemas]] analysis of Volume 2 Part Three.

## Critiques and Limitations

Marx's own presentation is itself polemical throughout: the circuit concept is constructed in explicit opposition to the Mercantile System (money-form fixation), to classical political economy (productive-capital-form fixation, ignoring the specifically capitalist character of the process), and to what he calls "vulgar economists," whom he accuses of depicting capitalist production as mere symmetric commodity exchange (C-M-C, c-m-c) that erases the fact that the surplus product "has not cost the capitalist anything" but is "the embodiment of surplus labour."

A live interpretive dispute recorded within the source itself: Marx quotes and rejects Samuel Bailey's argument (from Bailey's 1825 *A Critical Dissertation on the Nature, Measures, and Causes of Value*) that value is only "a relation between contemporary commodities," denying that values at different points in time are comparable. Marx argues Bailey conflates exchange-value (a form) with value itself, and that capital value must be "compared with itself in the different phases of its circuit," which occur in succession rather than simultaneity — an editorial footnote in the source notes Bailey as a "vulgar economist" who nonetheless exposed contradictions in Ricardian theory. This exchange is a candidate for a `debates/` page on the temporality of value in Marx versus Ricardian/Bailey value theory, not yet built.

Marx's discussion of industrial capital's circulation "cutting across the commodity circulation of the most varied modes of social production" — listing slave production, peasant production (Chinese, Indian ryots), communal production (Dutch East Indies), and what the text terms "half-savage hunting peoples" as sources of commodities entering the capitalist circuit — reproduces the racialized, civilizational hierarchy language characteristic of 1870s European political economy; this is source content reproduced for the historical record, not wiki-voice characterization, and is flagged here per this wiki's reflexivity standard rather than smoothed over.
