# Analogism

## Definition as Coined

In *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]* (esp. Ch.9–11), [[descola-philippe|Descola]] defines **analogism** as the [[modes-of-identification|mode of identification]] that posits **discontinuity of both interiorities and physicalities** among existing beings — a world of singularities — then recomposes order through webs of **analogy, correspondence, pairing, hierarchy, and sacrifice**.

Unlike [[animism]] (shared souls, different bodies) or [[naturalism-ontology|naturalism]] (shared matter, different minds), analogism multiplies differences and links them by resemblance of relations (microcosm/macrocosm, signatures, humors, destinies).

## Semantic History

Descola draws the Great Chain of Being (Lovejoy), Renaissance signatures (Foucault), classical Chinese correlative cosmology (Granet — noted but not principal case), Nahua component personhood (López Austin), and West African systems (Bambara, Dogon, Samo, Héritier on Samo). "Analogism" is his coinage for a combinatorial cell, not a native term.

## Operationalizations

- Double difference on the two axes.
- Order via: hierarchical encompassment; sacrificial mediation; nagual/tona-type co-essences; humoral and directional correspondences.
- Collective morphology: **one** mixed hierarchical cosmos-society (not hybrid segments like totemism) — e.g. Chipaya and Andean examples in Ch.11.
- Epistemology of "signatures of things" (Ch.12): reading resemblances as real connections.

## Applications

Premodern Europe and Christian-Platonic chains of being; Nahua/Maya component ontologies; West African sacrificial and classificatory systems; Descola's narrative of transition from animist gift to protective analogism (Caribou-Man → Lord Bull) in Siberian/Mongol pastoral histories (Ch.15).

## Critiques and Limitations

Breadth of "analogism" may lump historically distinct systems; China is acknowledged but underdeveloped in BNC relative to Mexico. Sacrifice "co-distributed" with analogism is a strong claim — animist offerings (bear, tapir) are argued to be structurally different.
