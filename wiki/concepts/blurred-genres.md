## Definition as Coined

In "Blurred Genres: The Refiguration of Social Thought" (opening essay of *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983; first delivered 1980), [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] argues (attributed) that three developments are "true together": there has been "an enormous amount of genre mixing in intellectual life"; social scientists are turning "from a laws and instances ideal of explanation toward a cases and interpretations one"; and analogies drawn from the humanities are coming to play in social understanding the role analogies from crafts and technology long played for the physical sciences. Geertz names this convergence **the refiguration of social thought**.

Geertz holds that interpretive explanation "is a form of explanation, not just exalted glossography": it issues not in laws (Boyle's), forces (Volta's), or mechanisms (Darwin's) but in *constructions* (Burckhardt's, Weber's, Freud's) — "systematic unpackings of the conceptual world in which condottiere, Calvinists, or paranoids live." Theory, he argues, "moves mainly by analogy, a 'seeing-as' comprehension of the less intelligible by the more... What the lever did for physics, the chess move promises to do for sociology."

## Semantic History

The essay diagnoses genre-blurring across then-current work — Geertz's roster includes philosophy as literary criticism (Cavell), science as belles-lettres (Lewis Thomas, Eiseley), history as equations and tables (Fogel and Engerman, Le Roy Ladurie), theoretical treatise as travelogue ([[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]), ideology as historiography (Edward Said), epistemology as political tract (Feyerabend). He argues the grand curricular rubrics ("Natural Science," "Social Science," "The Humanities"), read as "a borders-and-territories map," obscure what scholars actually do.

Geertz sorts the humanities analogies feeding social theory into three, each illuminating and obscuring different subject matter:

| Analogy | Sources / exemplars | What it captures | Tends toward |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Game** ("life is a game") | Wittgenstein's language games; Huizinga; von Neumann–Morgenstern game theory; [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] as chief exemplar | conduct as rule-governed, strategic, self-rewarding ("society as a grand plurality of accepted conventions") | face-to-face interaction |
| **Drama** ("life is a stage") | ritual theory (Harrison, Fergusson) and Kenneth Burke's "symbolic action"/dramatism; [[turner-victor|Turner]]'s [[social-drama]] as ritual wing | the temporal and collective dimensions of action; theatre–religion (communion) vs. theatre–rhetoric (persuasion) affinities | collective intensities, carnivals, insurrections |
| **Text** ("life is a text") | Paul Ricoeur's "inscription"; Alton Becker on Javanese *wayang* as text-building | the "broadest, most venturesome, and least well developed"; fixation of meaning that outlasts the event | imaginative forms — jokes, proverbs, popular arts |

Geertz reads the text analogy — meaning "inscribed" and so surviving the evanescent act — as the deep warrant for his own [[thick-description]] program, and faults ritual theory for making "vividly disparate matters look drably homogeneous."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurable construct. The refiguration is offered as a description of a change in explanatory taste — challenging three "mainstream" assumptions Geertz names: the strict theory/data separation ("the 'brute fact' idea"), the ideal of a formal vocabulary purged of subjective reference ("the 'ideal language' idea"), and claims to moral neutrality ("the 'God's truth' idea"). What is wanted, he argues, is explanation of "action to its sense" rather than "behavior to its determinants": "the anatomization of thought is wanted, not the manipulation of behavior."

## Applications

The blurred-genres diagnosis frames the rest of *Local Knowledge*: the "cultural systems" essays ([[common-sense]], [[art-as-a-cultural-system]]), the reframing of understanding as reading symbolic forms ([[experience-near-experience-distant]]), and the treatment of law as "a species of social imagination" ([[local-knowledge]]). It is a charter statement of the "interpretive turn" across the human sciences and a companion to the [[thick-description]] manifesto of *[[geertz-interpretation-of-cultures-1973|The Interpretation of Cultures]]*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics of [[interpretive-anthropology]] read the humanities-analogy program as licensing under-determined, unfalsifiable readings and as abandoning causal and predictive ambition (the standing charge against Geertz that culture "explained" becomes culture merely "glossed"). Geertz's own guard is the pairing of over- and under-interpretation — "telling stories about people only a professor can believe" versus reducing them to "ordinary chaps out... for money, sex, status, and power." The diagnosis is itself period-bound: it names a 1970s–80s moment in the disciplines, and reads in part as advocacy for the turn it describes.
