# Lamarckian Inheritance (in Social Science)

## Definition as Coined

Lamarck (1809) held that an organism's behavioral responses to environmental needs produce
structural modifications, and that these acquired modifications are then inherited:
"actions becoming habitual have occasioned the development of the organs which execute
them," and such modifications are "preserved by reproduction to the new individuals which
arise." As [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] notes (drawing on Conway Zirkle), belief in the
heritability of acquired characters was "held almost universally for well over two thousand
years" and was still widespread when Darwin's *Origin* appeared; only after August
Weismann's germ-plasm attack of the 1880s did its American defenders form "a clearly
self-conscious group: the neo-Lamarckians."

## Semantic History

*(Attributed — Stocking, "Lamarckianism in American Social Science, 1890–1915," in
*[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race, Culture, and Evolution]]*, essay 10.)*

Stocking's central claim is that the Lamarckian doctrine, more than "Social Darwinism,"
"provided one of the last theoretical links between biological and social theory" in
American social science between 1890 and 1915. Unilinear [[social-evolutionism]] depended on
it: Comte called inheritance of acquired characters an "incontestable principle," Spencer
was "the father of neo-Lamarckian biology" (and debated Weismann in 1893), and
[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s evolutionary scheme "is given meaning only in its terms."
The doctrine let acquired habits become hereditary "race" traits — so that "race" and
"civilization" could be silently interchanged. The Washington
school under John Wesley Powell (the Bureau of American Ethnology) were "both unilinear
cultural evolutionists and Lamarckians"; G. Stanley Hall's recapitulationism was
"explicitly Lamarckian."

Its biological collapse was gradual, not sudden — from Weismann's 1880s attack to the
Mendelian consensus took roughly two decades, with T. H. Morgan still speaking of
"suspended judgment" in 1909; Lamarckianism "died not with a bang but a whimper." Its
demise was consequential for social science: as Stocking argues via
[[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]]'s "The Superorganic" (1917) and its critique of Gustave Le
Bon, once the biological legitimacy of inheritance-by-acquirement was gone, turning "race"
into "culture" required only "the substitution of a word" — "for 'race' read 'culture' or
'civilization,' for 'racial heredity' read 'cultural heritage,' and the change had taken
place." The concept is thus, in Stocking's account, the hinge on which the modern
[[culture]] concept turned.

## Operationalizations

In period social science the doctrine operationalized group difference as heritable
adaptation: Frank Baker (1890) held "the adaptations of each individual are transmitted to
its offspring"; [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s targets Ripley and Bean explained specific "racial"
physiologies (Jewish lung capacity, the "Jewish nose") as acquired characters become
hereditary. Stocking stresses an analytical point often missed: **Lamarckian
environmentalism was not the opposite of racism** — because it confused social and physical
heredity, environmentalists (e.g., Carlos Closson) could be as racially deterministic as
strict "Weismannist" hereditarians. Its racial import "depended in part on the tense of its
application": Ward's egalitarian version held environment could still change racial traits,
where Brinton held races fixed at a past "plastic stage." These are documented period
positions, recorded as attributed, not as findings.

## Applications

Central to the vault's account of the [[race]]/[[culture]] separation and to the history of
[[social-evolutionism]] and [[historical-particularism]]; the necessary background to the
Boasian achievement (see [[boas-franz]], [[culture]]). James Mark Baldwin's "organic
selection" (the Baldwin effect, 1895–97) is recorded here as a sophisticated attempt to
preserve the appearance of inherited adaptation without literal Lamarckism.

## Critiques and Limitations

The doctrine is biologically obsolete (superseded by Weismannian/Mendelian and later
molecular genetics; do not state its mechanism in wiki voice). Its analytical interest is
historical: it explains why nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century "race" so readily
absorbed what would now be called "culture," and why the two had to be disentangled. Later
epigenetic phenomena are a distinct modern topic and should not be conflated with the
period doctrine described here.
