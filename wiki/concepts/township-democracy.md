# Township Democracy (Communal Self-Government)

## Definition as Coined

[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] made the New England **township** (*commune*) the
structural and historical origin of American liberty. In America, he argued, political
organization built *upward*: "the organization of the township preceded that of the county,
the county that of the state, the state that of the Union" — the inverse of the French
pattern where the center creates and staffs the locality. The township is sovereign over
strictly local matters and subordinate to the state only in matters of common concern; the
town, not the state, executes most public business.

He described the concrete form: a town of 2,000–3,000 governed directly by **town meeting**
(no representative council), administered through roughly nineteen annually elected offices
(selectmen, assessors, collectors, constable, clerk, treasurer, overseers of the poor,
school and road officers), most paid per service. He contrasted this multiplicity with the
single French mayor, treating the diffusion of small offices as the engine of civic
engagement. Township liberty, he warned, is historically rare and fragile — it "arises
almost secretly," needs long consolidation by custom, and is readily crushed by centralizing
power if not deeply rooted in habit.

## Semantic History

Tocqueville's township is the ancestor of a durable theme: local self-government as the
"school of liberty," participatory democracy, and subsidiarity. It connects to his broader
distinction between **governmental** centralization (control of national concerns — which
America had) and **administrative** centralization (control of local concerns — which
America lacked), the latter being what he thought decisive for freedom.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; described institutionally from the New England case (chiefly
Massachusetts/Connecticut), then generalized, with Tocqueville himself noting that township
vigor weakened moving south and west as the county absorbed administration.

## Applications

Township self-government is, for Tocqueville, both an expression of
[[sovereignty-of-the-people|popular sovereignty]] and a practical restraint on the
[[tyranny-of-the-majority|majority]]: decentralized administration means "municipal bodies
and county administrations form so many hidden reefs which hold back... the flood of the
people's will." It is continuous with his account of [[voluntary-association]] as the civic
infrastructure of a free democracy.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Idealization: the New England town is a most-favorable case, generalized to "America"
  despite Tocqueville's own note of its regional decline.
- The upward-building historical narrative is a stylized reconstruction serving his
  point-of-departure thesis.
