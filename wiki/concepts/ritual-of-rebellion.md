# Ritual of Rebellion

> Analytical concept associated with [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]], developed in his Frazer Lecture (1952) and popularized in *[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict in Africa]]* (Lecture V, “The Licence in Ritual”). Study: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Gluckman.)* **Rituals of rebellion** are ceremonies **organized to exhibit rebellion and protest** — gender role reversal, temporary kings of low status, subjects insulting and threatening kings, licensed obscenity — that are nonetheless **intended and believed to preserve and strengthen** the established order (often for fertility, first-fruits, rain, or national unity). The performers are **rebels, never revolutionaries**: they attack incumbents or dramatize tensions without seeking to alter the nature of offices or ultimate values, which remain on a mystical plane beyond question.

Gluckman places the type within his series problem: custom emphasizes conflicts in certain ranges of relationship yet establishes cohesion in wider society or over longer time. European analogues (officers waiting on men at Christmas) and Frazer’s *Golden Bough* temporary-king and gender-reversal material supply comparative frames.

### *Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa* (1963), Essay III — the Frazer Lecture text

*(Attributed — Gluckman, "Rituals of Rebellion in South-East Africa," Frazer Lecture, delivered University of Glasgow 1952, published 1954; reprinted in *[[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963|Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa]]*.)* Gluckman frames the lecture as a direct engagement with [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]'s *Golden Bough* — opening from Frazer's priest-king of Nemi and dying-and-resurrected agricultural gods (Adonis, Tammuz, Osiris, Dionysus) — crediting Frazer's insight linking agricultural ceremony to political process but arguing Frazer's "intellectualist" comparative method, lacking modern fieldwork, could not analyze the social roles and groups actually enacting the ritual; Gluckman frames his own sociological reading as extending, not refuting, Frazer.

Core thesis, verbatim: "these ritual rebellions proceed within an established and sacred traditional system, in which there is dispute about particular distributions of power, and not about the structure of the system itself. This allows for **instituted protest**, and in complex ways renews the unity of the system." Gluckman calls such stable-in-structure orders **"repetitive"** social systems (as against developing/revolutionary ones), and argues that in them a rebel/pretender must himself be a legitimate member of the royal family, so that rebellion against a bad king is waged in defence of the institution of kingship itself.

Case material: the **Zulu Nomkubulwana** rite (women's agricultural ceremony honoring the goddess "Princess of Heaven" — licensed transvestism, girls herding/milking cattle normally taboo to them, "Bacchantic" behaviour by married women, planting of a neglected symbolic garden while men hide) — a rite Gluckman never personally observed in performance; by his own 1937 Zululand fieldwork it had lapsed, and elderly informants blamed its neglect for poor harvests. The **Swazi incwala** (via Hilda Kuper's *An African Aristocracy*, 1947): regiments and the royal clan chant songs of hatred and rejection ("You hate the child king," "King, they hate thee") alternating with songs of triumph and loyalty, the king undergoes seclusion and a frenzied "Silo" (wild monster) dance, and the ceremony closes with gourd-casting and burning before feasting and license.

Gluckman explicitly notes the **Lozi (Barotse) negative case**: rituals of rebellion are largely absent among the Lozi, whom he attributes this to their elaborate secular/institutional (judicial-council) channels for the same tensions, plus the absence of a hunger period and of comparable first-fruits ceremonies — evidence that the ritual form is one solution among others to a structural problem, not a universal necessity.

## Semantic History

- **Hoernlé / early seed:** Gluckman traces the key idea to work with A. W. Hoernlé (1931) on Zulu women’s Nomkubulwana rites.
- **Frazer Lecture 1952:** *Rituals of Rebellion in South-East Africa* — monographic statement of the type.
- **Custom and Conflict (1955/56):** public, compressed formulation as Lecture V in the six-lecture cohesion-through-conflict series.
- **Politics, Law and Ritual (1965):** embeds the type in Ch. IV (state/civil strife: **rebellion vs revolution** — oust incumbents, preserve offices; frailty of authority; mystical value of kingship not of the king as person) and Ch. VI (conflict in ritual; ceremonial vs ritual; negative cases). Source: [[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965]].
- **Afterlife:** Teaching staple of political and ritual anthropology; reworked by later ritual theory (e.g. Turner’s liminality — related but not identical); feminist and postcolonial critiques question how far “licence” serves hierarchy.
- **Turner's recasting:** In *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (ch. 5),
  [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] analyzes the same phenomena — the Zulu Nomkubulwana, the
  Ashanti Apo, Holi — as rituals of **status reversal** and reads them not merely as
  safety-valves reaffirming a fixed order but as a dialectic that "purges structure by
  [[communitas]]" and regenerates it. (Turner cites Gluckman's 1954 Nomkubulwana analysis
  directly but uses "status reversal," not the phrase "ritual of rebellion," in that chapter.)
  See [[liminality]], [[communitas]].

## Operationalizations

| Feature | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Licensed protest | Insult, obscenity, role reversal, temporary elevation of low status |
| Order-blessing claim | Belief that performance brings prosperity, rain, unity, successful kingship |
| Rebel not revolutionary | Same offices/structure remain; ultimate values not open to overthrow |
| Strong-order condition | Works where institutions are accepted; weak/irreconcilable conflicts get other channels (e.g. Barotse secular councils) |

**Type cases in Gluckman 1955/56:** Zulu Nomkubulwana agricultural rites (women as men, lewd songs, men hide); Swazi/Zulu national “drama of kingship”; installation and circumcision rites; Tallensi complementary ritual groupings (via Fortes).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Functionalist charge:** protest always serves order — may underplay genuine subversion and women’s/subjects’ interests.
- **Scope:** Gluckman himself limits the analysis to accepted orders without revolutionaries; industrial and revolutionary contexts differ.
- **Not all ritual:** many rituals lack licensed protest; the type is selective.
- **Historical change:** Zulu agricultural rites Gluckman describes were no longer performed by his 1930s fieldwork — reconstruction and colonial-era conditions matter.
- **[POSITION — Wilson vs. Gluckman]** Monica Wilson's Frazer Lecture "Divine Kings and 'the Breath of Men'" (1959) argued that Gluckman's ritual-of-rebellion reading of Nyakyusa/Ngonde cleansing rites is better explained as ritualized **"confession"** (a formal admission-and-reconciliation of anger) than as "rebellion." Gluckman (in *Order and Rebellion*) rejects the substitution as a "serious logical fallacy" — a narrow process like confession cannot explain a complex, month-long national ceremony — and argues the readings are compatible once it is clear he analyzes the ritual's political *function*, while Wilson analyzes its psychological *content*; he states he does not think "we are at odds" once the distinction is clarified.
- **[POSITION — Reay vs. Gluckman]** Marie Reay (*Oceania*, 1959) charged that Gluckman's analysis was covertly psychological. Gluckman rebuts this as arising from a textual misquotation — readers silently inserting "out" after his verb "act," turning a sociological claim ("act rebellion") into a psychological one ("act out rebellion") — and states he will use "perform" instead of "act" in future writing to avoid the ambiguity.
- **G. D. Gibson**'s review (*American Anthropologist*, 1955) welcomed the lecture as opening the door to psychoanalytic follow-up on catharsis-by-ritual-rebellion; Gluckman accepted this as complementary to, not a threat to, his sociological analysis.
