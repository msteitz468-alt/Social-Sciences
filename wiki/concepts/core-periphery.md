# Core–Periphery

## Definition as Coined

**Core–periphery** names a relational structure of the world-economy: zones that control accumulation, technology, and state capacity (core) versus zones whose accumulation is externally constrained (periphery). Wallerstein (2004) credits Prebisch and the UN Economic Commission for Latin America with launching the conceptual language; world-systems analysis makes it a permanent structural feature, not a temporary lag.

### Wallerstein's own definitions (primary — 1974 / 2004)

The primary sources sharpen this into two complementary statements (attributed). In *[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|WSA: An Introduction]]* (2004) core–periphery is **relational and process-based**: "Core-periphery is a relational concept. What we mean by core-periphery is the degree of profitability of the production processes" — core-like processes being those "controlled by quasi-monopolies," peripheral processes "those that are truly competitive," with a "constant flow of surplus-value" from the latter to the former ([[unequal-exchange]]). Because core-like and peripheral *processes* cluster in different states, one speaks loosely of "core states" and "peripheral areas," but the underlying relation is between production processes, not places, and a given process migrates from core-like to peripheral over decades as its quasi-monopoly decays.

In *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]* (1974) the emphasis is on **state strength**: "World-economies then are divided into core-states and peripheral areas. I do not say peripheral states because one characteristic of a peripheral area is that the indigenous state is weak, ranging from its nonexistence (that is, a colonial situation) to one with a low degree of autonomy (that is, a neo-colonial situation)." Wallerstein argues a world-economy structurally *requires* unevenly strong states (if all were equally strong, the world division of labor would be blocked; if none were strong, capitalists could not secure property and monopolies), with a self-reinforcing "tipping mechanism" (tax revenue → bigger bureaucracy/army → more tax revenue) that turns small initial gaps into large ones. The periphery is distinguished from the [[external-arena|external arena]] (regions trading only in luxury "preciosities," outside the division of labor).

## Semantic History

From ECLA structuralism and dependency into Wallersteinian historical systems. Everyday IR/geography uses ("core Europe") often lose the relational/exploitative sense. [[amin-samir|Samir Amin]] (2004) defines peripheries negatively (not-centers) and rejects "catch-up" as the system's normal path under current monopolies of the triad.

## Operationalizations

Trade composition; wage levels; state strength; technological monopolies (Amin's "five monopolies" of the triad — attributed). Boundaries of core/periphery/semiperiphery are fuzzy and historically mobile — a feature of the theory, not a measurement bug, but a validity challenge.

## Applications

Polarization theses; imperialism as permanent (Amin); active vs marginalized peripheries after Bandung; critiques of [[modernization-theory]].

### Amin 2004 elaborations (attributed)

Centers = regions with national bourgeois hegemony and state capacity to control accumulation (within external constraints they can partly shape). Peripheries = regions that do **not** control local accumulation (negative definition); not synonymous with precapitalist or stagnant. Post-Bandung typology: active peripheries (own project/strategy: e.g. East Asia, some Latin America/India) vs marginalized/passive peripheries (scripts written externally). **Marginalization is a false concept** — the issue is *mode* of integration, not degree. Five monopolies of the triad redefine the global law of value so industrialization alone does not end peripheral status.

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement disputes; alleged economic reduction; mobility of East Asian cases used both to refute and to refine the model (Amin treats Korea/Taiwan as special geostrategic exceptions).
