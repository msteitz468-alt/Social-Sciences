# Othermothers

## Definition as Coined

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]], drawing on Troester 1984.) Collins
distinguishes **bloodmothers** (biological mothers) from **othermothers** — women who
"assist bloodmothers by sharing mothering responsibilities" and who, she argues, "have
traditionally been central to the institution of Black motherhood." Othermothers include
grandmothers, sisters, aunts, cousins, neighbors, and non-biological **"fictive kin"**
incorporated into family roles. Collins presents these arrangements as a traditional,
African-derived, and functionally adaptive feature of **women-centered** Black
extended-family networks — where "women-centered" denotes the centrality of women, not the
absence of men.

**Community othermothers** extend mothering to non-kin children of the wider Black
community ("our children"), which Collins links to Black women's political and community
activism. The related concept **motherwork** names the whole constellation of
bloodmother/othermother/community-othermother activity, through which — she argues —
political consciousness emerges from everyday lived experience and the gendered
public/private division is challenged.

## Semantic History

The terms appear in *Black Feminist Thought* (1990; 2nd ed. 2000, Ch. 8) and recur in
Collins's account of activism (Ch. 9) as the basis of "activist mothering" (Nancy Naples
1991, 1996). Collins situates the practice against the capitalist "private property" model
of children embedded in the "traditional family ideal."

## Operationalizations

Documented ethnographically and through oral history and literature (Ella Baker via
Cantarow 1980; Leith Mullings's Harlem research 1997; Bebe Moore Campbell 1989) rather than
by survey measures. Mullings found Black women activate distinct, overlapping kin/social
networks for reproduction vs. emotional and economic support.

## Applications

Underpins Collins's argument that motherhood can be a **symbol of power** (e.g., Mamie Till
Bradley's open-casket decision for her murdered son Emmett Till, 1955) and a site of
[[self-definition|self-definition]] — while remaining, as an *institution*, "fundamentally
contradictory" (a site of exploited labor as well as empowerment).

## Critiques and Limitations

Collins records counter-evidence that resists romanticizing these networks: Elaine Bell
Kaplan's (1997) study of 32 Black teenage mothers found extended kin support often
*failed* — many adult mothers were unsupportive, and only 4 of 32 relied on family, most
relying on friends — contradicting sociological assumptions about the reliable Black
extended family. The related **"superstrong Black mother"** controlling image (see
[[controlling-images]]) shows how a positive account of othermothering can be turned into a
mechanism of self-sacrifice and silence. Whether "maternal politics" counts as feminism
proper is a live dispute (Julia Wells 1998 vs. Collins, Gilkes, and Naples), recorded here
as a position, not adjudicated.
