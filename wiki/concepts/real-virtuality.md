# Real Virtuality (Culture of Real Virtuality)

Castells’s concept for the cultural form of the [[network-society|network society]]: not a separate “virtual world,” but a **culture of real virtuality** in which digitized multimedia networks integrate communication into a shared symbolic environment. Canonical: *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Ch. 5; 2010 preface updates via mass self-communication and Web 2.0.

## Definition as Coined

Castells (attributed): what is historically specific is “not its inducement of virtual reality but the construction of *real virtuality*.” A system generating real virtuality is one in which “reality itself (that is, people’s material/symbolic existence) is entirely captured, fully immersed in a virtual image setting… in which appearances are not just on the screen through which experience is communicated, but they become the experience.” The medium absorbs the whole of human experience “as in that unique point of the Universe that Jorge Luis Borges called ‘Aleph.’” All experienced reality has always been virtually perceived through symbols; the new system’s specificity is electronic integration of all communication modes into one comprehensive, diversified, malleable multimedia text.

**Closing linkage (Ch. 5):** space of flows and timeless time are material foundations of this culture — “make-believe is belief in the making.”

**2010 update:** online communities develop not as a virtual world separate from reality but as real virtuality hybridized with everyday interaction; **mass self-communication** (self-generated, self-directed, self-selected, potentially global) reshapes the media environment.

## Semantic History

Arc: Gutenberg galaxy (print) → McLuhan galaxy (mass TV) → diversified “new media” / customized cottages → computer-mediated communication and virtual communities → multimedia fusion. Dialogue with Eco, Postman, Baudrillard, and critiques of mass culture (Marcuse/Habermas positions recorded as interlocutors).

## Operationalizations

TV and audience research; Internet/CMC diffusion and inequality; multimedia industry strategies; interacting vs interacted users; 2010 social-networking cases.

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; media and cultural sociology; politics of media framing (Conclusion: political actors exist through and by media networks).

## Critiques and Limitations

Totalizing “Aleph” metaphor; uneven global access (Castells documents digital inequality); commercial oligopoly vs interactivity tension; later platform capitalism literatures (not ingested) reframe the same terrain.
