# Base and Superstructure

The architectural metaphor at the core of [[historical-materialism]]: the sum total of a
society's relations of production constitutes its economic structure (the **base**, or
"real foundation"), on which rises a **legal and political superstructure** to which
correspond definite forms of social consciousness.

## Definition as Coined

Marx's 1859 *Preface* to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* (quoted in
full in [[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Stalin 1938]], the wiki's
current source for the text): "The sum total of these relations of production constitutes
the economic structure of society — the real foundation, on which rises a legal and
political superstructure and to which correspond definite forms of social consciousness.
The mode of production of material life determines the social, political and intellectual
life process in general. It is not the consciousness of men that determines their being,
but, on the contrary, their social being that determines their consciousness." When
productive forces conflict with the relations of production, "then begins an epoch of
social revolution," and "with the change of the economic foundation the entire immense
superstructure is more or less rapidly transformed."

## Semantic History

- **1852 (*Eighteenth Brumaire*).** Marx already writes that upon forms of property "a
  whole superstructure is reared" of feelings, illusions, and habits of thought — a looser,
  more mediated usage (see [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]]).
- **1859 (*Preface*).** The canonical compressed statement above.
- **Engels's late correspondence (1890s).** Softens toward "determination in the last
  instance," conceding reciprocal action of the superstructure (not yet primary-ingested).
- **1938 (Stalin's codification).** The doctrine at its most rigid — spiritual life as
  "reflection" of material life — though the 1938 text itself insists ideas "themselves
  then react upon social being": new ideas, once arisen from material needs, become "a
  most potent force" organizing and mobilizing the masses. Stalin's *Marxism and Problems
  of Linguistics* (1950, not ingested) later removed language from the superstructure
  entirely.
- **Post-war Marxism.** [[structural-marxism|Althusser]] reworks the metaphor as
  structure-in-dominance with relative autonomy of superstructural instances;
  [[western-marxism|Western Marxists]] (Gramsci's hegemony, Williams's cultural
  materialism) progressively dissolve the topographical metaphor; critics from Weber
  onward treat it as the paradigm of economic reductionism.

## Operationalizations

Rarely operationalized directly; functions as an explanatory schema. Archaeological
materialism (Childe and successors) implicitly operationalizes base as
subsistence-technology evidence and reads institutional/ideological change against it
(see [[marxist-archaeology]]).

## Applications

Structures the entire [[historical-materialism]] research program; underwrites
[[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]] debates in political sociology; supplies the frame for
materialist analyses of religion, law, and culture across sociology and anthropology.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Reductionism.** The standing charge: reading ideas, law, and politics off the economy
  fails for cases of superstructural causation (Weber's Protestant ethic as the classic
  counter). Defenders respond with "last instance" determination and reciprocal action —
  present already inside Stalin's 1938 text, which attacks the "Economists" and Mensheviks
  precisely for "vulgar materialism" that denies ideas any role.
- **Metaphor trouble.** Critics note the building metaphor cannot specify *how much*
  autonomy the superstructure has without becoming unfalsifiable — either everything is
  determined (false) or determination is "in the last instance" (untestable).
- **Boundary problems.** Whether science, language, and the family sit in base or
  superstructure has never been stably resolved; Stalin's own 1950 linguistics
  intervention is the emblem of the difficulty.

## Lenin 1894/1921: material vs. ideological relations; politics as "concentrated economics" (attributed)

In *[[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894|What the "Friends of the People" Are]]* (1894), Lenin's earliest sustained use of the schema quoted Marx's 1859 Preface at length and glossed its method as "singling out the economic sphere from the various spheres of social life, by singling out production relations from all social relations as being basic, primary, determining all other relations." He distinguished **"material social relations"** (those that "take shape without passing through man's consciousness," e.g. production and exchange) from **"ideological social relations"** (those that "before taking shape, pass through man's consciousness"), arguing only analysis of the former permits observation of recurrence and regularity — hence scientific generalization — while ideological relations alone yield mere description. Against Mikhailovsky's claim that inheritance is a superstructure on procreation/family relations rather than on production relations, Lenin countered that private property and inheritance presuppose exchange and the specialization of social labor, arising historically only once small monogamous families and commodity exchange had developed; against Mikhailovsky's derivation of national ties from gentile/kinship extension, he argued Russian national unification (from the 17th century) was produced by growing inter-regional exchange and market consolidation under merchant-capitalist leadership — national ties, on this account, are "bourgeois ties," a product of commodity-exchange relations, not kinship.

In the 1920–21 trade-union controversy (*[[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]*, with Bukharin credited jointly), Lenin's slogan "**politics is the most concentrated expression of economics**" restated the base/superstructure relation programmatically: he rejected the term "industrial democracy" (used by Bukharin and Trotsky) as theoretically confused, arguing all forms of democracy are political superstructure ultimately determined by relations of production, so isolating an "industrial" democracy from politics-in-general is meaningless — criticizing Bukharin's attempt to "combine" a political and an economic approach to the unions as "theoretical eclecticism" and a violation of "the ABC of Marxism."

## Plekhanov 1908: the five-term formula (attributed)

The canonical Plekhanovite expansion ([[plekhanov-fundamental-problems-of-marxism-1908]]): "1. The state of the forces of production; 2. Economic relations conditioned by these forces; 3. The socio-political regime erected upon a given economic foundation; 4. The psychology of man in society...; 5. Various ideologies reflecting this psychology" — offered as "a monistic formula... impregnated with materialism" against eclectic reciprocal-action views. The insertion of *social psychology* as a distinct mediating term (4) between regime and ideology is the formula's signature move, and the hinge later Marxist debates over mediation turned on.

## Plekhanov 1895: function of the productive forces (attributed)

Plekhanov's *Monist View* ([[plekhanov-monist-view-of-history-1895]]) quotes the 1859 Preface as "the interpretive key" while glossing it against economism: "only in a popular speech could one talk about economy as the prime cause of all social phenomena. Far from being a prime cause, it is itself a consequence, a 'function' of the productive forces" — with ideology "of the higher order" (science, philosophy, art) conditioned both by the surplus that supports intellectual strata and by changing relations of production.
