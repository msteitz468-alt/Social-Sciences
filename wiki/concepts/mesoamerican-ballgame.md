# Mesoamerican Ballgame

## Definition as Coined

The **Mesoamerican ballgame** is the rubber-ball sport played in formal courts across Mesoamerica — one of Kirchhoff’s defining culture-area traits as cited by [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]]. Among the Maya it appears as elite and mythic performance (Classic art; Hero Twins associations) and as youthful diversion at contact (Yucatan communal houses, with gambling).

## Semantic History

- **Formative–Classic:** Courts at major centers; iconography of play, captives, and decapitation in some contexts.
- **Terminal Classic / “Toltec-Maya” [[chichen-itza]]:** Great Ball Court and relief programs (including decapitation themes) are central to Mexicanized political-religious display; C&H link large-scale sacrifice themes partly to Toltec-period influence (theory/position — contested).
- **Cotzumalhuapa:** Gulf-related ballgame kit and Mexican calendar elements in Terminal Classic Pacific Guatemala (as of C&H).
- **Contact:** Landa-era youth diversions include the ball game.

## Operationalizations

Court architecture; ballgame paraphernalia; mural/vase scenes; ethnohistoric rules (incomplete).

## Applications

[[classic-maya]] ritual politics; [[chichen-itza]]; comparative Mesoamerica.

## Critiques and Limitations

Rules reconstructed imperfectly; “sacrifice = every game” popular readings are overstated; regional variation large. Coe & Houston 2015 is a survey synthesis, not a ballgame monograph.
