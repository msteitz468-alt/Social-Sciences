# Capital/Income Ratio (β)

Aggregate stock-to-flow ratio at the center of [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s [[piketty-capital-dynamics]] and of *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*. Denoted **β** (beta): capital stock measured as a multiple of annual national income (“years of income”).

## Definition as Coined

β measures how large the accumulated capital stock is relative to the annual flow of national income. Piketty uses it as the book’s central descriptive statistic for the “metamorphoses of capital” and as the stock variable in the two fundamental laws: capital’s income share α = r × β ([[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]), and long-run β → s/g ([[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]). Whenever the book refers to the capital/income ratio without qualification, he means capital stock to **national income** (not disposable household income)—an economic rather than strictly monetary view of income, including public in-kind transfers.

As reported from his series: Britain and France’s national capital today is about **five or six years** of national income, slightly below eighteenth–nineteenth century and pre-WWI levels of about **six or seven years**. Europe’s ratio has tended higher than the United States except during the 1914–1945 aftermath: nineteenth/early twentieth century ~6–7 in Europe vs ~4–5 in the US; early twenty-first century private wealth in Europe again near ~6 vs slightly more than 4 in the US. In the eight richest countries, private capital was between **two and 3.5 years** of national income in 1970 and between **four and seven years** in 2010. France and Britain: national capital in 2010 roughly five to six years of NI (a bit more than six in France), versus less than four in the 1980s and barely more than two in the 1950s.

## Semantic History

National-accounts capital-stock and wealth-to-income ratios long predate 2013 (Petty, King, Giffen, Foville, Goldsmith, postwar balance sheets). Piketty’s synthesis standardizes **β** as the organizing descriptive statistic across centuries, couples it to distributional series ([[historical-wealth-accounts]], [[top-income-shares]]), and embeds it in the inequality narrative of capital’s “comeback” after mid-twentieth-century lows. Expressing wealth in years of **disposable** income inflates the ratio (disposable income ~70–80% of NI today vs ~90% when taxes were ~10% of NI): private capital of 4–7 years of NI corresponds to about 5–9 years of disposable income in the 2000s.

## Operationalizations

- **Private vs national capital**: private wealth (households + foundations/nonprofits financed by private gifts) vs national capital (private + public). Foundations generally own <5–10% of private wealth (US ~6–7%; Ancien Régime French church property ~7–8% of private wealth). Private wealth in 2010 accounts for virtually all national wealth in Britain/France (>90–99%).
- **Asset composition**: agricultural land → housing + industrial/financial assets; net foreign assets (Britain pre-WWI nearly two years of NI; Japan early 2010s ~70% of NI, Germany nearly 50%).
- **Market-value sensitivity**: real estate and equity prices dominate short-run β; Piketty estimates asset-price rebound accounts for about **one-quarter to one-third** of the 1970–2010 rise in national capital/NI in rich countries. Bubbles: Japan ~4 → nearly 7 years of NI in the 1980s then ~6 from mid-1990s; Spain ~8 years on the eve of 2007–08; US private fortunes 5 → 4 years of NI in 2008–09.
- **Scope exclusions**: durable household goods treated as consumption (typically 30–50% of NI in value, small vs 5–6 years private wealth); valuables ~5–10% of NI today (down from ~10–15% late nineteenth/early twentieth century).
- **Saving definition for dynamics**: net of depreciation; private saving = household saving + corporate retained earnings.

## Applications

Part Two of *Capital* and the study page [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]]: Europe’s structurally higher β linked to lower demographic growth (Europe/Japan population growth <0.5%/year 1970–2010 vs 1.0–1.5% in US/Canada/Australia). Piketty frames the post-1970 rise as emergence of a new [[patrimonial-capitalism]]—driven by slow growth (β = s/g), privatization of public wealth, and asset-price catch-up. Italian extreme case: private capital 240% → 680% of NI 1970–2010 while public capital 20% → −70%. Links to [[wealth-inequality]] via concentration of ownership of the stock, and to capital’s income share via α = r × β.

## Critiques and Limitations

What counts as “capital,” how to value public assets and historically slaves, and the split between **volume** (saving) and **price** effects are contested measurement issues ([[piketty-capital-debate]]). Britain 1970–2010 is a noted residual case where observed savings seem inadequate for the steep private-wealth rise (possible undervalued 1970s public assets and low-priced privatizations). Tobin’s Q (market/book value) patterns differ across “Rhenish” and Anglo-Saxon capitalism, complicating market-value series. Piketty treats capital’s price as always partly a social and political construct reflecting property rules and institutions.
