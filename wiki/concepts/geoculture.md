# Geoculture

The **cultural-ideological framework** of a world-system in [[world-systems-analysis]]: coined by [[wallerstein-immanuel|Wallerstein]] "by analogy with geopolitics" to name the norms and modes of discourse "widely accepted as legitimate within the world-system." His central historical claim is that the modern [[world-economy]] operated for centuries *without* a geoculture, and acquired one only after the French Revolution.

## Definition as Coined

Wallerstein's glossary (*[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|WSA: An Introduction]]*, 2004, attributed): "geoculture. A term coined by analogy with geopolitics. It refers to norms and modes of discourse... widely accepted as legitimate within the world-system... a geoculture does not come into existence automatically with the onset of a world-system but rather has to be created."

The **historical turning-point is the French Revolution** (1789), which made two durable changes: it "made change, political change, into a 'normal' phenomenon" rather than an aberration, and it "reoriented the concept of sovereignty, from the monarch or the legislature to the people." The concept of the "people," Wallerstein adds, "which began as a concept of inclusion, turned rather quickly into a concept of exclusion."

## Semantic History

In reaction to the Revolution, Wallerstein argues, three **ideologies** — conservatism, liberalism, and radicalism — emerged (in his precise sense: strategies for handling normalized change), none of which had existed before. Over the nineteenth century **centrist liberalism became dominant**, pulling conservatism and radicalism toward it until they functioned as "mere variants or avatars of the liberals." This liberal geoculture — gradual suffrage plus education, expanding state protections, and the forging of citizens into nations — was implemented as much by nominal conservatives (Disraeli, Bismarck, Napoleon III) as by liberals. Wallerstein dates the *unravelling* of this geoculture to the **world revolution of 1968**, which broke centrist liberalism's automatic dominance.

## Operationalizations

The geoculture is identified through the dominant ideologies, the [[antisystemic-movements|antisystemic movements]] (worker/socialist, ethnic/national, women's) and their shared "two-step" strategy (first take state power, then transform society), and the **[[structures-of-knowledge|social sciences]]** themselves — which Wallerstein reads as a geocultural apparatus, its nomothetic/idiographic disciplinary division premised on Western dominance of the world-system.

## Applications

The concept lets world-systems analysis treat ideology, social movements, and the disciplines as *systemic* features to be historicized inside the world-economy rather than as autonomous cultural facts. See [[world-systems-analysis]], [[structures-of-knowledge]], [[antisystemic-movements]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics of world-systems analysis have long charged it with **economic reductionism** and a thin treatment of culture; "geoculture" is in part Wallerstein's answer, but critics counter that it still derives culture from the world-economy's needs and underweights cultural autonomy and meaning (the objection pressed from Geertzian and post-structuralist directions). The periodization (no geoculture before 1789; centrist-liberal dominance 1848–1968) is itself a strong, contestable historical thesis.
