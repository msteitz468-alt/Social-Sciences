# Redistribution

## Definition as Coined

**Redistribution** (or **pooling**) is the **centralized collection of goods from the members of a group and their redivision within that group** — a "within" relation, the collective action of a group, as opposed to [[reciprocity]], the "between" relation of two parties. *(Attributed — one of [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s [[forms-of-integration|forms of integration]] (centricity), taken up and developed by [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] in *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 5, 1972.)* [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s chief as "a tribal banker" — collecting, storing, and redistributing food "for the benefit of the whole community" — is Sahlins' prototype.

## Semantic History

Polanyi established redistribution as one of the ways economies are integrated (alongside reciprocity, householding, and market exchange), grounded in a "centricity" of institutional pattern. Sahlins folded it into his reciprocity analysis, arguing that redistribution is **not categorically distinct from reciprocity but is "a centralized, formal organization of kinship-rank reciprocities"** — the analytic variable is the *degree of centralization*, not a break in kind. This resists an evolutionary story of reciprocity giving way to redistribution.

## Operationalizations

Sahlins distinguishes redistribution by whether the central position is **achieved or ascribed**: the Siuai [[big-man]]'s centrality is a personal achievement that dissolves with him, while the Nootka chief's is built into lineage office ("a shunting station" fixed by structure). He notes variants in scale — some chiefdoms (Moala, Tikopia, pastoralist chiefdoms) fragment the flow into many small transactions, while others accumulate on a large scale "approaching the classical magazine economies of antiquity."

## Applications

- **Chiefly economy and the [[big-man]]:** redistribution names the economic basis of primitive politics — chiefly generosity as the price and proof of rank. Sahlins observes the ideological doubleness of chiefly giving: framed simultaneously as reciprocal (mutual) and as generous (the chief gives more), because pure reciprocity would deny inequality while pure generosity would expose exploitation — "everywhere the indigenous category for exploitation is 'reciprocity.'"
- **Intensification:** redistribution is the channel through which leadership mobilizes the surplus the [[domestic-mode-of-production]] does not spontaneously yield (Lévi-Strauss on the Nambikwara chief; Malo on Hawaiian storehouses "to keep the people contented").

### Kirch 1984 — Polynesian first-fruits, *‘inasi*/*makahiki*, and elite-limited redistribution

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch accepts that Service/Sahlins/Fried treated redistribution as fundamental to the [[chiefdom]] level, but **qualifies the equity assumption** for stratified Polynesian cases.

**From first-fruits to regularized taxation.** Ancestral surplus appropriation was (on his reconstruction) sacrificial first-fruits to the chief as divine exemplar mediating [[mana]]. As chieftainship expanded, first-fruits became systems of regular taxation still in ritual guise. Peaks: Tongan **\*‘inasi*** (twice yearly, mandatory yams to the Tuʻi Tonga; *‘inasi ‘ufimui* and *‘inasi ‘ufimotua*) and Hawaiian **\*makahiki*** (four-month Lono season; annual *hoʻokupu* of barkcloth, cordage, feathers, dogs, etc.). Society Islands *pararaa matahiti* is listed as a parallel elaboration. Goldman’s frame (as Kirch cites it): shift from voluntary mutual honoring to compulsory unequal reciprocity. Alleged *‘inasi* function was Tuʻi Tonga mediation with deities for fertility; in Kirch’s political reading, tribute from all parts **validated** Tuʻi Tonga and *hau* supremacy and financed a non-food-producing chiefly class. Dual paramountship split the work: Tuʻi Tonga as ritual cement; *hau* as secular enforcer of tribute logistics.

**Stratification and the fate of redistributed goods.** In less stratified societies, first-fruits and ceremonial goods returned largely to households via feasts asserting rank and *mana*, and could alleviate famine. With increased stratification (Hawaiʻi, Tonga, Tahiti), the proportion actually redistributed to the people at large **decreased**; surplus supported chiefly establishments, public works, craft specialists, and elite political action — yet kinship ideology was never fully divorced, and over-extraction risked rebellion/usurpation (Malo: kings put to death for oppression of the *makaʻāinana*).

**Hawaiian redistribution limited to chiefs (range_6 / Earle).** Against models that treat *hoʻokupu* as provisioning the commoner populace, Kirch (citing Malo 1951:143; also Oliver on Tahiti; Earle 1977) holds that redistribution of *hoʻokupu* was **essentially limited to the chiefly class and soldiers**: “No share of this property, however, was given to the people.” *Makahiki* thus served as a political vehicle for the paramount to reward supporters within the elite. Commoners might still conceive tribute as royal fertility mediation — ideology and material flow diverge. Kirch further rejects functionalist origins of hierarchy as redistributors of dispersed resources (cf. Sahlins 1958) or irrigation managers from population pressure: first-fruits and tribute obligations were already part of Ancestral Polynesian Society. Tonga’s magazine/yam-storage path vs Hawaiʻi’s peripatetic chieftainship (no magazine economy despite irrigation apogee) shows how storage–redistribution coupling varies with ecological and political choice.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Because Sahlins treats redistribution as centralized reciprocity, the boundary with [[generalized-reciprocity]] is deliberately blurred, which some find analytically loose. The functional reading of chiefly redistribution (as both logistic provisioning and a "ritual of communion and subordination") risks circularity. As with the whole scheme, claims are stated in attributed voice and rest on mid-century ethnography.

### Adams (*The Evolution of Urban Society*, 1966, attributed)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] reads early Mesopotamian temple/palace economies (ration systems; Gelb; temple herding and textile-copper trade links; seed and plow-animal centralization) as fitting [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s **redistributive** model, contrasting with Mesoamerica's hyperdeveloped **markets** mediating an ecological mosaic. Temples as reserve and inter-community networks; Eanna *sanga* ranks and early redistribution; Aztec palace tribute as redistributive power at the conquest-state terminus. Surplus mobilization is institutional, not automatic — see [[social-surplus]] (Orans/Polanyi layer) and [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]].

### Service (*Origins of the State and Civilization*, 1975, attributed)

Redistribution is the engine of Service's [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]: he argued that in early polities **political power organized the economy, rather than the reverse** — "the system was redistributive, allocative, not acquisitive: personal wealth was not required to gain personal political power." A redistributor (a [[big-man]] or emergent chief) amasses a "fund of power" by collecting and reallocating goods across ecologically specialized or task-divided producers, which requires followers' confidence in the redistributor's fairness and administrative skill. Success is self-reinforcing — a "snowballing" effect, since "successes in the redistributive process are tremendously supportive of the bureaucracy and lead to further development of specialization... inasmuch as the effectiveness of the system is increasingly obvious." Service used this mechanism to explain the transition from big-man to hereditary [[chiefdom]], and cited David Malo's account of Hawaiian royal storehouses as evidence that redistribution builds personal loyalty to the chief, not merely abstract functional interdependence.

### Renfrew (*The Emergence of Civilisation*, 1972 / 2010, attributed)

[[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] links third-millennium [[mediterranean-polyculture|olive/vine polyculture]] to regularized redistribution that later concentrates under palace control — one coupling in the [[multiplier-effect]] (not a sole prime mover). Seals/sealings (e.g. Lerna House of the Tiles) figure as possession/ownership and palace-precursor administration. In the 2010 preface he **accepts Halstead’s critique** that *Emergence* overemphasized Mycenaean palaces as general subsistence redistributive centres; redistribution was “probably on a more modest scale,” while polyculture may still be central without the simple redistributive path originally proposed.

### Dickinson (*The Aegean Bronze Age*, 1994, attributed)

[[dickinson-oliver-t-p-k|Dickinson]] similarly rejects **total** Mycenaean palace redistribution of subsistence; land remains the economic base and craft specialists are mostly part-time farmers, with only elite/palace-maintained specialists full-time. Linear B records specialised sectors (e.g. wool) without equating the palace to a whole-economy redistributive state.

### Postgate (*Early Mesopotamia*, 1994, attributed)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate]] adopts Polanyi's tripartite typology of economic integration — reciprocity, redistribution, market/commercial exchange — but explicitly stakes out a **calibrated, anti-pure-redistribution position** for early Mesopotamia: the three modes "are not mutually exclusive," and "one of the complexities of early Mesopotamian society is precisely the co-existence of these three modes and their variations" (as of Postgate 1994). This pushes back against a pure-redistribution reading of the [[temple-economy|temple and palace economies]], where Postgate documents private lease and (restricted) sale alongside institutional redistribution — see [[temple-economy]] and [[land-tenure]]. Postgate separately adopts, citing Polanyi, a three-function analytic distinction for currency (standard of accounting; medium of exchange; means of payment), endorsing Polanyi's insistence that "these three roles should be kept distinct: the existence of currency in one of them does not necessarily imply its existence in another."
