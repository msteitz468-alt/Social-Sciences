# Baptism of Money

## Definition as Coined

As reported for the southern [[cauca-valley-afro-colombians|Cauca Valley]], the **baptism of money** is an illicit rite in which a bill is baptised (in place of or as if it were a child) so that it will return to its owner with increase, while a paired bill is spent into circulation. [[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1980, Ch. 7) reads it as a folk theory of the **secret of capital** — money that begets money — inverted against the use-value paradigm in which money is a neutral medium of exchange for want-satisfaction.

He tabulates a structural analogy (attributed):

| Legitimate | Illicit |
|---|---|
| Baptism of child | Baptism of money |
| Spiritual rebirth / social person | Capital's self-expansion / unnatural fertility |

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Local folklore (Cauca) | Sinful magic of self-multiplying money |
| Taussig 1980 | Allegory of M–C–M′ / capital vs use-value money (Aristotle's household economy contrast) |
| Wider Latin American folklore | Variants of baptised/charmed money exist regionally — do not collapse without citation |

## Operationalizations

Narrative motif and reported belief; not a frequency measure in the source.

## Applications

- Pair with [[devil-contract]] as Cauca superstitions that, Taussig argues, systematically endorse the contradiction between use-values and exchange-values.
- Bridge to Marx's money and capital chapters without equating folk rite with *Capital*'s categories — **homologous reading, attributed**.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Structuralist paradigm tables can over-systematise scattered lore.
- Christian baptism symbolism may carry meanings (sin, personhood) not exhausted by the capital analogy.
