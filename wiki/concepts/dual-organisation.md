# Dual Organisation

## Definition as Coined

**Dual organisation** (moiety system) is the division of a society into two exogamous
halves — moieties — such that every person belongs to one moiety, must marry a member of the
other, and reckons descent through one line (matrilineal or patrilineal). Where the two
moieties are the only marriage classes, the children of two sisters, or of two brothers,
necessarily fall in the same moiety and are classed as siblings, while cross-cousins fall in
the opposite moiety — the structural basis of much [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|
classificatory terminology]] and of [[cross-cousin-marriage]]. The term entered 19th-century
ethnology; [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] made it the anchor of his reconstruction of Melanesian
social history.

## Semantic History

- **19th-c. ethnology:** moiety systems described among Australian Aboriginal and North
  American peoples; treated as an elementary form of social organisation.
- **Rivers 1914:** argued (attributed) that dual organisation with matrilineal descent was
  the *most ancient reconstructible* form of Melanesian society — now confined to the Banks
  Islands, northern New Hebrides, and part of Ysabel, but formerly far more widespread,
  inferred from terminological survivals in regions (Fiji, Buin) where it had lapsed.
- **The fission/fusion dispute:** the near-unanimous view held moieties arise by **fission**
  (a single group splitting in two). Rivers argued the opposite for Melanesia — dual
  organisation arose by **fusion** of two originally distinct peoples (an aboriginal
  population and immigrant "sitting-interment people"), reading inter-moiety hostility,
  ascribed physical/mental contrasts, and Bismarck-Archipelago origin myths of "light" and
  "dark" ancestors as folk-memory of two fused peoples. He conceded this was among his least
  secure claims — "the bulk of my scheme survives even if dual organisation arose by fission."

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by: exogamous two-class marriage; a single matrilineal or patrilineal descent
rule; moiety-linked terminology, joking/insult relations (Pentecost's Tagaro vs. Subwe
moiety insults), and sometimes moiety [[totemism]]. In the Banks Islands the moieties (called
*veve*, also "mother") were largely unnamed — people spoke of "the same side" or "the other
side of the house."

## Applications

Rivers' Melanesian survey: the Banks Islands and Pentecost as the purest surviving cases;
inferred former dual organisation in Fiji, Santa Cruz, and the matrilineal Solomons
(*kema*/*venahuhu* groups). He distinguished it from superficially similar dual structures
elsewhere (Tikopia, Tanna), which he attributed to different processes — cautioning that
"the likeness may be the result of convergence."

## Critiques and Limitations

Rivers' fusion theory won few adherents and his broader migration reconstruction was
abandoned (see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]); the fission view remained standard.
Later kinship theory ([[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]) treated moiety exogamy as the
simplest form of [[restricted-exchange]] within [[alliance-theory]] — a "principle of
organization" giving form to reciprocity rather than a fixed historical institution — and read
the ranked-castes-crossed-by-exogamous-moieties structure of the [[bororo|Bororo]] and Guana (in
*[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]*) as "the asymmetry of the classes...
balanced by the symmetry of the moieties," detaching the structure from Rivers' historical
narrative. The recurring caution — that two
societies with dual organisation need not share a history — is Rivers' own and remains sound.
