# Fascism as a Response to Market Society (Polanyi)

## Definition as Coined

[[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s structural theory of fascism (Chapters 19–20 of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*), developed from his earlier essay "The Essence of Fascism" (1933). Fascism is "a reform of market economy achieved at the price of the **extirpation of all democratic institutions**, both in the industrial and in the political realm," accompanied by a "re-education designed to denaturalize the individual" — a "political religion."

Its cause is **structural, not national**. Polanyi rejects explanations by local factors (the Versailles Treaty, Junker militarism, "Italian temperament"): fascism "appeared in defeated countries like Bulgaria and in victorious ones like Jugoslavia," across "every type of religious, cultural, racial, and political background," and so must be explained by a common cause — **the deadlock of market society**. When the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] could no longer function and the [[double-movement|protective countermovement]] had stalemated with the market (the "separation of the economic and political sphere" under strain, popular democracy pressing against market discipline), fascism offered a way out by abolishing democracy and subordinating society to a reorganized market/corporate order.

Polanyi distinguishes a fascist "**move**" from a fascist "**movement**": fascism was "an ever given political possibility, an almost instantaneous emotional reaction in every industrial community since the 1930's" — its arrival measured not by party membership but by the sudden, near-effortless collapse of democratic-constitutional resistance at a market impasse. He periodizes its fortunes directly against the world market's condition: auxiliary and undeveloped 1917–23; "faded out as a political force altogether" during the 1924–29 market restoration (Dawes Plan, Locarno, gold-standard return); "a world power within a few years" after the 1930 general crisis.

## Semantic History

Polanyi's is a *systemic* theory of fascism, contrasting with theories rooting it in national character, class alliance (though he engages the peasantry's role), mass psychology, or totalitarian ideology. It anticipates later structural and political-economic accounts and remains a reference point for debates on the relation between market liberalization, democracy, and authoritarian backlash. Polanyi reads Soviet "socialism in one country" as the *other* response to the same market collapse — "what appeared as Russian autarchy was merely the passing of capitalist internationalism."

## Operationalizations

Identified by the collapse of constitutional resistance at a market deadlock rather than by fascist-party strength; traced through the interwar sequence of "save the currency" governments and the failure of Labor/socialist governments over the currency-vs-social-services deadlock (the fall of Britain's Labour government 1931; Brüning; the hamstrung Blum experiment).

## Applications

The culmination of the [[double-movement]]: fascism (and Soviet collectivization, and the New Deal) are alternative resolutions of the impasse of a market society that could no longer self-regulate. Sets up the concluding argument on [[freedom-in-a-complex-society]] (fascism's answer to "the reality of society" is to reject freedom).

## Reflexivity and Limitations

- Polanyi wrote in 1944 as a refugee from fascism; the theory is engaged, not detached. Its **1944 vocabulary** includes period racial-classification terms (used descriptively, to map fascism's spread across "races" and refute racial explanations) — recorded here as historical usage, not endorsed as analytic category.
- The structural account is criticized for underweighting ideology, agency, antisemitism, and national specifics; comparative fascism scholarship treats market deadlock as at most one condition among several.
- The claim that fascism is essentially a *market* phenomenon is contested by those who stress its political and cultural autonomy.
