# Functional Prerequisites

## Definition as Coined

A **functional prerequisite** (or functional imperative) is a condition that any [[social-system|social system]] must meet to persist as a system. The vocabulary is not solely [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s — he takes his departure from the 1950 paper "The Functional Prerequisites of a Society" by Aberle, Cohen, Davis, Levy, and Sutton — but *The Social System* (1951) gives the concept its most systematic development. Parsons argued a social system must (a) not be radically incompatible with the biological and personality needs of its component actors or with stable cultural integration, and (b) secure a minimum of **"support"** — adequate actor motivation to fulfill [[social-role|role-expectations]] and abstain from disruptive [[deviance]].

He grounded the "**motivational problem of order**" in two biological properties of human nature: **plasticity** (capacity to learn many alternative behavior patterns rather than being genetically fixed) and **sensitivity** (accessibility to influence by others' attitudes). To these, cultural prerequisites are equally necessary: language and a minimum of shared culture (empirical knowledge, expressive symbolism, value-orientation) are preconditions of a social system's existence.

## Semantic History

Parsons connected functional prerequisites to the machinery of **allocation** — the distribution of scarce "movable" elements (personnel, facilities, rewards) among statuses and roles, "always relative to demand" — and to **integration**: the negative regulation of private interests and the positive institutionalization of collective functions through leadership and authority (following Weber on the legitimacy of authority). In his later (1953+) work the loose list of prerequisites was tightened into the famous **four functional imperatives (AGIL)**: Adaptation, Goal-attainment, Integration, Latency (pattern-maintenance) — the scheme most associated with Parsonian [[structural-functionalism]].

The related concept of **functional prerequisites of a society** was elaborated by Marion Levy and others; the whole apparatus was a defining target of the anti-functionalist reaction.

## Operationalizations

The prerequisites are analytic categories, not measured quantities. Comparative work (e.g. Levy) attempted to derive society-level "requisites" systematically, but critics held the derivations were not empirically constraining.

## Applications

- The framework within which Parsons compared the "invariant points of reference" for the structural differentiation and variation of societies (Chapters IV–V of *The Social System*).
- Foundational to Parsons's account of [[social-equilibrium]] and, through AGIL, to the whole later functionalist research program.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is contested. The standard objections: it is **teleological** (explaining an item by the "needs" it serves risks treating a consequence as a cause), it tends to assume that whatever exists is functionally required (a conservative bias), and the list of "prerequisites" cannot be derived non-arbitrarily. These charges are central to the broader critique of functionalism (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]); Robert Merton's distinction of *manifest* and *latent* functions, and his concepts of *dysfunction* and *functional alternatives*, were in part attempts to discipline the looseness Parsons's usage invited.
