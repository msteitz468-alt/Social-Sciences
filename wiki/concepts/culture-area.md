# Culture Area

## Definition as Coined

In Americanist anthropology, a **culture area** is a geographic region sharing a characteristic culture pattern, usually treated as a historical product of diffusion and environment at a broad scale (Wissler and others). [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, Ch. 5) retained the concept as a **natural-history taxonomy** for historical classification but argued it is insufficient for explaining cross-cultural regularities.

## Semantic History

Classic Boasian/Americanist tool; critiqued and refined throughout the twentieth century. Steward's contrast of **area** vs **[[culture-type]]** is a mid-century methodological hinge for neo-evolutionism:

| Culture area | Culture type (Steward) |
|---|---|
| Total element content / unique pattern | Selected functionally interrelated features |
| Emphasizes contrasts and historical uniqueness | Emphasizes parallels and regularities |
| Diffusion and historic connection (uniformities) | Independent recurrence (regularities) |
| Weak for deep-time developmental typology alone | Built from [[cultural-core]] + [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]] |

He also held that ethos/value-system classifications often rest on essentially the same basis as culture areas. Related: co-tradition and "Marginal" constructs — Steward noted their limits for developmental taxonomy. Adjacent but different problem: Chicago [[natural-area]] (urban ecology).

## Operationalizations

Trait inventories and pattern maps (area); core + integration level (Stewardian type). Steward discussed Murdock's South American classification as area-justifiable but not equivalent to his own ecological-developmental typology for irrigation zones.

## Applications

Aboriginal America classifications (Ch. 5); contrast cases where the same type appears in multiple areas ([[patrilineal-band]]); Great Basin as ecological configuration vs area label (Ch. 6).

## Critiques and Limitations

Steward: area method groups by total uniqueness and diffusion, missing independent functional parallels; historic-depth failures when only synchronic trait maps are used. Broader critiques: boundary arbitrariness; colonial mapping of "tribal" spaces.
