# The Mindful Body (three bodies)

## Definition as Coined

**The mindful body** is the framework [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] and Margaret Lock
proposed in "The Mindful Body: A Prolegomenon to Future Work in Medical Anthropology" (*Medical
Anthropology Quarterly*, 1987), and which Scheper-Hughes applies throughout
*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]*. It analyzes the body at three
mutually implicated levels: (1) the **individual body-self** — the existential, phenomenologically lived
body; (2) the **social body** — the body as a symbol "good to think" with, a representational medium for
nature, society, and culture; and (3) the **body politic** — the body as an object of regulation,
surveillance, and control by power. The program's premise is that Cartesian mind/body dualism has
crippled medical anthropology, and that illness and healing must be read across all three bodies at once.

## Semantic History

The framework draws on Mauss's "techniques of the body," Douglas's "two bodies," Merleau-Pontian
phenomenology, Gramsci's hegemony, and Foucault's **[[biopower]]**. In *Death Without Weeping*
Scheper-Hughes reworks it by borrowing [[taussig-michael|Taussig]]'s image of a "nervous system" to link
"the three bodies: the existential body self, the representational social body, and the body politic — all
of them 'nervous'" under conditions of hunger and state terror. It became a foundational statement of
**[[critical-medical-anthropology]]**.

## Operationalizations

Applied to the Alto, the framework treats **[[nervoso]]** as the pivot where the three bodies meet: a
trembling, fainting body-self encodes (social body) the exhaustion of hungry labor and communicates
(body politic) an oblique protest against a "nervous," post-authoritarian political order. Somatic idioms
— *fraqueza*, *sangue fraco* (weak blood), "wasted livers" — are read simultaneously as physiology, social
commentary, and political inscription rather than reduced to any one level.

## Applications

- **Against somatization-as-pathology.** The framework licenses Scheper-Hughes's break with the
  Kleinmans' somatization model, recasting bodily-idiom distress as meaningful communication rather than a
  "primitive defense mechanism."
- **Embodiment and class.** Drawing on Boltanski, she argues popular classes "communicate with and through
  the body" (a somatic culture) while biomedicine, modeled on bourgeois psychological interiority,
  pathologizes this as deviance.

## Critiques and Limitations

As a broad heuristic rather than a testable theory, the three-bodies scheme is sometimes criticized for
elasticity — nearly any bodily phenomenon can be narrated across its three levels. Its value is
programmatic: it reoriented medical anthropology toward embodiment and power. Its claims here are
attributed to Scheper-Hughes and Lock.
