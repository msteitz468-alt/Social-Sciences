# Social Surplus

## Definition as Coined

In [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]'s archaeological usage (*[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]*, 1936/1951), **social surplus** (surplus of foodstuffs / cultivators' surplus) is production beyond what food-producers need for their own subsistence, accumulated and redirected so that non-food-producing specialists — priests, scribes, smiths, officials, merchants, soldiers — can exist as classes. Childe treats such accumulation as a **precondition** of the village-to-city transition ([[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]]): as reproductive works (irrigation, drainage) grow more ambitious, "the need for an accumulated stock of surplus foodstuffs would increase."

The phrase draws on a longer political-economy vocabulary of surplus (physiocrats, classical economists, Marxian [[surplus-value|surplus value]]) but is **not identical** to Marxian surplus value (which is a relation of exploitation within commodity production). Childe's archaeological surplus is a **subsistence/specialization threshold**, not a theory of value.

## Semantic History

- **Political economy:** surplus product as the share of output available beyond necessary consumption.
- **Lenski 1966 (sociological):** in *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]*, [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] defines surplus as goods and services over and above the minimum required to keep producers alive and productive; the **second [[laws-of-distribution|law of distribution]]** assigns nearly all of that surplus to **power**. Surplus size (tied to technology) is the master variable for degree of inequality across society types in [[ecological-evolutionary-theory]]. Related to but not identical with Childe’s specialist-enabling surplus: Lenski’s surplus is a distributive-theory construct, not only an urban-revolution threshold.
- **Childe 1930s–50s:** archaeological operationalization — food surplus → full-time specialists → cities, writing, class, state; pairs with the two revolutions. *[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]* (1936) is the coining popular statement; *[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* (1942) restates surplus concentration (temple/palace) as the urban-revolution mechanism and extends the narrative through classical antiquity.
- **Processual / systems archaeology:** surplus reworked as systemic variable (redistribution, energy capture) without always retaining Childe's class narrative.
- **Critiques:** surplus theory of state origins contested (war, ritual, irrigation, trade as co-drivers); "surplus" can be circular (inferred from specialists it is meant to explain); low-density urbanisms and complex non-state societies strain the threshold model.

## Operationalizations

Childe: grain stores, livestock capital, temple/royal estates, metal-using industries requiring specialized labor, urban settlement size. Modern: settlement hierarchy, storage architecture, inequality metrics, craft specialization indices — without requiring Childe's full package.

## Applications

Load-bearing for [[neolithic-revolution]] (storage and rudimentary surplus among self-sufficing villages) and especially [[urban-revolution]] (surplus large enough for full-time specialists and state/temple institutions). Links archaeological periodization to [[historical-materialism|materialist]] class analysis in popular form. Related but distinct: Marxian [[surplus-value]] on the value form under capitalism.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mesopotamia-centric type-case; teleology from surplus to city; underplays ritual, kinship, and coercion as independent drivers; absolute dates and demographic measures pre-radiocarbon in Childe's texts. Keep all causal surplus→state claims attributed.

### Adams — gross deployable wealth (1966, attributed)

In *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]*, [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] (ch. II) treats surplus as **not** an automatic per-capita efficiency residual that engenders elites. He follows [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] (and collaborators, 1957): actual agricultural surpluses are defined and mobilized in institutional settings; collective symbols and institutions of primitive states convert peasant leisure into storehouse foodstuffs. Drawing on Martin Orans (1966), he shifts the unit of analysis from **per-capita production−consumption** (incalculable from usual archaeological data; implies efficiency as the main change factor) to **gross deployable wealth** concentrated at political/religious centers. Gross surplus:

- requires transport and institutional mechanisms for concentration and reallocation, not only farm productivity;
- directs attention to urban (or other) centers where utilization creates specialist classes and monuments by which we identify the [[urban-revolution]];
- makes surplus an interdependent component of territorial control, political superordination, and technology rather than an independent cause.

Mesopotamian specialized production units (temple/palace herding, ration systems, composite multi-zone subsistence) and Mesoamerican ecological-mosaic trade/markets illustrate different institutional paths to deployable wealth (attributed). See [[redistribution]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]].


### Trigger — appropriation ratios (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) estimates that roughly **ten farm families supported one to two non-producers** across early civilizations, with free-farmer tax often ~1/10–1/5 of produce; ~70–90% of labour went to food production and surplus was never large. He privileges **rights to surplus and labour** over land title as such ([[wealth-appropriation]]). Synthesis ratios — not measured census facts. Complements Adams’s gross deployable wealth framing without identical operationalization. Study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]].

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) radicalizes the surplus problem: under pre-state demography, **surplus does not exist until the embryonic state extracts it** — otherwise it is “consumed” in leisure and cultural elaboration. All claims **attributed**. Complements Childe’s threshold and Adams’s institutional mobilization by centering **coercion** (Sahlins, Chayanov, Boserup via Scott). Links: [[wealth-appropriation]], [[grain-hypothesis]], [[state-formation]], [[domestic-mode-of-production]].

**Necessary but not automatic.** The Neolithic agro-complex (grain-and-manpower module) is necessary scaffolding for state design, but surplus for nonproducers is not an automatic efficiency residual. Archaic states require appropriable agro-pastoral surplus to feed clerks, artisans, soldiers, priests, and aristocrats; ancient transport logistics demand maximum arable land and workers within the smallest radius — the late-Neolithic resettlement camp on rich alluvium is the preexisting nucleus. Elective affinity: farming without a state was common; states without alluvial grain farmers, Scott claims, did not exist.

**Domestic mode, Chayanov, Boserup → compulsion.** Where no preexisting settled nucleus exists, population must be assembled (Spanish *reducciones*; mission stations). Pre-state [[domestic-mode-of-production|domestic mode of production]] (Sahlins): open access via group membership; no incentive beyond local sufficiency. Chayanov: more workers than dependents → reduced work effort. A peasantry with basic needs met will **not** automatically produce an elite-appropriable surplus — it must be compelled. Only unfree/coerced labor (corvée, forced deliveries, debt bondage, serfdom, communal bondage, slavery) created surplus under early-state demography when means of production were not monopolized. Boserup (via Scott): without land monopoly, lower classes must be made personally unfree or they find other subsistence; only when the world is “fully occupied” and means privately controlled can land control alone call forth surplus.

**Grain tax as extraction, not residual.** Cultivators supply a fund of rent; grain tax unlikely less than a fifth of harvest by agrarian comparison; tax walks cultivators closer to the subsistence precipice. Cereals uniquely make surplus **fiscally legible** — visible simultaneous harvests, storage, rations — so the form of surplus that scaffolds the early state is cereal, not generic calories ([[grain-hypothesis]]). Barbarian–state relations can be read as a contest for the right to appropriate surplus from the same sedentary grain-and-manpower module — the shared prize of state formation and barbarian accumulation ([[nonstate-spaces]]).

**Contrast with Childe/Adams.** Childe’s surplus enables specialists and cities as civilizational step; Adams’s gross deployable wealth stresses institutional concentration. Scott’s position: surplus for elites is a **political achievement of coercion and confinement**, not a natural fruit of Neolithic productivity that states merely harvest — and that achievement is fragile, disease-ridden, and often abandoned with welfare gains for those who leave ([[societal-collapse]]).
