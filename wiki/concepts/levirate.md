# Levirate

## Definition as Coined

The **levirate** is the marriage rule or preference linking a widow to her deceased husband’s brother (or a designated kinsman). Standard comparative term in ethnology; extensively illustrated in [[lowie-robert-h|Lowie]]’s *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* marriage chapters and systematically distinguished in [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]’s Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950).

## Semantic History

Biblical and classical legal usage (Hebrew levirate) → Victorian comparative kinship → modern kinship studies. ASKAM’s load-bearing contribution is the **true levirate vs widow inheritance** distinction (below). Related institutions often conflated under one label: sister-replacement, sororal polygyny, ghost marriage / raising seed to the dead, widow-concubinage.

## Operationalizations

| Form | Definition (R-B / ASKAM) |
|---|---|
| **True levirate** | Brother cohabits with widow still of child-bearing age to raise children counted **as the deceased’s**; widow remains wife of the dead man; brother is **surrogate**, not strictly husband |
| **Widow inheritance** | Brother (or heir) **becomes** husband/father; different jural outcome for children’s affiliation |
| **True sororate (parallel)** | Barren wife’s sister bears children counted as barren wife’s (e.g. Zulu) |
| **Sister-replacement** | Sister replaces deceased wife as new wife of the widower |
| **Sororal polygyny** | Man marries sisters as co-wives (may be approved or banned depending on structure) |

Further axes: mandatory vs preferential; junior vs senior levirate; restriction to lineage segment (Ashanti *yafunu*); relation to inheritance of property and of widows as duty of heir.

## Applications

### Nuer (Evans-Pritchard 1951)

*(Attributed — *[[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-1951|Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer]]*; study [[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]].)* Classic **true levirate**: marriage bonds not severed by death; widow remains wife of the dead husband; children she bears afterward (by brother or stranger) count as his. Nuer refused the word *kwen* (“to marry”) for a brother taking a deceased brother’s wife — he “provided his brother’s wife with a hut.” Preferred levir = full brother (unmarried brothers’ rights paramount).

**Distinct from [[ghost-marriage]]:** in ghost-marriage a pro-husband *actually marries* (new cattle + rites) in a dead man’s name; in levirate the legal husband already married her and the brother enters an existing family. **Distinct from widow-concubinage:** lover not of husband’s kin (*ciek ma lak*).

### Lowie (*Primitive Society*)

Preferential mating and family chapters — comparative surface form (see *[[lowie-primitive-society-study|Primitive Society]]* study).

### Radcliffe-Brown, ASKAM Introduction (attributed)

R-B distinguished **true levirate** (Hebrew; African [[nuer]], [[zulu]] and many others) from **widow inheritance**. Both, with sororate variants, exemplify **unity of the sibling group**: the sibling set can supply a substitute spouse so that the alliance and (in true levirate) the dead man’s social parenthood continue.

He summarized [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]]’s Lozi–Zulu contrast: Zulu approve wife’s younger sister marriage, object to divorce as destroying permanence, and use levirate and sister-replacement to continue alliance; Lozi object even to classificatory sister of the wife as co-wife (polygyny jealousy would spoil sisterly love). “In the very different Lozi system the levirate and sororate could not possibly have the functions they have in the Zulu system” because sibling-group unity and lineage solidarity are major among Zulu and relatively minor among Lozi (attributed).

### Volume cases (attributed ethnographic reports)

- **Zulu (Gluckman):** cattle transfer and corporate agnatic lineage make pater rules and levirate/sororate structurally coherent; permanent legal family ideal.
- **Lozi (Gluckman):** wives not inherited; cognatic system without corporate lineage — levirate cannot serve Zulu-type functions.
- **Ashanti (Fortes):** inheritance and levirate thought a duty of the heir for inherited property; in practice restricted to a lineage segment (sometimes *yafunu*, “children of one womb,” uterine descendants ≤4–5 generations); man’s sister’s son as prospective heir may address *wofa*’s wife as wife (*yere*) — potential levirate explains gravity of adultery with uncle’s wife; *kuna aware* widow remarriage; young people averse by Fortes’s fieldwork moment (sample: only 3% women admitted inherited wives, all over 40).
- **Nuba (Nadel):** Nyaro — marriageable widows mostly non-compulsory levirate with husband’s brother or gens-kinsman; Tullishi — modified levirate with husband’s full brother, sister’s son, or other matrilineal relative recommended not enforced; orphan care linked to levirate + heir’s duty when grain goes to matrilineal heirs.
- **Nuer (Evans-Pritchard chapter):** levirate, ghost-wives, widow-concubinage, and bridewealth as they build local clusters; true levirate as surrogacy for the dead husband’s name (ghost marriage terminology not always explicit in R-B’s Introduction; EP chapter develops the complex).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Surface similarity across cases may hide different property, descent, and pater/genitor logics — always attribute and date.
- Colonial legal codes and cash economies altered widow inheritance practices after classic fieldwork moments.
- “Levirate” as a single label collapses true levirate, widow inheritance, and related forms — use the ASKAM distinction in wiki prose.
- Preference vs enforcement varies; Ashanti and Nuba cases show duty-language with weak contemporary practice.
