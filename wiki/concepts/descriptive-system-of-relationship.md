# Descriptive System of Relationship

## Definition as Coined

In [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871), the **descriptive system** is one of two radical forms of kinship terminology. Relatives are indicated by **describing** the path of relationship (or by terms that preserve lineal/collateral distinctions familiar in European systems), rather than by the wide **mergers** that define the [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory system]]. Morgan assigns the descriptive form to the **Aryan, Semitic, and Uralian** “families.”

## Semantic History

Founding-era typology; later kinship theory renamed and subdivided European/“Eskimo” types in other frameworks (e.g. Murdock — not yet ingested). “Descriptive” vs “classificatory” remains a historical landmark pair even where modern formalisms supersede it.

## Operationalizations

Schedule comparison: presence/absence of mergers of father with father’s brother, etc. (inverse of classificatory diagnostics).

## Applications

Part I of *Systems*; contrast class for American Indian, Dravidian-type, and Hawaiian-type systems.

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary may oversimplify; “descriptive” European systems still classify; Morgan’s family labels are period constructs.
