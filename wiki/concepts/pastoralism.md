# Pastoralism

## Definition as Coined

**Pastoralism** denotes a subsistence and social orientation organized around the herding of domestic (or intensively managed) animals — sheep, goats, cattle, and regionally camels, yak, reindeer, etc. — rather than around wild game alone or plant cultivation alone. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] pastoralism appears as: (1) a **component of early farming packages** (sheep/goat pens, village flocks); (2) a **successor economy** after PPNB town collapse in the Jordan valley; (3) a **primary Neolithic form** in some regions (Deccan cattle pastoralism; Saharan cattle herding); and (4) a **landscape-shaping force** (East African pastoral burning).

## Semantic History

Ethnography long treated nomadic pastoralism as a distinct “stage” or ecological niche. Archaeology complicated the picture: animal management grades from hunting through corralling and village herding to full nomadism; plants and animals need not domesticate together; cattle may precede crops in North Africa (Wendorf/Nabta claims as of Mithen 2003). Mithen uses pastoralism comparatively without a single evolutionary ladder.

## Operationalizations

- **Faunal profiles:** high sheep/goat percentages; smaller body size than wild; slaughter-age peaks (e.g. Beidha goats ~80% of bones, kill peak ~2 years — meat-oriented, not milk, as Mithen reports).
- **Architecture and dung:** pens, corrals; compacted dung layers with fodder (Uan Afuda Barbary sheep management ~7000 BC — management short of true domestication).
- **Art and ritual:** Saharan “pastoral” rock-art period with cattle scenes after round-head figures (Mori via Mithen); cattle as social/ritual beings among Acacus herders after ~6500 BC.
- **Mobility signatures:** seasonal sites among ruins (‘Ain Ghazal as herder gathering place after town collapse); ash mounds with hoofprints (Utnur, Deccan).
- **Landscape proxies:** grassland structure altered by burning and grazing since ~2000 BC in East Africa (as of Mithen 2003).

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **Southwest Asia:** [[abu-hureyra|Abu Hureyra]] switch from gazelle hunting to sheep/goat herding; Beidha and PPNB goat husbandry; Jordan valley **nomadic pastoralism** after PPNB economic collapse; Zagros/Iranian plateau expansion seeking grazing and arable land after ~8000 BC.
- **South Asia:** [[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] local zebu and sheep trajectories; Bagor hybrid wild+domestic ovicaprids; southern India farming mainly as **cattle pastoralism** ~3000 BC.
- **Africa:** possible early cattle domestication at [[nabta-playa|Nabta Playa]] (~9000 BC claim, preceding West Asian cattle ~7500 BC consensus — contested, attributed); sheep/goats from West Asia at Nabta ~5800 BC; Sahara aridification driving southward pastoral spread; Merimde/Fayum ovicaprids of West Asian ultimate origin.
- **Europe:** LBK cattle emphasis; Scandinavian early farmers with cattle and ephemeral dwellings after Ertebølle; far-northern hunting giving way to pastoralism by ~1000 BC in Mithen’s sketch.
- **Mesopotamia:** sheep/goat flocks supporting craft towns (Yarim Tepe); specialized hunting supply sites in a broader agro-pastoral system.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Management ≠ domestication** (Barbary sheep corrals produce no domestic cousin — Mithen).
- Regional **priority claims** (African vs West Asian cattle) are contested and date-stamped as of 2003.
- Pastoralism coexists with farming, hunting, and fishing; pure types are rare.
- Ethnographic “nomad” images can be projected anachronistically onto early village herders.
- Four-way non-identity: herding package ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ ethnic pastoralist identity.

## Barth densification (1961) — the ethnographic benchmark

*(Attributed — [[barth-fredrik|Barth]], *[[barth-nomads-south-persia-1961|Nomads of South Persia]]*, fieldwork 1957–58.)* The classic ethnographic account of full pastoral nomadism: [[basseri|Basseri]] sheep/goat herding on a ~300-mile migration circuit (il-rah) in Fars, with household herds ~100 head, a ~60-head viability threshold, and ~100% annual return on herd capital at 1958 prices. Barth argued the migratory cycle was biologically necessary for the nomad sheep strain (70–80% reported mortality when kept year-round in one zone) and — against "general reports" — that the migrations themselves caused no particular livestock losses. His [[sedentarization]] mechanism (exit at both wealth extremes) made pastoral nomadism a demographically open system continuously exchanging population with sedentary society, and his Ch. X model tied degrees of political centralization to an ecological/trade gradient (Qashqai hierarchy → Basseri chiefship → acephalous Kerman-margin tribes). Ethnographic benchmark for the "nomad" pole of this page's spectrum — dated to 1957–58, not a timeless type.

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* ch.17.)*

- **Neolithic pastoral nomadism** treated as a relatively late outcome of the Neolithic process, not the origin of herding: a **post-village choice** combining dual criteria (animal mobility + residential mobility).
- Not invented only in arid zones; arid facies (Sinai, Black Desert, Qdeir) participate in a wider PPNB world.
- Pure pastoral nomadism may post-date mixed cultivator-herder villages; “mastery of space” via gregarious stock is part of Cauvin’s power-over-nature framing of the Revolution’s effects.
- Keep distinct from ethnographic Bedouin projection and from PPNA village herding experiments.

### Sherratt — plough and pastoralism (1981)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*, Ch.6–7, 10–11. )* Pastoral sectors expand with fallow created by plough cultivation and with steppe interfluve use after cart/horse complexes; woolly sheep intensify landholding stakes. Hungarian Plain and SE European sequences show settlement dispersal and cattle-export alliance systems co-varying with secondary-products horizons. [[pastoralism]] here is village-linked and exchange-linked, not only nomadic.

### Anthony densification (2007) — steppe herding and wagon pastoralism

*(Attributed — [[anthony-david-w|David W. Anthony]], *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* 2007.)* Pontic-Caspian and Eurasian-steppe densification; economic claims attributed, not ethnographic present.

- **Steppe as barrier then corridor:** Anthony argues the Eurasian steppe belt was long a barrier to idea/technology transmission until horse, sheep/cattle herding, and later wheel/wagons turned it into a corridor of transcontinental communication (R1-003). Wagons enable mobile “family farms” and deep-steppe residence (R1-079).
- **Rapid herding adoption ~5200–5000 BCE:** Key forager groups on the Dnieper adopt cattle-and-sheep herding; the package diffuses rapidly to Volga and Ural — economic, ritual, and political transformation Anthony links to dialects ancestral to PIE (R2-060). Cattle/sheep convert “useless” grass into wool, felt, milk products, meat, and wealth; boom-bust volatility and stock theft drive conflict (R3-006). Cattle herding and status hierarchy appear together; elite funerals sacrifice cattle, sheep, and horses (R2-078).
- **PIE economic lexicon:** Speakers reconstructed as farmers *and* stockbreeders — bull, cow, ox, ram, ewe, lamb, pig; dairy terms; wool shearing/weaving; ard and yoke; movable wealth *peḱu-; cattle-driving/raiding verbs (R2-016). Male-centered cattle-raising worldview in creation myths (R3-002). Cow sacred as supreme milk producer vs mare/goat yields (Vainshtein via Anthony) (R4-075).
- **Yamnaya pastoralism typology:** Rejects dependency models that require agricultural states: if “nomadic pastoralism” means high residential mobility pastoral economy, it appears with [[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]] **before** states; Iron Age militarized Scythian form is later (R4-069). Shishlina: seasonal moves valley pastures ↔ plateau 15–50 km; sacrifices in graves sheep/goat-dominant (65%), cattle 15%, horse 8% (R4-071). Dairy-rich diet markers: 0 caries in 428 adult middle-Volga Yamnaya-Poltavka teeth; cribra orbitalia peak; lactose-tolerance mutation argued west of Urals ~4600–2800 BCE (R4-074 — date-stamped as of Anthony’s 2007 genetic summary).
- **Riding and herd scale:** Horses first as winter meat, then riding for herd management; by 4800–4600 BCE in Khvalynsk funerals with domesticates; riding likely by ~4200–4000 BCE in raids (R5-070; R3-069 economic effects).
- **Later horizons:** Usatovo sheep/goat economy with horses and coastal trade (R5-012); Srubnaya herding/gathering without cereal agriculture east of Don at Krasnosamarskoe (R5-058).
- **Anti-parasite position:** Average nomad “probably ate better than the average agricultural peasant”; steppe metal production and peaceful frontier integration alongside predatory episodes (R5-071). Herding package ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ ethnic “nomad” identity.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017; pastoralism as nonstate/barbarian hinterland and fiscal illegibility, not as a Neolithic origin stage.)*

- **Spectrum, not subspecies.** Scott rejects essentialist “subspecies” of subsistence (hunting, foraging, pastoralism, farming): same people practiced all four, combined them for millennia; activities bleed into one another. Mesopotamian alluvium peoples already mixed agriculture, pastoralism, and hunting-gathering; mosaic diversity was safety. Apparent “regression” to pastoralism/foraging after state abandonment may be emancipation or prudent adaptation.
- **Fiscal sterile hinterland.** Specialized pastoralism disperses people and products; Ottomans (pastoral-founded) still hard-pressed to tax herders; Lindner via Scott: sedentary-paradise revenue dream “had no place for pastoral nomads.” Nonstate spaces—pastoralism, foraging, slash-and-burn—are stigmatized as “barbarian” in state discourse; Chinese “raw” barbarians beyond Yellow/Yangzi grain basins.
- **Two-way continuum ([[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]]).** Nomad–sedentary connection as two-way street under environmental/social pressure (Lattimore Han–Mongol parallel).
- **Shadow empire / horseback states.** Politically mobilized mounted pastoralist confederations as strongest competitors for agrarian surplus—Barfield’s “shadow empire” or “state in waiting”; robust cases as “horseback states” (vs hunters/swiddeners who only “nibble”). Kradin-type thesis via Scott: degree of nomad centralization proportional to neighboring agricultural civilization; shadow empires need sedentary manpower/revenue depots and may fall when the host collapses. Contest with states for the grain–manpower surplus module. Links: [[nonstate-spaces]] · [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] · [[state-formation]] · [[societal-collapse]].

## Barker 2006 densification (*The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006; Europe + synthesis-layer pastoralism only from assigned ranges. Genetics as of Barker 2006 / mid-2000s.)*

- **Victorian ladder foil:** Victorian prehistorians (as Barker summarizes) treated hunting → **pastoral** → agricultural as an invariable progress sequence; late 19th-century evidence (no pastoral phase in North America; Swiss lake villages with cereals + animals) undermined universal herding intermediate stage, but hunting/farming dichotomies remained embedded.
- **European Neolithic herding within agro-pastoral packages:** LBK fauna cattle-dominant with pigs and sheep; ceramic sieves for cheese (secondary products); seasonal stock moves alluvium↔loess; Early British/Irish Neolithic often mobile forager-farmers with **herding** and ‘tethered mobility’ around ancestral monuments (Thomas via Barker) — pastoralism as component of mixed systems, not a separate evolutionary stage.
- **Against LGM “proto-herding”:** Magdalenian reindeer herding/herd-following and Bahn horse-taming claims met strong scepticism; consensus (as of Barker 2006) is specialization and concentrated seasonal harvesting, **not** Lapp-like herding. Domestic **dog** already late Pleistocene companionship/hunting/transport component.
- **NW Europe mobility inversion:** Striking feature in parts of NW Europe: sedentary or semi-sedentary foraging land occupation transformed into **more mobile** foraging-cum-farming systems with ceremonial monuments as fixed points — contrary to traditional “farming settles people” theory.
- **Sahara / Africa synthesis note (theory-adjacent):** Sahara early Holocene foragers experimented controlling Barbary sheep under first aridification (risk reduction), reverted to foraging when wetter, then with irreversible desiccation committed to cattle/sheep/goat **pastoralism** and small-scale sorghum — pastoral commitment as historically contingent, not ladder stage.
- **Europe demic foil:** Barker holds many Mesolithic foragers practised behaviours presaging Eurasian **agro-pastoralism** long before contact; piecemeal adoption of package components including livestock often first for social value of exotic foods/livestock rather than staple role.
