# Organic National State

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|The Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]* (1986, Chs. 13–15), [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] distinguishes the **organic national state** (or **organic state**) from medieval **territorially coordinating** / federal states. An organic state is near-unitary: dominant classes (property owners as a *class*, not feudal household dynasties) and central institutions (court and/or parliament) form a coordinated organism capable of high [[infrastructural-power|infrastructural penetration]] and fiscal-military mobilization across the realm. England’s path toward a **class-nation** of gentry, nobility, burghers, and placemen is his paradigm; constitutional routes are more organic than most absolutisms.

> "By 1450 it was a territorially coordinating, but not a unitary, 'organic' state." / "The second phase of the modern state began to appear, the organic state." / "Organic states, especially constitutional states, were novel in history over such extensive territories."

## Semantic History

Mann’s term is not the organicism of romantic nationalism alone; it is organizational — classes and state fuse as an extensive political organism within a diplomatically regulated [[multi-power-actor-civilization]]. Related later vocabularies (nation-state, national bourgeoisie) should not silently equate; Mann dates full mass “nations” largely to industrial societies (Vol. II). Precursor: late-medieval *Ständestaat* / king-in-Parliament as coordination without organic unity (two territorial levels: king and local magnate). Constitutional and absolutist regimes are **subtypes of a single form**: weak state relative to powerful civil-society groups, increasingly coordinating them toward an organic class-nation.

## Operationalizations

Indicators in Mann’s narrative: standardized language/culture bounded by the state (~1600 England virtually complete as cultural-linguistic unit); public legislation and pomp of the public monarch; magnates great *as a class* rather than household dynasties; dual court/Parliament arenas forcing state-bounded roles; fiscal extraction and permanent war state; decline of dual king/magnate territorial federalism; religion (esp. Protestant national churches) reinforcing political-network boundaries. Social legislation read as collective organization of dominant groups, not greater [[despotic-power]] over society.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ch. 13 tail / Ch. 14** — Three forces transform English state: war costs, inflation, coordinating role for a ruling class approaching the organic phase. Military Revolution (~1540–1660) enhances territorially centralized power *and* commodity forms; fiscal vs mobilized paths map onto constitutional vs absolutist regimes. Elizabethan “nation” ideology: gentlemen of the counties *are* the nation in Parliament — one class fully, universally, politically organized; masses excluded and passive. Settlements of 1660/1688 cement organic unity; Civil War and 1688 are **failed royalist coups**, not social revolutions, in Mann’s narrative; “the nation was a class.”
- **Absolutism contrast** — Absolutist states not infrastructurally stronger than constitutional ones; remaining difference mainly despotic power. Constitutionalism reinforces organic capitalist class; absolutism blocks or crosscuts it; war tests infrastructural capacity (Spain, France). European monarch no ancient compulsory-cooperation emperor; private property deeply embedded.
- **Ch. 15 conclusions** — Early agricultural dynamic under [[normative-pacification]] harnessed to (1) capitalism, (2) modern organic state, (3) competitive multistate civilization. States supplant Christendom as main normative-pacification instruments after schism. By mid-18th c.: civil society bounded by national state; real-life capitalism presupposes military and political power; major actors = property owners and class-nations. Paradox: puny late-feudal/early-modern state (<1% GNP if lucky) decisively structured modern national parameters.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ideal-typical; English path over-weighted; “organic” can be misread as naturalizing the nation — keep as Mann’s attributed construct. Gender and popular exclusion are structural in his account (masses passive; franchise tiny). Challenges to class theories of absolutism (Anderson, Wallerstein, Mousnier) are Mann’s positions — record as such.
