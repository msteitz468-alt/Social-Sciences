# Earmarking (of money)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]*, [[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]] treats **earmarking** as the set of social practices by which people and organizations **mark** monies — assigning restricted uses, separate storage, moral labels (clean/dirty, gift/wage/relief), timing rules, and relational meanings — so that money is never only a homogeneous medium of exchange.

Earmarking is the mechanism that produces [[multiple-monies|multiple monies]]. Nigel Dodd (2017 foreword) notes that "earmarking" altered sociological language for qualitative differences among monies classically viewed as homogeneous.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Zelizer 1994 | Household, gift, and welfare earmarks in the U.S. c. 1870–1930; earmarking as social process, not individual quirk |
| Official/state earmarking | Ration coupons, scrip, savings bonds, restricted relief cash, food stamps; also state *suppression* of private earmarks while creating its own |
| Recipients' **counter-earmarking** | Poor clients' and families' systems that subvert or rework official restrictions |
| Dodd 2017 | Three interpretations: (1) meanings without changing objective form; (2) practices modifying use/allocation/appearance; (3) production of new currencies |
| Later Zelizer | [[relational-work]] — earmarking as matching media to ties; policy applications (EITC form; Financial Diaries) |

## Operationalizations

Dimensions Zelizer repeatedly uses for domestic and other monies (attributed):

1. **Allocation** — who gives whom what form of claim (gift, entitlement, dole, wage).
2. **Timing** — dole (ask each time) vs allowance (regular) vs lump ritual transfers.
3. **Uses** — housekeeping vs personal pocket money vs ceremonial/burial spending.
4. **Quantity** — norms of "proper" amount not reducible to productivity or market price alone.

**Techniques** (Ch. 7 catalogue, attributed): separate storage (jars, envelopes, tin cans, accounts); physical modification or special currencies; restricted convertibility; labeling and moral ranking; third-party intermediaries (caseworkers, Christmas Clubs, insurance agents).

## Applications

- Housewife's money (dole → allowance → consumer-manager models); class-inverted working-class patterns.
- Tips, bonuses, Christmas gifts of cash; immigrant remittances and ritual monies.
- Charitable cash with strings; mothers' pensions; casework budgets; burial insurance as contested earmark.
- "Dirty money" illustrations (e.g., prostitution earnings budgeted differently from welfare income — attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

Earmarks can **enforce inequality** (women's monies as dependency; welfare restrictions on choice) even while they express meaning — Zelizer states both sides. Duration of earmarks is cultural and social, not fixed by psychology alone. Not identical to behavioral economics' "mental accounting," though related at the surface of restricted fungibility.
