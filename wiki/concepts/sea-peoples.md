# Sea Peoples

## Definition as Coined

**Sea Peoples** is a **modern scholarly label** for diverse groups that Egyptian sources of the late New Kingdom describe as attacking Egypt by land and sea. [[cline-eric-h|Cline]] (2014) stresses that the Egyptians did **not** use the collective term "Sea Peoples"; they named separate groups — including Peleset, Tjekker, Shekelesh, Shardana (Sherden), Danuna, and Weshesh — portrayed with varied dress and gear, suggesting multiple origins rather than a single ethnos.

## Semantic History

- **Ancient (emic fragments):** Egyptian temple and stele inscriptions under Merneptah (~1207 BCE) and Ramses III (year 8 ~1177 BCE) list named foreign groups and describe battles in the Nile delta and related campaigns.
- **Modern (etic):** Nineteenth- and twentieth-century scholarship bundled these groups as "Sea Peoples" and often cast them as the primary agents of Eastern Mediterranean destruction. Later work treats the label as a convenient etic category whose **origins, unity, and causal role** remain contested.
- **Migration of the term:** popular history often reifies "the Sea Peoples" as a people; specialist practice increasingly treats them as a coalition or set of movements with unknown/mixed homelands (Sicily/Sardinia/Italy, Aegean, western Anatolia, Cyprus, Eastern Mediterranean — all proposed).

## Operationalizations

- **Textual:** named groups in Egyptian inscriptions; letters from Ugarit and other sites mentioning enemies/ships (context-specific).
- **Archaeological:** destruction horizons roughly contemporary with the texts — **association, not identity**.
- **Ethnic equations:** e.g., Peleset ≈ Philistines — argued from later biblical/near-eastern and Philistine material culture, not proven by the Egyptian label alone. Enforce four-way non-identity (people ≠ pottery ≠ language ≠ genes) when making such links.

## Applications

Central to narratives of [[late-bronze-age-collapse]]. Cline uses the 1177 BCE Egyptian confrontation as chronological shorthand while denying Sea Peoples monocausality.

Additional positions Cline reports:

- **Two waves / multiphase:** Merneptah ~1207 (Shardana, Shekelesh, Eqwesh, Lukka, Teresh among named groups) and Ramses III ~1177; Finkelstein: process lasting to ~1130, not a single event.
- **Victims and opportunists:** some groups as displaced peoples settling after others’ collapses; Yasur-Landau: many migrants as humbler post-palatial opportunists, not prime cause; Philistine urbanization as hybridization rather than pure military takeover at several sites.
- **Structural (Susan Sherratt):** Sea Peoples as phenomenon of trade evolution subverting palaces ([[sherratt-susan]]).
- **Outside Egypt:** Shikila/Shekelesh “who live on ships” in a Hittite/Ugarit-related letter — rare non-Egyptian ethnonym match.
- **Peleset ≈ Philistines** is the firmest later identification Cline allows; other name-equations (Shekelesh–Sicily, Shardana–Sardinia, Eqwesh–Achaeans, etc.) remain hypotheses with reverse-migration counter-schools.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Homeland unknown; no departure site identified (as of Cline 2014); scholarly majority (via Cline) leans Aegean/western Anatolia/Cyprus more than Western Mediterranean for many groups.
- Risk of circular reasoning: sites destroyed “by Sea Peoples” because Sea Peoples are defined as the destroyers of those sites; settlers ≠ destroyers.
- Diverse iconography and names undermine single-origin invasion models.
- Hittite heartland: little secure Sea Peoples activity — internal collapse arguments stronger there.
