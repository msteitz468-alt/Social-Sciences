# La Pensée Bourgeoise (Western Society as Culture)

## Definition as Coined

**"La Pensée Bourgeoise"** — "bourgeois thought," a deliberate echo of Lévi-Strauss's *La Pensée Sauvage* — is the title of chapter 4 of [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976, attributed) and names his demonstration that **Western capitalist society is itself organized by a symbolic/cultural code**, not by pure material utility. The analysis is offered "by way of a challenge" to the conventional wisdom that the West, unlike "primitive" societies, is governed by the utilitarian postulates of economic science. Its thesis: "the uniqueness of bourgeois society consists not in the fact that the economic system escapes symbolic determination, but that the economic symbolism is structurally determining."

## Semantic History

Sahlins reworks the base/superstructure and economy/society/ideology divisions into a **structural-comparative** model: cultures differ not by which subsystem is causally dominant but by which institutional domain is the *privileged site of symbolic production* from which a classificatory grid is thrown over the whole culture. "The peculiarity of Western culture is the institutionalization of the process in and as the production of goods, by comparison with a 'primitive' world where the locus of symbolic differentiation remains social relations, principally kinship relations." His compressed formula: **"money is to the West what kinship is to the Rest."** Because in the West functional spheres (market, state, church) are structurally separated and all subordinated to the economy, base/superstructure Marxism "appears true" of the West specifically — a "false consciousness" that mistakes a local institutional design for a universal law (see [[practical-reason]], [[historical-materialism]]).

## Operationalizations

The demonstration decodes two everyday American systems as symbolic classifications (all **attributed illustrations**, not wiki-voice claims about American diet or dress):

- **The food system.** The edibility of animals follows a cultural classification, not nutritional or economic logic: cattle and pigs are edible, dogs and horses tabu, and cuts are ranked by social value (steak vs. tongue/entrails). Sahlins anchors the point in Saussure's *sheep*/*mouton*: English has butchery pairs *pork*/*beef* (Norman) beside the live animals *pig*/*cow*, yet no such pair for *cheval* or *dog* — "history without structure would not seem to explain the classification." Economic value tracks a meaningful classification; the classification does not track economic value.
- **The clothing system.** Dress is a code — a "language" of distinctions by season, occasion, sex, age, and class; the garment is a sign whose value is differential (crediting advertising's own folk-taxonomy, e.g. Stephen Baker's gendered object-symbolism: wool masculine, silk feminine). Sahlins calls the cross-domain homologies "symbolic synapses" (crediting James Boon), stressing they are "the true armature of the cultural order," not the analyst's invention.

Value itself is read through Saussure: "exchange-value is acquired by producing objects that are not the same as other products... Cadillacs as opposed to Chevrolets, suits as opposed to overalls, steak as opposed to entrails." Production for gain is "the production of a symbolically significant difference"; Saussure's value passage, Sahlins says, "deserves the place in the study of Western economy" that Ranapiri's account of the Maori *[[hau]]* holds in anthropological economics.

## Applications

The chapter is the empirical proof of [[cultural-reason]]'s claim that "it is culture which constitutes utility," and the ground of the **"bourgeois totemism"** thesis (see [[totemism]]): where classic totemism uses differences among natural species to mark differences among social groups, market society *inverts* the relation — "every conceivable distinction of society is put to the service of another declension of objects," social distinctions mapped onto manufactured-product differences. "Fetishism and totemism: the most refined creations of the civilized mind."

## Critiques and Limitations

Sahlins's capstone claim is that the West is not "radically different" from "the savage mind" — "we are just as logical, philosophical, and meaningful as they are" — its distinctiveness lying in a *mode* of symbolic production institutionalized and disguised as economic growth, which "conspires to conceal the symbolic operating of the system." The demonstration is programmatic and illustrative rather than systematically evidenced (its examples are chosen to persuade), and the food/clothing analyses are Sahlins's own — attributed, not established findings about American culture. The framework's Marxist stakes are pursued on [[historical-materialism]] and [[structural-marxism]].
