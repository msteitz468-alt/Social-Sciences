# Domus Complex

## Definition as Coined

In *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017), [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] uses the **domus** (from the household) for a unique coevolving concentration of **tilled fields, seed and grain stores, people, and domestic animals** — an unprecedented multi-species evolutionary module that also attracted thousands of uninvited hangers-on. He calls such locations **late-Neolithic multispecies resettlement camps**: ideal conditions for later state making, yet involving more drudgery than hunting and gathering and “not at all good for your health.”

The place of the *domus* and fixed residence in the civilizational narrative is, Scott argues, “so deep as to be invisible; fish don’t talk about water!” There is no warrant for reading modern sedentary “givens” back as a universal human aspiration. There is also no single “magic moment” when *Homo sapiens* crosses a line from foraging to agriculture — planting is one minor event in a deep history of landscape modification starting with *Homo erectus* and fire.

## Semantic History

- **Latin *domus*:** household / house.
- **Archaeological “domus”:** Neolithic house societies and residential packages in specialist literature.
- **Scott 2017 package:** Political-ecological emphasis — multi-species entanglement, labor discipline, epidemiological storm, and inversion of agency (who domesticates whom). Domestication expands beyond planting/pastoralism to **whole-environment** domestication; the preindustrial preeminent tool was fire, not the plough.
- **Bridge to statecraft:** The domus supplies the grain-and-manpower module that the embryonic state later harnesses ([[grain-hypothesis]], [[legibility]]). Expansive domestication, Scott provocatively suggests, might apply not only to fire, plants, and animals but also to slaves, state subjects, and women in the patriarchal family — with Algaze’s claim that “Uruk urban institutions, in turn, domesticated humans.”

## Operationalizations

Scott’s module is read through multiple evidence classes (as he synthesizes them):

| Dimension | Indicators Scott emphasizes (2017) |
|---|---|
| Architecture / storage | Fixed residence, granaries, yards, gardens, orchards as colonizing “domus module” |
| Flora | Founder crops (einkorn/emmer, barley; pulses; flax); nonshattering indehiscent spikes; “floral basket cases” dependent on human attentions |
| Fauna | Sheep/goat as first noncommensal domesticates; restricted diet/mobility; age–sex profiles of bone finds as management signature |
| Commensals | Sparrows, mice, rats, crows; quasi-invited dogs, pigs, cats; microparasite trains (fleas, ticks, lice, etc.) |
| Independent “domus effect” | Uninvited commensals show some of the same physical changes as full domesticates |
| Human labor / body | Women’s bent-under toes and deformed knees from grinding (~9 kya grain villages); cereal annual/daily metronome as early “civilizing process” |
| Pathology | Crowding, feces concentration, zoonoses; iron-deficiency anemia signatures on grain diets; livestock brain-size reduction and neoteny under domestication |

**Self-domestication (speculative register):** Scott proposes that humans are products of self-domestication under sedentism, crowding, and cereal diets — intended and unintended — analogous to other domus species. He follows Helen Leach on a possible “distinctive syndrome” of domestication (size, tooth reduction, facial shortening) from a shared environment and softer diets (humans, dogs, pigs). He cautions: only ~240 human generations since first agriculture and ~160 since widespread — sweeping biological conclusions are premature; limbic/emotional-reactivity parallels remain unproven.

## Applications

- **Revision of the progress narrative:** Domus life as deskilling and contraction of attention to the natural world, diet, space, and perhaps ritual breadth — despite contributions to large-scale societies. Forager multi-tempo knowledge vs cereal monoculture’s metronome.
- **Epidemiology:** Sedentism made *Homo sapiens* more of a “herd animal”; multi-mammalian aggregation generated new zoonoses; of ~1,400 known human pathogens, Scott reports ~800–900 as zoonotic; measles, smallpox, influenza, etc., as “civilizational effects” of urbanism and agriculture (as of his secondary base, 2017).
- **Fertility paradox:** High infant mortality among early agriculturalists compensated by sedentism-driven fertility gains (earlier weaning on soft grain foods, earlier menarche, extended reproductive life) — demographic overtaking, not superior health.
- **Fragile genetic foundation:** Handful of crops and livestock; simplified landscape needing constant defense; continuous subsidy from “excluded nature” (wood, fish, game, wild plants); collapse-prone under drought, pests, or disease.
- **State capture:** Embryonic states batten onto dense grain-and-manpower concentrations; the domus is the preexisting nucleus for elaborating a state — not created by the state. Ritual calendar (ceremonial ploughing, harvest rites, grain gods) reorganized around the staple once cereals dominate.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Synthetic label**, not a specialist archaeological culture type — densified from Scott’s 2017 synthesis of secondary literature.
- **Speculative human-domestication claims** are flagged as such by Scott; do not promote to wiki voice.
- **Agency inversion** (Pollan’s potato; Evans-Pritchard on Nuer–cattle mutual parasitism) is rhetorical-analytic, not a formal evolutionary model.
- **Four-way non-identity:** The domus is an etic agro-ecological module, not an ethnos, language family, or genetic population. Do not silently equate Neolithic “package” distributions with peoples.
- **Disease and paleopathology figures** inherit Scott’s secondary sources; date-stamp and keep attributed pending specialist reanalysis.
