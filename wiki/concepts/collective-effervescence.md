# Collective Effervescence

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms of Religious Life]]* (1912), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] argues that when individuals assemble and a common passion is shared, the group enters a state of **extraordinary stimulation** — he writes of **general effervescence**, **effervescent social environments**, and **collective effervescence** — in which people feel acted upon by forces outside themselves. In the midst of an assembly, the individual feels "dominated by a moral force which is greater than he"; after collective effervescence, people "believe themselves transported into an entirely different world from the one they have before their eyes." Out of this effervescence itself, Durkheim claims, "the religious idea seems to be born" (attributed). Periods of assembly (corroboree, ceremony) contrast with dispersed, low-intensity daily life; religion lives especially in the former and must be periodically renewed by the cult.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Durkheim 1912 | Ritual assembly produces heightened collective emotion and the experience of sacred force; society "creates" the gods in these moments |
| Later sociology of religion / ritual studies | Standard term for emotional amplification in crowds, festivals, political rallies, and worship |
| Cultural sociology / social movements | Extended to secular assemblies (reception not yet ingested) |
| Collins 2004 (*[[collins-interaction-ritual-chains-2004|IRC]]*) | Re-specified as the intense shared-emotion phase of [[interaction-ritual|interaction ritual]] (mutual focus + entrainment); outcome long-term residue is individual [[emotional-energy|emotional energy]], not only group sacredness. Local, stratified, conflictual memberships — not global moral integration of whole societies |

## Operationalizations

Not measured by Durkheim. Later proxies: physiological synchrony, self-report awe, crowd density × shared focus, ritual frequency. Collins (2004) treats collective effervescence as process intensity within the IR model and proposes multi-channel measures for the related EE continuum (see [[emotional-energy]]).

## Applications

- Explains how the totemic emblem concentrates force: the emblem is the tangible focus of assembled energy.
- Links integration (cf. *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]*) to ritual rhythm of assembly and dispersal.
- Positive and piacular cults are techniques for producing or repairing collective states.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Mechanism is phenomenological and ethnographic-illustrative, not experimentally isolated.
- Risk of circularity: sacred explained by effervescence, effervescence identified by sacred outcomes.
- Colonial descriptions of Australian ceremonies are Durkheim's sources — period, mediated.
