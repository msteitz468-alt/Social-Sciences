## Definition as Coined

Craftsmanship is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s idealized model of intrinsically meaningful
work, built as a deliberate ideal-type against which modern [[alienation]] can be measured.
Mills specified six features (attributed): (1) no ulterior motive beyond the product and
process; (2) daily work-details meaningful because tied to the product; (3) the worker
controls his own working action; (4) the work develops the worker's own skills and self;
(5) no split between work and play or culture; (6) the craftsman's livelihood infuses his
whole mode of living.

Crucially, Mills marked the model as a heuristic, not a historical claim: "we do not mean to
imply that there ever was a community in which work carried all these meanings... we entertain
serious doubts that this is so." The model "has become an anachronism"; Mills used it "as an
explicit ideal in terms of which we can summarize the working conditions" of modern,
especially white-collar, workers.

## Semantic History

Mills drew the intrinsic-work tradition from Ruskin (the pre-capitalist artisan; profit for
its own sake as morally corrosive) and Marx (individuals' nature depending on the conditions
of their production), and it is kin to [[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]]'s [[instinct-of-workmanship]].
Mills's own later formulation of the [[cheerful-robot]] and his methodological essay *The
Sociological Imagination* (1959) — which closes on craftsmanship as a model for intellectual
work — extend the concept from manual to mental labor.

The fullest statement of the intellectual version is the Appendix to *The Sociological
Imagination*, **"On Intellectual Craftsmanship,"** among the most widely assigned methodological
essays in the discipline. There Mills argued (attributed) that "scholarship is a choice of how to
live as well as a choice of career; ... the intellectual workman forms his own self as he works
toward the perfection of his craft" — so that "craftsmanship is the center of yourself and you are
personally involved in every intellectual product." He opposed **craft to methodology**: the
signature precept "Let every man be his own methodologist; let every man be his own theorist; let
theory and method again become part of the practice of a craft" rejects procedural method-worship
(Gitlin glosses the distinction: "Methodology was rigor mortis... Craft was work done with respect
for materials, clarity about objective, and a sense of the high drama and stakes of intellectual
life"). Mills's practical apparatus — keeping a **"file"** that fuses life-experience with
professional work, and the techniques for stimulating the [[sociological-imagination]] (see that
page) — makes the craftsman, not the technician, the bearer of good social science.

## Operationalizations

Craftsmanship is not measured; it functions as a contrastive ideal. Mills warned (via Henri
De Man) against assuming that actual employees consciously measure their work against it —
the destruction of craft is "slow historical fact... not of psychological relevance to the
present generation," since statistically their parents were not craftsmen either. The concept
is applied by identifying how far modern work departs from each of the six features.

## Applications

The ideal organizes Mills's chapter on Work: rationalization has "expropriated" not only
ownership but "rationality itself" from work; the salaried intellectual and Hollywood
screenwriter have "responsibility... but no real authority"; the assembly-line office
dissolves whole-task competence into fragmented operations. It connects to the
[[the-big-split]] (work judged by leisure's standards) and remains a touchstone in the
sociology of work and the critique of deskilling.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because Mills himself labels craftsmanship an anachronistic ideal-type, its analytic value
depends on the honesty of the contrast rather than any empirical claim about the past. Jacoby
(2002) argued that Mills's lament for craftsmanship risks elitism — "scorning the pleasures of
the unwashed" — and reads at times "more Matthew Arnold than Max Weber." The model's romantic
lineage (Ruskin) is both its rhetorical power and its vulnerability.
