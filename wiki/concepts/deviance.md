# Deviance

## Definition as Coined

The analytic concept of deviance runs through Merton's "Social Structure and Anomie" (1938); [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s Chapter VII of *The Social System* (1951) gives it a systematic action-theoretic definition, in two complementary registers:

- **from the individual-actor reference point** — "a motivated tendency for an actor to behave in contravention of one or more institutionalized normative patterns";
- **from the interactive-system reference point** — "the tendency on the part of one or more of the component actors to behave in such a way as to disturb the equilibrium of the interactive process."

Parsons treated the system-level (equilibrium-disturbance) conception as "the more important perspective for the analysis of social systems." Deviance and conformity are **relative** to the system referred to: judgment cannot be made "without specific reference to the system or sub-system to which it applies."

Parsons's genetic account is the **vicious circle**: a disturbance in alter's behavior frustrates ego's expectations, generating an *ambivalent* motivational structure (an alienative and a conformative [[need-disposition]] together) which, absent counteracting [[social-control]], tends to escalate. He derived a fourfold classification (crossing conformative/alienative with active/passive) that, he claimed, restates from the motivational side **Merton's** anomie typology — Merton's "innovation" and "ritualism" mapping to the compulsively conformative types, "rebellion" and "retreatism" to the alienative types — and an eightfold typology when object-focus/pattern-focus is added.

## Semantic History

Parsons's deviance is anchored to his equilibrium model, so "deviance" carries the sense of *disturbance of a normative order* — the framing later labeling theorists (Becker, Lemert) and conflict theorists rejected, arguing deviance is not a property of the act but of the social reaction that labels it. Illness, for Parsons, is a type of deviance — "predominantly passive-alienative," combined with a conformative dependency component — which is the bridge to his [[sick-role]] analysis. This absorption of illness into deviance is one of the most cited and most contested moves of the theory (see [[medical-model]]).

### Goffman, *Stigma* Ch 5 (1963; attributed)

[[goffman-erving|Goffman]] did not equate [[stigma]] with deviance. He treated stigma processes as **general features of social life** (normal and stigmatized as perspectives/roles everyone plays in some connexions) while mapping how a possible field of "deviance" might be bounded. **Social deviants** — persons in open, somewhat rebellious refusal of social place regarding basic institutions (family, age-grade, sex-role division, legitimate employment with a single ratified personal identity, class and race segregation), gathered into a sub-community (**deviant community**) — would "presumably constitute [the] core" of such a field (period examples: prostitutes, drug addicts, delinquents, criminals, jazz musicians, bohemians, hobos, etc.). Neighbouring categories include the in-group deviant vs the isolate; licensed high-status and [[sick-role|sick]] roles as discounted (non-reidentifying) deviation; and the quietly disaffiliated hobbyist. Lemert is cited on the uselessness of simply counting the stigmatized. Labeling monographs (Becker et al.) are not yet ingested — file this as Goffman's boundary work, not as a full labeling-theory digest.

## Operationalizations

Parsons offers a typology, not a measure. His scheme is inferred from the motivational structure of role-disturbance, and connects (via the [[anomie]] link) to Merton's more directly operationalizable strain framework.

## Applications

- The foundation of Parsons's account of [[social-control]] as "the obverse of the theory of the genesis of deviant behavior tendencies."
- The theoretical basis of the [[sick-role]]: illness as *legitimated* deviance, managed by the physician as an agent of social control.
- Goffman's stigma scheme as a related but non-identical microsociology of spoiled identity (*[[goffman-stigma-1963|Stigma]]*; [[goffman-stigma-study]]).
- In anthropology, [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]]'s [[cultural-relativity-of-normality|cultural relativity of the normal]] (*[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*, 1934) recast deviance as a **fit** between temperament and a culture's [[cultural-configuration|configuration]]: the "abnormal" is whoever falls outside the segment of behaviour their culture rewards, so that trance, aggression, or the "berdache" role may be honored in one culture and pathologized in another. This anticipates the later labeling insight that deviance is conferred, not intrinsic.

## Critiques and Limitations

The equilibrium framing presupposes a normative consensus from which conduct "deviates," inheriting the standing objections to [[structural-functionalism]]. Labeling theory countered that deviance is conferred by reaction, not intrinsic to conduct; conflict theory countered that what counts as "deviant" encodes the interests of the powerful.
