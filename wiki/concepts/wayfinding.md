# Wayfinding

## Definition as Coined

In *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* Ch. 13, [[ingold-tim|Ingold]] contrasts **wayfinding** with **navigation** and map-use. Dwelling movement is "not between locations in space but between places in a network of coming and going that I call a region." In wayfinding, "people do not traverse the surface of a world whose layout is fixed in advance… Rather, they 'feel their way' through a world that is itself in motion." Mapping, on this view, is closer to narrative re-enactment of journeys than to reading a cartographic plot *(Ingold 2000, Ch. 13, attributed)*. Knowledge form: "we know as we go."

## Semantic History

Common-language and design "wayfinding" pre-exist. Ingold's contribution is the theoretical opposition to (1) cognitive-map models of spatial knowledge (Tolman lineage; Gell and others as discussed), and (2) the building-perspective split between mapmaking and map-using that treats indigenous knowledge as just another cartography. He aligns Micronesian *etak* and similar practices with temporal wayfinding rather than abstract grid navigation. Connects to [[taskscape]] (paths as taskscape made visible) and to stories that draw people into landscape rather than standing as maps of it (Ch. 3).

## Operationalizations

Ethnography of travel, hunting paths, pilgrimage; critique of mental-map experiments; design of physical wayfinding systems (adjacent applied field — different literature).

## Applications

Hunter-gatherer and pastoral mobility; landscape knowledge; colonial cartography vs local region-knowledge; Ch. 12 globe/sphere topology (global environment as world seen from without vs sphere lived from within).

## Critiques and Limitations

The wayfinding/navigation binary can be overdrawn — skilled navigators also dwell; maps enter many non-Western practices. Political economy of cartography (property, conquest, administration) needs analysis beyond phenomenology of movement.
