# Immodest Claims of Causality

## Definition as Coined

**Immodest claims of causality** is [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s term, developed across
*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999), for confident assertions
about *why* a disease is distributed as it is — advanced by clinicians, epidemiologists, and social
scientists alike — that mistake the effects of poverty and inequality for cultural difference or
individual behavior. His definitional passage (attributed): "scholars often weaken their contributions
to an understanding of infectious diseases by making 'immodest claims of causality' regarding the
distribution and course of these disorders. These claims are immodest because they are wrong or
misleading. They are immodest because they distract attention from the modest interventions that could
treat and, often enough, cure people... And they are immodest because they distract attention from the
preventable social disorder that exacerbates biological disorders."

Farmer's simplest version of the diagnostic: on any given disease, experts from different disciplines
reach "altogether incompatible conclusions," each "advanced with great confidence" — and "because
discrepant opinions about the same topic cannot all be correct, some are necessarily immodest claims of
causality."

## Semantic History

The concept originates as an object of the **sociology of knowledge**: Farmer treats competing causal
claims about [[hiv-aids|AIDS]] and [[tuberculosis]] as cultural artifacts to be analyzed, reading
scholarly journals and the popular press as evidence of how disciplines "make their object." It is the
critical hinge between his two positive commitments — **[[structural-violence]]** (the causation he
argues is really doing the work) and **[[biosocial-analysis]]** (the method that would keep claims
"modest" by holding biology and social structure together). In his Chapter 9 taxonomy Farmer names five
recurring pitfalls of sociomedical scholarship on the "new" tuberculosis:

1. **Conflating structural violence with cultural difference** — inflating a discipline's favored
   variable (culture, personality, price) into "an omnibus explanation."
2. **Minimizing the role of poverty and inequality.**
3. **Exaggeration of patient agency** — treating constrained people as freely choosing (the rhetoric
   of "noncompliance").
4. **Romanticism about "folk healing"** — valuing local healing for its "local roots" when it does not
   change TB case-fatality ("if folk healing worked, the world's wealthy would be monopolizing it").
5. **Persistence of insularity** — anthropologists resisting basic infectious-disease epidemiology.

## Operationalizations

Farmer identifies immodesty by a diagnostic contrast: a study whose *data* point to structural causes
but whose *conclusions* recommend cultural or educational remedies. His worked examples: the Nyamathi
et al. Los Angeles study, which found poor African American and Latina women already well-informed about
HIV yet concluded that "culturally sensitive education" was the answer; the Ellerbrock Florida study,
which omitted the words *poverty, racism, sexism, and powerlessness* though >90% of the women earned
under $10,000/year; and the pulmonologists' verdict that "patient non-compliance is the most serious
remaining problem in the control of tuberculosis." Against these, his own [[do-kay|Do Kay]] TB study is
built as a test: patients who shared "cultural" (sorcery) beliefs but differed in access to financial
support differed sharply in cure — isolating access, not belief, as the operative cause.

## Applications

The concept is the analytic spine of the [[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]] debate page,
and cross-cuts [[tuberculosis]] (the "noncompliance" critique) and [[hiv-aids]] (the "exotic"
culturalist AIDS literature). It also turns reflexively on Farmer's own discipline and on his own
earlier writing (see [[farmer-paul]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The term is `contested` and partly rhetorical: it labels an *error*, and labels made from one position
in a live dispute are not neutral. Farmer's own stance is not that culture is irrelevant — he insists
its causal weight varies by setting (potentially greater in wealthy societies, lesser where the binding
constraint is destitution) — but critics may read the "immodest" charge as itself a confident causal
claim in favor of the structural pole. Recorded in attributed voice as Farmer's critical concept, not a
wiki-voice verdict on any named study.
