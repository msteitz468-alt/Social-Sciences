# Socialization

## Definition as Coined

"Socialization" predates [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]], but Chapter VI of *The Social System* (1951) gives it a precise action-theoretic definition: the process by which an actor acquires, through learning, the orientations required for satisfactory functioning in a [[social-role|role]] not yet mastered. Parsons distinguished it sharply from [[social-control]]: socialization concerns the *learning* of role-expectations; social control concerns counteracting *deviance* from already-learned expectations.

Parsons broadened the term beyond its usual reference to child development: "the learning of any orientations of functional significance to the operation of a system of complementary role-expectations. In this sense, socialization … goes on throughout life." Childhood socialization is nonetheless paradigmatic because the major [[value-orientation]] patterns are "laid down" early and are among the most stable learned elements of personality — the "core" of what Parsons (crediting **Abram Kardiner**) called **basic personality structure**.

The infant's three enabling attributes are **plasticity** (capacity to learn alternative patterns), **sensitivity** (capacity to form attachments), and **dependency** (the "fulcrum" giving socializing agents leverage). Parsons enumerated five learning mechanisms (from the parallel *Values, Motives and Systems of Action*, with Shils): reinforcement-extinction, inhibition, substitution, imitation, and **identification** — "the most important of the learning mechanisms for the acquisition of value patterns." His core theorem: value-orientation patterns "can only be internalized from outside through reciprocal attachments" — i.e. through identification within a complementary role relationship reaching the level of shared value and loyalty.

## Semantic History

Parsons drew heavily and openly on **Freud**, reinterpreting psychoanalytic material in role-theoretic terms — most strikingly recasting the Oedipus conflict not as sexual rivalry but as the child's pressure to renounce the erotic component of the infantile mother-attachment. He distinguished **[[primary-socialization|primary]]** socialization (acquisition of basic personality structure) from **situational role-specification** — a secondary process ([[secondary-socialization]], chiefly via imitation and secondary identification) connecting generalized value-orientations to concrete adult role content (e.g., the American school as a "microcosm of the adult occupational world"). Berger and Luckmann's (1966) primary/secondary socialization distinction is a phenomenological reworking of this terrain.

## Operationalizations

Parsons offered a conceptual apparatus, not a measure; socialization outcomes are inferred from role-conduct, personality typing, and clinical/developmental observation.

## Applications

- The mechanism by which [[value-orientation]] becomes [[need-disposition]] — the personality–social-system "transformation formula."
- The source, when identification is disturbed, of *primary alienative need-dispositions* and thus of [[deviance]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Parsons's model is a leading target of the **"oversocialized conception of man"** critique (Dennis Wrong, 1961): it can read as if actors simply internalize and enact society's values, leaving little room for conflict, resistance, or the gap between internalized norm and situated conduct.
