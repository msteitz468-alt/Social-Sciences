# Unilineal Descent

> Central structural principle in [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]’s Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950); illustrated across the volume’s African cases.

## Definition as Coined

Radcliffe-Brown defined kinship as based on **social** descent (parent–child recognition, not mere biology): “Where the term ‘descent’ is used in this essay it will refer not to biological but to social relations.” Within that frame he distinguished:

1. **Cognatic** reckoning — all descendants of recognized ancestors through both sexes within practical limits.
2. **Unilineal** reckoning — through males only (**agnatic**/patrilineal) or through females only (matrilineal).

Agnates, he wrote, are cognates “if they are descendants by male links from the same male ancestor.” Matrilineal kin are those connected by female links only from a common ancestress. He held that few if any societies lack *some* recognition of unilineal descent; what matters is “the degree of emphasis on the unilineal principle and how it is used.”

The load-bearing theoretical claim is structural, not evolutionary: **unilineal reckoning makes exclusive corporate kin groups possible**. Cognatic kinship, he argued, cannot partition a society so that every person belongs to one and only one group of a given set; only unilineal descent can. Where kinship is the chief source of cohesion in non-literate societies, corporate unilineal groups (clan, sub-clan, lineage) “tend to become the most important feature of social structure” (attributed).

## Semantic History

Older ethnology spoke of clans, gentes, and “mother-right/father-right” often as evolutionary stages or origin puzzles. Radcliffe-Brown condemned “pseudo-historical” origin-of-clans conjectures and reframed unilineal descent as a **structural principle** enabling corporate continuity, not a conjectural first stage of the clan. Victorian evolutionary uses of related vocabulary live on [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]] and [[mother-right]]; ASKAM’s term is comparative-analytic, not stage-historical.

[[forde-daryll|Forde]] restated the same point for double systems: parenthood is dual, so unrestricted bilateral recognition would proliferate kinship indefinitely; societies typically stress affiliation through *one* parent to yield mutually exclusive unilineal groups of both sexes — without thereby making patrilineal and matrilineal reckoning mutually exclusive as principles (see [[double-descent]]).

## Operationalizations

| Criterion | What observers look for |
|---|---|
| Exclusive membership | Person belongs to one lineage/clan of a set (not dual membership of the same set) |
| Lineage definition | Agnatic: male ancestor + male-line descendants of *n* generations (woman belongs to father’s lineage; her children do not). Matrilineal: progenetrix + female-line descendants (man belongs to mother’s lineage; his children do not) |
| Corporate functions | Control of land use; vengeance / indemnity for a member’s killing; ancestral or shrine cult |
| Continuity | Group persists beyond an individual’s or elementary family’s life |
| Terminology | Often paired with [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory terms]] expressing lineage unity (“fathers,” “female fathers,” etc.) |
| Marriage regulation | Unilineal systems primarily forbid marriage within lineage/clan (exogamy); cognatic systems forbid marriage among cognates within a degree |

**Contrast case (cognatic):** Radcliffe-Brown used the early historic Teutonic *sib* — each person’s cognates within a degree, no two persons with the same sib, partnership-like wergild — to show what unilineal corporate membership is *not*. [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] treated Lozi kinship as an African cognatic approximation (no corporate unilineal group; “the child belongs to both sides”).

## Applications

ASKAM and Introduction illustrations (all attributed):

- **Patrilineal emphases:** Nguni ([[zulu]], [[swazi]]), [[nuer]], Tswana lineages (with anomalous FBD marriage per Schapera), Zulu *ulusendo*/*umdeni* corporate segments (Gluckman).
- **Matrilineal emphases:** Ashanti *abusua*/lineage (Fortes — corporate jural-political organization); Bemba and Central African qualified mother-right types (Richards); “mother-right” ideal type via Nayar *taravad* as comparative foil.
- **Double unilineal:** Yakö *kepun* (patri) + *lejima* (matri) (Forde); Nuba Nyaro gentes + matri-clans (Nadel) — see [[double-descent]].
- **Minimum unilineal recognition:** Lozi (Gluckman) as cognatic pole of the volume continuum.
- **Political extension:** Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer lineages in political organization; Kuper’s Swazi kinship–state link (monographs pointed to from the Introduction).

Radcliffe-Brown’s volume map: “Unilineal kinship receives only a minimum of recognition, if even that, in the Lozi; matrilineal kinship is emphasized in the Ashanti, and patrilineal kinship in the Zulu and Nuer: both matrilineal and patrilineal kinship are made use of in the construction of the system of the Yako.”

### Tallensi densification (Fortes 1949)

*[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]* supplies the classic monographic demonstration that **corporate agnatic unilineal structure does not exhaust kinship**. Fortes held (attributed): agnatic lineage ties found corporate units with shared jural/ritual/political interests; cognatic and matrilateral ties are personal and non-corporate yet structurally necessary — the centrifugal counterweight to centripetal patriliny ([[complementary-filiation]]). Domestic joint-family segmentation parallels lineage segmentation ([[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group]]). Type society: [[tallensi]]; study: [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Later kinship theory questioned how tightly “descent ideology” matches practice; cognatic, house-based, and network analyses reappraised African and Oceanic cases.
- African diversity exceeds pure unilineal types; ASKAM itself documents intermediates and colonial modification (Ashanti, Yakö).
- Corporate-function checklists can reify analytical constructs as observed peoples — treat lineage/clan as etic organizational types with ethnographic date-stamps.
- Treat unilineal descent as an **attributed analytic principle** of structural-functional kinship theory, not a finding that every African society “is” unilineal.
