# Plebiscitary Leader-Democracy

## Definition as Coined

Weber's term (*plebiszitäre Führerdemokratie*) for the form modern mass democracy takes once the party **"machine"** displaces rule by notables. In *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (1919), Weber traces the rise — "the children of democracy, of mass franchise, of the necessity to woo and organize the masses" — of disciplined party apparatuses run by professional politicians *outside* parliament: the English **caucus** (Chamberlain, Gladstone's "grand demagogy"), the American **boss** and Tammany Hall, the **spoils system**. "The man whom the machine follows now becomes the leader, even over the head of the parliamentary party… the creation of such machines signifies the advent of plebiscitarian democracy." Weber poses a stark choice (attributed voice): **"leadership democracy with a machine" or "leaderless democracy"** — "the rule of professional politicians without a calling, without the inner charismatic qualities that make a leader," i.e. "the rule of the clique." The price of leadership is that the following must "obey him blindly" — the "soullessness" and "intellectual proletarianization" of the followers. Weber's practical proposal for Germany: a directly, plebiscitarily elected *Reich President* as a "safety-valve of the demand for leadership."

## Semantic History

The concept fuses Weber's [[charismatic-authority|charisma]] with his sociology of party organization (drawing on **Ostrogorski**'s comparative study of mass parties). It is Weber's distinctive contribution to democratic theory: a realist, "elite/competitive" account of democracy as a mechanism for *selecting leaders* rather than for expressing a popular will — a line later systematized by Schumpeter's *Capitalism, Socialism and Democracy* (1942). The concept became controversial after 1945 when **Wolfgang Mommsen** (*Max Weber und die deutsche Politik*, 1959) argued that Weber's advocacy of a plebiscitary leader helped prepare the intellectual ground for charismatic *Führer* politics — a reading vigorously contested by others (e.g. Beetham) who stress Weber's constitutional-liberal framing and his fear of bureaucratic ossification.

## Operationalizations

- A **mass party machine** with paid officials/entrepreneurs, outside and above the parliamentary party.
- **Direct plebiscitary appeal** of a leader to the electorate (elected presidency, caucus-nominated leader).
- **Following bound to personality**, expecting spoils; blind discipline.
- **Parliament demoted** to leader-selection or ratification; proportional representation as the marker of the *leaderless* alternative.

## Applications

A foundational concept for the sociology of political parties, democratic elitism, populism, and personalist leadership; the bridge from Weber to Schumpeterian competitive-elitist democratic theory. In this wiki it specializes [[charismatic-authority]] into the institutional setting of mass democracy, stands against [[bureaucracy|bureaucratic]] "leaderless" administration, and bears on the [[the-state|state]]'s political direction.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The Mommsen controversy.** Whether Weber's plebiscitary-leader ideal is a prescient realism or a dangerous precursor to authoritarian leadership remains a genuinely open interpretive dispute — described here as competing positions, not adjudicated.
- **Thin democracy.** Critics of democratic elitism (participatory and deliberative theorists) reject the reduction of democracy to leader-selection and the acceptance of follower "soullessness."
- **Dated party sociology.** Weber's machine/notable typology reflects early-twentieth-century mass parties; media-driven and cartel/catch-all parties fit it imperfectly.
- **Normative ambivalence.** Weber both fears and welcomes the plebiscitary leader (as a counterweight to bureaucracy) — an unresolved tension in the concept itself.
