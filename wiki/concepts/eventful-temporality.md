# Eventful Temporality

## Definition as Coined

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, "Three Temporalities: Toward an Eventful Sociology") distinguishes three ways of conceptualizing social time in historical sociology and argues the third is the most adequate but currently subordinate:

- **Teleological temporality** — "the attribution of the cause of a historical happening … to abstract transhistorical processes leading to some future historical state. Events in some historical present … are actually explained by events in the future." (Traced to Comte, Marx, Spencer, Tönnies, Durkheim; exemplified by Wallerstein and by Tilly's master-process urbanization.)
- **Experimental temporality** — treats sequences of occurrences as causally independent, interchangeable "trials" governed by time-invariant laws; the temporality tacit in the comparative method and in variable-based sociology (exemplified by Skocpol's quasi-experimental reading of revolutions). It "rests on two fundamental assumptions: a uniformity of causal laws across time and a causal independence of every sequence of occurrences from previous and subsequent occurrences."
- **Eventful temporality** — "takes into account the transformation of structures by events"; denies both experimental assumptions. Social life is countless happenings, most of which reproduce structures; **[[historical-event|events]]** are "that relatively rare subclass of happenings that significantly transforms structures."

## Semantic History

"Eventful" anglicizes the French *événementiel* of the Annales historians; Braudel used *histoire événementielle* pejoratively (events as "froth on the waves of history"), opposing it to structural and conjunctural history. Sewell rehabilitates the term, following [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (who anglicized it as "evenemential"). Sewell notes historians "live in the narrative element" and are thus, like Ruth Benedict's deep-sea fish that cannot name water, largely unselfconscious about the event as a theoretical category — a self-description of his own path before engaging sociology and anthropology.

## The Three Assumptions of Eventful Temporality

1. **Path dependency** — "what has happened at an earlier point in time will affect the possible outcomes of a sequence of events occurring at a later point in time." (Sewell notes the term was formalized by economists — Arthur 1988 — regarding technology lock-in; on its own it does not require that causal structures change.)
2. **Temporal heterogeneity of causal structures** — a fully eventful temporality also denies that causal laws are uniform over time: "events must be assumed to be capable of changing not only the balance of causal forces operating but the very logic by which consequences follow." Example: the French Revolution converted noble status "from a prime asset … into a powerful liability."
3. **Global contingency** — contingency "characterizes not only the surface but the core or the depths of social relations." This is not permanent flux: "an assumption of global contingency means not that everything is constantly changing but that nothing in social life is ultimately immune to change." This is the sharpest break from teleological temporality, which allows only surface contingency.

Summary (attributed): "social relations are characterized by path dependency, temporally heterogeneous causalities, and global contingency."

## Operationalizations

Eventful temporality is realized methodologically as [[eventful-sociology|eventful sociology]]: narrative reconstruction of ramified sequences that establishes the synchronic structure being transformed, then traces its transformation. It reworks comparative method as a context of discovery rather than justification. It is a sibling to Andrew Abbott's processual/narrative critique of [[general-linear-reality|general linear reality]] and his [[event-occurrence-distinction|event/occurrence]] and [[turning-point]] apparatus, though Abbott's terms are methodological where Sewell's are substantive.

## Applications

The taking of the Bastille (see [[historical-event]]); the "uneven development" of Marseille's dockworkers; a defense, via Michael Mann, that eventful causality operates even at the most macro-historical scale.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded as a position: whether eventful temporality can support generalization or only narration; and whether it applies at macro scale (Sewell's own postscript answers the latter affirmatively). See [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].
