# Urban Revolution

## Definition as Coined

[[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]]'s label for the second great economic transformation after the [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]]: the rise of cities and "civilization."

**Primary coining text** — *[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]* (1936; rev. 1951), chs. VI–VII:

> "A second revolution transformed some tiny villages of self-sufficing farmers into populous cities, nourished by secondary industries and foreign trade, and regularly organized as States."

Accumulated **[[social-surplus|surplus foodstuffs]]** is the precondition of village → city; irrigation and alluvial reclamation demand collective labor and enable coercion; trade and metallurgy produce full-time specialists. The revolution earns its name by marked population expansion and new classes (priests, officials, merchants, artisans, soldiers) that could not exist as classes in self-sufficing food-producing communities. Type theatres: Sumer (temple economy — god as capitalist, writing for accounts), Egypt (royal accumulation, Menes), Indus (planned cities). Scene of the drama: semi-arid belt Nile–Ganges (attributed).

In *[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]* (ch. I stage summary) he restates: about five thousand years ago in the Nile, Tigris–Euphrates, and Indus valleys, riverside villages became cities when society "persuaded or compelled the farmers to produce a surplus of foodstuffs over and above their domestic requirements, and by concentrating this surplus used it to support a new urban population of specialized craftsmen, merchants, priests, officials, and clerks. Writing was… a necessary by-product of this urban revolution" (attributed).

*MMH* ch. VI prelude package (6000–3000 B.C., as of Childe): harnessing oxen and winds; plow, wheeled cart, sailboat; copper smelting; solar calendar — technical foundations that without new economic organization remain incomplete.

## Semantic History

- **Childe (*MMH* primary; *WHIH* extension):** surplus → specialists → writing → class → state; retardation thesis (*MMH* ch. IX) — after the revolution only four comparable applied-science advances in ~2000 years (place-value notation, industrial iron, alphabet, aqueducts); "the urban revolution, made possible by science, was exploited by superstition"; first ~2000 years of civilization ≈ Bronze Age metal monopoly by elites; expansion creates secondary centers.
- **Processual / systems archaeology:** Childe's later trait-lists of urbanism reworked as systemic variables.
- **Contemporary:** multi-pathway urbanization; secondary states; low-density urbanism; rejection of single surplus→city teleology. Phenomena: [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]].

## Operationalizations

**Childe (attributed, *WHIH* chs. V–VI):**
- **Surplus:** Sumerian barley yields (documents c. 2500 B.C.: average eighty-six times sowing) make surplus easy once floods controlled; temple/"gods" concentrate and redistribute (**[[social-surplus]]**).
- **Specialists:** temple households as enlarged patriarchal units with division of labour; craftsmen lose independence under patronage.
- **Writing:** invented as temple accounting (not pure curiosity).
- **Class/state:** stratified societies (masters/slaves or merchants/labourers); divine kingship / *ishakku*; residence-based political units reduce internal violence relative to kinship feuding (claimed).
- **Type-cases:** Sumer sequence al ’Ubaid → Uruk → Jemdet Nasr → Early Dynastic; Egypt (Predynastic → Menes → pyramids — more extreme surplus concentration); Indus (Harappa, Mohenjo-daro — merchant wealth, script, municipal drains; citadel/granary as surplus symbol).
- **Limits:** metal cost confines industrial equipment to elites; masses keep stone tools; literacy confined to learned clerks; science divorced from workshop empiricism.

**Modern:** settlement hierarchy, craft production, administrative technology, inequality metrics — without requiring Childe's full package.

## Applications

Load-bearing concept for Childe's popular synthesis; links archaeology to [[historical-materialism|materialist]] surplus/class analysis without full Marxist stage theory. Pairs with [[neolithic-revolution]] as the two revolutions that kink the population graph. Feeds [[civilization]], [[urbanization]], [[state-formation]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Mesopotamia-centric type-case; surplus theory contested; writing-as-threshold excludes complex non-literate urbanisms; absolute dates and diffusion story pre-radiocarbon (1957 note in *WHIH* already deflates early Mesopotamian dates); progress narrative teleological; colonial hierarchical language in barbarian/civilized contrast — attribute only. Keep all program claims attributed.

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]] keep Childe's urban-revolution problem (cities, specialists, writing, monuments, stratification) as a comparative frame while **testing and complicating** the package (as of 2007). By ~5000 years ago SW Asia already had the urban "civilization" package while much of the world remained foragers or marginal farmers (attributed, ch. 8 intro). Settlement analyses measure size hierarchy and placement; on broad plains interacting towns may form interlocking hexagons (central-place efficiency); later settlements track trade routes, frontiers, and administration more than raw resources (attributed, ch. 7).

Load-bearing critique from Trigger (via W&O): **urbanism and the state are not isomorphic** — Mesopotamia formed out of cities while Egypt remained largely non-urban for much of its history; pre-industrial urban trajectories are plural (attributed). Egypt case densifies this: urbanization is one strategy, not an indispensable condition of cultural complexity; weak external military threats, environmental uniformity, and political centralization may have inhibited early urbanism (attributed, Egypt ch.). Harappan civilization shows advanced town planning and municipal water/sewage yet weak palace/tomb wealth displays; some called it chiefdom rather than state for lack of monumental wealth differential (attributed). Shang/China markers include walled towns and craft specialization but early Chinese societies less urban than Sumerian peers (attributed). V. Gordon Childe is cited as Marxist historical scientist working toward a science of history during culture-history eras (*Man Makes Himself*) (attributed, ch. 1 notes). Mass-produced plain pottery is noted as a complexity indicator, not aesthetic decline (attributed).

### Adams — *The Evolution of Urban Society* (1966, attributed)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]] (*[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|EUS]]*; [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|study]]) reworks Childe's term as the comparative object of a **two-case multilinear analysis** (southern Mesopotamia + central Mexico). Key moves (all attributed):

1. **Societal over cultural variables.** Childe's famous trait-list (ten criteria) is a "mixed bag" with degree problems; useful talk of an Urban Revolution requires a **functionally related core of institutions**, not fortuitous trait association. Adams prioritizes stratification and organized political authority over urbanism as formal settlement type — "not generalizing about cities" as such.
2. **Ramp vs step.** The course of the Urban Revolution is an ascending curve as **ramp** (cumulative social differentiation: classes; military/political/administrative elites; tribute–trade–redistribution networks) or **step** (abrupt plateau in monumentality, art, style). Both are polar abstractions; mixed curve B often fits better. Protoliterate [[uruk|Uruk]] and Classic [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] exemplify a style/monumentality **step** plateau not demonstrably surpassed later in each sequence; social differentiation better fits a **ramp** (Mesopotamia closer to smooth A; Mexico more pulsed → lean B).
3. **Homotaxial polity sequence.** [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence|Theocratic polities → militaristic polities → conquest states]], correlated across sequences (Uruk–Teotihuacan; Early Dynastic–Tula; Akkadian–[[aztec-empire|Aztec]]).
4. **Food-Producing vs Urban Revolution.** Distinct process types; millennia can separate effective food production from urban take-off; no clear gradual population build-up as automatic precipitative threshold (as of Adams 1966).
5. **City and hinterland.** Urban Revolution concerns a network of city and outlying settlements (surplus extraction; symbiotic region / key area), not the urban nucleus alone.

All mid-1960s chronologies and demographic estimates date-stamped; keep causal claims attributed.

### Sanders & Price — primary/secondary urbanism (1968, attributed)

[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]] (1968) recast Childe's Urban Revolution as a **sociological** process — population growth + socioeconomic differentiation + **nucleation** (nucleation the distinctive feature of urbanism specifically) — rather than a technological one, adding a Darwin/Durkheim **population-density → specialization** mechanism as the driver. They distinguish **"primary urbanism"** (large nucleated rural centers under intensive/hydraulic agriculture growing into cities) from **secondary urbanism** (centers generated by trade/colonization from a primary one), and separate **urbanism from statehood**: all urban societies are states, but not all states are urban — "[[state-formation|civilizations without cities]]" (Classic Maya) are states lacking the local [[symbiotic-region|symbiosis]] that drives nucleation. [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] is their type-case. They reject Childe's writing criterion (absent in the Andes) but retain monumental architecture for its clear labor-organization prerequisites.
