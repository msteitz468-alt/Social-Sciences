# Heterosexual Matrix

[[butler-judith|Judith Butler]]'s term (in *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]*, 1990) for the **grid of cultural intelligibility through which bodies, genders, and desires are naturalized** — the regime that requires a stable sex expressed through a stable gender defined oppositionally through the compulsory practice of heterosexuality. Butler adapts the term from Monique Wittig's "heterosexual contract" and Adrienne Rich's "compulsory heterosexuality." All statements below are **attributed** to Butler (or, where noted, to her interlocutors).

## Definition as Coined

Butler argued that "'intelligible' genders are those which in some sense institute and maintain relations of coherence and continuity among sex, gender, sexual practice, and desire." The "heterosexualization of desire requires and institutes the production of discrete and asymmetrical oppositions between 'feminine' and 'masculine,' where these are understood as expressive attributes of 'male' and 'female'." Genders that fail this coherence — where gender does not "follow" from sex, or desire from gender — "appear only as developmental failures or logical impossibilities," and their persistence exposes the matrix's regulatory limits.

## Semantic History

- **Sources.** The concept fuses Wittig's argument that sex is "a specifically political use of the category of nature that serves the purposes of reproductive sexuality" with Rich's "compulsory heterosexuality" and [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s account of sexuality as a productive apparatus.
- **From prohibition to production.** Extending Foucault's critique of the repressive hypothesis to the incest taboo, Butler argued the taboo does not repress a pre-given heterosexuality but *produces* it: "the taboo against homosexuality… in effect creates the heterosexual 'dispositions'," and "the illusion of a sexuality before the law is itself the creation of that law."
- **Bodies That Matter.** Butler later preferred "heterosexual hegemony" to soften the term's apparent rigidity — an acknowledgment that "matrix" read as too totalizing.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. The concept is displayed through readings of the incest taboo (Lévi-Strauss, Rubin, Freud), of psychoanalytic gender formation (gender identity as "melancholic" incorporation of the prohibited same-sex object), and of the grammar of gender attribution. It is Butler's genealogical answer to the question that organizes the [[sex-gender-distinction-debate]]: the category "women" achieves stability, she argued, "only in the context of the heterosexual matrix."

## Applications

Central to queer theory's account of heteronormativity; used across gender studies, sociology of sexuality, and cultural anthropology to analyze how kinship, law, and everyday categorization enforce the sex→gender→desire chain. Connects to the wiki's [[gender-order]] and [[sex-category]] concepts, and contrasts with the more empirical, interactional treatments in [[doing-gender]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Totalization.** Critics (and Butler herself, retrospectively) found "matrix" implied a monolithic, seamless system; Wittig's own totalizing view of heterosexuality is one Butler simultaneously used and criticized as "an inevitable comedy" impossible to embody fully.
- **Cross-cultural projection.** The framework's roots in Euro-American structuralism and psychoanalysis raise the four-way-non-identity concern (see [[feminist-anthropology]]): the sex/gender/desire grid is not obviously a cultural universal, and projecting it risks the "epistemological imperialism" Butler herself charged against Irigaray.
