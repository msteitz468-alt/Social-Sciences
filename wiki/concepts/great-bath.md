# Great Bath

The **Great Bath** is the large, carefully waterproofed brick tank on the citadel (western high) mound of [[mohenjo-daro]], conventionally labeled since the 1920s excavations and treated in [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]] as the earliest known public water tank in the ancient world. The name is an **etic archaeological label**, not an emic Indus term (script undeciphered).

## Definition as Coined

As used in Indus archaeology, “Great Bath” designates the rectangular public tank east of the so-called granary/great hall on Mohenjo-daro’s citadel, with north and south staircases, surrounding colonnades and rooms, and elaborate waterproof construction. Kenoyer (1998): approximately **12 m north–south × 7 m wide × maximum depth 2.4 m**.

## Semantic History

- **1920s discovery era:** Marshall and colleagues identified the structure among the citadel’s monumental buildings; popular literature sometimes called it the “first swimming pool.”
- **Mid-century:** Functional readings linked the bath to ritual purity, elite ceremony, and state religion — often bundled with “College of Priests” and “granary” labels that later scholars treat as unsupported.
- **Recent synthesis (Kenoyer 1998):** Retains “Great Bath” as conventional name; rejects recreational swimming; endorses scholarly consensus on **special religious purification** rather than ordinary public bathing; cites Jansen on architectural constraints between the main N–S street and the adjacent “granary” foundation.
- Parallel hydraulic monuments (e.g. [[dholavira]] reservoirs) are **analogous** water architecture, not the same building type or label.

## Operationalizations

Physical features used to identify and analyze the structure (Kenoyer 1998):

| Feature | Description (attributed to Kenoyer 1998 / Jansen / Marshall) |
|---|---|
| Tank size | ~12 m N–S × 7 m wide × max 2.4 m deep |
| Access | Wide stairs north and south; possible wooden tread sockets; ledge at stair foot for circulation without full immersion |
| Waterproofing | Finely fitted edge-laid bricks with gypsum plaster; thick **bitumen** (natural tar) along sides (and presumably under floor) |
| Drainage | Floor slopes to SW outlet into brick drain → rectangular timber-covered space → corbeled arch drain along northern terrace of adjacent “granary” |
| Colonnades | Brick colonnades on E, N, S (W missing by erosion; Marshall assumed western colonnades); stepped column edges may have held wooden screens |
| Rooms / water | Eastern rooms include a well possibly supplying the tank; rainwater collection possible but no inlet drains found |
| Adjacent ordinary baths | Substantial building just north with eight small rooms and ordinary bathing platforms — contrast used to argue special (not routine) function |

## Applications

- **[[mohenjo-daro]] urban plan:** Anchors the northern citadel monumental cluster (with so-called granary/great hall, “college,” assembly hall, later Buddhist stupa overlying other structures).
- **[[indus-religion]]:** Primary architectural case for water as purifying medium in Indus ritual practice (attributed consensus, not decoded theology).
- **[[indus-polity-debate]]:** High visibility of citadel monuments used by Kenoyer as elite legitimation architecture; not proof of a single priest-king state.
- Comparative *wasserluxus* (Jansen’s term via other synthesizers) at other Indus sites — linked by analogy, not identity of institution.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Function remains interpretive:** No texts explain the tank; “religious purification” is consensus inference from form, waterproofing investment, and contrast with ordinary house baths.
- **Bundle of citadel labels:** Early “granary,” “college,” and “assembly hall” names are, as Kenoyer and Possehl stress elsewhere, form-based guesses; the Bath’s ritual reading is stronger than the granary’s storage reading but still unproven.
- **Swimming-pool tourist trope:** Explicitly rejected by Kenoyer.
- **Dating of construction phases** relative to the adjacent foundation (Great Bath drain cuts NE corner of earlier “granary” foundation) matters for sequence but does not yield an emic name.
