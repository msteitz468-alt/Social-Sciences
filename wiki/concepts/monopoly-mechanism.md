# Monopoly Mechanism

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Elias, *The Civilizing Process*, Part Three/Four.)* The **monopoly mechanism** names the process-tendency whereby free competition among many rival power units (warriors, lords, territories) leads, if uninterrupted, toward elimination contests and the concentration of the means of physical force and of fiscal extraction in fewer hands — culminating in relatively effective central monopolies that define the [[the-state|state]] figuration. Elias treats Weber's [[monopoly-of-violence]] as the *outcome* of a historical mechanism, not only a definitional criterion.

## Semantic History

Built against mosaic great-man chronicles of royal "genius"; parallel, in Elias's framing, to monopolization tendencies in economic competition (analogy, not identity). Distinct from later formal models of bargaining/war in political science — same problem-space, different method.

## Operationalizations

Elimination contests; aggregation of territories; dual monopoly of force and taxation; transformation of warrior elites into courtiers ([[courtization]]).

## Applications

European late-medieval and early-modern [[state-formation]]; comparative notes on France, England, German lands, Italian city regimes in *[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Scope conditions (when competition does *not* produce monopoly); Eurocentrism of the core cases; relationship to colonial extraction monopolies (later debates).
