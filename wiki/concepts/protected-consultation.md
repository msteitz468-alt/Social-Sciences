# Protected Consultation

> [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]’s process criterion for **democracy**: the extent to which relations between government agents and people under their jurisdiction combine **breadth**, **equality**, **binding consultation**, and **protection** from arbitrary action (*[[tilly-identities-boundaries-social-ties-2005|Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties]]* 2005).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly 2005.)* Four elements (also as democracy’s process definition in Ch. 12):

1. Relatively **broad** public political participation  
2. Relatively **equal** participation  
3. **Binding consultation** of participants regarding state policies, resources, and personnel  
4. **Protection** of participants (especially minorities) from arbitrary state action  

**Protected consultation** names their combination (often treated as a multiplicative product). Regimes high on protected consultation are democratic; low are undemocratic. Crossed with **governmental capacity**, Tilly sketches a crude regime typology (high/low capacity × democratic/undemocratic).

## Semantic History

Developed across Tilly’s democratization program of the late 1990s–2000s; related to “trust and rule” and contention work. Distinct from purely institutional checklists (elections only) and from substantive equity definitions of democracy — though Tilly says effective [[citizenship]] is necessary under institutional, substantive, or process definitions.

## Operationalizations

| Dimension | Low | High |
|---|---|---|
| Breadth | Narrow selectorate | Near-universal adult participation |
| Equality | Categorical barriers | Equal weight of participants |
| Consultation | Decree / rubber stamp | Binding influence on policy/personnel |
| Protection | Arbitrary coercion | Rights enforceable against agents |

**Democratization** = increase in protected consultation; **[[de-democratization]]** = decrease.

## Applications

- Comparing regime trajectories without assuming irreversible modernization  
- Linking categorical inequality and trust-network integration to democratic outcomes (Ch. 13 conjectures)  
- Explaining why citizenship is necessary but not sufficient for democracy  

## Critiques and Limitations

- Multiplicative “product” is a heuristic, not a validated index.  
- Continuous dimensions are hard to measure cross-nationally; Freedom House-style scores are proxies Tilly uses cautiously.  
- Ch. 13 claims are explicitly **conjectures**.  
- Eurocentric historical path (local → national citizenship) is a type case, not a universal sequence.
