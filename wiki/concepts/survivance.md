# Survivance

## Definition as Coined

**Survivance** is a term coined by the Anishinaabe scholar **Gerald Vizenor** (*Fugitive Poses*,
1998) for an active Native presence that exceeds mere survival: "survivance … is more than
survival, more than endurance or mere response; the stories of survivance are an active presence …
an active repudiation of dominance, tragedy, and victimry." Introduced to archaeology and museum
critique via Sonya Atalay's chapter in *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary
Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010). Claims here are attributed. The term is **migrated** — a
literary/critical coinage taken up as an analytic and curatorial category.

## Semantic History

Vizenor built survivance against "victimry," illustrating it through Charles Eastman (Santee Dakota
physician, present at the Wounded Knee aftermath, 1890), whose writing Vizenor reads as "stories of
native courage and survivance … presence rather than absence." The concept migrated into museum
studies and [[indigenous-archaeology]] as a frame for representing Native peoples as agents, not
casualties.

## Operationalizations

The **National Museum of the American Indian** (NMAI, opened 2004) adopted survivance in its
curatorial text: Native societies facing the "firestorm of Contact" reshaped their cultures by
adopting the very tools ("guns, churches, and governments") used to control them, and thereby "kept
them alive." Founding director W. Richard West Jr. framed the choice against victimhood: "We do not
want to make the National Museum of the American Indian into an Indian Holocaust Museum … What we
are talking about in the end is cultural survivance. We are still here" (position).

## Applications

**Atalay's critique.** Atalay (Ojibwe archaeologist) argues (finding/position) that the NMAI's
"Guns and Bibles" exhibits invoked survivance while **stripping it of the context of struggle** —
displaying Native adaptation without the "terror, genocide, and forced conversion" those objects
represent: "one cannot appreciate … the power of Native survivance if the stories and memories of
our histories are not placed within the context of struggle." She contrasts the NMAI's benign
framing with the Australian Museum's 1988 "Pieces of Paradise" exhibit, which foregrounded
Indigenous resistance. The concept thus becomes a lever for debating how much struggle and atrocity
survivance-framed representation must carry — a live curatorial dispute recorded, not adjudicated.
Atalay ties it to the omitted [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]]/sovereignty
controversies (NAGPRA; Kennewick).

## Critiques and Limitations

Survivance's strength — refusing victimry — is also its risk: as Atalay argues, it can be used to
elide colonial violence and produce a comfortable, consumable "we are still here" narrative. It is
"conceptually aligned with, but distinct from," [[indigenous-archaeology]]'s decolonizing program.
As a representational category, its meaning shifts with who deploys it and to what curatorial end.
