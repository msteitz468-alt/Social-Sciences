# Capitalist World-Economy

## Definition as Coined

In [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]], the **capitalist world-economy** is the modern historical system: a single economic division of labor spanning multiple states, oriented to endless accumulation of capital, lasting (per Wallerstein) roughly five centuries. Distinct from a world-empire (single political center extracting tribute) and from a closed national economy.

## Semantic History

Braudel's *économie-monde* ("world-economy" as a universe unto itself) is a major source; Wallerstein binds it to capitalism as system logic and to [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] structure. Rival uses of "world economy" in IR and economics often mean international trade among national economies — a different unit of analysis.

## Operationalizations

Commodity chains; trade and production hierarchies; wage and surplus appropriation gradients; hegemonic leadership of the system. Empirical operationalizations are disputed and data-heavy; this wiki's first source (2004 volume) is largely conceptual/programmatic.

## Applications

Program baseline for all world-systems pages; Amin restates really existing global capitalism as polarizing; Arrighi and others analyze hegemonic reorganizations of the same system.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether "capitalism" as world-economy starts in the sixteenth century (vs nineteenth industrial) is a standing dispute (Brenner and others — not yet ingested). Gender and household production critiques (von Werlhof in the 2004 volume) challenge productivist definitions of accumulation.
