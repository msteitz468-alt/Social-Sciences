## Definition as Coined

A chinampa is a raised, artificial cultivation plot built within a lake or wetland
canal system, sometimes called (in English-language popular writing) a "floating
garden," though the plots are anchored, not floating. Chinampas belong to a broader
category of raised-field cultivation used widely across New World tropical lowlands,
including by the lowland Classic Maya. Coe and Koontz (2013) note the technique may
have originated with the Teotihuacanos during the Classic period, though this
attribution is not certain.

## Semantic History

The term survives in modern Spanish and English via the Xochimilco district south of
Mexico City, where chinampa cultivation continues to be practiced and where the term is
now most commonly encountered by visitors and scholars alike. Its use for the entire
lake-based agricultural system of the Basin of Mexico is a retrospective, etic
extension from the surviving Xochimilco case back onto the pre-Conquest capital.

## Operationalizations

Construction method, per Coe and Koontz (2013): thick layers of aquatic vegetation were
cut from canal surfaces and piled into mats; mud dredged from canal bottoms was spread
over the mats; willows were planted to anchor the resulting plot. Newly built chinampas
were light enough to support only cane-and-thatch houses; older, more consolidated
plots could carry stone-and-mortar houses, some of two stories with patios and gardens.

[[tenochtitlan]] itself was, in Coe and Koontz's phrase, "in effect... a chinampa
city," laid out with north–south canals for canoe traffic bordered by lanes, crossed by
larger canals, with rectangular house plots between them.

## Applications

Chinampa zones near Tenochtitlan produced a substantial agricultural surplus,
supplemented by field crops grown on nearby land and by maguey cultivation on the
surrounding hill slopes (Coe & Koontz 2013). The axolotl, a large larval salamander
found in the chinampa canals, was eaten stewed in a yellow chile sauce.

Saltiness in the eastern lake threatened chinampa cultivation during floods; the
Texcocan poet-king Nezahualcoyotl responded by building a 10-mile (16 km) dike to seal
off a freshwater lagoon for Tenochtitlan's use (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Construction Model: Documented Revision (Sanders 1965 vs. Armillas 1971)

[CONTRADICTION — documented revision, both positions preserved] Sanders (1965:44),
as restated in Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979), originally described chinampa
formation as construction of narrow rectangular islands built up by layering mud and
vegetation, edged with huejote (willow) trees, irrigated by bucket/canoe, and
cropped almost continuously (only 3–4 months' rest per year), with produce
transported to urban markets by canoe. Armillas's (1971) intensive survey of the
Chalco-Xochimilco lakebed substantially revised this picture: Sanders, Parsons &
Santley (1979), reporting Armillas's findings, describe the lake bottom as an
"exceedingly shallow saucer" only 1–2m deep over most of its surface, and argue
chinampa formation proceeded primarily through a dense drainage-ditch network that
lowered the local water table, with colonization spreading from natural islands and
mainland peninsulas rather than (or not only) by mud-and-vegetation island
construction. The major ditches doubled as canoe-transport arteries, and large dikes
regulated flooding. Armillas further argued that the geometric regularity of most
chinampa plots (Mixquic being an explicit exception) indicates centralized planning,
attributing the system to local Late Horizon states (Tlahuac, Mixquic, Xochimilco) or
the Tenochca themselves (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979, citing Armillas 1971). Both
the original Sanders (1965) construction model and the Armillas (1971) revision are
recorded here per the wiki's contradiction protocol; Armillas's account is the
authors' updated position as of 1979.

## Scale, Productivity, and State Planning

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) estimate that roughly 10,000 ha of chinampa fields
were created in the southern Basin of Mexico during the Late Horizon (c. 1350–1519
CE), with a further few thousand hectares reclaimed in the walled-off southwestern
corner of Lake Texcoco; combined, they estimate a total of about 12,000 ha of
chinampas in cultivation by 1519, which they argue could sustain a population of at
least 228,000. They describe the Chalco-Xochimilco chinampa system as, in their
words, "probably the most intensive and productive kind of agriculture practiced in
the New World in pre-Hispanic times," and calculate that chinampa cultivation yielded
roughly four times the maximal dry-farming yield for only slightly more labor input —
a productivity gain the authors treat as a key mechanism enabling the Basin's
Late Horizon population to support a large non-food-producing urban population.

Calnek (1972), from documentary sources combined with Armillas's (1971) survey data,
argued that the 15th-century expansion of the Lake Chalco-Xochimilco chinampa zone
was a state-planned colonization effort — that "existing state organizations were the
entrepreneurs that created the chinampa area" rather than chinampa agriculture having
produced the Late Horizon states, reversing the causal direction proposed for
Teotihuacan's earlier development. Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) accept this
reading but note that, once created, chinampa agriculture "enormously modified the
Aztec political system," which they treat as an example of a broader feedback process
between agricultural intensification and state organization.

## Critiques and Limitations

The productivity and carrying capacity implied by chinampa agriculture feed directly
into the contested population estimates for Tenochtitlan (see [[tenochtitlan]]);
because the surviving Xochimilco chinampas are a much-reduced remnant of the
pre-Conquest system, extrapolating pre-Conquest yields or population support from the
modern zone is an inference, not a direct measurement, and should be treated as
attributed reasoning rather than a settled finding.
