## Definition as Coined

A chinampa is a raised, artificial cultivation plot built within a lake or wetland
canal system, sometimes called (in English-language popular writing) a "floating
garden," though the plots are anchored, not floating. Chinampas belong to a broader
category of raised-field cultivation used widely across New World tropical lowlands,
including by the lowland Classic Maya. Coe and Koontz (2013) note the technique may
have originated with the Teotihuacanos during the Classic period, though this
attribution is not certain.

## Semantic History

The term survives in modern Spanish and English via the Xochimilco district south of
Mexico City, where chinampa cultivation continues to be practiced and where the term is
now most commonly encountered by visitors and scholars alike. Its use for the entire
lake-based agricultural system of the Basin of Mexico is a retrospective, etic
extension from the surviving Xochimilco case back onto the pre-Conquest capital.

## Operationalizations

Construction method, per Coe and Koontz (2013): thick layers of aquatic vegetation were
cut from canal surfaces and piled into mats; mud dredged from canal bottoms was spread
over the mats; willows were planted to anchor the resulting plot. Newly built chinampas
were light enough to support only cane-and-thatch houses; older, more consolidated
plots could carry stone-and-mortar houses, some of two stories with patios and gardens.

[[tenochtitlan]] itself was, in Coe and Koontz's phrase, "in effect... a chinampa
city," laid out with north–south canals for canoe traffic bordered by lanes, crossed by
larger canals, with rectangular house plots between them.

## Applications

Chinampa zones near Tenochtitlan produced a substantial agricultural surplus,
supplemented by field crops grown on nearby land and by maguey cultivation on the
surrounding hill slopes (Coe & Koontz 2013). The axolotl, a large larval salamander
found in the chinampa canals, was eaten stewed in a yellow chile sauce.

Saltiness in the eastern lake threatened chinampa cultivation during floods; the
Texcocan poet-king Nezahualcoyotl responded by building a 10-mile (16 km) dike to seal
off a freshwater lagoon for Tenochtitlan's use (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Critiques and Limitations

The productivity and carrying capacity implied by chinampa agriculture feed directly
into the contested population estimates for Tenochtitlan (see [[tenochtitlan]]);
because the surviving Xochimilco chinampas are a much-reduced remnant of the
pre-Conquest system, extrapolating pre-Conquest yields or population support from the
modern zone is an inference, not a direct measurement, and should be treated as
attributed reasoning rather than a settled finding.
