# Long Mound

## Definition as Coined

**Long mound** / **long barrow** designates elongated Neolithic funerary monuments — earthen long barrows, stone-revetted mounds, and related forms — distributed especially in north and north-west Europe. In *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998), [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] treats them as a primary early monumental tradition whose ground-plans echo LBK longhouses (see [[death-of-the-house]]). The Barkaer (Jutland) case opens the book: structures long read as enormous houses re-interpreted as massive funerary monuments at the start of the local Neolithic.

## Semantic History

Nineteenth-century barrow digging and culture-history typology ("long barrow" as period/type fossil); mid-20th c. cultural affiliations (e.g. TRB, Windmill Hill); later sequence archaeology and symbolic readings of the house–tomb analogy. Distinct from round barrows of the Bronze Age and from chambered [[passage-grave|passage graves]] (though long mounds may contain or develop chambers).

## Operationalizations

Plan elongation; timber or stone chambers/facades; faunal and human bone deposits; relationship to enclosures and settlements; radiocarbon sequences (source-dependent).

## Applications

Early Neolithic monumentality along the agricultural frontier; comparison with enclosure tradition; link to ancestry debates (Bradley: first mounds often closed single burials, not yet "ancestor theatres").

## Bradley 1998 — empirical arc (attributed)

- **Morphology:** Ground plan copies older longhouses; complementary distribution with longhouses and megaliths; secure longhouse–long-barrow coincidence mainly **Kujavia, Poland**.
- **Chronology:** Often later than loess LBK settlement climax; associated mainly with TRB/Michelsberg-type horizons in the fourth millennium BC (as of Bradley’s sources).
- **Mortuary sequence:** Oldest long mounds (with limited exceptions) cover **individual inaccessible graves** under a completed earthwork; later: accessible chambers/passages and multi-individual remains — ancestry/access is a *developed*, not founding, feature.
- **First-generation Polish long barrows:** Closer organizationally to longhouses; built in groups with settlement-like spacing/alignment (Sarnowo vs Brześć Kujawski).
- **Brittany:** Early long/oval mounds mid-fifth millennium BC (e.g. Le Petit Mont phase 1); often closed deposits; later rebuilt toward circular/pentagonal cairns with [[passage-grave|passage graves]].
- **Denmark:** Sequence starts ~500 years after Brittany; main change from ~3400 BC toward passage graves and stronger agricultural impact (Madsen, Midgley, Skaarup via Bradley).
- **Barkaer:** Opening case — “longhouses” re-read as funerary mounds ([[barkaer]]).

Prototype thesis: see [[death-of-the-house]].

## Critiques and Limitations

"Long barrow" lumps diverse structural traditions (earthen vs megalithic; regional groups). House–mound analogy is morphological and symbolic, not ethnic identity. Always date claims to excavation and publication.

## Europe in the Neolithic densification (Whittle 1996) — longhouse transformed, regional early mounds

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 6–7. Parallel to Bradley 1998 above; do not overwrite that densification.)*

**Longhouse → shrine thesis.** Whittle’s most likely source for the long-mound/monument idea is the [[longhouse]] tradition: as longhouses changed and largely disappeared in primary Neolithic areas, monuments began to appear in secondary and primary areas — “the longhouse for the living of past generations was transformed into shrine for the ancestors of a timeless past”; form foreign to daily existence housing spirits of imagined ancestors (attributed; cf. [[death-of-the-house]] and Bradley’s house–tomb analogy without equating the two programmes).

**Early forms and chronology (Whittle position).** Claims for mid-fifth-millennium beginnings of western constructions remain uncertain; he prefers a monument horizon from **later fifth / around or a little before 4000 BC**. Earliest Brittany monuments may not have been passage graves in trapezoidal mounds but **various long mounds enclosing individual cists**, perhaps contemporary with standing menhirs; Les Fouaillages (Guernsey) first phase radiocarbon suggests before 4000 BC. Preferred Brittany development: early long cairns covering cists → very large Morbihan long mounds (Er Grah, Le Moustoir, Saint-Michel, 90–125 m) covering multiple cists and ending **closed** without external access, with axe/bead offerings — parallel track to the accessible [[passage-grave]] tradition (attributed).

**Kujavia and TRB.** Early TRB mounds raised over individual burials at Sarnowo and Wietrzychowice (Kujavia) are important date markers for the early long-mound horizon: pattern of early closed graves under sealing mounds; outliers in the Lublin area (e.g. Sławinek trapezoidal, Milocin-Kolonia triangular, ~50 m). In Kujavia, TRB sites more dispersed onto sandy/morainic soils away from black-soil “islands” of Danubian tradition; notable burial mounds; claimed ardmarks under barrow 8 at Sarnowo on sandy soil near a stream — not the most obvious place for sustained cereal cultivation (attributed, as of Whittle 1996). Cerny-horizon constructions at Passy (Yonne) may mark beginnings of monumentalisation in the Paris-basin longhouse world (attributed).

**Multiple origins caution.** Unwise to insist on single origins: menhirs could rework sacred trees; circular mounds the monumentalisation of round huts more characteristic of forager occupations; Ertebølle elongated shell middens with inserted burials (Bjørnsholm long barrow near a midden) another possible source for the long-mound idea — not reducible to forager “cargo-cult” imitation of farmers (attributed).

**Southern Britain mortuary architecture.** Fussell’s Lodge: linear mortuary area defined by three posts → later longhouse-like trapezoidal post enclosure → chalk/earth mound; ~50 individuals mostly incomplete/disarticulated, spatially sorted age groups, pots and ox skull offerings — ancestral cult house later sealed as inaccessible monument. Wayland’s Smithy I: ≥14 individuals mostly disarticulated/incomplete in wooden frame without mound initially; later small mound then transepted monument above (attributed).

**Scandinavia.** From ENII: earthen long barrows and stone-kerbed long/round mounds (dolmens/dysser); long barrows/early dolmens have few or no bodies while some later passage graves have tens; Scandinavian passage graves largely in **round** mounds — the longhouse idea “must therefore in some sense have faded” (attributed). Cursus monuments in southern/central Britain may enhance pathways of ancestors and spirits, often incorporating pre-existing mortuary structures/long barrows (attributed).
