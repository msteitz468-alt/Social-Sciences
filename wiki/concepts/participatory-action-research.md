# Participatory action research (PAR)

## Definition as Coined

A research methodology, championed in his later career by
[[whyte-william-foote|William Foote Whyte]], in which (attributed) "researchers
invite some members of the organization studied to participate with them in all
phases of the process, from research design through data gathering and analysis,
and on to the practical application of research findings." PAR contrasts with
research done *on* subjects: subjects become co-inquirers. Whyte credited the
practice as existing under other labels since at least the 1960s and consolidated
it in a cluster of publications c. 1989–1991 (*Participatory Action Research*,
ed., 1990; *Social Theory for Action*, 1991).

It appears in this wiki chiefly because Whyte presented PAR, in the 1993
[[whyte-street-corner-society-1943|*Street Corner Society*]] appendix, as his
mature answer to a problem [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|*SCS*]] left
unresolved.

## Semantic History

Whyte located PAR's origin, in his own telling, in a talk his former Cornerville
research assistant Angelo Ralph Orlandella ("Sam Franco") gave at Whyte's Cornell
retirement, which "set me to thinking of my research in a new perspective." The
term sits within a broader family of participatory/action-research traditions
(Kurt Lewin's action research; Freirean participatory research in the global
South) that Whyte's usage does not fully subsume — his is the
industrial/organizational-sociology strand.

## Operationalizations

PAR is graded by *how far* and *in which phases* subjects participate. Whyte held
that it works only where the researcher is embedded in "a continuing organization
capable of developing a long-term relationship" — which is why, he said, he could
not have practiced it in the 1930s under Harvard's "pure science" norm and as a
lone researcher.

## Applications

Whyte offered PAR as a structural remedy for two *SCS*-era problems:

1. **The researcher–informant asymmetry.** Where *SCS* relied on ad hoc
   [[reciprocal-obligation|interpersonal reciprocity]] with Doc — which the
   [[whyte-boelen-cornerville-debate|Boelen critique]] recast as exploitation —
   PAR makes subjects committed partners in outcomes, so they "become less
   concerned with what they are getting personally."
2. **The critical-epistemology challenge.** PAR "opens up ways in which at least
   some members of the organization studied add their own insider voices to those
   of the outsiders," addressing (in Whyte's view) the postfoundational critics'
   objection that the ethnographer monopolizes representation.

## Critiques and Limitations

PAR trades classic detachment for engagement, raising questions about
independence and about whose interests the "action" serves; it is impractical
where no durable host organization exists; and critics note it does not fully
escape the authority problem it targets (the professional still frames the
inquiry). Whyte presented PAR as complementary to, not a replacement for,
[[participant-observation]].
