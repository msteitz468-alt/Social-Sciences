# Practical Reason

## Definition as Coined

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]*, 1976) uses **"practical reason"** to name one of the two "paradigms of anthropological theory" — the paradigm holding that "the cultural order is to be conceived as the codification of man's actual purposeful and pragmatic action." Against it he sets **[[cultural-reason]]**, in which "human action in the world is to be understood as mediated by the cultural design." The book's programmatic claim is that the relation between the two "cannot be compromised; in Louis Dumont's term, the relation can only be an encompassment" — and that cultural reason encompasses practical reason, not the reverse: "it is culture which constitutes utility."

Sahlins distinguishes three genera of practical reason (Preface): **subjective utilitarianism** (culture as the sedimented resultant of individuals maximizing means to ends — "Economic Man" with relativized preferences); **objective/ecological naturalism** (culture as adaptive behavior selected by material/biological advantage); and **praxis theory** in the Marxist sense (culture as the expression of productive activity). All three, he argues, share "an impoverished conception of human symboling" — they treat meaning as the dependent variable of a material logic.

## Semantic History

The term is Sahlins's coinage for a polemical purpose, but it gathers a long anthropological lineage he traces as a "repetitive and cyclical opposition from which anthropology has been unable to escape these past hundred years." He builds it as a "paradigmatic opposition" between [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]] (practical reason: "words here are merely the names of things"; culture as "the sedimentation within culture of the logic of nature") and [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] (cultural reason: "the seeing eye is the organ of tradition"). The lineage of practical reason runs (attributed) through [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s biological functionalism ("every cultural achievement... is an instrumental enhancement of human anatomy"), [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s cultural ecology, [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s technological determinism, and the ecological neofunctionalism of Rappaport and Vayda — which Sahlins calls "ecology fetishism," redescribing cultural phenomena in biological terms and evacuating their meaning.

Sahlins also credits [[marx-karl|Marx]] with recognizing the trap: he quotes Marx's own critique of bourgeois utilitarianism — "the apparent stupidity of merging all the manifold relationships of people in the one relation of usefulness... arises from the fact that, in modern bourgeois society, all relations are subordinated in practice to the one abstract monetary-commercial relation." Practical reason, on this reading, is itself a culturally specific — Western, bourgeois — construction masquerading as universal fact (see [[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]).

## Operationalizations

Sahlins offers a "law of diminishing returns to functionalist explanation" (crediting Bergmann and R. C. Kelly): "the more distant and distinct the cultural practice from the register of its purported function, the less this function will specify the phenomenon" — an argument for the relative autonomy of cultural domains and a diagnostic for where practical-reason explanations fail. His deeper claim (Conclusion) is that natural/material forces are *indeterminate* with respect to social form: "an industrial technology in itself does not dictate... whether it will be operated by men or women, in the day or at night... to produce hand-fed dogs or stall-fed cattle"; selection sets only "limit conditions," a negative determination of the impossible, not a positive specification of cultural form — "before there can be natural selection there is a cultural selection: of the relevant natural facts."

## Applications

The concept organizes Sahlins's whole critique of materialist and utilitarian anthropology, and underwrites his reading of Western capitalism as itself a symbolic order ([[la-pensee-bourgeoise]]). It also frames his later historical anthropology: the opposite pole of practical reason is at stake in the [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate|Cook debate]], where Obeyesekere's insistence that Hawaiians treated Cook with ordinary "practical rationality" is precisely the universalized practical reason Sahlins rejects.

## Critiques and Limitations

Sahlins's opposition is deliberately stark, and he is aware of the charge of idealism, which he denies: "it is not that the material forces and constraints are left out of account, or that they have no real effects on cultural order" — the claim is that the *nature* of the effects cannot be read off from the nature of the forces alone. Critics of the culturalist program (materialist and Marxist anthropologists) reverse the charge, holding that Sahlins's "encompassment" of practical by cultural reason understates material determination. The framework's status as a live dispute is why the concept is marked contested; its Marxist stakes are worked out on [[historical-materialism]] and [[structural-marxism]].
