# Scholastic Disposition

Bourdieu's term (French *disposition scolastique*; Greek *skholè*, leisure) for the point of view of the scholar freed from the urgencies of practice — the distinctive [[habitus|disposition]] generated by the leisure and distance the academic position affords, which silently structures how intellectuals perceive the social world. Central to the reflexive argument of *[[bourdieu-homo-academicus-1984|Homo Academicus]]* (1984) and elaborated in *Pascalian Meditations* (1997).

## Definition as Coined

Bourdieu argues (attributed) that scholastic *skholè* — release from the necessity of immediate practical response — is the social condition of the theoretical, contemplative relation to the world. Its unnoticed effect is the **scholastic fallacy**: projecting the scholar's contemplative relation to practice into the heads of the agents studied, treating practical actors as if they were theorists. Objectifying this disposition is a task of [[participant-objectivation]].

## Semantic History

Implicit in the "theory of practice" critique of intellectualism in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline]]* (1972) and *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|Logic of Practice]]* (1980); named as the scholastic point of view / *skholè* in *Homo Academicus* (1984) and given its fullest treatment in *Pascalian Meditations*.

## Operationalizations

Diagnostic rather than metric: identified through the [[academic-field|academic field]]'s conditions (tenure, distance from economic and political urgency) and through the systematic errors — the scholastic fallacies — it produces in social theory.

## Applications

- Grounding the critique of rational-actor and rule-following models as projections of the scholastic disposition.
- Explaining the intellectual's characteristic misrecognition of ordinary practice.

## Critiques and Limitations

Some argue the concept risks a self-serving move — discrediting rival theories as "scholastic" while exempting Bourdieu's own. Attribute; the charge is part of the [[reflexive-sociology|reflexivity]] debate.
