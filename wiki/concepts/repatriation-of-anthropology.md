# Repatriation of Anthropology

Marcus and Fischer's term for the late-twentieth-century trend of turning ethnographic practice toward **home societies** — especially by anthropologists who first worked abroad — as the condition for a distinctive **cultural critique** (*[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|ACC]]* Ch. 5).

## Definition as Coined

**Repatriation** names multiple modes of domestic ethnography (policy-oriented data, reformist attention to the disadvantaged, and critical-spirit work). Marcus and Fischer concentrate on the **critical** mode: Janus-faced projects that link foreign and domestic subjects so that knowledge of other cultures examines unconscious assumptions at home.

## Semantic History

Distinct from museum/artifact "repatriation." In ACC it is a professional and epistemic relocation of anthropology's object. Later "anthropology at home" literatures partially absorb the trend without always retaining the dual-promise critical frame.

## Operationalizations

Career sequences (abroad then home); funding and permit constraints; prestige still attached to foreign first projects; topics ranging from corporations and laboratories to rock music and urban diversity (as Marcus & Fischer illustrate the trend).

## Applications

Organizes ACC's argument that cultural critique is not optional ornament but one of anthropology's two modern promises; pairs with [[defamiliarization]] techniques in Ch. 6.

## Critiques and Limitations

Marcus and Fischer note that fully dual ethnography (equal seriousness at home and abroad, strongly linked) remains rare; reformist and administrative domestic ethnography may lack critical edge; "home" is itself stratified and not a unitary culture.
