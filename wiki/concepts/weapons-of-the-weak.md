# Weapons of the Weak

Title metaphor and tactical inventory from [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* (1985). Prefer [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] for the analytical program; this page holds the named repertoire and book-title concept.

## Definition as Coined

Scott’s preface inventory:

> “the ordinary weapons of relatively powerless groups: foot dragging, dissimulation, desertion, false compliance, pilfering, feigned ignorance, slander, arson, sabotage, and so on.”

He called these Brechtian or Schweikian forms of class struggle. They are not the opposite of politics but politics under constraint — often the only available politics when open defiance is dangerous.

## Semantic History

The phrase travels as (1) book title, (2) portable tactic list, (3) shorthand for Scott’s resistance program. Later popular and scholarly use often detaches the list from Sedaka’s material transformation and from the book’s rethinking of [[hegemony]]. Scott also insists these weapons are durable **before and after** revolution — foot-dragging against colonial, capitalist, *and* purportedly socialist states (Vietnam and Tanzania as comparative asides, not Sedaka data).

## Operationalizations

In Sedaka (1978–80): combine sabotage and boycotts; threshing piece-rate skirmishes; mutuality among tenants; theft and livestock attacks; reputation warfare (e.g. miser nicknames); covered-track compliance. Ch. 7 supplies the definitional filter that keeps the inventory from meaning “everything the poor do.”

## Critiques and Limitations

Laundry-list citation without intention criteria; romanticization risk; under-specification of when weapons fail (Sedaka’s combine campaign largely failed).
