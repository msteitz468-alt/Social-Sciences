# Hohokam Core–Periphery Model

## Definition as Coined

In **Southwestern archaeology**, the **Hohokam core–periphery model** treats the Phoenix Basin Hohokam as a cultural and demographic core and outlying river valleys as peripheries. *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* Chapter 15 ([[whittlesey-stephanie-m|Stephanie M. Whittlesey]]) argues that three mid-to-late twentieth-century frameworks — **Gladwinian**, **core–periphery**, and **Hohokam regional system** — share an overarching structure: (1) a central zone around which “Hohokam culture” varies, (2) colonization as the process establishing core–periphery contrast, and (3) trait lists defining that contrast.

**Not the same concept as** sociological/world-systems [[core-periphery]] (Wallerstein/Prebisch). Whittlesey notes that Hohokam core–periphery language *apparently* draws on Wallerstein (1974) though Wilcox and Shenk (1977) do not acknowledge that debt; keep the wiki pages distinct.

## Semantic History

| Model | Attribution (as of Whittlesey 1997) | Core move |
|---|---|---|
| **Gladwinian** | Harold & Winifred Gladwin; expanded by Emil Haury | Pre-Classic Phoenix Basin as immigrant core; Colonial expansion into favorable niches; Sedentary “retraction” to Gila–Salt |
| **Core–periphery** | Wilcox & Shenk 1977; rapid uptake after 1978 SAA / Doyel & Plog 1980 | Restrict “Hohokam” to core populations; peripheries dependent or ranked relative to core; irrigation and exotics mark the core |
| **Regional system** | Processual / interaction-sphere lineage (Wilcox and others) | Adaptive system linking settlements; culture/ethnicity de-emphasized as variables |

Haury’s earlier Desert vs River Hohokam dichotomy, Whittlesey argues, environmentally explained core–periphery *differences* while keeping a monothetic culture — foreshadowing the later model by decades.

The U.S. Bureau of Reclamation **structured the [[lower-verde-archaeological-project-study|Lower Verde Archaeological Project (LVAP)]] research design** in core–periphery terms: Phoenix Basin core; **northeastern periphery** = lower Verde + Tonto Basin; **northern periphery** = Agua Fria and tributaries (Whittlesey & [[ciolek-torrello-richard|Ciolek-Torrello]], Ch. 1).

## Operationalizations

Trait suites used to mark “Hohokamness” and core vs periphery (Whittlesey Ch. 15, attributed):

- Decorated pottery (red-on-buff percentages)
- Ballcourts (form, size, investment)
- Cremation mortuary complex vs inhumation
- Courtyard-group site structure
- Large-scale canal [[irrigation]], population density, exotics (shell, mirrors, palettes)

LVAP lower Verde findings Whittlesey uses against simple periphery ranking:

- Pre-Classic buff ware only ~**7.7%** of ceramics regionally; [[scorpion-point-village|Scorpion Point Village]] **4.2%**, CTC **14.3%** (Table 15.1)
- Scorpion Point ballcourts small/informal (Court 1 ~20×9 m; Court 2 ~2×4.6 m) vs larger courts elsewhere in the same “periphery”
- Cremation present but attenuated; Sacaton-age extended inhumations also present
- Courtyard groups present (Scorpion Point, CTC) — variable expression across “peripheries”
- Economic reverse of Wallerstein expectations: little evidence peripheries supplied the core; stronger evidence Phoenix products moved outward; cotton at Scorpion Point (18% ubiquity) may signal exchange production without world-system dependency

## Applications

- Interpreting Pre-Classic ballcourt-period distributions and CAP/CRM project designs in the “northern” and “northeastern” peripheries (including LVAP and CAWCS)
- Lerner (1984) applied Wallerstein-influenced core–periphery to lower Verde survey data (cited and revisited in LVAP)

## Critiques and Limitations

Whittlesey’s LVAP position (attributed):

1. Core–periphery as currently defined may fit **state-level / modern capitalist** systems better than prehistoric Hohokam; using it requires accepting minimally chiefdom-level organization — efforts to identify complex societies in the prehistoric Southwest she judges largely unsuccessful (citing Reid & Whittlesey 1990; Whittlesey & Ciolek-Torrello 1992).
2. **Diversity among and within “peripheries”** undermines a single hierarchical map; each river valley integrated into Hohokam-related interaction differently.
3. Defining cores, boundaries, empty niches, and static dependency is methodologically weak; McGuire (1992) questions the assumption that all groups/relations can be ranked.
4. Doelle & Wallace (1991) and others accept regional interaction but reject simplistic core-initiated change.
5. Whittlesey concludes the model has **outlived its usefulness as a heuristic** and proposes unpacking geographic, religious (“desert regional cult”), economic, and social bases of regional variability instead of monothetic colonization narratives.

LVAP synthesis (Ch. 18–Epilogue): core–periphery framing is **shattered** for the lower Verde case; the valley is better read as a north–south Transition Zone corridor with indigenous traditions, mobility, and joint use — not a passive Phoenix appendage.

Date-stamp: this critique is **as of Whittlesey et al. 1997**; later Southwest literature continues the debate.
