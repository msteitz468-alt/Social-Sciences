# Nomenklatura

## Definition as Coined

**Nomenklatura** originated as an **emic administrative term** in the Soviet party-state: lists of posts whose appointment required approval by a specified party organ. In Western and late-Soviet analytic usage it named both that **appointments system** and the **social group** occupying the higher posts. Mawdsley and White (2000), following that dual usage, treat Central Committee members as the regime's own definition of the top political elite selected through the nomenklatura system — the *nomenklaturnye verkhushki*.

Trotsky spoke of "the bureaucracy," Djilas of the [[new-class]], and Michael Voslensky of the nomenklatura as a ruling class; by the late 1980s Russian writers themselves used *elita* / *praviashchaia elita* for the same circle (Mawdsley & White 2000, preface).

## Semantic History

- **Administrative (emic):** hierarchical lists of jobs and the party committees empowered to fill them.
- **Critical-analytic (mid–late Soviet / émigré):** Voslensky and others as anatomy of a ruling stratum with material privileges and hereditary tendencies.
- **Empirical-operational (Mawdsley & White):** measurable elite population via CC membership + job-slot structure rather than an undifferentiated "ruling class" slogan.

**Do not equate** with Mills's US [[power-elite]] without attribution — different institutional bases (corporate–military–political vs party-state appointments). **Do not conflate** with Anthony's archaeological [[elite-recruitment]] (language-shift patronage).

## Applications

Soviet and comparative communist elite studies; debates on whether the Soviet elite was a class, a status group, or an office-based political estate. Primary collection source: [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-2000]] / [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]].

### Mawdsley & White 2000 (attributed)

- Regime chose top posts through nomenklatura; those chosen sat on party bodies that exercised domination — making the Soviet elite *more* clearly definable than plural Western hierarchies.
- CC members = top tier (*nomenklaturnye verkhushki*); wider lists they cite on the order of ~0.5 million posts / ≥3 million people in late estimates (Ch. 7).
- Privileges attached primarily to **office** within the nomenklatura system (special distribution, housing, services), not to the CC badge alone.
- Post-1991: substantial network continuity and **power conversion** into private property (Komsomol economy, nomenklatura privatisation; Zaslavskaia's "renewed oligarchy" as recorded position).

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary problem (how far down the lists); token CC members (workers, dairymaids) blur pure office-elite definitions; post-1991 continuity claims contested in later transition literature; polemic Voslensky-style uses can overstate class coherence.
