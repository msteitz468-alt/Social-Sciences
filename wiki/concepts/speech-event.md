# Speech Event

## Definition as Coined

In [[hymes-dell|Hymes]]'s ethnography of speaking (*[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations]]* Ch. 2), descriptive units nest as:

| Unit | Role |
|---|---|
| **Speech situation** | Contexts or settings in which speech may occur (not mere relabeling of any activity) |
| **Speech event** | Activities governed by rules or norms for the use of speech |
| **Speech act** | Minimal unit of the set; not identifiable with sentence form |

A speech act may fill a whole event or situation (e.g. a one-word alarm); the same act type may recur in different events; the same event type in different situations. Discourse can be analyzed paradigmatically and syntagmatically in terms of acts. A joke as act differs from a party as situation and from a conversation as event.

Hymes insists the **communicative (or speech) event** is the central starting point for ethnography of communication — not the isolated sentence or abstract code (Ch. 1).

## Semantic History

Austin/Searle **speech-act** philosophy supplies philosophical vocabulary Hymes engages but does not equate with ethnographic **speech event**: acts are not reducible to deep-structure performative verbs; they require ethnography and may be more than "speech" in a narrow sense. Folklore's performance/genre turn (Abrahams, Ben-Amos, Bauman, etc.) is treated as a special contributor to event analysis (Ch. 6).

## The 1972 Statement of the Nesting

Hymes's chapter 1 of *[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972) gives the explicit three-level nesting later carried into *Foundations*: **speech situation** (context associated with, or marked by the absence of, speech — ceremonies, fights, hunts, meals — not itself governed throughout by speech rules) > **speech event** (activities directly governed by rules/norms for speech) > **speech act** ("the minimal term of the set," a level distinct from the sentence). His illustrative nesting: "a party (speech situation), a conversation during the party (speech event), a joke within the conversation (speech act)" (attributed, Hymes). The same act-type can recur across different event-types, and the same event-type across different situations — nesting is not fixed. [[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]]'s Introduction to the same volume frames the speech event as the sociolinguistic analogue of the sentence for grammar — the basic unit sociolinguistic description takes as its object (attributed, Gumperz).

## Operationalizations

- Identify culturally recognized events with norms for speaking (meeting, sermon, teasing, narrative performance, classroom recitation, etc.).
- Analyze with [[speaking-model|SPEAKING components]].
- Distinguish event from situation and from act; test by commutation of components (who, key, channel, etc.).

## Applications

Core to [[ethnography-of-speaking]] and [[ethnography-of-communication]]. Bilingual-education description should start from speech event and repertoire, not from "a language" alone (Hymes Ch. 5). Classroom ethnography of speaking (e.g. Philips, cited by Hymes) is an applied path.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of checklist ethnography if SPEAKING is applied without cultural analysis of what counts as an event.
- Boundary between "speech" events and non-speech communicative events is intentional (ethnography of *communication* broadens the frame) but can blur analytic focus.
- Cross-cultural comparison requires etic grid plus emic validation (Hymes's own demand).


### Bauman & Sherzer 1974 densification

*(Attributed — [[bauman-sherzer-explorations-ethnography-speaking-1974|Bauman & Sherzer 1974]].)* Section IV centerpiece: formal rules for acts/events; events emerge in performance. Cases: Wolof greeting (Irvine); Māori encounter (Salmond); Cuna *namakke/sunmakke/kormakke* constellations (Sherzer); Jewish storytelling subordinated to larger speech events (Kirshenblatt-Gimblett); Sacks joke-in-conversation sequential organization.
