## Definition as Coined

Marx introduces fictitious capital in *Capital* vol. 3, Chapter 25 ("Credit and Fictitious Capital") and gives it its fullest definitional treatment in Chapter 29 ("Banking Capital's Component Parts"): "The formation of fictitious capital is known as capitalization. Any regular periodic income can be capitalized by reckoning it up, on the basis of the average rate of interest, as the sum that a capital lent out at this interest rate would yield." Fictitious capital is capital only in a titular, bookkeeping sense — a claim or title to future revenue that circulates and is priced as though it were capital, while the real capital it purportedly represents either no longer exists, exists only once (not twice, in both the claim and the underlying asset), or was never capital at all.

Marx's paradigm case is the national debt: the state's creditor holds a promissory note, a claim on future tax revenue, and a saleable title, but "the capital lent to the state... has been consumed, spent by the state. It no longer exists" — yet interest continues to be paid and the claim continues to be bought and sold as capital. "The capital of the national debt remains purely fictitious, and the moment these promissory notes become unsaleable, the illusion of this capital disappears." Government bonds and public securities are, on this analysis, "paper duplicates of annihilated capital" (Marx approvingly cites Sismondi on this point in Ch. 30).

## Semantic History

Fictitious capital is Marx's own term (unlike [[merchant-capital]] and [[ground-rent]], which he inherits and redefines), coined specifically to name a phenomenon he treats as the logical extension of the [[trinity-formula]]'s capital fetish: once interest-bearing capital's self-valorizing character has been naturalized as a property of money itself ("as completely the property of money to create value, to yield interest, as it is the property of a pear tree to bear pears" — Ch. 24), any income stream whatsoever can be retrospectively assigned an imaginary "capital" of which it is treated as the interest. Marx traces this logic to its most absurd extension in contemporary (especially German) statistical practice, which capitalized workers' wages as though they were interest on an implied "capital" of labour-power — citing Von Reden's *Vergleichende Kulturstatistik* (1848), which assigned German-Austrian/Prussian agricultural workers a "capital" of 1,500 thalers, English workers 3,750 thalers, French workers 2,000 thalers, and Russian workers 750 thalers. Marx calls this "the absurdity of the capitalist's way of conceiving things" reaching its climax, and notes it is refuted by two facts: the worker must actually labour to "receive" this "interest," and — unlike under slavery, where the worker does have an actual capital value (his purchase price) — he cannot sell the capital value of his own labour-power to a third party.

## Operationalizations

- **National debt** — capitalized tax revenue; no real capital corresponds to the outstanding claims (Ch. 25, Ch. 29, Ch. 30).
- **Shares and joint-stock securities** — an "ownership title, pro rata, to the surplus-value which this capital is to realize"; the underlying capital does not exist twice, once as share value and once as invested capital — it exists only in the enterprise, and the share is merely a claim on future surplus-value. Market price of shares fluctuates independently of the real capital's value and moves inversely with the rate of interest at a constant yield (worked example: a £100 nominal share yielding 10% instead of 5% is capitalized at a market value of £200) (Ch. 29).
- **Price of land** — capitalized rent; land price can rise purely from a fall in the interest rate, with no increase in actual rent, "concealing the real relation" and making rent appear to the buyer as ordinary interest-bearing capital return (Marx, Ch. 46; also Ch. 37). Marx generalizes: purchase of land transfers a title, it does not create it — "the private property of particular individuals in the earth will appear just as absurd [from a higher socio-economic formation] as the private property of one man in other men"; societies and nations "are not the owners of the earth. They are simply its possessors, its beneficiaries" (Ch. 46).
- **Banker's capital** — "The greater part of banker's capital is therefore purely fictitious and consists of claims (bills of exchange) and shares (drafts on future revenues)." Deposits, apart from the reserve fund, "never exist as real deposits" but are simply credits with the banker; the same underlying capital appears duplicated or triplicated across different hands under different titles (Ch. 29). Adam Smith is quoted (*Wealth of Nations* II.iv) illustrating how the same money can serve successively as the instrument of multiple loans/deposits.
- **Devaluation in crisis** — securities' collapse in value during a crisis, so long as it does not reflect any actual standstill in production, leaves "the nation... not a penny poorer by the bursting of these soap bubbles of nominal money capital," since securities represent only accumulated claims and legal titles to future production, not capital itself (Ch. 29). The House of Lords documented an aggregate 1847 devaluation of £114,762,325 in English government bonds, dock/canal, and railway stock.

## Applications

**National debt and Dr. Price's compound-interest fantasy.** Marx traces the ideological lineage of treating fictitious capital as self-multiplying automatically through an extended critique of Dr. Richard Price (*An Appeal to the Public on the Subject of the National Debt*, 1772), who calculated that a penny at compound interest since "our Saviour's birth" would exceed the value of the solar system in gold — a calculation William Pitt the Younger took seriously enough to embed in the 1786 sinking-fund legislation for the British national debt. Marx's refutation: compound-growth claims presuppose a constant profit rate that in fact falls with accumulation (the tendential fall in the rate of profit, argued earlier in the volume). Real capital accumulation is not a compound-interest process for two structural reasons — technological/productivity growth continually devalues existing capital, and the falling rate of profit sets an ever-tightening constraint on surplus labour-time.

**Credit's dual character.** Chapter 27 states what the ingest brief identifies as the concept's most theoretically consequential passage: "If the credit system appears as the principal lever of overproduction and excessive speculation in commerce, this is simply because the reproduction process, which is elastic by nature, is now forced to its most extreme limit... a great part of the social capital is applied by those who are not its owners." Credit accelerates both the productive forces and the world market **and** the violent "outbreaks of this contradiction, crises" — giving credit's "principal spokesmen, from [John] Law through to Isaac Pereire, their nicely mixed character of swindler and prophet."

Yet Marx theorizes credit's dual character as also pointing beyond capitalism. Credit drives the joint-stock company, which he calls "the abolition of capital as private property within the confines of the capitalist mode of production itself": the functioning capitalist is reduced to a salaried manager, the capital-owner to "a mere money capitalist" drawing dividends as pure interest, and the form becomes "a necessary point of transition towards the transformation of capital back into the property of the producers... as their property as associated producers, as directly social property" — while simultaneously reproducing "a new financial aristocracy... company promoters, speculators and merely nominal directors" and "an entire system of swindling and cheating." Marx explicitly names cooperative factories as the positive counterpart: they abolish "the opposition between capital and labour... in the form that the workers in association become their own capitalist," so that joint-stock companies and cooperative factories are both "transition forms from the capitalist mode of production to the associated one," differing only in that joint-stock companies abolish the opposition "in a negative way," cooperatives "in a positive way." Cited empirically: English cooperative-factory accounting data (to 1864) showing cooperative profit exceeding the capitalist average even after paying managers' wages as ordinary variable capital.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Source-reliability caveat (Engels's editorial reconstruction).** Part Five of *Capital* vol. 3 — the material from which most of this concept is drawn — is heavily reconstructed by Engels from Marx's rough manuscript notes; Engels states so explicitly at several points (e.g., describing one passage as "incomprehensible in the original manuscript and has been newly drafted by the editor"). The three-case taxonomy of bank advances in Chapter 28 (unsecured personal credit as genuine capital advance; advance against securities as mere money advance; bill discounting as ordinary purchase/sale) is one such Engels reconstruction and should be flagged accordingly wherever cited. Engels's editorial insertions elsewhere in this material (marked "F.E." in the source) update Marx's analysis with post-1883 developments — e.g., the United Alkali Trust (over thirty former plants folded into one joint-stock company, roughly £6 million total capital) as a case of monopoly "preparing... its future expropriation by society as a whole," and the 1892 table of London bank reserves showing the pyramided, fictitious character of the whole reserve system persisting after Marx's death. These Engels-authored passages are attributed to Engels, not Marx, per the Voice and Attribution Protocol.

**Contested status.** The concept's core claim — that securities, national debt, and land price represent claims on future surplus-value rather than capital in being — is a live piece of Marxian value theory rather than an uncontested empirical finding; it depends on the labour theory of value and on Marx's broader account of capital as a social relation (see [[trinity-formula]]) rather than a thing. Marx's own presentation folds normative and analytic registers together (e.g., calling bankers and financiers "honourable bandits," Ch. 33) in ways that should be read as his polemical voice, not neutral description.
