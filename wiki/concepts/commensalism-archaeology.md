# Commensalism (archaeological indicator)

## Definition as Coined

In ecology, **commensalism** is a relationship benefiting one species without significantly harming the other. In Levantine prehistory, “commensal” species (especially house mouse *Mus*, certain birds, and canids) have been used as **biological markers of sedentism** — the claim that their presence in Natufian sites signals long-term human occupation and anthropogenic niches (Tchernov 1984, 1991; debated).

## Semantic History

Tchernov’s Natufian papers made commensals a signature of SW Asian sedentary foragers. Critics (e.g., Tangri & Wyncoll 1989) questioned whether commensal presence uniquely proves sedentism. Weissbrod et al. (in [[bar-yosef-valla-natufian-foragers-2013]]) revisit with ethnoarchaeology and ecology: “Was it truly a Natufian phenomenon?”

## Operationalizations

Micromammal taphonomy (distinguishing cultural vs natural deposition — Weissbrod et al. 2005 on el-Wad); relative abundance of human-associated taxa; comparison with ethnoarchaeological Maasai settlement data (Weissbrod).

## Applications

- Natufian el-Wad, Eynan, and other core-area sites as putative early anthropogenic niches.
- Dog–human association (Natufian puppy burials) as related but distinct domestication trajectory.

## Critiques and Limitations

Commensals can enter sites without year-round occupation; taphonomy is decisive; equating Natufian with the *origin* of commensalism overstates a single period. Keep findings attributed and method-bound.
