# Teleological Action

## Definition as Coined

**Teleological action** is the basic model of goal-directed action that [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]] (attributed, *[[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v1-1984|TCA Vol. 1]]*) takes as the analytic starting point of modern action theory: an actor realizes an end by intervening in the world with chosen means. When the actor calculates efficiency of means under technical rules, the action is **instrumental**; when the actor orients to the decisions of other actors under rules of rational choice, teleological action becomes **[[strategic-action]]**.

Habermas (attributed) argues that classical sociology — especially [[weber-max|Weber]] — starts from this solitary purposive actor and therefore models linguistic understanding as reciprocal teleological influence rather than as reaching understanding. That "switchpoint" is where Weber "parts company with a theory of communicative action."

## Semantic History

Rooted in Aristotelian practical philosophy and modern decision theory; in TCA it is the foil against which [[normatively-regulated-action]], [[dramaturgical-action]], and [[communicative-action]] are differentiated by world-relations and validity aspects.

## Operationalizations

Standard means-ends analysis; game-theoretic strategic extension when other actors enter as decision-makers.

## Applications

Baseline of [[purposive-rationality]]; necessary but insufficient model for a theory of society — must be expanded by communicative coordination (TCA program).

## Critiques and Limitations

Habermas does not reject teleological action; he rejects treating it as the *sole* fundamental concept of social action.
