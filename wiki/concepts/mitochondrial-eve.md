## Definition as Coined

"Mitochondrial Eve" (popularly "African Eve") names the single most-recent common mitochondrial ancestor of all living humans, as inferred from a 1987 study by Cann, Stoneking, and Wilson analyzing mtDNA from 147 individuals. The resulting tree had an African root and an estimated coalescence around 200,000 years ago: "The mutation rates that probably underlie present diversity place the common ancestor near 200 thousand years (ky) ago." Klein notes the popular label was applied after the fact: "the female bearer of the original African mtDNA type was popularly dubbed 'African Eve.'" (as of Klein 1999)

## Semantic History

Klein (attributed) cautions that the concept, properly understood, establishes only a genetic coalescence point, not the anatomical modernity of the ancestor herself — the two are frequently conflated in popular treatment but are logically separate claims. He further reports (attributed) that the original study's statistical method (maximum parsimony) was misapplied: reanalyses of the same dataset produce many equally parsimonious trees, some rooting outside Africa, which undermines the specific tree topology used to argue for an African root: "There are no statistical grounds for preferring African trees to their non-African alternatives." Klein is careful to separate this critique of the tree-topology argument from the coalescence date itself, noting that the roughly 200,000-year figure and a broader African-origin conclusion are separately supported by other genetic lines of evidence — mtDNA is not the sole pillar of the argument (as of Klein 1999).

Comparative data cited by Klein: Neanderthal mtDNA (from the Feldhofer type specimen) differs from living human mtDNA by roughly 27 substitutions on average, versus about 8 substitutions typically found among living humans, implying a Neanderthal–modern-human mitochondrial lineage split around 690,000–550,000 years ago (as of Klein 1999) — a finding Klein treats as independent support for deep separation between the two lineages, distinct from the mitochondrial-Eve coalescence question proper.

## Operationalizations

The concept is operationalized through molecular-clock coalescence analysis of mtDNA sequence diversity: a mutation rate is calibrated (often against fossil or biogeographic divergence dates), applied to observed sequence diversity among sampled populations, and used to estimate the time to most recent common ancestor. Klein reports mismatch-distribution analyses across 23 of 25 sampled populations suggesting population expansions beginning around 50,000 years ago in populations that were already isolated before expanding — raising the further possibility (a [POSITION], not settled) of multiple, temporally successive dispersals from different parts of Africa rather than a single wave.

## Applications

Klein treats the genetic evidence overall (mtDNA coalescence near 200 ka, Y-chromosome estimates, nuclear DNA tree topology, and the observation that genetic, dental, and cranial diversity are all greatest in Africa) as providing "strong if qualified support" for the Out of Africa 2 (recent African origin) model within [[modern-human-origins-debate]], while stressing this support is not conclusive on its own — closure, in his view, depends mainly on fossil form and dating rather than genetics alone.

## Critiques and Limitations

The central methodological critique, per Klein, concerns the original tree-building method rather than the coalescence estimate: parsimony-based tree reconstruction from the 1987 dataset does not statistically favor an African-rooted tree over equally parsimonious alternatives rooting elsewhere. Because the wider genetic case for Out of Africa 2 does not rest on tree topology alone, Klein regards the African-origin conclusion as still reasonably supported, but flags the popular "Eve" narrative as having outrun the statistical rigor of its founding analysis. As with all archaeogenetic claims in Klein's synthesis, this entire assessment is date-stamped "(as of Klein 1999)": Klein's second edition predates the post-2010 ancient-DNA and genome-wide admixture literature (e.g., Neanderthal and Denisovan nuclear genome sequencing), which substantially revised the simple "clean replacement, no interbreeding" reading of Out of Africa 2 that Klein treats as the stronger of two live variants.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Attributed; (as of Stringer 2012). Early nuclear aDNA horizon — absolute calibrations remain contested.*

### Construct and 1987 paper

Cann, Stoneking & Wilson (1987 *Nature*): ~150 global mtDNA types; most-parsimonious tree rooted common maternal ancestor in Africa ~200 ka — “Mitochondrial Eve” / “lucky mother” because mtDNA tracks the maternal line; mtDNA mutates faster than nuclear DNA (as of Stringer 2012). Framed as strong [[recent-african-origin|RAO]] evidence; heavily criticized (alternative trees not always African-rooted; calibration; “African” samples often African American); multiregionalists temporarily dismissed the results (attributed). Stringer holds that increasingly detailed later analyses showed the 1987 conclusions **essentially correct** though somewhat overinterpreted (as of Stringer 2012).

### Lucky mother vs bottleneck; not sole ancestor

Some calculations place last common mtDNA ancestor at **<150 ka**; living human mtDNA varies far less than great ape species’ (as of Stringer 2012). Low variation linked either to a recent **bottleneck** or to chance extinction of other maternal lines (“lucky mother”) — Eve need not have been a special female in special times; she gained unique status retrospectively through mtDNA’s good fortune (attributed). Eve is only the source of all known living human **mtDNA** variants; many other individuals contributed Y and autosomal DNA; mtDNA is inherited effectively as a unit like a single gene, while gene variants have separate coalescence histories (as of Stringer 2012).

### Haplogroups and African diversity

Living humans average ~50 substitutions vs reconstructed Eve mtDNA; haplogroups cluster shared mutations (as of Stringer 2012). Most ancient haplogroup **L** ancestral from Eve across most African populations; L0–L3 ordered by branching: L0 southern/eastern Africa (oldest among Khoisan); L1 mainly central/western (including forest “pygmy” populations); L2 most common in Africa (~25%); L3 youngest major group, common sub-Saharan especially Bantu-speaking populations, thought eastern African origin and **source of non-African M and N** (as of Stringer 2012). African mtDNA holds most ancient lineages and greatest diversity — consistent with African origin and largest ancient population size (attributed). Some studies: L0/L1 grew steadily early; L2 expanded recently; L3 grew rapidly ~70 ka — possibly linked to spillover into western Asia (as of Stringer 2012).

### Recalibrated clocks (Endicott, Ho, Metspalu, Stringer)

Standard calibrations often assume human–chimp split ~6 Ma; short-term human mtDNA rates (islands, family studies) are much faster than long-term chimp–human rates (purifying selection offered as explanation) (as of Stringer 2012). Collaboration with Endicott, Ho, Metspalu using rates **not** based on ~6 Ma split: younger ages — African Eve ~**135 ka** (vs ~200 ka), exit from Africa ~**55 ka**, Americas ~14 ka — consistent (per Stringer) with then-latest fossil/archaeological data (as of Stringer 2012). If correct: younger Neanderthal–modern divergence; multi-millennial gap between first modern fossils in Africa and Eve; doubt on early exits toward China and Australia; geneticists should reconsider chimp-calibration for recent events (attributed). Recalibration also links L3 origin/first expansion ~60 ka to East African behavioral evolution and Out of Africa; non-African founder subsample from mtDNA sometimes estimated as low as a few hundred (as of Stringer 2012). M/N mtDNA and Asian Y-DNA ages make pre-Toba Indians (if modern) extinct or replaced if they were early moderns after Toba ash ~73 ka (Petraglia via Stringer).

### Y-chromosome parallel

No evidence (as of Stringer 2012) of more ancient surviving Y variants from archaics; “Adam” previously ~80 ka, Cruciani et al. ~142 ka most likely central/northwestern Africa; non-African male common ancestor ~40 ka with lower diversity (attributed).
