# Peasant Dilemma

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 1), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] states the **perennial problem of the peasantry** as balancing the demands of the external world against the need to provision the household. Two **strategies**:

1. **Increase production** — step up labor on the holding, raise marketable produce; requires mobilizing land, labor, capital/credit; rare if funds of rent and ceremonial are heavy; facilitated when traditional rent liens weaken and/or ceremonial commitments can be refused (yeomen, rich peasants, kulaks; Protestant conversion cutting fiesta costs).
2. **Curtail consumption** — reduce diet and external purchases; rely on domestic-group labor; underconsumption as explanation of "traditionalism" and fear of novelty; supports maintenance of traditional social ties and ceremonial funds as insulation against outside penetration.

Strategies are not mutually exclusive; relative dominance tracks strength/weakness of the larger order; individuals differ in risk appetite. Literary clichés of the immovable peasantry are false: peasantry is always dynamic between two poles. A holding can also be a **sanctuary** in war and depression (caloric autonomy — "40 acres and a mule").

**Priority of needs (Wolf):** to the outsider the peasant is "a sheep to be shorn"; for the peasant, caloric minimum, [[replacement-fund]], and required ceremonial payments are **functionally and logically prior** to outsider demands (lord or merchant) — though culturally relative (China ≠ Puerto Rico).

**Chaianov block (quoted):** under land scarcity the peasant family intensifies labor, accepts lower remuneration per unit of work, rents/buys land at prices unprofitable from a capitalistic standpoint — to employ surplus family labor. This underwrites why production strategy is not simple profit maximization.

## Semantic History

Wolf's formulation sits between Chayanov's self-exploitation under land scarcity (quoted at length) and political accounts of surplus extraction. Prefigures later debates on moral economy, risk aversion, and middle-peasant commercialisation.

## Operationalizations

- When rent/ceremonial liens weaken → production strategy and internal stratification (English yeomen; Chinese rich peasants; Russian kulaks/"fists"; Middle American Protestant conversion cutting fiesta costs).
- When power structure is strong and markets threatening → consumption curtailment and communal ceremonial defense.
- Dual play in different contexts at once; periods alternate as the overarching order strengthens or weakens.

## Applications

- Interpreting "conservatism" as balance-sheet strategy, not essence.
- Predicting conditions for peasant differentiation and for closed-community defense.
- Link from household funds to [[peasant-movements]] when balance collapses under war/state failure.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Binary can oversimplify multi-cropping, migration, wage labor, and state subsidies.
- Agency of women and non-heads of household under-specified in the primer's "man/holding" language.
