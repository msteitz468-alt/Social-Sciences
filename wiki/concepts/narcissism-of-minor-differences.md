# Narcissism of Minor Differences

## Definition as Coined

Sigmund Freud's term for the phenomenon whereby communities that are close neighbors — adjoining, intertwined, similar — invest disproportionate hostility in the small differences that distinguish them. Freud developed the idea across several works (from "The Taboo of Virginity," 1917, to *Civilization and Its Discontents*, 1930), treating such hostility as a relatively harmless discharge of aggression that binds each group internally.

*Note: this page is currently sourced only through Appadurai's discussion in* Fear of Small Numbers *(2006); the Freudian texts themselves are not ingested — the coinage history above is standard-reference background stated at summary level.*

## Semantic History

The concept migrated from psychoanalysis into political analysis. Michael Ignatieff (*The Warrior's Honour*, 1998), drawing on the Yugoslav wars, used it to explain why groups like Serbs and Croats — with centuries of interwoven histories, languages, and identities — came to invest so much in mutual hatred. [[appadurai-arjun|Arjun Appadurai]] (2006) then extended it further: he argues the insight can be scaled from psychodynamics to "complex, large-scale, public forms of violence" by seeing narcissistic wounds "at the level of public ideologies about group identity" as incitements to [[predatory-identity|predatory identities]] — with his own [[anxiety-of-incompleteness]] (the small gap between majority status and total national purity) supplying the mechanism Freud's essay lacked.

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized as a measured variable; used interpretively in discourse analysis of majoritarian rhetoric and nationalist mobilization.

## Applications

Ignatieff (1998): Serb–Croat enmity in the 1990s. Appadurai (2006): Nazi Germany, Rwanda, Kosovo, Mumbai — cases where proximity and intimacy accompanied extreme degradation of victims.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested as an explanation: critics of the psychoanalytic register question whether an individual-level mechanism can be scaled to collective violence at all — Appadurai's reworking is itself a response to that limitation, replacing the psychic economy with a demographic-political one.
