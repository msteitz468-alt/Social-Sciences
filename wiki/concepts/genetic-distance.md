# Genetic Distance

## Definition as Coined

**Genetic distance** is a quantitative measure of how different two populations
are in allele (gene-type) frequencies. In [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]'s
program (summarized in *Genes, Peoples and Languages*, 2000), distances computed
from classical markers (blood groups and proteins) and later DNA polymorphisms
are the raw material for evolutionary **trees** and for comparing genetic,
geographic, and linguistic differentiation.

Cavalli-Sforza noted (attributed, as of 2000) that several formulas exist, are
"fairly complex," and yet yield "very similar results overall" for broad
human-population comparisons.

## Semantic History

- Classical marker era (ABO, Rh, HLA, proteins) → early DNA (restriction sites,
  microsatellites, mtDNA, Y) → genome-wide SNP distances and *f*-statistics
  ([[admixture-statistics]]). Vocabulary shifted from "genetic distance" trees
  toward admixture graphs; the older scalar remains in use for descriptive work.
- Race classification debates often misuse distance: large distance ≠ discrete
  "race" (see [[race-and-genomics-debate]]; Cavalli-Sforza 2000 ch. 1).

## Operationalizations

Pairwise allele-frequency distances; tree-building (Edwards & Cavalli-Sforza
methods; neighbor-joining, etc.); correlation of genetic with geographic miles
(isolation by distance) and with linguistic classification depth.

## Applications

Human continental structure; European synthetic maps (with
[[principal-component-analysis]]); tests of gene–language co-dispersion;
Parma-valley village differentiation under [[genetic-drift]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Present-day distances average over unknown histories of admixture and replacement
  (Reich 2018 critique of the present-day-as-archive bet).
- Tree models assume splits without subsequent mixture — often false for humans.
- Different marker systems and ascertainment schemes change fine structure.
