# Informal Eviction

## Definition as Coined

In [[desmond-matthew|Desmond]]’s Milwaukee research (*[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]* 2016), **informal evictions** are forced moves that remove tenants without a completed judicial eviction — paying tenants a few hundred dollars to leave by week’s end, removing the front door, hiring “heavies,” or other off-the-books pressure. Desmond: nearly half of forced moves among Milwaukee renting families are informal evictions “that take place in the shadow of the law” (Prologue, attributed).

## Semantic History

Court-centered “eviction” statistics and tenant folk definitions (sheriffs, lock changes, curb piles) systematically miss informal removals. Desmond designed MARS items to capture forced moves given tenants’ understandings; asking only “Have you ever been evicted?” vastly underestimates involuntary removal (About This Project / Notes).

## Operationalizations

MARS 2009–2011: of forced moves, **48% informal**, 24% formal court eviction, 23% landlord foreclosure, 5% building condemnation — implying roughly two non-judicial forced moves per judicial eviction (Desmond 2016). Excluding informal eviction drops forced-move estimates toward AHS-like levels that understate the problem.

## Applications

Core to *Evicted*’s claim that court dockets understate the true volume of forced mobility and that due process is absent for a large share of displacements.

## Critiques and Limitations

Survey recall and classification boundaries (what counts as “forced”) remain measurement challenges; informal practices vary by local law and landlord type.
