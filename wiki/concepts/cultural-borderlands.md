# Cultural Borderlands

Developed by [[rosaldo-renato|Renato Rosaldo]] in *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]* (1989), drawing on Chicano writing (Américo Paredes, Sandra Cisneros) and Gloria Anzaldúa's *Borderlands*.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Rosaldo 1989.)* Rosaldo used "cultural borderlands" to name the zones of crossing, mixture, and difference — around lines of "sexual orientation, gender, class, race, ethnicity, nationality, age, politics, dress, food, taste" — that classic anthropology's picture of bounded, homogeneous cultures rendered marginal. He argued these are not empty transitional spaces but "sites of creative cultural production" that a renewed social analysis must study directly.

## Semantic History

*(Attributed.)* Rosaldo argued classic norms produced zones of "cultural visibility and invisibility": subordinate groups (Mexican Indians in "refuge regions," Philippine "cultural minorities") were credited with "culture," while dominant groups (ladinos, lowland Filipinos, North American professionals) were treated as culturally transparent — a "dark ratio" inversely linking culture and power. He drew the term's charge from Gloria Anzaldúa's *Borderlands/La Frontera* and its figure of "the new mestiza."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Rosaldo argued the analyst's own tacit norm (North American upper-middle-class professional life) is the unmarked baseline against which others are said to "have culture" or to "somatize"; making that baseline visible is part of the method.

## Applications

Rosaldo read three Chicano narratives — Américo Paredes's *"With His Pistol in His Hand"*, Ernesto Galarza's *Barrio Boy*, and Sandra Cisneros's *The House on Mango Street* — as a trajectory in which the "Chicano warrior hero" moves "from bounded cultural purity through the mockery of patriarchs to encounters at the border zones of everyday life," reworking identity away from the "unified subject" toward multiplex, borderland identities. The concept pairs with his call to put **culture in motion** (agency/structure/events; see [[culture]]) and with the [[positioned-subject|positioned subject]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Rosaldo held that in a globally interdependent, "postcolonial" world the classic notion of an "authentic culture" as an autonomous, internally coherent whole survives only as a "useful fiction."
