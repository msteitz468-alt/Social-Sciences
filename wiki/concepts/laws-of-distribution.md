# Laws of Distribution (Lenski)

## Definition as Coined

[[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] (*[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]*, Ch. 3) states two **laws of distribution** derived from postulates about man and society:

1. **First law (need / survival of cooperation):** Men share the product of their labors to the extent required to ensure the survival and continued productivity of those whose actions are necessary or beneficial to themselves — compliance is required for species survival under a social division of labor.
2. **Second law (power over surplus):** Goods and services **above** the minimum required to keep producers alive and productive (the **surplus**) are distributed by **power** — the probability of persons or groups carrying out their will even when opposed (Weber’s definition as Lenski cites it). Qualification: “nearly all” of the surplus.

## Semantic History

The dualism is Lenski’s device for synthesizing radical (power/conflict) and conservative/cooperative insights: selfish interests compel cooperation, so pure power-only distribution cannot exhaust the product. Distinct from juridical “laws of distribution” in classical political economy and from Marxian laws of motion — Lenski’s are middle-range sociological propositions for comparative stratification.

## Operationalizations

Surplus size inferred from technology and productivity; power via force and institutionalized power (position, property). Degree of inequality predicted to rise with surplus until industrial reversal.

## Applications

Core mechanism of [[ecological-evolutionary-theory]]; applied type-by-type in Chs. 5–12 of *Power and Privilege*:

- **Hypotheses (Lenski):** technologically primitive societies distribute largely by need (little surplus); with technological advance an increasing share is distributed by power.
- **Industrial modifiers:** citizenship rights and progressive redistribution re-expand need/utility shares without abolishing power over residual surplus; occupational systems distribute most cash income for non-property-elite members.
- **Ch.13 scorecard:** issue of how rights are acquired is scored as mixed precisely because necessities follow the first law and surplus the second.

## Critiques and Limitations

How to measure “minimum required” and surplus cross-culturally; whether altruism, ideology, or citizenship rights systematically violate the second law (Lenski treats ideology and later citizenship/redistribution as modifiers); functionalist critics reject power primacy over surplus; later wealth data may re-weight industrial “need” shares.
