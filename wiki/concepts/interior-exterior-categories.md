# Interior and Exterior Categories

## Definition as Coined

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (*[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]*, Ch. 3): **interior categories** belong to a particular organization's internally visible structure (staff vs line, enlisted vs officers, faculty vs students, management vs workers). **Exterior categories** do not originate in that organization but often install systematic differences in activities, rewards, power, and prospects within it (gender, race, citizenship, ethnicity, class). Labels mark relation to the organization at hand — endpoints of a continuum from very local to omnipresent — not fixed content.

**Matching** an interior boundary with an exterior pair (white/black, citizen/foreigner) imports established scripts and common knowledge, **lowers the cost** of maintaining inequality, and lets [[organizational-emulation|emulation]] reinforce [[exploitation]] and [[opportunity-hoarding]].

## Semantic History

Organizational sociology of roles meets stratification categories; Tilly's pairing makes the **match** itself the inequality engine (not interior hierarchy alone, nor exterior prejudice alone). Classic vignette: domestic service — interior servant matched to regionally varying exterior categories (Mexican, Irish, black women in 1930s U.S.).

## Operationalizations

Audit of which exterior markers predict interior rank; hiring and promotion channel analysis; formal rules that ban matching vs informal matching that persists; conversion hypotheses (gradients and separate frontiers tend toward reinforced interior–exterior matching; reverse movements rare when costs of distinction rise).

## Applications

Gendered job titles and sex-typed establishments (Petersen–Morgan occupation-establishment segregation); racialized citizenship and New Deal administration; immigrant niches; soft-skills hiring mapping race onto job fitness; affirmative-action implications (break match of exterior categories to reward sites, not only hire-point decisions).

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary between "interior" and "exterior" can be porous (professional credentials are both); intersection of multiple exterior categories complicates simple matching models; Tilly's race vs gender structural contrast (race often coincides with household/community boundaries; gender cuts through them) implies different intervention paths — not full interchangeability in practice.
