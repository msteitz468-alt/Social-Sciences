# Synoikism

## Definition as Coined

In classical Greek usage, **synoikism** (from *syn* “together” + *oikos* “home”) names the process by which multiple rural communities abandon dispersed villages and coalesce into a single city, often on a defensible or strategically chosen site. [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] and [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] in *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* (1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]]) apply the term **as historical analogy** to the founding of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] c. 500 BCE: not slow village-to-city growth, but deliberate multi-community relocation onto an unoccupied mountain fortress in a former central-valley no-man’s-land.

## Semantic History

- **Greek political history:** Documented cases (Megalopolis 368 BCE; Syracuse under Gelon from 485 BCE) show forced and voluntary resettlement, wall-building, cult relocation, and power-building as well as defense. Nancy Demand’s study (as summarized by Marcus & Flannery) stresses overwhelming external threat to autonomy as the usual motive, with unintended consequences for the old autonomous *polis*.
- **Oaxaca application (1996):** Marcus & Flannery treat Monte Albán as a New World case of the same *kind* of political act — rapid founding, defensive wall, summit public architecture, abandonment of many valley-floor villages within 10–15 km — while insisting the Greek cases are **analogies**, not evidence of diffusion or identity.
- **Possible second instance:** They float [[jalieza|Jalieza]]’s spectacular Monte Albán IIIa growth as a possible later valley example of urban relocation/synoikism (attributed hypothesis).

## Operationalizations

| Diagnostic (Marcus & Flannery 1996) | Oaxaca application (attributed) |
|---|---|
| City where none existed | Monte Albán uninhabited in late Rosario; est. ~5,280 by Early MA I; ~17,242 Late MA I |
| Multi-community participation | Blanton’s three dense residential clusters in early high-density zone; Etla abandonments at end of Rosario |
| Defensible / strategic site | 400 m mountain; ~3 km western/northern defensive walls |
| Distance of oikists | Many founders from within 10–15 km (parallel to Megalopolis pattern as analogy) |
| Political motive | Dual: external-threat defense (strain) + power-building (interest); authors prefer SJM-led confederacy scenario over anti-SJM coalition |

## Applications

- Primary case: founding of [[monte-alban]] and the start of [[zapotec-civilization]] state formation ([[state-formation]]).
- Theoretical home: [[action-theory-archaeology]] — actors create larger political units with unintended economic consequences (urban labor supply; need for piedmont irrigation).
- Related survey evidence: Settlement Pattern Project population explosion and piedmont strategy under MA I–Ic.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Analogy risk:** Greek–Oaxaca structural comparison can overfit; authors flag it as analogy, not proof.
- **Who founded Monte Albán?** Rival scenarios (Tlacolula+Valle Grande vs SJM-led Etla confederacy) remain **positions**; Marcus & Flannery prefer the latter.
- **Disembedded-capital alternative:** [[blanton-richard|Blanton]]’s “disembedded capital” reading (in *[[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]]* and related work) emphasizes neutral location and confederacy without requiring the full Greek synoikism package — record as competing interpretive frame, not refuted here.

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — the analogy restated

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The 2022 volume independently draws the same explicit analogy between Monte Albán's founding and Greek synoecism — particularly the founding of Athens — citing the same sources as the 1996 *Cloud People* treatment above (Hignett 1958:34; Osborne 2019:155), reading the population aggregation of 9th-century-BC Attica as a decision by discrete communities "to cooperate" for conflict minimization and to "insert themselves into the wider world." Framed, as in 1996, as analogy rather than diffusion or identity, and integrated here into the authors' broader [[collective-action-theory|collective-action]] account of Monte Albán's founding: they characterize Period I's institutional form as "more faceless polities that did not materially celebrate or legitimize chiefs or rulers," consistent with collective-action expectations rather than the "neoevolutionist" chief/ruler-legitimizing model. They also float the post-100 BC growth of the Huamelulpan urban complex in the Mixteca Alta as a further possible instance of the same nucleation dynamic (plain text — no established page).
