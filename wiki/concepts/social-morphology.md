# Social morphology

## Definition as Coined

**Social morphology**, in [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]’s usage, is the study of the **substratum of collective life** — collective “ways of being” as distinct from “ways of functioning”: how many people associate, how they are distributed on the territory, how segments combine, the design of dwellings, the network of communications, political division of space, etc. (*[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|Rules]]* Ch. I, IV–V; subsequent notes). He treats these morphological facts as genuine [[social-fact|social facts]]: they impose themselves on individuals as do legal and moral rules (attributed).

In Chapter V he locates the principal **efficient causes** of social facts in the constitution of the **inner social environment** — especially its **volume** and **[[dynamic-density|dynamic (moral) density]]** — rather than in individual psychology or in a purely external (cosmic) environment (attributed). Morphology is thus both a **classificatory** resource ([[social-types]]) and an **explanatory** one.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Usage |
|---|---|
| *Division of Labor* (1893) | Dynamic density as cause of the division of labor; population volume + moral density |
| *Rules* (1895) | Morphological facts in definition of social fact; basis of social species; inner social environment as key explanatory locus |
| *Année* / notes (e.g. 1899 “Social Morphology”) | Subfield label for the material substratum |
| Later human geography / urban sociology | Partial absorption; “morphology” vocabulary thins in Anglophone theory while density/urbanization variables remain |

## Operationalizations

- Population volume and density; urban concentration.
- Segmentary structure (horde, clan, polysegmentary compounds) — [[social-types]].
- Communication and transport networks; settlement form.
- In *Suicide*: family density, religious community structure, political integration as structural conditions of rates.

## Applications

- Causal engine of specialization and organic solidarity (*Division of Labor*).
- Classification of societies (*Rules* Ch. IV).
- Bridge from “anatomical” social facts to “physiological” currents of opinion (Ch. I: ways of being as consolidated ways of acting).

## Critiques and Limitations

Attributed risks: morphological determinism (underplaying representations and politics); 19th-c. ethnographic ranking of segmentary forms; difficulty measuring “moral density” independently of the effects it is invoked to explain. Durkheim’s later religious sociology intensifies the role of [[collective-representations]] without abandoning morphological conditions (1895 “watershed” toward religion — subsequent letters in the Lukes volume).
