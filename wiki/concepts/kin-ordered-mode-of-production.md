# Kin-Ordered Mode of Production

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982, Ch. 3), [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] names the **kin-ordered mode of production** as the deployment of [[social-labor|social labor]] primarily through **kinship relations** — descent, marriage alliance, and related idioms — rather than through capitalist wage labor or tributary political extraction. Controllers of labor (elders, chiefs, lineage heads) act *as kin*, not as seignorial lords or capitalists. Wolf rejects "primitive" as analytically empty (after Meillassoux: calling peoples classless/acephalous/stateless says what they are not, not what they are) and notes kinship is empirically variable — coresidence often more significant than genealogy (Kroeber, Titiev, Leach, Fortes).

## Semantic History

Intervenes in debates over "primitive," "communal," and "domestic" modes. Adjacent to but not identical with Sahlins's [[domestic-mode-of-production|domestic mode of production]]: kin-ordered stresses **relations that mobilize labor** (including beyond the household). Against evolutionary chiefdom stages (Service), Wolf shifts from redistributive-society typology to how social labor is commanded — some "chiefs" remain locked in kinship; others break through toward tributary or capitalist partnerships. Not an evolutionary stage: most societies ethnographers met are secondary/tertiary products of expansion (citing Fried on the tribe as secondary phenomenon).

## Operationalizations

Who may call on whose labor; how products are shared; whether office is still coded as kinship; open- vs closed-resource variants. **Articulation** with mercantile wealth (fur trade putting-out, slave-trade capture, cash-crop peonage) reorganizes kin-ordered systems — specialization, dependency, ethnogenesis — without automatically installing capitalist purchase of labor-power.

## Applications

Wolf's ethnohistory of Northeastern woodlands ([[iroquois|Iroquois]], Huron, Abenaki), Great Lakes, Plains horse pastoralists, Northwest Coast, Siberian *iasak*, and West/Central African supply systems of the [[atlantic-slave-trade|slave trade]]: kin-ordered labor reorganized by trade, war, and middleman politics — not timeless tribes.

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Kinship" can smuggle functionalist assumptions or underplay inequality within kin systems (gender, age, slavery).
- Boundary with tributary mode is historically fuzzy (chiefdoms, early states).
- Not a claim that kinship is the *content* of culture — only that it is the primary *ordering of social labor* in this mode.
