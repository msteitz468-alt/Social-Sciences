## Definition as Coined

"Companion species" is [[haraway-donna|Donna Haraway]]'s term for entities — human and
non-human — bound together in relations of mutual constitution rather than prior,
separable existence. The term predates *Staying with the Trouble*: Haraway coined it in
*The Companion Species Manifesto* (2003), and in the 2016 book she elaborates rather than
re-coins it. As Haraway states there, the category "helps me refuse human exceptionalism
without invoking posthumanism" *(Haraway 2016)*, [[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]].

Haraway derives the term's etymology from *cum panis* — "with bread," "at table
together" — glossing companion species as "not 'posthuman' but 'com-post'": partners
constituted through shared worldly entanglement, not preceding it. As she puts it,
partners "do not precede the knotting."

## Semantic History

The concept's semantic arc runs from *The Companion Species Manifesto* (2003), where it
was developed primarily through Haraway's relationship with her dog and dog-training
practice, to *Staying with the Trouble* (2016), where it is generalized into a broader
relational ontology encompassing pigeons, sheep, orchids, acacia ants, and bacteria.
Across this arc the term is consistently attributed to Haraway and consistently framed
as "relentlessly becoming-with" — a formulation she credits in part to
[[despret-vinciane|Vinciane Despret]]'s notion that partners are "rendered capable"
through relational encounter: "becoming-with, not becoming, is the name of the game"
*(Haraway 2016, citing Despret)*.

## Operationalizations

Haraway does not operationalize companion species as a measurable construct; it functions
as a figurative and theoretical category deployed narratively across case studies rather
than coded or quantified. The term resists the kind of operational definition applicable
on a `measured_by` frontmatter field.

## Applications

In *Staying with the Trouble*, Haraway applies companion species across several
illustrations that mobilize the concept rather than test it empirically: racing-pigeon
fancying and citizen-science projects in Southern California (PigeonBlog, Cornell
PigeonWatch); Navajo-Churro sheep and Diné (Navajo) weaving practices at Black Mesa,
framed through the concept of hózhó (harmony/right relations); and ant-acacia and
orchid-bee mutualisms treated as instances of "symbiosis, the yoking together of
companion species, at table together." Haraway is explicit that these are illustrations
of her theoretical program, not subject-matter findings — for instance she states that
applying the term "art science activism" to Navajo weaving would be "a category mistake,"
since she treats Diné weaving as "cosmological performance" in its own right.

## Critiques and Limitations

The claims file record does not surface direct scholarly critique of the companion-species
concept specifically within this source; Haraway's own text flags the risk of
appropriative use of indigenous material (e.g., her use of the Navajo string game
na'atl'o' and Diné sheep-herding practice) as "not an innocent universal gesture," a
caution she applies reflexively to her own deployment of companion species across
cross-cultural illustrations. As with Haraway's other coinages, the term's figurative,
polyvalent character is itself sometimes read (attributed to critics generally, not
detailed in this source) as resisting the analytical precision expected of a social-
science concept.
