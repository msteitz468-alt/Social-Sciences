# Coastal Adaptation

## Definition as Coined

**Coastal adaptation** names forager (and early farmer) strategies that center marine and littoral productivity — shellfish gathering, fishing, sea-mammal hunting, coastal plant use — with settlement and technology organized around the shore. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] the concept is inseparable from **post-glacial sea-level rise**: many LGM and early Holocene coastal sites now lie underwater, biasing the archaeological record inland.

## Semantic History

Shell-midden archaeology (Danish *køkkenmøddinger* from the 1850s; Victorian Lubbock’s visits) made coastal Mesolithic economies visible early. Later work generalized maritime foragers as a pathway to [[complex-hunter-gatherers]] (Northwest Coast, Jōmon, Ertebølle, Mediterranean Cardial coasts). Peopling-of-the-Americas research elevated a **Pacific coastal route** as an alternative to the ice-free corridor ([[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]). Mithen folds these strands into a global narrative of “coastal catastrophe” and opportunity after the ice.

## Operationalizations

- **Middens:** shell mounds as occupation and dietary archives (Ertebølle type-site; Tagus/Sado estuaries; Amazon riverbank middens; Jōmon horseshoe mounds).
- **Bone chemistry and fauna:** marine vs terrestrial isotopic signals (Danish late Mesolithic marine emphasis — as of Mithen 2003).
- **Boats and island access:** dugouts (Tybrind Vig); Melos obsidian voyages from Franchthi; first settlement of Corsica, Sardinia, Balearics.
- **Sea-level geometry:** shore migrating toward sites (Franchthi; Elands Bay as “Franchthi of the South”); Doggerland drowning; estimates of rapid rise (~23–40 mm/year in the century after 7500 BC — as of Mithen 2003).
- **Negative evidence:** LGM coastal sites largely lost — coastal presence inferred from later patterns and submerged-land models.

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **Europe:** Franchthi coastalization and island colonization; Ertebølle marine diets, pottery, and middens; Téviec/Hoëdic Atlantic cemeteries; Doggerland loss; Storegga tsunami as coastal hazard narrative.
- **Americas:** coastal-route peopling hypothesis; Monte Verde near Pacific resources; later specialized riverine/coastal economies.
- **East Asia:** Jōmon woodland–coast–ocean resource mixes (e.g. Uenohara; Natsushima dating); early pottery not limited to coastal staple models.
- **Africa:** Elands Bay diet/settlement shifts as shore approached; North African LGM coastal hunting (Tamar Hat).
- **Sundaland/SE Asia:** drowned shelves as lost coastal landscapes for post-glacial reconfiguration.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Preservation bias** systematically under-represents the richest coastal phases — especially LGM and early Holocene shelves.
- Marine specialization can be seasonal or partial; “coastal” labels may overstate diet share without isotopes/zooarchaeology.
- Salinity and productivity change (Rowley-Conwy on Danish midden abandonment — via Mithen) show coastal economies are environmentally contingent, not permanent types.
- Do not equate coastal archaeological cultures with maritime ethnolinguistic identities.

## Meltzer 2009 — Pacific coastal route to the Americas

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009; radiocarbon/geological BP as he states.)*

### Timing and hazards

- Coastal route **open before ~23,000 years ago** but **impassable during the LGM** as northern Gulf of Alaska glaciers blocked southward travel; LGM coast faced icebergs, sea ice, glacier fronts hundreds of km across, crevassed and calving; irregular ice-free coastal segments may have been plant/animal refugia but ice-walled, cold, and unreliable landings.
- Long outer-coast reaches ice-free **16,000–14,500 BP**; by **13,400 years ago** glaciers retreated and coast was clear Alaska to Washington with plants/animals soon established.
- Cordilleran ice on the Pacific shelf often reached Pleistocene sea, potentially blocking coastal end-runs during maximum ice.
- Sea-level return ~110 m between 16,000–6,000 BP with major pulses at **16,000 and 12,500 BP**—drowned many early coastal sites. Countervailing **isostatic rebound** on newly deglaciated Alaska/BC coasts lifted some Pleistocene shoreline segments **above** modern sea level (hard to find under temperate rainforest, not impossible).

### Boat vs foot; evidence quality

- Late Pleistocene watercraft for N Pacific entry not directly documented; Sahul colonization >40 ka shows open-water capacity but N Pacific glacial conditions (dark winters, ice, hypothermia) differ.
- First N Pacific watercraft/island/marine-mammal evidence mostly postdates **10,000 BP**—too late for initial ice-skirt; Channel Islands (Arlington Springs, Daisy Cave) ~**10,960 BP** show watercraft but **too far south** to settle the Alaska-to-unglaciated path question.
- Meltzer: once outer coast ice-free, **walking viable**; drowned Pleistocene coastline less dissected than modern; **Occam’s razor favors walking** if coastal. Fedje et al. land/sea survey with bathymetry/coring—**none older than 10,300 BP** found yet (as of 2009); search early.

### Coupling to pre-Clovis / Monte Verde

- If people reached [[monte-verde]] by ~12,500 ¹⁴C BP and the [[ice-free-corridor]] was blocked/uninhabitable until ~12,000 BP, **coastal (or pre-LGM interior) entry** is required (Meltzer thesis).
- Monte Verde itself shows coastal resource use (seaweed on tools; ~30 km to coast; 2008 algae affirmation).
- Todd Surovell modeled that coasting all the way to Monte Verde should “backfill” interior North America with substantial pre-Clovis populations—absence of which (in his view) makes coastal entry implausible; Meltzer notes there is likewise no evidence of such a population via interior.
- Favored scenarios in Meltzer’s Figure 38 synthesis: (1) earlier entry down recently deglaciated coast in time for Monte Verde and/or (2) later entry down newly opened corridor at Clovis outset—not exclusive.

### Late Paleoindian coasts

Pacific Northwest and California coast groups on shellfish among regional Late Paleoindian packages; flightless sea duck *Chedytes lawi* (CA/OR coasts) hunted even in Paleoindian times (Daisy Cave) and went extinct only after ~7,000 years of predation—not near-instantaneous overkill (Meltzer vs Fiedel–Haynes preservation arguments).

Four-way non-identity: coastal route ≠ a people ≠ a language ≠ a genetic migration pulse. Source: [[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]] · [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]].
