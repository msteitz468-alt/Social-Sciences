# Coastal Adaptation

## Definition as Coined

**Coastal adaptation** names forager (and early farmer) strategies that center marine and littoral productivity — shellfish gathering, fishing, sea-mammal hunting, coastal plant use — with settlement and technology organized around the shore. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] the concept is inseparable from **post-glacial sea-level rise**: many LGM and early Holocene coastal sites now lie underwater, biasing the archaeological record inland. In Andean archaeology, [[moseley-michael|Moseley]] (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) embeds coastal adaptation in a conjectural **Pacific Rim colonization** scenario and in the long-run **Maritime-Oasis** developmental trajectory formalized as the [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]].

## Semantic History

Shell-midden archaeology (Danish *køkkenmøddinger* from the 1850s; Victorian Lubbock’s visits) made coastal Mesolithic economies visible early. Later work generalized maritime foragers as a pathway to [[complex-hunter-gatherers]] (Northwest Coast, Jōmon, Ertebølle, Mediterranean Cardial coasts). Peopling-of-the-Americas research elevated a **Pacific coastal route** as an alternative to the ice-free corridor ([[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]). Mithen folds these strands into a global narrative of “coastal catastrophe” and opportunity after the ice. Andeanists (Moseley and 1960s multi-national programs) made coastal adaptation a **civilization-origins** variable: not only Mesolithic complexity but Preceramic monumentality on a hyper-arid desert littoral.

## Operationalizations

- **Middens:** shell mounds as occupation and dietary archives (Ertebølle type-site; Tagus/Sado estuaries; Amazon riverbank middens; Jōmon horseshoe mounds; Andean Paloma, Ring Site, Huaca Prieta).
- **Bone chemistry and fauna:** marine vs terrestrial isotopic signals (Danish late Mesolithic; Chinchorro and Asia/Azapa >90% seafood as of Moseley 2001; Paloma low strontium / high protein; "surfer’s ear" as cold-water diving marker).
- **Boats and tackle:** dugouts (Tybrind Vig); Andean reed boats, float nets, cactus-thorn/shell/bone hooks, composite hooks, wooden harpoons; cotton nets as industrial-cultigen package.
- **Sea-level geometry:** drowned Pleistocene/early Holocene shores (Andean Paijan shoreline sites submerged; Meltzer on Pacific shelf); isostatic rebound exceptions.
- **Settlement packing:** littoral potable-water nodes; split residence once irrigation pulls farmers inland (Initial Period, Moseley).

## Applications

### Global (as of Mithen 2003)

- **Europe:** Franchthi coastalization; Ertebølle marine diets; Téviec/Hoëdic; Doggerland; Storegga tsunami narrative.
- **Americas:** coastal-route peopling; Monte Verde near Pacific resources.
- **East Asia / Africa / Sundaland:** as on prior page version (Jōmon mixes; Elands Bay; drowned shelves).

### Andean peopling and Lithic dispersal (as of Moseley 2001)

- Direct high-Andes migration unlikely (anoxia, glaciation, cold); more likely **continental margins** — Pacific Rim step-by-step colonization; coastal people venture to sierra for obsidian/game; permanent high Cordillera settlement relatively late (stair-step acclimatization). Routes partly submerged by post-glacial sea rise.
- Coastal Peru–Chile inhabited by ≥**11,500 calendar years ago** (widely accepted hemispheric baseline as of Moseley 2001); Monte Verde ~12,500 ¹⁴C ya (~14,850 cal ya) as best ancient Andean case then accepted.
- By ~10 ka, distinct lifeways: Northwestern Tradition, coastal **Paijan** (needle-nosed points for fish; ancestral to later monuments but early evolution submerged), Central Andean Lithic Tradition, **Atacama Maritime Tradition** (Jaguay, Ring Site, Tacahuay, Asia — early surviving maritime adaptations in the Americas).
- **Chinchorro** artificial mummification among Chilean fisherfolk; Moseley links coastal mortuary insistence on bodily integrity and early kin/mortuary beliefs to later ayllu/Inca mummy politics — attributed long-term cultural argument, not simple continuity of ethnicity.

### Andean Preceramic–Initial Maritime-Oasis (as of Moseley 2001)

- Fishing as oldest enduring arid-Andes livelihood beyond Paijan; industrial cultigens (cotton, gourd, bulrush) support nets and craft; comestible floodplain plants secondary.
- Preceramic monuments densest where fishery richest (~600 km stretch); Initial Period irrigation adds agrarian oases → **split residence** (fisherfolk littoral, farmers inland) while Maritime-Oasis poles densify on largest desert rivers (north) vs Arid Montane poles on largest highland basins (south).
- See [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]], [[caral]], [[el-paraiso]], [[aspero]].

### Meltzer 2009 — Pacific coastal route to the Americas

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009.)* Coastal route open before ~23 ka but impassable during LGM at northern Gulf of Alaska; outer coast ice-free 16–14.5 ka; by 13.4 ka clear Alaska to Washington. Sea-level return drowned many early sites; Occam favors walking once outer coast ice-free. If people reached Monte Verde by ~12.5 ka ¹⁴C BP while ice-free corridor blocked until ~12 ka, coastal or pre-LGM interior entry is required (Meltzer thesis). Four-way non-identity: coastal route ≠ a people ≠ a language ≠ a genetic migration pulse.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Preservation bias** systematically under-represents richest coastal phases — especially LGM/early Holocene shelves and Andean Paijan shorelines.
- Marine specialization can be seasonal or partial; isotopes required to bound diet share.
- MFAC over-reading: industrial/complementary cultivation and Supe inland centers (Caral) show coastal adaptation is not pure "fish only."
- Do not equate coastal archaeological cultures with maritime ethnolinguistic identities.
- Modern fishery productivity analogies (Moseley anchovy tonnages) illustrate ecological capacity, not measured Preceramic harvests.
