# Social regulation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* Book II ch. 5, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] introduces society as not only attracting individuals with unequal force ([[social-integration]]) but as a power **controlling** them. **Regulation** is the moral limitation of desires and the assignment of legitimate ends and rewards. Passions need a force exterior to the individual — moral, not physical; society alone can play this moderating role.

- Low / broken regulation → [[anomie]] → [[anomic-suicide]]
- Excessive regulation → [[fatalistic-suicide]] (footnote residual)

Normal regulation: moral consciousness grades social functions and average comfort levels; rules must be obeyed through respect, not fear alone; progressive societies need rules pliable enough that a certain amount of anomy is inseparable from a morality of progress — but chronic industrial anomy is pathological excess.

## Semantic History

Less often named as a freestanding concept than anomie; the dual-axis reading is largely a later systematization. Distinct from legal regulation alone — Durkheim’s emphasis is **moral** authority accepted as just.

## Operationalizations

- Economic crises and boom shocks as de-regulation moments
- Loss of religious, state-supremacy, and corporative limits on industry
- Divorce as weakened matrimonial regulation
- Occupational rates (trade/industry vs agriculture)

## Applications

- [[anomie]] · [[anomic-suicide]] · [[fatalistic-suicide]]
- Practical: [[occupational-group]] as organ that can limit greed while seeing enough of life to estimate needs; state alone too distant and levelling

## Critiques and Limitations

- Hard to measure “regulation” independently of outcomes (suicide) — circularity risk.
- Conjugal regulation analysis carries period gender assumptions.
- Boundary with power/domination theories (later conflict sociology) not drawn in *Suicide*.
