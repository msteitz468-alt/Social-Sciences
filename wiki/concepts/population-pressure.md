# Population Pressure

## Definition as Coined

The stress produced when a population approaches or exceeds the [[carrying-capacity]] of its land under existing subsistence technology. Malthus (1798) treated it as a check that misery and mortality resolve; Ester Boserup (*The Conditions of Agricultural Growth*, 1965) inverted the causality, arguing that population pressure is the primary **driver** of [[agricultural-intensification]] rather than a consequence of production limits.

## Semantic History

The concept migrated from political economy into anthropology and archaeology in the 1960s–70s, when Boserup's inversion supplied processual archaeology with an engine of change: Robert Carneiro's [[circumscription]] theory (1970) and the Basin of Mexico program made population pressure the prime mover of [[state-formation]]. [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979)]] argued from three millennia of survey data that population growth in a circumscribed, environmentally diverse basin drove intensification, competition, and political evolution — while explicitly qualifying that growth rates were slow and pressure operated locally and episodically, not as a constant. From the 1980s the prime-mover version lost ground: critics noted populations that stagnated below capacity, intensification without pressure, and the circularity of inferring pressure from the intensification it is meant to explain.

## Operationalizations

Archaeologically: survey-derived population estimates (sherd density → persons/ha) compared against reconstructed yields and cultivable land — the Basin of Mexico project is the type case ([[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]). The operationalization inherits every uncertainty in both the demographic and the productivity reconstructions, and the traditions disagree on how close to capacity "pressure" begins.

## Applications

Boserupian intensification studies; [[circumscription]] and warfare models; the Basin of Mexico synthesis; demographic readings of the Neolithic transition.

## Critiques and Limitations

Circularity (pressure inferred from its supposed effects); ethnographic cases of sub-capacity stagnation; critics of the Basin program (see [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] and the study page) argued its population estimates were too uncertain to bear the causal weight placed on them. Current usage treats population pressure as one variable in multivariate models rather than a prime mover.

### Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — Oaxaca as a below-capacity, consequence-not-cause case

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* Against prime-mover population-pressure accounts of state origins, the authors report that Valley of Oaxaca regional population was well below carrying capacity when [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] was founded (~500 BC) and that canal irrigation followed rather than preceded the founding (Wright 1986). They instead treat the subsequent rapid population growth (an estimated ~6% per year in Monte Albán's Early I, calculated from survey data by Blanton, Kowalewski, Feinman, and Appel 1982:41) as a **consequence** of the new capital's founding — via immigration and pronatalist incentives — rather than its cause, terming this a "coactive," circular/mutual-causal model. See [[circumscription]] for the parallel critique of Carneiro's model, and [[state-formation]].
