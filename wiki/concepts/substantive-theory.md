# Substantive Theory

## Definition as Coined

In [[glaser-barney-g|Glaser]] and [[strauss-anselm-l|Strauss]] (*[[glaser-strauss-discovery-grounded-theory-1967|The Discovery of Grounded Theory]]*, 1967), **substantive theory** is theory generated from research on **one particular substantive area** (work, juvenile delinquency, medical education, mental health, dying care, etc.). It is grounded in that area's data yet may carry general implications and serve as a springboard to [[formal-theory-grounded|formal theory]].

## Semantic History

1967 coinage within grounded theory as a **scope level**, paired with formal theory. Later qualitative methods often use "substantive" more loosely for any mid-range empirical theory; keep the Glaser–Strauss contrast in view when linking.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — a level of theoretical scope (area-specific vs conceptual-domain formal theory). Developed via [[theoretical-sampling]] and the [[constant-comparative-method]] until [[theoretical-saturation]].

## Applications

Dying-research theories (awareness contexts, social loss, status passage of dying) as illustrations in *Discovery*. Ch. X application criteria (fit, understandability, generality, control) are stated for substantive (and formal) grounded theory used by practitioners.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of over-generalizing a substantive theory as if it were formal; authors argue formalization usually should pass through multi-area comparative grounding, not one-area rewriting alone (Ch. IV).
