# Habitualization

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (*[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|The Social Construction of Reality]]*, 1966, Part Two):

> Any action that is repeated frequently becomes cast into a pattern, which can then be reproduced with an economy of effort and which, ipso facto, is apprehended by its performer as that pattern.

Habitualized actions become embedded as routines in the general [[stock-of-knowledge|stock of knowledge]], taken for granted and at hand for future projects. Habitualization applies even to non-social activity (their solitary matchstick-canoe builder); empirically the important part is coextensive with [[institutionalization]].

## Semantic History

Pre-1966 sociology spoke of custom, habit, and routine ([[folkways]], mores); Berger and Luckmann’s technical move is to place habitualization as the **genetic precondition** of institutionalization via reciprocal [[typification]]. Related but not identical: Weberian traditional action; pragmatist habit (outside current collection).

## Operationalizations

Theoretical. Psychological gains they attribute to habitualization: choices narrowed (many theoretical ways become one); relief from continuous decision under undirected instinctual structure; background of routine frees a foreground for deliberation and innovation; situations need not be redefined step by step.

## Applications

- Origins of institutions (A–B paradigm of reciprocal typification)
- Everyday [[reality-of-everyday-life|paramount reality]] routines
- Recipe knowledge in the social stock of knowledge

## Critiques and Limitations

Programmatic, not measured. Risk of over-generalizing “habit” without power analysis; B&L address control as inherent in institutions once formed, but the habitualization step itself is relatively power-thin.
