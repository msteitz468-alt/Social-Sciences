# Secondary State

## Definition as Coined

In [[fried-morton-h|Fried]]'s *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* (1967), a **secondary state** is one formed **because pre-existing states supplied the stimuli or the models** for organization. Processes of secondary formation **differ** from pristine paths; not all secondary states need traverse a long stratified-nonstate stage. Fried classed Crete, Greece, Troy, Persia, Rome, and the states of western and northern Europe as secondary, and held that **all contemporary states** are secondary in this sense. Modern **colonialism** appears as analogue for secondary state processes.

## Semantic History

Absorbed into archaeological **secondary state formation** (conquest, peer-polity interaction, colonial encapsulation, defensive state formation on frontiers). Pairs with [[pristine-state|pristine/primary]] in textbooks.

## Operationalizations

Fried applies Sahlins–Service **general vs specific evolution**: the leap to the state is general-evolutionary; subsequent development of particular states (even pristine ones) is specific/local-adaptive and divergent. Secondary formation multiplies specific pathways (stimulus diffusion of institutions requires receptive social climate — harder than diffusion of techniques).

## Applications

Imperial peripheries; frontier polities; colonial and postcolonial state construction; peer-polity clusters.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundary cases (China/Indus disputed as pristine vs secondary).
- “Stimulus” under-specified (trade, threat, missionary models, conquest).
- Risk of labeling all non-first cases “secondary” without process models.

### Service (*Origins of the State and Civilization*, 1975, attributed)

Service accepted Fried's secondary-state category but treated it, in effect, as the "non-precocious" complement of his preferred "precocious" (vs. absolute "pristine") framing — see [[pristine-state]]. His clearest secondary/contact-shaped cases are the ethnohistorically documented "modern primitive states" reached via his [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]] under colonial-era pressure: the Zulu, Ankole, Nupe, and slave-trading West African polities (Kongo, Ashanti, Dahomey), plus Polynesian cases (Hawaii, Tahiti, Tonga) and the Cherokee. He flagged Ankole and the West African slave-trading states as the *only* plausible cases of conquest-created states arising from prestate elements, but cautioned all arose under such unusual European-penetration circumstances that they cannot cleanly test conquest theory; where conquest did produce lasting subjugation (Ankole, Zulu, Nupe), he found a governmental apparatus already existed beforehand, so war did not itself create the state — a position bearing directly on the [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]].

### Connah 2016 — external trade as intensifier, not originator (attributed)

[[connah-graham|Connah]] (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* 2016) rejects treating precolonial African cities and states as merely **secondary** products of Mediterranean, Islamic, or Indian Ocean stimuli. Across regional cases he argues **external long-distance trade intensified** complexity that had already begun locally (e.g. Middle Niger urbanization pre-dating large-scale trans-Saharan trade; East African coastal indigenous complexity; Zambezia and forest West Africa without early large-scale long-distance trade as prime mover). With Lonsdale’s slow local politics and Renfrew’s local-conditions program, he treats the pristine/secondary cut as analytically empty for African trajectories (see [[pristine-state]], [[productive-land-hypothesis]]).
