# Linguistic Nationalism

**Linguistic nationalism** is the cluster of projects and ideologies that bind political nationhood to language — typically one people, one language, one standard — and that recruit grammar, folklore, and philology to boundary-making.

## Definition as Coined

Not coined by a single anthropologist; treated analytically in the [[language-ideology]] program (Silverstein on nationality’s linguistic imagination; Bauman & Briggs on Herder; Irvine & Gal on European/Macedonian differentiation; Errington on Indonesian) within [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000]] and densified from [[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000]].

Irvine and Gal (2000) characterize the Western European reigning form (often linked to Herder) as imagining **inherent, natural links between a unitary mother tongue, a territory, and an ethnonational identity** — persuasive through [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]], and [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]]. Fichte (1808) is quoted as: wherever a separate language is found, there is a separate nation with a right to self-rule; Meillet is cited as treating language as the principal factor of national sentiment in Europe.

## Semantic History

Herderian and Romantic philological traditions; nineteenth-century nation-state building; postcolonial national-language planning (e.g., Bahasa Indonesia). Language-ideology work re-specifies these as **semiotic and institutional regimes**, not natural awakenings of peoples. Irvine & Gal stress that metropolitan Europe had by no means achieved the monoglot ideal it projected onto Macedonia and colonial Africa.

## Operationalizations

Standardization histories; school and print capitalism; census language categories (mother tongue vs commercial language); purism and **register stripping** (Turkish/Greek lexicon purged as “alien”); ethnographic and linguistic maps for Great Power audiences; courtroom and parliamentary language rules.

## Applications

### Macedonia (Irvine & Gal 2000 — attributed)

Geographic Macedonia after Ottoman retreat became a site of competing Serbian, Bulgarian, Greek, and Macedonian autonomy claims, each using linguistic descriptions to authorize territorial loyalty. Irvine & Gal emphasize:

- **South Slavic dialect continuum** (scholarly consensus they report): Macedonian dialects share lexicon/phonology with Serbian but morphology more with Bulgarian — contested selectively via iconization.
- **Maps** that erase multilingualism and monochrome “one language per territory.”
- **Codification of literary Macedonian (1944):** preference for Western dialects partly for historical precedent, partly for maximal differentiation from Bulgarian and Serbo-Croatian.
- **Register stripping:** Bulgarian reformers from the 1840s purge Turkisms; Macedonian planners fear retained Turkisms would make the literary language sound “lower.”
- **Practical erasure:** interwar and post-war Greek Macedonia — denial of Slavic speech, name changes, jailing, 1950s language oaths (Karakasidou 1993).

Western observers first saw Macedonia as chaotic *because* local millet-based multilingual identity failed monoglot expectations — then nationalist projects remade practice toward the model.

## Critiques and Limitations

Not all nationalism is monoglot; some states regiment multilingualism. Avoid equating language family, ethnicity, and nation (four-way non-identity applies). Irvine & Gal treat nationalist philology as ideological data — not as neutral adjudication of “true” linguistic affiliation.
