# Absolute Rent

## Definition as Coined

In the exposition [[lenin-vladimir]] gives from [[marx-karl]] (*Capital* III; *Theories of Surplus Value*) in *The Agrarian Programme of Social-Democracy in the First Russian Revolution, 1905–1907* (Ch. III), **absolute rent** is rent arising from **private ownership of land** as a monopoly element — distinct from **differential rent**.

| Category | Source (Marx via Lenin) | Fate under capitalism / nationalisation |
|---|---|---|
| **Differential rent** | Limited land; fertility, location, productivity differences; arises from capitalist production **regardless** of private ownership | Under private ownership → landowner; under [[land-nationalisation]] → **state**; **cannot** be abolished under capitalism |
| **Absolute rent** | Private landownership monopoly; hinders free competition and levelling of profit between agriculture and industry; historically linked to low technical composition of agricultural capital and agricultural backwardness fixed by monopoly | **Not** inevitable under all capitalist agriculture — only with private ownership; nationalisation makes **abolition** possible and can reduce agricultural prices by that amount |

Kautsky (quoted by Lenin): differential rent from competition, absolute from monopoly; absolute rent is a deduction from surplus value borne jointly by workers and capitalists. Hence nationalisation is both (a) a **partial reform within capitalism** (change owner of differential rent) and (b) **abolition of a monopoly** hindering capitalism’s full development (absolute rent).

**Nationalisation of land under capitalist relations** = transfer of rent to the state. Pure case: state receives rent from agricultural entrepreneur paying wages and receiving average profit.

## Semantic History

- **Marx:** absolute rent as category of the critique of political economy; Lenin insists it is not a “rough note” contradicting *Capital* III’s distribution theory (contra Maslov).
- **P. Maslov (as Lenin frames):** repudiates absolute rent; treats the contradiction with Vol. III as so glaring it must come from unfinished notes; links rent theory to the “law of diminishing returns”; German translation of Maslov’s book (Lenin notes) omitted the absolute-rent repudiation.
- **Lenin’s 1901 *Zarya* critique / 1907 *Agrarian Programme*:** absolute rent arises from **private ownership**, not merely low composition of agricultural capital. Brickmaking analogy: low composition but no rent without land monopoly. Tenant can appropriate additional rent during lease (private ownership temporarily suspended for the tenant). Differential rent from successive investments on the same plot (Rent I vs II) is distinct.
- **Programme hinge (1907–08):** repudiating absolute rent = repudiating the economic significance of private landownership under capitalism → implies it “makes no difference” whether land is state or privately owned (only who gets differential rent) → theoretical path to [[municipalisation]] dualism and to denying nationalisation as accelerator of capitalism.
- **Agrarian “Critics of Marx” (David, Bulgakov):** related but not identical — Lenin attacks their use of diminishing returns and falsified Marx quotations on successive capital investments; absolute-rent doctrine is fully loaded in the *Agrarian Programme*, not in the David chapters alone. Ground rent is named as social barrier to agricultural development (with feudal survivals and labour rights).

## Operationalizations

| Measure / claim | Role of absolute rent |
|---|---|
| **Nationalisation programme** | Removes absolute rent; differential rent to state; freer investment of capital in agriculture and freer flow between branches |
| **Municipalisation programme** | If absolute rent is denied, dual private/public ownership looks coherent; question reduces to “where rent goes” (local needs vs central army) |
| **Ozol’s Second Duma speech** | (Lenin’s “Maslovism” critique) confuses abolition of feudal with bourgeois exploitation; ignores urban “rent” largely as ground rent; forgets or denies absolute rent; rejects more developed capitalist form an SR advocated |
| **Small farming caveat (Marx via Lenin)** | Absolute rent usually absent under self-consumption small farming; does not destroy rent theory as commodity production grows |
| **Finn’s “money rent”** | Pre-capitalist money rent (surplus product as money after labour rent and rent in kind) ≠ capitalist absolute rent; dies with latifundia |

## Applications

- **Why Marxism permits nationalisation in a bourgeois revolution (Lenin):** nationalisation is a **bourgeois measure** because absolute rent hinders development of capitalism. Municipalising big estates as programme content remodelled Marxism (Lenin) into Fabian opportunism.
- **Conclusion of *Agrarian Programme*:** first of three temporary reasons Russian SDs were deflected from the correct nationalisation programme = Maslov’s revision (repudiation of absolute rent + diminishing-returns doctrines).
- **Capital and land price (Marx/*Capital* III via Lenin):** capital spent on land price is withdrawn from cultivation; mortgage/usury as capital’s evasion of ownership barriers; alternative = free investment via nationalisation.
- **Overseas competition:** no-rent new lands undercut rent-inflated European prices — not an organic “law of diminishing returns.”

## Critiques and Limitations

**Maslov’s critique of Marx (as Lenin records it):** absolute rent contradicts Vol. III distribution theory (must be unfinished “rough notes”); without diminishing returns, Narodnik idyll might work; cultivation vs technical-progress split used to evade the diminishing-returns refutation.

**Lenin’s rebuttal (attributed):** only ignorance of Marx’s rent theory and *Theories of Surplus Value* (1905) allows the repudiation; absolute rent is from private monopoly; diminishing returns is relative to a fixed technical stage, not a cardinal feature of agriculture; Marx allows both diminished **and** increased productivity of additional investments.

**Limitations of this page:** exposition is **Lenin’s 1907 reading** of Marx/Kautsky; Maslov, Ozol, Finn, David appear as polemical targets in [[lenin-collected-works-vol-13-1962]]; absolute rent functions here as programme-theoretic hinge more than a full technical rent treatise.
