# Causewayed Enclosure

## Definition as Coined

A **causewayed enclosure** (older: "causewayed camp") is a Neolithic earthwork whose ditch circuit is broken by frequent undug **causeways**, producing a characteristic interrupted plan. Classic British examples (e.g. Windmill Hill tradition) and Continental counterparts (Michelsberg and related) form a major Early–Middle Neolithic monument class.

## Semantic History

Early interpretations stressed defense or cattle corrals; later work emphasizes ceremonial gathering, structured deposition (pottery, axes, human and animal bone), and episodic recutting. [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] (1998, Ch. 5) argues early enclosures were often associated with houses, then took on specialized ritual lives with stereotyped plans and deposits — "small worlds" set apart from ordinary settlement.

## Operationalizations

Interrupted ditch segments; bank/ditch profiles; deposition patterns; radiocarbon and pottery phasing; relationship to long mounds and later [[henge|henges]].

## Applications

Neolithic social aggregation; violence vs ritual debate at individual sites; prototype genealogy from house-enclosure practices ([[death-of-the-house]]).

## Bradley 1998 — sequence and deposits (attributed)

**Form:** Later Neolithic segmented enclosures show restricted space, one or more often-curving ditches, internal banks/palisades; unifying element is **segmentation** (ditches interrupted by causeways). Distribution vast (Sweden to Languedoc; Ulster to the Danube — as of Bradley’s synthesis).

**Sequence thesis:**
1. Early integration with settlement (sometimes after houses abandoned; sometimes protective boundary) — e.g. late Bandkeramik **Darion** (Belgium): segmented ditch + houses; defence reading plausible but multi-causeway hard to defend as pure fort; residential + segmented plan as link to later NW Europe.
2. Construction toward margins of the Neolithic landscape; **permeable** perimeters (Urmitz interrupted palisade before earthworks).
3. Specialised deposits and ceremonial centres as houses become ephemeral: fine pottery/axes, meat joints, human remains; feasting, animal sacrifice, rites of passage of the dead; episodic levelling and renewal; sections built by different surrounding groups (Edmonds 1993 via Bradley).
4. Later formal enclosures as **“small worlds”** / mythical settlements referencing past communities — parallel to long mounds referencing longhouses.
5. Final reversals: some British sites attacked/destroyed or rebuilt as defended settlements; latest anomalous circular forms (Flagstones, Stonehenge 1, Briar Hill inner ring) prefigure Late Neolithic circular monuments.

**Cases named:** Menneville (Rössen structured deposits); Mairy (Michelsberg); Boury (roles change over time — rampart levelled, then animal burials, then human bone); Noyen-sur-Seine (interrupted palisade → causewayed ditch; perforated human skull, etc.); Michelsberg type site (never residential in major phases).

**Link to prototypes:** Belgian late LBK interrupted ditches/palisades with houses inside (Keeley & Cahen) among earliest causewayed-style circuits; path from enclosing dead-house zones ([[death-of-the-house]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Functional diversity across sites is real — some defensive evidence exists; do not reduce all enclosures to one rite. Chronologies revised since 1998 (Bayesian modeling) may shift phase narratives.

## Europe in the Neolithic densification (Whittle 1996) — Sarup, Windmill Hill, social arenas

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 6–7. Parallel to Bradley 1998 densification above.)*

**Frame.** Invoking Sahlins on tribesmen’s concern with everyday spirits vs taken-for-granted supreme beings, Whittle suggests middle-Neolithic energy went into houses for mythical ancestors; later, as imagined ancestors were taken for granted, monuments may shift toward communal ossuaries while **ditched enclosures** enshrine more frequent, wider-ranging gatherings — another image of community in circular spaces of interrupted ditches: “If people honoured various kinds of ancestors at the houses of the dead, they constructed another kind of image of themselves at the enclosures” (attributed). Primary role of most enclosures: formally to bound special space, separate inside from outside, and invest associated activities with significance — **not primarily defence or permanent settlement**, though continuous barriers and attack/burning/killing evidence exist at some sites (Hambledon Hill, Crickley Hill) (attributed).

**Distribution and chronology (as of mid-1990s).** Middle Neolithic ditched enclosures from central-west France to southern Scandinavia and southern England to central Germany/Poland — discontinuous distributions; few in earlier Lengyel centre; SE TRB enclosures more often uninterrupted; Michelsberg/TRB common but none so far in Dutch TRB west group; Denmark ~30 known (none twenty years earlier); southern England 50+ known/suspected vs thousands of dolmens/passage graves. Layouts ~1 to several ha (Windmill Hill outer circuit >350 m; Halle–Dölauer Heide max ~500 m); 1–3+ circuits; interrupted ditches common in southern England (attributed). Possible deep source: later LBK and earlier Lengyel enclosure tradition of shared space within longhouse occupations, kept alive as cultural memory of a distant ancestral past (attributed; cf. Bradley on Darion/prototype genealogy without collapsing the arguments).

**Sarup (SW Fyn).** Began ENII Fuchsberg (later fourth millennium BC); two phases (second MNAIb Klintebakke) separated by three–four generations, each brief (tens of years); double circuit of shallow segmented ditches cutting a low promontory; phase 1 complex plank palisade/compartments; ditches received animal bone, flints, sherds/whole pots, human bone including skulls/jaws, charcoal; some segments recut/refilled three times; interior pits with whole pots and stone axe deliberately backfilled; phase 2 smaller segments redefining only the innermost part (after N. Andersen via Whittle 1996). Fuchsberg-phase ditched/palisaded enclosures ~30 or fewer known in Denmark by mid-1990s, plus Schleswig-Holstein/southern Sweden (attributed).

**Windmill Hill.** Dates ~3500–3400 BC, centuries after first Neolithic in the upper Kennet; three interrupted-ditch circuits in woodland/scrub not extensively open (contrast open long-barrow settings); little contemporary occupation immediately outside. Primary ditch fills held deliberately placed animal-bone groups (multi-animal multi-species partial remains), plant remains, flint, quern fragments, broken pots, human remains — not random discard; deposition varied segment-to-segment; outer circuit more human remains/articulated animal bone vs inner circuits more artefacts/disarticulated bone — map-like character of use (Whittle & Pollard reconstruction via Whittle 1996). Cattle (with other domesticates) prominent among ditch deposits; small quantities of game (attributed). Southern English causewayed enclosures generally carry more open bowls suited to presentation/consumption than settlement assemblages (attributed).

**Participatory ceremonialism (position).** Against readings of enclosures as establishing power via control of labour, production, and ritual, Whittle stresses remote settings and individuality of each deposition: intensive participatory ceremonialism reworking integration of separate communities and bringing past into present; “The enclosures are not symptoms of the differentiation of Neolithic society, but rather one of the means by which its world was consolidated”; animal-bone treatment could metaphorically parallel human-bone processing; inversion of celebrating domesticity in special out-of-the-way arenas while honouring ancestors closer to cleared land of the living (attributed). Late LBK enclosures (e.g. Darion): excavators’ defensive/frontier-vs-foragers reading fits wider evidence poorly in his view; shallow ditches, late not early timing, and indigenous-acculturation hypothesis undercut pure fort models (attributed, Ch.6).
