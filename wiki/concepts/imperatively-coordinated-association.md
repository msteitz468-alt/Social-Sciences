# Imperatively Coordinated Association

## Definition as Coined

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] takes Max Weber's category **imperatively coordinated association** (*Herrschaftsverband*) as the structural unit within which classes form: an association organized by a distribution of **authority** such that incumbents of some roles can issue binding commands and incumbents of others are expected to obey. Examples include the industrial enterprise, the political state, churches, and other organizations with a command hierarchy. Classes, on Dahrendorf's account, are conflict groups generated by that dichotomous authority structure, not only by property ownership (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, Ch. V).

## Semantic History

Weber's *Herrschaft* and association vocabulary (see [[types-of-legitimate-rule]], [[domination-herrschaft]], [[bureaucracy]]) supplied the term; Dahrendorf's innovation is to make the association the *locus of class formation*, generalizing class analysis beyond capitalist property relations to any authority-structured organization. This migrates "class" from a property-only Marxian sense toward a general conflict-group sense.

## Operationalizations

Identify associations with authority hierarchies; map the (analytically dichotomous) set of positions with and without authority; treat those aggregates as recruiting fields for conflict groups ([[quasi-group|quasi-groups]] → interest groups).

## Applications

Industrial enterprise (Ch. VII) and political state (Ch. VIII) are Dahrendorf's principal applications; the concept underwrites the claim that post-capitalist societies remain class societies wherever authority is unequally distributed.

## Critiques and Limitations

The forced dichotomy of authority (possession vs non-possession) is a deliberate analytical simplification; multi-layered hierarchies, professional autonomy, and cross-cutting memberships complicate pure two-camp maps — Dahrendorf discusses intermediate and bureaucratic roles as complications, not refutations.
