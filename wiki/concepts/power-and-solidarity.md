# Power and Solidarity

## Definition as Coined

Roger Brown and Albert Gilman's "The Pronouns of Power and Solidarity" (1960) proposed that European two-choice second-person pronoun systems (German *du*/*Sie*, French *tu*/*vous*, Russian *ty*/*vy*) are governed by two semantic dimensions: an asymmetric **power** dimension (the non-reciprocal T from superior to inferior, V from inferior to superior) and a symmetric **solidarity** dimension (mutual T between intimates/equals, mutual V between distant equals). This framework is treated in *Directions in Sociolinguistics* (Gumperz & Hymes, eds., 1972) as the founding case of address-rule study.

## Semantic History

Two chapters in the 1972 volume develop and qualify the framework:

- **Paul Friedrich** (Ch. 9, "Social Context and Semantic Feature: The Russian Pronominal Usage") argued that the power/solidarity dichotomy, applied to a corpus of over eight thousand pages of nineteenth-century Russian realist fiction, was too coarse to predict pronoun choice with accuracy; he proposed a refinement of **ten components** (topic, social context, age, generation, sex, genealogical distance, dialect, group membership, relative jural/political authority, and emotional solidarity) and documented a key qualitative complication — that past a certain threshold of power differential (e.g., peasant to landlord, Tsar, or God), Russian usage reverted from *vy* to *ty*, marking either total subordination or unchallengeable intimacy rather than the greater power *vy* usually symbolized.
- **Susan Ervin-Tripp** (Ch. 7, "On Sociolinguistic Rules") generalized the power-and-solidarity dimensions as likely universal axes of address-system alternation (see [[sociolinguistic-rule]]), while showing via Korean data (Howell 1967) that "solidarity" may itself decompose into separable intimacy and status-based components in some systems.

## Operationalizations

Brown and Gilman's original study measured T/V distribution across social relationships (family, rank, age) in European address systems. Friedrich's refinement measured component weighting through eight extended literary cases of pronominal "switching" and "breakthrough" (sudden mid-relationship shifts in T/V usage in Lermontov, Gogol, Gorki, Tolstoy, and Dostoevsky), treating these shifts as diagnostic of which components are most heavily weighted in a given relationship.

## Applications

Applied across address systems in Russian (Friedrich), English/American, Bisayan, Korean, and Yiddish (Ervin-Tripp); also drawn on analogically for Koya kin-term alternation (Tyler, ch. 8 of the same volume), where componential, context-sensitive alternation rules govern kin-term choice rather than pronoun choice.

## Critiques and Limitations

Friedrich's refinement is itself an attributed theoretical claim resting on a textual/archival evidence base (literary fiction, not direct fieldwork observation) — a limitation he acknowledged explicitly, noting his ten-component system is "a system that has been inferred by one analyst" and is largely subconscious to native speakers themselves.
