# Fordism

## Definition as Coined

### Gramsci's primary usage (attributed; now primary-ingested)

In "Americanism and Fordism" (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]*, 1971 Eng.; *Quaderni* 1929–35), [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]] analyses US industrial rationalisation (Ford, Taylor) as a socio-political programme to create a **new type of worker and man** — psycho-physical adaptation to mechanised labour, high wages as selection and persuasion, destruction of territorial trade unionism by force, and moral/sexual regulation (puritanism, monogamy, inspection of workers' private lives) as conditions of the new industrial order. Load-bearing claim:

> "Hegemony here is born in the factory and requires for its exercise only a minute quantity of professional political and ideological intermediaries."

On Gramsci's account, where American demographic and "anti-parasitic" preconditions exist, the whole life of the nation can be made to revolve around production; structure dominates superstructures more immediately and the latter are rationalised/simplified. He poses Americanism as possible **[[passive-revolution]]** (molecular transformation without explosion) versus accumulation of elements toward upheaval — an open strategic question, not a completed periodization. Taylor's "trained gorilla" is cited; Gramsci also notes that extreme mechanisation may free the brain for nonconformist thought once adaptation is complete — a dialectical residual, not a celebration of deskilling.

### Later systematization (regulation school / Harvey)

The term was systematized as a **regime of accumulation** by the [[regulation-theory|regulation
school]] (Aglietta 1979) and given its most influential social-theory statement by
[[harvey-david|David Harvey]] in *[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The Condition of
Postmodernity]]* (1989). Do not back-read Harvey's 1945–73 full regime into Gramsci's 1930s notes.

*(Attributed to Harvey.)* Harvey dates the "symbolic initiation" of Fordism to **1914**,
when Henry Ford introduced his **five-dollar, eight-hour day** for workers on the
automated assembly line established at Dearborn the year before (1913). What distinguished
Fordism from Taylorism (F. W. Taylor's *Principles of Scientific Management*, 1911) was,
in Harvey's words, "the explicit recognition that mass production meant mass consumption,
a new system of the reproduction of labour power, a new politics of labour control and
management, a new aesthetics and psychology." Fordism was thus "less … a mere system of
mass production and more … a total way of life."

## Semantic History

*(Attributed to Harvey.)* Harvey argues Fordism failed to spread in the interwar period
for two reasons: class relations were not yet conducive to routinized, deskilled labour,
and the state's mode of regulation had not been devised to match Fordist production. Both
were resolved only after 1945. Fordism reached maturity as a "fully-fledged and
distinctive regime of accumulation" in the **postwar boom (1945–1973)**, resting on:

- a **capital–labour accord** — the Wagner Act (1935) traded union collective-bargaining
  rights for ceding control over the production process; postwar purges of radical unions
  culminated in Taft-Hartley (1947);
- **Keynesian-welfarist state management** of demand and "bureaucratic-technical
  rationality," paired aesthetically with an austere functionalist
  [[high-modernism|high modernism]];
- an international order anchored by **Bretton Woods (1944)** and US hegemony.

Harvey stresses Fordism was **uneven**: bargaining privileges were confined to a
"monopoly sector," leaving a non-privileged "competitive sector," with inequalities
compounded by race, gender, and ethnicity — tensions feeding the civil-rights and
feminist movements and the rediscovery of poverty amid affluence. The regime "held firm
at least until 1973," when a property crash, the OPEC oil shock, and deep recession
shattered it, opening the transition to [[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]].

## Operationalizations

As a periodizing construct Fordism is operationalized by a bundle of co-varying features:
assembly-line mass production of standardized goods; rising real wages linked to
productivity; strong industrial unions and collective bargaining; Keynesian demand
management and welfare provision; and stable, homogeneous mass markets. Regulation
theorists formalize it as a coupling of a *regime of accumulation* with a *mode of
regulation* (see [[regulation-theory]]).

## Applications

- **Gramsci primary:** Americanism as passive-revolutionary rationalisation; factory-born [[hegemony]]; moral regulation of the industrial workforce (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971]]*).
- **[[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-study|Burawoy 1979]]:** ethnographic demonstration that consent is produced at the point of production — explicit charter from Gramsci's factory-hegemony line.
- The baseline against which [[flexible-accumulation]] is defined ("a direct confrontation
  with the rigidities of Fordism").
- Harvey's mapping of "Fordist modernism" against "flexible postmodernism" as paired
  political-economic and cultural formations (see [[postmodernity-epochal-break-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* The Fordism/post-Fordism periodization is contested: critics (Pollert,
Sayer) argue it overstates the coherence and homogeneity of the postwar order and treats
a partial US pattern as a universal regime; economic historians question how far "Fordist"
mass production ever dominated even at mid-century. Harvey himself concedes the model's
unevenness while defending it as a useful heuristic. The construct remains standard in
economic sociology and geography despite these reservations.
