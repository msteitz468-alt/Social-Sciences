# State Nobility (*Noblesse d'État*)

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, [[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996|The State Nobility]].)* The **state
nobility** is Bourdieu's name for the modern ruling stratum whose domination is legitimated by
academically certified "intelligence" rather than blood or God: the graduates of the top
[[grandes-ecoles]] and members of the *grands corps* who staff the summits of the state, the
large corporations, and cultural administration. The term is a deliberate provocation: like the
old nobility, this group's titles are state-guaranteed legal monopolies conferring "superior
essence"; unlike noble titles, academic titles "do not constitute property that can be bought
and sold, or passed down to descendants," and are variably tied to technical competence.

The historical thesis: "the state nobility, initially in the guise of the *noblesse de robe*,
and the academic title, as a 'patent of education' guaranteeing privileges, were born of
correlative and complementary inventions." The *noblesse de robe*, "of which contemporary
technocrats are the structural heirs (and sometimes the descendants), is **a body that created
itself by creating the state**" — inventing, in the process, the political philosophy of
disinterested "public service" directed to universal ends. Chancellor d'Aguesseau's 1693 speech
on merit, disinterestedness, and the public is read as the founding text of the technocrat's
sociodicy — "a Jacobin Gaullist or socialist ENA graduate from the 'liberating school' could
well make this speech today."

## Semantic History

Three supporting concepts travel with the term:

- **State magic.** The academic title is the paradigm of *state magic*: "acts of certification
  or validation through which an official authority, acting as an agent of the central bank of
  symbolic credit — the state — guarantees and consecrates a certain state of affairs." Official
  nomination is "a genuine ontological promotion, a transmutation" — the state's monopoly of
  legitimate *symbolic* violence, which Bourdieu holds is more decisive than the
  Weberian/Tillyan monopoly of physical violence.
- **The lengthening of the circuits of legitimation.** The "fundamental law of the economy of
  legitimation": consecration is worth more the more independent the consecrator appears
  (self-crowning is worthless). Historically, legitimation circuits lengthen — from personal
  inheritance through ever-more-mediated chains of academic consecration — raising symbolic
  efficacy ("physical coercion... give[s] way to the milder dissimulated constraints of symbolic
  violence") while raising legitimation costs and crisis risks.
- **The "liberating school" myth.** The meritocratic self-image of republican schooling is, on
  Bourdieu's reading, the old ideology of noble birth "restored beneath a democratic facade of
  an ideology of natural gifts and individual merit" — Pascal's "gain of 30 years without
  trouble" now delivered as academically certified precocity.

Against Weber, Bourdieu argues the rationalization of selection by examination is "not as simple
and one-sided as Max Weber leads us to believe": Weber registered but never integrated the
parallel between academic and noble titles; "progress in 'rationalization'... is in the sense of
Freud, more than Weber" — rationalization as ever-more-effective *justification* of arbitrariness.

## Operationalizations

The 1972 study of 216 top-company CEOs (see [[bourdieu-state-nobility-study]]): "state bosses"
(higher-civil-service families, Sciences-po/ENA/Inspection des Finances routes, state-linked
firms) versus "family bosses" (owners and heirs); the *bourgeoisie de robe*'s dominance of
technocratic and state companies; the prestigious-club and nobility-marker data ("we have
probably never seen so many bearers of noble or ennobled names in the highest echelons of the
state").

## Applications

- The organizing concept of the book's synthesis of [[field-of-power]],
  [[rites-of-institution]], and [[modes-of-reproduction]].
- The "dominated dominants" analysis: like the robe under the sword nobility, technocrats
  "enhance their interests by associating them, perfectly innocently, with causes that appear to
  them to be universal, such as emancipatory science, or, in other times, the liberating school."
- Comparative elite studies of credentialed ruling classes beyond France (Bourdieu's explicit
  invitation, by homology).

## Critiques and Limitations

The nobility analogy is analytic, not legal — critics ask whether it over-assimilates
credential-holders to a hereditary caste when the school-mediated mode is precisely *statistical*
(see [[modes-of-reproduction]]). The empirical base is French and dated (1960s–1972 data,
published 1989). The concept's polemical charge — aimed at the technocracy's meritocratic
self-understanding — is intrinsic to it; record its claims in attributed voice.
