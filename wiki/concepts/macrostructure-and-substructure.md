# Macrostructure and Substructure

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] distinguishes levels of social structure recursively: **microstructure** = "the interrelations between individuals in a group"; **macrostructure** = "the interrelations of these groups in a larger collectivity or of these larger collectivities in a still larger one." Substructures form **intersecting, not concentric, circles** — individuals belong simultaneously to overlapping groups (region, ethnicity, party, employer, union), so boundaries are neither sharp nor fixed.

The concept's analytic payload is a set of **level-inversion principles**: the same value standard has opposite implications at different levels.
- **Particularistic values** that integrate a substructure internally simultaneously create *segregating* boundaries between it and other substructures (a church's shared faith divides society into competing denominations).
- **Particularistic** standards from the macrostructure's viewpoint often act as **universalistic** standards *within* a substructure (religious devotion differentiates status inside a denomination).
- **Diverse universalistic** standards across substructures constitute a *particularistic* standard for the macrostructure as a whole.
- Conversely, a universalistically-defined mark of failure (low economic status) can become a particularistic value of solidarity within a substructure ("the solidarity of the proletariat").

## Semantic History

Blau builds explicitly on [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] ("the elements of differentiated social circles are undifferentiated, those of undifferentiated ones are differentiated") and on Gouldner's point that the relative **autonomy** of component parts is "a catalyst for social change," functioning as an analytically "exogenous" force within the structure. The concept is Blau's attempt to carry the [[social-exchange-theory|exchange-theoretic]] facets — integration, differentiation, organization, opposition — up through nested levels of structure.

## Operationalizations

Blau schematizes a 3×3 matrix of three facets (integration, differentiation, organization) crossed with the macrostructure and substructure levels, illustrating each cell (e.g., substructure differentiation creates cross-cutting integrative ties in the macrostructure; particularistic values diffuse from community into primary groups — Stinchcombe's "penetration").

## Applications

The framework for Blau's macro-chapters: it grounds [[cross-cutting-conflicts]], [[intergroup-mobility]] (the "big fish, little pond" dilemma), and the legitimacy of organizations within a value system (an "extremist opposition is illegitimate only as long as it is so defined by prevailing values"). It is the vehicle through which [[mediating-values]] extend exchange across nested structures.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The level-inversion principles are suggestive but loosely specified; what counts as "a level" is analyst-relative.
- This is the macro machinery Blau himself (1986) concluded could not be *derived* from micro exchange and required a distinct conceptual scheme — he subsequently rebuilt macrostructure on a wholly different (positional/quantitative) basis in *Inequality and Heterogeneity* (1977).
