# Art-Culture System

## Definition as Coined

[[clifford-james]] introduces the "art-culture system" in Chapter 10 of
*[[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]]* (1988) as a
model of how objects from non-Western societies circulate through Western regimes of
value. He formalizes it with a Greimas semiotic square generating four zones:
authentic masterpiece, authentic artifact, inauthentic masterpiece, and inauthentic
artifact. Objects move between these zones according to patterned institutional
routes — an object can be "upgraded" from ethnographic artifact to fine-art
masterpiece by museum or market recontextualization — but Clifford identifies a
structural blockage on the movement from zone four (inauthentic artifact, e.g. a
tourist curio or a repatriated/return-bound object) back to zone one, arguing the
system resists rehabilitating objects once coded as inauthentic or as belonging to a
living, changing culture rather than a frozen tradition.

## Semantic History

Clifford dates the system's historical consolidation to roughly 1900–1920, the period
of modernist "discovery" of non-Western art (converging with Boasian cultural
relativism), citing Elizabeth Williams's 1985 scholarship on the display of
pre-Columbian objects in Paris collections and Paul Rivet's programmatic
anti-aesthetic injunction at the Musée de l'Homme (objects displayed as ethnographic
evidence, explicitly not as art). He uses the 1984–85 Museum of Modern Art exhibition
*"Primitivism" in 20th Century Art* (curated by William Rubin and Kirk Varnedoe) as a
test case, arguing the show's central "affinity" thesis — resemblance between modern
and tribal art — was a loosely argued allegory produced by a curatorial selection
constructed to manufacture the resemblance it claimed to discover. He contrasts
Rosalind Krauss's "dark," Bataille-inflected reading of the affinity thesis against
Varnedoe's more affirmative position, presenting both as positions in an ongoing
dispute rather than adjudicating between them.

## Operationalizations

The semiotic square is Clifford's explicit operationalization: any object can be
provisionally placed in one of the four zones based on its current institutional
context (museum vs. market vs. curio shop) and the label applied to it (art vs.
artifact, authentic vs. inauthentic), with the model predicting characteristic
directions of movement between zones.

## Applications

Clifford applies the system to repatriation and refusal cases — Zuni refusals to loan
or exhibit war-god (Ahayu:da) figures, and repatriation actions by U'mista Cultural
Centre, Quadra Island, and Haida museums — reading these as communities contesting
their objects' assigned zone. He also discusses Michael Coe's account of "lost and
found" traditions and the Te Maori exhibition as cases where source communities
actively renegotiated an object's position in the system. Clifford connects Western
collecting itself to a possessive mode of self-fashioning, drawing on C.B. Macpherson,
Richard Handler, and Susan Stewart, and draws an explicit parallel to Marx's
[[commodity-fetishism]] in describing how collected objects are stripped of the social
relations that produced them. He also invokes Johannes Fabian's critique of the
"denial of coevalness" — the practice of treating source cultures as existing in a
different time than the collector — as underlying the system's authenticity coding.

## Critiques and Limitations

Clifford presents the art-culture system as his own analytic construction rather than
a contested import from another scholar, so the frontmatter records no external
priority dispute; the live contestation is internal to his case material — the
Krauss/Varnedoe dispute over the MoMA "Primitivism" show's affinity thesis is recorded
as a position dispute, not resolved in wiki voice.
