# Voluntary Association (the Art of Association)

## Definition as Coined

For [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]], the **voluntary association** is the
characteristic instrument by which democratic citizens act together, and the "art of
association" is the master civic skill of a free democracy. He treated associations in two
registers. In Vol. 1 he analyzed **political associations** as a safeguard against the
[[tyranny-of-the-majority|majority]]: "the freedom of association is a vital safeguard
against the tyranny of the majority," and "there are no countries where associations are
more necessary... than those whose social state is democratic," because aristocracies
possess natural secondary bodies that check power and democracies do not. In Vol. 2 he
analyzed **civil associations** — the endless American habit of combining for commercial,
moral, religious, recreational, and charitable ends — as the antidote to [[individualism]]:
what an aristocratic lord did alone, democratic citizens can do only by associating.

He distinguished escalating degrees of political association (mere shared doctrine → power
of assembly → full representative structure, "a government within a government") and
contrasted the American model (peaceful, persuasive, assuming minority status) with the
European (quasi-military, aiming to fight and claiming already to be the majority).

## Semantic History

Tocqueville's associationalism is the acknowledged ancestor of modern theories of **civil
society** and **social capital**: the claim that dense voluntary association builds the
trust, skills, and cross-cutting ties a democracy needs. The link between associations and
newspapers (each makes the other possible) prefigures work on media and collective action.
[[putnam-robert-d|Putnam]]'s *[[putnam-bowling-alone-2000|Bowling Alone]]* (2000) is the
major late-twentieth-century empirical reworking: multi-indicator U.S. decline of joining
and related civic stocks, filed as [[civic-social-capital]] / [[civic-engagement]] (not
Bourdieu's [[social-capital]]).

## Operationalizations

Not quantified by Tocqueville; read from observation of American civic life (e.g., the 1831
Philadelphia free-trade convention as an association approaching legislative character).
Later social science operationalizes it through associational membership and participation
rates — see Putnam's triangulated survey and membership series on [[putnam-bowling-alone-study]].

## Applications

Association is the pivotal *remedy* in Tocqueville's system. Against [[individualism]] and
the drift toward [[democratic-despotism]], it — together with local
[[township-democracy|self-government]], a free press, the jury, and
[[self-interest-properly-understood]] — reconstitutes isolated individuals into a public
capable of resisting a centralizing state and schooling itself in liberty.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Impressionistic and celebratory; Tocqueville did not weigh associations that entrench
  exclusion or faction.
- The civil-society/social-capital literature it seeded has itself been criticized for
  treating association as unambiguously benign — Putnam's own Ch 22 "dark side" and
  [[bowling-alone-debate]] record that line of critique.
