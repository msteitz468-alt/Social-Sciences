# Women's Wealth (doba)

The category of exchange valuables produced and controlled by [[trobriand-islanders|Trobriand]] women — bundles of dried banana leaves (*nununiga*) and fibrous skirts (*doba*) — brought to prominence by [[weiner-annette-b|Annette Weiner]]'s *[[weiner-women-of-value-1976|Women of Value, Men of Renown]]* (1976). Malinowski and H. A. Powell had noted women's ceremonial activity only in passing; Weiner's central empirical claim (attributed) is that these objects constitute a genuine wealth domain doing decisive economic, political, and cosmological work.

## Definition as Coined

*Doba* is the general Trobriand term for women's wealth: colorful decorated fiber skirts (traditionally red/purple) and *nununiga*, tied bundles of dried banana-leaf strips. Both are made from the leaves of one banana variety (*wakeya*), grown on trees women own and inherit from mothers and sisters. Weiner reports (attributed) that the bundles "have no use value, only exchange value" — they exist to be accumulated and given away, above all at the women's mortuary ceremony ([[sagali]]).

## Semantic History

The term is emic (*doba*); Weiner's **analytic** use of "women's wealth" is the migrated concept. Its force is comparative: against the New Guinea Highlands pattern in which women are "mere producers" and men the "transactors" (M. Strathern 1972), Weiner argued (attributed) that Kiriwina women both **produce and transact** — they are publicly recognized economic decision-makers. Her formulation: "If a woman cannot be wealthy without a man standing behind her, Kiriwina men cannot become powerful without a woman who in yams and doba exchanges comes first."

## Operationalizations

Women's wealth is quantified and publicly counted; magnitude is the measure of a woman's standing:

- To "be first" (become *napweyaveka*, "big-woman," of a ceremony) a woman must give away more than **5,000 bundles and 20–30 skirts** in a single day.
- Wealth is accumulated through **valova** — exchange of goods for bundles: a stick of tobacco ≈ 10 bundles; a pig ≈ 1,700 bundles (kula shell valuables and stone ax blades are excluded from valova).
- Bundles are graded by quality — new (*yawovau*), old (*yabwabogwa*), clean (*migileu*), dirty (*yapagatu*, repurposed as skirt fiber); using dirty bundles in a payment owed to yam-givers provokes public shaming.
- Weiner reported "inflation": where ~200 bundles once sufficed at a ceremony, cash income (tourism, remittances) had pushed the requirement to 300–400 by 1972 — a date-stamped observation of her fieldwork moment.

A husband is **obligated to help his wife accumulate doba** (via *valova* and via presentations from his "sisters"), in reciprocity for the [[urigubu|yam harvests]] he receives through her — one of the two linked circuits Weiner used to overturn Malinowski's "tribute" reading (see [[weiner-malinowski-trobriand-debate]]).

## Applications

Weiner interpreted women's wealth (attributed) as the material vehicle of [[reproduction-regeneration|dala regeneration]]: the skirt is "an embodiment of all that is 'womanness' — sexuality, reproduction, and nurture," and only women "have the power to transform dirty into clean" (dirty bundles refashioned into skirts; mourners' black bands replaced with red skirts). At a [[sagali]] women "cut" the deceased away from the reciprocal relationships built up over a lifetime, restoring a pure ancestral *baloma* to the [[dala]]. Because this wealth represents dala itself, Weiner argued it "exceeds explicit individual control," unlike male valuables, which are "ego-centered rather than dala-centered." The concept became the seed of her later, broader notion of **inalienable possessions** (1992).

## Critiques and Limitations

The framework rests on a single fieldworker's 1971–72 observation of a domain earlier ethnographers under-recorded; its cosmological reading (women = enduring/cosmic, men = ephemeral/historical) is interpretive and, critics note, risks reinstating a gender dualism the wider [[feminist-anthropology|anthropology of women]] questioned. Weiner herself flagged an open comparative question: whether Trobriand women's wealth is genuinely distinctive in Melanesia, or whether ethnographers elsewhere were "deceived by male comments" into missing comparable domains. Compare Marilyn Strathern's later reworking in *[[gender-of-the-gift|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988).
