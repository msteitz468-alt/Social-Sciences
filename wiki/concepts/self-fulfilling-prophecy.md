# Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

## Definition as Coined

[[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (*[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Ch. XIII) defined the **self-fulfilling prophecy** as, in the beginning, **a false definition of the situation** that **evokes a new behavior** which makes the originally false conception **come true**. The prophecy “perpetuates a reign of error” because the prophet cites the actual course of events as proof. He grounded it in W. I. Thomas's theorem ("If men define situations as real, they are real in their consequences") and illustrated with the bank-run type-case (Cartwright Millingville parable): rumor of insolvency produces the run that produces insolvency. He extended the pattern to ethnic and racial dynamics (unions, “moral alchemy,” institutional exclusion loops) and named a counterpart **suicidal prophecy** (a true prediction that fails because it was announced and acted upon).

## Semantic History

From Merton's mid-century essay into general social science, psychology, and everyday English. Counterpart concept in the same essay: the **suicidal prophecy** (a true prediction that fails because it was made and acted upon). Drift risk: any feedback loop labeled "self-fulfilling" without the original **false definition** clause.

## Operationalizations

Classic qualitative type-cases (bank failure; ethnic exclusion loops). Later experimental and survey work on expectancy effects and stereotype threat operationalize related mechanisms under different names — do not silently equate without design comparison.

## Applications

Ethnic and racial prejudice dynamics (as Merton argued); institutional trust and panic; educational and organizational expectancy effects (later literature).

## Critiques and Limitations

Scope conditions (when definitions fail to mobilize behavior); agency and resistance; difficulty of proving the *initial* definition was false independent of the outcome.
