# Primary Frame

**Primary frame** (Ridgeway): a fundamental cultural device people use to make sense of self and other and to coordinate social relations. In Ridgeway's program, **sex/gender** is such a primary frame in contemporary (especially U.S.) society — near-automatic, socially nearly mandatory categorization that organizes interaction before more situational identities.

## Definition as Coined

Ridgeway uses "primary frame" for category systems that (1) people apply quickly and largely automatically, (2) are culturally required for routine coordination (failure to classify produces social disruption), and (3) carry shared "common knowledge" beliefs that others are assumed to know. Sex/gender is her central case; the concept is developed fully in *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* (2011), building on earlier statements.

## Semantic History

Anchored in sociological and social-psychological work on categorization and coordination (person perception; expectation states), not identical to Goffman's frame analysis of situational definitions — Ridgeway's primary frame is specifically about **person categories** used to start the coordination of relations. Distinct from West & Zimmerman's [[sex-category]] (membership sustained by displays) though complementary: sex category is what is accomplished; primary framing is why the category is so interactionally load-bearing.

## Operationalizations

- Disruption when sex is unclassifiable (illustrations; person-perception findings).
- Automatic sex categorization research.
- Priming of gender stereotypes once categorization occurs.
- Contextual variation in how much the frame shapes behavior (salience, task relevance, anonymity vs public).

## Applications

Core of [[gender-as-primary-frame]]; applied to workplace job-matching and evaluation, household labor, and innovation sites (start-ups, courtship) where new practices form.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether gender is equally "primary" across cultures and historical periods is an empirical question the U.S.-focused book does not fully settle. Risk of over-generalizing automaticity; Ridgeway notes motivational and contextual modulation.
