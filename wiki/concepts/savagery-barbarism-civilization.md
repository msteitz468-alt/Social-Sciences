# Savagery, Barbarism, Civilization

## Definition as Coined

[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]’s tripartite periodization of human culture, adopted as the scaffold of [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]’s *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin]]* ch. I: **savagery**, **barbarism**, and **civilization**, each with lower/middle/upper stages marked by inventions and subsistence modes (e.g. fire, fish subsistence, bow; pottery; animal domestication, irrigation agriculture, iron; alphabet/writing as civilization’s threshold). Engels treats the scheme as a materialist periodization by production.

## Semantic History

Victorian progress vocabulary; superseded in archaeology by regional sequences, radiocarbon chronologies, and multi-linear models. Words “savage”/“barbarian” carry colonial hierarchical load — keep as **historical labels of the theory**, not as current wiki descriptors of peoples. Related to but not identical with Spencerian differentiation or Comtean three stages.

Stocking ([[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|*Victorian Anthropology*]], 1987) traces Morgan's tripartite scheme to an earlier eighteenth-century lineage: Turgot's stadial sequence (hunting/pastoral/agricultural/commercial) is one of several progressivist stage-schemes classical evolutionism inherited (attributed). The English words themselves carry older etymological freight: "barbarian" derives from the Greek contrast between intelligible speakers and those (notably the Scythians) whose speech sounded like reiterative mumbling; "savage" derives from Latin *sylva* (woods) — those who lived in forests rather than cities, a term applied more often to peoples Europeans encountered sailing West (finding, per Stocking's etymological account).

### Victorian usage (Stocking 1987)

Stocking argues (attributed) that Victorian "savagery" carried a **"paradoxical double image"**: a "philosopher savage" figuring the intellectualist evolution of religion ([[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s rational "ancient savage philosopher," see [[animism]]) alongside a "bestial savage" figuring the evolution of sex and marriage (McLennan's promiscuity-to-monogamy scheme, see [[primitive-promiscuity]]) — both readings, Stocking suggests, reflections of Victorian middle-class self-image rather than neutral ethnographic description.

Stocking documents (attributed) that "savagery" functioned in Victorian Britain as an analogical category applied not only to overseas non-European peoples but to **domestic subordinate social categories** — women, children, criminals, peasants, laborers, paupers, the insane, and the Irish — on the shared grounds of impulsiveness and deficient foresight; evolutionary theory supplied ideological justification for their continued subordination as merely reflecting uneven developmental stage. Stocking draws a sharp distinction, however, in the *mode* of the equation: for these domestic categories the relation asserted was one of **similitude** ("savage-*like*"), whereas for "true savages" (dark-skinned overseas peoples) it was asserted as **identity** — "other social categories might be like savages, but with true savages it was not a matter of similitude but of identity" (Stocking 1987). Herbert Spencer's *Descriptive Sociology* volume on "lowest and negrito races" tabulated the Tasmanians negatively on nearly every dimension of the savagery/barbarism/civilization scale (no general conceptions, few laws, scarcely any aesthetic sentiments) — Stocking treats this tabulation as a paradigm case of stage-thinking applied to an extinguished people, entangled with the "paleolithic equation" that helped distance Victorian anthropology from the moral weight of Tasmanian extermination (see [[antiquity-of-man]], the Tasmanian genocide material on [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987]]).

## Operationalizations

Morgan’s trait thresholds (pottery = lower barbarism, etc.). Engels notes unevenness and missing data outside Europe/America. Modern archaeology does not use these thresholds as global stages.

## Applications

Organizes *Origin*’s entire narrative: family forms and gens mapped onto stages; civilization = commodity production + monogamy + state (ch. IX).

## Critiques and Limitations

Unilinear, ethnocentric, empirically false as world history; see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]. MIA editorial notes in the ingested text already flag conflicts with later evidence — those notes are apparatus, not Engels.

**Primary source now ingested.** Morgan's own statement of the scheme is in *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]* (1877) — seven "ethnical periods" (Lower/Middle/Upper Savagery; Lower/Middle/Upper Barbarism; Civilization), each opened by an invention or art of subsistence (fire+fish; bow; pottery; animal domestication or irrigation+adobe; iron smelting; phonetic alphabet), with the Danish three-age system judged insufficient because its ages overlap. Morgan grounds the universality of the sequence in **psychic unity** ("the specific identity of the brain of all the races of mankind"). The closing racial-hierarchy framing (the "intrinsic superiority" of the "Aryan family") is period scientific racism, attributed to Morgan, never wiki voice. See the [[morgan-ancient-society-study|study page]].

### Childe's archaeological mapping (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] maps Morgan's ladder onto archaeological economies (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* ch. I — attributed): **savagery** ≈ Palaeolithic food-gathering; **barbarism** ≈ food-producing [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic]] (and Copper Age "higher barbarism"); **civilization** ≈ urban Bronze Age after the [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]]. He keeps Morgan's vocabulary while insisting economic "neolithic" is not a universal chronological stage. Hierarchical colonial load of the terms remains — treat as theory labels, not descriptors of living peoples.

### Scott densification (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017, esp. Ch. 7; counter-narrative to the stadial ladder on this page.)*

- **“Barbarian” as ironic political category.** Scott uses the term tongue-in-cheek as shorthand for **nonstate peoples**—labels invented in state centers to stigmatize non-subjects, not evolutionary stages. Ming “cooked” vs “raw” split groups of identical language/culture by tax registration and magistracy (“entered the map”); for Chinese and Roman sources, barbarians began where taxes and sovereignty stopped. Domestication lens: grain growers/bondspeople = domesticated subjects; foragers/nomads = wild—barbarians : subjects :: vermin : livestock :: weeds : crops.
- **Rejection of one-way civilization narrative.** Scott calls “fundamentally wrong” the story that primitives become sedentary state subjects while barbarians are those left outside: millennia of flux between sedentary and non-sedentary options; states *create* refugee barbarians by displacement; flight from states (secondary primitivism) is common; tribes as administrative fictions of the state (“states preceded tribes”).
- **“Golden age of barbarians” (counter-narrative).** Between first state appearance and state hegemony over nonstate peoples (Scott ventures multi-millennial span, perhaps until ~1600 CE), it was often *better* to be a barbarian: states as plunder/trade nodes enhancing barbarian ecology; multi-web subsistence, mobility, freer condition than the “celebrated yeoman farmer”—with melancholy caveats (slaving of other nonstate peoples; mercenary service that replenished the state).
- **Dark twins (Lattimore).** Civilization and barbarism as co-produced: “Civilization itself created its own barbarian plague.” Links: [[nonstate-spaces]] · [[pastoralism]] · [[societal-collapse]] · [[social-evolutionism]] · [[state-formation]].
