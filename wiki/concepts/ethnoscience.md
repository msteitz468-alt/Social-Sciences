# Ethnoscience

Ethnoscience (also "the New Ethnography," cognitive anthropology, or componential/formal
semantic analysis) is the approach, developed in American anthropology in the late 1950s and
1960s, that aimed to describe a culture in terms of the categories and classificatory logic
its own members use — recovered rigorously through the analysis of native terminological
systems. [[frake-charles-o|Charles O. Frake]] is one of its central figures.

## Definition as Coined

The program held that the ethnographer's task is to specify what one must know to behave
appropriately as a member — the culture's covert classificatory principles — by eliciting a
domain's terms and the contrast sets and taxonomic relations among them. Frake extended the
method from domains like disease, kinship, and firewood to **speech events themselves**: in
"Struck by Speech" he analysed the [[yakan|Yakan]] concept of litigation as one node in a
taxonomy of nested speech-event categories (*mitiŋ* 'discussion' ⊃ *qisun* 'conference' ⊃
*nawpakkat* 'negotiation' ⊃ *hukum* 'litigation'), distinguished along dimensions of focus,
purpose, roles, and integrity — an early bridge between formal semantics and the
[[ethnography-of-speaking|ethnography of speaking]].

## Semantic History

Ethnoscience began as an ambition to make ethnography replicable and formally explicit —
"an eliciting technique" that could yield a native's-eye account testable against further
questioning. It was closely associated with componential analysis of kinship (Lounsbury,
Goodenough) and taxonomy of folk domains (Conklin, Frake, Sturtevant). Over the 1970s the
program's stronger claims — that a culture *is* its terminological knowledge — were widely
criticized, and its energy dispersed into cognitive anthropology, folk-biological
classification, and the ethnography of communication.

## Operationalizations

Its methods are systematic elicitation ("What kinds of X are there?", "What are they
doing?"), contrast-set and taxonomic mapping, and componential (feature) analysis of terms.

## Applications

Beyond Frake's Yakan and Subanun work, the approach informed folk classification of plants,
color, and disease, and — via the analysis of speech-act and speech-event terminologies —
the [[ethnography-of-speaking|ethnography of speaking]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics (attributed) argued that ethnoscience mistook lexical structure for cultural
knowledge, privileged nameable and taxonomically neat domains, and could not readily handle
meaning-in-use, power, or practice — objections that the interpretive turn and practice
theory pressed. Its status is thus that of a productive but superseded formalism whose
techniques survive within cognitive anthropology.
