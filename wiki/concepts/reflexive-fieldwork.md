# Reflexive Fieldwork

The stance that ethnographic knowledge is not neutrally *collected* from informants but
**intersubjectively and dialogically constructed** in the encounter between an
anthropologist and interlocutors who are both historically situated — and that this
construction is itself a proper object of ethnographic writing. Given its sharpest early
statement in [[rabinow-paul|Paul Rabinow]]'s *[[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977|Reflections
on Fieldwork in Morocco]]* (1977), which took as its motto Paul Ricoeur's formula that
understanding is **"the comprehension of the self by the detour of the comprehension of
the other."**

## Definition as Coined

Rabinow argued (1977) that "facts" in fieldwork are not found lying about to be gathered
but are produced through a chain of interpretation: the informant already interprets his
own culture, the anthropologist interprets the informant's interpretation, and the
encounter forces both into a doubled self-consciousness. Data, on this view, are the
sediment of a relationship — shaped by the power asymmetries, language limits, and mutual
historicity of the two parties. Understanding is therefore always **partial**, and its
partiality is a condition, not a failure, of the work.

## Semantic History

The reflexive impulse predates the term: confessional and autobiographical fieldwork
writing runs back through Lévi-Strauss's *Tristes Tropiques* (1955) and the posthumous
publication of Malinowski's field diaries (1967). Rabinow (1977), alongside
[[crapanzano-vincent|Vincent Crapanzano]]'s *Tuhami* (1980), Kevin
[[dwyer-kevin|Dwyer]]'s *Moroccan Dialogues* (1982), and Jean-Paul Dumont's *The Headman
and I* (1978), turned confession into an epistemological argument. By the mid-1980s the
stance was generalized — and radicalized toward textual and political critique — in
*[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986), where "reflexivity"
came to name the whole crisis of ethnographic authority (see [[writing-culture-debate]]).
The meaning thus drifted from Rabinow's dialogical/hermeneutic sense toward a
literary-critical and self-critical one, a drift critics later charged with sliding into
narcissism.

## Operationalizations

Reflexive fieldwork is a stance rather than a measured variable. In practice it appears
as: (a) narrating access, brokerage, and the fieldworker's social position; (b)
foregrounding named dialogues and disagreements rather than a seamless third-person
survey; (c) treating informants as co-theorists rather than data sources. Different
traditions weight these differently — Rabinow's hermeneutic/dialogical version against
the later textualist and, later still, the feminist and decolonial versions.

## Applications

- [[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977|Rabinow's Moroccan fieldwork]] (1968–69) among the
  villagers of [[sidi-lahcen-lyussi]] and in Sefrou — the paradigm case.
- A precursor node for the [[writing-culture|Writing Culture]] program and the
  [[writing-culture-debate|crisis of representation]].
- In tension with, yet grown out of, [[interpretive-anthropology|Geertzian interpretive
  anthropology]] — Rabinow kept Geertz's [[webs-of-significance|webs of significance]]
  but insisted the webs of anthropologist and informant become, in the encounter, "at
  least partially intertwined."

## Reflexivity in Archaeological Fieldwork

The stance crossed into archaeology in the late 1980s–90s as an extension of [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]]
critique. [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] identified a contradiction in British fieldwork
(attributed): it recognizes that interpretation is embedded in excavation yet practises as if
data collection precedes interpretation — as if "description can be separated from
interpretation." His **reflexive methodology** at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]], programmatic in
*[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999), tried to build interpretive
self-awareness into routine method ([[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge|interpretation at the trowel's edge]]):
lab specialists visit trenches every 1–2 days; sampling follows negotiated priorities; diaries and
video situate coded records; integrated databases keep interpretations in flux; embedded anthropologists
study knowledge production; web/hypertext/VR aim at multivocal access — against the linear
"factory" model (excavation → finds processing → analysis → synthesis). Four themes (Hodder 1999):
reflexivity, relationality/contextuality, interactivity, multivocality. Bender, Hamilton, and Tilley's
Bodmin Moor project pursued **multivocality**, juxtaposing feature descriptions, diary
entries, and narrative in publication.

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]*, 2002) both extends and critiques this current (attributed). He faults the
reflexive projects for neglecting the fieldwork *encounter* as a bodily, material experience,
and proposes a reflexivity "that employs the very methods we seek to interrogate" — at
Hofstaðir, Iceland (1998), his team re-excavated a 1908 trench and inferred the earlier
excavator's practice from its physical remains, using archaeological method to interrogate
archaeological method. Archaeology, for Lucas, is not merely a textual practice but a
**material praxis**: it must actively materialize its own data, and its validity rests on the
[[archaeological-record|iterability]] of the representations it produces. Because
archaeological excavation is destructive and unrepeatable, the reflexive problem is sharper
than in ethnography — the "encounter" cannot be revisited, only recorded.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue reflexive fieldwork can substitute self-display for analysis, privileging
the anthropologist's experience over the people studied ("navel-gazing"); that its
partial-understanding humility can excuse thin ethnography; and (from
[[asad-talal|Asad]] and political-economy critics) that it locates the problem in
individual consciousness and writing rather than in the institutional and colonial
power that structures the encounter. Defenders reply that making the conditions of
knowledge explicit is more honest than the disappeared observer of the classic monograph.
