# Imagination as Social Practice

The foundational move of *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at
Large]]*: [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s claim that imagination has become a
collective social fact and a key site of agency. Attributed throughout.

## Definition as Coined

"There has been a shift in recent decades… in which the imagination has become a
collective, social fact" *(Appadurai)*. Its culminating formulation: the
imagination "has become an organized field of social practices, a form of work (in
the sense of both labor and culturally organized practice), and a form of
negotiation between sites of agency (individuals) and globally defined fields of
possibility… The imagination is now central to all forms of agency, is itself a
social fact, and is the key component of the new global order."

## Semantic History

Extends [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s [[collective-representations]] (imagination
granted the status of an objective social fact) and Benedict Anderson's "imagined
communities" — Appadurai's **"imagined worlds"** are "the multiple worlds that are
constituted by the historically situated imaginations of persons and groups spread
around the globe." He synthesizes three sources: mechanically produced images (the
[[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt School]]), Anderson's imagined community, and the
French *imaginaire*.

## Operationalizations

Three distinctions carry the argument: (1) imagination has "broken out of the
special expressive space of art, myth, and ritual" into everyday life; (2)
**imagination vs. fantasy** — fantasy is private and dissipative, imagination is
"projective," collective, and "a staging ground for action"; (3) imagination "as a
property of collectives," not the Romantic gifted individual. Appadurai rejects the
[[frankfurt-school|Frankfurt-School]] "opium of the masses" reading: "where there is
consumption there is pleasure, and where there is pleasure there is agency."

## Applications

The two decisive forces are **media and migration** (Appadurai's "theory of
rupture"), producing "deterritorialized viewers" and "diasporic public spheres."
Consumption reframed as "the social discipline of the imagination" (ch. 4); the
imagination as the medium through which ethnic "structures of feeling" and
[[production-of-locality|local subjects]] are produced (chs. 7, 9). Underlies the
whole [[global-cultural-flows]] framework.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics find the elevation of imagination optimistic and hard to falsify; the claim
that media consumption reliably yields "agency" is contested by media-effects and
political-economy scholarship.
