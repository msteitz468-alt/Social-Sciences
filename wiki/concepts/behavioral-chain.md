# Behavioral Chain

In [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]]’s [[behavioral-archaeology|behavioral archaeology]], a **behavioral chain** is the sequence of all activities in which an element participates during its “life” within a cultural system (attributed, Schiffer 1975d / 1995 Ch. 5). A *chain segment* is a specified portion of that sequence. The construct organizes life-history analysis at the scale of individual activities, as distinct from coarser **flow models** that group related activities into stages such as procurement, manufacture, and use (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 23 §17).

## Definition as Coined

Schiffer defined the chain as activity-ordered element biography, shifting emphasis from Marvin Harris’s actor-activity orientation toward element life history because of the material nature of the [[archaeological-record]] and the predictive properties of chains (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 5). Each activity on a chain is described by seven components (attributed):

1. A behavioral description of the activity  
2. The social unit of activity performance  
3. The frequency of activity performance  
4. Conjoined elements (critical co-occurring materials)  
5. Interactions / transformations of the focal element  
6. Pathways created to the archaeological record by activity outputs  
7. Points at which other elements’ chains integrate or diverge  

Output fraction \(C = I/b\) and pathways \(Y\) formalize how often material leaves systemic use into disposal streams (attributed).

## Semantic History

Rooted in early-1970s Arizona program work; published as “Behavioral Chain Analysis” (Schiffer 1975d) and reprinted as *First Principles* Ch. 5. Parallel to but not identical with European *[[chaine-operatoire]]* (operational sequence of manufacture): behavioral chains cover the full life history including use, reuse, and discard pathways, not only production. Elevated to program principle in Schiffer’s 1995 conceptual inventory (Ch. 23 §17). Later “people and things” / performance-characteristic work (Schiffer & Skibo) sits on the same life-history scaffold without replacing the chain vocabulary.

## Operationalizations

- **Ethnographic reconstruction:** partial Hopi maize chain ca. A.D. 1900 used as didactic example; Schiffer noted ethnographies are incomplete and many entries were guessed — accuracy of that case was “irrelevant for illustrating the basic principles” (attributed, 1995 Ch. 5).  
- **Refuse pathways:** in societies with developed disposal systems, most elements enter the record away from use locations; the chain’s output component must specify how and where discard occurs (links [[refuse-types]]).  
- **Extension to n-transforms:** chains may be extended through noncultural processes that eliminate, modify, or redeposit elements, supporting simulation of pathways from past activity to observed provenience (attributed).  
- **Inference principles:** three principles in Ch. 5 link chain structure to archaeological pattern prediction when combined with correlates and c-/n-transforms (see [[formation-processes]]).

## Applications

Formation-process modeling; ethnoarchaeology of discard and reuse; ceramic and lithic life-history analysis; program-level teaching of why spatial association ≠ activity association. Complements [[systemic-context]] / [[archaeological-context]] by specifying *which* activities produce which outputs.

## Critiques and Limitations

Chains are etic analytic constructs — completeness depends on ethnographic or experimental knowledge that is often thin. Residual assignment of unexplained formal variation to socio-/ideo-function without strong techno-function correlates is a standing risk Schiffer himself flagged in later technological-change work (attributed, Schiffer & Skibo 1987 / 1995 Ch. 22). Not a substitute for deposit-level identification of formation processes before social inference.
