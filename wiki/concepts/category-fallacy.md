# Category Fallacy

[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s term for the central methodological error of cross-cultural
psychiatry: taking a diagnostic category built for one culture and imposing it on another where it
does not fit. Coined in his 1977 paper and restated canonically in
*[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking Psychiatry]]* (1988).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman's definition (verbatim, attributed):

> "The reification of one culture's diagnostic categories and their projection onto patients in
> another culture, where those categories lack coherence and their validity has not been established,
> is a category fallacy (Kleinman 1977)."

The error is not mere inaccuracy but *incoherence*: the category may be reliably scored yet measure
nothing locally real. It is the precise inverse of the [[cross-cultural-psychiatry|reliability-is-not-validity]]
point — high inter-rater reliability on a translated instrument can mask a category fallacy.

## Semantic History

Coined in 1977, before Kleinman's major books, and carried through
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (where the [[neurasthenia]] material
raises it without naming it) into *Rethinking Psychiatry*, where it is defined and worked out. It has
become a standard term in cultural psychiatry and underwrites the DSM's later cultural-formulation
apparatus.

## Operationalizations

Kleinman dramatizes the fallacy with **Obeyesekere's reverse example**: imagine a South Asian
psychiatrist who operationalizes "semen-loss syndrome" (a coherent illness category in South Asia),
translates and back-translates the instrument into English, trains American psychiatrists to score it
with high reliability, and thereby derives a US "prevalence" of semen loss — a disorder with "neither
folk nor professional coherence" in America. Kleinman notes real cross-cultural psychiatry runs the
same procedure in the opposite direction: Western instruments applied in the non-Western world.

His central worked case is **depression**: patients with malaria, hookworm anemia, chronic diarrheal
disease, and the dysphoria of poverty can be scored, symptom by symptom, as meeting DSM-III/ICD-9
criteria for major depression, producing "both a distorted view of pathology and an inappropriate use
of diagnostic categories." He argues *dysthymic disorder* may itself be a category fallacy when
exported.

## Applications

- **Diagnostic non-equivalence:** American Indian mourning signs (prolonged grief, hearing the dead)
  are normative, not depressive; Japanese *taijinkyofusho* (fear of embarrassing others) is not
  DSM social phobia (fear of others' judgment); anorexia nervosa and agoraphobia may be Western
  culture-bound categories that don't export.
- **Diagnostic-system incommensurability:** DSM-III's six-month duration criterion for schizophrenia
  vs. ICD-9's absence of one yields "incommensurate findings" cross-culturally.

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman guards the concept against over-reading into blanket relativism: he holds that "certain
psychiatric diagnoses are valid worldwide — organic brain disorders, schizophrenia, manic-depressive
psychosis, certain anxiety disorders, and perhaps major depression," while others (dysthymia,
anorexia, agoraphobia, personality disorders) are culturally contingent. The category fallacy is thus
a caution against *unvalidated* projection, not a claim that all categories are cultural artifacts —
the opposite overcorrection (radical culturalism, "a total system that explains everything and thus
nothing") is a distinct error he equally rejects (see [[cross-cultural-psychiatry]] and
[[psychiatric-universalism-relativism-debate]]).
