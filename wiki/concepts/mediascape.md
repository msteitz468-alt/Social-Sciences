# Mediascape

One of [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s five [[global-cultural-flows|-scapes]];
with [[ideoscape]] it forms the pair of "landscapes of images." Attributed to
Appadurai throughout.

## Definition as Coined

"Mediascapes refer both to the distribution of the electronic capabilities to
produce and disseminate information (newspapers, magazines, television stations,
and film-production studios) … and to the images of the world created by these
media" *(Appadurai 1996)*. Mediascapes are "image-centered, narrative-based
accounts of strips of reality"; what they offer viewers is "a series of elements
(such as characters, plots, and textual forms) out of which scripts can be formed
of imagined lives."

## Semantic History

Extends [[imagination-as-social-practice|the argument]] that mass media have made
imagination a collective social practice. Appadurai argues that the farther
audiences are from direct metropolitan experience, "the more likely they are to
construct imagined worlds that are chimerical, aesthetic, even fantastic." The
scripts furnished by mediascapes "get disaggregated into complex sets of
metaphors by which people live" (invoking Lakoff and Johnson). The term is now
standard in media and communication studies.

## Operationalizations

Traced through television, film, cassette, and print repertoires; the
[[vernacular-globalization|indigenization of cricket]] via All-India Radio,
televised matches, and vernacular cricket magazines (Ch. 5) is Appadurai's fullest
worked case of a mediascape at work.

## Applications

The blurring of realistic and fictional landscapes; the production of
"deterritorialized viewers" and "diasporic public spheres" (Turkish films in
Germany, satellite Olympics for Koreans in Philadelphia, cassette sermons for
Pakistani cabdrivers in Chicago). Feeds [[deterritorialization]] and the
homogenization/heterogenization argument on [[globalization]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Like the other scapes, descriptively rich but hard to operationalize as a
variable; treats media reception optimistically as a site of "agency," which
media-effects and political-economy critics contest.
