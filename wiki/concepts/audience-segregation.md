# Audience Segregation

## Definition as Coined

Citing William James on multiple social selves, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (*[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, 1956) argued individuals foster the impression that the current routine is their only or most essential one; **audience segregation** ensures that those before whom one plays one part will not be the same individuals before whom one plays a different part in another setting (attributed).

Audiences often **resist** breaking segregation — saving time and emotional energy by treating the performer at occupational face-value. Urban life would be "unbearably sticky," Goffman suggested, if every contact shared personal trials (attributed).

Segregation also appears as **scheduling** and spatial control so contradictory performances do not collide; as **periodic audience change** to prevent performer over-attachment (dramaturgical loyalty technique); and as control of outsiders' access to front and back regions.

## Semantic History

From Jamesian multiple selves through Goffman to "context collapse" in social media (boyd et al. — not yet ingested): the failure of segregation when multiple audiences co-exist in one channel.

## Operationalizations

Map audience sets per role; note breaches (running into a client at a party); digital audience co-presence metrics.

## Applications

Occupational vs domestic roles; class and status performances; service work with rotating clienteles. Links: [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]], [[the-social-self]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Assumes capacity to separate audiences — less available to those under surveillance or in small communities (Goffman's own island illustrations show tighter information conditions).
