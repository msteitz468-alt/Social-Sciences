# Intersectionality

## Definition as Coined

The analytic claim that systems of race, social class, gender, sexuality, ethnicity,
nation, and age form **mutually constructing** features of social organization, which
shape — and are shaped by — the experiences of those located at their intersections. The
term was coined by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989/1991);
[[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]] developed the parallel sociological framework in
*[[collins-black-feminist-thought-2000|Black Feminist Thought]]*, where she credits the
shift to 1980s Black feminist scholar-activism (Angela Davis 1981; the Combahee River
Collective's "A Black Feminist Statement" 1982; Audre Lorde 1984).

Collins draws a precise distinction (attributed): *intersectionality* names **which**
oppressions interlock in a given case ("intersections of race and gender, or of sexuality
and nation"), while the [[matrix-of-domination]] names **how** those intersecting
oppressions are organized. Intersectional paradigms, she argues, "remind us that
oppression cannot be reduced to one fundamental type, and that oppressions work together
in producing injustice."

## Semantic History

Collins's own vocabulary shifted from "interlocking systems" and "race, class, and gender
studies" (1st edition, 1990) toward "intersecting oppressions" (2nd edition, 2000). The
2nd edition also **widened the set of axes**: where the paradigm was originally
race–class–gender, Collins adds **sexuality** (Ch. 6) and **nation** (Ch. 10) as further
mutually constructing systems, arguing that intersectional analysis "will remain hindered"
until nation is incorporated. The concept has since migrated far beyond its Black-feminist
origins into a general framework across sociology, law, education, and public policy.

## Operationalizations

Different traditions operationalize intersectionality differently, and the measurements
disagree. Collins pairs it with the [[matrix-of-domination]] and its four **domains of
power** (structural, disciplinary, hegemonic, interpersonal). West and Fenstermaker's
ethnomethodological program recasts "difference" as an ongoing interactional
accomplishment ([[doing-difference]]) rather than a matrix of structures — a contrast that
became the [[doing-difference-debate]]. Quantitative researchers operationalize it as
statistical interaction between demographic categories, which Collins and other structural
theorists regard as an **additive residue** the concept was meant to escape.

## Applications

Across *Black Feminist Thought*, Collins applies intersectionality to work and family
(Ch. 3), [[controlling-images]] (Ch. 4), the sexual politics of Black womanhood (Ch. 6 —
"sexuality as conceptual glue" binding intersecting oppressions), love and motherhood
(Chs. 7–8), and the transnational frame (Ch. 10). She stresses that a matrix of domination
"contains few pure victims or oppressors": a heterosexual Black woman oppressed by race and
gender may still hold heterosexual privilege over Black lesbians.

## Critiques and Limitations

(Attributed positions.) Critics charge that operationalizations often smuggle back the
additive model the concept rejects; that the metaphor of "intersection" describes rather
than explains; and — per the [[doing-difference-debate]] — that some formulations foreground
"difference without power." Collins's own counter-critique targets analyses that rank
oppressions (see [[standpoint-theory]] and [[black-feminist-epistemology]]): quantifying
who is "most oppressed," she argues, ironically reproduces positivist criteria.
