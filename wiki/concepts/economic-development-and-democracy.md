# Economic Development and Democracy

## Definition as Coined

[[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Ch II) advanced one of the most cited mid-century theses in comparative politics: **the more well-to-do a nation, the greater the chances that it will sustain democracy**. Economic development — wealth, industrialization, urbanization, education — was argued to shape class structure, civil society, and political culture in ways favorable to democratic stability. Rapid development without corresponding institutions, he warned, could intensify class struggle and extremism.

## Semantic History

The chapter became a founding statement of empirical [[modernization-theory]] in political sociology. Later democratization research (Przeworski and others; not yet ingested) reanalyzed the correlation as survival of democracy given development vs transition to democracy — a major reinterpretation. Dependency and historical-institutionalist critics challenged linear development–democracy models.

## Operationalizations

Lipset used comparative tables of GNP/income, communications, education, urbanization against classifications of European and Latin American regimes (democracies, unstable democracies, dictatorships). Methodological appendix discusses index construction and validity. Association ≠ automatic causation in careful readings; popular reception often simplified to "wealth causes democracy."

## Applications

- Conditions of the democratic order (*Political Man* Part I).
- Politics of rapid economic development and new states.
- Link to middle-class size and moderation (Aristotelian motif in Lipset's framing).

## Critiques and Limitations

Live debate: [[economic-development-democracy-debate]]. Issues include causation vs selection, authoritarian high-growth cases, resource curses, sequencing, and Western coding of "democracy." Hold as **attributed, reanalyzed** thesis.
