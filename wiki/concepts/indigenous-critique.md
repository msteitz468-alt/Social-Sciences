# Indigenous Critique

## Definition as Coined

In *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* (2021), [[graeber-david|Graeber]] and [[wengrow-david|Wengrow]] use **indigenous critique** for (attributed):

1. Taking seriously **social thought from outside the European canon**, especially Indigenous American interlocutors whom Western philosophers cast as history's angels or devils — roles that preclude real intellectual exchange.
2. The specific body of arguments by which Indigenous commentators (paradigmatically the Wendat statesman **Kandiaronk**, as mediated especially through Lahontan's *Dialogues*) criticized European inequality, greed, punitive criminal law, Christianity, and lack of freedom.
3. A historiographic thesis: that critique **influenced** Enlightenment equality/freedom discourse and was later **neutralized** by the invention of the standard meta-narrative of ambivalent civilizational progress and by stage theories of social evolution that sorted Indigenous peoples by food-getting mode and banished them from equal interlocution about the present.

They locate the origins of Enlightenment-style critique of European society "not with the philosophers of the Enlightenment… but with indigenous commentators… such as the Native American (Huron-Wendat) statesman Kandiaronk" (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Layer | Content |
|---|---|
| **Emic / historical** | 17th–18th c. Indigenous diplomatic, council, and conversational critiques as reported in Jesuit relations, travel literature, and Lahontan — mediated, translated, literary |
| **Etic 2021** | G&W's naming binds those materials to a thesis about the birth of the Left and against purely internal European auto-critique |
| **Reception** | Early modernists contest Lahontan authenticity and degree of influence — [[dawn-of-everything-debate]] |

Related: G&W's use of Bateson's [[schismogenesis]] for cultural contact and self-definition against the other (money-focused Wendat critique of Europeans; Sahlins on traditions framed against "living in the way of money").

**Kandiaronk (as G&W present him):** key strategist / Speaker of the Wendat Confederacy ("the muskrat," "Le Rat"); opponent of Christianity; famed orator; likely ambassador context to Louis XIV's court 1691; debated at Frontenac's table. Against sock-puppet readings of Lahontan's Adario, G&W follow scholars such as Barbara Alice Mann that basic arguments are Kandiaronk's (attributed position).

## Operationalizations

Historiographic argument chain, not a measurement construct: presence of Indigenous critical discourse → influence on *philosophes* → Turgot-style material stages and Rousseauan compromise as reply → evolutionary ladder → "egalitarian" as emptied ancestral residual.

## Applications

- *Dawn* ch. 2 ("Wicked Liberty") and ch. 11 ("Full Circle") return.
- Frames the book's refusal of Rousseau as sole origin of equality talk.
- Links to claim that theories of [[social-evolutionism|social evolution]] were first articulated in Europe **as a direct response** to the power of indigenous critique (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Source-critical problems of Lahontan and colonial mediation (Position 3 on [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]).
- Risk of reversing the noble-savage trope into a flattened "Indigenous philosopher" figure.
- Even if influence claims hold, they do not validate G&W's prehistoric reconstructions.
