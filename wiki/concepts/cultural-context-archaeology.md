# Cultural Context (Archaeological)

## Definition as Coined

In [[taylor-walter-w|Walter W. Taylor]]'s *[[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948|A Study of Archeology]]* (1948), an archaeological **cultural context** is a presentation (narrative or synoptic) of the **life-ways** of former site occupants, constructed by inference from material results of cultural behavior and the affinities among them (and with environment). It is **not** an aggregate of empirical categories (Stone, Bone, Copper objects, Environment treated as separate units). Taylor contrasts empirical headings with cultural headings (Food, Dress, Hunting, Textile Industry, Transportation, etc.) and states that headings such as "Artifacts of Wood/Stone" have **no place** in a cultural context.

Related: the **[[conjunctive-approach|conjunctive approach]]** is the program whose primary goal is building such contexts via cultural conjunctives. Taylor equates writing history (in the strong sense) with constructing cultural contexts with due regard for time — not merely arranging phenomena in temporal sequence.

## Semantic History

- **Historiography:** "context" as integration of facts into a picture of past actuality (Taylor's reading of modern historiography).
- **Taylor 1948:** technical demand on archaeological reporting against trait-list culture-history.
- **Later archaeology:** "context" becomes everyday excavation vocabulary (findspot, depositional context) **and** interpretive cultural reconstruction — do not collapse the two without noting which sense is meant.

## Operationalizations

Taylor: affinities quantitative/qualitative/spatial; quantification; provenience; labeled inference tested against further data. Modern: single-context recording, formation processes, etc., are related but not identical programs — link rather than equate.

## Applications

Load-bearing for evaluating whether a site report is "chronicle" vs historiography in Taylor's terms; critique of reducing [[archaeological-culture]] to trait lists; standard for the conjunctive approach's report outline.

## Critiques and Limitations

Inference-heavy (Taylor embraces this); data demands high; risk of ethnographic presentism when filling life-ways from sparse material; later formation-process critique reframes what "context" can securely mean (as of Schiffer and successors — separate sources).
