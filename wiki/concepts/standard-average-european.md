# Standard Average European (SAE)

## Definition as Coined

[[whorf-benjamin-lee|Benjamin Lee Whorf]] coined **Standard Average European (SAE)** for the typological cluster of Western European languages (English, French, German, Latin-derived, etc.) that, he argued, share a common set of grammatical and semantic habits: objectified **time** (as a continuum measurable in units), **mass/substance** nouns, binary **number**, and subject–predicate sentence form. SAE is the foil against which Hopi and other non-SAE systems are compared in "The Relation of Habitual Thought and Behavior to Language" (1939) and related essays in *[[whorf-language-thought-reality-1956|Language, Thought, and Reality]]*. The concept is **attributed** to Whorf; it is not a wiki-voice genealogical classification of languages.

## Semantic History

In Whorf's usage SAE is a **contrastive analytic construct**, not a genetic family (it cuts across Indo-European branches and excludes many European languages he would not treat as "average" in the same way). Later textbook and critical literature sometimes reifies SAE as if Whorf claimed all Europeans think identically — a flattening he did not need for the contrast. Neo-Whorfian and typological work may use "SAE" or related areal notions (e.g. Haspelmath's SAE features) under different operationalizations; those are later developments not primary here.

## Operationalizations

Whorf operationalizes SAE via recurring grammatical–semantic patterns in examples drawn mainly from English (with allusions to other European languages): pluralization and objectification of time words; substance and form in mass nouns; three-tense systems; subject–predicate logic. Hopi is the main contrast case (event/process orientation; different handling of time and duration).

## Applications

- Core of [[linguistic-relativity]] empirical illustrations in LTR
- Anchor of [[linguistic-relativity-debate]] (what exactly is being contrasted)
- Related to [[linguistic-typology]] as a *conceptual* (not genetic) grouping

## Critiques and Limitations

Critiques (as positions): SAE over-homogenizes Europe; Hopi contrast may be overstated; the construct is theory-laden (built to support relativity). Whorf used it as a working average for "our" languages, not a dialect atlas.
