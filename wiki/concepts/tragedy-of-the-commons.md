# Tragedy of the Commons

*(Attributed — Hardin 1968 as foil in [[ostrom-elinor|Ostrom]] 1990.)* The claim that common resources are doomed to overexploitation because each rational user gains the full benefit of extra use while sharing the cost of degradation — often used to prescribe **privatization** or **state control**.

## Definition as Coined

Hardin’s parable (pasture open to all) frames free access as inevitable ruin. Ostrom (*[[ostrom-governing-the-commons-1990|Governing the Commons]]*) treats it as one of three influential models (with prisoner’s dilemma and Olson’s collective-action logic) that became **policy metaphors** over-applied beyond their assumptions.

## Semantic History

Hardin 1968; Ostrom and others reframe “commons” as open-access vs common-property institutions; design principles for self-governance challenge inevitability.

## Operationalizations

Game models (PD); experimental CPRs; field cases of overgrazing/overfishing under open access vs regulated common property.

## Applications

Environmental policy rhetoric; fisheries; climate as “global commons.”

## Critiques and Limitations

Ostrom: metaphor can be harmful when it forecloses institutional alternatives already working; ignores nested rules and monitoring (see [[commons-governance-theory]]).
