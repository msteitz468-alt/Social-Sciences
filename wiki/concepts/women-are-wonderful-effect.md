# Women Are Wonderful Effect

The **women are wonderful effect** (Eagly) is the finding that the stereotype of women is evaluated as more **positive/“good”** than the stereotype of men, even while men’s stereotypic traits are rated **higher in status and power**. Ridgeway (2011) uses it to separate **liking** from **status** in gender beliefs.

## Definition as Coined

As Ridgeway reports (Eagly and Mladinic 1989, 1994): women’s stereotype scores higher on evaluative goodness; yet Glick et al. (2004) and Rudman et al. (2009) show men’s traits rated substantially higher on power/status — women’s traits roughly half as high on power/status scales in Glick’s student samples (as cited). Communal “wonderful” traits do not cancel diffuse male status advantage.

## Semantic History

Part of stereotype-content and social-role research; Ridgeway folds it into [[status-beliefs]] so that benevolent-seeming stereotypes still organize hierarchy.

## Operationalizations

Trait evaluation scales; separate ratings of warmth/goodness vs competence/status/power.

## Applications

Explains why “positive” views of women can coexist with inequality; caution against reading attitude positivity as status equality.

## Critiques and Limitations

Sample skew (students); cross-cultural variation in “wonderful” content; intersectional groups (e.g., “housewives” low status in Fiske et al. as Ridgeway notes in Ch. 1).
