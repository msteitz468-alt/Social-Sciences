# Societas and Civitas

## Definition as Coined

[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] (attributed, *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]*, Part II) argues that "the experience of mankind … has developed but two plans of government." The first and most ancient is a **social organization** (Latin *societas*), "founded upon gentes, phratries and tribes," in which "the government dealt with persons through their relations to a gens and tribe." The second and later is a **political organization** (*civitas*), "founded upon territory and upon property," in which "the government dealt with persons through their relations to territory" — the township, county, and state. Morgan's thesis: "It is impossible to found a political society or a state upon gentes … A state must rest upon territory and not upon persons, upon the township as the unit of a political system, and not upon the gens which is the unit of a social system."

## Semantic History

Morgan takes the Latin pair to name a distinction he claims is universal in ancient history. The organic series of *societas* is [[gens]] → phratry → tribe → confederacy (or, via coalescence on one territory, nation); the series of *civitas* is township/ward → county → state → nation. The concept is the political counterpart of his [[savagery-barbarism-civilization|ethnical-periods]] scheme and the culmination of Part II ("Growth of the Idea of Government"). It fed directly into [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]'s account of the origin of the state in *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State]]*.

## Operationalizations

The transition is dated to specific constitutional reforms Morgan reads as the invention of the state within the Aryan family:
- **Athens / Cleisthenes (509 BCE)** — Attica divided into ~100 demes (townships) with local self-government; citizenship by registration in a deme, not connection to a gens. Morgan calls Cleisthenes "the founder of the second great plan of human government." (Preceded by the failed Theseus coalescence, Draco's code, and Solon's property classes.)
- **Rome / Servius Tullius** — property classes and the *comitia centuriata* replacing the gentile *comitia curiata*; four city wards with a property census. Morgan judges Rome's outcome incomplete ("rested more upon property than upon territory"), yielding a mixed aristocratic-democratic government rather than Athens's fuller democracy.

Morgan presents the modern administrative hierarchy (township–county–state–nation; commune–department–republic; parish–shire–kingdom) as the lineal descendant of the Cleisthenic series.

## Applications

Organizes Morgan's comparative political history (Iroquois and Aztec *societas*; Greek and Roman transition to *civitas*); underpins his claim that gentile government was a [[military-democracy]], never a monarchy; and is the hinge on which [[private-property]] becomes, for Morgan, the prime mover of state formation.

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes` — the person-to-territory shift is a real and durable observation about state formation, but Morgan's framing embeds it in a discredited unilineal stage theory (see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]) and treats a single Athens/Rome pathway as the universal route to the state. The claim that "a state cannot be founded upon gentes" is asserted in Morgan's attributed voice, not established as law. Later political anthropology recognizes multiple pathways to state formation and kin-based states.
