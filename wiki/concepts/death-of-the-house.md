# Death of the House

## Definition as Coined

In *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998, Ch. 3), [[bradley-richard|Richard Bradley]] argues that two major Early Neolithic monument traditions — **[[long-mound|long mounds]]** and **enclosures** — take their prototypes from the **settlements of the [[linear-pottery-culture|Linear Pottery Culture (LBK)]]** on the loess, specifically from the **symbolic life-cycle of the house**. Houses were often abandoned while still structurally sound and replaced nearby as the original building decayed; occasionally groups of abandoned houses were enclosed by a ditch. Bradley proposes this pattern relates to the **social lifespan of the household** (e.g. relocation after a death / pollution concerns) rather than mere structural failure — the "**death of the house**" that is later monumentalized as the long-mound and enclosure traditions.

## Semantic History

Builds on long-standing morphological comparisons between long barrows and longhouses (Childe 1949 tradition; Hodder’s list of connections; plan comparisons such as Olszanica vs Kilham long barrow; Brześć Kujawski longhouses vs Sarnowo long barrows). Prior authors noted resemblance; Bradley’s move is to explain *why* domestic forms become funerary/ceremonial: everyday experience of settlements full of decaying "houses of the dead" among living houses made earthwork mounds easy to conceptualize — initially by **imitating the results of that natural process**. When Polish settlements shift to overlapping rebuilds on the same spots, decay-in-place memoriality fails → specialized long barrows as houses of the dead in cemetery layouts recalling whole settlements.

Competing origin stories Bradley engages: [[sherratt-andrew|Sherratt]] (1990, 1995) and [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] (1990 *Domestication of Europe*) offer different paths from ideology and conversion rhetoric; Bradley stresses **agency from household routines** rather than abstract *domus* alone or surplus instrumentality.

## Operationalizations

- House use-lives ~20–27 years despite massive construction; little repair/fire destruction/timber salvage → abandonment while sound (LBK settlement archaeology Bradley cites).
- Micro-sequences of house replacement (e.g. Langweiler 9); multi-phase sites (Köln-Lindenthal, Bylany).
- Enclosure of empty former-house space in late settlement phases.
- Complementary distributions of longhouses / long mounds / megaliths (secure longhouse–long-barrow coincidence mainly Kujavia).
- Chronology: long mounds often later than loess LBK climax; associated mainly with TRB/Michelsberg-type horizons in the fourth millennium BC (as of Bradley’s 1990s sources).

## Applications

Explains morphological kinship between LBK longhouses and north-west European long barrows without equating culture labels across regions (use `associated_with` / analogy carefully). Feeds [[monumentality]], Early Neolithic [[ritual-landscape]], and [[social-memory]] (abandoned houses as household history; mound cemeteries as settlement memory). Enclosure path: dead-house zones → freestanding ritual enclosures metaphorizing past communities (preview of [[causewayed-enclosure]] specialization).

## Critiques and Limitations

Ethnographic bridging (household death → house abandonment) is interpretive; LBK regional diversity is high; later Bayesian 14C and aDNA may re-phase sequences Bradley used (as of 1998 literature). Hold as **attributed thesis**, not wiki-voice law. Does not claim every long mound is a literal house conversion (contrast Glob’s late Barkaer reading).
