# Fractionation of Religion

## Definition as Coined

In White's (2021) synthesis of [[cognitive-science-of-religion|CSR]], **fractionation** is the bottom-up strategy of refusing a single essential definition of religion and instead dividing religious systems into smaller, recurrent components (e.g., supernatural agents, afterlife continuity, ritualized action, moralizing gods, misfortune explanations) that can each be linked to cognitive predispositions and cultural ecology.

## Semantic History

Builds on Wittgensteinian family-resemblance critiques of "religion" as a natural kind and on Boyer's program of explaining packages of religious concepts rather than "Religion" as a whole. Contrasts with top-down essential definitions (Tylor's [[animism|belief in spiritual beings]]; Durkheim's sacred/profane; Geertz's cultural system).

## Operationalizations

Topic lists and experimental paradigms target fractionated units (MCI agents; teleological world-reasoning; afterlife dualism; ritual form; costly ritual), not whole traditions as comparison units (White ch. 2–4).

## Applications

Structures White's textbook chapters 5–10; enables cross-tradition comparison without equating Islam/Buddhism/Christianity as bounded organisms.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue fractionation dissolves the lived unity of religious worlds and privileges lab-tractable pieces over meaning systems. CSR replies that unity can be recomposed after mechanisms are specified ([[csr-and-interpretivism-debate]]).
