# Provenience

**Provenience** is the **horizontal and vertical position** of a find within its immediate matrix. In Renfrew & Bahn 2012 it is one of three components of a find’s [[archaeological-context|context]], alongside **matrix** (surrounding material, usually sediment) and **association** (co-occurrence with other remains, usually in the same matrix). Without provenience, an object is largely severed from archaeological inference — the central reason looting and unprovenanced collecting destroy knowledge.

> **Spelling note:** *Provenience* is the standard North American archaeological spelling for findspot coordinates; *provenance* is often used in art-history and museum contexts for ownership history. Renfrew & Bahn use **provenience** in the field-recording sense. Both senses matter for heritage ethics.

## Definition as Coined

Textbook definition (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 2, attributed):

> A find’s context consists of its immediate **matrix** (the material surrounding it, usually some sort of sediment such as gravel, sand, or clay), its **provenience** (horizontal and vertical position within the matrix), and its **association** with other finds (occurrence together with other archaeological remains, usually in the same matrix).

Understanding context — whether [[artifact]], [[archaeological-feature|feature]], structure, or organic remain — is “crucially important” for reconstructing past activity at a [[archaeological-site|site]].

## Semantic History

- **19th c.:** Sealed associations (tools with extinct fauna) establish high antiquity — provenience-in-matrix as scientific warrant.
- **Stratigraphic excavation:** Vertical provenience becomes the backbone of sequence; horizontal provenience maps activity.
- **Total recording ideals:** Catalog numbers, notebooks, measured drawings, photography, digital media fix provenience before and as finds are lifted (Ch. 3).
- **Survey stage:** Individual provenience mapping may be skipped if secondary contexts are badly disturbed or finds too numerous — then sampling; total collection possible on small sites with resources.
- **Illicit market:** Unlabeled provenience on display often signals illicit antiquities; “unknown provenience” also flags fakes (Getty kouros case in textbook).
- **CRM / public archaeology:** Legal export proof and PAS-style reporting aim to keep portable finds tied to findspot data.

## Operationalizations

| Recording mode | Provenience expression |
|---|---|
| Grid excavation | Square, spit/level, depth, bag number |
| Single-context | Context number + 3D coordinates |
| Total station / GPS | X,Y,Z (lat/long or UTM); sometimes ~3 cm accuracy |
| Surface survey | Piece-plot or bulk by collection unit |
| Underwater / remote | Instrument fixes + depth |
| Museum / market | Findspot (if known) vs ownership **provenance** chain |

Primary context = material as originally deposited; secondary context = shifted by looters, other disturbance, or natural forces (Paleolithic tools in river gravels routinely secondary).

## Applications

- Demonstrating contemporaneity via association in undisturbed primary context.
- Reconstructing activity areas, depositional events, and formation processes.
- Sampling design for ecofacts and dating materials.
- Legal and ethical evaluation of collections (NAGPRA inventories; museum accession).
- Research value of metal-detector finds when findspot is reported (PAS; Frome hoard).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Looting destroys provenience** with matrix and association — irreversible (Renfrew & Bahn: tragedy of indiscriminate digging for rich finds; Mimbres bowls as case of permanent cultural-historical loss).
- Secondary contexts can still yield information if recognized as secondary; misreading them as primary corrupts reconstruction.
- Ultra-precise coordinates cannot redeem poor stratigraphic understanding or mixed fills.
- Cost and time: total piece-plotting is not always feasible; sampling trades resolution for coverage.
- Confusion of provenience (findspot) with provenance (ownership) muddles scholarship and restitution claims.
