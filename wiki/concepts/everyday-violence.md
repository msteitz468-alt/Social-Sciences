# Everyday Violence

## Definition as Coined

**Everyday violence** is [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]'s term, given its fullest
development in *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* (1992), for the
routinized, anonymous, taken-for-granted violence embedded in the ordinary institutional life of the
poor — hunger, treatable sickness left untreated, the bureaucratic processing of dead infants, the
"averted gaze" of clinic, church, and state. Her central (attributed) claim is that this mundane
violence is not separate from "extraordinary" political violence (death squads, the *disappeared*,
mutilated bodies) but *continuous* with it and its precondition: "the public routinization of daily
mortifications and little abominations… provided the text and blueprint for what only appeared later
to be aberrant… outbreaks of state violence."

## Semantic History

Scheper-Hughes assembles the concept from several sources: Franco Basaglia's "peacetime crimes"
(*crimini di pace*), [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]]'s "terror as usual," and a Walter Benjamin
epigraph (via Taussig) that "the 'state of emergency'… is not the exception but the rule." It sits
alongside — and is often paired with — Johan Galtung's and [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s
**[[structural-violence]]** and Bourdieu's **[[symbolic-violence]]**, but is distinguished by its focus on the *routinization* of
lethal harm and its explicit thesis linking the ordinary to the exceptional. Scheper-Hughes and
colleagues later anthologized the concept (the "violence continuum") well beyond the Brazilian case.

## Operationalizations

In *Death Without Weeping* the concept is operationalized through: the demographic record of
child death recorded and then not investigated in the *cartório civil* (cause of death left blank
for over three-quarters of registered deaths); the "symbolic violence" of the municipal cemetery,
where paupers' remains went to a common *depósito de ossos* while the wealthy held family vaults;
the medicalization of hunger (see [[nervoso]]); and the documentation of specific disappearances and
death-squad killings (e.g., "Nego De," Sérgio and Jorge Morena) whose victims' relatives dared not
claim the bodies — Elena Morena's verdict, "*Nem donos do corpo deles, eles são*" (they don't even
own their own bodies).

## Applications

- **The Foucault complication.** Scheper-Hughes both shares Foucault's suspicion of the state and
  contests his periodization: in "semi-feudal" Northeast Brazil the "softer" disciplinary apparatus
  (welfare, medical, psychiatric) is not yet in place, so the state still governs through direct
  violence on the body — a limit-case to *Discipline and Punish* (see [[biopower]], [[foucault-michel]]).
- **Rumor as encoded terror.** Following Taussig, she reads organ-theft and child-abduction rumors
  (the "Blue Van") as truthful about power relations even where factually false.
- **Beyond Brazil.** She extends the concept to "advanced" bureaucratic states, citing SWAT-team
  raids and the sanitized "technostrategic" discourse of nuclear weapons — "'Bio-power,' indeed."
- **Adjacent (Biehl).** *[[biehl-vita-study|Vita]]* (2005) documents ordinary authorized dying in
  southern Brazil's informal asylums without systematically deploying Scheper-Hughes's term;
  institutional doping, judicial protection of owners, family "cold business" of abandonment, and
  death without assistance at [[vita-porto-alegre|Vita]] sit in the same continuum as **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones
  of social abandonment]]** and **[[social-death]]** — record as adjacency, not identity of concepts.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's breadth is both its power and its vulnerability: critics ask whether folding hunger,
neglect, bureaucratic indifference, and death squads into a single "violence continuum" dilutes the
analytic edge of "violence" and risks collapsing distinct mechanisms (structural, symbolic, direct)
into one term. As an explicitly politically-committed concept (part of her [[militant-anthropology]]),
it is offered less as a neutral description than as an indictment — its claims are recorded here in
attributed voice.
