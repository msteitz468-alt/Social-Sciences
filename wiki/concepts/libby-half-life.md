# Libby Half-Life

The **Libby half-life** is the half-life of carbon-14 used in the founding radiocarbon age scale: **5568 ± 30 years** in *[[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Radiocarbon Dating]]* (Libby; body text of the 1950s editions). For reporting consistency, dates continued to be published on a rounded **5570-year** value after the accepted physical half-life was revised to **5730 ± 40 years** (Libby 1965 addendum citing Godwin 1962).

## Definition as Coined

Libby and collaborators derived the half-life from laboratory measurements of radiocarbon (mass-spectrometric assays of sample amount plus absolute counting). Chapter III of *Radiocarbon Dating* tabulates earlier bombardment-yield estimates (too high) and later mass-spectrometric values, settling on 5568 ± 30 years for dating calculations (attributed, Libby 1952/55).

## Semantic History

- **1950s body text:** 5568 ± 30 years as the working half-life of the method.
- **1962–65 convention:** physical half-life accepted as 5730 ± 40, but "for reasons of consistency in reporting, radiocarbon dates are reported on the old value of 5570 years" (Libby 1965 addendum; Godwin 1962).
- **Later practice:** "radiocarbon years BP" (present = 1950) remain on the Libby/conventional scale; conversion to calendar years requires **calibration** (tree rings, etc.), not merely rescaling by 5730/5568.

## Operationalizations

Age is computed from the decay law using the adopted half-life (or mean life). A 1% error in half-life appears as a 1% error in absolute age (attributed, Libby ch. III). Wiki and archaeological practice: report which scale is used; never mix uncalibrated Libby-scale BP with calendar BCE without calibration.

## Applications

Every raw date in Libby ch. VI and in early secondary users (e.g. Clark 1957 Star Carr / Stonehenge figures) is on the Libby half-life scale.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Libby half-life is not "wrong" as a convention, but it is not the best physical half-life; more importantly, atmospheric C-14 production was not constant, so even a perfect half-life does not yield calendar ages without calibration. Libby's own 1965 addendum flagged systematic young bias for some Egyptian Old Kingdom samples at 4000–5000 BP.
