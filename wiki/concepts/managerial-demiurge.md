## Definition as Coined

The "managerial demiurge" is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s term for the world-shaping power of
centralized administration in modern society. It means, in Mills's phrase (attributed), more
than a growing proportion of people living by bureaucratic rules: society "becomes an uneasy
interlocking of private and public hierarchies," with management and manipulation of the
population from below and an interlocking directorate above. The "demiurge" (a world-fashioning
craftsman-god) marks the manager as the characteristic power-figure of the [[new-middle-class]]
epoch, replacing the old captain of industry.

## Semantic History

Mills built the concept on Weber's account of [[bureaucracy]] but polemically corrected popular
usage: bureaucracy as "waste and red tape" is "inaccurate and misleading," since most
inefficiency reflects *incomplete* rather than excessive bureaucratization, and public
bureaucracy is largely a response to prior private centralization. The concept belongs to
Mills's larger argument, developed further in *The Power Elite* (1956), about the concentration
and interlocking of command.

## Operationalizations

Mills tracked the demiurge through concentration data (before WWII, 1% of manufacturing plants
employed over half of all wage-earners; ~400 men held a third of the top seats of the 250
largest corporations) and through the bureaucratization of the professions and management. He
identified three converging trends: (I) rationalization of higher as well as lower functions,
diffusing decision into "a vast system for passing the buck"; (II) fetishization of the
enterprise itself (the firm as quasi-moral entity, per Rathenau and Sombart); and (III) the
shift of power from explicit authority to hidden **manipulation**. His analytic triad —
**coercion** (force), **authority** (voluntary obedience), **manipulation** (impersonal,
undisclosed influence) — is a portable tool for the sociology of power.

## Applications

The concept organizes Mills's chapters on management, the professions, and the salaried
intellectual ("Brains, Inc."). It grounds his rejection of the "managerial revolution" thesis
(that managers displaced owners as a ruling class): for Mills, managers are "executors of
property," bureaucratized but socially fused with and answerable to the propertied class.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mills's refutation of the managerial-revolution thesis (Burnham, Berle) is itself a contested
position (see [[new-middle-class-politics-debate]] and the ownership-and-control literature).
"Manipulation," as Mills defines it, is difficult to distinguish empirically from legitimate
authority, and his sweeping "organized irresponsibility" claim generalizes beyond the American
evidence he assembles.
