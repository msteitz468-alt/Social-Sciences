# Complementary Opposition

> Structural process principle of [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary]] / Group B political order, associated with [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] (*The Nuer*, 1940; APS Nuer chapter) and developed for the [[tallensi|Tallensi]] by [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]]. See [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

In the APS Introduction, Fortes and Evans-Pritchard argued that in Group B systems stability is maintained by an **equilibrium at every line of cleavage**: force used by one segment is met by equal force from another; defeat does not found political dominance because there is no administrative machinery to convert victory into rule. Political relations among segments are processes of **fission and fusion** relative to the scale of conflict — the process later labeled complementary (or balanced) opposition.

In the APS Nuer chapter, Evans-Pritchard presented segmentary opposition as the constitution of an “anarchic state”: tribal segments unite against external enemies and divide in internal feuds; lineages map onto territorial political groups as a conceptual skeleton. Among the Tallensi, Fortes analyzed clanship networks and the Talis/Namoos cleavage as structuring conflict and collaboration without a sovereign apex.

## Semantic History

- **1940:** APS + *The Nuer* fix the process model for British Africanist political anthropology.
- **Textbook term “complementary opposition”:** Standard gloss for the Nuer/Tallensi logic in postwar teaching.
- **Critique wave:** Later Africanists and kinship theorists contested how well genealogical models match political practice, how colonial pacification shaped the “ordered anarchy” picture, and whether the model overgeneralized (see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

- Nested territorial and/or lineage segments with **relative** (not absolute) boundaries
- Feud and self-help rather than centralized adjudication
- Ritual mediators (e.g. Nuer leopard-skin chief) without executive power
- Fusion against more distant segments; fission when that opposition ends

## Applications

- [[nuer]] (APS condensation + full *Nuer* monograph)
- [[tallensi]] (Fortes APS chapter)
- Analogous patterns claimed for other acephalous systems (often with weaker evidence)

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested model — treat as attributed analytic framework. Common later challenges (not adjudicated here): ethnographic fit; colonial context of fieldwork; reification of lineage ideology vs practice; Galton-type diffusion of the *model* among anthropologists.
