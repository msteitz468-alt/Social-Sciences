# Turning Point

## Definition as Coined

In [[abbott-andrew|Abbott]]'s formulation (*Comparative Social Research* 16 [1997]: 89–109; *[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]* ch. 8, attributed), a **turning point** is a **narrative concept** (Arthur Danto's sense): it refers to **two points in time**, not one. What makes a turn a turning point rather than a minor ripple is passage of sufficient time on the new course that direction has clearly changed. Formally:

- **Trajectories** — first-level regimes: relatively smooth stretches with inertial / stable causal parameters (where standard regression-style prediction can work *by definition* of a trajectory).
- **Turning points** — second-level, relatively short consequential shifts that **redirect** a process by separating trajectories; only those abrupt turns **succeeded by a new regime** count.

Not all sudden changes are turning points. Continuously: what matters is separation of smooth patterns by a comparatively abrupt turn. Discretely (Markov image): long runs under sticky transitions; rare switches are turning points (near-decomposability à la Simon).

## Semantic History

Vernacular and disciplinary uses long predate Abbott: life-course work (Elder: trajectories and transitions; some events redirect paths; Sampson & Laub on delinquency); political realignment / critical elections; business-cycle turning points; scientific revolution (Kuhn). Sociology's use is “quite old” (Hughes). Abbott's move is to systematize logical/formal properties, distinguish trajectory vs turning point, and press the **hindsight** character that forbids purely prospective algorithms (stock-market analogy).

He also replies to variable-based methodological positions (e.g. Goldthorpe): the real alternative to variable approaches is **pattern-based** analysis of complex particulars, not simply “case studies” (attributed, ch. 8).

## Operationalizations

| Type (Abbott) | Direction of move |
|---|---|
| Focal turning point | Relatively random experience → fixed directional trajectory |
| Randomizing turning point | Stable trajectory → relatively random experience |
| Contingent turning point | Outcome depends on internal event sequence within the turn |

Duration is real: turning points are small relative to surrounding trajectories but have temporal extension; extremely gradual long “turns” should not be called turning points. Identification typically needs a moving window assessing “trajectoriness” vs chaos — and confirmation only after the new trajectory is established.

## Applications

Life course; institutional change; historical sociology; any domain where social structure is imaged as a limited menu of programmed inertial trajectories that actors hook into, with shocks forcing leaps (attributed). Links to structure-as-memory themes in ch. 7.

## Critiques and Limitations

Subjective life-course definitions of turning points as long “processes of course correction” (Hareven & Masaoka et al.) muddle the narrative two-time structure (Abbott's critique, attributed). Methods good at estimating trajectory regimes systematically miss the regime-shifting turns that are causally central — a built-in blind spot of GLR-style modeling (attributed paradox).
