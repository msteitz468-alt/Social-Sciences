# Secret Societies (Melanesian)

## Definition as Coined

In the Melanesian ethnographic literature, **secret societies** are graded male initiatory
associations centred on secret lodges, masked ghost-impersonation, and the buying of rank
through payments of pigs and shell-money. The Banks Islands complex — the graded club-house
society (**Sukwe**) and the bush "ghost" societies (**Tamate**) — was first documented by the
missionary [[codrington-robert-henry|R. H. Codrington]] (*The Melanesians*, 1891) and
re-analysed by [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]; the Bismarck Archipelago analogue is the
**Dukduk/Ingiet** complex. Hutton Webster (*Primitive Secret Societies*, 1908) gave the
comparative theory Rivers built on.

## Semantic History

- **Codrington 1891:** documented the Sukwe (village club, graded ranks seated by rank in the
  *gamal* club-house) and the Tamate (bush "ghost" societies with lodges called *salagoro*,
  masks, and badges protecting members' property) as **two separate institutions**.
- **Rivers 1914:** argued (attributed) they are **structurally linked** — no man may pass
  beyond the Sukwe rank of Tamatsiria without prior initiation into the Tamate liwoa society.
  He catalogued dozens of Banks Islands Tamate societies, each with its own initiation fee,
  croton-leaf badge, mask, and food-taboos, and read the whole complex as the **introduced
  cult of an immigrant people** (the [[kava-and-betel|kava-people]]) practised *secretly*
  precisely where the immigrants were few and weak, and *openly* (as the Solomons' *tindalo*
  ghost-cult) where they were dominant — "the open cult of the Solomons has become the secret
  cult of the Banks Islands." He interpreted initiation as a symbolic **death and rebirth** of
  the candidate.
- Rivers extended the analysis to the Bismarck Archipelago Dukduk/Ingiet, reading the
  paired male/female Dukduk/Tubuan masks (the female Tubuan annually "births" the seasonally
  "dying" Dukduk) as illuminating the otherwise-obscure male/female mask pairing of the Banks
  Tamate, and tying the markedly solar/seasonal Dukduk to a distinct later sun-cult migration.

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by: graded ranks bought with pig/shell-money payments; secret lodges; masks and
mystery sounds (bullroarer, *werewere*); ghost-impersonation; taboo-marks (badges/*soloi*)
protecting property; symbolic-death initiation. Distribution (Rivers): strongest on small
islands (Banks, Torres, Florida), weak/absent on large islands and in the Santa Cruz group —
a pattern Rivers tied to the seclusion that secrecy required.

## Applications

Central to Rivers' diffusionist reconstruction (chapters XXIV, XXXVII of
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|Melanesian Society]]*): the secret societies carry the
[[kava-and-betel|kava-people's]] ghost-cult, [[totemism|totemic]] survivals (the Kwat society's
marine-animal sub-societies), and individual-property norms (the property-protecting taboo)
into an indigenous population Rivers held to be more communistic.

## Critiques and Limitations

The rich *description* of Sukwe/Tamate and Dukduk is Codrington's and Rivers' lasting
contribution; the *diffusionist interpretation* — secret societies as an immigrant cult
practised in secret through numerical weakness — is attributed, contested, and part of the
abandoned migration scheme (see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]). The functionalists
would explain such associations by their present social functions (rank, wealth circulation,
social control) rather than by imagined origin.
