# Potential Affinity

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed throughout — Viveiros de Castro 1992 [1986].)* Viveiros de Castro develops "potential affinity" from the Araweté kin term **tiwã**, glossed as a "middle term" of kinship denoting nonrelatives, potential allies, and cross-cousins — "something between a brother and an enemy" — which he characterizes as marking "an internal exteriority" within the kinship system. Tiwã individuals (of the opposite sex to ego) are potential sexual partners and possible spouses, but the terms reserved for **actual** affines (wife's father, wife's mother, wife's brother — *hati*, *hačo*, *hado'i*) are explicitly excluded from the tiwã category. He states the general regional pattern this exemplifies: "actual affinity is strongly differentiated from potential affinity, a trait characteristic of the Amazonian 'Dravidian group' systems" — i.e., potential affinity is not unique to the Araweté but a structural feature Viveiros de Castro treats as widespread in Amazonian Dravidianate kinship.

The concept's mythological/cosmological extension is central to the book's argument: the Araweté gods (Maï hete) are defined in native terms as **ire tiwã oho**, "our gigantic potential affines." Viveiros de Castro argues that death functions structurally as marriage: "Death is like marriage (indeed it is a marriage): it transforms tiwã into actual affines" — the gods, structurally tiwã ("like enemies") to the living, become actual affines of humanity through the death-cannibalism-marriage complex he elsewhere calls divine cannibalism.

## Semantic History

Potential affinity is a **migrated** concept: it begins as description of an Araweté kin-terminological fact (the tiwã category) and is generalized by Viveiros de Castro, within the same book, into (a) a comparative Amazonianist typological category ("Dravidian group" systems generally distinguish actual from potential affinity) and (b) a cosmological/theoretical category describing the gods' and enemies' structural position relative to humans. The term's later career (outside the ingested source) runs through Viveiros de Castro's broader Amazonianist and perspectivist program, where potential affinity becomes a recurring diagnostic of Amazonian sociality's orientation toward exteriority — that later elaboration is not covered by this source and should be dated separately when ingested.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified in the ingested source. Viveiros de Castro identifies potential affinity through:
- **Terminological analysis**: the tiwã category's formal bifurcation into *hado'i* (brother-in-law, mediated by a forbidden/interposed sister) versus *apïhi-pihã* (formal friend, mediated by a shared spouse) — presented as symmetrical, inverse constructions from what he calls "the same generic ground of pure negative difference." His formal statement: "If a sister is the means of obtaining a wife, a wife is the means of obtaining a friend. If sister exchange equalizes brothers-in-law, the sharing of wives totalizes friends."
- **Marriage-distance rules**: marriage with actual, close cross-relatives (mother's brother's/father's sister's children) is disfavored and improper, while marriage with more distant tiwã is sanctioned and even preferred — a distance principle Viveiros de Castro treats as diagnostic of how potential (distant, "eludable") affinity differs from actual, close affinity, which is proscribed as dangerous.
- **Structural/comparative analysis of the historical Tupinambá** (see [[tupinamba]]) via H. Clastres's work on cannibalism and prophetism.

No survey instrument, census measure, or quantified index is used; the concept is entirely a product of ethnographic/linguistic and structuralist-comparative analysis.

## Applications

- **Eluding Affinity (Ch. 6 §4 of the source).** Viveiros de Castro's central argument is that Araweté society systematically **eludes or refuses affinity** through multiple simultaneous mechanisms: redundant/repeated alliances between the same kindreds, closure of kindreds through endogamy, oblique (avuncular-type) marriages, dispersed reciprocity, nontransitive terminology, and above all the institution of **apïhi-pihã** ("formal friendship") — spouse-sharing between two couples, described as an "anti-affine" bond: "An apïhi-pihã is an anti-affine, a sort of antidote to affinity." Friend relations are drawn from the same tiwã pool as affines but resolve into mutuality and sharing rather than exchange and reciprocity; Viveiros de Castro's summary formula: "the Araweté subordinate complementarity to redundancy, reciprocity to mutuality."
- **Cosmological privilege of anti-affinity.** Anti-affinity (friendship) is projected onto the world of the gods as an ideal condition — "celestial joyfulness" (*iwã tori*) is described as "properly an attribute of the gods, who keep to themselves." Viveiros de Castro explicitly invokes Lévi-Strauss's closing image in *The Elementary Structures of Kinship* — an impossible world of humanity living *entre soi* — as the interpretive frame for this Araweté ideal, marking a direct, critical dialogue with alliance theory: rather than affinity (marriage exchange) being the primary integrative mechanism of society, as in the classical Lévi-Straussian model, he argues Araweté society is organized around **eluding and neutralizing** affinity.
- **The Tupinambá captive as anti-affine.** Extending the concept historically, Viveiros de Castro (following and revising H. Clastres) reads the 16th-century Tupinambá war captive as an "affine sui generis": unlike an ordinary in-marrying man, the captive performs no bride-service, is fed rather than provisioning others, and is himself "future food" — a matrimonial prestation rather than a term of exchange. He frames the captive as "the object of a relationship" and, citing H. Clastres's observation of the semantic identity of "enemy" and "brother-in-law" (*tovaja*) among the Tupinambá, argues that Tupinambá warfare pursued the capture of symbolic "brothers-in-law" rather than of wives directly — a displaced desire for a world without stable affinity that H. Clastres calls a "ritual negation of alliance."
- **The "Anti-Social Contract."** Viveiros de Castro coins this phrase to describe vengeance as the structural principle "that makes enemies more necessary than brothers-in-law" among the Tupinambá — his own formal contribution building on but revising H. Clastres's anti-affinity thesis (see Critiques below).
- **Structural parallel with the Araweté.** He draws an explicit analogy between the Tupinambá "brother-in-law" captive structure and Araweté formal friendship: both, he argues, are forms in which "brother-in-law" status is transformed rather than avoided outright.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **H. Clastres's anti-affinity thesis (named position).** Hélène Clastres (1978) argued that Tupinambá vengeance and prophetism should be read through a "bias of negativity": as a desire for a world without affinity, connected to the Land-without-Evil theme and constituting a "ritual negation of alliance." Viveiros de Castro engages this thesis directly but qualifies it: with Carneiro da Cunha (Carneiro da Cunha & Viveiros de Castro 1985), he argues instead that Tupi-Guarani prophetism was "an affirmation of what remained essential for this society," since prophetic movements suspended work, political authority, and marriage rules but explicitly preserved warfare and cannibalism. This is a live scholarly disagreement between two closely related positions (both attributed), not a settled matter, and is recorded here as a position, not adjudicated.
- **Limits of quantification.** The concept is built entirely from terminological, mythic, and structuralist analysis; no statistical or comparative-sample measure of "potential affinity" as a cross-cultural variable is offered in this source. Any extension beyond the Araweté/Tupinambá comparison to other Amazonian Dravidian systems would require independent verification.
- **Single-fieldworker basis.** The Araweté terminological data underlying the concept rest on Viveiros de Castro's own 1981–83 fieldwork (with a 1988 return visit); the historical Tupinambá extension rests on 16th–17th-century colonial chronicles (Thevet, Léry, Cardim, Monteiro, Staden) read through his and H. Clastres's structuralist frame — an evidence class very different from live kinship-terminological elicitation, and correspondingly more interpretive.
- **Evidence class.** The concept spans ethnographic (kinship terminology, direct fieldwork observation) and textual/archival (colonial chronicles) evidence classes; the two are not independent of each other in Viveiros de Castro's argument — the historical material is read *through* the ethnographic model, not the reverse — a point worth flagging when weighing how much the Tupinambá extension is independently confirmed versus analogically projected.
