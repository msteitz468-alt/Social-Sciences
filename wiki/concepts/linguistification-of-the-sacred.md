# Linguistification of the Sacred

## Definition as Coined

The **linguistification of the sacred** (*Versprachlichung des Sakralen*) is [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s thesis (attributed), in [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|TCA Vol. 2]], that over social evolution "the socially integrative and expressive functions that were at first fulfilled by ritual practice pass over to communicative action; the authority of the holy is gradually replaced by the authority of an achieved consensus." Normative agreement once secured by the [[durkheim-emile|sacred]] and its ritual is progressively transferred to the rationally motivated agreement reached in [[communicative-action|communicative action]].

## Semantic History

The concept is the constructive hinge of Volume 2's Chapter V, fusing [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]. From Durkheim, Habermas takes the derivation of moral authority from the sacred and the collective consciousness; from Mead, the communication-theoretic account of how normatively guided interaction and the "generalized other" arise. His synthesis: as communicative action "takes on its own weight," a linguistification of the sacred necessarily arises, driving three parallel trends — the **rationalization of worldviews** (from concrete gods to general ideas), the **universalization of law and morality**, and **growing individuation**. He illustrates it with the evolution of law: the contract's development "from a ritual formalism into the most important instrument of bourgeois private law" exemplifies a religious consensus "set communicatively aflow."

## Operationalizations

Habermas argues that only a **morality** "developed into a discourse ethics," not science or art, can inherit the integrative authority vacated by the sacred; social integration shifts "from institutionalized values" to "the intersubjective recognition of validity claims raised in speech acts." Normative validity itself splits into the *social* validity of norms de facto recognized and the *ideal* validity of norms that deserve recognition — the ongoing detachment of normative validity from its sacred foundations. Mead's "ideal communication community" is, for Habermas, the idealized end-point projection of this process.

## Applications

The concept is Habermas's account of what the **rationalization of the [[lifeworld]]** consists in — the same trends [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] describes as "secularization" and "institutionalized individualism," and [[weber-max|Weber]] as the disenchantment/rationalization of worldviews. It supplies the normative baseline against which the [[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]] registers as pathology.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Evolutionism / teleology** (attributed to critics): the developmental-logic framing (Piaget/Kohlberg-style) is charged with a residual philosophy of history.
- **Reliance on Durkheim's sociology of religion** (attributed): Habermas himself flags a circularity in Durkheim's derivation (the moral traced to the sacred, the sacred to a collective that is itself a system of norms).
- **Secularization contested** (attributed): the empirical claim that the sacred's functions are progressively assumed by communication is disputed by later sociology of religion.
