# Social Object

## Definition as Coined

In the [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionist]] account systematized by [[blumer-herbert|Blumer]] (*[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]*, 1969), an **object** is anything that can be indicated or referred to. Objects may be physical (a chair, a tree), social (a friend, a president, a student), or abstract (a philosophical doctrine, a moral principle, a ghost). The defining SI claim (attributed to Mead via Blumer): **the nature of an object is constituted by the meaning it has for the person for whom it is an object**, and that meaning arises in social interaction, not merely from intrinsic properties or private psyche.

Thus "social object" here does not mean only "object that is a person"; it means objects as socially meaningful products — including the self as object to itself.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Mead | World of objects arises in social process; self as object to itself |
| Blumer 1969 | Explicit typology and methodological consequence: study meaning, not only stimuli or structural positions |
| Ordinary language / other theories | "Social object" may mean something else — always attribute SI usage |

## Operationalizations

Recover what the object is *for the actor* (and how that is formed and revised in interaction). Designs that treat objects as fixed external stimuli or as pure cultural labels without interactional formation miss the SI object.

## Applications

- Undercuts both naive realism (meaning inherent in the thing) and pure subjectivism (meaning only private mental accretion) — Blumer's second premise locates meaning in interaction.
- Ties to [[self-indication]]: acting toward objects requires designating them to oneself.
- Ties to [[joint-action]]: common objects organize multi-person lines of action.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Material constraints and power to impose definitions can be underplayed if "object as meaning" is taken as exhaustive.
- Distinct from Marxian "objectification" / reification and from [[objective-culture|Simmelian objective culture]] — related family of problems, different machinery; do not collapse.
