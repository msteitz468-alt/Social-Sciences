## Definition as Coined

In [[marx-capital-volume-2-1885]], Marx defines turnover as the circuit of capital "taken not as an isolated act but as a periodic process": "Turnover time... is the period of time that elapses from the moment that the capital value is advanced in a particular form until the return of the capital value in process in the same form." Turnover time (u) is the sum of production time (the duration capital stays in the production sphere) and circulation time (the duration it stays in the circulation sphere). Marx argues these two are mutually exclusive: "During its circulation time, capital does not function as productive capital, and therefore produces neither commodities nor surplus-value" — circulation time is a negative limit on production time and hence on valorization, not (as he argues classical/vulgar political economy mistakenly supposes) an independent source of value.

Marx adopts the year as the conventional unit (U) for measuring turnover, on the grounds that the principal food crops of the temperate zone — where capitalist production originated — are annual products. The number of turnovers per year is n = U/u: a capital with a three-month turnover time completes n = 4 turnovers per year; one with an eighteen-month turnover time completes n = ⅔ turnover per year.

Marx distinguishes production time from working time (labour-process time): production time can exceed working time where the object of labour undergoes a natural or chemical transformation requiring no further labour (corn sown, wine fermenting, tanning), where means of labour are held idle in reserve, or where normal interruptions (nights) occur. He argues capitalist production's structural tendency is to minimize this excess, since valorization occurs only during working time.

## Semantic History

Marx develops turnover analysis using only two of the three circuit forms treated under [[circuit-of-capital]]: Form I (M...M′, money capital) when the focus is surplus-value formation, and Form II (P...P, productive capital) when the focus is product formation. He states Form III (C′...C′, commodity capital) is not usable for turnover analysis of an individual capital, being reserved for the analysis of total social capital in Part Three.

Marx credits the Physiocrats — via Quesnay's *avances primitives* (original advances) and *avances annuelles* (annual advances) — with correctly locating a fixed/circulating-style distinction wholly within productive capital, grounded in how different capital-elements enter and are replaced with the value of the finished product. He extends this into the working-period/production-period vocabulary of Chapters 12–13: the "working period" is the number of successive, interrelated working days needed to complete a finished product in a given branch (a locomotive, roughly three months; a battleship, a year or more; timber, up to 100–150 years), a concept Marx presents as independent of the fixed/circulating capital distinction, though it interacts with it.

A significant historiographical claim recorded in the source: Marx argues that the divergence between the "real" (per-turnover-period) rate of surplus-value and the annual rate — which arises purely from turnover mechanics, not from any change in the degree of exploitation — was historically mistaken by classical economists for an "inexplicable influence deriving from the circulation process" on the rate of surplus-value (and, in its more complicated form, the rate of profit), and that "since the beginning of the 1820s, this phenomenon has led to the complete destruction of the Ricardian school." This is Marx's own characterization of an episode in the history of political economy, presented here in attributed voice.

## Operationalizations

**Annual rate of surplus-value.** Marx defines S′ = s′·n: the ratio of surplus-value produced in a year to the variable capital *advanced* (not the variable capital actually consumed over the year). He works a comparative example: capital A (£500 variable capital, five-week turnover, n=10/year) and capital B (£5,000 variable capital, turning over once in fifty weeks, n≈1/year) apply the same £100/week variable capital, employ labour at the same 100% "real" rate of surplus-value, and thus produce equal annual masses of surplus-value (£5,000 each) — yet A's annual rate of surplus-value is 1,000% while B's is only 100%, because A's smaller capital is recycled through many turnovers while B's larger capital sits advanced for the whole year. The annual rate equals the real rate only where n = 1; it exceeds the real rate where n > 1 and falls short of it where n < 1 (Marx works a 55-week-turnover example yielding S′ = 90 10/11%).

**Money-market effects.** Marx distinguishes the "money" turnover count (counting nominal completed cycles) from the "real" turnover count (crediting only actually-completed turnovers, with partial credit for one in progress at year's end) — in a worked example, £900 of capital "turns over" 5⅔ times by the first reckoning but only 5 4/17 times by the second. He states the general condition under which capital is "set free" by the turnover mechanism (temporarily surplus to a working period's needs) versus merely relieved by a successor capital, concluding that because exact proportionality between working period and circulation period is exceptional, the periodic setting-free of capital is "the rule" for social circulating capital generally, not the exception. Money capital set free this way, together with money released by fixed-capital depreciation reflux, is stated to "form one of the foundations" for the credit system once it develops.

**Fixed-capital renewal and the business cycle.** Marx argues that the multi-year cycle bound up in fixed capital's reproduction time — he estimates roughly a ten-year average lifespan for major branches of large-scale industry as of his writing, while noting this is simultaneously cut short by "moral depreciation" (obsolescence from cheaper or improved replacements, forcing replacement of machinery well before physical exhaustion) — is "one of the material foundations for the periodic cycle" of business through stagnation, moderate activity, over-excitement, and crisis, and that "a crisis is always the starting-point of a large volume of new investment."

## Applications

The working-period analysis (Chapter 12) works through branch-to-branch contrasts: a spinning mill (discrete weekly product) versus a locomotive works (continuous three-month working period) with equal capitals, equal composition, and equal working day — the locomotive-builder must lay out fresh circulating capital weekly for three months before any reflux, requiring roughly twelve times the circulating capital on hand as the spinner despite equal weekly outlays. Marx traces historical consequences: branches with long working periods and large sustained outlay (roads, canals) were historically often built at municipal or state expense rather than capitalistically; house-building shifted, with developed credit and joint-stock finance, from build-to-order (staged client payment) to speculative building on borrowed/mortgaged capital, a transformation he documents via testimony from an 1857 Parliamentary Select Committee on Bank Acts describing much of Belgravia, Tyburnia, and London's suburban villas as built this way.

Chapter 13 (Production Time) extends the analysis to agriculture and forestry: winter grain's roughly nine-month production time is largely uninterrupted by labour; timber-raising, requiring a reserve growing-stock of 10–40 times annual yield and a turnover of up to 150 years, is presented as structurally "unsuited to private and hence to capitalist production." Chapter 14 (Circulation Time) extends the analysis geographically: Marx's example of shipping a commodity to India (four months) and receiving payment (four months) — eight months total even absent selling delay — illustrates distance as a "permanently effective cause" of turnover differentiation, tied to documented reductions in India/China bill-of-exchange "usance" (credit terms) driven by accelerating transport and telegraph speed.

## Critiques and Limitations

The turnover-analysis chapters (15–17) contain a documented editorial dispute between Marx and Engels, relevant to the source-reliability character of the published text. In an editorial footnote signed "F.E.," Engels states that Marx's own manuscript turnover calculations were "incomplete" and "contain many errors and contradictions," that Engels retained only the arithmetically correct tables for publication, and that in Engels's judgment Marx ascribed "undeserved significance" to the phenomenon of money capital being "set free" by the turnover mechanism — Engels argues the more fundamental point is simply that a sum equal to capital I returns to the capitalist at the end of every turnover period regardless of whether it is technically superfluous for the current working period. This is an editorial disagreement over emphasis in reading an unfinished manuscript, not a claim in Marx's own voice, and should be treated as a `source-reliability` note on [[marx-capital-volume-2-1885]] rather than folded silently into Marx's position.

Marx's broader crisis-theory extensions from turnover mechanics — e.g., that long-turnover undertakings like railway construction generate demand pressure followed by "the inevitable crash," and a marginal manuscript note (inserted by Engels for future elaboration) framing periods of maximal capitalist exertion as regularly turning into periods of overproduction because realization of surplus-value is restricted by a majority "always poor and must always remain poor" — are attributed theoretical claims tied to later Marxist debates over underconsumptionist crisis theory, not independently established findings; they are flagged here as candidate seeds for a `debates/` page on Marxian crisis theory, not yet built.
