# Hypersegregation

## Definition as Coined

[[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Denton]] label as **hypersegregation** the condition in which African Americans are highly segregated from whites on **at least four of five** analytically distinct spatial dimensions: **unevenness** (evenness of distribution; typically the [[index-of-dissimilarity]]), **isolation** ([[isolation-index]]), **clustering** (contiguity of black areas into one large agglomeration), **centralization** (proximity to the CBD), and **concentration** (relative amount of physical space occupied). Cutpoint used in the 1980 analysis: any index exceeding **60** counts as "high" (*American Apartheid* Ch 3; *Demography* 1989).

## Semantic History

- **1989:** Massey & Denton, "Hypersegregation in U.S. Metropolitan Areas," *Demography* — technical introduction.
- **1993:** *American Apartheid* popularizes the term in underclass and public-policy debate.
- Later literature (not yet ingested) remeasures with newer censuses and debates cutpoints and dimensionality — date-stamp any update.

## Operationalizations

Five-index profile for metro areas with large black populations; 1980 result: **sixteen** hypersegregated metros (including Chicago, New York, Detroit, Philadelphia, Los Angeles, Atlanta, etc.), housing about **one-third** of all African Americans and ~41% of urban blacks (Massey & Denton 1993 Ch 3, attributed).

## Applications

Comparative racial/ethnic segregation (blacks vs Hispanics/Asians); link from spatial structure to [[urban-underclass]] formation; policy argument that desegregation is necessary to deconcentrate poverty.

## Critiques and Limitations

Arbitrary 60 cutpoint (authors note); ecological/aggregate measures mute household preferences vs discrimination as micro-causes (addressed partly via audits in Ch 4); later temporal change not covered in 1993 text.
