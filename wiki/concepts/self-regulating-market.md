# Self-Regulating Market

## Definition as Coined

For [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]], a market economy is "an economic system controlled, regulated, and directed by markets alone; order in the production and distribution of goods is entrusted to this self-regulating mechanism." More precisely, "it is an economy directed by market prices and nothing but market prices." Polanyi's key claims about it:

- **It is historically unprecedented.** Though markets as an institution had existed "since the later Stone Age," their role "was no more than incidental to economic life"; no economy before the nineteenth century was, even in principle, controlled by markets. The self-regulating market is "an entirely unprecedented" venture, "a complete reversal of the trend of development."
- **It requires the separation of society into an economic and a political sphere.** "A self-regulating market demands nothing less than the institutional separation of society into an economic and political sphere" — a singular departure, since "neither under tribal, nor feudal, nor mercantile conditions was there... a separate economic system in society." (This separation recurs across the book as a load-bearing idea — the market economy *disembeds* the economy; see [[embeddedness]].)
- **It requires that man and nature become commodities.** To organize production through markets, labor and land (and money) must be brought into the market as [[fictitious-commodities|commodities]] subject to supply, demand, and price — even though they are not produced for sale. "To include them in the market mechanism means to subordinate the substance of society itself to the laws of the market."
- **It is a utopia.** Because a self-regulating market "could not exist for any length of time without annihilating the human and natural substance of society," the attempt to institute one necessarily provokes society's protective [[double-movement|countermovement]].

## Semantic History

Polanyi distinguishes the self-regulating market sharply from **markets in general** (ancient and ubiquitous) and from **mercantilism** (heavily regulated; "the very idea of a self-regulating market was absent"). He also reverses the classical developmental sequence: against Adam Smith's individual "propensity to barter" generating local markets and then trade, Polanyi argues that **long-distance trade** (from the geographic dispersion of goods) engendered markets, which only sometimes gave individuals occasion to truck — "markets are not institutions functioning mainly within an economy, but without."

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; identified by whether economic life is directed by prices alone and by the presence of the institutional separation of economy and polity. The "always-embedded" reading (Fred Block) argues a *fully* self-regulating market has never existed and cannot — it is a limiting, utopian ideal.

## Applications

The pivot of *The Great Transformation* and of [[substantivism]]: the self-regulating market's disembedding drive ([[fictitious-commodities]]) provokes the protective [[double-movement]], whose necessity but incompatibility with market self-regulation drives the twentieth-century crisis (see [[fascism-and-market-society]], [[freedom-in-a-complex-society]]). The tension between the market and popular democracy follows from the same institutional separation (the "separation of the economic from the political sphere").

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The "always embedded" objection.** If a fully self-regulating market is impossible, the historical narrative of a onetime "disembedding" needs qualification (Block).
- **Formalist objection.** Formalists deny the sharp market/non-market break the concept rests on (see [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]).
- Polanyi's dating of the market's "maturation" to Industrial-Revolution England, and his reading of mercantilism and of specific episodes ([[speenhamland|Speenhamland]]), have been historically contested.
