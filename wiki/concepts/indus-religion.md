## Definition as Coined

"Indus religion" designates the reconstructed belief and ritual practice of the Indus
Civilization, inferred entirely from material and iconographic evidence in the absence
of any deciphered text (see [[indus-script]]). Possehl (2002) adopts a working
definition of religion as "an institution devoted to the world of deities, the
premise(s) of which are believed to be true by the adherents, but cannot be either
proved or disproved," encompassing afterlife beliefs, mythology, and
astronomy/astrology — an operational definition attributed to Possehl, useful for
scoping the topic but not itself a finding about the Indus Civilization.

## Semantic History

The dominant historical framework, proposed by [[marshall-john]] (1931) and still
treated by Possehl as broadly "sound" in outline though "no longer tenable" in specific
particulars, organizes Indus religion around a "Great Mother Goddess" and a "Great Male
God" dichotomy.

**The Great Mother Goddess** is inferred chiefly from terra-cotta female figurines,
which Marshall classified into three functional types — toys, sympathetic-magic
objects, and mother-goddess cult images — though Possehl notes the evidentiary basis for
the cult-image reading specifically is "not terribly robust." Marshall drew contested
parallels to mother-goddess cults documented in Baluchistan, the Northwest Frontier, and
the Aegean. Possehl's own, more cautious framing is that "female sexuality is deeply
engrained in Indus religion and ideology" — an attributed inference from the material
record in general rather than from any single body of data.

**A proposed Shaktism precursor**: Marshall suggested Indus religion may mark the
historical origin of the later Hindu Shaktist tradition (a sexual dualism, or "duality
in unity," between a god and a consort-energy). Supporting evidence offered consists of
a recurring plant/animal motif pairing (plant imagery read as female, echoing
lotus/genital imagery; animal imagery read as male) together with phallic/baetylic
stones and yoni ring-stones present in the Marshall-era corpus — all attributed to
Marshall's original interpretation.

**Seal 430**, the so-called "Seal of Divine Adoration," depicts a goat, a kneeling human
figure, a tree-enclosed human figure, and a row of seven attending figures below; other
Indus glyptic art shows similar seven-figure processions. Possehl speculatively — and
explicitly hedged, not promoted to wiki voice — links this motif to the Rgveda's "Seven
Sisters" (Sapta Sindhava) hymn (Rgveda VI.61.10–12), naming the seven rivers including
the Indus and Ghaggar-Hakra, judging a Harappan origin for the Vedic motif "at least as
possible" as independent invention by the Vedic poets. F. and B. Allchin (1982) offer an
alternative reading of the seven figures as the seven Rishis (seers) or the "seven
Mothers" of later tradition; A. Parpola offers yet another alternative — children of
Brahma and Sarasvati, or the seven sages of Ursa Major, or the Pleiades. These are three
explicitly rival, non-adjudicated readings of the same iconographic motif.

**Zoolatry and composite animals**: sealings show small animal figures (bull or cow)
carried above processions of figures, suggesting cattle held ideological as well as
economic significance; composite and mythical animals occur widely (three-headed
beasts, entwined tigers, human-torso quadrupeds, horned tigers, trunk-bearing unicorns,
tree-emergent unicorns). C. Grigson (1984) argues the frequently depicted "unicorn"
device is itself wholly mythical rather than a stylized representation of a real animal,
against Marshall, Mackay, and Possehl's own concurring view that the single horn is an
artistic convention for two horns shown in profile, supported by rarer two-horned
"unicorn-type" seals and by terra-cotta bull figurines with a single forehead horn from
Harappa, Chanhu-daro, and Lothal; Possehl concludes "the animal is probably a real bull"
and treats the later classical Greek report of an "Indian unicorn" (Ctesias, physician
at the Achaemenid court, c. 415–398 BCE) as unconnected.

**Mesopotamian iconographic parallels**: a Mohenjo-daro seal shows a half-human,
half-bull female monster attacking a horned tiger, which Possehl parallels to the
Mesopotamian goddess Aruru's creation of the bull-man Enkidu to fight Gilgamesh; a
"Gilgamesh"-type combat motif (a hero fighting flanking animals) also appears on Indus
seals. Both parallels, developed further by A. Parpola (1984), are framed by Possehl as
evidence of Indus–Mesopotamian contact and possible religious or ideological borrowing —
a "reasonable notion at face value," but attributed, not established.

**Water in Harappan ideology**: no direct evidence attests abstract water-sanctity as
such, but the scale of Mohenjo-daro's bathing and drainage infrastructure — the Great
Bath, and near-universal wells, drains, and bathing facilities — is presented (Marshall's
original argument, elaborated by M. Jansen, 1993a) as circumstantial evidence that
bathing carried religious-duty significance; Possehl endorses this as "safe... to
affirm," an inference rather than a direct finding.

**The Chanhu-daro seal 13** (F. R. Allchin's reading) depicts a short-horned bull (Bos
gaurus) above a prostrate human figure. Mackay read the scene as a bull attack or
defense; Allchin instead reads it as a sexual scene — the bull as consort/Heaven, the
exposed female figure signaling a "willing partner" rather than violence — tying the
image to Rgvedic and Atharvavedic Creation-myth imagery of a Heaven-Bull/Earth-female
dualism (citing Vedic epithets such as "Mata bharni"/"Earth Mother" and "Mate
osadhñam"/"mother of plants"). This reading is heavily speculative and attributed to
Allchin alone; Possehl relays it without independent endorsement beyond noting it is
"pregnant with suggestive meaning."

**Religious architecture**: no temples, pyramids, ziggurats, or palaces have been
identified anywhere in the Indus Civilization, in contrast to Egypt and Mesopotamia —
recorded here in wiki voice as a documented negative material fact. The further
interpretive claim that this absence reflects a distinct Harappan "organizing
principle," rather than lesser social or political complexity (explicitly not indicative
of merely chiefdom-level organization, per W. Fairservis 1986), is attributed to
Possehl's own synthesis. The best-attested candidate ritual structure is the Great Bath
at Mohenjo-daro, which Possehl calls the "only convincing example of a ritual structure
in the Mature Harappan," based on its elevation and separation from the Lower Town, its
water-filled construction, and an attached bathing-room annex — his own interpretive
judgment, not a settled finding. Other proposed ritual buildings are, in Possehl's
assessment, unconvincing: a hypothesized "College of Priests" (no corroborating evidence
beyond size and location); a supposed temple beneath the Mohenjo-daro Buddhist stupa
(Wheeler, 1968, calling this "pious hope... entirely unbased"; Jansen's excavation-record
review found no large building beneath it); House I in HR-A (an unusual structure
yielding the Bearded Man and Sad Man sculptures and many seals, but which "cannot be
demonstrated" as religious rather than political, per Jansen 1985); House XXX and House
L in HR-B (massive structures, but no more temple-like than any other large building);
and Block 11 and Block 8A in the DK-G Area (Mackay first termed Block 11 temple-like,
then reclassified it as a "great khan," or hostel — a documented internal reversal by
the same excavator). Claimed fire altars at Rakhigarhi, Kalibangan, Lothal, Vagad,
Nageswar (Gujarat), Navdatoli, and Dangwada carry, per Possehl, "little merit" in most
cases; the Kalibangan case (with an associated proposed ritual structure) is "by far the
strongest," while Nageswar's supposed fire altar is judged "in all likelihood a
regular... updraft kiln."

**Funerary practice diversity**: extended supine burial in pits or coffins is documented
at Cemetery R-37 (Harappa) and the Kalibangan cemetery; fractional burial (excarnation
followed by gathered secondary burial) occurs at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa (Mature Phase)
and at Nal (Early Harappan); evidence for cremation is weak and contested (post-cremation
urns at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa contain mostly pottery and ash, with "almost none of it
human"); a possible Mature Harappan crematory has been reported at Tarkhanwala Dera,
western Rajasthan (Ghosh, 1962). Possehl reads this diversity as consistent with ethnic
or cultural heterogeneity within the Indus population — an attributed inference, not a
direct finding.

Possehl's own explicit interpretive synthesis identifies two master themes: (1) a
regional diversity of religious practice — water-themed emphasis at Mohenjo-daro versus
a possible fire-themed emphasis at Kalibangan, echoing a proposed north–south
"provincial" opposition in seal style noted elsewhere in the source — and (2) the
absence of monumentalized religious expression, leading him to hypothesize a
household/domestic-hearth basis for Harappan religion as the most parsimonious account
given the absence of temples. Possehl explicitly flags this hearth-as-shrine idea as
speculation exceeding what the evidence can support.

**The Indus-to-Hinduism continuity thread**, a major attributed line of argument that
Possehl himself calls "difficult, complex, sometimes contentious, even politicized,"
proposes candidate continuities including male/female duality (proto-Shaktism), water
(and possibly fire) in worship, yoga-like ritual discipline, and a Heaven/Earth
male/female duality (per F. Allchin). Wheeler (1968) endorsed continuity between
Harappan water/bathing practice and later Hindu tank-bathing tradition. This entire
thread is recorded here as a position, not a wiki-voice claim of continuity — see
[[pashupati-seal]] for the seal-specific proto-yoga argument, and Allchin's own explicit
self-caveat that the Chanhu-daro seal should not be read as proof of Indo-Aryan
presence, essential to preserving non-adjudication of the associated Indo-Aryan
migration question (see debates/indus-aryan-transformation-debate).

## Operationalizations

Indus religion has been "operationalized" through iconographic analysis of seals and
sealings, typological classification of figurines, architectural assessment of
candidate ritual structures, and funerary-practice classification — never through any
deciphered text, since [[indus-script]] remains undeciphered. Every operationalization
therefore carries an interpretive gap between material pattern and religious meaning
that cannot be closed without decipherment or an independently dateable comparandum.

## Applications

The Great Goddess/Great God framework, the Great Bath, and the proto-yoga hypothesis
built on [[pashupati-seal]] together anchor most subsequent discussion of Harappan
belief and ritual, and feed directly into [[harappan-civilization]]'s Lifeways/Religion
section and into the broader Indus-to-Hinduism continuity debate.

## Critiques and Limitations

Every substantive claim about Indus religion in this source is attributed, not
wiki-voice, with the sole exception of the negative architectural fact (no temples,
palaces, or ziggurats have been found) and Possehl's stipulated working definition of
"religion." The politicized character of the Indus-to-Hinduism continuity thread is
explicitly flagged by Possehl himself and requires careful non-adjudication; four-way
non-identity cautions apply throughout — no iconographic or funerary pattern here
licenses an inference about the ethnicity, genetic population, or language of the
people who produced it.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Sacred trees, ideology, deities, and ritual practice

*(All interpretive claims attributed to [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] in *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities]]*; precise meanings of symbols, he insists, await [[indus-script]] decipherment.)*

### Sacred landscape without temples

Kenoyer argues that in the absence of formal temples, **sacred trees** in neighborhood centers or along roads to city gates may have been the most important Indus shrines. Two related trees — pipal (*Ficus religiosa*, heart-shaped leaf) and banyan (*Ficus indica*) — occur throughout the Indus Valley. Heart-shaped pipal leaves (often groups of three) appear on Early Harappan jars and, in the Indus period, on storage jars, domestic vessels, script contexts, faience ornaments, and shell inlay. Terracotta tablets show humans with water jars bowing before trees; seals show deities emerging from a pipal or standing under an arch of pipal leaves; many horned deities wear headdresses of three heart-shaped leaves. At Mohenjo-daro the deity is typically **in** the pipal tree; at Harappa, **beneath** an arch of leaves. Leaf counts (seven, eight, thirteen) are treated as symbolically significant. Later Hindu/Buddhist associations (fertility, protection, Bodhi tree, etc.) are analogies only — seals show protective/sacred power, but other meanings cannot be confirmed.

### Public vs private ideology

Kenoyer holds that Indus ideology is reflected in seals, figurines, painted pottery, and city layout. **Public symbols** (gateways, public buildings, seals, pottery, ornaments/headdresses) integrated ethnic communities under a shared religion/culture; **private symbols** (hearth form, household figurines) carried more local meanings. Symbolic objects increase dramatically with early cities; some motifs (pipal) predate cities, others (unicorn) are urban inventions. Complex technologies (bronze sculpture, high-temperature kilns, stoneware bangles, seals) served symbolic as well as economic ends (see [[craft-specialization]]).

### Geometric and protective motifs

Order/symmetry is a major theme in Indus art and architecture. Geometric pottery/seal motifs (intersecting circle, fish-scale, hatched triangle, checkerboard, circle-and-dot) may represent abstract concepts of order. The **endless-knot** on copper tablets may mark a specific cult/community (parallels at Rojdi; Mesopotamian and later Buddhist/Hindu analogies noted). **Swastika** is presented as an ancient Indus symbol of a universe divided into four sectors, left- and right-turning forms possibly marking different cults/schools. Kidney/heart-shaped motifs (likened to shell-bangle section / protective womb) appear over unicorns’ bellies, on pottery, and as elite ear ornaments. Early excavators’ phallic/yoni readings of conical stones and ringstones are largely rejected: many “phalli” were pestles, many rings architectural column bases (Dholavira; near Harappa Mound E gateway); only some conical pieces may be phallic or gaming pieces.

### Fertility cults and figurines

Kenoyer argues fertility cults for reproduction of people, plants, and animals were integral — based on nude male figurines, male animal seals, putative phallic stones, female figurines with accentuated breasts/hips, sexual intercourse on tablets, animal mating, and related scenes. Mother-goddess clay figurines for most people; bronze figurines for highest classes; rare natural eye-motif stone beads for powerful leaders (as on the “priest-king”), with painted terracotta/steatite copies for commoners. Female “mother-goddess” figurines are common at larger Indus sites and Baluchistan but less so in Gujarat and northern India — regional belief differences or perishable media. Terracotta figurines were likely made by potters for **domestic rituals under sacred trees or in courtyards**, then used as toys or discarded; none found intentionally buried under structures or on fixed shrines (unlike highlands west and Mesopotamia).

### Horned yogic deities

Seals/tablets show a male with horned headdress seated legs spread, heels to groin; Kenoyer traces pose and horned deity to earlier painted pottery, correlates the pose with advanced yoga *mulabandhasana*, and notes later *padmasana* of Buddha/Śiva as **continuity of practice form, not deity identity**. He identifies two distinct horned deities (short zebu-like horns vs wide water-buffalo horns); some seated figures suggest three or four faces — but he **resists** equating multi-faced Indus deities with later multi-faced Śiva (see [[pashupati-seal]]). Some horned figures are beardless with prominent breast/pectoral; a bull-horned clawed figure attacking a horned tiger may indicate male and female aspects of a horned deity — later *śakta/śakti* articulation is only a parallel, not proven Indus doctrine.

### Narrative rituals

Common narratives: figure on acacia branch gesturing to a tiger looking back; female (or ambiguous) figure strangling two tigers above an elephant (Harappa Mound ET tablet 1995) with reverse buffalo-spearing before seated horned deity and gharial; buffalo-spearing before seated priest/deity (later Durga–buffalo analogies noted without identification). Kenoyer allows independent invention for Gilgamesh-type two-animal hero combat parallels with Mesopotamia. Seal/tablet rituals may have been elite/priestly but performed **openly under pipal trees** to teach deity power and legitimize rulers; narrative amulets may commemorate festivals. Combination multi-animal figures synthesize local powers under shared deities — symbols of **religious integration**.

### Water, offering equipment, and Great Bath

Shell ladles and *Turbinella pyrum* libation vessels; ritual offering stands (soma-filter vs incense-burner interpretations remain open). Great Bath and bathing infrastructure remain central circumstantial evidence for ritual water use (Marshall/Jansen line, endorsed in Kenoyer’s urban chapters).

### Human sacrifice?

Kenoyer allows that human sacrifice or head-taking **may** have been practiced, with only one possible depiction (head offering on the famous pipal seal); bound-arm figurines might represent victims — highly provisional.

### Localization ritual change

Localization Era brings changes in burial and ritual alongside loss of seals/writing/weights as integration tools; some ritual crafts and symbols continue in transformed regional cultures (Cemetery H, Jhukar, Rangpur).
