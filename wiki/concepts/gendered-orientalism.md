# Gendered Orientalism

The extension of [[said-edward|Said]]'s [[orientalism]] to representations of Muslim women specifically: a discourse in which the Muslim woman figures as either downtrodden victim awaiting rescue or eroticized harem object — often both at once — and in which her condition indexes her whole civilization's backwardness. Abu-Lughod (*[[abu-lughod-do-muslim-women-2013|Do Muslim Women Need Saving?]]*, 2013) treats the post-9/11 "saving Muslim women" discourse as the latest phase of this formation.

## Definition as Coined

Not a single coinage. Key strands, each attributed:

- **Spivak's formula** — "white men saving brown women from brown men," the organizing epigram of the colonial rescue structure (Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, on sati and colonial discourse).
- **Ahmed's "colonial feminism"** — Leila Ahmed's analysis of turn-of-the-century Egypt, where Lord Cromer championed unveiling Egyptian women as a marker of their oppression while opposing women's suffrage at home and doing nothing for Egyptian women's education (attributed to Ahmed).
- **The colonial visual archive** — Malek Alloula's *The Colonial Harem* on French postcards of Algerian women; Marnia Lazreg's account of the staged May 16, 1958 French "unveiling" ceremony in Algiers (attributed).
- **Missionary precedent** — *Our Moslem Sisters* (1906, Cairo missionary conference proceedings, subtitled "A Cry of Need from the Lands of Darkness"): "They will never cry for themselves, for they are down under the yoke of centuries of oppression" — a direct template for later secular rescue rhetoric (Abu-Lughod 2013).

## Semantic History

Abu-Lughod (2013) argues the discourse has **secularized without disappearing**: missionary salvation → colonial-feminist civilizing → Cold War modernization → post-9/11 human-rights and humanitarian idioms ([[mahmood-saba|Saba Mahmood]]'s point about liberal feminism inheriting the missionary structure). Contemporary carriers she analyzes: the Laura Bush radio address of November 17, 2001 ("The fight against terrorism is also a fight for the rights and dignity of women"); the Time magazine Bibi Aysha cover (August 2010, "What Happens if We Leave Afghanistan?"); humanitarian imagery ("Afghan Women: Behind the Veil," Médecins du Monde 2002); and the mass-market abuse-memoir genre literary scholar Dohra Ahmad labels **"pulp nonfiction"** — which Abu-Lughod reads as fusing the victim register and the pornographic register in one commodity, analogous to what Marcus Wood calls "plantation pornography" in antislavery literature (all attributed).

## Operationalizations

Identified through discourse analysis: recurrent veiling imagery standing for the whole woman; collapse of political-historical causes into "culture" or "Islam"; the rescuer's structural superiority; selective attention (gender violence located only "elsewhere" — Abu-Lughod's critique of *Half the Sky*'s "this kind of oppression feels transcendent").

## Applications

Organizes Abu-Lughod's 2013 book and much of the [[saving-muslim-women-debate]]; applied to the [[honor-crime]] category, to burqa-ban debates, and to the "IslamLand" fantasy geography Abu-Lughod attributes to Ayaan Hirsi Ali's writing (miriam cooke's parallel coinage: "the Muslimwoman").

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics of this analytic (positions recorded, not adjudicated) argue it can shade into minimizing real violence against women or delegitimizing internal reformers; advocates reply that the point is causal accuracy (history and political economy over "culture"), not denial. See [[saving-muslim-women-debate]].
