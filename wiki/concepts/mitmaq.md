# Mitmaq

## Definition as Coined

**Mitmaq** (Quechua; Spanish *mitimaes*) designates communities **relocated by the state**—loyal groups planted in new or hostile provinces, and subversive or hostile populations moved into consolidated regions—for agrarian, political, and craft purposes. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) treats large *mitamaq* colonies moved great distances as one investment of imperial [[mita-labor|mit’a]]-supported policy alongside land reclamation, roads, and military drafts. Distinct from temporary rotational **mit’a**.

## Semantic History

Colonial chroniclers recorded mitmaq policy as a key instrument of Pax Incaica; modern ethnohistory treats it as both security and economic strategy (resource specialization, multi-zonal control). Scholars and Moseley use “mitmaq-like” for earlier multi-ethnic colonization (e.g. Tiwanaku Moquegua settlers; possible Santa Valley resettlement under Moche “Pax Moche”)—**analogy**, not proof of the full Inca institution.

## Operationalizations

**Inca (as of Moseley 2001):**
- Resettlement from fortified hilltops (*pukaras*) to low-lying localities after Titicaca conquest—yields, control, highway access; Colla capital rebuilt at Hatunqolla.
- Exile of hostile Chimor groups to distant colonies after c. AD 1470 conquest; carving Chimor into señoríos loyal to Cuzco; Chan Chan artisans moved to Cuzco.
- Craft transfer: ~10,000 skilled Lambayeque metallurgists and craftspeople brought into Chan Chan under Chimor (conquest administration)—parallel mechanism of forced specialist relocation before full Inca rule.
- Subsidized specialists and multi-ethnic state workforce in staple/wealth finance system.

**Historical/archaeological proxies:** *Visitas* and chronicles naming origin communities; intrusive ceramic/architectural styles; multi-ethnic cemeteries—always interpretive.

## Applications

Inca imperial integration alongside roads, decimal administration, elite education of conquered heirs, and corporate style. Explains multi-ethnic landscapes of Tahuantinsuyu. Comparative backdrop for Tiwanaku colonies (Omo/Chen Chen) and Wari Cerro Baúl as earlier colonization strategies under different labels.

## Critiques and Limitations

Scale and date of mitmaq-like practices before the Inca empire are debated; do not project the full imperial institution unchanged onto earlier periods without argument. Chimor craft transfer is conquest spoils, not necessarily Quechua *mitmaq* terminology. Osteological proof of colonization is rare (Moseley notes absence of Ayacucho skeletal signatures at [[pachacamac]] despite Huari-influenced tombs).

### From D'Altroy 2002

[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy (2002)]] treats resettlement as the state policy that most remade the Andean social landscape. He reports Cobo’s estimate that roughly a quarter to a third of the population was resettled on average as about right for the realm as a whole, and states that “several million people moved under Inca rule” — generating colonial litigation over lands given to state-resettled colonists. Colonists were still counted on homeland census rolls even when living hundreds of kilometers away.

**Goals (as of D'Altroy 2002).** (1) Disperse security threats (e.g. Qolla, Ayaviri, Chachapoya, Kañari) and staff garrisons/frontiers with loyal *mitmaqkuna* islands. (2) Congregate economic specialists — weavers, potters, metal smiths, maize/coca/pepper farmers, herders, masons, miners — especially expanded under Wayna Qhapaq. (3) Claim a divine mandate via ethnic microcosms (suburban [[cuzco]]; Copacabana sanctuary with 42 ethnic groups). State support for new colonies lasted only until self-sustaining (~1–2 years). Late policy also dedicated redoubtable ethnic groups (Chachapoyas, Kañari) as permanent military personnel; when Spaniards met Atawallpa at [[cajamarca]], he reportedly had ~15,000 Chachapoyas destined for resettlement.

**Control and identity.** *Mitmaqkuna* were required to wear traditional clothing and speak their own languages, with restricted local interaction, to keep allegiance to the state; Cobo reports torture for return home and execution for a second offense. They often received prime lands and were bitterly resented, yet sometimes forged local exchange ties. Archaeologically, D'Altroy stresses *mitmaq* communities are hard to find — perishable textiles may have been the main ethnic markers; without witnesses, scale could be underestimated by large factors. Cranial deformation practices (Cobo on Qolla) are one possible material track of ethnic movement.

**Cases.** Cochabamba state farms: most western-valley natives removed; ~14,000 farm workers from adjacent altiplano (permanent colonists + corvée from seven ethnic groups); 77 strips; 2,400 storehouses at Cotapachi before shipment toward Paria, Cuzco, armies. Huánuco Chupachu census (Julien via D'Altroy): of 4,108 households, 1,110 in Cuzco, 580 full-time out of province, 918 temporarily out (incl. 500 army), remainder largely relocated to specialized communities. Milliraya weaving enclave (~1,000 weavers, nearby altiplano origin rather than distant mitmaq in Spurling’s reconstruction) and Potrero-Chaquiago multi-craft sectors show craft colonization as social engineering as well as production. Narrative cases include Cañete/Guarco deportation with Chincha/Coayllo colonists; wartime transport of ~15,000 Cañari toward Cuzco. Royal estates (e.g. Yucay’s 2,400 dedicated men and families for Wayna Qhapaq) blurred into lifelong service statuses ([[yanakuna]], estate *mitmaqkuna*) distinct from temporary [[mita-labor]].
