# Societal Sector

## Definition as Coined

[[scott-w-richard]] and [[meyer-john-w]] introduced the societal sector in "The Organization of Societal Sectors" (originally 1983, revised as ch. 5 of [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]] — the chapter itself notes it is "a revised version of a paper first published about seven years ago," dating the concept to the 1983 original). Their definition: "A societal sector is defined as (1) a collection of organizations operating in the same domain, as identified by the similarity of their services, products or functions, (2) together with those organizations that critically influence the performance of the focal organizations: for example, major suppliers and customers, owners and regulators, funding sources and competitors." The adjective *societal* signals that sectors "are likely to stretch from local to national or even international actors," bounded "in functional, not geographical terms."

Scott and Meyer distinguished two dimensions along which a sector's environment can vary, each a matter of degree rather than a fixed type:

- **Technical environments**: "by definition, those in which a product or service is produced and exchanged in a market such that organizations are rewarded for effective and efficient control of their production systems."
- **Institutional environments**: "by definition, those characterized by the elaboration of rules and requirements to which individual organizations must conform if they are to receive support and legitimacy," with such rules originating from "regulatory agencies authorized by the nation-state," from "professional and trade associations," or from "generalized belief systems."

These imply two distinct conceptions of rationality: "technical environments emphasize a rationality that incorporates a set of prescriptions for matching means and ends in ways that are efficacious in producing outcomes of a predictable character. Institutional environments embrace a rationality that is suggested by the cognate term rationale: providing an account that makes past actions understandable and acceptable to others, that renders the organization accountable for its past actions." Scott and Meyer cautioned that the two are empirically hard to disentangle in practice, since "those who formulate institutional rules strive to make them appear technical in nature," and observed (following Selznick 1949) that "institutional procedures may become technical ones; conversely, many technical procedures, over time, become institutionalized."

## Semantic History

The technical/institutional distinction, cross-classified as strong or weak on each dimension (Fig. 5.1, attributed to Scott 1987b), produced a fourfold illustrative typology: utilities, banks, and general hospitals score strong on both; general manufacturing and pharmaceuticals score technically strong but institutionally weak; mental health clinics, schools, legal agencies, and churches score institutionally strong but technically weak; restaurants and health clubs score weak on both. Scott and Meyer treated cells with both dimensions weak as unstable: "where neither technical nor institutional environments are highly developed, it is difficult for organizations to develop or flourish."

"Societal sector" names the same underlying object that [[organizational-field]] names from a different angle within the volume — Scott and Meyer's own chapter cites parallel labels including Hirsch's "industry system," Benson's "interorganizational network," and DiMaggio and Powell's "organizational field," and [[scott-w-richard]]'s separate chapter (ch. 7) explicitly cross-references "organizational field" and "societal sector" as competing terms for the same unit of analysis. Scott and Meyer credited [[dimaggio-paul]] and [[powell-walter-w]] (1983) for the term "isomorphism," and drew on [[giddens-anthony]]'s structuration theory (via DiMaggio and Powell) for the idea that sectoral environments are themselves "formally organized," quoting their own earlier claim that "the environments of formal organizations are, to a surprising degree, themselves formally organized."

Scott and Meyer's technical/institutional distinction was itself contested within the same volume. [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) argued the dichotomy was intended analytically but was widely misread as a descriptive claim that whole classes of organizations are either market-driven or symbol-manipulating, and offered a counter-illustration: commodities markets, seemingly the paradigm case of a purely technical/competitive sector, are in fact heavily institutionally structured by both macro-level regulatory bodies (the SEC, CFTC, exchange rules) and micro-level reputational trading networks (citing Baker 1984, whose study of national securities markets found "tightly knit micronetworks promote reliable communication that dampens price volatility," contrary to standard microeconomic prediction).

## Operationalizations

The chapter's core "early evidence" hypotheses (H1–H4) frame technical and institutional sectors as predicting divergent organizational behavior: organizations in technical sectors will control and coordinate production activities, buffering them from environmental influence (H1), succeeding to the extent they achieve efficiency (H2); organizations in institutional sectors will not tightly control production activities but will instead buffer or [[decoupling|decouple]] them from formal structure (H3, attributed originally to [[meyer-john-w]] and [[rowan-brian]] 1977 — see [[decoupling]]), succeeding to the extent they conform to established norms and authorities (H4).

Beyond H1–H4, the chapter develops a substantially larger apparatus — the extraction record identifies nineteen numbered hypotheses (H1–H19) addressing sector centralization, fragmentation, professionalization, and administrative complexity, drawing in part on prior work by Meyer, Scott, and Deal (1981). **The chapter's hypothesis numbering is internally inconsistent in the available OCR** (the sequence jumps from H14 to a repeated "H16" with no clean H15, likely reflecting an OCR line-drop or a genuine authorial numbering quirk) — the full numbered list should not be treated as an authoritative verbatim inventory pending verification against a clean print copy. A representative sample: organizations in sectors highly developed both technically and institutionally develop more complex, conflict-prone administrative systems (H5); highly centralized, unified sectors produce organizations that are tightly coupled across levels with small administrative components (H12), while fragmented or federalized sectors produce complex cross-level linkages and enlarged administrative components (H13).

## Applications

Several studies cited by Scott and Meyer bear on the sector framework. Meyer, Scott, and Deal's (1978) study of 188 elementary schools in 34 San Francisco Bay Area districts found low consensus on school policy *within* schools/districts but high consensus among role groups (principals, teachers, superintendents) *across* schools — read as evidence that shared institutional environment, not organizational membership, produces belief consensus. Rowan (1982) found California school-district programs backed by a "balanced" institutional environment (consensus among state agencies, professional associations, and teacher-training institutions) were more likely to be both adopted and retained. Powell's (1988) comparative case study of a scholarly-book publisher and a public television station of comparable size found the more institutionally conflicted broadcaster (WNET) developed markedly more complex administrative structure — "organizations, such as WNET, that are located in environments in which conflicting demands are made upon them will be especially likely to generate complex organizational structures with disproportionately large administrative components." A contrary cross-societal finding is flagged: Carroll, Goodstein, and Gyenes (1988), studying Hungarian agricultural cooperatives, found fragmentation of decision-making *negatively* correlated with administrative ratio — the opposite of the U.S. pattern reported elsewhere in the chapter.

## Critiques and Limitations

Beyond Powell's critique of the technical/institutional dichotomy (above), Scott and Meyer's own framework generated a live internal disagreement over predicted outcomes: while DiMaggio and Powell's "master hypothesis" (see [[institutional-isomorphism]]) predicts that more structured environments produce homogeneity, Scott and Meyer argued that under conditions of centralized authority, institutional environments may instead generate *increased* diversity of organizational form, as centralized decision-makers create specialized forms by design. Scott and Meyer also cited counterweight positions from Grodzins ("a little chaos is a good thing") and Landau on redundancy and error-correction, and from Hannan and Freeman (1989), who warned that centralization-driven homogenization of organizational forms may diminish "the capacity of a society to respond to uncertain future changes" — treating sectoral diversity as a repository of alternative solutions rather than mere inefficiency.
