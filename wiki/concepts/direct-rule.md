# Direct Rule

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly, [[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]]
Ch. 4.)* **Direct rule** is "unmediated intervention in the lives of local communities,
households, and productive enterprises," replacing the "nearly universal system of
[[indirect-rule|indirect rule]]" in which rulers governed "through powerful intermediaries who
enjoyed significant autonomy, hindered state demands that were not to their own interest, and
profited on their own accounts from the delegated exercise of state power" — clergy, landlords,
urban oligarchies, professional warriors.

The mechanism of transition: "As war demanded greater resources... ever more rulers bypassed,
suppressed, or co-opted old intermediaries and reached directly into communities and households
to seize the wherewithal of war. Thus national standing armies, national states, and direct
rule caused each other." Indirect rule capped extraction (beyond a limit, intermediaries
obstructed or allied with popular resistance) and risked both predation *by* intermediaries and
rebellion *led by* them (Tilly's Ottoman timar→chiftlik→janissary case).

## Semantic History

Tilly dates the decisive break to the **French Revolution**: "no European state (except,
perhaps, Sweden) made a serious attempt to institute direct rule from top to bottom until the
era of the French Revolution." Revolutionary lawyers and officials displaced landlords, venal
officeholders, and clergy; the departmental grid and *représentants en mission* "installed
direct rule"; by 1792 "a genuine hierarchical bureaucracy took shape... one of the first systems
of direct rule ever to take shape in a large state." Napoleon consolidated it and conquest
exported it — "The age of direct rule had begun." Corollaries (all attributed): a shift from
reactive to **proactive policing** (Fouché); deliberate **homogenization** of populations
(expulsions, established churches, national languages and schooling), which in turn fed
state-led nationalism; and intrinsic **resistance** — counter-revolution followed "directly
from the process by which the new state established direct rule" (the Vendée). Costs and
gains: "access to citizens and the resources they controlled through household taxation, mass
conscription, censuses, police systems... at the cost of widespread resistance, extensive
bargaining, and the creation of rights and perquisites for citizens."

## Operationalizations

Presence of unmediated administrative instruments: censuses, household-level taxation, mass
conscription, national police, uniform territorial grids, national languages and school
systems. The contrast pair travels: colonial administration's direct/indirect-rule vocabulary
(see [[indirect-rule]]) names the same axis in imperial context.

## Applications

- The institutional hinge of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]]'s
  extraction→bargaining→rights loop: direct rule is what made mass [[citizenship]] possible and
  necessary — "direct rule and mass national politics grew up together, and reinforced each
  other mightily."
- Explains 19th-century homogenization ("life homogenized within states and heterogenized among
  states") and both varieties of nationalism.
- A standard comparative variable in state-formation and empire studies.

## Critiques and Limitations

The dichotomy compresses a continuum (Tilly's own cases — Prussian Junkers, English JPs — are
hybrids), and the French Revolution's paradigm status imports a Francocentric template.
Attributed throughout.
