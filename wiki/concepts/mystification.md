# Mystification (Goffman)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life]]* (1956), Ch. I, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] treated **mystification** as the restriction of contact and the holding of an audience in a state of mystification regarding the performer — a technique related to social distance, idealization, and the maintenance of awe (attributed). By limiting access to backstage facts and regulating the terms of approach, performers protect the fostered impression from the full force of familiarity that would dissolve it.

Mystification connects to **[[performance-idealization|idealization]]**, control of **[[front-and-back-region|regions]]**, and circumspect management of information conditions (audiences with little prior knowledge; sacred performers who cannot be disconfirmed by ordinary tests).

## Semantic History

Distinct from Marxist "mystification" (ideological concealment of exploitation). Goffman's sense is dramaturgical: awe-maintenance through restricted access. Secondary literature sometimes blends the two — keep them separate.

## Operationalizations

Access rules to high-status performers; ceremonial protocol; professional restriction of judgment to colleagues.

## Applications

Royalty and high office; sacred statuses; professional monopolies of judgment; "remove oneself from the facts" for elaborate shows (Ch. VI circumspection). Links: [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Thin standalone development relative to front/team/region apparatus; often absorbed into idealization and social-distance discussions.
