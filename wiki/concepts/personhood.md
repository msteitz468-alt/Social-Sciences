# Personhood

## Definition as Coined

**Personhood** is the culturally specific constitution of the social being. Following Mauss's
foundational essay on *la personne* (1938) and Jean La Fontaine (1985), anthropology distinguishes
three things usually collapsed in Western usage: the **individual** (a biologically distinct,
indivisible being), the **person** (an ensemble of social roles and relations), and the **self**
(a unique identity). Treated as a key concept of contemporary theory in
*[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010) via
John and Jean Comaroff's "On Personhood." Claims here are attributed. The concept is **migrated**:
emic local terms (e.g. Tswana *tiro*) are read through an etic analytic vocabulary (person/
individual/self).

## Semantic History

The Comaroffs argue (attributed; Southern Tswana fieldwork **documented in the 1970s**, framed
around the late-colonial period — not the ethnographic present) against treating the "autonomous,
rights-bearing individual" as a cultural universal or evolutionary endpoint, and against the "tired
antinomy" opposing a Western individual to a "relational, communalistic" non-Western self. Southern
Tswana personhood, they hold, is **both** relentlessly social/relational **and** highly
individuated — persons "build themselves up."

## Operationalizations

**Being-as-becoming (Comaroff & Comaroff).** Personhood was a continuous process of
self-construction through *tiro* ("work"/"labour"; reflexive *go itirela*, "to make for oneself,"
socially accountable — versus *go itira*, self-aggrandizing "contrivance"). A 1970 Mafikeng
vignette makes it concrete: asked whether a post-childbearing elderly woman had children, the ward
headman answered "Not yet … No, not yet" — because as long as one remains a "becoming" social being,
a flat "no" would declare social death (*sefifi*, "death pollution"; the authors link it, as a
modern parallel not an identity, to postcolonial "zombie" panics). Personhood was deliberately
**partible** — different facets shown to different audiences for protective/political reasons
(concealment as empowerment) — resonating with, but not reducible to, the Melanesian "partible
person" (Strathern); the authors coin **"autoplexy"** (tentatively) for this strategically multiple
selfhood. The fragmented biography was integrated into one narrative only at the funeral *tatolo*.
A gendered asymmetry ran through it: women were colonial-era "jural minors" (grammatically men
"married," women "were married"), female fertility associated with polluting "heat" against male
"coolness" — an attributed, historically-situated claim, not a timeless one.

## Applications

Personhood analysis anchors work on the body ([[embodiment]]), age and gender lifecycle
production, mortuary archaeology, and the archaeology of the self; it intersects
[[materiality]] (objects that co-create persons) and [[agency]]. In archaeology it warns against
projecting the modern individual onto past mortuary or household evidence.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Comaroffs flag their own boldest move — a "counter-teleological" hint that "Europe is evolving
toward Africa, not the other way around" — as undeveloped. Critics worry the person/individual/self
triad can harden into a new West/rest binary (the very antinomy the Comaroffs reject), and that
"partible personhood" travels too freely from Melanesia to other ethnographic contexts. As
ethnographic interpretation, every claim is dated to its fieldwork moment. See
[[postcolonialism]] for the colonial-encounter framing (missionary discourse casting Tswana as
both "primitive communists" and excessively "greedy" to justify instilling individualism).

## Sahlins densification (2013) — kinship as mutual personhood

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-what-kinship-is-2013|What Kinship Is—And Is Not]]* 2013.)* Sahlins defines kinship as [[mutuality-of-being]] — persons who participate intrinsically in each other's existence — and treats that intersubjectivity as continuous with personhood constructions that are not bounded individuals: Leenhardt dual unities; Maori "kinship I" (Johansen); name-sharing as total identity (Ojibway, Suya, !Kung); Achuar/Jivaro lifetime composition of the person by multiple contributors (Taylor); [[dividual]]-adjacent genealogies via Leenhardt/Bastide. Mystical interdependence (shared injury, mourning as mutual death, sorcery as "negative kinship") is the praxis of mutual personhood. Closing programmatic line (via [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]): Amazonian kinship as a "non-biological theory of life." See [[kinship-biology-vs-culture-debate]].
