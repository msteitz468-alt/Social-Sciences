# Delírio de fome (the madness of hunger)

## Definition as Coined

*Delírio de fome* — "the madness of hunger" — is a Nordestino popular expression for the terrifying
end-point of starvation: the bodily and psychic disintegration of the chronically or acutely hungry.
In *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* (Chapter 4)
[[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] treats it as the older, pre-medical "subjective voice" of
hunger — "the tragic experience of the body with frenzied hunger" — later displaced and concealed by
the medicalized idiom of **[[nervoso]]**. She opens the chapter with the 1965 case of "Rosa," who killed
her two starving children ("To stop them from crying for milk"); her companion Nailza attributed the act
to "the *delírio* of hunger," a state under which "many good people" could act and later repent.

## Semantic History

Scheper-Hughes situates *delírio de fome* in a long documentary lineage — 16th-century navigators'
diaries of "hunger madness" on Atlantic crossings, the wreck of the *Medusa*, and the Nordestino
literary tradition (Euclides da Cunha, Josué de Castro, Graciliano Ramos, Carolina Maria de Jesus). Her
argument is that scientific medicine only redefined hunger as a discrete clinical object — "protein-calorie"
or "protein-energy malnutrition" — comparatively recently, and largely after white Europeans experienced
famine in the Second World War (the Warsaw ghetto, the Dutch *hongerwinter*, the concentration camps),
"long endemic among black and brown populations." The concept's fate — its transformation into the
individualized, medicated syndrome *nervoso* — is the hinge of the book's argument about
**[[medicalization]]**.

## Operationalizations

She marshals comparative caloric data as an evidentiary strategy, setting Nordestino cane-cutters'
intake (Josué de Castro's ~1,700 cal/day; Nelson Chaves's <1,500) alongside acknowledged starvation
regimes (Warsaw ghetto ~600–800 cal; Buchenwald ~1,750; the Minnesota semi-starvation experiment's
~1,570) — endorsing Galeano's description of the Northeast as "a concentration camp for more than thirty
million people" as literal rather than metaphorical. She distinguishes the *zona da mata*'s "constant
and chronic" hunger from the *sertão*'s "cyclical, acute, and explosive" drought hunger.

## Applications

- **Distinguishing hunger from malnutrition.** She argues Alto/Nordestino hunger is "politically as
  well as economically caused," an endemic **famine**, not merely "malnutrition" — a critical-medical
  reframing (see [[critical-medical-anthropology]]).
- **Against the "small but healthy" thesis.** She reports and rejects David Seckler's hypothesis that
  chronically food-scarce populations may be adaptively "small but healthy," which Alto residents
  themselves dismissed as "a contradiction in terms."
- **Race-science flag.** Nelson Chaves's contested "pygmitization" thesis (comparing cane-cutters'
  stature to "the African pygmy") is recorded critically as a race-adjacent nutritional claim, not
  endorsed.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is a bridge, not a stable diagnosis: its analytic value depends on Scheper-Hughes's larger
(attributed) argument that *nervoso* is medicalized hunger. Where that argument is resisted, *delírio de
fome* reads as one folk expression among others rather than as the concealed truth beneath the clinic's
categories. Its documentation is also, by its nature, literary and historical as much as clinical.
