# Imaginative Geography

**Imaginative geography** is Edward [[said-edward|Said]]'s term (1978) for the arbitrary,
culturally produced division of space into "our" familiar territory and "their" alien
territory — a distinction that need not correspond to any real border and that the people
on the "other" side need not accept.

## Definition as Coined

Said argued (attributed) that the mind dramatizes distance and difference by designating a
familiar space that is "ours" and an unfamiliar space beyond it that is "theirs." This
"poetic" bounding of territory is not empirical geography but a projection: the Orient is
constructed as a stage, a theatrical space onto which Europe projects what it is not. The
line between Occident and Orient is drawn by Europeans, for European purposes.

## Semantic History

Coined within *Orientalism*; adopted widely in cultural geography, postcolonial studies,
and the study of borders and mapping as a standard tool for analyzing how spatial
categories encode power.

## Operationalizations

Traced through the reading of maps, travel writing, literature, and scholarship for the
ways they naturalize a boundary between self and other and assign moral/cultural qualities
to each side.

## Applications

Central to [[orientalism]] and [[postcolonialism]]; applied to the analysis of empire,
frontier, area studies, and the representation of Islam and the "East."

## Critiques and Limitations

As with Orientalism generally, critics note the risk of treating "the West" as itself a
unified imaginative agent. The concept is broadly accepted as an analytical tool even by
those who dispute Said's larger historical claims.
