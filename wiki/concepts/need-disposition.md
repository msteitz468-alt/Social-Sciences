# Need-Disposition

## Definition as Coined

In [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s action theory (1951), a **need-disposition** is the personality-level unit of motivation: an acquired (not purely biological) disposition to orient toward and be gratified by classes of objects and to conform with classes of standards. It is the correlate, within the personality system, of the [[social-role|role-expectation]] within the [[social-system|social system]]. When a cultural [[value-orientation]] pattern is internalized through [[socialization]], "to act in conformity with it becomes a need-disposition in the actor's own personality structure," relatively independent of instrumental payoff.

Parsons gave the need-disposition system two primary aspects: a **gratificational** aspect (what is gained, or its cost — corresponding to *cathectic* orientation) and an **orientational** aspect (the patterned "how" of the relation to objects — corresponding to *cognitive* orientation), with **evaluation** the process of ordered selection among alternatives (the third, integrative mode).

## Semantic History

The term is Parsons's adaptation of motivational psychology (Henry Murray's "need" concept, and psychoanalytic drive theory) into the action frame of reference. Its decisive role is in [[deviance]] and [[social-control]]: Parsons analyzed deviance as generated by an **ambivalent** motivational structure combining an *alienative* need-disposition (the negative component relative to complementary expectations) and a *conformative* need-disposition (the positive component). Where attachment and internalization are wholly absent, the attitude is not alienation but mere *indifference*. "Compulsive conformity" and "compulsive alienation" name states in which one component dominates while the other persists, repressed.

The concept did not survive intact outside Parsonian theory; motivational sociology largely abandoned the term, though its underlying claim — that social order rests on internalized dispositions, not just external sanction — persists in socialization research and in Bourdieu's cognate (and consciously anti-Parsonian) notion of *habitus*.

## Operationalizations

Need-dispositions are theoretical constructs inferred from role conduct and its disturbances (clinical observation, deviance), not directly measured.

## Applications

- The motivational underpinning of [[institutionalization]]: a standard is institutionalized when conforming with it *both* fulfills the actor's need-dispositions *and* optimizes others' reactions.
- The building block of Parsons's deviance typology and his account of the [[socialization]] of the child (identification as the mechanism by which value-orientations become need-dispositions).

## Critiques and Limitations

As with [[value-orientation]], the concept invites the "oversocialized" critique: it models the actor as a bundle of internalized dispositions, giving limited purchase on strategic, interested, or resistant conduct that is not a "disturbance" of an equilibrium but a rational pursuit of divergent ends.
