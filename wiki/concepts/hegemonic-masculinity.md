# Hegemonic Masculinity

The central concept of [[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s [[gender-relations-theory|theory of gender relations]], introduced in Carrigan, Connell and Lee (1985), developed in *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987), and restated for the masculinities field in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995). Deep treatment: [[hubs/theory/gender-relations-theory|Theory Hub — Gender Relations Theory]].

## Definition as Coined

**1987 statement (*Gender and Power*).** Connell defines hegemony (Gramsci) as social ascendancy beyond brute force into private life and cultural processes — embedded in doctrine, media, wages, housing design, welfare/tax policy — not gunpoint or unemployment threat alone. Hegemonic masculinity is "always constructed in relation to various subordinated masculinities as well as in relation to women"; the public face "is not necessarily what powerful men are, but what sustains their power and what large numbers of men are motivated to support." Cultural ideals need not match most men's personalities; fantasy figures (Bogart, Wayne, Stallone) often carry the model. The major reason for mass complicity: "most men benefit from the subordination of women, and hegemonic masculinity is the cultural expression of this ascendancy" — with a "fit" to [[emphasized-femininity]]. It must embody a successful **collective strategy** institutionalizing men's dominance (a "mix": domesticity and violence, misogyny and heterosexual attraction). Contemporary form: heterosexual, tied to marriage; key subordinated masculinity: homosexual.

**1995 restatement (*Masculinities*)** — the most-cited formulation (attributed; theoretical claim, not wiki-voice fact):

> "Hegemonic masculinity can be defined as the configuration of gender practice which embodies the currently accepted answer to the problem of the legitimacy of patriarchy, which guarantees (or is taken to guarantee) the dominant position of men and the subordination of women."

Three qualifications attached across both books:
- **Hegemony works through authority more than force**, though force often underpins it; not total cultural dominance — a "state of play" in a balance of forces.
- **Exemplars need not be the powerful**; fantasy or unattainable public models can carry the ideal if there is collective correspondence to institutional power.
- **Historically mobile** — when conditions for defending patriarchy change, bases of a particular hegemony erode.

## Semantic History

Hegemonic masculinity is one term in a **relational typology** of masculinities that Connell defines together; the others take their meaning only in relation to it:

- **Subordinated masculinity** — masculinities expelled from legitimacy; Connell's paradigm case in "contemporary European/American society" is the subordination of homosexual men, enforced through "an array of quite material practices" (legal, economic, and street violence). "Gayness, in patriarchal ideology, is the repository of whatever is symbolically expelled from hegemonic masculinity."
- **Complicit masculinity** — masculinities "constructed in ways that realize the [[patriarchal-dividend|patriarchal dividend]], without the tensions or risks of being the front-line troops of patriarchy." The majority of men, Connell argues, are complicit rather than hegemonic.
- **Marginalized masculinity** — the relation "between the masculinities in dominant and subordinated classes or ethnic groups," always relative to "the authorization of the hegemonic masculinity of the dominant group" (e.g. Connell argues black sporting stardom yields no "social authority to black men generally" — "no trickledown effect"). Connell reads the "protest masculinity" of marginal working-class men (after Adler's "masculine protest," made collective) as a marginalized form.

These form "a sparse framework": hegemony / complicity / subordination on one axis, marginalization / authorization on another. All the terms "name not fixed character types but configurations of practice generated in particular situations in a changing structure of relationships."

Connell traces the concept's genesis to school ethnography (competitive sport as the test of masculinity even for boys who reject it — already analyzed as body-politics in *Gender and Power* Ch. 4), combined with psychoanalytic work on character and gay-liberation theory. The companion concept **[[emphasized-femininity]]** is defined in *Gender and Power* Ch. 8 and is not restated in *Masculinities*. In the 2nd-edition Introduction (2005) he proposes an emerging global form, **"transnational business masculinity,"** borne by managers and entrepreneurs empowered by neo-liberal deregulation.

## Operationalizations

The concept is not measured on a scale; it is identified analytically. In the *Masculinities* [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history study]], particular masculinities are located by tracing men's "gender projects" against the three structures of gender (power, production, cathexis) and reading each configuration's position relative to the hegemonic pattern. Connell also points to observable proxies of the dominance the concept describes: the [[patriarchal-dividend|patriarchal dividend]]'s material index (income ratios, control of capital, office-holding) and everyday "enactments of hegemonic masculinity" (street harassment, boys' intrusion on girls' play, men interrupting women's speech).

## Applications

- **Everyday dominance.** Connell holds hegemonic masculinity "need not be thematized at all" most of the time — the routine operation of institutions run by heterosexual men achieves it silently; it becomes explicit ("gun lobby"–type politics) only during "crisis tendencies" in the [[gender-order]].
- **Exemplary imagery.** Connell contrasts the *imagery* of martial heroism (WWI recruiting posters) with combat reality (trench "live and let live" truces) to argue the hero image is a disciplinary cultural tool, not a description of how militaries work; and cites film history (Mellen) on Hollywood's narrowing of masculine heroes toward proof-by-violence as gender-equality pressure grew.
- **Institutional analysis** of corporations, the military, and the state as machineries that sustain a particular masculinity.
- The concept became the organizing frame of an entire subfield of the sociology of men and masculinities — see [[hubs/theory/gender-relations-theory|the Theory Hub]] for the empirical program and its afterlife.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is **contested** (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]). Connell records in the 2nd edition that it has "come under challenge from several directions" — Petersen (1998), Demetriou (2001), Jefferson (2002) — and that "whether to discard the concept of hegemonic masculinity, reconstruct it, or reaffirm it, is still sharply debated." Discursive-psychology critics (Wetherell and Edley 1999) argue masculinity is not a stable pattern men are "permanently committed to" but situational choices from a cultural repertoire; Demetriou argues the typology misses the "hybridization" by which hegemonic masculinity absorbs elements of subordinated ones. Connell's own reply: "we still require a way of theorizing gendered power relations among men … the concept of hegemonic masculinity, as developed in this book, is still essential." Connell and Messerschmidt's later reformulation (2005) is the standard response and is treated on the debate page.


## Archaeological uptake — Gilchrist (1999)

*(Attributed — Gilchrist, *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gender and Archaeology]]* ch.4.)* Gilchrist imports Connell-stream hegemonic masculinity into archaeological readings of warrior packages and counter-hegemonic masculinities (Treherne, Härke, Robb as she reviews), and warns that “masculinist” archaeology can become feminist backlash unless it challenges stereotyping of both sexes. Related: Amazon/warrior-women and high-status female burials (Vix, etc.) as pressure on male=warrior metonymy.
