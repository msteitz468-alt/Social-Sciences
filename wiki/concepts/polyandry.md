# Polyandry

> One woman in a marital/sexual relationship with more than one man. Mid-century debate (Fischer; Prince Peter of Greece and Denmark; [[leach-edmund|Leach]]; Kathleen Gough) turned less on whether “polyandry exists” than on **what counts as marriage**. Leach’s essay “Polyandry, Inheritance and the Definition of Marriage” (*Man*; reprinted *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]* Ch. 4) is a principal statement: **universal definitions of marriage are vain** because marriage is a **bundle of rights**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed positions — Leach 1961 reporting and arguing.)*

| Position | Claim |
|---|---|
| *Notes and Queries* (1951) | Marriage = union of a man and a woman such that children are recognized legitimate offspring of both partners |
| Fischer (1952) | Adelphic polyandry as polygamy is often **non-existent**: children have one legal father; other men have tolerated sexual access → **polykoity**, not polygamy; reserve polygamy for successive marriage rites |
| Prince Peter | Adelphic polyandry as species of polygamy; social fatherhood may be corporate (sibling solidarity) |
| Leach | Stop pedantry over the label; ask whether legitimacy-only definitions are adequate; distinguish classes of rights (sexual monopoly/disposal, cohabitation, heirship, descent-group membership, etc.) |

Leach’s working list of rights (bundle; Maine’s phrase) includes, among others: husband monopoly in disposal of wife’s sexuality (not necessarily exclusive use — crucial for adelphic polyandry); claims over children; property and inheritance; ritual status; affinity between groups.

## Semantic History

- **Evolutionary anthropology (e.g. McLennan):** polyandry linked to matriliny / primitive stages — Leach notes best-attested adelphic cases often express **patrilineal** ideals (Kandyan Sinhalese; Iravas; Tibetans per literature he cites).
- **Structural-functionalism:** Radcliffe-Brown (1941) interprets adelphic polyandry via **solidarity of the sibling group**.
- **Leach 1955/61:** definitional crisis → dual institutions in Sinhalese customary practice (formal affinity/sexual disposal vs informal cohabitation establishing heirship).
- **Gough (1959):** vigorous reply repudiating Leach’s “bundle of rights / no universal definition” move — position to record, not resolve here.

## Operationalizations

| Case (as Leach cites) | Analytic issue |
|---|---|
| Ancient Britons (Caesar via Fischer) | Shared wives; offspring of first conductor — one legal father |
| Todas; Smith secondary marriage | Closer to polyandrous polygamy if successive social fathers |
| Iravas (Aiyappan) | Corporate polyandry: eldest brother represents brother-corporation |
| Kandyan Sinhalese customary | Law may not recognize polyandrous marriage; custom may recognize joint heirship; Census undercounts “customary” unions |

**Hypothesis (Leach):** adelphic polyandry associates with systems where **women as well as men** hold property rights; shared wife cements economic solidarity of brothers where separate wives would split the patrimony. Also underattended link to **dowry**.

## Applications

- Stress-tests [[marriage]] definitions used in comparative kinship.
- Feeds [[affinity]] analysis: which rights transfer to groups vs individuals.
- Ceylon material (Leach fieldwork 1954, Leverhulme / Wenner-Gren) is **date-stamped documentation** of mid-1950s Kandyan/customary practice as Leach reports it — not “the Sinhalese” timelessly. *Pul Eliya* (1961) is a separate monographic treatment not yet ingested.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Legal vs customary mismatch.** Sinhalese state law vs village practice makes “is it polyandry?” a category fight.
- **Census vs ethnography.** Leach doubts Prince Peter’s claim that Ratnapura polyandry is the norm; 1946 Census sex-ratios of married persons do not show numerical dominance.
- **Evidence quality.** Much of the comparative case material is secondary (Aiyappan, D’Oyly, Bell, Parry); Leach’s Ceylon observations are short-field relative to classic monographs.
- **Gender and agency.** The 1950s debate is largely about jural fatherhood and property; women’s agency is thin in the sources Leach uses — flag as sampling/production bias.
