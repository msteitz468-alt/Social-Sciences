# Kaiko

## Definition as Coined

**Kaiko** is the Tsembaga Maring term (as recorded by [[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]], 1962–63) for the **pig festival** that culminates the multi-year ritual cycle. During the kaiko, large numbers of pigs are slaughtered and distributed; debts to ancestors and allies are addressed; dancing and visiting articulate regional relations. In Rappaport's analysis the kaiko is the hinge of [[ritual-regulation]].

## Semantic History

Emic festival name → analytic keystone of ecological anthropology via *[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]*. Textbook summaries often reify "the kaiko regulates pigs" as a simple homeostat slogan; the monograph's sequence (rumbim planting → herd growth → kaiko → uprooting → possible war) is more elaborate.

## Operationalizations

Rappaport timed herd size, labor burden, garden invasion by pigs, and festival staging among the [[tsembaga]]:

| Marker (1962–63) | Detail (attributed Rappaport) |
|---|---|
| Herd at kaiko outset (June 1962) | **169** pigs |
| Climax slaughter | *Konj kaiko* **7–8 Nov 1963**; ~**96** pigs killed those days |
| Herd at termination (Nov 1963) | ~60 juveniles + 15 adults (effective min ~60 juveniles; ≤40 without ethnographer's pigs) |
| Cycle length | ~5–20 years possible; Tsembaga historical mean ~12–15 years between festivals; ~8–12 years *rumbim*→uproot estimate |
| Purchase share | Only ~13% of 1962 herd bought outside the local population |

Not a universal Melanesian type — do not export the term without attribution.

## Applications

Central to the [[tsembaga]] society page and [[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-study]]; links local ecosystem (pigs/gardens) to regional system (allies, enemies, women, goods).

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether herd "pressure" *triggers* the kaiko (vs. political decision, ancestor debt, or prestige) is part of the critical literature Rappaport answers in the 1984 Epilogue ([[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]]).
