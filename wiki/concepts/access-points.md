# Access Points

## Definition as Coined

[[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (1990): **access points** are encounters between lay actors and the individuals or groups "responsible" for [[abstract-systems]] — the **meeting ground of facework and [[faceless-commitments]]**.

They are places of *vulnerability* for abstract systems (fallible persons represent the system) and junctions at which trust can be maintained or built up. Access-point demeanour typically displays trustworthiness, integrity, and "business-as-usual"/unflappability (Giddens illustrates with judicial gravity, medical professionalism, cabin-crew cheerfulness), with strict frontstage/backstage control (Goffman) as part of professionalism.

## Semantic History

Coined within the 1990 trust apparatus; builds Goffmanian interaction order into the theory of disembedding.

## Operationalizations

Illustrative, not scaled. Useful sensitising concept for ethnographies of clinics, airports, banks, courts, bureaucracies.

## Applications

- Explains how system trust is repaired or eroded in everyday interaction.
- Links to the "bargain with modernity" and limited possibilities of disengagement.
- Feeds reembedding among experts themselves (professional ethics; face-to-face updating of colleague trust).

## Critiques and Limitations

Thin formalisation; depends on Goffmanian micro-analysis for texture. Not a substitute for organisational ethnography.
