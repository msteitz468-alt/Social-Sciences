# Leveling Mechanisms

## Definition as Coined

**Leveling mechanisms** are practices that **check the crystallization of inequality**—preventing successful individuals from converting temporary advantage into durable rank or stratified control of resources. In forager ethnography the classic suite includes demand sharing, ridicule of pretension ([[insulting-the-meat]]), fission/mobility, and gift obligations.

*(Attributed densification — [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, especially Ch. 8.)* Lee’s core theoretical claim: despite large skill differentials among hunters, hierarchies of Big Men do not form. The !Kung are “**fiercely egalitarian**” and have evolved cultural practices that (1) cut arrogant/boastful hunters down to size and (2) help those down on their luck get back in the game. People rejoice when meat comes in, but self-aggrandizement is culturally blocked.

## Semantic History

Comparative term from mid-century economic and political anthropology; tightly associated with Fried’s [[egalitarian-society]] and with Lee’s Dobe material. Lee situates leveling inside the [[foraging-mode-of-production|foraging mode of production]]—generalized reciprocity, collective land access, tools owned by workers but food shared—rather than as freestanding “values.” Christmas ox episode (Ch. 1; Lee 1969b): humility-enforcing devices applied to the ethnographer as meat buyer, revealing that leveling targets pretension and aloof power, not only hunter pride.

## Operationalizations

Lee’s Dobe/ /Xai/xai package (document as 1960s–early 1970s ethnography):

### 1. Humility on return and insulting the meat

Successful hunters must not brag. Informant scripts: /Gaugo (“maybe just a tiny one”); /Xashe (“scratched its elbow”). Carrying parties banter about the “miserably small kill” and “pile of bones” while fully intending to carry the carcass. #Tomazho’s rationale: refuse the boaster so pride will not make him kill somebody; speak of meat as worthless to “cool his heart and make him gentle.” Full treatment: [[insulting-the-meat]].

### 2. Arrow ownership, [[hxaro]], and diffusion of meat credit

Rule: **owner of the arrow = owner of the meat** (right of formal distribution), even if another man shot it. Arrows circulate in hxaro so that glory and distribution risk spread. Women may own arrows and become meat owners. Lee: formal distribution brings prestige **and** risk of stinginess accusations; practices that diffuse responsibility are a blessing. Marshall (1976:297, quoted by Lee): society seems to want to extinguish the concept of the meat belonging to the hunter. 1964 quiver inventories of four Dobe men showed wide sharing (two had no arrows of their own).

### 3. Hunting magic and recovery aids for the unlucky

Retouching first-buck tattoos with fresh medicine; rubbing small-kill blood on the bow; discarding and remaking bow/quiver; oracle-disk divination before re-entry; correct butchery/distribution of men’s portions. Lee treats magic as modestly restoring confidence that unseen forces favor the quest—egalitarian-adjacent because it supports those down on luck without creating offices.

### 4. On-again, off-again hunting rhythm

Despite cooperative sharing and humility devices, men’s hunting work is erratic: good hunters may not hunt for weeks or months while others feed their households (e.g. #+Toma stopped 3 months in 1963 because hunting power was “cold”; Kumsa stopped months in 1968 and was fed by !Goshe kin). Lee concurs with Marshall that a too-energetic hunter may eventually draw envy; after hosting several distributions a hunter may stop to give others the limelight and enjoy reciprocal hospitality. Lee reads the two-phase burst/cool pattern as illustrating an economy based on **use value** rather than exchange value—pressure both to prove oneself by generous hunting **and** not to get too far ahead.

### 5. Fission / “vote with their feet”

In spatial organization (Ch. 12), larger camps are more dispute-prone (/Xai/xai 100–150: serious disputes ~every 2 weeks in 1968–9 vs Dobe/Mahopa 40–60: 3–4 times/year). Foragers can walk out of unpleasant situations “not when their food supply is exhausted, but well before that point, when only their patience is exhausted.” Camp splits when sharing cleavages persist (including mongongo flow interruptions as barometers of conflict, Ch. 7).

### 6. Leadership norms (anti-arrogance)

Leaders persuade, never enforce. Attributes that disqualify: arrogance, overbearingness, boastfulness, aloofness—may bring ostracism. Leaders are not more wealthy or leisured (“egalitarian redistributor” / modest band leader). Word *//kaiha* (“chief,” from *//kai* “wealth”) applied to Bantu/European rulers; of !Kung usually derisory. /Twi!gum: “we are all headmen… each one of us is headman over himself!”

### Skill variance that leveling must handle

Lee documents large success differentials so that leveling is not “there is no inequality of skill”:

- Of 127 bow-and-arrow hunters, 37 never killed a kudu; 43 killed 10+; those **34%** of hunters account for ~**79%** of kudu kills (Lee’s estimate from Table 8.6 logic).
- Better half of hunters over years: upward of two-thirds of meat; below-average half: about one-third.
- Long-run average only ~2–3 large antelope/year per hunter (Lee; may err high—Wilmsen’s /Xai/xai study: ~0.6 large animal per man per year).
- Age raises success but within oldest cohorts many low-kill men remain.

## Applications

- Explains how [[hunter-gatherer]] camps sustain relative equality despite variance in hunting skill and despite contact with stratified neighbours (Herero, Tswana).
- Links [[food-sharing]], [[insulting-the-meat]], [[hxaro]], and informal leadership into one reproductive system for [[egalitarian-society]].
- Used comparatively for demand sharing, ridicule of pretension, and gift drain in other immediate-return foragers.
- Christmas ox case: leveling as bidirectional—applied to ethnographer as well as native big-man aspirants (*[[lee-kung-san-study]]*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- “Leveling” can romanticize. Lee himself records homicide (~22 cases 1920–55), nonfatal fights, gender division (hunting as men’s occupation; reserved men’s meat portions by age ≥~35), and elder control open questions (Ch. 5: whether Australian-style male dominance constitutes “exploitation” left open; provisional claim that hierarchy appears far less developed in foraging societies as a group than in any other kind of human society).
- Clientage under pastoralists, cattle-post //kaihas recognized by Tswana without equivalent !Kung standing, and “outside” entrepreneurial leaders complicate the pure package under contact (Lee Ch. 12).
- Revisionist Kalahari work asks whether leveling describes a pristine forager ethic or a response to marginalization—keep as position, not wiki-voice verdict ([[kalahari-debate]]).
- Hard to quantify as a single index; operationalize as case-based speech events, sharing rules, residence shifts, sanctions of accumulation.
- Date-stamp all Dobe figures to fieldwork years (1963–4, 1967–9, 1973) and Wilmsen kill rates to 1973/1975–6.
