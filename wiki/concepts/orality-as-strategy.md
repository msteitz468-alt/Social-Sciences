# Orality as Strategy

## Definition as Coined

**Orality as strategy** is this wiki’s slug for [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]]’s argument in *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* (2009), Chapter **6½ “Orality, Writing, and Texts”**: the absence (or abandonment) of writing and fixed texts can be a **positionality vis-à-vis state formation** — a “jellyfish” variant of culture that maximizes room for maneuver in history, genealogy, and [[legibility]], parallel to [[escape-agriculture]] and escape social structure. Scott prefers **nonliteracy** or **orality** over “illiteracy” to mark a potentially positive medium rather than mere deficiency. All claims below are **attributed** to Scott (and authors he cites).

**Postliteracy (von Geusau via Scott):** Many hill peoples may be not preliterate but *postliterate* — having left texts behind through flight and changed social structure; the radical possibility is **active or strategic** abandonment of literacy. Scott states evidence for strategic maintenance (if not creation) of nonliteracy is almost entirely **circumstantial**, which is why he bracketed the chapter — yet holds the case is cut from the same cloth as swidden and dispersal.

## Semantic History

- **Civilizational stigma:** Lowland elites treat nonliteracy as a diagnostic of “barbarism”; bringing preliterate peoples into letters is a raison d’être of the developmental state.
- **Lost-script legends:** Widespread hill oral accounts (Akha, Lahu, Wa, Karen, Khmu, Chin, Hmong/Mien, etc.) that the people once had writing and lost it through improvidence or treachery — strategic positioning vis-à-vis valley kingdoms, sometimes claiming past padi-state life (kings, irrigated rice, writing).
- **Comparative literacy loss:** Greek Dark Age abandonment of Linear B after palace collapse; post-Roman partial loss of Latin literacy when imperial career incentives crumbled — literacy “hangs by a thread” when confined to a tiny elite (<1%) dependent on bureaucracy and clergy.
- **Scott’s inversion:** Standard narratives treat literacy acquisition as a one-way civilizational trip; Scott treats relationship to history as **diagnostic of relationship to stateness** — written traditions serve permanent centralization; oral traditions favor fleet-footed adjustment. “Peoples without history” stigma is rejected: how much history a people have is an **active choice** positioning them vis-à-vis text-based neighbors, not a low evolutionary stage.
- Links career-wise to [[legibility]] (lists, census, cadastral maps as elementary statecraft; Uruk economic tablets) and to *Seeing Like a State* / [[high-modernism]] only as affinity, not as the 2009 chapter’s label.

## Operationalizations

| Dimension | Scott 2009 claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Writing and the padi state | Hard to conceive a padi state without cadastral maps, corvée lists, receipts, codes — “lists, lists, lists”; population/household census as elementary statecraft |
| Lévi-Strauss via Scott | Writing appears necessary for the centralized stratified state to reproduce itself; seems to favor exploitation more than enlightenment |
| Disadvantages of fixed text | Permanence → orthodoxy; “dug up” fossils impede diplomacy when alliances change; privileges literate scribes |
| Advantages of orality | No single gold-standard genealogy/history; democratic distribution of storytelling; inherent presentness; selective forgetting with less friction; divergence when groups split |
| High-fidelity option | Orality can also preserve high fidelity when desired (Homeric/Serbian bardic technique; Akha–Hani mutual intelligibility after centuries; PaO historical chant) |
| Genealogical portfolio | Oral genealogies as shadow-ancestor portfolios for alliance bricolage; written genealogy freezes one claim |
| Peoples with less history | Lisu “active forgetting” (Jonsson); Vansina on Burundi vs Rwanda — fluid systems have less interest in detailed oral tradition |
| Oscillation | Text-based ↔ oral (as wet-rice ↔ swidden, hierarchical ↔ egalitarian) as advantages of states wax and wane |

**Refusal as strategy:** “To refuse or to abandon writing and literacy is one strategy among many for remaining out of reach of the state,” preferring knowledges that resist bureaucratic codification. Peasant rebellions often first attack paper documents; Akha narrative marks King Jabiolang’s great crime as initiating a yearly census.

## Applications

- **[[zomia]] / [[state-evasion]]:** Oral plasticity as cultural technology of the nonstate periphery alongside [[swidden]] and dispersal.
- **[[ethnogenesis]]:** Shape-shifting histories and genealogies enable identity repertoire performance; no authentic single Kachin tradition (Leach via Scott) — versions track vested interests.
- **[[legibility]]:** Writing as infrastructure of appropriation; stable named identity units as state effects.
- **Comparative maroon/stateless parallels:** Roma/Gypsies cited for rich orality without fixed history/shrines under persecution.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scott’s own caveat:** Strategic nonliteracy is the most speculative strand of the 2009 program — circumstantial evidence; do not promote to wiki voice.
- **Not all orality is anti-state:** Valley majorities long lived in oral culture inflected by elite literacy; oral epics can underwrite kings and empires as easily as fugitives.
- **Fidelity vs plasticity:** Scott notes both; critics may charge cherry-picking of “jellyfish” cases (Lisu) over high-fidelity ritual specialists.
- **Lost-script legends** may be ideological charter myths rather than historical postliteracy; Scott treats germ-of-truth as conceivable, not proven.
