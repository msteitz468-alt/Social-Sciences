# Bonding Social Capital

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Putnam 2000.)* **Bonding** (exclusive) social capital: inward-looking networks that reinforce exclusive identities and homogeneous groups. Examples Putnam gives: ethnic fraternal organizations, church-based women's reading groups, fashionable country clubs. Good for specific reciprocity and mobilizing solidarity ("getting by," per Xavier de Souza Briggs); can also underwrite sectarianism and exclusion — the dark side treated in *Bowling Alone* ch. 22.

Paired opposite: [[bridging-social-capital]]. Parent concept: [[civic-social-capital]]. Contrast Bourdieusian [[social-capital]] (different object).

## Semantic History

Popularized as a pair with bridging in late-1990s social-capital discourse; Putnam's formulation became the public-facing standard. Related older ideas: in-group solidarity, ethnic enclave networks, exclusive club membership — not always labeled "social capital."

## Operationalizations

Membership in homogeneous or exclusive associations; dense enclave network measures; sometimes inferred from racial/ethnic/religious homophily of ties. Putnam notes national series rarely separate bonding from bridging cleanly.

## Applications

- Ethnic enclave support and entrepreneurial labor markets (Putnam's illustration).
- Dark-side analysis of illiberal or exclusionary communities (ch. 22).
- Policy debates that treat "community" as ambiguous without the bonding/bridging split.

## Critiques and Limitations

Bonding is not inherently bad; bridging is not inherently good — both can harm or help. Measurement of the split is weak in most survey series. Feminist and racial critiques of mid-century "high SC" often target bonding stocks that excluded outsiders ([[bowling-alone-debate]]).
