# Applied Anthropology

## Definition as Coined

**Applied anthropology** names anthropological knowledge produced for practical use by sponsors outside the pure research university — historically including colonial administrations (indirect rule, native courts, labour and land policy), wartime and development agencies, and later NGOs and corporations. In *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (1973), applied work is a central mechanism linking the discipline to colonial power, not a marginal side-career.

## Semantic History

- **Interwar–postwar professionalisation:** British social anthropology advertised utility to colonial governments; posts and institutes (e.g. Rhodes-Livingstone Institute) blurred pure/applied lines.
- **1960s–70s critique:** New Left and Asad-volume essays treat "application" as structural complicity or at best ambivalent service ("reluctant imperialist").
- **Late 20th–21st c.:** Rebranded as development, medical, business, or public anthropology; ethical codes try to distance contemporary practice from colonial applied roles while the historical critique remains live on [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].

## Operationalizations

- Formal employment or consultancy under colonial/native affairs departments
- Government-funded research institutes with administrative reporting lines
- Monographs and surveys written to answer administrative questions (customary law, chieftainship, taxation, "detribalisation")

## Applications

Asad 1973 case material includes Eastern Nigeria administrative ethnography (Lackner), Nadel in the Sudan (Faris), Godfrey Wilson at the RLI (Brown), and broader sponsorship analysis (Feuchtwang, James).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Related late-century case: [[ferguson-james|Ferguson]] treats the international development industry and rural projects (Thaba-Tseka) as objects of anthropological analysis rather than as clients for improvement — a reflexive move continuous with Asad-volume concerns about knowledge–power ([[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]; [[anti-politics-machine]]).

- Pure/applied binary understates how "pure" functionalist theory was shaped inside colonial fields.
- Individual anthropologists' resistance to particular policies does not dissolve structural location (James's reluctance thesis).
- Postcolonial applied/development anthropology inherits and reworks, rather than simply exits, the same knowledge–power problem.

## From Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists* (2015)

*(Attributed — [[kuper-adam|Kuper]] 2015 Ch. 4–5; source [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]]. Historiography of British colonial applied anthropology, not a verdict on individual complicity.)*

- **Elective affinity and market.** Kuper frames Malinowski’s 1927 reading of Lugard’s *Dual Mandate* as identifying an elective affinity between functionalism and Indirect Rule (“Complete Surrender to the Functional Point of View”). Empire as a labour market: diploma courses for colonial cadets (Haddon, Marett); 1909–1913 RAI/BA lobbying for a national school of applied anthropology (“Tout Savoir, Tout Pardonner… Tout Gagner”) failed. India Office/Maine tradition of official research contrasted with Colonial Office philistinism in Africa/Oceania; cautionary Northcote Thomas episode; government anthropologists (Rattray, Meek) never a West African fixture.
- **IAI–Rockefeller–Malinowski monopoly.** Oldham/Lugard recruited Malinowski; Rockefeller funded LSE “applied anthropology” fellowships for Africa; Malinowski cornered the market, shutting out Seligman and competing R-B bids. IAI five-year plan largely drafted by [[richards-audrey|Richards]]; 1931 fellows (Fortes, Nadel, Richards, [[mair-lucy|Mair]], Wilsons, Hilda Kuper, et al.) — **none** made a career in colonial service. Actual applied output thin: Fortes on Tallensi marriage law; Nadel on courts/tax/Nuba amenability (Evans-Pritchard suspected job-angling); Richards *Land, Labour and Diet* and *Bemba Marriage*; Mair sent for culture change, later Hailey collaborator and LSE applied teacher. Prestige hierarchy: applied = less serious than [[social-structure]] (Richards and Mair acknowledged).
- **Use and non-use.** Seligman/Evans-Pritchard unused by Sudan government; Schapera worked closely with Bechuanaland administration; RLI (1938) applied centre, Wilson then reluctant Gluckman, publications often more academic than administrative; Gluckman 1947 signed Evans-Pritchard’s **campaign against** applied anthropology. Hailey *African Survey* (1938; team incl. Richards, Mair) reserved on anthropologists’ utility; Colonial Development and Welfare Act / CSSRC postwar grants (Firth + Richards) expanded social-anthropology fellowships while social/economic research remained ~9% of colonial research spend.
- **Backlash and Nadel 1955.** After Nadel’s London applied-anthropology lecture (1955), West African students violently attacked him and the discipline for sanctioning policy to “keep the African down” — emblem of the 1960s African-intellectual charge that anthropology propped traditional rulers and colonial rule (feeds [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]). Kuper’s position: collaborationist/spy charge overstated; loose elective affinity/symbiosis without consistent spy-service pattern.

## From Stocking, *After Tylor* (1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1995, Ch. 8, "Anthropological Institutions, Colonial Interests, and the First Cohorts of Social Anthropologists"; source [[stocking-after-tylor-1995]]. Institutional/market history, not a verdict on individual complicity.)*

- **Origins of the label.** Sir Richard Temple pushed institutionally for a "School of Applied Anthropology" (Cambridge Board of Anthropological Studies, 1904; British Association session, Birmingham, 1913), conceived in mentalistic terms as "tact" and "sympathy" — a "habit of mind" for administrators, missionaries, and traders. Malinowski's own capitalized coinage, "Practical Anthropology," dates to a 1929 article in the newly founded organ of the International African Institute, pitching functional theory's ability to solve land-tenure and labor problems for colonial administrators fearing "black bolshevism."
- **Mixed-to-disappointing practical record.** Government anthropologists — Northcote Thomas (Nigeria/Sierra Leone, 1908–1915), C. K. Meek (Nigeria), R. S. Rattray (Gold Coast), F. E. Williams (Papua) — produced valuable ethnography but had limited administrative influence and were never systematically institutionalized as a career path. Gordon Brown's *Anthropology in Action* (1935, Tanganyika) and the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute (est. 1937; directors Godfrey Wilson, then Max Gluckman) are Stocking's case studies of the program's frustrations: Wilson's fieldwork among urban copper miners was restricted after a 1935 strike in which soldiers killed seventeen miners, and he was barred from further access on the grounds his methods, and his status as a conscientious objector, "might cause discontent and unrest."
- **Postwar retrenchment.** Audrey Richards' 1944 retrospective judged the African Institute's practical program only a "moderate success," comparing Malinowski's promotional efforts to "advertising his goods... in a market in which there was little demand for them." By the late 1940s Evans-Pritchard and Firth both argued for anthropology's primarily academic/scientific value over administrative utility — Firth stating anthropologists were interested "in understanding social process, not directing it" — marking a shift toward applied anthropology redefined in narrower, academic terms.
- **Radcliffe-Brown's counter-position.** In a 1930 lecture on "Applied Anthropology," Radcliffe-Brown argued "pure science" should have priority over practical application at that stage of the discipline's development — a position institutionally in competition with Malinowski's culture-contact/applied program for Rockefeller funding (see [[salvage-ethnography]]).
- **Rockefeller funding as the market mechanism.** Malinowski's LSE chair and seminar, and the discipline's applied program generally, were substantially funded through Rockefeller Spelman Memorial money and a 1931 $250,000 Rockefeller commitment to the International African Institute (jointly sought with J. H. Oldham); total direct Rockefeller support to anthropology reached roughly $2.5 million by 1938, dwarfing the Royal Anthropological Institute's own funding. Stocking treats this as central to the discipline's *institutionalization*, while explicitly bracketing "the actual utilization of functionalist anthropology by colonial administrators, as opposed to its putative utility for the colonial system," as outside his narrative's scope.
