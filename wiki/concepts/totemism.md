# Totemism

## Definition as Coined

**Totemism** entered European ethnology in the nineteenth century as a label for systems linking social groups (esp. clans) to natural species or emblems. In *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms]]* (1912), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] treats **Australian clan totemism** (as in Spencer & Gillen et al.) as the **elementary form of religion**: the totem is name and emblem; totemic things are sacred; the totemic principle is an impersonal force (mana-like) that is ultimately **society itself** (attributed).

**Construct status:** In this wiki, Durkheimian totemism is an **etic analytical construct** built from colonial ethnography — not identical to any single Aboriginal people's self-understanding, language, or genetic population (four-way non-identity). Later anthropology contested the unity of "totemism" as one institution — decisively [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] (see below), building on Radcliffe-Brown.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| 19th-c. ethnology (McLennan, Frazer, etc.) | Evolutionary stage or type of "primitive" religion/social organization |
| Durkheim & Mauss 1903 | Totemic clans/moieties as genera/species of classification; things integrated into gentile divisions ([[primitive-classification]]) — prior to full religious theory of 1912 |
| Durkheim 1912 | Elementary religion; clan–species–emblem system revealing sacred = social |
| Mid–late 20th-c. anthropology | Often dissolved as artificial class; structural reinterpretations |
| Sociology textbooks | Still carries Durkheim's sense as classic case |

## Operationalizations

Clan membership × emblem/species prohibitions × ritual duties (intichiuma, etc.). Cross-cultural "totemism" indexes in HRAF-style work are contested for Galton and definitional reasons.

## Applications

- Durkheim's experimental case for general theory of religion and of the categories.
- Link to [[mechanical-solidarity|mechanical solidarity]] and segmental clan morphology (continuity with *Division of Labor*).
- *[[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-1903|Primitive Classification]]* (1903): Australian totemic organization as model of hierarchical classification of the universe (moiety/clan = genus/species); Frazer's reverse causal arrow rejected (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Ethnographic reliability:** dependence on Spencer & Gillen and period sources.
- **Universality:** Australia is not proven "simplest"; totemism not shown to be elementary everywhere.
- **Unitary concept abandoned** by much later anthropology while Durkheim's *sociological* point (sacred force as social) is often retained separately.

## Radcliffe-Brown's sociological theory of totemism (1929)

In "The Sociological Theory of Totemism" (Fourth Pacific Science Congress, 1929; in
*[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*), [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]
argued (attributed) that "totemism is not one thing but is a general name given to a number of
diverse institutions," and refused origin-speculation as "for ever incapable of inductive
verification." He praised [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|Elementary Forms]]*
as "an important and permanent contribution" but rejected its specific totemism theory: where
Durkheim held natural species become sacred *because* selected as clan emblems (suited to
graphic representation), Radcliffe-Brown held the reverse — species "are selected as
representatives of social groups... because they are already objects of the ritual attitude."
He substituted "**ritual relation**"/"ritual attitude" for Durkheim's "sacred," and stated a
general law: "Any object or event which has important effects upon the well-being... of a
society... tends to become an object of the ritual attitude." Totemism, on his account, is a
specialization of that general ritual relation to nature under two conditions — subsistence
dependence on wild species *and* segmentary (clan/moiety) organization — which is why the
Andamanese and Eskimo, having the first but not the second, lack it. See [[ritual-value]],
[[structural-functionalism]].

## Lévi-Strauss: the dissolution of totemism (1962)

[[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]], in *Totemism* and *[[levi-strauss-savage-mind-1962|The Savage Mind]]* (both 1962), delivered the influential structuralist verdict (attributed): **"totemism" is not a real institution but an illusion of earlier anthropology** — a heterogeneous set of facts wrongly unified. What is real is a universal **logic of classification**: natural species are used as a code for social differences. The relation is a **homology between two systems of differences** — "not between social groups and natural species but between the differences which manifest themselves on the level of groups on the one hand and on that of species on the other" — not a mystical identity between a clan and its totem. Species are chosen for their **conceptual value, not their utility** (the idea condensed in the slogan, from *Totemism*, that natural species are chosen because they are "good to think," against the functionalist "good to eat").

He paired this with the **totem/caste inversion**: totemic groups model social differences on a natural code and are exogamous, while castes model natural/occupational differences on a cultural code and are endogamous — "castes naturalize a true culture falsely, totemic groups culturalize a false nature truly." Totemic classification is one level (the natural-species level) of a classifying operation that runs from the universal down to the proper name (see [[science-of-the-concrete]], [[binary-opposition]]).

In *The Savage Mind* he also **contradicted Durkheim on the churinga** (attributed): where Durkheim held the Aranda sacred objects were sacred *because* they bore the totemic emblem, Lévi-Strauss (citing Strehlow's plain, unmarked churinga) held sacredness attaches "to the signified" — "a document does not become sacred by virtue of bearing a stamp... it bears the stamp because it has first been acknowledged to be sacred." See [[structuralism]].

## Sahlins: "bourgeois totemism" (1976)

[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], in *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976, attributed), extends Lévi-Strauss's account reflexively to the modern West. First, he argues Western evolutionary and biological social science "may be the highest form of totemism": "if totemism is, as Lévi-Strauss says, the explication of human society by the distinctions between species, then we have made an empirical science of it" — the West's narrative of rising "from savages" is a modern origin myth ("we are the only people who think themselves risen from savages; everyone else believes they descend from gods"). Second, in "[[la-pensee-bourgeoise|La Pensée Bourgeoise]]" he identifies a **"bourgeois totemism"** that *inverts* the classic relation: where totemic groups use differences among natural species to mark differences among social groups, market society puts "every conceivable distinction of society... to the service of another declension of objects" — class, sex, age, and occasion mapped onto manufactured-product differences (Cadillac/Chevrolet, suit/overalls, steak/entrails) to generate saleable, differentiated commodities. "Fetishism and totemism: the most refined creations of the civilized mind." See [[la-pensee-bourgeoise]], [[cultural-reason]].

## Rivers' diffusionist theory of totemism

[[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]] offered a distinctive account (attributed) in
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914). Puzzled that all
Melanesian totem-animals are indigenous fauna (so totemism could not have been imported
ready-made), he argued the immigrant [[kava-and-betel|kava-people]] brought not totemism itself
but a **belief in the incarnation of the dead in animal or plant form**, which crystallized
locally into totemic clans as each founding ancestor's descendants identified with the local
animal into which he was believed to have entered after death. He explained totemism's
association with matrilineal descent (though he held the immigrants patrilineal) by the logic of
intermarriage generation by generation, and read the Ingiet society's animal-form ancestor
images (Bismarck Archipelago) as the clearest surviving evidence. Like the rest of his scheme,
this belongs to the abandoned [[diffusionism|diffusionist]] program (see
[[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]).

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 4–6.)* Kuper reconstructs the **totemism** debates as a transformation of the [[primitive-society]] package after the matriarchy/exogamy consensus: McLennan linked totemism to matriliny and *jus connubii*; Robertson Smith (Semitic communion sacrifice); Frazer *Totemism* (1887) and *Golden Bough*; Australia as anthropological laboratory (Fison & Howitt; Spencer & Gillen — empirical pressure on the classic package); Tylor 1899 decoupling (exogamy without totemism); Frazer *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910); Freud *Totem and Taboo*; Durkheim *Elementary Forms* (Australian clan totemism as elementary religion). Kuper’s thesis: totemism functioned as a foundation myth / “Garden of Eden” for the social model, not as stable ethnography. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

## Descola: totemism as ontology (2005)

[[descola-philippe|Descola]] (*[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, Ch.7) accepts Lévi-Strauss's demolition of unitary "totemism" as a single institution but recovers **ontological** dimensions Lévi-Strauss set aside for classificatory analysis. Defining [[animism]] as resemblance of interiorities + difference of physicalities, he treats totemism as the cell in which **both interiorities and physicalities are continuous** within pre-differentiated hybrid classes — a "socio-natural image, single but fragmented," homology of *terms* (clan is like eagle) rather than only of *relations between differences*. Australia (Dreaming as continuous cosmogenic framework; Elkin inventory; Brandenstein taxonomic semantics) is exemplary; Algonquin cases illustrate **non-exclusive** minor totemic presence inside animist regimes. This is a combinatorial reading within [[four-ontologies]], not a return to evolutionary totemism-as-stage. See [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]].

## Ingold: totemic vs animic (2000)

[[ingold-tim|Ingold]] (*[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]*, Ch. 7) contrasts **totemic** and **animic** orientations in depiction and land relations (form already in land / essential vs life generating form / dialogical), drawing Aboriginal and northern materials. Not a return to unitary institutional totemism; a dwelling-perspective typology parallel to but not identical with Descola's ontological cells. See [[dwelling-perspective]], [[animism]].
