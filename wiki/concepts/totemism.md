# Totemism

## Definition as Coined

**Totemism** entered European ethnology in the nineteenth century as a label for systems linking social groups (esp. clans) to natural species or emblems. In *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms]]* (1912), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] treats **Australian clan totemism** (as in Spencer & Gillen et al.) as the **elementary form of religion**: the totem is name and emblem; totemic things are sacred; the totemic principle is an impersonal force (mana-like) that is ultimately **society itself** (attributed).

**Construct status:** In this wiki, Durkheimian totemism is an **etic analytical construct** built from colonial ethnography — not identical to any single Aboriginal people's self-understanding, language, or genetic population (four-way non-identity). Later anthropology contested the unity of "totemism" as one institution — decisively [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] (see below), building on Radcliffe-Brown.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| 19th-c. ethnology (McLennan, Frazer, etc.) | Evolutionary stage or type of "primitive" religion/social organization |
| Durkheim & Mauss 1903 | Totemic clans/moieties as genera/species of classification; things integrated into gentile divisions ([[primitive-classification]]) — prior to full religious theory of 1912 |
| Durkheim 1912 | Elementary religion; clan–species–emblem system revealing sacred = social |
| Mid–late 20th-c. anthropology | Often dissolved as artificial class; structural reinterpretations |
| Sociology textbooks | Still carries Durkheim's sense as classic case |

## Operationalizations

Clan membership × emblem/species prohibitions × ritual duties (intichiuma, etc.). Cross-cultural "totemism" indexes in HRAF-style work are contested for Galton and definitional reasons.

## Applications

- Durkheim's experimental case for general theory of religion and of the categories.
- Link to [[mechanical-solidarity|mechanical solidarity]] and segmental clan morphology (continuity with *Division of Labor*).
- *[[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-1903|Primitive Classification]]* (1903): Australian totemic organization as model of hierarchical classification of the universe (moiety/clan = genus/species); Frazer's reverse causal arrow rejected (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Ethnographic reliability:** dependence on Spencer & Gillen and period sources.
- **Universality:** Australia is not proven "simplest"; totemism not shown to be elementary everywhere.
- **Unitary concept abandoned** by much later anthropology while Durkheim's *sociological* point (sacred force as social) is often retained separately.

## McLennan's underdeveloped bequest and Frazer's three theories (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995)

(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]], 1995.) Stocking traces the concept's underdeveloped origin to J. F. McLennan (d. 1881), who defined totemism as a form of fetishism linked to (1) one fetish per tribe, (2) matrilineal hereditary transmission, and (3) association with exogamy — leaving the "why exogamy" question unresolved at his death. [[robertson-smith-william|Robertson Smith]] and Spencer and Gillen's later Arunta ethnography both built on this unresolved bequest (see [[robertson-smith-william]]).

[[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] himself offered **three successive, non-cumulative theories** of totemism's origin, each tied to a different phase of *The Golden Bough*'s editions: (1) c. 1890, an "external soul" theory (drawing on Edward Clodd and G. A. Wilken), later dismissed by Frazer himself as "at most only a partial solution"; (2) after 1896, stimulated by Spencer and Gillen's Arunta material, totemism as a system for the magical multiplication of food species by division of labor — the theory that led Frazer to conclude "magic has everywhere preceded religion"; (3) by 1905, the "conceptional theory," deriving totemism from Arunta ignorance of physiological paternity (totem assigned by where the mother believed conception occurred, not by descent). [[lang-andrew|Andrew Lang]] rejected all three in favor of a McLennan/Atkinson-derived account and treated the Arunta material as anomalous rather than paradigmatic — a position Stocking reads as anticipating both Boas's "premature classification" critique and Alexander Goldenweiser's empirical dismantling of the category.

**Goldenweiser's 1911 critique.** The Boasian Alexander Goldenweiser showed the supposedly defining features of totemism (exogamous clans, totemic names, religious regard, totem taboos, hereditary descent) co-occur only in a small minority of world cases — an early empirical demonstration that "totemism" as a unified cross-cultural institution does not hold up, prefiguring Lévi-Strauss's later structuralist dissolution of the category (below).

**Lévi-Strauss's retrospective dismissal.** Beyond the structuralist reanalysis recorded below, Lévi-Strauss later (undated, retrospective remark reported by Stocking) questioned whether totemism was ever a "real" phenomenon at all rather than a construct of "the Victorian evolutionary imagination," comparing the nineteenth-century vogue for "totemism" to that for "hysteria." Freud's *Totem and Taboo* and Durkheim's *The Elementary Forms of the Religious Life* are both noted by Stocking as stimulated in part by Frazer's totemism work and as carrying the debate into the interwar period.

## Boas: totemism as an artificial composite, not an evolutionary stage (1910/1916)

*(Attributed — [[boas-franz|Boas]], "Ethnological Problems in Canada" (1910) and "The Origin of Totemism" essays (1904/1910/1916), collected in *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]*, 1940.)*

Boas rejected the standard unilineal-evolutionary claim that totemism and maternal descent represent a universal earlier stage later superseded by paternal descent — arguing that the tendency of a trait to disappear once abandoned does not prove it was ever universal ("in many parts of the world they may never have existed"). He presented a North American counter-example: the least politically complex tribes (Mackenzie Basin) have paternal descent and no totemism, while the most politically elaborate (per John R. Swanton's eastern-U.S. data) have maternal descent and highly developed totemism — the reverse of the predicted sequence. Citing his own earlier work and confirmations by C. Hill-Tout and Father A. G. Morice, Boas argued British Columbia tribes had in some cases **adopted** totemism and maternal descent by diffusion from a prior paternal-descent base — a late acquisition, not an archaic survival (see [[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition]] for the regional case).

Boas's positive claim: "totemism" is **not a single psychologically unified phenomenon** but a label covering a contingent co-occurrence of two independent elements — exogamous incest-avoidance groupings, and particular religious ideas about ancestral/guardian beings. Where only one element occurs, no "totemism" develops. "Totemism is an artificial unit, not a natural one" (p. 318) — "a product of peculiar combinations of cultural traits that develop here and there," not a single evolutionary stage. He endorsed Alexander Goldenweiser's regional-variability critique (see the Goldenweiser section above) but went further, rejecting even Goldenweiser's line between "totemism proper" (human–animal relations) and the broader class of exogamic-marking customs.

**Distinguishing his problem from Wundt's/Durkheim's.** Boas explicitly disclaimed authorship of a unified "American theory of totemism" that Frazer and Durkheim attributed jointly to him, Alice C. Fletcher, and Charles Hill-Tout (deriving clan totems from individual guardian-spirit/manitou concepts extended over kinship groups) — he said this began as a specific observation about Kwakiutl material that Fletcher and Hill-Tout independently generalized. He also distinguished his own totemism problem (association of ethnic activities with exogamous kinship, generation, or locality groups) from Wundt's and Durkheim's "totemic viewpoint" (centered on man–animal identification), calling the latter "an entirely different problem" only incidentally connected to totemism as a social institution — a methodological move that anticipated Radcliffe-Brown's later "not one thing but... a number of diverse institutions" (below) and Lévi-Strauss's dissolution of the category.

**Proposed mechanism (explicitly speculative).** Boas suggested unilateral descent itself derives from post-marital residence patterns set by subsistence/property conditions (property in hunting territory favoring paternal descent; property in agricultural land favoring maternal descent) — offered as a possible explanation for maternal descent's prevalence among North American agricultural tribes, but explicitly framed as inference ("possibly"), not established fact. Once a unilateral descent system exists, group growth creates pressure to mark exogamic/incest-group boundaries (once individual genealogical tracing becomes impractical), and totemic markers (names, rituals, crests) serve this classificatory function.

## Radcliffe-Brown's sociological theory of totemism (1929)

In "The Sociological Theory of Totemism" (Fourth Pacific Science Congress, 1929; in
*[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*), [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]
argued (attributed) that "totemism is not one thing but is a general name given to a number of
diverse institutions," and refused origin-speculation as "for ever incapable of inductive
verification." He praised [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|Elementary Forms]]*
as "an important and permanent contribution" but rejected its specific totemism theory: where
Durkheim held natural species become sacred *because* selected as clan emblems (suited to
graphic representation), Radcliffe-Brown held the reverse — species "are selected as
representatives of social groups... because they are already objects of the ritual attitude."
He substituted "**ritual relation**"/"ritual attitude" for Durkheim's "sacred," and stated a
general law: "Any object or event which has important effects upon the well-being... of a
society... tends to become an object of the ritual attitude." Totemism, on his account, is a
specialization of that general ritual relation to nature under two conditions — subsistence
dependence on wild species *and* segmentary (clan/moiety) organization — which is why the
Andamanese and Eskimo, having the first but not the second, lack it. See [[ritual-value]],
[[structural-functionalism]].

## Lévi-Strauss: the dissolution of totemism (1962)

[[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]], in *Totemism* and *[[levi-strauss-savage-mind-1962|The Savage Mind]]* (both 1962), delivered the influential structuralist verdict (attributed): **"totemism" is not a real institution but an illusion of earlier anthropology** — a heterogeneous set of facts wrongly unified. What is real is a universal **logic of classification**: natural species are used as a code for social differences. The relation is a **homology between two systems of differences** — "not between social groups and natural species but between the differences which manifest themselves on the level of groups on the one hand and on that of species on the other" — not a mystical identity between a clan and its totem. Species are chosen for their **conceptual value, not their utility** (the idea condensed in the slogan, from *Totemism*, that natural species are chosen because they are "good to think," against the functionalist "good to eat").

He paired this with the **totem/caste inversion**: totemic groups model social differences on a natural code and are exogamous, while castes model natural/occupational differences on a cultural code and are endogamous — "castes naturalize a true culture falsely, totemic groups culturalize a false nature truly." Totemic classification is one level (the natural-species level) of a classifying operation that runs from the universal down to the proper name (see [[science-of-the-concrete]], [[binary-opposition]]).

In *The Savage Mind* he also **contradicted Durkheim on the churinga** (attributed): where Durkheim held the Aranda sacred objects were sacred *because* they bore the totemic emblem, Lévi-Strauss (citing Strehlow's plain, unmarked churinga) held sacredness attaches "to the signified" — "a document does not become sacred by virtue of bearing a stamp... it bears the stamp because it has first been acknowledged to be sacred." See [[structuralism]].

## Sahlins: "bourgeois totemism" (1976)

[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], in *[[sahlins-culture-and-practical-reason-1976|Culture and Practical Reason]]* (1976, attributed), extends Lévi-Strauss's account reflexively to the modern West. First, he argues Western evolutionary and biological social science "may be the highest form of totemism": "if totemism is, as Lévi-Strauss says, the explication of human society by the distinctions between species, then we have made an empirical science of it" — the West's narrative of rising "from savages" is a modern origin myth ("we are the only people who think themselves risen from savages; everyone else believes they descend from gods"). Second, in "[[la-pensee-bourgeoise|La Pensée Bourgeoise]]" he identifies a **"bourgeois totemism"** that *inverts* the classic relation: where totemic groups use differences among natural species to mark differences among social groups, market society puts "every conceivable distinction of society... to the service of another declension of objects" — class, sex, age, and occasion mapped onto manufactured-product differences (Cadillac/Chevrolet, suit/overalls, steak/entrails) to generate saleable, differentiated commodities. "Fetishism and totemism: the most refined creations of the civilized mind." See [[la-pensee-bourgeoise]], [[cultural-reason]].

## Rivers' diffusionist theory of totemism

[[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]] offered a distinctive account (attributed) in
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914). Puzzled that all
Melanesian totem-animals are indigenous fauna (so totemism could not have been imported
ready-made), he argued the immigrant [[kava-and-betel|kava-people]] brought not totemism itself
but a **belief in the incarnation of the dead in animal or plant form**, which crystallized
locally into totemic clans as each founding ancestor's descendants identified with the local
animal into which he was believed to have entered after death. He explained totemism's
association with matrilineal descent (though he held the immigrants patrilineal) by the logic of
intermarriage generation by generation, and read the Ingiet society's animal-form ancestor
images (Bismarck Archipelago) as the clearest surviving evidence. Like the rest of his scheme,
this belongs to the abandoned [[diffusionism|diffusionist]] program (see
[[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]).

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 4–6.)* Kuper reconstructs the **totemism** debates as a transformation of the [[primitive-society]] package after the matriarchy/exogamy consensus: McLennan linked totemism to matriliny and *jus connubii*; Robertson Smith (Semitic communion sacrifice); Frazer *Totemism* (1887) and *Golden Bough*; Australia as anthropological laboratory (Fison & Howitt; Spencer & Gillen — empirical pressure on the classic package); Tylor 1899 decoupling (exogamy without totemism); Frazer *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910); Freud *Totem and Taboo*; Durkheim *Elementary Forms* (Australian clan totemism as elementary religion). Kuper’s thesis: totemism functioned as a foundation myth / “Garden of Eden” for the social model, not as stable ethnography. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

## Descola: totemism as ontology (2005)

[[descola-philippe|Descola]] (*[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, Ch.7) accepts Lévi-Strauss's demolition of unitary "totemism" as a single institution but recovers **ontological** dimensions Lévi-Strauss set aside for classificatory analysis. Defining [[animism]] as resemblance of interiorities + difference of physicalities, he treats totemism as the cell in which **both interiorities and physicalities are continuous** within pre-differentiated hybrid classes — a "socio-natural image, single but fragmented," homology of *terms* (clan is like eagle) rather than only of *relations between differences*. Australia (Dreaming as continuous cosmogenic framework; Elkin inventory; Brandenstein taxonomic semantics) is exemplary; Algonquin cases illustrate **non-exclusive** minor totemic presence inside animist regimes. This is a combinatorial reading within [[four-ontologies]], not a return to evolutionary totemism-as-stage. See [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]].

## Ingold: totemic vs animic (2000)

[[ingold-tim|Ingold]] (*[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]*, Ch. 7) contrasts **totemic** and **animic** orientations in depiction and land relations (form already in land / essential vs life generating form / dialogical), drawing Aboriginal and northern materials. Not a return to unitary institutional totemism; a dwelling-perspective typology parallel to but not identical with Descola's ontological cells. See [[dwelling-perspective]], [[animism]].

## Evans-Pritchard: Nuer totemism against utilitarian and Durkheimian readings (1956)

*(Attributed — [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]], *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]*, 1956; fieldwork among the Nuer of the southern Sudan, 1930–36.)*

Evans-Pritchard's core thesis: "it is essential to an understanding of Nuer totemism that it should be appreciated that Nuer respect the natural species or class of objects because they regard them as being in some manner emblems or representations of Spirit." He explicitly distinguished the **totem** (the creature, *pwony*) from the **totemic spirit** (*kwoth*): "Nuer quite explicitly distinguish between them... it is not the species themselves, the material things one can see, that they pray and sacrifice to but the spirits associated with them... while the species are creatures on the earth the spirits are with God in the sky." Illustrative Nuer quote: "*jale lony pwonyde piny, cu kwothde jen a nhial*" — "the lion in its body (as a creature) walks on the earth, while its spirit is in the sky." Technically, he distinguished "totemic" (a relationship between a lineage and a species/class) from "totemistic" (the same relationship held by an individual); Nuer classification separates the totemic/totemistic spirits proper (*kuth piny*, spirits of the below) from nature sprites (*bieli*/*hieli*) and fetishes (*kulangni*, "medicines").

**Against Malinowski and Radcliffe-Brown.** Evans-Pritchard rejected functionalist/utilitarian readings of totem selection: "The facts of Nuer totemism do not, therefore, support the contention of those who see in totemism chiefly, or even merely, a ritualization of empirical interests" — footnoted directly against [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] (*Science, Religion and Reality*, 1926) and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] (Fourth Pacific Science Congress, 1929; see the Radcliffe-Brown section above). He aligned instead with Durkheim's own protest against utilitarian interpretations (citing *Sociology and Philosophy*, Eng. trans. 1954, p. 86), while rejecting Durkheim's positive theory (below). Evidence offered: recorded Nuer totems include lion, waterbuck, monitor lizard, crocodile, python and other snakes, tortoise, ostrich, cattle egret, weaver-birds, several tree species, papyrus, gourd, fish, bee, red soldier ant, rivers, cattle with certain markings, and more — and "there is no marked utilitarian element in their selection... those creatures which have excited the mythopoeic imagination of the Nuer... do not figure, or figure rarely and insignificantly, among their totems."

**Against a Durkheimian emblem-of-the-clan reading.** Evans-Pritchard argued Nuer totems are "not, at least primarily, diacritical signs of social differentiation": lineages are distinguished by ancestor names, spear-names, and honorific titles, not totems; many lineages share the same totem; people with the same totem intermarry where there is no kinship between them. His conclusion, the signature move linking totemism to [[spiritual-refraction]]: "It is not that the members of a group see themselves as a group through a totemic class of objects which are then further conceptualized collectively as a spirit of the class, but rather that the conception of Spirit, which is in itself quite independent of the social structure, is broken up along the lines of segmentation within the structure."

**Lienhardt's "emblems."** Evans-Pritchard preferred Dr. R. G. Lienhardt's term "emblems" over "totems" for the Nuer/Dinka material: "The word could appropriately be used in reference to the species and classes which are objects of respect by Nuer lineages, and perhaps more suitably than the word 'totems', for what I have described is certainly not what we are accustomed to think of as totemism" — attributed to Lienhardt's then-forthcoming monograph on Dinka religion, with R. T. Johnston cited reaching "much the same conclusion" for the Bor Dinka *jok*.

**Origin and Dinka provenance.** Evans-Pritchard judged the great majority of Nuer lineages holding totems to be of Dinka origin, transmitted via maternal/affinal links, and reported that "true Nuer" (*gat dila*) often despise totems, especially reptilian ones, "as something Dinka." The commonest Nuer aetiology for a totemic relationship is a twin-birth of a lineage ancestor and a member of the natural species — a belief he noted is also common among the Dinka (citing Seligman and Lienhardt). Other reported origin-mechanisms: a spirit "stamping its likeness" on a deformed foetus after a taboo breach; possession diagnosed by divination; inheritance through affinal/maternal links; and misfortune (death by a particular weapon, animal, or object generating totemic respect in descendants).

**Totems never seen.** Evans-Pritchard noted totems are sometimes never seen by their respecters — the Nuer *lou* serpent (which he stated he is "confident... does not exist"), plus comparative cases (a Dinka who first saw his totem as an adult; a Ghanaian chief who first saw his totem in an Oxford film; the Bechuanaland Kwena royal family living 100 years from their crocodile totem, citing Schapera 1953 and Ashton 1952) — his point being "a totem can be purely imaginary" and the relationship is "ideal," not perceptual. He distinguished the plural "crocodiles" (observed animals) from the singular type-concept "crocodile" ("his spirit"), a grammatical marking he read as evidence that Spirit attaches to the totemic *conception*, not to individual specimens.

**The triadic-relation argument.** Extending his analysis of the twins-are-birds dogma (see [[kwoth]]), Evans-Pritchard treated the totemic relationship as structurally analogous: a triadic relation (lineage, species, God), not a dyadic identity — "the stock explanation among the Nuer of a totemic relationship is that the ancestor of a lineage and a member of a natural species were born twins." Within the ritual context, "symbol and what it symbolized are fused," but this is an "ideal equivalence," not literal identity — Nuer, per Evans-Pritchard, "do not mistake ideal relations for real ones."

**Robertson Smith and sacrifice.** Evans-Pritchard also rebutted the totemic component of [[sacrifice|Robertson Smith's communion theory of sacrifice]] for the Nuer case as "unacceptable, since there is almost no evidence" (see [[sacrifice]]).
