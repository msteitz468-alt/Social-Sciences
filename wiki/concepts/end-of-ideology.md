# End of Ideology

## Definition as Coined

The **end of ideology** thesis, as [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] states it in *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]* Chapter XIII (a personal postscript), holds that in the stable industrial democracies of the mid-1950s the deep ideological cleavage between democratic left and right had declined to marginal disputes over "a little more or a little less" state intervention. Socialism as utopia of full nationalization was giving way among European socialist leaders; conservatives accepted welfare-state baselines. Lipset ties the claim to the Milan "Future of Freedom" conference (1955) and to related statements by Bell, Shils, Aron, and others. For him democracy is "the good society itself in operation," not only a means to other ends.

## Semantic History

Phrase associated with Daniel Bell's *The End of Ideology* (1960), Edward Shils, Raymond Aron, and Lipset's postscript. Became a symbol of 1950s liberal consensus sociology — and a target for 1960s New Left and later critics who saw renewed polarization, Third World revolution, and identity/cultural politics as refutation. Distinct from claims that *all* belief systems ended; the thesis concerned industrial-democratic left–right doctrinal intensity.

## Operationalizations

Qualitative: party programs, intellectual conferences, socialist abandonment of full socialization. Weak survey operationalization in Lipset's chapter itself. Later polarization and populism measures (not ingested) are used by critics as contrary indicators.

## Applications

- Capstone to *Political Man*'s argument that democratic class conflict can be institutionalized and de-dramatized under affluence and legitimacy.
- Contrast with extremist ideologies still alive at the margins and in poorer societies.

## Critiques and Limitations

Debate: [[end-of-ideology-debate]]. Charges of complacency, Western provincialism, and premature closure. Record as a **period thesis** (mid-1950s Atlantic democracies), not a permanent law.
