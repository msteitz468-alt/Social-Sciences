# Local Bridge (Network)

Graph-theoretic and sociological concept used by [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]] (1973) to specify when a weak interpersonal tie carries structural importance for diffusion and cohesion.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Granovetter, following Harary, Norman, and Cartwright 1965.)* A **bridge** is a line providing the *only* path between two points. In large social networks true bridges are rare; the sociologically useful object is the **local bridge**: a tie that is the shortest (or among the shortest feasible) connection between two network sectors for many actors.

Granovetter: a tie is a **local bridge of degree *n*** if *n* is the length of the shortest path between its two points *other than itself*, and *n* > 2. Higher degree means greater significance as a connection between sectors. An absolute bridge is a local bridge of infinite degree.

## Semantic History

- **Graph theory:** bridge as cut-edge (Harary et al.).
- **Granovetter 1973:** imports the concept to argue that, given the forbidden triad (no two strong ties with an absent third), **only weak ties may be local bridges** — strong ties almost never are, because strong A–B plus strong A–C forces a C–B path that bypasses A–B as unique connection.
- **Later network science:** betweenness centrality, edge betweenness, structural holes (Burt) — related formalizations of bridging, not identical; keep distinct until those sources are ingested.

## Operationalizations

- Path enumeration / shortest-path algorithms on sociograms or contact networks.
- In ethnographic reconstruction (West End argument): presence of weak ties that link otherwise separate peer-group cliques; Jacobs's intuitive "hop-skip" people as folk analogue.
- Granovetter also notes **liaison persons** / cut points in organizational analysis as related but not equivalent (a bridge has a liaison on each side; a liaison need not imply a bridge).

## Applications

- **Diffusion:** local bridges create more and shorter paths for information and influence (Davis 1969 path logic as used by Granovetter).
- **Mobility:** job tips often travel on non-redundant weak contacts (empirical illustration in [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]]).
- **Community organization:** density of local bridges proposed as predictor of capacity for collective action ([[west-end-network-fragmentation-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Strict "only path" bridges are rare; degree-*n* local bridges require full network data rarely available in 1970s community studies.
- Bridging *function* can be served by sets of weak ties, not only single edges (Granovetter footnotes this as deferred complexity).
- Does not by itself measure *content* of the bridging relation (trust, authority, resource flow).
