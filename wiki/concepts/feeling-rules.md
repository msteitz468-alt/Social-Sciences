# Feeling Rules

## Definition as Coined

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]]'s term (**feeling rules**) for the social guidelines that direct how we want to try to feel — the **rights and duties** of feeling that frame [[emotion-work|emotion work]] (attributed). We most sense a feeling rule as a rule when we feel a **"pinch"** between what we do feel and what we should feel, or when moving between cultures, roles, jobs, or marriages (attributed).

**Misfeeling** (attributed): violating rules of duration, strength, timing, or placement of feeling (e.g., not sad enough at a funeral; too angry at a "small" insult; grieving "too long").

Feeling rules are not merely display rules (how one should *look*): they concern how one should *try to feel*, though commercial settings often collapse the two under supervision of the smile.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |

| Related traditions | Goffman traffic rules of interaction (display-heavy); etiquette books; "doctrine of feelings" about women's emotionality (Ch 8) |

## Operationalizations

Sensed in talk about "I should feel…," rule reminders, and pinches; illustrated from student essays and occupational training; not a survey scale in the primary text.

## Applications

- Private: funerals, weddings, birthdays, romantic scripts, parental feeling.
- Occupational contrast: flight attendant must not let trust give way too easily to suspicion; bill collector must not let suspicion give way too easily to trust (attributed).
- Gender/status: [[status-shield|status shield]] — lower-status workers (disproportionately women in public-contact jobs) receive more aggression and must absorb it under job feeling rules (attributed).
- When private rules are taken into the market, they become standardized and thinner — more susceptible to estrangement (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Analytic concept; rules are often tacit and reconstructed from violations.
- Risk of over-systematizing fluid, contested norms.
- Corporate "feeling rules" may be contested by workers (slowdown, alternative backstage meanings of the smile) — rules are not automatically effective.
