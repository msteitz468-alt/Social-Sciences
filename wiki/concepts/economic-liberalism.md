# Economic Liberalism (Polanyi's account)

## Definition as Coined

In [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s Chapters 12–13, **economic liberalism** is the organizing creed of the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] — "a veritable faith in man's secular salvation through a self-regulating market." By the 1830s it crystallized into **three classical tenets**: "that labor should find its price on the market; that the creation of money should be subject to an automatic mechanism; that goods should be free to flow from country to country without hindrance or preference — in short, for a labor market, the gold standard, and free trade." Polanyi stresses the three were mutually dependent and formed "a coherent whole" (the 1846 Anti-Corn Law Bill as the "corollary" of Peel's 1844 Bank Act, both premised on a labor force disciplined by hunger since the 1834 Poor Law).

Polanyi's distinctive claims:
- **Laissez-faire was not natural but engineered.** "There was nothing natural about laissez-faire... laissez-faire itself was enforced by the state." Even the cotton industry, the model free-trade sector, was built on protective tariffs, export bounties, and machinery-export bans: "the free trade origins of the cotton industry are a myth."
- **The creed turned militant only in the 1830s.** The term *laissez-faire* originated with the mid-eighteenth-century Physiocrats, who envisioned not a self-regulating market but a policy directive for an all-powerful government; "not until the 1830's did economic liberalism burst forth as a crusading passion." Its "evangelical fervor" was a response to the magnitude of suffering its full realization required, not an original feature.
- **"Interventionism" is an empty accusation.** Liberals themselves invoke the state to establish *and* maintain the market (Polanyi's example: the American South appealed to laissez-faire to defend slavery, the North to "the intervention of arms to establish a free labor market"), so the liberal charge of "interventionism" against the [[double-movement|countermovement]] is a selectively applied slogan.

## Semantic History

Polanyi's polemical targets are Adam Smith (the "propensity to barter"), [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]] and William Graham Sumner, and — as the twentieth-century standard-bearers — **Ludwig von Mises** and **Walter Lippmann**. His "economic liberalism" is thus the intellectual object later revived and radicalized as *neoliberalism*, for which the double movement remains a common analytic frame.

## Operationalizations

Identified by the three tenets (labor market, gold standard, free trade) and by the "militant creed" of the self-regulating market. Polanyi distinguishes *economic* liberalism from laissez-faire narrowly (freedom from production regulation) and from political liberalism.

## Applications

One pole of the [[double-movement]]; the ideology whose utopian demand for a [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] Polanyi holds responsible for the [[hundred-years-peace|nineteenth-century order]] and its collapse. Central to [[substantivism]]'s critique of the "economistic fallacy."

## Critiques and Limitations

- Polanyi's reading is avowedly polemical; historians of economic thought contest his account of the Physiocrats, of Smith, and of how "planned" laissez-faire really was.
- The equation of classical economic liberalism with a single "creed" flattens real disagreements among liberals (currency, free trade, the poor law).
