# Subaltern

## Definition as Coined

**Subaltern** is an etic analytical term with three nested lives that must not be collapsed.

1. **Antonio [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]** (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]*, Notes on Italian History) used *subalterno* for subordinate social groups — classes and strata not holding [[hegemony|hegemonic]] power. Methodological criteria (attributed): the history of subaltern classes is **fragmentary and episodic**; only the State of the dominant classes has unity; research must track how subaltern groups are limited by, react to, and (rarely) achieve autonomy from the dominant historical unity — including phases of potential State-aspiration. "Subaltern functions" of intellectuals as hegemony *deputies* is a different notebook usage (role hierarchy), not this class sense.
2. **[[subaltern-studies|Subaltern Studies]]** (from 1982; Ranajit Guha et al.) adopted the term for non-elite groups in colonial and postcolonial South Asia — peasants, tribals, and other “people” whose political consciousness and insurgency were, the collective argued, autonomous from elite nationalist leadership and invisible to both colonial and elite-nationalist historiography.
3. **[[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty|Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak]]**, in "[[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study|Can the Subaltern Speak?]]" (1988; revised 1999), reworked the category as a **limit of representation**: the subaltern is not merely the unrecorded poor, but the figure for whom speech does not become *heard as speech* within dominant codes — and the **subaltern as woman** is “doubly in shadow.”

Spivak (attributed, CPR revision in [[morris-can-subaltern-speak-2010|Morris 2010]]) insists Bhubaneswari Bhaduri was *not* a “true” subaltern by class definition (middle-class, with clandestine access to bourgeois Independence politics); the essay’s force is that even such a limit-case of attempted “speech with the body” fails to be received.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Sense | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gramsci | Subordinate classes in non-hegemonic position | Military-political metaphor extended to class analysis |
| Guha / early Subaltern Studies | Autonomous subaltern political domain | Recovery of insurgent consciousness as historiographic task |
| Spivak 1988 | Gendered limit of representation; “the subaltern cannot speak” | Against both transparent-intellectual politics (Foucault/Deleuze as read by Spivak) and simple recovery of voice |
| Spivak 1999 (CPR) | Softens slogan; “inadvisable remark”; subalternity vs. hegemony as mobile relation | Bhubaneswari still mute *within family and new mainstream*; “new subaltern” under global financialization |
| Reception (Morris volume) | Classroom slogan vs. structural claim | Morris: silence is not simple archival absence; exit from subalternity is to be desired |

**Date-stamp:** meanings drift sharply 1982→1988→1999→classroom reception. Do not equate Gramsci’s class map, Guha’s peasant, and Spivak’s gendered mute figure without attribution.

## Operationalizations

- **Historiographic (Guha):** locate autonomous structures of subaltern consciousness (e.g. *Elementary Aspects of Peasant Insurgency*, 1983); read against elite and colonial archives.
- **Spivakian:** track *where representation fails* — complicity of *vertreten* (proxy/speaking-for) and *darstellen* (portrait/re-presentation); international division of labor; gendered double shadow; later, restricted permeability / cut off from social mobility (Cornell summarizing Spivak in Morris 2010).
- **Military-historical (Barrett 2010):** ordinary English “subaltern” = junior officer — a false friend that produced comic mistranslation (“Can Junior Officers Speak?”) and serious confusion with Spivak’s sense (Barrett on Imperial War Graves Commission material).

## Applications

- South Asian social history and the [[subaltern-studies]] program.
- [[postcolonialism|Postcolonial]] literary and cultural theory; feminist critique of archives.
- Extensions in the Morris volume: U.S. slavery and the death-bound subject (JanMohamed); WWI colonial war dead (Barrett); foreign domestic workers and biopower (Cheah); indigenous women and EZLN passage into hegemony (Franco).
- Historical archaeology’s use of the *question* of subaltern speech under unequal power (e.g. Orser) — often titular, not full Spivak apparatus.

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes`. Standing dispute: [[can-the-subaltern-speak-debate]].

- **Recovery position:** silence is missing records; oral history and archives can restore subaltern agency.
- **Spivak position:** retrieval is endorsed but does not dissolve [[epistemic-violence]]; “giving voice” can reinstate the transparent intellectual.
- **Internal SS:** Chatterjee (2010) argues Spivak’s intervention enabled a poststructural turn *also* driven by Indian political change (subaltern insertion into governmentality), not mere fashion.
- **Risk of hypostatization:** treating “subaltern” as a romantic identity to claim (Morris and Spivak both warn against this).
- **Scope:** Spivak’s archive cases are South Asian and gendered; extensions (slavery, war graves, FDWs, indigenous Latin America) are analogies or parallel applications, not automatic identity of “the subaltern.”

## Smith 2006 — subaltern heritage discourses (attributed)

*([[smith-laurajane]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006]]*, Part III.)* Alongside the AHD, popular and **subaltern** heritage discourses (labour, community, Indigenous) actively challenge received values — or challenge simply by existing. Dual face of heritage: elite consensus regulation **and** subaltern redefinition. Cases: industrial-museum visitors; [[castleford|Castleford]] Heritage Trust; Indigenous politics of **control** (Langford and others as Smith cites). “Inclusion” programs that invite excluded groups only to learn authorized values are assimilatory.
