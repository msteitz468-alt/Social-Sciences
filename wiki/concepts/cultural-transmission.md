# Cultural Transmission

## Definition as Coined

**Cultural transmission**, in the sense formalized by [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]
and [[feldman-marcus|Marcus Feldman]] (*Cultural Transmission and Evolution*, 1981)
and restated for a general audience in Cavalli-Sforza's *Genes, Peoples and Languages*
(2000, ch. 6), is the process by which customs, technologies, and knowledge pass
from person to person — especially across generations — so that culture accumulates
beyond a single lifespan. Cavalli-Sforza defines culture broadly as "the ensemble
of customs and technologies" learned from others (attributed, as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000).

The analytic core is a typology of **transmission paths** analogous to genetic
inheritance but with extra channels:

| Mode | Path | Genetic analogue |
|---|---|---|
| **Vertical** | parent → child | Mendelian inheritance |
| **Horizontal** | peer → peer (same generation) | (no simple genetic analogue) |
| **Oblique** | non-parent elder / teacher → younger | (partial: teacher–student) |
| **One-to-many** | chief, school, mass media → many | (no genetic analogue; post-agricultural acceleration) |
| **Concerted (many-to-one)** | community → individual | Highly conservative |

(As of Cavalli-Sforza 2000, ch. 6; refined from Feldman collaboration.)

Cavalli-Sforza stresses (attributed) that cultural “mutations” are often
**intentional**, unlike blind genetic mutation; acceptance faces cultural selection
then natural selection; modes predict different rates (vertical/concerted slow;
horizontal and one-to-many fast).

## Semantic History

- **1981 program:** Quantitative dual-track models of genes and culture (Cavalli-Sforza & Feldman).
- **1980s field applications:** e.g. cultural transmission among Aka Pygmies (Hewlett & Cavalli-Sforza 1986).
- **2000 popular synthesis:** ch. 6 of *Genes, Peoples and Languages* treats language evolution as cultural evolution and ties transmission modes to rates of cultural change and conservatism.
- **Adjacent vocabularies:** Boyd & Richerson *dual inheritance*; Durham *coevolution*; memetics (distinct, often looser). Do not equate without attribution.

## Operationalizations

Survey and ethnographic inventories of who teaches what to whom; formal models of
equilibrium frequencies under different mode mixes; linguistic change rates compared
to genetic distances (see gene–language work in Cavalli-Sforza 2000 ch. 5).

## Applications

- Explaining why some cultural traits change slowly (strong vertical transmission)
  while others spread rapidly (horizontal/oblique).
- Linking [[demic-diffusion]] (people move, carrying vertically transmitted language
  and genes) vs pure [[stimulus-diffusion]] (ideas move without people).
- African Pygmy cultural-transmission fieldwork (Aka; see also [[mbuti]] for related
  Central African forager documentation by Turnbull).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Anthropologists often reject culture-as-packet models and selection metaphors as
  too geneticist.
- Mode labels can blur in practice (teachers who are also kin; media as mass oblique).
- Gene–culture coevolution claims require careful separation of evidence classes
  (four-way non-identity: genes ≠ languages ≠ peoples ≠ archaeological cultures).
