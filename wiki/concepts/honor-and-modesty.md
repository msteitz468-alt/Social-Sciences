# Honor and Modesty (Awlad ʿAli)

## Definition as Coined

[[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]] places Awlad ʿAli within the circum-Mediterranean **honor** literature but specifies the local logic in *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-1986|Veiled Sentiments]]* (1986):

| Pole | Content (attributed) | Who |
|---|---|---|
| **Honor** | Virtues of **autonomy**, self-mastery, generosity, ability to protect dependents; moral worth → social precedence | Ideally adult free men / those who can enact independence |
| **Modesty (ḥasham)** | **Voluntary deference** to those who more closely embody honor ideals; sexual modesty and veiling as hierarchical respect — see [[hasham]] | Women, juniors, clients, dependents — the "honor of the weak" |

The ideology, she argues, **rationalizes inequality and control** in a system that idealizes agnatic equality and individual autonomy — primarily through morality that motivates people to perpetuate hierarchy without constant force. Ordinary conversation expresses sentiments congruent with this code (pride, anger, denial of attachment, stoicism). [[ghinnawa|Ghinnāwa]] poetry expresses non-virtuous vulnerability, love, and longing — protected by form, intimacy of context, and tradition.

## Semantic History

Builds on and criticizes generic Mediterranean "honor and shame" models by grounding terms in one community's practices and **dual discourses**. Distinct from Benedict [[shame-culture]] and from Du Bois [[the-veil]] (racial metaphor — do not conflate with Bedouin women's headcloth/veil). Links honor to stratification (widely noted) but specifies the autonomy → hierarchy → modesty-as-deference chain ethnographically.

## Operationalizations

- Who commands vs. defers; moral talk about *aṣl* and *ʿagl*
- Veiling matrix (before whom women cover)
- Discourse disjunction: same crisis, ordinary vs. poetic register
- Extreme sanctions (including rare honor killings) interpreted as responses to insubordination that destroys family moral standing

## Applications

## Critiques and Limitations

"Honor-shame" as regional type is contested in anthropology; Abu-Lughod's contribution is local logic + dual discourse, not a universal code. Contested whenever a single-community model is generalized. Her political reading of poetry as antistructure/freedom is theoretical, not a survey finding. *Writing Women's Worlds* further [[writing-against-culture|writes against]] treating honor/shame as a fixed cultural complex by reconstructing one educated daughter's particular negotiations (school as "greatest shame" to uncles; father's belief in her; town marriage hopes).

In *[[abu-lughod-do-muslim-women-2013|Do Muslim Women Need Saving?]]* (2013), Abu-Lughod deploys this ethnography against the public "[[honor-crime]]" category: Amnesty-style "women as property" definitions are unrecognizable against a code in which honor bound both sexes and 1980s Awlad ʿAli girls asserted moral agency and virtue within (not against) it — wedding songs figured brides with falcon imagery of power, not caged submission (attributed).
