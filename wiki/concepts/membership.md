## Definition as Coined

Garfinkel argued that ethnomethodology's central object was not the isolated actor of classical sociological theory but the "member" — a party to a setting whose skill with, knowledge of, and entitlement to the detailed work of producing that setting's affairs is presupposed by co-participants and relied upon, rather than explained. In *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]*, Garfinkel wrote that "a member must at the outset 'know' the settings in which he is to operate if his practices are to serve as measures to bring particular, located features of these settings to recognizable account." Membership, on this reading, is not a demographic category (age, occupation, group affiliation) but a practical, ongoing competence: the capacity to produce and recognize a setting's activities as what they are — a jury deliberation, a clinic intake, a bargaining exchange — using methods that are themselves taken for granted by everyone treated as competent.

Garfinkel used the phrase "What Anyone Knows" as a members'-category label for the shared background competence that inquirers (Suicide Prevention Center staff, clinic coders, jurors) were required to display in the course of situated decision-making. A "member," in this technical sense, is defined relationally: someone whose accounts and judgments other parties to the setting accept as adequate without demanding that the underlying competence be spelled out.

## Semantic History

The term entered ethnomethodology as a direct alternative to the "actor" of Parsonian action theory and the rule-following subject of conventional sociological method. Where Talcott Parsons's framework ([[parsons-talcott]]) treated actors as oriented to internalized norms and values, Garfinkel's "member" is oriented to the practical task of making a setting's affairs recognizable and accountable, drawing on Alfred Schutz's ([[schutz-alfred]]) phenomenology of the "world known in common and taken for granted." Later ethnomethodological and conversation-analytic work (associated with Harvey Sacks and colleagues, named in Garfinkel's acknowledgments) developed "membership categorization" as a technical apparatus for analyzing how members invoke and assemble category systems in talk — an elaboration beyond what appears in the 1967 text itself, which uses "member" more generally as the unit whose competence ethnomethodology studies. The concept has remained stable in its core sense across subsequent ethnomethodological literature: it denotes practical, demonstrated competence in a setting, not membership in a formal group.

## Operationalizations

Membership is not operationalized as a variable or measured quantity in Garfinkel's text; it is identified through demonstrations that reveal what members' ordinary competence consists of by disrupting it. The clinic-coding study (Chapter 1) showed that coders required unstated background knowledge of clinic organization to apply written instructions at all — evidence that "membership" knowledge, not the instructions themselves, did the interpretive work. The "boarder" demonstrations (Chapter 2), in which students adopted an outsider's stance toward their own households, were used to show by contrast what full membership in a household ordinarily supplies: mutual texture, disclosed attitude, and reciprocal orientation to co-residents that a non-member (a lodger) lacks.

## Applications

Garfinkel applied the concept across the volume's demonstrations: Suicide Prevention Center staff conducting "psychological autopsies," jurors reasoning about evidence, clinic personnel producing and reading case folders (Chapter 6, "'Good' Organizational Reasons for 'Bad' Clinic Records," with Egon Bittner, [[bittner-egon]]), and the coders in the psychiatric-clinic selection study (Chapter 7). In each case, competent performance depended on possession of practical, situated membership knowledge — of particular persons, of an organization's actual operating procedures, of shared history among the parties — that could not be reduced to explicit rules.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics have noted that Garfinkel's "member" risks circularity: membership is defined by the very competence it is invoked to explain, making the concept difficult to operationalize independently of the demonstrations that illustrate it. Because the concept is deliberately non-quantitative and resists standard measurement, it has had limited uptake outside ethnomethodology and conversation analysis; mainstream sociology has generally continued to work with actor- or agent-centered vocabularies. Within ethnomethodology, however, the member concept is foundational, underwriting Garfinkel's rejection of the "cultural dope" model of the actor (see [[practical-action]]) in which members merely enact preestablished cultural alternatives rather than actively accomplishing recognizable social order.
