# Relative Deprivation

## Definition as Coined

As developed in the reference-group program of [[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (*[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Chs. X–XI) drawing on *The American Soldier*: **relative deprivation** is the experience of deprivation **relative to a comparative standard** (a [[reference-group]] or reference individual), so that absolute improvement can coincide with increased felt deprivation if the comparison group rises faster or becomes more salient.

## Semantic History

From wartime survey analysis into political sociology, social movements, and inequality research. Drift: any grievance labeled "relative deprivation" without specified comparison group.

## Operationalizations

Classic: comparisons of satisfaction among soldiers by promotion opportunity, education, and theater — counter-intuitive patterns explained by differing reference frames. Later: Gurr and others quantify RD for conflict prediction — separate research program; do not collapse into Merton's formulation without note.

## Applications

Morale; class attitudes; race and ethnic inequality interpretations; revolution/rebellion theories (later, contested).

### In exchange theory (Blau)

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] folds relative deprivation into [[social-exchange-theory]] as **"diminishing collective marginal utility"** (see [[diminishing-marginal-utility]]): distributing more of a reward across a collectivity yields diminishing net gains in aggregate satisfaction, because abundance makes holders value it less and non-holders feel its absence more — "the marginal utility of increasing rewards eventually declines for entire collectivities as well as for individuals, though the mechanisms are different." He also uses it (drawing on Lipset) to distinguish **rightist extremism** (a reaction to *relative* deprivation of status among threatened middle strata) from **leftist opposition** (a reaction to *absolute* economic deprivation of the poorest) — see [[opposition]] — and to explain why a union's success in raising general wages can shift attention to differentials and undermine its own unity (Homans). Deprivation, for Blau, is felt against a [[fair-exchange|fair rate]], not an absolute level.

## Critiques and Limitations

Comparison-group selection often inferred after the fact; individual psychology vs structural explanation tension; weak predictive performance in some macro conflict models.
