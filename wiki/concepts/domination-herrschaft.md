## Definition as Coined

Weber distinguishes two related but distinct concepts. "Power" (Macht) is defined as "every Chance, within a social relationship, of enforcing one's own will even against resistance, whatever the basis for this Chance might be" — a concept Weber himself calls sociologically "amorphous," since almost any quality or constellation of circumstances could confer it in some situation (Ch.1 §16). "Rulership"/"domination" (Herrschaft) is the narrower and more precise concept: "the Chance that a command of a particular kind will be obeyed by given persons" (Ch.1 §16). Rulership requires only successful command-issuance and does not presuppose an administrative staff or organisation — a household head can exercise it — though normally a staff exists. Where one does, the organisation is a "ruling organisation" (Herrschaftsverband): members are subordinated, by a valid order, to relationships of rule (Ch.1 §16; example given: a Bedouin chief exacting tribute using followers as staff over non-member passersby). "Obedience" itself is defined precisely: the obedient conduct themselves as if they had made the command's substance their own maxim purely because of the formal relationship of obedience, irrespective of their own valuation of its content (Ch.3, line 170–174).

In Chapter 3, Weber restates rulership as "the chance that specific (or all) commands will be met with obedience on the part of a specifiable group of persons," and observes that every genuine rule-relationship requires a minimum of willed interest in obedience on the part of at least some of the ruled. Because a staff's obedience may rest merely on custom, affect, material interest, or value-rational motives, and no ruler can safely rely on these alone, Weber argues that every ruler "seeks to arouse and foster belief in their 'legitimacy'" (Ch.3, line 81–106). This move — from bare power to rule cultivated as legitimate — is what generates the three pure types of legitimate domination: [[charismatic-authority]], [[traditional-authority]], and [[legal-rational-authority]] (see [[legitimate-domination]]).

## Semantic History

Weber's own usage of Herrschaft was, per Tribe's Translation Appendix, historically flexible and even ambiguous — Edith Hanke's discussion of a 1910 letter from Weber to Michels shows Weber himself treating the term loosely before it hardened into the generic "organising principle" of any Verband (organisation) in Economy and Society. Reinhart Koselleck traces Herrschaft as having emerged as a German term separate from the Latin dominium.

The term has a contested translation history. Tribe's 2019 translation renders Herrschaft as "rule" or "rulership," explicitly rejecting two earlier English renderings: Talcott Parsons's "imperative control" or "authority" (a term Parsons borrowed from Nicholas Timasheff), and the choice made by Gerth and Mills, and later by Roth and Wittich, of "domination." Tribe argues both alternatives overemphasize coercion and improperly conflate Herrschaft with Macht (power) or Gewalt (violence), where Weber's concept is precisely the narrower, legitimacy-oriented one. This wiki follows the sourced convention of citing the concept as "Domination (Herrschaft)" for cross-literature findability while noting that the grounding translation (Tribe) prefers "rule/rulership" throughout the body text.

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized quantitatively; used as an ideal type. Weber explicitly notes that none of the three pure types of legitimate rule "has ever appeared in historically 'pure' form" — the typology's value lies in letting historical study identify in what respects and to what degree an empirical case of rule is charismatic, patriarchal, or bureaucratic (Ch.3, line 224–237). A single historical case can simultaneously be, in different respects, "feudal," "patrimonial," "bureaucratic," and "charismatic" (Ch.1, line 763–802) — which is precisely why Weber insists on unambiguous "pure" (ideal) types rather than blended average types.

## Applications

Weber's own illustrations — the Bedouin chief levying tribute via a following (Ch.1 §16), the political organisation and the state as the territorially bounded ruling organisation claiming a monopoly of legitimate physical force (Ch.1 §17) — are presented by Weber as illustrations of the ideal-typical structure of rule, not as established facts about those specific historical cases.

## Critiques and Limitations

The translation dispute itself constitutes an ongoing interpretive debate (see [[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]] where relevant): whether "authority," "domination," or "rule/rulership" best captures Weber's intended balance between legitimacy and coercion shapes how Anglophone sociology has received the concept, with "domination" (Gerth-Mills/Roth-Wittich) foregrounding coercive power in a way Tribe argues Weber did not intend. Weber's own definition of power as sociologically "amorphous" is itself a limitation he acknowledges — it is a residual, catch-all concept beside the sharper Herrschaft.

**Lukes 2005.** *(Attributed — *[[lukes-power-radical-view-2005|PRV]]* Ch. 3.)* Argues Weberian *Herrschaft*/domination used too broadly if it covers all hierarchical command; seeks a narrower sense of domination for the three-dimensional power problem (securing compliance of the dominated), distinct from legitimate hierarchical power and from power-as-capacity generally.
