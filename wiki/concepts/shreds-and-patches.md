# Shreds and Patches

## Definition as Coined

In the Conclusion of *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* (1920), [[lowie-robert-h|Lowie]] called civilization "that thing of **shreds and patches**" — a planless hodge-podge of borrowed traits — denying that culture history yields a fixed progress schedule that other peoples must reenact. The phrase is borrowed from Gilbert and Sullivan (*The Mikado*).

## Semantic History

Widely received as a slogan of Boasian **cultural atomism** (culture as only discrete traits without organic integration). In the **1947 Preface**, Lowie insisted the sentence was written in post–World War I disillusionment as a derogatory remark about *our* civilization, not a theoretical denial that cultural elements can be related; he pointed to his kinship-term work since 1915 against pure atomism. Later historians of anthropology still use "shreds and patches" as shorthand for extreme particularist trait-listing — with or without Lowie's clarification.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; a **historiographic image** tied to diffusion-heavy accounts of cultural growth and anti-unilinear politics.

## Applications

Closing rhetoric of *[[lowie-primitive-society-study|Primitive Society]]*; reception debates (Kroeber on negativism/sterility; later anti-Boasian critiques of under-theorization).

## Critiques and Limitations

If read as pure atomism, it understates integration, structure, and system — charges Lowie partly accepted as misreading. If read only as anti-progress-law, it survives as absorbed particularist common sense.
