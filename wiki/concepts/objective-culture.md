# Objective and Subjective Culture

## Definition as Coined

[[simmel-georg|Simmel]] distinguishes **objective culture** — the accumulated, condensed stock of the species' mental and practical labour: "the labour of countless generations… embedded in language and custom, political constitutions and religious doctrines… as objectified spirit" — from **subjective culture**, the degree to which an individual actually cultivates and assimilates that stock into a developed self. The two are not the same and can diverge sharply.

Objectification, for Simmel, is "the most significant and most far-reaching of the historical categories of mankind": because mind is deposited "in words and works, organizations and traditions," a person can be an *heir* to culture in a way biological heredity does not allow. But objective culture exists "as an objectively valid truth" independent of any mind — Newton's law "was valid before Newton" — residing neither in matter nor in individual subjectivity but in "the objective spirit." Only a fraction of it ever becomes live subjective value in any person or age; "a discovery preserved unread in an unknown book" is objectified mind that has ceased to be a cultural value.

## The divergence and its cause

Simmel's diagnosis of modernity is that **objective culture has outgrown subjective culture**. Compared with a century earlier, "objective culture" (technology, science, transport, art) has become extraordinarily refined, while "individual culture, at least in the higher strata, has not progressed at all to the same extent; indeed, it has even frequently declined." Language grows more precise in aggregate while individual speech becomes "less correct… and more trivial"; workers rarely understand the machines they run; even the educated use concepts as "sealed containers" whose content they never unpack. The eighteenth-century ideal of *Bildung* (formation of the whole person) gives way to nineteenth-century "education" as a stock of objective knowledge.

The cause Simmel names is the **division of labour**, in production and consumption alike: specialized labour "is completed at the expense of the development of the producer" — skills sharpen while "the total personality… often even becomes stunted." The mosaic and the poem are his images: "the whole becomes more perfect and harmonious, the less the individual is a harmonious being." The work of art is the extreme counter-case, resisting all division of labour and so remaining "the most perfectly autonomous unity." This divergence, radicalized, is the [[tragedy-of-culture|tragedy of culture]].

## Money's role

[[money-economy|The money economy]] is a chief agent of the split. By severing economic achievement from the total personality, money "atomizes" the individual and multiplies impersonal, one-function relationships; the commodity "becomes an objective given entity which the consumer approaches externally," its "subjective aura" dissolved. Simmel even suggests a fully realized functional depersonalization would exhibit "a profound affinity to… an extreme state socialism" (his own theoretical linkage, not a settled convergence). Objectification also has a mitigating side: because objectified goods (books, art, ideals like "fatherland") can be enjoyed by many without depletion, they reduce "the human tragedy of competition" — "man is the objective animal."

## Operationalizations

Simmel offers no metric, but frames the concept as a **ratio**: the size and refinement of the objectified stock (technologies, sciences, institutions, artworks) against the degree to which individuals actually assimilate it into personal cultivation. He points to observable proxies of a widening gap — division of labour and specialization indices, the shift from *Bildung* to "education," the multiplication of consumer objects, workers' inability to understand their machines, and the proliferation of coexisting styles — as symptoms that objective culture is outrunning subjective culture.

## Applications

- The analytic core of *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* ch. 6 and Simmel's diagnosis of modernity.
- Direct ancestor of Lukács's reification, Mannheim's sociology of knowledge, and Frankfurt-School culture critique.
- Underlies the [[tragedy-of-culture|tragedy of culture]] and connects to the [[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]] (the levelling of qualitative value) and the [[money-economy|money economy]] as agent of the split.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Lukács, Mannheim, and Arato argued Simmel abstracted the process from its specifically capitalist basis, treating an "objective process" where a social relation (class) was at work.
- The claim that individual culture has "declined" is an attributed cultural-pessimist judgment, not a measured finding.
- Some of Simmel's supporting contrasts (undifferentiated "primitive" communities; children equated with "primitive man") carry 1900 evolutionist framings, recorded as dated and attributed.
