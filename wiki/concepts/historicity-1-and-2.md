# Historicity 1 and Historicity 2

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]* (1995), [[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] distinguishes two sides of **historicity**, which English “history” confuses in one word:

- **Historicity 1** — the material **sociohistorical process**: “what happened,” including bodies, artifacts, buildings, dead, censuses, monuments, diaries, political boundaries — traces that limit what can later pass as history.
- **Historicity 2** — **historical narrative**: “what is said to have happened.”

> “the materiality of the sociohistorical process (historicity 1) sets the stage for future historical narratives (historicity 2).” (Trouillot 1995)

He argues people are **doubly historical**: engaged simultaneously in the sociohistorical process and in narrative constructions about it. Theories that treat this ambiguity as an accident to be corrected — either pure positivism (facts only) or pure constructivism (narrative only) — miss how **power works in the relation between the two sides**.

## Semantic History

| Pole Trouillot rejects (attributed characterization) | Problem |
|---|---|
| Positivism / storage model | Hides tropes and power; treats past as fixed content retrieved by professionals |
| Naive constructivism (narrative-as-fiction only) | Denies autonomy of the sociohistorical process; cannot explain why not any fiction passes as history, or limit cases such as Holocaust denial |

Trouillot’s first methodological choice is to **embrace the ambiguity** of both sides; the second is to study **how history works** (concrete production of narratives) rather than what history “is” in essentialist terms. Related but distinct: Sewell’s [[historical-event]]; Fabian’s [[coevalness]] / [[allochronism]] (temporal politics of ethnography — not named in *Silencing* Ch.1 but adjacent in the critical-turn kit).

## Operationalizations

1. Identify material traces and constraints (H1).
2. Identify narrative assemblies and commemorations (H2).
3. Locate [[historical-silences|silences]] at junctions (sources, archives, narratives, retrospective significance).
4. Ask whether a case fails because of sparse H1 traces, unthinkability (categories that block H2), or competing H2 appropriations under source abundance.

**Agent / actor / subject** (Trouillot, attributed): three capacities in the sociohistorical process — people are not always subjects constantly confronting history, but the **capacity** to become subjects is always part of their condition (strike capacity of workers as integral to the event).

## Applications

- Sans Souci ruins and Colonel’s missing portrait ([[sans-souci-milot]])
- [[haitian-revolution]]: H1 event vs H2 non-event / [[unthinkable-history]]
- [[columbus-commemorations]]: invented consensus mythicizes H2 while trivializing H1 process
- Disney’s America / slavery exhibits: authenticity as **present relation** to known past, not degree of archival knowledge (Ch.5)

## Critiques and Limitations

- Pair can be misread as dualism; Trouillot insists co-constitution.
- “Materiality blocks fiction” is a limit claim, not a return to naive positivism — facts still become facts because they matter, and are never created equal.
