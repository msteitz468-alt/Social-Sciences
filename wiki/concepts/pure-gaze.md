# Pure Gaze

Bourdieu's term for the historically and socially conditioned mode of perception that treats art (and, under an aesthetic attitude, everyday objects) as **pure form**. Opposed to the “popular aesthetic” that subordinates form to function and affirms continuity between art and life.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, Distinction.)* “The ‘pure’ gaze is a historical invention linked to the emergence of an autonomous field of artistic production” imposing its own norms on production and consumption. It “implies a break with the ordinary attitude towards the world, which, given the conditions in which it is performed, is also a social separation.” The pure aesthetic is “rooted in an ethic, or rather, an ethos of elective distance from the necessities of the natural and social world” — product of conditioning by ease.

## Semantic History

Builds on art-perception essays and museum studies; crystallized in *Distinction*’s opening and Postscript (vulgar critique of pure critiques). Engages Kantian disinterestedness as the misrecognized rationalization of a class ethos, not as universal aesthetic truth. Related but not identical to art-historical “beholder” constructs.

## Operationalizations

Same family as [[aesthetic-disposition]] indicators: refusal of ordinary popular subjects as vulgar; acceptance of formal/“meaningless” photo subjects; knowledge of legitimate works; preference for works requiring decoding competence. Postscript adds the affective definition: pure taste as **disgust at the facile**.

## Applications

Core of Part I; frames intellectual vs bourgeois modes of appropriating art in Part III; closes the book’s critique of philosophical aesthetics.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of over-unifying “dominant” taste (teachers ≠ employers); historical invention claim depends on autonomous art-field narrative; popular aesthetic may be romanticized as form/function only. Comparative work often finds mixed rather than pure gazes.
