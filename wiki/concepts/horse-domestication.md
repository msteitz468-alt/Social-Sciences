# Horse Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Horse domestication** names the process by which *Equus caballus* entered sustained human management. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] (*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, 2007) insists on **staging** that the literature often collapses:

1. **Dietary/symbolic horse keeping** — possibly from ~4800 BCE in the Pontic-Caspian steppes (winter meat incentive; funeral head-and-hoof deposits with cattle/sheep).
2. **Riding or driving with bit control** — evidenced by [[bit-wear-analysis]] (Botai ~3700–3500 BCE; Anthony argues riding likely began earlier west of Botai in the Pontic-Caspian).
3. **Specialized breeds / size variability** — often dated ~2500 BCE; may reflect breed diversification, not first domestication.
4. **Cavalry / massed mounted archery** — Iron Age after ~1000 BCE; distinct from early mounted *raiding*.

Domestication for meat ≠ riding ≠ cavalry (Anthony 2007).

**Gimbutas 1991 (attributed — earlier package, not staged like Anthony).** In *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* she places horse domestication between eastern Ukraine and northern Kazakhstan around **5000 B.C. or earlier**, at forest edges near rivers; domesticated form is tarpan (*Equus ferus gmelini*), not Przewalski. She treats riding as already essential to the Kurgan package by the mid-5th millennium: large-scale horse breeding, travel time cut by a factor of five or more, cattle-looting/horse-stealing raids, wealth accumulation, violence, and outward steppe migration. She asserts pastoral economy, large herds, horse riding, and male strength needed to control animals contributed to transition from “matrism” to “armored patriarchy” in southern Russia by ~5000 B.C. at latest. Load-bearing quote (her framing): “Horse riding changed the course of European prehistory. Coupled with the use of weapons, the mounted warrior became a deadly menace to the peaceful, unarmed agriculturalists. From the middle of the 5th millennium B.C., the swift horse became a carrier of unrest that continued for millennia.”

## Semantic History

Earlier size-variability methods (placing first domestication ~2500 BCE at sites like Csepel) are treated by Anthony as possibly late breed signals. Levine's age/sex models read both Dereivka and Botai as "wild" despite dissimilar profiles — Anthony treats those methods as ambiguous. The famous Dereivka "cult stallion" bit-wear case was **retracted** after AMS dating placed the specimen in the Scythian period (Anthony 2007; see [[dereivka]]).

Gimbutas 1991 (attributed) used Dereivka and related Sredniy Stog II evidence as pillars of early riding and horse cult: bones of 55 horses in a settlement of three dwellings (= 63% of domesticated animal remains, her figures); **antler-tine cheek pieces** (six at Dereivka) as possible bridle equipment; ritual pit with stallion skull/mandible/legs + two dog skulls/foreparts; calibrated C-14 she cites for Dereivka in the second half of the 5th millennium B.C. (UCLA 1466 4570–4150; Kiev 466 4460–4000; Kiev 465 4340–3810). She also cites Repin (Don) ~80% horse among domesticated animal bones; Petropavlovsk (N Kazakhstan) ~90% horse; earliest horse-cult artifacts at S’ezzhee (Samara River) ~5000 B.C. / early 5th mill. (bone horse figurine pendants; horse skulls/long bones in sacrificial hearths). Horse-head scepters of semi-precious stone in rich male kurgan graves mid–late 5th mill. across Moldavia, S Romania, Transylvania, Bulgaria, Macedonia (and Volga/N Caucasian parallels) are, for her, IE horse ideology plus suttee at Suvorovo and Casimcea. **Later AMS retraction of the Dereivka cult-stallion bit-wear specimen undercuts the strongest single osteological riding claim Gimbutas and others had leaned on for 5th-mill. bitting at that site**; faunal dominance and cheek-piece interpretation remain separately contested. Do not treat Gimbutas’s 5000 B.C. domestication/riding package as superseded only by silence — Anthony’s staging and the AMS correction are explicit later reworkings.

## Operationalizations

- **Faunal:** NISP/MNI horse dominance; head-and-hoof ritual deposits; stalling chemical signatures (Olsen on Botai). Gimbutas-style inventories: site-level horse % of domestic fauna; horse skulls in ritual hearths and barrow flanks; scepter distribution.
- **Pathology:** P₂ bevel ≥ ~3 mm and microwear matching experimental bitted horses ([[bit-wear-analysis]]). Gimbutas treated antler cheek pieces as bridle gear without the bit-wear standard Anthony later required.
- **Genetics (as reported by Anthony 2007):** highly diverse mare mtDNA vs. nearly homogeneous stallion Y-chromosome — many wild mares, few (possibly one) founding stallion lines.
- **Genetics (later layers):** post-2015 aDNA of horses is a separate research stream not in Anthony 2007 — do not back-fill.

## Applications

Steppe mobility models; [[yamnaya-culture]] wagon-and-riding package; [[botai-culture]] horse specialists; [[kurgan-hypothesis]] prestige and raiding capacity (Gimbutas: engine of Wave 1 and of OE–Kurgan collision); Indo-European technical vocabulary for equids and vehicles (associated, not identical, with speaking communities). Gimbutas aligns common IE *ekwos/*ekwa and horse-as-sacred/sacrificial riding animal from ≥ mid-5th mill. with Kurgan archaeology (her linguistic palaeontology package on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Organic bits leave different traces than metal; bitless control leaves little osteological signal; regional wild size gradients confound morphometrics; Botai people are **not** automatically Indo-European speakers (Anthony treats them as likely non-IE mounted foragers acquiring management ideas from western neighbors). Gimbutas’s early riding + mounted-warrior menace thesis collapses dietary keeping, control technology, and cavalry-like raiding into one 5th-mill. package — the collapse Anthony’s staging was designed to prevent. Keep all IE equations and 5th-mill. riding claims in attributed voice on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] and [[kurgan-hypothesis]]. Source for her claims: [[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991]].
