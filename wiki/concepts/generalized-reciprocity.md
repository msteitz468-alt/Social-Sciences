# Generalized Reciprocity

## Definition as Coined (anthropological — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], primary)

*(Attributed — Sahlins, *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 5, 1972.)* The **solidary extreme** of the [[reciprocity]] continuum: "transactions that are putatively altruistic, transactions on the line of assistance given and, if possible and necessary, assistance returned." Its ideal type is Malinowski's "pure gift"; its other faces are sharing, hospitality, kinship dues, chiefly dues, and noblesse oblige. The counter-obligation is **not stipulated** by time, quantity, or quality — "the expectation of reciprocity is indefinite" — and its pragmatic diagnostic is a **sustained one-way flow** that continues even without return. For generalized reciprocity "material flow is sustained by prevailing social relations," where for [[balanced-reciprocity|balanced exchange]] "social relations hinge on the material flow." Sahlins locates it in the innermost kinship-residential sectors, and shows it intensifies with wealth gaps and around food. See the umbrella page [[reciprocity]].

## Definition as Coined (civic — Putnam)

Norm of mutual helpfulness without strict tit-for-tat accounting. *(Attributed — Putnam 2000.)* For Putnam, a core mechanism of [[civic-social-capital]]: networks foster and are fostered by generalized reciprocity and trustworthiness. Distinct from balanced/specific reciprocity and from Bourdieu's gift-debt honour economy ([[gift-exchange]], [[reciprocal-obligation]]).

## Semantic History

The anthropological reciprocity typology (generalized / balanced / negative) predates Putnam: [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s Trobriand continuum and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]'s gift are its sources, and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (1965/1972) fixed the now-standard three-form scheme (the wiki's primary source is [[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]). Putnam later adapted the term to civic trust and everyday cooperation in mass society — a narrower sense than Sahlins'. Whyte's Cornerville [[reciprocal-obligation]] is a related but more personalistic network obligation concept.

## Operationalizations

"Most people can be trusted" items; honesty proxies; helping and freeriding survey items; experimental trust games (later literature). Putnam also uses behavioral honesty series (e.g. stop-sign compliance citations).

## Applications

- *Bowling Alone* ch. 8 trust and honesty trends.
- Link from dense networks to cheap cooperation (social capital mechanism list, Section IV).
- Dark-side caveat: reciprocity can be in-group only (bonding).

## Critiques and Limitations

Survey trust items are ambiguous (experience vs paranoia vs media); generalized vs particularized trust often conflated; experimental and survey measures diverge. Decline claims contested ([[bowling-alone-debate]]).
