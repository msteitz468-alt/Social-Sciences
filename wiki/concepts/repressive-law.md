# Repressive law

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] classified legal rules by their **sanction**. **Repressive law** (penal law) comprises rules whose sanction "consist[s] essentially in suffering, or at least a loss, inflicted on the agent" — demands on fortune, honor, life, or liberty. He treated it as the external index of **[[mechanical-solidarity]]**: repressive law "corresponds to the heart, the centre of the common conscience," and its relative volume measures how much of social life the [[collective-conscience]] embraces. (Contrast: **[[restitutive-law]]**, which merely restores disturbed relations and indexes [[organic-solidarity]].)

Durkheim's definition of **crime** follows from this (attributed): an act is criminal not by an intrinsic wrongness but because "it offends strong and defined states of the collective conscience." Inverting the common view via Spinoza: "we do not reprove it because it is a crime, but it is a crime because we reprove it." **Punishment** is accordingly a passionate **expiation** of the wounded collective sentiment, not primarily deterrence.

## Semantic History

Durkheim's usage is a **classificatory** move within his sociology of law, not a term of art in jurisprudence (jurists' public/private-law division he rejected as unscientific). Later Durkheimian penology (and the sociology of punishment after it) develops the "expiation" and "collective sentiment" line; the specific claim that repressive law's *share* declines with social evolution is largely abandoned, but the definition of crime by collective reaction remains influential.

## Operationalizations

Durkheim's comparative textual coding of ancient codes (attributed; single-scholar tallies):

| Code | Durkheim's reported balance |
|---|---|
| Pentateuch | Essentially all repressive; only isolated restitutive provisions |
| Laws of Manu | Criminal justice given supreme rank |
| Twelve Tables | ~49 penal vs. ~66 restitutive of 115 surviving fragments (Voigt) — "not far from half" |
| Salic law | ~9% non-repressive (Waitz edition) |
| Burgundian law | ~1/3 non-repressive |
| Visigothic law | Repressive still predominant, restitutive "almost equal" (full procedural + business books) |

The trend across these is offered as evidence that the repressive share shrinks — hence mechanical solidarity recedes — as societies advance.

## Applications

- The empirical backbone of *Division of Labor* Book I's argument for the historical shift from [[mechanical-solidarity|mechanical]] to [[organic-solidarity|organic]] solidarity.
- Origin claim: Durkheim argued penal law "was essentially religious in its origin" (India, Judea, Egypt, Germany, Greece, Rome), against a vendetta-origin theory — the private/mixed delict (Roman *delicta privata*) being "only an imperfect punishment," not the primitive prototype.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The tallies are Durkheim's own coding of selected codes, not independently replicated; comparability across legal traditions is doubtful.
- He rejected [[unknown|Henry Maine]]'s rival account (heavy primitive penal law reflects habitual violence) as "false," arguing instead that frequent violence is *tolerated* and that "luxuriant" religious/ritual criminality inflates the count — a live 19th-c. jurisprudential dispute, treated by Durkheim as settled (attributed).
- The claim that the repressive *share* falls with evolution is empirically weak by modern standards.
