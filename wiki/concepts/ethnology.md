## Definition as Coined

In Britain of the 1850s, "ethnology" — the "Science of Human Races" — was, per George W. Stocking, Jr. (*Victorian Anthropology*, 1987), the most general scientific framework for studying the linguistic, physical, and cultural characteristics of non-European "uncivilized" peoples, and is the most relevant proximate origin-point for later evolutionary and twentieth-century anthropology (attributed, Stocking). Stocking characterizes ethnology not as a product of disciplinary fission out of moral philosophy but as a *fusion* of natural-historical, moral-philosophical, and humanistic styles of inquiry, organized around a subject matter — "savagery" — excluded from the other emerging human sciences: "a science of leftovers or residues" (attributed, Stocking).

Stocking treats Prichardian ethnology (after [[prichard-james-cowles]]) as possessing the characteristics of a scientific paradigm in the Kuhnian sense: Prichard's *Researches into the Physical History of Mankind* served as a concrete exemplar generating particular ethnological puzzles within an a priori disciplinary worldview (attributed, Stocking). In his mature 1847–48 definition, Prichard framed ethnology's task as tracing the history of the tribes and races of man to determine their affinity or diversity of origin, drawing on anatomy, physiology, zoology, physical geography, history, archaeology, and philology — but subordinating physical-anthropological approaches to race in favor of cultural, and especially linguistic, approaches (finding).

## Semantic History

Early comparative philology, via Franz Bopp's genealogical classification of Indo-European languages, provided for several decades the methodological underpinning of ethnology, which sought to trace racial affinities on linguistic and other grounds back to a primitive unity analogous to the Indo-European language family (attributed, Stocking). Stocking characterizes the underlying structure of Prichardian ethnology with a tree metaphor: contemporary tribal "twigs" linked by racial "branches" to the trunk of a single human species, rooted near where Noah's ark was traditionally held to have landed in Southwest Asia (attributed, Stocking) — an image later inverted, per Stocking, when the regular "ladder" of cultural [[social-evolutionism|evolutionism]] was superimposed on the irregular outlines of the ethnological tree as the governing visual metaphor of the field (attributed, Stocking).

Ethnology entered a "state of crisis" (Stocking's term) after Prichard's death in 1849, as anomalous evidence accumulated that its assumptions could not accommodate (attributed, Stocking). Before 1850, ethnology combined comparative-anatomical (diversifying) and comparative-philological (unifying) orientations in asymmetrical tension; by the 1850s this balance broke down as philologists abandoned the assumption that all languages were genealogically related and physical anthropologists rejected language as the primary index of race (attributed, Stocking). Robert Gordon Latham was the field's most prominent Prichardian advocate after 1849, publishing the *Natural History of the Varieties of Man* (1850) and *Descriptive Ethnology* (1859) and arranging the ethnological exhibits at the Sydenham Crystal Palace, which in 1852 displayed thirteen life-size "ethnological groupings" of "savages" under "Natural History," juxtaposed with animals — an illustration of ethnology's status as popular science at the time (finding). The 1848 founding, and quick collapse, of the *Ethnological Journal* — in which Luke Burke openly advocated a rival polygenist viewpoint (see [[polygenism]]) — marked physical anthropology's growing assertion against the Prichardian tradition (finding).

The crisis sharpened decisively with the 1858–59 "revolution in human time" (see [[antiquity-of-man]]): the deep antiquity of man undercut ethnology's historical/philological argument for tracing all migrations back to a single recent origin point, even as it strengthened the biological case for monogenism by supplying enough time for races to have diverged (attributed, Stocking). Stocking's overarching thesis is that ethnology — organized around the problem of human unity and movement-in-space — was superseded by the synthetic discipline that came to be called "anthropology," reoriented from the problem of human unity to the problem of the origin of [[civilization]], understood as development-in-time rather than movement-in-space (attributed, Stocking).

Ethnology did not simply vanish, however. Stocking describes a later "neo-ethnological phase" in British anthropology beginning when the psychologist-turned-ethnologist W. H. R. Rivers announced his "conversion" from evolutionism to diffusion in 1911, after which his disciples Grafton Elliot Smith and William Perry developed extreme diffusionist ("heliolithic") theories in the early 1920s (attributed, Stocking; see [[diffusionism]]). By 1950, Stocking notes, E. E. Evans-Pritchard — by then favoring social anthropology as a form of historiography — could dismiss the earlier quarrel between evolutionists and diffusionists as merely "a family quarrel between ethnologists," of no concern to "functionalist anthropologists," which Stocking reads as evidence of a post-functionalist "dehistoricization" that blurred the earlier distinction between Tylorian evolutionism and neo-ethnological diffusionism (attributed, Stocking).

## Operationalizations

Not quantified in the Victorian period; ethnology's evidentiary base combined comparative philology (language classification), comparative anatomy/craniology, and reported custom, with disputed relative weighting among physical anthropologists and philologists after 1850.

## Applications

Prichard's *Researches into the Physical History of Mankind* (multiple editions to 1847–48) is ethnology's paradigmatic work; Latham's *Natural History of the Varieties of Man* (1850) and *Descriptive Ethnology* (1859) extended it; the 1852 Sydenham Crystal Palace ethnological displays popularized it; Rivers's post-1911 diffusionist program (elaborated by Elliot Smith and Perry) constitutes its twentieth-century "neo-ethnological" afterlife.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ethnology's philological unifying method and comparative-anatomical diversifying method proved mutually corrosive once specialists on each side rejected the other's key assumption (universal language relatedness; language as a racial index), a structural tension Stocking treats as the proximate cause of the field's mid-century "crisis." Its supersession by evolutionary anthropology (see [[social-evolutionism]]) reoriented the discipline from tracing origins in space to reconstructing development in time — a shift Stocking treats as the deep structural break separating "ethnology" from what became "anthropology" proper, though he cautions against reading this break too cleanly given the persistence of ethnological method (survivals-based reasoning, historical reconstruction) inside Rivers's ostensibly anti-evolutionary diffusionism.
