# Operational Model

## Definition as Coined

For [[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]] (*[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]*, 1968/1984), the **operational model** is the **observer's** model of how the local and regional systems work in material terms — trophic exchanges, pig–garden competition, population, warfare, trade — using measurable variables. It is deliberately distinguished from the people's [[cognized-model]].

## Semantic History

Part of 1960s–70s systems/human-ecology vocabulary; "operational" signals measurement and ecosystem specification (local ecosystem vs regional system). Later critics read it as privileging Western science over indigenous knowledge; Rappaport's later work insisted both models are necessary and that cognized models can be evaluated for adaptive value.

## Operationalizations

In the Tsembaga study: garden yields, energy expenditure, diet logs, pig census, [[carrying-capacity]] estimates, ritual-cycle timing relative to herd and fallow — see appendices of *Pigs* and [[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-study]].

## Applications

Allows claims that ritual has "practical results on the external world" (contra Homans-style internal-only functionalism) without claiming that Tsembaga *intend* ecological management in scientific terms.

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement error, short fieldwork window, and disputed nutritional arithmetic (App. 11 / McArthur) threaten operational conclusions; interpretive anthropologists contest the priority of operational variables. See [[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]].
