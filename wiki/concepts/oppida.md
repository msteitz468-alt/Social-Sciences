# Oppida

## Definition as Coined

**Oppida** (sing. *oppidum*) are large fortified late Iron Age settlements of temperate Europe. Classical authors (notably Caesar) used *oppidum* for major Gallic strongholds; modern archaeology re-operationalized the term for a class of extensive, often ramparted sites of roughly the last two centuries BCE.

Cunliffe (1997) treats Caesar’s word as a **portmanteau**, not a technical urban typology: *oppidum* is time- and place-specific, distinct from *vici* and *aedificia*, from *civitates* (Provincia towns), and from rare *urbs* (Gergovia, Alesia, Avaricum). Sites so labelled need not be tribal capitals (Helvetii twelve oppida; Bituriges twenty; Armorican cliff-castles also called oppida). For archaeological use he defines a class of **large developed oppida** as areas usually **in excess of ~10 ha**, enclosed by a continuous man-made or natural boundary, within which social, religious, and economic functions supporting large-community systems were performed—focal centres of regional (not always whole-tribe) political legitimacy (attributed).

## Semantic History

- **Classical:** place-label for defended centres, not a Greco-Roman city-state type.
- **Nineteenth–early twentieth c. archaeology:** Napoleon III–sponsored work at Alesia and Bulliot’s excavation of the Aedui oppidum at Bibracte (Mont Beuvray); Stradonice (Bohemia) looted for dealers (~80,000–100,000 items; gold-coin hoard 1877); Déchelette’s *Le Second Age du fer* (1914) presented oppidum culture (Stradonice, Mont Beuvray) as the culmination of Celtic achievement. La Tène itself was once among competing site interpretations (pile dwelling, fortified oppidum, bridge, ritual platform).
- **Post-1970 research:** Cunliffe notes a shift toward economic systems and world-systems models, with coinage and oppida (widely treated as urban in character) as research foci—partly reflecting broader archaeological interest in economics and a pan-European image of Celts convenient to contemporary European political framing (attributed reflexivity). Multinational Bibracte work (e.g. Mitterrand-era funding) continues the nationalist-to-European afterlife of the type-site.
- **Cunliffe 1997 synthesis:** production intensification after c.200 BCE + **Roman demand as catalyst**; command of trade routes and raw materials; oppida as features of the barbarian periphery adjacent to Rome—“Trade preceded the flag”—not identical to Mediterranean city-states.

## Operationalizations

| Dimension | Indicators (as Cunliffe 1997 frames them) |
|---|---|
| Enclosure | Continuous boundary, often >10 ha; enclosed area ≠ status (Kelheim ~630 ha, Heidengraben ~1,500 ha via promontory geography; Hengistbury ~120 ha behind a short rampart) |
| Defence types | Western *murus gallicus* (Caesar *DBG* 7.23: timber beams, stone facing, often iron-nailed; mainly Gaul to Upper Rhine/Danube; ~30 excavated); central European *pfostenschlitzmauer*/Kelheim type (vertical posts, drystone infill; Moselle–Moravia); overlap zone Upper Rhine–Danube; Manching rebuilt *murus gallicus* → Kelheim type; northern Gaul Fécamp dump rampart + wide flat-bottomed ditch (related forms in Britain) |
| Internal organization | Ordered streets/plots where excavated (Manching dense centre; Závist controlled streets) |
| Production | Craft specialization (iron, bronze, pottery, glass, coin pellets); service-centre role for hinterlands |
| Exchange media | Coin production/finds; gold/silver as gift and political legitimation more than capitalist commerce (attributed) |
| Position | Route nodes; iron (Manching, Kelheim), graphite (Třísov), amber route (Staré Hradisko), major rivers (Závist, Bratislava, Berne) |
| Fuzzy boundary | Hillfort vs oppidum (British 5–10 ha multivallate hillforts; Galatian fortified enclosures/emporia without classical “towns”) |

**Manching (Bavaria) as yardstick, not sole model** (Cunliffe 1997, attributed; only ~4.5 ha / ~3% excavated): ~380 ha, ~7 km timber-laced rampart (~60 tons iron for nails); dense central occupation with ordered streets; large-scale craft (iron, bronze jewellery, pottery, glass beads/bracelets from imported raw glass, gold/silver coin pellets); hinterland service centre. Sequence: third-century open farms → second-century population growth, craft specialization, coin production → destruction/rebuild with massive defences c.130s–120s BCE (Cimbri/Teutones insecurity context) → peak then decline after mid–first century BCE under political/economic reordering.

**Comparative trajectories** (same source): Bibracte (Aedui)—hilltop route nexus, ~135 ha, >5 km ramparts, “largest and richest” Aeduan oppidum; tribal councils and Vercingetorix’s confirmation as war leader 52 BCE; occupation second century–c.20 BCE then shift to Augustodunum (Autun) ~20 km away. Závist (Bohemia) expands from earlier hillfort to ~150 ha / 9 km perimeter, minting, ends in fire c.25/20 BCE. Levroux: open village then defended ~20 ha settlement c.80–70 BCE 1.5 km away. Basle: open Rhine settlement → fortified ~5 ha (cathedral hill) c.60 BCE, possibly tied to Rauraci/Helvetii movement 58 BCE. Celtiberian oppida-like settlements (Iberian inspiration) and Los Cogotas (~14.5 ha) are related but regionally distinct. Entremont (Saluvii) destroyed 123 BCE on the Provence interface.

**Coinage** (Cunliffe, attributed): gold and silver coins among Celts interpreted primarily for **gift exchange and political legitimation**—symbols of issuing authority (often naming kings in the first century BCE) that made holders beholden to the issuer; sporadic mintings may have legitimized leadership claims—not commerce “in the capitalist sense.” Coinage is one visible element, with route-node oppida, of a late Iron Age package replacing raid-and-feast display with potlatch-like conspicuous giving/consumption and control of goods, raw materials, and luxuries (especially wine) for clients.

## Applications

### Roman demand catalyst thesis (Cunliffe 1997 — theoretical)

Cunliffe’s core model (attributed throughout): after the Second Punic War, Roman economic transformation (citizen armies, rural depopulation, latifundia, slave labour) created structural demand for **slaves** and for **overseas markets** for surplus (especially wine), plus metals and other barbarian products. From c.200 BCE Rome grew dependent on supplies beyond its direct control. Concurrently, Celtic societies changed *sui generis*: raiding opportunities restricted by Rome, Dacia, and Germanic pressure; elite display shifted from raid-and-feast to commodity manipulation and intensified production. Oppida are large nucleated settlements with hierarchical labour mobilization, often evolving from undefended villages, high craft output for hinterlands, sometimes resident Roman traders (e.g. Cenabum/Orléans per Caesar), tribal assemblies and embassies—**functions of towns by mid–first century BCE**. Economic imperative (routes, raw materials, consumer goods) mattered; **one prime cause, perhaps the initial catalyst, was intensifying Roman demand for manpower and raw materials after c.200 BCE**. Oppida and their economies are features of the barbarian periphery adjacent to Rome; “Trade preceded the flag”; within about a century Rome absorbed most of the oppida zone. Retrospect formulation: tribes transform toward urban-based states where gifts, dues, and exchanges are articulated through **coinage**—gradual and uneven 200–50 BCE near Roman frontiers, then overtaken by Germanic, Dacian/Sarmatian, and Roman pressures.

### Zonal model and conquest geography

Three broad social zones in the second/first centuries BCE (Cunliffe generalization, attributed): (1) core tribes/early states with elected magistrates; (2) intermediate kingdoms with large oppida; (3) periphery still expressing status via sumptuous burial rites; older systems persist beyond to the Rhine and in much of Britain/Ireland. Concentric-peripheries reading of Europe: decreasing complexity away from the Mediterranean. In 52 BCE Caesar’s ability to pick off urbanized central Gaul oppida (Vellaunodunum, Cenabum, Noviodunum, Avaricum) was impossible in less-centralized Armorica/Belgica; SE Britain’s semi-urbanized oppida zone was later judged easy to integrate into the Roman system. Post-conquest: Bibracte adopts Roman-style houses and artisan growth before elite relocation to Autun; northern Suessones shift Pommiers → Villeneuve-Saint-Germain → Soissons; Bohemia/Moravia germanization ends large continental oppida there.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Definitional fuzziness:** Caesar’s portmanteau plus poorly excavated, functionally varied large settlements leave the class boundaries contested; hillfort vs oppidum and Iberian/British analogues resist a single checklist.
- **Urban projection:** risk of reading Roman or Mediterranean city expectations into nucleated fortified craft centres; Cunliffe himself stresses multi-function regional centres, not *polis* copies.
- **Causal weight of Rome:** the Roman-demand catalyst is an **attributed interpretive model**—Roman contact + internal raid obsolescence + commodity display; indigenous intensification without Roman proximity remains part of the package; regional trajectories (Manching vs Levroux vs Galatian fortresses) warn against a single evolutionary ladder.
- **Excavation bias:** yardstick sites (Manching) are still sparsely sampled; enclosed area and rampart mass can mislead without internal evidence.
- **Four-way non-identity:** oppidum ≠ Celtic language community ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people; Celtiberian oppida-like sites and Castro *castros* show parallel fortification without La Tène package identity.
- **Afterlife politics:** nineteenth-century nationalist excavation (Napoleon III, Bibracte) and late-twentieth-century pan-European framing both shape which sites and questions get funded—reflexivity content on the concept itself (Cunliffe 1997).
