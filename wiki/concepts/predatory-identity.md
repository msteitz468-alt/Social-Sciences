# Predatory Identity

## Definition as Coined

[[appadurai-arjun|Arjun Appadurai]] (2000; *Fear of Small Numbers*, 2006) defines predatory identities as "those identities whose social construction and mobilization require the extinction of other, proximate social categories, defined as threats to the very existence of some group, defined as a we." They emerge out of pairs (or larger sets) of identities with long histories of close contact, mixture, and mutual stereotyping; one member of the pair turns predatory "by mobilizing an understanding of itself as a threatened majority."

Key properties Appadurai attributes to them:

- **Almost always majoritarian** — based on claims about, and on behalf of, a threatened cultural majority seeking exclusive identification with the nation.
- **Identity turns predatory** when a majority identity mobilizes the [[anxiety-of-incompleteness]] — the gap between numerical majority and the fantasy of national purity and wholeness.
- **Pseudo-demographic reasoning** is characteristic: claims that the majority will "trade places" with the minority (rising birthrates, conversion, migration). The "inner reciprocity" of the census categories majority/minority — each can plausibly morph into the other — is what makes the fear mobilizable.
- **A project, not a fact**: predatory identities "require mobilization through the discourses of crisis and the practices of violence" — Appadurai's prime examples are Nazi "Germanness" and the Hindutva construction of a Hindu majority in India (which he calls "a double fiction," a colonial-census construct riven by caste).

## Semantic History

Introduced in a 2000 essay and consolidated in the 2006 book; the term has since circulated in genocide studies, South Asian studies, and analyses of majoritarian populism. Appadurai frames it explicitly as a correction to the we/they scapegoat tradition (Cooley, Mead, Freud, and the conflict tradition of [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]), which he argues treats we-making as a mechanical by-product of they-making and is not "deeply reflective" about how collective selves form.

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized as a measured variable; used interpretively in discourse analysis of majoritarian rhetoric and nationalist mobilization.

## Applications

Appadurai applies it to Nazi anti-Semitism (arguing, against Goldhagen 1996, that anti-Semitism had to be actively and expensively mobilized, not merely captured) and to the Hindu Right in India, 1985–2004 (the Shah Bano case, Masjid-vs-Mandal politics, "appeasement" discourse). He argues all majoritarianisms — liberal ones included — "have in them the seeds of genocide."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is diagnostic rather than predictive: Appadurai's own list of tipping-point conditions (state capture by racialized nationalists, census-made enumerated communities, border/population misfit, successful fear campaigns) is offered as "not intended to be exhaustive or predictive." Only the title chapter of the coining book is ingested; a fuller critical reception awaits ingest of the whole work and secondary literature.
