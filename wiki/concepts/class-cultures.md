# Class Cultures (Collins)

**Class cultures** are, in [[collins-randall|Randall Collins]]'s *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975), the distinctive outlooks, deference styles, sociability, and beliefs produced causally by occupational position — chiefly by the experience of **giving and taking orders** and by position in networks of communication, rather than by income or property as such.

## Definition as Coined

Collins argues (attributed) that "the observable behavior of individuals in everyday life" is the proper subject matter of a theory of stratification, and that a small number of variables explain outlook. The primary variable is the order-giving/order-taking continuum: those who give orders to many and take from few (upper class), those who both give and take (middle class), and order-takers only (working class). "One animal cows another to its heels: That is the archetypal situation of organizational life and the shaper of classes and cultures." He explicitly adopts [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]]'s (1959) generalization of Marx — occupational classes as "power classes within the realm of work," of which property is a special case.

## Semantic History

The term recasts the older sociological notion of "class culture"/"class subculture" (Warner, Centers, the community-study tradition) as a *causal* product of interaction rather than a descriptive stratum. It draws directly on [[weber-max|Weber]]'s surveys of the religious propensities of status groups (military nobility, bureaucratic elites, petty bourgeoisie, workers) and on [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s "social density," which Collins splits into two measurable variables.

## Operationalizations

Collins specifies four partly independent dimensions:

1. **Order-giving/order-taking** (authority) — order-giving produces "assuredness, cool composure," organizational identification; order-taking produces subservience, fatalism, distrust, an extrinsic/amoral orientation (propositions 1.1–1.8).
2. **Cosmopolitanism / mutual surveillance** — diversity and volume of communicative contact, split from Durkheim's "social density": high surveillance → conformity and reified belief; high cosmopolitanism → abstract, relativistic, long-range thinking (propositions 2.1–2.2). Collins reinterprets the "authoritarian personality" as an effect of low cosmopolitanism.
3. **Wealth** — an intervening resource enabling self-display, storable and inheritable beyond its organizational base.
4. **Physical demands of work** — shaping valuation of toughness versus refinement.

The individual's outlook is "the linear sum" of authority and density experiences across spheres: work, politics, home, sociable recreation, and community (proposition 3.0).

## Applications

Collins derives ideal-typical cultures — upper, upper-middle, lower-middle ("petit bourgeois" rigidity from the most contested class position), working, and lower — and uses the model to explain class endogamy (marriage, clubs, friendship), status-group formation, ethnicity (as a variable of the same model, not a separate category), and "education as pseudoethnicity" (his credential-society thesis in nascent form).

## Critiques and Limitations

Collins flags the "antiworking-class bias" of a literature written by middle-class researchers. Critics note that presenting cultures as ideal types along continua leaves the boundaries and the aggregation rule ("linear sum") loosely specified, and that folding ethnicity and education into a single stratification model risks over-unification. Claims here are **attributed** to Collins (1975), not asserted as findings.

## Lamont 1992 — French and American upper-middle-class cultures

[[lamont-michele|Michèle Lamont]] (*[[lamont-money-morals-manners-1992|Money, Morals, and Manners]]*; study: [[lamont-money-morals-manners-study]]) treats upper-middle-class culture as patterned [[boundary-work]] rather than a single lifestyle package. She argues French and American UMC men share three available standards of worth — moral, socioeconomic, cultural — but attach different relative salience: cultural boundaries sharpest among her French interviewees; socioeconomic among Americans; moral roughly equal. Internal [[class-cultures|class-cultural]] variation tracks occupation (social/cultural specialists vs for-profit workers) and trajectory (first- vs multi-generation UMC). National “class cultures” for her are institutionalized boundary patterns plus repertoires, not modal personality.
