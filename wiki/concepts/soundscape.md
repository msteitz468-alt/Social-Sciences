# Soundscape

## Definition as Coined

The acoustic environment as culturally experienced — the sound-world within which social life occurs. The term originates in R. Murray Schafer's acoustic ecology; this page documents its archaeological operationalization, principally as developed in [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]], who argued archaeologists too often assume "the past was mute."

## Semantic History

From a descriptive term in music/environmental studies (1970s), the concept migrated into anthropology (Feld's work on sound and sentiment) and, by the 1990s–2000s, into archaeology: Watson (2001) on Neolithic burial-chamber acoustics and Stonehenge; Houston and Taube (2000) on Maya "speech scrolls" and building acoustics; Hosler (1994) on West Mexican bells valued for sound over utility. Moore (2005) formalized a testable version for settlement archaeology.

## Operationalizations

Moore's operationalization rests on published acoustics (Egan 1988; Webster 1979): whispers (25–40 dB at source) are intelligible to roughly 2–4 m in quiet environments; normal voices (50–65 dB) to roughly 8–16 m; intensity falls ~6 dB per doubling of distance for point sources. His working rule: whisper ≤4 m, normal conversation ~8 m.

**Moore's hypothesis (attributed):** in egalitarian societies lacking formal dispute-resolution institutions, conflict is expressed in voice and, unresolved, produces group fission; dwelling spacing is therefore a material index of cohesion. Cohesive camps cluster within overlapping conversation range (~4–8 m apart — whispers private, talk shared); simmering conflict produces spacing beyond ~8 m ("controlled avoidance") (Moore 2005).

## Applications

- **Ethnoarchaeological**: Dobe !Kung camps ([[juhoansi]]) photographed/mapped 1947–1968 show circular plans with huts ~4 m apart; the 1980 plan shows dispersal beyond conversation range, attributed by Yellen (1984) to economic transformation and social conflict. Parallel cases: [[hadza]] camps (Woodburn), Efe camps (Fisher and Strickland 1991 — who explicitly excluded predator fear as the driver of close spacing), Nunamiut, Northern Paiute, Seri, and Gould's Tikatika (Ngatatjara) case, where Gould (1980) cautioned that most such social dynamics are not recoverable from material remains alone.
- **Archaeological**: Moore applied the framework to Andean Archaic/Early Formative sites — Acha-2, [[asana]], [[paloma]], and early Valdivia dwellings at [[real-alto]] — reading their tight house spacing as egalitarian-pattern soundscapes (Moore 2005, attributed interpretation).
- **Ritual sound**: Andean processions integrate structured sound and silence — ethnographic (Sallnow's Qoyllur Rit'i pilgrimage bands; Bastien's Kaata flutists), ethnohistoric (Inca funerals' noisy fiesta then silent cortege), and material (the Chimú maqueta from Huaca de la Luna positioning musicians at patio corners) — see [[procession]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Gould and Yellen (1987) proposed predator avoidance, not sound, to explain cross-continental spacing differences; Moore challenged the evidence (single-community samples, pastoral confounds, !Kung indifference to abundant predators) and argued vocal alarm-calling would subsume predation pressure within the soundscape framework in any case — both positions recorded, not adjudicated.
- The method's ethnographic base is foragers with sound-permeable dwellings; extension to societies with substantial architecture requires different variables (visibility, enclosure — see [[monumentality]]).
- Gould's caveat stands as a general limit: the social meanings of any specific acoustic arrangement are usually archaeologically unrecoverable; the framework licenses claims about interaction potential, not content.
