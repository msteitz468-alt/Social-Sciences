# Columbus Commemorations

## Definition as Coined

[[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] (*[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]*, Ch.4 “Good Day, Columbus”) analyzes **Columbus commemorations** as a case of historical production under **abundance** of sources and narratives. Until 1992, he argues, an alleged global agreement on the significance of Columbus’s first voyage — forged and recent — masked a history of **conflicts**. Silences appear in the **interstices of competing appropriations**, not primarily from missing facts.

> Creating a single manageable “Discovery” moment (October 12, 1492) **facilitates narrativization**: chronology replaces process; context fades; naming (“discovery”) is “a narrative of power disguised as innocence.”

Trouillot prefers formulations such as “stumbled on the Bahamas” / **conquest** over **discovery**, which centers Europe as foundation of others’ historicity.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Move (Trouillot, attributed) |
|---|---|
| 1492 as lived process | Fall of Granada and Atlantic voyages as disorderly series; landfall not “the event of the year” for contemporaries |
| Early modern | Columbus not immediately Spain’s favorite hero; later heroization |
| 1792 US | Tammany / Columbian Order banquet and monument as early New World public myth |
| Late 19th c. | Planned production of national traditions; Spanish 1892 quadricentennial (Cánovas; Spain as main character); Chicago 1893 World’s Fair (money/size; Yankee bazaar wrapping hemispheric expansion) |
| Ethnic US politics | Italian then Irish Catholic lobby success; Columbus Day legal holiday by 1912; naturalization “whitens” claimants |
| Latin America | Ambivalence then *Día de la Raza* / Day of the Americas — blending scripts |
| 1992 quincentennial | Unprecedented apparatus yet flop vs 1890s; activist redefinition as conquest; loud dissent blocked fixed packaging |

## Operationalizations

Commemorations **sanitize** lived history and package it for mass consumption; mass participation reinforces the illusion that 1492 contemporaries already knew the day was momentous. Annual fixed dates sever landfall from 1492 context. Successful commemorations **decontextualize** and thereby open competitive readings.

Linked to [[historical-silences]] fourth moment (retrospective significance) and [[historicity-1-and-2]]: celebrations trivialize H1 process while mythicizing H2.

## Applications

- Public history and holiday politics in the US and Latin America
- Naming debates (Holocaust / conquest / encounter / discovery) of the 1990s
- Epilogue: Port-au-Prince crowd dumps Columbus statue into the sea at fall of Duvalier (1986); Haitian *kolon* = Columbus / colonist

## Critiques and Limitations
