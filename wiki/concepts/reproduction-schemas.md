# Reproduction Schemas

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume 2]]*, Part Three (drafted mainly in "Manuscript VIII", 1878; published by [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] in 1885), [[marx-karl|Marx]] models the annual reproduction of the **total social capital** — not an individual capital's turnover — by dividing all production into two departments:

- **Department I** — means of production (commodities that enter productive consumption)
- **Department II** — means of consumption (commodities entering individual consumption of workers and capitalists)

Each department's annual product decomposes into constant capital (c), variable capital (v), and surplus-value (s). Marx's baseline numbers (simple reproduction, rate of surplus-value 100%):

| | c | v | s | Total |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I (means of production) | 4,000 | 1,000 | 1,000 | 6,000 |
| II (means of consumption) | 2,000 | 500 | 500 | 3,000 |

Marx argues three exchanges dispose of the whole product: II(v+s) is consumed within Department II; 4,000 Ic is exchanged among Department I capitalists; and the "major exchange" trades I(v+s) against IIc. Hence the **equilibrium condition of simple reproduction: I(v+s) = IIc** — Department I's wage-and-surplus portion must equal Department II's constant capital.

**All theoretical claims attributed to Marx.** The schema numbers are illustrative arithmetic, not empirical data (Marx and Engels flag the departmental ratios as chosen for convenience).

## Key Claims (attributed to Marx)

- **Dual condition of reproduction.** Reproduction requires both value realization (commodities sell at their value) and material adequacy (the physical composition of output must match the material requirements of continued production). Neither is automatically guaranteed.
- **Smith's dogma refuted.** Against Adam Smith's claim that the whole annual product's value resolves into wages + profit + rent (v+s), Marx argues a constant-capital component always remains: the year's labour adds only the new value (v+s); the constant-capital value is transferred, not newly produced. Smith's error rests on conflating the value of the annual product with the annual value product, itself rooted in missing the twofold character of labour.
- **Necessities and luxuries.** Department II subdivides into necessary means of subsistence (IIa) and luxuries (IIb); luxury-sector employment depends on how much surplus-value capitalists spend as revenue, making luxury workers the first casualties of crisis.
- **Against underconsumptionism.** Marx calls it "a pure tautology" to explain crises by lack of effective demand, noting that crises are typically preceded by periods of rising wages — Engels's footnote adds this "should be noted by prospective supporters of Rodbertus's theory of crises".
- **Fixed-capital replacement as crisis source.** Balanced simple reproduction additionally requires that the fixed capital falling due for full replacement in a year equal the annual wear-and-tear of the still-functioning fixed capital; when this balance fails, crises can arise "despite reproduction on a constant scale" — a disproportion internal to even ideal simple reproduction.
- **Money mechanics.** Even simple reproduction necessarily involves hoard formation (depreciation funds, gold production); the capitalist class as a whole casts into circulation the money that realizes its own surplus-value.
- **Expanded reproduction (Ch. 21, unfinished).** Accumulation requires I(v+s) > IIc; hoard formation (one-sided sales) is the precondition of accumulation, resolved socially by capitalists splitting into hoarders and reinvestors who face each other as sellers and buyers. Marx works multi-year schemas showing the two departments must expand in mutually conditioning proportion; the balance "is itself an accident" under capitalist anarchy — the conditions of normal reproduction are equally "possibilities of crisis". The chapter is a draft (Manuscript VIII) and breaks off mid-analysis.

## Operationalizations

The schemas are value models, not statistical instruments; later traditions operationalized them variously — Tugan-Baranovsky's three-department extension, mathematical equilibrium models (Lange, Koshimura, and others, per Mandel 1978), and comparisons with input-output tables. These are receptions, recorded on [[reproduction-schemas-debate]].

## Applications

- The framework for Marx's own analysis of simple and expanded reproduction, crisis possibilities, and the money mechanics of realization (*Capital* Vol. 2, Part Three).
- The analytical battleground of the Second International's accumulation and imperialism debates ([[reproduction-schemas-debate]]).
- Per Mandel (attributed), a forerunner of modern aggregation — input-output analysis and macro-income accounting — and of growth/two-sector models that engaged the schemas directly.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The expanded-reproduction chapter is an unfinished manuscript; how (and whether) equilibrium growth is possible became the [[reproduction-schemas-debate]] (Luxemburg, Bauer, Grossman, and others).
- "Neo-harmonicist" readings (Tugan-Baranovsky, Hilferding, Bauer, Bukharin, per Mandel's characterization) took the schemas as proof capitalism could grow proportionately; Mandel argues Marx meant the opposite — equilibrium as exception, disproportion as rule. Positions, not adjudicated here.
- The schemas abstract from foreign trade, credit, prices diverging from values, and technical change — extensions contested ever since.
