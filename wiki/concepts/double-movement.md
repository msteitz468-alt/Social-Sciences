# The Double Movement

## Definition as Coined

[[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s master concept for nineteenth- and twentieth-century social dynamics: "For a century the dynamics of modern society was governed by a **double movement**: the market expanded continuously but this movement was met by a countermovement checking the expansion in definite directions." He personifies it as "the action of two organizing principles in society":

- **Economic liberalism**, "aiming at the establishment of a self-regulating market, relying on the support of the trading classes, and using largely laissez-faire and free trade as its methods";
- **The principle of social protection**, "aiming at the conservation of man and nature as well as productive organization, relying on the varying support of those most immediately affected by the deleterious action of the market — primarily, but not exclusively, the working and the landed classes — and using protective legislation, restrictive associations, and other instruments of intervention as its methods."

The countermovement concentrates on the three [[fictitious-commodities|fictitious commodities]]: labor (factory law, unions, social insurance), land (land law, agrarian tariffs, conservation), and money (central banking). It is "vital for the protection of society," yet "in the last analysis... incompatible with the self-regulation of the market, and thus with the market system itself" — the tension that, for Polanyi, drove the system to breakdown.

## The paradox: "laissez-faire was planned; planning was not"

Polanyi's most cited claim overturns the liberal narrative of freedom-from-the-state. The free market was **deliberately engineered** by continuous, centrally organized state action (the 1834 Poor Law, enclosure administration, repeal of protective statutes, the gold standard) — "there was nothing natural about laissez-faire; free markets could never have come into being merely by allowing things to take their course... laissez-faire itself was enforced by the state." The protective countermovement, by contrast, arose **spontaneously**, un-coordinated and pragmatic. Hence: "Laissez-faire was planned; planning was not."

## Semantic History

Polanyi rejects the liberal "**myth of the antiliberal conspiracy**" (that protectionism was a concerted plot by nationalists and socialists — a thesis he attributes to [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], Sumner, Mises, and Lippmann), marshalling four proofs: the sheer diversity of interventions (turning Spencer's own 1884 catalogue against him); measures that flipped from individualist to "collectivist" form overnight; near-identical protective legislation enacted across ideologically opposite regimes (Victorian England, Bismarck's Prussia, the Third Republic, Habsburg Austria); and economic liberals themselves resorting to intervention (trade-union and antitrust law) when contract threatened the market. A. V. Dicey's own inquiry, Polanyi notes, found no "collectivist" public opinion preceding the legislation. The concept was later revived as a template for analyzing neoliberal globalization and its backlash.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; traced through the historical record of protective legislation and restrictive association for labor, land, and money, and their cross-national synchrony. Polanyi treats the American case (free land, labor, money until the 1890s, then rapid protectionism once the frontier closed and the gold standard was adopted) as a "striking proof, both positive and negative" that protection accompanies rather than precedes the self-regulating market.

## Applications

The organizing thesis of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*: the protective countermovement's incompatibility with market self-regulation produced cumulative institutional "strains" (unemployment, class tension, pressure on exchanges, imperial rivalry) that culminated in the collapse of the world economy and the rise of [[fascism-and-market-society|fascism]]. Structurally akin to Blau's [[dialectic-of-social-change|dialectic]] (equilibrating and disequilibrating at once), though Polanyi's is a historical, not general-theoretical, claim.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Functionalism and directionality.** Critics note the double movement can read as functionalist (society "reacts to protect itself") and under-specifies who mobilizes and why in any given case.
- **The class base.** Polanyi's insistence that the countermovement was cross-class and society-wide (not the project of one class) is itself contested; Marxists read it as understating class agency. Polanyi's own claim — "the fate of classes is much more often determined by the needs of society than the fate of society is determined by the needs of classes" — is a deliberate departure from class-interest theories of change.
- Later scholarship contests whether protection was as spontaneous, and liberalism as planned, as the slogan asserts.
