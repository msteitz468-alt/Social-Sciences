# Selective Acculturation

Acculturation path in which both generations learn the host language and culture **without fully abandoning** the home language and norms, typically supported by a co-ethnic community. Portes & Rumbaut present it as the path most protective of parental authority and most favorable for second-generation outcomes in *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001, ch. 3.)* Selective acculturation occurs when the learning process of both generations is embedded in a co-ethnic community large and institutionally diverse enough to slow cultural shift and promote partial retention of parents' language and norms. Associated with less intergenerational conflict, more co-ethnic friends, and second-generation fluent bilingualism (their term/operationalization).

## Semantic History

Part of the three-way typology with [[dissonant-acculturation]] and [[consonant-acculturation]]. In ch. 10 they elevate selective acculturation (with bilingualism) as a "third way" against nativism and forceful assimilationism — analytic construct and policy preference are intertwined in the text.

## Operationalizations

Fluent bilingualism indices; language use with parents/friends; community density; private bilingual schooling (Cuban Miami example). Cut-points and scales in Appendix C / ch. 6.

## Applications

In *Legacies*/CILS, selective acculturation (often indexed by fluent bilingualism) is associated with higher family cohesion, lower parent-child conflict, higher self-esteem, lower depressive affect, and higher educational aspirations/expectations (attributed multivariate results — see [[portes-rumbaut-legacies-study]]). Cuban private bilingual schooling in Miami is their showcase institutional case; Southeast Asian community patterns are treated as selective transmission of achievement drive even when fluent bilingualism is rare.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether bilingualism causes better outcomes or both reflect class/community resources is debated (authors note possible mutual reinforcement). Selective acculturation is exceptional under U.S. school language regimes (~¼ fluent bilinguals in CILS). Ch. 10 elevates it as a preferred "third way" — analytic construct and policy preference are intertwined.
