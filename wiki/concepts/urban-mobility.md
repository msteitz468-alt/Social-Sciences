# Urban Mobility

## Definition as Coined

[[park-robert-e|Park]] treats mobility as a primary ecological factor: transport and communication devices that increase mobility and concentration shape the city's ecological organization; mobility is measured "not merely by change of location, but rather by the number and variety of the stimulations to which the individual or the population responds" (attributed, *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* Ch I).

[[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] distinguishes **movement** from **mobility**: routine movement is not evidence of change; mobility is change of movement in response to a new stimulus — "routine → work; mobility → adventure." Mobility is "perhaps the best index of the state of metabolism of the city" and "the pulse of the community" (attributed, Ch II).

## Semantic History

Chicago 1920s usage stresses ecological flux and stimulation, not only stratification advancement. Distinct from later [[social-mobility|social mobility]] (class position change), though related through occupational and residential sifting. McKenzie adds a **mobility gradient**: increasing mobility from periphery toward the center of an ecological formation (Seattle illustrations).

## Applications

- Linking high-mobility areas to delinquency, gangs, vice, desertion, divorce (Burgess: "our studies of the city").
- Invasion entering at points of greatest mobility (McKenzie).
- Park: mobility and abstract thought; crises more frequent in high-mobility societies.

## Critiques and Limitations

Index validity; mobility valued ambivalently (vitality vs demoralization); "stimulation for stimulation's sake… partakes of the nature of vice" (attributed, Burgess).
