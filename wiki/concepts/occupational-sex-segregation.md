# Occupational Sex Segregation

**Occupational sex segregation** is the concentration of men and women in different occupations and jobs. In *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]*, Ridgeway treats it as a core **positional** inequality that both reflects and sustains [[status-beliefs|gender status beliefs]].

## Definition as Coined

As reported by Ridgeway (citing Charles and Grusky 2004; Cotter et al. 2004; Tomaskovic-Devey et al. 2006): the contemporary U.S. occupational structure is so sex-segregated that most people work in occupations largely filled by their own sex; **more than 40%** of women in the workforce would have to change occupations to eliminate existing segregation. Segregation is **greater still** at the specific-job level within organizations (Petersen and Morgan 1995; Tomaskovic-Devey 1993 — as cited).

## Semantic History

Classic stratification measure; horizontal segregation (different fields) and vertical segregation (authority ranks) both matter. Charles and Grusky (2004), as cited by Ridgeway: men more concentrated in stereotypic masculine skills (including manual jobs) and in top jobs; women more often in stereotypic feminine/service skills and lesser authority.

## Operationalizations

| Measure | Use in Ridgeway 2011 |
|---|---|
| Index / % who must change occupations | Level of segregation (~40%+ of women) |
| Job-title segregation within firms | Often higher than occupational segregation |
| Link to wages | Most of gender wage gap from different jobs, not unequal pay in same title (Petersen and Morgan 1995 et al.) |
| Nurturing-skill devaluation | Female-typed jobs pay less even at similar skill/education (England et al.) |

Ridgeway stresses segregation is **emergent** via job-matching (supply and demand under gender framing), not formal sex-typed hiring rules (usually illegal).

## Applications

Authority gap (women’s job ladders less often lead to CEO track — e.g., HR vs sales/finance); wage gap; peer vs hierarchical male–female encounters at work; continual re-gendering of **new** jobs (Reskin and Roos 1990 via Ridgeway).

## Critiques and Limitations

Cross-national variation (Charles and Grusky’s comparative work) is cited but not the book’s core case. Part-time and contingent work complicate full-time segregation indices.
