## Definition as Coined

Simmel treated superordination and subordination (*Über- und Unterordnung*) not as a
one-sided relation of coercion but as a **form of interaction**, in which the subordinate
party always retains some measure of spontaneity and thereby co-determines the
relationship. As he put it, even where domination "seems to 'crush'" the subordinate, "it
is based not only on coercion or compulsion to yield to it." Analyzing the relation
formally therefore means locating the reciprocal effect concealed beneath an apparently
unilateral influence: "it conceals an interaction, an exchange of influences, which
transforms the pure one-sidedness of superordination and subordination into a
sociological form."

Simmel distinguished two closely related but different phenomena within this general
domain: **authority**, in which a person's significance is transformed into an
"objectivity" — either radiating outward from an individual's own qualities (*generatio
aequivoca*) or descending onto a person from a super-individual power (church, state,
family) — and **prestige**, which "stems from pure personality" alone and never acquires
this super-subjective, quasi-objective character. Authority, Simmel argued, "does not have
[prestige's] ability to the same extent" to make unconditional followers, yet may in fact
rest on a "more profound freedom of the subject" than the enchantment of prestige, because
the believer in authority is the one who performs the transformation of personal into
super-personal value: "The believer in authority himself achieves the transformation."

## Semantic History

| Period/Text | Formulation | Note |

Simmel's typology of the *forms* domination can take — under an individual, under a
plurality, under an objective principle or law — bears a structural resemblance to Max
Weber's later three types of legitimate domination (traditional, charismatic,
legal-rational). The resemblance should not be overstated or treated as identity: Simmel's
concern in this chapter is **formal-sociological** — the reciprocity built into the
relation of domination as such, regardless of its legitimacy basis — whereas Weber's
typology is explicitly about grounds of **legitimacy claims**. Simmel does not use
Weber's vocabulary (traditional/charismatic/legal) here, and no direct influence in either
direction is established by this slice of the source; the parallel is the ingesting
editor's observation, not a claim in either author's text, and should not be asserted as
convergence without further sourcing.

## Operationalizations

Simmel's treatment is not quantified or operationalized in the survey sense; it proceeds
by comparative-historical illustration (Roman law, feudalism, caste, monarchy, hypnotic
suggestion, parliamentary representation) used to isolate the *sociological form* common
to materially disparate cases. The nearest thing to an operational criterion is his
device of asking, for any purportedly one-sided relation, whether the "superordinate"
party is nonetheless bound by norms, expectations, or reciprocal claims it cannot simply
set aside — the presence of such residual claims is his evidence of concealed
interaction.

## Applications

Simmel organizes domination into three forms, elaborated across this chapter:

- **Subordination under an individual.** A group ruled by one person achieves strong
  unification, whether the group forms an "inner unit" with its head or a solidary
  opposition party against him; Simmel notes that domination by one may proceed by
  **leveling** its subjects (making them formally equal beneath the ruler — his example is
  the correlation between despotism and equalization) or by **gradation** (organizing
  subjects into a power pyramid, built either downward from the ruler's waning authority
  or upward from below, as in feudalism's "mixture" of both). He also treats the caste
  relation to the Brahman as a case where "the formal characteristic of subordination
  under an individual may prevail even where there is a plurality of superordinate
  individuals."
- **Subordination under a plurality.** Rule by a collectivity (a state, a corporation, a
  crowd) has structurally different, and unevenly favorable or unfavorable, consequences
  for the subordinate compared with rule by an individual, because collective domination
  tends toward **objectivity** — it can be more impartial and legally consistent, but also
  more impersonally harsh, since "the disappearance of the individual behind the totality"
  removes the possibility of appeal to compassion. Simmel sharply distinguishes this
  objectivity-of-the-corporate-body from the very different psychology of the **physically
  present mass or crowd**, whose suggestibility and loss of individual critical function
  produce impulsive, sometimes atrocious, sometimes magnanimous, collective behavior (his
  examples include the Roman circus audience and, in a troubling comparative aside, lynch
  mobs).
- **Subordination under a principle or objective law.** Roman *lex*, medieval contract
  theories of the state, and the idea of a "higher tribunal" all illustrate how even
  ostensibly one-sided submission to an impersonal norm retains an interactional core:
  Simmel reads the etymology of Roman "lex" as originally meaning "contract," in which
  the king proposed and the people were "acceptors" — a linguistic trace of the
  reciprocity he argues is sociologically real even in law's most unilateral-seeming form.
  This third form is, strictly, the one where "immediate interaction is precluded," and
  Simmel argues its evaluation is historically variable: for "modern, objective man, who
  is aware of the difference between the spheres of spontaneity and of obedience,"
  subordination to an impersonal, uninfluenceable law "is the more dignified situation,"
  whereas older honor cultures felt personal devotion to a ruler as freer and more
  dignified than the "mechanical and passive" obedience to a rule. He grounds the
  contrast in the modern separation of **person from achievement**: "an American girl,
  who would work in a factory without the slightest feeling of humiliation, would feel
  wholly degraded as a family cook," and the impoverished Spanish nobleman kept his
  latent nobility as a lackey (a personal service) but destroyed it as a craftsman (an
  objective trade). Antiquity, by contrast, praised impersonal law directly — Aristotle's
  law as *to meson* ("the moderate," free from passion), Plato's rule of law as a curb
  on selfishness. The same separation lets Simmel describe **super-subordination without
  degradation**: hierarchy can preserve the subordinate's dignity precisely when what is
  obeyed is an objective office or principle rather than a person, and he faults socialism
  and anarchism for a "sociological error" in imagining domination could be abolished
  rather than reconstituted in impersonal form.

## Critiques and Limitations

Simmel's method here is avowedly formal and comparative-historical rather than empirical
in the survey or experimental sense; his illustrations (Roman law, English constitutional
history, feudal tenures, caste, hypnosis, party leadership) are drawn eclectically across
eras and societies to isolate a recurring *form*, not to establish a causal or
statistical generalization about any one case. He acknowledges this himself, noting that
sociological cognition works via "auxiliary construction[s]" of "absolute lines and
figures which in actual social life are found only as beginnings and fragments." A reader
should therefore treat his specific historical claims (e.g., about the Roman Empire, the
Turkish Sultan, or Indian caste) as illustrative material subordinate to the formal
argument, not as independently verified findings; this wiki carries them in attributed
voice accordingly. The concept has not, on the evidence of this slice, been subjected to
empirical operationalization or replication in later sociology — its afterlife appears to
run through subsequent formal and interactionist sociology rather than through
quantitative testing.
