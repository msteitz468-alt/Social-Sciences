# Progressive Global Wealth Tax

Headline policy proposal of Part Four of [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*: a **progressive annual tax on capital** (net wealth), ideally global or at least regional, coupled with high international financial transparency. Framed as the tool needed to regulate twenty-first-century globalized [[patrimonial-capitalism]] beyond updating the twentieth-century [[social-state]] and progressive income tax alone.

## Definition as Coined

Piketty’s ideal instrument is a progressive annual tax on **individual net wealth**—all financial and nonfinancial assets minus debt—at rates set by democratic deliberation. Illustrative schedules in the book include:

- **0%** below €1m; **1%** €1–5m; **2%** above €5m
- Optional modest rates: **0.1%** below €200k; **0.5%** €200k–€1m (as fairer replacement logic for property tax)
- Steeper top: **5%** (or **10%+**) on hundreds of millions / billions if the goal is blocking divergence of top wealth shares or reducing [[wealth-inequality]]

He explicitly calls a fully global capital tax **utopian** but useful as a **reference standard** against which alternatives should be measured. Countries can move incrementally at regional scale (especially Europe). Primary purpose is to **regulate capitalism**—stop indefinite increase of wealth inequality and improve financial/banking regulation via democratic transparency about who owns what—not to replace existing taxes; at most a modest supplement of a few points of national income (three or four at most). Revenue illustration: EU-wide 0/1/2% schedule would affect ~**2.5%** of the population and yield ~**2%** of European GDP, because private wealth exceeds five years of GDP and is concentrated in upper centiles. Flat 0.1% on a global private capital stock of ~five years of income would yield ~0.5% of global income.

## Semantic History

Builds on national wealth taxes, estate/inheritance taxes, and the twentieth-century progressive-tax revolution (interwar income and inheritance taxes). Early progressive capital taxes (Germany, Sweden late nineteenth/early twentieth century) had low rates and non-market assessments that inflation rendered useless; Germany and Sweden abolished annual capital taxes 1990–2010 largely, Piketty argues, because of archaic design, not only tax competition. French ISF (impôt de solidarité sur la fortune) is more modern (annual market values from the 1980s) but exemption-riddled and self-declared. Existing real-estate property taxes typically ignore financial assets and debt and are roughly flat, yet still raise ~1–2% of national income in the English-speaking world. Piketty ranks the capital tax as the “liberal form of capital control,” superior to protectionism (which does nothing against [[r-greater-than-g]]) and less violent than Soviet abolition of private means of production.

## Operationalizations

- **Base**: individual net wealth; market values; automatic information to tax authorities analogous to employer income reporting; pre-filled asset statements.
- **Transparency precondition**: automatic international transmission of banking data so foreign assets appear on statements; sanctions on noncompliant banks **and countries** (e.g. tariffs ≥30% on exports) rather than incomplete regimes such as early FATCA-style rules.
- **Complementary pillars**: progressive income tax, progressive estate tax, and progressive capital tax as three distinct roles—contributive (capital gauges ability to pay when top returns and income are hard to measure) and incentive (stock tax pressures low-return holders; insurance logic favors taxing capital-income streams).
- **Exceptional vs annual**: one-time capital levies for public debt (e.g. flat 15% of private wealth ≈ one year of NI; or 0/10/20% on <1m / 1–5m / >5m for ~20% of GDP)—France 1945 exceptional rates up to 25% (100% on Occupation-period additions). Annual rates must stay low (few percent) vs once-per-generation inheritance rates historically 1/3–2/3 in UK/US 1930–80.
- **“Financial cadaster”**: official wealth-distribution statistics ending reliance on Forbes lists; also crisis-management tool (Cyprus 2013 opacity; Troika lacking who-owned-what data).

## Applications

Response to [[r-greater-than-g]] and size-dependent returns (top fortunes growing ~6–7%/year while average wealth grows slower): “only a progressive tax on capital can effectively impede” explosive top-thousandth share trajectories. European political integration link: realistic technically given Europe’s high private wealth, but requires institutions beyond the ECB—budgetary parliament, debt pooling, joint corporate-tax base. Debt-reduction menu ranks exceptional capital tax first, inflation second, prolonged austerity worst. Contrasts with Chinese capital controls and emerging-country practice as imperfect substitutes. ## Critiques and Limitations

Political feasibility, capital flight, valuation of illiquid assets, double taxation relative to capital-income tax, and sovereignty objections dominate reception. National wealth taxes without automatic bank-information sharing invite evasion and exemption proliferation (France; Italy 2012: 0.8% real estate, 0.1% deposits/financial, stocks exempt—effectively regressive). Whether regulation-via-transparency is separable from revenue ambition is disputed. Piketty’s own “useful utopia” framing concedes the gap between ideal and near-term practice; nationalist protectionism and capital controls remain the default substitutes he ranks as inferior.
