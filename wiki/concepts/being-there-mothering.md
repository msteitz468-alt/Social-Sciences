# Being There (mothering standard)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Promises I Can Keep]]*, [[edin-kathryn|Edin]] and [[kefalas-maria|Kefalas]] report that the low-income unmarried mothers they interviewed often sum up good mothering in two words: **being there**. Analytic elevation of an emic standard: presence, provision of basics (Pampers, food, safe passage to school), and keeping the light on for a “prodigal” adolescent — not exclusively middle-class achievement cultivation.

## Semantic History

Documented in late-1990s/early-2000s Philadelphia–Camden fieldwork; published 2005. Contrasts with intensive/concerted-cultivation mothering ideals associated with middle-class parenting literature (not claimed as the mothers’ rejection of ambition for children, but as a different primary success criterion under constraint).

## Operationalizations

Qualitative coding of mothers’ self-evaluations and narratives in *Promises* Ch.5; not a psychometric index in the source.

## Applications

Explains how mothers can claim mothering success amid children’s school failure, teen pregnancy, addiction, or detention — if the mother remained present. Central to the book’s account of motherhood as a domain of valor ([[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]).

**Ch.5 detail (Edin & Kefalas, attributed):** pride in Pampers, potty training, school drop-off safety, feeding growing teens, keeping the light on for a “prodigal” adolescent; self-denial and brand/respectability displays under poverty; neighborhood as impediment; contrast with middle-class “hothouse” concerted cultivation — poor children grow in a “field” where mother ensures essentials and the rest is up to the child. Linked to [[redemptive-motherhood]] (turning-point narratives) and to the sequencing claim that **marriage** (not children) is what requires years of planning.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of romanticizing constraint if “being there” is treated as equivalent to all child outcomes.
- Authors themselves separate mothers’ self-assessment from external evaluation of child well-being.
- Class and race stereotypes can misread the standard as “low expectations” rather than a coherent ethic under scarce resources and dangerous neighborhoods.
