# Freedom in a Complex Society (Polanyi)

## Definition as Coined

The concluding argument of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]* (Chapter 21). [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] holds that the liberal conception of freedom — freedom as the *absence* of power and compulsion, secured by the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] — is an illusion, because in a complex society power and compulsion are ineliminable ("the reality of society"). This yields a trilemma among three responses to that reality:

- **Liberalism denies it** — clinging to "an illusionary idea of freedom" that requires the absence of regulation and power, and thereby (Polanyi argues) discrediting real freedom and paving the way for its opposite.
- **Fascism accepts it and rejects freedom** — glorifying power as the reality of society and negating the uniqueness of the person (see [[fascism-and-market-society]]).
- **Socialism accepts it and upholds freedom** — resigning itself to the reality of society "and upholds the claim to freedom, in spite of it," by consciously subordinating the market to a democratic society.

Polanyi frames the problem on two planes: an **institutional** level (a balance of freedoms gained and lost through regulation — "not a fundamentally new question") and a deeper **moral/religious** level (whether freedom is possible at all once the reality of society is accepted). The book's climactic formulation: "The discovery of society is thus either the end or the rebirth of freedom." He grounds this in "three constitutive facts in the consciousness of Western man" — knowledge of death (Old Testament), knowledge of freedom (the New Testament's uniqueness of the person), and knowledge of society (revealed by industrial society, with **Robert Owen** as its nearest vehicle).

## Semantic History

Polanyi defines **socialism** here (attributed) as "the tendency inherent in an industrial civilization to transcend the self-regulating market by consciously subordinating it to a democratic society." The argument extends a Christian-humanist tradition (Owen's critique of the "individualization" of man) into a post-market politics, and prefigures later social-democratic and communitarian critiques of market liberalism.

## Operationalizations

Not measured; a normative-analytic claim. Its concrete program is "taking labor, land, and money out of the market": the wage contract ceasing to be purely private, land incorporated into specific institutions (homestead, cooperative, township, park, preserve) rather than sold as a commodity, and money managed via "functional finance." Polanyi insists freedom must be *built* through deliberate institutional safeguards — headed by "the right of the individual to a job under approved conditions, irrespective of his or her political or religious views, or of color and race" — not assumed as an automatic by-product of the market.

## Applications

The normative culmination of the [[double-movement]] and the [[embeddedness]] thesis: once the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] is recognized as a utopia that must be transcended, the question becomes how to reconcile freedom with the reality of a complex, planned, and powerful society. Central to Polanyi's reception among democratic socialists and critics of neoliberalism.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The liberalism/fascism/socialism trilemma is schematic; liberal theorists reject the claim that their conception of freedom denies "the reality of society."
- Polanyi gestures at, more than specifies, how democratic planning avoids the coercions he ascribes to fascism — the institutional guarantees are named but not worked out.
- The religiously-inflected framing (three "revelations") is idiosyncratic and sits awkwardly with the book's otherwise secular analysis.
