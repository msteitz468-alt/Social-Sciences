# Cannibal Cogito

[[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]'s name for the logic of personhood he reconstructed from [[arawete|Araweté]] cosmology (fieldwork 1981–83): the self is constituted not by reflection on itself but by *becoming its Other* — deceased, enemy, god — with cannibalism as the operator of that becoming.

## Definition as Coined

In *[[viveiros-de-castro-enemys-point-of-view-1992|From the Enemy's Point of View]]* (1992 [1986]), Viveiros de Castro argued that the Araweté person is completed only in death: the celestial soul is devoured by the Maï gods, then recomposed and bathed into eternal youth — becoming a god, i.e. becoming the Other. "The cannibal cogito does not express the narcissistic geometry of representation, but the topological torsion of other-becoming"; the system is "a twisted circular band with no reverse side" in which deceased = enemy = god = self. Personhood is processual: "Having been, going to be, it is not, properly speaking, anything: it becomes." Where he read Gê (Central Brazilian) thought as constructing the person by "analytic judgments" of identity and opposition, Araweté/Tupi-Guarani thought proceeds by "synthetic judgments" that make time intervene — "here the Other is not a mirror, but a destiny" (all attributed to Viveiros de Castro 1992).

The formula's sharpest ethnographic anchors (all as documented 1981–83, attributed):
- The Maï are simultaneously the Araweté ideal and figures of the enemy (*awī*) — genipap-painted, ferocious, "eaters of raw flesh," cannibals. "The Maï are the Enemy — but the Maï are the Araweté. This is the problem — and divine cannibalism is the solution."
- The killer (*moropï'nã*) fuses with his slain enemy's spirit, sings in the enemy's first person, and alone among the Araweté is not eaten by the gods after death because he is already divinized: "Whereas the Tupinamba were eaten in order to avoid rotting, the Araweté killer is not eaten because he does not rot… Cannibalism is a metaphysical cuisine."
- Divine cannibalism "humanizes the gods, that is, socializes them" — cosmology is "immediately a sociology"; "That is why the gods devour the dead: so that the Araweté can be — can be real."

## Semantic History

Coined in the 1986 Portuguese monograph (*Araweté: os deuses canibais*), Englished in 1992. The construct antedates and feeds the vocabulary of [[perspectivism]] and [[multinaturalism]] (mid-1990s); in *[[viveiros-de-castro-cannibal-metaphysics-2014|Cannibal Metaphysics]]* (2009/2014) it is generalized, with Deleuzian tooling, into a program for anthropology as a whole ("Anti-Narcissus" — a title already used for chapter 10 of the 1992 book). In its travel from ethnographic construct to program slogan, the term drifted from a claim about Araweté eschatology to a claim about Amerindian thought generally — a scale promotion readers should keep dated and attributed.

## Operationalizations

None; the construct is interpretive. Its evidential base is ethnographic (a single ethnographer, ~12 months among ~136 people) plus 16th-century Tupinambá chronicles read comparatively — see [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]].

## Applications

Organizes the analysis of Araweté death (tripartite decomposition into corpse, terrestrial specter, celestial soul), the shaman/killer symmetry in song, and Viveiros de Castro's rereading of Tupinambá anthropophagy as "enemy-becoming" against Fernandes's sacrificial model. Widely cited in Amazonianist work on predation, alterity, and personhood, and in the [[ontological-turn]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The construct rests on one ethnographer's interpretation of a small, recently contacted population, extended by structural comparison; Viveiros de Castro himself repeatedly flagged the limits of his song translations and marked several analogies (Freudian unconscious, Stoic incorporeals, Aristotelian causes) as his own overlay the Araweté would not necessarily endorse. Critics of the wider ontological-turn program (see [[ontological-turn-debate]]) question the generalization from such constructs to "Amerindian thought." The Tupinambá extension is contested ground: [[tupinamba-cannibalism-debate]].
