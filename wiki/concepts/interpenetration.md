# Interpenetration

## Definition as Coined

**Interpenetration** is [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s term for the interdependence of
analytically distinct systems (personality, social, cultural). [[luhmann-niklas|Luhmann]]
redefines it in *[[luhmann-social-systems-1995|Social Systems]]* (1984) as a specific
**intersystem relation between systems that are each other's environments**: "interpenetration
exists when... both systems enable each other by introducing their own already-constituted
complexity into each other." The complexity each supplies arrives as *disorder* (not
pre-reduced information) for the receiving system, so neither system's autonomy or
[[autopoiesis]] is compromised. Interpenetration holds **only between autopoietic systems**.

The paradigm case is the relation of [[psychic-system|psychic]] and social systems, which
"come into being in the course of co-evolution": no social system could exist without the
environment of conscious systems, and consciousness deprived of society could not develop
beyond rudimentary perception — yet "the boundaries of social systems fall within the
consciousnesses of psychic systems" and vice versa, neither being the other's boundary.

## Semantic History

Luhmann's redefinition explicitly breaks with Parsons's usage (his 1978 article
"Interpenetration bei Parsons") and he later drops the term itself — because of its spatial
connotations — for Maturana's **structural coupling**. Two further shifts: (1) interpenetration
answers the [[double-contingency]] problem without recourse to subjectivity or
intersubjectivity; (2) it is **historically variable**, changing with societal complexity
(Luhmann sketches an evolutionary sequence from Greek tragedy's undifferentiated fate, through
the polis, to modern differentiated society). Integration works through **binary
schematization** (esteem/disdain, conformity/deviance), not point-for-point correspondence of
elements — and these binary schematisms are, for Luhmann, the precondition of the modern
"subject": "subjectivity is nothing more than the formulation for a result of interpenetration...
its end product."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Luhmann defines **morality** functionally as one such schematism —
"the totality of the conditions for deciding the bestowal of esteem or disdain within the
system."

## Applications

- Dissolves "the individual" as a foundation of social theory: the human being is an
  interpenetration of organic, psychic, and social systems, and belongs to society's
  *environment* (see [[social-systems-theory]], [[psychic-system]]).
- Grounds Luhmann's account of socialization as **self-socialization** ("all social
  interpenetration occurs as [[communication]]"), replacing role theory, needs theory, and
  natural-law accounts of the person.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because the term names two very different things in Parsons and in Luhmann, it is a recurring
source of confusion — Luhmann himself flagged the "very unclear understanding of
interpenetration [that] has gained currency." Do not read Parsons's normative-integrative sense
and Luhmann's complexity-supply sense as the same concept. As with the rest of the program, the
account is Luhmann's theoretical construction, not an established finding.
