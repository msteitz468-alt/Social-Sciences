## Definition as Coined

Glynn Isaac proposed in the 1970s that concentrations of Oldowan stone artifacts and animal bone at sites such as [[olduvai-gorge|FLK Zinj]] represented "home bases" or "base camps" — places to which early Homo repeatedly returned, in a pattern Isaac argued was uniquely human: "only humans (and not apes) repeatedly use a 'home base' or 'base camp' where garbage accumulates." Isaac explicitly linked the hypothesis to food sharing among group members and a sexual division of labor, framing all three as a linked package of distinctively human behaviors that, if valid, had emerged by at least 2 million years ago.

## Semantic History

The home-base hypothesis was, in Klein's words, "particularly popular in the 1970s" but by the time of Klein's writing (1999) "remains intriguing, but it has been placed in limbo, at least temporarily." The demotion reflects a methodological problem, not new counter-evidence: archaeologists have struggled to isolate a genuine human contribution to Oldowan bone assemblages from carnivore activity, stream transport, and natural accumulation under shade trees — all of which can produce superficially similar bone concentrations. A further complication (Klein, finding) is that at FLK Zinj the apparent duration of occupation implied by the bone accumulation (possibly five to ten years' worth) contrasts sharply with modern tropical hunter-gatherers, who rarely occupy a single spot for more than a few months — suggesting either a non-modern settlement pattern (e.g., a specialized stone-tool-processing or caching locale rather than a residential base) or that people were not in fact the primary bone accumulators there.

## Operationalizations

The concept is operationalized through taphonomic analysis of bone assemblages: cut-mark frequency and placement, carnivore tooth-mark frequency, skeletal-part representation, and spatial association with stone artifacts, weighed against alternative accumulating agents (carnivores, water transport, natural deaths near shade or water). No single criterion has proven sufficient to confirm "home base" status at any Oldowan site.

## Applications

The home-base concept anchored a broader 1970s-80s model of early Homo social organization built around central-place foraging, food sharing, and division of labor, and was closely tied to Isaac's hunting/scavenging and food-sharing arguments. It remains a live reference point in discussions of when central-place foraging — a hallmark of modern hunter-gatherer life — first appears in the archaeological record.

## Critiques and Limitations

The central critique is taphonomic equifinality: the same bone-and-artifact concentrations invoked as evidence for home bases can also result from non-human accumulation processes, and no method has cleanly separated the two at Oldowan-age sites. The anomalously long apparent occupation span at FLK Zinj further complicates a straightforward "residential base camp" reading. Klein treats the hypothesis as an open, unresolved question rather than a settled finding — a [POSITION], not a fact.

The decisive early critique is [[binford-lewis|Binford]]'s *[[binford-bones-1981|Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths]]* (1981), which argued from [[actualistic-studies|actualistic]] control data that the [[olduvai-gorge|Olduvai]] "living floors" are ravaged palimpsests fitting hominid marrow-**scavenging**, not home bases — the founding statement of the [[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting-versus-scavenging debate]].
