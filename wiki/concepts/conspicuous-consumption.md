# Conspicuous Consumption

## Definition as Coined

[[veblen-thorstein|Veblen]] coined **conspicuous consumption** in *[[veblen-leisure-class-1899|The Theory of the Leisure Class]]* (1899, ch. 4) for the consumption of goods and services as visible evidence of wealth, undertaken to command esteem rather than to satisfy physical want. Veblen argued that mere possession of wealth is not enough: "In order to gain and to hold the esteem of men it is not sufficient merely to possess wealth or power. The wealth or power must be put in evidence, for esteem is awarded only on evidence" (ch. 3). Consumption of superior goods — and connoisseurship in choosing them — is one of the two chief ways wealth is put in evidence, the other being [[conspicuous-leisure|conspicuous leisure]].

Veblen distinguished it from **vicarious consumption**: the consumption of food, dress, and dwelling by a wife, servants, or dependents, which redounds to the reputability of the master rather than the consumer (ch. 4). As households descend the social scale, Veblen argued, the master's conspicuous leisure disappears first while the wife's vicarious consumption persists as the last vestige of pecuniary decency — a "curious inversion" in which the husband labors so the wife may display.

## Semantic History

Veblen's technical term migrated into common speech, losing his value-neutral framing and acquiring a straightforwardly pejorative sense (wasteful showing-off). In economics it was formalized as the **Veblen good** — a good whose demand rises rather than falls as its price rises, a violation of the ordinary demand curve. In consumer research, marketing, and the sociology of consumption it underwrites the analysis of status signaling, luxury branding, and positional goods (cf. Fred Hirsch's *Social Limits to Growth*, Bourdieu's *Distinction*). The concept is one of the few coinages of classical social theory to become a standing term of both scholarship and journalism.

## Operationalizations

Veblen did not quantify it; his method was the "discursive review of such everyday facts as lie ready to hand" (his own phrase). Later operationalizations diverge:
- **Economics**: upward-sloping demand for "Veblen goods"; positional/status expenditure.
- **Consumer research**: measured via expenditure on visible vs. non-visible goods, brand prominence, and status-motive scales.
- These measures capture the *signaling* claim but not Veblen's evolutionary account of its origin, and they largely drop the vicarious-consumption dimension.

## Applications

Veblen argued (ch. 4) that as populations urbanize and grow mobile, "consumption begins to hold over leisure as an ordinary means of decency," because leisure requires personal acquaintance to be legible whereas goods can be displayed to strangers — hence the shift from a leisure-based to a consumption-based status economy in modern city life (his illustration: itinerant printers' public treating and drinking). He derived from it a hierarchy of reputable goods (intoxicants and choice foods as honorific male privileges), the persistence of domestic servants past their utility, and — via the pecuniary standard of living (ch. 5) — the ratchet by which standards rise readily but resist reduction, each class emulating the class just above it.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The account is monocausal in tone: status emulation is made to explain nearly all consumption, risking unfalsifiability (a charge Veblen half-anticipated by conceding the thinness of his data).
- It underweights consumption for pleasure, identity, or meaning not reducible to invidious display — later consumption sociology (Douglas & Isherwood, Miller) treats goods as communicative and constitutive, not merely competitive.
- Empirically, the strict "Veblen good" is a limiting case; most goods do not show upward-sloping demand.
- Veblen's evolutionary derivation from a "predatory/barbarian" stage is conjectural social evolutionism of 1899, not ethnographic evidence (see [[veblen-thorstein]]). The concept's descriptive value does not depend on that derivation, and most modern users detach it.
