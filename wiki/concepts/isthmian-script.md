## Definition as Coined

"Isthmian" (also called Epi-Olmec) is the name specialists have given to the hieroglyphic script attested on [[sites/la-mojarra]] Stela 1 and the Tuxtla Statuette, both from the Isthmus of Tehuantepec region of southern Veracruz. Coe & Koontz (2013) describe Stela 1's text — roughly 400 signs in 21 columns — as the longest known Mesoamerican inscription at the time of their writing; the same script recurs on the Tuxtla Statuette, an Epi-Olmec-style jade duck-billed winged figure carrying its own Long Count date.

## Semantic History

The script is distinct from, though related in tradition to, the far older and undeciphered signary on the El Cascajal serpentine block found near [[sites/san-lorenzo]] — Coe & Koontz note El Cascajal's signs "bear no resemblance to any later Mesoamerican script," so the two should not be treated as a single continuous writing tradition without further argument. Isthmian script is chronologically and stylistically closer to Izapan-tradition monuments (see [[sites/izapa]]) and to the early Long Count calendar (see [[concepts/long-count]]), dating toward the end of the Late Preclassic (2nd century CE, per La Mojarra's two Long Count dates).

## Operationalizations

The corpus available for study is small — essentially the La Mojarra stela and the Tuxtla Statuette — which limits any decipherment attempt to a handful of inscriptions.

## Applications

John Justeson and Terence Kaufman proposed a decipherment identifying Isthmian as a mixed logographic/phonetic writing system encoding a proto-Zoquean language, consistent with the known distribution of Mixe-Zoquean languages in the region (Coe & Koontz 2013). This is an attributed claim about a specific linguistic identification of the script, not an established wiki-voice fact.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coe & Koontz state plainly that "this proposed decipherment has not received general acceptance," and that acceptance is pending either the discovery of further Isthmian texts or a bilingual inscription that could independently confirm readings. The Justeson–Kaufman proposal is therefore recorded here as a **position**, not an accepted decipherment — a live methodological dispute over source reliability and reading confidence characteristic of small, undeciphered epigraphic corpora generally.
