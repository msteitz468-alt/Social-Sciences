# Enkrateia (Self-Mastery)

## Definition as Coined

*Enkrateia* (ἐγκράτεια) is the classical Greek term that [[foucault-michel|Foucault]], in *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984), takes as the **self-forming activity** (*ascesis*, ethical work) of Greek sexual ethics — what the free man does to himself to be capable of the right [[chresis|use of pleasures]]. He defines it (attributed) as "an active form of self-mastery, which enables one to resist or struggle, and to achieve domination in the area of desires and pleasures."

## Semantic History

Foucault (crediting the classicist Helen North) reports that **Aristotle was the first to distinguish systematically** *enkrateia* from *sōphrosunē* (σωφροσύνη, temperance/moderation). On Aristotle's axis: **sōphrosunē** is the stable virtue of choosing reasonable principles and taking pleasure in the mean itself (its opposite *akolasia*, deliberate immoderation); **enkrateia** is the effortful *struggle*, continence, "combat" (its opposite *akrasia*, being overcome despite holding correct principles). Foucault reads *enkrateia* as the **prerequisite of *sōphrosunē*** — the training that produces moderation as a settled state. This wiki folds *sōphrosunē* here as the companion/telos term rather than giving it a separate page; the two mark the effortful and the achieved poles of the same self-relation.

## Operationalizations — the agonistic, heautocratic structure

Foucault reconstructs *enkrateia* through four features (attributed):
1. **Agonistic** — pleasures and desires are cast as an enemy force to be resisted; the vocabulary is battle, wrestling, siege. Virtue is a "combat."
2. **Reflexive** — it is a "solo contest," a battle "between oneself and oneself," resting (per Plato) on a division of the soul into a better and a worse part, self-mastery being the better part's rule over the worse. This is contrasted with the later Christian linkage of concupiscence to an external tempter.
3. **Domination, not extirpation** — victory is stable rule over desire, not its abolition; the moderate man still feels desire but is never overcome (Socrates resisting Alcibiades proves the *capacity* to resist, not the absence of desire). Foucault names this the **"heautocratic"** structure of the subject — a relation of command-obedience, mastery-docility with oneself, opposed to the Christian "decipherment-purification."
4. **Isomorphic with rule of others** — the heautocratic relation is patterned on **household government** (Xenophon's *Oeconomicus*) and **civic government** (Plato's *Republic*): self-rule, household-rule, and city-rule are treated as one continuous art. Notably, classical Greek thought did **not** yet make *askēsis* a separate, specialized technique: "the master of himself and the master of others received the same training."

## Applications — freedom, virility, and truth

Achieved through *enkrateia*, moderation was characterized as **freedom** — not the preservation of an original innocence but active mastery; freedom's opposite was not natural determinism but **enslavement of the self by oneself** ("to be free in relation to pleasures was to be free of their authority; it was not to be their slave"). This freedom was **positive and political**: the self-mastery that qualified a man to govern others, giving the paired figures of the vicious tyrant (mastered by his own passions) and the virtuous ruler.

Foucault stresses that moderation had an essentially **"virile" structure** — being active toward what is by nature passive within oneself — and that *akrasia* was coded "feminine" as submission to pleasure *as such*, regardless of the sex of the object. This is a load-bearing case for the four-way non-identity: the Greek virile/effeminate line does **not** map onto the modern hetero/homosexual axis. Finally, moderation required a **relation to truth (*logos*)** in three forms — structural (reason ruling desire), instrumental (prudence about measure and timing), and ontological (the soul's self-recognition, the *Phaedrus* myth) — but, crucially, this relation "never took the form of a decipherment/hermeneutics of desire": it opened instead onto an [[arts-of-existence|aesthetics of existence]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The *enkrateia*/*sōphrosunē* distinction and its "heautocratic" reading are Foucault's synthesis of Aristotle, Plato, and Xenophon; the vocabulary of a "combat with oneself" is his gloss, attributed.
- Reading moderation as a "virile" mastery risks reinscribing the ancient gender hierarchy as analysis; Foucault records but does not fully neutralize this, and the Conclusion concedes the whole ethics "rested on a very harsh system of inequalities and constraints."
- The claim that *askēsis* was not yet autonomous in classical Greece is a **discontinuity** thesis pointing forward to the Christian pastoral; it is Foucault's periodization, not settled intellectual history.
