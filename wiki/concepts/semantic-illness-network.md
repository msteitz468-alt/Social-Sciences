# Semantic Illness Network

## Definition as Coined

Introduced by [[good-byron|Byron Good]] in "The Heart of What's the Matter" (1977), from
fieldwork with Mary-Jo DelVecchio Good on "heart distress" (*narahatiye qalb*) complaints in
Maragheh, Iran (1970s). Good defined illness as a "syndrome of experience" — "a set of words,
experiences, and feelings which typically run together for members of a society" (Good 1977:
27). The claim (attributed): an illness term's meaning is not its reference to a discrete
biological state but a "product of interconnections" — a network condensing personal,
social, and bodily domains (for heart distress: women's sexuality, contraception, grief,
household conflict, old age).

## Semantic History

Developed with M-J. Good through the 1980s (1980, 1981, 1982, 1992) as a signature method of
[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology]], and extended from Iranian popular medicine to
American clinical settings and Greco-Islamic humoral categories. In
*[[good-medicine-rationality-1994|Medicine, Rationality, and Experience]]* (1994) Good both
redeployed and self-critiqued the concept: the Turkish epilepsy study showed "fainting"
(*bayılma*) condensing a network of fright, grief, jinn attack, evil eye, and seizure — but
Good conceded (attributed) that semantic network analysis is "purely semiotic," with "little
way to represent the diversity of forms of authority and resistance associated with core
elements in a medical system" — blind to Bakhtinian heteroglossia and to whose interpretation
of an illness prevails.

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic elicitation of the associations, typical situations, and co-occurring complaints
surrounding a core symptom/illness term; the analyst maps the domains the term links. Distinct
from the Lévi-Straussian binary-opposition analysis Good also practices (e.g., the Jehovah's
Witness "blood" case in the 1994 book) — Good explicitly presents semantic networks as a
*different and better* means of connecting medical language to local social relations (Good &
Good 1982, 1992).

## Applications

- Heart distress in Maragheh, Iran (Good 1977) — the type case.
- *Bayılma*/fainting vs. *sar'a*/epilepsy in Turkey (1988 fieldwork): the broader folk category
  is less stigmatizing and condenses non-epileptic distress with seizure disorders.
- Complements [[explanatory-models]]: where EMs elicit episode-specific causal accounts,
  semantic networks map the standing cultural field a complaint draws on.

## Critiques and Limitations

The most consequential critique is Good's own (1994, attributed): the method is semiotic rather
than social — it maps meaning but not power, dialogue, or contested narrative authority. It also
shares the interpretive program's general exposure to the
[[critical-medical-anthropology|political-economy]] charge of underweighting structural
determinants of illness.
