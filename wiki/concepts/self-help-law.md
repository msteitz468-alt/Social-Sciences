# Self-Help (Legal Anthropology)

**Self-help** is the analytical category for a wronged party (or their lineage) enforcing
a claim directly, without a tribunal. In *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice and
Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (1957), [[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]] refines the term into an
analytical distinction load-bearing for his theory of the **[[jir]]**.

## Definition and Bohannan's distinction

Bohannan distinguishes (attributed) two things often lumped together as "self-help":

- **Self right-enforcement** — counteraction that leads to **correction**: the injured
  party takes action (which among the Tiv could formerly include armed "raids" or "taking
  in a matter of debt," *tôn a tô sa injô*) that restores the norm or exacts retribution and
  then ends. It is structurally the same series as the *jir*: breach → counteraction →
  correction.
- **Revenge / the feud** — counteraction that leads only to **repeated counteraction**,
  never reaching correction; the score is never settled.

## Semantic History

"Self-help" is an older term of jurisprudence for direct enforcement of a right without
recourse to a court. Bohannan's move is to split the ordinary lump usage into three: (1)
self-help *within* a jural institution (the litigant does his own detective and enforcement
work); (2) **self right-enforcement** proper (direct action, no tribunal); and (3)
**reprisal** (*iyev*, revenge). The first two Tiv class as "repairing the *[[tar]]*"; the
third as "spoiling the *tar*." The refinement lets the term do analytic work it cannot do as
an undifferentiated whole.

## Operationalizations

Bohannan "operationalizes" the distinction by a single criterion: does the counteraction
lead to **correction** (an end-state — the norm restored or retribution exacted) or to
**repeated counteraction** (an unending score)? The first is self right-enforcement; the
second is revenge/feud.

## The Tiv case

Bohannan reports a finding treated as analytically important: the Tiv did **not**
institutionalize the [[feud]]. A killing led not to regulated kin vengeance (as among the
[[nuer|Nuer]] or Bedouin) but to inter-lineage **war** — and ultimately to "correction" in
the idiom of witchcraft and magic (*tsav*), not blood-vengeance. He flags this absence as
puzzling and reserves it for future analysis.

Because British "pacification" **outlawed self right-enforcement** — Tiv could no longer
fight or raid to enforce claims — a substitute institution was required, and the imported
Grade-D courts filled that gap: "The British Administration thus brought, not merely the
courts, but the social system that made them necessary." Major offences (homicide,
slave-dealing, rape) were moved to European courts; witchcraft alone stayed with the moot,
since colonial law barred Grade-D courts from hearing it. The suppressed age-set
retaliatory institution *hoyo* is another displaced form of self-help.

## Applications and Limitations

The distinction lets Bohannan argue that "counteraction," not "sanction," is the right
analytical pivot for Tiv jural life — and, more generally, that whether self-help is a
substitute for courts or a different thing entirely depends on the folk system in which it
sits. The correction/repetition criterion is his etic construct, not a Tiv category.
