# Ethnicity Paradigm (of Race)

Omi and Winant’s name for the approach that treats U.S. race relations primarily through ethnicity theory — immigrant incorporation, culture, assimilation, and the “American creed” — and that underestimates durable racial domination.

## Definition as Coined

Part I, Ch. 1 of *[[omi-winant-racial-formation-2015|Racial Formation]]*: ethnicity-based theories of race (from Chicago and postwar liberalism through neoconservative colorblind successors) expect inclusion into a universal nationality; they were surprised by black power and anti-racist movements that refused assimilationist terms.

## Semantic History

Authors’ critical label for a research/policy tradition (Park, Gordon, Glazer, etc.), not the self-name of all ethnicity theorists.

## Operationalizations

Paradigm critique via intellectual history and policy orientation — not a survey measure.

## Applications

Explains limits of civil-rights-era liberal race policy framed as ethnicity inclusion; link to later colorblind rearticulation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ethnicity theorists may reject the characterization; boundary between ethnicity and race remains contested in the field.

**Do not conflate with Barthian ethnicity:** Omi–Winant’s “ethnicity paradigm” targets US *race*-as-immigrant-ethnicity models (assimilation, American creed). [[barth-fredrik|Barth]]’s *[[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-1969|Ethnic Groups and Boundaries]]* (1969) is a cross-cultural **organizational** theory of ethnic boundaries ([[ethnic-boundary-theory]]) — related vocabulary, different dispute. See [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]].

## Ethnicity as praxis in archaeology (Preucel & Mrozowski 2010)

*(Attributed — [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The reader frames ethnicity as **active praxis and identity**, not a
primordial given (see [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]]): interpreted through
practice in historical archaeology, and shown by material culture to cut across essentialist
categories. Audrey Horning argues the Northern Irish "two traditions" model is archaeologically
inadequate — shared ceramics and vernacular building forms show cross-community interaction
inconsistent with two bounded ethnicities, even as both communities self-identify as "a threatened
minority." Michael Blakey's African Burial Ground project made terminology itself a site of contest
("enslaved Africans," not "slaves"; rejecting forensic "race estimation" for culturally-salient
ethnic-origin categories). Sarah Nelson shows ethnicity mobilized for nationalism in Korean
prehistory. See [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[race]].
