# External System

## Definition as Coined

In *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]* (1950), [[homans-george-c|Homans]] divides the group's [[social-system|social system]] into an **external system** and an **[[internal-system|internal system]]**. The **external system** is the organization of group behavior as demanded by the environment: the activities that must be performed for the group to survive or meet external requirements, the interactions those activities require (e.g., layout of a factory room, chain of command, division of labor imposed by technology or management), and the sentiments that accompany that externally constrained pattern (Homans, Ch. 4 onward).

A group is first bounded by interaction relative to outsiders; everything outside that social system is **environment**. The external system is the part of the social system organized toward that environment.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Homans 1950 | External vs internal system as master analytic cut for small groups |
| Homans later (per Hare/Polley) | Softens claim that activity–interaction–sentiment are mutually dependent *within* the external system; treats external conditions more as **parameters/boundary conditions** for the internal system |
| Organization studies | Resonates with formal organization, task environment, contingency — without always using Homans's labels |
| Contrast with Parsons | Not identical to Parsonsian "adaptation" AGIL cell, though often compared |

## Operationalizations

In the Bank Wiring case: company wage plans, job classifications, physical room layout, production standards set by management — external constraints that organize necessary activity and interaction before informal elaboration.

## Applications

- Industrial work groups (Hawthorne Bank Wiring).
- Street-corner gangs in a neighborhood economy and police/racket environment (Norton Street / [[cornerville]]).
- Kinship groups facing ecological and subsistence requirements (Tikopia).
- Community studies of groups under changing external economy (Hilltown).

In Ch. 4 Homans also develops **pair relations** among the three elements inside the external system (sentiment–activity, activity–interaction, and — deferred or thinner — interaction–sentiment) and the **pyramid of interaction** in formal chains of command (origination of interaction downward). Later self-critique (Homans 1984, via Hare) softens mutual-dependence claims *for the external system*.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundary between "required by environment" and "elaborated for its own sake" is analytic, not always observationally sharp.
- Homans's own later revision of mutual-dependence claims for the external system.
- Risk of reading environment as pure constraint and underplaying how groups reshape environments.
