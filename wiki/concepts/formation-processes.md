# Formation Processes

The **cultural and noncultural processes that create and transform the historic and archaeological records** — and, in [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]]’s transformation theory, what must be identified before treating a deposit as evidence of past behavior of interest. Primary synthesis: *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987).

## Definition as Coined

Schiffer defines **formation processes** as the factors that create the historic and archaeological records, of two basic kinds (attributed, Schiffer 1987:7):

- **Cultural** — agency is human behavior: reuse, cultural deposition (discard, loss, abandonment, mortuary disposal, caching), reclamation, disturbance, and the archaeologist’s own recovery and analysis.
- **Noncultural (environmental)** — agencies of the natural environment: decay, weathering, sediment deposition and erosion, bioturbation, disasters, and related processes at artifact, site, and regional scales.

More narrowly, cultural formation processes are behaviors that affect or transform artifacts **after** their initial period of use in a given activity (attributed). Laws describing general regularities are **c-transforms** (cultural) and **n-transforms** (noncultural); ethnoarchaeology and experiment are primary laboratories for c-transforms (attributed, Schiffer 1987:22).

## Semantic History

### Predecessors Schiffer positions against

1. **Entropy view** (Ascher 1968): “time’s arrow” progressively reduces quantity and quality of evidence. Schiffer accepts this as a statistical generalization but stresses degradation is by **specific processes**, not time per se; even degraded deposits can support inferences; some environmental ecofacts accumulate through time (attributed, Schiffer 1987:8–9).
2. **Sampling-bias view** (Cowgill 1970; Collins 1975): successive populations from past behavior → deposited assemblage → recovered finds are biased samples.
3. **Transformation view** (Reid, Schiffer, Neff 1975; Schiffer 1972, 1976): the [[archaeological-record]] is a **transformed/distorted** view of artifacts as they once participated in a behavioral system; transformations are formal, spatial, quantitative, and relational; because formation processes themselves pattern, distortions can in principle be rectified with laws of those processes (attributed, Schiffer 1987:10).

Schiffer’s decisive break from pure entropy models: formation processes do not only degrade — they can **create patterning** unrelated to past behaviors of interest (attributed, citing also Binford 1978a and others; Schiffer 1987:10–11). Three tenets he holds as supported by actualistic work: processes (1) transform items on four dimensions, (2) can create non-behavioral patterning, (3) exhibit regularities expressible as usually statistical laws (attributed, 1987:11).

### Four dimensions of variability

Following Rathje and Schiffer, traces map onto **formal**, **spatial**, **frequency**, and **relational** dimensions; formation processes act on all four (e.g. principle of dissociation: co-used items separated in refuse; items never used together co-occur in dumps) (attributed, Schiffer 1987 Ch. 2).

### Four major cultural types (book architecture)

Reuse · cultural deposition · reclamation · disturbance (Part II). Environmental processes treated by scale: artifact · site · region (Part III).

### Later reception

From the 1980s the apparatus was often generalized as “cultural formation processes” (CFP). [[binford-lewis|Binford]] attacked the distortion/C-transform framing as smuggling a [[pompeii-premise|Pompeii premise]] (attributed). [[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] (2002, secondary in this vault) argued both sides can share a representational model of the record. Vocabulary (primary/secondary/de facto refuse; systemic/archaeological context) entered standard method training.

### Identification checklist densified (*First Principles* Ch. 18 = Schiffer 1983c)

Schiffer argued formation-process research had burgeoned experimentally and ethnoarchaeologically yet was rarely applied in recovery, analysis, and inference — “in no other area of archaeological methodology is there a greater disjunction between theory and practice” (attributed, Schiffer 1983c / 1995 Ch. 18). Unless deposit genesis is understood, behaviors of interest cannot be inferred from artifact patterns. **Four dimensions** of transformation (formal, spatial, quantitative, relational) structure the transformation view (Reid et al.; Schiffer and Rathje). Practical toolkit themes (attributed):

- **Simple artifact properties:** size (reduction, size-sorting, **McKellar Hypothesis** — small items likelier primary refuse in regularly maintained areas); density/shape/orientation/dip; use-life / usability at deposition; material-specific damage; accretions.
- **Complex properties:** quantity; vertical/horizontal distribution (clustering ≠ activity area); diversity (**Clarke Effect**); density/concentration; principle of dissociation; **Completeness Index (CI)** and **Fragmentation Index (FI)** (esp. ceramics/glass); reassembly/refitting; part representation.
- **Deposit properties:** sediments as artifacts; ecofacts as CFP/NFP traces; geochemistry and negative evidence; structure/context/site morphology conditioning process probabilities.
- **Strategies:** combine c-transforms / n-transforms with locality-specific empirical generalizations; hypothesis-testing when recovery is controlled; multivariate clustering of deposits when prior knowledge is thin; Lightfoot and Feinman Crooked Ridge reanalysis as demonstration that “big man” exotic–house associations can be formational (fill capacity, secondary refuse) rather than household inventory (attributed).

## Applications

- **Refuse and abandonment:** McKellar Principle (small residual primary refuse on maintained surfaces); de facto refuse vs curate behavior; gradual pueblo room abandonment (Reid at Grasshopper); waste streams and Arlo Guthrie trash-magnet effect (attributed, Ch. 4).
- **Chronology:** Hohokam radiocarbon reanalysis via old wood and deposit formation (Schiffer 1987 Ch. 11).
- **Social inference critique:** Broken K Pueblo ceramics (Hill 1970) reanalyzed as mixed formation processes rather than direct room-function / uxorilocal maps (Ch. 12; [[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]).
- **Ethnoarchaeology / middle-range dialogue:** overlaps [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]] and Binford site structure without collapsing the programs (see [[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983]]).
- Field strategies for floors too mixed for simple activity reading; micromorphology and fine recovery for residual primary refuse (later practice; cf. Lucas secondary).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Binford:** C-transform “distortion” of a living-system original still presupposes a Pompeii-like copy (attributed).
- **Lucas / critical fieldwork:** formation-process theory can retain a representational record model; natural baselines risk naturalizing cultural description (attributed secondary).
- **Schiffer’s own limits:** book is a principles checklist, not exhaustive; identification techniques “not yet well developed” as of 1987 (attributed, Ch. 10); ecofacts/environmental record largely outside scope.
- Risk of reducing all interpretation to formational accounting, or of CRM write-offs that discard disturbed but still informative deposits (Schiffer’s own caution against pure entropy write-offs).

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook presentation (2012, Ch. 2)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 2. Short pedagogical framing of cultural vs natural formation processes; does **not** rewrite or replace the Schiffer primary layer above. Schiffer is listed in the textbook’s further reading for formation processes but is not named in the body of this chapter section.)*

**Why formation processes matter.** Knowledge of the early human past depends on human activities and natural processes that form the [[archaeological-record]] and on further processes that determine, over long periods, what survives and what is lost. Most remains are modest discarded refuse from everyday activity, not monumental constructions. Renfrew & Bahn state that archaeologists have become increasingly aware of a whole series of **formation processes** affecting both how finds became buried and what happened after burial — “or in other words their [[taphonomy]].”

**Cultural vs natural (noncultural) formation processes.**

- **Cultural formation processes** — deliberate or accidental human activities: making/using artifacts, building or abandoning buildings, plowing fields, looting, etc.
- **Natural (noncultural) formation processes** — natural events that govern burial and survival of the archaeological record: e.g. volcanic ash at Pompeii (exceptional); gradual burial by wind-borne sand or soil; river transport of stone tools; animal burrowing and chewing of bone and wood.

The distinction is vital for accurate reconstruction (e.g. beaver tooth marks vs human tool cutmarks on timber; Early Paleolithic scavenging vs hunting debates resolved by improving discrimination of cultural vs natural signatures — Brain, Binford, et al.).

**Cultural processes before and after burial.** Crudely separable into (1) original human behavior before burial and (2) post-burial disturbance (plowing, looting) — though major sites involve complex sequences of use, burial, and reuse that complicate the twofold division. Original behavior for tools is modeled as four stages: **acquisition → manufacture → use/distribution → disposal/discard** (with possible rework/recycle); an artifact may enter the record at any stage. Identifying which stage is crucial for reconstructing activity and diet (e.g. threshing floor vs occupation floor plant remains). Human “destruction” can inadvertently preserve (burning carbonizes plants; fired clay hardens; Near Eastern tablets/timbers survive via fire).

**Primary vs secondary context.** Primary context = material as originally deposited; secondary = material shifted after original deposition by looters, other human disturbance, or natural forces. For Paleolithic archaeology spanning tens of thousands of years, natural forces invariably destroy much primary context.

**How to study formation processes.** Long-term **experimental archaeology** is presented as an effective approach (e.g. Overton Down experimental earthwork). Ethnoarchaeology of living peoples’ material culture is also central to understanding how the record forms (Introduction framing; Binford Nunamiut as exemplar).
