# Differentiation of Power (Blau)

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] defines **power** as "the ability of persons or groups to impose their will on others despite resistance through deterrence either in the form of withholding regularly supplied rewards or in the form of punishment" — i.e., control through **negative sanctions**. He narrows [[weber-max|Weber]]'s definition (power as the probability of carrying out one's will despite resistance) and Tawney's (capacity to modify others' conduct while resisting modification of one's own): for Blau, promising someone \$100 to act is *not* power, but threatening to take \$100 is. A crucial qualification follows: **regular rewards create power**, because their expected continuation makes their withdrawal a punishment — "the threat of being fired is a negative sanction that gives an employer power over his employees," even though the wage is nominally a positive one.

The **differentiation of power** is the process by which such power comes to be distributed unequally within a collectivity through exchange. Its root is [[unilateral-dependence]]: by supplying needed benefits others cannot reciprocate or obtain elsewhere, a person "attains power over others by making the satisfaction of their need contingent on their compliance." Power is inherently asymmetrical (net dependence after mutual restraints); interdependence of equal strength indicates its absence.

## Semantic History

Blau's concept sits between [[weber-max|Weber]]'s meaning-and-authority-centered power and the resource/dependence tradition later formalized by Emerson (power-dependence) and network exchange theory. Where [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] analyzed [[superordination-and-subordination]] as a *form* of interaction, Blau derives the hierarchy genetically from imbalanced exchange. His insistence that recurrent rewards convert into power (via baseline expectations) anticipates later "dependence" accounts.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by asymmetry in exchange: in Blau's work groups, an expert who is consulted by many but reciprocates with none accumulates deference and status; "status as expendable capital" is drawn on, depleted in use, and expandable through risk. Blau develops marginal-analysis models (bilateral monopoly, contract curve, elasticity of the demand for and supply of advice) to derive testable propositions about how changes in the number of experts or the pressure on consultants shift both consultation volume and the degree of status differentiation.

## Applications

The pivot of [[social-exchange-theory]]'s derivation of macrostructure: power differentiation, once established, is stabilized by [[legitimation]] (fair exercise → approval → authority) or resisted by [[opposition]] (exploitation → disapproval). Blau argues power secured by **multiple, and especially "joint," supports** (many dependents whose consensus is socially enforced) is disproportionately more secure — the powerful can insure against defections, so "the rich in power tend to get richer in power." He notes **tolerance is a sign of strength**: secure status permits magnanimity, insecure status breeds intolerance.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Against functionalist stratification.** Blau argues (contra Davis–Moore) that "once superior power has been attained by furnishing services, it can be sustained without furnishing these same services" — a self-perpetuating element he says functional theories of [[social-class-weberian|stratification]] neglect. This is a [POSITION], relevant to [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate|debates over functionalism]].
- The negative-sanction definition is contested: by folding withdrawn rewards into "negative sanctions," Blau arguably absorbs most influence into power, blurring the reward/coercion line he draws.
- Coercive force is treated as a limiting case ("differs only in degree") — critics note this understates the qualitative distinctiveness of violence.
