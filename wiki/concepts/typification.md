# Typification

## Definition as Coined

In the Schutz-derived analysis of Berger and Luckmann (*[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|Social Construction of Reality]]*, 1966, Part One), social interaction and knowledge proceed by **typifications**: others, situations, and experiences are apprehended under types that structure routine conduct.

Attributed claims:

- Even face-to-face encounters are structured by typificatory schemes (though flexible and renegotiable).
- Anonymity increases with distance from face-to-face: continuum from consociates to contemporaries, predecessors, successors.
- Reciprocal typification of [[habitualization|habitualized]] actions by types of actors **is** [[institutionalization]].
- Language typifies and anonymizes experience (e.g. “mother-in-law trouble”), depositing types in the social [[stock-of-knowledge|stock of knowledge]].
- Social structure, in their Part One formulation, can be described as the sum of typifications and of recurrent patterns of interaction established by means of them.

## Semantic History

Borrowed from [[schutz-alfred|Alfred Schutz]]’s phenomenology of the life-world (primary texts not yet ingested; B&L and Notes are the collection’s witness). Related: Weberian ideal types (analytic for the scientist) vs. everyday typifications (constitutive for actors) — related but not identical.

## Operationalizations

Phenomenological/interpretive. Empirical ethnomethodology and conversation analysis later pursue related problems with different methods (Garfinkel et al. not yet integrated from this source).

## Applications

Everyday interaction; role assignment; institutional definitions of actors; anonymity of modern social relations; language as typifying system.

## Critiques and Limitations

Can underplay strategic mis-typification and power over categories; B&L address power more at legitimation/universe-maintenance than at the micro-typification level.
