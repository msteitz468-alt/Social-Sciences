# Eastern Agricultural Complex

## Definition as Coined

The **Eastern Agricultural Complex (EAC)** is the archaeological label for indigenous **seed-crop cultivation** in the Eastern Woodlands of North America — oil and starch seeds including goosefoot (*Chenopodium*), sumpweed (*Iva*), sunflower, maygrass, little barley, knotweed, and related plants — **prior to and partly independent of** the later rise of maize as staple. Densified from [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner 2021]] (native-plant cultivation in Archaic–Woodland chapters) and prior textbook layers (Bruce Smith via Wenke & Olszewski).

## Semantic History

Archaeobotanical recognition mid–late twentieth century overturned assumptions that eastern agriculture began with Mesoamerican maize-beans-squash. “Complex” names a **suite of crops and practices**, not a single culture or simultaneous package everywhere.

## Operationalizations

- Morphological domestication signatures on seeds; assemblage ubiquity from flotation.
- Settlement location relative to floodplain and weedy habitats.
- Later **stable carbon isotope** studies track the **maize switch** (Late Woodland–Mississippian) as a distinct dietary event from earlier EAC gardening.
- Milner: step-like intensification rather than a single agricultural “revolution”; gardening of native plants deep into Hopewell times before maize dominance.

## Applications

- Archaic–Woodland context for [[adena-culture]] and [[hopewell-culture]] economies.
- Baseline before Late Woodland / Mississippian maize reliance (~significant for many by late first / early second millennium CE; northern maize heavy reliance often a few centuries later than southern Midwest/Southeast — as of Milner 2021).
- Comparative agricultural origins: independent Eastern Woodlands pathway.
- **Chiefdoms without maize:** Coles Creek and some Georgia coastal chiefdoms show institutionalized inequality with continued heavy foraging (Milner) — agriculture is not a necessary prerequisite for rank.

## Critiques and Limitations

Dates and staple importance of EAC taxa revised by ongoing flotation and AMS programs; do not treat “complex” as a people. Maize later becomes central in many Mississippian economies but wetlands/aquatic resources remain essential (Milner on [[cahokia]]).

## Bellwood 2005 — comparative “gentle and gradual” trajectory

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter|Bellwood]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* ch. 8; archaeobotanical figures **as of Bellwood 2005** / Bruce Smith and colleagues as he cites them.)*

Bellwood treats native Eastern Woodlands seed-crop domestication as an **independent agricultural focus** under way by 2000 BC, perhaps 3000 BC (Bruce Smith pers. comm. 2003 to Bellwood), centered on the Ohio and Tennessee valleys and adjacent Mississippi. Pre-maize native domesticates concentrate in the central interior north of ~34° latitude and are largely absent from southern/eastern coastal USA. Agriculture shows few signs of being very significant anywhere before ~500 BC; hunter-gatherers initially “toyed with” domesticates on the edges of rich nonfarming economies as scarcity hedges — a “gentle and gradual” trajectory Bellwood aligns with New Guinea Highlands and Amazonia, against “frenetic” Middle East, China, Mesoamerica/Southwest, and Andes.

**Crops (as of Bellwood 2005).** Non-food indigenous plants domesticated first: squash *Cucurbita pepo* and gourds (containers). Food domesticates include starchy *Chenopodium berlandieri* (goosefoot), oily *Helianthus annuus* (sunflower), *Iva annua* (sumpweed/marsh elder) — recognized by larger seeds and/or thinner seed coats. Maygrass, little barley, and knotweed abundant but lack clear domesticated morphology. All native to the Woodlands except possibly sunflower (Mexican or Southwestern origin possible). Apart from squashes and sunflower, none still cultivated today. Smith 2003 update (via Bellwood): domestication baselines ~3000 BC for squash, ~2000 BC for sunflower. Chenopods most important; Fritz (1993): cache of ~9 million goosefoot seeds at Ash Cave, Ohio, early 1st millennium AD; knotweed and little barley common ~2,000 years ago with initial maize.

**Bruce Smith’s “floodplain weed” model (position).** Domestication on anthropogenically disturbed alluvial terrain along major rivers under increased mid-Holocene alluviation; plants have “impressive nutritional profiles and substantial potential harvest yield values” (Smith 1987, 1992a, 1992b, 1995 as of Bellwood). Smith (1992b:200): a field 70×70 m of marsh elder + goosefoot could supply half the caloric needs of a 10-person household for six months. Yarnell (1993, 1994): Kentucky 650–250 BC paleofeces/flotation — possible plant dietary intake ~40% oily seeds, 36% starchy seeds, 20% nuts — ≥75% plant food from domesticated garden products (with regional variation; chenopods dominate eastern Tennessee, minor in west-central Illinois).

**Settlement and later maize.** By AD 1 (early Hopewell, Middle Woodland) pre-maize agriculture through Illinois, Tennessee, Kentucky, Ohio, and across the Mississippi into Missouri and Kansas; small settlements of circular huts mostly <2 ha on floodplain edges with burial mounds/ceremonial centers. Unlike Mexico/Peru/Middle East/China, no strong trend toward settlement concentration until maize-based Mississippian after AD 1000 — yet Early/Middle Woodland mounds can be large (Adena ≤20 m; embanked enclosures ≤10 ha; Hopewell animal-shaped mounds + widespread exchange). Population increase Early/Middle Woodland continuing Late Woodland/Mississippian (Buikstra et al. 1986). Earliest maize remains ~200 BC Holding site Illinois; dietary mainstay after AD 500 (see [[maize-domestication]]).

**Pottery note.** SE USA fiber-tempered pottery ~2500 BC Georgia/N Florida not yet found in agricultural contexts — precedes significant agriculture; 1200–600 BC paddle-impressed/stamped/incised non-fiber-tempered pottery east of Mississippi to New York; whether indigenous from fiber-tempered or Mesoamerican input (Ford 1969; Webb & Snow 1988) remains open (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Identity caution.** EAC is a crop suite and practice complex, not a people, language family, or genetic population. Language-family associations Bellwood floats for Eastern Woodlands (Algonquian, Iroquoian, Siouan, etc. as partial agricultural-dispersal candidates) are **positions** on family pages / the farming–language program, not wiki-voice equations of pots = crops = languages.

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 7.)*

- **Floodplain weed theory.** Barker reports Bruce Smith’s “floodplain weed theory”: Archaic foragers cultivated floodplain weeds early in the Holocene; older view that maize/squash/beans only arrived ~AD 1000 is rejected (attributed).
- **Package.** Oil-rich marsh elder/sumpweed (*Iva annua*), sunflower (*Helianthus annuus*), goosefoot (*Chenopodium berlandieri*), knotweed (*Polygonum erectum*), little barley (*Hordeum pusillum*), maygrass (*Phalaris caroliniana*), and *Cucurbita pepo* squash; also forest staples acorns/hickory (e.g. Icehouse Bottom) (attributed).
- **Context of abundance.** From ~5000 BC climatic aridity stabilized/aggraded rivers, increasing aquatic foods and open-habitat plants; yearly riverside settlement. Smith: continually disturbed habitats allowed floodplain weeds to establish permanently — intensification in a “stress-free” context of relative abundance (Smith 1995b: 212 via Barker) (attributed).
- **Chronology.** First clear cultivated plants: pepo squash rind fragments from Koster (Illinois) AMS-dated ~5000 BC; bottle gourd from Windover (Florida) of similar antiquity. From ~3000 BC morphological domestication markers: larger marsh elder and sunflower seeds, thicker squash rinds, thinner chenopod seed coats. By 500 BC full range of domesticated seed plants plus tobacco common at eastern Woodlands river-valley sites; Salts/Mammoth Cave coprolites dominated by chenopods, sunflower, maygrass, sumpweed (attributed).
- **Bottle gourd / pepo origin debate.** Introduction as Central American domesticates vs natural wild entry then local domestication vs Gulf float; balance of probability (Heiser 1989 via Barker): locally available plants domesticated locally. Alternative valuations: bottle gourds as shamanistic rattles (Prentice); pepo as net floats (Fritz) (attributed positions).
- **Hopewell and later maize.** Hopewell (~500 BC–AD 200): mixed horticulture + fishing + shellfish + hunting; mix of foraging and horticulture c. 5000–1500 BC foundation for increasing differentiation/inequality (Barker’s reading). Maize present from early first millennium AD but not staple for another thousand years; small-scale domestic/exchange spread (possibly women through marriage); then thinner pottery, heavier hoes, C4 residues, rising populations (attributed). Watson & Kennedy (1991) women’s-status rise vs Barker/Smith household-unit continuity (positions).
- **Synthesis.** Barker places EAC among multi-center independent early Holocene plant manipulations across the Americas (with tropical roots/tubers and highland cultivars), not as a secondary afterthought to highland maize.
