# Intimate, Effective, and Extended Networks

## Definition as Coined

In *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe]]* (1999), [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] models Palaeolithic social life from the **individual** outward as a hierarchy of overlapping **ego-based personal networks**. Following Turner and Maryanski on network content, he distinguishes three interlinked **resources** — emotional, material, and symbolic — differentially deployed by scale (attributed, Gamble 1999 ch. 2; Table 2.8). Network analysis, he stresses after Boissevain and Mitchell, is a **form of analysis** rather than a freestanding theory of action: individuals are creative mobile nodes, and society is ultimately “a network of networks” (attributed).

| Network | Approx. size (as of Gamble 1999) | Primary resources | Character of interaction |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intimate** | ~5 (range ~3–7) | Emotional (ontological security) | Dense face-to-face; multiplex ties; restricted code |
| **Effective** | ~20 (sample means ~10–23) | Material / logistical | Daily logistics, reproductive and political ends |
| **Extended** | ~100–400 | Symbolic / stylistic | “Friends of friends”; style and exchange negotiate ties beyond routine co-presence |
| **Personal** | Sum of the three | Mixed | Ego’s full negotiated network |
| **Global** | Open / “otherness” | Often weak or unfocused | Strangers, casual acquaintances; urban co-presence as unfocused interaction (Goffman via Gamble) |

(attributed, Gamble 1999 Tables 2.8–2.9; size estimates via Milardo and ethnographic parallels)

Gamble argues (attributed) that **emotional resources** cannot sustain significant extended networks (they need time, attention, and co-presence); **symbolic resources** are inappropriate as the main currency of the intimate network because they disembody action. Only societies that elaborate symbolic resources can maintain **significant extended networks** and, beyond them, a **global** network of otherness (attributed, chs. 2, 7).

## Semantic History

- **Network sociology and anthropology.** Density, centrality, transitivity, reachability; multiplex vs uniplex ties; small-world reachability of 3–5 links for stranger introductions (attributed via Gamble 1999). Steele’s (1994) simulations (via Gamble): low-connectivity “Poisson” and low-density lattices spread adaptive traits faster; high-density “Tribal” networks resist innovation (attributed).
- **Primate and social-cognition models.** Hinde’s tiers (interactions → relationships → structure); Dunbar’s grooming-time and language-as-vocal-grooming limits; Aiello & Dunbar neocortex → group-size arguments that Gamble re-reads against spatial evidence (attributed).
- **Gamble’s Palaeolithic application (1999).** Replaces Service/Steward band typologies and pure optimal-foraging frames with bottom-up personal networks performed at [[locale|locales]], generated by [[palaeolithic-rhythms|rhythms]], and scaled as [[social-landscape-palaeolithic|landscape of habit vs social landscape]] (attributed). Table 3.7 aligns Boissevain personal networks, Hinde primate networks, and Gamble’s Palaeolithic scheme: intimate/effective map onto the **landscape of habit**; extended/global map onto the **social landscape** (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Archaeological proxies (as of Gamble 1999 — all attributed/position, not wiki-voice species equations):

- **Intimate / effective co-presence:** hearths and conversation-scale seating (~4 speakers; Binford Mask Site / Dunbar); gathering residues; short-distance lithic refits (~personal space, e.g. Belvédère longest refits ~10 m).
- **Effective logistics / landscape of habit:** raw-material transfer radii — common scale ~40 km radius, upper bulk limit ~80–100 km; Geneste principle that distant stone arrives as later reduction stages; majority of transfers under ~20 km (as of Geneste / Féblot-Augustins via Gamble 1999).
- **Extended network / social landscape:** directed (not simple fall-off) transfers; bulk or regular movement beyond the 80–100 km threshold; novel items (shell, possibly ivory); ornament, music, figurative representation; structured burials; regional style zones and technocomplexes.
- **Network topology from transfer graphs:** Middle Palaeolithic star/wheel or low-density patterns vs Early Upper Palaeolithic circular/all-channel “tramlines” (Rhine, Danube, Carpathian crossings, Atlantic shells into SW France) (attributed, Gamble 1999 chs. 5–8).

## Applications

### Lower and Middle Palaeolithic Europe (as of Gamble 1999)

Gamble argues Neanderthal (and earlier European) societies were founded on **intimate and effective networks** individuals routinely constructed (attributed, ch. 5 summary). Life remained **local and usually immediate**; extended networks stayed “largely unnegotiated and undeveloped” (attributed). Of Féblot-Augustins’s 578 transfers (OIS 7–4), few exceeded 80 km and most lay under Geneste’s 20 km threshold (as of Gamble 1999). Skill transfer enabling long-term continental occupation worked by **intensifying effective networks** via emotion, materials, and gesture-embedded temporal rhythms — not by symbolic inscription of place (attributed). Neanderthals could intensify intimate networks toward a “quasi-effective” scale (larger co-operation at animal gatherings and workshops) while still lacking an external symbolic system that stretched relations beyond co-presence (attributed). Kuhn’s provisioning-people vs provisioning-places distinction is rephrased: Neanderthals carried embodied gesture-sequences as extensions of the body rather than stocking places for future action (attributed).

### Transition and Upper Palaeolithic (as of Gamble 1999)

Gamble holds that a **significant** Palaeolithic transition requires transformation of **power** and the appearance of **extended networks** with social occasions and **place** inside a social landscape — system integration *in absentia* (attributed, ch. 7). After ~33 Kyr bp (as of his chronology), settlement hierarchies and pronounced inter-/intra-regional material-culture variation mark structural change beyond mere typological “human revolution” checklists (attributed). Only **complicated** societies (Strum & Latour via Gamble) have significant extended networks; power then also touches the intimate network through externalized categorization, generating ambiguity that can seed ritual (attributed). Aurignacian-type transfer patterns read as high-density all-channel structures and directed exchange; Chatelperronian is modelled as still suited to wheel-shaped, high-mobility short-stay networks even when ornaments appear (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Theory-laden late-1990s synthesis; transfer-distance cutoffs and Neanderthal–modern network contrasts are **positions** revised by later archaeology, aDNA, and Neanderthal symbolic finds.
- Resource-to-scale mapping (emotion→intimate, material→effective, symbol→extended) is heuristic; the same object can shift gift↔commodity across network zones (Gamble cites Gregory/Valeri).
- Networks are not excavated institutions — they are inferred from residues of performance; circularity risk if transfer data both define and “prove” network type.
- Gamble rejects reading wheel vs circular topologies as two competing society-types (Neanderthal vs Cro-Magnon anatomy); individuals from both anatomical populations could define systems by different means (attributed, ch. 8).
