# Normative Pacification

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]* (1986, esp. Chs. 10–15), [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] uses **normative pacification** for reduction of predatory violence and enabling of cooperation across multi-power fields through shared ideological norms — paradigmatically medieval **Christendom** — without a single imperial monopoly of force. It substitutes for the coercive pacification typical of ancient extensive societies and for ancient-style [[compulsory-cooperation]]. Christendom combined **transcendent** ideology with **immanent** ruling-class morale of lords, confirming property and market relations within and between cells; extensive infrastructure previously costly as compulsory cooperation was provided, Mann argues, by ideological means without a state. Darker side in his account: savage treatment of those outside the ecumene (schismatics, heretics, Jews, Muslims, pagans).

> "The early feudal economically centered dynamic was primarily intensive because extensive power was already provided for by Christendom."

> "The combination of social and ecological diversity and competition with normative pacification led to that controlled expansionism and inventiveness…"

## Semantic History

Specific application of ideological power as extensive/[[diffused-power|diffused]] integration within the [[iemp-model]]. Related to Peace of God historiography and Durkheimian normative regulation; Mann's relative unorthodoxy is to treat Christianity as causal for European dynamism via **normative pacification**, not only Weberian doctrine-psychology (Protestant Ethic). Precursors in early civilizational religions as extensive diffuse normative identities (Ch. 1) and salvation ecumenes solving imperial contradictions of social identity (Chs. 10–11). After Protestant schism and religious wars, **states** begin to supplant Christendom as main instruments of normative pacification (Mann Ch. 15).

## Operationalizations

Cross-polity religious law and moral regulation; Latin literacy and dual bishopric/monastic hierarchies; just-war and anti-bandit campaigns (e.g. Pax Dei); papal interstate arbitration; pilgrimage; tithes and estates; property norms without strong state interference; later town-state legal alliances securing pacification begun by the church. Dark side: exclusionary violence against schismatics, heretics, Jews, Muslims, pagans.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ch. 12 (800–1155)** — Early feudal intensive dynamic possible because extensive power already provided by Christendom; multiplicity of networks + pacification → regulated class-riven competition ("rational restlessness") rather than stifled localism or pure anomie. Dynamism dated earlier than conventional town/Renaissance/Protestant starts (~800–1000 after external threats repulsed).
- **Ch. 13** — From ~1000, state judicial growth gives more precise bite to Christendom's pacification; after 1200 states enter more direct relations with people; by late Middle Ages military-fiscal changes weaken Christendom's integrating role.
- **Chs. 14–15** — Protestantism nationalizes twin pillars of Christendom; extensive religious power declines (secularization as loss of social-organizational capacity); early agricultural dynamic under normative pacification harnessed to capitalism, [[organic-national-state|organic states]], and diplomatically regulated multistate civilization. Competitive [[multi-power-actor-civilization]] + diversity + normative pacification = controlled expansionism/inventiveness.

## Critiques and Limitations

Degree of actual medieval "peace" is historically contested; Mann's functional loading of Christendom is a strong causal claim among European-miracle explanations. Darker exclusionary face is part of his own account — do not sanitize. Keep attributed.
