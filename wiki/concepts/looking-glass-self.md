# Looking-Glass Self

## Definition as Coined

In *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]* (1902), [[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]] names a form of social self the **reflected or looking-glass self**. In a large class of cases, he argues, self-reference takes the form of "a somewhat definite imagination of how one's self—that is any idea he appropriates—appears in a particular mind, and the kind of self-feeling one has is determined by the attitude toward this attributed to that other mind."

Cooley quotes:

> "Each to each a looking-glass  
> Reflects the other that doth pass."

He specifies **three principal elements** of a self-idea of this sort:

1. **the imagination of our appearance to the other person;**
2. **the imagination of his judgment of that appearance;**
3. **some sort of self-feeling**, such as pride or mortification.

Crucially, Cooley insists the second element is not optional: "The comparison with a looking-glass hardly suggests the second element, the imagined judgment, which is quite essential. The thing that moves us to pride or shame is not the mere mechanical reflection of ourselves, but an imputed sentiment, the imagined effect of this reflection upon another's mind." Whose mind matters: "We are ashamed to seem evasive in the presence of a straightforward man, cowardly in the presence of a brave one, gross in the eyes of a refined one."

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Cooley 1902 | Affective–imaginative process of self-formation through imagined appearance and judgment in particular other minds; part of the broader social self |
| James (empirical self) | Self-feeling and social self as components of the empirical me — Cooley develops the *mechanism* of social self-feeling |
| Mead | Accepts social genesis of the self but criticizes grounding in "self-feeling"; substitutes cognitive role-taking and the generalized other (see the Mead–Cooley self-origin debate) |
| Later social psychology / SI | "Reflected appraisals" research tradition; textbook staple of socialization chapters; often simplified to "we see ourselves as others see us" (losing Cooley's second element) |

## Operationalizations

Cooley does not measure. Later work operationalizes "reflected appraisals" (what actors believe significant others think of them) versus actual appraisals; discrepancies between self-view, reflected appraisal, and others' views are empirical objects. Textbook three-element checklists are pedagogical compressions of Cooley's 1902 formulation.

## Development (Cooley's illustrations)

Cooley traces looking-glass self-feeling in children as they connect their acts to changes in others' movements — appropriating influence over parent or nurse as they appropriate a limb or toy. He reports early attention-seeking (e.g., a girl of six months tugging skirts to attract notice), selective prestige before the end of the second year, and detailed notes on his child **M.**: from about the fourth month a "hurt" cry of personal slight; by about fifteen months a "perfect little actress" trapping attention and abashed at disapproval. He theorizes: "Imagination co-operating with instinctive self-feeling has already created a social 'I.'" Adolescent ambition he reads as "self-effectuation" — producing some effect upon other minds (illustrated via J. A. Symonds). Child material is **observational illustration**, not a developmental stage model; Cooley denies regular universal stages.

Looking-glass dynamics reappear outside the self chapters: **conformity** as voluntary imitation to hold a place in the imagined judgment of the group; **resentment** as the reflected self under attack; **conscience** as imagined approval/disapproval and ideal persons; **fame** of leaders as a symbol exceeding the private man.

## Applications

- Core mechanism in Cooley's account of how children and adolescents acquire "I"-feeling through imagined audience response.
- Precursor concept for [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]'s socially formed self (alongside Mead's role-taking).
- Bridges everyday vanity/pride analysis and institutional questions of honor, reputation, and conscience (Cooley Chs. VI, X).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Origin problem (Mead).** Self-feeling, Mead argued, presupposes a self to which feeling is referred and so cannot *originate* the self (attributed). Cooley describes the ongoing life of the social self more than its first emergence via significant symbols.
- **Particular others vs. generalized other.** Cooley's looking-glass is typically a *particular* other mind; Mead's [[generalized-other|generalized other]] organizes the community's attitude as a unity. The two are complementary in later SI textbooks, distinct in their authors' programs.
- **Period child-development and sex-difference remarks** in Cooley's illustrations are dated observational asides, not developmental science; attribute and date-stamp. Textbook simplifications that drop the second element ("we see ourselves as others see us") lose Cooley's essential point about **imagined judgment**.
