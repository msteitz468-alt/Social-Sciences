# Pedagogic Action

*(Attributed — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] & [[passeron-jean-claude|Passeron]], [[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|*Reproduction*]], Book I.)* The master concept of the theory of [[symbolic-violence]]: pedagogic action (abbreviated **PA** in the text) is any action of inculcation of meanings, whether diffuse (the whole social milieu), familial, or institutionalized (the school). Its foundational proposition opens the propositional system of Book I. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-study|Studies Hub — Reproduction]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed.)* The book's central thesis (proposition 1):

> "All pedagogic action (PA) is, objectively, symbolic violence insofar as it is the imposition of a cultural arbitrary by an arbitrary power."

Bourdieu and Passeron built the concept from a founding axiom of their theory of sociological knowledge (Axiom 0):

> "Every power to exert symbolic violence, i.e. every power which manages to impose meanings and to impose them as legitimate by concealing the power relations which are the basis of its force, adds its own specifically symbolic force to those power relations."

PA is symbolic violence in **two** senses, corresponding to a *twofold arbitrariness*: (1) the power relations between groups or classes are the basis of the *arbitrary power* that makes pedagogic imposition possible; (2) the selection of meanings inculcated reproduces the [[cultural-arbitrary]] of a group or class — arbitrary because "the structure and functions of that culture cannot be deduced from any universal principle." The degree of arbitrariness of a PA's power of imposition rises with the degree of arbitrariness of the culture imposed; but the authors insist the two are "logical fictions" — "There is no PA which does not inculcate some meanings not deducible from a universal principle: authority plays a part in all pedagogy, even when the most universal meanings (science or technology) are to be inculcated."

## Semantic History

- **Origin (*Reproduction*, French 1970; English 1977).** Coined in the *more geometrico* propositional apparatus of Book I. Bottomore's 1976 foreword glosses PA as "education in the broadest sense, encompassing more than the process of formal education."
- **Dominant PA.** The dominant PA in a social formation is the one that "most fully... corresponds to the objective interests... of the dominant groups or classes," in both its mode of imposition and its delimitation of content and audience.
- **The social-reproduction function of cultural reproduction.** PA "reproduces the cultural arbitrary which it inculcates" and thereby reproduces the power relations underlying its imposition — the hinge linking [[cultural-reproduction]] to social reproduction. On this point the authors' explicit polemical target is [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]: classical theories that treat education as neutral intergenerational transmission "sever cultural reproduction from its function of social reproduction."
- **School PA.** PA in general is treated first; the school's *specific* PA (institutionalized, exposed to the question of its own legitimacy) is characterized only from proposition 4 onward — see [[relative-autonomy-of-the-educational-system]].

## Operationalizations

- **Three loci of PA** distinguished by durability and mode: diffuse societal PA; family PA (produces the *primary habitus*); institutionalized PA (school). Each imposes a cultural arbitrary; their relative weights differ by class and history.
- **Mode of imposition.** PA's historically variable combination of instruments of symbolic violence with instruments of concealment/legitimation — from corporal punishment (English public-school cane, Koranic *falaqa*) to the American "soft approach" (affection and its withdrawal as sanction), which the authors argue is "equally arbitrary" but harder to perceive as coercion.
- **Success of a PA** = a function of the relation between the cultural arbitrary it imposes, the dominant cultural arbitrary, and the cultural arbitrary of the receiver's class of origin (via [[cultural-capital]] and pedagogic *ethos*).

## Applications

- **The examination and self-elimination.** The school's PA works less through what it transmits than through imposing the "fait accompli of the legitimacy of the dominant culture," largely by exclusion and [[self-elimination]] (Book II ch. 3).
- **"Non-violent" pedagogies.** Socratic non-directive teaching, Rousseauist natural education, pseudo-Freudian non-repression are analyzed as ideologies that *mask* PA's objective truth as violence — since a PA revealing its own violence would be self-destructive (the "paradox of Epimenides the liar" applied to pedagogy).
- **A general theory.** Symbolic violence via PA is claimed to cover "the healer, the sorcerer, the priest, the prophet, the propagandist, the teacher, the psychiatrist or the psychoanalyst" — with a stated *homology* between "the school system's monopoly of legitimate symbolic violence and the State's monopoly of the legitimate use of physical violence" ([[weber-max|Weberian]] parallel).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Functionalism and circularity.** Because a PA that revealed itself would dissolve, the theory can appear insulated from counter-evidence; critics read it as functionalist — see [[cultural-reproduction-debate]].
- **The "arbitrary."** Bourdieu and Passeron warned against the "moralizing reading" that turns *arbitrary* and *violence* into denunciation; the terms are meant as "logical constructs devoid of any sociological or psychological referent." Whether the double sense of *arbitrary* (of imposition, of content) is coherent is contested.
- **Agency of the dominated.** Bottomore (1976) already flagged that PA "of the dominated classes" and "counter-culture" are mentioned but undeveloped — the opening later exploited by resistance theory (Willis, Giroux).
