# Corner boys and college boys (Whyte)

## Definition as Coined

In [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|*Street Corner Society*]] (fieldwork
1936–40), [[whyte-william-foote|Whyte]] divided the younger men of
[[cornerville|Cornerville]] into two "main divisions" (attributed): "Corner boys
are groups of men who center their social activities upon particular street
corners… They constitute the bottom level of society within their age group… The
college boys are a small group of young men who have risen above the corner-boy
level through higher education." The contrast — not either term alone — is the
analytic unit; Whyte also identified **intermediaries** who could participate in
both groups while the social gap between them stayed narrow.

The pair is `migrated` in emic/etic terms: "corner boy" was partly Cornerville's
own usage, which Whyte raised into an analytic type.

## Semantic History

The distinction is a local instance of the general sociological problem of the
relationship between group solidarity and individual mobility. Whyte's specific
claim — that the two orientations are *structurally incompatible* rather than
merely different levels of achievement — is what gives the pair analytic force:
"The college boys were primarily interested in social advancement. The corner boys
were primarily interested in their local community. On this issue the club divided
and fell." The terms have not become general sociological vocabulary the way
"habitus" or "[[anomie]]" did, but the underlying finding — that upward
mobility from a tight working-class community exacts a cost in local ties —
recurs across urban sociology (Gans's *Urban Villagers*, and later work on
"the hidden injuries of class").

## Operationalizations

Whyte distinguished the two types through observable markers (attributed):

- **Group basis.** Corner-boy status came from personal relations and informal
  consensus in a stable street-corner gang (no constitutions, no voting); the
  college-boy nucleus originated in institutional sorting (a ninth-grade
  scholastic clique) and organized around individual accomplishment.
- **Money.** "The college boys fit in with an economy of savings and investment.
  The corner boys fit in with a spending economy… it is not possible to be thrifty
  and yet hold a high position in the corner gang." Prestige required free
  spending (see [[reciprocal-obligation]]).
- **Value orientation.** "Chick judged men according to their capacity for
  advancing themselves. Doc judged them according to their loyalty to their friends
  and their behavior in their personal relations."

## Applications

- **The mobility mechanism.** Whyte rejected a meritocratic reading: "Clearly, the
  difference in intelligence and ability does not explain the different careers of
  Chick and Doc." Career divergence tracked early institutional sorting and group
  allegiance, not innate ability — a claim about [[social-mobility]] with a class
  edge.
- **The settlement house** functioned to pull mobile individuals out of the corner
  world (see [[social-mobility]]), widening the gap between the two types.
- **Reflexive coda.** Whyte predicted the successful college boy would "report to
  upper-class people… that the corner boys are lazy and unco-operative" — the
  mobility success story feeds back to legitimate the meritocratic reading Whyte
  had just refuted.

## Critiques and Limitations

The binary can flatten a continuum (Whyte himself needed the "intermediary"
category, and figures crossed over). Because the types are defined partly by
outcome (who left, who stayed), explanations risk circularity. The
[[whyte-boelen-cornerville-debate|Boelen critique]] questioned whether Whyte, an
outsider dependent on corner-boy sponsors, could see the college-boy world
symmetrically. As with all of *SCS*, the picture is dated to the late-Depression
fieldwork moment.
