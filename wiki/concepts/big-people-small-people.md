# Big People / Small People (Sherpa)

## Definition as Coined

**Big people** and **small people** are emic Sherpa categories of relative social scale—Ortner’s English glosses for local talk of the wealthy/influential versus ordinary smallholders. In *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* (1989), [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]] uses them as analytic pivots for stratification, political economy, and differential motives for temple and monastery founding among the [[sherpa|Sherpas]] of Solu-Khumbu (*studied_by:* [[ortner-high-religion-study]]).

The pair is **relative and historical**, not a fixed dual class. Ortner reported that Sherpas explicitly said unrelated men were equal (egalitarianism of opportunity); “bigness” was achieved—via trade, lama status, or *pembu* influence—and not automatically heritable. She also analyzed **little big people**: monks and nuns as disadvantaged children (middle sons and daughters) of elite families, still “big” as unmarried elite offspring but at risk of decline into smallness through poor marriages.

Related local power terms: **pembu** (tax-collecting strongman/“governor”); **gembu** (*amali*)—state-created coordinator over pembu; personal-power vocabulary *tsachermu* (lama magical potency) and *ongchermu* (bully-force of political dominators).

## Semantic History

**Egalitarianism–hierarchy contradiction.** Ortner argued a single broad Sherpa contradiction—between egalitarian and hierarchical ethics—appears in the family as equal inheritance vs. birth-order authority, and at larger scale as relations between big and small people. Equal inheritance both contradicted hierarchy and produced micro-inequality (brothers) and macro-inequality (big/small). Competitiveness was, she claimed, the primary self-aware face of egalitarianism: people talked less of equality ideals than of not standing others getting ahead (*tatok*, *sheka-shek*, *ngargyal*).

**Genesis of smallness.** Smallness, Ortner argued, was created largely by **equal inheritance** of agricultural land (parcellization under population growth), compounded by **tax extraction** that stripped seed capital for large-scale trade that might compensate land shrinkage. Inheritance was the dominant cause; tax was “the difference that made a difference” without overturning the cultural construction of the system as ideally egalitarian. Big wealth came primarily from **trade**, not from rents, interest, taxes, or fines (which cushioned trade risks)—consistent, she held, with Sherpa society not really being a class system, though extraction occurred.

**Pembu and legitimacy.** Pembu dominance was not legitimate “hierarchy” in cultural values, but tapped hierarchical discourse via kinship-seniority roots and paternalistic protection. Absence of fixed hereditary succession intensified zero-sum competition among big people; small people could switch support to less exploitative protectors. Tax collecting, Ortner argued, violated egalitarianism more deeply than fraternal inequities—placing a permanent (though not totally insurmountable) obstacle before small people’s attempts to build surplus and enter trade (*applies:* [[practice-theory]]).

**Cultural illegitimacy of wealth.** Without direct class-sentiment texts from the early twentieth century, Ortner used modern expressions: mutual dislike between rich and poor; beliefs that the rich get rich by sin (stealing, killing animals) not past-life merit; pembu remembered as parasitic—“eating” others’ money. Kusang, a Tengboche sponsor, spat on the floor in disgust at the pembu role.

**Schema of big and small.** The [[cultural-schema|temple-founding schema]] mediates big/small: one must be small as well as big, big as well as small. Ideal personhood combines potency with humility (throne-behind-the-throne for a higher lama; admiration for *tsachermu* lamas responsive to the poor; dislike of arrogant *ongchermu* big men). Merit (*payin*) and prestige (*ming* “name,” *namdal* “reputation”) could not be disaggregated in founders’ practice.

## Operationalizations

| Indicator (as used by Ortner / peers) | What it indexes |
|---|---|
| Trade fortune, livestock concentration | Big-sector wealth (e.g. von Fürer-Haimendorf 1957 cattle: few wealthy families hold most yak) |
| Pembu/gembu office, tax share, free labor | Political bigness and extraction capacity |
| Land holdings, ability to endow religious institutions | Capacity for temple/monastery sponsorship |
| Wage labor / Darjeeling cash without estate inheritance | Small-people route to material power and self-respect |
| Middle-son / daughter position in elite families | Recruitment pool for monks/nuns (“little big people”) |
| Merit sponsorship + prestige talk | Dual legitimation language for big founders |

**Relative living standard.** Ortner stressed that even “small” Sherpas were better off than the smallest groups in many other Nepal communities—every family owned land, modern Sherpa-on-Sherpa tenancy was unheard of—so small people should be read as careful smallholding peasants with mobility up and down the ladder, not a deeply impoverished class (with the caveat that the poorest may have emigrated).

## Applications

### Big-people legitimation and monastery foundings

Ortner situated early-twentieth-century celibate monastery foundings in rising big-people wealth (British raj opportunities, trade boom, state tax-collecting posts) interacting with **declining legitimacy** (Rana revenue squeeze; collectors as alien-state agents; moneylending; intensified big–big rivalry). Enactment of the [[cultural-schema|schema]] culminating in temple founding could re-infuse positions with bigness by showing the hero as powerful yet altruistic—blending bigness and smallness as the essence of Sherpa “legitimacy.”

- **Tengboche (1916):** sponsors Karma, Kusang, Gembu Tsepal—traders; two were state tax collectors at times.
- **Chiwong (1923):** Sangye (Karma’s younger brother)—Darjeeling labor contractor → largest Solu fortune → unique endowment for fifty monks in perpetuity; also *guthi* tax-exempt land conversion.
- Prestige vs. merit motives: Kusang answered in moral terms (*gyewa*, *payin*); other informants stressed *ming*/*namdal*; Ortner treated both as aspects of legitimation (political *and* internal sense of moral worth).

### Small-people wage-labor empowerment

Ortner argued Darjeeling wage labor and petty enterprise (plus the potato as tax buffer and wage-surplus enabler) gave many small people material power and self-respect. Their participation in monastery foundings—labor and material contributions without which buildings would not have risen—expressed a **counterclaim**: denial that fewer material resources defined them as “small” and of no social consequence. She proposed **valorization** as an umbrella for claims of legitimacy, authenticity, and social value at any level; classic “legitimation” is the dominant actors’ subtype (*proposed:* valorization frame; *part_of:* [[practice-theory]]).

Solidarity of big and small rested on **inverse motives**: big making statements of smallness (humility/merit); small making statements of bigness (harnessing British/mountaineers/money as protectors, defeating poverty and illegitimate pembu). Evidence for small intentions was “very tentative,” she admitted—main evidence was high cross-class participation and merit language for all. Metaphorically, many small people enacted the schema: squeezed by pembu → leave for British “protection”/cash → return → stand up to reigning big men; Miller’s Darjeeling research linked wages to reduced debt and the breaking of purification/reentry controls, contributing to gembu decline.

### Little big people — monks and nuns

Monks and nuns, Ortner argued, were disadvantaged elite children (middle sons ~overrepresented at mid-century monasteries; daughters with uncertain dowry/status). Monasticism offered a third path: morally “high” without lay bigness—theoretically higher than the biggest lay people yet “smaller” (poorer, less self-interested) than the smallest villager. Rumbu monastery (1902) made this option realistic as Darjeeling was for small people. Their exuberant founding participation (e.g. Devuche nuns’ initiative, per Ngawang Samden) expressed subjective redefinition of place; Thami’s conversion to celibacy (~1952) was the religious analogue of political gembu overthrow.

### Life and Death on Mt. Everest (1999)

*(Attributed — [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study]].)* Mountaineering and trekking wages, Ortner argued, **leveled some old dependencies** (less debt-servitude) while producing **new inequalities**: hotel-owning retired sardars vs load-carriers into middle age; tourist-route residents vs Solu “internal proletariat”; ethnic climbing Sherpas as *zhindaks* (patrons) to Tamang/Gurung workers on little pay for future opportunities. Late-century identity politics sought recognition as climbers/“members,” not only laboring “Sherpas.” Spoilage narratives that erase pre-tourist big/small complexity are rejected in favor of remaking language.

## Critiques and Limitations

1. **Emic categories, etic historicization.** Big/small are folk relative rankings densified into a structural-historical model; they should not be reified as two castes or a Marxist two-class system—Ortner explicitly denied a full class system.
2. **Asymmetric evidence.** Big founders’ biographies (Karma, Sangye, Tsepal, Kusang) are richly narrated; small-people motives are avowedly thin and interpretive. Monastery labor as “feeling big” is Ortner’s reading, not a survey finding.
3. **Regional and temporal scope.** Categories are documented for Solu-Khumbu under Ortner’s and peers’ mid-twentieth-century fieldwork and oral history; Darjeeling diaspora and later tourism economies may reconfigure status idioms.
4. **Gender underplayed in main contradiction.** Ortner noted gender’s relevance but selected contradictions most relevant to monastery foundings; daughters appear mainly under “little big people,” not as a full half of the stratification model.
5. **Valorization concept.** Ortner’s umbrella term expands Weberian legitimation; it is her theoretical proposal within *High Religion*, not a settled disciplinary term.
6. **Identity caution.** Do not map big/small onto genetic, linguistic, or archaeological populations; it is an internal Sherpa status idiom within a documented historical political economy (*associated_with:* [[sherpa]], not identity with other Himalayan groups).
