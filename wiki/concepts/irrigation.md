# Irrigation

Artificial delivery of water to cultivated land, substituting for or supplementing
rainfall. In southern Mesopotamia — where mean annual rainfall (115–135 mm, per the
1929–59 record) fell far below the ~240 mm minimum needed for dry-farming — irrigation
was not a productivity enhancement but the precondition for cultivation at all
([[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]], citing Adams 1981). See
[[mesopotamia-geography]] for the river regimes and the broader environmental setting,
and [[agricultural-intensification]] for the labor/technology bundle (ploughing, seed
rate, salinization) irrigation interacts with.

## Definition as Coined

No single coiner; the concept is a cross-cultural analytic category rather than a
term introduced by one scholar. In the Mesopotamian case Potts (1997) treats
irrigation as encompassing both **gravity-flow canal systems** (drawing on natural
watercourse gradient) and **lift irrigation** (mechanical raising of water, e.g. the
shaduf), which had different technical preconditions and different distributions
across the Tigris and Euphrates.

### Hohokam / lower Verde (Whittlesey et al. 1997, attributed)

In the southern Southwest, canal irrigation on the Gila–Salt is a defining trait of the
[[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] construct; the [[lower-verde-archaeological-project-study|LVAP]]
models **Horseshoe Basin** irrigable land (~294 ha), crop yields, and carrying capacity
AD 750–1450 (Van West & Altschul), and documents prehistoric canals/aqueducts plus
historical brush diversion dams destroyed multiple times per year by floods. Whittlesey
(Ch. 3) stresses that post-contact river regulation, beaver loss, and grazing changed
the hydraulic baseline against which prehistoric systems must be reconstructed — modern
Verde is not the prehistoric canal environment.

## Semantic History

As an analytic category "irrigation" has been stable, but its explanatory loading has shifted: mid-20th-century theory (Wittfogel's hydraulic hypothesis; cf. [[state-formation]] discussions elsewhere in the wiki) treated large-scale irrigation as the driver of despotic state power, while later work — including the textual record Potts (1997) synthesizes — emphasizes locally managed, incrementally built canal systems and royal canal-building as legitimation display rather than a hydraulic command economy. The Mesopotamian evidence now functions mostly as a check on, not a support for, the hydraulic-state reading.

## Operationalizations

Archaeologically: canal traces and levee/basin soil signatures, satellite-visible relict levees, and dated canal stratigraphy (e.g. the Musaiyib canal section). Textually: canal year-formulae, water-management vocabulary, field texts recording flooding/leaching, and legal liability clauses (Code of Hammurapi §§53–56). These operationalizations disagree at the margins — texts over-represent royal and institutional works; the ground record preserves the aggregate system.

## Canal Terminology and Royal Canal-Building

Sumerian and Akkadian possessed an extensive technical vocabulary for water-management
facilities — dams, dykes, weirs, barrages, sluices, regulators, reservoirs — attesting
substantial ancient capacity to modify watercourses deliberately (attributed; Hruska
1988a, Steinkeller 1988, van Soldt 1988, van Driel 1988, Powell 1988, cited in Potts
1997). Canals were routinely named for the rulers who built them (e.g. *e-dšul-gi* for
Shulgi, *e-šarrum-kin* for Sargon; Stol 1980). Urukagina of [[lagash]] (late Early
Dynastic III) recorded digging the Ninadua-canal for Nanshe and extending it to the
sea; Ur-Nammu, founder of the Ur III empire, recorded digging the dNanna-gu-gal canal
and connecting it to the sea (attributed; Cooper 1986; Jacobsen 1960). Eanatum of
Lagash built the Lumagimdu canal and reservoir for Ningirsu; his nephew Entemena later
restored it using 648,000 fired bricks and c. 264,960 l of bitumen (Cooper 1986).
Numerous Old Babylonian rulers (Lipit-Ishtar, Gungunum, Rim-Sin, Hammurapi of
[[babylon]], and others) used year-formulae of the form "Year (in which): the NN canal
was dug" to commemorate canal construction (Edzard 1957; Renger 1990a); Assyrian kings
(Ashur-uballit I, Tukulti-Ninurta I, Assurnasirpal II, Sennacherib) built canals along
Tigris tributaries. The Code of Hammurapi (§§53–56) assigned liability to a farmer who
failed to maintain irrigation ditch banks and thereby damaged a neighbor's field
(Driver and Miles 1952–55). Classical sources add detail: Herodotus (*Hist.* I 193)
describes Babylonian canal irrigation, though his claim that the Euphrates never
overflows naturally is judged mistaken by Potts, possibly because Herodotus crossed
Babylonia during low water (Delattre 1888). Herodotus (*Hist.* I 185–9) also reports
the Neo-Babylonian queen Nitokris diverting the Euphrates into an excavated basin
above Babylon — an account generally thought to reflect construction actually
undertaken by Nebuchadrezzar II (604–562 BC) — and Cyrus the Great later using the
same diversion to capture Babylon via the shallowed riverbed. Xenophon (*An.* II iv
13) describes irrigation canals near Babylon in 400/401 BC. Arrian records that before
Alexander's arrival 10,000 Babylonians were engaged annually for three months closing
the Pallacopas canal, a major Euphrates offtake attested in cuneiform as *nar
Pal-lu-kat* from the reign of Neriglissar (559–556 BC).

## Levee and Basin Irrigation; the Shaduf

Two soil/irrigation regimes existed in the alluvium (attributed; Charles 1988, cited
in Potts 1997): **river levee soils** (coarser sediment, well drained, lower
groundwater salinity, but requiring mechanical lift — shaduf, attested from the Early
Dynastic period, or later diesel pump — since levee crests sit above easy gravity
flow; optimal for date palms) and **river basin soils** (finer clay, less permeable,
more prone to salinization and waterlogging, but easily fed by gravity-flow canal
irrigation; used mainly for winter cereals, legumes, flax, and vegetables in
crop-fallow rotation). The [[mesopotamia-geography|Tigris]]'s deep incised bed made it
generally unsuited to gravity-flow irrigation before mechanical lifting devices
existed: Adams (1965) argued the hand-operated shaduf could not draw water profitably
from the Tigris's deep bed, though pulleys and animal traction in Assyrian or later
times eased this. Because of this constraint most scholars have described southern
Mesopotamia as a "land of one river" (the Euphrates); W. Heimpel (1987, 1990)
challenges this, showing that in the late third millennium BC the inhabitants of
Lagash considered the Tigris their main water source, and that Nippur texts attest
diversion of Tigris water during a Kassite-period shortage — an open position, not a
settled fact.

## Salinization

Irrigation water itself is the mechanism of salinization: capillary movement carries
dissolved salts to the surface during evaporation, depositing them in the root zone
(Charles 1988). The Euphrates carries ~445 ppm dissolved chemical content on average,
the Tigris ~250 ppm; evaporation from the ~30 billion cu m of combined river water
used annually leaves an estimated 22,000,000 metric tons of dissolved chemicals
(not all salt — also lime and gypsum) on Iraq's arable land each year (Cressey 1958,
cited in Potts 1997). At 400 ppm salinity and 1,500 mm annual irrigation depth,
roughly 6 tons/ha of salt accumulate annually, raising soil salinity by ~0.5% in under
seven years if not leached — a level judged too high for economical crop production
(A. Arar, cited in Butz 1979). Already by the late Early Dynastic period Sumerian had
a developed vocabulary distinguishing land quality, including terms for "area with
salt appearing" (*du6-mun*) and "heavily salinated land" (*ki-mun*) (Hruska 1985a).
See [[agricultural-intensification]] for the associated Jacobsen–Powell debate over
whether the historical barley/wheat ratio shift reflects progressive salinization.

## Earliest Canals: Choga Mami

The earliest evidence in greater Mesopotamia for canal irrigation comes from
investigations at Choga Mami, dated to roughly 6000 BC (finding; Oates and Oates
1977). Some authorities have held that the southern alluvium could not have been
colonized by its earliest inhabitants without irrigation technology already in hand —
a hypothesis Potts (1997) qualifies, arguing instead for a mixed-origin early
population (hunter-gatherers from the north Arabian desert, agriculturalists carrying
Choga Mami Transitional pottery and rudimentary irrigation knowledge from the east,
and others carrying einkorn cultivation and Hassuna-related pottery from the north).

## Classical Sources

Beyond the canal-building notices above, Strabo (*Geog.* 16 i 10–11) articulates a
recurring ancient topos that responsible river/canal management — building dams
against overflow, keeping canal mouths clear — was the mark of a good ruler, and
neglect the mark of despotic or corrupt rule; he credits Alexander's admiral
Aristobulus with reporting that Alexander personally inspected and cleared Babylonian
canals and removed artificial cataracts the Achaemenid Persians had built to obstruct
river navigation for defensive purposes.

## The Hindiyyah Barrage: A Documented Modern Failure-and-Repair Sequence

Potts (1997) uses the 18th–20th-century history of the Hindiyyah Barrage as a
close analogy for the fragility of premodern gravity-flow irrigation systems. In the
late 18th century a dam built by 'Asif ul Daulah diverted Euphrates water north of
Babylon toward Najaf, creating the Hindiyyah canal (later a major arm of the
Euphrates); by 1830 the older Hillah branch was nearly dry. Successive 19th-century
governors (Haji Muhammad 'Ali Ridha Pasha, Najib Pasha) failed to redivert water to
the Hillah branch; 'Umr Pasha's 1854 mud-and-brushwood weir proved inadequate, and by
1885 the Hillah branch was nearly dry again. A new brick barrage built by the French
engineer M. Schoenderfer burst in July 1903, leaving the lower Euphrates dry for
nearly 150 miles; Sir William Willcocks's barrage, built a decade later, finally
restored water to the Hillah branch. Edzard (1957) draws a further modern analogy: a
shift in the Euphrates bed near Musaiyib in 1820 caused 40,000 people to abandon the
town of Hillah, offered as a plausible model for the population dispersals recorded in
Old Babylonian royal resettlement claims (Ishme-Dagan of Isin, Nur-Adad of Larsa,
Hammurapi of Babylon).

## Applications

Irrigation is the environmental precondition examined on [[mesopotamia-geography]]
(river regimes, delta progradation, salinity) and the input variable examined on
[[agricultural-intensification]] (fallow cycle, seeder-plough seed economy, yield
debates). See also [[sumerians]] for the population whose land-classification
vocabulary and canal-building royal inscriptions supply much of the textual evidence
here.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Much of the reconstruction above rests on cuneiform royal inscriptions and
  year-formulae, which record commemoration of canal-building, not necessarily
  engineering detail — the gap between what a text claims a king did and what was
  physically built is not always closable.
- The "land of one river" question (Tigris vs. Euphrates primacy) remains a live
  position dispute (Heimpel vs. the traditional view), not a resolved fact.
- Ethnographic/historical analogies (Hindiyyah Barrage, 1820 Hillah abandonment) are
  used by Potts and Edzard as plausibility arguments for ancient events, not as direct
  evidence — flagged here as analogy per the wiki's evidence-class rules.
