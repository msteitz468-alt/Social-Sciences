# Third World

**Third World** names, in mid-to-late twentieth-century political and development vocabulary, the non-aligned / non-industrialized countries of Asia, Africa, and Latin America — positioned against a "First" (capitalist industrial) and "Second" (socialist industrial) world. In [[escobar-arturo|Escobar]]'s analysis (1995, attributed), the Third World is **made** as an object of knowledge and intervention by [[development-discourse]], not simply discovered as a pre-existing bloc.

## Definition as Coined

Historically, Alfred Sauvy's *tiers monde* (1952) and Cold War geopolitics gave the label wide currency. Escobar's claim (attributed) is stronger: the postwar development apparatus produced "the Third World" and "underdevelopment" as a [[regime-of-representation|regime of representation]] — analogous to how [[orientalism]] produced "the Orient."

## Semantic History

- **1950s–80s:** standard label in policy, economics, and solidarity politics.
- **1990s–present:** partly displaced by "Global South," "developing countries," "emerging markets"; residual and critical uses remain.
- **Critical analysis:** Escobar and post-development writers treat the category as historically produced; the "unmaking" of the Third World is a stated political-intellectual task of [[post-development]].

## Operationalizations

World Bank / UN income classifications; Non-Aligned Movement politics; development planning units. Escobar operationalizes via discourse history (Truman, Point Four, three-worlds tale) rather than a single index.

## Applications

Central object of [[escobar-encountering-development-study]]; linked to [[postcolonialism]] and Cold War studies.

## Critiques and Limitations

The label erases internal diversity and can reify what it criticizes. Material differences in income, health, and infrastructure are real even when the *discourse* that packages them is historical — a tension in [[post-development-debate]]. Always date-stamp and attribute strong constructivist claims.
