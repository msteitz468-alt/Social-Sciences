# Scale Shift

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* One of three **robust processes** spotlighted in DOC Ch. 10 (with actor constitution and polarization): contention moves up (or, less emphasized, down) in **number and level** of coordinated action — local to national/transnational — changing actors and the character of interaction.

## Definition as Coined

> "By scale shift we mean a change in the number and level of coordinated contentious actions leading to broader contention involving a wider range of actors and bridging their claims and identities."

Scale shift not only spreads conflict; it creates new frames and raises stakes (Solidarity from Gdańsk as example). Downward scale shift (fragmentation) involves similar mechanisms in different concatenations; most contention stays local, but major episodes typically involve some upward shift.

## Pathways (DOC Ch. 10)

Two sometimes-linked pathways, both via **attribution of similarity** (“mutual identification of actors in different sites as being sufficiently similar to justify common action”) and **emulation** (“collective action modeled on the actions of others”):

1. **Diffusion / emulation** — along established lines of interaction; more common, often narrower reach.
2. **Brokerage / coalition formation** — less common, higher investment, more likely to cross segmented lines and produce durable new identities.

## Semantic History

DOC conclusions elevate scale shift as a robust process; related diffusion language in social-movement studies and Strang–Meyer “institutional equivalence.” Distinct from genetic/linguistic “diffusion” constructs elsewhere in this wiki.

## Operationalizations

Change in count and institutional/geographic level of coordinated sites; narrative identification of diffusion vs brokerage as triggers.

## Applications

*(DOC attributed illustrations:)* late-1960s student movements (diffusion + imperfect transnational brokerage); Freedom Summer as brokered scale shift into a “revolution beyond race”; Rwandan genocide expansion via radio/network diffusion and Interahamwe brokerage (authors’ illustration of scale-shift mechanisms in extreme violence).

## Critiques and Limitations

Scale is multi-dimensional (space, institution, category); measurement often narrative; authors stress these processes are pathways, not a general model of all contention.
