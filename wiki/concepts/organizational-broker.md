# Organizational Broker

*(Attributed — [[small-mario-luis|Small]] 2009.)* An organization that, both intentionally and unintentionally, **connects people to other people, organizations, and their resources**. In *[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*, [[childcare-center|childcare centers]] broker (a) **social ties** among parents and (b) **organizational ties** to nonprofits, businesses, and government agencies. Distinct from individual brokerage in network theory ([[structural-holes-theory|Burt]], tertius gaudens) — the broker is the **organization** and its institutional practices.

## Definition as Coined

Small redefines brokerage as "the general process by which an organization connects an individual to another individual, to another organization, or to the resources they contain" — more complex than the classical person who bridges two unconnected actors.

### Dimensions of brokerage

| Dimension | Variants (attributed — Small) |
|---|---|
| Driver | **Actor-driven** (staff introduce, refer) vs **institution-driven** (rules/routines auto-connect) |
| Intentionality | **Purposive** vs **nonpurposive** (unanticipated from participants' or sometimes directors' viewpoint) |
| Target | **Social ties** (person–person) vs **organizational ties** (center–external entity) |
| Motivation | Internal (director goals) vs external (state, funders, professions, neighborhood targeting) |

### Four mechanisms for organizational (nonsocial) resources

*(Attributed — Small Ch. 6–8.)*

1. **Validation** — membership/status certifies eligibility for goods or services elsewhere  
2. **Storage** — center holds information/goods for parents (newsletters, boards, packets) — mobilizes resources for least purposive parents  
3. **Referral** — directed sending of parents to external agencies  
4. **Collaboration** — joint provision (workshops, on-site specialists, testing at the center)

## Semantic History

Builds on Burt's demonstration of brokers' importance *inside* organizations but relocates brokerage to the **organization as broker of patrons' networks**. Related to Coleman-style closure and Putnam civic capital only loosely — Small's object is resource access via routine formal organizations, including bureaucratic ties often ignored by interpersonal social capital theory.

## Operationalizations

NYC centers survey (2004, n=293): batteries on six referral resources and five collaborative resources in the last 12 months, with follow-ups (sector, name, location) to reduce overreporting. Qualitative cases (e.g. Destino) show mothers gaining formal informational resources without center friendships.

Sector and place findings *(attributed — Small Table 7.2)*: government-funded centers had 49% more referral and 220% more collaborative ties than for-profits; net of center need, high-poverty neighborhood location associated with 28% more referrals and 44% more collaborative ties.

## Applications

Primary: childcare centers and urban mothers. Extensions: colleges as especially effective brokers; churches and CBOs with referrals/collaborations; even incongruous settings (e.g. bathhouse ethnography) can display the four mechanisms. Inequality: resource-poor and socially isolated mothers gain disproportionately when connected to a well-connected center; the **organizational isolate** lacks this channel entirely.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether brokerage is "unanticipated" depends on the actor (parent vs director vs state). Collaborative ties appear more dependent on state mandates than referrals. External validity beyond NYC licensing and Head Start/ACS funding regimes is open.
