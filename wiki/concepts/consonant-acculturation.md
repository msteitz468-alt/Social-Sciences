# Consonant Acculturation

Intergenerational pattern in which parents and children learn the host language and culture — and gradually abandon home language/culture — at **roughly the same pace**. Middle term of Portes & Rumbaut's three-way typology in *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001.)* Consonant acculturation is the opposite of [[dissonant-acculturation]]: learning and cultural shift occur across generations together. Most common when immigrant parents have enough human capital to accompany and monitor children's cultural evolution. Lays a basis for parental guidance and mutual support, but **does not guarantee success** if discrimination blocks mainstream acceptance.

## Semantic History

Analytic middle path between dissonant risk and selective community retention; less emphasized in the book's policy conclusion than [[selective-acculturation]].

## Operationalizations

Similar language proficiency across generations; lower conflict than dissonant cases; often co-occurs with higher parental education/SES in CILS.

## Applications

Explains relatively smooth joint Americanization among resource-rich families without strong ethnic institutional retention.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary with selective acculturation can blur when partial bilingualism remains; "same pace" is a continuum, not a sharp type.
