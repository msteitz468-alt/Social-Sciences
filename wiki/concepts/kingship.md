# Kingship

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003), [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] treats **kingship** as universal among early civilizations: a (practically hereditary) office embodying sovereignty, with **personal and sacred** power — not a modern secular/sacred split. Kings mediate conflicts at the ultimate level, stand symbolically “above” parties, and serve as principal intermediaries channeling energy between human and cosmic orders. Divinity claims tend to be stronger where rulers are remote ([[territorial-state]]) or secluded, and weaker where urban observation is close (some [[city-state]] systems, e.g. Mesopotamia in his reading). All Trigger claims **attributed**.

## Semantic History

Crosses emic royal titles (*tlatoani*, *oba*, *ajaw*, *lugal*/*ensi*, pharaoh, Sapa Inka, *wang*, etc.) and etic political-anthropological analysis of sacred kingship. Distinct from [[chieftainship]] as a class-state institution. Trigger rejects readings of early civilizations as pure theocracies in which priests rule above kings: priests are typically subordinates within a political order headed by kings (see also [[priesthood]]).

## Operationalizations

Trigger ch. 5 comparative survey across seven early civilizations (study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]). Succession may rotate among lineages or branches while remaining hereditary in practice. Retainer sacrifice and royal mortuary elaboration vary by case but support the sacred-person pattern.

## Applications

Links [[the-state]], [[state-formation]], [[early-civilization]], religious intermediaries, military command, and law as hierarchical reinforcement. Kingship is Trigger’s “master concept” for sociopolitical organization at this level.

### Geertz — Balinese theatre-state kingship (1980, attributed)

In *[[geertz-negara-1980|Negara]]*, [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] treated classical Balinese kingship as the master image of the [[theatre-state]]: higher politics aimed “to construct a state by constructing a king,” and “a king was constructed by constructing a god.” Kings were hierophanies (one body, not Kantorowicz’s two); sovereignty was one and many — “Kings were all Incomparable, but some were more Incomparable than others,” differentiated by cult scale. Court Brahmanas were celebrants-in-chief and parts of the king’s **regalia**, not ambassadors to the gods for a secular ruler. The king as court actor was Eliot’s “still point” — Great Imperturbable / ritual object — while remaining a political mobilizer of men and goods for ceremony. *Druwe raja*: tutelary “ownership” of the realm in the symbology of power, not tenurial property law. Study: [[geertz-negara-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

"King" flattens diverse offices; gender of sovereignty and collective/rotating succession complicate the singular office model. Specialist Egyptology, Andean studies, and Maya epigraphy revise divine-kingship models after 2001; keep Trigger’s comparative claims date-stamped.

### Postgate — ruler terminology, divine kingship, and selection (1994, attributed)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] adds a documentary Mesopotamian case that both illustrates and complicates the "kingship" master concept, drawn from cuneiform royal inscriptions, hymns, and legal texts.

**Diverse and non-standardized ruler terminology.** Postgate notes that terminology for "ruler" in the early third millennium BCE was diverse and non-standard: *ensi(ak)* (e.g., at Lagash), *en* (a priestly-ruler title, at Uruk), *sanga* (a priestly title, at Umma and Isin), and *lugal* (sometimes implying domination over other rulers). He uses the neutral term "ruler" specifically to sidestep this terminological debate. Under Akkad and Ur III, *ensi* was retained but shifted in meaning to denote a provincial governor rather than an independent city-ruler (as of Postgate 1994; see [[the-state]]).

**Divine kingship as an intermittent institution.** Divine kingship — marked by the divine determinative before a ruler's name, the horned headdress in iconography, and cult worship of the living or dead ruler — begins, per Postgate, with Naram-Sin (who styled himself "god of his city," Akkad, in explicit parallel with the great gods' relation to their ancient shrines) and continues with Šulgi (who called himself "god of his land") and other Ur III rulers such as Šu-Sin, who received a dedicated temple at Ešnunna. Postgate stresses this was not a stable, continuous institution: Ur III cylinder seals maintain iconographic distinctions between king and true god even in scenes depicting royal cult, and administrative evidence for cult worship of *living* kings (offerings to royal statues, priesthoods dedicated to Šulgi, month- and personal-names formed with royal names) coexists with clear boundary-markers. Postgate flags as unresolved why certain rulers claimed divinity and others (e.g. Gudea, who claimed divine parentage but not divinity) did not; he treats the claim as specifically tied to rulers founding or heading a *new* political entity lacking a traditional patron deity (as of Postgate 1994).

**The "divine metaphor" and Hammurapi's prologue.** Postgate identifies a recurring "divine metaphor" running through Mesopotamian royal ideology: each city's political identity and fortune is bound to its patron deity, such that political success is read as divine favor. He calls the prologue to Hammurapi's law collection "the classic formulation" of this metaphor: Babylon's hegemony is expressed as the god Marduk (Babylon's patron) being entrusted by Enlil (patron of Nippur, "king of lands") with the "Enlilship," and Marduk in turn entrusting his flock to Hammurapi the shepherd — an explicit three-party metaphor (deity of subordinate city → chief god → king). Postgate cautions against reading this divine framing as mere after-the-fact rationalization of secular power; he treats it as a genuine, persistent ideological structure reaching into the first millennium BCE (as of Postgate 1994; see [[code-of-hammurabi]]).

**Divine and popular selection of rulers.** Postgate reports that patrilineal descent from a previous ruler was documented as *not* being treated as an exclusive or sufficient qualification for kingship, even though sons often did succeed in practice; claims of explicit divine selection are most emphatic precisely where succession broke — as in UruKAgina's reform text (the god Ningirsu "took him by the hand... from among the 36,000 people") and Ur-Nammu's inscriptions stressing omens confirming divine approval. Independent of divine selection, popular or assembly selection is also attested: the Gilgamesh tradition of resistance to Kish overlords has Gilgamesh chosen by citizen assembly, and a historically attested revolt against Naram-Sin saw a Kish citizen raised to kingship by city vote. Postgate reads this as evidence that a ruler could be presented simultaneously as "selected by popular vote/acclamation" and "by the decision of the gods" — divine sanction supplying, in his phrase, the "clear necessity" of an unequivocal criterion of authority in the absence of a secular one, functionally comparable to what a secular society calls "luck" (as of Postgate 1994). The king's position between temple and palace, and the later formal shift of authority from temple to secular administration, is treated on [[the-state]] and the [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]].

### Assmann — Egyptian kingship models (1996/2002, attributed)

[[assmann-jan|Assmann]] (*[[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|The Mind of Egypt]]*) distinguishes an **incarnatory** model (Old Kingdom: king as living embodiment of Horus) from a **constellational/representative** model (king as "son of Re," begotten not embodied — canonical from the Fifth Dynasty, though Assmann suspects the shift is partly a Middle Kingdom retrojection). He derives Middle Kingdom "representative theocracy," loyalism, and royal merit-ideology from First Intermediate Period nomarch semiologies (local-deity commissioning, patronage) rather than directly from the Old Kingdom, and reads the two faces of royal power (Bastet/mild, Sekhmet/avenging) and the "rhetoric of decision" as the period's legitimating apparatus. The trajectory ends in his typology of theocracy: representative (classical pharaonic) versus direct (Herihor's Theban god-state, ruled by Amun's oracle) — cf. [[theology-of-will]]. Kingship required constant generation of [[maat|ma'at]] against *isfet*; killing was in principle a royal monopoly hedged by afterlife accountability (Instruction for King Merikare).

### Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

**Akkadian/Ur III royal ideology and divine kingship.** Van De Mieroop dates a genuine conceptual break in Mesopotamian kingship ideology to Naram-Sin of Akkad: earlier kings received cult only posthumously, but Naram-Sin, after crushing a major internal rebellion, "took the unprecedented step of making himself a god" while still living — an inscription frames citizens of Akkad as having petitioned the major city-gods (Ishtar, Enlil, Dagan, Ninhursaga, Enki, Sin, Shamash, Nergal) to grant him divinity for protecting the city, with Van De Mieroop noting the irony that the very rebellious cities' gods were invoked to deify him. He links this explicitly to a visual/ideological trajectory: the Stele of the Vultures shows god and king as two distinct figures, while Naram-Sin's own victory stele merges god and king into one figure — a deliberate argument, per Van De Mieroop, about kingship ideology's development. Naram-Sin's title "king of the four quarters of the universe," together with his living cult, "placed him in a very different realm from previous rulers." Ur III kings (Shulgi, and later rulers) continued royal divine cult after this Akkadian precedent.

**Personalist loyalty as the basis of later imperial kingship.** Van De Mieroop's account of the Neo-Assyrian and Neo-Babylonian imperial states describes a kingship resting on **personalized loyalty relationships** rather than codified law or impersonal bureaucratic administration: tax requests were issued "by letter as if this were a private arrangement," and tax exemptions granted "as if they were personal favors." Loyalty oaths (*adê*) sworn by entire populations to the king functioned as a mechanism of state legitimation and succession-enforcement distinct from either kinship-based or purely coercive models of rule — a large-scale imperial apparatus that nonetheless operated on a patrimonial/personalistic logic (Van De Mieroop himself does not invoke a Weberian frame for this contrast).

### Potts (1997, attributed) — law-as-ideology, denial of burial, and marsh burial

[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts (1997)]] adds three brief points bearing on Mesopotamian kingship's relation to law and death.

N. Yoffee (1988:101–103) argued that Hammurapi's "code" (281 rulings, cast as if-then/protasis-apodosis statements) rarely aligns with, and is often contradicted by, actual legal decisions preserved in second-millennium documents; no legal document from Hammurapi's reign or his successors' mentions the code, from which Yoffee infers "Hammurabi never intended that his rules be accorded the status of practical law" (Yoffee 1988:103) — i.e. the code functioned as royal ideological display rather than an operative legal instrument (position).

Denial of burial was deployed as extreme punishment: the Laws of Hammurapi §44 threatens that a rebellious king's soldiers' bodies "should be thrown on the plain in heaps... not granted any burial," condemning them to permanent ghostly existence as a threat to the living (see [[mesopotamian-religion]] for the underworld cosmology this punishment presupposes). This contrasts with some documented third-millennium royal concern even for defeated enemy dead — Eannatum "heaped up burial mounds" over Elam's dead, and Ningirsu did likewise for Umma's dead per Enlil's command.

Burial of kings in southern Mesopotamian marshes/swamps is attested in the third millennium and again in the Second Dynasty of the Sealand (c. 1025–1005 BCE), the Dynasty of Bazi (c. 1004–985 BCE), and the "Elamite" Dynasty (c. 984–979 BCE); P.-A. Beaulieu (1988:53) noted that Urukagina's "reforms" reference burial in the "reed thicket of Enki," and that per the Dynastic Chronicle, Ea-mukin-zeri (second ruler of the Second Dynasty of the Sealand) was buried in the "swamp of Bit-Hashmar." Classical sources (Strabo, *Geog.* 16.1.11; Arrian, *Anab.* 7.22.2, both drawing on Aristobulus) record Alexander the Great inspecting royal Mesopotamian tombs among the lakes and marshes near [[babylon|Babylon]] — "the greater number of the tombs of the Assyrian kings were built in the lakes and marshlands" (Arrian). Given the absence of securely identified royal tombs from most periods, Potts considers marsh burial near Enki's abode (associating the dead king with the freshwater-god's domain) a likely explanation, though this remains inferential.

Seal-loss legal machinery (Ur III/Old Babylonian public invalidation of lost seals) is treated in full on [[cylinder-seal]].
