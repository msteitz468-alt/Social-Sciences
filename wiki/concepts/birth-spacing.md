# Birth Spacing (forager demography)

## Definition as Coined

**Birth spacing** is the interval between successive live births. In hunter-gatherer demography it is treated as a key regulator of population growth under high female mobility and workload.

*(Densified from [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, Ch. 11, with Howell’s demographic work.)* Among Dobe [[juhoansi|!Kung]], Lee reported long intervals among more nomadic women (**mean 44.11 months** where the first child survived to the next birth), shortening with sedentarization (**36.17 months** for more sedentary women)—central findings of the 1963–73 birth registry test of a model first presented 1970 and published Lee 1972d.

## Semantic History

Opposed earlier emphasis on **infanticide** as the primary forager population check (Binford via Pfeiffer; Birdsell estimated 15–50% of children killed). Lee’s position: high infanticide is not necessary; under foraging conditions !Kung maintain very low fertility via long birth spacing with infanticide **<2%** (Howell 1976b:147). Mechanism of growth under sedentism is **shortened live-birth intervals**, not mainly reduced infanticide.

Lee situates spacing at the intersection of two systems: **production** (gathering mobility) and **reproduction** (pregnancy, lactation, infant care). A third system he calls the **“emotional economy”**—exclusive long maternal investment under nomadic spacing—is stressed under short spacing even when nutrition is met. Marxist + systems framing of Ch. 11 (Preface preview: births almost 4 years apart among nomadic women; spacing falls rapidly with sedentarization → population boom and child-rearing dislocations).

## Operationalizations

### Women’s work base (why spacing matters)

Lee reports women’s gathering supplies about **two-thirds** of all food consumed by a !Kung camp; men’s hunting and gifts make up the remainder. Subsistence: **2–3 workdays/week** per adult woman; each workday 3–20 km round trip with **7–15 kg** plant loads. Adult women walk ~**2400 km/year**; for at least half that distance they carry substantial burdens. On most gathering trips and every visit/group move, a woman carries each child under age 4; breast-feeding into third or fourth year. Over 4 years of dependency: ~**7800 km** carrying one child. Work unit: **kilogram-kilometer** (kg × km). Full 4-year carry of one child: ~**72,280 kg-km** (avg ~17,820 kg-km/yr). Child weights from 164 observations of 40 children (1967–9).

### Work cost of shorter intervals (modeled)

With mean 4-year spacing, average daily child burden over 10 years ~**9.2 kg**. At 5-year spacing: 2 children/10 years, avg **7.8 kg**; at 2-year spacing: 5 children, avg **17.0 kg** (up to 21.2 kg for 4 of 10 years). Average work/year (kg-km): 2-yr **32,064**; 3-yr **22,824**; 4-yr **17,808**; 5-yr **14,256** (Table 11.5). Postpartum sex taboos only during the first year of the baby’s life—so long spacing is not explained by multi-year coital abstinence. Emic saying: a woman who gives birth “like an animal” to successive offspring has a permanent backache.

### Adaptive interpretation (Lee’s theory)

Long spacing adaptive individually (better care, survival) and demographically (slows growth vs food supply); modest excess fertility absorbed by infant mortality, occasional infanticide, out-migration. Slow childhood growth also adaptive: smaller babies easier to carry—if !Kung children grew at Western rates, carrying burden would be “intolerable” (Lee). After ~6 months, !Kung children run ~75–80% of Western weight-for-age.

### Sedentarization–fertility model (Lee 1972d / 1979)

Reduced walking (partial agriculture, wild-grain milling, coastal/riverine resources) lowers maternal kg-km work; a mother can shorten birth intervals while keeping work effort roughly constant → higher fertility and population growth **even without expanded food supply**. Sedentarization removes “adverse effects of high fertility” that make high natural fertility maladaptive for mobile foragers.

### 1963–73 empirical test

- Registry from July 1963; field dates ±5 days, interview reconstructions ±30 days.
- **256** adult women followed 1963–73; **92** with two+ births → ≥1 measurable interval.
- Natural laboratory: !Goshe (mud-hut village; milk + grain important) vs /Du/da (classic foraging, 5–6 camp moves/year).

**Results (Lee 1979):**

| Measure | Value |
|---|---|
| All successive births (165 intervals, 92 women) | mean **37.23** months |
| More nomadic, first child survived (n=55) | mean **44.11** months |
| More sedentary, first child survived (n=65) | mean **36.17** months |
| Whole population 1963–8 | **39.89** months |
| Whole population 1968–73 | **32.15** months |
| Sedentary drop across periods | **8.53** months |
| Nomadic drop across periods | **5.85** months |

!Goshe: four young women with successive live births **22, 23, 21, 20** months apart (1968–73). Difference 1963–8 vs 1968–73 significant at 0.05 (t-test). Case histories: /Tam (more nomadic) avg ~43.67 mo; N#isa (cattle posts) avg ~34.67; N!uhka (!Goshe) live-birth interval **23** months, birth-to-reconception ~14 months.

**Caveats Lee states:** 10-year cutoff overrepresents short intervals; if longer single-birth completions included, nomadic mean might fall between **4 and 5 years** as the model postulated. Gonorrhea terminated fertility for many of 63 mid-reproductive-age women with zero pregnancies 1963–73 (Howell 1979). Parallel fertility bursts among recently settled Inuit/Canadian natives (Schaefer 1971; Freeman 1971).

### Mechanism: lactational amenorrhea (Lee’s preferred path)

Nursing 2–3 years, vigorous, frequent, on demand, day and night; mother’s sexual life active in latter half without conception. Continuous nursing suppresses ovulation enough to yield ~**3.7 years** (44.11 months) mean interval for nomadic women. Cultural norm: nursing older children (2–3 yr standing to nurse); primary weaning reason is following pregnancy; without pregnancy nursing may continue to 5+ years; social pressure only around age 6. Lee: carrying child on hip may enhance contraceptive effect—“rather like carrying your contraceptive on your hip.” Inferred lactational amenorrhea ~**24.8–27.8 months** vs 10–12 months for India/Alaska/Rwanda nursing populations.

**Against Frisch critical-fatness (as applied by Howell) as primary explanation:** Lee argues (1) nutritional amenorrhea implies semistarvation ill-health, unlikely as routine regulator; (2) clinical nutrition of lactating !Kung adequate (Metz et al.; Truswell & Hansen); (3) Howell 1979 found ~half of conceptions below critical fat threshold; (4) other studies fail weight-gain–conception link. Favors **suckling-stimulus → pituitary/prolactin** pathway.

**Sedentary diet path:** nomadic diet lacks soft weaning foods so breast milk remains important into year 3; at !Goshe etc., cows’ milk and grains reduce need for breast milk → lower lactation/nipple stimulation → ovulation may resume by **11–14 months** → observed **20–23 month** intervals. Effects statistical/net, not uniform.

### Emotional economy (Lee’s third system)

Nomadic long spacing: exclusive maternal attention ~44 months including ~36 months breastfeeding; weaning at ~3 years when child has mature teeth/motor/language/social skills. Shorter spacing (weaning ~12 months) produces severe separation trauma; Lee describes most miserable children as 1.5–2-year-olds with younger siblings on the way; contrasts with placid nomadic infant care (Draper 1976; Konner 1976). Short spacing stresses emotional economy even if nutrition/health met; “a generation of !Kung children may be growing up bearing the psychological scars of the too-rapid transition.” Several women sought European birth-control pill rumors.

### Reproductive schedule (Howell via Lee)

Girls’ puberty usually **15–17**; first pregnancies delayed by postmenarcheal adolescent sterility; first child typically **18–22**; then 4–8 further pregnancies spaced **3–5 years** until menopause after 40. Howell noted !Kung fertility appears lower than other populations.

## Applications

- Links women’s economic role ([[sexual-division-of-labor]]) to demographic rates under the [[foraging-mode-of-production]].
- Models of Neolithic demographic transition when mobility falls and weaning foods appear.
- Comparative forager demography; critiques of infanticide-first population regulation models.
- Policy/contact: cattle-post milk and grain as proximate drivers of fertility rise (*[[lee-kung-san-study]]* natural laboratory design).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Sample: 92 women with measurable intervals; truncated 10-year window; gonorrhea confounds null-pregnancy group—Lee flags these.
- Mechanisms (critical fatness vs nursing intensity) disputed; Lee adjudicates for suckling stimulus—record as **position**, not settled physiology.
- “Emotional economy” and generational scars are interpretive theory.
- Adaptive claims (slow growth “for” carrying; population balance) are teleological-risk Lee language—keep attributed.
- Sedentarization confounded with diet change, health care, wage work, gonorrhea, and Bantu contact—not a pure mobility experiment.
- Date-stamp all figures to 1963–73 Dobe-area registry and Howell publications; do not write ethnographic present.
- Infanticide rate <2% is Howell’s figure via Lee—not an independent wiki-voice universal for foragers.
