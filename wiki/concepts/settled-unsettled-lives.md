# Settled and Unsettled Lives

[[swidler-ann|Ann Swidler]]'s distinction between two conditions under which culture works differently, central to [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]]. Introduced in 1986 and developed in Chapter 5 of *[[swidler-talk-of-love-2001|Talk of Love]]* (2001). All claims are **attributed** to Swidler.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Swidler.)* "The contrast is intended to differentiate situations in which new strategies of action are being developed and tried out (unsettled) from situations in which people are operating within established strategies of action (settled)." The distinction applies both to phases of an individual life and to whole historical periods; "there are… more and less settled lives and more and less settled cultural periods."

## Semantic History

- **Settled lives.** Culture and action are **loosely coupled**: people "can live with a loose fit between culture and experience… profess ideals they do not follow, utter platitudes without meaning them." Here culture supplies a [[cultural-toolkit|tool kit]] of resources from which diverse [[strategies-of-action|strategies of action]] are built; its influence is "facilitative rather than determinative." Its causal trace is "cultural lag" — reluctance to abandon strategies for which one already has the cultural equipment.
- **Unsettled lives.** Culture is more visible — "there appears to be 'more' culture" — because people actively use it to learn new ways of being. Explicit **ideologies** constitute new actors (selves, families, corporations), teach new styles and skills, and model new forms of authority. This influence is direct and powerful but "strong but not deep": ideologies compete with entrenched tradition and common sense and do not supplant the whole repertoire.
- **Values, differently.** In unsettled lives people change values while retaining strategies of action; in settled lives values serve "not as determiners of ends but as tools for fine-tuning action within established life strategies."
- **The Weberian debt.** The concept reworks Weber's *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|Protestant Ethic]]*: ascetic Protestantism is the paradigm case of ideology directly reshaping conduct in an unsettled period. Swidler adds that an ideology's ultimate success depends on structural/historical factors (which groups adopt it, who wins), not on its internal content alone — and stages the Weber-vs-Walzer question of whether Calvinism triumphed by directly reshaping believers or by serving displaced elites' pre-existing interests, synthesizing that Calvinism "played an independent causal role by providing elites with a new understanding of what their 'interests' might be."

## Ideology, Tradition, and Common Sense

*(Attributed — Swidler, building on Comaroff and Comaroff 1991.)* Cutting across the settled/unsettled axis, Swidler grades cultural material by its **mode of holding** on a three-part continuum:

- **Ideology** — explicit, self-conscious, articulated, aspiring to a unified answer; defines and bounds a (often sectarian) community.
- **Tradition** — articulated and visible but presented as fixed/expected; sustains diverse solidary groups through practice more than belief.
- **Common sense** — unselfconscious, taken-for-granted, internally contradictory but assumed shared; sustained by ordinary conversation, not group ritual.

These describe the mode of holding a belief, not fixed properties of beliefs: the same content can be held by different people as ideology, tradition, or common sense, and can transform among them over time (an ideology naturalizing into common sense, or common sense "reactivated" as explicit ideology — Geertz on modern "ideologized" Islam, where believers come to "hold" rather than be "held by" their beliefs). Swidler's "common sense" ≈ the Comaroffs' "hegemony"; she adds "tradition" as the intermediate category.

## Operationalizations

The distinction is not measured but diagnosed by the relation between culture and action: a life or period is *settled* where actors operate within established [[strategies-of-action|strategies of action]] and can tolerate a loose fit between professed culture and conduct (the observable trace is "cultural lag"); it is *unsettled* where new strategies are being built and culture becomes salient, explicit, and effortful. The mode-of-holding continuum (ideology / tradition / common sense, above) supplies a finer diagnostic: how self-consciously and how coherently a piece of culture is held indexes how settled the situation is.

## Applications

The distinction organizes the whole toolkit program's account of variation in culture's causal power, and reappears in the analysis of love: mythic romantic love is the ideology-like, highly elaborated culture that flourishes at the unsettled "gap" of marriage-formation, while sustaining a marriage runs on more settled, prosaic-realist common sense (see [[swidler-talk-of-love-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Positions.)* The settled/unsettled contrast is a matter of degree, not a clean binary, and critics note the difficulty of independently identifying an "unsettled" period except by the cultural ferment it is invoked to explain. The claim that ideological influence is "strong but not deep" is a strong empirical wager awaiting the ingestion of critics.
