# Transposable Schemas (Sewell's Theory of Structure)

## Definition as Coined

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, "A Theory of Structure: Duality, Agency, and Transformation," *AJS* 1992; reprinted in *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*) reconstructs the concept of **structure** to achieve three goals: "(1) to recognize the agency of social actors, (2) to build the possibility of change into the concept of structure, and (3) to overcome the divide between semiotic and materialist visions of structure."

Structure, on his account, is the interplay of two elements:

- **Schemas** — "generalizable procedures applied in the enactment/reproduction of social life," Sewell's preferred replacement for [[structuration-theory|Giddens]]'s "rules" (which wrongly connote formal prescriptions). Schemas range in depth from Lévi-Straussian deep structures to superficial conventions, and are **transposable** — "they can be applied to a wide and not fully predictable range of cases outside the context in which they are initially learned" (a term Sewell takes from [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], preferring it to Giddens's "generalizable"). Schemas are **virtual**.
- **Resources** — media of power, of two kinds: **nonhuman** (objects) and **human** (strength, dexterity, knowledge, emotional commitments), reformulating Giddens's [[allocative-and-authoritative-resources|allocative/authoritative]] split. Resources are **actual** (they exist in time-space) and unevenly but universally distributed.

## Semantic History

The concept originates in Sewell's 1992 *AJS* article "A Theory of Structure," written to give theoretical grounding to what social historians "already did from professional instinct" — borrowing structure/agency vocabulary from sociology and anthropology. It is a deliberate rework of two Critical-Turn programs: it takes "duality of structure" from [[structuration-theory|Giddens]] and "transposable" from [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]]'s definition of [[habitus]], while rejecting Giddens's underspecification and Bourdieu's determinism. Reprinted as the core theoretical chapter of *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* (2005), it became one of the most cited theoretical statements in historical and cultural sociology.

## The Duality of Schemas and Resources

Sewell argues Giddens's definition of structure as wholly "virtual" is self-contradictory, since resources plainly exist in time and space: "If structures are virtual, they cannot include both schemas and resources. And if they include both schemas and resources, they cannot be virtual." His resolution: structure has a **dual character**, composed simultaneously of virtual schemas and actual resources, each generating the other — "schemas are the effects of resources, just as resources are the effects of schemas." Resources are instantiations of schemas that "can be read like texts"; schemas unvalidated by resources are abandoned, resources without schemas decay. This reworks Giddens's [[duality-of-structure|duality of structure]] and [[rules-and-resources|rules and resources]].

## The Five Axioms of Transformability

Against [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]]'s [[habitus]] — which Sewell says "retains precisely the agent-proof quality that the concept of the duality of structure is supposed to overcome" and makes "significant social transformations seem impossible" — Sewell proposes five axioms that make structures inherently transformable (attributed):

1. **Multiplicity of structures** — societies rest on many structures at different levels and modalities, so actors are "far more versatile than Bourdieu's account of a universally homologous habitus would imply."
2. **Transposability of schemas** — schemas can be extended to new, not-fully-predictable cases; "there is no fixed limit to the possible transpositions" (drawing out an implication Sewell says Bourdieu left undeveloped). This grounds his definition of [[agency]].
3. **Unpredictability of resource accumulation** — "the resource consequences of the enactment of cultural schemas is never entirely predictable," so schemas are differentially validated and thus modifiable.
4. **Polysemy of resources** — resources, like texts, are ambiguous in meaning; e.g., the factory teaches capitalist property relations but "can also teach the necessarily social and collective character of production."
5. **Intersection of structures** — structures overlap, so resources can be claimed under rival schemas and schemas borrowed across domains (the factory as resource in both capitalist production and worker solidarity).

Summary (attributed): "Structures … are sets of mutually sustaining schemas and resources … But their reproduction is never automatic. Structures are at risk … in all of the social encounters they shape."

## Varieties of Structures

Sewell adds a typology on two dimensions — **depth** (how pervasive/taken-for-granted the schemas) and **power** (how much and what kind of resources mobilized): language (very deep, low power → durable, but a poor general paradigm because it neglects resources); states (often low depth, high power → mutable); capitalism (high power with a deep, exceptionally transposable schema Sewell locates in "the conversion of use value into exchange value," so that surface instability reinforces the deep commodity-form schema).

## Operationalizations

Analytically, structure is decomposed into the schemas actors know and can transpose and the human/nonhuman resources they command; transformation is traced through the five axioms — where multiplicity, transposability, unpredictable accumulation, polysemy, and intersection let ordinary structure-reproducing action tip into structure-changing action. [[agency]] is read off the same two components (schema-knowledge and resource-control). In [[eventful-sociology|eventful analysis]] the framework is applied to identify how [[historical-event|events]] rearticulate structures.

## Applications

Sewell applies the framework across his own work and demonstrations: capitalism (the exceptionally transposable commodity-form schema), the state, and language as varieties of structure; the taking of the Bastille (see [[historical-event]]) and the Marseille dockworkers as events/conjunctures where schemas and resources are rearticulated; and as a corrective to [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s singular, purely cultural conception of structure (see [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Sewell's account is itself a critique of [[structuration-theory|Giddens]] (incoherent "virtual" structure) and [[practice-theory|Bourdieu]] (habitus's determinism), recorded on those pages. His own risk, which he notes, is "lapsing into … de facto idealism" if resources are treated as mere effects of schemas. See [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].
