# SPEAKING Model

## Definition as Coined

[[hymes-dell|Hymes]] groups components of [[speech-event|speech events]] and acts into a mnemonic **SPEAKING** (*[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations]]* Ch. 2). He stresses that the code word "has nothing to do with the form of an eventual model and theory" — it is a convenience, not a causal theory. A French regrouping **PARLANT** shows the set is not wholly ethnocentric.

| Letter | Components (Hymes) |
|---|---|
| **S** | **Setting** (time, place, physical circumstances) and **Scene** (cultural definition of the occasion) |
| **P** | **Participants** — speaker, addressor, hearer, addressee (dyadic speaker–hearer model is inadequate) |
| **E** | **Ends** — purposes-outcomes (conventional expected results) and purposes-goals (participants' strategies) |
| **A** | **Act sequence** — message form + message content; "how something is said is part of what is said" |
| **K** | **Key** — tone, manner, or spirit (mock vs face-value; may override content) |
| **I** | **Instrumentalities** — channels (oral, written, etc.) and forms of speech / speech styles |
| **N** | **Norms** of interaction and of interpretation (including belief systems; cf. Malinowski dogmatic context) |
| **G** | **Genres** — categories such as poem, myth, tale, proverb, riddle, curse, prayer, oration, lecture, etc.; independent of events |

Underlying the mnemonic is a larger **etic heuristic grid** (sixteen/seventeen components in the fuller inventory). Rules of speaking are **relations among components**, not free-floating prescriptions; hierarchies of which components define events differ by society.

## Fuller 1972 Componential Statement

Hymes's chapter 1 in *[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972) gives the earlier, richer 16–17-component exposition underlying the mnemonic (attributed, Hymes; a revision of his 1967 paper). He justifies grouping "some sixteen or seventeen components" under eight letters partly by appeal to Miller's (1956) finding that human memory works best with **7±2 categories**. The 1972 componential detail:

- **Setting/Scene**: Setting = "the time and place of a speech act and, in general, the physical circumstances"; Scene = the "psychological setting," the cultural (re)definition of an occasion (formal↔informal, serious↔festive) independent of physical setting.
- **Participants**: speaker, addressor, hearer, addressee — Hymes argues the traditional dyadic speaker–hearer model specifies "sometimes too many, sometimes too few, sometimes the wrong participants," citing Wishram Chinook formal scenes (source/spokesman/audience) and Yana myths (speech-form gender fixed by character, not narrator).
- **Ends**: purposes-outcomes (community-ascribed) vs. purposes-goals (participants' own), plus latent/unintended effects — illustrated with Waiwai *oho*-chant varying by outcome (marriage, trade, work, feast, peace-making).
- **Act sequence**: message form + message content; "how something is said is part of what is said" — failure to record form is called "the most common, and most serious, defect" in reports of speaking.
- **Key**: tone/manner/spirit, roughly modality; can override overt content (sarcasm); signaled verbally or nonverbally (wink, gesture, dress, music).
- **Instrumentalities**: channels (oral, written, telegraphic, semaphore, modes of oral use) and forms of speech (language/dialect, code/mutual-intelligibility, variety/register) — sociolinguistics privileges "varieties."
- **Norms**: of interaction (turn-taking, interruption, register rules) and of interpretation (belief systems needed to read norms correctly cross-culturally, e.g. Arab vs. American directness norms, Black vs. white American hesitation-marker norms) — overlaps Garfinkel's "ad hoc-ing."
- **Genres**: poem, myth, tale, proverb, riddle, curse, prayer, oration, lecture, etc. — analytically independent of events (a sermon-genre feature usable outside church).

Hymes's French mnemonic parallel **PARLANT** (participants, actes, raison/résultat, locale, agents, normes, ton, types) is offered to show SPEAKING "is not wholly ethnocentric." He proposes a "sociolinguistic commutation test" — what contrast (if any) results when a component is shifted — as the formal-analysis complement to the mnemonic, worked through componentially on three Abipon (Argentina) events.

## Semantic History

Rooted in Jakobson's functions/components of the speech event, Prague and Firthian contextualism, and Burke's dramatism. Became the most portable teaching tool of the ethnography of speaking — and therefore the most often reduced to a student checklist, a use Hymes's own "nothing to do with eventual model" warning anticipated.

## Operationalizations

1. Identify a [[speech-event]] or act.
2. Fill components ethnographically (emic categories under etic headings).
3. State rules as relations (co-occurrence and alternation; Ervin-Tripp).
4. Compare across events and communities without assuming universal ranking of components.

Ch. 9 develops **key** (mock vs face-value) and **reciprocal vs reflexive** role construal as load-bearing for interpreting syntactic/semantic examples in actual conversation.

## Applications

- Field guide for [[ethnography-of-communication]]
- Teaching inventory in linguistic anthropology
- Applied classroom and bilingual-education ethnography (Hymes Ch. 5)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Checklist capture:** Filling S–G without analyzing relations among components yields description without theory.
- **Mnemonic ethnocentrism:** English SPEAKING / French PARLANT are conveniences; component inventory is the real apparatus.
- **Not a complete theory:** Hymes presents it as preliminary heuristic ("toward toward a theory"); formal rule statement and comparison remain open tasks.
