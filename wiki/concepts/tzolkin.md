# Tzolk'in (260-Day Sacred Almanac)

## Definition as Coined

[[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] describe the **260-day sacred almanac** (commonly labeled **Tzolk’in** in modern scholarship) as determining the Maya pattern of ceremonial life and providing a basis for prophecy. Structure: numbers **1–13** prefix a sequence of **20 day names**. Yukatek day names they list: Imix, Ik’, Ak’bal, K’an, Chikchan, Kimi, Manik’, Lamat, Muluk, Ok, Chuwen, Eb, Ben, Ix, Men, Kib, Kaban, Etz’nab, Kawak, Ajaw. The cycle is one of three most common counts shared by all Mesoamerican peoples (with the 365-day year and 52-year [[calendar-round|Calendar Round]]); the [[long-count|Long Count]] is the Maya-unique absolute day count independent of the 260- and 365-day cycles.

## Semantic History

- **Shared Mesoamerican deep time:** Ancient concept across [[mesoamerica]], not a Maya invention exclusive to the lowlands.
- **Classic practice:** Birth dates and patron deities of destiny recorded; year-bearers (four almanac days that can open the [[haab|Haab]]) were Classic Ak’bal, Lamat, Ben, Etz’nab; by Spanish Conquest shifted to K’an, Muluk, Ix, Kawak.
- **Animate time:** Basic unit *k’in* (day); twenty *k’ins* form a *winal*; each represented by deities—time itself animate and ordering (Sharer & Traxler). Each day of the 260-day calendar and head-variant numerals had patron deities.
- **Postclassic / Conquest:** Ritual obligations and destiny governed by the almanac; Yucatan children had multiple names (*paal kaba*, *naal kaba*, *coco kaba*). Divination central; highland shamans use the 260-day calendar and red beans (*mech*). Cyclic history: each passing cycle brought possible repeated destiny; elite calendar knowledge was a power source, though farmers needed basic knowledge for daily life.
- **Ethnographic continuity:** Among modern Kaqchikel (Kakchiquel), parents may still name children by 260-day birth date; highland survival contrasts with greater lowland Yukatek loss in some comparative sketches (see also [[calendar-round]]).
- **Related counts:** 819-day count associated with four world quadrants ruled by color/directional aspects of K’awiil; thirteen named *k’atuns* with special patrons and Postclassic Yucatan *u k’ahlay k’atunob* (~256/260-year) prophecy cycle.

## Operationalizations

| Element | Content (attributed S&T 2006) |
|---|---|
| Length | 260 days = 13 × 20 |
| Day name set | 20 named days (Yukatek list above) |
| Coefficients | 1 through 13 |
| Destiny | Deity of the birth day governs temperament—beneficent or hostile |
| Pairing with Haab | Least common multiple with 365-day year = 18,980 days = 52 Haab = Calendar Round |
| Year-bearers | Only four almanac days seat the Haab New Year (Classic set vs Conquest set) |
| Prophecy | K’atun cycles and almanac-based divination |

**Origin hypotheses:** Astronomical bases proposed; possible link to human gestation; **no agreement** as to origin/meaning (Sharer & Traxler).

## Applications

- Scheduling ceremonial life and personal destiny in [[classic-maya]] and broader Mesoamerican societies.
- Epigraphic dates paired with [[long-count]] Initial Series and [[haab]] for unique historical placement within Calendar Rounds.
- Ethnographic reconstruction of highland ritual calendars; colonial Books of Chilam Balam and related almanacs (see [[mayan-languages]]).
- Divination practice (beans, crystals, altered consciousness contexts) as reported ethnohistorically.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Origin disputed** — gestation, astronomy, or other bases lack consensus; do not assert a single cause in wiki voice.
- **Ethnographic present trap** — modern Kaqchikel or highland practice must not be projected unchanged onto Classic lowland courts.
- **Elite knowledge vs popular use** — authors note elite guarding of calendrical power alongside farmer-level practical knowledge; distribution varied.
- **Not a solar year** — must not be confused with the [[haab]] or with Gregorian accuracy claims (Sharer & Traxler reject superior solar-year precision myths; lunar measurement was where high accuracy is evidenced).
- Label **Tzolk’in** is a modern scholarly convenience; period sources use diverse terminology.
