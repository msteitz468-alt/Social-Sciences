## Definition as Coined

Haraway coins "Chthulucene" as her own alternative name for the current planetary
epoch, proposed as a "kind of timeplace for learning to stay with the trouble of living
and dying in response-ability on a damaged earth" *(Haraway 2016)*,
[[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]]. Etymologically the
word compounds Greek *khthôn* (chthonic — of, in, or under the earth and seas) with
*kainos* ("-cene," new/recently made/fresh), which Haraway treats as the temporal
quality shared by all three "-cene" namings in the book: "thick presence," ongoing and
unfinished rather than fixed to a single date *(Haraway 2016)*. She states directly: "As
a suffix, the word kainos, '-cene,' signals new, recently made, fresh epochs of the
thick present" *(Haraway 2016)*.

The name is anchored to a real species, the spider *Pimoa cthulhu*, which lives under
stumps in redwood forests near Haraway's home in North Central California and was named
by arachnologist Gustavo Hormiga. Haraway deliberately alters the species epithet's
spelling from *cthulhu* to *chthulu* in her coinage, a change she frames as distancing
the term from H. P. Lovecraft's monster Cthulhu: "These real and possible timespaces are
not named after SF writer H. P. Lovecraft's misogynist racial-nightmare monster Cthulhu
(note spelling difference), but rather after the diverse earthwide tentacular powers and
forces" — she names Naga, Gaia, Tangaroa, Spider Woman, and Pachamama as the kind of
figures the Chthulucene instead evokes, explicitly flagging Lovecraft's creature as
"misogynist" and racially charged *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Semantic History

Haraway states the concept directly in ontological terms: "the Chthulucene is the
timespace of the symchthonic ones, the symbiogenetic and sympoietic earthly ones, those
now submerged and squashed in the tunnels, caves, remnants, edges, and crevices of
damaged waters, airs, and lands. The chthonic ones are those indigenous to the earth in
myriad languages and stories" *(Haraway 2016)*. The earth of the Chthulucene is
explicitly [[sympoiesis|sympoietic, not autopoietic]]: "The earth of the ongoing
Chthulucene is sympoietic, not autopoietic... Poiesis is symchthonic, sympoietic, always
partnered all the way down, with no starting and subsequently interacting 'units'"
*(Haraway 2016)*.

Haraway positions the Chthulucene against the two dominant rival periodizations she
critiques at length, [[anthropocene|the Anthropocene]] and [[capitalocene|the
Capitalocene]] — feeding [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]], where her position is that
neither term alone adequately names the present and that a third frame is needed
*(Haraway 2016)*. She lists explicit objections to "Anthropocene" as a name (it centers
a universal Species Man/Man+Tool agent of history; it presumes bounded utilitarian
individualism; it is contained within systems theory and the Modern Synthesis, which
cannot handle sympoiesis/symbiogenesis; and it is a term chiefly meaningful to wealthy
intellectuals, not idiomatic to indigenous peoples or much of the world's climate/land
vocabularies) *(Haraway 2016)*, while nonetheless saying she will keep using
"Anthropocene" sparingly because it is entrenched and less controversial than
Capitalocene *(Haraway 2016)*. She frames the [[plantationocene|Plantationocene]] (a
term she attributes to a discussion with Anna Tsing and others) as a further
alternative/complement naming plantation systems' role in the same crisis.

Haraway describes her own positioning via a further self-coined term: "I am a
compostist, not a posthumanist: we are all compost, not posthuman... we inhabit the
humusities, not the humanities" — an explicit rejection of the "posthumanist" label
*(Haraway 2016)*. She frames "tentacular thinking" — thought conducted through
many-armed, entangling, non-vertebrate figures (spiders, cnidarians, squid, fungal
tangles, root systems) rather than bounded individualist models — as the cognitive
style proper to the Chthulucene: "Tentacularity is about life lived along lines—and such
a wealth of lines—not at points, not in spheres" *(Haraway 2016)*. She also develops the
figure of Terrapolis, an "SF game of response-ability" she describes as a fictional
integral equation defining an n-dimensional niche space, offered in reaction against
Kantian cosmopolitanism and Heideggerian "worlding" *(Haraway 2016)*.

Haraway credits Isabelle Stengers's concept of "cosmopolitics" as giving her "courage"
for the Chthulucene project ([[stengers-isabelle]]) *(Haraway 2016)*, and credits Thom
van Dooren and [[despret-vinciane|Vinciane Despret]] with teaching her that living and
dying well in the Chthulucene requires "join[ing] forces to reconstitute refuges...
which must include mourning irreversible losses" *(Haraway 2016)*. She credits
[[latour-bruno|Bruno Latour]] for the term "earth-bound" ("thanks for that term, Bruno
Latour–in-Anglophone-mode") *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Operationalizations

The Chthulucene resists operationalization by design — Haraway offers it as a
figurative, mythopoetic periodization rather than a dated geological boundary marker (in
contrast to the stratigraphic ambitions of Anthropocene proponents). She gestures toward
alternative iconography for the epoch, proposing the figure of Medusa/Potnia Theron (the
Minoan-Mycenaean "Mistress of the Animals") as a chthonic counter-image to masculine
sky-god hero narratives, and noting that Gorgonian corals are named for the same myth
*(Haraway 2016)*.

## Applications

Haraway develops the Chthulucene most extensively through her chapter "Making Kin:
Anthropocene, Capitalocene, Plantationocene, Chthulucene," where it organizes her
slogan "Make Kin Not Babies!" and her broader program of [[making-kin|making kin]] as
oddkin beyond genealogy *(Haraway 2016)*. In the book's closing speculative fabulation
("The Camille Stories"), the Chthulucene frames a fictional multi-generational
lineage of humans symbiotically bonded to monarch butterflies, presented as an
enactment-in-fiction of Chthulucene living rather than a forecast *(Haraway 2016)*.
Haraway also links the term to Octavia Butler's fiction, associating the Yoruba/Santería
orisha figure Oya (invoked in a Butler protagonist's name) with "the chthonic entities of
a thousand names, generators of persisting times called the Chthulucene" *(Haraway
2016)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Chthulucene is a live, contested proposal rather than an established periodization;
it sits inside the broader, unsettled dispute over how to name the current epoch
(documented on [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]]), where Haraway's own position
competes with the Anthropocene (Stoermer/Crutzen), the Capitalocene (associated by
Haraway with Jason Moore and Andreas Malm), and the Plantationocene. Because the term
is Haraway's own coinage within a single theoretical work, its standing outside her
program and that of scholars directly citing her (e.g., Anna Tsing) is, as of this
ingest, unaudited in the collection. The deliberate spelling distinction from
Lovecraft's "Cthulhu" is Haraway's own framing device, not a claim independently
verified by this source; the wiki records it as her stated intent.
