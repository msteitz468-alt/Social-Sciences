# Technological Rationality

## Definition as Coined

**Technological rationality** is [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s term, developed in *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964, esp. Chs. 6–7), for a form of reason in which scientific-technical calculability and social domination have fused into a single order. Marcuse's central and most contested claim (attributed) is that this fusion is **internal to modern science's method, not an external misuse of a neutral instrument**: "the technological society is a system of domination which operates already in the concept and construction of techniques." He rejects "the traditional notion of the 'neutrality' of technology," citing Marx's "the handmill gives you society with the feudal lord; the steam-mill society with the industrial capitalist."

His summary formulae: "**Technological rationality has become political rationality**," and "**the technological a priori is a political a priori**... there is no such thing as a purely rational scientific order; the process of technological rationality is a political process." The Galilean mathematization of nature, he argues, "separated the true from the good, science from ethics," expelling ends and values from the "objective" domain and reducing Good, Beauty, and Justice to subjective "preferences." Technology thereby "has become the great vehicle of reification... in its most mature and effective form," making domination appear as impersonal, calculable necessity.

Marcuse extends the critique to **operationalism** — the doctrine (he cites P. W. Bridgman on the concept of length) that a concept is synonymous with the operations that measure it. Transposed to political language, operationalism produces the "hypnotic noun" and the closed "universe of discourse" of Ch. 4: "freedom," "democracy," and "peace" become self-validating terms "immune against contradiction," and analytic/ordinary-language philosophy (Wittgenstein, Austin, Ryle) functions as an "affirmative," therapeutic acceptance of the given (see [[positivism]]).

## Semantic History

The concept is Marcuse's radicalization of Weber's [[rationalization]]: he seizes on "the double meaning of 'rationalization'" — the same rational enterprise that raises productivity "produced a pattern of mind and behaviour which justified and absolved even the most destructive and oppressive features." Where Weber saw an [[iron-cage|iron cage]] and disenchantment, Marcuse (with Horkheimer and Adorno's *Dialectic of Enlightenment*, and Husserl's critique of Galilean science) locates domination in the very structure of instrumental reason. The concept became central to later critical philosophy of technology and to debates over whether technology is politically neutral.

## Operationalizations

None; the concept is argued philosophically, through the history of science (Galileo, Husserl's *Lebenswelt*, the operationalism of Bridgman, Born, Heisenberg, Popper) and through Marcuse's polemical readings of empirical social science — the Hawthorne studies, opinion-poll definitions of "democracy," operational definitions of "political activity" — which he presents as **his interpretation, not independent findings** about those studies.

## Applications

Technological rationality is the epistemic core of [[one-dimensionality]]; it links to [[rationalization]], grounds the language critique that closes the "universe of discourse," and sets up the alternative of a redirected technology in [[pacification-of-existence]]. It is a foundational reference for the critical philosophy of technology.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue (attributed) that Marcuse conflates science with its social uses and reads a single "logic of domination" into heterogeneous practices; that the "neutrality of technology" he rejects is defensible; and that treating instrumental reason as inherently repressive leaves no rational ground for the alternative he wants — a tension Habermas pursued in distinguishing instrumental from communicative rationality.
