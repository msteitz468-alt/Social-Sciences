# Partial Connections

## Definition as Coined

**Partial connections** is [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]]’s concept from the monograph *[[strathern-partial-connections-1991|Partial Connections]]* (1991; AltaMira updated edition 2004/5). In the book’s own framing, the phrase names **relations through partition** — connection produced by *cutting out* connecting material, leaving remainders and gaps — rather than relations that complete a whole or map one total system onto another. Strathern (2004 Preface) says that in this volume “partial connections” does not say more or less than “writing anthropology”: the methodological problem of writing and the comparative problem of relating cases share the same imagery of proportion, scale, and incomplete fit.

*(Attributed — Strathern 1991/2004.)* Partiality only works **as a connection**: a part by itself is a whole; the analytic is of how parts extend, sever, and re-center without composing an organic totality. She credits [[haraway-donna|Donna Haraway]]’s cyborg language with permitting “partial” connections more usefully than 1980s talk of collage, montage, and fragmentation. Compatibility without full comparability; incomplete *and* partisan (echoing Clifford’s [[partial-truths]]).

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Method stance:

1. **Refuse holist units of comparison** that count societies as containers of traits (even sophisticated Galton/network solutions still locate attributes “in” societies).
2. **Keep complexity at its own scale** — scale-switching multiplies *and* loses information so that “amount” of information stays constant; every level of detail replicates complexity (Cantor dust / coastline / Koch curve as *images*, not proofs).
3. **Write with cuts** — section lacunae, remainders, prosthetic extensions of borrowed images; comparison as series of partial fits, not recovery of wholes.
4. **Melanesian personhood as method mirror** — persons as circuits of extensions cut from the same material; creative severance makes partial kinship/visibility (e.g. mother’s brothers only partially connected to sister’s sons); Strathern’s “Melanesian cyborg” differs from Haraway’s half-human/half-machine because components are not heterogeneous materials but analog images (shell = matrilineage, man = bamboo pole).

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed positions.)* Can be read as licensing indeterminacy or as reifying Melanesia as inverse of the West (standing caution also on *Gender of the Gift*). Strathern herself treats fractal/Cantor language as **numerical metaphor**, not mathematics (2004 Preface; Mosko on selective chaos analogies; contrast Eglash’s mathematical use of Cantor sets). De la Cadena stresses *careful* historical co-labor, not free-floating relativism. The book’s anti-summary form is deliberate and can frustrate extraction into checklist “findings.”
