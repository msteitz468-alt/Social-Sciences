## Definition as Coined

Weber defines "class position" as the typical chance (*Chance* — probability) of (1) provision with goods, (2) outer social standing, and (3) inner personal fate, following from the power of disposition (or its lack) that a person holds over goods, education, and marketable skills (*Leistungsqualifikationen*), and from the form in which these can be valorized for income within a given economic order. A "class" is the set of persons sharing a class position (Ch. 4 §1).

Weber distinguishes three kinds of class: a **propertied class**, whose class position is primarily determined by differences of property; an **acquisitional class** (*Erwerbsklasse*), whose class position is primarily determined by chances of market valorization for goods and services; and a **social class**, the totality of class positions among which personal or generational mobility is easy and typically occurs (Ch. 4 §1).

Within propertied classes, Weber distinguishes positively privileged propertied classes — typically rentiers, monopolizing costly consumption goods, sales policy, wealth accumulation, and capital investment or lending (from slaves, land, mines, industrial plant, shipping, loans, or securities) — from negatively privileged propertied classes, typically the unfree, the déclassé, the indebted, and the poor, with "middle classes" (*Mittelstandsklassen*) lying between. Within acquisitional classes, positively privileged classes are entrepreneurs (merchants, shipowners, industrialists, agricultural entrepreneurs, bankers, and under some conditions professionals or skilled workers) who monopolize the management of production and influence economic policy; negatively privileged acquisitional classes are typically workers, with independent farmers and craftsmen forming a middle class (Ch. 4 §1–§2).

## Semantic History

Weber explicitly departs from any assumption that class position generates organized class action automatically: "class position and class denote in themselves only the actual existence of equal (or similar) typical interest situations" — sociation on the basis of shared class interests (organized class action) can arise from any of the three class bases, but need not. He states that in pure form, propertied-class structure is not inherently dynamic or revolutionary — positively and negatively privileged propertied classes can coexist without conflict, even forming solidarity (e.g., against the unfree) — though certain contrasts, such as rentier against déclassé or creditor against debtor, can generate revolutionary struggles aimed at redistributing property rather than transforming economic structure (Ch. 4 §1).

**[FINDING, Weber's own illustration]** Weber offers as a historical case the absence of class conflict between non-slaveholding poor whites and slaveholding planters in the U.S. South, noting that poor whites were, in his account, "much more hostile to blacks than were planters in much the same position, who for their part were often governed by patriarchal feelings." This is presented here as Weber's own dated illustrative claim about a historical case, not as an independently verified finding of the wiki.

Weber notes that sociated (organized) class action is easiest to mobilize against those with directly adverse interests (workers against employers, not against shareholders), where a mass basis of shared class position exists, where concentration (a single workplace) enables assembly, and where an outside intelligentsia supplies or interprets clear aims (Ch. 4 §1–§2).

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Class position is identified via power of disposition over goods and market-valorizable skills (*Leistungsqualifikationen*), and the resulting typical *Chancen* of provision, standing, and fate. Types: propertied, acquisitional (*Erwerbsklasse*), social class (mobility set).

## Applications

Weber references Karl Marx's unfinished conclusion to *Das Kapital*, Volume III, as clearly intended to address the problem of proletarian class unity despite the qualitative differentiation of labour — a POSITION noted en route to Weber's own discussion of the displacement of skilled by semiskilled and machine-aided labour. This distinguishes Weber's market-based, multi-dimensional class concept (propertied/acquisitional/social, defined via disposition and market valorization) from the Marxian concept centered on relation to the means of production and historical class struggle — see [[class-struggle]] for the Marxian framework and [[historical-materialism]].

[[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]] made programmatic use of the Weberian framework in
*[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951): his conceptual vocabulary, he stated, is
"basically" Weber's ("with Marx behind Weber"). Mills analyzed the [[white-collar]]
[[new-middle-class]] along Weber's separate dimensions — in *property-class* terms the
white-collar worker is "in exactly the same property-class position as the wage-workers"
(propertyless), yet claims higher [[status-group|status]]; and he used Weber's *social class*
(mobility set) to predict a new class forming between lower white-collar and wage-worker
positions. Following Weber, Mills insisted that objective class position and subjective
consciousness be analyzed separately, using this to reject both "no classes in America"
arguments and the Marxian expectation of automatic proletarianization (see
[[new-middle-class-politics-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber's translator, Keith Tribe, observes (POSITION) that class analysis was not central to Weber's own sociological programme before this fragment, and that the chapter itself is incomplete — its relation to the action-theoretic framework of Chapters 1–2 and the domination typology of Chapter 3 remains unclear. Weber's own framework should not be read as reducible to, or as a direct precursor of, Marx's: class position for Weber is explicitly plural (three distinct class types) and does not entail organized solidarity as a structural necessity, in contrast with the Marxian expectation of an emergent, unified proletarian class-for-itself.

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, 1959) draws heavily on Weber's **authority** and *Herrschaftsverband* categories to rebuild class as conflict groups — but he deliberately **does not** take Weber's market-based class typology (propertied / acquisitional / social class) as the definition of class. For Dahrendorf, Weberian market class and status are nearer to **stratum** description; **class** is reserved for authority-based conflict groups ([[conflict-theory]], [[imperatively-coordinated-association]]). The two Weberian inheritances in this wiki thus diverge: Mills uses Weberian class/status for white-collar stratification; Dahrendorf uses Weberian authority for conflict-group formation.
