# Contextualization Cue

[[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]]'s signature concept and the core analytic device of [[interactional-sociolinguistics]]: the surface features of message form through which speakers signal, and listeners infer, what activity is going on and how content is to be understood.

## Definition as Coined

Verbatim (*[[gumperz-discourse-strategies-1982|Discourse Strategies]]*, Ch. 6): "constellations of surface features of message form are the means by which speakers signal and listeners interpret what the activity is, how semantic content is to be understood and how each sentence relates to what precedes or follows. These features are referred to as contextualization cues." And: "Roughly speaking, a contextualization cue is any feature of linguistic form that contributes to the signalling of contextual presuppositions." Realizations include code, dialect, and style switching, prosody, lexical and syntactic options, formulaic expressions, and openings/closings/sequencing strategies. Cues are "habitually used and perceived but rarely consciously noted and almost never talked about directly," so they "must be studied in process and in context rather than in the abstract." Cues channel [[conversational-inference]] within an **activity type** (term from Levinson 1978; frame/schema lineage via Goffman 1974 and Bartlett 1932) — the activity "does not determine meaning but simply constrains interpretations by channelling inferences" (all attributed to Gumperz 1982).

## Semantic History

Developed out of Gumperz's 1970s work on code-switching and prosody (chapter versions with Deborah Tannen 1979 and Hannah Kaltman 1979 predate the book); Ch. 4 of the 1982 book explicitly re-derives metaphorical [[code-switching]] as "a shift in contextualization cues... not accompanied by a shift in topic," softening the 1972 situational/metaphorical dichotomy into a difference of degree. The concept became foundational for interactional sociolinguistics and later work on [[indexicality]] and framing in discourse analysis.

## Operationalizations

- **Miscommunication comparison:** naturally occurring miscommunication compared with functionally similar successful passages "to derive generalizations about subculturally and situationally specific aspects of inferential processes" (Gumperz 1982).
- **Playback panels:** recorded passages played to listener panels of differing ethnic/linguistic backgrounds; divergent interpretations traced back to specific formal cues (elicitation modeled on Frake 1969).
- **Synchrony measures:** Erickson & Schultz's (1982) indices of interactional asymmetry/"uncomfortable moments," which Gumperz cites as reliable across coders and correlated with how much usable information students obtained from counselors.

## Applications

The mechanism behind the interethnic "crosstalk" findings (Gumperz 1982, Chs. 6–8, attributed): failed cue-recognition is read **attitudinally**, not linguistically — "a speaker is said to be unfriendly, impertinent, rude, uncooperative." Signature cases: the British airport cafeteria "Gravy" example (falling rather than rising intonation heard as rudeness by British hearers, an offer by Indian English conventions); the London bus driver's stressed "Exact change, *please*"; Indian English stressed-background rhetoric causing Americans to interrupt before the low-pitch main point; Black English rising-intonation "I don't wanna read" heard as refusal by white teachers but as "encourage me" by Black listeners. Also applied to sermon and political-rhetoric style switching (Ch. 9).

## Critiques and Limitations

Gumperz himself flags limits: cue meanings are scalar and conventional, so analysis "relies implicitly on our knowledge as members" — the analyst's-interpretation problem later critics pressed (recorded as a live methodological position on [[gumperz-john-j]]). Cue-based explanation of interethnic friction has also been criticized for underplaying structural racism relative to communicative difference; Gumperz's own qualification (Postscript): "not all problems of interethnic contact are communicative in nature."
