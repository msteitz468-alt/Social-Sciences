# Natural Area

## Definition as Coined

In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925), [[park-robert-e|Robert E. Park]] treats a **natural area of human habitation** as the unit within which ecological forces produce "orderly and typical grouping of its population and institutions" (attributed). [[mckenzie-r-d|R. D. McKenzie]] records that continuous invasion and accommodation yield well-defined areas with selective and cultural characteristics — "**natural areas**" (term of the Chicago Department of Sociology) or ecological "formations" — which may contain subformations (attributed). Centers of formations may be marked by **highest land values**; peripheries / boundaries between formations by **lowest land values** (attributed, McKenzie).

[[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] describes the city as differentiating into "natural economic and cultural groupings"; segregation "offers group and individuals a place and role"; areas "accentuate traits, attract and develop their kind" (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Use | Sense |
|---|---|
| Park 1925 | Ecological unit + research object (composition, selection, entry/exit, ritual, leadership) |
| Burgess 1925 | Chicago inventory: hobohemia, slums, rooming houses, Latin Quarter, Ghetto, Little Sicily, Black Belt, Deutschland, bright-light areas |
| McKenzie 1925 | Formations from invasion/succession; land-value definition of center/periphery |
| Later critique | "Natural" may naturalize segregation and underplay covenants, violence, and planning |
| Defense (Suttles via Sampson 2019) | Not only planner contrivance; multi-causal residential selection (moral, political, ecological, economic) |

## Operationalizations

- Qualitative community monographs (program: Anderson, Zorbaugh, Thrasher, etc.).
- Land-value maps; mobility and rate gradients by area.
- Later census tracts as statistical proxies (reception).

## Applications

- Human-ecology mapping of Chicago and Seattle illustrations in *The City*.
- Correlating [[juvenile-delinquency]], vice, and mobility with areas of deterioration.
- Settlement and social-agency districting debates (Burgess on ecological vs political boundaries).

## Critiques and Limitations

Normative "natural"; risk of reifying segregation; political boundaries seldom coincide with ecological/cultural ones (Burgess: Black Belt as one cultural community across multiple ecological areas and arbitrary wards).
