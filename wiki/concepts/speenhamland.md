# Speenhamland

## Definition as Coined

**Speenhamland** is the allowance system introduced by the Berkshire magistrates at the Pelican Inn, Speenhamland, on 6 May 1795: parish poor-relief scaled to the price of bread, guaranteeing every poor person a minimum income "in aid of wages" regardless of earnings — a "**right to live**." For [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] it is the paradigm case of society's self-protective countermove ([[double-movement]]) against the emerging labor market, and a load-bearing episode in *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*.

Polanyi's argument: Speenhamland was intended to protect the rural poor (and served the squirearchy's interest in preventing wage rises and labor mobility once the Act of Settlement's parish-binding was loosened), but **paradoxically pauperized them** — by subsidizing wages from the rates it destroyed the incentive to work, drove productivity and standard wages down toward pauper levels ("once on the rates, always on the rates"), and prevented the formation of a competitive labor market during the most active years of the Industrial Revolution (1795–1834). Only its abolition by the **Poor Law Reform Act of 1834** established a competitive national labor market — "industrial capitalism as a social system cannot be said to have existed before that date" — and was thus "the true birthday of the modern working class." Polanyi further argues that because Speenhamland actually *depressed* wages, classical economists misattributed the low rural wage to a natural "iron law of wages" (Malthusian population, Ricardian rent), locking naturalistic foundations into political economy.

## Semantic History

Polanyi drew on the 1834 Poor Law Commissioners' Report and on Harriet [[martineau-harriet|Martineau]]'s vivid depictions of Speenhamland-era demoralization. In his hands Speenhamland becomes an analytic emblem — the impossibility of the labor market coexisting with a "right to live" — and a cautionary tale invoked in twentieth-century debates over the welfare state, the minimum income, and the universal basic income.

## Operationalizations

Polanyi cites poor-rate expenditure (total relief falling from ~£8m in 1818 to under £6m by 1826; Southern England paying ~3.3% of income in poor rates) and the sequence of laboring-class hardship: (1) Speenhamland pauperization 1795–1834; (2) the harsh workhouse-test decade after 1834; (3) the competitive labor market to trade-union recognition in the 1870s.

## Applications

Polanyi's evidence that the road to the free market ran through deliberate state action (its abolition), that the labor market is a constructed and violent institution, and that the [[double-movement|protective countermovement]] and the market are mutually incompatible.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The Blaug reanalysis.** Mark Blaug, "The Myth of the Old Poor Law and the Making of the New" (1963), argued that Polanyi (and the 1834 Commissioners on whom he relied) overstated Speenhamland's geographic prevalence, its persistence, and its demoralizing effects — the allowance system was less widespread and less economically disastrous than the Report claimed, and the "myth" served the reformers' agenda. Later cliometric work (Boyer and others) further qualified the standard account. Polanyi's Speenhamland thesis should therefore be read as **contested**, in attributed voice; the empirical case against his reading is substantial.
- Because Polanyi leaned on a partisan primary source (the Reform Commissioners), his account inherits their bias.
