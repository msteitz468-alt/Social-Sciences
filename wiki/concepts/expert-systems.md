# Expert Systems

## Definition as Coined

In *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]* (1990), [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] defined **expert systems** as systems of technical accomplishment or professional expertise that organise large areas of the material and social environments in which we live today. They are the second type of [[disembedding]] mechanism (alongside [[symbolic-tokens]]).

Expert systems bracket time and space by deploying modes of technical knowledge that are valid independently of the practitioners and clients who use them at particular locales — the lay person trusts the *system* (medicine, architecture, aviation, law, science) more than any single known expert, yet meets the system at [[access-points]] staffed by fallible persons.

## Semantic History

Distinct from computer-science "expert systems" (AI rule engines) — Giddens’s term is sociological. Adjacent to Beck’s [[reflexive-scientization|reflexive scientization]] and the sociology of expertise, but coined within the disembedding/trust frame of 1990.

## Operationalizations

Theoretical-illustrative (air travel, medical care, buildings, investment). Trust in expert systems is argued to require a measure of *ignorance* on the lay side (faith blocks off full scrutiny); awareness of experts’ own ignorance can undermine that faith.

## Applications

- Constitutes, with symbolic tokens, [[abstract-systems]].
- Frames the "bargain with modernity": pragmatic acceptance of systems one cannot opt out of under globalised conditions.
- Connects to deskilling/reskilling of everyday life and to high-consequence risks when expertise fails or cannot know its own limits.

## Critiques and Limitations

Programmatic; risk of homogenising diverse professions. Does not replace STS fine-grained studies of expertise. Do not equate with "technocracy" as a political slogan without attribution.
