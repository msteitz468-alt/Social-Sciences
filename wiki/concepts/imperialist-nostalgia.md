# Imperialist Nostalgia

Coined by [[rosaldo-renato|Renato Rosaldo]] in *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]* (1989).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Rosaldo 1989.)* Rosaldo defined imperialist nostalgia as the paradoxical longing of agents of colonial or "civilizing" change for the very forms of life they helped to destroy — "a person kills somebody, and then mourns the victim." It uses a **pose of innocent yearning** both to capture imaginations and to conceal complicity with domination.

Rosaldo argued it operates alongside "the white man's burden" — the civilizing mission for which putatively static "savage" societies serve as a stable reference point defining civilized progress — and that its persuasive force derives from its disguise as innocent tender recollection (childhood memory), which "transform[s] the responsible colonial agent into an innocent bystander."

## Semantic History

*(Attributed.)* Rosaldo noted, following sociologist Fred Davis, that "nostalgia" was coined in the late 17th century by a Swiss physician (Greek *nostos*, return home + *algos*, painful condition) for a pathological homesickness among Swiss mercenaries abroad — with symptoms of melancholia, weeping, anorexia, and attempted suicide. He used this history to argue that the modern sense of nostalgia as a natural, panhuman sentiment is itself culturally specific, undercutting its claim to innocence.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Rosaldo proposed to **"dismantle rather than demystify"** the ideology — presenting it as a "montage" of heterogeneous voices whose contradictions surface — rather than short-circuiting analysis by unmasking a "real" interest behind it.

## Applications

Rosaldo traced the discourse across a montage of voices writing about the [[ilongot|Ilongot]]: Philippine Constabulary lieutenant Wilfrid Turnbull (whose 1929 nostalgic retrospective contradicts his own 1909 report of ordering a punitive, head-taking expedition); New Tribes Mission evangelists; the anthropologist William Jones (killed by Ilongots in 1909); and [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] and himself — all held "complicit" because "processes of drastic change often are the enabling condition of ethnographic field research." He linked the pattern to the anthropological trope of the **"vanishing savage"** ([[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski's]] salvage-ethnography doctrine; Lévi-Strauss's *Tristes Tropiques*) and to the [[ethnographic-present|ethnographic present]]. He distinguished his own reading from [[clifford-james|James Clifford's]], who located the "vanishing" pattern in the act of writing itself; Rosaldo insisted it is tied to the **imperialist project**, not merely to textuality. See [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Rosaldo distinguished imperialist nostalgia from the demystifying critic's opposite error, exemplified (in his reading) by Marshall Berman, who "combats overly romantic visions… by simply standing them on their head" — dismissing all longing for pre-modern life as fantasy and thereby reproducing an ideology of progress that privileges modernity.
