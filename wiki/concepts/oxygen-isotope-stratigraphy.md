## Definition as Coined

Marine oxygen-isotope stratigraphy uses the ratio of the heavier oxygen isotope
(18O) to the lighter (16O) preserved in the calcium-carbonate tests of foraminifera
from deep-sea sediment cores as a proxy for global ice volume. Per
[[klein-human-career-1999]]: during glacial periods, the lighter 16O isotope is
preferentially locked up in continental ice sheets (evaporated seawater falling as
snow returns disproportionately little 16O to the ocean), so the seawater remaining in
the ocean — and the foraminifera tests that form within it — becomes enriched in 18O
relative to 16O.

## Semantic History

Stages derived from the 18O/16O curve are numbered sequentially, with odd numbers
assigned to interglacial (warm) stages and even numbers to glacial (cold) stages. The
Holocene is isotope stage 1. The Last Interglacial corresponds to stage 5, with
substage 5e as its warmest phase, comparable in warmth to stage 1. The Last Glaciation
comprises stages 4 through 2, including a milder substage 3.

Klein reports (finding) that deep-sea cores reveal roughly eight major glaciations
within the past 780,000 years, and perhaps fifty over the past 2.5 million years — far
exceeding the four glaciations (Günz, Mindel, Riss, Würm) proposed by the classic
early-20th-century Alpine scheme of Penck and Brückner (1909). Klein states plainly
that "the four-glaciation Alpine scheme survives in some textbooks, but it should now
be totally abandoned" (as of Klein 1999) — a clear case of superseded periodization
that this wiki should not launder into current pages.

## Operationalizations

Deep-sea floor sediment sequences are treated as more continuous and reliable climate
archives than land-based sequences, because glacial advances on land tend to erase the
physical evidence of earlier glaciations, whereas marine sedimentation is largely
uninterrupted. The most continuous land-based analogues to the marine record are polar
ice cores (Greenland, Antarctica) and long lake or loess sequences (e.g., Lake Baikal,
Lake Biwa, Grande Pile), which can be cross-correlated against the marine
oxygen-isotope curve.

Klein also notes (finding) that interglacial peaks comparable in warmth to stage 1 or
substage 5e have, within the past 800,000–900,000 years, lasted only around 10,000
years each, while intervening cold (glacial) periods lasted around 100,000 years each —
implying that hominin populations lived mainly under glacial rather than interglacial
conditions through most of later prehistory.

## Applications

Oxygen-isotope stages are the standard chronological scaffold for dating hominin sites
and fossil occurrences across the [[pleistocene]], superseding the Alpine four-glaciation
scheme. See `quaternary-glaciation` for the broader climatic phenomenon the isotope
record documents, and [[encephalization]] for a case where Pleistocene chronology
frames the tempo of hominin brain evolution.

## Critiques and Limitations

The method depends on continuous, undisturbed deep-sea sedimentation and careful
foraminifera sampling; it records global ice volume, not local climate directly, so
regional correlations (e.g., a specific site's occupation to a specific isotope
substage) require additional independent dating controls (paleomagnetism,
radiopotassium) to avoid circular reasoning.
