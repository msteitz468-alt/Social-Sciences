# Transformative Preservation

## Definition as Coined

**Transformative preservation** is [[pitcher-m-anne|M. Anne Pitcher]]'s term (*[[pitcher-transforming-mozambique-2002|Transforming Mozambique]]*, 2002) for the process by which, "where the ruling party in power survives the transition, as it has in Mozambique, state institutions and party elites have taken advantage of restructuring to fashion new constituencies of supporters and to maintain some of the political and economic control they have exercised since independence." Pitcher coins it "to draw attention to the continuing influence of the socialist period on the emerging market economy." The transition, on this view, *redirects* rather than *withdraws* state power — leaving the political economy "somewhere between Marx and the market."

## Semantic History

The concept is deliberately dialectical: it names simultaneous **rupture and continuity**. Pitcher (attributed) builds it partly on David Stark and László Bruszt's post-socialist framing of change "not as transition from one order to another but as transformation — rearrangements, reconfigurations, and recombinations." Where dependency critics saw loss of sovereignty and neo-liberals saw state retreat, Pitcher's term captures elite and institutional persistence *through* the sale of state assets.

## Operationalizations

See `operationalized_as`. Empirically Pitcher reads transformative preservation off: (1) party/government notables becoming owners or directors of new financial, industrial, and agricultural firms; (2) the state acting as facilitator — granting incentives, brokering land to commercial operations — not merely as neutral regulator; (3) durable social networks and constituencies surviving the formal institutional break.

## Applications

Coined for Mozambique's Frelimo state; Pitcher frames it as a contribution to the comparative literature on **[[post-socialist-transition|post-socialism]]** and the politics of economic reform, portable to other cases where an incumbent party manages its own market transition. Her named evidence (attributed): a sitting and former prime ministers, ex-ministers of Defence, Justice, State Security, Trade, and Agriculture, Graça Machel, Eneas Comiche (an architect of privatization), Armando Guebuza, and former Major-General Jacinto Veloso became owners, directors, and bank presidents; the state retained majority "cash-cow" enterprises (telecoms, electricity, ports/railways) and equity in privatized joint ventures via a dedicated management office. See [[mozambique]] and [[privatization]].

Three companion framings from the same work (all attributed to Pitcher):
- **"Interrupted continuities"** — practices labelled "recolonization" (returning Portuguese capital, revived *regulos*, racial/ethnic hierarchies) are better read as continuities suppressed then resurfaced, not fresh reintroductions.
- **"Low modernism"** — post-transition officials keep a modernist appetite for discrete grand projects (the Mozal smelter, spatial development corridors) without the totalizing rationalizing plan of [[high-modernism|high modernism]].
- **"Troubled alliance"** — the actual state–capital relationship, against the government's preferred "smart partnership" gloss; partnership rhetoric legitimates state involvement while obscuring power imbalances.
