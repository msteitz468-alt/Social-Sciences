# The Great Arc of Culture

## Definition as Coined

[[benedict-ruth|Benedict]]'s image (Ch. II of *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*): "In culture too we must imagine a great arc on which are ranged the possible interests provided either by the human age-cycle or by the environment or by man's various activities. A culture that capitalized even a considerable proportion of these would be as unintelligible as a language that used all the clicks, all the glottal stops… Its identity as a culture depends upon the selection of some segments of this arc." Culture-building is thereby "like speech: selection is the prime necessity."

## Semantic History

The arc underwrites Benedict's [[cultural-relativism]] and [[configurationism]]: because the range of possibilities is so wide, cultures are radically diverse and non-hierarchical **selections**, not stages on one evolutionary ladder. Benedict develops the phonetic/selection analogy in her own voice; within *Patterns of Culture* it is **not** explicitly credited to [[sapir-edward|Sapir]] (whose parallel linguistic-patterning work is a cognate influence and who is cited elsewhere in the book for a different point). The image later fed the general anthropological commonplace that "culture selects."

## Operationalizations

Used rhetorically rather than measured; the linguistic analogy (a language selects a small set from all articulable sounds) is its working model. Corollary: "Each culture from the point of view of another ignores fundamentals and exploits irrelevancies" — one elaborates wealth, another death, another adolescence, while passing over the rest.

## Applications

Frames the argument of *Patterns of Culture* (esp. Ch. II, "The Diversity of Cultures") and grounds the claim that environmental/biological "hints" are only "pin-point potentialities" a culture elaborates far beyond the original impulse (Benedict's example: warfare is not the "expression of the instinct of pugnacity").

## Critiques and Limitations

The arc is a metaphor, not an inventory — it specifies no way to enumerate or bound "the possible," and the claim that temperamental raw material is roughly constant across humanity (so that culture does the selecting) is asserted rather than demonstrated. As a relativizing device, however, it remains one of the concept's most durable formulations.
