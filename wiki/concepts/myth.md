# Myth (Tylorian nature-myth theory)

## Definition as Coined

[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] devoted three chapters of *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, Vol. I ch. VIII–X) to myth as **a lawful product of the human mind**, not arbitrary invention — material for the study of "laws of Imagination" by the [[comparative-method]]. His central claim: "Myth arose in the savage condition prevalent in remote ages among the whole human race... while higher and later grades of civilization... continued it not merely in toleration but in honour" (Tylor 1871) — the [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]] applied to myth, resting on [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] as the warrant for comparing myths across unrelated peoples. (All attributed to Tylor.)

## Semantic History

Tylor's engine of myth is **[[animism]]**: "First and foremost among the causes which transfigure into myths the facts of daily experience, is the belief in the animation of all nature, rising at its highest pitch to personification." Hence **nature-myth**: sun, moon, dawn, wind, and storm personified into narrative (the Māori Maui cycle, Algonquin Day/Night myths). Tylor staked out a **position against Friedrich Max Müller**: where Müller derived myth from a "disease of language" (myths as misunderstood metaphors, verbal metaphor decaying into story), Tylor held "material myth to be the primary, and verbal myth to be the secondary formation" — real perceived analogies in nature come first, verbal metaphor elaborates later. This material-vs-verbal dispute is the classic nineteenth-century debate on the origin of myth. Tylor's closing formula — **"Myth is the history of its authors, not of its subjects; it records the lives, not of superhuman heroes, but of poetic nations"** — reframed myth as ethnographic evidence about the mentality of its makers, a move that outlived his evolutionism and fed later functionalist (Malinowski's "myth as charter"), structuralist (Lévi-Strauss), and symbolic approaches, all of which rejected the nature-personification thesis.

## Operationalizations

Tylor was methodologically cautious about *interpreting* particular myths: mere resemblance is insufficient and can be pushed to absurdity (his own reductio: reading "Sing a Song of Sixpence," or the biography of Julius Caesar, as a solar myth). Legitimate nature-myth reading, he held, requires **independent corroboration** — a retained etymology, self-conscious naming (Helios = Sun), or a testable external fact. He reports an actual empirical check: writing to New Zealand to confirm that the *piwakawaka* (fantail) sings at sunset, to corroborate his solar reading of the death of Maui. He also read **eponymic myths** (personified ancestors named after tribes/places) and ape-descent or giant/dwarf legends as often encoding real ethnological memory of neighbouring or displaced populations.

## Applications

The nature-myth program organized comparative mythology in the later nineteenth century (alongside Müller's solar mythology and Grimm). Tylor's "myth as evidence about its makers" is the durable legacy, standard in the anthropology and folklore of narrative.

## Critiques and Limitations

Solar and nature mythology collapsed by the early twentieth century — Andrew Lang's polemics against Müller, and the general rejection of the reductive "everything is a sun-myth" method, discredited the whole school (Tylor's own reductio anticipated the objection). The evolutionist "myth = savage philosophy, surviving into civilization" frame fell with unilinear [[social-evolutionism|evolutionism]] (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). Later theory relocated myth's meaning from personified nature to social function (Malinowski), logical structure (Lévi-Strauss), or symbol, keeping Tylor's insight that myth reveals its makers while discarding his account of what it is made from.

## The structural study of myth (Lévi-Strauss, 1955/1958)

[[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] "The Structural Study of Myth" (in *[[levi-strauss-structural-anthropology-1958|Structural Anthropology]]*) offered the most influential twentieth-century theory of myth (attributed). Its claims:

- **Myth is language, but a special part of it.** Like language, "meaning lies in the *combination* of elements, not the elements themselves"; but myth adds a third temporal register beyond Saussure's *langue* (reversible) and *parole* (irreversible) — it "always refers to events... long ago" yet functions as a timeless pattern. Against Jung's "archetype," no isolated element inherently possesses a meaning.
- **Mythemes.** Myth's units are **"gross constituent units"** or **mythemes** — not isolated relations but **bundles of relations** recurring across the narrative, to be read both diachronically (the story) and synchronically (like an orchestra score read down the columns).
- **Myth mediates contradiction.** In the worked **Oedipus** analysis, the mythemes sort into columns (over-/under-rating of blood relations; the slaying of monsters denying autochthony vs. names signalling it), and "the purpose of myth is to provide a logical model capable of overcoming a contradiction" — here, the contradiction between the belief in human autochthony and the knowledge of birth from two parents. Because the contradiction is real, the myth generates endless variants.
- **A myth is all its versions.** Structural analysis abandons the search for an "authentic" original: "we define the myth as consisting of all its versions" — Freud's reading of Oedipus is itself "a version." Mythical thought moves from opposition toward **mediation** (the trickster — coyote, raven — as a mediator between herbivore and predator).
- **No qualitative gap.** "The kind of logic in mythical thought is as rigorous as that of modern science... the difference lies, not in the quality of the intellectual process, but in the nature of the things to which it is applied."

Myth and ritual, Lévi-Strauss added ("Structure and Dialectics"), are not simply redundant: their correspondence, where it exists, is often a **structural inversion** visible only against a *neighbouring* society's ritual (his Pawnee "pregnant boy" / Plains age-grade analysis). This program is developed at length in *Mythologiques* (the raw/the cooked). See [[structuralism]], [[binary-opposition]], [[bricolage]].

**Vansina (1961, attributed) — myth as a *type of oral tradition*:** In *[[vansina-oral-tradition-1965|Oral Tradition]]*, [[vansina-jan|Vansina]] treats myth not as a stage of mind but as a class of historical source: a **didactic tale explaining the world, culture, or society in terms of religious causes**, usually recited during an enacting rite and set in a "sacred time" distinct from profane time. He explicitly **rejected Feder's classification** lumping myths together with aetiological myths and hero/saint legends, arguing myth uniquely interprets natural/supernatural relations beyond the moral order. Because myths are transmitted with religious care yet are explanation-driven, they are valuable for the history of belief but treacherous for specific events (watch for anachronism). He distinguished **aetiological myths** — explaining the origin of a cultural trait or natural feature *without* religious causation, in four subtypes (local legends, tales of natural phenomena, popular etymologies, tales of cultural traits). Citing Baumann, "aetiological myths are the sources best suited for providing accurate information about cultural history," yet few are reliable because "adapted to their aim, which is to instruct or edify" — the trustworthy detail is what is incidental to the moral. See [[oral-tradition]], [[oral-testimony]].

**Andaman (Radcliffe-Brown 1922, attributed):** myths express and systematise **social values** of natural phenomena and tradition; major vs minor motives across versions; function parallel to ceremonial (maintain collective sentiments); anti-intellectualist critique of purely etiological readings. Biliku/Puluga–Tarai weather personification as case. [[andamanese]]; [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].
