# Culture of Poverty

## Definition as Coined

Oscar Lewis’s construct: a **self-perpetuating subculture** among some of the poor, with distinctive values and behaviors that (on his account) transmit poverty independent of immediate structural conditions. Variants in Banfield and in popular conservative underclass discourse.

## Semantic History

- **Lewis (late 1950s–60s):** ethnographic formulation (Mexico, Puerto Rico, etc.).
- **1960s U.S. policy/Moynihan moment:** pathologizing readings of black family and ghetto life; liberal backlash after Moynihan.
- **Liebow 1967:** the earliest major ethnographic rebuttal — *[[liebow-tallys-corner-study|Tally's Corner]]* argues streetcorner behavior is "a direct response to the conditions of lower-class Negro life rather than... mute compliance with historical or cultural imperatives"; father–son similarity is "independently produced look-alikes" facing the same failures, not cultural transmission; alleged traits like present-orientation are situational; the corner offers only [[shadow-values]], not an autonomous value system (attributed).
- **1980s underclass debate:** conservative revival (linked to welfare and cultural explanations).
- **Wilson 1987:** rejects elevating culture-of-poverty to *central* explanation; substitutes **[[social-isolation]]** and **[[concentration-effects]]** — culture as **response** to structure, not autonomous engine (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*).
- **Massey & Denton 1993:** reject autonomous culture-of-poverty; allow structurally produced **[[culture-of-segregation]]**.
- **Stack 1974:** *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]* (fieldwork The Flats c. 1968–early 1970s) rejects self-perpetuating CoP: features often labeled cultural (unemployment, crowding, low wages) are definitions of poverty itself; Flats families show organized multi-household [[domestic-network|domestic networks]], [[swapping]], and [[child-keeping]] as adaptive responses to structural racism and AFDC rules — not fatalism or disorganization (attributed; study [[stack-all-our-kin-study]]).
- **Desmond 2016:** engages Lewis/Stack in Notes — patterned responses under scarcity and broken institutions are misread as autonomous “culture”; poverty debate should recover **exploitation** and extractive markets (housing, food, credit), not only what the poor “lack” (*[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]*).
- **Edin & Kefalas 2005:** reject claims that the poor hold a deviant subculture that denigrates marriage; report “surprisingly mainstream” marriage norms and a *higher* practical bar under constraint; reject welfare-opportunism and peer-status motives as primary explanations of early nonmarital childbearing (*[[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Promises I Can Keep]]*; study [[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]).

## Operationalizations

No stable consensus measure; often inferred from behavior the theory seeks to explain (Wilson’s circularity critique of conservative uses).

## Applications

Major rival frame in [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]; foil for structural urban poverty theories. [[stack-all-our-kin-study|Stack (1974)]] is a primary ethnographic counter: cooperative [[domestic-network|domestic networks]] and obligatory [[swapping]] under AFDC-era Midwestern urban poverty, with an explicit Conclusion challenge to self-perpetuating CoP (attributed). [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson (1999)]] is **not** a culture-of-poverty restatement: he insists on [[decent-and-street]] plurality, defensive code knowledge, and structural joblessness/police distrust as conditions of the [[code-of-the-street]] (attributed). [[desmond-evicted-study|Desmond (2016)]] stages public moral judgment of poor consumption (e.g. food-stamp stigma) against extreme [[housing-cost-burden]] and [[eviction]] cascades (attributed). [[bourgois-in-search-of-respect-study|Bourgois (1995/2003)]] treats Lewis (including *La Vida*’s El Barrio setting) as a foil: [[street-culture]] is resistance **and** self-destruction under structural apartheid, not an autonomous culture transmitting poverty irrespective of labor markets and racism (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

Blaming-the-victim charges; circular inference from behavior to culture; ethnographic overgeneralization; weak as primary cause once labor markets, segregation, and housing extraction are specified (Wilson; Massey & Denton; Desmond — attributed). Anderson’s majority-decent and code-switching claims further undercut totalizing cultural accounts when the street-code literature is misread as culture-of-poverty (position/clarification from Anderson 1999). Bourgois’s explicit critique of Lewis and of “underclass” heat in the field is another ethnographic counter-position from the same neighborhood Lewis made famous (attributed; [[el-barrio]]).
