# Reasonable Man (Barotse Law)

Concept from [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]'s "The Reasonable Man in Barotse Law" (1955; broadcast summary of *The Judicial Process among the Barotse of Northern Rhodesia*), reprinted in [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]]. Lozi fieldwork 1940–2, 1947.

## Definition as Coined

Gluckman argued the "reasonable man" is the mechanism by which [[lozi|Barotse (Lozi)]] judges — like English judges — focus abstract legal rules onto the varied circumstances of life. He distinguished (attributed throughout):

- the **generally reasonable person**, and
- the **reasonable incumbent of a particular social position** — reasonable husband, wife, father, councillor, headman, king — since Barotse legal reasoning is organized by social position rather than by abstract concept (right, duty, contract, negligence);
- the **"reasonable wrongdoer"** corollary: thieves and adulterers also behave in stereotyped, customary ways, which judges use as circumstantial evidence of guilt;
- the **"upright" man** as a higher-than-legal ethical standard: courts function as "courts of morality," publicly praising or shaming beyond what law can compel ("It is hard, but it is the law").

## Semantic History

Gluckman cited a traveller's 1896 account involving "the reasonable councillor" as evidence of a pre-protectorate pedigree, subsequently redefined by judges to meet changing conditions (e.g., a statute fixing the maximum permissible labour-migration absence before a wife could claim divorce). The concept anchored his side of the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]] over using Western legal categories cross-culturally — Bohannan's folk-system objection is the standing critique.

## Operationalizations

Case method: Gluckman built the argument from named Barotse cases with verbatim cross-examination (the Violent Councillor, the Eloping Wife, the Dog-in-the-Manger Headman, the Unfaithful Councillor). In the Dog-in-the-Manger case, judges enforced generosity without overturning land-tenure law by using the court's administrative power to discharge an "unsatisfactory" headman — Gluckman's illustration of judicial "manipulation" of logically independent rules and of the semantic flexibility of legal words.

## Applications

[[lozi]] judicial process · comparative jurisprudence (English reasonable-man standard) · [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Gluckman himself recorded (1955) that judges' presumptions about how reasonable people behave can rest on rumor or prejudice — the standard carries built-in space for both social control and bias. Bohannan's position: exporting the English category imposes an analytical system on a folk system (see debate page).
