# Anatomically Modern Humans

Etic classificatory label for fossil and living humans whose **skeletal anatomy** falls within or near the range of recent *Homo sapiens*, contrasted with archaic humans (including [[neanderthals]]). Distinct from **[[behavioral-modernity|behavioral modernity]]**: anatomy and archaeology need not coincide — a central problem in the [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

In *[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993|In Search of the Neanderthals]]* (1993), Stringer & Gamble use **Moderns** for anatomically modern people (European Cro-Magnons as type case) versus **Ancients** for Neanderthals and other pre-modern contemporaries (attributed). The label picks out a skeletal package, not a behavioural package and not (without further argument) a species-boundary verdict.

As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), the hallmarks they treat as consistently co-occurring only from ~100 ka are: shorter rounder skull; smaller brow ridge and face; prominent chin; lighter skeletal build. Earlier “borderline Moderns” (Ngaloba, Jebel Irhoud, Omo 2, etc.) display modern traits disparately — not all combined in any single specimen (attributed).

## Semantic History

Mid–late twentieth-century palaeoanthropology separated **skeletal modernity** from the cultural Upper Palaeolithic “package” once assumed to arrive with it. That separation underpins Recent African Origin timelines in which African (and Levantine) anatomical moderns predate European behavioural moderns by tens of millennia.

Stringer & Gamble (1993) make the lag explicit: earliest anatomically modern specimens of Africa and the Levant “may have looked like us in many ways, but they were not us” — lacking ~100 ka of subsequent change (attributed). They further argue modern behaviour may have spread rapidly, “rather like a virus,” on pre-existing modern anatomy after a lag of perhaps ~70 ka (position; see [[behavioral-modernity]], [[exaptation]]).

Semantic competitors and near-synonyms in the same literature include: “early moderns,” “proto-Cro-Magnoids” (1950s label for [[skhul]]/[[qafzeh]]), “archaic *Homo sapiens*” (for middle Pleistocene non-Neanderthal, non-modern specimens such as Broken Hill / Kabwe), and regional subspecies labels (e.g. *H. s. rhodesiensis*). Multiregionalists and replacement advocates read the same specimens under different species and ancestry assignments (as of Stringer & Gamble 1993).

## Operationalizations

Criteria vary by author; common osteological markers (as synthesized by Stringer & Gamble 1993 for Levantine early moderns at Skhul/Qafzeh) include:

| Domain | Modern pattern (as of S&G 1993) | Contrast with Neanderthals |
|---|---|---|
| Vault | High, rather short, rounded; parallel-sided | Long, low, broad |
| Face | Broad but low nasal opening; hollowed cheeks; little midfacial projection | Large nose; midfacial prognathism; inflated cheeks |
| Mandible | Clear chin (in full modern package) | Little/no chin; massive jaws (e.g. Kebara) |
| Brain size | ~1550 ml average at Skhul/Qafzeh (large; not diagnostic alone) | Female ~1300 / male ~1600 ml; Amud >1700 ml |
| Body proportions | Tropical: long forearms/tibiae; males ~1.83 m, females ~1.70 m at Skhul/Qafzeh | Cold-adapted stocky; short distal limbs |
| Pelvis | Modern-type morphology | Extended pubic ramus; differently shaped |

Genetic operationalizations of “modern humans” as of 1993 (mtDNA similarity implying recent origin; Cann–Stoneking–Wilson 1987 African “Eve” ~200 ka) are **horizon-bound** and contested; later nuclear aDNA reframed admixture with Neanderthals without dissolving the skeletal classificatory label.

## Applications

- **Levant corridor chronology:** Skhul/Qafzeh early moderns with Middle Palaeolithic industries, dated (as of S&G 1993) by TL/ESR ~100–90+ ka — older than Kebara Neanderthal ~60 ka — are the key case that anatomy ≠ industry and that moderns need not postdate Neanderthals locally ([[skhul]], [[qafzeh]], [[kebara]], [[tabun]]).
- **African origin sequences:** Omo Kibish 1, Klasies River Mouth fragments (~120–70 ka), contested Border Cave provenience; Jebel Irhoud / Ngaloba as borderline rather than full modern (as of S&G 1993).
- **European Cro-Magnons:** type case for Moderns entering with/after early Aurignacian; S&G note tropical body proportions retained into glacial Europe and reject Wolpoff’s long-distance locomotion explanation for limb proportions (attributed positions).
- **Origins debate scaffolding:** RAO uses early African/Levantine moderns as evidence of African morphological priority; multiregionalists treat regional archaic→modern transitions (including Neanderthal→European) as continuity ([[recent-african-origin]], [[multiregional-evolution]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Boundary specimens.** Which fossils count as “modern” (Klasies, Border Cave, Omo, Qafzeh/Skhul, Ngaloba, Irhoud, Australian robust vs gracile series) is contested; the same bones are “early modern,” “borderline,” or “archaic” depending on program (as of Stringer & Gamble 1993).
- **Anatomy ≠ behaviour.** Levantine early moderns used Middle Palaeolithic tools indistinguishable at industry level from Neanderthal assemblages; S&G themselves treat modern physique and modern behaviour as potentially different geographic/temporal origins (position).
- **Proto-Cro-Magnon problem.** If Skhul/Qafzeh are ~60 ka older than European Cro-Magnons, a simple derivation is problematic; S&G float a brief premature “Out of Africa 2” that “came to nothing,” or eastward dispersal toward Australia without early westward entry into Europe (attributed scenarios).
- **Species and admixture.** Whether AMH and Neanderthals were separate species, subspecies, or open gene-flow populations was live in 1993; post-2010 aDNA showed low-level Neanderthal ancestry in non-Africans, complicating pure-replacement and pure-continuity slogans alike — without dissolving the utility of the skeletal label.
- **Sampling and dating.** Absolute TL/ESR ages that reordered the Levant (as of 1993) remain method-dependent and have been revised; Border Cave and similar MSA associations stay provenience-fragile. Always date-stamp classificatory assignments and absolute ages.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Attributed; (as of Stringer 2012).*

### Shared modern package and diagnostic list

Shared modern physical features Stringer highlights: slender skeleton vs robust predecessors; higher/rounder braincase; smaller brow ridges; prominent chin. Diagnostic skeletal list for *H. sapiens*: large brain volume; neurocranial globularity/increased vault height; rear-view vault wider at top; higher/more evenly arched temporal; decreased face height tucked under vault; small divided brow; narrow interorbital; midfacial/nasal projection; bony chin even in infants; simplified/shrunk tooth crowns; lightly built tympanic; short nearly circular pubic ramus; no iliac pillar; femora oval, thickened A–P (as of Stringer 2012).

**Terminology history:** Initially Stringer accepted “anatomically modern sapiens” (Skhul, Cro-Magnon) vs “archaic sapiens” (Neanderthals, Broken Hill); during the 1980s he increasingly limited *sapiens* to fossils closely resembling living humans (attributed).

### African fossil sequence and boundary specimens

- **Omo Kibish / [[herto]]:** among oldest definite humans “like us” worldwide; Herto Ar/Ar sandwich 154–160 ka; Omo Kibish redatings ~195 ka; authors named Herto *H. sapiens idaltu* — Stringer argues subspecies unnecessary vs other early moderns (e.g. late Ice Age Australians) (as of Stringer 2012).
- **[[jebel-irhoud]]:** ~160 ka (possibly older); Stringer doubts full modernity overall (farther from modern than Omo Kibish and Herto peers); Hublin emphasizes child’s chin and some modern face/braincase features; synchrotron study of child’s jaw: death ~8, slow modern-like growth (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Guomde** (Kenya) perhaps ~250 ka; **Ngaloba**, **Singa** doubted as fully modern; **Omo 2** more primitive than Omo 1 (attributed).
- **Gunz et al.:** late archaic/early modern African crania (Irhoud, Omo 2, Ngaloba) intermediate and more shape-variable among themselves than Neanderthal, heidelbergensis, erectus, or modern groups (via Stringer 2012).
- **Broken Hill (Kabwe):** new ESR/U-series work suggests ~200–300 ka (closer to ~200 ka), not ~500 ka — undercutting a long gradual heidelbergensis→sapiens sequence; implies rapid transition ~250 ka and/or multi-lineage African variation (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Iwo Eleru** (Nigeria): U-series ≤~20 ka, LSA context ~13 ka charcoal, yet cranial shape closest to late archaic Africans (Ngaloba, Irhoud, Omo 2) — archaic-looking people persisted long after modern morphology appeared (as of Stringer 2012).

### Growth, diet, body form (modern vs Neanderthal)

Collaborative synchrotron study: early moderns (e.g. Skhul, Qafzeh) matured dentally at recent-human slow rates while Neanderthals grew somewhat faster (thinner enamel) (as of Stringer 2012). Cro-Magnon/Oase isotopes differ from Neanderthals: more marine/freshwater resources already in earliest sampled European moderns; Tianyuan Cave ~40 ka confirms freshwater fish; Stringer interprets dispersing moderns as extracting more nutrition than Neanderthals (attributed). Early Cro-Magnons and earliest north China modern skeleton more African-like in limb proportions than cold-adapted Neanderthals (Trinkaus via Stringer); Tianyuan foot bones suggest shoes by ≥40 ka (attributed). Forensic cranial tests based on modern regional patterns confuse early modern skulls >20 ka (e.g. Předmostí as “African,” Zhoukoudian Upper Cave as “Australian”) — different past regionality under recent African origin, not close relationship to those living populations (as of Stringer 2012).

### Anatomy vs behavior

Klein’s decoupling (morphology first, modern behavior ~50 ka) is counterintuitive if behavior drove gracilization — behavioral change should precede morphology (Stringer noting the implication, as of 2012). See [[behavioral-modernity]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]].
