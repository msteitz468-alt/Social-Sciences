# Ghost-Marriage

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]], *[[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-1951|Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer]]* 1951; study [[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]].)* **Ghost-marriage** is a **vicarious legal marriage** in which a living kinsman (*pro-husband*) marries a wife **to the name of a dead man** who died without legal male heirs. Cattle are paid and marriage rites performed **in the dead man’s name**; the **legal husband is the ghost**, not the living cohabitant. Nuer categories: the woman is *ciekjooka* (wife of a ghost); children are *gaatjooka* (children of a ghost). The resulting **ghost-family** comprises ghost (pater) + wife + children + the kinsman who begets and acts as father.

Evans-Pritchard classed ghost-marriage as a **variety of simple legal marriage** (a simple legal family is contained in its structure).

## Semantic History

English “ghost-marriage” is Evans-Pritchard’s analytic label for a Nuer institution already described in his articles of the 1940s. Comparative kinship later used it as a type case of “raising seed to the dead” / continuing a name under agnatic descent, often paired with (and confused with) the [[levirate]]. Victorian ethnology sometimes collapsed both under levirate; ASKAM and Evans-Pritchard insist on the jural difference.

## Operationalizations (Nuer, as of 1930s fieldwork)

*(Attributed — Evans-Pritchard 1951.)*

| Feature | Ghost-marriage | True [[levirate]] (Nuer) |
|---|---|---|
| Legal husband | The **ghost** (name in which cattle paid / rites done) | The **dead man** who already married the widow |
| Pro-husband action | **Actually marries** (new bridewealth + rites) | Enters an **existing** marriage as cohabitant; Nuer refuse *kwen* “to marry” for this |
| Widow status | Married as ghost’s wife (*ciekjooka*) | Remains wife of dead husband |
| Children’s pater | Ghost | Dead husband (even if brother is genitor) |

**Rationale (Nuer ideology as reported):** a daughter does not carry her father’s lineage (“A daughter, that is an unrelated person”); a man’s name must continue; neglect risks haunting. Obligation falls chiefly on a brother (esp. full junior brother), natural son, paternal nephew, or sister’s son if cattle came from MB. Frequency: Evans-Pritchard estimated ghost-marriages “must be almost as numerous as simple legal marriages.”

**Variants:** woman marries a wife to a dead kinsman (esp. uterine brother); rarely a man marries a wife to a dead barren kinswoman who then “counts as a man.”

## Applications

- Central empirical case in Nuer kinship teaching and descent theory.
- Distinguishes **pater** (name in which cattle paid) from **genitor** — “children of the cattle.”
- Related: woman-marriage (woman as pater of another woman’s children); widow-concubinage (lover not of husband’s kin — *not* ghost-marriage).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Single classic ethnography; date-stamp to 1930s Nuerland.
- Do not equate ghost-marriage with levirate or widow inheritance.
- Comparative use must preserve Nuer jural categories rather than flatten to “widow remarriage.”
