# Ritual Regulation (of Ecological Systems)

## Definition as Coined

[[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]]'s central thesis in *[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]*: among the [[tsembaga]], **ritual** (especially the cycle culminating in the [[kaiko]]) **regulates** relations among people, pigs, and gardens and articulates the local ecosystem with the regional system of other local populations. He frames ritual as both **homeostat** (maintaining variables in viable ranges) and **transducer** (translating state changes in one subsystem into effects in another).

**Eight effects** (Ch. 1 thesis list, attributed): (1) people–pigs–gardens / fallow and forest; (2) pig slaughter and pork distribution; (3) nondomesticated-animal consumption; (4) possible marsupial conservation; (5) redispersion of people and land; (6) warfare frequency; (7) severity of fighting; (8) exchange of goods and personnel among local groups.

## Semantic History

1968 formulation → 1970s textbook icon of ecological anthropology → sustained critique as functionalist teleology / vulgar materialism → 1984 Epilogue defense and refinement (formal vs final cause, units, negative feedback). Related to but not identical with Stewardian [[cultural-ecology]] (Rappaport is more systems/cybernetic).

## Operationalizations

Timing of rumbim planting/uprooting and kaiko relative to pig herd, garden stress, truce length, and warfare; quantitative appendices support the operational side. Latent-function language (Merton) bridges cognized and operational models.

## Applications

Type-case for arguing against Homans-style claims that ritual has no practical effect on the external world. Feeds debates on functionalism, adaptation, and the autonomy of meaning.

## Critiques and Limitations

Core of [[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]]: circularity, selection of confirming variables, nutritional reanalyses, political agency underplayed, comparison limits beyond Maring.
