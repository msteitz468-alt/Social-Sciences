# Reverse Anthropology

Coined by [[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]] in *[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]* (1975), Ch. 2 ("Road belong culture"). All claims are Wagner's, attributed.

## Definition as Coined

Wagner argues that colonized tribal peoples, placed in a "fieldwork situation through no fault of their own" by European expansion, produce their own objectifying comprehension of the West — a **reverse anthropology** — whose paradigmatic form in Melanesia is the cargo cult. The mirror-image formula: "we look at the natives' cargo, their techniques and artifacts, and call it 'culture,' whereas they look at our culture and call it 'cargo.'" "Cargo" is thus "an antisymbol to 'culture'; it metaphorizes the sterile orders of technique and self-fulfilling production as life and human relation, just as 'culture' does the reverse."

Wagner (following Kenelm Burridge's *Mambu*, 1970, capital-C "Cargo" vs. lowercase "cargo") holds that cargo is not primarily material wealth but symbolizes "the redemption of native society." He explicitly rejects deprivation/social-injustice explanations of cargo cults as reductive ("explanations in terms of disturbance and injustice belittle human achievements to the level of correctives"), proposing instead that cargo cult is "an interpretive counterpart of anthropology itself… a pragmatic sort of anthropology that invents in anticipation of the future."

**Illustration (Wagner's, via Peter Lawrence, *Road Belong Cargo*, 1964):** Yali of the Madang district, taken to Port Moresby in 1947, encountered administration favor toward native custom and Western evolutionary museum displays; interpreting these through his own categories, he returned to launch a revival of traditional ceremonial in order to "bring the cargo" — for Wagner, "the 'road belong cargo' became the 'road belong culture.'"

## Semantic History

The term traveled well beyond Wagner. It became a touchstone of the "New Melanesian Ethnography" and, via [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]] (who pairs it with [[controlled-equivocation]] and calls Wagner's Daribi dictum — "their misunderstanding of me was not the same as my misunderstanding them" — "the best definition of culture ever proposed"), of the ontological turn. In later usage it names any project of taking seriously the studied people's own theorizing of the anthropologist's world, rather than only the reverse.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Wagner's rejection of deprivation and political-economy accounts of [[millenarianism|cargo cults]] is a position, not a settled verdict; materialist and colonial-history explanations remain live alternatives in the cargo-cult literature.
- The concept's force depends on Wagner's broader [[invention-and-convention]] apparatus; detached from it, "reverse anthropology" risks becoming a slogan for symmetrical goodwill rather than a specific claim about symbolization.
