# Exchange Reservoir

In Libby's radiocarbon theory, the **exchange reservoir** is the interconnected set of carbon pools — atmospheric CO2, living biosphere, surface ocean, and related compartments — among which carbon mixes fast enough that living matter shares a roughly uniform **contemporary specific activity** of C-14. Dating begins when a sample is removed from that exchange (death, deposition) and its C-14 decays without replenishment (attributed, Libby *[[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Radiocarbon Dating]]*, chs. I–II).

## Definition as Coined

Libby argues that cosmic-ray neutrons produce C-14 from atmospheric N-14; the carbon burns to CO2 and enters the global exchange system. Equilibrium requires that production equal decay over the whole reservoir inventory. Samples for dating must have left that reservoir at a known event (organism death) and not exchanged carbon afterward (attributed, Libby 1952/55).

## Semantic History

"Reservoir" language persists in modern radiocarbon as **reservoir effects** (marine, hard-water) — cases where a sample's carbon is drawn from a pool not in full equilibrium with the atmosphere. Libby's ch. II already compares biosphere wood assays with shell and discusses ocean carbonate.

## Operationalizations

- **Contemporary assay:** measured specific activity of modern wood/plant carbon as the zero-age baseline.
- **World-wide uniformity tests:** samples from many latitudes to check that geomagnetic and geographic effects do not destroy the common baseline (Libby ch. II).
- **Inventory estimates:** grams of carbon per cm² of earth surface and total radiocarbon inventory (ch. I; revised figures in 1965 addendum).

## Applications

Foundation of all radiocarbon age calculations; failure modes when samples grow in or incorporate carbon from non-exchange or slow-exchange pools (limestone-derived bicarbonate, deep ocean, old wood reuse).

## Critiques and Limitations

Libby's 1960s addenda already note tension between cosmic-ray production-rate estimates and inventory, possible missing ocean carbonate, and geomagnetic-field variation as a production modulator. Later calibration science is the full accounting of production and reservoir variation over time.
