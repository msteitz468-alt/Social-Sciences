# Heritage

## Definition as Coined

**Heritage** is the past selectively curated as inheritance — the designation of sites, objects,
and practices for protection, ownership, access, and transmission. In *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary
Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010) it is treated as "the legitimization of political structures
through the selective invocation of the past," and as an increasingly contested political terrain:
"the past is an increasingly contested political terrain." International charters organize it
around three rights — **ownership, access, inheritance** (Meskell, after a Grotius/Locke lineage).
All claims here are attributed.

## Semantic History

Preucel & Mrozowski situate heritage within **neoliberalism**: heritage is "typically globalized
and regarded as a universal product belonging equally to all peoples," a "peculiarly Western
conceit … one that appropriates other people's pasts under the pretext of a common humanism" and
increasingly "promoted as an economic development tool linked to tourism and elite consumption" —
a commodity. Meskell adds that "global world heritage" is in part "a remnant of colonialism,"
tied to an Enlightenment knowledge project in which archaeological spaces served as "performative
regalia for the colonizers" (after Benedict Anderson). Appadurai proposes a normative distinction
between **history** (open, mobile) and **heritage** (fixed, essentialist), and warns of "predatory"
identities that require eliminating others' memories — reframing "the past as a scarce resource"
(1981) whose contested economy drives violent social dramas.

## Operationalizations

**Charters and law (the editors' "Three R's": Repatriation, Reconciliation, Restitution).**
The **Venice Charter** (1964) → ICOMOS; the Australian **Burra Charter** (1979), the international
standard, defining "cultural significance" broadly and admitting that a place "may have a range of
values for different individuals or groups." Instruments the editors survey: the creation of
**Nunavut** (Inuit land claim, 1993/1999) and the Inuit Heritage Trust; **NAGPRA** (the Native
American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990) with its categories of sacred objects and
objects of cultural patrimony and its "cultural affiliation" test; South Africa's **Truth and
Reconciliation Commission** (1995); and the Metropolitan Museum's 2006 return of the looted
**Euphronios krater** to Italy. See [[repatriation-of-anthropology]].

**Tourism, dispossession, and violence (Meskell, "Sites of Violence").** Heritage as tourism
economy produces dispossession: the forced relocation of the village of **Gurna** atop the Luxor
Tombs of the Nobles (deaths and injuries; ~1,300 families removed), paralleled by the Bidul
Bedouin at Petra. It also becomes a target: the **1997 Temple of Hatshepsut massacre** at Luxor
(Islamist militants killed 58 foreign and 4 Egyptian tourists; a note left read "No Tourists in
Egypt"), after which British press coverage recruited the site into "global world heritage"
against Egypt itself (Simon Jenkins: it "no longer belongs to Egypt, but to the world"). El Saadawi
called Egyptology "an example of cultural genocide" reducing a civilization to "stones or ruins"
(position, recorded not endorsed). The Taliban's destruction of the **Bamiyan Buddhas** frames
the ethical-absolutism-vs.-relativism dilemma.

**Multivocality (Hodder, Çatalhöyük — Ch. 31, the volume's coda).** Hodder distinguishes the
"past as play" (global commercial pastiche) from the "past as passion" (local identity, ownership),
then argues the two are not opposed but interact: the local mayor rebrands an agricultural festival
as the "Çumra Çatalhöyük Festival" and calls the site both "the source of Anatolian civilisation"
and "for all humanity"; Goddess-movement tourists, Istanbul "Friends of Çatalhöyük," carpet
dealers, and local villagers all engage it differently. Hodder concludes there is "no solution to
the paradoxes" and that even "making sense of it all … is itself a construct," modelling a
reflexive, multivocal heritage practice.

## Applications

Heritage anchors debates over [[repatriation-of-anthropology|repatriation]], the ethics of
[[indigenous-archaeology|community-engaged research]] (the New York African Burial Ground),
[[nationalism-and-archaeology|nationalist mobilizations]] of the past, and [[social-memory|public
memory]]. Blakey's African Burial Ground project models a "multiple clientage" ethic distinguishing
the **ethical client** (descendant community) from the **business client** (the funding agency).

## Critiques and Limitations

Heritage universalism ("belonging to all humanity") can mask appropriation and dispossession; its
tourism economy can dispossess living communities and invite violence; and its charters, though
inclusive on paper, are enforced through state and transnational (UNESCO, World Bank, ICOMOS) power.
The concept is inseparable from who holds the power to designate — a standing reflexive concern of
the [[archaeological-pragmatism|new pragmatism]].

### Shanks & Tilley 1987 — museum/heritage critique (*Re-Constructing Archaeology*)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987]]*, Ch. 4.)* Interpretive studies of 1980s British museums treat **heritage** presentation (e.g. Beamish Open Air Museum) as visiting a **mythical past**; related modes include commodified chronometric display, aesthetic high culture (*Homo Artifex*), narrative-as-information, period-room simulacrum, shop-front commodification, and Jorvik-style hero-archaeologist spectacle. Proposed alternative: [[redemptive-aesthetic]] (heterogeneity, democratized authorship, open construction, montage/irony, non-permanent and community use). Single-volume interpretive cases, not multi-site findings.
