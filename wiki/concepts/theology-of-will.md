# Theology of Will

## Definition as Coined

[[assmann-jan|Jan Assmann]]'s term for the New Kingdom (especially Ramesside) religious doctrine that historical events — battle outcomes, careers, fortunes — are the direct expression of divine volition: the god is author of "everything that occurs." It displaces the Middle Kingdom's [[connective-justice|connective justice]], in which outcomes followed from social reciprocity; [[maat|ma'at]] is reinterpreted as something the god "gives to whom he pleases" (Amenemope) rather than a principle realized through solidarity ([[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|Assmann 1996]]).

## Semantic History

Assmann pairs the concept with **personal piety** ("giving god into one's heart") — the post-Amarna current in which individuals place themselves under direct divine guidance, distinct in his analysis from local cults, domestic religiosity, and popular religion. The trajectory he draws: Ramesside personal piety and "holy war" theology (Qadesh read as Amun's intervention) → the Twentieth/Twenty-first Dynasty Theban "god-state," where Amun ruled directly through oracular proclamation — the theology of will institutionalized as direct theocracy under Herihor (all Assmann's argument).

## Operationalizations

Readings of the Qadesh Poem and Bulletin, Ramesside miracle and votive stelae, the Instruction of Amenemope, and Twenty-first Dynasty oracular decrees.

## Applications

Assmann's typology of theocracy (direct vs. representative), his account of the Third Intermediate Period, and his explanation for why oracles — which he dates as an institution only to the New Kingdom, Egypt being "not a fundamentally divinatory culture" — proliferated in this era.

## Critiques and Limitations

Positions recorded: Assmann himself notes a structural tension between a radically inscrutable divine will and Egyptian magic's assumption of a knowable, influenceable order; critics of the broader schema (connective justice → theology of will) note it is a narrative sequence built from elite textual genres whose survival is uneven across periods.
