# Erasure (semiotic)

In Irvine and Gal’s (2000) language-ideology toolkit, **erasure** is the process by which ideology renders some persons, activities, or sociolinguistic phenomena **invisible** or unremarkable when they do not fit the ideological scheme. It is not physical deletion by default but cognitive/representational omission — what the scheme cannot see. Practical eradication becomes more likely when the awkward element fits an “alternative, threatening picture.”

## Definition as Coined

[[irvine-judith-t|Irvine]] and [[gal-susan|Gal]] ([[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000|2000]]):

> Erasure is the process in which ideology, in simplifying the sociolinguistic field, renders some persons or activities (or sociolinguistic phenomena) invisible. Facts that are inconsistent with the ideological scheme either go unnoticed or get explained away. So, for example, a social group or a language may be imagined as homogeneous, its internal variation disregarded. Because a linguistic ideology is a totalizing vision, elements that do not fit its interpretive structure—that cannot be seen to fit—must be either ignored or transformed.

Semiotic erasure need not mean practical eradication of the awkward element. When the “problematic” element is seen as fitting some alternative, threatening picture, “the semiotic process involved in erasure might translate into some kind of practical action to remove the threat, if circumstances permit.”

## Semantic History

Third member of the iconization–fractal-recursivity–erasure triad (see [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]]). Distinct from everyday “erasure,” Derridean *sous rature*, or archival destruction; slug `erasure-semiotic` keeps the language-ideology technical sense vault-unique. Related uses appear in Kroskrity’s Arizona Tewa chapter (*Regimes*, ch. 9) on what prior ethnography and local ideology omit.

## Operationalizations

Ask what variation, multilingual practice, contact history, or register a national, colonial, folk, or scholarly language ideology must ignore to remain coherent — then document that omission in texts, maps, grammars, censuses, or school policy. Distinguish (a) representational omission from (b) active policy of elimination.

## Applications (Irvine & Gal 2000 cases)

| Case | Erasure (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Nguni *hlonipha*** | Ideology of clicks as pure foreignness and of sharp Nguni/Khoi domination **erases** historically attested complexity: Khoi multilingualism, trade and ritual specialists, intermarriage, Nguni refugees in Khoi society. Later European writers, after precolonial kingdoms declined, described *hlonipha* as “women’s speech,” erasing its political dimension and male use (king/chief name avoidance; whole-tribe respect). Folk ideology centers the daughter-in-law/father-in-law dyad, understating the actual wider distribution. |
| **Senegal** | Maps and grammars erase multilingual political life (Wolof as language of state in Sereer-speaking Siin/Saluum); “mixed” varieties (e.g. Baol Sereer–Wolof) ignored; Cangin varieties left unstudied once labeled dialect of Sereer; Fula’s genetic relation to Wolof/Sereer denied for generations on racial grounds; Lamoise’s Sereer grammar strips Wolof loans, aristocratic political discourse, and non-mission prayer as “error.” French overthrow of “Wolofized” administration is practical erasure paired with representational schemes. |
| **Macedonia** | Western observers erase Ottoman **millet** logics (religion/administration as primary identity; multilingual repertoires without monoglot “total” categories). Linguistic maps show monochrome single-language territories, no multilingualism. Interwar Greek Macedonia: Slavic speech denied, censuses ask only Greek, names changed, speakers jailed; 1950s “language oaths” never to speak Slavic again (Karakasidou 1993) — semiotic erasure become practical policy. Within philology, inconvenient features explained away (Bulgarian: phonology as “new developments”; Serbian: morphology ignored; Greek: grammar ignored for lexicon). |

## Critiques and Limitations

Easy to overclaim (analyst’s “erasure” may be a different research focus). Requires positive evidence of the scheme *and* of the omitted phenomena, not only absence from a single text. Distinguishing representational from practical erasure matters for ethical and political analysis.
