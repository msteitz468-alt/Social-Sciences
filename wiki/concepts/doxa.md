# Doxa

*(Attributed — Bourdieu.)* That which goes without saying: the universe of the undiscussed, where objective structures and [[habitus]] are so well adjusted that the social world appears as natural. Distinct from orthodoxy/heterodoxy (the universe of discourse and contested opinion). **Coined in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]* (1972)** (see Original Formulation below) and reworked in *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]*, where doxa is tightly paired with [[practical-sense]], [[hexis]], field entry, and collective [[misrecognition]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, ch. 4.)* Practical belief is not a "state of mind" nor arbitrary adherence to instituted dogmas, but a state of the body. Doxa is the relationship of immediate adherence established in practice between a habitus and the [[social-field|field]] to which it is attuned — the pre-verbal taking-for-granted of the world that flows from practical sense.

> "Practical belief is not a 'state of mind', still less a kind of arbitrary adherence to a set of instituted dogmas and doctrines ('beliefs'), but rather a state of the body. Doxa is the relationship of immediate adherence that is established in practice between a habitus and the field to which it is attuned, the pre-verbal taking-for-granted of the world that flows from practical sense."

Practical faith, Bourdieu argued, is the condition of entry every field tacitly imposes — selection and shaping of entrants (rites, examinations) to obtain undisputed, pre-reflexive, naive, native compliance with the field's fundamental presuppositions: "the very definition of doxa." Crisis and explicitization convert doxa into orthodoxy vs heterodoxy (the universe of opinion).

## Original Formulation (*[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]*, 1972)

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, *Outline*, ch. 4 "Structures, habitus, power: basis for a theory of symbolic power".)* The concept is **coined here**, before *Logic* reworks it as "a state of the body." In *Outline* doxa names the limiting experience of a **quasi-perfect fit** between objective and internalized structures, in which the social world appears self-evident and beyond question:

> "in the extreme case, that is to say, when there is a quasi-perfect correspondence between the objective order and the subjective principles of organization … the natural and social world appears as self-evident. This experience we shall call **doxa**, so as to distinguish it from an orthodox or heterodox belief implying awareness and recognition of the possibility of different or antagonistic beliefs."

The wiki's signature gloss originates in this text: **"what is essential goes without saying because it comes without saying."** Bourdieu opposes the field of *doxa* (the undiscussed, "the sum total of the theses tacitly posited on the hither side of all inquiry") to the **field of opinion**, the universe of competing discourse within which *orthodoxy* and *heterodoxy* are defined:

> "**Orthodoxy**, straight, or rather straightened, opinion, which aims, without ever entirely succeeding, at restoring the primal state of innocence of doxa, exists only in the objective relationship which opposes it to **heterodoxy**, that is, by reference to the choice — *hairesis*, heresy — made possible by the existence of competing possibles."

Two claims here are load-bearing and predate *Logic*: (1) **doxa has political stakes** — "the dominated classes have an interest in pushing back the limits of doxa and exposing the arbitrariness of the taken for granted; the dominant classes have an interest in defending the integrity of doxa or, short of this, of establishing in its place the necessarily imperfect substitute, orthodoxy"; (2) the limit between doxa and discourse is itself **an object of class struggle** ("the struggle for the imposition of the dominant systems of classification"). *Outline* also states the founding equation of a theory of symbolic power: "The theory of knowledge is a dimension of political theory because the specifically symbolic power to impose the principles of the construction of reality … is a major dimension of political power." Crisis (culture contact, class division) — not phenomenological *epochē* — is what practically breaks self-evidence and "brings the undiscussed into discussion."

## Semantic History

- **Greek *doxa*.** Opinion/belief; Bourdieu retools the term for the undiscussed, not for mere "opinion" as opposed to *epistēmē*.
- **Origin in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline]]* (1972), not *Logic*.** The *Logic* "state of the body" formulation (below) is a later re-casting; the original coinage (above) frames doxa through the objective/subjective correspondence and its political stakes.
- **Phenomenology's natural attitude.** *Logic* Book I introduction credits phenomenological knowledge with reflecting unreflexive familiarity, but faults it for excluding conditions of possibility — coincidence of objective and internalized structures that produces the illusion of immediate understanding.
- **Structuralist shared cipher.** Immediate understanding requires objective attunement to the same signs; that coincidence defines the scope of phenomenological validity while remaining invisible to phenomenology itself.
- **Instruments of thought.** The unthinkable at a given time includes not only ethical/political barriers but lack of problematics, concepts, methods — "why good intentions so often make bad sociology" (Preface).
- **Collective belief / social magic (Book II).** Vision of the world as division of the world; the magical limit (*ex instituto*) produces difference by arbitrary institution and produces collective belief as ignorance of its own arbitrariness; "the magical frontier is … everywhere, in things and in bodies."

## Operationalizations

- **What cannot be questioned.** Ethnographic markers of "that's just how it is"; harmony of practical sense and objectified *sens* producing a common-sense world with consensus objectivity (festivals, commonplaces, sayings).
- **J-curve / low dispersion.** Doxic relation most visible when group practices show little dispersion and each member helps impose on others the same constraint they impose on him (ch. 6); breaks often take sudden collective form when circular control fails (e.g. urbanization collapsing the fiction of the religion of honour among [[kabyle]] as Bourdieu analyzed transition).
- **Official vs unofficial knowledge.** Same generative schemes can produce official (public, often Islam-inflected, ethnography-collectible) and clandestine knowledge under different logics — doxa is thicker around officialized order.
- **Spatial naturalization.** Meanings objectified in house and landscape yield fully only through practices structured by the same schemes — doxic self-evidence of guest seating, loom, "keeping to the wall" (Appendix).
- **Cultural imperative.** Culturalization/denaturalization of biological and climatic necessities (ritual time vs climate; clothing as status independent of weather).

## Applications

- **[[kabyle]] classifications.** Sexual, agrarian, and house cosmologies as doxic order; ritual officialization of transgression as most powerful denial (harvest as murder turned sacrifice).
- **Field *doxai*.** Practical faith as entry condition; belief as inherent part of belonging — cannot be chosen by instantaneous will (vs Kantian pragmatic faith).
- **Kinship categories as performatives.** Terms of address as public imputations instituting frontiers ("She's your sister") with magical power of the group they help make (Book II).
- **Scientific/academic doxa.** Legitimate problems and methods in the scientific field; intellectual *skhole* as theodicy of privilege (Book I introduction).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Observer access.** How the sociologist knows the boundary of the undiscussed without imposing a scholastic grid is a reflexivity problem Bourdieu addresses via [[practice-theory]] but does not close.
- **Consent and over-reading.** Doxa can be read as total consent; Bourdieu's crisis, hysteresis, and orthodoxy/heterodoxy leave room for challenge, yet critics charge functionalist over-integration.
- **Relation to ideology.** Doxa is pre-discursive; ideology critique often works on explicit representations — mapping between them is contested.
- **Collective "bad faith."** Formulations of groups lying to themselves (twofold objective truth of ritual) risk sounding prosecutorial if not kept in attributed voice as theoretical construction.
