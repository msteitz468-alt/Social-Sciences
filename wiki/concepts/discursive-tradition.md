# Discursive Tradition

[[asad-talal|Talal Asad]]'s concept for analyzing Islam (and by extension any tradition) without reducing it either to a fixed essence or to a mere label for whatever Muslims happen to do: a tradition consists of discourses that instruct practitioners in the correct form and purpose of practices, relating an authorizing past and an aimed-at future to present conditions. Coined in "The Idea of an Anthropology of Islam" (1986) and deployed at length in *Genealogies of Religion* (1993), especially ch. 6 on Saudi public argument.

## Definition as Coined

Asad's key formulation in *Genealogies*: "Muslims in Saudi Arabia (as elsewhere) disagree profoundly over what orthodox Islam is, but as Muslims their differences are fought out on the ground of that concept." Orthodoxy is not a settled doctrine but a *relationship* — the ongoing, contested work of defining correct practice; in Asad's words, "like all practical criticism, orthodox criticism seeks to construct a relation of discursive dominance." Determining orthodoxy amid changing conditions involves constructing discursive coherence and an authoritative narrative, achieved collaboratively between speaker and audience.

## Semantic History

The concept was Asad's answer to two rejected alternatives: Orientalist essentialism (Islam as a fixed civilizational text — see [[orientalism]]) and the "fundamentalism" frame, which Asad calls "a product of lazy thinking... convenient to many policy makers... in Western governments." It also displaces the "manipulation of symbols" and "negotiation" idioms of Western social science, which Asad argues import "a deliberative, rationalistic stance into descriptions of relationships where that notion is not appropriate."

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. In practice the concept is operationalized by identifying (a) the authoritative texts and pedagogies a community argues *from* (Qur'an, hadith, jurisprudential classifications), (b) the institutional sites of instruction and correction (sermons, *nasiha*, the *mutawi'a*), and (c) the live arguments over correct practice through which orthodoxy is constructed — rather than by measuring belief-statements, which the concept was designed to displace.

## Applications

Asad's exemplary application is the Saudi tradition of *nasiha* (morally corrective advice), analyzed through the sermons of the *khatib* Al Za'ayr and the May 1991 open letter to King Fahd signed by several hundred 'ulama. Attributed findings:

- *Nasiha* is grounded in the hadith "Religion is integrity" (*ad-din an-nasihatu*) and is obligatory bidirectionally — the ruler owes just institutions; every Muslim owes moral watchfulness, first over himself, then others.
- Its procedure is internally disciplined (verify the transgression, consult 'ulama, avoid provocation and haste, do not judge by immediate results) and requires knowledge and gentleness — "it is violence (*unf*) and not emotion that is disapproved."
- The Islamic legal-moral scheme (*wajib*/*mandub*/*mubah*/*makruh*/*haram*) "forces specific questions onto people who belong to that tradition" as new practices appear — the tradition's mode of absorbing change.
- Contemporary Islamic critical discourse is "a part of modernity and not a reaction to it": its expanded scope presupposes modern institutions and Western-educated classes. In the 1991 episode, Asad argues the monarchy's real danger lay not in losing the argument but "in his conceding a domain of public argument in which he becomes accountable."
- For Za'ayr, *din* "relates more to how one lives than to what one believes" — virtues (mastery of prayer, self-command, sound judgment), not states of mind — supporting Asad's broader case against belief-centered definitions of [[religion]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept has been criticized (in the anthropology of Islam after 1986) for privileging orthodoxy and textual authority over lived, ambivalent, or indifferent religiosity — a debate that postdates and lies outside this source. Within *Genealogies*, the concept's load-bearing contrast is with Kant's public/private uses of reason: Asad argues Middle Eastern polities lack (and do not aspire to) the Kantian confinement of religious criticism to the private domain, and that this difference marks distinct traditions of practical reason, not a deficit of rationality — "the ruthlessness of secular practice yields nothing to the ferocity of religious" (his closing point against equating religion with coercion, citing Nazism and Stalinism as secular coercion).
