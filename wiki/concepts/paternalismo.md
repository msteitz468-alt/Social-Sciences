# Paternalismo (patron-client dependency)

## Definition as Coined

*Paternalismo* is the Brazilian (and broadly Iberian) idiom for the hierarchical,
personalistic relation binding a dependent (client, worker, *morador*) to a superior
(*patrão*, *dono*, *senhor de engenho*, benefactor) through ongoing asymmetric exchange.
In [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s analysis of the [[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do
Cruzeiro]] and the sugar town of "Bom Jesus da Mata" (*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death
Without Weeping]]*, 1992), it is the "ethic of the *casa grande*, of the big house and
the sugar barons," historically rooted in the plantation and slavery and persisting into
the late-twentieth-century Nordeste. Scheper-Hughes argues (attributed) that the Alto poor
live within a **dual ethic**: an egalitarian, collectivist ethic of open, balanced
[[reciprocity]] among equals (the "ethic of the *mata*"), and — in tense dialectic with it
— the hierarchical, dyadic ethic of *paternalismo* toward patrons, which "locks foresters
into servility, dependency, [and] loyalty to those who exploit them" and "contains the
seeds of class betrayal."

## Semantic History

The term migrates from a vernacular description of "good bossism" into an analytic concept
for a mode of domination. Scheper-Hughes situates it in a lineage of Brazilian and
Iberian-American scholarship on rural "dyadic contracts," *coronelismo* (rule by local
strongman "colonels"), and peasant deference. Where an older functionalist literature
could read patron-client ties as adaptive risk-buffering, her (attributed) reading —
drawing on Bourdieu's "bad faith economy" (1977) — recasts them as domination misrecognized
as care: "desperation can be called loyalty and exploitation can masquerade as care and
nurturance." The Brazilian Peasant League leader Francisco Julião's image of the patron's
"cunning" (entering "as tame as a lamb… with claws concealed") supplies the concept's
radical-political edge.

## Operationalizations

On the Alto, a *patrão* is judged "good" or "bad" not by wages — which remained a pittance
(Black Irene earned roughly US$1/week for washing and ironing after some forty years'
service, as of 1987) — but by the flow of **gifts over time**: meat and produce, used
clothing, payment of medical bills and registration fees, and contributions to marriages,
baptisms, schooling, and **burials**. A mother could call a boss "good" straightforwardly
because "he paid for the *mortalha* [funeral supplies] for my [deceased] children." The
relation is thus "a kind of bartering for existence" in which the patron is morally
obligated to rescue the client from starvation, sickness, or prison, while structural
wages and rights are withheld.

## Applications

- **The "good boss / bad boss" morality tale.** Scheper-Hughes reads recurrent
  good-boss/bad-boss narratives (Lordes's exploitative Dona Rita and Dona Fátima vs. her
  generous Dona Júlia) as an Alto morality tale that individualizes the *mau patrão* as a
  scapegoat — obscuring, she argues, that the bad boss is "the true flowering of the logic
  and violence of *paternalismo*," not an aberration.
- **The paradox of the good boss.** Her central (attributed) claim inverts common sense:
  it is the *good* boss (a Dickensian Fezziwig against Scrooge) who most threatens the
  dependent's autonomy, because "the 'good boss' in a 'bad faith' economy rescues not only
  the exploited worker but also the exploitative and colonizing social system itself."
- **The addiction of dependency.** Invoking a parable from the Italian psychiatrist Franco
  Basaglia and Albert Memmi's account of dependency as "hypnosis," she frames *casa*, *rua*,
  and *mata* as "a dynamic tension that locks the social classes into a ruse… in which
  exploitation parades as benevolence." People "give up on individual bad bosses" but never
  on the *idea* of patronage — because to do so would be to admit there is no safety net at
  all.
- **The Church as patron.** Folk-Catholic charity (the German *Madre* of the *colégio*
  holding "open court" with food baskets and medications) is analyzed as continuous with,
  not an alternative to, the paternalist ethic.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is **contested** in register rather than existence: the *description* of
patron-client exchange is widely shared, but Scheper-Hughes's *interpretation* — patronage
as misrecognized domination and self-colonizing dependency — is an attributed,
politically-committed reading (part of her [[militant-anthropology|militant anthropology]])
that a functionalist or rational-choice account of clientelism as insurance would resist.
As an emic term elevated to etic analysis, *paternalismo* also risks flattening the
genuine ambivalence Scheper-Hughes documents, in which the same relation is experienced as
both exploitation and care. Compare [[reciprocity]] (the egalitarian counter-ethic she sets
against it) and [[symbolic-violence]] (domination that operates through misrecognition).
