# Everyday Forms of Resistance

> Primary formulation from [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* (1985). All mechanism-and-meaning claims in **attributed voice**. Study: [[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]]; hub: [[hubs/studies/scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]].

## Definition as Coined

Scott argued that the literature on peasantries had over-weighted organized rebellions and revolutions — rare, often crushed, and when successful often producing a stronger extractive state — while under-studying the **constant, prosaic struggle** of subordinates against those who extract labor, food, taxes, rents, and interest. He named this domain **everyday forms of peasant resistance** (title metaphor: [[weapons-of-the-weak]]):

> “the prosaic but constant struggle between the peasantry and those who seek to extract labor, food, taxes, rents, and interest from them. Most forms of this struggle stop well short of outright collective defiance.”

**Class resistance** (Ch. 7 first approximation): any act(s) by member(s) of a subordinate class **intended** either to mitigate or deny superordinate claims (rents, taxes, prestige) or to advance subordinate claims (work, land, charity, respect). Operational bounds Scott added: include self-interested acts that deny appropriators; exclude “beggar-thy-neighbor” survival that exploits fellow subordinates; infer intention from systematic patterns when speech is mute; goals are usually Hobsbawm’s “working the system… to their minimum disadvantage,” not revolution.

Shared features of the Brechtian/Schweikian forms: little formal coordination; implicit understandings and informal networks; often individual self-help; typically **no direct symbolic confrontation** with authority.

## Semantic History

- Pre-1985: rebellion-centric peasant studies; Bloch’s “patient, silent struggles”; Hobsbawm on peasant politics; Thompsonian moral economy; slavery studies’ everyday conflict over work and autonomy (as Scott mobilizes them).
- **1985 *Weapons of the Weak*:** concept locked to Sedaka class struggle under Green Revolution / Muda irrigation and given an explicit definitional chapter.
- Post-1985: portable research program across anthropology, history, sociology, development studies; also critiqued as definitional inflation. *[[scott-domination-arts-resistance-1990|Domination and the Arts of Resistance]]* (1990, vault-primary) generalizes the material as [[public-transcript]] / [[hidden-transcript]] and subsumes everyday resistance under the broader category of [[infrapolitics]] — the material-domination column of Scott's three-domain typology.

## Operationalizations

In [[sedaka|Sedaka]] (fieldwork 1978–80; change frame 1967–79): anti-combine sabotage and boycotts; field piece-rate struggles (“trade unionism without trade unions”); tenant mutuality; paddy theft as contested right; livestock killing; gossip and nicknames; ritual and charity claims; party affiliation as benefit-seeking under repression. Not a survey index — thick description plus local institutional records.

**Coral-reef metaphor:** multiplied petty acts aggregate into political-economic barriers; historians notice the shipwreck, not the reef.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Inflation:** if intention is ethnographer-inferred, almost any noncompliance can be recruited.
- **Open politics demoted:** parties and episodic revolt appear in Sedaka but the framing privileges the infra-political.
- **Case limits:** one Muda Malay village is not global peasantry.
- **Gender:** women act (transplanting boycotts) without gender as co-equal structure.
