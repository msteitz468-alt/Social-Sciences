# Moral individualism (cult of the individual)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] identified one growth-point within the general decline of the [[collective-conscience]]: sentiments attaching to the **individual person** intensify, giving rise to the **"cult of the individual"** — a genuinely *collective*, society-wide moral fact whose *object* is nonetheless the individual. As concrete shared beliefs and customs thin out with social differentiation, what remains universally common is an abstract, generalized **respect for human personality and dignity** — "in ourselves as in those like us" (invoking the Kantian formula).

Crucially (attributed), this is **not egoism**: it binds individuals to society precisely by requiring recognition of others' (and one's own) dignity. Durkheim insisted personality itself *develops with* the division of labor, not against it — the individual becomes an "autonomous source of action" through partial emancipation from hereditary and collective environments, itself a product of social complexity. He denied that personality was fuller in segmental societies, where apparent variety was really impersonal: "it is society, it is the race acting in and through him... His liberty is only apparent and his personality borrowed."

## Semantic History

- **Distinct from [[individualism]]** in the Tocquevillean/utilitarian sense (atomizing withdrawal, self-interest). Durkheim's moral individualism is a *social* sentiment, the emergent common morality of organically solidary societies — not the dissolution of society but its modern moral bond.
- Durkheim develops the theme across his corpus (notably "Individualism and the Intellectuals," 1898, written amid the Dreyfus Affair — pending ingest). This page seeds from *Division of Labor* (1893).

## Operationalizations

Indirect: the historical thinning of shared concrete beliefs (declining penal law, contracting religion, disappearing proverbs — see [[collective-conscience]]) alongside the *intensification* of sentiments protecting the person; the spread of human-rights and dignity norms as the "rallying-point of so many people."

## Applications

- The residual content of the modern [[collective-conscience]] and the moral counterpart of [[organic-solidarity]].
- Grounds Durkheim's cosmopolitan argument that "the ideal of human fraternity can be realized only in proportion to the progress of the division of labor" — larger, more differentiated societies (an incipient "European society") extend the same logic from persons to nations.
- The normative basis of his [[forced-division-of-labor|critique of inherited inequality]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Whether a "cult of the individual" can supply sufficient social cohesion is Durkheim's own open question — he judged organic-solidarity morality "not yet" adequately developed (attributed).
- The claim that dignity-respect is a *collective* rather than individual value is contested by liberal-individualist readings.
- Reads partly as normative hope (a diagnosis of what *should* bind modern society), not settled description — keep in attributed voice.
