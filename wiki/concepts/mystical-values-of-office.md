# Mystical Values of Political Office

> Fortes & Evans-Pritchard, Introduction to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940). Related to, but not identical with, Frazerian [[divine-king|divine kingship]].

## Definition as Coined

Fortes and Evans-Pritchard argued that an African ruler is not merely someone who can enforce will on subjects. The ruler is (attributed) “the axis of their political relations, the symbol of their unity and exclusiveness, and the embodiment of their essential values.” Credentials are **mystical**, from antiquity; Europeans, they held, can impose force and replace many secular functions but “have no mythical or ritual warranty for their authority” and cannot enter these sacred precincts.

They stressed that **mystical values** attach to political office across cultural idioms: among the [[bemba]], [[banyankole]], [[kede]], and [[tallensi]], the symbols differ while the **function** — binding the political community — is comparable on an abstract plane.

## Semantic History

- **1940 APS:** Canonical British SF statement linking political integration to ritual-symbolic legitimation rather than interest alone.
- **Relation to Frazer:** Frazer’s [[divine-king]] centers ritual killing/renewal of embodied cosmic life. APS “mystical values” is broader: sacred warranty of office, Earth/ancestral cults, drums, leopard-skin mediators, without requiring Frazer’s regicide thesis.
- **Later political anthropology:** The theme survives in discussions of sacred kingship, ritual of rebellion (Gluckman), and symbolic dimensions of the state; the exact APS phrasing is period-specific.

## Operationalizations

Indicators in APS and case essays (attributed):

- Regalia, taboos, and succession ritual fencing the king/chief’s body and office
- Public ceremonies dramatizing fertility, health, prosperity, peace, justice
- Earth cults and earth-linked mediators (Tallensi *tindaana*; Nuer leopard-skin chief)
- Royal drums and cult objects (e.g. Ankole Bagyendanwa)
- Refusal of ritual service as a check on rulers (e.g. Bemba *bakabilo*)

## Applications

- **Group A:** Kingship as focus of whole-community moral/legal norms; ritual functions may outlast colonial stripping of secular power.
- **Group B:** No single person represents political unity of “the people”; mystical values are **distributed** at cardinal points of structure — myths, dogmas, ritual powers of segments and of sacred mediators regulating inter-segment relations.
- Colonial contrast: European force lies *outside* the native system’s inherent values; cannot create the same moral ties.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Functional circularity risk:** Explaining solidarity by symbols that “stand for” solidarity needs independent evidence of mechanism (later critics of SF).
- **Colonial production:** Some ritual offices were remade under Indirect Rule; APS partly documents transformation as well as “tradition.”
- **Not a universal African essence:** Editors’ own comparative method requires stripping cultural idiom; the concept is an analytic claim about function, not a folk ontology.
