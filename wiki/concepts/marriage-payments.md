# Marriage Payments (bridewealth / lobola)

> [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]], Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950); case detail throughout the volume (Gluckman, Fortes, Kuper, Schapera, Wilson, Forde, Nadel, Evans-Pritchard).

## Definition as Coined

Radcliffe-Brown rejected treating African marriage payments as simple “wife purchase.” He defined terms for analysis:

- **Marriage payment** — the major payment or payments made by the bridegroom to the wife’s kin.
- **Counter-payment** — payment from the wife’s kin to the husband where it occurs (e.g. Nkundo *nkomi*).

He argued that African marriage typically involves a **series of prestations** both ways (payments, gifts, services). Without state registration, “legal” marriage — giving children definite legitimate status — is created by transactions involving both kin bodies; “in most African marriages a payment of goods or services by the bridegroom to the bride’s kin is an essential part of the establishment of ‘legality’.”

Central theoretical framing (attributed): the payment is the **objective instrument of a legal transfer of rights** over the woman (Old English *mund* analogy; Roman *manus*/*coemptio* foil), not a commercial sale. Her kin retain residual rights to protect her against ill-treatment. Additional aspects he listed: indemnity/compensation for loss of a daughter (often recycled to obtain a wife for a brother); gift-exchange establishing alliance (citing Mauss); continuing obligations and possible repayment unlike completed purchase.

## Semantic History

| Layer | Label / usage |
|---|---|
| Colonial courts | *lobola* as “wife-purchase,” sometimes “immoral”/invalid (R-B cites South African official doctrine; Kenya Supreme Court 1917: “so-called marriage by the native custom of wife-purchase is not a marriage”) — attributed by R-B to ignorance or “blind prejudice” |
| Emic regional terms | *lobola* / *ukulobola* (Nguni); *bogadi* (Tswana); *tiri nsa* / *tiri sika* (Ashanti); Yakö marriage money; Nyamwezi *nsabo* (*kukwa* form) |
| Structural-functional anthropology | **Bridewealth** / marriage payment validating marriage, transferring rights, affiliating children |
| Later critique | Feminist and legal anthropology on commodification, women’s agency, cash inflation, and whether “payment” language still smuggles purchase models |

Victorian evolutionary literature often folded bride-price into stages of marriage evolution; ASKAM treats payments as jural instruments within living systems, not stage markers.

## Operationalizations

- **Legality marker:** presence and completion of prescribed payments/formalities distinguish legal marriage from irregular unions.
- **Child affiliation:** size/form of payment can determine whether children go to father’s or mother’s kin (Brass, Southern Nigeria; Nyamwezi *kukwa* vs *butende*; Nguni “Cattle beget children” / “The children are where the cattle are not”).
- **Pater vs genitor:** among Zulu (Gluckman), cattle transfer rights to *all* children of the wives to husband and agnates “whoever are their genitors”; illegitimate child belongs to mother’s agnatic lineage unless redeemed by genitor’s cattle or subsequent marriage. Lozi contrast: father in law is the **genitor**; no corporate unilineal transfer via cattle.
- **Divorce returnability:** many societies require return of marriage payment and counter-payment, with qualifications (e.g. children remaining with father → non-returnable or partial). Bride-service is non-returnable.
- **Payment chains:** cattle/goods from A’s marriage of sister fund B’s marriage — create linked sibling and cross-cousin structures (Lovedu systematizes cross-cousin marriage via cattle chains; Shangana-Tonga claims; Nkundo asymmetrical generations).
- **Cattle-milk complex** (East/South Africa): Wilson on Nyakyusa; Transkei — drinking milk from another lineage’s cattle may bar later marriage; marriage completed when wife first drinks husband’s herd’s milk.
- **Ashanti *tiri nsa*:** Fortes — girl’s mother’s brother (*wofa*) must accept *nsa* and hand it to lineage head for legal marriage; divorce requires MB acquiescence and return of *nsa*.

## Applications

ASKAM cases (attributed findings/reports):

- **Nguni father-right:** marriage payment determines possession of children; infancy still with mother (e.g. *ubulunga* cow necklace, milk of her lineage cattle); full father’s-lineage incorporation later.
- **Zulu vs Lozi (Gluckman):** Zulu cattle → absolute lineage membership of children; Lozi lack corporate lineage and cattle-transfer logic of Southern Bantu; father and mother’s brother share marriage payment of girls equally among Lozi.
- **Ashanti (Fortes):** *tiri nsa*/*tiri sika* as legalization within matrilineage authority structure; not cattle bridewealth.
- **Yakö (Forde):** marriage money; repayment on divorce; interaction with claims of physiological paternity vs marriage-derived social fatherhood.
- **Nuba (Nadel):** bride-price usually paid by father; optional help from mother’s brother; modified levirate recommendations.
- **Nuer (Evans-Pritchard chapter / 1951 monograph):** bridewealth builds local clusters; ghost-wife and levirate linked to cattle and pater rules (see [[levirate]], [[ghost-marriage]]). Primary densification: *[[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-1951|Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer]]* — ideal historically ~**40** cattle, usual ~**20–30**, floor ~**16**; “children of the cattle”; pater = name in which cattle paid ([[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]]).
- **Tswana *bogadi*, Swazi *ukulobola*, Nyakyusa cattle payments** (Schapera, Kuper, Wilson chapters as used in Intro and volume).

R-B’s summary functions of African marriage payment: legal instrument; sometimes indemnity; sometimes alliance gift-exchange; in South Africa and Nilotic region, derivation of cattle may fix children’s social position; successive use of same cattle links families; sacred cattle link living to ancestors so payments carry special significance.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Size and form vary enormously; “payment” is not universal even in Africa (service, multi-stage gifts, small tokens).
- Colonial cash economies transformed amounts, timing, and conflict patterns after the ASKAM fieldwork moment — date ethnographic claims.
- Purchase-language residual: even anthropological “bridewealth” can reify economic reduction; R-B’s multi-aspect reading still privileges jural transfer.
- Women’s agency, consent, and post-marital bargaining under-documented in structural accounts focused on kin-group alliance.
- Dual marriage forms (full vs small/no payment → father- vs mother-right children) show payment is a variable within a society, not a single-society constant.

## From Leach, *Rethinking Anthropology* (1961)

*(Attributed — Leach.)*

- **Ch. 3 rank propositions.** Direction and content of payments index relative status of wife-giving and wife-receiving [[local-descent-group|local descent groups]]: costly **consumer-goods brideprice** associates with senior givers; **dowry** with junior givers; symbolic-only transfers leave hierarchy freer. Kachin *hpaga*-type wealth objects sit in this comparative frame with Batak and (contrast) Lovedu.
- **Ch. 5 Kachin–Lakher contrast.** Ordinary Jinghpaw: expensive but less elaborate bridewealth; marriage near-indissoluble after religious rite; children absolutely of husband’s lineage. [[lakher|Lakher]]: very expensive and complicated (*angkia* main payment; *puma* to wife’s MB’s lineage; *lokheu* further transfer); divorce easy/frequent; widows freer — **hire of procreative powers** vs Jinghpaw absolute person-transfer. Same descent form; different payment/stability regime. Challenges Gluckman-style scales that read divorce rates as simple “Father Right” strength.
