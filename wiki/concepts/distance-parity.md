# Distance-Parity Model

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]], *Rethinking World-Systems*, 1999.)*

Stein's alternative to the [[world-systems-analysis|world-system model]] for ancient
interregional interaction. Its load-bearing statement: "This distance-parity model
suggests that the core's ability to exercise hegemonic power decays with distance, thereby
leading to increasing parity or symmetry in economic and political relations with
increasingly distant peripheries. Under these conditions, core-periphery interaction is
expected to be limited in scale and influence such that the peripheral political economies
do not necessarily develop the specialized dependency relations predicted by the
world-system model." Where [[world-systems-analysis|Wallersteinian]] models take core
dominance as their premise, the distance-parity model treats the degree of asymmetry as a
**variable** governed by transport cost — making symmetric, autonomous relations the
*expected* outcome in far peripheries rather than an anomaly.

## Semantic History

The term is [[stein-gil|Stein's]] own 1999 coinage and has not drifted far from its
original definition, but it stands on older ideas it recombines: Paul Bairoch's (1988)
"tyranny of distance" (which Stein borrows as the phrase for his core mechanism) and Philip
Kohl's (1987) "technological parity" thesis for the ancient Old World (that ancient cores and
peripheries shared material technology, unlike the modern colonial gap). Stein's contribution
is to weld transport economics to a **testable correlate set**, turning a general observation
about distance into an archaeological prediction. The concept is deliberately positioned as a
*complement* to the [[trade-diaspora|trade-diaspora]] model — the large-scale, top-down
counterpart to that concept's site-scale, bottom-up analytic — and as a *rival* to the
[[world-systems-analysis|world-system]] model's assumption of core dominance. In later
usage Stein increasingly folds it into the broader language of interaction **"networks"**
(preferred over "systems") to avoid the deterministic overtones of world-systems vocabulary.

## The Mechanism: Transport Cost and the "Tyranny of Distance"

The primary variable is the cost of moving goods and force over distance — Paul Bairoch's
(1988) **"tyranny of distance."** Stein assembles the pre-industrial transport economics
(attributed to his cited sources): a human porter carries ~35–40 kg some 30–35 km per day
but eats ~1 kg of food per 17 km, so at ~300 km half the cargo is consumed in transit and
by ~600 km all of it is (Bairoch 1988); Drennan's (1984) figures make overland human
portage (3,214 cal/ton-km) roughly seven times costlier than downstream river transport
(449 cal/ton-km), setting a practical ceiling of ~275 km for moving staples overland.
Above a distance threshold, exchange therefore shifts from bulk goods to high
value-to-weight prestige items (shown for Mesoamerica by Drennan and Cowgill; for the Inka
by D'Altroy & Earle's staple-finance-near-capital vs. wealth-finance-in-distant-provinces
contrast), maritime transport being the standing exception that lets bulk goods travel far.
The same logic constrains **military** power: early empires (Sargon, Alexander) could
mount periodic campaigns but not permanent distant garrisons, because a soldier carried
only ~3 days' supply (Mann 1986) — hence reliance on intimidation and local allies over
occupation. Stein reads Alfred Crosby's (1986) failed medieval colonizations — the Crusader
states and Norse Vinland — as natural experiments: both failed for lack of any real
technological edge over locals *and* distance-driven decay of reinforcement (Latins never
exceeded ~20% of the Crusader-state population).

## Operationalizations: The Eight Correlates

Stein turns the model into a testable prediction set. As distance from the core increases,
the distance-parity model expects (paraphrased from his numbered list): (1) no major change
in the intensity or specialization of local agricultural, pastoral, and craft production;
(2) a local economy still oriented to generalized subsistence and locally controlled
exchange; (3) low exchange volume, dominated by high value-to-bulk goods; (4) **symmetric**
exchange with no exclusive core control — both core and local administrative systems
visible over different stages of production and circulation; (5) no sudden increase in local
social complexity; (6) local incorporation of core prestige goods/ideology "on local
terms" (appropriated and transformed into local idiom, so their presence ≠ dominance —
Moorey 1987; Thomas 1991); (7) core influence, where present, restricted to peripheral
elites rather than the whole population; (8) settlement patterns showing continuity with the
pre-contact configuration, not the dendritic/primate reorganization of an extractive
economy. These are set point-for-point against the **eight world-system correlates**
(surplus reorganization for export, asymmetric core-controlled exchange, a sudden complexity
rise, dendritic settlement reorganization, and so on) — so that a single well-stratified
peripheral site with pre-contact and contact phases can adjudicate between the two.

Underlying the model is Michael Mann's (1986) definition of power ("the ability to pursue
and attain goals through mastery of one's environment") and his four sources — ideological,
economic, military, political. Stein adds that the four factors structuring any interregional
power balance (resource distribution; demography, offset by disease barriers such as West
Africa's malaria "White Man's Grave"; technology, whose *organizational* component is
culturally embedded and less transferable than tools; and sociopolitical complexity, offset
by factionalism or Flannery's "hypercoherence" vulnerability) each vary independently — so
power asymmetry cannot be read off any single one.

## Applications

The model's primary test is [[hacinebi|Hacınebi Tepe]], ~1,300 km upstream of Uruk, where
the [[stein-hacinebi-study|excavation]] found production continuity, symmetric exchange, and
no complexity jump across the contact horizon — the predicted distance-parity signature (see
[[uruk-expansion]]). Stein reads the whole Uruk network as a **gradient**: direct control in
the Susiana core, coercive influence at the large fortified Syrian colonies close to home
([[habuba-kabira|Habuba Kabira]], Tell Sheikh Hassan), and autonomous enclaves surviving
only at local sufferance at the far edge (Hacınebi, Godin V). He generalizes the model
cross-culturally to Inka provincial rule, Aztec imperial organization, central Mexico–Gulf
Coast interaction, and 17th-century European trade with the kingdom of Hueda.

## Critiques and Limitations

The model shares the falsifiability problem of its rival: local symmetry at one far-edge
site (Hacınebi) is compatible with system-wide asymmetry, as [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze's]]
"decay with distance" concession itself allows — so a distance-parity result at the
periphery does not by itself refute an aggregate world-system claim (Matthews 2003 and Kohl
2001 judged Stein and Frangipane to have "moved too far in the opposite direction"). The
model is also built substantially on ethnohistoric and modern transport figures applied to
the 4th millennium BCE by analogy — legitimate as a mechanism generator, but a bridging
assumption, not a measurement. Its unresolved contest with the world-system reading is the
substance of the [[uruk-world-system-debate]].
