# Strategic Essentialism

## Definition as Coined

**Strategic essentialism** names (in reception of [[spivak-gayatri-chakravorty|Spivak]] and related postcolonial/feminist theory) the *tactical* adoption of a simplified group identity for political action — knowing the identity is not a pure essence. It is widely attributed to Spivak; its textbook form is often more stable than Spivak’s own shifting uses.

## Semantic History

**Important for this ingest:** the 1988 text of "[[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study|Can the Subaltern Speak?]]" (appendix in [[morris-can-subaltern-speak-2010]]) does **not** make “strategic essentialism” a named master concept. Extractors found related concerns (essentialist utopian politics from conflating *vertreten*/*darstellen*; coalition politics in notes) but not the slogan as such.

What *does* appear in the Morris 2010 volume:

- **Partha Chatterjee** (attributed) notes that “strategic essentialism” is one of several tags under which Spivak’s essay was received, and that “Spivak herself would vacillate” on it; for the Subaltern Studies project in India, he argues, the deeper shift was toward a consciously poststructural method, not the slogan.
- Classroom and activist reception often **promotes** the term as Spivak’s “answer” to the crisis of representation — a simplification Spivak’s own revisions complicate.

Later Spivak interviews and secondary literature (outside this volume) document both her introduction of the idea and her later discomfort with its reification as a program.

## Operationalizations

As usually taught: (1) identity is constructed; (2) political work sometimes requires speaking *as* a group; (3) the essentialism is temporary and self-critical. Operational failure mode: the “strategy” is forgotten and the essence hardens into nativism or institutional identity politics — exactly the reduction Spivak and Birla (Morris 2010) warn postcolonial studies against.

## Applications

- Feminist and anti-colonial coalition politics (reception literature).
- Tension with Spivak’s CSS thesis that the gendered [[subaltern]] cannot be recovered as a pure speaking subject — strategic essentialism is *not* a license to claim “I am the subaltern and therefore I speak.”

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes`. Spivak’s vacillation (Chatterjee); risk of legitimating essentialism without the “strategic” qualifier; thin presence in the primary CSS text means wiki claims about the *essay* should not center this term. Prefer [[subaltern]], [[epistemic-violence]], and the *vertreten*/*darstellen* analysis for CSS-proper.
