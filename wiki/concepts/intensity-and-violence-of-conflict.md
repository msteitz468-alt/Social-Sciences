# Intensity and Violence of Conflict

## Definition as Coined

In Ch. VI of *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] distinguishes **intensity** (how much the parties are involved; energy of the conflict) from **violence** (the means employed — ranging from discussion and negotiation to physical force). The two vary partly independently: conflict can be intense yet non-violent (regulated industrial dispute) or violent yet limited in social intensity.

## Semantic History

The distinction lets conflict theory retain Marx's insight that conflict drives change while rejecting the assumption that class conflict must escalate to revolutionary violence. It became standard vocabulary for analyzing democratic class conflict.

## Operationalizations

Pluralism vs superimposition of conflict lines, mobility vs immobility, and the presence of recognized regulation procedures are proposed as structural variables affecting intensity and violence (see [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict]], [[social-mobility]]).

## Applications

Explains why post-capitalist industrial and political conflicts can remain endemic without producing Marxian revolution: institutionalization and plural conflict lines lower violence.

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement of "intensity" is qualitative in Dahrendorf; later strike and protest research operationalized related variables statistically. The normative preference for low-violence regulated conflict is a liberal political stance as well as an analytic claim.
