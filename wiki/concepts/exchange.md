# Exchange (Simmel)

## Definition as Coined

For [[simmel-georg|Simmel]], **exchange** is "a sociological phenomenon *sui generis*, an original form and function of social life" — not a derivative of utility and scarcity but their precondition. It "is not the mere addition of two processes of giving and receiving, but a new third phenomenon." Exchange is "the purest and most developed kind of interaction," and thus the exemplary form of [[sociation]]: "most relationships between people can be interpreted as forms of exchange."

Crucially, exchange does *not* presuppose a pre-existing society; it helps create one. "Exchange is one of the functions that creates an inner bond between men — a society, in place of a mere collection of individuals." Indeed "it is almost a tautology to say that exchange brings about socialization: for exchange is a form of socialization."

## Exchange as sacrifice, and the objectification of value

Economic exchange is exchange **of sacrifices**: unlike love or conversation (which enrich both parties without loss), "economic exchange… always signifies the sacrifice of an otherwise useful good." Simmel argues the isolated subsistence producer undergoes the *same* psychological process — weighing sacrifice against gain, with "nature" as his exchange partner — so exchange with others is a species of a more general sacrifice/gain schema. On this basis exchange is "just as productive and value-creating as is production itself."

Through exchange, objects "express their value reciprocally," and subjective valuations are lifted into objective [[value|economic value]]: "the relativity of valuation signifies its objectification." Objectivity here means "validity for subjects in general," since each party must acknowledge the good's worth *for the other*. (Simmel doubts that "equal value" precedes exchange; he treats the idea of equivalence as "a product of the experience of many exchanges.")

## Money as the reification of exchange

As groups outgrow direct barter, the exchange function throws off two structures: a specialized class of merchants and money itself. "The merchant is the personified function of exchange, and money is the reified function of being exchanged" — "the reification of the pure relationship between things as expressed in their economic motion." Ultimately "the relations between the objects are really relations between people," and [[money-economy|money]] represents this activity "in a concrete, independent, and, as it were, congealed form." (Robbery and gift, by contrast, are the "most primitive" one-sided ownership changes; exchange, requiring rough parity of power, marks an advance that binds justice to ownership transfer.)

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Philosophy of Money* (1900) | Exchange as sociation *sui generis*; the purest form of interaction; money as reified exchange |
| Marx (contrast) | Exchange grounded in production and the commodity form; Simmel's "solipsistic" starting point contrasts |
| Mauss, *The Gift* (1925; later) | The gift as total social fact — a distinct but adjacent theory of non-market exchange |
| Homans *The Human Group* (1950; ingested) | Not yet exchange theory — AIS mutual dependence and group systems; reciprocity appears as control case (Whyte) without reinforcement formalism |
| Later [[social-exchange-theory]] (Homans 1958/1961 *Social Behavior*; [[blau-exchange-power-1964]], ingested) | [[social-exchange]] as reward-transaction under unspecified obligation — Homans's reductionist vs. Blau's emergentist versions (see [[exchange-theory-reductionism-debate]]) |

Simmel's usage should not be collapsed into ordinary economic "exchange": he means the general *form* of reciprocal transfer, of which market exchange is the developed economic case.

## Operationalizations

Exchange is analyzed as a form, not measured, but its economic realization is operational: the **exchange ratio / price** at which goods trade, and — as groups outgrow barter — the emergence of a merchant class and of [[money-economy|money]] as the standing, quantitative medium of exchangeability. The degree to which a society's transfers run through money rather than gift, robbery, or barter indexes how far exchange has been reified.

## Applications

- Grounds Simmel's relational theory of [[value]] and his account of [[money-economy|money]] as reified exchange.
- Central to [[formal-sociology]]: exchange is the purest *form* of [[sociation]].

## Applications (Sahlins — the diplomacy of primitive trade)

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 6 "Exchange Value and the Diplomacy of Primitive Trade," 1972.)* Sahlins offers a specifically **substantivist theory of exchange value** against the formalist account of price. He argues that competitive markets — "competitive and price-fixing" — are "universally absent from primitive society," so the double competition (among buyers and among sellers) that fixes market price cannot operate; instead **"social relations, not prices, connect up 'buyers' and 'sellers'"** through exclusive, partner-specific trade relations. Exchange rates are set by **"the diplomacy of economic good measure"** — a customary tendency to over-reciprocate that, over repeated exchanges, converges on a stable customary rate. Rates stay stable in the short run and adjust in the long run not by re-pricing but by the dissolution and reformation of trade partnerships: "the primitive analogue of the business price mechanism is not the customary exchange rate; it is the customary exchange relation." He criticizes Bohannan & Dalton (1962) for reading a "market principle" into Trobriand [[gimwali]], and endorses [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s modes-of-integration frame. This complements the [[reciprocity]] typology ([[balanced-reciprocity]] at the social periphery) rather than Simmel's account of exchange as sociation.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Generalizing "exchange" to nearly all interaction (even orator/audience, hypnotist/subject) risks stretching the concept past economic exchange into metaphor.
- The claim that the solitary producer "exchanges with nature" universalizes a market schema that critics (Blumenberg, from a Marxian angle) read as a "solipsistic economy" starting point, contrasting with Marx's social-relational account.
