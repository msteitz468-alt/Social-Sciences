# Moral career

## Definition as Coined

In "The Moral Career of the Mental Patient" (*[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]*, 1961), [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] uses **career** in a broadened sense: any social strand of a person's course through life, taking a natural-history perspective that neglects unique outcomes in favor of changes basic and common to members of a social category. Such a career "can no more be a success than a failure" (attributed).

The **moral** aspect is "the regular sequence of changes that career entails in the person's self and in his framework of imagery for judging himself and others." Career is **two-sided**: internal (image of self, felt identity) and public (official position, jural relations, style of life). The concept lets the analyst move between self and significant society without relying only on what the person "says he thinks he imagines himself to be" (attributed).

The paper is "an exercise in the institutional approach to the study of self" (attributed).

### Sociological definition of mental patient

Goffman restricts **mental patient** to those who pass through **hospitalization** — the point at which the psychiatric view alters social fate. He excludes undiscovered "candidates," office patients, and outpatient psychotherapy clients; he includes anyone, however robust in temperament, caught in hospital machinery — so effects of being treated as a patient stay distinct from clinical psychopathology (attributed).

### Three phases

Popular/naturalistic career falls into **prepatient**, **inpatient**, and **ex-patient** phases. Essay 2 develops the first two; the extrapatient phase is named but not developed in the body. Note 5: roughly a third of ex-patients experience readmission — a "repatient" phase (attributed).

### Stigma extension (1963; attributed)

In *Stigma* Ch 1, Goffman applied moral career to **persons who have a particular stigma**: they tend to have similar learning experiences regarding their plight and similar changes in conception of self — a similar moral career that is both cause and effect of commitment to a similar sequence of personal adjustments. He distinguished the natural history of a **category of persons with a stigma** from the natural history of the **stigma attribute itself** (origins, spread, decline of an attribute's capacity to stigma — e.g. divorce in the American upper middle class).

**Two initial socialization phases (attributed):** (1) learn and incorporate the standpoint of the normal — identity beliefs of wider society and a general idea of what possessing a particular stigma would be like; (2) learn that one possesses a particular stigma and, in detail, the consequences of possessing it. Timing and interplay of these phases establish patterns for later development.

**Four patterns by timing (attributed):** (1) inborn stigma, socialized while incorporating standards; (2) protective capsule of family/neighbourhood, then shock of wider world; (3) late-onset stigma or late discovery of discreditability; (4) socialization in an alien community, then a second "real" way. Later phases include affiliation cycles with the [[the-own-and-the-wise|own]], ambivalence, learning to [[passing|pass]], and voluntary disclosure as unlearning concealment (*Stigma* Ch 2). Retrospective turning points reorganize biography (discovering the own are human; isolating/hospitalization period; once-removed experiences and literature).

## Operationalizations

### Prepatient phase (attributed)

- **Extrusory model:** starts with relationships and rights; ends at hospital entry with hardly any of either; moral aspects typically begin with abandonment, disloyalty, and embittering experiences of the circuit.
- **Career contingencies** (occupational-sociology term): socio-economic status, visibility of offense, proximity to hospital, availability of treatment alternatives, etc., separate offenses *usable* as grounds from those *so used*.
- **Circuit of agents:** **next-of-relation** (person the prepatient sees as most available of those he should depend on); **complainant** (often makes first effective move); **mediators** (specialists with professional distance — physicians, lawyers, police, clergy, social agencies).
- **Alienative coalition:** next-of-relation presses talk with professionals; mediators and next-of-relation align; prepatient becomes third man.
- **Betrayal funnel:** progressive narrowing of options; unrealistic pictures of hospital life; stripping managed so that those who bring him in are insulated from full confrontation with what is done; the person turned to for protection against commitment is the one administrators need as deputy/guardian.
- **Retroactive character:** until arrival, destiny uncertain; once held, the past is rewritten as having always been leading to patienthood (case history as moral reconstruction).

### Inpatient phase (attributed)

- From taxing anonymity/"not-hereness" to "settling down" / presenting for conventional interaction.
- Fate outlines follow other segregated establishments; orientation via the **ward system** (graded wards, services, parole statuses).
- Mortification continues; psychiatric definitions attack prior self; patients construct **apologias** and **sad tales**; case records and information control; staff collusion in moral experience.
- Moral loosening (e.g., marriage-moratorium imagery; risk calculus around discharge) and discharge as role-without-full-commitment where developed.

Goffman closes toward an institutional theory of the self as an object of social control, not a private property of the person — with "amoral arts of shamelessness" among adaptations (attributed).

## Applications

- Learning sequences of [[stigma|stigmatized]] identity outside total institutions (*Stigma*).
- Analogous career analyses for other total-institution entries (prisoner, recruit, novice).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Phase model can over-smooth case variation and class/race differences in commitment pathways.
- "Betrayal funnel" is Goffman's analytic frame; families and clinicians may reject implications of bad faith — record as attributed analysis, not wiki-voice fact about motives.
- Thin on extrapatient / community career relative to prepatient and inpatient phases.
- Mid-century U.S. commitment and hospital ecology.
