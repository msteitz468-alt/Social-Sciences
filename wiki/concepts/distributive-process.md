# Distributive Process

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (1966), [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] equates **social stratification** with the **distributive process** — the process by which scarce values are distributed — rather than solely with the study of social classes or strata as structural units. Classes and strata are, on his account, structural units that *sometimes* emerge from the workings of that process. The master question is “who gets what and why?”

## Semantic History

Older stratification research often started from classes/strata and prestige hierarchies. Lenski’s redefinition prioritizes **process and causes** over structural taxonomy and over consequences of stratification (then more amenable to survey research). Related but distinct: economic [[redistribution]] as a mechanism within distributive systems; Childean [[social-surplus|social surplus]] as a threshold concept Lenski incorporates into the surplus/power law.

## Operationalizations

Across five society types by subsistence technology; measurement via historical/ethnographic reports of inequality, political power, property, and mobility — not a single quantitative index.

## Applications

Organizing frame for the entire 1966 volume and for later ecological-evolutionary teaching texts (not yet ingested). Properties of distributive systems Lenski analyzes include degree of inequality, vertical mobility, and class hostility. Education, race/ethnicity/religion, sex, age, and citizenship are treated as *resources* in the distributive process (etic “class systems”), not only property and occupation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether “distributive process” dissolves class analysis too far; dependence on secondary comparative data; tension with approaches that treat culture, gender, or race as primary rather than as class systems among others (Lenski addresses the latter in industrial chapters).
