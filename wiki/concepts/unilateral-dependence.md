# Unilateral Dependence and the Four Alternatives to Compliance

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]]'s account of the origin of power turns on **unilateral dependence**: when a person supplies services others need and cannot readily get elsewhere, and they have nothing he needs in return, their one-sided dependence "obligates them to comply with his requests lest he cease to continue to meet their needs." Reciprocal dependence (interdependence) yields exchange among equals; *unilateral* dependence yields power.

The concept's signature is Blau's **schema of the four alternatives to compliance** (adapting Emerson's power-dependence framework). An individual who needs a service another can supply has four alternatives to simply submitting:

1. **Reciprocation** — supply the other a service he wants enough to induce a return (→ reciprocal exchange);
2. **Alternative sources** — obtain the needed service elsewhere (→ exchange with a different partner);
3. **Coercive force** — compel the other to furnish it (→ domination);
4. **Doing without** — resign oneself to do without, or suppress the need (requires changing the values that generate it).

"If they are not able or willing to choose any of these alternatives, they have no other choice but to comply." The schema is Blau's master key: read one way it specifies the **four conditions of independence** (strategic resources, available alternatives, coercive capacity, low need); read another, the **four requirements/strategies of power** (deny outside access, monopolize supply, block coalitions, cultivate the need); and read a third, the **structural implications** of each (exchange systems; competition and going rates; coalition-formation and political struggle; ideological formation and conflict).

## Semantic History

The four-alternatives schema systematizes and reframes Richard Emerson's contemporaneous power-dependence formulation (Blau credits it in a footnote). It anticipates the later exchange-network research program (Cook, Emerson) that made "alternatives" and "dependence" the calculable variables of experimental exchange networks.

## Operationalizations

Each alternative maps to an observable condition: possession of reciprocable resources; number of alternative suppliers (independence rises with the gap between a relation's value and its best alternative); capacity to coerce or form coalitions ("there is strength in numbers"); and the intensity of need — which Blau stresses is *not fixed*, since consumption generates new needs (deepening dependence) while ascetic or revolutionary [[value-orientation|values]] devalue material wants (a source of independent strength).

## Applications

Foundational to [[differentiation-of-power]] and thus to the whole macro-derivation of [[social-exchange-theory]]. The fourth alternative — suppressing need through ideology — is the hinge to [[opposition]]: "a revolutionary ideology... makes exploited and oppressed groups less dependent on material rewards and thus frees their energies to fight existing powers." The four issues that structure conflict between powerful and powerless (collective organization; competition vs. monopoly; the political control of coercive force; ideological struggle) are the four alternatives read as arenas of struggle.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Only **actual** provision confers power, not potential: dependence on someone who *could* supply a benefit is merely "potential power" until an accustomed baseline exists whose withdrawal is felt as punishment.
- Like the parent program, the schema is analytically elegant but treats "need" as partly endogenous, which risks explaining any outcome after the fact.
