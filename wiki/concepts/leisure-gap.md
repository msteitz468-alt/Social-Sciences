# Leisure gap

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]*, [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] wrote that just as there is a wage gap at work, there is a **"leisure gap"** at home: most women work a first shift for pay and a [[second-shift|second shift]] of unpaid domestic labor (Ch. 1, attributed). Averaging major 1960s–70s time-use studies, she reported women worked roughly **fifteen hours longer each week** than men — an extra month of twenty-four-hour days per year (attributed).

## Semantic History

Built on the "double day" documented in time-use research (Szalai cross-national; Robinson Michigan; Walker & Woods Syracuse — Appendix). Hochschild's contribution is naming the **leisure** inequality as the domestic twin of the wage gap and tying it to marital meaning (worth of time, gratitude, love). Later debates on whether the gap closed (e.g. cautious hails of end-of-gap that Hochschild treats skeptically when methods drop busy respondents — Appendix, attributed) keep the construct live.

## Operationalizations

- Aggregate diary time: paid + unpaid work vs residual free time / sleep
- Within-couple leisure asymmetry (Holt: Evan more leisure; footsteps observation)
- Qualitative: women talking about sleep "the way a hungry person talks about food" (Ch. 1, attributed)
- Compositional: women more multitasking and three-sphere juggling (job, children, house); men more two-sphere (job, children); men more flexible task timing

## Applications

Companion metric to second-shift task shares; used to interpret why "supermom" media images function as cover for grim time reality (Ch. 1, attributed). Pocketbook and hours-at-work alone do not close the gap in her sample analyses (Ch. 15).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Dependent on what diaries code as leisure vs secondary activity
- 1960s–70s base period for the ~15-hour claim; update with later time-use when ingested
- Middle-class sample may understate or overstate gaps relative to national populations
