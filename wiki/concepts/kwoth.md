# Kwoth (Nuer Spirit)

**Kwoth** is the Nuer term Evans-Pritchard renders as "Spirit" or "God," recorded during his fieldwork among the Nuer of the southern Sudan (1930–36) and analyzed at length in *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]* (1956). Evans-Pritchard treated *kwoth* as "the key to their very different philosophy," drawing an explicit parallel to how Zande *mangu* (witchcraft) had been "the key to Zande philosophy" in his earlier study.

## Definition as Coined

Evans-Pritchard states his own translation choice was interpretive, not a native Nuer distinction: "I have tried to overcome it by speaking of 'God' when the word has a sense near enough to our own conception of God to permit the usage; of 'Spirit' where it refers... to spiritual nature rather than to any definite, precise, or stated form; and of 'a spirit', 'the spirit', and 'spirits' where it specifically denotes some particular spiritual figure or figures other and lesser than God." As a noun, *kwoth* means only "Spirit"; *kwoth nhial* / *kwoth a nhial* ("Spirit of the sky/heavens") specifies the high god. *Nhial* means both "the sky" and "on high."

Etymologically, per Evans-Pritchard, "the Nuer *kwoth*, like the Latin *spiritus*, the Greek *pneuma*, and the English derivatives of both words, suggests both the intangible quality of air and the breathing or blowing out of air... it is an onomatope and denotes violent breathing out of air."

Nuer creation vocabulary distinguishes **cak** (creation *ex nihilo*, or creation by thought/imagination — usable only of God, or of men for imaginative acts such as naming or storytelling) from **tath** (to make something out of already-existing material). Evans-Pritchard corrected Prof. Westermann's translation of a Nuer creation dictation on this distinction, comparing it to the Hebrew *br'* ("created") versus "made" in Genesis 1. God is not *ghau* ("universe") itself but *cak ghaua*, "creator of the universe."

## Semantic History

Evans-Pritchard argued Nuer religious thought is **"polyonymous, but not henotheistic"**: Nuer treat neighboring peoples' high gods (Dinka *nhialic*, Shilluk *jwok*) as different names for the same *kwoth a nhial*, not distinct deities — evidence he adduced is that Nuer borrowed neighboring peoples' *lesser* spirits (Dinka *deng*, Shilluk *nyikang*) but never their high-god names, since those were already identified with *kwoth a nhial*.

His most-quoted analytical resolution concerns the relation between God and the spirits of the air (see [[spiritual-refraction]]): "the spirits of the air are, nevertheless, being Spirit, also God. They are many but also one. God is manifested in, and in a sense is, each of them." He stated the logic explicitly: "The relation of these spirits to God is not a disjunction, an either...or, but a conjunction, a both...and."

Later in the book, discussing the Nuer particle **e** ("is"), Evans-Pritchard analyzed why saying of something "*e kwoth*" ("it is Spirit/God") does not assert identity, distinguishing three senses: (1) **elliptical statements**, where rain, lightning, or pestilence are called God because they are his "instruments" (*nyin kwoth*) or "signs" (*gwreth*); (2) **symbolical statements**, where a totemic creature "is" Spirit only for persons in a totemic relationship to it, not as a general predication; and (3) **near-identity statements**, for nature sprites and fetish-bundles, which "have no meaning of any kind outside" being a sign or abode of Spirit. Such statements are asymmetrical: "one can say of rain or pestilence that it is God [but] one cannot say of God that he is rain or pestilence." Evans-Pritchard framed this as a rebuttal of Lévy-Bruhl's "prelogical mentality" and "law of mystical participation" (*Les Fonctions mentales dans les sociétés inférieures*, 1910; *La Mentalité primitive*, 1922): "no contradiction is involved in the statement... we cannot speak here, as Lévy-Bruhl does, of mystical participation... because the two things are not thought to be linked by a mystical bond but simply by a symbolic nexus." His own account: "A is B" statements are elliptical for "A and B have something in common in relation to C" — a triadic, not dyadic, relation (e.g., cucumber–ox–God in sacrifice; twins–birds–God in the twins-are-birds dogma). He named Tylor and Max Müller, alongside Lévy-Bruhl, as sharing a "second error" of mistaking Nuer "ideal" (conceptual/symbolic) equivalence for claims of "real" (substantial) identity.

Late in the book Evans-Pritchard summarized Nuer religion as "pneumatic and theistic," adding that whether it is properly called monotheistic "is largely a matter of definition": it can be so described because "at no level of thought and experience is Spirit thought of as something altogether different from God," while simultaneously **modalistic** — one Spirit "differently thought of with regard to different effects and relations." His closing formulation: "A theistic religion need not be either monotheistic or polytheistic. It may be both. It is a question of the level, or situation, of thought rather than of exclusive types of thought. On one level Nuer religion may be regarded as monotheistic, at another level as polytheistic; and it can also be regarded at other levels as totemistic or fetishistic."

## Operationalizations

Not operationalized as a measured variable. Evans-Pritchard's method was philological and contextual: tracing Nuer usage of *kwoth* and its cognate vocabulary (*cak*/*tath*, *cuong* — "in the right," both forensic and moral — *duer*/*gwac* — deliberate versus unintentional fault — *doar* — Nuer self-designation before God as "simple/foolish/ignorant," expressing creatureliness, not self-deprecation before other peoples — and *yop* — the danger of praising good fortune, distinguished by Evans-Pritchard from *peth*, "evil eye," which concerns covetousness rather than danger-from-attention) across invocations, sacrificial contexts, and everyday speech recorded during 1930–36 fieldwork.

## Applications

*Kwoth* is the organizing term of the whole book and grounds the [[spiritual-refraction]] thesis: the hierarchy from God through spirits of the air, *colwic* (souls of persons killed by lightning), totemic spirits, and fetishes tracks the segmentary structure of Nuer society, with each level of Spirit corresponding to a level of social grouping. Evans-Pritchard used a delayed-homicide settlement ceremony he witnessed in western Nuerland as his key illustration: successive speakers addressed Spirit by different titles depending on group affiliation — *kwoth* (unqualified), *kwoth a nhial* ("Spirit who is in the sky"), *kwoth wic(da)* ("spirit of our home/community"), *kwoth ngoap(na)* ("spirit of our fig-tree," a lineage totem), *kwoth rieng(da)* ("spirit of the flesh," the sacerdotal power of leopard-skin priests) — all addressed over a single sacrificial victim, which he took as evidence that one Spirit was being invoked in different refracted aspects, not distinct deities: "may be compared to a war between European powers in which each prays for victory to the God of its fathers, Lord of its battle-line. Those engaged in the struggle do not believe that two distinct deities are being appealed to."

## Critiques and Limitations

Evans-Pritchard explicitly distanced his account from the inherited comparative-religion vocabulary of his predecessors: "the considerable literature on primitive religions which appeared towards the end of the last century and the early years of the present century made us familiar with such terms as animism, fetishism, totemism, mana, tabu, and shamanism, [but] their meanings are still obscure." He also stated his own epistemic limit at the book's close: "We feel like spectators at a shadow show watching insubstantial shadows on the screen. There is nothing Nuer can say of the nature of God other than that he is like wind or air. They can speak of their experience of Spirit but can tell us nothing of Spirit itself... Nuer religious conceptions are properly speaking not concepts but imaginative constructions." He drew repeated, self-consciously flagged analogies to Hebrew/Old Testament conceptions of God (citing Seligman and missionary Ray Huffman making similar observations) as a heuristic for the Western reader, explicitly not a claim of historical or genetic connection.

The material rests on a single ethnographer's fieldwork (1930–36), supplemented by post-fieldwork correspondence with Dr. Godfrey Lienhardt (Dinka religion) and Dr. Mary Smith (American Presbyterian Mission, Nasser, who reviewed the manuscript chapter by chapter). Evans-Pritchard noted his own status as "a *ger*, what they call a *rul*, an alien sojourner, among them for only a year" of dedicated religious study within the longer 1930–36 project.
