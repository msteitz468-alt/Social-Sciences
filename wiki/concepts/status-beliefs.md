# Status Beliefs

**Status beliefs** are widely shared cultural beliefs that people in one social category are more esteemed, status-worthy, and (in achievement cultures) more competent at what "counts" than people in another category. In Ridgeway's gender program, **gender status beliefs** — especially **diffuse** beliefs favoring men's general competence — constitute gender as a distinct principle of inequality.

## Definition as Coined

Within [[expectation-states-theory]], status beliefs attach status value and competence expectations to status characteristics. Ridgeway emphasizes that gender status beliefs make inequality **categorical** (one is unequal *as* a man or woman), not only positional, while still depending on everyday positional inequalities for long-run credibility ("If you're so smart, why aren't you rich?").

## Semantic History

Rooted in Berger et al.'s status characteristics / expectation states program; Ridgeway's [[status-construction-theory]] explains formation and diffusion; *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* treats contemporary U.S. gender stereotypes (including "women are wonderful" warmth alongside male competence/status) as the cultural content of these beliefs.

## Operationalizations

- Group status and competence ratings (e.g. stereotype content work Ridgeway cites: Fiske et al.).
- Experimental status characteristic designs.
- Distinction **specific** vs **diffuse** status beliefs (limited task domains vs general worthiness).

## Applications

Explains bias in evaluation, influence, leadership legitimacy, glass-ceiling and maternal-wall dynamics, and why women in high positions are not fully status-equivalent to men in the same positions (Ridgeway 2011).

## Critiques and Limitations

Consensual "what most people think" stereotypes can differ from personal endorsement; change in personal attitudes may outrun consensual beliefs (Ridgeway's lag argument). Cross-national variation in content requires separate evidence.
