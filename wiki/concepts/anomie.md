# Anomie

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* (1897), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] develops **anomy** (English editions often “anomy”/“anomie”) as a state of **de-regulation**: society’s moral power fails to limit and order individual desires. Unlike the animal, whose needs equilibrate with material conditions, human capacity for feeling is, he argued, “an insatiable and bottomless abyss” if nothing external restrains it. Unlimited desires are insatiable by definition and a sign of morbidity.

Only **society** (or an organ of society) — the sole moral power superior to the individual and spontaneously respected — can set the point beyond which passions must not go and estimate legitimate rewards for classes of function. When society is disturbed by painful crisis **or** abrupt beneficial transition, it is momentarily incapable of this influence; appetites are more exigent just when traditional rules lose authority.

**Textual origin (1893).** The term first appears in Durkheim's corpus in *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]*, where the **[[anomic-division-of-labor|anomic division of labor]]** names a *deficient regulation between differentiated social functions/organs* — "the relations of the organs are not regulated, because they are in a state of anomy" — producing industrial crises and capital–labor conflict. This 1893 sense is structural/organizational; the 1897 *Suicide* sense (below) applies the same core idea — deficient regulation — to *individual desire*. Same concept, different unit of analysis.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Division of Labor* (1893) | Deficient regulation **between functions/organs** — the [[anomic-division-of-labor]]; industrial crises, class conflict; the term's textual origin |
| *Suicide* (1897) | Deficient regulation of **individual desire**; economic and conjugal forms; chronic industrial anomy |
| Later (Merton et al.) | Reworked as **strain** between **cultural goals** and **institutionalized means** — related but different construct (see below) |
| Everyday / policy English | Vague “normlessness” or social breakdown — looser than Durkheim’s regulation thesis |

### Merton's reworking (*Social Structure and Anomie*, 1938; *[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Chs. VI–VII)

[[merton-robert-k|Merton]] retained the label **anomie** (explicitly via Durkheim) but relocated the mechanism. He argued (attributed) that when cultural emphasis falls heavily on success **goals** with far less support for prescribed **means**, institutional procedures are vitiated, technical expediency dominates, and society develops what Durkheim called anomie (normlessness). **Mertonian specificity:** anomie is the end-state of a process driven by **disjunction of cultural success-goals and class-structured legitimate means** (especially the American pecuniary-success ethos), not only general deregulation of appetites. Groups emphasizing means over goals tend toward **ritualism** rather than anomie.

He distinguished **cultural structure** (normative values) from **social structure** (organized set of social relationships) and proposed five **modes of individual adaptation** (role behavior in situations, not personality types):

| Mode | Cultural goals | Institutional means |
|---|---|---|
| Conformity | accept | accept |
| Innovation | accept | reject / substitute |
| Ritualism | reject / scale down | accept |
| Retreatism | reject | reject |
| Rebellion | replace | replace |

Landmark treatment: [[merton-social-structure-and-anomie-study]]. Continuities chapter (VII) addresses sociological vs psychological anomie, Srole’s subjective scale, Hyman’s and Cohen’s critiques, and cumulative anomie–deviance processes — still under `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` on the study page.

Durkheim held that modern culture can naturalize anomy: the “longing for infinity” is treated as moral distinction though it “can only appear within unregulated consciences which elevate to a rule the lack of rule from which they suffer.”

## Operationalizations

In *Suicide*:

- **Economic anomy** — rise of suicide in financial crises *and* in prosperity shocks (Italian unity boom, Prussian post-1870, expositions); industrial/commercial occupations high rates; poverty as restraint/protection thesis.
- **Domestic / conjugal anomy** — widowhood crisis; parallel of divorce/separation rates with suicide across Europe and Swiss cantons; sex-asymmetric effects of divorce on marital “coefficients of preservation.”
- **Chronic industrial anomy** — century-long freeing of industrial relations from religious, political, and [[occupational-group|corporative]] regulation without replacement.

Distinct from [[egoistic-suicide|egoism]]: both involve society’s insufficient presence in individuals, but egoism is deficient collective activity/meaning; anomy is lack of check-rein on basically individual passions. One may be anomic without egoistic and vice versa.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Period gender theory of conjugal anomy (woman “less need” of strict regulation) is attributed and contested.
- “Poverty protects” thesis conflicts with many later findings on absolute deprivation and mental health — reanalyze, do not promote to wiki voice.
- Mertonian and other successor concepts should not be silently equated with Durkheim’s 1897 sense — see the Merton layer above and [[merton-social-structure-and-anomie-study]].
- **Gouldner 1970 (attributed):** in *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|Coming Crisis]]*, anomie is reframed as the **normal pathology of utilitarian culture** (not only Merton's deviance under strain): middle-class utility standards themselves generate moral deregulation; property and marketability subvert utility; Parsons's anomie is read as **de-differentiation** within system analysis. Distinct layers — Durkheim 1897 / Merton / Parsons / Gouldner — must not be collapsed.
