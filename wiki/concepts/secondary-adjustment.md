# Secondary adjustment

## Definition as Coined

In "The Underlife of a Public Institution" (*[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]*, 1961), [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] defines **secondary adjustments** as:

> any habitual arrangement by which a member of an organization employs unauthorized means, or obtains unauthorized ends, or both, thus getting around the organization's assumptions as to what he should do and get and hence what he should be.

They "represent ways in which the individual stands apart from the role and the self that were taken for granted for him by the institution" (attributed).

**[[primary-adjustment|Primary adjustments]]** are the collaborative stances in which the individual is transformed into a co-operator — the "normal," "programmed," or built-in member who gives and gets what has been systematically planned (attributed). The full set of secondary adjustments in an establishment is its **[[underlife]]**.

### Conceptual cautions (Goffman's own, attributed)

- Not all "informal" or "unofficial" activity is secondary adjustment — recess informality can be primary; unofficial understandings can further official aims.
- Some secondary adjustments become accepted **perquisites**; some must remain unofficial to work; management may deliberately allow what the participant experiences as getting away with something.
- What is primary for one category of participant can be secondary for another; historical and cross-societal legitimation shifts the line.
- A third possibility: **over-attachment** — embracing the entity more than asked (junior officer going down with the ship; inmate declining discharge).
- Analyze secondary adjustments relative to authorized-but-not-quite-official systems when official doctrine is little honored.

Essay 1 already introduces secondary adjustments and inmate vocabulary of "angles" and "ropes" for ways of making out under the privilege system (attributed).

### Types and ecology (Essay 3, attributed)

- **Disruptive** vs. **contained** secondary adjustments — Goffman restricts primary attention to contained ones (temporary by definition if disruptive).
- **Make-dos** — using available materials for unauthorized ends.
- **Working the system** — timing, scavenging, assignment shopping, therapy-as-meet, food routes, etc.
- **Spatial ecology:** off-limits regions; **free places** (reduced surveillance); **group territories**; **personal territories**; **stashes** (portable and fixed) and unofficial **transport** systems.
- **Private coercion**, **economic exchange**, **social exchange** (solidarity, ritual supplies, cigarette systems), **information** as a good under uncertainty.
- **Patron relationships** (ward and assignment systems; unpaid work as "therapy"; ground parole for work).
- **Removal activities** and ritual insubordination (Essay 3 conclusions).

| Context | Sense |

| Everyday speech | Sometimes collapsed into "resistance" — Goffman's range includes quiet self-protective arrangements |

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic inventory of unauthorized means/ends; mapping free places and exchange systems; distinction from formal privileges. Central Hospital case (St. Elizabeths materials): ~7,000 patients, one-year participant observation (see [[goffman-asylums-study]]).

## Applications

- Mental-hospital underlife as detailed case.
- Comparative notes on prisons and other total institutions.
- Micro-analysis of self-protection under extreme constraint — identity "in the cracks" (Essay 3 conclusions, attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boundary between secondary adjustment and staff-tolerated informal custom is fuzzy (Goffman flags this).
- Romanticizing "making out" can underplay exploitation within inmate systems.
- Tied to high-surveillance enclosed settings; portability to open organizations is a separate claim.
- Goffman notes bias: underlife more visible than full primary co-operation; weak patient social control and little reactive solidarity at Central Hospital (attributed caveat).
