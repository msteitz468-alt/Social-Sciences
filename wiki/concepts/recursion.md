# Recursion (linguistics)

**Recursion** is the property by which a structure can contain another structure of the same type — a phrase inside a phrase, a clause inside a clause — in principle without limit. [[everett-daniel|Everett]] states the standard definition as "the ability to put one item inside another of the same type" (formally, a function whose procedure references itself), illustrated by mirrors-within-mirrors and by Russian **matrioshka** nesting dolls. In [[universal-grammar|generative linguistics]], and especially in **Hauser, Chomsky & Fitch (2002)**, recursion is proposed as the possibly-unique core of the human language faculty. This page treats recursion as an **etic analytical concept**; the substantive claims about which languages have it are **attributed and contested**.

## Definition as Coined

The term is inherited from mathematics and computer science (a self-referential procedure; a definition that invokes itself). Applied to language, it names structural **embedding**: e.g. *the nails [that Dan bought]*, a relative clause nested inside a noun phrase. A standard diagnostic (Everett's exposition): if a language has recursion, there is **no longest sentence** — any sentence can be extended by further embedding.

## Semantic History

Recursion migrated from a technical property of formal systems into a claim about human nature. Its stakes rose sharply once **Hauser, Chomsky & Fitch (2002)** identified it as the plausibly-unique component of the "faculty of language in the narrow sense" — the engine of linguistic creativity. That move converted a descriptive notion into a testable universal: a natural language lacking recursion would be theoretically anomalous.

## Operationalizations

- **Positive test (Everett):** "If you find a phrase within a phrase, the language has recursion, period." Look for embedded relative clauses, sentential complements, embedded possessors, or embedding marked by intonation.
- **No-longest-sentence test:** unbounded extendability implies recursion.
- Everett distinguishes **iteration** (repeating/adding items after a sentence) from recursion (true nesting), rejecting Ian Roberts's claim that "iteration is a form of recursion" with a reductio (iterated dog-barking would then be "recursive").

## Applications — the Pirahã case (attributed)

Everett claims [[piraha|Pirahã]] sentences show **no recursion**: where English embeds ("bring the nails *that Dan bought*"), Pirahã uses three juxtaposed independent sentences (**parataxis**), interpreted jointly. He reports no phrase-within-phrase, a single-modifier-per-phrase and single-possessor-per-phrase constraint, no embedding via intonation (per a three-year project with Robert Van Valin), and no coordination or disjunction. Crucially, Everett insists recursion **does** appear in Pirahã **discourse and story structure** (ideas nested inside ideas) and in general cognition — "you can have a nonfinite language with a finite grammar." He attributes the grammatical absence to a cultural cause, the [[immediacy-of-experience|immediacy of experience principle]], not to an accidental gap: Pirahã "doesn't want it; it doesn't allow it because of a cultural principle." His alternative account locates recursion in the brain's general cognitive powers (citing Herbert Simon's "The Architecture of Complexity," 1962), not in a language-specific module.

## Critiques and Limitations

The claim is disputed on both data and theory (see [[piraha-recursion-debate]]): critics contend Pirahã does have embedding, or that a single language's lacking recursion would not refute [[universal-grammar|UG]] (recursion as an available capacity that need not surface). Everett reports Chomskyans redefining recursion after his challenge ("If at first you don't succeed, redefine success"). Because the Pirahã data are single-observer, the concept's application here is entangled with the epistemics of one fieldworker's corpus.
