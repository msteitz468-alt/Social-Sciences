# General and Specific Evolution

Primary coining: [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall D. Sahlins]], Ch. II of *[[sahlins-service-evolution-culture-1960|Evolution and Culture]]* (Sahlins & Service, eds., 1960), pp. 12–44. The joint Introduction (Sahlins & Service) and [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]]’s Foreword endorse and frame the distinction. Later restated as the spine of [[service-elman-r|Service]]’s [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Sahlins 1960.)* In both biological and cultural spheres, evolution moves simultaneously in two directions:

1. **Specific evolution** — adaptive diversification: “descent with modification,” the production of diverse forms (species / culture types) as life or culture fills varied opportunities. Taxonomy is **phylogenetic** (lines of descent; historical and genetic relations). “Advance” in this perspective is **relative** to environmental circumstances.
2. **General evolution** — progressive advance: “the successive emergence of new levels of all-round development,” sequences of higher (more complex, more energy-capturing, more integrated) forms. Taxonomy classes forms in **stages or levels without reference to phylogeny**. Progress criteria are **absolute**, not habitat-relative.

Sahlins insisted these are **not different concrete realities** but **aspects of the same total process** — two contexts in which the same evolutionary events can be placed. Any given change can be viewed either as adaptation or as overall progress.

He credited [[tylor-edward-burnett|E. B. Tylor]] (*Primitive Culture*, 1871 opening chapter) with already framing cultural evolution both “stage by stage” and “along its many lines”; twentieth-century anthropology, he held, failed to heed that dual advice, and that failure became “the very heart” of polemics over unilinear / multilinear / universal evolution and history vs evolution.

## Semantic History

**White’s Foreword (1960, attributed):** White accepted both aspects as evolution — “specific evolution is just as much evolution as general evolution” — and equated **specific ≈ multilinear**, **general ≈ unilinear**. He rejected readings that collapse specific evolution into “history” or treat general evolution as Kroeberian “summarized history.”

**Service and neo-evolutionary typology:** Service took the distinction as organizing spine for *[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]* and the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]; general levels (band / tribe / chiefdom) vs specific adaptive variants within levels.

**Carneiro 2003 (attributed):** [[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] accepted the two *views* of change but preferred a **Spencerian** restriction of the word “evolution” to increasing complexity. He argued that treating entire culture-histories (e.g. Rome’s rise *and* fall) as “specific evolution” collapses aggregation and disintegration; decline, for Carneiro, is devolution. He also rejected White’s neat equation of specific=multilinear / general=unilinear: a unique society’s increase in complexity can still be general evolution in Sahlins’s sense without parallel cases. Robert Murphy (1971, as Carneiro cites) reduced “specific evolution” to ordinary “history.”

**Later Sahlins:** After the economic and structuralist turns (*Stone Age Economics*, *Culture and Practical Reason*), Sahlins largely left neo-evolutionary stage theory behind; the 1960 distinction remains historically load-bearing for mid-century anthropology and processual archaeology even where its author moved on.

## Operationalizations

*(Attributed — Sahlins 1960.)*

**Specific (relative):**
- Phylogenetic / historical classification of culture types and adaptive pathways
- Adaptive “advance” = maintain or better position under environmental threat, or exploit the same environment more effectively
- Inventiveness + diffusion as raw materials of cultural phylogeny; [[cultural-ecology]] and superorganic environment as selectors
- Cultural relativism of adaptive specializations (Eskimo technology vs agricultural “higher” forms is not a single ranking)

**General (absolute criteria Sahlins listed):**
- **Thermodynamic / energy** — amount of energy concentrated or total energy transformation of a cultural organization (White’s program; *not* mere efficiency of conversion)
- **Organization / level of integration** — segmentation, specialization of parts, special means of integration (bands → tribes → chiefdoms → archaic states / nation-states as successive general levels; Morgan’s societas/civitas generalized)
- **All-round adaptability and dominance range** — higher forms characteristically exploit more kinds of resources more effectively and thus have greater geographic/ecological range ([[law-of-cultural-dominance]])

Unit of general cultural taxonomy: a cultural system of a **sociopolitical entity** (not a trait list or culture area total pattern).

## Applications

- Organizing vocabulary for the whole of *Evolution and Culture* (Harding on specific adaptation/stability; Kaplan on dominance; Service on evolutionary potential as inverse of specific specialization)
- Service’s political stages and processual archaeology’s long-term complexity questions
- Sahlins’s resolution of mid-century controversy: White’s program is primarily **general**; Steward/Kroeber/Murdock concerns are primarily **specific**; the unilinear charge against classical evolutionists is largely a charge of omission (they were generalists), not commission of a phylogenetic single-line doctrine
- Carneiro’s history of evolutionism and political unilinearity thesis (band → village → chiefdom → state)

See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[social-evolutionism]], [[universal-evolution]], [[multilinear-evolution]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Collapses or confuses Darwinian (all adaptive change) vs Spencerian (complexity-increasing) meanings of “evolution” (Carneiro’s critique).
- “General” progress language invites ethnocentric ranking even when authors claim objective complexity/energy measures; Sahlins himself noted anthropocentric ideology risks while defending absolute criteria.
- Measurement of energy and “higher” remains contested; Sahlins caveated energy metrics.
- Post-processual and agency critiques reject stage/progress frames altogether.
- Sahlins’s own later work de-emphasized this program without formally retracting the dual-aspect claim.

## Primary formulation (1960) — load-bearing moves

*(All attributed to Sahlins, *Evolution and Culture* Ch. II.)*

1. **Two aspects, one process** — not two theories competing for exclusive truth.
2. **Taxonomy must switch** with aspect: phylogeny for specific; cross-cutting stages for general.
3. **Higher ≠ better adapted in every niche** — higher forms are often more *generalized*; many lower specialists outlast higher types.
4. **Culture continues evolution by new means** — diffusion, coalescence of lines, and leapfrogging of stages differentiate cultural from biological phylogeny, but the dual aspect remains.
5. **Feudalism is not a general stage** antecedent to modern civilization; classical and oriental civilizations represent the general pre-modern level; feudalism is a specific stage with evolutionary potential (links to [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]]).
6. **History vs evolution dissolved:** evolution is in one respect history (specific) and in another not (general); White, Kroeber, Steward, Murdock are each “right” in one aspect and “wrong” if they monopolize the word.
7. **Programmatic close:** evolutionism as the “central, inclusive, organizing outlook of anthropology.”
