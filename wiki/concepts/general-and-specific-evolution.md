# General and Specific Evolution

## Definition as Coined

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]], in the lead essay of *Evolution and Culture* (Sahlins & Service, 1960), distinguished two aspects of evolution and applied them to culture:

- **Specific evolution** — adaptive diversification: “descent with modification,” the production of diverse culture types as culture fills varied opportunities for human existence.
- **General evolution** — progressive advance: “the successive emergence of new levels of all-round development,” sequences of higher (more complex) forms.

Sahlins held both to be real aspects of cultural evolution, not alternatives that cancel each other.

## Semantic History

The distinction reorganized mid-century neo-evolutionism: it mapped roughly onto White’s interest in culture-as-a-whole grades ([[universal-evolution]]) versus Steward’s interest in limited adaptive parallels ([[multilinear-evolution]] / [[cultural-ecology]]), without equating the pairs exactly. [[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]] (foreword to *Evolution and Culture*, 1960) accepted both as evolution: “specific evolution is just as much evolution as general evolution,” and equated specific≈multilinear, general≈unilinear.

[[carneiro-robert-l|Robert L. Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*, 2003 — attributed) accepted the two *views* of change but preferred a **Spencerian** restriction of the word “evolution” to increasing complexity (definiteness, coherence, heterogeneity). He argued that treating entire culture-histories (e.g. Rome’s rise *and* fall) as “specific evolution” collapses aggregation and disintegration into one term; decline, for Carneiro, is devolution, not evolution. He also rejected White’s neat equation of specific=multilinear / general=unilinear: a unique society’s increase in complexity can still be general evolution in Sahlins’s sense without parallel cases. Robert Murphy (1971, as Carneiro cites) reduced “specific evolution” to ordinary “history.”

## Operationalizations

- **Specific:** ethnographic/archaeological adaptive pathways, culture types, ecological specialization (Stewardian program affinity).
- **General:** stage sequences and complexity indices (Service’s [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]]; White’s energy measure; scale analysis / Guttman scalograms of trait accumulation — as discussed in Carneiro 2003).
- Measurement of “higher” remains contested (ethnocentric ranking risk vs claimed objective complexity scales).

## Applications

Organizing vocabulary for Sahlins & Service 1960; Service’s political stages; processual archaeology’s long-term complexity questions; Carneiro’s history of evolutionism and his political unilinearity thesis (band → village → chiefdom → state as common line for autochthonous states). See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[social-evolutionism]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Collapses or confuses Darwinian (all change) vs Spencerian (complexity-increasing) meanings of “evolution.”
- “General” progress language invites ethnocentric reading even when authors claim objective complexity measures.
- Post-processual and agency critiques reject stage/progress frames altogether.
- Yoffee and others retain evolutionary questions while rejecting neo-evolutionist stage “factoids.”
