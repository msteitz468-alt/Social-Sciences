# High-Consequence Risks

## Definition as Coined

[[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (1990) analysed modernity’s **risk profile**, emphasising **low-probability, high-consequence** dangers remote from individual or even state control and potentially life-threatening for millions or for humanity as a whole. He argued such risks will not disappear (even optimising scenarios leave reconstructible technical knowledge and new technological disturbances); the [[juggernaut-of-modernity|juggernaut effect]] is inherent.

Illustrative inventory (attributed; Figure 7 and surrounding discussion): nuclear war / nuclear winter; ecological hazards (ozone, rainforest, topsoil, "socialised nature"); industrialised military force; institutionalised risk arenas (investment markets, arms race as mutual outguessing). Many are heavily **counterfactual** — clear demonstration would require unthinkable events — producing public numbness and a secular return of "fate"/fortuna as anxiety management.

Giddens explicitly engages [[beck-ulrich|Beck]]: "Chernobyl is everywhere"; no "others" to blame for certain global hazards.

## Semantic History

Parallel and partly independent of Beck’s [[risk-society]] thesis (manufactured hazards, relations of definition). Giddens ties risk tightly to [[trust]], [[ontological-security]], and abstract-system dependence.

**Risk vs danger vs security** (Giddens, developing Luhmann and others): risk is tied to modern future-orientation and calculative openings; not identical with traditional "danger."

## Operationalizations

Not a single index. Risk profile elements (Giddens lists multiple: intensity/globalisation of risk; created environment; institutionalised risk environments; awareness of risk as risk; limits of expertise; etc.).

**Adaptive reactions** (fourfold, attributed): pragmatic acceptance; sustained optimism; cynical pessimism; radical engagement (via social movements).

## Applications

- Baseline for ontological insecurity under high modernity.
- Grounds political need for [[utopian-realism]].
- Cross-link to [[risk-society]] without merging the two programs.

## Critiques and Limitations

List-based diagnosis can age with new hazards; probabilities remain essentially untestable for many items (Giddens’s own point). Differential social distribution of risk (class, race, geography) is acknowledged more sharply in Beck and later risk sociology — do not read Giddens 1990 as a full inequality of risk theory.
