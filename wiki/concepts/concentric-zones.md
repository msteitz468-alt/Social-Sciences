# Concentric Zones

## Definition as Coined

In "The Growth of the City" (*[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]*, 1925), [[burgess-ernest-w|Ernest W. Burgess]] argued that typical processes of urban expansion "can best be illustrated… by a series of concentric circles" numbering successive zones of extension and the types of areas differentiated in expansion. The chart is "an ideal construction of the tendencies of any town or city to expand radially from its central business district" (attributed, Burgess).

| Zone | Label (Chicago program) | Character (attributed, Burgess) |
|---|---|---|
| I | Loop / central business district | Department stores, skyscraper offices, stations, hotels, theaters, museums, city hall; economic, cultural, political center |
| II | Zone in transition / deterioration | Invaded by business and light manufacture; slums, "bad lands," rooming houses, Latin Quarter, immigrant colonies (Ghetto, Little Sicily, etc.), underworlds of crime and vice; also regeneration (missions, settlements, radical centers) |
| III | Zone of workingmen's homes | Skilled thrifty workers escaped deterioration but near work; second immigrant settlement / second generation; "Deutschland" relative to the Ghetto |
| IV | Residential zone | High-class apartments or exclusive restricted single-family districts |
| V | Commuters' zone | Suburbs or satellite cities within a thirty- to sixty-minute ride of the CBD |

The main fact of expansion, Burgess argued, is that each inner zone tends to extend by **invasion** of the next outer zone — a process he called [[ecological-succession|succession]], "studied in detail in plant ecology" (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Moment | Sense |
|---|---|
| Burgess 1925 | Ideal construction of radial expansion and area types; Chicago illustration |
| Mid-century textbook sociology | Often reified as *the* American city form; perennial "punching bag" (Sampson 2019 reception) |
| Later urban morphology | Multi-nuclei, sector, and political-economy models revise geometry while often retaining invasion/succession language |
| Neighborhood-effects research | Geometry secondary; process and place differentiation retained (Sampson reception) |

Burgess's own caveat is load-bearing: "It hardly needs to be added that neither Chicago nor any other city fits perfectly into this ideal scheme" — lake front, river, railroads, historical industrial location, and community resistance to invasion complicate the diagram (attributed).

## Operationalizations

- **Ideal-type diagram** (Chart I in *The City*).
- **Historical zone migration** on Chicago: early history packed all zones into the present business-district circumference; "best families" residences later became deterioration boundaries (attributed illustration).
- Later quantitative gradient studies and census-tract ecology (reception; Shaw & McKay not yet ingested).

## Applications

- Chicago growth and [[natural-area|natural areas]] inventory (hobohemia, Black Belt, Deutschland, satellite loops).
- Linking expansion rate to [[social-disorganization|organization/disorganization]] "metabolism" and [[urban-mobility|mobility]] indices.
- Burgess's West Side Jewish cross-section (Ghetto + Lawndale) as microscope application of the scheme.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Over-schematic; Burgess denied perfect fit (ideal type).
- Neglect of political economy, planning power, and institutional racism (standing reception critiques summarized by Sampson 2019).
- Other morphologies (ports, multi-centered metros) fit poorly.
- "Invasion" and "deterioration" language carries normative load.
