# Network Enterprise

Castells’s term for the characteristic organizational form of the informational / global economy: production organized as a **network** (internal and external) rather than solely as a vertical bureaucracy or atomized market. Canonical: *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Ch. 3.

## Definition as Coined

Castells (attributed): the network enterprise is “that specific form of enterprise whose system of means is constituted by the intersection of segments of autonomous systems of goals.” Components are both autonomous and dependent vis-à-vis the network and may belong to other networks/goal-systems. Performance depends on **connectedness** (structural ability to facilitate noise-free communication between components) and **consistency** (shared interests between network goals and components’ goals). Related thesis: the logic of the network can be more powerful than the powers *in* the network (multinationals as nodes of international networks rather than sovereign “transnationals”).

## Semantic History

Trajectories Castells synthesizes: mass production → flexible production / post-Fordism; Toyotism; SME networks (e.g. Benetton model); strategic alliances; horizontal corporation; Cisco-type networked firm. Comparative East Asian institutionalization: Japanese *kigyo shudan*/keiretsu; Korean chaebol; Chinese family business / *jiazuqiye* networks — different cultural-institutional paths to network forms. Cultural glue: [[spirit-of-informationalism]].

## Operationalizations

Organizational case literatures; Ernst’s network types (supplier, producer, customer, standard coalitions, technology cooperation); multinational network structures; relation to labor individualization and [[flexible-employment]].

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; economic sociology of globalization; work process under the informational paradigm (Ch. 4 “networkers”).

## Critiques and Limitations

Breadth of “network” can blur firm boundaries analytically; uneven performance of Asian networks under globalization (Castells notes path-dependence); power asymmetries inside networks underplayed if morphology is overemphasized.
