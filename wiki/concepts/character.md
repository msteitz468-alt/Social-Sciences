# Character

**Character**, in [[sennett-richard|Richard Sennett]]'s usage, names the long-term ethical and emotional dimension of the self — the traits a person sustains across time and answers for to others: loyalty, mutual commitment, the pursuit of durable purpose, and the capacity to compose experience into a coherent life narrative. Sennett draws the term from a classical (Horatian) idea of character *as a connection to the world, as being necessary for others*, and sets it against **personality**, the shorter-run register of feelings and self-presentation. His 1998 essay *[[sennett-corrosion-of-character-1998|The Corrosion of Character]]* argues that the "flexible" [[flexible-employment|new capitalism]] erodes precisely this long-term dimension. Primary treatment in this wiki is from that book; the term has a much longer history in moral philosophy and psychology not yet ingested here.

## Definition as Coined

Sennett anchors character in the question **"Who needs me?"** — the self understood as necessary to others over time. In his framing (attributed): "The qualities of good work are not the qualities of good character," and character depends on being able to answer *for* oneself across a sustained arc of relationships and commitments rather than in the moment. This is an explicitly relational and temporal definition: character is what a person builds by rooting into institutions, families, and communities slowly enough to become "a long-term witness to another person's life."

## Semantic History

The word carries a documented drift. In classical usage (Horace) it marked a person's necessity to others; through the Protestant era it fused with the **driven, self-disciplining** self Sennett reads out of [[weber-max|Weber]]'s *[[spirit-of-capitalism|Protestant Ethic]]* (see [[protestant-ethic-debate]]) — worth proved through delayed gratification and hard labor. Sennett's own move (attributed) is to detach character from that self-punishing ethic (which he refuses to sentimentalize) while insisting that its *temporal* core — commitment sustained over the long term — is what flexible capitalism now corrodes. Against character, Sennett sets postmodern theories of the fragmented, episodic self (Rushdie, Bauman, Jameson, Turkle), which he treats as descriptively apt for flexible-capitalist psychology but unable to register the "gravity and pain of failure."

## Operationalizations

Character is not operationalized quantitatively. Sennett accesses it through **life-narrative interviews** — asking subjects (Enrico and Rico, Rose, the downsized IBM programmers) to tell the story of their working lives — and reading the coherence, or its absence, of the narratives they can construct. The evidence class is [[participant-observation|ethnographic]] and small-n; the construct's operationalization is one of the central objects of the [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]].

## Applications

- **Enrico and Rico** (*Corrosion*, Ch. 1): the janitor father whose linear, cumulative work life sustained a coherent character, versus the consultant son whose flexible career leaves his "emotional, inner life adrift."
- The bakery workers' weak work-identity ("I'm not really a baker") and Rose's risk narrative ("you're always starting over") as instances of character-erosion.
- Sennett's closing claim (attributed): "a regime which provides human beings no deep reasons to care about one another cannot long preserve its legitimacy."

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics charge that Sennett's "character" trades on nostalgia — treating a historically brief, unionized, male-breadwinner stability as a natural baseline — and that its evidence base is a handful of vivid but unrepresentative vignettes rather than systematic data (see [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]]). The construct also risks conflating the *loss of a particular institutional order* with the *loss of character as such*. Defenders reply that the concept names a real and measurable narrative dimension of selfhood that survey and labor-market data leave invisible.
