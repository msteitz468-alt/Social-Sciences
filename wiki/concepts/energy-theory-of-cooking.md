# Energy Theory of Cooking

*(Attributed — Wrangham 2009, Ch. 3.)* Claim that cooking's primary biological importance is **net energy**: gelatinization of starch, denaturation of protein, and softening that cuts digestive cost consistently increase usable calories from plant and animal foods — contrary to nutrient tables that often treat cooking's energy effect as zero on average.

## Definition as Coined

Wrangham argues familiar advantages of cooking (safety, taste, reduced spoilage, opening tough foods) are real but secondary to energy gain. He frames the claim as scientifically "new" relative to both popular nutrition and earlier cooking-history advocates who lacked a nutritional mechanism.

## Semantic History

Intervenes in contradictory nutritionist claims (cooking does nothing / raises / lowers caloric value). Targets Atwater-system food tables (USDA; McCance & Widdowson) that, on Wrangham's reading, report zero average cooking effect on energy while acknowledging water and vitamin changes. Epilogue extends the critique: Atwater factors miss digestive cost and variable digestibility; soft processed food contributes to modern obesity.

## Operationalizations

*(As Wrangham reports experimental and clinical findings.)*

| Mechanism | Evidence cited |
|---|---|
| Starch gelatinization | Ileal digestibility cooked starch ≥95% vs raw wheat 71%, potato 51%, plantain 48%; GI rises with cooking |
| Protein denaturation | Belgian egg studies: cooked protein ~91–94% vs raw ~51–65% ileal; BSA trypsin digestion 4× after cooking |
| Softness / digestive cost | Beaumont–St. Martin fistula observations; Oka soft-pellet rats heavier/fatter at equal intake; Secor pythons: grinding −12.3%, cooking −12.7%, both −23.4% digestion cost |
| Non-heat denaturation | Acid, salt, drying (marinades, jerky, fruit–meat mixes) as partial analogues |

## Applications

- Mechanistic core of [[cooking-hypothesis]] and [[obligate-cooked-diet]].
- Underwrites time-budget claims: soft food → fewer chews → ~four hours/day saved vs great-ape-like raw diets.
- Bridges to [[expensive-tissue-hypothesis]]: extra net energy funds gut reduction and brain.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Wrangham acknowledges Maillard products, vitamin losses, and dripping losses but subordinates them to net energy gain — a weighing others may reverse for specific micronutrients.
- Animal models (rats, pythons) and modern clinical ileal studies are analogs, not direct Pleistocene measurements.
- Contested among nutrition scientists; keep as Wrangham's program claim.
