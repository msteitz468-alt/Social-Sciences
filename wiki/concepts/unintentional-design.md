# Unintentional Design

## Definition as Coined

In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]* (Part III), [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] uses **unintentional design** for the patterned but unplanned outcomes of multi-species histories — forests and patches that work without being the product of a single designer's will. Related figures in the same arc: **resurgence** (life coming back in blasted or simplified landscapes), **serendipity** of historical conjuncture (e.g. fire exclusion creating Oregon lodgepole–matsutake timing), and **disturbance** as condition of possibility rather than only damage *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Semantic History

_(from Tsing 2015)_ Pushes against both romantic wilderness (nature without humans) and fully planned industrial forestry. Pines, fungi, and people appear as historical actors in polyphonic assemblages. In the Spore Trail, Tsing also likens intellectual life itself to peasant woodland / unintentional design *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Operationalizations

- Japanese red pine–matsutake under peasant deforestation and later satoyama volunteer work.
- Oregon Cascades: industrial timber → cutover → fire exclusion → lodgepole → matsutake flush as unintended historical product.
- Contrast with scalability projects (plantations, scientific forestry that "stops history") that try to erase such emergent design *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Applications

- [[tsing-mushroom-study]] · [[patchiness]] · Japanese woodland restoration ("matsutake crusaders") in the same book

## Critiques and Limitations

Must not erase power: unintentional is not apolitical. Industrial design, state forest policy, and abandonment still structure the field of possibilities; resurgence is not redemption *(Tsing 2015)*.
