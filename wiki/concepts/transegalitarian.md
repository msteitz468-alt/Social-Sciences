# Transegalitarian Society

## Definition as Coined

In late-twentieth-century archaeology (notably Brian Hayden and related literature; used programmatically by [[bogucki-peter|Peter Bogucki]] in *[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|The Origins of Human Society]]*, 1999), a **transegalitarian** society is one that has moved beyond the strong egalitarian norms of [[pleistocene-band-society|Pleistocene band]] / classic forager organization but has **not** yet crystallized into hereditary elite/commoner systems (chiefdoms or stratified societies). Inequality is present, often unstable and competitive, and frequently organized around ambitious households and [[aggrandizer|aggrandizers]].

## Semantic History

- **Egalitarian / rank / stratified / state** (Service, Fried) left a conceptual gap for late foragers and early farmers who show competitive prestige, feasting, and household differentiation without permanent hereditary offices.
- **Transegalitarian** fills that gap as an etic middle category — "trans-" signaling transition or intermediate position, not a single ethnographic type.
- Bogucki (1999, Ch.6) makes the category central to explaining pathways from post-glacial foragers and early agriculturalists toward institutionalized inequality.

## Operationalizations

Bogucki (1999, attributed) ties transegalitarian dynamics to:

- **Households** as primary units of production, consumption, and decision-making under risk
- **Aggrandizers** who seek status, labor, and wealth
- A larger demographic/economic segment that **drops behind** (the synergy he argues is under-theorized by pure "prime mover" trade/irrigation/craft models)

Archaeological proxies commonly invoked in this literature (attributed, not wiki-voice identity): storage, competitive feasting facilities, exotic prestige goods, burial differentiation, household size/wealth variation.

## Applications

- Post-[[natufian-culture|Natufian]] and early Neolithic Southwest Asia
- Late Neolithic Europe
- Hohokam and other North American cases treated in Bogucki Ch.6
- Bridge to [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]] and [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]]

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Category vs stage:** risk of smuggling unilinear evolution back in via a named intermediate "stage."
- **Ethnographic analogy:** modern competitive forager/horticultural cases are not fossil Pleistocene–early Holocene societies; analogy must be flagged.
- **Date-stamp:** Bogucki's regional cases are as of 1999 research.
