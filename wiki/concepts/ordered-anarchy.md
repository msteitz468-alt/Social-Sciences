# Ordered Anarchy

> Coined in [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]] for acephalous political order; foundational to political anthropology. See [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

Evans-Pritchard's term (attributed) for a society that is *stateless yet orderly*: "the Nuer have no government, and their state might be described as an ordered anarchy." It is "an acephalous kinship state" — lacking "governmental organs... legal institutions... developed leadership... organized political life," yet not chaotic, because order is produced structurally rather than administratively. "In Nuerland legislative, judicial, and executive functions are not invested in any persons or councils"; there was, he reported, no court and "in a strict sense Nuer have no law." He "lived in intimacy for a year with Nuer and never heard a case brought before an individual or a tribunal of any kind."

Order rested instead on: **balanced [[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary opposition]]** (fission/fusion keeping groups in check); **self-help** backed by public opinion ("the club and the spear are the sanctions of rights"; "what chiefly makes people pay compensation is fear that the injured man and his kin may take to violence"); the **[[feud]]** as regulated reparation; and the ritual mediation of the **[[leopard-skin-chief]]**. "The larger the segment involved the greater the anarchy that prevails" — cohesion increases as the community narrows.

## Semantic History

"Ordered anarchy" became a keyword of the anthropology of stateless societies, generalized (with Fortes, *African Political Systems*, 1940) into the type of the acephalous/segmentary polity, contrasted with centralized "primitive states." It anticipates later interest across anthropology and political science in **order without the state** (stateless dispute-resolution, self-enforcing norms). Evans-Pritchard tied Nuer statelessness to a democratic, egalitarian ethos: they were "deeply democratic," and "it is impossible to live among Nuer and conceive of rulers ruling over them."

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed in the field by the *absence* of institutions (no chief with authority, no court, no legislation) combined with the *presence* of orderly outcomes (feuds settled, compensation paid, raids coordinated) — with the leopard-skin chief and, latterly, the prophets as the only ritual agents of any cross-segment reach.

## Applications

The organizing concept of the [[nuer|Nuer]] case and, through it, of segmentary political anthropology generally; a reference point for the [[feud]], for stateless-order theory, and for debates on how acephalous societies coordinate collective action (raiding, defence) without command.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether "ordered anarchy" describes reality or idealizes it is part of the [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate|segmentary-model debate]]: critics argue the "order" is overstated and the "anarchy" understates real leadership, coercion, and history (prophets, war leaders, colonial disruption). The colonial setting — Evans-Pritchard observed a people *being forcibly brought under a state* — complicates reading their "statelessness" as a stable baseline. Attributed voice throughout.
