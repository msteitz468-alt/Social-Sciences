# Power and Privilege

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* (1966), [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] centers stratification theory on **power** and **privilege** rather than prestige. **Power** (Weber): probability of persons or groups carrying out their will even when opposed; manifests as force and as institutionalized power (office/position and property). **Privilege**: possession or control of a portion of the goods and services constituting the societal surplus (and related rewards). Prestige is treated as largely derivative once a significant surplus exists.

## Semantic History

Both words are long-standing; Lenski’s contribution is their **pairing as the primary axes** of a comparative theory that demotes prestige-centered stratification research. Dialogues with elite theory, Marxism, and functionalism without equating power solely with property (cf. [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] on authority; [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] on the [[power-elite]]).

## Operationalizations

Power classes as aggregations sharing a similar position with respect to force or a form of institutionalized power; privilege read from control of surplus, land, offices, and incomes in each society type.

## Applications

Title concepts of the 1966 synthesis:

- **Force vs institutionalized power:** force seizes; institutionalized power (position, property; authority and influence) retains and legitimates.
- **Agrarian peak:** proprietary state and governing class concentrate privilege; expendables at the floor.
- **Industrial reversal:** political diffusion and lower elite income shares reduce *correlation* of power and privilege relative to agrarian extremes (attributed; mid-1960s measures); property wealth can remain highly skewed even when income shares compress.
- **Dialogue:** [[power-elite]] / [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]] treated as degree by regime type, not absolute myth-or-fact.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether prestige is truly secondary; measurement of power independent of outcomes; underplaying of cultural and status orders relative to Weber’s multidimensionality (Lenski retains multiple [[class-system-lenski|class systems]] but still privileges power analytically).
