# Five-Year Plan

## Definition as Coined

The **Five-Year Plan** (пятилетка) is the Soviet multi-year planning instrument; the **First Five-Year Plan** (roughly 1928/29–1932) marks the break from NEP market regulation toward forced industrialization under physical and political command. [[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] treats planning as latent in Marxist thought and in pre-revolutionary Russian developmentalism (Witte), activated institutionally via Goelro and Gosplan and politically by the post-opposition drive for catch-up industrialization (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 11, 14–15).

## Semantic History

- **Antecedents:** Carr notes planning is not exclusively socialist — any reaction against laissez-faire implies it. Bolsheviks had little time for planning theory in civil war; Lenin was impressed by German wartime "state monopoly capitalism" as material preparation for socialism.
- **Institutional build-up:** Goelro (electrification); Gosplan; "control figures"; post-[[scissors-crisis]] recognition that market instruments alone could not regulate exchange.
- **Genetic vs teleological:** Carr reports planners' debate between extrapolative ("genetic") methods and goal-driven ("teleological") targets; the latter won as tempos accelerated.
- **First FYP:** Optimum drafts under Stalin's post-opposition pressure; emblematic projects (Dneprostroi, Turksib); finance/"dictatorship of finance" dethroned by physical controls and state orders; prestige enhanced when the 1929 world capitalist crash made Soviet planning appear a pioneering model (Carr).
- **Link to agriculture:** Carr argues the plan's food and labour needs made [[collectivization]] the agrarian counterpart of industrial planning — not a side-issue.

## Operationalizations

| Element (Carr) | Role |
|---|---|
| Gosplan control figures / five-year drafts | Quantitative targets for industry and investment |
| Physical vs financial controls | Direct state orders replace credit/monetary steering |
| Means-of-production priority | Heavy industry over consumer goods (linked to [[socialism-in-one-country]] self-sufficiency) |
| Labour intensity | Shock work, discipline, longer/more intensive shifts; productivity via physical effort where machines lag |
| Private-sector share | Carr: private share of national income >50% in 1926–27 → "insignificant" by end of FYP |

## Applications

- Core instrument of Stalin-era "revolution from above" (Carr's phrase for industrial and agrarian transformations).
- International demonstration effect after 1929 — planning as alternative to market crisis.
- Carr rejects calling the planned economy mere [[state-capitalism]]: no entrepreneurs, no free market, no class appropriating surplus in the capitalist sense — "the material, economic half" of socialism (Carr's thesis; contested by others — see [[russian-question-debate]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Carr's short book lacks source apparatus; tempos, famine linkage, and human cost are treated more fully in specialist literature and later chapters of his large history. Opposition and Right (Bukharin) critiques of over-ambitious tempos appear in Carr as defeated positions. Do not treat plan targets as achieved outcomes without independent verification.
