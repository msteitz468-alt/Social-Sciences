# Adolescence

## Definition as Coined

In early twentieth-century psychology, **adolescence** named the years around puberty as a distinct developmental stage. G. Stanley Hall's *Adolescence* (1904) cast it as an inevitable period of idealism, rebellion, and emotional storm. [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] retained the stage label but challenged the inevitability of its emotional content.

## Semantic History

- **Hall (1904):** biological-psychological universal — [[storm-and-stress]] as destiny of the stage.
- **Mead (1928):** asked whether difficulties belong to "being adolescent or to being adolescent in America"; used Manu'a ethnography to argue for cultural variability of the stage's experience (*[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]*).
- **Later:** youth studies, life-course sociology, and psychological anthropology rework the category; the Mead–Freeman afterlife reopened whether Mead's Samoan adolescence was accurately described ([[mead-freeman-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Mead operationalized via **girls' life histories** (~fifty girls, three Ta'ū villages): household position, work, sex experience, conflict cases, comparison with American educational pressures. Not a psychometric battery; ethnographic and comparative.

## Applications

Type-case for [[culture-and-personality]]; staple of education and parenting debates; teaching example of Boasian natural-experiment logic.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether "adolescence" is a portable etic stage or a Western historical construct remains contested; Mead's specific Samoan portrait is reanalyzed-contested. Always date-stamp claims to fieldwork/publication moment.
