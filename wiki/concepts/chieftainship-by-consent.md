# Chieftainship by Consent

## Definition as Coined

**Chieftainship by consent** names [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] account, from his [[nambikwara|Nambikwara]] fieldwork (1938) in *[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]* (1955), of a form of leadership that rests on **consent, not coercion**: "consent is at the origin of power, and it is also consent that maintains its legitimacy." The Nambikwara chief has no coercive power; he can act only by persuading the band to share his view, and holds his position by continuous **generosity** — he must possess a surplus of food, tools, and ornaments to redistribute on demand.

## Semantic History

Lévi-Strauss glossed the Nambikwara word for chief, *uilikandé*, as "he who unites/binds together," arguing the chief is the *cause* of the group's desire to constitute itself as a group, not the effect of a pre-existing group's need for authority. He explicitly framed the case as a contribution to political philosophy: it supports Rousseau's intuition that consent and contract are "the raw materials of social life," against Hume's view that they are secondary; and it refutes, he argued, the Freudian "primal father" theory of the origin of leadership.

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by: non-hereditary succession subject to the sounding of public opinion (a designated successor may refuse); leadership by persuasion; obligatory redistribution (a chief left no wealthier than his band); and the chief's **polygyny** as both a reward and a functional exchange — the group cedes monogamous security for the collective security an empowered, resourced chief provides. Lévi-Strauss compared this trade-off to modern national insurance (the Beveridge Plan).

## Applications

A foundational text of [[political-anthropology|political anthropology's]] treatment of "leadership without the state." It anticipates later formulations of the **big-man** and of consensual/acephalous leadership, and of the chief as a "banker" through whom wealth passes without being owned (Lévi-Strauss's own image for the [[bororo|Bororo]] chief).

## Critiques and Limitations

The account rests on a single small band observed briefly in 1938 during demographic collapse; it is as much political philosophy (Rousseau, Montaigne, whom Lévi-Strauss invokes) as ethnography, and should be read as attributed interpretation. Later political anthropology (Fried, Service, Sahlins, Clastres) refined and disputed the evolutionary place of such leadership.
