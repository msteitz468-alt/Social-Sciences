# Fund of Rent

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] defines **rent** as a permanent charge paid because of some superior claim to the cultivator's labor on the land — in labor, produce, or money. Where someone exercises effective superior power or **domain** over a cultivator, the cultivator must produce a **fund of rent**. This production **critically distinguishes the peasant from the primitive cultivator**: it is spurred by a social order in which power demands payments and transfers wealth; the peasant's loss is the power-holder's gain — the fund of rent is part of the fund of power controllers may draw on.

## Semantic History

Classical "rent" (Ricardo, Marx, Maine) re-specified as a **fund category** in the peasant household budget alongside caloric minimum, replacement, and ceremonial. Ties to Weberian domain (patrimonial, prebendary) and Polanyi's critique of land as commodity under mercantile domain. Distinct from but compatible with Marxian ground-rent and [[surplus-value]] analyses.

## Operationalizations

- Labor dues, produce shares, money rent, taxes, usury-linked transfers.
- Organized by [[types-of-domain]]: patrimonial, prebendary, mercantile, administrative (modern state extensions).
- Weakening of rent liens enables production-increase strategies and the rise of rich peasants / yeomen / kulaks ([[peasant-dilemma]]).
- **Many kinds of peasantry:** many ways rent is produced and siphoned → structural variety, not one peasant type.

## Exemplars (Wolf 1966)

- **Mecklenburg:** after seed/feed, 4,000 lb remained; 2,700 lb dues to the lord (more than half effective yield); only 1,300 lb for household (~1,600 kcal per capita daily) — cultivator forced to garden/livestock for calories.

## Applications

- Comparative profiles of agrarian political economies by domain mix.
- Peasant dilemma dynamics when rent claims intensify or collapse.
- Bridge to [[peasant-movements]] as latent opposition to surplus siphoning.

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Rent" as umbrella can collapse tax, tithe, merchant profit, and feudal dues — Wolf separates domain types partly to avoid that.
- State vs private claimants differ; primer emphasizes structural transfer more than legal form.
