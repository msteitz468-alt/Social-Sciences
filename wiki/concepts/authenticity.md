# Authenticity (commodity and cultural)

## Definition as Coined

This page densifies **authenticity** as treated in Brian Spooner’s "Weavers and dealers: the authenticity of an oriental carpet" (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986). Spooner **inverts common sense**: authenticity is not an intrinsic property discovered by connoisseurs but a **social construct** produced along the path from weavers to dealers to consumers, entangled with imperfect [[commoditization]] and Kopytoff-style resingularization *(Spooner)*.

Medieval relics (Geary in the same volume) similarly require **authentication** (identity of the saint, efficacy, *inventio* narratives) for value — a parallel politics of knowledge *(Geary)*.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Art/antiquities markets | Provenance, period, “genuine” vs fake |
| Spooner 1986 | Oriental carpet: dealer lore vs production knowledge; cultural distance; ethnicity as commodity |
| Geary 1986 | Relic authenticity = identity + veneration + efficacy of *this* body |
| Later heritage/tourism studies | UNESCO, staged authenticity — not yet primary-ingested |

## Operationalizations

- **Knowledge asymmetry:** producers’ technical knowledge vs dealers’/collectors’ authenticity discourse *(Spooner)*.
- **Path dependence:** object biography and market position jointly make “authentic.”
- **Industrial clones:** mass products that fail authenticity tests of the craft regime.
- **Relic parallel:** transfer breaches meaning; value reconstructed by narrative and miracle *(Geary)*.

## Applications

- Turkmen/Baluch weaving and Western carpet markets *(Spooner)*.
- Relic gift, *furta sacra*, and sale *(Geary)*.
- Appadurai: politics of value includes “authenticity and authentication” among demand-side instruments *(Appadurai)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Authenticity talk can naturalize elite taste and exoticize producers. Spooner’s inversion is a thesis for carpet markets, not a universal law of all goods.
