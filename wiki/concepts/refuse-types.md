# Refuse Types

Schiffer’s typology of how materials leave use and enter (or re-enter) deposits — load-bearing vocabulary for reading activity areas without assuming a [[pompeii-premise|Pompeii]] freeze. Source: *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes of the Archaeological Record]]* (1987), mainly Ch. 4 (attributed throughout).

## Definition as Coined

**Cultural deposition** transforms artifacts from [[systemic-context|systemic]] to [[archaeological-context|archaeological]] context via discard, loss, abandonment, mortuary disposal, and caching (attributed, Schiffer 1987, citing Willey and McGimsey 1954). Within discard/abandonment, Schiffer distinguishes:

| Type | Definition (attributed, Schiffer) |
|---|---|
| **Primary refuse** | Artifacts discarded **at** their locations of use |
| **Secondary refuse** | Artifacts discarded **elsewhere** (even adjacent to the activity area) |
| **Residual primary refuse** | Small items left behind after maintenance of activity areas (**McKellar Principle**) |
| **De facto refuse** | Still usable (or reusable) tools, facilities, structures left when an activity area is **abandoned** |
| **Provisional refuse** | Temporary storage of discards pending later disposal |
| **Abandonment-stage refuse** | Discard under relaxed maintenance when departure is anticipated |
| **In-transit refuse** | Discard along paths/trails |
| **Abandonment caches** | Specialized de facto refuse stored for anticipated return |

**Curate behavior** (Binford’s term, used by Schiffer): removing still-usable items from an abandoned area for use elsewhere — **donor curate set** vs **founding curate set** at the destination (attributed).

## Semantic History

Primary/secondary refuse (Schiffer 1972) became classroom standard. De facto refuse and curate behavior link Schiffer’s deposition chapter to Binford’s mobile-forager ethnoarchaeology. Later practice multiplies subtypes (clutter, child’s play, ritual dumps) without abandoning the core primary/secondary/de facto cut.

## Operationalizations

- **Population/intensity hypothesis:** larger, more intensively occupied activity areas → higher secondary:primary refuse ratio (attributed, Schiffer 1976a/1987).
- **McKellar Principle:** regularly maintained areas still leave small residual primary refuse — directs flotation, fine mesh, attention to microdebitage and tiny sherds (attributed, McKellar 1983 campus study).
- **Clarke Effect:** variety of discarded types tends to increase with occupation span (attributed) — confounds functional classification from surface collections.
- **Schlepp Effect** (Daly): large animals more thoroughly processed at kill sites → more primary refuse there; analogous short waste streams at quarries/workshops (often secondary refuse after maintenance, not primary).
- Abandonment mode: rapid/unplanned → more de facto refuse; gradual planned → curation, draw-down, room reuse as dumps (Grasshopper, Reid; attributed).

## Applications

Activity-area inference; pueblo floor assemblages; survey surface collections; ethnoarchaeology of camps and compounds; critique of Hill’s Broken K room-function maps ([[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]); Hohokam house floors and de facto vessels as chronological samples.

## Critiques and Limitations

Sullivan noted primary/secondary do not exhaust possibilities. “Primary” can be stretched to manufacture/refurbish loci “within the spirit” of the concept (Keeley; Schiffer). Field deposits are usually mixed bags of processes (Schiffer 1987 Ch. 10) — typology is analytical, not a guarantee that a floor equals one refuse type.
