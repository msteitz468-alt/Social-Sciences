# Polygenism

**Polygenism** is the doctrine that the human races originated as separate species or lineages — that mankind is not one but several biological species — as against **monogenism**, the doctrine (orthodox in Christian tradition and defended by [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]]'s [[ethnology]]) that all humans descend from a single origin. In [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking's account]] the polygenism–monogenism dispute was the axis along which early-nineteenth-century racial science organized itself, and its transformation by [[darwin-charles|Darwin]] was central to the emergence of evolutionary anthropology. This page is written in **attributed / position** voice: the racial-species claims below are recorded as historical positions, held and contested, never as findings — modern population genetics establishes the biological unity of *Homo sapiens* and treats these nineteenth-century racial typologies as scientifically defunct.

## Definition as Coined

The polygenist impulse in European thought is old — Stocking traces it (attributed) at least to Paracelsus, and to **Isaac de La Peyrère's** *Praeadamitae* (1655), which argued the plural origin of mankind, the greater antiquity of gentile peoples, and a merely local Noachian Flood (La Peyrère was forced to abjure both the theory and his Calvinism). It ran against Christian orthodoxy, so was more openly held in France than in evangelical Britain. Monogenism was the default assumption of emergent physical anthropology around 1800, even among writers (Charles White; Blumenbach's five-race scheme) whose typologies pointed toward hierarchy.

## Semantic History

- **Pre-Darwinian hardening (1800–1859).** Stocking documents a growing inegalitarian, hereditarian, polygenist-leaning physical anthropology: in France (Bory de Saint-Vincent, Virey, William Edwards; later **Paul Broca**, who remodeled anthropology on anatomical lines), in America (**Samuel Morton**'s craniometry), and in Britain (**Robert Knox**, *The Races of Men*, 1850 — "race is everything in human history"; **J. B. Davis and Thurnam**, *Crania Britannica*, on Morton's axiom that head form was "not transmutable"; **John Beddoe**'s "index of nigrescence"). Physicist Rudolf Wagner observed that on the eve of the *Origin*, single-pair descent was widely thought "perfectly antiquated."
- **The Darwinian transformation (1859–).** [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking]] argues (attributed) that Darwinian evolution changed the terms of the dispute rather than simply vindicating monogenism: it produced what looked like a "new monogenism" but was, as [[huxley-thomas-henry|Huxley]] put it, a reconciliation "of all that is good in the Monogenistic and Polygenistic schools" — a common origin so remote that the races had had time to diverge substantially (see [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]). Even Darwin conceded a naturalist meeting Negro and European specimens "for the first time" would likely call them "good and true species." In *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|Descent of Man]]* ch. VII he stages that polygenist-style case (constancy, zoological provinces, distinct lice, alleged hybrid infertility) only to defeat it: fusion, variability, graduation, and nearly valueless sterility evidence support **sub-species** rank and evolutionary [[monogenism]]; alleged race-cross sterility is “almost valueless.”
- **The institutional face.** In Britain the dispute took organizational form in the [[victorian-ethnology-anthropology-schism-debate|schism]] between the monogenist [[ethnology|Ethnological Society]] and [[hunt-james|James Hunt]]'s polygenist Anthropological Society of London (1863).

## Operationalizations

Polygenist argument rested on craniometry (Retzius's cephalic index; cranial-capacity comparison), ancient Egyptian racial imagery read as evidence of fixed types, differential tropical mortality among whites, and claims — later judged "almost valueless" even by Darwin — of limited fertility between "racial" crosses (Broca's "eugenesic" hybridity test, applied e.g. to Tasmanian Aboriginal people; see [[race]]).

## Applications

The doctrine underwrote hard hereditarian racism (Knox, Hunt) and colonial-era racial hierarchy. Stocking stresses its entanglement with the [[ethnology]]/anthropology institutional split and its partial absorption, post-Darwin, into evolutionary racialism — which explained racial hierarchy as an outcome of development in time rather than of separate creation.

## Persistence after Darwin (Stocking 1968)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race,
Culture, and Evolution]]*, essay 3, "The Persistence of Polygenist Thought in Post-Darwinian
Anthropology.")* Stocking argues polygenist thinking did not die with Darwin's *Origin*
(1859) but survived by adopting evolutionary vocabulary. Alfred Russel Wallace (1864)
reconciled Darwinism with polygenism by holding that races differentiated *before* acquiring
full human intellect, after which race types became static; James Hunt refused to apply
Darwinism to anthropology "until we can free the subject from the unity hypothesis." Within
physical anthropology the polygenist-descended tradition ran through Broca and
Paul Topinard (who, unable to find any "pure race," retreated to race as a
statistical "type") to William Z. Ripley's *Races of Europe* and Carlos Closson's cephalic-
index "anthroposociology," culminating in **Madison Grant's *The Passing of the Great Race*
(1916)**, which treated human races as separate biological species with "Mendelism" as a
superficial trapping over pre-Darwinian polygenist roots. Stocking presents this survival as
a central component of the "total pattern" of racial thought that [[boas-franz|Boas]] attacked
in *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*. These are documented
period positions, not findings.

## Critiques and Limitations

Monogenists (Prichard) answered polygenism with philological and environmentalist arguments for a single origin; the [[antiquity-of-man|revolution in human time]] paradoxically strengthened the *biological* case for monogenism (allowing time for divergence) while weakening ethnology's *historical* argument. The modern verdict — the biological unity of the human species and the incoherence of fixed racial "types" — was consolidated by Boasian anthropology (see [[boas-franz]], [[race-and-culture-debate]]) and later population genetics. Polygenism survives only as a documented episode in the history of scientific racism.
