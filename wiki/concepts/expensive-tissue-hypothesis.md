# Expensive-Tissue Hypothesis

*(Aiello & Wheeler 1995; as used and revised by Wrangham in *[[wrangham-catching-fire-2009|Catching Fire]]*.)* Trade-off model: brains are metabolically costly; a reduction in another expensive tissue — especially gut — can free energy for encephalization if diet quality rises enough to support a smaller digestive tract.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — as Wrangham reports Aiello & Wheeler.)* Primates (and humans) face a metabolic budget. Expanding brain share of resting metabolism requires either raising total BMR or cutting other expensive organs. Human guts are small relative to body size for a primate; high-quality diet (easy to digest, energy-dense) makes that reduction viable. Wrangham reports Aiello & Wheeler estimated gut reduction saves at least ~10% of daily energy requirement, available for brain tissue.

## Semantic History

Originates in 1990s biological anthropology as an alternative/complement to pure "meat made us smart" stories: **diet quality** (not meat alone as magic) is the intermediate variable. Wrangham (2009) accepts the trade-off but **revises the chronological assignment**: meat (plus nonthermal processing) for the habiline brain rise; **cooking** for the *Homo erectus* expensive-tissue / encephalization package. Later literature (not ingested) has tested and contested which tissues trade off and how strongly.

## Operationalizations

- Comparative organ-mass residuals (gut vs brain) across primates.
- Diet-quality indices correlated with relative gut size.
- Fossil proxies: dental reduction, ribcage/torso shape (australopithecine large-gut morphology vs human), endocranial volume series as Wrangham cites (~350–400 → 450 → 612 → 870 → 950 → 1200 → 1400 cc across stages).

## Applications

- Load-bearing for [[cooking-hypothesis]]: cooking as the diet-quality jump that pays for *erectus* brains and small guts.
- Dialogue with [[hunting-hypothesis]]: meat can raise diet quality but Wrangham argues it does not alone explain *erectus* masticatory reduction or the full energy package.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Assignment of which dietary change (meat, USOs, cooking, all of the above) funds which brain jump is model-dependent — Wrangham's two-step split is a position, not consensus.
- Expensive-tissue trade-offs have been reanalyzed in the broader literature (not primary-ingested here); keep Aiello & Wheeler and Wrangham's use carefully attributed.
- Brain-size series and species attributions follow Wrangham's popular summary, not a primary paleontological ingest.
