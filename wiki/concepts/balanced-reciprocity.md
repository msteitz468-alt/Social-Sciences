# Balanced Reciprocity

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 5, 1972.)* **Balanced reciprocity** is the midpoint of the [[reciprocity]] continuum: **direct exchange in which the reciprocation is the customary equivalent of the thing received, without delay** — or, more loosely, returns of commensurate worth within a finite and narrow period. It is "less personal" than [[generalized-reciprocity]] and, in Sahlins' phrase, "more economic": "social relations hinge on the material flow," so the relation breaks down if the return does not come within the expected time. Type-cases include much "gift-exchange" and "trade," marriage prestations, friendship compacts, peace agreements, and "primitive money."

## Semantic History

The term is Sahlins' fixed midpoint between Malinowski's "pure gift" and outright barter/seizure. It absorbs Mauss's account of the gift as a **social compact** — Sahlins quotes Mauss that in archaic societies groups meeting must "move away, or... resort to arms; or else... come to terms." Balanced reciprocity is the coming-to-terms.

## Operationalizations

Diagnostic: **intolerance of one-way flow.** Sahlins locates balanced reciprocity in the intermediate kinship-residential sectors and, above all, at social peripheries where economic interdependence must be secured against the pull toward [[negative-reciprocity]] — via trade-partnerships, trade-friendships, and silent trade. He identifies four "contracts" typically struck through balanced exchange: (a) formal friendship/kinship pacts, (b) affirmation of corporate alliances (inter-community feasts), (c) peace-making (wergild, feud settlement), and (d) marital alliance.

## Applications

- **Marriage and alliance:** Sahlins qualifies the "balanced" label — marital prestations are often *asymmetrical in quality* (women against cattle; *oloa* against *toga*), and this imbalance is functional: the "shadow of indebtedness" keeps an alliance alive, whereas a perfectly settled exchange would cancel the debt and let the parties part.
- **Primitive money:** Sahlins hypothesizes (after Firth) that token-value money as a medium of exchange is associated specifically with **"tribal"-level** economies marked by heavy balanced exchange in peripheral sectors (western/central Melanesia, aboriginal California), and is suppressed in chiefdoms, which internalize and centralize exchange.
- **[[exchange|Primitive trade]]:** in ch. 6, balanced exchange at customary rates is secured by "the diplomacy of economic good measure" between trade partners — see [[exchange]].

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Sahlins raises but leaves open whether balanced reciprocity is **inherently unstable**: sustained honorable dealing can build trust and slide toward generalized reciprocity, while a single reneged exchange can collapse into hostility. The category is an ideal-type midpoint on a continuum; empirical exchanges grade between the three forms rather than sitting exactly on them. Stated in attributed voice.
