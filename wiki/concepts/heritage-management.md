# Heritage Management

**Heritage management** is the organized stewardship of archaeological and cultural remains — sites, landscapes, monuments, collections, and associated meanings — under legal, institutional, and public pressure. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 treat it as the forward-looking face of archaeology: the past is not only researched but **conserved, contested, commercialized, and destroyed**, so management decisions are ethical and political as well as technical. The operational arm in development contexts is often [[cultural-resource-management]]; the engagement frame is [[public-archaeology]]. Its confrontation with destruction — the [[illicit-antiquities-trade]] in [[unprovenanced-antiquities]], and [[restitution]] under [[cultural-property]] law — is treated in depth via [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Renfrew 2000]] and [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

No single coiner. In Renfrew & Bahn’s usage, heritage management covers:

1. **Protective legislation** and guardianship of monuments and landscapes.
2. **Mitigation** when development cannot avoid remains (survey → conservation → salvage).
3. **Museum and collection ethics** (acquisition, display, repatriation).
4. **International instruments** (UNESCO 1970 illicit-traffic Convention; 1972 World Heritage Convention).
5. **Responses to destruction** — construction, agriculture, war, looting, and ideological targeting (Bamiyan; Ayodhya).

The textbook’s English institutional landmark for commercial “Heritage” branding is the 1983 repackaging of the Historic Buildings and Monuments Commission as **English Heritage**.

## Semantic History

- **19th–early 20th c.:** National monument schedules (England 1882; US Antiquities Act 1906) protect selected sites as public goods.
- **Post-WWII:** Newly independent states assert control over heritage; repatriation and excavation-consent questions displace colonial collecting assumptions.
- **1960s–90s:** US NHPA/NEPA stack and analogous planning regimes create industrial-scale CRM; NAGPRA (1990) embeds descendant rights.
- **Late 20th–21st c.:** “The Heritage” as tourism product; World Heritage listing as prestige/incentive; simultaneous acceleration of destruction (“more ancient remains lost in the last two decades than ever before,” Renfrew & Bahn 2012, attributed).
- Management scale shifts from **isolated sites** to **entire landscapes** and traditional cultural properties.

## Applications

- **Urban salvage → monument:** Mexico City Great Temple; York waterfront archaeology.
- **Anti-looting:** Sipán rescue; Mimbres Foundation education and purchase; criminal cases (Schultz 2003; UK 2003 Cultural Objects Act).
- **Portable finds:** Frome hoard left in situ for excavation after PAS notification.
- **War damage response:** Mostar bridge rebuild; Baghdad museum looting 2003; Cairo 2011 — textbook stress on prior warnings ignored.
- **Identity politics:** FYR Macedonia Vergina emblem conflict; Zimbabwe ethnonym from ruins; Maoist reframing of Zhoukoudian labor origins.

## Critiques and Limitations

- “Preservation” law often **clears** destruction after documentation rather than saving sites.
- Looting for the art market destroys [[provenience|matrix, provenience, and association]] — a looted vase is an attractive object but poor evidence (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 2, attributed).
- Collectors and some museums are major destruction agents by funding illicit excavation (“collectors are the real looters” — Renfrew-side position).
- Counter-position ([[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Cuno 2008]]): retentionist export/ownership law is poor site protection; better tools are scientific excavation, restored [[partage]], Merryman's preservation–knowledge–access triad, and [[encyclopedic-museum|encyclopedic museums]] with loans/exchanges — filed at [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].
- Tourism funds archaeology yet wears sites and risks Disneyfication.
- Who manages and interprets the past is skewed by gender, colonial history, linguistic hegemony, and majority–minority politics within nations, not only Europe-vs-Other.
- International regimes lack direct enforcement; nation-state law remains primary (Cuno stresses CPIA/CPAC and bilateral MOUs as the real U.S. teeth behind UNESCO 1970).

## Recency, Contested Designation, and Managed Silence (Schofield & Johnson 2006)

Schofield & Johnson extend heritage management to the "recent and contemporary past," broadly AD 1900–present ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch.7; see [[cultural-resource-management]] for the fuller UK/US institutional contrast). Designation decisions on very recent sites are recurrently contested by recency alone: England's Greenham Common Cold War missile-alert facility (GAMA) was scheduled less than ten years after decommissioning; a Twyford Down roads-protest monolith was refused protection despite an English Heritage recommendation. Documentary and aerial-photograph survey found only 14 of 242 WWII radar stations and 10 of 981 heavy anti-aircraft sites surviving intact in England, prompting the chapter's call for a broader strategy against loss of 20th-century material culture.

At the Nevada Test Site (928 nuclear tests, 1951–1992), a Desert Research Institute archaeological programme (from 1978) and the resulting Atomic Testing Museum in Las Vegas (from 1998) illustrate heritage management applied to Cold War weapons infrastructure — treated by the chapter as a case where "Cold War archaeology" only became institutionally indispensable in the 1990s.

Denis Byrne's account of post-1965-coup Bali (Byrne 1999, attributed) is used to argue that heritage management premised on material survival can fail where "material traces of a painful past are almost entirely absent" — there, individual and community memory alone preserved an account of events subversive to the official version, constituting what Byrne calls "noise" in an officially imposed silence. A leaked internal South African Heritage Resources Agency (SAHRA) memo documenting developer pressure on heritage decision-making is discussed in the same volume in connection with the Prestwich Street burial-ground dispute in Cape Town — see [[prestwich-street-debate]] for the full contested case.

## Heritage as "Emergencies of the Moment" and Weaponized Destruction (Hall 2000)

[[hall-martin|Martin Hall]], in *[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]* (2000), frames heritage through Raphael Samuel's argument that "memory is historically conditioned, changing colour and shape according to the emergencies of the moment" and is "inherently revisionist," and that heritage itself is "a nomadic term, which travels easily... capricious enough to accommodate widely discrepant meanings" (Samuel 1994). [ATTRIBUTED — Samuel 1994, as reported by Hall]

**Apartheid-era South African tercentenaries (1987–88).** Hall reads a cluster of colonial-anniversary celebrations as heritage mobilized to serve contemporary racial politics. The 1987 tercentenary of Paarl was staged with costumed re-enactment of Simon van der Stel's arrival and a buried time capsule. The 1988 celebrations combined four anniversaries — 150 years since the Great Trek and the Voortrekkers' defeat of the Zulu at Blood River, 300 years since Huguenot refugees reached the Cape, and 500 years since Bartolomeu Dias's landing at Mossel Bay — staged, in Hall's account, as "overwhelmingly white affairs" organized "largely as if South Africa's black majority did not exist." The Huguenot tercentenary elided slavery in what Hall calls a "spring amnesia." The official Great Trek commemoration (a replica ox wagon travelling Cape Town to Pretoria) was rivalled by a dissident, more conservative Afrikaner wagon festival led by Carel Boshoff, staged after historian Hans Heese's 1984 genealogical research revealed slave ancestry among leading Conservative Party families — a revelation that provoked threats of libel action despite the archival evidence. The Dias quincentenary re-enactment reserved its landing beach for white bathers (a "coloured" clergyman, Rev. Pieter Klink, was ordered off the beach) and cast Khoikhoi roles with white enthusiasts in blackface, omitting that the historical 1488 landing ended in a skirmish that killed several Khoikhoi men. [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000, drawing on Heese 1984 and contemporary press coverage; see [[district-six]] for the related Cape Town heritage case]

**The Yugoslav wars as heritage weaponized.** Hall treats the destruction of cultural property in the Croatian, Bosnian, and Kosovo wars of the 1990s as heritage management's inverse — heritage claimed and destroyed as an instrument of ethnic conflict. Documented cases include: the shelling of Vukovar's historic precinct and Eltz Manor House/Municipal Museum (1991); the year-long siege and shelling of Dubrovnik's old city and of the 4th-century World Heritage site Diocletian's Palace at Split; the destruction of Mostar's 1566 Stari Most ("old bridge," a UNESCO-recognized symbol of inter-ethnic Bosnian unity) by shelling and demolition in November 1993 (Ozkan 1994; Woodward 1995); the 1992 burning of Sarajevo's National Library and Oriental Institute, destroying 5,263 bound Arabic/Persian/Turkish/Hebrew/Serbo-Croatian-Bosnian manuscripts along with tens of thousands of Ottoman-era documents (Riedlmayer 1995, 1998); and an estimated more than 1,200 Muslim and Croat religious and cultural sites (mosques, historic centers, cemeteries, churches, monasteries) destroyed or damaged across Bosnia-Herzegovina (Riedlmayer 1995). Croatia's Museum Documentation Centre characterized such attacks as targeting "the Croatian National identity" (Šulc 1997); some scholars framed the scale of destruction as "assassination of culture" under the Genocide Convention rather than incidental "collateral damage" (cited via Hodder 1996) — Hall records this as a live position split rather than an adjudicated verdict. [POSITION — collateral damage vs. cultural genocide/"assassination of culture"; attributed to Riedlmayer 1995/1998, Šulc 1997, Hodder 1996, Woodward 1995]

Hall also documents the 1990s "web war" dimension of this heritage conflict: competing nationalist and diaspora websites (Serbian, Croatian, Bosnian, Albanian, and the Domovina Net Sarajevo solidarity site) each staged rival heritage claims and atrocity narratives online, described by The Economist (15 May 1999) as "the first web war." [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000]

## Smith 2006 — management as governance of meaning ([[authorized-heritage-discourse]])

*(Attributed — [[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* 2006.)*

**Three linked points (Ch. 3).** (1) The AHD, by privileging innate aesthetic/scientific value and physicality, masks heritage’s real cultural-political work of present meaning-making. (2) Technical management/conservation framed by the AHD is itself a **cultural process** that creates value and meaning (part of heritage performance). (3) Heritage management regulates cultural and social value and meaning — including arbitrating [[dissonant-heritage|dissonance]] — while professional attention stays on fabric.

**Charters as authorizing institutions.** UNESCO/ICOMOS conventions and charters define what heritage is, why it is significant, and how it should be managed: **Venice Charter** (conserve as found; expert fellowship; civilization language); **World Heritage Convention** (universality; natural/cultural hierarchy; List imbalance; property); **Burra Charter** (cultural significance and community openings that still retain fabric/expert trusteeship); UNESCO **intangible** instruments (openings that negotiate listing and universality). Management regimes named by region: CRM (US), Archaeological Heritage Management (Europe), Cultural Heritage Management (Australia — under Indigenous critique of “resource” and archaeological privilege).

**Depoliticizing technology.** Expert knowledge sets agendas; wider social debates about the past’s utility for the present are reduced to “bite-sized” disputes over meaning, ownership, or management of specific sites. Treating dissonance as a site-specific problem to be mitigated for “sustainable” heritage resurrects consensual AHD ideals (against Tunbridge & Ashworth-style management framing of dissonance as separable).

**Cases.** Country-house visitation as managed identity performance; Riversleigh WHS as scientific/natural management marginalizing [[waanyi|Waanyi]] country claims; industrial museums and [[castleford|Castleford]] as community management from below; Indigenous politics where the **issue is control** of the heritage process (not only ownership of objects).

## Meskell 2012 — heritage management as a parastatal, and "biodiversity trumps culture" (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[meskell-lynn|Lynn Meskell]], *[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|The Nature of Heritage]]* 2012; South African fieldwork c. 2003–2009. Central case: [[kruger-national-park|Kruger National Park]].)*

Meskell's ethnography documents heritage management as it actually operates inside a **parastatal
conservation agency** ([[kruger-national-park|SANParks]]). Her findings (attributed): across
political regimes, state power devalues the archaeological past relative to nature conservation;
South Africa split heritage across two departments (Arts and Culture; Environmental Affairs and
Tourism) with no unified authority, while **SANParks employed no full-time archaeologist** across 22
parks; and archaeology sits under a "People and Conservation" division aligned with
development/education rather than science, producing recurrent "archaeologies of failure" (the
Masorini reconstruction; abandoned rock-art tourism projects). She reads biodiversity as a **racially
"neutral," cosmopolitan idiom** (via Escobar's "invention of biodiversity" and Agrawal's
**environmentality**) that bypasses the contested politics of cultural heritage and secures
international funding. On the policy side she documents heritage recast serially since 1994 as
burden → asset → burden ("made and unmade"), the "making heritage pay" imperative (Maropeng Visitor
Centre; nine Cabinet "Legacy" projects), and McKinsey-led commercialization ("Operation Prevail").
She offers **New South Wales, Australia** — integrated cultural-heritage management, ~14 archaeologists
and ~80 mostly-Aboriginal heritage staff, the living-significance Burra Charter, post-*Mabo* land
rights — as a comparative model (attributed to Byrne, Brayshaw & Ireland 2001). Concepts introduced:
[[negative-heritage]], [[past-mastering]]. See also [[thulamela]], [[makuleke]].

## Anti-Trafficking and Multi-Stakeholder Conflict (Brodie et al. 2006)

[[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006]] densifies management under market and war pressure:

- **Investigation:** Hicks’s ARPA-based looting-scene protocol and experiential education (U.S.-bound).
- **Landscape loss:** Lydian tumuli metrics; Ulúa Valley mound destruction — management after the fact cannot recover context.
- **Conflict heritage:** Iraq museum and sites 1991–2005; Afghanistan looting + iconoclasm; Hague rules (Prott).
- **Park politics:** Mortensen on [[copan]] — WHS/tourism/descendant/research conflict as management structure, not only "looting."
- **Affect and education:** Tubb (emotion); Lazrus (shared patrimony messaging) as demand-side cultural strategies.
