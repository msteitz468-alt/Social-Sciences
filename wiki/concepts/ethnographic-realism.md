# Ethnographic Realism

The set of literary conventions through which classic ethnographies produce an effect of transparent, total cultural description — omniscient third-person voice, ethnographic present, suppression of the fieldworker's subjectivity, etc. Named as critical object in Marcus & Cushman (1982) and throughout *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* (1986).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to volume contributors / Marcus & Cushman.)*

Realism as rhetorical achievement of transparency, not as automatic correspondence. Clifford: ideology of transparent representation reduced writing to method. Marcus treats Willis *Learning to Labour* as state of the art *within* realist/naturalist conventions while still experimentally useful. ACC retains realist descriptive aims while embracing ironic epistemology.

## Semantic History

From unmarked professional style to named foil of experimental and post-modern ethnography. Distinct from philosophical realism about social facts (Rabinow’s “representations are social facts” is not a defense of monographic realism).

## Operationalizations

Rhetorical checklist of realist monographic devices: omniscient voice, ethnographic present, arrival stories as scientific bookends, politics confined to introduction, pastoral eternalizing, quantification as thoroughness (Rosaldo).

## Applications

Critiques of Evans-Pritchard, Malinowski, Geertzian monologue, Le Roy Ladurie’s novelistic realism, travel/ethnography inheritance (Pratt).

## Critiques and Limitations

Defenders of monographic method treat “realism” as epistemic virtue not mere style; see [[writing-culture-debate]]. Marcus: multi-locale remains ideal largely outside completed realist ethnography of markets.

## Stocking's earlier historicist treatment (attributed — *After Tylor*, 1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*]]; a decade prior to and independent of the *Writing Culture* critique above, though covering the same target text.)*

Stocking's reading of [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* introduction (see [[ethnographic-authority]]) identifies the same realist devices later theorized by Marcus, Cushman, and Clifford — present-tense narration, the "author/reader equation," and a totalizing "Ethnographer" persona — as constitutive of the founding text of the genre, calling the result a "euhemerist myth." He connects this rhetorical construction directly to the coining, in later terminology, of "**the ethnographic present**" — see [[ethnographic-present]].
