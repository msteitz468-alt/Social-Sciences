# Archaeological Culture

The foundational **etic** unit of [[culture-historical-archaeology]]: a recurring package of material traits, not a self-named people and not a biological race. Canonical English definition: [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] 1929 Preface to *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]*.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Childe 1929.)*

> "Such a complex of regularly associated traits we shall term a ‘cultural group’ or just a ‘culture’."

> "We assume that such a complex is the material expression of what would to-day be called a ‘people’."

- **Race** is reserved for cases where the complex is regularly and exclusively associated with a specific skeletal type.
- **Ethnic** = adjective from "people" (≈ German *völkische*).
- Culture is **not necessarily a chronological unit**: it may persist long in one place or arrive late elsewhere.

## Semantic History

Continental archaeology already used *Kultur* / culture-group language before 1929; Childe's Preface is the English-language charter that made the definition portable for Anglophone prehistory. Later processual and post-processual critiques attacked the culture = people equation and the migration/diffusion default. aDNA (e.g. [[corded-ware-culture]], [[bell-beaker-culture]] as of Reich 2018) sometimes supports demographic turnover under culture labels and sometimes shows material packages spreading without matching ancestry — vindicating Childe's own warning that culture ≠ race while complicating culture = people.

## Operationalizations

| Inference (Childe 1929) | Rule |
|---|---|
| Movement of people | Near-complete transfer of the trait complex over a wide area |
| Relation between cultures | One or more elements reappear in a different context |
| Survivals | Shared traits from an earlier overlaid culture (time factor) |
| Influences | Juxtaposed cultures, survival excluded; minimal term (trade, imitation, conquest…) — "often a confession of ignorance," never itself an explanation |
| Accidental agreements | Sporadic foreign types in a culture (trade/imitation), even if occasionally copied |
| Total / ethnic influence | New pottery style or non-obviously superior weapons changing whole habits |
| External relations | Obviously superior devices or fashions among users who already had the habit |
| Sequence (local) | Stratigraphy (or geology) preferred |
| Sequence (relative) | Typology only under strict conditions (multiple correlated series; fixed anchors; within one culture or continuously interrelated area) |
| Absolute chronology | Synchronisms with historically dated Aegean / Egyptian / Mesopotamian phases |

Concrete agreements (a definite implement type, a specific grain) outweigh abstract ones ("polishing of stone," "agriculture").

## Applications

- Every named culture in *Danube* (e.g. [[danubian-i-culture]], [[vinca-culture]], [[aunjetitz-culture]], [[lausitz-culture]], densified [[corded-ware-culture]] / [[bell-beaker-culture]]).
- Continent-scale application in *[[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958|Dawn]]* (6th ed.): mesolithic named industries through Early Bronze Age culture maps; Retrospect restates cultures as assemblages that “should reflect the distinctive behaviour patterns of human societies.”
- Still the default label for pottery/grave packages in European prehistory, even when theoretical programs have moved on.
- **Americanist stream (*[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Willey & Phillips 1958]]*):** prefers **[[archaeological-phase|phase]]** as the practicable everyday unit; reserves “culture” (and “civilization” on Classic/Postclassic stages) for **maximum** segmentations of culture-history — larger than phase, product of integration via [[archaeological-horizon|horizon]] and [[archaeological-tradition|tradition]]. Same etic package logic as Childe, different scalar vocabulary and explicit social-equation cautions.
- **Clarke / New Archaeology stream (*[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* 1968/1978):** [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]] reformed the unit as a **[[polythetic-classification|polythetic]]** material package — "a polythetic set of specific and comprehensive artefact-type categories which consistently recur together in assemblages within a limited geographical area" (attributed, Definitions) — nested between [[archaeological-assemblage|assemblage]] and [[culture-group]] / [[technocomplex]], and explicitly **not** equated with race, tribe, or language. See [[clarke-analytical-archaeology-study]].

## The Critique of the Culture Concept (Lucas 2002)

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]*, 2002) traces the concept's critical afterlife (attributed). He gives
Childe's most explicit statement — the **1935** definition, in which the traits of a culture
"are the creations of a single people… united by common social traditions" — as the clearest
form of the culture = people equation, while noting Childe grew **increasingly cautious**
over his career about identifying a culture with any linguistic, ethnographic, or political
unit. Lucas distinguishes (after Taylor 1948) a **holistic** sense of culture (the generic
human attribute) from the **partitive** sense (a particular bounded group's culture), and
argues post-1960s critique mostly targeted the holistic sense while the partitive sense was
attacked only for its "polythetic"/fuzzy boundaries — a point traceable to Childe himself.

Key critical positions he records:

- **Colin Renfrew (1977):** the bounded "culture group" is a **fiction** generated by the
  archaeologist's arbitrary choice of type site and similarity threshold, not an objective
  entity (paralleling Ford's argument that types are not "real"). Lucas judges that Renfrew
  reformulates rather than overturns bounded-entity thinking.
- **Hodder (1982a):** whether material discontinuity is random or patterned is an *empirical*
  question; markedly discontinuous cultural areas "often do relate to peoples in some
  non-material sense," but interpretation as political, language, or self-conscious tribal
  units "is not necessarily relevant" — the question is what strategies led to boundary
  maintenance.
- **Sian Jones (1997):** culture is *not* equivalent to ethnicity; ethnicity is one
  contextually-expressed dimension of culture (her Romanization case shows pottery and
  architecture "Romanizing" at different rates within one group).
- **Díaz-Andreu (1996):** grouping potentially different groups "under a single name on the
  basis of a common material culture" is a **political decision**, not a discovered fact; she
  urges dropping the partitive culture concept — which Lucas frames as an open provocation
  given the consequences for area/period specialisms.

These reinforce the wiki's four-way-non-identity rule: the material package, the language,
the genetic population, and the self-conscious people are four different things.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Culture = people is an **assumption**, not demonstrated (Childe states it as assumption in 1929; *Dawn* multiplies folk/people narrative agents — see [[pots-and-peoples-debate]]).
- Migration and influence language can underwrite nationalist prehistories (Kossinna problem); Childe distances "race" but retains ethnic readings of total trait transfer.
- Absolute dates and many culture boundaries in 1929/1958 are superseded; the **definition** remains historically load-bearing.
- Four-way non-identity (culture / language / genes / people) must be enforced on every culture page — see Identity Cautions.
- *Dawn* preface notes that not all farmers were potters — undercutting ceramic packages as automatic economic/people proxies.

## Braidwood 1959 — traditions are not cultures

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]*.)* Prehistoric “cultures” are known only fragmentarily from material remains. Stone-tool **traditions** (pebble, core-biface, flake, chopper) are shared manufacturing habits, **not** total cultures or migratory peoples; older parallel-phyla racial-migration readings of Western tool sequences are critiqued. Culture (Redfield: conventional understandings) is broader than any single industry. Complements Childe’s culture definition without equating the two programs. See [[archaeological-assemblage]].
