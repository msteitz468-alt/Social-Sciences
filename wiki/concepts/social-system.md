# Social System

## Definition as Coined

The term "social system" long predates [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] — he credits L.J. Henderson's insistence on the concept of *system* in scientific theory, and Henderson's reading of Pareto — but *The Social System* (1951) gives it its most influential technical definition. Parsons defined it in attributed voice:

> A social system consists in a plurality of individual actors interacting with each other in a situation which has at least a physical or environmental aspect, actors who are motivated in terms of a tendency to the "optimization of gratification" and whose relation to their situations, including each other, is defined and mediated in terms of a system of culturally structured and shared symbols.

For Parsons the social system is one of **three analytically irreducible but interpenetrating action systems** — alongside the personality system and the cultural system. None reduces to the others: "Social systems thus do not 'repress' or 'project,' nor are they 'dominant' or 'submissive'; these are mechanisms of the personality." The three meet at a single point — a [[value-orientation]] pattern that is simultaneously a [[need-disposition]] of the personality system and a [[social-role|role-expectation]] of the social system (Parsons's "transformation formula").

Parsons distinguished a **society** — a social system meeting all [[functional-prerequisites|functional prerequisites]] of long-run self-sufficient persistence, including biological reproduction and [[socialization]] of the next generation — from a **partial social system**, which must be located within a society context.

## Semantic History

The elementary unit of a social system, Parsons argued, is the *act*, but for macroscopic analysis the operative unit is the **status-role** (see [[social-role]]) — "the particle of mechanics," not mass or velocity. Parsons introduced the concept of **double contingency**: in interaction, unlike simple stimulus–response, alter's possible reactions are themselves contingent on ego's action and vice versa; this reciprocal contingency is a precondition for genuine symbolization and thus culture. He defined a **mechanism** as "an empirical generalization about motivational processes stated in terms of its relevance to the functional problems of an action system."

The concept's meaning has drifted sharply since 1951. Under [[structural-functionalism]] "social system" carried strong connotations of boundary maintenance, equilibrium tendency, and normative integration — the very features [[conflict-theory|conflict theorists]] and interactionists later attacked as a conservative reification (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]). Later systems theory (Luhmann) redefined social systems as systems of *communication* rather than of interacting actors, breaking with Parsons's action-theoretic grounding.

## Operationalizations

Parsons's scheme is a conceptual apparatus, not a measurement instrument; it generates typologies (the [[pattern-variables]], the four principal types of social structure) rather than indices. A standing methodological complaint is that "social system" is hard to bound empirically — Turner (1991) records the critical question of whether Great Britain is "one social system or several."

## Applications

- The frame within which Parsons analyzed [[socialization]], [[deviance]], [[social-control]], belief and [[ideology|ideology]], [[expressive-symbols|expressive symbols]], and the [[sick-role|sick role]].
- The organizing concept of postwar [[structural-functionalism]] and, later, of [[social-equilibrium|equilibrium]]-based models of change.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is contested. Critics charged that treating society as a self-equilibrating system built in a **static bias** and a normative-consensus assumption that cannot handle conflict, power, and change (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]). Parsons himself conceded that the persistence of a stabilized system "is a theoretical assumption, not an empirical generalization." The boundary-definition problem (what counts as one social system) remains unresolved.
