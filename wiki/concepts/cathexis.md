# Cathexis (Gender Structure)

In *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987), [[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]] adapts Freud's term **cathexis** (psychic charge or investment) to name one of the **three main structures of gender relations** — alongside **labour** (production / sexual division of labour) and **power** (the overall subordination of women and dominance of men). The **structure of cathexis** is the social organization of desire and emotional attachment as a gendered pattern.

## Definition as Coined

Connell argues that sexual desire is not unstructured impulse but is shaped as **gendered emotional attachment**, applying to heterosexual and homosexual desire alike. The structure of cathexis:

- organizes who is desired, how, and with what emotional investments;
- is multilevelled (individual attachments, couple patterns, cultural erotic codes);
- interacts with labour and power rather than reducing to either (desire is not merely a reflection of economic interest or of domination, though it is shaped by both).

In the later *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* formulation, the three-fold model is restated as power, production, and cathexis; a fourth structure (symbolism) is added in *Gender* (2002) / the *Masculinities* 2nd-edition Afterword.

## Semantic History

Freud used cathexis for libidinal investment in objects; Connell relocates it from individual psychic economy to **social structure** — a move parallel to treating labour and power as structural, not only interactional. The term is less widely cited as a free-standing concept than [[hegemonic-masculinity]] or [[gender-order]], but it is load-bearing inside [[gender-relations-theory]]: without cathexis, the model would reduce gender to work and domination and miss the organization of sexuality that gay liberation and feminist theory made central.

## Operationalizations

Analyzed through patterns of heterosexual couple formation, sexual display that marks sex difference, homosexual attachments as identification rather than "opposites attract," and institutional links between family emotional life and state/military demands. Not measured as a single index.

## Applications

- Explains why "opposites attract" folklore and sex-difference display are structural, not natural.
- Frames the life-history groups in *Masculinities* that probe crisis tendencies in the structure of desire.
- Connects gender theory to sexual politics (Part IV of *Gender and Power*).

## Critiques and Limitations

The Freudian vocabulary can obscure the specifically sociological claim (desire as social practice). The structure is the least empirically inventoried of Connell's three in the 1987 book itself — richer in programmatic definition than in extended case analysis. Later queer and affect theory rework desire without using Connell's term; the *relation* (gendered organization of attachment) remains live even where the word does not.
