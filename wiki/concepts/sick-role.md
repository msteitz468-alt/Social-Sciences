# Sick Role

## Definition as Coined

The **sick role** is [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s single most influential applied concept, set out in Chapter X of *The Social System* (1951) — "Social Structure and Dynamic Process: The Case of Modern Medical Practice." It founded medical sociology. Parsons argued that being sick is not merely a biological "condition" but a genuine **social role**, evidenced by "the existence of a set of institutionalized expectations and the corresponding sentiments and sanctions." Illness is analyzed as a form of *legitimated* [[deviance]] and the sick role as a mechanism of [[social-control]].

Parsons specified **four institutionalized components** (verbatim):

1. **Exemption from normal role obligations** — relative to severity, and "requires legitimation by and to the various alters involved," with the physician serving as legitimating agent and "court of appeal" (guarding against malingering).
2. **Not held responsible; cannot get well by will** — "the sick person cannot be expected by 'pulling himself together' to get well by an act of decision or will … His 'condition' must be changed, not merely his 'attitude.'" This is "the bridge to the acceptance of 'help.'"
3. **Obligation to regard being sick as undesirable and to want to get well** — the first two exemptions are thus *conditional*: legitimation holds only so long as the sick person genuinely wants out of the state.
4. **Obligation to seek technically competent help and to cooperate** — "in the most usual case, that of a physician," articulating the patient role with the physician role in "a complementary role structure."

In [[pattern-variables|pattern-variable]] terms the sick role is contingent (open to anyone), typically temporary, "negatively achieved" (through failure to keep well), universalistic, functionally specific, affectively neutral, and collectivity-oriented toward the physician. The physician's own role is achievement-based, universalistic, functionally specific, affectively neutral, and — unlike the businessman's — **collectivity-oriented** rather than self-oriented.

## Semantic History

Parsons treated the doctor–patient relationship as latent **"unconscious psychotherapy"**: the physician's universalism, specificity, and neutrality provide an "Archimedean place to stand" outside ordinary reciprocities, so that ordinary good medical practice performs the same reintegrative work as deliberate therapy. He also argued the sick role *insulates* deviance: "The sick thus become a statistical status class and are deprived of the possibility of forming a solidary collectivity" — one cannot rationally propose that "everybody get sick." Parsons noted the danger of **secondary gain** (the privileges of sickness becoming motivationally attractive), making the "balance of motivations to recover" a control problem.

The concept became foundational to medical sociology and was extensively revised: Eliot Freidson distinguished conditional, unconditionally legitimate, and illegitimate sick roles; critics noted its poor fit for **chronic illness and disability** (where recovery is not the goal), its assumption of a compliant patient, and its neglect of lay/contested illness. It nonetheless remains the field's canonical starting point.

## Operationalizations

The four components are ideal-typical expectations, not a scale; empirical medical sociology has tested and qualified them (e.g., variation by condition, chronicity, and social status) rather than measured a single "sick role" quantity.

## Applications

- The founding case of medical sociology and of the [[medical-model]] as a *social* institution.
- A worked illustration of [[deviance]]-as-managed-by-[[social-control]] and of the [[pattern-variables]] applied to a concrete professional–client relationship.

## Critiques and Limitations

The model presupposes acute, curable illness and a cooperative patient; it fits chronic illness, disability, mental illness, and stigmatized or contested conditions poorly. Its framing of illness as deviance and of the physician as benign social-control agent inherits the standing objections to [[structural-functionalism]], and later medical sociology (Freidson; the sociology of the body; disability studies) developed largely as a critical response to it.
