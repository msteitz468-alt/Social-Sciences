# Chunkey

A Native North American game: a disk-shaped stone roller is bowled across a packed-clay yard while players hurl poles toward the spot where the stone will stop. Emic term (from Choctaw; Mandan *tchungkee*, Muskogean *chungkee*, Dakota *ha-ka*) that migrated into archaeological typology as a label for the stone discoidals themselves. Treatment here from [[pauketat-cahokia-2009|Pauketat (2009)]], where chunkey carries a major interpretive load.

## Definition as Coined

As documented by 18th–19th-century observers (James Adair, William Bartram, George Catlin, Rudolph Kurz), chunkey was played from the Caddoan heartland to Florida and north through the upper Midwest and Missouri River. It was a serious gambling game: Adair and Kurz record men wagering ornaments, clothing, wives, and even their own liberty. Likely derived from an older, widespread hoop-and-pole children's game (Pauketat 2009).

## Semantic History

- **~600 CE:** earliest known chunkey stones, restricted to western Illinois / eastern Missouri, including the Cahokia floodplain — the game predates [[cahokia]]'s rise by centuries (Pauketat 2009, [FACT]).
- **~1050 CE:** Gregory Perino's typology distinguishes fine "Cahokia-style" stones (thin, deeply concave, sometimes perforated) from earlier thick disks and later heavier "southern-style" stones. Cahokia-style stones appear **first at Cahokia ~1050 and only after 1050 at southern sites** (Shiloh, Angel, Moundville, Towasahgy, Obion). Terry Norris (2005) sourced the red-stained quartzite to Thebes Gap, ~130 miles south of Cahokia. A cache of fifteen Cahokia-style stones accompanied the Mound 72 beaded burial (1050–1100 CE).
- **Post-Cahokia:** stickball ("the little brother of war") gradually supplanted chunkey as the dominant eastern sport, which Pauketat associates with rising inter-village violence after Cahokia's decline.
- **Modern archaeology:** the "Chunkey Player" red-flintclay pipe (found near Ocmulgee, Oklahoma, carved ~1100 CE) was long misattributed as locally made; Thomas Emerson's PIMA mineral analysis (2000–2003) proved it and ~two dozen similar red-stone sculptures were carved of flintclay from ~20 miles west of Cahokia — made at Cahokia and exported ([[spiro]] connection; archaeometric sourcing, material evidence class).

## Operationalizations

Operationalized archaeologically as the typology and distribution of stone discoidals: Perino's Cahokia-style vs. earlier thick and later southern-style stones; raw-material sourcing (Norris's Thebes Gap quartzite match; Emerson's PIMA analysis for the related flintclay carvings). The chronology-by-typology inference depends on secure contexts — many discoidals are surface or looted finds.

## Applications

Pauketat argues (attributed — his interpretation) that the post-1050 radiation of Cahokia-style stones tracks the spread of Cahokian religion and politics — chunkey as a "soft power" vehicle, possibly a ritualized alternative to warfare and a peacemaking tool carried by envoys. He further reads cosmological symbolism into the game (rolling stone as solar motion; cross and eye motifs as cosmic order) — attributed voice throughout. The cross-language-family name similarities (Siouan *tchungkee*, Choctaw *chunkey*, Muskogean *chungkee*) are presented as circumstantial support for a common Cahokian-era origin; Pauketat notes archaeologists "are not in total agreement."

## Critiques and Limitations

The stone typology and distribution chronology are material facts; the inference from stone distribution to religious-political missionization is interpretation and depends on Pauketat's broader Cahokia-centric model of Mississippian origins (see [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]]). Ethnohistoric accounts document the game 600+ years after Cahokia's fall — bridging arguments are analogy, not observation.
