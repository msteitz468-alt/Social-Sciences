# Anthropology of modernity

In [[escobar-arturo|Escobar]]'s framing (1995, attributed), the **anthropology of modernity** is a research orientation that studies modern Western institutions — especially the economy, planning, and expert knowledge — as cultural and historical constructions, rather than treating them as neutral baselines against which "traditional" societies are measured. Development is a privileged site for this anthropology because it exported and imposed those constructions globally after 1945.

## Definition as Coined

Escobar proposes (attributed) mapping the invention of development as part of a broader anthropology of modernity: how certain representations become dominant and shape what can be imagined and done. An anthropology of modernity "centered on the economy" questions development economics as culture. It relies on ethnographic approaches to institutions and discourses, not only to village communities.

## Semantic History

Related to Paul Rabinow's and others' projects on modern forms; to science-and-technology studies; and to the reflexive turn in anthropology. Escobar's use is specifically tied to critiquing development and imagining [[post-development]]. A cognate program is [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s (*[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]*, 1996) **"theory of rupture"** — modernity as a break driven by media and migration acting on [[imagination-as-social-practice|the imagination]], analyzed through [[global-cultural-flows]] rather than through development discourse *(attributed)*.

## Operationalizations

- Cultural critique of economics (growth, capital, the underdeveloped economy).
- Institutional ethnography of planning (food, rural development).
- Genealogy of client categories (peasant, woman, environment).

## Applications

Underwrites [[escobar-encountering-development-study]] and [[development-discourse]].

## Critiques and Limitations

"Modernity" is a contested umbrella; the program risks reifying a single Western modernity (later multiple-modernities debates). Escobar's version is program-specific and should be attributed.
