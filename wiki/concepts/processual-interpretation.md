# Processual Interpretation

Willey and Phillips’s (1958) term for archaeology on the **explanatory** level of organization. Historically important as a **pre-New Archaeology** use of “processual” for regularity-seeking explanation — continuous with, but not identical to, later “processual archaeology” (Binford et al.).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958.)*

> Processual interpretation is the study of the nature of what is vaguely referred to as the culture-historical process. Practically speaking, it implies an attempt to discover regularities in the relationships given by the methods of culture-historical integration… we are no longer asking merely what but also how and even why.

They rejected the then-current label “functional interpretation” as too narrow (function as sole explanatory principle). On this level formulations must be cultural **and** social: efficient causes of cultural change are “people or groups of people.” At this point “the archaeologist is in effect a cultural anthropologist” (with activities still conditioned by descriptive formulations). Walter Taylor 1948 is cited as a reconcilable levels scheme.

## Semantic History

| Sense | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Willey 1953a “processual” | (Footnote) description of how specific cultures function in specific times/places — **descriptive** level in 1958 terms |
| W&P 1958 “processual interpretation” | **Explanatory** regularities and causality |
| Binford / New Archaeology “processual archaeology” | Scientific program (hypothesis testing, systems, ecology, sampling) that radicalized aims and methods beyond culture-historical unit systems |

Do not collapse the three. W&P’s diagram places processual interpretation opposite ethnology, above culture-historical integration / ethnography, above field observation.

## Operationalizations

W&P state American archaeology had done **so little** on this level that naming it was hard; progress lagged “precisely because unit formulations have been put together with so little reference to their social aspect.” Their historical-developmental stages (Part II) are explicitly a **gray borderland** between description and explanation — not full processual interpretation.

## Applications

- Intellectual bridge from American culture-history to neo-evolutionary and New Archaeology programs (Steward, White, Binford) that took regularity and causality seriously.
- Historiographic anchor for “when did American archaeology claim the word process?”

## Critiques and Limitations

- W&P’s own Part II synthesis does not deliver full explanatory process — they admit as much.
- New Archaeology claimed W&P-style culture-history remained taxonomic, not scientific.
- Post-processualism rejected law-seeking process as the wrong goal.
- The word “processual” is now so strongly tied to 1960s–80s New Archaeology that W&P’s 1958 usage must always be date-stamped.
