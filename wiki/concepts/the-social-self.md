# The Social Self

## Definition as Coined

**Social self** is not a single coinage. Three closely related but non-identical uses structure the wiki:

### Cooley (1902)

In *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]*, [[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]] defines:

> "The social self is simply any idea, or system of ideas, drawn from the communicative life, that the mind cherishes as its own."

He means by "self" what common speech designates by first-person pronouns — the psychologist's **empirical self** — and qualifies it "social" not because a non-social self exists, but to emphasize the social aspect: the "I" of common language "always has more or less distinct reference to other people as well as the speaker." Distinctive content is a characteristic **my-feeling or sense of appropriation**, not a fixed inventory of objects (body, property, opinions). A major form is the **[[looking-glass-self|looking-glass or reflected self]]** (imagined appearance + imagined judgment + self-feeling). The **group self or "we"** is the same process at collective scale.

### James (1890)

William James's *Principles of Psychology* distinguishes material, social, and spiritual selves; the social self is recognition from others. Cooley cites James and develops the *mechanism* of social self-feeling.

### Mead (1913 / 1934)

For [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], the **self** is not a physiological given but a social emergent: "The self is something which has a development; it is not initially there, at birth, but arises in the process of social experience and activity." Its defining characteristic is **reflexivity** — "it is an object to itself." "The self, as that which can be an object to itself, is essentially a social structure, and it arises in social experience"; "it is impossible to conceive of a self arising outside of social experience" (attributed, *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]*).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| James 1890 | Social self as recognition from others; self-feeling as mark of me/mine |
| Cooley 1902 | Empirical self as my-feeling; social self as communicative ideas cherished as own; looking-glass mechanism; personal ideas as immediate social reality |
| Mead 1913 / 1934 | Self as cognitive social structure via role-taking; "essentially a cognitive rather than an emotional phenomenon"; criticizes Cooley/James self-feeling as unable to explain *origin* |
| [[symbolic-interactionism]] | Processual, interactionally formed self, continually re-formed in situated action — textbook blends Cooley and Mead |

See [[mead-cooley-self-origin-debate]] for the live theoretical dispute.

## Operationalizations

- **Cooley:** Not measured. Illustrated by child observations (his children M. and R. learning "my"/"mine"; early looking-glass acts), literary cases, and everyday pride/shame.
- **Mead:** Developmental path through [[play-and-the-game|play and the game]] and the [[generalized-other|generalized other]]; analytic split into [[the-i-and-the-me|"I" and "me"]].
- **Later research:** Reflected appraisals, identity measures, self-concept inventories — downstream of both lines.

## Applications

- Cooley: pride, vanity, honor, humility, conscience, hostility, leadership fame, and social problems (denied self-expression in work, immigrant status, racial caste) as phases of the social self.
- Mead: derivation of mind as "individual importation of the social process"; [[symbolic-interactionism|SI]]'s processual self.
- Both: against individualist contract theories that start from pre-social selves.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Origin dispute.** Mead: self-feeling presupposes a self and cannot originate it. Cooley describes the ongoing life of social self-feeling more than first emergence via significant symbols. Open — see debate page.
- **Period asides.** Cooley's sex-difference generalizations and some race-caste illustrations in Ch. VI are period ideology; attribute, do not promote to wiki voice.
- **Mead text provenance.** Most-quoted Mead passages are editorially reconstructed (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]).
- **Affect vs cognition.** Sociology of emotion partly restores Cooley's affective emphasis against Mead's cognitive priority.
