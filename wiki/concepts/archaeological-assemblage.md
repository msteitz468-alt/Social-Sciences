# Archaeological Assemblage

Level in [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]]'s hierarchy (*[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]*): the associated set of **contemporary artefact-types** that stands between type and culture.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Clarke, Definitions, 1978 ed.)*

> "An associated set of contemporary artefact-types. To be distinguished rigorously from the loose physical or geographical aggregate."

Contemporaneity and physical association are built into the assemblage definition so that culture can be defined as polythetic type categories recurring **in assemblages** within a limited area.

## Semantic History

"Assemblage" long meant "things found together." Clarke sharpened it against two failures: (1) **palimpsest maps** that compound types never in contemporary association into illusory cultures; (2) loose geographic aggregates treated as cultural units. Americanist [[archaeological-component|component]]/[[archaeological-phase|phase]] language addresses related problems with different scalar vocabulary (Willey & Phillips).

**Braidwood mid-century popular usage** (*[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* 1959, attributed): objects that “go together” make a picture called an **assemblage**; successive layers of Troy differ as successive **mail-order / Sears Roebuck catalogues** differ; cross-site dating and priority guesses rest on similarity and complexity of catalogues when radiocarbon is scarce. Tool-making **traditions** (pebble, biface, flake, chopper) are habits of manufacture, **not** full cultures or peoples — industries may mix traditions (Levalloiso-Mousterian). Related: [[archaeological-culture]], [[braidwood-robert]].

## Operationalizations

- Must be a set of **artefact-types** in **association** and **contemporaneity**, not a bag of objects from a region.
- Assemblages exhibit phase/time/system pattern regularities parallel to lower ranks (ch. 6).
- Culture is expressed by a comprehensive set of types and **represented by** a group of assemblages containing some of those types — the culture relation is the mapping between assemblage group and type set.

## Applications

- Foundation for polythetic [[archaeological-culture]] definition.
- Hygiene against illusory cultures from distribution-map artefacts.
- Link to settlement/activity analysis when assemblages are site- or context-controlled (later ethnoarchaeology densifies this — Binford — outside Clarke's core).

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Contemporary" and "associated" are themselves archaeological inferences (stratigraphy, formation processes).
- Clarke's book under-develops site-formation controls later central to assemblage integrity (Schiffer et al.).
- Single-material "industries" may be aspects of technocomplexes rather than full sociocultural assemblages.
