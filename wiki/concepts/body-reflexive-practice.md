# Body-Reflexive Practice

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s named account (in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]*, 1995, ch. 2 "Men's Bodies") of how bodies enter gender without either determining it or being reduced to a passive surface. The **argument** is already fully worked in *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987) Ch. 4 "The Body and Social Practice"; the 1995 label crystallizes it.

## Definition as Coined

> "With bodies both objects and agents of practice, and the practice itself forming the structures within which bodies are appropriated and defined, we face a pattern beyond the formulae of current social theory. This pattern might be termed body-reflexive practice."

Bodies are "addressed by social process and drawn into history, without ceasing to be bodies." Practice is **onto-formative** (after Kosík). Gender is "social practice that constantly refers to bodies and what bodies do, it is not social practice reduced to the body."

**1987 formulation (same claim, different phrasing).** Social practices constructing gender "do not express natural patterns, nor do they ignore natural patterns; rather, they **negate** them in a practical transformation." "My male body does not confer masculinity on me; it receives masculinity (or some fragment thereof) as its social definition." The physical sense of maleness grows through "a life-history-in-society," not from chromosomes or the penis alone. Competitive sport systematically promotes ideal masculinity by embedding force/skill and the superiority of men over women and of hegemonic over other masculinities **in the body**.

## Semantic History

The 1995 term is defined against two positions Connell already rejects in 1987:
- **Biological determinism / sociobiology** — "the body is a natural machine which produces gender difference." Connell rejects it on evidence (negligible measured psychological sex differences) and cites Kemper: "When racist and sexist ideologies sanction certain hierarchical social arrangements on the basis of biology, the biology is usually false."
- **Semiotic / social-constructionist body theory** — "the body is a more or less neutral surface or landscape on which a social symbolism is imprinted" (he reads Foucault's disciplined bodies and Turner's "body practices" this way). Connell objects that here "the signified … vanishes" and the body becomes passive: "the body is a field on which social determination runs riot."

He also rejects the "commonsense compromise" (biology + culture both contribute) as incoherent, because the two levels are "not commensurate" — biology is always smuggled back in as the more "real."

## Operationalizations

Body-reflexive practice moves in **circuits**, illustrated from the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history]] cases: bodily pleasure → socially structured fantasy → new social relationships → further bodily experience. Connell attributes a kind of agency to bodily processes within these circuits (in one case, "'agency' does not seem too strong a word for what [the informant's] sphincter, prostate gland and erectile tissues here managed between them"). Applied to sport and industrial labour, masculinity is "embodied" through disciplined performance that is at once symbolic and physically real — and the body can be **recalcitrant**, breaking down under the demands of masculine performance (the alcohol/drug crises of several informants; disabled men's adaptive strategies, after Gerschick and Miller).

## Applications

- Grounds Connell's claim that difference is constituted as dominance *through the body*, which in turn makes a "degendering strategy" (not merely a rights strategy) necessary in gender politics (see [[gender-relations-theory]]).
- Connects the sociology of gender to the sociology of the body, sport, disability, and health, where masculine practice can be "hostile to bodies' own physical well-being."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is programmatic rather than tested: it is illustrated through a small set of life-history cases and stated as a theoretical resolution of the nature/culture impasse rather than an empirically validated mechanism. Its reach beyond the illustrative cases depends on the same qualitative evidence base as the rest of the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|study]].
