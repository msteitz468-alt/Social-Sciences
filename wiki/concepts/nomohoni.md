# Nomohoni

## Definition as Coined

In [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-1968|Chagnon 1968]], **nomohoni** denotes **treachery** — specifically the ultimate form of violence on his graded scale: massacre under the guise of friendship or feasting, as opposed to an ordinary raid (kill one or more enemies and flee). Spindler's foreword glosses the series: chest-pounding and side-slapping duels → club fighting → spear throwing → raiding → nomohoni.

## Semantic History

In-text use is already analytic: Chagnon elevates an emic term into the capstone of a comparative violence typology (also in his 1967 *Natural History* article). English "treachery" / "massacre" carry moral load that the teaching case both exploits and analyzes.

## Operationalizations

Case anchors in the 1968 text include the **1950 massacre** of Kaobawa's group (revenge treachery; some Iwahikoroba-teri refused to participate and helped people escape); later mutual fear structuring feast attendance (men sometimes leaving women/children concealed); failed 1965 treachery plot; reverse treachery memories in the Mahekodo-teri relationship. Frequency is not given as a rate; nomohoni is the named extreme, not the modal conflict form.

## Applications

- [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-study]] — warfare chapter; alliance mistrust
- [[yanomamo]] — upper bound of intervillage violence as of mid-1960s documentation

## Critiques and Limitations

Do not inflate the named extreme into the typical pattern of Yanomamö politics. Casualty and incidence claims are case-based. Graphic massacre details are historically real in Chagnon's report and ethically sensitive in re-use — attribute and date-stamp.
