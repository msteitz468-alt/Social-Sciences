# Mother Goddess (Neolithic / Cauvin)

Analytical construct in prehistoric religion: a female divinity associated with fertility, maternity, and often wild animals. [[cauvin-jacques|Jacques Cauvin]] (*[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Birth of the Gods]]* 2000) treats the **Goddess** as one pole (with the **bull**) of the [[revolution-of-symbols|revolution of symbols]] beginning in the [[khiamian|Khiamian]] Levant ~9500 BC, later made “explicitly theological” in seventh-millennium [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] art.

## Definition as Coined

For Cauvin (attributed): from Khiamian onward, art humanises the female form as supreme authority — not fully alien to humans — seeding creationist theologies and a personalised unifying principle reconciling man and nature. At Çatalhöyük he reads obese birthing goddesses on panther thrones, infant or feline in arms, as fertility, maternity, royalty, and mistress of animals — traits of the Mother-Goddess “who dominates the oriental pantheon” until male-dominated monotheism (his phrasing).

## Semantic History

- Victorian and early twentieth-century mother-goddess reconstructions of prehistory.
- Mid-century Anatolian Neolithic art: [[mellaart-james|Mellaart]] at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (*[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|1967]]*) supplies the type imagery — pregnant plaster reliefs, enthroned Level II goddess with felines as “Mistress of Animals” birthing a child, breasts incorporating animal jaws/beaks/tusks, portable goddess statues (maiden/mother/crone aspects in his reading), and a schist plaque he interprets as *hieros gamos*. He infers a fertility cult with a principal goddess and important social position of women in an agricultural society (attributed; sample limited to his “religious quarter”).
- Cauvin: pushes the goddess back to PPNA/Khiamian and makes her causal for neolithisation, using Mellaart’s seventh-millennium archive as “explicit theology.”
- Late twentieth–twenty-first century critique: rejects universal fertility-goddess reading; alternative readings of female figurines (toys, ancestors, individuality, initiation); Hodder-project contextualism weakens shrine-as-temple framing ([[catalhoyuk-shrine-house-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Counts and contexts of female figurines; association with bucrania; mural hierarchical scale; ethnographic/historical analogy to later goddesses (flag as analogy).

## Applications

Ideology-before-economy arguments; history of religions long-range narratives; feminist and anti-feminist prehistoric myth-making (reflexive minefield).

## Critiques and Limitations

Highly contested. Göbekli Tepe animal imagery often read against fertility-goddess models. Risk of projecting Bronze Age and classical goddesses backward. Keep in **attributed** voice; never wiki-voice “the Neolithic worshipped a mother goddess.”
