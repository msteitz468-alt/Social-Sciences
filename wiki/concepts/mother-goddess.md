# Mother Goddess (Neolithic / Cauvin)

Analytical construct in prehistoric religion: a female divinity associated with fertility, maternity, and often wild animals. [[cauvin-jacques|Jacques Cauvin]] (*[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Birth of the Gods]]* 2000) treats the **Goddess** as one pole (with the **bull**) of the [[revolution-of-symbols|revolution of symbols]] beginning in the [[khiamian|Khiamian]] Levant ~9500 BC, later made “explicitly theological” in seventh-millennium [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] art. [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]] (*[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]*, 1991) offers a parallel but distinct European synthesis: a multi-aspect [[old-europe|Old Europe]] Goddess of life, death, and regeneration continuous from the Upper Paleolithic (attributed; see [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]]).

## Definition as Coined

For Cauvin (attributed): from Khiamian onward, art humanises the female form as supreme authority — not fully alien to humans — seeding creationist theologies and a personalised unifying principle reconciling man and nature. At Çatalhöyük he reads obese birthing goddesses on panther thrones, infant or feline in arms, as fertility, maternity, royalty, and mistress of animals — traits of the Mother-Goddess “who dominates the oriental pantheon” until male-dominated monotheism (his phrasing).

For Gimbutas 1991 (attributed): the earliest religious experiences arose from the “sovereign mystery and creative power of the female as the source of life”; the Great Mother Goddess who gives birth from her womb becomes a metaphor for Nature as cosmic giver and taker of life within the eternal cycle of life, death, and rebirth. Multiple categories, functions, and symbols are “all aspects of the unbroken unity of one deity” — a Goddess who is ultimately Nature herself. She holds the Paleolithic and Neolithic Goddess to be parthenogenetic, creating life out of herself (the “primeval, self-fertilizing ‘Virgin Goddess’”), with “no traces in Paleolithic art of a father figure.”

## Semantic History

- Victorian and early twentieth-century mother-goddess reconstructions of prehistory.
- Mid-century Anatolian Neolithic art: [[mellaart-james|Mellaart]] at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (*[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|1967]]*) supplies the type imagery — pregnant plaster reliefs, enthroned Level II goddess with felines as “Mistress of Animals” birthing a child, breasts incorporating animal jaws/beaks/tusks, portable goddess statues (maiden/mother/crone aspects in his reading), and a schist plaque he interprets as *hieros gamos*. He infers a fertility cult with a principal goddess and important social position of women in an agricultural society (attributed; sample limited to his “religious quarter”).
- Cauvin: pushes the goddess back to PPNA/Khiamian and makes her causal for neolithisation, using Mellaart’s seventh-millennium archive as “explicit theology.”
- **Gimbutas 1991 (European strand):** rejects labeling Paleolithic female miniatures as “Venuses” or “fertility charms” designed to arouse male sexuality; she holds their functions were “considerably more important: the giving and protection of life, as well as death and regeneration.” She posits Upper Paleolithic Goddess iconography (abstract/hieroglyphic X’s, V’s, triangles, meanders; representational vulvas, breasts, birds’ feet; animal symbols) continuing into the agricultural era, evolved with changing economy. Neolithic “renewed flowering” with ceramics c. 6500 B.C. (her dates) produces thousands of figurines, temple models, and ritual articles; she treats Old Europe symbolism 6500–3500 B.C. as an “essential key” to Goddess iconography. She equates Goddess-among-animals imagery (e.g. Hacılar seated on leopard) with later *Potnia Theron*; reads Bird Goddess, Snake Goddess, stiff “White Goddess of Death,” and regeneration symbols (frog, hedgehog, bucranium-as-uterus, bee, butterfly) as aspects of one cyclic deity. She argues degradation of the Goddess began with Indo-Europeanization of Old Europe in the 4th–3rd millennia B.C. ([[kurgan-hypothesis]]; [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]), intensified by Hebraic-Christian traditions — death/regeneration demonized as the witch, deity removed to the heavens, earth a “place of exile.”
- Late twentieth–twenty-first century critique: rejects universal fertility-goddess reading; alternative readings of female figurines (toys, ancestors, individuality, initiation); Hodder-project contextualism weakens shrine-as-temple framing ([[catalhoyuk-shrine-house-debate]]). Gimbutas’s long-range continuity, folklore analogies, and temple-as-worship-place claims remain central points of dispute on [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]].

## Operationalizations

Counts and contexts of female figurines; association with bucrania; mural hierarchical scale; ethnographic/historical analogy to later goddesses (flag as analogy).

**Gimbutas 1991 typology (attributed).** She distinguishes at least twenty female and five male anthropomorphic and hybrid figurine types by posture, facial features, masks, headgear, and associated symbols. For analytic purposes she arranges deities in **four main groups** under one Goddess-as-Nature unity:

1. **Generative / life-propagating forces** — birth-giver, pregnant Earth Mother, Bird Goddess as nourisher, Snake as vitality, Nurse/Madonna, Mistress of Animals; epiphanies deer, elk, bear, pig, ram.
2. **Destructive Death Goddess** — stiff nude (“White Goddess of Death”), poisonous snake, bird of prey (vulture, owl); white-clad women; found at graves singly or in threes.
3. **Goddess of Regeneration** — uterus/pubic triangle/fetus symbols (toad, frog, hedgehog, bull head, triangle/double triangle/hourglass; also bee, butterfly, moth); death and regeneration as one deity; tomb as womb.
4. **Prehistoric male deities** — only 3–5% of the Neolithic sculpture corpus (her count); partners/consorts/brothers of goddesses, not superordinates: “Sorrowful Ancient”/Dying Vegetation God, ithyphallic youth, phallic Snake/Hermes prototype, centaurs, Master of Animals.

Findspots (altars, oven platforms, offering places, caves, graves, temple models) are, for her, keys to function. She prefers “temple” over “shrine” for Old European/Anatolian Neolithic–Copper Age buildings and treats clay models as temple replicas, not mere dwelling replicas. Exemplary contexts she cites: Achilleion two-room temples (altar + workshop; ~200 figurines, only two male); Çatalhöyük as temples of death and regeneration (vulture murals, breasts over vulture skulls — regeneration out of death); Sabatinivka Snake Goddess bench; Maltese temples as regenerating body of the Goddess; Lepenski Vir fish-woman-bird hybrids and pubic-triangle floor plans.

## Applications

Ideology-before-economy arguments; history of religions long-range narratives; feminist and anti-feminist prehistoric myth-making (reflexive minefield). Gimbutas’s package anchors Position A on [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]] (peaceful matricentric temple communities vs Kurgan patriarchal sky-god religion) and feeds social-structure claims on [[matrifocality]] (matristic/gylany, not matriarchy).

## Critiques and Limitations

Highly contested. Göbekli Tepe animal imagery often read against fertility-goddess models. Risk of projecting Bronze Age and classical goddesses backward. Gimbutas’s method commitments (Paleolithic–historic folklore continuity; morphological analogy such as bucranium–uterus; ethnographic parallels as confirmatory) are themselves disputed; male absence from temples explained by “worship in nature which has left little record” is an argument from silence.
