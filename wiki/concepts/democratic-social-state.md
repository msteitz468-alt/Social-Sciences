# Democratic Social State (État Social)

## Definition as Coined

[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] used **social state** (*état social*) as the master
explanatory variable of his sociology: "the condition of society is normally the result of
circumstances, sometimes of laws... but, once it is established, we can consider it as the
fundamental source of most of the laws, customs, and ideas." A society's social state — the
distribution of rank, property, and standing — is prior to and shapes its politics and its
[[mores]]. He argued the Anglo-American social state was "eminently democratic... from the
birth of the colonies," rooted in the rough equality of New England's first settlers and
deepened as partible inheritance dissolved even the weak, non-hereditary planter
"aristocracy" of the South.

The **democratic** social state is the one defined by [[equality-of-conditions|equality of
conditions]]; the aristocratic social state is its ideal-typical opposite. Much of
Tocqueville's method is to hold the two social states side by side and attribute observed
customs to a society's position on that axis rather than to race, climate, or national
character.

## Semantic History

*État social* is Tocqueville's contribution to a lineage running from Montesquieu's concern
with the "spirit" of a polity toward the modern sociological premise that social structure
conditions ideas and institutions — a premise later systematized very differently by Marx
(mode of production) and Durkheim (social morphology). Tocqueville's version is distinctive
in making the *distribution of standing*, not the economy as such, the base.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; inferred from inheritance law, property distribution, manners, and the
presence or absence of hereditary rank.

## Applications

The concept grounds the whole architecture of *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy
in America]]*: from the democratic social state Tocqueville derives
[[sovereignty-of-the-people|popular sovereignty]], [[individualism]], the softening of
[[mores]], and the political dangers of equality. It is the "fundamental source" from which
his other concepts descend.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The primacy of "social state" over economy and politics is asserted, not demonstrated, and
  contrasts sharply with materialist accounts.
- Like [[equality-of-conditions]], the construct brackets the racial and gender boundaries of
  the "democratic" society it describes.
