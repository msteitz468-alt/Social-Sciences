# Academic Capital

The species of [[symbolic-capital|capital]] specific to institutional power within the university — the capacity to control the instruments of reproduction of the professorial corps (careers, appointments, juries, curricula) — as distinguished by Bourdieu in *[[bourdieu-homo-academicus-1984|Homo Academicus]]* (1984) from **scientific** and **intellectual** capital.

## Definition as Coined

Bourdieu argues (attributed, 1984) that the [[academic-field|academic field]] is organized around an opposition between two forms of accumulated authority: **academic capital** — temporal, institutional power over reproduction, accumulated slowly through positions in the machinery of the university — and **scientific/intellectual capital** — the renown and authority conferred by peers and the wider intellectual world. The two are partly independent and often inversely distributed: the temporally dominant (holders of academic power) are frequently the scientifically/culturally dominated, and vice versa.

## Semantic History

A specification of the general capital theory of *[[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]* and the practice monographs. Distinct from generic [[cultural-capital]] (credentials, cultivation) in that academic capital is specifically **positional power inside the institution**. Extended in *The State Nobility* (1989) to the field of the grandes écoles.

## Operationalizations

Indicators (Ch. 3, *Homo Academicus*): seats on the Conseil Supérieur / consultative committees, thesis-jury participation, directorships, control of research institutes and appointments, capacity to place and reproduce students — contrasted with indicators of scientific prestige (citations, translations, scientific prizes) and intellectual notoriety (media presence, general-intellectual visibility).

## Applications

- Explaining the **reproduction of the corps** and the professorial control of academic **time** (making juniors wait).
- Mapping the structure of the field of power internal to the university.
- Analyzing the succession crisis and May 1968 as a rupture in the temporal order governed by academic capital.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's neat inverse relation to scientific capital is a France-1960s finding, not a universal law; in systems where research grants and metrics themselves confer institutional power the two capitals may fuse. Attribute and date-stamp.
