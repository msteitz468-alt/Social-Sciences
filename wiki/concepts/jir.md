# Jir

*Jir* is the [[tiv|Tiv]] concept glossed by [[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]] as "court,
case, moot" — the organizing category around which the Tiv systematize their institutions
of dispute-settlement, and the centerpiece of *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice
and Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (1957). Bohannan treats it as the Tiv **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk system]]** equivalent of what English speakers organize around the word "law."

## Definition as Coined

In Tiv usage *jir* is simultaneously the tribunal, the hearing, and the matter heard — a
duality (court/case) Bohannan declines to resolve by translation. Two institutions are
both *jir*: the British-introduced Grade-D Native Authority court and the indigenous
**moot** (*jir* "at home"). Both aim to "repair the *[[tar]]*" — to restore the peace of
the lineage territory. The person who judges is *or-jir* (plural *mbajiriv*); the elders
who investigate a case are *mbatarev* ("men of the *tar*").

## Semantic History

*Jir* is a Tiv word, not a coinage; Bohannan's contribution is analytic, not lexical. He
notes that Tiv label the two kinds of *jir* by context — "*jir* of the D.O." or "*jir* of
the N.A." for the government court, "*jir* at home" (*jir sa ya*) or "*jir* of the lineage"
(*jir ityô*) for the moot — and that the colonial period stretched the concept: an
institution (the introduced court) that "the Tiv concept *jir*, applied originally to
meetings... somewhat similar to those of the present-day moot, provided a framework and a
vocabulary" to absorb. "Whereas there have always been disputes (*iyongo*), today there are
*jir*."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; Bohannan "operationalizes" *jir* only in the ethnographic sense
of eliciting it from Tiv usage and mapping which acts, personnel, and settings Tiv gather
under the one word (court and moot; *or-jir*/judge; *mbatarev*/investigating elders;
*lumun*/concurrence; *sa apela*/imposed decision).

## The analytical claim

Bohannan's analytical reading (attributed): the *jir* falls in the middle column of a
three-part series — **breach of norm → counteraction (*jir*) → correction**. Where English
"law" is organized primarily around **rules** (*tindi*) that are broken, the Tiv organize
their jural life around the **counteraction** itself. "Whereas there have always been
disputes (*iyongo*), today there are *jir*." A right answer is believed to exist to every
dispute; the parties bring it to the *jir* to *discover* that answer, and must concur
(*lumun*) in it. A decision imposed without concurrence is *sa apela* — "settled by force,"
arbitrary, contrary to custom.

## Applications

The *jir* frames every substantive domain in the book: marriage disputes, **debt** (*injô*)
cases, "criminal" matters (theft, *ifer*), market and age-set disputes, and the in-lineage
moot. Bohannan uses it to argue that Tiv classify obligations under "debt," not under
imported categories of "tort" and "contract," and dispute under "*jir*," not under "the
court" — a demonstration of why the folk system must be rendered before the analytical one.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether an institution-centered concept like *jir* can support cross-cultural comparison
(as against a rule-centered concept like Lozi *mulao* or Western "law") is the substance of
the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. Bohannan's insistence that comparison begin from *jir*
rather than from "law" is exactly what Gluckman attacked as impeding comparison.
