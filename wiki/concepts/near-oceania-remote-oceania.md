## Definition as Coined

Proposed by Roger Green (Green 1991b), explicitly as a replacement for the historically "sterile" tripartite Polynesia/Melanesia/Micronesia scheme (Dumont d'Urville 1832), which Kirch reports Green regarded as analytically bankrupt for organizing Pacific prehistory (attributed, Kirch 2017). The distinction is not merely geographic but, in Kirch's framing (attributed, quoted), "encapsulates two major epochs in the history of the Pacific islanders."

- **Near Oceania**: New Guinea, the Bismarck Archipelago, and the Solomon Islands as far east as Makira/Santa Ana — the region of greatest biogeographic diversity in Oceania, first reached by humans roughly 40,000 years ago (late Pleistocene). Contains both Austronesian-speaking and Non-Austronesian (Papuan-speaking) populations.
- **Remote Oceania**: all Pacific islands lying north, east, and southeast of Near Oceania. Inhabitants speak exclusively Austronesian languages; the region was not settled by humans until after ca. 1200 BCE, in some cases (New Zealand) not until ca. 1000 CE (attributed, Green 1995, via Kirch 2017).

## Semantic History

The construct superseded the racial-typological Polynesia/Melanesia/Micronesia scheme, which mixed linguistic, geographic, and somatic-typological criteria and has been judged (attributed, Thomas 1989; Tcherkézoff 2008, via Kirch) to lack culture-historical unity except in the Polynesian case. Green's boundary instead tracks a demonstrable biogeographic and demographic discontinuity rather than an inherited colonial ethnographic taxonomy.

## Operationalizations

The Near/Remote boundary functions in the literature as:
- A **disease-biogeography boundary** — Near Oceania (with the Anopheles malaria vector) versus malaria-free Remote Oceania, a distinction that materially shaped demographic history (see [[austronesian-expansion]] and the α-thalassemia evidence for Melanesian admixture prior to Remote Oceanic dispersal).
- A **settlement-chronology boundary** — Pleistocene first arrival (Near Oceania) versus Holocene, largely post-Lapita arrival (Remote Oceania).
- A **linguistic boundary** — Near Oceania hosts both Austronesian and Papuan (Non-Austronesian) language phyla; Remote Oceania is exclusively Austronesian (Oceanic subgroup).

## Applications

Structures the regional organization of Kirch's *On the Road of the Winds* (2017) and much subsequent Pacific archaeology; frames the demographic paradox that Near Oceania, despite far longer occupation, has markedly lower population density than parts of Remote Oceania — attributed by Kirch in part to disease burden and carrying-capacity constraints on hunting-and-gathering economies in Near Oceanic rainforests.

## Critiques and Limitations

The boundary is a coarse etic device: it groups together, on the Near Oceania side, populations of very different linguistic affiliation and settlement history, and (per a 2017-reported revision of Sahlins's 1963 chiefs-versus-big-men typology) political-organization patterns correlate more cleanly with the Austronesian/Papuan linguistic split than with the older Near/Remote or Melanesia/Polynesia geographic splits (attributed, Guiart 1963; Scaglion 1996, via Kirch 2017) — a caution against treating "Near Oceania" as culturally homogeneous. As with all such regional constructs, it should not be read as asserting identity between language, biology, and culture within either zone (four-way non-identity principle).
