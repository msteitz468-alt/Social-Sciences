# Fractal Recursivity

**Fractal recursivity** is the second of Irvine and Gal’s (2000) three semiotic processes of ideological linguistic differentiation: an opposition that organizes difference at one scale is **projected** onto other scales — within groups, between subgroups, onto roles within a person, or onto larger regional/civilizational contrasts — so the same axis of distinction reappears in nested form.

## Definition as Coined

Per [[irvine-judith-t|Irvine]] and [[gal-susan|Gal]] ([[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000|2000]]):

> Fractal recursivity involves the projection of an opposition, salient at some level of relationship, onto some other level. For example, intra-group oppositions might be projected outward onto intergroup relations, or vice versa. Thus the dichotomizing and partitioning process that was involved in some understood opposition (between groups or linguistic varieties, for example) recurs at other levels, creating either subcategories on each side of a contrast or supercategories that include both sides but oppose them to something else.

They liken the process to geometric fractals, segmentary kinship, and anthropological treatments of segmentation or [[schismogenesis]] (citing Bateson, Evans-Pritchard, nationalist ideologies, gender rituals). Oppositions do **not** define fixed groups: they supply “discursive or cultural resources to claim and thus attempt to create shifting ‘communities,’ identities, selves, and roles, at different levels of contrast.” When reproduced within a single person, they concern roles/activities more than contrasting identities. Mimesis is partial.

## Semantic History

Formulated with [[iconization]] and [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]] as a set in the SAR *Regimes of Language* volume. Draws on longer semiotic and social-theoretic interest in recursive boundary-making. Becomes standard language-ideology vocabulary after 2000. Related Balkan/“nesting orientalisms” literature (e.g. Bakić-Hayden) is adjacent; Irvine & Gal stress the **linguistic-ideological** mechanism.

## Operationalizations

Identify a locally salient binary (pure/mixed, modern/backward, cultivated/vulgar, native/foreign, Europe/Asia) and show its reapplication at finer or coarser social/linguistic levels in discourse, policy, scholarship, or register structure.

## Applications (Irvine & Gal 2000 cases)

| Case | Recursive projection (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Nguni *hlonipha*** | Contrast between Bantu and Khoi consonant repertoires (clicks as emblematically foreign) is mobilized to express social distance and deference **within** Nguni — foreignness-at-the-boundary recurs as respect-register abnormality inside the language. |
| **Senegal** | European–African hierarchical contrasts (white/black, complex/simple, dominant/subordinate) model a putative history of conquests *within* Africa (Fula over Wolof over Sereer). Differences among Sereer varieties are read as less vs. more “penetrated by Islam,” replicating the larger Sereer–Wolof contrast. |
| **Macedonia / Balkans** | East/West orientalism projects onto Europe (civilized West vs. “Near East” / Balkans), then onto the Balkans themselves (Macedonia as “Balkan of the Balkans”), then onto families (sons to different national schools) and individuals (“Levantine type” as composite East/West). Within literary codification, native/foreign opposition recurs as register-stripping of Turkisms and choice of dialect bases for maximal differentiation from neighboring standards. |

## Critiques and Limitations

“Fractal” is metaphorical; not all recursive oppositions are self-similar in a strong geometric sense. Best used with ethnographic or historical specificity, not as free-floating geometry of culture. Partial mimesis is part of the concept — overclaiming perfect nesting is a misuse.
