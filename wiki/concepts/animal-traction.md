# Animal Traction

## Definition as Coined

**Animal traction** is the harnessing of domestic animals — typically cattle (oxen), later equids in some transport contexts — to pull agricultural or transport equipment. In European prehistoric archaeology it is a load-bearing element of [[sherratt-andrew|Andrew Sherratt]]’s **[[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]]**: traction joins milk and wool as renewable products that reorganize land use, surplus, settlement spacing, and exchange after initial meat-oriented husbandry.

Sherratt (1981) concluded that “the spread of the plough was arguably the most important development in Old World prehistory after the adoption of cereal cultivation itself.” He distinguishes the light **ard**/plough of fourth–third millennium contexts from later heavy mouldboard ploughs.

## Semantic History

Within *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]* (1997):

- **Ch.6 (1981):** assembles cart and plough evidence from Mesopotamia through Europe and India; New World model wheels without draught animals show that **animals**, not the wheel idea alone, are critical.
- **Ch.8 (1987):** replies to Chapman — cart **introduced** to Europe; plough earlier in Near East, mid-fourth millennium in Europe; wool ~3000 BC; package as **traction complex** transmission.
- **Ch.9 (1986):** wooden wheels (Plachidol Pit-Grave; Zürich-Akademiker / Horgen) and fixed vs rotating axle technology; Anatolian vs Pontic routes.
- **1997 annotations:** would route ox-traction through **Anatolia as well as** Pontic steppes; chariot later through Balkans rather than only Caucasus.
- **Self-correction:** abandoned original claim that TRB *origins* were plough-driven; TRB beginning reassigned to different process (loess × indigenous fusion).

## Operationalizations

As of Sherratt 1981–97:

- **Pictograms / seals:** Uruk plough and cart signs; Akkadian cylinder-seal plough scenes; Cretan hieroglyphic sole-ards.
- **Models and skeuomorphs:** wagon-cups ([[baden-culture|Baden]] Budakalász/Szigetszentmárton); pottery cart with protomes (Radošina); [[funnel-beaker-culture|TRB]] yoked-ox handle (Krężnica Jara); pecked ox-cart scene (Züschen); Bronocice four-wheeler motif (~3500–3100).
- **Burials:** paired bovid graves (Alsónémedi, Budakalász; [[globular-amphora-culture|Globular Amphora]] dozen+ examples) read as draught pairs (sometimes cow/calf to spare draught oxen).
- **Plough-marks:** buried soils under monuments; Danish/NW European horizon ~3500 BC as indigenism test case in the introduction.
- **Wheels and yokes:** waterlogged NL/DK wheels (3rd mill. Corded Ware period contexts in his dating); Vinelz and Petersfehn yokes possibly 3rd mill.
- **Pathology:** osteological draught signatures refined by later literature he notes (Davis, Benecke) without importing post-1997 residue chemistry.

## Applications

- **Settlement restructuring** with plough: more extensive clearings, pastoral fallow, shorter site duration; Hungarian Plain dispersal phases co-timed with traction evidence (Ch.10–11).
- **Social inequality markers:** village headmen buried with draught oxen vs followers (as of Sherratt 1984 SE Europe / steppe discussion).
- **Exchange reach:** equid transport extends bulk and prestige circuits (up to ~1000 km examples he cites in Near Eastern proto-urban contexts).
- **Comparative:** Mesoamerica shows urbanism without animal traction — so traction is enabling for Old World scale/rarity patterns, not a universal urban prerequisite (Sherratt 1981).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Direct European vehicle finds are rare and preservation-biased.
- Plough-mark horizons are not self-interpreting social revolutions.
- Local invention vs introduction remains disputed (Chapman vs Sherratt 1987).
- Dates are of essay composition; dendro-calibrated vs uncalibrated conventions vary by paper — treat as of Sherratt’s cited horizon.
