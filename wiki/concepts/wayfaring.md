# Wayfaring

## Definition as Coined

**Wayfaring** is [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s term (developed in
*[[ingold-lines-2007|Lines]]*, 2007) for the fundamental mode by which inhabitants
move through and come to know the world: travelling **along** a path, where the
journey itself is a way of knowing, and where the traveller's life is "led" as a
continuous line of movement and growth. Ingold opposes it to **transport** — being
carried across a surface between predetermined points, where movement is merely the
means of getting a body (or goods) from an origin to a destination and contributes
nothing to knowledge (attributed, Ingold 2007).

## Semantic History

*(Enriched from [[ingold-lines-2007|Lines]] during integration.)* The wayfarer is the
descendant of Ingold's earlier "dwelling" inhabitant (see [[dwelling-perspective]],
[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]); in
*Lines* the dwelling perspective is recast in terms of the line the wayfarer leaves
and follows. The contrast wayfaring/transport aligns with his later
inhabitant/occupant and [[meshwork]]/network oppositions. In *[[ingold-being-alive-2011|Being
Alive]]* (2011) Ingold goes further, stating that he now **regrets his earlier emphasis on
"dwelling"** and prefers **wayfaring** as the primary term — dwelling can suggest a settled
attachment to place, whereas wayfaring keeps movement and openness at the centre
(attributed, Ingold 2011).

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — an interpretive contrast. It reorganizes descriptions of
travel, navigation, perception, and place: a place, for Ingold, is not a bounded node
but a **knot** where the lines of many wayfarers are tied together (attributed).

## Applications

Ingold builds the contrast from a wide ethnographic base (all attributed as his
readings): the **Inuit of Igloolik** (Aporta), for whom travelling is "a way of being"
that helps define who the traveller is; the **Batek** of Malaysia (Lye), who say tuber
roots "walk" as people do and treat root-growth and human wayfaring as "phenomena of
the same kind"; the **Foi** of Papua New Guinea (Weiner), whose paths become "conduits
of inscribed activity"; and the **Orochon** reindeer-hunters of Sakhalin (Kwon), where
a winding outbound hunting path (wayfaring) and an approximately straight return
sledge-path to a cache (transport) run side by side without intersecting — showing the
two modes coexist. Transport, Ingold stresses, is defined **not** by mechanized power
but by "the dissolution of the intimate bond that, in wayfaring, couples locomotion and
perception": a reindeer-rider or a sailor remains a wayfarer, while a passenger "does
not himself move but is rather moved."

The contrast reorganizes **knowledge**: the wayfarer "knows as he goes" — knowledge
"grows as we go along" rather than being assembled "upwardly" from fixed point-
observations into a cognitive map (a direct application of the [[dwelling-perspective]],
against the "cognitive map" model). It also reframes **place**: against the settler/
nomad opposition, "Wayfaring… is neither placeless nor place-bound but place-making,"
and place is a knot in the [[meshwork]]. Ingold's programmatic summary: "An ecology of
life… must be one of threads and traces, not of nodes and connectors… Ecology… is the
study of the life of lines."

## Critiques and Limitations

As with Ingold's oppositional pairs generally, the wayfaring/transport contrast is a
heuristic ideal-type rather than an empirical classification; critics note the risk of
romanticizing pre-modern "movement" and of reading a philosophical contrast as a
historical narrative of decline. Ingold himself concedes the pair is never pure — "no
line… even with a ruler… can ever be perfectly straight," and transport "always entails
an element of wayfaring."
