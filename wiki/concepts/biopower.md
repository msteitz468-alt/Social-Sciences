# Biopower

## Definition as Coined

**Biopower** (*bio-pouvoir*) is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s term (attributed), introduced in the concluding chapter of *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I ("Right of Death and Power over Life"), for the modern form of power that "brought life and its mechanisms into the realm of explicit calculations and made knowledge-power an agent of transformation of human life." Foucault frames it by contrast with classical sovereign power: "The right which was formulated as the 'power of life and death' was in reality the right to take life or let live. Its symbol, after all, was the sword" — a power of "deduction." From the seventeenth century this was displaced: "the ancient right to take life or let live was replaced by a power to foster life or disallow it to the point of death."

## Semantic History

Foucault distinguishes **bio-history** (the interference of biological and historical processes) from **bio-power** proper. He describes biopower as organized around **two poles**:
- an **"anatomo-politics of the human body"** — discipline, the optimization of the body's forces, its "docility" and integration into production (the first pole to form); and
- a **"bio-politics of the population"** — the regulation of "births and mortality, the level of health, life expectancy and longevity," the "species body."

"The disciplines of the body and the regulations of the population constituted the two poles around which the organization of power over life was deployed" (attributed). The anatomo-political pole is developed at full technical length in *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* as [[disciplinary-power]] and [[docile-bodies]] — read the two books as paired poles, not as synonyms. In Foucault's later Collège de France lectures (not ingested here) "biopolitics" and "governmentality" are developed further; in reception, "biopolitics" often names the population pole specifically, "biopower" the whole formation.

## Operationalizations

Foucault exhibits biopower through eighteenth–nineteenth-century demography (citing Quesnay, Moheau, Süssmilch), public health, the army and the school, and eugenics. He ties it to capitalism — biopower "was without question an indispensable element in the development of capitalism," enabling "the controlled insertion of bodies into the machinery of production" — and to the ascendancy of **the norm over the law**: "A normalizing society is the historical outcome of a technology of power centered on life."

## Applications

- **Sex as the pivot.** Foucault's key link to the rest of the book: sex "was at the pivot of the two axes along which developed the entire political technology of life... a means of access both to the life of the body and the life of the species." This is why the [[deployment-of-sexuality]] became so heavily invested.
- **The species as political object.** Revising Aristotle: "modern man is an animal whose politics places his existence as a living being in question."
- **War, racism, genocide** (attributed, charged material extracted in full): under biopower, "massacres have become vital"; "the power to expose a whole population to death is the underside of the power to guarantee an individual's continued existence." Foucault locates modern **state racism** at the junction of the older "symbolics of blood" and the "analytics of sexuality," naming Nazism as "the most cunning and the most naive... combination of the fantasies of blood and the paroxysms of a disciplinary power," accompanied by "systematic genocide." He is explicit this danger arises "because power is situated and exercised at the level of life, the species, the race," not from a "return of the ancient right to kill."

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Under-specification** (attributed): the two poles and the state's role are sketched programmatically in this volume; the concept was elaborated only later (lectures), so single-volume readings are partial.
- **Agency and resistance** (attributed): as with the [[analytics-of-power]], critics ask what resistance to biopower looks like. Foucault notes resistance itself came to invoke "life... man's concrete essence" as its ground: "It was life more than the law that became the issue of political struggles."
- Later theorists (Agamben, Hardt & Negri, Esposito — not ingested) substantially revised the concept; treat this page as anchored to Foucault's 1976 statement.
