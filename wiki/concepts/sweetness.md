# Sweetness

## Definition as Coined

As analytic object in *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]*, **sweetness** is both a widespread human liking for sweet taste and a historically variable complex of meanings, sources, and social uses. [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] centers sucrose's rise without equating all sweetness with cane sugar; honey precedes sugar in European imagery and endearment language.

Mintz engages species predisposition ("sweet tooth") *and* cultural convention: demand is not self-evident; meaning arises from use. Sucrose is "protean" — syrup to crystal, brown to white, medicinal to decorative to caloric staple.

## Semantic History

- **Pre-sucrose Europe:** honey as primary sweetener and metaphor; Chaucer sparse on sugar; Shakespeare multiplies sugar imagery still among rarities.
- **Mintz's English trajectory:** exotic/rare → elite medicine, spice, and display ("subtleties") → luxury sweetener → mass necessity tightly coupled with tea.
- **Modern notoriety:** by the late twentieth century sugar is ubiquitous enough to be the object of nutrition wars (Mintz's opening framing).
- **Linguistic bridge:** Indo-European *swād* as source of both "sweet" and "persuade"; sugared/honeyed speech as outside+inside meaning bridge.

Related dietary structure: traditional **starch-core + flavor-fringe** diets (Mintz, drawing on Rozin "flavor principle"); modern high-flesh/fat/sugar regimes invert the archaic pattern.

## Operationalizations

Distinguish sucrose from other sweeteners; track five early uses (medicine, spice, decoration, sweetener, preservative) before sugar-as-food; per-capita lb/year and caloric share; meal position (dessert/"pudding"); class/age/sex differentials (often missing from national series).

## Applications

British meal structure; confectionery and royal/political sugar sculpture; tea-and-sugar; working-class treacle and jam; contemporary anti-sugar campaigns.

## Critiques and Limitations

Biological preference arguments vs cultural construction — Mintz holds both without reducing history to either. National averages understate differentials. Artificial sweeteners and HFCS (Mintz Ch. 5) reopen what "sweetness" sources count as.
