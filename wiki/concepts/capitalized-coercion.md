# Capitalized Coercion

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly, [[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]].)*
Tilly's analytic pair: **capital** — "any tangible mobile resources, and enforceable claims on
such resources," accumulated in **cities**, defining "a realm of exploitation" — and
**coercion** — "all concerted application, threatened or actual, of action that commonly causes
loss or damage," concentrated in **states**, defining "a realm of domination." "Cities shape the
destinies of states chiefly by serving as containers and distribution points for capital...
States themselves operate chiefly as containers and deployers of coercive means." Where
accumulation and concentration of coercion grow together, states form; where capital does,
cities.

The relative regional presence of the two generated **three trajectories of state formation**:

- **Coercion-intensive** (Russia, Poland, Hungary, Brandenburg-Prussia, Sweden, Castile):
  few cities, little capital; rulers "squeezed the means of war from their own populations,"
  allying with armed landlords, enserfing peasants, "building massive structures of extraction."
  Ivan III/IV paying servitors in land ("the quintessential coercion-intensive strategy");
  Poland as "the exception that proved the rule" — landlords so strong the crown fielded 16,000
  men against neighbors' hundreds of thousands, and was partitioned.
- **Capital-intensive** (Venice, Genoa, Ragusa, the Dutch Republic): abundant capital, weak
  lords; rulers "relied on compacts with capitalists... to rent or purchase military force, and
  thereby warred without building vast permanent state structures" — municipal and national
  government fused, war outsourced to condottieri and navies, finance by funded public debt
  (Venice's Monte Vecchio; Amsterdam raising "1 million florins at only 3 percent in two days").
- **Capitalized coercion** (England/Britain, France): both present in rough balance; "rulers
  could play one against the other, using purchased force to check the holders of private
  armies and national armies to persuade the holders of private capital" — at the price of
  concessions (Magna Carta, Parliament, the Bank of England 1694). "In the long run... the
  combination gave rulers of capitalized-coercion states the decisive advantage in warfare; as
  a consequence, their sort of state — the [[national-state]] — won out."

## Semantic History

The scheme generalizes and corrects Tilly's own 1975 war→extraction model, absorbing Elias's
tax/military "two sides of the same monopoly" — which Tilly amends to "a trio. The missing
member, credit" — and Ardant's fiscal "physiology" (the fit between fiscal system and regional
economy). Path dependence is explicit: "the path followed by a state up to a certain point in
time limited the strategies open to its rulers beyond that point" — "Why didn't Venice or
Russia become England?... their previous histories haunted them." All paths **converged** on
the national state after ~1750 under war's selection pressure, and the closing chapter reapplies
the typology to the post-1945 world (new states inheriting coercion-intensive endowments).

## Operationalizations

Regional city density and capital concentration versus armed-landlord power, mapped over the
European "urban column" (northern Italy to the Low Countries — the band of fragmented
sovereignty; national states formed "at the edges of the urban column"). Rulers' modes of
acquiring coercion — **seizing, making, or buying** — index the trajectory; the five-part
revenue typology (tribute, rents, flows, stocks, income taxes) indexes monetization.
Ideal-typical: Tilly concedes "all states were more composite than my simple typology demands"
(Britain, Prussia, Iberia as composites).

## Applications

- The explanatory core of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]] and of the book's answers
  to its six questions about European [[state-formation]].
- Explains representative institutions (bargaining with capital), serfdom's second edition
  (coercion-intensive paths), and the [[city-state]]'s rise and extinction.
- Widely reused in comparative state-formation research beyond Europe.

## Critiques and Limitations

The trajectories are constructed retrospectively from the national state's victory, inviting
selection-on-the-outcome objections; the composite-state concession limits classificatory bite;
and the capital/coercion pair compresses culture, law, and religion into two variables — as
critics from institutionalist and cultural corners have pressed. Attributed throughout; a
model, not a finding.
