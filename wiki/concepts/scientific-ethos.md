# Scientific Ethos

## Definition as Coined

[[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (*[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Chs. XVII–XVIII especially) described the **ethos of science** as an affectively toned complex of values and norms held to be binding on the man of science — “not codified” as a formal code but internalized (he preferred “latter-day… superego”). Four institutional imperatives:

| Imperative | Core sense (Merton, attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Universalism** | Truth-claims judged by preestablished impersonal criteria, not the particular attributes of the claimant; ethnocentrism and status particularism strain the norm |
| **Communism** (communalism) | Common ownership of findings; secrecy and patenting as institutional strains |
| **Disinterestedness** | Institutional demand, not personal virtue — peer “policing” and verifiability support it; priority competition creates illicit incentives with scant opportunity |
| **Organized skepticism** | Suspended judgment and scrutiny of claims, including sacred domains — generates conflict with other institutions |

Ch. XVII links hostility to science (e.g. Nazi purge/utility pressures) to clash between scientific ethos and other social orders; Ch. XVIII ties affinity of science to **democratic** structure via universalism.

## Semantic History

Founding vocabulary of the sociology of science. Later often acronymized **CUDOS** (with variants). Contested by interest, constructivist, and laboratory-studies traditions that treat the ethos as ideology, aspiration, or partial description rather than operating institutional code.

## Operationalizations

Historical-comparative analysis of science under democratic vs authoritarian structures; deviance from norms (fraud, particularism) as strain evidence. Later: citation and priority disputes as tests of "communism" and recognition systems (not all in this volume).

## Applications

Science under Nazism and other political hostilities; relation of science to economic and religious structures (Puritanism–science essays); engineering and industrial research tensions.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether the four norms describe actual scientific practice; gender and exclusion histories; commercial science and classified research; post-Mertonian sociology of scientific knowledge.
