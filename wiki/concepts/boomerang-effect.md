# Boomerang Effect

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s term (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Ch. 1) for the way modern mega-hazards eventually strike back at the very agents and institutions that produce and profit from them: "Sooner or later the risks also catch up with those who produce or profit from them... The formerly 'latent side effects' strike back even at the centers of their production" (Beck 1986: 38).

## Definition as Coined

Risks cannot be permanently externalized onto the poor, the distant, or the future. Beck argued that hazards display "a social boomerang effect in their diffusion: even the rich and powerful are not safe from them." The effect operates not only physically (pollutants "dip under borders" — acid rain, dying forests in countries without heavy industry) but through secondary media: **ecological expropriation**, where legal ownership persists but the social and economic value of contaminated land collapses.

## Semantic History

The concept underwrites Beck's larger claim that "risk positions are not class positions." He conceded that risks initially "adhere to the class pattern, only inversely" (the poor bear more), but argued the boomerang effect marks the limit of that stratification: "the class-specific barriers fall before the air we all breathe." Reduced to a formula: "**poverty is hierarchic, smog is democratic**" (Beck 1986: 37). Beck was careful to add that this equalization is *within a hazard's scope* and does not erase international inequality — the "new international inequalities" section names Bhopal and the transfer of hazardous industry to the global South, where the boomerang still returns to wealthy exporters via imported contaminated food.

## Operationalizations

Not measured; illustrated. Beck marshalled agricultural-chemical intensification data and species-decline "red lists" (from the German Council of Environmental Advisors, 1985) as illustrations, and the return of exported pesticides "in the fruit, cacao beans and tea leaves."

## Applications

Central to the risk-society thesis's political claim: because hazards ultimately implicate their producers, risk generates a novel, cross-class, potentially global political constituency (a tendency Beck linked to the "utopia of a world society" and later to "world risk society"). It also grounds his contrast of the two epochs' "cultures" — class society's culture of *visible* wealth versus risk society's *invisible* hazards: "The visible cannot compete with the invisible."

## Critiques and Limitations

The equalizing claim is the most criticized element of the risk-society thesis: environmental-justice research documents that exposure remains strongly stratified by class, race, and geography, suggesting the boomerang is slower and more unevenly distributed than the "smog is democratic" formula implies (see [[risk-society]] critiques). Beck's own text supplies the counter-evidence in its international-inequalities discussion.
