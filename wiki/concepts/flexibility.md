# Flexibility

**Flexibility** is the watchword of the "new capitalism" — the promise that dismantling rigid bureaucratic routine will free institutions and workers to adapt. [[sennett-richard|Richard Sennett]], in *[[sennett-corrosion-of-character-1998|The Corrosion of Character]]* (1998), argues (attributed) that in practice the term names not freedom but **a new system of power and control**: "Revulsion against bureaucratic routine and pursuit of flexibility has produced new structures of power and control, rather than created the conditions which set us free." This page treats flexibility as Sennett anatomizes it — a management-and-ideology construct with personal consequences for [[character]]. Its labor-market face is treated at [[flexible-employment]].

## Definition as Coined

The word entered English in the fifteenth century from the observation that a tree "may bend in the wind" yet spring back — naming a capacity *both to yield and to recover*. Sennett's central rhetorical point (attributed) is that modern flexibility practices keep only the bending: "The practices of flexibility, however, focus mostly on the forces bending people," dropping the recovery/restoration half of the original metaphor.

## The Three-Element Anatomy

Sennett's core analytic framework (attributed) holds that "the system of power which lurks in modern forms of flexibility" has three elements:

1. **Discontinuous reinvention of institutions** — change of a *ruptural* kind (Sennett borrows the anthropologist Edmund Leach's distinction between continuous and irreversible change), pursued through "reengineering," delayering, and vertical disaggregation. Sennett marshals evidence (American Management Association and Wyatt Companies studies of the early 1990s) that repeated downsizing often *lowered* profits and productivity, yet persists because reorganizing firms' stock prices rise regardless.
2. **Flexible specialization of production** — Piore and Sabel's northern-Italian model of firms occupying temporary market niches through "permanent innovation," enabled by reprogrammable machinery and instant market data. Sennett stresses (attributed) that which "flexibility" a society gets depends on how it defines the common good — contrasting Michel Albert's "Rhine" and "Anglo-American" models.
3. **Concentration without centralization of power** — the claim that flat, networked firms decentralize power is false: SIMS-style monitoring gives the top a comprehensive view while pushing risk and unachievable targets downward. Sennett adopts Bennett Harrison's phrase "concentration without centralization" and the image of an "archipelago" of activities commanded from a mainland. "Domination from the top is both strong and shapeless."

## Semantic History

Sennett traces a migration in the word's ethical charge (attributed): early-modern philosophers (Locke, Hume) equated the flexible self with responsiveness to sensation, and Adam Smith's moral theory rested on such sympathetic bending; nineteenth-century political economists after Smith (J.S. Mill) recast flexibility as *entrepreneurial virtue* and personal freedom. The contemporary "new political economy," Sennett argues, "betrays this personal desire for freedom" by turning flexibility into a structure of control. (Note: Adam Smith and J.S. Mill do not have pages in this wiki; they are cited here in attributed voice as figures in Sennett's genealogy.)

## Operationalizations

Labor economists operationalize flexibility through contingent- and non-standard-work shares, working-time diversification, and flextime uptake (see [[flexible-employment]] for the Castells-based measurement layer). Sennett deliberately resists treating flexibility as a scalar: for him flextime is not simply a benefit but a *rationed privilege* (citing Lotte Bailyn) whose most "flexible" form — home-working — often *increases* electronic surveillance, exchanging "one form of submission to power — face-to-face — for another which is electronic."

## Applications

- The computerized Boston bakery (*Corrosion*, Ch. 4) as flexible specialization that deskills while appearing to upskill.
- Flextime and Jeannette's remotely-monitored accounting team as "concentration without centralization" in miniature.
- The Davos elite as the flexible regime's "victors," at ease with fragmentation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Sennett's account is essayistic and polemical: it generalizes a power-structure reading from selected cases and management literature, and critics argue it understates flexibility's genuine benefits to some workers and conflates distinct phenomena (post-Fordist production, financialization, welfare retrenchment) under one word. See [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]] for the dispute over whether flexibility corrodes character and social bonds as Sennett claims.
