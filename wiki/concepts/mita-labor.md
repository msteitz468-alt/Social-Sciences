# Mit'a (Labor Tax)

## Definition as Coined

**Mit’a** (Quechua) designates a **‘turn’ of labor** and equal exchange of work. At folk level, Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) pairs it with Aymara *ayni* (service rendered with obligatory repayment of equal kind) under a creed of reciprocity: when something is rendered, something of equal kind or value will be returned. Ayni and mit’a allow a household to temporarily mobilize more labor than it has workers—essential for mountain agropastoralism. Spanish colonial *mita* (especially Potosí mining drafts) is a transformed successor, not identical to the Inca institution.

## Semantic History

From local reciprocal labor among kindred to state draft extracting tribute as work; colonial mita carries heavier coercion and different economic aims. Keep the registers distinct. Segmented adobe construction, canal modules, and Chan Chan wall segments are read as mit’a-like modular organization from Preceramic mesh-bag fill through Gallinazo/Moche to Inca (as of Moseley 2001)—**inferred organization**, not ethnohistoric labels on every building.

## Operationalizations

**Folk level (as of Moseley 2001):** Ideally same type of labor returned; strict accounting; couples work defined field rows; multi-ayllu projects assign each ayllu a section; workers expect to be fed; labor “banked” across generations.

**Imperial Inca:** Labor as coin of the realm; ayllu as taxation unit under local kurakas; decimal organization of male household heads (10 → 100 → … → Hunu Kuraka of 10,000). Three labor types: **agricultural taxation** (both sexes; threefold land division—gods, state, community), **mit’a service** (annual draft of able-bodied males—construction, military, roads, *chaski* runners), **textile taxation** (fiber doled out; women spin/weave). Rotating force of 20,000 corvée laborers reputedly worked decades on Sacsahuaman. Mit’a invested in conquest land, reclamation, terracing (Urubamba), [[mitmaq]] colonies, and tens of thousands of km of roads. Staple finance feeds temporary mit’a; wealth finance converts surplus via artisans into elite reciprocity goods.

**Earlier and coastal archaeology:** Gallinazo/Moche segmented construction and makers’ marks on >100 million bricks at Huaca del Sol; Pampa Grande Fortaleza chamber-and-fill with mixed makers’ marks (labor organized differently from Sol); Wari provincial cell-like rooms as possible mit’a barracks; Chan Chan *ciudadela* walls erected in mit’a segments (as of Moseley 2001).

## Applications

Explains monument scale without currency; labor-levy economy of Tahuantinsuyu and earlier kuraka-class wealth-finance strategy (mit’a land → staples → artisans → elite goods). Comparative handle for corporate construction organization on the coast and sierra.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Labor tax” is an etic gloss; indigenous categories emphasize reciprocity and obligation to huacas/rulers. Colonial sources may overstate or reshape Inca practice. Archaeological “mit’a-like” modular construction is an analogy from Inca ethnohistory—valid hypothesis-generator, not proof of identical institutions in Gallinazo or Chan Chan.
