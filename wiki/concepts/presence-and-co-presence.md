# Presence and Co-Presence

## Definition as Coined

In *[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|The Constitution of Society]]*, Giddens treats **co-presence** as the condition of interaction in which agents are mutually available to one another (drawing on [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s full-conditions of co-presence), and **presence-availability** as the means by which actors can come together. Social integration is reciprocity under co-presence; system integration extends beyond it.

## Semantic History

Reworks Goffmanian interaction order and Merleau-Ponty spatiality of situation within structuration. Electronic media transform presence-availability by separating communication from bodily transportation.

## Operationalizations

Encounter analysis; mediated vs face-to-face; total institutions' enforced/denied co-presence patterns.

## Applications

- [[social-and-system-integration]]
- Front/back regionalization and surveillance
- Historical claim: until ~last century, remote contacts still eventually required co-presence carriers

## Critiques and Limitations

Digital mediation (post-1984) requires updating presence-availability — not in primary 1984 text.

## From Gamble, *Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]*; as of Gamble 1999.)*

Gamble applies Giddens’s **social integration** (what people do together under co-presence — Goffman) vs **system integration** (patterns extended *in absentia*) to the Palaeolithic. What marked differences among Palaeolithic societies, he argues, was the mix of emotional, material, and symbolic **resources** brought to interactions and the **rules** limiting network size — not the mere fact of co-presence (attributed, ch. 2). Goffman’s hierarchy (social occasion → situation → gatherings of two+ individuals in immediate presence) is adapted into his [[locale]] typology.

**Gatherings** depend on attention in co-presence and embodied artifacts; **social occasions** externalize the environment so locales become places via disembodied symbolic artifacts that structure attention across absence — “the past is now used to organize the future by making references between place, person and object … when they are apart” (attributed, ch. 8). Disembodied information (art, tracks, artifacts) extends intimate/effective networks *in absentia* and structures the archaeological record (attributed, ch. 3).

Neanderthal societies, on Gamble’s reading, were “built on co-presence” with evidence for social rather than system integration; co-operation was routine negotiation within intimate and effective networks, not an external organizing principle requiring symbolic language to stretch relations across time/space (attributed, ch. 5). Conversational group-size limits (~4; ~1.7 m) and hearth seating ethnoarchaeology supply uniformitarian anchors for co-present performance (Dunbar; Binford Mask Site via Gamble). The European transition after ~60–21 Kyr bp is framed as individuals becoming creative agents **beyond** the limitations set by co-presence rules — [[release-from-proximity]] (attributed).
