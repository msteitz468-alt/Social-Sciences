# Structural Violence

## Definition as Coined

**Structural violence** names harm done not by an identifiable actor but by the social, economic, and
political *structures* — poverty, racism, gender inequality, unequal access to resources — that place
some people systematically in harm's way. The term was coined by the Norwegian peace researcher **Johan
Galtung** (1969), who contrasted "personal" or "direct" violence (a perpetrator, a blow) with the
"indirect," "built into the structure" violence that "shows up as unequal power and consequently as
unequal life chances." Where direct violence has an author, structural violence is the violence of
arrangements: it is present "when human beings are being influenced so that their actual somatic and
mental realizations are below their potential realizations."

## Semantic History

The concept migrated from peace studies into liberation theology (the "institutionalized violence" of
the Medellín conference, 1968) and then, decisively for this wiki's coverage, into
[[medical-anthropology|medical anthropology]] through **[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]**, who made it the
organizing frame of *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999) and
*Pathologies of Power* (2003). Farmer's reworking (attributed) narrows and sharpens Galtung's broad
usage into a claim about *disease*: "The sickness of [the poor] is a result of structural violence:
neither culture nor pure individual will is at fault; rather, historically given (and often
economically driven) processes and forces conspire to constrain individual agency. Structural violence
is visited upon all those whose social status denies them access to the fruits of scientific and social
progress." In Farmer's usage the concept is tightly bound to **[[embodiment]]** — structural violence
is how social forces "come to be embodied as biological events" — and to his critique of
**[[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest claims of causality]]**, which he treats as the intellectual
error that renders structural violence invisible by recoding it as cultural difference or individual
choice.

The term sits in a family of neighboring concepts that this wiki records separately: Galtung's original
peace-studies usage; [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]'s **[[everyday-violence]]** (the
routinized violence of ordinary institutional life); and [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s
**[[symbolic-violence]]** (the misrecognized violence of imposed categories). Farmer explicitly draws
on the second — "structural violence requires its apologists, witting or unwitting" — folding symbolic
violence in as the ideological work that sustains structural violence.

## Operationalizations

Structural violence resists direct measurement; it is inferred from patterned disparities. In Farmer's
program it is operationalized through the *gradient*: disease risk and outcome tracked against social
position (race and place as proxies for poverty in U.S. TB data; partner *profession* rather than
partner *number* as the [[hiv-aids|HIV]] risk factor among [[do-kay|rural Haitian women]]). Its clearest
empirical signature is the "access" finding: where clinical access barriers are removed, survival
differences between Black and white, male and female, poor and rich AIDS patients *disappear*
(Chaisson, Keruly, and Moore, cited by Farmer) — showing the biology to be structured, not fated. The
concept's most powerful illustration in *Infections and Inequalities* is emic: Anita Joseph, dying of
AIDS, traces her own causal chain — "My parents lost their land to the water... that is what makes us
poor" — from the [[do-kay|Péligre dam]] to her migration to the city to her infection.

## Applications

- **[[tuberculosis]]** — Farmer's three-stage embodiment mechanism (poverty and racism raise the odds
  of infection, of progression to active disease, and of poor outcome) is the paradigm case.
- **[[hiv-aids]]** — the "political economy of risk": who is exposed and who is shielded is set by
  economic, political, and gender forces, not by "culture" or attitude.
- Structural violence is also invoked to explain why direct violence accompanies inequality (mass
  incarceration, death squads, police brutality) — extreme inequality, on this view, "requires" other
  forms of violence to maintain the status quo (see [[everyday-violence]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is `contested`. Critics charge that it is *too capacious* — if every disparity is
"violence," the word loses analytic edge and its causal claims go unspecified (who or what is the agent?).
A second critique, internal to anthropology, is that a heavy structural-violence frame can flatten local
meaning and agency, treating the poor only as victims of forces; Farmer partly concedes this in his own
reflexive self-critique (see [[farmer-paul]]). A third is definitional drift: Galtung's original usage,
liberation theology's, and Farmer's are not identical, and the term is sometimes deployed as moral
labeling rather than analysis. Recorded here in attributed voice throughout: structural violence is a
framework's claim about causation, not a wiki-voice finding.
