# New Antisemitism

## Definition as Coined

The claim (attributed to its proponents — [[wistrich-robert|Wistrich]], Taguieff, Bauer, and others) that since roughly 1967, and intensively since 2000, a new form of [[antisemitism]] has emerged in which hostility is directed at Israel as the "collective Jew": demonization, delegitimization, and double standards applied to the Jewish state carry forward the functions and imagery of classical antisemitism under an anti-Zionist vocabulary.

## Semantic History

- "New antisemitism" was used in the 1970s (e.g., Forster & Epstein 1974) for post-war right-wing and Arab-state hostility.
- The current sense crystallized after the Second Intifada and Durban conference (2000–2001), in French debate (Taguieff's *nouvelle judéophobie*, 2002) and Anglophone scholarship.
- Wistrich's *[[wistrich-lethal-obsession-2010|A Lethal Obsession]]* (2010) is the maximal continuity statement: anti-Zionism as the contemporary mutation of a single longue-durée demonological tradition.

Earlier definitional formulations Wistrich endorses: Harris O. Schoenberg (1981, quoted) — the new antisemitism "asserts that the Jewish people is imaginary… that the Holocaust never happened and that the Jewish people, accordingly, has no rights accorded to other peoples"; Jean Améry (1969) — antisemitism is "contained in anti-Zionism as the thunderstorm is part of the cloud." Wistrich's own component vocabulary: **Holocaust inversion** (Jews/Israelis recast as Nazis), **"antisemitism without Jews"** (high stereotype endorsement in near-Jewless populations — Poland, Spain, Malaysia, Japan), and the displacement of "Jew" by "Zionist" as the operative term.

## Operationalizations

Surveys separate "classic" stereotype items from Israel-related items and measure their correlation; the IHRA working definition (2016) operationalizes the concept in policy, and is itself contested for that reason. How the concept is operationalized largely determines whether the phenomenon is found — a standing measurement dispute.

Survey evidence marshaled by proponents (all single-poll findings, attributed via Wistrich 2010): ADL European surveys 2005–2009 (~36% "too much power in business," ~45% "talk too much about the Holocaust," rising dual-loyalty scores); Pew 2006 on European Muslim attitudes (unfavorable views of Jews: 47% British Muslims vs. lower general-population rates); CST (UK) and CNCDH (France) incident series showing spikes synchronized with Middle East violence (Oct 2000, 2006 Lebanon, Jan 2009 Gaza — the CST's highest-ever monthly total). Critics reply that incident-spike correlation shows conflict-driven hostility, not continuity with classical antisemitism — the inference is precisely what [[new-antisemitism-debate]] disputes.

## Applications

Used in monitoring reports (EUMC/FRA, ADL), national commissions on antisemitism, and historiography of post-1945 anti-Jewish politics (Soviet anti-Zionism, Islamist discourse).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics (Klug, Lerman, Finkelstein, and others) argue the concept conflates political criticism of a state with racism, is deployed to shield Israeli policy from critique, and lacks a stable operational boundary. The dispute is recorded at [[new-antisemitism-debate]]; this page does not adjudicate it.
