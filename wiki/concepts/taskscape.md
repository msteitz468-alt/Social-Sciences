# Taskscape

## Definition as Coined

[[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]] introduces **taskscape** in "The temporality of the landscape" (essay lineage 1993; collected as Ch. 11 of *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]*, 2000). A **task** is any practical operation carried out by a skilled agent in an environment as part of normal business of life. The **taskscape** is "the entire ensemble of tasks, in their mutual interlocking" — "to labour what the landscape is to land." As with landscape, one can ask what a taskscape is *like*, not how much of it there is: it is qualitative and heterogeneous *(Ingold 2000, Ch. 11, attributed)*.

## Semantic History

Ingold sets taskscape against both naturalistic landscape-as-neutral-backdrop and culturalistic landscape-as-symbolic ordering of space. Under the [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]], landscape is "an enduring record of – and testimony to – the lives and works of past generations who have dwelt within it"; landscape is ultimately the **congealed** or embodied form of the taskscape. Temporality of the taskscape is essentially **social** and **rhythmic** (resonance of interlocking activity cycles), not metronomic clock-time; music, more than painting, mirrors that temporal form. Paths make the taskscape visible in the landscape. In Ch. 17 he opposes the qualitative taskscape to abstract homogeneous labour-time under capitalist "time is money." Heavy uptake in **landscape archaeology** as an alternative to static site maps; related later to multi-species and craft studies.

## Operationalizations

- Archaeology: material traces as residues of interlocking tasks and temporalities.
- Anthropology: everyday dwelling without sealing "economy" from "ritual" as separate domains — rites and ceremonies are "as integral to the taskscape as are boundary" works (Ch. 11).
- Industrial ethnography: task-orientation vs clock regulation (railroaders' *tekhnē* of time-keeping; Ch. 17).

## Applications

Bridging archaeology and sociocultural anthropology (Ingold's stated aim); environmental and landscape studies; craft/work ethnography; critique of cultural ecology as merely stacking symbolic layers over pragmatic adaptation (Part II roadmap).

Within historical archaeology, Lu Ann De Cunzo (2004) applied the concept to farming landscapes and "cultures of agriculture" in Delaware, arguing that farming people create taskscapes through rhythmic action and interaction that shapes community formation ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). Paul Mullins (2000: 767) and Ann Stahl (2002: 829) criticized phenomenological/taskscape-influenced landscape approaches — Mullins for risking neoliberal individualism ("elevat[ing] the 'individual' ... to a position that is, at best, loosely related to power relations"), Stahl for remaining rooted in Cartesian mind-body dualism (as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of aestheticizing work and under-specifying property, conflict, and domination within "interlocking tasks." Not a standardized measurement construct. Clock-time and commodity perspectives remain real overlays even if task orientation is argued as primary (Ch. 17).
