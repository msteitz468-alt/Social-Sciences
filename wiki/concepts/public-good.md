A public good is, in the standard economic definition, a good whose benefits cannot be withheld from non-payers and are not diminished by any one consumer's use. James S. Coleman, in *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990), adopts this economic definition and extends it sociologically to phenomena not conventionally treated as goods at all — sanctions against norm-violators, and social capital itself — arguing that the public-good character of these social resources produces systematic underprovision relative to their social value.

## Definition as Coined

Paul Samuelson's (1954) criteria are the source definition Coleman adopts: a public good is characterized by nonrivalry ("nonconservation") and nonexcludability — it "cannot be supplied to one without being supplied to all" within a bounded domain — in contrast to private goods, which are divisible, alienable, conserved by use, and free of externalities. Coleman states this definition explicitly before extending it: "a public good is defined... by nonconservation and nonexcludability" (attributed to Samuelson 1954). His own extension treats sanctions against externality-imposing actors (e.g., breaking trust, breaking a code of ethics) as themselves a public good, since an effective sanction benefits the whole community, not only the sanctioner — which is the analytic hinge connecting public goods to the [[free-rider-problem]] and to [[social-norm]] formation.

## Semantic History

Samuelson's 1954 definition entered mainstream welfare economics as the formal criterion for market failure requiring collective (often state) provision. Coleman's move is to carry this criterion out of the economics of tangible goods and into the sociology of norms and relationships: because sanctioning a norm-violator is nonexcludable among the norm's beneficiaries, it inherits the same underprovision logic as a lighthouse or national defense. He generalizes the argument furthest in his treatment of [[social-capital-coleman]], where he argues that most forms of social capital are themselves a public good — a by-product of activity undertaken for other reasons, whose benefits accrue to persons other than those whose effort sustains it, producing systematic underinvestment relative to social value. The formal restatement in chapter 30 (Part V) recasts the same public-good logic within Coleman's quantitative linear system of action, treating an indivisible event with consequences for multiple actors (a "common project") as generating positive or negative externalities whose efficient provision is blocked, in practice, by the second-order free-rider problem even though a frictionless model would predict the collectively efficient outcome.

## Operationalizations

**The action-rights bank and common-project matrices**: Coleman formalizes 2-actor and 3-actor "common project" payoff matrices (tables 10.1–10.3). With two actors, bilateral exchange of rights of control suffices to reach a social optimum and no norm is needed; with three or more actors, no pairwise exchange suffices — motivating the conceptual device of an "action-rights bank," a hypothetical central clearinghouse selling shares in the right to control each actor's contribution, bridging his account of norms to majority-voting systems (rights held collectively and partitioned into votes).

**Public bads**: Coleman analyzes overgrazing of a commons as the parallel negative case, where each herd-owner's self-interested grazing increase imposes costs on others, treated identically to positive-externality public-good cases.

**Social capital as public good**: unlike physical capital (private, alienable, benefits fully captured by the investor) and unlike human capital produced through schooling (benefits largely captured by the individual), social capital is mostly a by-product of activity undertaken for other reasons, and its benefits accrue to others — producing systematic underinvestment. Coleman's examples: mothers whose associational ties support a school's social capital but who rationally leave for paid work; families that move for job opportunities, severing ties that supported others' norm enforcement, at a net community loss despite a net personal gain. He contrasts this with social capital inside for-profit business organizations, whose benefits are captured by the investing actor and thus not systematically underprovided. He also credits Loury's (1987) point that social capital's "practical inalienability" — it is not private, divisible property of any one beneficiary — further distinguishes it from ordinary private goods.

## Applications

Coleman applies the public-good/free-rider frame to education, arguing its public-good character (a family does not internalize many of the future costs and benefits the child-as-adult will generate for the wider society) is one explanation for why societies collectively restrict families to earmarked vouchers rather than unconditional cash transfers — alongside a second, independent explanation from precommitment reasoning (citing Elster 1979). He formally links public goods to [[corporate-actor|corporate actors]]: when rights over an indivisible, externality-laden event cannot be efficiently exercised individually, actors have an interest in transferring those rights collectively, forming a corporate actor to hold them — though he notes corporately held rights are not thereby guaranteed to be exercised in the common interest of all rights-holders, an agency problem internal to corporate actors.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman's extension of the public-good concept from tangible economic goods to sanctions and social capital is a theoretical move, not an empirically settled classification — whether "social capital" behaves enough like Samuelson's textbook public good to inherit its underprovision prediction is argued by analogy (shared nonexcludability among beneficiaries) rather than demonstrated by independent measurement of over- or under-provision. The formal chapter-30 restatement depends on Coleman's broader linear-system apparatus (Cobb-Douglas utility specification, competitive-equilibrium solution), which trades generality for tractability; predictions derived there inherit the same simplifying assumptions. Coleman's own qualifier — that a frictionless model predicts efficient collective provision "disregarding free-rider problems," while the second-order free-rider problem in practice blocks it — marks the underprovision claim as conditional on real-world transaction costs and imperfect social capital, not a universal law.
