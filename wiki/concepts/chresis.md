# Chrēsis (Use of Pleasures)

## Definition as Coined

*Chrēsis aphrodisiōn* (χρῆσις ἀφροδισίων), "the **use** of pleasures," is the phrase [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] takes in *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984) as the classical Greek **mode of subjection** in sexual ethics — the way the free man was invited to govern his relation to the [[aphrodisia]]. He defines it (attributed) not as a code of permitted and forbidden acts but as a strategy: "prudence, reflection, and calculation in the way one distributed and controlled" one's acts. The moral question posed by *chrēsis* is not *what* one does but *how, when, and how much*.

## Semantic History

*Chrēsis* is ordinary Greek for "use"; Foucault's move is to make the sexual "use of pleasures" the hinge of an ethics organized around **stylized self-regulation rather than law**. In his periodizing scheme the term marks the pole opposite the later Christian "hermeneutics of desire": where the Christian subject will be asked to *decipher* and *purify* desire, the Greek subject is asked only to *use* pleasures well. This is why Foucault treats the phrase as the title-concept of the whole volume — it names an ethics of the right measure, not of prohibition or interpretation.

## Operationalizations — the threefold strategy

Foucault identifies (as his own analytic schema derived from the sources, not an explicit ancient taxonomy) three variables governing the right use of pleasures (attributed):
1. **Need** — pleasure should answer natural need, not manufacture desires beyond it. He reads Diogenes the Cynic's provocative public masturbation ("if breakfast be not absurd, neither is it absurd to breakfast in the marketplace") as the extreme statement of reducing the *aphrodisia* to minimal natural satisfaction, and Xenophon's Socrates as the moderate version — pleasure sustained *through* need, not eliminated. Two forms of immoderation follow: excess of fulfillment (*plēthora*) and excess of artifice (contrived, "unnatural" means).
2. **Timeliness (*kairos*)** — the "right time," a pervasive Greek ethical-technical category (also central to medicine, navigation, statecraft), applied to sexual practice at the scale of life-stage, season, and hour. Foucault flags as remarkable Xenophon's explanation of the wrongness of incest by a violation of the *right time* (age-mismatch of parents) rather than by an intrinsic prohibition.
3. **Status** — unlike the "modulated universality" of Christian morality (general principles specified by circumstance), classical sexual ethics had few universal precepts; standards were tailored to one's rank and way of life. The higher one's public standing and authority, the more rigorous the self-imposed moderation required — a ruler's self-mastery being at once precondition and proof of fitness to rule others (Xenophon's Agesilaus and Cyrus).

## Applications

*Chrēsis* is the connective tissue of *The Use of Pleasure*: the same "how/when/how much" logic recurs across [[dietetics]] (regimen of the body), the [[ethics-foucauldian|economics]] of marriage, and the erotics of the love of boys, where the boy's honor is said to turn not on refusing or accepting suitors but on the *right use* of the situation ("these things are not, in themselves and absolutely, good or bad; they vary according to who practices them"). The work of holding to the right use is [[enkrateia]]; its telos is a [[arts-of-existence|stylization of existence]] rather than obedience to a code.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Foucault concedes the threefold strategy is **his** reconstruction, not a Greek doctrine — a synthetic reading of scattered prescriptive texts; its neatness is his, and is attributed accordingly.
- The contrast with a "codifying" Christian morality is the same strong-discontinuity thesis that classicists dispute for the [[aphrodisia]]; treat the sharp *use-versus-code* opposition as Foucault's frame.
- Because *chrēsis* is drawn from an elite male archive, the "freedom" it stylizes is, on Foucault's own admission in the Conclusion, the freedom of "the smallest minority of the population," resting on "a very harsh system of inequalities and constraints" affecting women and slaves.
