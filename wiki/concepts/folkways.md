# Folkways

## Definition as Coined

[[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]] coined **folkways** in *[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]*
(1906) for the habitual, uniform ways of acting that a group develops unconsciously to
satisfy its needs. In his account they arise not from reason or design but from the
repetition of "petty acts": *"ways of doing things were selected, which were expedient...
All at last adopted the same way for the same purpose; hence the ways turned into customs
and became mass phenomena"* (§1). The young acquire them *"by tradition, imitation, and
authority,"* and they become *"uniform, universal in the group, imperative, and
invariable"* (§1).

Sumner's core attributed claims:

- **Folkways are made unconsciously.** They are *"not creations of human purpose and wit...
  like products of natural forces which men unconsciously set in operation"* (§3). No one
  invents them; their origins are *"lost in mystery"* (§7).
- **They are a societal force.** By habit and custom a folkway *"exerts a strain on every
  individual within its range; therefore it rises to a societal force"* (§2) — a
  formulation close to [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s external, coercive
  [[social-fact|social fact]], reached independently.
- **They are subject to two strains:** a *strain of improvement* (better adaptation of
  means to ends, driven by pain) and a *strain of consistency* with one another (§5). The
  strain toward consistency is the germ of a society's integrated "ethos."
- **They become "right" and "true."** Because folkways are traditional and simply exist,
  *"in the folkways, whatever is, is right"* (§31); notions of right, rights, and morals
  are *"products of the folkways"* (§31), not independent standards brought to test them.
- **Folkways become mores when a welfare judgment is added.** *"When the elements of truth
  and right are developed into doctrines of welfare, the folkways are raised to another
  plane... Then we call them the [[mores]]"* (§34). Mores are folkways plus a philosophy of
  societal welfare.

Folkways still form continuously: Sumner instances the usages of "stage coach times,"
street cars, and the telephone (§22) — the mechanism is not confined to "primitive"
society.

## Semantic History

The term entered sociology fully formed in 1906 and has been remarkably stable, largely
because it is inseparable from Sumner's paired term [[mores]]. In mid-20th-century American
sociology "folkways" became a standard label for the lightest, least morally-charged
customs (etiquette, dress, manners) — the low end of a **folkways → mores → laws**
gradient of normative intensity used in introductory teaching, where violating a folkway
draws mild disapproval and violating a more draws serious sanction. This textbook gradient
is a simplification of Sumner: he treated folkways and mores as developmental stages of the
same process (welfare-judgment added), not merely as degrees of severity.

## Operationalizations

Sumner offered no measure; folkways are read qualitatively off the ethnographic and
historical record as recurrent "use and wont." Later traditions supplied cognate,
sometimes operationalized constructs: [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s [[social-fact|social facts]]
(externality + constraint), and — at the level of durable embodied disposition —
Bourdieu's *habitus*. Each competes with folkways as an account of
how supra-individual regularity is generated and reproduced.

## Applications

Sumner used the concept to reinterpret institutions as sedimented folkways: *"Institutions
and laws are produced out of mores"* (§61). Property, marriage, and religion are the
*"most primary institutions,"* all of which *"began in folkways"* and became institutions
only when a concept was joined to a structure (§61). Laws are downstream of custom:
*"legislation, to be strong, must be consistent with the mores"* (§62) — a claim with
direct implications for reform (see [[mores]], Ch. XV; the anti-reform reading is
attributed to Sumner on [[social-darwinism]]).

### Kroeber restatement

[[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]] (*[[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963|Culture Patterns & Processes]]*, §116) restates [[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]]'s folkways/mores vocabulary in a Boasian culture chapter: folkways as traditional ways of the group; mores as folkways charged with sanction and rightness; moral codes as culturally relative without collapsing into meaninglessness of all standards (paired with his [[ethos-and-eidos]] discussion of values).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Circularity / unfalsifiability.** If whatever exists is "right" and every custom is by
  definition adaptive to its conditions, the framework risks explaining every outcome after
  the fact. Sumner's own §65 relativism ("for the men of the time there are no 'bad'
  mores") sharpens this: the concept describes persistence but weakly predicts change.
- **Under-theorized agency and power.** Folkways as unconscious mass sediment leaves little
  room for interest, conflict, or deliberate institution-building — a gap
  [[historical-materialism|conflict-theoretic]] and later practice-theoretic accounts press.
- **Second-hand evidence.** The illustrative base is uncritically compiled period
  ethnography (see [[sumner-folkways-1906]] reliability notes); the concept is sounder than
  the catalogue of "savage" cases marshaled for it.

The construct nonetheless survived its evolutionary scaffolding: "folkways" and "mores"
remain live analytic vocabulary long after Sumner's [[social-darwinism|social Darwinism]]
was abandoned.
