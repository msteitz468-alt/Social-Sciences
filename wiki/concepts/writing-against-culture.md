# Writing Against Culture

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to [[abu-lughod-lila|Lila Abu-Lughod]], "Writing Against Culture," in Fox ed., *Recapturing Anthropology*, 1991; enacted in *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* 1993.)* A strategy for ethnographic writing that **works against the anthropological culture concept's tendency to essentialize, homogenize, and timelessize "others"** by refusing typifying generalization and instead reconstructing how particular individuals live, contest, and feel the institutions social science names.

Abu-Lughod's stock example: instead of "The Bongo-Bongo are polygynous," ask how three women and their husband in one community actually live "polygyny." Instead of labeling Mediterranean societies "honor and shame" cultures, ask how an Egyptian schoolgirl waiting for arranged marriage in the 1980s lived that complex.

## Semantic History

**Positive career of "culture."** Abu-Lughod noted (attributed) that culture had been a progressive anti-racist concept — learned, not racial, pluralizing human difference against evolutionary hierarchy — yet still tends to make difference solid and separate. She linked this to critiques of anthropology's "incarceration" of non-Western peoples (Fabian), culture-collecting that detaches experiences from temporal occasions (Clifford), and the professional need for discrete others for relativism or cultural self-critique.

**Three effects of generalization.** In the *Writing Women's Worlds* introduction she argued (attributed) that ethnographic generalization:

1. is a language of **power** (professional discourse of expertise standing outside what it describes — extending Dorothy Smith's critique of sociology as ruling apparatus to anthropology's intersocietal colonial-linked origins);
2. produces **homogeneity, coherence, and timelessness** — flattening conflict, doubt, and historical change inside the cultural boundary;
3. invents **discrete bounded cultures** ("the Nuer," "the Awlad ʿAli Bedouin") as "dangerous fictions" populated by generic cultural beings.

**Storytelling as method.** Refusing generalization via narrative, she held, (1) highlights the constructed quality of typifying typicality; (2) shows particulars as always constitutive of experience; (3) makes visible how people strategize, feel, and contest within limited, historically changing discourses — representing practice textually rather than only asserting it (Bourdieu-adjacent but textual).

**Relation to Writing Culture.** The program is a **feminist and halfie** response to the [[writing-culture-debate|Writing Culture]] crisis: it accepts that ethnographies are partial narratives (Clifford, Bruner) but rejects both naive realism and experimental forms that remain unreadable or solipsistic while leaving global power intact. Critical ethnography serving "writing against culture" is, she stated, **a project for the West** — not automatically good for the subjects whose words are fixed and made public.

**Later circulation.** The phrase became a stock citation in reflexive anthropology, Middle East ethnography, and feminist method debates. It is a writing strategy, not a claim that cultural difference is unreal.

## Operationalizations

In *Writing Women's Worlds*: five chapters titled with social-science institution names (patrilineality, polygyny, reproduction, [[patrilateral-parallel-cousin-marriage|patrilateral parallel-cousin marriage]], honor and shame) filled with **named women's stories, arguments, songs, and disagreements** from 1980s [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] life rather than rules. Companion *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study|Veiled Sentiments]]* had already analyzed dual discourses of honor and poetry; this book pushes further into narrative particularity ([[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]).

Paired with [[tactical-humanism]]: humanist conventions (individuals, dailiness, traces of the ethnographer, familiar discourse) as limited political tactics against othering.

## Applications

- Cited as alternative to both classic holism and purely experimental polyvocal texts.

## Critiques and Limitations

Abu-Lughod herself listed limits (attributed): critical ethnography may not serve hosts; personal stories can be more intrusive than conventional ethnography; humanism is Western local language; anthropology remains on the side of power. Textual reform does not decolonize institutional production (Fox). Subjects may prefer formal portraits and high poetry over candid everyday talk fixed in print. The strategy can be misread as pure particularism or anti-theory; she denied privileging micro over macro, arguing particulars also carry "larger forces."
