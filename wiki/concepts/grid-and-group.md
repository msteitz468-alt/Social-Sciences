# Grid and Group

Core two-dimensional scheme of [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]'s *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]* (1970; clarified in the 1973 Pelican revision). Parent program: [[grid-group-analysis]]. Study: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]].

## Definition as Coined

Douglas defined **grid** as the dimension of order/classification — the scope and coherent articulation of the categories that constitute a world view available to an individual. She defined **group** as the dimension of pressure — the experience of having little option but to consent to the overwhelming demands of other people in a bounded membership. Cosmologies, ritual styles, and [[body-symbolism|body symbolism]], she argued, vary with position on these two axes.

## Semantic History

- **1970 first edition:** grid/group introduced; early diagrams later judged complicated by Douglas (1996 intro).
- **1973 Pelican revision:** main difference is clarification of grid and group; shift from hard separate quadrants toward scatter patterns of group pressure and classificatory coherence readable from ethnography or historical biography.
- **1980s–90s cultural theory:** Thompson, Ellis, Wildavsky and Douglas's risk work restate related ideas as cultural biases (hierarchy, egalitarianism/enclave, individualism, fatalism) — related lineage, not identical to 1970 definitions; do not collapse without attribution.
- Not the same as everyday "in-group/out-group" or small-group sociology labels.

## Operationalizations

Douglas operationalizes via comparative reading of ethnography and institutional cases (who can constrain whom; how complete and exclusive the classification system is). Bernstein's positional/personal family control and speech-code strength are the proximate model ([[bernstein-basil]], [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]). Later formalizations (e.g. Gross & Rayner) exist outside this source.

## Applications

Ritual vs anti-ritual; witchcraft cosmologies; bodily control and trance; sect and hierarchy; Bog Irish Friday abstinence; Nilotic and hunter cases; Western student/Catholic anti-ritualism. See [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Dimensions can be read circularly into cases; early diagrams confusing even to the author later; ethnographic illustrations are secondary; two axes may underfit multi-dimensional variation. Contested as both too schematic and too powerful.
