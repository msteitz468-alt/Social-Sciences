# World-Economy

A foundational analytical concept of [[world-systems-analysis]], contrasted with the **world-empire**. A **world-economy** is a large-scale social system integrated by a division of labor but *not* unified under a single political authority; a **world-empire** is integrated by both a division of labor *and* a single political center. [[wallerstein-immanuel|Wallerstein]] took the term from Fernand Braudel's *économie-monde* and made it the load-bearing unit of analysis.

## Definition as Coined

Wallerstein's definitions (attributed). In *[[wallerstein-world-systems-analysis-introduction-2004|World-Systems Analysis: An Introduction]]* (2004): "What we mean by a world-economy (Braudel's économie-monde) is a large geographic zone within which there is a division of labor and hence significant internal exchange of basic or essential goods as well as flows of capital and labor. A defining feature of a world-economy is that it is not bounded by a unitary political structure." Its glossary crystallizes the pair: "A world-empire (such as the Roman Empire, Han China) is a large bureaucratic structure with a single political center and an axial division of labor, but multiple cultures. A world-economy is a large axial division of labor with multiple political centers and multiple cultures."

What unifies a world-economy, absent political or cultural homogeneity, is "the division of labor which is constituted within it." The historical thesis (from *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]*, 1974) is that a new European world-economy formed in the "long sixteenth century" (c.1450–1640) and — uniquely — survived rather than being converted into a world-empire.

## Semantic History

- **Braudel:** *économie-monde* — a self-contained economic world with a center, middle zones, and margins, in the *longue durée*.
- **Wallerstein (1974– ):** the world-economy as the true **unit of analysis** for social change, displacing the "nation-state" or "national society." Prior world-economies, he argues, "either fell apart or were forcibly transformed... into world-empires"; the modern one endured because it fused with **[[capitalist-world-economy|capitalism]]**.
- The distinction is the hinge of his whole argument that **capitalism requires a world-economy**: only where no single state can override economic actors can the "endless accumulation of capital" be given systemic priority — "a capitalist system cannot exist within any framework except that of a world-economy."

## Operationalizations

A world-economy is identified by a genuine, integrated **axial division of labor** (see [[axial-division-of-labor]]) spanning multiple sovereign states — as distinct from mere long-distance luxury trade between separate systems (the **[[external-arena]]** in Wallerstein's terms). The boundary question — what is *inside* the division of labor versus in the external arena — is operationally central and is argued case by case in *MWS I* (e.g., eastern Europe as periphery versus Russia or the Ottoman Empire as, at that time, external arena).

## Applications

The concept anchors world-systems accounts of the origin of capitalism, [[core-periphery]] polarization, [[world-system-hegemony|hegemonic cycles]], and the interstate system. See [[world-systems-analysis]], [[capitalist-world-economy]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The world-economy-as-unit move is exactly what critics contest: orthodox Marxists (Brenner) charge it substitutes exchange/market relations for the relations of production as the definition of capitalism; comparative-historical sociologists (Skocpol) argue it makes states epiphenomenal to economics; and the boundary between "inside the division of labor" and "external arena" can look drawn to fit the thesis. The concept's own author treats it as a research program's premise, not a proven fact — its warrant is the historical reconstruction in the *Modern World-System* volumes.
