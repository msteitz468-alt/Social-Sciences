# Appropriation of Wealth

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]* (2003), [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] analyzes **appropriation of wealth** as the set of mechanisms through which tiny upper classes in early civilizations captured agricultural and craft surpluses — taxes, rents, corvée labour, tribute, and related institutional extractions. He argues rights to surplus and labour were often more important than ownership of land as such. Related to but not identical with [[social-surplus]] and [[redistribution]]. All quantitative ratios are **Trigger’s synthesis estimates**, not census facts.

## Semantic History

Sits at the intersection of Marxist surplus extraction, Polanyian redistribution, and comparative early-state fiscal anthropology. Trigger’s ch. 17 organizes cross-case mechanisms rather than a single-prime-mover theory. He rejects both pure gift-trade economies and whole-economy administrative models as complete descriptions (partial engagement with Polanyi).

## Operationalizations

Trigger 2003 ch. 17–18 (study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]):

| Claim (attributed) | Content |

| Free-farmer tax | often ~**1/10–1/5** of produce |
| Labour share | ~70–90% of labour to food production; surplus never large |
| Mechanisms | tax, rent, corvée, tribute (tribute vs tax distinguished methodologically) |
| Polity effect | city-state vs territorial regimes shape who controls exotic goods and elite craft |

## Applications

Class systems in [[early-civilization]]; links [[state-formation]], [[market]], and elite craft control under [[territorial-state]] vs [[city-state]] exchange regimes. Religion projects appropriation cosmically via sacrifice (farmers feed gods as they feed upper classes) — Trigger’s “constitution” reading of cult.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ratio estimates and mechanism labels are synthesis-level; specialist economic histories revise local fiscal systems after 2001. Do not promote Trigger’s ratios to wiki-voice demography.

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) reframes early appropriation as extraction from a **grain-and-manpower module** under coercion — tax in grain and labor plus bondage — rather than voluntary surplus. All claims **attributed**. Complements Trigger’s tax/rent/corvée/tribute matrix; deeper on fiscal **legibility** of cereals ([[grain-hypothesis]]). Links: [[social-surplus]], [[the-state]], [[legibility]].

**Grain as the appropriable form.** Archaic states, in the crudest material terms, were all agrarian and required an appropriable surplus of agro-pastoral products to feed nonproducers (clerks, artisans, soldiers, priests, aristocrats). Only cereal grains, Scott argues, serve as a taxation basis that is visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, and “rationable”; no historical lentil, yam, or cassava states. Cereals grow above ground and ripen roughly simultaneously — enabling one-operation harvest confiscation, medieval-tithe sheaf models, and fields classifiable by soil and yield. Tubers stay underground and spoil faster; Frederick the Great’s potato orders are cited as understanding that tuber planters are harder to disperse by armies. Umma lowest-class daily ration ~two liters of barley in beveled bowls; grains as standards of measurement and value including labor. One Ur III account claims barges carried half the Ur-region barley harvest to royal depots; “hard to imagine the early state without grain.”

**Grain tax and fund of rent.** Taxes in kind or labor meant cultivators supplied a “fund of rent”; Scott judges grain tax unlikely less than a **fifth of the harvest** by agrarian-historical comparison — close to Trigger’s free-farmer ~1/10–1/5 band from a different evidence path. Taxes pushed cultivators closer to the subsistence precipice so crop failure after tax could mean ruin; elites might occasionally disburse stored grain in famine to keep population intact. Early state “battens” onto dense grain-and-manpower concentrations, colonizing and intensifying them as elite niche-construction — fattening “the goose that lays the golden eggs.”

**Bondage, corvée, and booty.** Only through unfree, coerced labor (corvée, forced deliveries, debt bondage, serfdom, communal bondage, slavery) was surplus brought into being under early-state demography when means of production were not monopolized (Boserup via Scott). Slavery/bondage was, Scott disputes consensus, crucial in Mesopotamia for (1) textile export labor (Uruk workshops up to ~9,000 women — ≥20% of inhabitants if city ~40–45k), (2) disposable proletariat for onerous work (canals, walls), (3) elite status token. War as continuous human-resources policy: captives over territory; Weberian “booty capitalism” — military campaign for profit; loot lists prioritize livestock and prisoners by skill. Walls keep taxpaying cultivators **in** as much as barbarians out.

**Recording machine.** Peasantries understand the state as a recording/measuring machine; first act of many peasant rebellions is burning the records office. Uruk Level IV tablets: lists of barley, captives, slaves; land taxed by size, soil quality, expected yield; labor gangs of captives/slaves/corvée ubiquitous. Sumerian kingship symbol “rod and line” = surveyor’s tools. Overland grain requisition reach ~48 km with draft animals on flat alluvium (greatly extended by water); the core nearest capital is both key to power and Achilles’ heel — squeezed first in crisis; elites “all thumbs and no fingers” for fine-tuning appropriation.
