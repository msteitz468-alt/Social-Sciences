# Context of situation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Coral Gardens and Their Magic]]* Vol. II (1935), [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] argued that the speech of a pre-literate community "is never framed to be taken outside its **context of situation**," and that language "exists only in actual use within the context of real utterance." The real linguistic fact is not an isolated word (a dictionary figment) but an **utterance with its setting** — spoken words plus facial expression, gesture, bodily activities, the group present, and the part of the environment on which people are engaged. **Meaning**, on this view, is the **function of words** within that setting: what the utterance does (co-ordinates work, binds parties, releases magical force, educates children), not a mental duplicate of ideas transferred from speaker to hearer.

## Semantic History

- **Malinowski 1935 (primary for this wiki):** Parts IV and VI of *Coral Gardens* Vol. II state the doctrine fully and document it on garden terminology and spells. He widened "context" beyond co-text of sentences to pragmatic and cultural setting; opposed normative grammar, historicist philology, and missionary reverse-translation.
- **Relation to *Argonauts* (1922):** The earlier book already required corpus of native texts and treated magic as verbal action; Vol. II generalizes the linguistic theory.
- **Later reception (not yet ingested as primary):** The phrase entered British contextual linguistics (J. R. Firth) and influenced ethnography of speaking / functional approaches to meaning. Record reception as positions when those sources are ingested — do not assert a wiki-voice lineage beyond what primary texts warrant.
- **Near-neighbours in sociology:** Not the same as Thomas's [[definition-of-the-situation]] (subjective meaning assignment in interaction); Malinowski's construct is ethnographic-linguistic and centers public cultural activity.

## Operationalizations

Malinowski's working method (attributed):

1. Record vernacular texts in context of use (or reconstruct the pragmatic setting for second-hand narratives).
2. Interlinear rendering with **fixed label-equivalents** (consistent glosses even when free translation must rephrase).
3. Free translation justified by ethnographic knowledge of situation.
4. Grammatical commentary (classificatory particles, possessives, *b*/*l* verb modifiers, telegraphic style).
5. For magic: place spells in **situation of magic** (belief, rite, force-conveyance) and report both **coefficient of weirdness** and **coefficient of intelligibility** ([[magic]], [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]]).

## Applications

- **Garden terminology:** *buyagu*, *pwaypwaya*, *dayma*, *kaulo*, *towosi*, *kayaku* — each disambiguated by gardening, legal, or council context ([[trobriand-islanders]], [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]]).
- **Famine narrative (*molu*):** second-hand narrative whose meanings borrow from past scarcity experience and gesture/activity context.
- **Speech-in-action:** plot allotment talk after *kayaku*; co-ordination in communal work; fire-making instruction (*munumunu*); legal and gift formulae as binding acts.
- **Magical formulae:** Part VII corpus (Formulae 1–44) commented under sociological / ritual / dogmatic heads — spells meaningless without that frame.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Functional reduction of meaning** — later linguistic anthropology may treat "meaning as function in situation" as incomplete for reference, structure, or ideology (file as debate when sources ingested).
- **Reconstructive risk** — ethnographer supplies situational frame; single-observer bias.
- **Vol. I dependence** — Vol. II assumes technological and calendrical context described in the missing tillage volume.
- **Colonial recording** — immobilising "volatile" speech on paper under unequal power relations is itself a production condition.
