# Petty Bourgeoisie

## Definition as Coined

In Marxist class analysis, the **petty bourgeoisie** are independent producers who own their means of production and work them largely without employing wage labor (self-employed own-account workers), positioned between capital and labor. *[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]* (Wright) operationalizes them via **self-employment** measures and analyzes their long-run decline and late-20th-century partial revival in the United States (Ch. 4).

## Semantic History

Classical Marxism predicted secular destruction of independent producers under capital concentration. Post-industrial and human-capital arguments sometimes predict new niches for consultants and knowledge-based self-employment. Wright treats the theoretical status of self-employment carefully (not all self-employed are "independent producers" in the classical sense).

## Operationalizations

Census and survey self-employed vs employee; sectoral shift-share decompositions; time series (US mid-20th c. through 1990). Employer vs pure petty bourgeois cut by number of employees.

## Applications

*Class Counts* Ch. 4 "The fall and rise of the American petty bourgeoisie"; comparative class distributions (Ch. 2); permeability analyses treating petty-bourgeois cells as distinctive.

## Critiques and Limitations

Home-based childcare and sector reclassifications can artifactually move self-employment rates (Wright notes); "petty bourgeois" label over-assimilates heterogeneous self-employment. Attributed.
