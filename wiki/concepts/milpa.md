# Milpa

## Definition as Coined

[[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] treat **milpa** / **swidden** as among the oldest cultivation methods in the Maya area: clear forest or fallow, burn, plant **maize, beans, squashes**, and other domesticates (including manioc), then multi-year fallow (they report roughly 1–6+ fallow years per cultivation year as a range). It is an **extensive** agriculture form still common in contemporary Maya communities. The term is emic/colonial (field of maize agriculture) that has migrated into archaeological analysis as a named subsistence strategy—often over-extended as *the* ancient Maya system.

## Semantic History

- **Archaic–Preclassic:** Earliest cultivation form in their developmental sketch after territorially based foraging; maize domesticated from teosinte (*Zea mexicana*), possibly Balsas River region; domestic maize in highland Mexico over 6,000 years ago; spread to coastal Chiapas and eastern Mesoamerica (Belize; Lake Yojoa, Honduras) by 3000 BC (as of Sharer & Traxler 2006). Preclassic lowland diet mixed—bone isotopes at Cuello show maize under 30% of diet; modern households may get up to ~75% from maize—do not project modern maize dominance onto Preclassic villages.
- **“Swidden hypothesis” (older):** Claim that ancient Maya relied on swidden alone. **Rejected** by Sharer & Traxler: pure swidden cannot support large dense Classic populations; Classic demography required diverse intensive methods. No direct archaeological proof of ancient swidden as such (pollen shows early maize; ash-preserved fields at [[ceren]] show tilled maize rows under volcanic ash—intensive garden/field texture rather than proof of long-fallow burn cycles alone).
- **Intensification mosaic:** Continuous cultivation on fertile alluvium; terracing (Río Bec ~150,000 ha; Caracol/Xunantunich/Maya Mountains ≥40,000 ha Classic); household gardens (Hansen Preclassic plots at Nakbe/[[el-mirador]] with swamp muck; Ceren plant inventory: maize, beans, squash, agave, cacao, chiles, cotton, malanga, manioc); arboriculture (ramón potentially ~10× maize yield per land unit; cacao plantations inferred at Copan, Quirigua, Lubaantun); raised fields akin to Central Mexican [[chinampa]]s; irrigation (e.g. [[kaminaljuyu]] canals from Lake Miraflores by ~600 BC); canals/reservoirs and bajo drainage.
- **Expansion + intensification model:** Logical/conjectural scenario—early mix of hunting/gathering + long-fallow swidden; later shorter fallows, arboriculture, continuous cultivation, raised fields, limited animal husbandry/aquaculture as population grew.
- **Collapse historiography:** Early ecological position blamed swidden for destroying soil fertility and forcing abandonment—**superseded** by evidence of diversified intensive agriculture. Revised position: intensification under population pressure increased vulnerability to erosion, fertility loss, and water shortage; lake cores and Copan Valley sediments show deforestation runoff—self-induced degradation may have tipped a stretched subsistence system ([[classic-maya-collapse-debate]]).
- **Colonial–modern continuity:** After Conquest, men continued maize/beans cultivation (often with steel tools); agricultural-cycle beliefs survived while accommodating Christianity; ethnography of living Maya (including native-born scholars) remains a reconstruction source for ancient practice—**analogy flagged**, not identity.

## Operationalizations

| Mode | Character (attributed S&T 2006) |
|---|---|
| Extensive milpa/swidden | Clear–burn–plant; multi-year fallow; low density support |
| Kitchen gardens | Infield diversity; Ceren/Nakbe evidence |
| Terracing | Hillslope soil and water control at scale |
| Raised fields / wetlands | Canal–ridge systems; fish in canals |
| Arboriculture | Tree crops (ramón, cacao, fruit) raising effective yield |
| Hydraulic systems | Reservoirs, irrigation canals, bajo drainage |
| Protein side | Hunting/fishing essential throughout; dog and turkey limited husbandry; stingless bees |

Gendered training (Landa via Sharer & Traxler): boys associated with field cultivation and hunting (*milpa*); girls with tortillas, housekeeping, weaving, spinning, markets, and kitchen gardens.

## Applications

- Modeling [[classic-maya]] subsistence carrying capacity and [[agricultural-intensification]].
- Interpreting settlement dispersal vs urban cores (Ricketson Uaxactun survey density beyond pure swidden support).
- Comparing modern highland-to-lowland migration soil exhaustion with ancient multi-method sustainability (authors: ancient success harmonized with dispersed forest ecosystem; every adaptive system has limits—when exceeded, Maya adaptations ultimately failed).
- Collapse debates: from single-cause swidden ruin to multi-factor intensification vulnerability.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Milpa ≠ the whole system.** Treating Classic demography as swidden-only is a known failure mode.
- **Absence of direct archaeological “burn field” signatures** makes ancient milpa often inferred rather than observed; Ceren preserves cultivation texture under exceptional preservation, not a universal template.
- **Ethnographic analogy** from contemporary milpa to Classic royal economies must be labeled hypothesis-generating.
- **Maize dominance** is time- and status-variable (Preclassic isotopes vs modern diet shares).
- Ecological collapse arguments require dated sediment/pollen evidence and must not sole-cause from environment ([[societal-collapse]] protocol: environment usually *contributed_to*, rarely sole *caused_by*).
