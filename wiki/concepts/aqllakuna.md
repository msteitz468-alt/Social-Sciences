# Aqllakuna (Chosen Women)

## Definition as Coined

**Aqllakuna** (singular *aqlla*; also *aclla*, *acllacuna*; Spanish “Chosen Women”; OCR *agllakuna*) are, as of [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002), the only girls to receive formal instruction under Inca rule. Attractive girls were taken from their families at about age ten and placed in Inca settlements under provincial governor supervision. In the sequestered **House of the Chosen Women** (*aqllawasi*), lifelong virgin religious instructors taught religion, weaving, cooking, and *chicha*-making. Cobo said that as many as **200 women of various ages** could be found in the largest *aqllawasi*. After about four years, girls were ready to serve as *mamakuna* (priestesses) or to marry men who merited the honor for service to the Inca. The cloth they wove and the *chicha* they brewed were consumed in great amounts in festivities and sacrifices to the Sun; *chicha* production began by chewing maize or plants, spat into jars to ferment.

Alias note: older literature often writes **aclla / acllacuna**; D'Altroy uses *aqllakuna*. *Mamakuna* names the senior/priestess career path within or adjacent to the institution.

## Semantic History

D'Altroy places *aqllakuna* among the specialized labor statuses that, in the latter decades of Inca rule, increasingly augmented and replaced rotating corvée: *mitmaqkuna*, [[yanakuna|yanakuna]], and *aqllakuna* (Murra 1980b via D'Altroy). Women’s institutions (*mamakuna*/*aqllakuna*) produced for the state, the Sun, aristocrats, and ceremonial largess; specialized workshops appear early, but many craft enclaves were part of a late reorganization under Wayna Qhapaq. Census practice tracked sex-specific life-stage and ability categories (not chronological age), supporting different recruitment streams including *aqllakuna*. In Cuzco’s urban plan, Hatunkancha (“Great enclosure”) southeast of Awkaypata included the house of the *mamakuna* or *aqllakuna* among its interior buildings. Provincial architecture routinely included sequestered *aqllakuna* sectors alongside Sun temples and *kancha*/*kallanka* forms.

## Operationalizations

**Training and dual destiny.** Formal sequestration (~age ten), ~four years of instruction, then either religious career (*mamakuna*) or honorific marriage. Among nobility, the most prestigious secondary wife came by the emperor’s order; multiple wives equaled household labor wealth. Marriage to an *aqlla* was a **sign of special favor** for commoner valor in triumphs—class-stratified rewards also included clothing, gold/silver plates, captured women, weapons, and flocks for commoners versus administrative sinecures, lands, multiple wives, and litter/stool privileges for nobles.

**Textile economy.** Tens of thousands of coordinated workers supplied state cloth. Male weavers (*qompi kamayuq*) made many finer and feathered textiles, but *mamakuna* made the finest *qompi* for sacrifices, idols, and the ruler; *aqllakuna* and lords’ wives also wove fine cloth—including annual garment sets for the Inca by officials’ wives (Polo). Royal clothing was often woven by *aqllakuna*/*mamakuna*; emperor’s gold-, silver-, and feather-embroidered vestments involved royal concubines as well. Military consumption of cloth was probably the largest single use (blankets; annual clothing/sandal sets), with finer textiles as battle awards.

**Provincial centers — archaeology.** At [[huanuco-pampa|Huánuco Pampa]] (Morris and Thompson 1985 via D'Altroy), though the site could house up to ~15,000, permanent population was only a fraction; a north-sector compound (single narrow doorway, 50 regular buildings) yielded weaving/spinning tools and *chicha* jars **consistent with permanent *aqllakuna***. At Puerta de La Paya (Argentina), Ambrosetti’s ~202 burials with spindle whorls and weaving tools of young women were “almost certainly” *aqllakuna* set to work by the Inca; related counts mention >1,000 spindle whorls with young women. Morris observed that Inca provincial centers often lack cemeteries—ethnic Incas treated presence as temporary; proper burial was ancestral homeland—so many dead may have been transported home, making *aqllakuna* burials at work sites an important exception pattern.

**Machu Picchu caution.** Bingham recovered a majority of young women among buried individuals, feeding a “house of the chosen women” interpretation; D'Altroy flags that it is **not certain** those young women represent a cross-section of the settlement’s residents—sampling and preservation bias.

**Ritual and royal person.** Atawallpa’s clothing and anything he touched were stored for annual burning to thwart witchcraft (Pedro Pizarro via D'Altroy)—clothing often *aqllakuna*-woven. High-elevation *qhapaq ucha*-type offerings dressed children/youths or paired metal statuettes in finery, linking textile prestige to imperial sacrifice. Atawallpa’s reported plan for captured Spaniards included castrating some for women’s institutions (as D'Altroy recounts the mutual-deception plans before Cajamarca)—illustrating how Spaniards and Incas both imagined the institution as a destination for captives/servitors.

## Applications

- **State ritual economy:** cloth and *chicha* for Sun festivals and sacrifices ([[inca-empire]], [[cuzco]]).
- **Class and gender hierarchy:** only girls with formal instruction; marriage redistribution as honor and labor wealth.
- **Provincial architecture:** sequestered *aqllawasi* sectors at installations ([[huanuco-pampa]]).
- **Permanent specialist layer** with [[yanakuna]] and [[mitmaq|mitmaqkuna]] against rotating [[mita-labor|mit'a]].
- **Military reward structure:** *aqlla* marriage as elite favor for commoner valor.
- **Sun cult institutions:** lands, herds, storehouses, and female religious personnel (including *aqllawasi* associations at major shrines such as Pachacamac, as D'Altroy notes in Sun-cult institutional scale).

## Critiques and Limitations

**Source gender bias.** D'Altroy notes sources that detail married rights of men are essentially silent on divorced or widowed women—so *aqllakuna* career exits other than *mamakuna* service or awarded marriage are poorly lit.

**Archaeological over-identification.** Young female burials with spindle whorls (Puerta de La Paya; Machu Picchu) invite *aqllakuna* labels; D'Altroy treats Puerta de La Paya as near-certain and Machu Picchu as uncertain. Provincial “lack of cemeteries” complicates demographic inference.

**OCR and synonymy.** *Mamakuna* and *aqllakuna* overlap in palace descriptions (Hatunkancha house of *mamakuna* or *aqllakuna*); keep both terms as D'Altroy’s dual destinies (priestesses vs rewarded brides) rather than collapsing them.

**Not free labor market specialists.** Like *yanakuna*, *aqllakuna* are institutional dependents in a “supply on command” economy, not voluntary craft guilds; recruitment was seizure into sequestration under state authority.
