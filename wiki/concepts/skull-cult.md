# Skull Cult (PPNB)

Etic label for recurrent **Post-mortem skull treatments** in the Pre-Pottery Neolithic B of the Levant (and related zones): skull removal after burial or exposure, caching, plaster or bitumen facial modelling, masks, and special deposition (classic type-cases: [[jericho|Jericho]], Nahal Hemar, later [[ain-ghazal|‘Ain Ghazal]] and others). [[cauvin-jacques|Cauvin]] (2000) treats the **skull cult** as a principal ideological trait of PPNB cultural unity during diffusion.

## Definition as Coined

“Cult” is archaeological shorthand — not a recovered emic theology. Practices vary by site and phase; interpretation ranges from ancestor veneration to social memory, status, or other ritual programs.

## Semantic History

- Kenyon’s Jericho plastered skulls as textbook images.
- Expansion of the corpus across southern and northern Levant and beyond.
- Cauvin: packages skull cult with rectangular architecture and subsistence production as PPNB signature.
- Ongoing debate on whether a single “cult” is the right umbrella.

## Operationalizations

Presence of detached skulls, modelling materials, find contexts (houses, caches), demographic profile of selected skulls (where studied).

## Applications

PPNB identity and diffusion arguments; household ritual vs public cult; links to sedentary village life and social memory.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Cult” over-unifies diverse practices; not all PPNB sites show the full package; do not equate with any living religion. Date-stamp site claims.
