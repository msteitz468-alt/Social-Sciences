# Law of Cultural Dominance

Primary formulation: David Kaplan, Ch. IV of *[[sahlins-service-evolution-culture-1960|Evolution and Culture]]* (Sahlins & Service, eds., 1960), pp. 69–92. White’s Foreword glossed it as a thermodynamic statement of cultural dominance spanning specific and general contexts.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Kaplan 1960.)*

> “That cultural system which more effectively exploits the energy resources of a given environment will tend to spread in that environment at the expense of less effective systems.”

Put another way: a cultural system will tend to be found precisely in those environments in which it yields a **higher energy return per unit of human labor** than any alternative system available.

Kaplan distinguished:

- **Specific dominance** — a specialized culture type dominating a particular environment (e.g. highly effective arctic or arid foraging techniques holding off agriculture).
- **General dominance** — generally higher culture types having greater **dominance range** because they exploit more kinds of resources more effectively in most environments.

The law is thus relevant both to specific evolution (local replacement/persistence) and to general evolution (why higher forms expand).

## Semantic History

Formulated inside the Sahlins–Service neo-evolutionary program alongside [[general-and-specific-evolution]] and the [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]]. Organic analogy: paleontological “dominant types” embodying successive structural–functional improvements. Related to White’s energetic measure of cultural development ([[energy-law-of-cultural-evolution]]; Cottrell 1955 cited as parallel idea). Kaplan noted that scholars had already described the *phenomenon* without naming a law — [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] on U.S./China culture contact (1952), Barth on ecological ethnic niches in North Pakistan (1956), Lattimore on Inner Asia (1940).

Service (Ch. V) treats dominance and potentiality as an endemic contest: dominance of higher over lower forms is the strongest *inhibitor* of potential in unspecialized forms; a frequent consequence of specific evolution is reduction of diversity as dominant types spread.

## Operationalizations

Kaplan openly admitted a circularity risk analogous to biological adaptation: the law offers **no independent measure** of thermodynamic effectiveness separate from the fact that a system prevails and spreads. He hoped the formulation would stimulate independent energy measures (Zimmerman; Sahlins Ch. II). Dominance is always **relative to environment** — not moral superiority or universal military conquest.

## Applications

*(Attributed — Kaplan 1960 cases and mechanisms.)*

**Illustrative cases:**
- California hunter-gatherers vs agriculture in certain environments
- Plains equestrian adaptations vs farming
- Fried on culture contact with U.S. Indians
- China and Lattimore’s steppe/sown cleavage (Chinese expansion south vs limits north)
- Barth on ecological relationships of ethnic groups in North Pakistan
- Eskimo specialized arctic technology adopted in part by Western sojourners
- Japan as instructive modern case of adopting dominant industrial forms

**Mechanisms of dominance (Kaplan):**
- Ecological/economic pressure and competitive displacement
- Military conquest
- Threat-induced transformation (societies reorganize under pressure)
- Nationalist resistance that still spreads the dominant type’s political/ideological forms
- Ideology/politics spreading farther and faster than technology (colonial partial cultural dominance); apparent “ideology as prime mover” confuses *spread* (specific) with *origin* (general)

**Long-run claim (Kaplan):** as dominant culture types rise and spread, diversity of cultural types is **steadily reduced** toward variants of a single culture type — a historical tendency he treated as already far advanced.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Circularity** of thermodynamic effectiveness measured by prevalence (Kaplan’s own caveat).
- Progress/dominance language risks ethnocentric ranking; “dominance” as ecological energy return ≠ moral or civilizational superiority.
- Reduction-of-diversity thesis can be read as teleology of Western industrial monoculture — record as Kaplan’s 1960 position.
- Post-processual and postcolonial critiques of evolutionary dominance frames.
- Empirical competition with diffusionist and world-systems accounts of cultural replacement that do not require a general energy law.
