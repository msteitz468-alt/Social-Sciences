# Modes of Identification

## Definition as Coined

In *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, [[descola-philippe|Descola]] defines **modes of identification** as elementary schemas by which humans apprehend continuities and discontinuities between themselves and other existing beings at the levels of **[[interiority-and-physicality|interiority and physicality]]**. Identification operates "well upstream from the categorizations of beings and things that taxonomies reveal" — as prepredicative ontological discrimination rather than empirical natural-history judgment.

The four combinatorial outcomes are the [[four-ontologies|four ontologies]]:

| | Physicalities continuous | Physicalities discontinuous |
|---|---|---|
| **Interiorities continuous** | [[totemism]] (ontological) | [[animism]] |
| **Interiorities discontinuous** | [[naturalism-ontology|naturalism]] | [[analogism]] |

## Semantic History

Descola reuses anthropological terms (animism, totemism, naturalism) with **new combinatorial definitions**, distinct from Tylor's evolutionary [[animism]], Frazerian/Durkheimian totemism, and philosophical naturalism. He explicitly revises his own earlier pairing of animism as mere reverse of Lévi-Straussian totemism, which he argues still imported nature/society. Identification is analytically prior to [[modes-of-relation]] and to instituted "society."

## Operationalizations

- Two binary axes → four cells (Figure 1 in BNC).
- Schemas are not exclusive total models of whole populations at all times; hybrid and minority modes appear (e.g. Algonquin totemic features within animist regimes).
- Distinguished from *aliud* vs *alter*: identification first specifies *what kind of other* something is before relations of exchange, predation, etc. engage.

## Applications

Comparative cosmology; reanalysis of Amazonian, Australian, Mesoamerican, African, and Western materials; critique of universal [[nature-culture-distinction]]; museum and pedagogical expositions of the four regimes.

## Critiques and Limitations

Typological risk; dependence on secondary ethnography outside Amazonia; debate with [[perspectivism]] over whether crossed viewpoint inversion is universal in animism (Descola: no — optional graft). Heuristic status acknowledged by Descola (Preface; Epilogue).
