## Definition as Coined

Hawaiki (variously spelled Havaiki, and rendered Kahiki in Hawaiian) is the mythologized ancestral homeland recalled in Polynesian oral traditions across the Polynesian Triangle. As an emic concept, it names an ancestral place of origin from which a people's founding ancestors voyaged — a category of indigenous historical memory rather than a fixed geographic referent.

## Semantic History

Patrick Vinton Kirch, building on Kirch and Green (1987, 2001, *Hawaiki, Ancestral Polynesia: An Essay in Historical Anthropology*), identifies the mythologized homeland archaeologically and linguistically with the actual Western Polynesian homeland region — Tonga, Samoa, Futuna, 'Uvea, and Niuatoputapu — where the [[ancestral-polynesian-society]] developed following Eastern Lapita settlement in the second half of the first millennium BCE. This is an argued, attributed correspondence between an emic tradition and an etic archaeological reconstruction, not an assumption that oral memory transmits literal geographic fact unmediated.

The term itself has drifted regionally as Polynesian populations dispersed and lost direct memory of the original homeland's location: Maori tradition uses Hawaiki; Hawaiian oral tradition instead applies the cognate term Kahiki to Tahiti — a real, but different, place from the original Western Polynesian homeland. This is a textbook case of emic-term drift, comparable to other migrated terms (taboo, totem, caste) that carry both an original and a later, reapplied meaning.

## Operationalizations

Kirch and Green's identification rests on the [[triangulation method|methods/triangulation-method]]: convergence of historical-linguistic reconstruction (Proto Polynesian vocabulary), comparative ethnography, archaeology (Lapita-descended Polynesian Plain Ware sites such as Nukuleka, Tonga, and Mulifanua, Samoa), and biological anthropology, cross-checked within an explicit language phylogeny.

## Applications

Anchors the identification of the Ancestral Polynesian Society's homeland region and frames later Eastern Polynesian oral traditions of voyaging "from Hawaiki" as (attributed) folk-historical memory of the actual Western Polynesian dispersal, rather than literal navigational record.

## Critiques and Limitations

Because Hawaiki survives only in oral tradition recorded long after the events it purports to describe, its identification with a specific archaeological homeland is an argued inference, not a direct correspondence — the term-drift case (Kahiki = Tahiti in Hawaiian usage) is itself evidence that a single emic term does not reliably track a single fixed referent across the Polynesian-speaking world. Treat any claim equating "Hawaiki" with a specific geographic location as attributed to the scholar making the argument, not as settled wiki-voice fact.
