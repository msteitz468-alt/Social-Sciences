# Primary adjustment

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]* Essay 3, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] defines **primary adjustment** as the stance in which an individual co-operatively contributes required activity under required conditions — with the support of institutionalized welfare standards, incentives and joint values, and designated penalties — and is thereby transformed into a co-operator: the "normal," "programmed," or built-in member. He "gives and gets in an appropriate spirit what has been systematically planned for, whether this entails much or little of himself." In short, he is officially asked to be no more and no less than he is prepared to be, and dwells in a world congenial to that programming (attributed).

Goffman constructs the "clumsy term" primarily to get to **[[secondary-adjustment|secondary adjustments]]** — unauthorized means and/or ends that get around organizational assumptions about what the member should do, get, and **be** (attributed).

Primary adjustment is not mere psychological obedience; it is acceptance of the organizational definition of self as a working basis for action. Modes of adaptation in Essay 1 (colonization, conversion, playing it cool) interlock with this distinction without being identical to it.

## Semantic History

Paired term introduced with secondary adjustment in the underlife essay; less widely borrowed as a standalone keyword than secondary adjustment, underlife, or total institution.

## Operationalizations

Observable co-operation with house rules, schedule, therapy definitions, and staff-assigned identity without systematic unauthorized workarounds. Goffman notes that even "well-adjusted" inmates typically mix primary and secondary lines; the boundary is a matter of social definition and can shift by role, period, and society (attributed).

## Applications

- Analytic foil for mapping [[underlife]].
- Organizational analysis beyond total institutions: any establishment that programs members has a primary-adjustment baseline against which secondary adjustments are visible.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Binary can flatten mixed stances.
- Staff-defined "good patient" may be primary adjustment from the institution's view and secondary strategy from the inmate's.
- Over-attachment (embracing the entity more than asked) is a third analytic possibility Goffman flags but does not treat as a "major social problem."
