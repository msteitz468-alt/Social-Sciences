# Imagined Nostalgia

[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s coinage (ch. 4 of
*[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]*) for a nostalgia
manufactured by consumption, without any actual remembered loss. Attributed to
Appadurai.

## Definition as Coined

Mass merchandising "teach[es] consumers to miss things they have never lost,"
creating "experiences of losses that never took place" — **"nostalgia for things
that never were."** This "inverts the temporal logic of fantasy": rather than
imagining a desired future, the consumer is taught to mourn an unlived past. A
paired coinage, **"armchair nostalgia,"** is "nostalgia without lived experience or
collective historical memory," where the viewer supplies the nostalgic faculty to
an image that supplies a memory of a loss never suffered.

## Semantic History

Extends Fredric Jameson's "nostalgia for the present" (films that
project a future from which the present already looks lost) to merchandising and
youth-market advertising generally — an aesthetic Appadurai labels *histoire
noire*: "Buy now… because your period will soon be out of date." Distinguishes
"primary" from "ersatz" nostalgia. Repetition in modern consumption thus produces
"not… simulacra in time but… the simulacra of time" itself.

## Operationalizations

Read through U.S. gift-catalog rhetoric and back-to-the-future advertising (Pepsi,
Levi's, Ralph Lauren); connected to Grant McCracken's **"patina"**
(age as an index of status) and its refinement into "imagined nostalgia" once loss
itself is merchandised.

## Applications

Part of Appadurai's larger claim that consumption is "the social discipline of the
imagination" and that late-industrial society runs on "the aesthetic of
ephemerality" — "the key link between nostalgia for capitalism and capitalist
nostalgia." Feeds [[mediascape]] and [[imagination-as-social-practice]].

## Critiques and Limitations

An essayistic, illustrative concept rather than a measured construct; its evidence
base is advertising rhetoric read interpretively.
