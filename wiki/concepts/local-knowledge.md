## Definition as Coined

The title essay of *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]* — "Local Knowledge: Fact and Law in Comparative Perspective" (Storrs Lectures, Yale Law School, 1981) — makes [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s argument (attributed) that **law is "not a bounded set of norms, rules, principles, values... but part of a distinctive manner of imagining the real."** "At base, it is not what happened, but what happens, that law sees; and if law differs, from this place to that... what it sees does as well." The organizing concept is the **legal sensibility**: a culturally specific, determinate sense of justice — differing across cultures in determinacy, in strength relative to other modes of thought, and in "particular style and content... the symbols they deploy, the stories they tell, the distinctions they draw, the visions they project." His compressed formula: "Facts and law we have perhaps everywhere; their polarization we perhaps have not."

As a title, "local knowledge" is also the volume's keynote value: the largeness of mind that comes "from... seeing ourselves amongst others, as a local example of the forms human life has locally taken, a case among cases, a world among worlds."

## Semantic History

Geertz's second key term is **"skeletonization of fact"** — legal process's defining operation, "the reduction of [fact] to the genre capacities of the law note," narrowing moral issues until determinate rules can decide them. He pairs it with ethnography's "schematization of social action," finding "a more than passing family resemblance." Legal facts, on this view, "are made not born, are socially constructed... by everything from evidence rules, courtroom etiquette, and law reporting traditions, to advocacy techniques, the rhetoric of judges, and the scholasticisms of law school education." So the Western question of "how to get [law and fact] together" inverts into "how to tell them apart." He models adjudication (borrowing from Franz von Benda-Beckmann) as a tacking between the **"if-then"** idiom of general precept and the **"as-therefore"** idiom of the concrete case — while flagging this as "a rather too Western way of putting things."

The touchstone illustration is the **Regreg case** (Bali, 1958, from Geertz's fieldwork): a man expelled from his village council for refusing his rotational duty is driven to social death and psychosis; even the paramount traditional king (the Dewa Agung) cannot reverse the *adat* council's unanimous verdict. What mattered was not whether the accusations were true or the rules fair ("Everyone I talked to agreed that they were [repellent]") but the disruption of clear social categories — the Balinese proverb: "to leave the community of agreement is to lie down and die."

## Operationalizations

Geertz demonstrates the "legal sensibility" comparatively through three non-Western idioms, each fusing "is" and "ought" differently from Western legal positivism's separation of fact and law. He flags the three as a simplifying "precaution," "not... the only three I might have used":

| Idiom | Tradition | How it joins fact and law |
|---|---|---|
| **haqq** | Islamic | truth/reality *and* right/duty/valid-claim are one term; law construed as **a species of fact**; a legal order obsessed with **normative witnessing** (*shahada*) — establishing fact by certifying the moral character of witnesses (the *'udul*/accredited witnesses); "reality as being in its essence imperative, a structure not of objects but of wills" |
| **dharma** | Indic | duty/right conduct **is** transcendentally-defined position in the social order; fact construed as **a species of law** — "the *dharma* of the king to defend the *dharma*"; a legal order obsessed with sorting moral **kind** (verdicts) rather than moral character |
| **adat** | Malay / Indonesian | "not custom but outlook, *volksgedachte* not *volksgebruik*"; justice as "social consonance"/propriety realized through **public consensus procedure** — "Propriety to be preserved must be seen being preserved" (Geertz critiques the Dutch *adatrecht* school for reducing it to mere usage) |

Geertz notes all three are "more comparable to the Western notion of 'right' (*Recht*, *droit*) than... to that of 'law' (*Gesetz*, *loi*)." The program he draws is **comparative law as "cultural translation"** — "to formulate the presuppositions... characteristic of one sort of legal sensibility in terms of those characteristic of another" — not institutional taxonomy or a search for universals (*quod semper aequum et bonum est*). He adopts **"legal pluralism"** as the umbrella term for the modern coexistence of unreconciled legal sensibilities within one polity (case studies: pre-1974 Ethiopia; post-colonial Indonesia's three-cornered struggle among judges, prosecutors, and police), and, borrowing Richard Rorty's normal/abnormal discourse (after Kuhn), casts the task as hermeneutic — "Meaning, in short, not machinery."

## Applications

The essay is a foundational statement for [[legal-anthropology]] that deliberately distances itself from the field's classic "social control" studies (Malinowski, Llewellyn & Hoebel, Gluckman, Bohannan), rejecting both the "rule-centered" (Pospisil) and "process-centered" (dispute) paradigms. It extends [[interpretive-anthropology]] and the [[blurred-genres|refiguration]] program to law, and reframes the [[comparative-method|comparative method]] itself: against Santayana ("one compares only when one is unable to get to the heart of the matter"), Geertz holds "it is through comparison, and of incomparables, that whatever heart we can actually get to is reached." Law joins religion, art, ideology, and [[common-sense|common sense]] as another cultural system, "a species of social imagination."

## Critiques and Limitations

Against Grant Gilmore's Holmesian view that law "reflects but in no sense determines the moral wisdom of a society" and needs prior consensus to function, Geertz argues law is constitutive, not merely reflective, and must "compass dissensus" — a position recorded here as one side of a live jurisprudential dispute. More broadly, the "legal sensibility" reading inherits the standing charges against interpretive anthropology: the comparison illuminates rather than tests, and the three idioms are elegant reconstructions built substantially on other scholars' materials. The essay's own OCR-independent caution is that its "if-then/as-therefore" and fact/law framing is admittedly Western scaffolding placed over non-Western practice.
