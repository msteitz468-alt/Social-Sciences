# Strategies of Action

The pivotal concept linking culture to action in [[swidler-ann|Ann Swidler]]'s [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]]. Introduced in her 1986 ASR article and developed in Chapter 4 of *[[swidler-talk-of-love-2001|Talk of Love]]* (2001). All claims are **attributed** to Swidler.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Swidler, Talk of Love.)* "I call these larger patterns 'strategies of action.' These I define as ways actors routinely go about attaining their goals. Thus my focus is on 'means' rather than ends." Strategies of action are "general solutions to the problem of how to organize action over time, rather than specific ways of attaining particular ends" — general, transposable, and typically held several at a time, sometimes in tension.

## Semantic History

- **The anti-Parsonsian reversal.** The concept exists to displace the [[parsons-talcott|Parsonsian]] model in which internalized values determine the ends of action. Swidler argues that "a person's available strategies of action shape the kinds of goals he or she pursues, instead of the other way around": what carries across situations is the style and skill-set, not the goals (immigrants change objectives while retaining strategies).
- **Not rational calculation.** Despite the word "strategy," Swidler stresses no instrumental calculation is implied — what people want is itself a product of the cultural shaping of their [[cultural-toolkit|capacities]], which are organized into generalized strategies, not chosen case by case. She credits Hans Joas (via Dewey/pragmatism): means and goals are chosen reciprocally — "only when we recognize that certain means are available to us do we discover goals which had not occurred to us before."
- **Where values re-enter.** Within an already-adopted strategy, values do guide fine-grained choices — but as one more cultured capacity that makes enacting a strategy possible, not as the origin of the strategy (a point elaborated for [[settled-unsettled-lives|settled lives]]).

## Operationalizations

Inferred, not measured: recurrent ways of organizing life over time — reliance on kin alliances, individual market competitiveness, gang/turf loyalty, wide casual-acquaintance or fictive-kin networks. Strategies are inherently **social**: a kin-reliance strategy needs others who reciprocate; market skills require an actual market. Individuals hold multiple strategies mobilized differently by situation.

## Applications

- **"Cultured capacities."** Culture equips people for strategies by shaping (1) the kind of self one is, (2) internalized skills/styles/habits (from time-consciousness to skill at judging others' character — "literature as equipment for living," Burke), (3) group-membership markers, and (4) worldviews that give confidence to act.
- **Culture and social structure.** Strategies of action are "the major links between culture and social structure": culture shapes the selves, skills, and worldviews from which strategies are built, but in periods of change, cultural persistence depends on whether the strategies it sustains can survive within social-structural constraints.
- **Modernization.** Culture drives non-Western modernization not via enduring traditional values but by furnishing "a repertoire of capacities for action that can be mobilized to achieve new objectives" (revitalization movements, revivalism, modernizing ideologies) — undercutting the values thesis.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Positions.)* The standing question is whether "strategies of action" reintroduces the instrumental means–ends rationality Swidler disowns, and how the concept relates to [[habitus]]'s account of durable disposition (both explain regularity of practice without rule-following; habitus claims deeper structuring, strategies-of-action more plural switchability). Because strategies are reconstructed from the regularities they explain, critics raise the same circularity charge leveled at habitus.
