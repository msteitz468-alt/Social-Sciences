# Theory of Balance

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), chapter 11, [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]
named and attacked the **theory of balance** — the image of American democracy as a
self-equilibrating system of countervailing powers. He traced it (attributed) to a carry-over
from market ideology: government as "a sort of automatic machine, regulated by the balancing of
competing interests," each restrained only by "legalistic and amoral interpretations of what the
traffic will bear." Eighteenth-century equilibrium (market sovereignty; Adams on equilibrium vs
despotism) is the classical pedigree; anti-New Deal practical conservatism and Cold War
anti-totalitarian rhetoric give it modern life.

Mills held (attributed) that although "quite irrelevant to the political economy of modern
America," balance rhetoric is "the only rhetoric that prevails widely among the managerial elite
of corporation and state."

## Semantic History

Mills's targets include romantic pluralism and, in his reading, images such as Riesman's
"veto groups" (recorded as **Mills's characterization** of a rival position — not a wiki-voice
summary of Riesman). The theory's **prime focus**, he said, is Congress and the middle levels.
When generalized into a master model of the power system, it becomes "historically unspecific,"
whereas as a model it fits only certain phases — notably the Jacksonian period and, under
differing circumstances, the early and middle New Deal.

## Operationalizations

Mills's critique is theoretical and historical rather than a formal test:

1. **Independence assumption fails** — major vested interests often coincide and ally under the
   umbrella of government rather than freely balancing.
2. **Scale asymmetry** — units that count at the top are few and weighty beyond comparison with
   dispersed middle/lower groups.
3. **No effective countervail** at the top against the coalition of big businessmen as political
   outsiders and ascendant military men.
4. **Government not mere framework** — interests are vested *inside* the hierarchical structure
   of the state ("governmentalization of the lobby").

## Applications

Principal foil in [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]. Ties to Mills's claim that balance
describes the [[middle-levels-of-power]] and that treating it as a picture of the whole
legitimates [[organized-irresponsibility]] (no one is responsible if everyone balances everyone).

## Critiques and Limitations

Pluralists deny that balance is "only" middle-level; later empirical programs (community power,
issue-area studies, network analysis) operationalize dispersion and concentration differently.
Mills's chapter is a critical diagnosis, not a symmetric literature review of pluralism — the
strongest pluralist statements should be filled from their own sources when ingested.
