# Cultural Translation

In British social anthropology, a common metaphor for what ethnographers do when they interpret another culture into the language of anthropology. [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]]’s *Writing Culture* essay ("The Concept of Cultural Translation in British Social Anthropology") critiques the metaphor by stressing **inequality of languages**, institutional power, and the limits of individual experimental rewriting.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Asad 1986 and the British SA tradition he criticizes.)*

Asad maps the rise of “translation of cultures” (Lienhardt, Beattie, Leach, Gluckman) and notes Malinowski did *not* use the frame. He argues that contesting a culture is not the same as translating it; responsible criticism requires a contestable addressee. Extended critique of Gellner’s “Concepts and Society” (excessive charity, sentence-matching, Nuer twin/bird, Leach Kachin, “bobility,” Berber *baraka*).

## Semantic History

From relatively uncritical professional self-description to power-critical concept in Asad 1986; feeds later anthropology of translation and decolonizing method talk.

## Operationalizations

Not a metric; a critical diagnostic of how anthropological knowledge is produced across linguistic and political inequality (Benjamin/Pannwitz; Arabic example of strong languages shaping weak ones).

## Applications

Asad on British SA and Gellner; brake on pure textual experiment within [[writing-culture]] (individual style cannot overcome “stiffness” produced by power).

## Critiques and Limitations

Asad’s essay itself is a critique of naive translation metaphors; experimentalists may under-weight his institutional argument. See [[writing-culture-debate]] Position 2.
