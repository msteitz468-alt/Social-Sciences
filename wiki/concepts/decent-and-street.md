# Decent and Street (orientations)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[anderson-code-of-the-street-1999|Code of the Street]]* (1999 Ch 1), [[anderson-elijah|Anderson]] treats **decent** and **street** (or “ghetto”) as poles of **value orientation** — evaluative labels **residents themselves use** in status contests — not pure types. Individuals of either orientation may coexist in the same extended family; the same person may exhibit both depending on circumstances. **Code-switching** is common: decent youth especially cultivate repertoires that display street competence (“I can take care of myself”) without full street investment. Most residents, Anderson reports, are decent or trying to be; knowledge of the [[code-of-the-street]] is largely **defensive** and necessary for public life even for decent families who oppose its values.

**Decent families** (attributed): hope for the future; work and “make do”; ally with schools and churches; instill “backbone” and responsibility; male authority/provider ideals where a man is present; grandmother faith supports under strain.

**Street families / extreme street-oriented** (attributed): deeper alienation; at the extreme, criminal-element “thug life,” hustle/game plans, severe distrust, modeling of dealers and certain public figures; public life as zero-sum competition where losing can feel worse than death.

## Semantic History

Emic moral vocabulary of Philadelphia field sites → Anderson’s dual typology → widespread adoption in urban ethnography and education research. Not equivalent to middle-class vs underclass pure class labels: both orientations exist among the poor. Distinct from monolithic [[culture-of-poverty]]: plurality and situational performance are the point (Anderson 1999).

## Operationalizations

Primarily qualitative. Later work sometimes scales “street orientation” as attitudes — risk of binary flattening.

## Applications

Child socialization; school behavior; “campaigning for respect”; grandmother and decent-daddy roles; John Turner’s oscillation; Robert/Rob’s conversion requiring sustained code-switching (Anderson 1999).

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary risk; outsider elevation of residents’ labels; stigma when “street” is read as character rather than situational adaptation under joblessness and weak policing. Always retain Anderson’s majority-decent claim when using the typology.
