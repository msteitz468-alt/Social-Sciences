## Definition as Coined

A founder event (or founder effect) occurs when a population is established from a small number of founding individuals, so that whatever genetic variants those founders happened to carry — including rare, otherwise unremarkable variants — become disproportionately common in their descendants. Combined with a subsequent population bottleneck, a founder event can drive a rare disease-causing mutation to substantially elevated frequency purely as a matter of demographic chance, with no explanatory relationship between the mutation and any trait under selection.

## Semantic History

The concept is a standard tool of population genetics generally; David Reich's *Who We Are and How We Got Here* (2018) applies it specifically to argue against selection-based explanations for elevated disease-mutation frequencies in several human populations (as of Reich 2018).

## Operationalizations

- **Ashkenazi Jews**: Reich reports that elevated rates of Tay-Sachs and Gaucher disease among Ashkenazi Jews are attributable to a medieval founder bottleneck — a random demographic event — rather than to the selective-advantage hypothesis proposed by Cochran, Hardy, and Harpending (2006), who had argued the diseases were byproducts of selection for high IQ tied to medieval moneylending occupations. Reich states the evidence contradicts the selection account and favors the founder-bottleneck explanation (as of Reich 2018).
- **Indian jatis (castes)**: Reich reports that India's endogamous jati groups show founder-event signatures — genetic drift and elevated disease-variant frequencies consistent with long-term endogamy following small founding population sizes for individual jatis (as of Reich 2018). See [[caste]].
- **The Ari of highland Ethiopia**: Reich describes the Ari caste system (Cultivators, Blacksmiths, Potters subgroups) as showing strong, long-persisting endogamous barriers — genetic and social differentiation Reich calls the strongest evidence of sustained endogamy he knows of, extending at least 4,500 years, older than documented endogamy in India (as of Reich 2018).
- Reich also references founder-event dynamics in Finnish and other population-genetic disease-mapping contexts as a general phenomenon distinguishing demographic history from selection (as of Reich 2018).

## Applications

Founder-event reasoning underlies Reich's broader argument in [[reich-who-we-are-2018]] that many population-specific disease-mutation frequencies reflect demographic history (small founding groups, endogamy, bottlenecks) rather than natural selection for an associated trait — a caution Reich extends to popular and pseudo-scientific claims about group differences (as of Reich 2018). See also [[admixture]] and [[sex-biased-admixture]] for related demographic mechanisms Reich uses to explain population genetic structure.

## Critiques and Limitations

Reich's own presentation treats the founder-event explanation as the more parsimonious and better-evidenced account relative to selection-based alternatives for the Ashkenazi disease cluster, but does not claim founder events rule out selection in all cases — the two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive in principle, and distinguishing them requires population-genetic evidence beyond mutation frequency alone (as of Reich 2018).
