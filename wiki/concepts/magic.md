# Magic (occult sciences)

## Definition as Coined

[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], in *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, Vol. I ch. IV), analyzed magic — the "occult sciences" (divination, astrology, sorcery, omens) — as a system resting on a **mistaken association of ideas**: the magician "mistakes an ideal connexion for a real one," treating a relation that exists only in thought as if it held in the world. He called it "one of the most pernicious delusions that ever vexed mankind." (Attributed voice: Tylor's intellectualist-evolutionist verdict, not a wiki-voice claim that magic is false.)

## Semantic History

Tylor's core diagnosis — magic as reasoning by **association of ideas** applied wrongly to reality — was systematized by [[frazer-james-george|James Frazer]] in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* into the famous two laws of **[[sympathetic-magic]]**: the Law of Similarity (like produces like — *homeopathic/imitative* magic) and the Law of Contact/Contagion (things once in contact continue to act on each other — *contagious* magic). Frazer held (attributed) that magic is "a false science as well as an abortive art," and he added the influential (and contested) evolutionary sequence **magic → religion → science** as successive human "theories of thought" (see [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]; [[frazer-golden-bough-study]]). He also treated [[taboo]] as negative magic — "electrical insulators" of dangerous spiritual force. Later anthropology moved away from the "mistaken science" framing: Malinowski gave magic a psychological function (managing anxiety where technical control fails); the Durkheimian school ([[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] and [[hubert-henri|Hubert]], *[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|A General Theory of Magic]]*, 1902–03; primary-ingested) analyzed it socially against religion; [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s *[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937|Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande]]* (1937; primary-ingested) showed magic as one side of a triangle with [[witchcraft]] and [[poison-oracle|oracles]], internally coherent within its web of belief. In Tylor's own scheme, modern magic and spiritualism are [[survivals]] of primitive [[animism]].

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by its **underlying inferential error**: a practice is "magic" when its efficacy is claimed via an ideal association (similarity or contact) rather than a real causal mechanism. This intellectualist criterion competes with functional (Malinowski) and social (Mauss) criteria that classify magic by what it does or how it stands to religion, not by whether its reasoning is valid. Frazer operationalized subtypes (homoeopathic vs contagious) under the [[sympathetic-magic]] heading.

### Hubert & Mauss, *A General Theory of Magic* (1902–03; primary-ingested)

*(Attributed — [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] & [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]], *[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|Esquisse d'une théorie générale de la magie]]*; see [[mauss-hubert-general-theory-magic-study]].)* The Durkheimian counter-definition: magic is defined not by rite-structure or inferential error but by **social circumstance** — "A magical rite is any rite which does not play a part in organized cults — it is private, secret, mysterious and approaches the limit of a prohibited rite." Frazer's two criteria (sympathy; constraint vs. conciliation) both fail, since religion also has sympathetic and constraining rites; magic and religion form a continuum between the poles of [[sacrifice]] and the evil spell. Purely individual superstitions are not magic — "Actions which are never repeated cannot be called magical." The **magician is a social status**: the disabled, ecstatics, itinerants, blacksmiths, strangers, and women hold magical power "not through their individual peculiarities but as a consequence of society's attitude towards them and their kind"; "It is public opinion which makes the magician and creates the power he wields." **Belief is a priori and collective** — "Magic is believed and not perceived"; magical judgments are collectively confirmed "synthetic a priori judgments" unfalsifiable by contrary experience, and the magician's sincere-yet-willing belief mirrors the public's ("two sides of the same coin"). The efficacy question resolves into **[[mana]]** as a category of collective thinking (see that page). Conclusion: "Magic is, therefore, a social phenomenon," standing between religion and science/technique, with "a taste for the concrete" and a genealogical role as protector of early technique and science (alchemy → chemistry). Pocock's foreword to the English edition claims the work's importance is that it "contributes to the dissolution of 'magic' as a category."

## Applications

### Malinowski, *Argonauts* (Trobriands 1915–18; attributed)

Malinowski treated magic as, from the native point of view, indispensable to canoe-building, sailing, gardening, and [[kula|Kula]] success. He analyzed systems of related spells (canoe magic; *mwasila* Kula magic; *kayga'u* shipwreck protection); argued magic is a power of man (not a force of nature) transmitted by tradition and never "invented" in native dogma; linked myth to magic as mutual authentication (myth crystallizes into formulae; magic testifies to myth); and provided linguistic analyses of spell structure (*u'ula* / *tapwana* / *dogina*) in Ch. XVIII. Frazer's preface stresses magic's role in the Kula enterprise. Distinct from Frazer's intellectualist false-science model ([[sympathetic-magic]]) — Malinowski's account is institutional and psychological within ongoing technical work. See [[malinowski-argonauts-study]].

### Malinowski, *Coral Gardens* (1935; same fieldwork; attributed)

**Vol. I** densifies **garden magic as institution**: public magic closely interwoven with work yet **not confused** with technique; hereditary *towosi* as organiser/supervisor; inaugurative, growth, harvest, and *vilamalia* (village-plenty / appetite) rites; multiple named systems (Kaylu'ebila, Momtilakayva, etc.); App. I magic–work chart. Magic clusters where activity is important, dangerous, chance-ridden, and not fully mastered by technique.

**Vol. II** densifies the **theory of the magical word**: each rite produces/generates force conveyed by the accredited *towosi*'s authentic spell (voice/breath); **no local word for [[mana]]** yet force in belief and behaviour; dual **coefficients of weirdness and intelligibility**; Formulae 1–44 with fourfold commentaries; only fully translatable within the [[context-of-situation]] of belief and rite.

See [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]] · [[hubs/studies/malinowski-coral-gardens-study]].

### Evans-Pritchard, Azande (late 1920s; attributed)

*[[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-1937|Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic]]* treats Zande *ngua* as **magic / medicines / leechcraft / closed associations** — one indigenous word-field. Magic commands medicines (usually vegetable) with *sima* spells that **instruct** rather than beseech; it is the "chief foe" of [[witchcraft]], paired with [[poison-oracle|oracles]] as the other side of a combat triangle. Classifications track situations (gardens, hunting, craft, love, theft, sickness, vengeance). **Vengeance-magic** (*bagbuduma*) is morally compulsory after death by witchcraft and gained prestige when colonial rule forbade direct vengeance. Witch-doctors (*abinza*) are the corporation of medicine-eating diviners/leeches; the Mani association exemplifies closed communal magic under colonial Unlawful Societies law. Payment and teacher goodwill condition potency. Distinct from Frazerian [[sympathetic-magic]] laws and from Malinowski's anxiety-compensation focus: E-P's account is a **system ethnography** of magic as practical action against mystical injury. See [[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study]] · [[azande]].

Magic is a key case for Tylor's [[survivals|doctrine of survivals]] (its persistence into modern life as folklore, astrology, spiritualism) and for the intellectualist program carried by Frazer's *Golden Bough* catalogues (weather control, image-harm, fertility, flesh diet, etc.); it is a founding topic of the anthropology of religion and of witchcraft studies. Malinowski's Trobriand garden and Kula corpora are type-cases for **institutional** and **linguistic** analysis of magic against pure intellectualism. Evans-Pritchard's Azande corpus is the type-case for magic as **counter-witchcraft technology** inside a coherent misfortune system.

## Critiques and Limitations

The "mistaken science" framing is the chief target: it judges magic by whether it works as physics, ignoring that magical acts may be expressive, social, or anxiety-managing rather than instrumental (Malinowski), and that within a coherent belief-system magical reasoning is not simply irrational ([[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study|Evans-Pritchard]]; [[rationality-debate]]). The Tylor–Frazer evolutionary sequence magic→religion→science is rejected as unilinear conjecture. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[hubs/theory/intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]].

**Andaman (Radcliffe-Brown 1922, attributed):** protective power of fire, fibres, bones, weapons; *oko-jumu* medicine-men; power analysed as experience of society's moral force (Durkheimian), compared to *mana*/*orenda*/*wakan* without a single Andaman abstract name — heat/fire symbolism. [[andamanese]]; [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].

## From Taussig *Shamanism* (1987 ingest)

[[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1987) treats Indian magical power in SW Colombian yagé circuits as an "unconscious colonial creation" effective in shaman–patient dialogue and purchased by white colonists against envy and fate ([[yage]]; [[wild-man]]; [[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-study]]). Continues his 1980 refusal of anxiety-relief functionalism ([[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]).
