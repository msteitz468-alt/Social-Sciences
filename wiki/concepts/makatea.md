## Definition as Coined

Makatea (a Polynesian term meaning "white stone," also the proper name of a Tuamotu island) is a classificatory term, migrated from indigenous usage into geological/archaeological analytical vocabulary, for an island type formed when an atoll or an old high island surrounded by a barrier reef becomes tectonically uplifted — via plate-margin uplift or lithospheric flexure/point-loading from a nearby hot-spot island. Kirch gives the example of Rarotonga, whose volcanic mass warped and elevated nearby islands by as much as 80 meters (attributed, Kirch 2017).

## Semantic History

The term names both the process (tectonic uplift converting reef limestone into exposed karst terrain) and, by extension, islands exhibiting that geomorphology — a raised, often steep-sided limestone landform typically ringing a smaller interior basin or plateau. As a migrated term it sits alongside the broader vocabulary of Pacific island typology (atoll, high island) that Kirch situates within the eight-stage evolutionary cycle of midplate hotspot islands (from high volcanic island through fringing reef, barrier reef with lagoon, submergence, and ring atoll) originating with Darwin's (1842) subsidence model (see atoll formation).

## Operationalizations

Used descriptively to classify islands by landform and, critically, by habitability constraint: makatea terrain is characteristically spongelike karst with no surface water and little soil, making prehistoric settlement materially difficult.

## Applications

Kirch cites Henderson Island as a marginal-habitability makatea case: despite lacking surface water, soil, or easy landing, prehistoric Polynesians nonetheless maintained settlement there for roughly six centuries — an illustration of Polynesian settlers' capacity to occupy ecologically marginal islands, discussed in relation to Pitcairn-Henderson-Mangareva regional exchange networks.

## Critiques and Limitations

As a landform classification, "makatea" carries no cultural or linguistic-affiliation implications of its own; it is a geological/environmental category and should not be conflated with any particular archaeological culture, though it materially constrains the kind of subsistence economy (reliance on marine resources, arboriculture, water catchment/storage) that settlement of such islands required.
