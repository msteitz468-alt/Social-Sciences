# Housing Cost Burden

## Definition as Coined

Share of income devoted to housing costs (rent + utilities). U.S. policy convention long treated ~**30%** of income as the affordability ceiling. In *[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]*, [[desmond-matthew|Desmond]] documents **extreme rent burden** among poor renting families as the structural condition enabling eviction and poverty reproduction.

## Semantic History

Mid-century progressive housing consensus around the 30% rule (Epilogue); late 20th–early 21st century rise in burden as rents rose while poor incomes stagnated. Desmond situates Milwaukee and national AHS patterns within that shift.

## Operationalizations

- Policy thresholds: >30% burdened; >50% severely burdened.
- Desmond Prologue (attributed): majority of poor U.S. renting families spend **over half** of income on housing; **at least one in four** over **70%** on rent and utilities.
- Ethnographic extremes: e.g. Arleen’s $550 rent on $628 welfare (~**88%**).
- MARS: median rents by bedroom; thin rent gaps between high- and low-crime neighborhoods; serious housing problems common without lower average rent.

## Applications

Prologue statistics; court and landlord chapters; Epilogue argument that unassisted poor renters (majority of poor renters) face an affordability crisis that vouchers and public housing only partially address (~67% of poor renters without federal assistance in 2013, per Desmond).

## Critiques and Limitations

Income measurement, utilities inclusion, household composition, and quality-adjusted rent complicate cross-study comparison; landlord-side “overcharge” claims (voucher premium) depend on hedonic controls (Notes).
