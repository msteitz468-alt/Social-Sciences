# Social Approval

## Definition as Coined

For [[blau-peter-m|Blau]], **social approval** is a fundamental social reward — the expressed esteem, agreement, or recognition that people supply one another — and a chief medium through which conformity and contribution are rewarded. He distinguishes approval in general from **respect** (or esteem), a specific type entailing unilateral recognition of superior ability by an ostensibly objective standard: approval of opinions tends to be *reciprocal* (shared values), whereas respect is *asymmetric* — "you cannot simultaneously command someone's respect and look up to him" in the same domain.

Blau's central proposition is that approval's value depends on its **scarcity and genuineness**. Approval given indiscriminately — bestowed on mediocre and excellent alike — "loses its value as an incentive" and undermines respect for the approver's judgment. Two consequences follow: persons of secure, superior status can afford to give approval sparingly (raising its worth), while those of inferior or insecure status are pressured to give it freely (eroding respect for their discrimination). Approval also "cannot be bartered" without corruption: Blau uses the language of **"simony and prostitution"** for the sale of goods (approval, salvation, love) whose worth rests on their being freely, spontaneously given. Quoting [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]: "Action can be coerced, but a coerced show of feeling is only a show."

## Semantic History

The concept draws on symbolic-interactionist and small-group traditions (approval as the currency of the [[looking-glass-self|reflected self]]) but Blau recasts it as an economic-style good subject to supply, scarcity, and depreciation. His "dilemma of approval" — one supports another by expressing approval, yet expressing it too freely depreciates its value — parallels his "dilemma of love" and "dilemma of leadership," instances of a recurring theme that social goods rest on incompatible conditions.

## Operationalizations

Treated qualitatively; indexed in Blau's work by discrimination in praise (the most respected discussion-group members express agreement *less* freely and disagree somewhat more) and by whether a consultant's peer acceptance rises or falls with how he uses superior status.

## Applications

Approval is the reward that legitimates the fair exercise of power (the "secondary exchange, of fairness for approval" — see [[fair-exchange]], [[legitimation]]), the sanction that enforces [[indirect-exchange|indirect exchange]] and norms, and, in [[social-integration|group integration]], the reward exchanged for conformity — but, per Homans (quoted approvingly by Blau), conformity earns approval without conferring superior status, "because... it is in ample supply. Any old fool, so to speak, can conform."

## Critiques and Limitations

- The scarcity-value logic treats approval like a currency; critics of exchange theory question whether esteem behaves so market-like, and whether "value" here is anything but the behavior it is invoked to explain.
- The account is developed from American work-group and experimental settings; its cross-cultural generality is asserted, not demonstrated.
