# Bertan

**Bertan** — the [[ilongot|Ilongot]] term ("class, kind, species, group") for their largest category of social affiliation — is the analytical centerpiece of Renato Rosaldo's *[[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-1980|Ilongot Headhunting, 1883–1974]]*, where its historicization carries the book's argument against timeless social structure.

## Definition as Coined

*(Emic; documented 1967–74; attributed to Rosaldo.)* Bertan is polysemous: a classifier par excellence applicable to anything enumerable (animals, plants, words — *binartan*, "divided into shares," is used of meat after a hunt), and, narrowly, "a bilaterally claimed category of affiliation that putatively once was or now is residentially concentrated" — people sharing a name, food prohibitions, and putative common origin, united by *betrang* ("body," shared kin substance). Bertan became salient above all in [[feud]] and covenant, when one bertan gathered as an action group against another.

## Semantic History

The founding puzzle (Rosaldo 1967–69): two sociologically disparate kinds of group bore the one name — 13 co-residential, largely endogamous bertan (~165 persons each; Rumyad endogamy 85–87% in marital histories) and 54 residentially dispersed bertan that never acted collectively. Rosaldo's resolution was diachronic: concentration → dispersal → evanescence are phases of a single developmental trajectory, so both phases carry the name. Of 67 bertan names collected, 45 were plain place names — identity rooted in place-association, not descent essence; bertan names (unlike local-cluster names, which change with every move) persist across a succession of places, which is why oral memory reifies bertan as more corporate than they were.

Part III of the monograph radicalizes the point: reconstructed back to 1883–1905, "Rumyad" dissolves into five autonomous, mutually hostile groupings (Besilid, Pasigiyan, Peknar, true Rumyad, Yamu) whose later unity was produced by repeated, datable episodes of forced re-concentration — the 1883/1889 smallpox epidemics, the 1942–45 crisis, the mid-1950s retreat — "the bertan and the feud, far from being a timeless bedrock of synchronic social structure, are historically conditioned and socially constructed" (Rosaldo). The 1860s fault line between true Rumyads and Peknars was still socially live in 1974, manifest as the split between New Tribes converts (Ringen) and the unconverted Kakidugen–Pengegyaben clusters.

## Operationalizations

Rosaldo's 1967–69 census operationalized the category by name-collection and marital histories: 67 named bertan; 13 residentially concentrated (~165 persons each; endogamy 85–87% for the Rumyad clusters, ~80% overall) vs. 54 dispersed; name etymologies tabulated (45 place names, 9 geographical features, 9 plants, 4 other).

## Applications

Bertan liability structured feud (any member of the offending bertan was a valid target — collective, diffuse liability, contrasted by informants with "the custom of those who arrest"); bertan identification established relatedness among strangers ("What is your bertan?" — Kadeng's 1954–55 journey home); bertan identity was strategically fluid (after Luku's oath-breaking 1952 raid, Kakidugen people reclassified themselves as Tabaku rather than "true Rumyad"). Individuals could actively maintain at most about five bertan names — more allies "can be to place oneself in a cross fire."

## Critiques and Limitations

An emic category of one society at one documented moment. Rosaldo's own analysis is the standing caution: treating bertan as corporate descent groups (the received structural reading of cognatic societies) mistakes a naming convention imposed on a moving population for a bounded unit.
