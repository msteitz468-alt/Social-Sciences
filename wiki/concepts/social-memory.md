# Social Memory

## Definition as Coined

**Social (or collective) memory** — after Maurice Halbwachs (1925) — is a group's shared
remembering, understood not as a neutral record of the past but as a present-oriented, socially
framed construction. In archaeology (uptake from the 1990s) it names how communities materialize
memory in monuments, depositional practices, commemoration, and landscape. Treated in
*[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010)
as a central concept of contemporary theory; all claims here are attributed.

## Semantic History

The concept moved from Halbwachs's sociology through Connerton (*How Societies Remember*, 1989)
and Nora's *lieux de mémoire* into archaeology, where **forgetting** is theorized as constitutive
of memory rather than its opposite — "remembering is only possible on the basis of forgetting"
(Forty 1999, via Mills). Barbara Mills identifies practices of forgetting-as-memory-work: secrecy,
mortuary destruction (Küchler's *malanggan*), transmission error, ritual "retirement" of powerful
objects, and politically-motivated destruction ("negative heritage" / "past mastering" — the
Bamiyan Buddhas, per Meskell). The concept overlaps with [[heritage]] and, where mobilized for the
nation, with [[nationalism-and-archaeology]]. A parallel lineage from Halbwachs is the Assmanns'
[[cultural-memory]] (communicative vs. institutionalized long-duration memory), applied to
pharaonic Egypt in [[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|Assmann's *The Mind of Egypt*]].

## Operationalizations

**Depositional practice (Mills, Chaco Canyon; Ancestral Puebloan, Classic Bonito phase
~1030–1100 CE, tree-ring dated).** Dedicatory and termination offerings — strands of shell,
turquoise, and jet beads placed in kiva niches, floor vaults, and pilasters across a size-graded
hierarchy of ritual structures — materialize memory work; the deliberate masonry sealing of ten
bead-filled niches at Chetro Ketl's Great Kiva is a "symbolic forgetting." Large caches of
collectively-owned paraphernalia at Pueblo Bonito (wooden staffs, cylinder jars, the Room 33
burial cluster) represent the **ritual retirement of inalienable objects**, reinterpreted by Plog
and Heitman (2006) as evidence of a Chacoan "house society." Repeated repainting of cylinder jars
(Crown & Wills) made "the vessels … repositories of collective memory." Mills flags a taphonomic
caveat: early unscreened excavations under-recover this evidence, and her Puebloan (Zuni/Hopi)
comparisons are **explicitly marked as ethnographic analogy**, grounded in documented cultural
continuity, not identity.

**Public memory and power (Shackel, U.S. historical archaeology).** Public memory is "produced
and controlled by groups with power" to naturalize inequality through material culture and
commemoration (attributed, Shackel, after Halbwachs, Bodnar, Wolf). His tactics: the exclusionary
past, the patriotic/commemorative past, and nostalgia/heritage-legitimation. Cases: Harpers Ferry
histories that "forget" post–Civil War industrial exploitation; the Robert Gould Shaw / 54th
Massachusetts Memorial (its meaning shifting from Shaw to the Black regiment across the 1897,
1981, and 1997 rededications); the reconciliationist 1961 Civil War Centennial staged amid the
civil-rights crisis; and the still-contested **Heyward Shepherd "faithful slave" Memorial**
(1931), a live dispute (NAACP vs. United Daughters of the Confederacy vs. National Park Service)
recorded as competing positions, not adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Applications

Chaco deposition; American commemorative monuments; the elite manipulation of dynastic memory at
Vijayanagara (Sinopoli); and — as an active production of *absence* — Trouillot's **historical
silence**, the power-laden silencing of African and other pasts through source selection and
disciplinary framing (Schmidt & Walz), distinct from a mere evidentiary gap. See
[[nationalism-and-archaeology]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Memory claims can outrun their material evidence, and the archaeological signature of
"remembering" versus routine deposition is often ambiguous (recovery bias compounds this). The
concept is strongest where independent lines — deposition, oral tradition, documents — can be
"tacked" together, and weakest when a single evocative context is read as intentional
commemoration. As present-oriented construction, every social-memory claim is dated to its
producers.

### Yoffee 2005 (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths]]*, citing Van Dyke & Alcock 2003) treats **social memory** as fabrication that makes the state appear natural and ever-present — legitimacy by transmuting the past into a reference for the present. Collapse of Mesopotamian **civilization** (vs individual states) is framed as mutation/suffocation of cultural memory and identity; regeneration of states often consciously models predecessors. See [[societal-collapse]], [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

## Bradley 1998 — persistence of memory (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* (esp. Ch. 6) is a major archaeological operationalization of public ritual time and monument memory:

- Drawing on **Bloch** (Merina ritual history): public ritual form can stay stable while scale, location, and political uses change — appearance of stability enables political change.
- **Rowlands** (1993): **inscribed** practices (building memorials) vs **incorporated** practices (destroying/removing objects so memory resides in absence); Bradley’s BA example: burial mounds with grave goods give way to river deposits of similar objects.
- Earthwork monuments as distinctive material culture interpretable across generations more than portable artefacts; monuments as **mnemonics** of a vanished past (Ch. 10).
- Household memory: abandoned longhouses and long-mound cemeteries as commemorations of household/settlement history ([[death-of-the-house]]).
- Accessible multi-deposit tombs: remains as resources for the living — visit, rearrange, circulate — vs closed single burial cutting off contact.
- Stonehenge as “Theatre of Memory”; circular ceremonial forms present massive continuity while subtly adapting ([[stonehenge]], [[henge]]).
- EBA appropriation of open arenas as elite burial places harnesses sacred places to particular people ([[cairnpapple]]).

Cross-links: [[monumentality]], [[ritual-landscape]], [[ritual]], [[bradley-richard]].

## Moore 2005 — architecture, houses, and memory without monumentality (attributed)

Susan Gillespie's (2000a, 2000b) reading of [[house-society|house societies]] — that "a key function of houses is to anchor people in space and to link them in time," and that their archaeological visibility rests on an **"objectification of perpetuity"** (narrative practices such as myth and genealogy, and material practices such as durable construction, elaboration over generations, heirlooms, and burial incorporation) — grounds [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore's]] (2005) application to North Coast Peru. Moore reads Chan Chan's royal ciudadelas and the [[sican-culture|Naymlap]] legend's palace-at-Chot narrative as both objectifying perpetuity: durable adobe architecture and burial-within-compound for the former, and mythic charter plus the burial of Naymlap/Cium "so that posterity might regard him as immortal and divine" for the latter (see [[chan-chan]], [[sican-culture]]).

Moore closes his argument with an explicit counterpoint against equating social memory with monumentality: an ethnoarchaeological case from Soconusco, Chiapas (Moore and Gasco's fieldwork), in which Doña Josefina's household encoded memory in a simple line of cobblestones marking a torn-down house, and a fifty-year-old smoke-darkened post moved between successively rebuilt houses. Moore uses this to argue "not all houses reflect 'house' societies... memory can be encoded in a simple line of stones" — i.e., social memory does not require monumental or even durable architecture (attributed, Moore 2005).

## Whittle 1996 — place, ancestors, re-created community (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]* 1996, Ch. 9–10; mid-1990s European Neolithic synthesis.)*

Whittle operationalizes social memory for mobile/dispersed Neolithic communities without equating memory only with ranked monumentality:

- **Attachment before sedentism:** People did not immediately settle down; increasing attachment to place by **tradition, memory, gatherings, ritual, presence of the dead**. Fixed places picked by commemoration and re-use while occupations continued to shift; density cannot be read from episodic aggregation points.
- **Community continually re-created:** Sense of community re-affirmed by periodic gatherings, gift exchange, sharings of food and drink, joint ritual, and webs of materiality — the Neolithic community had **continually to be re-created**.
- **Longhouse memory → ancestral shrines:** By ~4000 BC the longhouse largely abandoned as structure but **memory transformations** turn house into ancestors/spirits, creating senses of time and descent distinguishing Neolithic people from predecessors; later ditched enclosures as social arenas celebrating domesticity, fertility, hospitality, shared origins.
- **Iberian closed→access tombs:** Early closed Iberian tombs → later access + larger collectivities as a shift in ancestor/social-group memory; [[los-millares]] passage graves as multi-generation deposition and circulation of remains — collectivity and ancestral home for dispersed communities, not primarily prestige ranking.
- **SE Europe:** Gradual community creation binds living and dead; time/descent via reoccupation, dead in living space, figurines; burial grounds create categories of known individual forebears as collectivity of time past.
- **Twofold Neolithic historicity:** Sense of beginnings/time past **and** timelessness/repetition generation to generation; slow histories over many generations vs modern event-history.

Cross-links: [[ritual]], [[monumentality]], [[los-millares]], [[impresso-cardial-culture]], [[bradley-richard]] (parallel monument-memory program).

## Smith 2006 — heritage as remembering (attributed)

*([[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* 2006.)* Heritage is defined as a cultural process that engages **acts of remembering** to create ways of understanding and engaging the present. Memory is active **doing** (Wertsch, Connerton, Halbwachs, Nora, Samuel as Smith uses them), not passive storage. Sites function as Samuel’s **theatres of memory** — settings that give gravitas and occasion for transmitting meaning (Waanyi oral history in country; industrial museums; Castleford festival/Forum). Emotional/present authenticity of remembering can outweigh fabric accuracy (country-house and museum visitor talk). Labour-heritage visitors: memory work dominant; reactionary nostalgia rare (~2% in Smith’s industrial-museum sample).
