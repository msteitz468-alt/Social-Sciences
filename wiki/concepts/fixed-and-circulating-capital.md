## Definition as Coined

In [[marx-capital-volume-2-1885]] (Chapter 8), Marx presents fixed and circulating (fluid) capital as a distinction that "arises exclusively from the specific manner in which this value circulates" and that applies only within productive capital — never to money capital or commodity capital as such. Fixed capital is the portion of constant capital (means of labour: machines, buildings) that retains its independent use-form across repeated labour processes, transferring its value to the product only gradually, in proportion to its own wear, until it is fully used up. Circulating (fluid) capital comprises the components of productive capital — raw materials, ancillary materials, and labour-power (variable capital) — whose value passes entirely into the product in a single production cycle and must be wholly renewed with each new cycle.

Marx states this distinction is not a property of things but a function determined by role: an ox is fixed capital as a draught animal but not once it is eaten; cattle being fattened for slaughter are raw material (circulating capital), not fixed capital; a machine is commodity capital in the hands of its manufacturer and becomes fixed capital only once installed by the buyer who employs it productively. He offers the counter-example of ancillary materials fully consumed in a single labour process (coal for a steam engine, gas for lighting a factory): though they never physically leave the production process, their whole value enters the product each cycle, making them formally circulating, not fixed, capital.

## Semantic History

**Quesnay and the Physiocrats.** Marx locates the origin of the distinction in Quesnay's *avances primitives* (original advances) and *avances annuelles* (annual advances), applied specifically to the farmer's capital, since Quesnay held agricultural capital to be the only genuinely productive capital. Marx quotes Quesnay directly: "The annual advances consist of the expenditures annually made for the work of cultivation; these advances must be distinguished from the original advances which form the fund for the commencement of cultivation." Marx credits Quesnay with correctly locating the distinction wholly within productive capital and with correctly grounding it in the different ways the two kinds of advance enter and are replaced or reproduced with the value of the finished product — one wholly replaced yearly, the other bit by bit over a longer period. Marx states later Physiocrats (Dupont de Nemours, Le Trosne, Turgot) progressively identified the *avances* more directly with "capital" as a general term. Marx argues the Physiocrats' own doctrine — that labour's value-contribution equals only the value of workers' necessary subsistence, with surplus-value ascribed to nature's "collaboration" in agriculture rather than to labour as such — barred them from discovering the constant/variable capital distinction, but that their fixed/circulating distinction itself, being confined to reproduction periods, remained "untouched" by the confusion Marx attributes to their successors.

**Smith's generalization and, per Marx, regression.** Marx credits Adam Smith's only advance over Quesnay as generalizing the distinction from farmer's capital to every form of productive capital (*avances annuelles* → circulating capital; *avances primitives* → fixed capital), but argues that "in the development of his presentation, he falls far behind Quesnay." Marx's central polemic is that Smith conflates two genuinely distinct oppositions: (1) the fixed/circulating distinction, which belongs wholly within productive capital, and (2) the distinction between productive capital and capital-in-circulation (commodity capital and money capital). Marx illustrates the resulting incoherence with several thought experiments: by Smith's criterion that circulating capital is capital that "changes masters," a machine-builder's machine (which circulates as his commodity capital before sale) would count as circulating rather than fixed capital; a copper mine, where no raw material is purchased and nothing "changes hands," would by Smith's own definition consist entirely of fixed capital; Smith's seed-corn example — seed called "fixed capital" because it goes "backwards and forwards between the ground and the granary" without changing masters — is refuted by Marx's observation that whether seed is subtracted directly from the harvest or the whole crop is sold and new seed bought makes no difference to its status as circulating (fluid) capital, since it is fully consumed and replaced each cycle either way.

Marx further argues that Smith's list of the components of "circulating capital" — money, provisions/stock awaiting sale, materials not yet made up, and finished-but-unsold work — actually equates circulating capital with commodity capital plus money capital, categories that belong to the *circulation* process, not to productive capital at all; and that money is never a form of productive capital in the first place. Marx also refutes physical-property definitions of fixed capital used by earlier economists (immobility, durability): ships and locomotives are fixed capital for their users yet operate only by moving; houses are fixed capital and immobile; a spinning machine exported before use never functions as fixed capital in its country of production at all, while factory buildings, railways, and soil improvements, once produced, must function as fixed capital wherever they are located or lie useless. Marx calls Smith's resulting classification of all social wealth into consumption fund, fixed capital, or circulating capital — as though all capital must be one or the other, "just as a mammal is by natural necessity either male or female" — a "great error," since commodity capital and money capital are neither.

**The core charge: burying the source of surplus-value.** Marx's most consequential claim is that because Smith defines the wage-portion of circulating capital in terms of workers' *means of subsistence* (following the Physiocratic model, without the Physiocrats' premises) rather than labour-power itself, Smith "buries" the crucial characteristic of this portion of capital — that it is *variable*, value-creating, and the source of surplus-value — beneath the merely formal fact that it belongs to the "fluid" part of productive capital with respect to turnover. Marx states this confusion "was uncritically taken over by his successors" and "echoed... from one generation down to the next for a whole century," and names Ramsay specifically as having made this "basic error" of confusing the categories of fixed and circulating capital with the categories of [[constant-and-variable-capital]].

**Ricardo.** Marx argues Ricardo introduces the fixed/circulating distinction only to present exceptions to the labour theory of value (a topic Marx defers to Volume 3), and that Ricardo uncritically inherits Smith's conflation: Ricardo defines fixed capital as means of labour and circulating capital as "capital that is to support labour" (wages), a schema in which the portion of capital laid out on raw materials and ancillaries "completely vanishes," fitting neither side. Marx quotes Ricardo's own qualification that the fixed/circulating distinction is "not essential, and in which the line of demarcation cannot be accurately drawn," and comments this brings Ricardo "happily... once again back with the Physiocrats" but now merely as a subjective distinction Ricardo himself deems superfluous. Marx finds it more surprising that Ricardo — who otherwise presented value and surplus-value "consistently and clearly" — should inherit this confusion uncritically, more so than later "apologists" or Smith himself. Marx traces the downstream consequences through John Stuart Mill (all circulating capital identified with wage-capital), Ramsay (constant/variable fully reduced to fixed/circulating, counting all means of production as fixed and only wage-capital as circulating), and the "most recent English and Scottish economists" (MacLeod, Patterson), who reduce fixed/circulating to a banking distinction between "money at call" and "money not at call."

## Operationalizations

Marx works a numeric example: a £10,000 machine lasting ten years has a ten-year turnover time; its value trickles out as money throughout its working life, accumulating in a money reserve (amortization) fund, until final replacement — see [[turnover-of-capital]]. He distinguishes ordinary depreciation (proportional value transfer via wear) from "moral depreciation" (loss of exchange-value from cheaper or improved replacements becoming available, independent of physical wear) and from repair/maintenance costs (a separate additional capital outlay not contained in the originally-advanced fixed-capital value). He documents this distinction as administratively contested in practice: railway companies could shift expenses between "capital account" and "revenue account" to inflate dividends artificially, and Marx cites a table of widely differing average per-mile repair costs across English railway companies (London & North Western £370, Midland £225, Great Northern £310, and others), attributing most of the variance to differing accounting conventions rather than real cost differences.

## Applications

Marx applies the fixed/circulating distinction to explain why fixed capital's turnover characteristically encompasses several turnovers of circulating capital within it, and connects the durability and renewal cycle of fixed capital to the "material foundations" of the periodic business cycle — see [[turnover-of-capital]]. He extends the analysis to the "working period" (Chapter 12), showing this concept — the number of successive working days needed to complete a product — is independent of, though it interacts with, the fixed/circulating distinction: a locomotive-builder must advance roughly twelve times the circulating capital of a spinner with equal weekly outlays, because the locomotive's three-month working period ties up circulating capital before any product returns to money form.

## Critiques and Limitations

The distinction's own critics are internal to the source: Marx's treatment of Smith and Ricardo is itself a sustained critique, arguing both confused fixed/circulating (a distinction about mode of value-transfer) with constant/variable (a distinction about role in valorization), with the effect of obscuring the origin of surplus-value in labour. This is Marx's own polemical framing and is recorded here in attributed voice, not as an adjudicated verdict on Smith or Ricardo's overall systems. Smith and Ricardo hold no dedicated thinker pages in this wiki as of this ingest.

Marx's discussion also flags cases at the margin of the fixed-capital category: cattle held as breeding stock (part of the herd remains in production while another part is sold as annual product) lengthens overall turnover in a manner analogous to fixed capital but, Marx states explicitly, "does not constitute fixed capital in the categorical sense" — an example of the distinction's own boundary requiring careful, non-mechanical application even within Marx's corrected framework.
