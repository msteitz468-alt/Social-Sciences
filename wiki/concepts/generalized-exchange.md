# Generalized Exchange

> **Not to be confused with [[indirect-exchange]]** (Peter Blau's sociology of collectively-mediated reward). This is Lévi-Strauss's kinship concept.

## Definition as Coined

**Generalized exchange** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] term (*[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]*, 1949) for a marriage system in which women circulate in a single direction around a cycle of exchange units: "if an A man marries a B woman, a B man marries a C woman," and so on until the cycle returns to A. Its elementary form is **marriage with the mother's brother's daughter** (matrilateral [[cross-cousin-marriage|cross-cousin marriage]]), which prescribes one cross-cousin and prohibits the other (the father's sister's daughter) — distinguishing it sharply from the symmetric [[restricted-exchange]] of bilateral cross-cousin marriage.

## Semantic History

The concept is the second pole of the distinction organizing [[alliance-theory]]. Lévi-Strauss credited Hodson and Held with earlier "inklings," and Granet with parallel insight on ancient China, but named and generalized the type himself. He derived it structurally as the way a **harmonic regime** (descent and residence rules aligned) achieves complexity, since harmonic regimes "are incapable of going beyond the stage of moiety organization" through descent/residence alone.

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by unidirectional, cyclical wife-transfer between named units: the [[kachin|Kachin]] *mayu ni* (wife-givers) / *dama ni* (wife-takers) relationship; the Gilyak *axmalk* / *imgi* relationship; matrilateral cross-cousin marriage among Naga, Toda, central Indian tribes, and (Lévi-Strauss argued) the archaic feudal system of China. Requires a minimum of three (in strict clan-level cases, four or five) exchanging groups.

## Applications

Generalized exchange, Lévi-Strauss argued, is a "collective speculation" conducted "on credit": a group gives a wife and trusts the cycle to eventually return one — "an element of trust" absent from the immediate reciprocity of restricted exchange. This gives it greater integrative power (it can bind the whole society into one cycle) but also builds in **instability**: because it "presupposes equality" among lineages yet rewards accumulation, it tends toward **anisogamy** (status-unequal marriage), hypergamy, and — Lévi-Strauss's account of caste — the closing of high-ranked lineages into endogamous castes (see [[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-study]]). He read the elaborate Kachin/Chin bride-price systems as expressing this tension, insisting "women are exchanged for women" and the purchase apparatus runs *within* the exchange, not instead of it.

## Critiques and Limitations

The "internal limits" instability argument is a structural deduction, richly illustrated but not a demonstrated historical dynamic; [[leach-edmund|Leach's]] Kachin work contested aspects of the stability claim. Like all alliance-theory concepts it treats women as circulated objects (see [[exchange-of-women]]) and rests on secondary sources. Real systems are frequently hybrids of generalized and restricted exchange (the Naga cases Lévi-Strauss himself analyzed).
