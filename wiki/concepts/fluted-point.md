# Fluted Point

## Definition as Coined

A **fluted point** is a lanceolate stone spearpoint distinguished by one or more
**flutes** — long channel-flake scars driven up from the base along the face,
thinning the base for hafting. Fluting is a technically demanding, distinctively
**New World** knapping signature: the [[clovis-culture|Clovis]] point (larger,
shorter flutes) and the later [[folsom-site|Folsom]] point (smaller, more finely
fluted, often full-length) are its diagnostic types, and their recognition in the
1920s–1930s anchored the definition of the earliest [[paleoindian|Paleo-Indian]]
cultures.

## Semantic History

*(Enriched from [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] during integration — the
type's role as the "index fossil" of early Americans and debates over whether

### Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009.)*

**Discovery sequence:** 1927 [[folsom-site]] fluted point between extinct bison ribs; ~half a dozen years later, larger, less finely made fluted points with mammoth near Clovis, NM ([[blackwater-draw]])—“generalized Folsoms” → **Clovis points**. Clovis found in deposits **below** Folsom → older; Folsom points largely western plains; Clovis continent-wide.

**Shared morphology:** lanceolate shape; base and partial sides ground smooth (protect sinew/socket); flutes from base partially up one or (more commonly) both faces. Classic Clovis: parallel sides, flutes usually **<½** face length, relatively long/thick; miniatures known (possible children’s toys). Texas sample: freshly minted Clovis ~slightly over **10 cm** long, **3 cm** wide, **7.5 mm** thick. Base size often uniform—points fitted to hafts, not vice versa—implying hafts harder/costlier to make. Folsom: often **full-length** fluting (flakes up to ~5 cm long yet <1.5 mm thick from both faces of points ≤4 mm thick); average just over **5 cm × 2 cm** (thinner/shorter than Clovis). Fluting disappears after Folsom though lanceolate form and ground haft edges persist.

**Failure rates:** Clovis points broke while fluting **10–20%** of the time; Folsom nearly **40%**. Early bloodletting/bayonet analogies failed when flutes recognized as sandwiched in foreshafts/socket hafts. Strength-of-haft and deeper-penetration ideas weak given 10,000 years of successful unfluted points. Frison and Bradley: fluting may not have enhanced performance. Meltzer floats style, symbolic hunting prowess, or pre-hunt ritual (some fluted points preserve **red ocher** staining)—hard to test.

**Origin and “American invention”:** Howard (1935 USSR trip) found no Clovis fluted points in Soviet museums. ~50 fluted points in Alaska (mostly ≥~66°N, surface or with younger artifacts); ~60 in former ice-free corridor; secure ¹⁴C ages **<10,500 BP**—too young for southbound Clovis colonizers; stylistically more akin to later fluted types than Lower-48 Clovis. Meltzer: fluting invented **south of the ice sheets** and spread including northward (“migratory backwash”). Origin candidates: Great Plains/Southwest (oldest known fluted points) vs eastern North America (Ronald Mason diversity-as-age argument—more and more varied eastern fluted points; eastern points clearly predating western ones not yet found as of 2009). Adovasio: unfluted **Miller lanceolate** from [[meadowcroft]] Stratum IIa would make a fine fluted-point precursor.

**Chronology of disappearance:** Clovis/related forms disappear after ~**10,900 BP** (central/western) and ~**10,600 BP** (eastern); by ~**10,500 BP** regionally distinctive Late Paleoindian points had supplanted the Clovis fluted form. Late Paleoindian point types are useful period/region labels but styles lack discrete boundaries, can overlap, and need not equal ethnic groups—“fashion” can change within a population.

**Diffusion vs people-movement:** Meltzer rejects Clovis-as-diffusion among preexisting pre-Clovis people as primary explanation: would require lightning-fast faithful copying of a non-revolutionary technique across vast space; should see substantial pre-Clovis presence and Clovis grafted onto fluteless assemblages—neither seen (as of 2009). Rapid people-movement more likely for Clovis radiation. No well-marked trail of Clovis fluted points from Alaska to the Lower 48.

## Operationalizations

Classified by the [[typological-method]]: point outline, flute length and count,
basal grinding, and manufacture sequence. Fagan reports the caution — pressed by
lithic-replication experiments (Flenniken) — that reducing and resharpening a
single point can shift it between named "types," so **point typology is a weaker
proxy for distinct peoples than culture-historical archaeology once assumed**
(attributed). Frison experiments (via Meltzer): many Paleoindian point types on game found no significantly superior lethality among forms.

## Applications

Fluted points, often found with mammoth and bison remains, are the primary
material marker used to trace Clovis and Folsom horizons—and historically to
read First Americans as specialized big-game hunters (stereotype Meltzer problematizes).
Atlatl use: wooden/bone atlatls unknown from Paleoindian contexts; Hutchings’s fracture-velocity studies show some Paleoindian points shattered at velocities only attainable with atlatl (Meltzer).

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether a shared point style indexes a shared **people**, a shared **technology
diffusing** across groups, or merely a **functional** solution is unresolved —
the four-way non-identity caution (pots/points ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes)
applies. High fluting failure rates make pure functional explanations incomplete.
“Pre-Clovis” assemblages need not be fluted; Monte Verde’s toolkit is non-Clovis.
