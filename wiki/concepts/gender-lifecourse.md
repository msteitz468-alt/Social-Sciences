# Gender and the Lifecourse

Analytical frame treating **gender as cumulative and transient across a lifetime** — expanding, receding, and transforming with the ageing body — rather than as a fixed adult male/female binary. Developed for archaeology in [[gilchrist-roberta|Roberta Gilchrist]]’s *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gender and Archaeology]]* (1999), especially chapters 5–7.

## Definition as Coined

Gilchrist (glossary and ch.5) distinguishes chronological age (calendar), social age (normative culturally imposed behaviour on age groups), and physiological age. Gender identity and roles, she argues, are performed through the **lifecourse**: childhood, adolescence, adulthood, old age — with rites, spaces, and material culture marking transitions. In the coda she states categories of gender, body, and sexuality are transient “not just between cultures and through time, but within the course of a single lifetime.”

## Semantic History

Life-course analysis entered social science before gender archaeology. Feminist archaeology’s early “make women visible” phase often treated adult women as a class; third-wave concerns with difference, age, and embodiment (as surveyed by Gilchrist 1999) push toward syncopated male/female lives and the embodiment of ageing, not only “finding children” or “finding the elderly.”

## Operationalizations

- Mortuary patterning by age-and-sex packages (e.g., Anglo-Saxon knives; Copper Age Tiszapolgár — cases Gilchrist reviews).
- Spatial sequences of medieval parish church and castle that stage lifecycle and gender (Gilchrist’s own examples).
- Rites of passage (van Gennep lineage) as materializable thresholds.
- Grandmother hypothesis / old-age productivity debates as caution against androcentric adult-male default.

## Applications

Gilchrist ch.5 survey cases; ch.6 elite women’s castle seclusion as a **stage** of high-status female lifecourse rather than timeless domesticity; coda program: address embodiment of ageing and intergenerational tension, not merely age-group visibility.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of projecting modern life-stage psychology onto deep prehistory; thin evidence for childhood/old age in many assemblages; age osteology uncertainty. Gilchrist’s own method preference — multi-source, historically particular — is the control she recommends.
