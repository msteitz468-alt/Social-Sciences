# Stalled revolution

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]* (1989), [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] named a **stalled revolution**: women have changed (mass paid employment and shifted identity), but most workplaces remain inflexible and most men have not fully adapted at home. The strain leads her "to speak of a stalled revolution" (Ch. 2, attributed). An unstalled society would include flexible hours, parental leave, affordable housing near work, community supports for domestic labor (invoking Dolores Hayden), and men whose manhood includes active parenting; a stalled one "lacks social arrangements that ease life for working parents, and lacks men who share the [[second-shift|second shift]]" (attributed).

## Semantic History

The phrase organizes 1980s–present sociology of gender and family change. It is a **diagnosis of incomplete transformation**, not a claim that no change occurred (Hochschild cites slow rises in male domestic share, e.g. Juster 1965–81). Later editions' afterword still speaks of stalls: old-fashioned fatherhood ideals, unfriendly workplaces, undervaluation of small acts of care (2012 afterword, attributed as later reflection). Related vocabularies: "incomplete gender revolution," "stalled gender revolution" in demography and comparative family research — record those when secondary sources are ingested.

## Operationalizations

Not a single index. Indicators Hochschild uses:

- Female labor-force participation rise without equal male second-shift share
- Workplace clocks designed for family-free workers
- Cultural cover-up (supermom / flying-hair imagery) that privatizes structural strain
- Marital tension patterns: faster-changing women, slower-changing men (Ch. 14)
- "Third stage of economic development" (dual earners) with "second stage of fatherhood" still common (Ch. 12, drawing on John Nash; attributed)

## Applications

- Frame for all couple cases in *The Second Shift* — marriage as "magnet" for stall strains; [[gender-strategy|gender strategies]] as basic marital dynamic (Ch. 2).
- Explains why private [[family-myth|family myths]] and [[emotion-work]] stand between the stall and divorce (Holt case).
- Policy and cultural implication: children can be victims when women work but men, workplaces, and culture lag; "most working mothers are already doing all they can. It is men who can do more" (Ch. 15, attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Periodization and national scope: U.S. dual-earner focus of the 1980s; cross-national variation and later cohort change need other sources.
- Risk of teleology ("revolution" implies a known endpoint of full equality).
- Class: blue-collar and upper-middle strains differ (money/day care vs career clocks and unstable help) while the dual ideal of traditional care vs 50-50 runs "top to bottom" (Ch. 13, attributed) — stall is not only middle-class.
- Does not by itself adjudicate whether maternal employment harms children; Hochschild cites NAS/Hoffman literature as mixed-to-reassuring while insisting care *work* remains undervalued (Ch. 15).
