# Bridging Social Capital

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Putnam 2000.)* **Bridging** (inclusive) social capital: outward-looking networks spanning diverse social cleavages. Examples: civil rights movement, many youth service groups, ecumenical religious organizations. Better for linkage to external assets and information diffusion ("getting ahead," Briggs); Putnam cites [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s [[strength-of-weak-ties]] for the value of distant acquaintances.

Paired opposite: [[bonding-social-capital]]. Parent: [[civic-social-capital]]. Related network mechanics: [[strength-of-weak-ties]], [[tie-strength]], and — at the ego-network level — [[structural-holes|Burt's structural holes]] (bridging as a community-scale cousin of brokerage across holes; do not equate silently, and do not rename Granovetter's or Burt's findings as civic social capital in wiki voice).

## Semantic History

Emerges with bonding as Putnam's master typology; absorbs (sometimes loosely) network-sociology talk of bridges and weak ties. Pre-Putnam: Tocquevillian cross-cutting association; civil-rights and ecumenical organization studies.

## Operationalizations

Cross-cleavage membership; intergroup ties; weak-tie/bridge measures in network surveys. National civic series rarely measure bridging cleanly; state SC indices usually mix formal membership and trust without a pure bridging subscale.

## Applications

- Job information and political allies (via weak-tie citation).
- Putnam's agenda challenges (more bridging than grandparents' era by 2010 — a POSITION).
- School and workplace integration as bridging projects (ch. 22 discussion).

## Critiques and Limitations

Scalar leap from dyadic weak ties to community "bridging capital" is theoretically risky. Cross-cutting ties can also transmit conflict. Measurement thin relative to concept's popularity ([[bowling-alone-debate]]).
