# Urban Cluster (polynucleated Middle Niger city)

## Definition as Coined

In the McIntosh program (*[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]*, 2005), an **Urban Cluster** (also “polynucleated sprawl”) is the characteristic Middle Niger city form: a constellation of mounds and satellite settlements — not a single wall-and-citadel nucleus — whose aggregate functions, specialists, and population constitute urbanism. Type case: the [[jenne-jeno|Jenné-jeno]] Urban Complex (main tell + Hambarketolo + numerous satellites).

## Semantic History

Developed against Western/Near Eastern expectations of the solitary nucleated city with palace/citadel. Related to broader “dispersed” or multi-centric urban forms in comparative archaeology; McIntosh also discusses a clustered alternative in early northern China (Ch. 6). Distinct from modern suburban “sprawl” metaphor — here clustering is the *urban* pattern of specialist and corporate accommodation.

## Operationalizations

Site survey + excavation: contemporaneity of satellites; artefact diversity / functional complementarity maps; aggregate area and population estimates within fixed radius (e.g. 1 km); absence or presence of elite architecture at a single apex mound.

## Applications

[[jenne-jeno]]; [[dia-shoma|Dia]] Urban Cluster; comparative Mesopotamian and Chinese cases in McIntosh 2005 Ch. 6; densifications in Connah 2016 and Oxford Handbook African Archaeology.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether functional complementarity without ranked settlement hierarchy equals political [[heterarchy]] is contested (Connah: risk of reading absence of palace as positive heterarchy). Clustering can also appear under hierarchical systems.


## McIntosh 2005 densification

*(Attributed — Ch. 4 “Polynucleated sprawl.”)*

**Urban Complex / Urban Cluster:** satellite communities orbit one or more larger primaries; neither fully inside a single urban perimeter nor mere subordinate villages of a classic settlement hierarchy. Physical separateness reinforces corporate identity while co-location enables markets and mutual services.

**Jenné-jeno type case:** ~70 sites; max clustering ~AD 700–1050 (Clark 2003); after ~1000–1100 more activities nucleate on fewer larger sites. Jenné-jeno shows all activity classes; 29 satellites show 1–3 only — functional complementarity.

**Population aggregates:** program low-end guesses treat the cluster, not the single mound, as the urban demographic unit (10–26k within 1 km; up to ~42k if most tells simultaneous).

**Political link:** McIntosh reads clustering as ecological risk-buffering *and* as instrument of resistance to centralization ([[heterarchy]]). Connah: pattern is robust; political inference contested.
