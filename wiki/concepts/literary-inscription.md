# Literary Inscription

Analytic concept from Latour and Woolgar's *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]*: laboratory activity is organized as a system of **writing** — material processes that culminate in figures, diagrams, papers, and citations. The ethnographer's "Ariadne's thread" through the apparent disorder of the bench.

## Definition as Coined

Latour and Woolgar (via the fictional observer of Ch. 2) treat the lab as a system of **literary inscription**: [[inscription-device|inscription devices]] transform substances into documents; office work compares documents; articles and arguments are the valued products. Even grinding rat brains makes sense, they suggest, once the end product is seen as a highly valued diagram entering a paper.

## Semantic History

- **1979:** organizing principle of laboratory ethnography in *Laboratory Life*.
- Later Latour (*Science in Action*, etc.) generalizes inscription and "immutable mobiles" — not yet primary-ingested.
- Related but distinct from Derrida-style literary theory; here "literary" means document-producing practice inside science, not aesthetic literature.

## Operationalizations

- Count and map inscription devices vs. mere "machines" (matter transformers without directly usable written output).
- Trace cost and energy inputs into a folder of figures.
- Follow statement careers across drafts and publications.

## Applications

Salk neuroendocrinology lab; TRF fact-construction narrative; general laboratory studies.

## Critiques and Limitations

Lab members (as reported) resented being reduced to "writers" indistinguishable from any other authors; they insisted they wrote *about* neuroendocrinology. Authors respond that the *aboutness* is itself an achievement of inscription and persuasion, not a given.
