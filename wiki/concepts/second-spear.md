# Second Spear (*umbaga*)

## Definition as Coined

[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] reported that Azande always say of [[witchcraft]] that it is the *umbaga* or **second spear**. In hunting, the man who first spears and the man who plunges the second spear jointly kill and share the meat. Analogously, if a man is killed by an elephant, **the elephant is the first spear and witchcraft the second** — together they killed him. The same holds in war. Witchcraft explains **why events are harmful to a particular person**, not the mechanics of how elephants charge or granaries collapse. (Attributed: E-P 1937, Ch. II.)

## Semantic History

### The granary (pedagogic twin)

Evans-Pritchard used the collapse of an old granary: everyone knows termites eat supports and people sit under granaries for shade. Western thought sees only **coincidence** of two independent chains. Zande philosophy, he argued (attributed), supplies the missing link — witchcraft explains why *those* people were under *that* granary at the moment it fell.

### Later uptake

Philosophy of social science and anthropology of risk/misfortune recycle "second spear" / secondary-causation language. Keep the Azande grounding when using the term as E-P's.

## Operationalizations

Evans-Pritchard's test (attributed): Zande belief **in no way contradicts empirical knowledge of cause and effect**; the sensory world is as real; actors **foreshorten** the chain of events and select the cause that allows intervention and determines social behaviour. Natural and mystical causation **supplement** each other.

Even when taboo breach is also cited (e.g. sex before weaning; incest and leprosy), death may still take witchcraft as second spear: without it they would have been ill but would not have died — and vengeance still proceeds.

Diagnosis of the second spear proceeds via [[poison-oracle|oracles]], not laboratory isolation of *mangu*.

## Applications

Core of [[evans-pritchard-witchcraft-oracles-magic-study]]; key to reading witchcraft as a misfortune idiom rather than failed physics. Central exhibit in the [[rationality-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Metaphor is E-P's analytic highlight of Zande speech, not a formal Zande "theory" chapter. Over-generalizing "all witchcraft is second-spear reasoning" flattens other African and European witch idioms.
