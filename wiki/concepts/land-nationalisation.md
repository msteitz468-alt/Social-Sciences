# Land Nationalisation

## Definition as Coined

In [[lenin-vladimir]]’s *The Agrarian Programme of Social-Democracy in the First Russian Revolution, 1905–1907* (written Nov–Dec 1907; first pub. 1908 confiscated; book form 1917), **nationalisation of the land** is defined in two registers:

1. **Legal-political:** transfer of **all** land to **state ownership**. The state is entitled to draw rent and to lay down general possession/use rules nationwide (e.g. prohibition of intermediaries/sub-letting; transfer only to the direct tiller). In a really democratic state, state ownership **requires** disposal through local/regional self-government within national laws. The difference from [[municipalisation]] is **not** “bureaucracy vs local administration,” but whether private ownership is abolished completely or retained for one land category (“agrarian bimetallism”).
2. **Economic (capitalist relations):** nationalisation = **transfer of rent to the state**. Pure nationalisation: the state receives rent from the agricultural entrepreneur who pays wages and receives average profit. Rent presupposes wage-labour and capitalist farming.

Negative content for the mass peasantry, Lenin argued: abolish landlordism and the fetters of allotment ownership; “ownership by the people” clothes peasant unity without a worked-out economic theory.

## Semantic History

Lenin situates the demand in a longer Marxist and Russian sequence:

- **[[marx-karl]]:** capital remoulds traditional agrarian relations; form of landed property confronting nascent capitalism does not suit it; nationalisation appears as radical-bourgeois repudiation of private landed property (*Theories of Surplus Value*, as Lenin quotes) — but the radical bourgeois often lacks courage because attack on one form of property endangers property in the conditions of labour, and “the bourgeois has territorialised himself.”
- **Russian mass politics 1905–07:** Peasant Union congresses, First and Second Dumas — peasant deputies and Trudovik Bill of the 104 declare for nationalisation (including readiness to relinquish allotment property rights). Lenin reads this as **peasant “clearing of estates” for capitalism**, not commune socialism or SR “socialisation.”
- **RSDLP programmes:** 1885 Emancipation of Labour draft abstract; 1903 “cut-off lands” too limited; **Stockholm 1906** dual system (municipalisation of privately owned lands + nationalisation of forests/waters/colonisation lands if revolution develops favourably; division if not); Lenin’s 1907–08 argument that the SD programme in the Russian bourgeois revolution can **only** be nationalisation of the land.

Semantic drift Lenin fights: equating nationalisation with (a) bureaucratic centralism, (b) socialism, or (c) something feasible only at “high” agricultural capitalism after landowners are divorced from agriculture.

## Operationalizations

| Operationalization | Content (Lenin) |
|---|---|
| **Full nationalisation** | All land to state; differential rent to state; absolute rent abolishable; free tillage; unfenced land for new capitalist-suitable farms |
| **Trudovik Bill of the 104** | National land fund; management by local self-government elected by universal suffrage; leave allotments/small holdings with owners pending gradual nationalisation; priority to local/agricultural population |
| **Dual Stockholm system** | Public property on confiscated landlord land (municipal) + state on forests/colonisation; **private** allotments preserved — Lenin’s target as dualism |
| **Transition to division** | Nationalisation may become temporary bridge to private property of new farmers after the bourgeois revolution; still retains material/moral significance as thorough “unfencing” |

Programme constraint: nationalisation “cannot be carried out or bear fruit” without complete democratisation of the whole state (republic, abolish standing army, election of officials, etc.). Scope of agrarian and political changes must correspond.

## Applications

- **American vs Prussian path:** nationalisation as agrarian face of the **American** (peasant-farmer) path of bourgeois agrarian evolution vs Stolypin–Junker **Prussian** path of private ownership and forced commune break-up in landlords’ interests.
- **Absolute rent hinge:** Marxism may put nationalisation in a **bourgeois** revolution programme because [[absolute-rent]] hinders capitalist development; nationalisation is a bourgeois measure removing that monopoly barrier (and transferring differential rent to the state).
- **1908 “Political Notes” / “New Agrarian Policy”:** Lenin “unreservedly” for bourgeois nationalisation as only consistent slogan directing historically necessary break-up of old landownership against landlords and toward free farmers from the peasant mass; either peasant revolt + confiscation + republic, or Stolypin ruin.
- **Moral/international significance (Lenin):** proletariat helps strike a blow at one form of private property while supporting the most consistent bourgeois revolution against bourgeois half-heartedness.
- **Not tied to socialism’s destiny:** SD links socialism’s fate to **neither** landlord monarchy with private ownership **nor** farmers’ republic even with nationalisation; independent proletarian party defends socialism whatever the democratic agrarian outcome.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critiques **of** nationalisation as recorded in this source (strongest form via Lenin’s opponents):

- **Maslov / municipalisers:** nationalisation “hopelessly utopian” because peasants would not voluntarily transfer their land (Maslov 1905 as Lenin quotes); later, small proprietors fear big capital and hope in central authority.
- **John / Kostrov (Stockholm):** nationalisation isolates SD from peasantry; risk of counter-revolutionary Vendée if allotments nationalised.
- **Plekhanov:** nationalisation does not eliminate economic basis of tsarism; is “anti-revolutionary”; municipalisation as “guarantee against restoration.”
- **Tsereteli (Second Duma, as Lenin lists):** officials; injustice to small nationalities; nationalisation as weapon for restoration.
- **Borisov / high-capitalism view:** nationalisation only when Russia is no longer a peasant country — Lenin rejects as un-Marxist and schematic.
- **Finn:** nationalisation/municipalisation would transfer pre-capitalist “money rent,” not capitalist rent — Lenin: power extracting that rent is feudal latifundia; once broken, money rent dies with them.

**Limitations of wiki record:** nearly all counter-positions are **mediated by Lenin** in [[lenin-collected-works-vol-13-1962]]. Economic magnitudes (landownership tables, Duma bills) are Lenin’s statistical synthesis of 1905–07 sources. Live dispute filed at [[nationalisation-vs-municipalisation-debate]].
