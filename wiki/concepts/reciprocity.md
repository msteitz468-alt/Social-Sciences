# Reciprocity

## Definition as Coined

In economic anthropology, **reciprocity** is the class of "vice-versa" movements of goods **between two parties** (A⇄B), distinguished by [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] from **[[redistribution|pooling/redistribution]]**, the centralized collection and redivision *within* a group. *(Attributed — Sahlins, *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, ch. 5 "On the Sociology of Primitive Exchange," 1972.)* Following [[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]], reciprocity is a "between" relation (symmetry); pooling is a "within" relation (centricity) — though at the most general level "pooling is itself an organization of reciprocities."

Sahlins' key move is to treat reciprocity not as balanced one-for-one exchange but as **"a whole class of exchanges, a continuum of forms,"** whose intervals are "intervals of sociability." The classification is "more than formal": it is a moral as well as a mechanical scheme, running from solidary generosity to unsociable seizure.

## Semantic History

The concept descends from [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s Trobriand continuum of exchanges (*[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]*, 1922, from pure gift to [[gimwali]] barter), [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]'s [[gift-exchange|gift]] and its three obligations (to give, receive, repay), and Thurnwald's fieldwork. Sahlins fixed the now-standard **three-form typology** (see below); the term "negative reciprocity" he credits to Gouldner. Note that [[generalized-reciprocity]] later migrated into political sociology via [[putnam-robert-d|Putnam]]'s civic-trust usage, a narrower sense than Sahlins'.

## The three forms (Sahlins' continuum)

- **[[generalized-reciprocity]]** — "the solidary extreme": putatively altruistic giving; return expected only "if possible and necessary," indefinite in time and amount; the "pure gift," sharing, hospitality, kinship and chiefly dues. Diagnostic: a sustained one-way flow does not break the relation.
- **[[balanced-reciprocity]]** — "the midpoint": direct, equivalent, and prompt exchange; trade, marriage prestations, peace agreements, "primitive money." Diagnostic: the relation fails if not reciprocated within a narrow period.
- **[[negative-reciprocity]]** — "the unsociable extreme": "the attempt to get something for nothing with impunity" — haggling, barter with strangers, theft, seizure, the raid.

## Operationalizations (the sectoral model)

Sahlins' central thesis: **reciprocity inclines toward the generalized pole with close kinship and toward the negative pole with kinship distance** ("kindred goes with kindness" — Tylor). He maps the three forms onto concentric **kinship-residential sectors** (household → lineage → village → tribe → intertribal): generalized modes dominate the innermost sphere, balanced reciprocity the intermediate, and negative reciprocity the periphery. Two further axes modulate the flow: **kinship rank** (noblesse oblige; generosity as the "starting mechanism" of achieved rank — the [[big-man]]) and **wealth** (the greater the wealth gap, the more generalized the giving needed to sustain a given sociability). **Food**, as symbolically charged, circulates preferentially by generalized reciprocity even where durables undergo balanced exchange.

## Applications

The typology organizes the analysis of exchange, alliance, and politics across the primitive world; it underlies Sahlins' treatment of [[redistribution]] as "a centralized, formal organization of kinship-rank reciprocities" (so reciprocity and redistribution differ in degree of centralization, not in kind), of the [[big-man]], and of [[balanced-reciprocity|primitive trade]]. It is among the most widely cited frameworks in economic anthropology and diffused into sociology, archaeology, and social-capital theory. [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] deploys a two-tier version ethnographically in *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]*: an "open and balanced" reciprocity among the shantytown poor (the ethic of the *mata*) set against the asymmetric, hierarchical exchange of **[[paternalismo]]** (patron–client dependency) — the two in "tense dialectic."

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Sahlins himself notes the scheme describes *how* social distance shapes exchange but does not explain *when* exchanges occur — that lies in "the larger cultural structure and its adaptive response to its milieu." The sectoral model idealizes; real societies show self-interest and stinginess within close kinship (Firth's Maori proverbs; the Siriono). The typology is an etic ideal-type continuum, stated in attributed voice, not a set of discrete natural kinds. [[weiner-annette-b|Weiner]] (1976) presses a deeper objection: that the whole reciprocity paradigm (Mauss's segmentation of give/receive/repay; Sahlins's "generalized reciprocity" resting on Malinowski's "pure gift") reduces exchange to cause-and-effect and misses its work of **[[reproduction-regeneration|regenerating enduring identity]]** across generations — see [[weiner-women-of-value-study]], [[womens-wealth]].
