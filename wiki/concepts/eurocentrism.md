# Eurocentrism

One of [[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s four **[[four-haunts|haunts]]** of **[[historical-archaeology]]** (1996, Ch. 3): the centering of Europe as the measure of historical significance and the default focus of modern-world archaeology — and, equally, as a **research-design failure** when non-European and beyond-Europe contexts are omitted.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Orser 1996 application among wider critical usage.)* Eurocentrism as haunt shapes which sites get dug, which narratives get written, how non-European actors appear (if at all), and even mundane conventions (calendars, Greenwich meridian, atlas layouts). Orser asked whether it is "friend or foe" — inescapable starting point of a field born in colonial archaeology vs. distortion to combat. He engaged Samir Amin-type critiques and archaeological set-pieces such as Great Zimbabwe attribution struggles (European refusal to credit African builders as a classic Eurocentric pathology in the literature he cites).

## Semantic History

Long career in postcolonial theory, dependency theory, and world history before archaeological uptake. Orser imports it as a **mandatory analytical haunt** for the modern world, paired with colonialism, capitalism, and modernity — not as a freestanding culture-war slogan.

## Operationalizations in Historical Archaeology (Orser)

| Form | Example (Orser 1996) |
|---|---|
| Naturalized colonial archaeology | Equating HA with European colonies outside Europe (Setzler/Harrington lineage) without analyzing colonialism |
| Research geography | SHA founding map sidelining Europe-as-excavation-object |
| Expansion narratives | Omitting Ottoman/Mediterranean frames that shaped European expansion (Silberman and related points Orser mobilizes) |
| Ceramic hierarchies | Valuing European industrial wares over hand-built "colono" productions ([[colono-ware]]) |
| Inside-Europe coloniality | Anglocentrism and cultural suppression in Ireland ([[gorttoose]]) |

## Applications

Orser's program deliberately pairs Brazil ([[palmares]]) and Ireland ([[gorttoose]]), urges **reattaching Europe** as dig object and **looking beyond Europe**, and treats Eurocentrism as both past ideology and present professional habit.

## Critiques and Limitations

Open position space: does a ~1492 "modern world" periodization reinstall Eurocentrism under another name? Orser partly answers via Bartlett (1993) on medieval European "incipient" haunts (950–1350) and via mandatory beyond-Europe research design — recorded as his response, not wiki-voice resolution. Relation to [[postcolonialism]] more generally is wider than this archaeological packaging.
