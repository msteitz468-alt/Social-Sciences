# Monopoly of the Legitimate Use of Physical Force

## Definition as Coined

[[weber-max|Max Weber]], in *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (1919), defined the state sociologically not by its ends but by its specific *means*: **"a state is a human community that (successfully) claims the monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force within a given territory."** (Attributed voice — this is Weber's definitional stipulation, not a natural-kind fact.) Three elements are load-bearing: (1) **claim** — the monopoly is asserted, and only *successfully* claimed states are states; (2) **legitimacy** — force is authorized by a recognized right, distinguishing the state from mere banditry (see [[legitimate-domination]]); (3) **territory** — the monopoly is spatially bounded. Other actors may use force "only to the extent to which the state permits it"; the state is "the sole source of the 'right' to use violence." Weber credits the underlying intuition to Trotsky at Brest-Litovsk ("Every state is founded on force") while insisting force is the state's *specific*, not its *only*, means.

## Semantic History

Weber's formulation crystallized ideas present in his contemporaneous work on *Herrschaft* (domination) later collected in *Economy and Society*; the 1919 lecture is its most quoted statement. In twentieth-century social science the phrase migrated from a definitional stipulation to a near-axiom of state theory: it anchors Tilly's bellicist account of European state formation ("war made the state"), Elias's *civilizing process* (the pacification of internal space via the royal monopoly of violence and taxation), and the political-science vocabulary of "state capacity," "fragile states," and "ungoverned spaces." The qualifier *legitimate* — essential in the German — is frequently dropped in casual usage, collapsing Weber's Herrschaft-grounded concept into a crude "state = whoever has the guns," which loses the point.

## Operationalizations

- **State-formation history:** degree to which a central authority has disarmed rival armed powers (feudal magnates, private armies, mercenary bands) and concentrated the means of administration and coercion — Weber's own parallel to the capitalist's concentration of the means of production.
- **Comparative state capacity:** territorial reach of courts, police, and tax authority; the "reach of the state" into peripheries.
- **Fragility/collapse indices:** presence of competing legitimate-violence claimants (warlords, militias, insurgents) as the operational marker of a contested or absent monopoly.

## Applications

[[elias-norbert|Elias]] (*[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*, 1939) process-ualizes Weber's monopoly: free competition among warriors tends toward dual monopolies of physical force and taxation ([[monopoly-mechanism]]); pacified internal spaces and “violence confined to barracks” rewire everyday fears toward [[self-constraint]]. See [[civilizing-process]] and [[elias-civilizing-process-study]].

### Tilly's historicization (1990/1992, attributed)

In *[[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]]*, [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]
calls Weber's definition "historically contestable" and treats the monopoly as an *achievement*
of a specific process — the **bifurcation of violence**: states built "fearsome coercive means
of their own as they deprived civilian populations of access to those means" (weapons seizures
after rebellions, duel prohibitions, arms licensing, demolition of magnate castles, disbanding
of private armies, especially 1400–1700). Only after that disarmament did Weber's definition
"finally beg[i]n to make sense for European states." Consequences Tilly draws: declining
interpersonal violence alongside intensifying inter-state war; coup-proofing ("almost
impossible for a dissident faction to seize power... without the active collaboration of some
segments of the state's own armed forces"); and the US as the outlier that "cling[s] to
civilian possession of firearms." See [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]].

## Applications (general)

Foundational to the sociology of the state and to [[political-sociology|political sociology]] generally; the defining reference point in state-formation theory, security studies, and the analysis of civil war and state failure. Within this wiki it is the criterion by which [[the-state|the state]] is distinguished as a social form, and it sits with [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]] (why the monopoly is *obeyed*) and [[bureaucracy|bureaucratic administration]] (how it is *exercised*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Descriptive vs. normative slippage.** "Legitimate" in the definition is *sociological* legitimacy (belief-in-validity by the ruled), not moral justification; critics note the phrase is easily misread as endorsing state violence.
- **Empirical incompleteness.** Few states have ever held a *complete* monopoly; private security, vigilantism, licensed self-defense, corporate and paramilitary force, and imperial/colonial "indirect rule" all qualify the claim. The definition arguably describes an ideal-typical limit rather than an achieved condition.
- **Non-territorial and supranational power.** Diaspora authority, transnational organizations, and networked violence fit the territorial frame awkwardly.
- **Feminist and decolonial critiques** highlight violence the "legitimate monopoly" naturalizes (domestic, colonial, carceral). The concept remains indispensable but is a starting point for argument, not a settled boundary.
