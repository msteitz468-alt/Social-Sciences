# Mesopotamian Trade (Long-Distance)

## Definition as Coined

Long-distance exchange of goods into and out of the Mesopotamian alluvium, tracked from Neolithic obsidian circulation through the Old Babylonian period (c. 1500 BCE). [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] proposes a three-way analytic distinction of traded goods by their social reach, explicitly to caution against treating "trade" as a single homogeneous system: **essentials** (salt, grindstones — of near-universal relevance), **staples** (metal tools, export textiles — affecting a broad sector of society), and **luxuries** (elite goods circulating on a small scale) (attributed, Postgate 1994).

## Semantic History

Early accounts of Mesopotamian trade tended to treat it as a single undifferentiated phenomenon driven by state/temple demand for scarce raw materials (metals, semi-precious stones, and timber, all absent from the alluvium). Postgate's synthesis instead traces a long, evidentially uneven network stretching from Neolithic obsidian circulation, through the Uruk-period colonial expansion, to the well-documented Old Assyrian merchant colony at Kanesh and the Ur III–Old Babylonian Gulf trade — and explicitly flags where the evidence licenses only inference rather than certainty about the trade's social organization.

## Operationalizations

- **Neolithic and Ubaid antecedents.** Long-distance obsidian trade is attested from Neolithic times across the Near East; Mesopotamian Neolithic obsidian has been sourced via scientific analysis to the Lake Van region and central Turkey, alongside similar-range pottery-style diffusion, though the social organization of this early trade remains speculative (attributed, Postgate 1994). Ubaid-period grindstone trade is evidenced by a cache of several hundred grindstones recovered by a Soviet excavation team in a single room at Yarim Tepe (North Iraq, c. 4500 BCE), inferred by analogy with later historical volcanic-lava grindstone manufacture in the same locality to represent a specialized trading venture or way-station; an Old Babylonian Mari letter documenting grindstones shipped downriver via the Habur suggests a western route variant.
- **Uruk-period expansion.** Distinctive Uruk fine wares (red burnished ware) and administrative "hardware" (numeral tablets, clay sealings) are found as far apart as Godin Tepe (Iranian plateau) and Habuba Kabira (Euphrates bend, Syria). At Habuba Kabira/Jebel Aruda, a walled colonial city with South Mesopotamian-style temples, construction, and decoration is read as an actual trading colony (supported by clay sealings), in contrast with the Uruk presence in Iran, which Postgate describes as appearing "grafted on to the existing society" — flagged explicitly as an inference from archaeology rather than a certain reconstruction (attributed, Postgate 1994).
- **The lapis-lazuli route.** Jebel Aruda evidence demonstrates an eastward branch of the Uruk trading system reaching Afghanistan. Excavated sites document the lapis/carnelian network: Shortughai (Oxus valley) links to the Indus/Harappan-Mohenjo Daro culture; Shahr-i Sokhta (southeast Iran) functioned as a caravan city on the route through Elam to Sumer/Akkad in the third millennium BCE, with a specialized lapidary industry importing raw lapis/carnelian and exporting worked stone; Tepe Hissar lay on a northern route; Tepe Yahya manufactured chlorite bowls for export, found on both Gulf shores. Mesopotamian pottery in the Gulf region indicates sea-borne trade from the Ubaid period onward. The Sumerian epic of Enmerkar and the Lord of Aratta records legendary trade negotiations between Uruk and a distant land for lapis lazuli and carnelian, exchanged for a donkey caravan of grain — read by Postgate as reflecting substantive economic trade rather than pure diplomatic gift exchange, though the source is literary/epic, not administrative, and should be read in that register.
- **The Gulf trade — Tilmun, Magan, Meluhha.** Ur's merchant archive, excavated by Woolley in the city's merchant quarter and analyzed by Oppenheim and Leemans, documents sea-borne trade down the Persian Gulf to **Tilmun** (Bahrain), where excavation confirms a contemporary sea-port with further connections south and east. Imports to Ur included luxury goods and bulk **copper**, recorded in a memorandum on copper imported from Tilmun with weights given in both "Tilmun stone" and "Ur stone" standards. Survey and excavation now situate the copper source in the mineral-rich mountains of **Magan** (Oman) — Postgate treats the Oman = Magan identification as no longer controversial, and the Indus = **Meluhha** identification likewise no longer controversial, attested via Indus-style carnelian beads, seals, and weights found in both Bahrain and Mesopotamia (attributed, Postgate 1994). Mesopotamian exports southward (mostly archaeologically unrecovered, being perishable or manufactured) included textiles, wool, leather, sesame oil, and barley.
  - **The contraction pattern.** A notable geographic contraction appears over time: Pre-Sargonic Lagash and Akkad-Dynasty rulers claim direct trade reaching Meluhha, Magan, *and* Tilmun; by Ur III times ships reach only as far as Magan; by Old Babylonian times, only as far as Tilmun. Postgate offers two explicitly non-exclusive, unresolved explanations: (a) Tilmun intermediaries increasingly controlled or blocked further access to Magan, or (b) a broader shift from state-sponsored to private trading enterprise, implying reduced capital investment (attributed, Postgate 1994; recorded here as a position, not a settled conclusion).
- **The Old Assyrian Assur–Kanesh trade and the karum.** The Old Assyrian merchant colony trading tin and textiles between Assur (~1,600 km distant) and Kanesh (Kültepe, central Anatolia) is identified from cuneiform tablets that surfaced on the antiquities market at Kayseri in the late 19th century, confirmed in situ by Bedřich Hrozný's 1920s excavations at Kültepe. Donkey caravans exported tin and textiles across North Mesopotamia and over the Taurus to Anatolian trading colonies, returning with gold and silver; standard tin packages weighed 65 minas (~32.5 kg) per donkey-side. The Kanesh **karum** ("quay," originally a harbor unloading point, later an institutionalized merchants' association) was the administrative center of Assyrian trade during "Level II" (roughly four-plus generations), recording partnership capital funds (*naruqqum*), business-debt settlement, inheritance, and distribution to native courts and minor trading posts (other *karum*s, *wabartum*). Trade was regulated by both Anatolian native rulers and the Assyrians' own karum institution, with export and import taxes, smuggling attested, and Assyrians barred from trading in local Anatolian textiles or in luxury items reserved as a monopoly of the "City House" (*bīt ālim*) at Assur (attributed, Postgate 1994). Archaeologically, the merchant colony left almost no distinguishing material culture — only the cuneiform tablets reveal the foreign community — which Postgate uses as an explicit methodological caution: "there is no conceivable way in which we could have hypothesized the Kültepe trade from the economic and social constants of the two geographical areas" (attributed, Postgate 1994).
- **The merchant (*tamkarum*): state agent or private operator?** A terminological distinction existed in early (pre-Sargonic) texts between the ordinary "merchant" (Sumerian *damgar*, Akkadian *tamkārum*) and the specifically foreign trader (Sumerian *gaeš*/*garaš*); by the second millennium the single term *tamkārum* covers both internal and external trade. Postgate reconstructs three palace motivations for using merchants as agents rather than conducting trade directly: offloading the task of converting surplus goods into silver, converting a physical-goods problem into a transferable "paper" debt-collection risk, and avoiding transport/storage costs. At Larsa merchants bought palace goods on roughly one-third down / two-thirds deferred terms; at Sippar the palace supplied merchants ("Overseers of the Merchants") with a narrower range of staples (wool, sesame, cattle) via a "royal tablet."
- **The Ur III trade-monopoly question (open).** Some scholars have proposed that the Ur III state held a total monopoly on external trade. Postgate rejects this as unproven: "our sources are still too one-sided to allow us to deny that the merchant class could have been engaged in independent trading of its own" (attributed, Postgate 1994). Whether Ur III/Old Babylonian merchants were palace employees, independent agents, or some hybrid is described by Postgate as a genuinely open scholarly question; he argues modern employer/employee categories fit poorly, since a merchant could have "a recurrent engagement" without being a full-time "employee," and could conduct private business while still counting as a palace agent. This is recorded here as an open position, not adjudicated by the wiki.
- **[ANALOGY, explicitly flagged] The diaspora-merchant comparison.** Postgate argues foreign trade had structural reasons to run somewhat independently of direct state control — cross-border ventures require "neutrality" — and draws an explicit cross-cultural analogy to Jews and Armenians as diaspora trading minorities in medieval Europe and the Near East ("'Trade before the flag' is a universal human experience"). Postgate himself flags this as analogy, not evidence, consistent with the wiki's Evidence-Class Framework rule on flagged ethnographic/historical analogy: it is a hypothesis-generator, not a demonstrated parallel.
- **After Ur III.** Following the fall of Ur III, Postgate argues the merchant class, organized as the karum, stepped into the administrative vacuum left by the collapsed bureaucratic state, continuing to organize both foreign trade and internal redistribution on its own initiative; by the Larsa/Hammurabi period the head of the karum (*wakil tamkārī*) mediated palace–merchant relations, and the karum could act as a local legal/administrative authority even in non-commercial matters.

## Applications

Feeds [[temple-economy]] (merchants as institutional agents; temples as banks financing trade voyages, e.g. the Nanna Temple at Ur financing Tilmun voyages), [[money-and-silver]] (silver as the medium settling trade balances), [[ur]] (Gulf trade base), and [[sumerians]] (Uruk-period colonial expansion). Also documents overland and riverine trade beyond the Gulf and Anatolian networks: Tigris/Euphrates riverine traffic contributing to Assur's and Mari's wealth; overland desert-tribe trade (including slave-trading, prohibited by a royal edict of Samsu-iluna); and a documented aromatics trade running a northern overland route from Larsa to Zabban, Arrapha, and Šimurrum.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Archaeological visibility is asymmetric.** Imports (metals, stones) are far more archaeologically visible than Mesopotamian exports (textiles, foodstuffs, oils), which were perishable or fully consumed abroad — the trade balance reconstructed from finds alone would misleadingly appear one-directional.
- **Social organization is frequently inferred, not documented.** Postgate repeatedly cautions against over-confident reconstruction from purely archaeological evidence (e.g., at Habuba Kabira/Jebel Aruda) and states plainly that some trade networks (Kültepe) could not have been predicted from economic or social first principles at all — a standing caution against assuming trade infrastructure must leave a proportionate settlement/administrative footprint.
- **The state-control question remains genuinely open** for several periods and routes (Ur III monopoly question; employee vs. agent status of the *tamkarum*) and should not be resolved in wiki voice.
- **The diaspora-merchant analogy is Postgate's own comparative illustration**, not Mesopotamian evidence, and should not be cited as if it were.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

Van De Mieroop's account of the **Old Assyrian Assur–Kanesh (*karum*) trade** deepens the network already documented above (Postgate 1994). The merchant colony at Kanesh (Kültepe, central Anatolia), attested through roughly 22,500 recovered tablets from "Level II" (c. 1970–1834 BCE), was a private, family-firm-driven long-distance trade in tin and textiles moving between Assur and Anatolia, run largely independently of direct palace control. The archive is notable for including women's letters (Document 5.2, per Van De Mieroop) documenting gendered household and business tension between Assur-based wives and their traveling-merchant husbands — an unusually direct window onto the domestic side of the trading households behind the tablet-and-envelope contracts. This reinforces, from an independent source, Postgate's own emphasis above on the *karum*'s partnership capital funds (*naruqqum*), debt settlement, and inheritance business, and on the archive itself — rather than any distinctive material culture — as effectively the sole evidence for the merchant community's existence.
