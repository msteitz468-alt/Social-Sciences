# Class-Boundary Permeability

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Wright, *[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]* Part II.)* **Class-boundary permeability** is the degree to which people's lives cross class locations — via **mobility** (dynamic), **friendship**, and **cross-class marriage** (static ties). Class structures differ not only in the distribution of people across locations but in how tightly lives are **bounded** by those locations. Permeability matters because class shapes interests, strategic capacities, and experiences only insofar as lives are structured by class positions.

Wright contrasts **dimensional permeability** (crossing property, authority, or skill boundaries) with **locational permeability** (especially ties across the working-class boundary).

## Semantic History

Bridges Marxist concern with class formation/closure and mobility-table sociology. Wright engages Bourdieu on habitus-shaped interaction, Dahrendorf authority conflict, and Weberian market closure — as competing hypotheses for *which* boundary is least permeable (Marxist expectation: **property** hardest to cross).

## Operationalizations

Permeability-event matrices; loglinear models with dimensional variables (property, authority, skill); country interactions (US, Canada, Sweden, Norway, Japan, etc.). Measurement caveats: sample size, asymmetric friendships, farmer cells.

## Applications

*Class Counts* Chs. 6–8: among men, property boundary typically least permeable; cross-national variation (esp. North America vs Scandinavia); friendship and marriage patterns as class-formation indicators.

## Critiques and Limitations

Events ≠ full lived experience; operational class schemes drive results; gender patterns require separate specification. Single-program operationalization — attributed Wright.
