## Definition as Coined

In "Common Sense as a Cultural System" (in *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983; first 1975), [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] argues (attributed) that common sense is not a raw, universal apprehension of reality but is itself a **cultural system** — "historically constructed and... subjected to historically defined standards of judgment," on a par with myth, painting, epistemology, religion, science, ideology, art, and law. Its defining rhetorical move is to *deny* that constructed status and present itself as "the immediate deliverances of experience, not deliberated reflections upon it": "it is not a case at all, just life in a nutshell. The world is its authority." Where religion rests on revelation, science on method, and ideology on moral passion, common sense rests on the claim that it simply reports "the way things go."

Geertz frames the inquiry with Wittgenstein's image of language as an old city: anthropology has attended to the "old city" (haphazard traditional practice) but neglected the "squared-off suburbs" of systematized thought. Common sense, he proposes, is one such analyzable "suburb."

## Semantic History

Because common sense claims to be pre-cultural, its cross-cultural variability is easy to miss. Geertz's central demonstration is the **intersexuality case** (drawn from Robert Edgerton and W. W. Hill), where the same biological given yields opposite common-sense construals: American common sense treats the sex binary as an inviolable natural fact and intersexuality as a "moral and legal enigma" to be surgically corrected; Navaho common sense treats intersexed persons as "divinely blessed," bearers of the tribe's prosperity; Pokot common sense treats intersexuality matter-of-factly as a simple biological "error" ("God made a mistake"), like a botched pot. His upshot: "given the given, not everything else follows... God may have made the intersexuals, but man has made the rest."

A second case rereads [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s Zande witchcraft material (attributed) as being about Zande *common sense* rather than "primitive metaphysics": witchcraft is a "dummy variable" invoked precisely when ordinary expectations fail (a normally quick-healing cut festers; a carefully made pot cracks), and where a mundane cause is visible the potter blames his own negligence. "Witchcraft does not celebrate an unseen order, it certifies a seen one" — sealing the common-sense world "against the doubts its inevitable insufficiencies inevitably stimulate."

## Operationalizations

Common sense, Geertz argues, has no stable content, no settled logic, and no summarizable conclusions across cultures; it can be characterized only by its **stylistic features or "quasi-qualities"** — its tone, not its propositions. He names five (each substantivized with "-ness"):

| Quasi-quality | Definition (attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Naturalness** | Represents matters "as being what they are in the simple nature of the case" — an air of "of-courseness." (Not philosophical naturalism: a non-naturalistic cosmology, e.g. Walbiri "dreaming," can also confer it.) |
| **Practicalness** | Frames matters in a "folk-philosophical sense of sagacity" — prudence, levelheadedness — a quality it *bestows* on things, not one identical to material utility. |
| **Thinness** | "Represents them as being precisely what they seem to be, neither more nor less" — "sobriety, not subtlety... realism, not imagination" (Butler: "everything is what it is and not another thing"). |
| **Immethodicalness** | Comes in "epigrams, proverbs, obiter dicta, jokes, anecdotes" — caters "to the pleasures of inconsistency" (Whitman: "I contradict myself... I contain multitudes"); illustrated by contradictory Ba-Ila proverbs. |
| **Accessibleness** | The insistence that "any person with faculties reasonably intact can grasp common-sense conclusions" — no acknowledged specialists; its tone "even anti-expert." |

(Geertz mentions but does not adopt a sixth, "earthiness.") The "practicalness" quasi-quality carries an embedded debate: whether "primitive" knowledge is merely utilitarian ([[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]; Evans-Pritchard on the Zande) or richly intellectual/classificatory ([[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]) — Geertz reports the field consensus (as of 1975) had moved to the Lévi-Strauss view.

## Applications

The essay is a template for treating any "everywhere-found cultural form" interpretively rather than functionally: its programmatic close urges anthropology "away from functionalist accounts of the devices on which societies rest toward interpretive ones of the kinds of lives societies support" — an explicit statement of [[interpretive-anthropology]]'s opposition to [[structural-functionalism|functionalism]]. Common sense sits alongside religion, [[ideology]], [[art-as-a-cultural-system|art]], science, and law as one of the cultural systems Geertz analyzes across his career (compare the [[models-of-and-for]] apparatus of the religion essay).

## Critiques and Limitations

Because common sense is characterized only by tonal "quasi-qualities" and never by content, the account resists the usual tests: it describes a style, and the five qualities are Geertz's own analytic construct, "somewhat unstandard properties." The same under-determination charge leveled at [[interpretive-anthropology]] generally applies — the readings persuade as interpretations rather than compel as demonstrations. Geertz concedes common sense is "not usually a very tightly integrated" system, which limits how far the "cultural system" framing can be pushed.
