# Insulting the Meat

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]], from Dobe-area !Kung ethnography in *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, Ch. 8.)* After a successful hunt, camp members **belittle the kill and the hunter**—complaining that the animal is thin, small, a “pile of bones,” or worthless—so that the hunter cannot convert hunting success into arrogance or lasting dominance. Lee labels this complex of practices **insulting the meat**. It is a migrated emic–etic hybrid: the speech is !Kung social practice; the analytic label is Lee’s (and the textbook tradition that followed him).

The package includes (1) **humility on return** (the hunter must not brag or even announce the kill with pleasure); (2) **carcass-site banter** by the carrying party; and (3) explicit cultural theory of why boasting is dangerous, as stated by informants such as #Tomazho.

## Semantic History

Entered introductory anthropology as the textbook example of a **[[leveling-mechanisms|leveling mechanism]]** in egalitarian foraging societies. Related practices of enforced humility appear across hunter-gatherer ethnography under different labels. Lee also applied the same humility-enforcing frame to himself in the Christmas ox episode (Ch. 1): the ridiculing of the kill he bought as meat buyer revealed “fiercely egalitarian” norms aimed at his aloofness and arbitrariness—affection plus leveling, not mere ingratitude (Lee 1979; earlier essay Lee 1969b).

## Operationalizations

**1. Understated kill report (return to camp).** Announcing a kill is treated as arrogance and strongly discouraged. Humility and understatement are correct demeanor.

- **/Gaugo** (/Xai/xai): a hunter must not come home and brag of killing a big one; he sits in silence until asked what he saw, then replies he is “no good for hunting,” saw nothing—“maybe just a tiny one”—from which listeners smile, knowing he has killed something big.
- **/Xashe** (southern districts): when asked what he saw, the successful hunter replies he saw nothing; pressed, he may say he “just might have scratched its elbow,” then suggests going out in the morning with others to “have a look”—so that a carrying party of three to six brings the meat home together.

**2. Insults at the kill site during butchery.** When the carrying party finds an unmolested carcass, banter about the “miserably small kill” and useless exertions begins immediately and continues through butchery. Sample speech Lee records: “You mean to say you have dragged us all the way out here to make us cart home your pile of bones? Oh, if I had known it was this thin I wouldn’t have come.” The hunter answers with self-demeaning agreement (cook liver for strength and leave the rest for hyenas; even a duiker would be better). The party has **no intention of abandoning the kill**. “The heavy joking and derision are directed toward one goal: the leveling of potentially arrogant behavior in a successful hunter” (Lee 1979, Ch. 8).

**3. Explicit rationale — cool the heart.** **#Tomazho**, famous /Xai/xai healer:

> “When a young man kills much meat, he comes to think of himself as a chief or a big man, and he thinks of the rest of us as his servants or inferiors. We can’t accept this. We refuse one who boasts, for someday his pride will make him kill somebody. So we always speak of his meat as worthless. In this way we cool his heart and make him gentle.”

Result, on Lee’s reading: skill differences do not produce a Big Man prestige hierarchy.

**Related structural devices (not verbal insult, but same egalitarian package):**

- **Arrow ownership = meat ownership:** “the owner of the arrow is the owner of the meat,” even if another man shot it—ownership = right of formal distribution; arrows circulate in [[hxaro]], blurring credit. Women may own arrows and become meat owners. Marshall (1976:297, cited by Lee): the society seems to want to extinguish the concept of the meat belonging to the hunter.
- Formal meat distribution is eagerly anticipated; meat rarely falls below ~20% of diet and may peak ~90% in good periods, overall ~**40% of calories** from game vs ~**60% vegetable**—high social value of meat relative to energy return of hunting vs gathering.

## Applications

- Supports analyses of [[egalitarian-society]] reproduction—how material success (especially big-game kills: few hunters kill as many as five big animals a year, yet the “big seven” species may supply >50% of annual wild-game meat) is socially prevented from becoming permanent hierarchy.
- Anchors the broader suite of [[leveling-mechanisms]] Lee describes: cutting arrogant hunters down to size **and** helping those down on their luck (hunting magic, tattoo retouch, arrow-sharing).
- Comparative teaching case for demand sharing and ridicule of pretension among [[hunter-gatherer]] groups.
- Reflexive ethnography: Christmas ox incident as the ethnographer subjected to the same device (*[[lee-kung-san-study]]*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Does **not** mean !Kung lack conflict or inequality of hunting skill. Lee documents marked differentials: e.g. of 127 men, 37 never killed a kudu while 43 killed 10+; he estimates those 43 (**34%**) account for ~**79%** of kudu kills. Age raises success but does not erase within-cohort spread; better half of hunters may provide upward of two-thirds of meat over years.
- Leveling targets **status crystallization** and boastful demeanor, not skill variance itself.
- Do not treat the practice as a timeless law of egalitarianism; document as 1960s Dobe/ /Xai/xai ethnography (and Lee’s comparative claims), in attributed voice.
- Verbal insult is one practice among several (arrow sharing, stop-and-go hunting rhythms, fission—“vote with their feet”). Isolating the speech event without the structural devices understates the package.
- Gender: hunting is men’s main occupation; women’s complaints Lee heard more often concerned men not bringing **enough** meat, not exclusion from the insulting ritual per se.

## Christmas ox densification (*Dobe Ju/'hoansi* App. A)

*(Attributed — Lee, "Eating Christmas in the Kalahari," reprinted as App. A of *[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|The Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* 4th ed.; original *Natural History* 1969.)* Lee bought a large ox as a Christmas thank-you for the /Xai/xai camp. People systematically **insulted the meat** — thin, worthless — until after the feast, when //Gaugo and others explained the leveling script: arrogance must be cooled; no act of giving is pure generosity without calculation. The episode is the textbook performance of [[insulting-the-meat]] applied to the ethnographer. Cross-link: [[lee-dobe-juhoansi-study]], [[leveling-mechanisms]].
