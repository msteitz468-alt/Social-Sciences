# Non-contractual elements of contract

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893, Book I ch. 7), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] argued — against [[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]] — that a contract cannot be the foundation of social order because **every contract presupposes social conditions the parties did not create**: "everything in the contract is not contractual. The only engagements which deserve this name are those which have been desired by the individuals... But wherever a contract exists, it is submitted to regulation which is the work of society and not that of individuals" (attributed).

Contract law supplies "the juridical consequences of our acts that we have not determined... what we cannot foresee individually is there provided for... this regulation imposes itself upon us, although it may not be our handiwork, but that of society and tradition." His conclusion: "a contract is not sufficient unto itself, but is possible only thanks to a regulation of the contract which is originally social."

## Semantic History

The phrase "the non-contractual element(s) in contract" became a standard shorthand in sociology (notably via Talcott Parsons's reading of Durkheim) for the general thesis that markets and exchange rest on a prior moral/legal order. It is one of the most frequently cited arguments of *Division of Labor* and a keystone of the sociological critique of utilitarian and rational-choice foundations for social order.

## Operationalizations

Durkheim's evidence (attributed): the **growth** of contract regulation — French Civil Code provisions on validity, guarantee against eviction and hidden defects, mandatory disclosure, the judge's power to grant delays, and art. 1135's imputation of obligations from "equity, usage, or the law" even when unexpressed. Custom and diffuse professional/occupational morality add further non-legal but still social and imperative regulation. A practical/cognitive argument reinforces it: parties lack the time, foresight, and calculating capacity to renegotiate every contingency, so pre-established, socially inherited "normal conditions of equilibrium" (contract law) are the "fundamental norm" underlying individual contracts.

## Applications

- The load-bearing argument in the [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]]: restitutive/administrative law has *grown*, not shrunk, under industrialism, refuting Spencer's prediction that positive social control recedes.
- Grounds the claim that [[organic-solidarity]] is a **moral** fact, and that society can strip an unjust contract of authority even when mutually consented to (linking to the [[forced-division-of-labor|justice of contract]]).
- A precursor of the general sociological principle that economic action is embedded in social institutions.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Durkheim rejected both Kant's personality-derivation and Spencer's organism-analogy derivation of contractual rights as historically ungrounded rationalizations (attributed) — a strong claim about the priority of the social over the individual that individualist theorists dispute.
- As a demonstration it is illustrative (Civil Code examples) rather than systematic.
