# Scissors Crisis

## Definition as Coined

In Soviet economic and party discourse of the early NEP years, the **scissors** named the diverging paths of industrial and agricultural price indices. [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]]'s report to the twelfth party congress (April 1923) used a diagram of opening blades; the term stuck. When industrial prices rose relative to agricultural prices, peasants received less industrial goods for a unit of grain — a crisis of the town–country exchange on which [[new-economic-policy|NEP]] depended. The reverse opening (agricultural prices soaring) held the cities "to ransom" (Carr's phrasing).

Primary documentation here is [[carr-edward-hallett|E. H. Carr]]'s narrative in *[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979|The Russian Revolution: From Lenin to Stalin]]* (1979), Ch. 6.

## Semantic History

- **1922–23 classic scissors:** After NEP recovery, city demand for food outstripped peasant demand for industrial goods (summer 1922: agricultural prices soared relative to industrial). Trusts then formed selling syndicates; from late 1922 industrial prices rose "dramatically" at agriculture's expense. At the widest opening (October 1923), Carr reports the industrial-to-agricultural price ratio at **three times the 1913 level**, concurrent with currency depreciation, wage grievances, and a wave of strikes.
- **Policy response:** The Central Committee appointed a seventeen-member "scissors committee"; Trotsky refused to serve and instead sent his 8 October letter and supported the platform of the 46. Wholesale (and some retail) price controls closed the blades by early 1924; a party conference endorsed the compromise days before Lenin's death (January 1924).
- **Second scissors (1924–25):** After the 1924 harvest, fixed grain prices failed; private traders flooded the market; rye prices doubled (Dec 1924–May 1925); scissors reopened **in favour of the peasant**. The excellent 1925 harvest produced a marketing disaster: prosperous peasants held stocks; export/industrialization hopes evaporated; Carr says the crisis "split the party."
- **Aftermath:** Carr argues the 1923 crisis showed free-market regulation of exchange was impracticable; price control became permanent; holding industrial prices down to preserve the peasant "link" remained party doctrine into the planning debates.

## Operationalizations

| Indicator (Carr) | 1923 peak reading |
|---|---|
| Industrial vs agricultural price ratio (vs 1913) | ~3× at October 1923 widest opening |
| Industrial output (year to 1 Oct 1924) | 2½× 1920 but only ~40% pre-war; metals ~28.7% pre-war |
| Policy instruments | Wholesale/retail price maxima (salt, paraffin, sugar); currency reform (chervonets, March 1924); Commissariat of Internal Trade (May 1924) |

## Applications

- **Left Opposition prehistory:** Scissors debates fused economic critique with charges of planless leadership (Trotsky's letter; platform of the 46) — see [[left-opposition]].
- **Planning turn:** Carr treats the scissors as demonstrating that NEP recovery itself required **non-NEP** instruments (price command), foreshadowing the managed economy of the [[five-year-plan]].
- **Peasant–worker exchange:** Links permanently to [[peasantry-as-class]] and the grain-collection crises of 1927–29 that preceded [[collectivization]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Carr's account is a synthesis without footnotes (1979 distillation of the multi-volume *History of Soviet Russia*). Price-ratio magnitudes and institutional detail should be cross-checked against specialist economic histories (he thanks R. W. Davies and Alec Nove for related chapters). The "scissors" metaphor is emic/party jargon elevated to analytic category — keep attribution when generalizing beyond 1920s USSR.
