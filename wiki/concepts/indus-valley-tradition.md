# Indus Valley Tradition

## Definition as Coined

As presented by [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] (1998), following [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]], the **Indus Valley Tradition** is an etic cultural-tradition construct borrowed from Americanist archaeology (Willey & Phillips “tradition” concept). It covers the long-run sequence of human adaptations in the greater Indus Valley and adjacent regions from plant/animal domestication through urban integration and later localization — **not** only the Mature Harappan “civilization” spike.

Kenoyer’s terminological split (attributed):

- **“Indus Valley civilization”** — the period of urban expansion and cultural integration over a large region (Integration Era / Harappan Phase).
- **“Indus Valley Tradition”** — the developmental process from earliest food-producing communities through localization.

Shaffer also identifies **Baluchistan** and **Helmand basin** cultural traditions evolving **alongside** the Indus Valley Tradition; Kenoyer expects further eastern traditions as peninsular research grows. Highland-related Mehrgarh phases are grouped with the Baluchistan tradition rather than forced into the Indus-plain sequence.

## Semantic History

Pre-Shaffer literature often treated “Harappan civilization” as a relatively brief urban package (sometimes still framed against Egypt/Mesopotamia checklists). Shaffer’s Tradition vocabulary, adopted and tabulated by Kenoyer 1998, re-embeds cities in multi-millennial process. Parallel vocabularies remain active:

| Scheme | Rough mapping to Kenoyer/Shaffer eras |
|---|---|
| Early / Mature / Late Harappan | Late Regionalization ≈ Early Harappan; Integration ≈ Mature/Harappan Phase; Localization ≈ Late Harappan |
| Possehl’s [[indus-age]] | Longer chronological umbrella with domain geography; complementary, not identical |
| Kenoyer table (1998) | Early Food Producing / Neolithic-Chalcolithic ca. 6500–5000 BCE; Regionalization / Early Harappan ca. 5000–2600; Integration / Harappan Phase 2600–1900; Localization / Late Harappan 1900–1300; post-Indus Indo-Gangetic Regionalization (e.g. PGW ca. 1200–800; NBPW later) |

Kenoyer notes limited scholarly consensus on terminology partly because academic traditions are nationally dispersed (Pakistan, India, Europe, America, Japan).

## Operationalizations

**Eras** (Kenoyer 1998 following Shaffer) — each subdivided into **phases** defined by artifacts, painted pottery, and technologies; a phase is region- and time-limited but may overlap neighbors via exchange networks:

1. **Early Food-producing Era** — scattered villages/camps; food production; no elaborate ceramics. Type representation: Mehrgarh Periods IA, IB, IIA (ca. 6500–5000 BCE) — domestic wheat and barley, cattle/sheep/goat, rectangular mud-brick houses with storage cubicles, bitumen-coated baskets; coarse chaff-tempered ceramics only at period’s end; shell and colorful-stone ornaments.
2. **Regionalization Era** (ca. 5000–2600 BCE) — crafts including ceramics, metallurgy, lapidary, faience, sealmaking; regional styles and trade networks; later part often called **Early Harappan** = formative urbanism (walled settlements, craft specialization, beginnings of writing as pottery graffiti). Within the Indus plain Shaffer names four important phases: **Balakot, Amri, Hakra, Kot Diji**. Amri Phase (type-site Amri, Sindh): southern Pakistan possibly to Rann of Kutch; Kot Diji Phase: northern Indus and Saraswati plains plus parts of Baluchistan; both late Regionalization ca. 3300–2600 BCE before large urban centers. Amri and Kot Diji styles especially feed later Integration material culture.
3. **Integration Era / Harappan Phase** (ca. 2600–1900 BCE) — synthesis of cultural groups into an overarching system; unifying symbols on pottery, ornaments, ritual objects; large cities and satellites; writing; [[standardized-weights|standardized weights and measures]]; taxation; hierarchical social order. Kenoyer counts >1,500 known settlements over ~680,000 km² (about twice Egypt or Mesopotamia as he frames it).
4. **Localization Era** (ca. 1900–1300 BCE) — decline/disintegration of Indus economic/political structure into localized states/chiefdoms; seals, writing, and chert weights (possibly for taxation) no longer needed; changes in burial and ritual. Kenoyer associates this span with Indo-Aryan languages becoming common in the northern subcontinent (attributed historical-linguistic framing, not a genetic or invasion claim). Previously integrated Indus Valley and Gujarat break into major localized cultures defined by new painted motifs/ceramics, geometric seals, and new burial customs (~600 years) — mapped by Kenoyer after [[mughal-rafique|Mughal]] 1990 as **Cemetery H**, **Jhukar/Late Kulli**, and **Rangpur IIb–IIc** areas.

**Indo-Gangetic tradition** (Kenoyer’s named extension): links Localization-era Indus Valley Tradition to a larger Ganga–Yamuna + greater Indus trajectory; Regionalization of the Indo-Gangetic tradition ca. 2000–300 BCE overlaps Indus localization; rice/millet agriculture, iron, and horse become widespread; later Integration aligns with Early Historic urbanization. Kenoyer’s Ch. 9 metaphor: first urban civilization fades as new cultures emerge at eastern and northern edges; >1000 years for political center to shift from Indus Valley to middle Ganga.

Dating: primarily [[radiocarbon-dating]] (HARP 70+ dates at Harappa; regional hundreds more) plus ceramic/settlement sequences; Kenoyer stresses **non-uniform** origin and regional time-lags. Food-production transition treated (following [[jarrige-jean-francois|Jarrige]]/Costantini) as indigenous highland–piedmont process after Mehrgarh, not simple Near Eastern diffusion.

## Applications

- Chronological scaffold for Kenoyer 1998 chapters on origins, cities, crafts, religion, and decline/transformation.
- Organizes [[craft-specialization]] claims (complex standardized crafts peak in Integration; elite communication crafts drop in Localization while many technical crafts continue).
- Frames [[middle-asian-interaction-sphere]] / [[meluhha]] long-distance trade as Integration Era phenomena that end as Localization fragments power and Gulf–Mesopotamia links.
- Underwrites Kenoyer’s argument that Indus cities grew from earlier villages in the same localities over hundreds of years, increasing in size and density into regional primaries surrounded by towns and villages — not short-term foundation by visionary rulers.

## Critiques and Limitations

- One of several competing periodizations ([[indus-age]], Early/Mature/Late Harappan, domain schemes); era labels can smuggle processual theory (integration/localization as social diagnosis).
- Construct is **etic**; do not equate Tradition with a people, language, or genetic population (four-way non-identity).
- Phase membership (especially highland vs plain; Hakra survey vs excavation series) depends on ceramic typology choices and survey intensity.
- Kenoyer’s Indo-Aryan association with Localization is an attributed linguistic-historical hypothesis, not archaeological proof of migration mechanism.
- Parallel traditions (Baluchistan, Helmand) are unevenly excavated; “expected eastern traditions” remain partly prospective as of 1998.

## Wright (2010) — Urban period chronology and scale

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]*, 2010, Ch. 5.)*

Wright dates the onset of the **Urban period to ca. 2600 BCE** and reports (via Possehl 1990:270) that the number of known sites increased from 463 in the Pre-urban period to 976 in the Urban period. She identifies five major Indus cities: Harappa (Upper Indus, ~150 ha), Mohenjo-daro (Lower Indus, 125–200 ha), Dholavira (Kutch, 47–100 ha), Ganweriwala (Cholistan, 81 ha), and Rakhigarhi (Ghaggar-Hakra, discussed only briefly given limited published material). Population estimates (150–200 people/hectare, by analogy with present-day settlements) yield roughly 20,000–40,000 for Mohenjo-daro and 25,000–30,000 for Harappa — a conventional methodological estimate, not a direct count.
