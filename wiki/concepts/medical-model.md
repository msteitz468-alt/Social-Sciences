# Medical model (mental hospitalization)

## Definition as Coined

In Essay 4 of *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]*, "The Medical Model and Mental Hospitalization," [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] analyzes the **medical model** as the professional and institutional frame that presents mental-hospital work as a form of **personal service** on the analogy of somatic medicine — expert treatment of a condition for a client — under conditions that are simultaneously those of a [[total-institution]]. The term was already current in clinical and administrative discourse; Goffman's contribution is the sociological analysis of its use as a language of explanation and role support inside the hospital (attributed).

### Personal-service / "tinkering" model (attributed)

Goffman defines a personal-service occupation ideally as one whose practitioner performs a specialized personal service for a set of clients. He analyzes an ideal **server–client–object** triangle, fee relation, symptoms vs. signs, repair cycle, and workshop imagery. Being served a summons, for example, is not a personal service for the recipient — the contrast clarifies that service implies assistance **desired** by the recipient (attributed).

### Strains when applied to institutional psychiatry (attributed)

- **Custodial vs. service** functions; involuntary admission undermines the client-desire premise.
- Stigma and diffuse mandate; the ward as a bad observation site for the conditions the model claims to treat.
- Medical frame of reference accounts not only for dosage but for hours of meals, folding of linen, and other non-clinical decisions — each official goal "lets loose a doctrine" with "inquisitors and martyrs."
- Doctor–patient interaction under constraint: the "psychiatric line"; complaints read as signs (of illness) rather than symptoms-in-the-patient's-terms or legitimate grievances.
- Case-record remaking of the patient; group therapy as small-group indoctrination translating gripes into personality problems.
- Coercive maintenance practices (ward language of privilege, ECT, lobotomy, forced medical discipline) justified within medical language even when closer to institutional maintenance than personal service.
- Closing bind (attributed): hostility to the hospital can be read as proving the need for the hospital; the service ideal can crush those it claims to serve.

Goffman cites period literature including Thomas Szasz and others as interlocutors; those citations are positions in a wider debate, not endorsements in wiki voice.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Clinical/administrative (pre-1961) | Psychiatry as branch of medicine; hospital as treatment site |
| Goffman 1961 | Medical service model under strain inside batch custodial life and involuntary patients |
| Anti-psychiatry / labeling reception | "Medical model" as target of critique (broader than Goffman's essay) |
| Later psychiatry | Biopsychosocial and recovery models position themselves against a narrowed "medical model" |

## Operationalizations

Role analysis of server–client claims; inventory of decisions justified in medical language; observation of ward-level doctor–patient encounters; distinction between **normative** (personal service) and **maintenance** medicine (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Goffman does not adjudicate clinical validity of diagnoses; he analyzes social organization.
- "Medical model" as a polemical slogan in later culture wars is downstream of, and broader than, this essay — keep attributed and periodized.
- Helmreich and others place Goffman near Laing and Szasz in critical stance toward psychiatry; that is reception POSITION, not identical to Goffman's stated project (structure of the self; partisan inmate-world correction of professional literature).
