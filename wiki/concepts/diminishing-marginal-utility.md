# Diminishing Marginal Utility (social application)

## Definition as Coined

The principle of **diminishing marginal utility** — that each additional unit of a good yields a smaller increment of satisfaction than the last — originates in 1870s marginalist economics. This page concerns its **transposition to social rewards** by [[social-exchange-theory|exchange theorists]], chiefly [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] (following [[homans-george-c|Homans]]): "the profits from exchange decrease with the number of exchanges."

Blau adapts the principle with an important qualification: social rewards, unlike food, "do not have a point of complete satiation; instead, as more of them are obtained, their significance declines." The **shape** of the decline depends on expectations — a sharp drop once a goal-specific expectation is met (a medical degree once earned), a gradual decline for open-ended ones (wealth, fame, power). And it is opposed by a countervailing principle: rising expectations from past attainment *increase* the significance of further rewards, "an effect opposite to that of... diminishing marginal utility."

## Semantic History

From a theory of consumer value (economics) to a theory of the changing significance of approval, deference, and other social rewards (Blau), and — in Blau's most original extension — to a *collective* form (see Applications). The migration is deliberate: Blau holds that "concepts that have been fruitful in economic theory can be adapted for use in sociological analysis outside the strictly economic sphere."

## Operationalizations

Inferred from valuation: Blau reports that newcomer caseworkers not yet accepted valued colleague approval more than easily available approval, while established popular workers (for whom colleague approval was abundant) valued it less — the declining significance of an abundant reward.

## Applications

- Regulates [[social-exchange]] volume: transactions between partners stabilize where declining marginal utility no longer justifies the cost.
- Grounds Blau's reading of [[relative-deprivation]] as **"diminishing collective marginal utility"**: distributing more of a reward across a collectivity yields diminishing net gains in aggregate satisfaction, because abundance makes holders value it less and non-holders feel its absence more.
- Explains why [[fair-exchange|scarce, hard-won rewards]] (colleague approval) are valued above easily obtained ones.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The transposition assumes social rewards behave like economic goods, which the concept's critics (and Blau's own hedge that there is "no point of complete satiation") partly concede they do not.
- "Significance," unlike cardinal utility, is not directly measurable, so the curve is illustrative rather than estimated.
