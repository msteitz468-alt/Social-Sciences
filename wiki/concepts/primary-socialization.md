# Primary Socialization

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (1966):

> Primary socialization is the first socialization an individual undergoes in childhood, through which he becomes a member of society.

Attributed features:

- Significant others are **imposed** (not chosen); socialization is never a purely free adoption of the social world.
- Proceeds through **emotional identification**; the child takes on the significant others’ roles and attitudes, and their world becomes *the* world — not one world among many.
- Internalization of language crystallizes the objective and subjective symmetry of reality.
- Echoes Mead: progression toward a [[generalized-other|generalized other]]; self as reflected from significant others ([[the-social-self]]).
- Establishes the deepest layer of subjective reality; later [[secondary-socialization]] builds on it and is typically more brittle.

They describe successful primary socialization as something like a “confidence trick”: the world is presented as the only world, though it is historically and socially particular.

## Semantic History

Builds on Meadian and Schutzian social psychology while placing primary socialization inside the societal dialectic of [[externalization-objectivation-internalization]]. Distinct from vague “upbringing” by the technical contrast with secondary socialization and by the reality-construction framing.

## Operationalizations

Process theory, not a scale. Indicators in later research: early attachment, language acquisition, primary-group mediation of culture.

## Applications

Childhood as induction into the societal dialectic; identity formation; class/culture-specific “worlds” as primary realities; basis for comparing successful vs unsuccessful socialization.

## Critiques and Limitations

Family-centric; less on state, media, and peer primary influence in modern childhood. The “confidence trick” metaphor is interpretive, not a testable mechanism claim.
