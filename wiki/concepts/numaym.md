# Numaym

## Definition as Coined

Kwak'wala **'nə'me'ma / numaym** ("one kind"; members are *'nE'me'mot*, "numaym fellows") — the constituent unit of each [[kwakiutl|Kwakwaka'wakw]] tribe/village, and per [[boas-franz|Boas]] "the ultimate units bound together by strict social obligations." In *Kwakiutl Ethnography* (ed. Codere 1966) Boas describes the numaym as best understood **not as a set of living people but as a fixed number of named positions**: each position carries a name, a "seat" or "standing place" (rank), and privileges; he counts 658 such seats among the thirteen tribes from Fort Rupert to Nimkish River and Knight Inlet (Boas 1925). Each numaym has an origin ("home") myth — an ancestor descending from the sky, emerging from the sea, or coming from underground, usually in animal form, who removes the mask and becomes human.

## Semantic History

- **Boas's own characterization shifted and remained tense.** In the same 1966 chapter Boas states the numaym is "neither strictly patrilineal nor matrilineal" — a child may within limits be assigned to any line of descent, even by bequest to unrelated lines — making the terms sib, clan, and gens "inapplicable"; yet he also writes that "the concept of patrilineal descent pervades this system" (patrilocal residence, children reckoned to the father's numaym by default, a woman inheriting the primogeniture line receiving a man's name and passing the position to her son). The ingested edition preserves both statements; treat the numaym as a fundamentally cognatic/optative-affiliation institution with a strong patrilineal-patrilocal default, not a unilineal descent group.
- **Nobility and commoners.** Boas infers from the house-founding stories that membership was never limited to the founder's blood descendants: direct descendants form the nobility; others accepted as members are the "house-men" (*bEgwi't*) of the chief.
- **Later theory.** The numaym became a standard exhibit in the critique of unilineal [[descent-theory]] and (in later literature, not yet ingested) a prototype case for Lévi-Strauss's "house society."

## Operationalizations

Boas's roster method: ~20 tribes of the two Kwa'g'ul dialect groups enumerated with their constituent numayma by name and gloss (Ge'xsEm "chiefs," Gi'g'Elgam "the first ones," etc.). Of the numaym names, Boas counts only a handful as local/place names; most are collective forms of ancestor names or honorifics. Boas flags that some list variants were never rechecked with a new informant, and that identifying a numaym's original home site archaeologically "does not seem to be possible" — shell mounds are ubiquitous.

## Applications

- **Property.** Hunting grounds, berry patches, river and salmon-fishing sites, and trap sites were numaym-exclusive, defended sometimes lethally against trespass (Boas 1921; a remembered killing at Dzawade is recounted).
- **Succession.** Primogeniture regardless of sex was the stated rule — with rank passing through a firstborn daughter — but Boas records that "the family histories indicate that there is no full agreement," some informants holding that a younger brother displaces a firstborn daughter; women could not hold winter-ceremonial offices, complicating female succession.
- **Ceremonial solidarity.** In the [[kwakiutl|winter ceremonial]], a numaym bore collective liability: when a poor member's son "fell" in a dance and atonement was unaffordable, numaym fellows had to contribute non-returnable gifts, "because the failure to atone... would reflect upon the social standing of the whole numayma" (Boas ms., 1966).
- **Scale.** Boas estimates old villages at 500–800 inhabitants, implying an average numaym of roughly 100 people.

## Critiques and Limitations

The construct is Boas's synthesis of Hunt-recorded family histories whose tellings disagree (Boas himself attributes discrepancies to records belonging to different numayma and to individuals carrying different names in different numayma). Documentation moment: 1886–1930s fieldwork on a society already transformed by depopulation and the potlatch ban. Spellings vary in the corpus (numaym, numayma, 'nE'me'ma); OCR of the ingested edition garbles Kwak'wala diacritics, so native terms here are approximate.
