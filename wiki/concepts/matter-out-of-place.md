# Matter Out of Place

## Definition as Coined

In *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (1966), [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] recovers and makes load-bearing the definition of **dirt as matter out of place**. After abstracting pathogenicity and hygiene from the modern notion of dirt, she writes (attributed):

> "If we can abstract pathogenicity and hygiene from our notion of dirt, we are left with the old definition of dirt as matter out of place. This is a very suggestive approach. It implies two conditions: a set of ordered relations and a contravention of that order. Dirt then, is never a unique, isolated event. Where there is dirt there is system. Dirt is the by-product of a systematic ordering and classification of matter, in so far as ordering involves rejecting inappropriate elements."

In the Introduction she had already stated the order thesis: "Dirt offends against order. Eliminating it is not a negative movement, but a positive effort to organise the environment." In the 2002 preface she restates the relativist edge: "there is no such thing as dirt; no single item is dirty apart from a particular system of classification in which it does not fit" — while still affirming that everyone finds dirt offensive; *what counts* as dirt depends on the classifications in use.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Pre-Douglas proverbial / philosophical | "Matter out of place" as folk or literary definition of dirt |
| Douglas 1966 Ch. 2 | Analytic core of pollution theory: dirt = residual of classification |
| Douglas 2002 preface | Universal offensiveness of dirt + culturally relative content |
| Later cultural studies / sociology | Citation phrase for boundary maintenance, risk rhetoric, aesthetic order |

Douglas's formulation is not a coinage of the English phrase but its **systematization** as the hinge of comparative pollution analysis (see [[purity-pollution-theory]]).

## Operationalizations

1. Identify the local **ordered relations** (taxonomy of persons, animals, spaces, times, substances).
2. Identify **contraventions** and residuals (what is rejected to keep the pattern).
3. Read dirt-avoidance and cleansing as **positive order-making**, not only negative fear or germ-killing.
4. Do not treat "our" hygiene as non-symbolic and "their" purity as merely symbolic — Douglas argues both express symbolic systems; difference is detail (Ch. 2).

## Applications

- Secular dirt and household order (shoes on the table, food on clothing, bathroom objects in the living room).
- [[ritual-pollution|Ritual pollution]] systems (caste food rules, bodily emissions, death impurity) as the same logic under different intensity.
- [[anomaly|Anomalies]] (creatures, persons, statuses that do not fit classes) as structurally related residuals.
- Environmental and technological **risk** selection (Douglas & Wildavsky 1982: risk like taboo — selective accusation protecting a vision of community; 2002 preface).

## Critiques and Limitations

- If read as pure idealism, the definition can underplay material disease ecology; Douglas compounds hygiene and convention rather than denying pathogens.
- Relative definition requires ethnographic depth in *local* classification — Douglas criticized free-floating "universal anomaly/disgust" readings (e.g. of Leach or of biological universal-disgust claims) that skip the division of labour and local categories (2002 preface).
- "No absolute dirt" does not mean "no dirt" — offence is treated as universal; content is not.
