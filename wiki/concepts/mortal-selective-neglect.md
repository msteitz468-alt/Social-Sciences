# Mortal (Selective) Neglect

## Definition as Coined

**Mortal neglect** — earlier "ethnoeugenic selective neglect" (Scheper-Hughes 1984), a phrasing she
later called "unfortunate" — is [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s term for the culturally
supported, graded withdrawal of maternal investment from infants judged unlikely to thrive. In
*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* (Chapters 7–8), Alto mothers
distinguished infants who were "keepers"/"thrivers" from those seen as born "already wanting to die,"
*dificil de criar* ("difficult to raise"), or a *criança condenada* ("condemned child"), and let the
latter die "*a míngua*" — of neglect/wasting. Crucially, she argues, this is *not* Western "child abuse":
it is "not motivated by anger, hate, or aggression"; mortally neglected infants were often "prettily
kept… dusted with sweet-smelling talcum powders," dying with candles in their hands, "offered up" to
Jesus and Mary.

## Semantic History

Scheper-Hughes worked through several labels — "ethnoeugenic selective neglect" (1984), Claire Cassidy's
"benign neglect" (rejected as misleading in a North American register), finally "mortal neglect." The
concept sits within a comparative literature on selective infanticide and infant stigmatization (Nuer
"crocodile children," Irish "changelings," Bariba "witch babies," Mehinaku *kanupa*), which she treats
as analogy and context — never as evidence that Nordestinos are unusually prone to it.

## Operationalizations

She grounds the concept in reproductive life-histories and healer/mother testimony: a midwife withholding
strengthening tea from a "puny and wasted" infant ("it is better in some cases if it dies… it will never
be worth anything") while not permitting outright starvation; the "given-up/giving-up complex" (Engel);
statistical estimates that up to 43% of the 255 child deaths in her sample were attributed to folk
"doomed-child" categories (*doença de criança*, *ataque de menino*, "weakness," "fright sickness"). The
practice is bound up with **[[infant-mortality]]**: acute "child attack" often reflected treatable
diarrheal dehydration culturally coded as untreatable.

## Applications

- **Coexistence with love.** The Lordes/"Zezinho" narrative (a neglected infant she rescued in 1966 who
  survived to become his mother's favored "elect" son, murdered in 1987) demonstrates her claim that
  "mortal selective neglect and intense maternal attachment coexist" — a doomed infant who shows a
  "hidden talent for life" can be greeted with lasting love.
- **Scapegoat and triage.** She reads the practice through Girard's sacrificial "generative scapegoat"
  and through household survival-triage — the "best bets" for survival are invested in, others "let go."

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is contested on two fronts. Empirically/interpretively, Marilyn Nations and Linda-Anne Rebhun
argue the maternal calm it rests on masks a shell-shocked grief rather than a genuine "letting go" (see
[[mother-love-scarcity-debate]]). Ethically, critics charge that ascribing agency to neglectful mothers
courts "victim-blaming"; Scheper-Hughes anticipates this, insisting on "the confusion between causality
and blame" — a death at a mother's own hands, under these conditions, need not entail moral blame. All of
its claims are recorded in attributed voice.
