# Restitution (of Antiquities)

**Restitution** is the return of a looted, stolen, or illegally exported antiquity to its
country or community of origin. In the antiquities-trade literature it is distinguished
from **repatriation** of colonial-era and human remains (see
[[repatriation-of-anthropology]]) by its focus on **recently** trafficked material and on
[[cultural-property]] law. [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s
*[[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Loot, Legitimacy and Ownership]]* (2000) treats restitution
as important but **secondary** to his real target — stopping present-day looting.

## Definition as Coined

Restitution operates on objects already removed: a source state or community seeks return
of an item held by a museum, dealer, or private collector. Renfrew (attributed) is explicit
that his book is "**not about repatriation undertaken in restitution of earlier wrongs**"
(the Parthenon Marbles, Pergamum Altar, Venus de Milo — whose claims he grants "undoubted
merits") but about halting the looting that continues now. He takes **1970** as the working
line dividing the historic collections of the great museums from the contemporary illicit
trade.

## Semantic History

- **Post-war origins:** "restitution" enters heritage vocabulary via the return of art
  looted in the Second World War, then the decolonization-era return of objects taken under
  colonial rule.
- **1970 onward:** the [[cultural-property]] conventions reframe restitution around the
  *recently* trafficked object; source states (Turkey, Greece, Italy) use restitution suits
  strategically to raise due-diligence standards.
- **Terminological split:** in current usage **restitution** tends to denote return of
  illicitly-traded antiquities, while **repatriation** denotes return of human remains and
  colonial-era collections (see [[repatriation-of-anthropology]]) — though the words are
  often used interchangeably.
- **Renfrew's narrowing (2000):** he deliberately brackets historic-wrong repatriation to
  keep the focus on deterring present looting — restitution as prevention, not redress.

## Operationalizations

Restitution is operationalized through distinct legal-institutional routes, which differ in
who initiates and who bears cost:

| Mode | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Court-ordered / settled | Source-state suit; due-diligence or title ruling | Lydian Hoard (Turkey v. Met, 1987→settled) |
| Charity-mediated | Object assigned to a charity, then returned | Aidonia Treasure → Greece |
| Voluntary institutional | Holder returns without a formal request | Getty returns to Italy; British Museum, Salisbury Hoard |
| Refused despite proof | Holder contests the claim | Weary Herakles (Boston MFA/White–Levy) |

Underlying instruments: [[cultural-property]] law (1970 UNESCO; 1995 Unidroit's "stolen"
definition), bilateral MOUs, and the [[unprovenanced-antiquities]] presumption that defeats
a good-faith purchaser's title.

## The Deterrence Rationale (attributed)

The distinctive archaeological argument Renfrew endorses (following **Ricardo Elia**):
the chief value of restitution is **not recovery of lost knowledge** — context destroyed by
looting cannot be restored — but **market suppression and deterrence of future looting**.
Return the object and you weaken the incentive to dig the next one; you do not undo the
epistemic loss already suffered. This reframes restitution from a property remedy into a
**preventive** instrument.

## Documented Cases (attributed — Renfrew 2000)

| Object | Origin | Holder | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Lydian Hoard / Treasure | Uşak, Turkey (looted mid-1960s) | Metropolitan Museum (acq. 1966–70) | Turkey sued 1987; due-diligence ruling; **returned** |
| Aidonia Treasure | Greece (Mycenaean) | dealer Michael Ward (1993 sale) | Greek suit; assigned to a US charity, **returned** to Greece after 2 yrs |
| Weary Herakles | Perge, Turkey | Boston MFA / White–Levy (upper half) | Casts matched Antalya lower half (1992); return **refused** |
| Salisbury Hoard shields | Wiltshire, England | British Museum (bought from Lord McAlpine) | Returned to rightful landowner once looting uncovered |

Restitution here is variously **court-ordered/settled** (Lydian, Aidonia), **refused despite
proof** (Weary Herakles), or **voluntary** (the Getty's unilateral returns to Italy;
British Museum's Salisbury return). Turkey and Greece are shown using restitution suits to
raise international standards of due diligence.

## Applications and Limits

- A lever of [[cultural-property]] law, powered by the [[unprovenanced-antiquities]]
  presumption and Unidroit's "stolen" definition.
- **Limits (Renfrew's own caveats):** restitution recovers the object, never the context;
  it is post hoc, reaching only cases that surface; and "the final destination of the
  material is ultimately a secondary consideration" beside stopping the looting.
- Critics note restitution can reward the trade — Elia questions US tax relief for
  "charitable donation" of looted material back through a dealer.

## Critiques and Debates

The nationalism-vs-internationalism dispute over *where* antiquities belong is filed at
[[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]] and framed on [[cultural-property]]; the demand-side
looting thesis at [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]. Distinguish this antiquities sense
