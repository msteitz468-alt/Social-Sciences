## Definition as Coined

The "antiquity of man" names the mid-Victorian recognition — what George W. Stocking, Jr. (*Victorian Anthropology*, 1987) calls "the revolution in human time" — that humanity had existed for a far greater span than biblical chronology (roughly 6,000 years) allowed, coeval with extinct Pleistocene fauna. Stocking frames 1858 as the pivotal year of this "independent intellectual revolution," parallel to and intertwined with the Darwinian revolution of 1859 (attributed, Stocking).

## Semantic History

Evidence for prehistoric human artifacts alongside extinct-animal remains had circulated well before 1858 without dislodging orthodoxy: John Frere reported flint tools to the Society of Antiquaries as early as 1797, with further finds in Britain, Belgium, and France around 1830, but leading geologists resisted the implications and held to the recency of man (finding). The Catholic priest John McEnery, excavating at Kent's Hole (Devonshire), was, per Stocking, so deferential to geological orthodoxy that he interpreted flints found with extinct animals as deposited after the biblical Flood rather than as evidence of antediluvian man (finding). Charles Lyell himself resisted Philippe-Charles Schmerling's 1833 Liège discoveries of human remains with extinct species, and as late as 1855 still held that "we have every reason to infer that the human race is extremely modern" (quoted in Stocking; position). Jacques Boucher de Perthes's 1847 publication on Somme-valley (Abbéville) prehistoric finds was rejected by French geologists as amateurish and made little impression when sent to the British Archaeological Association in 1849 (finding).

The turning point came in a cluster of finds and reassessments across 1857–59. In 1857, Neanderthal remains were discovered in a limestone cave in Rhenish Prussia, and the unusually dry winter of 1853–54 had already exposed prehistoric Swiss lake dwellings (finding). In 1858, the opening of Brixham Cave near Kent's Hole, investigated under the careful supervision of a Geological Society of London committee, produced human artifacts in situ with extinct-animal bones under controlled excavation conditions that made rejecting the evidence untenable — and this in turn reopened credibility for Boucher de Perthes's earlier Abbéville findings (finding). John Evans, reporting to the Society of Antiquaries in 1859 after an Easter visit to Abbéville, concluded it was now beyond doubt that the region had been peopled by man "in a period of antiquity, remote beyond any of which we have hitherto found trace" (quoted in Stocking; attributed). Lyell announced his own conversion to the deep-antiquity view at the 1859 Aberdeen meeting of the British Association, by which point, in Stocking's assessment, "the revolution in archeology was well-nigh accomplished" (attributed, Stocking). Lyell consolidated the case in *The Antiquity of Man* (1863), which merged the archaeological "revolution in human time" with the broader Darwinian revolution — though Lyell himself could argue for deep human antiquity without fully committing to a Darwinian account of human development (finding).

Stocking argues the archaeological revolution supplied two essential preconditions for a Darwinian view of man: it bridged the temporal discontinuity paleontology had assumed between man and earlier animal forms, and it supplied the expanded time frame needed to make the gradual evolution of a culture-bearing animal from an apelike creature seem plausible (attributed, Stocking). He treats William Whewell's Bridgewater-Treatise-era defense of miracles — insisting on an unbridgeable discontinuity between ape and man requiring supernatural causation — as diagnostic of the pre-1858 framework that the antiquity-of-man revolution and Darwinism together undermined (attributed, Stocking). At the same time, establishing human antiquity was methodologically double-edged for Darwinism: it supplied the necessary time for gradual development but also opened a widening evidentiary "gap" that had to be filled without invoking divine assistance, since prehistoric archaeology itself said little about the origin of specifically human mental and cultural capacities — "having got man back to the pliocene, or even the miocene, it was necessary to get him out, without asking or allowing any assistance from his erstwhile creator" (quoted in Stocking; attributed). For [[ethnology]], the 1858–59 revolution cut two ways: it undercut ethnology's historical/philological argument for tracing all human migration to a single recent origin point, even as it strengthened the biological case for monogenism by allowing enough time for observed racial differences to have developed (attributed, Stocking). The revolution also sharpened a decades-long scholarly dispute over whether the Aryans (see comparative philology under [[ethnology]]) originated in Asia or Europe, into which Robert Gordon Latham's *Comparative Philology* (1862) intervened, arguing — against prevailing Asian-origin orthodoxy and to the detriment of his own reputation — for a European origin (position).

## Operationalizations

Established through stratigraphic association of worked flint tools with extinct-fauna remains under controlled excavation (Brixham Cave, 1858), corroborating unstratified or contested prior finds (Abbéville, Kent's Hole); no absolute (radiometric) dating method was yet available — chronology rested on faunal and geological stratigraphy alone.

## Applications

Anchors the reconstructed chronology used by [[social-evolutionism]] to place "savage," "barbarous," and "civilized" stages in deep time rather than merely in geographic sequence; underlies the shift Stocking identifies from ethnology's problem of human unity-in-space to anthropology's problem of the origin of civilization understood as development-in-time (see [[civilization]]). In *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|Descent of Man]]* Darwin takes high antiquity as already demonstrated (Boucher de Perthes; Lyell; [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]]) — “the indispensable basis for understanding his origin” — and does not re-argue it.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Victorian antiquity-of-man revolution rested entirely on relative (stratigraphic/faunal) dating with no absolute chronology, leaving actual time-depths (pliocene, miocene) speculative even as the qualitative fact of deep antiquity became secure. Stocking's account also stresses how contested and resisted the evidence was for six decades (1797–1858) before acceptance, cautioning against reading 1858 as a sudden discovery rather than the tipping point of a long-accumulating, repeatedly rejected evidentiary record.

## Lubbock, *Pre-Historic Times* (1865/1872)

*(Attributed — Lubbock.)* *[[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872|Pre-Historic Times]]* is a major English synthesis arguing the deep antiquity case after the 1858–63 tipping point: Quaternary fauna coeval with humans; cave and river-drift (Somme) industries; rejection of Ussher-scale chronology via geology, deltas, and climate/astronomical arguments (Croll et al. as he reports them). He organizes the material into [[palaeolithic]] vs [[neolithic]] vs Bronze vs Iron ([[three-age-system]]), making deep time a **periodized archaeological narrative** rather than only a geological fact. Ethnographic chapters treat living metal-less peoples as analogical evidence for prehistoric conditions — method, not absolute dating.
