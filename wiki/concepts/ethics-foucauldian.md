# Ethics (Foucauldian) — Moral Subjectivation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984), [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] draws a distinction (attributed) that governs his late work: within any **morality** one must separate the **moral code** (the prescribed rules and their enforcing authorities), the **morality of behaviors** (actual conduct measured against the code), and **ethics** proper — "the manner in which one ought to 'conduct oneself'," i.e. how one constitutes oneself as an **ethical subject** in relation to the code. "A rule of conduct is one thing; the conduct that may be measured by this rule is another. But another thing still is the manner in which one ought to 'conduct oneself'." Ethics, in this sense, is the relation of the self to itself — the object of a distinct "history of ethics/ascetics" alongside a history of codes and a history of behaviors.

## Semantic History

Foucault narrows the ordinary word "ethics" to this technical sense: **subjectivation**, the self-forming side of morality. The move lets him reframe the standard question of continuity or break between pagan and Christian morality. Rather than asking what code-elements Christianity borrowed or added, he asks "how… the forms of self-relationship… were defined, modified, recast, and diversified" — observing (attributed) that moral **codes** across the long pagan-to-Christian span "revolve around a rather small number of rather simple principles" and are comparatively stable, whereas the real "field of historicity" lies in the modes of ethical **subjectivation**. This inverts the usual emphasis of moral history from rules to self-fashioning, and connects directly to the [[arts-of-existence|arts of existence]].

## Operationalizations — the four aspects

Foucault specifies **four dimensions** along which ways of "conducting oneself morally" differ (attributed; illustrated throughout with conjugal fidelity):
1. **Ethical substance** — "the way in which the individual has to constitute this or that part of himself as the prime material of his moral conduct." (For the Greeks: the [[aphrodisia|acts and their pleasures]]; for Christianity: desire/the flesh.)
2. **Mode of subjection** (*mode d'assujettissement*) — "the way in which the individual establishes his relation to the rule and recognizes himself as obliged to put it into practice" (belonging to a group; heir to a tradition; answering an appeal to give one's life a beautiful form).
3. **Self-forming activity** (*travail éthique*, ascesis) — "the forms of elaboration… that one performs on oneself… to attempt to transform oneself into the ethical subject of one's behavior" (memorized precepts and self-examination; sudden renunciation; relentless combat; continuous decipherment of desire). For the Greeks this is [[enkrateia|self-mastery]].
4. **Telos** — the mode of being the ethical subject aims at (self-mastery; tranquility of soul; purification ensuring salvation).

"No moral conduct… does not call for the forming of oneself as an ethical subject; and no forming of the ethical subject without 'modes of subjectivation' and an 'ascetics' or 'practices of the self' that support them."

## Applications — code-oriented vs. ethics-oriented moralities, and the Greek case

Every morality combines code and subjectivation, but the two can be weighted differently. A **code-oriented** morality emphasizes the code and its enforcing authorities, so that "subjectivation occurs basically in a quasi-juridical form" (Foucault tentatively links this juridifying tendency to the medieval penitential system). An **ethics-oriented** morality emphasizes the forms of subjectivation and practices of the self, the code itself remaining "rather rudimentary." His **central thesis**: Greek and Greco-Roman moral thought was "much more oriented toward practices of the self and the question of *askēsis* than toward codifications of conducts." The four-aspect grid then structures the volume's substantive readings — the [[dietetics|dietetic]] regimen of the body, the *economics* of marriage (the husband's self-delimiting domination, not a reciprocal fidelity), and the *erotics* of the love of boys (the "antinomy of the boy": the only honorable male object of pleasure, yet unable to embrace the passive role without endangering his future status as a free citizen). The grid has since become a widely used template for empirical studies of ethical self-formation across the social sciences.

## Extension in the imperial period (*The Care of the Self*)

*[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, 1984) runs the same grid forward into the first two centuries CE, where the mode of subjection shifts (attributed). Foucault reads a **new conjugal ethic** in Musonius Rufus, Hierocles, Plutarch, and Seneca — marriage reconceived as a bond "dual in its form, universal in its value, and specific in its intensity," and a **"conjugalization of the aphrodisia"** in which the husband's restraint is now justified "by the nature of the relationship, its universal form and the mutual obligations that derive from it" rather than by status and the demonstration of rule. He insists this remains **a stylistics of the bond, not a juridical code**: "what is proposed is not a regulation that would draw a division between permitted and forbidden acts… It is instead a mode of being, a style of relations." In parallel the love of boys undergoes a **deproblematization** (Plutarch's *Dialogue on Love*, ps.-Lucian's *Amores*), displaced by a "new erotics" of the reciprocal couple. The volume's Conclusion states the load-bearing thesis for this concept: a **continuity of themes** across the pagan/Christian divide masks a **discontinuity of the mode of subjectivation** — the same rules would later be re-founded on finitude and the Fall, obedience to divine law, a purificatory hermeneutics of desire, and self-renunciation. See [[care-of-the-self]] and [[cultivation-of-the-self]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The four-aspect grid is Foucault's analytic construction, not a Greek scheme; its clean symmetry is his, and is attributed. Critics ask whether the sharp **code vs. ethics** opposition understates how much codification the Greeks did practice (attributed).
- The reframing risks aestheticizing morality; Foucault concedes the Greek "aesthetics of existence" "rested on a very harsh system of inequalities and constraints (particularly in connection with women and slaves)."
- Whether this ethics of self-formation supplies a **normative** standpoint (a basis for critique) or only a descriptive typology is contested in the reception (attributed), and connects to the longstanding "no exit"/agency charge against Foucault's [[analytics-of-power|analytics of power]].
