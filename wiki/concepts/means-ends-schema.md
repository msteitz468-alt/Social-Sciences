# Means-End Schema

The **means-end schema** is the relational core of the [[unit-act|unit act]] in
[[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]]'s [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic theory of
action]]: the structure by which an actor selects means, under given conditions, toward ends, judged
by the norm of the intrinsic (rational) means-end relationship. Attributed to Parsons's reconstruction
in *[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure of Social Action]]* (1937).

## Definition as Coined

Rational action, in Parsons's analysis, involves "the fundamental elements of 'ends,' 'means' and
'conditions' of rational action and the norm of the intrinsic means-end relationship," with
rationality measured by the conformity of means-choice to scientifically warranted expectations.
Parsons noted the same underlying structure runs, in different vocabularies, through his four writers:
what Pareto called "logical" action, Weber called "rational" (*zweckrational*) action — differences of
terminology, not substance.

## Semantic History

Parsons inherited the schema from the [[utilitarianism|utilitarian]] tradition, where it was central
to Marshall's marginal-utility theory, but argued utilitarianism could not sustain it without an
extrascientific postulate (the natural identity of interests) — the "utilitarian dilemma." On
Parsons's reading, the intermediate means-end sector differentiates into three subsectors on the
principle of an act's progressively broader relations to the rest of the action system:

- **technological** — the intrinsic means-end relation of the isolated act;
- **economic** — allocation of scarce means among a plurality of ends;
- **political / coercive-power** — the use of others' action as means, where the concept of authority
  and the [[problem-of-order|problem of order]] enter.

Parsons argued Marshall fused these together under "activities" and thereby "suppressed the element of
coercive power entirely," whereas Pareto and Weber differentiated them more clearly.

## Operationalizations

Economic rationality is an **emergent property** of the schema — meaningless for a single unit act,
since it presupposes a plurality of ends among which scarce means are allocated. Parsons tied the
schema to a tentative "law of increasing rationality" (analogized to entropy) that he identified with
Weber's [[rationalization]].

## Applications

The analytical spine of Parsons's action theory and the bridge to economic theory; underlies the
subsector analysis of Pareto and the [[voluntaristic-theory-of-action]]'s tripartite structure.

## Critiques and Limitations

As with the [[unit-act]], critics note the schema builds a rationalist, individual-actor starting
point into the theory. Its explanatory (not merely descriptive) status rests on [[analytical-realism]].
