# Social Skill

*(Attributed — Fligstein; Fligstein & McAdam 2012.)* **Social skill** is the ability to induce cooperation by appealing to and helping to create shared meanings and collective identities. Skilled social actors empathetically relate to others' situations and provide reasons to cooperate; they "read" multiple conceptions of interest and identity inside and outside their group and frame action accordingly.

## Definition as Coined

In *[[fligstein-mcadam-theory-of-fields-2012|A Theory of Fields]]* (esp. Ch. 2), social skill is the complex of cognitive, affective, and linguistic facilities that make actors more or less effective as strategic actors in [[strategic-action-field|fields]]. It is rooted in symbolic interactionism ([[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]): selves are shaped in interaction; skilled actors produce meaning for others and thereby for themselves. Skilled actors keep goals somewhat open-ended and "take what the system will give," in contrast to ideal-type rational actors with fixed preferences. All humans need some skill to survive; some are more skilled than others. Use of skill depends on **position** as well as personal capacity — incumbents sustain group identity and advantages; challengers sustain opposition and exploit openings.

## Semantic History

- Fligstein's earlier social-skill papers supply the microfoundation later built into SAF theory with McAdam.
- Related language: institutional entrepreneurship, framing (Snow et al.), charismatic or skilled leadership in movements — SAF theory generalizes these as ordinary field processes, not only "unsettled times."
- Not the everyday English "social skills" of etiquette manuals; technical term for strategic meaning-making capacity.

## Operationalizations

Hard to measure directly; authors use historical reconstruction of who assembled coalitions, framed identities, and brokered settlements. Method chapter discusses entrepreneurship and social skill under different philosophies of science.

## Applications

Field emergence (who forges a new order); reproduction (who holds coalitions together); crisis (who re-settles). Civil rights and mortgage cases illustrate skilled and failed strategic action under shifting exogenous conditions.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of post-hoc attribution ("winners were skilled"). Tension with structural accounts that deny differential skill. Authors insist skill never floats free of resource endowments and field position. Methodologically they treat "entrepreneur" as a **role** conditioned by field state (emergence / reproduction / crisis), not a fixed personality type (*Theory of Fields* Ch. 6).
