# Split Inheritance

## Definition as Coined

**Split inheritance** is the modern analytic (etic) label for a royal succession convention that [[daltroy-terence-n|Terence N. D'Altroy]] (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002) reports as central to Cuzco politics. As of D'Altroy 2002, in theory each royal succession created a new [[panaqa|panaqa]] (also spelled *panaca*): the “most able” son of the deceased ruler became the new sovereign, while his other descendants became custodians of the dead ruler’s properties, usually under a brother’s leadership. A *panaqa*’s duties included perpetual veneration of its ancestor and care for his assets through a cult founded around his mummy. The office and public state resources thus “split” from the deceased monarch’s private estate: the new ruler inherited command, not the bulk of his father’s personal holdings.

D'Altroy situates the convention within a broader political synthesis: stripped to essentials, Inca government was a monarchy with father-to-son succession, but an elaborate hierarchy fused Inca kinship, ancestor worship, ethnicity, and class; mummies of long-dead kings and queens and oracular idols participated in state affairs through cults staffed by descendants. From a sanctified plane, a dead emperor continued to participate in [[cuzco|Cuzco]]’s ceremonial and political life, with mummies customarily kept in town houses or estate sanctuaries.

## Semantic History

The label is etic; the institutions it packages—royal *panaqa*, mummy cult, estate tenure—are emic. D'Altroy reports that the **most widely accepted** scholarly view (as of 2002) is that a new *panaqa* was added every royal generation as a result of split inheritance. Competing models he records include: (a) the Betanzos tradition that all ten royal groups were already present from the pre-imperial era; (b) Sarmiento’s placement of Lower Cuzco kin under the first five rulers and Upper Cuzco under later kings (history written into hierarchy); (c) Pachakuti’s reorganization of existing groups into a dual design; and (d) Rowe’s (1985a) suggestion that one Upper Cuzco royal group may have been shifted to Lower Cuzco every two generations to balance moieties. D'Altroy himself is “most comfortable with the idea that kin groups were added or dislodged over the generations, and that stories of the Inca past were revised to rationalize the organization as it existed at any point,” treating the Spanish-described arrangement as a “snapshot” frozen by invasion.

In expansion theory, Conrad and Demarest (1984) applied the concept as a **driver of imperial growth**: the new emperor inherits office and state resources while the deceased’s personal resources stay with kin for perpetual veneration, forcing each new emperor to expand as near-Cuzco resources exhaust. D'Altroy explicitly rejects that causal package for imperial origins (see Critiques).

## Operationalizations

**Succession rule.** D'Altroy reports Andean succession (after Rostworowski) typically favored vigorous actors—“most able” son regardless of birth order, or successive able brothers—rather than European-style primogeniture, which Spaniards later misread as the only legitimate path.

**Estate economics.** As of D'Altroy 2002, a deceased ruler’s estates were normally left to his *panaqa*; under split inheritance the throne-passer had to develop his own kin group’s resources. The *qoya*’s lands were held separately and left to her own relatives at death. Parcels of rulers, aristocrats, and communities intermingled; Colonial litigation and personal choice (including skipped generations) complicate ownership. Every province set aside lands for each ruler; the most elegant estates lay in a ~100 km Vilcanota/Urubamba stretch between Pisac and Machu Picchu (the “Sacred Valley”), including sites such as [[ollantaytambo|Ollantaytambo]] and [[machu-picchu|Machu Picchu]]. Estates supported deified stature, descendants’ sustenance and wealth, and political and ceremonial activity.

**Matrilateral affiliation.** Sitting rulers, D'Altroy reports, did not belong to their father’s kin group; they founded their own *panaqa* and identified closely with their mother’s—making women’s lineage instrumental in succession politics and alliance.

**Land constraint.** Some estates were created by formidable engineering (e.g., Wayna Qhapaq’s Yucay swamp reclamation; Waskhar’s Pomabamba river diversion) as best fields were spoken for. Waskhar, citing Pedro Pizarro, groused that the dead “had all that was the best of his kingdom” and proposed abolishing or confiscating familial and Sun estates—poorly received because it threatened the whole privileged-wealth system.

## Applications

- **Cuzco political structure:** *Panaqa* as advisory force, succession brokers, and estate corporations under split inheritance ([[panaqa]], [[cuzco]]).
- **Royal estates and class concentration:** Prime resources converted to private domains held first by the living ruler, then nominally in trust for his reverence after death; on an empire-wide scale resources committed were not especially great, but manors show an endogamous upper class concentrating power at the empire’s heart (D'Altroy’s synthesis).
- **Dynastic war:** Waskhar’s threats to bury mummies and alienate royal/religious properties, and the later destruction of Thupa Inka Yupanki’s mummy, as maximum violence against the ancestor-property system that split inheritance presupposed.
- **Expansion explanations:** Position in Conrad–Demarest vs. D'Altroy’s counter-argument (see Critiques); late campaigns partly readable as plays to royal politics and succession instability.

## Critiques and Limitations

**As imperial-origin engine.** D'Altroy finds the Conrad–Demarest expansion argument unpersuasive (as of 2002): Pachakuti and Thupa Inka Yupanki annexed virtually the entire empire according to the sources used; Wayna Qhapaq and Waskhar still claimed near-Cuzco estates despite ostensible shortages; Andean transport precluded long-distance food for daily subsistence. What chronicles describe as resource shortage is “more plausibly explained as jealousy over royal estates… The situation may well have been cause for political infighting, but it does not work well in explaining imperial origins.”

**Static property model.** Archaeology challenges inviolate *panaqa* control: at Ollantaytambo, D'Altroy reports (Hollowell/Protzen), both Pachakuti and Thupa ordered important buildings; multiple imperial construction phases and stone types show estate design was not inviolate even for the founding emperor’s *panaqa*.

**Formation models remain plural.** Generative split-inheritance *panaqa* hierarchy is the modal scholarly view D'Altroy records, not a wiki-voice fact; Betanzos, Sarmiento, dual reorganization, and diarchy readings compete ([[dual-organization]]).

**Competition costs.** D'Altroy argues competition woven into Inca politics virtually guaranteed periodic Cuzco upheavals—useful for expansion-era selection of effective leaders, ruinous in an established empire (final dynastic war). Full-sister marriage and power consolidation had only modest effect in limiting the claimant pool.
