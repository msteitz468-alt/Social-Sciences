## Definition as Coined

Presentism and historicism name two opposed stances a historian of a discipline can take toward its past. Presentism judges and organizes past thought by the interests and categories of the present discipline — reading history backward from where the field ended up, treating earlier figures chiefly as "precursors" of later, validated positions. Historicism instead privileges the actors' own questions, categories, and context, reconstructing what a past thinker's ideas meant and did within their own historical moment, independent of later disciplinary outcomes. George W. Stocking, Jr. frames this distinction as his own explicit methodological self-consciousness in *Victorian Anthropology* (1987): understanding Victorian sociocultural evolutionism requires examining the early-nineteenth-century "dark age" usually skipped over by anthropologists seeking Enlightenment ancestors, and warns against a presentist historiography that reads directly from Enlightenment "precursors" to 1860s evolutionism while ignoring the intervening intellectual currents (attributed, Stocking): "While the Enlightenment has traditionally been a fertile source of ancestors for anthropologists seeking to anchor their own ideas more deeply in time, the early nineteenth century has been a kind of 'dark age,' barren of theoretical interest" (quoted in Stocking).

## Semantic History

Stocking treats the distinction as itself historically embedded: he reads the German idealist *Natur-/Geisteswissenschaften* split, William Whewell's category of "palaetiological sciences," and J. S. Mill's *Logic of the Moral Sciences* as successive, period-specific attempts to resolve the same underlying methodological problem — whether human history could be assimilated to natural science — and insists each attempt must be read in its own historical context rather than projected backward or forward onto the others (attributed, Stocking). Stocking also acknowledges a reflexive limit on the historicist ideal: the very decision to study Victorian anthropologists' thinking in the first place reflects the historian's own present interests, "whether these be 'historicist' or 'presentist' in motivation" (attributed, Stocking) — i.e., historicist method cannot fully escape the presentist fact of its own selection and framing.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; functions in Stocking's work as a methodological self-audit applied to each chapter of disciplinary history — e.g., resisting the temptation to read Prichard (see [[prichard-james-cowles]]) as a proto-Darwinian rather than as an outgrowth of biblical anthropology in his own period's terms (attributed, Stocking), and resisting the temptation to treat "ethnology" (see [[ethnology]]) as a failed early draft of "anthropology" rather than as a coherent paradigm in its own right.

## Applications

Programmatic organizing principle of *Victorian Anthropology* as a whole: applied to the reading of Prichard's ethnology, the early-nineteenth-century "hiatus" between Enlightenment progressivism and 1860s evolutionism, and the shifting Victorian content of "civilization" (see [[civilization]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Stocking's own text registers the internal tension of the pair: pure historicism is an ideal rather than a fully achievable stance, since the historian's questions are always shaped by present disciplinary concerns. The distinction is offered as a working discipline for the historian of anthropology rather than as a resolved epistemological doctrine.
