# Extensification and Intensification (Mintz)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985), [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] names two processes by which inside meanings of foods are acquired as consumption spreads:

**Intensification** — consumption replicates, imitates, or emulates that of others, usually of higher social status; ceremonial attachment (e.g., wedding cake with sculptured sugar) percolates down society. Those in power are responsible both for the presence of the new products and, to a degree, for their meanings.

**Extensification** — larger numbers become familiar with a substance on a regular basis; glamorous-luxury status declines; *and*, especially among the British working classes, new uses and meanings arise without imitation, "at a great remove from the practices of the privileged," often because sugar products mattered more as calories than as status. Those in power may control availability, but new users inform products with meaning.

Mintz stresses that in sugar's national diffusion, controllers also shaped *some* meanings; the conjuncture of downward intensification and independent invention reveals power's relation to inside meaning. Classes did not experience the two as separate processes; the distinction is analytic.

## Semantic History

Mintz also uses "extensification"/"intensification" for ritualization of sugar in the consumption chapter: extensification as regular everyday familiarity detaching meanings from past elites; intensification as continuity with ceremonial meanings (holiday sweets, funeral sweets, sweetness imagery) even when the original political content of court ritual does not spread. The pair has traveled into food studies as a portable model of mass adoption. Not to be confused with agricultural extensification/intensification (land-use jargon) or with [[intensifiers]] as Mintz's drug-food category.

## Operationalizations

Historical tracking of: who consumes; in what combinations (tea+sugar); at what meals and rites; whether use is emulative of elite models or innovated under different constraints (factory time, fuel cost, women's wage labor).

## Applications

British sugar 17th–20th c.; wedding cakes; jam after free-trade cheapening (~1870 working-class necessity); tea as work break vs middle-class "the tea."

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary between emulation and innovation is porous; later researchers may re-specify by region, gender, and commodity. Mintz's own text notes French and other European paths differ.
