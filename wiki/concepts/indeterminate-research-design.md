## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) hold that a research design is indeterminate when
"virtually nothing can be learned about the causal hypotheses," treating this as a more
severe failure than violations of [[conditional-independence]] or [[unit-homogeneity]]:
"A determinate research design is the sine qua non of causal inference"
([[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994]]).

## Semantic History

KKV specify exactly two situations that render a design indeterminate: "1. We have more
inferences to make than implications observed. 2. We have two or more explanatory
variables in our data that are perfectly correlated with each other—in statistical
terms, this is the problem of [[multicollinearity]]." The first condition follows from
their premise that each observation can support at most one causal inference: "each
observation can help us make one inference at most; n observations will help us make
fewer than n inferences if the observations are not independent." They illustrate this
with a three-case study carrying seven candidate explanatory variables: "With seven
causal variables and only three observations, the research design cannot determine
which of the hypotheses, if any, is correct."

## Operationalizations

KKV insist that no amount of qualitative depth substitutes for a determinate design:
"No amount of description, regardless of how thick and detailed... can extract much
about any of the causal hypotheses with an indeterminate research design." The
prescribed remedy is disaggregation — increasing the effective number of
[[observable-implications]] (see [[research-design]]) even without collecting new data,
by treating a single case as a source of multiple observations at finer levels of
analysis. KKV caution against a narrower failure mode within case selection: choosing
cases where the dependent and explanatory variables both covary in a
theory-confirming pattern "can describe or explain nothing" (a joint-selection version
of [[selection-bias]]). They permit a narrow exception for mixed sampling on rare or
skewed marginal distributions (not joint selection on both variables), citing Kohli's
(1987) three-state study of Indian antipoverty policy as a case that was indeterminate
at the state level and was redeemed only by disaggregating to many sub-observations.

## Applications

The concept is applied in KKV's chapter 4 treatment of case and observation selection,
alongside worked examples including Skocpol on revolutions, Porter on competitive
advantage, Tilly on state survival, Laitin on Somalia/Yoruba, Achen and Snidal on
deterrence, Inkeles and Rossi on industrialization, and CDC cancer-cluster studies. KKV
also apply the concept critically to the "method of structured, focused comparison,"
crediting it as useful for hypothesis generation but denying it "provide[s] a
methodology for causal inference" whenever the resulting design carries more variables
than observations.

## Critiques and Limitations

The "sine qua non" framing generalizes a regression-derived diagnostic (insufficient
degrees of freedom, perfect collinearity) to qualitative case-study research broadly;
this extension is part of what is contested in the
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] and the
[[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]], where critics argue that case-based
inference (e.g., [[process-tracing]]) can generate warranted causal knowledge by a
different logic than the observations-versus-inferences accounting KKV propose.
