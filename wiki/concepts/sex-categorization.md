# Sex Categorization

**Sex categorization** is the (largely automatic) process of classifying a person as male or female for the purpose of organizing social relations. Central mechanism in [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Ridgeway]]'s [[primary-frame]] account: without sex categorization, routine coordination stalls.

## Definition as Coined

Ridgeway treats sex categorization as socially constructed in its criteria and displays yet nearly unavoidable in practice: people find it extremely difficult to complete ordinary exchanges with someone they cannot place as male or female. Categorization primes cultural gender beliefs that then shape judgment and behavior to varying degrees by context.

## Semantic History

Related to but not identical with West & Zimmerman's **[[sex-category]]** (achieved membership sustained by identificatory displays within the [[doing-gender]] program). Ridgeway's usage is social-cognitive / coordination-focused: the act of placing the other, and its framing consequences. Kessler & McKenna's gender attribution and ethnomethodological work on categorization are adjacent traditions.

## Operationalizations

Person-perception and automaticity research; experimental and observational evidence that gender stereotypes are primed once sex is categorized; contextual factors that intensify or reduce use (motivation, goals, anonymity).

## Applications

Workplace applicant/employer evaluation, leadership, household negotiation, innovation-site interaction — any setting where people must coordinate under uncertainty (*[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* Ch 2–5).

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary categorization language in the 2011 text reflects the cultural frame Ridgeway analyzes; later gender scholarship on nonbinary identities is outside this source's scope. Automaticity claims are research-program claims, not uncontested psychology consensus.
