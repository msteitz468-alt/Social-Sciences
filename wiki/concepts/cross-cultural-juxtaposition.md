# Cross-Cultural Juxtaposition

Second of two anthropological techniques of [[defamiliarization]] in Marcus and Fischer (*[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|ACC]]* Ch. 6). More empirical and dramatic than [[epistemological-critique]]: it matches **substantive facts about another culture** as a probe into **specific facts about a domestic subject of criticism**.

## Definition as Coined

Marcus and Fischer hold that **defamiliarization by cross-cultural juxtaposition** is the classic technique pioneered by [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]] (e.g., Samoan vs. American adolescence) and the most frequent demonstration of cultural relativism. They argue very little fully realized juxtaposition exists: typically either serious foreign ethnography is paired with impressionistic home knowledge (Mead), or serious domestic ethnography lacks strong foreign linkage (e.g., Warner's Yankee City only generally informed by Australian work), or parallel designs suppress defamiliarization (Whiting child-rearing studies). **Strong form:** dual ethnographic projects equally committed in their own contexts and equally engaged in cultural criticism.

## Semantic History

Names a long comparative practice; ACC specifies conditions for critical (not merely typological) use under the experimental moment.

## Operationalizations

Paired monographs; dual field sites; explicit critical linkage; contrast with controlled regional comparison and with pure epistemological revision without dual ethnography.

## Applications

Evaluates Mead-style public anthropology as incomplete dualism; sets a standard for repatriated cultural critique; connects to [[mead-freeman-debate]] only as representation controversy, not as ACC's main topic.

## Critiques and Limitations

Marcus and Fischer themselves mark the strong form as largely unmet; dualism can oversimplify the "third perspective" of multiple cultural references that enter real critical projects.
