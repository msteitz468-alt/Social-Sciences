# Lithic Technological Modes

## Definition as Coined

[[clark-grahame|J. G. D. Clark]] (1969) proposed a five-mode classification of stone-tool technology. [[phillipson-david-w|David W. Phillipson]] adopts the scheme throughout *African Archaeology* (2005) because it **avoids correlating technological stages with finite time periods** and artificial culture-historical compartments, while still allowing broad comparative trends (as of Phillipson 2005).

| Mode | Rough content (Phillipson’s use) | African exemplars (attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Simple flakes/cores; little standardisation | [[oldowan-industry]] |
| 2 | Bifaces/handaxes (and African cleavers) | [[acheulean-industry]]; late facies include [[sangoan-industry]] |
| 3 | Prepared-core (incl. Levallois-like) flake/blade production | Former “Middle Stone Age”; [[aterian-industry]]; early southern MSA |
| 4 | Prismatic blade industries (Upper Palaeolithic–type) | Patchy in Africa; more common in N Africa with fine isotropic stone |
| 5 | Microliths, often backed, for composite tools | Former “Late Stone Age”; includes trajectories through [[howiesons-poort-industry]] |

## Semantic History

Modes are **homotaxial** (recurrent order) world-wide but not watertight: earlier elements often continue alongside later ones. Phillipson notes limited adoption of the scheme partly because continuity with earlier modes is overlooked (as of Phillipson 2005). Parallel terminologies persist: North Africanists often use Middle/Upper Palaeolithic and Epipalaeolithic/Mesolithic labels for mode-3/4/5 horizons (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Operationalizations

Mode assignment is typological/technological, not chronological: a mode-5 industry in Africa may be tens of millennia older than a European Upper Palaeolithic assemblage with different mode profile (Phillipson’s Africa-priority argument for cultural modernity; as of Phillipson 2005).

## Applications

- Organising [[phillipson-african-archaeology-2005]]’s continent-scale narrative.
- Cross-regional comparison without forcing European period names onto sub-Saharan sequences.
- Linking industries ([[oldowan-industry]], [[acheulean-industry]], [[howiesons-poort-industry]], [[aterian-industry]]) to a shared metalanguage.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Modes describe **technology**, not societies, languages, or genes.
- Named industries within a mode remain fuzzy compartments on continuous variation (Phillipson’s own industry-construct critique).
- Mode 4’s African distribution is uneven and raw-material sensitive.
- Absolute dating still required; mode alone never dates a layer.
