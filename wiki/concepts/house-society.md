# House Society (société à maisons)

## Definition as Coined

Lévi-Strauss defined the house as "a corporate body holding an estate made up of both material and immaterial wealth, which perpetuates itself through the transmission of its name, its goods, and its titles down a real or imaginary line, considered legitimate as long as this continuity can express itself in the language of kinship or affinity and, most often, of both" (Lévi-Strauss 1983:174). The concept emerged from his reading of Boas's repeated failures to fit Kwakiutl kinship into unilineal or bilateral models — Boas finally settling on the Kwakiutl term [[numaym]], "a type of structure without equivalent in the archives of ethnology" — and from Kroeber's puzzlement at the Yurok, whose named, perpetual, locality-bound units Lévi-Strauss reinterpreted as "houses" (Lévi-Strauss 1983:170–72).

Lévi-Strauss extended the model to European medieval noble houses, Heian Japan, ancient Greece, Polynesia, Indonesia, Melanesia, and Africa, and framed house societies as transitional between kin-based and territory-based organization — a form arising "where political and economic interests, on the verge of invading the social field, have not yet overstepped the 'old ties of blood'... these interests must inevitably borrow the language of kinship... And inevitably too, they borrow it only to subvert it" (Lévi-Strauss 1983:186–87).

## Semantic History

From a solution to a Boasian classification problem (1970s–1983), the concept was taken up in the 1990s by Southeast Asianists and Americanists (Carsten and Hugh-Jones 1995; Waterson 1990, 1995) and then by archaeologists (Gillespie 2000a, 2000b; Joyce 2000; Tringham 2000; Kirch 2000), for whom its attraction is diachronic: the house institution outlasts any ethnographic-present snapshot, reflecting "the cumulative outcome of strategic choices made by generations" (Gillespie 2000a:11).

## Operationalizations

Gillespie (2000a:12–13) grounded archaeological visibility in the "objectification of perpetuity": narrative devices (origin myths, genealogies) and material practices — durable and elaborated construction, burial within structures, heirloom transmission, reincorporation of earlier architectural elements (Tringham's Opovo ovens), transformation of houses into temples (Kirch's Tikopia "holy houses" via Firth). Tringham (2000:130–31) arrayed European "open" Neolithic sites against Anatolian tells as two poles of a continuum of materially encoding continuity, neither inherently "more continuous."

## Applications

- **North Coast Peru (Moore 2005, attributed):** Chimú royal ciudadelas at [[chan-chan]] satisfy the three criteria — bounded space, corporate wealth (storage from ~110 to 7,620 m²), and objectified perpetuity (burial platforms, sequential additions, post-mortem transformation into ceremonial precincts) — while intermediate elite compounds are ambiguous and commoner barrios (and Moore's own Manchán excavations, where houses were demolished and rebuilt with no continuity) clearly fail the test. Moore read the Naymlap dynastic legend ([[sican-culture]]) as a house-society "social charter," with the palace of Cium buried, remembered, and transformed into a huaca. He cautioned: "'house' societies do not occur just because houses do."
- Waterson (1990) on named, perpetuated, ceremonially-focal Indonesian houses; Joyce (2000) on heirlooms and referential landscapes.

## Critiques and Limitations

Three definitional debates recorded (positions, not adjudicated):
1. **Stratification**: all of Lévi-Strauss's cases were ranked societies, but nothing in the definition requires it (Carsten and Hugh-Jones 1995:10–11); Waterson (1995:49) asks whether an unstratified house society could exist.
2. **The physical house**: the Moken sea nomads show house-society features anchored to islands of origin, not buildings (Waterson 1995:50) — the "house" need not be a structure.
3. **Dominance**: houses coexist with rival institutions (the medieval Church; Heian religious orders); "If we are to call these 'house societies,' that is certainly not all there is to be said about them" (Waterson 1995:49; cf. McKinnon 1995:172).

Lévi-Strauss's evolutionary "transitional" framing (kin → house → territory) is itself a theoretical claim carrying his broader assumptions and should not be asserted as a finding.
