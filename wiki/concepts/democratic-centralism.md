# Democratic Centralism

## Definition as Coined

**Democratic centralism** was the Bolshevik and later CPSU organisational doctrine combining formal electoral accountability of higher party organs to congresses with binding discipline and central direction. Mawdsley and White open their revolutionary-elite chapter with Lenin's 9th Congress (1920) gloss: representatives assemble and elect a responsible organ that must govern; democratic centralism "consists of the congress verifying the CC, removing it, and naming another."

## Semantic History

From factional organisational debates in Russian Social Democracy through Comintern template for communist parties worldwide; Soviet practice increasingly stressed **centralism** (apparatus, slate elections, limited write-ins) over competitive **democratic** content — a tension Mawdsley & White document in CC election procedures from the early 1920s onward.

## Operationalizations

Party rules on congress–CC–Politburo relations; voting procedures for CC lists; rights of plenums to remove members for discipline violations.

## Applications

Explains formal legitimacy of the CC as elite roster; frames Khrushchev-era "restoration of Leninist norms" and Gorbachev-era attempts to revitalise elective party bodies.

Mawdsley & White document the gap between formal congress→CC accountability and practice: printed slates with limited write-ins from the early 1920s; CC plenums decisive in **1957** (Anti-Party Group) and again under Gorbachev, yet never stably institutionalised against the top leadership; 1990 restructuring as "de-elitization" of the CC amid failed democratisation ([[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics treat it as ideological cover for oligarchy or personal dictatorship; defenders treat degeneration as historical betrayal of Leninist norms — record both as positions.

## Carr 1979 on faction ban and discipline

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Ch. 4) details the tenth congress (March 1921) resolutions "On the Syndicalist and Anarchist Deviation" and "On the Unity of the Party": complete abolition of fractionalism; platforms banned; unconditional obedience after decision; secret clause allowing expulsion of CC members by two-thirds vote. Lenin: "during a retreat discipline is a hundred times more necessary." Carr treats this as foundational for later central monopoly of power against the [[left-opposition]]. Complements Mawdsley & White on slate elections and CC practice.
