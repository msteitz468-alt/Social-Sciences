# Ideology

## Definition as Coined

"Ideology" is an old and heavily contested term (Destutt de Tracy’s “science of ideas”; Napoleon’s pejorative; Marx and Engels’s *German Ideology*; Mannheim’s [[sociology-of-knowledge]]; Parsons’s belief-system typology). This page records major sociological treatments side by side — **not** a single wiki-voice definition.

### Mannheim (1929/1936)

In *[[mannheim-ideology-and-utopia-1936|Ideology and Utopia]]*, [[mannheim-karl|Mannheim]] insists the word and problem **precede and exceed Marxism**, though Marxism contributed greatly to the original modern statement. He distinguishes:

| Conception | Meaning (attributed to Mannheim) |
|---|---|
| **Particular** | Scepticism toward an opponent’s ideas as more or less conscious disguises of a situation whose true recognition would not accord with his interests — continuum from calculated lie to unwitting self-deception; intermediate between simple lie and structural error |
| **Total** | Ideology of an age or historico-social group when concern is the **total structure of the mind** (Weltanschauung, conceptual apparatus) as outgrowth of collective life |
| **Special total** | Unmasks the adversary’s total viewpoint only |
| **General total** | Includes one’s own standpoint — the step into the [[sociology-of-knowledge]] |

Further axes: **non-evaluative** vs **evaluative** conceptions; **static** vs **dynamic** evaluative measures. Paired with [[utopia]]: both may transcend the situation; ideologies tend to stabilize the order or fail to transform it, utopias to shatter it when enacted. [[false-consciousness]] is reformulated evaluatively-dynamically (lag or leap relative to the present, not failure of a static absolute).

### Parsons (1951)

[[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] (*The Social System*, Ch. VIII) located ideology within a fourfold typology of belief systems, crossing empirical/non-empirical with existential/evaluative: **science** (empirical-existential), **philosophy** (non-empirical-existential), **ideology** (empirical-evaluative), and **religious ideas** (non-empirical-evaluative). He defined ideology as:

> a system of beliefs, held in common by the members of a collectivity … oriented to the evaluative integration of the collectivity, by interpretation of the empirical nature of the collectivity and of the situation in which it is placed, the processes by which it has developed … the goals to which its members are collectively oriented, and their relation to the future course of events.

To count as ideology (rather than mere science or instrumental belief) there must be **evaluative commitment institutionalized as part of the role of collectivity membership**. Ideology functions as the primary basis of **cognitive legitimation** of [[value-orientation]] patterns — it “rationalizes” why one pole of a pattern-variable dilemma is right.

### Comaroff & Comaroff (1991)

In *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]*, [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] define ideology (after Raymond Williams) as "an articulated system of meanings, values, and beliefs … [the] worldview of any social grouping" — the possession of a *particular* group, borne in manifestos, texts, and practices, and therefore contestable. Its analytic force comes from the **contrast with [[hegemony]]**: "Hegemony homogenizes, ideology articulates. Hegemony, at its most effective, is mute; … all the while, ideology babbles on." The two are interdependent — hegemony is the naturalized part of a dominant worldview that "no more appears as ideology at all"; the ideologies of the subordinate give voice to contradictions a prevailing hegemony can no longer conceal. This reworking, resting on Marx's two theories of ideology (see [[false-consciousness]]), is the framework of the [[colonization-of-consciousness]] thesis.

### Language ideology program (linguistic anthropology, 1979–2000)

A semi-independent track: **[[language-ideology|language / linguistic ideologies]]** as cultural representations of language structure and use, loaded with moral and political interests (Silverstein 1979; Irvine 1989; Irvine & Gal 2000; Kroskrity ed. *[[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|Regimes of Language]]* 2000). Not a simple application of Marx/Mannheim to language — emphasizes semiotic mediation ([[indexicality]]), multiplicity, partial awareness, and institutional [[language-regimentation|regimentation]]. Neutral vs critical readings of “ideology” treated as gradient within the program (Woolard & Schieffelin 1994, as cited by Kroskrity).

## Semantic History

- **Destutt de Tracy:** “science of ideas” (late 18th c.).
- **Napoleonic pejorative** and 19th-c. political usage (as Mannheim sketches).
- **Marxian:** ideology as class-bound distortion / superstructure (see [[historical-materialism]], [[false-consciousness]]).
- **Mannheim:** generalizes total ideology; opens SoK; pairs with [[utopia]]; develops [[relationism]].
- **Parsons:** functional-integrative belief system; cognitive distortion types (glossing malintegration; vulgarization; romantic/utopian over-idealization); counter-ideology of deviant sub-cultures vs reinterpretive deviant movements.
- **Postwar / constructionist:** Berger & Luckmann demote ideology analysis from exclusive SoK object in favour of everyday knowledge ([[social-construction-of-reality]]).
- **End-of-ideology (mid-1950s–60):** [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Ch XIII), with Bell, Shils, and Aron, argued that intense left–right doctrinal conflict among democratic parties in advanced industrial societies had declined to marginal disputes over state intervention — while [[democratic-class-struggle|class voting]] continued “without red flags” ([[end-of-ideology]]; [[end-of-ideology-debate]]). Period diagnosis, not wiki-voice law.

Parsons’s neutral, functional-integrative framing (ideology as legitimation of shared values) sits in explicit tension with the Marx–Mannheim tradition; on the Marxian reading the integrative frame is itself ideological — a tension recorded, not adjudicated. The end-of-ideology claim is a further mid-century position about *intensity and party doctrine*, not a resolution of the definitional dispute.

## Operationalizations

- Mannheim: historical-sociological typology and perspective imputation — not a scale.
- Parsons: typology of belief systems, not a measure.
- Later empirical ideology research (survey scaling; content analysis; left–right dimensions) largely outside both frameworks.

## Applications

- Mannheim: transition from ideology-critique to [[sociology-of-knowledge]]; scientific politics and styles of thought; unmasking and reciprocal unmasking.
- Parsons: institutionalization of science, ideology, philosophy, and religion; bridge between [[value-orientation]] and SoK vocabulary.
- Political sociology and media studies: mass belief systems (operational families often independent of both classics).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is intrinsically contested. Parsons’s integrative framing understates particular-interest and domination arguments; the Marx–Mannheim framing faces the reflexivity problem (is the critique itself ideological?) that Mannheim tries to answer via general total ideology and [[relationism]] ([[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]]). No definition commands consensus.

## Ideology in archaeology: past societies and present writing (attributed — Johnson 2011)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.6 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson reports a
neo-Marxist synthesis holding that ideology works through **three mechanisms**: it (1)
**naturalizes** existing inequality as timeless and inevitable; (2) **universalizes**
sectional (e.g. upper-class) interests as general interests; and (3) **masks** real
inequalities and conflict. On this account ideology sits atop the Marxist
"infrastructure" of forces and relations of production, as part of the political-legal
"superstructure" (see [[historical-materialism]]).

Johnson emphasizes that archaeologists study ideology in **two directions**: as a
phenomenon of past societies (e.g. legitimating elite power), and as operating in
**present archaeological writing itself**. He cites [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger's]]
work as showing how successive interpretations of North American prehistory
ideologically portrayed Native American cultures as "backward and unchanging" — an
application of the naturalizing/masking mechanisms above not to a past society's own
self-understanding, but to archaeology's own historiography. This second direction —
ideology critique turned on the discipline's own production of knowledge — is treated
by Johnson as a standing methodological lesson rather than a one-off case, and connects
to this wiki's broader Reflexivity requirement for discipline and study pages.

## Shanks & Tilley 1987 (archaeological critical theory)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]*, Ch. 3.)* Drawing on Althusser (1971), S&T define ideology as a form of **signifying practice** that constitutes subjects in specific ways and circumstances in order to **reproduce rather than transform** the social totality; ideology exists and has effectivity only through subjects; as a form of power it subjugates subjects. It is an imaginary relationship of people to their conditions of existence — not mere illusion or "false consciousness," but representations (discourses, images, myths, practices) concerning real relations; it naturalizes, appears obvious, and systematically suppresses the nature of its own construction in signifying practices. Material culture is a primary medium of such practices. They also turn ideology critique on archaeology itself: no discourse on the past is neutral; academic archaeology can reproduce relations of dominance (Ch. 7; museums, *National Geographic*, cultural capital). Links: [[material-culture]], [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

### *Re-Constructing Archaeology* densification (1987/1992)

*(Attributed — *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987]]*, Chs. 3–4, 7–8.)* **Value-freedom** is itself ideology: separating facts from values imports capitalist values into economic archaeology, systems theory (homeostasis as pathology), cultural evolution (politicized time), and mathematical archaeology. **Museum ideology:** reified objects, homogeneous history, high-culture *Homo Artifex*, heritage myth, hero-archaeologist spectacle — opposed by a [[redemptive-aesthetic]]. **Style as ideology (Ch. 7):** ceramic design as imaginary resolution of social contradiction (boundedness/non-closure) at Fjälkinge TRB tomb; three levels of art/style relation to social reality. **Modern design as ideology (Ch. 8):** beer-can graphics mediate consumption vs disciplinary control of drink in Britain and Sweden. Critical archaeology makes values explicit rather than claiming neutral method.

## Althusser, *For Marx* (1965) — lived relation, not Early-Works consciousness

*(Attributed — [[althusser-louis|Althusser]], *[[althusser-for-marx-1965|For Marx]]*, esp. “Marxism and Humanism” + Glossary.)* Ideology is the “lived” relation between men and their world, or a reflected form of this unconscious relation; it is distinguished from a science not by simple falsity (it can be coherent) but because the **practico-social predominates** over the theoretical. Historically it precedes the science produced by an [[epistemological-break]], and it **survives alongside science** as an essential element of every [[social-formation]] — including socialist and even communist society. Humanism is an ideological concept: it can designate real relations and serve as a practical slogan without providing knowledge of essences ([[theoretical-anti-humanism]]; [[marxist-humanism-debate]]). Ideology is profoundly **unconscious** — a structure imposed involuntarily — not “false consciousness” as a region of mere error. **Note:** the 1970 Ideological State Apparatuses / interpellation theory is **not** in *For Marx* and is not filed from this source.

## Reading the Past (2003) — Giddens's three functions, and "reading off" burial (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 4.)* Following [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (1979/1981), Hodder & Hutson report ideology functioning in three ways in Marxist archaeology: representing sectional interests as universal; denying or transmuting contradictions; and naturalising the present (reification). They draw a methodological consequence, following Miller and Tilley's distinction between "power to" (capacity to act, integral to all social practice) and "power over" (domination): if burial remains *deny* contradictions rather than naturalise/represent them, then burial data cannot simply be used to "read off" social organisation. Hodder & Hutson add their own qualification — since material culture in archaeology serves as both evidence of social reality and as ideology simultaneously, "all ideologies that appear to 'mask', in the process 'reveal'."
