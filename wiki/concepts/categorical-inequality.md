# Categorical Inequality

## Definition as Coined

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (*[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]*, 1998) treats **categorical inequality** as durable unequal access to valued resources organized around **[[categorical-pairs|paired categories]]** (black/white, male/female, citizen/foreigner, Muslim/Jew, etc.). Even when markers look biological, categories depend on social organization, belief, and enforcement. Complex multi-category systems typically resolve into **bounded pairs** with distinct boundary relations for each pair.

## Semantic History

Longer roots in Weberian closure, Marxian class categories, and boundary scholarship; Tilly's 1998 formulation makes **organizational mechanisms** (not attitudes alone) the engine and names four interlocking processes. Adjacent but distinct: [[symbolic-boundaries]] / [[boundary-work]] (meaning and demarcation) vs Tilly's resource-control focus.

## Operationalizations

Case comparison of how categorical pairs structure job assignment, citizenship rights, migration niches, firm hierarchies; matching of [[interior-exterior-categories|interior organizational categories]] to exterior categories; residual analysis after individual human-capital controls (illustrative in Tilly, not a single survey design).

## Applications

Firms and gender segregation; ethnic niches and chain migration; racialized states (e.g. South Africa illustrations); citizenship inclusion/exclusion; nutrition and stature by class as model of categorical resource inequality.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of over-generalizing "category" across race, class, gender, citizenship; relative weight of class vs ascriptive categories contested; mechanism claims are theoretical with illustrative evidence until tested case by case.
