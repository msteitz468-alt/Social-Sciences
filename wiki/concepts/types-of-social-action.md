## Definition as Coined

Weber defines four types of orientation by which social action can be determined (Ch. 1 §2):

1. **Purposive rationality (Zweckrationalität)** — using the expectations of others' and objects' behaviour as conditions or means for one's own rationally pursued ends; Weber describes this type as rationally weighing means against ends, ends against consequences, and competing ends against one another.
2. **Value rationality (Wertrationalität)** — a conscious belief in the unconditional intrinsic value (ethical, aesthetic, religious, etc.) of a form of conduct purely for its own sake, irrespective of outcome; Weber states value-rational action is made at the "bidding" of "demands" the actor believes imposed on them.
3. **Affect, especially emotion** — action arising from actual emotions and feelings; Weber notes that when "sublimated," affectual action moves toward value-rationalization or purposive action.
4. **Tradition** — action from ingrained habituation.

Weber argues affectual and value-rational action share that the meaning of the action lies in the act itself rather than its outcome (Weber's examples: instant revenge or instant enjoyment versus duty, honour, or cause). Weber states that from the standpoint of purposive rationality, value-rational action "must always be irrational," the more so the more absolute the values invoked (Ch. 1 §2).

## Semantic History

Tribe's Translation Appendix explains his rendering *Zweckrational* as "purposively rational" rather than "instrumentally rational": Tribe argues "instrumentally rational" wrongly directs attention to the means rather than to the *Zweck* (end) that governs the choice of means, and rejects "end-rational" because English "end" carries too broad a semantic range.

## Operationalizations

Weber classifies these as ideal types, not exhaustive or usually pure categories — Weber states real social action is very rarely oriented solely to one type, and the four exist as conceptually pure constructs "created for sociological ends," against which actual, mixed action can be assessed and compared (Ch. 1 §2). Weber notes that rigorously traditional behaviour and rigorously affectual comportment both lie at, or beyond, the extreme border of meaningfully oriented action, since each can shade into mere reactive response to familiar stimuli.

## Applications

Weber applies the typology throughout the chapter, e.g., in distinguishing "interest-determined custom" (purposively-rational orientation to shared expectations, as in market-price formation) from mere custom or convention (Ch. 1 §4), and in his account of the four bases of a [[legitimate-order]]'s validity, each of which parallels one of the four action-orientations (Ch. 1 §7).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber's own economic-theory illustration cautions that pure economic "laws" are themselves ideal-typical constructions of the purposively-rational course action would follow if rigorously and unambiguously pursued — Weber notes real action approximates this only rarely, citing the stock exchange as a near-limiting case (Ch. 1 §6). The typology's four categories are therefore heuristic sorting devices, not empirical descriptions of how any given action is actually motivated.
