# Contradictory Class Locations

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[wright-erik-olin|Erik Olin Wright]], *[[wright-class-counts-1997|Class Counts]]* / *Classes*.)* **Contradictory class locations** are positions in the class structure that are simultaneously exploiter and exploited (or otherwise cross-cut by antagonistic material interests) along different dimensions of class relations. Among employees, Wright differentiates locations by **authority** (organizational assets: managers and supervisors extract surplus via monitoring/control while remaining wage-dependent) and **skills/expertise** (scarce credentials and knowledge enabling "skill rents" and loyalty-enhancing privileges). Combined with the property axis (capitalists/employers, petty bourgeoisie, employees), this yields a **map of class locations** (typically twelve cells in the Comparative Class Analysis Project operationalization).

Wright's rationale: the classical two-class model cannot locate the "middle class" of managers, supervisors, and experts without either assimilating them to capital or labor or inventing a free-floating third class. Contradictory locations keep them **inside exploitation relations** while acknowledging their dual interests.

## Semantic History

Developed in Wright's 1970s–80s work (*Class, Crisis and the State*; *Classes* 1985), drawing on Roemer-style exploitation theory and efficiency-wage / labor-extraction arguments (Bowles–Gintis). Distinguished from:

- **Weberian** status/market-situation middleness without exploitation microfoundations
- **Millsian** [[new-middle-class]] as propertyless salariat (descriptive occupation axis)
- **Bourdieusian** capital composition (Wright uses skills for *power in production and labor markets*, not lifestyle symbolic capital — he cites Bourdieu on cultural correlates but brackets them from the class definition)

*Class Counts* (1997) is the large-n comparative operationalization, not the first formulation.

## Operationalizations

Survey items on ownership, number of employees, supervisory/managerial duties, credentials and occupational skill codes. Wright candidly notes **operational arbitrariness** at cut-points (methodological appendices) and that "capitalists" in samples are mostly small employers, not the ruling class.

## Applications

Comparative class distributions; mobility/friendship/marriage permeability across property, authority, and skill boundaries; class consciousness models; class–gender mediated locations. Study: [[wright-class-counts-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Cut-points and cell counts are convention-laden; survey frames under-represent capital owners and non-employed poor; feminist critics challenge class primacy and household class assignment (addressed in *Class Counts* Part III as debate, not settled). Attributed throughout.
