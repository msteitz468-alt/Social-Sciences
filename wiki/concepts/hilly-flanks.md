# Hilly Flanks

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert|Braidwood]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* 3rd ed. 1959.)*

The **hilly flanks** (also "hilly-flanks zone") are the intermediate **rain-watered** foothill and grassland belt that builds up toward the high mountain ridges of Iran, Iraq, Turkey, Syria, and Palestine — roughly the zone surrounding the Tigris–Euphrates drainage at elevations of about **2,000 to 5,000 feet**, receiving roughly 10–20+ inches of winter rainfall. Braidwood contrasts this zone with:

1. **Breasted's fertile crescent** emphasis on **irrigated alluvial lowlands** of the Tigris–Euphrates (and Nile) — fertile only with irrigation, too dry/hot for rainfed farming;
2. the **high alpine ridges** (Lebanon–Amanus, Taurus, Zagros) too rugged for early farmers.

In his account, the earliest **village-farming communities** and the natural co-occurrence of wild wheats, barley, and potential domesticates make this zone the primary **[[nuclear-area|nuclear area]]** for western Asia's **food-producing revolution**.

## Semantic History

- **Braidwood mid-century:** natural-habitat / hilly-flanks alternative to Childe's **oasis** hypothesis; Jarmo as type village in the Kurdish hills; Natufian and Karim Shahir as incipient-era candidates within or adjacent to the zone.
- **Textbook reception:** "natural habitat" hypothesis often paired with Braidwood/Jarmo in later surveys (e.g. Wenke & Olszewski; Binford's critical restatement).
- **Later archaeology:** multi-regional farming and refined Levantine/Zagros sequences rework absolute dates and primacy claims; the *geographic* insight (piedmont vs pure desert oasis vs pure alluvium) remains a standard framing device.

## Operationalizations

Elevation bands, rainfall, wild cereal/animal ranges, and early village site distributions (Jarmo, Hassuna-level sites, Syro-Cilician bases, Jericho as geographic-but-not-environmental edge case in Braidwood's text).

## Applications

Organizes agricultural-origins debates against oasis and pure riverine primacy; anchors [[jarmo]], [[natufian-culture]], and [[neolithic-revolution]] pages; foil for Binford's packing/demographic-ecological model.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested as a sufficient *causal* theory (why domesticate in a rich natural habitat? — Harlan-type abundance critique in later literature); chronology of "earliest" villages revised by later radiocarbon/calibration and Levantine PPNA work; multi-center farming weakens exclusive nuclear Near East reading. Keep as attributed mid-century program concept.
