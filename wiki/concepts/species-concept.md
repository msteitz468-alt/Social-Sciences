## Definition as Coined

The species concept as used in modern biological anthropology and paleontology is a
population-oriented definition: a species is bounded by actual or potential
interbreeding among its members, not by resemblance to a fixed type specimen.
[[klein-human-career-1999]] frames this as a direct replacement of an earlier
"typological" definition, under which a species was defined by a designated type
specimen and atypical individuals were dismissed as "sports" or deviants whose
anomalous features did not count toward the species definition.

## Semantic History

Klein argues (attributed) that the typological definition was "incompatible with
evolution as promulgated by Darwin," because it fixed species as static types and
could not accommodate the variability within a population that natural selection
requires as raw material. The population (biological) species concept displaced it as
the evolutionary synthesis consolidated: species membership is defined by
interbreeding rather than by conformity to an ideal form.

## Operationalizations

Applying the population species-concept to fossils is a distinct methodological
problem from applying it to living organisms, because interbreeding cannot be
observed directly in extinct populations. Klein states that paleontologists must
therefore rely solely on morphology, inferring likely species boundaries from the
range of morphological variability documented within the closest living relatives of
the fossil group in question — treating that variability as a proxy for what a single
interbreeding population could plausibly encompass.

Klein further argues (attributed) that because the fossil record is generally
discontinuous — long periods of morphological stasis punctuated by comparatively
brief episodes of change (see [[punctuated-equilibrium]]) — fossil species usually
present distinct beginning and end points in the record that plausibly correspond to
real reproductive boundaries. This is offered as a partial resolution of the
"continuum-dividing" problem: the difficulty of drawing a non-arbitrary species
boundary across a lineage that, in principle, changes continuously through time.

## Applications

The species concept underlies every taxonomic judgment applied to hominin fossils in
this wiki — for example, whether [[homo-erectus]] and [[homo-heidelbergensis]] are
distinct species or stages of a single evolving lineage, and whether specimens
assigned to "Homo soloensis" belong within H. erectus. See [[cladistics]] for the
complementary method used to organize taxa by shared derived characters once species
boundaries are drawn.

## Critiques and Limitations

The population species-concept, though preferred to the typological definition, is
difficult to operationalize on fossil material precisely because its defining
criterion — interbreeding — is unobservable; morphology-based inference from living
relatives is itself an assumption that only imperfectly proxies reproductive
boundaries. This tension is a standing methodological limitation on all
hominin taxonomic assignments, not a solved problem.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Attributed; (as of Stringer 2012). Species concepts as applied to modern-human origins and Neanderthal–modern relations.*

### Morphological vs Biological Species Concept

Morphological species concept (skeletal variation) leads Stringer and many anthropologists to treat Neanderthals as specifically distinct (*Homo neanderthalensis*, King 1864 restored in the 1980s) (as of Stringer 2012). Some multiregionalists apply the **Biological Species Concept (BSC)** — largest interbreeding community reproductively isolated from others — to claim *H. neanderthalensis* and *H. sapiens* same species/fully interfertile (attributed). BSC conflicts with fertile hybridization among many mammal species (wolves/coyotes, bison/cows, chimps/bonobos, monkeys); **species concepts are humanly produced categories** that may or may not match nature (as of Stringer 2012). Morphological difference of Neanderthals from moderns is comparable to that demarcating distinct species among recent primates and fossil mammals, yet closely related mammal species may still hybridize — so Neanderthal–Cro-Magnon hybridization was possible (attributed). Geometric morphometrics (Harvati) provides strong evidence that modern–Neanderthal skull shape differences are at the level of species differences among recent primates (via Stringer 2012).

### RAO, interbreeding, and species status

Stringer: RAO never precluded modern–archaic interbreeding; hybridization would not necessarily collapse Neanderthals into *H. sapiens* — depends on scale and impact (as of Stringer 2012). Hybridization evidence undermines or modifies the concept of a biological species as genetically self-contained and emphasizes fluidity of species concepts as human approximations; morphological criteria still support separating Neanderthals, moderns, heidelbergensis, *erectus* even if they interbred sometimes (attributed). Lumping Neanderthals and moderns into one species would force *H. sapiens* to encompass contradictory morphologies (high rounded vs long low vault; no continuous brow vs strong continuous brow; chin vs no chin; suprainiac fossa; inner ear; pelvis) — worse if antecessor/*erectus* added (as of Stringer 2012). Hybridization evidence does not yet require lumping all later Pleistocene humans as one species, though widespread hybridization might force that; morphological species differences still justify separate names (attributed). Clifford Jolly’s baboon-hybridization analogy: concentrate on biology, avoid semantic traps; fossil species-level taxonomy only approximates real-world complexities (via Stringer).

### Splitting vs lumping extremes

Some morphologists name ≥10 *Homo* species over 2 Ma (*ergaster, erectus, georgicus, antecessor, heidelbergensis, rhodesiensis, helmei, floresiensis, neanderthalensis, sapiens*); multiregionalists often regard only *H. sapiens* as valid over the last million years (as of Stringer 2012). Extreme 19th–mid-20th-century splitting: within 50 years of King’s 1864 name, many fossils assigned separate species (*spelaeus, grimaldii, priscus*, etc.); pendulum to lumping ~1950 (attributed historiography).

### Early modern classification disputes

[[herto]] as new subspecies *idaltu* vs Stringer’s inclusion within broad early *H. sapiens*; [[jebel-irhoud]] as not fully modern (Stringer) vs Hublin’s emphasis on chin/modern traits — species/subspecies boundaries for early moderns are interpretive, not pure measurement facts (as of Stringer 2012). Multiregional single-species stance made [[homo-floresiensis]] hard to accept as distinct (pathology preferred) (attributed).
