# Dissonant Heritage

## Definition as Coined

**Dissonant heritage** names the contested, conflictual quality of heritage — the fact that commemorating one group's past can silence or wound another's. Tunbridge & Ashworth (1996) made the term central to heritage management literature. **[[smith-laurajane|Laurajane Smith]]** (*[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006|Uses of Heritage]]* 2006) radicalizes it: dissonance is **not** a special class of difficult sites separable from ordinary "heritage," but **intrinsic** to heritage as a cultural process of negotiating identity, memory, and power *(attributed)*.

## Semantic History

- Management literature often pairs "heritage" with occasional "dissonant heritage" problems to be mitigated for sustainable tourism and social stability.
- Smith argues that reducing dissonance to **site-specific management problems** resurrects the [[authorized-heritage-discourse|AHD]]'s consensual ideal and obscures that management **regulates** memory, identity, and place.
- Load-bearing (Smith): **"Heritage is dissonant"** — constitutive social process of legitimizing and challenging values and identities in the present.

## Operationalizations

- Country-house visiting: grandeur vs servant histories; discomfort within authorized narratives.
- Riversleigh: scientific/World Heritage fossil values vs Waanyi "country" and control.
- Castleford: working-class community remembering vs external dismissal of local heritage claims.
- Indigenous heritage: control over human remains, sites, and knowledge as resources of power.

## Applications

Analytic hinge between [[authorized-heritage-discourse]] and subaltern uses of heritage; densifies [[heritage]], [[heritage-management]], [[social-memory]], [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

If "all heritage is dissonant," the term risks losing contrastive force for extreme cases (atrocity sites, contested monuments). Smith's point is theoretical: treat conflict as constitutive, not exceptional. Tunbridge & Ashworth's management recommendations remain a live alternative position in the literature.

## Related — negative heritage (Meskell)

[[meskell-lynn|Lynn Meskell's]] **[[negative-heritage]]** ([[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|*The Nature of Heritage*]] 2012) picks out the specific subclass of heritage anchored in atrocity, repression, or a painful past — where dissonant heritage names contestation in general, negative heritage names harm-anchored heritage a post-conflict nation must "master" (see [[past-mastering]]). Her South African cases (the Skukuza dog cemetery; forced-removal testimony around [[kruger-national-park|Kruger]]) illustrate the overlap and the distinction.
