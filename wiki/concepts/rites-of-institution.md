# Rites of Institution

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu.)* A **rite of institution** is a consecration ritual that does not mark
a *transition* (Van Gennep's rite of passage) but *institutes a durable difference*: "it does
not demarcate a before and an after so much as it differentiates — and elevates — those destined
to occupy eminent social positions from those over whom they will lord" (Wacquant's gloss). The
concept was coined in Bourdieu's 1982 essay "Rites of Institution" (in *Language and Symbolic
Power*); *[[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996|The State Nobility]]* deploys it at full scale on the
French elite-school system: "we can describe the process of separation leading to the
consecration... as a rite of passage, in the sense of Arnold van Gennep, **or better yet, as a
rite of institution**."

The elite concours is the paradigm: "selection is also 'election,' exams are also 'trials,'
training is also 'ascesis,' isolation is also initiatory retreat, and technical skills are also
charismatic qualifications." The concours enacts "a *numerus clausus*, an act of closure" that
"institutes an order... a legitimate, magically produced, and juridically guaranteed division of
the social world" — as in Marc Bloch's account of the generalization of dubbing.

## Semantic History

The concept reworks the Durkheimian sacred and Mauss's "universe of belief" for modern
credentialing. Key mechanics (all attributed, from *The State Nobility* Part II):

- **Social magic and symbolic capital.** Recognized restriction converts monopoly into
  nobility; each member shares, "in a logic that is truly one of magical shareholding," in the
  group's concentrated symbolic capital.
- **Consecrating those who consecrate themselves.** Consecration works only on the already
  predisposed — "the die is essentially cast in advance"; each prior consecration (prizes,
  honors) is "both cause and effect of the predisposition to recognize academic goals and
  values," a "circular reinforcement."
- **Ascesis and conversion.** Preparatory-class hazing, austerity, and "symbolic confinement"
  are a charismatic initiation: dispossession then restoration ("the institution puts itself in
  the position of being able to restore the value it initially took away"); the specific ascetic
  content is arbitrary ("archery, cricket, rugby, Latin, music, mathematics... can function as a
  basis for pedagogic ascesis") — form, not content, does the work. "We are often told that they
  taught us nothing at Eton. It may be so, but I think they taught it very well."
- **Noblesse oblige.** Via Norbert Elias: "One is born noble, but one becomes noble" — statutory
  assignation produces a subjective transformation toward the assigned essence; the certificate
  "produces what it certifies as much as it ratifies it" ("Become who you are").
- **Durability.** Unlike technical competence, "dignity... does not die... It does not age" —
  social magic "almost always intervenes... to transcend anthropological limits."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; a template for analyzing consecration ceremonies — dubbing,
ordination, baptism, circumcision, commencement, the concours — by what boundary they institute
and for whom.

## Applications

- The engine of [[state-nobility]] production in the [[grandes-ecoles]] system.
- Extends [[symbolic-violence]]: the "Oedipal effect" of academic verdicts on children's
  identity, with no court of appeal.
- A general tool for analyzing state nomination and certification (see the "state magic"
  material on [[state-nobility]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The passage/institution contrast is sharper as polemic than as taxonomy — many classic rites of
passage also institute durable statuses, and Van Gennep scholars contest the reading. Note also
a source limit: *The State Nobility* uses the term programmatically; the full theoretical
derivation lives in the 1982 essay, not yet ingested.
