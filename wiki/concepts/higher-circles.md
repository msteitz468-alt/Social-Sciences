# Higher Circles

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] used **higher
circles** for the social and institutional world of those who occupy the national command posts —
the milieu in which corporate, political, and military elites meet, share schools and clubs,
intermarry into upper strata, and form a recognizable status community. The phrase names the
*social formation* around power; [[power-elite]] names that formation when analyzed in terms of
power.

## Semantic History

Chapter 1 ("The Higher Circles") opens the book by contrasting ordinary men of the
[[mass-society]] — confined to job, family, neighborhood — with those who "look down upon" and
mightily affect those everyday worlds. Mills treated the American higher circles as virtually an
**unopposed bourgeoisie** historically (no durable rival aristocracy after Revolution, Jackson,
Civil War), yet **not** a closed nobility. Later Domhoff and others adopted "higher circles" as a
title and research object; keep their operationalizations attributed separately when ingested.

## Operationalizations

Mills layered:

1. **Local society** — small-city old and new upper classes, property and status cliques (cannot
   be summed into a national elite).
2. **Metropolitan 400** — big-city upper social class (Boston, New York, Philadelphia, etc.),
   Social Register world, prep schools and clubs as national unifiers.
3. **Command-post personnel** — very rich, chief executives, high military, political outsiders
   — overlapping with but not identical to Society.
4. **Celebrities** — status-market figures who mingle with higher circles without heading the
   dominating hierarchies.

Unity of higher circles, he argued (attributed), rests on three keys: shared psychology/style of
life, institutional structure of the big three hierarchies, and explicit coordination at crisis
points.

## Applications

Scaffold for the whole book: chapters on local society, Metropolitan 400, celebrities, very rich,
executives, corporate rich, warlords, military ascendancy, and political directorate fill in the
higher circles before the synthetic chapters on balance theory and the power elite.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Higher circles" is looser than "power elite"; critics who grant a status upper class may still
deny a unified *power* elite. Mills's own distinction between Society and command posts is the
correct internal check against collapsing prestige into power.
