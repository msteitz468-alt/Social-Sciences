# Code of Hammurabi

## Definition as Coined

The "Code of Hammurabi" refers to the collection of legal provisions carved on a basalt stele erected late in the reign of Hammurabi of [[babylon]] (r. 1792–1750 BCE, per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]'s middle-chronology-adjacent dates — approximate, not universally agreed). The best-preserved copy was found in the temple of Shamash at Sippar; it was carried off as war booty to Susa by the Elamites in the 12th century BCE, rediscovered by French excavators in 1901, and is now in the Louvre. The stele stands roughly eight feet tall, with an upper relief showing Hammurabi before a seated god (Marduk or Shamash), a long prologue recounting Hammurabi's pious deeds, at least 282 numbered provisions, and an epilogue invoking divine punishment on anyone who defaces the monument.

Copies of the text survive on clay tablets spanning from the Old Babylonian period through the Chaldean (Neo-Babylonian) dynasty of the 6th century BCE — evidence of a very long scribal transmission and reception history independent of the original stele.

## Semantic History

The label "Code" is, per Roux, **misleading** — a position, not a settled description. Roux argues the text is not a systematic legislative reform or exhaustive legal corpus comparable to Justinian's Institutes or the French Code Civil; Mesopotamian legal practice instead operated as an evolving "common law." On this reading the "Code" as we have it is better understood as an accumulation of royal legal decisions (dînat sharrim) recorded for judicial reference — and, per the position Roux advances, functioning at least partly as royal apologia or a monument of exemplary cases rather than as enacted, binding legislation. This bears on a live "legislation vs. royal apologia/monument" debate about the text's actual legal function; record both readings as positions rather than adjudicating.

Hammurabi's text was also not the first such collection: earlier law collections are attested from Ur-Nammu and his son Shulgi (Third Dynasty of Ur), Lipit-Ishtar of Isin, and the Laws of Eshnunna attributed to Bilalama, alongside the earlier social "reforms" of Urukagina of Lagash. Hammurabi's is the most complete example to survive, not (as once claimed) the oldest.

Separately, rulers since Urukagina are recorded as periodically "ordaining mêsharum" (justice) — remitting debts and obligations and fixing commodity prices — an economic-regulatory royal act distinct from the ordinary legal-decision tradition represented by the "Code" itself. A later such mêsharum-act survives as the "edict" of King Ammisaduqa, a successor of Hammurabi.

## Operationalizations

The stele's provisions are used by historians and archaeologists as evidence for Old Babylonian social stratification, economic regulation, and family/property law. Society under the Code was divided into three classes: **awīlu** (free men), **muškēnu**, and **wardu** (slaves). The muškēnum class is left untranslated by Roux; proposed glosses ("plebeian," "commoner," "villein," "poor," compare Arabic meskîn) are treated as inadequate. Roux's own tentative reading takes muškēnum as some kind of "state dependant" — a person with a specific bundle of military/civilian obligations and privileges — a contested translation, not a settled classification.

Penalties and fees scaled by class: for example, the fee for a life-saving surgical operation was 10 shekels of silver for an awīlum, 5 for a muškēnum, and 2 for a slave (§§215–17). The lex talionis ("eye for eye") applied when the victim was an awīlum (§196), but a money payment of 1 mina of silver substituted when the victim was a muškēnu (§198), and half the slave's value when the victim was a slave (§199). Verbatim provisions include §218 (a surgeon's hand cut off for causing an awīlum's death via a bronze lancet) and §229–231 (an architect put to death if a house he built collapses and kills the owner, or made to pay slave-for-slave compensation if it kills a slave instead).

Slaves were recruited from war captives and their descendants, and from impoverished free people who sold themselves or their children to creditors; they were marked by shaving or branding, and harboring a fugitive slave was severely punished. Slaves could nonetheless be freed or adopted, and — as under the earlier Ur III laws — some could acquire property or marry daughters of free men (§§175–6).

Family-law provisions include: adultery punishable by death, subject to pardon by the husband or king (§129); a soldier's wife not punished for taking another man if she lacked food in his absence (§134); divorce provisions (§§138, 141); a requirement that a diseased wife be kept and maintained "so long as she lives" (§148); a widow's usufruct of her husband's property (§§150, 171); a deceased woman's dowry passing to her sons rather than her father (§162); and protections distinguishing a first wife's sons from a slave-girl's children (§168).

The **ilkum** institution (§§26–41) tied land, livestock, and grain grants from the king to specific service obligations (rēdum "gendarme," bā'irum "sailor," nāsh biltim "tribute-bearer"), chiefly military; the resulting fief (ilkum) was heritable but not alienable to a wife or daughter, and forfeitable if the holder deserted. Roux compares this arrangement to European feudal lord–liegeman bonds and suggests, citing another scholar, that it was likely introduced by Hammurabi himself as a device to bind subjects to crown service — an attributed interpretive claim.

## Applications

The Code is a primary evidentiary basis for [[babylon]] as an Old Babylonian capital, for [[amorites|Amorite]] dynastic rule in Mesopotamia, and for the broader institution of early Mesopotamian kingship and law — see [[the-state]]. Roux offers an evaluative assessment (attributed, not wiki-voice fact) that the Code, "cruel as it sometimes appears," is in family and property law "surprisingly close to our modern ideas of justice" and shows real effort to protect women and children from arbitrary treatment.

## Critiques and Limitations

The central limitation is interpretive: whether the text should be read as enacted, generally applicable law or as a royal monument of exemplary judicial decisions is unresolved and is treated here as a position, following Roux, rather than settled fact. The translation of muškēnum remains contested. The Code's completeness (282 numbered provisions on the best-preserved stele) should not be read as evidence it was the *first* Mesopotamian legal collection — see Semantic History above.

### Postgate (*Early Mesopotamia*, 1994): reform-collection, not legislation

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] independently reinforces Roux's
"reform-collection, not legislation" reading: neither the Code of Hammurabi nor its
predecessors (Ur-Nammu, Lipit-Ishtar, Eshnunna) defines court constitution, officials'
functions, evidentiary standards, or which acts counted as legally valid — these
existed as unwritten background alongside the codes, recoverable only from the mass of
ordinary legal-transaction and lawsuit documents. Postgate surveys the "code in
practice" debate directly: contract language that changes to track CH's shepherd
provisions after Hammurabi's reign, and judges' letters invoking "the ordinance of our
lord (the king)" as a verdict's basis, argue for genuine legal effect; but the
difficulty scholars have had finding any court case that cites a specific code
provision, and the codes' apparent coverage of only a fraction of actual court
business, argue the codes were "only a gloss" on the far larger unwritten legal
system. Postgate records both readings as open positions rather than adjudicating
between them. He also offers, as his own explicit speculation, a hypothesis that CH's
scaling of *talion* punishment by social class (contrasted with the Ur III code's
purely monetary penalties for accidental injury) reflects "the traditional law of the
desert" — an Amorite legal tradition intruding on the older southern urban tradition —
though he states this cannot be confirmed without comparative evidence. See
[[mesopotamian-law]] for the wider judicial system (the local-council/court/king
pyramid, oaths, the river ordeal, and documentary evidentiary practice) that Postgate
argues the Code sits within.

### Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

Van De Mieroop reports the strong scholarly consensus, crediting Kraus (1960) as the turning point, that "the modern designation" of the text "is wrong: it is not a code of law but a monument presenting Hammurabi as an exemplary king of justice." He traces the historiography: Driver & Miles (1952–5) and Kohler & Peiser (1904–23) originally read the text as enforceable, Roman-law-style legislation; Kraus first noted that its casuistic "if... then" format matches omen and medical texts rather than statute, prompting Bottéro's (1992) reevaluation of it as a "King of Justice" monument; no Old Babylonian legal record or letter is known to cite the code in an actual ruling (Veenhof 1997–2000, with a few possible exceptions per Roth 1997 and Stol 2004); and major life domains, such as animal husbandry, go entirely unaddressed — all evidence against reading it as a comprehensive statute.

Scholars diverge on what the monument positively **is**, and Van De Mieroop records this as a position set rather than a resolved question: a scholarly legal treatise (Westbrook 1989); royal propaganda addressed to court elites (Wells 2005); or a public symbolic guarantee of justice available regardless of literacy (Bahrani 2007). This reinforces, from an independent current-textbook synthesis, the "not enacted legislation" reading Roux and Postgate already record above — see also [[mesopotamian-law]].
