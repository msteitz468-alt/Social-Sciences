# Nuclear Area

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert|Braidwood]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* 3rd ed. 1959.)*

A **nuclear area** is a region that Braidwood treats as a **primary hearth** of the food-producing stage: not merely where farming later appears by diffusion, but where environment and culture together make an independent (or first) transition plausible. For western Asia he identifies the **[[hilly-flanks|hilly-flanks zone]]** as the **nuclear Near East** — of greatest interest to "people of the western cultural tradition" because, he claims, that tradition's heritage began within it.

Two requirements he states for an **[[incipient-cultivation|era of incipient cultivation and animal domestication]]**: (1) the natural environment of a nuclear area with a suite of domesticable plants and animals; (2) a cultural "atmosphere of experimentation" / receptiveness by the end of the Ice Age — the second not reducible to environment alone.

## Semantic History

- **Braidwood 1959:** nuclear Near East = hilly flanks; Egypt and southern Mesopotamia treated as secondary for *earliest* village farming (river control later enables civilization).
- **Processual critique:** nuclear-zone "settling-in" models attacked as inadequate explanation (Binford 1968 and later).
- **Later multi-regionalism:** multiple independent nuclear areas worldwide (China, Mesoamerica, Andes, etc.) — Braidwood himself notes other areas may have been nuclear for other traditions; densify when comparative chapters are integrated.

## Operationalizations

Presence of wild domesticable suite; early village site density; assemblage complexity sequences; early radiocarbon pins (as of 1959).

## Applications

Pairs with [[hilly-flanks]] and [[incipient-cultivation]]; organizes contrast to oasis and pure alluvial-origin stories; links [[jarmo]] as type primary village-farming community.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Nuclear" can smuggle teleology (western heritage narrative); primacy claims are chronology-sensitive; cultural "receptiveness" is under-specified (Braidwood admits as much). Attribute all causal claims.

**Debate page:** [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]]. Source: [[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959]].

## Sanders & Price — "Nuclear America" and the relative nuclear area (1968, attributed)

A parallel Americanist usage. [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]] (1968) applied **"Nuclear America"** (a term they credit to Kroeber's 1948 *Anthropology*) to the combined cultural-historical unit of Mesoamerica + the Central Andes + the Intermediate Area, and used "nuclear area" as an explicitly **relative, scalar** concept — "areas that were particularly vigorous foci of culture change" with "strong extra-local repercussions," admitting "nuclear areas within nuclear areas" (e.g. the Basin of Mexico within Mesoamerica). On their account the influence a nuclear area exerts depends on (1) the recipient's own autochthonous development, (2) the local repercussions of domination, and (3) the **exportability** of the nuclear area's institutions to different settings — the framework by which they read [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]]'s and Monte Albán's reach. Distinct from Braidwood's food-production-hearth sense above, and from the [[symbiotic-region|symbiotic region]] (defined by *internal* ecological complementarity rather than radiating influence). Note the term differs again from Kroeber's Andean-focused usage.

## Chang — the "North China Nuclear Area" (1963, attributed)

[[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] (*[[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|The Archaeology of Ancient
China]]*, 1963) applied "nuclear area" to East Asia, in a sense close to Braidwood's food-production
hearth. His **North China nuclear area** is the small basin at the confluence of the Huanghe, Fenhe,
and Weishui rivers (the Henan/Shansi/Shensi border), which he argued was the independent birthplace
of Chinese farming and the radiating center of subsequent cultural growth. He offered a six-point
case: an ecotonal "hilly-flanks"-plus-waterside-fisherman position; the region's sole Huanghe-basin
Mesolithic assemblage (Sha-yüan); the only stratigraphic hint of a pre-[[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]]
Neolithic; the importance of fishing at its Yangshao sites; its lead role in the Yangshao→Lungshan
transition; and its enduring historic centrality. On this framework the [[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]]
horizon "expanded explosively" out of the nuclear area, and peripheral regions (Kansu, the Northeast,
South China) were **marginal** recipients — Chang read Yangshao ceramic variation as showing "the
nuclearity of the Nuclear Area and the marginality of Kansu." He generalized the nuclear-vs-marginal
distinction into a proposed universal problem in cultural classification (citing Braidwood's "Levels
in Prehistory" and Steward). Chang's monocentric application was later superseded by multi-regional
models (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]) — the concept survives, its single-hearth
application for China does not. Source: [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963]].

## Liu & Chen (2012) — the erosion of the Central-Plain "nucleus" paradigm

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]].)* Liu and Chen describe the
pre-Cultural-Revolution paradigm as treating the Central Plain (middle/lower Yellow River Valley) as
the sole "nucleus" of Chinese civilization, with southern Neolithic sites (Beiyinyangying,
Qianshanyang, Qujialing) dismissed as minor peripheries lacking a continuous developmental sequence
— "regarded as the peripheries of the Central Plain with minor significance for Chinese civilization
proper." From the 1970s onward this nuclear-area paradigm was progressively displaced by evidence of
independent complex-society development in the Yangzi Valley ([[hemudu|Hemudu]]/Liangzhu), the
northeast (Hongshan/Niuheliang), the east (Dawenkou/Longshan), and Sichuan (Sanxingdui) — a shift
Liu & Chen attribute to accumulating fieldwork data rather than to a single theoretical reversal.

Two competing multiregional reformulations followed. **Su Bingqi's** *quxi leixing* ("regional
systems and local cultural series," early 1980s) identifies six relatively stable regional cultural
divisions (*quxi*) that formed after 10,000 BP within the area later encompassed by historical China,
each locally developed and interacting with the others as roughly equal partners — an explicit
rejection of any single nucleus. **Yan Wenming's** "flower and petals" model (1987) is a competing
multiregional formulation that still privileges the Central Plain as the structural center — "seeing
the Central Plain as the center of a flower and cultural traditions in the surrounding areas as
layers of petals," emphasizing "the leading role of the Central Plain" — unlike Su Bingqi's more
equal-weighting model. Both replace Chang's single-nucleus diffusionism, but they disagree with each
other over whether any center still deserves analytical priority; see
[[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]] for the fuller position record. Source:
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]].
