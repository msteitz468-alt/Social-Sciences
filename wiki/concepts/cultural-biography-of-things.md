# Cultural Biography of Things

## Definition as Coined

[[kopytoff-igor|Igor Kopytoff]] (in *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986) proposes that things, like persons, can be given **cultural biographies**. Production of commodities is material *and* cognitive — things are culturally marked as certain kinds of thing; commodity status shifts over time and between persons, revealing a **moral economy** behind the objective economy of visible transactions *(Kopytoff)*.

[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]’s complementary methodological fetishism insists that meanings are inscribed in forms, uses, and **trajectories** — things-in-motion illuminate human contexts *(Appadurai)*.

## Operationalizations

Canonical questions *(Kopytoff)*: What biographical possibilities inhere in the thing’s status, period, and culture? Where does it come from and who made it? Career so far and ideal career? Recognized ages/periods and cultural markers? How does use change with age, and what happens at the end of usefulness?

Things have **partial** biographies (physical, technical, economic, social) as persons have many biographical registers *(Kopytoff)*.

Method page: [[object-biography]].

[[hodder-ian|Hodder]] (*[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* 2012) engages Appadurai/Kopytoff social-life strands as part of the “return to things” but argues biographies and materiality studies often remain anthropocentric relative to TT/TH object dependences ([[entanglement-theory]]).

## Applications

- Slavery as capture → commodity phase → reinsertion/singularization → potential recommoditization *(Kopytoff)*.
- Suku hut life cycle; Renoir and car biographies as culturally judged careers *(Kopytoff)*.
- SLOT cases: medieval relics (Geary); oriental carpets (Spooner); Indian cloth “collective biography” (Bayly); Solomon ritual objects (Davenport).

## Critiques and Limitations

“Biography” is metaphoric; mute archaeological things yield thinner narratives (Renfrew works with assemblage wealth, not named object life-histories). Slavery analogy is analytic, not identity of persons and pots.
