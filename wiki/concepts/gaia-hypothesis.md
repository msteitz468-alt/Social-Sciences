## Definition

The Gaia hypothesis originated with James Lovelock and Lynn Margulis as a proposal that
Earth's biosphere, atmosphere, oceans, and soil form a self-regulating system that
maintains conditions for life (atmospheric homeostasis produced by and for the
biosphere). It is a scientific hypothesis in Earth-systems science, not a concept
Haraway coins; she relays it in *(Haraway 2016)*,
[[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]], as part of her
argument about [[sympoiesis]] and the [[chthulucene|Chthulucene]]. Haraway states that
Isabelle Stengers and Bruno Latour "evoke the name of Gaia in the way James Lovelock and
Lynn Margulis did, to name complex nonlinear couplings between processes that compose
and sustain entwined but nonadditive subsystems as a partially cohering systemic whole"
*(Haraway 2016)*, explicitly attributing the original concept to Lovelock and Margulis.

## Semantic History (Origin and Drift) — as of Haraway 2016

Per Haraway's account, Margulis co-founded Gaia theory with Lovelock, and initially
described the relevant biospheric processes as "autopoietic" — self-producing and
self-organizing — a framing Haraway reports Margulis held before "sympoietic" was
available as a term: "Perhaps she would have chosen the term sympoietic, but the word
and concept had not yet surfaced" when Margulis called her own processes "autopoietic,"
following Gaia theory with Lovelock *(Haraway 2016)*. This is Haraway's characterization
of Margulis's earlier position, dated to the period before Dempster's 1998 coinage of
"sympoiesis"; it should be read as (as of Haraway 2016) reporting on Margulis's
scientific framing, not as the wiki's own assessment of Earth-systems science.

## Haraway/Stengers Reframing

Haraway's central move is to reframe Gaia through Stengers's account and her own
sympoiesis framework rather than to treat Gaia as a settled scientific consensus.
Haraway reports Stengers's framing of Gaia as "intrusive," "ticklish," and a fearful
power that "intrudes on our categories of thought" — explicitly not a nurturing mother,
not a resource, and not a person: Gaia "does not care about human intentions" and "puts
into question our very existence" *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway quotes Stengers: "She is
what specifically questions the tales and refrains of modern history... it is the
answer we... may be able to create as we face the consequences of what we have
provoked" *(Haraway 2016)*, and attributes to Stengers the phrase "multi-faced Gaïa" for
this framing.

Haraway's own theoretical argument, distinct from both Lovelock/Margulis's original
formulation and from Stengers's intrusive-power framing, is that "Gaia is a system
mistaken for autopoietic that is really sympoietic" *(Haraway 2016)* — directly folding
Gaia into her [[sympoiesis]] concept rather than accepting a self-organizing,
autopoietic reading of the hypothesis.

Haraway also credits Bruno Latour with the proposal that scholars must learn to tell
"Gaïa stories" or "geostories" in which "former props and passive agents have become
active," without a single overseeing plot-author; Latour's tellers of such stories are
termed the "Earthbound," distinguished from "Humans" who Latour frames as remaining "in
the Holocene" *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway nonetheless critiques Latour's reliance on Carl
Schmitt's "political theology" (the enemy as *hostis*, trials of strength) as making it
"unnecessarily hard" for Latour to tell a genuinely new, non-heroic Gaia story, arguing
his "Earthbound" figure needs "a more tentacular, less binary life story" *(Haraway
2016)*. She further credits Eduardo Viveiros de Castro and Déborah Danowski with a
conference, "The Thousand Names of Gaia," aimed at de-Eurocentering Gaia discourse
beyond its ancient Greek/Eurocultural origin *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Operationalizations

In its original Earth-systems register the hypothesis is operationalized through biogeochemical measures of atmospheric and biospheric homeostasis (e.g., regulation of atmospheric composition, temperature, and ocean chemistry). As mobilized by Haraway and Stengers it is not operationalized at all — it functions as a figurative and philosophical reframing rather than a measurable claim. This ingest carries only the latter register.

## Applications

Within *(Haraway 2016)* Gaia is applied not as a testable Earth-systems model but as a resource for narrating the [[chthulucene|Chthulucene]]: Stengers's "intrusive Gaia" and Latour's "geostories" are used to argue for a non-heroic, more-than-human way of telling planetary change, which Haraway folds into her [[sympoiesis]] framework. Its wider application in the environmental humanities is as a boundary object between Earth-systems science and cultural theory.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Gaia hypothesis is, independent of Haraway's use of it, a contested scientific
proposal within Earth-systems science — debates over whether biospheric homeostasis
reflects genuine self-regulating feedback versus a looser, non-teleological set of
biogeochemical couplings are part of its reception history; this ingest, drawn solely
from a theoretical/philosophical source, does not carry a scientific literature
assessment of that dispute and should not be read as adjudicating it. As mobilized in
*(Haraway 2016)*, Gaia functions doubly: as a scientific hypothesis credited to Lovelock
and Margulis, and as a rhetorical/philosophical figure (via Stengers and Latour) that
Haraway explicitly reframes through her own [[sympoiesis]] concept. Readers should not
conflate these two registers — Haraway's claim that "Gaia is really sympoietic" is her
own attributed theoretical position, not a restatement of Lovelock or Margulis's
original scientific claims. The collection's coverage of the primary Earth-systems
science literature on Gaia (Lovelock's and Margulis's own publications) is, as of this
ingest, absent; this page rests entirely on Haraway's secondhand characterization
*(Haraway 2016)*.
