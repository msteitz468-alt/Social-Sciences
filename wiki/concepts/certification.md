# Certification

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* **Validation** of actors, performances, and claims by external authorities — and its opposite, **decertification** (withdrawal of validation by key certifying agents). A recurrent relational mechanism in [[contentious-politics-theory|contentious politics]].

## Definition as Coined

> "Certification entails the validation of actors, their performances, and their claims by external authorities. Decertification is the withdrawal of such validation by certifying agents."

Certification is selective: certifying sites recognize a **radically limited range** of identities, performances, and claims among the multiple identities any person or site may hold. Regimes also sit in international validation structures; withdrawal can have direct effects (aid, sanctions, intervention) and indirect ones (emboldening insurgents; elite abandonment).

## Semantic History

DOC mechanism vocabulary (formalized by Chs. 4–5; applied heavily in Ch. 7 revolutionary trajectories). Related to recognition, sponsorship, and international human-rights/diplomatic standing — not identical to Weberian legitimacy alone.

## Operationalizations

External authority statements and acts that raise or lower the standing of claimants or regimes (diplomatic recognition, aid cuts, election monitoring endorsement, UN “we are a nation” performances, media framing by authoritative outlets).

## Applications

*(DOC attributed illustrations:)* Kenyatta trial / Mau Mau decertification blocking moderation pathway; U.S. and regional **decertification of Somoza** (Carter-era aid cuts; Costa Rica, Venezuela, Panama, Mexico); U.S. certification of Philippine opposition 1986; contrast cases where certification/decertification intersect differently with brokerage and appropriation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Authority set is researcher-specified; certification shades into ordinary legitimacy politics; post-hoc risk if only successful certifications are coded.
