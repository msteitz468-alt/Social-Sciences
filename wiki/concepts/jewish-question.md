# Jewish Question

The **Jewish Question** (*Judenfrage*) names a cluster of modern European debates about Jewish emancipation, citizenship, assimilation, and the alleged incompatibility of Jews with the modern nation or with critical reason. [[nirenberg-david|Nirenberg]] (2013, attributed) treats it as continuous with a much longer history of [[anti-judaism|thinking with Judaism]], not as a purely modern invention — while also showing how modern philosophy and revolution retool Jewish figures for new projects.

## Definition as Coined

Nirenberg notes (attributed) that the term was brought into general circulation in the mid-nineteenth century by young German philosophers of “critical critique.” [[marx-karl|Karl Marx]]’s 1844 “On the Jewish Question” (response to Bruno Bauer) is the most famous intervention in that moment. The “question” form already frames Jews as a **problem to be solved** by the non-Jewish polity or by critique.

### Marx 1844 (via Nirenberg, attributed)

- Conversion and political emancipation cannot free Germany of “Judaism” because Judaism is not only a religion but an **attitude** of alienation; the worldly cult is haggling, the worldly god is money.
- Fundamental insight Nirenberg endorses: the Jewish Question is about basic **tools of relation** (money, property, mediation) that culture has thought essentially Jewish and that can produce “Jewishness” in their users.
- Critical cost Nirenberg stresses: Marx **exploited** rather than historicized the habit linking capitalism to Jewishness, elevating emancipation *from Judaism* into a revolutionary program — confusing figural and real Jews.

Primary Marx texts on this point are **not yet fully ingested** in this wiki; the above is Nirenberg’s reading, cross-linked to [[marx-karl]].

## Semantic History

| Moment | Frame |
|---|---|
| Emancipation era | Can Jews be citizens without ceasing to be Jews? |
| Bauer / Marx | Political vs human emancipation; religion vs “real” Judaism of money |
| Racial antisemitism | *Judenfrage* as elimination or segregation problem (late 19th c. onward) |
| French Revolution precursor | ~32 National Assembly debates; 1791 emancipation as citizenship limit-case (Nirenberg Ch. 11) |
| Post-Holocaust | Whether invoking the history of the Question is critical reflection or special pleading (Nirenberg’s methodological anxiety in the Introduction) |

## Operationalizations

Studied as **discourse and political program**, not as a valid “question” with a single answer. Modern survey measures of [[antisemitism]] are a different evidence class.

## Applications

- Marx and Young Hegelian exchange (Nirenberg Intro + Ch. 13).
- French Revolutionary regeneration and incomplete conversion (Ch. 11).
- German philosophy from Kant to Heine (Ch. 12).
- Arendt’s co-responsibility thesis and its critics (Epilogue).
- Companion myth-axis: [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]] (Cohn) as a genocidal answer to the “question.”

## Critiques and Limitations

Accepting the Question’s framing risks treating Jewish existence as a puzzle for non-Jews to solve. Nirenberg insists (attributed) his special pleading is for *critical history of ideas*, not for or against Jews, Judaism, or Israel. Do not launder the Question’s premises into wiki voice.
