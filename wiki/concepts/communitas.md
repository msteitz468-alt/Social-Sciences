# Communitas

> Concept coined by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] in *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The
> Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure]]* (1969), chapters 3–4. Turner's most
> influential and most contested concept, paired throughout with **structure**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Turner throughout — this is a theoretical construct, not a finding.)*
**Communitas** is a second model of human interrelatedness, contrasted with structure:
society experienced as "an unstructured or rudimentarily structured and relatively
undifferentiated *comitatus*, community, or even communion of equal individuals." Turner
uses the Latin *communitas* rather than "community" precisely to mark a *modality of social
relationship* rather than an area of common living.

Communitas gives recognition to "an essential and generic human bond, without which there
could be no society." It is glimpsed, Turner argues, in the [[liminality|liminal]] phase of
[[rites-of-passage|rites of passage]] — where neophytes, stripped of status, meet as equals —
and more generally "in the interstices of structure, in liminality; at the edges of
structure, in marginality; and from beneath structure, in inferiority." Turner adopts Martin
Buber's language of the "I–Thou" and the "essential We" as the closest verbal approximation
("Community is where community happens").

**Structure vs. communitas.** The two are "immortal antagonists" that "constitute one
another and are mutually indispensable." Social life is a **dialectical process** of
alternation: "the immediacy of communitas gives way to the mediacy of structure, while, in
rites de passage, men are released from structure into communitas only to return to structure
revitalized by their experience of communitas." Turner distinguishes communitas from
Durkheimian **solidarity**, whose force depends on an in-group/out-group contrast; communitas'
boundaries are "ideally coterminous with those of the human species" (compare Bergson's "open"
vs. "closed" morality).

## The Three Forms (the definitional core)

| Form | Definition (attributed to Turner) |
|---|---|
| **Existential / spontaneous** | "Approximately what the hippies today would call 'a happening'" — a transient, immediate bond involving "the whole man in his relation to other whole men"; cannot be adequately expressed in a structural form; "of the now." |
| **Normative** | Existential communitas "organized into a perduring social system" under the pressures of time, resource mobilization, and social control (e.g. a religious order after its founding generation). |
| **Ideological** | Utopian models of society based on remembered existential communitas — attempts to describe and to legislate the conditions for it. |

Turner's key dynamic claim: it is "the fate of all spontaneous communitas in history to
undergo … a 'decline and fall' into structure and law." What is routinized in a religious
movement is not only the leader's charisma but "the communitas of their first disciples."

## Semantic History

- **Antecedent (1967):** in "Betwixt and Between" ([[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|Forest of
  Symbols]]) Turner describes the equality of neophytes in the liminal phase without yet naming
  it; "anti-structure" appears in the same volume's witchcraft essay.
- **1969 (*The Ritual Process*):** the concept named and given its three-form typology and the
  structure/communitas dialectic — the definitional statement.
- **1974 onward:** carried into Turner's processual symbology and performance work; picked up by
  ritual studies, theology, history, and social-movement theory. As it diffused, "communitas"
  loosened toward a general name for intense fellow-feeling, shedding Turner's specific pairing
  with structure — the drift the [[communitas-scale-debate]] contests.

## Operationalizations

Communitas is not a measured variable but an interpreted quality; Turner identifies it by a
recurring cluster of liminal attributes (which double as its diagnostic markers): homogeneity,
equality, anonymity, absence of property and rank, humility, unselfconsciousness, sexual
continence or its antithesis, acceptance of suffering, and "sacred folly." Where these cluster —
in a seclusion lodge, a mendicant order, a millenarian movement, a countercultural "happening" —
Turner reads communitas. The three forms are distinguished operationally by *durability and
institutionalization*: spontaneous (transient, uninstitutionalized), normative (organized into a
lasting system), ideological (codified as a utopian model).

## The Powers of the Weak

Communitas is symbolized cross-culturally, Turner argues, by the *structurally inferior* —
"the powers of the weak." Examples he assembles: subjugated autochthones with ritual power
over political conquerors; small nations as moral exemplars; the court jester; the
matrilateral side in patrilineal societies (Tallensi *bakologo* shrines; the Nuer
leopard-skin priest) and the patrilateral *ntoro* in the matrilineal Ashanti; the "holy
poor" of medieval Europe; folk-tale "third sons" and "simpletons"; and the humble outsiders
of Tolstoy, Dostoevsky, and Twain. Millenarian movements and the 1960s counterculture display
liminal attributes — homogeneity, equality, anonymity, humility, sexual continence or its
antithesis, abolition of rank, acceptance of suffering.

## Status Reversal

In rituals of **status reversal** (chapter 5), structurally low groups temporarily wield
ritual authority over their superiors — the Ashanti **Apo** ceremony of licensed "plain
speaking," Halloween, the Zulu Nomkubulwana rites (which [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] analyzed
as [[ritual-of-rebellion|rituals of rebellion]]), Holi in North India. Turner reads these not
merely as safety-valves reaffirming a fixed hierarchy but as a dialectic that "purges structure
by communitas" and regenerates it: "structure, scoured and purified by communitas, is displayed
white and shining again." Society (*societas*) is thus "a process rather than a thing — a
dialectical process with successive phases of structure and communitas."

## Applications

- The [[ndembu]] Kanongesha installation (the chief humbled and reviled before elevation).
- The Franciscan Spirituals and the Bengali Sahajiya as movements of communitas declining
  into structure (Turner's two extended case studies).
- Monasticism, millenarianism, cargo cults, and the 1960s counterculture.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Scale/applicability.** The most persistent charge — which Turner answers in his 1977
  Foreword — is that liminality and communitas overgeneralize from small-scale preliterate
  societies to complex ones (see [[communitas-scale-debate]]). Turner himself concedes that
  modern communitas-seeking groups "have not yet developed a structure capable of maintaining
  social and economic order over long periods."
- **Romantic/normative charge.** Critics read communitas as importing a value-laden nostalgia
  for spontaneous fellowship into what claims to be analysis.
- **Relation to Durkheim.** Turner distinguishes communitas from
  [[collective-effervescence]] and solidarity, but the family resemblance to Durkheim's
  effervescent assembly is close and much discussed.
