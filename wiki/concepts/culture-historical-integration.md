# Culture-Historical Integration

[[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] and [[phillips-philip|Phillips]]’s name for archaeology’s **primary task on the descriptive level**: organizing primary data into meaningful human history that has both **structure** (space-time) and **content** (cultural context).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958.)*

The term covers almost everything the archaeologist does organizing primary data: typology, taxonomy, formulation of archaeological units, investigation of relationships in function and natural environment, and determination of internal dimensions and external relationships in space and time — yet these activities remain essentially **descriptive**. Explanatory concepts (acculturation, diffusion, stimulus diffusion) may be used, but the aim is to describe what happened to specific units at specific times and places, not to draw general laws.

Analogized to **ethnography with the time dimension added** — with the caveat that archaeological formulations lie mainly in the cultural aspect of behavior.

## Semantic History

- **1953 paper title:** “An Operational Basis for Culture-Historical Integration.”
- Positioned against Walter Taylor’s charge that Americanists overdid “chronicle” at the expense of “cultural context”: W&P agree historically but argue integration is **both** scales and content, not separable levels.
- Footnote: Taylor’s “chronicle”/“historiography” and Willey’s earlier (1953a) “historical”/“processual” map to this **descriptive** level — not to 1958 “[[processual-interpretation|processual interpretation]]” (explanatory regularities).
- Everyday practice of [[culture-historical-archaeology]] under another name; European culture-history (Childe) is the parallel program without this Americanist label.

## Applications

- Part I of *[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory]]* is the charter.
- Every regional sequence monograph that builds phases and correlates them is doing culture-historical integration in W&P’s sense.

## Critiques and Limitations

W&P argue lack of progress toward explanation came from unit formulations with too little social aspect. New Archaeology later radicalized the critique: description without explicit theory of process is incomplete science. Post-processualism attacked the neutrality of “integration” itself. The term remains useful historiographically for mid-century Americanist method.
