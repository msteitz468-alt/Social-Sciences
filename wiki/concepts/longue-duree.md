# Longue Durée

## Definition as Coined

In Braudelian historiography, the **longue durée** is the time-scale of slowly changing structures (geography, material life, enduring economic and social frameworks), contrasted with the *conjoncture* (medium-term cycles) and *histoire événementielle* (events). Fernand Braudel's classic statement is "Histoire et sciences sociales: La longue durée" (*Annales*, 1958).

## Semantic History

Adopted into [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]] by [[wallerstein-immanuel|Immanuel Wallerstein]] as the preferred temporal frame for analyzing the [[capitalist-world-economy|modern world-system]] (~500 years) rather than short-run policy or national-event narratives. The 2004 FBC anniversary volume is explicitly titled and framed under this scale. Meanings drift between: (a) Braudel's three-tiered historical time; (b) any long-run structural analysis; (c) rhetorical marker of Annales/world-systems affiliation.

## Operationalizations

No single quantitative measure. In practice: multi-century reconstruction of commodity chains, hegemonic successions, demographic-material constraints, and knowledge structures. Absolute dates and periodizations remain program-specific and contested.

## Applications

- Braudel on the Mediterranean and early modern material life (not yet ingested).
- Wallersteinian modern world-system since the long sixteenth century.
- Volume contributors (Arrighi on hegemony; van der Linden on internationalism; Collins on intellectual structures over the second millennium CE) claim longue-durée scope with varying depth.

## Critiques and Limitations

Event-centered and short-run institutionalist historians charge structural over-determination; cultural historians charge neglect of meaning and contingency; even within Annales, the relative weight of structure vs agency remains disputed. World-systems uses of the term inherit these tensions.

## Parallel to archaeological "process" (attributed — Johnson 2011)

Matthew Johnson's textbook synthesis (*[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological
Theory]]*, Ch.11) draws an explicit parallel between Braudel's longue durée and
processual archaeology's own move from "culture history" to "culture process": both
seek underlying long-term trends beneath surface variability or short-term events,
asking "why" rather than "when/who." Johnson notes this parallel was recognized only
retrospectively by archaeologists rather than by design, and treats it as one instance
of a broader point — that archaeological theory's history runs in structural parallel
with historical theory's history even where the two traditions developed independently
and use different jargon. See [[annales-school]], [[processual-archaeology]].
