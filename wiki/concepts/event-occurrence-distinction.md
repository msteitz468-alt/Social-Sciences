# Event vs Occurrence Distinction

## Definition as Coined

[[abbott-andrew|Abbott]] (attributed; “Event Sequence and Event Duration,” *Historical Methods* 1984; *[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]* prologue and method chapters) distinguishes:

- **Events** — abstractions, the narrative equivalents of **concepts** in standard methods (e.g., “medical education,” “professionalization step”).
- **Occurrences** — actual happenings used to **indicate** that an event has taken place (e.g., “founding of a medical school”), the narrative equivalents of **indicators**.

Thus “medical education” is an event of which “the founding of a medical school” is one possible occurrence. The analysis, Abbott notes, is exactly equivalent to the standard view of measurement then being adumbrated in LISREL-style work.

## Semantic History

Motivated by the failure of order-statistics seriation on Wilensky-type professionalization data: mixing once-only occurrences with many-times occurrences biases sequence order on probabilistic grounds even when “shapes” of likelihoods over time are the same (attributed, *Time Matters* prologue — the “Wilensky problem”). Forces careful [[colligation]] (binding occurrences into coherent events at one narrative level) before [[sequence-analysis]].

## Operationalizations

- Choose a coherent narrative level so that sequence comparison is not an apples-and-oranges order-statistic artifact.
- Trade off order statistics, medians, and rates on occurrence distributions (Abbott's 1984 simulative analysis).
- Encode cases as strings over an event alphabet for [[optimal-matching]] or related metrics.

## Applications

Professionalization and institutionalization sequences; life-course and stress research (Abbott claims the philosophical argument has implications there, attributed); prerequisite hygiene for any formal sequence comparison.

**Distinct from Sewell's "historical event."** Abbott's distinction is *methodological* (events as abstractions/concepts, occurrences as their indicators). [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]]'s [[historical-event]] is a *substantive* theory: an event is a ramified sequence of *occurrences* (actual happenings) that durably transforms structures. The two are complementary siblings within the eventful/temporal turn — see [[eventful-temporality]], [[eventful-sociology]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Under-specified colligation silently biases sequence orderings — Abbott discarded two draft chapters of *The System of Professions* for this reason (attributed, prologue). Later event-history critiques of sequence work sometimes miss that Abbott had already pressed measurement issues in 1984 (he notes a 1999 print attack by Larry Wu on related grounds).
