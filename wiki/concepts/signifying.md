# Signifying

## Definition as Coined

Claudia Mitchell-Kernan distinguished two senses of signifying in her 1972 chapter "Signifying and Marking: Two Afro-American Speech Acts" (in Gumperz & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*). The first sense — a tactic within game-activity/verbal dueling — was the sense most treated in prior folklore literature (Abrahams, Kochman). Mitchell-Kernan's own analytic focus was the second, less-studied sense: "a way of encoding messages or meanings in natural conversations which involves, in most cases, an element of indirection" — an alternative message form chosen for artistic merit that need not define an entire speech event.

## Semantic History

Signifying is an **emic term migrated to analytic use** (a "migrated" concept in this wiki's terminology): an African American vernacular term for a recognized in-group verbal practice, turned by Mitchell-Kernan into a named analytic category within linguistic anthropology. Her defining criterion was indirection: the correct semantic (referential) interpretation of a signifying utterance cannot be reached by considering the dictionary meanings of its lexical items alone, and requires shared cultural/background knowledge operating on two levels — recognizing that signifying is occurring, and correctly reinterpreting the utterance.

## Operationalizations

Mitchell-Kernan operationalized signifying through analysis of naturalistic tape-recorded conversation from an urban Black community in Oakland, California, identifying the practice by the presence of a communicative gap between literal and intended meaning, and by informants' own metalinguistic glosses (e.g., "Barbara was trying to rank Mary").

## Applications

- Conveying negative import toward an addressee.
- Establishing dominance or rank between speakers.
- Serving flirtatious or persuasive "rapping."
- A closely related device Mitchell-Kernan termed **marking** — a narrative technique of reproducing not just the content but the manner (phonology, paralinguistic mimicry) of a quoted speaker's utterance, offering implicit comment on that speaker's background, personality, or intent (illustrated in her chapter by an "Uncle Tom" mimicry example).

## Critiques and Limitations

Mitchell-Kernan explicitly positioned her chapter as filling a gap left by prior folklore-centered treatments of signifying (which focused narrowly on the verbal-dueling sense); her framework situates code choice as communicative strategy rather than mere grammatical variation, contrasting with contemporaneous dialectological approaches (Labov, Stewart, Wolfram) that treated African American speech primarily as a dialect-variation problem.
