# Hierarchy of the Sciences

## Definition as Coined

[[comte-auguste|Comte]] proposed a classification of the **fundamental (abstract) sciences** determined by the real affinities of their phenomena — not by faculties of mind (contra Bacon/D'Alembert-style schemes he rejected). Order follows mutual dependence: each science rests on those that are more general, simpler, and more independent (*Positive Philosophy*, Introduction ch. II, Martineau).

Canonical scale (as stated in the Introduction):

1. Mathematics  
2. Astronomy  
3. Physics  
4. Chemistry  
5. Physiology / biology (organic physics)  
6. Social physics / [[sociology]]

Comte excluded from this fundamental scale the **practical arts** and **concrete** natural history (botany, zoology, mineralogy as secondary applications). Tag line of the practical relation of science: "From Science comes Prevision; from Prevision comes action" — while still insisting science's primary destination is knowing laws, not industry alone.

Book VI ch. IV elaborates sociology's dependence on the whole prior scale and its role completing positive philosophy. Book VI ch. XIII restates the hierarchical succession from mathematics to sociology as the means by which understanding reaches the definitive point of view of positive philosophy.

## Semantic History

- Founding encyclopedic device of [[positivism]]
- Echoed and transformed in later "unity of science" movements; not identical to logical-positivist unity-of-science theses
- Sociology's "last place" became both a prestige claim and a methodological subordination to natural-science models — a standing reflexive issue for the discipline

## Operationalizations

Rate of becoming "positive," Comte argued, tracks generality, simplicity, and independence: astronomy early, social science latest. The hierarchy structures the entire *Cours* (Books I–VI). Book V (biology) is the bridge to social science in the system but was **missing from files at this ingest** — known programmatically from Introduction and Book VI.

## Applications

- Justifies order of exposition in the *Positive Philosophy*
- Underwrites why social method must wait on biology and the inorganic sciences
- Frames sociology's dependence without reducing social laws to physical laws (unity of method, not of doctrine)
- Remedy for overspecialization: a specialty of scientific generalities under the positive method (Introduction)

## Critiques and Limitations

Later critics dispute the linear ranking, the exclusion or subordination of psychology and economics, the biology→society analogy, and the politics of "dependence." Treat hierarchy as Comte's program, not as wiki-voice taxonomy of modern disciplines.
