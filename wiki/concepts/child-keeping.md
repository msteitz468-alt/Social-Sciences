# Child-Keeping

## Definition as Coined

In *[[stack-all-our-kin-1974|All Our Kin]]*, **child-keeping** is the Flats term Stack analyzes as temporary (and sometimes permanent) **fosterage within domestic networks** — children move to grandmothers, aunts, friends, and other essential kin who assume parental rights and duties for a period. Stack (attributed): people often regard child-keeping as part of the flux of residence and alliance, not as pathological "broken homes."

## Semantic History

- **Emic:** "gimme a little sugar" / keeping someone's child as ordinary mutual aid.
- **Stack 1974:** links to comparative fosterage literature; distinguishes folk parental rights from state/welfare legal parenthood.
- Contrasts with policy discourse that reads multi-household child residence as family disorganization.

## Operationalizations

Stack's AFDC survey tables (frequency, patterns, expectations, status/location of biological mother) plus residence life histories. She argues AFDC statistics **undercount** actual child-keeping relative to ethnography (attributed range roughly 20–35% in The Flats).

## Applications

[[stack-all-our-kin-study]]; [[domestic-network]] recruitment and sanctions over rights in children.

## Critiques and Limitations

AFDC sample is welfare cases, not all households; "keeping" spans brief babysitting-like stays to de facto adoption — Stack discusses transactions without a fixed legal clock for permanence.
