# Anomaly

## Definition as Coined

In *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*, [[douglas-mary|Douglas]] treats the **anomaly** — that which falls between or outside categories — as structurally produced by classification itself. Dirt is the residual of ordering; ambiguous things "can seem very threatening," and [[taboo]] "confronts the ambiguous and shunts it into the category of the sacred" (2002 preface; attributed).

In Ch. 2 she distinguishes **anomaly** (does not fit a given set of categories) from **ambiguity** (can be read two ways); both threaten pattern. Cultures, she argues, respond with a limited repertoire of treatments (attributed: Douglas 1966), including settling for one interpretation, physically controlling, avoiding, labeling dangerous, or using the anomaly in ritual.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Douglas 1966 Ch. 2–3 | Residual/interstitial case in classification; load-bearing for pollution |
| Douglas 1966 Ch. 3 (Leviticus) | Dietary abominations as taxonomic anomalies (e.g. pig as borderline; swarmers as cross-habitat) — **later repudiated by Douglas** |
| Douglas 2002 | Warns against circularity: one must not infer anomaly *from* prohibition then "find" anomalous traits; anomaly is like similarity — anything may have anomalous features |
| Kuhn / philosophy of science | Different sense (theory-threatening observation) — do not conflate without argument |
| Later anthropology | Interstitial persons, monsters, hybrids, "matter out of place" cases |

## Operationalizations

Map the local taxonomy; identify interstitial cases (dietary, sexual, status, bodily, temporal); record ritual and social responses. Douglas's 2002 methodological caution: **do not** define anomaly solely by the prohibition that is to be explained.

## Applications

- **1966 Leviticus reading** (historical position): clean land animals = cud + cloven hoof model; pig/camel/hyrax as borderlines; swarming creatures as defeating land/water/air classes. *Status:* Douglas later called this a "major mistake" (circularity; weak social-system link; wrong theology of "abominable" creatures) and replaced it with covenant and body–altar modelling (*In the Wilderness* 1993; *Leviticus as Literature* 1999; 2002 preface).
- Interstitial social roles and witchcraft accusation (Ch. 6): witches as "social equivalents of beetles and spiders in the cracks of the walls."
- Creative composting of anomalies (Ch. 10): Lele pangolin cult — anomaly used for power and renewal, not only avoidance.
- Sex-role contradictions generating sexual pollution (Ch. 9).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Circular operationalization risk (Douglas's own 2002 critique of her 1966 practice).
- Not every interstitial case is polluted; systems also **use** formlessness and anomaly creatively.
- Requires thick local categories; free-floating universal "anomaly = sacred" claims (which Douglas associated with some Leachian readings) skip social roots.
