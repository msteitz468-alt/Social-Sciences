# Gimu

## Definition as Coined

[[benedict-ruth|Benedict]] (1946) defines *gimu* as the reciprocal of *[[on-obligation|on]]* in which **even the fullest repayment still falls short** (“one never repays one ten-thousandth”). There is **no time limit**. She groups under *gimu*:

- **chu** — duty to the Emperor, the law, Japan
- **ko** — duty to parents and ancestors (and by implication descendants)
- **nimmu** — duty to one’s work

Both *chu* and *ko* are **compulsory** and “man’s universal lot”; elementary schooling is called “gimu education.” *Gimu* is **never defined as “unwilling”** — contrast *[[giri]]*.

## Semantic History

*Chu* and *ko* are Chinese loan-words, Benedict notes, but Japan made them **unconditional**, departing from Chinese ethics in which *jen* (benevolence / good interpersonal relations) conditions loyalty and filial piety (a ruler without *jen* may righteously be rebelled against). Japanese *gimu*, in her reading, does not accept that touchstone.

## Operationalizations

Typological contrast with *giri* in Benedict’s obligation table; illustrated with family films, marriage stories, and wartime *chu* (including 1945 surrender as repayment of *chu* once the Emperor ordered it).

## Applications

Explains, in Benedict’s account, limitless loyalty, filial duty, and the possibility of a national right-about-face when the Emperor pivots policy.

## Critiques and Limitations

Same cautions as *on*/*giri*: wartime synthesis, elite moral language, risk of national essentialism ([[national-character-debate]]).
