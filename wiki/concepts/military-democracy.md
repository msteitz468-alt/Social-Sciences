# Military Democracy

## Definition as Coined

[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] (attributed, *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]*, Part II) uses **"military democracy"** for the form of government characteristic of gentile society ([[societas-and-civitas|societas]]) in the Upper Status of barbarism — Homeric Greece and early Rome. It is the third of his three governmental stages: (1) a council of chiefs alone (Lower Status of barbarism); (2) council plus a general (with confederacy); (3) council + popular assembly + general (Upper Status). Morgan: "the so-called kingdoms of the heroic age were military democracies, and nothing more." He insists "Monarchy is incompatible with gentilism … it was impossible … for a kingdom to arise by natural growth in any part of the earth under gentile institutions."

## Semantic History

The concept is Morgan's polemical correction of the classical record: he argues the Homeric *basileus* and the Roman *rex* were **elective (or constituency-confirmed) military commanders** with religious and (later) judicial functions but no civil sovereignty — mistranslated as "king." He traces one office-lineage across the ethnical periods: Iroquois "Great War Soldier" → Aztec *Teuctli* → Greek *basileus* → Roman *rex*, all "a general in a military democracy." He derives the modern executive (king/emperor/president) from this war-chief office, the modern senate from the council of chiefs, and the modern representative assembly from the popular assembly. Engels adopted the term in *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|The Origin of the Family]]*.

## Operationalizations

Diagnostic features (attributed): an elective/deposable general rather than a hereditary monarch; a council of chiefs (proto-senate); a popular assembly (agora/*comitia*); decision by these bodies, not by a sovereign; the office checked by design (e.g. the Iroquois dual war-chiefs, compared by Morgan to the Roman dual consuls). Grecian tyrannies are read as **usurpations** alien to gentile principles, not survivals of gentile monarchy.

## Applications

The core of Morgan's dispute with **George Grote**, who characterized primitive Grecian government as monarchical, reposing on personal feeling and divine right; Morgan counters that it "was essentially democratical, reposing on gentes, phratries and tribes … on the principles of liberty, equality and fraternity." Also underlies his deflationary reading of the "Aztec empire" as a confederacy/military democracy rather than a feudal monarchy — a reading he presses against the Spanish chroniclers.

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes` — the term usefully names the non-monarchical, council-and-assembly character of many pre-state polities, and Morgan's democratic reading of Homeric and early Roman government was influential. But it is embedded in his unilineal stage theory (see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]) and asserts a universal impossibility ("monarchy is incompatible with gentilism") in attributed voice, not as an established finding. The office-lineage across Iroquois, Aztec, Greek, and Roman cases rests on the [[gens|type-case universalization]] that later anthropology rejects as method.
