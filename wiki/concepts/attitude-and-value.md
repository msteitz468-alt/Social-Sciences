# Attitude and Value (Thomas–Znaniecki)

## Definition as Coined

In the Methodological Note to *[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|The Polish Peasant in Europe and America]]* (Vol I, 1918), [[thomas-william-isaac|Thomas]] and [[znaniecki-florian|Znaniecki]] introduce **social values** and **attitudes** as the fundamental data of social psychology and sociology (attributed):

- **Social value:** "any datum having an empirical content accessible to the members of some social group and a meaning with regard to which it is or may be an object of activity." Examples range from food and a piece of poetry to a university, a scientific theory, or a myth.
- **Attitude:** "a process of individual consciousness which determines real or possible activity of the individual in the social world" — the individual counterpart of the social value. Speech and gesture express attitudes; the attitude is always fundamentally an attitude *toward something*.

They argue that every attitude corresponds to numerous values and every value to numerous attitudes; science must keep both poles and not subordinate one wholesale to the other as art theory or economics sometimes subordinate attitudes to values (attributed).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Thomas & Znaniecki 1918 | Paired fundamental data for documentary social psychology |
| Interwar American sociology | "Attitude" becomes a survey and scale construct (Thurstone, Likert) often detached from the value pole |
| Blumer 1939 appraisal | Contests how tightly *Polish Peasant* theory is grounded in its documents |
| Symbolic interactionism | "Attitude" partly displaced by meaning, self, and definition of the situation, but the subjective-factor charter remains |

## Operationalizations

In *The Polish Peasant*, operationalization is **interpretive and documentary**, not scalar: letters and life-records are read for attitudes manifested toward familial, economic, religious, and community values. Later psychometrics reused the word "attitude" under different measurement commitments — do not equate Likert scales with the 1918 schema.

## Applications

- Analytic key to the correspondence series in Vol I (familial solidarity, economic aspiration, religious attitudes).
- Bridge from European cultural philosophy (Znaniecki) to American empirical case study (Thomas).
- Ancestor vocabulary for later debates on subjectivity in sociology ([[definition-of-the-situation]], [[symbolic-interactionism]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Theory–data gap (Blumer):** whether the schema is induced from documents or imposed on them — see [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study]]; Blumer's 1969 Ch. 6 appraisal of *Polish Peasant*.
- **Ambiguity of "attitude":** the term's later psychometric career creates anachronistic readings of 1918.
- **Blumer's scientific-concept critique (1955/1969 Ch. 4):** attitude fails to meet requirements of clear instances, class distinction, and cumulative generic knowledge; using attitude as a tendency that *controls* the act begs the question — the act is constructed via [[self-indication]], not released from a readiness (attributed, [[blumer-herbert|Blumer]]). Distinct from Thomas & Znaniecki's attitude–value *pair* as documentary analytic; do not collapse 1918 and mid-century attitude psychology.
- **Vol I coverage:** full deployment across disorganization/reorganization awaits Vols III–V.
