# Anatolian Hypothesis

## Definition as Coined

The **Anatolian hypothesis** is [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s proposal, set out systematically in *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]* (1987), that the principal early dispersal of [[indo-european-languages|Indo-European]] speech into Europe co-travels with the **Neolithic farming expansion** from **Anatolia**, via first European farmers in Greece/Crete, rather than from a later **steppe/Kurgan** homeland. Renfrew states the load-bearing move as going earlier than Kurgan (~3500–3000 BC), Corded Ware, and Beaker unities because none of those complexes is large enough for pan-European IE: "The alternative, then, is to go earlier… This was the adoption of farming" (Renfrew 1987).

The mechanism he prefers is a **demic wave of advance** (allied to Ammerman–[[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]]): bulk of each new farming population not of local mesolithic ancestry; no organized migrations required — "no individual on this model needs to have moved more than forty to sixty kilometres in his or her entire life" — with density rising from ~0.1 to ~5–10 persons/km² (attributed — Renfrew 1987). Language of first Greek farmers ~6500 BC would then be carried and diverge analogously to Latin → Romance.

Anthony (2007) restates the hypothesis as the principal alternative to a Pontic-Caspian homeland dated 4000–3500 BCE: Indo-Hittite / early IE in Anatolian Neolithic (~7000 BCE, e.g. Çatal Höyük); dialect carried to Greece with farming ~6700–6500 BCE; PIE then spreads with agriculture — requiring first Anatolian/PIE split ~6700–6500 BCE, so that by 3500 BCE the IE family should already be bushy and ~3,000 years past any shared wagon vocabulary (Anthony 2007).

## Semantic History

- **1973/1978 sketches** — Renfrew's preface credits earlier articles as origins of the argument.
- **1987 book** — full program: critique of linguistic palaeontology + culture=people equations; three processes of language history (colonization, replacement, continuous development); replacement models (Demography/Subsistence, Élite Dominance, System Collapse); European application; Indo-Iranian dual hypotheses; Celtic ethnogenesis; Dumézil critique; comparative analogies (Polynesia, Bantu).
- **Later label** — "Anatolian hypothesis" is the conventional name in later literature (including Reich 2018 and Anthony 2007) for Renfrew's farming-dispersal position on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]].
- **Post-2007** — Anthony’s wool/wheel critique and, after 2015, aDNA demography (Reich 2018) became the main attributed counters; the hypothesis remains a live position label even where many scholars treat its pan-European farming=IE equation as weakened.

## Core claims (attributed — Renfrew 1987)

1. **Reject pan-European solutions from later complexes.** Kurgan dispersal ~3500–3000 BC, Corded Ware/Beakers ~2900–2000 BC, and Urnfields fail geographic or chronological scope (no Beakers in SE Europe; little Corded Ware in Greece/Italy; Urnfields cannot explain Greek IE before 1500 BC).
2. **Farming is the only sufficiently early pan-European process.** Plant domesticates (and some animals) largely imported; first European farmers in Greece/Crete before 6000 BC (calibrated: Greece before ~6500 BC; Orkney/Europe by ~3500 BC).
3. **Demic over pure acculturation for the primary spread.** Seed-corn genealogy traces to Greek EN and western Anatolia; Renfrew argues human ancestry "broadly" tracks the same path.
4. **Homeland east of Greece.** Seaborne Anatolia→Greece wave (Melos obsidian precedent for pre-farming sea travel); west Anatolian farmers themselves part of a wider advance; candidate nuclear zone further east (e.g. Konya Plain/Çatal Höyük or beyond) as of his 1987 synthesis.
5. **Non-IE pockets as mesolithic-survival tips.** Basque, Etruscan (and lost languages) as denser-coastal or partial-acculturation survivors — "tips of icebergs."
6. **Post-farming replacements are mostly élite dominance**, not second demic waves (Romans, Magyars as historical types; mounted warfare for some later east-European episodes).
7. **Indo-Iranian more complex** — Hypothesis A (early farming wave) and Hypothesis B (later mounted steppe élite dominance) both left open; Indus speech by 3000 BC could already have been IE (his position, not consensus).
8. **De-emphasize shared "IE society."** Original farming communities modeled as egalitarian/segmentary; warrior aristocracies and hierarchical chiefdoms cannot be original shared PIE heritage — challenge to [[dumezil-georges|Dumézil]]-style reconstructions.

## Critiques and Limitations

### Anthony 2007 — wagon vocabulary and related counters (attributed)

*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* is the primary steppe-side critique of the Anatolian/first-farming model. Key counter-arguments, all Anthony’s positions:

1. **Wagon vocabulary as fatal chronological mismatch.** Late PIE reconstructs a five-term wagon/wheel semantic field (*kwékwlos, *rot-eh₂-, axle, thill, *wégheti) that cannot predate wheeled vehicles (~4000–3500 BCE archaeological appearance). If first farmers carried IE to Greece ~6700–6500 BCE, by 3500 BCE daughter languages should already have diverged for millennia and could not share this inherited package. **“The wagon vocabulary is incompatible with the first-farming/language-dispersal hypothesis”** (Anthony 2007). Wagon terms are not post-PIE loans: they lack inter-branch loan sound patterns and show inheritance from PIE; if these classic reconstructions were false, comparative method would be useless (Anthony 2007).

2. **Wool vocabulary reinforces the late *terminus post quem*.** Woven wool textiles require long-fiber mutant sheep; broad Near Eastern and European wool-butchering patterns appear after ~3400/3300 BCE (with earlier tantalizing hints). PIE sheep lexicon and *HwlHn- ‘wool’ (cognates almost all branches including Hittite) place speakers in a world where wool was speakable after the secondary-products horizon — not in earliest Neolithic farming (Anthony 2007).

3. **Rejection of independent *kwel→wheel creation.** Against Gray’s suggestion that *kwékwlos could form independently in each branch from *kwel- ‘turn’: multiple PIE turn/roll roots exist; *kwel- would not freeze unchanged for millennia while the rest of the vocabulary changed (Anthony 2007).

4. **Rejection of Renfrew’s slow-evolution Stage 1–2 model.** A pan-European PIE dialect chain lasting ~6500–3000 BCE with late wagon vocabulary requires unrealistically uniform and slow change; contradicts Neolithic material-culture diversity (Childe’s “bewildering diversity”); and exceeds plausible tribal language territories under foot transport (Mallory/Nettles scales: most West African cultivator languages <10,000 km²; even generous 250–500k km² implies 20–40 language families in Neolithic Europe). Bantu diversification after ~2,000 years yields 500+ languages — Europe by 3500 BCE should have been highly diverse, not one PIE (Anthony 2007).

5. **First Anatolian farmers likely not IE speakers.** First Neolithic farmers of Anatolia likely migrated from northern Syria and probably spoke **Afro-Asiatic**; if so, Renfrew’s first-farming hypothesis should spread *that* language to Greece, not PIE (Anthony 2007).

6. **Anatolian IE internal pattern does not match Neolithic time-depth.** Earliest documented Anatolian IE languages (Hittite, Palaic, Luwian) showed little diversity; only Luwian had many speakers by 1500 BCE; all borrowed extensively from older prestigious non-IE (Hattic, Hurrian) — not the pattern expected if evolving in Anatolia since the Neolithic. Early Hittite/Palaic speakers were intruders among Hattic city/kingdom populations (Anthony 2007). Melchert places Proto-Anatolian ~3400 BCE from Hittite–Luwian divergence comparable to modern Welsh/Irish (~2,000 years); Melchert and Lehrman agree Pre-Anatolian separation from archaic PIE ~4000 BCE — far later than Renfrew’s ~6700–6500 BCE farming split (Anthony 2007).

7. **Gray–Atkinson phylogenetic dates do not dissolve the problem.** Anthony reports Gray and Atkinson cladistic dating (Pre-Anatolian ~6700 BCE ±1200; Pre-Tocharian ~5900; etc.) as matching Anatolian farming colonization of Greece chronologically, but raising the same **wagon-vocabulary synchronization** problem for a deep Neolithic PIE (Anthony 2007).

8. **Weak Caucasian/Anatolian homeland case on loans.** Many proposed Semitic→PIE loans rejected; accepted loans could travel far; Nichols shows PIE/Kartvelian/Semitic contacts were indirect via unknown intermediaries; Uralic relations deeper than Caucasian (Anthony 2007).

Anthony’s positive alternative: Pontic-Caspian steppe homeland ~4500–2500 BCE; Pre-Anatolian as early *westward* split from archaic steppe PIE ~4200–3900 BCE (before wagons), not as the farming homeland of the whole family — see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] Position A and [[indo-european-languages]].

### Other critiques and limitations

- **Steppe counter-position** ([[gimbutas-marija|Gimbutas]]; Mallory; Anthony 2007 primary; genetic demography per Reich 2018): later steppe migration as main European IE vector; wagon vocabulary and branch structure as linguistic constraints — see debate page.
- **Reich 2018:** Anatolian hypothesis “has lost its best evidence” — its premise that no later movement was demographically large enough for a second IE wave is contradicted by Corded Ware/steppe aDNA (Reich’s assessment as rival proponent, not wiki adjudication).
- **West Mediterranean vulnerability:** Renfrew admits his theory is "probably… incompatible" with strong indigenous western Mediterranean Neolithic models (Barker, Lewthwaite) (attributed — Renfrew 1987).
- **Pre-aDNA horizon:** 1987 demography lacks nuclear ancient DNA; genetic assessments belong to later sources (as of Reich 2018 and subsequent aDNA).
- **Four-way non-identity:** farming package ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people; Renfrew himself insists language ≠ race ≠ culture (citing Max Müller); Anthony likewise rejects equating language with pottery packages.
- **Glottochronology:** Renfrew rejects fixed Swadesh rates as decisive; would need ~2× rate change to fit farming chronology (attributed). Anthony reports Kruskal–Black most frequent first split ~3000 BCE — “in the ballpark” for a late PIE, not a 7th-millennium one (Anthony 2007).
- **First-farmers-spoke-IE is "sheer hypothesis"** — Renfrew’s own framing of the load-bearing linguistic leap (Renfrew 1987).

## Reflexivity

A late Critical Turn product of British processual archaeology: **anti-migrationist** against culture-historical Kurgan/Corded equations, yet **demographically migrationist** where farming expands. Reacts against Kossinna–Childe–Gimbutas migration packages and against wartime racial misuse of IE origins, while reopening language as a legitimate archaeological problem. National and identity stakes make attributed voice and debate-page treatment mandatory. Anthony’s 2007 critique is itself a synthesis product of steppe archaeology + comparative linguistics, written **before** nuclear aDNA confirmed large-scale steppe demography — his wagon/wool arguments stand or fall as linguistic-chronological claims independent of genetics; the aDNA layer (Reich 2018) is a separate later densification of the rival Position A.
