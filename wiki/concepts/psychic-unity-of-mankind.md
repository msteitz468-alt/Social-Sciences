# Psychic Unity of Mankind

## Definition as Coined

The doctrine that the human mind is everywhere essentially the same in its basic constitution and operations, so that similar cultural forms can arise **independently** in different societies facing similar conditions. The German ethnologist Adolf Bastian gave it its classic formulation (*Elementargedanken*, "elementary ideas"); [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] built *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* on it as a working assumption. Tylor grounded his comparative method on "the uniformity which so largely pervades civilization," "ascribed, in great measure, to the uniform action of uniform causes" — like producing like because minds are alike. (Attributed: a founding assumption of evolutionist anthropology, not a settled finding.)

## Semantic History

For Tylor the doctrine had a double edge, both **anti-racist for its day** and **evolutionist**: it asserted the fundamental mental equality of all peoples (against polygenist race-science that held "savages" to be a different, lower kind of human) — the "savage" is a rational philosopher reasoning correctly from limited data — while simultaneously licensing the ranking of *cultures* (not minds) on a single developmental ladder, since uniform minds pass through uniform stages. Later anthropology split these: [[boas-franz|Boasians]] kept psychic unity but used it *against* evolutionism — *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* states "no fundamental difference in the ways of thinking of primitive and civilized man" while denying that shared processes entail a unilinear cultural ladder; similar traits need not mean parallel independent invention (diffusion or accident); cognitive and structural anthropology (Lévi-Strauss's universal structures of mind) gave it new, non-evolutionist forms; evolutionary psychology revives a version as a universal evolved architecture.

## Operationalizations

Psychic unity functions as the **enabling premise of the [[comparative-method]]**: only if minds are uniform can a custom in one society illuminate a similar custom in an unrelated one, and can independent parallel invention be assumed. Denying it (or admitting diffusion) undercuts the inference from cross-cultural similarity to parallel development — which is precisely the Boasian move.

## Applications

Underwrites all of Tylor's comparison of "lower tribes" with "higher nations" and his reconstruction of universal culture-stages; also his intellectualist theory of religion ([[animism]]), which requires that the "primitive philosopher" reason as any human would.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics note the doctrine does too much: it makes **independent invention** the default explanation of cross-cultural similarity and thereby underrates **diffusion** and historical contact (the diffusionist and Boasian objection). It also slides between the defensible claim (humans share a mental constitution) and the evolutionist inference (therefore cultures climb a single ladder), which does not follow. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[comparative-method]], [[historical-particularism]], [[race-and-culture-debate]].

## Stocking's thesis: a shift in function, Prichard to Tylor (1987)

Stocking's central historiographical argument in [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|*Victorian Anthropology*]] (1987) is that "psychic unity" did not enter classical evolutionism as a stable idea but **changed function** as it passed from [[prichard-james-cowles|Prichard]]'s ethnology to Tylorian evolutionism (attributed). For Prichard, uniformities of religion and custom across geographically and racially distant peoples were used as an **endpoint** — evidence *for* the single origin (monogenesis) of the human species, within a biblical-chronology and biblical-anthropology framework. "In contrast to E. B. Tylor, for whom psychic unity was a necessary premise in tracing a uniform sequence in the evolution of religion, Prichard used uniformities of religion to establish the psychic unity of man" (Stocking 1987). For Tylor and the classical evolutionists, by contrast, psychic unity became a **starting premise** — no longer a conclusion supporting common origin, but the enabling assumption that let cross-cultural similarity be read as independent parallel invention, licensing reconstruction of a universal developmental sequence via the [[comparative-method]].

Stocking argues (attributed, following John Burrow) that this shift let classical evolutionism preserve the doctrine of human unity while nonetheless incorporating a substantial body of racialist assumption: rather than treating races as aboriginally and irreducibly distinct (the polygenist position — see [[polygenism]]), evolutionism located racial and cultural mental differences **within evolutionary time**, so that "mankind was one... not because it was everywhere the same, but because the differences represented stages in the same process" (Burrow, quoted by Stocking). Herbert Spencer's evolutionary associationism supplied the period's most influential mechanism for this reconciliation, but at a cost: because Spencer's Lamarckian scheme allowed habitual cultural practice to become hereditary racial instinct, the analytic line between "racial" and "cultural" determinism collapsed for much of the evolutionist generation — race and culture became, in effect, mutually convertible categories rather than independent variables (attributed; Stocking notes this collapse did not apply uniformly, citing Francis Galton as a non-Lamarckian "social Darwinist" for whom race and culture remained comparatively less interactive).
