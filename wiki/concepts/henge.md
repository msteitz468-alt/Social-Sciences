# Henge

## Definition as Coined

A **henge** is a class of Late Neolithic ceremonial enclosure in Britain and Ireland, classically defined by a roughly circular bank-and-ditch circuit (often **ditch inside, bank outside** — the reverse of many defensive earthworks), with one or more entrances. Internal features may include timber circles, stone settings, pits, or later burials. The type-name is a back-formation from [[stonehenge|Stonehenge]] (itself atypical in several respects).

## Semantic History

Culture-historical type lists; processual social archaeology (territorial/ceremonial centers); post-processual and landscape readings of performance, access, and astronomy. [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] (1998, Chs. 7–9) places henges in a longer sequence from [[passage-grave|passage graves]] and circular ceremonial forms through increasingly controlled or appropriated spaces, and argues that equating henges and stone circles as mere material substitutes (earth vs stone by geology) is insufficient — landscape setting and permeability matter.

## Operationalizations

Bank/ditch polarity; entrance number; internal architecture; dating (often multi-phase); relationship to cursus, avenues, and field systems.

## Applications

Wessex ceremonial complexes ([[avebury]], Stonehenge landscape); Orkney comparisons; Early Bronze Age reuse as burial foci ("closed circles").

## Bradley 1998 — arena, permeability, landscape (attributed)

**Classic attributes:** External bank + internal ditch; Late Neolithic; structured deposits; secondary cremations; Grooved Ware; elaborated entrances; timber circles interiors. Many features originate in northern Britain (Harding & Lee via Bradley) — contrast southern concentration of most causewayed enclosures.

**Arena thesis:** Bank outside, ditch separating interior actors from bank audience; limited entrances screen interior — threefold space: (1) interior participants; (2) bank observers; (3) those denied any view. Irish embanked enclosures often lack ditch (softer division).

**Against Burl’s geology equivalence:** Freestanding stone circles are not mere rock-zone substitutes for henges; rubble banks *can* be built where chosen (Mayburgh); practical geology does not explain freestanding circles.

**Experience contrast:**
- **Henge insider** (Avebury exemplar): cut off from horizon by massive earthwork; world-in-itself; outsider on bank sees landscape and skyline barrows.
- **Permeable stone circle** (Orkney Stenness/Brodgar; Castlerigg): integrates horizon, water, other monuments; monolith height may mirror distant hills (Richards via Bradley).

**Orkney problem for typology:** Stenness and Brodgar among earliest dated “henges” but **no secure outer bank** — problematic for bank-defined typology; Stenness has no earlier timber circle (unlike Stonehenge-type secondary stone-for-timber).

**Chronology:** Latest pottery from henges normally Beaker phase; freestanding circle finds can extend later but often secondary burials.

**Sequence link:** From [[passage-grave]] exteriors (platforms, monolith rings, external art/quartz) → open circular arenas → EBA closure/appropriation ([[cairnpapple]]; Ch. 9).

## Critiques and Limitations

Definitions vary (Class I/II; "henge enclosure" vs "henge monument"); many sites are multi-phase and resist single-function labels. Always cite excavation sequence.
