# Gumlao

## Definition as Coined

**Gumlao** is a Kachin political category analyzed by [[leach-edmund|Leach]] (1954) as the **egalitarian, anti-hereditary-chief** ideal of hill political order — the polar opposite of hierarchical [[gumsa]]. Leach's brief formula: gumsa regard society as ordered by an hereditary aristocracy; gumlao repudiate all notions of hereditary class difference.

Gumlao communities, in the ideal, refuse thigh-eating chiefly dues and hereditary *duwa* rank; they may still have headmen and ritual specialists. Leach stresses that gumlao is not a separate "tribe": of two lineages of the same clan, one may be gumsa and another gumlao; languages are shared; conversion both ways appears in myth and history.

## Semantic History

- **Colonial labels:** *Kamlao* / rebel villages vs *kamsa* / gumsa; administrators who met the system on its merits were "few and far between" (Leach).
- **Mythic charter:** Origin stories (e.g. via Kawlu Ma Nawng) cast the archetype gumlao leader as priest (*dumsa*), blacksmith (*n-dup*), and child of a levirate wife of an elder brother in a chiefly lineage — a minor aristocrat of ambition whom ultimogeniture somewhat defrauds.
- **Leach 1954–64:** Gumlao as ideal type in [[gumsa-gumlao-oscillation]]; same verbal categories, opposite valuations; modern parties (c. 1952) can re-label older gumsa/gumlao hostilities.

## Operationalizations

Ideal-type contrasts with gumsa on domain, class, *hka* (debt/feud), marriage rules, succession, land title, and ritual (Leach's side-by-side tabulation, Ch. VI). Empirical gumlao areas may re-hierarchize; "gumlao" can be a **symbol of hostility** between neighbours rather than a fixed constitution.

## Applications

[[gumsa-gumlao-oscillation]]; Kachin historical reconstruction (Hukawng, Triangle, Duleng, etc.); models of rebellion when mayu–dama rank is overplayed.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of collapsing diverse anti-chief movements into one type; colonial under-documentation; later historiography may dispute timing and frequency of "spontaneous" gumlao emergence (Leach already notes skepticism that gumlao first appeared only c. 1870).
