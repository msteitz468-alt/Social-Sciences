# Extensive Power

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]*, 1986, Ch. 1): **extensive power** is the ability to organize large numbers of people over far-flung territories in order to engage in minimally stable cooperation. Paired with [[intensive-power]]; primary structures of society combine both.

> "Extensive power refers to the ability to organize large numbers of people over far-flung territories in order to engage in minimally stable cooperation."

Cross-classified with [[authoritative-power]]/[[diffused-power]]: extensive+authoritative ≈ militaristic empire; extensive+diffused ≈ market exchange (Fig. 1.1 ideal types).

## Semantic History

Part of Mann's organizational-logistical methodology of power history within the [[iemp-model]]. Not to be confused with world-systems "extensive" production or Braudelian expanse without the organizational definition. Draws on Owen Lattimore's three radii of extensive integration (military action most extensive; civil administration less; economic integration least for staples until late medieval/early modern Europe — Mann qualifies and criticizes equating integration with extent alone).

## Operationalizations

Logistical radius of stable cooperation: military march ranges (~90 km routine control limit for much of agrarian history, Mann), trade networks, religious ecumenes, imperial posts, diplomatic systems. Artillery, printing, and ocean navigation (~1450–1500) change extensive power worldwide in his European chapters.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann across *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Empires** — Assyrian/Persian extensive reach vs low intensive mobilization; Persian empire >3,000 km E–W as extreme extensive claim with loose suzerainty; Rome's invention of extensive territorial citizenship adding to intensive Greek-style core.
- **Ideology** — Civilizational cultures/religions often more extensive than other power organizations; Catholic Christendom ~1 million km² as most extensive medieval network; Christian ecumene combines extensive + intensive, largely [[diffused-power]].
- **Economy** — Market exchange as extensive+diffused; early feudal dynamic primarily intensive because extensive power already provided by Christendom (Mann Ch. 12); early modern capitalism and national states as coordinated extensive alliance.
- **Geopolitics** — Multistate civilization as extensive political-diplomatic organization without single hegemonic state.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Minimal stability" is qualitative; empirical estimates depend on secondary logistics literature Mann cites (including the ~90 km figure). Keep as Mann's comparative dimension.
