# Professional Revolutionary

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lenin-what-is-to-be-done-1902|What Is to Be Done?]]* (1902), especially Section IV, [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] defined the **organization of revolutionaries** as consisting “first and foremost of people whose profession is that of a revolutionary.” Distinctions between workers and intellectuals, and of trade/profession, must be **obliterated** inside that organization: what matters is professional revolutionary qualification. He contrasted this with the **organization of workers** for economic struggle—trade organization, as wide as possible, as public as conditions allow.

Average mass workers, he granted, can display enormous energy in strikes and street battles and determine the movement’s outcome; but struggle against the **political police** requires special qualities—**professional revolutionaries**. The task is not only that masses advance concrete demands but that “the masses of the workers ‘advance’ an increasing number of such professional revolutionaries from their own ranks.”

Famous programmatic paraphrase at the close of IV.C: “Give us an organization of revolutionaries, and we shall overturn the whole of Russia!”

## Semantic History

| Layer | Sense |
|---|---|
| 1897 St. Petersburg League dispute (as Lenin recounts) | “Old” members: first consolidate League of Struggle into **organization of revolutionaries** controlling funds and circles; “young” defended published workers’-fund rules |
| Primitive methods / *kustar* work | Spontaneous circle growth → leaflets, local paper, demonstrations → wholesale arrests; no continuous organization with past continuity |
| Five propositions (IV.C) | Stable leaders for continuity; more mass spontaneity → more need for such organization; chiefly professional revolutionaries; under autocracy restrict membership to professionally trained fighters of political police → harder to catch, **wider** active circle outside |
| Narodovolist charge (Lenin’s rebuttal) | Militant centralized organization not specifically Narodovolist; Zemlya i Volya tradition; no serious fighting tendency can dispense with it; mass rise **imposes** the duty of equal or better organization |
| “Amateur” vs revolutionary | Weak theory, narrow outlook, trade-union-secretary type, inexperienced vs police = “wretched amateur”; task is exalt amateurs to revolutionaries, not degrade revolutionaries to amateurs |

(An anthology **editor** headnote paraphrases Bolshevism as “authoritarian organization of dedicated professional revolutionaries”; that is editorial voice, not Lenin’s Section I–II wording—prefer Lenin’s own organizational chapters for the concept.)

## Operationalizations

Lenin’s organizational rules (attributed):

1. **Begin** with solid foundation of strong organization of revolutionaries (not with elaborate wide workers’ constitutions first)—else gendarmes get the “most accessible” forms and Zubatov-type legal unions remain what masses can join.
2. Small compact core of reliable, experienced, hardened workers with secret agents in principal districts can perform trade-union functions **as Social-Democrats desire** without elaborate mass membership apparatus under autocracy.
3. Centralize **secret** functions in few professionals; keep many **loose/public** organizations (trade unions, self-education, democratic circles) **distinct**—do not erase the line of demarcation.
4. Only serious organizational principles under autocracy: **strict secrecy, strict selection of members, training of professional revolutionaries**—not “broad democratic principles” (full publicity + election to all functions), which he called a “useless and harmful toy” under gendarme selection.
5. Working-class agitators: Party **maintain** talented workers; they may go underground and change place of activity—must not be left eleven hours a day in the factory if they are to enlarge experience and survive gendarmes.
6. Specialization + centralization; shift weight from local absorption to **national** work; all-Russian organization of revolutionaries to lead all-Russian trade unions and an all-Russian newspaper apparatus (large “factory,” not handicraft local press).

## Applications

- **Critique of St. Petersburg 1897/1900 rules:** detailed wide workers’ organization confused with organization of revolutionaries; SD reduced to “executive groups” for economic struggle.
- **Against Svoboda’s “dozen wise men vs hundred fools” demagogy:** conclusion should be a **committee of professional revolutionaries** (student or worker capable of qualifying)—German mass movement values tried leaders (Bebel, Liebknecht), he argued.
- **Against Economism and *kustar* methods:** restricted work cannot build good revolutionary organization; both opportunist “easy” economic organization and “excitative” terror bow to primitivity.
- **Continuity crisis:** B——v and Lenin: growth of labor movement outstrips revolutionary organizations; leaders lag spontaneous rising—see [[spontaneity-vs-consciousness-debate]], [[vanguard-party]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Democratic / anti-“conspirative” counter-position (as Lenin records opponents):** fear of Narodovolism; mass cannot proceed by secret paths; “broad democratic principles” in party rules; contempt for secret struggle with police as mistaken for political struggle; Svoboda-style appeal from leaders to the “crowd.”
- **Premature-attack fear:** Lenin answered that strong organization **safeguards against** premature attacks and prepares successful ones; without it, unsound Economism and unsound terror meet.
- **Evidence class:** programmatic design under tsarist illegality—not a universal sociology of parties. Secrecy-subordination of other conditions is explicitly tied to **autocracy**.
- **Later reception (beyond this source):** the professional-revolutionary formula became a global template and a standing democratic critique of Leninism; those afterlives are not primary-extracted here—record as open on [[spontaneity-vs-consciousness-debate]].
