# Expressive Symbols

## Definition as Coined

In [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s classification of cultural pattern types (belief systems / expressive symbols / [[value-orientation]] standards, keyed to the cognitive/cathectic/evaluative modes), **expressive symbols** are "that part of the cultural tradition relative to which expressive interests … have primacy" — the symbolism of the **communication of affect** (*The Social System*, Chapter IX). Expressive primacy is not hedonism: "the essential point is the primacy of 'acting out' the need-disposition itself rather than subordinating gratification to a goal outside the immediate situation." Expressive symbol-systems stand to expressive action as belief-systems stand to instrumental action; Parsons distinguished **artistic creation** (instrumental production of new expressive symbols) from expressive action proper (acting out through existing symbolic patterns).

Parsons located the prototype of the expressive symbol within interaction: a discrete act acquires "superadded" meaning through association, becoming symbolic of the actor's attitude — "the response of the mother to the crying of a child comes … to be felt as 'symbolic' of her attitude." He explicitly credited **Durkheim's** criterion of a symbol: its meaning is "superadded" to its intrinsic properties.

## Semantic History

Parsons classified expressive-symbolic objects into four types (the persons of ego and alter; their bodies; environing physical objects such as clothing and premises; and cultural objects, paradigmatically works of art), cross-cut by pattern-variable-defined attachment types (receptiveness-response, love, approval, esteem). At the collectivity level he distinguished three forms of shared expressive symbolism — common "style," purely expressive collective observance ("recreation," e.g. birthdays), and evaluative/moral **ritual** marked by "solemnity" — reconciling **Durkheim's** theory of ritual (symbolizing group solidarity) and **Malinowski's** (funeral ceremonial as management of individual emotional strain) as *complementary* components of his own scheme rather than rivals.

The concept fed forward into the later Parsonian analysis of culture and into cultural sociology's interest in symbolism and ritual, though Parsons's specific action-theoretic vocabulary did not survive as a live research idiom.

## Operationalizations

An analytic classification, not a measure; expressive symbolism is identified by the primacy of cathexis and the "superadded" quality of meaning.

## Applications

- Parsons's treatment of **symbolic leadership** (the British monarch "reigning" vs. "governing"; Weber's [[charisma|charismatic]] leadership and its routinization), of **projection/scapegoating** (crediting R.F. Bales on projection), and of the **artist's** role as the expressive counterpart of the scientist.
- Cross-links to religious symbolism and to Parsons's account of [[ideology]] and belief systems (Chapter VIII).

## Critiques and Limitations

As part of the Parsonian cultural apparatus, the concept shares the general charge of over-schematization; its four-fold and pattern-variable cross-classifications were criticized as taxonomy outrunning evidence.
