# Organized Irresponsibility

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] used
**organized irresponsibility** for the structural situation in which the scale and interlocking
of corporate, military, and political hierarchies allow consequential decisions and non-decisions
without a clear locus of public accountability. Responsibility is diffused across organizations
even as effective power concentrates. He closed the book arguing that elite success proceeds
"within the American system of organized irresponsibility."

## Semantic History

The phrase ties the institutional analysis of the [[power-elite]] to the moral diagnosis of the
[[higher-immorality]] and to the political architecture of [[mass-society]] (no checking public).
Mills linked it to the ideology of the [[theory-of-balance]]: if power is imagined as automatic
equilibrium among many interests, no one is responsible for outcomes. In the conservative-mood
chapter he associated mindlessness of the powerful and "romantic pluralism" with unaccountability.

## Operationalizations

Not a scale. Indicators in Mills's prose (attributed):

- Hierarchical diffusion of decision (committees, staffs, official secrets)
- Denial of power by men of power ("public service" rhetoric)
- Middle-level stalemate that absorbs attention while top decisions go undebated
- Structural immorality: white-collar crime, campaign finance, corruption as symptoms of a
  system, not odd events ("Well, another one got caught today")

## Applications

- Ethical and political capstone of *The Power Elite*
- Connects to *White Collar*'s political indifference: a mass that cannot hold the top to account
- Used in later social criticism as a label for elite unaccountability (keep later uses attributed)

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics charge moralism or underspecified causal links between hierarchy and "irresponsibility."
The concept is diagnostic and polemical — always attributed; never a wiki-voice finding that
"America is organized irresponsibility."
