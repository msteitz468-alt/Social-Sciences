# Maternal Thinking (under scarcity)

## Definition as Coined

**Maternal thinking** was coined by the philosopher Sara Ruddick (1980, 1989) for the distinctive
practical reasoning that arises from the work of mothering — organized around the demands of preserving,
nurturing, and training a child, and around dispositions such as "holding" (world-protection,
world-preservation, world-repair) and "maternal optimism." [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]
adopts the term as the "touchstone" of *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without
Weeping]]* (Chapter 8) but reworks it for conditions of chronic scarcity and high
**[[infant-mortality]]**, proposing a "pragmatics of motherhood" in place of a "poetics of motherhood":
"these shantytown women create their own culture, but they do not create it just as they please."

## Semantic History

Ruddick's concept was framed within, and largely from, the modern Western family. Scheper-Hughes's
critical reworking argues that Ruddick's account risks making "mother love appear altogether natural" and
that a singular "womanly ethic of care" can "doubly marginalize poor women." On the Alto, she argues,
Ruddick's "maternal optimism" gives way to "maternal pessimism," and "holding" to its opposite, **letting
go** — a "calm and reasonable resignation" (*"deixe as coisas como são…"*) she links to the Christian
notion of "holy indifference," not to peasant fatalism. She positively retains one Ruddick claim — that
"all mothers-in-the-world are adoptive," i.e., that nurture is an active, socially variable decision, not
an automatic consequence of birth.

## Operationalizations

The concept is operationalized through the pattern of **delayed attachment**: infants treated as
provisional "visitors," often unnamed or unbaptized until about a year, referred to generically as *nenê*
("baby"), granted full personhood ("*formada*") only gradually (a "delayed anthropomorphization,"
borrowing O.W. James). Grief and the term *saudade* were reserved for children who had accrued a *história*;
*pena* (pity, as for animals) was used for dead infants. This grades directly into
**[[mortal-selective-neglect]]** and the **[[political-economy-of-the-emotions]]**.

## Applications

- **Situated ethics.** Drawing on forced-choice analogues (the Judgment of Solomon; a father made to
  select a son from Nazi execution), Scheper-Hughes argues maternal moral reasoning under scarcity departs
  from abstract universalist ethics (Kohlberg) toward concrete, relational triage — reframing Gilligan's
  "different voice" as inflected by class position more than by gender.
- **Critique of Winnicott.** She reads D.W. Winnicott's "good enough mother" and "holding" as presupposing
  a security absent on the Alto, and notes he is "strangely silent on the topic of the 'good enough' baby."

## Critiques and Limitations

The reworked concept is the crux of the [[mother-love-scarcity-debate]]: whether scarcity genuinely reshapes
maternal *feeling* (Scheper-Hughes) or whether observed detachment masks a suppressed grief (Nations &
Rebhun). Ruddick and cultural-feminist readers may resist the charge that "maternal thinking" naturalizes
mother love. All substantive claims are attributed.
