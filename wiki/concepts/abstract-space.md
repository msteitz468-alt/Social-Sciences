# Abstract Space

Lefebvre’s term for the dominant spatial form associated with capitalism/neocapitalism in *[[lefebvre-production-of-space-1991|The Production of Space]]*.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Lefebvre.)* Abstract space takes over from historical space of accumulation as labour becomes abstract social labour. It functions “objectally” as things/signs and formal relationships — “glass and stone, concrete and steel, angles and curves.” Formal and quantitative, it erases distinctions of nature, historical time, and body (age, sex, ethnicity). Dominant centres mould peripheral spaces, often violently. Lived experience is “crushed, vanquished by what is 'conceived of'.” Pseudo-subject concealing the real subject = state/political power. Buttressed by positive knowledge, violence, and bureaucracy.

**Paradox (later chapters):** abstract space is at once the set of locations where contradictions are generated, the medium in which they evolve and tear space apart, and the means by which they are smothered under an appearance of consistency.

**Formants** (Ch. 4 elaboration): geometric, optical-visual, and phallic; homogeneity is a *goal* of practice, not a given essence. Homogenization may be more political than economic — “abstract space is a tool of power” (later chapters).

## Semantic History

Core of Lefebvre’s history of space after [[absolute-space|absolute]] and historical spaces. Anglophone geography often pairs it with Harvey’s spatial fix / [[time-space-compression]] without full identity of mechanisms — keep attributions separate.

## Operationalizations

Interpretive: grids, zoning, real-estate abstraction, planned new towns, tourist-leisure spaces, motorways/airports/information lattices, Haussmannian or Brasilia-type planning. Not a single quantitative index.

## Applications

Critique of urbanism and planning as producers of conceived space; analysis of contradictions (centre/periphery, housing vs residence, leisure as consumption of space) and openings toward [[differential-space]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Catch-all risk for “modern urban ills”; under-specified homogeneity; global South undertheorized in the charter text.
