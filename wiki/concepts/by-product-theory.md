# By-Product Theory of Pressure Groups

[[olson-mancur|Mancur Olson]]'s (1965, ch. VI) positive theory of why some large
economic groups nonetheless sustain powerful lobbies despite the
[[free-rider-problem]]. The **by-product theory** holds that the large pressure
groups that *do* exist are by-products of organizations whose primary,
non-political function supplies the [[selective-incentives|selective incentives]]
— coercion or private benefits — needed to hold a [[latent-group|large latent
group]] together. Their political power "does not derive from their lobbying
achievements, but is rather a by-product of their other activities." The
**special-interest theory** is Olson's companion account for concentrated
business lobbies.

## Definition as Coined

Olson (1965): the large organized economic lobbies (labor unions, farm
organizations, professional associations) share one feature distinguishing them
from unorganized latent groups (consumers, white-collar workers, migrant labor)
— "these groups are also organized for some other purpose. The large and powerful
economic lobbies are in fact the by-products of organizations that obtain their
strength and support because they perform some function in addition to lobbying
for collective goods." Only an organization with a non-political function can
make a **"tied sale"** — bundling a collective (lobbying) good with a
non-collective (private) benefit — that induces a rational member of a large
group to bear part of the lobbying cost.

The **special-interest theory** explains concentrated business lobbies
differently: industries are typically divided into oligopoly-sized groups (a few
large firms), which are small enough to count as
[[latent-group|privileged or intermediate groups]] and can organize voluntarily
without selective incentives — so business's disproportionate lobbying strength
reflects group *size*, not by-product benefits.

## Semantic History

The theory was framed against the pluralist "group theory" (Bentley, Truman,
Latham, Commons) that Olson accused of the "anarchistic fallacy." Where pluralism
saw lobbies as the natural expression of shared interest, Olson explained them as
side-effects of organizations built for coercion (the union shop) or private
service (insurance, journals). The concept was later absorbed into interest-group
theory and resource-mobilization accounts of social movements, which likewise
locate organizational capacity in resource streams external to the cause itself.

## Operationalizations

The test is whether an organization controls a non-political function generating
excludable benefits or coercive leverage. Olson's cases: labor unions (power as a
by-product of collective bargaining and the employer-enforceable union shop);
the AMA (by-product of organized medicine — the *Journal*, malpractice defense,
hospital-staffing control); farm bureaus (government-created selective incentives
via county agents; the "Kirkpatrick" cooperative model tying patronage dividends
to political membership); business trade associations (statistics, credit,
technical research). Counter-case: the National Farmers Organization, which
eschewed such institutions and struggled to mobilize — which Olson offered as a
potential future test of the theory.

## Applications

Explains the observed roster of powerful economic lobbies and the absence of
lobbies for the "forgotten groups" (consumers, taxpayers). Frames analyses of
why concentrated producer interests out-organize diffuse consumer interests in
regulatory politics, and informs resource-mobilization theory in the study of
social movements.

## Critiques and Limitations

Olson conceded the theory is "less helpful in some cases than others" and "not at
all sufficient" for philanthropic, religious, and ideological "mass movement"
organizations, where the link between individual motive and organizational
purpose is "so rich and obscure" that the economic theory offers little; he noted
the theory could be rescued by positing comprehensive psychological motives (a
"warm-glow" moral benefit) only at the cost of making it unfalsifiable. Critics
of the broader programme (Green and Shapiro 1994) extend this to the by-product
theory's dependence on an elastic notion of [[selective-incentives]] (see
[[free-rider-problem]], [[collective-action-theory]]).
