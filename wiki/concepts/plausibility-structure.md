# Plausibility Structure

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (1966, Part Three): a **plausibility structure** is the specific social base and social processes required for the maintenance of a given subjective reality (or of a [[symbolic-universe]]). Subjective reality is socially sustained; collapse or withdrawal of the plausibility structure threatens that reality.

Key attributed claims:

- Conversation with significant others is the most important vehicle of reality-maintenance.
- Within the plausibility structure the individual feels “at home”; outside it, doubt and alternative definitions press in.
- **Alternation** (radical transformation of subjective reality, e.g. religious conversion) requires a new plausibility structure (novitiate, community, re-socialization) and typically nihilation or reinterpretation of the old biography.
- Therapy is difficult without an adequate plausibility structure backing the therapeutic definition of reality.

## Semantic History

Coinage closely associated with this treatise and Berger’s subsequent sociology of religion (not yet ingested). Related ideas: reference groups; Meadian significant others; “social base” of belief — but B&L’s term stresses the **maintenance of reality itself**, not only of attitudes.

## Operationalizations

Qualitative: networks, congregations, total environments that make a worldview “real.” Not a quantitative index in the 1966 text.

## Applications

Conversion and re-socialization; sects and counter-worlds; identity change; maintenance of minority realities in pluralistic societies; crisis vs routine conversation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Can slide toward social determinism of belief (“only the group makes it true”); B&L also allow partial transformations and “cool alternation.” Power and coercion in imposing plausibility structures are underplayed relative to conversation and identification.
