# Fang (方) Polity

## Definition as Coined

In Shang [[oracle-bone-inscription|oracle-bone]] usage, **fang** (方) labels certain named groups or places outside or at the edge of the Shang royal domain — often opponents, hunting grounds, or partners in raid and tribute (e.g. Ch'iang fang, Tu fang). Modern scholarship uses *fang* as an analytic class of **named polities/regions** in the Shang world order.

## Semantic History

Emic Shang category → traditional historiography of "four fang" / border peoples → twentieth-century epigraphic maps of Shang political geography → anthropological models of segmentary or networked early states. Chang (*[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*, 1980) treats *fang* within the exterior domain (*waifu*) of the Shang state network, distinct from interior capital officers and from subordinate *yi* closer to the royal core.

## Operationalizations

- **Epigraphic:** count of oracle-bone occurrences; warfare vs alliance verbs; tribute of captives, animals, or goods.
- **Keightley "state score" (as summarized by Chang 1980):** occurrences × number of sovereignty/territory/religion/warfare/exchange criteria met — a quantitative proxy, not a polity census.
- **Archaeological:** material "Shang" traits beyond the core must not be equated with *fang* names (four-way non-identity).

## Applications

Mapping Shang military campaigns and captive sacrifice (especially Ch'iang); modeling resource flows (metals, salt, cattle) from periphery to [[anyang|Anyang]]; comparing late Shang "civilization sphere" models ([[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

*Fang* names are elite Shang labels, not self-ethnonyms of the groups so labeled. Inscription density tracks royal interest, not demographic size. Equating a *fang* with an archaeological culture or genetic cluster is a claim requiring argument — default link is `associated_with`, not identity.
