## Definition as Coined

In "From the Native's Point of View: On the Nature of Anthropological Understanding" (in *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983; first delivered 1974), [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] borrows a distinction from the psychoanalyst Heinz Kohut and repurposes it for ethnography (attributed). An **experience-near** concept is "one that someone... might himself naturally and effortlessly use to define what he or his fellows see, feel, think, imagine, and so on, and which he would readily understand when similarly applied by others." An **experience-distant** concept is "one that specialists of one sort or another... employ to forward their scientific, philosophical, or practical aims." Geertz's examples: "love" is experience-near, "object cathexis" experience-distant; "caste" and "nirvana" are experience-near for Hindus and Buddhists, "social stratification" and "religion" experience-distant.

Geertz stresses it is a matter of degree, "not a polar opposition," and *not* a hierarchy anthropology should climb in either direction: "confinement to experience-near concepts leaves an ethnographer awash in immediacies... confinement to experience-distant ones leaves him stranded in abstractions."

## Semantic History

The distinction enters anthropology as Geertz's reframing of a long methodological argument usually conducted in other vocabularies — inside/outside, first-person/third-person, phenomenological/objectivist, cognitive/behavioral, and most commonly **emic/etic** (borrowed from linguistics' phonemics/phonetics). Geertz's move is to dissolve the question of anthropological understanding away from the psychology of the fieldworker. The occasion is the posthumous *A Diary in the Strict Sense of the Term* of [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]], which dismantled "the myth of the chameleon fieldworker... a walking miracle of empathy": if ethnographic knowledge does not rest on "an almost preternatural capacity to think, feel, and perceive like a native," how is it possible? — "What happens to *verstehen* when *einfühlen* disappears?"

## Operationalizations

Geertz's answer is procedural, not psychological: "The trick is not to get yourself into some inner correspondence of spirit with your informants... The trick is to figure out what the devil they think they are up to." Understanding proceeds by "searching out and analyzing the symbolic forms — words, images, institutions, behaviors — in terms of which... people actually represented themselves to themselves and to one another." Across cases the movement is "a continuous dialectical tacking between the most local of local detail and the most global of global structure" — Geertz names this Dilthey's **hermeneutic circle**, the same oscillation a literary critic uses reading a poem (his example: Leo Spitzer on Keats's "Ode on a Grecian Urn") or an ordinary spectator uses following a baseball game.

## Applications

Geertz works the distinction through three of his own fieldsites (each documented as of its fieldwork moment):

- **Java** (1950s) — the *batin*/*lair* (inner felt realm / outer observed behavior) and *alus*/*kasar* (refined/vulgar) contrasts; the goal of being "smooth inside and out."
- **Bali** — personhood as standardized "dramatis personae" rather than biographical individuals ("It is dramatis personae, not actors, that in the proper sense really exist"); *lek*, mistranslated "shame," glossed by Geertz as closer to **stage fright** — fear that the culturally assigned public performance "will be botched."
- **Morocco** (Sefrou, from the mid-1960s) — the *nisba*, a relational-adjective form placing a person by tribal, occupational, or regional attachment: it "classifies him, but... does not type him; it places him without portraying him," producing a context-relative "mosaic" of identity Geertz calls "relativism squared."

The closing formulation: understanding "the form and pressure of... natives' inner lives is more like grasping a proverb, catching an allusion, seeing a joke — or... reading a poem — than it is like achieving communion." The concept is a cornerstone of [[interpretive-anthropology]]'s account of understanding and a direct rebuttal to empathy-based accounts of fieldwork.

## Critiques and Limitations

Geertz's own relativism formula concedes the limit: "we can never apprehend another people's... imagination neatly, as though it were our own," but "we can apprehend it well enough... not by looking behind the interfering glosses that connect us to it but through them." Later reflexive and *Writing Culture* critiques targeted precisely the interpretive claim to construe another's symbolic world — the authority to say "what the devil they think they are up to" is the authority those critiques contested. The distinction is heuristic, not operational: it names a graded feel of concepts, not a test that sorts them.
