# Academic Field

The [[social-field|field]] of higher-education and research institutions treated as a structured space of positions defined by the distribution of specific forms of capital. Bourdieu's application, in *[[bourdieu-homo-academicus-1984|Homo Academicus]]* (1984), of his general field theory to the university world that produces academics themselves.

## Definition as Coined

Bourdieu argues (attributed, 1984) that the university is not a community of peers pursuing knowledge but a **field of forces and struggles** whose agents are distributed by the volume and structure of their capital, and who compete over the legitimate principle of academic domination. The academic field is a site of permanent struggle over the boundary between the **temporally dominant** (institutional/administrative power — control of reproduction, juries, careers) and the **culturally/scientifically dominant** (intellectual renown, scientific authority).

## Semantic History

Bourdieu earlier developed "field" for art, religion, and class-lifestyle ([[bourdieu-distinction-1984|Distinction]]); *Homo Academicus* specializes it to the academy, and the later *The State Nobility* (1989) and *Science of Science and Reflexivity* extend it to the field of grandes écoles and the scientific field respectively. Usage since has generalized "academic field"/"scientific field" as a standard object of the sociology of higher education and science.

## Operationalizations

Constructed via [[correspondence-analysis|multiple correspondence analysis]] of French professors across the four faculties (arts, science, law, medicine) and within the arts/human-sciences faculty, using indicators grouped as: inherited economic/social capital; inherited/acquired [[cultural-capital|cultural capital]]; [[academic-capital|academic power capital]]; scientific prestige capital; intellectual notoriety capital; political-economic power capital; political dispositions.

## Applications

- The structural opposition between faculties and within faculties (Ch. 2–3, *Homo Academicus*).
- The [[cultural-reproduction|reproduction]] of the professorial corps through control of succession and academic time.
- Analysis of May 1968 as a synchronization of partial crises across the field (Ch. 4–5).

## Critiques and Limitations

France-specific and 1960s-specific; the sharp temporal/cultural pole opposition may not travel to systems with different funding, tenure, and prestige structures. Critics note the reflexive risk of the analyst's own position in the field shaping the account — a risk Bourdieu foregrounds rather than hides (see [[participant-objectivation]]).
