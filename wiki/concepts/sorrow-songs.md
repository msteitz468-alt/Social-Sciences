# Sorrow Songs

## Definition as Coined

In *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903), [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]] names the **Sorrow Songs** the Negro folk-songs — “songs of the weary at heart” — that stand before each chapter as musical epigraphs and receive full analysis in chapter XIV. He claims:

> “the Negro folk-song—the rhythmic cry of the slave—stands to-day not simply as the sole American music, but as the most beautiful expression of human experience born this side the seas… the singular spiritual heritage of the nation and the greatest gift of the Negro people.”

`emic_or_etic: migrated` — songs are emic cultural production; “Sorrow Songs” is Du Bois’s analytic label organizing them as a national gift and historical message.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Forethought / chapter bars | Structural device of the book |
| Ch. XIV (1903) | Aesthetic-historical thesis; ten master songs; development stages; hope that songs “sing true” |
| Later scholarship | Spirituals, blues genealogy, cultural appropriation debates — thicken from later sources |

Du Bois distinguishes true folk-song from debased commercial and minstrel imitations and from some “gospel hymn” dilutions.

## Operationalizations

Not quantitative. Method in ch. XIV: reception history (Port Royal, Higginson, Fisk Jubilee Singers under George L. White from 1871); listing master songs paired with chapters; notes on African musical precedence over English words; biblical borrowing and improvisation; scarcity of love-songs; prevalence of mother, toil, death, Judgment.

## Applications

- Refutes the “joyous slave” thesis — songs articulate the slave’s message
- Three gifts of Negro people to America: (1) story and song; (2) sweat and brawn; (3) Spirit — “Would America have been America without her Negro people?”
- Continuous with [[black-church]] music (Preacher–Music–Frenzy triad in ch. X)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Canon formation:** Selecting “master songs” is an authorial aesthetic act, not a folk inventory.
- **Sole American music:** A 1903 polemical claim against cultural neglect — not a musicological monopoly thesis for all later genres.
- **Romanticization risk:** Hope and beauty must not erase the violence and labor the songs encode.
