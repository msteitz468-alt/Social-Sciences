# Calling (Beruf)

## Definition as Coined

For [[weber-max|Weber]] in *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]*, the **calling** (*Beruf*) is a worldly occupation conceived as a **task set by God** — a life-task and definite field of work with religious dignity. The idea of **duty in a calling** is "what is most characteristic of the social ethic of capitalistic culture" and in a sense its fundamental basis: an obligation felt toward the content of professional activity whether it uses personal powers or capital (Ch. 2, via Franklin).

In *[[weber-economy-society-1921|Economy and Society]]* Ch.2 §24, Weber states a more technical, everyday-occupational definition (Tribe notes this is *Beruf* in the ordinary sense of occupation, not only "vocation"): occupation is "that mode of specification, specialisation, and combination of the work done by a person that serves that person with ongoing *Chancen* for provision and acquisition." Occupations distribute by unfree vs free division, by specification/specialisation of function, and by autocephalous vs heterocephalous valorisation; typical occupations and income chances relate to classes and social hierarchies (cross-ref. Ch.4). The PE religious-ethical sense and the ES occupational sense are two layers of the same German word — linked by the elective-affinity argument that Protestant ethics helped make continuous specialised occupational duty a moral habit usable by capitalism.

## Semantic History

- **Linguistic claim (Ch. 3):** Neither predominantly Catholic peoples nor classical antiquity had an expression of similar religious connotation for worldly occupation; predominantly Protestant peoples do. Modern meaning comes from **Bible translations** (Luther's German; English *calling*), not from ethnic "Germanic spirit." Luther uses *Beruf* at Sirach xi.20–21 in the modern sense; it then spreads in Protestant everyday speech.
- **Lutheran limit:** Weber argues the mere Lutheran idea of the calling is of **questionable direct importance** for the capitalistic spirit — more traditionalistic (*in vocatione* rather than *per vocationem* as proof); task of investigation shifts to Calvinism and the sects (Ch. 3).
- **Calvinist transformation:** Calling becomes site of methodical proof of election and rationalization of worldly conduct ([[inner-worldly-asceticism]]).
- **ES technical layer:** Ch.2 §24 treats *Beruf* as the sociological unit of specialised work-for-provision within market or organisational assignment — the neutral skeleton under the PE ethical content.
- **Migrated status:** Emic Protestant usage → etic sociological category of modern occupational duty; later secular "vocation" talk retains the ghost of religious meaning ([[iron-cage]]).

## Operationalizations

Weber's operation is philological-historical plus ethical-psychological, not a survey item. Critics (Brentano, Robertson — per Giddens) attack the uniqueness of the Protestant calling concept; Weber's notes expand the philology and reject simple pre-Reformation identity.

## Applications

- Bridge concept between religious ethics and [[spirit-of-capitalism]].
- Explains labour performed as absolute end in itself — "not a product of nature" but of long education; capitalism historically needed this ally (Ch. 2).
- Baxterian pastoral ethics: waste of time as deadly sin; labour even for the wealthy; faithfulness in calling (Ch. 5).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Historical uniqueness of *Beruf/calling* semantics disputed.
- Risk of reading modern occupational ideology back into seventeenth-century pastoral literature.
- Lutheran vs Calvinist distinction is ideal-typical; empirical mixtures expected (Weber's own method notes).
