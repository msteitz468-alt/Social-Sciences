# Do-It-Yourself Biography

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s term (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Ch. 5; drawing on Ley 1984's "elective biography" and Gross 1985's "do-it-yourself biography") for the life course that, under [[individualization]], is no longer given by ascribed status but must be self-constructed — yet self-constructed *from institutional building blocks*, not freely from nothing.

## Definition as Coined

Where industrial society supplied a "standard biography" fixed by class, gender, and family, reflexive modernity produces a **"reflexive biography"** in which "the individual himself or herself becomes the reproduction unit of the social in the lifeworld" and must become "the planning office with respect to his/her own biography" (Beck 1986: 90, 135). The transition is "from standard to elective biography." Crucially, the elective biography is an *institutionally dependent* individual situation: its raw materials are the labor market, the education system, welfare regulation, and consumption — "The apparent outside of the institutions becomes the inside of the individual biography."

## Semantic History

The concept names the specific *biographical form* that [[individualization]] takes, and should be distinguished from individualization as the broader structural process. It anticipates later "reflexive biography" and "choice biography" literatures in the sociology of the life course and youth transitions, and is close to [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s account of the reflexive self-narrative in late modernity.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by de-standardized life-courses: non-linear education-to-work transitions, discontinuous employment, later and more variable family formation. Beck read the reattribution of *fault* as a symptom — social failures (unemployment, divorce, failed exams) increasingly experienced as personal biographical failure rather than "blows of fate."

## Applications

Underlies Beck's analyses of gender and the family (the "negotiated family"), of the [[detraditionalization|destandardization of labor]], and of the psychologization of socially-produced distress. It became a workhorse concept in life-course sociology and youth studies.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue the "elective" language overstates real biographical freedom, especially for the working class and the precariously employed, for whom institutional dependency dominates the "choice" — a version of the broader [[individualization-death-of-class-debate|individualization-vs-class]] dispute. Beck's own insistence that individualization is "not emancipation" is the internal check on the concept.
