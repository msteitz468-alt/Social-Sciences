# Formal Pragmatics

## Definition as Coined

**Formal pragmatics** (earlier **universal pragmatics**) is [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s (attributed) reconstructive program for the **general pragmatic presuppositions** of speech oriented to reaching understanding. Against a merely empirical pragmatics of usage, and against a semantics limited to truth conditions of sentences, formal pragmatics analyzes **utterances** as speech acts that simultaneously raise [[validity-claims]] and establish interpersonal relations.

In *[[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v1-1984|TCA Vol. 1]]* Habermas (attributed) connects Bühler's organon model of language functions (representational, expressive, appellative) with analytic philosophy of language, generalizing validity "beyond the truth of propositions" to the pragmatic level. Illocutionary force specifies which claim is raised; the aim is a theory of meaning that can explain the **coordinating mechanism** of linguistically mediated interaction — which intentionalist semantics (Grice et al.), modeled on consequence-oriented action, cannot do.

## Semantic History

| Phase | Label / move |
|---|---|
| 1970s essays | "Universal pragmatics"; ideal speech situation; strong quasi-transcendental cast |
| TCA Vol. 1 (1981/1984) | "Formal pragmatics"; embedded in action typology and Weber reconstruction; more historically situated |
| TCA Vol. 2 | Presupposed as infrastructure of [[lifeworld]] communication |
| Later reception | Debates over whether formal pragmatics is transcendental philosophy in disguise (Habermas denies foundationalism in the TCA preface) |

Related but distinct: Hymes's [[communicative-competence]] (ethnography of speaking) — empirical, comparative, not reconstructive-universal in Habermas's sense. See also [[communicative-competence-debate]].

## Operationalizations

- **Reconstructive, not survey.** Formal pragmatics claims to reconstruct competences speakers already have (analogous to Piaget/Kohlberg developmental reconstruction), not to sample attitudes.
- **Pure modes** of speech (constative, regulative, expressive) as analytic idealizations; everyday speech is multi-claim.
- **Boundary with empirical pragmatics:** systematically distorted communication and strategic uses of language are studied empirically against the formal-pragmatic baseline (TCA Ch. III.F).

## Applications

- Methodological engine of the [[theory-of-communicative-action]].
- Basis for discourse ethics (redemption of rightness claims).
- Bridge from speech-act theory into sociological action theory.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Transcendental smuggling** (attributed): despite anti-foundationalist disclaimers, the apparatus is charged with a priori legislation over empirical communication.
- **Thin ethnography** (attributed): formal-pragmatic types underdetermine the diversity of speech communities documented by linguistic anthropology.
- **Power and ideology** (attributed): a baseline of "undistorted" communication may obscure how validity forms themselves are historically produced.
