# Ritual Insult (Sounding / the Dozens)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[labov-language-inner-city-1972|Language in the Inner City]]* (1972, ch. 8), [[labov-william|William Labov]] treated **ritual insult** as a named analytic object: a rule-governed [[speech-event|speech event]] in [[black-english-vernacular|Black English vernacular]] peer culture, known in Harlem primarily as **sounding**, and regionally as playing the dozens, woofing, joning, signifying (Chicago), cutting/capping/chopping (West Coast), and related labels. Labov framed the chapter as both an ethnography of speaking (Hymes 1962) and a test bed for discourse formalization: rules connecting what is said to what is done, and sequencing rules for multi-party exchanges.

Labov proposed a uniform underlying structure for most sounds:

**T(B) is so X that P**

where **T** is the target (normally a relative of antagonist B—especially the mother), **X** is a pejorative attribute (old, fat/skinny, ragged, ugly, immoral, poor, black, etc.), and **P** is a proposition quantifying the degree of X. Simpler surface forms (e.g. “Your mother look like Flipper”) were argued to be recoverable under deleted material of the same model. Rhymed dozens and some “I took/fucked your mother…” forms required an **alternative model** (assertion of sexual degradation of the opponent’s mother) alongside the main scheme; Labov held that the majority of Jets/Cobras/Thunderbirds sounds fit T-is-so-X-that-P.

## Semantic History

The practice itself is longstanding in African American speech communities; Dollard (1939), Abrahams (1962, 1964), and Kochman (1968, 1970) documented dozens and related verbal dueling before Labov’s formalization. Labov stressed that **superordinate metalinguistic labels vary more than the verbal behavior**: regional terms shift, expert sounders can be “untrustworthy on ‘sounding’,” and “people talk much more than they talk about talk.”

Kochman (Chicago) divided the field differently: sounding = initial exchanges; signifying = personal insults; dozens = insults on relatives. In New York as Labov reported it, **dozens** was more specialized to rhymed couplets / ritualized insults against relatives, while **sounding** covered those plus simpler insult forms. Labov deliberately used Harlem’s dominant term *sounding* for the institution as a whole. Relation to [[signifying]]: Labov treated “signifying” as one regional/analytic label among several for ritual insulting, and also linked the epic toast “Signifying Monkey” (where the Monkey “signifies” by reporting that the elephant sounded on the lion, culminating in “playin' the dozens”)—a different sense from Mitchell-Kernan’s conversational indirection (see [[signifying]] for both lives of the term).

## Operationalizations

**Data (Labov 1965–68, south-central Harlem).** Primary corpora for the “shape of sounds”: Jets microbus return trip (13 members, ~180 sounds in 35 minutes); Thunderbirds group session (Boot, Money, David, Roger and others; ~60 fully identified sounds); Cobras trip (~35 sounds). Multi-track recording allowed counting who laughed (positive evaluation metric).

**Speech-event structure.** Ritual sounding is a **three-party** event: antagonists A and B plus **audience C**. Exchange structure is not simple ABAB dialogue but sound + evaluation alternating: A-1 *e* B-1 *e* A-2 *e* B-2… Competitive success depends on **transforming** the opponent’s prior sound (topping); when one antagonist clearly loses, a second stage of group sounding against the loser can open. Audience evaluation is overt and immediate: positive primarily **laughter** (countable), plus exclamations or repetition of the striking part; negative evaluations are group-specific (Jets: “Tha's phony!”, “corny”, “weak”, “lame”; Cobras: “You fake!”, “That ain't where it's at”).

**Ritual vs personal (load-bearing distinction).** Labov held that ritual status turns on **P being obviously untrue as shared knowledge** (an AB-event that P is false). Attributes X may truly apply; if P is deleted (“Your family is poor!”), the insult becomes personal. Personal insults are answered by **denial, excuse, or mitigation** (short I-D dyads); ritual sounds are answered by **another sound** (long chains). Denials normal for personal insults are “unthinkable” with sounds—to deny a sound is to admit that its falsity is not obvious shared knowledge. When a sound becomes “too ordinary / too possible,” response flips to the personal pattern (e.g. house-visit anecdote → “You's a damn liar”). Cross-group or stranger sounding is more dangerous and often fight-provoking; extended ritual sounding is characteristically an **in-group** process.

**Formal rules (Labov’s discourse scheme).**
1. **Interpretation (Rule 1):** If A utters S before B and audience C referring to target T(B) in proposition P, and B believes A believes P is not true and that B knows P is not true, then S is a sound (heard as T(B) is so X that P).
2. **Production (Rule 2):** If A has sounded on B, B sounds on A with a new untrue P' targeting T(A).
3. **Sequencing (Rule 3):** the response to a sound is a sound.

**Goffman / Sacks frame.** Labov formalized Goffman’s four properties of ritual sounding: (1) a sound opens a field meant to be sustained; (2) third-person role is necessary; (3) any third person can become a player, especially on a failure; (4) considerable **symbolic distance** insulates the event from other interaction. From Sacks: sounding is judged not serious (joking pathway) yet still requires a P'-governed reply, not only a laugh. **Applied sounding** embeds in other interaction and need not follow full session rules; only symbolic distance is obligatorily preserved. Rel’s “Your mother's a duck” to Stanley (Jets video session) redefines a personal challenge as ritual play—if Stanley takes it seriously he implies the absurd could be true; rituals as “sanctuaries” free actors from personal responsibility for named attributes.

**Standards of excellence.** Positive evaluation favors: syntactic complexity (left-hand embedding suspending the final proposition); short closing **coda sounds** by a third party; **minimax** semantic shifts with minimal formal change. Peer culture, Labov argued, develops high competence in syntax, semantics, and rhetoric under norms “far removed from the classroom.” Traditional rhymed dozens reward store, memory, and delivery more than improvisation; younger adolescents often know few older couplets. Labov also reported that white peer-group “insults” (NY investigators Cohen, Wald) were limited fixed routines occupying little time—not a speech event in the sense sounding is for Black groups.

## Applications

- Documenting **verbal skill** in BEV peer culture against school-based deficit pictures ([[deficit-difference-debate]]); linked to competitive singing, toasts, rifting, louding.
- Discourse analysis of multi-party speech events and audience evaluation ([[speech-event]]).
- Sociometric / status analysis: verbal leadership (e.g. Boot among Thunderbirds) and power overrides of skill (Money fails against Boot—Labov analogized to [[whyte-william-foote|Whyte]] on bowling under Doc; Stevie crushed by Rel’s applied sound despite age-group verbal leadership).
- Sampling critique: “lames” (isolates outside full peer participation) lack daily practice in sounds/dozens and fail as informants for this repertoire (see [[south-central-harlem-youth]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Labov’s formalization is etic analysis of an emic practice; regional terminology and Kochman’s tripartite Chicago scheme show that “sounding / signifying / dozens” are not a single folk taxonomy. Graphic sexual and excretory content in rhymed openers can overshadow Labov’s finding that **less than half** of cited sounds need be classed as obscene, and that meaning often derives from value-opposition (“their way of being good and our way of being bad”) rather than uniform sexual degradation. Ritual safeguards collapse with younger speakers, strangers, or weak P—social danger is real (Labov cites Swett 1966 and other cases of dozens escalating to violence). Cross-city similarity claims rest on exploratory interviews and parallel corpora (Kochman Chicago; Wortham San Francisco), not a single national sample. The chapter analyzes primarily male adolescent peer groups in south-central Harlem 1965–68; generalizing “the” Black speech community from that frame is a standing caution (see Identity Cautions on [[south-central-harlem-youth]] and [[black-english-vernacular]]).
