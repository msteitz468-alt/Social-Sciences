# Ritual Landscape

## Definition as Coined

**Ritual landscape** denotes a region whose archaeological signature is dominated by ceremonial monuments, structured deposits, and processional or visual relations among places — as opposed to dense domestic settlement or later field systems. In British prehistory the phrase is closely associated with Wessex (Stonehenge–Avebury region), Cranborne Chase, and comparable complexes.

## Semantic History

Processual "ceremonial centers"; post-processual and heritage usages; critique that "ritual" dumps residual non-economic behavior. [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] (1998) argues that later agricultural reorganization (field systems, roundhouse settlements, barrow cemeteries) often **reworks symbolic schemes already established** in these landscapes — continuity of structure across an apparent ritual→farming divide (Ch. 10).

## Operationalizations

Monument density and intervisibility; scarcity of domestic debris; avenues/cursus; later superimposition of fields and farms; depositional patterning.

## Applications

Neolithic–Bronze Age Britain and Ireland; comparative monumental landscapes elsewhere (use carefully — not a universal stage).

## Bradley 1998 — continuity thesis (attributed)

Bradley argues the archaeological distinction between **domestic landscapes** (food production) and **ritual landscapes** (specialised monuments) is **unwarranted** for later southern British prehistory — both are built from the same circular elements and cosmology (Ch. 10). Cranborne Chase house-building and settlement enclosure drew on a symbolic code of considerable antiquity; rapid settlement change was expressible through established forms.

**Stonehenge landscape:** After initial construction, burial mounds concentrate in the wider area; large/rich barrows on higher ground skyline from the centre; Avenue approaches through imposing barrow groups; Woodward & Woodward concentric barrow rings echoing site organisation. Cross-references between monument and landscape work across intersecting time scales — “Theatre of Memory” (Yates analogy via Bradley).

**Avebury:** Sited in a chalk basin ringed by hills; skyline EBA barrows; Windmill Hill and Silbury visible from bank; insider cut off from horizon despite “circular” landscape siting. Contrast Windmill Hill causewayed enclosure (built across contours, one-facing) with Avebury’s continuous-horizon design.

**Mesolithic baseline:** Many sacred places in the landscape, none constructed for the purpose; votive deposits in wet places continue into Neolithic.

**Later prehistory:** Open monuments become less accessible and go out of use; enclosures replaced by farms, burial mounds by fields — yet a thread of continuity remains as ritual is “domesticated” into houses, boundaries, and storage pits.

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary ritual/domestic is modern; many landscapes mixed. Phrase can reify a tourist geography. Prefer site-level evidence over the label alone.
