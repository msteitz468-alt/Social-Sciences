# System and Lifeworld

## Definition as Coined

**System and lifeworld** is [[habermas-jurgen|Habermas]]'s proposal (attributed), in [[habermas-theory-of-communicative-action-v2-1987|TCA Vol. 2]], to "conceive of societies simultaneously as systems and lifeworlds." The distinction rests on two modes of integration (a distinction Habermas draws from David Lockwood):

- **Social integration** "attaches to action orientations" — the [[lifeworld]] is integrated through normatively secured or communicatively achieved consensus.
- **System integration** "reaches right through" action orientations — the economy and administrative state are integrated through "the nonnormative steering of individual decisions not subjectively coordinated," the market being the paradigm case.

A provisional formula: "societies are systemically stabilized complexes of action of socially integrated groups." System development is measured by increases in steering capacity; lifeworld development by the differentiation of culture, society, and personality.

## Semantic History

Habermas's innovation is to make the two a **methodological** duality, not merely two objects: system integration is accessible from an external, observer's perspective (functional analysis, systems theory), while lifeworld structures "have to be gotten at hermeneutically," from participants' internal perspective. His standing objection to [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] and to Luhmann is that each dissolves this duality — subordinating action theory to systems theory, or hypostatizing the lifeworld into "society." He frames the history of social theory since Marx as "the unmixing of two paradigms that could no longer be integrated into a two-level concept of society."

## Operationalizations — uncoupling

Across social evolution, system and lifeworld undergo a "second-order process of differentiation": each grows internally more complex/rational, and they grow apart from one another. Habermas traces four mechanisms of system differentiation, each institutionalized in a social formation: segmental differentiation (kinship/exchange), stratification (rank/prestige), state organization (political power), and the **money medium** (bourgeois private law). In tribal societies system and social integration are "tightly interwoven" (the exchange of women normed by marriage rules); in modernity the social system "definitively bursts out of the horizon of the lifeworld," becoming accessible "only to the counterintuitive knowledge of the social sciences." The lifeworld, "at first coextensive with a scarcely differentiated social system, gets cut down more and more to one subsystem among others" as delinguistified **[[steering-media]]** (money and power) steer intercourse "disconnected from norms and values."

## Applications

The framework's payoff is the diagnosis of modern pathology. "The rationalization of the lifeworld makes possible the emergence and growth of subsystems whose independent imperatives turn back destructively upon the lifeworld itself" — the **[[colonization-of-the-lifeworld]]**. Crucially, Habermas holds the lifeworld's **material** reproduction can be transferred to system integration without pathology, but its **symbolic** reproduction (cultural transmission, social integration, socialization) cannot.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Reifying the boundary** (attributed to critics): treating economy and state as norm-free "systems" is charged with naturalizing power and depoliticizing the market — a major line of critique (Fraser, McCarthy).
- **Functionalism smuggled back** (attributed): the reliance on system/functional categories sits uneasily with Habermas's critique of [[structural-functionalism]].
- Habermas's own caveat: the lifeworld's shrinking to a subsystem must not be read causally, as though lifeworld structure depended on rising system complexity — the reverse holds.
