# Identity Shift

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* Change in **shared definitions** of who “we” and “they” are — and of relations across that boundary — during contention. Not merely private attitude change; a public relational redefinition.

## Definition as Coined

> "Conceived of as alteration in shared definitions of a boundary between two political actors and of relations across that boundary, identity shift makes a difference in war, nationalist mobilization, revolutions, and many other forms of political struggle."

Paired in Ch. 6 with **radicalization** (increasing contradiction of claims/programs/descriptions across a boundary) and **convergence** (extremes’ contradictions push between-extreme actors into closer alliances).

## Semantic History

DOC trajectories chapter (U.S. antislavery; Spanish transition); Italian unification (Ch. 8); Paris 1789 nation/citizen categories as early touchstone. Later Tilly [[political-identity]] program (*IBST* 2005) continues the relational-identity line.

## Operationalizations

Observable shifts in public self- and other-designation; realignments of annexationist vs autonomist, moderate vs radical, racial vs national frames, etc.

## Applications

*(DOC attributed illustrations:)* American antislavery → Civil War identity reorganizations; Spanish exit from Franco (centrist disappearance, PSOE consolidation); Italian 1860 annexationist cascade; Soviet/nationality identity politics.

## Critiques and Limitations

Hard to isolate from category formation and framing; cognitive vs relational emphasis remains debated; DOC stresses relational mechanisms without denying cognitive conversion.
