# Things before discourse

## Definition as Coined

In *[[martineau-how-to-observe-1838|How to Observe Morals and Manners]]* (1838), [[martineau-harriet|Harriet Martineau]] stated the "grand secret of wise inquiry into Morals and Manners": **begin with the study of THINGS, using the DISCOURSE OF PERSONS as a commentary upon them** (Part II introduction).

**Things** include institutions (political, religious, social) and **records** — architectural remains, epitaphs, civic registers, national music, and other "manifestations of the common mind." She argued these embody the action of a nation more comprehensively and faithfully than any variety of individual voices: "The voice of a whole people goes up in the silent workings of an institution; the condition of the masses is reflected from the surface of a record."

**Discourse** (conversation, opinion, testimony) remains necessary but secondary: it interprets and fills out what things indicate. To start from individual testimony is "beginning at the wrong end." Single-class informant circles (e.g. merchants only) and desultory contradiction among witnesses make person-first inquiry hopeless.

## Semantic History

Martineau’s formulation is a founding-era methodological rule, not a later Durkheimian "[[social-fact|social fact]]" (Durkheim 1895/97), though the family resemblance — treat objectified social products as more reliable than subjective report — is historically suggestive and must not be equated without attribution. It parallels [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]]’s emphasis on institutions and [[mores]] without using her Things/Discourse formula. Later ethnographic methods rebalance toward participant discourse and interaction; that rebalancing is a later debate, not her claim.

## Operationalizations

Across *How to Observe* Part II, "Things" become a working checklist:

| Domain | Example Things |
|---|---|
| Religion | Places of worship, clergy status, superstitions, holy days, suicide patterns ([[licentious-ascetic-moderate-religion]]) |
| Moral notions | Epitaphs/cemeteries, popular idols, songs, literature, treatment of the guilty, convict conversation |
| Domestic state | Soil and aspect, markets, tenure, manufacturing and commercial conditions, health/vital registers, marriage forms, condition of women and children |
| Liberty | Feudal residual, police origin, class gradation, servant relations, metropolis vs provinces, press freedom, free education/badging, universities, persecution for opinion |
| Progress | Charity hierarchy (relief → prevention), arts for rich vs many, multiplicity of objects of interest |

Discourse is gathered **after** Things, across classes, and used as commentary (Part II Ch. VI). Mechanical tools (query lists, journal, note-book) support both layers ([[social-observation]]).

## Applications

Core organizing principle of [[social-observation]] as Martineau taught it; structures all of Part II of *How to Observe*.

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Things" still require interpretation; records and institutions are not self-explanatory.
- Traveler access to things is uneven (gender, class, language, police surveillance).
- Official records carry production bias she only partly theorizes.
- The rule can undervalue emic meaning if discourse is demoted too far — a tension later ethnography would press.
- She still uses extensive comparative discourse and second-hand travel reports as illustrations, so practice is mixed.
