# Archaeological Record

The **material evidence base of archaeology** — surviving deposits, structures, artifacts,
ecofacts and their stratigraphic and spatial relations — and, more consequentially for
theory, the **contested question of what kind of thing that evidence is**. Whether the
record is a static "fossil" of past behaviour to be read, a present-day set of material
relations produced by [[formation-processes|formation processes]], a text to be
interpreted, or a sequence of durational events constituted in excavation, is one of the
defining disputes of archaeological theory.

## Definition as Coined

The phrase has no single coiner; it hardened into a theoretical object with the New
Archaeology of the 1960s, which made "the archaeological record" — rather than the artifact
or the culture — the thing method had to interrogate. [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*Critical
Approaches to Fieldwork*, 2002) argues that the notion is **historically contingent**: how
the record is conceived has tracked successive conceptions of culture. He periodizes three
overlapping orientations, each tied to a disciplinary division of labour — the site as a
**repository of objects** (evolutionary typology, c.1880–1920: [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt
Rivers]]), as a **repository of an assemblage indicative of a culture group** (culture
history, c.1920–1960: [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]], Kidder, [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]),
and as a **repository of behavioural patterns / structured activities** (processual and
post-processual, c.1960–present). Lucas insists these are not "completely disjunctive":
earlier orientations persist inside later paradigms.

## Semantic History

Lucas identifies a foundational tension already present in early fieldwork between the
**"total record"** ideal — that excavation should recover everything ([[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt
Rivers]], Worsaae) — and the view that recording is **inherently selective** ("one always
selects what to record": Petrie, [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]; cited via Carver 1990).

The record's status became an explicit theoretical battleground in the **Binford–Schiffer
debate** (attributed):

- **[[schiffer-michael-b|Michael Schiffer]]** (*[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* 1987, attributed) argued the record is a **transformed/distorted** view of artifacts as they once participated in a behavioral system ([[systemic-context]] → [[archaeological-context]]); **c-transforms** and **n-transforms** intervene; formation processes can **create** patterning, not only erase it; inference requires identifying those processes rather than reading patterns as pure behavioral correlates.
- **[[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]]** countered that the very idea of a "C-transform" is
  incoherent, since it presumes the record was, at formation, already a fossilized
  "Pompeii" of a living system from which it could then "distort" — "how can the original
  distort the copy?" (Binford 1981). The **[[pompeii-premise|Pompeii Premise]]** — reading remains as an
  unmediated frozen moment — is the target Binford attacks.
- Schiffer (1987 Ch. 13, attributed) further stresses, via Patrik (1985), that what we call the "archaeological record" is finally the **documents** of recovery and analysis — archaeologists' activities are themselves cultural formation processes.

Lucas's own reading (attributed) is that **both** men share a *representational* model:
the archaeological (static) context stands in for the systemic (living) context. His
central original claim is that the record is conventionally described and recorded in a
**naturalistic nomenclature** (measurements, soil descriptions, physical properties) even
where it is recognized as anthropogenic — and that the commensurability of natural and
cultural deposits under the same descriptive categories is what quietly constructs the
record as a "natural phenomenon."

## Operationalizations

The record is operationalized through excavation and recording systems, and the choice of
system encodes a theory of what the record *is*. Lucas argues (attributed) that the
stratigraphic unit as defined by both the [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] and the
geoarchaeological/geological school has **no temporality of its own** — only a place in a
sequence — because units are treated as spatially-bounded *objects* (products of
production) rather than as durational *events*. He proposes foregrounding the **event-character
and duration** of units (see [[eventful-temporality]], [[event-occurrence-distinction]]) and
embracing the palimpsest — the superimposition of differently-paced events — as intrinsic
to a living site rather than as noise to be dissected away. Competing operationalizations of
the record's degradation predate Schiffer: Ascher's (1968) entropy model and Cowgill's
sampling-bias model, both superseded once Schiffer showed formation processes can *create*
patterning, not merely erase it.

## Applications

- The **section-vs-plan** dispute over the primary stratigraphic document
  ([[stratigraphic-recording-debate]]): section-based [[box-grid-excavation|grid excavation]]
  vs. plan-based [[single-context-recording|single-context recording]] resting on the
  [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]].
- Binford's (1964) research-design critique — treating sampling strategy and research
  design as constitutive of "the record" — reoriented Americanist fieldwork (see
  [[disciplines/archaeology|archaeology]]).
- **Binford *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983)**
  (attributed): the record is **here with us in the present** — material things and
  arrangements of matter, not symbols or past observations; archaeology must **decode**
  statics into statements about past dynamics via a "science of the archaeological
  record," [[ethnoarchaeology]], and [[actualistic-studies]] under
  [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]]. Facts do not speak for
  themselves; pattern recognition is not meaning.
- Lucas's reflexive extension: at Hofstaðir, Iceland, re-excavating a 1908 trench to read
  the *previous excavation* as part of the record (see [[reflexive-fieldwork]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's instability is the point: there is no theory-neutral "record." Lucas's
concluding position (attributed) is that archaeology's facts "are primarily not sites or
artefacts, but the textual and graphic materialisations which stand in for them," and that
the validity of interpretation rests not on an independent Level-I reality but on the
**iterability** of those representations — since the excavated site itself, once dug, cannot
be revisited (excavation as destruction redeemed by recording). This makes the record less
a found object than a produced one, and puts the politics and craft of recording at the
centre of the discipline's epistemology.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook framing (2012)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012. Brief pedagogical framing; does not replace Lucas, Binford, or Schiffer layers above.)*

**Always-there material, historically new awareness.** Renfrew & Bahn stress that material evidence of the archaeological record has long existed; what is new is awareness that archaeological methods can yield information about the past, and that every view of the past is a product of its own time. Archaeologists study past humans and societies primarily through material remains — buildings, tools, and other artifacts constituting **material culture**. For more than 99 percent of humankind’s span, past material culture is the only significant cultural source.

**Understanding requires formation knowledge.** “We can only understand the archaeological record — that is to say, what we find — if we understand in much greater detail how it came about, how it was formed.” [[formation-processes]] and [[ethnoarchaeology]] are therefore central; the bulk of the record is humanly made artifacts and everyday discard, not only monuments. Primary vs secondary context, cultural vs natural formation, and differential preservation structure what can be claimed (Ch. 2; see [[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]).

**Competing metaphors in the textbook.** Processual framing: data do not speak for themselves; interpretation and testing are required. Postprocessual framing (as reported): many interpretive archaeologists treat the record as a **text of meaningful signs**, strongest when objects have been carefully placed. Renfrew & Bahn’s own cognitive program treats finds as partly products of thoughts and intentions (Popper’s “world 3”) while insisting on explicit, testable procedures rather than unstructured empathy (see [[cognitive-archaeology]]).
