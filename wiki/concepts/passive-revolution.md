# Passive Revolution

> [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s concept (*rivoluzione passiva*) in the Notes on Italian History and related political notes of *[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]*. **Attributed voice** throughout. Editors call his use "one of the cruxes" of the Notebooks.

## Definition as Coined

Gramsci derives "passive revolution" rigorously from two principles of political science (tracking Marx's 1859 Preface, editors note):

1. No social formation disappears as long as the productive forces developed within it still find room for forward movement.
2. A society does not set itself tasks for whose solution the necessary conditions have not already been incubated.

The phrase's cue is **Vincenzo Cuoco** on the Neapolitan revolution of 1799, but Gramsci "completely modified and enriched" it. In the Risorgimento, he argued, the Moderates achieved national unification as a **revolution-restoration**: transformation without the Jacobin-style popular mobilisation that would have made the bourgeoisie truly hegemonic over peasantry and South. Piedmont could play a state-leading role for a group that should lead — producing, in Gramsci's formula, **dictatorship without hegemony**.

Related couplets (editors' framing of Gramsci's oppositions): State/civil society, force/consent, domination/leadership, [[war-of-position|war of manoeuvre / war of position]]. Gramsci **relates** passive revolution to war of position (and asks whether war of position can become manoeuvre again); he treats passive revolution as a **criterion of interpretation**, not as a political programme (unlike Gioberti-style moderate theorists).

He also sketches "the history of Europe seen as 'passive revolution'" (Restoration, molecular *trasformismo*, Crocean historiography as soft ideological cover) and links the concept to fascism/corporativism and to [[fordism|Americanism]] as possible passive-revolutionary paths of rationalisation without socialist explosion.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Sense |
|---|---|
| Cuoco (cue) | Neapolitan 1799 "passive" vs French active revolution |
| Gramsci *Quaderni* | Revolution-restoration; Risorgimento; Europe; fascism as war of position; molecular change / *trasformismo* |
| Postwar Marxist historiography | Italian and Latin American state formation "from above" |
| Neo-Gramscian IR / political economy | Global restructuring, passive revolution of capital, "trasformismo" of left parties |

Later uses often stretch the term to any non-ruptural change; Gramsci's load-bearing constraints (productive-force room; incubated conditions; leadership without popular hegemony) should be restored when citing the primary.

## Operationalizations

- **Moderates vs Action Party:** Moderates exercise intellectual–moral leadership; Action Party fails to elaborate autonomous popular programme → absorbed/*trasformismo*.
- **Piedmont-function:** A state substitutes for missing class hegemony — "dictatorship without hegemony."
- **Molecular change:** Gradual absorption of opposition personnel and demands rather than frontal clash.
- **Criterion not programme:** Analytic of how restoration succeeds; not a recipe for progressive strategy (though linked strategically to [[war-of-position]]).

## Applications

- Italian Risorgimento and North–South / city–countryside structure (*[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971]]*).
- European history after 1815 as passive-revolutionary sequence (Gramsci's sketch).
- Fascism and corporativism as candidate passive revolutions (Gramsci; contested in later historiography).
- Americanism/Fordism: whether rationalisation is passive revolution or molecular accumulation toward explosion (Gramsci's open question).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Ambiguity between descriptive historical type and strategic warning — reception splits accordingly.
- Risk of functionalism: every non-ruptural change labelled passive revolution loses specificity.
- Notebook censorship and fragmentary form: fascism passages especially opaque (Hoare: require prior political experience to read).
- Primary source for this wiki: 1971 English selection only.
