# Four Haunts (Historical Archaeology)

[[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s (1996) label for four all-pervasive historical forces that, he argued, "haunt" **[[historical-archaeology]]** and the modern world: **colonialism**, **[[eurocentrism|Eurocentrism]]**, **capitalism**, and **modernity**. Conceptual architecture of **[[global-historical-archaeology]]**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Orser 1996, esp. Ch. 3.)* Orser called them "haunts" because of their shadowy, all-pervasive character — "trailing the field like four quiet shadows," present "at every site, on every laboratory table, within every map and chart," sometimes center-stage, sometimes ghostwriters. He held that each incorporates "a complex set of relations between real men and women," not vague forces floating overhead, and that they must be defined so as to make sense **to historical archaeologists** and in language consistent with **[[mutualism-historical-archaeology|mutualism]]**.

He stated two chapter goals: (1) define each haunt for HA relevance; (2) cast each in mutualist terms. He disclaimed exhaustive treatment of any one.

## Semantic History

Orser's 1996 packaging of four already-contested scholarly terms into a single archaeological heuristic. Each term has independent careers in history, political economy, and postcolonial studies; the contribution is the **joint, mandatory co-presence claim** for modern-world archaeology and the "haunt" metaphor for unacknowledged structural presence.

## The Four Forces (as Orser characterizes them)

### Colonialism — "historical archaeology's constant companion"

Orser traced the field's early equation with colonial sites (Setzler 1943 floating "Colonial-Archaeology"; Harrington 1952 making colonial archaeology a major subdivision with a benign definition of transplanted European culture under homeland control). He argued colonial archaeology became naturalized — books can treat French colonialism in Illinois without examining colonialism itself. Against Quimby–Spoehr-type **acculturation** effectiveness measures, he highlighted resistance readings (e.g., Rubertone on Narragansett wampum in cemeteries challenging colonial authority). Mutualist claim: "colonialism is ultimately a mutualist endeavor" — relations between colonizer and colonized, not one-way transfer.

### Eurocentrism — "friend or foe?"

Centering of Europe as unmarked standard of historical significance, value, and narrative focus — including calendars, Greenwich, atlas conventions, and research design (which sites get dug). Orser engaged Samir Amin-type critiques and archaeological examples such as Great Zimbabwe attribution struggles. As research-design failure: SHA founding geography sidelining Europe-as-object; Mediterranean/Ottoman omission in expansion narratives (Ch. 8).

### Capitalism — "the powerful face"

Orser linked HA capitalism literature (Handsman, Leone, etc.) to definitions usable archaeologically (including Curtin-type commercial definitions and Marx–Wolf mode-of-production language). Material signatures include estate landscapes, commodity regimes, Georgian-order patterning (Leone/Annapolis stream as engaged peer literature). He placed capitalism among the haunts that structure inequality and commodity-bounded identity-making.

### Modernity — "the world of modern life"

Definitionally contested; Orser related modernization vs dependency debates and Dussel-type critiques of the "myth" of modernity. Modernity for HA is not merely "recent time" but the mind-sets and institutional forms co-produced with the other three haunts — cross-cutting effective scales in multi-scalar analysis.

## Putting It All Together

*(Attributed — Orser 1996 Ch. 3 close + Ch. 8.)* Orser argued the four are **inseparable** in the modern world: the modern world *is* their convergence. Gender, race, and class domination are "gendered by the four haunts" in his wording — produced through haunt-structured relations rather than free-floating identity categories. Elites at [[palmares|Palmares]] and [[gorttoose|Gorttoose]] win definitional and real battles "by manipulating the four haunts, the very framework of modern life." Making haunts explicit **liberates** research agendas; haunt-free modern sites would be extraordinary.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of forcing local histories into four Euro-Atlantic master processes.
- Tension between critiquing Eurocentrism and periodizing the modern world from 1492 (Orser partly answers via Bartlett 1993 on medieval European "incipient" haunts and beyond-Europe research design).
- Hermeneutic alternatives (e.g., Yentsch's topic-to-topic circles) may reject haunt-first framing — Orser records the disagreement as framework choice, not as a closed empirical question.
