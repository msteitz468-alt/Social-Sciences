# Power Networks

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|The Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]* (1986, Ch. 1), [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] defines societies as constituted of **multiple overlapping and intersecting sociospatial networks of power** — not unitary systems or totalities. A "society" in rigorous usage is only a network of interaction with some cleavage at its boundaries relative to denser internal patterning; Mann prefers the Latin sense of *societas* (loose confederation of stratified allies) over Romance unitary "society." The four principal network types are the [[iemp-model|IEMP]] sources: ideological, economic, military, and political — overlapping networks *and* organizational means of attaining human goals, whose primacy is organizational, not motivational.

Mann's load-bearing formulation:

> "Societies are constituted of multiple overlapping and intersecting sociospatial networks of power."

He adds the anti-totality polemic: societies are not social systems; there are no subsystems/levels of a whole reducible ultimately to mode of production, culture, or military form; humans are "social, but not societal, animals" — they need power relations for varied goals without generating identical sociospatial totalities.

## Semantic History

Mann's formulation sits in neo-Weberian historical sociology against Parsonian systems theory, structural-functionalism, orthodox Marxism, evolutionism, and diffusionism — orthodoxies he holds are marred by conceiving society as an unproblematic unitary totality. Related network language in later sociology (formal social-network analysis, world-systems) is adjacent but not identical: Mann's networks are *organizational means of power* (logistics, control, communication), not only graphs of ties. Institutionalization partially merges networks into dominant structures; the driving force of history is restless drives that **outrun** institutions — intentionally or *interstitially* (e.g. his reading of the bourgeoisie rising "between the pores" of feudalism and creating new overlapping networks, not merely changing one society).

## Operationalizations

Measured by logistics and communication: capacity to organize and control people, materials, and territories. Cross-classified as [[extensive-power]]/[[intensive-power]] and [[authoritative-power]]/[[diffused-power]]. Mass compliance is explained as **organizational outflanking** — subordinates lack collective organization to resist, embedded in power organizations controlled by others — more than pure value consensus, force, or exchange. Organizations and functions are "promiscuous": real networks lack simple one-to-one mapping onto ideal-typical IEMP sources.

## Applications

Attributed throughout *SSP* Vol. I (Mann 1986):

- **Ch. 1 apparatus** — [[iemp-model]] as network theory; causal model of mess → institutionalization → interstitial reorganization; sources as "tracklaying vehicles" laying different gauges of track.
- **Prehistory–early civilization (Chs. 2–4)** — loose overlapping prehistoric networks; alluvial agriculture as unusually [[social-caging|caged]] network interacting with peripheral networks; Mesopotamia as dual small city-states within a segmental [[multi-power-actor-civilization]], not a unitary endogenous dynamic.
- **Empires and multi-power fields (Chs. 5–9)** — two interaction levels (imperial core vs civil-society forces) in Assyria and Rome; Persia sitting loosely atop cosmopolitan Middle Eastern networks; Greek multistate "archipelago" without unitary economic system.
- **Ideological ecumenes (Chs. 10–11)** — Christian ecumene as transcendent network cutting across economic/military/political networks; salvation religions as solutions to imperial contradictions of social identity.
- **European dynamic (Chs. 12–15)** — medieval Europe as multiple acephalous networks under Christendom; dual public king / private magnate political networks; capitalism and national states as overlapping extensive networks (state-bounded vs more extensive economic society); contemporary confederal dilemma: "In which society do you live?"

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics may charge underspecification relative to formal network methods, residual Eurocentrism of the narrative spine ("world time" along a western leading edge), and underplaying of non-organizational cultural meaning. Mann himself stresses theory captures only broad contours (broken lines in his causal diagram). Keep as Mann's construct unless independent convergence is documented.
