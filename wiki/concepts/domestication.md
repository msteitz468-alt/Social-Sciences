# Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Domestication** denotes the process by which human groups and plant or animal populations enter long-term mutual dependence such that the non-human population’s reproduction, morphology, and/or behavior change under (conscious or unconscious) human selection. In archaeology it is analytically distinct from **agriculture** (systematic, purposeful modification of plant/animal environments to increase productivity — W&O definition) and **cultivation** (growing plants from seeds, bulbs, shoots, etc. — Blumler & Byrne via W&O: cultivated plants need not be domesticated; cultivation may long precede domestication) and from **[[sedentism]]** (year-round residence). The three often co-occur but need not (attributed textbook frame: [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 6).

Rindos (via W&O): domestication as **coevolutionary mutualism** in which a taxon diverges from its gene pool and enters symbiotic protection/dispersal with its consumer; humans alter dormancy, dispersal, defenses, and animal traits (meat, milk, fleece, docility, etc.). Classic illustration: maize cob loses natural seed dispersal.

## Semantic History

Victorian and early anthropological usage often collapsed domestication into a stage of “civilization.” Mid-20th-c. Near Eastern prehistory (Braidwood; Childe’s [[neolithic-revolution]]) made plant/animal domestication a research problem with archaeological criteria. Later work split coevolutionary (Rindos), climatic-stress, population-pressure (Cohen), cultural-materialist (Harris), resource-reliability (Hayden), social/feasting (Bender; Hayden), and symbolic (Hodder *domus*) models. Genetic and microbotanical methods revised centers and dates repeatedly — attribute each claim to its source year. As of W&O 2007: after ~2.5 million years as hunter-foragers, domestication and cultivation began ~10,000 years ago not by single-center diffusion alone but independently in many places involving many species.

Historically, largest complex civilizations rested mainly on six plant genera as caloric engines: wheat, barley, millet, rice, maize, potatoes (other crops and animals important but secondary — W&O pattern claim).

## Operationalizations

**Plants (as of W&O 2007 textbook survey):** tougher rachis and simultaneous ripening; weaker glumes; wider environmental tolerance; six-row vs two-row kernel arrangements — against wild brittle-rachis, tough-glume ecology. Morphometrics, seed non-shattering, phytoliths/starch, aDNA.

**Animals:** presence outside natural range; morphological change (e.g., horn form); abrupt relative abundance increase; age/sex profiles differing from wild herds (preferential young male slaughter for milk/wool economies).

**Method note:** flotation recovers carbonized plants central to domestication research; faunal analysis with taphonomic attention reconstructs diet (W&O methods chapter). Criteria and thresholds vary by taxon and lab tradition; textbook surveys lag specialist revisions.

**Intention debate (positions):** Rindos — human intention neither archaeologically recoverable nor logically necessary; people could select variation but not create it or know future importance of products. Counter-position — intentional selection sped domestication almost from the beginning.

## Applications

- **SW Asia (best-known / possibly earliest center as of W&O 2007):** domestic sheep, goats, cattle, pigs, wheat, barley across a wide zone; Natufian and other Epipaleolithic cases; Ohalo II (~19 kya) wild cereals without agricultural lifeways then; Abu Hureyra possible early domesticated rye (Hillman; contested). By ~8 kya domestic sheep/goats widespread into Greece/S Europe; cattle on Anatolian Plateau ~7.8 kya; pigs substantial mammal bone share at many sites 8–4.7 kya.
- **Mesoamerica:** maize chronology revised (Fritz: may begin shortly before ~3500 BC among less-mobile foragers; earliest Tehuacán cobs re-dated ~≤4,700 years ago — preservation debates). Substantial farming/villages scanty until shortly after 2000 BC.
- **Eastern North America:** indigenous oil/starch seeds (goosefoot, sumpweed, sunflowers, etc.); morphological domestication signals ~2050–1050 B.C., but Bruce Smith argues cultigens not major staples until ~1,000 years later; maize important staple only after ~A.D. 1000. O’Brien coevolutionary model (after Rindos) via W&O.
- **Indus / South Asia:** Mehrgarh shortly after 7000 BCE; independent zebu (*Bos indicus*) domestication (Meadow; Loftus et al. DNA via W&O); cotton ~7000–5000 BCE timing unclear.
- **China / Andes / other centers:** rice/millet; potatoes — independent packages in world-prehistory teaching.
- Flannery “third-choice foods”: major seed crops from harder-to-process but storable, plentiful, genetically malleable annuals first eaten heavily under necessity.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Equating domestication with progress or with “the Neolithic package” fails comparative cases; centers of origin multiply; sequences reverse ([[sedentism]] before cereals in SW Asia vs plants before villages in Mesoamerica).
- Oasis Hypothesis (Childe) weakened when wild cereal habitats and timing failed to match; climate returns without full oasis package (McCorriston & Hole; Moore & Hillman Younger Dryas; Richerson, Boyd & Bettinger Holocene climate enabling agriculture).
- Harlan’s wild-emmer harvest experiment: natural-habitat dwellers may have had least incentive to farm.
- Health-cost literature (Cohen & Armelagos and successors) challenges simple improvement narratives (positions).
- Genetic claims supersede rapidly — date-stamp “(as of Author Year)”; W&O 2007 is a mid-2000s textbook snapshot.
- Four-way non-identity: domesticates ≠ archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ people.

## Natufian context ([[bar-yosef-valla-natufian-foragers-2013]])

The [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] documents intensive wild-plant use, storage, and grinding **before** morphological cereal domestication is secure. Rosen (2013 phytoliths) finds no clear Late Natufian cultivation signature in her samples (small-seeded weeds often exceed cereals). Dog–human association and [[commensalism-archaeology|commensal]] niche construction (Tchernov tradition; Weissbrod et al. 2013 critique) are related but distinct trajectories from plant domestication. Path-to-farming positions: [[natufian-younger-dryas-debate]]; [[agricultural-revolution]].

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Plant definition:** origin of agriculture tied to domestic cereals/legumes/flax requiring human intervention in plant life cycles; key trait = non-shattering ears that “wait for the harvester” (Zohary via Mithen); single-gene mutations + synchronized germination.
- **Sickle pathway:** sickle-cutting (vs basket-beating) preferentially collects rare non-brittle mutants; repeated reseeding can fix domestic forms; Hillman & Davies: ~20 cycles ideal, ~200–250 years realistic (attributed).
- **Natufian lag:** Natufian cereals morphologically wild; first domestic grains ≥1 millennium later (Abu Hureyra, Tell Aswad, [[jericho|Jericho]]).
- **Dog first:** Mallaha/Hayonim size-reduced dogs as true domestication via village waste/vermin niches; woman–puppy burial among persuasive early cases (attributed).
- **Sheep/goat:** after dog, earliest livestock; Beidha goats ~80% of bones, smaller body size, slaughter peak ~2 years (not milk-oriented); geography debated — central (Syria/SE Turkey) or eastern Fertile Crescent by 8000 BC or earlier; Ganj Dareh strongest early goat case; Abu Hureyra herding by ~7500 BC after gazelle-drive phase (as of Mithen 2003).
- **Pathways into herding:** Hole — manage scarcity via forage, fences, orphans; Mithen — Natufian/PPNA pets, permanent-settlement breeding; women and children possibly critical carers (attributed).
- **Ideology by-product:** Karacadağ wild wheat closest to modern domestic wheat (~30 km from Göbekli; **1997 genetics as of Mithen**) — intensive cultivation to feed ceremonial gatherings may make domestication accidental by-product of ideology, not only Younger Dryas stress (theoretical).
- **Flax/textiles:** Nahal Hemar linen yarn (Alon & Bar-Yosef via Mithen) among earliest linen evidence; flax also at Jericho, Tell Aswad, Abu Hureyra (wild vs domestic unclear).
- **Çatalhöyük economy:** revised toward ordinary mixed farming rather than exceptional specialized economy alone (Mithen ch. 11 synthesis).
- **Exchange:** Turkish-source obsidian on all Early Neolithic sites; seed-grain may travel same networks as furs/feathers/meat/nuts (attributed).

Four-way non-identity: domesticates ≠ archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ people. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]], Technical Box VI.)*

Roberts identifies the primary morphological marker of cereal domestication as **loss of natural seed dispersal** — a tough, non-shattering rachis selected by sickle harvesting, plus free-threshing grain (Zohary and Hopf 1993); most early domesticates were self-pollinating, reproductively isolating them from wild progenitors. Crucially, Hillman and Davies' (1990) experimental work showed the shift to domestic-type morphology could occur in **under 200 years** once particular harvesting methods were adopted, even without deliberate selection — so domestication is archaeologically visible only some time after the cultural pressures began. See [[agricultural-revolution]], [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]].

## Ucko & Dimbleby seminar densification (1969)

*(Attributed to chapter authors in *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969|The Domestication and Exploitation of Plants and Animals]]* 1969; uncalibrated-era dates.)*

### Editorial frame (Ucko & Dimbleby)

- Domestication is a **multi-millennial, multi-regional process**, not a single catastrophe; recurs in different areas and times; still ongoing.
- Critiques “hunters near starvation” and “only farming frees luxury culture”; cites Hole & Flannery leisure claim and ethnographic cases.
- Childe’s [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]] remains historiographically central but needs continuous revision under radiocarbon and environmental evidence.
- Desiccation as sole Near Eastern driver lacks direct support; Würm II Mediterranean/Zagros read as arid/cold with early post-glacial humidity increase (van Zeist pollen); [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] shifts attention to Anatolia; anthropogenic near-desert extension without climate change (Raikes).
- Pottery need not accompany domestication (pre-pottery villages).

### Plant ecology and genetics

- **Harris:** agriculture as man-modified ecosystems; generalized vs specialized ecosystems; plant domestication likelier among generalized gatherer-hunter-fishers at **ecotones**; vegeculture vs seed-culture as under-compared pathways.
- **Hawkes:** weedy tendency and **rubbish-heap / habitation-weed** pathway; primary vs secondary crops; rejects pure desiccation determinism in favour of plant attributes and human ecological agency.
- **Zohary:** progenitor geography for wheat and barley; non-brittle rachis as **result** of planting/harvest cycles; selfing as preadaptation; agricultural dispersal of founder cereals (as of Zohary 1969).
- **Darlington:** “silent millennia” of **unconscious** crop selection under tillage/reaping; Vavilov diversity centres re-read as secondary; strong **single Nuclear Zone** (Anatolia–Iran–Syria) monogenesis for Old World agriculture before ~7000 B.C. — POSITION, competes with multi-origin papers in the same volume (Alexander & Coursey yams; Pickersgill chilies; etc.).
- **Flannery:** [[broad-spectrum-revolution]]; cultivation on margins of wild-cereal optima; early farming not clear dietary improvement; four real consequences of domestication (means of production, labour division, stratification foundations, environmental irreversibility). See [[flannery-kent-v]].

### Method implication

Direct plant and animal remains, not tools alone, are the load-bearing evidence (Grimes; J. M. Renfrew plant methods; Bökönyi/Chaplin/Ducos animal methods). See [[paleoethnobotany]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[flotation]].

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* ch.5–6 and Postscript.)*

- **Pre-domestic agriculture:** cultivation detectable before full morphological domestication (Hillman/Anderson/Willcox weed-suite and related methods); barley-focused at [[jerf-el-ahmar|Jerf el Ahmar]]; einkorn/rye issues at Mureybet; Aswad emmer (Van Zeist); Netiv Hagdud tough-rachis percentages debated.
- **Corridor restriction:** earliest farming evidence in Levantine corridor triad, not pan-Levant.
- **Animals:** herding generalises in PPNB; bull symbolism precedes cattle economic preference and domestication at Mureybet; Halula domestic goat from ~8000 BC (no local wild goat — imports), cattle ~7700 BC; Aşıklı cultivated cereals **without** herding.
- **Zagros correction (notes):** Ganj Dareh not PPNA-contemporary farming centre after 14C revision; eastern Zagros agriculture not before eighth millennium BC as of his sources.
- Causal order: domestication follows [[revolution-of-symbols]] in his model — see [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]].

### Diamond GGS (1997, attributed)

[[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] (*[[diamond-guns-germs-steel-1997|GGS]]* Ch.7–9) emphasizes **unconscious selection** in early plant domestication, few “blockbuster” crops, and the **Anna Karenina principle** for big-mammal domestication (many necessary criteria; failure of any blocks success). “Ancient fourteen” large herbivores; major five nearly all Eurasian. Geographic distribution of candidates, not peoples’ inventiveness, is his preferred contrast. Contested reception: [[guns-germs-steel-geographic-determinism-debate]].

## Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Theoretical/position register throughout; derivative “connect the dots” synthesis.)*

### Expansive spectrum and fire

- **No magic moment:** There is no single line from hunting/foraging to agriculture or from savagery to civilization; planting a seed is one minor event in a deep history of landscape modification starting with *Homo erectus* and **fire** (Ch.2).
- **Fire as semidomesticate / niche construction:** Fire is mankind’s oldest landscaping tool — a “semidomesticate” with a life of its own; deliberate disturbance ecology and niche construction create mosaics of biodiversity and reduce the “radius of a meal.” Anthropogenic fire effects may overwhelm crop/livestock domestications in aggregate but register less because spread over hundreds of millennia. Cooking externalizes digestion, expands the edible range; Wrangham cooking–brain hypothesis reported; Scott argues fire has in a real sense **domesticated us** (species-level fire dependence) (Ch.1).
- **Whole-environment domestication:** Before the plough, fire was the preeminent preindustrial tool for domesticating **environments**, not only species; intermediate techniques short of full domestication include burning, weeding wild stands, pruning, coppicing, transplanting, selective hunting, stream management, raising juveniles (Ch.2).
- **Thin Anthropocene:** Scott dates a “thin” Anthropocene from hominid fire use (~400,000+ years ago) with further leaps at permanent settlement/agriculture/pastoralism (~12 kya) and the state (~6 kya in Mesopotamia) (Introduction).

### Process, not Rubicon

- Morphological markers (nonbrittle rachis, larger seeds; skeletal domesticates) appear well after cultivation; best analyses replace singular events with cultivation processes lasting **up to three millennia** and multiple scattered domestications of major crops; long “low-level food production” of plants not entirely wild and not fully domesticated (Introduction). By ~11–12 kya Fertile Crescent populations were modifying local “wild” plant communities millennia before clear morphological domestication of grains (Ch.2).
- **Founder crops** as self-pollinating annuals of disturbed soils (einkorn/emmer, barley, lentils, peas, chickpeas, bitter vetch, flax); “fully domesticated” = floral “basket case” dependent on human attentions; some taxa (oats, bananas, dogs, pigs) resist full domestication (Ch.2).
- Conventional subsistence “subspecies” (hunting, foraging, pastoralism, farming) make little historical sense: same people practiced all four; activities bleed into one another (Ch.2).

### Mutual / self-domestication and the *domus*

- **Expansive domestication of humans:** Broadest understanding of domestication as **control over reproduction** might apply not only to fire, plants, and animals but also to slaves, state subjects, and women in the patriarchal family (Preface provocation). Algaze (via Scott): “Early Near Eastern villages domesticated plants and animals. Uruk urban institutions, in turn, domesticated humans.”
- **Mutual agency:** Unclear who domesticated whom (dogs/humans); uninvited commensals; humans strapped to ploughing/planting/weeding/reaping routines — domesticated by the *[[domus-complex|domus]]* (Introduction; Ch.2). Pollan potato-slave and Evans-Pritchard Nuer–cattle mutual parasitism inverted as agency (Ch.2).
- **Self-domestication (speculative, flagged by Scott):** Humans as products of self-domestication under [[sedentism]], crowding, and cereal diets, analogous to other domus species; Helen Leach “distinctive syndrome” of size/stature/tooth reduction/facial shortening from shared domus environment (Ch.2). Caution: only ~240 human generations since first agriculture / ~160 since widespread — sweeping biological conclusions premature (Ch.2).
- **Deskilling frame:** Late Neolithic as contraction of attention to the natural world, diet, space, and perhaps ritual breadth despite enabling large-scale societies; cereal routines as early “civilizing process” metronome (Ch.2).

### Crop epidemiology and fragility

- Fixed continuous monocropped fields as permanent feedlots for pests, pathogens, and obligate weeds (“parasite” = “beside the grain”); northern European unprotected modern-barley experiment: yield cut ~half (disease/animals/weeds) — defense labor explains agriculture’s intensity (Ch.3). Domus rests on a “slender and fragile genetic foundation” — handful of crops/livestock, simplified landscape needing continuous subsidy from excluded nature (Ch.3).
