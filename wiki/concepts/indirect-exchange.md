# Indirect Exchange

## Definition as Coined

**Indirect exchange**, in [[blau-peter-m|Blau]], is exchange mediated by a social norm and the collectivity rather than conducted directly between two partners: "social norms substitute indirect exchange for direct transactions between individuals." Conforming to a norm means refraining from an advantageous direct transaction and being **compensated by the collectivity** (with [[social-approval|approval]]) for the advantage foregone — producing "indirect chains of exchange" (John helps Bill, Bill helps Joe, Joe helps John) in place of reciprocal dyads.

The paradigm case Blau analyzes is the **prisoner's dilemma** (via Rapoport; Deutsch's experiment found ~two-thirds of subjects make the distrustful choice): individually rational self-interested choices yield a jointly worse outcome, and only a social norm — prohibiting the tempting defection "as a matter of principle" — can rationally sustain the collectively optimal result. Norms are thus needed "precisely in situations where unrestrained pursuit of self-interest defeats the self-interest of all."

## Semantic History

The idea generalizes Lévi-Strauss's "generalized exchange" (indirect, cyclical reciprocity) from kinship to social norms at large, and prefigures later rational-choice accounts of norms and public goods (Coleman, Olson). Blau's contribution is to make indirect exchange the mechanism by which [[mediating-values|shared values]] extend exchange across a collectivity too large for direct reciprocity.

## Operationalizations

Identified structurally: whether conformity is rewarded by a specific partner (direct) or by third parties / the collectivity (indirect). Blau's cases: restriction-of-output norms rewarding solidarity over individual gain; professional detachment (the psychiatrist rewarded by colleague approval, not by patients — Parsons's psychotherapy case as "prototype"); bureaucratic impartiality; organized philanthropy and noblesse oblige.

## Applications

The mechanism carrying [[social-exchange]] into complex structures under [[mediating-values]]; the exchange-theoretic account of [[social-control]] and normative conformity; and the analytic basis for [[legitimation]] once authority operates through substituted indirect exchanges (subordinate ↔ peer approval; collectivity ↔ superior's contribution).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Explaining norm-conformity as compensated indirect exchange risks the program's general circularity: the "compensation" (approval) is posited to explain the very conformity by which it is inferred.
- The prisoner's-dilemma framing assumes the payoff structure it needs; whether real normative situations have that structure is an empirical question Blau treats illustratively.
