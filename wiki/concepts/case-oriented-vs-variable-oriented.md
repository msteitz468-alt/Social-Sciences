# Case-Oriented vs. Variable-Oriented Research

## Definition as Coined

The organizing distinction of [[ragin-charles|Charles Ragin]]'s *[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]* (1987) — a contrast between two strategies of social research (attributed throughout):

- **Case-oriented** methods are **holistic**: they "treat cases as whole entities and not as collections of parts (or as collections of scores on variables)," so relations between parts are understood within the context of the whole. Their goals are "both historically interpretive and causally analytic," producing "limited historical generalizations which are sensitive to context." Case-oriented work "give[s] precedence to the goal of appreciating complexity over the goal of achieving generality"; it uses theory to guide interpretation and the identification of causal factors, and prefers invariant statements about narrowly defined categories to probabilistic statements about broad ones.
- **Variable-oriented** methods are "radically analytic (because [they] break cases into parts — variables — that are difficult to reassemble into wholes)," theory-centered, and concerned with "assessing the correspondence between relationships discernible across many societies… and broad theoretically based images." They "give precedence to generality over complexity" and typically **test** hypotheses derived from theory.

Ragin ties this to a distinction between the **observational unit** (used in data collection and analysis) and the **explanatory unit** (used to account for the pattern of results) — a duality he argues is especially pronounced in comparative work (e.g., class-voting studies using individual-level data but societal-level explanations).

## Semantic History

The contrast crystallizes long-standing methodological tensions (the qualitative/quantitative and comparative/statistical divides; see [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]). Ragin's move (attributed) is to reframe them as a single metatheoretical divide he seeks to "bridge and transcend" rather than to choose sides on. In the 2014 introduction he restates the contrast as a seven-part **"conventional versus alternate template"** — Variables/Sets, Measurement/Calibration, Dependent variables/Qualitative outcomes, Given/Constructed populations, Correlations/Set-theoretic relations, Correlation matrices/Truth tables, Net effects/Causal recipes — locating the deepest difference in the **asymmetry** of set-theoretic (subset) relations versus the symmetry of correlation. He notes the case-oriented ("historically interpretive") pole "leans heavily on Weber."

## Operationalizations

The poles are not measured but instantiated in methods. Case-oriented logic runs on J.S. Mill's method of agreement and indirect method of difference and small-N comparison (see [[comparative-method]]); variable-oriented logic runs on multivariate statistical control across many cases. Ragin's [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]] operationalizes a **synthesis**: each truth-table row is a "type" of case (holistic/configurational), while the same conditions are examined across all cases (analytic) — "holistic in the way it compares different situations" yet bounded, since identical configurations pool into one row and the selected conditions cap the space of possible "wholes" (4 binary conditions → only 16 types).

## Applications

- **Strengths/weaknesses (Ragin's synthesis):** case-oriented work tends toward particularism (and is "incapacitated by a large number of cases" — comparisons grow combinatorially); variable-oriented work tends toward "abstract, and sometimes vacuous, generalizations" and is "incapacitated by complex, conjunctural causal arguments." Ragin reverses Neil Smelser's claim that N dictates method: "it is not the number of relevant cases that limits the selection of method… but the nature of the method that limits the number of cases."
- **Theoretical bias:** single-case studies foreground human agency and specific junctures; wide cross-national studies, disaggregating cases into variables, bias toward structural explanation.
- Ragin's three "combined strategy" exemplars (Shorter & Tilly's *Strikes in France*; Paige's *Agrarian Revolution*; Stephens's *Transition from Capitalism to Socialism*) are read as *combining* the strategies without genuinely *synthesizing* them.

**KKV as the pure variable-oriented template.** [[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994|King, Keohane & Verba]]'s *Designing Social Inquiry* (1994) is, in Ragin's terms, the fullest programmatic statement of the **variable-oriented** pole applied to qualitative research: it recasts the "case" as a bundle of [[observable-implications|observations]], defines causation via counterfactual [[causal-effect|causal effects]], and prescribes variable-based rules ([[selection-bias|avoid selecting on the dependent variable]], increase n, control confounds). Where Ragin seeks to *transcend* the divide with set-theoretic tools, KKV argue (attributed) the divide is merely stylistic and the variable-oriented logic is the one logic — the position contested at [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]].

See [[qualitative-comparative-analysis]], [[comparative-method]], [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]], [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The dichotomy is a heuristic contrast, not a strict taxonomy: real studies mix strategies, and Ragin's own point is that the poles are "surprisingly complementary." Treating the distinction as an either/or — or reading it as ranking one pole above the other (as Ragin accuses Smelser of doing for the case-oriented side) — misuses it. Whether QCA truly transcends the divide, rather than sitting on the variable-oriented side with configurational features, is part of the live dispute over QCA's standing.
