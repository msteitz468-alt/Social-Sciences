# Pragmatic Solidarity

## Definition as Coined

[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s term for solidarity with the poor that takes the form of
delivering concrete resources — effective medicines, food, clinical care — rather than
sympathy, research, or advocacy alone. In *Pathologies of Power*
([[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003]]) he defined it against both charity (which demeans)
and development (which defers), arguing that the victims of [[structural-violence|structural
violence]] identify their own needs and that "pragmatic solidarity" means meeting them now.

## Semantic History

Coined within Farmer's fusion of [[liberation-theology]] (the preferential option for the
poor, "accompaniment") with clinical medicine; taken up in global health as a program label
for the Partners In Health model of care delivery in Haiti, Peru, and Russian prisons. Usage
has stayed close to Farmer's original sense.

## Operationalizations

The concept is operationalized as the actual transfer of goods and services to the destitute sick:
second-line drugs and lab capacity purchased for Tomsk TB patients; antiretrovirals delivered to poor
Haitians through community health workers. Its measurable target is reduction of the [[outcome-gap]] —
the rich–poor outcome disparity that pragmatic solidarity is meant to shrink. Farmer contrasted it
explicitly with solidarity that stops at sentiment: "medicine becomes pragmatic solidarity when it is
delivered with dignity to the destitute sick."

## Applications

- Farmer offered MDR-tuberculosis treatment in Peruvian slums and Russian prisons as the
  paradigm case: treating "untreatable" patients rebuts cost-effectiveness arguments that
  ration care away from the poor (see [[tuberculosis]]).
- Used to reframe human rights work: making social and economic rights real rather than
  litigating civil-political violations after the fact.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics within global health have argued that the model is charismatic and donor-dependent
and thus hard to scale or reproduce without a Farmer-like broker; Farmer's response was that
scalability objections are themselves examples of managerial logics that ration care for the
poor (Farmer 2003).
