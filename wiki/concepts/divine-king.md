# Divine King (killing of the divine king)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]*, [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] argued that certain kings and priest-kings **embody** a divine or vegetative life on which the land's fertility depends; because that life must not decay with the king's natural weakness or death, the king is **killed** (or a temporary king, son, or animal substitute is killed) and the office — with the embodied life — passes to a vigorous successor. The charter case is the **King of the Wood** (*Rex Nemorensis*) at Nemi, who holds office by slaying his predecessor after breaking the Golden Bough. (All attributed to Frazer.)

## Semantic History

Became a staple of comparative religion and political anthropology discourse; later Africanist literature (e.g. discussions of Shilluk and other "divine kingship") engaged and revised Frazer. Distinct from mere sacral legitimation of monarchy: the load-bearing claim is **ritual killing / replacement timed to protect embodied cosmic life**.

## Operationalizations

Indicators in Frazer's scheme: regicide or mock-regicide rites; temporary kings; sacrifice of the king's son; succession myths; taboos fencing the king's body (burden of royalty).

## Applications

Organizing thesis of much of *The Golden Bough* (esp. ch. 24–28 and returns to Nemi); literary and popular reception of "sacred kingship."

## Critiques and Limitations

Comparative type overgeneralized from heterogeneous cases; Nemi reconstruction is conjectural; many "parallels" are literary or poorly sourced; later ethnography often finds different logics of kingship and sacrifice. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]], [[dying-and-rising-god]].

**Related (not identical):** Fortes & Evans-Pritchard’s [[mystical-values-of-office]] (1940) generalizes sacred warranty of African political office without requiring Frazer’s ritual regicide thesis.
