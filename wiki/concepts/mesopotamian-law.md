# Mesopotamian Law

Per [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]], the surviving Mesopotamian
"law codes" — Ur-Nammu (Ur III), Lipit-Ishtar of Isin, Eshnunna, and the [[code-of-hammurabi|Code
of Hammurabi]] — are **not** comprehensive legislation, nor case-law digests in the
modern sense, but reform-collections and royal apologia layered on top of a much
larger **unwritten** body of customary and case law that must be reconstructed from the
mass of ordinary legal-transaction documents (sale contracts, loan agreements,
lawsuits, letters). This is Postgate's own load-bearing interpretive position, argued
at length; treat it as attributed, not settled wiki-voice fact, though he marshals
converging evidence for it.

## Definition as Coined

Postgate frames Mesopotamian judicial institutions as serving four functions: (1)
settling disputes between individuals or groups; (2) enforcing decisions and
punishing offenses against society ("criminals"); (3) administering government
enactments; (4) authenticating and recording acts as legally valid. Crucially, neither
the Code of Hammurabi nor its predecessors defines court constitution, officials'
functions, evidentiary standards, or which verbal or enacted forms counted as legally
valid — these existed as unwritten background, recoverable only from the documentary
record of actual transactions and disputes (Postgate 1994).

## Semantic History

**The judicial pyramid.** Mesopotamian justice operated through a three-level
hierarchy: local councils, judges/courts, and the king. The **local council** — village
elders (*ālum*) or a town-quarter body (*bābtum*) headed by a "mayor" (*rabiānum*,
linking to central authority) — was the court of first resort. The Code of Hammurabi
itself reflects this level's role: a ward gives formal warning about a dangerous ox
(CH §251); a village is collectively liable for an uncaught robber's losses (CH §23) —
collective responsibility rather than adjudication per se. Judicial records show wards
examining witness statements and empaneling as a jury-like body drawing on local
knowledge, though not as a permanent court, and no direct documentation survives from
the local-council level itself (its decisions were apparently not usually written
down). The **king** stood as ultimate judicial authority and legitimator, a role
traceable to the *ensi* at [[lagash]] being "answerable to the gods" for social justice,
later assumed by the kings of Akkad and Ur III. Cases could be escalated to the king by
appeal, or automatically for "a case of life" (*dīn napištim*, e.g. murder), per
Eshnunna-code tradition.

**The Nippur homicide trial (Text 15:3).** A rare full trial-transcript-style record
(after Jacobsen 1959): three men — Nanna-sig, Ku-Enlila the barber, and Enlil-ennam, a
slave of Adda-kalla — killed the priest Lu-Inana; his wife Nin-dada was told and kept
silent. The king at Isin, Ur-Ninurta, referred the case back to the Assembly of Nippur
for verdict rather than deciding it personally. The Assembly debated whether Nin-dada
should also be executed — some argued she should die alongside the killers for
failing to report or avenge the murder, others questioned her guilt — and ultimately
ruled that all four, the three killers and the wife, should be put to death,
reasoning that her silence implied complicity, "her guilt is greater" than the
killers'. This case illustrates both the appellate role of the king (referring the
matter onward rather than ruling) and the Assembly's judicial function (see
[[city-state]]).

**Judicial procedure.** Courts sought and weighed both oral and written evidence:
testimony from litigants and witnesses; judges could seek clarification by writing to
local authorities or requesting further local investigation. Old written documents —
even a fifty-two-year-old damaged tablet in one case — were accepted as evidence,
generally alongside corroborating oral testimony.

**Oaths and the river ordeal.** Witnesses swearing oaths was standard practice, usually
unremarked in the record; oaths of promise not to challenge a transaction were common
in everyday legal acts. The "oath of purgation" — a solemn appeal to divine sanction —
was used when evidence was absent or contradictory: CH §266 allows a shepherd to clear
himself before a god for stock lost to a lion attack or illness, paralleling Ur
III-period shepherd oaths at the temple of Nin-mar(ki) in Girsu. Oaths were sworn on
divine symbols (Gula's dog, Marduk's weapon), sometimes with the symbol itself brought
to the disputed location. The **river ordeal** was a stronger sanction than the oath,
reserved by Hammurabi's time for otherwise-untestable grave accusations (sorcery,
adultery, homicide); in the earlier Akkad period it had been used even for trivial
civil disputes over ownership or debt. Its restriction to serious cases is, per
Postgate, "a late development," plausibly linked to increasing literacy reducing
reliance on oral-testimony disputes.

**Seals, oaths, and the constitutive-vs-evidentiary status of documents.** The
earliest land-sale documents (c. 2700 BCE, stone, sometimes carved) predate
cylinder-seal sealing, since stone cannot be sealed by rolling. Early clay house/field
sales at Shuruppak (c. 2500 BCE) are unsealed — validity rested on witnessed spoken or
enacted acts, with tablets possibly serving as mere administrative records rather than
strictly evidentiary instruments. Late Early Dynastic Girsu documents show a herald
performing symbolic sealing acts (driving a peg into a wall, applying oil) — public
authentication by city authority via the herald's own seal. By the Old Babylonian
period virtually every legal document was sealed (typically by the "ceder of right")
and witnessed — a norm Postgate compares to modern cheque-signing, persisting into the
first millennium BCE. Written loan and debt contracts are not attested before the
Akkad period at the earliest, though loans must have existed earlier without
documentary trace. Peripheral regions (Susiana, the Middle Euphrates) show more
elaborate written curse-formulae against oath-breakers than southern Mesopotamia —
Postgate attributes this to the relative weakness of the written record there, versus
reliance on oral or enacted sanctions (a claim about regional variation in evidentiary
practice, attributed).

## Operationalizations

**"The code in practice."** Postgate surveys the scholarly debate over whether the law
codes had genuine legal-practical effect. Evidence *for* practical application:
post-Hammurabi herding contracts specify shepherd liability for foot-rot deaths
exactly as CH's shepherd provisions do — absent in earlier contracts, suggesting
standard contract forms changed to conform to the code; judges' letters explicitly
invoke "the ordinance of our lord (the king)" (*şimdat šarrim*) as the basis for
verdicts, which Postgate says makes it "perverse to resist the conclusion that these
ordinances were indeed royal laws committed to writing, and applied in practice."
Evidence for *limited* application: scholars have had "the greatest difficulty" finding
textual proof that any code provision was directly cited or applied in a specific
case; the codes were likely "only a gloss" on a much larger body of unwritten
traditional law and covered only a fraction of actual court business; terminological
ambiguity between "royal ordinance" and separate economic "edicts" complicates the
evidence. Postgate does not resolve this debate; both readings are recorded as
positions.

The codes also incorporate pre-existing traditional legal devices with enhanced
status: assessing a negligent farmer's or tenant's liability by comparison to
neighboring yields (CH §§42, 55, 62) has a precedent in a Rim-Sin-era Larsa document
("he shall pay like those to his left and right"); the shepherd/lion-attack
distinction, already implicit in Ur III oaths of purgation, is formalized in CH.

## Applications

**The talion-as-Amorite-intrusion hypothesis (Postgate's own speculation, explicitly
flagged as such).** The Ur III law code penalizes accidental death or injury
monetarily; the Code of Hammurabi instead scales *talion* punishment ("eye for eye,"
CH §§196–9) by the victim's social class — free man (*awīlum*), *muškēnum*, or slave.
Postgate offers, explicitly as his own opinion ("In my opinion"), the hypothesis that
the appearance of physical/talionic punishment specifically in the *latest* of the
codes represents "the traditional law of the desert" — an Amorite or nomadic legal
tradition — intruding on the older southern urban legal tradition, arriving with the
Amorite dynasty to which Hammurabi belonged. Postgate himself states this cannot be
confirmed without comparative evidence from earlier Amorite Babylon or a comparable
northern state, and it is recorded here strictly as his own attributed speculation, not
a settled finding.

## Critiques and Limitations

The central limitation, as with [[code-of-hammurabi]], is that the surviving codes are
a fraction of the actual legal system: the local-council level of justice left almost
no direct documentary trace, so reconstruction of everyday legal practice depends
heavily on incidental legal-transaction documents (sale contracts, marriage contracts,
inheritance divisions — see [[household]]) rather than on the codes themselves. The
talion-as-Amorite-intrusion hypothesis is unconfirmed speculation by Postgate's own
admission. The "code in practice" question — legislation versus royal apologia/gloss —
remains an open scholarly dispute, recorded here as competing positions rather than
resolved.
