# Double Descent

> Charter cases in *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]* (1950): [[forde-daryll|Forde]] on the [[yako|Yakö]]; [[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] on [[nuba|Nuba Hills]] (Nyaro, Tullishi). Framed in Radcliffe-Brown’s four-type kinship typology.

## Definition as Coined

**Double descent** (also double unilineal kin-group organization, dual descent) names systems that recognize **both** a patrilineal and a matrilineal series of groups, each carrying **different** rights and obligations. It is not the same as bilateral/cognatic kinship (where no exclusive unilineal corporate partition exists).

[[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] placed double lineage systems as one of four ideal types: father-right, mother-right, purely cognatic systems, and double lineage systems. Of the last: “Every individual has a well-defined set of relationships within his patrilineal lineage and clan, and another within his matrilineal lineage and clan.” He argued double unilineal organization provides “a special way of organizing a system of divided right by a cross-segmentation of the society.”

[[forde-daryll|Forde]]’s theoretical opening: ascription of status within groups based on one unilineal reckoning “does not exclude the concurrent establishment of rights and obligations… with kinsmen in other lines of descent.” Patrilineal and matrilineal reckoning “are not, as was once assumed, mutually exclusive. Both may be operative as principles of affiliation and group organization in distinct social fields, so that rights of succession and inheritance fall into distinct categories.”

## Semantic History

- **Older assumption:** matrilineal and patrilineal “systems” as mutually exclusive types, often ordered as evolutionary stages ([[mother-right-and-father-right]], [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]).
- **ASKAM 1950:** insisted double unilineal organization is possible, empirically attested in Africa, and often misunderstood; Forde noted systems had long been encountered but rarely closely studied (Herero cited as still awaiting full analysis via Schapera 1945).
- **Post-ASKAM refinement:** debates over how corporate both poles must be; whether ritual/spiritual affiliation (e.g. Ashanti *ntoro*) counts as “descent group”; morphological vs evolutionary readings of “transitional” dual forms (Nadel’s caution).
- **Ashanti correction:** [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] rejected equal double-descent readings of Ashanti (Murdock-style equality; Rattray on *ntoro* as corporate patri-sib with marriage ban). Fortes held matriliny (*abusua*) alone provided corporate jural-political organization; *ntoro* expressed ritual value of paternity without corporate group or inheritance rights — matrilineal unilineal with complementary filiation, not equal double descent (attributed).

## Operationalizations

| Case (ASKAM) | Patrilineal pole | Matrilineal pole | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Yakö (Forde) | *eponama* / *kepun* — residence, farm sites, oil palms, patri-locality, *epundet* shrine | *lejima* (matriclan) — inheritance, homicide compensation, mystical fertility; corporately organized | Full simultaneous development of both until colonial/Western modification |
| Nyaro / Kao-Nyaro (Nadel) | Gentes — local/patrilocal | Matri-clans — social identity; dual membership | Nadel: effective integration; “success” case of dual structure |
| Tullishi (Nadel) | Weaker/“oblique” link to father’s matri-clan; local group not fully recognized “in descent terms” | Matri-clan identity; inheritance tensions | Nadel: incomplete double; “failure” relative to Nyaro — **evaluative framing, attribute** |
| Herero (cited) | Double unilineal noted in literature | Awaiting full analysis as of Schapera 1945 | Comparative pointer only |
| Ashanti (Fortes — **not** double) | *ntoro* ritual paternity, not corporate patri-sib | *abusua* corporate matriliny | Correction against equal dual-descent readings |

Nadel’s morphological note: “Though a fully fledged double-descent system may be rare, the combination of paternal and maternal affiliation in some form is widespread.” He used “transitional” in a morphological sense and left open whether it could mean an evolutionary phase.

## Applications

- **[[yako]]:** Forde’s full ethnography of dual corporate organization; half-siblings share father’s *kepun* but diverge in matrilineal rights; marriage money and divorce interact with both poles; Western institutions modifying matrilineal features in the generation of fieldwork.
- **[[nuba]]:** Nadel’s comparison of Nyaro (full dual exogamy: maternal clan + local/gens) vs Tullishi (avoidances focused on own clan + father’s clan; “oblique” relationship to father’s matrilineal group). Nadel theorized sex polarity and dual recognition: incomplete dual structure (Tullishi) associated with open sex-antagonism; full dual (Nyaro) absorbs/neutralizes it better; unilineal systems can host sex antagonism without dual structure (independent variable) — all attributed.
- **Volume continuum:** Radcliffe-Brown’s diagnostic — Lozi cognatic minimum; Ashanti matrilineal; Zulu/Nuer patrilineal; Yakö both.
- **Neighboring Cross River peoples** (Forde): Ekumuru, Abayong, Agwa’aguna, Enna, Abini, Agoi, Asiga with similar dual kin-grouping.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Corporate threshold:** How “double” must both sets of groups be (full corporation vs complementary filiation / ritual affiliation)? Fortes’s Ashanti correction is the classic caution against over-counting dual poles.
- **Nadel’s success/failure language** (Nyaro vs Tullishi) is structural-functional evaluation — record as position, not wiki-voice fact about integration quality.
- **Colonial change:** Forde and Nadel both note Western institutional impact; double systems at mid-century are not timeless.
- **Rarity vs overlooking:** Nadel held full double systems seemed unusual but possibly overlooked; do not treat rarity as evolutionary residue.
- Later ethnography rechecked partitioning of rights, residence, and inheritance under cash economies and legal change.

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Conclusion.)* David Schneider’s Yap ethnography, produced under postwar [[descent-theory]] orthodoxy, became a textbook “double descent” case (exogamous patrilineages + matrilineal clans). Schneider’s 1970s reanalysis, beginning from Yap conceptions (*tabinau* rights via work/exchange, not classic patrilineal corporation), is Kuper’s exhibit that theory defines which facts count — no single exception could refute the [[primitive-society]] package.
