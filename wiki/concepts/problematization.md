# Problematization

## Definition as Coined

**Problematization** is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s name (attributed) for the object of a "history of thought" as distinct from a history of behaviors or of representations: "to define the conditions in which human beings 'problematize' what they are, what they do, and the world in which they live." In *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984) it is the pivot that reorients the whole sexuality project. Foucault's guiding question — "why is sexual conduct, why are the activities and pleasures that attach to it, an object of moral solicitude?" — refuses the ready answer of "fundamental interdictions": **"the interdiction is one thing, the moral problematization is another."**

## Semantic History

Though the term is consolidated in 1984, Foucault presents problematization as the retrospective description of his *whole* body of work, listing his earlier studies as instances of the same method (attributed): "a problematization of madness and illness" out of medical and social practices; "a problematization of life, language, and labor" under epistemic rules; "a problematization of crime and criminal behavior" out of punitive practices under a disciplinary model. *The Use of Pleasure* adds the problematization of sexual activity via practices of the self. The concept thus reframes the earlier "archaeology" and "[[genealogy]]" as two dimensions of one analysis: an **archaeological** dimension examining the forms of problematization themselves, and a **genealogical** dimension analyzing their formation out of practices. Foucault situates his study "at the point where an archaeology of problematizations and a genealogy of practices of the self intersect."

## Operationalizations

There is no measurement; problematization is reconstructed textually and historically. Its analytic payoff in *The Use of Pleasure* is a **counterintuitive finding** (attributed): the intensity of moral problematization does *not* track the strength of prohibition. The domains of most intense Greek moral solicitude — the body, the wife, boys, and truth — were precisely those in which a free man could act "without encountering any major prohibition." "The moral solicitude is strong precisely where there is neither obligation nor prohibition." Problematization is therefore what a history of morality must explain when it stops equating morality with its code (see [[ethics-foucauldian]]).

## Applications

The concept licenses Foucault's method for the whole volume: instead of asking what deep interdictions the Greeks hid behind their sexual austerity, he asks in what forms sexual behavior "was problematized, becoming an object of concern, an element for reflection, and a material for stylization" — i.e., an [[arts-of-existence|art of existence]]. Beyond Foucault, "problematization" has become a widely borrowed methodological term across the social sciences and critical policy studies (e.g., "how a problem is problematized") — a migration from his specific historical usage to a general analytic of how issues get constituted as objects of thought and governance.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Critics note the term's **retrospective** character: applied backward across two decades of very different books, it can look like a unifying gloss imposed after the fact rather than a method consistently followed (attributed).
- In wide reception "problematization" is often loosened into a synonym for "framing" or "construction," losing Foucault's specific claim that it names the conditions under which something *becomes thinkable as a problem* — a drift this page flags as semantic dilution.
- Like all of Foucault's genealogical apparatus, problematization reads its archive tactically; specific historical claims made under its banner are his readings, not settled history.
