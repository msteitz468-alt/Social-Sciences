# Nonstate Spaces

## Definition as Coined

**Nonstate spaces** are regions and livelihood systems hard for states to tax and administer: rugged terrain, wetlands, mobile herding, diverse foraging, [[swidden]]. [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] develops the concept most fully for highland mainland Southeast Asia in *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* (2009) — now **vault-primary** — and projects the same logic onto archaic hinterlands in *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017).

Scott’s load-bearing move (2009): nonstate space is **not** simply “hills” or high altitude. States arise where **concentrated grain production** is feasible; places where geography makes durable authority hard (mountains, swamps, forests, trackless marsh, some deltas and deserts) are nonstate relative to that appropriation module. In mainland SE Asia the grain niche is low-elevation irrigated rice → “valley states” vs “hill peoples”; Andean patterns can reverse elevation where the grain niche differs. Ideal **state space** = ideal space of appropriation: manpower and fields concentrated within cheap reach of a core.

Clinically, Scott defines **“barbarian”** as a *political* category — people adjacent to a state but not in it, “on the outside looking in” — not a culture, lack of culture, or evolutionary stage toward taxpayer status. “The line on the frontier where the barbarians begin is that line where taxes and grain end.” Ming “cooked” vs “raw” splits could divide identical language/culture groups by tax registration and Han magistracy (“entered the map”).

Scott uses “barbarian” ironically as shorthand for nonstate peoples — terms invented in state centers to stigmatize non-subjects. Barbarians : domesticated subjects :: wildlife : livestock :: weeds : crops — the undomesticated that threaten the grain-state *domus* (2017 framing continuous with 2009 civilization-as-subject-coding).

## Operationalizations

### Geographic residual and friction of terrain (Scott 2009)

Mountains, steppes, dense forest, swamps, deltas, deserts, sea — geographies unsuitable for intensive grain appropriation. Many ethnonyms are state geographic labels (“hill people”). Flat land lowers transport cost; rugged terrain multiplies it (Ibn Khaldun via Scott).

**Friction of terrain** (Scott 2009): political space measured in **travel-time**, not flat km. Premodern oxcart teams can eat a cartload of grain before ~250 km; foot travel ~24 km/day on flat dry ground, much less in hills; water transport is the great friction-nullifier. Standard maps mislead; “funhouse” maps that enlarge high-friction zones better demarcate state vs nonstate space. Effective agrarian state radius becomes tenuous beyond roughly a few hundred km on good roads—far less in hills. **Altitude niches** order the SE Asian massif (e.g. Hmong often 1000–1800 m).

**Post-1945 scope limit (Scott):** distance-demolishing technologies (rail, all-weather roads, telegraph/phone, airpower, helicopters, IT) so reduce friction that the analysis “largely ceases to be useful” for late-20th-c. SE Asia—the **last great enclosure**.

### Zomia as nonstate region (Scott 2009)

[[zomia]] (term from Willem van Schendel; popularized by Scott): lands above ~300 m from Vietnam’s Central Highlands to NE India across five SE Asian states and four Chinese provinces — ~2.5 million km², ~80–100 million minority peoples. Scott’s thesis: largest remaining incompletely incorporated region; peoples as **maroon/runaway** communities over two millennia; residence = **[[shatter-zone]]** / zone of refuge; signal trait = relative **statelessness** (rare plateau/trade-node hill states are exceptions). Hills as cultural refusal as well as political resistance (Braudel/Wolters altitude limits on “civilization”).

### Subsistence that defeats taxation

**Escape agriculture (Scott 2009):** analyze crops and systems for **escape value** — [[swidden]] (mobile, diverse, hard to cadastre); root/tuber crops (underground, staggered harvest); mixed portfolios; foraging at the extreme (Jonsson gradient via Scott: forage → swidden → fixed wet-rice as increasing appropriability). Michael Dove and Harold Conklin’s swidden teaching is acknowledged in the 2009 preface.

**Scott 2017 archaic list (kept):**

- Hunters/gatherers: dispersed, mobile, perishable diverse takings
- Horticulturalists: forest plots, underground harvest
- Swiddeners: diverse and mobile
- Specialized pastoralists: dispersal; Ottoman efforts to tax at lambing/shearing still difficult
- Wetland multi-web foragers: common-property free-living resources; no single monopolizable staple; diverse tempos defy central accounting

Scott (2017) argues what is required for state making is not ecological richness as such but **wealth as appropriable, measurable dominant grain** plus an administrable cultivating population. Southern Mesopotamian wetlands produced wealth and towns for more than a millennium without states; the Nile Delta was rich yet coded as hostility/resistance; Chinese state power “hopped” between loess patches, leaving hills and delta as barbarian/diverse zones.

### Social forms: evasion and prevention

Physical dispersion, mobility, segmentary/“jellyfish” kinship, acephaly, pliable ethnic identities, prophetic/millenarian leadership — dual program of **avoiding incorporation** and **preventing indigenous state crystallization** ([[state-evasion]]). Open common property underwrites exit. Temporary “shadow empire” / booty-capitalist hill hierarchies (Barfield via Scott) can parasitize valley hosts and collapse with them.

**Walls and flight:** State elites wall to keep taxpaying cultivators *in* as much as barbarians out (Lattimore on Great Wall(s); anti-Amorite walls; Ur III centralization — 2017). Flight from early state domains was, Scott holds, quite common but relegated to obscure legal codes because it contradicts the civilizing-benefactor narrative.

**Secondary primitivism:** Over time an increasing share of nonstate peoples were not “pristine primitives” but ex–state subjects who chose (often desperately) to keep the state at arm’s length — Clastres-adjacent “going over to the barbarians”; refuge zones as shatter zones of linguistic/cultural complexity. State breakdowns often experienced as improvement in safety, nutrition, and social order (Scott’s position).

## Applications

### Zomia and SE Asian state formation (Scott 2009)

Hill–valley dialectic as coevolution: impossible, Scott argues, to account for valley states without Zomia’s role in formation and collapse. Padi cores concentrate manpower (often via slave-raiding and war, not only attraction); hills absorb refugees and never-subjects. Civilization discourse recodes state-subject status. Orality, ethnogenesis-as-construction, and millenarian politics operationalize nonstate life as adaptive repertoire. Study: [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study]].

### Wetlands and early towns without states (Scott 2017)

Scott (via Pournelle) argues Ubaid multi-web wetland subsistence (fish, birds, turtles, diverse plants) supported sedentism and proto-urban settlements (~6500 BCE southern alluvium) without plough-grain state modules. Concentration alone does not yield states; the state module needs concentrated *agricultural* fixed-field manpower.

### Golden age of barbarians (Scott 2017)

Scott posits a multi-millennial period from the first states until perhaps ~1600 CE when nonstate peoples thrived relative to state subjects: better to be barbarian when states exist but are not too strong. States as juicy sites for plunder *and* as trading posts (ores, timber, hides, slaves for textiles, grain, metals). Even at Roman and early Han peaks, effective state control was modest; vast majority of world population remained nonstate (hunters, gatherers, marine collectors, horticulturalists, swiddeners, pastoralists, ungoverned farmers).

**Late-barbarian balance sheet (Scott’s position):** multi-food-web subsistence → less vulnerability to single failure; likely healthier and longer-lived (especially female); more advantageous trade → more leisure; freer than the “celebrated yeoman farmer” under hierarchical agriculture and the state.

**Two deeply melancholy aspects:** (1) trade goods included other nonstate peoples sold into bondage — chain of predation reinforcing the state core; (2) sale of martial skills as mercenaries (catching runaways, repressing revolts) so barbarians “willingly dug their own grave.”

### Raiding, protection rackets, dark twins (Scott 2017 + 2009)

Density of grain, population, and livestock is both source of state power and fatal vulnerability to mobile raiders. Pure raiding is self-liquidating; stable extraction becomes a protection racket hard to distinguish from the archaic state itself. Reverse tribute (Han–Xiongnu, etc.) as joint sovereignty. Lattimore: civilization created its own barbarian plague — “dark twins.” Mounted pastoralist confederations as Barfield’s “shadow empire” / “state in waiting.” Zomia as dark twin of valley state-making (2009 conclusion).

### Collapse and emancipation

Abandonment of the state may be experienced as emancipation — greater freedom/mobility, avoided combat — without denying predation outside the state. Apparent “regression” to foraging/pastoralism/swidden may be prudent adaptation (Adams’s nomad–sedentary continuum; 2009 secondary adaptation thesis). Well-being of a population must never be confounded with power of a court.

### Global family of extrastate zones (Scott 2009)

Scott generalizes beyond Zomia: orang laut and mangrove/delta refugia (“watery Zomia”); maroons; Cossacks; Berbers; Gypsies; pastoral nomads; colonial conquest refugees — diagnostic commonalities of ethnic/linguistic complexity, appropriation-resistant subsistence, and political amphibiousness.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Romanticization risk** — Scott himself marks barbarian slaving, mercenary service, and hill predation; nonstate life is not a utopia. Keep all welfare comparisons as **Scott’s position**.
- **Contested Zomia construct** — geographic/analytic unity disputed; massif diversity may exceed one political-ecology thesis; van Schendel’s and Scott’s maps differ.
- **Post-1945 self-limit** — does not answer all earlier exceptions (highland states, terraced wet-rice, willing incorporation); elective affinity ≠ universal law.
- **Constructionist over-read** — treating nearly every cultural trait as escape strategy risks functionalism; specialists defend deeper cultural continuity.
- **State-source bias:** Knowledge of barbarian threats comes from self-interested court scribes (“Barbarian Histories”); Scott frames this as historiographic problem.
- **Four-way non-identity:** “Barbarian” ethnonyms in state sources are political/administrative labels, often loose confederacies — not coherent cultures, languages, or genetic populations. Amorites, Scythians, Xiongnu, Zomia labels as Scott reads them, were usually military or administrative agglomerations of disparate peoples.
- **Golden-age dating (2017):** Scott calls precise dates a “fool’s errand”; ~1600 CE is a generous marker of state hegemony, not a hard archaeological boundary.
- ***Seeing Like a State* still pending** as full modern-legibility primary; 2009/2017 do not replace 1998 high-modernism densification.
