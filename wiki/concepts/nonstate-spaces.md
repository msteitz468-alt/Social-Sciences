# Nonstate Spaces

## Definition as Coined

**Nonstate spaces** are regions and livelihood systems hard for states to tax and administer: rugged terrain, wetlands, mobile herding, diverse foraging, swidden. In *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017), [[scott-james-c|Scott]] projects the logic of *The Art of Not Being Governed* (2009; Zomia / Southeast Asian highlands — not yet vault-primary) onto the deep past of the first states and their hinterlands.

Scott argues that what is required for state making is not ecological richness as such but **wealth as appropriable, measurable dominant grain** plus an administrable cultivating population. Areas of great but diverse abundance — wetlands offering dozens of subsistence options to a mobile population — are, because of their **illegibility and fugitive diversity**, not zones of successful state making. Southern Mesopotamian wetlands produced wealth and towns for more than a millennium without states; the Nile Delta was rich yet coded as hostility/resistance; Chinese state power “hopped” between loess patches, leaving hills and delta as barbarian/diverse zones.

Clinically, Scott defines **“barbarian”** as a *political* category — people adjacent to a state but not in it, “on the outside looking in” — not a culture, lack of culture, or evolutionary stage toward taxpayer status. “The line on the frontier where the barbarians begin is that line where taxes and grain end.” Ming “cooked” vs “raw” splits could divide identical language/culture groups by tax registration and Han magistracy (“entered the map”).

Scott uses “barbarian” ironically as shorthand for nonstate peoples — terms invented in state centers to stigmatize non-subjects. Barbarians : domesticated subjects :: wildlife : livestock :: weeds : crops — the undomesticated that threaten the grain-state *domus*.

## Operationalizations

**Geographic residual:** Mountains, steppes, dense forest, swamps, deltas, deserts, sea — geographies unsuitable for intensive grain appropriation. Many ethnonyms are state geographic labels (“hill people”). Flat land lowers transport cost; rugged terrain multiplies it (Ibn Khaldun via Scott).

**Subsistence that defeats taxation (Scott 2017):**

- Hunters/gatherers: dispersed, mobile, perishable diverse takings
- Horticulturalists: forest plots, underground harvest
- Swiddeners: diverse and mobile
- Specialized pastoralists: dispersal; Ottoman efforts to tax at lambing/shearing still difficult
- Wetland multi-web foragers: common-property free-living resources; no single monopolizable staple; diverse tempos defy central accounting

**Walls and flight:** State elites wall to keep taxpaying cultivators *in* as much as barbarians out (Lattimore on Great Wall(s); anti-Amorite walls; Ur III centralization). Flight from early state domains was, Scott holds, quite common but relegated to obscure legal codes because it contradicts the civilizing-benefactor narrative. Archaic codes fill with anti-flight injunctions states often could not enforce.

**Secondary primitivism:** Over time an increasing share of nonstate peoples were not “pristine primitives” but ex–state subjects who chose (often desperately) to keep the state at arm’s length — Clastres-adjacent “going over to the barbarians”; refuge zones as “shatter zones” of linguistic/cultural complexity. State breakdowns often experienced as improvement in safety, nutrition, and social order (Scott’s position).

## Applications

### Wetlands and early towns without states

Scott (via Pournelle) argues Ubaid multi-web wetland subsistence (fish, birds, turtles, diverse plants) supported sedentism and proto-urban settlements (~6500 BCE southern alluvium) without plough-grain state modules. Concentration alone does not yield states; the state module needs concentrated *agricultural* fixed-field manpower.

### Golden age of barbarians

Scott posits a multi-millennial period from the first states until perhaps ~1600 CE when nonstate peoples thrived relative to state subjects: better to be barbarian when states exist but are not too strong. States as juicy sites for plunder *and* as trading posts (ores, timber, hides, slaves for textiles, grain, metals). Even at Roman and early Han peaks, effective state control was modest; vast majority of world population remained nonstate (hunters, gatherers, marine collectors, horticulturalists, swiddeners, pastoralists, ungoverned farmers).

**Late-barbarian balance sheet (Scott’s position):** multi-food-web subsistence → less vulnerability to single failure; likely healthier and longer-lived (especially female); more advantageous trade → more leisure; freer than the “celebrated yeoman farmer” under hierarchical agriculture and the state.

**Two deeply melancholy aspects:** (1) trade goods included other nonstate peoples sold into bondage — chain of predation reinforcing the state core; (2) sale of martial skills as mercenaries (catching runaways, repressing revolts) so barbarians “willingly dug their own grave.”

### Raiding, protection rackets, dark twins

Density of grain, population, and livestock is both source of state power and fatal vulnerability to mobile raiders. Pure raiding is self-liquidating; stable extraction becomes a protection racket hard to distinguish from the archaic state itself. Reverse tribute (Han–Xiongnu, etc.) as joint sovereignty. Lattimore: civilization created its own barbarian plague — “dark twins.” Mounted pastoralist confederations as Barfield’s “shadow empire” / “state in waiting.”

### Collapse and emancipation

Abandonment of the state may be experienced as emancipation — greater freedom/mobility, avoided combat — without denying predation outside the state. Apparent “regression” to foraging/pastoralism may be prudent adaptation (Adams’s nomad–sedentary continuum). Well-being of a population must never be confounded with power of a court.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Romanticization risk** — Scott himself marks barbarian slaving and mercenary service; nonstate life is not a utopia. Keep all welfare comparisons as **Scott’s position (2017)**.
- ***Art of Not Being Governed* not yet ingested** — Zomia-depth primary densification still pending; this page’s 2017 material is the archaic hinterland extension.
- **State-source bias:** Knowledge of barbarian threats comes from self-interested court scribes (“Barbarian Histories”); Scott frames this as historiographic problem.
- **Four-way non-identity:** “Barbarian” ethnonyms in state sources are political/administrative labels, often loose confederacies — not coherent cultures, languages, or genetic populations. Amorites, Scythians, Xiongnu, etc., as Scott reads them, were usually military agglomerations of disparate peoples.
- **Golden-age dating:** Scott calls precise dates a “fool’s errand”; ~1600 CE is a generous marker of state hegemony, not a hard archaeological boundary.
