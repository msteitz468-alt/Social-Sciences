# Encyclopedic Museum

An **encyclopedic museum** (also "universal museum") is a museum type and
programmatic ideal: an institution that collects and displays representative
works from **many of the world's cultures under one roof**, aiming (in its
advocates' terms) at cosmopolitan education rather than a single national
narrative. The term is central to
[[cuno-james|James Cuno]]'s *[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns Antiquity?]]*
(2008) and to the cultural-internationalist side of
[[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

Cuno (attributed, 2008) contrasts **encyclopedic museums** with **national
museums**: the former, on his account, present cultural objects as evidence of
a shared human past and of cultural hybridity; the latter serve modern nation-
states' identity projects. Exemplars in his framing include the British Museum,
the Louvre, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, and the Art Institute of Chicago.
Neil MacGregor (as cited by Cuno) is treated as a principal institutional
spokesman for the British Museum as a "world under one roof."

## Semantic History

- **Enlightenment / nineteenth-century institutional type:** national collections
  assembled through exploration, purchase, partage, and colonial power — later
  rebranded as "encyclopedic" or "universal" in museum advocacy.
- **Early twenty-first century program:** Cuno, MacGregor, and allied directors
  articulate the type as a **normative defense** against restitution pressure and
  retentionist acquisition bans.
- **Critics' usage:** opponents often hear "encyclopedic" / "universal" as a
  legitimating label for collections built under empire and sustained by markets
  in [[unprovenanced-antiquities]].

## Operationalizations

| Marker (as used in debate) | Claim |
|---|---|
| Multi-regional permanent collection | Breadth across continents and periods |
| Public access / free or broad entry | Merryman "access" principle |
| Research and publication | Merryman "quest for knowledge" |
| Refusal of pure national-origin lock | Cultural internationalism |

## Applications

- Justifies continued acquisition and retention policies at major Western museums
  (attributed program of Cuno 2008).
- Grounds resistance to total [[restitution]] of pre-modern objects to source
  states when the object is framed as world heritage rather than national
  patrimony (position, not wiki-voice finding).
- Paired with advocacy of restored [[partage]] as a cooperative collecting path.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Power asymmetry:** most encyclopedic museums sit in former imperial or
  wealthy states; critics treat the ideal as post-hoc rationalization of
  historical extraction (counter-position on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]).
- **Acquisition ethics:** the ideal does not, for Renfrew and allies, license
  post-1970 purchase of unprovenanced material.
- **Colonial genealogy:** Edward Said's analysis of empire and culture is both
  cited by Cuno (in defense of hybridity / contrapuntal reading) and by critics
  (as indictment of museum collecting).
