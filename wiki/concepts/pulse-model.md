# Pulse Model (Middle Niger)

## Definition as Coined

R.J. McIntosh’s **Pulse Model** (1993; *[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]* Ch. 3, 2005) is an empirically testable hypothesis about how climate and floodplain resource “pulses” drive successive phases of **specialist emergence and clustered settlement accommodation** on the Middle Niger — producing the complex of specialists recognized as Middle Niger urbanism, without requiring a coercive centralized authority as the organizer.

## Semantic History

Embedded in [[historical-ecology]] and self-organization language (self-organizing landscape phases I–IV with increasing specialist counts). Bridges paleoclimate/geomorphology (Ch. 2) to excavation and survey expectations (Chs. 4–5).

## Operationalizations

Predicted locations of early specialist sites; increasing specialization intensity; reciprocal relations among specialist corporations; archaeological signatures of clustered vs dispersed specialists under wetter/drier or high/low-flood regimes.

## Applications

Middle Niger basins (including Méma landform complexity thought-experiments); ground-truthing against Jenné-jeno and hinterland data.

## Critiques and Limitations

Model status is **hypothesis** (McIntosh’s own framing) — not a demonstrated law. Ethnographic Mande specialist corporations used as deep-time analogy must be flagged (not identity with first-millennium groups). Climate–society coupling risks over-reading environmental pulse as sole driver (McIntosh stresses co-evolution, not simple determinism).


## McIntosh 2005 (full statement)

*(Attributed — *[[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005|Ancient Middle Niger]]* Ch. 3.)*

The Pulse Model is “an empirically testable hypothesis about the conditions of the self-organizing landscape that allowed the horizontally complex cluster of specialists that we recognize as Middle Niger urbanism.” The “pulse” evokes ecological/climatic patterning of millennia before mid-first-millennium BCE cities, but the model is **primarily about mental and symbolic conditions** of occupational specialization and corporate identity — how multiple discrete corporations shared a mosaic landscape without coercive central authority.

### Self-organizing landscape phases (idealized)

| Phase | Specialists (approx.) | Sedentary | Signature transform (attributed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | 3 | 0 | Farmers, fisherfolk, herders add a season of gathering together — co-evolving landscape conception |
| **II** | 7 | 1 | First true sedentism (farmers); first artisan specialists (potters); new interaction code for peaceful access among non-kin |
| **III** | 9 | 6 | Village norm; multiple artisans sedentary; attenuated cattle transhumance; clustered multi-group community |
| **IV** | 11 | 8 | Non-linear shift to incipient urbanism; camel herders, blacksmiths; more-of-same becomes radical difference |

### Corporate clustering without coercion

Physical clustering lets corporate groups remain socially discrete while exchanging goods/services; spiritual/symbolic property of each corporation (ancestral, craft secrets) anchors identity. McIntosh contrasts Tragedy of the Commons with **Mutual Harvest / Augmentation of the Commons** via reputation-driven canons.

### Ground-truthing (qualified)

MacDonald LSA Méma sequences (Kobadi → Bérétouma/Ndondi Tossokel → Faita) and Togola IA clustering (88 of 109 sites in 13 clusters; first-millennium explosion then abort in some basins) treated as partial, non-falsifying support — not proof of all model steps.

### Mande ethnohistory bridge

Three “core rules,” nyama power, nyamakalaw specialists (esp. blacksmiths), onomasticon and *dalimasigi* pilgrimage — **historic/ethnographic analogy** for deep-time reputation and specialist secrecy. Not identity with Phase I/II populations.
