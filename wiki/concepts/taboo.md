# Taboo

## Definition as Coined

**Emic:** Polynesian *tapu* / *tabu* — marked-off sacred or forbidden status. **Etic (Frazer):** in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (ch. 19–22), [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] treated taboo as a system of negative rules protecting sacred or dangerous persons (kings, priests, mourners, women in childbirth, warriors), acts, things (iron, blood, head, hair, names), and words — continuous with the logic of [[sympathetic-magic]] and the vulnerability of the soul. (Attributed: Frazer's comparative systemization, not a universal native category.)

## Semantic History

Migrated from Polynesian ethnography into general European languages and into analytic anthropology. Frazer's four-fold catalogue (acts, persons, things, words) shaped textbook treatment. Durkheimian tradition reconceived related phenomena under [[sacred-and-profane]]; Freud (*Totem and Taboo*) psychologized the term; later anthropology often prefers local categories or "avoidance rules" / "pollution" frameworks ([[douglas-mary|Douglas]]) over pan-human "taboo."

**Douglas 1966 / 2002 (attributed):** in *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*, taboo is a spontaneous coding practice that protects distinctive categories of the universe — local consensus on how the world is organised — and shunts ambiguity into the sacred; dirt bridges modern "dirty and dangerous" with foreign taboo. 2002 restatement: taboo depends on community-wide complicity; physical nature and bodies are imagined to avenge broken taboos; rules will be as repressive as leading members want; when controllers of opinion want a different way of life, taboos lose credibility. See [[purity-pollution-theory]], [[ritual-pollution]], [[matter-out-of-place]], [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]].

## Operationalizations

Frazer: any prohibition explained by contagious danger or sacredness of the marked entity. Competing operationalizations: pollution/purity structures ([[ritual-pollution]]); social boundary maintenance; psychological ambivalence. Douglas: inventory classification scheme + automatic-harm sanctions protecting it; distinguish pollution (structure of ideas; intention irrelevant) from witchcraft/sorcery (psychic powers of persons).

## Applications

Central to Frazer's account of kingship and the burden of royalty; comparative religion; literary and psychoanalytic uptake. Douglas: secular micro-taboos (chipped cups, polite convention), dietary law, sex pollution, risk selection as taboo-like accusation (*Risk and Culture*, 1982).

## Critiques and Limitations

Lifting *tapu* into a worldwide type erases local meanings; circular if every prohibition is labelled taboo; Frazer's catalogues inherit second-hand source bias. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]. Douglas (2002) noted her own examples were mainly conservative and would balance with radical taboos protecting redistributive visions.

**Andaman (Radcliffe-Brown 1922, attributed):** initiation (*aka-op*) sequential food taboos; Biliku offences (beeswax, yams, cicada) analysed as seasonal risk-statements not absolute moral law; mourning and name avoidances. [[andamanese]]; [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].

**Radcliffe-Brown's "Taboo" (Frazer Lecture, 1939, in *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*; attributed):** he judged the cross-cultural use of "taboo" "undesirable and inconvenient" (the Polynesian *tabu* means simply "forbidden") and proposed replacing it with **ritual prohibition** — "a rule of behaviour which is associated with a belief that an infraction will result in an undesirable change in the ritual status of the person who fails to keep to the rule" — defined via the paired concept of **[[ritual-value|ritual value]]**. He surveyed and rejected the standard magic/religion criteria (Durkheim's obligatory/optional; Frazer's; Malinowski's), argued the holy/unclean opposition is not universal (Hawaiian *kapu*), and reversed Frazer: the social function of ritual is "essential," not an accidental by-product of erroneous belief. Douglas (2002) credited him with teaching her generation that taboos have a protective function, then applied the insight more inclusively (not only to "primitives"). See [[ritual-value]], [[structural-functionalism]].
