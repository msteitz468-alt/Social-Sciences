# Taboo

## Definition as Coined

**Emic:** Polynesian *tapu* / *tabu* — marked-off sacred or forbidden status. **Etic (Frazer):** in *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]* (ch. 19–22), [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] treated taboo as a system of negative rules protecting sacred or dangerous persons (kings, priests, mourners, women in childbirth, warriors), acts, things (iron, blood, head, hair, names), and words — continuous with the logic of [[sympathetic-magic]] and the vulnerability of the soul. (Attributed: Frazer's comparative systemization, not a universal native category.)

## Semantic History

Migrated from Polynesian ethnography into general European languages and into analytic anthropology. Frazer's four-fold catalogue (acts, persons, things, words) shaped textbook treatment. Durkheimian tradition reconceived related phenomena under [[sacred-and-profane]]; Freud (*Totem and Taboo*) psychologized the term; later anthropology often prefers local categories or "avoidance rules" / "pollution" frameworks ([[douglas-mary|Douglas]]) over pan-human "taboo."

**Douglas 1966 / 2002 (attributed):** in *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]*, taboo is a spontaneous coding practice that protects distinctive categories of the universe — local consensus on how the world is organised — and shunts ambiguity into the sacred; dirt bridges modern "dirty and dangerous" with foreign taboo. 2002 restatement: taboo depends on community-wide complicity; physical nature and bodies are imagined to avenge broken taboos; rules will be as repressive as leading members want; when controllers of opinion want a different way of life, taboos lose credibility. See [[purity-pollution-theory]], [[ritual-pollution]], [[matter-out-of-place]], [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]].

## Operationalizations

Frazer: any prohibition explained by contagious danger or sacredness of the marked entity. Competing operationalizations: pollution/purity structures ([[ritual-pollution]]); social boundary maintenance; psychological ambivalence. Douglas: inventory classification scheme + automatic-harm sanctions protecting it; distinguish pollution (structure of ideas; intention irrelevant) from witchcraft/sorcery (psychic powers of persons).

## Applications

Central to Frazer's account of kingship and the burden of royalty; comparative religion; literary and psychoanalytic uptake. Douglas: secular micro-taboos (chipped cups, polite convention), dietary law, sex pollution, risk selection as taboo-like accusation (*Risk and Culture*, 1982).

## Critiques and Limitations

Lifting *tapu* into a worldwide type erases local meanings; circular if every prohibition is labelled taboo; Frazer's catalogues inherit second-hand source bias. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]. Douglas (2002) noted her own examples were mainly conservative and would balance with radical taboos protecting redistributive visions.

**Andaman (Radcliffe-Brown 1922, attributed):** initiation (*aka-op*) sequential food taboos; Biliku offences (beeswax, yams, cicada) analysed as seasonal risk-statements not absolute moral law; mourning and name avoidances. [[andamanese]]; [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]].

**Hawaiian *kapu* and the archaic state (Kirch 2017, attributed):** in *[[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|On the Road of the Winds]]* (Ch. 8, citing Valeri 1985; Ladefoged 1998) [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] treats the late-prehistoric Hawaiian **kapu system** as an unusually intense elaboration of the pan-Polynesian *tapu* — a machinery of gender- and rank-differentiating prohibitions that helped constitute the Hawaiian **[[archaic-state|archaic state]]**. The *kapu moe* ("prostrating taboo") obliged commoners to lie face-down before the highest chiefs; women were forbidden certain foods (pork, bananas, some fish) and cooked in separate earth ovens — a rule archaeologically visible as separate cooking facilities at Kahikinui, Maui (Kirch 2014). Kapu is the Hawaiian cognate of *tapu*, entangled with [[mana]] as the enforcement ideology of chiefly sanctity.

**Tikopia *tapu* as economic regulation (Firth, *Primitive Polynesian Economy*, 1939; attributed, fieldwork 1928–29):** the fullest emic documentation of working *tapu* in the wiki. [[firth-raymond|Firth]] recorded a dual semantic range ("sacred" and "reserved/prohibited") and two structural kinds: constant *tapu* (temples, the chief's person) versus individually imposed, removable *tapu* with concentrated economic effects — chiefs and their eldest sons reserving coconuts or closing reefs ahead of feasts or against scarcity. Sanction was graded by sign and rank: plain *sakilo* frond (commoners' orchards), knotted *pini* (a spirit "contained within"), *famatua* mature frond (chiefs only; breach believed to sicken or kill) — and calibrated by design: Pa Fenuatara set a conservation *tapu* deliberately "without any formula for making a man ill," resting on prestige alone, while a young bachelor's *tapu* simply failed for want of authority. Institutional machinery included *asi/asiasi* (formal single-occasion suspension, distinct from full "untying," *vete*) and the *pipi* frond-sign, which Firth explicitly classed as etiquette, **not** *tapu* proper. Economic verdict *(Firth's)*: *tapu* functioned as resource conservation and production direction — one imposed coconut *tapu* yielded "a considerable increase of the wealth of the community." See [[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study]], [[tikopia]].

**Hawaiian *tabu* in transformation (Sahlins 1981, attributed):** *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities]]* makes the Hawaiian tabu system the wiki's fullest case of a taboo concept's **semantic drift under practice**. [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] argues tabu was not passively "reflected" social structure but constitutive of it — "in the order of practice, as the organization of it" — and that its own logic became "the mechanism of revaluation of persons and objects it had otherwise originally defined." Chiefs' ad hoc use of tabu to engross European trade (tabus on commoners' pig trade, tabus timed to chiefly arrival) "reordered the semantic field of 'tabu'": lacking ritual finality but serving determinate material interest, it "shifted the meaning of tabu toward a dominant sense of legal interdiction, as in a proprietary right" — "In Hawaii today, if one sees a sign reading KAPU on a fence, it means 'no trespassing,' and the sanction behind it is somewhat different than that of the gods." "As in many revolutions, the decisive subversion of the system was a work of the people in power: an abuse of power." Sahlins quotes A. M. Hocart (1933) against reducing tabu to economic close-seasons, while noting ritual becomes property-protection where no detection apparatus exists. He documents decades of gendered transgression (women eating forbidden foods with Europeans aboard ship from 1779; commoner men defying trade tabus; even Kaʻahumanu secretly eating forbidden food, "as much as her life was worth"), arguing the formal **1819 abolition** (*ʻai noa*, "free eating": Liholiho publicly eating with chiefly women, 19 Nov 1819; the pro-tabu rebel Kekuaokalani killed at Kuamoʻo that December) was no sudden rupture — against Kroeber's "cultural fatigue" — since "the tabus began to disintegrate in Cook's time." The abrogation was itself a ritual act (tabu-removal always is in Polynesia), read by Sahlins as a prolongation of Kamehameha's death ceremonies; within a few years the Kaʻahumanu group imposed Calvinist Christian tabus under Bingham as their *kahuna nui*. See [[hawaiians]], [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]].

**Hubert & Mauss, "negative magic" (1902–03, attributed):** in *[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|A General Theory of Magic]]* they class "sympathetic taboos — negative magic as we prefer to call it" as an important but understudied field ("we are hardly in a position to do more than call attention to the subject"), and argue the automatic sanctions of such prohibitions are socially underwritten: "it is, of course, society which really imposes a belief in these automatic sanctions and which supports them." See [[mauss-hubert-general-theory-magic-study]], [[magic]].

**Radcliffe-Brown's "Taboo" (Frazer Lecture, 1939, in *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*; attributed):** he judged the cross-cultural use of "taboo" "undesirable and inconvenient" (the Polynesian *tabu* means simply "forbidden") and proposed replacing it with **ritual prohibition** — "a rule of behaviour which is associated with a belief that an infraction will result in an undesirable change in the ritual status of the person who fails to keep to the rule" — defined via the paired concept of **[[ritual-value|ritual value]]**. He surveyed and rejected the standard magic/religion criteria (Durkheim's obligatory/optional; Frazer's; Malinowski's), argued the holy/unclean opposition is not universal (Hawaiian *kapu*), and reversed Frazer: the social function of ritual is "essential," not an accidental by-product of erroneous belief. Douglas (2002) credited him with teaching her generation that taboos have a protective function, then applied the insight more inclusively (not only to "primitives"). See [[ritual-value]], [[structural-functionalism]].
