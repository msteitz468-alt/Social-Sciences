# Wild Man

## Definition as Coined

In [[taussig-michael|Taussig]]’s 1987 analysis, the **wild man** is less a people than a **colonial and curative image**: European wild-man traditions, Amazonian *auca* mythology, highland figurations of lowland savagery, and the montaged shaman-as-devil that rubber terror and later yagé healing both put to work. Closing thesis: colonizers provided the colonized with a “left-handed gift of the image of the wild man — a gift whose powers the colonizers would be blind to, were it not for the fact that it was given in the first place so as to facilitate the creation of the colonial reality” (paraphrased attributed; see study page for full chain). Only colonized reciprocation, he claimed, makes colonizers able to see and use those powers — a dialogical colonial imagination (attributed).

## Semantic History

- **European medieval/early modern wild man.** Half-human/half-animal, forest master of animals and plants (Bernheimer and art-historical sources as Taussig cites) — already available for New World projection via “marvels of the East.”
- **Colonial American uses.** *Auca* as ur-mythology of unconquered savagery; Huitoto/Witoto as **image** in highland curing songs and rubber-era ethnology — **not** identity with a self-conscious people (four-way non-identity).
- **Wild woman.** “Wild Woman of the Forest” (*La Montañerita*) transformed into Our Lady of Remedies in local Catholic–indigenous history (Taussig ch. 10) — gendered wildness routed into Marian devotion.
- **Healing circuit.** Colonists take yagé and see the shaman as devil; highland healers draw lowland “wild” power; magic of the Indian as “unconscious colonial creation” weaponized against envy and fate (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Image/construct analysis. **Identity cautions (mandatory):**

- Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people.
- Huitoto/Witoto ethnonyms in rubber literature and curing songs are **associated_with** colonial wild-man imagery; do not equate pots, genes, or police *muchachos* with “the wild man.”
- *Auca* is a relational label of savagery/rebellion, not a stable ethnie.

## Applications

- [[putumayo-rubber-terror]] colonial mirror of production ([[mimesis]] of attributed savagery)
- [[yage]] curing images; Santiago Mutumbajoy and other healers as documented
- European wild-man historiography (as secondary apparatus in Taussig)
- Race magic / *las tres potencias* (as Taussig frames racialized spiritual power)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Romanticization risk.** Reading wildness as redemptive healing power can re-enchant the colonial stereotype; Taussig’s own point is that the image is colonial all the way down (attributed).
- **Gender.** Wild woman / witch / montañerita strands need separate tracking from male wild-man/shaman composite.
- **Reception.** Later Amazonian ethnography may contest Taussig’s highland–lowland composite; keep claims date-stamped to his 1969–85 fieldwork circuit.
