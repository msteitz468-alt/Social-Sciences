# Distinction (Bourdieu)

Bourdieu's concept of **social distinction through taste and lifestyle** — the process by which agents mark hierarchical difference via cultural practices, and misrecognize that marking as natural taste. Central object of *[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]* (1979).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, Distinction.)*

> "Taste classifies, and it classifies the classifier. Social subjects, classified by their classifications, distinguish themselves by the distinctions they make, between the beautiful and the ugly, the distinguished and the vulgar, in which their position in the objective classifications is expressed or betrayed."

Distinction is thus both a **strategy** (marking distance, claiming rarity) and a **structural effect** of relational positions in [[social-space]]. Profits of distinction accrue to rare cultural competence; the ideology of natural taste naturalizes the mode of acquisition (early domestic vs scholastic). Aesthetic sense is itself a sense of distinction.

## Semantic History

**Not** identical to Veblen’s [[invidious-distinction]] (pecuniary emulation, conspicuous waste/leisure). Both treat consumption as status-marking; Bourdieu’s version is built on capital forms, [[habitus]], misrecognition, and multi-domain lifestyle homology rather than on pecuniary canons of waste. Everyday English “distinction” (elegance) is the ideology the concept sociologizes.

## Operationalizations

Oppositional taste patterns across music, food, décor, body, sport; correspondence maps of lifestyles; rarity and legitimacy of practices; manner of appropriation (ease vs pretension vs necessity).

## Applications

Organizes the entire *Distinction* research program; portable slogan for cultural sociology; links to symbolic struggles and classification struggles in the Conclusion.

## Critiques and Limitations

Later omnivore research revises exclusive highbrow distinction; risk of reading all culture as zero-sum distinction; national forms of distinction vary. Keep separate from Veblen on the concept page and in links.

## Lamont’s boundary approach (1992)

[[lamont-michele|Lamont]] (*[[lamont-money-morals-manners-1992|Money, Morals, and Manners]]*) accepts that people compete to legitimize definitions of high status but rejects treating all cultural practices as automatically classifying in a closed semiotic system. She argues conditions under which difference creates inequality must be specified (boundary strength and consensus), and that moral and socioeconomic standards compete with taste-based distinction — especially outside Parisian intellectual milieus (attributed; cf. [[bourdieu-distinction-study]]).
