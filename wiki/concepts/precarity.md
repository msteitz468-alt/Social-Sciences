# Precarity

## Definition as Coined

Broader critical vocabulary for insecure livelihood. In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]*, [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Tsing]] defines **precarity** as *life without the promise of stability* — once imagined as the fate of the less fortunate, now (she argues) the condition of all lives. She further defines it as *the condition of being vulnerable to others*: unpredictable encounters transform us; shifting assemblages remake us; a precarious world is without teleology; indeterminacy is frightening yet also what makes life possible *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Semantic History

Migrates from European labor-movement and sociological uses (nonstandard employment, flexible work) into anthropology and environmental humanities. Tsing's 2015 formulation expands beyond the job market to dual precarity: planetary livability under human disturbance, and the collapse of postwar development's promise of "standard employment" even as everyone still depends on capitalism *(Tsing 2015)*. Related wiki employment concept: [[flexible-employment]].

## Operationalizations

- **Labor sociology:** nonstandard jobs, benefits erosion, flexible contracts.
- **Tsing (matsutake):** commercial wild-mushroom picking without wages or security; Open Ticket foragers' haunted freedom; ruined industrial forests that still host multispecies life; global supply chains that end expectations of progress by letting lead firms abandon control of labor *(Tsing 2015)*.

Tsing argues precarity is not an exception that "drops out" of the system but may be the condition of our time — or that our time is ripe for sensing it *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Can become a catch-all that flattens class and regional difference. Tsing's expansive ecological-economic use differs from narrower employment-only definitions; readers who want hard labor-market metrics may find the earthwide sense under-specified. She pairs precarity with structural analysis of salvage accumulation rather than treating noticing as sufficient politics *(Tsing 2015)*.
