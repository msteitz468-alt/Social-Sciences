# Cultural Determinism

## Definition as Coined

**Cultural determinism** is the doctrine that human behavior, character, and difference are
explained by culture rather than biology — that, in the strong form [[freeman-derek|Freeman]]
attacks, culture is "omnipotent" and human nature an "undifferentiated raw material... moulded
into shape by its society." The label is largely a critic's term; the position it names was, on
Freeman's history, crystallized by Alfred Kroeber and Robert Lowie in 1915–1917 (Kroeber's
"superorganic"; the tag *omnis cultura ex cultura*) as cultural anthropology dissociated itself
from the biology of the eugenics era. This page records the concept **as Freeman narrates and
attacks it in *[[freeman-margaret-mead-and-samoa-1983|Margaret Mead and Samoa]]* (1983)** —
attributed throughout; his history of ideas is itself a partisan reconstruction.

## Semantic History

*(Freeman's framing, attributed.)* Freeman traces a line from Darwin (1859) and Galton (coiner
of "nature and nurture," 1874, and "eugenics," 1883) through the hereditarian high tide of the
1900s–1910s (Pearson, Davenport, Grant), against which [[boas-franz|Boas]] declared a "parting
of the ways" in 1916. Within months Kroeber and Lowie issued "intellectual manifestos"
excluding biological variables from cultural explanation. Freeman argues Boas then posed the
"intractable problem" — differentiating the inherited from the culturally acquired — and
designed the Samoa study (with the young [[mead-margaret|Mead]] as, in Freeman's charge, an
investigator "ideally suited" to the desired conclusion) to *confirm*, not test, cultural
determinism, in a pre-Popperian era when "one sought to prove [theories] to the very hilt."
*[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* thereby became the doctrine's
celebrated empirical "negative instance." Later 20th-century usage treats "cultural
determinism" mostly as a foil — few self-identify with the strong form — and the term now
mainly marks one pole of the nature–nurture axis.

## Operationalizations

Not measured; a doctrinal commitment. Freeman locates its methodological expression in Ogburn's
rule (exhaust cultural explanations before invoking biological ones), which he says Mead carried
into the field "already set."

## Applications

- The theoretical stake of the [[mead-freeman-debate]]: whether Samoa was a valid "negative
  instance" refuting biological determinism about [[adolescence]].
- The organizing commitment (per Freeman) of Boasian [[culture-and-personality]] and, more
  broadly, of American cultural anthropology from the 1920s.
- Freeman's proposed replacement: an **interactionist paradigm** giving "full cognizance to
  biology, as well as to culture" (Mayr's "open programs of behavior").

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether "cultural determinism" is a fair description of Boas, Kroeber, and Mead — or a straw
target Freeman built to stage a nature-culture synthesis — is itself contested (see
[[mead-freeman-debate]], Position C). Boas's actual position included biological anthropology
(anthropometry, the immigrant head-form study); the "omnipotence of culture" reading is
strongest in Freeman's telling. Record the doctrine and its critique both in attributed voice;
adjudicate neither.
