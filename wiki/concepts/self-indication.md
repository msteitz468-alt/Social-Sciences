# Self-Indication

## Definition as Coined

**Self-indication**, in [[blumer-herbert|Blumer]]'s vocabulary (*[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]*, 1969), is the moving communicative process in which the human being makes indications to himself — notes things, designates them, judges and organizes responses — and thereby constructs action. It is how the third SI premise operates: meanings are handled and modified through an interpretive process.

Blumer ties this tightly to [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]]'s view of the self as process (the human being as object to himself; interaction with oneself) rather than as a fixed structure of attitudes or internalized norms.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Mead (reconstructed) | Self-interaction; conversation of I and me; making indications to oneself |
| Blumer 1969 | Preferred SI term for the interpretive formation of the act |
| Later SI | Micro-mechanism of meaning-in-use; bridge to ethnomethodology/conversation analysis only by loose analogy — do not equate |

## Operationalizations

Studied by recovering how actors note, define, and re-define objects and situations in the course of action — not by treating "interpretation" as a residual after structural variables are entered. Method partner: [[exploration-and-inspection]].

## Applications

- Distinguishes SI from stimulus–response and from pure structural determination: the organism does not merely release a response; it constructs action through self-indication.
- Links individual acts to [[joint-action|joint action]]: each participant's self-indication is part of fitting lines of action together.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Hard to observe "from outside"; relies on close acquaintance and actors' accounts.
- Critics claim it over-intellectualizes routine or coerced behavior.
- Must not be read as pure individualism: Blumer insists meanings arise in social interaction (premise 2).
