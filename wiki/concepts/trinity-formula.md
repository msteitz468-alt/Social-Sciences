## Definition as Coined

Marx opens Chapter 48 (assembled by Engels from several manuscript fragments found at different points in Part Six — an editorial fact noted explicitly in the source) with the formula itself: "Capital-profit (profit of enterprise plus interest), land-ground-rent, labour-wages, this trinity form holds in itself all the mysteries of the social production process." He immediately reduces it to a "second," more compressed trinity: "capital-interest, land-ground-rent, labour-wages." The formula names the way capitalist society's surface economic life presents value as flowing spontaneously from three independent "factors" — capital, land, and labour — treated as parallel and equally natural sources of income, when in Marx's analysis all revenue is in fact objectified social labour, divided after the fact among capitalist, landowner, and worker.

Marx calls the three terms radically heterogeneous — "like that of lawyer's fees, beetroot and music" — because capital "is not a thing, but rather a definite social relation of production pertaining to a particular historical social formation, which is manifested in a thing and lends this thing a specific social character." The formula's individual pairings are each, in Marx's analysis, a category error: land (a use-value, having no value in Marx's technical sense) is paired with rent (an exchange-value); capital (a value) is paired with interest (more value than the original sum — "4 is not 5"); and labour is paired with "price of labour," which Marx calls "just as irrational as a yellow logarithm," since labour itself, not its exercise (labour-power), cannot coherently have a price. He states the deeper reason vulgar economics is comfortable with these "prima facie absurd and complete contradictions": it "does nothing more than interpret, systematize and turn into apologetics the notions of agents trapped within bourgeois relations of production."

## Semantic History

The trinity formula's root is the "capital fetish" analyzed earlier in the volume, in Chapter 24 ("Interest-Bearing Capital as the Superficial Form of the Capital Relation"). There Marx argues that in interest-bearing capital, "the capital relationship reaches its most superficial and fetishized form": the circuit M–M′ (money directly yielding more money, with no visible mediating process) obscures capital's origin in production even more thoroughly than commercial capital's M–C–M′. "Capital appears as a mysterious and self-creating source of interest, of its own increase... It becomes as completely the property of money to create value, to yield interest, as it is the property of a pear tree to bear pears." This passage — extending the logic of [[commodity-fetishism]] from commodities to capital itself — is the direct conceptual ancestor of the trinity formula's capital-profit leg; Chapter 48 explicitly formalizes what Chapter 24 first names "the capital mystification in the most flagrant form."

Marx traces the mystification's genesis through five cumulative stages across the volume: (1) the immediate production process, where the struggle over the working day still keeps the relation "transparent... in [the workers'] consciousness"; (2) the development of relative surplus-value, where productive forces "appear... as shifted from labour to capital"; (3) the circulation process, where value realization "seem[s] not just to be realized only in circulation but actually to arise from it"; (4) the transformation of surplus-value into profit and of values into prices of production through capital's equalization across spheres, which "conceals the true nature of surplus-value"; and (5) the division of profit into profit of enterprise and interest, which completes "the autonomization of the form of surplus-value" (Ch. 48). Marx credits classical political economy (Smith, Ricardo) with dissolving much of this "false appearance and deception... this religion of everyday life" by reducing interest to a part of profit and rent to the surplus above average profit, and ultimately reducing value and surplus-value to labour — but notes that even its best representatives "remained more or less trapped in the world of illusion their criticism had dissolved."

## Operationalizations

- **Capital-fetish germ (Ch. 21–23).** Before the full fetish statement of Ch. 24, Marx builds the groundwork: interest is defined as "nothing but a particular name, a special title, for a part of the profit which the actually functioning capitalist has to pay to the capital's proprietor" — a purely distributive category, not a separate source of value. He argues there is **no "natural" rate of interest** — unlike commodity prices or wages, which converge on an underlying law (price of production, value of labour-power) under free competition, the split between interest and profit-of-enterprise is "dictated by competition" alone, with "no 'natural' limits." Chapter 23 traces how even an owner-operator using no borrowed capital comes to mentally split his own profit into "interest" (attributed to mere ownership) and "profit of enterprise" (misrecognized as a wage for his own supervisory activity, even "wages of superintendence") — an ideological effect Marx argues conceals capital's antithesis to labour, since interest-bearing capital as such "does not have wage-labour as its opposite but rather functioning capital."
- **Vulgar economics' own testimony.** Marx quotes Adam Smith ("Wages, profit and rent are the three original sources of all revenue, as well as of all exchangeable value") and Storch ("the causes of material production are at the same time the sources of the original revenues that it yields") as instances of the very dogma his critique targets (Ch. 48, footnote 50).
- **The "religion of everyday life."** Marx's summary formulation: capital-profit/land-rent/labour-wages "completes the mystification of the capitalist mode of production, the reification of social relations, and the immediate coalescence of the material relations of production with their historical and social specificity: the bewitched, distorted and upside-down world haunted by Monsieur le Capital and Madame la Terre, who are at the same time social characters and mere things."
- **Historical delimitation.** Marx states the mystification is not a feature of production as such but is historically specific to generalized commodity production: it is absent where production is for direct use, where "slavery or serfdom forms the broad basis" (domination is then visible and direct rather than mediated through impersonal "factors"), and largely absent in "primitive communities where an indigenous communism prevails" or under the corporate/professional norms of "the guild system of the Middle Ages." The manuscript breaks off mid-sentence immediately after this point (Engels's editorial note).

## Applications

**"Smith's dogma" and the reproduction schema (Ch. 49).** Marx uses the reproduction schemas developed in *Capital* vol. 2 (Department I: means of production; Department II: means of consumption) to refute what he calls "the incredible blunder" running through political economy since Adam Smith — the belief that the value of the total annual social product resolves entirely into wages + profit + rent with no remainder to replace constant capital. He restates the identity "Average profit plus rent is... equal to surplus-value," and distinguishes gross income (wages + profit + rent) from net income (surplus-value alone).

**The illusion of competition (Ch. 50).** Marx demonstrates through a worked numerical example (a capital of 500 = 400c + 100v + 150s, with the 150 of surplus-value split variously between profit and rent) that shifting the wage/profit/rent division changes only distribution, never the underlying value magnitude — refuting the vulgar-economic procedure of treating wages, profit, and rent as independently pre-given "elements" that supposedly determine price, rather than as post-hoc divisions of value already produced. He identifies the circularity this produces (attempting to derive wages, then profit, then rent as separately price-determining collapses into "the price of labour is thus determined by itself... we do not know how the price of labour is determined") and offers a structural explanation for why the illusion is persistent rather than merely a mistake: "the capitalist mode of production... constantly reproduces not only the material product but also the socio-economic relations... Its result thus constantly appears as its premise, and its premises as its results." Marx notes the trinity-form analogy is extended even to non-capitalist producers by analogy — a small peasant who "pays himself wages as a worker, lays claim to profit as a capitalist and pays himself rent as a landowner" — reinforcing "the illusion that capitalist relationships are the natural condition of any mode of production."

**Relations of distribution and relations of production (Ch. 51).** Marx states the chapter's central thesis: "the relations of distribution are essentially identical with these relations of production, the reverse side of the same coin, so that the two things share the same historically transitory character." He criticizes John Stuart Mill (*Some Unsettled Questions of Political Economy*, 1844) for conceding the historicity of distribution relations while treating production relations as natural and constant, calling this only a partial, "still timid and restrained" advance. He restates the volume's closing historical-materialist formula: "each particular historical form of this process further develops the material foundations and social forms. Once a certain level of maturity is attained, the particular historical form is shed and makes way for a higher form. The sign that the moment of such a crisis has arrived is that the contradiction and antithesis between... the relations of distribution... and... the productive forces... gains in breadth and depth."

**The "realm of freedom" passage.** Immediately preceding the trinity-formula fragments (per Engels's editorial note, "This is where Chapter 48 begins in the manuscript"), Marx sets out one of the volume's most frequently quoted passages, given here in full for its centrality to any treatment of Marxian social theory: "The real wealth of society and the possibility of a constant expansion of its reproduction process does not depend on the length of surplus labour but rather on its productivity and on the more or less plentiful conditions of production in which it is performed. The realm of freedom really begins only where labour determined by necessity and external expediency ends; it lies by its very nature beyond the sphere of material production proper. Just as the savage must wrestle with nature to satisfy his needs, to maintain and reproduce his life, so must civilized man, and he must do so in all forms of society and under all possible modes of production. This realm of natural necessity expands with his development, because his needs do too; but the productive forces to satisfy these expand at the same time. Freedom, in this sphere, can consist only in this, that socialized man, the associated producers, govern the human metabolism with nature in a rational way, bringing it under their collective control instead of being dominated by it as a blind power; accomplishing it with the least expenditure of energy and in conditions most worthy and appropriate for their human nature. But this always remains a realm of necessity. The true realm of freedom, the development of human powers as an end in itself, begins beyond it, though it can only flourish with this realm of necessity as its basis. The reduction of the working day is the basic prerequisite."

**The Classes fragment (Ch. 52).** *Capital*'s final chapter, and the manuscript's famous unfinished ending: "The owners of mere labour-power, the owners of capital and the landowners, whose respective sources of income are wages, profit and ground-rent — in other words wage-labourers, capitalists and landowners — form the three great classes of modern society based on the capitalist mode of production." Marx immediately complicates this: even in England, "the most widely and most classically developed" case, "this class articulation does not emerge in pure form," since "middle and transitional levels always conceal the boundaries." He poses the chapter's organizing question — "What makes a class?" — and rejects the naive answer (identity of revenue source) by reductio: if shared revenue-type sufficed, "doctors and government officials would also form two classes," and the internal fragmentation of interest among capitalists and landowners (vineyard-owners, field-owners, forest-owners, mine-owners, fishery-owners) would multiply classes indefinitely. Engels's editorial note records exactly where the text ends: "(At this point the manuscript breaks off. — F. E.)" Marx died in 1883 without resolving the question; it remains formally open in the text.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Engels's 1895 defense against Loria, and the "historical transformation problem."** Engels's Supplement and Addendum (his last piece of writing, dated May 1895, two months before his death) directly defends the trinity formula's underlying value theory against contemporary critics. He rebuts the Italian economist Achille Loria's charge that Marx's law of value is meaningless because commodities are never actually sold at their labour-determined values. Engels's counter-argument — flagged in the extraction as the historically load-bearing thesis of the essay — holds that the law of value governed real, direct exchange ratios during the long historical period of **simple commodity production** (when producers owned their own means of production and exchanged products directly: peasants, handicraftsmen, inter-community exchange), and "undergoes a modification" only with the onset of capitalist production, at which point prices of production (not direct values) become the governing average. Engels dates this operative span explicitly: "for the entire period of simple commodity production... from the beginning of the exchange that transforms products into commodities down to the fifteenth century of our epoch... commodity exchange dates from a time before any written history, going back to at least 3500 B.C. in Egypt, and 4000 B.C. or maybe even 6000 B.C. in Babylon; thus the law of value prevailed for a period of some five to seven millennia." This is Engels's own historical-economic argument, attributed to him and dated 1895 — it is not an archaeologically verified chronology, and should not be promoted to wiki voice on any culture or period page. Engels also engages more favorably with Werner Sombart and Conrad Schmidt's contemporary readings of Marx's value theory, while rejecting both as incomplete — Sombart's characterization of value as "not an empirical fact but an ideal or logical one" and Schmidt's characterization of it as "a fiction, though a theoretically necessary one." This entire cluster of claims belongs to the [[transformation-problem-debate]].

**Engels on the stock exchange (1895, unfinished).** Engels's companion fragment, explicitly labeled incomplete in the source, argues that between 1865 (when Marx wrote the bulk of the volume) and 1895 the stock exchange's role expanded dramatically, tending "to concentrate the whole of production... in the hands of stock-exchange speculators," and explicitly links this financialization to contemporary colonization: "in whose interest the European powers divided up Africa a few years ago, and the French conquered Tunis and Tonkin... Mashonaland and Natal taken possession of for the stock exchange by Rhodes." This is Engels's own analytical claim, attributed and dated 1895; it should not be promoted to wiki voice on any phenomena/imperialism page without independent corroboration.

**Editorial-construction caveat.** As with the fictitious-capital material in Part Five, Chapter 48 itself is explicitly noted by Engels to have been assembled from manuscript fragments "found at various points" rather than written as a continuous chapter — a source-reliability fact relevant to any close reading of the formula's presentation order.
