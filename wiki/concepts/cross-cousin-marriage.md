# Cross-Cousin Marriage

## Definition as Coined

**Cross-cousin marriage** prefers or prescribes marriage with the children of opposite-sex siblings of one's parents (MBS/MBD; FZS/FZD), as opposed to parallel cousins. Major topic in [[lowie-robert-h|Lowie]]'s preferential-mating discussion (*[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]*). 1947 Corrections note Radcliffe-Brown's **matrilateral** / **patrilateral** and Warner's **symmetrical** / **asymmetrical** terminology.

## Semantic History

Victorian catalogues → Lowie distributional treatment → structural anthropology (Lévi-Strauss alliance theory) later retheorizes. Keep Lowie's comparative-distribution claims distinct from later alliance theory.

## Operationalizations

Matrilateral vs patrilateral; symmetrical vs asymmetrical; preferential vs prescribed; relation to [[exogamy]] of [[sib]]s.

## Applications

Marriage chapter of *[[lowie-primitive-society-study|Primitive Society]]*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Later theory is not Lowie's; do not back-project alliance models into 1920 text.

## Rivers' Oceanic treatment

[[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]], in *[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of
Melanesian Society]]* (1914), gave the concept its most systematic early Oceanic
development (attributed). He coined "cross-cousins" as the correlative of Tylor's
"cross-cousin marriage," and argued that a system's terminology can "at once" diagnose the
rule's present or past existence — in coastal Fiji, mother's brother is classed with
father-in-law and cross-cousins with spouse's siblings (the reciprocal *veivungoni* class),
and at Nausori the cross-cousin is given the very term for husband/wife. Rivers read such
mergers as [[survivals|fossils]] licensing inference of a lapsed rule, and held (contra a
direct derivation from [[dual-organisation|dual organisation]]) that Oceanic cross-cousin
marriage was a *late* development out of [[gerontocracy|gerontocratic]] control of marriage.
See Oceanic relationship-system discussion on [[rivers-melanesian-society-1914]] and the [[genealogical-method]].

## Lévi-Strauss's alliance-theory treatment

[[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] *[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]* (1949) made cross-cousin marriage the central test case of [[alliance-theory]] (attributed). His argument: cross- and parallel cousins are biologically equidistant yet treated as socially opposite, so the rule cannot be biological — it must be a rule of **exchange**. Using a "sign" (+/−) diagram, he showed that two cousins whose fathers are same-signed (both wife-receivers, i.e. parallel cousins) cannot exchange sisters, while cross-signed cousins (cross-cousins) can: "cross-cousins are recommended for the same reason that parallel cousins are excluded."

He further split the concept by direction, mapping each onto one of his two exchange forms:
- **Bilateral / symmetrical** cross-cousin marriage (both MBD and FZD) realizes **[[restricted-exchange]]** — direct, reciprocal exchange between paired groups.
- **Matrilateral** (MBD only, prohibiting FZD) realizes **[[generalized-exchange]]** — women circulating one way around a cycle of groups; Lévi-Strauss privileged it as the "gamble on the future" of indirect exchange.

He distinguished true **preferential** unions (cross-cousin marriage, able to be an exclusive rule) from merely **privileged** ones (levirate, sororate, avuncular marriage) grafted onto another system, and — against Tylor, Rivers, and Morgan — denied that cross-cousin marriage *derives from* [[dual-organisation]], treating both as expressions of one underlying reciprocity. On whether such rules are "prescriptive" or "preferential," see the [[prescriptive-preferential-debate]].
