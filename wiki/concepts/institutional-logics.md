# Institutional Logics

## Definition as Coined

[[friedland-roger]] and [[alford-robert-r]] introduced institutional logics in "Bringing Society Back In: Symbols, Practices, and Institutional Contradictions" (ch. 10 of [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]]), which the extraction record identifies as the concept's canonical source, building on their earlier joint paper "Powers of Theory" (Alford and Friedland 1985). They defined institutions generally as "both supraorganizational patterns of activity through which humans conduct their material life in time and space, and symbolic systems through which they categorize that activity and infuse it with meaning" — institutions are "simultaneously material and ideal," neither purely structural nor purely cultural.

Friedland and Alford argued that "the central institutions of the contemporary capitalist West—capitalist market, bureaucratic state, democracy, nuclear family, and Christian religion—shape individual preferences and organizational interests as well as the repertoire of behaviors by which they may attain them," and that because these institutions are "potentially contradictory," they make "multiple logics available to individuals and organizations." Their core definitional sentence: "Each of the most important institutional orders of contemporary Western societies has a central logic—a set of material practices and symbolic constructions—which constitutes its organizing principles and which is available to organizations and individuals to elaborate."

The five central institutional logics they specify, verbatim:

- **Capitalism**: "accumulation and the commodification of human activity"
- **State**: "rationalization and the regulation of human activity by legal and bureaucratic hierarchies"
- **Democracy**: "participation and the extension of popular control over human activity"
- **Family**: "community and the motivation of human activity by unconditional loyalty to its members and their reproductive needs"
- **Religion (or science)**: "truth, whether mundane or transcendental, and the symbolic construction of reality within which all human activity takes place"

## Semantic History

Friedland and Alford framed institutional logics as a corrective to what they called a contemporary "theoretical retreat from society," taken along "two paths, one toward the utilitarian individual and the other toward the power-oriented organization." They targeted rational-choice economics directly — "without priced commodities, the postulate of rational utility maximization quickly becomes tautological, a liberal trope, not subject to falsification" — naming public-choice theory, agency theory, and new institutional economics (Williamson 1975) among the reduced individual-level accounts they reacted against.

They also mounted an extended critique of Theda Skocpol's state-autonomy theory, arguing it is circular: factors Skocpol treats as explaining "autonomous state action" are, in Friedland and Alford's reading, "aspects of autonomous state action" rather than independent causes — "a set of assumptions is transformed into a theory." A parallel critique targeted Orloff and Skocpol's (1984) account of U.S./U.K. welfare-state divergence for smuggling in unacknowledged ideological factors while denying them theoretical status.

Friedland and Alford engaged Michel Foucault at length: they credited him with "shatter[ing] the subject-object dualism of the human sciences" by bridging power and discourse through the concept of discipline, but criticized him for analyzing "power, but without agency, without conflict" — "the inhabitants of the asylum are silent; we only hear the voices of their most sophisticated keepers." Their conclusion: "we should not be forced to choose between an acultural analysis of power and an apolitical analysis of culture." They likewise engaged Mark Granovetter's [[embeddedness]] concept critically, arguing that "social networks per se do not have any content and as such do not entail interests, values, motives, beliefs... without content... it will be impossible to explain what kinds of social relations have what kind of effect" — embeddedness, on their reading, is necessary but insufficient without specifying which institutional logic gives a network its content. They further criticized Habermas and Offe for depoliticizing production by treating state rationality as subordinate to capitalist requirements, in a move they compare to [[parsons-talcott]]'s.

Friedland and Alford's earlier "Powers of Theory" work supplies a companion three-level schema: "an adequate social theory must work at three levels of analysis—individuals competing and negotiating, organizations in conflict and coordination, and institutions in contradiction and interdependency," mapped onto pluralist, managerial, and class traditions in political sociology, each with a "home domain" and blind spots elsewhere.

**Note on the wiki's scale framework:** this three-level schema (individual/organization/institution) is theory-specific to Friedland and Alford's argument and should not be silently equated with the wiki's general micro/meso/macro/global scale framework — the two partially overlap but are not identical.

## Operationalizations

Friedland and Alford treat institutional contradictions as observable through politicized conflict: "institutional contradictions are the bases of the most important political conflicts in our society; it is through these politics that the institutional structure of society is transformed." Their examples: U.S. feminists extending the capitalist-market logic into the family by pressing for contractual valuation of household labor; capitalists securing corporate juridical personhood in the early twentieth century to extend liberty/capitalism logic to the firm; and workers pushing for workplace democratization and due-process rights, extending democracy's logic into the capitalist firm. Edelman's (1985) study of grievance-procedure diffusion in unionized versus nonunionized firms found "unionized organizations were no more likely than nonunion organizations to adopt grievance procedures," with both sectors following "roughly an exponential growth curve" — interpreted as evidence that class conflict operates institutionally, not only at the immediate "point of production."

## Applications

DiMaggio and Powell's introduction cites Friedland and Alford's chapter as the direct source for treating institutional domains as bearing distinct action-logics — cognitive/rational in the market and bureaucracy, affective in the family, evaluative in religion — and links it to empirical illustrations elsewhere in the volume: DiMaggio's (ch. 11) account of competing "democratic versus elite" models of the American art museum in the 1920s, and Galaskiewicz's (ch. 12) study of Minneapolis business leaders sustaining a "communitarian rather than a pure market model" of the corporate role — both read as contests between institutional logics rather than simple technical adaptation. [[powell-walter-w]] (ch. 8) separately cites Friedland and Alford for the claim that "profit and risk are culturally constructed categories whose definitions vary widely across nation-states and within particular time periods."

## Critiques and Limitations

Friedland and Alford's own chapter records a critique of a fellow contributor to the logics/cognitive line of institutionalism: Mary Douglas's *How Institutions Think* (1986) is credited with the claim that institutions "require a cognitive base that naturalizes and rationalizes the conventions which constitute the institution," but faulted for failing to theorize how the incompatible organizing principles of different institutions actually come into conflict, despite recognizing that they do. A separate internal citation notes Schneider's (1987) critique of Clifford Geertz — engaged at length elsewhere in the same chapter as an exemplar of interpretive/cultural analysis — for conflating "significance and signification" without an adequate evidentiary base; Friedland and Alford's own account inherits some exposure to the same charge, since institutional logics are identified through interpretive reading of "material practices and symbolic constructions" rather than through an independently specified measurement procedure.
