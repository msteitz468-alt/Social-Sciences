## Definition as Coined

Pochteca were the long-distance merchants of Aztec society: a numerically small but
socially powerful class, treated by the emperor in ways resembling nobility. A pochteca
merchant who died on an expedition was believed to go to the Sun God's paradise, the
same afterlife reserved for warriors who died in battle. Pochteca answered directly to
the royal palace and the tlatoani, and traveled widely to obtain luxury goods — quetzal
feathers, amber, and similar prestige items — for the crown (Coe & Koontz 2013).

A closely allied specialized group, the oztomeca, traveled disguised in local dress,
spoke local languages, and gathered military intelligence alongside trade goods; Coe
and Koontz describe them as often serving as "the vanguard for the Aztec takeover of
another nation."

## Semantic History

Pochteca membership was hereditary. Twelve merchant guilds are recorded, all centered in
Tenochtitlan-Tlatelolco under head merchants based there (see [[tenochtitlan]]). The
principal trade route linked the capital to the Putún Maya port-of-trade at
Xicallanco on the Gulf of Mexico; the Aztec garrison town of Tochtepec, in northern
Oaxaca, served as a key nodal point for caravans moving to and from the "hot country."
Per Sahagún, exports consisted largely of cotton mantles and textiles drawn from royal
tribute warehouses, plus cast-gold jewelry (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Operationalizations

"Some have seen [the pochteca] as an entrepreneurial middle class in formation,"
Coe and Koontz report, because pochteca paid taxes in luxury goods rather than land
produce and grew wealthy through trade — a characterization the authors report but do
not fully endorse; it is recorded here as a POSITION, not a wiki-voice finding.

Pochteca wealth display was formally sanctioned: successful merchants had to creep back
into the city at night after profitable expeditions rather than display their gains
openly. Per van Zantwijk (via Coe & Koontz 2013), the guild hierarchy built in a
leveling mechanism — rising in guild rank required progressively more costly
ritual outlay, from a Song Feast banquet at lower ranks to the sacrifice of purchased
slaves at higher ranks. Pochteca had their own patron gods: Xiuhtecuhtli, the Fire God,
and Yacatecuhtli, "Nose Lord," depicted with a traveler's staff and a woven fan.

## Applications

The pochteca's dual commercial/intelligence role (via the oztomeca) linked Aztec
long-distance trade directly to imperial expansion, feeding tribute flows into
[[tenochtitlan]] and the wider Triple Alliance economy; see [[redistribution]] for the
broader institutional context of tribute-based exchange.

## Critiques and Limitations

The "entrepreneurial middle class" reading is explicitly a reported position, not an
endorsed conclusion — Coe and Koontz note it without adopting it, and the wealth-
display sanctions and forced ritual expenditure cut against any straightforward
market-class analogy. The evidentiary base is documentary (Sahagún, tribute lists,
van Zantwijk's synthesis) rather than independently archaeological, and should be read
with that limitation in mind.
