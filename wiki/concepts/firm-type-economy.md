# Firm-type economy

Analytical contrast term introduced by [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] in *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963|Peddlers and Princes]]* (1963) for continuous, administratively coordinated business organization — Indonesian *toko* (stores; originally a Chinese word) and *perusahaan* (factories/enterprises, from *usaha*, “effort”) — opposed to the **[[bazaar-type-economy|bazaar-type]]** *pasar*.

## Definition as Coined

Against the bazaar’s fragmented person-to-person transactions, Geertz described the Western **firm-centered** economy as trade and industry through “impersonally defined social institutions which organize a variety of specialized occupations with respect to some particular productive or distributive end” *(attributed, Geertz 1963)*. In Modjokuto, “progress toward more effective patterns of economic activity… takes the form of a movement, hesitant and circumscribed, away from a bazaar-type economy toward a firm-type economy” *(attributed)*.

Embryonic store features Geertz listed: permanent location, full business day, more fixed prices, regular clerks, inventory adjusted to urban tastes, bookkeeping-based planning, aggressive customer search.

## Semantic History

- **1963.** Ethnographic middle-range term for organizational takeoff toward continuous enterprise — **not** Coase/Williamson firm theory.
- **Two routes in the same book.** Modjokuto: peddlers building *toko*/*perusahaan* out of bazaar skills. Tabanan: aristocrats building firm-type enterprises by redirecting *seka* and political loyalty (“managerial revolution… involves no revolution” — Geertz on Gadarata-type shareholder acquiescence).
- **End-state demand.** Geertz held both paths eventually require the peddler and the prince to become **professional managers** under a differentiated economic ethic *(attributed)*.

## Operationalizations

**Modjokuto continuum (cases):**
- *Toko Kudus* — transitional, still bazaar-embedded (circuit traders, kin textile network, NU youth leadership).
- Masjumi “work-and-pray” garment store-factory — display merchandising, piecework labor, break with pasar credit distribution.
- *Toko Ibrahim* — fully developed Javanese store (Hadji / Muhammadiyah; Bata agency); clearest *pasar*→*toko* development.
- *Perusahaan* cases: garment factories, Airship Hat Factory, bean-curd neighborhood plants, mechanized *gula djawa* sugar factory; *geradjen* sawmills; *annemer* contracting.

**Tabanan continuum (cases):**
- Gadarata and Ksatria trading concerns (seka/shareholder structure under noble leadership).
- Lord of Krambitan enterprises (tire-recapping with Chinese partnership, hotel, buses, tourism).
- Aristocratic women’s weaving factory; Den Pasar–Gilimanuk bus *seka*; Airlangga Ice Factory (most fully developed firm in Geertz’s Tabanan set).

## Applications

- *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-study|Peddlers and Princes]]* primary coining and cases.
- Proposition 5 of Geertz’s conclusion: major pretake-off innovations are **organizational rather than technical** — machines purchased and adapted; success turns on mobilizing people and capital *(attributed)*.
- Sociological benchmark: the firm as marker of economic differentiation and of a specific economic ethic regulating free rationality *(attributed)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Success stories may overstate generalizability; Geertz himself notes Modjokuto firms often fail to **grow** and Tabanan firms fail to **rationalize**.
- National capital scale and state sponsorship (Bellah-type argument Geertz cites) limit purely local firm paths.
- Quasi-public business–government fusion on the Tabanan path carries, in Geertz’s own warning, totalitarian risk if politicized enterprise becomes the general model *(attributed position, not wiki verdict)*.
