# Constant and Variable Capital

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), Chapter 8, [[marx-karl|Marx]] divides capital advanced in production into:

- **Constant capital (c)** — the part laid out in means of production (raw materials, auxiliaries, instruments). Its value is **transferred** to the product (wholly or by wear and tear); it does not create new value.
- **Variable capital (v)** — the part laid out in [[labor-power]]. Its value is reproduced by labour and a surplus is added; it is “variable” because it changes magnitude in the process.

**Attributed to Marx.** This is not the circulating/fixed capital distinction of classical economics (though related to turnover); it is a value-theoretic distinction tied to the origin of [[surplus-value]].

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Capital* I Ch. 8 | c/v as functional roles in valorization |
| *Capital* I Ch. 25 | Ratio c:v as [[organic-composition-of-capital]] |
| Later economics | Often rejected or restated as capital intensity / wage share |

## Operationalizations

- Rate of surplus-value uses **v** in the denominator (s/v), not total capital.
- Rate of profit (flagged, fuller in later volumes) uses total capital C = c + v.
- Machinery raises the share of c relative to v historically (Part 4; Part 7) — technical and value composition.

## Applications

- Explains why lengthening the working day or raising intensity acts on living labour (v), while machines transfer value but do not create surplus-value.
- Bridge to [[organic-composition-of-capital]] and the [[industrial-reserve-army]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Measurement of c and v in national accounts is theory-laden; not a standard statistical category today.
- Critics argue all capital is “variable” in market valuation; Marx’s distinction is internal to labour theory of value.
