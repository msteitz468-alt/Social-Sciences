# Constant and Variable Capital

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), Chapter 8, [[marx-karl|Marx]] divides capital advanced in production into:

- **Constant capital (c)** — the part laid out in means of production (raw materials, auxiliaries, instruments). Its value is **transferred** to the product (wholly or by wear and tear); it does not create new value.
- **Variable capital (v)** — the part laid out in [[labor-power]]. Its value is reproduced by labour and a surplus is added; it is “variable” because it changes magnitude in the process.

**Attributed to Marx.** This is not the circulating/fixed capital distinction of classical economics (though related to turnover); it is a value-theoretic distinction tied to the origin of [[surplus-value]].

## Operationalizations

- Rate of surplus-value uses **v** in the denominator (s/v), not total capital.
- Rate of profit (flagged, fuller in later volumes) uses total capital C = c + v.
- Machinery raises the share of c relative to v historically (Part 4; Part 7) — technical and value composition.

## Applications

- Explains why lengthening the working day or raising intensity acts on living labour (v), while machines transfer value but do not create surplus-value.
- Bridge to [[organic-composition-of-capital]] and the [[industrial-reserve-army]].

## Volume 2: Against the Fixed/Circulating Conflation

In *[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume II]]* Chapters 8–11, Marx insists at length that the c/v distinction (defined by each element's role in valorization — transferring existing value vs. creating new value) is categorically distinct from the fixed/circulating distinction (defined by mode of value-transfer to the product — gradually vs. all at once), a distinction inherited from classical political economy.

Marx argues Adam Smith conflated the two: Smith's "circulating capital" mixes the fixed/fluid opposition (internal to productive capital) with the separate opposition between productive capital and capital in its circulation forms (commodity capital, money capital); Smith also forgets labour-power in his list of circulating capital's components, substituting workers' means of subsistence instead — which, per Marx, buries the crucial character of the wage-portion of capital as *variable* (value-creating) capital beneath the merely formal fact of its rapid turnover. Marx credits Ricardo with inheriting this confusion uncritically, arguing that once wage-capital is grouped as "circulating" opposite fixed capital, "the all-important distinction between variable and constant capital is thereby obliterated, and with it the whole secret of surplus-value formation and of capitalist production." Marx traces this conflation forward through Barton, J.S. Mill, and Ramsay.

By contrast, Marx credits the Physiocrats' *avances primitives*/*avances annuelles* distinction (see [[quesnay-francois]]) as untouched by this confusion, since it was framed solely in terms of differing reproduction periods within productive capital — though the Physiocrats' separate doctrine ascribing surplus-value to agriculture/nature rather than labour barred them from discovering the constant/variable distinction itself.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Measurement of c and v in national accounts is theory-laden; not a standard statistical category today.
- Critics argue all capital is “variable” in market valuation; Marx’s distinction is internal to labour theory of value.
