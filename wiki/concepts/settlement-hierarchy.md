# Settlement Hierarchy

## Definition as Coined

An **etic** ordering of settlements in a region by size, function, or administrative rank — typically reconstructed from site areas and artifact densities in [[archaeological-survey|regional survey]] — used to infer political-economic centralization, service catchments, and urban–rural structure.

## Semantic History

Settlement-pattern archaeology (Willey and others) shifted attention from single sites to regional systems. [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]]'s Mesopotamian surveys (*Land behind Baghdad*; *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*, 1981) made hierarchy operational through hectare classes, period maps, and rank-size analysis of alluvial cities and villages. Later landscape archaeology multiplies hierarchy measures (catchment, network centrality, off-site densities).

## Operationalizations

- **Site area (ha)** as proxy for population and centrality (Adams 1981 tables and figures).
- **Rank-size distributions** (primate vs log-normal systems) — see [[rank-size-rule]].
- **Functional hierarchy** (temple city, town, village, hamlet) inferred from size + special finds — higher inference load.

## Applications

Southern Mesopotamian urban origins and later imperial landscapes in Adams 1981 (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-study|Heartland study]]*):

- Size classes and period maps from Ubaid through Late Islamic.
- ED I: ~81% of southern settlement area in sites >10 ha (~71% north) — "first predominantly urbanized society" (attributed Adams).
- Northern early Uruk "flattop"/convex vs southern primate [[uruk|Uruk]]; Sasanian often convex rather than classic primacy.
- Emic caveat: ancient Babylonian "city-ness" was roughly all-or-nothing; hierarchy analysis is etic (Adams Conclusion).

Comparative pristine urbanism in *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Evolution of Urban Society]]* (1966).

## Critiques and Limitations

Area ≠ population; multi-period mounds inflate or blur contemporaneous hierarchy; mobile pastoralists under-detected (Adams and Jacobsen both flag this). Hierarchy can be read as political centralization without independent evidence of administration.

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|Early History of the Ancient Near East]]* 1988. Complements Adams 1981 hectare/rank-size operationalization; co-author of *The Uruk Countryside* with Adams.)*

**Centralization measures.** Nissen adopts settlement-geography patterns opposing centralization and decentralization: growth of centrality as hierarchical subordination; **degree of centrality measures organizational stage** of a settlement system. Center–surroundings mutual dependence: surroundings depend on central functions (temples, warehouses, administration); center requires tribute/taxes; archaeologically legible once surroundings form settlements → “center of the first order” / simple **two-tier** system, with max/min spatial limits set by transport and client capacity. Expansion beyond max transport radius produces neighboring simple systems rather than pure spatial expansion of one; proximity of first-order centers creates competition, new ranking, and a higher-ranking **second-order** center/system. When suitable plains allow, the process repeats into **third- and fourth-order** systems — all attested, he argues, in the ancient Near East. Typology (fig. 11): (a) isolated settlements in narrow valleys; (b) simple system on a small plain; (c) three-tiered and (d) four-tiered systems on larger plains. Ecological-unit size is linked to organizational level but is not sole explanation (northern plains lag vs Susiana).

**Empirical steps (attributed).** Behbehan plain Late Susiana: Tepe Sohz ~12 ha center with 2–3 ha satellites = simple two-tier. Next phase: fully developed Susiana multilevel systems (centers such as Susa) — probably first **three-tier** systems — while contemporary Lower Mesopotamia still at isolated settlements. Late Uruk Uruk hinterland: more than tenfold site increase; aggregate area 60 → 440 ha; **four-tier hierarchy headed by Uruk**. ED I consolidation: fewer sites, larger average size, inhabited area expands; later ED urban dominance (~9/10 population in sites >30 ha in Uruk hinterland). Empty concentric strip 12–15 km from Uruk (~2–3 km wide) interpreted as sphere-of-influence border under predominantly political/administrative center–hinterland relations. Limitation: qualitative-only central places miss size signal; Akkad-period surface dating of hierarchy tiers is poorly recoverable because mass pottery is nearly identical to ED III and Ur III.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Oaxaca tiers

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; demography largely from the Settlement Pattern Project — [[blanton-richard|Blanton]], [[kowalewski-stephen|Kowalewski]] et al.; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

| Phase | Hierarchy (attributed) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Rosario (700–500 BC)** | **Three-tier** maximal chiefdom | Paramount (SJM) / subchief villages / hamlets; ~70–85 communities; buffer zone ~80 km² |
| **Monte Albán II (~100 BC–AD 200)** | **Four-tier** administrative hierarchy | Tier 1 Monte Albán only “city” (~416 ha, ~14,500); Tier 2 six “towns” 970–1950 (SJM, Tejalapan, Dainzú, SMT-23 etc., 15–22 km from capital); Tier 3 ≥30 large villages 200–700; Tier 4 >400 small villages <200. Only Tiers 1–3 thought administrative; central-place lattice |
| **Monte Albán IIIa (~AD 200–500)** | Valley ~115,000; **peer Tier-1** centers | Monte Albán ~16,500 on 1196 terraces; [[jalieza|Jalieza]] ~12,835; possible DMTG dispersed Tlacolula cluster ~12,292; 8–9 Tier-2 frontier centers 2000–4500; 38 fortified/defensible sites housing ~two-thirds of population |

They treat unambiguous four-tier hierarchy plus regular central-place spacing and valley-wide single capital as indicators that the whole Valley of Oaxaca was under one **state** centered at Monte Albán — a level of integration not seen before Period II. Caveats they stress: sheer size ≠ political importance; population-from-surface estimates are tentative; full understanding needs excavation at every tier. Early villages (1700–1200 BC) clustered on *yuh kohp* humid alluvium; regional perspective urged because no civilization can be understood without its network of cities, towns, and villages (Flannery’s urging of Blanton’s survey).

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — full-coverage survey update and Rosario–Late I trajectory

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022, drawing on the Valley of Oaxaca, Nochixtlán Valley, Mixteca Alta, and Coixtlahuaca full-coverage surveys — "one of the largest 'macroregional' archaeological datasets in the world.") Full-coverage survey is defined as walking every accessible area without gaps or sampling, recording settlement distribution, agricultural features, fortifications, roads, boundary markers, public architecture, and artifact inventories. Regional population is estimated by two standard methods: house-foundation counts × persons/house (5–10, per Blanton 1978:29–30), or sherd-scatter area × density (10–25 persons/ha, up to 100/ha for very dense sites; Parsons 1971; Sanders 1965; Blanton, Kowalewski, Feinman, and Appel 1982:10–12) — both explicitly flagged as imprecise but the best available absent census data (Drennan, Berrey, and Peterson 2015).

**Settlement-hierarchy depth over time in the Valley of Oaxaca:** Rosario phase (700–500 BC) = **two levels** above hamlets ([[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]] dominant); Early Monte Albán I = **four levels** (Monte Albán added as new top tier); Late Monte Albán I = hierarchy quadrupled in number of ranked centers, spreading from eastern Tlacolula to Ejutla in the south. Civic-ceremonial hierarchy (sites with 2+ mounds): Rosario ~14 sites; Early I 57 sites (Monte Albán alone at top; 4 sites with 10–14 mounds; 12 with 5–7; 40 with 2–4); Late I 94 sites, five-level hierarchy (Kowalewski et al. 1989: chs. 4–6). Total site counts: 72 (Rosario) → 259 (Early I) → 643 (Late I). Regional population: Early I ~15,000 across 250+ settlements; Late I tripled to more than 51,000–55,000 (figures differ slightly between passages in the source, likely reflecting different Late I sub-phase cutoffs) across ~750 settlements, with ~75% of Late I growth occurring within 20 km of Monte Albán.

**Highland Oaxaca overall** grew roughly ninefold from ~10,000 (ca. 1000 BC) to ~90,000 by ca. 300 BC (most growth after 500 BC), then to ~108,000 by 300–100 BC. The Coixtlahuaca Valley (Mixteca Alta) held the largest single population concentration in highland Oaxaca by the end of the 700–300 BC period — about 50,000 people over 18 sq km, nearly half the valley's population, with a full five-tier settlement hierarchy including a center of ~30,000 — but was almost entirely abandoned by 300–100 BC except for one Level II town and two small villages, only to rise again as a premier Mesoamerican city-state in the 15th century.

## Basin of Mexico multi-tier hierarchies (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979)

Attributed to [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study: [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]). The Basin of Mexico Survey Project's site typology (site size, occupational density, architectural complexity, derived population, location relative to other sites) tracked the emergence of increasingly complex hierarchies over time: a first well-defined **three-level hierarchy** (hamlets/small villages, large villages, regional centers) appeared by First Intermediate Phase Two, with six substantial sites showing definite or probable civic-ceremonial architecture. By **First Intermediate Phase Three**, population had clustered into six principal regional groupings, two of which ([[cuicuilco]] and [[teotihuacan]]) showed "extreme nucleation" into single centers of 20,000+ people; the authors describe a **four-level hierarchy** in which Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan were "so much larger and more complex than 13 other small regional centers" that the latter were likely subordinate to them.

At the peak of Middle Horizon urbanization, [[teotihuacan]] itself (per Millon's Teotihuacan Mapping Project) covered 20 km² with an estimated mean population of 125,000 (possible maximum 200,000) — a scale the authors describe as ranking with the greatest pre-industrial urban centers in the world — while the rest of the Basin's Middle Horizon settlement sample (10 provincial centers, 17 large villages, 77 small villages, 149 hamlets, plus quarries and salt-making stations) formed the subordinate tiers beneath it. By the **Late Horizon** (Aztec), the authors describe a **three-tier** hierarchy reconstructed jointly from survey and ethnohistoric/tax-census sources, in contrast to the deliberately-imposed single-center dominance of the Middle Horizon system.

The project's population-density-to-hierarchy calibration itself is method-dependent: site type (hamlet <100 people; small nucleated village 100–500; larger classes above) was assigned from a sherd-density visual grading scale cross-checked against burial-derived Crude Mortality Rate estimates and against 16th-century tax-census figures, with the authors reporting "a remarkable level of agreement" (~20% divergence) between the independent methods — see [[archaeological-survey]] for the full survey-factor reliability figures underlying these hierarchy counts, and [[seriation]] for the chronological assumptions (Basin-wide ceramic-sequence uniformity) on which phase-by-phase hierarchy comparisons depend.

## Diyala trajectory (Adams 1965)

*(Attributed — [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965|Land Behind Baghdad]]; study [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study]].)* The Diyala survey supplies the first full multi-millennium settlement-hierarchy trajectory for a Mesopotamian region (sites / built-up ha / % in towns, per Adams's tables): Ubaid 22 sites; Warka–Protoliterate 43; Early Dynastic 96 / 384 / 77%; Akkadian 97 / 403 / 76%; Ur III–Isin-Larsa 129 / 462 / 71%; Old Babylonian 152 / 380 / 63%; Cassite 104 / 230 / 56%; Middle Babylonian 34 / 53; Neo-Babylonian 53 / 75 / 19%; Achaemenid 57 / 100 / 38%; Seleucid-Parthian 199 / 1,507 (average site size jumps 3.5→8.8 ha; true cities appear); Sasanian 437 / 3,489 (59% urban); Early Islamic/Samarran 329 / 2,198 excluding Baghdad and Samarra, each of which alone exceeded all other regional settlement combined. Adams read the falling town-share across the second millennium BCE as ruralization under political disruption, and the metropolitan Baghdad/Samarra scale as a new settlement type ("not merely urban but metropolitan") — urbanism in the basin being "cumulative rather than merely cyclical."
