# Organic Crisis

> [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s analysis of **crisis of authority** / organic crisis of parties and hegemony in *[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]* (State and Civil Society). **Attributed voice** throughout.

## Definition as Coined

In notes on political parties in periods of organic crisis, Gramsci describes situations where social classes detach from their traditional parties; the immediate situation becomes delicate and dangerous; "caesarist" solutions or police-form domination may appear. The crisis of the parties becomes a crisis of the State when parties had embodied State life.

Canonical formulation under "Wave of Materialism" and "Crisis of Authority" (attributed):

> "If the ruling class has lost its consensus, i.e. is no longer 'leading' but only 'dominant', exercising coercive force alone, this means precisely that the great masses have become detached from their traditional ideologies… **The crisis consists precisely in the fact that the old is dying and the new cannot be born; in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear.**"

Organic vs conjunctural: elsewhere (relations of force) Gramsci distinguishes organic (durable structural) movements from conjunctural (immediate, almost accidental) ones — analysis must not mistake the second for the first. An organic crisis is a crisis of the hegemonic system itself, not only a business-cycle downturn (though economic crisis may precipitate it).

## Semantic History

- **Gramsci:** Crisis of authority; detachment masses–parties; interregnum; morbid symptoms; opportunity and danger for historical materialism / new culture.
- **Later left theory:** "Organic crisis of capitalism," "crisis of the state form," often stretched beyond party-hegemony detachment.
- **Journalistic uptake:** The "old is dying…" sentence circulates as a free-floating epigraph — restore context (loss of consensus; leading vs dominant) when using it analytically.

## Operationalizations

- **Leading vs dominant:** loss of consent while coercion remains.
- **Party–masses detachment:** empirical sign in electoral/organisational collapse and rise of "anti-party" or caesarist figures (Gramsci's related discussion of progressive vs reactionary Caesarism).
- **Interregnum symptoms:** scepticism toward all ideologies; reduction of politics to cynical economics; generational blockage of those who might exercise hegemony.
- **Not self-resolving:** force alone may not restore leadership; reabsorption by the ruling class remains possible (passive-revolutionary or caesarist paths).

## Applications

- Interwar Europe / Italian fascism as context for the notes (editors: entry point to fascism analysis — without crude equation fascism = capitalism).
- Later analyses of party-system collapse and populist caesarism (attribute the extension).
- Link to [[war-of-position]]: positional struggle intensifies when trench-systems crack but new hegemony is not yet organised.

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Morbid symptoms" is diagnostic metaphor, not a checklist with fixed cut-points.
- Risk of applying "organic crisis" to every polarisation cycle — loses contrast with conjunctural crisis.
- Notebook opacity on fascism (censorship) requires care; Hoare stresses prior political experience as reading key.
