# Carceral Archipelago

## Definition as Coined

In *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* Part Four, chapter 3 ("The carceral"), [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] describes a **carceral archipelago** (also "carceral network," "carceral continuum") extending well beyond legal imprisonment: agricultural prison sections, colonies for poor and vagrant children, almshouses, orphanages, apprentice establishments, factory-convents, charitable societies, and so on — a series of institutions that communicate punishments "according to quality and quantity" and make possible a continuous passage from slight irregularity to crime.

He would date the **completion** of the carceral system not to a penal code or cellular law but to the official opening of **Mettray** (22 January 1840) — a disciplinary form at its extreme ("cloister, prison, school, regiment") and, he argues, the first training college in pure discipline (cadres subjected to the same apprenticeships as inmates).

Key thesis (attributed): **"The carceral network does not cast the unassimilable into a confused hell; there is no outside."** It takes back with one hand what it seems to exclude with the other. In a panoptic society of which incarceration is the "omnipresent armature," **the delinquent is an institutional product** — "although it is true that prison punishes delinquency, delinquency is for the most part produced in and by an incarceration which, ultimately, prison perpetuates in its turn."

## Semantic History

- **1975:** Foucault's synthesis term for the continuum of coercive-corrective institutions.
- **Reception:** "carceral" and "carceral state" entered US critical race and prison studies (often with different scope — mass incarceration, racialized confinement — not identical to Foucault's nineteenth-century European genealogy). Keep the wiki concept anchored to Foucault's continuum-of-institutions thesis; later usages should be attributed separately when ingested.
- Related but distinct: Goffman's [[total-institution]] (interactional analysis of closed establishments) — complementary, not synonymous.

## Operationalizations

Foucault lists major **effects** of the carceral system (attributed):

1. **Continuous gradation** from disorder → offence → departure from a norm (crime, sin, and bad conduct no longer held in separate institutional orders).
2. **Disciplinary careers** — recruitment of major delinquents through linked establishments (assistance → orphanage → school → workshop → prison); "there is no outside."
3. **Naturalization of the power to punish** — lowering the threshold of tolerance to penality by playing legal justice and extra-legal discipline against one another.
4. **The norm** — emergence of "judges of normality" (teacher-judge, doctor-judge, educator-judge, social worker-judge) everywhere.
5. **Power-knowledge armature** — carceral texture enables the capture of the body and the formation of knowledge the new economy of power needs (link to the human sciences).
6. **Solidity of the prison** — its resistance to abolition rooted in the panoptic régime of which it is a concentrated form, not mere state repression.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **"No outside"** can be read as totalizing; Foucault still records resistance (workers' press, Fourierists, Béasse case) earlier in Part Four.
- Euro-French institutional series; colonial carceral systems and racialized mass incarceration are different historical objects (flag when linking).
- Functional reading of "uses" of delinquency is a thesis, not a demonstrated conspiracy of a single author of the system.
