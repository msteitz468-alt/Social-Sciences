# Centre and Periphery

## Definition as Coined

**Centre–periphery** (core–periphery) is the relational concept, drawn from dependency
theory and Wallerstein's [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems analysis]], that a
dominant **core** and a subordinate **periphery** form a single structured system
linked by asymmetric exchange — so that neither can be understood in isolation. In
archaeology it was adapted (Rowlands, Frankenstein, Ekholm & Friedman, and centrally by
[[kristiansen-kristian|Kristian Kristiansen]] in *[[kristiansen-europe-before-history-1998|Europe
before History]]*, 1998) to argue that prehistoric "barbarian" Europe evolved not
autonomously but through its shifting relations to Mediterranean and Near-Eastern cores
(attributed).

## Semantic History

The concept migrated from Prebisch's and Wallerstein's analysis of the modern capitalist
world-economy into anthropology and archaeology in the late 1970s–1980s. Kristiansen
(drawing on **Ekholm & Friedman**'s "imperialism" in ancient world-systems and
**Frankenstein & Rowlands**' 1978 prestige-goods model of Early Iron Age southern
Germany) generalized it into a claim that European prehistory as a whole is legible as
core–periphery relations of shifting scale — states, chiefdoms, and tribes all occupying
positions in one interaction field (attributed, Kristiansen 1998). Critics warn the
transfer risks importing a modern economic logic and an over-diffusionist, core-driven
picture onto qualitatively different, non-capitalist societies.

## Operationalizations

In archaeology the relation is traced through the **flow of prestige goods, metals, raw
materials, and stylistic influence** between regions, and through the appearance of
"peripheral" elites whose power rested on controlling access to core-derived valuables
(the [[prestige-goods-economy]]). Kristiansen operationalizes cycles of core dominance
and peripheral response as recurrent centralisation/decline sequences (attributed).

## Applications

In *[[kristiansen-europe-before-history-1998|Europe before History]]* Kristiansen applies
it at several scales (all attributed):

- **The Mediterranean core vs. the temperate-European periphery** — Phoenician and Greek
  colonial expansion as an "economic take-off" that restructured barbarian Europe through
  the prestige-goods it fed northward.
- **The [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]] princely centres** as the paradigm case: a
  periphery whose "Fürstensitze" (Heuneburg, Hochdorf, Vix) rose on controlled access to
  Mediterranean valuables and **collapsed when that exchange contracted** — feeding, on
  his account, the outward Celtic/La Tène expansions.
- **Nested peripheries** — the [[nordic-bronze-age|Nordic]] zone as a far periphery
  linked via the Carpathians→Lausitz→Scandinavia corridor; southwest Iberia (Tartessos)
  and the Caucasus/Urartu frontier as further cases.
- Kristiansen insists (against the older diffusionist reading) that peripheries are
  **active agents**, selectively accepting and transforming core-derived goods and value
  systems.

## Critiques and Limitations

The prehistoric application is contested: critics argue it (1) projects a
capitalist/modern core–periphery logic onto qualitatively different societies, (2)
over-emphasizes external (diffusionist) causes over internal dynamics, and (3) casts
peripheries as passive recipients. Kristiansen and others reply that the model is used
heuristically, with peripheries as active agents. *(Enriched during integration.)*
