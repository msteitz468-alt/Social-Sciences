# Relations of Definition

[[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s analogue (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, esp. Ch. 2 "The Politics of Knowledge in the Risk Society") to [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s "relations of production": the rules, institutions, and competences — science, law, expertise, the mass media — that determine how risks are identified, measured, attributed, and adjudicated, and whose voice counts in doing so.

> **Terminology note:** In the 1986 text Beck develops the full substantive argument (the entire "politics of knowledge" chapter) but the crystallized phrase "relations of definition" as a named Marx-analogue is elaborated most explicitly in his later work; this page uses it as the settled name for the argument as it appears here.

## Definition as Coined

Because modern risks are invisible and knowledge-dependent, whoever controls their *definition* controls their social and political reality. Beck argued that "in class positions being determines consciousness, while in risk positions, conversely, consciousness (knowledge) determines being" (Beck 1986: 54). Risk victims lose "cognitive sovereignty": they become "incompetent in matters of their own affliction," dependent on external experts who effectively "choose the victims." Decisions about acceptable values, causal-proof standards, and measurement are simultaneously decisions about who is harmed, who is compensated, and who is responsible — so knowledge production and political consequence are inseparable.

## Semantic History

The concept extends the Marxian frame from *production* to *definition*: where classical political economy asked who owns the means of production, Beck asks who owns the means of defining risk. The term became central to Beck's later "second modernity" writings and to environmental sociology's critique of expert risk assessment.

## Operationalizations

Not measured. It is worked out through Beck's analyses of the **"acceptable level" (Grenzwert)** as a legitimating device ("a phony trick" that permits a quantum of poisoning while declaring it harmless by fiat), of the **asymmetry of scientific error** ("insisting on the purity of the scientific analysis leads to the pollution and contamination of air, foodstuffs, water, soil, plants, animals and people"), and of the **"latent side effect"** as a mode of licensing harm. Related: Beck's coinage **"second-hand non-experience"** for the inverted epistemic structure whereby abstract scientific knowledge, not personal perception, grounds belief in risk.

## Applications

Grounds the risk-society claim that "the sciences' monopoly on rationality is broken... There is no expert on risk," and connects to [[reflexive-scientization]] (the demonopolization of scientific authority) and to [[subpolitics]] (definitional battles as a site of politics outside the formal system). Beck argued that lay non-acceptance of official risk definitions "is not something to be reproached as 'irrationality'... but indicates that the cultural premises of acceptability contained in scientific and technical statements on risks are wrong."

## Critiques and Limitations

As a diagnostic frame rather than a measurement apparatus, it is difficult to falsify. Critics of Beck's broader program question whether it over-credits lay counter-expertise and under-specifies when definitional contests are actually won by lay actors versus absorbed back into expert institutions.
