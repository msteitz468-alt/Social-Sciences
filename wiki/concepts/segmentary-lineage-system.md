# Segmentary Lineage System

> Crystallized in [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]]. Contested model — see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]].

## Definition as Coined

In [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s account of the [[nuer|Nuer]] (attributed throughout), a **segmentary system** is a social order composed of nested groups that are "distinct groups only in relation to each other." Two coordinate structures are involved. The **political (territorial) system** segments a tribe into primary, secondary, and tertiary sections down to the village. The **lineage system** segments a clan — "the largest group of agnates who trace their descent from a common ancestor and between whom marriage is forbidden" — into maximal, major, minor, and minimal lineages (*thok dwiel*). The defining dynamic is **balanced/complementary opposition**: "the members of any segment unite for war against adjacent segments of the same order and unite with these adjacent segments against larger sections." A political system is thus "an equilibrium between opposed tendencies towards fission and fusion... two aspects of the same segmentary principle."

The system's signature is the **relativity of the group**: "a man is a member of a political group of any kind in virtue of his non-membership of other groups of the same kind"; "there is... always contradiction in the definition of a political group, for it is a group only in relation to other groups." Whether and on which side a man fights "depends on the structural relationship of the persons engaged."

## Semantic History

The idea of a society built of like segments descends from Durkheim's *segmental society* (*[[durkheim-division-of-labor-study|The Division of Labor]]*), but Evans-Pritchard gave it its precise ethnographic form and the fission/fusion mechanics. In *The Nuer* the **lineage** system is explicitly *not* identical with the **political** system: the dominant clan's lineages are "a conceptual skeleton" and "a system of values... providing the idiom in which [tribal segments'] relations can be expressed and directed," but "a clan is never a community and can never act corporately." Maximal lineages *correspond* to primary sections, major to secondary, minor to tertiary, minimal to villages — "though the correspondence is not exact." Extended by Evans-Pritchard and Fortes into a comparative program (*African Political Systems*, 1940; not ingested), "segmentary lineage" became the mid-century template for African political ethnography, and later a byword for a model criticized as too neat.

## Operationalizations

- **The blood-wealth boundary.** The tribe is operationally the widest group within which homicide compensation (*cut*) is payable; where no compensation is paid, groups are separate tribes — the field criterion distinguishing tribe from section.
- **Genealogical correspondence.** Lineage tiers are mapped onto territorial sections; Evans-Pritchard noted dominant-clan genealogies of politically important tribes were deeper and more consistent than those of minor clans, arguing "the tribal system draws out and segments the dominant clans" (i.e., politics shapes genealogy, not only vice versa).
- **Telescoping.** Nuer genealogies were systematically foreshortened — collateral lines "grafted on," names dropped — keeping founder-to-present depth roughly constant (~10–12 generations), which underwrites the constancy of [[structural-time|structural time]].

## Applications

The Nuer are the type-case; the model was applied across segmentary/acephalous societies (Tallensi, Bedouin, and beyond) and underpins the anthropology of **[[ordered-anarchy|stateless order]]** and the structural theory of the **[[feud]]**. Its idiom of fission/fusion also organizes Evans-Pritchard's treatment of the age-set system (segmentary in form) — though he stressed age-sets, unlike lineages, were *not* structurally interdependent with the political system.

## Critiques and Limitations

The model was subsequently attacked as (a) a **native ideology or an anthropologist's idealization** rather than a description of behaviour (Kuper, Holy, Verdon, Karp); (b) empirically loose — Evans-Pritchard himself conceded "no clear-cut distinction between fighting against another tribe and fighting against a segment of one's own tribe," and much idiosyncratic migration/feud history; and (c) better replaced, for Nuer expansion specifically, by **ecological/materialist** accounts (Sahlins; Kelly, *The Nuer Conquest*, 1985). See [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]. Because the core claims are single-source and contested, they are stated here in attributed voice.
