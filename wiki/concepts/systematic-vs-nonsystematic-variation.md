## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) model observed variation in any social phenomenon as
arising from two separate factors: a systematic component and a nonsystematic
component. They write: "the variance in the Conservative vote across districts or the
variance in conflict between West Bank communities can be conceptualized as arising
from two separate factors: systematic and nonsystematic differences." KKV are explicit
that neither component is inherently more important than the other, but that
separating them is a core task of inference: "The systematic component is not more
important than the nonsystematic component ... distinguishing between the two is an
essential task of social science."

## Semantic History

The distinction has clear statistical ancestry (signal versus noise, fixed versus
random effects in regression models), but KKV's 1994 treatment formalizes it as a
foundational concept for qualitative as well as quantitative political science,
introducing it via a paired pair of formal terms: the "realized variable" y, denoting
the values actually observed, and the "random variable" Y, the hypothetical variable
representing what could have occurred under repeated (hypothetical) replication of the
same underlying conditions. KKV state: "we define Y as a random variable because it
varies randomly across hypothetical replications ... we often use the term realized
variable to refer to y and random variable to refer to Y."

KKV also clarify, against a possible misreading, that a "systematic" factor need not
itself be constant in value — what makes a factor systematic is that its effect (or
its pattern of change) is persistent and predictable, not that the factor's own value
never changes.

## Operationalizations

KKV attach the systematic/nonsystematic distinction to two rival background
philosophical positions about the nature of randomness itself, which they present as a
genuine, unresolved dispute rather than adjudicate: "Perspective 1" (a probabilistic
world, in which randomness is irreducible and inherent in nature and society: "nothing
a researcher does to analyze data can have any effect on reducing the fundamental
amount of nonsystematic variation existing in various parts of the empirical world")
and "Perspective 2" (a deterministic world, in which apparent randomness is only the
analyst's current ignorance: "Given the right explanatory variables, the world is
entirely predictable"). KKV note the two perspectives are usually observationally
equivalent in practice, and that "a choice between the two perspectives depends on
faith or belief rather than on empirical verification."

Formally, the decomposition underlies the estimator criteria KKV introduce in Chapter
2 — unbiasedness, efficiency, and consistency, combined in mean square error (MSE) —
which they apply to both descriptive and causal estimators (see
[[descriptive-inference]] and [[causal-inference]]).

## Applications

The decomposition is KKV's basic tool for explaining why any single observation or
case may deviate from a theory's prediction without falsifying it — deviation may
reflect nonsystematic (chance) variation rather than a wrong systematic model. It
underlies their treatment of [[measurement-error]], where they distinguish constant
(non-biasing) versus subgroup-correlated (biasing) measurement error using the same
systematic/nonsystematic vocabulary.

## Critiques and Limitations

The systematic/nonsystematic decomposition, and the deeper probabilistic-versus-
deterministic choice KKV attach to it, is a metaphysical commitment KKV themselves
concede cannot be settled empirically. Whether qualitative, small-n research can
meaningfully estimate a "nonsystematic" component at all — as opposed to simply
absorbing all deviation into unmodeled specifics of the case — is part of the broader
dispute recorded on [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]] and
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]]; this page does not adjudicate that dispute
in wiki voice.
