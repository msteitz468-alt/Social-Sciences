# Post-Glacial Recolonization

## Definition as Coined

**Post-glacial recolonization** denotes the reoccupation of regions abandoned at the [[last-glacial-maximum|Last Glacial Maximum]] as ice sheets retreated and climates warmed — by plants, animals, and humans. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] the classic case is **northern Europe**: polar deserts emptied of people at the LGM, then resettled from southern French and Spanish refugia through Bølling, Allerød, [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] setbacks, and into Holocene Mesolithic woodlands.

## Semantic History

Culture-historical archaeology mapped late Upper Palaeolithic facies northward; processual programs then sought rate and process. Housley et al.’s 1997 AMS campaign (reported in Mithen) made recolonization a quantified chronological problem. Parallel language applies to biotic recolonization, American ice-sheet margins, Yangtze vegetation flip-flops, and Arctic Holocene expansion (e.g. Zhokhov) — same process class, different datasets.

## Operationalizations

As of Mithen 2003 (Europe-focused):

- **Chronometric:** AMS dates on cut-marked bones (human-contemporary by definition) from 45+ northern European sites (Housley et al. 1997 via Mithen).
- **Phased advance:** refuge limit near Loire tributaries; after 15,000 BC into upper Rhine; by 14,500 BC middle Rhine, Belgium, southern Germany; ~1 km/year average; ~four centuries later northern France, northern Germany, Denmark; ~12,700 BC first return to Britain after ~10,000 years (Britain still a peninsula).
- **Two-stage model (Housley et al.):** ~500-year **pioneering** phase (tool-scatter overnight camps, summer exploration) then **residential** phase (permanent northern base camps on reindeer/horse).
- **Environmental scaffolding:** Bølling birch/pine; Allerød woodland to southern Scandinavia; Younger Dryas tundra relapse from ~10,800 BC; tree pollen return ~9600 BC; beetle and lemming climate indices.
- **Motives (Mithen’s position):** population rebound insufficient alone; prestige resources, social tension, and curiosity as exploration drivers.

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **Sites:** Meiendorf/Stellmoor reindeer hunting; Paris-basin Magdalenian camps (Pincevent, Verberie, Étiolles); Gönnersdorf/Andernach; Creswell Crags hare trapping; Gough’s Cave; [[star-carr|Star Carr]] early Mesolithic woodland life.
- **Sea-level coupling:** Doggerland occupation and drowning; coastal reorientation ([[coastal-adaptation]]).
- **Beyond Europe:** post-LGM human and plant re-entry into formerly harsh zones in Asia and the Americas; Arctic Holocene settlements; Yangtze corridor vegetation recovery enabling rice trajectories.
- **Mesolithic formation:** recolonizing lineages become Star Carr–type forest foragers after Younger Dryas ends.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Chronologies are **region-specific**; global “recolonization” is a family of processes, not one migration.
- Pioneering/residential stages are **models** — low-density pioneers leave few traces and are easy to miss.
- Younger Dryas interrupts linear northward narratives; local abandonments reverse gains.
- Absolute rates (~1 km/year) are averages over coarse site samples — do not treat as ethnographic travel speed.
- Pre-aDNA genetic stories of who recolonized whom are thin in Mithen 2003; later aDNA revises ancestry maps — date-stamp.
