# Peasant Coalition

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 3), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] reframes peasant social organization as **forms of coalition** — alignments that pool force, share risk, regulate access to land and labor, and mediate the larger social order. Against approaches that only ask about the *quality* of interpersonal relations (Redfield's harmony vs Foster et al. on tension), Wolf asks what organizational forms peasants build.

**Three distinguishing criteria** (cross-cutting matrix):

| Criterion | Poles |
|---|---|
| Interest strands | manystranded vs singlestranded |
| Number of parties | dyadic vs polyadic |
| Social position | horizontal (peasant–peasant) vs vertical (peasant–superior) |

**Type exemplars (Wolf):**

- **Manystranded, dyadic, horizontal** — friendship, neighbor, bilateral kin ties.
- **Manystranded, dyadic, vertical** — **patron–client** (economic aid + legal protection for labor, votes, information, praise, exclusive loyalty; patrons compete by buying support with favors).
- **Manystranded, polyadic, horizontal** — [[closed-corporate-peasant-community]] (restricted membership, joint estate, leveling).
- **Manystranded, polyadic, vertical** — multifocal **descent groups** (Chinese *tsu*; built-in patron–client via senior/wealthy lines).
- **Singlestranded, polyadic, horizontal** — **associations** / sodalities (mutual-aid, burial, sugar-making, credit); Anderson & Anderson on associations as adaptive under modernization; may accrete prestige functions but remain task-centered.

## Semantic History

Shifts peasant sociology from culture-personality "folk" traits toward political organization and network form. Feeds later patron-client and social-capital literatures; sits with political anthropology of the 1960s–70s. Comparative regional table in Ch. 3 (Wolf) maps coalition mixes by area.

## Operationalizations

- Who is allied with whom, on what terms, with what boundary?
- Single- vs many-stranded; dyadic vs polyadic; vertical vs horizontal.
- Stability under pressure; patron competition; state substitution of centralized patronage for personal patrons (Wolf on modernizing regimes).
- Revolution/reform may *break open* corporate communities (Wolf on Mexican Revolution) or attack large descent groups (Chinese Communists via Fried).

## Applications

- Comparative rural politics without assuming village harmony or atomism (Marx "sack of potatoes" as one position on class unity — see [[peasantry-as-class]]).
- Bridge from household funds to collective action and its limits.
- Explains why millennial myths unite but do not organize ([[peasant-movements]]).
- Neotechnic change favors associations over corporate/descent resistance forms.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Typology can underplay class conflict *inside* coalitions.
- Primer survey; deep cases deferred to cited monographs.
- Gendered coalition membership under-specified.
