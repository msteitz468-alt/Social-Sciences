## Definition as Coined

Simmel argued that the sheer numerical size of a group is a *formal* determinant of its sociological character, independent of the group's content or purpose — a quantitative change in membership produces a qualitative change in social form. He introduced the problem by noting that "large' and 'small' groups are extremely crude scientific designations, indeterminate and vague," useful only "as a suggestion that the sociological form of the group depends upon its quantitative aspects," while insisting the connection could, in principle, be traced more exactly for certain small numbers of persons.

## Operationalizations

Simmel did not quantify group size numerically in a strict operational sense, but treated it as a graded formal variable with several observable correlates he analyzed across historical and comparative material:

- **Small groups** (socialist communities, religious sects, aristocracies) permit direct, face-to-face comparison of contribution and reward, close personal surveillance, and undifferentiated custom as a regulatory form. Simmel held that "justice in the distribution of production and reward can easily be realized in a small group... can be safeguarded there by its members," while "in the large group they are difficult." He argued small groups have an absolute as well as relative numerical ceiling: an aristocracy, for instance, "must be 'surveyable' by every single member of it," so that "each element must still be personally acquainted with every other."
- **Large groups and "the mass"** show, Simmel argued, a paradoxical relation to radicalism: ordinarily large groups show "less radicalism and decisiveness" because they can only be organized around ideas "accessible even to the lowest and most primitive" among their many heterogeneous members — but when a mass in physical proximity is activated, it becomes "ruthlessly radical," since "innumerable suggestions swing back and forth, resulting in an extraordinary nervous excitation."
- **Group size, radicalism, and cohesiveness**: Simmel proposed that, in general, "small parties are more radical than large ones," and that numerical growth dilutes ideological cohesion — he cited the Social-Democratic party as "forced by the fact of its large membership to dilute its qualitative radicalism."
- **Objective institutions/superstructures**: Simmel's central formal claim is that large groups compensate for the loss of direct personal cohesion by generating "organs which channel and mediate the interactions of its members" — "offices and representations, laws and symbols of group life, organizations and general social concepts" — which are "the abstract form of group cohesion whose concrete form can no longer exist after the group has reached a certain size." He drew an explicit analogy to the logical concept: "the most general concepts... gain, as it were, a separate existence," just as the large group's unity "finds expression in the group organs... only at the price of a great distance between all of these structures and the individual."
- **Custom, law, morality**: Simmel located custom as the regulatory form proper to intermediate, smaller groups (an estate or class), while law is the form suited to the largest groups requiring "surer guarantees," and individual morality answers to the freedom of the single person — the differentiation of these three norms out of an undifferentiated "normative as such" tracks group size.

## Applications

Simmel applied the size variable to: socialist communes (the Familistère de Guise), religious sects (Waldenses, Mennonites, Herrnhuter), historical aristocracies (Sparta, Venice, Poland, the Germanic gens), voting procedure (Roman tributim/centuriatim voting versus Greek mass voting), political parties (German conservatives, Social-Democrats), labor coalitions, and numerically-designated administrative bodies (the Frankfurt guild "Six," the Barcelona senate "the Hundred," the Germanic Hundreds).

## Critiques and Limitations

Simmel himself flagged the crudeness of "large" and "small" as scientific categories and acknowledged that a fully exact numerical sociology was, for the foreseeable future, "a wholly fantastic enterprise." He addressed the sorites-style objection that a continuous quantitative series cannot in principle yield a discrete qualitative break (the "heap of grain" problem), resolving it by arguing that intermediate-sized groups display a genuine mixture of small-group and large-group traits rather than posing an unanswerable borderline question. He also noted, in a footnote appended to this material, that his historical examples were drawn largely from secondary sources and could not all be checked against the latest research — a methodological caveat he treated as acceptable given that the essays' purpose was conceptual demonstration rather than historical verification.
