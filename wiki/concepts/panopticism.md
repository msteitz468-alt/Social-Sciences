# Panopticism

## Definition as Coined

In *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* (1975/1977), [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] names **panopticism** the general principle of a new political anatomy whose object is not the relations of sovereignty but the relations of [[disciplinary-power|discipline]]. He takes Jeremy Bentham's **Panopticon** (circular building, central tower, peripheral cells) as a **diagram of a mechanism of power reduced to its ideal form** — not merely a prison blueprint.

Foucault's major effect claim (attributed): the Panopticon is to "induce in the inmate a state of conscious and permanent visibility that assures the automatic functioning of power" — surveillance permanent in its effects even if discontinuous in its action; the architectural apparatus creates a power relation "independent of the person who exercises it," so that "the inmates should be caught up in a power situation of which they are themselves the bearers." Bentham's principle: power should be **visible** (the tower) and **unverifiable** (the inmate never knows whether he is being looked at at any moment, but must be sure he *may* always be).

## Semantic History

- **Bentham (late 18th c.):** utilitarian architectural proposal for prisons, workhouses, schools, etc.
- **Foucault (1975):** abstracts the design into a **generalizable diagram** — a way of defining power that increases the productive force of society while economizing the prince's force; opposite of the king's body as spectacular expenditure.
- **Later reception:** "panoptic" becomes a slogan for CCTV, digital surveillance, and social media; critics argue the metaphor is overstretched when applied to platforms without Bentham's architectural asymmetry or Foucault's disciplinary institutions. Keep the concept's load-bearing sense tied to Foucault's diagram and its institutional swarming, not every visibility technology.

## Operationalizations

Foucault does not measure panopticism; he exhibits it:

| Element | Function (Foucault, attributed) |
|---|---|
| Peripheral cell / central tower | Sees without being seen; lateral invisibility among inmates |
| Visible + unverifiable power | Automatic functioning; inmate as bearer of the relation |
| Polyvalent application | Prison, school, hospital, workshop, asylum — same diagram |
| "Swarming" of mechanisms | Closed fortresses de-institutionalize into flexible controls (school observing parents; hospital as sanitary observatory) |

Preceding model in the chapter: the **plague town** (partitioned, inspected, recorded) vs. the **leper** (binary exclusion). Panopticism, Foucault argues, combines the techniques of individualizing partition with the utopia of a transparent society.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Overgeneralization** (attributed): treating all modern surveillance as panoptic flattens differences among sovereign spectacle, disciplinary institutions, and later control/security assemblages (reception debates; Deleuze's "societies of control" is a common foil — not yet ingested).
- **Historical:** Bentham's design was rarely built as specified; Foucault treats it as diagram, not as diffusion study — a method choice, not a diffusion claim.
- **Agency:** the "bearer of the power relation" formula is often read as totalizing; Foucault also maps resistance and polyvalent use of the same mechanisms.
