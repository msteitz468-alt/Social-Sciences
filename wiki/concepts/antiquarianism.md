# Antiquarianism

## Definition as Coined

**Antiquarianism** denotes the early modern and Enlightenment practices of collecting, describing, and interpreting material remains of the past — coins, inscriptions, monuments, barrows, "curiosities" — before archaeology professionalized as a discipline. [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|A History of Archaeological Thought]]*, 2006) distinguishes **text-aided** antiquarian/classical traditions from **antiquarianism without texts** (northern European prehistory, New World mounds, etc.) and treats the **impasse of antiquarianism** as the problem relative dating and the three-age system later unlocked.

## Semantic History

Renaissance recovery of classical antiquity (Italy → northern Europe); cabinet-of-curiosities collecting; county chorography (Camden, Aubrey, Stukeley in Britain); Scandinavian royal/antiquarian offices; Enlightenment conjectural history. Trigger emphasizes that without relative chronology, non-textual antiquarianism could not order deep time — hence the Scandinavian breakthrough ([[thomsen-christian-j|Thomsen]], [[worsaae-jens-j-a|Worsaae]]) as exit from the impasse.

## Operationalizations

Corpus collection; illustration and typology of objects; association of finds with historical peoples named in texts (Gauls, Britons, Israelites); barrow-opening; early stratigraphic observations without full chronological system.

## Applications

Trigger's chs. 2–3 survey: Renaissance Italian antiquaries; classical archaeology's formation; northern European text-poor antiquarianism; recognition of stone tools as human artifacts; scientific antiquarianism; Romantic nationalism's use of antiquities; New World antiquarian debate (Moundbuilders).

## Contested Issues

Whether antiquarianism is proto-archaeology (progressive narrative) or a different knowledge culture (cabinets, virtuosi, local elites). Trigger records both cumulative technical gains and social-context drivers (middle-class and aristocratic patronage). Colonial antiquarianism of non-European pasts feeds later [[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology|evolutionary]] and nationalist misuses.
