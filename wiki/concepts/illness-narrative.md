# Illness Narrative

The story a sick person and their intimates tell to give coherence to the events and long-term
course of suffering — the organizing concept and title of [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* (1988).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman defines it in attributed voice: "The illness narrative is a story the patient tells,
and significant others retell, to give coherence to the distinctive events and long-term course
of suffering. The plot lines, core metaphors, and rhetorical devices that structure the illness
narrative are drawn from cultural and personal models for arranging experiences in meaningful
ways." His strong claim is constitutive, not merely representational: "The personal narrative
does not merely reflect illness experience, but rather it contributes to the experience of
symptoms and suffering."

## Semantic History

The concept is part of a broader late-1980s "narrative turn" across medicine, psychology, and
anthropology. Kleinman's version is distinctive in tying narrative directly to the
[[illness-disease-distinction|illness/disease distinction]] and [[explanatory-models]]: the
clinician "must first piece together the illness narrative as it emerges from the patient's and
the family's complaints and explanatory models; then… interpret it in light of the different
modes of illness meanings." The concept seeds later medical-humanities work on "narrative
medicine."

## Operationalizations

Kleinman distinguishes sub-forms:

- **Retrospective narratization** — reworking an illness account after a catastrophic (or
  narrowly averted) outcome; it "functions like ritual myth" (cf. Turner 1967), "gives shape
  and finality to a loss," and can serve political commentary (cf. Taussig 1980). Because its
  purpose is "significance and validity in the creation of a life story," it "can readily be
  shown to distort the actual happenings" — an ominous variant is narrative tailored to
  disability or malpractice litigation (by patient *and* physician).
- **Narratization among the elderly** — illness woven into a life story's "denouement,"
  establishing "a kind of final expertise" and reaffirming bonds with survivors.
- **Empathic witnessing** — the clinician's task of being with the sick person "to facilitate
  his or her building of an illness narrative that will make sense of and give value to the
  experience."

Kleinman names "ethnography, biography, history, psychotherapy" as the appropriate research
methods for the personal world of suffering, and argues that standardized symptom scales are
"scientifically replicable but ontologically invalid" for it.

## Applications

- Paddy Esposito reinterprets his fatal myocarditis through Conrad's *Lord Jim* and *Victory*:
  "It's this darn illness that has given me that second chance."
- Gordon Stuart, dying of metastatic cancer, rejects stage-theories of dying ("What rot… Who
  says you work your way eventually to acceptance—I don't accept it!"), grounding Kleinman's
  claim that "there is no single, timeless pathway toward death."
- Kleinman's own formative failure (1973), when a paralyzed teenager rejected his rote "courage
  and hope" rhetoric, drives the book's question: "How is crippling disability made meaningful?"

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman himself insists on "the limits of interpretation," proposing four validity criteria for
any reading of illness meaning — "correspondence to reality, coherence, usefulness in the context
of a person's problem, and aesthetic value" — and warns against "excessive speculation… about
hidden meanings." Because case narratives in the book are, by the author's note, anonymized and
sometimes composite (e.g., Mrs. Paget "an amalgam of the experiences and stories of several
chronic patients"), they are illustrative constructions, not verifiable single-patient records.
