# Class Struggle

## Definition as Coined

In the [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848),
[[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] open with the thesis that "the history of
all hitherto existing societies is the history of **class struggles**." Class struggle is the
antagonism between social classes defined by their opposed positions in production —
"oppressor and oppressed" — which "stood in constant opposition to one another, carried on an
uninterrupted, now hidden, now open fight." Its outcomes are epochal: each struggle ends
"either in a revolutionary re-constitution of society at large, or in the common ruin of the
contending classes." For the authors it is not one social process among many but the motor of
historical change itself, the empirical core of [[historical-materialism|historical
materialism]].

## Semantic History

The 1848 formulation universalizes struggle across all past epochs (freeman/slave,
patrician/plebeian, lord/serf, guild-master/journeyman). The authors' own later qualification
— that this holds for *written* history, primitive communal societies excepted — appears in
Engels's 1888 note, not in the body ingested here, and is flagged for that reason. The
*[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852) thickens the concept: struggle
is not only proletariat vs bourgeoisie but also among [[class-fraction|class fractions]]
(Legitimist land vs Orleanist capital), between parliamentary representatives and the mass
represented, and involving [[peasantry-as-class|peasantry]] and [[lumpenproletariat]] as
political bases — while surface slogans (“republicans vs royalists,” “Reaction”) “veil the
Class Struggle.” Across the twentieth century the concept broadened well beyond Marxism:
Weberian and stratification sociology retained class conflict while denying its primacy over
status and power, and the "conflict tradition" it founded now treats struggle over many axes
(not only class) as a general mechanism of social order and change. The distinction between a
class "in itself" (a shared economic position) and "for itself" (a self-conscious political
actor) — present in embryo in the *Manifesto*'s account of the proletariat's development from
"an incoherent mass" to a "self-conscious, independent movement" — became a central later
elaboration; the *Brumaire*’s peasantry is the classic case of a mass that is a class by
conditions but not by organization.

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*,
1959) reconstructs class struggle as **class-type group conflict** arising from authority
structures in [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated associations]],
not solely from property. He sustains Marx's link of structure, conflict groups, and change,
but rejects treating class struggle as the history of *all* past society, as always escalating
to violent revolution, and as determined by ownership alone. He splits [[intensity-and-violence-of-conflict|intensity
from violence]] and treats [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict|institutionalization]]
(collective bargaining, parties, industrial democracy) as reducing violence without abolishing
conflict — the principal postwar sociological answer to why Marx's revolutionary prognosis
failed in the West while class-like conflict persisted (see [[conflict-theory]]).

## Operationalizations

The *Manifesto* points to concrete, observable markers of the struggle's intensification: the
proletariat's progress from individual resistance and machine-breaking, through trade-union
"combinations," to legislative victories such as the English ten-hours' bill, and finally to a
centralized "national struggle between classes" — "every class struggle is a political
struggle." Later empirical traditions operationalize class conflict through strike rates,
union density, party formation, and voting, and debate whether such institutionalized conflict
confirms or refutes the revolutionary prognosis.

## Applications

Foundational to [[historical-materialism|historical materialism]] and to the entire conflict
tradition in sociology; applied in labour history, political sociology, world-systems
analysis, and materialist archaeology. It stands in explicit contrast to the integrative,
consensus-centred frame of [[social-integration|Durkheimian]] and functionalist sociology.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics charge that treating class struggle as *the* motor of history is reductive — that
national, religious, ethnic, and gender conflicts are not derivative of class, and that the
predicted sharpening into two camps did not occur (see [[bourgeoisie]], [[proletariat]]). The
concept's analytic value (conflict as an endemic feature of stratified societies) is widely
accepted even where its Marxist teleology is rejected. Dahrendorf's authority reconstruction
(position) preserves endemic conflict while relocating its structural basis and denying
revolutionary necessity — itself a major critique *within* the conflict tradition rather than
from outside it.
