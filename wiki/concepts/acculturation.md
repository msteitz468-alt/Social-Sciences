# Acculturation

An analytical concept for **culture change under intergroup contact**. In *[[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963|Culture Patterns & Processes]]* (Ch. 10), [[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]] treats acculturation as the major external-change process alongside ordinary diffusion, with subtypes by dominance, assimilation, voluntary adoption, and nativist reaction.

## Definition as Coined

Kroeber (attributed) aligned with the interwar anthropological definition (including the 1935 American Anthropological Association memorandum lineage he discusses): acculturation refers to phenomena that result when groups of individuals having different cultures come into **continuous first-hand contact**, with subsequent changes in the original culture patterns of either or both groups. He distinguished:

- **With dominance / assimilation** — subordinate group absorbs dominant culture (colonial and minority situations; American Indian cases as the interwar research bias he noted via Linton and others).
- **Without full assimilation** — persistent dual or hybrid patterns; minorities maintaining religion/identity under host polities (his illustrations: Jewish and Christian communities in Asia).
- **Voluntary or spontaneous acculturation** — adoption without conquest (Meiji Japan, selected trade frontiers, etc., as he framed them).
- **Nativism, revivals, messiahs** — reactive movements reconstituting or inventing tradition under pressure (Ghost Dance / related movements partially treated in the abridgement).

Related Ch. 10 topics: resistance to diffusion; **marginal cultures**; internal marginality; assimilation and technological **uniformization** (his closing discussion of modern mass culture and Soviet nationality policy as illustrations of homogeneity pressures).

## Semantic History

Nineteenth-century usage was looser; 1930s U.S. anthropology (Redfield, Linton, Herskovits, AAA memo) made "acculturation" a research program often focused on Native American–Euro-American contact. Kroeber's textbook chapter generalizes the concept into his process/change system. Later critiques: colonial power erased under "contact"; one-way assimilation bias; "nativism" pathologized resistance. Contemporary "globalization," "hybridity," and "decolonizing" vocabularies partly replace, partly continue, the same problems.

## Operationalizations

Contact continuity; direction of trait flow; dominance structure; retention vs. replacement; reactive movements. Kroeber stressed that interwar case samples were skewed toward dominated indigenous groups and underrepresented peer-civilization and voluntary cases.

## Applications

- Culture-change typology with internal processes (fashion, revolution, subsistence shifts) and diffusion.
- Bridge from Boasian historical particularism to applied/colonial anthropology of the mid-twentieth century.
- Contrast with [[stimulus-diffusion]] (idea without full form) and simple trait diffusion.

## Critiques and Limitations

Power asymmetry often under-theorized; "acculturation" can naturalize domination as mutual "contact." Nativism/messiah typology can exoticize political resistance. Kroeber's own late remarks on uniformity show awareness of monotony and intolerance costs of homogenization, still within a mid-century liberal-national frame.
