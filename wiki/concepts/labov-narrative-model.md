# Labov Narrative Model

## Definition as Coined

[[labov-william|William Labov]] and Joshua Waletzky (1967) proposed a general framework for oral narratives of personal experience; Labov developed it fully as chapter 9 of *[[labov-language-inner-city-1972|Language in the Inner City]]* (1972), “The Transformation of Experience in Narrative Syntax,” on materials from [[south-central-harlem-youth|south-central Harlem]] preadolescents, adolescents, and adults (with comparison materials from other urban/rural white and Black subjects). Waletzky “primarily carried out” the narrative analysis for that chapter (Labov’s acknowledgments).

proposed_by: [[labov-william]] · refines: [[narrative-analysis]] · applies: [[observers-paradox]]

**Definition.** Labov defined **narrative** as a method of recapitulating past experience by matching a verbal sequence of clauses to the sequence of events (inferred) that actually occurred. A **minimal narrative** is two temporally ordered clauses with **temporal juncture** (changing clause order would change the inferred event sequence). Only certain independent clauses are narrative clauses; subordinates and habituals (*used to*, *would*, general present) do not support narrative. **Free clauses** lack temporal juncture and can sit anywhere without altering sequence.

## Semantic History

Labov & Waletzky 1967 focused on clause placement of evaluation within a structural skeleton. The 1972 chapter **expanded** that scheme: evaluation is not only a section between complicating action and result but a secondary structure whose focus radiates as “waves of evaluation that penetrate the narrative”; analysis also turns to **internal clause syntax** and classes of evaluative devices. The model became a founding reference for structural narrative analysis in linguistics, sociolinguistics, and (with different commitments) narrative studies—parallel to, but methodologically distinct from, interpretive [[narrative-analysis]] in anthropology (e.g. Rosaldo’s process reading of emplotment).

Underlying questions mapped to parts (Labov): Abstract — “what was this about?”; Orientation — “who, when, what, where?”; Complicating action — “then what happened?”; Evaluation — “so what?”; Result — “what finally happened?”; the **coda** answers none of these five—it deflects further questions and returns speaker and hearer to the present.

## Operationalizations

### Six-part structure

A fully formed narrative may show:

1. **Abstract** — one or two clauses summarizing the whole story (includes evaluation as well as what happened; not a substitute for telling).
2. **Orientation** — time, place, persons, activity/situation; often free clauses and past progressives; may be delayed strategically.
3. **Complicating action** — the temporally ordered narrative core; **only this is essential** to recognize a narrative.
4. **Evaluation** — means indicating the **point** of the narrative (why told / “so what?”). Good narrators ward off “So what?” so the appropriate response is registering reportability (“He did?”). Reportability hierarchy: danger of death/injury always reportable; ordinary events lose reportability. Evaluative devices mark events as terrifying, weird, wild, crazy, amusing—not ordinary.
5. **Result / resolution** — what finally happened.
6. **Coda** — signals the narrative is finished; may bridge story-end to present; related to turn-taking end-of-sequence problems (Sacks).

Chainings and embeddings of these units are allowed.

### Evaluation types

- **External evaluation:** narrator stops and addresses the listener with the point—Labov found this common among **middle-class** narrators and in therapeutic interviews.
- **Embedding scale:** (1) quote self-sentiment at the moment; (2) quote self addressing another; (3) introduce a third person evaluating the antagonist (older skilled traditional working-class narrators). Middle-class speakers were reported as less likely to embed deeply.
- **Evaluative action:** show what people did rather than state the point.
- **Suspension of action:** concentrate attention before resolution.

### Four classes of evaluative elements

Narrative clauses show **basic syntax** (simple subjects, simple past, limited auxiliaries)—simpler than ordinary conversation. Departures from basic narrative syntax carry marked evaluative force:

| Class | Function (Labov) | Examples / notes |
|---|---|---|
| Intensifiers | Select and strengthen one event | Gestures + deictic *this/that*; expressive phonology (vowel lengthening); quantifiers (*all*); repetition (intensifies + suspends action); ritual utterances |
| Comparators | Compare what happened to what might have but did not | Negatives, futures, modals/quasimodals, questions, imperatives, or-clauses, comparatives/superlatives; negatives “express the defeat of an expectation” |
| Correlatives | Conjoin two actual events in one independent clause | Progressives, multiple *-ing* participles, double appositives; high complexity, largely beyond young narrators |
| Explicatives | Finite subordinate clauses | *while/though/since/because/that*; suspend action; simple causative most frequent |

### Elicitation methods

Labov elicited narratives of personal experience to obtain **casual speech** and mitigate the [[observers-paradox|observer’s paradox]]: deep involvement / partial reliving means the speaker is “no longer free to monitor his own speech as he normally does in face-to-face interviews.” Prototype **Danger of Death** question (“Were you ever in a situation where you were in serious danger of being killed…?”); fight section: “Were you ever in a fight with a guy bigger than you?” then pause and “What happened?” Questions are **Yes-No** commitments (subject says “yes,” then must justify with narrative); open prompts (“Can you tell me something amusing…”) were judged generally unsatisfactory. Topic schedule, not fixed questionnaire; mechanical overuse of danger-of-death yields “ludicrous results.” Narratives remain discourse directed to an outside observer—not free of interactive effect—but reduce monitoring.

### Age and comparison findings (Harlem corpus, attributed)

Labov reported that use of many **syntactic devices for evaluation** develops late, rising geometrically from preadolescents → adolescents → adults. On ten fight narratives per age group (Harlem PA 10–12, TA 13–16, Ad), after length correction, all four evaluative categories increased; intensifiers roughly 1:2:3; correlatives and explicatives roughly 1:4:8. Near-universal adult devices in the sample: **negatives and quantifiers**. Correlatives practically outside preadolescent range. Six Inwood white working-class adolescent fight narratives showed evaluative profiles closer to **Black preadolescents** than Black adolescents (small n; Labov declined a strong “advanced” claim but held Black speakers were “certainly not behind,” supporting his proposition that the highest concentration of verbal skills is in BEV culture). Vicarious experience narratives (e.g. TV retellings) often lacked orientation and evaluation; personal fight narratives (e.g. Norris W., age 11; Boot; Larry H. of the Jets) were highly evaluated. Fight narratives in BEV characteristically showed a **two-part structure**: cool verbal exchange displaying rights and mouth skill, then physical action where the fighter “goes crazy.”

Closing claim (attributed): Labov argued that “the black English vernacular is the vehicle of communication used by some of the most talented and effective speakers of the English language,” and that working-class vernacular has a distinct advantage for dramatic personal narrative over more educated external-evaluation styles when devices are concentrated and embedded.

## Applications

- Foundational structural model in linguistic narrative analysis (see [[narrative-analysis]] for the broader method family).
- Technique for obtaining vernacular / casual speech in variationist fieldwork ([[variationist-sociolinguistics]], [[observers-paradox]]).
- Comparative developmental measures of evaluative syntax across age and peer-group membership ([[south-central-harlem-youth]], [[black-english-vernacular]]).
- Exemplars analyzed in full: Boot’s rock-war narrative; Larry H.’s cigarette fight (Jets); John L.’s adult fight narrative; Norris W. self-aggrandizement fight story.

## Critiques and Limitations

The model is an **etic** structural scheme for oral personal-experience narrative in interview and peer settings; it does not claim universality of climax/reportability aesthetics across cultures (Rosaldo’s later critique of culturally specific narrative forms is complementary, not contradictory—different research objects). Only complicating action is definitionally essential; fully formed six-part narratives are analytic ideals, not obligatory folk templates. Evaluation “waves” revise but do not discard Labov & Waletzky 1967—later narrative studies sometimes reify a rigid six-box checklist Labov himself treated more flexibly. Age-development claims rest on small matched fight-narrative samples; Inwood white comparison n=6. The Danger of Death prompt is culture- and ethics-sensitive and can be over-applied. Discourse remains directed to an outside observer—casual speech, not unmonitored peer talk (group sessions solve that for other chapters). The model does not equate narrative skill with school literacy; Labov’s educational argument is that vernacular narrative competence is real and high, yet misrecognized by school evaluation.
