# Reembedding

## Definition as Coined

In *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]* (1990), [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] defined **reembedding** as processes by which [[faceless-commitments]] (trust in [[abstract-systems]]) are sustained or transformed by **facework** — trust relations in circumstances of co-presence. All [[disembedding]] mechanisms, he argued, interact with reembedded contexts of action that may either support or undermine them.

## Semantic History

Counterpart to disembedding within Giddens’s 1990 trust apparatus. Not to be confused with Polanyi’s embedded/disembedded economy, or with "re-embedding" as a normative policy slogan in later globalisation debates, without explicit attribution.

## Operationalizations

Theoretical. Illustrated via professional ethics among experts, access-point demeanour (doctors, cabin crew, judges), and colleagues’ face-to-face "updating" of trust (Giddens cites Boden on business/academic travel to "see the whites of the eyes").

## Applications

- Blocks a pure "world of strangers" reading of modernity: impersonality and intimacy are reworked together.
- Explains why abstract systems remain vulnerable and repairable at [[access-points]].
- Feeds the phenomenology of modernity (displacement ↔ reembedding as paired dialectics).

## Critiques and Limitations

Attributed program concept; thin on comparative ethnography in the 1990 text itself. Later intimacy writings (*Modernity and Self-Identity*, 1991) elaborate personal reembedding further.
