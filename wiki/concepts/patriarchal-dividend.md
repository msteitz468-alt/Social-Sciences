# Patriarchal Dividend

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s named term (in *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]*, 1995) for the collective benefit men draw from the subordination of women — the payoff that makes the mass of men complicit in the [[gender-order]] even when they are not its "front-line troops."

## Definition as Coined

Connell defines it in the discussion of complicit masculinity (attributed voice):

> "the majority of men gain from its hegemony, since they benefit from the patriarchal dividend, the advantage men in general gain from the overall subordination of women."

The dividend runs in "honour, prestige and the right to command," and also as "a material dividend." It is the mechanism that links the mass of ordinary, non-hegemonic men to the [[hegemonic-masculinity|hegemonic]] pattern: complicit masculinities are those "constructed in ways that realize the patriarchal dividend, without the tensions or risks of being the front-line troops of patriarchy" — men who may respect their partners, share housework, and eschew violence, yet still bank the advantage.

## Semantic History

**Prehistory in *Gender and Power* (1987).** The *term* "patriarchal dividend" is not used in 1987, but the mechanism is already load-bearing: Connell argues the major reason men collaborate in sustaining hegemonic images is that "most men benefit from the subordination of women, and hegemonic masculinity is the cultural expression of this ascendancy." Ch. 1 inventories material advantages (wages, capital control, office-holding, violence apparatuses). The 1995 coinage names that benefit structure.

The concept sharpens Connell's relational account of gender: because gender is a *structure* and not an aggregate of individual attitudes, a man can be personally egalitarian and still receive a structural dividend he did not individually claim. In the 2nd-edition Afterword Connell scales the concept up to a **"patriarchal dividend on a world scale,"** and, in his "draft balance sheet," argues men's *disadvantages* (exposure to violence, industrial injury, imprisonment) are "broadly speaking, the conditions of the advantages," not offsetting costs — an explicit rejection of "competing victims" accounting. Class and race, he adds, mean the men who receive most of the dividend and the men who pay its costs are largely not the same men.

## Operationalizations

Connell gives the "material dividend" concrete indices for rich capitalist countries (attributed, c. 1990s figures):
- men's average incomes "approximately double women's average incomes" (in the Afterword, US men's median income given as 197% of women's, 1990);
- concentration of capital — of 55 US billion-dollar fortunes in 1992, only 5 were mainly in women's hands, nearly all inherited from men;
- office-holding — men roughly ten times more likely than women to hold parliamentary office (averaged across countries);
- time-use — men and women work roughly equal total hours per year, but the paid/unpaid split diverges sharply.

These are `associated_with` indicators of the structural advantage, not a single validated measure.

## Applications

- Explains **complicity** as the modal relation of men to patriarchy in Connell's typology, and thereby why gender-justice politics "must be a source of disunity among men" — reform runs against a shared material interest (see [[gender-relations-theory]]).
- In the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history study]], marginal working-class men are described as having "lost most of the patriarchal dividend" through class position — showing the dividend is unequally distributed among men.
- Widely adopted across the sociology of gender as shorthand for the collective, structural payoff of gender inequality.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept treats "men in general" as a category with a shared interest; critics of Connell's framework (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]) press on whether that category holds once class, race, and sexuality are taken seriously — a tension Connell himself acknowledges when he insists the men who benefit and the men who bear the costs are often different men, while declining to abandon the analytic category "men."
