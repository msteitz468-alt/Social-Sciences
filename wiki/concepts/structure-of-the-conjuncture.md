# Structure of the Conjuncture

## Definition as Coined

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities*, 1981; *Islands of History*, 1985) uses **"the structure of the conjuncture"** to name the emergent, situational regularities of practice in an event — a "pragmatics" driven by actors' interests that "defeated both intention and convention." As glossed by [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (*[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, ch. 7), it refers to "the particular meanings, accidents, and causal forces that shape events": the local, contingent, semantic and ritual conditions under which inherited cultural categories are applied to novel circumstances and thereby revalued.

The concept rests on Sahlins's "possible theory of history" — that "the transformation of a culture is a mode of its reproduction," because "in action in the world … the cultural categories acquire new functional values." Because "the world is under no obligation to conform to the logic by which some people conceive of it," categorical application carries both "objective risk" (the world may not fit) and "subjective risk" (categories are inflected by actors' interests). Sahlins's paradigm case is Captain Cook's arrival and death in Hawaii; see [[sahlins-marshall]] and [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]].

## The Coining (*Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities*, 1981)

*(Attributed — now primary via *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors]]*.)* The term first appears in the 1981 monograph's chapter on the Kauai incident: "Practice, rather, has its own dynamics — a 'structure of the conjuncture' — which meaningfully defines the persons and the objects that are parties to it. And these contextual values, if unlike the definitions culturally presupposed, have the capacity then of working back on the conventional values." The book's governing question — "The great challenge to an historical anthropology is not merely to know how events are ordered by culture, but how, in that process, the culture is reordered. How does the reproduction of a structure become its transformation?" — is answered by reversing the cliché (after Jean Pouillon): "plus c'est la même chose, plus ça change."

The 1981 Conclusion supplies the concept's semiotic machinery. **Interest vs. sense**: "'Interest' and 'sense' (or 'meaning') are two sides of the same thing, the sign, as related respectively to persons and to other signs. Yet my interest in something is not the same as its sense" — illustrated by Saussure's five-franc piece (its social exchange-value vs. what *I* do with it). Interest is explicitly not individualist: "Interest is a social fact, and the individual is a social being." In action, signs acquire new conceptual values by two mechanisms: "(1) insofar as they are placed in novel relationships with objects in the referential process; and, (2) insofar as they are placed in novel relationships with other signs in the instrumental process." Reference is never a copy of the world — "Reference is always a symbolic reference... the process of redefinition is motivated in the logic of their cultural categories" (the Hawaiians' conclusion that the British were not gods because they ate with women "was no simple sequitur to the empirical fact of commensality" but followed from the tabu logic of gender). Whether a subjective revaluation has structural effect depends on the culture-as-constituted: the tropes available, the institutional freedom to improvise, and the actor's hierarchical position ("a privileged position... may amplify the consequences of an individual's action" — chiefs vs. commoners). The structure of the conjuncture is a *sui generis* level: "a working-out of the categories of being and things as guided by interests and fitted to contexts," in which "relationships themselves are put at issue, not just this or that cultural category" — "This... is what makes transformation really radical." The book closes: "the historical process unfolds as a continuous and reciprocal movement between the practice of the structure and the structure of the practice."

## Sahlins's Restatement (*Islands of History*, 1985)

*(Attributed — now primary via *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*.)* Sahlins defines the term as "a situational set of relations, crystallized from the operative cultural categories and actors' interests" — "the practical realization of the cultural categories in a specific historical context, as expressed in the interested action of the historic agents, including the microsociology of their interaction." He distinguishes his usage explicitly from **Braudel**, for whom *conjoncture* denotes intermediate-duration cycles between the *longue durée* and the event: Sahlins's use is "more literal" (French *conjoncture*, "a situation resulting from a meeting of circumstances") and "definitely evenementielle," allowing "more than Braudel does for the structuration of the situation." He aligns it with [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s [[duality-of-structure|structuration]] (a preexisting structure plus unintended and intended consequences). The double "**risk of the categories in action**" — objective and subjective — is its engine, and it operates wherever inherited categories meet contingency: in the Cook conjuncture (priests reading the returned Cook as Lono, the king reading an out-of-season god as a rival), in Hone Heke's flagstaff war (see [[mytho-praxis]], [[maori]]), and generally as the "third term" mediating structure and [[historical-event|event]] — "no event sans system." Against the antinomy of structure and history he holds that "all structure or system is, phenomenally, evenemential... Event is the empirical form of system."

## Semantic History

Sahlins developed the term to reconcile Lévi-Straussian structuralism ([[levi-strauss-claude]]) with historical change, adopting [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s [[duality-of-structure|duality of structure]] to make structure both the medium and outcome of conduct. It was central to the anthropological rehabilitation of the event against the Annales dismissal of *histoire événementielle*.

## Sewell's Uptake and Reversal

Sewell adopts the term into his own [[historical-event|theory of the event]] (naming the proposition "historical events are shaped by particular conditions"), but **reverses** it: a structure of the conjuncture, he argues, itself arises from a prior **"conjuncture of structures"** — the coming-together of previously disjoint or differently connected structures. This follows from his insistence, against Sahlins, that structures are **multiple** (not singular per society) and comprise **[[transposable-schemas|resources as well as schemas]]** (not culture alone). He applies it to the Bastille: the semantic ambiguity of *le peuple*, the Bastille's prior symbolic reputation, and the bloody theatricality of the assault together constituted the conjuncture that let crowd violence be articulated to popular sovereignty and produce the modern concept of "revolution."

## Operationalizations / Applications

Identified case by case, never in advance: the analyst reconstructs which particular meanings, accidents, and forces made a given rupture cascade into a durable [[historical-event|transformation of structures]]. Applications in the ingested source: Cook in Hawaii (Sahlins); the taking of the Bastille and the dockworkers' 1849–51 radicalization (Sewell).

## Critiques and Limitations

Sewell's principal reservations (attributed) target Sahlins's singular and purely cultural conception of structure, and whether Sahlins adequately credits actors' reflective creativity — recorded on [[sahlins-marshall]]. The interpretation of the Cook case itself is disputed (see [[sahlins-obeyesekere-cook-debate]]).
