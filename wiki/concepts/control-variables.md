# Control Variables

## Definition as Coined

In quantitative social research, **control variables** are attributes introduced so that the association between a test independent variable and a dependent variable can be read as a **net** or quasi-experimental effect. [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] (*[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*, 1985, esp. chs. 2, 6, 10) argued (attributed) that the control approach is widely treated as both **desirable and possible**, yet often rests on assumptions nonexperimental social data do not meet.

The practice, as he described it, says in effect: *here is what the influence of each variable would be, had results been found under random assignment.* Social knowledge, he countered, often gives no grounds for that counterfactual.

## Semantic History

Control logic descends from experimental design and from Mill's canons via survey analysis (Lazarsfeld, Rosenberg, partial correlation, path models). Mills's [[abstracted-empiricism]] attacked survey craft from the outside as method-led thinness; Lieberson attacked **from inside** quantitative sociology: the control simulation of experiment is often **logically invalid**, not merely theoretically thin. Related later languages include covariate adjustment, selection on observables, and "all else equal" regression — each inherits the same identification burden.

## Operationalizations

Lieberson's critical theses (all attributed):

1. **Unmeasured selectivity within controls** (ch. 2): the reason one controls (populations differ) is also the reason controls fail — selective processes operate *inside* control categories. Experimental analogue holds only if assignment within control combinations is chance, or residual sorting has no bearing on the outcome.
2. **Controls can worsen inference**: incomplete controls are not always closer to truth; under some S− selectivity patterns they understate or reverse true effects (Charts 2.1–2.2).
3. **Orthodox vs. nontraditional thesis** (ch. 6): the field treats controls as routinely necessary for causal isolation; Lieberson's counter is that controls are **only safe when they are unnecessary** in a strong sense — i.e., when selectivity and contamination problems are already solved — and are otherwise ritual.
4. **Do not control until you know why the control is associated with the test variable**: understand the social process that produces nonrandom association of control with independent variable; otherwise controlling is premature.
5. **Four conditions for legitimate controls** (ch. 10, attributed summary): restrictive circumstances under which statistical control can stand in for experimental control; outside those, "descriptive pretence" (reporting nets as if causal) is the residual legitimate use.
6. **Neutral-variable test** for residual selectivity (ch. 11): within levels of the test variable, the joint association of controls with a neutral outcome should match if unmeasured selection is exhausted.

Worked illustrations (design examples, not vault findings): private vs. public schools; teenage parenthood (Card); race and income; contextual effects and Hauser's cautions; Coleman educational reports.

## Applications

- Everyday multivariate sociology and demography.
- Policy evaluation from observational data.
- Path models and structural equation practice that partial out covariates to claim "effects."

Lieberson's constructive reorientation (chs. 10–11): shift from control simulation toward **outcome analysis** (why is Y what it is?), hierarchies of causal forces, [[superficial-vs-basic-causes]], and [[transformational-causal-principle|transformational principles]] that render input-side controls uninformative.

## Critiques and Limitations

Defenders of modern causal inference argue that clearer identification assumptions, design-based strategies, and sensitivity analysis address many 1985-era abuses without abandoning adjustment. Lieberson anticipated the endless-regress objection (controls require further controls ad nauseam) and replied that some situations simply make the control procedure **inappropriate**, and that theory should be rebuilt to need fewer mock-experimental controls.

The pro-control position Lieberson attacks is stated programmatically by [[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994|King, Keohane & Verba]] (1994), who argue (attributed) that controlling for confounds is central to non-experimental causal inference — while adding their own cautions: including *irrelevant* controls costs efficiency (proportional to the control's correlation with the main explanatory variable) but not bias; one must **not** control for variables that are consequences of the explanatory variable (post-treatment bias; see [[omitted-variable-bias]]); and matching can seduce the analyst into anomalous "special" cases that carry a new [[omitted-variable-bias|omitted-variable]] confound. KKV also reject the "most similar" vs. "most different" systems framing as inattentive to "similar in relation to what," proposing leverage over the causal hypothesis as the criterion instead. His position is filed on [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]] (internal quantitative critique) and relates to [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]] without being identical to Mills. See also [[selectivity]], [[comparative-method]], *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*.
