# Functional Differentiation

## Definition as Coined

**Functional differentiation** is, in [[luhmann-niklas|Luhmann]]'s [[social-systems-theory|systems
theory]], the form of societal differentiation characteristic of **modernity**: society
reproduces itself across autonomous **function systems** — economy, law, science, politics, art,
religion, education — each operating by its own **binary code** (e.g. legal/illegal,
true/false, payment/non-payment) and each "closed specifically with respect to" that code,
"afford[ing] indifference to all other schematisms." Luhmann sets it alongside two other basic
schemata: **segmentary** differentiation (into like units) and **stratificatory**
differentiation (into ranked strata, a centre/periphery order).

## Semantic History

The idea of differentiation runs from Spencer and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s
[[durkheim-division-of-labor-study|division of labour]] through [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]. Luhmann's
distinctive claim is about the **modern transition** from a **stratified ("monocontextural")**
to a **functionally differentiated ("polycontextural")** society, roughly completed by the end
of the eighteenth century. Its decisive consequence is the **loss of an apex or centre**: no
function system can "control, dominate, or substitute for any other," and none can represent
society as a whole. Correlatively, society's older "other-reference" (religion, transcendence, a
hierarchical world order) had to be replaced by each system's "accompanying **[[self-reference]]**"
— the economy's self-reference condenses into **money** (its elemental operation being
*payment*), the political system's into the **state** (its self-description, not an empirical
referent), the education system's into ***Bildung*** (c. 1800). Because "there is no societal
subsystem for perceiving environmental interdependencies," a rational self-description of
society as a whole becomes, for Luhmann, nearly impossible — "every statement of fact on this
level becomes 'ideological.'"

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct; a typology of societal forms and a historical thesis about
modernity. Luhmann distinguishes it sharply from **hierarchy** (a special case of
differentiation, not identical to it).

## Applications

- The macro-sociological payoff of [[social-systems-theory]] and the frame for Luhmann's later
  studies of individual function systems.
- Grounds his account of modern latency, of the impossibility of a "good society"
  representative of the whole, and of system rationality having no apex.
- Reframes the classical differentiation tradition (Durkheim, Parsons) in code-and-closure
  terms.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics ask whether "functionally differentiated with no centre" understates real hierarchies
of power and money across systems (a charge close to Habermas's — see [[luhmann-habermas-debate]]).
The neat one-code-per-system picture is also contested empirically. In *Social Systems* itself
functional differentiation is treated relatively briefly and developed fully only in Luhmann's
later society trilogy. Attributed to Luhmann throughout.
