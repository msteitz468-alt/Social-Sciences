# Joint Action

## Definition as Coined

In [[blumer-herbert|Blumer]]'s formulation (*[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]*, 1969), **joint action** is the societal organization of conduct that arises when different acts of diverse participants are articulated or linked. A joint action has a distinctive character in its own right — identifiable as a marriage, a trading transaction, a dinner party, a debate, a game, a day's work in a factory, and so on — even though it is built from individual lines of action.

Blumer attributes the underlying social theory of the act and interaction to [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], but the term and its systematic use in SI methodology are Blumer's organizing vocabulary.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Blumer 1969 (and related essays) | Core unit of human group life: ongoing fitting-together of lines of action |
| Mead (as reconstructed by Blumer) | Social act / cooperative act as precondition of mind and self — precursor vocabulary |
| Later SI / organizational ethnography | Coordination, collective action, institutional performance as joint action |

## Operationalizations

Not a survey scale. Recovered by naturalistic study of how participants indicate, interpret, and align acts — [[exploration-and-inspection]]. Contrasts with treating "group" solely as structure of positions or as aggregate of attitude scores.

## Applications

- Explains how institutions and large-scale organization are *enacted* in interaction without dissolving them into pure individual psychology (Blumer's answer to the astructural charge, in attributed form).
- Frames society as a vast network of ongoing joint actions rather than a static system of roles alone.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Critics argue "joint action" still under-specifies power, coercion, and unequal capacity to define situations.
- Risk of circular description (whatever people do together is "joint action") unless analytic elements are inspected carefully.
- Distinct from rational-choice "collective action" and from purely structural "social action" (Weber) — do not equate silently.
