# Closed Corporate Peasant Community

## Definition as Coined

[[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]] developed the **closed corporate peasant community** in papers of 1955 ("Types of Latin American Peasantry") and 1957 ("Closed Corporate Peasant Communities in Mesoamerica and Central Java"), and integrates it into the coalition typology of *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 3 and Selected References). In the 1966 matrix it is the **manystranded, polyadic, horizontal** coalition: a community that is **corporate** (joint rights and liabilities; membership boundary) and **closed** (restricted entry; defense of land and people against outsiders). Members typically cannot freely alienate community land shares to outsiders. Heavy [[ceremonial-fund]] obligations (cargo/fiesta) level wealth and symbolize solidarity. Wolf treats it as **adaptive organization** under predatory states and markets — not timeless folk essence.

Related leveling/joint-access forms Wolf invokes: Russian *mir*, Near Eastern *musha'a*, cargo systems. Selective pressures may favor leveling (mir, musha'a, cargo) or forced selection of heirs/disinherited under hierarchical domain.

## Semantic History

One of mid-century economic anthropology's most cited community types; debated as over-generalized, romanticized, or historically specific to colonial/post-colonial highland settings. Distinct from open, stratified, or purely dyadic rural settlements. Wolf (1966) links Mexican Revolution success less to land reform alone than to **breaking open** Indian corporate communities and hooking state machinery to village organizers.

## Operationalizations

- Membership restriction and endogamy / residence rules.
- Communal land or regulated access; bar on alienating shares to outsiders.
- Cargo / fiesta systems as redistribution and boundary maintenance.
- Dual stance: closed to outside encroachment, internally leveling.
- Often under prebendary/colonial domain conditions that make community-level rent collection and defense rational.

## Applications

- Mesoamerican and Javanese comparative cases (Wolf).
- Coalition chapter: corporate community as one peasant organizational form among patron-client, descent groups, associations.
- Explains ceremonial intensity as political-economic defense (links [[ceremonial-fund]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Historical contingency vs universal type — later literature contests closure as stage or strategy.
- Gender and internal elite capture under-specified in short primer.
- Primary papers (1955, 1957) not yet separately ingested; densify from full articles when acquired.
