# Forms of Integration (reciprocity, redistribution, householding, exchange)

## Definition as Coined

[[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s comparative typology (Chapter 4 of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*) of the principles by which an economy is instituted and held together. He identifies principles "not primarily associated with economics" that order production and distribution in non-market societies:

- **Reciprocity** — mutual give-and-take between symmetrically placed groups (kin, moieties, Kula partners). "Today's giving will be recompensed by tomorrow's taking."
- **Redistribution** — goods collected to a center and distributed out from it (headman, chief, temple, despot). Illustrated by the Kwakiutl potlatch, the Bergdama band, and the storehouse economies of Hammurabi's Babylonia and New Kingdom Egypt.
- **Householding** — production for one's own group's use (Greek *oikonomia*, "the etymon of the word 'economy'"), institutionalized in closed self-sufficient groups (patriarchal family, manor, village).
- **Exchange** — barter/truck via the market pattern; the fourth principle, but structurally different from the others (see below).

Each form depends on a **supporting institutional pattern**: reciprocity on **symmetry** (dual/paired social organization), redistribution on **centricity** (a collecting-and-redistributing center), householding on **autarchy** (self-sufficient closure), and exchange on the **market** pattern.

## Semantic History

The typology descends from the economic anthropology Polanyi drew on — Malinowski (Kula, "the whole tribal life is permeated by a constant give and take"), Thurnwald (who "discovered that... the practice of storage and redistribution was of the most general application"), Firth, Lowie — and was systematized in Polanyi's later "The Economy as Instituted Process" (1957) as the canonical statement of the **[[substantivism|substantivist]]** framework. It fed directly into Marshall Sahlins's typology of reciprocities (*Stone Age Economics*, 1972).

## Operationalizations

Identified by the institutional pattern present, not measured cardinally: is there a symmetrical dual structure (reciprocity), a redistributive center (redistribution), a self-sufficient closed unit (householding), or a price-making market (exchange)? Polanyi stresses the market pattern is "more specific" than the other three — it is a single-function institution generated by the motive of barter, whereas symmetry, centricity, and autarchy are general traits attaching to institutions that serve many functions.

## Applications

The evidentiary heart of Polanyi's claim that gain-through-exchange was "no more than incidental to economic life" before the nineteenth century, and thus that the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] is a historical exception (see [[embeddedness]], [[substantivism]]). Overlaps the anthropology of the [[gift-exchange|gift]] (Mauss) and Whyte's ethnographic [[reciprocal-obligation]]. Polanyi's summary: "all economic systems known to us up to the end of feudalism in Western Europe were organized either on the principles of reciprocity or redistribution, or householding, or some combination of the three."

## Critiques and Limitations

- Formalists argue the typology understates the presence of maximizing/exchange behavior *within* reciprocal and redistributive systems (see [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]).
- The neat mapping of each form to one institutional pattern is idealized; real economies mix forms (Polanyi concedes combinations).
- The ethnographic cases are secondhand and mid-century; several (potlatch, Kula) have since been reinterpreted.
