# Body Symbolism

Analytic focus of [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] across *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (esp. external boundaries) and *[[douglas-natural-symbols-1970|Natural Symbols]]* (esp. "The two bodies"). Program: [[grid-group-analysis]] / [[purity-pollution-theory]].

## Definition as Coined

Douglas treated the human body as a medium that **symbolizes society**: social structure and social control are expressed in rules of bodily control, purity, trance, and the treatment of margins and waste. In *Natural Symbols* she distinguishes **two bodies** — the physical individual and the social body — and argues that natural symbols of the body always serve social intentions ([[natural-symbols]]).

## Semantic History

- 1966 (*Purity and Danger*): body models threatened social boundaries; orifices and margins.
- 1970 (*Natural Symbols*): full comparative program linking body style to [[grid-and-group]]; hierarchy / sect / individualism as body images.
- Later embodiment literature (Csordas, etc.) reopens the body as lived experience — related research field, not identical to Douglas's social-mapping claim.

## Operationalizations

Correlate strictness of bodily control with strength of social control; read trance, spirit possession, and waste symbolism against group boundaries; avoid universal body-meaning checklists.

## Applications

Nilotic trance; sectarian closed-body imagery; anti-ritualist sincerity and spirit/matter splits; pollution rules. See [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of functional circularity (body rules "express" society because society is read from body rules); gender and individual agency under-specified; universalist body-schema psychology disagrees on naturalness.
