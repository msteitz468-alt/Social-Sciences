# Truth–utility distinction (Pareto)

## Definition as Coined

[[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]] insisted that the **logico-experimental “truth”** of a theory and its **social utility** (or any other utility) are **different questions**. They “are not only not one and the same but may, and often do, stand in flat contradiction” (§843; first major inductive encounter §219; Ch IV §§368, 401, 445, 568–579; Ch XI).

An experimentally absurd part of a doctrine does not make the whole doctrine socially worthless; a socially useful belief need not be experimentally true.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Usage |
|---|---|
| Ch II §219 | First full encounter: absurd experimental part ≠ whole doctrine absurd; non-LE ≠ socially detrimental |
| Ch III–IV | Rationalizations and theories transcending experience; free speech may help truth without helping order |
| Ch VI §928 | Divine-origin myths ≈ universal-suffrage dogma from LE standpoint; utility of either not *a priori* |
| Ch XI | Imaginary ideals as imperfect means to real ends; false beliefs may be socially useful (§§1768–72, 1876–96) |
| Later | Precursor talk for “functionalism of ideology,” Strauss, elite theory of myths — partial receptions |

## Operationalizations

Three derivation problems (§1399): (1) logical form; (2) experimental reality; (3) social utility — must not be collapsed. Sociology of utility indices in Ch XII (utility of/for individual or community; ophelimity FOR a community) builds quantitative language for the utility side without equating it to experimental truth.

## Applications

- Method rule of [[paretian-sociology]] and [[logico-experimental-method]]: science studies uniformities; practice may need non-true beliefs.
- Critique of reformers who assume demolishing a theology demolishes the social function of its residues.
- Elite analysis: Class II beliefs may stabilize obedience even when LE-false ([[group-persistence]], [[lions-and-foxes]]).
- Warning against using scientific prestige to smuggle non-experimental principles (Ch V pseudo-sciences).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Can be read as cynical license for noble lies — Pareto frames it as analytic separation, not a prescription; his tone is often anti-humanitarian.
- “Utility” is multi-dimensional (for whom? at what horizon?) — Ch XII tries to disaggregate.
- Risk of insulating any residue from criticism by invoking utility without evidence.
