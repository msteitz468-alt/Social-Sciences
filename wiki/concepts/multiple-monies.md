# Multiple Monies

## Definition as Coined

[[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]]'s central thesis in *[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]* (building on AJS 1989 "Special Monies"): there is **no single, uniform, generalized money** in modern society. People create and maintain **multiple monies** — socially differentiated currencies earmarked for particular relations, occasions, and moral worlds — "much as they create distinctive languages for different social contexts."

People respond with anger, shock, or ridicule to the **misuse** of monies for the wrong circumstances (e.g., offering a thousand-dollar bill for a newspaper, tipping a restaurant's owner).

**Social monies**, for Zelizer, are defined by **uses and meanings**, not physical form alone.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Classical social theory (as Zelizer reconstructs) | One market money; only quantity differs (Simmel's "qualitatively communistic character") |
| Anthropological special-purpose money ([[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]] lineage) | Restricted spheres of exchange in nonindustrial societies — Zelizer extends the insight to modern legal tender |
| Zelizer AJS 1989 / book 1994 | Multiple monies / special monies as modern social fact |
| Dodd foreword 2017 | Three readings of earmarking from meaning-marking to production of new currencies; **monetary pluralism** as contemporary context |
| Afterword 2017 | "Special monies" subtitle misread as anomalies; critique applies to "real" money too; three components of money (accounting systems; media; practices) |

## Operationalizations

- **[[earmarking]] practices** — labels, separate purses/accounts, restricted convertibility, timing and quantity norms.
- **Moral sanctions** for misuse.
- **Institutional distinctions** — food stamps, scrip, tips vs wages, allowances vs gifts, in-kind vs cash relief, Christmas Clubs, industrial burial insurance.
- Later: digital/virtual monies and local currencies as further multiplication (Dodd; Zelizer afterword).

## Applications

| Domain (Zelizer 1994) | Examples (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Domestic | Dole, allowance, pin money, housekeeping money, children's pocket money, joint account |
| Gift | Tips, bonuses, remittances, Christmas money, courtship tokens, ritual *gelt* |
| Welfare | Grocery orders, mothers' pensions, therapeutic cash with budgets, food stamps |
| Moral quality | "Dirty" / "bad" money vs "honest" money (prostitution earnings; robbery proceeds; blood money for burial) |

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested by utilitarian economics and by classical sociological monetization narratives that treat qualitative distinctions as residual atavisms.
- Risk of reading multiple monies as only "soft" intimate exceptions — Zelizer explicitly rejects that confinement.
- 1994 evidence is U.S. historical (c. 1870–1930) and domain-selected; generality of the claim outruns any single case set.
