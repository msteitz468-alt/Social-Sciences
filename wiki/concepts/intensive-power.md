# Intensive Power

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]*, 1986, Ch. 1): **intensive power** is the ability to organize tightly and command a high level of mobilization or commitment from participants, whether the area and numbers covered are great or small. Paired with [[extensive-power]]. Intensive control may cover much of life or push participants far (even to death) without loss of compliance.

> "Intensive power refers to the ability to organize tightly and command a high level of mobilization or commitment from the participants, whether the area and numbers covered are great or small."

Cross-classified with [[authoritative-power]]/[[diffused-power]]: intensive+authoritative ≈ army high command over troops; intensive+diffused ≈ general strike (Fig. 1.1).

## Semantic History

Used throughout Vol. I for local feudal, village, production, and civic solidarities versus extensive ecumenes and empires. European "intensive phase" (Ch. 12) is a major narrative application: early feudal dynamic primarily intensive because extensive pacification was already supplied by Christendom. Factory/industrial intensity largely reserved for Vol. II.

## Operationalizations

Mobilization depth: discipline, morale, surveillance, everyday regulation, fiscal-manpower extraction — not only territorial size. Contrast extensive empires (high territory, low everyday penetration) with small intensive polities (Greek polis, manor, village).

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ch. 1** — Pike phalanx as military reorganization and morale (not pure military determinism); army command as paradigmatic intensive+authoritative power.
- **Greece (Ch. 7)** — Democratic polis as most intensive democratic collective power over small space before industrial capitalism (Mann); achievements of intensity not extensiveness; Greek citizen less able to hide from the state than Persian subject.
- **Rome** — Intensive Greek-style citizenship fused with extensive territorial citizenship; high wartime mobilization rates among citizens (Mann citing Hopkins).
- **Medieval Europe (Ch. 12)** — Village and manor as intensive local cellular institutions; yield-ratio and population growth technology intensive, product of local autonomy legitimized by extensive Christendom; Protestantism later adds intensive penetration that helps turn political boundaries into fuller "societies" (Mann Ch. 14).
- **Empires** — Persian and many imperial forms: vast [[extensive-power]], notably inferior intensive power.

## Critiques and Limitations

Hard to quantify commitment; ideal-typical cells blur in practice. Keep as Mann's comparative dimension.
