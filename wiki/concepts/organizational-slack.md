# Organizational Slack

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed lineage as Hirschman presents it in *[[hirschman-exit-voice-loyalty-1970|Exit, Voice, and Loyalty]]* 1970.)* **Organizational slack** names surplus or unused efficiency that buffers firms and organizations. Cyert and March (1963) introduced the term in *A Behavioral Theory of the Firm*; Simon's "satisfactory" rather than maximum profits, Leibenstein's X-efficiency, and related work form the "slack economy" backdrop. Hirschman's **radical step**: slack is not only present in given amounts but **continuously generated** by entropy characteristic of surplus-producing human societies — "There's a slacker born every minute." Firms are permanently and randomly subject to decline; [[exit-option|exit]] and [[voice-option|voice]] are recuperation forces reclaiming performance. See [[exit-voice-loyalty]].

## Semantic History

| Source | Move (as Hirschman reports / extends) |
|---|---|
| Perfect-competition taut economy | Every firm barely getting by; one false step undoes it — paradox given social surplus |
| Utopian progress-as-discipline (Steuart, Nieto Arteta) | Hope that progress bars backsliding — "cruelly disappointed" |
| Simon; Cyert & March; Becker; Leibenstein; Postan | Elements of a theory of the slack economy |
| Hirschman *Strategy of Economic Development* (1958) | Development = calling forth hidden/badly utilized resources; pressure squeezes slack |
| Hirschman EVL (1970) | Continuous generation of slack; dual reactions (extirpate vs rationalize); recuperation via exit/voice |

Hirschman also noted political analogues: citizen apathy as political "reserves"; Dahl on unused political resources; Almond & Verba blending of active and deferential citizens — mixture of alert and inert, parallel to exit's alert/inert customers.

## Operationalizations

Gap between actual and potential performance; quality decline without cost reduction (random lapse); soft-budget public enterprise; political unused influence reserves. Not independently measured in EVL — doctrinal and conceptual background for recuperation analysis.

## Applications

Firms under imperfect competition; public bureaucracies; political systems needing reserves for crisis voice; any organization with performance latitude above subsistence.

## Critiques and Limitations

Slack is hard to measure independently of the decline narrative. EVL uses it mainly as ontological premise (continuous entropy) rather than a formal measurement program. Distinct from unemployment/depression "slack" and from externality debates Hirschman carefully separates.
