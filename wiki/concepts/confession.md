# Confession (as a Technique of Power-Knowledge)

## Definition as Coined

In [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s analytic use (attributed), **confession** is the West's central procedure for producing the truth of the self and of sex — a ritual of discourse in which the one who speaks is also the subject of the statement, unfolding "within a power relationship" with an authority who "requires the confession, prescribes and appreciates it, and intervenes in order to judge, punish, forgive, console, and reconcile." It is the "nucleus" of the [[scientia-sexualis]]: "the confession of the flesh" continually expanded from the Counter-Reformation pastoral into medicine, psychiatry, and psychoanalysis. This is a **migrated** concept — a religious practice turned analytic category (its two lives, penitential and clinical, are both recorded here).

## Semantic History

Foucault traces confession from the Lateran Council's obligatory periodic confession and the post-Tridentine intensification of self-examination (the demand to tell not just acts but "all the insinuations of the flesh"), through its "plurisecular" generalization: "The seventeenth century made it into a rule for everyone." By the nineteenth century the same imperative to "tell everything" appears in secular and literary registers (he cites Sade and the anonymous *My Secret Life*) and, above all, in the clinic — refashioned by "a clinical codification of the inducement to speak," causality, latency, interpretation, and medicalization (the five mechanisms; see [[scientia-sexualis]]).

## Operationalizations — power in the listener

The concept's analytic payoff is a claim about [[power-knowledge]]: in confession, "the agency of domination does not reside in the one who speaks (for it is he who is constrained), but in the one who listens and says nothing." The confessor/analyst "was the master of truth. His was a hermeneutic function." Confession thus produces "men's subjection: their constitution as subjects in both senses of the word." Foucault stresses the West made confession feel like *liberation* — "we demand that sex speak the truth... and we demand that it tell us our truth" — masking its role as a technique of power.

## Applications

Confession links religious practice, the interview, the clinical case, and psychoanalytic technique into one genealogical line; it underwrites [[scientia-sexualis]] and the account of modern selfhood organized around sex. Beyond Foucault the concept informs studies of therapy culture, the interview method, and "confessional" media.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Over-generalization** (attributed): treating the medical interview, the police interrogation, and the analytic session as one "confessional" form risks flattening real institutional differences.
- **Historical continuity** (attributed): the pastoral-to-clinic lineage is contested by historians. Recorded as Foucault's thesis.
