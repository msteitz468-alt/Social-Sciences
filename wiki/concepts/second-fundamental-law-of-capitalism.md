# Second Fundamental Law of Capitalism

Long-run accumulation relation that, in [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s framework, explains why the capital/income ratio rises when growth slows. Core of [[piketty-capital-dynamics]] in *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*.

## Definition as Coined

Piketty’s **second fundamental law**: in the long run the capital/income ratio **β** is related to the net savings rate **s** and the growth rate **g** by

**β = s / g**

Example: if s = 12% and g = 2%, then β = 600% (six years of national income). A country that saves a lot and grows slowly accumulates an enormous capital stock relative to income; in a quasi-stagnant society, wealth accumulated in the past acquires disproportionate importance.

Unlike the [[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]] (α = r × β), which is an accounting identity always true by construction, β = s/g is a **dynamic equilibrium**: the economy tends toward it if savings rate is s and growth is g, but that state is never perfectly realized in practice. It is asymptotic—several decades of stable s and g are needed; country-level β dynamics are long-run phenomena, unlike rapid individual fortunes.

## Semantic History

Steady-state capital-accumulation logic from growth theory (related to Harrod–Domar / Solow-type stock-flow identities), renamed and centered in an inequality narrative. Piketty reads Marx’s “principle of infinite accumulation” / falling rate of profit as the limit case where g ≈ 0: if s > 0 and g near zero, β → infinity and either r → 0 or α = r × β devours all national income—only structural productivity and population growth balance accumulation. The second law thus reframes classical and Marxist accumulation debates as comparative-static implications of **g**.

## Operationalizations

- **s**: savings **net of capital depreciation**. Annual depreciation in developed economies ~10–15% of national income absorbs nearly half of gross savings (typically 25–30% of NI), leaving net savings ~10–15% of NI. Private saving = direct household saving + firm retained earnings (retained earnings can be up to half of private savings; ignoring them understates s and over-attributes wealth growth to pure stock-price rise).
- **g**: overall growth of **national income** = per-capita growth + population growth. Europe with near-zero demographic growth and total g ~1.5–2% can expect β of 6–8 years (for s ~10–12%); US with demographic growth ~1% and total g ~2.5–3% only 3–4 years.
- **Sensitivity**: with s = 12%, g = 1.5% → β = 8 years; g = 1% → β = 12 years (twice the capital intensity of g = 2%).
- **Assumptions**: (1) applies to **accumulable** capital (pure natural-resource value independent of past investment can raise β without savings); (2) asset prices evolve on average with consumer prices (short-run price swings often dominate volume effects); (3) independent of *why* residents or government save—β = s/g is the only stable ratio for given s and g.
- **Cross-country 1970–2010 private net saving (% NI)** as Piketty tabulates: US 7.7; Japan 14.6; Germany 12.2; France 11.1; Britain 7.3; Italy 15.0; Canada 12.1; Australia 9.9. Japan emblematic: s ~15%, g barely above 2% → capital stock of six to seven years of NI.

## Applications

Explains the rebound of [[capital-income-ratio|β]] under slowing growth after the Trente Glorieuses and the structural private-capital comeback Piketty calls new [[patrimonial-capitalism]]. For Britain, France, Germany, and the US over 1910–2010 or 1870–2010, he argues the global evolution of the capital/income ratio is well explained by β = s/g: Europe’s higher long-run β fits saving and especially growth differences; 1910–1950 decline fits low national savings and wartime destruction; faster rise 1980–2010 than 1950–1980 fits decreased growth between periods. The mid-century low was *lower* than pure accumulation logic predicts because real estate and stock prices were depressed (rent control, regulation, political climate)—price catch-up after 1950, accelerating after 1980. International implication: slight differences in g or s can generate large capital imbalances and foreign investment. Pairs with [[r-greater-than-g]] for twenty-first-century projections and with golden-rule discussion (Phelps: r = g implies α = s; historically and prospectively r > g so α > s).

## Critiques and Limitations

Steady-state assumptions; endogeneity of s (and of g); role of capital gains vs saving volume; pure natural capital and land; and whether asset-price catch-up is complete are debated ([[piketty-capital-debate]]). Britain 1970–2010 savings flows under-predict the private-wealth rise relative to other countries. The law does not explain short-term shocks (world wars, 1929, bubbles) but indicates the potential long-run equilibrium once shocks dissipate. Critics of the broader program often accept the accounting tendency while disputing its inequality implications once progressive taxation, human capital, and public wealth are fully modeled.
