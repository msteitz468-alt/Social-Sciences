# Spatial Polarization

Pattern in which growth dynamics concentrate high-income professional and profit centers and expand low-wage, informal, or downgraded work within the same metropolitan space — producing sharper class and spatial divides rather than a uniform middle-class upgrading. Central to Part Three of *[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]*.

## Definition as Coined

Sassen links spatial and socioeconomic inequality to the occupational structure of advanced [[producer-services|producer services]], high-income gentrification's demand for low-wage personal services, and the "downgrading" of manufacturing (declining unionization, sweatshops, homework).

## Semantic History

Related to dual-city and divided-city theses; distinct from pure racial [[residential-segregation]] accounts (though race and nationality enter Sassen's labor-market chapter) and from Wilson-type [[urban-underclass]] frames focused on joblessness and social isolation — Sassen emphasizes growth-linked low-wage employment as well as inequality of earnings.

## Operationalizations

Earnings distributions; occupational shares at high and low ends; social geography of gentrification and residual manufacturing; informal labor markets.

## Applications

New York, London, Tokyo comparisons in *The Global City* chs. 8–9; broader global-cities inequality literature.

## Critiques and Limitations

Magnitude and necessity of polarization contested; some cities may show different trajectories; measurement depends on household vs individual income and on spatial scale.
