# Externalization, Objectivation, and Internalization

## Definition as Coined

In *[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|The Social Construction of Reality]]* (1966), [[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] analyze society as an ongoing **dialectical process** of three moments:

1. **Externalization** — human beings ongoingly pour out into the world in physical and mental activity; social order exists only as product of this activity.
2. **Objectivation** — the products of that activity attain a reality that confronts the producers as a facticity external to them (institutions, language, the social world “as if nature”).
3. **Internalization** — the objectivated world is retrojected into consciousness in the course of [[primary-socialization|socialization]], so that the objective structure becomes a subjective structure of consciousness.

Their formula for the three characterizations of the social world:

> Society is a human product. Society is an objective reality. Man is a social product.

They argue that analysis leaving out any moment is distortive; only with transmission to a new generation (internalization as effectuated in socialization) does the fundamental social dialectic appear in its totality (Part Two; restated from the internalization side in Part Three).

## Semantic History

The triad synthesizes, without naming in the main text, Marxian activity/world production, Durkheimian facticity of society, and Meadian internalization of the social process (Notes and Introduction). Related earlier vocabularies: Marx’s objectification / alienation; Durkheim’s exteriority and constraint of [[social-fact|social facts]]; Mead’s [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]] and [[generalized-other]]. Berger and Luckmann’s contribution is to make the three moments a single continuous dialectic of the social construction of reality, not three separate theories.

**Objectivation** (their spelling in the English text) is the moment at which human products confront producers as objective reality; **[[reification]]** is an extreme of objectivation in which authorship is forgotten.

## Operationalizations

Theoretical analytic moments, not measurement constructs. Empirical programs using the framework typically track: production of institutional order (externalization/habitualization); facticity and control of institutions (objectivation); induction via socialization and conversation (internalization).

## Applications

- Genetic account of [[institutionalization]] and [[legitimation]]
- Link between objective sociology and [[social-psychology|social psychology]] (Part Three bridge)
- Structure–agency interface: people produce a world that then produces them ([[structure-vs-agency-debate]])
- Corrective against pure voluntarism and pure reifying objectivism (Conclusion)

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics of constructionism often charge soft relativism or underplaying material constraint; Berger and Luckmann insist the constructed world has hard facticity (cannot be wished away) and that reification analysis is itself a critical tool. The triad is programmatic theory, not a tested causal model — empirical leverage depends on later research traditions.
