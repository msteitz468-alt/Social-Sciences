# Complex Society

## Definition as Coined

**Complex society** is a comparative cover term for societies with pronounced social differentiation, specialized economic and political roles, and hierarchical decision-making — including [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] and early [[pristine-state|states]]/[[civilization|civilizations]]. [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]] ch. 7 treat explaining how such organizations evolved as a core research problem of world prehistory, surveying typologies (Service; Fried) and multi-causal models rather than a single definition.

Primary ranking variables (W&O synthesis as of 2007):

1. Degree of differential access to wealth, power, and prestige  
2. Extent to which that differential access is **inherited** vs earned  
3. Occupational specialization and how integrated those occupations are in the economy  
4. Degree of centralization of political power in a government  

W&O identify the change from band-level **functional redundancy** (each group holds nearly all survival skills) to **functional interdependence** as at the heart of origins of complex societies.

## Semantic History

Supplanted older singular “[[civilization]]” ladders for many archaeologists while retaining evolutionary comparison. Ethnographic categories of Elman Service and Morton Fried — devised mainly for extant/recent cultures — have been widely applied to prehistoric societies (useful abstractions that fit no case precisely and can mislead). W&O (with McGuire) argue band/tribe/chiefdom/state labels are **static descriptive types**, poorly suited to analyzing origins; underlying variability is continuous and multidimensional.

Later refinements as of the 2007 textbook:

- **Heterarchy** (Crumley): elements unranked or rankable in multiple ways; power “counterpoised” rather than simply nested hierarchy.
- **Corporate vs network strategies** (Feinman et al.): network = centralized leadership, restricted wealth, ostentatious prestige; corporate = wider wealth access, shared authority, communal ritual/public works — without requiring full egalitarianism.
- **Collapse** reframed (Kenoyer et al.): often decentralization/localization, not mass extinction of people/ideas.
- Post-processual and comparative critiques attack the premise of a science of history from cross-cultural comparison (W&O note sustained attack; Shanks & Tilley cited in notes).

Reflexivity (W&O): confusing cultural complexity with cultural **worth** is an old error used to justify colonialism; classification by stratification, size, and energy/information exchange is a research tactic, not ultimate ranking of piety or justice.

## Operationalizations

Archaeological proxies (W&O ch. 7 survey):

- **Settlement hierarchy** and relative placement; hexagonal central-place patterns on plains; shift from resource-tracking to trade/frontier/administrative siting.
- **Monumental architecture** and energy diversion (White energy-capture metric retained as interest despite criticism).
- **Mortuary differentiation** — juveniles with rich goods often read as ascribed status (Binford tradition; Carr caveats noted).
- **[[craft-specialization]]** and functional differentiation; workshop districts; administered economies.
- **Writing, seals, ration systems** in some cases (e.g., [[uruk]]).
- **State operational definition (Wright & Johnson):** ≥ three levels of specialized decision-making hierarchy.

**Urbanism ≠ state** (Trigger via W&O): Mesopotamia formed out of cities; Egypt remained largely non-urban for much of its history; trajectories plural.

**Mississippian application:** intensive maize, trade, large populations, elite mortuary sacrifice — yet most archaeologists do not class them as states because nearly all people remained full-time maize farmers with few specialists (W&O).

## Applications

Organizes textbook case chapters (as of W&O 2007):

| Region / case | Notes in textbook framing |
|---|---|
| SW Asia / [[uruk]] | Primary state formation; full civilization checklist by end of Uruk; multi-causal (irrigation not primary) |
| Egypt | Challenges Wittfogel/warfare/urbanism monocausality; slow urbanism |
| Indus / [[mohenjo-daro]] | Urban literate culture; weak wealth differentials; state vs chiefdom dispute |
| China / Shang | North China after ~3000 BCE |
| Mesoamerica / [[teotihuacan]], Olmec, Maya | Multiple developmental cores; ecological constraints (no draft animals) |
| Andes / Inka | Maritime + agricultural base; circumscription/warfare debated |
| Europe | Neolithic–Bronze Age chiefdoms; Stonehenge; “Mafia model” (Lewthwaite) |
| North America / [[cahokia]], [[chaco-canyon]] | Complexity ceilings short of pristine states |

Parallel independent evolution of the six earliest civilizations (productive agriculture with elite surplus appropriation, national cults, monumental architecture, bureaucratic hierarchies) is presented as evidence against purely random or intention-only histories (W&O ch. 16). Complex societies have existed only five or six millennia yet nearly replaced simpler forms (W&O).

Causal-model survey (ch. 7): leisure-time rejected; techno-ecology; population pressure; Wittfogel irrigation; Carneiro circumscription/warfare; Marxian class models; Harris; evolutionary archaeology; Trigger holistic; Brumfiel–Earle political models — none treated as sole sufficient cause.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Package thinking and ethnocentric trait lists; equifinality of archaeological signatures.
- Service/Fried types misapplied; continuous multidimensional variation.
- Heterarchy and dual-processual alternatives challenge unilinear ladders.
- Comparative science-of-history premise contested.
- Unit of selection shifts with interdependence (societies as groups vs individuals) — theoretical, not demonstrated law.
- North American / temperate ceilings: absence of draft animals, developed metallurgy, extensive warm arid alluvial plains as constraining factors (W&O SW/NA conclusion) — ecological argument, not moral ranking.
- Ideology and social relations legitimizing hierarchy remain incompletely understood even where environment, demography, and agriculture correlate (W&O SW Asia conclusion).

### Yoffee 2005 (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] notes “complex society” as archaeological **shorthand** for multi-group societies with centralizing institutions — sometimes chosen to *avoid* the chiefdom/state identification problem. He then “complicates complexity”: a major trend in earliest states is the [[evolution-of-simplicity]] (legibility projects), so complexity measures alone mislead. Source: [[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005]].

### Connah 2016 (attributed)

[[connah-graham|Connah]] (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]*) operationalizes social complexity mainly through **urbanism and state formation**, while stressing their non-identity and African diversity of forms. Common denominators (Ch. 12): productive land + people, local exchange, elite hierarchy, indigenous then world-religion ideology, craft/metallurgy, population pressures, and external trade as **autocatalytic intensifier** (Diamond via Connah). [[heterarchy]] is noted as an alternative horizontal model (McIntosh; Middle Niger) but cautioned as often resting on missing hierarchy evidence. Package is comparative archaeology across Africa, not a trait-list checklist of “civilization.”
