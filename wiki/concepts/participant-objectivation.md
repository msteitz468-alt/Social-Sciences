# Participant Objectivation

Bourdieu's term (French *objectivation participante*) for the reflexive operation of **objectifying the objectifying subject** — turning the tools of social science back onto the analyst's own position, interests, and categories of thought. The methodological heart of [[reflexive-sociology|reflexive sociology]] as Bourdieu practised it, and the explicit program of *[[bourdieu-homo-academicus-1984|Homo Academicus]]* (1984), where he objectifies the very academic world he inhabits.

## Definition as Coined

Bourdieu argues (attributed, 1984) that scientific objectivity requires objectifying not only the object but the **social conditions of possibility of the objectifying gaze itself**: the analyst's position in the [[academic-field|academic field]], the leisure/distance of the scholastic point of view ([[scholastic-disposition|skhole]]), and the specific interests that inhabit apparent disinterestedness. Distinct from mere introspection or confessional reflexivity — the object is the *social* subject (position, trajectory, capital), not the private psyche.

## Semantic History

Roots in the "objectify the objectifying subject" demand of *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline]]* (1972) and *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]* (1980); named and enacted at book length in *Homo Academicus* (1984); systematized later in *Science of Science and Reflexivity* (2001) and the 2003 Huxley Lecture "Participant Objectivation." Only partly continuous with [[reflexive-sociology|Gouldner's reflexive sociology]] and with the ethnomethodological and feminist-standpoint senses of "reflexivity" — record convergences, not identity.

## Operationalizations

A stance, not an index. In *Homo Academicus* it is realized by (a) constructing the field relationally so the analyst appears as one position among others; (b) using the [[correspondence-analysis|correspondence analysis]] to break with the pre-notions the analyst shares with the objectified peers; (c) foregrounding the "scandal" of studying one's own world and the risks of both proximity and denunciation.

## Applications

- Sociology of intellectuals and of science ([[academic-field]]).
- Fieldwork reflexivity in anthropology (contrast with [[reflexive-fieldwork]]).
- The distinction between the **empirical individual** and the **epistemic individual** (the agent as bundle of pertinent field properties), used to justify anonymization and relational construction.

## Critiques and Limitations

Charged with infinite regress (who objectifies the objectifier?) and with a residual claim to a privileged reflexive vantage that exempts Bourdieu's own science. Defenders reply that participant objectivation is collective and never complete, a regulative demand rather than a guarantee. See the critique history on [[bourdieu-homo-academicus-study]].
