# Relationism

## Definition as Coined

[[mannheim-karl|Mannheim]]’s name for the epistemic stance of the [[sociology-of-knowledge]]: knowledge claims of a certain class are **relational** — formulable only in terms of the **perspective** of a given social-historical situation — without that relatedness amounting to arbitrary **relativism**.

From *[[mannheim-ideology-and-utopia-1936|Ideology and Utopia]]* Part V:

> Relationism does not signify that there are no criteria of rightness and wrongness in a discussion. It does insist, however, that it lies in the nature of certain assertions that they cannot be formulated absolutely, but only in terms of the perspective of a given situation.

He argues relationism becomes relativism only when measured against an older static ideal of eternal, unperspectivistic truths independent of the observer — an “alien” ideal of absolute truth. For historical-political knowledge, he treats that ideal as the wrong model (the “2×2=4” epistemology generalized from formal/natural-science paradigms).

**Particularization** is the associated critical operation: redefining the scope and limits of the perspective implicit in given assertions — moving from absolute claim to restricted scope — between pure sociological description and full determination of truth-value.

## Semantic History

- Built from historicist *Standortsgebundenheit* and the generalization of the total conception of [[ideology]] to include one’s own standpoint (special → general total ideology).
- Offered as remedy for “vague, ill-considered, and sterile” relativism that, Mannheim says, follows when social conditioning of thought is seen but not scientifically incorporated.
- English “relationism” as a technical term is largely Mannheimian reception vocabulary; later philosophy of science and standpoint theory develop related problems under other labels.
- [[social-construction-of-reality|Berger & Luckmann]] (1966) relocate the field: study what *passes for* knowledge and how reality is socially constructed, bracketing ultimate validity — a third path that still attracts relativism charges (see [[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

- Definition of **perspective**: manner of viewing the object; categories, thought-models, level of abstraction, presence/absence of concepts, counter-concepts — attributable by criteria to epoch/situation/group.
- Imputation of styles of thought to social locations (class and other groups; class often most significant but not sole).
- Analysis of “talking past one another” and heterogeneous discussion (Scheler’s “epoch of equalization” as context).
- Peasant-boy paradigm: acquisition of perspective through detachment paths (urbanization, education, clash of worlds).

## Applications

- Classical SoK empirical program and Mannheim’s political sociology of synthesis ([[free-floating-intelligentsia]]).
- Methodological reflexivity in historical sociology: genesis relevant to validity when it affects content/form (*Sinngenesis* vs mere *Faktizitätsgenesis*).
- Critique of natural-science model as sole epistemological template for social knowledge.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Relativism charge:** opponents deny that relationism escapes self-undermining: if all historical-political knowledge is perspectival, so is the sociology of knowledge.
- **Objectivity routes** Mannheim sketches (translation between perspectives; dynamic synthesis) remain programmatic and contested.
- **Rejection of “truth as such”** as dualistic fiction is philosophically high-cost; many sociologists adopted methods without the epistemology.
- Particularization’s “third answer” on validity is easy to misread as either mere description or full truth-adjudication.
