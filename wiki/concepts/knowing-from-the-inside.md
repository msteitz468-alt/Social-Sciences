# Knowing from the Inside

## Definition as Coined

**Knowing from the inside** is [[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s name (in
*[[ingold-making-2013|Making]]*, 2013) for the mode of inquiry he advocates across the
"4 A's" (anthropology, archaeology, art, architecture): to know a thing or a world by
**joining with it in its making** — studying *with* and learning *from* materials,
practices, and people — rather than by standing outside and making a study *of* them.
It recasts research as a practice of skilled, attentive participation, continuous with
the [[dwelling-perspective]] and [[enskilment]] (attributed, Ingold).

## Semantic History

*(Enriched from [[ingold-making-2013|Making]] during integration.)* The formulation
sharpens Ingold's long-standing contrast between knowledge "grown" through engaged
practice and knowledge "assembled" from detached observation; it reframes anthropology
itself as an "art of inquiry" conducted through [[correspondence]] rather than
description.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct — a methodological orientation. In practice it aligns
research with apprenticeship and making: learning a craft, following a material, walking
a landscape, as ways of generating knowledge.

## Applications

- **"Anthropology is not ethnography"** (Ch. 9's thesis): Ingold sharply distinguishes
  anthropology — a forward-looking practice of *studying with* people to imagine ways of
  living — from ethnography understood as the descriptive documentation *of* them; he
  coins **"anthropography"** for the writing that does the latter.
- **Across the "4 A's"**: the same participatory, practice-based knowing is proposed for
  archaeology (excavation as a "line of correspondence," after Edgeworth) and
  architecture as much as for anthropology.
- **Pedagogy**: Ingold's own Aberdeen "4 As" course — making a handaxe, building,
  kite-flying, string-making, handwriting exercises — is offered as knowing-from-the-
  inside in practice. He grounds the stance in Gibson's "education of attention,"
  Bateson's "deutero-learning," and Polanyi's tacit knowledge (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics ask how "knowing from the inside" yields communicable, testable knowledge, and
whether it collapses the analytic distance that comparison and critique require.
