# Societal Selection

## Definition as Coined

**Societal selection** is [[sumner-william-graham|Sumner]]'s extension of the selection
principle from organisms to social forms, developed chiefly in Chapter V of
*[[sumner-folkways-1906|Folkways]]* ("Societal Selection"). The idea is that the
[[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]] selects not only among individuals but
among **customs, groups, and institutions**: [[folkways]] that serve their purpose
persist and spread; maladapted ones produce pain, loss, and — at the group level —
extinction or subjugation. Selection thus operates on the mores themselves, and taboos
*"act selectively, and thus affect the course of civilization"* (§35).

Chapter V ("Societal Selection") states the mechanism directly: *"the mores are... an
engine of social selection"* (§170). The mores sort individuals by conformity — those who
*"submit and consent"* may attain social success, dissenters *"may be trodden under
foot"* — and Sumner explicitly folds infanticide (Ch. VII) and slavery (Ch. VI) into the
same selective framework. His most fully worked example is a **four-stage model of
religious persecution** (§233, §269): (1) the church taught the wickedness of dissent;
(2) the masses drew the deduction and enacted it by *lynching* — *"the mob lynched
heretics long before the church began to persecute"*; (3) the hierarchy took over and
formalized it into the Inquisition, *"selection by murder"*; (4) the apparatus was
*"vulgarized by the masses again"* into witch-hunting, which Sumner notes was
**not** selective. The model is a striking (if characteristically bottom-up) claim that
mass mores, not elite doctrine, drive persecution.

Sumner applied a strong, and now notorious, version of the principle to persons and
"races." His class analysis (§§47–51), built on Galton's and Ammon's
statistical grading of "societal value," treats the distribution of human worth as a
curve of probable error and speculates — in Galton's eugenic register —
about the "capital value" of children of different classes. His §55 claim that *"masses of
men who are approximately equal are in time exterminated or enslaved"* applies the
selection logic to whole populations. These passages are **attributed to Sumner and
flagged**: they are the point where societal selection shades into
[[social-darwinism|social Darwinism]]'s eugenic and racial hierarchy (see Critiques, and
[[sumner-william-graham]] Reflexivity).

## Semantic History

"Societal/social selection" belonged to the confident selectionist sociology of
1880–1910 (Spencer, Sumner, Gumplowicz, Ammon). It fell with [[social-darwinism]]: the
naturalistic fallacy at its core, the eugenics it licensed, and the Boasian critique of
racial hierarchy discredited it as a master principle by mid-century. A chastened,
non-normative descendant survives in cultural-evolutionary theory (selection among
cultural variants / memetic and dual-inheritance models) and in organizational ecology
(selection among organizational forms) — but these disavow the ranking of human groups
that Sumner's version carried.

## Operationalizations

None in Sumner beyond the Galton/Ammon class curve (§§47–51), which measures "societal
value" only by proxy and which Sumner himself admitted *"has no unit... we could never
verify it statistically"* (§49). The absence of a defined unit of selection is the
concept's central technical weakness.

## Applications

- Explaining the persistence of adaptive [[folkways]] and the elimination of maladaptive
  ones (§§5, 35).
- Grounding a group-level account of conquest and subordination (§55) — the most
  criticized application.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Unit-of-selection incoherence.** The "selected" entity slides among custom, individual,
  group, and race with no consistent unit, so the mechanism explains everything and
  predicts little.
- **Naturalistic fallacy and eugenic politics.** Reading conquest, inequality, and poverty
  as the verdict of a beneficial selection converts description into justification — the
  standard indictment of [[social-darwinism]].
- **Racial hierarchy.** The application to "races" and "nature peoples" reflects 1906
  evolutionary racism, not evidence (see [[sumner-folkways-1906]] reliability notes).
