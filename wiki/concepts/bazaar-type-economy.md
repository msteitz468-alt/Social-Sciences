# Bazaar-type economy

Analytical contrast term introduced by [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] in *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963|Peddlers and Princes]]* (1963) for the organizational logic of the traditional Javanese *pasar* (and the regional petty-trading pattern centered on it), opposed to the **[[firm-type-economy|firm-type economy]]** of shops and factories.

## Definition as Coined

Geertz defined a **bazaar economy** as one in which “the total flow of commerce is fragmented into a very great number of unrelated person-to-person transactions,” in contrast to a Western **firm-centered** economy where trade and industry run through impersonally defined institutions organizing specialized occupations toward productive or distributive ends *(attributed, Geertz 1963)*.

The *pasar* (etymology via Persian *bāzār* / Arabic) is “at once an economic institution and a way of life” — not only the central market square but “the whole pattern of small-scale peddling and processing activity” of the region *(attributed)*.

Geertz analyzes it from three angles:
1. **Flow of goods and services** — unbulky, portable goods; high turnover; low volume per sale; circular wholesale chains before final consumption; processing fused into the trading network.
2. **Regulatory mechanisms** — (a) **sliding price** / bargaining as information system and skill arena; competition framed as buyer–seller, not primarily seller–seller; (b) **credit balances** as primary integrative mechanism (revolving, multi-party, trading on others’ capital); (c) **risk fractionation** via composite trading coalitions rather than genuine corporate organization.
3. **Social and cultural system** — interstitial historical origins (long-distance trade, Islamization); *wong dagang* (“trader” as foreigner/wanderer); absence of guilds; hyperspecialization absorbing labor; impersonal, highly specific commercial ties.

## Semantic History

- **1963 coining (this source).** Middle-range dual typology for pretake-off urban commerce in Indonesia; not identical to Boeke’s colonial dualism, though it reworks dual-economy imagery around **organization** rather than culture-bound “economic mentality” alone.
- **Relation to Weber.** Geertz critiques Weber’s image of bazaar “unscrupulousness”; the problem, he argues, is lack of **organization**, not lack of business orientation *(attributed)*.
- **Later reception.** The bazaar/firm contrast entered economic anthropology and development ethnography as a vocabulary for petty commodity trade vs continuous enterprise; later **[[informal-economy]]** literature overlaps but is not the same construct (informality is often legal-regulatory; bazaar-type is organizational).

## Operationalizations

Primary ethnography: [[javanese-modjokuto]] market (1952–54), with Alice Dewey’s *Peasant Marketing in Java* cited as companion description. Emic markers of the continuum: *pasar* vs *toko* (“modern” / *moderen* vs *kolot*).

## Applications

- Core case: Modjokuto in *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-study|Peddlers and Princes]]* — progress as hesitant movement **from bazaar-type toward firm-type**.
- Comparative foil for [[balinese-tabanan]]’s aristocratic firm path ([[modjokuto-tabanan-economic-modernization]]).
- Entrepreneurial type: bazaar-bred peddler as *homo economicus* of shrewdness, liquidity passion, and short-run opportunism — strengths for individual initiative, weaknesses for sustained firm-scale coordination *(attributed)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Dual typology can flatten intermediate forms (Geertz’s own continuum of stores and putting-out systems shows this).
- Chinese commercial control of the “jump” from peddling to large merchandising is structurally central but secondary to the reformist-Muslim innovator narrative.
- Period-bound pretake-off / take-off framing ([[modernization-theory]]); post-1959 Indonesian political economy outside the ethnography by design.
- “Bazaar” is etic analytical English; *pasar* is the emic institutional core.
