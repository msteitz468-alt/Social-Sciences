# Sacralization (sacrificial)

## Definition as Coined

In Hubert & Mauss's *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice]]* (1898), **sacralization** names one of the two general directions of the sacrificial scheme: when the [[sacrifier]]'s initial religious state is **neutral** (no prior sacred charge), the rite's function is to **impart sacred character** — notably in initiation and ordination sacrifices. Entry rites are elaborate; exit may be reduced. After immolation, sacred force often flows from victim toward the sacrifier (food communion, *ida*-type portions, anointing).

Paired opposite: [[desacralization]] (including expiation), which removes charge. Hubert and Mauss insist both directions use the **same scheme elements**, differently weighted — not two species of rite with different origins.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Hubert & Mauss 1898 | General function of the scheme: induce sanctity |
| Evans-Pritchard *Nuer Religion* 1956 | Maps **collective/confirmatory** sacrifice (initiation, marriage, death, age-sets) onto sacralization — "bringing Spirit to man" — while subordinating it to piacular analysis for understanding Nuer religion |
| Broader sociology | "Sacralization" also used outside sacrifice (secular → sacred in cultural sociology); do not collapse those senses with this technical sacrificial use |

## Operationalizations

Code rites by initial state of the subject (neutral vs already charged), direction of force after immolation (toward sacrifier vs away into elimination), and relative development of entry vs exit sequences.

## Applications

- Initiation, ordination, covenant-confirmation, and many first-fruit / building rites when the aim is to consecrate persons or installations.
- Agrarian dual-object rites: one pole sacralizes consumers of the harvest while another desacralizes the crop (Hubert & Mauss Ch. IV).
- Limit case: [[sacrifice]] of the god — sacralization pushed until the victim is personified divinity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary sacralization/desacralization is an analytic convenience; Hubert and Mauss themselves show pure and impure sacred can reverse treatment. Evans-Pritchard's Nuer application treats collective sacralizing rites as festally rich but religiously secondary — a case-driven reweighting, not a refutation of the typology.
