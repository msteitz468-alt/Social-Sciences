## Definition as Coined

In Chapter 2 of *[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]* ("Studies of the routine grounds of everyday activities"), Garfinkel argued that societal members treat the familiar scenes of everyday life as "natural facts of life" that are simultaneously moral facts: matters are not simply so, but so because it is morally right or wrong that they are so. He wrote: "For members not only are matters so about familiar scenes, but they are so because it is morally right or wrong that they are so." Members, Garfinkel held, use "background expectancies" as a scheme of interpretation that renders appearances recognizable as familiar events, while being largely unable to articulate what those expectancies consist of: "The member of the society uses background expectancies as a scheme of interpretation... Demonstrably he is responsive to this background... When we ask him about them he has little or nothing to say." He characterized these expectancies as ordinarily "seen but unnoticed" — operative in every recognition of a familiar scene, yet invisible as an object of members' own attention.

## Semantic History

Garfinkel credited Alfred Schutz ([[schutz-alfred]]) as the near-sole precedent for describing these seen-but-unnoticed background expectancies, which Schutz had called the "attitude of daily life" and the "world known in common and taken for granted." Garfinkel elaborated Schutz's phenomenological account into an explicit list, in the medical-school breaching study, of eleven constitutive background expectancies of "the world known in common" — including the objective necessity of determinations, undoubted correspondence of appearance and object, reciprocal affectability, socially standardized meaning-production, temporal identity of determinations across occasions, a shared scheme of interpretation ("What Anyone Knows"), and the interchangeability or reciprocity of standpoints. Garfinkel's methodological innovation beyond Schutz was procedural: rather than describing background expectancies analytically, he proposed making the taken-for-granted visible by deliberately breaching it — his stated strategy was to "start with familiar scenes and ask what can be done to make trouble," multiplying senseless features to produce bewilderment, anxiety, shame, or indignation, thereby revealing how the expectancy ordinarily sustains recognizable order. He was explicit that these were "demonstrations," not experiments, describing them (via Herbert Spiegelberg's phrase) as "aids to a sluggish imagination."

## Operationalizations

Background expectancies are not directly measured; they are inferred from the affective and interactional disturbance produced when a breaching demonstration violates them. Garfinkel's own quantitative illustration — flagged by him as suggestive rather than a genuine finding — was a correlation between subjects' initial subscription to the "natural facts" of premedical life and their anxiety under a fabricated, contradicting record in the medical-school interview study: r = .026 before incongruous material was introduced, rising to r = .751 afterward, among 28 subjects, of whom 25 were "taken in" by the fabrication. Garfinkel cautioned: "Under no circumstances should they be considered as findings," underscoring that the demonstrations were designed to reveal the structure of background expectancies qualitatively, not to establish a quantitative law.

## Applications

Garfinkel deployed the concept across a series of breaching demonstrations ([[breaching-experiment]]): the "please clarify what you mean" conversations (23 students, 25 encounters), in which insistence on literal clarification of ordinary remarks provoked hostility and bewilderment; students adopting a "boarder" stance in their own homes, both as passive observation (33 cases) and active role-play (49 cases), which produced family members' stupefaction and demands to restore normal appearances; distrust conversations (35 accounts) in which feigned suspicion of hidden motives produced anger and, in one extended case, lasting relational damage; the standard-price bargaining study, used to argue against Talcott Parsons's ([[parsons-talcott]]) "institutionalized one price rule" as a fully internalized norm; the ticktacktoe demonstration (247 trials), in which an inexplicably moved mark was frequently read as a meaningful, ironic gesture; and the close-face-approach study (79 accounts), in which physical breach of ordinary interactional distance provoked attributions of sexual intent. In each case, disturbance served as evidence for the expectancy's ordinary, unremarked operation.

## Critiques and Limitations

Garfinkel's own demonstrations were small-scale, drawn from student assignments rather than controlled sampling, and he repeatedly cautioned against treating quantitative patterns within them (such as the .026/.751 correlation) as genuine findings — a limitation later commentators have sometimes elided by citing his demonstrations as though they were rigorous experiments. The reliance on students as both experimenters and reporters raises questions about selection and reporting bias in which reactions were recorded. Because background expectancies are identified only through their breach, the concept offers little independent means of specifying their content prior to a demonstration — a feature Garfinkel treated as consistent with their being "seen but unnoticed" rather than as a methodological weakness. The concept is closely tied to [[practical-action]] and to [[reflexivity-of-accounts]], since members' accounts of a breached scene are themselves reflexively tied to the occasion of the breach.
