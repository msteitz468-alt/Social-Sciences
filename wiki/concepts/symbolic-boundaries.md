# Symbolic Boundaries

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lamont-money-morals-manners-1992|Money, Morals, and Manners]]* (1992), [[lamont-michele|Michèle Lamont]] defines **symbolic boundaries** as the types of lines individuals draw when they categorize people — criteria distinguishing worthy from less worthy persons, “their sort of folks” from “the sort they don’t much like.” Operationally she recovers them from labels and implicit purity criteria in descriptions of people interviewees do not want to associate with, people to whom they feel superior or inferior, and people who arouse hostility, indifference, or sympathy.

She organizes evaluation into three analytical standards:

| Type | Centered on (Lamont 1992) | Illustrative signals |
|---|---|---|
| **Moral** | Moral character | Honesty, work ethic, integrity, consideration; US *phony* / climber / low-morals; French *salaud* / intellectual honesty |
| **Socioeconomic** | Success and position | Money, power, professional standing, *patrimoine*, fame |
| **Cultural** | Cultivation and intellect | Refinement, intelligence, cosmopolitanism, brilliance, expertise, high culture, self-actualization |

These are types whose **relative salience** varies — not a single hierarchy fixed by high culture.

## Semantic History

Longer lineage (as Lamont engages it): Durkheimian classification and purity; Weberian status honor; **Barth on ethnic boundaries** (now ingested: *[[barth-ethnic-groups-boundaries-1969|Ethnic Groups and Boundaries]]* 1969 — [[ethnic-boundary]], [[ethnic-boundary-theory]], [[diacritical-features]]); Kai Erikson on boundaries existing when repeatedly defended; Zerubavel on cognitive distinctions. Barth’s organizational focus (boundary over cultural “stuff”; ascription) is a major anthropological ancestor of later boundary sociology; Lamont generalizes beyond ethnicity to everyday moral/cultural/socioeconomic evaluation. [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]’s [[distinction]] / [[cultural-capital]] supplies the principal foil: Lamont keeps the insight that cultural style can reproduce class, but refuses to predefine high culture as the main axis or to treat difference as automatically hierarchical. Thomas Gieryn’s science-studies “boundary work” is adjacent terminology for demarcation; Lamont generalizes boundary processes to everyday status evaluation (see [[boundary-work]]).

## Operationalizations

- **Lamont 1992:** semi-directed interviews; whole-interview relational ranking on three 5-point scales; comparative coding across four sites; dual-coding reliability checks.
- **Contrast with survey cultural-capital measures:** open-ended recovery of signals vs questionnaires that build high-culture items in a priori.
- Later literature (Lamont & Molnár 2002 and after — not ingested) distinguishes symbolic from social boundaries and extends domains; treat as post-1992 development.

## Applications

[[lamont-money-morals-manners-study]] — France/US upper-middle-class comparison; workplace gatekeeping; national repertoires + structure explanation of boundary patterns; inequality only when boundaries are strong and widely shared.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Interview-elicited boundaries may overstate discursive exclusion relative to observed co-presence.
- Three-type scheme is analytic; signals (e.g. self-actualization) can load on multiple standards.
- Generalizability beyond white male UMC samples is an open program (Lamont flags gender and race).
- Contested relation to [[cultural-capital]]: Lamont’s “boundary approach” claims greater empirical adequacy without full inconsistency with Bourdieu.
