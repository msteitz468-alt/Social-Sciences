# Glass Ceiling

The **glass ceiling** names barriers that slow or block women's movement into **top** organizational authority (not merely middle management). In *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* (2011), [[ridgeway-cecilia-l|Ridgeway]] treats it as one of two especially consequential workplace sites where modest, repeated gender-frame biases intensify into major inequality (with the [[maternal-wall]]).

## Definition as Coined

Ridgeway (citing organizational research) uses the glass ceiling for the difficulty women face in reaching substantial authority at the **elite top** of organizations. She reports women have increasingly entered middle management while progress to the very top remains slow (Gorman and Kmec 2009; Reskin and Ross 1995; Smith 2002 — as cited by Ridgeway 2011).

## Semantic History

Popular and policy usage preceded formal measurement debates. Analytic controversy (as Ridgeway summarizes): whether the ceiling is only the **additive** cumulative result of the same bias level found lower in hierarchies, or an **intensification** of bias at the top (Baxter and Wright 2000; Elliot and Smith 2004; Gorman and Kmec 2009). Gorman and Kmec (2009), as cited by Ridgeway, find that in some contexts (e.g., internal promotions in large organizations) higher authority **ratchets up** bias intensity.

## Operationalizations

- Representation in Fortune 500 top executive positions (Ridgeway cites Catalyst 2008: ~15% women).
- Promotion and evaluation studies comparing ranks.
- Expectation-states / status-belief mechanisms: authority roles culturally more congruent with male status and agency → heightened incongruity for women leaders.

## Applications

Ridgeway links the ceiling to [[gender-as-primary-frame|gender-frame]] effects on evaluation and legitimacy: women leaders face a balancing act between agentic leadership and gender prescriptions; **backlash** and legitimacy deficits can follow agentic behavior. She cites Eagly, Johannesen-Schmidt, and Van Engen (2003) meta-analysis: women leaders modestly more likely to use **transformational** leadership (combining agentic goals with collaborative style), which she treats as a possible wedge against the ceiling if successful women leaders change experiences that sustain stereotypes (attributed program claim).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Measurement dispute (additive vs intensified bias).
- Class and race cut the "ceiling" unevenly; Ridgeway's U.S. synthesis is not a full intersectional map.
- Organizational change and sector variation (e.g., biotech vs IT start-ups in Ch. 6) show ceilings are not uniform.
