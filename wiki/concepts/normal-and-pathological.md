# Normal and pathological (Durkheim)

## Definition as Coined

In Chapter III of *[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|The Rules of Sociological Method]]* (1895), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] argues that science can and must distinguish **normal** from **pathological** social facts. The criterion is **not** desirability, moral repugnance, or an observer’s ideal, but **generality relative to a [[social-types|social type]]** (and phase of development): what is normal is what is generally present in societies of that species under corresponding conditions; the average type is the healthy type (attributed). Pathology is deviation from that type — e.g., an abnormal *intensity* of a fact that is itself normal in milder form.

He famously holds that **crime is normal**: no society is entirely free of it; crime consists in offending strong collective sentiments, and a society without crime would require a homogeneity of conscience incompatible with social life; crime can also play a useful role in the evolution of morality (attributed). The institution of repression is as universal as crime. In the First Edition Preface he anticipates the charge of “justifying crime” and answers that normality of crime does not cancel the normality of punishment, nor the revulsion crime inspires.

## Operationalizations

- **Generality within type** — presence across cases of a social species.
- **Phase-sensitivity** — a fact normal for an early phase may be pathological later (or vice versa).
- **Intensity vs. presence** — suicide and some crime forms: the fact’s existence may be normal while its rate is pathological.
- **Not** individual clinical diagnosis as the sociological criterion (though medical analogy structures the vocabulary).

## Applications

- Crime and punishment as paired normal facts (*Rules* Ch. III; First Preface).
- Abnormal forms of the division of labor (*Division of Labor*).
- Pathological elevation of the suicide-rate in modern Europe (*Suicide*).
- Practical sociology: science can yield “rules of action for the future” once normal conditions are known (First Preface rationalism).

## Critiques and Limitations

Attributed lines of critique: (1) the average-as-healthy criterion confuses statistical commonality with desirability; (2) “social type” classification is itself contested, so normality inherits type-errors; (3) declaring crime “normal” is politically or morally dangerous (Durkheim’s reply: punishment remains normal; revulsion is compatible with functional necessity); (4) teleology creeps back when “usefulness for evolution” supplements the average-type test; (5) later critical theory asks how “pathology” relates to critique and emancipation (Lukes flags the live question in his 2013 Introduction — secondary).
