# Symbolic Classification

## Definition as Coined

**Symbolic classification** names the comparative object of Durkheim & Mauss's 1903 essay and of the research tradition [[needham-rodney|Needham]]'s Introduction helps consolidate: culturally organized schemes that classify the cosmos and social life together (moieties and totems, Zuñi regions, Chinese correlative sets, right/left, etc.).

Durkheim & Mauss contrast **symbolic** (moral/religious, speculative, hierarchical, totalizing) classification with **technological** classification (practical distinctions tied to means of livelihood). Only the former is their object; they treat it as a form of **[[collective-representations|collective representation]]** with social origin. Later anthropology often studies correspondences and dual symbolic classification **without** the 1903 causal thesis.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| D&M 1903 | Moral/religious classifications continuous with science; social morphology as model |
| Needham restatement | Same object; drops innate-incapacity claim and strong causality |
| Hertz → right/left studies | Dual symbolic classification as specialized research line |
| Needham constructive residual | Concordance of symbolic and social order varies by descent/alliance type; structural not merely formal |
| Contemporary anthropology | Correlative cosmologies, dualisms, taxonomic thought — often structuralist or cognitive, not Durkheimian-causal |

## Operationalizations

- Totemic assignment tables (Mount Gambier, Wotjobaluk, Zuñi clan–region–color tables).
- Dual schemes (moieties, Winter/Summer, right/left).
- Correlative sets (Chinese animals, elements, directions, cycles).
- Structural concordance tests (Needham): cognatic ≈ weak; simple lineal ≈ partial; prescriptive alliance ≈ strong structural concordance.

## Applications

- Comparative cosmology and social morphology ([[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-study]]).
- Kinship as classificatory thought (Kroeber, Hocart, Leach — as Needham cites).
- Bridge to *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|Elementary Forms]]* categories and to mid-century structural anthropology (*Pensée sauvage*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Equating symbolic order with social order risks "sociologistic metaphysic" (Evans-Pritchard, as Needham quotes on religion).
- Formal one-to-one correspondence is often false; structural concordance is weaker and harder to falsify.
- Second-hand ethnography and period sinology limit any single classic case.
