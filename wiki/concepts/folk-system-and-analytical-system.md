# Folk System and Analytical System

A methodological distinction formulated by [[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]] in
*[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (1957) to
govern how ethnographers should describe institutions of social control — and, more
broadly, any domain of social life.

## Definition as Coined

Bohannan argued (attributed): a **folk system** is "a systematization of ethnographic
fact for purposes of action" — the categories a people themselves use, live by, and act
within; it is learned by the good ethnographer, not created. An **analytical system** is
"a systematization of ethnographic fact (including the folk system) for purposes of
analysis" — created by the anthropologist to explain material gathered. Crucially, folk
systems "are never right or wrong. They 'exist'"; their key "is the language in which
[they are] stated."

His worked example (attributed to Bohannan, drawing on [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]):
the [[nuer|Nuer]] agnatic genealogy is a folk system; Evans-Pritchard's rendering of the
same field as "a [[segmentary-lineage-system|lineage system based on the principle of
segmental opposition]]" is an analytical system.

## Semantic History

Bohannan coined the pairing in 1957, by analogy with "folk etymology," to name a
distinction that had been made implicitly before (notably in [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s
Nuer work) but never labelled. It has since been widely read as one formulation of the
**emic/etic** distinction (Pike, Harris) and of the actor's-vs-observer's-categories problem;
Bohannan's own emphasis — that the folk system is *learned, not created*, and that its key
"is the language in which it is stated" — gives it a more strongly linguistic cast than the
emic/etic vocabulary that partly displaced it.

## The load-bearing warning

The point of the distinction is a hazard: "The anthropologist's chief danger is that he
will change one of the folk systems of his own society into an analytical system, and try
to give it wider application than its merit and usefulness allow." Western "law," in
Bohannan's analysis, is precisely such a folk system — the English-speaking countries'
action-categories for social control — routinely mistaken for a neutral analytical
science. Thus the Tiv organize dispute around **[[jir]]** (counteraction), not "rules";
around **debt** (*injô*), not "tort" and "contract" — and to describe them in the latter
terms is to impose one folk system on another under the guise of analysis.

## Operationalizations

In practice the distinction is "applied" by a discipline of description: render the people's
own action-categories first, in their own language (the folk system), and mark every
analytical grid the ethnographer imposes *as* analytical. Bohannan's own caveat (recorded in
the 1957 text and reinforced by his 1968 self-criticism) is that the two are not opposites to
be chosen between: the analytical system takes the folk system as part of its data, and
comparison must proceed from folk categories rather than around them.

## Applications

Bohannan's paradigm application is the [[tiv|Tiv]] material of *Justice and Judgment*: the
Tiv organize dispute around **[[jir]]** (counteraction), not "rules"; obligations under
**debt** (*injô*), not "tort" and "contract"; wrongs under *ifer*/*kwaghbo*/*kwaghdang*, not
"crime" and "sin." In each case he shows that reaching for the English term imposes one folk
system on another under the guise of analysis. The distinction has since been applied far
beyond law wherever the actor's categories and the analyst's categories must be kept
separate.

## Critiques and Limitations

The distinction is the crux of the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. Critics ([[gluckman-max|Gluckman]],
Hoebel, Nader) charged that so strict a priority of the folk system makes cross-societal
comparison impossible — Gluckman's phrase was "cultural solipsism." Bohannan replied that
comparison is not impossible but must build comparative categories out of ethnographic
inquiry (a Boasian route) rather than defining categories first and then classifying (a
Tylorian route), and that his position was never that Tiv culture is unique. Bohannan also
conceded that his own marriage chapters lapsed from the discipline the concept demands.
