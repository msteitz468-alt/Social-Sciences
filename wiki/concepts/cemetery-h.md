# Cemetery H

**Cemetery H** is both (1) a **locus at [[harappa]]** (a large cemetery with painted burial urns and extended inhumations first excavated in the 1930s) and (2) by extension an **etic culture/phase label** for related northern Late Harappan / Localization Era materials — Kenoyer’s **Punjab Phase** of the [[indus-valley-tradition]] (c. 1900–1300 BCE frame in his tables).

It is **not** a self-name of an ancient people. Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ ethnos.

## Definition as Coined

Originally the cemetery designation “H” at Harappa (distinct from Mature Harappan Cemetery R-37). The label then expanded to painted ceramics and burial customs of the post-Integration northern regional culture. In Kenoyer’s Shaffer-based scheme, this is the northern pole of Localization Era breakup, alongside **Jhukar Phase** (south) and **Rangpur Phase** (Gujarat).

## Operationalizations

### Burial modes (Harappa; Kenoyer 1998)

- **Earlier Cemetery H:** Layout like Harappan coffin burials, pottery at head and feet. Painted jars with high flaring rims (new style associated with western highland links); large ledge-rim jars and heavy dish-on-stands with strong Harappan links.
- **Later Cemetery H:** Adults **cremated**; children placed in large urns covered with a second pot; large urns heavily decorated with new motifs.
- Contrast with **final Mature Harappan** cemetery pottery trend toward plain/unslipped vessels — reemergence of heavily decorated burial pottery signals major ritual/ideological change (Kenoyer).

### Painted motifs (attributed)

New combinations of animal, plant, and human themes unknown on earlier Harappan painted ceramics, including: double serpents with horned headdress and two pipal leaves; humped bulls with three pipal leaves from the forehead; flying peacocks with antelope horns or pipal-leaf tails; bull-body / antelope-horns / human-head figure with bangle-covered arms. Style unique, but trefoil/pipal headdresses and horned figures argue for **incorporation of some Indus beliefs** into new rituals (Kenoyer 1998 citing Vats plates).

### Regional distribution

Cemetery H / Punjab Phase ceramics: northern Pakistan as far as Swat (mixing with local traditions) and east into the Ganga-Yamuna Doab with gradual settlement expansion. Trade with western highlands and coast breaks down (lapis, turquoise rare; marine shell declines); faience refined, possibly compensating for lost shell/carnelian; copper continues.

### HARP technology note

1996 Harappa excavations: room pot c. **1730 BCE** (Punjab Phase / Harappa Periods 4–5) with 133 beads — including earliest known Indus **glass** bead (red-brown) and azure faience possibly imitating lapis; tubular drilling of small beads after Ernestite supply cut off ([[chanhudaro]] technology horizon ends).

## Applications

- Primary northern evidence for the [[indus-transformation-debate]] / Localization Era — transformation and regionalization rather than single-event collapse.
- Mortuary baseline for bioarchaeology of Late Harappan Punjab ([[bioarchaeology-of-the-indus]]).
- Ceramic and motif continuity/discontinuity studies in [[indus-religion]].
- Contrasts with southern **Jhukar** trajectories at [[mohenjo-daro]], [[chanhudaro]], Amri (circular geometric seals; script loss).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Invasion baggage:** Older equations of Cemetery H with Aryan newcomers are not supported by Kenoyer’s gradual-transition and continuity arguments; bioarchaeology is cited against mass-migration massacre models, but genetic results post-1998 must be date-stamped separately.
- **Locus vs culture:** Not every Punjab Phase sherd equals the Harappa cemetery population.
- **Chronology:** Absolute ranges differ by author (Kenoyer Localization to ~1300 BCE; Possehl Harappa Period 5 1800–1500 BCE band); calibration status often unstated in secondary synthesis.
- **Four-way non-identity:** Do not equate Cemetery H pottery with a language family or Vedic ethnos without separate argument.

## Wright (2010) — R37 vs. Cemetery H strata, and Kennedy's matrilocality inference

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Chs. 9 and 11.)* Wright distinguishes Harappa's two adjacent cemetery areas, R37 and Cemetery H, both in the low-lying southwest sector of the city outside the walled/gated areas. R37 (excavated by four separate archaeological teams over time) contains an estimated ~200 individuals — explicitly noted by Wright as far too few to represent Harappa's full Urban-period population. Cemetery H itself comprises two strata: an earlier stratum (earth burials, complete remains in the eastern section, fractional remains in the west, possibly reflecting deaths away from home) and a later stratum of pot burials, with skeletal remains placed in large painted jars, some containing multiple interments.

Physical-anthropological analysis (Hemphill et al. 1991, team led by Kenneth Kennedy) found strong genetic affinity among R37 females (n=84), and reported that R37 females showed close genetic affinity to the earliest Cemetery H stratum's females — evidence, on this reading, of **matrilocality** (post-marital residence with the bride's kin). By contrast, R37 males showed closer genetic affinity to males from *outside* Harappa than to R37 females, which Kennedy (2000:306) interprets as "one-way gene flow by cemetery R37 males into the sedentary population of cemetery R37 females." Wright reports this alongside Hemphill et al.'s (1991) finding that the R37 population's general health was judged good, attributed partly to a diversified diet of domesticated and hunted/foraged foods; paleopathology showed spinal arthritis common in both sexes (possibly from head-carried loads, per Lovell 1994) and higher female cavity incidence and canine-enamel growth-disruption lines, explained via attributed competing hypotheses of preferential treatment of sons (Lukacs and Joshi 1992) or differential food-preparation practices (Kennedy 2000).

Wright notes an apparent internal inconsistency in her source's stratum labeling for Cemetery H (a "Stratum I"/"Stratum II" ordering that reads as reversed against the "lower/older" stratum description elsewhere in the same account) — flagged here for any future reconciliation against primary excavation reports, not resolved in this synthesis.
