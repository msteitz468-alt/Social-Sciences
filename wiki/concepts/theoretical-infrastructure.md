# Theoretical Infrastructure

## Definition as Coined

For [[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]] (*[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|Coming Crisis]]*, 1970), the **infrastructure of social theory** is the sub-theoretical layer that carries a theory: [[domain-assumptions]], structure of sentiments, personal reality of the theorist, and the social settings (university, class, generation, welfare state) that sponsor theory-work. The "superstructure" of formal concepts and propositions rests on this infrastructure.

Gouldner treats infrastructure as what explains the *career* of theories — acceptance, rejection, "irrelevance" to new generations — better than logical refutation alone. New Left indifference to older theory, on his account, is an infrastructure mismatch (new sentiments / old languages), not only a technical disagreement.

## Semantic History

Gouldner's 1970 usage is deliberate (base/superstructure echo without full Marxist reduction). Related: Mannheimian [[sociology-of-knowledge]]; Raymond Williams's "structure of feeling" (Gouldner acknowledges Williams on "structure of sentiments"); later "thought style" and "epistemic culture" literatures. Do not silently equate with Marxist economic base.

## Operationalizations

Qualitative-historical in *Coming Crisis* (periodization of Western sociology; case of Parsons; comparative Soviet note). Survey items on functionalism and religiosity appear as symptomatic indicators, not a full infrastructure metric.

## Applications

- Explains Functionalism's rise in Depression-to-postwar U.S. and its "entropy" under welfare-state and generational pressure (Gouldner 1970).
- Frames rival 1960s sociologies ([[symbolic-interactionism|dramaturgy/Goffman]], ethnomethodology, exchange theory) as competing infrastructures, not only competing propositions.
- Anchors [[reflexive-sociology]]: knowing one's own infrastructure.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of reducing theoretical disagreement to biography or generation; difficulty of independent evidence for "sentiments"; base/superstructure metaphor invites over-determinism Gouldner only partly resists. His own epilogue admits possible projection of his career onto the "objective" crisis narrative.
