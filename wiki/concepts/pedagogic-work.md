# Pedagogic Work

*(Attributed — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] & [[passeron-jean-claude|Passeron]], [[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|*Reproduction*]], Book I §3.)* The third pillar of the theory of [[symbolic-violence]] (abbreviated **PW**): the process of inculcation through which a [[pedagogic-action]] produces its durable effect. PW is what distinguishes lasting PA (family, school) from the "discontinuous and extraordinary actions of symbolic violence" of the prophet, the sorcerer, or the intellectual creator.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed.)* Proposition 3:

> PA "entails pedagogic work (PW), a process of inculcation which must last long enough to produce a durable training, i.e. a habitus, the product of internalization of the principles of a cultural arbitrary capable of perpetuating itself after PA has ceased."

PW "tends to reproduce the social conditions of the production of that cultural arbitrary... through the mediation of the [[habitus]], defined as the principle generating practices which reproduce the objective structures." The authors offer (flagged as *analogy*, not literal claim) a biological comparison: education is to the cultural order as the transmission of genetic capital is to the biological order — if habitus is the analogue of genetic capital, inculcation is the analogue of generation.

## Semantic History

- **Three axes of productivity.** PW's "specific productivity" is measured by (1) **durability** — how long the habitus persists; (2) **transposability** — its capacity to generate practices across many fields (the authors cite Panofsky's thesis that Scholastic PW's "habit-forming force" shaped Gothic architecture and manuscript layout); (3) **exhaustivity** — how completely it reproduces the arbitrary's principles. PW's effects outlast political coercion's, which always faces its own succession problem.
- **Primary vs. secondary PW.** *Primary* PW produces the *primary habitus* "characteristic of a group or class, which is the basis for the subsequent formation of any other habitus." *Secondary* PW (the school's) has a productivity that is "a function of the distance" between the habitus it targets and the pre-existing primary habitus — "the success of all school education... depends fundamentally on the education previously accomplished in the earliest years of life, even and especially when the educational system denies this primacy." Habitus formation is *irreversible*: it can only be overwritten by another irreversible process (the authors quote Husserl on the non-fungibility of a "German" versus a "Chinese" formation).
- **Modes of inculcation** range between **conversion** (total habitus substitution — the "total institution": barracks, convent, prison, asylum, boarding school) and **maintenance/reinforcement** (elite finishing institutions requiring only "ostentatious appearances" of training because selection pre-filters for habitus proximity), and between **implicit** pedagogy (unconscious inculcation by imitation of a master, "very profitable for the dominant classes") and **explicit** pedagogy (codified principles).

## Operationalizations

- **The "legitimate need" thesis.** PW produces both the legitimate cultural product *and* the "legitimate need" for it: "a 'cultural need' is a cultivated need." Church-going, museum-going, and aesthetic "taste" are cultivated dispositions misrecognized as innate — the authors attack "the myth of an innate taste" as another form of *genesis amnesia*.
- **Practical vs. symbolic mastery.** "Symbolic mastery of a practice... must logically and chronologically follow practical mastery": school grammar does not create a new practice but gives explicit, systematic form to principles already practically mastered (comparanda: Piaget, Vygotsky).
- **Class and the "gift."** Traditional-mode dominant PW produces the "cultivated relation to legitimate culture as a relation of familiarity" and the **ideology of the "gift"** — "the negation of the social conditions of the production of cultivated dispositions" — favoring children already equipped with the requisite [[cultural-capital]].

## Applications

- **The sociodicy function.** Traditional-mode dominant PW has lower productivity on classes distant from the dominant primary habitus and so functions to *exclude* them while producing misrecognition of the mechanisms of exclusion — the authors name this (by analogy with *theodicy*) the **sociodicy function**, illustrated by the museum, which filters its public by presupposing a cultural decoding code.
- **Group integration through habitus, not consensus.** PW produces "intellectual and moral integration" via shared habitus rather than a shared "repertoire of representations" — an explicit rejection of consensus-sociology accounts.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Determinism.** The stress on irreversibility and the primacy of early inculcation invites the determinism charge Bourdieu repeatedly rejected (1990 preface) — see [[cultural-reproduction-debate]].
- **The biological analogy** and the "genetic capital" metaphor are, by the authors' own flagging, illustrative; taken literally they overstate closure.
