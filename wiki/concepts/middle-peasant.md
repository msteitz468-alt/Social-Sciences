# Middle Peasant

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasant-wars-1969|Peasant Wars of the Twentieth Century]]* (1969, Conclusion), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] defines **middle peasantry** as a peasant population with **secure access to land of its own** cultivated with **family labor**. Within fields of power, this stratum (together with "free" poor peasants outside landlord domains) holds the **minimal [[tactical-mobility|tactical freedom]]** to challenge overlords — unlike landlord-dependent poor peasants/landless laborers (no tactical power of their own) and rich peasants (local power derivative of external domains). Wolf cites Alavi (1965) on internal leverage of middle holdings.

Wolf’s full triad (attributed, 1969):

| Stratum | Power position | Revolutionary likelihood (Wolf) |
|---|---|---|
| Poor / landless inside landlord domain | No independent tactical power | Low unless external force breaks domain (Yucatán Constitutionalist army; 1917 army collapse; Chinese Red Army) |
| Rich peasant | Local power derivative of external domains; employer/moneylender/notable | Low until superior domains are destroyed |
| **Middle peasant** | Own land + family labor → internal leverage | Pivotal when commercial crisis hits traditional matrix |
| Free/peripheral poor | Subsidiary livelihoods outside landlord control | Also tactically mobile |

## Semantic History

- **Pre-Wolf agrarian usage:** Russian *serednyak*, Chinese middle-peasant categories in CCP land-class analysis (including “new middle peasants” after reform), and colonial/postcolonial agrarian sociology already stratified peasantries.
- **Wolf 1969:** Converts stratification into a **revolutionary-recruitment hypothesis**: the culturally "traditional" middle peasant is paradoxically most instrumental in "dynamiting" the peasant social order because commercialism threatens his balance while his social relations remain traditional; he also transmits urban ideas via children sent to town (“training of the cities,” Tillion via Wolf).
- **Case vocabulary inside the book:** Tyumenev on Russian Centre middle peasantry and Communist power; Vietnamese 2.5–10 ha village rulers; Algerian Badissia/1954 organizers (Launay); Mexican free-village leadership resources; not identical operationalizations but one thesis family.
- **Afterlife:** Standing claim (and target) in peasant-revolution debates; see [[wolf-peasant-wars-study]] Critiques.

## Operationalizations

Wolf: secure own land + family labor + power-domain position. Contrasts: rich peasant (employs labor, moneylends, state-co-opted notable); poor/landless (livelihood inside landlord domain). Not a pure income quantile without the power-domain criterion. CCP political categories and Russian survey strata are historical instruments as well as descriptions — do not silently equate them.

## Applications

Attributed anchors in Wolf 1969: Morelos free-village leadership; Russian Central Agricultural Region communes (middle peasantry of the Centre); CCP northern bastion and land-class practice; Vietnamese middle owners; Algerian middle-peasant organizers; Oriente squatters as free/peripheral analogue (not cane proletariat). Full case detail: [[wolf-peasant-wars-study]] · [[hubs/studies/wolf-peasant-wars-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested whether "middle" is decisive across cases or an artifact of successful-zone labeling and secondary synthesis.
- Kinship cross-cuts of rich/poor (Wolf notes poor-peasant interests often crosscut class alignments).
- Gender and household internal hierarchy under-specified.
- Must not be equated in wiki voice with "the peasantry" as a whole ([[peasantry-as-class]]).
