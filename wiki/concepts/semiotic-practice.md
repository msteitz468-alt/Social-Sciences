# Semiotic Practice

## Definition as Coined

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, "Refiguring the 'Social' in Social Science: An Interpretivist Manifesto," *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* ch. 10) defines **semiotic practices** as "any practices that communicate information by means of some sort of signs and are therefore open to interpretation." The term deliberately extends the "linguistic turn" beyond language and discourse to include iconic, ludic, spatial, technical, gestural, ritual, disciplinary, and kinesthetic practices (following Roger Chartier's insistence that experience is not reducible to discourse).

The concept anchors Sewell's positive social ontology: "The social … is best understood as, first, an articulated, evolving web of semiotic practices … that, second, builds up and transforms a range of physical frameworks [the [[built-environment|built environment]]] that both provide matrices for these practices and constrain their consequences."

## Semantic History

Sewell positions the concept against two poles. Against Keith Baker's claim that politics and "the social" are purely and literally linguistic/discursive (no "social realities independent of symbolic meanings"), he argues Baker collapses a needed dialectic and has little to say about work, kinship, or material culture. With Chartier, he holds "the world of meaning is wider than the world of speech and writing." He also affirms language's distinctive **reflexive capacity** (the ability to comment on and evaluate semiotic practice, including itself) as what makes language "a particularly important form of semiotic practice" — recognizable only "if we are willing to restrict the notion of language to its proper scope, rather than claiming that all human action is linguistic." His running illustration is basketball, whose knowledge and strategy are "above all bodily or kinesthetic," not primarily linguistic. (The Baker–Chartier disagreement over whether the social is exhaustively discursive is not yet promoted to a `debates/` page.)

## Operationalizations

- **Paradigmatic vs. mechanistic explanation.** Sewell distinguishes explaining action by specifying the codes/paradigms that enable it (interpretive, analogous to langue/parole) from specifying cause and effect (mechanistic, modeled on natural science). He argues both are legitimate on a single ontology.
- **Articulation.** Distinct semiotic practices ("language games") join through *articulation* (the "action or process of jointing"), preferred to the post-structuralist "suturing." Imperfect fit between modalities is "an important source of changes in the overall shape of the games in question — which is to say, of social life" (examples: basketball rule changes, Reddy's history of emotions, the Bastille's fusion of crowd violence and popular sovereignty).
- **Scope, not level.** The micro/macro distinction is recast as a difference in the scope/scale of articulated semiotic practices, not in "levels of abstraction."

## Applications

Underwrites Sewell's reconciliation of interpretive and quantitative method: capitalist relations are semiotically constituted as quantitative via the commodity form (Marx; Postone's "real abstraction"), so quantification is a historically produced, interpretable feature — the basis of his "hermeneutical quantification." See [[eventful-sociology]], [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded as position: the Baker–Chartier disagreement over whether the social is exhaustively discursive; and the general worry that broadening "semiotic" risks losing the specificity of language. Sewell's answer is the language-plus-built-environment dialectic rather than either reductive materialism or pure discursivism.
