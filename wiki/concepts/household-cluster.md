# Household Cluster

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Marcus C. Winter in Flannery ed. 1976.)* The **household cluster** is the house plus surrounding storage pits, burials, middens, and features that can be reliably associated with the same household — an analytical unit above the bare house floor and below the courtyard group or ward. Flannery, introducing Winter’s term in *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]*, wrote that the cluster “consists of the house and all the surrounding storage pits, burials, middens, and features.” Winter insisted on a sharp distinction: the **household cluster** is archaeological remains; the **household** is a group of interacting people. Analysis reconstructs composition, compares activities, and studies inter-household relations at the level between house/activity area and community.

Winter sketched a **typical** Early–Middle Formative cluster as perhaps one house, two to six large storage pits, one to three graves, and an open area of 20–40 m; features of one cluster at [[tierras-largas]] might scatter over ~**300 m²**. In growth comparisons (Ch. 8) he reported that most Early and Middle Formative clusters could be bounded roughly by a circle **20 m in diameter** or a 20×15 m rectangle (~300 m²), a pattern roughly constant ~1400–400 B.C. (with some Guadalupe-phase exceptions). Only ~**3–10%** of surface area within excavated clusters at Tierras Largas yielded primary deposits.

## Semantic History

Introduced as a Formative Mesoamerican recovery unit in *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]); later household archaeology (Wilk & Netting and others) refined co-residence vs household without always retaining Winter's term. Within the volume’s multi-scalar ladder (Flannery & Winter), the cluster sits above activity area / feature / work area / house and below courtyard group, residential ward, and community — the load-bearing unit for [[household-archaeology]] as practiced on Formative Oaxaca.

## Operationalizations

- **Horizontal association:** house floor + pits, ovens, burials, drains, middens mapped as one unit (Winter’s Cluster No. 7 template: record house, pits, burials, other features, refuse, and inferences together).
- **Spatial scale:** ~300 m² / ~20 m diameter working envelope; inter-cluster spacing Early Formative often ~20–40 m (margins ~20 m, centers avg. ~40 m per Winter 1972 via Whalen); Rosario-phase Tierras Largas spacing ~35 m (~1.5× San José spacing).
- **Sampling designs:** probability quadrats that can hit parts of multiple clusters (Winter’s 3.7% random sample at Tierras Largas); long transects that may cross-section an entire cluster (house → midden → bell-shaped pit → burial). Household counts estimated from cluster area, random sample squares, and spacing — Winter stressed sampling/stats alone were insufficient without “common sense” spacing considerations (confidence intervals sometimes large, e.g. ~5–30 clusters).
- **Feature inventory (Winter, Oaxaca):** bell-shaped storage pits (major EF/MF signature); burials (often infants/children near houses vs specialized cemeteries elsewhere); ovens (hemispherical/rectangular subterranean; circular above-ground domed) — none pottery kilns, none inside houses; 1200–500 B.C. Oaxaca houses usually lacked hearths (portable braziers); refuse middens; drainage ditches (SJM, Tierras Largas); various smaller pits.
- **Proxy problem:** structures ≠ households remains standing; multi-structure compounds and sequential occupation blur units.

## Applications

### From Flannery (ed.) 1976

- **[[tierras-largas]] (Winter):** sampling showed ~**8–12 household clusters** per period, separated by courtyards or open work areas 20–40 m across; each cluster ≥300 m² with at least one house plus pits, burials, ovens, drainage, activity areas. Exemplar Late Tierras Largas Cluster (~1250 B.C.): partial ~4×6 m whitewashed wattle-and-daub house; 8 bell-shaped pits west of house; burials (adult male >40; female 20–30; female prone); agriculture/hunting/food prep plus ceremonial items (macaw feathers, Gulf Coast turtle-shell drum). Table 8.1 best estimates hold household numbers near ~5–10 across Early–Middle Formative subphases despite hectare fluctuations — Winter’s “modal” hamlet size argument.
- **[[san-jose-mogote]]:** extensive houses and household clusters (e.g. Areas A/C; Pyne’s six extensively excavated EF houses/clusters); Flannery proposed that 8–12 households may be the size of (or smaller than) **one residential ward** at a large village like SJM — sequence for large villages: house → household cluster → courtyard group → residential ward → community.
- **Tomaltepec (Whalen):** 5–8 clusters estimated; centers 40–45 m apart validating Winter’s spacing; higher-status and cemetery segregation no greater than ordinary household spacing.
- **Guatemalan Pacific Coast (Flannery comparison):** idealized Cuadros-area cluster on clay/refuse platform with outdoor clam-baking hearths and less highland-style bell-pit storage; La Victoria mounds interpreted as clusters with 30–40 m gaps analogous to Tierras Largas; some Ocós sites 200–300 m² possibly single clusters or courtyard groups.
- **Exchange test (Winter & Pires-Ferreira):** house-by-house obsidian source proportions at Tierras Largas and San José Mogote used to distinguish individual-household reciprocity (high inter-household source variation) from elite pooling (uniformity across clusters).

## Critiques and Limitations

Association of pits/burials with a single house can be stratigraphically ambiguous; lifecycle stage may mimic rank differentials; suprahousehold arenas (public buildings, sodalities) are outside the unit. Winter and Flannery both warned that small exposures miss the ~300 m² envelope. Flannery noted courtyard-group readings may be projected from later patio architecture onto sparse maps. Primary deposits are sparse within clusters (3–10% of surface at Tierras Largas), so “empty” open space is structural, not necessarily unoccupied community.
