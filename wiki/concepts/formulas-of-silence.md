# Formulas of Silence

## Definition as Coined

In *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]* Ch.3, [[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] names two complementary **formulas** through which historiography manages what was [[unthinkable-history|unthinkable]]:

1. **Formulas of erasure** — tropes that tend to **erase directly** the fact of a revolution (or its relevance). Favorites of generalists and popularizers (e.g. textbooks). “It” did not really happen; it was not that bad or that important.
2. **Formulas of banalization** — tropes that **empty singular events of revolutionary content** so the string of facts, “gnawed from all sides,” becomes trivial. Favorites of specialists. Recasts the Haitian Revolution as mere race war, disease story, or derivative of French politics.

> “Both are formulas of silence.” (Trouillot 1995)

Joint effect (attributed): “whatever has not been cancelled out in the generalities dies in the cumulative irrelevance of a heap of details.”

## Semantic History

Trouillot argues these rhetorical families are **formally identical** to late-eighteenth-century discourse that could not admit a slave revolution — continuity of unthinkability into professional history, not a clean break. He proposes structural similarities (at the level of generalities) with silences around American slavery and the Holocaust: frontal denial of fact/relevance vs sweetening via isolated details (some slaves better fed; some Jews survived; each convoy explained alone).

Not unique to Haiti: “erasure and banalization are not unique to the Haitian Revolution.”

## Operationalizations

| Formula | Typical carrier | Haitian Revolution examples (Trouillot, attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| Erasure | Textbooks, world histories, Age of Revolutions surveys | Revolution omitted or reduced to “revolt”; not among radical political revolutions of the age |
| Banalization | Specialists of colony/Revolution | Yellow fever as principal victor; external French intrigue as necessary and sufficient; early rebel demands trivialized; terminology that resists former slaves as main actors |

Also: Haiti used as **pretext** (litmus test for the Black race) without regard to Haitian history as such — significance that ironically reinforces silence.

## Applications

- [[haitian-revolution]] historiography; [[unthinkable-haitian-revolution-debate]]
- Comparative silence analysis (slavery; Holocaust) — analogy of form, not identity of events
- Distinction from Ch.2 silences (available facts underused) and Ch.4 silences (conflict under abundance)

## Critiques and Limitations

- Binary can blur; many texts mix partial acknowledgment with domestication.
- Attribution required — not a field-wide closed typology.
- Risk of reading all specialist detail as bad-faith banalization; Trouillot’s target is detail that **destroys singularity** of the revolutionary sequence.
