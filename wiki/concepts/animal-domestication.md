# Animal Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Animal domestication** is the process by which humans and animal populations enter lasting mutual dependence so that reproduction, morphology, and/or behavior change under human management — analytically distinct from hunting and from plant [[domestication|domestication]], though often bundled in farming packages. [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] treats dogs, sheep, goats, cattle, pigs, and regionally zebu, water buffalo, and camelids across independent and diffused trajectories.

## Semantic History

Childe’s Neolithic package fused plants and animals; later zooarchaeology separated pathways, centers, and sequences (animals before crops in North Africa cattle claims; dogs before plants in Natufian contexts). Hole-type models stress scarcity management, fencing, and orphans; Mithen adds Natufian/PPNA pets and possible central roles for women and children in taming. African Barbary sheep corralling shows **management without domestication outcome**.

## Operationalizations

As of Mithen 2003:

- **Morphology:** size reduction through levels (Mehrgarh cattle/sheep smaller over time; Beidha goats smaller than wild).
- **Demography:** slaughter peaks (Beidha ~2 years; few young → not milk-oriented).
- **Taxon dominance shifts:** goats 80% at Beidha vs gazelle dominance in Natufian/PPNA.
- **Range extension:** boat transport of cattle, pigs, sheep, goats to Cyprus (Shillourokambos, Mylouthkia wells with domestic cereals and goat fills).
- **Facilities:** pens, dung layers with fodder (Uan Afuda).
- **Dog:** early size reduction and village niche; dog burials (Skateholm); Natufian human–canid associations.
- **Priority claims:** Nabta cattle ~9000 BC vs West Asian cattle ~7500 BC consensus — if Wendorf right, Africa earlier (contested position as of Mithen).

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **Levant / Fertile Crescent:** dog; PPNB goat herding; sheep/goat after gazelle at Abu Hureyra; pathways into [[pastoralism]]; Jordan valley nomadic pastoralism after town collapse.
- **Anatolia/Cyprus:** Aşıklı, Çayönü, Shillourokambos packages; second-wave farmers bringing full animal suite.
- **Europe:** first farmers boat sheep/goats to Greece ~7500 BC; LBK cattle emphasis; Ertebølle foragers adopt farming later.
- **South Asia:** local zebu and sheep at Mehrgarh; domestic ovicaprids at Bagor.
- **East Asia / New Guinea:** pigs with gardens; Hemudu water buffalo and pigs with rice.
- **Africa:** possible independent cattle; sheep/goats/wheat/barley ultimately West Asian at Merimde/Fayum/Nabta.
- **Comparative sequences (Mithen):** animals before crops (N. Africa cattle; Andes vicuña) as one of several divergent orders.
- **Central Andes (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]):** camelids (llama/alpaca lineage; wild vicuña/guanaco hunting first) almost certainly domesticated in highland grasslands; guinea pig (*cuy*) appears in mid-elevation Preceramic contexts (e.g. Kotosh tradition sites). Moseley frames Andean domestication as long, risk-driven transplanting across elevation belts rather than a single Neolithic "revolution," with herding gradually replacing hunting on the Titicaca puna while coastal Maritime-Oasis economies remain largely non-pastoral.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Kill-off profiles and size trends admit multiple interpretations (hunting selection vs herding).
- **Commensal / pet pathways** are hard to see archaeologically.
- Single-site “firsts” supersede rapidly; African cattle priority remains debated.
- Domesticate package ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ ethnic group (four-way non-identity).
- Pre-aDNA genetics of herd origins in Mithen 2003 is provisional (as of Mithen 2003).

## Ucko & Dimbleby 1969 densification

*(Attributed to chapter authors in *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]* 1969.)*

### Definitions and criteria

- **Bökönyi:** domestication = capture and taming of animals with particular behavioural characteristics, removal from natural living area and breeding community, maintenance under controlled breeding for profit; essence = intention to **breed** under artificial conditions (“living food conserves”). Phases: **animal keeping** (no purposeful selection) vs **animal breeding** (selective breeding + feeding control). Recognition criteria: age/sex proportions ≠ wild; species outside wild range; morphological change; art; husbandry gear — last four most reliable; first two most interesting for early stages. Local domestication best evidenced by **transitional** individuals. Age-structure-alone arguments need caution (Hopkins on Shanidar immature kills).
- **Chaplin:** non-morphological criteria (MNI, age/sex models, settlement context) alongside morphology.
- **Berry:** genetic implications; no inevitable morphological markers of domestication (island/rat analogies).
- **Ducos (Palestine):** age-profile methodology; argues against clear PPN/Natufian domestication for many taxa (possible Caprinae exception); Pottery Neolithic hunter-pastoralists (as of Ducos 1969).
- **Grigson / Payne:** absolute size limits for aurochs vs cattle; metrical sheep vs goat metacarpals (population clustering, not sure individual ID).
- **Cranstone:** ethnographic husbandry spectrum (prestige vs economy; reindeer continuum; management techniques) as interpretive resource for archaeology.

### Regional patterns (as of 1969)

- **Reed (Near East synthesis):** domesticates as cultural artifacts diverting ecosystem energy; earliest zone lower hills/adjacent plains east Mediterranean–SW Iran; dog and ruminants preadapted (cellulose conversion); **village life** primary requirement for earliest ruminant/pig domestication; Natufians more complex culture but **no** domestic animals vs Zawi Chemi domestic sheep (Perkins); dog at Çayönü/Jarmo; pig early at Çayönü; cattle late (Argissa ~8500 BP Asia later — as of Reed 1969); Palestine “hiatus” skepticism.
- **Clutton-Brock (Jericho):** carnivore remains; osteologically secure domestic dog **not** demonstrated (period caution vs Zeuner).
- **Allchin (India/Pakistan):** sparse evidence; Baluchistan early cattle/sheep/goat; local zebu vs imported horse; separate late Stone Age vs western pastoralist pathways (non-identity flagged).
- **Watson (China):** dog/pig early in Yang Shao; millet vs rice regional packages.
- **Flannery:** caprines as surplus **banking** under erratic dry-farming; wool/secondary products ecological chains (*Stipa*).
- **Ryder:** fleece evolution after domestication (kemp → wool; shears; secondary products).
- **Jewell:** after Neolithic sheep/goat/cattle/pig, major meat domestication “practically at an end”; modern re-domestication advocacy.
- **Piggott:** food refuse is socially selected — “Custom, not Calory is King.”

### Sherratt — primary vs secondary products (1981–97)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*. )* After initial [[animal-domestication|domestication]] for meat (and related slaughter products), Sherratt’s **[[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]]** models a later shift to renewable **secondary** products (milk, wool, [[animal-traction|traction]]). Kill-off economics, fleece transition, and draught proxies are the zooarchaeological toolkit; horse domestication/riding claims are revised within the volume (1995 rewrite of Ch.7). Do not equate secondary-product horizons with initial domestication events.

### Diamond GGS (1997, attributed)

[[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] (*[[diamond-guns-germs-steel-1997|GGS]]* Ch.9): only ~14 ancient species of big herbivorous domestic mammals; Major Five (sheep, goat, cow, pig, horse) overwhelmingly Eurasian in origin; six failure classes (diet, growth rate, captive breeding, disposition, panic, social structure). Livestock also seed **crowd diseases** (Ch.11) — the “lethal gift” proximate factor. All attributed; secondary synthesis.

## Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017, esp. Ch.2–3; synthesis, not zooarchaeological primary data.)*

- **Sheep/goat as first noncommensal domesticates (Near East):** Profound revolution converting long-term prey into penned, protected, milked/wooled, then slaughtered stock; human global invasive success tied to battalions of domesticates (Ch.2).
- **Preadaptation traits:** Herd behavior/social hierarchy, environmental tolerance, broad diet, crowding/disease adaptability, breeding under confinement, muted fright-and-flight; **gazelle** — most hunted for millennia via desert kites — does **not** survive under domestication despite herd behavior (Ch.2). Affinity with Diamond’s failure-class inventory, but Scott’s framing is domus-module evolution, not continental biogeography.
- **Management signatures:** Restricted diet and mobility; unprecedented crowding in enclosures/wadis/caves; culling young males and post-reproductive females to maximize fertile females; **age–sex profiles of bone finds** as strongest archaeological evidence of active management (Ch.2).
- **Behavioral hallmark:** Lower reactivity/wariness toward other species including humans; uninvited commensals (pigeons, rats, mice, sparrows) show similar reduced wariness — independent “domus effect” beyond deliberate breeding (Ch.2). Belyaev-type silver-fox experiment reported: rapid tameness with lop ears, piebaldness, raised tail (Ch.2).
- **Morphology:** Reduced sexual dimorphism (e.g. diminished male sheep horns), higher fertility, neoteny (early adulthood; juvenile skull morphology; shorter face/jaw). **Brain-size reductions** under domestication (as Scott reports): sheep ~24% over ~10 kya; ferrets ~30% vs wild polecats; pigs >1/3 smaller — limbic system disproportionately affected (aggression/flight/fear thresholds) (Ch.2).
- **Pathology of confinement:** Stress, trauma, narrower diet, same-species infection; chronic arthritis, gum disease; confined llama newborn mortality ~50% vs wild guanacos via muddy feces-rich corrals (Ch.2). Fertility gains (earlier reproductive age, more frequent conception, longer reproductive lives — captive-rat generation figures cited) more than offset high newborn mortality — paradox of ill health + high infant mortality with compensating fertility, paralleling agriculturalists’ demographic expansion (Ch.2).
- **Zoonoses of the multi-species resettlement camp:** Of ~1,400 known human pathogens, ~800–900 zoonotic; shared-disease counts with domesticates/commensals (poultry 26, rats/mice 32, horses 35, pigs 42, sheep/goats 46, cattle 50, dogs 65 — list Scott flags as now longer); suspected origins measles←rinderpest (sheep/goats), smallpox←camel/cowpox-bearing rodent, influenza←waterfowl ~4.5 kya (Ch.3). Livestock as epidemiological partners of [[sedentism]] and the *[[domus-complex|domus]]* — densify health narrative under [[agricultural-revolution]].
- **Human “self-domestication” parallel (speculative):** Scott proposes humans as products of self-domestication under sedentism/crowding/cereal diets, analogous to other domus species — explicitly labeled speculative (Ch.2). Slavery/bondage later cast as extension of reproductive-control logic of domestication to human captives (Ch.5 speculative note) — see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]].

Source: [[scott-against-the-grain-2017]].

## Bellwood densification — *First Farmers* (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; zooarchaeological claims as of that synthesis.)*

### Criteria and Old vs New World contrast

- Animal domestication invoked when relatively undisputed signs of human control/breeding — e.g. **transport out of homeland** (SW Asian goats/sheep into Europe/Africa; pigs/chickens from China/SE Asia into Pacific). In homeland zones hard to distinguish husbandry from hunting; criteria often long-term bone metric and age/sex trends (attributed).
- **Thesis (Bellwood):** animal domestication is a major determinant of Old vs New World expansionary success differences; New World agriculturalists relied heavily on hunted meat for lack of major domestic meat animals → weaker agricultural/language-family expansion under the [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] (attributed).

### SW Asia / PPN sequence (as of Bellwood 2005)

- By end of [[pre-pottery-neolithic|PPNB]] (probably well before): full complement goat, sheep, cattle, pig added as wild species declined under intensive hunting (Legge & Rowley-Conwy 1987, 2000 via Bellwood).
- **Hallan Çemi** (SE Anatolia): suggested pig management during Younger Dryas, contemporary with late [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] / Abu Hureyra rye experiments — if substantiated, alters animal-domestication perspectives at Levant peripheries (attributed).
- **Zagros PPNB-era:** Ganj Dareh, Tepe Guran, Ali Kosh — goat herding; Ali Kosh claimed sheep domestication; Ali Kosh on dry Deh Luran Plain **outside** wild cereal ranges — agriculture introduced, not local cereal domestication (attributed).
- **Post-8000 BC:** increasing animal domestication + legumes as fodder (Miller 1992) central to “lifting lid” off PPN; origins of specialized sheep/goat pastoralism; Zarins (1990) major pastoralist dispersal from PPN Levant 7th millennium BC into desert/oasis (Palmyra, N Arabia) (attributed).
- **Europe:** genetic data (Troy et al. 2001 via Bellwood) — European cattle from W Asian not European wild stock; wild sheep/goats not native to Europe — full SW Asian animal package with ceramic Neolithic after Cyprus/Crete exceptions (attributed).

### Reverse order: Africa (positions as of Bellwood 2005)

- **Marshall & Hildebrand (2002):** early cattle domestication E Sahara from desire for predictable resources; cattle herding + pottery **preceded** plant domestication; early animal domestication with **mobility not sedentism** — reverse of Near East order (position).
- **Skeptics** (MacDonald, Gifford-Gonzalez; Sherratt 1995): domesticated cattle only ~6000–5000 BC with sheep/goat from SW Asia — coincidence if independent 7th-millennium Sahara cattle (position).
- **South Asia:** local zebu at Mehrgarh; possibly goats — but Bellwood treats Mehrgarh agricultural *economy* as culturally western-linked despite local animal domestications (see [[domestication]]).

Four-way non-identity: domesticates ≠ archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ people. Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Liu & Chen densification — *The Archaeology of China* (2012)

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]], ch. 4, "Domestication of Plants and Animals." aDNA/mtDNA claims date-stamped "(as of Liu & Chen 2012)"; genetic lineage associations are `associated_with`, never identity, per the four-way non-identity rule.)*

### Pig: six criteria, multiple independent centers

Liu & Chen state six criteria used in Chinese archaeology to identify pig domestication: (1) reduced molar/dental size and distorted tooth alignment; (2) age-related culling (1–2 years); (3) a high proportion of pig bones in the faunal assemblage; (4) cultural association (burial with humans); (5) linear enamel hypoplasia (LEH) frequency; (6) stable-isotope dietary overlap with humans (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Applying these criteria, the earliest identified domesticated pigs are at [[jiahu|Jiahu]], Cishan, Dadiwan, and Kuahuqiao, dating ca. 7000–5000 BC (Luo 2007 summary).

Liu & Chen (via Luo 2007) argue archaeological and genetic evidence supports **multiple independent centers of pig domestication** in China — Jiahu (north-central), Kuahuqiao (central Yangzi), and Xinglongwa-culture sites in the Northeast — each showing locally distinct pig morphology, rather than a single point of origin diffusing outward. Coefficient-of-variation analysis of pig M3 tooth length across Neolithic assemblages (Kuahuqiao, Xinglongwa, Xipo: CV 8.1–9.8) shows far greater variability than modern wild-boar populations (Turkey, Henan: CV 3–3.8), consistent with mixed wild/domestic/hybrid populations at early sites (data via Payne & Bull 1988; Ma Xiaolin pers. comm.), reinforcing the continuum framing on [[domestication]].

At Xinglongwa, two complete adult pig skeletons (bound legs) were found in a human burial — the earliest known instance of complete pigs in human burial in China, a practice that later became widespread animal sacrifice in the north China Neolithic (Yang & Liu 1997). Interpretation is disputed (position): Yang & Liu (1997) read it as wild-pig totemism; Luo (2007) and Yuan & Yang (in press) argue instead for a special human-pig relationship distinguishing pigs from other wild animals. At Xipo (middle Yangshao, 4000–3500 BC) 84% of identifiable faunal bones are pig, predominantly domesticated; Liu & Chen (via Ma 2005) suggest pig-rearing intensification there was linked to ritual feasting for social competition.

### Dog: Canis lupus chanco ancestry, Nanzhuangtou

Chinese domestic dog (*Canis familiaris*) is believed to derive from the Chinese wolf, *Canis lupus chanco* (Olsen & Olsen 1977). The earliest reported Chinese domestic dog is from **Nanzhuangtou**, Hebei (ca. 10,000 cal BP), identified by shortened mandibular dental length relative to wolf (Yuan & Li 2010) (as of Liu & Chen 2012) — `associated_with` the wolf lineage, not asserted as a clean species-replacement event. Complete dog skeletons were intentionally buried in cemeteries and near houses at [[jiahu|Jiahu]] (ca. 7000 BC) and at the bottoms of pits at Cishan (ca. 6000 BC); dogs were also commonly consumed as food in domestic refuse elsewhere (Dadiwan, burnt bones; Kangjia, fragmented consumed bones). Liu & Chen note dogs appear to have become sacrificial offerings associated with human burials in the late Neolithic, especially in the lower Yellow River region, a practice later routinized in Shang ritual (Gao & Shao 1986; Yuan & Flad 2005).

### Sheep, goat, and cattle: Near Eastern origin, introduced via distinct lineages

Sheep and goats were originally domesticated in the Fertile Crescent (Zeder et al. 2006). Ancient mtDNA from Chinese sheep (2500–1500 BC) shows lineage A predominant (95.5%) with a Near Eastern lineage B present only in trace amounts — read by Liu & Chen as a distinct East Asian lineage alongside limited Near Eastern introduction, `associated_with` rather than identical to the Near Eastern domestic population (Cai et al. 2007, 2011) (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

Domestic taurine cattle trace to the 8th-millennium-BC Near East; zebu (indicine) cattle to ca. 7000 BC in the Indus Valley (Bradley & Magee 2006). Modern Chinese cattle breeds show northern (taurine), southern (zebu), and central (taurine-zebu hybrid) mtDNA zones, indicating introduction via separate northern and southern routes rather than local domestication (Cai, X. et al. 2007).

### Water buffalo: no indigenous domestication

No evidence supports indigenous domestication of the Chinese swamp buffalo (*Bubalus mephistopheles*). Ancient DNA (6000–1600 BC, Wei River valley) shows clear separation from modern domestic swamp buffalo, challenging the earlier view that buffalo domestication was linked to Neolithic Yangzi rice cultivation (Yang, D. et al. 2008) (as of Liu & Chen 2012) — a case where the genetic evidence directly overturned an older archaeological inference.

### Horse: late-2nd-millennium steppe introduction, contested locally

Osteoarchaeological consensus (Levine et al. 2003) places earliest horse domestication in Central Asia ca. 5,000 years ago; most archaeologists hold that horses were introduced to China from the steppe only in the late 2nd millennium BC (Flad et al. 2007 et al.). Genetic data (mtDNA/Y-chromosome) complicate this picture with possible local Przewalski-mare admixture — whether local wild-horse domestication occurred in the Ordos region remains an open question (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

### Chicken: disputed early claim

mtDNA analysis (834 domestic chickens, 66 red jungle fowl) suggests domestic chicken (*Gallus gallus domesticus*) derives from red jungle fowl with **multiple independent domestication events** across South/Southeast Asia — Yunnan, south/southwest China, and the Indian subcontinent (Liu, Y.-P. et al. 2006) (as of Liu & Chen 2012). The earliest claimed Chinese chicken remains, from Cishan, Hebei (ca. 6000 BC), have "not been accepted by many archaeologists" per Liu & Chen (position); better-supported evidence is chicken bone at Kangjia, Shaanxi (2500–2000 BC) and clay chicken figurines at Shijiahe-culture sites (2500–2000 BC).

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Chs. 3–4, 5, 7–8; genetics **(as of Barker 2006)**.)*

- **Method: process not event.** Barker stresses 1960s criteria (size diminution, bone morphology, herd mortality) are all problematic: Holocene size reduction affects non-livestock; young-animal bias can result from selective hunting as well as herding; animal husbandry must be studied as process (Payne 1972b). Sieving is non-negotiable (Sitagroi: pick-and-shovel biased to cattle while sheep/goat actually dominated). Multi-proxy house-byres (phosphates, footprints, milk-strainer pottery; Thayngen Weier dung/puparia/leaf fodder) can be strong when integrated (attributed).
- **Modern DNA multi-origin patterns (as of Barker 2006).** Suggested: cattle independently SW Asia, India, probably Africa; pigs multiple times across Eurasia; two sheep pathways from two mouflon; three goat domestications; two water buffalo; multiple horse on Eurasian steppes; alpaca descends from vicuña (Kadwell et al. 2001). Caveat: hard to separate data from archaeological-model expectations; aDNA needs dual-lab replication against contamination (attributed).
- **SW Asia sequence (as Barker reports).** PPNA Levantine people still hunters not herders (gazelle principal; possible Wadi Faynan 16 domestic goats). First widespread sheep/goat herding at beginning of PPNB in Zagros/Taurus (Ganj Dareh, Ali Kosh, Çayönü) then rapid spread (Çatalhöyük penning, Jericho, ‘Ain Ghazal, Beidha). Cattle and pigs domestic only **later** PPNB (N Levant / S-central Anatolia 7th millennium BC; size-diminution evidence debated). Early Çatalhöyük: only dogs/sheep/goats domestic at start. Secondary products (milk, cheese, wool, traction) and region-wide agricultural reliance more Pottery Neolithic (Moore; Sherratt) (attributed).
- **Gazelle not herded; Barbary sheep managed.** Natufian/PPNA gazelle = hunting not herding (Legge & Rowley-Conwy). Haua Fteah Barbary-sheep “herding” rejected (Klein & Scott). Uan Afuda (Tadrart Acacus): stall-feeding of **wild** Barbary sheep ≥500 years before local domestic cattle/sheep — “wrong animal, wrong place” relative to PPNB models (de Lernia via Barker) (attributed).
- **Independent African and South Asian cattle (as of Barker 2006).** Loftus et al. 1994 / Hanotte et al. 2002 / Bradley & Loftus 2000: African taurine so different they diverged perhaps ~20,000 years ago and were domesticated independently in Africa (Sahara and Nile equally likely arenas); Indian *Bos namadicus* / zebu separate domestication in South Asia; zebu later introduced to East Africa perhaps 2nd millennium BC. Mehrgarh: Meadow — fully domestic goats from outset; cattle and sheep still being domesticated (size diminution) (attributed).
- **Americas.** Dog only important domestic for most American farmers; llama/alpaca/guinea pig restricted to Andes (attributed).
- **Identity / package caution.** Domestic stock, pottery, crops, and iron often appear **separately** in Africa — not a single colonist package (Barker’s Bantu critique). Cross-links: [[natufian-culture]], [[mehrgarh]], [[bantu-expansion]], [[pastoralism]], [[domestication]].
