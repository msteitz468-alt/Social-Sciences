# Prehistoric Intoxicants

## Definition as Coined

**Prehistoric intoxicants** here names the analytical set of **alcoholic drinks** and **psychoactive plant substances** reconstructed for later prehistoric Europe, especially as developed by [[sherratt-andrew|Andrew Sherratt]] in “Cups that Cheered” (1987) and “Sacred and Profane Substances” (1991), collected in *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]*. Alcohol is treated as a **social lubricant** and hospitality medium; narcotics as part of sacred/profane consumption regimes — not as moral pathology.

Sherratt links alcohol’s European spread to the broader **[[secondary-products-revolution|secondary products]]** / fourth-millennium package (with plough, horse, wool): a second-generation farming introduction rather than a Mesolithic universal.

## Semantic History

- **Ch.15 (1987):** inter-regional liquid-serving pottery from a “Linkup” phase ~3700 BC through Baden → Globular Amphorae → Corded Ware → Bell Beakers; SE European **wine** model with temperate **mead/beer/mixed** substitutes; speculative cord decoration ↔ cannabis association; beakers as hospitality / proto-symposium gear.
- **Ch.16 (1991):** opium (*Papaver*, Bandkeramik contexts; Murciélagos capsules) and cannabis (e.g. Gurbănești pipe-cups, polypod braziers); Chassey *brûle-parfums*; TRB collared-flask/poppy hypothesis; communal sacred consumption (e.g. Nietleben; Nitriansky Hrádok death-pit) shifting toward Corded/Beaker **personalised drinking** as **profanation** (not simple secularisation); Balfarg Grooved Ware residues (henbane + honey + barley/oats) as of his cited analyses.
- **Residue cases** he cites: Ashgrove (honey), Egtved (mixed mead/ale/fruit), Hochdorf (pure mead).
- **1997 SPR notes:** many “milk” vessel forms with metal models reassigned as more probably alcoholic.

## Operationalizations

- Vessel morphology (cups, beakers, collared flasks, braziers, pipe-cups)
- Chemical/botanical residues (as of studies available to Sherratt 1987–91)
- Contextual association with graves, death-pits, and communal deposits
- Iconography and decorative programmes (cords, etc.) — highly interpretive

## Applications

- Feasting and alliance politics in later Neolithic–Bronze Age Europe
- Corded Ware / Bell Beaker drinking assemblages as culture-historical markers **without** equating pottery styles with peoples or languages ([[pots-and-peoples-debate]])
- Elite “symposium kit” extended into metal vessels (Ch.17 with Taylor)

## Critiques and Limitations

- Many plant–vessel equations remain **hypotheses** (TRB poppy/flask; cord–cannabis).
- Residue methods and contamination standards have advanced since 1991 — treat empirical cases as of their analytical date.
- Do not collapse intoxicant use with [[secondary-products-revolution]] as a single causal engine; Sherratt bundles them historically, not as logical identity.
