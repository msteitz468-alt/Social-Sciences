# Ghost Population

## Definition as Coined

A **ghost population** is a human population that no longer exists in unmixed
form and has not been directly sampled (ancient or modern), but whose past
existence and ancestry contribution can be **reconstructed statistically** from
the bits of genetic material it left in present-day or later ancient people
(Reich 2018, Ch. 4, "Humanity's Ghosts"). It is an inferred entity, defined by
its genetic footprint rather than by any bones, artifacts, or self-description.

## Semantic History

The idea generalizes the older population-genetics notion of unsampled
"admixing sources" into a central working object of the aDNA era. As whole-genome
[[admixture-statistics]] and admixture graphs matured (2010s), reconstructing
ghosts became routine, and named ghosts — [[ancestral-north-indians]] (ANI),
[[ancestral-south-indians]] (ASI), the [[ancient-north-eurasians]] (ANE) — became
load-bearing units of prehistoric narrative.

## Operationalizations

A ghost is inferred when sampled populations behave, under *f*- and *D*-statistics
or admixture-graph fitting, as mixtures of sources that include a component
matching no sampled group. Its properties (divergence, admixture proportion) are
estimated; sometimes a later-sampled ancient individual (e.g. the Mal'ta boy for
ANE) partially "fills in" a previously ghostly source.

## Applications

Reconstructing the ancestral streams of Europeans and South Asians; the ANE
contribution to both West Eurasians and Native Americans; unsampled archaic
contributors in Africa. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018|Reich (2018)]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Ghosts are **model-dependent**: their existence and parameters depend on the
reference populations and graph assumed, and an unmodeled ghost can distort other
inferences. A ghost population is a *genetic* construct — it must not be silently
equated with a language, an archaeological culture, or a self-conscious people
(the four-way non-identity). Use `associated_with`, not identity, when relating a
ghost to a named culture.
