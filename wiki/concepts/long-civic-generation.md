# Long Civic Generation

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Putnam 2000.)* Label for the highly civic birth cohorts — roughly those born **1910–1940**, core **1925–1930** — whose replacement by less engaged cohorts he argues is the largest single driver of late-twentieth-century U.S. [[civic-disengagement]]. Book dedication names personal exemplars of this generation.

## Semantic History

Built from standard life-cycle vs generational analysis in survey research; "long civic generation" is Putnam's memorable name for the mid-century high-engagement cohorts. Related: "greatest generation" popular discourse — not identical and more hagiographic.

## Operationalizations

Cohort charts holding age constant: newspaper reading, memberships, voting, trust, volunteering by birth year (e.g. Fig 71 in *Bowling Alone*). Education often controlled.

## Applications

- Ch 14–15 causal story (~half of civic decline attributed to generational succession).
- Explains delayed visibility of disengagement (high-civic cohorts still large in 1960s–70s).
- "Indian summer" of volunteering among older cohorts in the 1990s.

## Critiques and Limitations

Cohort labels risk reification; WWII/civic experience mechanisms are partly speculative; later cohorts may form different civic styles not captured as "low engagement" ([[bowling-alone-debate]]). Causal claim remains attributed.
