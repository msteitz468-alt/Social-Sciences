# Virtual and actual social identity

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-stigma-1963|Stigma]]* (1963), [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] argued that society establishes means of categorizing persons and attributes felt ordinary for each category; social settings establish who is likely to be met there. First appearances let us anticipate category and attributes — **social identity** (preferred to "social status" because personal attributes such as honesty are involved as well as structural ones such as occupation) (attributed).

Anticipations become normative expectations and righteously presented demands. We typically notice them only when fulfillment is in question. The character imputed in potential retrospect is **virtual social identity**; the category and attributes that could in fact be proved constitute **actual social identity** (attributed).

[[stigma|Stigma]] is a special discrepancy between virtual and actual social identity of a deeply discrediting kind. Other discrepancies reclassify without stigma or raise estimation (attributed).

Ch 3 brackets social and personal identity together against **ego** (felt) identity — subjective sense of situation, continuity, and character (drawing on Erikson; attributed). Analytic division of labor: social identity → stigmatization; personal identity → information control; ego identity → feelings about stigma and advice received (attributed).

## Semantic History

Goffman 1963 coinage within interactionist identity language. Textbook stigma often drops the virtual/actual pair and keeps only "discrediting attribute."

## Operationalizations

Conceptual pair for mixed-contact analysis; not a psychometric construct in Goffman.

## Applications

Foundation of the stigma definition; information control when actual identity is discreditable; biography "fixes" identity across role multiplicity (Ch 2).

## Critiques and Limitations

"We" of the expecting normals is built into the virtual side; later work stresses power over who sets category stereotypes.
