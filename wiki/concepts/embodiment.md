# Embodiment

## Definition as Coined

**Embodiment**, in [[medical-anthropology|medical anthropology]] and social epidemiology, names the
process by which social conditions become biologically incorporated — how "fundamentally social forces
and processes come to be embodied as biological events" ([[farmer-paul|Farmer]], attributed). The body
is treated not as a pre-social given on which society acts from outside, but as the register in which
social position is literally written: as differential exposure, disease, disability, and death. The term
is used across several traditions — [[the-mindful-body|Scheper-Hughes and Lock's "mindful body"]],
Margaret Lock's "local biologies," Nancy Krieger's "ecosocial" epidemiology — that share the claim that
biology and biography are not separable.

## Semantic History

The concept crystallized in the 1980s–90s as medical anthropology and critical epidemiology sought
language for how "the social gets under the skin." This wiki's coverage enters through
[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s usage in *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and
Inequalities]]* (1999), where embodiment is the mechanism that connects **[[structural-violence]]** to
disease. Farmer's Chapter 10 states the book's whole argument as "Biological Expressions of Social
Inequalities," and closes on "an awesome responsibility — to prevent social inequalities from being
embodied as adverse health outcomes." His list of what becomes embodied is deliberately concrete:
"racism, gender inequality, poverty, war, migration, colonial heritage, coups d'état, and
structural-adjustment programs... become embodied as increased risk."

## Operationalizations

Embodiment is made tractable through mechanism-tracing rather than a single index. Farmer's paradigm is
the [[tuberculosis|tuberculosis]] chain: poverty and racism raise the odds of *infection* (crowded,
airless housing — the "lung blocks"), of *progression* to active disease (malnutrition, HIV,
addiction compromising cell-mediated immunity), and of poor *outcome* (restricted access to therapy) —
three biological transitions, each socially patterned. In social epidemiology, embodiment is
operationalized through gradients (the socioeconomic-status/health relationship, "pervasive and
persistent" across virtually every health measure) and through natural experiments where removing a
social barrier collapses a biological disparity (the "access" finding in AIDS survival).

## Applications

- **[[tuberculosis]]** and **[[hiv-aids]]** — the embodiment of poverty as infectious-disease risk and
  mortality is the running argument of *Infections and Inequalities*.
- **[[structural-violence]]** — embodiment is the bridge concept: structural violence names the social
  arrangement; embodiment names how it becomes biology.
- **[[the-mindful-body]]** and **[[everyday-violence]]** — cognate framings from the critical-medical
  wing of the subfield.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's strength — refusing a clean nature/society split — is also the source of its main hazard:
if pressed loosely it can imply that all biology is "really" social, obscuring genuinely biological
mechanisms (drug resistance, viral virulence) that embodiment analyses actually depend on. Farmer's own
corrective is the "Luddite trap" warning: recognizing the social roots of disease must *not* be taken to
devalue clinical technology, since equitable access to that technology is exactly what closes embodied
disparities. Farmer's specific embodiment claims are recorded in attributed voice; the general
principle (social conditions register biologically) rests on convergent evidence across epidemiology and
anthropology and is stated more plainly.

## Gender, performativity, and the produced body (2010)

*(Attributed — [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The reader develops embodiment as central to the archaeology of gender
and [[personhood]]. Rosemary Joyce ("Girling the Girl and Boying the Boy") applies Judith Butler's
**performativity** to Mesoamerica, arguing gendered adulthood is *produced* through lifecycle ritual
and bodily modification rather than expressing a natural sex (see [[joyce-rosemary]]). Ingold treats
embodiment as "a movement of incorporation rather than inscription" — forms generated in process, not
stamped from a template. The Comaroffs' **partible/fragmented** Tswana self is likewise an embodiment
claim (different facets shown to different audiences). See [[feminist-archaeology]],
[[chaine-operatoire]].

## Reading the Past (2003) — no natural body, and the produced sex/gender boundary (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson, Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., ch. 6.)* Hodder & Hutson report [[mauss-marcel|Marcel Mauss]]'s "techniques of the body" (1935) as an early statement that bodily functions such as walking, swimming, and sleeping must be learned and therefore there is no natural adult body — the essay in which Mauss coined the term "habitus." They further report the argument, associated with Judith Butler and with Thomas Laqueur's history of a one-sex model giving way to a two-sex model, that even biological sex is not simply "natural" but produced through discourse — a claim they hold does not deny biological reality but shows that non-empirical factors determine which bodily differences come to count. On this basis they distinguish an "archaeology of the body," which treats the body as an object or sign of culture, from an "archaeology of embodiment" (after Csordas), which treats the body as the subject of culture.
