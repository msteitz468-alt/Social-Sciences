# Liminality

> Concept named by [[van-gennep-arnold|Arnold van Gennep]] (the *limen* or margin of a
> rite of passage) and developed into a general theory of social process by
> [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] in the essay "Betwixt and Between: The Liminal Period in
> Rites de Passage" (*[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]*, 1967) and
> in *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Turner.)* **Liminality** is the condition of the ritual subject during the
marginal phase of a [[rites-of-passage|rite of passage]] — the phase during which "the state
of the ritual subject (the 'passenger') is ambiguous; he passes through a realm that has few
or none of the attributes of the past or coming state." Liminal entities are "neither here
nor there; they are betwixt and between the positions assigned and arrayed by law, custom,
convention, and ceremonial."

Turner's central claims (all attributed):

- **Structural invisibility.** The liminal persona is "structurally, if not physically,
  'invisible'" — no longer classified under prior categories, not yet under the new ones;
  society's secular definitions have no slot for a "not-boy-not-man." Hence liminal persons
  are commonly secluded (the Ndembu term for the liminal period is a locative meaning
  "seclusion site," *kung'ula*).
- **Biological symbolism of the unclassified.** Liminality is expressed through imagery of
  death, decomposition, and catabolism (no longer classified) and of the womb, gestation,
  and the newborn (not yet classified): "the neophytes are neither living nor dead from one
  aspect, and both living and dead from another."
- **Pollution and danger.** Following [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]'s
  *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (1966) — that pollution "is a reaction
  to protect cherished principles and categories from contradiction" (see
  [[ritual-pollution]]) — Turner holds that what resists clear classification is treated as
  polluting; liminal personae "nearly always and everywhere are regarded as polluting." Turner
  refines Douglas by distinguishing the *statics* of pollution (ambiguous states) from the
  *dynamics* (the essentially unstructured character of transition itself), calling liminality
  "the essentially unstructured (which is at once destructured and prestructured)."
- **Reduction and leveling.** Liminal persons "have nothing" — no status, property, insignia,
  rank, or kinship position; they are a "tabula rasa," "human *prima materia*," often
  symbolically sexless or bisexual, ground down to be "fashioned anew."
- **Authority and equality.** The liminal group has its own simple structure: total authority
  of instructors over neophytes, and total equality *among* neophytes — the germ of
  **[[communitas]]**.
- **The sacred and the *sacra*.** Following Jane Harrison, Turner distinguishes three modes
  by which sacred things are communicated in the liminal phase: *exhibitions* ("what is
  shown"), *actions* ("what is done"), and *instructions* ("what is said"). The Ndembu
  "mystery of the three rivers" (white/red/black) is a paradigm liminal *sacrum*.

## Semantic History

- **van Gennep 1909:** names the *limen* as the middle phase of transition rites.
- **Turner 1964/1967 ("Betwixt and Between"):** liminality as the site of anti-structure;
  the pollution and structural-invisibility analysis; sacra typology.
- **Turner 1969 (*The Ritual Process*):** liminality paired with **[[communitas]]**; a long
  binary-opposition table (transition/state, communitas/structure, equality/inequality,
  anonymity/nomenclature, nakedness/clothing, silence/speech, sacredness/secularity,
  humility/pride). Turner distinguishes the **liminality of status elevation** (the neophyte
  humbled before being raised — "for an individual to go higher on the status ladder, he must
  go lower than the status ladder") from the **liminality of status reversal** (structurally
  low groups temporarily granted ritual authority — see [[communitas]] and
  [[ritual-of-rebellion]]).
- **Institutionalized liminality.** In complex societies, Turner argues, what was a
  transitional quality in tribal ritual can become a *permanent state* — monastic and mendicant
  orders (the Rule of St. Benedict; "Instead of the seclusion lodge, we have the church"),
  and, he suggests, the modern countercultural "liminars" (hippies) who assume the attributes
  of the structurally inferior to achieve communitas.
- **Afterlife:** liminality migrated far beyond anthropology into ritual studies, theatre and
  performance studies (via Turner's own later work and Richard Schechner), literary theory,
  history, and religious studies — often loosened into a loose metaphor for any "in-between"
  condition.
- **Reflexive turn (Rabinow 1977):** [[rabinow-paul|Paul Rabinow]] applied liminality to the
  *fieldwork* situation itself in *[[rabinow-reflections-morocco-1977|Reflections on Fieldwork
  in Morocco]]* — arguing that systematic questioning pushes the informant into "a liminal,
  self-conscious world between cultures … one could almost say it is 'unnatural'," and that
  fieldwork is "a process of intersubjective construction of liminal modes of communication"
  between anthropologist and informant (see [[reflexive-fieldwork]]). This transposes Turner's
  ritual-subject liminality onto the epistemology of the ethnographic encounter.

## Operationalizations

Turner reads the Ndembu **Mukanda** circumcision seclusion as paradigmatic (attributed,
fieldwork 1950–1954): novices stripped of clothing at the *mukoleku* gateway, former names
discarded and all called *mwadyi* ("novice"), food shared without favor to chiefs' sons,
a circumcision song declaring "even if your child is a chief's son, tomorrow he will be like
a slave." He compares this "stripping and leveling" explicitly to Goffman's **total
institutions** (*[[goffman-erving|Asylums]]*, 1962) and to Benedictine monastic leveling.

## Applications

- [[ndembu]] Mukanda (boys) and Nkang'a (girls); the Kanongesha chief installation.
- The Swazi king's black-painted seclusion during the Incwala (via Hilda Kuper).
- Monasticism; millenarian movements; the 1960s counterculture (all in *The Ritual Process*).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The extension from tribal ritual to complex, large-scale societies drew the charge of
  overgeneralization — that "liminality" and "communitas" lose analytic purchase outside the
  small-scale settings in which they were defined ([[communitas-scale-debate]]).
- As the term diffused, it was frequently stretched into a vague synonym for "in-between,"
  losing Turner's specific processual and structural content.
