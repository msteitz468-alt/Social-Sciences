# Symboling

## Definition as Coined

[[white-leslie-a|Leslie A. White]] defines **symboling** as the ability "freely and arbitrarily to originate and bestow meaning upon a thing or event, and, correspondingly, the ability to grasp and appreciate such meaning." Meanings so bestowed (holy water, prices, Sabbath, incest categories, kinship classifications) cannot be grasped by the senses alone. Articulate speech is the most important expression of symboling. Culture is the extrasomatic continuum *dependent upon* symboling; man is defined by the capacity to symbol (*[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|The Evolution of Culture]]*, Ch. 1).

White insists the human/subhuman difference is of **kind**, not degree — against Darwin and later gradualist psychologists: no intermediate stages for holy water, sin, uncles, or prices.

## Semantic History

White's 1940 essay "The Symbol" was widely reprinted; *Evolution of Culture* restates it as the foundation of culturology. Adjacent but not identical: Mead/Blumer **[[significant-symbol]]** (interactionist; gesture completed in the other); semiotic and symbolic anthropology (meaning systems without White's evolutionary energy program). Cognitive and primatological work since has contested the sharp "kind not degree" break White required.

## Operationalizations

Not a quantitative measure; a threshold criterion for culture-bearing. Used to partition ideological, sociological, attitudinal, and technological components of culture as all speech/symbol-dependent (White Ch. 1).

## Applications

Origin of culture; critique of animal "mind" gradualism; foundation for treating culture as extrasomatic and culturologically autonomous.

## Critiques and Limitations

Primatology and comparative cognition report graded communicative and proto-cultural capacities; philosophers of mind dispute nonsensory "meaning" criteria; linguistic anthropology does not need White's evolutionary package to study symbols. Keep White's claim attributed.

## Mithen — visual symbols and cognitive fluidity (1996)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], [[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996]].)* Creating/reading visual symbols requires three processes that Early Humans possessed in *different* domains (technical form-imposition; social intentional communication; natural-history attribution of meaning to tracks) and only combined under [[cognitive-fluidity]]. Five properties of visual symbols listed; earliest UP art (Hohlenstein-Stadel, Chauvet) already high skill — not gradual scribbles. Outside vs inside meanings (Aboriginal/San analogy flagged). Personal adornment ~40–30 kya as technical–social integration.

## Tattersall — paleoanthropological symbolic leap (2012)

*(Attributed — [[tattersall-ian]], [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]].)* Without using White’s term “symboling,” Tattersall’s program converges on a similar **kind-not-degree** break: modern humans reorganize the world into mental symbols recombined endlessly; this capacity is lacking in great apes, in closest extinct relatives, and even in earliest anatomically modern humans (attributed). Methodologically he insists stone technology is a weak proxy — confident inference requires overt ornaments/images (Blombos engraved ochre and beads as early firm African signals; Ice Age cave art as first overwhelming evidence). Language is the preferred cultural stimulus that activates biologically prepared but previously fallow potential ([[exaptation]] lag after ~200 ka anatomy — as of his 2012 chronology). Cross-link [[behavioral-modernity]] for the full archaeological argument; keep White’s culturological and Tattersall’s paleoanthropological uses distinct (same phenomenon-cluster, different disciplinary programs).
