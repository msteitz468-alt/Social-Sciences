# Pompeii Premise

Name for treating archaeological deposits as **frozen moments of ongoing life** — complete systemic inventories interrupted in place — so spatial pattern maps directly onto past behavior. Anchors the Binford–Schiffer dispute over how the [[archaeological-record]] relates to living systems. Full charter statement in *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* Ch. 20 (Schiffer 1985a).

## Definition as Coined

### Positions (record both; do not adjudicate)

- **Binford’s attribution (as Schiffer reports Binford 1981a):** Schiffer’s views predicated on the assumption that inferences are possible only from Pompeii-like de facto refuse assemblages; transformation theory implies sites short of Pompeii preservation are information-poor (and, Binford claimed, popular with CRM as a write-off rationale) (attributed via Schiffer 1985a/b; 1995 Ch. 19–20).
- **Schiffer’s rejection:** Position he does not hold — much of *Behavioral Archeology* (1976) rests on **secondary refuse** analysis; sites formed other ways yield important, sometimes unique information; CRM stance is “There is no site that cannot provide relevant information for some … problem” (attributed, Schiffer 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20; cf. 1975c).
- **Schiffer’s “real” Pompeii premise:** The real premise — implicit in analyses that proceed without ascertaining [[formation-processes|formation-process]] effects — is that one is dealing with Pompeii-like preservation: the archaeologist can treat **house-floor assemblages at any site as if they were Pompeii-like systemic inventories** (attributed, Schiffer 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20). He holds this default underlay early New Archaeology floor analyses and much later work (Hill, Longacre, Jorgensen Table Rock, Ciolek-Torrello Grasshopper late-abandonment class, etc., as he surveys them).

[[schiffer-michael-b|Schiffer]]’s [[formation-processes|formation-process]] program rejects naive Pompeii readings of most prehistoric deposits, while modeling transforms between [[systemic-context]] and [[archaeological-context]]. In Schiffer 1987/1995, **Pompeii ≈ the extreme de facto refuse case**: nearly complete systemic inventories left at abandonment; most sites are depleted by curate behavior, scavenging, draw-down, lateral cycling, collecting/looting, and maintenance (attributed, Ch. 4, 12; 1995 Ch. 20).

## Semantic History

Teaching shorthand for “don’t read floors as activity maps without formation-process control.” Rapid-burial catastrophe sites remain real limiting cases, not the general model. At Broken K, Schiffer argues abandonment was **not** catastrophic Pompeii despite substantial de facto refuse (attributed, 1987 Ch. 12; 1995 Ch. 21). Shared ground with Binford: most sites are not little Pompeiis (attributed, Schiffer 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20 conclusion).

## Operationalizations

- Contrast de facto refuse and abandonment assemblages with secondary refuse and cleaned floors; model clean-up and McKellar residual primary refuse.
- Eight major process families producing floor-contact artifacts (use-period primary/loss; abandonment-period refuse; de facto refuse; ritual; squatters; postabandonment secondary refuse; collapse/aeolian contributions; disturbance) (attributed, Schiffer 1985a / 1995 Ch. 20).
- **Depletion processes** relative to systemic inventory: curate behavior (incl. intrasettlement); lateral cycling; draw-down; scavenging (can also enrich last deposits); collecting/looting — compare de facto refuse to ethnoarchaeographic, catastrophic, or secondary-refuse reference inventories (attributed, same).
- Ethnoarchaeological site structure (Binford Nunamiut) as anti-Pompeii empirical program under different branding.

## Applications

Critique of New Archaeology social inferences from room floors ([[hill-broken-k-pueblo-study]]); general method training; Hohokam de facto house samples as chronological resources when pathway is controlled; Foote Canyon exercise on gradual abandonment / curate-priority index (heuristic only; attributed, Schiffer 1985a).

## Critiques and Limitations

The label is partisan. Schiffer’s “distortion” language was itself accused of smuggling a Pompeii-like original. Careful formation-process work and middle-range ethnoarchaeology both oppose naive Pompeii reading while disagreeing on theory language.
