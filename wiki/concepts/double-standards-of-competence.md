# Double Standards of Competence

**Double standards of competence** are stricter standards of evidence applied to members of lower-status categories to demonstrate ability, relative to higher-status peers. Developed in expectation-states research (Foschi); used by Ridgeway (2011) to explain gendered evaluation bias.

## Definition as Coined

When gender operates as a diffuse status characteristic, performance expectations favor men on many tasks; observers then require **more proof** from women to infer high ability and may more readily attribute men’s success to ability and women’s to luck or effort. Ridgeway cites this pattern in workplace evaluation and promotion (e.g., Lyness and Heilman 2006: promoted women had higher prior evaluations than promoted men — as reported).

## Semantic History

Technical EST concept, not ordinary “double standard” morality. Distinct from moral double standards in sexual behavior (Ridgeway’s college hook-up discussion is related socially but is a different mechanism).

## Operationalizations

Laboratory standard-of-evidence designs; field studies linking evaluation thresholds to promotion; audit experiments.

## Applications

Glass-ceiling evaluation; STEM assessment; leadership suitability judgments under status incongruity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Scope conditions of EST (task orientation, collective orientation) may not hold in all workplaces; conscious anti-bias procedures can dampen but not automatically eliminate double standards.
