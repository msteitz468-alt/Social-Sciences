# Qhapaq Ucha (Capacocha)

## Definition as Coined

**Qhapaq ucha** (also *capacocha*, *qhapaq hucha*; OCR *ghapaq ucha*) is the Inca state ritual that, as of [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002), “epitomized imperial ideology.” Nominally it paid homage to the Creator and Sun, but it also reinforced the legitimacy of the Inca lineage, [[cuzco|Cuzco]]’s social structure, and pan-Andean shrine networks. Human children—the most celebrated Inca offerings—were “apparently rarely consecrated and were mostly associated with Sun worship and important events in the life of the Inca.” Two standout rituals with human sacrifice were *qhapaq ucha* and *itu* (the latter for times of great need—drought, epidemic, earthquake—or as a positive celebration such as a provincial lord whose daughter became the ruler’s wife).

Alias note: Spanish and English literature commonly write **capacocha**; D'Altroy’s form is *qhapaq ucha*.

## Semantic History

D'Altroy reports that Incas did sacrifice humans to gods and ancestors, but the scale never approached Aztec Mesoamerica’s tens of thousands; more commonly practices demanded immense quantities of goods and llamas. Occasions for human sacrifice included ceremonies of great solemnity dedicated to the Sun; the ascendancy, well-being, and death of an emperor; and military victory. Specific Cuzco *wak'a* (Huanacauri, Pachakuti’s birthplace, shrines of his wife) could receive human sacrifices; Cobo reported that **31 of 332** capital-area shrines were owed human sacrifice. Calamities (earthquakes, eclipses, epidemics) and critical divinations also triggered offerings. Installation rites (Cobo): two hundred children aged four to ten among other sacrifices at a new emperor’s installation; Betanzos’s epic of Pachakuti’s funeral design calls for a thousand boys and girls buried where he slept and enjoyed himself—figures D'Altroy treats within saga/source constraints.

Rationale (as chroniclers explained, per D'Altroy): send humanity’s best to join Creator, Sun, and other deities, and to accompany rulers into death. Cobo reasoned they would not sacrifice their own children without expecting reward—different logic from Aztec endless blood-debt repayment. High-elevation shrines (following Reinhard) meshed religious and political goals: homage to mountain deities, and a context for Incas to interpose themselves as mediators between society and the supernatural, claiming cultural foundations of residents—an analog to abduction of ancestor statues.

## Operationalizations

**Recruitment and scale.** Victims were usually beautiful boys and girls recruited empire-wide; war prisoners were sometimes executed for Sun and Inca power. On rare dramatic occasions (death or investiture of a ruler) victims could number in the thousands; Andean chroniclers’ maximal figures of 1,000–4,000 “may have been more symbol than reality,” never approaching Aztec extremes.

**Cuzco ceremony (Molina’s best description, at monarch’s ascension).** All empire towns sent one or two ~10-year-old boys and girls plus fine cloth, camelids, and gold/silver/shell figurines. Children dressed in finery and matched as married couples. Sacrifices to Wiraqocha for health, long life, peace, perennial victory; some children strangled, others’ hearts torn out with blood painted on image faces; similar sacrifices to Thunder, Mother Earth, Mother Cloud, Huanacauri; other offerings to all Cuzco *wak'a*.

**Empire-wide logistics (Molina).** Priests dispatched to the four quarters with sacrificial items for *wak'a* by rank. Parties left the city in **straight-line paths**, deviating for neither mountain nor ravine, transferring burdens; children who could walk did so, others carried by mothers; Inca and flocks used the royal road. Gold, silver, and shell interred at each provincial shrine; children only at the most exalted locations; processions continued to markers at the edge of the civilized world ~500 leagues (~2,000 km). *Khipukamayuq* and *villcakamayuq* kept accounts; knowledgeable provincials were required so ceremonies could be conducted correctly as they changed over time. Straight paths parallel [[zeqe-system|zeq'e]] logic.

**High-altitude archaeology (as of D'Altroy 2002 / Reinhard).** Sites include Llullaillaco (Argentina) at 6,739 m—world’s highest archaeological site—with one extraordinarily well-preserved and two lightning-damaged child mummies plus gold, silver, and *Spondylus* figurines (Reinhard 1999). Frozen bodies of sacrificed children, adolescents, and adults of both sexes known from Cerro el Plomo and Copiapó (Chile); Cerro del Toro, Aconcagua, and Llullaillaco (Argentina); Nevado Ampato and Mt. Ausangate (Peru); plus Isla de la Plata off Ecuador—probably *qhapaq ucha*. Nevado Ampato “Juanita”: teenage girl in rich textiles; most likely walked voluntarily, struck on the head while drunken; two other children below as a possible symbolically married couple matching chroniclers. Material grammar of deposits: paired human statuettes and llama figurines in gold, silver, *Spondylus*—Reinhard infers homage to male Sun and female Moon plus Mother Sea. All 50+ recorded sites over 5,200 m lie south of 15°S; Reinhard counters that all culturally identifiable remains above that elevation so far are Inca. Summit shrines also appear among >50 peaks with Inca constructions (including Aconcagua).

**Cuzco archaeological check.** Bauer found surface human burial at only three Cuzco *wak'a*—nothing approaching thousands of victims; systematic excavations of Cuzco shrines not yet done as of 2002. Principal high-peak offerings (gold, silver, *Spondylus*, children) match Molina’s account.

## Applications

- **Imperial ideology and landscape claim** across Tawantinsuyu ([[inca-empire]], [[huaca]]).
- **Life-crisis of the ruler:** installation, well-being, death; military victory; related *itu* crisis rites.
- **Zeq'e / four-quarters ritual geography:** straight-path processions, ranked *wak'a*, specialist memory ([[zeqe-system]], [[khipu]]).
- **High mountain mediation:** imperial shrines as political as well as religious claims (Reinhard via D'Altroy).
- **Textile and metal offerings:** children/youths or paired metal statuettes dressed in finery at high-elevation deposits.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Chronicle vs archaeology.** D'Altroy is explicit that archaeological support for sacrifice at chronicle scale in Cuzco was still lacking as of 2002; high peaks better document the practice than capital shrine counts. Maximal victim figures may be symbolic.

**Comparative scale.** Human sacrifice existed but was exceptional relative to goods and camelid offerings; never Aztec-scale mass slaughter.

**Southern bias of extreme-altitude sample.** Debate whether truly high sites are a south Andean phenomenon Incas borrowed, or Inca-driven (Reinhard’s position that identifiable remains above 5,200 m are Inca so far).

**Source character.** Installation and funeral victim counts often rest on Cobo, Betanzos, and Molina—filtered Spanish and Andean voices within D'Altroy’s composite method; treat numbers as attributed ethnohistory, not verified census.
