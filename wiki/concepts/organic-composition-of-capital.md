# Organic Composition of Capital

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), Chapter 25, [[marx-karl|Marx]] defines the **composition of capital** in two senses:

1. **Value-composition** — ratio of [[constant-and-variable-capital|constant capital (c)]] to variable capital (v).
2. **Technical composition** — ratio of mass of means of production to mass of living labour that sets them in motion.

He calls the value-composition **organic composition** insofar as it is determined by the technical composition and mirrors changes in the latter. “Wherever I refer to the composition of capital, without further qualification, its organic composition is always understood.”

**Attributed to Marx.**

## Operationalizations

- Accumulation with rising productivity tends to raise c relative to v (relative diminution of the variable part).
- **Concentration** of capital (growth of individual capitals with accumulation) vs **centralisation** (expropriation of capitalist by capitalist; competition and credit) — both reshape composition and scale.
- Bridge to production of a relative surplus population (→ [[industrial-reserve-army]]).

## Applications

- Core of the [[general-law-of-capitalist-accumulation]].
- Machinery chapters (Part 4) supply the technical mechanism that raises composition.

## Volume 3 Extensions (1894)

In *[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume III]]* (ed. Engels, 1894), Ch. 8, Marx restates the full definitional triple: **technical composition** (ratio of living labour to means of production, in physical/technical terms), **value composition** (ratio of variable to constant capital, in value terms), and **organic composition** as "the value composition, in so far as this is determined by its technical composition and reflects it."

- **Differing compositions across branches as the premise of the transformation problem.** Given a constant rate of surplus-value across spheres, capitals of equal size but differing organic composition produce very different masses of surplus-value and hence differing individual rates of profit — the empirical fact that observed profit rates nonetheless equalize across spheres is the problem Marx's transformation of values into [[price-of-production|prices of production]] (Ch. 9) is built to resolve.
- **Rising composition as the driver of the tendential fall.** In Part Three, Marx argues that a rising organic composition of social capital, at a constant rate of surplus-value, necessarily produces a falling rate of profit — the law he holds no prior economist had correctly derived, attributing their failure to never having properly distinguished constant from variable capital or separated surplus-value from profit. See [[tendential-fall-in-the-rate-of-profit]].
- **Agriculture's historically lower composition.** Marx treats agriculture's comparatively lower organic composition of capital, historically, as a premise of his argument for absolute [[ground-rent]] (Ch. 45) — landed property can obstruct the equalization of profit rates in agriculture, permitting the sale of agricultural products at their value rather than at the (lower) price of production, yielding surplus profit convertible into rent.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Empirical measurement of “organic” composition is disputed.
- Link to long-run profit rate is incomplete without later volumes.
