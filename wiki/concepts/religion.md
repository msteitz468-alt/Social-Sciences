# Religion (as analytical category)

The analytic category "religion" — the assumption that there is a cross-culturally identifiable domain of belief and practice, definable in universal terms — is itself one of the most contested constructs in the social sciences. Its status was transformed by [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]]'s *Genealogies of Religion* (1993), which argued that the category is a historical product of Western Christian history rather than a neutral analytic instrument (see [[definition-of-religion-debate]]).

## Definition as Coined

Tylor (1871) proposed the minimal definition "belief in spiritual beings." [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] ("Religion as a Cultural System," 1966) defined religion as "(1) a system of symbols which act to (2) establish powerful, pervasive, and long-lasting moods and motivations in men by (3) formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and (4) clothing these conceptions with such an aura of factuality that (5) the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic." Asad quotes this definition as the culmination of a long universalizing tendency and takes it as the starting point of his genealogical critique.

## Semantic History

Asad's genealogy (1993) traces the category through several transformations, each attributed here to his argument:

- **Medieval Christendom:** "religion" was not a universal phenomenon but a domain constituted by Church authority — authenticating miracles and relics, authorizing shrines, compiling saints' lives, mandating confession (Fourth Lateran Council, 1215). The term "religious" originally denoted only monastic life under the Rule of St. Benedict (roughly 6th–12th centuries), being extended to nonmonastic orders and lay piety only from the 12th century. Asad cites the 5th-century bishop of Javols — "Nulla est religio in stagno" ("There can be no religion in a swamp") — to illustrate that for medieval Christians religion was a site where universal truth was authoritatively *produced*, not a universal human phenomenon.
- **Early modernity:** Lord Herbert of Cherbury's *De veritate* made the first systematic attempt at a universal, belief-centered definition ("Natural Religion"), a response to the wars of religion and to Europe's expanding awareness of non-Christian traditions (including the Jesuit–Franciscan "quarrel of the rites" over whether Chinese ancestor veneration was "religious"). Locke measured revelation against Natural Religion; Kant (1795) completed the essentialization by distinguishing a single universal "religion itself" from the many contingent "historical confessions" that merely serve as its "vehicles."
- **The modern settlement:** Asad argues the post-Reformation formation of the modern state required the forcible redefinition of religion as private, interior *belief* separated from public power — with early modern "toleration" functioning as an instrument of state consolidation rather than benign pluralism. Religion was thereby constructed as a new historical object: "anchored in personal experience, expressible as belief-statements, dependent on private institutions, and practiced in one's spare time."
- **Anthropological inheritance:** what appears self-evident to anthropologists — religion as symbolic meaning tied to a general order of existence — is, per Asad, this specific Christian history universalized: "From being a concrete set of practical rules attached to specific processes of power and knowledge, religion has come to be abstracted and universalized."

## Operationalizations

Traditions disagree sharply. Tylorian minimal definitions ask about beliefs in spiritual beings; Geertzian definitions ask about symbol systems and their functions; Durkheimian definitions turn on the [[sacred-and-profane|sacred/profane]] division; the sociology of religion typically operationalizes via survey items on belief, attendance, and affiliation — which Asad's critique implies presuppose the privatized, belief-centered Protestant model. Asad's own counter-program directs attention instead to authorizing discourses, disciplinary practices ([[discipline]]), and institutional power that *form* religious dispositions and define what counts as religion in a given epoch.

## Applications

Asad's argument that the religion/power separation is "a modern Western norm, the product of a unique post-Reformation history" has been foundational for the anthropology of secularism and of Islam: applying the privatized-belief model to Muslim traditions, where religion and politics are differently coupled, is on his account analytically misleading (see [[discursive-tradition]]). His analysis of Saudi *nasiha* (Ch. 6 of *Genealogies*) presents a tradition in which *din* "relates more to how one lives than to what one believes."

## Critiques and Limitations

The universalist position retains serious defenders — comparative religion and the cognitive science of religion proceed on the assumption of a definable cross-cultural domain — and critics of Asad note that his argument, if pressed, threatens the intelligibility of cross-cultural comparison as such. The dispute is recorded at [[definition-of-religion-debate]]; the wiki does not adjudicate it. Asad himself closed the medieval chapters by insisting his account explains "partly what 'religion' was, not... what part of it is 'true religion.'"
