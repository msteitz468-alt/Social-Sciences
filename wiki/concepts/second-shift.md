# Second shift

## Definition as Coined

In *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]* (1989), [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] used **second shift** for the unpaid domestic labor — housework and child care — that follows a first shift of paid employment. An interviewee proposed the industrial metaphor: after claims work she was "on duty" again at home cooking, caring for children, doing laundry (Ch. 1, attributed). Parallel to the wage gap, Hochschild argued, there is a [[leisure-gap|leisure gap]] at home: most women work one shift at the office or factory and a second shift at home (attributed).

## Semantic History

The phrase entered popular and academic language after 1989 (Publishers Weekly, as quoted in later editions: the concept "has entered the language"). It sits downstream of earlier time-use work on the "double day" (Alexander Szalai's cross-national studies and U.S. diary research by Robinson, Walker & Woods — synthesized in Hochschild's Appendix). Related older terms (double day, dual burden) remain in use; **second shift** specifically frames domestic labor as a *shift* continuous with industrial time discipline. Later work on the time bind, outsourced intimacy, and global care chains extends the program without replacing the core term.

## Operationalizations

| Measure | Source (as of Hochschild 1989/2012) |
|---|---|
| ~15 extra hours/week for women | Hochschild's average of major 1960s–70s time-use studies → "extra month a year" of 24-hour days |
| Male share of housework in her N=50 | ~20% equal; ~70% substantial but unequal (less than half, more than a third); ~10% less than a third (Ch. 1; Appendix restates ~18% equal half of tasks) |
| Task type | Women do ~two-thirds of daily rigid jobs (cook, clean); men more flexible/periodic tasks (car oil, repairs) |
| Responsibility | Even when hours are shared, wives more often manage appointments, gifts, mood, baby-sitter check-ins |

Juster (cited by Hochschild): male slice of second shift nationally ~20% (1965) → ~30% (1981) — slow change, not equality.

## Applications

- Canonical case: **Nancy and Evan Holt** — leisure gap, passive male refusal, [[family-myth|upstairs/downstairs myth]], displaced "Joey's Problem" (*[[hochschild-second-shift-study]]*).
- Analytic forum: the second shift as site where gender ideologies, [[economy-of-gratitude|gratitude]], and [[gender-strategy|strategies]] are enacted (Ch. 13).
- Comparative note (literature, not her sample): gay and lesbian partners more often share — gay partners by specialization, lesbians by similar tasks (Ch. 1, attributed secondary claim).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Time-use estimates depend on diary methods, sample frames, and what counts as "work"; later studies report narrowing of some gaps — treat the ~15-hour figure as **Hochschild's 1960s–70s synthesis**, not a timeless constant.
- Sample overrepresents middle-class dual-earners with young children (Appendix); generalizability to other family forms is program, not proven.
- Concept can be flattened to "hours only," missing Hochschild's emphasis on **responsibility**, multitasking, rigid vs flexible tasks, and emotional consequences.
- Links to *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|Managed Heart]]* emotion concepts: sustaining unequal arrangements often requires [[emotion-work]] under [[feeling-rules]] of the [[stalled-revolution]] (Holt case), not commercial [[emotional-labor]] per se.
- **Ridgeway bridge (2011):** *[[ridgeway-framed-by-gender-2011|Framed by Gender]]* does not use Hochschild's "second shift" label as a primary term, but Ch. 5 [[household-division-of-labor]] and family-devotion vs [[ideal-worker-norm]] schemas supply a status-frame account of the same unpaid load — feeding the [[maternal-wall]] and workplace inequality. Change lever (Ch. 7): men taking major routine caregiving.
