# Nationalism and Archaeology

## Definition as Coined

**Nationalism and archaeology** names the study of how archaeological evidence and
interpretation are mobilized to construct, legitimate, or contest national and ethnic origins.
Emerging as an explicit critical topic in the 1980s–90s (Trigger; Kohl & Fawcett; Díaz-Andreu &
Champion), it treats the discipline as, in Balibar's phrase (via Appadurai), an instrument for
"producing the people" and "a critical player in the economy of remembering and forgetting" for
nation-states. Discussed across *[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary
Archaeology in Theory]]* (2010); all claims attributed.

## Semantic History

Díaz-Andreu argues "archaeology developed as the result of the need to find data which would
permit the reconstruction of the remotest periods of the national past" — the 19th-century
crystallization of nationalist history drew archaeology into the service of origin myths. She
adopts Kedourie's distinction between **political nationalism** (post-1789; nation = state; no
cultural content) and **cultural/ethno-nationalism** (mid-19th c.; a nation = a culture that ought
to become a state) — the latter being what gave archaeology its new importance. Hobsbawm's
"invention of tradition" and Anderson's "imagined communities" are the standing frames. The
doctrine is the inverse companion of diffusionist [[ex-oriente-lux|"Oriental priority"]] and of
Kossinna's nationalist culture-history.

## Operationalizations

**Spain (Díaz-Andreu).** Spain hosted **four competing nationalisms** — Spanish/Castilian, Catalan,
Basque, Galician — each building an antagonistic archaeological past. Under Franco, archaeology had
an explicit political role: Roman and Visigothic Spain as precedents for national unity, and
early-Francoist searches for an "Aryan presence" with German teams (reported as historical fact,
critically framed by Díaz-Andreu, not endorsed). Catalan (Bosch Gimpera's school), Basque
(Basque-Cantabrist purity theory — flagged as an uncritical culture=people=language equation *as
historically practiced*), and Galician (Celtic-origin) archaeologies each served regional
nationhood. Post-Franco devolution depoliticized the discipline; nationalist expression now shows
mainly in **publication language choice** rather than interpretive content — a documented case where
a method-theory shift (post-war "modified positivism") changed a discipline's political salience.

**Vijayanagara (Sinopoli).** The empire (founded 1330s; capital abandoned after 1565) layered new
authority onto a pre-existing sacred landscape — building the Ramachandra temple to tie its rulers
to Rama "by addition rather than replacement," and adopting a **revivalist** Chola-imperial temple
style to claim universal southern rule, while its courtly architecture borrowed contemporaneously
from the Deccan Sultanates. Successor "nayaka" states kept invoking Vijayanagara (and
Krishnadevaraya's reign) as legitimating memory, building gopuras far larger than the empire's own
— prompting Sinopoli's caution that "the most monumental constructions of a state may refer as much
to memories of power as to its actual presence." Vijayanagara memory remains contested between
Kannada- and Telugu-speaking communities in modern India.

**Africa (Schmidt & Walz).** Archaeology has overturned settler-colonial narratives: Great Zimbabwe
attributed to indigenous Shona ancestors against Phoenician/Sheba myths; indigenous origins for
Swahili urbanism against "Shirazi/Persian" transplantation models; indigenous Eritrean precursors
against Ethio-Sabaean foreign-founding stories — each a case of archaeology contesting a
racialized national/colonial historiography. See also Shackel's U.S. commemorative cases under
[[social-memory]].

## Applications

State museums and monuments; contested religious-site claims (e.g. Hindu-temple-under-mosque
disputes in India, per Appadurai); [[heritage]] designation; and the racialized "foreign founder"
narratives that [[postcolonialism|postcolonial]] and indigenous critique target.

### Trigger's comparative survey (2006)

*(Attributed — [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], [[trigger-history-archaeological-thought-2006|HAT]] 2006.)* Trigger embeds nationalism across the history of archaeological thought: Scandinavian patriotic antiquarianism after Napoleonic shocks; [[kossinna-gustaf|Kossinna]]'s ethnic *Siedlungsarchäologie* and Nazi afterlife; European national archaeologies and suppressed alternatives; colonial evolutionary denigration of indigenous pasts ([[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology]]; Great Zimbabwe myths); national traditions in Japan, China, India, Israel/Palestine, Egypt (Pharaonic vs pan-Arab/Islamic framings), Africa, and Latin America; post-Soviet ethnogenesis and renewed ethnicity concepts. He pairs social-context drivers (including middle-class interests) with [[data-constraint-on-interpretation|data constraint]] that eventually undermines some nationalist schemes. See [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]], [[kossinna-gustaf]].

## Critiques and Limitations

The "archaeology serves the nation" thesis can itself flatten cases: Horning warns against
importing a strict colonizer/colonized binary into settings like Northern Ireland, where the actual
history is "a patchwork quilt." And the same evidence that debunks one national myth can be
recruited for another. Every attribution of an ancient culture to a modern nation crosses the
four-way non-identity boundary and must be argued, not assumed. See nationalism and
[[ethnicity-paradigm]].

### Cuno 2008 — retentionist law as nationalist instrument (position)

[[cuno-james|James Cuno]] (*[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns Antiquity?]]*) argues that
modern nation-states use archaeology and patrimony law to manufacture identity continuity with
pre-national pasts (case chapters on **Turkey** and **China**; Iraq under Ba'thist branding as
reported). On his account, "nationalist retentionist cultural property laws" are political
instruments, not site-protection science, and the unprovenanced-acquisition dispute is a
**surrogate** for museums-vs-nation-states ownership politics. He draws on nationalism theory
(Anderson, Gellner, A.D. Smith, Maalouf, Sen, Appiah, Said as he reads them) to claim culture is
hybrid/"mongrel" and national purity claims are elite constructions. These are **advocacy
positions** filed at [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] and
[[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]] — not wiki-voice findings about those states.

## Smith 2006 — AHD and nationalism (attributed)

*([[smith-laurajane]], *[[smith-uses-of-heritage-2006]]*.)* The [[authorized-heritage-discourse|AHD]] was both constituted by and constitutive of nationalist ideology; identifying “national heritage” imaginatively constitutes the nation. Billig’s **banal nationalism** applies to everyday heritage tourism that continually “flags” national identity. English country house as nationalizing icon; Australian landscape AHD at [[riversleigh|Riversleigh]] (bushman, real Australia, deep-time national pride). Sub-national identity work (class, local, Indigenous) shows more conscious agency against nationalizing AHD. Cross-link [[heritage]], [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]].
