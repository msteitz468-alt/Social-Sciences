# Self-Interest Properly Understood

## Definition as Coined

*L'intérêt bien entendu* — [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]]'s name (Vol. 2 of
*[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*) for the ethical doctrine
he found Americans reciting to explain their own conduct: not that virtue is beautiful, but
that it is *useful* — that helping others and serving the public is, rightly understood, in
one's own long-run interest. Americans, he wrote, "enjoy explaining almost all their actions
by the principle of self-interest properly understood" and "take pleasure in demonstrating
how the enlightened love of themselves continually leads them to help one another."

Tocqueville's appraisal is deliberately two-edged. The doctrine "does not inspire great
sacrifices" and produces no heroic virtue, but it "forms a multitude of citizens who are
controlled, temperate, moderate, farsighted, self-possessed" — a reliable, low-grade,
universally teachable civic ethic well-suited to democratic conditions. He added a
load-bearing hedge: Americans often act from genuine, disinterested impulse but *attribute*
their conduct to self-interest — "they would rather do honor to their philosophy than to
themselves."

## Semantic History

The idea is older (Enlightenment "enlightened self-love," Mandeville, the Scottish
moralists); Tocqueville *migrated* it into a sociological observation about how an entire
democratic culture moralizes. The concept feeds later rational-choice and social-capital
literatures, and debates over whether civic virtue can be built on interest.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; read from how Americans narrate their own behavior. The gap Tocqueville
flags — between the motive avowed and the motive acted on — is itself a methodological point
(self-report ≠ behavior).

## Applications

The doctrine is one of the American remedies for [[individualism]]: by teaching that private
interest is served through public engagement, it draws isolated citizens into
[[voluntary-association|association]] and public life. Tocqueville even traced its extension
to religion — Americans present faith too as serving well-understood interest, this-worldly
and next.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The avowed-motive / real-motive gap makes the doctrine hard to assess as a causal force.
- Whether an interest-based ethic can sustain the sacrifices a polity sometimes needs is a
  question Tocqueville raises but leaves open.
