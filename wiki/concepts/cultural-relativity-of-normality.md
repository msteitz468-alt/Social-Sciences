# Cultural Relativity of Normality

## Definition as Coined

[[benedict-ruth|Benedict]]'s argument (Ch. VIII, recapitulating her 1934 paper "Anthropology and the Abnormal"): "those are disoriented whose congenial responses fall in that arc of behaviour which is not capitalized by their culture. These abnormals are those who are not supported by the institutions of their civilization." Normality is not a fixed psychological category but the fit between an individual's temperament and the segment of [[the-great-arc-of-culture|the arc]] the local [[cultural-configuration|configuration]] rewards. "The statistically determined normal on the Northwest Coast would be far outside the extreme boundaries of abnormality in the Pueblos."

## Semantic History

Benedict's extension of [[cultural-relativism]] from values to psychiatric categories. She distinguishes **two** kinds of "abnormal": (1) the misfit whose temperament the culture does not capitalize (the friendly man in treacherous [[dobu|Dobu]], regarded as merely silly; the reflective, danger-averse Crow informant branded incompetent; the turbulent Zuni "witch"), and (2) the extreme *over*-development of the locally valorized type, which is licensed rather than pathologized in its own generation (Puritan conversion-terror; the "unbridled egoist" of modern business, "not described in our manuals of psychiatry because they are supported by every tenet of our civilization"). The thesis was influential in cross-cultural psychiatry and anti-psychiatry.

## Operationalizations

Comparison of the socially valued and stigmatized "types" across cultures against Western psychiatric categories: trance/catalepsy (pathology in the modern West; sainthood in medieval Catholic Europe; the pathway to shamanic authority among the Shasta and in Siberia; the Zulu diviner's calling); homosexuality (pathologized in Western psychiatry; honored in Plato's Greece; the institutionalized North American "berdache" role, e.g. the Zuni We-wha, "the strongest person in Zuni"); and the Kwakiutl "megalomaniac" ideal, "recognized as abnormal in our civilization" yet "the essential attribute of ideal man" there.

## Applications

The culminating argument of *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* and the basis of its closing ethical plea: cultural relativity is "not a doctrine of despair" but grounds for "a more realistic social faith," tolerance of the deviant, and "self-education of the patient" toward accepting difference — Benedict argued cultures permitting more deviation "cannot be shown to suffer from their peculiarity" (contrast the conformity-anxious *Middletown*).

## Critiques and Limitations

Criticized for implying no cross-cultural standard of suffering or dysfunction exists, and for Benedict's retrospective clinical judgments about historical actors (the Puritan divines as "the psychoneurotics of Puritan New England"), which are her attributed interpretations, not established fact. Defenders read the thesis as **methodological** (suspend the assumption that one's own norms are universal) rather than a denial of distress. Its relation to biological accounts of temperament links it to the standing nature–nurture / sociobiology dispute.
