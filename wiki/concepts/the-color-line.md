# The Color-line

## Definition as Coined

[[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]] opens *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903) with the thesis:

> “the problem of the Twentieth Century is the problem of the color line.”

In chapter II he expands the definition beyond the United States:

> “the relation of the darker to the lighter races of men in Asia and Africa, in America and the islands of the sea.”

He treated the Civil War as a phase of that problem, with Negro slavery as the real cause beneath technical shibboleths of union and local autonomy.

Later theoretical successor language: [[racial-formation-theory]] (Omi & Winant) treats the color-line’s reproduction through [[racial-project|racial projects]] and [[racialization]], including colorblind containment after formal civil rights.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Souls* Forethought / ch. II | Master problem of the century; global race hierarchy |
| *Souls* ch. IX | Everyday structure of contact — physical, economic, political, intellectual, social, religious |
| *Essays* (*Economic Revolution*, *Black North*, c. 1903–06) | Peonage, white primary, Atlanta Massacre; Northern urban color-line (licenses, draft-riot exclusions, wage gap) — national not Southern-only |
| Later sociology / civil rights | Segregation, Jim Crow, apartheid analogies; sometimes narrowed to U.S. South only |
| Massey & Denton 1993 | Residential color line as enduring, institutionally maintained structure after formal civil-rights equality — *[[massey-denton-american-apartheid-1993|American Apartheid]]* uses Du Bois's color-line epigraph for Ch 2 and argues the housing color line undercuts progress elsewhere |

The phrase predates and outlives *Souls* in public discourse; this page records **Du Bois’s classical-era** formulation as coined for wiki network purposes (popularization and analytic sharpening, not necessarily first English use).

## Operationalizations

In *Souls*, Du Bois maps the color-line through:

1. **Physical proximity** — “physical color-line on the map” in Southern communities  
2. **Economic relations** — post-slavery capital/skill gap; crop-lien; peonage  
3. **Political relations** — ballot, disfranchisement, taxation without voice  
4. **Intellectual contact / public opinion** — near-absence of elite interracial intellectual community  
5. **Social contact** — travel, theatres, marriage bars  
6. **Religious / benevolent enterprise**

From *[[du-bois-essays|Essays]]* (attributed, c. 1903–06), additional operationalizations:

7. **Agricultural peonage** — perpetual debt, criminal labor contracts, vagrancy laws, barriers to Black land purchase in the black belt  
8. **White primary** — all whites in, no Negroes; farce general election (Atlanta example: 700 votes in a city of 100,000, as Du Bois reports)  
9. **Northern urban color-line** — occupational exclusion, licensing bars, suffrage realty restriction (NY 1821–war), wage differentials (~⅓ less), sex-ratio skew under domestic service demand (→ [[black-urban-north]])

Concrete illustrations include Georgia school funds he reports as roughly four dollars for white children to one for Negro children; double systems of justice; lynching as racial terror intertwined with crime accusation and mutual disbelief across the line.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Global scope in one sentence can outrun the book’s largely U.S. Southern evidence base — treat global claim as Du Bois’s thesis, not as demonstrated world history in this volume.
- Binary darker/lighter framing is of its era; later race theory multiplies axes (class, gender, ethnicity) without erasing the historical color-line’s force.
