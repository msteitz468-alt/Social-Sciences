# Patrilateral Parallel-Cousin Marriage

## Definition as Coined

**Patrilateral parallel-cousin marriage** is preferential or practiced marriage with the child of the father's brother (FBS/FBD; Arabic often *ibn ʿamm* / *bint ʿamm*). Distinct from [[cross-cousin-marriage|cross-cousin marriage]] (children of mother's brother or father's sister). Long a stock typifying "institution" in Middle Eastern and Mediterranean ethnography.

## Semantic History

Classificatory kinship vocabulary separates parallel cousins (same-sex sibling's children of parent) from cross cousins. In Arab Bedouin and many Middle Eastern contexts, the **FBD preference** became a classic structural type — linked by analysts to agnatic solidarity, property, and endogamy. [[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]] in *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* (Ch. 4) keeps the social-science title as a foil: she presents how particular [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] women and men **lived, contested, praised, and cursed** cousin marriage in the 1970s–80s, rather than stating "the Bedouin practice FBD marriage" as a cultural rule ([[writing-against-culture]]).

## Operationalizations

- Preferential vs prescribed; frequency vs ideology.
- Brideprice patterns: Abu-Lughod reported (attributed, 1980s Awlad ʿAli) lower within-family brideprice with investment in gold for the bride.
- Cousin-right as claim **and** face-saving bluff (not automatic enforcement).
- Relation to [[honor-and-modesty]] displays at weddings (honor cloth / public proof of virginity as situational performance).

## Applications

- *Writing Women's Worlds* Ch. 4 — Gateefa, Sabra, Selima wedding narratives; henna songs that both glorify and damn the uncle's son; wedding shrinkage under religious propriety ([[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]).
- *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-study|Veiled Sentiments]]* — same community's agnatic preference as part of honor ideology (1978–80).
- Comparative institution pages: [[marriage]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Treating FBD preference as a timeless "Arab culture" trait erases historical change (settlement, schooling, bride preferences, piety) and women's vetoes, delays, and refusals. Abu-Lughod's method insists the type-label is an ethnographic writing problem as much as a kinship fact. Do not equate the classificatory preference with genetic population structure or with universal practice.
