## Definition as Coined

Coleman argues, in *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990, chs. 14–15), that social choice is the problem of how control over an indivisible corporate action can be partitioned among the members of a collectivity. He proposes four criteria a "good" social-choice procedure must satisfy: **consistency** (Arrow's axioms of logical coherence), **veridicality** (the outcome genuinely reflects members' interests), **action-potential** (the procedure produces an implementable decision), and **nondivisiveness** (the procedure does not itself provoke secession or revolt). Coleman presents this four-criterion framework as his own synthesis rather than a restatement of Arrow's original problem.

## Semantic History

The formal root of the concept is Condorcet's 1785 discovery that consistent individual preferences can aggregate into cyclical, inconsistent collective preferences (Condorcet's paradox). Arrow's 1951 impossibility theorem generalized this into a proof that no aggregation rule can satisfy a small set of reasonable consistency axioms simultaneously for three or more options. Gibbard (1973) and Satterthwaite (1975) extended the result to show no non-dictatorial rule is strategy-proof. Coleman, writing four decades after Arrow, reframes the theorem's practical stakes for corporate actors — parties, committees, legislatures — rather than treating it as a purely formal result.

## Operationalizations

Coleman discusses and compares several concrete decision procedures against his four criteria: plurality voting; the Borda count (rank-weighted scoring); elimination by similarity, including a party-primary-restricted variant; majority-runoff; Condorcet's weakest-link pairwise elimination; and **Borda elimination**, Coleman's own name for a modification of Nanson's 1883 method, which he argues has "some promise" because it selects the Condorcet winner when one exists and performs well on consistency and veridicality, though its action-potential and nondivisiveness properties remain untested. He also reports Brams and Fishburn's (1978, 1983) approval voting, and sketches pairwise-rights and fungible-vote-bank schemes intended to transmit intensity of preference — noting that none of these can generally be made incentive-compatible without penalizing voters with genuinely extreme preferences.

Coleman distinguishes Arrow's **independence from irrelevant alternatives (IIA)** axiom — empirically violated in individual choice too, per Tversky's (1972) "elimination by aspects" theory and the Debussy/Beethoven-recordings example (originally Debreu 1960) — and argues the fault may lie with the axiom rather than with social-choice procedures per se, since reasonable choice does not require IIA.

## Applications

Coleman illustrates cyclical majority outcomes with the 1983 Chicago Democratic mayoral primary, in which a third candidate's entry (Washington) split the vote between Byrne and Daley and reversed the two-way outcome — his own empirical illustration, not an independently verified finding. He extends social-choice logic to informal community and executive decision-making absent formal voting rules, drawing on his own earlier work (*Community Conflict*, 1957): extended discussion brings latent interests into play, and a norm favoring consensus and disfavoring self-interested claims emerges endogenously because divisive claims damage both individual members and the collectivity's strength. Executive decision-making, he argues, generates sycophancy among "courtiers," countered institutionally by structured-dissent mechanisms such as NASA's nonadvocacy policy review.

Coleman also proposes an ethical-theory extension: the "morally correct" outcome of a corporate decision is endogenously defined by the internal weighing of interests within the system itself, with no external Archimedean standpoint — illustrated, with explicit caution, via the Jonestown mass-suicide case as "correct" from within that community's internal weighing but "wrong" from the perspective of the wider American social system.

## Critiques and Limitations

Coleman treats Arrow's impossibility result and its extensions as establishing that no procedure can jointly guarantee consistency, veridicality, action-potential, and nondivisiveness — a permanent design constraint rather than a solvable engineering problem. He further argues that classical political philosophy's normative devices for adjudicating rights allocation (Rawls's veil of ignorance, Bentham's utilitarian calculus, Kant's categorical imperative) rest on empirically false assumptions — either total ignorance of one's future position or a homogeneous mix of future activities — and so cannot explain actual interpersonal or intergroup conflict over constitutions; he proposes replacing them with a power-weighted interest-aggregation model. The micro-to-macro transition in social choice is, on Coleman's account, a problem of institutional design (agendas, coalitions, discussion structure), not simple vote aggregation — describing it as mere aggregation is, he argues, doubly mistaken.
