# Primitive Promiscuity

**Primitive promiscuity** is the hypothesis that the earliest human sexual condition was undifferentiated — "loose, transitory, and in some degree promiscuous" — preceding any regulated marriage, and giving way through polyandry to matrilineal reckoning and only later to patrilineal monogamy. Proposed by [[mclennan-john-ferguson|John Ferguson McLennan]] in *Primitive Marriage* (1865) and, independently, foreshadowed by [[bachofen-johann-jakob|Johann Bachofen]]'s mother-right (1861), it became a load-bearing claim of Victorian [[social-evolutionism|evolutionary]] theories of the family. This page is written in **attributed / position** voice: the sequence is recorded as a Victorian hypothesis that mainstream anthropology later abandoned.

## Definition as Coined

McLennan reconstructed marriage as evolving from an original stage of "promiscuity in the connection of the sexes" (evidenced, notoriously, by a single ethnographic footnote on wife-lending). Because paternity could not be determined under promiscuity, kinship was first traced through the female line; female infanticide, adaptive under conditions of perpetual primitive hostility, produced a shortage of women that drove **exogamy** and **marriage by capture** and then **polyandry** before male kinship and monogamy emerged. Related terms McLennan coined or transmitted include **exogamy** and **[[totemism]]**. [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] traced marriage from "communal marriage" through capture to exogamy; [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s *Ancient Society* offered a parallel scheme.

## Semantic History

- **Contested from the start.** Even among evolutionists the sequence was disputed (a **position**): Lubbock treated polyandry as an "exceptional phenomenon" not a necessary stage; [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] doubted the theory "taken as a whole." [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]] defended the priority of the patriarchal family, arguing (with [[darwin-charles|Darwin]], reasoning from the sexual jealousy of male primates) that a pair-bonded patriarchal condition fitted the biological evidence better than an original promiscuity. See [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]], [[morgan-mclennan-debate]].
- **Collapse.** [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], reviewing the debate in 1885, initially thought "converging research" supported the early prevalence of female-line kinship, but by 1896 noted a "reaction" against "primitive matrimonial anarchy"; late-Victorian ethnographers (Fison, Howitt) could locate no actual society exemplifying it. Edward Westermarck's *History of Human Marriage* (1891) mounted the decisive critique. Stocking's verdict (attributed): "primitive promiscuity," "matriarchy," "female infanticide," and "marriage by capture" as universal evolutionary stages were "largely a fantasy of the Victorian male anthropological imagination," and the analytic problem was replaced by the universality of the **incest taboo**.

## Operationalizations

None valid; the hypothesis rested on inference from scattered custom (levirate, wife-lending, matriliny) read as [[survivals]] of an earlier stage.

## Applications

The hypothesis organized Victorian theories of the origin of the family and of [[myth-of-matriarchy|matriarchy]], and — Stocking argues (attributed) — was conditioned by contemporary anxieties over Victorian marriage law, sexuality, and the emergent women's-rights movement, rather than being a straightforward defence of patriarchy (Bachofen's *Mutterrecht* was neither a response to Maine nor read by McLennan until 1866).

## Critiques and Limitations

Beyond Westermarck's empirical demolition, later kinship theory rejected the whole developmental frame: matriliny is not evidence of prior promiscuity, and terminological systems do not encode a universal marital sequence. The concept persists in the wiki only as a documented episode in the history of kinship theory (see [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988|Kuper 1988]] on the "invention" of primitive society).
