# Palaeolithic

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872|Pre-Historic Times]]* (1865; 1872 ed. read here), [[lubbock-john|John Lubbock]] names the earliest of his four prehistoric epochs the **"Paleolithic" period** (modern spelling **Palaeolithic**): "That of the Drift; when man shared the possession of Europe with the Mammoth, the Cave bear, the Woolly-haired rhinoceros, and other extinct animals" (attributed, Ch. I). Characterized by unground stone implements associated with extinct Quaternary fauna — later chapters on cave men and river-drift gravels (Somme, etc.) supply the material case.

## Semantic History

- **Pre-Lubbock:** Danish three-age system (Thomsen et al.) had a single Stone Age; Boucher de Perthes and British geologists established human–extinct-fauna association without Lubbock's label.
- **Lubbock 1865:** splits Stone Age into Palaeolithic vs [[neolithic|Neolithic]]; popularizes the terms in English.
- **Later archaeology:** subdivided (Lower/Middle/Upper; Mode 1–5 industries; regional sequences); absolute dating (radiocarbon, luminescence) replaces Lubbock's relative geology. The term remains standard period vocabulary.

## Operationalizations

Lubbock: faunal association + unpolished lithics + geological context (drift, caves). Modern: techno-typology + absolute dates + paleoenvironment — far beyond 1865 methods.

## Applications

Founding period tag for deep prehistory in this wiki's deep-time framework (`late-prehistory` / `behavioral-modernity` etc. are later conventions). Anchors [[antiquity-of-man]] as material culture periods rather than biblical time.

## Critiques and Limitations

Europe-first classification (Lubbock says he applies it for the present only to Europe); evolutionary-progress baggage when paired with "modern savages" chapters; single Stone Age residue still used loosely in popular writing.

**Childe 1929:** *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|Danube]]* Ch. II surveys Central European Palaeolithic/epipalaeolithic provincialism (Mousterian–Aurignacian–proto-Solutrean; Maglemose/Shell Mounds) as background to Neolithic colonization — attributed culture-history, not a primary Palaeolithic monograph.

**Clark 1961:** *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* divides the period into **Lower Palaeolithic** (hand-axe industries, Mousterian/Levallois, African and South Asian survivals; culture defined by works not zoology) and **Advanced Palaeolithic** (his label for Upper Palaeolithic blade industries: Aurignacian, Gravettian, Solutrean, Magdalenian; cave art; Siberia colonization) before the [[mesolithic|Mesolithic]] (attributed, Clark 1961 chs. 2–3). Absolute chronology uncalibrated.

### Childe — Palaeolithic savagery (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

Childe maps Morgan's [[savagery-barbarism-civilization|savagery]] onto the archaeological Palaeolithic/Pleistocene as a food-gathering economy lasting nearly the whole of deep prehistory, with extracorporeal tools as the adaptive equipment (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* chs. I–II — attributed). Upper Palaeolithic "brilliant" specialized cultures (e.g. Magdalenian) are *not* the agents of the [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]] in his narrative — over-specialization for Ice Age hunting is a dead end relative to humbler southern groups.

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]]'s Palaeolithic scaffolding (as of 2007 textbook chronology) places first stone tools ~2.6 Ma Africa; *Homo habilis* ~2.5 Ma; *Homo erectus* grade and Lower Paleolithic hand-axes (bifaces) ~1.8 Ma; Acheulian present in East Africa >1 Ma but earliest European Acheulian ~600 kya; regular northern temperate European occupation only ~500 kya (Isernia, Boxgrove, etc.); early anatomically modern humans in Africa ~160 kya; Neandertals well-established western Eurasia ~130 kya; *H. sapiens* radiation from Africa ~100 kya; Australia colonization ~60–50 kya; Upper Paleolithic industries ~50–45 kya; Aurignacian ~40 kya; Neandertals extinct ~32 kya (attributed timeline tables — round numbers).

They stress *H. erectus* cultural absences relative to later humans: no secure figurines, wall painting, rock carving, or elaborately non-utilitarian stone tools; no intentional burials, whereas death ritual and grave goods are widespread after ~30 kya (attributed). Methodological notes: nonsite Paleolithic survey (Olszewski Abydos Survey) maps landscape density, not only "sites"; Cagny l'Epinette "living floor" reanalysis (Dibble et al.) shows stream deposition — Pompeii-like freezes are rare (attributed); cut-mark debate on hunting vs scavenging (Binford, Bunn, Shipman) remains open (attributed). Richard Klein's "behavioral breakthrough" after ~50 kya (genetic mutation enabling fully modern language/culture) is recorded as a position separating anatomical from behavioral modernity (attributed). Settlement-location logic: Paleolithic/early agricultural sites track material resources; later sites increasingly track trade routes, frontiers, and administration (attributed, ch. 7). **Genetics/origins claims from this textbook stay attributed** — see [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

### Gamble — *The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]*; as of Gamble 1999.)*

Gamble’s program is a **social archaeology of the Palaeolithic** against a field he argues has never been a seed-bed for social theory — debates stayed on formation processes and lithic/faunal variability (Binford–Bordes, taphonomy) while ecological natural prehistory and a “civilizing process” curtain separated Us/Other at the Middle–Upper / Neanderthal–Modern transition (merely shifted ~30,000 years). Society is the unifying focus: no different explanatory standards on either side of the curtain; social life central to stasis and change; individual [[agency]] and [[intimate-effective-extended-networks|personal networks]] from ~5 Myr, not only the richer Upper Palaeolithic.

**Analytic frame (Table 3.1):** [[locale|locales]] (encounters → gatherings → social occasions/place) · [[palaeolithic-rhythms|rhythms]] (chaîne opératoire, taskscape, paths/tracks) · regions ([[social-landscape-palaeolithic|landscape of habit vs social landscape]]). Method of “tacking” (Wylie) between ~15-minute episodes and 70,000-year averaged assemblages. Central question: [[release-from-proximity]] and social complication, not origins of band society.

**European chronology notes (as of Gamble 1999):** Pleistocene start ~2.3 Myr BP (Zagwijn); Brunhes/Matuyama at 775±10 Kyr BP as Lower/Middle Pleistocene boundary; short chronology of first undisputed European occupation around Cromer IV / OIS 13 (~528–478 Kyr BP) — position Gamble supports; transformation of the record after ~500 Kyr BP (Roebroeks & van Kolfschoten via Gamble). Flexible boundaries of Palaeolithic Europe (peninsular cul-de-sac; weak eastern edges). Nine-region model retained for central/western behavioural variation.
