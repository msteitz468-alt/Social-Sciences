# One-Dimensionality

## Definition as Coined

"One-dimensionality" is [[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]]'s name, in *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964), for the condition of "advanced industrial society" in which the critical, negative, or oppositional dimension of thought and existence has been absorbed into affirmation of the established order. Marcuse defines the pattern (attributed) as one "in which ideas, aspirations, and objectives that, by their content, transcend the established universe of discourse and action are either repelled or reduced to terms of this universe." Where two-dimensional thought holds the given reality ("what is") against its unrealized possibilities ("what could be"), one-dimensional thought collapses that tension: the real is taken as rational, and alternatives become literally unthinkable.

Marcuse argues the flattening operates across three registers, which give the book its three parts:

- **One-dimensional society** (Part 1): domination now works through **technology rather than terror**, delivering rising living standards that "contain" qualitative change. The former class antagonists — bourgeoisie and proletariat, business and labour, East and West — converge in a "unification of opposites" that stabilizes the system. Marcuse calls this the **containment of social change**, "perhaps the most singular achievement of advanced industrial society."
- **One-dimensional thought** (Part 2): dialectical "negative thinking" is defeated by operational, positivist, and ordinary-language reason (see [[technological-rationality]]).
- **The Happy Consciousness** (Part 1, Ch. 3): the resulting psychology — "the belief that the real is rational, and that the established system, in spite of everything, delivers the goods" — in which the atrophy of the critical faculties makes conscience an evacuated, private category.

Marcuse framed the whole book as vacillating (attributed) "between two contradictory hypotheses: (1) that advanced industrial society is capable of containing qualitative change for the foreseeable future; (2) that forces and tendencies exist which may break this containment," and declined to decide between them — see [[the-great-refusal]].

## Semantic History

The concept fuses several inheritances. Its two-dimensional/negative pole descends from Hegelian dialectics (the "power of the negative") and from Marx's [[alienation]] and ideology-critique; its diagnosis of an administered, calculable order that "delivers the goods" extends Weber's [[rationalization]]; its account of instinctual integration draws on Marcuse's own Freudian *Eros and Civilization* (1955), via [[repressive-desublimation]]. The term is the summary label of [[critical-theory|Frankfurt-School critical theory]] in its most pessimistic postwar phase. In later usage "one-dimensional" became shorthand across the New Left for a consumer society that neutralizes dissent by absorbing it; Marcuse himself qualified the thesis after 1968, when student and Third-World movements seemed to supply the refusing "substratum" the book had located only tentatively.

## Operationalizations

Marcuse offered no measure; one-dimensionality is diagnosed structurally and rhetorically. Its indicators, on his account (attributed): the integration of organized labour into the "welfare-warfare state" (he cites US business–labour collusion over missile contracts, the British Labour Party's retreat from nationalization, Western European communist parties' parliamentary accommodation, all as **illustration, not evidence**); the marketing of "high culture" as commodity, liquidating its antagonistic force; the "functionalized" language of politics and advertising (see [[technological-rationality]]); and the individual's identification with their commodities — "they find their soul in their automobile, hi-fi set, split-level home."

## Applications

The concept anchors Marcuse's reworking of [[alienation]] (identification with imposed existence as a *more* total alienation, not its end), his culture critique (see [[mass-society]]), his account of [[false-needs]] and [[repressive-desublimation]], and his critique of [[technological-rationality]]. It sits at the center of the [[critical-theory|critical-theory]] program and of the [[revolutionary-subject-debate|debate over the revolutionary subject]].

## Critiques and Limitations

One-dimensionality is among the most contested diagnoses in postwar social theory (recorded here in attributed voice). Critics charge it with functionalist over-integration — treating the system as seamlessly closed and audiences as wholly passive, understating conflict, reception, and resistance (a charge parallel to that against [[mass-society]] theory); with an élitist reliance on the theorist to know "true" from [[false-needs|false needs]] over the heads of those said to hold them; and with self-refutation, since the book itself is an act of the critical negation it declares foreclosed. Marcuse conceded the deepest tension: critical theory, on his account, retains no "demonstrable agent of change" and so "cannot offer the remedy." The events of 1968 are usually read as both partial vindication and refutation. Written throughout in attributed voice.
