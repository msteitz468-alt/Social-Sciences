# r > g

Central inequality mechanism of [[piketty-thomas|Thomas Piketty]]’s *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* (2013/2014) and of the associated research program [[piketty-capital-dynamics]]. Treated as the book’s “fundamental inequality” and, in the conclusion, as the principal destabilizing force of a market economy based on private property.

## Definition as Coined

Piketty defines **r** as the average annual rate of return on capital (profits, dividends, interest, rents, and other capital income as a percentage of the capital stock’s market value) and **g** as the growth rate of the economy (annual increase in income or output). When **r > g** holds significantly and durably, he argues, wealth accumulated in the past grows faster than output and wages: owners of inherited capital need save only a portion of capital income for their stock to grow faster than the economy, so concentration of capital can attain levels he judges incompatible with meritocratic social justice.

He presents the inequality as a **historical fact**, not a logical necessity. From antiquity to the seventeenth century, he reports, combined demographic-plus-economic growth never long exceeded about 0.1–0.2% while pure returns on capital averaged roughly 4–5% (land in agrarian societies), so **r** was typically 10–20 times **g**. France 1820–1913 is his dense illustration: **r** ≈ 5% vs **g** ≈ 1%, capital income ~40% of national income; saving one-quarter of capital income yields a ~10% savings rate—enough for wealth to grow slightly faster than income. With **g** ≈ 1% and **r** ≈ 5%, saving only one-fifth of capital income keeps inherited capital growing with the economy; saving more drives rising [[wealth-inequality]]—conditions he calls ideal for an “inheritance society.”

Canonical closing formulation: wealth from the past automatically grows more rapidly, even without labor, than wealth stemming from work; “the past tends to devour the future”; the entrepreneur tends to become a rentier. Piketty insists **r > g has nothing to do with market imperfection**—the more perfect the capital market (in the economist’s sense), the more likely **r** is to exceed **g**.

## Semantic History

The symbols **r** and **g** are standard macro/growth-theory notation (including Phelps’s 1961 “golden rule” **r = g** for maximal capital accumulation). Piketty’s contribution in the 2013 synthesis is elevating the **gap** into a long-run political diagnosis of divergence, inheritance weight, and democratic risk—not inventing the symbols. He contrasts his milder divergence story with Marx’s principle of infinite accumulation (which, in his reading, implicitly assumes near-zero long-run productivity growth). Rent and rentier, he notes, shifted pejoratively in the twentieth century; modern talk of “market imperfections” or monopoly rents confuses pure capital return with imperfect competition—an argument that ties the semantic history of rent to the claim that **r > g** survives freer markets.

## Operationalizations

- **Average real r**: pretax capital income from national accounts (rents, profits, dividends, interest, royalties; excluding interest on public debt) divided by national capital stock; pure economic return vs after-tax return (Piketty reports average tax rates on capital income around 30% in most rich countries ca. 2010).
- **g**: overall growth of national income = per-capita growth + population growth (the same **g** that enters [[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]] β = s/g).
- **Size-dependent returns**: Piketty argues larger stakes may earn higher average **r** (scale economies in portfolio management; risk tolerance). Illustrations: if average **r** = 4%, the wealthiest might get 6–7% and less wealthy 2–3%; 1987–2013 top 1/20-million adults’ average wealth grew ~6.4%/year real vs ~2.1% for average world wealth per adult; US university endowments 1980–2010 pure real returns ~8.2% average, ~10.2% for Harvard/Yale/Princeton.
- **Multi-decade regimes**, not single years: Belle Époque **r** ≫ **g**; mid-twentieth-century wars and progressive fiscal pressure temporarily compressed returns; prospective frontier **g** ~1–1.5% with **r** ~4–5% would restore **r > g** as twenty-first-century norm.

## Applications

Organizes Parts Two–Four of *Capital* and the study object [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]]: links high [[capital-income-ratio|β]] and the capital share via [[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]] α = r × β; underpins the return of [[patrimonial-capitalism]] and inheritance flows (French simulations: central scenario g=1.7%, r=3% → inheritance flow stabilizes ~16–17% of national income; alternative g=1%, r=5% → ~24–25%, near 1870–1910); grounds policy urgency for a [[progressive-global-wealth-tax]] as response to **r > g** and size-dependent returns; structures the live dispute filed under [[piketty-capital-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether **r > g** is a causal theory of inequality or a tendency requiring additional mechanisms (saving behavior by fractile, progressive taxation, wars/shocks, demography) is central to reception. Critics question measured **r** (especially including housing), the endogeneity of **g** and **s**, and whether unequal returns or labor-income “[[supermanagers]]” dominate recent Anglo-Saxon inequality. Piketty himself states divergence is not perpetual and is one of several possible futures; heavy indiscriminate capital-income taxation could push private **r** below **g** but, he argues, risks killing accumulation—hence his preference for progressive wealth taxation over pure rate suppression. See [[piketty-capital-debate]] for position recording.
