# Social Revolution

## Definition as Coined

In *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979), [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]] defines **social revolutions** (attributed) as "rapid, basic transformations of a society's state and class structures... accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below." What distinguishes a social revolution is a double **coincidence**: "the coincidence of societal structural change with class upheaval; and the coincidence of political with social transformation."

Skocpol deliberately builds *success* into the definition — a social revolution has occurred only if state and class structures were actually transformed — arguing (attributed) that successful transformations arise from different macro-structural contexts than failed attempts.

## Semantic History

- **Distinguished from neighboring concepts** (Skocpol 1979):
  - **Rebellion** — subordinate-class revolt that does *not* produce structural change.
  - **Political revolution** — transforms state structures but not class/social structures, and need not proceed through class conflict.
  - **Industrialization / other social processes** — transform social structures without political-institutional upheaval.
- The term predates Skocpol (it carries a long Marxist usage, where social revolution is the transformation of the mode of production; cf. [[marx-karl|Marx]], "the period of social revolution"). Skocpol's contribution is the **state-and-class dual-structure** definition that makes the state a defining term, not merely a superstructural effect — see [[structural-theory-of-revolution]].
- **Lenin 1917 (attributed strategic usage).** In *[[lenin-state-and-revolution-1917|State and Revolution]]*, “social revolution” is the proletarian overthrow that **[[smash-the-state-machine|smashes]]** the bourgeois state and installs a [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]] — a programme, not Skocpol’s dual-structure success criterion. Do not equate the two. See [[marxist-theory-of-the-state]], [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].
- **Lenin 1917, terminological usage (attributed; *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964|Collected Works, Vol. 24]]*).** Lenin distinguished a "**social revolution**" — any political upheaval transferring power between classes, including the bourgeois February 1917 Revolution — from a "**socialist revolution**," accusing the bourgeois press (*Finansovaya Gazeta*) of deliberately conflating the two terms to alarm the public. On this narrower, older usage a social revolution need not be socialist; it is class-power transfer in general, not Skocpol's dual state-and-class-structure success criterion. He also argued the Provisional Government's adopted April Conference resolution should describe the Soviets of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies as "a form of state which does not exist and never did exist in any country," representing "the first steps towards socialism" and the only state form compatible with socialist revolution — and held that "the objective conditions for a socialist revolution... have been ripening with tremendous rapidity as a result of the war," citing the ruin of small enterprises, capital concentration, and the development of monopoly into "state monopoly capitalism," and describing the Russian Revolution as "only the first stage of the first of the proletarian revolutions which are the inevitable result of war" (citing the 1912 Basle Manifesto's forecast). In *[[lenin-left-wing-communism-1920|"Left-Wing" Communism]]* (1920) he formulated the "**fundamental law of revolution**": that the exploited "cannot live in the old way" and the exploiters "cannot rule in the old way" simultaneously, plus majority working-class realization of necessity and a crisis among the rulers — see [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]], [[class-struggle]].
- Later revolution theory (the "fourth generation") widened the concept to include agency, ideology, and non-agrarian and negotiated/urban revolutions that Skocpol's agrarian, structural definition excludes.

## Operationalizations

Skocpol identifies a social revolution by the joint presence of three empirical elements (her France/Russia/China cases): (1) incapacitation/breakdown of the central old-regime state; (2) widespread class-based (especially peasant) revolt from below; (3) a mass-mobilizing leadership that consolidates a new, more centralized and bureaucratic state. Absent any element, the case is classed as a political revolution, a failed/abortive revolution, or reform from above (Japan, Prussia). Rival traditions operationalize revolution differently — by "multiple sovereignty" (Tilly), value-system breakdown (Johnson), or the class character of the new ruling group (orthodox Marxism).

## Applications

Skocpol's three positive cases — the French (1787–1800), Russian (1917–), and Chinese (1911–1949) revolutions — plus a set of "broadly resembling" agrarian cases she names (Mexico, Yugoslavia, Vietnam, Algeria, Cuba, Bolivia, Angola, Mozambique, Guinea-Bissau, Ethiopia). See [[france-russia-china-revolutions]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Agrarian and structural bias.** The definition is built for agrarian old-regime states with a revolting peasantry; it fits urban, negotiated, colonial, or "refolutionary" (1989) cases poorly. Skocpol herself denied it could ground a "general theory of revolutions."
- **Success built into the definition** risks selection on the outcome — a standing methodological objection (only "successful" cases are social revolutions, making failed cases definitionally separate).
- **Under-weighting of agency, ideology, and contingency** — the concept's structural framing is the target of the [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

## Wolf *Peasant Wars* (1969) — related (not synonym)

[[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] analyzes twentieth-century **peasant wars** (rebellions and revolutions with peasants as principal actors) as responses to capitalist commodity expansion and stratified [[tactical-mobility]], not as a formal definition competing with Skocpol’s **social revolution**. Overlap of cases (Russia, China; Skocpol also names Mexico, Vietnam, Algeria, Cuba as broadly resembling) without identity of concept: Wolf’s object is agrarian revolutionary process and recruitment; Skocpol’s is dual transformation of state and class structure. See [[wolf-peasant-wars-study]] · [[peasant-movements]] · [[middle-peasant]].

## Lenin: Marx's 1859 Preface formula and the 1917 sequence (attributed)

In *[[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894|What the "Friends of the People" Are]]* (1894), Lenin's earliest exposition of the concept quoted Marx's 1859 Preface to *A Critique of Political Economy* at length as its canonical statement: relations of production correspond to the development of material productive forces, and "**social revolution**" begins when productive forces come into conflict with existing production relations (see [[base-and-superstructure]], [[historical-materialism]]). He glossed capitalism's "socialisation of labour" (industry's specialization combined with capital concentration, merging separate production processes into one interdependent social process while ownership remains private) as producing an "irreconcilable contradiction" between social production and private appropriation that "must adapt itself... and become social, that is, socialist."

In the 1917 insurrectionary writings (*[[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]*), Lenin applied the concept practically: he called the October events "the third Russian revolution" (after 1905 and February 1917), one that "should in the end lead to the victory of socialism," and, defending the January 1918 dissolution of the Constituent Assembly, argued the clash between Soviet power and the Assembly "results from the entire course of the Russian revolution," which could not "stop at the victory of the bourgeois revolution" — wartime conditions having created objective conditions favorable to social (as against merely political/bourgeois) revolution. He distinguished the war-time worker-peasant military alliance (which he assigned to "the bourgeois revolution") from the economic alliance underpinning socialist construction (assigned to "the socialist revolution"), summarized as "The alliance of the workers and peasants = α and ω of Soviet power... the first = bourgeois revolution / the second = socialist revolution" (*[[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921]]*).
