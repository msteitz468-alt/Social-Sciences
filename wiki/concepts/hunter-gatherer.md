# Hunter-Gatherer (Forager)

## Definition as Coined

A society subsisting on **wild foods** — hunted game, gathered plants, and fish or shellfish
— without domesticated crops or herds. The *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*
symposium (1966; pub. 1968) is the point at which the category was consolidated as the
organizing object of a research field, and where its instability was first thoroughly
aired.

*(Attributed — Lee & DeVore 1968.)* The editors framed the hunting way of life as "the most
successful and persistent adaptation man has ever achieved," covering over 99% of the roughly
two-million-year span of "Cultural Man," and estimated that of ~150 billion humans who ever
lived, over 60% lived as hunter-gatherers. The symposium declined to fix a strict
definition: an ideal "evolutionary" definition (strictly Pleistocene economies — no metal,
firearms, dogs, or contact with non-hunters) was judged impractical because, as
[[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] noted, "nowhere today do we find hunters living in a world of
hunters." It "agreed to consider as hunters all cases presented," as convenient shorthand.

## Semantic History

The construct drifted repeatedly. **First**, from a *hunting*-centred to a *gathering*-centred
model: [[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Lee's]] data showed hunting supplies only ~20–40% of diet
across most of the world, with gathering (50%) and fishing (33%) dominant except at high
latitudes — "the 'hunting' way of life appears to be in the minority." **Second**, the
category's coherence was challenged from within: James Deetz argued that grouping societies
by "total absence of productive economy" lumps radically different cultures (Kwakiutl,
Eskimo, Arapaho) while splitting culturally similar ones with partial cultivation, so
"Man the Gatherer" or "Man the Potter" might cut the field as well. [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]
proposed redefining it as "a certain type of behavior" existing in "pure, blended, and even
highly diluted forms," not a fixed list of subsistence-pure tribes.

## Operationalizations

- **Ethnographic Atlas coding** (Murdock 1967): % dependence on gathering vs. hunting vs.
  fishing; the symposium widened "hunting" to include sea-mammal pursuit and "gathering" to
  include shellfishing.
- **Murdock's strict inventory** (Chapter 2): excluded *mounted hunters*, *sedentary
  fishermen*, and *incipient tillers*, enumerating **27 surviving groups** worldwide "(as of
  Murdock 1968)."
- **Demographic profile:** densities rarely above one person per square mile; local groups
  ("bands") modally ~25 persons and language communities ~500 (the "magic numbers"; see
  [[carrying-capacity]]).
- **Type question** (Murdock, discussion 35a): hunter-gatherers show "a surprisingly narrow
  range of variation" in social organization (nuclear family + local community, uniform
  [[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labour]], flexible residence, rarely unilineal
  descent) — "except for the Australians," who are unilineal and strongly patrilocal.

## Applications

The category underwrites hunter-gatherer studies as a subfield; the
[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] thesis; band-organization theory
([[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[patrilocal-band-model-debate]]); and the use of living
foragers to model the Pleistocene ([[ethnographic-analogy]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

The category conflates ecologically and organizationally disparate societies (Damas showed
this even *within* the Eskimo culture area); it silently imports a "hunting ideology" onto
gathering-based economies; and its analytic weight rests on the fraught assumption that
living foragers, mostly confined to marginal habitats after long contact, represent past
foragers (see [[kalahari-debate]], [[ethnographic-analogy]]). Freeman argued modern foragers
are "many thousands of times more likely to be representative of all modern hunting-gathering
groups than all such groups of the present are to be representative of the total range of
hunting-gathering adaptations past and present." The term is best used with its dating,
region, and evidence class specified.

**Cooking-universal densification (Wrangham 2009).** *[[wrangham-catching-fire-2009|Catching Fire]]* mobilizes foragers (Hadza day structure; !Kung via Marshall; Inuit via Stefansson; calorie shares across nine groups) as secondary evidence that cooking is universal, that evening camp meals are the social norm (raw snacks mostly out of camp), and that men’s long hunting hours presuppose cooked food. These are Wrangham’s program uses of ethnography for the [[cooking-hypothesis]], not new primary forager fieldwork — date and attribute accordingly.

**Analogical use in *Origins* (Leakey & Lewin 1977).** *[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]* deploys living foragers — the !Kung and G/wi of the Kalahari (via [[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Richard Lee's]] fieldwork), the Hadza, BaMbuti net-hunters, and Arctic Eskimo groups — as **[[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic analogy]]** for the Plio-Pleistocene past, explicitly flagged by the authors as a hypothesis-generator rather than evidence. They report the !Kung diet as roughly one-third meat and two-thirds plant food, endorse [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins']] [[original-affluent-society|"original affluent society"]] characterization, and conclude that foragers "should probably more properly be called gatherer-hunters" (attributed, Leakey & Lewin 1977). They also propose a four-level typology of foraging societies by dependence on hunted meat (Hadza/Paliyans low; !Kung ~30–40%; BaMbuti cooperative net-hunting; Eskimo near-total), arguing — as an attributed and contestable interpretive claim — that women's social status is "inversely proportional to the amount of meat hunted" (see [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]], [[food-sharing]]).

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Chs. 3–4, 7–8.)*

- **Artefact/settlement binaries fail.** Barker argues traditional functional interpretation of artefacts via ethnographic analogy is unreliable as a forager/farmer signature: pottery, sickles, grinding stones, polished axes, and microliths all have cross-cutting uses; some foragers made pottery long before farming; some farmers lacked pottery. Victorian equation houses = sedentism = farming is “extremely dangerous” — mobile foragers can leave non-site scatters; sedentary foragers with houses/silos exist ethnographically and archaeologically; pastoralists and farmers use mixed settlement forms (attributed).
- **Terminology traps (South Asia).** Geometric microlith sites labelled “Mesolithic” often include Holocene herders; early handmade pottery labelled “Neolithic” was used by foragers, forager-farmers, and farmers (attributed).
- **Forager complexity and choice.** Early Natufians as delayed-return collectors with permanent settlement without agriculture ([[abu-hureyra]]); American and African cases of foragers adopting crops tactically while preserving foraging lifeways for millennia (SW maize storage; Nile domesticates as one resource among generalized procurement); African ethnography: most farmers also foraged, most herders also farmed and foraged — kaleidoscope better than one-way forager→farmer process (attributed positions).
- **Iconography method.** Do not split material into “everyday” vs “ritual”; ethnographic art (Aborigines, Kalahari San) largely produced in ritual/trance contexts (attributed). Cross-links: [[sedentism]], [[broad-spectrum-revolution]], [[natufian-culture]], [[barker-graeme]].

## Lee 1979 densification (*The !Kung San*)

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979; study [[lee-kung-san-study]]; Dobe-area !Kung / Ju/'hoansi fieldwork 1963–64, 1967–69, 1973.)*

- **Not living fossils.** Contemporary foragers are fully historical humans; evolutionary use requires first accounting for contact with agricultural, pastoral, or industrial societies — "nowhere today do we find… hunters living in a world of hunters" (echoing Sahlins). The category remains a primary socioeconomic form with evolutionary priority, but cases are not pristine time capsules.
- **Work and diet (July–Aug 1964 Dobe diary, recalculated).** ~**2.4** subsistence days/adult/week; **2,355 Cal** + **96.3 g protein**/person/day; gathering ~**70%** of calories, hunting ~**30%**; full work week including tools and housework ~**42 hrs** average. Modest seasonal adult weight loss (~**1–2%**) vs agricultural hungry-season losses of 4–6.5%. See [[original-affluent-society]], [[lee-what-hunters-do-study]] (1968 figures refined).
- **Method of inference.** Dual tools *(attributed, Ch.15)*: **uniformitarian** reconstruction (same ecological processes; measurable energy, space, resources, vital statistics) plus **Marxist mode-of-production** analysis for consciousness, gender, and social relations — mechanical animal-ecology models insufficient. Specific ethnographic numbers must not be pasted onto specific archaeological sites; extract underlying principles across variability.
- **Social relations of foraging.** Environment as "storehouse"; no amassed surplus; generalized reciprocity / sharing as ethical core of the mode; low capital, portability, collective land rights (*n!ore* / *k”ausi*); work as intervening variable between population and food (scarcity met by working harder, not only by dying). Large dry-season aggregations raise work *and* conflict — social motives override pure least-effort.
- **Transition.** Lee revises early "full-time pristine forager" impressions with mafisa cattle work, farming attempts, wage labor, and 1970s state/military incorporation, while arguing early Dobe contact was with adjacent precapitalist neighbors and that cash/sedentarization (not wild-resource collapse) can produce the heavier seasonal stress Wilmsen reported. *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Wilmsen 1989]]* (primary) counters that San "foraging" is underclass livelihood within deep regional integration — see [[kalahari-debate]], [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]].
