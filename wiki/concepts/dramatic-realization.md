# Dramatic Realization

## Definition as Coined

[[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (*[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, 1956) argued that while in others' presence the individual typically **infuses activity with signs that dramatically highlight and portray confirmatory facts** that might otherwise remain unapparent or obscure; he must mobilize activity so it expresses during the interaction what he wishes to convey — sometimes in a split second (attributed).

Some statuses (prizefighter, surgeon, violinist, policeman) have instrumentally essential acts that also vividly convey claimed attributes. Others face **visibility problems** (medical nursing that looks like "just visiting"; undertakers charging for visible caskets because other costs cannot be dramatized).

**Expression vs action dilemma** (attributed): energy spent making performance *apparent* may divert from doing the task well; those who perform well may lack time or talent to make it apparent. Organizations may delegate dramatic function to a specialist who expresses meaning without doing the task.

## Semantic History

From 1956 technical sense to looser "making a show of work" (make-work, presenteeism).

## Operationalizations

Observation of accentuated gestures, uniforms, instantaneous decisive displays; comparison of invisible vs visible components of occupational work.

## Applications

Service work, medicine, ceremonial offices, aristocratic "arts of superiority" (Adam Smith, cited). Links: [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]], [[impression-management]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Visibility bias: what can be dramatized is not always what matters instrumentally; Goffman treats that as a structural dilemma, not a moral failing alone.
