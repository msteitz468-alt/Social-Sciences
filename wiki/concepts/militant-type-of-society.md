# Militant Type of Society

## Definition as Coined

Spencer’s polar social type organized for **chronic militancy** (war and preparation for war). In the dedicated *Principles* chapter he argued, a priori and by illustration:

- Corporate action for offence/defence must combine all capable fighters; non-combatants form a **permanent commissariat**.
- Development of the type **binds the society tightly**; units lose individuality — “under the militant type the individual is owned by the State.”
- Regulative form tends toward **despotism** and graded **status** (masters above, subjects below), with **regimentation** extended from army to civil life.
- Government is both **positively and negatively regulative** (commands what one shall do as well as shall not).
- **Fundamental principle: compulsory cooperation.**

contrasts_with: [[industrial-type-of-society]] · proposed_by: [[spencer-herbert]] · part_of: [[social-evolutionism]]

Spencer warned against errors of comparison: militant traits of a vast nation cannot be expected in a small “savage” horde equally militant; most real cases display the type **incompletely**; types are habitually **mingled**.

## Semantic History

Related to but **not identical** with Comte’s [[theological-military-system]] (intellectual stage + military organization in Comte’s philosophy of history). Spencer’s type is an organizational constitution keyed to war vs industry, not to fetichism/polytheism/monotheism. Later “garrison state,” militarism, and authoritarian-regime typologies sometimes echo the construct without Spencerian evolutionism — link only with attribution.

## Operationalizations

Trait cluster (Spencer): universal militant cooperation; commissariat subordination of workers; centralization; status; positive regulation; economic bias toward self-sufficiency and protection; character traits he associated with militancy (revenge, loyalty, faith, anti-initiative — attributed characterology). Metamorphosis: war reversion reintroduces militant traits into more industrial societies.

## Applications

Used throughout *Principles* Parts II–IV: ceremonial subordination, slavery/serfdom as commissariat labour, revenue by force and *corvée*, priestly war blessing, sex division of labour under hunting/war regimes, narrowing of political heads and councils under militancy.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Ideal/polar type, not a census category.
- Survival arguments (“communities with complete militant cooperation win”) are functional-selection stories, not demonstrated historical laws.
- Ethnographic and historical illustrations are 19th-c. source-bound.
- Ethical-political use: Spencer valorized the industrial pole; reading the type as neutral description without that valence misstates the *Principles*.
