# Colorblind Racism

**Color-blind racism** is the name given by [[bonilla-silva-eduardo|Eduardo Bonilla-Silva]] to the dominant racial ideology of the post–civil rights United States: the set of interpretive frames and rhetorical practices through which whites explain persistent racial inequality as the outcome of nonracial dynamics, thereby defending the racial order while disclaiming racism. A related but distinct usage — colorblindness as the hegemonic *racial project* — is developed by [[omi-michael|Omi]] & [[winant-howard|Winant]]. The two treatments are kept attributed and separate below; do not merge them into a single wiki-voice definition.

## Definition as Coined

Bonilla-Silva (*[[bonilla-silva-racism-without-racists-2018|Racism without Racists]]*, 2003; 5th ed. 2018) defines color-blind racism as the ideology that "acquired cohesiveness and dominance in the late 1960s" and "explains contemporary racial inequality as the outcome of nonracial dynamics" — market forces, naturally occurring preferences, and imputed cultural limitations of minorities — rather than the biological/moral inferiority asserted under Jim Crow (attributed). He calls it "racism lite": it "otherizes softly" and serves as "the ideological armor for a covert and institutionalized system in the post–civil rights era." On his [[racialized-social-system|structural theory]], ideology is produced by the racial structure, so the racism persists even without avowed racists — hence *racism without racists*.

## The Four Central Frames (Bonilla-Silva, attributed)

| Frame | Definition | Typical expression |
|---|---|---|
| Abstract liberalism | Using liberal ideas (equal opportunity, individualism, choice, meritocracy, anti-coercion) in an abstract way to oppose concrete race-conscious remedies | "I'm for equal opportunity, that's why I oppose affirmative action"; "nothing should be forced" |
| Naturalization | Explaining racial phenomena (segregation, endogamy) as natural and universal | "birds of a feather flock together"; "they do it too" |
| Cultural racism | Attributing minorities' standing to fixed cultural traits rather than biology | "Blacks/Mexicans don't emphasize education/hard work" |
| Minimization of racism | Claiming discrimination is no longer central to minorities' life chances | "it's better now than in the past"; "they play the race card" |

Bonilla-Silva argues **abstract liberalism** is the foundational and hardest-to-recognize frame, and that the four interlock as "an impregnable yet elastic wall": their pliability — allowing exceptions ("the exceptional black"), ranging from crude to gentle, and citing color-blind ideals (King's "dream") — makes the ideology resilient to direct rebuttal (attributed).

## The Style of Color Blindness (attributed)

Bonilla-Silva analyzes a distinctive *style* or "race talk," treated as socially learned rules "beyond good and evil" rather than evidence of individual moral character: (1) avoidance of overt racial epithets; (2) **semantic moves** that shield the speaker ("I'm not prejudiced, but…"; "some of my best friends are…"; "I'm not black, so I don't know"; "yes and no, but…"; "anything but race"); (3) **projection** of racism onto minorities (e.g. reading black clustering as "self-segregation"); (4) **diminutives** ("a little bit against affirmative action"); (5) **rhetorical incoherence** that spikes on charged topics (interracial marriage, self-segregation). Bonilla-Silva reports younger, educated, middle-class whites are more adept at this softer style; older, working-class whites express racial resentment more bluntly. Whites also deploy racial narratives — impersonal **story lines** ("the past is the past"; "if Jews/Italians/Irish made it, how come blacks haven't?"; "I didn't get that job because of a black man") and first-person **testimonies** — that do ideological work.

## Omi & Winant: Colorblindness as Racial Project (attributed)

In *[[omi-winant-racial-formation-2015|Racial Formation]]* (3rd ed., 2015), [[omi-michael|Omi]] & [[winant-howard|Winant]] analyze **colorblindness** as the hegemonic contemporary [[racial-project|racial project]]: a racial common sense claiming the civil rights movement's goals are achieved by *ignoring* race, so that race-conscious remedies are recast as "reverse racism." This is analytically distinct from Bonilla-Silva's account — a project within [[racial-formation-theory|racial formation theory]] rather than a four-frame ideology coded from interviews — though the two converge on colorblindness as leaving racial structure intact.

## Semantic History

Post–civil rights neoconservative and neoliberal rearticulation of race; "post-racial" rhetoric intensified around the Obama presidency. Bonilla-Silva argues color-blind racism remained hegemonic even under Trump, whose insistence that he is "the least racist person" he reads as genuflection to color-blind norms (attributed; see [[the-new-racism]]).

## Operationalizations

Bonilla-Silva operationalizes the concept as interview-coded frames, style, and stories, cross-checked against survey items (the [[bonilla-silva-racism-without-racists-study|1997 college survey and 1998 Detroit-Area Study]]); Omi & Winant work through discourse and policy analysis. The traditions disagree on whether colorblindness is best framed as coded ideology, racial project, or institutional practice.

## Applications

The four-frame framework is a standard analytic tool in the sociology of race, applied to affirmative-action and welfare attitudes, opposition to school and residential integration, interracial-marriage reservations, and — beyond whites — the partial, indirect uptake of color-blind frames among Black respondents (a minority, per Bonilla-Silva). It underwrites his reading of the [[the-new-racism|new racism]] regime, the [[white-habitus|white habitus]], and the projected [[latin-americanization-of-race|Latin Americanization]] of the U.S. racial order.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested on whether ordinary color-blind discourse should be labeled *racism* and whether interview coding warrants the strong ideological reading (see [[structural-vs-attitudinal-racism-debate]]). Bonilla-Silva's own stance is that of an engaged, political sociologist.
