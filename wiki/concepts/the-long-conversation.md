# The Long Conversation

> Coined by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991).

## Definition as Coined

"The long conversation" is the Comaroffs' image for the [[christian-mission|missionary]]–[[southern-tswana|Tswana]] encounter as "the drawn out process of colonization": a protracted "exchange of signs and substance" in which "each party was to try to gain some purchase on, some mastery over, the other" — the churchmen "to convert the Tswana to Christianity; the Tswana, to divert the potency of the churchmen to themselves." It began not with "a crashing military onslaught" but "with polite ceremony," in which "there was, hidden in the politesse, oblique forewarning of later struggles."

## Semantic History

The term crystallizes a mode of analysis: colonization as dialogue rather than decree. Its coining shifts attention from conquest to the **media** of the encounter — space, water, language, the body — through which "the vital signs and practices of a new [[hegemony]] were instilled into Tswana consciousness." In the exchange, signifiers were "set afloat, fought over, and recaptured on both sides": Tswana seized mission forms toward unforeseen ends, while "some of the ways of the Africans interpolated themselves … into the [[habitus]] of the missionaries."

## Operationalizations

The Comaroffs trace it across concrete domains — the politics of *space* (settlement plans, the water mill, the church vs. the chief's tree), of *water* (the rainmaking debates), and of *language* (translating *Modimo*/*badimo*, standardizing Setswana, new names) — each a site where contestation itself transmitted colonial forms.

## Applications

The connective concept of the [[colonization-of-consciousness]] argument and of *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*; widely borrowed as a model for other colonial and missionary encounters. A key corollary: the encounter *objectified* Tswana culture itself, producing the very category **setswana** ("Tswana ways") as a bounded system opposed to **sekgoa** (European ways) — a distinction the Comaroffs argue was a product of the conversation, not a precolonial given.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Conversation" can imply more symmetry than the sharply unequal encounter possessed; the Comaroffs stress that "although the churchmen were to prove more capable of imposing their designs upon the colonial field, the Tswana were hardly passive recipients." As with the whole program, the Tswana side is recovered largely from the European record (see [[historical-ethnography]]).
