# Social Appropriation

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* Mechanism by which **existing social sites** (churches, schools, associations, everyday networks of fate and trust) are **turned into vehicles** of contentious claim-making once threat/opportunity attributions push toward innovative action.

## Definition as Coined

> "When attributions of threat and opportunity lead to the kinds of innovative collective action… particular actors frequently appropriate existing social space and collective identities in the service of these interpretations. We call that mechanism… social appropriation."

Authors first illustrate with Black churches in the American civil rights movement; Ch. 4 compares Mau Mau and Philippine Yellow Revolution appropriation patterns. Appropriation and brokerage involve **foregone** framings and linkages as much as strategies adopted (Communist non-participation in the Yellow Revolution as “dog that didn’t bark”).

## Semantic History

DOC mobilization chapter; related to resource-mobilization “bloc recruitment” and McAdam’s organizational-infrastructure arguments for civil rights. Distinct from mere resource possession: the site is **converted** to public contention.

## Operationalizations

Transfer of organizational resources, membership, and identities into public claim-making; dual tracks of sectoral left umbrellas vs middle-class Church/business umbrellas (Philippines).

## Applications

*(DOC attributed:)* Mau Mau radical oathing and appropriation of Kikuyu social space; Philippine BAYAN/KASAMA/BISIG sectoral tracks vs BANDILA, JAJA, NAMFREL, Church; NPA skill at Church/union infiltration *without* joining the Yellow uprising.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary with ordinary organizational change; intentionality varies; success of appropriation does not determine outcome once certification and repression intervene.
