## Definition as Coined

Sex-biased admixture describes cases where the male and female ancestors contributing to a mixed population's descendants came disproportionately from different source populations — detectable because the Y chromosome passes only through the paternal line and mitochondrial DNA only through the maternal line, while autosomal DNA and (with more nuance) the X chromosome reflect both. David Reich treats a mismatch between paternal-line, maternal-line, and genome-wide ancestry proportions as a genetic signature of asymmetric social power between mixing populations — very often, in the cases Reich discusses, of male dominance and reproductive coercion (as of Reich 2018).

## Semantic History

Reich develops the concept across several case studies in *Who We Are and How We Got Here* (2018), presenting it as recovering demographic and social history — including histories of conquest and inequality — that is invisible in autosomal ancestry proportions alone (as of Reich 2018).

## Operationalizations

- **Post-conquest Latin America**: Reich reports that in the Antioquia region of Colombia (settled and isolated from the 16th to 19th centuries), about 94% of Y chromosomes are of European origin versus about 90% of mitochondrial DNA of Native American origin, while total genome-wide ancestry is only about 80% European — a pattern Reich attributes to repeated waves of male-only European immigration mixing with a smaller founding female Native American population (as of Reich 2018).
- **African Americans**: Reich cites a study (Bryc et al., using 23andMe data on more than 5,000 self-described African Americans) finding average European ancestry of 27% genome-wide but only 23% on the X chromosome, implying separate male (about 38%) and female (about 10%) European-ancestor contributions — i.e., a roughly fourfold greater contribution from European American men than European American women (as of Reich 2018). Sociologist Orlando Patterson, in discussion with Reich, noted the male sex bias was likely far greater during the era of slavery than the modern average suggests, and that the bias has partially reversed since the civil-rights era due to increased black-male/white-female coupling (as of Reich 2018).
- **South Asian caste formation**: Reich reports that the formation of India's population roughly 4,000–2,000 years ago shows strong sex bias: higher-status endogamous groups carry more West-Eurasian-related ancestry, concentrated on the paternal (Y-chromosome) line, while mitochondrial DNA remains largely of local South Asian origin — consistent with West-Eurasian-related males occupying higher positions in an emerging caste system and sometimes marrying lower-ranking local women (as of Reich 2018). See [[caste]], [[ancestral-north-indians]].
- **Yamnaya/steppe migration into Europe**: the male-biased character of steppe-ancestry spread associated with the [[yamnaya-culture]] and [[corded-ware-culture]] is discussed by Reich in terms comparable to these other cases, though Reich notes the Y-chromosome/mitochondrial-DNA method becomes "nearly useless" for resolving events more than about 10,000 years in the past, since too few surviving lineages remain for statistical power; whole-genome X-chromosome-versus-autosome comparison offers more resolution but is complicated by strong natural selection acting on the X chromosome (as of Reich 2018).
- **Reduced non-African X-chromosome diversity**: Reich (citing Keinan and Reich 2009) reports reduced X-chromosome genetic diversity in non-Africans relative to autosomes, beyond what equal male/female founding would predict, suggesting repeated waves of male-biased gene flow into the ancestral non-African population after its split from African populations — a pattern also documented as reduced X-chromosome diversity in Central African Pygmy populations relative to neighboring Bantu-speaking groups (as of Reich 2018).
- **Counterexample — the Pacific**: mixed Pacific Islander populations show the reverse pattern from Reich's other cases: East Asian-derived ancestry is inherited predominantly through female rather than male lineages, per initial mitochondrial/Y-chromosome studies. Reich reports two proposed explanations without adjudicating between them: matrilineal property inheritance with males as the primary inter-island movers in early Pacific societies, or later-arriving, socially advantaged Papuan-ancestry males mixing with an already-established East Asian-related female population. Reich explicitly cites this case as a caution against assuming anthropological expectations will always be confirmed by genetic data (as of Reich 2018).

## Applications

Reich frames sex-biased admixture as, in his words, part of a broader "genomics of inequality" — a way genetic data records histories of conquest, colonization, and reproductive inequality that documentary and archaeological records alone may not capture (as of Reich 2018). This is the organizing concept of the chapter of *Who We Are and How We Got Here* addressing genomics and social inequality.

## Critiques and Limitations

Reich cautions that sex-biased admixture signatures are read as evidence of a general demographic pattern (asymmetric male/female contribution) but that the underlying *social* mechanism — conquest, elite intermarriage, differential mobility, inheritance rules — must be argued from historical or archaeological context rather than assumed from the genetic pattern alone, as the Pacific counterexample illustrates (as of Reich 2018). The method also degrades sharply in time-depth, per the roughly 10,000-year limit Reich states for Y-chromosome/mtDNA-based inference.
