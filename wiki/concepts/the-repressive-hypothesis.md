# The Repressive Hypothesis

## Definition as Coined

**The repressive hypothesis** is the name [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] gives, in *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I, to the story he sets out to refute (attributed): that from the seventeenth century modern Western — "Victorian," bourgeois — society **repressed** sex, confining it to the conjugal bedroom, imposing silence, and tolerating illicit sexuality only in marginal sites (the brothel, the asylum). In its usual form the narrative ties this repression to capitalism (sex repressed in the service of the labor imperative) and offers a "speaker's benefit": to speak of sex now feels transgressive and liberating. Foucault: "If sex is repressed... then the mere fact that one is speaking about it has the appearance of a deliberate transgression."

## Semantic History

The label is Foucault's coinage for a target, not a self-description by its holders — chiefly the Freudo-Marxian tradition of Wilhelm Reich and, in this vault, [[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]] (see [[repressive-desublimation]]). Foucault raises "three serious doubts": (1) is sexual repression really an established historical fact from the seventeenth century? (2) does power actually work mainly through repression and prohibition? (3) was the twentieth-century critique of repression a genuine rupture, or part of the same historical network it denounces? He is careful *not* to claim sex was never prohibited — only that it is "a ruse to make prohibition into the basic and constitutive element" of the history of sex.

## Operationalizations

Against the hypothesis Foucault marshals: the "discursive explosion" around sex from the eighteenth century (see [[discourse-foucauldian]]); the "multiple implantation of 'perversions'" and multiplication (not reduction) of sexualities; the building of a [[scientia-sexualis]] on [[confession]]; and the productive, not repressive, character of the power at work ("this power had neither the form of the law, nor the effects of the taboo... it produced and determined the sexual mosaic"). His conclusion: "We must therefore abandon the hypothesis that modern industrial societies ushered in an age of increased sexual repression."

## Applications — the hypothesis as itself a discourse-effect

Foucault's sharpest move (Part Four, "Periodization") is genealogical: he argues the theory of repression **originated as a class-differentiating discourse**. The techniques of sexual control formed first in the privileged classes, which "first tried [sexuality] on themselves" as "an intensification of the body," an "autosexualization" — "the bourgeoisie's 'blood' was its sex." Only later did the bourgeoisie seek a new internal "bar" by claiming that "our sexuality... is subjected to a regime of repression so intense." Repression thus becomes, on Foucault's reading, a claim the bourgeoisie makes *about itself*, not a universal fact — and the interwar Reichian critique and the "sexual revolution" unfold *within* the [[deployment-of-sexuality]], as "a tactical shift and reversal," not against it. See [[repressive-hypothesis-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Social historians (attributed) contest Foucault's dismissal of real repression and censorship, and his specific periodization.
- Feminist and Marxist readers (attributed) argue that reframing repression as merely a discourse-effect risks obscuring material coercion and inequality. Recorded here as Foucault's thesis, not as settled history.
