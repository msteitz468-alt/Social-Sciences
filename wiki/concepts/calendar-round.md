# Calendar Round

## Definition as Coined

The **Calendar Round** combines a **260-day** sacred cycle (13 numbers × 20 day names) with a **365-day** Vague Year (18×20 named “months” + 5 Wayeb days). The paired date repeats every 52 Vague Years. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] treat it as older than the [[long-count|Long Count]] and still living in parts of the highland Maya world into modern ethnography.

## Semantic History

- **Classic:** Used with Long Count on Initial Series; Period Endings and New Year rites structure space-time.
- **Contact Yucatan:** Landa-era Vague Year beginning c. 16 July (Julian) as reported; **no leap-day intercalation** (sophisticated leap corrections once attributed to Maya are rejected in C&H narrative for the Vague Year itself).
- **Modern:** Highland groups retain Calendar Round practice; Coe & Houston note Yukateko lowland loss of the 260-day calendar in contrast — continuity is regional, not pan-Maya.

## Operationalizations

52-year completion cycles; directional New Year associations; pairing with Long Count for unique dates within historical spans.

## Applications

[[classic-maya]] ritual time; [[zinacantan]] and highland ethnography; codices.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Vague Year” drifts vs tropical year without intercalation — a known feature, not a bug, in the system as described. Ethnographic survivals must not be projected unchanged onto Classic lowland practice.

### From Sharer & Traxler 2006

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]].

**Definition and scope.** Combination of the 260-day almanac and 365-day [[haab|Haab]] produces a **52-year Calendar Round** (LCM **18,980** days). Except for the Maya (who had the [[long-count|Long Count]]), the 52-year cycle was the largest calendrical unit of all Mesoamerican societies; Mexica *xiuhmolpilli* (“year bundle”) with New Fire renewal is the Central Mexican analogue they cite.

**Year-bearers.** Only four almanac days can occupy the first position of the Haab: Classic year-bearers **Ak’bal, Lamat, Ben, Etz’nab** (52 = 4×13 possibilities); by Spanish Conquest year-bearers had shifted to **K’an, Muluk, Ix, Kawak**.

**Cyclic history and elite power.** From Postclassic sources they report a notion of **cyclic history** (each cycle bringing possible repeated destiny); supernatural forces ruled numbers, time, and cosmos; elite calendrical knowledge was a source of power, though farmers needed basic knowledge for daily life.

**Pairing with Long Count.** Classic Initial Series couple Long Count with Calendar Round (almanac day + Haab day). By Late Classic, abbreviated period-ending dates still carry Calendar Round components (e.g., K’atun 16 2 Ajaw 13 Sek for 9.16.0.0.0). Postclassic *u k’ahlay k’atunob* further abbreviates to *k’atun*-ending Ajaw designations cycling every thirteen *k’atuns* (~256 years).

**Mayapan chronological use.** They prefer traditional ~20-year *k’atun* (not 24-year Ajaw K’atun) because three Mayapan stelae preserve traditional *k’atun*-ending dates; founding equated with K’atun 8 Ajaw (AD 1185–1204) and abandonment with return of K’atun 8 Ajaw 256 years later; Gaspar Xiu’s 260-year span and 13 Central Plaza floor renovations align with one 13-*k’atun* cycle (Milbrath & Peraza Lope correlations with Chilam Balam noted).

**Conquest loss.** Colonial wars, disease, and book-burning destroyed elite calendrical institutions; much specialized Calendar Round / cosmology knowledge expired with the elite, even where fragments of day-keeping survived regionally.
