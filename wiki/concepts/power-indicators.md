# Power Indicators

Domhoff’s methodological triad for assessing power claims in power-structure research (*[[domhoff-who-rules-america-2022|Who Rules America?]]*, Ch. 1).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Domhoff.)* Claims about who has power should be checked against multiple **indicators**, classically:

1. **Who benefits** — distribution of income, wealth, and other valued outcomes (Domhoff cites high top-share wealth/income figures and CEO-to-worker pay ratios as benefit evidence — treat numbers as his synthesis of dated sources, not timeless wiki facts)  
2. **Who governs** — occupancy of key decision positions (corporate boards, cabinets, agencies, courts)  
3. **Who wins** — outcomes of concrete policy conflicts and legislative battles  

No single indicator is sufficient; convergence strengthens a domination claim. In Ch.8 he asserts the power elite scores “very high” on all three and that the American power elite holds a greater wealth/income share than counterparts in any other major industrialized democracy (attributed comparative claim). Explicitly anti-conspiracy: leadership is large, visible, and studyable.

## Semantic History

Part of Domhoff’s bid to make elite/class claims **falsifiable with organizational data**, against pure reputational or purely theoretical assertions. Related to community-power “who governs” language (Dahl) but applied nationally to class/corporate structure.

## Operationalizations

Tax and wealth data; cabinet/appointee biographies; roll-call and issue-case studies; network maps — as deployed across WRA chapters.

## Applications

Scaffold for the book’s empirical chapters; standard teaching device in power-structure methods.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Who benefits” can be structural without intentional power; “who wins” depends on issue selection; non-decisions (Bachrach/Baratz; Mills) may escape the triad unless carefully included.
