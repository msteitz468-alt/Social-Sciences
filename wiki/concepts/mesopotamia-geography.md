# Mesopotamia (Geographical Setting)

The physical stage of early Near Eastern civilization: the lowland basin of the Tigris
and Euphrates and its bordering desert, steppe, and mountain fringes. This page holds
the environmental and geographical framing that the [[city-state]], [[temple-economy]],
[[land-tenure]], and [[urbanization]] pages presuppose.

## Definition as Coined

"Mesopotamia" is a **later Greek coinage** ("the land between the rivers"); per
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], the ancient inhabitants had no term for the
country as a whole, using either the vague ("the Land," Sumerian *kalam*) or the
precise (Sumer, Akkad, Assur, Babylon). Per [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate
1994]] the toponyms themselves are late and partly obscure: Akkadian *māt akkadim* is
named for Sargon's still-unidentified capital; "Sumer" (*šumeru*) is first attested only
under Sargon's successor Rimush; Sumerian *ki-en-gi(r)* probably means "land of the
Sumerian tongue." Roux frames the field of study as a triangle of roughly **240,000 sq
km** bounded by Aleppo, Lake Urmia, and the head of the Gulf.

## Semantic History

Geographically the basin divides at the **Hit–Samarra line** (Roux 1992): north of it
the two rivers run in stable, incised valleys cut into plateau (so ancient cities such
as Mari, Nineveh, Nimrud, and Assur remain on or near their banks); south of it they
build a flat alluvial delta, meander, raise their own beds, and shift course — which is
why southern cities are now stranded ruin-mounds miles from any modern watercourse. Roux
also identifies a long-running **north–south opposition** (later Assyria vs.
Sumer-Akkad/Babylonia) that is cultural as often as it is political; Postgate treats the
north–south cultural distinction similarly, as a recurrent structuring fact rather than a
fixed ethnic boundary (four-way non-identity applies).

## Operationalizations

- **The rivers.** The Euphrates (Roux: ~2,780 km) is the slower, higher, more
  controllable stream; the Tigris (~1,950 km) is faster and more entrenched. Postgate
  notes that central-plain settlement historically concentrated on the **Euphrates**
  because it is easier to divert (higher elevation entering the plain, steeper gradient)
  and, per soil scientists he cites, carries more nutrient-rich silt (~20% better yields
  under modern controlled conditions, as of Postgate 1994).
- **Irrigation regime.** Because the flood peak comes in spring — too late for the winter
  cereal crop and too early for summer — southern agriculture depends on **perennial
  irrigation** (canals, sluices, dykes), not the Nile's convenient basin flood (Roux).
  Postgate details the engineering: gravity-flow supply through regulators, the
  levée-and-agricultural-cell layout (orchards and gardens nearest the fresh-water
  levée, less thirsty cereals pushed out), long-furrow field strips, and dyke protection
  (the basic *iku* plot ~60×60 m). Cereals needed only 4–6 waterings via a rota; river
  survey evidence (Adams & Nissen 1972, cited by Postgate) shows many small shifting
  canal branches consolidating into fewer permanent courses only in the mid-third
  millennium.
- **Resources.** Roux stresses Mesopotamia's **resource poverty**: abundant clay and
  bitumen but no metal ore, hard stone, or good timber — all imported from early times
  (copper from Iran/Anatolia/Oman, tin from the east, cedar from the Lebanon/Amanus,
  stone and lapis via the Gulf and overland). This import dependence underwrites the
  [[mesopotamian-trade|long-distance trade]] system.

## Applications

The geography is load-bearing for the wiki's Mesopotamia pages: the alluvium's
productivity-plus-import-poverty underlies [[urbanization]] and [[mesopotamian-trade]];
the irrigation regime underlies [[temple-economy]] and [[land-tenure]] (institutions
held systematic advantages in canal construction and risk-spreading — Postgate); and the
canal-and-river landscape frames the [[city-state]] as a settlement form.

## Critiques and Limitations

Two environmental questions are genuinely **disputed** in the literature this page rests
on:

- **The Gulf shoreline.** Whether the head of the Gulf once reached Ur/Lagash and
  retreated southward is unresolved: Postgate 1994 contrasts the older Lees & Falcon
  (1952) shoreline-retreat model with later drilling data (Nützel) suggesting little net
  change; Roux 1992 reports a partial reconciliation (Ice-Age low stand, mid-Holocene
  high stand ~1–2 m above present near Ur/Eridu). Record as an open position, not fact.
- **Salinization.** The **Jacobsen–Adams salinization thesis** (since the 1950s) holds
  that rising soil salinity drove the later-third-millennium wheat→barley shift and the
  18th–17th-century abandonment of southern cities. Postgate is **skeptical** — "a closer
  examination of the evidence fails to substantiate their case in detail" — while
  affirming that *local* salinity was a real, farmer-recognized problem (Early Dynastic
  land surveys record "salt" land; Šulgi-era surveys grade plot quality). This is a
  standing empirical dispute; see also the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] on irrigation
  management as a driver of political complexity (Postgate likewise rejects the strong
  Wittfogel version). All environmental-cause claims here are attributed and dated, per
  the wiki's rule that climate/environment are contributors, rarely sole causes.

## Climate, Rivers, and Soils (Potts 1997)

[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts (1997)]] adds substantial physical detail
to Roux's and Postgate's sketches, drawn largely from Iraqi hydrological and soil-science
literature.

**Climate.** The core of ancient southern Mesopotamia has a "dry continental variant of
the Mediterranean type" climate: wet winters (November–March) from eastward-moving
cyclones, hot dry summers (May–October); average annual rainfall of 115–135 mm (1929–59
data) falls far below the ~240 mm minimum needed for dry-farming, and 200 mm was recorded
on only three occasions across decades of records at Diwaniyah, Hai, and Nasiriyyah
(finding, per Potts 1997). Timing matters more than aggregate amount: rain after March has
little effect on the harvest, and warm January temperatures (+10°C) are needed for
uninterrupted plant growth (attributed; Adams 1981). Dew supplements rainfall and
irrigation in the arid zone; Theophrastus (*Hist. Plant.* VIII vi 6) already noted Egypt,
Babylon, and Bactra received useful nourishment from dew where rain was scarce.

Holocene climatic stability is a **live dispute**: P. Buringh (1957) and R. McC. Adams
(1981) held no important climate change occurred since human occupation of the alluvium
began (c. 5000/4000 BC), and that the plain was never wet enough for sustained
dry-farming; David and Joan Oates (1977), using Zagros pollen cores, argue for an arid
overall climate with detectable wetter/drier oscillations — e.g. marsh formation near Umm
Dabaghiyah suggesting wetter conditions c. 6000–5000 BC. John Kutzbach (1981) showed that
early-Holocene orbital forcing raised summer solar radiation c. 7000 BC by 6–7% above
present, intensifying the African-Eurasian summer monsoon; H.A. McClure's playa-lake study
in the Rub al-Khali (1984, 1988) found this mid-Holocene wet phase ended c. 4000 BC, with
approximately modern aridity reached by c. 1000 BC (Kay and Johnson 1981; Sanlaville
1992). Micro-climatic shifts persisted into historical times: date palms fruited around
Jabal Sinjar and Tell Afar in the 10th century AD (they do not currently fruit north of
Hit), and Neumann and Sigrist (1978) found barley-harvest dates in cuneiform texts shifted
3–8 weeks later between the Old Babylonian (c. 1800–1650 BC) and Neo-Babylonian (c.
600–400 BC) periods, interpreted as reflecting warmer Old Babylonian and cooler,
wetter Neo-Babylonian conditions relative to today.

**Rivers.** The [[irrigation|Tigris]] rises in the Taurus Mountains, runs c. 2032 km, and
drains 68,975 sq km above its delta head at Samarra; because more than half its drainage
is mountainous, it loses comparatively little water to evaporation, but its steep gradient
has cut a deep incised bed unsuited to gravity-flow irrigation before mechanical lift
devices. Discharge at Baghdad ranges from 158 to c. 13,000 cumecs — an 80-fold spread. The
Euphrates rises in central Turkey, runs c. 2720 km, and drains 163,120 sq km above its
delta head at Ramadi (nearly 3× the Tigris drainage); its gentler gradient produces a
slower, more evaporation-prone river that R. McC. Adams (1981) described as
"anastomising" — naturally splitting and rejoining in multiple shifting channels — in
contrast to the Tigris's single incised bed, which (combined with human diversion works)
explains why the ruin-mounds of Sumer and Akkad do not align with the modern river course.
Mean annual discharge at Hit is ~710 cumecs versus 1240 cumecs for the Tigris at Baghdad.
Of the 27 cu km of water in the Shatt al-Arab, 22 cu km originate in the Karun
(Khuzistan, Iran) rather than the Tigris-Euphrates system itself (Cressey 1958).

**Silt and salt loads.** In spate the Euphrates carries 1000–4000 ppm sediment, the Tigris
up to 25,000 ppm at peak — four to five times levels found in the Nile (Charles 1988); the
Tigris deposits an estimated 40,000,000 cu m of sediment annually, 90% of it in the lower
alluvial plains between Baghdad and Basra (Cressey 1958). See [[irrigation]] for the
mechanism and rate of resulting salinization.

**Soils.** The traditional view of superabundant Mesopotamian soil fertility traces to
Herodotus (*Hist.* I 93), who claimed grain yields of 200- to 300-fold; Potts states this
is contradicted by every modern soil scientist who has worked in Iraq — P. Buringh (1960)
concluded "the natural fertility of the soils of the Lowland Mesopotamian Plain is...
low," and soils have poor particle aggregation (in one experiment 97% of a dry cloddy soil
sample dispersed through a 0.25 mm sieve after 30 minutes of soaking) and very low organic
content (~0.05% or less, versus >3% for soils classed "fertile temperate"). Two soil types
form in the alluvium — coarser, better-drained **levee soils** and finer, more
salinization-prone **basin soils** — detailed on [[irrigation]] along with the
associated cultivation regimes.

## Delta Progradation and the Head of the Gulf — A Long-Running Debate

Whether, and how fast, the Tigris-Euphrates delta has advanced (prograded) southward
into the Gulf — and correspondingly whether the Gulf head once reached inland as far as
[[ur]] and [[eridu]] — has been argued continuously since antiquity and remains
unresolved (position; per Potts 1997).

Pliny (*Nat. Hist.* VI) already recorded the phenomenon: at the founding of Alexandria
(southernmost Mesopotamia) by Alexander the Great in 324 BC the settlement was ~1.8 km
from the sea; by Augustus's time Juba reported it 50 miles from the sea; by Pliny's own
lifetime (d. AD 79) it was reported 120 miles from the Euphrates mouth. Later
progradation-rate estimates varied widely: W.G. Carter (1835) calculated ~882 m/yr; W.K.
Loftus (1857) proposed 23 m/yr; Sir Henry Rawlinson (1857) proposed ~58.6 m/yr,
explicitly critiquing Pliny's implied rate as exaggerated; J. de Morgan (1900) used
Rawlinson's benchmark plus Sennacherib's 696 BC campaign and Nearchus's 325 BC voyage to
place the ancient Gulf head near modern Kut al-Amara.

In 1952 geologists G.M. Lees and N.L. Falcon argued tectonic subsidence had more than
cancelled out siltation, finding "no acceptable historical evidence that the head of the
Gulf was ever very far up-country from its present position" — a view quickly adopted by
most scholars, displacing de Morgan's model (though V. Gordon Childe was still repeating
it in 1952). In 1975 C.E. Larsen revived a modified progradation model incorporating the
Flandrian Transgression (the worldwide post-glacial sea-level rise beginning c. 17,000
BP, most rapid c. 12,000–8,000 BP), arguing for 150–180 km of progradation over the last
5000 years (30–36 m/yr) and concluding that cuneiform sources linking third-millennium Ur
and Eridu to the sea "appear to have more validity than was previously thought"; Larsen's
hypothesis was cautiously accepted by Adams (1981) and J.-L. Huot (1989).

P. Sanlaville's (1989) sea-level reconstruction for the Gulf is the most detailed
synthesis Potts reports: sea levels ~15–20 m lower than today at the start of the Ubaid
period (c. 5500 BC), reaching approximately modern levels by the end of Ubaid (c. 3800
BC), rising further to a maximum ~1–2 m above modern sea level c. 3500 BC, retreating to
approximately modern levels by c. 2800 BC, then oscillating within ±0–1 m until roughly
the time of Christ. On this basis H.J. Nissen's (1988a, 1988b) argument that sea levels
up to 2.75 m higher than today made much of lower Mesopotamia uninhabitable during the
Ubaid period is contradicted by Sanlaville's data (Oates 1991, 1993): Ubaid-period sea
levels were in fact *lower* than today, so sea level cannot explain any supposed absence
of early settlement in the alluvium. H. Waetzoldt (1981) adds independent faunal and
philological evidence against an inland Gulf reaching Ur/Eridu/Tello: excavated faunal
assemblages from Abu Salabikh, Nippur, Umm al-Hafriyat, Uruk, Isin, and Tell al-Hiba show
higher proportions of freshwater than salt/brackish fish species throughout, and the
Sumerian term *a-ab-ba* ("sea") is used ambiguously in Ur III texts for both nearby
lake/marsh expanses and the distant open Gulf. S.W. Cole (1994) independently showed a
large marsh around Borsippa (far from any projected Gulf shoreline) was routinely called
"the sea" (Akk. *tamirtu*) in first-millennium texts, meaning cuneiform "sea" references
must be treated cautiously.

Site elevation and sedimentation data bear on the same question: the plain near Tell
Oueili lies ~6 m above modern sea level; at [[uruk]] the Eanna sounding surface sits ~7 m
above sea level with sterile soil at 0.99 m, meaning the site's original elevation was
scarcely 1 m before ~6 m of subsequent cultural deposit accumulated (Adams and Nissen
1972); at Musaiyib on the middle Tigris ~6 m of alluvium accumulated since the mid-3rd
millennium BC, and in the Diyala region Adams (1965) estimates ~8 m of sediment
build-up since the 4th millennium (roughly 20 cm/century).

## Marshes and the Sealand

Southernmost Mesopotamia's marshes have shifted extent repeatedly. A 1950s excavation of
an irrigation canal near Musaiyib revealed a 6 m deep stratigraphic sequence of
alternating alluvial, canal, and marsh-bed deposits between the Old Akkadian and medieval
periods, documenting a recurring association between canal use and marsh formation
(Harris and Adams 1957). B. Meissner (1920) argued the absence of ancient mounds in the
Hor al-Hammar region proved it had lain underwater; G. Roux's 1960 survey showed this
reflected insufficient exploration rather than submergence, locating a string of mounds
from Tell Lahm to north of Basra, and a cylinder of Nabonidus (555–539 BC) found at Tell
al-Lahm confirms the area was unsubmerged well into the historical period (Saggs 1957).
No consensus exists on when the *modern* Hor al-Hammar swamp formed (position): a 1944
British Naval Intelligence handbook attributed it to post-1870 Euphrates flooding, while
the separately attested medieval "Great Swamp" (Kufa to Basra) is traced by the early
Islamic historian al-Baladhuri to Sasanian-period floods (between the reigns of Kavadh I,
AD 499–531, and Khusrau II, AD 590–628) when both rivers burst their dykes.

Southernmost Mesopotamia was known in cuneiform sources from the early 2nd millennium BC
as *mat tamti(m)* (Sum. KUR A.AB.BA), the "**Sealand**" (Brinkman 1993). The First
Sealand Dynasty's founder Ili-ma-AN was contemporary with Old Babylonian kings
Samsu-iluna (1749–1712 BC) and Abi-eshuh (1711–1684 BC); by the reign of the Kassite king
Nazi-Maruttash (1307–1282 BC) the Sealand had been annexed, and Kassite-period texts (a
Tell Kirbasi cattle record; grain and date deliveries to Nippur) indicate it functioned as
a stockbreeding and agricultural hinterland supplying the Kassite capital Dur-Kurigalzu
and the [[nippur]] temple. In the Neo-Assyrian period the Sealand was home to the
Chaldaean tribe Bit-Jakin; it remained a province under Neo-Babylonian rule through
Nebuchadrezzar II (604–562 BC) and again under Achaemenid Cambyses (529–522 BC).
