# New Class

## Definition as Coined

Milovan Djilas, in *The New Class* (1957), argued that communist systems had generated a **new ruling class** whose basis was political control of nationalised property and the party monopoly, not private ownership of capital. Mawdsley and White (2000) list Djilas's new class alongside Trotsky's "bureaucracy" and Voslensky's [[nomenklatura]] as competing designations for the Soviet ruling group; their own operational choice is Central Committee membership as a researchable elite population.

## Semantic History

Lineage runs through Bolshevik opposition critiques of bureaucratisation, Trotsky's *The Revolution Betrayed*, Djilas's Yugoslav dissident analysis, and later nomenklatura literature. Distinct from Western "new class" / knowledge-class debates (Gouldner et al.) — same phrase, different referent; keep domains separate unless a source explicitly bridges them.

## Operationalizations

Djilas: political monopoly + privilege structure. Mawdsley & White: do not adopt "new class" as their primary measure; they test privilege and representativeness empirically (Ch. 7) while using elite/CC language.

## Applications

Comparative communist elite theory; class-vs-elite debate in Soviet studies; foil to official claims that the USSR was a classless or worker-led society.

### In Mawdsley & White 2000 (positions recorded)

They list Djilas alongside Trotsky's bureaucracy and Voslensky's [[nomenklatura]] as competing labels; they do **not** adopt "new class" as their operational measure. Chapter 7 records a longer critical lineage (Sol'ts, Rakovskii, Ciliga, Trotsky, Djilas) and tests privilege/heritability empirically. Their attributed close: a secure elite could remain relatively ascetic; an endangered elite sought private property—bridging socialist privilege to post-communist ownership without settling the class ontology.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Class" vs "estate"/"elite"/"status group" disputes; property-control criterion hard to operationalise; does not by itself specify which organisational tier counts; Western "new class"/knowledge-class literature is a **different** debate sharing a phrase.
