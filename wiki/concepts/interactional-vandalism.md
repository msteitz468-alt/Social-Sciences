# Interactional Vandalism

## Definition as Coined

**Interactional vandalism** is a concept [[duneier-mitchell|Mitchell Duneier]] developed (with Harvey Molotch) in *[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]]* (1999) for the tactical exploitation of shared conversational norms — the ordinary "practices behind the social bond" — used *against* people of higher social status to seize control of an interaction. Duneier's definition (attributed): "a kind of interactional vandalism. But unlike assaults against property, interactional vandalism leaves victims unable to articulate easily what has happened."

The name deliberately migrates the term *vandalism* from property to talk: the "damage" is done to the conversational order itself, and — unlike a broken window — leaves no visible trace the victim can point to.

## Semantic History

The concept emerged from Duneier's chapter "Talking to Women," analyzing how a few men on Sixth Avenue drew passing (usually white, usually higher-status) women into unwanted street conversations. It draws on the tradition of **conversation analysis** (the Sacks–Schegloff study of talk's sequential organization) rather than on [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] by name, though it sits squarely in the interaction-order lineage Goffman founded. It has since been widely reprinted (notably as a Duneier–Molotch article) and taught as a case of micro-level power in public space.

## Operationalizations

Duneier operationalizes it through **conversation-analytic transcription** of tape-recorded encounters (attributed):

- A compliment or question normally forms an **adjacency pair** demanding an immediate response; the woman instead produces long silences (2–3 seconds) or no response — by CA convention, a "disaffiliative" signal.
- The man ignores the cue and presses on ("disaffiliative escalation"), forcing the woman into what Duneier calls **"technical rudeness"** (walking away mid-exchange) to end it.
- A control comparison: the same woman answers Duneier's own simple questions instantly, showing her discomfort is specific to the man, not to strangers generally.

## Applications

- Names a mechanism of **gendered power exercised from a position of class subordination**: men with little social status seize control of the interaction itself.
- Marks the **limit of [[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]]'s sidewalk model**: her "eyes upon the street" restraints assume shared interactional norms that cannot be taken for granted across sharp race-class-gender divides.
- Contributes to the sociology of **street harassment** and public space as gendered.

## Critiques and Limitations

The published analyses rest on a small number of transcribed encounters (Duneier studied 3 of 21 "regulars" who habitually entangled passersby), so the concept's evidentiary base is a few close cases rather than a sample. Duneier's further reading — that "white liberal guilt" compounds the women's difficulty in rebuffing poor Black men — he flags himself as speculative. The framing of both parties as "victims" of a structural situation has been read as either even-handed or as under-naming the harassment.
