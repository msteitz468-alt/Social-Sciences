# Infrastructural Power

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (1984 *AES* article "The Autonomous Power of the State"; developed in *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]*, 1986, esp. Chs. 1, 5, 8–9, 13–15) defines **infrastructural power** as the capacity of the state to *penetrate* civil society and logistically implement political decisions throughout its realm — as distinct from [[despotic-power|despotic power]] (the range of actions elites can attempt without routine institutionalized negotiation with civil-society groups). In Vol. I Ch. 5 he writes:

> "Infrastructural power refers to the capacity to actually penetrate society and to implement logistically political decisions. What should be immediately obvious about the despots of historic empires is the weakness of their infrastructural powers and their dependence upon the class of aristocrats for such infrastructure as they possessed."

Authoritative reach is also measured by military logistics; Mann holds that when routine control along a route march greater than about **ninety kilometers** is impossible, larger-area control cannot be tightly centralized or penetrate everyday lives intensively. Diffused power separately requires a *universal* infrastructure (markets, literacy, coinage, class/national culture).

## Semantic History

Becomes standard vocabulary in state theory and comparative historical sociology; often borrowed without the full [[iemp-model|IEMP]] apparatus. Contrast with Weberian [[bureaucracy]] (organizational form) and Marxian relative autonomy of the state (class conditioning): Mann's pair is logistical-organizational. Geopolitical diplomacy is a second form of political-power organization (hegemonic empire vs multistate civilization), not reducible to internal state configuration alone.

## Operationalizations

Penetration indicators in Mann's narrative: roads and staging posts, coinage, weights/measures, literacy/records, standing administration, census, law courts' reach, satrap/provincial systems, fiscal extraction. Preindustrial ceilings on intensive penetration and double-edged diffusion of infrastructure into civil society (coinage, literacy empowering decentralized elites) are recurring themes.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Ancient empires (Chs. 5, 8)** — Historic imperial despots high on formal despotism, low on infrastructure ("territorially federal"); Egypt: pharaoh's infrastructural control more limited than formal despotic powers; Persia as ideal-type patrimonial regime — center despotic with universal pretensions, infrastructural power "feeble" (Xenophon: locals only dimly aware of empire); satraps, royal roads, Aramaic correspondence as borrowed/partial infrastructures; Persian subject in some senses freer to hide from the state than Greek citizen despite constitutional despotism.
- **Rome (Ch. 9)** — Shift from [[empire-of-domination]] toward territorial empire with higher territorial control within agrarian logistics; legionary economy as developed [[compulsory-cooperation]]; still bounded by logistical constraints on all agrarian societies.
- **Medieval–early modern Europe (Chs. 12–15)** — Early feudal states: judicial bite but weak coercion of collectives; medieval kings lacked formal Roman-style despotism yet by 1450 were **territorially coordinating**, not unitary/organic — two levels (king and local magnate). Organic/constitutional states later achieve greater infrastructural reach over extensive territories than any extensive state hitherto (Mann); absolutist states are **not** infrastructurally stronger than constitutional ones — remaining difference mainly despotic power; colonial logistics leave colonials independent most of the year. States supplant Christendom as main instruments of [[normative-pacification]] after religious wars.

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement often qualitative; risk of teleology toward modern infrastructural states; gender and local intermediary power may be under-specified. Mann's ~90 km march rule and mid-1980s secondary logistics literature are dated hypotheses — attribute, do not promote to wiki-voice military science.
