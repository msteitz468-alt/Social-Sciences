# Depoliticization

**Depoliticization** names the process or effect of removing issues from the sphere of political contestation — treating them as technical, natural, or administrative instead. In this vault's primary base for the term's development-studies use, [[ferguson-james|Ferguson]] (1990) argues that the **"development" apparatus** systematically depoliticizes poverty and social structure in Lesotho while expanding state bureaucratic power ([[anti-politics-machine]]).

## Definition as Coined

Broader political theory uses "depoliticization" for many regimes (technocracy, market naturalization, etc.). Ferguson's specific claim (attributed): development discourse constructs Lesotho's problems as those of an isolated, traditional, agricultural LDC needing technical rural development — thereby denying the political-economic realities of labor migration, South African regional domination, and domestic power — and project practice extends government into rural areas under an anti-political cover.

## Semantic History

Long career in political theory and sociology; in anthropology of development, Ferguson's *Anti-Politics Machine* is a landmark operationalization. Adjacent vocabularies: anti-politics, technization, rendering technical (later Tania Li and others — not yet ingested).

## Operationalizations

- Content analysis of planning documents' problem-construction.
- Ethnography of project encounters where political conflict is renamed technical failure or "attitude" problems.
- Tracking state institutional expansion (district administration, police, party structures) concurrent with "failed" development goals.

## Applications

Central to [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]; linked to [[bureaucracy]] and [[instrument-effects]]; dialog with [[post-development]] without identity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of totalizing: not all technical policy is depoliticizing in the same way; actors sometimes re-politicize projects (Ferguson records resistance and party-political use of committees). Keep claims attributed to cases and authors.
