# Red Terror

## Definition as Coined

**Red Terror** names the Soviet revolutionary state's systematic repression (executions, hostages, Extraordinary Commissions, press suppression) during the civil war — and the **theoretical dispute** over whether that system is legitimate revolutionary self-defence or a fatal departure from Marxist and democratic socialism. Two primary poles now ingested:

| Pole | Source | Core move (attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| Defence | [[trotsky-leon]], *[[trotsky-terrorism-and-communism-1920]]* (1920) | Revolution breaks the foe's will; when resistance is ferocious, repression becomes a system of terror; class direction distinguishes Red from White |
| Critique | [[kautsky-karl]], *[[kautsky-terrorism-and-communism-1919]]* (1919) | Terrorism is not the essence of revolution; planned state terror fails historically; Soviet terror is the inevitable result of Communist methods that break with democracy |

## Semantic History

- **Pre-1917 socialist usage:** Jacobin "terror" already debated among Marxists; Engels (1870, as Kautsky cites) treated terror as "reign of the frightened"; Marx 1871 (as Kautsky cites) repudiated terror for the Commune.
- **Kautsky 1919:** Dual inquiry — natural history of revolutionary terror (French Revolution → failure) and 1871 Commune as **non-terrorist**, non-Soviet prototype wrongly claimed by Bolshevism; Ch.VII humanisation thesis; Ch.VIII application to Soviet Extraordinary Commissions, hostages (Mensheviks/Left SRs), shooting as "Alpha and Omega."
- **Soviet 1918–21 practice:** Bolshevik framing ties institutionalisation to White war, intervention, and attacks (Uritsky, etc.); Trotsky claims Nov 1917 transfer was relatively bloodless and terror not inevitable then.
- **Trotsky 1920:** Book-length reply consolidating civil-war ethics against Kautsky.
- **Later reception:** Service, liberal totalitarian narratives, Trotskyist isolation of civil-war necessity, Žižek's Stalin-path reading of Trotsky's book — all **positions**.

## Operationalizations

### Trotsky 1920 (defence)

| Measure | Function in his argument |
|---|---|
| Executions | Expediency when imprisonment fails |
| Hostages | "Immanent" in civil-war terrorism; Marx on Commune cited |
| Press suppression | Enemy press as military weapon |
| Party monopoly | "We have suppressed the Mensheviks and the SRs — and they have disappeared" |

White Terror **retards** a rising class; Red Terror **hastens** a doomed class's destruction (attributed).

### Kautsky 1919 (critique)

| Claim (attributed) | Thrust |
|---|---|
| Terrorism ≠ essence of revolution | Popular myth; Bolsheviks and then Noske-type socialists both resorted to terrorist regimes |
| Historical failure | Jacobin terror produced new capitalists, cadre depletion, factional slaughter, mass apathy, Thermidor — not socialism |
| 1871 Commune | Practised almost no terror; hostages framed as life-saving; factions did not terrorise each other; humanity constitutive of DoP's effect |
| Civilisation thesis | 19th-c. proletariat + Marxism + democracy drop terror from the programme; war reversed humanisation |
| Soviet terror | Inevitable result of methods (expropriation without organisation, break with democracy); first decree abolished death penalty — culpability at the method, not only motives |
| White does not justify Red | Ends do not sanctify contradictory means |
| Free press | Sole real check on corruption; withheld under dictatorship; secret commissions worse than public 1793 tribunal |
| Closing | World revolution only through "democracy and humanity," not cannons and destruction of adversaries |

## Applications

- **Kronstadt 1921 (Avrich):** Oranienbaum executions, hostages, ice-assault casualties — rebels framed as White agents; see [[kronstadt-debate]], [[avrich-kronstadt-1921]].

- **Civil-war Russia:** Trotsky's multi-front/blockade condition vs Kautsky's method-internal necessity.
- **Paris Commune as battleground:** Trotsky enlists Marx's *Civil War in France* for severity; Kautsky enlists Marx/Engels against terror and for a non-Soviet, non-terrorist Commune.
- **Labour compulsion:** Trotsky's [[militarization-of-labour]]; Kautsky's critique of compulsory labour on the bourgeoisie only and arbitrary "worker" demarcation as foundation of arbitrary rule.
- **Debate home:** [[smash-or-capture-the-state-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Both primary texts are **partisan** (war commissar vs Second International theorist). Casualty scales, White atrocities, Murzilovka-type documents, and industrial statistics are claims of the authors, not wiki-voice facts.
- Kautsky's French/Commune history is selective for argument; specialist historiography may diverge.
- Trotsky's class-direction criterion rejects Kautsky's absolute sacredness-of-life / Quaker framing as forbidding revolution itself.
- Internal Bolshevik later tensions (Trotsky 1904 substitutionism vs 1920 defence; 1930s anti-Stalinism) remain career tensions, not silent overwrite.

## Service 2000 on Cheka origins, Civil War force, and NEP-era terror

Robert Service, *[[service-lenin-2000|Lenin: A Biography]]* (2000), supplies a biographical path into Red Terror practice (attributed throughout):

- **Cheka origins (December 1917).** Service reports Lenin made the case at Sovnarkom for a secret political police — the Extraordinary Commission — headed by Felix Dzierżyński on Lenin’s recommendation; powers against “counter-revolution and sabotage” deliberately vague; free of Sovnarkom interference; “Extraordinary” because even Lenin thought temporary. At this stage Service says Lenin did **not** yet call for extensive mass terror, but treats the step as fateful: untrammelled by legality and expandable at will.
- **Force unbound by laws.** In the convalescence-period polemic *Proletarian Revolution and Kautsky the Renegade*, Service reports Lenin’s formulation (as Service quotes): “Dictatorship is the power relying directly upon force unbound by any laws.” Service links this Kautsky polemic to Lenin’s wartime insistence on class-specific “democracy” and iron authority.
- **Civil War spirit of overdoing blows.** Service argues Lenin preferred to overdo blows rather than risk opponents surviving. To Gorki’s concern about calibrating Cheka/Red Army force, Service quotes Lenin: “By what measure are you to gauge how many blows are necessary and how many are superfluous…?” Service reports telegrams and orders in a sadistic register (e.g. mass-impact shootings, holiday-skipping executions) while noting high command often prioritised victories over literal implementation; Cheka protected by Lenin, criticised but never seriously punished for “excesses.”
- **Post-August 1918 path.** Service’s narrative treats the August 1918 shooting of Lenin and the Civil War food/grain war as intensifying the terror path already opened by Cheka founding, Constituent Assembly dispersal, and class-war decrees (Penza-type exemplary terror among them).
- **NEP-era intensified political terror.** Service’s thesis for 1921+: economic retreat required **maximum political discipline** — shoot “bandits” on the spot; intensify repression; Civil Code should enshrine the “essence and justification of terror”; death-penalty aspects for Menshevik/SR activity; “not caught napping by a second Kronstadt”; SR show trial, clergy executions, intelligentsia deportation. Service’s closing NEP formula: bare-minimum economic reform paired with deep military/political repression (see [[new-economic-policy]], [[kronstadt-debate]]).

## Carr 1979 on Cheka and reciprocal terror

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 1–3, 12, 17) narrates (attributed): Cheka founded six weeks after October for combating counter-revolution and sabotage; first death sentence June 1918; April 1918 anarchist arrests; July SR coup attempt and German ambassador's assassination; summer 1918 assassinations and attempt on Lenin — **"Red terror" and "White terror" both entered the political vocabulary**. Labour/concentration camps under Cheka or ordinary courts. After opposition defeat, March 1928 instruction on penal policy extended OGPU concentration camps for political offenders; 1928 as year of "powerful and despotic authority" and harsh penalties. Carr's later chapters fold terror into patterns of dictatorship under Stalin without a separate theory of terror.
