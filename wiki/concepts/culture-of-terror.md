# Culture of Terror

## Definition as Coined

[[taussig-michael|Taussig]]’s **culture of terror** names the organized cultural production of fear under colonial and dictatorial violence — not only the physiological state of terror but a **social** order in which rumor, racial mythology, narration, and ritualized cruelty make arbitrary violence thinkable and workable. Most people, he noted, know torture and the culture of terror only through others’ words; his concern is “the mediation of terror through narration” and how to write effectively against it (attributed). Guillermo O’Donnell’s “culture of fear” after Argentine military rule (post-1976) focused Taussig’s attention on the phrase family (attributed acknowledgment).

Paired throughout *[[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-wild-man-1987|Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man]]* with [[space-of-death]] and [[epistemic-murk]].

## Semantic History

- **Putumayo rubber boom (~1900–1912).** Core case: company employees’ “diseased imagination” (Judge Rómulo Paredes, 1911, as Taussig cites) that saw Indian attack everywhere while organizing massacre; stories of savagery binding colonial society together.
- **Dictatorship analogies.** Timerman (Argentina), Dorfman (Chile/*Imbunche*), Asturias (Guatemala) used as comparative illuminations of modern death-spaces — not as identical cases.
- **Healing half of the book.** Culture of terror’s imagery (wild man, devil, jungle savagery) reappears as the raw material shamanic healing montages against itself (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Interpretive, not survey-based. Indicators in Taussig’s reconstruction include:

- Circulation of atrocity and savagery stories (Hardenburg, *La Sanción*/*La Felpa*, Casement blue book)
- Ritualized punishment (stocks, sport torture) as theater
- Debt-peonage “phraseology of conquest” (*conquistar*, selling of debts as persons)
- Colonial [[mimesis]]: savagery attributed to Indians mirroring savagery perpetrated by colonists (colonial mirror of production)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Complicity of critique.** Taussig argued that even critical representations (Casement’s FO realism, Hardenburg’s melodrama) could fortify the colonizing imagination they opposed — a reflexivity that complicates “speaking truth to terror” (attributed).
- **Agency of Indigenous actors.** Muchachos (Indian rubber-company police) and local media complicate simple victim/perpetrator binaries; record both without laundering company responsibility.
- **Not a general law of colonialism.** Case-bound to rubber boom and Taussig’s comparative dictatorial materials.
