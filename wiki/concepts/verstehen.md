## Definition as Coined

Weber grounds sociology in the "construing and understanding" of social action (Ch. 1 §1). Weber distinguishes two modes: *aktuell* (direct/immediate) understanding of an intended meaning — e.g., grasping that 2×2=4, or reading anger from a facial expression — versus explanatory understanding, which grasps the motivational context that makes an action meaningful, e.g., why a woodcutter chops wood (for wages, exercise, or anger) (Ch. 1 §5). Weber holds that all construal aspires to *Evidenz* (self-evident clarity), which may be rational (logical/mathematical) or grounded in empathetic reliving (Ch. 1 §5).

Weber argues that the capacity to replicate an action is not a condition for understanding it, illustrating with the maxim "one need not be Caesar to understand Caesar" — full inward reliving aids the clarity (*Evidenz*) of understanding but is not an absolute requirement (Ch. 1 §5). Weber insists that even the most self-evident construal "cannot on that basis alone claim to be causally valid" — it remains, in Weber's words, "an especially self-evident causal hypothesis" (Ch. 1 §6).

## Semantic History

Tribe's Translation Appendix renders *Verstehen* as "understanding"/"understandable," retaining the German term where necessary, and explains that Tribe deliberately avoids "interpretive sociology" for *verstehende Soziologie* because that phrase conflates *Verstehen* with *Deutung* (which Tribe renders "construal/construing," rejecting "interpretation" as implying a fixed target-language grid). Tribe cites Peter Ghosh's distinction that *Deutung* names the process while *Verstehen* names the outcome, and criticizes Parsons's inconsistent renderings ("subjectively understandable," "comprehension"). Tribe separately notes *Evidenz* is a false-friend term: unlike English "evidence" (material supporting a proposition), German *Evidenz* means "obviousness/self-evidence," and is glossed case-by-case rather than fixed-translated.

## Operationalizations

Weber links meaning-adequacy (whether a construal forms a typically "proper" meaningful context) to causal adequacy (the empirically known statistical chance that a sequence of events recurs) — Weber argues a true causal construal of typical action requires both together; meaning-adequacy alone yields only unintelligible statistical probability, while causal regularity without meaning-adequacy yields no sociological "rule" (Ch. 1 §7). Weber illustrates the limit case with Gresham's Law (rational construal with strong statistical confirmation) against Eduard Meyer's hypothesis on the causal significance of the Persian Wars (resting on rational *Evidenz* alone, without comparable statistical test) (Ch. 1 §6).

## Applications

Weber treats the "rationalistic" method of *Verstehen* sociology as a methodic convenience, not a claim about the real predominance of rational action — Weber warns of a "constant danger of misplaced rationalistic construction" and uses the [[ideal-type]] of purposively-rational action as a baseline against which irrational "disturbances" (e.g. a stock-exchange panic) are explained as deviations (Ch. 1 §1).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber notes several limits on the reliability of construed meaning: unconscious or repressed motives can conceal the real context of action from the actor; externally similar actions can rest on very different meanings; and actors are often driven by contradictory imperatives whose relative strength can only be determined by the actual outcome (Ch. 1 §6). Weber also argues it is a mistake to treat "psychology" as the ultimate foundation of a *Verstehen* sociology, holding sociology's relation to psychology to be no closer than to any other discipline (Ch. 1 §1).
