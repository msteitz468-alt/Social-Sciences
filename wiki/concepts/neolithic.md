# Neolithic

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872|Pre-Historic Times]]* (1865; 1872 ed.), [[lubbock-john|John Lubbock]] names the later stone epoch the **"Neolithic" period**: "The later or polished Stone Age; a period characterized by beautiful weapons and instruments made of flint and other kinds of stone; in which, however, we find no trace of the knowledge of any metal, excepting gold, which seems to have been sometimes used for ornaments" (attributed, Ch. I). Contrasted with the [[palaeolithic|Paleolithic]] Drift period of extinct fauna and unground implements.

## Semantic History

- **Lubbock 1865:** polish + absence of utilitarian metal as the diagnostic cut within the Stone Age.
- **Childe and after:** "Neolithic Revolution" re-centers farming, pottery, villages — a package Lubbock did not fully articulate as such (his Swiss lake chapters discuss cultivated plants among pile-dwellers). *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]* (1929) operationalizes the early Neolithic as SE-derived farming cultures ([[danubian-i-culture]], [[vinca-culture]]) expanding on loess corridors — still periodization-by-culture rather than the later Revolution label.
- **Contemporary archaeology:** multi-regional, multi-pathway domestication; absolute dates; rejection of single-package revolution ([[play-farming]], multi-centric origins). The **word** Neolithic remains standard.

## Operationalizations

Lubbock: ground/polished lithics + no bronze/iron tools. Modern: subsistence and settlement criteria dominate; polish alone is insufficient.

## Applications

Deep-time tag (`neolithic` period) across this wiki; paired with Bronze/Iron Ages in [[three-age-system]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Polish criterion under-predicts food-producing societies without polished axes and over-predicts foragers with ground tools; Europe-centered origin of the term.

### Childe — Neolithic Revolution (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

In *[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|What Happened in History]]*, [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] re-centers the Neolithic as the **[[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]]**: food production (cultivation ± stock-breeding) distinguishes [[savagery-barbarism-civilization|barbarism]] from savagery; the polished axe is an archaeological hall-mark but not a universal economic criterion; "neolithic" in the economic sense is not a single world-time (Maoris still neolithic in A.D. 1800 — attributed). Package traits and Near Eastern theatre densify the semantic history above; absolute dates remain (as of Childe 1954). See also *[[childe-man-makes-himself-1936|Man Makes Himself]]*.

### Clark 1961 — European “Neolithic” status and secondary groups

*[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* ch. 6 questions the status of European “Neolithic” cultures relative to Near Eastern food producers: European peasantries receive farming from the SE but develop regional sequences (Danubian, Impressed ware, Funnel-neck Beaker, etc.); **secondary** and **tertiary** Neolithic groups form by Mesolithic acculturation on poorer soils; Battle-axe and Beaker peoples mark the late Neolithic/Copper Age transition (attributed). See [[danubian-i-culture]], [[bell-beaker-culture]], [[mesolithic]].

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]] treat the **Neolithic** not as Lubbock's polish criterion but as the multi-regional onset of food production and related settlement complexity after the Pleistocene (as of 2007). Systematic plant cultivation is recent (<1% of genus *Homo* existence); independent agricultural economies appeared in widely separated regions roughly between ~10,000 and 3,500 years ago (SW Asia wheat/barley; China rice/millet; Mexico maize; Andes potatoes) after millions of years of exclusive foraging (attributed, ch. 6). They distinguish **cultivation** (growing plants; need not be domesticated) from **domestication** (Rindos coevolutionary mutualism) and note sedentism may precede domesticated cereals in SW Asia by ~1,000 years, while Mesoamerican plants domesticated millennia before sedentary villages (attributed).

SW Asian Neolithic complexity signals they survey include Jericho walls; Levantine ancestor cults; Ain Ghazal figures; Urfa/Göbekli Tepe and Nevali Çori cult buildings ~8400 BCE; [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] (size, rooftop access, shrines, obsidian); contrast with small, uniform Jarmo lacking controlled resources; pottery after ~6500 BCE and Halaf/Samarra styles by ~5500 BCE (attributed, ch. 8). Wenke's Fayyum project is cited as studying why Egyptian agriculture lagged SW Asia by ~2,000 years (Epipaleolithic ~7000 B.C.; earliest known farmers ~5500 B.C. — as of W&O 2007). Regional packages elsewhere (Egypt SW Asian cereals + Nile resources; Indus wheat/barley/zebu/cotton; North China millet/pigs; Andean potatoes/maize/quinoa after ~4000 B.C.) illustrate convergent solutions without inevitability (attributed, later chapters). **Horizon:** absolute dates and genetics are textbook 2007 — attribute and date-stamp.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)*

- **Lubbock coinage restated:** Victorian John Lubbock’s 1865 *Prehistoric Times* introduced Palaeolithic and Neolithic as key prehistoric periods (attributed, ch. 1).
- **Kenyon PPNA:** houses/burials “Neolithic” without pottery → Pre-Pottery Neolithic A at [[jericho|Jericho]]; challenges Childe’s indivisible “Neolithic package” (attributed, ch. 7).
- **Holocene start sequence:** YD ends ~9600 BC; sedentary farming villages (Jericho) then West Bank PPNA densification (Netiv Hagdud, Gilgal); PPNB rectangular plastered houses after ~9000 BC (attributed).
- **Not Jericho alone:** West Bank then northern Fertile Crescent reframed as centres; Jordan valley may be peripheral to key social/economic/ideological developments; Göbekli / Jerf el Ahmar ideology-before-farming positions (attributed).
- **By 5000 BC Rubicon:** farming + specialists as foundational historical transition (attributed, ch. 1); narrative remit of the book is 20,000–5000 BC as history’s “coming of age.”
- **European Neolithic entry:** leap-frog farming through Balkans; Mesolithic absorption/collapse at Iron Gates; hard labour of early farmers vs Mesolithic foragers (attributed).

Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## Bellwood densification — *First Farmers* (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; regional packages as of that synthesis.)*

- **Europe:** European Neolithic mainly ceramic package with full SW Asian cereals, legumes, domestic animals (exceptions: Cyprus Preceramic; faint aceramic echo Crete/mainland Greece). Early Neolithic cultural diversity **lower** than late Mesolithic or Late Neolithic — homogeneity → diversity pattern Bellwood reads as colonization signature (cites Childe 1956) (attributed). Agriculture Mediterranean + temperate Europe S of Baltic, Aegean→British Isles ~2500 years (~6500–4000 BC as of Bellwood 2005); ~millennium standstill near W edge Great Hungarian Plain; Baltic/Scandinavia after ~3500 BC (attributed).
- **China:** Early pottery can **predate** confirmed agriculture (parallel Sahara/Sudan Nile); full multi-hectare agricultural villages ~7000 BC; Peiligang–Cishan continuum Yellow River ~6500–5000 BC; Pengtoushan culture Yangzi ~7000–5500 BC (attributed). See [[yangshao-culture]], [[rice-domestication]], [[jomon-culture]] contrast.
- **SE Asia:** Strong clear-cut Neolithic spread S China→Mainland and Island SE Asia; Hoabinhian not convincingly transformed *in situ* to agriculture (Bellwood 1997b; Higham via Bellwood). Incised zone-impressed pottery + rice ~2500–1500 BC with early homogeneity then regionalization (attributed).
- **Formative / Neolithic languages:** Neolithic/Formative languages likely natural languages with genetic relatives, not colonial-type pidgins — whether such languages rooted subsequent families is a core [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] question (attributed).

Do not equate ceramic “Neolithic” with food production worldwide (Jōmon pottery without grain agriculture). Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## British Isles densification — Cunliffe *Britain Begins* (2013)

*(Attributed throughout — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* 2013; radiocarbon and modern-DNA claims date-stamped as of that synthesis. The Neolithic is treated as a **package of practices and behaviours** — food production, pottery, polished stone, monuments, reduced mobility — **not** as a single ethnic or linguistic identity; enforce four-way non-identity against pots=people equations.)*

### Package and tempo

Cunliffe frames a four-century transformation of Britain and Ireland, **c.4200–3800 BC**, by practices constituting the Neolithic package — changes he calls fundamental and irreversible (attributed). Core elements he lists:

- **Food production:** barley and emmer wheat plus domestic cattle, pigs, and sheep, all first carried by boat from the adjacent continent (attributed).
- **Sedentism and houses:** crop-tending and field protection make home bases more important; large timber-built houses appear for the first time (attributed).
- **Material culture:** well-made **carinated bowls** in the first two centuries of the fourth millennium (first artificial material via controlled pyrotechnics in the isles); ground/polished stone axes; leaf-shaped arrow-heads and plano-convex knives (attributed).
- **Diet shift:** dependence on hunting/gathering falls rapidly; even coastal populations abandon maritime protein for land animals — Cunliffe reads a major behavioural/value readjustment, possibly sea-food taboos; isotope analyses of human bone support a stark terrestrial-protein turn at Neolithic start (attributed findings + interpretation).
- **Early communal works:** surplus energy under coercive social powers (his mechanism, not a demonstrated polity) first yields Sussex Downs flint mines (forty-first/fortieth century BC: Harrow Hill, Blackpatch, Cissbury), then long barrows, long mortuary enclosures, cursus monuments, and causewayed camps from the end of the thirty-eighth century BC (attributed).

Spread across varied landscapes is rapid in **4100–3800 BC**, with equally rapid demise of indigenous hunter-gatherer lifestyles via myriad local decisions (attributed). Until recently radiocarbon allowed only “fuzzy prehistory”; short-life samples, refined calibration, and Bayesian sequence analysis now date monument events within a generation or two (attributed method claim, as of Cunliffe 2013).

### How the package arrived — historiography and Cunliffe’s model

Cunliffe narrates the Mesolithic–Neolithic debate as swinging **invasion → indigenous acculturation → renewed immigration** (with DNA) (attributed historiography):

| Phase of debate | Position (as reported) |
|---|---|
| Until 1960s | Continental influx brings fully fledged “primary” Neolithic; remaining indigenes later acculturate into “secondary” Neolithic |
| 1970s | Invasion model rejected as simplistic and colonialist-anachronistic; homeland of immigrants hard to identify |
| Acculturation alternative | Late Mesolithic communities in sea contact adopt and locally adapt lifeways, technologies, beliefs |
| Recent (as of 2013) | Shift back toward significant immigration, partly from precise radiocarbon: remarkably uniform new culture over much of Britain/Ireland in as little as ~300 years |

Continental setup (attributed): after sixth-millennium Neolithic “colonization,” NW Europe is relatively stable until ~4000 BC, when social tensions (possibly climate-exacerbated) renew mobility. Two European axes had already brought farming to NW France by the mid-fifth millennium — **Linearbandkeramik** from eastern Europe to the Seine by 5500 BC; **Impressed Ware** from the west Mediterranean to Atlantic France. Maritime interface ~650 km; favoured landfalls include Pas de Calais/Picardy → greater Thames or Sussex plain, and western Channel Armorica → SW Britain/Ireland. Breeding stocks imply sturdy vessels and repeated journeys (skin boats on light frames, analogy to Irish currachs — ethnographic analogy flagged).

**Cunliffe’s archaeological model** (attributed, 2013): significant population influx around 4000 BC along **two streams**:

1. **East-side (probably largest):** settlers from NE France, Belgium, and southern Netherlands (middle Neolithic Northern Chassey / Michelsberg worlds) across the eastern Channel–south North Sea — greatest impact on British Neolithic development.
2. **West-side (dispersed Atlantic):** Armorican/western French movements along existing Atlantic networks into Ireland, Welsh coast, western Scotland, and perhaps SW Britain — closed polygonal chambers and simple Morbihan-type passage tombs; Achnacreebeag (west Scotland) pot with late Castellic arc motifs (c.4300–c.3900 BC Morbihan parallels). Evidence he calls **tenuous**; simplest explanation is Armorican settlers on Irish Sea networks.

Earliest settlements (41st century BC) concentrate in SE Britain especially the greater Thames; spread to central southern Britain by the 40th century; 39th–early 38th century rapid expansion west into Ireland (early package including carinated bowls by the 38th century BC) (attributed). Early **rectangular timber halls** from White Horse Stone (Kent) and Yarnton to eastern Scotland, Llandegai (N Wales), and ≥46 rectangular houses in Ireland mark place-making and group cohesion; burning of three large Scottish halls (Claish, Crathes/Warren Field, Balbridie) may signal ritual closure (attributed speculation).

**Two-phase periodization** (attributed): **initial Neolithic c.4200–3700 BC** (inflow of people, technologies, belief systems by multiple continental routes; pan-insular similarity of polished axes, leaf arrow-heads, carinated bowls) and **developed Neolithic c.3700–2500 BC** (local inventiveness, regional cultures; after ~3700 continental contact harder to see in the east, while western passage graves keep Atlantic idea-exchange alive). After pioneer mobility, Cunliffe argues decreased continental contact and a distinct British/Irish regional Neolithic, with the Atlantic seaways ~3000 BC as the main exception ([[newgrange]], Anglesey, Orkney sharing knowledge with Armorica and Atlantic Iberia).

By the thirty-eighth/thirty-seventh centuries, successful farming communities are **hybrids** inheriting most genes from indigenous islanders after ~ten generations of intermarriage and emulation; non-adopters “die out”; then ~forty generations (twelve centuries) of regional adaptation under endemic inter-group hostility (attributed population thesis — culture ≠ genes ≠ language). Modern-DNA estimates he cites (as of 2013) suggest **10–30%** of present British/Irish genes of ultimate Near Eastern origin arriving with Neolithic pioneers — dating of genetic inflows **highly uncertain**; maternal groups J, T1, U3 among candidates; do not equate haplotypes with ethnic identity.

### Monumental sequence and regional character (British/Irish)

First monumentalization is the large timber hall; over the next ~1,500 years communities build timber funerary houses, long barrows/cairns, megalithic chambers, causewayed camps, cursus, and henges (attributed). Eastern Britain: mortuary enclosures and long barrows/cairns from mid-thirty-eighth to thirty-fourth centuries (Fussell’s Lodge, Nutbane; Wayland’s Smithy multi-phase; South Street ard marks under long barrow). Western/Atlantic: megalithic tomb building introduced around the Irish Sea probably from Armorica end fifth / start fourth millennium — portal tombs (~400), court tombs (~400, N Ireland; Clyde cairn parallels in west Scotland), passage graves (~230 Ireland, Atlantic seaways to Orkney/Iberia) culminating in Boyne giants ~3000 BC (see [[megalith]], [[newgrange]]). Severn–Cotswold group (~175) as British hybrid of Atlantic megalithic and eastern long-barrow traditions (attributed).

**Causewayed enclosures** (radiocarbon: south/east Britain from mid-thirty-eighth century, ~four centuries’ maintenance): concentrate SE of the Wash–Severn axis; Magheraboy (west Ireland) ~4000 BC anomalously early if dates hold. Broad consensus (as reported): assembly places for social cohesion — worship, feasting, competitions, boundary agreements, gift/wife exchange, ancestral homage — liminal sanctuaries rather than forts; some southern examples show attack and burning (Carn Brae, Hembury, Crickley Hill, Hambledon Hill) (attributed). Cursus monuments spread as causewayed floruit ends (thirty-sixth century); Dorset Cursus, Stonehenge Cursus 2.7 km (attributed).

**Third millennium:** henges and [[stonehenge]]/[[avebury]]-class ritual landscapes; **Grooved Ware** (flat-bottomed, line/stab decoration; ~1000 years from thirty-second/thirty-first century; pan-British/Irish; invariably associated with henges — elite-ceramic hypothesis cautioned) (attributed). Some writers propose Orkney-first origins for henges and Grooved Ware; Cunliffe allows the hypothesis but requires more precise chronology (attributed position). ~2500 BC Beaker-related mobility opens the Chalcolithic; full Bronze Age ~2100 BC (his shorthand).

### Identity cautions (British Neolithic)

- Package = practices, not peoplehood or language.
- East- and west-side streams are archaeological connectivity narratives, not named ethnic groups.
- Genetic percentages are modern-population models (as of Cunliffe 2013), not direct aDNA of early farmers, and carry dating uncertainty.
- Megalithic tomb types mark regional preferences and networks, not one “megalithic race.”

## Matthews densification — push/pull theories of Neolithic origins (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]]; term "Neolithic
revolution" attributed to Childe 1936, consonant with the coinage record above.)* Matthews
frames Southwest Asian and Mesopotamian Neolithic-origins theory as a live dispute between
**push** and **pull** explanations, not yet resolved. Push (environmental/demographic)
positions include: Childe's oasis/desiccation theory (contradicted by an independent
palynological record — pollen from Lake Zeribar, west Iran, shows rainfall *increasing*
rather than decreasing after the start of the Holocene, van Zeist and Bottema 1977 — a
[CONTRADICTION] flag against Childe by an independent evidence class); Younger Dryas theories
(Moore and Hillman 1992; Bar-Yosef and Meadow 1995) and seasonality theories (McCorriston
and Hole 1991; H. E. Wright 1993), which read the terminal-Pleistocene cold/dry episode as
forcing intensified resource exploitation; and population-pressure "prime mover" theories
(Binford 1968; Flannery 1969; Mark Cohen's *The Food Crisis in Prehistory*, 1977), critiqued
by Henry 1989, Price and Gebauer 1995, and Brian Hayden (1992) for lacking archaeological
evidence of pre-domestication economic stress. Pull (social/symbolic) positions include
Barbara Bender's (1978) feasting/social-integration model, Brian Hayden's (1992; 1995)
competitive-feasting model — which reverses the usual causal order by treating social
hierarchy as preceding and stimulating agriculture rather than the reverse — and Jacques
Cauvin's (2000) cognitive/symbolic argument ("the birth of the gods") that a transformation
in human thought preceded and drove economic change, itself criticized by Ian Hodder (2001)
for resting on "some inexorable internal logic"; Hodder's own model locates the change in a
shift of "symbolic structure" centered on the house (the *domus*). Matthews reports the
current consensus favors **multivariate, locale-specific** interpretations rather than any
single push or pull mechanism (Watson 1991). Dates in this material are given as BC without
an explicit calibration statement in the source. See also the Hallan Çemi pig-domestication
dispute (Rosenberg 1999 vs. Peters et al. 1999) as a related live empirical dispute over
incipient animal husbandry in the same period, recorded on [[domestication]].

## Bradley 1998 — dual Neolithic of north-west Europe (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]*: Conventional Neolithic “package” (monuments + farming + new material culture) often **falls apart** beyond the loess longhouse zone (Thomas 1993 via Bradley). Dual pattern:

- **Loess settlement Neolithic** (LBK and successors): massive houses, domestic residues, flat cemeteries, monuments virtually absent until late enclosures.
- **Monument-dominated Neolithic** (TRB/Michelsberg and Atlantic margins): mounds and specialised enclosures eclipse scarce houses; intensive agriculture often late relative to first monuments.

Agricultural frontier ~5000–4000 BC better explained by social negotiation with stable hunter-gatherer populations than by soil limits alone; assimilation more likely than native disappearance. Field divided between economic crisis models (Rowley-Conwy etc.) and ideological conversion models (Jennbert, Thomas, Hodder, Whittle, Tilley); Bradley studies **monuments as the unambiguous new feature** in their own right. See [[monumentality]], [[death-of-the-house]], [[agricultural-revolution]].

## Facing the Ocean densification (Cunliffe 2001) — spread routes and the maritime-transmission thesis

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]]* 2001, Chs. 4–13; an earlier, independent Cunliffe synthesis with a distinct emphasis from *Britain Begins* 2013.)*

**Cardial and Bandkeramik corridors.** Cunliffe (2001) traces two Near-Eastern-derived spread routes into Atlantic Europe: a southern Mediterranean route via Cardial Ware (cardium-shell-impressed pottery), reaching Italy, Mediterranean France, and southern Spain from the early 7th millennium BCE, with cereals attested in eastern Iberia by 5300 BCE and pottery reaching the Atlantic coasts of Iberia and France 5500–4500 BCE; and a northern route via the Bandkeramik (Linear Pottery) culture, established in the middle Danube/Hungarian Plain by the mid-6th millennium BCE and reaching northern France, southern Belgium, the Netherlands, and the North European Plain by 5000 BCE. He treats the mechanism of spread (migration vs. indigenous adoption) as debated and regionally variable, not resolved by a single model.

**Maritime-transmission thesis (Cunliffe's own interpretive position).** In the book's concluding "Longue Durée" chapter, Cunliffe (2001) argues that the rapid spread of the Neolithic package (polished stone axes, pottery, seed grain, domesticated animals) through Armorica, Britain, and Ireland is better explained by transmission via sea along long-established gift-exchange networks than by step-by-step overland colonization. This is his own synthetic argument, presented as an interpretation of the evidence, not a wiki-voice fact; it should be read alongside, and does not simply confirm, the two-stream (east-side/west-side) model he later develops in *Britain Begins* (2013), summarized above.

**Portuguese megalithic sequence and radiocarbon overturning diffusionism.** Cunliffe (2001) periodizes coastal Portugal as Neolithic I (Cardial-ware farming colonists, 6000–5500 BCE in northern Extremadura, adopted by local foragers to the south by ~5000 BCE) followed by Neolithic II (c. 4800–3400 BCE), when megalithic tombs appear in the Alentejo/Tagus/Algarve/Extremadura. He states plainly that it was formerly believed the Portuguese megaliths were introduced from the Mediterranean, but that radiocarbon chronology "has shown that this is most unlikely to be the case" — see [[megalith]] for the fuller indigenous-origin argument.

Source: [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001]].

## Whittle densification — *Europe in the Neolithic* (1996)

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic: The Creation of New Worlds]]* 1996. Continental survey c. 7000–2500 BC / ~3000 BC SE; mid-1990s cal BC; **pre-aDNA**.)*

Whittle redefines the European **Neolithic** less as a technological package or demic event and more as a **set of beliefs, values, and ideals** about people’s place in the scheme of things — descent, origins, time, relations among people, and a universe peopled by spirits and ancestors. A sense of the sacred, not secular surplus logic, guides values: cooperation, sharing, solidarity, mutuality, honour, esteem. Community must be **continually re-created** by gatherings, gift exchange, sharings of food and drink, joint ritual, and materiality. Little sign of pronounced established difference within groups (age/gender differences possible; true egalitarianism rare in ethnography — his caution).

**Process claims (attributed):**

- People did not immediately settle down; attachment to place grew through tradition, memory, ritual, and the dead.
- Herding and cereal/legume cultivation combine with continued hunting, fishing, and collecting — not instant “mixed farmers.” Broad-spectrum orientation maintained while staples shift; cereals “as much for drink as for food”; cattle valued as subjects/partners as well as meat.
- Way of life largely creation of **indigenous people**; forager social ethic prolonged; yet new conceptual order — time, beginnings, categorisation, separation from an undivided cosmic economy of sharing — so “creation of new worlds” and “prolongation of old ones” together (Ch. 10).
- **Many Neolithics:** SE tells; central European [[longhouse]] lives ([[linear-pottery-culture]]); Atlantic megalithic sequences; Mediterranean [[impresso-cardial-culture]] — regionally individual histories (Ch. 1–9).

Chronological envelope (as of Whittle 1996): Neolithic societies after ~7000 BC; SE ~7000/6500–5500 first generations, to ~3000 BC; central/west Med from before ~6000 BC; central Europe from ~5500 BC; NW Europe from before ~4000 BC; elsewhere to ~2500 BC. See [[farming-spread-europe-debate]] for his indigenous Position B vs demic orthodoxy. Source: [[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]].
