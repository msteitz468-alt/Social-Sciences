# Mother-Right

## Definition as Coined

**Mother-right** (*Mutterrecht*) in [[bachofen-johann-jakob|Bachofen]] (1861), as filtered through [[engels-friedrich|Engels]], names an alleged early condition in which descent and inheritance ran through the female line and mothers (as the only certain parents) held a higher social position than under later father-right and [[monogamy|monogamy]]. Engels accepts mother-right as historically prior to father-right among gentile peoples while rejecting Bachofen’s religious explanation of its overthrow.

## Semantic History

Victorian writers often fused (a) **matrilineal descent**, (b) **matrilocal residence**, and (c) **female political authority** under one label. Twentieth-century kinship theory split these; ethnographic matriliny does not entail matriarchy. Engels’s “mother-right” is closer to matrilineal gens plus claimed higher female status than to modern “matriarchy” slogans — but still a unilinear stage concept. See [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]].

## Operationalizations

- Bachofen: classical literary traces of female-line kinship and pre-monogamous orders.
- McLennan: “kinship through females only” (Engels: applied too rigidly to later stages).
- Morgan/Engels: original gens reckoned in female line; shift to father-right tied to [[private-property|property]] in herds and the need for agnatic heirs under emerging monogamy.

## Applications

Central to *Origin* ch. II (family sequence) and to Greek material (ch. IV; *Oresteia* as father-right’s victory). Feminist and Marxist receptions sometimes reclaimed mother-right as deep history of women’s status — still attributed programs, not findings.

## Critiques and Limitations

No robust evidence for universal primitive matriarchy; matriliny is one kinship option among many; dating and mechanism of any “overthrow” are speculative. Engels’s property-mechanism is a theoretical model, not a demonstrated world history.
