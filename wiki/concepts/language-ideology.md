# Language Ideology

**Language ideology** (also *linguistic ideology*) designates culturally shaped ideas about language — its nature, structure, use, and social value — and the ways those ideas mediate between linguistic form and social structure. The concept organizes a major research program in [[linguistic-anthropology]] from the late 1970s through the 1990s–2000s, consolidated in works such as [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|Kroskrity (ed.), *Regimes of Language* (2000)]].

## Definition as Coined

Two charter definitions structure the program (as cited by [[kroskrity-paul-v|Kroskrity]] in *[[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|Regimes of Language]]* intro):

1. [[silverstein-michael|Michael Silverstein]] (1979:193) — **linguistic ideologies** as “sets of beliefs about language articulated by users as a rationalization or justification of perceived language structure and use.”
2. [[irvine-judith-t|Judith T. Irvine]] (1989:255) — **language ideology** as “the cultural system of ideas about social and linguistic relationships, together with their loading of moral and political interests.”

[[irvine-judith-t|Irvine]] and [[gal-susan|Gal]] ([[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000|2000]]) further define linguistic ideologies as conceptual schemes with which participants *and* observers frame linguistic varieties and map those understandings onto people, events, and activities — “suffused with the political and moral issues pervading the particular sociolinguistic field” and subject to the interests of their bearers’ social position. Ideologies are held not only by local participants but by linguists and ethnographers who map language and people; “there is no ‘view from nowhere.’” Subsequent program work broadens beyond explicit rationalizations to include implicit, partial, and contradictory representations that **[[language-regimentation|regiment]]** practice and link linguistic difference to political and identity projects.

### Irvine & Gal: three consequences and three processes

Irvine and Gal organize their 2000 essay around **consequences** of ideologies of linguistic differentiation:

1. How participants’ boundary ideologies may contribute to **language change**.
2. How the **describer’s** ideology shapes linguistic description (grammars, maps, dictionaries).
3. How ideologies **authorize political action** on the basis of linguistic relationship or difference.

They identify three semiotic processes by which ideologies “recognize” (or misrecognize) linguistic differences — locating, interpreting, and rationalizing sociolinguistic complexity by identifying varieties with “typical” persons and activities:

| Process | One-line claim (attributed) | Page |
|---|---|---|
| [[iconization]] | Indexical links recast as icons of group essence; contingent connections treated as necessary | [[iconization]] |
| [[fractal-recursivity]] | Opposition at one scale projected onto other scales (intra/inter, person/role, region) | [[fractal-recursivity]] |
| Erasure (semiotic) | Scheme renders non-fitting persons, activities, or facts invisible or residual | [[erasure-semiotic]] |

Cases: Nguni *hlonipha* and click acquisition (language change); 19th-c. Senegal linguistic description (scholarship); Macedonian nationalist contestation (politics). Full densification on the process and source pages.

## Four dimensions (Kroskrity 2000 — cluster concept)

Kroskrity (intro) treats language ideologies as a **cluster concept** with four interconnected features structuring the SAR volume:

| # | Dimension | Claim (attributed to Kroskrity) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Interest** | Perceptions of language/discourse are constructed in the interest of specific social or cultural groups; “true/good/beautiful” language is interest-laden, not disinterested. |
| 2 | **Multiplicity** | Ideologies are multiple because meaningful social divisions (class, gender, clan, elites, generations) produce divergent perspectives as indices of membership. |
| 3 | **Awareness** | Members display varying degrees of awareness — from explicit articulation to ideologies of practice read from usage (discursive vs practical consciousness; Giddens cited). |
| 4 | **Mediation** | Ideologies mediate between social structures and forms of talk. Silverstein’s (1985:220) **total linguistic fact**: “irreducibly dialectic… meaningful sign forms contextualized to situations of interested human use and mediated by the fact of cultural ideology.” |

## Semantic History

- **Boas/Bloomfield suppression.** Kroskrity argues Boas (1911) treated native “secondary explanations” as misleading; Bloomfield similarly sidelined speakers’ models — suppressing research on cultures of language until later recovery.
- **1979–1980s.** Silverstein’s Peircean formulation; speakers’ awareness of [[indexicality|indexical]] (nonreferential) structure; recovery of members’ models uneven in [[ethnography-of-speaking]] and [[variationist-sociolinguistics]] (Labov 1979 explicitly diminished ideology after the Steve K. study, as Kroskrity reads him).
- **1992–1998 institutionalization.** AAA sessions; *Pragmatics* special issues; *Language Ideologies: Practice and Theory* (Schieffelin, Woolard & Kroskrity 1998).
- **1994 SAR seminar → *Regimes of Language* (2000).** Political-economic emphasis; Western language philosophies, nationalist discourses, U.S. political discourse, and academic nationalism models treated as language-ideological acts. Title joins “politics (without language)” and “language (without politics).”
- **Irvine & Gal (2000).** Three semiotic processes of differentiation: [[iconization]], [[fractal-recursivity]], [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]]. Essay also redirects the field from [[speech-community]] as primary unit toward **linguistic differentiation** as ideologically mediated boundary-work (Barth ethnicity + ethnography of speaking + critique of Anderson’s monoglot naturalization). See [[irvine-gal-language-ideology-linguistic-differentiation-2000]].
- **Relation to general [[ideology]].** Not a simple application of Marx/Mannheim to language; semiotic mediation, partial awareness, and multiplicity within single polities. Neutral (shared commonsense) vs critical (mystification) readings treated as gradient (Woolard & Schieffelin 1994; Bourdieu).

## Operationalizations

- Ethnography of explicit metalinguistic talk and implicit practice; **ideological sites** (Silverstein 1998) and **multisited** analysis (Philips on Tonga).
- Historical-semiotic reconstruction of language philosophies (Locke, Herder — Bauman & Briggs).
- Irvine & Gal process analysis of boundary-making and linguistic differentiation (maps, grammars, respect registers, nationalist philology).
- Analysis of **[[language-regimentation|regimentation]]** by schools, courts, missions, media, nation-states; [[monoglot-standard]] regimes.
- Tracking **register stripping** and **register stretching** under monoglot purity ideologies (Irvine & Gal on Balkan and Senegal cases).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of treating “ideology” as residual for any metalinguistic belief.
- Case studies often elite/institutional; vernacular multiplicity undersampled in some chapters.
- Relation to [[variationist-sociolinguistics]] and [[linguistic-relativity]] uneven (Silverstein’s Whorf chapter addresses the latter).
- Program is heavily North American linguistic anthropology; European CDA and sociology-of-language traditions are parallel rather than fully integrated.
- Kroskrity notes the 1994 seminar did **not** impose synthetic orthodoxy — multiplicity of approaches is built into the program.
- Irvine & Gal themselves note that it is easier to detect positioning in earlier scholars than in one’s own; their toolkit is not a claim to view-from-nowhere.
