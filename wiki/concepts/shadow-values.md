# Shadow Values

## Definition as Coined

In the Conclusion of *[[liebow-tallys-corner-1967|Tally's Corner]]* (1967), [[liebow-elliot|Elliot Liebow]] wrote that the man who fails as breadwinner, husband, and father "increasingly... turns to the streetcorner where a shadow system of values constructed out of public fictions serves to accommodate just such men as he, permitting them to be men once again provided they do not look too closely at one another's credentials."

Liebow's two explicit qualifications (footnoted against Hyman Rodman's "lower-class value stretch," *Social Forces* 1963): (1) shadow values are **not of the same order** as mainstream values — "derivative, subsidiary in nature, thinner and less weighty, less completely internalized... value images reflected by forced or adaptive behavior rather than real values"; (2) they are **not separately invocable** — "a shadow cast by the common value system in the distorting lower-class setting," separable from the parent system only analytically.

## Semantic History

Developed from Rodman's value stretch but deliberately weaker: where a stretch adds genuinely held alternative values, Liebow's shadow denies the streetcorner an autonomous value system at all — the men share mainstream standards and fail by them. This is the concept's argumentative point against [[culture-of-poverty]] accounts. Charles Lemert (2003 foreword) reads it against Merton's "Social Structure and Anomie" and as a resonance (unacknowledged by Liebow) with Du Bois's double consciousness. Later cognates that must not be collapsed into it: Anderson's [[code-of-the-street]] (a public order of interaction, with [[decent-and-street]] plurality), and Wilson's culture-as-response-to-structure formulations.

## Operationalizations

Never formally operationalized. Liebow identifies shadow values ethnographically through the gap between public accounts and observed behavior (manly-flaws talk vs who actually leaves marriages; exploiter talk vs actual treatment of women). Measurement of "thin" vs "internalized" values remains the concept's open empirical problem — survey value-scales cannot distinguish them, which is part of why the culture-of-poverty dispute stays unresolved.

## Applications

The theory of manly flaws (marital failure reframed as excess of manliness) and the "Exploiter of Women" posture are the book's paradigm public fictions. The concept anchors Liebow's most-quoted claim: father–son similarity reflects "independently produced look-alikes" facing the same conditions, not cultural transmission.

## Critiques and Limitations

Rests on one corner, two dozen men, 1962–63, no formal design (Liebow's own caveats). The line between a "thin" shadow value and a genuinely held subcultural value is hard to draw observationally — the same evidential gap the [[culture-of-poverty]] debate turns on; positions in [[wacquant-urban-ethnography-debate]] differ on whether later ethnographers preserved or lost Liebow's both-decent-and-street resolution.
