# Ice-Free Corridor

## Definition as Coined

The **ice-free corridor** is the hypothesized overland route by which the first
Americans, having crossed [[beringia]] into Alaska, could have travelled south to
the heart of the continent: a deglaciated gap opening between the coalescing
**Laurentide** (continental) and **Cordilleran** (mountain) ice sheets along the
eastern foot of the Rockies. For decades it was the default answer to *how the
first people got south of the ice*, paired with a [[clovis-culture|Clovis]]-first
chronology. It is an **etic geological-analytical construct**.

## Semantic History

*(Enriched from [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] during integration — the
corridor's rise as the standard model and the mounting doubts about when it was
actually open and habitable.)*

### Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009; ages as radiocarbon/geological BP as he states.)*

**Clovis-first historical model:** mid-20th-century consensus matched Clovis ages to glacier retreat; corridor opened ~**12,000 years ago** along eastern Rocky flanks; first Americans then radiated rapidly (Meltzer summarizing the older model). W. A. Johnston (1933) first proposed Mackenzie-valley southward route after ice retreat; Howard and Antevs tied dry Bering Strait + open corridor to estimated Clovis age.

**Blockage and habitability (as of Meltzer 2009):**
- Laurentide and Cordilleran coalesced along a nearly **2,000 km** stretch of western Canada (roughly BC–Alberta border) for thousands of years around the LGM.
- Interior route effectively blocked by glacial expansion as early as ~**28,000 years ago** until late glacial times.
- After retreat, corridor opened **zipper-like** from northern and southern ends, but newly deglaciated terrain was initially an **“impenetrable mess”** of meltwater lakes, mud flats, and katabatic winds; fully restocked plants/animals and a **habitable** passageway perhaps not until ~**12,000 BP**. **Open ice ≠ habitable corridor.**
- Ancient DNA from fossil bison and brown bears shows **absence of gene flow** between Alaskan and south-of-ice populations for much of **25,000–13,000 years ago** (as of Meltzer 2009)—independent evidence-class support for a barrier. Genetic populations ≠ archaeological cultures.

**Monte Verde constraint:** people could not have gone fast enough via interior to reach [[monte-verde]] ~500 years before the corridor opened (geological evidence obstructed interior from before ~28,000 until after ~12,000 BP). Options: pre-LGM departure from Alaska or **coastal** southward route.

**Archaeological emptiness (as of 2009):** no pre-Clovis sites in interior corridor region; very few Clovis-age; most <10,500 BP; none in New World argued to precede LGM—seemingly precluding interior entry for the moment, with caveat that new discoveries may overturn. Fluted points in Alaska/corridor (~50 / ~60 known) with secure ¹⁴C are **<10,500 BP**—too young for southbound Clovis colonizers; stylistically more like later fluted types; Meltzer argues fluting invented **south** of the ice and spread northward (“migratory backwash”).

**Pendulum of chronology:** 40 years before 2009, interior closed 27–12 ka BP; 20–30 years earlier, corridor thought open much of Wisconsin; ~10 years earlier opinion swung back to closed/impassable much of late Pleistocene. Swings driven by radiocarbon/other dating of glacial features—current dates seemed secure and DNA-supported but can change.

**Late “backwash”:** after ~10,500 BP, bison and some Paleoindian complexes (e.g. Mesa) move **north** into the corridor/Beringia—not the classic southbound first-entry narrative (Meltzer Ch. 9).

## Operationalizations

Reconstructed from glacial geology and till stratigraphy dated by radiocarbon.
Fagan reports the key problem: the corridor was a **late-glacial** feature; for
much of the Last Glacial Maximum the ice sheets coalesced, and even when a gap
existed it may have been a barren, game-poor, waterlogged deglacial landscape
unable to support migrating foragers — and no corridor site is older than
[[clovis-culture|Clovis]] (attributed, Fagan; Rutter's three-zone breakdown,
Stalker's contested Medicine Hat evidence). Meltzer’s habitability lag and aDNA
gene-flow barrier refine the same critique (as of 2009).

## Applications

Its timing is decisive for the [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]: if the
corridor opened only ~13,000–12,000 years ago and was not habitable until then,
pre-Clovis sites like [[monte-verde]] require an **alternative route** — motivating the Pacific **coastal-route**
hypothesis (Fladmark tradition; Meltzer’s favored scenarios include earlier coastal entry and/or later corridor Clovis).

## Critiques and Limitations

The corridor cannot easily account for pre-Clovis occupation south of the ice,
and the coastal alternative is hard to test because its sites are drowned. Route-availability
dates are method-dependent and have swung repeatedly. See
[[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]. Four-way non-identity: a geological passageway is not a migration event.
