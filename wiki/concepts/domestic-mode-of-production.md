# Domestic Mode of Production

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, 1972, chs. 2–3.)* The **domestic mode of production (DMP)** is Sahlins' name for the mode of production characteristic of tribal and primitive economies, in which the **household (domestic group) is the dominant productive institution** — "the household is to the tribal economy as the manor to the medieval economy or the corporation to modern capitalism." The DMP is organized by kinship and the sexual division of labor, deploys simple tools under human control, and is oriented to **use-value (livelihood)** rather than exchange-value or surplus. Because it produces to a finite customary standard of wants and stops when that standard is met, the DMP "harbors an anti-surplus principle" and tends structurally to [[underproduction]].

Sahlins frames it in Marxian terms: the DMP resembles Marx's "simple circulation of commodities" (C→M→C′, producing for consumption) rather than the capitalist "general formula for capital" (M→C→M′, producing for limitless accumulation). It is explicitly an **ideal type** summarizing many concrete domestic economies.

## Semantic History

Sahlins revived Karl Bücher's "independent domestic economy" (against which [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] had polemicized in 1921) and relocated it "chez Marx," fusing it with [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]]'s theory of the peasant family farm. The concept extended [[mode-of-production|Marx's mode-of-production]] vocabulary to kin-ordered societies while resisting the "primitive-communal mode" of orthodox Marxist evolutionism. It became a standard reference in economic anthropology and peasant studies.

## Operationalizations

The DMP's tendencies are made measurable through **[[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov's rule]]**: labor intensity per worker rises with the household's consumer/worker (dependency) ratio. Sahlins reworks village datasets — Scudder's **Mazulu** (Valley Tonga, 1956–57, 20 households) and Pospisil's **Botukebo** (Kapauku, 1955, 16 households) — into "intensity profiles," distinguishing the empirical ("real") slope from the theoretical ("true") Chayanov slope derived from customary per-capita consumption. Deviations measure how much the surrounding social/political structure lifts production above the household norm. At Mazulu, surplus labor produced 7.67% of output (net village *underproduction*); at Botukebo, under a [[big-man]] system, 35.37% (net surplus).

## Applications

- **Underproduction and household failure** ([[underproduction]]): resources and labor go systematically underused; a predictable fraction of households fail to meet their own subsistence needs.
- **Intensification** ([[big-man]], [[redistribution]]): the DMP's centrifugal, anti-surplus tendency is overcome only by forces outside the household — kinship obligation, chiefship, and especially the Melanesian big-man, who mobilizes household surplus through generosity converted into political dependency. Sahlins revises his own earlier (1958) view that surplus *causes* chieftainship, arguing leadership generates surplus, the two developing *pari passu*.
- **Anarchy and dispersion:** considered purely as production, the DMP is "a species of anarchy," tending to maximal spatial dispersion of households; kinship and political institutions counter this centrifugal drift.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* Chayanov's rule holds "in general but not in detail"; real communities deviate in patterned ways, so the DMP is a baseline modified by politics, not a law. Critics question how far a model built on Russian peasant farms and reworked village statistics generalizes across tribal horticulture, foraging, and pastoralism, and whether the household is really the unit everywhere. The framework is a single-author theoretical construct in attributed voice; its ethnographic data inherit the fieldwork dates and colonial framing of the mid-century sources Sahlins reworks.
