# Social structure

## Definition as Coined

In British social anthropology as formulated by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] (notably *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]*, 1922, Chs V–VI and Ch. I's concern with the "structure of Andamanese society"), **social structure** names the **system of actually existing social relations and institutions** that form a society as a whole — to be studied in its present functioning ("social physiology"), not primarily as a product of conjectural historical origins. Radcliffe-Brown treated the mass of institutions, customs, and beliefs as "a single whole or system that determines the life of the society" (attributed). His mature definition comes in "On Social Structure" (1940, in *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]*, 1952): "We do not observe a 'culture', since that word denotes, not any concrete reality, but an abstraction... direct observation does reveal to us that these human beings are connected by a complex network of social relations. I use the term 'social structure' to denote this network of actually existing relations." He drew the load-bearing distinction between **social structure** — "the set of actually existing relations, at a given moment of time," directly observed — and **structural form**, "what the field-worker describes," the general pattern abstracted from particular instances; and defined **social personality** as "the position occupied by a human being in a social structure." (Evans-Pritchard, in *The Nuer*, is noted as restricting "social structure" to persistent groups — a documented divergence.)

## Semantic History

- **Pre-anthropological:** "structure" and "social structure" circulate in 19th-c. social thought (organic analogies, Comtean/Spencerian systems) without the fieldwork-linked British program sense.
- **Radcliffe-Brown / British SF:** structure as the network of actual person-to-person relations (vs. "culture," an abstraction), distinguished from **structural form** (the abstracted pattern); function as contribution to structural continuity; Andaman "simple" structure as type-case. Three branches of study: social **morphology** (classification of structural types), social **physiology** (how systems persist), and structural change.
- **Parsons / U.S. sociology:** structure as institutionalized roles and value-orientations within a [[social-system|social system]] ([[structural-functionalism]]; *[[parsons-social-system-1951|The Social System]]*).
- **Merton:** codifies functional analysis without requiring total-system unity postulates ([[manifest-and-latent-functions]], [[dysfunction]]).
- **Leach (1954):** In *[[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954|Political Systems of Highland Burma]]*, [[leach-edmund|Leach]] treated ethnographic "social structure" as a **model of ideal patterns** (relations regarded as correct), necessarily equilibrated as construct, not as a naturally equilibrated empirical system. Structure in practical situations is "a set of ideas about the distribution of power"; individuals hold contradictory ideas without embarrassment because expression is cultural/ritual. Historical facts are never in equilibrium; the appearance of natural equilibrium is an illusion examined in the Kachin case ([[gumsa-gumlao-oscillation]]). See [[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-study]].
- **Leach (1959/61):** *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]* Ch. 1 rejects R-B comparison of social structures as natural types ([[butterfly-collecting]]) in favour of organizational ideas as **mathematical patterns** (topology of connectedness); Ch. 2: any structural analysis of kinship is discussion of **ideal** behaviour, not statistical norms ([[ethnographic-algebra]]). Lévi-Strauss (1953) noted similarity between Leach’s Jinghpaw view of structure and his own (Leach’s report).
- **Later:** network formalization; Marxist and critical uses; Giddens's [[duality-of-structure]] reworks structure as rules/resources reproduced in action ([[structuration-theory]]).

## Operationalizations

| Tradition | How "structure" is gotten at | Disagreement |
|---|---|---|
| Radcliffe-Brown ethnography | Map local groups, kinship/family, ceremonial and belief systems as one interdependent whole | Meaning from systemic relation, not trait lists |
| Parsonsian systems theory | Institutionalized roles, pattern variables, functional prerequisites | Abstract systemic model vs concrete kinship morphology |
| Network analysis | Nodes/edges, density, brokerage | Formal relations vs institutional/cultural content |
| Structuration / practice theory | Rules and resources reproduced in interaction | Structure not only external constraint |
| Leach (Kachin) | Ideal-type category structures (gumsa/gumlao/Shan) + power-distribution ideas; ritual as expression | Model vs Nature; equilibrium of model only |

## Applications

- Andaman ethnography: structure of local group / tribe / family ([[andamanese]]; [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]]).
- Comparative kinship and political organisation in later British anthropology (to densify with African Systems, Australian work).
- Kachin Hills: structure as model; [[gumsa]]/[[gumlao]] poles; [[mayu-dama]] ([[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-study]]).
- Sociology: class structure, organizational structure, world-system structure — related but not identical concepts.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Ahistoricism:** critics argue structure-function analysis freezes process and underplays conflict, colonialism, and historical transformation (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]], Gouldner on Functionalism).
- **Whose structure?** Ethnographer's systemic synthesis vs participants' categories (parallel issue to Malinowski's ethnographer-as-synthesizer).
- **Contested term:** same words cover incompatible objects (kinship morphology vs role system vs network vs generative rules) — always name the tradition.
