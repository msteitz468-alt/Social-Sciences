# Core Clinical Tasks (functions of health care systems)

[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s claim that all health care systems perform the same set of
universal "clinical" functions — where "clinical" means "not… a specific activity of professional
medical care, but… these general health care functions." Set out in
*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* (1980).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman identifies **five core clinical functions** (verbatim, attributed):

1. "The cultural construction of illness as psychosocial experience."
2. "The establishment of general criteria to guide the health care seeking process and to evaluate
   treatment approaches that exist prior to and independent of individual episodes of sickness."
3. "The management of particular illness episodes through communicative operations such as labeling and
   explaining."
4. "Healing activities per se… from drugs and surgery to psychotherapy, supportive care, and healing
   rituals."
5. "The management of therapeutic outcomes, including cure, treatment failure, recurrence, chronic
   illness, impairment, and death."

The load-bearing claim: these functions "are performed by all health care systems… the system as a whole,
not just the healer, heals." Kleinman argues "'clinical'… is not a Western category, but a category
intrinsic to all societies."

## Semantic History

The typology synthesizes Kleinman's earlier papers (1973–1977) and frames the whole comparative project
of *Patients and Healers*: the functions are performed variably across the popular, folk, and professional
sectors of the [[health-care-system]]. Function 2 draws on Schutz's "structures of relevance" and
Schwartz's "hierarchies of resort."

## Operationalizations

Function 4 contains a load-bearing internal distinction: **"curing of disease"** ("effective control of
disordered biological and psychological processes") versus **"healing of illness"** ("the provision of
personal and social meaning for the life problems created by sickness"). Kleinman argues modern
biomedicine excels at curing and often fails at healing — physicians are "taught to cure, not to care"
(Eisenberg) — while indigenous healers, treating mostly self-limited disease, chronic non-fatal illness,
and [[somatization]], "must heal." He also names **"cultural iatrogenesis"** — clinical problems
"systematically built into health care systems" by discrepancies between the [[clinical-reality|clinical
realities]] of different sectors.

## Applications

- Grounds the argument that healing must be evaluated on cultural, social, behavioral, and physiological
  levels (see [[symbolic-healing]]).
- Frames the [[illness-disease-distinction]] as a clinical *task* (constructing illness), not just an
  analytic distinction.
- Provides the comparative scaffold for placing biomedical and indigenous practice "side by side."

## Critiques and Limitations

The universality claim is argued, not assumed — Kleinman explicitly declines to add a sixth
"health-maintenance/prevention" function (contra Dunn's broader "health systems"), keeping the scheme
"clinical" (sickness-management) in scope, and warns against "medicocentric" bias in imputing
Western-style "therapeutic systems" to all societies. As an etic grid it flattens real differences in how
sectors divide the functions.
