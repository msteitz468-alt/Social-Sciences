# Decomposition of Labor

## Definition as Coined

Alongside [[decomposition-of-capital]], [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, Ch. II) describes the **decomposition of labor**: the industrial working population differentiates by skill and stratification so that a homogeneous [[proletariat]] of the early-industrial image no longer holds. Skill hierarchies, status differences, and organizational differentiation fragment the "working class" as a single conflict actor.

## Semantic History

Connects industrial-sociology findings on skill and occupation to class theory; pairs with the rise of the [[new-middle-class|new middle class]] as further evidence against simple bourgeoisie/proletariat polarization (see also [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]).

## Operationalizations

Skill grades, occupational status scales, white-collar expansion, differential organization and mobility chances within wage-earning populations.

## Applications

Supports Dahrendorf's claim that post-Marx industrial societies remain conflictual without converging on two revolutionary camps.

## Critiques and Limitations

Later labor sociology (deskilling vs upskilling debates, dual labor markets) reopens whether decomposition is secular or cyclical; Dahrendorf's mid-century Western cases do not settle the long-run trajectory.
