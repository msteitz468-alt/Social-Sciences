# Cattle before Crops

Analytical phrase for Holocene African pathways in which **pastoralism / stock-keeping appears independently of, and often prior to, cultivation** — a pattern Mitchell and Lane highlight as of universal comparative interest in *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|The Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology]]* (2013, Ch. 1), citing Marshall & Hildebrand (2002).

## Definition as Coined

“Cattle before crops” names sequences where domestic animals (especially cattle, also caprines) enter economies of foraging or specialized herding **without** simultaneous cereal agriculture. It challenges Near Eastern-derived expectations that “Neolithic” packages always couple plants and animals.

## Semantic History

- **Marshall & Hildebrand 2002:** phrase popularized for African pastoral origins (as cited by Mitchell & Lane).
- **Handbook 2013:** editors list the pattern among Africa’s contributions to world comparative archaeology alongside independent metallurgy debates, heterarchical urbanism, and Indian Ocean connections.
- Regional chapters densify: Sahara/Sahel herding and pottery with wild resources (Barich; di Lernia’s critical reappraisal of early Nabta cattle); Nile and Horn “cattle before plants” sequences (Haaland; Curtis); southern African LSA livestock centuries before Iron Age farmers (Sadr Ch. 44).

## Operationalizations

Archaeological: AMS faunal dates vs first cultivated plants; ceramic + lithic associations; genetic livestock phylogenies (date-stamped). Linguistic: livestock vocabulary transfers (e.g. Khoe → southeastern Bantu — Ehret via Sadr).

## Applications

- North Africa / Sahara herding reappraisals (fragile early *Bos* claims vs later introductions — positions attributed on [[nabta-playa]] and related pages).
- Sahel pastoral immigration then farming (Breunig).
- Southern Africa: LSA sheep/cattle before Bantu-associated farming villages (Sadr — attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

Early cattle claims (e.g. Nabta/Bir Kiseiba) are **contested** (di Lernia vs earlier optimistic readings — record both). “Cattle before crops” is a **pathway type**, not a universal African law; many regions show other orders or long multi-resource mosaics. Never equate herders with a single language or genetic population.

### Regional densification notes (same handbook)

*(Attributed — *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]*; cross-chapter synthesis R7-057.)* Independent regional syntheses converge on domestic animals preceding archaeologically visible plant cultivation in Sahara–Sahel, Horn, East Africa, and southernmost Africa (Egypt is the major exception with a Near Eastern package of both). Lane (Ch. 40): East African Pastoral Neolithic as “one of the few examples in the world where herding preceded cultivation.” Sadr (Ch. 44): southern LSA livestock centuries before Bantu-associated farming villages. Uncontroversial Nabta domestic *Bos* later than optimistic early claims (see [[nabta-playa]]). Tichitt pearl-millet villages already agropastoral packages (see [[dhar-tichitt]]) — not a pure cattle-before-crops case. Four-way non-identity: herding horizon ≠ Khoe language ≠ San genetic lineage ≠ Bantu demic package.
