# Sociolinguistic Rule (Alternation and Co-occurrence)

## Definition as Coined

Susan Ervin-Tripp, in "On Sociolinguistic Rules: Alternation and Co-occurrence" (Ch. 7 of Gumperz & Hymes, eds., *Directions in Sociolinguistics*, 1972), proposed that sociolinguistic competence is organized by two general classes of rule. **Alternation rules** govern the *choice* among alternative ways of speaking — which address term, which code or style is selected for a given social configuration (the paradigmatic axis). **Co-occurrence rules** govern what forms *must* or *may* accompany one another once a choice is made (the syntagmatic axis), subdivided into **horizontal co-occurrence** (predictability between items at the same level of structure within a stretch of discourse — e.g., lexical register consistency) and **vertical co-occurrence** (predictability across levels of structure — phonology, lexicon, syntax — such as a syntactic register normally co-occurring with a matching phonological realization). The editors explicitly labeled these the sociolinguistic analogues of Jakobson and Halle's paradigmatic/syntagmatic axes.

## Semantic History

Ervin-Tripp built the framework directly on Brown and Ford's (in Hymes, ed., 1964) address research and on Geoghegan's (1971) information-processing/componential formalism, which she used to diagram American address rules as a binary-selector flow chart. She generalized Brown and Gilman's (1960) power-and-solidarity dimensions (see [[power-and-solidarity]]) as likely universal axes underlying alternation in address systems, while showing via Korean data (Howell 1967) that "solidarity" may itself decompose into separable intimacy and status components in some systems.

## Operationalizations

- American address-term selection modeled as a flow chart of binary selectors (child/adult addressee; status-marked settings such as courtrooms or faculty meetings; colleague-rank equality triggering obligatory first-name reciprocity; occupational/courtesy titles; a senior alter's option to license first-naming).
- Cross-cultural comparison of address systems: English university/boarding-school, Bisayan, Korean, nineteenth-century Russian gentry (via Friedrich, ch. 9 of the same volume), Yiddish, and Soviet-era Russian.
- Register and style distinguished as a co-occurrence-defined alternation domain (following Hymes 1964 and Halliday, McIntosh & Strevens), with **metaphorical alternation** (code-switching within a single situation to convey meaning) distinguished from ordinary situational alternation.
- Labov's (1966) New York City social-stratification data on phonological variables (r), (th) cited as evidence that co-occurrence operates systematically across style levels within and between social classes.

## Applications

The framework's illustrative case is the "Poussaint incident": a policeman's "What's your name, boy?"/"What's your first name, boy?" exchange with Dr. Alvin Poussaint, analyzed as a precise, rule-governed racial insult in which the address term "boy" selects a category neutralizing adult/occupational-rank status.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ervin-Tripp herself closed the chapter with a methodological caution distinguishing three referents of "rule" (generative/interpretive structure; speakers' reported beliefs about practice; normative standards of correctness), warning that behavioral rules and speakers' own reports of their rules are systematically different — self-report is not behavior (citing Labov's index of linguistic insecurity).
