# Mutualism (Historical Archaeology)

[[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s (1996, Ch. 2) **mutualist** or **nets** perspective for historical archaeology: men, women, artifacts, and landscapes are nodes in open nets of relationship; past peoples are not isolable "cultures" detached from world connection. Epistemic core of **[[global-historical-archaeology]]**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Orser 1996 citing Carrithers 1992.)* Mutualism proposes that individuals and their social relationships are "the basic stuff of human life" (Carrithers 1992:11). Against culture-as-cause ("people do things because of their culture"), Orser (via Carrithers) held that people "do things with, to, and in respect of each other, using means that we can describe, if we wish to, as cultural." He traced open-net imagery through Durkheim, Radcliffe-Brown, and Alexander Lesser's "weblike, netlike connections." **Artifacts become "the symbolic surrogates of netlike connections."**

## Semantic History

Orser's archaeological adaptation of Carrithers's mutualism (itself drawing social-psychology mutualism and network anthropology). Distinct from biological mutualism — do not equate. Later vault programs such as [[entanglement-theory]] (Hodder 2012) share a relational emphasis but are not the same package and are not claimed by Orser 1996.

## Three Implications (Orser, via Palmares)

1. **Technology as relationship.** Firearms at [[palmares|Palmares]] matter less as objects than as the relationships that allowed people to need, acquire, and use them (trade, law-breaking, multiethnic alliance).
2. **Multi-scalar nets.** Relationships run from interpersonal mutual understandings (Carrithers) to Atlantic-scale webs (Wolf 1982 on slave trade linking Palmaristas, Portuguese, Dutch, natives, Angolan warriors).
3. **Methodological reassembly.** Wolf's opening warning: researchers falsify reality when they disassemble interconnectedness and fail to reassemble it. Treating Palmares as a sealed "culture" or a site as a bag of sherds undervalues creativity and connection.

Orser stressed that partners in contact were **not equals** — nets are real, power is uneven (later Ch. 7: Foucault netlike power + Lipman-Blumen + Wolf structural/tactical power).

## Antinet Foil

Orser engaged Schuyler (1988): major HA contributions "at the site level" as historic ethnographies of culture "packages." Orser accepted the necessity of site reports and excavation limits but rejected **isolation** and community closure as analytic defaults. Anti-isolation illustrations before Palmares: Silcott multi-scalar trade; Ozarks/Moser farmstead Expo badge; Millwood plantation medal.

## Dialectical Speech (Ch. 7 extension)

Mutualism requires **simultaneous** top-down and bottom-up analysis — not Glassie/Beaudry "inside out" alone, not pure bottom-rail history, not pure elite history. Elite–subaltern relations are often "shouting matches," not equal discourses. Elite gardens encode subalterns primarily as **negation/invisibility**.

## Operationalizations

- Artifact distributions as relationship evidence ([[colono-ware]]; Palmares pipes vs. Caribbean comparanda).
- Landscape as invented/created space (Ch. 6): mutualist regions and "transpatial relations" (e.g., two Fort Oranges + Oudepost I as one Dutch colonial region with local identities).
- Multiscalar dialectics (Marquardt via Orser Ch. 8): agency, contradiction, structure, power; effective scale.

## Applications

[[palmares]] (hard case against isolation); [[gorttoose]] (estate nets and peasant resistance); program slogan "think globally, dig locally."

## Critiques and Limitations

Network metaphors can under-specify domination (Orser tries to correct with uneven power and the [[four-haunts]]). Relation to Actor-Network Theory and later entanglement programs is historiographic, not internal to 1996. Isolationist site ethnography remains a live alternative research style in CRM and local HA.
