# Gimwali

## Definition as Coined

Trobriand **emic** term for **ordinary barter** — "trade pure and simple" with haggling over utilities — as distinguished by [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] from ceremonial [[kula|Kula]] exchange of [[vaygua|vaygu'a]]. In *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* Ch. VI gift typology, gimwali is the seventh class: mutual advantage, equivalence adjusted by bargaining, often between industrial and agricultural districts.

## Semantic History

| Moment | Sense |
|---|---|
| Boyowan emic (1915–18) | Open barter; also scorn term for improper gift/Kula conduct ("done like a gimwali") |
| Malinowski 1922 | Analytic pole of gift continuum opposite pure gifts and ceremonial deferred barter |
| Later economic anthropology | Marker for commodity-like exchange within non-market societies |

## Operationalizations

Malinowski's criteria (attributed): mutual advantage; newly manufactured goods; no ceremony, magic, or special partnership; pronounced interest shown in the exchange (opposite of ceremonial etiquette of throwing gifts down). Related: *vava* (fish-for-vegetables gimwali between non-wasi villages).

## Applications

- Contrasts with [[kula]] and wasi in [[gift-exchange]] theory
- Precursor evidence in [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]
- Society: [[trobriand-islanders]]; study: [[malinowski-argonauts-study]]

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-ethnographer period portrait; later Massim work may refine the emic range of the term. The gift/gimwali contrast is analytic as well as native — Malinowski ordered a continuum natives do not name as one scale.
