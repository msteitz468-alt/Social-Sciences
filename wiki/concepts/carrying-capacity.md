# Carrying Capacity

## Definition as Coined

An ecological measure: the maximum population an environment can sustain given a population's
level of extractive efficiency and behaviour. Imported into anthropology and archaeology to
model the population ceilings of foraging and horticultural societies. Treated here as
elaborated at the *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium (1968),
where it anchored the population-ecology papers.

## Semantic History

The concept was developed for range and wildlife management and, in colonial Africa, for
setting "native reserve" population limits; it was then applied to foraging and swidden
populations. A recurring finding at the symposium was that human groups stabilize **below**
absolute capacity: [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] cited carrying-capacity studies finding
populations "at about 20–50 per cent of capacity," and Washburn proposed that stabilizing at
20–30% of carrying capacity was the most successful long-term strategy — an argument that
fed the [[original-affluent-society|affluent-society]] thesis (foragers are not pressed
against subsistence limits).

## Operationalizations

*(Attributed — Man the Hunter contributors.)*
- **Density equilibrium** (Birdsell): a foraging population approaches carrying capacity for
  its technology and social behaviour, with social factors stabilizing numbers below the
  saturation point; because human rates of increase are high, populations reach effective
  capacity "in Pleistocene time perspectives... instantaneous[ly]" and tend to overshoot when
  restoring depleted numbers.
- **Rainfall–density regression** (Birdsell 1953): across 123 uniform Australian tribal
  territories, a logarithmic relation of area to mean annual rainfall (curvilinear r ≈ 0.8),
  taken as evidence of an equilibrium density system near capacity; unearned resources (river
  mouths, tidal reefs) broke the relation.
- **Budding-off threshold** (Birdsell): expanding groups split off colonists at ~60–70% of
  carrying capacity.
- **Editors' estimate:** "one-per-square-mile" as a useful figure for Pleistocene carrying
  capacity, with agricultural densities later exceeding it by a factor of ten to a thousand.

## Applications

Carrying capacity underlies models of foraging population regulation (infanticide, birth
spacing, the "magic numbers" 25 and 500 — see [[hunter-gatherer]]), of the below-capacity
"affluence" argument, and of Pleistocene demographic reconstruction by
[[ethnographic-analogy]].

**Horticultural / ritual ecology ([[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]], *[[rappaport-pigs-for-the-ancestors-1968|Pigs for the Ancestors]]*, 1968).** Among the [[tsembaga]] (1962–63), Rappaport estimated maximum numbers of **people and pigs** supportable indefinitely by described swidden procedures without changing individual intakes and without environmental depletion. He stressed anthropological CC is not mere survival density but support **without degradation**, and that other density-dependent factors may limit populations *below* that ceiling. Parameterized estimates put the 1962–63 Tsembaga population **well below** computed human CC ranges (hundreds of persons under various assumptions); pig CC was estimated separately at herd max and min. A stated goal of the study was to show how [[ritual-regulation]] (the [[kaiko]] cycle) keeps trophic demands within territorial CC. Methodological caveats (defining absolute vs anthropocentric degradation; fallow length; constant crop mix and age-sex structure; measurement error) are explicit in Ch. 3 and Appendix 10. Critics later used the "well below CC" finding to question how much "regulation" was doing — see [[ecological-anthropology-ritual-regulation-debate]].

### Kirch 1984 — Polynesian islands: Bayliss-Smith, Hawaiian sigmoid, Tikopia regulation

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch treats population growth as fundamental to Polynesian social evolution yet **not a prime mover**, and insists population is never solely a dependent or independent variable: “The views of Malthus and of Boserup must both find their place in our models.” Absolute Malthusian ceiling is rejected as an analytical default.

**Bayliss-Smith dual-constraint model.** Kirch adopts Bayliss-Smith’s (1974) island model: population is regulated by **ecological constraints** (land area, periodic disasters, environmental deterioration from over-exploitation, productivity limits, nutritional requirements) *and* **cultural constraints** (fertility/mortality controls, “social” or trade production beyond bare subsistence, psychological perceptions of diet/needs, technology and agricultural skill). K is density where *r* = 0 and is **not static** — disasters, degradation, and irrigation can shift it; over multi-decade timescales Kirch redefines island carrying capacity as the population capable of surviving severe disaster-induced famine, so K fluctuates stochastically.

**Hypothetical growth trajectories (Fig. 27).** Extinction (mystery islands); pure exponential (colonization only — unsustainable if unchecked); logistic/sigmoid; overshoot/crash; oscillation around K; step growth if K rises via intensification or new crops.

**Hawaiian archaeological demography.** Combined West Hawaiʻi sample of 113 dated residential sites yields a **typical sigmoid**: small early population; rapid expansion ~AD 1200–1600; slowing in the 17th century; peak ~AD 1650–1700 with possible pre-contact local decline. Kirch cautions pure logistic (density-dependence at all densities) poorly fits colonizing sequences, and notes leeward dry areas (West Hawaiʻi, Kahoʻolawe) have lower intensification ceilings than windward valleys still increasing at Cook’s arrival. Kahoʻolawe is cited as overshoot/crash under human-induced degradation. In the epilogue: high contact densities in Tonga, Hawaiʻi, and Easter without any absolute carrying capacity necessarily reached; demography is as much a product of social-political systems (e.g. pigs retained despite demographic cost as political-ritual goods) as of ecology.

**[[tikopia|Tikopia]] cultural regulation.** 1929: 1,281 on 4.6 km² (278/km²) near traditional agricultural capacity; after Christian abolition of abortion/infanticide and curtailment of overseas voyaging, ~1,750 by 1952 (380/km²) exceeded capacity under environmental stress (1952–53 *onge* famine). Emic concept *fakatau ki te kai* (“measured according to the food”) underwrites controls — celibacy of junior males, coitus interruptus, abortion, infanticide, sea-voyaging, and ultimately war between divisions — aimed at culturally specified living standards, not bare survival.

**Cross-island variation.** Tonga likely reached oscillating steady-state more than a millennium before contact; Easter appears (on Kirch’s 1984 reading) to have overshot with late prehistoric crash; islands were at different stages of the demographic cycle when Europeans enumerated them (after Golson). Stratification levels (Sahlins 1958 criteria) correlate with maximal political-unit population and arable density, but Kirch refuses to assign causal primacy to demography → stratification or the reverse without diachronic archaeological demography.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is contested. Birdsell himself argued that "marginal" hunter environments are a
"pseudo-concept" (areas marginal for farmers are not marginal for foragers), undercutting any
single "optimum environment." Others (Alland, Woodburn) argued disease and medical factors,
not just environmental capacity, regulate populations; Washburn noted human hunter densities
fall far below those of forest monkeys or baboons in comparable habitat, so a partly
carnivorous "bipedal niche" trades density for behavioural plasticity. Deevey cautioned that
food supply alone does not appear to set band and tribe size limits — social forces are more
determinative, with environmental capacity only an upper bound rarely reached.
