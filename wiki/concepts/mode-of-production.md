# Mode of Production

## Definition as Coined

In the [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]] (1848),
[[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] treat a **mode of production** as the
historically specific way a society produces and exchanges its material life — its
**instruments of production**, its **relations of production**, and the **property relations**
corresponding to them. The concept does the central explanatory work of
[[historical-materialism|historical materialism]]: the [[bourgeoisie|bourgeoisie]] "is itself
the product of a long course of development, of a series of revolutions in the modes of
production and of exchange," and epochs are named for their dominant mode (feudal, bourgeois).

## Semantic History

In the *Manifesto* the term is used narratively rather than defined technically. *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867) elaborates the **capitalist** mode as process of production of capital: [[commodity]] cell-form, [[labor-power]] as commodity, [[surplus-value]], accumulation, and [[primitive-accumulation|historical origin]]. The full base/superstructure formula of the 1859 *Preface* is on the wiki via its verbatim quotation in [[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Stalin 1938]] (see [[base-and-superstructure]]).

**Stalin 1938 (Soviet codification, attributed).** *[[stalin-dialectical-and-historical-materialism-1938|Dialectical and Historical Materialism]]* fixes the definition — mode of production as the unity of **productive forces** (instruments of production plus the people who operate them, with their production experience and labor skill) and **relations of production** (relations of men to each other in production: co-operation, domination/subordination, or transitional) — and canonizes exactly **five** types of relations of production in historical order: primitive communal, slave, feudal, capitalist, Socialist. Each is characterized by who owns the means of production and (for slave/feudal) the degree of ownership of the worker. The productive forces are "the most mobile and revolutionary element," their changes compelling correspondence of the relations of production. This rigid five-stage schema, obligatory in Soviet-bloc scholarship, is the version Wolf, Balibar, and the anthropological debates below were largely reacting against. The *Manifesto* dynamic remains: at "a certain stage," feudal property relations "became no longer compatible with the already developed productive forces; they became so many fetters." *Capital* I supplies the internal law of motion of the bourgeois mode once established. In twentieth-century anthropology and archaeology the concept was extended (and contested) as a way to classify non-capitalist societies — the "Asiatic," "ancient," and "primitive-communal" modes — feeding
debates over evolutionary typologies of society.

**Wolf 1982 (anthropological political economy).** In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]*, [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] treats mode of production as a **research-orienting supposition**, not a Systems-Marxist universal law (1997 preface). Definition (attributed): a specific, historically occurring set of social relations through which [[social-labor|labor is deployed]] to wrest energy from nature by tools, skills, organization, and knowledge. Utility "does not lie in classification but in its capacity to underline the strategic relationships involved in the deployment of social labor." For parsimony he constructs **three** modes only — capitalist, [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]], [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] — denying that the trinity is exhaustive or an evolutionary sequence. Mode ≠ society (different levels of abstraction); mode ≠ technology. Marx's longer list (primitive-communitarian, slaveholding, Germanic, Slavonic, peasant, feudal, Asiatic, capitalist) is noted as unequal in criteria; some were accessory or rest on outdated history. Wolf begins exposition with the **capitalist** mode though it is later historically (18th c.), because understanding it unlocks the others. Capstone (Afterword): relationships of a mode "possess force" — they subject populations to imperatives and impart directionality; men make history not under conditions of their choosing.

**Balibar 1965 (*Reading Capital* Part Five, attributed).** In *[[althusser-reading-capital-1965|Reading Capital]]*, [[balibar-etienne|Étienne Balibar]] treats Marx’s construction of the mode of production as an [[epistemological-break]] with the philosophy of history. A “mode” is “a system of forms which represents one state of the variation of the set of elements which necessarily enter into the process considered.” Invariant elements: worker; means of production (object of labour; means of labour); non-worker. These are linked **twice** — by a **property** connection (separation/appropriation of surplus labour) and by a **real material appropriation** connection (*wirkliche Aneignung* in the labour process). Marx’s [[combination-combinatory|combination]] is the relationship between these two connections and their interdependence — **not** a strict combinatory of fixed factor-natures. In capitalism the two connections take homologous form as separations; earlier modes need not. Classes are [[bearer-trager|bearers (*Träger*)]] of the three-term economic relation, not pre-given subjects. **Reproduction** analyzes permanence of social relations (simple reproduction as formal conditions of all reproduction). **Transition** is itself a structured mode with special [[dislocation-decalage|dislocation]] (e.g. manufacture: capitalist property without specifically capitalist productive forces / formal without real subsumption) — not an irrational hiatus between two structures. Althusser’s “ideal average” / *Kerngestalt*: the theoretical object is the concept of the mode, not England as impure historical example. See [[structural-marxism]], [[hubs/theory/structural-marxism]].

## Operationalizations

The *Manifesto* individuates modes qualitatively by their characteristic instruments (guild
handicraft vs. manufacture vs. steam-powered "Modern Industry"), their class relations
(lord/serf vs. bourgeois/proletarian), and their property forms (feudal vs. bourgeois private
property). It offers no quantitative measure; later Marxist scholarship debates the criteria
sharply.

Wolf operationalizes by asking who commands social labor and how surplus moves: (1) **capitalist** — wealth buys [[labor-power]]; producers severed from means of production; ceaseless accumulation and technical change (three intertwined characteristics); "There is no such thing as mercantile or merchant capitalism… only mercantile wealth. Capitalism… must be capitalism-in-production"; (2) **tributary** — producers keep means of production; surplus exacted by political/military means (Amin's term covering Asiatic–feudal continuum); (3) **kin-ordered** — labor mobilized through kinship idioms. Boundaries with mercantile putting-out, commodity peonage, and slavery are specified so market involvement does not automatically equal capitalism ([[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]).

## Applications

The organizing concept of [[historical-materialism|historical materialism]] and its
periodization of history; widely used in economic sociology, comparative-historical sociology,
anthropology, and archaeology to characterize how societies reproduce themselves materially.
In economic anthropology, [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] adapted the term for kin-ordered societies as the
[[domestic-mode-of-production|domestic mode of production]] (*[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, 1972),
relocating [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]]'s peasant-farm analysis "chez Marx."

**Wolf 1982** uses the three-mode toolkit to organize global ethnohistory of European expansion: Iberian America, [[north-american-fur-trade|fur trade]], [[atlantic-slave-trade|slave trade]], Asian conquest trade, industrial cotton and other commodities, and the making of new laborers — always asking how capitalist circuits articulated with other modes rather than erasing them (Mandel via Wolf: articulated multi-modal system under a capitalist world market). See [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]] and [[anthropological-political-economy]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics question whether world history sorts cleanly into a sequence of modes, and whether the
forces/relations "fetters" mechanism is a genuine causal law or a retrospective narrative. The
extension to pre-capitalist societies (especially the "[[asiatic-mode-of-production|Asiatic mode of production]]") is widely
regarded as the weakest and most Eurocentric part of the schema. Wolf collapses Asiatic/feudal into one [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary continuum]] precisely to avoid that reification — at the cost of breadth some Marxists still reject. [[wittfogel-karl-a|Wittfogel]] (*[[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957|Oriental Despotism]]* Ch. 9) reconstructs AMP's rise under Marx/Engels/early Lenin and its Soviet-era suppression as a political as well as theoretical event — a Cold-War historiography of the mode, not a replacement for Marx's primary texts (not all yet ingested). Reception of *EPWH* charged Systems Marxism and under-attention to culture/meaning; Wolf replies that modes are guides not laws and that culture is relational and power-laden (1997 preface; Afterword). As throughout, the framework's claims are recorded in attributed voice, not as findings.

**Graeber & Wengrow (2021), attributed.** *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* ch. 5 argues that "mode of production" (and ecological/optimal-foraging reductions) cannot explain Pacific Coast forager diversity — notably why Northwest Coast societies developed slavery and potlatch hierarchy while neighbouring Californian foragers rejected slavery and ranked display. They prefer [[schismogenesis]] and politics of values over mode-typing. See [[dawn-of-everything-debate]].

## Lenin: five coexisting "socio-economic structures" in transitional Russia (attributed)

In "Left-Wing" Childishness and the Petty-Bourgeois Mentality (1918), reproduced by Lenin in the Tenth Congress tax-in-kind speeches (*[[lenin-tax-in-kind-1921|The Tax in Kind]]*, 1921, and *[[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]*), Lenin proposed that post-1917 Russia's economy was not a single mode of production but a mixture of **five coexisting "socio-economic structures"**: (1) patriarchal, largely natural, peasant farming; (2) small commodity production (the majority of grain-selling peasants); (3) private capitalism; (4) state capitalism; (5) socialism — arguing Russia's vastness meant these types were "intermingled" and that the petty-bourgeois element (types 1–2) predominated numerically. He held the real economic struggle was not between state capitalism and socialism (4 vs. 5) but between the petty bourgeoisie and private capitalism (1+2+3) jointly resisting state interference from either state-capitalist or state-socialist direction (4+5) — because petty producers oppose accounting and control as such, whatever its political character. He argued state capitalism, arising from amalgamation of small enterprise via free exchange under NEP, was "nothing to fear" and represented "a step forward" from the disorganized status quo, generalizing: "Capitalism is a bane compared with socialism. Capitalism is a boon compared with medievalism, small production, and the evils of bureaucracy which spring from the dispersal of the small producers" — hence "some capitalism is inevitable" as an intermediary link given Russia could not transition directly from small production to socialism (see [[state-capitalism]], [[peasantry-as-class]], [[new-economic-policy]]).

Earlier, in *[[lenin-what-the-friends-of-the-people-are-1894|What the "Friends of the People" Are]]* (1894), Lenin had already applied the base/relations-of-production framework programmatically, arguing (via Marx's 1859 Preface) that "the development of the social-economic formations is a process of natural history" — society's succession of modes following objective laws analogous to natural-historical processes, not conscious contractual design — and treating Russian handicraft "domestic system" production as already "pure capitalism" despite lacking machine industry, on the grounds that it exhibited commodity economy, concentration of the means of production, and wage-dependence (see [[base-and-superstructure]], [[historical-materialism]]).

## Lenin 1899: home-market theory and the three-stages schema (attributed)

*[[lenin-development-of-capitalism-in-russia-1899|The Development of Capitalism in Russia]]* (1899) opens (Ch. I) from a general-theory premise attributed to [[marx-karl|Marx]]: a market is "a category of commodity economy," becoming specifically a *capitalist* category only once commodity relations gain complete sway — so any home-market analysis must begin from simple commodity economy and trace its transformation into a capitalist mode of production. Against Narodnik economists who held that the ruin of small producers shrinks the home market, Lenin argued (via *Capital* I) the reverse: the **expropriation of the direct producers** itself creates the home market, on two sides at once — turning former means of subsistence and means of production into capital (a market chiefly for **means of production**, concentrated in Department I) and turning the expropriated producer into a seller of labor-power and buyer of subsistence goods (a market chiefly for **articles of consumption**, Department II). He held the home market grows chiefly on account of Department I — means of production — making its growth "to a certain extent independent" of, though never wholly divorced from, personal consumption; the same dual mechanism reappears empirically in his treatment of the differentiating Russian peasantry (see [[peasantry-as-class]]).

Lenin's empirical chapters on industry (Chs. V–VII) organize Russian economic history into a **three-stages schema**, presented as a continuous rather than discrete progression: (1) **small commodity production** (primitive, unchanging hand technique; little separation of industrialist from agriculturalist; no sharp capitalist/proletarian opposition); (2) **capitalist manufacture** (division of labor turns the producer into a "labourer performing one detailed operation," still by hand, but generating both concentrated capital and a differentiated wage-dependent population, with lingering land-ties producing "intermediary elements" that blunt class opposition); (3) **large-scale machine industry** (a full technical revolution, "socialising" production, completing the separation of industry from agriculture "once and for all" and sharpening class contradiction to its "highest point," while also generating countervailing pressure toward "planned regulation of production"). Lenin argued small commodity production tends to develop into manufacture, and manufacture "with enormous rapidity" into machine industry — illustrated biographically by major factory owners (Savva Morozov among others) who personally passed through all three stages — and defended this schema, in a footnote, against Held's and Bücher's rival classification (which he accused of "confus[ing] the manufactory with the factory") as "more correct and sound."

**The 1907 preface: Junker path vs. revolutionary path.** Writing after the 1905 Revolution, Lenin's Second-Edition Preface (July 1907) applied the mode-of-production framework prospectively to Russia's unresolved agrarian transition, setting out two "objectively possible" lines of development: the **"Junker" path** — the old landlord economy survives and slowly transforms itself into capitalist economy while retaining feudal features, under the political predominance of the liberal-monarchist bourgeoisie and landlords (the outcome he associated with the Cadets, Stolypin, and the June 1907 "coup d'état"); or the **revolutionary path** — landlordism destroyed outright by revolution, freeing small peasant farming to develop rapidly under proletarian and peasant-mass predominance, producing (in Lenin's view) the conditions most favorable "under commodity production" for the working class's later socialist task. This Junker-vs-revolutionary schema, read together with the "dual role of the peasantry" thesis of the same preface (see [[peasantry-as-class]]), is the germ of Lenin's later, more general framework for analyzing agrarian transition paths.

### Childe's livelihood revolutions (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

Without using Marx's full mode-of-production apparatus, [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] periodizes by **methods of securing a livelihood**: food-gathering; food-producing neolithic self-sufficiency; Bronze Age temple/palace surplus economy with metal monopoly; Iron Age money/slave/imperial forms; feudal/guild Europe (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed). Useful bridge between [[mode-of-production]] vocabulary and archaeological stage labels; do not equate Childe's stages with Marxian modes.
