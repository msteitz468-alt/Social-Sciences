# Aesthetic Disposition

Bourdieu's term in *[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]* for the cultivated capacity to consider **form rather than function** — not only in consecrated works of art but in unconsecrated cultural objects and even natural objects. It is the dispositional basis of the [[pure-gaze]] and one dimension of dominant-class [[habitus]].

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, Distinction, first-edition introduction and Part I.)* The aesthetic disposition is “the capacity to consider in and for themselves, as form rather than function, not only the works designated for such apprehension” but objects beyond the consecrated sphere. It presupposes distance from economic necessity and a break with ordinary practical attitudes toward the world.

## Semantic History

Earlier Bourdieuian empirical work on photography (*Un art moyen*, 1965) and museums (*L’Amour de l’art*, 1966) prepared the construct. *Distinction* (1979) generalizes it into a class-habitus dimension opposed to the popular subordination of form to function and to petit-bourgeois [[cultural-goodwill]]. Later cultural sociology often treats “disinterested” highbrow orientation as this disposition’s operational cousin, sometimes without Bourdieu’s necessity/distance mechanism.

## Operationalizations

In *Distinction*’s main survey: judgments of whether “a beautiful photograph” can be made of objects ranked from facile (first communion, sunset) to difficult (cabbages, car crash, pregnant woman, metal frame) — measuring capacity to autonomize representation. Rates rise with educational capital (e.g., metal-frame “beautiful photo”: 6% manual workers → 50% secondary/higher teachers, as reported). Composer identification and museum/gallery practices serve as related competence indicators. Factorial contrasts within classes oppose cultural-capital-rich to economic-capital-rich fractions.

## Applications

Organizes Part I’s aristocracy of culture; underwrites the homology between legitimate aesthetic judgment and lifestyle stylization (food manner, décor, body). Used comparatively in later cultural-capital research as the highbrow pole of taste space.

## Critiques and Limitations

Survey items capture stated judgments, not viewing practices; gender differences in disgust at “horrible” objects complicate a pure education story; later omnivore findings question whether the disposition still organizes exclusive highbrow rejection of popular forms. Always date-stamp to French 1960s–70s measurement.
