# Crisis of Representation

Diagnostic concept for late-twentieth-century uncertainty about how the human sciences can adequately describe social reality. In anthropology, named and centered by [[marcus-george-e|Marcus]] and [[fischer-michael-m-j|Fischer]] in *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique]]* (1986) and tightly linked to the *[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]* moment.

## Definition as Coined

Marcus and Fischer hold that a **crisis of representation** arises from uncertainty about adequate means of describing a rapidly changing, interdependent world — an uncertainty that suspends confidence in general, totalizing social theory and makes intimate description (especially ethnography) the live site of theoretical innovation.

## Semantic History

Broader humanities/social-science discussion of representation (poststructuralism, feminist standpoint, postcolonial critique, Said's *Orientalism*) precedes and surrounds the anthropological use. Within anthropology the phrase becomes a label for the 1980s experimental/textual moment; later writers sometimes treat "the crisis" as overstated or as already absorbed into ordinary reflexive method.

## Operationalizations

Not measured; used as a **periodizing and diagnostic** frame (what problems ethnography must solve; why form and content of monographs are experimental).

## Applications

Frames ACC Ch. 1; organizes [[writing-culture]] claims about authority and partial truths; routes debates about whether the crisis is epistemological, political, or both ([[writing-culture-debate]]). Retrospective teaching cases include popular ethnographies whose authority and harm were already disputed before the 1980s label — notably Turnbull's *[[turnbull-mountain-people-1972|The Mountain People]]* ([[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]; [[turnbull-mountain-people-study]]) and the Yanomami ethics cluster ([[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]]).

## Clifford's Historical-Critical Companion (1988)

*(Attributed to [[clifford-james|Clifford]], *[[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988|The Predicament of Culture]]*, 1988.)* Clifford's 1988 volume works the crisis of representation through cases (Griaule, ethnographic surrealism, collecting, Orientalism, Mashpee) under the organizing concept of **"the predicament of culture,"** glossed as "ethnographic modernity" — a condition of off-centeredness in which cultural identity is inescapably hybrid, relational, and historically contingent rather than rooted in a bounded, continuous tradition. Clifford argues identity/authenticity is not an essence but "a political, cultural invention, a local tactic," always staged relationally against an external, often dominating alternative. He explicitly rejects any single master narrative of global cultural change, arguing that ethnographic histories are "condemned to oscillate between two metanarratives: one of homogenization, the other of emergence; one of loss, the other of invention" — neither of which can claim the whole story.

## Marcus's Retrospect: Reflexivity as Ideology (1994/1998)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-george-e|Marcus]], "On Ideologies of Reflexivity," in *[[marcus-ethnography-thick-and-thin-1998|Ethnography Through Thick and Thin]]*.)* Looking back, Marcus described the 1980s critique as the convergence of three strands — confessional fieldwork accounts exposing the "messiness" of fieldwork, the colonial-context critique, and the hermeneutic critique of interpretation — and argued that what "postmodernism" practically meant in the social sciences is a **field of tolerated reflexive styles**: (1) the *null/subjectivist* confessional form (dead-ends in anthropology; weightier in feminism); (2) *sociological/Bourdieusian* reflexivity, valorized only as a research tool (Marcus: "tone-deaf" to subjective self-criticism; Bourdieu in turn publicly dismissed Marcus's brand as "nihilistic… narcissistic self-criticism" — a recorded polemic, see [[bourdieu-pierre]]); (3) *anthropological/locational* reflexivity — "reflexivity as a politics of location" (Myers's phrase), Marcus's preferred variant, generative of "messy texts"; (4) *feminist positioning*, most fully realized in Haraway's situated knowledges, though liable to formulaic identity-confession. Adopting Graham Watson's distinction, Marcus separated **essential** reflexivity (a property of all discourse) from this **ideological** reflexivity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue the "crisis" overstates literary diagnosis relative to institutional power, funding, and field access; others hold that scientific monographic standards never collapsed. Record positions on the debate page; do not promote "crisis" as wiki-voice fact about the whole discipline.
