# Cultural Configuration

## Definition as Coined

[[benedict-ruth|Benedict]]'s term (in *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*, Ch. III) for the patterned wholeness of a culture — the "more or less consistent pattern of thought and action" into which a culture's heterogeneous customs are drawn by characteristic purposes. A culture is "more than the sum of its traits," analogous to a **Gestalt** in which "the whole determines its parts… their very nature." The dominant purpose "selects from among the possible traits in the surrounding regions those which it can use, and discards those which it cannot."

## Semantic History

Central to [[configurationism]]; explicitly modelled on Gestalt psychology's part–whole holism, with Stern's "configuration of personality," Worringer's art-history, and Dilthey's *Weltanschauungen* as cited forerunners. Benedict's stronger versions equated the configuration with a psychiatric type ([[apollonian-and-dionysian|Apollonian/Dionysian]], "paranoid," "megalomaniac"). Later anthropology softened "configuration" toward looser notions of cultural coherence, **ethos**, and "thin" coherence — retaining the holism while dropping the whole-culture personality diagnosis (cf. [[thin-coherence-of-culture]]).

## Operationalizations

Identified interpretively: infer the dominant emotional/purposive "set" a culture expresses across ritual, kinship, economy, and emotion, then read each institution as its expression. Benedict conceded the **origin** of a configuration is underdetermined — archaeology cannot say why one small region came to express "a consistent and particular attitude toward existence"; she flagged this as a live gap rather than resolving it.

> **Not Kroeber's "growth configuration."** Benedict's *cultural* configuration (the synchronic personality-like patterning of a whole culture) is distinct from [[growth-configuration|A. L. Kroeber's "growth configuration"]] in *[[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944|Configurations of Culture Growth]]* (1944) — a diachronic rise–peak–decline profile of a single activity. Shared Boasian holism, different concepts.

## Applications

The analytic unit of *Patterns of Culture* — Benedict argued "the significant sociological unit… is not the institution but the cultural configuration," since the same institution (the Kula, a mourning rite) means different things in different wholes.

## Critiques and Limitations

Criticized for **over-integration** — treating internally diverse, conflicted societies as governed by a single psychological key, and handling residual non-conforming practices as contained exceptions. Benedict herself warned against forcing every culture onto "the Procrustean bed of some catchword characterization" and noted that some cultures are un-integrated ("scattered," e.g. certain interior British Columbia groups).
