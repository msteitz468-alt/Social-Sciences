# Reciprocity and Imbalance

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] posits two opposed tendencies running through social life: a **strain toward reciprocity** (individuals seek to discharge obligations and avoid social debt) and a **strain toward imbalance** (individuals also aspire to accumulate a favorable balance — a "credit" that elevates status). The central and generative claim is that **the two are inseparable across levels**: "balanced social states on one level produce and are produced by imbalanced states on another." Reciprocal attraction is established by imbalance in exchange; exchange is "balanced through imbalance of power"; legitimation reciprocates for the *just* exercise of power while opposition retaliates for the *unjust*, each restoring balance in one respect while creating new imbalances in others.

This cross-level dynamic is the engine of Blau's [[dialectic-of-social-change|dialectic]]: because "every social process restoring equilibrium engenders some new imbalances," social structures never settle into stable equilibrium but change through "intermittent reorganizations."

## Semantic History

The idea descends from [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] (reciprocity and gratitude as the cement of association — see [[faithfulness-and-gratitude]]) and from Mauss's cycle of obligation ([[gift-exchange]]), but Blau's distinctive move is to make imbalance *constitutive* rather than pathological. Against the equilibrium imagery of [[structural-functionalism]] (Parsons), where reciprocity stabilizes, Blau argues reciprocity *destabilizes* at adjacent levels — a deliberately anti-equilibrium reading of the same relational fact.

## Operationalizations

Not measured; a qualitative, directional principle. Its empirical traces are the phenomena it predicts: that unilateral service generates deference (imbalance of power from balance of benefit), that fair exercise of power generates legitimating approval, that exploitation generates opposition.

## Applications

Underlies [[differentiation-of-power]] (power *is* institutionalized imbalance of exchange), [[legitimation]] and [[opposition]] (reciprocation vs. retaliation for the exercise of power), and the concluding [[dialectic-of-social-change]]. It is Blau's answer to how a theory built on reciprocity can also explain conflict, hierarchy, and change: the same mechanism that balances one relation unbalances another.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The "levels" at which balance and imbalance trade off are not always sharply specified, making the principle hard to falsify — it can retrospectively locate an offsetting imbalance for any observed balance.
- Whether social systems in fact tend toward equilibrium (functionalism) or perpetual imbalance (Blau) is itself contested; Blau's own 1986 retrospective concedes the macro-level claims are the book's least secured.
