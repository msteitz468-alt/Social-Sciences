# Panaqa

## Definition as Coined

**Panaqa** (also *panaca* in Spanish orthography; OCR variants *panaga*) names the royal kin groups of the [[inca-empire|Inca]] capital. As of [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002), in the 1532 snapshot the apex of society was the emperor and family; below stood two classes of aristocratic Inca kin and one of honorary nobility; the most exalted aristocrats were **ten royal kin groups called *panaqa***. In theory, under the convention modern analysts call [[split-inheritance|split inheritance]], a new *panaqa* was created with each royal succession: the “most able” son became the new sovereign, while other descendants became custodians of the deceased ruler’s properties, usually under a brother’s leadership. Duties included perpetual veneration of the ancestor and care for his assets through a cult founded around his mummy (*mallki*), with personal golden “brother” idol (*wawqi*) and hair/nail statue (*bulto*) as additional surrogates.

Alias note: Spanish and secondary literature often write **panaca**; D'Altroy’s synthesis uses *panaqa* for the Quechua form.

## Semantic History

Colonial sources disagree on when and how the ten groups formed. D'Altroy records the **modal modern view** (as of 2002): a new *panaqa* added every royal generation via split inheritance. Alternatives include Betanzos’s tradition that all ten groups were present from the pre-imperial era; Sarmiento’s mapping of Lower Cuzco kin to the first five rulers and Upper to later kings; Pachakuti’s reorganization of existing groups into a dual design; and Rowe’s (1985a) generational shift of one Upper group into Lower to balance moieties. Rostworowski notes some royal kin may have fallen away; chronicles name extra groups called *panaqa* or claiming royal status. D'Altroy prefers a dynamic model—groups added or dislodged over generations, past revised to rationalize the organization of the moment—so that the arrangement reported to Spaniards was a **snapshot** frozen by invasion.

Elite kin formed matching halves **Upper (Hanan) and Lower (Hurin) Cuzco**; at Spanish arrival each half included five royal and five non-royal kin groups. Upper Cuzco *panaqa* were more powerful late, dominating civil, military, and religious hierarchies and holding grander estates; Pachakuti’s descendants (Hatun ayllu) and Thupa Inka Yupanki’s scion (Qhapaq ayllu) were especially powerful, with rivalry culminating in Qhapaq ayllu’s virtual eradication as the Spaniards arrived. Below the *panaqa* sat ten noble (non-royal) Inca kin groups considered distant relatives of royalty, and “Incas by Privilege”—ethnic groups of the Cuzco region in mythohistory.

## Operationalizations

**Political force.** Though the emperor was theoretically absolute, D'Altroy reports Cuzco’s royal *panaqa* were a force to be reckoned with: advisory court, many policy officials, influence over succession. Candidates and kin groups negotiated constantly; murderous succession intrigues gave kin leverage. Highest-ranking *panaqa* were those closest to the current emperor, not the most ancient.

**Mummy politics.** From a sanctified plane the dead emperor continued in ceremonial and political life. Pedro Pizarro: most of Cuzco’s people served the dead; mummies were daily brought to the main square in an age-ranked ring, “toasted” the living and each other with *chicha*, and received burned food offerings. Each ruler or *qoya* was served by male and female mediums; mummies attended only the most solemn festivals, sending brother-images for lesser occasions. D'Altroy’s synthesis: with assistants, royal mummies “ate, drank, urinated, visited one another, sat at councils, and judged weighty questions.” Waskhar is said to have had his mother officially marry his father’s mummy for genealogical legitimacy—plausible and maybe necessary in the Inca view of power.

**Spatial and ritual embedding.** Ten *panaqa* maintained Cuzco residences; Hanan Cuzco (northwest of main plazas; last five royal *panaqa*) was socially and topographically above Hurin. Year-long cycles of *panaqa* and affiliated *ayllu* venerated assigned [[zeqe-system|zeq'e]] shrines; many lines paired a royal *panaqa* with a non-royal *ayllu*. In Citua/Qoya Raymi purification, members of three royal *panaqa* and three non-royal *ayllu* (two of each in some quarters) relayed sacrificial ashes outward along the four roads. Royal families re-imagined the past for political gain and, at the end of the last dynastic war, destroyed Thupa Inka Yupanki’s mummy as part of an effort to eradicate history.

**Estates.** Deceased rulers’ estates normally passed to their *panaqa*; every province set aside lands for each ruler, with the densest elegant estates in the Sacred Valley. Yucay alone dedicated 2,400 men and families to Wayna Qhapaq’s holdings; Thupa is reported with 4,000–4,500 workers at each of three empire locations (Rostworowski via D'Altroy). Atawallpa’s Cajamarca ransom was gathered primarily from *panaqa* assets (his father’s assets not to be touched).

## Applications

- **Monarchy as negotiation:** Ideal absolute divine kingship tempered by aristocratic *panaqa* practice ([[cuzco]], [[inca-empire]]).
- **Complementary royal couple:** *Qoya* of pure Inca blood (sister or first cousin), counsel, wealth she could bequeath; maternal *panaqa* affiliation shaped Atawallpa (Hatun ayllu) vs. Waskhar (Qhapaq ayllu).
- **Succession crises:** Contested transitions (Urqon displaced; Amaru Thupa set aside for Thupa; Wayna’s kin coup; final civil war); full-sister marriage as claimant-pool reduction.
- **Zeq'e and ideology:** Shrine-line duties, ancestor–Sun fusion (Punchao holding rulers’ incinerated organs), and colonial afterlife—Polo’s 1559 seizure of still-venerated royal mummies.

## Critiques and Limitations

**Diarchy minority view.** Some scholars support Zuidema’s proposition of two kings always (Upper and Lower Cuzco), reading *panaqa* names as status titles rather than genealogical kindreds. D'Altroy notes the diarchy remains a minority viewpoint because it elevates poorly attested figures and because one moiety leader usually ranked higher and spoke externally.

**Source partisanship.** Native historical projects conflicted by royal kin group (Betanzos for Pachakuti/Atawallpa; Probanza de Qhapaq Ayllu for Thupa; Quipucamayos for Wiraqocha)—flexible, kin-centered pasts hard to map onto European linear chronology.

**Estate inviolability.** *Panaqa* “ostensibly” controlled estates, but multi-phase construction at Ollantaytambo and inter-ruler building orders show design was not static. Waskhar’s land complaints and Atawallpa’s purge show structural tension and catastrophic vulnerability of the system.

**Collapse test.** After the empire disintegrated, only among Incas proper did royal mummies and idols retain potency for decades; Sun cult and worship of Mango Qhapaq’s descendants appeared as a recent veneer over local religions outside the core.
