# Cynical and Sincere Performance

## Definition as Coined

[[goffman-erving|Goffman]] (*[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, 1956) argued that when an individual plays a part he implicitly requests observers to take seriously the impression fostered — that matters are what they appear to be (attributed).

| Pole | Definition (Goffman, attributed) |
|---|---|
| **Sincere** | Performer is fully taken in by his own act; convinced the staged impression is the real reality |
| **Cynical** | Performer is not taken in; no belief in his own act and no ultimate concern with the audience's beliefs (may still perform for other ends) |

Cynicism is **not** only private gain: performers may delude for the audience's or community's good; audiences may **force** delusion (placebos; customers demanding to hear the shoe size they want). Movement along the continuum has securities at each pole; **careers of faith** may cycle belief ↔ cynicism (Shetland hotel managers; army recruits; professional insulation). Transitional mixtures (Kroeber's "pious fraud" shamanism) are allowed.

Park on person-as-mask is cited: the mask of the self we would like to be can become second nature and "truer self" (attributed via Goffman).

Dramaturgical **loyalty** requires being taken in *enough* not to sound hollow; **discipline** requires not being carried away by one's own show — a practical middle between poles.

## Semantic History

Often vulgarized as "Goffman says everyone is fake." Primary text is a continuum with forced sincerity/cynicism and moral-request structure of performances.

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic assessment of belief in occupational ideologies; not a two-item survey without loss.

## Applications

Service work, professions, confidence games, political performance. Links: [[impression-management]], [[goffman-presentation-of-self-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Binary labels mislead; "sincere" is not authenticity outside social process. Gouldner's "soul-selling" reading treats cynicism as the program's center of gravity — a position, not Goffman's self-description.
