## Definition as Coined

Simmel defined the stranger not as "the wanderer who comes today and goes tomorrow" but as "the person who comes today and stays tomorrow" — "the potential wanderer" who is spatially fixed within a group yet has not organically belonged to it from the start, and who imports qualities that cannot originate from the group itself. The stranger's formal position is "the unity of nearness and remoteness": in relation to him, "distance means that he, who is close by, is far, and strangeness means that he, who also is far, is actually near." Simmel is careful to distinguish this from mere non-relation — the inhabitants of Sirius, he notes, are not strangers in any sociologically relevant sense, since "they do not exist for us at all; they are beyond far and near." The stranger, by contrast, is "an element of the group itself," like "the poor and like sundry 'inner enemies.'"

From this formal position Simmel derives several linked traits, each attributed to him as theoretical argument rather than established finding: (1) the historical association of the stranger with **trade** — since a closed economy needs no intermediary for goods produced within the circle, the trader who supplies what originates outside it must be a stranger, a role Simmel illustrates with "the classical example... the history of European Jews"; (2) **objectivity**, which Simmel defines not as passive detachment but as "a particular structure composed of distance and nearness, indifference and involvement" — hence the practice of Italian city-states calling judges from outside, since "no native was free from entanglement in family and party interests"; (3) the stranger's tendency to receive unusual **confidences**, "openness... which sometimes have the character of a confessional," precisely because of this distance; (4) a form of relation based on abstract, general commonalities (nationality, occupation, "generally human" traits) rather than the specific, incomparable commonalities that bind more organically connected persons — which Simmel argues can even intrude, as a "trace of strangeness," into the most intimate relationships once partners recognize their bond is not wholly unique.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Simmel argues the type from formal structural position (spatial fixity plus non-organic origin) rather than from measured social distance; illustrated with historical/institutional examples — the fixed head-tax (*Beede*) levied on medieval Jews in Frankfurt regardless of fluctuating individual wealth, contrasted with Christian citizens' fortune-indexed tax, is offered as evidence that "the Jew had his social position as a Jew, not as the individual bearer of certain objective contents."

## Applications

Applied by Simmel to trade and finance as historically stranger-occupied spheres; to the sociology of domination (outside judges as more "objective" adjudicators); to confession and disclosure patterns; and to a general theory of how relationships combine general (diluting, distancing) and specific (binding) commonalities — the stranger being the limiting case where only general commonalities operate.

## Critiques and Limitations

The account is a formal-sociological sketch derived from illustrative historical cases (medieval Jewish taxation, Italian judicial practice, Greek/Barbarian relations) rather than systematic comparative or ethnographic evidence; Simmel explicitly disclaims exhaustiveness ("the following statements... are by no means intended as exhaustive"). The heavy reliance on the European-Jewish trader example as paradigmatic reflects Simmel's own early-20th-century German-Jewish context and should be read as historically and positionally situated rather than a universal sociological law.
