# Internal System

## Definition as Coined

In *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]* (1950), [[homans-george-c|Homans]]'s **internal system** is the organization of group life that develops from the relationships among members themselves — the elaboration of [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, and sentiment]] *beyond* the requirements of the [[external-system|external system]]. It includes the group "as a whole" (solidarity, common social activity, shared sentiments toward the group) and **differentiation within the group** (cliques, rank orders, leadership, personal liking and antagonism) (Chs. 5–6 and following).

Homans's central hypotheses concern **mutual dependence** among the three elements inside this internal elaboration: e.g., more frequent interaction tends to be associated with more positive sentiment (liking), higher rank with more interactions originated, and so on — always as inductive hypotheses illustrated by cases, not as proven laws.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Homans 1950 | Internal system as the main site of mutual dependence hypotheses |
| Informal organization literature (Barnard, Mayo tradition) | Close cousin: informal structure alongside formal (external) requirements |
| Later Homans | Internal system remains the home ground of group dynamics; exchange theory re-explains some regularities |
| Contrast | Not "internalization" in the Berger–Luckmann sense — different concept family |

## Operationalizations

- Clique maps and friendship choices (Bank Wiring; women's association matrix example).
- Rank orders from interaction origination and deference (Norton Street leadership).
- Social activities (games, conversation, mutual help) not required by the formal task.

## Applications

- Why output restriction and "a fair day's work" emerge as **group** norms, not only company rules.
- Whyte's bowling–rank relationship as internal-system rank protecting itself.
- Leader as person at the center of interaction and sentiment networks (Ch. 8).
- Social control as distribution of activities/interactions/sentiments that keep behavior near norms (Ch. 11).

### Rank and leadership (Homans)

**Social rank** (evaluation of the person) is distinguished from broader **status**. Leadership is defined interactionally: the leader is the person of highest rank who originates action for the group, maintains widest useful contacts, and is the focus of positive sentiment — “least free” because most constrained by the need to live the norms (Bank Wiring’s Taylor; Norton Street’s Doc). Internal system **reacts on** the external system (e.g., output restriction protecting the group against rate-cutting fears).

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Beyond external requirements" is a residual definition — depends on how thickly one describes the environment.
- Mutual-dependence hypotheses are correlational illustrations from few cases.
- Thin on conflict and power except where rank and control chapters address them; conflict case (Electrical Equipment Company) stresses system strain.
