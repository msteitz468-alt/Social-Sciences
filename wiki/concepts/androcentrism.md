# Androcentrism

**Male-centered bias** in the production of knowledge — models, narratives, methods, and professional practice that take male experience as default or universal. In archaeology, a load-bearing diagnostic of [[feminist-archaeology|feminist / gender archaeology]] from Conkey & Spector 1984 and *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (1991) through [[gilchrist-roberta|Gilchrist]]’s 1999 synthesis.

## Definition as Coined

[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gilchrist (1999)]] glossary: androcentrism is “the tendency to privilege male-biased, or male-centred, values and assumptions.” Second-wave feminist scholars applied the diagnosis across history, anthropology, primatology, and the natural sciences (Kelly-Gadol; Rosaldo & Lamphere; Haraway; Keller — as Gilchrist surveys). In the Engendering program, androcentrism structures hypothesis formulation so that prehistory remains “largely unchallenged male territory.”

## Semantic History

Second-wave critiques identified androcentrism as root of women’s scholarly invisibility. Early gender archaeology answered with **visibility** of women. Gilchrist (1999) periodizes a further shift: once androcentrism critique and equality of recognition are established, third-wave agendas turn to **difference**, masculinity, and the instability of sex/gender categories — without discarding the androcentrism diagnostic. Professional androcentrism (who digs, who gets fieldwork grants, which specialisms are gendered) remains part of the object (Gero; Gilchrist ch.2).

## Operationalizations

- **Narrative:** Man the Hunter, tool-maker-as-male, castle-as-warrior-bastion, architect-magician art histories ([[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]]; Handsman on Lepenski Vir in Engendering).
- **Method:** [[gender-attribution]] gatekeeping; de-gendering of women’s labour (shellfishing, ground stone, cooking).
- **Practice:** demographics and funding (Gero lithics/palaeobotany/fieldwork tracks; “archaeological housework”).
- **Analogy:** ethnographic sex-role projection into deep prehistory (Gilchrist ch.3).

## Applications

*Engendering* cases ([[genderlithics]], households-with-faces, Shell Mound Archaic, Lepenski Vir exhibit). Gilchrist ch.2–3 reviews evolutionary and prestige models as androcentric defaults; ch.6 contests military-only personification of the medieval castle ([[contested-garden]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics of feminist archaeology sometimes treat androcentrism diagnosis as political rather than epistemic; [[wylie-alison|Wylie]] and Gilchrist answer that gender research can meet scientific standards. Risk of mirror-image essentialism if “women” replace “men” as unexamined universal — Gilchrist’s third-wave difference and multi-gender analyses are the control she recommends.
