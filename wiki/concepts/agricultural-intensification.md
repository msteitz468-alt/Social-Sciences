# Agricultural Intensification

## Definition as Coined

**Agricultural intensification** is the process of raising production by applying more
labor, capital, or organizational effort **per unit of land** (or by more intensive
cropping cycles), rather than only by expanding the cultivated area. In Oceanic
archaeology as developed by [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]]
(*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*,
1984, Ch. 7), intensification — wet taro irrigation, dryfield systems, arboriculture,
aquaculture, storage — is tightly coupled to the political economy of chieftainship and to
demographic and environmental pressure.

Kirch defines **development of production systems** as encompassing three components:
(1) **adaptation** to environmental conditions; (2) **expansion** (more land cleared, herds
enlarged); (3) **intensification** — increased labour input (permanent facilities or daily
labour) for relative yield increase. Intensification characterizes all Polynesian sequences
and was, in Sahlins’s phrase Kirch adopts, “intensification of domestic production by
political means and for public purposes” (Sahlins 1972:148).

**Not the same as** Mintz’s cultural [[extensification-and-intensification|intensification/
extensification]] of consumption meanings (*Sweetness and Power*); that is a different
concept sharing a word.

## Semantic History

Classic land-use economics (notably Ester Boserup’s population-driven intensification
thesis) entered archaeology and economic anthropology mid-century. Kirch applies a
Polynesian comparative version: “the wet and the dry,” production for subsistence vs.
social/political demand, a formal **production-function** model of surplus, and West
Hawaiʻi as a detailed dryfield case. Debates continue over whether intensification is
driven primarily by population, by chiefly demand, or by both — Kirch’s 1984 position
rejects single-factor determinism.

## Operationalizations

*(Attributed — Kirch 1984 Ch. 7 and related chapters.)*

### Production function and surplus

Kirch (drawing on Sachs and Friedman) models yield **Y = V + S**, where **V** is household
subsistence and **S** is surplus financing the public/political economy (especially chiefly
actions) — not a “superfluous” residue that automatically causes stratification. With
growing population, positive dS/dt and dy/dL are required. The production function has
critical regions where surplus growth falls, then surplus declines relative to yield, then
Y = V. **Intensification** (irrigation, fishponds, dryfield permanent facilities) shifts the
curve to a new technology T₂; environmental degradation lowers the function; hazards can
suddenly cut yield/surplus.

Kirch’s key causal claim: **proximate** cause of intensification frequently lies in prestige
or public-economy demands; **population growth** is an **ultimate** rather than proximate
cause. Competitive chiefs tend to raise the S/V ratio over time absent limiting conditions.
Against earlier Sahlins (1958), Kirch follows later Sahlins: chieftainship **generates**
surplus rather than merely resulting from it — “Leadership continually generates domestic
surplus.”

### First-fruits → tax

Ancestral surplus appropriation was likely sacrificial **first-fruits** to the chief as
divine exemplar (APS baseline; Tikopia *muakai* “foremost foods” recycled in chiefly
exchange — Firth). As chieftainship expanded, first-fruits became systems of **regularized
taxation** still in ritual guise — Hawaiian *makahiki* (Lono cult) and Tongan *‘inasi* as
peaks — with corvée formalized especially in Hawaiʻi. Goldman frames the shift from
voluntary mutual honoring to compulsory unequal reciprocity. In less stratified societies
most surplus returned via redistribution/feasts asserting rank and [[mana]]; with increased
stratification the proportion redistributed decreased as surplus supported chiefly
establishments, public works, and craft specialists.

### Wet vs dry

Following Jacques Barrau (1965a), Kirch holds Oceanic agricultures incorporate a fundamental
**wet/dry dichotomy** in crops, edaphic-hydrologic modifications, and ritual/symbolism —
rooted in taro (*Colocasia*, favours inundation/pondfields) vs greater yam (*Dioscorea
alata*, well-drained soils/mounding); sweet potato further strengthened the dry half
especially in East Polynesia. Hawaiian folk agronomy: “of two kinds is the land, the dry
and the wet.”

**Taro water-control categories** (as of Kirch 1984):
1. Atoll pits tapping Ghyben-Herzberg aquifer with organic mulch;
2. Drained raised garden beds in swampy coastal alluvium;
3. True pondfields/terraces irrigated from springs/streams (four hydrological types by
   ditch/spring complexity).

Irrigated taro yields ~30–60 metric tons/ha — considerably exceeding even intensive dryfield
systems. Literature overemphasized irrigation; Kirch argues intensive **dryfield** systems
had **equal significance**.

### Archaeological and ethnohistoric signatures

Irrigation complexes, terraces, field walls, fishponds, tool assemblages, settlement
encroachment onto marginal lands; ethnohistoric labour regimes (corvée, first-fruits,
chiefly plantations); *ma/*masi* pit-ensilage of breadfruit (PPN reconstructable; Yen:
intensification of production whose famine distribution passes into chiefly hands).

## Applications

### Sequences and trends (Kirch 1984)

- **Two-phase model:** (1) adaptation — reassortment on transfer, local constraint
  adjustment, heavy wild exploitation buffering colonizers; local emphases (arboriculture,
  irrigation, dryfield) established; (2) expansion and intensification — form of
  intensification reflects initial adaptation; process understood via demography, social
  relations, and political change.
- **Five parallel trends** (from Tikopia and Marquesas cases): colonization-phase heavy wild
  exploitation; subsequent agricultural expansion and animal husbandry; domestic animals
  never fully replace colonization-phase wild protein; ecosystem successional “regression”
  under management/degradation; later intensification (e.g. breadfruit pit storage) — most
  significant for evolutionary analysis of [[chiefdom]]s.
- **Tikopia:** shifting cultivation expansion then multi-storeyed orchard intensification;
  pig elimination under human–pig competition at high density.
- **West Hawaiʻi dryland systems** (North Kohala/Lapakahi, Lalamilo–Waimea, Kona): hearth of
  the powerful ‘Umi–Liloa line including Kamehameha; permanent field units with stone/earthen
  borders (“modified integral swidden” — Rosendahl); peak development c. AD 1600–1800
  matching peak population growth (1984 chronology); lateral expansion at limits by late
  1600s; production function argued near Region II–III (surplus diminishing relative to
  yield) — explaining expansionist warfare and expansion–rebellion–contraction cycles.
  Dryland labour intensity greater than western-island irrigation (women farmed on
  Maui/Hawaiʻi Island but not westerly islands — Kamakau as cited).
- **Hawaiian irrigation:** large systems limited until population expansion after ~AD 1200;
  Anahulu large pondfields linked to Kamehameha’s 1795 conquest surplus demands; *koʻele*
  fields for chiefs/konohiki worked by corvée (~1/3 of Halawa taro fields under
  konohiki/chiefs).
- **Fishponds, craft specialization, pig ritual demands** (e.g. *luakini* consecration up to
  800 hogs — Malo) as intensified production components under chiefly control.

### Reject simple hydraulic determinism

Kirch accepts Earle’s rejection of **Wittfogel-style necessity** of centralized management
for Hawaiian irrigation scale/complexity, but holds water allocation still provided
opportunity for chiefly control — linguistic (*wai*, *waiwai* “wealth,” *kanawai* “law”
literally “pertaining to water”), dry-season flow shortfalls (Halawa), ditch layouts
reflecting social relations. Political competition funded by commoner goods/services drove
intensification (Hommon/Earle); Kirch moderates Earle’s dismissal of demography: population
change mattered, but competition was a significant driving force — possibly more intense in
marginal leeward West Hawaiʻi than in Earle’s windward valleys (echoing Sahlins on cultural
inversion of natural ecology).

Population pressure **alone** is an inadequate cause: it ignores cultural population
regulation and social relations of production. Hierarchical surplus demands for chiefly
politics mattered; intensified production itself created new opportunities for political
control (water, ritual timing, scarce-resource distribution). Island circumscription and
finite arable land set limits — dryfield systems hit limits and degradation risk faster than
water-control systems.

### Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979 — Basin of Mexico, "terracing is a process, not an invention"

*(Attributed — [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]], *The Basin of
Mexico*, 1979.)* The authors state their Basin agricultural-history model "is derived
essentially from [[population-pressure|Boserup]] and assumes a process of
increasingly more intensive land use as the population increases," explicitly
adopting Boserup's reversal of the evolutionist narrative that intensification is
progress — instead, extensive systems have a lower labor input per unit output than
intensive ones, and Boserup's colonization sequence runs from extensive agriculture,
through fissioning into unsettled land as population grows, to shortened fallow once
fissioning is foreclosed, and finally to full intensive agriculture. On this basis
Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) characterize terracing, permanent and floodwater
irrigation, and drainage systems not as inventions adopted once and for all, but as
forced, incremental responses to land pressure: "terracing... is a process, not an
invention... only resorted to when land availability is reduced to the degree that
the fallow cycle must be shortened." They treat the appearance of ground basalt hoes
in the archaeological record the same way — as an intensification indicator rather
than a technological innovation for its own sake — noting hoes appear earliest in the
already densely settled southern Basin (First Intermediate Phase Two) and only much
later, in the Middle Horizon and after, in the sparser north, a distribution they read
as tracking the same population-pressure gradient. See also [[carrying-capacity]] for
the Logan and Sanders (1976) fissioning/intensification thresholds (20–30% and
50–80% of capacity) this model relies on, and [[chinampa]] for the productivity gain
(~4× dry-farming yield) the authors attribute to the Basin's most intensive
technique.

### Mesopotamian intensification: seed economy, the seeder plough, and the barley/salinity debate (Potts 1997)

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]], Ch. III.)* In
southern [[mesopotamia-geography|Mesopotamia]] intensification centered on maximizing
yield-per-seed and yield-per-labor within an [[irrigation|irrigation]]-dependent,
salinity-constrained alluvium, rather than on expanding cultivated area.

**Barley dominance and the salinity debate.** Barley (Sum. *še*) so dominated southern
cereal cultivation that the term came to mean "grain" generally: an Ur III "seed-and-
fodder text" from Girsu (TuT 5, year Shulgi 47) records barley at over 98% of cereal
cultivated, versus 1.7% for emmer wheat and 0.15% for free-threshing wheat (Maekawa
1984:90). Jacobsen (1982) interpreted the rising barley:wheat ratio over time as a sign
of progressive soil salinization (see also the parallel Jacobsen–Adams thesis recorded on
[[mesopotamia-geography]]); Powell (1985) countered that barley's dominance may reflect
factors other than salt tolerance — it out-yields wheat generally (also attested in
Attica, 329/8 BC, where barley was 90.3% of cereal production) and ripens 2–3 weeks
earlier, avoiding rust. Butz (1979) notes a complicating wrinkle: barley's low irrigation
requirement paradoxically *raises* soil salinity over a growing season despite the crop's
own salt tolerance. This is recorded here as a live, unresolved position dispute, not
adjudicated in wiki voice.

**Fallow-cropping cycle.** LaPlaca and Powell (1990) identify a two-year cropping-fallow
cycle in pre-Sargonic Girsu agricultural texts — fields flooded/leached then
ploughed/sown if previously fallow; harvested then fallowed if cropped — matching the
structure of *The Farmer's Instructions* and identical to the 20th-century Iraqi
"Niren-Niren" system leaving 50% of fields fallow (Charles 1990). LaPlaca and Powell
interpret the flooding instruction that opens *The Farmer's Instructions* as primarily
for leaching accumulated salts and softening fallow soil; Waetzoldt (1990) offers an
alternative flood-control reading that Potts judges less contextually likely.

**The ard and the seeder plough.** The Mesopotamian plough was a shallow "ard" (scratch
plough), loosening only the upper 15–20 cm of topsoil without turning it — suited to arid
conditions since it avoids exposing the fragile B-horizon to erosion; David and Joan
Oates (1977) suggest ard use dates at least to the Samarran period. Ploughing with
draught animals produced roughly a 400% productivity increase over human-labor tilling
alone (Hruska 1984). The **seeding plough** (Sum. *gišnumun-gar*; Akk. *epinnu*),
attested from the Early Dynastic period onward and requiring a 2–3 person team (driver,
seed-dropper, animal-turner), is described by Potts as "arguably the most important piece
of technology ever developed in Mesopotamia." *The Farmer's Instructions* specifies 8
furrows per 6-m width (*nindan*), seed spaced every ~3.33 cm, at 1 *gin* (1/60 *sila*) of
seed per *nindan* — a rate that, projected over a standard 6.48-ha *bur* field, yields the
widely-attested cuneiform metrological constant of **240 *sila* per *bur*** (Powell 1985,
1984), though Maekawa (1990) shows the rate varied in practice at Umma/Girsu (8, 10, or
12 furrows per *nindan*, reaching 300 l/*bur* at ten furrows).

**Sowing rates and the Halstead resolution.** Ur III average barley yield was ~30 *gur*
(9000 l) per *bur* (≈1000 kg/ha; Maekawa 1984), with ratios up to 1:76 (seed:harvest)
attested in the pre-Sargonic era. Butz and Schröder (Butz 1983; Butz and Schröder 1985)
argued such yields were impossible without modern fertilization and proposed a multi-year
(e.g. 7-year) accounting cycle to explain them; Postgate (1984) countered there was no
evidence for such a cycle. **Paul Halstead** resolved the dispute by showing the
extraordinary yield *ratios* reflect unusually *low seed expenditure* — enabled by the
seeder plough's precision — rather than extraordinary absolute yields: Ur III seed rate
was ~32 kg/ha versus 50–128 kg/ha for modern broadcast-sown Iraqi barley, meaning only
25–64% of the modern seed rate was needed (Halstead 1990). Comparable savings (~50%) are
reported for the 19th-century British seed drill relative to broadcast sowing (Halstead
1990). This resolution is treated as settled among the cited authorities, not a live
dispute.

**Plough-team economics.** Draught oxen did not graze freely on the Iraqi steppe, so
their feed came out of land otherwise available for human food (Renger 1990). A
pre-Sargonic Girsu text (VS 14.184) records a seed:fodder ratio of 1:2 for 52.92 ha of
initial ploughing, described as unusually costly (Hruska 1984); Powell (1984) suggests a
more typical seed:feed:labor-ration ratio near 1:1, with a four-ox team receiving ~30
*sila* of feed daily. Cuneiform texts attest two-pair ox teams (paralleled in Laws of
Hammurapi §242, which sets higher rent for front oxen); comparative sub-Saharan African
data show a second ox pair slightly reduces team speed but extends the working day up to
20%, while a third pair gives diminishing returns. Ur III plough teams at Girsu covered
1.2–2.25 *iku* (0.43–0.81 ha)/day, far exceeding ethnographic ard-ploughing rates recorded
in Iran (~0.05 ha/day; Hruska 1990) — at that rate, ploughing what an Ur III team covered
in ~10 days would take an Iranian ard team ~130 days. Ur III state agriculture at Girsu
operated at large scale: one text (BM 18060, covering Shulgi 42–Amar-Sin 3) records
~22,917 ha/year under cultivation on average, with average Ur III field size (~36 ha)
roughly 4–10× larger than typical Old Akkadian-period fields (Liverani 1990).

**Legumes.** Cultivated legumes (lentil, garden pea, chickpea, broad bean, vetch, and,
from Neo-Assyrian times, alfalfa as fodder) served as nitrogen-fixing soil enrichers
integral to the fallow-rotation system and were used in the reclamation of salinized land
(Charles 1985). Species varied in tolerance: lentil (drought-tolerant but
moisture-sensitive), field pea (improved by irrigation), grass pea (*Lathyrus sativus*,
drought/waterlogging-resistant famine crop, though overconsumption causes lathyrism),
chickpea (salt tolerant, frost intolerant), and broad bean (least drought resistant,
benefiting most from irrigation).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Boserupian one-way sequences oversimplify; social production for prestige and ritual can
  drive intensification without Malthusian stress; ethnohistoric labour figures are often
  chiefly ideology.
- Kirch’s production-function regions and West Hawaiʻi chronology are **1984-era** models —
  volcanic-glass hydration dating and site-frequency curves have methodological caveats
  Kirch himself discusses; later work revises Hawaiian and East Polynesian chronologies.
- Keep claims attributed when single-author; do not promote political-competition primacy
  or hydraulic rejection to unattributed wiki voice beyond documented multi-author
  convergence.
- The Mesopotamian barley/salinity dispute (Jacobsen vs. Powell/Butz) remains open; do not
  assert either position as settled fact. The Halstead seed-economy resolution, by
  contrast, is treated by the cited literature as having settled the Butz/Postgate yield
  dispute.

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — Valley of Oaxaca small-scale irrigation and the market-buffering reading

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* Well/pot irrigation and check-dam/small-channel diversion were already in use in the Valley of Oaxaca by the San José phase (Flannery, Kirkby, Kirkby, and Williams 1967), requiring no central management or outside-household labor — a technology base the authors note was already largely in place across [[mesoamerica|Mesoamerica]] by the Early Formative. Beginning in Early [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] I, settlement shifted toward the agriculturally riskier piedmont near the new capital, where small-scale irrigation from intermittent streams was practiced, most pronounced within 18–20 km of Monte Albán.

The authors interpret this piedmont intensification as tied to **exchange opportunities** (trading surplus grain to the city) and to the "economic buffering" of market exchange during dry years — explicitly **not** simple population-pressure-driven land shortage, since ample unoccupied prime farmland (e.g., the Valle Grande) remained available at the time. This complements their broader critique of population-pressure prime-mover models (see [[population-pressure]], [[circumscription]]): Monte Albán I involved higher per-capita exaction rates than Rosario-phase San José Mogote, they argue, partly offset by these small-scale householder-built water-control systems, which regularized maize yields and, together with sunk investment in terraces and adobe houses, reduced incentives to out-migrate (Feinman and Nicholas 1992; Nicholas 1989).
