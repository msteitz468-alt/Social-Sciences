# Value (Simmel's relational theory)

> **Scope note.** This page treats **value** as developed in [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]'s *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|Philosophy of Money]]* (1900). Marx’s labour theory of value is on [[labor-theory-of-value]] (*[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital I]]*) — do **not** merge the two. Marginalist theories still await their own pages. Neither is the wiki’s settled “theory of value.”

## Definition as Coined

For Simmel, value is **not a property of objects**. "The value of objects, thoughts and events can never be inferred from their mere natural existence and content"; value is "an addition to the completely determined objective being, like light and shade, which are not inherent in it but come from a different source." Reality and value are "two different languages" through which the same content becomes intelligible; neither is derivable from the other.

Value is therefore "never a 'quality' of the objects, but a judgment upon them which remains inherent in the subject" — yet this subjectivity is not caprice: value is as fixed a fact of consciousness as reality itself. Its deepest root, Simmel argues, lies *beyond* the subject/object split.

## The genesis of value: distance and sacrifice

Simmel's central genetic claim is that value arises from **distance** between the desiring subject and the object: "value does not originate from the unbroken unity of the moment of enjoyment, but from the separation between the subject and the content of enjoyment." Objects become valuable precisely because they resist us — "objects are not difficult to acquire because they are valuable, but we call those objects valuable that resist our desire to possess them." Enjoyment consumes value; value is reinstated only as the object is again held at a distance.

This distance has two limits: too little (like air, immediately available) precludes value; too much (unattainability) dissolves desire into "a vague wish." Hence "a certain proportion between scarcity and non-scarcity, and not scarcity itself, is the condition of value," and — reversing the usual causal story — "objects are not hard to get because they are scarce, rather they are scarce because they are hard to get." **Sacrifice** is constitutive, not merely a price: "the sacrifice not only increases the value of the desired object but actually brings it about."

## Objectivity through exchange

Subjective valuations become **objective economic value** through [[exchange]]: "the relativity of valuation signifies its objectification." Utility alone cannot generate economic value — "there is originally in the world of practice no single value, any more than there is originally in the world of consciousness a number 'one'"; demand becomes economic only through comparison with the demand for something else, i.e. through the exchange relation. Objectivity is defined relationally: "the equation, objectivity = validity for subjects in general, finds its clearest justification in economic value," because exchange requires each party to recognize the object's worth *for the other*. Historically, this objectivity was first fixed by external social standardization (fixed rates, sacred forms of transaction) before individuals could bargain rates freely.

Simmel presses this against the **[[labor-theory-of-value|labour theory of value]]**: "labour power, too, enters the category of value only through the possibility and reality of exchange" — it cannot be an exchange-independent standard from which prices deviate. "Value is, so to speak, the epigone of price." ([[money-economy|Money]] is then "the autonomous expression of this relationship," value objectified as substance.) Marx’s counter-architecture (abstract labour, SNLT, [[commodity-fetishism]]) is developed in *Capital* I, not answered here.

## Semantic History and Contrasts

| Tradition | What fixes value |
|---|---|
| Simmel (1900) | A subjective judgment objectified through [[exchange]]; value = relation, not substance |
| Marginalism (Menger, Böhm-Bawerk) | Marginal utility; Simmel's account is subjective-relativist, close to this wing |
| Marx / Ricardo (labour theory) | Socially necessary labour time; Simmel argues this presupposes rather than explains exchange |

Frisby notes Simmel struggled to escape a "regressus in infinitum" and "circulus vitiosus" in grounding value (value of A in B and vice versa) and that Hans Brinkmann charged him with using "two concepts of value in an undifferentiated manner," weakening his critique of Marx.

## Operationalizations

Simmel's value is philosophical, not a measurement construct, but it becomes *quantitatively* legible in economic life: value is objectified through [[exchange]] and made measurable as **price**, and [[money-economy|money]] is "value turned into a substance." Simmel treats price as prior to value in practice ("value is… the epigone of price"), so the operational trace of value is the exchange ratio itself — how much of one good is given up for another — with money as the universal denominator. Non-economic values (aesthetic, moral) have no such common scale, which for Simmel is precisely what distinguishes economic value.

## Applications

- Grounds Simmel's theory of [[money-economy|money]] as reified [[exchange]] and the "autonomous expression" of the value relation.
- Underwrites his critique of the labour theory of value and his affinity with marginal-utility economics.
- Feeds the later cultural sociology of value and valuation (money's "moral depreciation" of objects, as Durkheim noted in his 1901 review).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The relational/relativist account risks circularity, which Simmel himself acknowledged.
- Its assimilation of economic value to a general philosophy of value (aesthetic, moral) is powerful but blurs the line between an economic theory and a metaphysics of relation.
- Simmel's supporting ethnographic contrasts (the "arbitrary" valuations of "primitive culture," children and "primitives" bracketed together) are period-typical 1900 evolutionist framings, recorded as attributed and dated, not as findings.

## Appadurai’s use of Simmel (1986)

[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]’s introduction to *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* takes Simmel’s first chapter as the best guide to economic value (judgment, distance, reciprocal sacrifice; exchange as source of value, scarcity as external manifestation) and redirects it toward **[[regimes-of-value]]** — conditions of circulation in space and time — and toward **politics** as the link between exchange and value *(Appadurai via Simmel)*. Do not merge Appadurai’s political-anthropological program into Simmel’s philosophy of money; they share the exchange-source thesis and then diverge.

Davenport (same volume) juxtaposes two kinds of value in the Eastern Solomons — material/economic vs mystical/spiritual — as an ethnographic dual register, not a Simmelian theory *(Davenport)*.
