## Definition as Coined

Weber defines charisma (§10) as "the personal quality that makes an individual seem extraordinary, a quality by virtue of which supernatural, superhuman, or at least exceptional powers or properties are attributed to the individual... rendering that individual a 'leader' (Führer)" (Ch.3, line 1275–1289). Weber notes the concept was originally applied to prophets, healers and legal sages, hunting-band leaders, and military heroes, and states it is conceptually indifferent to objective ethical evaluation: what matters sociologically is how followers judge the quality, not whether it is "true" or "good" by some external standard.

Charisma is validated, per Weber, by the ruled's recognition of "personal proof" (Bewährung), originally demonstrated through miracle. Weber is careful to distinguish this from popular sovereignty: genuine charisma is not *legitimated* by recognition but rather imposes an obligation on those "called" to acknowledge the duty owed; acceptance is described as a personal dedication born of enthusiasm, or of despair and hope, not conditional on mass approval in a plebiscitary sense. If proof fails, charismatic authority — "the grace of God" in its genuine sense — can simply vanish (Ch.3, line 1299–1328).

## Semantic History

Weber himself notes (Ch.3, line 218–223) that the term "charisma" ("gift of grace") is taken from ancient Christian terminology and was first clarified for the sociology of Christian religious organization by Rudolf Sohm's *Kirchenrecht*, with further elaboration by Karl Holl (*Enthusiasmus und Bußgewalt*). Weber's adoption of the term is thus itself an instance of a migrated concept: an emic theological category (a divinely bestowed gift of grace, in Pauline usage) repurposed as an etic sociological category applicable to prophets, war leaders, and — after routinization — hereditary monarchs and consecrated officials alike (see [[charismatic-authority]]).

## Analytic Use (Operationalizations)

Not quantified. Charisma is judged, within Weber's framework, entirely through the followers' recognition of personal proof, not by any external or observer-administered test; Weber states explicitly that "charisma can only be 'aroused' or 'put to the test,' not 'learned' or 'taught'" (Ch.3, line 1562–1598), which rules out any pedagogical or credentialing operationalization even under routinization (see below).

Pure charisma is, per Weber, "specifically disconnected from the economy" (wirtschaftsfremd) — a "calling" or "mission" that disdains economic exploitation of the gift of grace as income, even though Weber notes this ideal is "not always" honored in fact. Pure charismatic activity is provisioned by patronage (gifts, foundations, bribery, honoraria) or by begging, looting, or extortion; from a rational-economic standpoint, charismatic rule is typically "uneconomic" (Ch.3, line 1385–1408).

Weber describes charisma as "the great revolutionary force in eras bound up with tradition" — differing from "reason" as a revolutionary force in that reason's impact is external and intellectualizing, whereas charisma produces an inner transformation of dispositions born of suffering or enthusiasm (Ch.3, line 1423–1432).

### Routinization of Charisma

Weber's term for charisma's transformation into a permanent institution is Veralltäglichung — "routinisation," glossed by Tribe as "to reduce charisma to an everyday matter" (Ch.3, line 1433–1463). Genuine, ideal-typical charismatic rule exists, per Weber, only in its early emergent stage; once a community of believers, warriors, or disciples becomes permanent, it must become more traditional or more rational (legal), or both — driven by followers' interest in the community's continuation and, more strongly, by the administrative staff's interest in securing their own position on a permanent, materially secure everyday basis.

Weber catalogues six modes by which the succession question is typically resolved (Ch.3, line 1465–1561): (a) renewed search for a person bearing the right charismatic features (traditionalisation — Weber's illustration is the Dalai Lama); (b) revelation via oracle, lot, or divine judgement (legalisation); (c) designation of a successor by the incumbent bearer, acknowledged by the community; (d) designation by the charismatically-qualified staff, recognized by the community (not an "election" in the modern sense, but an obligatory identification of the "real" bearer); (e) hereditary charisma, transmitted by blood or kinship; (f) charisma of office, transmitted ritually (e.g., priestly charisma via anointment or laying on of hands), producing an "indelible spiritual character" independent of the office-holder's personal qualities. Routinisation is additionally driven by the staff's material interest — patronage and booty sustain followers only during a ruler's early triumphant phase, so the mass of disciples come to want a long-term livelihood from their "calling" (Ch.3, line 1562–1598).

## Applications

Weber's illustrations — the Dalai Lama (traditionalisation of succession), Roman magistracies/dictators (designation by incumbent), pre-bureaucratic Japan and the "lineage state" (hereditary charisma), priestly anointment and royal coronation (charisma of office) — are presented as instances of the ideal type, not established facts about those societies (Ch.3, line 1465–1620).

## Anthropological Reframing (Geertz, attributed)

In "Centers, Kings, and Charisma" (*[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983),
[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] argues that Weber's concept "suffers from an uncertainty of referent:
does it denote a cultural phenomenon or a psychological one?" and objects to the modern collapse
of charisma into mere personal magnetism ("Everyone from John Lindsay to Mick Jagger has been
called charismatic"). Following Edward Shils, Geertz relocates charisma in the relation between
individuals and "the **active centers of the social order**" — the points "where its leading
ideas come together with its leading institutions." His load-bearing redefinition: charisma "is
a sign, not of popular appeal or inventive craziness, but of being near the heart of things." So
charisma can arise "in science or art as readily as in religion or politics," and its
"flamboyant expressions tend to appear among people at some distance from the center... who want
very much to be closer" ("Heresy is as much a child of orthodoxy in politics as in religion").

Geertz's probe is "the inherent sacredness of sovereign power": at any complex society's center
sits "both a governing elite and a set of symbolic forms expressing the fact that it is in truth
governing." He analyzes the **royal progress** — rulers stamping territory "with ritual signs of
dominance" — across three cases with contrasting idioms: Elizabethan England (charisma built from
"virtue and allegory"; the cult of the imperial virgin), fourteenth-century Majapahit Java
(Hayam Wuruk; "splendor and hierarchy," the court as copy of the cosmos, the mandala of the
*Negarakertagama* — "analogy, not allegory"), and Alawite Morocco (Mulay Hasan; power symbolized
directly as personal potency and *baraka*, "movement and energy," the mobile court/*mehalla*).
His broader claim: even in democratic and revolutionary regimes "the ingenerate tendency of men
to anthropomorphize power" persists, and Weber's fully demystified "iron cage" is challenged by
the enduring charismatic figures of the twentieth century. (Geertz's cognate analytic concept,
the "exemplary center," is developed at length in *Negara: The Theatre State*, 1980, not here.)

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber's own framework flags a limitation: since pure charisma is defined by followers' subjective recognition of "personal proof," it resists any independent verification and can only be studied retrospectively, through its routinization or collapse. The concept's migration from a specific early-Christian theological vocabulary (Sohm) into a general sociological category has itself been a site of scholarly discussion about how much of the term's original religious weight survives its analytic generalization.
