# Legitimation (Berger & Luckmann)

## Definition as Coined

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (1966, Part Two §2):

> Legitimation as a process is best described as a ‘second-order’ objectivation of meaning. Legitimation produces new meanings that serve to integrate the meanings already attached to disparate institutional processes. … Legitimation ‘explains’ the institutional order by ascribing cognitive validity to its objectivated meanings. Legitimation justifies the institutional order by giving a normative dignity to its practical imperatives.

Knowledge, they argue, precedes values in legitimation: one must know “what is” before “what ought.”

**Distinct from** Weberian [[legitimate-domination]] / [[legitimate-order]] (belief in the validity of an order of *domination*). B&L legitimation is universe-maintenance of the institutional order as a whole, not only political Herrschaft.

## Semantic History

### Levels of legitimation (attributed)

1. **Incipient / linguistic** — simple vocabulary that objectivates the institutional order (“cousin,” kinship terms).
2. **Rudimentary theoretical** — proverbs, moral maxims, wise sayings.
3. **Explicit sectoral theories** — specialized legitimation of particular institutions (e.g. kinship theory).
4. **[[symbolic-universe|Symbolic universes]]** — bodies of theoretical tradition integrating provinces of meaning into a symbolic totality; highest level.

### Conceptual machineries of universe-maintenance

Mythology → theology → philosophy / science, with increasing theoretical sophistication and specialist roles. **Therapy** (keeping actual or potential deviants within the universe) and **nihilation** (conceptually liquidating everything outside the universe) are twin maintenance procedures.

### Social organization

“Says who?” — experts vs practitioners; monopolistic vs pluralistic situations; ideology as legitimation tied to power interests; intellectuals, sects, revolutions as carriers and challengers of universes.

## Operationalizations

Levels and machineries are analytic, not a survey scale. Later sociology of religion and cultural sociology often operationalize “plausibility” and worldview maintenance with interview and historical methods (outside this source).

## Applications

Religion and secular symbolic universes; ideology critique reframed; identity and biography under a canopy of meaning; death and anomie as problems for which universes provide nomic protection.

## Related sense: legitimation of power (Blau)

A distinct exchange-theoretic sense, not to be collapsed with the B&L meaning-legitimation above. In [[blau-peter-m|Blau]]'s *[[blau-exchange-power-1964|Exchange and Power]]* (1964), **legitimation** is the process by which mere [[differentiation-of-power|power]] becomes **authority**: the fair, moderate exercise of power earns the collective [[social-approval|approval]] of subordinates ("a secondary exchange, of [[fair-exchange|fairness]] for approval"), and this collective approval transforms power into legitimate authority. Blau's defining criterion of authority is that **compliance is enforced by the collectivity of subordinates itself**, not imposed by the superior — "compliance is voluntary for the collectivity, but social constraints make it compelling for the individual." He adds a cognitive-dissonance mechanism (subordinates who choose to comply inflate the value of compliance). This sense is closer to Weberian [[legitimate-domination]] than to B&L's universe-maintenance; it is developed on [[social-exchange-theory]] and [[differentiation-of-power]].

## Related sense: legitimation by founding analogy (Douglas)

[[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] (*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How Institutions Think]]*, 1986) supplied a cognitive mechanism for legitimation that she found missing from institutional economics (Arrow 1974 a partial exception): a convention becomes an institution only when a **naturalizing analogy** grounds its formal structure "in the physical world, or in the supernatural world, or in eternity, anywhere, so long as it is not seen as a socially contrived arrangement" — and "founding analogies have to be hidden" to work. Pressed for justification, an institution's legitimacy bottoms out in claimed fit with the nature of the universe, not mutual convenience. Compatible with, but developed independently of, the B&L levels above.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of functionalist reading (every idea “serves” order); B&L also stress conflict between universes, pluralism, and revolutionary challenge. Relation to Weberian legitimacy should not be collapsed — different objects (institutional meaning-order vs. domination vs. Blau's power-into-authority).
