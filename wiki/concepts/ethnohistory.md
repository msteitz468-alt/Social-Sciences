# Ethnohistory

## Definition as Coined

Ethnohistory is the reconstruction of the past of peoples whose history is not, or not mainly, recoverable from their own written records — combining [[oral-tradition|oral tradition]] with several **auxiliary disciplines**. In *[[vansina-oral-tradition-1965|Oral Tradition]]* (1961/1965), [[vansina-jan|Jan Vansina]] named the four disciplines "of greatest use to the historian of non-literate peoples" — **archaeology**, **cultural history** (diffusionist ethnology), **[[historical-linguistics|historical linguistics]]**, and **physical anthropology** — which "together with oral tradition" constitute "the resources used by the ethnohistorian, according to the views of members of the American school" (attributed).

## Semantic History

The term consolidated in mid-20th-century American anthropology (the journal *Ethnohistory* dates to 1954) around the study of Native American peoples through combined documentary, archaeological, and traditional evidence. Vansina imported and generalized the program for **Africa**, where it underwrote the founding of academic African history: proof that societies without writing nonetheless possess a documentable past. A crude precursor framing that Vansina records is **M. Herskovits's** division of evidence into "hard" materials (history and archaeology) and "soft" materials (oral tradition and ethnology) — a binary far coarser than this wiki's eight-class Evidence-Class Framework, but recognizably an attempt at the same weighting problem.

## Operationalizations

Ethnohistory in Vansina's hands is **independent convergence** across evidence classes: archaeology (which he judged "the most necessary and the most useful" auxiliary, and which can be led *to* sites by tradition, as at Lake Kisale); linguistics (genetic relationship, from which ethnic relationship can only *sometimes* be inferred — an early four-way non-identity caution); and physical anthropology (used cautiously — e.g., Bushongo/Kwa genetic distinctness read as supporting a tradition of separate origin). Vansina was sharply critical of the diffusionist "cultural history" method (age-area, Vienna-school culture strata) as largely speculative. See [[oral-tradition]] for the source-critical core.

## Applications

The African historiography Vansina helped found rests on ethnohistorical method; the Kuba, Rwanda, and Burundi reconstructions in *Oral Tradition* are its worked demonstrations (see [[kuba]]). The interdisciplinary model — oral tradition disciplined by archaeology, linguistics, and, latterly, archaeogenetics — remains the template for the deep history of non-literate regions.

## Critiques and Limitations

Ethnohistory inherits the reliability problems of each of its evidence classes: the distortion of oral tradition (see [[oral-tradition-reliability-debate]]), the muteness of material remains on language and ethnicity, the weak absolute dating of linguistics (glottochronology — flagged at [[historical-linguistics]]), and the standing danger of silently equating an archaeological culture, a language, a genetic population, and a self-conscious people.

## Trigger’s North American ethnohistory method (1985)

*(Attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* 1985, esp. Ch. 4 opening; densifies the North American/Eastern Woodlands program beside Vansina’s African formulation above.)*

### Not a parallel ethnocentric field

Trigger holds ethnohistory should label a **set of techniques** for studying native history, not a separate ethnocentric field walling off literate vs nonliterate societies. Ethnohistory “takes up where prehistoric archaeology leaves off” and covers the **same span** as White New World history — not a parallel track that leaves natives outside “real” history. Canadian historians, he argues, generally treated native people as “part of the setting for European activities,” while ethnologists mined the same texts only to reconstruct pre-contact cultures; systematic use for native American history awaited ethnohistory.

### Lineage

- **North American beginning:** A.G. Bailey’s *The Conflict of European and Eastern Algonkian Cultures, 1504–1700* (1937) — first to centre changing native responses to early European encroachment; influenced by Harold Innis and T.F. McIlwraith; neglected until 1969 Toronto reprint.
- **Historical ethnography** (Swanton, Speck, Fenton) enriched traditional-culture reconstruction via documents + archaeology + linguistics + ethnography, but for historical ethnography — not systematic post-contact culture change.
- **US acculturation tradition** (Redfield, Linton, Herskovits 1936; Linton 1940; Spicer 1961–62) fed ethnohistory; Linton’s distinction between **directed** and **nondirected** contact (amount of European power to change native behaviour) was the period’s chief theoretical innovation; 1950s land-claims work intensified archival ethnohistory.
- Journal *Ethnohistory* since 1953; Trigger notes a minority of anthropologists define ethnohistory as how nonliterate peoples **perceive** their own history (Gubser, Dening, Blanchard) rather than documentary/oral reconstruction of their past.

### Source criticism and interest groups

- **Oral traditions** require source criticism (derivation; alternative versions). Tribal societies often treat “history” as mythical charters validating current relations; Parker found Iroquois forgot palisaded villages/longhouses within ~200 years; Clarke’s Wyandot history lacked awareness of former numbers/ways of life — yet Copway’s Ojibwa traditions preserved late-seventeenth-century data later corroborated by documents. Independent verification needed.
- **European observers** often recorded behaviour accurately but ascribed ethnocentric motives; factual descriptions can be too brief or literary to serve as anthropological documents. Ethnohistorians must master historians’ skills **and** interpret alien-collected data about non-Western peoples.
- Work **systematically backward** through records: ethnographic knowledge of descendants/related tribes evaluates fragmentary old sources, but recent cultures mislead for earlier stages. Culture patterns frame decisions but do not dictate them — same culture, opposed goals by interest/position.
- **Interest groups** (Trigger’s analytical device, developed in *Children of Aataentsic*): specific groupings sharing common goals in concrete historical situations (corporate or historian’s constructs) — within tribes: clan, sex, age, trade, war, community, faction; Europeans: traders, officials, farmers, religious orders. Interests can cut across ethnic lines. Class and interest group are not congruent. In Iroquoian society, frequent opposition between **war chiefs** and **peace chiefs** (one of each per localized clan) — the two native interest groups most visible to Europeans.

### Archaeology + documents + ethnography

Archaeology once consumed ethnohistorical data for late prehistoric interpretation; now ethnohistorians need archaeology for late prehistoric/protohistoric baselines and poorly described early historical material culture — archaeological findings are “essential for ethnohistorical studies.” Combination of archaeological and ethnohistorical data has a “synergistic effect,” permitting conclusions neither source alone could yield. Reliable archaeological data are preferable to unverified ethnographic speculation when setting early ethnographic information in historical context — the anti-[[ethnographic-present]] methodological corollary of Trigger’s Ch. 3 critique.

Worked demonstration in *Natives and Newcomers*: St Lawrence lowlands Iroquoians ([[huron]], [[petun]], [[neutral-iroquoians]], [[iroquois]]) across Chapters 4–5.

## *The Children of Aataentsic* (1976/1987) — the fuller method statement

*(Attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]*, ch. 1, "Ethnohistory and the Role of Anthropology"; the earlier, more expansive version of the *Natives and Newcomers* statement above — cite this source page for the book-length demonstration.)*

Trigger states ethnohistory's "precise objectives... are still rather uncertain": some anthropologists take it to embrace all historical approaches in anthropology, but its principal interest is "using historical documents and oral traditions to study the history of nonliterate peoples." He flags the term itself as potentially perpetuating an "invidious distinction" between primitive and complex societies, justified mainly by the greater methodological complexity of studying nonliterate, small-scale societies.

**Dual competence, or dilettantism.** Ethnohistory requires genuine interdisciplinary competence: anthropological knowledge of a people's beliefs and values is needed to evaluate biased European-authored sources, while historiographical technique (source criticism, manuscript-variant analysis, assessment of translator/editor bias) is needed to avoid "the common anthropological error of assuming that a written document necessarily means what it says." Lacking either skill set, Trigger warns, "an ethnohistorian will remain a dilettante."

**Four contributions of anthropology**, per Trigger: (1) precise identification of terms (e.g., "wampum" not "porcelain," "longhouse" not "cabin"); (2) bias-correction via better terms ("headman"/"spirit" rather than "chief"/"devil"); (3) **ethnographic analogy** — checking historical accounts against later, better-documented stages of the same or closely related cultures, a technique Trigger himself flags as risk-laden (he elsewhere criticizes fellow Iroquoianists for treating pre-1650 Huron culture as simply an earlier stage of general Iroquoian development, given documented Huron/Iroquois differences in burial customs, torture patterns, kinship terminology, and pottery/house/fortification styles); (4) cross-cultural comparative data from other contact situations worldwide (Africa, New Guinea, the Canadian Arctic), with the acknowledged risk of "erroneous selection of theories" distorting the evidence to fit.

**Triangulation.** Where independent European observers recorded the same events from different vantage points — French versus Dutch accounts, for instance — Trigger reads them against one another "a process analogous to surveyors' triangulation," approximating an Indian perspective even absent direct Indian testimony.

**Interest groups**, Trigger's central analytical device in this book: groupings that "emerge as a result of common interests in real historical situations," sometimes with recognized corporate existence, sometimes constructs of the historian, membership requiring only implicitly shared goals and mutual support in common action — not the abstract comparative categories of sociologists or ethnologists. He traces the approach to the sociological theorizing of George C. Homans (rendered "Romans" in some printings — likely an OCR/typesetting artifact) and to Jean-Paul Sartre's later historical theory (1965), citing Gustave Lanctot's *Canada and the American Revolution* (1967) as a strong prior exemplar. He explicitly rejects both an individual-biographical approach (Indian documentation is too thin for it) and gross structural/modal-personality analysis as inadequate, offering interest-group analysis as the "middle course." He analogizes the evidentiary position of Indians to that of European lower classes and peasantries — records produced by a small literate class with no personal experience of their subjects' lives — and warns that, uncorrected, ethnohistory risks becoming "nothing more than a restudy of European colonization."

**Against nomothetic ethnohistory.** Trigger criticizes ethnohistorical exemplars that subordinate historical narrative to generalization about "cultural processes" — naming E. H. Spicer's *Cycles of Conquest* (1962, which he nonetheless calls "the most important published to date"), Harold Hickerson's work on the Ojibwa fur trade, and A. F. C. Wallace's history of the Seneca — quoting Hickerson's (1970:7) description of ethnohistory as using "historiographical methods to lay a foundation for the formulation of general laws: in a word, ideographic means to nomothetic ends." Trigger positions his own book in the opposite camp: valuing the detailed explanation of one specific historical situation as an end in itself.

**Source criticism, worked examples.** Trigger defends Gabriel Sagard's *Grand Voyage* (1632) and *Histoire du Canada* (1636) as broadly reliable — self-aggrandizing and given to borrowed anecdotes (a Sagard story about a young Huron man cooking French-style closely parallels one Marc Lescarbot told of a young Micmac; Trigger judges it "stolen," though "faithfully" representing Huron sentiment) but not the "untutored, credulous" figure the 18th-century Jesuit historian Charlevoix made him out to be — an explicit rebuttal of Charlevoix's polemical dismissal (dispute_type: source-reliability). He treats the traditional account of missionary Nicolas Viel's 1625 "martyrdom" (murdered by Huron canoe companions, per Le Clercq 1691) as a later fabrication: contemporary sources (Sagard, the Jesuit Relations) describe only a canoe-accident drowning, and the murder claim first surfaces in Paul Le Jeune (1634) and Brébeuf (1636), apparently relayed by the Kichesipirini with a motive (discouraging the Jesuits from residing with a rival village) — a further source-reliability case for how a documented death is elaborated into an origin-legend of martyrdom.
