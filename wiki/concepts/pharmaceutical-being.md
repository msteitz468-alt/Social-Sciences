# Pharmaceutical being

## Definition as Coined

In *[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]*, [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]] analyzes Catarina and others as
**pharmaceutical beings** (attributed): persons whose lives are shaped by the circulation and
interruption of medications — psychiatric drugs, pain relief, whatever the informal pharmacy
can supply — more than by continuous medical care or family support. Hospital files show Catarina "becoming a pharmaceutical double of herself": "a pharmaceutically mediated set of signs and symptoms was reported and treated. There was no human voice… and no specific physical condition was explored" (attributed, Biehl 2005). The pharmacy becomes a
site of dependence, desire, and control inside the **[[zone-of-social-abandonment]]**.

## Semantic History

- Coined in the contemporary medical-anthropology moment that also produced "pharmaceutical
  citizenship," the anthropology of pharmaceuticals, and critical work on **[[medicalization]]**.
- Distinct from medicalization alone: the point is not only that social problems become
  diseases, but that *being itself* is reorganized around drug access and dosage in places
  where diagnosis and care have already failed. Biehl tracks "pharmaceuticalization of care" and families working as "proxy-psychiatrists" (attributed).
- Drug names enter selfhood: Catarina embeds Akineton/biperiden in the self-name "Catkini"; dictionary lines include "Desire is pharmaceutical / It is not good for the circus" (Catarina via Biehl).
- Related later literature (not yet ingested) on pharmaceuticalization and biological
  citizenship may refine or rename the construct — date-stamp uses to *Vita* 2005 until then.

## Operationalizations

Ethnographic: medication lists and interruptions (haloperidol, chlorpromazine, prometazine, biperiden and overlapping side-effect profiles); dependence on volunteer "nurses" who control
the pharmacy; Catarina's notebook entries about drugs, body, and desire; archive traces of
polypharmacy across public psychiatry; discharge "into the world, to medicate herself" (São Paulo Hospital, Jan 1995).

## Applications

- *[[biehl-vita-study|Vita]]* Part Three ("Pharmaceutical Being") and related chapters on care
  and exclusion; geriatric-house routine of doping new residents for family presentation (city officials via Biehl).
- Biehl: medication is "the medium through which the household, the model service, and state policies come together" — writing Catarina as "well" in a home that did not exist (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Single-case density; risk of over-extending "being" language from one life.
- Does not replace clinical or epidemiological accounts of drug effects — it names a *social*
  form of life under intermittent pharmacotherapy.
- Must not be read as denying organic disease: the later MJD diagnosis reopens historicity without canceling the pharmaceutical career (see [[biehl-vita-study]]).
