# Paradox of Voter Turnout

## Definition as Coined

The **paradox of voter turnout** (also "the paradox of not voting") is the rational
choice puzzle that self-interested instrumental voters should almost never vote,
because in any large electorate the probability that one vote is decisive is
negligible while voting carries positive costs — yet substantial numbers vote. It is
distinct from the [[social-choice|paradox of voting]] in the Condorcet sense (cyclical
majorities). The instrumental calculus is usually written R = pB − C, amended by
Riker and Ordeshook (1968) with a D-term for civic duty or the consumption value of
the act.

## Semantic History

Downs (1957) framed turnout as a collective-action problem: an election outcome is a
collective good to which any single vote contributes negligibly. Riker and Ordeshook
(1968, 1973) added the **D-term** — five sources of psychic satisfaction (complying
with the ethic of voting, affirming allegiance, affirming partisan preference, the act
of deciding, affirming efficacy) — turning the model into pB + D > C. Later variants
proliferated: minimax-regret decision rules (Ferejohn and Fiorina 1974, a non-utility-
maximizing rule), misperception-of-p accounts, subelectorate/patronage pivotalness
(Schwartz 1987), and game-theoretic endogenous-p models (Palfrey and Rosenthal 1983;
Ledyard). Fiorina (1990) called turnout "the paradox that ate rational choice theory."

## Operationalizations

The instrumental calculus pB + D > C, with p estimated from electorate size and
closeness. Schwartz (1987) noted that even a voter willing to pay $10,000 to
unilaterally determine the outcome has an expected value of voting of "no more than a
dime" at p ≈ 1/100,000. Testable marginal hypotheses derived from the model include:
collective benefits B should not affect turnout when p ≈ 0 (lopsided elections), and
B's effect should grow with perceived closeness (a closeness × benefits interaction).

## Applications

Central case study in *[[green-shapiro-pathologies-1994|Pathologies of Rational Choice
Theory]]* (ch. 4). [[green-donald|Green]] and [[shapiro-ian|Shapiro]] argue (attributed)
that the successive rational-choice rescues exemplify the book's
[[method-driven-research|methodological pathologies]]:

- **The civic-duty D-term** is "scarcely more than a tautology" — Riker and Ordeshook
  never independently measure the benefit, inferring it from the bare fact that people
  vote, so they "reduce the paradox of voter turnout to the same logical status as the
  paradox of attendance at free concerts." Green and Shapiro also note the civic-duty/
  turnout correlation was already established by social psychology (Campbell, Gurin,
  and Miller, *The Voter Decides*, 1954) before rational choice repackaged it.
- **Game-theoretic endogenous-p models** produced high-turnout equilibria that
  "collapsed" once even a small degree of strategic uncertainty was allowed (Palfrey
  and Rosenthal 1985 concede: "we have come full circle and are once again beset by the
  paradox of not voting").
- **Aldrich's (1993) domain restriction** — recategorizing turnout as a "low-cost,
  low-benefit" activity outside rational choice's proper scope — is
  [[method-driven-research|arbitrary domain restriction]]: nothing in the theory
  specifies the threshold, and turnout is empirically not always low-cost (Jim Crow-era
  Black voters facing intimidation and poll taxes; Latin American voters enduring
  violence and long lines). "Turnout was banished from rational choice theory only when
  it became apparent that no satisfying theoretical solution could be worked out."

Green and Shapiro's own probit reanalysis of Uhlaner's (1989) data found no evidence of
the union-membership × turnout interaction she claimed, and Green and Cowden (1992)
found collective stakes drove turnout in a lopsided 1974 Boston busing referendum,
against the closeness-dependence prediction. They summarize the closeness literature as
one that "offers a set of predictions that exhausts the range of possible empirical
observations."

## Critiques and Limitations

Rational choice theorists dispute that the turnout literature is a fair test case,
holding that turnout is a genuinely marginal domain and that duty/expressive terms are
legitimate extensions; the exchange is part of the
[[rational-choice-controversy-debate|rational choice controversy]]. Green and Shapiro's
constructive proposal is a **partial-universalist** treatment — rational maximizing as
one factor among several, varying by person and context — plus original data collection
rather than repurposed survey items.
