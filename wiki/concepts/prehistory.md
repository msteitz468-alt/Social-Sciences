# Prehistory

## Definition as Coined

The concept of a **prehistory** — a recoverable human past preceding written records — took hold only after the mid-19th-century acceptance of human antiquity (Darwin's *Origin of Species*, 1859; the 1859 vindication of Boucher de Perthes's Somme finds). [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] gives a working definition in *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* (1957): prehistory is concerned with **preliterate societies**, its lower bound taken pragmatically as the appearance of tools "shaped in conformity with a recognizable tradition" and its upper bound the appearance of "a more or less continuous written record," which marks the transition to history proper (attributed, Clark 1957).

## Semantic History

The boundary is **time-transgressive, not a single date**: literacy diffused unevenly, taking roughly five thousand years to spread, so "the prehistoric period lasted much longer in some regions than others" (attributed, Clark 1957). Clark's illustrative end-dates: Mediterranean littoral by mid-first-millennium BCE (Greek/Punic colonization); temperate Europe not until incorporation into medieval Christendom; South Africa 1652 (Dutch settlement); Australia 1788 (founding of Sydney); much of the interior Americas only after post-Columbian contact.

A terminological distinction Clark records: the French tradition separates **la préhistoire** (Old Stone Age to Neolithic, before civilizational impact) from **la protohistoire** (later prehistoric cultures showing the impact of literate civilizations, e.g. via metallurgy), whereas British archaeologists "have generally preferred to hold to the concept of prehistory as a unitary field"; Clark suggests "primary and secondary prehistory" as a possible middle formulation (position, Clark 1957).

## Operationalizations

Clark frames prehistory as **a social study operating at a higher level of abstraction than history proper** — it deals, in his own 1953 words, "not with individuals... but with societies, including their internal stratification and their local organization, and their relations to one another and to the world of nature of which in the final resort they form an integral part" (attributed, Clark, *The Study of Prehistory*, 1953). It remains "fundamentally historical in the sense that it deals with time as a main dimension" (attributed, Clark 1957). Evidence is overwhelmingly material; where inscriptions exist, archaeology plays for Clark "an ancillary, if not subservient, role."

## Applications

Prehistory as a construct organizes the whole knowledge-layer of prehistoric [[archaeology]] and underwrites the [[three-age-system]] periodization and the concept of the [[archaeological-culture]]. Clark's own program, [[economic-prehistory]], treats the reconstruction of prehistoric economic life as prehistory's central aim.

In *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961) Clark operationalizes prehistory as a **shared world history** before continuous written records: each region is followed until literacy or colonial contact, so the upper bound remains time-transgressive; the lower bound is cultural (tool-making tradition) rather than purely zoological (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 2 and Retrospect). The book’s Retrospect treats cultural evolution — speech, institutions, writing — as largely independent of organic evolution once *Homo sapiens* is in place, and as the medium through which human societies diversify and “advance” unevenly (attributed; progress framing is a POSITION).

## Critiques and Limitations

The **"prehistoric peoples have no history, so prehistorians attempt the impossible"** objection is recorded by Clark, who answers it by distinguishing history in the "rigorous, academic sense" (post-writing) from history in a "broader evolutionary connotation" comprehending all of humanity (position, Clark 1957). The prehistory/history boundary is also politically loaded: defining a people as "prehistoric" until contact with literate (usually colonizing) societies embeds a diffusionist and Eurocentric frame — a reflexivity concern visible in Clark's own text (see [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]]).

### Gamble 1994 — progress agenda and purpose without progress

[[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] argues mid-19th-c. prehistoric archaeology was dedicated to demonstrating **progress** toward civilization (at worst as a branch of Spencerian Social Darwinism), and that even after progress is formally rejected, the research agenda still privileges cradles-and-drift and "why civilization was not universal" over "why humans were everywhere" (attributed, *[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* ch. 1–2). He further claims a double standard: purpose is restored to history after agriculture/civilization while earlier timewalkers are left to natural drift — an implicit continuation of progress science under another name (attributed, ch. 11). Alternative: **purpose without progress** (Collingwood's wholly human plan of history; Vico against judging origins by present cultivated standards), with [[global-colonization]] as the process that returns active agency to deep prehistory without teleology (attributed).

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

As of [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]], prehistory as a recoverable deep past is framed as **historically recent**: widespread acceptance of multi-million-year human antiquity is only about a century old; until the nineteenth century most Westerners accepted a ~6,000-year biblical chronology, whereas "almost all knowledgeable scientists" now accept tool-using hominin ancestors by about 2.5 million years ago (attributed). They define the **archaeological record** as the collective material residue of more than two million years — the only evidence for >99% of the human past before writing — and contrast it with historical documents as a physical record of what did happen (attributed, ch. 2).

On the intellectual history of the concept, they restate the [[three-age-system]] (Thomsen 1836; Worsaae; anticipations in Lucretius and Yuan K'ang) as the foundational relative periodization of prehistory, and they contrast early nineteenth-century relic-hunting (Belzoni, Schliemann) with modern objectives: patterns, meaning, and explanation of cultural variability rather than treasure (attributed, ch. 1). They reject divine-will historiography (static created world; history as post hoc description of God's will) as incompatible with analysis for knowable, testable causes (attributed). Culture-history writing, they warn, often arranged world cultures as a ladder culminating in Western societies and treated agriculture and urbanism as natural products of progressive peoples — "there cannot be an absolutely neutral and value-free history" (attributed). Equifinality in cultural reconstruction (many processes can yield similar finds) means prehistorians can only assign relative probabilities within a theory, never conclusive confirmation like celestial mechanics (attributed).
