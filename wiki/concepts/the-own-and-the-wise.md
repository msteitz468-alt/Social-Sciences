# The own and the wise

## Definition as Coined

In *[[goffman-stigma-1963|Stigma]]* Ch 1, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]] named two categories of persons before whom the stigmatized need not feel shame or special restraint:

### The own

Those who **share the stigma** — instruct in "tricks of the trade," provide a circle of lament, comfort of feeling at home and accepted as normal (attributed). Corporate life ranges from informal half-worlds and "sad tales" to formal associations, lobbies, publications, and professionalized representatives (attributed). The own are stratified by apparent degree of stigma; alliances "across the line" and **in-group purification** ("near-ing") express ambivalence (Ch 3; attributed).

### The wise

"**Normals** whose special situation has made them intimately privy to the secret life of the stigmatized individual and sympathetic with it," accorded a measure of **courtesy membership** in the clan (attributed). Goffman noted "wise" as borrowed from usage once current among homosexuals. Two routes: occupational (nurses, specialists, police, etc.) and relational (spouses, kin — often carrying [[courtesy-stigma]]). Before the wise, the person with a fault "need feel no shame nor exert self-control" (attributed). Relation can be uneasy; the stand-in may be tested for sincerity (attributed).

## Semantic History

Stock citation in stigma ethnography and identity-politics analysis (*Stigma* Ch 3 in-group/out-group alignments).

## Operationalizations

Membership categories in mixed-contact analysis; not survey items in Goffman.

## Applications

Support groups; professional–client relations; politics of who may speak for the group; moral-career learning among the own.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Wise" can romanticize professional allies; later disability studies contest who counts as own vs. authorized speaker.
