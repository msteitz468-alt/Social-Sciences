# Ultimate and Proximate Causes (Diamond)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[diamond-guns-germs-steel-1997|Guns, Germs, and Steel]]* (1997), [[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] borrows the **ultimate / proximate** distinction (familiar from evolutionary biology) to structure answers to **Yali’s question** (why peoples differ in wealth and power):

- **Proximate causes** — the immediate factors of conquest and inequality: weapons, epidemic diseases, metal technology, oceangoing ships, political organization, writing.
- **Ultimate causes** — the deeper historical and environmental conditions that explain *why* those proximate factors arose earlier or more completely in some regions than others — especially continental differences in domesticable biota, axes of diffusion, intercontinental barriers, and area/population.

All load-bearing claims **attributed to Diamond**.

## Semantic History

Biology’s proximate (mechanism) vs ultimate (evolutionary function/history) contrast is older; Diamond’s innovation is applying it programmatically to comparative world history at continental scale. Critics treat the mapping as smuggling [[environmental-determinism]] under scientific vocabulary — see [[guns-germs-steel-geographic-determinism-debate]].

## Operationalizations

Diamond’s chain (attributed): environment/biota → food production → denser populations, specialists, livestock-linked germs, surplus → technology and centralized political organization → proximate conquest package.

## Applications

Organizes GGS Parts 2–3 (food production as ultimate bridge; germs/writing/tech/government as intermediate) and the Epilogue’s four environmental factor sets.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether “ultimate” environmental factors leave enough room for culture, contingency, and institutions is the core of the GGS debate. The dualism can flatten multicausal histories into a single chain.
