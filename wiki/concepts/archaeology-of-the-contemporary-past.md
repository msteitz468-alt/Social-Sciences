## Definition as Coined

Victor Buchli and Gavin Lucas (2001a) used "archaeology of the contemporary past" to describe archaeological work on garbage, forensics, and family history extending into the twentieth century and present. Buchli and Lucas argued that in mainstream historical archaeology, temporal distance between subject and object of study is normally preserved, whereas in archaeologies of the contemporary past that distance is "no longer present," undermining any pretence of archaeological detachment (Buchli and Lucas 2001c: 8–9, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). Buchli and Lucas also coined "critical empiricism" for an approach combining scientific method and interpretive practice within historical archaeology (Buchli 1999: 11; Buchli 2002b: 133; Buchli and Lucas 2001d; Lucas 2001).

Dan Hicks's phrase "the loss of antiquity" (Hicks 2003: 316–17) captures a related rejection of definitions of the archaeological past as necessarily distant — a stance the editors of this Companion adopt in defining historical archaeology as "a contemporary and creative practice" ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Semantic History

Richard Newman (2001: 8), in an overview of the historical archaeology of Britain, argued instead for a cut-off terminus at the end of the Victorian age: "We are probably too close to the twentieth century's cultural detritus to be able to focus on the nature of its archaeology" (quoted in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]) — a position the editors reject.

Sarah Tarlow and Susie West (1999, eds.) framed the goal of contemporary British historical archaeology as "de-familiarising" the recent past (Tarlow 1999a: 264), which the editors of this Companion critique as still limiting "familiar" past to British material and distinct from North American traditions.

Bill Rathje observed that archaeologists studying contemporary material no longer have to wait until "after the dust settles" (Rathje 2001: 67, quoted in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]) — his "garbology" project, excavating landfill sites and domestic refuse, aimed to "demystify aspects of contemporary domestic consumption" (Rathje 2001), including a "first principle of food waste" finding less food is wasted when fewer variations in food use occur (e.g., regular sliced bread wasted under 10% of purchase versus specialty breads wasted over 35%).

## Operationalizations

**Heritage management (UK vs. US).** The UK applies no "fifty-year rule" — sites of any date can receive official heritage recognition. The US National Register of Historic Places applies a fifty-year rule that can be set aside for properties of "exceptional importance"; in 1994 the Register listed 2,025 properties achieving significance within the past 50 years, of which 464 reflected history since 1950 and 77 since 1974 (Shull and Savage 1995: 3, as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

**Survival assessment.** An English Heritage programme (from 1994) documented the typology, distribution, and deployment strategy of major twentieth-century military site classes, combining documentary research (Dobinson et al. 1997) with wartime and contemporary aerial-photograph comparison (Anderton and Schofield 1999; Schofield 2002b): of 242 WWII radar stations, only 14 survived in original recognisable form; of 981 heavy anti-aircraft gun sites, only 10 survived intact, most others removed by post-war suburban expansion.

**Public engagement.** The *Change and Creation: Historic Landscape Character 1950–2000* project extended landscape characterisation to the recent landscape and engaged the public (not only academics or heritage managers) via a website posing questions about memory and landscape change (Bradley et al. 2004: 7, 11).

## Applications

**Nevada Test Site / Atomic Testing Museum, Las Vegas.** The Nevada Test Site, established in late 1950, hosted 928 nuclear-weapon tests between 1951 and October 1992. Archaeological survey of the site began as early as 1963 (Worman 1969) and expanded after the Historic Preservation Act of 1966; the Desert Research Institute established a long-term archaeological programme by 1978. The Nevada Test Site Historical Foundation, granted non-profit status in 1998, established the Atomic Testing Museum combining historic-preservation and public-history missions ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

**Conflict archaeology.** Excavation of the modern conflict record serves differing social functions depending on context: American missing-in-action recovery teams' work relates to memory, commemoration, and proper burial (Hoshower-Leppo 2002), while excavation of Argentina's "disappeared" (Crossland 2002) relates instead to retribution, justice, and the production of evidence for war-crimes tribunals — the same excavation methods serving distinct commemorative versus juridical purposes ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

A chapter closing reflection (Schofield & Johnson, in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]) identifies a core interpretive tension in managing recent material culture: preservation decisions are shaped by "cultural bias ... introduced ... by our participation in free-market economies," illustrated by the contrast in material culture between East and West Berliners at the fall of the Berlin Wall, and by a "schizophrenic" coexistence of eco-critique of disposable consumer culture alongside pervasive material abundance.

The distinction between the archaeology of the contemporary past and mainstream historical archaeology is itself debated: whether the difference is merely chronological or, as Buchli and Lucas argue, a more fundamental loss of archaeological detachment when temporal distance collapses.
