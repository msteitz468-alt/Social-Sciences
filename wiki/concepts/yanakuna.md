# Yanakuna

## Definition as Coined

**Yanakuna** (singular often *yana*) names, in [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]]’s synthesis (*[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|The Incas]]*, 2002), one of the most important specialized labor statuses created under Inca rule: **lifelong servants**, set alongside *mitmaqkuna* (colonists) and [[aqllakuna|aqllakuna]] (Chosen Women). As of D'Altroy 2002 (following Murra 1980b), in the latter decades of their rule the Incas increasingly augmented and even replaced rotating corvée workers with permanent specialists. Local officials (Santillán) assigned duties under supervision; ethnic lords chose workers (e.g., new miners at each royal succession), giving leverage in local relations (Moore 1958). The category thus marks a shift from household-based rotating [[mita-labor|mit'a]] toward permanently attached personnel in the state and elite economy.

## Semantic History

D'Altroy’s 2002 treatment embeds *yanakuna* in the political economy of Tawantinsuyu rather than as a free-standing ethnography of personal dependence. The term sits in a triad of permanent specialized statuses—**mitmaqkuna, yanakuna, aqllakuna**—contrasted with ordinary *hatun runa* household heads who owed two to three months of rotating labor per year. Royal and aristocratic estates were maintained by *mitmaqkuna* and *yanakuna* in lifelong service, and are carefully distinguished from state and Sun farms, which were mostly tended by rotating corvée (though thousands of colonists were also used later). The category could absorb political outcomes of conquest: after late subjugation, Chachapoyas leaders were made into *yanakuna* rather than decimal officials—illustrating use of lifelong service as an alternative to standard administrative incorporation on the northern margin.

Colonial and ethnohistoric sources (as D'Altroy reads them) do not always cleanly separate the Sapa Inca’s “state” versus private resources; full-time artisans working for themselves, the state, or elite patrons were rare even in [[cuzco|Cuzco]] or on royal estates—most craft was part-time—so *yanakuna* service is better framed as permanent attachment than as a modern occupational “profession.”

## Operationalizations

**Estate labor.** Rural manors and elite grants—especially Urubamba royal estates such as [[machu-picchu|Machu Picchu]], Pisac, and [[ollantaytambo|Ollantaytambo]]—converted prime resources to aristocratic holdings to maintain deceased emperors’ descendants and political/ceremonial activity ([[panaqa|panaqa]], [[split-inheritance|split inheritance]]). Yucay (Wayna Qhapaq): 2,400 men and families dedicated to the holdings; Thupa Inka Yupanki is reported with 4,000–4,500 workers at each of three empire locations (Rostworowski via D'Altroy). These dedicated workforces sit in the same labor field as lifelong *yanakuna* and *mitmaq* service on private domains, as opposed to state/Sun farm corvée.

**Herding.** Camelids were wealth, transport, food, clothing, and prestige. State and Sun herds were carefully tabulated; prime uses were military (pack llamas, wool/leather, sacrifices and feasts). D'Altroy reports that state herding was partly a specialized adult duty—**yanakuna near Cuzco**—contrasted with community child and adolescent herding.

**Administrative geography.** The decimal structure was installed throughout the central empire, but in the far north and south may have been regularly applied only among colonist communities; there is no evidence for decimal officials in Chile and Argentina. Making Chachapoyas leaders *yanakuna* rather than decimal officers is D'Altroy’s explicit example of alternative incorporation.

**Relation to other extractive modes.** *Yanakuna* complement: (1) rotating mit'a on hale household heads (~15–20% of population ≈ ~2 million workers); (2) *mitmaqkuna* resettlement and military/craft islands; (3) *aqllakuna* sequestered female institutions. Together they form the permanent specialist layer of late imperial “supply on command” (La Lone 1994 via D'Altroy).

## Applications

- **Royal/aristocratic estate economy** vs state/Sun farms ([[panaqa]], [[mita-labor]], [[inca-empire]]).
- **Late imperial shift** from corvée dominance toward permanent specialists (Murra via D'Altroy).
- **Frontier incorporation** without full decimal offices (Chachapoyas leaders as *yanakuna*).
- **Specialized herding** near the capital as adult permanent duty.
- **Political economy of dependence:** detachment from ordinary community labor cycles into lifelong elite/state service (with [[mitmaq]] and [[aqllakuna]] as sibling categories).

## Critiques and Limitations

**Thin dedicated treatment in D'Altroy 2002.** Relative to *mitmaq*, mit'a, and *aqllakuna*, *yanakuna* appear mainly as definitional elements in labor-status lists and estate passages rather than as a full institutional chapter. Claims about personal status (slavery analogies, heritability, gender, manumission) should not be filled from outside knowledge; this page stays within D'Altroy’s operational gloss: lifelong servants on estates and specialized tasks.

**Evidence classes.** Estate workforce figures (Yucay 2,400; Thupa’s multi-site thousands) rest on colonial/documentary sources D'Altroy cites; archaeological separation of *yanakuna* from *mitmaq* or temporary retainers is often difficult. Early sources often blurred state vs private resources of the Sapa Inca.

**Not equivalent to mitmaq.** Colonists (*mitmaqkuna*) and lifelong servants (*yanakuna*) are co-listed but distinct statuses; military *mitmaq* islands and permanent ethnic soldiery (Chachapoyas, Kañari) are nearest military analogues, not simple synonyms of *yanakuna*.
