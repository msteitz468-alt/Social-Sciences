# Mode of Development

In Castells’s framework, the technological mode that specifies sources of productivity (agrarian, industrial, [[informationalism|informational]]), analytically **distinct** from the mode of production (capitalism, statism). Allows the claim that informationalism restructures capitalism without abolishing it. See *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Prologue.

## Definition as Coined

Castells (attributed): modes of development are “the technological arrangements through which labor works on matter to generate the product, ultimately determining the level and quality of surplus.” In the agrarian mode, surplus depends on quantitative increases of labor and natural resources; in the industrial mode, on new energy sources and ability to decentralize energy use; in the informational mode, on technology of knowledge generation, information processing, and symbol communication. Each mode has a structurally determined performance principle (industrialism oriented to economic growth/maximizing output; informationalism oriented toward technological development and accumulation of knowledge).

**Dual axis:** modes of production (capitalism vs statism) × modes of development (industrialism vs informationalism) must be kept analytically distinct and empirically interrelated (Touraine/Bell lineage, reworked).

## Semantic History

Borrowed/adapted from broader Marxist and post-industrial vocabularies; Castells’s dual axis is the load-bearing move against collapsing “post-industrial society” into a single evolutionary stage that replaces capitalism.

## Operationalizations

Industrialism vs informationalism; interaction with capitalist restructuring; contrast with Soviet industrial-statism failure (teased in Prologue; developed in Vol. III — not ingested).

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; [[informationalism]]; analysis of East Asian developmental states as organizers of technology and institutions.

## Critiques and Limitations

Typology risks reifying stages; empirical separation of “development” from “production” is analytical, not a clean historical split.
