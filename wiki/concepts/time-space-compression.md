# Time-Space Compression

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to [[harvey-david|David Harvey]], *[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The
Condition of Postmodernity]]*, 1989, p. 240.)* Harvey defines the term to "signal …
processes that so revolutionize the objective qualities of space and time that we are
forced to alter, sometimes in quite radical ways, how we represent the world to
ourselves." "Compression" captures both a "speed-up in the pace of life" and the sense
that "the world sometimes seems to collapse inwards upon us" as spatial barriers are
overcome. Harvey stresses that compression is **relative** — always measured against a
preceding state of affairs — and that his history of it, drawn from the European case, is
avowedly "somewhat ethnocentric."

The concept is rooted in Marx's claim that capital strives to reduce the turnover time of
circulation "to the twinkling of an eye" and to "annihilate space through time" — to
create the world market by reducing the time and cost of movement. Because spatial
barriers can be reduced only through *fixed*, immovable investment in particular places
(railways, ports, telecommunications), the drive to overcome space produces new spatial
fixity that later obstructs accumulation — Harvey's "spatial fix" contradiction, tied to
"creative destruction."

## Semantic History

Harvey (attributed) identifies **two great historical episodes** of intense compression:

- **c. 1847–1918.** The crisis of overaccumulation that "swept out of Britain in 1846–7"
  produced, on Harvey's argument, a "crisis of representation" answered by modernism —
  Manet's decomposition of pictorial space, Cubism's abandonment of linear perspective,
  Joyce's and Proust's temporality. The material drivers: railways, telegraph, steam
  shipping, the Suez Canal, then (1910–14) telephone, wireless, cinema, automobile, and
  aircraft. Harvey reads the outbreak of the First World War as a compression the era's
  representational systems could not master (following Kern 1983).
- **Post-1972.** Under [[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]], satellite
  communication (cost/time invariant with distance), container shipping and air freight,
  just-in-time production, and dematerialized money (after the 1971–3 breakdown of
  Bretton Woods) produced a second, more intense round — registered culturally as
  [[postmodernism|postmodernism]]'s ephemerality, pastiche, and simulacra.

The term diffused widely into [[globalization|globalization]] studies, urban geography,
and social theory, often detached from Harvey's specifically Marxist framing. It sits
alongside Anthony Giddens's near-contemporary [[time-space-distanciation|time-space
distanciation]] — the two are complementary but not identical: distanciation stresses the
stretching of social relations across space-time, compression the felt collapse of
distance.

## Operationalizations

Time-space compression is not directly measured; different traditions index it by proxies
— declining transport and communication cost and time per unit distance (Harvey's
"shrinking map" image), shortening capital turnover time, and collapsing product
half-lives (Harvey cites a Fordist product half-life of 5–7 years falling to under 18
months in "thoughtware"). Critics note these proxies conflate distinct processes and that
"compression" is a metaphor doing analytical work.

## Applications

- Harvey's own: linking the [[fordism|Fordism]]→[[flexible-accumulation|flexible-
  accumulation]] transition to the rise of [[postmodernism|postmodernism]] (see
  [[postmodernity-epochal-break-debate]]).
- The **paradox of place**: Harvey argues that "the less important the spatial barriers,
  the greater the sensitivity of capital to the variations of place within space" — so
  compression *heightens* rather than erases the significance of place, feeding
  place-marketing, heritage industries, and localist politics.

## Critiques and Limitations

*(Attributed.)* The concept has been criticized as economically over-determined
(compression treated as driven fundamentally by capital's turnover imperative); as
experientially uneven — Doreen Massey's "power-geometry" argues compression is lived very
differently by the jet-setting executive and the refugee, a differentiation Harvey's
general formulation underplays; and as resting on illustrative rather than systematic
evidence. Harvey concedes the ethnocentrism of a European-derived history. The term
nonetheless remains a standard reference point for theorizing globalization and modernity.
