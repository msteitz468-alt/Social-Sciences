# Sacred and Profane

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms of Religious Life]]* (1912), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] defines religion by the division of the world into two domains: the **sacred** and the **profane**. Sacred things are "things set apart and forbidden"; beliefs and rites express their nature and organize relations with them. His canonical definition (attributed):

> "A religion is a unified system of beliefs and practices relative to sacred things, that is to say, things set apart and forbidden—beliefs and practices which unite into one single moral community called a Church, all those who adhere to them."

The second element is "no less essential": religion is inseparable from the Church and thus "eminently collective." The dualism is absolute in *kind* (two heterogeneous worlds) though the *content* of what is sacred varies by society. The sacred is contagious; the profane is ordinary utilitarian life. Magic, for Durkheim, also deals with sacred forces but **lacks a church**. Gods and the "supernatural" are not definitional: some religions lack gods; "supernatural" is a late idea.

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Durkheim 1912 | Defining dualism of religious life; not supernatural vs natural, not gods required |
| Mid-century functionalism / comparative religion | Standard textbook definition of religion |
| Structuralism / later anthropology | Often relaxed; many systems show graded or ambiguous sacredness (Durkheim's own piacular chapter notes ambiguity of pure/impure sacred) |
| Douglas *Purity and Danger* (1966) | Attacks Robertson Smith/Frazer claim that primitives *confuse* holy and unclean; pollution idiom can be fine relative algebra (Havik); dirt/uncleanness as matter out of place applies across sacred and secular without requiring Durkheim's dualism as the only frame — see [[ritual-pollution]], [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]] |
| Contemporary cultural sociology | Sacred as that which is set apart and defended (Alexander et al.; reception not yet ingested) |

## Operationalizations

Binary or graded coding of spaces, times, objects, and persons as set-apart vs everyday; taboo/interdiction inventories; ritual purity rules. Durkheim does not quantify; later work sometimes scales "sacredness."

## Applications

- Anchors Durkheim's rejection of animist and naturist definitions of elementary religion.
- Structures Book III: negative cult protects sacred/profane separation; positive cult brings them into communion under rule.
- Extends to civil religion and political "sacred" in later reception (flag: not yet ingested).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Dualism too sharp for many ethnographic cases (overlap, everyday sacred, multiple grades).
- "Church" requirement may undercount diffuse or individualistic religiosity (Durkheim treats these as incomplete or secondary).
- Content of sacred filled from period Australian ethnography — case-dependent.
- Douglas (1966): same patterning principle for sacred and secular dirt; relative purity grades (cow-dung pure for mortals, impure for gods) show hair-splitting distinction rather than holy/unclean confusion — pressures absolute dualism toward graded/context-relative readings without abandoning set-apart logic.

## Insoll 1999 — no sacred/secular compartmentalization as Muslim ideal

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]*, citing Mawdudi among others.)* Against Western archaeological habit of segregating religion from other life spheres, Insoll restates a Muslim ideal: no distinction between spiritual and secular; shari’ah governs all domains. Archaeological consequence: diet, dress, domestic privacy, magic, warfare, trade, and burial belong inside [[archaeology-of-religion]], not only mosques. This is an **attributed ideal**, not a claim that all Muslims observe it — ideals vs realities is the book’s standing method. Complements but does not replace Durkheim’s sacred/profane binary (different problem: definition of religion vs. life-system unity).
