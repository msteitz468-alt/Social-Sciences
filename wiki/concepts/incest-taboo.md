# Incest Prohibition

## Definition as Coined

The **incest prohibition** — the rule barring sexual relations and marriage between close kin — is treated across anthropology as a near-universal (its *scope* of prohibited kin varying by society). Its explanation is one of the discipline's oldest problems. This page records [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] influential retheorization in the *[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]* (1949).

## Semantic History

Lévi-Strauss (attributed) reviewed and rejected the three standing families of explanation:
- **Rationalist/eugenic** (Maine, Morgan): avoidance of consanguineous harm — rejected as anachronistic ("appearing nowhere in our society before the sixteenth century") and empirically ungrounded.
- **Psychological/instinctive aversion** (Westermarck, Havelock Ellis): innate horror bred by childhood familiarity — rejected as circular and contradicted by cases where childhood intimacy generates desirable marriage bonds.
- **Sociological/historical-survival** (McLennan, Spencer, Lubbock, Durkheim): a residue of past practice — rejected for "establishing a universal phenomenon on an historical sequence."

His own thesis: the prohibition is the **hinge between nature and culture** — "the fundamental step... by which... the transition from nature to culture is accomplished." It is not merely negative but the positive **rule of reciprocity**: "The woman whom one does not take... is, for that very reason, offered up." Hence its famous reformulation: the prohibition "is less a rule prohibiting marriage with the mother, sister or daughter, than a rule obliging" them "to be given to others. It is the supreme rule of the gift." See [[nature-culture-distinction]], [[exchange-of-women]], [[alliance-theory]].

## Operationalizations

The prohibition is identified by the boundary of prohibited kin (which relatives are barred) and its correlate, the positive rule of [[exogamy]] (into which category one *must* marry). Lévi-Strauss stressed that endogamy and exogamy are "different perspectives on a system of fundamental relationships," not independent institutions.

## Applications

Grounds the whole argument of the *Elementary Structures*: because cross- and parallel cousins are biologically equidistant yet treated as socially opposite ([[cross-cousin-marriage]]), the prohibition cannot be biological; it must be social — the rule that launches exchange. Lévi-Strauss cited Mead's Arapesh ("we give our sisters to other men, and other men give us their sisters... Don't you want a brother-in-law?") and Evans-Pritchard's Nuer as native theory that incest avoidance is experienced as a positive obligation to exchange.

## Critiques and Limitations

The 1967 preface conceded the genetic angle was treated "too casually" in 1949 while maintaining a purely sociological explanation. On Freud's *Totem and Taboo*, Lévi-Strauss judged it explained "not the beginning of civilization but its present state" — why incest is *unconsciously desired*, not why it is prohibited. The reciprocity thesis is contested by later biological and evolutionary accounts of inbreeding avoidance (the Westermarck effect), which the discipline has partly rehabilitated; and by the feminist objection that "giving" sisters and daughters names women as objects, not agents (see [[exchange-of-women]]).
