# Moral Economy

> Dual modern lineage: E. P. Thompson (English crowd / food riots; no vault thinker page yet) and [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] (*The Moral Economy of the Peasant*, 1976). This vault page is densified first from Scott’s *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* (1985); full 1976 primary ingest still pending.

## Definition as Coined

In *Weapons of the Weak*, Scott treats moral economy as the **normative environment** through which peasants interpret material change — not a Parsonian value consensus above conflict, but a field of **divergent interpretations**. Large farmers once needed loyal labor and political followings; poor households needed land, wages, loans, and emergency help. Reciprocal practices (feasts, *zakat peribadi*, advances, living rents, personalism) euphemized economic power (Scott via Bourdieu) while partially socializing crop proceeds.

When double-cropping and combines remade the material base, elites abandoned practices no longer in their interest; the poor defended older norms as rights — the social use of property. Charges of stinginess (*lokek*, *berkira*, *kedekut*) and arrogance (*sombong*) are, in this frame, class claims about legitimate wealth.

## Semantic History

- **Thompson:** English food riots as defense of customary price and market norms.
- **Scott 1976:** Southeast Asian peasantry, subsistence security, risk, and rebellion thresholds (un-ingested primary here).
- **Scott 1985:** moral economy as ongoing ideological struggle *inside and after* Green Revolution transformation — not only a trigger for open revolt. The “remembered village” is selective but not pure mystification.
- Later: development studies and food studies generalize the term; Popkin-style rational-peasant challenges (outside this ingest unless engaged by Scott) and romanticization critiques.

## Operationalizations

In [[sedaka|Sedaka]]: disputes over *zakat*, *sedekah*, feast-giving, living vs dead rents, combine adoption, Village Improvement Scheme allocation, Village Gate conflicts — all read as fights over the social use of property within the Malay community (the unit where such claims were sanctioned).
