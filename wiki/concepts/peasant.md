# Peasant

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966), [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]] defines **peasants** as rural cultivators — they raise crops and livestock in the countryside — who are **not** farmers in the American entrepreneurial sense (a business combining purchased factors for market profit) and **not** merely "primitives who farm." The distinguishing mark is the **character of involvement** with a larger social order: peasants are cultivators whose **surpluses are transferred to a dominant group of rulers** that uses those surpluses to underwrite its own standard of living and to feed non-farming specialist groups. In primitive society (Wolf quoting [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]), producers typically control means of production and exchange for culturally defined equivalents; under civilization, control of means of production and disposition of labor passes to executive/administrative groups backed by force, and goods move first to a center and only later are redirected.

Wolf rejects two common false marks: (1) isolation vs involvement — primitives also trade, marry, fight, and enter networks (Malinowski's Kula, Australian desert exchanges, Plains Indians in American history); (2) the label "traditional" / "tradition-bound" — labels describe badly and do not explain *why* tradition persists.

Critically, production of a **[[fund-of-rent]]** distinguishes peasant from primitive cultivator: a social order in which some men can through power demand payments from others. "Peasant" so far denotes **no more than an asymmetrical structural relationship** between surplus producers and controllers — there are many kinds of peasantry, not one, because modes of producing and siphoning rent vary (see [[types-of-domain]]).

Wolf further distinguishes **peasant-primary** agrarian societies (peasants the vast majority and underwriters of the whole social structure) from **industrial-secondary** ones, where remaining peasants are secondary and food increasingly comes from "factories in the field" staffed by wage agricultural workers, not peasants. Both orders threaten the peasant — surplus demands or competitive obsolescence.

## Semantic History

Pre-Wolf: European and colonial agrarian usages; Marxist [[peasantry-as-class]] (allotment farmers as class and not-a-class, *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|18th Brumaire]]*); Redfield's folk–urban continuum and "peasant society and culture." Wolf 1966 crystallizes a political-economic anthropological type for the Foundations series and development-era readers. Later peasant studies (Shanin, Scott, moral economy, *Peasant Wars*) extend, historicize, or contest the type — pending primary ingest. Distinct from [[peasantry-as-class]] (Marx on representability) though both address agrarian cultivators under asymmetric power; Wolf cites Marx's "sack of potatoes" in the coalitions chapter as a position on peasant class unity.

## Operationalizations

- **Household vs enterprise:** peasant runs a household provisioning unit, not a profit-maximizing firm (Chayanov-adjacent; long Chaianov block on family labor and non-capitalist land valuation).
- **Four-fund budget:** caloric minimum (~2000–3000 kcal/person/day as physiological figure Wolf cites); [[replacement-fund]]; [[ceremonial-fund]]; [[fund-of-rent]] — all claimed before discretionary accumulation.
- **Domain environment:** surplus transfer organized through [[types-of-domain]] (patrimonial, prebendary, mercantile, plus administrative domain as modern extension) in variable mix.
- **Ecotypes:** [[peasant-ecotype]] paleotechnic (five forms; three major) vs neotechnic production base.
- **Social forms:** [[peasant-coalition]] types (three-criteria matrix); [[closed-corporate-peasant-community]].
- **Strategies:** [[peasant-dilemma]] — raise production vs curtail consumption.
- **Civilization threshold:** functional division cultivators/rulers — not the city per se; Wolf stresses the *state* as power apparatus (Near East ~3500 BCE; Middle America ~1000 BCE as his approximate anchors).

## Applications

- Comparative peasantry across major world regions (Wolf's map and case illustrations).
- Development policy critique: peasant world is ordered infrastructure, not empty void awaiting capital.
- Bridge from anthropology of "primitives" to complex agrarian civilizations and industrial aftermath ("built upon the ruins of peasant society").
- Link to [[peasant-movements]], jacquerie, millenarianism, revolutionary professional staff under state breakdown.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Typological: risk of freezing a historical spectrum into one analytical "peasant."
- Second-hand comparative cases in the primer.
- Contested boundary with commercial farmers, rural proletarians, and "post-peasant" populations.
- Adjacent but not identical to Marx's potato-bag formula ([[peasantry-as-class]]).
