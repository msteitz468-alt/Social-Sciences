# Mesopotamian Religion

**All content on this page is attributed** — either to the ancient texts themselves (myths, hymns, rituals) or to [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux's (1992)]] synthesis and interpretation of them. None of it is asserted in wiki voice as historical fact about ancient belief; it describes what the surviving corpus says and how Roux reads it.

## Definition as Coined

Mesopotamian religion, as reconstructed here, denotes the shared theological and ritual system — pantheon, cosmology, ritual calendar, afterlife beliefs, and ethical framework — that emerged from Sumerian religious tradition and was carried forward, with modification, through the successive political cultures of ancient Iraq. Roux argues that although Sumerian religion disappeared as a *national* cult with the Sumerians as a distinct people by c. 2000 BCE, it was substantially inherited with little modification by the Amorites, Kassites, Assyrians, and Chaldaeans — meaning the second- and first-millennium Assyro-Babylonian religious system he describes throughout his book is fundamentally continuous with the Sumerian system reconstructed here (attributed).

Roux further argues that Mesopotamian religion pervaded public and private life more thoroughly than in "any other antique society," because people felt "utterly dependent" on divine will: land was theoretically owned by the gods even under great empires (Assyrian kings served as agents of the god Ashur; Lagash's governors served Ningirsu) (attributed).

## Semantic History

**Source base and dating problem.** The corpus underlying this reconstruction — epics, myths, rituals, hymns, prayers, incantations, god-lists, precepts, and proverbs — survives chiefly through the temple library of Nippur (Sumer's religious center) and the palace/temple libraries of Assur and Nineveh. Surviving copies date from roughly 1900 BCE to the last pre-Christian centuries, but Roux infers — explicitly flagging this as inference beyond "positive evidence" — that oral tradition reaches back to the Early Dynastic period or earlier, based on recognizable deities and mythological scenes on Uruk- and Jemdat Nasr-period cylinder seals, and on architectural continuity suggesting some gods were already worshipped in the Ubaid period (attributed).

**Theological systematization.** Roux frames the pantheon and its myths as the product of a slow process carried out by multiple priestly "schools" across different cities, eventually converging on a broadly shared pantheon even as each city retained its own patron god and local legends. The divine society was modeled on Sumerian human society; the number of distinct deities shrank over time through "internal syncretism" that nonetheless never reached monotheism (attributed).

### The pantheon

Roux's own organizing scheme (presented as reconstruction, not indigenous doctrine) arranges deities in a rough hierarchy: at the base, benevolent spirits, evil demons, and personal "guardian-angel" gods (the domain of magic rather than religion proper); then minor craft, tool, and nature deities (rivers, mountains, minerals, plants, animals, fertility, birth, medicine, winds and storms) — possibly originally the most numerous class; then Netherworld gods (Nergal, Ereshkigal) and warrior gods (Ninurta); then the astral deities — the moon-god **Nanna/Sin** (controller of lunar months, "who knew the destinies of all") and the sun-god **Utu/Shamash** (god of justice); and at the summit, the three great gods **An, Enlil,** and **Enki**.

- **An (Anu):** embodied "the overpowering personality of the sky"; main temple at [[uruk|Uruk]]; originally supreme sovereign and begetter of the gods with unappealable authority, but in classical Sumerian myth remained aloof from earthly affairs — "a majestic though somewhat pale figure" (Roux).
- **Enlil:** patron god of Nippur, at some point elevated to supreme rank as Sumer's effective national god, later displaced by Babylon's Marduk (Roux notes Enlil was "certainly less of an usurper" than Marduk — an explicit value judgment about legitimacy of succession, attributed). His name means "Lord Air"; he is credited with separating earth from sky (thereby creating the world) and with making humanity and installing kingship. A translated hymn (quoted by Roux) credits Enlil with enabling cities, sheepfolds, kingship, river floods, fish spawning, bird nesting, rain, and the growth of plants, grain, and fruit trees.
- **Enki/Ea:** god of fresh waters (rivers, lakes, springs, wells); the meaning of his Sumerian name is uncertain and linguists dispute the meaning of the Semitic name "Ea" — an open philological question Roux states directly. His chief quality is intelligence ("broad ears"); he is patron of techniques, sciences, arts, and magic, and holds the *me*'s — described as "key-words of the Sumerian civilization" tied to the attribution of destinies. He is credited with conceiving mankind and with saving mankind from the Flood (see below).
- **Female pantheon:** the mother-goddess Ninhursag (also Ninmah, Nintu) is prominent; **Inanna (Ishtar)**, goddess of carnal love, has no husband or children but many discarded lovers, and is portrayed as beautiful and voluptuous yet capable of perfidy and violent anger — traits that over time elevated her to a war-goddess role alongside the male war-gods.
- **Dumuzi:** Inanna's one seemingly genuinely loved consort. Roux interprets him as a fusion of two prehistoric deities — a protector of herds and flocks, and a god of vegetation that dies in summer and revives in spring (attributed scholarly synthesis).

### The Sacred Marriage rite

A New Year ceremony in which the king, ritually standing in for Dumuzi, consummated union with Inanna (embodied by a priestess) — believed necessary to secure cattle reproduction and plant renewal. Attested in erotic/affectionate love poetry and royal hymns, most explicitly the hymn to Iddin-Dagan (3rd king of the Isin dynasty, dated by Roux to 1974–1954 BCE), which is quoted describing the king's ritual approach and union with Inanna followed by public feasting. The rite probably originated at Uruk but was performed elsewhere, and continued at least until the end of the Isin dynasty (1794 BCE, Roux's date); afterward Dumuzi's status declined to that of a minor deity, though a month retained his Semitic name-form Tammuz (still used in the Arabic calendar). The Tammuz cult was revived in the Levant in the last pre-Christian centuries as the Greek Adonis — a Levantine vegetation deity Roux describes as "more or less akin to Osiris" — mourned at Jerusalem, Byblos, Cyprus, and later Rome; a Greek myth in which Persephone and Aphrodite contest for Adonis, resolved by Zeus, is presented by Roux as a distorted survival of the Inanna/Dumuzi myth reaching the Aegean by an unknown transmission route — explicitly speculative diffusionism, attributed.

**Inanna's Descent to the Netherworld** (two versions survive, one Sumerian, one Assyrian): Inanna descends to challenge her sister Ereshkigal — called by Roux "the Sumerian equivalent of Persephone," an explicit analogy — for control of the underworld, shedding an item of clothing or jewelry at each gate. She is killed, then resurrected by Enki, but must supply a substitute to return to earth; she ultimately selects Dumuzi, who is seized by demons; his sister Geshtin-anna (goddess of vines) mourns him; the resolution has Dumuzi and Geshtin-anna each spend half the year in the underworld.

## Tales of Creation

Roux's synthesis of Mesopotamian cosmology (attributed): the earth was pictured as a flat disc ringed by mountains, floating on a freshwater ocean (*abzu/apsû*); the sky vault rested on the mountains, separated from earth by atmosphere (*lil*); a mirroring hemisphere below held the Netherworld and spirits of the dead; the whole cosmos (*anki*, "sky-earth") floated within a boundless primeval salt-water ocean. "Earth" itself was conceived as essentially equivalent to Mesopotamia, centered on Babylon (the Babylonian view) or Nippur (the Sumerian view).

Multiple, sometimes inconsistent creation accounts coexisted — a genuine plurality of traditions, per Roux, not a single canonical myth: Anu creating the heavens while Enki created the *apsû* (his own abode); creation attributed collectively to the general assembly of the gods; creation attributed to four great gods acting together; and a chain-creation incantation against toothache-causing "worms" (Anu → sky → earth → rivers → canals → marshes → the worm) that Roux compares to the nursery rhyme "The House That Jack Built," cautioning it "should perhaps not be taken too seriously."

The dominant Sumerian schema, per Roux: the primeval ocean, personified as the goddess Nammu, alone begot a male sky and female earth joined together; their offspring, the air-god Enlil, forcibly separated sky from earth and, together with earth, engendered all living creatures. This "forceful separation of heaven and earth by a third party" model was adopted across Sumer, Babylonia, and Assyria and underlies the fullest surviving creation account.

### Enuma Elish

The Babylonian epic **Enuma Elish** ("When on high...") is a seven-tablet poem composed in the Old Babylonian period (early 2nd millennium BCE), though all surviving copies date to the 1st millennium BCE. Marduk, patron god of Babylon, is the hero in most copies (replaced by Ashur in an Assyrian recension). The poem internally still calls Marduk "the Enlil of the gods" at one point — textual evidence Roux uses to argue the original hero was Enlil, before Marduk's cult "usurped the rank and prerogatives" of the Sumerian god (Roux's inference, attributed).

Narrative (quoted opening lines given by Roux): Apsu (fresh waters), Tiamat (salt waters), and Mummu (clouds — an identification Roux himself flags with a "?") originally formed one commingled body before any gods existed. The first gods to emerge were Lahmu and Lahamu (representing silt), then Anshar and Kishar (sky/earth horizons); Anshar and Kishar beget Anu, who begets Ea (Enki). Younger, "turbulent and noisy" gods born from Apsu and Tiamat disturb their parents, who plot to destroy them; Ea paralyzes Mummu with a spell and slays the sleeping Apsu, then founds his own temple on the *apsû* and, with his wife Damkina, fathers Marduk — described in quoted verse as having four eyes, four ears, fire-breathing speech, and enormous stature. Tiamat, enraged, raises an army of dragons and serpents under her son Kingu; the gods are terrified until Marduk agrees to fight on condition of being made king. Granted kingship at a banquet, Marduk arms himself with bow, lightning, flood-storm, the four winds, and a net; he defeats Tiamat's army, captures Kingu, kills Tiamat, and splits her body to form the sky and the underworld/earth's underside. He then fixes the courses of sun, moon, and stars, and creates mankind (quoted verse: "I will establish a savage, 'man' shall be his name... He shall be charged with the service of the gods that they might be at ease") from the blood of the executed Kingu, on Ea's advice. The 600 gods are divided, 300 to heaven and 300 to earth; the gods build Marduk's temple Esagila in Babylon and "proclaim his fifty names."

Roux reads Enuma Elish as reframing creation as the end-point of a cosmic battle (Order vs. Chaos, Good vs. Evil) rather than a single god's free act — a distinct philosophical move he attributes to the poem itself — and ties its imagery of primordial confusion between sea, sky, and land to the real observed landscape at the mouth of the Shatt-al-Arab (misty mornings blending sea, sky, and land), an environmental/experiential explanation for the myth's origin that is explicitly Roux's own interpretive claim, not established fact. He also argues the myth functioned for Babylonians as a non-rational explanation of cosmic origin, of mankind's servitude to the gods, of humanity's innate wickedness (born from Kingu's evil blood), and of Marduk's (originally Enlil's) legitimacy — and that it was recited in full on the fourth day of the New Year (*akitu*) Festival precisely because Babylonians believed the cosmic struggle between Order and Chaos was never permanently resolved (attributed; see below).

## Life, Death, and Destiny

Roux describes divine access as graded like human social hierarchy: kings dealt with major gods (Marduk), while commoners and peasants dealt with minor agricultural deities (Ashnan the barley-god, Shumuqan the cattle-god) or occasion-specific deities (Gula, goddess of childbirth; Pasag, protector of travelers), or through one's personal god or the clergy in emergencies. Mesopotamians related to their gods, in Roux's phrase, "as servants look to good masters" — submission and fear combined with genuine admiration and love; obedience to divine will was the paramount virtue and chief civic duty (offerings, temple attendance, care of the dead, prayer, penance, taboo observance); sin brought severe punishment. Roux explicitly cautions against viewing this religion as "purely formal," citing emotionally genuine hymns and prayers.

**Ethical content:** divine favor went to those living a "good life" — kindness, compassion, righteousness, sincerity, justice, and respect for law and order — illustrated by a quoted excerpt from a Babylonian "Counsel of Wisdom" ("To the feeble show kindness... Do not insult the downtrodden...").

**Rewards and limits:** piety brought divine help and protection, comfort in distress, health, social standing, wealth, many children, long life, and happiness — but immortality was explicitly denied to humans, the subject of myths explaining this denial (the legend of Adapa; the Gilgamesh cycle — see [[sumerian-king-list]] for the historicity question around Gilgamesh). Quoted verse: "Only the gods live for ever under the sun / As for mankind, numbered are their days / Whatever they achieve is but wind."

**Afterlife beliefs**, reconstructed mainly from "Inanna's Descent" and the Sumerian Gilgamesh cycle, are described by Roux as "scanty and often contradictory." Burial goods indicate a belief that the dead needed possessions, food, and drink from the living. The underworld ("land of no return") was ruled by Ereshkigal and her husband Nergal (god of war and pestilence); the dead crossed a river by ferry — Roux draws an explicit comparison to the Greek Hades — and removed their clothing; conditions were wretched (quoted verse: "Where dust is their food, clay their sustenance; Where they see no light and dwell in darkness..."). Other sources suggest the sun-god Utu illuminated and judged the underworld's dead during his nightly transit, implying some differentiated treatment. Roux's own assessment: "the Sumerian idea of hell was as vague as ours," much of the literature being "poetical embroidery on a loose theme."

**Theodicy.** The problem of the innocent suffering is addressed in the Babylonian poem *Ludlul bêl nēmeqi* ("I will praise the Lord of Wisdom" — the "Babylonian Job"), quoted at length ("Who knows the will of the gods in heaven?..."), in which a ruined, diseased, formerly prosperous man is eventually pitied and saved by Marduk. Roux interprets Babylonian "pessimism" as metaphysical rather than ethical in character, rooted in the objectively unpredictable and violent natural environment of the Tigris-Euphrates valley (flood, drought, sandstorm) — his own explanatory thesis linking environment to religious mood. Per the wiki's evidence-class rules, because Roux asserts a mechanism (not just a correlation), this should be treated as `contributed_to`, attributed to Roux, not as a wiki-voice causal fact.

## The Akitu (New Year) Festival

The *akitu*, held each spring especially at Babylon, combined the ritual reenactment of Creation with the king's ceremonial reinstatement, culminating in an assembly of the gods who "decreed the Destinies" — believed necessary to secure another year's cosmic and agricultural order. As reconstructed for the first millennium BCE, the festival synthesized two older strands: an ancient fertility cult with seasonal feasts and the Sacred Marriage ceremony (attested only in certain cities, up to the first half of the 2nd millennium BCE), and a cosmogony developed by Nippur theologians in which Enlil creates the world after defeating Tiamat and Chaos; in the historical festival, Marduk fuses Enlil's role as champion/king of the gods with his own identity as Babylon's fertilizing city-god (attributed).

The festival began on "zagmuk" in the month of Nisan (March–April) and lasted 11–12 days, documented in fragmentary ritual tablets covering roughly the first six days plus isolated later details (days 9, 11, 12):

- **Day 2:** the *sheshgallu* (chief priest) performs purification and a secret prayer to Marduk.
- **Day 3:** two apotropaic wooden statuettes (wielding serpent and scorpion) are made.
- **Day 4:** the *sheshgallu* recites the Enuma Elish in full.
- **Day 5:** a temple purification rite (a *mashmashu* priest with censer, torch, and Tigris water and cedar resin); a "scapegoat" sheep is beheaded and its parts cast into the river along with the officiants, who then leave the city until the festival's end. Later on Day 5 the king undergoes a **"negative confession"** ritual before Marduk's statue — surrendering his royal insignia to the *sheshgallu*, being struck on the cheek, and reciting a denial of wrongdoing ("I did not sin, lord of the countries... I did not destroy Babylon..."); the priest reassures him, restores his insignia, and strikes him again — whether tears flow is read as an omen of the god's favor or wrath.
- **Day 6:** the god Nabû arrives from Barsippa by boat; the two "statuettes of evil" are decapitated and burned.
- **Day 9:** the king "takes the hand" of Marduk (a recurring phrase used across the corpus as shorthand for royal legitimacy) and installs him with the other gods in the *ubshukkinna* chapel; a grand procession of divine statues, led by Marduk's chariot, proceeds down the Processional Way, out through the Ishtar Gate, to the *bît akîtu* (a garden temple), where the gods remain three days.
- **Day 11:** the gods return to Esagila to again "decree the Destinies of the Land."
- **Day 12:** gods and officiants disperse to their home cities and temples.

Roux frames the festival's stakes in explicitly attributed, interpretive terms: "It was felt that mankind shared in the great renewal undergone by nature, that the past was abolished, that the cosmos momentarily reverted to chaos, that the fate of the country depended upon the judgement pronounced by the gods." The phrase "took the hand of Bel/Marduk" recurs later as a marker of political—ritual legitimacy in Babylon, performed even by foreign rulers such as the Achaemenid Cambyses (538 BCE, as viceroy) — see [[chaldaeans]].

## Operationalizations

"Mesopotamian religion" is not a native category but an etically reconstructed one; how
it is operationalized therefore shapes what it names. Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux
1992]], the reconstruction is assembled from several distinct evidence classes, which do
not always agree: **textual** (myths, hymns, prayers, incantations, god-lists, rituals,
omen-letters — chiefly from the temple library of [[nippur|Nippur]] and the palace
libraries of Assur and [[nineveh|Nineveh]], surviving copies dating c. 1900 BCE to the
last pre-Christian centuries); **iconographic** (deities and mythological scenes on
[[uruk-culture|Uruk]]/Jemdat Nasr [[cuneiform|cylinder seals]] and later reliefs, used to
infer that some gods were already worshipped before the textual record); and
**architectural** (temple-rebuilding continuity at [[eridu|Eridu]] taken as evidence of
cultic continuity from the Ubaid period). Because each class is silent where the others
speak, any single reconstruction of "the pantheon" is a scholarly synthesis, not a
transcription of indigenous doctrine.

## Critiques and Limitations

- This entire reconstruction rests on a single-author secondary synthesis (Roux 1992) of primary texts of very different genres and dates (myths, hymns, omen-letters, royal inscriptions) spanning nearly two millennia; treat any specific claim about "Sumerian religion" as a claim about the *earliest attested layer* of a tradition that was itself continuously reworked.
- Continuity claims (Sumerian religion "inherited... with little modification" by later peoples) are Roux's own historiographic judgment and should not be read as implying the four groups (Sumerians, Amorites, Kassites, Assyrians, Chaldaeans) were a single ethnic or genetic population — religious continuity is a cultural-transmission claim only (four-way non-identity rule).
- Interpretive claims linking environment to religious "pessimism," or diffusionist claims about Tammuz/Adonis transmission to Greece, are explicitly speculative in Roux's own framing and are recorded here as attributed positions, not findings.

## Religion as Politics (Postgate 1994, attributed)

Where Roux's synthesis reconstructs pantheon, cosmology, and ritual as belief content, [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] adds a documentary, institutional argument: in early Mesopotamia religious ideology and political ideology were not separable spheres — "religious" acts were routinely political acts, and political events required theological explanation.

**The god as owner of the city-state.** Land was, in theory, owned by the city's patron deity, with the ruler and temple staff acting as the deity's stewards and agents. Formal offerings made by rulers to temples — recorded, for example, in stone bowl inscriptions by En-mebaragesi, Me-salim of Adab, and Lugal-zagesi at the Ekur (Enlil's temple at Nippur) — were themselves political acts constituting a public claim to regional hegemony, with temple personnel's acceptance functioning as tacit acknowledgment of that claim; the ruler making such an offering took a special "bur-gi" (bowl-presentation) name for the occasion. Postgate reads the underlying logic as a "circular concept": prosperity is treated as evidence of divine approval, and pleasing the gods is what secures prosperity — "the religious counterpart of the secular argument... success is self-justifying" (as of Postgate 1994).

**Temple-building and installation as political acts.** Temple-building was among the most substantial religious duties of a ruler and was staged as a communal undertaking: rulers are depicted molding the first brick or carrying the first construction-basket (Ur-Nanše, Ur-Nammu), while the wider population and outside labor contingents did the bulk of the work — Gudea's reconstruction of Ningirsu's temple Eninnu at Girsu, undertaken (per Gudea's own account) to rescue Lagash from a water shortage, imported materials such as timber and lapis lazuli explicitly for the temple's glorification rather than the ruler's private display. The Sacred Marriage rite (see above, Roux's account) likewise carried an explicit political payload in the royal hymns Postgate cites: in the hymn to Iddin-Dagan, the goddess Inanna "decrees the shepherdship of (all) countries as his destiny" for the king performing the rite — the ritual union functioning as a legitimation ceremony as much as a fertility rite (as of Postgate 1994).

**Theological handling of political collapse.** Because the Mesopotamian pantheon was shared across cities, a single city's political disaster posed a theological problem — a patron god could not simply "fail." Postgate contrasts two documented resolutions: the fall of Akkad is explained in the surviving literary tradition as the goddess Ištar having *deserted* the city (not having been defeated), with blame placed on Naram-Sin's impiety in ignoring divine omens; the collapse of the Ur III state, by contrast, carries no such blame and is instead framed as an "unaccountable decision" of the high gods Anu and Enlil. Postgate treats this asymmetry — assigning royal culpability in one collapse narrative but not the other — as evidence that theological explanation of political disaster was worked out case by case, not according to a fixed doctrine (as of Postgate 1994; see [[state-formation]]).

These findings connect directly to the king's position between temple and palace authority (see [[kingship]], [[the-state]]) and to the historiographic dispute over how far temple economic and political control extended in the earliest Sumerian city-states ([[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]]).
