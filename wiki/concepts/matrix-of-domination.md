# Matrix of Domination

## Definition as Coined

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]].) The **matrix of domination** is the
overall organization of hierarchical power relations in any society — "the overall social
organization within which intersecting oppressions originate, develop, and are contained."
A specific matrix has two features: (1) a particular arrangement of intersecting
oppressions ([[intersectionality]] — race, class, gender, sexuality, citizenship,
ethnicity, age); and (2) a particular organization of its **domains of power**. Where
intersectionality names *which* systems interlock, the matrix names *how* they are
organized, and it is historically and geographically specific — Collins contrasts Senegal,
the U.S., and the U.K. as differently structured matrices sharing the same categories.

## The Four Domains of Power

Developed most fully in the 2nd edition (Ch. 12), the framework distinguishes four
interrelated domains (all attributed to Collins):

| Domain | Function | Example | Site of resistance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Structural** | *organizes* oppression through interlocking institutions (law, labor markets, schools, housing, banking) | segregation legitimated by *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896); post-*Brown* "rhetoric of color-blindness" | legislative reform, class actions, mass social movements |
| **Disciplinary** | *manages* oppression through bureaucratic hierarchy and surveillance (drawing on Foucault 1979) | organizational rules that regulate without explicit racism/sexism | "insider resistance" — working the cracks of bureaucracy |
| **Hegemonic** | *justifies* oppression through ideology, culture, and consciousness (schools, religion, media) | recycling [[controlling-images]] — "yesterday's jezebel becomes today's 'hoochie'" | counter-hegemonic knowledge; "the power of a free mind" |
| **Interpersonal** | *shapes* everyday lived experience and individual consciousness | routine, often unnoticed discriminatory practices | everyday resistance, as numerous as individuals |

Collins summarizes: "The structural domain organizes oppression, whereas the disciplinary
domain manages it. The hegemonic domain justifies oppression, and the interpersonal domain
influences everyday lived experience." A matrix, she stresses, "contains few pure victims
or oppressors" — each person draws varying penalty and privilege from multiple simultaneous
systems (echoing Audre Lorde's "piece of the oppressor which is planted deep within each of
us" and Toni Cade Bambara's "revolution begins with the self").

## Semantic History

The bare phrase appears in the 1st edition (1990); the fully articulated four-domains
model is a 2nd-edition (2000) development, which Collins ties to her companion work
*Fighting Words* (1998).

## Operationalizations

The matrix is deployed qualitatively — mapping a given injustice across the four domains to
locate where resistance is possible — rather than as a quantitative index. It underwrites
Collins's argument that resistance ([[black-feminist-thought|activism]]) must show
"comparable complexity" to domination.

## Applications

Applied throughout *Black Feminist Thought* to work/family, controlling images, sexuality,
motherhood, activism, and — extended via **nation** — the transnational "global gendered
apartheid" (Antrobus 1995).

## Critiques and Limitations

The framework is descriptive-organizational rather than predictive; critics note it
catalogues domains without specifying their causal weighting. Its reliance on Foucault for
the disciplinary domain sits somewhat uneasily beside its structural-Marxist inheritance.
