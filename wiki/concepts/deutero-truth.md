# Deutero-Truth

## Definition as Coined

In *[[rappaport-ritual-religion-making-humanity-1999|Ritual and Religion in the Making of Humanity]]* (1999, esp. Ch. 9), [[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]] adapts [[bateson-gregory|Bateson]]'s **deutero-learning** (second-order / set-learning — learning *how* to learn and what to expect under classes of circumstances) to name **deutero-truths**: propositions deutero-learned in everyday experience. Their validity, he argues, depends on communal acceptance and enactment; acting on them increases the likelihood that the conditions they assume will prevail (self-validation).

Examples (attributed): fatalism, instrumentalism, competitiveness as integrated abstract assumptions — related to "Basic Personality," "National Character," and "ethos" vocabularies. He notes affinity to Bourdieu's *habitus* without equating programs.

## Semantic History

Bateson's learning categories → Rappaport's contrast with truths of **[[sanctity]]**. Deutero-truths are high-level and relatively invulnerable to ordinary criticism, but they are **not** full functional equivalents of sanctification: they cannot by themselves specify crimes, legitimate authority, or reciprocity equivalencies, and they lack the liturgical ground of unquestionableness that [[ultimate-sacred-postulates]] provide. In Ch. 11, *doxa* / *idia phronesis* (private opinion, calculation of private advantage) are treated as deutero-truths of individuals antithetical to [[logos-rappaport|Logos]]'s unifying order.

## Operationalizations

Distinguish: (1) USP / sacred truths established in [[liturgical-order|liturgical]] acceptance; (2) deutero-truths acquired in mundane interaction; (3) didactic specifics that may become deutero-truths only after routine enactment. Ask whether a claim's force rests on ritual association with USPs or on experiential self-validation.

## Applications

- Explains orderliness that is neither pure coercion nor pure sacred postulate.
- Diagnoses modern inversion when low-order material assumptions usurp the place of high-order sacred truths ([[adaptive-structure]], Ch. 14 "breaking of the Holy").
- Bridges Batesonian cybernetics and Rappaport's religion theory without collapsing sanctity into habitus.

## Critiques and Limitations

The term is Rappaport's synthesis, not a consensus ethological or sociological construct. Critics of functional/adaptive religion theory will read "self-validating" as circular; interpretive anthropologists may prefer *habitus* or ideology without the sanctity contrast. Always attribute; do not treat as a finding independent of the 1999 program.
