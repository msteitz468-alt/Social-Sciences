# Culturology

## Definition as Coined

[[white-leslie-a|Leslie A. White]] named **culturology** the science of culture: culture treated as a real order of phenomena with its own structure and regularities, to be explained by cultural determinants rather than reduced to psychology or biology. In *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|The Evolution of Culture]]* he holds that beliefs, institutions, and technologies form sectors of cultural systems; the culturologist formulates laws of cultural systems as physicists and biologists formulate laws of theirs (Ch. 1; index: "Culturology, 28").

## Semantic History

White's label never became the discipline's ordinary self-name (anthropology/ethnology remained standard), but the **program** — culture as extrasomatic system, anti-reductionism, evolutionary regularities — entered mid-century neo-evolutionary debate. [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] engaged White as the "universal evolution" / culture-in-general pole opposite [[multilinear-evolution]] and [[cultural-ecology]]. Adjacent lineages: Kroeber's [[superorganic]]; Durkheimian social facts (White cites the parallel). Later cultural materialism keeps energy and infrastructure priorities while often reintroducing etic behavioral science White would have rejected as psychologism.

## Operationalizations

- **Energy × technology** comparisons across cultural systems ([[energy-law-of-cultural-evolution]]).
- Sector analysis of cultural systems: technological, sociological, ideological (and attitudinal) components (White Ch. 1).
- Culturological solutions to classic problems (e.g. incest taboo explained by cooperative-group size and social organization rather than innate aversion — Ch. 5).

## Applications

Organizing principle of *Evolution of Culture* Parts One and Two; earlier *Science of Culture* (1949) and the essay "The Symbol."

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics treat "culturology" as rebranding evolutionary anthropology with physics envy; particularists deny culture-level laws; psychological and cognitive anthropology reject the exclusion of mind. The term itself is largely historical; the live issues survive under other names ([[universal-evolution]], cultural materialism, dual inheritance theory).
