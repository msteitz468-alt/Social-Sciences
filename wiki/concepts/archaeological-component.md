# Archaeological Component

Minimal **site-level** unit in American culture-historical archaeology: the manifestation of a given [[archaeological-phase|phase]] at one locus. Canonical mid-century statement: [[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] and [[phillips-philip|Phillips]], *[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory in American Archaeology]]* (1958), building on W. C. McKern.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — McKern 1939, as quoted by Willey & Phillips 1958.)*

McKern defined the component as “the manifestation of any given focus at a specific site.” It is **not** itself a taxonomic rank in the Midwestern system; the focus (≈ phase) is the basic unit, comprising components. The term distinguishes a **site** (which may hold several foreign occupations) from a **single specified cultural manifestation** at that site. Several culturally variant components may occur at one site.

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958.)*

Working assumption: no phase “worthy of the name” fails to appear in more than one component. In practice a phase is often first defined from a single component (a site or a level), with the expectation that later components will modify the definition. It is theoretically and actually possible for a phase to consist of a single component — with social implications they treat carefully (component ≈ community is a working analogy, not a demonstration).

## Semantic History

McKern’s Midwestern Taxonomic System made “component” nearly universal in eastern North American archaeology. Willey & Phillips retained it as the bottom of their unit hierarchy (component → phase → maximum culture/civilization), replacing McKern’s “focus” with **phase** for the next higher unit. Later settlement archaeology and single-context recording densified what a “component” means operationally (activity areas, formation processes) without displacing the term.

## Operationalizations

| Question | McKern / W&P practice |
|---|---|
| What is at this site? | Isolate components (occupations) before equating sites as wholes |
| Multi-component sites | Stratigraphy or seriation separates foreign occupations |
| Link to phase | Shared physiognomic trait complexes across components |

## Applications

- Eastern North American area chronologies and site monographs (McKern tradition).
- Any multi-occupation site report that separates “components” before regional synthesis — the everyday vocabulary of Americanist field reporting.

## Critiques and Limitations

Willey & Phillips themselves note that equating component with ethnographic “community” (Murdock) is logical on paper but fails in practice: archaeology cannot know whether grouped components would have been one society, one language, or one self-recognized people. The component remains an **etic material unit**. See also [[pots-and-peoples-debate]].
