# Devil Contract

## Definition as Coined

In the southern [[cauca-valley-afro-colombians|Cauca Valley]] of the 1970s, as reported by [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]], some male sugarcane wage workers were said to enter **secret contracts with the devil** to increase production and thus wages. Taussig treats the complex as an emic category he then interprets through Marxian [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]]: short-term monetary gain under wage labour purchased at the cost of long-term barrenness and death.

Attributed consequences and motifs (Taussig 1980, reporting local claims of the 1970s):

- Land bought with devil-wages becomes sterile; livestock dies (*secar*); cane fails to ratoon.
- Money spent only on luxuries (butter, sunglasses, fancy shirt, liquor) — not productive capital.
- The contractor dies prematurely and in pain; may become a *muñeco*/puppet of the devil.
- Contract made in secrecy with a sorcerer and figurine (*muñeco*); black-cat / books-of-magic variants reported.
- Sharing devil money with friends may allow them to use it as ordinary money.
- Foremen believe and fire high producers; militants and political leaders also hold the belief.
- Contracts occur in **proletarian plantation labour**, not on own peasant plots, peon-for-peasant work, or nonmarket coastal contexts under use-value ethos.
- **Men only** for production contracts; women's magic targets love/reproduction (*oeconomia* logic).

## Operationalizations

Qualitative presence of the lore; sex-specificity (men); restriction to wage cane work. Not surveyed as a rate in the source.

## Applications

- Type-case for magical belief as **critique of capitalist production** rather than anxiety-magic ([[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]).
- Paired with [[baptism-of-money]] as Cauca twin superstitions endorsing the logic of use-/exchange-value contradiction (Taussig's Part II conclusion).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Interpretive leap from lore to systematic anti-capitalist critique is Taussig's thesis, not a proven native theory.
- Secondary pairing with Bolivian Tio risks false equivalence.
- Gendered restriction needs explanation beyond the book’s main binary.
