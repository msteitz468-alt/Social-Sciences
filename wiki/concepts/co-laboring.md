# Co-laboring (Ethnographic)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015|Earth Beings]]* (2015), [[de-la-cadena-marisol|Marisol de la Cadena]] names her method **co-laboring**: working with Mariano and Nazario Turpo so that analysis is composed *with* them as thinkers, not extracted *from* them as informants. Story 1 stands in for a conventional introduction: agreeing to remember, translating, and carefully co-laboring. She distinguishes co-labor from simple “collaboration” branding — the intent is to cancel the bifurcation that assigns theory to the anthropologist and culture/belief to the local (attributed).

## Semantic History

Collaborative, dialogical, and reciprocal ethnography have longer histories (and colonial ironies). De la Cadena’s version is specifically tied to [[partial-connections]] and to interlocutors whose practices enroll [[earth-beings]] that her epistemic tools cannot access directly. Related: Viveiros de Castro on anthropology as translation; Asad on cultural translation.

## Operationalizations

Practices described (attributed): shared work on Mariano’s archive; coca *k’intu* openings that welcome tirakuna into conversation; refusal to demand isomorphic explanation (*suerte* episode); writing as composed stories rather than purified “them” vs “us”; limits of what each can learn already present in what the other reveals.

## Applications

Central method claim of *Earth Beings*; template for ontological-turn ethnography that takes Andean interlocutors as conceptual partners. Contrasts with museum/tourism “collaboration” that multiculturalizes earth-beings as culture (Stories 5–6) — related word, different politics.

## Critiques and Limitations

Power asymmetry remains (academic capital, book authorship, language). De la Cadena flags her “selfish” request that Mariano help her think. Nazario’s death (2007) ends the living co-labor; the book is composed without him (Preface).
