# Archaeological Feature

An **archaeological feature** is a **non-portable** humanly made or modified component of a site or landscape — in essence a non-portable [[artifact]]. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 reject broadening “artifact” to cover hearths, postholes, and storage pits, preferring the separate term **features**, which combine into **structures** (buildings of all kinds: houses, granaries, palaces, temples).

## Definition as Coined

Textbook definition (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 2, attributed):

> Some researchers broaden the meaning of the term “artifact” to include all humanly modified components of a site or landscape, such as hearths, postholes, and storage pits — but these are more usefully described as **features**, defined in essence as **non-portable artifacts**.

Simple features may alone, or with hearths, floors, ditches, etc., evidence complex features or **structures**. Features are typically left in situ only temporarily; as excavation proceeds they are destroyed and must be fully recorded (description, scaled drawings, photography, digital media) as part of the total site archive (Ch. 3).

## Semantic History

- **Field archaeology tradition:** Features as the architectural and cut-fill grammar of sites (pits, postholes, ditches, walls).
- **Wheeler box-grid / open-area methods:** Different strategies for correlating feature relationships in plan and section.
- **Aerial and remote sensing:** Negative features (ditches, pits) and positive features (banks, mounds, walls) produce crop-marks, soil-marks, and geophysical anomalies — more features discovered via crop-marks than any other prospection form (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 3). Smallest features (postholes) rarely show except on clearest large-scale images.
- **Geoarchaeology / micromorphology:** Distinguishes humanly produced or modified sedimentary features from natural ones; experimental earthworks (Overton Down) show banks/ditches can “fossilize” in relief within ~16 years.
- **Single-context recording / Harris matrix:** Features as stratigraphic units in sequence (related method pages owned elsewhere).

## Operationalizations

| Detection / recording mode | Feature signal |
|---|---|
| Surface survey | Exposed walls, platforms, earthworks, artifact rings around buried features |
| Aerial / satellite | Crop-marks over ditches vs walls; LIDAR upstanding relief |
| Geophysics | Magnetic, resistivity, GPR anomalies |
| Geochemistry | Phosphate peaks over stalls, drains, hearths |
| Excavation | Cut, fill, interface; associations of artifacts/ecofacts within fills |
| Archive | Numbered feature records; total station / GIS coordinates |

Primary vs secondary context applies to feature fills: later disturbance can remix contents.

## Applications

- Reconstructing buildings from posthole plans and floor deposits.
- Activity-area analysis (hearths, butchery, storage).
- Settlement layout without full excavation (geophysics + surface features).
- CRM significance: features structure eligibility and mitigation design.
- Environmental archaeology: buried plow marks, ditches, field systems as land-use features (South Street Neolithic ard marks; Kuk drainage networks).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Natural disturbances (periglacial cracking, leveled modern boundaries, quarries) **mimic** archaeological features on air photos; experienced local analysts needed.
- Augering/probing can damage fragile features and artifacts.
- Feature fills are not pure behavioral snapshots — [[formation-processes]] and [[taphonomy]] apply.
- Terminological overlap with “structure,” “context,” and “stratigraphic unit” varies by national recording tradition.
- Once excavated, features exist only as records — archive quality is the feature’s afterlife.
