# Shotgun House

Vernacular house type that [[deetz-james|Deetz]] (1996), following **John Vlach**, treats as the most explicitly **African** architectural form in America — end point of a path from West Africa through the West Indies to New Orleans, then inland along transport routes.

## Definition as Coined

*(Formal differences as Deetz/Vlach state them.)*

1. Gable end faces the thoroughfare (Anglo houses typically long axis parallel to the road).
2. Entry through the gable end (not long-side/central passage).
3. Rooms in a front-to-back line (often three or more), doors aligned — folk etymology "fire a shotgun through"; Vlach prefers Yoruba *to-gun* ("place of assembly") as name source.
4. Deep front porch as intermediate room-space; relatively small rooms ("architecture of intimacy," Vlach).

## Semantic History

Folk American type name; scholarly African and Haitian connections advanced by Vlach (as Deetz narrates). Haitian urban shotgun: Arawak porch/front-entry elements + French building techniques + Yoruba two-room plan = creolized Caribbean form (Vlach via Deetz).

## Operationalizations

Plan and module dimensions compared to West African and Haitian examples; distributional history (New Orleans after 1810 free people of color from Haiti; railroad expansion to Mississippi, West Virginia coalfields).

## Applications

- Deetz's continuum of African American dwellings: shotgun and **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]** (free expression) ↔ SC slave houses ↔ Chesapeake earthfast quarters with African *additions*.
- Contrast with **[[georgian-worldview|Georgian]]** bilateral symmetry and privacy segmentation in Anglo housing.

## Critiques and Limitations

Diffusion path and Yoruba name etymology are argued positions, not wiki-voice facts. Multiple American narrow-house traditions can converge formally. Treat African derivation as **attributed claim** requiring independent architectural history.
