# Institutionalization of Class Conflict

## Definition as Coined

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] argues (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, Ch. II and VI–VIII) that advanced industrial ("post-capitalist") societies have **institutionalized** class conflict: rather than abolishing classes or driving toward revolutionary polarization, they embed conflict in recognized organizations and procedures — trade unions, employer associations, collective bargaining, political parties, industrial democracy — which regulate conflict and reduce its violence without eliminating it.

## Semantic History

The idea joins industrial-relations research on collective bargaining with sociological class theory. It is Dahrendorf's principal answer to why Marx's revolutionary prognosis failed in the West while class-like conflict persisted. Related citizenship arguments (Marshall) and "end of ideology" / affluent-society literatures are surveyed in the book as partial allies with unsolved problems remaining.

## Operationalizations

Indicators include legal recognition of unions, established bargaining machinery, party competition articulating class interests, and the "institutional isolation" of industrial conflict from the wider political order (Ch. VII).

## Applications

Core of the post-capitalist application chapters: industrial conflict (enterprise authority, industrial democracy) and political conflict (democratic vs totalitarian channeling of authority conflict).

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics (positions) argue institutionalization can mask continuing structural inequality, depend on historically specific postwar settlements, and fail under deindustrialization or weakened unions — empirical horizons beyond Dahrendorf's mid-century cases.
