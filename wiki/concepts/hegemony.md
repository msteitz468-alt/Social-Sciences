# Hegemony

> [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci's]] concept, reworked for anthropology by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991). This page states the concept in **attributed voice** — its definition is itself contested.

## Definition as Coined

In [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci's]] *Prison Notebooks*, hegemony names the "cultural leadership" by which a dominant group secures consent — its worldview becoming "common sense" — rather than ruling by coercion alone. The Comaroffs note that Gramsci nowhere defines it precisely (it functions as "a relatively empty sign"), and decline to adjudicate "the 'real' meaning" as "an exercise in futility."

## The Comaroffs' reworking (attributed)

The Comaroffs take hegemony to refer to "that order of signs and practices, relations and distinctions, images and epistemologies — drawn from a historically situated cultural field — that come to be taken-for-granted as the natural and received shape of the world." Paraphrasing [[habitus|Bourdieu]]: it consists of "things that go without saying because, being axiomatic, they come without saying." Its power lies in what it *silences* — "what it prevents people from thinking and saying" — and so "hegemony, at its most effective, is mute."

The load-bearing move is the **hegemony/[[ideology|ideology]] distinction**:

> "Hegemony homogenizes, ideology articulates. Hegemony, at its most effective, is mute; by contrast … all the while, ideology babbles on."

Hegemony is the naturalized part of a dominant worldview that "has hidden itself in orthodoxy" and "no more appears as ideology at all"; [[ideology]] is the articulate, contestable expression of a particular group's interest. The two exist in "reciprocal interdependence," and hegemony stands to ideology "as form to content" — a matter of degree along a "[[consciousness|chain of consciousness]]," not a difference of kind. Once its contradictions are exposed and "the ineffable is put into words, hegemony becomes something other than itself. It turns into ideology and counterideology."

## Semantic History

From Russian Social-Democratic debates → [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci's]] cultural theory of consent → Raymond Williams's "dominant system of lived meanings" and Stuart Hall's cultural studies → the Comaroffs' anthropological usage, which fuses it with [[habitus|Bourdieu]] and Marx's two theories of ideology (see [[false-consciousness]]). The Comaroffs insist hegemony is **never total** (Williams), "always threatened by the vitality that remains in the forms of life it thwarts," and so "a process as much as a thing."

## Operationalizations

Measured negatively and historically: what a community treats as unremarkable "custom" or "convention," and — diagnostically — what *moves out* of the hegemonic into open ideological dispute (the Comaroffs' example: the denaturalizing of race and gender discrimination in the twentieth century). Making hegemony requires control over "modes of symbolic production" — schooling, ritual, socialization, bodily discipline, style — sustained until signs cease to be remarked.

## Applications

The organizing concept of the [[colonization-of-consciousness]] thesis and of *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's imprecision in Gramsci has licensed incompatible uses; Laclau and Mouffe's fully discursive reading was criticized (Geras) for "robbing the concept of any principle of historical constraint." Charges of functionalism and of unfalsifiability recur. See [[ideology]] and the [[false-consciousness]] page for the Marxian lineage.
