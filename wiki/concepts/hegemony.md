# Hegemony

> Primary formulation from [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]]'s *[[gramsci-selections-from-prison-notebooks-1971|Selections from the Prison Notebooks]]* (1971 Eng.; *Quaderni* 1929–35). Later anthropological and cultural-studies reworkings are **secondary** and attributed separately. **All mechanism-and-meaning claims in attributed voice.**

## Definition as Coined (Gramsci)

Gramsci does not offer a single dictionary definition; the concept operates across the Notebooks as the leadership/consent pole of class power. Load-bearing primary formulations in the 1971 selection:

**1. Intellectuals as hegemony's deputies** ("The Intellectuals"): Two superstructural levels — [[civil-society]] (ensemble of "private" organisms) and political society/the State — correspond to **hegemony** and **direct domination**. Intellectuals exercise "the subaltern functions of social hegemony and political government," comprising:

1. The **"spontaneous" consent** of the great masses to the general direction imposed on social life by the dominant fundamental group — historically caused by the prestige (and confidence) arising from that group's position in production.
2. The **apparatus of state coercive power** that legally enforces discipline on those who do not consent — constituted in anticipation of crises of command when spontaneous consent fails.

**2. State formula** (State and Civil Society):

> "State = political society + civil society, in other words hegemony protected by the armour of coercion."

**3. Factory formula** ("Americanism and Fordism"):

> "Hegemony here is born in the factory and requires for its exercise only a minute quantity of professional political and ideological intermediaries."

**4. Crisis of hegemony** (= crisis of authority): when the ruling class is no longer "leading" but only "dominant," exercising coercive force alone, the masses detach from traditional ideologies — the [[organic-crisis]] interregnum in which "the old is dying and the new cannot be born."

**5. Relations of force** (Modern Prince): hegemony appears as the third (political) moment of relations of force — ethico-political leadership, not only economic-corporate organisation; dual perspective of the Centaur (force/consent, authority/hegemony).

**6. East/West strategy:** In the West, hegemony is fought as [[war-of-position]] across civil society's trenches; in 1917 Russia, gelatinous civil society allowed war of manoeuvre against the State.

Editors (Hoare & Nowell Smith) and later readers note the term's relative openness in the fragmentary notebooks — a real textual fact, not a licence to invent meanings.

## The Comaroffs' reworking (attributed)

[[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991) take hegemony as "that order of signs and practices… that come to be taken-for-granted as the natural and received shape of the world" — "things that go without saying." Load-bearing distinction:

> "Hegemony homogenizes, ideology articulates. Hegemony, at its most effective, is mute; by contrast … ideology babbles on."

This is a **productive anthropological specification**, not a restatement of Gramsci's full political theory (party, war of position, State armour). The Comaroffs note Gramsci nowhere defines hegemony precisely and decline to adjudicate the "real" meaning as "an exercise in futility."

## Critiques and Limitations

- Textual openness in the notebooks licenses incompatible systems (Geras vs Laclau–Mouffe is one famous quarrel).
- Functionalism charge: consent explained as always-already serving dominance.
- Unfalsifiability charge when every stability = hegemony and every break = crisis of hegemony.
- Must not silently equate Gramsci, Comaroff, Hall, and Laclau–Mouffe — date and attribute.
- Four-way non-identity reminder: hegemonic culture ≠ language family ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people.

## Reflexivity

The concept's career tracks Western Marxism's shift from economic determinism toward culture and consent — itself a political history (defeat of post-WWI revolutions; fascism; Comintern; Eurocommunism; cultural studies). Prison censorship shaped vocabulary; the 1971 English selection shaped Anglophone reception more than the later complete critical edition.

## Scott’s rethink (*Weapons of the Weak*, 1985)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* 1985, Ch. 8. Secondary to Gramsci’s primary formulation above; do not overwrite Gramsci with Scott.)*

Scott treated “hegemony” (via Gramsci, Althusser, Femia, and related Marxist/cultural theory as he read them) as a claim that class rule works mainly through consent and ideological domination, with coercion backgrounded until consent fails — implying that external agencies (parties) must supply critical consciousness the masses cannot produce alone. Against this family of arguments, and against Moore/Hoggart/Piven-Cloward equations of inevitability with justice, Scott advanced **five theses** from the [[sedaka|Sedaka]] ethnography and comparative reflection:

1. Subordinates often **penetrate and demystify** prevailing ideology via daily material experience.
2. Theories of hegemony frequently **confound inevitability with justice** — subordinates may resign pragmatically without granting legitimacy.
3. Hegemonic ideology is an **idealization** that generates internal contradictions usable as critique in its own terms (e.g. social use of property, Islamic charity norms).
4. **Trade-union consciousness** is not merely Lenin’s obstacle to revolution but the only plausible basis for it.
5. Historically, **bearers of a new mode of production** (e.g. capitalists/mechanizing farmers) typically shatter older dominant norms; peasants and workers often defend earlier interpretations of those norms.

Methodological corollary: do not infer ideological consent from public compliance — see [[calculated-conformity]] and [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]]. Study: [[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]].

**1990 generalization** (*[[scott-domination-arts-resistance-1990|Domination and the Arts of Resistance]]*, vault-primary): Scott systematized the attack, distinguishing a **thick** theory (subordinates believe the values that subordinate them) from a **thin** theory (ideology secures only a sense of inevitability/resignation) and rejecting both — the thick against the record of slave, serf, and untouchable dissent; the thin against the record of subordinates repeatedly rebelling on *over*-estimates of their own power. He argued genuine hegemony requires conditions almost never met (prospective mobility into the dominant stratum, or total atomization and surveillance), and inverted Gramsci: subordinates are freer at the level of thought (safe offstage — the [[hidden-transcript]]) and more constrained at the level of action. Positions on all sides filed at [[hegemony-resistance-debate]].
