# Staff God

## Definition as Coined

**Staff God** is an etic label for a recurring Andean deity image: a frontal figure holding staffs, often with feline fangs, claws, and serpent or raptorial hair/brows — classically the Lanzón and related carvings at [[chavin-de-huantar|Chavín de Huántar]], and later ray-headed staff-bearers on Tiwanaku's Gateway of the Sun ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]; [[scarre-human-past-2013]]).

## Semantic History

Rowe's Chavín analyses made the type a horizon marker; subsequent work tracks variants into Paracas textiles (e.g. Karwa), Pukara, Wari, and Tiwanaku. Continuity claims are iconographic (`associated_with`), not proof of unbroken cult identity across millennia.

## Operationalizations

Presence/absence and stylistic attributes in stone, textile, and metal; use as proxy for "Early Horizon interaction sphere" — contested when over-extended.

## Applications

Moseley uses Staff God imagery to discuss Early Horizon northern-sphere integration and Middle Horizon highland imperial ideologies. Frontispiece of his 2001 book: female Staff God on painted cotton from Karwa.

## Critiques and Limitations

"Same god" readings can collapse distinct regional supernaturals; gender variants (Karwa female staff figures) complicate a single-deity model. Always attribute period and site.
