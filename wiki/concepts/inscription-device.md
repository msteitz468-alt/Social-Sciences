# Inscription Device

In *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]*, any item of apparatus or **configuration** of items that transforms a material substance into a **figure or diagram directly usable** by office-space workers in argument. Distinct from "machines" that only transform matter between states (centrifuge, evaporator) without producing a ready-to-argue inscription.

## Definition as Coined

Latour and Woolgar: an inscription device yields written output treated as having a direct relationship to "the original substance," so discussion brackets intervening material activity and costs. Examples in the Salk lab include assays (bioassay, radioimmunoassay, etc.) and purification cycles that produce peaks, spots, and slopes; amino-acid analyzers that yield letter codes (Glu, His…); further devices that order sequences.

## Semantic History

Coined in the laboratory-studies ethnography of 1979; later STS and ANT inherit and extend "inscription" apparatus. Bachelard's **phenomenotechnique** is an acknowledged related resource in the book.

## Operationalizations

Identify which instrument chains produce argument-ready traces; note that components alone (e.g. a counter) may not be inscription devices until configured into a usable assay.

## Applications

Laboratory ethnography; reconstruction of how substances become paper figures; cost accounting of fact production.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary between "machine" and "inscription device" is analytic, not emic; depends on whether output is "directly usable" in the office culture under study.
