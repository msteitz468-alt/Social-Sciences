# Authoritative Power

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]*, 1986, Ch. 1): **authoritative power** is power actually willed by groups and institutions — definite commands and conscious obedience. Contrasted with [[diffused-power|diffused power]] (spontaneous, decentered practices not explicitly commanded).

> "Authoritative power is actually willed by groups and institutions. It comprises definite commands and conscious obedience."

Requires a **logistical infrastructure** for commands, supplies, and surveillance (vs universal infrastructure for diffused power). Cross-classified with [[extensive-power]]/[[intensive-power]]: intensive+authoritative ≈ army command; extensive+authoritative ≈ militaristic empire.

## Semantic History

Distinct from Weberian legitimate domination (Mann downplays pure legitimacy/illegitimacy polarity because power is normally double-edged — organizational outflanking more than full value consensus). Authoritative ≠ necessarily legitimate. Political power in the [[iemp-model]] is characteristically centralized-territorial and geopolitical-diplomatic — authoritative territorial regulation — but real states mix authoritative and diffused means.

## Operationalizations

Command hierarchies, military orders, law with enforcement, bureaucracy, satrap/provincial magistracies, fiscal-military extraction. Reach measured via logistics (men, supplies, commands along route marches).

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Military power** — Paradigm of authoritative, concentrated-coercive organization; high command over own troops as intensive+authoritative cell.
- **Empires** — Army option and [[compulsory-cooperation]] as authoritative economic-military packages; Assyrian/Persian imperial administration; Roman legionary economy and magisterial provinces.
- **States** — Centralized territorial rule-making; medieval dual public authority of kings vs private magnate power; early modern permanent war state and public legislation.
- **Rome** — Second base of Roman power: "authoritative power of class culture" absorbing conquered elites (alongside compulsory cooperation).

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary with diffused power is ideal-typical; markets, nations, and religions mix both. Logistical ceilings mean extensive+authoritative claims often exceed real intensive penetration — Mann's standing critique of unitary imperial models.
