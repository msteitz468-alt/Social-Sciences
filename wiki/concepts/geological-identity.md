# Geological Identity

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed, Tilley 2010 Ch. 1 and Ch. 10.)* Analytic framing of *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes]]*: **geologies and topographies** are not mere environmental backdrop but materials and forms through which prehistoric (and modern interpretive) **identities** are constituted. To inhabit granite was, on Tilley's account, to have a different identity from chalk or pebbles — "placial" not merely spatial relationships.

## Semantic History

Series subtitle *Geologies, Topographies, Identities* states the program; builds on 1994 place/path phenomenology by making **lithology and landform type** comparative axes across southern England (Parts II–IV: chalk; pebble; sandstone/slate; granite).

## Operationalizations

Regional cases:
- Chalk — white exposures, limited water, flint/sarsen; [[stonehenge]], [[northern-cranborne-chase]], [[south-dorset-ridgeway]]
- Pebbles — unique multi-colour aesthetics; [[east-devon-pebblebeds]]
- Sandstone/slate — sparse rock, near-invisible monuments; [[exmoor]]
- Granite — tors as non-domesticated megaliths; [[bodmin-moor]], [[west-penwith]]

Ch. 10 rejects **geological determinism**: rock *invites creative response* unpredictable in advance; no predictive model transfers from one landscape to another.

## Applications

Organizes the six regional studies; densifies [[phenomenological-archaeology]] beyond single-region 1994 cases.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of essentialising rock→culture; modern aesthetic response may not map to prehistoric meaning; keep all identity claims attributed.
