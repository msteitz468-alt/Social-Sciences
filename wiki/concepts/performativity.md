# Performativity (Gender)

The analytical concept, introduced by [[butler-judith|Judith Butler]] in *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]* (1990), that **gender is performative**: it is constituted by the acts and gestures said to express it, rather than expressing a prior inner identity. Distinguish from J. L. Austin's linguistic "performative" (an utterance that does what it says), from which Butler borrows the term, and from the everyday sense of "performance" as a chosen act — a conflation Butler spent later work correcting. The wider framework built on this concept is [[gender-performativity]]. All statements below are **attributed** to Butler.

## Definition as Coined

Butler defined the mechanism thus: "what we take to be an internal essence of gender is manufactured through a sustained set of acts, posited through the gendered stylization of the body." There is, on this view, "no gender identity behind the expressions of gender; that identity is performatively constituted by the very 'expressions' that are said to be its results." Gender "is not a noun, but neither is it a set of free-floating attributes… the substantive effect of gender is performatively produced and compelled by the regulatory practices of gender coherence."

## Semantic History

- **Austin → Derrida → Butler.** The term descends from speech-act theory (the performative utterance), routed through Derrida's account of citation and iterability. Butler stated she "took my clue… from Jacques Derrida's reading of Kafka's 'Before the Law'."
- **1990 vs. 1999.** The 1990 text, Butler conceded, "sometimes reads as if gender is simply a self-invention." The 1999 preface recast performativity as "not a singular act, but a repetition and a ritual, which achieves its effects through its naturalization in the context of a body" — explicitly **not** voluntarist theatrical choice.
- **Linguistic vs. theatrical.** Butler noted her theory "waffles between understanding performativity as linguistic and casting it as theatrical," concluding (in *Excitable Speech*) that the two are "chiasmically" related.
- **Drift into general use.** In cultural theory "performativity" now travels well beyond gender (performative nationalism, performative markets); these uses invoke Butler's constitution-not-expression sense, often loosened back toward the voluntarism she rejected.

## Operationalizations

The concept is not measured; it is demonstrated interpretively. Its signature demonstration is **drag**: reading Esther Newton's *Mother Camp*, Butler argued drag stages a "double inversion" ("appearance is an illusion") that separates anatomical sex, gender identity, and gender performance, revealing "the original identity after which gender fashions itself is an imitation without an origin." Contrast the sociological operationalization of a neighboring intuition in [[doing-gender]], where gender is an interactional accomplishment assessable through [[sex-category|sex-categorization]] and accountability.

## Applications

Foundational to queer theory; widely applied in cultural anthropology, sociology of gender and sexuality, performance studies, and analyses of language and the body. It reframed the [[sex-gender-distinction-debate|sex/gender distinction]] and the politics of identity categories.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Voluntarism misread.** The drag illustration was widely taken to imply gender is freely chosen; Butler rebutted this and cautioned "parody by itself is not subversive."
- **Materiality.** Critics charged that performativity dissolves the material body into discourse; Butler answered in *Bodies That Matter* (1993).
- **Empirical distance.** Social scientists fault the concept's reliance on textual reading rather than systematic observation — the contrast with [[doing-gender]]'s empirical program is the standing methodological divide.
