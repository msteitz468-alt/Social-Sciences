## Definition as Coined

"Political indifference" is [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s term for being "a stranger to all
political symbols" — detached from prevailing symbols without forming new counter-attachments,
with personal desires and anxieties segregated from political meaning. In
*[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951) Mills argued (attributed) that widespread
indifference — not loyalty or insurgency — is "the major sign of both the impasse of liberalism
and the collapse of socialist hopes" and "the heart of the political malaise of our time." He
stressed that indifference need not be irrational: following Weber, it can be a *reasoned*
renunciation of political belief in a world experienced as meaningless.

## Semantic History

Mills built the concept by showing that both the liberal model of political consciousness (the
alert individual citizen) and the Marxist model (the class-conscious proletariat) share a
rationalist psychological assumption — that political consciousness naturally arises given
opportunity — and that neither can account for a stratum being passively *indifferent* rather
than loyal or insurgent. He used the Greek sense of "idiot" (the privatized man) for the
politically "inactionary" citizen. The concept feeds directly into his "politics of the
rearguard" thesis and, later, into *The Power Elite* and *The Sociological Imagination*.

## Operationalizations

Mills read indifference off voting and attention data: only about half of eligible voters
actually voted, so nonvoters were as decisive as voters ("government by default"); as many as
80% reported voting from family tradition rather than principle; only 21% of the public had "a
reasonably accurate idea of what the Bill of Rights is." He located white-collar workers at
exactly the national average of political attention — neither more nor less indifferent than
other strata — consistent with his "rearguard" thesis.

## Applications

Political indifference is the pivot of Mills's political argument: [[mass-society]] and the
[[status-panic]] absorb energies that might become political; centralization increases the felt
distance from power ("an understandable response to a condition of powerlessness"); and the
[[new-middle-class]] becomes a "rearguard" (see [[new-middle-class-politics-debate]]). It
remains a reference in political sociology's literature on participation and apathy.

## Critiques and Limitations

Mills's diagnosis rests on interpretation of survey and voting data rather than a dedicated
study of non-participation, and later political-behavior research complicates the simple
apathy picture (with accounts of rational abstention, mobilization barriers, and stratified
participation). Reading indifference as evidence of structural "powerlessness" risks
circularity. The claim is recorded in attributed voice.
