# Aggrandizer

## Definition as Coined

An **aggrandizer** (in archaeological social theory, especially Hayden's late-twentieth-century usage; adopted by [[bogucki-peter|Bogucki]] 1999) is an individual — typically acting through a **household** — who actively seeks to increase personal and kin status, access to labor, and wealth within a still-fluid social field. Distinct from a hereditary chief: aggrandizement is **achievement-oriented** and competitive; success is not automatically heritable until later institutionalization.

## Semantic History

- Related to Melanesian **[[big-man]]** ethnography but generalized as an archaeological agent type for late foragers and early farmers.
- Contrasts with systems models that treat elites as automatic products of irrigation, trade, or surplus.
- Bogucki (*[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|Origins]]* Ch.6) argues aggrandizers alone are insufficient: if everyone could successfully aggrandize, inequality would not structure; a **demographic/economic "drop-down"** of other households supplies the relative elevation of a few.

## Operationalizations

| Proxy domain | Typical material signals (attributed literature) |
|---|---|
| Feasting | Large middens, special food species, serving vessels |
| Prestige exchange | Exotic raw materials, crafted valuables |
| Storage / debt | Facilities exceeding household subsistence need |
| Mortuary | Differential grave goods short of fully hereditary elite pattern |

## Applications

- [[transegalitarian]] societies in Bogucki's Holocene chapters
- Pathways into [[chiefdom]] and [[stratified-society]]
- Position material for [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]] (agency/social-logic side)

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Individualism bias:** may underplay collective institutions and ideology that enable or block aggrandizement.
- **Ethnographic projection:** big-man models travel poorly to some regions.
- **Visibility:** unsuccessful aggrandizers leave little record; sample bias toward winners.
