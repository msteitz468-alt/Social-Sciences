# The Micro–Macro Problem (Coleman's Boat)

The problem of how the properties and behaviour of **social systems** relate to
the actions of the **individuals** that compose them. [[coleman-james|Coleman]]
frames it as the central task of social theory and diagrams it as three
linked relations — later nicknamed **"Coleman's boat"** for the shape of the
diagram: a downward **macro→micro** arrow (social conditions shape individual
situations and interests), a horizontal **micro→micro** arrow (individuals act
purposively), and an upward **micro→macro** arrow (individual actions combine into
system-level outcomes). His signature illustration is Weber's Protestant-ethic
argument recast in these three steps.

> Attributed to Coleman. The hardest link, he argues, is the upward micro→macro
> transition — the combination or "composition" of many actions into a system state.

## Definition as Coined

Coleman insists that the horizontal macro→macro relation between social states is
best explained by descending to individual actors and rising again (see
[[methodological-individualism]], the *internal analysis of system behavior*). His
worked example is Weber's *Protestant Ethic*, which he reads as three implicit
propositions — doctrine → individual values (macro→micro); values → economic
orientation (micro→micro); orientations → capitalist organization (micro→macro) —
and faults Weber for being "almost totally silent" on the third, the aggregation
step. He catalogues six forms of interdependence (externalities, bilateral exchange,
market exchange, collective decision, formal organization, norms/sanctions) by which
micro actions compose into macro outcomes.

## Semantic History

The three-relation schema was diagrammed in Coleman's 1986 AJS address and *Foundations*
and later nicknamed "Coleman's boat" (or the "bathtub"); it became a canonical
figure in **analytical sociology**, where the underdeveloped upward arrow (the "type
3" or micro→macro transitional problem) is treated as the core explanatory challenge.
Coleman applies the same critique to "frustration theories of [[revolution]]," which
he argues wrongly treat a macro phenomenon as the simple aggregation of individual
attitudes.

## Operationalizations

Coleman treats the micro→macro transition formally in the
[[linear-system-of-action]], where individual exchanges aggregate into a system-level
equilibrium of value and power. As a diagnostic, he shows applied job-training
research commits a micro→macro fallacy when it infers an aggregate unemployment
effect from individual employment gains that may merely displace other workers.

## Applications

The schema frames Coleman's accounts of [[social-norm|norm]] emergence,
[[collective-behavior]], the construction of [[corporate-actor|corporate actors]],
and [[revolution]] — each presented as a micro→macro transition rather than a
macro-level regularity.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics question whether every macro→macro relation reduces to a trip through the
individual level, and note the schema's relative neglect of genuine macro→macro
causal relations and of downward causation. The composition (micro→macro) step —
which Coleman himself calls the hardest and least developed — often lacks a general
solution outside idealized market-like conditions.
