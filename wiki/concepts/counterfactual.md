## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) use the counterfactual to define a causal effect: the
causal effect of an explanatory variable on a single unit is the difference between the
value that would be realized if the variable took one value, and the value that would be
realized — counterfactually — if it took another, holding everything else constant up to
the point of divergence. KKV write that although counterfactual conditions "are obviously
counter to the facts, they must be reasonable and it should be possible for the
counterfactual event to have occurred under precisely stated circumstances" (King,
Keohane, and Verba 1994). Careful specification of exactly what is held fixed while the
explanatory variable changes is, in their account, the crux of a defensible counterfactual
— for example, holding constant a post-treatment consequence of incumbency (candidate name
recognition) rather than incumbency itself biases an estimated effect toward zero.

## Semantic History

The counterfactual approach to causation has a philosophical lineage running through Hume
and, in its modern analytic form, David Lewis; KKV adopt it via Paul Holland's (1986)
statistical formalization of Donald Rubin's causal model, applying it identically to
qualitative and quantitative social-science research. In KKV's usage the term denotes a
methodological requirement built into the definition of a causal effect itself — "we
define causality as a theoretical concept independent of the data used to learn about it"
— rather than a loose figure of speech about "what might have happened." KKV explicitly
position their formal apparatus (see [[causal-effect]]) as extending, not superseding,
this Holland/Rubin lineage.

## Operationalizations

KKV formalize the counterfactual through three related quantities: the "realized causal
effect" for a single unit (a theoretical, unobservable difference between the value
actually realized and the counterfactual value); the "random causal effect" (the same
difference as it would vary across hypothetical replications); and the "mean causal
effect" — the expectation of that difference, which is the systematic quantity actually
estimated in empirical research (see [[causal-effect]]). Because only one of the two
counterfactual conditions is ever realized for any given unit, KKV hold that a causal
effect for that unit can never be observed directly — the "Fundamental Problem of Causal
Inference," following Holland (1986) — and that no amount of data or design quality
eliminates this in principle (see [[causal-inference]]).

## Applications

KKV apply the counterfactual definition to defend their "single logic" thesis: because
causal effects are defined identically via counterfactual comparison regardless of
whether data are qualitative or quantitative, the same underlying causal-inference logic
governs both traditions (see [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]]). They also use
it to rebut several rival accounts of causality raised against their framework — Ragin's
(1987) "multiple causation"/equifinality critique, Little's (1991) causal-mechanisms
view, and Lieberson's (1985) symmetric/asymmetric causality distinction — arguing in each
case that the apparent rival account is either compatible with, or presupposes, a prior
counterfactual definition (see [[causal-effect]] for the specifics of each response).

## Critiques and Limitations

Jon Elster (1983) argued, before KKV's formalization, that "the meaning of causality
can not be rendered by counterfactual statements" in situations where a third factor
produces both the apparent explanatory and dependent variables — a position KKV
characterize as a warning about the practical difficulty of inference rather than a
refutation of counterfactual causal definitions, noting that Elster himself grants
counterfactual statements "an important role in causal analysis" (Elster 1983:36, via
KKV). This exchange remains a live methodological position rather than a settled
question and is recorded in its strongest form on
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]]. A further limitation, flagged by KKV
themselves, is that counterfactual causal-effect definitions require unit homogeneity or
conditional independence assumptions to become estimable in practice (see
[[unit-homogeneity]] and [[conditional-independence]]); the counterfactual definition by
itself specifies what a causal effect *means*, not how it can be *learned* from data.
