# Cultural Arbitrary

*(Attributed — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] & [[passeron-jean-claude|Passeron]], [[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|*Reproduction*]], Book I §1.)* The content-side term of the theory of [[symbolic-violence]]: the culture that [[pedagogic-action]] imposes is *arbitrary* in the precise sense that it cannot be derived from any universal principle. The concept anchors the claim that every education imposes a particular selection of meanings that owes its authority to power, not to reason or nature.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed, proposition 1.2.1):*

> "The selection of meanings which objectively defines a group's or a class's culture as a symbolic system is arbitrary insofar as the structure and functions of that culture cannot be deduced from any universal principle... not being linked... to 'the nature of things' or any 'human nature'."

In the authors' foreword the term is defined operationally: a cultural arbitrary is defined "by the fact that it cannot be deduced from any principle" — "a logical construct devoid of any sociological or, a fortiori, psychological referent." Its arbitrariness is *twofold*, and the authors stress "the primordial relationship between the arbitrariness of the imposition and the arbitrariness of the content imposed": the culture is arbitrary both in its **content** (a particular selection, not a universal) and in its **form** (imposed by an arbitrary power, not derived from thought).

## Semantic History

- **Guard against the moralizing reading.** Bourdieu and Passeron insisted (authors' foreword) on "the right to use the term arbitrariness to designate that and only that which is yielded by the definition we give it," refusing the "twilight debates" over the Saussurean/Chomskian arbitrariness of the sign. *Arbitrary* is not a term of denunciation.
- **Socio-logically necessary.** Though it cannot be deduced from a universal, a cultural arbitrary "is not arbitrary in every respect": it "owes its existence and intelligibility to its social conditions of production." Its arbitrariness is revealed only by comparative or imaginary-variation method, against **genesis amnesia** — "the naive illusion that 'things have always been as they are.'"
- **Dominant cultural arbitrary.** The dominant cultural arbitrary is the one "expressing the objective interests... of the dominant groups or classes"; the objective degree of arbitrariness of a PA's power of imposition rises with the arbitrariness of the culture imposed.

## Operationalizations

- **"Demands what it does not give."** The school's clearest expression of the cultural arbitrary (Book II ch. 2): "in not explicitly giving what it demands, the system demands uniformly of all its students that they should have what it does not give" — i.e., the "correct" relation to language and legitimate culture acquired outside school.
- **Relation to language.** The concept is applied through *linguistic capital*: bourgeois "abstraction, formalism... and euphemistic moderation" versus working-class expressiveness are treated as socially constituted *relations to language*, not intrinsic linguistic properties — the arbitrary norm masquerading as the neutral standard.

## Applications

- **Ethnocentrism as product.** PA "tends to produce in those who undergo it... misrecognition of the objective truth of that culture as a cultural arbitrary (ethnocentrism)" — reproduced across dominant and dominated classes alike (linked to [[ethnocentrism]]).
- **Devaluation of dominated culture.** Compulsory schooling devalues customary law, home medicine, craft technique, and folk art by imposing recognition of the dominated classes' own arbitrary as illegitimate (Michelet's "hedge-school of the witch and the shepherd").

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Does the double sense cohere?** Whether "arbitrary of imposition" and "arbitrary of content" are one concept or two is contested; critics argue the term slides between a strong (nothing is better than anything) and a weak (nothing is universally derivable) reading.
- **Relativism worry.** The claim that even science and technology are inculcated with a component of authority (proposition 1.3) is read by some as corrosive relativism — which the authors deny, distinguishing the *arbitrariness of imposition* from the truth-value of what is imposed. See [[cultural-reproduction-debate]].
