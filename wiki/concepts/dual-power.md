# Dual Power

## Definition as Coined

Lenin coined "dual power" (*dvoevlastie*) in the Pravda article "The Dual Power" (April 9, 1917) to name what he called the "highly remarkable feature" of the Russian Revolution: alongside the bourgeois Provisional Government there had arisen a second, rival government — the Soviets of Workers' and Soldiers' Deputies — "a revolutionary dictatorship... based on revolutionary seizure, on the direct initiative of the people from below," of the same type, Lenin argued, as the Paris Commune of 1871. He opened the analysis with the maxim that "the basic question of every revolution is that of state power" ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964]]).

Lenin held the arrangement to be structurally transitional: the Provisional Government held the formal organs of state but lacked its own armed force, while the Soviet rested directly on armed workers and soldiers but had voluntarily ceded authority. He called the coexistence an "interlocking of two dictatorships" that "cannot last long," since "two powers cannot exist in a state" — a proposition he restated in the Draft Platform (*The Tasks of the Proletariat in Our Revolution*, April 1917) and defended into June: "Dual power still remains. The basic question of every revolution, that of state power, is still in an uncertain, unstable, and obviously transitory state" ("Has Dual Power Disappeared?", June 1917).

## Semantic History

- **1917 (coinage).** A conjunctural diagnosis of Russia between February and October, tied to Lenin's explanation of why the Soviets surrendered power: "insufficient class-consciousness and organisation of the proletarians and peasants" amid a "gigantic petty-bourgeois wave," rather than any mistake of leaders ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964]]).
- **1920 retrospect.** In [[lenin-left-wing-communism-1920|"Left-Wing" Communism]] Lenin treated the 1917 dual-power period as a stage in a generalizable Bolshevik sequence, giving the concept a life beyond the Russian case.
- **Later social science.** The term migrated from Bolshevik polemic into the comparative sociology of revolutions, where rival-sovereignty situations are treated as a general feature of revolutionary crises. That analytical afterlife is not yet documented from ingested sources — claims here rest on Lenin's own texts (as of the 2026-07-16 ingest).

## Operationalizations

In Lenin's usage the marker of a dual-power situation is the coexistence of (a) a government holding formal state organs without independent armed force and (b) a class-based counter-authority possessing armed force and popular sanction but not exercising formal sovereignty. Lenin's catechism *Political Parties in Russia and the Tasks of the Proletariat* (April 1917) operationalized the party landscape by position taken toward it: undivided bourgeois power (Cadets and rightward), continued dual power with Soviet "control" (Mensheviks/SRs — "control without power," Lenin objected, "is an empty petty-bourgeois phrase"), or undivided Soviet power (Bolsheviks).

## Applications

Lenin applied the concept throughout April–June 1917 to argue for "patient explanation" and winning Soviet majorities rather than immediate overthrow ("We are not Blancists... we stand for proletarian class struggle"), and to interpret each cabinet crisis of the spring as an expression of the unstable equilibrium rather than an accident. The concept anchors his account of why the February revolution's outcome was, in his phrase, "an amazingly unique situation" without precedent in earlier revolutions ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964]]).

## Later Usage — Autumn 1917 (Vol. 26)

By September–October 1917, writing in what the Collected Works collect as Volume 26 ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-26-1964]]), Lenin used the concept retrospectively to argue the arrangement had already exhausted itself. In "Can the Bolsheviks Retain State Power?" he stated that in 1905 the Soviets existed only "in embryo," lasting a few weeks, and that "dual power means paralysis for the Soviets" — they could develop fully only by taking over full state power outright, not by sharing it. He drew six comparative advantages of the Soviets as a prospective sole state apparatus (an armed force bound to the people; an intimate, verifiable, renewable bond with the majority; direct, recallable-personnel democracy; close contact with the varied trades enabling rapid reform; an organisational form for training the "vanguard" of the oppressed classes; and a fusion of parliamentary and direct-democratic advantages), framing resolution of dual power toward exclusive Soviet power as the condition of the Soviets' own institutional maturation, not merely a tactical preference.

Lenin also applied the "dual power" diagnosis polemically against continuing coalition politics: he read the September 1917 Democratic Conference vote (Soviets of Workers'/Soldiers' Deputies 192–83 against coalition; Peasants' Deputies 70–102 for) as evidence the arrangement's popular base had shifted decisively against continued power-sharing with the bourgeoisie, and cited Martov's own concession at the Conference that the Soviets were "the new fabric of the revolutionary state that has replaced the outworn state fabric of the old regime" as confirmation from an opposing camp.

## Critiques and Limitations

Within the 1917 debates themselves, Lenin's dual-power reading was contested: Kamenev objected in Pravda that Lenin's scheme wrongly assumed the bourgeois-democratic revolution completed, and Menshevik/SR leaders held that Soviet "control" over the Provisional Government was a workable arrangement — positions Lenin answered in "Letters on Tactics." As a general analytic, the concept carries its origin's partisanship: it was coined inside a strategic argument, not as neutral description, and uses of it inherit Lenin's premise that rival sovereignty must resolve toward one class power ([[lenin-collected-works-vol-24-1964]], attributed throughout).

## Carr 1979 narrative

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Ch. 1) restates dual power as the phrase coined for the Provisional Government–Petrograd Soviet rivalry after February 1917, but **qualifies** it: what followed the autocracy's collapse was "not so much a bifurcation of authority (the 'dual power') as a total dispersal of authority" — local Soviets, factory committees, peasant land seizures, soldiers' committees, army disintegration. Initial Soviet (and early Bolshevik: Kamenev, Stalin) stance treated February as bourgeois revolution requiring cooperation with the Provisional Government; Lenin's April Theses redefined the poles as class antagonists. By September Bolshevik majorities in Petrograd and Moscow Soviets revived "All power to the Soviets." Cross-link [[russian-revolution]], [[lenin-vladimir]].
