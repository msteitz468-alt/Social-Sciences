## Definition as Coined

"Naturecultures" is [[haraway-donna|Donna Haraway]]'s earlier coinage refusing the
nature/culture binary — the claim that what appear as separate domains of "nature" and
"culture" are always already mutually constituted and cannot be analytically split. The
term predates *Staying with the Trouble*; the claims extracted from this source did not
locate the exact word "naturecultures" within the read ranges, but found Haraway using
cognate formulations consistent with the same conceptual commitment, most notably
"naturalsocial ecologies" and "naturalcultural history" *(Haraway 2016)*,
[[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]].

## Semantic History

In *Staying with the Trouble*, Haraway's cognate usage appears in at least two registers.
First, she describes "four naturalsocial ecologies of a damaged planet" — coral reefs,
Madagascar forests, the circumpolar North, and Black Mesa — as her book's "critical
zones." Second, in the Navajo-Churro sheep and Black Mesa material, she frames the
imposition of a nature/culture split as a colonial apparatus: "White-settler divisions of
the world into nature and culture split Navajo lifeways into colonial apparatuses of
ecology and economics." She also credits Ursula Le Guin's "carrier bag theory of fiction
and of naturalcultural history" as a method commitment underwriting her own rejection of
Anthropos-centered "History," folding the naturecultures refusal into her broader SF
program.

Haraway's sympoiesis discussion in this book states directly that "sympoesis enlarges
and displaces autopoesis and all other self-forming and self-sustaining system
fantasies. Sympoesis is a carrier bag for ongoingness, a yoke for becoming-with, for
staying with the trouble of inheriting the damages and achievements of colonial and
postcolonial naturalcultural histories" — consolidating the naturecultures commitment
(no clean nature/culture split) within her account of sympoiesis. The concept has since
been consolidated more broadly in [[multispecies-studies|multispecies studies]] as a
foundational premise for treating humans and non-humans as co-constituted rather than
ontologically separate categories.

## Operationalizations

Naturecultures is not operationalized as a measurement construct; it functions as a
standing theoretical refusal that governs how Haraway frames case material (e.g., in the
Navajo-Churro sheep, Black Mesa mining, and orchid-bee sections) rather than a variable
subject to coding or indexing.

## Applications

Haraway applies the naturecultures commitment across the Black Mesa/Navajo-Churro sheep
narrative (the Hwéeldi/Long Walk of 1863, the 1930s federal stock-reduction killings of
Navajo-Churro sheep, Peabody Energy coal mining, and the sheep's restoration via Lyle
McNeal's Navajo Sheep Project and Black Mesa Weavers for Life and Land), and to the
ant-acacia and orchid-bee mutualism material via [[companion-species|companion-species]]
framing ("the yoking together of companion species, at table together"). Haraway is
explicit that the Black Mesa historical-political narrative functions in her text as
material in service of her theoretical argument (sympoiesis, response-ability,
Chthulucene) rather than as an independent ethnographic or historical finding — any
wiki use of the underlying Diné historical claims (Hwéeldi, stock reduction, Peabody
Energy mining history) on society or phenomenon pages should be sourced to a primary
historical or ethnographic study, not asserted from this theoretical work.

## Critiques and Limitations

The claims record does not surface direct external critique of naturecultures within
this source. Because the exact term does not appear in the read ranges of this
particular book (only cognate phrasings), any wiki claim that Haraway "uses" the term
*naturecultures* in *Staying with the Trouble* itself should be qualified — the concept
is present and consistent with her earlier work, but the book's own vocabulary favors
"naturalsocial ecologies" and "naturalcultural history."
