## Definition as Coined

Weber defines a social relationship as "the meaningful content of the mutual disposition of several persons and the comportment arising from it," and states it "consists entirely and quite exclusively of the Chance that action will be social in a (meaningfully) manifest sense" (Ch. 1 §3) — independent of "solidarity" or its opposite. Weber holds that a social relationship need not be mutually symmetrical: participants can attribute different meanings to the same relationship (as in one-sided friendship or love), and Weber states a perfectly symmetrical relationship is "in reality a rarity" (Ch. 1 §3).

Weber applies the same Chance-logic to collective entities: Weber writes that a "state" ceases to "exist" sociologically once the Chance that particular meaningfully-oriented social action will occur disappears — a claim Weber contrasts explicitly with the legal either/or of validity (Ch. 1 §3).

## Semantic History

The core term is *Chance*, which Tribe's Translation Appendix leaves untranslated throughout — Chance/Chancen names Weber's recurring technical concept of contingency, opportunity, and calculable probability, retained in German specifically to flag its regular technical recurrence rather than being folded into ordinary English "chance." Weber's related term *Verband* ("organisation") is distinguished from a mere social relationship by requiring a director and staff (Ch. 1 §12) — see the Translation Appendix's account of *Verband* versus *Verein* ("association").

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Weber distinguishes practice (a Chance of regularity dependent solely on actual performance) from custom (a practice familiar through lengthy exercise), and from "interest-determined custom," whose empirical persistence rests solely on purposively-rational orientation to similar expectations, as in market price formation (Ch. 1 §4).

## Applications

Weber builds appropriation and closure directly on the social-relationship concept: relationships are "open" when participation is not proscribed to any willing and able party, or "closed" when rules exclude, restrict, or condition participation (Ch. 1 §10). Within closed relationships, monopolized Chancen can be allocated freely, rationed, or permanently appropriated to individuals or groups; permanently appropriated Chancen are "rights," and heritable, alienable appropriated Chancen constitute "property" (Ch. 1 §10). Members of a closed relationship are termed "Genossen" (legally regulated members, "Rechtsgenosse") (Ch. 1 §10).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber notes rational closure arises specifically when participants believe monopolizing a relationship improves their own Chancen, as against believing openness would improve Chancen through wider dissemination (Ch. 1 §10) — meaning openness and closure are themselves strategic, interest-driven states rather than fixed properties, and Weber gives guilds and medieval democratic cities as examples of relationships that historically oscillate between the two (Ch. 1 §10).
