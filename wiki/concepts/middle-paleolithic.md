## Definition as Coined

"Middle Paleolithic" is a periodization term used in Europe and western Asia largely synonymously with the [[mousterian]] — the stone-tool complex named for the Le Moustier rockshelters (France), where Édouard Lartet and Henry Christy began excavating in 1863. As Klein notes, "the artifact assemblages the Neanderthals made are now usually assigned to the Mousterian Industrial Complex," and the two labels ("Mousterian" and "Middle Paleolithic") are used near-interchangeably for this material in Europe and western Asia.

## Semantic History

Outside that core region, the term loosens considerably. In eastern Asia, "Middle Paleolithic" is applied more broadly to any artifact assemblages that are roughly contemporary with the Mousterian, irrespective of tool typology — a usage that, per Klein, reflects the poorer state of the regional evidence rather than a shared technological tradition: "in eastern Asia... the artifact assemblages it refers to are still poorly known and poorly dated." The period runs, in its European/west Asian sense, from roughly 250,000–200,000 years ago (dated via radiopotassium, uranium-series, and OSL methods at various African and European sites) to regionally staggered end dates: sub-Saharan Africa around 50,000–45,000 years ago, northern Africa and western Asia around 45,000–40,000 years ago, eastern Europe just before 40,000 years ago, western Europe around 40,000 years ago, and parts of southern Spain possibly persisting to 30,000 years ago (Klein 1999).

## Operationalizations

In its strict (European/west Asian) sense the period is operationalized by Mousterian/MSA tool typology (sidescrapers, points, denticulates; frequent [[levallois]] prepared-core reduction; hand axes, blades, and formal bone tools rare) bracketed by the dating methods above. In its loose (east Asian) sense it functions mainly as a chronological placeholder rather than a technological category.

## Applications

The term is used to bracket the archaeological record of [[neanderthals]] and their contemporaries against the subsequent Upper Paleolithic/Later Stone Age (see [[upper-paleolithic-revolution]]), a contrast Klein treats as behaviorally stark: rare hafting, no unequivocal projectile-point evidence (with limited possible exceptions), near-absence of formal bone/antler/ivory artifacts, minimal art or ornamentation, little raw-material transport (usually under 5 km), no clear structures, and lower site density than the Upper Paleolithic. Klein (attributed) reads this behavioral gap as reflecting "an underlying biological contrast in basic cognitive capacity" between Neanderthals/archaic humans and fully modern humans, rather than mere cultural drift — a position, not a settled finding.

## Critiques and Limitations

The chief limitation is regional inconsistency: the term carries real typological content in Europe and western Asia but functions as a loose chronological catch-all in eastern Asia, where the underlying assemblages are poorly dated and poorly characterized. Users of the term outside its European core should therefore not assume typological equivalence with the Mousterian.

## From Gamble, *Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]*; date-stamp as of Gamble 1999.)*

Gamble’s Chapter 5 treats European societies ~300–60 Kyr BP (Neanderthal societies) with three [[palaeolithic-rhythms|rhythms]] — paths/tracks, [[chaine-operatoire|chaîne opératoire]], taskscape — asking what environments they could cope with and whether social life varied across the continent.

**Technology and transfers (as of 1999 literature as he synthesizes it).** Widespread [[levallois|Levallois]] after the Saalian (~after 300 Kyr BP; Bridgland OIS 8 on Lower Thames); White & Pettitt (via Gamble): Levallois is the only major technological innovation 500–200 Kyr BP justifying a Lower/Middle division. Soft-hammer prismatic blades by at least ~100 Kyr BP — “Upper Palaeolithic technology that lacks Upper Palaeolithic types” when blanks are rarely retouched to standard types (Northern Middle Palaeolithic Blade Group). Of Féblot-Augustins’s 578 transfers (OIS 7–4), few exceed 80 km; most under Geneste’s 20 km threshold (Lower ~75% <20 km; late MP Western Europe ~83% <20 km; Central Europe only ~48% <20 km with 19% >80 km). Quina Mousterian (mostly OIS 4): IL <10%, provisioning territories 5–10 km, artifacts not landscape-transported unlike Levallois assemblages; MTA (OIS 3) territories ~15–20 km. Only Mode 8 products (often Levallois) travel >11 km from source.

**Social reading (Gamble’s position).** Neanderthals lived in **[[social-landscape-palaeolithic|landscapes of habit]]** not a social landscape; life local and usually immediate; extended networks largely unnegotiated; society founded on [[intimate-effective-extended-networks|intimate and effective networks]] (attributed). Greater habitat range than earlier periods but not all habitats (e.g. absence from England in Eemian optimum OIS 5e as preference against mature oceanic deciduous forest — attributed). Social life **varied** across Europe (SW star-shaped vs NC linear transfers; regional cave vs open preferences). Interaction not necessarily dependent on spoken language but involved communication dedicated to social relations (attributed summary answers). Material culture patterning ascribed to strong intimate/effective networks and **absence** of elaborate time–space distanciation via symbols (attributed).

**60–33 Kyr late Middle Palaeolithic.** Regionality of late Mousterian and leaf-point assemblages is real, not a dating artifact (attributed). Specialized single-species faunas do not cleanly separate MP from UP behaviour; prime-aged hunting long predates the transition (attributed). Mousterian variants better described as **tradition** than culture; MP claims a social taxonomy (tradition) but not culture/technocomplex typology (attributed, ch. 8).
