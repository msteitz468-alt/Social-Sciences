# Logos (Rappaport)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[rappaport-ritual-religion-making-humanity-1999|Ritual and Religion in the Making of Humanity]]* (1999, Ch. 11 "Truth and order"), [[rappaport-roy-a|Rappaport]] develops **Logos** (and plural **logoi**) as the name for the encompassing order that is true, lasting, and unifying — neither mere private meaning nor mere positive law. Drawing on Heraclitus (via Heidegger, Kirk, and related readings), he treats Logos as the "original unifying unity of what tends apart," the transcendent lasting order in which flux occurs; its antithesis is *sarma* (muddle, randomness, chaos).

Logos, he argues, is not only theoretical: it "claims a man," is the norm of true life, and is "common" (universal and equally apprehensible). Human particular *logoi* relate to the general Logos as parts to whole.

## Semantic History

Rappaport inverts a simple correspondence theory of truth toward order-as-truth: liturgical orders establish and present Logoi. Comparative cases he treats as **logoi**: Egyptian Ma'at, Zoroastrian Asha, Vedic Rta, Nahuatl *Nelli*, Sioux Wakan-Tanka, Navajo Hozho, Aboriginal Dreaming/*djugaruru*, Maring *nomane* (with discussion of whether Maring counts as full Logos). He answers the "pseudo-Logos" objection (liturgy is conventional, Logos is discovered nature) by arguing that moral aspects of order must be constructed and accepted, while still accommodating unalterable natural processes — Logoi sit at the intersection of natural law and conventional understanding.

Private *doxa* / *idia phronesis* and [[deutero-truth|deutero-truths]] stand between Logos and comprehension as fragmenting forces.

## Operationalizations

- Map a tradition's highest-order order-concept and its relation to [[liturgical-order]] and [[ultimate-sacred-postulates]].
- Ask whether law and meaning remain coherent or have inverted (Ch. 14: money, Gnostic Lie, law–meaning dissonance).
- Closing program (POSITION): "post-modern science and natural religion" as recovery of ecological Logos — humanity as "that part of the world through which the world as a whole can think about itself" (attributed climax).

## Applications

Binds formal ritual theory to adaptive/evolutionary claims: sanctity stabilizes Logoi against language's vices (lie and alternative); breaking of the Holy is fragmentation of Logos. Complements [[cybernetics-of-the-holy]] as the order-side of the same architecture.

## Critiques and Limitations

Philosophical and comparative claims are program-internal; specialists in Heraclitus, Ma'at, or Dreaming may reject the equivalences. The ecological-Logos close is explicitly normative/religious as well as analytic — Hart's foreword already flags the book as in some degree religious as well as about religion.
