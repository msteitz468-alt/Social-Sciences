## Definition as Coined

"Indus Age" is a periodization term coined by Gregory L. Possehl to organize his 2002
synthesis *The Indus Civilization: A Contemporary Perspective*. Possehl defines it,
in attributed voice, as a roughly 6,000-to-7,000-year span running from the earliest
South Asian agriculture and pastoralism (c. 9,000–10,000 years BP) through the [[harappan-civilization|Indus/Harappan Civilization]] to the widespread adoption of iron
technology (c. 1000 BCE), a terminus he treats as coinciding with the emergence of
Vedic ideology in the Early Iron Age. Possehl states this explicitly as his own
analytic construct, presented as the organizing device for the book as a whole — it is
not a term with independent currency outside his synthesis, and should not be treated
as a settled disciplinary period name.

## Semantic History

The term has no meaningful drift to record yet: it originates with Possehl (2002) and,
as of this ingest, is not attested as adopted by other scholars. Any future spread or
contestation of the term should be added here rather than assumed.

## Operationalizations

Possehl operationalizes the Indus Age via a seven-stage chronological scheme (his
Table 2.2), built partly on his own fieldwork chronology (Rojdi) and partly on the work
of Jim Shaffer and others. He is explicit that stage boundaries are heuristic, not
historically "hard" — periods are anthropologically fuzzy transitions, and even
"beginning/end" for a stage is a debatable construct. Dates below are century-level
approximations per his table, all BCE unless marked BP:

| Stage | Content | Approx. dates |
|---|---|---|
| One | Beginnings of village farming and pastoral camps (Kili Ghul Mohammad Phase; Burj Basket-marked Phase) | 7000–4300 BCE |
| Two | Developed village farming/pastoral societies (Togau; Kechi Beg/Hakra Wares) | 4300–3200 BCE |
| Three | [[early-harappan|Early Harappan]] (Amri-Nal, Kot Diji, Sothi-Siswal, Damb Sadaat phases) | 3200–2600 BCE |
| Four | Early Harappan–Mature Harappan Transition | 2600–2500 BCE |
| Five | Mature Harappan (Sindhi, Kulli, Sorath, Punjabi, Eastern Harappan phases) | 2500–1900 BCE |
| Six | Posturban Harappan (Jhukar, Cemetery H, Late Sorath, etc.) | 1900–1000 BCE (regionally variable) |
| Seven | Early Iron Age of Northern India/Pakistan (Painted Grey Ware, etc.) | 1000–500 BCE |

Possehl explicitly cautions that treating these stages as simultaneous or uniform
across the whole Greater Indus region misrepresents the record; date precision should
be read as `approximate`/`disputed` per phase rather than `exact`.

## Applications

The scheme structures Possehl's entire 2002 synthesis and its chapter organization
(food production → ch. 2; urbanization → ch. 3; foreign trade → ch. 12, per his own
forward-pointers). It provides the master timeline against which individual site
chronologies (e.g., [[mehrgarh]], [[kot-diji]], [[kalibangan]]) are cross-checked.

## Relation to Kenoyer / Shaffer Indus Valley Tradition

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] 1998 following [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]]; full construct page [[indus-valley-tradition]].)*

Kenoyer distinguishes the popular label **“Indus Valley civilization”** (the period of urban expansion and cultural integration) from Shaffer’s **Indus Valley Tradition** — an Americanist “tradition” concept (Willey & Phillips lineage) covering human adaptations from plant/animal domestication through integration of diverse communities across the greater Indus Valley and adjacent regions. Parallel traditions (Baluchistan, Helmand) evolve alongside. Four **eras** structure the Tradition:

| Era | Content (Kenoyer/Shaffer) | Approx. dates (Kenoyer tables) |
|---|---|---|
| Early Food-producing | Villages/camps; food production; limited ceramics (Mehrgarh IA–IIA) | ca. 6500–5000 BCE |
| Regionalization | Crafts, regional styles, trade; later part = **Early Harappan** formative urbanism | ca. 5000–2600 BCE |
| Integration | Harappa Phase; cities, writing, weights, hierarchical order | 2600–1900 BCE |
| Localization | Breakdown into localized groups; Late Harappan | 1900–1300 BCE |

Kenoyer further links Localization-era developments to an **Indo-Gangetic Tradition** (Regionalization ca. 2000–300 BCE overlapping Indus localization; Integration from ca. 300 BCE with the Mauryan Empire).

**How this relates to Possehl’s “Indus Age.”** Both schemes are **etic periodization constructs** organizing the same deep sequence from early farming through urban and post-urban horizons into the Early Iron Age / second urbanization. They are **not** competing ethnonyms. Rough mapping (heuristic, not identity of stage content): Possehl Stages One–Two ≈ Early Food-producing + early Regionalization; Stage Three Early Harappan ≈ late Regionalization; Stage Four Transition + Stage Five Mature Harappan ≈ Integration; Stage Six Posturban ≈ Localization; Stage Seven Early Iron Age overlaps Kenoyer’s Indo-Gangetic Regionalization / PGW–NBPW horizon. Prefer the scheme of the author being cited; when comparing Possehl and Kenoyer/Shaffer chronologies, keep stage names attributed and do not collapse “Indus Age” into “Indus Valley Tradition” as synonyms — the former is Possehl’s book-organizing label (2002), the latter Shaffer’s tradition concept as operationalized by Kenoyer (1998).

## Critiques and Limitations

Because the scheme is a single author's synthetic construct rather than a
field-wide consensus periodization, it should be cited as Possehl's own scheme, not
as an established disciplinary standard. Possehl himself flags the fuzziness of stage
boundaries and warns against treating the "Neolithic" label (which his Stage One/Two
partly replaces, following Robert Braidwood's "era of primary village farming
communities and pastoral camps") as settled terminology either. The same single-author
limitation applies to Kenoyer’s operationalization of Shaffer’s Indus Valley Tradition:
widely used in Anglophone South Asian archaeology, still a research program’s frame
rather than a field-wide mandatory standard. Primary Kenoyer source:
[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]].
