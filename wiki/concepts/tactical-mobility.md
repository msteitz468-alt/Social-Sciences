# Tactical Mobility (Wolf)

## Definition as Coined

In the Conclusion of *[[wolf-peasant-wars-1969|Peasant Wars]]* (1969), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] builds on Richard Adams's definition of **power** as control one party holds over another's environment, exercised through mutual estimates of **tactical control**. **Tactical mobility** (Wolf's "tactically mobile peasantry") is the **latitude of movement** a peasant stratum has in that field: enough independent resources or distance from landlord/state domains to pursue rebellion. Poor peasants fully inside a landlord's domain lack it; middle peasants with family land and free/peripheral poor with subsidiary livelihoods (casual labor, smuggling, livestock) can have it.

## Semantic History

- Adams (1966) on tactical power estimates between parties.
- Wolf (1969) applies the idea to revolutionary recruitment: not grievance alone but **position in a power field**.
- Related to but distinct from military "tactical mobility"; here the unit is social-structural leverage before and during uprising.
- Later agrarian and guerrilla studies reuse "peripheral" and "freeholding" leverage without always citing Wolf's phrase.

## Operationalizations

Indicators in Wolf's cases: secure family land; settlement only marginally controlled from outside; peripheral location vs state center; mountain redoubts; ethnic/linguistic difference enabling autonomous communication and solidarity. External domain-breaking force (e.g. Red Army, collapsing army, Constitutionalist intervention) can *grant* latitude to otherwise constrained poor peasants.

## Applications

Wolf marks tactically mobile peasantries in: Morelos free villages (Mexico); Central Agricultural Region communes (Russia); CCP northern bastion after Long March (China); Viet Nam rebellion bases (e.g. Nghe An); Algerian fellahin / Kabylia; Oriente squatters / Sierra Maestra land-seekers (Cuba) — explicitly *not* Cuba’s plantation sugar proletariat.

**Multipliers** (attributed, Conclusion): peripheral location vs state center; mountain redoubts; ethnic/linguistic difference (Morelos Nahuatl, Kabylia Berber, Oriente Afro-Cuban element); non-physical communal fortresses (Russian mir; Mexican free villages). External domain-breaking force can *grant* latitude to otherwise constrained poor peasants (Yucatán peons; 1917 army collapse; Red Army).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Risk of post-hoc labeling of successful rebel zones as "mobile."
- Secondary-source geography may over-smooth local variation.
- Does not by itself explain leadership, ideology, or post-revolutionary outcomes (Wolf separates recruitment from long-range strategy and stresses peasants alone cannot make revolution).
