## Definition as Coined

This page synthesizes Possehl's (2002) treatment of gender representation across the
[[indus-age|Indus Age]], drawing primarily on the figurine sequence excavated at
[[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] and Nausharo (analyzed by C. Jarrige 1991; Jarrige et al. 1995)
and on figurines and burial data from the Mature [[harappan-civilization|Harappan]]
civilization proper. Every claim here is heavily interpretive and is recorded in
attributed voice throughout; this page is an analytical synthesis, not a settled
finding.

## Semantic History

The archaeology of gender emerged as a distinct program in the 1980s–90s (Conkey and Spector),
shifting from the unexamined projection of modern sex roles onto the past toward explicit analysis
of how gender was materially constituted. Applied to the Indus by [[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl 2002]]
(drawing on the figurine record), it replaces the older, uncritical "mother goddess" reading of
female figurines with a more cautious inquiry — while conceding that gender inferences from
Harappan material remain heavily interpretive and thinly evidenced.

## Operationalizations

Gender in the Indus is operationalized chiefly through: terracotta figurine attributes (ornament,
hairstyle, posture, sex markers); iconography on seals and sculpture; skeletal sexing of cemetery
burials where preserved; and division-of-labor inferences from craft contexts. Possehl stresses
each is a weak proxy — figurines need not depict social reality, and skeletal sexing at Lothal was
itself disputed (Sarkar vs. Kennedy).

## The Mehrgarh–Nausharo Figurine Sequence

Jarrige's (1991; Jarrige et al. 1995) analysis of the long Mehrgarh-Nausharo figurine
sequence, as reported by Possehl, traces a change over time:

- **Periods I–II**: simple seated figurines with ambiguous gender, broad
  hips/buttocks suggestive of female form, coated in red ochre (echoing ochre use in
  Mehrgarh burials).
- **Period III (Togau)**: no human figurines are found; bull figurines dominate
  instead.
- **Period IV (post-4000 BCE) onward**: composite, clearly female figurines appear —
  elongated rodlike heads, prominent hips, large or conical breasts, elaborate
  necklaces, joined legs, no arms.
- **Periods V–VI**: increasingly elaborate coiffure and jewelry individuation; arms
  appear.
- **Period VII (c. 2900–2600 BCE)**: female figurines appear with wigs/hairstyles,
  some holding modeled infants; the first clear **male** figurines appear (turban
  headdress, later two-strand hairstyle, male genitalia, loincloths, a "stiffer"
  carriage per Jarrige).

## The Gender-Ratio Reversal and Its Interpretation — Flagged as Analogy, Not Finding

By the end of Period VII (sub-phase VIID), male figurines rise from near-absence to
roughly 85% of human figurine representation (Jarrige 1991: 92). In a late VIID
deposit, the sole figurine holding an infant is male, while the sole female figurine
in that same deposit lacks the earlier fertility markers (broad hips, etc.), retaining
only fuller breasts (Jarrige 1991: 91–92, quoted by Possehl).

Jarrige, echoed by Possehl, suggests this may reflect changing gender roles around
parenting and reproduction-related representation across the Early Harappan and the
Early Harappan–Mature Harappan Transition. **This reading is explicitly flagged by
Possehl himself as an analogy to modern Western increases in male involvement in
child-rearing, not as direct evidence of Indus Age parenting practice** — this wiki
follows that caution: the "changing parenting roles" interpretation is recorded here
as an interpretive hypothesis inspired by a modern analogy, not a finding to be
promoted to wiki voice.

Possehl also explicitly warns against the older "Neolithic mother goddess cult"
interpretive frame once applied broadly to prehistoric female figurines
cross-culturally, describing it as having "serious limitations." His own tentative
position — flagged by him as tentative, "balance of evaluation tips slightly" — is
that pre-Period-VII female figurines with infants may represent an idealized
motherhood/fertility concept rather than goddess worship or mere toys, while allowing
that toy use, ritual use, and mnemonic use are all ethnographically plausible
functions for figurines generally, and are not mutually exclusive.

## Gender in Mature Harappan Figurines and Sculpture

Mature Harappan figurines are more varied than the Mehrgarh series. Gender markers
recorded by Possehl: beards and genitalia mark males (also seen on steatite/limestone
sculpture at Mohenjo-daro); prominent breasts without pubic marking mark females,
**except** the well-known bronze "dancing girl," which shows clear female pubic
marking. Some figurines show ambiguous or dual sex markers; Possehl tentatively
attributes this to possible androgynous representation, cross-dressing, or
gender-fluid/third-gender representation, flagging an explicit **analogy** (not a
claim of religious continuity) to later Hindu iconography — specifically the
half-male, half-female Ardhanarisvara form of Siva — while stating plainly that no
direct religious continuity is being asserted.

Women are shown holding or nursing children in several examples (Mackay records four);
no examples of males holding children are noted at Mature Harappan sites, a contrast
with the single Mehrgarh Period VII male-with-infant figurine. A figurine from Mound F
at Harappa (near the granary structure) shows a woman kneading bread or grinding
grain (identification per M. S. Vats), which Possehl reads as evidence for a female
role in food preparation — explicitly caveated that this does not establish that men
were excluded from that task, only that women are the ones depicted doing it.

## Gender Data from Cemeteries

At Lothal, joint/double burials produce disagreeing reports: Sarkar reports one
double-male pair and two pairs of male-plus-indeterminate remains, while Kennedy
reports two male-female pairs and one male-male pair. Possehl defers to Kennedy's
assessment as the more careful analysis but records the disagreement explicitly as a
live position-recording matter rather than adjudicating it further.

## Applications and Critiques

This material feeds interpretive questions about division of labor, ritual practice,
and social organization in both the pre-urban [[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] sequence and the
Mature [[harappan-civilization|Harappan civilization]], and intersects with the
[[pashupati-seal|Pashupati Seal]] and other iconographic evidence for Indus belief and
ritual. Every inference here rests on a small, unevenly distributed figurine and
burial sample, is mediated by the excavators' and analysts' own interpretive
frameworks, and should not be read as settled knowledge of Indus Age gender roles or
belief.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Ornament, burial sex patterns, female power, and representation

*(Attributed to [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] in *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities]]*, drawing on Dales & Kenoyer cemetery work, HARP finds, and figurine studies.)*

### Bangles and ornaments as gendered and ranked media

Kenoyer argues bangles were worn by **both men and women** and served as public nonverbal symbols of ethnic identity, social status, and ritual power — simple clay for common people; shell, bronze, and gold for the more affluent — and that no other early civilization used bangles to the same extent. Placement on figurines typically 3–4 at the wrist and 2+ above the elbow bilaterally; bronze figurines show full left-arm stacks. In the Harappa cemetery, **only white shell bangles** appear, on **left arms of middle-aged women (35–55)**; bangle width decreases over time in some sequences. Distinctive **chevron-incised shell bangles** occur at most Harappan Phase settlements but in limited quantity — not available to all — yet all widths share the same chevron orientation (unity motif across labor/status variation). No individuals at Harappa were buried wearing terracotta, faience, copper, or stoneware bangles — only shell; other materials may have been removed or broken at death (later Hindu parallel noted as analogy).

Adult women commonly buried with left-arm shell bangles and steatite disc-bead anklets; **truncated cylindrical throat amulets** almost exclusive to adult females (black stone, serpentine, steatite, faience) — possibly married/cult markers; several adult females accompanied by copper/bronze mirrors. Rare male burial wealth includes the 340-steatite-bead necklace with gold on burial 196a. Shared bangles and beads signal cultural/religious integration while material and style rank economic and socio-ritual status — unifying at distance, hierarchical on close inspection.

### Female social and ritual positions

Predominance of female terracotta figurines and powerful female deities on seals (alongside male deities) suggest, for Kenoyer, that **some urban women held important social and ritual positions** and that female deities helped legitimate beliefs and political power — a framing he contrasts with modern subcontinent cities. Female deities battling tigers (Harappa tablet), mother–infant imagery, and cup-lamp headdresses feed this reading. Initial genetic-trait analysis (Hemphill, Lukacs, Kennedy) suggested many Harappa cemetery women related by descent while men were not — compatible with **matrilocal burial**; Kenoyer stresses need for further samples before confirming a powerful position of certain women via residence rules (see [[bioarchaeology-of-the-indus]]).

### Headdress, ethnicity, and labor

Wide variety of headdresses/ornaments on figurines reflects ethnic diversity and changing styles; fan-shaped headdress common at Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, with Harappa four-flower variant and side cup-lamps; elaborate styles required skilled hairdressers. Less elaborate figurines show women grinding grain and preparing food; bronze female sculptures (e.g. Mohenjo-daro “dancing girl” type) are not mere terracotta copies and may mark special ethnic communities or bronze votive rituals. Overlap of male/female hair and ornaments (braids, bangles, chokers, long skirts) may reflect style fluidity or cross-dressing; Nausharo male figurine with feminine headdress holding infant; seven-figure long-skirted processions often called female but lack breasts.

### Male representation and childhood

Male terracotta figurines less varied, often bare-headed or turbaned; stone sculptures show elaborate braids/double buns and forehead fillets; most male terracotta figurines are **nude** (unlike short-skirted females), which Kenoyer reads as fertility-ritual use rather than literal male nudity in life. Tiny infant/child terracotta figurines common (possibly votive); most infant figurines male — possible cultural bias for male children; mother–infant burial at Harappa records childbirth mortality. Children’s toys (*pittu* discs, tops, wheeled animals, miniature cooking vessels) socialize gendered household skills (miniature cooking vessels “prepare girls for running households” — ethnographic analogy flagged).

### Occupational flexibility

Despite undeciphered writing, Kenoyer infers occupational classes paralleling later Early Historic specialists but finds **no indication** Harappan specialists were organized into rigid *jati*/caste — social hierarchy may have been relatively flexible. Allahdino jewelry hoard interpreted as hereditary ornaments of a woman/family hidden for safekeeping rather than goldsmith stock.

### Catalogue-depth notes

Mehrgarh/Nausharo figurines (Cat. 1–5) document red pigment in hair part (possible later *sindūr* analogy — interpretive), male/infant groups in burned Nausharo rooms, and graduated necklaces; burial shell bangles Cat. 90 (elderly woman, seven bangles left arm) anchor the cemetery pattern.

### Limits

All gender-power and matrilocal inferences are **provisional**; figurines are not transparent social mirrors; cemetery samples are class- and site-skewed; ethnographic analogies (Hindu ornament removal, *pittu*, *sindūr*) are hypothesis generators only.

## Wright (2010) — dancing-girl statuettes, gendered figurine markers, and Clark's gender-variant subset

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Chs. 9–10.)* Wright records two copper-alloy female statuettes produced by lost-wax casting at Mohenjo-daro (HR-5721, 10.8 cm, hair in a coil/bun, multiple arm bangles; and DKG-1278), conventionally called "dancing girls," both nude and posed as if dancing — found respectively in a relatively small house alongside a copper elephant statuette and steatite seals. At Harappa, two comparable human statuettes were found in *public* rather than residential space (Mound F, the Great Granary area), unlike the Mohenjo-daro pattern; some scholars have doubted their authenticity as Harappan objects given their differing aesthetic, though Wright's own view is that the difference reflects local city-state stylistic variation rather than a non-Indus origin.

Following Sharri Clark's (2003, 2007) figurine typology (see [[indus-religion]]), Wright reports gendered stylistic markers: female figurines show prominent breasts, fan-shaped headdresses, covered genitalia, and multiple bead strands; male figurines occasionally show curled, V-shaped, or rounded horned headdresses, nudity with articulated genitalia, and single ornaments. A very small subset of figurines shows ambiguous or combined-gender attributes — for example a bearded figure with breasts and a fan headdress (Clark 2003) — interpreted by Clark as possible androgyny or cross-dressing, and by Clark's broader reading of the sexless, hands-raised seated figurine type as communicating "shamanistic transcendence" precisely because a shaman was believed to harness both male and female sexual potential (Clark 2003:319). Wright records this as one live interpretive possibility among several, not a settled reading of Indus gender variance.

**Matrilocality and dental pathology.** Wright reports Hemphill et al.'s (1991) and Kennedy's (2000) biodistance findings that R37 cemetery females at Harappa show strong genetic affinity to one another and to the earliest Cemetery H stratum's females, read as evidence of matrilocal post-marital residence, against R37 males' closer affinity to males from outside Harappa (see [[cemetery-h]] for detail). She also reports female-specific dental pathology at R37 — higher cavity incidence and canine-enamel growth-disruption lines than in males — explained via competing attributed hypotheses of preferential treatment of sons (Lukacs and Joshi 1992) or differential food-preparation and nutrient-ratio practices (Kennedy 2000:306), who characterizes the overall pattern as "similar to that encountered in other ancient farming-herding communities." Grave-good evidence at R37 shows shell/stone throat amulets and shell arm-bangles (up to fourteen on one burial) placed with female burials, and copper/bronze mirrors found in several female burials (Kenoyer 1998) — no seals or figurines appear in any R37 burial of either sex.
