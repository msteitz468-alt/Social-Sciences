## Definition as Coined

[[haraway-donna|Donna Haraway]] coins "SF" in *Staying with the Trouble* as a
deliberately polysemous sign, glossed variously as "science fiction, speculative
fabulation, string figures, speculative feminism, science fact, so far" and elsewhere
"science fantasy" *(Haraway 2016)*, [[haraway-staying-with-the-trouble-2016|Staying with the Trouble]].
As Haraway states: "An ubiquitous figure in this book is SF: science fiction,
speculative fabulation, string figures, speculative feminism, science fact, so far."

Haraway theorizes SF as operating in a "triple sense of figuring": (1) SF as a method of
tracing and tracking threads within dense practices; (2) the string figure as the pattern
or assembly itself, which "solicits response" and must be carried onward; and (3)
string-figuring as the practice of passing on and receiving — making and unmaking —
"becoming-with each other in surprising relays." She elaborates the string-figure trope
as "a theoretical trope, a way to think-with a host of companions in sympoietic
threading, felting, tangling, tracking, and sorting."

## Semantic History

Haraway situates the string-figure image within an explicit ethnological history: she
notes that 19th- and 20th-century European and Euro-American ethnologists "collected"
string-figure games worldwide, often surprised to find host communities already knew
such games — sometimes in far greater variety than the collectors' own repertoire — and
observes that string-figure games "came late to Europe, probably from Asian trade
routes." Haraway uses this history to foreground the entangled colonial politics of
comparative anthropology's diffusion-versus-independent-invention debates, and treats her
own use of illustrative examples (such as the Navajo string game na'atl'o') as "not an
innocent universal gesture" given that colonial history.

Haraway also credits [[stengers-isabelle|Isabelle Stengers]], drawing on Félix Guattari,
with relaying to her the cat's-cradle image of "com-menting" — thinking-with as
becoming-with — as a model for passing patterns between players without demanding
fidelity, only "a particular kind of loyalty": "com-menting, if it means thinking-with,
that is becoming-with, is in itself a way of relaying... the demand that you do not
proceed with 'mechanical confidence.'"

A key method commitment folded into SF, per Haraway's own attribution, is Ursula Le
Guin's "carrier bag theory of fiction" — the idea (from Le Guin's essay of that title)
that stories can function as containers or nets holding multiple actors, human and
non-human, rather than following the "hero and weapon" narrative of Man the Hunter.
Haraway writes: "A careful student of dragons, Le Guin taught me the carrier bag theory
of fiction and of naturalcultural history." She treats this as a foundational method
commitment for her own SF program and for the Camille Stories in Chapter 8 — an avowed
"sym fiction," or exercise in speculative fabulation, built explicitly as an "SF" object
in the polysemous sense: "the richest humus for their inquiries turned out to be
SF—science fiction and fantasy, speculative fabulation, speculative feminism, and string
figures."

## Operationalizations

SF resists operationalization in any measurement sense; Haraway presents it as a
figurative and methodological device rather than a variable or index. Its "application"
is narrative and interpretive — a lens applied to cases (pigeon fancying, Navajo weaving,
orchid-bee mutualism, the Camille fable) rather than a coded instrument.

## Applications

Beyond the Camille Stories, Haraway applies SF to Octavia Butler's fiction, treating
speculative-fabulation readings of Butler's work as instances of the same "SF" logic
extended to a different literary case. She also frames the Crochet Coral Reef project
(founded 2005 by the Wertheim sisters, building on Daina Taimina's 1997 crocheted models
of hyperbolic space) as an "SF"/"science art activist worlding" exercise in
response-ability and "intimacy without proximity."

## Critiques and Limitations

Haraway's own text flags a risk internal to the concept: applying SF/string-figure
language indiscriminately across cultural contexts risks erasing the specific colonial
histories some of its illustrative material carries (e.g., she distinguishes Navajo
weaving, which she calls "cosmological performance," from the label "art science
activism," which she says would be "a category mistake" if applied to it). The claims
record does not document external scholarly critique of the SF concept specifically
within this source.
