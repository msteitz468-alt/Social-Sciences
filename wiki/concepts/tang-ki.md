# Tang-ki (Taiwanese spirit-medium healer)

The *tang-ki* (Hokkien; "divining youth," a spirit medium/shaman) is the central sacred **folk-sector**
healer of the Taiwanese [[health-care-system]] as documented by [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]] in
*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* (1980), based on Taipei fieldwork in
**1969–70 and 1975–78**. (Described here in the past tense of that documentation moment.)

## Definition as Coined

In the Taiwan Kleinman documented, a *tang-ki* was a folk healer who, while possessed by a guiding deity,
diagnosed and treated sickness — taking the pulse, interpreting *ch'ien* (temple fortune papers),
prescribing charms, ritual, and Chinese herbs, and providing crisis intervention and what would elsewhere
be called psychiatric care. The *tang-ki* belonged to the **sacred** side of the folk sector (alongside
Taoist priests and soul-calling specialists), distinct from its **secular** side (herbalists,
bone-setters).

## Semantic History

*Tang-ki* healing sits within Hokkien Taiwanese popular religion; Kleinman contrasts its elaborated
spirit-possession tradition with the stronger feng-shui emphasis of Cantonese Hong Kong (Potter 1970;
Jordan 1972). As an emic role it is not equivalent to any biomedical or professional category — Kleinman
treats it as a healer type constituted by its own [[clinical-reality]].

## Operationalizations

As documented, the *tang-ki* operated a shrine to which sufferers came; while entranced by the possessing
deity the medium diagnosed the cause of sickness (often an offended spirit, ancestral fault, or *ch'i*
imbalance), interpreted *ch'ien* fortune papers, and prescribed charms, ritual acts, and Chinese herbs.
Kleinman treated the *tang-ki*'s efficacy as measurable only against the [[explanatory-models]] and
[[clinical-reality|clinical reality]] of the shrine — not against biomedical categories — and studied it
through structured patient follow-up (see Applications).

## Applications (in Kleinman's analysis)

Kleinman's follow-up study of *tang-ki* patients at a Taipei shrine (structured home visits with 12 of 19
consecutive patients, plus the intensive multi-year case of "Mr. Chen") is the empirical core of his
theory of [[symbolic-healing]]:

- Healing proceeded through a three-stage ritual: a sickness is labeled with a sanctioned cultural
  category, the label is ritually manipulated, and a new label ("cured") is applied — "a meaningful
  symbolic form that may be independent of behavioral or social change."
- His paradoxical conclusion: "Mr. Chen's disease was not effectively treated; Mr. Chen's illness was
  effectively treated… Mr. Chen's illness was healed by the cultural construction of clinical reality."
- The mechanism he proposed was social-learning and anxiety-reduction — the patient adopting the
  "strong, powerful" attributes of his possessing god as "metaphoric balancing" — explicitly "not some
  mystical mechanism of symbolic action."

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman flagged his own study's limits: small sample, likely bias toward positive evaluations (dropouts
were probably dissatisfied), no physical exams, and the general unreliability of indigenous-healing
outcome studies. The account is date-stamped to 1969–78 Taiwan and should not be read as a timeless
description; *tang-ki* practice and its legal status changed (the government began enforcing rules against
unlicensed folk practitioners from 1975). Kleinman warns against over-reading symbolic efficacy — the
efficacy is in the treatment of the illness, not necessarily the disease.
