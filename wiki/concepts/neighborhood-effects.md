# Neighborhood Effects

## Definition as Coined

**Neighborhood effects** are consequences of **ecological context** — the social, institutional, and spatial properties of residential areas — for individual life chances and for rates of behavior at the community level. In *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (2012), [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] treats them as a **family** of effects (not a single coefficient): on crime, health, civic life, altruism, mobility, leadership networks, and more, mediated by community processes and higher-order spatial structure. Wilson’s foreword frames the program as elevating **collective processes in contextual settings** against independent self-maximizing individual models.

## Semantic History

Intellectual lineage through [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] ecology and [[social-disorganization-theory|social disorganization]]; late-20th-century revival via underclass / [[concentrated-poverty]] research ([[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]]) and multi-level statistical methods. Contested by selection-bias critiques and by “placelessness” / community-lost narratives Sampson writes against. Experimental turn (Moving to Opportunity) reshaped the debate in the 2000s; Sampson’s ch. 11–15 engage that literature and invert the usual selection critique.

**Sampson’s theory of context (GAC ch. 15 synthesis, attributed):** ecometric multi-scale measurement; durable concentrated inequality and social reproduction; cognitive/cultural mechanisms (looking-glass disorder; [[legal-cynicism]]; cultural structures); [[collective-efficacy]] and organizational density; extralocal spatial inequality and immigration diffusion; **selection as structural sorting**; migration and elite networks as interlocking higher-order structure; contextual causality against individualist/experimental reduction.

## Operationalizations

Compositional measures (poverty, [[concentrated-disadvantage]] PCA); process measures ([[collective-efficacy]], disorder, organizations, legal cynicism); spatial lags / Moran’s I; multi-level models; SSO; quasi- and true experiments. Sampson and [[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]] emphasize **[[ecometrics]]** — neighborhood/collective phenomena demand their own measurement logic, not stand-ins for individual traits.

Key GAC empirical anchors (Sampson 2012, attributed):
- **Durable structure:** community-area poverty 1960–2000 **r=0.78**; concentrated-disadvantage 1990→2000 **r>0.9** (Chicago and U.S.); percent black **r≈0.96–0.98**.
- **Process mediation:** CE mediates structure→violence; concentrated poverty/disadvantage forecast disorder; CE predicts lower observed disorder and violence after controls (with Raudenbush et al.).
- **Spatial interdependence:** Moran’s I e.g. 0.66 (disadvantage), 0.80 (immigration), 0.72 (crime); high-CE spatial clusters coincide with low-homicide clusters (>70%); ~75% of homicide hotspots in low-low CE spatial category (with Morenoff & Raudenbush).
- **Developmental:** severe concentrated disadvantage → verbal-ability deficit **>4 points** among African American children, lingering after exit (with Sharkey & Raudenbush).
- **Incarceration ecology:** tract incarceration–disadvantage **r≈0.80–0.82** in Chicago, race-contingent.

## Selection, structural sorting, and experiments (Sampson 2012, attributed)

- **Puzzle:** nearly half the PHDCN sample moved and virtually every neighborhood is connected by mobility flows, yet poverty, violence, and social processes remain entrenched in relative rank.
- **Structural sorting:** residential mobility is simultaneously individual action and a **spatial-social network among neighborhoods**; selection is itself a neighborhood effect. Strong version (chs. 11–14): **“neighborhoods choose people rather than … people choose neighborhoods.”**
- **MTO critique:** results mixed, not uniformly null (adult mental health, girls’ outcomes positive; male adolescent delinquency/health adverse; nulls on several economic/cognitive outcomes). Limits: MTO-equivalent population ~**5%** of PHDCN; confounds moving with neighborhood change; cannot estimate entry *into* poverty or change around never-movers; destinations remain hypersegregated with **no significant treatment differences** on a dozen PHDCN social-process measures; poverty reduction modest (degree not kind). “Included-variable bias”: controlling post-treatment pathways (income, depression, peers) can **induce** bias if they mediate neighborhood effects.
- **Level of experiment:** individual voucher counterfactuals ≠ neighborhood-level experiments that randomize ecological units.

## Applications

PHDCN / *[[sampson-great-american-city-study|Great American City]]*; policy debates on housing mobility, community development, and crime; public health “neighborhood as context” literature. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/sampson-great-american-city-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Selection into neighborhoods; scale of “neighborhood”; effect heterogeneity; Chicago (or U.S.) specificity (Sampson replies with national disadvantage inertia matching Chicago); risk of reifying ecological correlations; experimentalist insistence that only RCTs settle existence of effects.
