## Definition as Coined

According to Donna Haraway (*Staying with the Trouble*, 2016), "Capitalocene" names the current geological/historical epoch as one shaped fundamentally by capitalism's mode of organizing nature, rather than by an undifferentiated "humanity" (the charge she and others level at "Anthropocene"). Haraway is explicit that the term is not hers: "Capitalocene (Andreas Malm's and Jason Moore's term before it was mine)." Per Haraway's account, Andreas Malm first proposed the term in a 2009 seminar in Lund, Sweden, while a graduate student; the term was subsequently developed at length by Marxist sociologist Jason W. Moore, coordinator of the World-Ecology Research Network and author of *Capitalism and the Web of Life*. Haraway states she began using the term independently in public lectures from 2012, crediting Malm and Moore as prior originators (*Haraway 2016*).

## Semantic History

As relayed by Haraway, Moore's core argument is that capital and power develop *through* the web of life rather than acting *upon* an external "nature": "cheap nature is at an end; cheapening nature cannot work much longer to sustain extraction and production ... because most of the reserves of the earth have been drained, burned, depleted, poisoned, exterminated, and otherwise exhausted" (Moore, as quoted by Haraway) *(Haraway 2016)*. Haraway herself dates the Capitalocene's origin not to the steam engine or the English industrial revolution but to "the great market and commodity reworldings of the long sixteenth and seventeenth centuries," pointing to "networks of sugar, precious metals, plantations, indigenous genocides, and slavery" as constitutive of the epoch from the outset *(Haraway 2016)*. This dating claim is Haraway's own attributed position within the broader naming debate — see [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]].

Haraway also records a methodological warning about the term's use: told "in the idiom of fundamentalist Marxism, with all its trappings of Modernity, Progress, and History," the Capitalocene risks becoming "much Too Big" — a totalizing, deterministic master-narrative — subject to the same critique she levels at "Anthropocene" *(Haraway 2016)*. This is her own attributed caution, not a description of Moore's or Malm's intentions.

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct. It functions as a periodizing and explanatory frame competing with "Anthropocene," "Plantationocene," and Haraway's own "Chthulucene" for naming the current epoch of ecological crisis; each frame carries different causal emphases (a generic humanity vs. capital relations vs. plantation labor regimes vs. multispecies sympoietic entanglement).

## Applications

Haraway situates the Capitalocene as one of "three timescapes" — alongside the Anthropocene and her proposed Chthulucene — organizing *Staying with the Trouble*'s argument; she treats the Anthropocene and Capitalocene together as forces that have worked to exterminate "chthonic ones" and multispecies flourishing *(Haraway 2016)*. She reports that systems engineer Brad Werner, in a 2012 American Geophysical Union talk, argued that global capitalism's resource depletion is destabilizing "earth-human systems" such that the only "scientific" response is collective revolt — a position Haraway cites in support of the Capitalocene framing *(Haraway 2016)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Haraway's own attributed critique is that neither "Anthropocene" nor "Capitalocene" alone adequately names the present: both risk totalizing narration, and she proposes "Chthulucene" as a needed supplementary or alternative frame — a position recorded on [[naming-the-anthropocene-debate]] alongside Moore's and Malm's positions. The term's authorship is itself a live attribution question: Haraway credits Malm's 2009 seminar use as prior to Moore's fuller theoretical development, while noting her own independent public use from 2012 — a priority question that belongs on the debate page rather than resolved here in wiki voice.
