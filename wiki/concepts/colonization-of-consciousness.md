# Colonization of Consciousness

> Coined by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991).

## Definition as Coined

The Comaroffs argue that "the final objective of generations of colonizers has been to colonize [the] consciousness [of the colonized] with the axioms and aesthetics of an alien culture" — a force "at once ideological and economic, semantic and social." The signature double-genitive frames the whole study: they were "drawn back to the colonization of their consciousness and their consciousness of colonization." Colonialism, on this account, operates "as much [through] the politics of perception and experience as [through] formal governance": "no habit is too humble, no sign too insignificant to be implicated."

## Semantic History

The idea condenses an older worry (Ezekiel Mphahlele's charge that Christianity achieved "the conquest of the [black] mind") into an analytic built on [[gramsci-antonio|Gramscian]] [[hegemony]]. In *Of Revelation and Revolution* it works on **two levels**: the explicit conversionary *message*, and the deeper *media* of the encounter (space, water, language, the body, time) — the media, the Comaroffs argue, mattering more. Crucially, colonization of consciousness does **not** require acceptance of doctrine: "colonized peoples frequently reject the message of the colonizers, and yet are powerfully and profoundly affected by its media." To argue over rainmaking "in empirical terms" was already to be drawn into "the modes of rational debate, positivist knowledge, and empirical reason at the core of bourgeois culture."

## Operationalizations

The Comaroffs treat formal church membership as a poor index; what registered the process was the uptake of *sekgoa* (European ways) generally — building forms, dress, naming, wage labor, the clock and calendar, literacy. See *[[the-long-conversation]]* for the mechanism.

## Applications

Its home application is the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]] mission encounter (*[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*). The concept has been widely borrowed across the anthropology and history of colonialism, missions, education, and the body.

## Critiques and Limitations

Critics argue the phrase can imply an overdetermined, one-way remaking of subjectivity that understates the colonized's agency — a reading the Comaroffs pre-empt with their emphasis on contestation, [[bricolage]], and a graded "[[consciousness|chain of consciousness]]." Because the evidence is largely a European archive (see [[historical-ethnography]]), how far the *consciousness* of the colonized is really recovered — rather than inferred — is contested. Distinguish sharply from [[false-consciousness]]: the Comaroffs do not claim the Tswana were deluded about their interests, but that the *forms* within which they thought and acted were reshaped.
