# Ecofact

An **ecofact** (also “eco-fact”) is a **non-artifactual organic or environmental remain** that nonetheless informs past human activity and environments. Renfrew & Bahn 2012, following [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] and other ecological-approach pioneers, treat ecofacts as **equally revealing** as [[artifact|artifacts]] for many questions — diet, climate, landscape, and human impact — and note that until recent decades archaeologists often neglected them, studying sites as self-contained artifact packages.

## Definition as Coined

Textbook definition (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 2, attributed):

> Non-artifactual organic and environmental remains or ecofacts include **human skeletons, animal bones, and plant remains, but also soils and sediments** — all of which may shed light on past human activities. They are important because they can indicate, for example, what people ate or the environmental conditions under which they lived.

Portability and human manufacture distinguish [[artifact|artifacts]]; ecofacts need not be intentionally modified, though many are culturally selected, burned, butchered, or deposited. Human remains sit at the boundary: classificatory “ecofacts” in the textbook’s broad list, studied methodologically by [[bioarchaeology]].

## Semantic History

- **Pre-ecological archaeology:** Organic remains collected unevenly; monumental and typological artifacts dominated.
- **Ecological approach (mid-20th c.):** Clark and others elevate plant and animal evidence for economic prehistory.
- **Processual environmental archaeology:** Ecofacts become multi-proxy climate and subsistence datasets; sites placed in geomorphological and biological landscape context; environment treated as variable in space and time (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 6).
- **Specialization:** [[paleoethnobotany]], [[zooarchaeology]], geoarchaeology, [[pollen-analysis]], [[isotope-analysis]] professionalize ecofact analysis.
- Boundary disputes: some traditions fold animal bones under “bioarchaeology” (Clark’s original coinage); New World usage usually reserves bioarchaeology for humans.

## Operationalizations

| Ecofact class | Typical methods |
|---|---|
| Plant macroremains | Flotation, dry sieving, SEM; crop-processing analysis |
| Phytoliths / starch | Microscope protocols on sediments and tools |
| Pollen | Stratified cores in/out of sites |
| Animal bone / shell / otoliths | [[zooarchaeology]], [[taphonomy]], seasonality |
| Human bone / soft tissue | [[bioarchaeology]], aDNA, isotopes |
| Soils / sediments | Micromorphology; Butzer primary/secondary/tertiary cultural deposits |
| Isotopic substrates | Collagen, enamel, foraminifera, ice, speleothems |

**Formation literacy is mandatory:** differential preservation (charring bias; acid soils destroy bone; shells outlast bone in middens), recovery choices, and post-depositional mixing structure every ecofactual claim (Renfrew & Bahn R6-078).

## Applications

- Subsistence: meals vs diet; only stomachs/feces prove consumption.
- Environment: pollen, microfauna, mollusks, beetles, phytoliths as climate/vegetation proxies.
- Domestication and farming origins (Abu Hureyra, Kuk, Jarmo multidisciplinary teams).
- Human impact: pollution cores, island extinctions, forest clearance, transported landscapes.
- Context of artifacts: ecofacts in association fix function (hearth fuel, butchery, storage).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Never “true” past environments** from single proxies — aim for best multi-method approximation (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 6).
- Abundance ≠ importance (chance charring; pollen production bias; human wood selection in charcoal).
- Macrofauna often **diet** evidence more than **climate** evidence.
- Looting and poor recovery discard ecofacts while chasing salable artifacts — irreversible information loss.
- Category is etic and pedagogical; field recording systems may use other labels (sample, organic, environmental).
