# Business class and working class (Lynds)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[lynd-middletown-1929|Middletown]]* (1929), [[lynd-robert-s|Robert S. Lynd]] and [[lynd-helen-merrell|Helen Merrell Lynd]] divide Middletown's gainfully employed population into two groups that structure nearly every chapter comparison:

- **Business class** — those whose work primarily addresses **people** (selling, promotion, management, professional services, and related white-collar activities).
- **Working class** — those whose work primarily addresses **things** (making, moving, or handling material goods; industrial and manual occupations).

The Lynds present this as an analytical convenience grounded in local occupational structure, not as a full Marxist theory of class. They report roughly **71% working class / 29% business class** among the gainfully employed (1920 frame, attributed); intensive family schedules used 124 working-class and 40 business-class households (native-born white; school-age children).

## Semantic History

The labels reuse everyday American English ("business men," "working people") but fix a **binary occupational dichotomy** as the book's master variable. This is distinct from:

- Marxian [[bourgeoisie]] / proletariat (relation to means of production, surplus value);
- Weberian [[social-class-weberian|class / status / party]];
- Warner-style multi-class reputational ladders (later community studies);
- The Lynds' own later *Middletown in Transition* (1937) refinements (not yet ingested).

Post-1929 reception often treats "Middletown's two classes" as synonymous with middle vs working class in popular speech — a drift that flattens the Lynds' people-vs-things occupational criterion.

## Applications

- Throughout *Middletown*: housing quality, marriage age and divorce talk, child-rearing ideals, school attainment, leisure (car, clubs, reading), church participation, charity, and civic leadership are repeatedly compared **by class**.
- Society page: [[middletown]] · Study: [[lynd-middletown-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Binary oversimplification:** flattens internal stratification (foremen, small shopkeepers, professionals) and underplays race/ethnicity as cross-cutting cleavages (critics; see study page).
- **People/things criterion** is fuzzy at margins (clerical workers, service occupations).
- **Not a probability sample of "American class"** — one city's occupational structure in 1924–25.
- Still powerful as an **organizing device** for reading how a 1920s midwestern city distributed life-chances across a two-class lens the authors made explicit.
