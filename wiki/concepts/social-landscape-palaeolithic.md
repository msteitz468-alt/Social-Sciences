# Social Landscape (Palaeolithic)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe]]* (1999), [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] defines the **social landscape** as a spatial concept distinguished by **the scale negotiations achieve**: the spatial outcome of individuals developing their **extended** networks and the appearance of a **global** network of otherness (attributed, ch. 3). In Tindale’s phrase for Aboriginal Australia, such networks deal with “tracks, travel, trespass and trade” (via Gamble 1999). Just as the group transcends the individual, the social landscape encompasses personal networks without replacing the everyday [[locale|locales]] of performance (attributed).

Gamble pairs the social landscape with a second regional concept, the **landscape of habit** (after Gosden 1994:182) — his rename of the earlier “local hominid network” (Gamble 1993–98) to avoid confusion with personal-network terminology. The landscape of habit is the common structure of forager space-time behaviour centred on the individual: everyday routines at locales, [[palaeolithic-rhythms|rhythms]] of acquisition, manufacture, and movement, encompassing subsistence *and* social behaviour under Giddensian routinization (attributed).

| Concept | Network emphasis | Spatial scale (as of Gamble 1999) | Social principle |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Landscape of habit** | Intimate + effective; weak extended | ~40 km common radius; upper bulk transfers ~80–100 km | Exclusion; absolute strangers possible |
| **Social landscape** | Extended + global | No hard upper limit in principle (potential global network) | Exclusive *and* inclusive; strangers relative |

(attributed, Gamble 1999 ch. 3 summary)

## Semantic History

- **1986 settlement model.** In *The Palaeolithic Settlement of Europe*, Gamble argued social relations dominated settlement behaviour, but the nine European regions were large enough that pre-~40 Kyr periods still looked ecologically determined — a result he later attributes to group-centred analysis (attributed, 1999 ch. 3).
- **1999 redesign.** Locales + rhythms + regions become the framework for *society*, not only settlement; landscape of habit vs social landscape replaces site-catchment concentric models with itineraries of paths and tracks (Leroi-Gourhan world-image as itinerary vs Vita-Finzi & Higgs catchment — via Gamble) (attributed).
- **Table 3.7 mapping.** Boissevain interactional networks → locales; intimate/effective → landscape of habit; extended/global → social landscapes at regional scale (attributed).
- Distinct from later symbolic [[landscape-as-mythopoesis|landscape-as-mythopoesis]] readings; here landscape is produced by **performance of social life** across locales and regions (attributed).

## Operationalizations

**Landscape of habit (as of Gamble 1999):**

- Geneste’s SW French Middle Palaeolithic raw-material tiers: local ≤5 km (bulk of stone); regional 5–20 km; distant 30–80 km (<5% of stone but high tool utilization) (via Gamble).
- Worldwide sample: common habit scale ~40 km radius, upper limit ~100 km (Gamble 1993d via 1999).
- Ethnographic support: Aka exploration ranges averaging ~35 km combined (Hewlett et al. via Gamble); Wobst mating-network models — rarely exceed ~300 km diameter, mostly under ~80 km (via Gamble).
- Neanderthal land-use dimensions vary by region with climate/resource “plaid” mosaics (Guthrie via Gamble); longer transfers still within ~2–3 days’ walking of locales (attributed, ch. 5).

**Social landscape (as of Gamble 1999):**

- Regular or directed transfers beyond the 80–100 km threshold; novel non-stone items (shell, ivory); non-simple fall-off curves.
- Ethnographic long-distance transfers 600–3,600 km among foragers; Mt William greenstone moved up to 800 km (average 228 km) as social value, not mere utility (McBryde via Gamble) — no “magic prestige distance” (could be 5 or 500 km).
- Clarke’s culture/technocomplex scales map only onto Upper Palaeolithic social landscapes; Middle Palaeolithic stays at **tradition** within landscape of habit (attributed, ch. 8).
- Europe’s nine analytical regions ~150,000 to >1,000,000 km² as containers for comparing network scales (as of Gamble 1999).

## Applications

### Neanderthals and earlier Europe (as of Gamble 1999)

Gamble concludes Neanderthals lived in **landscapes of habit rather than a social landscape**: evidence for the latter lacking; hematite scraps and objects such as the Tata plaque do not betoken negotiated acts beyond co-presence using resources unavailable to intimate/effective networks (attributed, ch. 5). Transfer patterns: star-shaped in SW France, predominantly linear in north-central Europe (Féblot-Augustins via Gamble). After the Eemian, some spatial extension of subsistence territories, but dimensions of the landscape of habit remain small (Geneste via Gamble). Society without a social landscape is still society — a *different form* (attributed).

### Upper Palaeolithic (as of Gamble 1999)

Gamble maintains the social landscape does **not** appear before the Upper Palaeolithic; its construction splices landscapes of habit so [[agency]] extends across time and space, has no spatial boundaries in principle, and produces increased inter-regional variability under attenuation constraints (attributed, ch. 8). Early Upper Palaeolithic movement patterns read as circular/all-channel networks (linear raw-material “tramlines” along Rhine and Danube, across Carpathians, Atlantic shells into SW France) (attributed). After ~33 Kyr bp, high-density circular systems predominate and regional archaeological incoherence is reduced (attributed). Recognizing a social landscape does **not** require huts, hearths, and art everywhere — Iberia’s Ebro-frontier case shows stretching space-time is opportunity not inevitability, and domination/replacement is option not always outcome (attributed).

In the social landscape, “objects now become people”: transfers express wider structures; exchange gestures become operational sequences comparable to manufacture; system integration *in absentia* (Giddens via Gamble) (attributed). Landscapes of habit move objects *with* people as portable affordances; social landscapes personify artifacts as relational tokens (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- Hard to operationalize without circular inference from the same transfer data used to define networks.
- Pre-aDNA, pre-full Neanderthal-symbolism literature (as of 1999); later finds may shrink the Middle/Upper contrast.
- Nine-region European frame may be inadequate for long-term colonization processes at Eurasian scale (Gamble himself notes eastern boundaries are weak — attributed).
- Remains an interpretive frame for Palaeolithic society, not a mapped territory.
