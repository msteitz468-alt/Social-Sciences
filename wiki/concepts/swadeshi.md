# Swadeshi

## Definition as Coined

**Swadeshi** (roughly “of one’s own country”) names indigenous production and, in early twentieth-century Indian nationalism, the program of **home industry** and boycott of foreign goods — especially British cloth. C. A. Bayly’s "The origins of swadeshi (home industry): cloth and Indian society, 1700–1930" (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986) argues that cloth’s power as nationalist symbol rested on **pre-existing** social roles of cloth in India — status marking, transmission of purity/pollution/holiness, and pledges of protection — not only on colonial economic grievance *(Bayly)*.

## Semantic History

| Period | Sense (Bayly, attributed) |
|---|---|
| Precolonial / early colonial | Cloth as transformative medium (status, substance, protection along production–gift path) |
| Colonial textile PE | Partial commoditization under monetization and British manufactures |
| After 1905 | Swadeshi/boycott as unifying nationalist ideology; foreign cloth as material symbol of loss of country |
| Gandhi era | Khadi, spinning, sacrifice, purity; mills and Khadi Bhawans as institutional forms |
| Tagore etc. | Internal debates on swadeshi’s cultural politics *(Bayly)* |

## Operationalizations

Bayly frames a **collective biography of cloth** and **partial commoditization**: things retain qualities of makers and exchangers even in a fully monetized economy — inverse of slavery’s person→thing trajectory *(Bayly)*. Kingship mediates singularization–commoditization. Colonial legitimacy crises (including 1857) rework cloth-political discourse *(Bayly)*.

Appadurai’s intro previews Bayly: production/exchange/consumption as “political discourse”; Gandhi’s rhetoric as language of **commodity resistance** *(Appadurai)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Bayly’s essay is historical synthesis (not a single ethnography). Nationalist and colonial voices appear as positions he analyzes — do not convert either into wiki-voice moral verdict.
