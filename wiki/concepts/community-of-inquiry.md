# Community of Inquiry

## Definition as Coined

In Peirce’s pragmatism, the **community of inquiry** is the social process that produces and tests belief — not solitary certainty. Tavory and Timmermans (*[[tavory-timmermans-abductive-analysis-2014|Abductive Analysis]]*, 2014, Ch. 7) make it a **methodological chapter**, not a pious afterword: qualitative research ironically maps how the social world shapes others while hiding how researchers’ own relations craft theories. “Other people’s fingerprints” appear in workshops, courses, colloquia, reviews, awards, citations, and debates with subjects.

## Semantic History

- **Peirce:** inquiry settles **doubt** into **belief** (a calm state one does not wish to change), not ultimate Truth; dogmatic alternatives (tenacity, authority, a priori elite taste) fail; belief should be tested by communal experience, including the method of inquiry itself (“belief correction”).
- **T&T appropriation:** community provides **conditions of emergence** and **criteria of warrant** for theory–observation relations (not only Latourian “trials of strength” after the fact). They refuse naive congeniality: the community is often combative, manipulative, and competitive; full democratic openness remains out of reach.

## Operationalizations

Three functions that also become **evaluation criteria** for qualitative work:

| Criterion | Question |
|---|---|
| **Fit** | Does the claim follow from presented evidence while transcending mere restatement? Account for variation (dataset, temporal, intersituational)? |
| **Plausibility** | Are better alternative explanations available? |
| **Relevance** | What are the implications for other research? |

Fit is not self-evident: data are rarely conclusive; theories can absorb variation with enough clauses; the researcher must **convince** the community. Weak tactics: “been there” authority and volume metrics (N interviews, pages of notes). Stronger: transparency enabling reanalysis / “virtual witnessing” (Shapin & Schaffer literary technologies). Extreme poor-fit example they cite: Moller’s claim that no dying person valued money, contradicted by his own extensive quoted narrative.

Practical sites: U.S. **job talks** (~45 min + ~45 min Q&A); peer review (e.g., their Jansen example); published controversies (e.g., Klinenberg–Duneier as they discuss).

## Applications

Structural condition of [[abductive-analysis]]; reflexivity about lone-genius mythology.

## Critiques and Limitations

Gatekeeping and fashion; community can enforce wrong standards. Authors stress craft improvement under real agonism, not ideal speech.
