# Mousterian

Archaeological industry label for much of the European and Near Eastern **Middle Palaeolithic**, frequently `associated_with` [[neanderthals]] (not identical to them). Central to the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]] and to Stringer & Gamble's archaeology of the Ancients (*[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]* ch. 7).

## Definition as Coined

Type associations at Le Moustier; expanded by François Bordes' quantitative facies system. As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), the Mousterian is "one such Middle Palaeolithic stone industry in Europe, and is generally associated with the classic Neanderthals" — a traditional equation that Levantine fossils (e.g. [[kebara]] Neanderthal and [[qafzeh]] moderns, both with Middle Palaeolithic tools) show cannot be read as a species–industry identity (attributed, S&G 1993).

Traditional European periodization defined the **Middle Palaeolithic** by retouched tools (scrapers etc.) with hand axes rare, against a Lower Palaeolithic (Acheulean) of widespread large bifacial hand axes. The progressive idea that tools gradually became smaller and finer had long been abandoned; absolute dates further overturned simple industry-to-industry sequences (attributed, S&G 1993).

## Semantic History

### Chronology and scope (as of Stringer & Gamble 1993)

S&G adopt ~**250–200 ka** as the start of the Middle Palaeolithic (favoured by many scholars as of 1993), partly because after this hand axes decline while the [[levallois|Levallois]] technique becomes much more common; Callow on La Cotte argued the MP was firmly established by ~200 ka after a ~50,000-year transition. The **later** European Middle Palaeolithic lasts ~**130–35 ka**, spanning the last interglacial and early-glacial phases, coinciding with classic Neanderthals in SW Europe and the Middle East, with some overlap with the earliest [[aurignacian|Aurignacian]] (~40 ka). TL dating placed classic Mousterian assemblages at Grotte Vaufrey, Combe Grenal, Le Moustier, and Pech de l'Azé in this later phase (as of S&G 1993).

French **Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition (MAT)** reintroduces large hand-axe-like implements after ~75 ka; **Micoquian** (from La Micoque level 6) labels assemblages of this age with regional variants (e.g. Bockstein asymmetrical knives; Pradnik knives, Poland) (attributed, S&G 1993).

### Culture-historical "peoples" vs functional variability

Bordes treated SW French assemblage types as five **cultural traditions** ("tenacious cultures"; parallel phyla at Combe Grenal etc.). Processual and reductionist readings — [[binford-lewis|Binford]] functional settlement models; Dibble/Rolland expedient vs curated technology; Dibble's continuous scraper-reduction series — recast facies as activity, raw-material, and use-life patterning. Stringer & Gamble (1993) explicitly **dispense** with Bordes's model of five distinct sedentary Neanderthal tribes lasting millennia at low density with little inter-group contact; they argue temperate/glacial survival requires mobility (see [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).

### Four-way non-identity

Mousterian ≠ Neanderthal ethnicity ≠ a single language or genetic population. Industry–fossil associations vary regionally; Levantine Middle Palaeolithic is shared by Neanderthals and early anatomically modern humans (as of S&G 1993). Use `associated_with`, not identity.

## Operationalizations

- **Bordes facies / quantitative typology** (Typical; Denticulate; Charentian Quina/Ferrassie; MAT): cumulative graphs of tool-type frequencies; flake-production technique treated by Bordes as largely raw-material conditioned.
- **Levallois vs disc-core** production modes within assemblages (high Levallois e.g. Ferrassie vs low/rare e.g. Quina).
- **Reduction and edge-class readings** (Dibble; Barton): scraper "types" as stages of resharpening; notches/denticulates as expedient edges vs points/scrapers varying with use intensity.
- **Expedient vs curated** (Dibble & Rolland): near-source vs distant raw-material regimes as drivers of retouch intensity, alternative to five traditions.
- **Settlement-function mapping** (Binfords): base camps vs work camps as reinterpretations of Bordes groups.

Tool inventory once established remains relatively limited (hand axes, flakes, Levallois flakes/blades, side scrapers, points, cores, pebble/chopping tools); assemblage-type patterns continue ~200–130 ka without a clear progressive industry sequence — S&G: "Progress … could not be a more inappropriate description" (attributed, 1993).

## Applications

- Anchor industry for European/Near Eastern [[neanderthals]] archaeology and the Middle–Upper Palaeolithic transition ([[chatelperronian]], [[aurignacian]]).
- Test case for culture-historical vs functional/processual systematics ([[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).
- Contrast baseline in Stringer & Gamble's Ancient vs Modern behavioural comparison: MP raw materials generally within ~50 km; campsites often unstructured relative to early UP villages; style and long-distance exchange argued as weak before ~40 ka (attributed, S&G 1993, chs. 7–9).
- La Ferrassie Neanderthal remains: S&G note they might be relatively late and are not necessarily associated with the "La Ferrassie" Mousterian variant also found at the site (attributed, 1993).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Species–industry equation** fails in the Levant (Kebara / Qafzeh).
- **Facies-as-ethnicity** rejected or heavily qualified by functional, reduction, and raw-material models; ethnoarchaeological land-use complexity undercuts coexisting "peoples" readings (Binford).
- **Typology ≠ mental template**: continuous reduction and unmodified used flakes undermine type-as-blueprint assumptions (Dibble; Keeley via S&G 1993).
- Absolute dating (as of 1993) dissolved simple progressive sequences; many site ages and industry assignments have been revised since.
- Relationship to late transitional industries ([[chatelperronian]], Uluzzian, Szeletian) is disputed (local evolution vs imitation of incoming Modern packages).

## Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999. The Mousterian
is an archaeological **industry**, not a biological population or language.)*

Klein dates the Mousterian/Middle Stone Age ~250–200 ka to a regionally staggered end (sub-Saharan
Africa ~50–45 ka; W Asia/N Africa ~45–40 ka; W Europe ~40 ka), made by [[neanderthals]] in Europe
and near-modern/archaic humans elsewhere. Knappers used the [[levallois|Levallois]] prepared-core
method; typology is dominated by sidescrapers, points, and denticulates, with formal bone tools,
art, and long-distance transport rare. Klein reads this cross-continental **"sameness,"** against
the diversity of the succeeding [[upper-paleolithic-revolution|Upper Paleolithic]], as reflecting
"an underlying biological contrast in basic cognitive capacity" — a contested claim (see
[[behavioral-modernity]]). On the meaning of Mousterian variability he sides with a chronological/
task reading over Bordes's ethnic facies (see [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).
