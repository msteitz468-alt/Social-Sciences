# Democratic Despotism (Soft / Tutelary Despotism)

## Definition as Coined

In the climax of Volume 2 of *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*,
[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] argued that despotism in a democratic age would take
"an unprecedented" form — not the violent, limited tyranny of a Roman emperor but something
"more widespread and kinder; it would debase men without tormenting them." He confessed the
old vocabulary failed him: "the old words *despotism* and *tyranny* are not suitable. This
is a new phenomenon which I must, therefore, attempt to define since I can find no name for
it."

The load-bearing passage describes an "immense and protective power" standing above equal,
isolated citizens:

> "It is absolute, meticulous, ordered, provident, and kindly disposed. It would be like a
> fatherly authority, if, fatherlike, its aim were to prepare men for manhood, but it seeks
> only to keep them in perpetual childhood... it covers the surface of social life with a
> network of petty, complicated, detailed, and uniform rules... it does not tyrannize but it
> inhibits, represses, drains, snuffs out, dulls so much effort that finally it reduces each
> nation to nothing more than a flock of timid and hardworking animals with the government as
> shepherd."

Crucially, this soft despotism is **compatible with elective forms**: citizens "combine
centralization with the sovereignty of the people," periodically choosing their supervisors
and then returning to dependency — "citizens leave their state of dependence just long
enough to choose their masters and then they return to it."

## Semantic History

Often rendered as "soft despotism" or "tutelary despotism." Its afterlife is large:
mid-20th-century mass-society theory, critiques of the welfare/administrative state (from
both left and right), and debates over bureaucratic paternalism all cite the passage.
Tocqueville himself located the danger not in grand political oppression but in "the minor
things of life" — "it is, above all, in the details that we run the risk of enslaving men."

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; a projection deduced from the growth of [[individualism]] plus the observed
expansion of European administrative centralization (state absorption of charity, education,
religion, finance, associations, industry).

## Applications

The concept is the endpoint of Vol. 2's chain: [[equality-of-conditions|equality]] →
[[individualism|isolation]] → dependence on a central power. Tocqueville's proposed
correctives are the same civic institutions urged elsewhere: elected local secondary bodies,
[[voluntary-association|voluntary associations]], a free press ("by far the most effective
democratic instrument of freedom"), and an independent judiciary. The book's final thesis
insists the outcome is *chosen*, not fated: equality "can lead to slavery or freedom."

## Critiques and Limitations

- A projection, not an observation — Tocqueville describes a possible future, and its
  "prophetic" reception can obscure how conjectural the argument is.
- Rests on the deductive, single-premise method of Vol. 2 (see [[tocqueville-democracy-study]]).
- Politically promiscuous: invoked to condemn very different states, which can strain the
  concept's analytic precision.
