# Brokerage

**Brokerage** is a multi-tradition concept. Do not silently merge Burt’s network social-capital usage with DOC’s contentious-politics mechanism or Small’s organization-as-broker.

---

## Burt — network brokerage / structural holes (1992, 2005)

*(Attributed — [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]], *[[burt-structural-holes-1992|Structural Holes]]* 1992; *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]* 2005.)* **Brokerage** is action that spans [[structural-holes|structural holes]] — connecting non-redundant clusters so the broker gains information and control benefits as the [[tertius-gaudens|tertius gaudens]]. Value is defined by the **social context around** relations, not by contact volume. Burt’s first stylized fact: **brokers do better** (evaluation, compensation, promotion). Second: the mechanism is [[vision-advantage|vision]] — higher risk of good ideas and adaptive implementation. By 2005 brokerage is **complementary** to [[network-closure|closure]]: peak performance when closure is high *within* a group and brokerage runs *beyond* it ([[structural-autonomy]]; [[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]] Position 3).

Operationalization: low [[network-constraint|network constraint]]; bridge relations; multi-sample manager evidence (supply-chain, investment banking, electronics, etc.). Failure mode of *unrestrained* brokerage: organization chaos (conflicting goals; units competing) — cured, Burt argues, by closure’s reputation mechanism.

## McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly — contentious-politics mechanism (2001)

*(Attributed — McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly 2001.)* A **relational mechanism** in [[contentious-politics]]: the linking of two or more previously (or currently) unconnected social sites by a unit that mediates their relations with one another and/or with yet other sites. Sites may be persons, cliques, organizations, places, or programs. DOC treats brokerage as creating **new collective actors**.

Distinguished from [[organizational-broker|Small’s organizational broker]] (organization-as-broker in routine social capital): DOC brokerage is a **process mechanism inside episodes of claim-making**, not a standing organizational property alone.

### Definition as Coined (DOC)

> "Brokerage, a mechanism that recurs throughout Parts II and III of the book, we define as the linking of two or more previously unconnected social sites by a unit that mediates their relations with one another and/or with yet other sites."

Authors note affinity with Burt-style structural holes when sites hold complementary resources; breaking brokerage can transform politics and undermine intermediaries (citing Gould).

### Semantic History

Network brokerage / tertius lineage; DOC centers it as among the most robust mechanisms across Part II episodes and Part III applications. Ch. 10 pairs brokerage with diffusion as alternate pathways into [[scale-shift]].

### Operationalizations (DOC)

Identify mediating actors or organizations that create new cross-site ties during contention; compare centralized vs decentralized brokerage (e.g. Philippines Church/business umbrellas vs Mau Mau traders, taxis, militants).

### Applications (DOC)

*(Attributed findings as illustrations:)* Mau Mau radical oathing networks and Kikuyu intermediaries; Philippine Cardinal Sin, NAMFREL, BANDILA, JAJA; South African UDF brokerage; U.S. antislavery and Spanish transition coalitions; Italian unification; Soviet nationalities politics; scale shift via Freedom Summer–style bridging.

### Critiques and Limitations (DOC)

May be over-attributed when multiple mechanisms co-occur; brokerage vs diffusion pathways for scale shift differ in reach and investment cost (DOC Ch. 10).

---

## Cross-tradition caution

Burt measures **ego-network position** and competitive returns; DOC measures **episode-level mechanisms** producing collective actors; Small measures **organizations as brokers** of routine social capital. Affinities (bridging disconnected sites) are real; identity is not.
