# Free-Floating Intelligentsia

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mannheim-ideology-and-utopia-1936|Ideology and Utopia]]* Part III, [[mannheim-karl|Mannheim]] (using Alfred Weber’s terminology) names a **socially unattached intelligentsia** (*freischwebende Intelligenz*): an **unanchored, relatively classless stratum** “not too firmly situated in the social order,” whose social location and educational formation, he argues, make possible a more comprehensive view than party-bound partial perspectives.

He does **not** claim intellectuals have no social ties; he claims relative detachment compared with classes locked into immediate economic-political interests, and that modern education binds intellectuals across origins into a common intermediate sphere. Two courses face this stratum: affiliation with parties/classes, or cultivation of a dynamic synthesis of perspectives (the path he associates with a political sociology of the whole rather than party-school training alone).

## Semantic History

- German *freischwebende Intelligenz* enters English sociology largely through the 1936 Wirth–Shils translation and subsequent textbook reception.
- Often misread as claim that intellectuals are socially rootless in a strong sense; Mannheim’s text stresses *relative* classlessness and educational mediation.
- Later sociology of intellectuals (Gramscian organic intellectuals; Bourdieu field analysis; New Class debates) largely rejects or retools the “free-floating” image while keeping the problem of intellectuals’ structural location.
- Linked in Mannheim to the breakdown of clerical monopoly on knowledge and the rise of a free intelligentsia after democratization and social/vertical mobility (Part I).

## Operationalizations

No measurement model in the source. Indicators he discusses narratively: educational institutions; party schools vs open forum; career paths of intellectuals between classes; capacity to participate in multiple thought-styles.

## Applications

- Prospect of **scientific politics**: synthesis of partial political-ideological perspectives as task of political sociology.
- Analysis of **communicability** of political knowledge (lecture vs workshop; club; party schools; free-choice education).
- Contrast with bureaucratic, conservative, liberal, socialist, and fascist styles of thought (Part III ideal-types) as carriers of partial theory–practice meanings.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Romanticization charge:** overstates intellectuals’ detachment; understates class origin, patronage, university and state employment, and party organization.
- **Political charge:** can read as self-privileging of the intellectual’s standpoint (the synthesis claim).
- **Historical:** Weimar/LSE intellectual formation is a thin base for a general figure of modern intellectuals.
- Remains useful as a **named problem** (how if at all can partial standpoints be mediated) even where the empirical claim of free-floatingness is rejected.
