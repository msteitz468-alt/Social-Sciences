# Activity, Interaction, and Sentiment

## Definition as Coined

In *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]* (1950), Ch. 2, [[homans-george-c|George Homans]] defines three **elements of social behavior** for the analysis of small groups:

1. **Activity** — "what men do" in a broad sense: work, play, ceremony; not only muscular motion but the kinds and amounts of behavior that can be compared across persons and groups (Homans; "function" in earlier draft language, later renamed activity).
2. **Interaction** — the sheer fact that one person has communicated with another (verbal or nonverbal), independent of message content; variables include **frequency**, **order** (who originates action), and ratios of talking time. Homans prefers *interaction* to *communication* to avoid conflating channel with content. A **group** is defined by relative interaction frequencies (Ch. 4).
3. **Sentiment** — internal states of the body/person: drives, emotions, feelings, attitudes — liking, respect, pride, antagonism, approval, hunger, etc. Homans lumps states psychologists would separate; observation is largely inferential (expression, speech, fieldworkers' reports), not physiological measurement.

The three are treated as **variables that may be mutually dependent**: changes in one are associated with changes in the others (hypotheses developed across the book's cases).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Homans thesis / early papers (1941–47) | "Function" later renamed activity; interaction and sentiment already in play (Hare/Polley intro) |
| Homans 1950 | Full triad; mutual dependence inside external and internal systems |
| Homans later (1984, per intro) | Self-critique: mutual dependence of the three should not be asserted the same way for the **external** system — environment sets parameters |
| Mid-century small-group research | Parallel to Bales categories and sociometry (Moreno) without identity of schemes |
| Later exchange theory (Homans 1961+) | Behavior and reward vocabulary partly supersedes the triad as master scheme |

## Operationalizations

- **Interaction:** counts of contacts, participation matrices, clique maps (as in Bank Wiring and women's association examples); Chapple-style timing of originations.
- **Activity:** job classifications, output rates, ceremonial participation, "a fair day's work" norms as activity standards.
- **Sentiment:** field inference; sociometric choices (Moreno); attitude items — Homans is cautious about reliability.

## Applications

- Bank Wiring Observation Room: output restriction as activity norm; clique structure as interaction; liking/antagonism as sentiment (*Human Group* Chs. 3–6).
- Norton Street Gang: bowling performance as activity linked to rank/sentiment ([[whyte-street-corner-society-study]]).
- Tikopia family: kinship duties as activity; respect and affection as sentiment; interaction order in household.
- Organization of the book's hypotheses about rank, leadership, and [[social-control]].

## Core mutual-dependence hypotheses (Homans, attributed)

Homans states these as *hypotheses*, not proven laws (Chs. 5–6, 17):

1. **Interaction ↔ sentiment (liking):** more frequent interaction between persons → more favorable sentiments (and vice versa), *ceteris paribus* — weakened or reversed when interaction is chiefly the giving/receiving of orders (authority).
2. **Sentiment ↔ activity:** persons who like one another interact in additional activities beyond external-system requirements (helping, games, social contact); dislike can produce vicious circles of reduced help and further antagonism.
3. **Activity ↔ interaction:** joint activities increase interaction frequency; specialization can narrow interaction range.
4. **Rank ↔ norms/activity:** the more closely a member’s activities realize group norms, the higher his social rank (evaluation); higher rank associated with wider interaction range and more origination of interaction.
5. **Element values** initially set by “given” factors (environment, size, composition, history), then modified by internal-system elaboration within those limits.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Sentiment observability.** Homans concedes sentiments are inferred, not counted like interactions — a standing reliability issue.
- **Lumping.** Psychologists reject collapsing drives and interpersonal attitudes into one element.
- **External-system mutual dependence.** Homans's own later correction (intro apparatus).
- **Not exchange theory.** Do not read 1961 reinforcement propositions back into the 1950 triad without attribution.
