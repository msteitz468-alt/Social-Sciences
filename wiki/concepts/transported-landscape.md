# Transported Landscape

## Definition as Coined

A **transported landscape** is the suite of organisms and practices that voyaging
colonizers deliberately and accidentally move to new islands — the domesticated plants
and animals, commensal species, weeds, and technological/ecological knowledge that
**recreate a familiar productive environment** far from the homeland. In
*[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]*
(1984, Ch. 6), [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] adopts **Edgar Anderson**’s concept:
through purposeful and accidental transport of adventive plants and animals, colonizers
transform biota and create artificial landscapes; on Oceanic islands, competitive weeds
and predators had drastic effects on vulnerable endemics.

Kirch **extends** Anderson’s concept: humans also carry a **cultural concept of
landscape** and actively modify new environments according to that mould — including the
efficacy of fire in clearing forest for garden land, diversion of streams to feed irrigated
pondfields, and even notions of “proper” political division of the landscape (radial
segmentary territories). Colonizers did not merely adapt to pristine islands; they
**imported a landscape**, with cascading effects on native biota and later
[[carrying-capacity]].

## Semantic History

The phrase crystallizes a long-standing observation in Oceanic prehistory (portable “canoe
plants,” pigs, dogs, chickens, rats). Kirch’s 1984 formulation stresses purposeful
environmental rearrangement as part of colonizing success and as a dominant theme of
Polynesian prehistory: contact-era constraints were often products of prior human action,
not stable natural baselines. He rejects (as of 1984) the thesis that prehistoric Oceanic
peoples avidly practised a conservation ethic precluding major ecological change until
Europeans. The concept has since traveled in Pacific and island archaeology more broadly.

## Operationalizations

*(Attributed — Kirch 1984 Ch. 2 and Ch. 6 unless noted.)*

### Purposeful introductions — canoe plants and the domestic triad

- **Crop plants:** Wild relatives of Oceanic crops do not occur east of the Solomons, so
  cultigens had to be purposely introduced throughout Polynesia. Five staple groups
  dominated: aroids, yams, breadfruit, bananas, and (in East Polynesia) sweet potato.
  Transfer included ethnobiological concepts of method (e.g. taro wet / yam dry). Plant
  transfer was subject to **reassortment** — partial packages on distant islands (e.g.
  failed or incomplete transfers on Easter Island).
- **Domestic triad:** pig, dog, and fowl — skeletal remains in Lapita and early East
  Polynesian sites. Of the triad, the **pig** (often going feral) had the most significant
  impact on native vegetation. Pig presence is strong indirect evidence for agriculture
  (Yen 1973a as cited).
- **Famine plants:** Many famine foods (bitter yam, ti, *aka*/Pueraria) were Polynesian-
  introduced adventives maintained feral in secondary vegetation (e.g. ‘Uvean *toafa*
  burned during *honge* to induce sprouting).

### Accidental stowaways

- **Pacific rat (*Rattus exulans*):** skeletal remains ubiquitous in Polynesian
  archaeological sites; among the most consequential inadvertent introductions.
- Geckos and skinks; synanthropic arthropods; ~a dozen anthropophilic land-snail species
  carried on canoes/plants — snails appear early after colonization (e.g. Tikopia c. 900 BC;
  Yanuca Lapita; Huahine; Hane; Hawaiʻi — as of Kirch 1984).
- Stowaways may have had **greater impact on native biota** than some purposeful
  introductions.

### Archaeological and paleoecological signatures

- Introduced cultigens and domesticates in early sites; pollen/charcoal evidence of
  clearance; invasive commensals; ethnobotanical continuity of canoe-plant rosters across
  archipelagos; gaps where transfers failed.
- **Fire-driven vegetation change:** *Dicranopteris linearis* fern savannah (*toafa*) on
  many tropical high islands created and expanded by fire for swidden — thin volcanic soils
  lose A horizon, laterize, and support little cultivation, reducing arable land as
  populations burgeoned. Futuna Tavai site buried under up to 2.5 m of erosional debris;
  erosion from first centuries AD under *toafa* (as of Kirch 1984).
- **Erosion dual effects:** interior erosion may enrich valley-bottom alluvium (Spriggs
  Aneityum; Tikopia) but gains did not always outweigh degradation (Kirch’s position).
- **Faunal impact often early:** megapode extinction, shellfish diversity/size reduction
  within decades–centuries of colonization; moa extinction by predation + fire habitat
  destruction (NZ); ≥40 Hawaiian bird species extinctions linked to lowland dry-forest
  destruction (Olson and James 1982 as cited); Easter Island scrubby rainforest → treeless
  landscape (Flenley palynology); massive NZ forest clearance (≥8 million acres by European
  arrival per Cumberland estimate as cited).
- Marine shellfish population structure changes without extinction (Swadling; A.J. Anderson).

### Cognitive / institutional landscape

Not only biota: fire clearance norms, irrigation technology templates, and political
geometry of land division are part of the transported package in Kirch’s extension.

## Applications

- Polynesian dispersal and colonization chapters in Kirch 1984; human impact on island
  ecosystems as a **dominant process** (with demography, intensification, competition)
  transforming [[ancestral-polynesian-society]] endpoints.
- Contrast between islands that received full vs. partial transported packages
  ([[rapanui|Easter Island]] severe transfer failures; temperate [[maori|New Zealand]]
  agricultural adaptation).
- Links to [[agricultural-intensification]]: cumulative human ecosystem impact pushed
  descendants toward intensive agriculture after colonization-phase overexploitation;
  buffering (*ma/*masi storage) and degraded arable bases shape political economy.
- Island vulnerability once isolation is broken (Fosberg 1963a as cited): rapid entropy when
  change sets in.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Intentionality varies (crops vs. stowaway rats); “landscape” metaphor can blur ecological
  scale.
- Later literature densifies (as of post-1984 Pacific archaeology) with aDNA of commensals, refined paleoecology, and revised
  extinction chronologies not available in 1984 — date-stamp claims “(as of Kirch 1984).”
- Human impact is **contributor** to social change via altered production bases, not sole
  `caused_by` for chiefdom evolution; Kirch embeds it in multi-process models.
- Conservation-ethic critique is Kirch’s 1984 position against a then-common popular/
  academic thesis — not a timeless ethnographic claim about all Oceanic ethics.
