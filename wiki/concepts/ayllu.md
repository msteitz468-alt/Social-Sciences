# Ayllu

## Definition as Coined

**Ayllu** (Quechua) names Andean corporate collectives—alliances of households and kindred that exchange labor and jointly own land and other resources. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) argues arid montane farming and herding demand simultaneous tasks in widely separated places; a married couple is the minimal economic unit but not self-sufficient. The autonomous unit of production and reproduction is therefore the ayllu: not simply people, property, and things but an **organizational charter** allowing problems of different scope and scale to be addressed by folk collectives of variable size and inclusiveness.

## Semantic History

Colonial and ethnographic sources use *ayllu* for community groups of varying scale; archaeological use projects the institution backward as a model for earlier periods (Moseley’s “Inca model of statecraft” method). Meanings drift among “lineage,” “village corporation,” “tributary unit,” and maximal ethnic “mountain ayllu.” Moseley also applies “maximal ayllu / nested hierarchies” as analogy for diffuse Middle Horizon polities (e.g. Moche Phase V; debated Tiwanaku segmentary organization) and for Chimor’s ancestor-glued apex—**analogy, not proven identity** of the Inca-period institution.

## Operationalizations

**Charter principles (as of Moseley 2001):** Kinship charters based on real or fictive descent emphasizing **unity, duality, hierarchy, and replication**. Unity via founding ancestor; duality applied to founder’s heirs (e.g. older/younger siblings → intermarriageable moieties); replication when moiety founders’ heirs subdivide into quarters (*suyus*) that can split again; hierarchy because older siblings’ descendants are senior. Individuals ranked by genealogical proximity to founders; access to labor, land, water, and assets uneven—communal yet unequal.

**Mallqui ayllus:** At contact, most prevalent were small kin collectives tracing descent from a human mummy (*mallqui*); dual organization adaptive for endogamy and retaining property within the collective. Nested scale: mallqui → *huaca* ayllus (object founders integrating adjacent communities; Cuzco’s ten royal lineages claimed the rock of Manco Capac) → *apu*/mountain ayllus embracing ethnic groups in the thousands as nested mirror-image hierarchies under one charter. Founders’ relics among most sacred holdings; capture could hold an ayllu hostage.

**Governance and cult:** Internal cargo systems (civil/religious rotating offices; *vara* staffs) subsidiary under hereditary *kuraka* dual rule. *Llacta* mortuary complexes as *huacas sepulturas*; *mallquipavillac* attendants managing ancestral debt and political economy. Reciprocity (*ayni*, [[mita-labor|mit’a]]) and *tinku* rituals of dual joining. Imperial taxation used local community/ayllu as basic unit under decimal administration.

**Archaeology:** Often inferred rather than directly attested—treat pre-Inca applications as analogy. Nazca Cahuachi “confederacy akin to maximal ayllu” (Silverman via Moseley) is interpretive.

## Applications

Foundation of Moseley’s Chapter 3 folk-to-state ladder; linked to mit’a, ayni, mitmaq, dual organization, and huaca cult. Explains how Incas nested folk charters into Tahuantinsuyu; how Chimor royal kin-groups and ancestor platforms “glued” the apex like an ayllu (as of Moseley 2001). Model for extensive/nodal states arising from folk economic archipelagos ([[tiwanaku-culture]], [[vertical-archipelago]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of ethnographic present and Inca-centric projection onto Preceramic and Early Intermediate societies. Always attribute period and source. “Maximal ayllu” as political model for Moche/Tiwanaku/Wari is Moseley’s interpretive scaffolding, not a proven continuity of emic terminology. Ancestor-hostage and mummy-cult details rest on ethnohistory + selective archaeology.

### From D'Altroy 2002

[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy (2002)]] synthesizes the highland *ayllu* as the core kin-corporate landholding unit of traditional central Andean communities “just as they were in Inca times” (his continuity claim — treat as ethnohistory + ethnographic analogy). Members believe they share a common ancestor and place of origin (*paqarisqa*/*pacarina*); hold productive resources collectively and allocate by family size and status; ideally include varied farmlands and pastures so households access full production zones. A highland community (*llaqta*) usually contained one or more *ayllu*; sizes varied to several lineages and hundreds of households. *Ayllu* were often divided into two or three opposing parts — upper (*hanan*) and lower (*hurin*), or left-right-center (*ichuq-allawqa-chaupi*) — structuring marriage exchange so spouses were preferably taken from the other side of a flexibly defined *ayllu*, keeping resources in the larger group (see [[dual-organization]]).

**Land and household.** Rights derived from *ayllu* membership by birth; newly married couples received a *tupu* of maize land (year’s subsistence need) — Garcilaso equated ~0.5+ ha, but D'Altroy stresses size varied with ecology and fallow; another *tupu* with a son, half with a daughter; allotments reduced at children’s marriage and returned to common holdings on the last of a generation’s death for redistributive assignment by the lord. Higher-status households held more lands across more zones; elites’ multi-wife right increased household labor. Typical complete household = male head (*hatun runa*), wife, children, unmarried/widowed adult kin; woman dominant in the domestic sphere (Cobo on *chicha* as cuisine essential). Descent: some Andean systems ambilineal; Inca royalty especially weighted the female line (emperors identified with mother’s kin); non-royal Inca *ayllu* and most highland peoples seem patrilineal (Rowe; cf. Zuidema, Rostworowski).

**Reciprocity and verticality.** Households distributed members across ecozones for self-reliance ([[vertical-archipelago]]); balanced labor exchange *ayni* and asymmetric *minka* (repaid with produce share). When men were called for military/[[mita-labor]] duty, neighbors tended fields. Farming teams of ~7–8 men with *chakitaqlla* foot plows, women following to plant; harvest celebration *Aymoray*. Kin terminology extended beyond blood to statues (*wawqi* alter egos), landscape ancestors, and (via Urton’s modern ethnomathematics) arithmetic/weaving designs. Imperial decimal units drafted *ayllu*/ethnic modules under hereditary *kuraka*; late continuity claim: many highland communities still moiety-divided for marriage and still divide civic tasks among *ayllu* (Isbell, Urton via D'Altroy).

**Cuzco scale.** Non-royal *ayllu* beyond the ten royal [[panaqa]]; *panaqa* and affiliated *ayllu* jointly tended assigned [[zeqe-system]] *wak’a*; mythic founding of ten Pacariqtambo *ayllu* into Hanan/Hurin halves. Do not equate community dual *ayllu* with imperial Hanan/Hurin without argument.

## De la Cadena 2015 — Ayllu as Socionatural Relation

[[de-la-cadena-marisol|Marisol de la Cadena]] (*[[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015|Earth Beings]]*, fieldwork [[pacchanta|Pacchanta]] 2002–2007) retheorizes ayllu beyond the land-corporation definition standard in Andeanist ethnography. Via Justo Oxa (bilingual teacher): “Ayllu is like a weaving… people, animals, mountains, plants… are like the threads”; a *runa* is always **in-ayllu** with other beings (attributed). Thus ayllu is a **relational mode** — the socionatural collective of humans, [[earth-beings|tirakuna]], animals, and plants with relations integrally implied (resonance with Wagner’s fractal person; attributed) — not only an institution of collective property. Separation risks *wakcha* (orphan) status.

Political consequences (attributed): Mariano Turpo as *personero* was chosen by the ayllu and spoke **from** it (*ayllumanta parlaqta*), not merely *for* it as a liberal representative separable from the represented. “Peasant movement” is a [[controlled-equivocation|controlled equivocation]] that made the Lauramarca confrontation audible to modern politics while earth-beings remained “ontologically unconstitutional.” At the 1969 land-transfer ceremony, *santa tira* / *pukara* / *inqaychu* practices co-composed the event with state reform narrative. Mining threats (Sinakara/Ausangate, 2006 demo) target the relational emplacement of ayllu, not only land titles — see [[cosmopolitics]] and Epilogue of *Earth Beings*.
