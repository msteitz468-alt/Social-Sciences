# Thought Collective

## Definition as Coined

[[fleck-ludwik|Ludwik Fleck]] (*Genesis and Development of a Scientific Fact*, 1935) defined the **thought collective** (Denkkollektiv) as the social carrier of a **thought style** (Denkstil): the shared preconditions of cognition that determine which questions are reasonable and which answers count as true or false. Fleck argued the style is essentially hidden from its members: "The individual within the collective is never, or hardly ever, conscious of the prevailing thought style which almost always exerts an absolutely compulsive force upon his thinking" (1935, p. 41, as quoted in Douglas 1986). Fleck distinguished the collective proper (true believers) from the looser "thought community" of formal members, and described a structure of inner elite, mass periphery, and "ossification at the rim."

## Semantic History

- **1935** — coined by Fleck in a study of the stabilization of syphilis as a scientific fact; largely ignored (reviewers preferred Popper's contemporaneous *Logik der Forschung*).
- **1962/1979** — Kuhn's *Structure of Scientific Revolutions* cited Fleck (the first public attention since 1937); the 1979 English translation carried Kuhn's ambivalent foreword calling the concept "intrinsically misleading… an individual mind writ large." Kuhn's paradigm concept is widely read as its descendant.
- **1986** — Mary Douglas (*[[douglas-how-institutions-think-1986|How Institutions Think]]*) treated the thought collective as the modern equivalent of [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim's]] social group and the thought style as the equivalent of collective representations, proposing the updated vocabulary **"thought world"** and linking it to Nelson Goodman's *Ways of Worldmaking* (1978) and Howard Becker's *Art Worlds* (1982) as independent convergences.

## Operationalizations

Never formally operationalized. Fleck offered structural markers (stability gradient; center–periphery ranking). Douglas (1986) supplied a functional test she derived from Fleck's "self-sustaining loop": communication within a collective, "irrespective of content or logical justification," corroborates the thought structure for sociological reasons (Fleck 1935, p. 103).

## Applications

Douglas (1986) used the concept to explain Fleck's own reception (Popper inside a strong Vienna collective, Fleck outside any philosophical collective), and generalized it into her theory that institutions do the classifying, remembering, and forgetting for their members. In the sociology of science it feeds the lineage running to Kuhn's paradigms and constructivist science studies (see [[social-construction-of-reality]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Kuhn (1979) objected that the concept smuggles individual-mind language into a collective subject; Rawls (1971) rejected any treatment of society as "an organic whole with a life of its own." Douglas (1986) answered that the crude reading is a strawman — "of course institutions cannot have minds" — and reversed the metaphor: the individual mind is "society writ small."
