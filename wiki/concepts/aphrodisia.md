# Aphrodisia

## Definition as Coined

*Aphrodisia* (τὰ ἀφροδίσια, "the works of Aphrodite," *erga Aphroditēs*) is the classical Greek term that [[foucault-michel|Foucault]], in *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984), takes as the **ethical substance** of Greek sexual morality — the material that Greek moral reflection worked upon. He defines it (attributed) as "the acts, gestures, and contacts that produce a certain form of pleasure." It is a nominalized adjective covering acts-and-their-pleasures, rendered by the Romans as *venerea*, and — this is Foucault's central historical claim — **not coextensive with the modern concept of "sexuality" nor with the Christian concept of "the flesh."** The Greeks, he argues, had no unifying category naming a domain or a nature behind these acts.

## Semantic History

Foucault's point is comparative and periodizing: the *aphrodisia* name **acts**, where "the flesh" (Christian) will name a fallen condition to be deciphered and "sexuality" (modern) will name a domain of the self to be known. Where later moralities classify and interrogate, the Greeks (on Foucault's reading) produced **no systematic inventory of licit and illicit acts** and showed little concern with the morphology of the act (positions, gestures). Their concern was the **dynamics** of the activity — its force and intensity — bound in a single circular "ensemble" of act, desire (*epithumia*), and pleasure (*hēdonē*). Quote: "the ontology to which this ethics of sexual behavior referred was… an ontology of a force that linked together acts, pleasures, and desires." This is what Foucault calls an *ontology of force*, not of classification — and it sets the term's meaning permanently against the later Christian elision of pleasure and intensification of desire as the site of moral concern (all attributed to Foucault).

## Operationalizations — the two variables

Foucault reconstructs moral judgment of the *aphrodisia* as turning on two axes (attributed), neither of them the type of object or practice:
1. a **quantitative** variable — degree, frequency, intensity; **moderation versus excess** is the ethical question, not the choice of partner or act;
2. a **role/polarity** variable — the **active** position (penetrating, *aphrodisiazein*) versus the **passive** position (penetrated, *aphrodisiasthēnai*), held to be **isomorphic with social and political role** (the free adult male as active subject; women, boys, and slaves as legitimate "object-partners"). He reads Aristotle's "the female, as female, is passive, and the male, as male, is active" as emblematic.

Sexual pleasure was held (per Foucault's reading) to be **natural and necessary** (for procreation and the endurance of families and cities) yet **ontologically inferior** (shared with animals, mixed with privation) and paradoxically **the most intense** of pleasures — a combination giving it a "stasiastic" (rebellious) and "hyperbolic" potential that required regulation not because it was vicious but because its natural force tended to overshoot nature's own measure. This is explicitly contrasted with the Christian doctrine of the Fall as the source of excessive desire. Sexual ethics was structurally continuous with **alimentary ethics** — food, wine, and sex as "analogous ethical material" posing a shared question of right *use* ([[chresis]]).

## Applications

The *aphrodisia* organize the whole of *The Use of Pleasure*: their **use** ([[chresis]]) is governed by need, timeliness, and status; their mastery is the work of [[enkrateia]]; their management as a matter of health belongs to [[dietetics]]; and the free man's relation to them across the domains of body, marriage, and the love of boys is the subject of Foucault's four-aspect analysis of [[ethics-foucauldian|moral subjectivation]]. The activity/passivity structure generates what Foucault calls the "antinomy of the boy" (see [[ethics-foucauldian]]): the freeborn boy is the only honorable male object of pleasure yet cannot embrace the passive "object" role without jeopardizing his future status as a free citizen.

## Persistence into the imperial period (*The Care of the Self*)

In *[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, 1984) Foucault uses Artemidorus' 2nd-century-CE dream-book (*Oneirocritica*) to argue that the classical structure of the *aphrodisia* **persisted as everyday common sense** long after the classical philosophers — dreams of sex are interpreted by the dreamer's active/passive position and the social status of the partner, not by the form of the act. "Sexual dreams are social dreams": the sexual scene resolves into predictions about business, family, and civic career. The point is continuity — the same schema, now unreflective valuation rather than philosophy — which sets up the volume's real question of what *did* change (the [[care-of-the-self|care of the self]], the [[ethics-foucauldian|conjugal ethic]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

- The archive is **prescriptive, elite, and male** — philosophers' and physicians' texts (Plato, Aristotle, Xenophon, the Hippocratic corpus), not a record of ancient sexual behavior. Foucault's *aphrodisia* is a reconstruction of how a small class of free men were told to think, not a description of what Greeks did; note the sampling frame.
- The sharp **Greek/Christian/modern discontinuity** is Foucault's thesis, contested by classicists and historians who find more continuity (and more codification) in ancient sexual morality than his reading allows (attributed).
- The active/passive isomorphism draws on and is qualified by K. J. Dover's work on Greek homosexuality; Foucault's use of it is attributed, not settled fact. On the four-way non-identity: the Greek "virile/effeminate" distinction does **not** map onto the modern hetero/homosexual axis — a point Foucault stresses and this wiki records as his reading, not a timeless truth about sexuality.
