## Definition as Coined

Weber defines an "order" as present when action is on average oriented to definable principles, and states the order is "validated" when this orientation occurs because the principles are recognized as binding or exemplary (Ch. 1 §5). The Chance that actors actually orient action to a conception of a legitimate order is what Weber calls the order's "validity" — Weber gives the example of an official's routine office attendance reflecting duty and order-validity, not merely habit or self-interest (Ch. 1 §5).

Weber distinguishes convention from Law by their outward guarantee: an order is "convention" when deviation meets general, tangible social disapproval, and "Law" when deviation meets physical or mental coercion applied by a specialized staff whose task is to enforce conformity or punish contravention (Ch. 1 §6). Weber holds that the decisive feature of "law," as he uses the term, is the existence of a staff dedicated to enforcement — not the form of coercion or the presence of a formal judicial body; Weber notes even a clan practicing reprisal or feud can satisfy the concept if the reaction "validates" some order (Ch. 1 §6).

Weber sets out four bases on which actors ascribe legitimate validity to an order (Ch. 1 §7): (a) tradition, (b) affective/emotional belief, (c) value-rational belief, and (d) positive statute whose legality is believed — itself legitimated either by agreement among interested parties or by imposition grounded in a believed legitimate rule of some persons over others. Weber holds tradition to be the original and most universal basis, reinforced historically by superstitious fear of the magical consequences of deviation, while today, per Weber, belief in legality is the most common basis (Ch. 1 §7).

## Semantic History

Tribe's Translation Appendix notes the German *Geltung* (rendered "validity" here, but variably elsewhere as "enactment," "prevailing," or "currency" depending on context) denotes a processual, not fixed, empirical/legitimacy status — Tribe deliberately avoids imposing one single fixed English rendering across the text. Tribe separately notes Weber's *Herrschaft* (rendered "rule"/"rulership," never "domination" or "authority") is the closely related organizing-principle concept for any *Verband* founded on an order's validity.

## Operationalizations

Weber argues that, unlike jurisprudence, sociology recognizes no absolute alternative between an order's validation and non-validation — multiple, even mutually contradictory orders can be simultaneously "valid" for the same group or even the same actor, illustrated by a duelist orienting to a code of honour when concealing an affair of honour but to criminal law when surrendering to authorities (Ch. 1 §5).

## Applications

Weber uses the legitimate-order concept to ground [[social-relationship]] persistence and, later in the chapter, the definitions of organisation, political organisation, and the state, each requiring a validated order enforced by staff (Ch. 1 §12, §17).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber sharply criticizes Rudolf Stammler (in *Wirtschaft und Recht*) for conflating empirical and normative validity, treating "order" as a Kantian "form" opposed to "content," and denying any possible causal relationship between order and actual action — Weber calls this "hopelessly muddled" (Ch. 1 §5). Weber also notes the contrast between agreement-based and imposed legality is only relative in practice: majorities can effectively impose on dissenting minorities, and minorities can manufacture nominal majorities, such that, in Weber's words, "the principle of majority is mere facade" (Ch. 1 §7).
