# Theoretical Saturation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[glaser-strauss-discovery-grounded-theory-1967|The Discovery of Grounded Theory]]* (1967), [[glaser-barney-g|Glaser]] and [[strauss-anselm-l|Strauss]] define **theoretical saturation** of a category as the point when **no additional data** are being found that develop properties of that category — repeated similar instances yield empirical confidence that the category is saturated. Saturation guides when to stop sampling for a category under [[theoretical-sampling]] and when delimiting theory under the [[constant-comparative-method]].

## Semantic History

Core GT stop-rule term. Later qualitative research often uses "saturation" more loosely (theme exhaustion without theoretical sampling) — flag the 1967 technical sense (category properties, theory-driven sampling).

## Operationalizations

Judgmental, theory-driven, **category-specific** — not a fixed sample-size rule. Research nears closure when core categories are saturated and the framework forms a systematic, usable, publishable theory (Ch. IX) — though theory-as-process can still develop after publication.

## Applications

Theoretical sampling (Ch. III) and constant comparative method (Ch. V) in *Discovery*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Hard to audit; critics (later literature) argue it can be claimed too early or used rhetorically. Contested as a criterion of completeness. Because theoretical sampling for generation does not code extensively enough for provisional tests, saturation is a **generation** stop-rule, not a verification sample-size claim.
