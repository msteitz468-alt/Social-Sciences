# Complexity (Luhmann)

## Definition as Coined

[[luhmann-niklas|Luhmann]] gives **complexity** a precise relational definition: "an
interconnected collection of elements [is] 'complex' when, because of immanent constraints in
the elements' connective capacity, it is no longer possible at any moment to connect every
element with every other element." Complexity is thus a **threshold** — the point past which
total interconnection fails and **selection becomes forced**. His central formula: "Complexity...
means being forced to select; being forced to select means contingency; and contingency means
risk."

Luhmann distinguishes two senses: (1) a structural/objective sense tied to element-connectivity,
and (2) complexity as "a measure for indeterminacy or lack of information" — the information a
system lacks to fully observe itself or its environment.

## Semantic History

The concept comes from general systems theory (Ashby's "requisite variety"), which Luhmann
turns into a foundational sociological premise: **the environment is always more complex than
the system** — "for each system the environment is more complex than the system itself." No
system can match its environment's variety, so it "must compensate for its own inferior
complexity by superior order." This **complexity gradient** is constitutive, not incidental:
without it there would be "only undifferentiated chaos." Luhmann's slogan is "only complexity
can reduce complexity" — reduction reconstructs a complex nexus by a second nexus with fewer
relations, never a return to ontological atoms. Complexity is also **temporalized**: systems
raise their own complexity by shrinking the duration of their elements to vanishing events,
buying "maximum freedom vis-à-vis time" at the cost of constant reproduction. Complexity
"cannot be observed" directly — any observation of it is already a reduction — which is why the
theory, in describing complexity, "cannot help but perform the very operations it describes."

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct, despite the information-theoretic framing; it functions as an
analytic and a "methodological recipe" — to seek "theories that can succeed in explaining the
normal as improbable."

## Applications

- The problem to which [[social-systems-theory|system formation]], [[meaning]],
  [[self-reference]], and [[functional-differentiation]] are all responses.
- Grounds Luhmann's reorientation of functional analysis toward complexity rather than system
  maintenance, and his account of madness as a psychic system's "hyper-complexity."

## Critiques and Limitations

Luhmann uses "complexity" both as a rigorous relational concept and as a near-metaphysical
condition of all order, and critics note the two are not always kept distinct. The claim that
complexity is unobservable and self-implicating protects the concept from measurement but also
from test. Attributed throughout to Luhmann.
