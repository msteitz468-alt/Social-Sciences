# Informational City

Castells’s term for the urban form emerging with the informational / [[network-society|network society]]: metropolitan space reorganized around the [[space-of-flows|space of flows]], advanced services, and dualized local places. Distinct from (but in dialogue with) Sassen’s [[global-city|global city]]. See *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Ch. 6; earlier monograph *The Informational City* (1989) not yet ingested.

## Definition as Coined

Castells (attributed): the informational city is not a form but a **process** — the expression of the space of flows in urban structure. Against the “electronic cottage” / end-of-cities thesis: telework diversifies but does not dissolve cities; home-centeredness combines with mobility; agglomeration of advanced services and face-to-face micro-networks of decision-making persist (engaging Sassen).

**Regional variants (attributed):**

- **American profile:** not fully Edge City, but the relationship among fast ex-urban development, inner-city decay, and suburban obsolescence.
- **European metros:** business center as networked economic engine; elites often re-appropriate historic central areas (UK exception: upscale suburbs/villages).
- **Mega-cities:** globally connected nodes that can be locally disconnected — concentration of wealth/innovation with landscapes of exclusion outside nodal landing places.

## Semantic History

Builds on 1989 *Informational City*; 1996 places it under full space-of-flows theory. Relation to mega-city, Edge City, and [[global-city]] constructs: complementary scales and emphases rather than synonyms. Castells: global city is “not a place, but a process.”

## Operationalizations

Advanced-services location and hierarchies (e.g. FedEx); new industrial spaces (electronics division of labor); Pearl River Delta and other mega-urban regions; dual labor and residential structure.

## Applications

[[network-society-theory]]; [[urbanization]]; contrast/complement [[global-city-theory]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Process definition hard to measure; American/European typology dated to 1990s cases; relation to world-city network measurement literature (not ingested) unresolved on this page.
