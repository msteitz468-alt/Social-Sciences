# Military Ascendancy

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), chapters 8–9, [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]
argued (attributed) that the United States had undergone a **military ascendancy**: the
professional military hierarchy ("warlords") became a major institutional order alongside
corporations and the political directorate. Of the three circles composing the [[power-elite]]
in the mid-1950s, "it is the military that has benefited the most in its enhanced power."
Ascendancy is specified in **two senses**: as **personnel** (officers in civilian and corporate
roles) and as **justifying ideology** (a military definition of reality centering "defense" and
international affairs).

## Semantic History

Mills wrote against an older American antimilitary tradition and against reducing the phenomenon
to a "military clique" or coup. Ascendancy, he argued, came substantially via **civilian
default** — weak civil service, politics hidden behind expertise, public and elite acceptance of
military definitions — not only via military ambition. WWII's corporate–military merger hardened
into a **permanent war economy**. Later reception (Wolfe's afterword; military sociologists such
as Moskos) contested whether the military remained antidemocratic or ascendant in Mills's sense;
record those as positions on [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]] and the study page.

## Operationalizations

Indicators Mills used (attributed, mid-1950s frame):

- Budget share (he cited roughly 30–50%+ of federal budget for military/security; order-of-
  magnitude claim, not a modern recalculation)
- Board traffic ("get yourself a general"); officers as corporation executives
- Displacement of professional diplomacy; bypass of diplomats/UN in favor of military channels
- Science, education, and mass PR oriented to military metaphysics
- Career interchange among military, corporate, and political posts

## Applications

- Explains the *shape* of the power elite "just now" (1950s): easiest to specify unity via
  military ascendancy without reducing the elite to the military alone.
- Links to [[organized-irresponsibility]]: grave decisions (including undebated nuclear matters
  in Mills's closing diagnosis) under official secrecy.
- Contrasts with simple Marxian "economic man rules" and simple liberal "political man rules."

## Critiques and Limitations

Wolfe (Afterword) and later writers questioned the trajectory of military ascendancy under
changed global conditions; others stressed racial integration and civic roles of the armed forces
as counter-images. All of Mills's 1950s operational claims are date-stamped; do not update them
silently into wiki-voice present tense.
