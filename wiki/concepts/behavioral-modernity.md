## Definition as Coined

**Behavioral modernity** denotes the suite of behaviors held to distinguish modern
*Homo sapiens* from earlier and contemporary archaic humans — symbolic expression (art,
ornament, notation), technological innovation and standardization, planning depth, and
cumulative culture. It is defined etically by an archaeological "package" rather than by any
single trait, and it is distinguished sharply from **anatomical** modernity: the two need
not appear together, and their apparent non-coincidence is the concept's central problem.

## Semantic History

The term crystallized in the modern-human-origins debate of the 1980s–1990s as fossil and
genetic evidence converged on a recent African origin for anatomically modern people
(~200–100 ka) while the richest archaeological signature of "modern" behavior — the Eurasian
**Upper Paleolithic** — appeared only ~50–40 ka. [[klein-richard|Richard Klein]], in
[[klein-human-career-1999|*The Human Career*]] (1999), gave the concept its most influential
strong form: a **late, relatively abrupt threshold** (~50–40 ka), possibly caused by a
**neural/genetic transformation** conferring the fully modern capacity for language and
innovation (attributed). The rival reading — Sally McBrearty & Alison Brooks' "the revolution
that wasn't" (2000) — treats modern behavior as **gradually and cumulatively assembled**
across the African Middle Stone Age, denying any single threshold or biological switch. The
concept has since been criticized as Eurocentric (privileging the European record) and as a
checklist that can conflate preservation and sampling bias with real behavioral change.

### Phillipson — African priority of cultural modernity (2005)

*(Attributed — [[phillipson-african-archaeology-2005]], Ch. 4 close; **as of Phillipson 2005**.)*

Phillipson argues that archaeological, palaeontological, and genetic studies point to sub-Saharan Africa’s key rôle in both anatomical and cultural/behavioural modernity: processes beginning possibly ~250 ka and continuing into the period ~50–35 ka when their end-results appear relatively suddenly in Europe. **“Both humanity itself and cultural/anatomical modernity were African developments”** — a Eurocentric reading yields a biased picture (Phillipson). Technologically he ties this to African trajectories through mode-3 prepared-core industries into mode-5 microlithic composite tools (including [[howiesons-poort-industry]] priority relative to European Upper Palaeolithic checklists); he is cautious about over-reading single-site symbolic “firsts” (e.g. [[blombos-cave]]). Aligns more with gradual/African-assembly readings than with Klein’s late neural switch — record as **position**, not adjudication.

## Klein's Formulation (attributed, as of Klein 1999)

Klein's version is the concept's sharpest statement, and the reason the term is inseparable
from him:

- **The anatomy/behavior lag.** "The modern physical form evolved before the modern capacity
  for culture." Anatomically modern or near-modern Africans (and the Skhul/Qafzeh people of
  the Levant, ~120–80 ka) were, in Klein's reading, "cognitively human, but not cognitively
  modern in the sense that all living people are" — their tool kits, absence of art, and
  sparse populations resembled those of the Neanderthals.
- **The abrupt shift.** "Before this time [~50 ka] morphology and behavior appear to have
  evolved more or less in tandem, very slowly, but after this time morphology remained
  relatively stable while behavioral (cultural) change accelerated rapidly." Klein denies that
  an "early Upper Paleolithic" is a true transitional stage.
- **The neural hypothesis.** "What could explain this better than a neural change that
  promoted the extraordinary modern human ability to innovate?" Klein specifically ties the
  change to the capacity for "a fully vocal language, phonemicized, syntactical, and
  infinitely open and productive" (invoking Bickerton). He does **not** claim Neanderthals had
  apelike brains; brain size was already modern nearly everywhere by ~200 ka, so any change at
  ~50 ka must be *organizational*, not volumetric.
- **Klein's own hedge.** He concedes "the neural hypothesis for modern human origins is not a
  proper scientific hypothesis, since it is not currently testable" from fossils — because
  skulls give only speculative evidence for brain structure. He nonetheless prefers it to the
  purely social/technological alternative, which he calls "more circular" for failing to
  explain why organization or technology should change so suddenly.

## Operationalizations

Klein's diagnostic "package" — the traits he treats as present after ~50–40 ka and largely
absent among earlier near-moderns and Neanderthals: blade-based stone technology; large,
standardized, and diverse formal tool types (endscrapers, burins, backed and truncated
pieces, bifacial points); **routine** manufacture of formal bone, ivory, and antler artifacts;
unequivocal art and personal ornamentation (beads); structured dwellings; elaborate,
grave-good burials; long-distance transport/exchange of raw materials; systematic fishing and
fowling; and sharply increased artifact variability across space and time. The counter-
inventory (McBrearty & Brooks) instead itemizes African Middle Stone Age "firsts" —
pigment use, blades, barbed points, beads, and long-distance transport appearing piecemeal
and much earlier — to argue the package was assembled gradually, not switched on.

## Applications

The concept organizes the archaeological side of the [[modern-human-origins-debate]]: it lets
"modern origins" be posed as a *behavioral* rather than merely anatomical event, and it is the
frame within which the [[upper-paleolithic-revolution]], the Middle-to-Upper-Paleolithic
transition, and contested early African symbolic finds (Blombos ochre and beads, Katanda
bone points, Enkapune Ya Muto ostrich-eggshell beads) are argued. It also structures the
reading of the Neanderthals: whether the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate|Mousterian]]
"sameness" reflects a cognitive ceiling or merely different preservation.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **The gradualist critique** (McBrearty & Brooks 2000; and much subsequent African MSA
  research): the "revolution" is an artifact of a Eurocentric, well-sampled European record;
  in Africa the traits accumulate over >100 kyr, undermining both the abrupt threshold and a
  single-mutation cause.
- **Untestability.** As Klein himself grants, the neural/genetic mechanism cannot be tested
  from fossil endocasts; a favored genetic candidate (*FOXP2*) has not delivered a clean
  behavioral switch.
- **Taphonomy and sampling.** Organic ornaments and structures preserve poorly; "absence" in
  the Middle Paleolithic may be non-preservation, not non-behavior — Katanda-type MSA barbed-bone-point finds (Klein's own conceded "most serious obstacle") show how a single well-dated site can
  strain the whole model.
- **Checklist problem.** Bundling heterogeneous traits into one "package" risks treating a
  gradient of behaviors as a single on/off variable, and privileges materially visible proxies
  for cognition over unpreserved ones.

## Stringer & Gamble 1993 Layer (attributed; do not overwrite Klein)

*Source: [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993|Stringer & Gamble, In Search of the Neanderthals (1993)]]*, especially chs. 6–9. Date-stamp: **as of Stringer & Gamble 1993** (pre-full aDNA horizon). This layer sits **beside** Klein's formulation above — parallel attributed program, not a replacement.

### Anatomy ≠ behaviour

S&G argue that African fossil sequences show morphological transition Ancients → borderlines → early moderns before ~100 ka, well before Upper Palaeolithic / Late Stone Age ~40 ka: anatomy and archaeological behaviour **no longer expected to march in step**. Earliest anatomically modern Africa/Levant specimens "looked like us but 'were not us,'" lacking ~100 ka of subsequent change (attributed, ch. 6). Archaeology of earliest anatomically modern humans in the Middle East (e.g. [[qafzeh]]) "suggests a more complicated picture than a single centre for the origins of both modern physique and modern behaviour" (attributed, ch. 9). Levantine Middle Palaeolithic tools accompany both Neanderthals ([[kebara]]) and much older moderns (Qafzeh) — technology ≠ species (attributed, ch. 7).

### Where modern behaviour originates (uncertain)

Geographical origin of **anatomically** modern humans: S&G place somewhere in Africa. Source of **modern behaviour** they treat as less certain. Time-lag they emphasize: earliest modern-looking East Africa (Omo Kibish perhaps ~130 ka), Middle East (Qafzeh, Skhul ~100 ka), Australia (~55 ka?), symbolic explosion after ~40 ka — hard to name one centre. Early Australian dates might suggest SE Asian cultural evolution then rapid world spread; the Middle East as crossroads is another candidate exporting behaviour east and west. "The source of modern behaviour will remain conjectural until we have a better understanding of which evolutionary forces and mechanisms actually shaped modern behaviour" (attributed, S&G 1993).

Wherever new behavioural developments occurred, S&G argue they did so rapidly (**switch**, not dimmer): behaviour based on symbolically organized culture spread "rather like a virus among the host population," adapting to modern anatomy already present for perhaps ~70,000 years, then expanding with the host. Global expansion beginning ~60–40 ka is, for them, evidence of **replacement** not continuity as the mechanism of the human revolution — without pinning the exact origin locale (attributed POSITION, 1993).

### The behavioural package and the 60–40 ka window

Their summary table of behavioural changes (art, bone tools, blades, ceramic, body ornament, artifact styles, built hearths, structured living space, burials, storage, huts, microliths, quarries, regional art, long-distance exchange, chains of connection, harsh-environment occupation, social storage, sea voyaging, colonization of extreme habitats, spoken language, forward planning) identifies **60–40 ka** as crucial — "only after this point that so many of the elements associated with modern behaviour are to be widely found" (not yet the entire later cultural package) (attributed, 1993). Focusing on European evidence, they argue Modern behaviour was of a completely different order of complexity from predecessors; replacement of Neanderthals by Moderns is the only plausible explanation given absence of evidence for gradual change from one way of life to the other (POSITION). Comparison is framed at three levels (ch. 7 program): **campsite construction**, **landscape use**, **expansion into new habitats**.

### Speech, culture, and social scale

- **Davidson & Noble**: "There can be no such thing as culture without language… It will therefore be misleading to talk of culture for any hominids before fully modern humans." S&G resist pinning modernity on one element: spoken language adds incalculably to culture elaboration, but single-element definitions risk circularity (attributed vs POSITION, 1993).
- **Neanderthal speech**: referring ch. 4 anatomy, S&G argue Neanderthals could communicate and no doubt spoke, albeit simply and slowly; they lacked **complex** spoken language because they did not need it for their social life. Robert Whallon: last-developing language element probably tense-modality referring to the future, appearing in colonization contexts for long-term planning and extended networks (attributed via S&G, 1993).
- **Tool-assisted hominids without symbolically organized culture**: Neanderthal material culture stone-dominated, not symbolically organized — "They did not have culture in the sense we understand it today and recognize it over the last 40,000 years." Artifacts may follow prototypes, but style (as S&G define it) played little part; MP hand axes/scrapers are repetitive like elaborate bird nests — birds nest-assisted, Neanderthals tool-assisted (attributed POSITION, 1993). Kuhn on stone-technology organization: diversity within uniformity — narrow stereotype of Neanderthals unwarranted (via S&G).
- **Social scale**: Neanderthal mating network ~175–500 for biological reproduction, but little interest beyond that limit in social ties; group self-sufficiency (food, mates, raw materials) basic strategy rather than negotiated alliances. Lack of art understandable — no need for ceremonial/negotiation props; fundamental Ancient/Modern difference is **social** (attributed, 1993). Style and long-distance social "insurance policies" (figurines, ornaments; Venus tradition 23–21 ka transcending archaeological cultures) only after ~40 ka; colonization of harsh environments and oceans as Modern-scale solutions (links [[gamble-clive]], *Timewalkers*).

### Technology caveats (not Modern markers alone)

[[levallois|Levallois]] is unlikely, in their view, to indicate the same forward planning as UP blade cores. Blade/PUP industries (Howiesons Poort, Amudian, Haua Fteah, Tabun) appear and disappear before genuine European Upper Palaeolithic; Boker Tachtit shows flake→blade shift within one sequence without fixed species equation. Australia colonized by Moderns with only rudimentary (not European UP blade) tools — **colonization is a better guide to fundamental behavioural change than stone typology** (attributed, 1993). [[aurignacian|Aurignacian]] is their European "true" UP package (blades + bone/ivory points + art + ornaments + organized campsites); [[chatelperronian|Châtelperronian]] is read as Neanderthal **imitation**, not independent invention of modern behaviour.

### Relation to Klein

Both S&G (1993) and Klein (1999) separate anatomical from behavioural modernity and emphasize a late, relatively abrupt Eurasian package after a lag. S&G stress **social networks, style, and colonization** and an imitation reading of late Neanderthal industries; Klein's sharpest statement adds an explicit **neural/genetic** hypothesis for the capacity switch. Gradualist African MSA critiques (McBrearty & Brooks 2000 and after) target both abrupt-threshold programs — file as later POSITION, not as S&G's own 1993 stance.

## From Gamble, *Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999) — network reading

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]*; as of Gamble 1999. Parallel to Klein and Stringer & Gamble layers above — do not overwrite them.)*

Gamble re-reads “modern” behaviour as the appearance of significant **[[intimate-effective-extended-networks|extended networks]]** and a **[[social-landscape-palaeolithic|social landscape]]**, not as a trait checklist or a single neural switch. Neanderthals, he argues, became capable prime-age hunters **without** an external symbolic system inscribed on the landscape or personifying objects at gatherings; transferable skill remained social engagement with the world rather than analysis of it (attributed, ch. 5). Society was founded on intimate and effective networks; extended networks remained largely unnegotiated; spatial patterning is not dramatically different from Boxgrove/Bilzingsleben scales despite longer transfers and Levallois chaînes (attributed). Against Aiello & Dunbar’s group-size→language selection, he holds selection for vocal communication came from **intensity and limited spatial extension** of interaction, not from ~150-person group size, and would not select for full symbolic language using material culture as external referent (attributed).

The 60–21 Kyr European frame is a long, **polythetic** transition “far removed from the expectations raised by phrases such as ‘the human revolution’” (attributed, ch. 6). Continuities (prime-aged hunting, regional occupation intensity) sit beside innovative rhythms (transfer distances, shells, ornament/sculpture/music); major **structural** change after ~33 Kyr bp: settlement hierarchies and pronounced regional material-culture variation (attributed). Two social systems — Neanderthal and Cro-Magnon — based on distinct uses of emotional, material, and symbolic resources; pure population-replacement narratives misrepresent transition subtlety; **agency** creates taskscapes irrespective of fossil name-tags (attributed).

[[release-from-proximity|Release from proximity]] “now occurred” in this transition (Rodseth et al. via Gamble): system integration via symbolic resources beyond co-presence limits (attributed, ch. 7). Only **complicated** societies (Strum & Latour) have significant extended networks; Middle Palaeolithic power was embodied gesture, Upper Palaeolithic power plays on personified artifacts as substitutes for co-presence (attributed, ch. 8). Landscape of habit → social landscape is not reduced to a single factor (language, memory, technology) nor pure gradual exteriorization; in Europe it was long-term innovation **and** contingent population replacement (attributed, ch. 3).

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]]. Attributed; (as of Stringer 2012). Parallel to Klein and S&G 1993 layers — densifies checklist, African MSA evidence, demography, and Neanderthal symbolism without overwriting them.*

### Checklist of “modern” behaviors

Stringer lists a common archaeological checklist while noting disputes over usefulness, universality, and archaeological inferability: complex/changing tools; formal bone/ivory/antler/shell artifacts; art (abstract and figurative); organized structures/hearths; long-distance transport; ritual/burial treatment; cultural buffering of extremes; complex food-gathering/processing (nets, traps, fishing, cooking); higher hunter-gatherer-like densities (as of Stringer 2012). He judges modern humans had developed **most** checklist attributes by ~60 ka, though not always present as a package (attributed).

### Human Revolution historiography and rival programs

- **~35 ka European Human Revolution** (Stringer co-organized the 1987 Cambridge conference): Cro-Magnon arrival linked to complex language, art, specialized technologies vs “behaviorally inferior” Neanderthals — later criticized as Eurocentric (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Klein (via Stringer):** ~50 ka African mutations enhanced brain function/language; morphology first, behavior later; pre-50 ka evidence unconvincing; reorganization not size (brains already modern ≥200 ka) (attributed). Stringer **disagrees** with a unique genetic switch but agrees language was critically important; frontal heightening and parietal expansion already at Omo Kibish and [[herto]] ~100 ka earlier — no clear physical brain change at 50 ka (as of Stringer 2012). Previc dopamine-critical-level ~80 ka hypothesis also recorded (via Stringer).
- **Mellars:** accelerated African change ~60–80 ka (blades, end scrapers/burins, microliths, leaf points, bone tools, perforated shells, engraved ochre, differentiated cave occupation, marine fish, possible undergrowth burning); neurological switch + climatic fluctuations (possibly Toba ~73 ka) (via Stringer).
- **McBrearty & Brooks:** advanced technologies, range expansion, specialized hunting/fishing/shellfishing, long-distance trade, symbolic pigments across African MSA ~100–250 ka — early assembly then later export; “eureka moment” quest Eurocentric (via Stringer). They link behavioral and morphological species origin to early MSA, not late MSA/LSA near the exodus (as of Stringer 2012).

### African MSA symbolic evidence (as of Stringer 2012)

- **Still Bay / Howiesons Poort:** sophisticated stone (Still Bay heat-treated); shell/ostrich-eggshell beads; symbolic red ocher; single-grain OSL (Jacobs, Roberts et al. 2008): Still Bay brief episode ~72 ka; Howiesons Poort ~65–60 ka — shorter than some prior 50+ ka spans; successors after a gap returned to more conservative MSA (attributed).
- **[[blombos-cave]]:** Still Bay points; earliest pressure flaking ~55 ka before Solutrean Europe; engraved red ochre; hundreds of *Nassarius* beads with piercing, wear, color modification (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Pinnacle Point PP13B ~160 ka:** ~60 hematite pieces, possible composites, earliest definite shellfish; brilliant red selection suggests symbolic use (via Stringer).
- **Shell beads + red ocher** spanning early modern range ~last interglacial humidity peak — South Africa to Morocco and into western Asia at Skhul and Qafzeh; multiple Moroccan/Algerian *Nassarius* sites ~80–100 ka (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Skhul ~115 ka:** oldest known symbolic burial (man with wild boar jaw); shell beads; heated pigments. **Qafzeh ~100 ka:** pierced shells, red ochre, burials including child under deer antlers (as of Stringer 2012).
- **Limits:** no strong figurative/representational art before European material ~40 ka; no symbolic burials older than Skhul/Qafzeh ~100 ka (Herto suggestive of ritual handling); red pigment use in Africa beyond 250 ka (Kapthurin, Olorgesailie), stronger from ~120 ka (as of Stringer 2012).

### “Flickering candle,” demography, Australia

African modernity signals intermittent until near final exodus ~55 ka; small populations lose innovations (Tasmania analogy; Still Bay/Howiesons Poort rise and fall) (as of Stringer 2012). Powell, Thomas, and Shennan simulations: cultural accumulation accelerates when subpopulation density and intergroup migration pass a critical threshold; genetic data suggest Africa may have reached such a threshold ~100 ka (via Stringer). Earliest Australia (~45 ka) largely lacks typical behavioral-modernity markers despite seafaring and Mungo ocher burial/cremation ~42 ka — plausibly low density and founder isolation, not non-modernity; mainland “Human Revolution” after ~12 ka as density rose (as of Stringer 2012).

### Bead systems and release from proximity

d’Errico and Vanhaeren: early MSA/MP *Nassarius*/ostrich eggshell beads reinforce social networks via exchange/gift-giving without large status differences; by ~40 ka rarer materials and specialist-made beads (e.g. Aurignacian ivory per Randall White) mark status and group identity (via Stringer). Gamble (following Rodseth): symbolism with language gives modern humans “[[release-from-proximity|release from proximity]]” (as of Stringer 2012).

### Neanderthal symbolism and Stringer’s 1993 revision

Spanish MP sites (Cueva Antón, Cueva de los Aviones; Zilhão): shells as pendants with pigments; Aviones ~50 ka argued too old for Cro-Magnon influence — strong evidence some Neanderthals expressed themselves symbolically on a par with many MSA Africans (as of Stringer 2012). Châtelperronian at Grotte du Renne (Arcy): Neanderthal remains + pierced teeth and worked bone/ivory — four interpretations (modern products/mixing; trade/scavenging; acculturation; independent parallel “modernization”) (attributed). Stringer revises 1993 Stringer–Gamble claim that Neanderthals could “emulate” but not “fully understand”: if they made/used pendants they participated in symbolism; therefore modern-behavior-from-MSA-genes-alone cannot be right unless Neanderthals had similar mutations or hybridization (as of Stringer 2012). Divje Babe “flute” rejected (carnivore punctures) (attributed).

### Clothing proxy

Body-louse mtDNA clocks (Stoneking; Toups et al.) estimate body-louse origin ~80–170 ka as proxy for regular clothing/bedding in Africa before diaspora (as of Stringer 2012).

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Mitchell & Lane (intro), Lawrence Barham (Ch. 24), Lyn Wadley (Ch. 25), Marlize Lombard (Ch. 26), Laura Basell (Ch. 27), Robert Foley (Ch. 20), Christian Tryon (Ch. 8) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**.*

Mitchell and Lane state that current evidence strongly suggests Africa is where the suite of cognitive skills/practices termed “behavioural modernity” first crystallised, arguably before effective *H. sapiens* dispersal beyond the continent. Barham historicizes the concept: it dates to late-1980s synthesis of fossil, genetic, and archaeological data (**Out of Africa II**); earlier Africanists assumed anatomical and behavioural modernity co-evolved, with European Upper Palaeolithic as innovation benchmark. Chronology revolution: MSA now **pre-dates anatomical modernity by ≥60–80 kya** at Kapthurin ~285 kya, Twin Rivers ~265 kya, Wonderwerk >276 kya; Klasies ~120 kya; Omo I ~195 kya (**as of handbook ~2012**). Cann et al. (1987) mtDNA + Stringer & Andrews (1988) → RAO; McBrearty & Brooks (2000) continent-wide trait list → gradual, regionally variable African modernity.

**Henshilwood & Marean (2003) critique (via Barham):** trait lists invalid (many “modern” traits are latitude-specific foraging responses). Definition: modernity = **social life via symbols**; material threshold = art + artefact style + socially organized space = “fully symbolic sapiens behaviour.” Barham reads field consensus (~2012) as crystallizing on symbol use, earliest unambiguous evidence Africa/Near East <200 kya within *H. sapiens*. **Challenges Barham records:** (1) Neanderthal ornaments/art threaten species-exclusive link; (2) “all markers together” may be contingent; (3) style, structured space, ochre pre-date *H. sapiens* — ochre functionally ambiguous (Wadley); (4) African/Near Eastern symbolic packages 120–60 kya are **episodic not cumulative** (d’Errico & Vanhaeren) — demography/ecology (Powell et al.); Barham prefers **“demographic modernity”** under population pressure; (5) gradualists emphasize hierarchical technologies/language-like rules before *H. sapiens*.

Lombard concludes southern African finds toppled Europe-first or post-50–40 kya models; material innovations form a mosaic; modern hunter-gatherer behavioural repertoire evolved during the southern African MSA — bone tools at [[blombos-cave]]/[[sibudu-cave]], beads, engraved ochre tradition to ≥100 kya, compound adhesives ≥70 kya, HP arrows 60–64 kya, snare/trap arguments, bedding/hearth maintenance. Wadley holds that hunting-and-gathering with out-of-body weapons is not restricted to *H. sapiens* (Schöningen spears ~400 kya; MSA points at Aduma and Kapthurin >350 kya), so theoretical frameworks need not separate MSA from LSA despite the MSA “grand moment.” Basell prefers “Middle Stone Age” over Clark modes because several modes can co-occur and more than one hominin species could have produced MSA artefacts; eastern Africa lags South/North Africa on beads/engraved ochre antiquity. Foley: modern anatomy and demographic bottlenecks appear to **precede** archaeological “modern behaviour” by tens of millennia. Tryon: heat treatment of rock by 164 ka in South Africa (Brown et al. 2009).

## Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*Source: [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]. All claims attributed; date-stamp empirical material **(as of Tattersall 2012)**.*

### Core thesis: symbolic/linguistic leap after modern form

Tattersall argues that ancestors made a qualitative leap from non-symbolic, nonlinguistic information-processing to the symbolic and linguistic condition of living humans — “unparalleled in history” — and that this leap was made **well after** the species acquired its distinctive modern biological form (attributed). Humans are not “an improved version” of apes but an “altogether unprecedented presence”; the uniquely modern sensibility was an **abrupt, recent** event within the tenure of people who looked exactly like us, crucially abetted by language / language-like symbolic recombination (attributed — Prologue, chs. 13–14). He defines the uniting trait as **symbolic capacity**: organizing the world into mental representations that can be recombined endlessly, creating alternative imagined worlds that ground cooperation and culture (attributed).

This is a **late-threshold, language-centered** position adjacent to [[klein-richard|Klein]]’s neural-switch model but framed through **exaptation** and cultural stimulus rather than a single late neural mutation as the sole story: anatomical *H. sapiens* arose as a relatively short-term developmental reorganization (~200 ka in his 2012 chronology); cognitive potential lay fallow until a cultural “keystone” (language invention in a small biologically prepared community) tipped the system into full symbolic use (attributed). FOXP2 is treated as necessary but not sufficient; many genes are involved; a single “language gene” is an illusion (attributed).

### Method: what counts as evidence of symbolism

Stone-tool skill and complexity are **not** ironclad proxies for symbolic thought; with few exceptions, confident inference of symbolic intent requires **overtly symbolic** objects (ornament, image, notation) (attributed). From the entire Neanderthal time–space range Tattersall finds nothing both confidently associated with Neanderthals and unambiguously interpretable as modern cognitive processes — skillful and complex, but “not in the way that we are” (attributed; contrast Stringer 2012’s more open reading of Neanderthal pendants above). Cro-Magnon European Upper Paleolithic art (~35–10 ka peak for cave art he highlights) is the first **overwhelming** evidence of minds that thought as we do; African MSA evidence supplies earlier **firm intimations** (attributed).

### African MSA symbolic sequence (as of 2012)

- **Skhul** (>100 ka): pierced marine shells and heated pigments — slender and “a bit arguable” (attributed).
- **Aterian** North Africa: beads and related signals; imperfectly dated in his synthesis (attributed).
- **[[blombos-cave]]** (~76–75 ka): perforated *Nassarius* beads; ochre plaques with deliberately engraved cross-hatching — earliest objects Tattersall **confidently** reads as symbolic; pressure-flaked fire-hardened silcrete tools (attributed).
- **Pinnacle Point**: occupation from ~164 ka; shellfish expansion; multi-stage silcrete heat-treatment by ~72 ka so complex it may imply forward planning approaching symbolic cognition — but technology alone remains only an arguable harbinger (attributed).
- **Klasies, Diepkloof, Enkapune ya Muto, Katanda**: further MSA ornament/engraving/bone-point signals in his inventory (attributed).

Earliest firm symbolic sensibility among newly evolved *H. sapiens* comes from Africa or its immediate environs; Eurasian/Australian spreads follow; Ice Age European art gives a **minimum** date for full modern sensibility, not the origin (attributed).

### Language as stimulus and lag after anatomy

Anatomically modern Levantine forays (Skhul/Qafzeh) with essentially Neanderthal-like lifestyles show that modern bodies alone did not immediately overwhelm competition; rapid Old World takeover awaited the behavioral package (attributed — see also [[modern-human-origins-debate]]). Language is Tattersall’s preferred cultural stimulus for abstract thought (communal possession; infinite combinatorics); theory-of-mind is a rival stimulus he finds less explanatory of the archaeological lag (attributed). Analogies he uses (Nicaraguan sign-language emergence; Ildefonso’s acquisition of language; Jill Bolte Taylor’s stroke) illustrate how language transforms experience — illustrations, not fossil evidence (attributed).

## Renfrew's sapient paradox (2003) as related framing

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-figuring-it-out-2003|Figuring It Out]]* 2003.)* Renfrew accepts a relatively early modern package (anatomical/genetic near-identity with ~40 kya humans; UP art and speech capacity) and treats the long lag until sedentism/farming/urbanism as the **[[sapient-paradox]]** — a problem *for* behavioral-modernity narratives that treat the Upper Palaeolithic "human revolution" as the decisive break. His resolution shifts explanation to Holocene **[[material-engagement]]** and a **material-symbolic** stage (revision of [[donald-merlin|Donald]]), not to a further neural revolution. Complements and tensions with [[cognitive-fluidity]] (Mithen) and with gradualist African models of modernity: all remain attributed positions. Genetic claims in Renfrew 2003 are pre-large-scale aDNA.

## Gamble — *Settling the Earth* (2013)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], *[[gamble-settling-the-earth-2013|Settling the Earth]]* 2013, esp. Box 6.1.)* Gamble treats "modern behaviour" as **semantically inflated** after Omo Kibish-scale early *sapiens* and reviews McBrearty & Brooks-style skill packages with a critique of checklist teleology (attributed). Terra 3 expansion (after 50 ka) is not read as the automatic payoff of a modern package already present in late Terra 2; the structural question is social — [[going-beyond]], universal kinship, storage/defence — not a single aesthetic or lithic revolution (attributed, chs 6–7; Europe’s UP demoted as a regional fit-for-purpose model). Horizon: as of 2013.
