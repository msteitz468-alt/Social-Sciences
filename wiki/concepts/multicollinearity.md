## Definition as Coined

Multicollinearity originates as a linear-regression diagnostic term for near-perfect
correlation among predictor variables, which inflates coefficient variance and can make
individual effects statistically inestimable. King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) generalize
the term beyond regression to any research design "where we can perfectly predict one
explanatory variable from one or more of the remaining explanatory variables," which
they treat as one of the two conditions rendering a design indeterminate (the other
being more inferences than observed implications; see
[[indeterminate-research-design]]) ([[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994]]).

## Semantic History

KKV's move is to migrate multicollinearity from a purely statistical-estimation problem
to a general logical property of any [[causal-inference]] design, quantitative or
qualitative: whenever two or more explanatory variables covary perfectly, "no
estimation method can give us unique estimates of the parameters," regardless of how
many observations are available. This reframes multicollinearity as a structural
feature of the case(s) and variables chosen — a design problem, not merely a
statistical-technique problem — and ties it directly to case-selection practice
(e.g., choosing cases along a size/proximity dimension where the two candidate
explanatory variables covary in lockstep, leaving no leverage to distinguish their
effects).

## Operationalizations

Operationally, KKV treat multicollinearity as diagnosable whenever a researcher's
selected cases or units leave two or more candidate causes empirically inseparable —
the classic illustrative confound being size and proximity (or similarly conflated
explanatory variables) that vary together across the observed cases such that the
design can "explain a lot with a little" only by leaving the two candidate mechanisms
indistinguishable. Where regression treats multicollinearity as a matter of degree
(variance inflation), KKV's generalized version is a binary indeterminacy condition:
perfect or near-perfect covariation among explanatory variables removes the possibility
of a unique causal estimate, however the estimate would be produced.

## Applications

KKV apply the concept to worked examples in their case-selection chapter — the same
set used for [[indeterminate-research-design]], including Skocpol on revolutions,
Porter on competitive advantage, Tilly on state survival, Kohli on Indian antipoverty
policy, Laitin on Somalia/Yoruba, and Achen and Snidal on deterrence. Kohli's
three-state study of Indian antipoverty policy is cited as an instance redeemed only by
disaggregating to many more sub-observations, since at the state level the two
variables of interest were left inseparable.

## Critiques and Limitations

Generalizing a regression term to qualitative research design has been questioned as
part of the wider [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] over whether case-study
and mechanism-based inference (see [[process-tracing]]) should be judged by
variable-oriented statistical standards at all, or whether within-case evidence can
break apparent multicollinearity in ways a purely cross-case, variable-scoring
framework cannot register.
