# Scapegoat

## Definition as Coined

From the biblical rite (Leviticus) in which a goat bears the community's sins into the wilderness. [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]] generalized a comparative type: **transference of evil** onto inanimate objects, animals, or persons, and **public expulsion** of evils (occasional or periodic), including human scapegoats in classical antiquity (*[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|Golden Bough]]* ch. 55–58). (Attributed comparative expansion.)

## Semantic History

Entered general language as "one who bears blame for others"; analytic anthropology retained and then often replaced Frazer's type with pollution/expulsion frameworks, sacrifice theory, and historically grounded case studies. Overlaps Frazer's [[divine-king]] and temporary-king patterns where the expelled bearer is royal or divine.

## Operationalizations

Frazer's matrix: vehicle of evil (object/animal/person) × timing (occasional/periodic) × material vs embodied evil.

## Applications

Comparative religion; ritual theory; political metaphor; literary use.

### Hubert & Mauss on expulsion (1898; attributed)

In *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice]]* Hubert and Mauss treat scapegoat-type rites (Azazel goat; elimination of impurity onto a victim that is expelled or destroyed) as **[[desacralization]]** rearrangements of the unitary sacrificial scheme, not as a separate origin of all sacrifice. They credit Frazer's work on the god-sacrifice and scapegoat lines while rejecting the Smith–Frazer move of reducing all sacrificial forms to one arbitrarily chosen principle (totemic communion or expulsion). See [[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Homogenizes heterogeneous expulsion and purification rites; biblical type may not travel; second-hand catalogues. See [[frazer-golden-bough-study]].
