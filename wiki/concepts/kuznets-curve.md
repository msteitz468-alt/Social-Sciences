# Kuznets Curve

Mid-twentieth-century hypothesis that income inequality first rises then falls with economic development (an inverted U). In [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*, the optimistic reading of the curve is the chief twentieth-century foil to his own long-run account of capital and inequality.

## Definition as Coined

Simon Kuznets proposed (notably in his 1955 American Economic Association presidential address in Detroit) that income inequality would **automatically decrease** in advanced phases of capitalist development, regardless of policy choices or country differences, until it stabilized at an acceptable level—“growth is a rising tide that lifts all boats.” The schematic **Kuznets curve**: inequality everywhere follows a **bell curve**—rises in early industrialization (when a minority first benefits) then falls as larger fractions share growth; the advanced equalizing phase was often dated to the late nineteenth/early twentieth century. The claim is diametrically opposed to Ricardian/Marxist inegalitarian-spiral narratives and, in Piketty’s framing, to the dynamics of [[r-greater-than-g]].

## Semantic History

**Data origin.** Kuznets’s 1953 *Shares of Upper Income Groups in Income and Savings* covered only the **United States 1913–1948**, using federal income-tax returns (income tax created 1913) and his national-income estimates—among the first ambitious historical measurements of this kind. Progressive income taxes around WWI (US 1913, France 1914, Britain 1909, India 1922, Argentina 1932) made income-share measurement possible more broadly. Kuznets found a sharp reduction in US income inequality 1913–1948: top decile share fell from **45–50%** of national income to roughly **30–35%**—a decrease of nearly 10 percentage points, equal to about half the income of the poorest 50% of Americans.

**From limited series to general optimism.** Piketty notes that Kuznets himself knew the 1913–48 compression was largely accidental (Depression, WWII), but the 1955 address offered a more optimistic **internal-development** interpretation. Intent, Piketty argues, was partly to keep underdeveloped countries “within the orbit of the free world”—so the theory of the Kuznets curve was “in large part… a product of the Cold War.” Postwar social science and policy often generalized the inverted-U as a stage law of development. Piketty’s project (with Atkinson, Saez, Alvaredo, and the World Top Incomes Database network) extends Kuznets’s tax-based top-share method spatially and temporally—explicitly continuing and revising that measurement tradition.

## Operationalizations

- **Classic**: inequality measures (top shares, Gini) against GDP per capita or development stage—expect rise then fall.
- **Kuznets’s own operationalization**: top-income shares from tax returns linked to national income for one country over 35 years.
- **Piketty’s preferred instruments**: long multi-country [[top-income-shares]] and [[historical-wealth-accounts]] series; capital/income ratios and wealth concentration—not short mid-century samples alone.
- **Compositional test**: whether falls in total [[income-inequality]] come from labor-income compression (sectoral mobility / education) or from collapse of capital incomes and [[wealth-inequality]].

## Applications

Introduction and inequality chapters of *Capital* as the narrative Piketty revises. His central empirical rebuttal: the sharp reduction in income inequality in almost all rich countries **1914–1945** was due above all to world wars and violent economic/political shocks (especially for large fortunes), **not** Kuznets’s tranquil process of intersectoral mobility. Further: labor-income inequality did **not** decrease structurally from 1900–10 to 1950–60 in France or the United States; the only reason total income inequality diminished in the first half of the twentieth century was the reduction of capital-income inequality—contrary to the optimistic mechanical sectoral-shift prediction. The rise of a [[patrimonial-middle-class]] and wartime destruction of private capital explain mid-century compression better, on his account, than a universal development curve. Late-twentieth-century return of top shares (especially Anglo-Saxon [[supermanagers]] and rising β under [[piketty-capital-dynamics]]) further undermines automatic long-run equalization. See [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]], [[piketty-capital-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Piketty’s revision is itself debated: other literatures retain **conditional** Kuznets patterns for specific transitions (rural–urban shift, dual-economy models) without claiming automatic global equalization. Development economics still uses inverted-U language for particular inequality–growth episodes. Whether 1914–45 shocks “explain away” the curve or are the historical content any serious theory must include is a framing dispute. Kuznets’s measurement pioneering remains foundational even for critics of the optimistic theory—Piketty’s own top-share program is genealogically Kuznetsian. Keep theoretical claims attributed; do not assert the inverted-U as established law in wiki voice.
