# Ethic of Responsibility

## Definition as Coined

*Verantwortungsethik*, one pole of the great concluding contrast of [[weber-max|Weber]]'s *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (1919). Weber holds that "all ethically oriented conduct may be guided by one of two fundamentally differing and irreconcilably opposed maxims": the [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends|ethic of ultimate ends]] or the **ethic of responsibility** (attributed voice). Under the latter, **"one has to give an account of the foreseeable results of one's action."** The responsible actor "takes account of precisely the average deficiencies of people"; as Fichte said, he "does not even have the right to presuppose their goodness and perfection," and he does not "feel in a position to burden others with the results of his own actions." Crucially, this ethic confronts the fact that "the decisive means for politics is violence" and that "the attainment of *good* ends is bound to the fact that one must be willing to pay the price of using morally dubious… means" — whoever "contracts with violent means… is exposed to its specific consequences," the "diabolic forces lurking in all violence." Weber insists this is *not* unprincipled opportunism.

## Semantic History

The dichotomy is Weber's own coinage in the 1919 lecture and has become a permanent fixture of political theory and applied/political ethics. Its most important gloss is Weber's *refusal to treat the two ethics as simply opposed at the end*: the mature politician who feels responsibility "with heart and soul" yet reaches the point of *"Here I stand; I can do no other"* shows the two ethics as "not absolute contrasts but rather supplements, which only in unison constitute a genuine man — a man who can have the calling for politics." Later readers (Raymond Aron, Wolfgang Mommsen, and the "dirty hands" literature — Walzer) have made *Verantwortungsethik* a touchstone for debating consequentialism, tragic choice, and the moral costs of political action.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Analytically identified by: (1) explicit weighing of *foreseeable consequences*; (2) acceptance of *personal answerability* for outcomes ("these results are ascribed to my action"); (3) willingness to use, and answer for, morally dangerous means; (4) refusal to offload blame onto "the world, or the stupidity of other men, or God's will."

## Applications

The organizing frame for the ethics of political leadership, statecraft, war and its justification, and any role where the use of power carries unavoidable moral cost; widely invoked in international relations, professional ethics, and the "dirty hands" problem. In this wiki it pairs with [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends]] and grounds Weber's account of what the political *calling* demands, given the [[the-state|state]]'s reliance on the [[monopoly-of-violence|means of legitimate violence]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- **False dichotomy?** Critics argue conviction and consequence are entangled in any serious ethics; Weber himself half-concedes this in the "supplements" passage.
- **Consequences are unknowable.** Weighing "foreseeable results" presumes a predictability politics rarely affords — the "paradoxical" gap Weber notes between action and outcome cuts against the ethic's own demand.
- **Cover for expediency.** The ethic can be misused to license any means as "responsible"; Weber's insistence that it is not opportunism is a stipulation, not a safeguard.
- **Decisionism.** Some read Weber's ultimate "choice" between ultimate value-commitments as an irrationalist decisionism (a line pressed by later critics of Weber's value-pluralism).
