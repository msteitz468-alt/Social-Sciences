# High Modernism

## Definition as Coined

**High modernism**, in [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]]’s *[[scott-seeing-like-a-state-1998|Seeing Like a State]]* (1998, vault-primary), is "a strong, one might even say muscle-bound, version of the self-confidence about scientific and technical progress" — a faith, borrowing the legitimacy of science while itself uncritical and unscientific, in linear progress, mastery of nature (including human nature), and the rational design of social order. It is aesthetic as well as technical (rational order equated with visual, geometric regimentation) and, Scott insists, **must not be confused with scientific practice**.

## The 1998 Theory (vault-primary; all attributed)

- **The four-element recipe for catastrophe.** The most tragic episodes of state social engineering combine (1) administrative ordering of nature and society ([[legibility]] — the *capacity*), (2) high-modernist ideology (the *desire*), (3) an authoritarian state (the *determination*), and (4) a prostrate civil society (the *leveled terrain*). All four are necessary; utopian aspiration per se "is not dangerous."
- **Lineage and pantheon.** High tide in WWI German mobilization under Walther Rathenau; twin "productivism" lineages of American Taylorism and European energetics (following Rabinbach); a politically ambidextrous pantheon — Saint-Simon, Le Corbusier, Rathenau, Robert Moses, McNamara, Monnet, Lilienthal, the Shah, Lenin, Trotsky, Nyerere — with Nazism as the diagnostic right-wing case. Lenin's conversion to Taylorism after 1917 is Scott's emblem of the left variant.
- **Where it flourishes / what checks it.** It thrives on crises of state power (war, depression, revolution, decolonization) and unimpeded state capacity; it is checked by a believed-in private sphere, liberal political economy's respect for the economy's complexity, and — "by far most important" — working representative institutions (Scott's parallel to Sen on famine).
- **Characteristic failure pattern.** Plans for a standardized, contextless subject ("no gender, no tastes, no history"); visual/miniaturized order substituting for working order (model farms, parade-ground villages, "miniaturization" as fallback); "thin" planned institutions generating an informal "dark twin" that actually keeps them running; hubris — planners "regarded themselves as far smarter and farseeing than they really were and, at the same time, regarded their subjects as far more stupid and incompetent than they really were."
- **The case matrix:** Le Corbusier's "The Plan: Dictator" and the death of the street vs. [[jacobs-jane|Jane Jacobs]]'s functional order; Brasília (via Holston — "brasilite," the unplanned city holding 75% of the population by 1980) and Chandigarh; Lenin's *What Is to Be Done?* vs. [[luxemburg-rosa|Luxemburg]] and Kollontai; Soviet collectivization (production failure, appropriation success; "second serfdom"); Tanzanian ujamaa villagization 1973–76 (≥5 million relocated, ~70% of the population in villages within three years by Nyerere's own figures), with Ethiopia's Dergue as the harsher parallel; monocrop agronomy vs. polyculture and [[metis]].
- **Scott's own limits:** not an attack on agronomic science, the state, or epistemic knowledge as such — the danger is "the combination of the universalist pretensions of epistemic knowledge and authoritarian social engineering." His conclusion offers four rules of thumb: small steps, reversibility, plan on surprises, plan on human inventiveness.

## Semantic History

Term coined as Scott's package in 1998 (he credits the design-literature usage); densified above from the 1998 primary, plus *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017) lineage notes below.

In the 2017 book Scott connects early-state projects of making landscapes legible and taxable — and later draining wetlands into orderly grain fields — to a broader **administrative aesthetic of simplifying nature for control**. He writes that the early state strives to create a “legible, measured, and fairly uniform landscape of taxable grain crops,” and that the work of civilization (more precisely the state) consists in “the elimination of mud and its replacement by its purer constituents, land and water.” Marshes seen as untamed waste; state’s civilizing work as draining/canalizing mud into taxable fields (with comparative gestures to China, the Netherlands, English fens, Mussolini, Saddam). These moves are continuous with the high-modernism critique, though *Against the Grain*’s focus is earliest states, not twentieth-century schemes.

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed, not measured: Scott identifies high modernism through its texts (manifestos, plans, doctrine — CIAM, *What Is to Be Done?*, Nyerere's policy statements), its aesthetic signature (geometric order legible from above), and the gap between planned and lived order (Holston's Brasília ethnography; the "two maps" heuristic). From the 2017 lineage extension:

- Uniform taxable grain landscape vs multi-web wetland diversity
- Cadastral survey, standardized measures, and forced concentration of cultivators
- Political landscaping that may overreach and undo ambitious states (Ur III, Qin — as Scott reads them)

## Applications

- Development failures and planned social engineering (1998 primary — see The 1998 Theory above)
- Archaic [[legibility]] via grain states and state landscaping in Scott 2017
- Contrast with [[nonstate-spaces]] that frustrate simplification

### Light continuity — “last enclosure” (*Art of Not Being Governed*, 2009)

*[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* does **not** center the term high modernism, but Scott describes a modern-state **“last enclosure”** of the upland periphery ([[zomia]]): integrate and monetize so activity is legible, taxable, assessable, and confiscatable — settle [[swidden|swiddeners]], replace open commons with closed property or freehold, seize timber and minerals, promote cash monocropping and plantations, extend roads/administration. Thematic affinity with the 1998 high-modernist diagnosis (standardization, simplification, forced sedentarization) and with [[legibility]]; keep as **career-program linkage**, not identity of classical padi statecraft with twentieth-century schemes. See [[state-formation]], [[southeast-asian-state-formation]], [[state-evasion]].

### Worked case — Frelimo's Mozambique (Pitcher 2002)

A concrete twentieth-century application, from [[pitcher-transforming-mozambique-2002|Pitcher's *Transforming Mozambique*]] (attributed): Pitcher explicitly imports Scott's high modernism (via David Harvey) to read the post-independence Frelimo state's transformative project — the **Political and Organizational Offensive** (a ten-year plan, 1980–90), the ideal **communal village** (*aldeia comunal*, 250–1,000 families on a grid with party offices, clinic, school, shop), giant mechanized **state farms** and irrigation mega-projects (Vale do Limpopo; a 400,000-hectare Cabo Delgado/Niassa scheme), central planning as the "scientific" instrument of socialist victory (Samora Machel), and the abolition of "traditional" authority and custom in the name of "modern man." Pitcher stresses the **colonial continuity**: communal-village *blocos* differed little from the Portuguese *concentração* scheme, and northern villages were grafted onto colonial *aldeamentos* — paralleling Tanzanian villagization. Villagization reached only ~20% of the rural population and the schemes collapsed. This is one of Scott's own case-types (Soviet collectivization, *ujamaa*) worked through an African post-colony. See [[mozambique]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- Keep continuity claims as **Scott’s career program linkage**, not identity of Bronze Age statecraft with twentieth-century high modernism.
- **Critique from within the framework (Pitcher 2002, attributed, POSITION):** Pitcher disputes Scott's premise that high-modernist schemes take hold where civil society is "weakened or prostrate." From her Mozambique evidence she argues rural society was never actually prostrate — only *presumed* so by planners — so the state "disoriented rural populations without being able to reorient them." She thus reads the *failure* of high modernism as evidence against one of its stated preconditions. Now that the 1998 text is vault-primary, note the friction directly: Scott's fourth element (prostrate civil society) is a condition for schemes *fully succeeding as imposed designs*; his own cases (ujamaa's evasions, kolkhoz foot-dragging) already show civil society reasserting itself — Pitcher sharpens rather than refutes, and the 1998 disclaimers (R1 preface) anticipate part of her point. Positions stay attributed.
- **Reception critiques (post-1998, attributed):** development economists and planning scholars have argued the case selection is failures-only, that welfare-state legibility (which Scott concedes benefits: public health, education, equality before law) sits awkwardly with the catastrophe framing, and that Hayekian readings overclaim him as a market liberal — a reading Scott's own text disavows (capitalism as an equal agent of simplification; the market as itself an instituted formal system).
