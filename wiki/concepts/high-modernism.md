# High Modernism

## Definition as Coined

**High modernism**, in [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]]’s *Seeing Like a State* (1998), names an ideology of scientific, linear progress that licenses authoritarian redesign of society and landscape — confidence that experts can and should remake cities, agriculture, and populations on simplified, geometric, legible plans. Classic failures Scott analyzes in that book (scientific forestry, planned capitals, Soviet collectivization, Tanzanian villagization, etc.) are **not yet densified from primary ingest** of the 1998 text.

## Semantic History

Scaffold densified lightly from *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* (2017) **lineage notes only**. Full semantic history, key-text table, and scheme-by-scheme analysis await *Seeing Like a State* ingest.

In the 2017 book Scott connects early-state projects of making landscapes legible and taxable — and later draining wetlands into orderly grain fields — to a broader **administrative aesthetic of simplifying nature for control**. He writes that the early state strives to create a “legible, measured, and fairly uniform landscape of taxable grain crops,” and that the work of civilization (more precisely the state) consists in “the elimination of mud and its replacement by its purer constituents, land and water.” Marshes seen as untamed waste; state’s civilizing work as draining/canalizing mud into taxable fields (with comparative gestures to China, the Netherlands, English fens, Mussolini, Saddam). These moves are continuous with the high-modernism critique, though *Against the Grain*’s focus is earliest states, not twentieth-century schemes.
