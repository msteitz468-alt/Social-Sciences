# Care of the Self (Epimeleia Heautou)

## Definition as Coined

*Epimeleia heautou* (ἐπιμέλεια ἑαυτοῦ), Latin *cura sui* — "to attend to oneself," "to take care of oneself" — is the precept that [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] makes the organizing principle of *[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III of the *History of Sexuality*, 1984). He defines it (attributed) not as an attitude but as a **labor**: *epimeleia* is the word used of a household manager's occupations, a ruler's care for subjects, the care of the sick, the honors owed the gods — and, turned on oneself, it "establishes its necessity, presides over its development, and organizes its practice." The care of the self is the principle of the whole imperial-era **art of existence** (*technē tou biou*).

## Semantic History

Foucault stresses the precept is **ancient, not an imperial invention** — he traces it to Xenophon's Cyrus, a Spartan aphorism, and centrally to **Socrates** (Plato's *Alcibiades* and *Apology*), where Socrates presents himself as a "master of the care of the self," sent to remind men to concern themselves "not with their riches, not with their honor, but with themselves and with their souls." What changes in the first two centuries CE (per Foucault) is that this Socratic precept is generalized and placed at the center of a whole [[cultivation-of-the-self|cultivation of the self]], circulating across Platonist, Epicurean, and Stoic doctrine (Seneca's *sibi vacare*; Epictetus, "the highest philosophical development of this theme"; Marcus Aurelius; Plutarch). It becomes an imperative, an attitude, a body of taught procedures, "a social practice," and eventually "a certain mode of knowledge." The concept has a long afterlife: its techniques of self-monitoring, Foucault notes, "will have a long career in Christian spirituality, but they will take on quite different values in it" — the semantic hinge between an ancient care of the self and a Christian hermeneutics of the soul.

## Operationalizations — the practices

Foucault reconstructs the care of the self through concrete practices (attributed):
- **Time set aside** — morning and evening **self-examination** (Pythagorean in origin, taken up by the Stoics), and periodic **retreats** (*anachōrēsis*). Seneca's nightly review is characterized by Foucault *not* as a judicial interrogation aimed at guilt but as an administrative inspection — the *speculator sui* reactivating rational principles for future conduct.
- **Filled activity, not rest** — "taking care of oneself is not a rest cure": bodily regimen, reading and note-taking, talks with a confidant, and **correspondence** disclosing the state of one's soul.
- **The guide/director** — a recognized relation in which one seeks out "a man of good reputation" (Galen) whose frank counsel one can test; the care of the self is thus **intrinsically social**, a "soul service" of reciprocal obligation, *not* egoistic withdrawal.
- **Medicalization** — a tightening correlation of self-care with medicine (Plutarch: philosophy and medicine share "a single field"; Epictetus's school as a "dispensary for the soul," its pupils "patients"); the passions treated on the model of bodily disease.
- **Monitoring of representations** — Epictetus's images of the night-watchman at the gate and the assayer testing a coin, applied to incoming mental representations before assent. Foucault flags the formal similarity to, and functional difference from, later Christian practice: it assesses "the relationship between oneself and that which is represented," not a hidden meaning to be deciphered.
- **Conversion to self** (*epistrophē eis heauton* / *conversio ad se*) — the common goal: a turning from external agitation toward the relation of self to self, culminating in a positive **joy in oneself** (Seneca's *gaudium*, contrasted with the precarious, externally-caused *voluptas*): "learn how to feel joy… I mean your very self and the best part of you."

## Applications

The care of the self reorganizes the ethics of the [[aphrodisia]] in the imperial period: it drives the heightened [[dietetics|medical vigilance]] over the sexual act, the [[ethics-foucauldian|new conjugal ethic]] (the husband's restraint now justified by the nature of the bond, not status), and the deproblematization of the love of boys. In the volume's Conclusion Foucault names it as the engine of the era's intensified austerity — "an art of existence dominated by self-preoccupation" — explicitly *against* the thesis that Christianity, or a tightened legal code, produced that austerity. Beyond Foucault, "care of the self" became a widely cited term in ethics, education, and the sociology of health and self-help.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Foucault's reconstruction rests on an elite, largely male, prescriptive archive (Seneca, Epictetus, Plutarch, Galen); it documents an ideal urged on cultured men, not a description of how imperial subjects lived — note the sampling frame.
- The sharp **ancient care of the self vs. Christian hermeneutics of desire** distinction is Foucault's periodizing thesis (developed toward the projected but unpublished Christian volume); the continuity/discontinuity with Christianity is a live scholarly debate he stages and takes a side in, recorded here as his position.
- Whether the care of the self furnishes a usable contemporary ethics (a late-Foucauldian ethics of self-fashioning) or is only a historical reconstruction is contested in the reception (attributed); see [[arts-of-existence]] and [[ethics-foucauldian]].
