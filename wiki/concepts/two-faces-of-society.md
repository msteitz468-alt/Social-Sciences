# Two Faces of Society

## Definition as Coined

In *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]* (1959, Ch. V), [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] argues that society is "Janus-headed": sociological analysis needs **two faces** — an **integration/values** image (order as consensus of values; the sociological "Utopian") and a **coercion/interests** image (order as constraint and domination of some by others; the sociological "Rationalist"). Neither face alone is adequate; each is a heuristic principle that generates different testable propositions.

## Semantic History

Dahrendorf presents the duality as continuous with a long Western philosophical conflict over social order (Aristotle/Plato, Hobbes/Rousseau, Kant/Hegel) filtered into scientific theory. In mid-century sociology the integration face was associated with structural-functional and consensus models; the coercion face with the conflict tradition descending from [[marx-karl|Marx]] and from elite theory. Dahrendorf's move is not to choose one but to insist both are "equivalent" for analysis — a framing that became a teaching staple of postwar theory courses as the "consensus vs conflict" contrast (see [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]).

## Operationalizations

Heuristic, not measured. The integration face orients research to shared norms, value consensus, and system equilibrium; the coercion face orients research to authority distributions, opposed interests, and conflict groups within [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated associations]].

## Applications

Foundation of Dahrendorf's [[conflict-theory|conflict theory]]: once the coercion face is admitted as legitimate, class can be rebuilt as authority-based conflict groups. Used pedagogically as the dual-image frame for comparing functionalist and conflict programs.

## Critiques and Limitations

Later critics (positions) charge that the "two faces" formula can become a textbook dualism that freezes theory into two camps rather than integrating multi-dimensional accounts of order and conflict. Dahrendorf himself treats the faces as complementary heuristics, not as two societies.
