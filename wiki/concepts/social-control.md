# Social Control

## Definition as Coined

"Social control" is older sociological vocabulary (E.A. Ross, 1901; the Chicago tradition), but [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]'s Chapter VII of *The Social System* (1951) gives it a precise systemic definition: "The theory of social control is the obverse of the theory of the genesis of deviant behavior tendencies. It is the analysis of those processes in the social system which tend to counteract the deviant tendencies." Crucially, Parsons argued mechanisms of control do not *eliminate* deviant motivation — no system is perfectly equilibrated — but "account not for their elimination but for the limitation of their consequences, and for preventing their spread to others beyond certain limits."

Taking **psychotherapy as the prototype**, Parsons identified four constitutive elements of an effective control mechanism:

1. **support** — retaining ego in a solidary, cathected relationship (addressing the anxiety component of strain);
2. **permissiveness** — tolerating some deviation under strain;
3. **denial/refusal of reciprocity** — alter's refusal to reciprocate ego's deviant expectations, breaking the vicious circle;
4. **conditional manipulation of rewards** — shifting the balance via love, approval, esteem.

He classed control mechanisms into three types: those that "nip in the bud" incipient deviance; **insulating/isolating** mechanisms that limit its spread (crime and illness the leading American exemplars); and **reintegrative** "secondary defenses" that reverse the vicious circle (psychotherapy the prototype).

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Ross 1901 / Chicago tradition | Early popularization of "social control" as sociology's problem of order |
| Homans 1950 (*The Human Group*) | Process inherent in mutual dependence of activity–interaction–sentiment–norms; not a separate organ; equilibrium = restoring changes after small deviations |
| Parsons 1951 (*The Social System*) | Obverse of deviance theory; support / permissiveness / denial of reciprocity / conditional rewards; psychotherapy as prototype |
| Mid-century labeling / critical criminology | Control as productive of deviance / instrument of domination |

Parsons's most-quoted corollary is that a degree of "disorganization" is functionally necessary: "A society in which there is a good deal of 'disorganization' and 'pathology' is almost certainly the necessary price of dynamic openness to progressive change" — over-tight integration mobilizes control against *any* innovation. He also credited **Durkheim** (following the *Deux lois de l'évolution pénale* and *The Division of Labor*) with seeing that punishment has a **ritual/expressive** function — consolidating the sentiments that uphold violated values — not merely a protective one.

After Parsons the concept split: the labeling tradition recast "social control" as itself productive of deviance (control agencies confer deviant status), and critical criminology treated it as an instrument of domination — inverting Parsons's integrative framing.

## Operationalizations

Parsons offers an analytic paradigm, not a measure; the four elements and three types are ideal-typical categories derived from the psychotherapy model and extended by analogy to other roles (parental, judicial, executive, academic).

## Applications

- The [[sick-role]] and the physician's role as an "insulating" and "reintegrative" control mechanism — Parsons's claim that ordinary good medical practice is "unconscious psychotherapy."
- Ritual, "safety-valve" institutions (Parsons's example: American youth culture), and the informal sanctions of everyday interaction.

- **Homans (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*, 1950).** In the small group, [[homans-george-c|Homans]] finds no separate “control” activity: control is *implicit* in already-analyzed relations among [[activity-interaction-sentiment|activity, interaction, sentiment]] and norms (reciprocity on the Nortons; output restriction in Bank Wiring). Effective control = [[social-equilibrium|equilibrium]]: small departures from the *existing degree* of obedience produce changes that restore that degree (Le Chatelier / Pareto-colored definition). Punishment can fail (clique B driven further from the norm). Formal “binging” and informal avoidance; Durkheimian note that punishment also heals collective sentiments of witnesses. Distinct from Parsons’s psychotherapy prototype — Homans’s is inductive from field cases, not AGIL.

## Critiques and Limitations

The framing assumes an equilibrium to be restored and a consensus to be upheld, so it inherits the standing objections to [[structural-functionalism]] (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]). Later theorists rejected the therapeutic/integrative model, treating "control" as coercive labeling or as class domination rather than benign re-equilibration.
