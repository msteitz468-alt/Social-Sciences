# Internal Governance Unit (IGU)

*(Attributed — Fligstein & McAdam 2012.)* An **internal governance unit** is a body *within* a [[strategic-action-field]] that helps produce and reproduce field order — administering rules, certifying actors, settling routine disputes, managing information, or representing the field to outsiders — so that incumbents need not rely solely on continuous direct application of power.

## Definition as Coined

Authors treat IGUs as a distinctive element of SAF theory: standard incumbent/challenger imagery understates the organizational machinery of stability. Examples include trade associations, professional licensing bodies, standards organizations, and analogous movement or state-field offices (worked out in Ch. 3 and revisited in Ch. 7). IGUs typically favor incumbent interests even when framed as neutral administration.

## Semantic History

Adjacent to organizational "regulatory" and "associational" infrastructure and to state regulatory agencies (which may be *external* fields or nested state SAFs rather than IGUs proper — specify per case). The IGU label is the authors' synthesis term for field-internal governance capacity.

## Operationalizations

Identify organizations whose mandate is field administration rather than ordinary competition for the field's primary rewards; track their role in settlement and crisis.

## Applications

Mortgage-market and higher-education illustrations; general claim that stable fields usually develop such units.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary between IGU and ordinary powerful incumbent organization can be fuzzy; capture of "neutral" IGUs by incumbents is expected under the theory rather than anomalous. Authors list functions including rule compliance, information/standardization, liaison to state and other external fields, and status-quo defense in conflict (*Theory of Fields* Chs. 1, 3).
