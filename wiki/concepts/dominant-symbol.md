# Dominant Symbol

> Concept coined by [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]] in "Symbols in Ndembu Ritual"
> (*[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]*, 1967) — the founding statement
> of his theory of the ritual symbol.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed to Turner.)* A **ritual symbol** is "the smallest unit of ritual which still
retains the specific properties of ritual behavior … the ultimate unit of specific structure
in a ritual context" — empirically an object, activity, relationship, event, gesture, or
spatial unit. Turner distinguishes:

- **Dominant symbols** — treated as *ends in themselves*, referring to axiomatic values
  (e.g. the Ndembu *mudyi* milk tree, *mukula* red-gum tree, *chikoli* thorn tree, *nfunda*
  medicine); they recur across many rituals with high constancy of meaning (Turner invokes
  Radcliffe-Brown's proposition on recurrent symbols and Whitehead's "eternal objects") and
  possess relative autonomy from any single ritual's aims.
- **Instrumental symbols** — *means* to the explicit goals of a particular ritual,
  intelligible only within the total symbol-system of that rite.

**The three properties of dominant symbols** (Turner's core analytic apparatus, attributed):

1. **Condensation** — "many things and actions are represented in a single formation."
2. **Unification of disparate significata** — referents interconnected by analogous qualities
   or by association "in fact or thought," often via trivial links, letting a symbol "bracket
   together the most diverse ideas and phenomena."
3. **Polarization of meaning** — significata cluster around two poles: an **ideological**
   (or **normative**) pole, referring to social, moral, and jural facts, and a **sensory**
   (or **orectic**) pole, referring to physiological and emotional facts (frequently "gross"
   and "flagrantly physiological" — blood, milk, semen, feces). At the sensory pole meaning
   is tied to the symbol's outward form (the milk tree's white latex = breast milk).

Turner's key thesis: in ritual, dominant symbols bring ethical/jural norms into contact with
strong emotional stimuli, producing an "interchange of qualities" between the poles — norms
become "saturated with emotion" and gross emotions "ennobled." Ritual is thereby "a mechanism
that periodically converts the obligatory into the desirable" (answering a Durkheimian
problem). This is the source of a symbol's **multivocality** (polysemy): "a single symbol may
stand for many things," a "fan" of referents linked by a simple mode of association (e.g.
"whiteness" links white clay to semen, ritual purity, innocence from witchcraft, and
solidarity with the ancestors).

Turner draws on Sapir (referential vs. condensation symbols — ritual symbols are *both*,
"multireferential"), Jung (sign vs. living symbol), and Lewin's field theory (the symbol as a
dynamic "force" in an activity field, not a static cultural entity).

## Semantic History

- **1957 (*Schism and Continuity*):** processual analysis of Ndembu social life, the
  empirical base.
- **1964/1967 ("Symbols in Ndembu Ritual"):** the dominant/instrumental distinction and the
  three properties — read against Nadel and [[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]] (who restricted
  analysis to conscious native exegesis) and against psychoanalytic interpreters (who reduced
  the ideological pole to rationalization). Turner argues both camps are one-sided.
- **1969 (*The Ritual Process*):** the *chijikijilu* ("blaze"/"beacon," from *ku-jikijila*,
  to blaze a trail) as the symbol connecting the known world to the unknown realm of the
  shades; the same polarization apparatus applied to the Isoma and Wubwang'u rituals.

## The Color Triad

Turner's most portable finding: Ndembu ritual is organized by a tripartite classification of
**white, red, and black** — the only colors for which Ndembu have primary terms — which he
argues (explicitly "to stimulate controversy") is among the earliest and most widespread human
classifications because it derives from universal *bodily* experience (white = milk/semen,
red = blood, black = feces/decay/death). This organic theory of classification is set
*against* [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] and Mauss's sociological theory that classification is
modeled on social morphology: "the human organism and its crucial experiences are the *fons
et origo* of all classifications." See [[primitive-classification]] and [[symbolic-classification]].

## Operationalizations

Read via [[symbolic-exegesis]]'s three levels (exegetical / operational / positional): a
dominant symbol is multivocal in isolation but becomes "univocal" within a single binary
opposition or ritual context — the *positional* meaning determines which sense is paramount.

## Applications

- The *mudyi* milk tree (breast milk + matriliny) in Nkang'a; the *mukula* red tree
  (circumcision blood + mature moral community); *nfunda* medicine ("a symbol —
  *chinjikijilu* — for Mukanda itself") among the [[ndembu]].
- The white/red/black triad across Central African and comparative material.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The universal-bodily-origin claim for the color triad is Turner's own attributed argument,
  offered provocatively; it remains contested and does not license wiki-voice equations across
  cultures (evidence-class caution: material/archaeological parallels are convergent patterns,
  not identity).
- The dominant/instrumental line can blur in practice; multivocality makes falsification of a
  reading difficult, a standing worry about interpretive symbol analysis.
