# Naturalism (Ontology)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*, [[descola-philippe|Descola]]'s **naturalism** is the [[modes-of-identification|mode of identification]] that combines **continuity of physicalities** (one material nature under universal laws of matter and life) with **discontinuity of interiorities** (reflective consciousness, culture, language as human — or contested edge-case — prerogatives). It is the **structural reverse of [[animism]]**, not of totemism.

**Disambiguation:** not social-Darwinist political "naturalism," not meta-ethical philosophical naturalism alone — an anthropological **ontology of identification** whose local expression is modern [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture dualism]].

## Semantic History

Ch.3 ("The Great Divide") genealogizes Western dualism: landscape perspective; Greek *phusis*; Christian creation; autonomy of Nature and of Culture (Tylor, Boas, German *Kultur*); dualism as anthropology's default object. Descola renounces his own earlier definition of naturalism as mere "belief in the self-evidence of nature" as ethnocentric and internal to Western cosmology. Viveiros de Castro's gloss: naturalism is "multiculturalist" (one nature, many cultures); animism "multinaturalist" (one culture/soul type, many natures/bodies) — terminology Descola partially resists.

## Operationalizations

- Same physical continuum (Descartes→Darwin molecular and thermodynamic continuity).
- Different interiorities (mind, culture, symbols).
- Collective form: exclusive human **societies** set against **asocial nature** (Ch.11).
- Morality of the "thinking reed" (Ch.12): human exceptionalism as ethical problem-space.
- Unique among modes in **constantly generating heterodox challenges** from within (ethology of animal culture/language; anti-mentalist cognitive science; animal rights; land ethic) — which Descola argues have **not yet** produced a new mode of identification.

## Applications

Modern West as paradigm; rights-of-nature and animal-person debates as internal naturalist tensions; foil against which Amazonian, Australian, and analogist systems are compared; Latour's hybrids as symptoms of dualism under stress.

## Critiques and Limitations

Whether "the West" is a single naturalist regime is contested; historical gradualists (Montaigne, Condillac) already press the formula. Descola's claim that heterodoxies have not exited naturalism is a strong presentist judgment (as of 2005).
