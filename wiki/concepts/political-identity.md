# Political Identity

> Relational concept of identity in [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]’s later work, especially *[[tilly-identities-boundaries-social-ties-2005|Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties]]* (2005, Chs. 4 and 14). Identities are **not** durable encompassing attributes of persons; they are **social relations and their representations**.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Tilly 2005.)*

- **Identities in general:** social relations and their representations, as seen from the perspective of one actor or another — context-shifting, not fixed essences.  
- **Political identities:** public representations of relationships — **us to them** and **us to us** — in settings where **government** is a party (object, third party, or claimant).  
- **Four components** (Ch. 14): a boundary; relations within the “us”; relations across to “them”; stories about the boundary and relations.

Actors make **claims** in the names of identities: demand, petition, threaten, attack, seize, etc. Contentious politics is mutual public claim-making by paired identities.

## Semantic History

| Foil / dialogue | Contrast |
|---|---|
| Dispositional / psychological identity | Fixed traits or inner selves cause action |
| Brubaker & Cooper (2000) | Caution against reifying “identity”; Tilly keeps a stripped relational version |
| Category membership alone | Insufficient without public relational representation and claim-making |
| Citizenship | Special political identity/contract binding categories to state agents |

## Operationalizations

| Feature | Observable |
|---|---|
| Boundary | Us–them distinction activated in public |
| Stories | Narratives of origin, right, threat, destiny |
| Claim performance | Petitions, marches, riots, litigation in a named identity |
| Government party | State as target, guarantor, or co-identity |

High-risk sacrifice and altruism/destruction in claim-making (Ch. 4 “Do Unto Others”) run through **identity–trust networks**, by-product commitments, and mutual coercion — not pure egoistic calculation.

## Applications

- Nationalism and ethnic politics as identity performances  
- Social movements as packages of campaign + repertoire + WUNC displays (Ch. 15)  
- Democratization as reconfiguration of citizen–agent identities ([[citizenship]], [[democratization]], [[de-democratization]])  
- Inequality politics when categorical identities organize participation rules

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Three bad answers** Tilly rejects (Ch. 14) for how political identities form/constrain — extract as positions: essence, pure invention, pure interest aggregation (see source claims for exact wording).  
- Risk of expanding “political” until all identity is political.  
- Empirically multi-sited illustrations; not a single ethnography of identity change.
