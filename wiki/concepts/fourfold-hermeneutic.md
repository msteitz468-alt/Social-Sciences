# Fourfold Hermeneutic

Analytical claim by [[shanks-michael|Shanks]] and [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] in *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]* (1987; 2nd ed. 1992, Ch. 5): archaeology (and history) involve a **quadruple** act of interpretation — expanding [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]’s [[double-hermeneutic|double hermeneutic]] by adding the alien-culture and past–present layers that, they argued, make historical understanding the hardest social-science task.

## Definition as Coined

Shanks & Tilley (attributed) set Giddens’s double hermeneutic (natural science = single hermeneutic of scientists’ discourse about an object-world that does not answer back; social science = dual: meaningful subject-world + social science as a form of life with technical concepts) as the baseline, then argued archaeology and history require **four** layers:

1. **Disciplinary:** working within the contemporary discipline of archaeology (its concepts, procedures, forms of life).
2. **Present social:** living within contemporary society as an active participant — “gaining knowledge of that which is to be human,” interacting and struggling over beliefs and social/political values.
3. **Alien culture:** trying to understand an alien culture involving meaning frames radically different from the interpreter’s own (the “treble anthropological hermeneutic” shared with ethnography).
4. **Past–present:** transcending past and present — the past as a form of life not directly accessible, which must be reconstructed; the distance between present and past frames is **productive of discourse**, not a pure obstacle.

They wrote: archaeology and history “are the most difficult of all disciplines because the process of acquiring a historical understanding involves a **quadruple act of interpretative endeavour**.”

## Semantic History

- **Giddens double hermeneutic** (1976/1982/1984): intersection of lay frames of meaning and social-science metalanguages — see [[double-hermeneutic]].
- **Shanks & Tilley fourfold** (1987): expands that structure for archaeology by making explicit (iii) cross-cultural/alien meaning frames and (iv) temporal reconstruction of an inaccessible past.
- The term is **programmatic**, not a standard later technical term across archaeology; reception folds into broader [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] / interpretive language (hermeneutics, multivocality, theory-ladenness). Later Hodder method writing densifies recursive interpretation at the trowel’s edge without routinely using “fourfold hermeneutic” as a label.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Shanks & Tilley used it to argue:

- Hermeneutics is **not an alternative methodology** or rote formula; “correct” understanding is context-dependent and context-confined.
- The fourfold structure **undermines** any attempt to fix once and for all how the past should be understood via methodological rules; it requires multivalent plurality of approaches and a highly developed theoretical structure.
- Against late-nineteenth-century romantic hermeneutics (Dilthey; Collingwood as they read him): one cannot get “back inside” the past by transcendental empathy.
- Against scientistic archaeology’s claim of stringent objectivity via restricted methodologies: such claims, they argued, work only by reductions that virtually “exorcize” human beings from the project.

Dialectics and theory–data interplay (theoretical object vs real object; data as resistances) sit beside the fourfold claim in the same chapter — hermeneutics is the diagnosis; dialectics is their move “beyond” pure hermeneutics toward explanation as production of knowledge.

## Applications

- **Program charter** for [[social-archaeology]] and [[post-processual-archaeology]]: interpretation as constitutive of archaeological knowledge, not a post-hoc gloss on recovered facts.
- **Contrast** with [[processual-archaeology]] / [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]] ambitions to bridge statics and dynamics via independent testing instruments (as S&T characterize them).
- **Empirical chapters** of *Re-Constructing Archaeology* (TRB ceramics; beer cans) are presented not as “tests” of the fourfold claim but as critical encounters that enact past–present mediation (authors’ self-description, Ch. 9).
- Related museum program: [[redemptive-aesthetic]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded as positions in the 1992 Appendix and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]:

- Critics charged **unfettered subjectivity**, **hopeless relativism** (proliferation of incommensurable pasts), inability to distinguish **fictional from real** pasts, and disciplinary **impotency**.
- Shanks & Tilley replied with a **materialist** account of knowledge production (subjectivity, material culture, and archaeological texts as embodied practices); **contingent** rather than absolute objectivity; networks of resistance in the object world; rejection of both pure fictionalism and new-archaeology “scientific” pasts as idealist; “not just anything goes.”
- The fourfold label itself is thin in later textbook synthesis; the underlying claims (theory-laden interpretation; past–present mediation) were widely absorbed even where the phrase was not.
