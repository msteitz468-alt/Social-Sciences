# Avunculate

## Definition as Coined

The **avunculate** is the special relationship between a **mother's brother** and his **sister's children** — often including authority, inheritance rights, joking or license, or caregiving — treated by [[lowie-robert-h|Lowie]] (*[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]*, Kinship Usages) as a major kinship usage that can shift or share paternal authority.

## Semantic History

Victorian and early Boasian comparative catalogues → mid-century structural kinship → remains a standard descriptive label. Not identical with matriliny: avuncular prominence can occur with various residence and descent combinations.

## Operationalizations

Specify: which rights (discipline, property, marriage consent); sister's son vs sister's daughter; whether coexists with strong father role.

## Applications

*[[lowie-primitive-society-study|Primitive Society]]* kinship-usages chapter; Americas, Oceania, Africa case illustrations Lowie surveys.

### Tallensi mother’s brother (Fortes 1949)

In *[[fortes-web-of-kinship-tallensi-1949|The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi]]*, [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] analysed the **mother’s brother (*ahab*) / sister’s child (*ahay*)** relationship as the nuclear form of extra-clan matrilateral kinship under patriliny — personal and non-corporate, yet not voluntary, and extended to the MB’s inner lineage. Attributed pattern (1930s–40s): gifts (*piini*, including the hen path), land tenure privileges, fosterage of the sister’s son, intermediary roles in marriage (*poyasama* dual loyalty), and ritual shares (e.g. neck meat; matrilateral *boyar* derivation; *bakologo* connections). Father’s sister (*ba-pok*) was “female father” with **limited jural force** by comparison — Fortes’s contrast prevents equating all cross-sex parental siblings. The avunculate here is the operational heart of [[complementary-filiation]], not a separate matrilineal authority system. Study: [[fortes-web-of-kinship-study]]; society: [[tallensi]].

## Lévi-Strauss: the avunculate within the atom of kinship (1945)

[[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] ("Structural Analysis in Linguistics and in Anthropology," 1945; in *[[levi-strauss-structural-anthropology-1958|Structural Anthropology]]*) made the avunculate the entry point for structural kinship analysis (attributed). He argued it cannot be understood as a two-term (uncle–nephew) relation determined by descent — [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown's]] account "arbitrarily isolates particular elements of a global structure which must be treated as a whole." The avunculate is one node in a four-term **[[atom-of-kinship|atom of kinship]]** (brother, sister, father/husband, son/nephew) governed by a law of correlation: "the relation between maternal uncle and nephew is to the relation between brother and sister as the relation between father and son is to that between husband and wife." Grounded in the [[incest-taboo|incest prohibition]] and the [[exchange-of-women]], the maternal uncle "does not emerge — he is present initially." See [[atom-of-kinship]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Surface "mother's brother important" packages hide different institutional logics — always attribute and date.

## Radcliffe-Brown / ASKAM densification (1950)

In the Introduction to *[[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950|African Systems of Kinship and Marriage]]*, [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] restated the mother’s-brother complex as an application of structural principles (sibling-group unity, generation, lineage unity, rank)—building on his 1924 paper “The Mother’s Brother in South Africa.” Attributed summary (claims ranges 1–2):

1. **Terminology.** Under sibling-group unity, MB is often “male mother” (*umalume* and cognates); FZ “female father.” Competing generation principle places first-ascending female cognates as “mother” (rarer for MB-as-father in Africa). Omaha-type systems treat whole mother’s patrilineage as “mothers” / “mother’s brothers”; Shona–Ndau–Shangana-Tonga call MB (and his male descendants) “grandfather,” still requiring explanation from the MB–ZS social relation, not terminology alone.
2. **Father-right pattern (authority vs affection).** Where jural control lies with agnatic kin (F, FB, own lineage), mother is primarily affectionate care extended to her sibling group: MZs “mothers,” MBs “male mothers.” MB does **not** exercise authority (reserved to “fathers”) but shows affectionate interest and aid; sister’s son is specially privileged in MB’s household.
3. **Privileged familiarity.** Conventionalized symbolic behaviour expresses that privilege: joking at uncle’s expense; taking uncle’s property (*vasu*; Junod’s South Africa); extension to mother’s lineage including ancestors (SE Africa: own-lineage ancestors punish; mother’s ancestors approached for help via MB/MBS; sister’s/daughter’s sons grab sacrificial offerings). R-B filed this with [[joking-relationship|joking]] as permitted disrespect calibrated by structural position.
4. **Exceptions to generation-respect.** Proximate-generation inequality is generalized, but MB (and sometimes FZH) may be treated with disrespect or privileged familiarity requiring structural explanation (§V).
5. **Mother-right / father-right framing.** Under ideal mother-right, jural possession of children rests with mother and her siblings; under father-right, jural sibling ties of the woman are severed at marriage while affectionate MB indulgence characteristically remains (philotes vs jural separation). Even strong patriliny (Nuer, Zulu—via Gluckman following Evans-Pritchard) still determines a child’s social position **through the mother** within the father’s system.
6. **Marriage regulation.** R-B theorized MBD preference or ban as compatibility of institutionalized MB relations with relations to wife’s parents; Ashanti (Fortes) vs Bemba (Richards) wife’s-sister rules are structure-dependent illustrations in the same Introduction.

**Not identical with matriliny:** avuncular prominence under father-right is affectionate/privileged, not necessarily jural succession. African case material in ASKAM: Zulu “male mothers” (Gluckman); Tswana *malome* (Schapera); Ashanti MB under matriliny (Fortes); Central Bantu MB authority variation (Richards). Study: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]].

## Tikopia densification (Firth 1928–29)

[[firth-raymond|Firth]]'s *[[firth-we-the-tikopia-study|We, the Tikopia]]* supplies a textbook
**father-right** avunculate in a strictly patrilineal Polynesian society — the pattern R-B predicted.
The mother's brother (**tuatina**) stands to his sister's child (**tama tapu**, "sacred child") in a
*tautau laui* ("free/good") relationship of privileged familiarity and affection, in explicit contrast
to the constrained (*tautau pariki*) father–son relation; and he carries a heavy ritual and gift load
across the child's life cycle (naming, first fishing, superincision, marriage, burial), with the flow
running so that "the junior generation receives more than it gives." Firth models this as a **"diagonal"**
transmission principle (mother's-brother → sister's-son) crosscutting the **"vertical"** (father → son)
line — the mechanism by which matrilateral ties bind a patrilineal society together. Two cautions Firth
stresses: the *tuatina* is a **graded category**, not a single figure (he criticizes textbook talk of
"*the* mother's brother"), and obligation does **not** override personal antagonism — a feuding true
*tuatina* may simply absent himself, a more distant one stepping in, with no compulsion applied. The
mirror figure, the father's sister (**masikitaŋa**), is a sacred quasi-paternal authority. Source:
[[firth-we-the-tikopia-1936]].
