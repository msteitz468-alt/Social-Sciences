# Sovereignty of the People

## Definition as Coined

The doctrine that legitimate political authority derives from the people predates
[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] (Rousseau above all). This page records his specific
treatment in *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*. Tocqueville
argued that in America the doctrine is not a "hidden or barren notion" as elsewhere but is
"acknowledged in custom, celebrated by law," expanding "without impediment." Present from
the colonies' founding but long constrained (by colonial subordination and by the influence
of educated elites), it was fully released, he held, by the Revolution and by partible
inheritance. "The people reign in the American political world like God over the universe."

He treated the doctrine's logical endpoint as **universal suffrage**, arguing that partial
restriction is inherently unstable — "the further the limits of electoral rights are pushed
back, the more we feel the need to push them back" — until full suffrage is reached. Its
culmination makes the people genuinely self-governing, with administrators reduced to
closely watched agents.

## Semantic History

Where Rousseau's popular sovereignty is a normative theory of legitimacy, Tocqueville's is a
described social fact — a doctrine actually lived in American custom and law. He also drew a
key corollary: because the majority speaks in the people's name and faces no external check,
popular sovereignty carries within it the danger of the [[tyranny-of-the-majority]].

## Operationalizations

Not quantified; read from suffrage law and civic practice. Its boundary — "the people" of
1830s America excluded the enslaved, Native peoples, and women — is a standing qualification
(cf. the race chapter treated on [[tocqueville-democracy-study]]).

## Applications

The doctrine is the animating principle beneath Tocqueville's Vol. 1 institutions:
[[township-democracy|township self-government]], the jury (which he called as direct a
consequence of popular sovereignty as universal suffrage), and the freedom of the press
("censorship and universal suffrage... cannot for long coexist"). It connects
[[equality-of-conditions|equality of conditions]] to democratic political form.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Tocqueville presents suffrage's expansion as quasi-inevitable, understating the political
  conflict such expansions actually involved.
- The gap between the doctrine's universalism and its exclusions in practice is central and
  underdeveloped in his account.
