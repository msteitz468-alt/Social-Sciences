# Paleoindian

**Etic classificatory label** for the earliest widespread archaeological complexes of the Americas in late Pleistocene / earliest Holocene time — not an ethnonym, language family, or genetic population.

## Definition as Coined

In North American usage, **Paleoindian** (also Paleo-Indian) groups Clovis, Folsom, and related late-glacial hunter-gatherer archaeological cultures, often contrasted with later Archaic traditions. Meltzer and others discuss **Paleoarchaic** labels for western unfluted assemblages that do not fit a Plains big-game stereotype. After Folsom, late Pleistocene first Americans (assumed ancestors of modern American Indians) were called Paleoindians—not Paleolithic peoples—because Folsom “looked nothing like what anyone expected a Pleistocene human occupation to look like” (Meltzer 2009).

## Semantic History

### Fagan 1987/2004 layer

[[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] reports the term came into use after the [[folsom-site|Folsom]] (1927) and [[clovis-culture|Clovis]] (1932) discoveries defeated the [[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]]–Holmes "no early man" orthodoxy. Originally read through a "big-game hunter" image, the concept broadened by 2004 to cover a **diversity of local adaptations**; [[monte-verde]] forced recognition that earliest Americans were often **generalized foragers**.

### Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009; radiocarbon BP.)*

**Search-bias origin of the big-game stereotype:** almost all ~two dozen sites in the decade after Folsom were **kill sites** with extinct animals; “boneless” sites rare; archaeologists cast Paleoindians as specialized big-game top predators—stereotype uncritically applied beyond the Plains (cites Steven Simms).

**Clovis vs Folsom geographic breadth:** unlike Folsom (mostly plains), Clovis sites occur across the continent and diverse environments—PNW coniferous forests, desert Southwest, western plains, SE mixed forests, near-tundra Northeast. No subsequent North American occupation so widespread amid deglacial upheaval.

**Stylistic diversification after Clovis:** as Clovis populations expanded and cultural drift intensified, links between distant groups broke down; Clovis/related forms disappeared after ~10,900 BP (central/western) and ~10,600 BP (eastern); by ~10,500 BP regionally distinctive Late Paleoindian points supplanted the Clovis fluted form. “After Clovis, Paleoindians go their separate ways stylistically, if not literally.” Late Paleoindian point types are useful period/region labels but need not equal ethnic groups.

**Settlement and social scale:** increasing stylistic diversification and shrinking style/stone-conveyance zones = relaxed pressure for distant-kin contact, reduced scale/openness of social systems, progressive landscape filling—Late Paleoindians no longer continent-spanning as Clovis ancestors. YD radiocarbon plateaus stretch the apparent “explosion” (900 ¹⁴C years ≈ 1,360 calendar years).

**Paleoindian vs Paleoamerican terminology politics:** shift to “Paleoamerican” (esp. Center for the Study of the First Americans / Bonnichsen, tied to Kennewick) subtly implies first peoples were not ancestors of American Indians. As of 2009 all indications are today’s American Indians descend from first Americans; “Paleoindian” should stand until reliable evidence shows otherwise (Meltzer thesis).

**At least two Pleistocene migrations (interim archaeological conclusion):** Paleoindian and (later) Paleoarctic microblade complex—excluding later Holocene (e.g. ancestral Eskimo). Paleoarctic trail too late and technologically absent from pre-Clovis/Clovis to mark first Americans.

## Operationalizations

Usually by **diagnostic points** and radiocarbon ranges; subsistence and mobility models vary by region and author.

### Regional Late Paleoindian packages (Meltzer)

- **Plains/Rockies Folsom+:** tied to *Bison antiquus*; arroyo traps common; large communal kills <3% of Folsom sites; stone ranges large then shrink (Folsom type site outcrops 190–450 km).
- **Eastern forests (Dalton etc.):** resource-rich; denser site counts; mostly local stone; acorn processing by Dalton times = landscape learning; “primary forest efficiency” rejected (adding low-ranked foods lowers efficiency until mobility blocked).
- **Great Basin — Paleoarchaic:** Great Basin Stemmed makers ranged into uplands beyond fluted groups’ lowland marsh/pluvial-lake focus (Beck & Jones); coprolites (e.g. Danger Cave) show mixed plant/animal/insect meals; generalized subsistence seamless across Pleistocene–Holocene boundary so Basin archaeologists abandon “Paleoindian big-game hunter” label and call the span **Paleoarchaic**.
- **Pacific Northwest / California coast:** shellfish among regional packages.
- **Balanced diet beyond iconic kills:** LaBelle—most sites are small kills, quarries, scatters, isolated points—often foothills/mountains; Allen, Hanson, Lubbock Lake multi-taxon lists (deer, pronghorn, lagomorphs, fish, mussels, etc.).

### Technology and camps

- Gourmet butchering (Binford): incomplete carcass use; not making pemmican or storing large meat quantities as later bison hunters.
- Atlatl: fracture-velocity evidence for some points; wooden atlatls not preserved.
- Structures: temporary Clovis wood/perishable; Folsom examples at Mountaineer (CO; aspen/clay houses, 10,400 BP bison), Hanson lodge floors; Agate Basin camp with needles/ocher.
- Ritual: Powars II red-ocher mine; Lake Theo / Jones-Miller features; Lindenmeier as possible rendezvous.
- Few caves inhabited before ~10,500 BP except [[meadowcroft]] and Paisley (as of Meltzer 2009).

### Arbitrary Paleoindian/Archaic boundary

Geologists’ **10,000 BP** Pleistocene end is arbitrary (nice round number—Hopkins) but coincides with YD end; traditional archaeological split of mobile Paleoindian “big-game hunters” vs Archaic “broad-based foragers” is **equally arbitrary**—Paleoindians already used wider foods, smaller territories, new tech; major later Holocene differences (villages, pottery, mounds, plant domestication) come much later and regionally uneven (Meltzer).

## Critiques and Limitations

Four-way non-identity: Paleoindian ≠ First Americans (genetic) ≠ Amerind (Greenberg) ≠ any living nation. “Paleoindian” packages diverse regional records under one chronological umbrella. Kill-site sample bias still distorts textbooks. YD did not catch Paleoindians “flat-footed” in Meltzer’s assessment. Genetics/linguistics claims about Paleoindians stay **(as of Meltzer 2009)** and non-identical to archaeological complexes.
