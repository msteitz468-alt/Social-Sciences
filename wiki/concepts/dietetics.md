# Dietetics (Regimen)

## Definition as Coined

*Dietetics* — the art of *diaita* (δίαιτα), "regimen" or "way of life" — is the domain in which [[foucault-michel|Foucault]], in *[[foucault-use-of-pleasure-1985|The Use of Pleasure]]* (1984), locates the Greek **medical** problematization of the [[aphrodisia]]. His framing thesis (attributed): Greek medicine's reflection on sexual activity was **"dietetic" rather than "therapeutic"** — concerned with integrating the sexual act into the overall management of health, not with sorting pathological from normal sexual practice. The sexual act enters medicine as a matter of *regimen*, not of law.

## Semantic History

*Diaita* named "a whole art of living." Foucault reports two competing Greek origin-stories for it (both attributed): the Hippocratic *Ancient Medicine* makes dietetics the **origin** of medicine (early humans discovered the sick need a different diet than the healthy); Plato makes it a **late, degenerate** development (Asclepius cured directly; protracted regimen arose only once men abandoned the rough healthy life). Either way, regimen covered a conventional list — exercises (*ponoi*), foods (*sitia*), drinks (*pota*), sleep (*hypnoi*), and sexual relations (*aphrodisia*): "everything that needed to be 'measured'." It operated on two planes at once, **body and soul**, especially in the Pythagorean tradition.

## Operationalizations — measure, timing, expenditure

Foucault reconstructs the dietetic treatment of the *aphrodisia* as **qualitative regulation**, never numerically precise ("more amply," "less," "as little as possible"), keyed to the balance of heat/cold/dry/moist (attributed):
- **The diet of pleasures** organizes sexual frequency by a **seasonal calendar** (more in winter, least in summer) on principles of compensation and imitation. Foucault contrasts this "gradual oscillation between more and less" with the later Christian pastoral's binary of permitted and forbidden periods (keyed to the liturgical calendar, menstruation, pregnancy) — citing J.-L. Flandrin for the Christian comparison.
- **Risks and dangers**: despite the context-dependent, "neutral" valuation of the act, Foucault documents a "constant suspicion" across the sources — a restrictive tendency ("the lesser evil"), specific vulnerable organs, and the semen-conservation logic behind athletic abstinence.
- **Act, expenditure, death**: three foci of anxiety attach to the act itself — its violence/form (the Hippocratic "ejaculatory schema"; the Democritean tag that "coition is a brief epilepsy"), its **cost** (semen as the body's most precious, concentrated vital substance, so that expenditure is inherently costly), and its **link to death** (reproduction as the mortal individual's share in a species-level immortality).

## Applications

Foucault triangulates three cultural responses to the same act–expenditure–death anxiety-complex (attributed, knowledge-layer comparison): classical Greek **dietetics** — a technique of existence, rarefaction and distribution without codifying acts; the ancient Chinese **"bedroom arts"** (via Van Gulik) — an erotic art of willed semen retention; and the Christian **doctrine of the flesh** — a juridico-moral codification tying the act's involuntary violence to the Fall. He closes Part Two by making regimen of the *aphrodisia* "a privileged domain for the ethical formation of the subject" precisely because sex was the most violent, costly, and death-adjacent of the pleasures — its mastery paradigmatic for [[arts-of-existence|making one's life into an *oeuvre*]]. Regimen is thus one of the three "great arts of self-conduct" alongside the [[ethics-foucauldian|economics]] of marriage and the erotics of boys.

## Intensification in the imperial period (*The Care of the Self*)

*[[foucault-care-of-the-self-1986|The Care of the Self]]* (Vol. III, 1984) carries the dietetics of the *aphrodisia* forward into imperial medicine (Galen, Rufus of Ephesus, Soranus, Aretaeus, Celsus). Foucault's explicit finding (attributed): "an intensification, much more than a radical change; an increase of apprehension and not a disparagement of the body." The imperial physicians heighten the **anxiety** around the sexual act — extending its correlations across the whole organism (Galen's pneuma physiology), analogizing it to **epilepsy** and the "family of convulsions," cataloguing pathologies of excess (satyriasis, gonorrhea with its "feminizing" wasting), and **valorizing abstention** for both sexes. The act is not pathologized into deviant *forms* (as modern sexuality will be); rather, "at the root of sexual acts" it "uncovered… an element of passivity that was also a source of illness." This tightened fusion of medicine and ethics is one strand of the broader [[care-of-the-self]].

## Critiques and Limitations

- The dietetic/therapeutic distinction is Foucault's organizing gloss on the Hippocratic corpus, Diocles, and Plato's *Laws*; the physiological theories he reports (competing semen-origin accounts) are the Greeks' own, doubly attributed.
- The **Greek/Chinese/Christian** triangulation is a schematic knowledge-layer comparison, not a controlled cross-cultural study; the Chinese material is Foucault's citation of Van Gulik, not independently ingested here.
- As throughout the volume, the archive is prescriptive medical-philosophical text, not a record of practice; the "suspicion" of the *aphrodisia* is a textual tendency Foucault reads, attributed.
