## Definition as Coined

"Civilization" originated as a legal term denoting the conversion of a criminal proceeding into a civil one before broadening, over the eighteenth century, into the general Anglo-French term for both the *process* and the cumulative *achievement* of human progress. Samuel Johnson refused to include the word in his 1755 dictionary, preferring "civility." According to George W. Stocking, Jr., in *Victorian Anthropology* (1987), the plural "civilizations" did not appear until the nineteenth century — in Enlightenment usage, civilization (like "man") was conceived as singular, a fact Stocking argues limited the era's capacity for cultural relativism: the Enlightenment had, in his words, "civilization, but no culture in the modern anthropological sense" (attributed, Stocking).

## Semantic History

By roughly 1830, per Stocking's account, the British idea of civilization had narrowed from the more generalized eighteenth-century concept to imply a specific bundle of contents reflecting recent British experience: the factory system and free trade, representative government, middle-class standards of material comfort, sexual restraint and labor-discipline, and Protestant Christianity (attributed, Stocking). Stocking argues this narrowing was driven by evangelical and utilitarian impulses that linked civilization to middle-class virtues of labor-discipline and delayed gratification rather than aristocratic vices, sharpening the antithesis between "savagery" and "civilization" (see [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]]) and displacing the residual primitivism of the "Noble Savage" that had complicated eighteenth-century usage (attributed, Stocking).

Stocking's central historiographical thesis for the volume is that the idea of "civilization" had already been problematic for a century by the time of the Great Exhibition (1851) and Crystal Palace, and that mid-nineteenth-century sociocultural evolutionism (see [[social-evolutionism]]) must be read against this prior, largely unexamined early-nineteenth-century debate (attributed, Stocking). He argues that for several decades after 1851, civilization in Britain shifted from being a *problem* requiring defense into an unquestioned *assumption*; the live residual question became why not all of mankind had shared equally in its progress — a question sociocultural evolutionism was built to answer (attributed, Stocking).

The word's Victorian trajectory culminated in E. B. Tylor's opening 1871 definition of "culture" in *Primitive Culture*, which explicitly equated the two: "Culture or Civilization, taken in its wide ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society" (quoted in Stocking; attributed). Stocking argues that this equation — and Tylor's retention of a singular, hierarchical, stage-graded sense of the term even while endorsing (in an 1876 review) the German Darwinist Friedrich von Hellwald's call to speak of plural "civilisations" — compromised the term's later anthropological (i.e., plural, relativist) potential from the outset (attributed, Stocking). On Stocking's reading, Tylor's "culture" thus lacked the historicity, integration, behavioral determinism, relativity, and above all plurality of the modern (Boasian) anthropological concept of [[culture]] — the two words did not become synonymous in the sense later anthropologists assumed.

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct in the Victorian sources ingested; treated qualitatively as a graded, singular scale (savagery → barbarism → civilization) within [[social-evolutionism]]'s comparative method (see [[comparative-method]]).

## Applications

Organized the Great Exhibition's (1851) implicit ranking of nations and its ethnological displays; underwrote Tylor's definitional fusion of "culture" and "civilization" in *Primitive Culture* (1871); and structured the Victorian evolutionary "ladder" against which "savage" societies were placed, as documented on [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Stocking's own historiographical point is the chief critique available in this source: treating "civilization" as a timeless or self-evident term risks presentism (see [[presentism-and-historicism]]), since its content shifted markedly between the Enlightenment (singular, universalist, still tolerant of the Noble Savage), the 1830s British middle-class narrowing, and the post-1871 Tylorian equation with "culture." Twentieth-century Boasian anthropology (see [[culture]]) rejected the singular, hierarchical sense of civilization in favor of a plural, relativist concept of cultures — a rupture Stocking treats as more consequential than the terminological continuity of the word "culture" itself would suggest.

### Childe — civilization as Urban Revolution (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] operationalizes **civilization** archaeologically as the outcome of the [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]]: cities supported by concentrated [[social-surplus|social surplus]], full-time specialists, writing, and class division, first in Nile / Tigris–Euphrates / Indus alluvia (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed). Bronze Age civilization concentrates surplus in narrow elites; Iron Age innovations (cheap iron, alphabet, coinage) temporarily widen distribution before slavery and imperial contradictions stall progress. Not identical to Enlightenment "civilization" singular — Childe uses a materialist, urban-literate threshold while still writing a progressive main-stream narrative.

### Clark 1961 — farming, cities, culture

*[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* ties civilization to mixed farming as the economic base that alone allows large permanent settlements and the division of labour needed for rapid technological advance; life in cities then intensifies social, intellectual, and spiritual development (attributed, ch. 4 and Retrospect). Culture (learned tradition) rather than further organic evolution drives the last ~50,000 years of social change once *Homo sapiens* is in place (attributed, Retrospect). Progressive and adaptive framing — POSITION, not wiki-voice law.

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]] operationalize **civilization** / early complex society through multi-variable **sociocultural complexity** ranking (as of 2007): differential access to wealth, power, and prestige; inheritance vs achievement of that access; occupational specialization and economic integration; centralization of political power (attributed, ch. 7). By ~5000 years ago, they write, the Babylon region already had cities, schools/libraries, international trade, roads, taxes, and temples — elements "collectively called civilization" — while much of the world remained foragers or marginal farmers (attributed). They follow [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s comparative finding (Egypt, Mesopotamia, China, Mesoamerica, South America, early Yoruba): wide economic variation but one constant — upper-class appropriation of surpluses via tributary relations — plus limited core forms of class hierarchy, political organization, and religious paradigm, with freer variation in art styles and cultural values (attributed).

They report that early complex societies apparently evolved **coercive sociopolitical elites** monopolizing power, prestige, and wealth with sanctioning ideologies (e.g. Egyptian divine kingship), and that "collapse" is usually decentralization/localization (Kenoyer via W&O) rather than mass extinction of people or ideas (attributed). Ancient civilizations varied greatly yet shared institutions permitting "pervasive exploitation of the many by the few" framed as religious and civil virtue (attributed, Andean chapter intro). They **reject** the leisure-time explanation of civilization: many agricultural groups never became states; Peru may have evolved complexity without primary agriculture; many foragers had more leisure than early farmers (attributed). Reflexivity: confusing cultural complexity with cultural worth is an old error used to justify colonialism; stratification/size/energy-exchange ranking is a research tactic, not ultimate ranking of piety or justice (attributed). Six "primary civilizations" / pre-industrial states structure their comparative frame (Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus, China, Mesoamerica, Peru/Andes — Inka as the only primary case without writing, as of W&O 2007).

### Braidwood — *Prehistoric Men* (1959)

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* 3rd ed.)* Civilization is defined by a “mind-content” list of traits of urban literate life (not reproduced here as a closed checklist); **impossible without food production** but **not entailed** by it — many food-producers never become civilized. Earliest villages on the **[[hilly-flanks|hilly flanks]]**; **river control** in Egypt and southern Mesopotamia is everyone’s business and supplies the germ of civilization after farming spreads from the flanks (Ubaid → Warka/Proto-Literate sequence; writing and art). Near East as double first (village-farming + civilization) for the western tradition is his program claim — multi-regional later research weakens exclusivity. See [[urban-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]].


### Trigger — early civilization as class society (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]*) narrows comparative use of civilization to **[[early-civilization]]**: the earliest and simplest form of **class-based** society. Writing is neither necessary nor sufficient; the category is evolutionary but not Steward arid-irrigation unilinear. Cross-case findings (two political forms; religious mutual-dependence package; economic diversity) are positions on the study page [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], not wiki-voice definitions of “civilization” as moral progress. Complements Childe’s [[urban-revolution]] package without equating the terms.

### Renfrew — civilisation as multi-subsystem take-off (1972, attributed)

In *[[renfrew-emergence-of-civilisation-1972|The Emergence of Civilisation]]*, [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] treats Minoan–Mycenaean society as [[civilization|civilisation]] recognised polythetically (city and writing not sole criteria) and explained as **endogenous** third-millennium subsystem coupling via the [[multiplier-effect]], not as Oriental transplant. Civilisation here is a culture-process outcome (artificial multi-dimensional environment / insulation) rather than a moral scale. See [[minoan-civilization]], [[mediterranean-polyculture]].

### Dickinson — accepts Renfrew’s definition; process over invasion (1994, attributed)

[[dickinson-oliver-t-p-k|Dickinson]] (*[[dickinson-aegean-bronze-age-1994|The Aegean Bronze Age]]*) explicitly accepts Renfrew (1972: 7) that peak Aegean BA is a civilisation, then explains rise and collapse via cultural process rather than invasion, automatic foreign influence, or mono-catastrophe — and asks what legacy remained for later Greek civilisation.

### Connah — *African Civilizations* (2016, attributed)

[[connah-graham|Connah]] uses **civilizations** (plural) for precolonial African trajectories of urbanism and state formation without equating them to a singular Victorian ladder. The 3rd edition covers the **whole continent** (including Egypt and North Africa) against older tropical-Africa-only frames and against treating Egypt as non-African. Operational focus is archaeological evidence for cities and states under local environmental and social conditions ([[productive-land-hypothesis]]; trade as intensifier). Explicitly calls for African cases to play a larger role in **global social theory** of complexity (attributed, Ch. 12).
