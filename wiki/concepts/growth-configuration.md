# Growth Configuration

[[kroeber-alfred-l|A. L. Kroeber]]'s term (in *[[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944|Configurations of Culture Growth]]*, 1944) for the shape a culturally productive activity traces in **time, space, and degree of achievement** as it rises, culminates in a "florescence," and declines. Kroeber's programme was to describe these configurations empirically and comparatively across civilizations and activities while **deliberately bracketing their content and their causes**: "a clearer and surer understanding of how cultures behave historically seems antecedent to why they behave as they do." All claims below are **attributed to Kroeber**.

> **Not Benedict's "configuration."** Kroeber's *growth* configuration (a rise–peak–decline profile of one activity over time) is a distinct concept from [[cultural-configuration|Ruth Benedict's "cultural configuration"]] (the synchronic personality-like patterning of a whole culture in [[configurationism]]). The shared word marks a shared Boasian holism, not a shared concept.

## Definition as Coined

The book investigates "the frequent habit of societies to develop their cultures to their highest levels spasmodically" — in clustered bursts rather than at a steady rate. Kroeber examines "their growth configurations — configurations in time, in space, and in degree of achievement," treating the roster of [[genius-clustering|clustered genius]] as the measure of a growth. He chose the descriptive word "growth" precisely because it carries less causal freight than "cycle" (which he avoided for its Spenglerian implications) or "progress."

## Semantic History and Findings

Kroeber's substantive conclusions about the shapes these configurations take (attributed, from the Review chapter):

- **Types of configuration.** Growths range from simple single-peak cases (isolated, unilingual Egypt) through **prelude/prodromal** growths (the Carolingian and Othonian "renaissances," the Chaucerian constellation — real patterns that stayed abortive because the wider culture was underdeveloped), **aftermath/revival** growths (the Twenty-sixth-Dynasty Egyptian "renaissance," Neo-Platonism, the Ming revival — "a factitious appearance of being new… owing to content assimilation rather than pattern development"), and **prologue-and-epilogue** growths where a central peak is flanked by lesser pulses (Spain, ancient Greece).
- **Growth curves have no single normal form.** Testing whether florescences follow a symmetrical curve, Kroeber found peak-early, peak-late, and symmetrical configurations all attested, with skew "if anything, more frequent for single activities" and symmetry commoner for total-culture composites: "either there is no normal form of time-quality curve in florescences, or… the difficulties of quality estimation… render curve representation too uncertain." This is a deliberately *negative* result against law-like regularity.
- **Duration is highly variable.** Growths run "from as little as thirty or forty years to as much as five hundred or a thousand," tending to be longer in proportion as they produce values posterity rates great; a tentative ~300-year regularity for philosophy is flagged as low-confidence.
- **Pattern exhaustion, not death.** A growth ends because "any such pattern fairly early takes a particular direction… gradually pushed to its limits," and "the very selection which at the outset is necessary… is almost certain later on to become a limitation." Exhaustion may bring a reconstituting **lull** (the early-eighteenth-century pause in European science) or degenerate into repetitive **"Byzantinism."** The pattern is exhausted, but the culture need not die (see [[kroeber-spengler-debate]]).
- **The polyphonic composite.** In a multinational civilization "each nation shows its own crests, and… the several culminations replace each other, like instruments in an orchestra, so that there is a larger polyphonic configuration for the supernational civilization as a whole" — Kroeber's image for European culture treated as one growth.

## Operationalizations

A configuration is reconstructed by dating an activity's recognized masters (per textbook/encyclopaedia consensus) and reading the rise, peak, and fall off their temporal distribution — the method of [[genius-clustering]]. Kroeber conceded the activities compared are "not strictly comparable" (Greek philosophy dissociated from religion, Indian philosophy interwoven with it; ancient vs. modern science) but knew "no other procedure."

## Applications

- The unit of analysis throughout *[[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944|Configurations]]* — every chapter (philosophy, science, philology, sculpture, painting, drama, literature, music, national power) traces one class of growth configurations.
- Kroeber's headline case that **Occidental art music is essentially a single, relatively late growth**, contrasted with literature's nationally fragmented florescences, is a configuration-level claim.
- A reference point for later macro-comparative and quantitative studies of cultural efflorescence (Sorokin's *Social and Cultural Dynamics*, later cliometric "golden age" studies) — Kroeber noted he encountered Sorokin and Toynbee only after writing.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept is avowedly descriptive and renounces causal explanation, which critics read as its central weakness: it names recurrent shapes without a mechanism, resting finally on the claim that "cultures appear to grow in patterns and to fulfill or exhaust these." The "degree of achievement" axis depends on canonical value judgements (Kroeber's own rankings follow textbook consensus and reflect early-20th-century, Europe-weighted taste, which he acknowledged and regretted). And the negative growth-curve result — no normal form — limits how far the framework can be turned into predictive generalization, a point Kroeber embraced rather than resisted.
