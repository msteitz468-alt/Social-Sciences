# Patrimonial Capitalism

Regime type in [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s political economy: a capitalism organized around **patrimony** (accumulated and inherited wealth) rather than primarily around labor earnings and meritocratic mobility. Central framing concept of *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* Parts Two–Four and of [[piketty-capital-dynamics]].

## Definition as Coined

Piketty uses **patrimonial capitalism** for societies in which private capital is large relative to income (high [[capital-income-ratio|β]]), capital ownership is highly concentrated, and inheritance of past fortunes tends to predominate over saving from present earned income—especially when [[r-greater-than-g|r > g]] holds durably. The nineteenth century and Belle Époque are the archetype (Europe: top decile wealth share ~90%, top centile ~50–60%+; middle 40% owning little more than the bottom half). He argues the wars and political shocks of 1914–1945 temporarily “wiped away the past,” creating the illusion that capitalism had been structurally transformed; by mid-century capital had “largely disappeared,” only to approach eighteenth–nineteenth-century levels again a half-century later.

Beyond short-term asset-price noise, he reports a long-term 1970–2010 trend in all rich countries: strong comeback of private capital—“the emergence of a **new patrimonial capitalism**.” Three reinforcing factors: (1) slower growth especially demographic, with high saving → higher long-run β via β = s/g (dominant long-run force); (2) gradual privatization/transfer of public wealth to private hands in the 1970s–80s; (3) long-term catch-up of real estate and stock prices accelerating in the 1980s–90s in a political climate more favorable to private wealth than the immediate postwar decades.

## Semantic History

“Patrimonial” evokes inheritance, family fortune, and the rentier worlds of Balzac and Austen. In Piketty’s usage it names a **regime type of inequality structure**, not Weberian patrimonial domination (a different concept family—do not equate). Related phrases in the same program: “inheritance society,” “society of rentiers,” “hyperpatrimonial” vs “hypermeritocratic” inequality (same numerical total-income top shares, different labor/capital composition). Keynes’s “euthanasia of the rentier” appears as an impressed reaction to the collapse of the pre-WWI rentier world. Postwar France’s mixed economy he labels “capitalism without capitalists” / state capitalism—foil to the later “promised land of the new private-ownership capitalism.”

## Operationalizations

- **High β**: Europe today ~5–6 years of national income, scarcely less than pre-WWI; private capital in eight rich countries 2–3.5 years of NI in 1970 → 4–7 years in 2010.
- **Wealth concentration**: Belle Époque Europe top 10% ~90% of wealth; France top decile ~90% (1910–20) → 60–70% (1950–70) → ~60–65% today; top centile ~60% → 20–30%. Incomplete recovery from 1914–1945 shocks is part of the concept’s historical content.
- **Inheritance flows**: annual inheritance as share of national income; French simulations for 2010–2100 (central ~16–17% of NI; high-r low-g alternative ~24–25%).
- **Private dominance of national wealth**: Britain/France 2010 private share >90–99% of national wealth; French private wealth ~6 years of NI vs public wealth ~1/20 of that.
- **Cultural markers**: literary rentier arithmetic (4–5% capital-to-rent conversion); entrepreneur→rentier logic once fortunes pass a threshold.

## Applications

Frames the book’s arc from capital’s metamorphoses (land → housing/industrial/financial assets without loss of macroeconomic importance) through distributional Parts Three–Four. Contrasts with postwar exceptional growth, progressive taxation, and the [[social-state]]. Qualifies pure “return to Belle Époque” stories via the historically new [[patrimonial-middle-class]] (middle 40% owning ~quarter to third of wealth). Interacts with Anglo-Saxon [[supermanagers]]: twenty-first-century inequality need not simply replicate the nineteenth—less extreme top wealth shares, expanding earned-income hierarchy, stronger wealth–income correlation; one can be both supermanager and “medium rentier.” Policy chapter: regulating “globalized patrimonial capitalism” requires tools beyond updating the twentieth-century fiscal/social model—ideally a [[progressive-global-wealth-tax]] plus financial transparency. See also [[wealth-inequality]], [[income-inequality]], [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]], [[piketty-capital-debate]].

## Critiques and Limitations

How fully contemporary US inequality (labor-income top vs capital top) fits a pure patrimonial model is an internal tension Piketty discusses. Whether “new patrimonial capitalism” overstates continuity with Belle Époque given the middle class of wealth, progressive residual taxation, and human-capital premium is debated. Privatization and asset-price channels can raise private β without classical accumulation; Soviet-bloc oligarch enrichment is pure transfer, not β = s/g—showing that ownership reassignment can mimic “capital’s comeback.” Reception dispute: [[piketty-capital-debate]].
