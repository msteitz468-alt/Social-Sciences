# Social Organism

## Definition as Coined

In *Principles of Sociology* Part I, Spencer asked “What is a society?” and answered that a society is an **entity**: permanence of relations among component parts constitutes the individuality of a whole as distinct from the individualities of its parts. He then developed systematic **likenesses** between societies and individual organisms — growth, structural differentiation, mutual dependence of parts (“consensus of functions”), and specialized systems — while insisting on **unlikenesses**. proposed_by: [[spencer-herbert]] · part_of: [[social-evolutionism]]

Load-bearing unlikenesses Spencer stressed include: the social aggregate’s **discreteness** (units are not a concrete continuous whole); absence of a **social sensorium**; and the ethical-political point that **society exists for the benefit of its members**, not members for the society (contrast with organism parts existing for the whole).

## Semantic History

Organic metaphors for polity are ancient. Spencer’s mid-Victorian version embeds them in a general evolutionary formula ([[differentiation-and-integration]]) and a three-system anatomy ([[sustaining-distributing-regulating-systems]]). Later “organicism” became a pejorative for over-extension of the analogy. Andreski (1969) argued cybernetic and general-systems talk largely continues the same move. Structural-functionalism absorbed structure/function and interdependence vocabulary while often dropping explicit organism talk and evolutionary teleology.

## Operationalizations

- **Entity test:** permanence of arrangements among units.
- **Growth:** size increase by multiplication of units and by compounding of groups ([[social-compounding]]).
- **Structure/function:** progressive specialization of parts with tightening mutual dependence.
- **Systems of organs:** sustaining (industry), distributing (commerce/communication), regulating (government/military; later industrial self-regulation).
- **Type:** predominantly militant vs industrial constitutions as modes of the organism’s activity.

Not a measurement construct in the modern survey sense; a classificatory-analytic framework.

## Applications

Spencer used the frame to organize the *Principles*: political institutions as regulating-system evolution; industrial institutions as sustaining-system evolution; ecclesiastical institutions as specialized regulative-moral apparatus. Comparative illustrations (travellers’ ethnography + history) fill the cells — attributed illustrations, not modern ethnographic evidence.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Analogy is heuristic, not proof (Spencer’s own summary qualifications; standard organicism critique).
- Tension with individualist laissez-faire (parts-for-whole logic vs. society-for-members claim and anti-statist politics).
- Risk of reifying “society” as a super-person; Spencer’s “no social sensorium” and society-for-members clauses push against that, inconsistently with some militant-type rhetoric (“individual owned by the State” as type trait).
- Evidence class limits: 19th-c. comparative compilation, not controlled comparison.
