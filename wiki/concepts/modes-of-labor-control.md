# Modes of Labor Control

[[wallerstein-immanuel|Wallerstein]]'s thesis (in *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-1-1974|The Modern World-System I]]*, 1974) that different **forms of labor control** are not stages on a single evolutionary ladder but are **structurally allocated to different zones** of one and the same [[world-economy]] at the same time — a signature and much-debated claim of [[world-systems-analysis]].

## Definition as Coined

Wallerstein argues (attributed) that in the sixteenth-century European world-economy each zone developed the mode of labor control suited to its role in the [[axial-division-of-labor|axial division of labor]] and its products: **wage labor** and free tenancy in the **core** (northwest Europe); **sharecropping** in the **[[semiperiphery]]** (e.g. Mediterranean Europe); and **coerced cash-crop labor** — the "second serfdom" of eastern Europe and, in the Americas, **slavery** and the *encomienda* — in the **[[core-periphery|periphery]]**. The provocative corollary is that all of these, including the coerced forms, are part of a single **capitalist** world-economy: capitalism, for Wallerstein, "combines" free and coerced labor across its zones rather than requiring free wage labor everywhere.

## Semantic History

This directly challenges the orthodox Marxist identification of capitalism with free wage labor. Where classical accounts read serfdom and slavery as *pre*-capitalist, Wallerstein reads the early-modern "second serfdom" and New World slavery as **created by, and functional for**, the emerging capitalist world market — coerced labor producing cash crops (grain, sugar, silver) for core profit. The mode of labor control is thus an effect of world-economic position, not an index of a society's own developmental stage.

## Operationalizations

The mapping is read jointly from **product** (bulk essentials vs. high-value manufactures), **zone** (core/semiperiphery/periphery), and **state strength** (strong core states enforce free contract; weak peripheral states permit or enforce coercion). Wallerstein pairs the labor-control map with the strong-core/weak-periphery [[core-periphery|state-strength]] thesis: coercion is feasible where the indigenous state is weak and the landlord class strengthened by export profit.

## Applications

The concept underlies world-systems accounts of the "second serfdom," Atlantic slavery, and colonial labor regimes as *products of* world-market integration rather than survivals. See [[axial-division-of-labor]], [[core-periphery]], [[world-systems-analysis]].

## Critiques and Limitations

This is one of the framework's most contested claims. Robert Brenner and the "political Marxists" argue it inverts cause and effect — reading class relations off market position (a "neo-Smithian" error) rather than explaining market outcomes by the balance of class forces — and that calling coerced-labor peripheries "capitalist" empties the term. Defenders reply that the point is precisely that capitalism is a *world*-system combining multiple labor forms, and that the zones are intelligible only in relation to one another, not as separate societies each on its own ladder.
