# Gender Attribution

The practice of assigning male/female (or gendered activity) labels to archaeological materials — tools, spaces, grave goods, images — as a step in reconstructing past gender. Central **method debate** in [[feminist-archaeology|gender archaeology]]: is secure attribution a prerequisite for gender research, or a limiting gate that reproduces binary categories and ethnographic projection?

## Definition as Coined

[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gilchrist (1999)]] glossary: gender attribution “attempts to link specific activities, artefacts or spaces with men or women, often proposing material correlates for the sexual division of labour.” In the *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering]]* program, Conkey & Gero argued that **gender attribution is not required** as a prerequisite for gender research; [[wylie-alison|Wylie]] rejected “gender inaccessible” as an ontological barrier.

## Semantic History

Early gender archaeology often sought to “find women” by attributing artefacts to female users. Critiques noted circularity (ethnographic sex-role projection → attribution → “evidence” for those roles). Gilchrist (1999 coda) returns to the issue: approaches of gender attribution “now vie with more abstract considerations” such as gender embedded in technology’s social relations (Dobres) or conveyed through representations of the individual (Knapp & Meskell) — yet the empirical discipline still prioritizes **visibility**.

## Operationalizations

- Association of tools with sexed burials or domestic vs “public” spaces.
- Iconographic sexing of figures.
- Osteological sex + grave-good packages ([[bioarchaeology]]).
- **Failure mode (castles):** Gilchrist finds male and “female” artefacts too mobile for room-sexing; prefers architecture + documents ([[contested-garden]]).
- **Alternative:** relational/technology and task-differentiation approaches (Spector; Gero [[genderlithics]]) that do not require prior sexing of every object.

## Applications

Gero genderlithics; Spector task differentiation and awl narrative; burial analyses with third-gender/mortuary mismatch cases (as Gilchrist reviews Hollimon, Whelan, etc.); Gilchrist’s multi-source castle reading.

## Critiques and Limitations

Over-reliance reifies binary sex, smuggles presentist division of labour, and blocks research where osteology or “smoking-gun” associations are absent. Under-attention to material visibility can drift into untestable abstraction. Gilchrist keeps both horns: yes to visibility beyond the sexual division of labour alone; no to attribution as sole gate; multi-source evidence to catch ambiguity.
