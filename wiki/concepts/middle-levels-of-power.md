# Middle Levels of Power

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956), [[mills-c-wright|Mills]] placed
**middle levels of power** immediately below the national [[power-elite]]: professional
politicians of Congress, pressure groups, and the new and old upper classes of town, city, and
region. He argued (attributed) that the classical image of democratic **[[theory-of-balance|
balance]]** — bargaining, checks, countervailing interests — **describes these middle levels
accurately** and becomes ideological falsehood only when generalized as a master model of the
*whole* power system, including the top.

## Semantic History

The three-tier map is load-bearing for the book: **unified top** (power elite) /
**stalemated middle** (middle levels) / **fragmented bottom** ([[mass-society]]). Party
politicians lose relative ground as "political outsiders" (corporate and military men) occupy
the executive; Congress and parties remain real arenas but for secondary issues. Labor leaders,
regional blocs, and ethnic stakes appear here as middle-level forces, not as co-equals of the
command posts.

## Operationalizations

| Middle-level arena | Mills's characterization (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Congress | Social composition of brokers/parochial interests; seniority; relegation as executive supremacy grows |
| Parties | Weak at the top; more meaningful at middle and bottom |
| Pressure groups / lobby | Real at middle levels; at the top, "governmentalization of the lobby" as vested interests sit inside the state |
| Local/regional upper classes | Local decisions (e.g. Hunter's Regional City narrative); cannot aggregate into national elite |

## Applications

- Analytical wedge against pluralism: grants the pluralist description *for a tier*, then
  relocates national decisions upward ([[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]).
- Explains why academic and journalistic "power studies" focused on Congress and interest groups
  miss the command posts (the analyst, Mills said, is often himself on the middle levels).
- Connects to *White Collar*: the new middle class is politically formless — a rearguard at the
  middle/bottom, not a counter-elite.

## Critiques and Limitations

Pluralists reverse the hierarchy: what Mills calls middle levels *are* national power. State-
autonomy and issue-area research further fragments "the top." The tier image is a theoretical
map, not a measured stratification of decision cases — treat as attributed architecture.
