# Mediterranean Polyculture

## Definition as Coined

In *[[renfrew-emergence-of-civilisation-1972|The Emergence of Civilisation]]* (1972), [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] treats the early third-millennium adoption of **vine and especially olive** cultivation alongside cereals as a decisive subsistence innovation in the southern Aegean: **Mediterranean polyculture**. Domestication/exploitation of vine and olive (he cites Sitagroi for vine; early bronze age Crete and Cyclades for olive oil/stones and oil lamps) enabled intensive use of hill slopes below ~700 m (abundant sun, little rain, no winter frost) and is linked, in his model, to new settlement distributions and to surplus supporting craft specialisation and social ranking — one subsystem in the [[multiplier-effect]] coupling that produces Minoan–Mycenaean civilisation.

## Semantic History

- Agronomic/geographic "Mediterranean polyculture" predates Renfrew as a description of olive–vine–cereal farming.
- Renfrew 1972 makes it **load-bearing for Aegean civilisation origins** (not mere backdrop ecology).
- Later Aegean archaeology debates timing, scale, and necessity of the olive–wine package for palace economies; treat Renfrew's third-millennium chronology and causal weight as **his** position (as of 1972 secondary + then-available archaeobotany).

## Operationalizations

Archaeobotanical finds (olive stones, grape evidence), oil lamps, settlement shift into true Mediterranean climate zones, and inferred surplus/redistribution. Renfrew presents these as strongly suggested rather than as a single decisive archaeobotanical proof for every island.

## Applications

Southern Aegean Early Bronze Age → second-millennium palace economies ([[minoan-civilization]], [[mycenaean-civilization]]). Comparative contrast with northern Aegean trajectories that lack the same package emphasis in his settlement maps.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Archaeobotanical and dating evidence has expanded since 1972; absolute timing of widespread olive cultivation remains discussed.
- Polyculture is necessary but not sufficient in Renfrew's own multiplier model — social, craft, trade, and symbolic couplings also required.
- Risk of environmental determinism if decoupled from social process (Renfrew himself embeds it in subsystem interaction).
- **Dickinson 1994 (attributed):** notes Hansen’s critique of early olive/grape domestication arguments; still treats orchard husbandry as the major BA farming development of palace interest (Linear B figs/olives/oil/wine; drinking-vessel boom for wine), while allowing extensive polyculture may be mainly palace-scale rather than universal village practice.
