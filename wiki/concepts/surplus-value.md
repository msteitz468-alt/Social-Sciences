# Surplus-Value

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), [[marx-karl|Marx]] defines **surplus-value** as the increment of value that money acquires when transformed into capital — the Δ in M–C–M′ — originating in production from unpaid labour of the working class once [[labor-power]] has been purchased at its value and set to work beyond the time needed to reproduce that value.

**All theoretical claims attributed to Marx.**

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Classical PE “profit” / “surplus” | Often unexplained residue or circulation mark-up |
| Marx *Capital* I | Unpaid labour of labour-power beyond its own value; absolute vs relative forms |
| Later Marxism | Distribution into profit, rent, interest (Vol. III) |
| Mainstream economics | Opportunity cost / residual claims — different ontology |

## Absolute and Relative Surplus-Value

| Form | Mechanism (Marx) | Historical note |
|---|---|---|
| **Absolute** | Prolong the working day beyond necessary labour-time | General groundwork of the capitalist system; formal subjection of labour to capital |
| **Relative** | Shorten necessary labour by raising productivity (cheapening means of subsistence → lower value of labour-power) | Revolves technical process and social composition; real subjection of labour to capital |

Co-operation, manufacture, and machinery (Part 4) are analyzed as methods of producing relative surplus-value. The working day (Ch. 10) is the historical theatre of struggle over absolute surplus-value (Factory Acts, etc.).

## Operationalizations

- **Rate of surplus-value** s/v — degree of exploitation of labour-power (distinct from rate of profit s/C on total capital).
- **Constant capital (c)** vs **variable capital (v)** — only v produces surplus-value (→ [[constant-and-variable-capital]]).
- Productive labourer under capitalism: one who produces surplus-value for capital (“not a piece of luck, but a misfortune”).
- Mass of surplus-value depends on rate and number of labourers employed; minimum capital thresholds appear historically.

## Applications

- Explains capital as self-expanding value without treating profit as a mark-up invented in exchange alone.
- Feeds accumulation analysis (Part 7): surplus-value converted into additional capital.
- Empirical documentation of English factory conditions and the working-day struggle is illustration of absolute SV struggle, not independent modern survey data — date-stamp mid-19th century.

## Volume 2 Extensions (1885)

In *[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume II]]*, Marx extends the analysis from the production of surplus-value to its turnover and realization.

- **Annual rate of surplus-value.** Marx argues the annual rate of surplus-value S′ diverges from the "real" rate s′ produced within a single turnover period, according to S′ = s′ · n, where n is the number of turnovers of variable capital per year. Two capitals employing the same labour at the same real rate of exploitation can show wildly different annual rates purely as an artifact of turnover speed. Marx characterizes this divergence, once mistaken by classical economists for an independent effect of circulation on exploitation, as having contributed to "the complete destruction of the Ricardian school" from the 1820s onward.
- **Realization and the "famous monetary problem."** Marx poses and answers the question of where the additional money comes from to realize surplus-value in circulation, given that the capitalist class as a whole advances only the value of its capital. His resolution: individual capitalists' own prior spending of money as personal revenue (not as capital advance) pre-funds the money that returns as the money-form of their surplus-value; this holds for the capitalist class as a whole under simple reproduction. The residual structural source of net new money is the gold/silver-producing sector, whose product is already money in its natural form.
- **Hoard formation vs. real accumulation.** Marx distinguishes real accumulation (surplus-value transformed into productive capital) from monetary accumulation/hoarding (surplus-value stored as latent money capital); genuine growth of the *national* money hoard, as opposed to money merely withdrawn from existing circulation, occurs only through exchange with gold/silver-producing countries.

## Volume 3 Extensions (1894)

In *[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume III]]* (ed. Engels, 1894), Marx traces surplus-value through its "mystified" transformation into profit and its subsequent distribution.

- **Profit as the transformed, mystified form of surplus-value.** Subtracting surplus-value s from total commodity value (C = c + v + s) leaves the "cost price" k (= c + v), giving C = k + s. Once surplus-value appears, in ordinary capitalist accounting, as an increment on the *total* capital advanced rather than on variable capital alone, it takes the mystified form of profit p: C = k + p. Marx states the rate of profit p′ = s/C (surplus-value over total capital advanced) as against the rate of surplus-value s′ = s/v (surplus-value over variable capital alone) — "two different standards for measuring the same quantity." Because all portions of capital appear equally as sources of the "excess value," the capital relation is mystified: surplus-value "denies its own origin ... becomes unrecognizable."
- **Division of surplus-value among claimants.** The single pool of social surplus-value produced in production is subsequently redistributed among industrial (average) profit, commercial profit, interest, and [[ground-rent]] via competition and the formation of [[price-of-production]] — a redistribution, not a new creation, of value.

## The Russian Realization Controversy (Lenin, 1899)

In *[[lenin-development-of-capitalism-in-russia-1899|The Development of Capitalism in Russia]]* (1899), [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] took up a dispute with Russian Narodnik economists ("N.–on" [N. Danielson] and "V. V.") over whether surplus-value can be **realized** — sold, converted back into money — on the home market alone. Lenin argued (attributed) that N.-on and V. V. wrongly reduced the whole realization problem to realization of surplus-value alone, treating realization of constant capital as unproblematic; he held the real theoretical difficulty lies with constant capital, requiring exchange between Department I (means of production) and Department II (articles of consumption), a distinction he charged the Narodnik economists with overlooking. Quoting *Capital* Vol. II, Lenin argued Marx held the foreign market "must be entirely discarded" from the analysis of realization, since introducing it only relocates the same unsolved problem across several countries rather than resolving it — so the Narodniks' appeal to a foreign-market "way out" for surplus-value realization evaded rather than solved the theoretical question. He distinguished Marx's "underconsumption" passages (on the contradiction between capitalism's drive to expand production and the limited consumption of the proletarianized masses) from the Narodnik reading that these passages implied surplus-value cannot be realized or that capitalism is impossible: for Lenin the passages describe a real contradiction, not an impossibility theorem. He extended this criticism to Eduard Bernstein (*Die Voraussetzungen*, 1899), whom he read as similarly misconstruing the same Marx passage as self-contradictory, and to Mikhail Tugan-Baranovsky's related misreading in "Capitalism and the Market" (1898). See [[labor-theory-of-value]] for Lenin's parallel diagnosis of Smith's "dogma" as the doctrinal root of the Narodnik error.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Transformation of surplus-value into profit/prices of production is Vol. III terrain — incomplete in Vol. I.
- Contested by theories that locate profit in entrepreneurship, time-preference, or market power without labour exploitation.
- Immiseration-adjacent claims in later chapters are positions within Marx’s framework, not wiki-voice forecasts.
