# Surplus-Value

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marx-capital-volume-1-1867|Capital, Volume I]]* (1867), [[marx-karl|Marx]] defines **surplus-value** as the increment of value that money acquires when transformed into capital — the Δ in M–C–M′ — originating in production from unpaid labour of the working class once [[labor-power]] has been purchased at its value and set to work beyond the time needed to reproduce that value.

**All theoretical claims attributed to Marx.**

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| Classical PE “profit” / “surplus” | Often unexplained residue or circulation mark-up |
| Marx *Capital* I | Unpaid labour of labour-power beyond its own value; absolute vs relative forms |
| Later Marxism | Distribution into profit, rent, interest (Vol. III) |
| Mainstream economics | Opportunity cost / residual claims — different ontology |

## Absolute and Relative Surplus-Value

| Form | Mechanism (Marx) | Historical note |
|---|---|---|
| **Absolute** | Prolong the working day beyond necessary labour-time | General groundwork of the capitalist system; formal subjection of labour to capital |
| **Relative** | Shorten necessary labour by raising productivity (cheapening means of subsistence → lower value of labour-power) | Revolves technical process and social composition; real subjection of labour to capital |

Co-operation, manufacture, and machinery (Part 4) are analyzed as methods of producing relative surplus-value. The working day (Ch. 10) is the historical theatre of struggle over absolute surplus-value (Factory Acts, etc.).

## Operationalizations

- **Rate of surplus-value** s/v — degree of exploitation of labour-power (distinct from rate of profit s/C on total capital).
- **Constant capital (c)** vs **variable capital (v)** — only v produces surplus-value (→ [[constant-and-variable-capital]]).
- Productive labourer under capitalism: one who produces surplus-value for capital (“not a piece of luck, but a misfortune”).
- Mass of surplus-value depends on rate and number of labourers employed; minimum capital thresholds appear historically.

## Applications

- Explains capital as self-expanding value without treating profit as a mark-up invented in exchange alone.
- Feeds accumulation analysis (Part 7): surplus-value converted into additional capital.
- Empirical documentation of English factory conditions and the working-day struggle is illustration of absolute SV struggle, not independent modern survey data — date-stamp mid-19th century.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Transformation of surplus-value into profit/prices of production is Vol. III terrain — incomplete in Vol. I.
- Contested by theories that locate profit in entrepreneurship, time-preference, or market power without labour exploitation.
- Immiseration-adjacent claims in later chapters are positions within Marx’s framework, not wiki-voice forecasts.
