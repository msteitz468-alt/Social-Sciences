# Craft Specialization

## Definition as Coined

**Craft specialization** is production in which some people make goods (or perform services) beyond their own household’s needs, often as a substantial part of livelihood — the occupational face of the shift from functional redundancy to functional interdependence that [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]] place at the heart of [[complex-society]] origins.

**Brumfiel & Earle** (via W&O ch. 7) decompose occupational specialization into at least four dimensions:

1. **Independent vs attached** specialists (market/household vs elite/institutionally controlled)  
2. **Product type** — subsistence goods, wealth/prestige items, services  
3. **Intensity** — part-time vs full-time  
4. **Scale** — individual vs group/village/region  

Plus varied elite–citizen and elite–elite exchange arrangements.

Earle’s [[chiefdom]] definition (via W&O): greater specialization than tribes but usually **no permanent artisan class** as in states. States, by contrast, are framed with full-time specialists among their checklist features.

## Semantic History

Central to Childe’s [[urban-revolution]] and civilization checklists; refined as archaeologists recognized specialization without states and states with limited specialization (e.g., Mississippian mound societies denied state status partly for few full-time specialists — W&O). Bevel-rimmed bowls at [[uruk]] and administered ration economies mark early intrusion of government into commodity production (Nissen/Johnson via W&O). Late Uruk **Standard Professions List** ranks officials and professions by power and prestige — textual evidence of occupational hierarchy. Costin’s archaeological typology (independent/attached intensity matrices) is widely used in the wider literature W&O’s synthesis assumes.

## Operationalizations

Workshop debris, standardization, skill indicators, spatial segregation of production, distributional patterning, sealings/bullae tracking commodity flows, textual professions lists — equifinality remains.

W&O examples of inference:

- Mid–late 4th millennium [[uruk]]: many craft specialists and centralized pottery production; mass-mold bevel-rim bowls as possible ration/temple production.  
- [[teotihuacan]] by ~A.D. 100: hundreds of workshops; perhaps ≤25% of population as craft specialists (obsidian, ceramics, precious stones, slate, basalt, shell, feathers, basketry, leather, etc.); household production possibly state-taxed.  
- [[mohenjo-daro]] / Harappan: goldsmiths, potters, bead-makers, seal-cutters, brick masons; mass-produced goods with possible administered redistribution; standardized weights.  
- Shang (Zhengzhou via W&O comparative list): mass bronze casting, bone workshops, pottery kilns; hundreds of skilled full-time craftsmen.  
- Longshan: craft specialization and residential segregation at regional centers (Li Liu via W&O).  
- Hopewell exchange of copper, mica, obsidian, etc., and “consumption” of goods in mound burials keeping demand high.  
- Olmec: basalt heads >20 tons implying skilled specialists without metal tools; jade, magnetite mirrors, obsidian trade.

## Applications

Used across Mesopotamian, Egyptian, Harappan, Chinese, Mesoamerican, Andean, European, and North American case studies in world-prehistory teaching (W&O 2007):

- **Egypt:** royal-controlled crafts and pyramid labor as specialization under state command.  
- **Europe / Aegean:** *[[renfrew-emergence-of-civilisation-1972|Renfrew 1972]]* Ch. 16–17 treats craft specialisation (part-time → full-time; metal, stone vases, textiles, longships) as a culture **subsystem** whose coupling with trade, ranking, and [[mediterranean-polyculture]] drives [[multiplier-effect]] take-off; EB2 metallurgy boom read as largely local with Neolithic antecedents, not simple Oriental import (attributed). *[[dickinson-aegean-bronze-age-1994|Dickinson 1994]]* Ch. 5 surveys the full BA craft suite (pottery through weapons, frescoes, seals, writing): three-tier crafts; only luxury/palace specialists full-time; potters often contractual + farming; palace-tied crafts collapse with palaces (attributed).  
- **Andes:** craft specialization appears among preconditions before sustained warfare’s peak role in W&O’s multi-causal Inka-path summary.  
- **North America:** limited specialization at [[cahokia]] / Mississippian and [[chaco-canyon]] relative to Teotihuacán — comparative ceiling argument.  
- Early Mesoamerican villages: possible household/village craft specialties (salt, feathers, shell, grinding stones) before states.  
- San José Mogote (Oaxaca): rising exotic materials and craft (obsidian, jade, magnetite, shell) as rank ornaments 1400–850 BCE.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Over-reading prestige goods as hierarchy; underestimating household production.  
- Part-time specialization and attached vs independent distinctions hard to recover from debris alone.  
- Chiefdoms may show wealth display and trade without full-time artisan classes — specialization is scalar, not binary.  
- Administered vs market redistribution often underdetermined (Harappan seals undeciphered).  
- Ethnographic variability and mortuary equifinality (Carr on mortuary, etc.).  
- Mississippian warning: large populations and elite burial sacrifice without many specialists ≠ state by common archaeological definition (W&O).

## The Organisation of Craft and Labour in Early Mesopotamia (Postgate 1994)

*(Attributed — [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]], drawing on cuneiform administrative archives c. 3000–1500 BCE.)*

### Institutional vs private craft sectors

Postgate proposes that crafts using locally available raw materials (reed mats, palm-rib furniture) tended to remain household-based, while crafts requiring imported materials (metals, semi-precious stones) tended to be institution-initiated, given the capital and foreign-contact requirements involved — explicitly offered as a "presumption," not an established fact. The private/domestic craft sector is largely invisible in the textual record and is inferred to be present but simply undocumented, not absent. There is no firm evidence for craft guilds in this period; patron deities existed for some crafts (e.g., the brick-god Kulla) but it is unclear whether this reflects formal institutionalization, and there is little evidence for hereditary craft transmission outside the merchant class. Craft quarters within cities are only weakly attested (at Ur and Nippur, priests, scribes, and merchants had identifiable quarters, but trade-named city quarters are rare — the only possible case is a gate name at Assur). The only known apprenticeship contracts from this period (for a singer and a cook) are a scribal school exercise from Old Babylonian Isin, in marked contrast to well-documented Neo-Babylonian apprenticeship practice.

Temple and palace institutions were the principal patrons of craft production, with the balance shifting by period: temples were primary patrons in the Early Dynastic period, while the palace became more important under Ur III and at Mari. The Old Babylonian Ninurta Temple at Nippur retained regular craft employees (smiths, textile workers, stone-workers) and also hired workers ad hoc, such as goldworkers imported from Larsa for a specific job at an Ur temple.

### Ur III craft administration and scale

An Ur III archive from Ur's temple area records multiple craft departments — wood/ivory workers, gold/silversmiths, fine stone-workers, carpenters, smiths, leather-workers, rope-makers, and reed-workers — grouped under a single administrative office termed "craftsmen" (giš-kin-ti), with materials (gold, silver, lapis lazuli) stored in a central "chief store-house" (ganun-mah); the Pre-Sargonic Lagash archive shows a separate "craft storehouse." The Ur III state exercised extremely fine-grained control of production (e.g., four graded qualities of sesame oil; exact time allocations per pottery type in daily workshop output records) — a standardization practice that outlived the Ur III political system itself, with the unified craft-administration office adopted essentially unchanged by Išbi-Erra of the Isin Dynasty, attested via a leather-industry archive at Isin.

The **textile industry** was quantitatively the most important institutional craft in every period, based mainly on wool rather than flax. Scale figures include 600 tonnes of wool recorded in a single Ur III capital-city text, and a textile "factory" at Guabba (Lagash) employing over 6,000 workers, the great majority women and children. Pre-Sargonic Lagash divided institutional textile production into a "wool place" (ki siki) and a "flax place" (ki gu) by process; the "North Palace" at Ešnunna was partly devoted to textile production, possibly linked to the Abu Temple. Later Old Babylonian archives (the Ishchali temple; the Larsa state workshop) show continued non-domestic-scale textile production with detailed labour-time accounting (e.g., one fine robe required 384 textile-worker-days).

### The ration/wage terminology debate

Gelb proposed a terminological dichotomy between "rations" (še-ba), issued to slave or dependent workers, and "wages" (á), paid to free hired persons. Postgate reports this dichotomy "has not entirely stood the test of time": following Waetzoldt (1987), the distinction is better explained by the regularity of payment (monthly payments are termed "rations"-type, regardless of the recipient's servile or free status) than by servile status per se.

### The gurush/geme dependent work-teams

Lagash "workers" (erín in Ur III texts; šub-lugal, "royal subjects(?)," in Pre-Sargonic texts) served state duty in monthly rotating turns ("serving their turn," alternating with off-duty periods during which they worked as free wage-agents ("sitting out their turn," paid á, and often borrowing barley in advance). These rotating conscripts were distinct from full-time state farmers (engar) and were often city residents, deployed on seasonal tasks such as winter dike-repair and canal-clearing. A grain-milling establishment at Sagdana (near Girsu) employed male (guruš) and female (gemé) workers of varied backgrounds — prisoners of war, workers of miscellaneous professions, and principally tenants/squatters — some for only a few months, some accompanied by their families.

### The ilkum land-service system

*Ilkum* was a temporary civilian and/or military service obligation tied to usufruct of state land, likely a secularized descendant of the temple prebend system (see [[temple-economy]]); it is attested from Ur III precursors through the Code of Hammurabi and royal correspondence. Some office-holders (priestesses, merchants) were permitted to sell ilkum plots under the Code (§§36–37), implying that illicit sale had already been occurring among those forbidden it. Substitution for ilkum military service is directly attested — hired substitutes for a "royal campaign" are documented despite being forbidden by the Code — and later archives show soldiers trading service turns like commercial debts and credits. Not everyone in Old Babylonian society was liable to ilkum: Postgate's own view ("In my view," explicitly attributed) is that it applied specifically to holders of state-land allotments tied to a service duty, not to families holding property under traditional tenure.

### The muskenum status

The *muškēnum* was a middle social class named in the Old Babylonian law codes, positioned between free men and slaves. Postgate's best-attested characterization is that the muškēnum was semi-free, tied to land by service obligations to the palace, but not excluded from private income — the social order in practice appears to have been less rigidly stratified than the law codes suggest. An Isin royal inscription (probably of Enlil-bani) claims to have reduced muškēnum service obligation to "four days in the month" and cut the barley tax from one-fifth to one-tenth — a self-presentation as justice-restorer that should be read as propagandistic royal ideology, not literal social history.

### [POSITION] The status of Ur III labour — Gelb's "mass of serfs" vs. a revisionist mixed-status reading

Whether the Ur III guruš/gemé labour force was predominantly servile is a live historiographic dispute, not resolved here. The older view (associated with Gelb's earlier characterization) held that guruš constituted "the mass of serfs... the main productive labor" of the Ur III economy. Postgate reports that recent scholarship — citing Gelb's own 1979 revision — argues that considerably less of the Ur III labour force was truly servile than earlier assumed: these workers may belong instead among "free peasants and craftsmen working outside of the public households," not "a negligible force." Postgate does not adjudicate between the two readings; the wiki records this as an open position pending fuller treatment on a dedicated debate page.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Indus craft typology and control

*(All claims attributed to [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] in *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* unless noted.)*

### Four-category typology

With the rise of cities ca. 2600 BCE, Kenoyer argues specialized crafts became essential for producing **unique objects that distinguished high officials and rulers** from other elites and commoners; rulers and merchants could maintain status through control of economics and technology rather than (primarily) military monuments. He cross-classifies Indus urban crafts by **material source × technological complexity**:

| | Simple technology | Complex technology |
|---|---|---|
| **Local materials** | Woodworking, basketmaking, simple weaving, terracotta ceramics, house-building — **most regionally varied** | Stoneware bangles, painted pottery — considerable standardization depending on social/ritual importance |
| **Imported / nonlocal materials** | (e.g. simple working of brought-in stone/shell blanks) | Long carnelian drilling, seal carving, some metallurgy stages — **most standardized** |

Kenoyer’s generalization: local+simple crafts vary most across the Indus Valley; nonlocal+complex crafts are most standardized; local+complex crafts (stoneware, painted pottery) standardize in proportion to the finished object’s social/ritual weight.

### Direct vs indirect control — seals, weights, stoneware

Kenoyer argues that **stoneware banglemaking, seal production, and chert weight manufacture** may have been **directly controlled** in segregated workshops by rulers or the state to limit access to power symbols requiring standardized form, size, or decoration. Pottery and most ornaments, by contrast, were standardized through other mechanisms (family apprenticeship, market spread, shared tradition).

- **Stoneware and faience:** High-fired ceramics were more tightly controlled than low-fired wares. Stoneware bangles as power symbols were inscribed with names/titles of elites and used by elites mainly in the largest urban centers. Glazed faience was valuable and specialized but more widely available than stoneware. Indus faience technology (quartz frit >1000°C; regrind; ~920°C glaze) is treated as a complex, partly controlled craft.
- **Seals and tablets:** Specialized engravers made seals with writing in reverse for raised clay impressions; seals/wooden molds produced multiple terracotta and faience tablet copies. When elites fell in Localization, seal carvers lost jobs; molds for tablets were destroyed/discarded; traders stopped stamping bundles — **craft continuity** (many technical crafts continued) is analytically separated from **elite communication technologies**.
- **Weights:** See [[standardized-weights]] — cubical chert weights as possible taxation/control instruments concentrated at gateways; Kenoyer groups weight manufacture with seals/stoneware as candidate directly controlled crafts.
- **Pottery:** Absence of sealings or graffiti on pottery is used to argue pottery was **not** directly controlled by ruling elites of the larger cities; kilns are scattered at Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Chanhudaro, Nausharo, with some segregated kiln zones (e.g. NW Mound E at Harappa).

### Spatial organization of production

Preliminary stages of some crafts were practiced predominantly near raw-material sources (shell on the coast; copper mined and smelted near ores in Baluchistan, Rajasthan, or Oman); partly processed shell, agate, steatite, and copper were transported to large urban centers for final manufacture into ornaments, tools, or ritual objects. Metal production and distribution, Kenoyer argues, were likely monitored by dividing labor into distinct stages under a merchant or head artisan (e.g. lost-wax casting vs finishing; tablet fabrication vs inscription; bead casting vs stringing).

### Signature crafts

- **Beadmaking:** Among the oldest Indus-margin crafts (Mehrgarh ornaments centuries before pottery). Urban workshops produced unique styles; **constricted cylindrical drills** of rare metamorphic rock (“Ernestite”) at larger sites (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira, Chanhudaro) for hard agate/carnelian/jasper. Only confirmed long-carnelian workshop carefully excavated: **Chanhudaro** (Mackay) with full manufacturing sequence. Long carnelian traded to Mesopotamia (Ur royal burials); some beads use Indus drilling + Mesopotamian styles — possibly migrant Indus artisans of a “Meluhhan minority” (Parpola & Brunswig via Kenoyer; see [[meluhha]]).
- **Shell:** Chevron-incised *Turbinella pyrum* bangles as widespread unity symbols limited in quantity; coastal primary working + inland finishing.
- **Textiles:** Cotton/wool poorly preserved; figurine clothing and Mesopotamian texts on cotton from Meluhha support production; state control of weaving largely indirect (taxing raw materials/sales), Kenoyer suggests.
- **Metallurgy:** Bun-shaped copper ingots from multiple source regions; arsenical copper vs tin bronze for different tools; saw-cut depths on shell waste imply hard-edged metal tools.

### Social function (Kenoyer’s synthesis)

Each craft contributed to urban structure by **linking some communities and distinguishing others**: complex technologies and rare materials differentiated ruling elites; religious and cultural symbols on pottery, ornaments, and seals integrated multi-ethnic populations under shared ideology. Ornament hierarchies (terracotta/faience copies of rare eye-beads and gold for commoners; bronze figurines for wealthiest) operationalize status without deciphered texts. Ethnoarchaeology (Khambhat agate, Bengal/Pakistan shell workers, Harappa master potters) and experimental studies (jar manufacturing stages) are explicitly used as **analogy and process models**, not as direct evidence of Indus social relations.

### Localization and afterlife of crafts

In Localization / Punjab Phase, western highland and coastal trade links break down (lapis, turquoise, marine shell rarer); faience becomes more refined, possibly compensating for lost shell/carnelian. Diagnostic Integration artifacts — inscribed seals and weights, perforated vessels, etc. — drop out of many Gujarat sequences. Kenoyer tracks technical continuities into Early Historic practice (copper → possible indigenous iron path; faience → glass ca. 1700 BCE; long-bead drills replaced later by diamond tips) while finding **no convincing continuity** between Indus and Early Historic **writing, coinage, and weight systems** as institutional packages (weights/writing reappear in NBPW contexts as new configurations).

## Potts 1997 — Mesopotamian craft division of labor (textiles, pottery, metalworking)

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts, Mesopotamian Civilization 1997]], drawing on cuneiform archives and archaeological/experimental evidence.)*

### Textile factories, fulling, and tanning

Textile factories are attested in seven Ur III cities (Alsharraki, Lagash, Nippur, Puzrish-Dagan/Drehem, Umma, Ur, Uruk). Weavers — exclusively female, plus children — formed the largest labor category: Girsu texts record 1,097 female weavers plus 626 children (year Shulgi 48). Workers were a mix of war captives, slaves, free persons, and temple votaries drawn from a cross-section of society. The largest single mill, at Guabba (Lagash territory), employed over 4,272 female weavers and 1,800+ children (H. Waetzoldt estimates 15,000+ people worked in Lagash-territory textile factories in total; Ur employed an estimated 13,200 weavers under 60 foremen). Fullers, who cleaned and thickened woven cloth, required an estimated 7.7 work-days to treat each kilogram of finished cloth, using oil-to-alkali ratios of roughly 1:5.5 to 1:4 per kilogram depending on cloth type (Waetzoldt 1972).

Tanning was similarly specialized: the tanner (Sum. *asgab*, Akk. *aškapum*) was a dedicated craftsman, sometimes palace-attached in the Old Babylonian period (Renger 1984); late lexical sources name sub-specialists ("tanner for shields," "tanner for doors," "whip maker," "shoe-maker"). Two traditional tanning methods are described in ritual/technical texts — a "primitive" method (salt/sour milk/flour for dehairing, pomegranate skins or plant roots for tanning) and an "industrial" method (calcium for dehairing, gall nuts/oak bark/sumac/alum for tanning). An Umma summary tablet suggests an annual intake of roughly 2,150 skins from a single agricultural sector, though M. Sigrist (1981) suspects this undercounts the real total.

### Umma Ur III pottery workshop ledgers (MW 124/125) and the Renger household-production debate

The professional title "potter" (*bahar*) already appears in the Archaic professions/titles list of the Late Uruk and Jamdat Nasr periods, indicating that potting had become a specialist profession beyond household production by that date (Nissen, Damerow and Englund 1990). Two Ur III texts from Umma, MW 124 and MW 125 (Waetzoldt 1970–71), are year-long balanced "debit/credit" ledgers for two large pottery workshops: MW 124 records manufacture of nearly 2,000 vessels plus 12 other clay objects over 3,604.38+ workdays; MW 125 records manufacture of 66,711+ vessels plus 40 clay objects over 7,206.2 workdays, spanning 46 distinct vessel types. Potters worked in teams of two to ten under a supervisor; rations for potters ranged 30–60 sila of barley per month plus 3–4 minas of wool per year, with children receiving smaller allotments. Potters were attached to state kitchens, breweries, mills, temples, and elite households including Shulgi's own. Female potters are not attested for this period — Ur III industrial pottery production was predominantly male labor, in contrast to household pottery production elsewhere in Western Asia, which was more commonly women's work (Potts, citing his own note 12).

For the Old Babylonian period, J. Renger (1984) argued pottery reverted to household production, mostly by women, using domestic hearths or common kilns, citing texts including a Sippar potter's delivery of 1,380 vessels and an apprenticeship contract. Potts disputes this reading — a **live scholarly disagreement, recorded here as a position rather than adjudicated** — arguing the archaeological and technical evidence (continued fast-wheel use, uniform clay preparation, mass quantities of identically shaped sherds) indicates continued industrial-scale, professionalized production comparable to earlier periods, contradicting Renger's household-production model.

### Metalworking division of labor and workshop scarcity

Metalworking division of labor distinguished smiths (Sum. *simug*, Akk. *nappāḫum* — melting/casting), metalworkers (Sum. *tibira*, Akk. *gurgurrum* — working metal into objects), jewellers (Sum. *zadim*), and goldsmiths (Sum. *kù-dím*, Akk. *kutimmum*). Despite abundant metal artifacts, excavated metalworking facilities are rare; claimed workshop areas at Mari, Ur, Uruk, Khafajah, Kish, and Tell Asmar are all discounted by P.R.S. Moorey (1985). Only Isin-Larsa period Tell edh-Dhiba'i (with pot-bellows, crucibles, clay moulds, a tuyere fragment) was considered a definite metalworking site until recently; copper/bronze slag concentrations at Old Babylonian Mashkan-Shapir suggest metalworking was dispersed throughout residential neighborhoods there (Stone and Zimansky 1992).


## Wright (2010) — Indus organizational modes, value axes, and craft geography

*(Attributed throughout — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]*, 2010, Chs. 6–7.)*

Wright synthesizes four interrelated economic factors from Kaiser and Voytek 1983, Morrison 1994, Dorsey 1999, and Sinopoli 2003: **intensification** (higher output per unit labor/land), **diversification** (more product kinds), **specialization** (task/resource allocation, standardized output), and **organization** of production/distribution/exchange (household vs. community vs. centralized; independent vs. attached producers). H.M.L. Miller (2007:44) coined **"transformative crafts"** for materials made via pyrotechnology or chemistry (clay, ores, glazes) as distinct from **reductive** crafts (stone, shell — chipping/reshaping); Wright uses this distinction to organize the chapter.

**Three organizational modes, compared directly:** ceramic production at Mound E, Harappa was **independently operated**, family-run across generations — one pit kiln pre-dating a two-chambered kiln by several centuries at the same location, with no record-keeping devices (seals, weights) present, consistent with continuous household-workshop occupation (Wright 1991). Stoneware-bangle production at Mohenjo-daro was interpreted by Vidale (1989:178) and others as **administered/state-supervised**: a walled enclosure, a unicorn seal impression at a saggar's sealing point, script etched on many bangles ("additional indication of some type of undefined, administrative control"), and distribution restricted to only Harappa and Mohenjo-daro. Seal production — per Rissman 1989 and Wright's synthesis — was a formalized craft of skilled artisans linked by family ties, guildlike communities, or an undefined (possibly state-administered) bureaucratic structure. Wright treats these three modes as poles on a spectrum, not evidence for one civilization-wide administrative model.

**Stoneware bangles and carnelian beads as elaborated crafts.** Stoneware bangles were the most technically sophisticated and most spatially restricted bangle type (limited mainly to Harappa and Mohenjo-daro, with three examples at Balakot); production (Wright 1985; Vidale 1990, 2000; Blackman and Vidale 1992) used levigated clay, wheel-thrown hollow cylinders cut into bangles, fired in sealed ceramic saggars stacked in a larger jar — black-gray color from a reducing atmosphere, red from different clay/lower temperature/open saggars. Many stoneware bangles carried personal inscriptions etched before or after firing, unlike bangles of other materials — Wright reads this as evidence they were among the Harappans' most highly valued possessions, given the technology's likely guarded, specialized nature. Long-barrel carnelian beads (Kenoyer 1991, 1998) required chipping, intermittent heating, polishing, drilling, and reheating in a reducing atmosphere to deepen the red color via iron oxide; Kenoyer estimated **13–14 days of labor** for a single skilled worker to produce one long-barrel bead.

**Seal "schools."** Paul Rissman's 1989 stylistic study of 601 seals from Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, Chanhu-daro, and Kalibangan (verified by chi-square tests) found limited, site-clustered variation in head/neck treatment and "cage" motif style — hatched face + zig-zag cage predominant at Harappa; collared neck + straight cage at Mohenjo-daro/Chanhu-daro but absent at Kalibangan (independently corroborated by Franke-Vogt 1992:107 for Mohenjo-daro). Rissman (1989:159, 168) interprets these regional clusters as recognizable **"schools"** of producers, reflecting either rigid bureaucratic guidelines or informal social networks among artisans visiting relatives/friends at other workshops; some near-identical seals suggest shared or copying artisans. Seal iconography/style was uniform at Mohenjo-daro's deepest levels but became **more standardized** in later (upper) levels — a decrease in variability over time (Franke-Vogt 1992:108).

**Chanhu-daro as an artisan quarter.** Ernest Mackay, the original excavator, found production debris and craft variety so extensive he suggested the site was "populated chiefly by artisans" (Mackay 1943:38). Production was spatially segregated — seal production in the northern quarter; beads, weights, and metal in the southern portion — and Kenoyer (1997a) and Vidale (2000:58) explicitly *hypothesized* (not established) centralized bead production there "controlled by state institutions or important merchants connected with political elites," based on production-area quality and probable trade to the Royal Cemetery of Ur.

**Axes of Relative Value.** H.M.L. Miller's framework (2008:215, building on Kenoyer 1992/Vidale 1992, modified by Wright) ranks Indus craft objects on two axes — **technological elaboration** (low–high) and **raw-material access/procurement difficulty** (low–high). High on both axes: stoneware bangles, elaborate metal objects, etched/long-barrel carnelian beads, lapis lazuli, limestone, faience/glazed objects, statuary, and seal production. Low on both: simple terracotta, bone tools, basketry, simple woven cloth, simple stone beads. Wright, drawing on Lemonnier (1992:13), frames value-ranking as correlating with consumption patterns and social/economic status, and technological style itself as "a catalyst for important social transformations."

Wright's own synthesis states she deliberately avoided over-emphasizing centralized-production models, arguing prior scholarship had not treated agriculture, pastoralism, and craft production as dimensions of a single, integrally related political economy — her chapters argue these compete for the same land, water, resource access, and labor allocation (Wright 2010, Ch. 7 concluding synthesis).

## Shelach-Lavi densification — Bronze Age China (2015)

*(Attributed — [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]].)*

Shelach-Lavi documents craft specialization intensifying across the Erlitou-to-Shang
sequence: an Erlitou bronze foundry and turquoise workshop at the [[erlitou|Erlitou]] type
site; Shang bronze, bone, and pottery workshop specialization at [[zhengzhou|Zhengzhou]]
(Nanguanwai vs. Zijingshan workshop areas) and at [[anyang|Yinxu]] (Miaopu, Xiaomintun,
Tiesanlu); and metal/jade production at the Qijia, Siba, and Zhukaigou cultures, alongside
Lower Xiajiadian ceramic standardization. He describes **piece-mold bronze casting** —
originating in Erlitou phase III and reaching its greatest sophistication and scale under
the Shang — as a multi-step process (core, outer mold jacket, spacers, multi-pour casting
for complex parts) requiring roughly twelve specialist steps per vessel (Li 2007), a level
of technical division of labor comparable to the textile- and metal-workshop specialization
already documented for Mesopotamia and the Indus above.

He also records **household- or small-group-level craft specialization in the Neolithic**,
earlier and less centralized than the Bronze Age workshop economy: fast pottery wheels
became common for both prestigious and utilitarian ceramics (Underhill 2002); the quality of
Shandong Longshan **eggshell ware** implies a division of ceramic production into
subspecializations (potters, kiln masters); Liangzhu jade-carving required highly skilled
artisans and advanced drilling technology to produce *cong* tubes, with possible
household-level specialization suggested by kiln clustering at Kangjia (Liu 2004) and
jade-drilling tools recovered from a Liangzhu elite grave at Fuquanshan (Huang Xuanpei
2000). Shelach-Lavi is explicit about the limits of this evidence base: "the data available

## McIntosh 2005 — Middle Niger corporate specialists

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]] Chs. 3–4.)*

Specialization (subsistence and artisan) is the engine of the [[pulse-model]] and of urban heterogeneity: potters, weavers, leatherworkers, blacksmiths, herders, fishers, rice/millet/sorghum farmers. Clustering maintains corporate boundaries while enabling exchange. Iron-working sequence at [[jenne-jeno]] (on-site smelt+smith early; later smelt to satellites) is the best-dated craft trajectory. Ethnohistoric Mande *nyamakalaw* used as analogy for secrecy/reputation, not direct ethnic continuity.
