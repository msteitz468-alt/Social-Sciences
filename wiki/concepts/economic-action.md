## Definition as Coined

Weber defines "economic activity" (*Wirtschaften*) as the peaceful exercise of a power of disposition (*Verfügungsgewalt*) primarily oriented to "rational economic action," itself planfully oriented to economic ends. "Economically-oriented" action is action whose intended meaning is oriented to meeting a desire for utilities (*Nutzleistungen*) — the concrete, real or putative chances (*Chancen*) that one or more economic agents deem appropriate objects of provision (Ch. 2 §1–§2). Weber grounds the definition on two facts alone — that utilities are desired, and that effort is made to secure meeting that desire — deliberately avoiding grounding in "consumption needs" or "satisfaction," so that the definition also covers the modern acquisitive economy (*Erwerbswirtschaft*).

Weber distinguishes "economic action" proper from the broader category of "economically-oriented" action, which includes action primarily oriented to other ends that merely takes economic circumstance into account, or action primarily economic in aim but employing violence as a means. Following Franz Oppenheimer's distinction between economic and political means, Weber holds that direct confiscation or forceful compulsion should not itself be called "economic action," though the economic provisioning of the means of violence (a war economy) is economic action (Ch. 2 §2).

## Semantic History

Weber insists explicitly against treating economic action as a merely "psychic" phenomenon: it is constituted by a special kind of intended meaning, not subjective sensation alone. He also distinguishes economy from technology (*Technik*): technology concerns the rational calibration of means to ends irrespective of scarcity, while economic action becomes relevant specifically once the relative scarcity of alternative means enters deliberation — "costs" become an issue only from the economic standpoint.

Weber further denies any scientific warrant for positing a natural "economic state of nature," rejecting the use of contemporary "primitive" peoples (he names the Vedda and Amazonian groups) as evidence of a universal past stage — a POSITION taken against evolutionary-stage economic anthropology of his period.

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Ideal-typical construct built from constituent definitions (power of disposition, utilities, calculation). Distinguishes householding (*Haushalt*) from acquisition (*Erwerben*) and economic action proper from merely economically-oriented action (including violent means).

## Applications

Within economic action, Weber distinguishes budgetary householding (*Haushalt*) — continuous use and procurement of goods for one's own provision — from profit-making acquisition (*Erwerben*), oriented to gaining new powers of disposition over goods. He insists the two are not mutually exclusive within a single household (especially the small farmer) and, against the assumption that subsistence is analytically prior, argues that "rationally, 'subsistence' is not necessarily a more primitive category than 'acquisition,' 'wealth' [is] not [more primitive than] 'capital,' 'income' [is] not [more primitive than] 'profit'" even though householding was treated first historically (Ch. 2 §10–§11).

Capitalist calculation is defined as the comparison of an opening and closing balance sheet — the estimation of chances of acquisition and outcomes by comparing the estimated monetary value of acquisitional goods present at the start of an undertaking against the stock present at completion. This grounds Weber's technical vocabulary of capital, profit/loss, and capital risk (Ch. 2 §11).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber's own definitional apparatus is dense and highly technical; Keith Tribe's translation apparatus (Appendix A) documents the difficulty of rendering *Wirtschaften* (an intransitive verb for "economising activity" in the 1880s–90s subjectivist economics) into English, where "economise" carries a transitive, scarcity-driven connotation absent in the German. The concept presupposes the market-oriented, calculating actor central to Weber's broader account of [[formal-and-substantive-rationality]] and [[rationalization]], and should not be read as a universal anthropological claim about all economies — Weber's denial of an "economic state of nature" cuts both ways, cautioning against projecting the fully rationalized acquisitive economy backward as much as against positing a primitive baseline.
