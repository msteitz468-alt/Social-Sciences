# Conversational Inference

## Definition as Coined

Verbatim ([[gumperz-john-j|Gumperz]], *[[gumperz-discourse-strategies-1982|Discourse Strategies]]*, Ch. 7): "Conversational inference, as I use the term, is the situated or context-bound process of interpretation, by means of which participants in an exchange assess others' intentions, and on which they base their responses." Inference operates over multiple channels at once — "an inferential process that has as its input syntactic, lexical and prosodic knowledge" — and is guided by [[contextualization-cue|contextualization cues]] within hierarchically ordered judgments: culturally specific **activity-type** assessments feed lower-level discourse tasks (narrating, requesting) that Gumperz treats as universal in function but culturally specific in signalling (attributed).

## Semantic History

Gumperz builds the concept against three traditions he reviews (Ch. 7, all positions attributed): the [[ethnography-of-speaking]] (treats speech events as bounded miniature social systems, leaving in-course interpretation unaddressed); speech-act/discourse analysis in the Grice–Austin–Searle line plus AI schema theory (individual-cognitive, with abstract retrospective event labels); and [[conversation-analysis]] (shows conversation is rule-governed and cooperative but "does not account for the linguistic bases of conversational cooperation" and presupposes shared contextualization). He imports Grice's implicature and Sacks's parsimony principle — an accepted interpretive frame holds until signalled otherwise — while insisting interpretations are "negotiated, repaired and altered through interactive processes rather than unilaterally conveyed."

## Operationalizations

Adequacy of an inference is interactional, not truth-conditional (Postscript, attributed): reasonable given circumstances, confirmed by multi-level signalling, and implicitly accepted through negotiation. Empirical access: playback panels of differing backgrounds (the "speech activity" concept — "a set of social relationships enacted about a set of schemata in relation to some communicative goal" — supplies the frame variable); back-channel timing and conversational-synchrony breakdowns (Erickson & Schultz 1982; Bennett, Erickson & Gumperz 1976).

## Applications

Carries the interethnic-communication analyses of *Discourse Strategies* (the London counseling interview with a Pakistani-born teacher, the "Gravy" cafeteria case, the "we will kill Richard Nixon" rally prosecution — see [[interactional-sociolinguistics]]) and the redefinition of [[communicative-competence]] "in interactional terms as the knowledge of linguistic and related communicative conventions that speakers must have to create and sustain conversational cooperation" (Gumperz 1982, Postscript).

## Critiques and Limitations

The analyst-interpretation problem: readings of intent rest on the analyst's or panel's member knowledge (Gumperz is explicit about this for his own English-language analyses). Later conversation-analytic critics preferred sequential evidence over inferred intent — a recorded methodological position, not adjudicated here.
