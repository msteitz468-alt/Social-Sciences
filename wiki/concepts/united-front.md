# United Front

## Definition as Coined

The **united front** was Comintern tactical doctrine for temporary alliance between communists and non-communist forces — reformist labour parties in the West, and bourgeois or national-revolutionary movements in colonial/semi-colonial countries — against shared enemies. [[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] treats it as both a response to the receding postwar revolutionary wave and a complement to Soviet diplomacy (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 2, 9–10, 18).

## Semantic History

- **Western united front:** After the failure of early putschist hopes (e.g. March 1921 Germany), Comintern pressed work inside trade unions and parliaments (*Left-Wing Communism* line) and episodic common action with social democrats — always under suspicion of "rightism" and later reversed in the "third period" / social-Fascism turn (Carr, late 1920s).
- **Colonial united front:** Second Comintern congress (1920) theses (Lenin): alliance of national and colonial liberation movements with Soviet Russia; support even bourgeois-democratic national-revolutionary movements in backward countries (Carr, Ch. 10).
- **China test case:** Joffe–Sun Yat-sen agreement (1923); Borodin mission; CCP–Kuomintang cooperation; northern expedition; **April 1927 Shanghai massacre** under Chiang Kai-shek and subsequent disasters — for Carr, the nadir of the eastern united-front experiment and a weapon in the [[left-opposition]]'s indictment of Stalin–Bukharin policy.
- **Tension:** Carr repeatedly notes the contradiction between supporting native communists and needing state allies (Turkey, Germany, Italy under Mussolini) for diplomatic survival.

## Operationalizations

| Arena | Typical partners (Carr) | Failure modes |
|---|---|---|
| Europe | Social democrats, Labour parties, trade unions | Mutual distrust; Zinoviev letter (Britain); Arcos break 1927 |
| China | KMT / national movement | Ally turns on communists (1927) |
| Colonial general | National-revolutionary bourgeois movements | Religious/ethnic complications (Baku); weak local parties |

## Applications

- Key to [[comintern]] history between founding idealism and third-period isolation.
- Empirical stake in [[socialism-in-one-country]] vs internationalist critiques.
- Comparative template for later popular-front tactics (beyond Carr's 1929 cutoff).

## Critiques and Limitations

Carr's China narrative is high-level; specialist China historiography and CCP primary materials are not yet ingested. "United front" is emic Comintern language — distinguish from looser journalistic uses.
