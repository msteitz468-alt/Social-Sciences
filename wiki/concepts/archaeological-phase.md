# Archaeological Phase

Primary **practicable unit** of American culture-historical archaeology as formalized by [[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] and [[phillips-philip|Phillips]] (*[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory in American Archaeology]]*, 1958). Preferred to McKern’s “focus” for its stronger temporal implication; dominant usage in the Southwest, Middle America, and most of South America by mid-century.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Kidder, Jennings & Shook 1946, as cited by W&P.)*

> “A cultural complex possessing traits sufficiently characteristic to distinguish it for purposes of preliminary archaeological classification, from earlier and later manifestations of the cultural development of which it formed a part, and from other contemporaneous complexes.”

*(Attributed — Willey & Phillips 1958, modified Kidder.)*

> An archaeological unit possessing traits sufficiently characteristic to distinguish it from all other units similarly conceived, whether of the same or other cultures or civilizations, spatially limited to the order of magnitude of a locality or region and chronologically limited to a relatively brief interval of time.

W&P free Kidder’s definition of the requirement of necessary continuity with what precedes and follows — to allow poorly bracketed phases and intrusive new phases.

## Semantic History

“Phase” was already regional practice (esp. Southwest, Mesoamerica) before 1953. Phillips & Willey’s 1953 paper and the 1958 book made it the **hub** of a full unit system: components compose phases; phases are stacked in local/regional sequences; horizons and traditions integrate phases over larger space-time; “culture”/“civilization” are maximum segmentations. Processual and later archaeologies kept the word while criticizing the culture = people reading of phase groups. Contemporary CRM and research reports still organize sequences as named phases.

## Operationalizations

| Dimension | W&P guidance (1958) |
|---|---|
| Content | Physiognomic, recurrent, internally consistent trait complexes (Childe-like “common social traditions” as aspiration, not proof) |
| Space | Locality to region; **not** fixed — Lithic phases expected larger than sedentary Formative; Classic/Postclassic may enlarge again with sociopolitical scale |
| Time | “Relatively brief” but not standardized; may shrink as change rate rises with complexity |
| Subphases | Allowed when internal differentiation warrants |
| Social equivalent | Hoped-for ≈ society; authors state present chances are against phases having much social reality, yet one may “act as if” they did |

Phase has **no scale independent of the cultural situation** in which it is applied.

## Applications

- Every Americanist area chronology that stacks phases “like boxes” in parallel regional columns (W&P Ch. 2).
- Rouse’s “distributional correlation of phases” as the ideal extracultural-dating model they endorse.
- Stage chapters of *Method and Theory* treat Clovis, Folsom, Early Zacatenco, Hopewell, Teotihuacan II, etc. as **phases** placed into developmental stages.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Continuum problem:** phases intergrade in space and time; Spaulding criticized pure “arbitrary continuum slicing,” arguing real culture-history has punctuated stability/change. W&P acknowledge arbitrariness while treating units as necessary tools.
- **Social non-identity:** material phases need not map to languages, polities, or peoples ([[pots-and-peoples-debate]]).
- **Period vs stage conflation:** culturally correlated area schemes often promote “periods” into developmental stages — a danger W&P name explicitly.
- Later critique: normative phase packages underwrote migration explanations and blocked formation-process and behavioral analysis (New Archaeology afterlife).
