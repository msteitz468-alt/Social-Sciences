# Phonetic Pattern

## Definition as Coined

**Phonetic pattern** is [[sapir-edward|Edward Sapir]]'s term (in *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]*, 1921, Ch. III) for the "inner" or "ideal" sound-system of a language, distinct from its objective phonetic inventory. Behind the physically producible sounds lies "a more restricted 'inner' or 'ideal' system," and, in Sapir's words, "the inner sound-system... is a real and an immensely important principle in the life of a language. It may persist as a pattern... long after its phonetic content is changed. Two historically related languages or dialects may not have a sound in common, but their ideal sound-systems may be identical patterns." He called this "the underlying phonetic pattern (system, one might term it, of symbolic atoms)." (Attributed to Sapir.)

Sapir's key move is that phonetic elements cannot be meaningfully compared across languages until they are "weighted" by their functional value: the same objective difference between two *t*-sounds is functionally irrelevant in English but distinctive ("of real value") in Haida. "The objective comparison of sounds in two or more languages is... of no psychological or historical significance unless these sounds are first 'weighted.'"

## Semantic History

The 1921 concept anticipates the **phoneme** of later structural phonology, but Sapir does not use the word "phoneme" in *Language* — his term is "phonetic pattern" (or "ideal sound-system"). He developed the idea into a full statement in "Sound Patterns in Language" (1925), which became a founding text of American phonemic theory. The relationship between Sapir's psychologically-real "pattern" and the later, more formally defined phoneme is a matter of intellectual history: the pattern notion is broader (a system of relations) and explicitly mentalist. The wiki records this page under Sapir's own term to avoid anachronism (a dedicated `phoneme` page would attribute the term to later structural linguistics).

## Operationalizations

Sapir's operational test is native-speaker intuition, grounded in his own fieldwork: teaching a Nootka interpreter to write his language, Sapir found it "difficult or impossible to teach an Indian to make phonetic distinctions that did not correspond to 'points in the pattern of his language,'" while distinctions that did hit points in the pattern were grasped easily — the informant "transcribing an ideal flow of phonetic elements... as the intention of the actual rumble of speech" (Ch. III, Note 16). The pattern is thus inferred from which sound-distinctions speakers treat as meaningful.

## Applications

- **Diachronic stability**: "The phonetic pattern of a language is not invariable, but it changes far less readily than the sounds that compose it" (Ch. VIII). Sapir cites the English/Sanskrit stop-series correspondence to show the pattern outlives its individual members — connecting to [[linguistic-drift]] and [[historical-linguistics|phonetic law]].
- Foundational to structural phonology (via the 1925 paper).

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's reliance on "psychological reality" and native intuition was later seen as insufficiently formal by some structuralists (and behaviorists such as Bloomfield, who preferred distributional criteria). The mentalist framing — sounds as "symbolic atoms" in the speaker's mind — is exactly what distinguishes Sapir's approach from Bloomfieldian American structuralism and what generative phonology later partly vindicated.
