# Uniformitarianism

The assumption that **present-day processes resemble past ones** closely enough to license
inference from observed, contemporary process to unobserved, past process — a load-bearing
assumption for [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]], [[taphonomy]], and
[[ethnoarchaeology]]. As presented in
[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson's *Archaeological Theory*]] (2011), the
assumption is not uniform in strength across domains.

## Definition as Coined

Geological uniformitarianism originates with Charles Lyell's argument against
catastrophist geology: gradual, presently-observable processes (sedimentation, erosion)
were held sufficient, over long timescales, to explain the geological record without
invoking sudden catastrophic causes (attributed, as reported by Johnson 2011).
[[binford-lewis|Binford]] held that archaeological [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|
middle-range theory]] must be **grounded in a uniformitarian assumption**: that past
conditions resembled present ones closely enough for actualistic studies to serve as a
secure guide to interpreting the archaeological record (attributed, Johnson 2011,
line 3012–3027).

## Semantic History

Johnson (2011), synthesizing Binford's position, argues uniformitarian assumptions are
**graded by domain** (attributed):

- **Strongest** for physical and chemical processes (gravity, sedimentation) — following
  Lyell's argument against catastrophist geology.
- **Intermediate** for ecological and behavioural-ecology processes (e.g. herd behaviour
  relevant to domestication).
- **Weakest and most contestable** for human cultural behaviour — especially for
  non–*Homo sapiens sapiens* hominids of the Lower and Middle Palaeolithic, where even
  "home-base behaviour" cannot be safely assumed.

Johnson notes carbon-14 atmospheric variation as an example of imperfect *physical*
uniformity, requiring calibration against dendrochronology — a caution that even the
strongest-graded domain is not perfectly uniform (attributed, Johnson 2011).

## Operationalizations

Worked examples reported by Johnson (2011, wiki-voice methodological description, citing
Hillman 1984): grain-processing by-products (chaff, charred remains) are traceable via
ethnoarchaeological and experimental analogy to Turkish ethnographic fieldwork; animal
tooth-eruption sequences and seasonal breeding patterns are assumed uniform across time,
allowing inference of age-at-death and season of killing, and thence inferences about
meat versus dairy exploitation strategies.

## Applications

Central to [[taphonomy]] (bone survival, gnawing, transport) and to
[[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]]'s actualistic studies generally;
underlies [[ethnoarchaeology]]'s claim to bridge present observation and past inference.

## Critiques and Limitations

Two critiques reported by Johnson (2011, attributed, position recording): (1) the
uniformitarian assumption itself cannot be independently verified — analogy (whether
middle-range-theoretic or not) can never conclusively prove a claim about the past, and
the critique's force scales with scepticism toward universal cultural-evolutionary
stages; if cultures are historically unique rather than comparable via developmental
stages, ethnographic analogy loses force (e.g. using Inuit or !Kung San patterns as
templates for other hunter-gatherers is undermined once those groups are recognized as
having complex, non-"pristine" histories of their own). (2) Cultural ideas shape even
mundane behaviour (household space, refuse disposal), so archaeological deposition is not
a culture-free physical or ecological process — complicating uniformitarian assumptions
about *behaviour* specifically, as distinct from physical or biological uniformitarianism.
