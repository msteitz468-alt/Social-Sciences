# Sex-Temperament

Mead’s term for **culturally standardized personality patterns associated with sex categories** — the central construct of *[[mead-sex-and-temperament-1935|Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies]]* (1935). Study: [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]]. Theory: [[culture-and-personality]]. Author: [[mead-margaret]].

## Definition as Coined

Mead set as her problem “a study of the conditioning of the social personalities of the two sexes,” having shared “the general belief of our society that there was a natural sex-temperament which could at the most only be distorted or” channelled. The book’s load-bearing conclusion (attributed, 1935):

> “We are forced to conclude that human nature is almost unbelievably malleable, responding accurately and contrastingly to contrasting cultural conditions. … Standardised personality differences between the sexes are of this order, cultural creations to which each generation, male and female, is trained to conform.”

**Sex-temperament** thus names the *socially approved emotional and behavioural style* expected of men and of women in a given culture — not biological sex itself, and not merely costume or legal status.

## Semantic History

| Period | Use |
|---|---|
| 1935 | Mead’s title and analysis: temperament patterns grouped about sex-difference |
| 1950 preface | Mead: book “most misunderstood”; material illuminated **temperament** more than sex differences; readers found three-case pattern “too beautiful” |
| 1963 preface | Sex-role vs individuality framing for later American readers |
| Later gender theory | Absorbed into debates on sex roles, gender personality, social construction of masculinity/femininity; vocabulary shifts to “gender” / “sex roles” while Mead’s three-case slogan persists in teaching |

Distinguish carefully from later constructs: [[sex-category]] / [[sex-categorization]] (West–Zimmerman ethnomethodology); [[gender-order]] (Connell); biological sex. Mead’s term is **culture-and-personality era**: temperament moulded in childhood, standardized by institutions.

## Operationalizations

Mead does not scale or test temperament psychometrically. Operationalization is **comparative ethnographic contrast**:

1. Same temperament both sexes (Arapesh mild/parental; Mundugumor aggressive).
2. Complementary/reversed allotment by sex (Tchambuli/Chambri).
3. Attention to **deviants** who lack the approved pattern — evidence that culture selects from a wider human range.

She explicitly sets aside settling whether *any* universal sex differences exist; the construct targets **standardized social personalities**, not physiology.

## Applications

- Three Sepik cases: [[arapesh]], [[mundugumor]], [[chambri]] — see [[mead-sex-and-temperament-study]].
- Public anthropology argument about American/European sex-role training.
- Bridge from Samoa’s [[adolescence]] plasticity claim to adult sex-personality plasticity.
- Conclusion’s three “courses” (POSITION): complementary standardisation; single-pattern training; multi-temperament recognition (“whole gamut of human potentialities”).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Contested** as over-generalizing each society to one temperament type.
- “Too beautiful” three-case design — reception suspicion of confirmation.
- Uneven ethnographic depth across cases (Mead’s own acknowledgement).
- Period vocabulary and colonial conditions (pacification, government control) shape the record.
- Later work may reanalyze Chambri, Biwat/Mundugumor, Arapesh under different labels and methods — treat 1931–33 claims as dated and attributed.
