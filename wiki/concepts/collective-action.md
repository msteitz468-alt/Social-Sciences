# Collective Action

## Definition as Coined

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (1978): **collective action** is joint action in pursuit of common ends. Its extent is a function of (1) the group's interests, organization, and mobilization, and (2) its opportunity structure (power, repression/facilitation, opportunity/threat).

## Semantic History

Older uses in economics (public goods, free-rider) and sociology (crowd, social movement) diverge. Tilly's political-process definition centers **contention with governments and contenders**, not only voluntary association or irrational crowd behavior.

The rival economic strand descends from [[olson-mancur|Mancur Olson]]'s *[[olson-logic-collective-action-1965|The Logic of Collective Action]]* (1965), which treats collective action not as a given but as a *problem*: rational members of a large [[latent-group|latent group]] will not voluntarily contribute to a shared [[public-good|collective good]] (the [[free-rider-problem]]), so action requires [[selective-incentives|selective incentives]] or coercion. This [[collective-action-theory|Olsonian programme]] and Tilly's political-process tradition frame the same word from opposite starting points — Olson asks why collective action is so *rare*, Tilly's mobilization models ask how its *extent* varies — and much subsequent social-movement theory (resource mobilization) can be read as an answer to Olson.

## Operationalizations

Event catalogs of claim-making performances; strikes; contentious gatherings; violence as tracer. Quantitative models of resources × delivery probability ≈ mobilization.

## Applications

*FMR* program; later social-movement and contentious-politics research.

## Critiques and Limitations

Boundary of "joint" and "common ends" is fuzzy (Tilly acknowledges lack of sharp edges); interest definition is contested; free-rider problem treated but not closed.

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — scalar thresholds and coalescent communities

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* Applying [[collective-action-theory]] to Formative Oaxaca, the authors argue cognitive/informational limits on face-to-face cooperation (Dunbar 2008; Feinman 2011; Johnson 1982) create "scalar challenges" once communities exceed roughly 200–300 persons, and that beyond ~150–200 people social networks increasingly substitute "weak," role-based ties (Granovetter 1973, 1983) for the face-to-face familiarity that sustains cohesion in smaller groups. Above this threshold, one common institutional solution is hierarchical governance, though nonhierarchical strategies such as [[dual-organization|moiety/dual organization]] also occur (Feinman 2011).

They critique the **"moral economy"** tradition (Scott 1976, tracing to Durkheim and Marx) — which explains village solidarity as internalized socialization/ritual norms threatened by individual rationality — as yielding "limited insights" because it does not address scalar stress or cooperator problems. [POSITION: moral economy vs. collective-action readings of village solidarity.] Two ethnographic/historical counter-cases they cite: Samuel Popkin's (1979) fieldwork among rural Vietnamese peasants found "little evidence that norms, values, and role expectations alone can explain how people coexist in villages"; and G. W. Skinner (1971) argued nucleated villages were **not** the preferred condition of premodern Chinese peasant life — dispersed settlement near fields was generally preferred, and nucleation occurred specifically during dynastic collapse and social upheaval, dispersing again once order was restored.

Building on this, Stephen Kowalewski's (2006) concept of **"coalescent" communities** — nucleation as a response to socially chaotic conditions (war, colonial intrusion, demographic collapse, slaving pressure) enabling incorporation of culturally diverse refugees via new universalizing ideologies and collective, non-hierarchical leadership — is applied comparatively (prehistoric North America, New Guinea, Etruscan and Attic urbanism) and, at length, to the ethnographic case of **Ilahita** (Donald Tuzin 2001), an Arapesh coalescent village in the Sepik region of highland New Guinea that reached ~1,500 persons (versus a regional norm of ~300) via wartime refugee incorporation, organized around eight intersecting moieties and a community-wide men's society (the Tambaran) rather than centralized rule; the system lost influence after enforced colonial pacification beginning in the 1950s and was largely inoperative by the 1980s (documentation dates approximate; flagged here as ethnographic analogy, not evidence for Oaxaca). The authors read Ilahita's obligate public actions (feasting, artistic exchange, cooperative construction) through biologist Amotz Zahavi's (1975) **costly-signaling/"handicap principle"** concept (via Blanton 2015; Blanton and Fargher 2016) as costly-to-fake commitments that build confidence in shared goals.

For Formative Oaxaca specifically, they cite Chisholm (1968) and Halstead and Jones (1989) that farmers experience diminishing returns once a settlement's agricultural catchment exceeds ~2 km radius, and a calculation (Blanton 2004:213, using Kowalewski 1982:153 consumption estimates) that given dry-farmed maize productivity (~200 kg/person/year, max 2 ha/household), the maximum efficiently supportable community size without irrigation is roughly **700 persons**. Under defensive-nucleation conditions this catchment tolerance shrinks further, as cultivators reduce house-to-field distance to limit raid/theft risk (Kowalewski 2013).
