# Race (anthropological construct)

## Definition as Coined

**Race** entered anthropology from natural history as a scheme for partitioning humanity into hereditary bodily types (e.g., Blumenbach's five races; later finer European subtype systems). In the Victorian and early-twentieth-century race-science tradition, race was treated as a **stable hereditary essence** that explained mental capacity, cultural achievement, and national destiny. [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]], in *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* (1911/1938), argued that this common concept of **racial type** is logically and biologically misleading: populations are variable complexes of genetic lines; bodily form is environmentally unstable; and **no close connection between race and personality has been established**. All definitional moves below distinguish **period race-science usage** (attributed to its holders) from **Boas's critical redefinition** (attributed to Boas).

## Semantic History

| Phase | Sense |
|---|---|
| 18th–19th c. typology | Discrete races as natural kinds; often ranked |
| Polygenism vs monogenism | Separate creations vs single origin — both often racialist in practice |
| Darwin *Descent* 1871 | Races as sub-species of one stock; external differences via [[sexual-selection]]; period hierarchy language retained — see [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]] |
| Gobineau / Chamberlain / Grant | Race as destiny of civilization |
| Boas 1911/1938 | Type as statistical fiction; independence of race, language, culture |
| Postwar physical anthropology | Clines, populations, rejection of typological race (ongoing; date-stamp later science) |
| Cavalli-Sforza 2000 | Within-pop variation ~85% of worldwide pairwise variation; abandon traditional race classification; climate-driven surface traits; practical ancestry ≠ race typology — see [[race-and-genomics-debate]], [[cavalli-sforza-genes-peoples-languages-2000]] |
| Sociological race | Social classification and racism as social facts (adjacent; not identical to biological typology) |

## Operationalizations

Race-science operationalized race by anthropometry (cranial index, stature, skin, hair) and by assigning cultural "levels" to types. Boas operationalized **critique** by: measuring environmental plasticity (immigrant studies tradition; growth); analyzing family-line vs fraternal variability; showing mismatch of racial, linguistic, and cultural maps; reviewing mental-test and crime statistics for environmental confounds. Modern genetics (as of later literature) re-describes human variation in population-genetic terms — do not silently equate Boas's 1938 biology with post-2010 aDNA or GWAS results.

## Applications

Central object of *Mind of Primitive Man* Chs. 2–8 and 13; pivot of [[race-and-culture-debate]]; load-bearing for [[historical-particularism]] and for the wiki's four-way non-identity rule (archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ people).

## Critiques and Limitations

Boas's environmental and statistical arguments weakened typological race and race–culture equations in anthropology; later work both strengthened anti-typological conclusions (population genetics) and reopened trait-heritability debates under different designs. Contested: whether any biological clustering of humans deserves the name "race"; how social race relates to ancestry; misuse of both race-science and anti-race-science in politics. Record positions on [[race-and-culture-debate]]; do not assert a single wiki-voice settlement of live scientific disputes beyond documented convergence.

## Boas's Redefinition in *Mind* (1938)

Boas's critical program on race, as stated in the Preface and developed in Chs. 3–8 and 13:

| Boas thesis (attributed) | Chapter locus |
|---|---|
| Racial type concept misleading; needs logical and biological redefinition | Preface |
| Race–personality link never established | Preface |
| Populations are complexes of family lines, not pure types | Chs. 3–4 |
| Bodily form environmentally unstable | Ch. 5 |
| "Higher"/"lower" traits inconsistently distributed; brain arguments fail | Ch. 6 |
| Mental and social functions highly variable with conditions | Ch. 7 |
| Type, language, culture not permanently connected | Ch. 8 |
| No pure European races; national mental characters not hereditary essences | Ch. 13 |
| Evaluate individuals, not class-types | Ch. 13 close |

**Important hedges Boas himself makes:** he does not claim that no average biological differences of any kind could ever be found; he claims the crude race–culture and race–mind equations are unproved and that opportunity blocks fair tests. Record hedges; do not harden Boas into either later "race is biological destiny" or "biology is silent" slogans.

Date-stamp: biological claims are **as of Boas 1938**. Later population genetics and behavioral genetics are separate positions on [[race-and-culture-debate]].

**In the primary voice (*[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]*, 1940).** Boas's own gloss in the preface uses "race"/"racial" to mean "the assembly of genetic lines represented in a population" — a statistical population of variable family lines, not a discrete hereditary type. The empirical spine of the critique is the [[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study|immigrant head-form study]] (1910–1912): if the cephalic index — the era's most trusted racial marker — is itself environmentally plastic, then the whole apparatus of typological classification is unreliable. Boas coined the **genetic type vs. physiological type (ecotype)** distinction there, and explicitly limited it (pigmentation showed *no* plasticity). See [[anthropometry]], [[nature-nurture-debate]].

## The genomic era: ancestry vs. race (Reich 2018)

The Boasian and postwar critiques above rested largely on mid-20th-century
biology. [[reich-who-we-are-2018|Reich (2018)]] argues (attributed) that the
[[ancient-dna|ancient-DNA]] revolution both **strengthens** the anti-typological
case and **complicates** the strong "biology is silent on difference" reading. On
the strengthening side: present populations are recent products of repeated
[[admixture|mixture]], so the "serial founder" picture of long-isolated, quasi-
primeval races (Feldman 2002) is, on Reich's account, "untenable" — no population
is pure, and today's fault lines are only a few thousand years old. On the
complicating side: Reich contends the consensus that average biological
differences are too modest to study has hardened into an indefensible orthodoxy,
and that "ancestry" is a distinct, more precise vocabulary than "race" (a claim
disputed by e.g. Duana Fullwiley). Reich's related normative thesis — that science
should expect average differences in complex traits, including cognition — is
**contested and recorded, not settled**: see [[race-and-genomics-debate]] for the
full spread of positions (social-construction, ancestry-realism, hereditarian
stereotype claims, and precautionary restriction). Register discipline: keep
"ancestry"/"admixture" findings attributed and date-stamped (as of Reich 2018);
never let the ancestry vocabulary be silently re-read as biological race. See also
[[archaeogenetics]], [[genetic-ancestry-testing]], and the four-way non-identity
rule (archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ people).

## Racialism vs. racism in American archaeology (Echo-Hawk & Zimmerman 2010)

*(Attributed — Roger Echo-Hawk & Larry Zimmerman, "Beyond Racism," in
*[[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]]* 2010.)*
Writing from within archaeology, the authors draw a load-bearing distinction: **racialism** is
"the cultural idea that humankind is composed of racial groups that are biologically distinct" — a
"faith-based belief" they hold "the vast majority of Americans" share; **racism** is its extension
into action by "preferentially ranking the groups" (they further split *unilateral* from *conjoint*
racism). Their sharp claim is that even identity-affirming categories — "Native American
prehistory," Indian-studies programs — "practice and perpetuate racialism," since race has no
biological basis (traits vary as **clines** that cut across supposed groupings). They trace the
operationalization of race through phrenology, Morton's craniometry, and the **Moundbuilder myth**
(a lost white race destroyed by "savage" Indians, rationalizing dispossession), and read the
**Kennewick / Ancient One** case — the "Caucasoid → Caucasian" media slippage, the scientists'
NAGPRA lawsuit — as making "the racialism of American archaeology" visible. They record, without
endorsing, the counter-positions of Vine Deloria Jr. and Elizabeth Cook-Lynn (Indian-primacy
arguments they classify as "using racism to defeat racism"), and note their own complicity. This is
charged discourse reproduced for analysis; see [[indigenous-archaeology]],
[[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[repatriation-of-anthropology]].

## Victorian usage (Stocking 1987)

[[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking's]] *Victorian Anthropology* documents the prehistory of the Boasian critique. He stresses (attributed) that in mid-Victorian usage "race" carried much of the meaning later assigned to [[culture]] — "a summation of historically accumulated moral differences sustained and slowly modified from generation to generation" — because the widely accepted (semi-Lamarckian) inheritance of acquired characteristics made cultural habit readily convertible into hereditary "racial" tendency. On this view, Stocking argues, the analytic distinction between "racial" and "cultural" determinism largely collapsed for the Victorian evolutionists: [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] could speak of the "Italian race," and his ordering of peoples by cultural rank (Australian → Tahitian → Aztec → Chinese → Italian) doubled as an ordering by skin-colour saturation. Classical evolutionism, Stocking holds, preserved a doctrine of [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] while incorporating substantial racialism by relocating racial mental difference into evolutionary *time* rather than treating races as aboriginally distinct (as [[polygenism]] did) — but this move reduced the significance of cultural variety by treating it as graded distance from a single civilizational standard. Evolutionary racialism thereby gave later colonialism a more secular rationale than earlier polygenist or missionary framings: it "offered a secular explanation of how [the racial] hierarchy had arisen." See [[degenerationism]], [[social-darwinism]], [[victorian-ethnology-anthropology-schism-debate]].

## Race, culture, and the Lamarckian blur (Stocking 1968)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race,
Culture, and Evolution]]*, essays 3, 6, 10.)* Stocking's account of how "race" functioned in
American social science c. 1890–1915 turns on [[lamarckian-inheritance|Lamarckian
inheritance]]: because acquired habits were assumed heritable, "race" and "civilization"
were routinely interchanged — Henry Cabot Lodge's 1896 "race" ("ideas, traditions,
sentiments... an unconscious inheritance") is what would now be called *culture*, but
conceived as hereditary. Stocking's central historiographical device is that once
Lamarckianism lost biological legitimacy (Weismann → Mendel; and [[kroeber-alfred-l|Kroeber]]'s
1917 "Superorganic"), turning "race" into "culture" required only "the substitution of a
word": "for 'race' read 'culture' or 'civilization,' for 'racial heredity' read 'cultural
heritage,' and the change had taken place." He also documents (essay 3) the **persistence of
[[polygenism|polygenist]] thought after Darwin** under new evolutionary vocabulary, and
(essay 6) the fusion of Darwinian evolution, evolutionary ethnology, and polygenist race
into a single "raciocultural hierarchy" topped by "large-brained white men." Stocking's own
view is that "race" as an analytic category was "a characteristically nineteenth-century
phenomenon" whose intellectual framework was permanently altered by the Boasian turn — while
noting that racism itself persisted. Charged period claims (Bean's 1906 brain study, Ripley
and Bean on Jewish physiology) are recorded as documented positions, not findings.

## Darwin, *Descent of Man* (1871)

Primary source: [[darwin-descent-of-man-1871]]. Darwin applied ordinary natural-history species criteria to human races and concluded they should **not** rank as distinct species: they fuse when coexisting, every “racial” character is highly variable, and they graduate into each other (endless classifier disagreement from 2 to 63 races). Preferred rank: **sub-species**; evolutionary [[monogenism]] — once evolution is accepted, the monogenist–[[polygenism|polygenist]] dispute “will die a silent and unobserved death.” Mental similarities across races (Tylor, Lubbock) and numerous unimportant structural agreements argue common descent. External differences (colour, hair, features) are, he argued, not explained by climate, use/disuse, correlation, or [[natural-selection]] on utility; residual agency is [[sexual-selection]] by race-relative beauty standards (developed in Part II and ch. XIX–XX). He also described extinction of races chiefly as competition of tribe with tribe and race with race under colonial contact — period natural-history language recorded as **attributed position**, not wiki-voice justification. Victorian racial typology and hierarchical vocabulary throughout *Descent* remain period constructs.
