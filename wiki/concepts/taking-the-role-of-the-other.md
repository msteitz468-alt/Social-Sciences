# Taking the Role of the Other

## Definition as Coined

For [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]], **taking the role (or attitude) of the other** is the capacity of an individual to arouse in himself the response he is calling out in another — "a taking of the rôle of the other, a tendency to act as the other person acts. One participates in the same process the other person is carrying out and controls his action with reference to that participation." It is, Mead says, "not merely one of the various aspects or expressions of intelligence... but is the very essence of its character" — he goes so far as to reinterpret Spearman's psychometric "X factor" of general intelligence as "simply... this ability of the intelligent individual to take the attitude of the other" (attributed).

Role-taking is what distinguishes reflective (human) from animal intelligence: "the animal does not put himself in the place of the other person and say, in effect, 'He will act in such a way and I will act in this way.'"

## Semantic History

| Context | Sense |
|---|---|
| English moral sentimentalists (Hume, Adam Smith) | "Sympathy" / imaginative projection into another — a possible antecedent Morris notes |
| Mead, 1925/1934 | The behavioral mechanism generating meaning, self-consciousness, and reflective intelligence |
| [[symbolic-interactionism]] / role theory | Generalized into "role-taking" as a basic social-psychological process |

Morris suggests Mead "might well have been influenced by the English associational school" — Hume's sympathy, Smith's moral sentiments — in framing role-taking, though Mead recasts the mechanism in behavioral rather than affective terms.

## Operationalizations

Not measured directly. Developmentally, Mead locates its first form in the [[play-and-the-game|play stage]] — the child "has a set of stimuli which call out in himself the sort of responses they call out in others... the simplest form of being another to one's self" — and its organized form in the game and the [[generalized-other|generalized other]].

## Applications

- The mechanism of the [[significant-symbol]] (the gesture that arouses the other's response in oneself) and of **meaning**, which arises "through the individual stimulating himself to take the attitude of the other."
- The genesis of the [[the-social-self|self]]: one becomes an object to oneself by taking others' attitudes toward oneself.
- The source of **universality**: occupying another's perspective on the same object, and one's own, yields "that which can be in different perspectives" — the universal in the form of the response.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Mead's identification of role-taking with Spearman's "g" is a strong, dated claim bridging his social psychology and early psychometrics; it should be read as attributed, not endorsed.
- Whether role-taking suffices to explain moral or empathic understanding (vs. merely predictive perspective-taking) is a continuing question in social psychology.
- Editorially reconstructed text (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]).
