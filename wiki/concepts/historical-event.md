# Historical Event

## Definition as Coined

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, "Historical Events as Transformations of Structures: Inventing Revolution at the Bastille") defines a historical event as "(1) a ramified sequence of occurrences that (2) is recognized as notable by contemporaries, and that (3) results in a durable transformation of structures." Events begin with "a rupture of some kind — a surprising break with routine practice"; most ruptures are neutralized (repressed, ignored, explained away), and an occurrence becomes an event "only when it touches off a chain of occurrences that durably transforms previous structures and practices." Sewell holds that "lumpiness, rather than smoothness, is the normal texture of historical temporality."

This is a substantive theory of the event, distinct from Andrew Abbott's methodological [[event-occurrence-distinction|event/occurrence distinction]] (events as abstractions, occurrences as their indicators): Sewell's "occurrences" are the actual happenings whose ramified sequence *constitutes* an event.

## Semantic History

The concept crystallizes a strand running from the Annales debate over *histoire événementielle* through [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s theory of the event; Sewell generalizes Sahlins's cultural account by adding [[transposable-schemas|resources alongside schemas]] and a plural conception of structure. It belongs to the broader turn toward [[eventful-temporality|eventful temporality]] and [[eventful-sociology]].

## Sewell's General Propositions

From the Bastille essay (each attributed to Sewell):

- **Events rearticulate structures** — they are transformative rearticulations, above all when a rupture in one structural/spatial location produces reinforcing ruptures in others.
- **Events are cultural transformations** — acts of signification in which terms take on new authoritative meanings.
- **Events are shaped by particular conditions** — the [[structure-of-the-conjuncture|structure of the conjuncture]] (Sahlins's term): the local meanings, accidents, and forces shaping an outcome.
- **Events are characterized by heightened emotion** — signaling and driving structural dislocation; Sewell invokes Durkheim's "collective effervescence."
- **Events are acts of collective creativity** — dislocation produces the insecurity that is a condition of collective improvisation.
- **Events are punctuated by ritual** — spontaneous rituals realign participants to sources of power (Catherine Bell's definition of ritual).
- **Events produce more events** — because structures are multiple and overlapping.
- **Rearticulations must gain authoritative sanction** to become durable — usually ratification at a power node (often the state) of adequate scope.
- **Events are spatial as well as temporal** — depending on spatial concentration and the uneven spatial scope of the structures ruptured.
- **Bounding an event requires judgment** — events have a "fractal" character (sub-events within larger events).

## Operationalizations / Applications

The taking of the Bastille (July 12–23, 1789), a twelve-day event, is Sewell's demonstration: the crisis fused two previously separate things — the political theory of popular sovereignty (invoked by the Third Estate's National Assembly) and acts of crowd violence (July 14) — so that, over debates culminating July 20–23, the modern concept of **"revolution"** (a change of regime achieved by legitimate popular violence) was invented, where "revolution" had previously meant only "a great change in the affairs of a state." The event cascaded into the Great Fear and the abolition of feudalism on the Night of August 4. (Historical narrative is Sewell's attributed analysis, drawing on Godechot and others, not wiki-voice fact.)

## Critiques and Limitations

Distinguishing an "event" from a structure-reproducing "incident" is, Sewell concedes, a matter of degree and practical judgment — a difficulty he argues is eased (not removed) by treating structures as plural rather than singular. The rational-choice objection and Sewell's semiotic-plus-charismatic answer are recorded under [[eventful-sociology]] and [[structure-of-the-conjuncture]].
