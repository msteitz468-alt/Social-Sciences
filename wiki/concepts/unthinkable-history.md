# Unthinkable History

## Definition as Coined

[[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] (*[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]*, Ch.3 “An Unthinkable History”) applies [[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]’s **unthinkable** — that for which one has no adequate instruments to conceptualize — to the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]].

> “The Haitian Revolution thus entered history with the peculiar characteristic of being unthinkable even as it happened.” (Trouillot 1995)

> “In the unthinkable of an epoch, there is all that one cannot think for lack of the ethical or political dispositions which tend to make it what it is…, but also that which one cannot think for lack of instruments of thought such as problematics, concepts, methods, techniques.” (Bourdieu, as cited by Trouillot)

Trouillot’s load-bearing move (attributed): unthinkability is not only later forgetting. Contemporaries (planters, managers, many European observers) lacked categories in which a **successful slave revolution founding a modern black state** could appear as possible. La Barre (1790) writing that “a revolt among them is impossible” / “Freedom for Negroes is a chimera” is emblematic. When reality contradicts belief, people “devise formulas to repress the unthinkable and to bring it back within the realm of accepted discourse.”

Methodological question: “How does one write a history of the impossible?” If events cannot be accepted as they occur, later assessment is compromised — can narratives convey plots unthinkable in their own world?

## Semantic History

- **Bourdieu:** unthinkable as dispositional and instrument lack (not Trouillot’s invention).
- **Trouillot 1995:** specializes to Haitian Revolution + Western ontology of race/slavery; tracks continuity into modern historiography.
- **Path unthinkable → non-event:** after 1804, ostracism and Haiti’s decline made the revolution seem an “improbability… for which no one had a rational explanation.”
- **Debate page:** [[unthinkable-haitian-revolution-debate]]

Trouillot stresses the issue is **epistemological/methodological** more than mere ideological bias of post-1940s specialists: how far has Western discourse on slavery, race, and colonization broken the “iron bonds” of the philosophical milieu in which it was born?

## Operationalizations

1. Reconstruct the era’s **range of alternatives** (what counts as possible human action).
2. Test whether the event fits those alternatives without residual “impossibility.”
3. Track [[formulas-of-silence|erasure and banalization]] after the fact.
4. Distinguish unthinkability from sparse sources (Sans Souci) and from invented consensus under abundance (Columbus).

## Applications

- Haitian Revolution as non-event in Age of Revolutions corpora (Trouillot’s examples include generalist silence and specialist recycling of planter tropes).
- Ontology of degrees of humanity / scientific racism already in Enlightenment landscapes (Trouillot, attributed — dating racism only to the nineteenth century is, he argues, wrong).
- Structural analogy (formal, not identity) to Holocaust and American-slavery silences: erasure of fact/relevance vs heap of trivializing details.

## Critiques and Limitations

`contested: yes` as applied claim about the Haitian Revolution (see debate page). Risks:

- Overstating Western unanimity; understating Black Atlantic and Haitian thought that *did* conceptualize the revolution.
- Metaphor inflation (“unthinkable” for any surprise).
- Trouillot himself centers Western frames while noting Haitian epic historiography’s own limits (two unfortunate tendencies: Haitian epic vs foreign neo-eighteenth-century discourse).
