# Multi-Power-Actor Civilization

## Definition as Coined

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP Vol. I]]*, 1986) contrasts **multi-power-actor civilizations** — fields of plural states or power actors inside a broader cultural-ideological unit — with centralized **[[empire-of-domination|empires of domination]]** and later **territorial empires** (e.g. Rome in his narrative). Shared ideology, script, markets, and diplomatic norms can integrate more extensively than any single polity. In the European conclusions he treats competitive multi-power-actor civilizations as **one of the two main sources of social-power development** (alongside, historically, the empire-of-domination path):

> "competitive 'multi-power-actor civilizations' have been one of the two main sources of social-power development."

## Semantic History

Analytical polarity inside Mann's narrative and Ch. 1 geopolitical dual (hegemonic empire over clients vs multistate civilization), not a standard archaeological culture name. Related to city-state systems and international-society concepts but tied to [[iemp-model|IEMP]] [[power-networks]] overlap and [[normative-pacification]]. Alternation multi-power-actor ↔ empire of domination is a macrohistorical dialectic in his Near Eastern–Mediterranean story, not a simple cycle.

## Operationalizations

Plural autonomous polities/federations + shared pantheon/script/art/diplomacy or religious ecumene; competitive geopolitics within a civilizational field; markets and [[diffused-power]]; absence of single hegemonic mode of IEMP power; often intensive local organization with limited extensive coercive integration.

## Applications

Attributed to Mann in *SSP* Vol. I:

- **Mesopotamia (Chs. 3–4)** — Dual small intense city-states within segmental multistate civilization; comparative pristine cases mostly federal rather than Egyptian-style unitary cages.
- **Iron Age Near East–Mediterranean (Chs. 6–7)** — Classic multi-power-actor arena: Phoenicia and Greece as decentralized multi-power fields (markets, interstitial emergence on imperial marches, Greek intensive democratic polis; no central [[compulsory-cooperation]] redistribution). Athens' imperialism threatened Near Eastern hegemonic-empire pattern but was blocked by multistate resilience and triple identity (polis / Hellas / humanity).
- **Geopolitical types (Ch. 1, 8)** — Europe’s multistate diplomatic civilization humbled successive hegemonic pretensions; Assyria/Persia as revitalized empire-of-domination pole opposite Greece.
- **Medieval–early modern Europe (Chs. 12–15)** — Acephalous federation of villages, manors, small states under Christian [[normative-pacification]]; diversity + competition + pacification → "rational restlessness." Military Revolution finished small city-states and ordered a more equal multistate system (land as zero-sum stalemate). By mid-18th c.: multistate capitalist civilization whose major independent actors are individual property owners and **class-nations** ([[organic-national-state]]).

## Critiques and Limitations

Ideal-typical; many cases mix multi-actor and imperial phases; cultural "unit" boundaries are constructs. Mann's elevation of competitive multi-power systems as a general developmental source is his comparative thesis — not independent consensus.
