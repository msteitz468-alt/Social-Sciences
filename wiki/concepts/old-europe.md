# Old Europe

## Definition as Coined

**Old Europe**, as coined and developed by [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]], names Neolithic and Copper Age Europe **before** what she identifies as Indo-Europeanization — roughly the 7th to mid-4th millennia BCE in east-central Europe (later persistence in the west and on islands). In *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* (1991) she defines it as the peoples of Europe from the 7th to the 3rd millennia B.C. “referring to Neolithic Europe before the Indo-Europeans,” characterized by settled agriculture, population growth, sophisticated art, and “a complex symbolic system formulated around the worship of the Goddess in her various aspects” (Preface; attributed).

The construct is **etic** and program-defining: it organizes a pan-European synthesis, not a self-name of prehistoric peoples. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people: Gimbutas correlates archaeological assemblages, a non-IE speech world, and a Goddess religion — those equations are **her claims**.

[[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] (2007) adopts the label for Danubian farming towns as Europe’s most technologically advanced cultures ~6000–4000 BCE while **rejecting** the full goddess/invasion package — so “Old Europe” as agricultural culture-area and “Old Europe” as peaceful Goddess civilization are related but separable uses (see [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]] Position C).

## Semantic History

- **Gimbutas 1970s–1991:** full package — culture area + religion + social structure + Kurgan collision (*Goddesses and Gods of Old Europe*; *Language of the Goddess*; *Civilization of the Goddess*).
- **Goddess-movement reception:** popular books cast Old Europe as utopian matristic past destroyed by patriarchal invaders (reception on [[gimbutas-marija]], not wiki voice).
- **Anthony / Mallory steppe refinement:** retain agricultural Old Europe as non-IE farmer world facing steppe frontiers; narrow Kurgan culture; drop or quarantine goddess-patriarchy utopia framing.
- **Processual / feminist archaeological critique:** challenge universal Goddess readings, endemic-peace claims, and simple matriarchy; reanalyze figurines and “temples” contextually ([[mother-goddess]], [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]] Position B).

## Operationalizations (as of Gimbutas 1991)

Gimbutas operationalizes Old Europe through:

1. **Chronology** — radiocarbon calibrated with tree-ring counting (B.C. = calibrated; b.c. = uncalibrated); culture-group tables in appendices (as of early 1990s).
2. **Culture-group inventory** — Sesklo, Starčevo–Körös–Criş, Karanovo–Gumelniţa, [[vinca-culture|Vinča]], [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]], [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture|Cucuteni–Tripolye]], Lengyel, Tisza, TRB, Cardial/Impressed Ware, western megalithic sequences, etc., treated as regional styles within one civilizational field.
3. **Material “civilization” criteria** — towns with population concentration, multi-story temples, sacred script, 4–5 room houses, professional ceramicists/weavers/metallurgists, long-distance trade (obsidian, shells, marble, copper, salt) — her 5th–early 4th millennium east-central inventory (Preface; attributed).
4. **Religion** — Goddess typology (life / death / regeneration / subordinate male forms); temple models and figurine contexts; continuity from Upper Paleolithic symbolism (*Language of the Goddess* cross-ref).
5. **Social structure** — absence of IE-type chiefdom diagnostics (no male royal tombs, no hill-fort megarons in her sample); matrilineal/matristic reading of burial and settlement; **gylany** (Eisler) preferred over “matriarchy” (Ch.9; attributed).
6. **Contrast table** — Old European vs Indo-European symbolic/social structures at the end of Ch.10 (dual religions after collision).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Four-way non-identity:** archaeological cultures ≠ languages ≠ genetic populations ≠ peoples.
- **Religion and gender:** Goddess typology and gynocentric social structure are interpretive; contested by later figurine studies and gender archaeology.
- **Peace claim:** she cites absence of depicted warfare and scarcity of specialized weapons until ~4500–4300 BCE; critics note sampling, taphonomy, and ranking evidence (e.g. [[varna]]).
- **Chronology:** 1991 calibrated dates are of-their-time; always date-stamp; prefer current regional syntheses for absolute chronology.
- **Civilization redefinition:** she explicitly rejects androcratic warrior criteria for “civilization” — a normative-theoretical move (Preface), not a neutral descriptive finding.
- **Script:** Vinča/“Old European” signs as true writing remains highly contested (see [[old-european-script]]).
