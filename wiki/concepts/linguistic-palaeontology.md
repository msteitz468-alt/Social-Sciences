# Linguistic Palaeontology

## Definition as Coined

**Linguistic palaeontology** reconstructs aspects of a prehistoric speech community's environment, economy, technology, and social institutions from the **shared reconstructed vocabulary** of a language family. It is the cultural-historical use of results from [[historical-linguistics|comparative historical linguistics]]: where the comparative method recovers phonology and morphology, linguistic palaeontology asks what those recovered words imply about the world their speakers lived in.

Classic Indo-European examples include words for snow, temperate flora and fauna, honey and bees, sheep and wool, and wheeled vehicles (wheel, axle, thill, wagon) — used to constrain homeland geography and absolute chronology. In *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* (2007), [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] treats the reconstructed PIE lexicon as "a window onto the environment, social life, and beliefs of the speakers of Proto-Indo-European," while insisting that lexical evidence alone cannot settle every question (e.g. horse domestication).

## Semantic History

Nineteenth-century homeland hunting made linguistic palaeontology the dominant tool for locating the Indo-European "mother tongue." Specific roots were pressed hard: *lókʷs* (once "salmon") was taken to pin a northern homeland; *bhā́g-* ("beech") to place speakers west of the copper-beech limit in Poland. Anthony (2007) reports that those classic pins no longer hold: *lókʷs* is now usually glossed only as "trout-like fish," present across northern Eurasia including Black/Caspian drainages; beech cognates vary (oak, elder) and *Fagus orientalis* grows in the Caucasus.

[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]] (1987) heavily criticized free-form linguistic palaeontology as **circular**: archaeological cultures are chosen to match the lexicon and then used to "confirm" it. Anthony defends a **constrained** use. He accepts that reconstructed PIE is fragmentary, homogenized, and multi-period (like a dictionary), but holds that absolute certainty is not the standard of secular inquiry, and that some vocabulary items function like a dictionary's newest words — dating the reconstructable stage rather than floating timelessly. His central dating move: wagon terminology, like "hard disk" after 1978, dates late PIE after independent archaeological invention of wheeled vehicles (~after 4000 BCE, likely closer to 3500 BCE).

## Operationalizations

Anthony (2007) restates three rules for assigning meaning to reconstructions:

1. Prefer the most ancient attested meanings.
2. Prefer meanings consistent across all branches that preserve the root.
3. Confirm when the word decomposes into roots that independently point to the same meaning (e.g. *kʷtom* 'hundred' related to *dekʷ* 'ten' → decimal system).

Reliability thresholds from the comparative method apply: cognates in at least three branches, preferably including an ancient branch (Anatolian, Greek, Avestan, Old Indic, Latin, some Celtic), should produce a usable reconstruction; multi-branch roots like *kʷtom* are stronger. Clusters of related meanings, not single roots, should ground culture reconstructions. Anthony notes that at least ~1,500 unique PIE roots have been reconstructed, many appearing in multiple terms, so total reconstructed vocabulary exceeds that count — yet much of the real lexicon will never be recovered ("door" yes, "wall" no).

**Positive termini.** Anthony treats two vocabulary sets as birth-date constraints for late PIE:

- **Wool:** sheep vocabulary (sheep, ewe, ram, lamb) plus *HwlHn-* 'wool' with cognates almost all branches including Hittite — archaic PIE before Anatolian split. But meaning uncertainty (woven textile vs short natural wool/felt) **weakens** wool as a terminus.
- **Wheeled vehicles (stronger):** five-term semantic field — *kwékwlos* 'wheel' (from *kwel-* 'to turn' — "the thing that turns"; eight-language cognacy), second wheel root *rot-eh₂-*, *aks-/*h₂eks-* 'axle', *h₂ih₃s-* 'thill', *wégheti* 'to convey in a vehicle' (plus *-no-* wagon nouns). Most are late PIE o-stems; almost all derived from IE roots (not loans). Anthony argues these cannot be post-PIE inter-branch loans (wrong sound patterns) and rejects independent *kwel→wheel* re-creation in each branch. Wheeled vehicles were not invented before 4000 BCE; surviving evidence clusters ~3400–3000 BCE (e.g. Bronocice TRB mug ~3500–3350 BCE as oldest well-dated image; Uruk IVa pictograph ~3300–3100 BCE).

**Negative vocabulary.** PIE lacks reconstructable roots for spoke, iron, cotton, chariot, glass, coffee — items invented after daughter dispersal — but Anthony cautions that negative evidence is weaker because many real PIE words will never be reconstructed.

**Ecology and economy (as of Anthony 2007).** Temperate-zone flora/fauna (birch, otter, beaver, lynx, bear, horse) — not Mediterranean (no cypress, olive, laurel) and not tropical. Honeybee and *medhu-* 'mead' reconstructions exclude Siberia and Kazakh steppes (honeybees not native east of Urals). Solid *h₁éḱwos* 'horse' removes Near East, Iran, and Indian subcontinent as primary homeland candidates for the 4500–2500 BCE window (horses rare/absent there; numerous only on Eurasian steppes) while leaving temperate Europe west of Urals, Anatolia, and Caucasus still open on this criterion alone. Economic package: farmers and stockbreeders (bull, cow, ox, ram, ewe, lamb, pig, piglet; dairy terms; dog; wool shearing/weaving; ard and yoke; grain/chaff/furrow; *peḱu-* movable wealth). **Social vocabulary:** household, clan, chief (*weik-potis*), *re-* (later 'king' sense; Anthony suggests possible original 'regulator'), tribe; reciprocal gift/sacrifice with sky gods and praise poetry as chiefdom-compatible ideology. **Horse caveat:** speakers certainly knew the horse; lexical evidence alone is insufficient to prove domestication.

## Applications

- [[indo-european-languages]] homeland chronology and branch-order arguments on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]
- **Mallory 1989 (now primary):** Ch.4 reconstructs PIE environment (temperate trees, river/lake orientation; *mori problematic), economy (stockbreeding with limited agriculture; *owis, *ekʷos, pig *su/*porko, plough), settlement/technology (house, *ayos metal, wagons, weapons), social organization (patrilineal/patriarchal, *teutā, disputed *rēǵ-); Ch.5 religion (*dyēus ph₂tēr, [[tripartition]], horse sacrifice). Ch.6 uses palaeontology for homeland: cultural vocabulary time-depth ~4th millennium BC (bounds ~5000–2500 BC); beech argument limited; salmon (Diebold) can support Pontic-Caspian; horse “on balance” steppe/forest-steppe; wheeled vehicles 4th millennium Europe. Source: [[mallory-in-search-of-the-indo-europeans-1989]].
- Anthony's wool/wheel *terminus post quem* for late PIE after ~4000–3500 BCE (core of his refinement of the [[kurgan-hypothesis]])
- Rejection of Renfrew's first-farming IE dispersal as incompatible with wagon vocabulary (Anthony) and with Mallory’s multi-criterion stack
- Social-institutional reconstructions feeding [[elite-recruitment]] (oath *h1óitos*, guest-host *ghos-ti-*, patron-client sacrifice)
- Ecological exclusions (bees, horse, temperate package) that narrow homeland candidates before archaeology is mapped

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Circularity** (Renfrew 1987): matching lexicon to a preferred culture and treating the match as proof. Mallory (1989) still uses palaeontology as one **constraint among several**, not sole proof.
- **Semantic drift and loans:** ecological terms may reflect expansion zones, not origin; technology words may be borrowed; Anatolian wheel vocabulary is itself doubtful (hurki- / hišša-), so archaic PIE as preserved in Anatolian may predate wagons.
- **Absence ≠ absence:** unrecovered words and weak negative evidence.
- **Dictionary effect:** reconstructions homogenize multi-period speech; over-precision about a single "PIE culture" is illicit.
- **Four-way non-identity:** reconstructed lexicon constrains a speech community's world; it does not identify an archaeological culture, genetic population, or self-conscious people. Anthony's own applications keep association attributed.

Renfrew 1987, Mallory 1989, and Anthony 2007 are the vault's principal evaluations of the method's yield for the IE problem — all essential and attributed.
