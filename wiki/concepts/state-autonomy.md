# State Autonomy (Potential Autonomy of the State)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979), [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]] argues (attributed) that explaining revolutions requires conceiving of states as "administrative and coercive organizations... potentially autonomous from (though of course conditioned by) socioeconomic interests and structures." Her definition of the state: "a set of administrative, policing, and military organizations headed, and more or less well coordinated by, an executive authority," which "first and fundamentally extracts resources from society."

Because the state extracts resources and must both maintain domestic order and compete militarily with other states, its organizations "necessarily compete to some extent" with the dominant class over resources and can pursue interests — order, revenue, war-making, survival — that diverge from, and even oppose, dominant-class interests. The state is therefore **"Janus-faced,"** with "an intrinsically dual anchorage in class-divided socioeconomic structures and an international system of states." Skocpol calls this an **"organizational" and "realist"** view of the state: states are real organizations controlling territory and people, not analytic aspects of a mode of production or mere arenas of class conflict.

## Semantic History

- **Lineage.** Skocpol draws the concept from the Weberian–Continental tradition — [[weber-max|Max Weber]] (the state as a compulsory association claiming the monopoly of legitimate force), Otto Hintze (state organization conditioned by both class structure *and* interstate position), and Tocqueville — against the Anglo-American tendency to dissolve the state into "government" or "political system."
- **Against Marxist "[[relative-autonomy-of-the-state|relative autonomy]]."** Skocpol distinguishes her *potential autonomy* from the 1970s Marxist debate (Miliband, Poulantzas, Anderson, Therborn, Offe). She argues (position) that "relative autonomy" theorists still tie the state functionally to reproducing the mode of production, or treat it as "a materialized concentration of... class relations" (Poulantzas) — never as autonomous even against the entire class structure. Her claim is stronger: state managers can act against *all* propertied classes.
- **"Bringing the state back in."** The concept became the banner of a 1980s program (Evans, Rueschemeyer & Skocpol, *Bringing the State Back In*, 1985) reasserting the state as an independent actor in social science.

## Operationalizations

Skocpol treats autonomy as a **variable degree**, not a constant: the pre-revolutionary Russian autocracy was a more genuinely autonomous/bureaucratic state than Bourbon France, where the dominant class was entrenched *within* the state (venal offices, parlements). The degree of dominant-class leverage over the state is precisely what her comparison uses to explain differential old-regime breakdown (see [[france-russia-china-revolutions]]). Related empirical proxies used in the state-autonomy literature: bureaucratic professionalization, fiscal/extractive independence, and insulation of officials' recruitment from propertied elites.

## Applications

- The old-regime breakdown argument: states broke down where international military pressure met dominant-class capacity to block state-strengthening reform.
- The outcome argument: revolutionary state-builders forged new states more autonomous at home and more powerful abroad than the old regimes.
- Beyond revolution: the concept seeds comparative work on state capacity, developmental states, and social-policy formation.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Over-reification.** Critics argue Skocpol treats "the state" as a unitary actor with interests, obscuring internal divisions and the class backgrounds of state personnel.
- **The autonomy/embeddedness tension.** Later state theory (e.g., Evans's "embedded autonomy") argues effective states are *both* autonomous and socially embedded, complicating a pure autonomy claim.
- **Where does autonomy come from?** The concept describes autonomy better than it explains its sources — a standard objection. Contrast the Marxist [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]] and the Weberian account of [[bureaucracy]].
