# Reference Group

## Definition as Coined

In *[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Chapters X–XI, [[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (with the American Soldier / Columbia tradition) developed **reference group** theory: actors orient attitudes and self-appraisals to **groups that serve as frames of reference** — including **non-membership** groups. Distinct from mere membership-group analysis.

## Semantic History

Originated in social psychology; Merton's contribution was sociological structuralization — membership vs non-membership, positive vs negative reference groups, selection among potential reference groups, and linkage to [[relative-deprivation]], [[anticipatory-socialization]], [[role-set]], and [[status-set]].

## Operationalizations

Survey comparisons of satisfaction by relative standing (American Soldier); typology of group properties; structural contexts that shape which groups become reference points.

## Applications

Military morale; class and status attitudes; bureaucracy and professionalism; influence patterns ([[local-cosmopolitan-influentials]]). In [[social-exchange-theory|exchange theory]], [[blau-peter-m|Blau]] uses reference groups to explain how expectations (and hence the value of rewards) are set by others' attainments — his example (via Lipset) is American intellectuals who take European professors as their reference group and are frustrated by the comparison, feeding left-of-center political identification.

## Critiques and Limitations

Proliferation of group types without strong predictive theory; selection problem (which group is the reference group?) often post-hoc; overlap with identity and network concepts in later work.
