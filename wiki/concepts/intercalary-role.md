# Intercalary Role

Analytical concept of the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]] for structurally "in-between" positions caught between two hierarchies or groups. Source: [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]].

## Definition as Coined

Two layers, per Gluckman's 1963 autobiographical account:

1. **Fortes' "intercalary lineages" (1945).** In *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi*, [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] named the (only three) lineages linking the [[tallensi|Tallensi]] Namoo and non-Namoo communities across their dominant cleavage "intercalary lineages"; two were traditional peacemakers, one (Gbizug) belonging "equally to both and wholly to neither."
2. **Gluckman's generalization to roles (1949).** In "The Village Headman in British Central Africa" (with [[mitchell-j-clyde|J. C. Mitchell]] and [[barnes-john|J. A. Barnes]]), Gluckman adapted the term to a class of positions whose incumbents represent a superior hierarchy's authority within a smaller group of which they are also full members: the village headman, and by extension the foreman, shop steward, ward sister, NCO, and school prefect. Gluckman argued the headman's ambivalence arises because "he is the personality in whom the domestic-kinship and the political systems intersect" — entangled in kinship's diffuse moral sanctions while wielding political and legal sanctions over the same people; the chief, by contrast, stands largely outside the internal struggles of the groups he unites.

## Semantic History

- **1937–40 seed:** Gluckman's own Zululand analysis (fieldwork 1936–8; "Chief and Native Commissioner in Modern Zululand", 1949) identified the colonial-era [[zulu|Zulu]] chief as intercalary — a government "servant" under the Native Commissioner, yet expected by his people to oppose government policy and embody traditional values. Gluckman reported the balance of allegiance between chief and magistrate shifted situationally with whichever authority's rulings favored the individual.
- **1948–49 symposium:** the RAI/IAI Village Headman paper (published 1949) made the concept programmatic; other Rhodes-Livingstone researchers applied it (Cunnison on the Luapula Lunda, Turner on the Ndembu, Watson on the Mambwe). Parallel independent analyses: Colson on the Tonga chief; Kuper's *The Uniform of Colour* (1947) on the Swazi chief.
- **Contrast case:** Fallers' *Bantu Bureaucracy* (c. 1956) instead framed the Soga chief's strain as a Weberian tension between patrimonial and bureaucratic authority; Fallers later (1959) acknowledged the stimulus of Gluckman's Zululand analysis. Both framings are recorded as positions, not adjudicated.

## Operationalizations

Look for: dual accountability (upward to a hierarchy, inward to a membership group); structurally scripted ambivalence regardless of incumbent personality; ritual or idiom marking the office's burden (the Ngoni installation idiom "You have killed me," recorded by Barnes, July 1947; the Ila dictum "chieftainship is serfdom" quoted by Gluckman from Smith and Dale).

## Applications

[[zulu|Zulu]] chief vs Native Commissioner · Central African village headman ([[lozi|Lozi]], Yao, Ngoni) · industrial-society analogues (foreman, shop steward) — all attributed to the 1949 essays.

## Critiques and Limitations

Gluckman himself noted (1963 Introduction) that colonial administrators valued the headman as an "invaluable non-commissioned officer of native administration" — the concept was developed inside, and partly for, the colonial administrative frame; see [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]. The Weberian alternative (Fallers) shows the strain can be modeled without the intercalary frame.
