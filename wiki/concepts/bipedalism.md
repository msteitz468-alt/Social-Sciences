# Bipedalism

Habitual upright, two-legged walking — the freeing of the hands from a locomotory
function. *[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]* (Leakey & Lewin, 1977) treats
bipedalism's origin as a central puzzle of human evolution and argues its adoption was
the trigger for a self-reinforcing behavioral cascade leading to tool use and,
eventually, language.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Leakey & Lewin 1977.)* The authors' central thesis in this section is
that "the liberation of the hands from the basic function of locomotion" was the
pivotal adaptation enabling tool manufacture and eventually language, and that "this
behavioral complex, once initiated, fed back on itself" — a positive feedback loop. They
note upright walking is mechanically *less* efficient than quadrupedal locomotion, so
the total behavioral advantage of freed hands must have outweighed this locomotory
cost.

## Semantic History

*(Attributed — multi-factor account per Leakey & Lewin 1977.)* The authors argue no
single cause explains the origin of upright walking, presenting it instead as a
convergence of several factors, with "no single one of these... able to account for it
alone":
- **Predator defense/display** — an upright posture makes a primate look larger to
  potential predators.
- **Surveillance** — an advantage for spotting predators or prey over tall grass.
- **Carrying food** back to conspecifics or a home base (see [[food-sharing]]).
- **Carrying infants** — the authors note that a foot anatomically specialized for
  efficient bipedal walking loses its grasping ability, so infants could no longer
  cling unaided, increasing the need for the hands to carry them.
- **Tool use and tool-carrying.**

Two named positions on the primary driver are recorded:
- **[[washburn-sherwood|Sherwood Washburn]]**, "an American primatologist," is credited
  with the hypothesis that tool use was the major force driving hominins to
  bipedalism — "the use of tools was the major force in causing the hominids... to take
  to their hind legs."
- **[[darwin-charles|Charles Darwin]]**, quoted from *The Descent of Man*, proposed that
  canine-tooth reduction in early male hominins followed from the adoption of
  tools/weapons (stones, clubs) that replaced teeth as fighting implements: "The early
  male forefathers of man were probably furnished with great canine teeth, but as they
  gradually acquired the habit of using stones, clubs, and other weapons..." Leakey &
  Lewin state Darwin's proposed *causal order* is reversed from what they believe to be
  correct — in their reading, small canines preceded rather than followed tool use —
  but they credit Darwin's "perspicacity" in placing canine reduction early in
  evolution, decades before fossil evidence (Ramapithecus's small canine teeth)
  confirmed the timing as "still nothing short of remarkable."

The authors' own synthesis folds bipedalism's freed hands into their broader thesis
that social cooperation, not aggression, was "the key factor in the successful
evolution of *Homo sapiens*," including cooperative defense and food sharing as
socially cohesive behaviors enabled by upright posture.

## Operationalizations

The source's principal anatomical operationalization compares fossil pelvic, femoral,
and foot-bone morphology against the modern human striding gait. *Homo habilis*/skull
1470-associated leg and pelvis remains are described as permitting "an easy striding
gait in the same way as modern humans," while australopithecine thigh bones — showing a
smaller femoral-head "ball" and a longer, flatter "neck" than in *Homo* — are read as
less efficient for habitual bipedal walking, though the authors state the anatomical
difference is "certainly not dramatic." Australopithecines are nonetheless
characterized as "quite definitely habitual upright walkers," not knuckle-walkers.

## Applications

Bipedalism is treated as the enabling precondition for the [[food-sharing]] economy
(freed hands to carry food and, per the "carrier bag" hypothesis, to transport it in
containers) and, ultimately, for tool manufacture and cooperative behavior more broadly.
It anchors the authors' account of the [[food-sharing-hypothesis]] as an alternative to
tool-use-centered or hunting-centered ([[hunting-hypothesis]]) narratives of
hominization.

## Critiques and Limitations

The multi-factor account is explicitly presented by the authors as a synthesis rather
than a settled resolution — "no single one of these could account for it alone" — and
competes with the more monocausal Washburn tool-use hypothesis. The Darwin
canine-reduction argument is praised for its timing but its proposed causal mechanism
(tools replacing teeth as weapons) is explicitly rejected by the authors as reversing
the true sequence of hominin adaptation.

### Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*(Attributed — [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]; as of Tattersall 2012.)*

**Criterion and wooded habitats.** After big-brain and “Man the Toolmaker” yardsticks faltered, paleoanthropology returned full circle to Dubois: the most common claim uniting touted “earliest hominids” is bipedal ground walking. Tattersall’s key empirical warning: known “very early hominid” fossils come from **thickly wooded or mixed habitats**, not open grassland — they were “not obliged to walk upright by disappearance of ancestral habitat.” Forest fragmentation still pressured more terrestrial life, but leaving trees was a “huge leap in the dark” (predator exposure, new foraging).

**Rejection of single “key benefit” stories.** He reviews and largely rejects monocausal drivers: energetics inconclusive / early inefficient bipeds unlikely to have chosen upright walking for economy; thermoregulation/hair-loss slain by wooded associations (bipedality before full tree abandonment); Serengeti-style predator-spotting disposed of because Pliocene habitats were more closed; fruit-picking does not require full-time bipedality; monogamy/provisioning/concealed-ovulation models clash with marked early sexual dimorphism. Darwin’s tools/carrying conjecture is “sad for the original conjecture” because hominids were bipedal long before toolmaking.

**Pre-existing upright / exaptive thesis.** Core Tattersall thesis: abandon the single key-benefit frame; the first ground bipeds were already most comfortable standing and moving upright — descended from Miocene hominoids that habitually held the trunk erect in trees (e.g. *Pierolapithecus* ~13 Ma Spain; *Oreopithecus*). African apes knuckle-walk because their ancestors were arboreal quadrupeds; early hominids were the opposite — quadrupedal ground movement awkward. This is an [[exaptation]] reading: pre-existing upright posture co-opted for terrestrial locomotion. Acquisition of an in-line terrestrial foot remains a fundamental mystery in his account.

**Anatomical operationalizations he stresses.** Carrying angle (femur angles inward at knee) already in *A. anamensis* tibia — earliest undoubted specialized bipedality. *A. afarensis* (Lucy): short flaring pelvis, bipedal ankle, short climbing-advantageous legs, not Ardi-style grasping foot. Laetoli prints (~3.6 Ma): heel–side–ball–toe weight transmission, arched feet, short in-line big toe; experimental equal heel/toe depths argue straightened knee. Turkana Boy grade: **obligate** biped with long legs and near-modern proportions; ~1.5 Ma East Turkana footprints confirm long stride and basically modern foot. Versatility (ground bipedality + tree agility) as a stable long-term strategy for early bipeds, not a mere transition to obligate striding.
