# Nature/Culture Distinction

## Definition as Coined

The **nature/culture distinction** is the foundational opposition of [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] [[structuralism]], set out in the *[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]* (1949): whatever is **universal and spontaneous** in human beings belongs to nature; whatever is governed by a **rule** and varies between societies belongs to culture. "Wherever there are rules we know for certain that the cultural stage has been reached," and universality "is the criterion of nature." The [[incest-taboo|incest prohibition]] is unique in possessing both marks — universal *and* a rule — and so is precisely the threshold "by which... the transition from nature to culture is accomplished."

## Semantic History

The opposition is a [[binary-opposition]] Lévi-Strauss treated as basic to human thought and applied across his work (later: the raw and the cooked). Its earliest formulation in his corpus is arguably in *[[levi-strauss-tristes-tropiques-1955|Tristes Tropiques]]*, where he glosses [[caduveo|Caduveo]] facial painting, in the words of the 18th-century missionary Sánchez Labrador's informants, as marking "the passage from nature to culture, from the 'stupid' animal to civilized man" — one had to be painted to be human. In his mature (1966) preface, Lévi-Strauss qualified the distinction: given ape tool-use and communication, the line "is more tenuous and tortuous than was imagined twenty years ago," and the nature/culture contrast may be "an artificial creation of culture" rather than "a primeval fact."

## Operationalizations

Marked by the presence or absence of a rule; deployed as the master frame for reading the incest prohibition, exogamy, cooking, table manners, and classificatory systems as cultural impositions of order upon a natural continuum.

## Applications

Structures the argument of the *Elementary Structures* (incest prohibition as the nature→culture hinge) and recurs throughout structural anthropology as the primary opposition from which others (raw/cooked, wild/tame) are generated. For the [[bororo|Bororo]], Lévi-Strauss argued, an analogous nature/culture opposition organizes cosmology, with death classed as "anticultural."

## Critiques and Limitations

The opposition has been among the most criticized elements of structuralism: feminist and ecological anthropology ([[ortner-female-to-male-1972|Sherry Ortner's "Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?"]], 1972/74; later critiques by [[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]] and Philippe Descola) argued it is a Western folk category wrongly universalized, and that many societies do not organize thought around it at all. [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]] both *used* the opposition (to explain [[female-subordination|female subordination]] — woman as "closer to nature") and helped trigger the critique that dismantled its universality; see the [[nature-culture-gender-debate]]. Lévi-Strauss's own 1966 hedging anticipates the objection. Record it as an analytic construct of structuralism, not a cultural universal.

## Descola: dualism as local naturalist formula (2005)

[[descola-philippe|Descola]]'s *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]* treats the nature/culture opposition not as a human universal (nor only as a Lévi-Straussian analytic) but as the **local formula of [[naturalism-ontology|naturalism]]** — continuity of physicalities + discontinuity of interiorities — produced historically in the West (Ch.3 genealogy: landscape perspective, *phusis*, Christian creation, autonomy of Nature and of Culture). Many societies (Amazonian animists, Australian totemic systems, analogist cosmologies) do not organize experience around that split; Amerindian myths often narrate natural discontinuities emerging from a prior cultural continuum rather than culture emerging from nature. The positive alternative is the [[four-ontologies|four modes of identification]]. This generalizes and systematizes the feminist/ecological critique already linked above ([[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]], Ortner debate) into a comparative ontology. See [[hubs/theory/four-ontologies]].

## Ingold: dwelling against the dualism (2000)

[[ingold-tim|Tim Ingold]]'s *[[ingold-perception-of-the-environment-2000|The Perception of the Environment]]* (2000) treats the nature/culture split as the "underlying fault" of Western thought that also cleaves anthropology into sociocultural and biophysical divisions. His exit route is not a fourfold typology of ontologies but a [[dwelling-perspective|dwelling perspective]]: organism-persons inhabit environments through [[enskilment|skill]]; social relations are a subset of ecological relations; landscape and [[taskscape]] are not nature vs culture (Ch. 11: "the landscape is not nature… the taskscape is not culture"). He argues that "nature is a cultural construction" is incoherent (constructionism's paradox of nature as both product and precondition; Ch. 3). Parallel to Descola in refusing universal dualism; different architecture (developmental/ecological engagement vs modes of identification). See [[hubs/theory/dwelling-perspective]].
