# Megafaunal Extinctions

## Definition as Coined

**Megafaunal extinctions** denotes the pulse (or pulses) of extinction among large terrestrial animals at the end of the last ice age and into the early Holocene. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] the Americas are the focal case: a terminal Pleistocene loss of giant mammals coeval in scholarly debate with [[clovis-culture|Clovis]]-period hunting, climate change including the [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]], and (as a minority hypothesis) hyper-disease.

## Semantic History

Pleistocene extinction research long catalogued lost taxa; the modern debate crystallized around **Paul Martin’s overkill / “blitzkrieg”** model (Clovis hunters as primary agents) versus climate-driven and multi-causal alternatives. Mithen (2003) reviews Martin’s case, radiocarbon pruning of last-appearance dates, Monte Verde’s implication of long pre-Clovis presence, opportunistic Clovis foraging evidence, and climate-only critiques (e.g. E. C. Pielou) — without treating any single model as wiki-voice fact.

## Operationalizations

As of Mithen 2003’s synthesis:

- **Species tallies:** ~70% of North American large mammals (36 species) and ~80% of South American megafauna (46 species) extinct in the terminal Pleistocene pulse; Australia almost all of 16 large species known at 60 ka; Europe seven; Africa only two of forty-four (comparative framing attributed to Mithen).
- **Chronology:** Meltzer & Mead date compilations pruned by Grayson to fewer reliable determinations; only seven species (“ice-age seven”) tightly dated to 11,000–10,000 BC alongside Clovis (as of Mithen’s report of that literature).
- **Archaeological association:** Clovis points with mammoth (Naco, Lehner Ranch, Blackwater Draw stratigraphy); scarcity of multi-taxon kill sites used by Martin as compatible with rapid blitzkrieg; Gary Haynes drought die-off analogy for mammoth bonebeds.
- **Climate proxies:** Younger Dryas and post-LGM restructuring as alternative or co-drivers.

## Applications

As of [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]]:

- **Americas / Clovis:** Martin’s Clovis-first + overkill package; Mithen’s counter that Monte Verde shows people long before Clovis and mass extinction — if early people did not hunt large game, animals were not naïve; if they did, more kill sites should exist (“Martin loses either way”).
- **Clovis economy:** opportunistic foragers (small game, plants, fish at many sites) rather than specialist big-game hunters outside the Southwest — weakens pure overkill specialization.
- **Hyper-disease hypothesis** noted as alternative agent model.
- **End of Clovis:** last North American mammoth ~10,000 BC; Clovis point manufacture ceases as Holocene environments stabilize and cultures diversify (attributed narrative).
- **Old World:** European recolonization after LGM occurs amid changing fauna; African extinctions relatively minor in Mithen’s comparative table.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Dating quality:** last-appearance radiocarbon sets are highly sensitive to sample selection and contamination pruning — different compilations yield different “tight” coeval species lists.
- **Absence of kill sites** is ambiguous (preservation, discovery probability, scavenging vs hunting).
- **Climate-only models** struggle, Mithen reports via Pielou-type critiques, to explain why prior warmings did not produce the same unique terminal pulse — multi-causal interaction remains open.
- **Pre-aDNA / pre-later pre-Clovis consensus shifts:** peopling chronology after 2003 revises the human-presence baseline against which overkill is timed — date-stamp all genetic and corridor claims as of Mithen 2003.
- Extinction is a **phenomenon**; causal attribution is a **debate** — do not promote overkill or climate-only to wiki voice.

## Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009; radiocarbon BP as he states. Full overkill debate inventory belongs on [[pleistocene-overkill-debate]]; this section extracts the extinction-concept layer.)*

### Roster and timing

- **35 genera** of mammals vanished from the North American Pleistocene landscape—mostly very large megafauna of diverse adaptations/habitats (taxonomic data from E. Anderson 1984, Grayson 2007, Kurtén & Anderson 1980). Nearly all extinct genera (except Aztlanolagus and diminutive pronghorn) had adult body weight **≥50 kg**; estimates of **>100 million** such large mammals on Pleistocene North America; many genera native for hundreds of ka–Ma and weathered multiple glacial cycles.
- By **10,800 BP** all 35 were extinct—but **only about half** of the 35 genera are known to have survived until that moment (extinctions **not all simultaneous** at that date). Critical constraint on overkill and Firestone-impact simultaneity claims.
- Extinctions not limited to largest mammals—also storks, vultures, eagles, jays, blackbirds, a snake, several turtle genera/species, a spruce tree—one element of sweeping range changes in plants and animals.
- After competitor megafauna gone, plains **bison** populations “skyrocketed” (competitive release), fueled partly by expanding warm-season C₄ grasses; Meltzer states **no direct evidence** that grassland expansion caused megafaunal extinction. Folsom+ economies tied to *Bison antiquus*; Clovis-known bison kills few (~dozen, mostly Murray Springs); Folsom kills averaged ~15 animals; a few centuries later nearly 60.

### Archaeological predation evidence (as of Meltzer / Grayson & Meltzer)

- Secure archaeological predation evidence limited; Grayson & Meltzer “Requiem for North American Overkill” treated kill-site vetting as essential.
- Type-site pattern at [[blackwater-draw]]: partial butchery of waterhole deaths—not proof humans killed all/most mammoths there.
- Eastern North America: mastodon more abundant than mammoth yet rarely on Clovis menu; Kimmswick (mastodon + fluted points) exception; Mason-Quimby line of spatial overlap fluted points and mastodons is not kill-site density.
- Martin’s overkill scenario (band exits corridor ~11,500 BP; 16 km/yr killing wave; Tierra del Fuego in 1,200 years) requires **no pre-Clovis** humans (else defensive behaviors or earlier extinction); [[monte-verde]] et al. predate 11,500 BP—timing problem for strict blitzkrieg (Meltzer). Martin counters: Clovis is “the time of unmistakable appearance of paleoindian hunters using distinctive projectile points.”
- Fiedel & G. Haynes: continent as “gigantic island”; 14 kill sites “phenomenally rich” for ~300-year overlap—Meltzer rejects island analogy and notes only **two genera** with secure kills among 35 extinct genera.
- Signor-Lipps: if ¹⁴C scarce for fauna in shared years with humans, animals may already have been in decline before/by Clovis; if 100 million animals of 35 genera lasted until the very end then suddenly killed, many terminal ages expected—there are not (Meltzer).

### Climate side (not exclusive cause)

If genera disappeared at different times, complex end-Pleistocene climate changes over millennia become plausible. [[younger-dryas]] as unique “freak event” (Broecker via Meltzer) may help explain why taxa that survived prior cycles succumbed this time—**climate hypotheses underdeveloped** (Martin right on that point); need genus/species-specific responses (Grayson et al.). Do not promote climate-only or overkill-only to wiki voice.

**Genetics as of Meltzer 2009:** aDNA of extinct taxa discussed as emerging evidence class; date-stamp and do not freeze later paleogenomics into this layer.
