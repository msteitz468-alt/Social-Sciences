# Sedentarization

The settling of nomadic peoples into permanent residence — as state policy, economic drift, or demographic release valve. Distinguish from [[sedentism]] (the deep-prehistory emergence of settled life); sedentarization presupposes an existing mobile population.

## Definition and Barth's mechanism (1961)

[[barth-fredrik|Fredrik Barth]] (*[[barth-nomads-south-persia-1961|Nomads of South Persia]]*, fieldwork 1957–58, under a UNESCO project explicitly on "nomads and the problems of sedentarization") supplied the classic micro-economic mechanism, from the [[basseri|Basseri]] case. Nomads leave pastoralism at **both ends of the wealth distribution**:

- **Top exit — accumulation:** "the net productivity rate for the owner declines as the size of the herds increases" (hired shepherds skim and under-report; disaster losses grow); so "as a herd-owner's wealth grows, there are... growing economic incentives for him to transfer a part of his capital to another form... where the owner is more secure against capital losses" — in South Persia, land, which also buys entry to the landowning elite. Barth stressed the transfer often happens "with no thoughts of future sedentarization."
- **Bottom exit — impoverishment:** debt carried year to year forces invasion of productive capital; "once this downward spiral starts, it tends to accelerate." "The figure of 60 adult sheep and goats seems... to have been regarded as the threshold below which a downward movement was inevitable"; seasonal village wage-work then completes the slide to propertyless villager. Barth judged this "the numerically predominant form."
- **Why exactly two exits:** the tribesman's status is intermediate between village landowner and tenant; only the extremes of nomadic wealth are status-compatible with sedentary slots, so the broad middle has neither motive nor destination.
- **Braking and balance:** anticipatory inheritance fragments herds mid-career; reduced consumption and cheap partner-credit hold households in the middle range. At tribe level (Ch. IX–X), fertility implying ~trebling per generation is drained off by sedentarization, keeping nomad population, pastures, and herds in long-run balance — Barth's general model, since pastoral capital *is* the food supply and Malthusian starvation would destroy the economy itself. Footnoted alternative where settling is impossible: raiding and warfare.

## Operationalizations

Barth's operational markers: herd-size thresholds (~60 adult sheep/goats as the collapse threshold; large herds triggering land purchase at the top), traceable settled populations of nomad descent (Chahardonge and other Basseri-derived villages), and generational genealogies marking settled lines (his Fig. 8). Policy-driven sedentarization is read from administrative record (Reza Shah's 1930s program).

## Semantic History

Forced sedentarization as state policy is documented in the same source: Reza Shah's 1930s enforced settlement of Iranian tribes (most Basseri lost flocks; migration resumed on his 1941 abdication), and renewed administrative pressure plus Army rule by 1958. Barth's usage covers both policy-driven and drift forms; later nomadism studies (not yet ingested) made the term standard.

## Applications

Basseri case histories (a Labu Musa man's climb from 20 sheep to landownership; tents below the 60-head threshold in Barth's own camp settling within 18 months); whole-group settlements (Chahardonge villages; Farhadi subdivisions settled c. 1900 and 1925); historical disappearance of entire tribes (Lak Lurs, Ainalu, Baharlu) where sedentarization outran fertility. Reverse movement (villagers joining tribes) increases in times of chaos — the net flow judged "overwhelmingly in the direction of sedentarization" (Barth and informants, 1958).

## Critiques and Limitations

Single-source mechanism (as ingested): Barth had "no reliable data on the numerical frequency" of the two exits and flagged his demographic base as thin. The model presumes private herd ownership and an adjacent landed society offering status-compatible slots — its portability to other pastoral systems is an empirical question, per Barth's own Ch. X comparative caveats.
