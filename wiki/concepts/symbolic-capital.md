# Symbolic Capital

*(Attributed — Bourdieu, *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]*, ch. "Symbolic capital".)* Capital denied as capital — prestige, honour, renown, and credit that function as economic and political resources precisely when agents and groups [[misrecognition|misrecognize]] them as disinterested. Central stake of strategies of reproduction in the pre-capitalist economies *Logic* analyzes ([[kabyle]] honour, gift, name; Béarn marriage and patrimony).

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Bourdieu.)* The concept is **coined in *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]* (1972), ch. 4**, and reworked in *Logic* ch. 7. In an economy defined by refusal to recognize the objective truth of economic practices (naked self-interest), even economic capital can act only if recognized through conversion that renders unrecognizable the true principle of its efficacy. Symbolic capital is this denied capital, recognized as legitimate — i.e., misrecognized as capital — and, with religious capital, is perhaps the only possible form of accumulation when economic capital is not recognized.

*Outline*'s original formulations: symbolic capital is "a transformed and thereby disguised form of physical 'economic' capital [which] produces its proper effect inasmuch, and only inasmuch, as it conceals the fact that it originates in 'material' forms of capital"; it is "always credit, in the widest sense of the word, i.e. a sort of advance which the group alone can grant those who give it the best material and symbolic guarantees"; and, "in the form of the prestige and renown attached to a family and a name," it is "readily convertible back into economic capital [and] is perhaps the most valuable form of accumulation." The collective work of conversion is "**social alchemy**": "the work of denial which is the source of social alchemy is, like magic, a collective undertaking" in which, when the group lies to itself, "there is neither deceiver nor deceived."

> "Symbolic capital is this denied capital, recognized as legitimate, that is, misrecognized as capital… which, along with religious capital… is perhaps the only possible form of accumulation when economic capital is not recognized."

Symbolic capital is credit in the broadest sense — an advance, a credence only the group's belief can grant those who give the best symbolic and material guarantees. Exhibition of symbolic capital (always expensive in material terms) is one of the mechanisms that make capital go to capital. Transformation of any kind of capital into symbolic capital — legitimate possession grounded in the nature of its possessor — is the fundamental operation of social alchemy (paradigm: [[gift-exchange]]).

## Semantic History

- **Honour as accumulation (Preface).** Conducts of honour reconceived not only as obedience to rules/values (as experienced) but as more or less conscious pursuit of accumulation of symbolic capital; marriage ritual as strategy maximizing material and symbolic profit.
- **Kinds of capital (ch. 8).** Fighting strength, economic capital, and symbolic capital accruing from successful use of the others are mutually convertible under laws of equivalence yet produce specific effects only in specific conditions; social science is not social physics because cognition (recognition/misrecognition) is part of social reality.
- **Weberian charisma (ch. 9).** Bourdieu related symbolic capital to Weber's charisma but argued Weber, trapped in realist typologies, treated charisma as a particular form of power rather than a dimension of all power — another name for legitimacy, product of recognition/misrecognition/belief.
- **Articulation with [[cultural-capital]] and [[social-capital]].** Fuller typology in "forms of capital" essays and *Distinction*; *Logic* develops symbolic capital most fully in the honour economy and conversion/domination chapters.
- Related to status honor and Maussian gift prestige without identity of terms.

## Operationalizations

- **Honour, name, marriage value, political credit.** Convertibility rates vary by social formation and, in later work, by [[social-field|field]].
- **Matrimonial strategies.** "Fine marriage" maximizing economic and symbolic profits (Béarn); Kabyle distant/prestige marriage as public exhibition and increase of family symbolic capital at high economic cost; inverted haggling stepping up bridewealth as index of symbolic value on the matrimonial market.
- **Land and *nif* / *h'urma*.** Ethos of honour as transfigured expression of economic logic for groups whose patrimony is largely symbolic; alienated land, unavenged rape or murder as forms of the same offence against the master's *nif*.
- **Clientele and *thiwizi*.** Accumulation of honour produces a clientele marshaling labour at peak seasons; complete truth of appropriation of services is *thiwizi* as voluntary assistance that is also corvée.
- **Ruinous defence.** Group may withdraw credit on slight slur; defence of symbolic capital can be economically ruinous (honour price of family land fallen to strangers).
- **House spatial mapping.** Rifle as attribute of *nif* protecting *h'urma* in light upper parts of the [[kabyle]] house (Appendix).

## Applications

- **[[kabyle]] gift economy, land, and matrimonial strategies** (Book I–II of *Logic*) — densest empirical program in the ingested source.
- **Béarn.** Manipulation of genealogical tree to justify alliances serving material and symbolic capital; card-playing metaphor for marrying each child.
- **Conversion and legitimation (ch. 8).** Unbroken progression from symmetrical gift exchange to asymmetrical redistribution as basis of political authority; conversion of economic into symbolic capital disguising dependence under moral relations.
- **Class distinction and cultural fields** — gestured at; await *Distinction* and field monographs.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Capital metaphor.** Whether "capital" over-economizes culture and honour is a standing reception charge; Bourdieu answers that only a comprehensive balance-sheet of symbolic profits grasps rationality economism calls absurd.
- **Convertibility claims.** Strict mutual convertibility is asserted with "specific conditions" caveats; critics question rates, losses, and irreversibility.
- **Measurement.** Symbolic capital "less easily measured than land or livestock" (*Logic*) — operational fuzziness acknowledged in the source itself.
- **Charisma reduction.** Reading all legitimacy as symbolic capital may flatten Weberian and political-theological distinctions — position to record when Weber secondary literature is ingested.
