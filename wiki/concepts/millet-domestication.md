# Millet Domestication

## Definition as Coined

**Millet domestication** denotes the process by which two distinct millet species were brought under cultivation in north China: **foxtail millet** (*Setaria italica*), derived from the wild progenitor green foxtail (*Setaria viridis*), and **broomcorn millet** (*Panicum miliaceum*), whose wild progenitor remains uncertain (as of [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]]). As with [[rice-domestication|rice domestication]] in the Yangzi basin, Liu & Chen treat millet domestication in north China as a multi-millennial continuum rather than a single event, tracked archaeologically through grain morphology, phytolith and starch residue analysis, and shifting species proportions in flotation-recovered assemblages.

## Semantic History

Long before either species shows morphological domestication, use-wear and residue evidence from **Xiachuan** (ca. 23,900–16,400 BP) and **Shizitan Locality 9** (ca. 12,700–11,600 cal BP), both in Shanxi, indicates humans processed wild grasses — possible millet ancestors — more than 20,000 years ago in north China (as of Liu & Chen 2012, citing Liu et al. 2011). This places millet pre-domestication processing on a timescale comparable to, though independent of, the rice sequence in the Yangzi basin. The earliest securely **domesticated** millet remains (8,200–7,000 years ago) span a wide arc of north China from the Liao River valley to the upper Yellow River: Xinglonggou, Inner Mongolia (8200–7500 cal BP); Yuezhuang, Shandong (ca. 8000 cal BP); Dadiwan, Gansu (7800–7350 cal BP); Cishan, Hebei (7400–7200 cal BP); and several Peiligang-culture sites in Henan, including finds near [[jiahu|Jiahu]] (8000–7000 cal BP) (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Operationalizations

As of Liu & Chen 2012:

- **Species identification:** grain morphology (foxtail vs. broomcorn), phytolith analysis, and starch-grain residue on grinding stones (e.g., a Peiligang-culture site at Egou, Henan, where millet starch residue corroborates macrobotanical finds — Liu, L. et al. 2010a).
- **Broomcorn-to-foxtail shift:** millet grains from Xinglonggou, Yuezhuang, and Dadiwan are predominantly broomcorn early on; over time, assemblages show gradual grain-size increase and a proportional shift from broomcorn to foxtail dominance from the Neolithic into the early Bronze Age.
- **Dietary-proportion trend:** at Xinglonggou, Xinglongwa-period millet accounted for only 15% of flotation-recovered seeds, versus 99% in Lower Xiajiadian Bronze Age deposits (4000–3500 cal BP) at the same site — millet's rise from a minor component to a dominant staple is itself part of the domestication story, not a separate process.
- **Stable-isotope dietary confirmation:** millet comprised 75–85% of human diet at Jiangzhai and Shijia, Shaanxi (6900–6000 cal BP), and roughly 90% of pig/dog diet at Xipo, Henan (6000–5500 cal BP) (Pechenkina et al. 2005), indicating an established millet-based subsistence economy by the [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]] period.

## Applications

- Lu (1998, 2002), via experimental cultivation of green foxtail, argues the first millet farmers were mobile foragers who cultivated the cereal without needing to tend fields intensively, since green foxtail grows well unattended — though yields were too low to serve as a sole subsistence base (attributed).
- Millet-based subsistence fed into the north-China Neolithic sequence culminating in [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]] and later Longshan-horizon economies, paralleling the Yangzi rice trajectory feeding [[rice-domestication|Hemudu]] and successor cultures.
- [[jiahu|Jiahu]] sits at the geographic and cultural boundary between the millet-dominant north and the rice-dominant Yangzi zone, with both crops present in its early record.

## Critiques and Limitations

### The Cishan storage dispute (position)

Cishan, Hebei, has long been reported to have yielded roughly 50,000 kg of stored millet across 80 pits (Sun et al. 1981; Tong 1984), but this claim is disputed: no carbonized millet was actually preserved — the claim rests entirely on phytolith and starch analysis — and some pits held pig or dog skeletons beneath the putative "millet layers," inconsistent with straightforward grain storage. The nature and quantity of "millet storage" at Cishan remains an unresolved question (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Compounding the dispute, Lu et al. (2009) phytolith analysis of Cishan soil samples proposed broomcorn millet dated to 10,300 cal BP and foxtail millet after 8700 cal BP — dates Liu & Chen report have been questioned by Chinese archaeologists for unsystematic sampling (soil from pits without controlled excavation) and for AMS dates not derived from carbonized seeds (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

### General limitations

- As with rice, morphological, phytolith, and starch-residue identification methods can yield conflicting chronologies for the same site; Cishan is the clearest instance.
- Millet's minor early dietary role (as low as 15% of recovered seeds at Xinglonggou) versus its later dominance (99% at the same site) is itself only inferable from flotation sampling intensity, which varies by excavation.
- Four-way non-identity: millet-farming assemblages (Peiligang, Xinglongwa, Yangshao) are archaeological-culture constructs, not evidence of a single ethnic or linguistic population; Liu & Chen do not equate them with a people or language.
- Genetic (aDNA) work on millet domestication is not covered in Liu & Chen 2012's synthesis; any future genetic claims should be date-stamped independently.

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Chs. 5–6, 8.)*

- **East Asia package.** Main millets: foxtail (*Setaria italica*, wild ancestor green bristlegrass *S. viridis*) and broomcorn (*Panicum miliaceum*). Late Pleistocene northern China: Nanzhuangtou ~12,000 cal BC with pollen of green bristlegrass + pottery/grinders; parallel pottery+millet north and pottery+rice south (attributed). Peiligang-type middle Huanghe sites from mid-seventh millennium BC: foxtail and broomcorn millet as main crops (Wu’an storage >100 m³ foxtail; modern-like grain size at Dingzhuang); C4 isotopes as dietary mainstay; permanent terrace fields more likely than swidden on loess (Rowley-Conwy via Barker). Jiahu grows millet **and** rice together — Barker argues rice and millet cultivation developed more or less **synchronously** through the early Holocene from late Pleistocene intensification (contra Higham’s post-~6000 BC climate-stress model) (attributed position).
- **Yangshao.** Yangshao now dated c.5000–3000 BC (not older 3500–2500 textbook frame); millet cultivation and pig breeding mainstay at Banbo/Jiangzhai-scale villages (attributed).
- **Korea / Jōmon.** Tongsamdong foxtail+broomcorn AMS 3360 cal BC; Jōmon intensive barnyard grass with morphological millet changes; by c.1000 BC Late Jōmon Kazahari: domestic rice, broomcorn, foxtail (attributed).
- **South Asia indigenous millets.** Barker (via Fuller): native small millets gradually cultivated in Harappan-era kharif systems; Deccan Southern Neolithic takes browntop millet, bristley foxtail, mungbean, horsegram into cultivation piecemeal **independent of Harappan** (Fuller et al.); African millets incorporated piecemeal later, not a single Dilmun-trade package (attributed).
- **Africa.** Traditional Sahel staples include pearl/bulrush millet and finger millet; botanical-geography models place millet domestication through the Sahel. Dhar Tichitt and Karkarichinkat domestic cereals; Birimi (N Ghana) domestic-size pearl millet AMS ~1500 BC; Oued Chebi ~1000 BC pottery impressions of wild + cultivated pearl millet show gradual commitment (attributed). Morphological sorghum domestication can lag millennia of basket-harvest of morphologically wild staple (Nabta Playa c.6000 BC wild sorghum) (attributed).
- **Identity caution.** Millet assemblages ≠ people/language/genes. Cross-links: [[rice-domestication]], [[yangshao-culture]], [[bantu-expansion]].
