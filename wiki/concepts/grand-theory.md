# Grand Theory

## Definition as Coined

**"Grand theory"** is [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]]'s polemical term, from *[[mills-sociological-imagination-1959|The Sociological Imagination]]* (1959), for a style of sociological theorizing that consists in "the associating and dissociating of concepts" at so high a level of abstraction that its practitioners, Mills argued (attributed), "cannot logically get down to observation." His exemplar was [[parsons-talcott|Talcott Parsons]]'s *[[parsons-social-system-1951|The Social System]]* (1951). The charge is captured in Mills's epigram: **"Grand theory is drunk on syntax, blind to semantics"** — preoccupied with the definitional relations among concepts rather than their empirical reference, an "arid game of Concepts" Mills called **"the fetishism of the Concept."**

Mills performed the critique by quoting long passages of Parsons and supplying plain-English "translations" — rendering, for instance, a dense paragraph on value-orientation as: "People often share standards and expect one another to stick to them. In so far as they do, their society may be orderly." His verdict on *The Social System*: "It is only about 50 per cent verbiage; 40 per cent is well-known textbook sociology. The other 10 per cent... is of possible — although rather vague — ideological use" (attributed).

## Semantic History

The term is now used more loosely across the social sciences than Mills intended — often neutrally, for any large-scale, ambitious, systematizing theory (the sense in which the "grand theory" of the 1970s–80s, e.g. Quentin Skinner's edited volume *The Return of Grand Theory in the Human Sciences*, 1985, revalues the label). Mills's original coinage was purely pejorative and specific: it named the Parsonsian program's flight into abstraction, twinned with [[abstracted-empiricism]]'s flight into method as the two dominant "withdrawals from the tasks of the social sciences." The two failures are complementary — one abandons problems through "formal and cloudy obscurantism," the other through "formal and empty ingenuity."

## Operationalizations

Not a measured construct. Mills identified grand theory by symptoms: theorizing conducted at the highest "level of abstraction" with no systematic movement down to concrete, historically specific problems; the treatment of concepts as a self-contained system to be elaborated rather than as tools for defining and solving problems.

## Applications

Mills's substantive objection (attributed) was not only stylistic. He argued that Parsons's definition of order via the "institutionalization of patterns of value-orientation" effectively **excludes power, economy, and political conflict from view**, transforming institutional structures into a "symbol sphere" of legitimations — a move Mills likened (following Marx and Engels on Hegel) to treating "The Ideas" as autonomous historical agents. Because the scheme assumes "a sort of harmony of interests as the natural feature of any society," Mills held it **cannot formulate conflict, structural antagonism, revolution, or historical change** (see [[functionalism-and-social-change-debate]]). He cited David Lockwood's parallel objection and Alvin [[gouldner-alvin-w|Gouldner]]'s observation that Parsons's own concrete historical analyses smuggle in Marxist concepts of class and power his general theory officially excludes — "It almost seems as if two sets of books were being kept." As a constructive alternative Mills proposed plural "working models" of integration (his "correspondence" vs. "co-ordination" types), praising Franz Neumann's *Behemoth* over Parsons: "one *Behemoth* is worth, to social science, twenty *Social Systems*."

## Critiques and Limitations

The label is one side of a polemic: Mills reproduces and mocks Parsons's prose but does not state Parsons's program in Parsons's own terms, and neofunctionalist and Parsonsian scholars reject the charge that the value-order framework is empty or ideologically conservative (Mills's own footnoted claim that "the ideological meaning of grand theory tends strongly to legitimate stable forms of domination" is his position, not a settled finding). Parsons's defenders argue the abstraction is the necessary price of a general analytical scheme. The dispute is filed on [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]]; the target program on [[structural-functionalism]] and its critics on [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]].
