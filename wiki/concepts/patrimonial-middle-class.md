# Patrimonial Middle Class

[[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s name for the historically new intermediate wealth group that owns a non-trivial share of aggregate capital. He calls its emergence “the most important structural transformation to affect the wealth distribution over the long run” and a crucial reason why developed countries moved from a society of rentiers toward a society of managers (or from superrentiers to a less extreme rentier society). Core concept of *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* Part Three and [[piketty-capital-dynamics]].

## Definition as Coined

The **patrimonial middle class** is an intermediate group—operationally the **middle 40%** of the wealth distribution (between the poorest 50% and the richest 10%)—distinctly wealthier than the poorer half and owning, in contemporary rich-country series, **between a quarter and a third of national wealth**. It is defined by **asset ownership**, not by income-middle-class status. Piketty stresses that twentieth-century deconcentration of wealth benefited this middle 40% almost exclusively; the poorest half’s share hardly rose.

Orders of magnitude he reports:

| Regime (approx.) | Top 10% wealth share | Middle 40% | Bottom 50% |
|---|---|---|---|
| Europe 1910 (very high) | ~90% | ~5% | ~5% |
| Scandinavia 1970s–80s (medium) | ~50% | ~40% | ~10% |
| Europe 2010 (medium–high) | ~60% | ~35% | ~5% |
| US 2010 (high) | ~70% | ~25% | ~5% |

France: top decile wealth share ~90% (1910–20) → 60–70% (1950–70) → ~60–65% today; top centile ~60% → 20–30%; patrimonial middle class now owns about **a third** of national wealth. Britain: top decile >90% pre-WWI → 60–65% in the 1970s → ~70% today. Across Europe, what the top 10% lost mainly went to the middle 40%, not the poorest half (still ~5%, never >10% even in Sweden). Top centile wealth share fell nearly two-thirds in a century (~60% in 1910–20 to just over 20% in recent European series)—implying far fewer estates on the order of tens of millions of euros / multi-million annual capital yields.

## Semantic History

Distinct from the income-based “middle class” of postwar sociology and politics. Piketty’s “lower / middle / upper class” labels (bottom 50% / middle 40% / top 10%) are explicitly arbitrary illustrative designations; analysis rests on uniform deciles and centiles. “Patrimonial” stresses **wealth stocks**—typically primary residence plus mortgage amortization as the typical path into middle wealth—rather than occupational prestige or wage rank. Related older vocabulary: “middle class of wealth,” small property owners, homeowning strata; Belle Époque Europe had essentially **no** such middle class in this sense (middle 40% owning just over 5%, scarcely more than the bottom half).

US path differs from Europe’s linear twentieth-century story: Piketty describes a (white) patrimonial middle class already present in the nineteenth century, set back in the Gilded Age, recovering mid-twentieth century, then set back again after 1980—so US wealth inequality today can exceed early-nineteenth levels while Europe’s narrative remains tied to Trente Glorieuses optimism.

## Operationalizations

- **Fractiles**: bottom 50% / middle 40% / top 10% (and top 1%) of net wealth; average net wealth of middle 40% near the society-wide mean when that group owns ~35% (illustrative ~€175,000 per adult at mean wealth ~€175k–200k scales).
- **Housing capital** as typical vehicle; net-of-mortgage wealth matters (gross housing can overstate middle-class patrimony).
- **Cross-country tables** (Piketty Tables 7.1–7.3): even “medium” capital inequality (Scandinavia 1970s–80s) far exceeds high labor-income inequality; wealth is always much more concentrated than labor income.
- **Who gained**: French evidence that bottom-half share remained <5% throughout nineteenth–twentieth centuries; ~half of French people died with no or negative net wealth, a proportion that barely budged.

## Applications

Explains why total [[income-inequality]] fell in the first half of the twentieth century even though labor-income inequality did not decrease structurally 1900–10 to 1950–60 in France or the US: collapse of high capital incomes plus middle-class wealth ownership, not Kuznetsian intersectoral mobility alone (see [[kuznets-curve]]). Qualifies pure “return to Belle Époque” narratives under new [[patrimonial-capitalism]]: top wealth shares are rising again with β and slower growth, but a real middle class of wealth remains a structural difference from 1910. Feeds debates on [[social-mobility]], meritocracy, and whether twenty-first-century inequality will recombine medium rentiers with [[supermanagers]]. ## Critiques and Limitations

National housing-market dependence; debt-financed homeownership vs net wealth; treatment of pension wealth and public assets; Global South applicability limited in the 2014 book. Racial and gender composition of the “middle” (especially US white patrimonial middle class) is under-specified if the concept is used as a universal class category. Whether a quarter to a third of wealth for the middle 40% is politically stabilizing “crumbs” (Piketty’s own ironic phrasing) or a durable transformation is a normative and predictive dispute—not a definitional failure.
