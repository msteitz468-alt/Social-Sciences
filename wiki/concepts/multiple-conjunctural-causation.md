# Multiple Conjunctural Causation

## Definition as Coined

[[ragin-charles|Charles Ragin]]'s term (attributed) for a model of causation with three components, stated in *[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]*: "(1) most often, it is a combination of conditions that generate an outcome; (2) several different combinations of conditions may produce the same outcome; and (3) a given condition may have a different impact on the outcome depending on context." It is thus a **context-specific notion of causality** in which "a causal condition may have opposite effects, depending on context."

The three components correspond to three ideas: **conjunctural causation** (causes operate in combination, not in isolation — the "chemical causation" of J.S. Mill, where a phenomenon "emerges from the intersection of appropriate preconditions"); **equifinality** (multiple, distinct causal paths reach the same outcome); and **causal asymmetry / context-dependence** (a condition's effect depends on the configuration it sits in, and the causes of an outcome's absence are not the mirror image of the causes of its presence).

Ragin locates causal complexity in combinatorics, not variable-count: "social phenomena are complex and difficult to unravel not because there are too many variables affecting them… but because different causally relevant conditions can combine in a variety of ways to produce a given outcome" (attributed).

## Semantic History

The underlying idea is older than the label: Ragin credits J.S. Mill's "chemical causation" and "plural causation," and reads [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore]]'s *Social Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (1966) as an exemplar in which the same outcome arises from several causal conjunctures and the same condition (e.g., commercialization of agriculture) has different consequences in different contexts. Ragin's contribution (attributed) is to name the general class and give it a formal, testable representation in Boolean algebra. He distinguishes it sharply from **additive multiple causation** ("both X and Y cause Z"), which asserts that a change in *either* variable independently shifts the outcome — the opposite of conjunctural logic. He also folds two sub-cases under the label: **illusory difference** (apparently different conditions that are causally equivalent at a higher level of abstraction) and **illusory commonality** (apparently similar conditions with different causal effects by context).

## Operationalizations

In [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]], multiple conjunctural causation is expressed as a minimized **sums-of-products equation** (e.g., strike success `S = AC + BC`; peasant revolt `R = ABD + aCD`): each product term is a distinct sufficient combination, several combinations join by OR, and no single condition need be necessary or sufficient. Ragin's formal illustration: "if outcome Y occurs following the combination of X1 and X2 or the combination of X3 and X4, then none of the single conditions… is either necessary or sufficient to produce Y." Asymmetry is operationalized via **De Morgan's Law**: the equation for the outcome's absence is derived from — and looks structurally unlike — the equation for its presence. This is contrasted (attributed) with additive statistical models, which estimate each cause's separate, context-independent "net effect" and cannot represent conjunctural patterns without intractable higher-order interaction terms.

## Applications

- Ragin's worked **ethnic political mobilization** example: mobilization arises either from large size + economic advance, or from a strong linguistic base + high relative wealth (`E = SG + LW`) — two genuinely different recipes (equifinality).
- **Regionally based ethnic mobilization** can follow two opposed paths (ethnic inequality + centralization + multinational economic domination, vs. ethnic equality + decentralization + in-migration) — the same outcome from contrary conditions.
- The Wales vs. Belgium contrast, where ethnic inequality is present in one and absent in the other, shows a single condition being neither necessary nor sufficient across cases.

## Critiques and Limitations

The concept's leverage depends on QCA's machinery and inherits its limitations (see [[qualitative-comparative-analysis]]): results are bounded by the diversity of naturally occurring cases (a conjunctural claim covers only combinations that actually exist), and parsimonious equations may rest on simplifying assumptions about non-existent combinations. Critics of QCA ([[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]], Seawright) question the robustness of the identified combinations to case selection and measurement — bearing directly on how securely a given conjunctural claim is established. Causal asymmetry in QCA (presence vs. absence equations via De Morgan) is related to but not identical with Lieberson's [[asymmetrical-causation|asymmetrical / irreversible causation]] (*[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*, 1985) — different formalizations of direction-dependence. Ragin himself frames the reduced equations as delineating the **scope conditions** of causal variables rather than as mechanical confirmations or rejections of theories.
