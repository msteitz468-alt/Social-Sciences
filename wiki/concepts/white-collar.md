## Definition as Coined

"White collar" names the stratum of salaried, non-manual employees — managers,
salaried professionals, salespeople, and office and clerical workers — who own no
productive property and sell their services to large bureaucratic enterprises. The
term derives from the white dress shirt of the office worker, contrasted with the
"blue collar" of manual labor. Its most sustained analytic development is [[mills-c-wright|C.
Wright Mills]]'s *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar: The American Middle
Classes]]* (1951).

Mills argued (attributed) that the defining fact of the white-collar worker is
structural ambiguity: *in property terms* white-collar employees stand "in exactly
the same property-class position as the wage-workers" (both propertyless), yet they
claim — and are conventionally granted — higher social [[status-group|prestige]]. The
category is thus "more definable in terms of their relative differences from other
strata than in any absolute terms."

## Semantic History

The term entered American usage around 1919. Mills's 1951 treatment turned a
descriptive label into an analytic object, and in doing so imported the German
Weimar sociology of the *Angestellten* (salaried employees) — Emil Lederer, Hans
Speier, and Siegfried Kracauer's *The Salaried Masses* (1930) — whose authors had
worried about the salaried stratum's vulnerability to fascism. Mills reworked their
concerns for the American case.

Mills himself flagged the category as a **residual, negative construct**, quoting
Lederer and Marschak that the white-collar group "can be comprehended as an entity
only in contradistinction to the other classes." In his afterword to the 2002
edition, Russell Jacoby notes that as blue-collar work shrank the category grew "even
more elastic or elusive," until "white-collar" survives in general usage chiefly in
the phrase "white-collar crime."

## Operationalizations

Mills built his white-collar statistics by reclassifying Alba Edwards's census
occupational data (*Comparative Occupational Statistics for the U.S., 1870–1940*).
The white-collar/wage-worker line rested on "non-commodity-producing" character plus
Bureau of Labor Statistics criteria: fixed periodic payment, expected well-groomed
appearance, and street clothes at work. Mills cautioned that national occupational
figures were accurate only to within 3–4%. On this basis he found the white-collar
share of the total middle class rose from 15% (1870) to 56% (1940); the three
largest white-collar groups by 1940 were schoolteachers, salespeople, and office
workers.

## Applications

White-collar work is the locus of Mills's signature analyses: the [[personality-market]]
(the salaried employee sells personality, not just labor), the [[status-panic]] (the
insecurity of borrowed prestige), the rationalization of the office into a "white-collar
factory" (see [[rationalization]]), and the [[new-middle-class-politics-debate|political
formlessness]] of the salaried stratum. The category also anchors comparative work on
the salaried employee across national cases (Weimar Germany, Britain).

## Critiques and Limitations

The category's residual definition is its chief weakness: stretched to cover both
"money managers and telephone marketers" (Jacoby), bankers and social workers, it
loses discriminating power. Mills's own generalizations about a unified white-collar
condition have been questioned as the stratum internally diversified. His phenomenology
— the salesgirl's [[status-panic]], the clerk's alienated routine — was drawn from a
specific mid-century moment (128 New York interviews, 1946) and, as later studies of
affluent-but-insecure "white-collar sweatshop" managers show, does not straightforwardly
transfer to later configurations of salaried work.
