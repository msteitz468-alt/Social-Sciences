# Inside Meaning and Outside Meaning

## Definition as Coined

In *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985, esp. Ch. 4), [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]] distinguishes two senses of meaning in food consumption:

- **Inside meaning** — meanings acquired and conventionalized *within* rituals, schedules, meals, and the social group: what sugar (or tea-and-sugar) means to participants as they use it.
- **Outside meaning** — what consumption and its proliferated inside meanings signify for society as a whole and especially for those who rule it: how governors perpetuate status and profit from the diffusion of inside meanings and the consumption those meanings validate. Mintz: control of foods *and* the connotations they are made to carry can be "a means to pacific domination."

He insists the two must be analyzed together. Substances with inside meaning validate social events; they are historically acquired, culture-specific, and arbitrary as symbols (tea and sugar were once unassociated; colonial America replaced tea with coffee).

## Semantic History

Mintz's 1985 pair is the analytic charter for much subsequent food anthropology that refuses either pure symbolism (culture alone) or pure political economy (structure alone). Later food studies sometimes privilege one pole; Mintz's text keeps both load-bearing. Related but not identical: practice-theory treatments of everyday meaning ([[practice-theory]]); Veblenian display ([[conspicuous-consumption]]) as one pathway of inside-meaning formation under intensification.

## Operationalizations

Historical-anthropological: track who controls production, price, duties, and marketing (outside) alongside meal systems, ceremonial uses, hospitality, status display, and work-break practices (inside). Sugar imagery in literature and speech bridges both (Mintz: Indo-European *swād* underlies "sweet" and "persuade"; honeyed/sugared speech).

## Applications

- British sugar from court "subtleties" and medicinal spice to working-class tea break.
- Wedding cakes, funeral sweets, holiday confections as intensified ceremonial meanings.
- Political-economy pamphlets celebrating sugar as outside-meaning discourse of crown, capital, and "civilizing" empire.

## Critiques and Limitations

Risk of dualism if "inside" is residual culture. Gendered production of inside meanings (who cooks, serves, rations sugar in the household) is thinner in Mintz 1985 than in later feminist food studies. Comparative paths beyond Britain need separate research (Mintz notes French lag, U.S. imperialism briefly).
