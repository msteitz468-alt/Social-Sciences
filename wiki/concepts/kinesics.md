# Kinesics

Kinesics is the analytic framework for body motion as structured, patterned
communication, developed by [[birdwhistell-ray|Ray L. Birdwhistell]], whom the editors of
Gumperz and Hymes's 1972 *Directions in Sociolinguistics* credit as the "inventor of the
field." Birdwhistell's chapter in that volume, "A Kinesic-Linguistic Exercise: The
Cigarette Scene," is a worked microanalysis demonstrating the framework's core units and
methods.

## Definition as Coined

Birdwhistell modeled kinesics explicitly on descriptive and structural linguistics,
stating that "the theory and methodology of kinesics has been consistently influenced by
that of descriptive and structural linguistics" — specifically the Trager-Smith model.
By analogy to phone/phoneme, he proposed a hierarchy of units: the **kine** (an isolable
element of body motion), the **kineme** (a class of substitutable allokines), the
**kinemorph**, and the **kinemorpheme**. He further distinguished kinesic-proper behavior
from three other categories requiring separate treatment: **instrumental** behavior
(task-oriented acts, e.g. lighting a cigarette, not thereby devoid of signal value),
**demonstratives** (patterned illustrative gestures resisting strict linguistic analogy),
and **interactional behavior** (spacing and orientation phenomena, drawing on Hall's
proxemics and Scheflen's work).

## Semantic History

The volume's editors note (attributed, pp. 382–384) that kinesics' founding linguistic
model — the Trager-Smith descriptive-linguistic framework — had, by the time of
Birdwhistell's 1972 chapter, already been superseded within linguistics itself by
transformational-generative grammar, producing an acknowledged mismatch between
kinesics' methodological foundation and the linguistics mainstream. No further semantic
drift is recorded in the sources ingested so far.

## Operationalizations

Birdwhistell's operationalization in "The Cigarette Scene" analyzed an 18-second filmed
interactional sequence ("Doris and Gregory," 1956, from the unpublished multi-author
project *The Natural History of an Interview*) at "every third frame of a movie taken at
24 frames a second." He proposed six "tentative kinemes of juncture" (double cross,
double bar, triple cross, hold, single bar, tie) segmenting stretches of body motion
analogously to terminal junctures in phonology, and four **stress kinemes** (primary,
secondary, unstressed, destressed) tracking body-motion flow in parallel to linguistic
stress. He reported these track linguistic stress "in general" but diverge "in more than
20 per cent of the cases" — an impressionistic count from "several thousands of
exercises," not a formally sampled or controlled study. Birdwhistell explicitly hedged
that the relation of kinesic junctures to content shifts is "often but not always"
present and unresolved pending further research.

## Applications

Birdwhistell used kinesics to analyze a key utterance in "The Cigarette Scene" — Doris's
"I suppose all mothers think their kids are smart, but I have no worries about that
child's intellectual ability," containing two roughly one-second lags, and Gregory's
overlapping reply, "No, that's a very smart one." He supplemented the kinesic analysis
with a small informant-response sub-study: twelve women of comparable age and class to
"Doris" were shown a typescript of the utterance (eleven read it as standard "woman
talk," an apology-plus-pride pattern; one dissented), and a separate control group of six
produced systematic paraphrase substitutions on a five-minute recall test — a thin-n,
uncontrolled elicitation exercise, not a validated finding. Birdwhistell concluded that
"any discourse analysis, conversational analysis, communicational analysis, or
interactional analysis which would attend to but one modality — lexical, linguistic, or
kinesic — must suffer from... the assumption that the other modalities maintain a steady
or non-influential state," an explicit argument for multimodal analysis.

## Critiques and Limitations

Kinesics is marked here `contested: yes`. The 1972 volume's own editorial framing
(attributed to Gumperz and Hymes) describes the field as institutionally thin — dependent
on "a very few men" — and as methodologically contested even within its own small
research community: Birdwhistell, Norman McQuown, and Henry Brosin disagreed on how to
treat the same film corpus as of 1967. Birdwhistell himself stated an explicit
non-reductionist caveat: kinesic and linguistic suprasegmentals are "interdependent but
not equivalent" systems, and he rejected any claim that "the fuller channel carries the
'real' meaning" where the two diverge. The stress-kineme divergence figure (20%) and the
informant-response sub-study are both flagged in the source as impressionistic,
single-researcher or thin-n results, not controlled findings.
