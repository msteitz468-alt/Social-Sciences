# Earth-Beings (Tirakuna)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015|Earth Beings]]* (2015), [[de-la-cadena-marisol|Marisol de la Cadena]] translates Quechua–Spanish **tirakuna** (*tierra* + plural *-kuna*) as **earth-beings**: other-than-human beings who participate in the lives of **runakuna** (people, usually monolingual Quechua speakers) and who modern institutions “cannot know, let alone recognize” (attributed — Preface). Among tirakuna, **Apu** / *apukuna* can be the most powerful; **Ausangate** is the pivotal earth-being that is also a mountain in the Cuzco highlands.

Earth-beings are **not** symbols, beliefs about “nature,” or cultural representations of a shared physical mountain. De la Cadena’s load-bearing claim (attributed) is that they are participants in co-labored conversations, land politics, and ritual practice — present in the geometry of ethnography alongside documents, runakuna, and the anthropologist.

## Semantic History

Andean ethnography long recorded *apus*, *wamanis*, mountain lords, and *huacas*. De la Cadena’s move is to refuse the multicultural translation of these as **beliefs** about a single Nature and to place them in the [[ontological-turn|ontological-turn]] vocabulary of divergent worlds, while remaining partial-connection ethnography rather than pure philosophy. Related but not identical constructs: Moseley-type *huaca* cult; Amazonian nonhuman persons in [[animism]] / [[perspectivism]] — **associated_with**, not identity (Andean mountain–person relations are not jaguar–perspectivism).

## Operationalizations

No survey index. Presence is evidenced ethnographically: *k’intu* coca offerings blown toward named peaks; *despachos*; yachaq / paqu / altomisayoq practices; willakuy narratives; Mariano Turpo’s mediation between lettered archive and tirakuna; Nazario’s tourist “Andean shamanism” and NMAI collaboration. Ausangate is simultaneously earth-being and mountain visible from Cuzco — “more than one, less than many” (Haraway/Strathern via de la Cadena).

## Applications

- Mariano Turpo’s land struggle and [[cosmopolitics]] between lawyers and Ausangate (*Earth Beings* Stories 2–4); *despachos* as political pago; dual ceremonies with state and tirakuna.
- [[ayllu]] as socionatural collective of runakuna **and** tirakuna (Interlude 1; Story 3); *santa tira* / *pukara* / *inqaychu* at 1969 reform ceremony.
- Multicultural tourism and museum display as equivocations that enroll earth-beings as “culture” (Stories 5–6); NMAI photo is not Ausangate; *suq’a* ≠ ancestors.
- Mining conflict (Sinakara 2006): environment-frame victory that **withdraws** tirakuna from public negotiation (Epilogue).
- Anchor Andean case for the [[ontological-turn]] beside Amazonian Descola/Viveiros materials; related to [[munayniyuq]] (Ausangate as supreme owner of will).

## Critiques and Limitations

External critics of the ontological turn may re-read tirakuna as cultural belief; de la Cadena treats that reduction as itself a modern ontological commitment. Access for the anthropologist is mediated (she “could not know Ausangate” with usual epistemic tools — attributed). Single primary ethnographic source in vault so far; densify with Allen and other Andeanists when ingested.
