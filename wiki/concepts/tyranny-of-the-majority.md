# Tyranny of the Majority

## Definition as Coined

In Volume 1 of *[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*,
[[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] argued that the gravest danger of American democracy
is the **omnipotence of the majority**: because legislature, executive, courts, police,
press, and jury all ultimately answer to the same majority, a dissenter has no forum of
appeal — "to whom can he turn?" He distinguished this from mere *arbitrary power*: majority
tyranny can operate strictly through law and yet be tyranny, because "the maxim that in
matters of government the majority of a nation has the right to do everything I regard as
unholy and detestable." His general principle is regime-neutral: no earthly power — of a
majority, an aristocracy, or a king — should be unchecked, for "the seed of tyranny lies
there."

His most striking claim concerns tyranny not over the body but over the **mind**: majority
opinion so dominates that "I know of no country where there is generally less independence
of thought and real freedom of debate than in America." The despot of old chained the
body; the majority "leaves the body alone and goes straight to the spirit," saying: "You
will retain your place amongst men but you will lose the rights of mankind."

## Semantic History

The phrase predates Tocqueville (it appears in Madison and Adams), but he made it a
sociological diagnosis rather than a constitutional worry, tying it to
[[equality-of-conditions|equality]] and the absence of aristocratic counterweights. It
became a standard term in liberal and pluralist political theory (via J. S. Mill's "tyranny
of the prevailing opinion" and later minority-rights and pluralist arguments).

## Operationalizations

Not quantified. Tocqueville inferred it from institutional structure (short terms, weak
independent executive and judiciary, pledged delegates artificially amplifying majority
strength) and from impressions of intellectual conformity gathered among elite informants.

## Applications

The concept sets up Vol. 1's account of what **moderates** it: the absence of
administrative centralization, the legal profession and the bench/bar as an "aristocratic"
counterweight, the [[voluntary-association|freedom of association]], the jury as a civic
school, and religion. Tocqueville treats these as institutional palliatives, not principled
cures — and notably holds that they check *political* majority power while leaving the
*intellectual* tyranny of opinion essentially unchecked, an internal tension in his account.

## Critiques and Limitations

- The claim of near-total intellectual conformism is the most disputed: it generalizes from
  a short visit and genteel informants and sits awkwardly beside the fractious antebellum
  dissenting press. See [[tocqueville-tyranny-of-opinion-debate]].
- Tocqueville's own text is in tension over whether associations/juries/decentralization
  actually blunt majority tyranny (Ch. 8) given his claim (Ch. 7) that tyranny over thought
  is moderated by nothing.
