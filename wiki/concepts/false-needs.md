# False Needs (and True Needs)

## Definition as Coined

In *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964), [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]] distinguishes **true** from **false** needs. "False" needs, he argues (attributed), "are those which are superimposed upon the individual by particular social interests in his repression: the needs which perpetuate toil, aggressiveness, misery, and injustice." Their examples are "the needs to relax, to have fun, to behave and consume in accordance with the advertisements, to love and hate what others love and hate." Their satisfaction may be gratifying, but produces "euphoria in unhappiness." **True** needs he defines narrowly: "The only needs that have an unqualified claim for satisfaction are the vital ones — nourishment, clothing, lodging at the attainable level of culture," these being the prerequisite for realizing all other needs.

From the distinction Marcuse draws his argument (attributed) that consumer "freedom" is not freedom: "Free choice among a wide variety of goods and services does not signify freedom if these goods and services sustain social controls over a life of toil and fear — that is, if they sustain [[alienation]]." What matters is not "the range of choice open to the individual but... what can be chosen and what is chosen."

## Semantic History

The idea descends from Marx's account of the manipulation of needs and commodity fetishism and from the Frankfurt School's culture-industry critique; Marcuse gives it a Freudian depth via the instinctual economy of [[repressive-desublimation]]. The concept became a keystone of consumer-society critique and of green and "de-growth" arguments (Marcuse held that "pacification" requires *reducing* the overdeveloped standard of living; see [[pacification-of-existence]]).

## Operationalizations

None formal. Marcuse insisted (attributed) that "in the last analysis, the question of what are true and false needs must be answered by the individuals themselves — but only in the last analysis; that is, if and when they are free to give their own answer." He explicitly refused any external "tribunal" the right to decide, while conceding the paradox this creates for a theory of liberation whose addressees are, by hypothesis, not yet free — the problem of "who educates the educators."

## Applications

The concept grounds Marcuse's account of [[one-dimensionality]] (needs as the mechanism of "containment"), of the "welfare-warfare state" as "a state of unfreedom," and of [[repressive-desublimation]]. It feeds the [[revolutionary-subject-debate|debate over the revolutionary subject]]: if the working class's needs are administered, its consciousness cannot be relied on as the negation of capitalism.

## Critiques and Limitations

The true/false distinction draws the sharpest objection levelled at Marcuse (attributed): that deciding others' needs "false" is paternalist and undemocratic, presupposing precisely the privileged standpoint the theory claims no one may occupy — Marcuse's own "in the last analysis" caveat registers, without dissolving, the difficulty. Critics also note the normative thinness of the "vital needs" floor and the vulnerability of any list of "true" needs to the same historical drift the concept is meant to expose.
