# Chiefly Cycling

## Definition as Coined

**Chiefly cycling** is the repeated rise and collapse of chiefly authority within a region:
rank societies "shift back and forth between hereditary privilege and equality," and
paramount centers form, dominate their neighbors for a century or two, and then fragment.
The term is archaeologists' — *(attributed, [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery
& Marcus 2012]])* "archaeologists refer to such repeated shifts as 'cycling'" — and
Flannery and Marcus use ethnographically documented cycles to interpret the archaeological
record.

## Semantic History

The concept crystallized in Southeastern-U.S. and Mesoamerican archaeology (David Hally's
model of Georgia chiefdoms rarely holding peak strength more than one or two centuries;
David G. Anderson's simple/complex chiefdom oscillation). Flannery and Marcus ground it in
two ethnographic cases with **emic vocabularies** for the cycle:

- **Kachin** (northern Burma; Edmund Leach's early-1940s fieldwork): *gumlao* (all lineages
  equal) vs. *gumsa* (lineages ranked). Leach documented a 1940 factional split at Hpalang
  and predicted an eventual collapse back to achievement-based society.
- **Konyak Naga** (Assam; Fürer-Haimendorf, 1936–37 and 1962): *thenkoh* (unranked) vs.
  *thendu* (hereditarily ranked) villages. Here the collapse mechanism differed — a chiefly
  (*Ang*) lineage could simply die out as commoner numbers outgrew it, returning a village
  to achievement-based organization.

## Operationalizations

Archaeologically the cycle is read from **settlement hierarchies** (does a region have a
single paramount center, or several competing ones?) and from the **construction, burning,
and abandonment of temples and paramount residences**. Flannery and Marcus apply it to the
Formative Oaxaca sequence (San José Mogote and its rivals Huitzo and San Martín Tilcajete
building competing temples ~2,900–2,500 years ago) — explicitly flagging the Kachin/Konyak
comparison as **analogy**: *(attributed)* "it is likely that all the rank societies of this
era went through cycles like those of the Konyak Naga and Kachin."

## Applications

Beyond Oaxaca, the pattern is invoked for Southeastern U.S. Mississippian centers ([[natchez]],
Moundville, Etowah, the Coosa confederacy), Hawaiian chiefdoms (A.D. 1100–1400), and the
Maya lowlands (Nakbe → El Mirador → Calakmul supersession). Flannery and Marcus argue that
the *breaking* of the cycle — a paramount that consolidates rather than fragments — is one
route into [[state-formation|first-generation states]].

### Polynesian usurpation and political cycles (Kirch 1984, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch does not coin “chiefly cycling” as a term, but supplies a strong Polynesian process analogue: **usurpation** by prominent warriors and especially **junior collaterals** of ruling chiefs as a recurrent political theme (paradigm Hawaiian cases: ʻUmi over Hakau; Kamehameha over Kiwalaʻo), with Sahlins quoted that “usurpation itself is the principle of legitimacy.” Senior/junior opposition and *ariki*–*toa* rivalry over claims to *mana* structure status competition; usurpers still sought high-rank marriages to legitimize heirs. Diagrammatic island model (four phases from colonization/fission through full-land competition to territorial conquest and political consolidation) and Hommon-based **Hawaiʻi Island cycles ~AD 1550–1797** of conquest, amalgamation, and fission under dense populations and leeward dryfield limits. Late prehistoric Hawaiian politics also cycled via **makaʻāinana revolts** led by junior collaterals when paramounts “ate the powers of government too much” (Malo via Kirch/Sahlins) — expansion/contraction without fully severing kinship bonds between chiefs and people. Fits the family of cycling processes (rise, usurpation, fission, re-amalgamation) rather than the Kachin *gumlao/gumsa* achievement↔hereditary oscillation specifically. See [[hawaiians]], [[hereditary-rank]], [[state-formation]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Using ethnographic *gumlao/gumsa* and *thendu/thenkoh* dynamics to read prehistoric
sequences is hypothesis-generating analogy, not evidence (Evidence-Class rule 5). The
mechanisms differ case to case (egalitarian overthrow vs. demographic extinction of a
chiefly line), so "cycling" names a family of processes rather than one.

### Anderson's causal apparatus, and Pauketat's tautology critique (2007, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007]].)* David G. Anderson's *The Savannah River Chiefdoms* (1994 — a 459-page cut of an ~800-page Michigan dissertation) defines cycling as "fluctuation in administrative or decision-making levels" that is "an inherent aspect of chiefdoms," and proposes **16 causal factors** cascading from regional physiography/climate/resources through prestige-goods exchange and alliance networks to elite factionalism and succession, with the underlying driver "competition for prestige and power between rival elites." Pauketat's critique: the model risks **tautology** — the explanandum (macroscale political rise-and-fall) doubles as the explanans; "the macroscale pattern of political change is not the process." In his Ch. 5 conclusion he argues appeals "only to administrative cycling… do not come close to capturing" the community-formation histories he documents. He also undercuts the model's classic ethnological comparanda (Leach's Kachin, Southall's Alur) as artifacts of erased colonial-era history (multilingual demography, Shan royal intermarriage). See [[historical-processualism]].
