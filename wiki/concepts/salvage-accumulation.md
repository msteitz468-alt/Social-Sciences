# Salvage Accumulation

## Definition as Coined

In *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]* (2015), [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Anna Lowenhaupt Tsing]] defines **salvage** as taking advantage of value produced without capitalist control, and **salvage accumulation** as the process through which lead firms amass capital without controlling the conditions under which commodities are produced. Sites that are simultaneously inside and outside capitalism she calls **pericapitalist**. Supply chains, on this account, translate value across noncapitalist and capitalist regimes to the benefit of dominant firms *(Tsing 2015)*.

> "Salvage accumulation is the process through which lead firms amass capital without controlling the conditions under which commodities are produced. Salvage is not an ornament on ordinary capitalist processes; it is a feature of how capitalism works." *(Tsing 2015)*

## Semantic History

Distinct from classical **primitive accumulation** narratives that treat noncapitalist spheres mainly as historical prelude. Tsing presents salvage as ongoing and constitutive of contemporary capitalism — especially under global supply chains after the late-twentieth-century "shareholders' revolution," when U.S. firms shed internal production and contracted distant suppliers *(Tsing 2015)*. Related earlier figure: [[economy-of-appearances]] and [[capitalist-frontier]] in *[[tsing-friction-2005|Friction]]* (2005), which track resource-making and speculative performance rather than salvage per se.

Genealogy of illustrations she deploys: Conrad's ivory chain (violence translated as civilization); Melville's *Pequod* (indigenous harpooning skill salvaged into Yankee oil capital); Wal-Mart inventory legibility that lets lead firms ignore how goods are made *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Operationalizations

| Chain node | What is salvaged (Tsing) |
|---|---|
| Oregon foragers | Non-wage "searching," freedom trophies not yet alienated commodities |
| Open Ticket buyers | Performative competition translating freedom products toward export |
| Exporters/importers | Hours as pure inventory on tarmac — the conversion that counts for accumulation |
| Japan gift markets | Matsutake re-embedded as gift after brief commodity life |
| Factory skill examples | Skills learned outside the firm (e.g. sewing at home) appropriated at the plant |

Tsing denies that Open Ticket picking/buying itself is a capitalist formation: money changes hands but does not form investment; accumulation is downstream (Vancouver, Tokyo, Kobe) *(Tsing 2015)*. Matsutake is a capitalist commodity that begins and ends as gift, spending only hours as fully alienated inventory *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Critiques and Limitations

Reception themes (positions, not wiki adjudication): may under-specify class and state if over-extended; relation to informality literatures and to Marxian primitive accumulation contested; optimism about economic diversity (dialogue with Gibson-Graham) may underplay domination even while Tsing refuses "postcapitalist" safety in pericapitalist spaces *(Tsing 2015)*.
