# Atom of Kinship

## Definition as Coined

The **atom of kinship** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] term (from "Structural Analysis in Linguistics and in Anthropology," 1945; collected in *[[levi-strauss-structural-anthropology-1958|Structural Anthropology]]*) for the minimal unit of any kinship system. It is not the biological family but a structure of **four terms** — brother, sister, father (husband), and son (sister's son / nephew) — linked by two pairs of correlative oppositions such that "in each of the two generations there is always a positive relationship and a negative one." "This structure is the most elementary form of kinship that can exist. It is, properly speaking, the unit of kinship... the true atom of kinship."

## Semantic History

The concept arose from Lévi-Strauss's attempt to explain the **[[avunculate]]** (the special mother's-brother relation). Where [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] read the avunculate as a two-term relation determined by descent type, Lévi-Strauss argued it is intelligible only as one node in the four-term whole — "an interpretation such as Radcliffe-Brown's arbitrarily isolates particular elements of a global structure which must be treated as a whole." The maternal uncle is not a survival to be explained (against Lowie, Rivers): grounded in the [[incest-taboo|incest prohibition]] and the [[exchange-of-women|exchange of women]], "he does not emerge — he is present initially." The unit necessarily contains a relation of consanguinity, one of affinity, and one of descent.

## Operationalizations

Diagnosed by the law of correlation across the four terms: "the relation between maternal uncle and nephew is to the relation between brother and sister as the relation between father and son is to that between husband and wife" — so knowing one pair of attitudes lets you infer the others. The crude +/− notation Lévi-Strauss refined into four attitude-types: mutuality, reciprocity, rights, obligations. Tested comparatively (Trobriand vs Cherkess, Tonga, Dobu, Siuai) and diachronically (medieval Europe).

## Applications

The tractable test case that launched structural kinship analysis: it links the [[avunculate]] to [[alliance-theory]] and the [[exchange-of-women]], and models kinship as a system of relations (attitudes and terminology) rather than a genealogy of biological families. The avuncular relation, submerged in complex systems, "re-emerges... and tends to become reinforced each time the system... reaches a crisis."

## Critiques and Limitations

The model is Lévi-Strauss's own theoretical construct (attributed), not a demonstrated universal — the avunculate "has a wide distribution, [but] is by no means universal," and kinship "is a language; but it is not a universal language" (not every society makes kinship the primary regulator). Later kinship theory questioned whether a single four-term atom underlies all systems, and the androcentric framing (the woman as the exchanged term) draws the same feminist critique as the rest of [[alliance-theory]] (see [[exchange-of-women]]). Distinct from, but the theoretical companion of, the [[avunculate]] as a descriptive relation.
