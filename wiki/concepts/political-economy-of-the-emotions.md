# Political Economy of the Emotions

## Definition as Coined

The **political economy of the emotions** is [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s framing (the title
and argument of *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]*, Chapter 9) for the
claim that emotions — mother love, grief, mourning, resignation — are "not natural, universal, or private
but are historically and materially produced, distributed, and put at the service of the body politic."
It aims "to overcome the distinctions between 'natural' and 'socialized' affects, between 'deep' private
feelings and 'superficial' public sentiments," showing how emotion is shaped by political-economic context
as much as by culture. A São Paulo newspaper glossed the thesis as "Anthropologist Calls Mother Love a
Bourgeois Myth."

## Semantic History

The concept fuses Arlie Hochschild's "emotion work" ("emotion work… puts the mindful body at the service
of the body politic"), Elisabeth Badinter's historical argument that "mother love" is an ideological
invention correlated with the demographic transition, and the constructionist emotion theory of Catherine
Lutz and Lila Abu-Lughod (emotions as "historical inventions" and "rhetorical strategies" — "without our
cultures, we simply would not know how to feel"). It is the analytic capstone of
**[[maternal-thinking]]** and **[[mortal-selective-neglect]]**.

## Operationalizations

Scheper-Hughes operationalizes it through the semantics and ritual of grief on the Alto: the reservation of
*saudade* (bittersweet longing) for the individuated dead versus *pena* (distancing pity, "more appropriate
to… suffering animals") for infants; the near-absence of weeping at the death of infants under about eight
months ("*não tem história mesmo*" — the baby has no story yet); the perfunctory, music-less *velório de
anjinho* (angel-baby wake). She contrasts this point-for-point with North American clinically-mandated
grief norms (hospital booklets instructing bereaved parents to "see, hold, touch, and name your newborn";
"you have a right to cry") — both, she argues, are emotion norms serving different political economies.

## Applications

- **Two reproductive strategies.** She contrasts an "old" strategy (many births, selective investment in
  "best bets") with the "new" (few births, heavy per-child investment) — while warning that "investment"
  and "strategy" are themselves capitalist idioms, "a deep cultural distortion," not how Alto women think.
- **Critique of biomedical grief theory.** Freud, Bowlby, Kübler-Ross, and Lifton's stage models of
  "normal" and "disordered" mourning are recast (via Lutz) as a parochial "American ethnopsychology" that
  pathologizes both insufficient grief and non-Western norms.

## Critiques and Limitations

The strong claim that even *love* and *grief* are politically produced is philosophically contested — it
risks, critics note, dissolving any stable ground for cross-cultural moral or emotional comparison. The
most direct empirical challenge (Nations & Rebhun) holds that the "absence" of grief is a *mask* over real
feeling, which would blunt the concept's edge (see [[mother-love-scarcity-debate]]). Claims are attributed.
