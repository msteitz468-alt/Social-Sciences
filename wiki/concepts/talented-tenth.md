# Talented Tenth

## Definition as Coined

[[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]] uses **Talented Tenth** for the exceptional minority whose higher training supplies race leadership. The fullest programmatic statement is the essay **“The Talented Tenth”** (1903, in *The Negro Problem*; reprinted in *[[du-bois-essays|Du Bois: Essays]]*):

> “The Negro race, like all races, is going to be saved by its exceptional men. The problem of education, then, among Negroes must first of all deal with the Talented Tenth; it is the problem of developing the Best of this race that they may guide the Mass away from the contamination and death of the Worst, in their own and other races.”

In *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903), chapter VI (“Of the Training of Black Men”), the same phrase appears: by refusing “this Talented Tenth the key to knowledge,” no sane observer can expect them to “contentedly become hewers of wood and drawers of water.”

All theoretical and single-source claims remain **attributed** to Du Bois (1903).

## Core Program (attributed, “The Talented Tenth”)

Du Bois structures the essay as three tasks: (1) show historically that the Talented Tenth among American Negroes have been worthy leaders; (2) show how such men may be educated; (3) show their relation to the Negro problem.

**Education’s object.** Money-training yields money-makers; technical skill yields artisans — not necessarily men. Men require manhood: intelligence, broad sympathy, knowledge of the world and men’s relation to it — the Higher Education curriculum. Famous aphorism:

> “the object of all true education is not to make men carpenters, it is to make carpenters men.”

**Culture filtration.** Du Bois argues nations are not civilized from the bottom upward; culture filters top-down; the Talented Tenth rises and pulls the saveable up. Every isolated group needs “yeast.” Trade/industrial schools cannot replace the university’s transmission of culture across generations (attributed theory).

**Sequence.** He insists that to establish common and industrial school systems without **first** providing higher training of the best teachers is “simply throwing your money to the winds.” Industrialism “drunk with its vision of success,” he writes, still needs broadly cultured teachers of teachers.

**Closing levers.** “Education and work are the levers to uplift a people. Work alone will not do it unless inspired by the right ideals and guided by intelligence. Education must not simply teach work—it must teach Life.” The Talented Tenth must be leaders of thought and missionaries of culture among their people.

## Operationalizations

### From *Souls* ch. VI

- Northern Negro college graduates: 22 (1875–80) → 43 (1885–90) → nearly 100 (1895–1900)
- Southern Negro colleges: 143 → 413 → over 500 in the same periods
- By 1900 ~34 institutions of college grade; ~2,000 Negro bachelors; ~400 degrees from Harvard, Yale, Oberlin and leading colleges
- ~30,000 Black teachers making schools (including Tuskegee) possible

### From “The Talented Tenth” essay (denser tables)

- Negro college graduates to 1899: **1,914** from Negro colleges + **390** from white colleges; 2,079 men and 252 women
- ~**750** Negro college students enrolled; six important colleges: Atlanta, Fisk, Howard, Shaw, Wilberforce, Leland
- Cascade claim: colleges trained ~2,000 in Greek/Latin/math → they trained ~50,000 others in morals and manners → those taught thrift and alphabet to nine millions holding **$300,000,000** property (“most wonderful peace-battle of the 19th century”)
- Occupations of 1,312 graduates (Atlanta Conference, ~60% of graduates): Teachers **53.4%**; Clergymen **16.8%**; Physicians **6.3%**; Students 5.6%; Lawyers 4.7%; Government service 4.0%; Business 3.6%; Farmers/artisans 2.7%; Editors/secretaries/clerks 2.4%
- Function: “the group leader, the man who sets the ideals of the community”
- Property: 557 college-bred owned **$1,342,862.50** assessed real estate (1899); estimated group accumulations ~$10,000,000
- Living graduates **<3,000**; **<1,000** Negroes in college; of 164 schools for Negroes, **95%** of students do elementary/secondary work
- Commissioner of Education 1900: persons per million in secondary+higher education — whole country 4,362 (1880) → 10,743 (1900); Negroes 1,289 → 2,061 (share of average quota fell 30% → 24%); fivefold increase needed to equal national average

## Historical leadership ladder (essay)

Attributed examples Du Bois marshals: Revolutionary group (Phillis Wheatley, Paul Cuffe, Benjamin Banneker, James Derham, Lemuel Haynes); early nineteenth-century leaders and David Walker’s 1829 *Appeal*; 1831 Philadelphia Negro convention; Black abolitionists (Purvis, Remond, Pennington, Henry Highland Garnet [text: “Highland Garnett”], Sojourner Truth, Alexander Crummell, Frederick Douglass) as men who “made American slavery impossible”; post-emancipation political and moral leaders. Slavery is framed as “legalized survival of the unfit” nullifying natural internal leadership — period social-Darwinian vocabulary **turned against** slavery (attributed theory).

Du Bois rejects the “fashion” that leadership should have begun at the plow not the Senate as a “foolish and mischievous lie”: 250 years of plowing was vain till the war amendments.

## Applications

- Counter to industrial-only education and depreciation of higher learning → [[washington-du-bois-debate]]
- **Washington/Tuskegee paradox (essay):** demand for college-bred staff at Tuskegee (~30 chief teachers college graduates) should make Booker T. Washington firmest friend of higher training — yet one effect of his propaganda, Du Bois argues, has been to throw doubt on the expediency of such training for Negroes
- Church reform: college-bred preacher as reformatory force where the preacher was prewar group leader and the [[black-church]] greatest social institution
- Affinity with [[conservation-of-races]] race organizations (colleges, Academy) without identity of the two programs
- *Strivings* (1897) triad **work, culture, and liberty** together — sequential panaceas incomplete alone

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Elitism:** “Best” guiding “Mass” away from “Worst” naturalizes class hierarchy within the race; contested then and later.
- **Gender:** Essay and *Souls* ch. VI center college-bred *men*; women’s figures appear in graduate sex counts but leadership types are male-coded.
- **Historical specificity:** 1899–1901 institutional facts are date-stamped; not a timeless education formula.
- **Social-Darwinian and moral-uplift language:** keep attributed; do not promote to wiki voice.
