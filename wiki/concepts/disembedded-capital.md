# Disembedded Capital

## Definition as Coined

[[blanton-richard|Richard Blanton]] (1978) proposed the term for [[monte-alban]]: a regional capital deliberately established on neutral ground *disembedded* from the territory of any single participating polity, built to unite previously autonomous or partly autonomous communities. Blanton et al. (2022) cite comparative cases including Washington, D.C., Ottawa, Canberra, New Delhi, The Hague, ancient Jerusalem, and Brasília.

## Semantic History

Coined within the Valley of Oaxaca Settlement Pattern Project's processual milieu; retained in the 2022 second edition of *[[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca]]* but reframed from a functional-locational argument into a [[collective-action-theory|collective-action]] argument (a confederation solving a cooperation problem under military threat). The concept received some uptake (Adams 1991; Sabloff 1997, per Blanton et al. 2022) alongside sustained neoevolutionist criticism.

## Operationalizations

Archaeological indicators claimed by Blanton et al.: absence of prior occupation; agriculturally marginal location incapable of self-provisioning (implying negotiated external supply); anomalously rapid multi-origin in-migration; absence of any single antecedent center large enough to account for the population.

## Applications

Monte Albán is the type case; the authors extend the comparison to Greek synoecism (Athens) as analogy, not identity — see [[synoikism]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Sanders, Santley, Nichols, and Willey argued Monte Albán's location is explicable by agricultural (terrace/piedmont) advantages and a single dominant polity — no disembedding required; Blanton et al. (2022) counter that the ~2,000 mapped terraces were residential, not agricultural. Marcus and Flannery accept relocation but credit the San José Mogote elite rather than a neutral compact. The full dispute is recorded at [[monte-alban-founding-debate]].
