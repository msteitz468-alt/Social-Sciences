# Zomia

## Definition as Coined

**Zomia** names the highland massif of mainland Southeast Asia as a coherent historical **region of relative statelessness and refuge**. [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]] popularized and theorized the term in *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* (2009), taking it from **Willem van Schendel**, who identified the huge upland border area as distinctive enough for “Zomia studies.” Etymology (van Schendel via Scott): highlander term from Tibeto-Burman languages on the India–Bangladesh–Burma border — **Zo** “remote” (hills connotation) + **Mi** “people.”

Scott’s geographic working definition (attributed, 2009): virtually all lands above roughly **300 meters** from Vietnam’s Central Highlands to northeastern India, spanning five Southeast Asian countries (Vietnam, Cambodia, Laos, Thailand, Burma) and four Chinese provinces (Yunnan, Guizhou, Guangxi, parts of Sichuan) — about **2.5 million km²** with roughly **80–100 million** minority peoples. He confines Zomia eastward from the Naga and Mizo hills and Chittagong Hill Tracts; van Schendel would expand farther west.

## Operationalizations

- **Altitude / friction of terrain** relative to lowland padi cores (~200–300 m floor in Scott’s usage; niches e.g. Hmong often 1000–1800 m)
- **Relative statelessness** — rare, short-lived hill states on plateaus/trade nodes are exceptions
- **Comparable patterns** (not political unity): diverse hill agriculture, dispersal/mobility, rough egalitarianism
- **Temporal limit:** Scott insists his analysis **largely ceases after ~1945**, when distance-demolishing technologies (roads, rail, airpower, IT) crush friction of terrain

## Applications

Primary: *Art of Not Being Governed* / study [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study]]. Downstream: Scott’s deep-time hinterlands in *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* as extension of [[nonstate-spaces]].

Core thesis (attributed — Scott 2009): Zomia is the largest remaining region not fully incorporated into nation-states; hill peoples are best understood as **runaway, fugitive, maroon communities** who for two millennia fled valley state-making oppressions (slavery, conscription, taxes, corvée, epidemics, warfare) — not as living ancestors of wet-rice Buddhist civilization. Hill and valley **coevolve**; neither is intelligible without the other.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Contested as too large/heterogeneous a unit for single causal story.
- Risk of romanticizing autonomy; highland predation and hierarchy also exist (Scott notes exceptions and melancholy trade-offs).
- Four-way non-identity: ethnonyms (Hmong, Karen, Wa, etc.) are **not** fixed peoples = languages = genes; Scott’s constructionist ethnogenesis chapter radicalizes this.
- Post-1945 nation-state enclosure (“last great enclosure”) limits the thesis’s contemporary applicability by Scott’s own warning.
