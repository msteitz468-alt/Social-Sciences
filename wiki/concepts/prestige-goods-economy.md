# Prestige-Goods Economy

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed — Friedman & Rowlands 1977; Frankenstein & Rowlands 1978; applied by [[kristiansen-kristian|Kristiansen]] & [[larsson-thomas-b|Larsson]] 2005.)*

A **prestige-goods economy** (or prestige-goods system) is a political economy in which elite power rests on **controlling the supply of scarce valuables obtained through long-distance exchange** that are *required for social reproduction* — for bridewealth, alliance, initiation, funerary and other ritual payments. Because subordinates cannot marry, mature, or bury their dead without these goods, and because only elites command the external networks that supply them, control of *external* exchange converts directly into *internal* hierarchy. The model was developed by Jonathan Friedman and Michael Rowlands (and Susan Frankenstein) in the 1970s as a Marxist-anthropological alternative to purely local, [[chiefdom|redistributive]] accounts of ranking, and became a central tool of [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]] approaches in archaeology.

## Semantic History

### Ethnographic precursor — Gluckman on subsistence vs prestige (1965)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual]]*, Ch. II — not the Friedman–Rowlands model; an earlier Africanist dual-economy framing.)* Tribal economies aim at both **subsistence** and **prestige/power**. Limited tools and trade restrict hoarding’s utility for personal living standards; reputation is enhanced by **giving away** (Hogbin Solomon Islands quote). Ceremonial exchange goods (kula; potlatch asides) take symbolic value representing wider peace across tribal borders. Evans-Pritchard on the [[nuer|Nuer]]: the poorer the material culture, the more symbolic/social functions goods serve (cattle as nuclei of kinship). Media of exchange may move in relatively isolated circuits (Firth spheres). Distinct from archaeological prestige-goods **control of external valuables for elite social reproduction** (below) — do not equate without argument.

- **Friedman & Rowlands (1977); Frankenstein & Rowlands (1978):** formalized the model, the latter applying it to the Early Iron Age West-Hallstatt zone (elite control of Mediterranean imports).
- **Mary Helms (1979, 1988 *Ulysses' Sail*, 1993):** the influential companion argument that **geographical distance is itself a source of value and authority** — long-distance travel yields not just goods but *esoteric knowledge*, and "skilled craft" and access to the cosmologically distant confer legitimacy. [[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005|Kristiansen & Larsson]] lean heavily on Helms, arguing "an imitation speaks perhaps even more strongly than an import about the local significance of 'foreign' prestige goods" — locally-made foreign-style objects (faience, imitated Minoan/Mycenaean rapiers) being "foreign knowledge that is loaded with status."
- **[[kristiansen-larsson-rise-bronze-age-2005|Kristiansen & Larsson (2005)]]:** apply it to the Bronze Age, and track a shift over time from a restricted prestige-goods economy toward the **commoditization** of metal ("unit quantities of metal rather than as a restricted prestige item," possibly "proto-currency," after Shennan 1993) as production scaled up after 2000 BCE — i.e. prestige-goods control loosening into more commercial exchange.
- **[[kristiansen-kristian|Kristiansen]] (1982; developed in *Europe before History*, [[kristiansen-europe-before-history-1998|1998]]):** Kristiansen credits the term "prestige goods economy" to his own earlier work (Kristiansen 1982, applied to Corded Ware/Battle Axe cultures and the Bronze Age generally), alongside parallel independent formulations by Gilman (1981), Shennan (1986a), and Bradley (1984); he traces the model's genealogy explicitly to Friedman and Rowlands (1977, building on Ekholm's 1977 African case studies), its application to [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]] by Frankenstein and Rowlands (1978), and to the Roman Iron Age by Hedeager (1978b, 1987): "Prestige-goods societies, as a special type of archaic state formation dependent on the employment of foreign prestige-giving valuables to develop and maintain internal hierarchies, were first described by Friedman and Rowlands (1977)."

## Kristiansen 1998 — *Europe before History*: staple finance, hoard methodology, and elite cycles

*(Attributed — [[kristiansen-kristian|Kristian Kristiansen]], Europe before History, 1998; source [[kristiansen-europe-before-history-1998]]. Append-only layer; does not replace the Friedman–Rowlands / Kristiansen–Larsson framing above.)*

Following [[chiefdom|Earle]], Kristiansen distinguishes two ideal types of chiefdom/archaic-state political economy: **staple finance**, in which vertical relations of subsistence production and redistribution dominate and prestige goods are a dependent variable, and **prestige-goods (wealth) finance**, in which horizontal exchange relations dominate and are alone sufficient to establish control over labour and production. He treats the two as substantially convergent frameworks developed independently: "Wealth finance as defined by Earle has much in common with prestige goods economies as defined by Friedman and Rowlands." In pre-state societies he associates prestige-goods economies with "individualising, segmentary, 'pastoral' societies," and staple finance with "collective, territorial and 'agricultural' societies," and proposes that staple-finance chiefdoms tend to evolve into centralised archaic states while prestige/wealth-finance chiefdoms tend toward decentralised archaic states (after Friedman 1979; Friedman and Rowlands 1977).

He distinguishes two structural variants of prestige-goods circulation. In **tribal chiefdoms**, prestige goods move horizontally between peers through kinship networks without breaking those networks down, and centre–periphery relations stay indirect. In **archaic states or complex chiefdoms**, prestige goods instead establish a direct *vertical* relationship of dependency from centre to periphery and from chiefs to subordinates, cutting across traditional kinship — a new mechanism of political control that Kristiansen treats as marking the tribal-to-state transition: "In the former, prestige goods were used horizontally between peers... In the second case prestige goods were primarily used to establish a direct relationship of dependency."

Prestige-goods systems are, on his account, inherently **short-lived and expansive**: they rest on an externally derived value system that gives a rising elite a new power platform, but require a subsequent "consolidation phase" in which the foreign value system is translated into local idiom (myths rewritten, genealogies invented); elites who fail to secure locally-founded legitimacy generally cannot survive once cut off from the external centre — "prestige goods systems were often expansive and short lived." He links this to a shift in how prestige goods are consumed over time: in the initial change phase they appear mainly in individual burial display, while in the consolidation phase consumption redirects toward sanctuary/deity-directed deposition — meaning highly visible rich burials mark periods of *instability*, not stable consolidation. Underlying the whole system, per Kristiansen, is the logic of gift exchange itself: "the giver is always in a dominant position, as any gift has to be repaid in equal measure or with more" — inability to reciprocate can turn a client into a vassal or worse.

Kristiansen ties the **rise and fall of chiefly/warrior-elite centres** directly to the expansion and contraction of prestige-goods flows. Applied to the West Hallstatt "princely" centres, he endorses (while partly synthesizing with rival accounts) Frankenstein and Rowlands's (1978) argument that decline in Mediterranean trade undermined the Hallstatt kings' ability to sustain political support among vassals through prestige-goods redistribution, precipitating internal competition and warfare — a position he sets alongside Pauli/Bintliff's internal-revolt explanation and Daphne Nash's (1985) model of interdependence between wealthy agrarian centres and warrior peripheries. More broadly, he reads a macro-historical geographic shift in hoard deposition from eastern to western Europe over the Late Bronze Age as evidence of increasing competition and value inflation within the prestige-goods system, tied to pressure on established hierarchies and eventual transformation or collapse. In the book's synthetic conclusion he generalises this into a long-run cyclical model: Bronze and Early Iron Age Europe oscillated between sedentary, agrarian, hierarchical production/redistribution centres (staple-finance-leaning) and decentralised, pastoral, prestige-goods-exchanging warrior societies — explicitly framed not as evolutionary progress but as shifting dominance between two recurrent social strategies.

For reconstructing prestige-goods circulation from the archaeological record, Kristiansen proposes a **four-step hoard-analysis method** (Kristiansen 1974b): (1) whom the hoarded objects represent — a personal set, a smith/trader's stock, farmer's tools, or a communal gathering; (2) what they represent — a prestige deposit versus capital held for payment or distribution; (3) who deposited them and how — public ritual versus secret/hidden deposition; (4) where and why — the find context (wet or dry, settlement-associated or not). From this he derives a functional typology of hoards within the prestige-goods economy: scrap hoards (recycling/melting stock), production hoards for trade (identical new objects), distribution hoards (local or long-distance), personal hoards (multi-person sets of ornaments, weapons, tools), and community hoards (ritual gear or composite village assemblages).

## Operationalizations

Archaeologically inferred from: concentrations of exotic, long-distance valuables in elite burials/hoards; local imitation of foreign prestige forms; evidence that such goods circulated in restricted (elite) rather than general exchange; and settlement/burial hierarchies co-varying with access to imports. Distinguished from a [[redistribution|redistributive]] economy (where the center pools and returns *staple* produce) — here the load-bearing goods are *external* and *wealth*, not subsistence.

## Applications

Iron Age West-Hallstatt "princely seats" (Frankenstein & Rowlands); Bronze Age Europe (Kristiansen & Larsson): amber, gold, and metalwork moving between Nordic/Carpathian peripheries and Mediterranean centers, with elite power resting on controlling these flows and the [[twin-rulership|ritual and cosmological]] knowledge that traveled with them. Pairs naturally with the [[world-systems-analysis|centre–periphery]] framing of precapitalist interaction. In [[kristiansen-europe-before-history-1998|Kristiansen (1998)]] the model additionally organizes the [[nordic-bronze-age|Nordic Bronze Age]]'s elite metalwork (Nordic casting traditions combined with accepted foreign hammered imports) and the cyclical rise and decline of [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]] princely centres, framed throughout via [[centre-periphery|centre–periphery]] dynamics between agrarian cores and warrior peripheries.

### Kirch 1984 — Tonga–Fiji–Samoa *koloa* exchange and binding outer chiefs

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984, esp. Ch. 9 on [[tonga|Tonga]], following Kaeppler 1978 and Hjarno.)* The Tongan political-economic system extended to Samoa and eastern Fiji for **prestige goods and chiefly spouses**. Kirch (via Kaeppler) treats Tonga, Samoa, and Fiji as a larger social system while each remains culturally distinct.

**Flows (contact-era ethnohistory as Kirch synthesizes them).** Fiji → Tonga: large canoes, red feathers, decorated barkcloth, mats, baskets, sandalwood, sails, pottery. Tonga → Fiji: whale’s teeth (essence of Fijian wealth), Samoan fine mats, ornaments, barkcloth. Samoa → Tonga: fine mats (*kie hingoa*); Tonga → Samoa: barkcloth, sleeping mats, red feathers (often Fijian in origin). Parrot feathers marked high Tongan status (e.g. *pala tavake* head-dress of the Tuʻi Tonga). Most Fijian/Samoan imports were not utilitarian necessities scarce in Tonga (except large canoes — Tongan timber denudation made Fiji timber and *kalia* construction critical); they were valued as **\*koloa*** — prestige valuables for marriage alliances and ceremonial occasions (weddings, funerals, state/religious celebrations).

**Political function.** Acquisition of foreign prestige goods by the central polity **bound local chiefs to the political core**: tribute (*‘inasi* and other flows) inward to Tongatapu/Lapaha was balanced by outward distribution of *koloa* to outliers. Monopolization of prestige-good sources by paramount lines secured power over the multi-island system. Kinship alliances (paramounts as spouse-takers from Fiji and Samoa — Fale Fisi institutionalized Fijian marriage of the Tuʻi Tonga Fefine; Samoan women for high Tongan males) confirmed with exotic valuables. Tributary fleet demand intensified Tonga–Fiji canoe exchange after dual-paramountship integration of the archipelago. Kirch treats control of prestige-good exchange, with voyaging technology, as fundamental to consolidating the maritime [[chiefdom]] that by contact showed class stratification and multi-tiered political apparatus — a Polynesian ethnohistoric case of wealth-finance / prestige-goods strategy binding periphery to centre, not only an Iron Age European pattern.

## Critiques and Limitations

The model can over-attribute hierarchy to *external* control and understate internal production, agrarian surplus, and local agency; it risks circularity (exotic goods are read as prestige goods, whose control is then read as the source of the power that acquired them). Critics of Kristiansen & Larsson specifically charge that treating locally-made imitations as evidence of long-distance "knowledge transmission" lets almost any resemblance count as contact (see [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]). As with all [[world-systems-analysis|precapitalist world-system]] claims, whether "dependency" in the modern sense obtained in the Bronze Age is disputed (Edens & Kohl 1993: multiple centers, the ever-present option to raid rather than trade, and shared technologies made ancient centre–periphery relations weaker than modern ones).

## Cunliffe 1997 — West Hallstatt and Mediterranean prestige

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]], *The Ancient Celts* 1997; source [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]]. Append-only layer; does not replace Friedman–Rowlands / Kristiansen–Larsson framing above.)*

Cunliffe characterizes rich **Late [[hallstatt-culture|Hallstatt]]** burials as reflecting a prestige-goods economy: elite status maintained by manipulating rare valuables monopolized by paramount chieftains, who gift lesser-value items downward for goods and services; the system holds while exotic supply and monopoly hold. Support he cites includes graded burial status and manufacture of lower-value ornaments (brooches, bracelets) useful in gift exchange at Mont Lassois and the Heuneburg. Import range in Hallstatt D2–3 centres and princely graves (Vix, Heuneburg III, Hochdorf) is largely **wine-drinking equipment** of Greek *symposion* type — Cunliffe’s reading is adoption of the *symposion* idea as extension of indigenous feasting, not wholesale Hellenization. He rejects pure-indigenous intensification and pure Mediterranean-drive alone: for a brief window c.530–450 BCE Greek need for new partners and Hallstatt elite need for new aristocratic expression coincided along the **Rhône corridor** (Bragny-sur-Saône as port-of-trade). Mid-fifth-century collapse of the west Hallstatt core and flourish of the Marne–Moselle–Bohemia Early [[la-tene-culture|La Tène]] periphery is framed partly as disruption of that prestige-goods balance (Mediterranean trade readjustments, periphery producers’ appetite for southern luxuries, raiding against the core). In Cunliffe’s retrospect (c.750–450 BCE), West Hallstatt and Celtiberian cores intensify Mediterranean contact; luxuries facilitate elite expression without simply *causing* chiefdom development.

## Cunliffe 1997 — Later Iron Age reprise (raid → potlatch → Roman wine)

*(Attributed Cunliffe 1997.)* After c.200 BCE, with long-distance raiding constrained near Roman frontiers, Cunliffe argues elite display shifts toward potlatch-like conspicuous giving/consumption and control of commodities (especially Italian wine in Dressel 1 amphorae) articulated through [[oppida]] and coinage used for gift exchange and political legitimation rather than capitalist commerce. Late La Tène elite burials from Kent to Germany again illustrate a prestige-goods economy of feast equipment and imported wine kit in the later first century BCE — a structural rhyme with the earlier Hallstatt model under different Mediterranean supply conditions.
