# Self-Elimination (Unexamined Exclusion)

*(Attributed — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] & [[passeron-jean-claude|Passeron]], [[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-1990|*Reproduction*]], Book II ch. 3, "Exclusion and Selection.")* The central mechanism by which the school reproduces class inequality without visibly failing anyone: most of those excluded from each level of education "eliminate themselves before being examined." Also rendered "elimination without examination" and "unexamined exclusion."

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed):*

> "one begins to suspect that the functions of the examination are not reducible to the services it performs for the institution... as soon as one observes that most of those excluded from studying at the various levels of education eliminate themselves before being examined."

The concept redirects attention from the passed/failed binary (which "conceals the relation between candidates and non-candidates") to who never enters the pool. The mediating construct is **subjective expectation**: the internalized objective probability of success, differing by class, which converts objective class-linked chances into actual withdrawal behaviour. A "social category's collective chances constitute, through the process of internalization of the category's objective destiny, one of the mechanisms through which that objective destiny is realized" — an application of [[habitus]] to educational aspiration.

## Semantic History

- **Probability of candidature vs. probability of passing.** The empirical signature: "the inequalities between the classes are incomparably greater when measured by the probabilities of candidature... than when measured by the probabilities of passing." Class inequality lives mostly in who applies, not who fails.
- **Deferred elimination and relegation.** Working-class pupils who do enter disproportionately choose lower-tier branches with poorer onward prospects — "deferred elimination" and relegation into less valued tracks (see the Appendix's "translation" analysis in [[cultural-reproduction]]).
- **Critique of docimology and "wastage" research.** The concept is defined against technocratic research that treats dropout as "wastage" of "abandoned reserves of intelligence," explaining self-elimination by individual "lack of motivation" while ignoring what it owes to the school's own selection-and-concealment function.

## Operationalizations

- **The examination as legitimating device.** "Nothing is better designed than the examination to inspire universal recognition of the legitimacy of academic verdicts... since it leads the self-eliminated to count themselves among those who fail," while the successful attribute their success to personal gift.
- **French statistics (1961–62).** Manual workers' sons were 20.3% of *lycée* sixième intake versus 38.5% of the lower-tier CEG track; senior executives' sons 14.9% of lycée versus 2.1% of CEG — the class sorting occurs at entry and track choice, not at the exam. *(French educational system, early 1960s; do not generalize beyond frame.)*

## Applications

- **Grading bias.** Class bias is strongest in tests using "implicit, diffuse criteria" — the dissertation and the oral — where examiners judge "total persons" by style, accent, posture, and dress.
- **Technical vs. social selection.** Self-elimination lets the school "conceal its social function of legitimating class differences behind its technical function of producing qualifications" — social mobility of a few individuals thereby helping to *conserve* the class structure.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Over-determinism.** The reliance on internalized "objective probabilities" invites the charge that it denies agency to working-class students — the opening later contested by resistance theory (Willis); see [[cultural-reproduction-debate]].
- **Enduring influence.** The distinction between candidature and passing probabilities became a standard tool in the sociology of educational inequality and is among the book's least contested legacies.
