# Genderlithics

Analytical program-label coined as chapter title by [[gero-joan-m|Joan Gero]] (*[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* 1991) for research on **women’s roles in stone-tool production and use**, against the default equation of flintknapping with maleness.

## Definition as Coined

*(Attributed, Gero 1991.)* Genderlithics names the project of treating women as tool-makers and tool-users in lithic analysis — reconstructing gender relations mediated by material culture, not merely “mapping females onto” the record. The chapter epigraph (Boulding 1978) states the urgency of defining women as tool-makers and tool-users, which they have always been.

## Semantic History

1991 charter use in Gero’s chapter; subsequent gender archaeology extends “gendering” other technologies (ceramics, textiles) by analogy. Not a universal measurement construct — a research orientation within [[feminist-archaeology]].

## Operationalizations

*(Gero 1991, attributed.)*

**Problem diagnosis:** Stone tool fabrication cast as “exclusively male” (Thomas 1983); NSF-reviewer common sense; Man-the-Toolmaker historiography; experimental knapping literature virtually all male authors; microwear more gender-balanced among investigators; “utilized flakes” demoted relative to formal “tools,” systematically devaluing the expedient technology ethnoarchaeology and use-wear show to be real tools.

**Constraints examined (not automatic exclusions):** scheduling/childcare, access vs control of raw materials, upper-body strength (technique over force), social value of labor.

**Minimum criteria for modeling female participation:** assemblages from (1) dwelling/habitation areas, (2) **local** raw materials, (3) **expedient flake tools** — “probably… 90 percent of the archaeological record of stone tool manufacture.” Analytical dimensions: raw materials; degree of preparation; context of preparation/use.

**Case:** Huaricoto, Callejón de Huaylas, Peru (~2000 BC–AD 600): EIP midden shows ~96% flake tools / ~4% bifaces+points; local metashale dominates flakes — Gero models rising women’s household lithics as male prestige production shifts off formal stone.

## Applications

Huaricoto as Gero’s case; program claim extends wherever lithic users left habitation debitage and archaeologists privilege formal male-coded tool types. Cross-links to Jackson’s devaluation of ground stone (women’s plant processing) as parallel androcentric technology hierarchy.

## Critiques and Limitations

Gender attribution of individual tools remains underdetermined (Gero’s own point); risk of reverse sex-role stereotyping; ethnohistoric and experimental analogies carry their own androcentric filters; Huaricoto pattern is a model, not a sexed knapper census.
