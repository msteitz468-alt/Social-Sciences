# Space of Death

## Definition as Coined

For [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]] in *[[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-wild-man-1987|Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man]]* (1987), the **space of death** is a social and epistemological threshold — torture cells, rubber-forest killing grounds, fever-visions — in which extinction and illumination both become possible. He wrote that it is “important in the creation of meaning and consciousness, nowhere more so than in societies where torture is endemic and where the [[culture-of-terror|culture of terror]] flourishes,” and that one may think of it “as a threshold that allows for illumination as well as extinction” (attributed). Terror, he argued, is physiological **and** social: the space of death is “the mediator par excellence of colonial hegemony” where “the Indian, African, and white gave birth to a New World” (attributed).

The concept is not only carceral or genocidal. Part Two of the same book reuses the phrase for yagé and illness journeys (e.g. Florencio’s “walking in the space of death”) — the **same** threshold that rubber terror petrifies can, in healing montage, be walked through toward cure (attributed).

## Semantic History

- **Pre-Taussig cultural repertoire.** Taussig explicitly stocks the space with long Western underworld/evil imagery (Homer, Virgil, Bible, Dante, Bosch, Inquisition, Rimbaud, Conrad) and northwest Amazonian zones of vision, putrefaction, death, rebirth, and genesis. Conquest, he claimed, blends conqueror and conquered signifiers into a “common pool,” strategically out of joint with what they signify (attributed).
- **Critical-turn anthropology (1980s).** The term travels from Taussig’s Putumayo essays into wider literatures on state terror, torture, and colonial violence; often paired with [[culture-of-terror]] and [[epistemic-murk]].
- **Medical-anthropological extension.** In the same monograph, the space becomes a healer’s walking ground — wider than a “shadow-line,” fillable with words or emptied — used to spook stereotypes of “soul-flight” shamanism (attributed).

## Operationalizations

Not a measurement construct. Used as:

1. **Historical frame** for Putumayo rubber terror and analogous dictatorship torture (Timerman’s Argentine cells; Dorfman’s Chilean *Imbunche* allegory — as Taussig cites).
2. **Literary/ethnographic lens** for Asturias’s dictatorial Guatemala, Conrad’s Congo haze, and colonial travelogue “jungle.”
3. **Healing ethnography** for yagé nights and illness narratives in SW Colombia.

Related Marxian hinge: Taussig linked the space’s rupture of signification to [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]] — things become human and humans things under terror as under capital (attributed).

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Metaphor vs mechanism.** Critics of Taussig’s style may treat “space of death” as literary rather than causal; the book’s own Benjaminian method refuses “history as it really was” as the sole truth-mode (attributed program).
- **Scope creep.** Applying one Putumayo-and-dictatorship-derived frame to all colonial violence risks flattening regional difference — keep claims attributed and case-bound.
- **Identity caution.** The space is not a people, language, or genetic population; when populated with “Indian” images, those images are colonial creations under analysis ([[wild-man]]).
