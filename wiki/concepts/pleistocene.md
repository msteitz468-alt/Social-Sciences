## Definition as Coined

The Pleistocene is the geological epoch conventionally understood as the great period
of Quaternary glaciation, encompassing nearly the entire span of hominin evolution
from genus Homo onward. Per [[klein-human-career-1999]], the Pliocene/Pleistocene
boundary is conventionally placed near 1.75 million years ago, at the top of the
Olduvai paleomagnetic subchron; the early/middle Quaternary boundary is commonly
equated with the Matuyama/Brunhes paleomagnetic reversal boundary, dated to roughly
780,000 years ago.

## Semantic History

Klein notes (finding) that the Holocene — the present interglacial, sometimes termed
the "Present Interglacial" — is not climatically distinct in kind from earlier
interglacial phases of the Pleistocene; it is simply the most recent one. On this
basis, some specialists prefer to extend the term "Pleistocene" all the way to the
present, effectively equating "Pleistocene" with "Quaternary" as a whole, rather than
treating the Holocene as marking the epoch's true termination. Klein reports this as
an open terminological choice among specialists rather than a settled convention.

## Operationalizations

The epoch's boundaries are fixed using paleomagnetic stratigraphy (see
`potassium-argon-dating`, `paleomagnetic-dating`) rather than by biological or
climatic criteria alone, because polarity reversals are global and effectively
instantaneous events, giving a chronological marker usable across continents. Within
the epoch, finer subdivision rests on marine oxygen-isotope stratigraphy (see
[[oxygen-isotope-stratigraphy]]), which resolves dozens of glacial/interglacial
cycles that land-based stratigraphy cannot reliably preserve.

## Applications

Nearly all deep-time framing in hominin evolution studies — the emergence of
[[homo-habilis]], [[homo-erectus]], [[homo-heidelbergensis]], and [[neanderthals]] —
is anchored to Pleistocene chronology and, within it, to oxygen-isotope stage numbers.
The period tag `late-prehistory` (300k–50k BP) and `deep-prehistory`/`early-prehistory`
(older) in this wiki's temporal framework fall within the Pleistocene as defined here.

## Critiques and Limitations

The Pleistocene/Holocene boundary is a matter of convention rather than a natural
climatic break, since (per Klein) the Holocene is simply the most recent interglacial
and is not qualitatively different from earlier ones — a point of ongoing
terminological disagreement rather than a resolved dating question.
