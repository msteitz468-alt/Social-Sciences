# Thin Coherence of Culture

## Definition as Coined

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (attributed, "The Concept(s) of Culture," in *Beyond the Cultural Turn*, 1999; reprinted in *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*) introduces **"thin coherence"** to describe the degree of coherence a semiotic conception of culture actually implies. A Saussurean view requires that meaning rest on systematic relations among signs recognized by a semiotic community — but "this conception actually implies only a quite minimal cultural coherence — one might call it a thin coherence." It does **not** imply agreement in moral or emotional evaluation, shared community feeling, or any particular scope, depth, or boundedness.

The term is embedded in Sewell's larger move: culture should be understood as a **dialectic of system and practice**, not one or the other. "The important theoretical question is not whether culture should be conceptualized as practice or as a system of symbols and meanings, but how to conceptualize the articulation of system and practice" (he credits [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] for a parallel formulation). Culture is "the semiotic dimension of human social practice in general," relatively autonomous in its logic and its spatial configuration, "possessing a real but thin coherence that is continually put at risk in practice and therefore subject to transformation."

## Semantic History

Sewell first distinguishes two meanings of "culture" that are routinely conflated: culture as a **theoretical category** (an analytical aspect of social life, singular) and culture as a **concrete, bounded world** (a people's way of life, pluralizable as "cultures"). He argues the classic ethnographic model of "cultures" (Benedict, Mead, Malinowski, Evans-Pritchard, and Geertz on Bali) portrayed them as logically consistent, highly integrated, consensual, static, and clearly bounded — a model recent scholarship has "turned on its head." His five counter-theses: cultures are **contradictory** (Turner's Ndembu red symbolism; the Trinity), **loosely integrated**, **contested** (Scott's hidden transcripts; Sahlins on Hawaiian women and tabu), **subject to constant change** (Rosaldo's Ilongot), and **weakly bounded** (world religions, trade, colonialism, Appadurai's transnational forms).

## Operationalizations

Coherence, where it exists, must be **explained, not assumed**. Sewell argues much cultural practice is concentrated in powerful institutional nodes (religions, media, corporations, states) whose agents work not to impose uniformity but to **organize difference** — hierarchizing, encapsulating, excluding, marginalizing deviant practice — bringing disparate practices into semiotic relation; he likens this to a Gramscian hegemony. "Cultural coherence, to the extent that it exists, is as much the product of power and struggles for power as it is of semiotic logic."

## Applications

The concept licenses a historicized semiotics of culture (see [[transposable-schemas]] and Sewell's appraisal of [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]): if coherence is thin and contested, cultural change can be generated from within, at the sites where meanings are destabilized. It stands as a middle path between Geertzian culture-as-tightly-coherent-system and the sociological "toolkit" ([[culture-in-action|Swidler]]), which Sewell faults for abandoning any systemic concept of culture to gain causal-variable respectability.

## Critiques and Limitations

Recorded as position: some cultural-turn critics would push further to a fully decentered, boundary-less view of meaning; Sewell resists this, insisting "worlds of meaning" still have particular shapes that "hang together," if only partially and with shifting boundedness — whether called cultures, ethnoscapes, or hegemonies.
