# Health Care System (as a cultural system)

[[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]]'s model of the local health care system as a *cultural system* —
"much as language can be thought of as a cultural system linking thought and action, health care
systems can be considered cultural systems linking illness and treatment." Its fullest statement is
*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers in the Context of Culture]]* (1980).

## Definition as Coined

Kleinman analyzes health care "in the same way that political systems, religious systems, kinship
systems, language, and other symbolic systems are analyzed": "Health care systems are socially and
culturally constructed. They are forms of social reality." The model is deliberately **local and
clinical** (communities, neighborhoods, families) rather than macro-social — "a microscopic, internal,
clinical view."

## Semantic History

Kleinman traces the "medicine as a cultural system" idea from Rivers (1924) and Sigerist (1951) through
Victor Turner's Ndembu ritual analysis and Byron Good's (1977) semantic-network work, and adopts Eliot
Freidson's (1970) medical-sociological insight that "to understand any single component in health care,
one must locate it structurally within its social context." The three-sector model has become a standard
framework in medical anthropology and global health.

## Operationalizations — the three sectors

The system is "a local cultural system composed of three overlapping parts: the popular, professional,
and folk sectors" (all attributed to Kleinman):

- **Popular sector** — "the lay, non-professional, non-specialist, popular culture arena in which
  illness is first defined and health care activities initiated" (individual, family, social network,
  community). It is "the largest part of any system" yet "least studied": roughly **70–90% of all
  illness episodes are managed here** (in a Taipei survey of 115 families, 93% of episodes were first
  treated at home, 73% only there). It is the "nexus" and gatekeeper — lay people decide when and whom
  to consult, whether to comply, and whether care worked.
- **Professional sector** — "the organized healing professions," usually modern scientific medicine but
  including professionalized indigenous systems (traditional Chinese medicine, Ayurveda). Following
  Freidson, the modern profession "gained professional dominance… by forcing all other healing
  traditions to disband, submit… or retreat into the quasi-legal folk fringe" (Flexner Report, 1910).
- **Folk sector** — "non-professional, non-bureaucratic, specialist" healers, split into *sacred*
  (shamans/[[tang-ki]], Taoist priests, soul-calling) and *secular* (herbalists, bone-setters). Its
  efficacy Kleinman found "virtually" unstudied.

Patients move between sectors along the "trajectories of their illnesses," the popular sector forming
"an undifferentiated matrix" linking the more differentiated folk and professional sectors. Each sector
generates its own [[clinical-reality]], and the power "to legitimate a certain construction of reality…
is not equally distributed" — the professional sector is "paramount" because it is heavily
institutionalized.

## Applications

- Kleinman applies the model to both 1970s Taiwan (Western medicine, traditional Chinese medicine,
  popular care, sacred/secular folk healing) and Boston (scientific medicine, chiropractic, Christian
  Science, ethnic popular care), claiming cross-cultural generality.
- It frames the [[core-clinical-tasks]] as functions "of the system as a whole, not just the healer" —
  "the system as a whole… heals."
- It underwrites the critique of biomedical dominance and the [[medicalization]] thesis (Zola, Illich),
  which Kleinman adopts cautiously as "not systematically documented."

## Critiques and Limitations

Kleinman himself distinguishes his *local/clinical* model from macro political-economic models of health
(Navarro's Marxist analysis), and criticizes Illich for "attributing the failures of health care systems
solely to the machinations of the medical profession" as if independent of political context. The
three-sector scheme is an etic analytical grid; sector boundaries are explicitly "overlapping" and
blurred (a single person may be druggist, priest, shaman, and bone-setter). Later
[[critical-medical-anthropology]] argues the model still underweights political economy.
