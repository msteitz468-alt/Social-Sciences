# Huaca

## Definition as Coined

**Huaca** (Quechua *wak'a*) names sacred phenomena — mostly works of nature (peaks/*apu*, springs, rock outcrops) but also human constructions and mummified ancestors — in an animate landscape ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], attributed). Colonial sources at Chan Chan distinguish *huacas sepulturas* (burial/mausoleum platforms) from *huacas adoratorios* (temples of divinities).

## Semantic History

Spanish *huaca* also came to mean "idol" or "treasure burial," skewing looting and early archaeology. Modern usage restores the broader sacred-landscape sense while archaeologists still label major mounds "Huaca X."

## Operationalizations

Ethnohistory: ceque-linked shrines around Cuzco. Archaeology: platform mounds named Huaca del Sol, Huaca de la Luna, etc. — etic site names that preserve the emic word without proving identical theology.

## Applications

Foundation of Moseley's cosmos chapter; scales from local *ayllu* shrines to pan-Andean oracles (Pachacamac). Ancestor *mallqui* remain potent huacas requiring care.

## Critiques and Limitations

Do not treat every mound labeled "Huaca" as the same institution. Emic content is period- and community-specific.

### From D'Altroy 2002

[[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy (2002)]] treats *wak’a* as the units of a sacred geography that, among the Incas, took form as a four-part world converging at [[cuzco]], with hundreds of holy places joined in the [[zeqe-system]] — “the most elaborate ritual complex in the native Americas.”

**Local vs imperial.** Despite Cuzco’s might, D'Altroy reports that sixteenth-century religious practice among the general populace still revolved around local deities, shrines, and myths; most people believed their founding ancestor emerged from the landscape at a *paqarisqa* (pacarina). Local societies often embodied original ancestors in idols whose capture or destruction could spell disaster; progenitors were worshiped through sacrifices and periodic reopening of graves for offerings, some graves built with conduits for libations.

**Cuzco network.** Polo found Incas worshiped at more than 400 shrines in and around the capital; Cobo’s list of 332 *wak’a* (probably from Polo’s lost manuscript) includes positions, social affiliations, mythical history, and sacrifices. Sherbondy: more than one-third water/hydraulic. Lines radiating from the Sun Temple, most ~5–11 km with 3–15 shrines (Bauer); each line assigned to a Cuzco social group (*panaqa* or non-royal *ayllu*) on a ceremonial calendar. Common offerings on Cobo’s list: *mullu* (sea shell, especially rain rites), llamas, guinea pigs, precious metals, textiles, coca; human children rarely consecrated and mostly for Sun worship and critical events in the Inca’s life ([[qhapaq-ucha]]). Cobo: 31 of 332 capital-area shrines owed human sacrifice. Huanacauri and Qorikancha rank among the most exalted. The system was dynamic — at least 19 shrines associated with Pachakuti; even Wayna Qhapaq’s and Waskhar’s palaces were *wak’a* on lines (Rowe, Bauer via D'Altroy).

**Empire scale.** Albornoz found more than 2,000 *wak’a* in Chanka and Aymara lands of Chinchaysuyu alone; Polo ventured that every Andean town had one. [[qhapaq-ucha]] processions carried offerings (and, at highest sites, children) outward along straight paths to provincial shrines, with *khipukamayuq* and *villcakamayuq* keeping accounts. High-peak shrines claimed the sacred landscape of the southern Andes as well. Folk ecology (modern Ayacucho analogy in D'Altroy): snow peaks as home of *wamanis* controlling weather and owning flocks; summits as ancestor origin places for many late prehistoric societies (Reinhard).

### From Moore 2005 — huacas in procession

Via [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore (2005)]], ethnohistoric chroniclers record huacas as mobile ritual participants, not only fixed shrines. Cristóbal de Molina (parish priest in Cuzco, writing 1570–1584) describes the November **Ccapac Raymi** ceremony, whose processional circuits moved huacas from their temples, the Inca from his palace, and initiates from their tents, to the main Cuzco plaza (Haucaypata) and back out to hilltop shrines; at the close of one phase, participants "worshipped the huacas" upon returning to the plaza, after which priests "took the huacas back to their temples" (Molina 1964:39, via Moore 2005). Bernabé de Cobo (17th century) records that the shrine of Huanacauri — "among the most important shrines of the whole kingdom" — was commonly carried to war, "particularly when the king went in person" (Cobo 1990:74, via Moore 2005).

Rubinos y Andrade's account of the Sicán/Lambayeque founder Cium's palace records its transformation into "an eminent huaca" that appeared "as if it were virgin earth, and not man-made," until excavation revealed painted walls and wooden structure beneath — colloquially still called the House of the Cacique or Palace of Cium (attributed, Moore 2005; see [[sican-culture]]).
