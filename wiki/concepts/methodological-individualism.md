# Methodological Individualism

The doctrine that explanations of social phenomena should ultimately rest on
facts about individuals — their actions, beliefs, interests, and situations —
rather than on social wholes treated as explanatory primitives. It is a claim
about **explanation**, not (necessarily) about ontology or values; one can hold
that social structures are real and consequential while insisting that their
workings be explained through the individuals who compose them.

> Attributed voice — this is a contested methodological position, not a finding.

## Definition as Coined

The term is Joseph Schumpeter's (1908); the stance is often traced to Max Weber's
insistence that collective concepts refer to the actions of participating
individuals. [[coleman-james|Coleman]]'s *[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|
Foundations of Social Theory]]* gives it a sociological restatement as the
**internal analysis of system behavior**: a social system is explained by
descending to the actions and orientations of its component actors and composing
system behaviour back up (see [[micro-macro-problem|the micro–macro transition]]).

## Semantic History

The stance runs from Weber's interpretive sociology through Schumpeter's coinage to
Popper (*The Open Society*) and Hayek, and into [[rational-choice-theory|rational-choice
sociology]]. Coleman explicitly likens his "variant" to Popper's while noting his own
concern is social systems of any size. He is careful that the individual is the
"stopping point" for sociology but not for psychology, that the criterion for how far
an explanation must descend is **pragmatic** (sufficient if it grounds effective
intervention), and that [[corporate-actor|corporate actors]] can themselves be
treated as elementary actors for some purposes. It contrasts with Durkheimian
"social facts" and holism, which take collective phenomena as explanatory primitives.

## Operationalizations

As a research heuristic it appears in [[rational-choice-theory|rational-choice]]
modelling, analytical sociology's mechanisms, and microfoundations programmes in
economics.

## Applications

Coleman gives five points favouring "internal analysis of system behavior" over
system-level statistical explanation: data-adequacy problems with few large-N
systems; interventions occur at lower levels; internal-analysis explanations are
more stable and general; they are more "fundamental"; and they rest on a
"humanistically congenial image of man" that permits moral evaluation of systems
(his example: pure system-level theory cannot distinguish Nazi Germany from
Switzerland "in any evaluative sense").

## Critiques and Limitations

Emergentist and holist objections hold that social wholes have properties
irreducible to individuals, and that "individuals" are themselves socially
constituted (so the reduction is illusory). Coleman concedes that the macro→micro
relation is as important as the micro→macro (see [[micro-macro-problem]]) and that
interaction produces genuinely emergent phenomena — a "variant" that is far from
atomistic reductionism, which critics nonetheless charge it approaches.
