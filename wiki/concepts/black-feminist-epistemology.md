# Black Feminist Epistemology

## Definition as Coined

(Attributed to [[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]].) An alternative framework for assessing
knowledge claims, contrasted with positivist/Eurocentric science. Collins begins from the
premise (attributed) that "because elite White men control Western structures of knowledge
validation, their interests pervade the themes, paradigms, and epistemologies of
traditional scholarship," so Black feminist thought exists as **subjugated knowledge**.
Following Harding (1987) she distinguishes **epistemology** (standards for what counts as
true) from **paradigm** ([[intersectionality]]) and **methodology** (research principles).

Positivist knowledge validation, she argues, rests on four requirements: distancing
researcher from object; absence of emotion; exclusion of ethics/values; and adversarial
debate as the route to truth. Black feminist epistemology inverts these through **four
dimensions**:

1. **Lived experience as a criterion of meaning** — distinguishing knowledge from wisdom;
   those who have lived an experience are more credible witnesses than those who have only
   read or thought about it ("A heap see, but a few know").
2. **The use of dialogue** — knowledge claims are worked out through connected dialogue,
   not isolated reasoning; rooted in call-and-response and African communalist philosophy
   (bell hooks: dialogue as "humanizing speech").
3. **The ethic of caring** — individual uniqueness; the appropriateness of emotion in
   dialogue; and the capacity for empathy.
4. **The ethic of personal accountability** — a knowledge claim is assessed together with
   the character, values, and ethics of the person making it.

These become a framework for Black feminist thought only "when... politicized and attached
to a social justice project."

## Semantic History

Introduced in the 1st edition (1990); the 2nd edition (2000) links it to Collins's
companion monograph *Fighting Words* (1998) and sharpens its relation to
[[standpoint-theory]]. Collins deconstructs the category "intellectual" itself: Black
feminist intellectuals need not be academically credentialed (blues singers, Sojourner
Truth), and not all credentialed Black women are intellectuals in her sense.

## Operationalizations

Applied as a validation standard, not a research recipe: Black women agents of knowledge
must satisfy three sometimes-conflicting constituencies — ordinary Black women, the
community of Black women scholars, and dominant groups controlling tenure and publication.
Collins models the epistemology by writing in the first person ("we"/"our") and treating
scholarship and activism as a "both/and" unity.

## Applications

Grounds the whole of *Black Feminist Thought*; the four dimensions are exemplified together,
Collins argues, in traditional Black church call-and-response.

## Critiques and Limitations

(Attributed positions.) The framework invites the standard charges against situated
epistemology: relativism, and essentialism (that group location fixes a single valid
knowledge). Collins pre-empts one version herself: she **rejects** the standpoint
implication that greater subordination yields a purer vision — "by quantifying and ranking
human oppressions, standpoint theorists invoke criteria... that resemble those of
positivism." Her alternative is that truth is **partial and plural**: multiple groups speak
from situated standpoints, and "partiality, and not universality, is the condition of being
heard" (echoing Elsa Barkley Brown's "pivoting the center").
