# Culture (Tylorian concept)

## Definition as Coined

[[tylor-edward-burnett|E. B. Tylor]] opened *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871) with anthropology's founding definition:

> "Culture or Civilization, taken in its wide ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society." (Tylor 1871, Vol. I, ch. 1)

Three commitments are packed into it: culture is (1) a **complex whole** (an interconnected totality, not a list of traits), (2) **acquired** — learned socially, not inherited biologically ("as a member of society"), and (3) singular and graduated — Tylor equated "Culture or Civilization" and treated all societies as occupying grades on **one** developmental scale, "in so far as it is capable of being investigated on general principles."

## Semantic History

Tylor's is an **evolutionist, universalist, singular** concept: one Culture with higher and lower grades. This is the sense the concept has largely lost. [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] (*[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*, Ch. 9: culture as the structured totality of a people's reactions and activities) and his students pluralized it into **cultures** — distinct, historically-particular, non-ranked wholes, each to be understood on its own terms — severing culture from the evolutionary ladder and from the assumption of a single standard of advancement. [[kroeber-alfred-l|A. L. Kroeber]] (*[[kroeber-culture-patterns-processes-1963|Culture Patterns & Processes]]*, 1948 chs.) restates Tylor's learning clause ("capabilities and habits acquired… as a member of society") then pushes culture to a **[[superorganic]]** level: acquired, historically continuous, superindividual, explained primarily by other cultural facts (not race or individual organic constants), with [[ethos-and-eidos]] and multi-level [[culture-pattern|patterns]] as internal structure. Within the Boasian lineage, [[benedict-ruth|Ruth Benedict]] (*[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]*, 1934) recast a culture as an integrated **[[cultural-configuration|configuration]]** — a selection from [[the-great-arc-of-culture|the great arc]] of human possibility, elaborated into a consistent whole, "personality writ large" ([[configurationism]]); this holist, meaning-conferring sense fed directly into the later symbolic conception. Later usages narrowed further: culture as symbolic system (Geertz's "webs of significance"), as distinction and practice (Bourdieu; see [[aesthetic-disposition]], [[class-cultures]]), or as the whole "way of life" of a group (cultural studies). The German *Kultur*/*Zivilisation* contrast (Elias, [[hubs/theory/civilizing-process|The Civilizing Process]]) marks a divergent lineage. What Tylor made a natural-science object, twentieth-century anthropology made a plural, meaning-centred, relativized one.

**White's mid-century restoration of a singular scientific object:** [[white-leslie-a|Leslie A. White]] (*[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|The Evolution of Culture]]*, 1959, Ch. 1) redefined culture as "an extrasomatic, temporal continuum of things and events dependent upon [[symboling]]" — tools, institutions, beliefs, language, etc. — unique to the human species and requiring [[culturology]] as the science of culture-as-a-system. White claimed principle-continuity with Tylor's evolutionary science of culture while rejecting Boasian plural particularism as anti-theoretical; thermodynamic [[energy-law-of-cultural-evolution]] supplies his comparative axis ([[universal-evolution]]).

**Rosaldo's critical-turn "culture in motion":** [[rosaldo-renato|Renato Rosaldo]] (*[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]*, 1989) argued the classic concept — exemplified for him by Benedict's bounded, homogeneous "patterns" and by [[geertz-clifford|Geertz's]] and [[turner-victor|Turner's]] early treatment of culture as a "set of control mechanisms" — marginalizes conflict, improvisation, and change, and pushes **[[cultural-borderlands|cultural borderlands]]** to the edges. He held that culture pervades all conduct yet should be analyzed as **process** (agency, structure, events) rather than a static whole, and that classic ethnography rendered subordinate groups culturally "visible" while treating dominant groups as culturally transparent.

**Wagner's inventionist dissolution (1975):** [[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]] (*[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]*, 1975; rev. 1981) argued that culture is not an object the anthropologist discovers but something invented in the encounter: "an anthropologist 'invents' the culture he believes himself to be studying," and reciprocally — "He invents 'a culture' for people, and they invent 'culture' for him." Culture is never visible from within ("the culture in which one grows up is never really 'visible' — it is taken for granted") and becomes visible only through the contrast of culture shock; anthropology is "the study of man 'as if' there were culture," with culture as a necessary heuristic "prop." Wagner also traced the word's semantic career (Latin *colere*, "to cultivate" → agricultural refinement → the elitist "opera-house" sense → the anthropological sense as its "democratization"), arguing the marked/unmarked senses continuously derive from one another and that early anthropology's museum context produced a "wax museum" model of culture as finite, discrete assemblage — with Tylor's [[survivals]] doctrine making "the whole world of 'custom' into a gigantic living museum, which only anthropologists were privileged to interpret." His closing formulation: "Culture is what you make it." See [[invention-and-convention]] and [[reverse-anthropology]].

## Braidwood popularization of Redfield (1959)

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]*.)* Quotes Robert Redfield: culture as an “organized or formalized body of conventional understandings”; learned, non-genetic, group-specific ways of thinking, believing, living, and making tools. Used to ground the claim that standardized tool traditions evidence culture-bearing men.

## Stocking's historicization of the culture concept (1968)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race,
Culture, and Evolution]]*, essays 4 and 9 — the vault's standard account of how the modern
concept emerged.)*

Stocking's central historiographical claim, developed via
[[presentism-and-historicism|anti-presentist]] method, is that neither [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] nor [[boas-franz|Boas]] "invented"
the modern culture concept. He argues Tylor's 1871 usage lacked five elements crucial to
the modern concept — **historicity, plurality, integration, behavioral determinism, and
relativity** — and that Tylor used "culture" only in the singular ("Culture or
Civilization"), gradable and evaluative, closer to [[arnold-matthew|Matthew Arnold]]'s
humanist "culture" than to modern anthropology. Tylor "made evolutionary" the contemporary
humanist idea rather than founding the anthropological one (the "uses of invention").
Stocking traces two tributaries into the modern concept: Tylor's French-Enlightenment /
British-empiricist "science of civilization" and Arnold's German-transcendentalist /
Romantic integrative, "inner," way-of-life sense.

The decisive marker Stocking uses is grammatical: he reports finding **no instance of the
plural "cultures"** in Tylor, and none in American social science between 1890 and 1915
except in Boas's writing, with the plural appearing "with regularity only in the first
generation of Boas' students around 1910." He argues Boas did not arrive with the modern
concept but developed it gradually (c. 1887–1911) — "germinating and growing in" the older
word — chiefly as the **positive residue of his critique of the [[comparative-method]]**:
once the single evolutionary sequence was rejected, what remained was a plurality of
separate, historically-conditioned cultures ("before we seek what is common to all culture,
we must analyze each culture"). Stocking documents the shift textually — Boas revised
singular-evaluative "culture" passages of his 1894 "Human Faculty as Determined by Race"
into "civilization" for *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*
(1911), reserving "culture" for the plural, particularist sense — and flags an important
qualifier: **Boas was not a consistent relativist**, retaining a "general theory of
valuation" aimed at standards of "greater absolute truth." He casts the transformation as a
Kuhnian paradigm shift: Boas "did not define" culture but "create[d]... the context in
which the word acquired its characteristic anthropological meaning." Against Kroeber and
Kluckhohn's "cultural lag" narrative (Tylor founded the concept in 1871, then Boas
inexplicably delayed its clarification), Stocking argues there was no delay to explain — the
modern concept had simply not yet emerged, and Boas drove rather than hindered it.

## Operationalizations

The concept splits sharply by tradition over whether — and how — it is measured:

- **Trait- and element-based (comparative).** The Boasian trait-distribution surveys (Kroeber's Californian **culture-element** lists) and, at scale, George Murdock's **Human Relations Area Files** and *Ethnographic Atlas* operationalize culture as coded, tabulable traits for cross-cultural statistical comparison — the approach the [[comparative-method]] and Galton's-problem debates concern.
- **Configurational / qualitative.** In the [[configurationism|configurationist]] and interpretive lineages culture is deliberately **not** reduced to discrete variables: it is read holistically as a pattern, ethos, or "web of significance," described rather than measured (Benedict, Geertz).
- **Values / attitudes (survey).** Mid-century culture-and-values work (Kluckhohn; later Hofstede, Inglehart's value surveys) operationalizes culture as measurable value-orientations; Bourdieu operationalizes it as tastes, practices, and credentials indexing [[cultural-capital]].
- **Material (archaeological).** In archaeology, culture is operationalized as a **recurring assemblage of artifact types in time and space** — the [[archaeological-culture]] construct ([[culture-historical-archaeology]]) — inferred from patterned material remains, with the standing caution that this material "culture" is not straightforwardly a people, language, or gene pool (four-way non-identity).

## Applications

The concept is load-bearing across both wiki layers: it underwrites the comparative cross-cultural research programme (HRAF); the culture-and-personality school; **[[cultural-relativism]]** as a methodological stance; the archaeological definition of prehistoric groupings; and, in sociology, analyses of subcultures, [[cultural-capital]], and cultural reproduction ([[bourdieu-passeron-reproduction-study]]). It also migrates into public and policy discourse — the "culture of poverty" debate, multiculturalism, and the "culture wars" — where its scholarly plurality and its lay singular-hierarchical sense (closer to Tylor's) frequently collide.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Reification and boundedness.** [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]] and others charge that the plural concept turns cultures into discrete, internally homogeneous, billiard-ball "things," obscuring connection, flow, and power (*[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study|Europe and the People Without History]]*).
- **"Writing against culture."** Lila Abu-Lughod (1991) argues the concept's core work is to make and hierarchize **difference**, freezing the studied "other"; she and the [[writing-culture|Writing Culture]] critics urge discourses of practice and the particular instead (attributed).
- **Homogeneity vs. contestation.** As Rosaldo argues above, the classic concept marginalizes conflict, improvisation, and change and renders dominant groups culturally "transparent."
- **Identity conflation.** Equating culture with a people, a language, or a biological population is the recurrent error the [[archaeological-culture]] and [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate|migration]] literatures police; culture is learned and transmissible across those boundaries.
- **The nature/culture dichotomy** itself is contested (Descola, Ingold), as is the etic assumption that "culture" is a cross-culturally valid analytic given rather than a Western folk category.
