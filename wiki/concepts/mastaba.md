# Mastaba

Flat-topped, rectangular tomb superstructure — from Arabic *maṣṭaba* (“bench”) — used in Egyptological literature for the characteristic elite and early royal tomb form of the Early Dynastic and Old Kingdom, and for elite tombs surrounding pyramid fields.

## Definition as Coined

A mastaba is a low rectangular building over a burial (often with subterranean chambers and a chapel/serdab for cult). Lehner traces pyramid origins through Predynastic mounds and Archaic mastabas at [[abydos]] and [[saqqara]] before Djoser’s stepped stone complex (attributed, [[lehner-mark|Lehner]] *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|Complete Pyramids]]* 1997). Reisner’s principle (every grave implies a superstructure for marking and offerings) makes the mastaba the ordinary elite answer to the same problem the pyramid answers at royal scale.

## Semantic History

Emic tomb forms long predate the Arabic label; the term is an etic field label that became standard in excavation reports. Elite mastaba cemeteries (e.g., Giza Western and Eastern fields) remain a primary social-stratification evidence class beside royal pyramids. Pyramid Texts *Sakhu* (glorification) spells that mention sand tombs and mudbrick mastabas show the form was already archaic vocabulary by the late Old Kingdom.

## Archaic development (Lehner 1997)

*(Attributed — [[lehner-mark]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997]].)*

### Abydos royal pit-tombs and enclosures

1st-dynasty royal tombs at [[abydos]] are desert pit-and-mound graves with stelae pairs and valley “imitation palaces” — open rectangular mudbrick courts with niched façades (largest Khasekhemwy >5,000 m²). These courts are read variously as palace-display models, analogues of later valley temples (wadi as natural causeway), or precursors of Djoser’s niched enclosure wall, with the mounded grave eventually moving inside as stone mastaba then Step Pyramid. Architectural precursors inside the graves: Djet’s false flap-door; Den’s statue chamber as serdab prototype; Merneith/Den/Qa sequence of separate offering chambers → entrance passage → north-turned entrance (“within reach of early passage-mastabas and pyramids,” Petrie per Lehner). Superstructure debate: Dreyer hidden tumulus as primeval-mound magic plus low surface mound; O’Connor’s possible mudbrick “proto-pyramid” and boat fleet in Khasekhemwy’s enclosure.

### Saqqara cliff-edge niched mastabas

Along the high cliff edge of North [[saqqara]], 1st-dynasty builders laid a string of large niched-façade mastabas on the path from the Archaic settlement (capital “The Wall” / later *Ineb Hedj*, White Wall — [[memphis]]) into the plateau. Walter Emery (1936–56) excavated most; sophistication and size contrast with contemporary Abydos pit-graves.

**Royal vs official debate (positions, not wiki-voice):** Emery first assigned mastabas to officials (e.g. Hemaka, 3035) then to royalty on size/façade/sealings — leaving two apparently royal cemeteries. Counter-positions: Abydos as true royal burial (retainer stelae density; combined pit + valley enclosure larger than Saqqara mastabas); Saqqara as high-official tombs (Nomarch, Treasurer, etc. sealings nearly as common as kings’; Merka stela at 3505).

**Material signature:** Recessed panels painted yellow (wood) with mat-pattern faces simulating Per Wer / Per Nu reed-mat shrines; broader niches as Netherworld contact points (offering dishes still in place in 3503); hundreds of clay ox heads with real horns unique to 1st-dynasty North Saqqara (e.g. 3504). Mastabas as *simulacra* — false doors too narrow/low for exact real-door copies; niching “shows the construction” frozen in mudbrick.

**Pyramid-precedent features:**

| Feature | Mastaba example | Later pyramid parallel |
|---|---|---|
| Model buildings, terraces, boat-pit walls (simulated quay) | N of 3357 (Hor-Aha) | North mortuary temple / harbour |
| Developed north chapel | 3505 | Entrance / cult chapel |
| Great stone beams over burial chamber | 2185 | Pyramid chamber roofing |
| Stepped east-sloping corridor + first portcullis grooves | 3036 / 3035 (Ankhka, Hemaka) | Pyramid passage blocking |

### 2nd dynasty and beyond

Underground galleries south of Djoser (Hetepsekhemwy, Ninetjer) may once have been topped by long mastabas (Stadelmann); empty desert rectangles west of Saqqara may be valley-enclosure counterparts (dating contested: 2nd vs 3rd dynasty).

## Old Kingdom elite cemeteries and hybrid royal forms

- **Giza orthogonal mastaba fields.** East and west of Khufu’s pyramid, streets and avenues of a preconceived plan realize the unified cemetery begun west of [[meidum]] and the looser rows east of Sneferu’s [[dahshur]] pyramids (attributed, Lehner 1997). Reisner read the necropolis as a “community of *kas*” for Khufu’s court in the Afterlife.
- **Khentkawes.** Mastaba-like tomb (bedrock cube + masonry top, ~45.5 × 45.8 m, 17.5 m high) closes major royal sepulchres at [[giza]]; planned modular town along the causeway (attributed).
- **Shepseskaf Mastabat el-Fara'un** (South Saqqara): huge stone mastaba 99.6 × 74.4 m, ~70° outer slope, Buto-shrine form (vaulted top between vertical ends), small east mortuary temple, long causeway to unexcavated valley temple — royal burial that rejects the true-pyramid superstructure while keeping the complex package (attributed).
- **Iput.** Queen Iput’s monument north of Teti began as a mastaba and was transformed into a pyramid by her son Pepi I (attributed).
- **Later private pyramids.** 18th–20th dynasty Saqqara temple-tombs with mudbrick or stone pyramids at the west end of the chapel re-abbreviate the package; Nubian Aniba/Soleb tombs attach tiny pyramids directly to vaulted chapels.

## Operationalizations

- Plan/section typology (multi-room substructure, niched façade, stone vs mudbrick, portcullises, boat pits).
- Cemetery organization relative to royal [[pyramid-complex|pyramid complexes]] (orthogonal streets; rank by proximity to royal monument).
- Titles and sealings in chapel reliefs as administrative evidence.
- Construct history of “royal” vs “official” assignment (Abydos–Saqqara debate).

## Applications

Archaic Saqqara mastabas; Giza elite cemeteries; comparative non-royal burial beside [[pyramid-complex|pyramid complexes]]; genealogies of architectural features (false door, serdab, portcullis, stepped mound) feeding the Step Pyramid.

## Critiques and Limitations

“Mastaba” lumps heterogeneous plans across centuries; excavation quality varies wildly (early clearances destroyed stratigraphy; Emery’s royal ascriptions remain disputed). Do not equate mastaba form with a single social rank without titles/assemblage evidence. Missing Delta architectural precedents (e.g. Buto) may distort the apparent suddenness of Saqqara monumental niching.
