# Organizational Field

## Definition as Coined

[[dimaggio-paul]] and [[powell-walter-w]] defined the organizational field, in "The Iron Cage Revisited" (1983; ch. 3 of [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]]), as "those organizations that, in the aggregate, constitute a recognized area of institutional life: key suppliers, resource and product consumers, regulatory agencies, and other organizations that produce similar services or products." The field, not the individual organization or the dyadic exchange relationship, is the unit at which institutional homogenization (see [[institutional-isomorphism]]) occurs.

They argued that a field only becomes analytically visible once it undergoes **structuration**, a process (drawing on [[giddens-anthony]]'s structuration theory) with four parts: "an increase in the extent of interaction among organizations in the field; the emergence of sharply defined interorganizational structures of domination and patterns of coalition; an increase in the information load with which organizations in a field must contend; and the development of a mutual awareness among participants in a set of organizations that they are involved in a common enterprise." Structuration is prior to and productive of isomorphism: as a field structurates, its member organizations become progressively more alike.

## Semantic History

The term names a research object that several traditions had approached under different labels. [[scott-w-richard]] (ch. 7), citing DiMaggio (1986a), traced field-level analysis as superseding earlier organizational-set, organizational-population, and interorganizational-field models bound respectively by exchange, competition, or geography; he proposed "functional organizational field" as the preferred formulation, bounded functionally (exchange partners, funders, regulators, professional/trade associations) rather than geographically. Parallel terms noted across the volume include Hirsch's "industry system" and [[scott-w-richard]] and [[meyer-john-w]]'s "[[societal-sector]]" — Scott and Meyer's ch. 5 cites "organizational field" directly as a competing label for the same object, and DiMaggio (1986a) is quoted describing "the organizational field" as having "emerged as a critical unit bridging the organizational and societal levels in the study of social and community change," an advance over environments "described in general terms as turbulent, stable, or munificent."

DiMaggio's own later work (ch. 11 of this volume, on the professionalization of American art museums) showed field construction itself as a **professional project**: museum professionals actively built the field's structuration — its interaction density, domination patterns, and shared understanding — prior to, and as a precondition for, the isomorphism the 1983 paper described. This reverses a naive reading in which isomorphism is a background force acting on a pre-given field; the field itself is a historical accomplishment.

[[fligstein-neil]]'s treatment of organizational fields (ch. 13, on the rise of the multidivisional corporate form) offers a contrasting, power-based definition: fields are understood as arenas in which incumbent firms with dominant "conceptions of control" shape strategy and structure to protect their position, foregrounding contest and power rather than shared cognitive/normative convergence — a partial counterweight to the more consensual DiMaggio–Powell picture.

## Operationalizations

Tolbert and Zucker (1983), cited approvingly by DiMaggio and Powell, tracked municipal civil-service reform diffusion across U.S. cities and found that "early adoption of civil service reforms was related to internal governmental needs and strongly predicted by such city characteristics as the size of immigrant population, political reform movements, socioeconomic composition, and city size," but that "later, adoption is not predicted by city characteristics, but is related to institutional definitions of the legitimate structural form for municipal administration" — a shift from local/technical to field-level institutional prediction over time, read as an operational signature of field structuration. A parallel pattern was reported by M. Meyer, Stevenson, and Webster (1985) in the bureaucratization of urban fiscal agencies: "strong relationships between city characteristics and organizational attributes at the turn of the century, null relationships in recent years."

## Applications

DiMaggio and Powell summarized three case studies elsewhere in the volume — Galaskiewicz (ch. 12) on corporate philanthropy in Minneapolis, Brint and Karabel (ch. 14) on community-college vocationalization, and DiMaggio (ch. 11) on American art museums — as "remarkably convergent in suggesting how power and interests shape the evolution of organizational fields," each documenting goal-oriented elite intervention at critical developmental points, construction of fieldwide organizations staffed by professionals, and contests between competing [[institutional-logics]].

## Critiques and Limitations

Brint and Karabel (ch. 14), quoted by DiMaggio and Powell, offered an internal critique: "Our difficulties with the new institutionalism ... have less to do with its tenets than with its silences." Community-college vocationalization diffused only after sixty years of failed advocacy, which they argue is explained by attending to "the pursuit of organizational self-interests" and "the role of group struggle in shaping organizational structures and policies" alongside institutional models — a call to keep power and interest analysis inside field-level accounts rather than treating fields as purely normative/cognitive arenas.
