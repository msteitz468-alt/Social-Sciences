## Definition as Coined

Weber defines the formal rationality of economic activity as "the degree of calculation that is technologically possible for that activity, and the extent to which it is actually applied" (Ch. 2 §9). Substantive rationality, by contrast, is "the degree to which the given supply of goods for particular human groups... organised through economically oriented social action, was, is, or could be subjected to particular evaluative postulates (however constituted)." Weber stresses that these evaluative postulates are "extremely ambiguous."

Monetary calculation represents, for Weber, the "maximum of formal calculability," which is what makes the formal-rationality concept at least relatively unambiguous by comparison with substantive rationality, whose possible grounding value-standards — ethics, politics, utilitarianism, hedonism, social rank, egalitarianism, socialist or communist standards among others — are, Weber argues, "in principle quite without limit." Weber treats socialist and communist value-standards as only one group among many possible substantive standards, not as a privileged case.

## Semantic History

Weber states as a general theoretical claim that "formal and material rationality... are in all circumstances quite distinct in principle, even if they might empirically coincide on numerous individual occasions" — their coincidence across all cases is conceivable, he argues, "only under entirely unrealistic assumptions" (Ch. 2 §13). He specifies the material conditions under which formal rationality of monetary calculation reaches its maximum: market-based contest among relatively autonomous economic units (in which, per Weber, "money is... primarily a means of struggle, and price is the outcome of this struggle"); maximal market freedom, understood as absence of both irrational compulsory and rational voluntary monopolies, itself socially linked to factory discipline and the appropriation of the means of production ("a relation of rule"); and effective demand (rather than mere "wants") as the material regulator of production, mediated by the distribution of income.

## Operationalizations

Weber lists eight conditions under which capitalist calculation achieves maximal formal rationality (Ch. 2 §30): complete appropriation of the material means of production to owners operating in a free goods market; full owner autonomy in selecting managers; a free labour market unencumbered by worker appropriation of workplaces; absence of material/statutory limits on contract; full calculability of technical production conditions; formally rational administration and law; maximal separation of enterprise from household wealth; and maximal formal rationality of the monetary order itself.

## Applications

Weber contends that capitalist calculation "in its most formally rational guise... presupposes the struggle of man against man," since it requires actually contested market prices rather than conventionalized ones. He argues this formal rationality carries a "specifically material irrationality": maximal formal calculability is possible only where the worker is completely subject to the rule of entrepreneurs (Ch. 2 §22).

## Critiques and Limitations

Weber engages directly the contemporary (1919–1920) socialist-calculation debate. He cites Otto Neurath's work on war-economy calculation as insistent but under-elaborated, and notes that Ludwig von Mises's 1920 essay "Die Wirtschaftsrechnung im sozialistischen Gemeinwesen" appeared only as Weber's own text was being typeset. Weber argues that under a thoroughgoing planned economy, the loss of monetary and capitalist calculation entails an inescapable diminution of formal calculative rationality — "the fundamental, and ultimately, inescapable irrationality of the economy is one of the sources of any 'social' problematic, and above all, for every form of socialism." He is careful to state that this technical argument does not "refute" socialism as an ethical (substantively rational) postulate, since "no science is capable of 'refuting' such a position" — formal and substantive rationality remain analytically distinct registers, not a hierarchy of truth over value.

[[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]] (*[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]*, 1989) draws the darkest inference from the distinction: he argues (attributed) that purely *formal* (instrumental) rationality is "singularly incapable" of preventing atrocity, because nothing in its rules disqualifies genocidal social engineering as improper — the Holocaust "did not clash at any stage with the rational pursuit of efficient, optimal goal-implementation." He distinguishes the **rationality of the actor** (self-preservation, subjectively reasonable) from the **rationality of the action** (its objective consequences), arguing that when the two diverge, reason becomes "a suicidal weapon" that dismantles moral inhibition.
