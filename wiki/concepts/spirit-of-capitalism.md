# Spirit of Capitalism

## Definition as Coined

In *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]*, [[weber-max|Weber]] uses **spirit of capitalism** (*Geist des Kapitalismus*) for the ethos of *modern* Western capitalism: an ethically coloured maxim of life that treats the increase of capital as a duty and end, combined with strict avoidance of spontaneous enjoyment of life, honesty/credit, industry, frugality, and **proficiency in a [[calling-beruf|calling]]**. He introduces it via Benjamin Franklin's advice ("time is money…") as a provisional document "in almost classical purity," chosen partly because free of direct religious relationship (Ch. 2).

Weber insists this is **not** unlimited greed — greed is ancient and universal; capitalism may even *temper* irrational acquisitive impulse (Author's Introduction). It is an *ethos*, not mere business astuteness (Ch. 2).

## Semantic History

- **Weber 1904–5/1920:** Specific to modern Occidental bourgeois capitalism; he states capitalism existed in China, India, Babylon, classical antiquity, and the Middle Ages *without* this particular ethos (Ch. 2).
- **Method:** Cannot be defined by genus/differentia at the outset; it is an **historical individual** — a complex of elements united from the standpoint of cultural significance; the definitive concept comes at the end of investigation (Ch. 2). Other standpoints would yield other essential characteristics.
- **Vs Sombart (notes):** Sombart treats the ethical aspect as *result* of capitalism; Weber's hypothesis reverses the order for the modern spirit.
- **Vs adventurer capitalism:** Promoters, war finance, booty, speculative finance exist widely; they are not the spirit of rational free-labour bourgeois capitalism (Author's Introduction).
- **Afterlife:** "Protestant work ethic" in popular speech is a simplified descendant; scholarly use remains contested (see [[protestant-ethic-debate]]).

## Applications

- Core of [[protestant-ethic-thesis]] and [[weber-protestant-ethic-study]].
- Explains why capitalistic *form* can exist without capitalistic *spirit*, and why spirit can appear before full capitalistic order (e.g. New England complaints of calculating profit-seeking by 1632 — Weber's claimed reverse of simple materialist order) (Ch. 2).
- Links to [[iron-cage]]: once the economic cosmos stands on mechanical foundations, the spirit's religious root may die while compulsion remains.

## Critiques and Limitations

Contested whether Franklin represents Puritan residuals or secular utilitarianism; whether the ethos is cause, effect, or elective affinity; whether pre-Reformation and Catholic sources already held similar maxims (Brentano, Sombart — Weber replies in notes: Alberti as worldly wisdom/*masserizia*, not salvation-sanctioned ethos). Ideal-type construction is accused of historical violence (Weber accepts the charge as methodologically necessary).
