# Ceremonial Fund

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 1), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] defines the **ceremonial fund** as the outlay a cultivator must make to underwrite the ceremonial that surrounds social relations. Kinship, community solidarity, and status claims are "paid for" in feasts, sponsorships, and ritual goods. Wolf notes that ceremonial funds of Indian villages in Mexico and Peru, for example, can loom large relative to replacement funds — a man may spend the equivalent of one year's local wages as sponsor in a community ceremonial.

## Semantic History

Wolf's fund scheme places ceremonial expenditure as a structural claim on product, not residual "culture" after economics. Connects to cargo systems and closed corporate community analysis (Wolf's own 1955/1957 papers). Later moral-economy and ritual-economy literatures continue the theme under other labels.

## Operationalizations

- Community fiesta / cargo sponsorship costs; life-cycle and agricultural-cycle rites.
- **Universal surplus-producing effect:** ceremonial size varies by culture, but everywhere the need for a ceremonial fund produces surpluses beyond the [[replacement-fund]].
- Reciprocal feast chains that also soften lord–peasant asymmetry under patrimonial domain (Homans via Wolf on English villagers; Christmas/Easter dues and return feasts).
- Escape from ceremonial fund (e.g., conversion to sober Protestantism in Middle American cases Wolf cites via June Nash) frees resources for economic ascent — one path of the production-increase strategy in the [[peasant-dilemma]].
- Peasantry's effort size is also a function of the larger system's division of labor — ceremonial and replacement costs are not purely internal.

## Applications / Exemplars (Wolf 1966)

- **Mexico and Peru:** ceremonial funds "very large" vs caloric and replacement budgets; sponsorship of community-solidarity ceremonials.
- **Middle America:** sponsor may expend ≥ one year's local wages; two to twenty times that noted in particular communities (Beals Cherán; Guiteras-Holmes Tzotzil; Tax *Penny Capitalism*; Stein Hualcan for Andes).

## Applications

- Explains "irrational" ritual expense as social insurance and membership cost.
- Links ideology/ceremonial chapters to household economy.
- Mechanism for leveling and for elite display inside peasant communities.

## Critiques and Limitations

- "Ceremonial" bundles heterogeneous practices; power-laden sponsorship vs mutual aid not always distinguished.
- Quantities are illustrative, not a comparative dataset.
