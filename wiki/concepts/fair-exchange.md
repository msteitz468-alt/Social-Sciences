# Fair Exchange (norm of fair rates)

## Definition as Coined

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] distinguishes two rates in any exchange. The **going rate** is an empirical, supply-and-demand equilibrium tendency — "not necessarily, or even typically, identical with what is considered a fair rate of exchange." The **fair rate** is a *normative* standard prescribing the return a given investment deserves; its violation "evokes social disapproval." Norms of fairness thus "superimpose a secondary exchange, of fairness for approval, on the primary one": dealing fairly earns approval, exploiting an advantage earns disapproval, and this secondary exchange tends to restore equity — though great power can suppress or immunize a party against the feedback.

Blau ties his "fair exchange" to [[homans-george-c|Homans]]'s rule of **distributive justice** ("the rewards of each man be proportional to his costs... and the net rewards, or profits... proportional to his investments") but faults Homans for treating fairness as a natural sentiment rather than deriving it from social norms that channel investment toward socially useful ends.

## Semantic History

Descends from the "just price" tradition and from Homans's distributive justice; recast by Blau as a norm regulating the exchange of social (not only economic) rewards, and linked to [[relative-deprivation]] (deprivation is felt against the fair rate) and to equity theory (Adams) in later social psychology.

## Operationalizations

Indexed by disapproval and withdrawn sociability: Blau reports that consultants who exploited their superior status won *less* acceptance as companions; and Patchen's finding that workers were more satisfied comparing upward to professionals than to fellow workers is read as fairness judged by proportional investment.

## Applications

The normative regulator of [[social-exchange]]; the hinge between exchange and [[legitimation]] (fair exercise of power → legitimating [[social-approval]]) and between exchange and [[opposition]] (exploitation, i.e. demands far exceeding the fair rate, → disapproval and resistance).

## Critiques and Limitations

- What counts as "fair" is itself shaped by the going rate and by power, so the fair rate can rationalize existing inequality as much as constrain it — a tension Blau notes when great power immunizes the powerful against fairness feedback.
- The norm is treated as broadly consensual; conflict theorists question whether fairness standards are shared or imposed.
