# Fictitious Commodities (labor, land, money)

## Definition as Coined

[[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]]'s central concept of Chapter 6 of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]*. A commodity, empirically, is an object produced for sale. **Labor, land, and money are not commodities in this sense** and their commodity status is therefore a fiction:

> "Labor is only another name for a human activity which goes with life itself, which in its turn is not produced for sale but for entirely different reasons... land is only another name for nature, which is not produced by man; actual money, finally, is merely a token of purchasing power which, as a rule, is not produced at all... None of them is produced for sale. The commodity description of labor, land, and money is entirely fictitious."

Yet the [[self-regulating-market|self-regulating market]] requires them to be bought and sold as commodities (as wage-labor, real estate, and priced money): "it is with the help of this fiction that the actual markets for labor, land, and money are organized." The danger is that treating the human and natural substance of society as commodities, if left unchecked, would destroy it — the "**satanic mill**" that grinds men and nature. Hence each fictitious commodity provokes a protective countermovement (see [[double-movement]]): **labor** → factory acts, trade unions, social insurance; **land** → land laws, agrarian tariffs, conservation; **money** → central banking and monetary management.

## Semantic History

The concept crystallizes Polanyi's argument that market society commodifies "man and nature." Polanyi explicitly **distinguishes it from Marx's [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]]** — in a footnote he states: "Marx's assertion of the fetish character of the value of commodities refers to the exchange value of *genuine* commodities and has nothing in common with the fictitious commodities mentioned in the text." (This non-identity should be preserved: fictitious commodities is about the *false commodity status of non-produced things*, not about the mystification of value in genuine commodities. See [[commodity]], [[commodity-fetishism]].) The concept was later taken up in critical geography and ecological economics (money and nature as fictitious commodities; the extension to "knowledge" and "carbon" by later scholars).

## Operationalizations

Not measured; identified by the presence of markets in labor, land, and money and by the characteristic **protective institutions** each generates. Polanyi treats the appearance of factory law, land law, and central banking as the empirical signature of the fictitious-commodity problem being managed.

## Applications

The engine of the [[double-movement]]: because the three fictitious commodities cannot be fully marketized without destroying society, their commodification is precisely where the protective countermovement concentrates. Money is the pivotal case — Polanyi argues "central banking... was essentially a device developed for the purpose of offering protection without which the market would have destroyed its own children, the business enterprises," and that managing money "drew monetary policy into the sphere of politics."

## Critiques and Limitations

- The claim that labor/land/money are categorically *not* commodities is contested by formalists and by economists who treat them as factor markets like any other (see [[formalist-substantivist-debate]]).
- The "satanic mill" imagery is rhetorically powerful but hard to operationalize as a testable mechanism.
- Do **not** conflate with [[commodity-fetishism]] (Marx) — Polanyi himself flagged the difference; the two name different problems.
