# General Linear Reality (GLR)

## Definition as Coined

[[abbott-andrew|Andrew Abbott]]'s name (attributed; *Sociological Theory* 6 [1988]: 169–86; collected in *[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]*, ch. 1) for the **theoretical back-formation** from the general linear model (GLM): treating the social world *as if* social causality obeyed the rules of linear transformations. He distinguishes:

- **Entailment use** of the GLM — cautious testing of whether a substantive mechanism entails patterns of relations among variables.
- **Representational use** — linear models treated as maps of how society works; this is what he calls **general linear reality**.

Abbott quotes Blalock (attributed via Abbott): “These regression equations are the 'laws' of a science” — reifying entailed mathematics into a representation of reality.

## Semantic History

The phrase crystallizes Abbott's reverse-engineering of standard methods after the 1980s computerization of linear models widened the theory–research split (attributed, ch. 1 opening). Related internal quantitative critiques include [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] on mock experiments and [[ragin-charles|Ragin]] on [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]] strategies; Abbott's target is the *ontology* implicit in variable-based practice rather than selection bias alone. Later papers in *Time Matters* elaborate particular assumptions (sequence and time-horizon in chs. 5–6; entities in ch. 9; univocal meaning in ch. 2).

## Operationalizations

GLR is diagnosed in the theoretical openings of empirical articles and in path/regression practice, not measured as a scale. Abbott (attributed, prologue and ch. 1) lists **six** deep assumptions (ontological/metaphysical, not merely statistical):

| # | Assumption (Abbott) | Rough content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Fixed entities with attributes | Units of analysis are stable things; variables are their changing properties |
| 2 | Monotonic / size-ordered causal flow | Causality large→small or equal temporal “size”; no fleeting causes of enduring outcomes (“Cleopatra’s nose”) |
| 3 | Univocal meaning | An attribute has one causal meaning in a given study |
| 4 | Weak sequence | Order of events largely does not matter |
| 5 | Casewise independence | Cases independent; structural determination among cases rejected |
| 6 | Independence of context / main effects | Causal meaning does not depend on location in attribute space; “main effects” reified |

Ch. 1 works these as Assumptions A–F with exemplars (interactionism and Marxian/Weberian traditions as counters; historical sociology as story-based causality).

## Applications

- Critique target for Abbott's constructive alternatives (epilogue): **demographic** program (flows of units — birth, death, merger, division; see [[entity-processes]]); **structural/network** program ([[network-analysis]], [[harrison-white|Harrison White]]); **narrative** program ([[narrative-positivism]], [[sequence-analysis]]); **multivocality** program (ch. 2 on ambiguity).
- Cross-link to [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]: Abbott addresses both quantitative and qualitative audiences without collapsing narrative vs analysis into one mush dichotomy.

## Critiques and Limitations

Abbott himself insists the GLM is a “formidable and effective method” when used for entailment (attributed); the critique targets representational reification. Event-history and standard-methods communities have pushed back on whether sequence methods offer a full alternative (Abbott reports such reception in the prologue and epilogue). GLR is a polemical ideal type of *practice*, not a claim that every quantitative sociologist endorses all six assumptions.
