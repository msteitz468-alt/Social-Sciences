# Tributary Mode of Production

## Definition as Coined

In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982, Ch. 3), [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] designates the **tributary mode of production** as the major way of deploying [[social-labor|social labor]] in which the primary producer (cultivator or herdsman) is **allowed access to the means of production** while **tribute is exacted by political or military means**. Wolf quotes Marx (*Capital* III): where the direct laborer remains "possessor" of the means of production, surplus-labor for the nominal landowner "can only be extorted… by other than economic pressure." Deployment of social labor is thus a function of the **locus of political power**.

## Semantic History

Builds on Marxian Asiatic/feudal discussions while **collapsing them into one continuum** of competition among nonproducers for power at the top. Wolf adopts the term from [[amin-samir|Samir Amin]] (1973b). Reifying "feudalism" as a separate mode, he argues, converts a short European period into a type case; the classic "Asiatic mode" suffers from an ahistorical East–West freedom/despotism contrast. Connects to Wolf's earlier [[fund-of-rent]] and [[types-of-domain]] in *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966): peasants under domain are paradigmatic tributary producers.

## Operationalizations

- **Power continuum:** strong apex elite (strategic production + coercion, e.g. waterworks + standing army; peasantry often ally against intermediaries) vs strong local overlords (intercept tribute; faction fights; peasantry seeks protectors).
- **Oscillations** within one mode (centralized phases vs "feudal-like" breakdown) — not two modes.
- **Civilizations:** cultural interaction zones pivoted on a hegemonic tributary society; ideological models claim supernatural validation of surplus-taker status and displace real domination onto deity–subject relations.
- **Merchants:** circulate tribute but are politically defined and often curtailed; mercantile wealth remains **wedded to the tributary mode** until industrial capitalism.

## Applications

Organizes Wolf's treatments of agrarian empires (China, India, Near East, Mesoamerica, Andes), European seignorial orders, Mughal/Company land-revenue systems, hacienda and *repúblicas de indios*, and many colonial hybrids where merchant capital articulated with political extraction without installing wage-labor capitalism.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Breadth: may obscure differences between feudal, Asiatic, and other forms that other Marxists keep distinct.
- Risk of reading all non-capitalist states as one type.
- Gender and household labor under-specified relative to later feminist political economy.
- Must not be equated with "Asiatic mode" as a civilizational essence — Wolf's usage is relational and historical.
