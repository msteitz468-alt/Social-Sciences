# Mongongo (*Ricinodendron rautanenii*)

## Definition as Coined

The **mongongo** (also mangetti; !Kung *//“xa*; Herero mangetti; Tswana mongongo—Lee prefers “mongongo” for literature currency) is a Kalahari tree (*Ricinodendron rautanenii* Schinz, Euphorbiaceae) producing a fruit and oil-rich nut that, in [[lee-richard-b|Lee]]’s Dobe research, was the single most important wild plant food of the local !Kung.

*(Attributed — Lee, *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979, Ch. 4, 6–7.)* Mongongo alone accounts for nearly **half** of vegetable-diet calories among Dobe !Kung; only 14 of 105 edible plant species make almost three-quarters of vegetable calories. Even among the most acculturated !Kung, mongongo fruit + nut remain **one-third to one-half** of vegetable calories. All Dobe !Kung Lee asked agreed it is their most important vegetable food—superabundant, near all water holes, year-round, easy to collect, tasty, highly nutritious; only meat rivals it. One informant’s ideal diet: meat and mongongo for strength, honey for sweetness, wild orange for refreshment.

Lee’s programmatic claim for a whole chapter:

> “Focusing on the mongongo serves to demonstrate the security and adaptiveness of hunter-gatherer life and helps correct the common misconception that the way of life of these people is precarious and unpredictable.” (Lee 1979:182)

Classic refusal of cultivation—informant **/Xashe**:

> “You could do that if you wanted to… but by the time the trees bore fruit you would be long dead.” … “why should we plant when there are so many mongongos in the world?” (Lee 1979:204)

## Semantic History

Became the emblematic “affluent forager” staple after *Man the Hunter* and *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* (Ch. 7). Anchors arguments that foraging can rest on **reliable, abundant plant staples**, not only scarce game—central to [[original-affluent-society]] and to downplaying pure hunting models. Botanically described by Schinz (1898); ethnographic centrality is Lee’s (with Marshall, Story, Maguire, Wiessner as supporting observers).

## Operationalizations

### Ecology and distribution (Ch. 4, 7)

- **Habitat:** only on crests of fixed dunes in red/reddish-white loose sand (dune association); also sandy rock-outcrop groves (Tsodilo, Aha, /Twihaba, N!um!koma)—**never** calcrete/clay/flooded soils. No standing water on dunes—only rainwater in tree crotches Nov–Mar near nut groves.
- **Range:** narrow subtropical Southern African belt (S Angola/Zambia/Mozambique N; Ovamboland/N Botswana/Transvaal S); especially Kalahari sand, ~1000–1500 m, roughly **350–700 mm** isohyets; /Du/da near southern edge; southern local limit follows Eiseb Valley.
- **Trees:** 8–12 m, trunks to 1 m; wood useless for building. Propagates from kernel; up to a dozen saplings under each producer; !Kung say fruiting starts after ~**25 years**. ~Half of trees fruit (“female” *//‘xa di*); half sterile (“male” *//xa!go*)—agrees with botanists (Story).
- **Fruit layers:** peelable skin; edible dry/spongy mesocarp (date-like) edible 6–8 months post-fall; very tough outer shell (~half fruit weight—barrier to commercial exploitation); thin testa; kernel ~hazelnut-sized, cashew/almond-like. ~1/20 twins; *tchoro* (“spoiled”) empty nuts (up to 10% some groves) signal forest decline.

### Nutrition (Ch. 7)

| Item | Value (Lee 1979) |
|---|---|
| Flesh | **312** Cal/100 g |
| Kernel | **640–655** Cal/100 g |
| Per kg whole fruits home | **>1400** Cal |
| Typical 12 kg backload | **11,000–17,000** Cal for collector and family |
| Kernel fat / protein | **58.4** g / **28.3** g per 100 g |
| 200 nuts/day | ≈ **2500** Cal + **77** g protein |

Lee ranks mongongo with the world’s most nutritious foods; also cites tsin (544 Cal, 31.6 g protein/100 g), marula (642 Cal, 30.9 g), baobab kernel (506 Cal, 34.1 g protein) as comparably rich wild foods. Explicit forager-richness thesis: “foraging peoples are in many ways more richly endowed with food than agriculturalists or industrial peoples” (Lee 1979:188).

### Seasonality and reliability

- Fresh fall begins April (dates: 13 Apr 1964 drought; 1 Apr 1968; 20 Mar 1969 after exceptional rains).
- **Apr–Sep:** major activity both sexes; nearest groves first; **no stockpiling** when access easy.
- **Sep–Nov:** ~half of ground fruits lose flesh to insects; sorting flesh-usable vs roast-to-crack.
- **Nov–Mar:** only polished nuts remain; distant groves + hollow-bole water open; groups disperse.
- Early fall: new fall displaces previous year; **≥1/3** uneaten prior-year nuts still edible after a year on ground.
- **Drought-hardy:** similar mongongo amounts at Dobe #toa in good 1967–8 and drought 1963–4.
- **Extreme rain can damage:** Maun 1973–4 **1183 mm**; Wiessner reported March–April 1974 crop drastically reduced (January storms destroying buds); recovery by 1975–6. Elephants in rainy years may break branches. Challenges assumption that more rain is always better (also R3/R4 climate claims).

### Grove inventory and production estimates

Lee inventories **35** mongongo groves used 1963–9. Each of 10 major water holes has ≥1 large grove within 10 km. Ranking: **A** = eaten out every year (**17/35**); **B** = some years only; **C** = rarely—B+C cushion lean years. Grove = *//“xa/to* (“face of the mongongo”); dunes */to* or *#toa* nearly synonymous with mongongo-going in speech.

**Rough production (Lee flags roughness—no complete tree census):** ~950 edible fruits/tree (~12,046 Cal); ~95,000 fruits / linear km; mean grove length 7.7 km; 35 groves ≈ 269.5 km → ~25.6 million fruits and 325 million Cal/year. Utilization-weighted ~201.45×10⁶ Cal. Standing population Dobe+/Du/da 1967–9 ≈ **550** → first estimate ~**1000 Cal mongongo/person/day**; revised to ~**800 Cal/person/day** (~**40%** of ~2000 Cal daily need) after seasonality and ~20% flesh loss to insects. Short-term camp share can range ~**90%** (grove camps) to **5–10%** (late rains); rarely falls to zero.

### Labor and technology

- Women gather **20–30 fruits/min** active collection; 3–5 dump cycles yield **10–15 kg** backload. On-site roasting/cracking can cut carry weight ~**75%**.
- Without **nut-cracking stones** (unimproved calcrete hammer/anvil cobbles) the !Kung could not exploit hard-shelled mongongo; stones distributed through nut forests—new camps pick them up from abandoned sites.
- Portable wooden **mortar and pestle** (*!gi*): pulverizes nutmeats with roots/greens/meat/hide; Lee ranks almost equal to fire for !Kung; recipes most frequent among older persons; mortar speech (*!gi kokxoie*) heard up to **8 km**—homing signal and primary audible sign of camp.
- Measured cracking: one woman **558 nuts / 156 min** = **3.6/min**; others 4–6/min active; estimate 200–300 nuts/hour; **40–60 min per kg**; 10–15 kg load = **6–15 hours** cracking.
- Households keep only **2–3 days’** supply. Flesh cooked to applesauce pulp (*sukari*); nuts revert to collector after guests suck pulp; roast in sand-coal 4–6 minutes; crack on anvil (perfect halves ~50%). Kernel as body oil in winter dry season.
- Drill base for fire-making often mongongo wood; tree water in mongongo hollows (1–20 L/tree/season) strategically near food-rich dunes (water-source hierarchy Ch. 5).

### Ownership and sharing

Trees/groves **not individually owned**; most associated with long-resident family/sibling units; permission to eat rarely refused; some Bantu-post-adjacent groves used without asking. Lee: “national resource,” widely shared at every processing stage. Stage-wise rules: flesh at owner’s fire (nuts revert); shelled nuts may be sent via children; mixed dishes free at owner’s fire, rarely beyond. When conflict breaks out, sharing breaks down—interruptions in nut flow or evening seating signal cleavage; persistent cleavage → camp split. 1968 /Xai/xai song complains people won’t give mongongo to throw in the mortar for uncle.

*/tau* bag request system: requester hands rolled own bag so collector fills specified amount—avoids embarrassment over 10/20/50% of load. Bag-filling age-asymmetric (older→younger).

### Propagation and non-cultivation

!Kung partly unconsciously propagate mongongo—saplings on abandoned reddish-white-sand campsites kilometers from groves from carried fertile nuts; elephants also disperse via dung. Plants mature only near established groves; few isolated mature trees. Explicit refusal to plant near permanent water given 25-year wait and wild abundance (/Xashe).

### Place in plant hierarchy (Ch. 6)

105 edible plant species (85 in drought years 1963–4 + ~20 more in good rains 1967–8). Classes: I primary = **1** (mongongo); II major = 13; III minor = 19; IV supplementary = 30; V rare = 19; VI problematic = 23. Mongongo is areawide major across !Kung localities (with /tan, !xwa, sha, tsin). Multiyear revision upgrades other species; at /Du/da 1968 *!goroshe* outranked mongongo. Regional “cuisine” differences: no two water holes share the same major-food constellation.

## Applications

- Anchors [[original-affluent-society]] and plant-primary forager models: security rests largely on abundant, local, predictable vegetable food (Lee 1979:158); game scarce and unpredictable though highly esteemed.
- Operationalizes [[foraging-mode-of-production]]: high-yield plant staple + sharing ideology → minimal storage; positive energy balance (mongongo collecting “makes a major contribution to the positive energy balance,” Lee 1979:192).
- Spatial strategy and camp mobility: “eat their way out” of grove radius week by week.
- Comparative: Tonga agriculturalists may use wild plants more intensively than !Kung—unused edible margin as index of foraging security (Scudder comparison, Ch. 6).
- Technology: cracking stones + mortar as enabling infrastructure for a wild staple.

## Critiques and Limitations

- **Region-specific:** mongongo abundance is not generalizable to all foragers; Central Kalahari poorer (Tanaka’s +Kade majors are minor/lesser at Dobe without mongongo/baobab/marula).
- Production estimates explicitly **rough** (informal tree counts, mean lengths, stacked utilization fractions, 550 population assumption)—keep attributed and provisional (Lee 1979:201–03).
- Extreme-rain damage and insect flesh loss show “reliable” is relative, not invulnerable.
- Processing costs (hours of cracking) matter for net return; much eating outside formal meals complicates consumption estimates.
- Ecological change, fencing, cattle, and sedentarization affect access; Lee notes habitat pressures and contact context.
- Do not equate botanical species with a “people”—mongongo is a plant resource within [[juhoansi]] ecology, not an identity label.
- Date-stamp grove use and calorie shares to 1963–9 (and Wiessner 1973–6 rain damage notes).
