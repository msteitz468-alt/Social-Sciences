# The Gardening State

## Definition as Coined

In *[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]* (1989), [[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]] argues (attributed voice throughout) that **modern culture is a "garden culture"** and the modern state a **gardener**. Where the pre-modern ruler was a *gamekeeper* — content to let society reproduce itself with minimal intervention — the modern state is a *gardener* "armed with a detailed design of the lawn," who "treat[s] as weeds every self-invited plant" and possesses "machines and poisons adequate to the task of exterminating the weeds." The gardener classifies every element of the social world by its relation to the design — useful plant, tool, raw material, or weed — and experiences weeding as *creative, not destructive*: "Modern genocide, like modern culture in general, is a gardener's job."

## Semantic History

The metaphor is Bauman's crystallization of a wider theme in social theory (the modern ambition to design society) and became a signature of his subsequent work on modernity and ambivalence. Bauman pairs the gardening metaphor with **medicine and architecture** as parallel figures of the modern order-building impulse: gardening and medicine each "separat[e]... useful elements destined to live and thrive, from harmful and morbid ones, which ought to be exterminated." In the 2000 Afterword he restates it — "uprooting the weeds to enable useful plants to grow" — and ties it to modernity's endemic incompleteness (obsessive *modernization* as the substance of modernity).

## Operationalizations

The gardening state is a diagnostic metaphor, not a measured construct. Bauman "reads" it from a regime's or a culture's *posture* toward social order: the presence of a comprehensive design for the "perfect society"; the classification of populations into cultivable elements and "weeds"; the treatment of removal/extermination as *creation* of order rather than destruction; and the availability of the administrative and technical means ("machines and poisons") to act on the design. It is indexed qualitatively through ideology (e.g. eugenic and hygienic rhetoric) and through the reach of the state's social-engineering ambition once freed from countervailing social powers.

## Applications

- **Racism and the Holocaust as extreme social engineering.** Bauman argues modern racism (distinct from pre-modern heterophobia) "comes into its own only in the context of a design of the perfect society"; victims of Nazi and Stalinist genocide "were killed because they did not fit... the scheme of a perfect society," and their killing was framed by perpetrators as *creation* of a better world. Human "weeds" carrying an irremediable (genetic or ideational) taint could not be reformed like malleable material, only removed. → [[genocide]], [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]]
- **Documentary grounding.** Bauman quotes the Nazi ideologist R. W. Darré using the gardener metaphor almost verbatim to justify a state "breeding plan," as evidence that the logic was explicit in Nazi ideology, not merely an analyst's analogy.
- **The dangerous moment.** Periods of deep social dislocation, when society appears "formless" and maximally malleable, are when the gardening impulse becomes most dangerous — "waiting for a vision and a skilful and resourceful designer to give it a form."

## Critiques and Limitations

As a metaphor rather than an operational construct, the gardening state is powerful but hard to falsify; critics of Bauman's broader thesis (see [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]]) argue it risks folding all modern administrative ambition into a single trajectory toward genocide, understating the specificity of Nazi ideology and antisemitism. Bauman himself cautions that "from the fact that the Holocaust is modern, it does not follow that modernity is a Holocaust": the gardening drive is normally checked by the pluralism of the social world, and produces catastrophe only when that check fails.
