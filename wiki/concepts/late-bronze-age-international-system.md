# Late Bronze Age International System

## Definition as Coined

The **Late Bronze Age international system** is an etic label for the unusually dense web of diplomatic and commercial relationships among Eastern Mediterranean and Near Eastern polities in the later second millennium BCE. [[cline-eric-h|Cline]] (2014) makes this system the *explanandum* of [[late-bronze-age-collapse]]: what failed was not one empire alone but an interdependent multi-civilization network.

## Semantic History

Amarna letters (mid-14th c. BCE) have long supplied the classic picture of a “Great Powers Club” (Egypt, Hatti, Mitanni, Babylon, later Assyria) plus Canaanite vassals. Ugarit and Hattusa archives extend the picture into the 13th–early 12th centuries. Cline popularizes the system as a rare premodern “globalized” world, drawing analogies (attributed to Carol Bell and Susan Sherratt — **not** [[sherratt-andrew|Andrew Sherratt]]) to modern interdependence.

## Operationalizations

- **Textual:** Amarna, Hattusa, Ugarit archives; Egyptian royal inscriptions; Linear B administrative texts (Aegean internal).
- **Material:** multi-origin shipwreck cargoes ([[uluburun]]); imported pottery and prestige goods; Aegean List of Amenhotep III.
- **Network types** Cline lists: diplomatic, commercial, transportation, communication.

## Applications

Baseline against which collapse is measured; supports multi-causal and systems-cascade models over monocausal invasion. Strategic tin supply (Bell via Cline: tin ≈ oil) illustrates dependency risk.

## Critiques and Limitations

- “Globalization” is metaphorical — scale and speed differ from modern capitalism.
- Elite correspondence over-represents palace worlds; coastal entrepreneurs and hinterlands less visible.
- Regional unevenness: not every polity was equally networked.

## The "Club of the Great Powers" (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] gives the system's fullest textbook treatment, explicitly building on **Mario Liverani**'s model (*Prestige and Interest*, 1990) — the "Club of the Great Powers" label is his rendering of Liverani's framework, and Liverani should be recorded as the named theorist behind the concept's modern analytical form.

**Membership and hierarchy (1500–1200 BCE).** The "Great Kings" were Kassite Babylonia, Hittite Anatolia (Hatti), Egypt, and — in the north — first [[hurrians|Mittani]] and then (from the mid-14th century) Assyria; Elam sat on the eastern fringe and Mycenae was harder to characterize organizationally. Small Syro-Palestinian city-states (Jerusalem, Damascus, Byblos, [[ugarit|Ugarit]], etc.) were buffer/client states rather than independent or fully annexed territories — Van De Mieroop attributes their intermediate status specifically to being "sandwiched" between rival Great Powers each strong enough to block full integration or full independence. Status was policed through address terms: Great Kings called each other "brother" (equals); Syro-Palestinian rulers were "servants" of a patron Great King and switched patron allegiance opportunistically. When Assyria's Assur-uballit I first wrote to Egypt as an equal, Babylon's Burnaburiash II protested angrily — quoted evidence of status anxiety within the system. (Attributed: Van De Mieroop 2016.)

**Evidence base.** The primary textual record is the **Amarna letters** (Box 7.1): 350 cuneiform tablets from Akhetaten/Amarna, ca. 1365–1335 BCE, most (~310) from Syro-Palestinian vassals to Egypt, ~40 between Egypt and peer Great Kings (Babylonia, Assyria, Mittani, Hatti, Alashiya/Cyprus, Arzawa). Written mostly in Babylonian, the system's diplomatic lingua franca; comparable 13th-century correspondence survives for Egypt/Hatti/Assyria/Babylonia and at Ugarit — the Amarna find is not unique, only best-preserved. Treaty types recorded include rare between-equals treaties (the 1259 BCE Hattusili III–Ramesses II treaty after the 1274 BCE Battle of Qadesh is the most famous, stressing full equality and mutual non-aggression) and Hittite Great-King-to-vassal subservience treaties. Fugitive-extradition clauses recur across both types, later linked (attributed) to labor flight driven by intensifying social stratification (below).

**Marriage and gift exchange.** Egyptian kings demanded foreign princesses but never sent Egyptian royal women abroad, a one-way rule that caused recorded peer frustration and could destabilize succession (e.g., Assur-uballit I's military intervention in Babylon after his grandson's murder). Gift exchange — not tribute — functioned as the system's glue: a reciprocity norm of roughly equal-value return gifts, circulating goods unavailable locally under a "gift" fiction. Egypt was the sole gold source and thus structurally central; complaints about gift stinginess are recorded verbatim from Babylonian and Mittani kings. (Attributed: Van De Mieroop 2016.)

**Trade beyond gift exchange.** Elite exchange is better attested archaeologically than textually: Babylonian cylinder seals at Thebes (Greece), and above all the **Uluburun shipwreck** (off southern Anatolia, late 14th century) — ten tons of Cypriot copper and one ton of tin (the correct 10:1 bronze-alloy ratio), ebony, Lebanese cedar, ivory, murex-dye shells, Canaanite jewelry, Cypriot pottery, and a jeweler's scrap hoard including a Nefertiti-named scarab, cargo so heterogeneous the ship's origin cannot be determined. **Ugarit** functioned as a key trade hub: vassal first to Egypt then to Hatti, with multilingual archives (Ugaritic, Babylonian, Hittite, Hurrian, Cypro-Minoan), resident foreign merchants, a bronze workshop, and a purple-dye textile industry; trade there was chiefly private/entrepreneurial rather than state-run, though states occasionally tried to restrict rival access.

**Warfare as proxy competition (Liverani's model).** Van De Mieroop reads Late Bronze Age warfare as aimed at readjusting relative power within the shared system rather than at destroying rival great powers: proxy competition over Syro-Palestinian vassals (Egypt vs. Mittani, then Egypt vs. Hatti after 1340 BCE) was the dominant mode; direct clashes occurred mainly where buffer zones were thin. Mittani is the sole Great Power to lose full independence in this era, reduced to a Hittite vassal under Suppiluliuma I. The Battle of Qadesh (1274 BCE) was the rare direct great-power clash — Egypt in fact lost despite Ramesses II's propaganda, and subsequently contracted its defended frontier.

**Shared elite culture.** An "international style" developed in elite art and architecture, blending local and foreign influences; Van De Mieroop analogizes elite Babylonian-language use among non-Babylonian courts to 18th-century-CE European elites' use of French — possibly a marker of fashion/status as much as scribal necessity.

### Source-reliability correction: the Ugarit "letters baked in an oven" story

Van De Mieroop explicitly debunks a popular reading of Ugarit's final destruction (ca. 1190 BCE). The excavator Claude Schaeffer had claimed that letters describing an approaching enemy fleet were found "baked in an oven" mid-composition, implying a single dramatic last-stand moment as the city fell. Van De Mieroop calls this "an archaeological phantom" — the surviving Ugarit-Alashiya-Carchemish correspondence (in which Ugarit's king Ammurapi commits ships to Hittite service and reports enemy ships at sea) was written on different dates, not composed in one dramatic instant. This is recorded here as a corrected historiographic claim, attributed to Van De Mieroop 2016, not adopted in wiki voice as a settled dramatic scene.

### Debate 7.1 — synchronizing Late Bronze Age chronologies (open dispute)

Van De Mieroop flags an unresolved chronology dispute crossing evidence classes: textual/king-list synchronisms across Egypt, Hatti, Mittani, Babylonia, and Assyria (anchored by the Amarna correspondence and the Ramesses II–Hattusili III treaty) support a "low chronology" (Kitchen 2007), while scientific dating (radiocarbon, dendrochronology, and Icelandic ice-core ash from the Thera/Santorini eruption) supports a "long chronology" placing the eruption roughly a century earlier, in the late 17th century BCE (Manning 1999; Manning et al. 2006). Van De Mieroop states flatly that "the debate appears to be at an impasse (Wiener 2003)": adjusting any one chronological anchor (an "ultra-short" Mesopotamian chronology per Gasche et al. 1998, or an earlier Egyptian New Kingdom start per Bietak 2003) forces incompatible reconstructions elsewhere in the system. This is recorded as an open, cross-evidence-class dispute (textual vs. scientific/environmental dating) per the wiki's Chronological Uncertainty Protocol and Evidence-Class Framework; no `debates/` page has yet been created for it — flagged here as a candidate should the wiki's Late Bronze Age chronology coverage deepen.
