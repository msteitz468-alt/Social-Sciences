# Sacrifice (gift, homage, abnegation)

## Definition as Coined

[[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] gave the anthropology of sacrifice its first systematic theory in *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, Vol. II ch. XVIII). His governing analogy: **"As prayer is a request made to a deity as if he were a man, so sacrifice is a gift made to a deity as if he were a man."** Sacrifice is thus a department of [[animism]] — commerce between persons and spirits modelled on human social exchange. Tylor classified its development along three lines of intention (attributed to Tylor throughout):

- **Gift-theory** — the deity literally receives, values, or consumes the offering; the primitive and developmentally primary form (Tylor's evidence: the overwhelming majority of sacrifices across all levels are food/banquet offerings).
- **Homage-theory** — the offering's material value dwindles into a token of deference or tribute; "a valuable offering compromised for a smaller tribute or a cheaper substitute, dwindling at last to a mere trifling token or symbol."
- **Abnegation-theory** — the point becomes the worshipper's own self-deprivation, independent of any benefit to the deity.

Along all three, Tylor traced "the usual ritualistic change... from practical reality to formal ceremony."

## Semantic History

Tylor argued **gift-theory is original** and abnegation a late derivative: "to take this abnegation-theory as representing the primitive intention of sacrifice would be... to turn history upside down." This directly anticipates and opposes the later, more famous theories: W. Robertson Smith (*Religion of the Semites*, 1889) made **communion** (the shared sacrificial meal binding worshippers and god) primary rather than gift; Hubert and Mauss (*Sacrifice: Its Nature and Function*, 1898) analyzed sacrifice as a social mechanism of consecration mediating sacred and profane, rejecting the search for a single original motive; Frazer, Freud, and later Girard (mimetic scapegoat theory) each offered rival syntheses. Tylor's gift/homage/abnegation typology remains a standard starting point, usually cited as the intellectualist opening position the sociological theories reacted against. He also mapped **modes of transfer** — material consumption, transmission by fire, blood, essence, or the "soul" of the offering — and the cross-cultural devices for economizing sacrifice (part for whole, substitute victims, effigies).

## Operationalizations

Sacrifices are sorted by the **intention** they express (gift/homage/abnegation) and the **mode** by which the offering reaches the deity. Tylor read **human sacrifice** as the clearest evidence for abnegation, "because the value to the offerer utterly exceeds the value they can be supposed to have to the deity" — a claim resting on atrocity material (Moabite, Phoenician, Aztec, Khond *meriah*, Nicaraguan child sacrifice) that he documented and that is treated here as attributed period ethnography drawn from his colonial-era sources, not verified fact.

## Applications

The theory anchors Tylor's account of religious development and fed the entire subsequent anthropology and sociology of sacrifice (Robertson Smith → Durkheim → Mauss and Hubert; see [[collective-effervescence]] for the Durkheimian lineage).

### Hubert & Mauss, *Sacrifice* (1898; attributed)

Primary source now ingested: *[[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-1898|Sacrifice: Its Nature and Functions]]* (French 1898; Halls English 1964/1981). Study: [[hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study]] · [[hubs/studies/hubert-mauss-sacrifice-study|Studies Hub]].

Hubert and Mauss reject the search for a single original motive (gift, totemic communion, scapegoat). They offer two linked definitions:

1. **Ch. I:** "Sacrifice is a religious act which, through the consecration of a victim, modifies the condition of the moral person who accomplishes it or that of certain objects with which he is concerned."
2. **Conclusion:** the unitary procedure is "establishing a means of communication between the sacred and the profane worlds through the mediation of a victim, that is, of a thing that in the course of the ceremony is destroyed."

**Role-set:** [[sacrifier]] (*sacrifiant* — beneficiary/subject of effects) ≠ sacrificer (officiant) ≠ victim (destroyed intermediary). Every sacrifice is a consecration, but not every consecration is a sacrifice; vegetable offerings count when destroyed. Type cases: Vedic animal sacrifice (foundation for the scheme) and the Hebrew system, with Greek and other comparanda.

**Scheme (procedure):** entry (sacralize participants/place/instruments) → progressive consecration and identification of victim with sacrifier → immolation → disposition of remains (holocaust, priest shares, communion meal) → exit ([[desacralization]]). Represented as a **curve** of rising then falling religiosity.

**General functions:** [[sacralization]] (impart sacred character; initiation/ordination) vs [[desacralization]]/expiation (remove charge or impurity — impurity itself a kind of consecration). Same elements, opposite ends; pure and impure sacred are not exclusive. Special functions (building, first-fruits, agrarian rites) reweight the scheme; **sacrifice of the god** is the limit case where victim and divinity coincide.

**Social function (Conclusion):** "Religious ideas, because they are believed, exist; they exist objectively, as social facts. The sacred things in relation to which sacrifice functions, are social things." Abnegation nourishes collective forces; the victim **redeems** the sacrifier by dying in his place. Contractual exchange with the gods (*do ut des* sense) is real because gods too need the profane share.

[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s 1964 foreword: conclusions "rather lame," grammar of the rite "superb." His *Nuer Religion* maps personal/piacular → desacralization and collective/confirmatory → sacralization (see below).

### Sacrifice and magic (Hubert & Mauss 1902–03; attributed)

The magic essay (*[[mauss-general-theory-magic-1902|A General Theory of Magic]]*; primary-ingested) was conceived as the sequel-test of the 1898 sacrifice study: "We have proceeded along these lines in our study of sacrifice… In order to verify and broaden the conclusions of our researches, we decided to make magic the subject of a second study." There [[hubert-henri|Hubert]] and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] make sacrifice one **pole of the magic–religion continuum** — "the pole of sacrifice and the pole of evil spells" — and observe that sacrifice inside magical ritual covaries with a society's religion: "if there is no sacrifice in religion it is also lacking in magic" (Cherokee, Australian cases), so sacrifice "does not form in magic, as it does in religion, a tightly knit class of highly specialized rites." See [[mauss-hubert-general-theory-magic-study]].

### Mesoamerican human sacrifice (Coe & Koontz 2013, attributed)

[[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz]] (2013) treat human sacrifice as a
deep-time feature of Mesoamerican religion, traceable (in their reading) from the Olmec,
attested archaeologically in the 200+ dedicatory warrior burials at [[teotihuacan|
Teotihuacan]]'s Temple of Quetzalcoatl, and elaborated by the [[aztec-empire|Aztecs]] in
service of a cosmology in which the sun required human blood to forestall its extinction
(see the [[flowery-war|Flowery War]] fought to supply captives). They describe five Aztec
modes: heart excision over a stone with the heart offered in a *cuauhxicalli*;
decapitation (customary for female goddess-impersonators); gladiatorial sacrifice;
sacrifice by darts on a scaffold; and fire-then-heart sacrifice.

**Scale is a live position, not a settled figure.** Henry Nicholson held Aztec sacrifice
was practiced "on a scale not even approached by any other ritual system in the history of
the world"; many scholars counter that the Spanish vastly exaggerated it to justify the
Conquest. Coe & Koontz call the reported 80,000 victims at the 1487 Great Temple
dedication "probably a physical impossibility," offering instead an explicitly untestable
estimate of "several hundreds, perhaps even a few thousand" a year in the capital, and
reject **Michael Harner's** protein-scarcity cannibalism thesis. This material rests on
Colonial-era sources with a documented political interest in depicting savagery — record
it as attributed period ethnography and position, never wiki-voice fact.

## Critiques and Limitations

Tylor's is an **intellectualist, individualist, gift-first** theory, and the sociological tradition overturned each emphasis: Robertson Smith and Durkheim made sacrifice social and communal in origin, not a private gift; Mauss and Hubert denied any single primitive motive is recoverable. The developmental ranking (gift → homage → abnegation as an evolutionary sequence) falls with unilinear [[social-evolutionism|evolutionism]] (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). The typology survives as description even where the evolutionary story is rejected.

## Evans-Pritchard on Nuer sacrifice (1956)

*(Attributed — [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]], *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-religion-1956|Nuer Religion]]*, 1956; fieldwork among the Nuer of the southern Sudan, 1930–36.)*

Evans-Pritchard restricted his discussion to **bloody sacrifice** (animal or cucumber-surrogate), excluding cereal offerings, thrown tobacco/beads, and beer/milk libations as distinct minor categories. His central typology, explicitly mapped onto **Hubert and Mauss** ("Essai sur la nature et la fonction du sacrifice," *L'Année sociologique* vol. ii, 1899): **personal/piacular sacrifice** (made for individuals, to prevent or curtail danger — illness, sin, twins, plague; Hubert & Mauss's "desacralization," ridding man of Spirit) versus **collective/confirmatory sacrifice** (made on behalf of social groups at rites of passage — initiation, marriage, death, age-set formation — "sacralization," bringing Spirit to man). The types overlap in practice (mortuary sacrifice blends rite-of-passage form with piacular content). Evans-Pritchard explicitly subordinated the collective type: initiation and marriage sacrifices have "a maximum of festal, and a minimum of religious, significance," and are "least important to an understanding of the character of Nuer religion" relative to personal/piacular sacrifice — a direct qualification of structural-functionalist readings that would center the socially confirmatory function (see [[spiritual-refraction]], [[structural-functionalism]]).

**The four-act canon:** presentation (*pwot*, tethering), consecration (*buk*, rubbing with ashes), invocation (*lam*), immolation (*kam yang* for personal sacrifice, *nak* for collective sacrifice). No priestly intermediary is structurally required — "any man can offer sacrifice on behalf of himself or others" — though women assist with ashes and prayer but do not invocate or slay. Nuer sacrifice is directed at moral, spiritual, and social crises, not at nature, agriculture, weather, or fertility — Evans-Pritchard contrasted this explicitly with the pattern reported for other African peoples, attributing it to Nuer fatalism toward nature.

**Communion and gift theory.** Evans-Pritchard engaged **Robertson Smith**'s communion theory (*Lectures on the Religion of the Semites*, 1889 — sacrifice as a shared feast between god and worshippers, the victim consubstantial with the god) and rejected its totemic component for the Nuer as "unacceptable, since there is almost no evidence"; in none of Nuer sacrifice, he argued, does God partake of the flesh's essence — "What goes to him is the life." He accepted Hubert and Mauss's account of sacrifice as establishing communication between sacred and profane via an intermediary victim only in a mystical, not material, sense, and added (citing Georges Gusdorf) that Nuer piacular sacrifice aims at *separation* from God/danger, not union — a paradox he resolved by distinguishing material-plane union (dissolved) from moral-plane union (established). Against **Tylor**'s and **Spencer**'s gift theories (deity-as-chief receiving tribute; grave-offerings to ghosts), both inapplicable since Nuer have no chiefs and make no such offerings, Evans-Pritchard offered a qualified acceptance: Nuer do explicitly frame sacrifice as a gift ("they say that they are giving God a thing, a gift") but one that must be immolated — "the gift must be a life or something which stands for a life."

**Vita pro vita — substitution as the core meaning.** Evans-Pritchard's own summary formula: "*vita pro vita*" (life for life). Because God already owns all cattle ("thy cow," *yangdu*, used even of beasts killed by lightning or sickness) and "gains nothing" materially from sacrifice, the emphasis falls on the intentions of the sacrificer — "It is a question, Nuer say, of the heart." A poor man may substitute a goat or even a cucumber for an ox, and God will accept it: "If a cucumber costs man nothing and benefits God not at all, we have surely to seek deeper into the ideas of payment, substitution, or exchange for the meaning of sacrificial gifts" (verbatim). The **cucumber-as-ox substitution** (*kwol*, *Cucumis prophetarum*) is treated identically to an animal victim — cut in half and consecrated — and functions in Evans-Pritchard's broader theory of symbolic predication as the simplest case in a graded series (cucumber/ox → twins/birds → totem/Spirit → light/Spirit → fetish/Spirit). Cattle (and sheep and goats) are, per Evans-Pritchard, "not slaughtered except in sacrifice" — Nuer did not provide meat to guests, and he reported being unable to buy beasts from them for food for his own household. Documented famine exceptions: Lou Nuer, 1931 ("year of great hunger"); western Nuerland, 1935; Zeraf valley, 1943 (per Dr. P. P. Howell) — even famine slaughter was accompanied by an invocation asking that "the meat may be soft in their stomachs and not bring them sickness."

He identified several distinct Nuer terms nuancing the substitution/exchange logic of personal sacrifice: *kuk/kok* ("ransom/redemption," modeled on ordinary commercial exchange but a relation between persons, not a price for objects), *luel* ("to exchange," used in invocations — "Take the cow that it may be in exchange for the life"), *lor/luor* ("to go out and meet," propitiatory sacrifice intercepting an approaching danger before it arrives), *kier/kir* ("expiation" of a state of danger or miasma already present, not payment for a specific fault), and *col/cuol* ("to indemnify/atone," used of mortuary sacrifice, *col woe*, "the wiping out of debt"). His synthesis: despite differing emphases, all express "the central piacular idea of substitution of lives of cattle for lives of men" — flagged by Evans-Pritchard himself as only the "surface" meaning, pointing to further "interior meaning" he develops across the chapter that follows.

Special modes of slaughter carried symbolic logic: cutting an ox longitudinally (*bak*) marked rites of separation (incest, mortuary severance); lateral cutting (*ngok*) was associated with *colwic*-spirit rites and leviratic/adultery danger-avoidance; drowning was used for river-spirit sacrifice; suffocation for python-spirit sacrifice, which Nuer themselves marked as "a Dinka mode of sacrifice." Holocaust sacrifices (carcass abandoned, not eaten) occurred for communal dangers (plague, murrain, an advancing enemy) — Evans-Pritchard compared this to "the burnt offering of the Hebrews."
