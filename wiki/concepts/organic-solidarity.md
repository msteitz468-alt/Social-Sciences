# Organic solidarity

## Definition as Coined

In *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] named **organic solidarity** the cohesion that arises not from likeness but from the **interdependence of differentiated functions** — "the first [mechanical] is possible only in so far as the individual personality is absorbed into the collective personality; the second is possible only if each one has a sphere of action which is peculiar to him; that is, a personality" (attributed). He called it "organic" by analogy to the higher animals, where "the unity of the organism is as great as the individuation of the parts is more marked."

Durkheim's signature thesis (attributed): the division of labor **simultaneously increases social solidarity and individuates persons** — the individual "depends upon [others] in the same measure that he is distinguished from them," and thus on the society formed by their union. Socialization here proceeds *through* differentiation, not despite it. Its external index is **[[restitutive-law|restitutive/cooperative law]]**, and its cause is the rising **volume and [[dynamic-density]]** of society (Book II).

## Operationalizations

- **Legal index**: the growth of **[[restitutive-law]]** — society "does not regulate less" as labor divides, it regulates *differently*, defining the special relations between differentiated functions rather than imposing uniform practice.
- **Structural correlate**: the **organized society** — a system of qualitatively different, mutually dependent organs coordinated around a central regulative organ that itself depends on the organs it regulates (unlike the segmental chief, whose authority derives wholly from the [[collective-conscience]]); individuals grouped by occupational function, not kinship.
- **Strength test** (Book I ch. 4): organic ties, though more numerous, are argued to be *stronger* (harder to break) than mechanical ones — e.g. modern economic interdependence (detaching a manufacturing district from its port causes "rapid social death") vs. the ease of group secession among segmental peoples (attributed).

## Applications

- Organizes *Division of Labor* Books I–III; the **abnormal forms** ([[anomic-division-of-labor]], [[forced-division-of-labor]], and a third under-coordinated form) are framed as **failures to realize** organic solidarity, not counterexamples to it.
- Grounds Durkheim's anti-Spencer argument that contract rests on [[non-contractual-elements-of-contract|non-contractual social conditions]].
- Feeds the discipline's account of modern integration and the [[differentiation-and-integration]] lineage.

## Critiques and Limitations

- Empirically fragile as stated: whether restitutive-law growth actually indexes rising solidarity (rather than, e.g., state expansion or market complexity) is contested; the mechanical→organic sequence is a dated evolutionary schema.
- Durkheim conceded organic solidarity's morality "has not yet reached the high development" modernity needs — the theory is partly normative/programmatic, not purely descriptive (keep in attributed voice).
- The "organism" analogy imports functionalist assumptions (each part serves the whole) later criticized as teleological.
