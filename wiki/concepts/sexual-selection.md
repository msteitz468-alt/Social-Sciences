# Sexual Selection

## Definition as Coined

In *[[darwin-descent-of-man-1871|The Descent of Man]]* (1871), [[darwin-charles|Darwin]] defined **sexual selection** as selection depending on the advantage certain individuals have over other individuals of the **same sex and species, in exclusive relation to reproduction** — not from being better fitted to survive in the [[struggle-for-existence|struggle for existence]]. Males (with rare exceptions) acquire weapons, ornaments, song, odour, and pugnacity by advantage over other males; those characters are often transmitted preferentially to male offspring. He distinguished this from [[natural-selection]], which “depends on the success of both sexes, at all ages, in relation to the general conditions of life” (vol. II, ch. XXI summary).

Darwin recognized **two modes**: (1) **male combat** — analogous to selecting victorious game-cocks; (2) **female choice** — analogous to the breeder who gives beauty to poultry by taste; female birds, by long selecting more attractive males, add to their beauty. “The exertion of some choice on the part of the female seems almost as general a law as the eagerness of the male.” Secondary sexual characters of this kind “depend on the will, choice, and rivalry of the individuals of either sex.”

## Semantic History

| Phase | Sense |
|---|---|
| *Origin* (1859) | Brief allusion; not developed for man |
| *Descent* (1871) | Full comparative apparatus (Part II) + application to human race differences and sex differences |
| Wallace reaction | Wallace accepted combat but resisted female aesthetic choice and often reassigned bright male colour to protection/warning under natural selection; Darwin replied at length (birds, insects) |
| 20th c. eclipse | Long secondary to genetics-focused evolutionary theory |
| Late 20th–21st c. | Revival in behavioural ecology and sexual-selection research (female choice, runaway, honest signalling) — date-stamp later science separately from Darwin 1871 |

In the social sciences, the load-bearing 1871 use is Darwin’s claim that sexual selection, more than climate or natural selection on external traits, differentiated human [[race|races]] in colour, hair, and features (ch. VII → XIX–XX).

## Operationalizations

Darwin operationalized the concept by:

1. Cataloguing [[secondary-sexual-characters]] across phyla (weapons, display, song, colour).
2. Inferring combat from law-of-battle observations; choice from courtship display, bower-building, peahen/Argus cases.
3. Invoking inheritance laws (age-limited, season-limited, sex-limited transmission) to explain why young and one sex may remain unmodified.
4. For man: race-relative beauty standards, male contest for women, female choice where not blocked by “promiscuity,” infanticide, early betrothal, or slavery of women.

## Applications

- **Zoology:** Explains extreme ornaments that may be slightly injurious to survival (stag horns; bird plumes costing flight/safety) — reproductive advantage outweighs ordinary adaptation.
- **Human evolution (*Descent*):** Principal agency of external racial differentiation; hairlessness and beard development; secondary sex differences paralleling Quadrumana.
- **Boundary with natural selection:** Habit-of-life sex differences (e.g. blood-sucking female mosquitoes) are NS with sex-limited inheritance, not SS.

## Critiques and Limitations

Darwin himself called the human application short of “scientific precision” and left an unexplained residuum of spontaneous variation. Wallace contested female aesthetic choice and hairlessness explanations. Modern readers must treat Victorian race-beauty ethnography as **period constructs in attributed voice**, not evidence. Group-selection and eugenic appropriations of *Descent* ch. V (civilised nations checking elimination of the weak) are adjacent but distinct from sexual selection proper — see [[social-darwinism]], [[moral-sense]].
