# Archaeological Site

An **archaeological site** is a place where significant traces of human activity are identified. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 give a dual definition: (1) places where [[artifact|artifacts]], [[archaeological-feature|features]], structures, and organic/environmental remains ([[ecofact|ecofacts]]) are found **together**; (2) working simplification — places with **significant traces of human activity**. Sites are the primary spatial units of survey, excavation, CRM evaluation, and public heritage presentation.

## Definition as Coined

Textbook wording (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 2, attributed):

> Archaeological sites may be thought of as places where artifacts, features, structures, and organic and environmental remains are found together. For working purposes one can simplify this still further and define sites as places where significant traces of human activity are identified.

**Duration span:** surface scatters of tools or potsherds may represent hours of occupation; Near Eastern **tells** may encode millennia. Isolated monuments (Serpent Mound; Stonehenge) also count as sites. Structures (houses, granaries, palaces, temples) are complex features built from simpler ones (postholes, hearths, floors, ditches).

## Semantic History

- **Antiquarian:** Sites as findspots of treasures and ruins.
- **Culture history:** Sites as repositories of culture-diagnostic assemblages (see [[archaeological-record]] historiography).
- **New Archaeology:** Sites as samples of settlement systems; off-site and regional survey challenge site-centrism (Vita-Finzi & Higgs land-use analysis; GIS).
- **Formation theory:** Sites are products of cultural and natural [[formation-processes]], not frozen Pompeii snapshots.
- **Heritage management:** Sites become legal objects — scheduled monuments, National Register properties, World Heritage properties — filtered by significance criteria; landscape-scale management softens hard site boundaries.
- Textbook environmental turn: sites must be read in **geomorphological and biological landscape context**, not as self-contained packages (Ch. 6).

## Operationalizations

| Task | Site operationalization |
|---|---|
| Discovery | Survey, aerial/remote sensing, chance finds, detector reporting |
| Bounding | Surface scatter density, feature clusters, cadastral/management units |
| Assessment | Size, type, layout for dig decisions or non-invasive study |
| Excavation | Horizontal association (activities) + vertical stratigraphy (change) |
| CRM | Significance evaluation; most US surveyed sites fail criteria and are recorded then destroyed |
| Sampling | Total collection on small sites; sampling when finds too numerous or secondary contexts disturbed |

Contemporaneity is demonstrated by association of artifacts and features in undisturbed **primary context** (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 3).

## Applications

- Settlement archaeology and ranking studies.
- Multi-period urban sequences (York; Mexico City Great Temple salvage).
- Paleolithic findspots often in **secondary context** (river gravels) — still “sites” but formation-qualified.
- Off-site and landscape approaches (Cahokia GIS; garden archaeology; Doggerland as drowned landscape of many loci).
- Public archaeology: sites as visit destinations and identity symbols (Kakadu; national emblem sites).

## Critiques and Limitations

- “Significance” is legal-political, not purely scientific — CRM filters destroy many low-significance sites after documentation.
- Site boundaries are analytical and administrative; activity may be continuous across landscapes.
- Treating sites as closed packages neglects ecofacts and off-site land use.
- Looting and development erase sites faster than research can respond (Mimbres; conflict zones).
- Isolated monument sites vs refuse scatters demand different methods and expectations.
