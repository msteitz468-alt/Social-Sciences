# Three-Dimensional Power

*(Attributed to [[lukes-steven|Lukes]], *[[lukes-power-radical-view-2005|Power: A Radical View]]* 1974/2005.)* A **radical** way of identifying power that goes beyond pluralist decisionism and beyond agenda-control alone: the third dimension is the capacity to shape wants, beliefs, and perceived interests so that the dominated comply willingly — power at its most effective when **least observable**.

## Definition as Coined

Lukes maps three views:

1. **One-dimensional** — A has power over B to the extent A can get B to do something B would not otherwise do (Dahl); focus on concrete decisions and overt conflict.
2. **Two-dimensional** — includes **[[nondecision|nondecisions]]** and control of the agenda (Bachrach & Baratz); still largely behavioural.
3. **Three-dimensional** — includes latent conflict and the shaping of preferences; requires counterfactual reasoning about [[real-interests|real interests]].

2005: power is a **capacity**, not only exercise; PRV’s topic is power as **domination**, one species of power.

## Semantic History

Emerged from US community-power and elite–pluralism debates (Mills, Hunter, Dahl, Bachrach & Baratz, Crenson). 2005 edition engages Foucault-inspired work on willing compliance while rejecting ultra-radical “constituted subject” claims. Cross-links to [[hegemony]], [[ideology]], [[false-consciousness]] without identity.

## Operationalizations

Case illustrations (e.g. Crenson *Un-Politics of Air Pollution*); counterfactual real-interest ascription justified by explanatory purpose; observable inaction and non-issues as evidence.

## Applications

Community power; air pollution non-issues; critiques of pluralist complacency; later organizational and cultural studies of compliance.

## Critiques and Limitations

Essentially contested concept (Gallie — Lukes accepts this); real-interest counterfactuals attacked as paternalist or incoherent; false-consciousness language contested; operationalization hard where power is most invisible — by design.
