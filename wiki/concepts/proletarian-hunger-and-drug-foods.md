# Proletarian Hunger and Drug Foods

## Definition as Coined

[[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]]'s analysis, in *[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]* (1985), of how sucrose and companion **"drug foods"** entered industrial working-class consumption as cheap calories, palatability, habit, and time-savers — articulating plantation production with metropolitan wage labor.

**Core hypothesis (attributed):** "sugar and other drug foods, by provisioning, sating—and, indeed, drugging—farm and factory workers, sharply reduced the overall cost of creating and reproducing the metropolitan proletariat" — versus viewing plantations only as capital-transfer sources or outlets for home manufactures.

Sugar became "one of the people's opiates" and a symbolic demonstration that the producing system was successful. Mintz denies a conspiracy to wreck nutrition; rising sugar consumption was "an artifact of intraclass struggles for profit" eventuating in a world-market solution as industrial capitalism expanded mass markets once regarded as sinful or indolent.

## Semantic History

Builds on English social historians of budgets and diet (Davies, Eden, Burnett, Oddy, Torode, Boyd-Orr as Mintz cites them) and on political-economy debates over free trade vs West Indian preference. Joins [[factory-in-the-field]] production to factory-world *consumption* rhythms. Related: temperance as national-economic issue (sober factory labor); tea triumphing partly by economy of dilution and colonial production.

## Operationalizations

Working-class budgets (tea, sugar, treacle shares); per-capita sucrose series and caloric %; jam as butter substitute after ~1870; bread-and-spread meals when mothers wage-work; gender bias in meat vs sweet calories within households (as Mintz reports secondary literature).

## Applications

Industrial Britain phases ~1750–1850 vs post-1850 free-trade cheapening; broader modern processed-food diets; comparative notes on French lag and U.S. paths (Mintz Ch. 5).

## Critiques and Limitations

"Drugging" metaphor; agency vs structure; gender and domestic labor depth; cross-national variation; nutritional science debates Mintz notes but does not settle.
