## Definition as Coined

King, Keohane, and Verba (KKV) define two units as homogeneous "when the expected values
of the dependent variables from each unit are the same when our explanatory variable takes
on a particular value" (King, Keohane, and Verba 1994). They present unit homogeneity as a
"second-best" substitute for the impossible task of literally rerunning history on the
same unit under different counterfactual conditions (see [[counterfactual]]): if two
distinct units can be treated as homogeneous, then the comparison across them can stand in
for the comparison of one unit's factual and counterfactual states. KKV also identify a
weaker, still-acceptable alternative — the "constant effect" assumption — which requires
only that the causal effect (the difference between expected values) be the same across
units, not that the expected values themselves match: "we need only to assume that the
causal effect is constant... two congressional districts could vary in the expected
proportion of the vote for Democratic nonincumbents... but incumbency could still add an
additional ten percent" (KKV 1994).

## Semantic History

KKV present unit homogeneity as a term of art for their own methodological program rather
than as an inheritance from an established literature, though the underlying idea — that
comparative inference requires treating compared cases as sufficiently alike — is far
older and implicit in the practice of the [[comparative-method]] long before KKV's
formalization. KKV's contribution is to state the assumption explicitly, formalize it
alongside [[conditional-independence]] as one of two routes around the Fundamental Problem
of Causal Inference (see [[causal-inference]]), and assert its centrality in strong terms:
"The notion of unit homogeneity (or the less demanding assumption of constant causal
effects) lies at the base of all scientific research. It is, for instance, the assumption
underlying the method of comparative case studies" (KKV 1994). This is an attributed
claim about the discipline's foundations, not a wiki-voice fact — KKV are here making a
programmatic argument for the unity of qualitative and quantitative inference (see
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]]), not reporting a consensus finding.

## Operationalizations

KKV treat unit homogeneity as generally untestable directly, and instead call for
researchers to make the assumption's scope explicit: across what range of units is a
uniform effect (e.g., of incumbency) expected to hold? "Across what range of units do we
expect our assumption of a uniform incumbency effect to hold? All races for Congress? ...
Races in the past two decades only?" (KKV 1994). In practice, the assumption is
operationalized by researchers bounding the population of cases to which a comparative
inference is meant to generalize, and by strategies such as matching — selecting
comparison cases similar on background variables — as a way of approximating homogeneity
in small-n designs, though KKV caution that matching "can never be complete" and can
introduce its own anomalous-case bias by selecting deviant cases on unmeasured
dimensions.

## Applications

KKV ground the validity of the [[comparative-method]] explicitly in an assumption of unit
homogeneity or the weaker constant-effect version, arguing that comparative case-study
logic necessarily rests on treating compared units as homogeneous with respect to the
variable of interest. They also invoke unit homogeneity in their treatment of process
tracing and mechanism-based research: mechanism studies operating at the individual or
decision level must still confront unit homogeneity, along with endogeneity and
omitted-variable bias, at that level of analysis — "Studies of this sort must confront
the full set of issues in causal inference, such as unit homogeneity, endogeneity, and
bias, if they are to contribute to causal inference" (KKV 1994). Violations of unit
homogeneity (and of conditional independence) are treated by KKV as a less severe problem
than an [[indeterminate-research-design]], where "virtually nothing can be learned about
the causal hypotheses" regardless of unit homogeneity.

## Critiques and Limitations

KKV themselves flag unit homogeneity as generally untestable, which limits its use as a
verifiable methodological safeguard rather than a stated, defended assumption. Because
the assumption's scope (which units count as comparable, over what time range, under what
background conditions) is a matter of researcher judgment, it invites the same
scope-of-case disputes that recur across the [[comparative-method]] literature — KKV's
own congressional-incumbency example ("all races" vs. "races in the past two decades
only") illustrates that reasonable analysts can disagree about how narrowly to bound
homogeneous units. The constant-effect variant reduces the assumption's demands but does
not eliminate the underlying difficulty: it still requires that unmeasured heterogeneity
across units not interact with the explanatory variable in a way that changes the size of
its effect.
