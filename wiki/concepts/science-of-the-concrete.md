# The Science of the Concrete

## Definition as Coined

**The science of the concrete** is [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] name (*[[levi-strauss-savage-mind-1962|The Savage Mind]]*, 1962) for the classificatory knowledge of the sensible world characteristic of "savage" (untamed) thought: a genuine science that "was no less scientific and its results no less genuine" than modern science, but "restricted by its essence to results other than those... of the exact natural sciences" — a knowledge organized "on the plane of perception and the imagination." It is the cognitive counterpart of [[bricolage]].

## Semantic History

The concept overturns the evolutionist and utilitarian view of "primitive" thought. Against Lévy-Bruhl's "prelogical mentality" and Malinowski's claim that interest in totemic species is "the rumbling of their stomachs," Lévi-Strauss argues classification precedes and exceeds use: "animals and plants are not known as a result of their usefulness; they are deemed to be useful or interesting because they are first of all known." "Classifying, as opposed to not classifying, has a value of its own." Magic and science are "two parallel modes of acquiring knowledge," not sequential stages — the "Neolithic paradox" (agriculture, pottery, domestication achieved by "centuries of active and methodical observation") shows a rigorous science preceding writing.

## Operationalizations

Evidenced by exhaustive folk taxonomies exceeding practical need (Hanunoo: ~1,800+ named plant taxa, more than the scientific botany of the same flora; Navaho >500 plants; ~2,000-species memory thresholds), by systems of correspondences (disease/animal, totemic geography), and by classification running from the universal (species, categories) down to the particular (proper names — see [[totemism]]). The order is achieved through [[binary-opposition|binary]] dichotomies (the Hanunoo universe split by successive oppositions).

## Applications

Grounds Lévi-Strauss's reinterpretation of [[totemism]] (species as a code of differences, "good to think") and of naming, and underpins the claim that "the savage mind... is not... a mind of savages" but a universal mode of thought — the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] thesis in cognitive form. It anticipated and fed ethnoscience / cognitive anthropology (ethnobotany, folk taxonomy) and the study of traditional ecological knowledge.

## Critiques and Limitations

The romance of a fully rigorous "savage science" can flatten real variation and overstate systematicity; critics note Lévi-Strauss's evidence is secondary and selectively read for order. As an analytic frame it remains a claim of [[structuralism]] (attributed), valuable for dislodging the "prelogical" prejudice while leaving open how far any given folk taxonomy is "scientific" in his strong sense.
