# Structural Autonomy

*(Attributed to [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]].)* **Structural autonomy** is the network condition of competitive advantage: freedom from [[network-constraint|constraint]] in one’s own relations combined with constraint (or closure) among those one deals with — restated in *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]* (2005) as **closure within a group and brokerage beyond the group**.

## Definition as Coined

In 1992, structural autonomy formalized hole-rich ego networks plus constrained alters. In 2005 Burt (attributed) presents structural autonomy as the **general network concept of social capital** uniting the two stylized-fact pairs: brokers do better via [[vision-advantage|vision]]; closure supplies trust and collaborative alignment needed to *deliver* brokerage’s value, while also creating inertia.

## Semantic History

Moves Burt’s program from a strong anti-closure polemic (relative to Coleman) toward explicit **complementarity** ([[closure-vs-brokerage-debate]] Position 3).

## Operationalizations

Combinations of ego constraint and group closure; Figure 3.5 model in Burt 2005; market and manager performance associations (program evidence).

## Applications

Workgroups that need internal trust *and* external bridging; organizational design trade-offs between chaos (unrestrained brokerage) and arthritis (unrestrained closure).

## Critiques and Limitations

Measurement and causal claims inherit limits of constraint indices and corporate samples; “jointly optimal” mix is theoretical ideal, hard to engineer.
