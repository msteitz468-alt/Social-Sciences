## Formation and Training

Lewis Roberts Binford (1931–2011) came into archaeology by his own account as part of a cohort "trained in rather traditional ways" in the late 1950s and early 1960s: "Archaeological methodology was almost exclusively concerned with questions of chronology" (*In Pursuit of the Past*, attributed). He was trained at the University of Michigan, where his formal teachers are generally identified as **Leslie A. White** (the neo-evolutionist whose systems-oriented view of culture as an extrasomatic adaptive mechanism shaped Binford's later insistence that culture be studied as a system rather than a set of shared ideas), **James B. Griffin** (culture-historical Eastern Woodlands chronology and typology, the very tradition Binford would spend his career reacting against), and **Albert C. Spaulding** (statistical/taxonomic method in archaeology, and a lifelong intellectual reference point — Binford dedicated *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* to Spaulding, "Professor of Anthropology, University of California, Santa Barbara"). This genealogy is standard biographical record rather than a claim extracted verbatim from the three ingested primaries, which do not themselves narrate Binford's graduate coursework; it is recorded here as attributed fact pending direct ingestion of a Binford biography.

What the ingested primaries do supply directly is the formation's intellectual texture. Binford recalls that his cohort's chronology-first training collided almost immediately with radiocarbon dating: "Many of us thought that C14 would solve, once and for all, most of the traditional problems of chronology... our response... was to begin to look for ways of getting new information about the past and about subjects other than chronology itself" (*In Pursuit of the Past*, R2-35, attributed). That collision — a discipline suddenly freed from its central preoccupation and needing new questions to ask of its data — is, on Binford's own telling, the formative event of his generation, prior to and more general than any single teacher's influence. Dendrochronology, geology, and the recognition of well-stratified North American sites reinforced the same shift (*In Pursuit*, attributed).

By his own count Binford's entire career reduces to two ultimate research problems set early and never abandoned: "those ultimate concerns have been to explain the origins of agriculture and variability in the Mousterian... I have really worked on no other problems in my entire career" (*In Pursuit of the Past*, R2-50, attributed). Everything documented below — the New Archaeology charter, the Mousterian dispute with Bordes, the Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology, the utility indices — is, on Binford's own retrospective framing, instrumental to those two questions rather than a series of unrelated projects.

Binford held positions across several institutions over his career — Chicago, UC Santa Barbara, UCLA, the University of New Mexico (where he was based, per the *Nunamiut* title page, at the time of that book's writing), and Southern Methodist University — training successive cohorts of the "New Archaeology" generation. He was known for a combative, polemical public style; *In Pursuit of the Past* itself records him characterizing a Cambridge audience's critique of his work as employing the fallacious "when did you stop beating your wife" argument form (R1, attributed) and dissociating himself pointedly from what he considered undisciplined post-1960s offshoots of the New Archaeology — Behavioral Archaeology, Social Archaeology, "Astroarchaeology" — as "not... serious proposals within a scientific discipline, but rather a form of sociological posturing" (R2-53, attributed). His personal life included several marriages; **Sally R. Binford** was both a spouse and a working collaborator and co-editor during the *New Perspectives* period, credited jointly with Lewis on the 1966 paper that opened the Bordes dispute (R2-18) — the collaboration and the marriage are documented facts of the record, and the two should not be conflated into a single undifferentiated "Binford" byline where the ingested sources distinguish sole from joint authorship.

## Career Phases

1. **Traditional training and early career (late 1950s–early 1960s).** Chronology-centered fieldwork and typology in the culture-historical mode; the radiocarbon shock reorients the cohort toward new kinds of questions (R2-34, R2-35, attributed).
2. **The New Archaeology charter (1962–1968).** "Archaeology as Anthropology" (1962) and the buildup to *New Perspectives in Archeology* (1968, co-edited with Sally Binford): explicit propositional testing, systems view of culture, rejection of a priori interpretive convention. Binford states most of the volume's ideas dated from earlier in the 1960s, "before I left for France to work on the Mousterian" (R2-55, attributed) — i.e., the charter was substantially formed *before* the empirical program that would test it against Bordes.
3. **The Bordes engagement (1966–1969).** A year in France (Bordeaux, Combe-Grenal) mapping faunal and tool distributions level by level; joint 1966 paper with Sally Binford proposing behavioral conditions that could generate Bordes' observed Mousterian assemblage patterning without invoking discrete "cultures" or "tribes" (R2-18, R2-51, attributed). This is the direct empirical antecedent of the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]].
4. **The statics–dynamics turn (New Year 1969).** On the boat home from Le Havre to New York, Binford records formulating the problem that would organize the rest of his career: "The archaeological record is static... what we are interested in about the past is dynamics... how can we make inferences from statics to dynamics?" (R2-43, attributed). This moment is presented, in Binford's own retrospective narrative, as the pivot from *doing* New Archaeology to theorizing what doing archaeological science actually requires — the seed of [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]].
5. **Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology (1969–1973).** Fieldwork among Nunamiut caribou hunters at Anaktuvuk Pass, Alaska, funded by the Wenner-Gren Foundation (grant 2376), the Doris Duke Oral History Foundation (1970 and winter 1971), and the National Science Foundation (main 1971–1972 seasons). Construction of the meat, marrow, and grease utility indices; publication of *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* (1978).
6. **Comparative extension (1970s).** Continued ethnoarchaeological and actualistic work with Navajo sheep herders in the American Southwest, Central Desert Australian Aborigines (1974, as a guest of James O'Connell working with the Alyawara, supported by the Institute of Aboriginal Studies, Canberra), and — through students — the !Kung (Ju/'hoansi) of the Kalahari; this period fed a broader comparative hunter-gatherer research program contributing to what became the cross-cultural hunter-gatherer settlement-mobility literature of the later 1970s and 1980s.
7. **Synthesis and public science (1980–1983).** *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983), assembled from 1980–81 lecture tours of Britain and Scandinavia plus a 1981 South Africa trip (Kromdraai, Sterkfontein, Swartkrans, Klasies River Mouth, with C. K. Brain, Elizabeth Voigt, and Elizabeth Vrba); synthesizes middle-range theory, formation processes, and the early-hominid scavenging-versus-hunting debate for a broad audience, framed throughout as "a kind of progress report" rather than a finished monograph.
8. **Later career (1980s–2000s, outside the three ingested primaries).** Continued elaboration of middle-range theory and cross-cultural hunter-gatherer frames of reference at the University of New Mexico and Southern Methodist University; increasing institutional distance from the post-processual movement he had helped provoke.

## The Empirical Base

**Combe-Grenal and the Mousterian (1968–69).** Binford's direct engagement with François Bordes rested on a year spent, in Binford's words, "laboriously developing the distribution maps of the different levels of Combe Grenal" (*Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology*, attributed) — mapping faunal remains and stone-tool distributions level by level at Bordes' own type site in southwestern France. The empirical yield: small numbers of animals per occupation zone (suggesting short, small occupations); consistent differences in anatomical-part representation by species; correlations between tool-assemblage type and species/anatomical-part patterning that cut across Bordes' own typological facies; no level with a complete, skeleton-proportional bone sample of any species. This is a genuinely independent re-reading of Bordes' data set, not a rejection of Bordes' typology as such — Binford accepted Bordes' quantitative method as "sophisticated" while disputing what the patterning *meant* (functional/activity variability versus discrete ethnic "tribes" or "cultures"). The 1966 joint paper with Sally Binford preceded the France fieldwork and set out the testable behavioral conditions the fieldwork was designed to evaluate. The dispute is treated in full on [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]] and remains, per this wiki's existing summary page, only partially resolved.

**Nunamiut fieldwork (1969–1973).** Conducted at Anaktuvuk Pass, a sedentary Nunamiut Eskimo community of the Brooks Range, northern Alaska (126 permanent residents, 21 households, per an August 1969 census). Funding came from the Wenner-Gren Foundation, the Doris Duke Oral History Foundation, and the NSF; field assistants included Jack B. Bertram, Robert Hitchcock, and Robert Vierra, and Binford names Johnny Rulland as his "constant companion and friend" throughout the fieldwork, alongside dozens of individually credited Nunamiut informants (Mekiana, Ahgook, Paneack, and others). The utility indices were built from controlled, weighed butchering of three animals — a wild caribou bull, an old domestic ewe, and a domestic lamb — in Albuquerque, with gross and dry-bone weights recorded per anatomical part and marrow chemistry assayed by a chemistry graduate student. Binford is explicit about a methodological and ethical constraint the Nunamiut community itself imposed: informants regarded his systematic weighing and dismemberment of animals as "something approaching a sacrilege," since killing an animal without sharing it was seen by the Nunamiut as extreme antisocial behavior — and Binford reports he never personally killed another animal for study after the initial experiments (*Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology*, attributed). This is a documented instance of fieldwork practice constrained by host-community norms, not merely by the ethnographer's own research design, and it belongs in any critical account of "the empirical base" rather than being smoothed over.

That empirical base has itself been the subject of later methodological reanalysis: zooarchaeologists (notably R. Lee Lyman and Donald Grayson, working from the late 1980s onward) have questioned aspects of the utility-index construction and its cross-context applicability — this reanalysis literature is not among this wiki's currently ingested sources and is flagged here as an outstanding gap rather than asserted in any detail; the summary page and this hub should be updated once such a source is ingested, per the Contradiction and Replication Protocol.

**South Africa and Australia (1970s–1981).** The Central Desert Australian fieldwork (1974, as a guest of James O'Connell among the Alyawara) and the 1981 South African museum/site visits (Kromdraai, Sterkfontein, Swartkrans, Klasies River Mouth, with C. K. Brain, Elizabeth Voigt, and Elizabeth Vrba) extended the comparative hunter-gatherer and taphonomic base that underwrites *In Pursuit of the Past*'s critique of the "Man the Mighty Hunter" model of early hominid behavior. Binford's Olduvai residual-bone analysis (marrow-rich, low-meat lower-limb elements in excess after subtracting predator/scavenger patterning) is offered as evidence for early hominid marginal scavenging rather than confident hunting — itself an inference resting on the prior taphonomic baseline established by C. K. Brain's independent Hottentot-goat and cave-formation studies, which Binford repeatedly credits as a parallel and mutually reinforcing actualistic program.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Binford's own account of his reception is combative and self-consciously polemical. He records that "New Archaeology" was a label coined by opponents, not by its practitioners — Robert Braidwood is quoted as calling it "a religion," and Albert Spaulding is quoted calling Binford himself "the oldest new archaeologist in town" (R2-52, attributed) — a wry acknowledgment, in Binford's telling, that a program launched as insurgent had by the 1980s become an establishment to be defended rather than a challenge still being made. He explicitly disowned later self-styled successor movements (Behavioral Archaeology, Social Archaeology, "Astroarchaeology") as sociological posturing rather than science (R2-53, attributed), and he characterized the emergent post-processual critique from Cambridge (associated with Ian Hodder and students) as employing fallacious argument forms rather than substantive scientific engagement — a characterization that is itself a position in an active [[processual-postprocessual-debate]], not a settled verdict, and is recorded here as Binford's stated position, not this wiki's adjudication.

On production context: Binford's positivist, natural-science-modeled epistemology (explicit in *In Pursuit of the Past*'s repeated appeals to Popper and to natural-science method) was itself a reaction against what he characterized as unfalsifiable, "post hoc accommodative" culture-historical and interpretivist argument — a stance that later critics (from the post-processual side) read as its own unexamined positivist bias, particularly regarding meaning, symbolism, and the archaeologist's own interpretive position. Binford's fieldwork ethics record, as far as the ingested primaries document it, includes the explicit Nunamiut-imposed constraint on his own butchering experiments described above — a factual episode of a host community setting the terms of what research practice was locally acceptable, which Binford records and appears to have respected. No further disciplinary or misconduct record appears in the three ingested primaries; this section will be revised if such material is ingested.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

**Without Binford:** a shift from chronology-focused, culture-historical archaeology toward process-oriented, explicitly theorized archaeology was arguably coming regardless of any one figure — the radiocarbon revolution alone displaced chronology as archaeology's central preoccupation and forced the discipline to ask what else its data could answer, a structural pressure Binford himself names as the trigger for his cohort's reorientation (R2-34, R2-35). Structural-functionalist and systems-theoretic ideas were already circulating in anthropology more broadly (compare Leslie White's evolutionary systems view, one of Binford's own teachers) and in the "new" quantitative social sciences of the 1960s.

**Personal and contingent:** the specific *form* the reorientation took — grounding inference in years of first-hand, controlled ethnoarchaeological observation (Nunamiut caribou hunters, Navajo sheep herders, Central Desert Aborigines) rather than in armchair systems theorizing or in Taylor's under-operationalized "conjunctive approach" — is recognizably Binford's own contribution, and one that took a particular combative, empirically dogged, methodologically obsessive personal style to carry through a decade of fieldwork. The very specific 1969 "boat home" formulation of the statics–dynamics problem, and the subsequent decision to spend four Alaskan field seasons building utility indices from butchered caribou, are personal choices of research strategy, not structural inevitabilities — a differently constituted New Archaeology cohort might have pursued Spaulding's more purely statistical-taxonomic program, or Hill's Southwestern ceramic-sociology program, as the discipline's central methodological anchor instead, with a correspondingly different vocabulary (no "middle-range theory," no utility indices) surviving into the twenty-first century.

**What survived:** the demand for independently tested "linking arguments" between behavior and material residue (middle-range theory, now discipline-wide vocabulary even among scholars uninterested in Binford's positivism); ethnoarchaeology and actualistic studies as standard method categories; the utility-index approach in zooarchaeology (revised and contested, but not abandoned); the general critique of unexamined "home base"/"mighty hunter" narratives in Palaeolithic archaeology. **What did not survive unchallenged:** Binford's confident positivism and his dismissal of "social philosopher" approaches to meaning, which the post-processual movement he provoked displaced from disciplinary leadership from the 1980s onward, even as it retained much of his empirical apparatus.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (with analysis)

**1. The statics–dynamics formulation (boat home, New Year 1969):**

> "The archaeological record is static... what we are interested in about the past is dynamics... how can we make inferences from statics to dynamics?"

Analysis: this is the single sentence from which middle-range theory grows. It reframes archaeology's problem away from "what happened" narratives and toward an epistemological question — what licenses moving from a present, static thing to a claim about a past, dynamic process — and it does so as a personal realization dated to a specific moment, not a doctrine received from a teacher or read in a text.

**2. The uniformitarian justification for ethnoarchaeology (*Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology*):**

> "We must therefore proceed along the research path forged quite eloquently by our sister discipline geology in its adoption of the proposition of uniformitarianism. Is the formation of archaeological remains as a by-product of adaptive behavior a process that is operative in the contemporary world? Can we directly experience this process...?"

Analysis: Binford borrows geology's warrant wholesale — present processes as the key to past ones — and applies it to *behavior*, not just to sediment. The move licenses ethnoarchaeology as method (observe living behavior-to-residue linkages) but also quietly commits him to treating cultural behavior as sufficiently law-like, across time, for the analogy to hold; this is the premise post-processual critics would later target.

**3. The Nunamiut ethical constraint on the utility-index fieldwork:**

> Informants regarded the systematic weighing and dismemberment of animals as "something approaching a sacrilege."

Analysis: a rare moment where Binford records his host community imposing a limit on his research design rather than merely tolerating it — evidence that the utility-index data, foundational to a widely used archaeological method, were produced under negotiated, not unilateral, field conditions. A critical empirical-base account has to carry this alongside the r = .86 correlation coefficients.

**4. On the New Archaeology's naming and its factions:**

> "It was our 'opponents', not we, who coined the term 'New Archaeology'... as Albert Spaulding once commented — I'm the oldest new archaeologist in town."

Analysis: Binford's own wry acknowledgment that a program launched as a methodological insurgency had, within roughly two decades, become the thing to be defended — a compact statement of disciplinary generational turnover that anticipates his subsequent dissociation from both "sociological posturing" successor movements and the post-processual critique from the other direction.

**5. The residual-scavenging conclusion on early hominid diet:**

> "Earliest man, far from appearing as a mighty hunter of beasts, seems to have been the most marginal of scavengers."

Analysis: the payoff of the whole formation-process apparatus built through the book — Dart's and Ardrey's "killer ape" narrative is not refuted by counter-narrative but by re-diagnosing what agents and processes actually produced the bone assemblages advanced as evidence for hunting. The rhetorical move (build the method chapter by chapter, then let a single declarative sentence carry the conclusion) is characteristic of Binford's persuasive style throughout *In Pursuit of the Past*.
