# Dell Hymes — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[hymes-dell]]. Primary base: *[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations in Sociolinguistics]]* (1974). Biographical detail beyond the book's self-presentation remains thin until further sources are ingested — gaps flagged, not filled with outside invention.

## Formation and Training

Hymes writes that his doctorate was in **linguistics**, with a great deal of work in **anthropology** and **folklore**. He describes a "mixed and troublesome intellectual and professional heritage":

- anthropological **philology**, into which he felt "willy nilly cast" as a graduate student;
- **socialist aspirations**, chosen in undergraduate years;
- deepening affinity for the historiography of anthropology, folklore, and Amerindian linguistics.

Against this heritage, the 1960s generation of linguists "talked of the same goals" in different words yet "enthusiastically pursued a practice that appeared to deny them" — veering from roots in anthropology and, as he felt, in human realities. Formal interest linked to cognitive psychology reinforced rather than transcended autonomy of form.

He continues to practice comparative/philological work on American Indian languages even while building sociolinguistic foundations — insisting advances in one direction need not erase philology, reconstruction, and related traditions. *Foundations* is dedicated **in memory of Edward Sapir**, whose family and work "have touched on mine over the years in so many ways."

**Gap:** detailed teachers, dissertation topic, and early appointments are not established from *Foundations* alone (`trained_under: unknown` on the summary page).

### Verbatim load-bearing passages

**On sociolinguistics as rethinking, not correlation:**

Hymes opens: sociolinguistics could mean mere mutual use of data across departments, but "merits our attention just insofar as it signals an effort to change the practice of linguistics and other disciplines," because present practice "perpetuates a fragmented, incomplete understanding of humanity."

**On speech community:**

> "A speech community is defined, then, tautologically but radically, as a community sharing knowledge of rules for the conduct and interpretation of speech. Such sharing comprises knowledge of at least one form of speech, and knowledge also of its patterns of use. Both conditions are necessary."

**On SPEAKING:**

The mnemonic is for convenience; "the use of SPEAKING as a mnemonic code word has nothing to do with the form of an eventual model and theory."

**On self-liquidation:**

> "The final goal of sociolinguistics, I think, must be to preside over its own liquidation."

**On Goffman:**

> "I owe a special debt to Erving Goffman; sociolinguistics is discovering that at its core lie concerns that have long been his, and this book would not exist without his intervention."

## The Empirical Base

*Foundations* is not a field monograph. Hymes's empirical base as presented here:

- **Amerindian philology and ethnography** — Chinookan/Wishram illustrations of communicative networks, scenes, speech/silence; dedication to Sapir's Amerindian lineage.
- **Comparative ethnographic juxtaposition** — Paliyan silence, Gbeya, Anang, Araucanian, Flathead/Maori speech–music, etc., used as **taxonomy generators**, not as restudied cases.
- **Education and urban ethnography network** — Philips classroom participant structures; Center for Urban Ethnography affiliation.
- **Folklore performance literature** — performance and genre as structured events (cited scholars).
- **Technical linguistic examples** — Part Three (some/any, key, reciprocal vs reflexive, phonology of style, Chinookan symbolism, Aymara pronouns) to show that internal linguistic problems lead to social analysis.

**Critical assessment:** The book's strength is conceptual architecture and polemic precision; its limitation as evidence is reliance on secondary illustrations and invited-paper origins. The program's empirical payoff is distributed across a generation of later speech-event ethnographies, not concentrated in *Foundations* itself. Restudies and critiques of particular illustrations are not adjudicated here.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

### Reception

- Became a charter text of linguistic anthropology's sociolinguistic turn.
- Communicative competence migrated into language education (often de-ethnographized).
- SPEAKING became ubiquitous in textbooks — success that risks the checklist problem Hymes anticipated.
- Generative linguistics largely continued on a parallel institutional track; the "liquidation" of sociolinguistics into linguistics did not occur as hoped.
- Goffman link anchors microsociology–sociolinguistics recognition.

### Politics (as Hymes frames them)

Scientific and social concerns converge: unequal competence opportunities; bilingual education; critique of ideal-speaker methodology as potential intellectual imperialism in education; "libération" of speech as social achievement. Undergraduate socialist aspirations are part of his self-described heritage; Marx is a wrestling partner for the fetishism of autonomous language.

### Ethics record

No misconduct narrative in this source. Production context is U.S. academic anthropology/linguistics of the 1960s–70s: Amerindian languages under long colonial salvage conditions; urban ethnography and education research under inequality. Record as disciplinary context, not personal indictment.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

**What looks personal to Hymes:** the specific fusion of Amerindian philology, folklore performance, Burkean dramatism, and Sapirian vitalization into one "foundations" project; the SPEAKING mnemonic's pedagogical fortune; the self-liquidation slogan.

**What was structural and coming anyway:** multidisciplinary pressure on autonomous linguistics; Labovian variation; Goffmanian interaction order; speech-act philosophy; educational linguistics; Gumperz's interactional sociolinguistics. Without Hymes, some other organizer might have bundled these — but the **ethnography of speaking** brand, the **communicative competence** coinage against Chomsky, and the insistence that sociolinguistic description is a mode of *linguistic* description bear his signature.

**Counterfactual:** A linguistics that never took the Hymes turn would still have formal grammar and quantitative variation; it would be thinner on event-level ethnography, classroom interaction as linguistic data, and the conceptual license to treat appropriateness as competence. Linguistic anthropology's center of gravity would sit closer to Boas–Sapir grammar-and-culture without the 1960s speech-event apparatus.

## Conceptual mechanics (deep)

### Against correlation

Hymes's opening move is to refuse the weak reading of sociolinguistics as mutual data-lending between unchanged departments. Correlation of linguistic and social variables can be "partially useful" but cannot yield a theory of language if it presupposes that human life has already been "accurately and completely apportioned" among sciences. The strong program changes practice.

### Community before code

Starting with "a language" smuggles in three attributes that sort separately: provenance, intelligibility, and use. Bloomfield and Chomsky effectively reduce speech community to language (or make the community concept redundant). Hymes's radical tautology — community = shared rules of conduct and interpretation — restores use. **Language field**, **speech field**, and **speech network** allow graded participation; membership is not identical with grammatical knowledge of a named code.

### Competence rebuilt

Chomsky's competence is treated as a **halfway house**: deeper than corpus, still thinner than social knowledge. Performance confuses two aims (imperfection of use vs accomplishment of action). Hymes's four aspects (systemic potential, appropriateness, occurrence, feasibility) prevent collapsing everything into grammar or into behaviorism. He rejects Skinnerian "mands" functionalism while insisting functions taken for granted are ideological.

### Inside linguistics (Part Three)

To show the perspective is not external, Hymes argues technical cases: dual elementary functions (referential and stylistic); social dimensions of acceptability; sex-role and participant features in grammar; phonology with stylistic universals; Chinookan augmentative/diminutive symbolism; quantifiers needing key and conversation (Lakoff some/any); reciprocal vs reflexive role construal. "There is no way to analyze speech acts adequately without ethnography." Appropriateness is a relation between sentences and contexts, not a property of sentences alone.

### Education and liberation

Bilingual education is "a sociolinguistic subject par excellence." Rules of speaking are fundamental to bicultural success; language boundaries carry social meanings. Ch. 10 links scientific reconstruction to unequal opportunities for competence — liberté/égalité/fraternité of speech as achievements. This is Hymes's political ethics of the program, stated as scholarly stake rather than as a separate pamphlet.

## The Empirical Base (expanded critique)

Strengths of the base as *Foundations* presents it:

- Long Amerindian philological practice (not only program essays).
- Dense intertextual command of 1960s sociolinguistics, folklore, and education research.
- Willingness to use negative cases and silence as structure.
- Internal linguistic arguments (Part Three) that risk technical refutation — not only polemic.

Weaknesses:

- Illustrations often second-hand; little primary fieldnote audit trail in the book.
- Invited-paper structure produces repetition Hymes only partly excised.
- Comparative taxonomy remains "preliminary" by his own admission — elementary categories like an early Tylor/Morgan stage of ethnology of speaking.
- Gender, race, and class enter mainly via cited others (Bernstein, classroom studies, Black English/creole notes) rather than as sustained Hymes field analysis in this volume.
- No single community is described end-to-end as a model ethnography of speaking within *Foundations* itself.

Ethics: Amerindian examples inherit salvage/colonial documentation conditions; educational examples inherit school power relations. Hymes's emancipatory framing does not erase those conditions — it reframes what counts as adequate description under them.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics (expanded)

**Reception tracks:** (1) linguistic anthropology core canon; (2) applied linguistics/TESOL communicative competence (often de-ethnographized); (3) folklore performance studies alliance; (4) education research on classroom participant structures; (5) relative non-reception inside mainstream generative syntax.

**Politics:** Self-described socialist undergraduate formation; Marx as conceptual tool for fetishism of autonomous language; critique of ideal-speaker methodology as educational imperialism risk; liberation as equality of communicative opportunity. Not a party program — a professional-politics of linguistics.

**Ethics record:** No personal misconduct established from this source. Production context shared with mid-century U.S. anthropology's colonial entanglements and with urban research on subordinated speakers — standard reflexivity applies.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance (expanded)

Without Hymes, Gumperz, Labov, Goffman, Sacks, and Bernstein still exist — the 1960s–70s would still have produced interactional and variationist breakthroughs. What is harder to replace:

- the **packaging** of ethnography of speaking as linguistics' own next descriptive science;
- the **coinage fight** over competence that gave educators and anthropologists a shared weapon-word;
- the **SPEAKING** pedagogical infrastructure (for better and worse);
- the explicit claim that success means **self-liquidation** of the hybrid name.

Counterfactual linguistic anthropology without Hymes would likely remain closer to Boas–Sapir grammar-and-culture plus later relativism debates, with weaker event-level units and less systematic bridge to folklore performance and classroom ethnography.
