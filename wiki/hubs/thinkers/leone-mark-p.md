# Mark P. Leone — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[leone-mark-p]]. Depth here only. All interpretive and program claims in **attributed voice**. Primary densification rests on *[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Critical Historical Archaeology]]* (2010) — Leone's own career framing of fourteen abbreviated essays. **Scope flex:** single-primary hub (below multi-source hubs such as Binford/Childe); expand when Annapolis monographs, full *Current Anthropology* 28(3) discussion, and Potter 1994 are ingested. Full monographs and independent biographies remain outstanding; this page is a **primary-source intellectual biography from the author's retrospective**, not a multi-archive life.

## Formation and Training

*(Attributed — Leone 2010 Preface and Ending.)* Leone does not use the 2010 volume to narrate graduate coursework or named dissertation supervisors. What he does narrate as formative:

1. **New England Catholic social teaching and public-school labor ethics (Belmont, Massachusetts, ~1957–58).** Reading Dickens on poverty; parish instruction from Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891) and related social doctrine on wages, the dignity of labor, and the state's duty to protect workers against "men of greed." He presents this as the moral grammar that later motivated historical archaeology aimed at the poor and the exploited (*Ending*). Verbatim load-bearing line he quotes from Leo XIII: to "save … working people from the cruelty of men of greed, who use human beings as … instruments for money-making" is not human; wages and hours must sustain life and family. He learned this as "utterly" normal moral content in 1957–58, then later recognized it as the root of his professional anger at partial democracy and silenced labor in the American past.
2. **Psychoanalysis as a young professional** — undertaken for nonacademic reasons, lasting years — which he credits with teaching him to name and modulate feelings and to see structuralist-like symbolic connections ("third ear" / "third eye"). Without analysis, he claims, he could not have written the autobiographical framing of the 2010 book (Preface). He is careful: analysis was not training to be a better anthropologist; it was personal work that nonetheless enabled the professional method of feeling.
3. **Naomi Schemann** (1980, 1987) on how feelings are learned and gendered under power relations — intellectual license for treating emotion as method, not as private noise (Preface).
4. **Collingwood's *Autobiography*** as model for intellectual self-account and for prioritizing **questions** over mute data (Part 1 intro; book-assembly note, Thanksgiving 2009 in Washington, DC). Freud and Marx are named as deeper sources of motivation and power analysis than Collingwood alone.
5. **Late-1970s outdoor history shock** — Colonial Williamsburg and related museums as "authentic sets" where archaeology seemed commercially irrelevant; anger at "unmediated" political lessons from the past (Meese/Cheney-type rhetoric in his framing); structural/psychoanalytic reading of exhibits when records were closed (Ch. 1). The 11 September 1978 Raleigh Tavern bakery reenactment becomes a set piece: race and class structure performed as living history.

University of Maryland affiliation and the Archaeology in Annapolis partnership with Historic Annapolis (from 1981) are the institutional home for the mature program (*Toward a Critical Archaeology* opening). Nan Leone is credited in the Preface for enabling experimental risks (Shakertown, South Africa, public Annapolis).

## Career Phases

*(Periodized from essay dates and 2010 framing — attributed.)*

1. **Early Mormon and technology essays (1970s).** Washington LDS Temple as ideology of order/kinship/time (1977); Mormon town plans and fences as equality technology and multi-function material culture (1973). Awe at orderly Western landscapes coexists with later critical apparatus.
2. **Outdoor history critique (late 1970s–1981).** Williamsburg and Shakertown: why popular history exists; dual ideology of hiding relations of production and imposing capitalist values; archaeology's irrelevance to the museum product.
3. **Annapolis founding and ideology theory (1981–1987).** Archaeology in Annapolis (UMD + Historic Annapolis); *Recovering Mind* (1982); Paca Garden ideology (1984); Method as Message tours (1983); Forks/Clocks (1987); **Toward a Critical Archaeology** with Potter and Shackel (1987) — program charter + ethnography of Annapolis separations + ceramic segmentation.
4. **Legitimation, galleries, genealogy (1990s).** National Register critique with Potter (1992); DeWitt Wallace Gallery analysis (1994); subversive genealogy with Little (1993); African American alternative-voice exhibits (1995).
5. **Retrospective and failure honesty (2010).** *Critical Historical Archaeology*: feelings method; theory stripped from reprints; Part 3 titled **Failure and Success**; Catholic social-teaching root; later media on spiritual caches; Coda on teaching landscapes (Chesapeake / Bay of Naples comparison).

## The Empirical Base

**Annapolis / Archaeology in Annapolis (from 1981).** Collaboration University of Maryland Anthropology and Historic Annapolis, Inc. (private, state-chartered preservation org. founded 1952). Open sites named in the 1987 paper include Victualling Warehouse (fire chronological control 1790), Hammond-Harwood House, Thomas Hyde/Main Street house. Methods: stratigraphic excavation; ceramic assemblage variation as measure of mercantile segmentation; probate inventories for clocks/instruments/formality rise ~1710–1732; ethnographic database of guidebooks, reenactments, and tourist presentations (Potter: four book-length histories, twenty guidebooks, reenactments, interviews, years of participant observation).

Potter's central ethnographic finding (attributed, 1987): Annapolis history is presented as **fragments** — black history segregated temporally from white (18th vs 19th c.), erasing slavery as the principal relation; city vs Naval Academy separated by mixed temporal/institutional categories (Norris 1925 *Annapolis: Its Colonial and Naval Story*) despite economic interdependence. Leone wanted Maryland archaeology **not** to reinforce the "intact sophisticated eighteenth-century capital" veneer: the city "is not intact, is not sophisticated," but lives inside a carefully maintained historicity.

**[[william-paca-garden]].** 1760s layout; disappeared; reconstructed 1960s–70s after archaeological recovery of features. Leone's 1981 first visit ("entrancing… marvelous, and it was supposed to be"); analysis of perspective geometry against Renaissance/Baroque comparanda (Boboli, Villa Lante, Villa d'Este) and documentary Paca context. Load-bearing claim: planned gardens **work on** the viewer as participant; optical order naturalizes hierarchy while Paca's wealth rested on slavery — ideology, not taste. **Reliability note:** restored garden is the object — not a pristine 1760s surface; reconstruction archaeology quality is a standing caution.

**Forks, clocks, instruments (Annapolis inventories).** Leone & Shackel (1987) argue that between ~1710 and 1732, wealth polarization coincides with rising formality and scientific/musical instruments. Forks/plates segment and individualize diners; clocks and surveying gear project social order into nature and the heavens, sheltering hierarchy from scrutiny. Explicit rewrite of Deetz: "Deetz's Georgian mindset was the order of wealth and hierarchy."

**[[colonial-williamsburg]].** Not Leone's excavation program; object of critical reading (exhibits, Raleigh Tavern episode 1978, DeWitt Wallace Gallery 1994). Strength: institutional visibility. Limit: outsider critique of Foundation archaeology. DeWitt Wallace: decorative-arts gallery analyzed as capitalism's twin products — segmented production and categories of mental instability — via Foucaultian discipline and structural oppositions.

**Mormon cases.** Temple (DC Beltway landscape) and Arizona fences/Plat of Zion — material-culture analyses more than multi-year digs; early career awe later re-theorized as ideology of order, kinship, time, and equality technology.

**Public evaluation.** Self-selected ~10% questionnaire returns; three response categories at Shiplap (point-of-view data; more than objects; relevance to today). Leone also records failures: ideology slide show abandoned; Historic Preservation Commission conflict; limited permanent change in city messaging; Althusser+Lukács path did not reliably transform Annapolis power. Successes claimed: large Method-tour visitor totals; African American collaborative exhibits; later media around spiritual caches (Fleet Street bundle → *New York Times* and local editorial; NPR Wye).

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Leone's politics are explicit. On site significance and democracy (2010 Ch. 4 frame):

> Our job is to help in the unmasking of the partial in the first place. Our job is first and finally with the poor, those absent from democracy and power.

He argues legitimacy is "probably never achievable" under growing wealth inequality, but **unmasking** is; archaeology should equip the disfranchised with deep, mainline pasts for inclusion claims — not re-mask inequality by adding token "voices of the voiceless" that leave the democratic cover intact. Catholic social teaching and Marxist ideology theory are presented as convergent motivators, not as a secularization narrative.

On theory choice (Ch. 5): against eclecticism — theories have incompatible end-products (conflict vs functional stability); pick Marxism and work it inside a scientific community that can show you wrong. On feelings (Preface):

> I begin with feelings. My purpose is to make a guide for other historical archaeologists that shows how to start work. That is with your feelings, I suggest.  
> … My argument is that feelings are the preexisting condition.

Ethics of collaboration surface in the 1995 African American exhibit work (community questions from Barbara Jackson-Nash and others; Banneker-Douglass Museum; Franklin Street homes under parking lot; foodways and later hoodoo/cache interpretation) — still under institutional constraints of Historic Annapolis and the tourism economy. Historic Annapolis is both **partner** (enabling open sites) and **limit** (blocking some leftist public interpretations, per Leone's retrospective).

Reception in the wider field (textbook maps: Johnson; Hodder & Hutson) treats Annapolis as the U.S. neo-Marxist HA landmark. Critiques of "duped" ideology subjects and theory-circularity apply (see [[marxist-archaeology]]); Leone's 2010 answer is feelings-first problem choice and stripped data cores. Rival/adjacent: Orser's global program; British post-processual meaning focus; CRM significance regimes.

No misconduct scandal is recorded in the 2010 volume; the ethics record that matters is **public-program honesty** — success metrics and failure metrics both published by the author under the Part 3 title *Changing Things: Failure and Success*.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Without Leone, U.S. historical archaeology would still have Deetz's patterning, Orser's global charter, and African diaspora archaeology via Fairbanks–Deagan lineages. What would be thinner: (1) a **named critical public method** tying Althusser to open-site tours; (2) the **Paca Garden** as obligatory teaching case of landscape ideology; (3) the rewrite of Georgian order as **capitalist naturalization** rather than only mentalité; (4) an autobiographical method literature on **feelings** as problem source. The structural conditions (1980s theory crisis in HA; university–preservation partnerships; museum studies critique) would likely have produced some critical HA without Leone personally — McGuire and Paynter show parallel paths — but the Annapolis tour apparatus and the 1987 *CA* paper are hard to substitute.
