# Edward Burnett Tylor — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. The `thinkers/` summary owns overview/contributions/genealogy/critiques/historiography at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: summary links here by full path `[[hubs/thinkers/tylor-edward-burnett|…]]`; this page's `thinker_page` points back to `[[tylor-edward-burnett]]`.

All interpretive claims are in attributed voice; Tylor's theoretical positions — unilineal social evolution, the intellectualist ("armchair") theory of religion, the evidential use of survivals — are his, not wiki-voice fact, and are flagged where the discipline has since superseded them. This hub entry is built primarily from *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (2 vols., 1871); a full Stocking/Marett-grounded biographical treatment of the earlier and later career remains provisional pending direct ingestion of those biographies.

## Formation and Training

Edward Burnett Tylor was born on 2 October 1832 into a prosperous Quaker family in Camberwell, London; his father Joseph Tylor ran a brass foundry, and his elder brother Alfred became a respected geologist. As Quakers, the Tylors were barred by their Nonconformity from Oxford and Cambridge, which then required Anglican subscription for matriculation and degrees. Tylor therefore **never attended university and held no degree** — a fact of decisive importance for his career and self-understanding, and one that makes his eventual Oxford professorship (see Career Phases below) a genuinely unusual outcome for Victorian academic life. He was educated at a Quaker school (Grove House, Tottenham) and, at sixteen, entered the family business. His anthropology was entirely autodidactic, built from private reading rather than formal instruction.

The turning point was medical and accidental. Threatened by consumption (tuberculosis) in his early twenties, Tylor was sent travelling for his health. In 1855–56, in Cuba, he met **Henry Christy** (1810–1865), a Quaker banker, ethnologist, and archaeologist. Christy took the young Tylor with him on an extended journey through Mexico. The encounter converted a convalescent's tour into a scientific vocation: Christy modelled the disciplined observation of material culture and antiquities, and the Mexican journey produced Tylor's first book, *Anahuac; or, Mexico and the Mexicans, Ancient and Modern* (1861) — a travel narrative already showing his eye for custom, survival, and the comparison of ancient and modern practice.

Tylor's formation was thus that of the **gentleman scholar of independent means**: no laboratory, no fieldwork training, no disciplinary apprenticeship, but leisure, private income, wide reading in classical and modern languages, and access to the vast Victorian archive of traveller, missionary, and colonial-administrator reports out of which he would build his comparative science. *Primitive Culture* itself is explicit about this method of construction: Tylor tells the reader in his prefatory remarks that he has drawn on "the immense mass of accessible evidence" compiled from a worldwide literature of description, a phrase that recurs through the book whenever he generalizes about "the lower races." The formation, in other words, is legible in the finished text — a library science built by a man who read everything and travelled almost nowhere in the ethnographic sense.

## Intellectual Genealogy

Tylor had **no teacher in anthropology** — the discipline did not yet exist as a taught subject — and this absence is itself analytically important: he is a founder precisely because there was no chair to inherit. His decisive personal influence was Christy (the model of the scientific ethnologist), and his intellectual milieu was the mid-Victorian evolutionist ferment following Darwin's *Origin of Species* (1859), Lyell's uniformitarian geology, and the "antiquity of man" debates that pushed human prehistory back beyond the biblical chronology.

He worked alongside and in productive tension with the other Victorian evolutionists:

- **[[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]]** — the grander systematizer of [[social-evolutionism|social evolution]]; Tylor shared the developmental frame but was more the patient comparative ethnographer than the deductive philosopher.
- **[[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]]** — the American whose *Ancient Society* (1877) ran a parallel evolutionary scheme through kinship and technology; Morgan and Tylor are the twin founders of evolutionist anthropology on their respective sides of the Atlantic (see [[hubs/thinkers/morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan hub]]).
- **John Lubbock** (Lord Avebury) and **John Ferguson McLennan** — co-architects of the evolutionist reconstruction of prehistory, religion, and marriage; Lubbock's savagery/barbarism/civilization ladder and McLennan's speculative reconstructions of primitive marriage and totemism supplied comparative material Tylor draws on throughout *Primitive Culture*.

Downstream, his most consequential intellectual child was **James George Frazer** ([[frazer-james-george]]), who explicitly regarded Tylor as his master and extended the intellectualist theory of [[animism]] and [[magic]] into the vast comparative apparatus of *The Golden Bough* (1890) — Frazer's "if magic is everywhere condemned... it is set in opposition to religion" scheme is a direct descendant of Tylor's "mistaken association of ideas" account of magic in *Primitive Culture* I, ch. 4. Tylor also trained, in the loose Victorian sense, the first generation of professional British anthropologists through his Oxford teaching (from 1884), and the Pitt Rivers Museum, whose collection came to Oxford in 1884, gave his evolutionist typology of artifacts a material home — the typological arrangement of objects "from simple to complex" that structures Pitt Rivers displays to this day is Tylorian in logic even where the museum's founder supplied the collection.

The great **antagonist** in the genealogy is posthumous: **Franz Boas** ([[boas-franz]]), whose 1896 "Limitations of the Comparative Method" and subsequent historical particularism made Tylor's comparative method and its psychic-unity warrant the paradigm of everything early-twentieth-century American anthropology rejected (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]); on the British side, Malinowski's functionalism performed an analogous demolition of the evidential use of survivals, arguing that customs must be explained by their present function in a living social system rather than treated as fossils of an earlier stage.

## Career Phases

**1. Traveller and gentleman ethnologist (1856–1865).** The Mexican journey with Christy and *Anahuac* (1861). Tylor learns the craft of observation and begins amassing the comparative reading that will fill his major works.

**2. System-builder (1865–1871).** *Researches into the Early History of Mankind and the Development of Civilization* (1865) is the pivotal methodological book: it works out gesture-language, picture-writing, myth, and the "development of civilization," and already deploys survivals and the comparative method. It earns Tylor election to the **Royal Society (1871)** — extraordinary for a man without a degree. This phase culminates in *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (1871, 2nd ed. 1873), his masterwork and the founding statement of the discipline: two volumes running from the definition of culture, through the doctrine of survivals, language, counting, and mythology (Volume I), to a sustained treatise on animism as the origin and development of religion (Volume II).

**3. Institution-builder and teacher (1871–1896).** Tylor turns from theory-building to the professionalization of anthropology. He becomes **Keeper of the University Museum at Oxford (1883)**, **Reader in Anthropology (1884)** — the first such post in Britain — and finally **Professor of Anthropology (1896)**, again a first; contemporaries spoke of "Mr. Tylor's science" as effectively synonymous with the new discipline. He writes *Anthropology: An Introduction to the Study of Man and Civilization* (1881), the discipline's first textbook, condensing the evolutionist synthesis for students. In 1888 he delivers the first Gifford Lectures at Aberdeen, and in **1889** publishes "On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions; Applied to Laws of Marriage and Descent" — the pioneering attempt at **cross-cultural statistical correlation** ("adhesions" between customs), at which Francis Galton, in the discussion following the paper, raised the objection of case non-independence — related and historically connected societies cannot be treated as statistically independent trials — now formalized across the discipline as **Galton's problem**.

**4. Elder statesman and decline (1896–1917).** President of the Anthropological Institute; knighted in 1912. His mental powers failed in his last years; he retired in 1909 and died on 2 January 1917, by which time the evolutionist paradigm he founded was already under sustained attack from Boasian particularism in the United States and the first stirrings of functionalism in Britain.

## Major Works and Their Moves

| Work | Year | Move it makes | Reception |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Anahuac* | 1861 | Travel narrative; first exercise of the comparative-survival eye | Minor; a start, not a landmark |
| *Researches into the Early History of Mankind* | 1865 | Methodological groundwork: gesture-language, myth, survivals, the comparative method | Won him the Royal Society (1871); overshadowed by *Primitive Culture* |
| *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871]]* | 1871 | Defines [[culture]]; codifies [[survivals]] and the comparative method; builds the [[animism]] theory of religion on the "minimum definition" of belief in spiritual beings | The founding text of anthropology; immediate and lasting influence; still the canonical citation for the culture concept |
| *Anthropology* | 1881 | The discipline's first textbook — consolidates the field for students | Standard introduction for a generation |
| "On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions" | 1889 | First cross-cultural statistical correlation ("adhesions") | Pioneering; provoked **Galton's problem** — the founding methodological caveat of all comparative work |

The central move of *Primitive Culture* is announced in its first sentence — the definition of [[culture]] as "that complex whole" — and executed across two volumes. Volume I establishes the science (definition, development, survivals, language, the art of counting, mythology) and opens the theory of [[animism]]; Volume II is a sustained treatise on animism as the origin and development of religion, moving from the doctrine of souls through the future life, transmigration, nature-spirits, fetishism, and polytheism to monotheism, and to the rites (prayer, sacrifice, fasting) that accompany them. The book's architecture is itself an argument: by placing "culture" (chapter 1) before "survivals" (chapters 3–4) before "animism" (chapters 11 onward through Volume II), Tylor builds his theory of religion on foundations the reader has already been persuaded to accept.

## Conceptual Contributions and Their Mechanics

| Concept | Where coined/developed | How it drifted | Current use |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[culture]] | *Primitive Culture* I, ch. 1 (1871) | Singular/evolutionist → Boasian plural, relativized "cultures" → symbolic/practice senses | The discipline's central term; Tylor's definition still the canonical opening citation |
| [[survivals]] | *Primitive Culture* I, ch. 3–4 | Evidential engine of evolutionism → attacked by functionalism (present function over historical fossil) → obsolete as evidential method | Historical marker of how founding anthropology reasoned; the term itself persists loosely in ordinary usage |
| [[animism]] | *Primitive Culture* I, ch. 11 – Vol. II | "Minimum definition of religion" → religion-type label → critiqued by Durkheim/Evans-Pritchard → revived as "new animism"/relational ontology (Descola, Viveiros de Castro) | Fixture of the anthropology of religion, largely now cited as a foil to define positions against |
| [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]] | Working premise throughout, stated explicitly I, ch. 1 | Anti-racist premise + evolutionist license → Boasian anti-evolutionism retains the premise, drops the license → structuralist/cognitive-science versions | Enabling premise of any comparative claim; still contested in its scope |
| [[magic]] | *Primitive Culture* I, ch. 4 | "Mistaken association of ideas" → Frazer's sympathetic/contagious magic → functionalist and symbolic re-readings (Malinowski, Evans-Pritchard) | Founding topic of the anthropology of religion and witchcraft studies |

The **mechanics** of Tylor's system hang together tightly, which is worth stating precisely. Psychic unity licenses the comparative method (uniform minds → parallel independent invention → a custom observed here illuminates a custom observed elsewhere); the comparative method reconstructs a universal developmental scale ([[social-evolutionism]]); survivals supply the evidence for vanished earlier stages by flagging traits out of step with their surroundings; and animism applies the whole apparatus to religion, treating the "primitive philosopher" as a rational human reasoning correctly from limited data to the soul-idea. Pull out psychic unity and the comparative inference collapses (Boas's move); pull out the developmental scale and survivals become mere persistences explicable by present function (Malinowski's move). The system is thus a tightly coupled machine, which is why its critics could dismantle it by attacking a single joint rather than the whole.

**The founding definition of culture.** *Primitive Culture* opens with the sentence that made Tylor's name:

> "Culture, or Civilization, taken in its wide ethnographic sense, is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, custom, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society." (I, ch. 1)

Read closely, three commitments are doing the work. "**Complex whole**" asserts that culture is an integrated totality, not a heap of discrete traits — though Tylor's own working method (extracting single customs, such as a marriage rule or a counting system, and arraying them comparatively across unrelated societies) sits in real tension with this holism, a tension Boas would later exploit to devastating effect. "**Acquired… as a member of society**" severs culture from biological inheritance — the anti-racist core of the concept: apparent racial difference in achievement is, on this reading, difference of *acquired* culture, not of innate endowment. And "**Culture, or Civilization**" — the equation of the two terms — folds all peoples onto a single graduated developmental scale, the evolutionist commitment that the twentieth century would strip out while retaining the definition's opening clause. The single sentence thus contains both the concept's liberating move and the baggage later generations discarded from it.

**Psychic unity as method.** Tylor states the premise underwriting the whole comparative enterprise directly:

> "the uniformity which so largely pervades civilization may be ascribed, in great measure, to the uniform action of uniform causes." (I, ch. 1)

This is psychic unity as a research warrant: like causes — including like human minds confronting like problems — produce like effects, so cross-cultural similarity is treated as evidence of parallel independent development rather than requiring historical contact or diffusion to explain it. Boas's counter, decades later, is precisely a denial that this inference is safe: similar traits can equally arise from diffusion, borrowing, or accident, and superficially similar customs may carry entirely different meanings in their local context — the point Boas develops at length against Tylor's generation in *The Mind of Primitive Man* (see [[hubs/thinkers/boas-franz]]).

**The doctrine of survivals.** Tylor explains the term and its evidential use directly, using his own example of an old Somerset woman still working a pre-mechanized hand-loom:

> "These are processes, customs, opinions, and so forth, which have been carried on by force of habit into a new state of society different from that in which they had their original home, and they thus remain as proofs and examples of an older condition of culture out of which a newer has been evolved." (I, ch. 3)

He extends the category immediately to institutionalized practice and popular belief alike — "the ordeal of the Key and Bible, still in use, is a survival; the Midsummer bonfire is a survival; the Breton peasants' All Souls' supper for the spirits of the dead is a survival" — and states its polemical use plainly: "most of what we call superstition is included within survival, and in this way lies open to the attack of its deadliest enemy, a reasonable explanation." The method is thus explicitly double-edged from the start: an evidential tool for reconstructing developmental sequence, and a weapon for debunking contemporary belief (spiritualism above all — see Reception, Politics, and Ethics below) by exposing it as an archaic residue rather than a live truth.

**The minimum definition of religion and animism.** Opening Volume II's treatment of religion, Tylor deliberately sets the evidential bar as low as possible:

> "It seems best to fall back at once on this essential source, and simply to claim, as a minimum definition of Religion, the belief in Spiritual Beings." (II)

The move is calculatedly inclusive: by requiring only belief in spiritual beings — not a supreme deity, an afterlife doctrine, idols, or sacrifice — Tylor secures his claim, against writers such as Sir Samuel Baker who had flatly denied that some "lower races" possessed any religion at all, that religion is a human universal, and simultaneously makes it a single comparable variable for the comparative method to track across societies. He then names the doctrine built on that minimum:

> "The sense of Spiritualism in its wider acceptation, the general belief in spiritual beings, is here given to Animism. … Animism is, in fact, the groundwork of the Philosophy of Religion, from that of savages up to that of civilized men." (II)

Animism, on Tylor's account, resolves into "two great dogmas, forming parts of one consistent doctrine; first, concerning souls of individual creatures, capable of continued existence after the death or destruction of the body; second, concerning other spirits, upward to the rank of powerful deities." The theory casts the "primitive philosopher" not as confused or irrational but as a careful empirical reasoner drawing sound inferences — from dreams, visions, shadows, breath, and death — to the hypothesis of a detachable soul; Evans-Pritchard's later "if I were a horse" objection targets precisely this imputed reasoning process, arguing that Tylor projects a Victorian rationalist's own inferential habits onto peoples he never observed directly.

## The Empirical Base

Tylor's empirical base is the single most important thing to examine critically, because it is **not fieldwork**. He never conducted systematic ethnographic research; his one extended non-European experience was the 1856 Mexican journey with Christy, itself closer to antiquarian travel than to participant observation. *Primitive Culture* is instead built from a vast **compilation of secondary reports** — the accounts of travellers, missionaries, colonial officials, classical authors, and folklorists, collated on filing slips and arranged topically. Tylor is candid about drawing on "the immense mass of accessible evidence" rather than his own observation; the book's authority rests on synthesis and comparison of others' testimony, not on independent verification.

This method has three consequences that the twentieth century made damning:

1. **Source reliability.** The travellers', missionaries', and administrators' reports vary wildly in quality; many are the impressions of untrained, often prejudiced observers with slight command of the languages and cultures they describe (Tylor's own text shows him weighing conflicting reports against each other — as in his handling of Baker's denial of religion among the White Nile peoples against the "details of the religion of these White Nile tribes" recorded years earlier by Kaufmann, Brun Rollet, and Lejean — but the underlying archive remains only as good as its most compromised contributors).
2. **Decontextualization (Galton's problem in embryo).** The comparative method deliberately lifts a custom out of its social and historical setting in order to place it on a developmental ladder alongside superficially similar customs from unrelated societies. This treats cultures as independent cases, disregarding diffusion and shared descent — the non-independence Galton flagged against Tylor's own 1889 statistical paper and that remains the standing caveat of all cross-cultural comparison (the mandatory `galton_note` on this wiki's `comparisons/` pages traces directly back to this 1889 exchange).
3. **Circularity of survivals.** The evidential use of survivals presupposes the very stage-sequence (savagery → barbarism → civilization) it is meant to demonstrate: a trait is identified as a survival because it seems "out of step" with a society's otherwise higher stage, but "otherwise higher stage" is itself established by the same developmental scheme the survival is offered as evidence for.

The fair verdict — neither hagiography nor demolition — is Stocking's: Tylor was a rigorous and cautious synthesizer *within* the evidentiary and theoretical horizon available to him, and the founder who made anthropology a science by giving it a defined object (culture) and a defined comparative method, even though the substantive evolutionary theories he built on that base did not survive the methodological revolution his own discipline underwent in the following generation.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Tylor's reception has two broad phases. In his lifetime and for a generation after, he was the towering founder — contemporaries could speak of "Mr. Tylor's science" as more or less synonymous with anthropology itself, and *Primitive Culture* was the field's obligatory point of departure. From the 1910s–1920s, Boasian particularism in the United States and functionalism in Britain (Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown) made his evolutionism, his comparative method, and his evidential use of survivals the discredited old order against which the new professional discipline defined itself (see [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]). Since the later twentieth century, historians of anthropology — Stocking above all — have rehabilitated him as institution-builder and as a more careful, more empirically cautious thinker than the caricature of the armchair evolutionist allows.

On politics and ethics, two things must be held together rather than resolved into a single verdict. Tylor was a **liberal reformer**, and his anthropology carried an explicit reformist edge. The closing pages of *Primitive Culture* state the discipline's civic mission directly:

> "Thus, active at once in aiding progress and in removing hindrance, the science of culture is essentially a reformer's science."

The sentence caps an argument that ethnography has "double help" to give the promoters of sound and the reformers of faulty modern culture: to trace a doctrine of development is to honour one's ancestors by continuing their progressive work, while to expose "the remains of crude old culture which have passed into harmful superstition" — spiritualism prominent among his named targets — is "a harsher, and at times even painful, office," but one urgently needful nonetheless. The doctrine of survivals is, in this light, never merely descriptive scholarship; it is Tylor's instrument for debunking contemporary practices he judged irrational by exposing their archaic pedigree.

His psychic-unity premise was, for its own day, a genuinely **anti-racist** commitment: it asserted the fundamental mental equality of all humankind against the polygenist race-science of contemporaries who held non-European peoples to be innately and permanently a lower order of humanity. Yet the very same evolutionist framework installed a **developmental hierarchy of cultures** — the savage/barbaric/civilized ranking that structures *Primitive Culture* throughout — placing Victorian European civilization at the summit of a single universal scale, and furnishing, whatever Tylor's personal intentions, an intellectual scaffolding congenial to the colonial administrative order that supplied much of his raw data via missionaries and colonial officials. Holding both facts together — the anti-racist premise of a single human mental capacity, and the ethnocentric hierarchy built on top of it — is the honest reading of Tylor's politics; neither fact cancels the other, and the reflexive point (the Victorian-imperial production context of the "immense mass of accessible evidence" itself) awaits the fuller treatment a dedicated Stocking ingest would supply.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

What does anthropology look like without Tylor? The counterfactual sharpens which of his moves were personal contributions and which were structural, arriving regardless. The **evolutionist frame was structural** — Spencer, Morgan, Lubbock, and McLennan were building versions of it simultaneously and independently; had Tylor never written a word, Victorian anthropology would still have been evolutionist, driven by the same post-Darwinian, post-uniformitarian intellectual current. The **cross-cultural statistical method** of the 1889 paper was genuinely ahead of its time and might, absent Tylor, have been delayed by a generation or more.

But two contributions bear his distinctly personal stamp. The first is the **naming and constituting of "culture" as the object of a science** — the particular definition, and the framing of anthropology as *the science of culture*, is specifically Tylorian, and it is the durable core that outlived every one of his substantive theories: a Boasian anthropologist rejecting unilineal evolution wholesale still opens with Tylor's sentence, or a visible descendant of it. The second is [[animism]] as the **intellectualist theory of religion**, which — transmitted through Frazer's far more widely read *Golden Bough* — set the terms for the anthropology of religion for the better part of half a century, defining the position (religion as a form of mistaken proto-science, generated by rational inference from inadequate premises) that Durkheim's social-fact theory of religion, Malinowski's functionalist account of magic and ritual, and Evans-Pritchard's *Theories of Primitive Religion* (1965) all define themselves against. Tylor's substantive evolutionary theories are dead; his concept of culture and his framing of what a theory of religion must explain are questions the field is still, in a real sense, answering.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

The indispensable modern source is **George W. Stocking Jr., *Victorian Anthropology* (1987)**, which situates Tylor in the mid-Victorian evolutionist moment and substantially corrected the mid-twentieth-century functionalist caricature of him as a naive armchair theorist; Stocking's essays in *Race, Culture, and Evolution* (1968) are also key to the historiographical rehabilitation. R. R. Marett's *Tylor* (1936) is the early disciple's memoir-biography, useful as a period document even where superseded as scholarship. Neither Stocking's nor Marett's volume has yet been directly ingested into this wiki; the genealogy, career-phase, and reflexivity material above therefore rests on standard disciplinary-history knowledge rather than on a first-hand ingest of either biography, and should be treated as provisional pending that ingest.

The primary text underlying this hub entry is *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* (2 vols., John Murray, 1871; revised 2nd ed. 1873), read here in full across both volumes; *Researches into the Early History of Mankind* (1865) and *Anthropology* (1881) complete the core corpus but are cited from secondary knowledge, not direct ingest. Tylor's papers are held at the Pitt Rivers Museum and the Bodleian Library, Oxford.
