# Michael T. Taussig — Intellectual Biography

## Formation and Training

Michael T. Taussig (b. 1940) is an Australian-born anthropologist whose formation, as reconstructed from the 1987 acknowledgments and the two ingested monographs, runs through 1960s Sydney oppositional culture ("Sydney Push"), curiosity about anthropology (Clare Hutchison, Chandra Jayawardena), psychiatric nursing work at Villa 21 of Shenley Mental Hospital in England (1967) with Frank Atkins — whom Taussig credits with showing how psychiatry could be "as soberly radical and responsible as it was fantastically shamanic" — and late-1960s London School of Economics activism. Anti-imperialist networks and Andrew Pearse helped redirect romantic notions of "lending my medical self to the guerrilla struggle" into Colombian fieldwork beginning in 1969. Spanish-language apprenticeship (Suzette Macedo, Pam Cobb) and early contacts with Sutti Ortiz, Malcolm Deas, Orlando Fals Borda, and María Cristina Salazar shaped entry into Colombian rural politics and letters. First Colombian book appeared under the Puerto Tejada pseudonym Mateo Mina — Mina a common name of African-descended placer miners and of bandit Cenecio Mina — signaling political solidarity with southern Cauca’s Black peasantry from the start.

The early Colombian years are not a mere prelude. Acknowledgments name Luis Carlos Mina and Alfredo Cortes of the peasant syndicate; María Cruz Zappe, Tomás Zapata, Eusebio Cambindo, Felipe Carbonero as elders who brought back slavery and free-peasant epochs in epic verse; Olivia Mostacilla and Regina Carabalí as instructors; Marlene Jiménez and Guillermo Llanos as the household that "initiated my absorption into the kindness as well as the violence of Cauca." These names reappear as the moral and narrative ground of both monographs: the 1980 book’s free peasantry history and the 1987 book’s closing murder of Marlene. Fieldwork is political friendship and bereavement, not only sampling.

The medical-shamanic thread is continuous. Shenley Villa 21 taught Taussig that psychiatry could be shamanic; Putumayo yagé taught him that shamanism could be historical montage; the university doctor’s scientific model of healing becomes the explicit foil in "To Become a Healer." Career identity as anthropologist never fully renounces the healer/patient dyad as the unit of knowledge.

Medical training is widely reported in secondary literature and consistent with the acknowledgments’ hospital and "medical self" language; formal `trained_under` genealogy is not fixed from these sources alone and remains `[[unknown]]` on the summary page pending a biography ingest. Institutional appointments (Michigan, Sydney, Columbia — as often listed secondarily) should not be wiki-voice dated until a biographical source is ingested.

Genealogy is more **elective affinity and citation** than departmental lineage. Taussig’s intellectual party is the Benjaminian Marxist modernism of the critical turn, not Boasian particularism or British structural-functionalism. Practice theory is adjacent via [[implicit-social-knowledge]] and envy-as-antenna but is not Bourdieu imported wholesale; ISK is more imageric and less dispositional than [[habitus]]. *Writing Culture* (1986) is a sibling mood, not a membership card — Taussig routes crisis-of-representation through terror and Benjamin rather than through Clifford’s travel-writing genealogy alone.

## Career Phases

1. **Arrival and Cauca immersion (1969–mid-1970s).** Almost four years with Anna Rubbo among Afro-Colombian free peasants and cane workers around Puerto Tejada; peasant syndicate politics; oral history of slavery and *comuneros*; first Colombian publications as Mateo Mina.
2. **Devil book (1980).** International reputation: Cauca devil contracts + Bolivian Tio (via Nash et al.) as commodity-fetishism ethnography.
3. **Putumayo densification (field recurrent through 1985; book 1987).** Terror archive + yagé ethnography; space of death / culture of terror / wild man / montage.
4. **Later experiments (post-1987; not ingested).** *Mimesis and Alterity* (1993); fictocritical and diary forms; continued teaching — collection gap.
5. **Anniversary re-entry (2006–10).** *Sun Gives without Receiving*; 2009 preface self-critique of 1980 analytic distance.

**1980 load-bearing claim (attributed):** as peasant cultivators become landless wage labourers they invoke the devil in production contexts where use-value peasantry does not; devil flavor marks capitalist metaphysics of labour.

**1987 load-bearing claim (attributed):** Putumayo terror and yagé healing share the space of death; colonial wild-man imagery organizes both massacre and cure; epistemic murk is instrument of power; montage is the form adequate to that field.

### How the two monographs argue with each other

Read as a diptych, 1980 and 1987 share: (1) fetish structure — a cultural image that mediates political-economic force; (2) colonial capitalism in Colombia — cane and rubber as extraction regimes with racialized labour; (3) anti-functionalist magic; (4) mixed primary/secondary modes (Bolivia secondary in 1980; Putumayo archive historical in 1987).

| Dimension | 1980 Devil | 1987 Shamanism |

| Core image | Devil / Tio | Wild man / space of death |
| Primary living ethnography | Cauca | Putumayo yagé circuit + Cauca close |
| Historical depth | Abolition to cane boom | Rubber boom scandal + conquest memory |

The 2010 *Sun Gives* chapter and 2009 preface to Devil pull the earlier book toward the narrative and *dépense* concerns that 1987 already performed — a retrospective alignment of the oeuvre.

Mechanics: Taussig’s concepts are **image-first**. They name how representations work in political economy and ritual, not variables for coding. That is their power and their vulnerability to charges of unfalsifiability.

### Verbatim analysis — Author’s Note (1987)

> Killing and torture and sorcery are real as death is real. But why people do these things, and how the answers to that question affect the question—that is not answerable outside of the effects of the real carried through time by people in action. That is why my subject is not the truth of being but the social being of truth, not whether facts are real but what the politics of their interpretation and representation are.

This paragraph is the anti-relativist insurance policy and the method claim at once. Facts of killing are not denied; the object is the **politics of interpretation** — which is exactly where Casement’s FO style, Hardenburg’s melodrama, and company *conquistar* speech do causal work. Readers who want only body counts will be frustrated; readers who want only discourse without corpses will be rebuked.

### Verbatim analysis — colonial hegemony and terror (1987 ch.1)

> …we would be unwise to overlook the role of terror. And by this I mean us to think-through-terror, which as well as being a physiological state is also a social one whose special features allow it to serve as the mediator par excellence of colonial hegemony: the space of death where the Indian, African, and white gave birth to a New World.

"Think-through-terror" is imperative method: terror is not only what actors feel but a medium of rule. The New World birth image binds African slavery, Indigenous conquest, and white Christian minority rule into one death-space culture — deliberately hemispheric, then specified in Putumayo detail.

### Verbatim analysis — montage and shamanism (Author’s Note)

> …the carrying over into history of the principle of montage, as I learned that principle not only from terror, but from Putumayo shamanism with its adroit, albeit unconscious, use of the magic of history and its healing power.

Learning method from the object is the 1987 book’s signature. Montage is not decorative modernism pasted onto ethnography; it is claimed as the indigenous-and-terror-taught form of historical presentation. Whether that claim romanticizes "unconscious" shamanic modernism is a standing critique; the text’s answer is that the magic is also a colonial creation.

### Style as politics

Taussig’s prose — long montaged sentences, literary citation, refusal of topic-sentence bureaucracy — is itself a claim about what kinds of sentences can carry terror without laundering it into FO moderation or melodrama’s thrills. Whether that claim succeeds is the central reception split. Admirers find the only honest form; detractors find obscurantism. The wiki records both as positions; it does not launder style into truth.

## The Empirical Base (fieldwork/data examined)

### Cauca (1980 primary)

Multi-year residence and political engagement among Afro-Colombian free peasants and cane cutters (Puerto Tejada region); oral history of abolition, *indiviso*, plantain–cocoa ecology, Liberal/Conservative wars, cane agribusiness "Green Monster." Strength: depth, historical ethnography, named rites (devil contract, baptism of money). Limit: male-centered devil contracts; plantation owners’ archives thinner than worker lore in the book’s balance.

### Bolivia (1980 secondary)

Explicitly synthesised from June Nash and others — **not** Taussig’s own mine residence at the same intensity. Wiki must not launder this into primary mine ethnography.

### Putumayo terror (1987 historical)

Casement blue book, Hardenburg, Iquitos press, Paredes file, Whiffen, Robuchon, Rocha, company speech. Strength: sustained reading of mediation itself as data. Limit: not a new demographic census of the boom; death-toll figures remain period claims.

### Putumayo/SW Colombia healing (1987 ethnographic)

Recurrent fieldwork 1969–1985; yagé nights with Santiago Mutumbajoy and others; colonist patients (José García, Eliseo); Florencio illness narrative; silleros/Capuchin road history; soldiers; priests; market *magia*; closing Cauca Marlene chapter. Strength: multi-year circuit, racial and class range of patients, historical memory in song. Limit: anonymity; author as participant; male shaman as theoretical center despite women in the ethnography; regional specialists may revise.

**Sampling frame.** Taussig’s Colombia is a specific SW corridor: southern Cauca cane frontier; Nariño highlands; Putumayo foothills and rivers; market towns where herbalists, *magia* books, priests, soldiers, and shamans cross. Generalizations beyond that corridor are his theoretical risk, not the wiki’s wiki-voice.

**Time depth.** Ethnographic present is banned. Cauca free-peasantry claims attach to abolition 1851–52 onward and 1970s fieldwork; Putumayo terror claims attach to ~1900–1913 documentation; yagé claims attach to 1969–85 sessions. When Taussig cites sixteenth-century entrada (Pérez de Quesada) or Capuchin chronicles, those are historical layers inside the montage, not timeless Indian essence.

**Evidence classes mixed.** Ethnographic strong (Cauca; yagé); textual/archival strong for rubber scandal; survey/statistical weak; genetic/linguistic minimal. Independent convergence is limited: the books are powerful single-author interpretive syntheses. Promotion of any claim to wiki voice requires either multi-source documentary consensus (rubber scandal basic facts) or clear attribution.

**Ethics record:** political solidarity with peasant struggle; pseudonym Mateo Mina; some names changed 1987; no Tierney-style scandal in these sources. Colonial complicity is the book’s topic, including anthropology’s (Whiffen). Positionality is declared more through montage craft than through confessional reflexivity chapters.

**Gender, race, limits.** Both monographs center male ritual specialists while depending on women as patients, household anchors, Marian devotees, and — in Marlene’s case — narrative closure through gendered violence. Afro-Colombian Cauca is central; Indigenous Putumayo is central; white colonists appear as patients, managers, and missionaries. The racial triangle (*las tres potencias*) is explicit in 1987, yet the theoretical "Indian magic" composite can still flatten ethnonymic diversity (Inga, Siona, Cofán, Huitoto-as-image). Future society pages must disaggregate what Taussig montages.

### Debt, stocks, and the refusal of smooth explanation

Company and local speech treated peons’ debts as alienable obligations that behaved like persons being sold; Rocha heard this as deformation of language; Taussig heard it as the linguistic face of a regime that already treated persons as rubber-delivery functions. The stocks freeze the body while the forest and the world market refuse to freeze value — an image Taussig uses to refuse pinning terror into the "stocks of explanation." The metaphor warns the analyst: explanation can mimic the station manager’s gaze. That warning is why his books frustrate policy readers and why they remain theoretically alive.


## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

- Celebrated as pathbreaking on colonial violence and on experimental form.
- Criticized as difficult, literary, insufficiently quantitative on rubber demography.
- Political location: anti-imperialist, peasant-syndicate-adjacent, dictatorship-aware — not neutral science.
- Ong’s 1987 engagement shows rapid uptake and productive disagreement on whether devil/*hantu* images always mediate use-/exchange-value contradictions.
- 2009 anniversary self-critique softens omniscient Marxist voice toward storytelling and Bataillean *dépense*.

### Reception map (positions, not verdicts)

**Position A — Landmark theorist of colonial violence and magic.** Holds that Taussig revealed how terror and commodity fetishism work through images; method innovations justified by object.

**Position B — Literary anthropologist, weak historian.** Holds that death tolls, company accounts, and demographic structure are under-specified; style obscures testable claims.

**Position C — Romantic of use-value / wildness.** Holds that peasantry and shamanic wildness are idealized as critique of capital; risk of inverted exoticism.

**Position D — Productive foil.** Ong and others take the mediating-image formula and revise its content (e.g. *hantu* claiming humanity rather than use-/exchange-value elegance).

The wiki records A–D without adjudication. `replication_status` on the studies is `reanalyzed-contested` (1980, anniversary self-critique) and `not-applicable` (1987 interpretive historical ethnography).

### Politics of citation

Citing only the yagé montage chapters while skipping Putumayo terror produces a wellness-compatible Taussig the 1987 book rejects. Citing only culture of terror while skipping healing montage produces a tragedy without the book’s claim that healing trips power’s disorder. Responsible use in this wiki keeps the diptych intact.

### Medical anthropology claim

[[medical-anthropology]] can claim the yagé half; [[symbolic-healing]] can claim efficacy-through-image. Taussig refuses the therapeutic reduction that would make yagé "really" psychotherapy. Efficacy, for him, runs through colonial history and epic theater, including the patient’s purchase of Indian magical power as colonial product. That makes him awkward for applied clinical appropriation and central for critical medical anthropology.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Without Taussig, English-language anthropology of the 1980s would still have had Wolf, Mintz, Nash, and *Writing Culture* — but it would have lacked this particular splice of **commodity fetishism + colonial terror narrative + yagé montage**. The portable concepts (space of death, culture of terror, epistemic murk) would likely have been invented under other names in state-terror sociology or trauma studies; the Putumayo–Cauca paired national field might have stayed split between rubber historians and cane ethnographers. Counterfactual: a more conventional historical monograph on Arana could have fixed death tolls better and taught image-politics worse. The field got the reverse tradeoff.

If the 1987 book had never been written, the 1980 devil book might have remained Taussig’s sole citation classic in economic anthropology, and medical anthropology’s experimental wing would have drawn its montage metaphors from film theory without a Putumayo case. If only 1987 existed without 1980, the wild-man analysis would lack the use-/exchange-value scaffolding that makes colonial capitalism’s metaphysics explicit. The diptych is the legacy.

Influence pathways observable even without full citation bibliometrics in this vault:

1. **Anthropology of violence** — culture of terror as reusable frame for dictatorships and extractive frontiers.
2. **Medical anthropology** — yagé nights as anti-communitas ritual; healing as historical image-work.
3. **Experimental ethnography** — permission structure for non-linear, literary monographs in the 1990s–2000s.
4. **Occult economies / millennial capitalism** — devil contracts as ancestors of later witchcraft-and-neoliberalism literature (Comaroffs et al. — not yet ingested).
5. **Amazonian studies** — obligatory cite on rubber boom representation, even when specialists disagree on demography.

Negative legacy pathways: selective citation that wellness-washes yagé; selective citation that aestheticizes terror without the book’s anti-catharsis clause; graduate-seminar difficulty that becomes status performance rather than reading.

`trained` remains unknown from these sources. Rivalry is more intertextual than personal: functionalist magic theory, historicist anthropology, Turnerian ritual theory, and human-rights realism are structural opponents. Afterlives include occult-economies literature, anthropology of violence, ayahuasca studies (often detaching yagé from Putumayo terror — a drift the 1987 book would resist), and experimental ethnographic writing programs.

Taussig’s significance for this wiki is double: (1) he supplies a dense Colombian national field linking cane, mines (via Nash), and rubber; (2) he forces method pages and concept pages to handle image, narrative, and montage as first-class social facts. He is not a founder of a school with a large named student cohort in these sources; he is a **reference-point author** whose vocabulary escaped its cases. Hub status is justified by two major monographs ingested, consequential controversy over style and evidence, and rich primary text for verbatim analysis.

### Comparative placement among ingested thinkers

Relative to other wiki thinkers already on disk:

- Versus [[marx-karl]]: Taussig is a cultural reader of fetishism, not a reconstruction of *Capital*’s value theory; he needs Marx but does not replace him.
- Versus [[malinowski-bronislaw]]: explicit foil on magic-as-anxiety; also a foil on ethnographic authority’s omniscient calm.
- Versus Frankfurt [[adorno-theodor-w|Adorno]]/Horkheimer on [[mimesis]]: shared interest in imitative relations under domination; Taussig’s colonial mirror and later *Mimesis and Alterity* are Amazonian/New World specifications still only partly ingested.

- Versus Boasian historicists: Taussig’s history is Benjaminian shock, not culture-area trait distribution.

He is closer to a **writer-anthropologist of the critical turn** than to a methods textbook author. Graduate training that assigns only his concepts without his cases produces slogan Taussig; this hub exists to keep cases attached.
