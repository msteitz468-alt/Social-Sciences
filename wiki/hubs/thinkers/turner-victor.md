# Victor Turner — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. The [[turner-victor|summary]] owns overview/contributions/
> genealogy/critiques/historiography at normal resolution; this page owns the depth.
> Reciprocal link: the summary points here by full path; this page's `thinker_page` points
> back to [[turner-victor]].

Victor Witter Turner (1920–1983) built one of twentieth-century anthropology's most portable
conceptual toolkits out of the ritual life of a single Central African people. From roughly
two and a half years among the [[ndembu]] he extracted a theory of the ritual symbol, a method
for reading it, and — crossing what he called the "threshold" between African ethnography and
general social theory — the paired concepts of **[[liminality]]** and **[[communitas]]** that
now circulate far beyond anthropology. This analysis reconstructs how he got there: the
Manchester formation that taught him to see conflict and process, the Ndembu fieldwork that
gave him his symbols, the two ingested books in which the theory took shape, and the
controversy over how far the concepts can travel.

## Formation and Training

Turner was born in Glasgow in 1920, the son of an electrical engineer and an actress; the
theatrical inheritance is worth noting, because the dramaturgical metaphor — society as
performance, ritual as drama — became the through-line of his whole career. He read English
literature at University College London before the war, served as a non-combatant (bomb-disposal)
during it, and came to anthropology afterward, taking his training at University College and then
at Manchester under [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]. His self-description in *The Ritual Process*
is exact and revealing: "I was reared in the orthodox social-structuralist tradition of British
anthropology, which… regards a 'society' as a system of social positions"; but "like other major
insights, the structural viewpoint has become in the course of time a fetter and a fetish." The
whole arc of his thought is the loosening of that fetter without discarding what structure
genuinely explained.

The decisive institutional fact is the **Rhodes-Livingstone Institute** (founded 1938 in Lusaka,
directed successively by Godfrey Wilson and Gluckman), which sent Turner and his wife Edith into
the field. Turner joined it as a Research Officer; it continued to support him after his
employment ended. The Institute and the Manchester department together constituted the
[[manchester-school|Manchester School]], whose distinctive commitments — conflict as normal and
often cohesive, the [[extended-case-method|extended case]] over the normative composite, social
process over static equilibrium — were the water Turner swam in before he ever wrote about a
symbol.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Teacher.** [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] is the load-bearing tie: Turner's earliest book, *Schism
and Continuity in an African Society* (1957), is a Manchester extended-case study of Ndembu
village fission, and Gluckman's conflict-and-cohesion program (see
*[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955|Custom and Conflict in Africa]]*, [[ritual-of-rebellion]])
is the frame Turner inherits and then transcends. Even in the ritual books, Gluckman is a
constant presence — cited on the exaggeration of sex differences by custom, on the Swazi Incwala,
on the Zulu Nomkubulwana rites of status reversal.

**The source that made the theory.** [[van-gennep-arnold|Arnold van Gennep]]'s 1909 *Les rites
de passage* supplied the separation/margin/aggregation scheme. Turner's originality lay in
seizing on the *middle* phase, which van Gennep had merely named, and making its content and
experience — [[liminality]], anti-structure, [[communitas]] — into a general theory. Van Gennep
had been marginal to the Durkheimian establishment; Turner is, in effect, his posthumous
executor.

**Antagonist retained.** British [[structural-functionalism]] (Radcliffe-Brown) is the tradition
Turner reacts against and never fully leaves. He keeps structural analysis of Ndembu village
organization (citing [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]]'s "irreducible principle" for matriliny) while
insisting that "the 'social' is not identical with the 'social-structural.'"

**Contemporaries and interlocutors.** Turner belongs to the founding cohort of
[[symbolic-anthropology]] alongside [[geertz-clifford|Clifford Geertz]] (the interpretive wing)
and [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] (the classification/pollution wing). Douglas is especially
important inside the texts: Turner adopts her *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and
Danger]]* (1966) hypothesis that the unclassifiable is treated as polluting (see
[[ritual-pollution]], [[purity-pollution-theory]]), then refines it. He engages [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] at arm's length —
accepting that ritual symbolism shows "homologies, oppositions, correlations, and transformations"
but insisting "the whole person, not just the Ndembu 'mind,' is existentially involved." And he
names a methodological debt to [[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]], the dedicatee of *The Forest of
Symbols*, whose insistence on native exegesis he made a charter.

## Career Phases

**1. Manchester village studies (1950s).** Fieldwork 1950–1954; *Schism and Continuity* (1957)
and the Rhodes-Livingstone Paper *Chihamba, the White Spirit* (1962). Here Turner is a
card-carrying Manchester analyst of social structure and conflict, working out the
[[social-drama]] as a unit for tracking village fission. He recounts coming to ritual only
reluctantly, after nine months of kinship, genealogy, and census work: "I would have to overcome
my prejudice against ritual and start to investigate it."

**2. The theory of the symbol (1958–1967).** The essays collected in *The Forest of Symbols* —
"Symbols in Ndembu Ritual" (read 1958, published 1964) and "Betwixt and Between" among them —
build the [[dominant-symbol]] apparatus and the [[symbolic-exegesis]] method, and launch
[[liminality]]. This is Turner reconceiving the symbol as a dynamic "force" in a Lewinian field,
against a static classificatory view.

**3. The processual turn (1966–1969).** The 1966 Morgan Lectures, published as *The Ritual
Process* (1969), cross from Ndembu ethnography to general theory: [[communitas]], the three
forms, the structure/communitas dialectic, the powers of the weak, status elevation and reversal.

**4. Symbol, performance, pilgrimage (1970s–1983).** *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors* (1974) and
work on Christian pilgrimage (with Edith Turner), the formal four-phase social drama, and the
collaboration with theatre director Richard Schechner that carried his ideas into performance
studies. Turner moved to the United States (Cornell, Chicago, Virginia); his 1958 conversion to
Roman Catholicism increasingly informed his interest in ritual, pilgrimage, and communitas.
(These later works are not yet ingested.)

Consider the mechanics of the two most consequential moves.

**The symbol as a charged, two-poled thing.** Turner's definition — the ritual symbol is "the
smallest unit of ritual which still retains the specific properties of ritual behavior" — is
deliberately atomistic; he works up from symbols because "the Ndembu have a paucity of myths."
The analytic engine is **polarization**: a [[dominant-symbol]] clusters its meanings at two
poles, an "ideological" (normative, social, jural) pole and a "sensory" (physiological, "frankly,
even flagrantly, physiological") pole. In ritual the two poles exchange qualities — norms become
"saturated with emotion," gross drives are "ennobled" — so that ritual works as "a mechanism that
periodically converts the obligatory into the desirable." That last clause is Turner answering a
Durkheimian question (why social obligation is *felt* as desirable) with a mechanism rather than a
postulate. It is the hinge of his whole symbol theory, and it is why he insists, against
[[wilson-monica|Wilson]] and Nadel, that the analyst must attend to the sensory pole the
informants underplay, and, against the psychoanalysts, that the ideological pole is not mere
rationalization but refers to "social facts… that have an empirical reality exterior to the
psyches of individuals."

**Liminality → communitas.** Turner's most cited passage defines liminal personae as "neither
here nor there; they are betwixt and between the positions assigned and arrayed by law, custom,
convention, and ceremonial." Stripped of status, property, insignia, and kinship position, the
neophyte becomes "a *tabula rasa*, a blank slate," "human *prima materia*." Crucially, the liminal
group has a structure of its own: total submission to instructors, total equality among equals. In
"Betwixt and Between" Turner names this equality only descriptively; in *The Ritual Process* he
names it **communitas** — "an unstructured or rudimentarily structured and relatively
undifferentiated *comitatus*, community, or even communion of equal individuals." He uses the Latin
to mark it as a *modality of relationship*, not a place. And he sets it into a dialectic:
"maximization of communitas provokes maximization of structure, which in its turn produces
revolutionary strivings for renewed communitas." The three forms — existential/spontaneous ("what
the hippies today would call 'a happening'"), normative, and ideological — chart the "decline and
fall" of spontaneous fellowship into institution and law, "the fate of all spontaneous communitas
in history."

## The Symbol Theory in Its Own Terms

Two concepts do the work in *The Forest of Symbols*, and it is worth quoting Turner's own
architecture before summarizing it, because the mechanics matter more than the labels.

**The dominant symbol, illustrated.** Turner introduces the whole apparatus through a single
object: at *Nkang'a*, the girls' puberty ritual, "a novice is wrapped in a blanket and laid at the
foot of a *mudyi* sapling." The tree — *Diplorrhyncus condylocarpon* — "is conspicuous for its
white latex, which exudes in milky beads if the thin bark is scratched," and "therefore I propose
to call it 'the milk tree' henceforward." From that single observable property Turner works
outward through three data classes he names explicitly — "(1) external form and observable
characteristics; (2) interpretations offered by specialists and laymen; (3) significant contexts
largely worked out by the anthropologist" — the method he calls **symbolic exegesis**. Ndembu women
give him a stack of meanings for the same object: the milk tree is "the 'senior' (*mukulumpi*) tree
of the ritual"; it stands for "human breast milk and also for the breasts that supply it"; it is
"the tree of a mother and her child." A male ritual specialist's text, which Turner translates
literally, pushes the meaning up a level of abstraction: "The milk tree is the place of all mothers
of the lineage... It represents the ancestress of women and men... That is the place of our tribal
custom (*muchidi*), where we began." From this Turner concludes that "at one level of abstraction
the milk tree stands for matriliny, the principle on which the continuity of Ndembu society
depends," and that "at its highest level of abstraction, therefore, the milk tree stands for the
unity and continuity of Ndembu society" — a gloss one "educated Ndembu" gave him directly: "the
milk tree was like the British flag above the administrative headquarters. 'Mudyi is our flag,' he
said."

But the third data class — context, what people actually *do* with the symbol — contradicts the
first two. In the puberty ritual's early phases, Turner observes, "it is the women who dance around
the milk tree and initiate the recumbent novice," and the tree becomes "the 'flag' of Ndembu
women" specifically: "it mobilizes them in opposition to the men. For the women sing songs taunting
the men and for a time will not let men dance in their circle." The same object that informants
describe as the harmonious "flag of the Ndembu" is, in action, a rallying point for one sex against
the other. Turner treats this discrepancy as data, not noise — the disjunction between normative
gloss and observed behavior is where a symbol's social work actually shows.

**Three properties, and the poles-of-meaning thesis.** From the milk tree and comparable cases
Turner derives three "major empirical properties of dominant symbols": **condensation** (many
things and actions represented in a single formation); **unification of disparate significata**
(the milk tree stands for "women's breasts, motherhood, a novice at *Nkang'a*, the principle of
matriliny, a specific matrilineage, learning, and the unity and persistence of Ndembu society," all
linked by "the themes of nourishment and dependence"); and, the analytically load-bearing one,
**polarization of meaning**: "Not only the milk tree but all other dominant Ndembu symbols possess
two clearly distinguishable poles of meaning. At one pole is found a cluster of significata that
refer to components of the moral and social orders of Ndembu society... Let us call the first of
these the 'ideological pole,' and the second the 'sensory pole.'" At the sensory pole, other
dominant symbols represent "blood, male and female genitalia, semen, urine, and feces" — the "gross"
in a double sense Turner insists on: "gross in a general way, taking no account of detail," and
"gross" as "frankly, even flagrantly, physiological." Engaging Sapir's referential/condensation
distinction, Turner credits Sapir's stress on the symbol's "predominance of emotional or *orectic*
quality" but argues Sapir "tends to underestimate the importance of what I have called the
ideological (or, I would add, normative) pole of meaning." The payoff is a mechanism, not just a
taxonomy: "Ritual, scholars are coming to see, is precisely a mechanism that periodically converts
the obligatory into the desirable... Norms and values, on the one hand, become saturated with
emotion, while the gross and basic emotions become ennobled through contact with social values. The
irksomeness of moral constraint is transformed into the 'love of virtue.'" This is Turner's own
answer to a question he inherits from Durkheim — why obligation is *felt* as desirable — worked out
symbol by symbol rather than asserted as postulate.

**Dominant versus instrumental symbols.** Turner distinguishes symbols that anchor a whole ritual
from symbols that merely serve its stated purpose. Dominant symbols — the *ishikenu* tree in
curative cults, the *nfunda* medicine and the *chikoli* tree in boys' circumcision, the milk tree
itself at circumcision, the red *mukula* tree in the post-circumcision phase — "possess considerable
autonomy with regard to the aims of the rituals in which they appear" and can be treated, he
suggests, borrowing from Whitehead, as "eternal objects" — "relatively fixed points in both the
social and cultural structures." **Instrumental symbols**, by contrast, "must be seen in terms of
its wider context, i.e., in terms of the total system of symbols which makes up a given kind of
ritual" and function as "means of attaining" a ritual's "explicitly expressed goals" — fruit-bearing
branches used to make a woman fecund, for instance, valued for what they are *for* rather than what
they axiomatically *are*.

**Isoma and the anatomy of affliction.** *The Ritual Process* opens its own symbol chapter with a
second worked case, the women's cult **Isoma**, performed for a woman who has suffered repeated
stillbirths or miscarriages. Turner glosses the name's folk etymology through *lufwisha*, "to make
die one after the other," and reconstructs the etiology in an informant's own words: a matrilineal
kinswoman is believed to have gone to a stream source and spoken a *chisaku* — "a curse spoken by a
living person to arouse a shade" — against the sufferer. As the informant explains it to Turner: "At
Isoma they behead a red cock. This stands for the *chisaku* or misfortune through which people die,
it must go away... The *chisaku* is death, which must not happen to the woman patient; it is
sickness..., and this suffering is from the grudge (*chitela*) of a witch (*muloji*)." Turner reads
the rite's implicit aims as threefold: "restoration of the right relation between matriliny and
marriage; reconstruction of the conjugal relations between wife and husband; and making the woman,
and hence the marriage and lineage, fruitful" — a diagnosis that treats a woman's reproductive
misfortune as a symptom of strain in the whole matrilineal-marital system, not a private biological
fact. Isoma is Turner's clearest demonstration that Ndembu ritual symbolism is a working theory of
social affliction, encoded in trees, cursed water, and a beheaded cock, and recoverable only by
holding form, exegesis, and context together.

## The Empirical Base

Turner's authority rests on the [[ndembu]] corpus, and it is worth examining critically what that
base is and is not. It is **deep**: some 2.5 years of participant observation among a people of
roughly eighteen thousand, with an unusual density of named informants and verbatim exegesis. The
ritual specialist **Muchona the Hornet** and the lay assistant **Windson Kashinakaji** are not
anonymous "informants" but analyzed persons; the essay "Muchona the Hornet, Interpreter of
Religion" makes the ethnographer–informant relationship itself an object of study, and reflects
honestly on its costs (Turner worries that returning Muchona to a world that "could only make a
'witchdoctor' of him" is a kind of death). Turner's reflexivity extends to his data: in the
*nfunda* medicine preparation he carefully records where his own observation diverged from
informants' accounts of ingredients (he did not himself see cindered foreskins added), and reasons
about whom to believe rather than smoothing the record. This is a model of how to report the seams
in ethnographic evidence.

It has characteristic **limits**. First, the base is a single society at a single fieldwork moment
(early 1950s), under colonial administration, mission activity, and labor migration — Turner notes
modern shame about ritual nakedness creeping in, and the recency of the Kanongesha Native Authority
as an administrative creation. The Ndembu of *The Forest of Symbols* are not a timeless people (see
[[ndembu]] Identity Cautions). Second, the theory's most portable claims outrun the Ndembu evidence.
The **color-triad universality** argument Turner offers explicitly "to stimulate controversy,"
reaching to the Dogon, the Bemba, the Upanishads, and Palaeolithic red ochre — this is his own
attributed comparative argument, not a wiki-voice fact, and it strains the evidence-class boundary
between one society's classification and a claimed human universal. Third, and most consequential:
the comparative chapters of *The Ritual Process* (the Franciscans, the Sahajiya, medieval Europe,
the hippies) rest on *secondary literature*, not fieldwork. When Turner reads McKim Marriott's Holi
account he even disputes Marriott's own framing — insisting it is "the liberated experience of
communitas," not "libido," that "inundates all established hierarchies." The move from a
participant-observed Ndembu seclusion lodge to a claim about the Franciscan Spirituals is a change
of evidence class that the concepts' critics fasten on.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Turner's reception is a study in a concept outrunning its author. "Liminality" and "communitas"
escaped anthropology within a decade, colonizing ritual studies, theology, history, English
literature, political science, and — via Schechner — theatre and performance studies. In his 1977
Foreword Turner records, with some pride and some defensiveness, that the book had been "cited
repeatedly by scholars in such diverse fields as history, the history of religions, English
literature, political science, theology, and drama."

The specific channel of that escape is worth naming. Richard Schechner, the theatre director and
founder of performance studies, took Turner's social-drama sequence and liminal/liminoid distinction
directly into rehearsal and performance theory, and the two co-taught and co-published; through that
collaboration the vocabulary reached theatre departments before it reached most of sociology. Ritual
studies (Ronald Grimes and others) adopted the dominant-symbol/polarization apparatus as a working
method for reading non-Ndembu ritual, and pilgrimage studies — Turner's own later terrain, with
Edith Turner, on Christian shrines — used communitas as its organizing concept for the affect of
mass devotional travel. Each uptake kept the vocabulary and dropped, to varying degrees, the Ndembu
evidentiary base that had disciplined it; that is precisely the mechanism the overgeneralization
critique below targets.

The persistent charge is **overgeneralization** — that concepts forged from preliterate ritual lose
purchase in complex societies. Turner reports it fairly: "More than once I have been accused of
overgeneralizing and of misapplying concepts like 'liminality' and 'communitas.'" His reply mixes
the adage "the proof of the pudding is in the eating" with a genuine concession: modern
communitas-seeking groups "have not yet developed a structure capable of maintaining social and
economic order over long periods." The dispute is recorded, unadjudicated, on
[[communitas-scale-debate]]. A related worry is that **communitas smuggles a value-preference** —
a Catholic-convert's romance of fellowship — into what claims to be analysis; and its family
resemblance to Durkheim's [[collective-effervescence]] is close enough that some read it as a
renamed effervescence with the sociology of religion attached.

On politics and ethics the record is comparatively quiet — Turner is not a Mead or a Chagnon. The
reflexive point that matters is his relation to **colonial knowledge**: the Manchester School's
distinctive virtue was to make the colonial situation, labor migration, and social conflict objects
of analysis rather than background, and Turner's honesty about his informants and his data is part
of that. The later reflexive/"Writing Culture" critique nonetheless lands on him: the analyst's
authority to read a people's symbols *better than they articulate them* — Turner's **positional**
level of meaning — is precisely the authority that generation questioned.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

What does anthropology look like without Turner? The [[social-drama]] and the extended-case method
were Manchester property; someone in Gluckman's circle would likely have developed processual case
analysis. The color-triad argument would probably have been made by someone (the comparative
materials were available). But **liminality-as-a-general-theory and communitas are hard to imagine
without him.** Van Gennep's scheme sat available for sixty years; it took Turner's particular
combination — a Manchester eye for process, a literary sensibility trained on drama, an Ndembu
corpus rich in exegesis, and a personal preoccupation with ritual deepened by conversion — to turn
the neglected middle phase into a theory that theatre directors and theologians would use. The move
that was *structural* (processual case analysis, coming anyway) can be separated from the move that
was *personal* (the liminality/communitas synthesis, contingent on Turner). His counterfactual
significance is therefore high: remove him and the vocabulary that now lets a historian call a
carnival "liminal" or a movement "communitas" does not obviously exist.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

The primary base for this analysis is Turner's own prefatory and autobiographical framing in the
two ingested volumes — the Introduction and essay-provenance notes of *The Forest of Symbols*, and
the Foreword and Morgan-Lecture framing of *The Ritual Process*, including the 1977 Foreword's reply
to critics. These are cross-checked against his self-citations to *Schism and Continuity* (1957) and
*Chihamba* (1962). The wiki's Turner coverage is at present **two-volume-anchored**; the later
performance and pilgrimage works (*Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors*, 1974; the pilgrimage studies with
Edith Turner) are the obvious next ingests, and would let this analysis do fuller justice to the
performance turn and to Edith Turner's own contribution, which the ritual books already flag by
crediting her as a fieldwork collaborator "from the beginning of this venture." Turner's reputation
has risen rather than settled since his 1983 death, and the concepts' very ubiquity is now itself an
object of study — a sign of a thinker whose terms became infrastructure.
