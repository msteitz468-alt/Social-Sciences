# James George Frazer — Deep Analysis

## Formation and Training

James George Frazer was born in Glasgow in 1854 into a solidly middle-class Scottish Presbyterian household; he died in Cambridge in 1941, long after the paradigm he embodied had been dismantled by the disciplines he helped to create. His formation was **classical, not ethnographic**. He was educated for the traditional Scottish–Cambridge path of the humanities: Glasgow University, then **Trinity College, Cambridge**, where he took the Classical Tripos and was elected a **Fellow of Trinity** — a status he held for the rest of his life. The Fellowship gave him the material condition of his career: a library, a room, a stipend, and no requirement of fieldwork.

Frazer never conducted ethnographic research. He never lived among a non-European people as an observer, never learned a field language for research purposes, and never designed a study of living ritual on the ground. His "field" was the Cambridge library and a correspondence network of missionaries, colonial officers, travellers, and folklorists who sent him reports. That absence is not a biographical curiosity; it is the **epistemic signature** of his entire corpus. Everything he wrote about "savage" belief is mediated by other people's observation, filtered through Victorian and Edwardian genre conventions of the traveller's tale and the missionary letter.

Intellectually he was formed first as a **classicist** reading Latin and Greek religion, myth, and antiquities, and second as a **comparative anthropologist** under the spell of [[tylor-edward-burnett|E. B. Tylor]]'s intellectualist evolutionism. The classical training supplied the problem that *The Golden Bough* never leaves: the strange priesthood of Diana at **Nemi** — the *Rex Nemorensis*, the King of the Wood who holds office only until a stronger challenger slays him. The anthropological training supplied the method: treat that classical puzzle as a **survival** of a once-general stage of human thought, and explain it by marshalling parallels from "lower" cultures worldwide. Between those two poles — Virgil and Nemi on one side, Central Australia and the White Nile on the other — Frazer built the largest comparative apparatus of late Victorian anthropology.

Personal style mattered. Contemporaries and biographers (Ackerman above all) describe a **reclusive library worker**: shy, industrious, married to Lilly Grove (herself a writer on dance and folklore), knighted in 1914, famous for the bulk of his footnotes rather than for conversation or institutional empire-building. He did not found a school in the sense of training fieldworkers; he founded a **literature** — a prose style and a mythic vocabulary that literary modernism would strip for parts even as anthropology discarded the theory.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Upstream (masters and donors of method).**

- **[[tylor-edward-burnett|Edward Burnett Tylor]]** is the decisive intellectual creditor. Frazer worked inside Tylor's package: [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity of mankind]], the [[comparative-method]], [[survivals]], and the intellectualist reconstruction of "primitive philosophy." Where Tylor made [[animism]] the minimum definition of religion and treated [[magic]] as mistaken association of ideas, Frazer *systematized* magic into the dual laws of similarity and contact, subordinated taboo to negative magic, and pushed the evolutionary story into an explicit **magic → religion → science** sequence. Frazer was not Tylor's formal pupil in a classroom sense; the relation is one of **program inheritance** — see the summary's `trained_under` note and the Tylor hub's downstream claim that Frazer was Tylor's most consequential intellectual child.
- **Wilhelm Mannhardt** supplied the vegetation-spirit program that Frazer amplified into a world-historical thesis. Mannhardt's studies of European peasant customs (May-trees, leaf-clad mummers, corn-spirits) gave Frazer the type-cases for reading Adonis, Attis, Osiris, and European folk-rites as embodiments of the yearly death and revival of plant life. Frazer repeatedly cites Mannhardt as authority on solar and vegetation interpretations of fire-festivals and May processions, even where he later revises Mannhardt's readings.
- The broader Victorian evolutionist cohort — **[[spencer-herbert|Spencer]]**, **[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]**, Lubbock, McLennan — furnished the stage-language and the confidence that "savagery" and "civilization" could be arrayed on one developmental ladder ([[social-evolutionism]]; [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]).

**Lateral rivals and partial allies.**

Within comparative religion, Frazer's intellectualism competed with sociological accounts of the sacred (Durkheim's *Elementary Forms* appeared as Frazer's third edition was still expanding) and with diffusionist and historical-particularist challenges. He shared with Robertson Smith certain interests in sacrifice and Semitic religion but drove them toward vegetation and kingship rather than toward totemic sacrament as a sociological type. His *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910) entered a crowded field already contested by McLennan, Durkheim, Freud, and others.

**Downstream (users, rebels, and scavengers).**

- **Bronisław Malinowski** began as an admirer of Frazer's learning and dedicated early work in a Frazerian climate; as a fieldworker and functionalist he then **rejected** conjectural history and [[survivals]], insisting that rites be explained by present social function. The functionalist break is the institutional death of Frazer's method in British social anthropology.
- **A. R. Radcliffe-Brown** likewise built structural-functionalism against evolutionist conjectural reconstruction.
- **Franz Boas** and Boasian particularism in the United States made the global comparative method Frazer practised the paradigm of methodological error (decontextualized trait-lifting, psychic-unity shortcuts, unilinear stages).
- **E. E. Evans-Pritchard**, in *Theories of Primitive Religion* and related work, attacked the intellectualist reconstruction as "If I were a horse" rationalism — projecting a Victorian philosopher's ratiocination onto peoples whose thought is embedded in social relations, not in failed proto-science.
- **Literary modernism** — T. S. Eliot's notes to *The Waste Land*, Yeats, and a generation of poets and novelists — absorbed Frazer's *imagery* (the wounded king, the wasteland, the dying god, the golden bough) while ignoring or inverting his evolutionary ranking. Anthropology discarded the theory; literature kept the myth-bank.
- **Jonathan Z. Smith** and later historians of religions dismantled the **[[dying-and-rising-god]]** construct as a Frazerian (and Cumontian) synthesis that the primary texts do not uniformly support — a historiographical demolition of one of Frazer's most influential packages.

The genealogy is thus double-edged: Frazer **extends** Tylor and Mannhardt into the last great Victorian synthesis, and then becomes the **negative pole** against which professional anthropology defines method for half a century.

## Career Phases

**1. Classicist Fellow and first comparative sketches (to 1890).**  
Trinity Fellowship; classical scholarship as base; absorption of Tylor and Mannhardt; preparation of the first *Golden Bough*. The Nemi problem is already the spine: why does Diana's priest kill his predecessor, and why must he first pluck a bough identified with Virgil's Golden Bough?

**2. First and second *Golden Bough* (1890–1900).**  
The 1890 two-volume *Golden Bough* announces the program to a wide public. The 1900 second edition expands the comparative dossier. Frazer's reputation as the voice of comparative religion is made in these years; the book is read far beyond anthropology.

**3. Maximal system-building (c. 1900–1915).**  
*Totemism and Exogamy* (1910) in four volumes; the **third edition** of *The Golden Bough* in **twelve volumes** (1911–15) — the most extreme expression of the Victorian comparative catalogue. Knighthood in 1914 marks public recognition at the moment when the younger generation is already turning toward fieldwork. *The Belief in Immortality* (from 1913) continues the intellectualist program on another axis.

**4. Abridgement, biblical folklore, and elder status (1918–1941).**  
*Folk-Lore in the Old Testament* (1918) applies the same comparative machine to Hebrew narrative. The **1922 author's abridgement** of *The Golden Bough* — the edition most widely read and the one ingested for this wiki — compresses the twelve-volume apparatus into a single continuous argument. Frazer lives on into the age of Malinowski and Evans-Pritchard as a monument more than as a living research program. He dies in Cambridge in 1941.

Across these phases the intellectual *moves* do not radically reverse; they **accrete**. Frazer revises subordinate interpretations (e.g. solar vs. purificatory readings of fire-festivals; the precise identity of mistletoe and the Golden Bough; Balder as lightning-stroke vs. midsummer victim) while holding fixed the intellectualist core: magic as false science, religion as propitiation of personal powers, science as empirical successor, and the divine king / vegetation god as the key to Nemi.

The spine of the career is *[[frazer-golden-bough-study|The Golden Bough]]*. Frazer states the book's object with characteristic modesty-of-ambition and immodesty-of-scope:

> "The object of this book is, by meeting these conditions, to offer a fairly probable explanation of the priesthood of Nemi."

Two questions organize the whole:

> "first, why had Diana's priest at Nemi, the King of the Wood, to slay his predecessor? second, why before doing so had he to pluck the branch of a certain tree which the public opinion of the ancients identified with Virgil's Golden Bough?"

Everything else — Australian tooth-customs, Shilluk regicide, Adonis gardens, Balder's mistletoe, European Midsummer fires — is, in Frazer's design, **evidence on the way home to Nemi**.

### Mechanics: the intellectualist machine

Frazer's system, like Tylor's, is a **coupled apparatus**. Pull one joint and the structure fails — which is exactly what twentieth-century critics did.

1. **[[psychic-unity-of-mankind|Psychic unity]]** licenses treating a custom in Latium, a custom on the White Nile, and a custom in Central Australia as expressions of the same "first crude philosophy of life." Frazer is explicit at the outset:

> "For recent researches into the early history of man have revealed the essential similarity with which, under many superficial differences, the human mind has elaborated its first crude philosophy of life."

2. The **[[comparative-method]]** then *lifts* traits from their contexts and arrays them as parallels. Galton's problem (case non-independence) is not controlled; diffusion and shared history are not systematically distinguished from independent invention.

3. **[[sympathetic-magic]]** supplies the content of that "crude philosophy." Frazer's canonical analysis:

> "IF we analyse the principles of thought on which magic is based, they will probably be found to resolve themselves into two: first, that like produces like, or that an effect resembles its cause; and, second, that things which have once been in contact with each other continue to act on each other at a distance after the physical contact has been severed."

Both branches fall under one name:

> "Both branches of magic, the homoeopathic and the contagious, may conveniently be comprehended under the general name of Sympathetic Magic, since both assume that things act on each other at a distance through a secret sympathy."

The associationist psychology is the engine:

> "Homoeopathic magic is founded on the association of ideas by similarity: contagious magic is founded on the association of ideas by contiguity."

And the verdict:

> "magic is a spurious system of natural law as well as a fallacious guide of conduct; it is a false science as well as an abortive art."

4. **[[taboo]]** is folded into the same machine as the *negative* face of practical magic:

> "If we give the general name of magic to the whole erroneous system, both theoretical and practical, then taboo may be defined as the negative side of practical magic."

5. **Religion** is defined contrastively, as personal propitiation rather than impersonal coercion:

> "By religion, then, I understand a propitiation or conciliation of powers superior to man which are believed to direct and control the course of nature and of human life."

The crucial fork, in Frazer's words:

> "Are the forces which govern the world conscious and personal, or unconscious and impersonal? Religion, as a conciliation of the superhuman powers, assumes the former member of the alternative."

Pure magic, he argues, assumes the latter — and is therefore kin to science:

> "Wherever sympathetic magic occurs in its pure unadulterated form, it assumes that in nature one event follows another necessarily and invariably without the intervention of any spiritual or personal agency. Thus its fundamental conception is identical with that of modern science."

The flaw is not lawfulness but *which* laws:

> "Legitimately applied they yield science; illegitimately applied they yield magic, the bastard sister of science. It is therefore a truism, almost a tautology, to say that all magic is necessarily false and barren; for were it ever to become true and fruitful, it would no longer be magic but science."

6. From this contrast Frazer derives **priority of magic over religion** (a theoretical claim, not a dated archaeological finding):

> "it becomes probable that magic arose before religion in the evolution of our race, and that man essayed to bend nature to his wishes by the sheer force of spells and enchantments before he strove to coax and mollify a coy, capricious, or irascible deity by the soft insinuation of prayer and sacrifice."

7. **Public magic** then becomes a theory of political evolution: the successful magician rises toward chieftainship and kingship. Frazer's most political line:

> "For the rise of monarchy appears to be an essential condition of the emergence of mankind from savagery."

And the famous rehabilitation of magic's side-effects:

> "if the black art has done much evil, it has also been the source of much good; that if it is the child of error, it has yet been the mother of freedom and truth."

### Verbatim load-bearing passages — divine king, vegetation god, closing thesis

**On killing the divine king.** Frazer's mechanism is intellectualist and "sympathetic": the man-god's bodily vigour is bound to the world's fertility, so he must be slain *before* decay contaminates the cosmos:

> "The man-god must be killed as soon as he shows symptoms that his powers are beginning to fail, and his soul must be transferred to a vigorous successor before it has been seriously impaired by the threatened decay."

And:

> "by putting him to death before his natural force was abated, they would secure that the world should not fall into decay with the decay of the man-god."

The Shilluk (as reported to Frazer via Seligman) become the living type-case for Nemi:

> "the king must not be allowed to become ill or senile, lest with his diminishing vigour the cattle should sicken and fail to bear their increase, the crops should rot in the fields, and man, stricken with disease, should die in ever-increasing numbers."

Frazer's bridge back to the Alban hills:

> "On the whole the theory and practice of the divine kings of the Shilluk correspond very nearly to the theory and practice of the priests of Nemi, the Kings of the Wood, if my view of the latter is correct."

Read closely: the phrase **"if my view of the latter is correct"** is doing honest work. The Shilluk material does not independently *prove* Nemi; it is recruited as a parallel *under* the theory that also interprets Nemi. That circularity is the standing vulnerability of the comparative method as Frazer ran it.

**On dying-and-rising vegetation gods.** Frazer's synthesis, in his own words:

> "Under the names of Osiris, Tammuz, Adonis, and Attis, the peoples of Egypt and Western Asia represented the yearly decay and revival of life, especially of vegetable life, which they personified as a god who annually died and rose again from the dead."

Gardens of Adonis are read as homeopathic charms:

> "All these Adonis ceremonies, if I am right, were originally intended as charms to promote the growth or revival of vegetation; and the principle by which they were supposed to produce this effect was homoeopathic or imitative magic."

Hunger, not mysticism, is the alleged mainspring:

> "Hunger, felt or feared, was the mainspring of the worship of Adonis."

**On the external soul and the Golden Bough.** The Nemi resolution in the abridgement identifies the bough with mistletoe and the priest with an oak-spirit whose life is deposited outside his body:

> "so long as the soul remains unharmed in the place where he has deposited it, the man himself is immortal; nothing can kill his body, since his life is not in it."

And the climactic identification:

> "As an oak-spirit, his life or death was in the mistletoe on the oak, and so long as the mistletoe remained intact, he, like Balder, could not die. To slay him, therefore, it was necessary to break the mistletoe, and probably, as in the case of Balder, to throw it at him."

> "Here Virgil definitely describes the Golden Bough as growing on a holm-oak, and compares it with the mistletoe. The inference is almost inevitable that the Golden Bough was nothing but the mistletoe seen through the haze of poetry or of popular superstition."

**On the closing evolutionary parable.** In "Farewell to Nemi" Frazer states the sequence that names the [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]:

> "the movement of the higher thought, so far as we can trace it, has on the whole been from magic through religion to science."

Magic and science share faith in order; they differ in how order is known:

> "whereas the order on which magic reckons is merely an extension, by false analogy, of the order in which ideas present themselves to our minds, the order laid down by science is derived from patient and exact observation of the phenomena themselves."

He then undercuts triumphalism with a provisionalist coda often forgotten by caricaturists:

> "In the last analysis magic, religion, and science are nothing but theories of thought; and as science has supplanted its predecessors, so it may hereafter be itself superseded by some more perfect hypothesis, perhaps by some totally different way of looking at the phenomena - of registering the shadows on the screen - of which we in this generation can form no idea."

The three-thread web is the emblem of the whole system:

> "we may illustrate the course which thought has hitherto run by likening it to a web woven of three different threads - the black thread of magic, the red thread of religion, and the white thread of science"

And the book ends not with a theorem but with a tableau — succession formula and Angelus over the empty grove:

> "The temple of the sylvan goddess, indeed, has vanished and the King of the Wood no longer stands sentinel over the Golden Bough. But Nemi's woods are still green… Le roi est mort, vive le roi! Ave Maria!"

That ending reveals something the theory chapters hide: Frazer persuaded as much by **literary cadence** as by inference. Classic studies, as the Studies Hub standard notes, persuade as texts — and *The Golden Bough* is among the most textually seductive works ever written under the banner of science.

## The Empirical Base

Frazer's empirical base must be examined without either ancestor-worship or show-trial rhetoric.

**What it is.**  
A vast **secondary compilation**: classical authors (Virgil, Servius, Pliny, Caesar, Strabo…), missionary and traveller reports, colonial ethnography (Seligman on the Shilluk is a star witness in the abridgement), European folklore collections, and Mannhardt's peasant-custom dossiers. Frazer's skill was **taxonomic and rhetorical** — the arrangement of instances into an overwhelming series that makes the next inference feel inevitable. He did not invent the comparative catalogue (Tylor and others already worked that way), but he took it to industrial scale.

**What it is not.**  
It is **not fieldwork**. There is no participant observation, no language competence in the societies that carry his central theoretical weight, no control for the production conditions of the reports he cites. The Shilluk regicide complex, Australian increase rites, Cambodian Fire and Water kings, and European Midsummer fires enter the argument as **already-interpreted descriptions**. When Frazer writes that "nowhere is the theory of sympathetic magic more systematically carried into practice for the maintenance of the food supply than in the barren regions of Central Australia," he is depending entirely on the ethnography of others (Spencer and Gillen and their successors in the pipeline of citation).

**Characteristic failure modes of this evidence class.**

1. **Source quality variance.** Missionary and traveller reports range from careful to fantastic; Frazer's prose flattens them into a single evidential plane.
2. **Decontextualization.** A rite's meaning in situ is replaced by its role as an instance of Law of Similarity or vegetation-spirit embodiment.
3. **Galton's problem ignored.** Worldwide distribution is treated as evidence of psychic unity and ancient stage rather than of diffusion, empire, or shared literary transmission.
4. **Theory-laden selection.** Cases that fit the Nemi pattern (kings killed at signs of decay; combat succession; life in a tree or parasite plant) are foregrounded; awkward cases are relegated or allegorized.
5. **Survivals circularity.** European peasant customs are read as degraded survivals of the same ancient philosophy the "savage" cases are used to reconstruct — each end of the comparison props up the other.
6. **Classical puzzle as destination.** Because the book must return to Nemi, the comparative voyage is teleological. The mistletoe = Golden Bough identification is offered as "almost inevitable," but inevitability here is narrative as much as evidential.

**Frazer's own caveat** deserves quotation, because it coexists uneasily with the confidence of the catalogues:

> "All our theories concerning him and his ways must therefore fall far short of certainty; the utmost we can aspire to in such matters is a reasonable degree of probability."

The fair historiographical verdict (Stocking, Ackerman) is that Frazer was a **rigorous armchair synthesizer within a method the discipline later rejected as a method**. He was not a fraud; he was the terminal genius of a research program whose enabling premises (unilinear stages, context-free comparison, intellectualist "primitive philosopher") did not survive contact with long-term fieldwork and historical particularism. Restudies did not "refute" each cited custom one by one so much as **evacuate the inference rules** that made the customs speak Frazer's plot.

**Replication / afterlife status of the study-as-object.**  
For the research act of *The Golden Bough* as such, see [[frazer-golden-bough-study]] and the Studies Hub analysis. In brief: the descriptive compilations remain a quarry; the explanatory system is treated in contemporary anthropology as **historical**, not live theory; several mid-level constructs ([[dying-and-rising-god]] especially) have been subjected to pointed specialist demolition.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

**Reception — three publics.**

1. **General educated public and literature (1890–mid-20th c.).** *The Golden Bough* was a bestseller of ideas. Its dying gods, waste lands, and sacred kings entered the bloodstream of English-language modernism. Eliot's apparatus to *The Waste Land* is only the most famous tip. This public cared little for the truth of the Shilluk parallel and much for the mythic pattern.
2. **Anthropology as emerging profession (c. 1910–1960).** After the fieldwork revolution, Frazer became the name of the error one was trained not to commit: armchair comparison, conjectural history, evolutionary ranking, intellectualist mind-reading. Malinowski's functionalism and Boas's particularism are, among other things, **anti-Frazerian method charters**. Evans-Pritchard's "If I were a horse" line is the polished philosophical version of the same rejection.
3. **Historians of the discipline and of religions (c. 1970–present).** Stocking re-situated Frazer inside Victorian anthropology without rehabilitating the theories. Ackerman's biography made the man and the work historically legible. J. Z. Smith and others treated the dying-god package as a case study in how scholarly categories manufacture the phenomena they claim to find. Comparative religion departments still assign Frazer as ancestor and caution.

**Politics.**  
Frazer was not a colonial administrator and not a race-science polemicist of the polygenist type. His framework inherits Tylor's **psychic unity** (shared human mind) while installing a **civilizational hierarchy** in which European science sits at the summit of the three-thread web and "savagery" occupies the black-and-white early chequer. The political effect of that hierarchy, whatever Frazer's private liberalism, was to make empire's subject peoples into living museums of Europe's past — a standard evolutionist move. His progressivist defence of monarchy as the path out of "savagery" and his language of "barbarous customs" are period-marked and cannot be laundered into neutral description. At the same time, his insistence that magic is *failed reasoning* rather than racial incapacity places him with the monogenist, psychic-unity liberals against biological race hierarchy. Holding both — anti-racist monogenism *and* ethnocentric developmental ranking — is the honest political reading, as with Tylor.

**Ethics of knowledge production.**  
The ethics problem is structural: knowledge extracted under colonial and missionary conditions, stripped of indigenous interpretive authority, and redeployed to narrate a European story about the growth of reason. Frazer's footnotes sometimes name informants and local observers; more often the "savage" is a composite figure who believes what the theory needs him to believe. There is no Frazerian fieldwork scandal of the Chagnon–Tierney type, because there was no Frazerian fieldwork. The ethical ledger is about **epistemic extraction and representation**, not interpersonal field conduct.

**Christianity and the danger of the parallel.**  
Readers from the first edition onward noticed that a theory in which gods die and rise with the crops, and in which scapegoats carry communal evil, brushes against Christian narrative. Frazer's abridgement includes cautious surmises (e.g. Easter and Adonis at Bethlehem as possible grafting) without a full frontal assault. The literary public often heard more anti-Christian implication than the Edwardian gentleman-scholar was always willing to shout. That ambiguity — comparative solvent applied to all religions including the reader's, then partially withdrawn in tone — is part of the book's cultural force.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

What does anthropology look like without Frazer?

**Structural (would have happened anyway).**  
The crisis of armchair evolutionism was overdetermined. Boas's critique of the comparative method, the institutionalization of fieldwork (Malinowski, the Torres Strait generation, American graduate ethnography), Durkheimian sociology of religion, and the sheer accumulation of contextualized monographs would have killed unilinear conjectural history whether or not Frazer had written twelve volumes. [[cultural-evolutionism-debate|The cultural evolutionism debate]] does not require Frazer as its sole villain; he is simply its most readable emblem.

**Personal (Frazer's stamp).**

1. **The vocabulary of magic.** Even enemies of the intellectualist theory still teach **homoeopathic vs. contagious** magic. That dual typology is Frazer's lasting analytic brand, downstream of Tylor's associationism but fixed in Frazer's wording.
2. **Sacred kingship as a comparative problem.** Without Frazer, Nemi remains a classical antiquarian curiosity; with him, "the killing of the divine king" becomes a world-comparative theme that Africanists, medievalists, and political anthropologists still circle — usually to revise or reject his mechanism while keeping the question.
3. **The myth-bank for modernism.** No other anthropologist has so thoroughly colonized English-language literature. The counterfactual without Frazer is a different *Waste Land*, a different popular sense of what "ancient religion" feels like.
4. **[[dying-and-rising-god]] as category.** This package, more than the dual laws of magic, organized a century of comparative-religion syllabi and then a generation of critique. Its invention and demolition are both part of Frazer's legacy.
5. **The scale model of what not to do.** Pedagogy needs a concrete negative example of decontextualized comparison. Frazer is that example in countless methods courses. Ironically, this pedagogical afterlife keeps him central.

**What did *not* survive.**  
The magic → religion → science sequence as history of thought; the unilinear ranking of peoples; the claim that pure magic is older than religion as a global stage; the full vegetation-god reduction of Near Eastern and classical cults; the identification of the Golden Bough with mistletoe as solved classical philology; the political thesis that monarchy is the essential exit from savagery. These live as **positions in the history of ideas**, recorded on [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]] and [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], not as findings.

**Counterfactual genealogy compressed.**  
Without Frazer, Tylor's intellectualism still exists but lacks its most elaborate ritual application; Mannhardt's vegetation spirits remain a folklore research program rather than a key to world mythology; literary modernism finds other antiquarian sources (maybe Jane Harrison and the Cambridge Ritualists alone; maybe classical philology without the ethnographic flood). With Frazer but without the fieldwork revolution, something like Frazerianism might have lasted longer as orthodoxy — but the fieldwork revolution was coming from institutional and colonial logistical changes, not from a single rival book. Frazer did not delay fieldwork so much as provide the **contrast class** that made fieldwork's epistemic claims sharp.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

**Primary texts.**  
The stemma of *The Golden Bough* is itself a historiographical object: **1890** (2 vols.) → **1900** (3 vols.) → **1911–15** (12 vols., third edition) → **1922** author's abridgement (the common reading text; **ingested** for this wiki as [[frazer-golden-bough-1922]]). Arguments shift in emphasis across editions (fire-festival theory; Balder; mistletoe); serious citation should specify the edition. Other major works: *Totemism and Exogamy* (1910); *Folk-Lore in the Old Testament* (1918); *The Belief in Immortality* (1913–24). Frazer also produced classical editions and translations (e.g. Pausanias) that fed the same comparative habit from the other direction.

**Biographical and critical scholarship.**

- **Robert Ackerman, *J. G. Frazer: His Life and Work* (1987)** — the standard modern biography; essential on the Cambridge milieu, the marriage, the working methods, and the reception arc.
- **George W. Stocking Jr.** — *Victorian Anthropology* (1987) and later essays situate Frazer in the evolutionist moment and track the transition "after Tylor"; indispensable for not reading Frazer anachronistically.
- **E. E. Evans-Pritchard, *Theories of Primitive Religion* (1965)** — the classic mid-century demolition of intellectualist theories, Frazer prominent among the targets.
- **Jonathan Z. Smith** — essays dismantling dying-and-rising-god as a scholarly construct; required for the concept's historiography.
- Literary-critical studies of *The Golden Bough*'s rhetoric and modernist uptake form a parallel industry.

**Archives.**  
Trinity College, Cambridge, and related holdings preserve correspondence and papers; the published correspondence (where edited) is a window on how the ethnographic pipeline actually worked — who wrote to Frazer, what he asked for, how reports were reshaped in the note-factory.

**How the reputation has moved.**  
| Period | Dominant image |
|---|---|
| 1890–c. 1914 | Master of comparative religion; public intellectual of anthropology |
| c. 1920–1960 | Armchair ancestor to be rejected; functionalist and Boasian foil |
| c. 1960–1985 | History-of-theory specimen; Evans-Pritchard's intellectualist target |
| c. 1985–present | Historicized figure (Ackerman, Stocking); literary influence revalued; selected concepts retained as vocabulary; dying-god thesis under specialist erasure |

The wiki's division of labour tracks that movement: the summary page [[frazer-james-george]] keeps the network view; this hub keeps the depth; [[frazer-golden-bough-study]] analyses the research act; [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]] holds the theory as theory; [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] holds the dispute without adjudicating it into wiki voice.
