## Formation and Training

Leslie Alvin White (1900–1975) entered anthropology in the late 1920s into what Carneiro and Brown, in their foreword to the 2007 reprint of *The Evolution of Culture*, call the grip of particularism — Elman Service's later label for the climate was "barefoot empiricism." White absorbed Boasian **anti-evolutionism** as a graduate student and, by his own account, held it sincerely before he could no longer defend it. His own preface to *The Evolution of Culture* ([[white-evolution-of-culture-1959]]) is the primary conversion narrative he offers the reader:

> "The author absorbed the antievolutionary doctrines of the Boas School as a graduate student. But as he began to teach, he found, first, that he could not defend this point of view, and later that he could no longer hold it."

This sentence is doing real biographical work: White frames his conversion not as youthful contrarianism but as a **pedagogical failure of the received orthodoxy** — he could not answer his own students' questions about cultural change within a strictly particularist, anti-evolutionary frame, and the frame broke under that pressure rather than under abstract theoretical dissatisfaction. Carneiro and Brown periodize the turn concretely: teaching at the University of Buffalo (1927–1930) began the break; after joining the University of Michigan (from 1930) White fully undertook the rehabilitation of cultural evolution as a legitimate scientific object.

Institutional hospitality White thanks in the 1959 preface traces the long gestation of the book: Michigan sabbatical leaves; Yale Anthropology (1947–48, under Linton and Murdock); Harvard's Department of Social Relations and Peabody Museum anthropology (1949–50, Hooton, Kluckhohn, Brew); the Laboratory of Anthropology, Santa Fe (1954–55); and Wenner-Gren Foundation support under Paul Fejos. Harry Elmer Barnes is singled out for particular encouragement and for what White calls encyclopedic historical knowledge that shaped the book's world-historical scope. *The Evolution of Culture* grew out of a long-running Michigan course, also titled "Evolution of Culture," which White additionally taught as a visitor at Yale, Harvard, and Berkeley — the classroom, not the field, is the crucible of this book, a fact that matters for reading its empirical base (see below).

The campaign chronology Carneiro and Brown reconstruct runs: a 1939 American Anthropological Association paper defending evolutionary thinking; the pivotal 1943 *American Anthropologist* article "Energy and the Evolution of Culture," which they call the first major step "on the road back"; a run of 1940s–50s essays refining the position against diffusionist and functionalist objections; *The Science of Culture* (1949) as an essay collection in which only the concluding energy essay addresses evolution proper; and finally *The Evolution of Culture* (1959) as the book-length synthesis, published in the Darwin Centennial year — a coincidence White and his publishers were not shy about noting.

The genealogy's structural fact is that White is not a founder of anthropology but a **program-restorer** after a generation of program-killing. Without Boasian anti-evolution as a target, his life's work loses its adversary; without Tylor and Morgan as an ancestral charter, it loses its claimed legitimacy. Steward occupies a peculiar double position in this genealogy — closest rival on questions of scope and method, yet named by White's own contemporaries as a co-architect of evolution's mid-century revival. Steward's own genealogical placement of White is unambiguous and worth quoting directly, since it is Steward's positioning of White rather than White's self-positioning: in the introduction to *Theory of Culture Change*, Steward writes that his multilinear approach is "to be clearly distinguished from the views of L. H. Morgan, E. B. Tylor and other nineteenth-century writers and of V. Gordon Childe and Leslie White among contemporary authors," adding that "whereas these writers have sought to formulate cultural development in terms of universal stages, my objective is to seek causes of culture change." Steward here groups White with the nineteenth-century unilinear evolutionists on the axis that mattered most to him — universal-stage ambition versus causal, area-specific analysis — even though White himself denied that his theory reduced to a stage sequence for particular cultures.

## Career Phases

1. **Boasian formation (through the mid-1920s).** Graduate absorption of particularism and institutional hostility to stage theory and to evolutionary explanation generally.
2. **Conversion and early teaching (Buffalo, 1927–30; Michigan, from 1930).** White finds he cannot defend anti-evolution to students pressing him on cultural change; the classroom, not fieldwork, precipitates the theoretical break.
3. **Campaign essays (1939–1949).** The 1939 AAA paper; the 1943 "Energy and the Evolution of Culture"; *The Science of Culture* (1949); a run of polemics against treating diffusion as a refutation of evolution and against the functionalist either/or between process and structure.
4. **Synthetic volume (1959).** *The Evolution of Culture* carries the argument through the fall of Rome — conceived as the first installment of a planned trilogy running to the modern industrial world.
5. **Unfinished industrial arc.** A manuscript on the *Fuel Revolution* was completed but, per Carneiro and Brown, left unpublished in White's lifetime; a third volume grew to roughly 1,400 manuscript pages and later surfaced as *Modern Capitalist Culture*. The published, public White effectively ends his story at Rome; the private, unpublished program aimed at explaining democracy, industrial capitalism, and — in White's own probabilistic language — the shape of the century from 1958 to 2058.

The five phases trace a single throughline: a man converted by pedagogical failure who then spent the rest of his career building, teaching, and only partially publishing an ever-larger evolutionary edifice, with the published 1959 volume standing as a fragment — the "ancient world" volume — of an intended full arc from Paleolithic energy capture to twentieth-century industrial capitalism.

### Verbatim: culture and symboling

> "By culture we mean an extrasomatic, temporal continuum of things and events dependent upon symboling. Specifically and concretely, culture consists of tools, implements, utensils, clothing, ornaments, customs, institutions, beliefs, rituals, games, works of art, language, etc."

Analysis: three load-bearing commitments sit inside this single sentence. First, culture is **extrasomatic** — not lodged inside the individual organism, a formulation adjacent to but distinct from Kroeber's [[superorganic]]. Second, it is a **temporal continuum**, a stream or flow rather than a snapshot inventory of traits — this is the phrasing that licenses treating culture as a proper object of *evolutionary*, not merely comparative, analysis. Third, culture is **dependent upon symboling** — White's chosen technical term, deliberately distinct from ordinary "symbolism," for the capacity to freely and arbitrarily assign meaning to a thing or event that has no intrinsic, sensorily determined connection to that meaning. Holy water is White's own paradigm case: chemically indistinguishable from ordinary water, yet functioning entirely differently because of an assigned, non-physical meaning. White insists this capacity is a difference in **kind, not degree** — there are, on his account, no intermediate evolutionary steps toward prices, uncles, or the Sabbath. That sharp threshold is precisely what later comparative-cognition research has pushed back against; it is also what allows White to claim, methodologically, that culturology can proceed without reduction to individual psychology.

### Verbatim: the energy law

> "Culture advances as the amount of energy harnessed per capita per year increases, or as the efficiency or economy of the means of controlling energy is increased, or both."

And the accompanying process formula:

> "This conception can be expressed by a simple formula, E×T → P, in which E represents the energy involved, T the technological means of utilizing it, and P, the product or result which serves a need of man."

White subsequently refines the formula to separate human from nonhuman energy sources — `E(H×N)×T → P` — so that cultural advance can be tracked either as the proportion of nonhuman to human energy rises, or as the quantity of need-serving goods produced per unit of human labor rises. Environment is admitted into a fuller formula, `E×T×V → P`, but is explicitly scoped to **particular** cultures rather than to the general law; White's analogy is that the physicist abstracts away air resistance when stating the law of falling bodies, and the general cultural-evolutionary law abstracts away habitat variation in the same spirit. This is the formal core of the "basic law of cultural evolution" attributed to White in the secondary literature: a law about the trajectory of culture as a whole, stated at a level of abstraction that deliberately brackets the local ecological variation Steward makes his own object of study.

### Verbatim: rejection of "neoevolutionism"

> "The theory of evolution set forth in this work does not differ one whit in principle from that expressed in Tylor's Anthropology in 1881… Neoevolutionism is a misleading term."

Analysis: White refuses the historiographic compromise that would make his position safer by relabeling it "neo." He wants continuity of *principle* with Victorian evolutionism — the claim that culture as a whole has a general developmental history subject to law — while updating the demonstration with twentieth-century energy science and archaeology. Steward's vocabulary of "universal evolution" is, from this angle, the rival's taxonomy rather than White's own preferred brand name, though it correctly names the scope White in fact claims: culture-as-a-whole, not culture-in-a-particular-environment.

### Verbatim: technological determination

The technological-determinist commitment is stated at length and defended against the obvious objection — that it cannot explain fine-grained cultural minutiae — rather than qualified away:

> "The theory of technological determination, direct or indirect, of social organization is thus effective and fruitful; it will carry us far. The difficulties that we encounter in its application to minutiae are technical in nature rather than fundamental. They are the practical difficulties of actual measurement and correlation among numerous and specific variables; they are not due to inadequacies of our premises or to shortcomings of our theory."

White poses the alternative explanations for social form as a closed set — instinct, ideas, or free will — and dismisses each in turn: instinct because "no one… would want to argue that any cultural form is determined by genes and chromosomes"; free will because "to argue that a people determines its social organization by its own free will and choice really explains nothing and shuts the door to further inquiry as well." This is White's characteristic rhetorical move throughout *Evolution of Culture*: present technological determination not as one hypothesis among several live options but as the only position left standing once the alternatives are shown to be pseudo-explanations. The same technological-determinist logic is extended in Part Two to ideology and belief:

> "In Chapter I we set forth a theory of technological determination of cultural systems. Social systems and ideologies were seen as functions of technological systems. But since philosophies express experience, they must reflect the influence of social life as well as of systems of energy and tools."

This passage matters because it shows White is not a crude one-variable reductionist in his own self-description — technology determines social organization, and social organization together with technology conditions ideology, so the causal chain runs through two links rather than one — but the ultimate anchor of the whole system remains resolutely material and energetic, which is exactly the feature Steward's cultural-ecological method and later cultural-materialist restatements each try to complicate or replace with more local, variable-adaptive causal chains.

### Verbatim: state-church

> "To accomplish all this, the new society would have to have a special mechanism… Such a special mechanism was developed. We shall call it the state-church."

> "What the brain and central nervous system are to biological evolution, the state-church is to sociocultural evolution."

Analysis: White does not treat the state and organized religion as two separate institutions that happen to arise together; he treats them as two aspects — secular-military and ecclesiastical-theological — of a single integrative mechanism required once agricultural surplus and occupational differentiation outrun the coordinating capacity of kinship-based organization. Its functions, on White's account, are to coordinate occupational differentiation and public works, to manage class conflict generated by surplus and stratification, and to organize military defense and offense of the resulting civil order. The coercive element is explicit rather than euphemized: agriculture does not, for White, "automatically" produce civilization — food producers must be compelled to yield a surplus, and the state-church is the mechanism of that compulsion as much as of its administration.

## The Empirical Base

*The Evolution of Culture* is emphatically **not** a primary ethnography, and White does not present it as one. Its empirical mode is comparative synthesis, built from two very different kinds of secondary material stitched together across the book's two parts: ethnographic illustrations for the pre-agricultural material (Australian section systems, kinship-terminology types, potlatch and kula treated as social ritual) and archaeological and historical syntheses for Part Two's civilizational arc (Nile and Mesopotamian sequences, Childe's and Braidwood's competing hypotheses on agricultural origins, temple-economy reconstructions, Greek natural philosophy, and the plateau and fall of Rome). The book's warrant is therefore the coherence of the comparative reading White constructs from others' primary work, not new fieldwork or new archival discovery of his own — consistent with its origin as a decades-long lecture course rather than a monograph built from a single research campaign.

Strengths, on White's own design terms: a clear and consistently applied thermodynamic comparative axis (energy per capita, tool efficiency); an explicit three-way distinction of evolutionary, historical, and functional modes of explanation that keeps White's own claims disciplined about what kind of question he is answering; and a refusal of purely psychologistic "someone got the idea" origin stories for events like the adoption of agriculture or the incest taboo, which White treats as requiring structural rather than individual-inventive explanation.

Weaknesses and later pressures on the empirical base:

- The global curve under-specifies **multilinear**, ecologically differentiated developmental paths — this is precisely Steward's standing methodological critique, developed independently rather than as commentary on White. Steward's cultural ecology asks how a *particular* culture's core institutions are shaped by its adaptation to a *particular* environment; White's energy law operates one level up, abstracting habitat away as noise around a general trend. The two programs are not strictly incompatible — Sahlins and Service's later synthesis treats them as complementary "general" and "specific" evolution — but *within* *Evolution of Culture* itself White does not attempt Steward's fine-grained ecological reconstruction.
- The **"primitive" society White describes in his Summary** functions as a normative ideal-type — liberty, equality, fraternity, mutual aid, production for use — that valorizes kinship-based equality in ways later ethnography of inequality, slavery, and intergroup violence complicates; White footnotes captives and foreigners as falling outside the ideal-type, but the ideal-type nonetheless remains load-bearing for the moral coloring of his civilizational narrative (see Reception, Politics, and Ethics below).
- Chronological anchors White offers — human origins at roughly a million years, agricultural onset at roughly 8000 BCE — are period-typical for 1959 and have since been substantially refined by radiometric and archaeogenetic work; later chronology (as of post-2010 literature) supersedes White's specific dates without automatically invalidating the energy-law argument built on top of them, since the argument concerns relative trajectory more than absolute date.
- White's treatment of writing as a control technology of a literate minority, rather than as an unambiguous culture-building advance, produces a sharp internal tension with Morgan's and Tylor's older use of literacy as the very threshold of civilization; White resolves this tension on energy grounds within his own frame, arguing that writing's real function was social-control and revelation-stabilization rather than "culture-building" per se — a claim that stays inside attributed voice here since it rests on White's interpretive reading of the archaeological and historical record rather than on independent convergent evidence.

Fieldwork is a minor factor in this particular volume's warrant: White's early Pueblo/Southwest ethnographic work is not the evidentiary base of *Evolution of Culture*; the book's warrant is the culturological reading White constructs across the comparative-synthetic record, not a body of data he personally collected.

Several quantitative claims recur through the text as illustrative anchors rather than as independently re-verified findings, and should be read and cited that way: human energy output on the order of 1/20 horsepower per capita in self-reproducing "primitive" systems; the Agricultural Revolution characterized as "sudden" relative to roughly a million years of prior cultural history even though it unfolded over centuries or millennia (on White's own reckoning, well under one percent of that longer span for a five-thousand-year transition); literacy rates in early urban cultures estimated at probably well under one percent of the population, with priests forming the literate majority; and comparative food-energy ratios and calorie tables (for the United States and other societies) presented as mid-century secondary data. These figures anchor White's rhetorical argument; they are not this wiki's independent measurements and should not migrate onto subject-matter pages as findings.

White's Part One is, in addition to being an energy treatise, effectively a **social-morphology textbook**: kinship is treated as sociological rather than biological, terminologies as encoding social relations, and classificatory versus descriptive kinship systems are reviewed alongside the extension of kinship idiom to intertribal relations via clans and moieties as "meta-genealogical" devices. Nomenclature typologies (Hawaiian, Crow–Omaha, Eskimo; the Lowie–Kirchhoff fourfold scheme) are reviewed with attention to multi-factor determination and developmental lag — the Crow–Omaha thesis in particular. White argues the Agricultural Revolution pushes kinship terminology toward more descriptive, territorially and property-organized ("civil") idioms as against purely genealogical ones. This morphological inventory extends to social segments and classes (age, marital, sex — including berdache and eunuch statuses as White catalogs them), the shift from sex-based to craft-based division of labor after the Agricultural Revolution, the shaman as the first social specialist preceding the later priest, rank systems that in many "primitive" cases lack true slavery, and slavery itself as a post-surplus institution. Integration and regulation are treated as functions of custom understood as an economy of prediction and social solidarity, distinguishing ethics from etiquette and absolute from relative ethical systems, and tracing the sequence from headman to chief to king alongside the emergence of law, tort, police, councils, and secret societies as regulatory mechanisms.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

**Reception.** Carneiro and Brown's 2007 foreword frames the reception arc starkly: when White began his campaign, cultural evolutionism was practiced by essentially no one in American anthropology; by his death in 1975 it had regained major disciplinary standing, with White treated as the primary figure and Childe and Steward as secondary contributors to the revival. They further note, writing decades later, a subsequent wave of postmodern anti-science antievolutionism that White did not live to contend with directly, and observe that archaeologists — rather than sociocultural anthropologists — became the principal standard-bearers of evolutionary and energy-systems questions in the decades after White's death. Within the neo-evolutionary network his Michigan teaching built, Sahlins and Service reworked the universal-versus-specific evolution distinction into a formal complementarity in *Evolution and Culture* (1960); cultural materialism (associated with Marvin Harris) retained White's infrastructural-priority logic in a more probabilistic form; and processual archaeology absorbed White's systems and energy vocabulary into its own explanatory apparatus.

**Politics of the text.** The book's closing Summary presents an explicitly moral-political evaluation of "primitive" social organization inside what is otherwise a thermodynamic historical narrative — describing it, in White's language, as offering something close to liberty, equality, fraternity, production for use, and human rights standing above property claims. This is not a neutral stage-description: theology is characterized as functioning as an "instrument of class rule" once civilization emerges; monotheism is read as an expression of centralized imperial civil power; and writing is characterized as a control technology of a literate minority more than a straightforward culture-building advance. These are conflict-theoretic colorings of White's account of the post-agricultural order. White is therefore not a neutral stage-lister in the Victorian evolutionist mold he otherwise claims descent from; he is simultaneously a critic of civilizational coercion and a theorist who still labels the agricultural energy leap an "advance" under his own energy law — a tension the text does not fully resolve and that later readers of White (as of the Carneiro and Brown foreword) note without adjudicating.

**Ethics record.** No misconduct scandal is central to the primary sources ingested for this hub entry. The general colonial-archive problem applies in the background sense common to mid-century comparative synthesis: White's comparative material is drawn substantially from salvage-era and imperial-era ethnography without the reflexive apparatus later anthropology developed around fieldwork power and representation. This should be attributed and date-stamped rather than either ignored or retrospectively moralized against a text that predates the disciplinary reflexivity turn.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

**Without White**, mid-century American anthropology might still have recovered evolutionary and processual questions through Childe's archaeology and Steward's cultural ecology — both were independently converging on related concerns. But the **energetic master-key**, the culturology brand, the sustained anti-anti-evolutionist polemics sustained across two decades of essays, and the Michigan teaching pipeline that trained Sahlins, Service, and their cohort would very likely have been thinner or differently shaped. Steward's own taxonomy needed a named universalist pole to define his multilinear alternative against; absent a living, combative opponent occupying that pole, "universal evolution" might never have been named as a distinct position at all, and the mid-century debate structure itself might have taken a different shape.

**What was structural anyway:** the availability of thermodynamics as an explanatory vocabulary was in the intellectual air well beyond anthropology by the 1940s; dissatisfaction with pure Boasian particularism among a rising generation of graduate students was building independently of White; and archaeology's own need for long-run process explanation (visible already in Childe) would have generated pressure toward evolutionary questions with or without White's personal campaign. White's distinctive contribution was to **personalize the fight and name the science** — to turn a diffuse dissatisfaction into an organized, teachable, polemically sharpened research program with an institutional home at Michigan.

**What survived:** energy and subsistence as durable first-pass explanatory priorities across cultural-materialist, ecological, and processual-archaeological successor programs; the tripartite distinction of evolutionary, historical, and functional explanation as a still-useful meta-analytic tool; the debate structure that now constitutes the [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]; and, perhaps most durably, the institutional memory within the discipline that Boasian anti-evolutionism was itself a historically specific moment in anthropology's history rather than theory's permanent terminus.

**What did not survive:** culturology as a discipline name in its own right; the single global evolutionary curve as an active research design (superseded within a decade by Sahlins and Service's general/specific complementarity and by Steward's multilinear alternative gaining ground); strict kind-not-degree symboling as an uncontested consensus position; and the unfinished *Fuel Revolution* project as a completed, published public theory of industrial-era cultural evolution.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

- **Primary for this hub:** *The Evolution of Culture* (1959), especially the Preface, Chapters 1–2 and 11–16; the Carneiro and Brown foreword to the 2007 Left Coast Press reprint, itself treated here as a position document rather than as neutral editorial apparatus.
- **Adjacent primary (cited within the 1959 text but not separately ingested):** *The Science of Culture* (1949); the 1943 "Energy and the Evolution of Culture" article; a 1945 essay distinguishing evolution, history, and functionalism as interpretive modes; 1945 and 1947 essays defending evolutionary theory against diffusionist and functionalist objections.
- **Dialogic primary (ingested):** Julian Steward, *Theory of Culture Change* (1955) ([[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955]]) — the direct methodological counterpoint used throughout this hub entry for the universal-versus-multilinear contrast; V. Gordon Childe's *Social Evolution* (1951) and *What Happened in History* are cited within White's text but not separately ingested here.
- **The Carneiro and Brown foreword (2007) as historiography in its own right.** The reprint foreword is itself a **position document**, not a neutral introduction: it casts White as anthropology's pivotal generalizer of the mid-century evolutionary revival, characterizes Boasian particularism in the deflationary "just the facts" register, narrates evolutionary theory's disciplinary death and resurrection as a single arc with White at its center, treats Childe and Steward as secondary to White within that arc, situates the 1959 book as consolidation rather than breakthrough, and identifies a later postmodern anti-science antievolutionism as a second wave White did not live to contest, with archaeologists emerging as its principal opponents. This hub entry uses the foreword for reception history; its evaluative claims about White's centrality should not be merged into White's own 1959 voice, since they are Carneiro and Brown's retrospective historiographic judgment, made nearly half a century later.
- **Outstanding for future ingest:** a full scholarly intellectual biography of White; the *Fuel Revolution* and *Modern Capitalist Culture* manuscripts, which would substantially extend the empirical and reception picture into the unpublished industrial-era portion of his program; documentation of the Michigan student network beyond Sahlins and Service; and archival correspondence with Boasian-school colleagues that would sharpen the Formation and Training section above beyond what the 1959 preface alone can support.
