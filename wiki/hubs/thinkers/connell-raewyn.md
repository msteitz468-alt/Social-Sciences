# Raewyn Connell — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[connell-raewyn]]. Reciprocal: this page's `thinker_page` points to the summary; the summary links here by full path.

Australian sociologist (b. 1944); published as **R. W. Connell** through the 1990s and as **Raewyn Connell** after gender transition in the 2000s. Architect of the dominant framework in the critical study of men and masculinities, and of a practice-based structural theory of gender that reorganized late-twentieth-century gender sociology. Ingested primary texts in this vault: *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987) and *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995; 2nd ed. 2005).

## Formation and Training

Connell's early career combined class analysis, education research, and Australian political sociology (*Ruling Class, Ruling Culture*, 1977; *Class Structure in Australian History* with T. H. Irving, 1980). The late-1970s schools study with Ashenden, Kessler and Dowsett (*Making the Difference*, 1982; *Ockers and Disco-Maniacs*, 1982) put sexual politics inside everyday institutional life — competitive sport, peer hierarchies, and the making of femininities and masculinities in secondary schools. Connell credits feminism "especially in the life and work of my wife and partner Pam Benton" as formative: "issues about gender were never just contemplative, but always had to do with social action." Gay-liberation theory (collaborations with Tim Carrigan and John Lee) supplied the relational critique of heterosexual masculinity that sex-role theory could not. The Freudian Left and existential psychoanalysis (Sartre's "project") supplied tools for personality as practice without reducing gender to socialization.

In the *Gender and Power* Preface, Connell writes as a heterosexual man who rejects the claim that "men are equally oppressed," inventories men's advantages, and lists reasons men might still support change — a positionality the later transition does not erase from the 1987 text but recontextualizes in biography.

## Intellectual Genealogy

| Relation | Figures / currents |
|---|---|
| Positive resources | Gramsci (hegemony); Juliet Mitchell, Gayle Rubin (gender as complex structure); Sartre / practice theory; Habermas (crisis tendencies, critically); gay liberation; second-wave feminism |
| Against | Parsonsian sex-role theory; sociobiology / Goldberg-style biological hierarchy; "class first" Marxism; dual-systems theory as separate capitalism+patriarchy; categorical universalism about "women"/"men" |
| Collaborators | Carrigan, Lee (1985); schools team (Ashenden, Kessler, Dowsett); later Messerschmidt (2005 reformulation) |
| Field descendants | Messner, Messerschmidt, large masculinities ethnography (sport, school, military, work) |

Genealogy is reciprocal with the program: Connell trained a generation less as a school than as a **vocabulary** — hegemonic/subordinated/complicit/marginalized — that others inhabit and contest (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]).

## Career Phases

1. **Class and education (1970s–early 1980s).** Ruling-class and Australian class structure; schools ethnography that already shows multiple masculinities in institutional gender regimes.
2. **Gender theory synthesis (mid-1980s–1987).** Carrigan, Connell & Lee 1985; *Gender and Power* as outline of a systematic social theory of gender — labour/power/cathexis, gender order/regime, hegemonic masculinity and emphasized femininity, crisis tendencies, sexual politics.
3. **Masculinities field (1990s–2005).** *Masculinities* life-history study; world-historical account of modern masculinity; masculinity politics; 2nd edition + Connell & Messerschmidt 2005 reformulation.
4. **Southern Theory and global knowledge (2000s–).** Critique of metropole-centered social theory (*Southern Theory*, 2007) — not yet ingested here; extends the same anti-categorical, historically specific method to the geopolitics of knowledge.
5. **Public intellectual after transition.** Continues writing as Raewyn Connell; the earlier "R. W." corpus is the same author.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages

On **emphasized femininity** (*Gender and Power*):

> "One form is defined around compliance with this subordination and is oriented to accommodating the interests and desires of men. I will call this 'emphasized femininity'."

On **hegemony** (same book):

> "Ascendancy of one group of men over another achieved at the point of a gun, or by the threat of unemployment, is not hegemony. Ascendancy which is embedded in religious doctrine and practice, mass media content, wage structures, the design of housing, welfare/taxation policies and so forth, is."

On the **body** (1987):

> "My male body does not confer masculinity on me; it receives masculinity (or some fragment thereof) as its social definition."

On **structure** (1987):

> "the concept of structure is more than another term for 'pattern' and refers to the intractability of the social world."

On men's anti-sexist politics (1987):

> "The difficulty of constructing a movement of men to dismantle hegemonic masculinity is that its logic is not the articulation of collective interest but the attempt to dismantle that interest."

These passages commit the program to relational structure, cultural (not merely coercive) power, practical transformation of bodies, and a politics that cannot be a "men's movement" in the interest-group sense.

## The Empirical Base

| Project | Design | Limits (Connell's and critics') |
|---|---|---|
| Schools research (late 1970s) | Institutional ethnography of Australian secondary schools | Nationally specific; period-bound |
| *Gender and Power* illustrations | Secondary stats (wages, violence, office-holding) + cited ethnographies | Theory-building, not primary n; mid-1980s data age |
| *Masculinities* life histories | Four purposive groups of Australian men | Small n; single country; strategic illumination, not map |

Connell states the external-validity limit of the life-history study himself. The framework's warrant is cumulative adoption across the field, not the original samples alone. Historiography of gender (Ch. 7 of *Gender and Power*) is secondary synthesis — strong on rejecting origins arguments, thin as primary prehistory (Connell notes the synthesis gap).

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

**Reception.** *Masculinities* voted among the most influential books in Australian sociology (2003); massive citation of hegemonic masculinity across disciplines; sustained debate (Petersen, Wetherell & Edley, Demetriou, Jefferson) answered in 2005. *Gender and Power* is the quieter structural panel — more cited via concepts (gender regime, emphasized femininity) than as a whole.

**Politics.** Explicitly feminist and gay-liberation-aligned; rejects men-as-equally-oppressed; supports intensive ("liberated zones") and extensive strategies; warns of "rejuvenated high-technology patriarchy" if reproductive technologies stay under undemocratic control. Working-class feminism is treated as real but structurally weaker than intelligentsia-based women's liberation.

**Ethics / identity.** Authorship name change (R. W. → Raewyn) is a biographical fact relevant to citation practice; the 1987 self-description as heterosexual man is part of the text's production context, not a defect to erase. No misconduct record in the ingested sources; the ethics stakes of the program are political (who theory serves) rather than research-misconduct.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Without Connell: sex-role theory and trait psychology would likely have retained more of the "male role" field into the 1990s; "masculinities" plural and hierarchical might have arrived more slowly via ethnography alone (Willis, Cockburn, Messner) without a unifying structural vocabulary. Emphasized femininity and the gender-regime inventory might not have a standard name. Hybrid masculinities work is a direct descendant of the typology. Counterfactual limit: feminist structural theory (Mitchell, Hartmann, Rubin) and ethnomethodological doing-gender would still exist — Connell's distinctive contribution is the **relational masculinities grid inside a practice-based gender structure**, not the invention of gender sociology as such.
