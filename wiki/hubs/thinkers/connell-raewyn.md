# Raewyn Connell — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[connell-raewyn]]. Reciprocal: this page's `thinker_page` points to the summary; the summary links here by full path.

Australian sociologist (b. 1944); published as **R. W. Connell** through the 1990s and as **Raewyn Connell** after gender transition in the 2000s. Architect of the dominant framework in the critical study of men and masculinities, of a practice-based structural theory of gender, and — with *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]* (2007) — of a major program in the geopolitics of social knowledge ([[southern-theory]]). Ingested primary texts in this vault: *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987), *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995; 2nd ed. 2005), and *Southern Theory* (2007).

## Formation and Training

Connell's early career combined class analysis, education research, and Australian political sociology (*Ruling Class, Ruling Culture*, 1977; *Class Structure in Australian History* with T. H. Irving, 1980). The late-1970s schools study with Ashenden, Kessler and Dowsett (*Making the Difference*, 1982; *Ockers and Disco-Maniacs*, 1982) put sexual politics inside everyday institutional life — competitive sport, peer hierarchies, and the making of femininities and masculinities in secondary schools. Connell credits feminism "especially in the life and work of my wife and partner Pam Benton" as formative: "issues about gender were never just contemplative, but always had to do with social action." Gay-liberation theory (collaborations with Tim Carrigan and John Lee) supplied the relational critique of heterosexual masculinity that sex-role theory could not. The Freudian Left and existential psychoanalysis (Sartre's "project") supplied tools for personality as practice without reducing gender to socialization.

In the *Gender and Power* Preface, Connell writes as a heterosexual man who rejects the claim that "men are equally oppressed," inventories men's advantages, and lists reasons men might still support change — a positionality the later transition does not erase from the 1987 text but recontextualizes in biography.

Genealogy is reciprocal with the program: Connell trained a generation less as a school than as a **vocabulary** — hegemonic/subordinated/complicit/marginalized — that others inhabit and contest (see [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]).

## Career Phases

1. **Class and education (1970s–early 1980s).** Ruling-class and Australian class structure; schools ethnography that already shows multiple masculinities in institutional gender regimes.
2. **Gender theory synthesis (mid-1980s–1987).** Carrigan, Connell & Lee 1985; *Gender and Power* as outline of a systematic social theory of gender — labour/power/cathexis, gender order/regime, hegemonic masculinity and emphasized femininity, crisis tendencies, sexual politics.
3. **Masculinities field (1990s–2005).** *Masculinities* life-history study; world-historical account of modern masculinity; masculinity politics; 2nd edition + Connell & Messerschmidt 2005 reformulation.
4. **Southern Theory and global knowledge (2000s–).** *[[connell-southern-theory-2007|Southern Theory]]* (2007): empire and sociology's founding under [[global-difference]]; critique of Coleman/Giddens/Bourdieu general theory and of metropolitan globalisation sociology; Australia as settler "branch office"; readings of African indigenous sociology, Iranian *Gharbzadegi*/Shariati, Latin American dependency and culture, Indian Subaltern Studies/Nandy/Das; land and [[dirty-theory]]; program for multi-centred social science and democracy. Extends the same anti-categorical, historically specific method to the geopolitics of knowledge ([[southern-theory]]).
5. **Public intellectual after transition.** Continues writing as Raewyn Connell; the earlier "R. W." corpus is the same author.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages

On **emphasized femininity** (*Gender and Power*):

> "One form is defined around compliance with this subordination and is oriented to accommodating the interests and desires of men. I will call this 'emphasized femininity'."

On **hegemony** (same book):

> "Ascendancy of one group of men over another achieved at the point of a gun, or by the threat of unemployment, is not hegemony. Ascendancy which is embedded in religious doctrine and practice, mass media content, wage structures, the design of housing, welfare/taxation policies and so forth, is."

On the **body** (1987):

> "My male body does not confer masculinity on me; it receives masculinity (or some fragment thereof) as its social definition."

On **structure** (1987):

> "the concept of structure is more than another term for 'pattern' and refers to the intractability of the social world."

On men's anti-sexist politics (1987):

> "The difficulty of constructing a movement of men to dismantle hegemonic masculinity is that its logic is not the articulation of collective interest but the attempt to dismantle that interest."

These passages commit the gender program to relational structure, cultural (not merely coercive) power, practical transformation of bodies, and a politics that cannot be a "men's movement" in the interest-group sense.

On **Southern theory** as relational (*Southern Theory* Introduction):

> "Similarly, I use the term 'Southern' not to name a sharply bounded category of states or societies, but to emphasise relations—authority, exclusion and inclusion, hegemony, partnership, sponsorship, appropriation—between intellectuals and institutions in the metropole and those in the world periphery."

On **metropolitan viewpoint**:

> "Most important, they picture the world as seen from the rich capital-exporting countries of Europe and North America-the global metropole."

On **modernity's self-creation myth**:

> "the rest of social science formed itself on ethnocentric assumptions that amounted to a gigantic lie-that modernity created itself within the North Atlantic world, independent of the rest of humanity."

On **global difference** (Ch. 1):

> "difference between the civilisation of the metropole and other cultures whose main feature was their primitiveness. I will call this the idea of global difference."

These passages commit the Southern Theory program to relational geopolitics of knowledge (not a Third-World essentialism), historicisation of sociology under empire, and a democratic vocation for multi-centred social science.

## The Empirical Base

| Project | Design | Limits (Connell's and critics') |
|---|---|---|
| Schools research (late 1970s) | Institutional ethnography of Australian secondary schools | Nationally specific; period-bound |
| *Gender and Power* illustrations | Secondary stats (wages, violence, office-holding) + cited ethnographies | Theory-building, not primary n; mid-1980s data age |
| *Masculinities* life histories | Four purposive groups of Australian men | Small n; single country; strategic illumination, not map |
| *Southern Theory* (2007) | Comparative reading of metropolitan general theory + four peripheral intellectual formations + Australia case + land | Text-based; English-language/Antipodean standpoint; OCR-dependent ingest in this vault; not a single fieldwork sample |

Connell states the external-validity limit of the life-history study himself. The gender framework's warrant is cumulative adoption across the field. *Southern Theory*'s warrant is argument and close reading, not new survey n — case selection (Africa, Iran, Latin America, India, Australia) is strategic illumination of dependence challenged.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

**Reception.** *Masculinities* voted among the most influential books in Australian sociology (2003); massive citation of hegemonic masculinity across disciplines; sustained debate (Petersen, Wetherell & Edley, Demetriou, Jefferson) answered in 2005. *Gender and Power* is the quieter structural panel. *Southern Theory* is widely cited in global sociology, decolonial social science, and curriculum critique; it densifies Connell's reputation beyond gender into sociology-of-knowledge.

**Politics.** Explicitly feminist and gay-liberation-aligned in the gender corpus; *Southern Theory* adds anti-imperial knowledge politics and four democratic ends for world social science (compassion; contesting untruth; movement-relevant knowledge; democracy inside knowledge production). Neoliberal constriction of social knowledge is a central antagonist. Working-class feminism is treated as real but structurally weaker than intelligentsia-based women's liberation (1987).

**Ethics / identity.** Authorship name change (R. W. → Raewyn) is a biographical fact relevant to citation practice; the 1987 self-description as heterosexual man is part of the text's production context, not a defect to erase. No misconduct record in the ingested sources; the ethics stakes are political (who theory serves; whose knowledge counts) rather than research-misconduct.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Without Connell on **gender**: sex-role theory and trait psychology would likely have retained more of the "male role" field into the 1990s; "masculinities" plural and hierarchical might have arrived more slowly via ethnography alone without a unifying structural vocabulary. Without Connell on **Southern Theory**: critiques of the classical canon and of Northern general theory would still exist ([[postcolonialism]], dependency, Subaltern Studies, Hountondji), but the integrated reconstruction of sociology's imperial founding + close reading of Coleman/Giddens/Bourdieu + constructive multi-centred program under one Australian feminist theorist's signature would be thinner. Counterfactual limit: world-systems, dependency, and postcolonial literary theory already named the problem — Connell's distinctive move is making **sociology's own general theory** the object and demanding Southern texts be read *as theory*.
