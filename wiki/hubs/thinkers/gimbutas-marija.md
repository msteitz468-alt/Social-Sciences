## Formation and Training

Gimbutas’s 1991 acknowledgments frame formation as **thirty years of research and excavation in Europe**, embedded in a network of regional excavators who shared Neolithic data, and in Indo-Europeanist colleagues (Edgar Polomé, Wolfgang Meid, Martin Huld, Eric Hamp, Miriam Robbins Dexter). Dating guidance for calibrated radiocarbon came from Hans Suess and Rainer Berger at UCLA. Former students (Karlene Jones-Bley, Patricia MacDonell, Michael Everson, Kristina Kelertas, Susan Skomal, Starr Goode, Zipporah Sabsay) assisted illustration and editorial work; Joan Marler edited the final volume.

Lithuanian-American background and wartime displacement are standard biographical facts outside this source’s prose; full training genealogy (teachers, dissertation, institutional path from Lithuania/Europe to Harvard/UCLA) awaits dedicated biographical sources. What *Civilization* makes clear is the **method formation** that structures the book: culture-historical mastery of eastern and central European Neolithic–Copper Age assemblages; willingness to integrate mythology and comparative Indo-European studies; and a polemical insistence that religion is not optional decoration for “scientific” archaeology.

## Career Phases

1. **Eastern European prehistory and Kurgan coinage (1950s–60s)** — *Prehistory of Eastern Europe* (1956) introduces blanket **Kurgan tradition** for barrow-building steppe pastoralists; *Bronze Age Cultures* (1965) consolidates culture-historical maps.
2. **Field director years (late 1960s–1980)** — Obre, Anza, Sitagroi (with Renfrew), Achilleion, Scaloria: firsthand Neolithic temple, figurine, and settlement data that feed later Goddess chapters.
3. **Goddess program monographs (1974–1989)** — *Goddesses and Gods of Old Europe*; *Language of the Goddess*: iconographic system and symbolic lexicon.
4. **Synthetic summation (1991)** — *Civilization of the Goddess*: pan-European culture catalogue (chs.1–6) + religion, script, society (7–9) + Kurgan endgame (10); addresses “what is civilization?” polemically.
5. **Reception and afterlife (1990s–present)** — Goddess-movement popularization; processual/gender-archaeology critique; Anthony narrowing; post-2015 aDNA reweights demography of steppe movements without validating full cultural package.

## The Empirical Base

**What she digs.** Acknowledgments list Neolithic excavations: Obre (Bosnia 1967–68), Anza and Sitagroi (Macedonia 1968–70), Achilleion (Thessaly 1973–74), Scaloria (SE Italy 1978–80). These supply temple models, figurine contexts, and stratigraphic feel for SE European sequences.

**What she synthesizes.** The bulk of *Civilization* is **secondary culture-history**: regional sequences, excavators’ plans, published 14C dates (calibrated as of early 1990s), metallurgical and cemetery inventories (Varna, Karbuna, Rudna Glava, Ai Bunar), Cucuteni megasite plans, megalithic monuments of the west and north. Reliability varies with the quality of each underlying excavation — 19th-c. finds (Turdaș 1874) and modern area digs are not equal witnesses.

**Iconography as system.** Ch.7–8 treat thousands of figurines and signs as a **decodable symbolic language**. Strength: forces attention to ritual architecture and image context that materialist syntheses sidelined. Weakness: typology can become circular (images classified by the theology they are said to prove); ethnographic and classical analogies (Potnia Theron, Artemis, folklore witches) risk presentist reading.

**Horses and waves.** Ch.10 inventories S’ezzhee, Dereivka, Sredniy Stog, early/late Yamna, Suvorovo, Usatovo, Baden–Vučedol–Ezero, Globular Amphora, Corded Ware. Later work (Anthony bit-wear AMS retraction on Dereivka cult stallion; aDNA) revises specific pillars without erasing the broader steppe–farmer interaction problem. Treat horse-riding early dates and ethnic labels as **her 1991 package**.

**Peace and equality.** Negative evidence (no weapon graves until late; no hill forts early) is real as a pattern claim but under-determined: ranking (Varna gold), late enclosures, and trauma studies require case-by-case balance she often subordinates to the dual-structure narrative.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

**Academic.** Renfrew names her the foremost recent archaeological rival on IE origins while rejecting pan-European Kurganization as language solution. Processual archaeology resisted culture=people=language migrationism and Goddess universalism. Gender archaeology often separates valuable attention to female imagery from matristic civilization claims.

**Popular Goddess movement.** Eisler’s endorsement and goddess-spirituality reception cast Indo-Europeans as destroyers of a utopian feminine world — Anthony explicitly flags this as cultural reception of the Kurgan story, not identical to the empirical steppe package. Gimbutas’s own prose is already strongly dualistic (peaceful gynocentric vs warlike patriarchal) and future-oriented in the Preface (“refocus our collective memory” as industrial “progress” threatens life on earth) — political-moral framing is internal to the 1991 book, not only a later distortion.

**Ethics record.** No misconduct scandal comparable to Chagnon–Tierney attaches to her fieldwork in sources ingested here. Reflexive issues are **interpretive overreach, ethnic labeling, and popular instrumentalization** rather than excavation fraud.

**aDNA irony.** Post-2015 genomic evidence for large-scale steppe-related ancestry movements into Europe (as of Reich 2018) rehabilitated a *demographic* steppe vector many processual critics had doubted — while still not licensing the Goddess civilization package. Gimbutas’s afterlife is thus split: half-vindicated on migration scale, still contested on religion and gender utopia.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

**Without Gimbutas:** Pontic-Caspian homeland arguments would still exist (Schrader, Childe’s steppe options, Mallory), but the **named multi-wave Kurgan package** and the **Old Europe** brand would not structure textbooks and culture wars the same way. Renfrew’s Anatolian hypothesis partly defines itself against her. Goddess spirituality’s prehistoric charter loses its principal archaeological author.

**Structural vs personal:** radiocarbon calibration of European Neolithic and the eventual aDNA steppe signal were structural scientific developments; the fusion of those chronologies with a gynocentric civilization narrative and a three-wave ethnic invasion story is **personal program-building**. Anthony’s later work shows the steppe homeland can be held without the full dual-structure theology.

**Counterfactual on feminism and archaeology:** second-wave interest in prehistoric gender would have found other anchors (Mellaart’s Çatalhöyük goddess, Bachofen revivals), but Gimbutas supplied the most complete *archaeological* narrative linking figurines, towns, script, and collapse.

### Verbatim primary passages (analyzed)

**On civilization (Preface):** “I reject the assumption that civilization refers only to androcratic warrior societies. The generative basis of any civilization lies in its degree of artistic creation, aesthetic achievements, nonmaterial values, and freedom which make life meaningful and enjoyable for all its citizens, as well as a balance of powers between the sexes.”

*Analysis:* This is a **normative redefinition**, not an empirical discovery. It licenses treating Neolithic artistic complexity as “civilization” while excluding warrior hierarchy criteria. Critics who keep state/urban/writing definitions will talk past her; the dispute is partly about the word’s intension.

**On Kurgan waves (Ch.10):** “The Kurgan tradition became manifest in Old European territories during three waves of infiltration: I at c. 4400–4300 B.C., II at c. 3500 B.C., and III soon after 3000 B.C. This chronology does not represent the evolution of a single group but of a number of various steppe peoples who shared a common tradition…”

*Analysis:* She builds in multi-group plurality (useful against caricatures of one “Kurgan race”) while still packaging shared tradition as IE-bearing pastoralism. Anthony later keeps multi-stage out-migration but drops broad Kurgan culture and pure invasion framing.

**On dual structure (Ch.10 conclusion theme):** collision of two symbolic structures in which Indo-European prevailed while Old European survived as undercurrent — without which European myth and symbol “cannot be comprehended.”

*Analysis:* Makes the archaeological narrative a **master key to European comparative mythology**. High payoff if true; high cost if the dual package over-simplifies regional hybridity and multi-causal collapse.
