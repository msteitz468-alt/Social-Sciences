## Formation and Training

The ingested primary source — *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967), the sole ingested Garfinkel volume this analysis is built from — is not a biographical document, and none of the biographical facts below are drawn from it; they are supplied as brief orienting background and flagged as such throughout. Harold Garfinkel (1917–2011) is widely reported in the secondary literature to have earned his doctorate at Harvard under [[parsons-talcott]], and to have spent the bulk of his career at the University of California, Los Angeles, where the volume's front matter locates him ("Author affiliation University of California, Los Angeles," l.1–9 of range 1). Beyond these two facts — Harvard training under Parsons, and a UCLA career — this analysis does not assert further biographical detail; a fuller Formation and Training account awaits a biographical source in the collection.

What *is* securely grounded in the 1967 volume is the intellectual formation Garfinkel himself narrates in the Preface: two decades of engagement with four named authors. "For twenty years their writings have provided me with inexhaustible directives into the world of everyday activities," he writes of Talcott Parsons, Alfred Schutz, Aron Gurwitsch, and Edmund Husserl (range 1, l.107–114). Of these, Parsons receives the most striking acknowledgment — striking because Garfinkel's mature program is often glossed, textbook-fashion, as simply anti-Parsonian. In his own words, "Parsons' work, particularly, remains awesome for the penetrating depth and unfailing precision of its practical sociological reasoning on the constituent tasks of the problem of social order and its solutions" (l.114–118). This is admiring, not merely oppositional, language. The formation Garfinkel describes is one of building *through* Parsons's formulation of the Hobbesian problem of order — how is social order possible at all? — while relocating its solution from an internalized normative system to members' situated, practical work of producing recognizable order moment to moment. The debt to phenomenology (Schutz, Gurwitsch, Husserl) supplies the technical vocabulary — indexicality, the natural attitude, the world taken for granted — that lets Garfinkel restate the Parsonian question in terms alien to Parsons's own structural-functionalist idiom.

By the time of the 1967 Preface, Garfinkel was already the convener of a decade-old research circle: he thanks by name Egon Bittner, Aaron V. Cicourel, Lindsey Churchill, Craig MacAndrew, Michael Moerman, Edward Rose, Harvey Sacks, Emmanuel Schegloff, David Sudnow, D. Lawrence Wieder, and Don Zimmerman as collaborators "over the prior ten years," with Sacks singled out: "his extraordinary writings and lectures have served as critical resources" (l.84–91). The book also documents its institutional and funding scaffolding — a U.S. Public Health Service Senior Research Fellowship, a California State Department of Mental Hygiene grant, Air Force Office of Scientific Research support, work conducted through the Center for the Scientific Study of Suicide at the Los Angeles Suicide Prevention Center (1963–64), and the Organizational Behavior Project at Princeton (l.120–176). This funding pattern — psychiatric, military-behavioral-science, and organizational-research money underwriting studies of jurors, clinic clerks, and a gender-transitioning patient — is itself a piece of production-context evidence for the Reception, Politics, and Ethics section below.

## Intellectual Genealogy

**Trained under (as reported in the secondary literature, not the 1967 source): [[parsons-talcott]]** at Harvard. The 1967 volume confirms the intellectual relationship even without confirming the institutional one: Garfinkel repeatedly engages Parsons's technical apparatus directly — crediting Parsons's concept of "collectivity membership" from *The Social System* and *Theories of Society* (range 3, footnote 1, p.76), invoking Parsons's treatment of "ascription" as a "relation concept" to frame Agnes's simultaneous subscription to sex-as-ascribed and lived experience of sex-as-achieved (range 4, l.783–788), and directly testing a specific Parsonian claim — the "institutionalized one price rule" as evidence of full normative internalization — against the bargaining-demonstration data, finding it wanting (range 2, l.1599–1663). The relationship recorded in this source is thus neither straightforward discipleship nor straightforward rebellion: Garfinkel treats Parsons's concepts as usable technical resources to be redeployed against Parsons's own solution to the order problem.

**Developed from: [[schutz-alfred]].** Schutz is the single most heavily cited living intellectual resource in the volume — credited for background expectancies and the "world known in common and taken for granted" (range 2, l.93–102), for the eleven constitutive background expectancies enumerated via his phenomenology (range 2, l.926–993), for "common sense knowledge of social structures" as a technical term (range 3, p.76, citing Schutz's *Collected Papers* I–III), for "common sense models" used by jurors (range 3, p.106), and — in Chapter 8 — for the fourteen-part inventory of "the rationalities" drawn directly from Schutz's 1953 *Economica* paper "The Problem of Rationality in the Social World" (range 6). Garfinkel's contribution, on this evidence, is best described as an empirical and methodological radicalization of Schutzian phenomenology: where Schutz described the structures of the natural attitude philosophically, Garfinkel converts them into breachable, testable, sociologically observable objects.

**Reacted against: Durkheim** (the specific target is "certain versions of Durkheim" on social facts as objective things) — Garfinkel's opening programmatic move substitutes "the objective reality of social facts as an ongoing accomplishment of the concerted activities of daily life" for social facts as sociology's fundamental, externally-given principle (range 1, l.31–46). Durkheim also reappears constructively, not only as a foil: the "non-contractual elements of contract" supply Garfinkel's model for background trust as the condition of rational action (range 4, l.2538–2543), and the Chapter 6 "contract" reading of clinic records is explicitly Durkheimian in sense, referring to "normal transactions between clientele and remedial agencies" rather than colloquial legal contract (range 5, p. citing "we use the term 'contract'…").

**Rival/contrast: [[goffman-erving]].** Garfinkel positions the Agnes analysis directly against Goffman's dramaturgical framework (citing *The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life*, 1956): he credits Goffman's descriptive acuity about impression management but argues Goffman's episodic model cannot handle Agnes's continuously developmental passing, and — the sharper point — that Goffman's calculating, strategically-managing actor is in tension with the sociological requirement that members treat most matters in a *trusting*, taken-for-granted manner for mutual judgments of normality to hold at all (range 4, l.2196–2199, 2558–2621). This is a genuine theoretical rivalry recorded in the primary text, not merely a citation.

**Named collaborators within the volume itself:** Robert J. Stoller, M.D. (co-author of Chapter 5, the Agnes case, and of its 1967 Appendix); Saul Mendlovitz of Rutgers Law School (co-author of Chapter 4, the jury study); [[bittner-egon]] (co-author of Chapters 6 and 7). The "Conferences on Ethnomethodology," a working group meeting since February 1962 at UCLA/Colorado, is named as the site where the etcetera clause was developed collectively, with Bittner, Garfinkel, Craig MacAndrew, Edward Rose, and Harvey Sacks listed as participants (range 2, l.1797–1846). This documents ethnomethodology's earliest institutional form as a small seminar circle, not a solitary theoretical exercise.

## Career Phases

Three phases are visible within the 1967 volume's own internal chronology, corroborated by its front-matter dating of individual chapters.

**Phase one — the theoretical statement (Chapter 1, largely new for 1967).** The programmatic essay "What is ethnomethodology?" lays out the definition, the three constituent problematic phenomena (indexicality, reflexivity of accounts, and analyzability-as-accomplishment), and four numbered methodological "policies," closing with the volume's thesis statement: "The demonstrably rational properties of indexical expressions and indexical actions is an ongoing achievement of the organized activities of everyday life. Here is the heart of the matter" (range 1, l.1811–1813).

**Phase two — the empirical demonstrations, 1954–1964 (Chapters 2–5, 7–8), substantially pre-dating the 1967 book.** The volume's front matter records that Chapters One (in part), Two, Three, and Eight were previously published — Chapter Two in *Social Problems*, Winter 1964; Chapter Three in *Theories of the Mind*, 1962; Chapter Eight in *Behavioral Science*, January 1960 (range 1, l.215–233). This means the famous breaching demonstrations of Chapter 2, the documentary-method "counselor" experiment of Chapter 3, and the rationalities analysis of Chapter 8 belong to a research program Garfinkel had been running since at least the mid-to-late 1950s — the book is a consolidation, not a single burst of work. The Agnes case (Chapter 5) is dated internally to October/November 1958–August 1959 (the source itself is inconsistent on the October vs. November first-contact date — flagged below), the earliest fieldwork instance being the Los Angeles Suicide Prevention Center collaboration, which the text dates to 1957 ("joint work began 1957," range 1, l.780–920).

**Phase three — the 1967 consolidation and its immediate crisis (the Agnes Appendix).** The volume was compiled and sent to press in 1966–67; a note appended to Chapter 5 — written, on the evidence of the text, after the volume was "already in press" — records that in February 1967 Garfinkel learned from Stoller that Agnes had disclosed, the previous October, that she had deceived the research team about the biological basis of her condition (range 6). This means the book's publication and the collapse of its central empirical chapter's premise occurred within months of each other — an extraordinary and largely self-documented moment of a research program's empirical ground shifting under it in real time, discussed at length below.

## The Empirical Base

The empirical materials in *Studies in Ethnomethodology* fall into three evidentiary registers, and they do not carry equal weight. Garfinkel himself is unusually candid about the first: the breaching demonstrations of Chapter 2 are explicitly *not* experiments. "My studies are not properly speaking experimental. They are demonstrations, designed, in Herbert Spiegelberg's phrase, as 'aids to a sluggish imagination'" (range 2, l.126–131). The samples are small, uncontrolled, and drawn overwhelmingly from Garfinkel's own undergraduate students performing assignments on family members and acquaintances, not strangers: 23 students / 25 encounters in the "please clarify" demonstration; 33 students in the passive "boarder" demonstration; 49 cases in the acting-out "boarder" demonstration; 35 accounts in the distrust demonstration (only 2 of 35 attempted with strangers); 68 and 67 students respectively in the two bargaining demonstrations; 247 games in the ticktacktoe demonstration; 79 accounts in the "nose-touching" demonstration. Reported outcomes (reactions of bewilderment, anger, "Drop dead!," a sister's "We're not rats, you know") are vivid and, on the evidence assembled, strikingly patterned across independent replications by different students against different subjects — a real strength of the design, since it is closer to a distributed set of small field trials than a single anecdote. But Garfinkel's own honesty about the correlation statistics from the "fake applicant" medical-interview study is instructive: he reports r = .026 rising to r = .751 between subscription to "natural facts" and anxiety, and immediately flags it — "Under no circumstances should they be considered as findings" (range 2, l.1408–1420) — because the assessment procedures were crude and reasoning post hoc. This is a thinker who builds theoretical claims on demonstrations he is explicit are illustrative rather than probative, and says so in the text; the wiki should treat the demonstrations accordingly — as evidence for the *plausibility and reproducibility of a reaction pattern*, not as controlled tests of a causal hypothesis.

The second register — the jury study (Ch. 4), the clinic-records study (Ch. 6), and the selection-criteria reanalysis (Ch. 7) — rests on more conventional social-scientific procedure: a 661-case sample drawn from a 3,305-case initial cohort (every fifth record), coded by a graduate assistant, analyzed with chi-square and life-table methods, explicitly checked against 23 prior published studies for methodological adequacy (range 5). This is Garfinkel at his most conventionally rigorous, and it is also where his critique bites hardest against the discipline's existing quantitative literature — the "in-in"/"out-out" comparison critique, applied to reanalyze Katz & Solomon (1958) and Myers & Schaffer (1954), reverses or changes their reported findings using the *same* published data. This is a genuine methodological contribution independent of the phenomenological program, and probably the least controversial empirical material in the book.

The third register is the Agnes case, and it is the volume's central and most consequential empirical episode. The chapter (Ch. 5) reports roughly 35 hours of taped conversation between Garfinkel and Agnes personally, plus collaborative work with Stoller and psychologist Alexander Rosen, totaling approximately 70 hours across the three researchers (range 4, l.240–242, 2093–2094). Garfinkel is candid, within the chapter itself, about the limits of what the researchers actually knew: he lists seven areas — including "possibility of an exogenous hormone source" — on which the team obtained no information from Agnes despite the extensive talk (range 4, l.2096–2110). A UCLA urological resident privately doubted the diagnosis on clinical grounds (inferring anal intercourse, suspecting exogenous estrogen) and opposed the surgery as "neither necessary nor ethical" — Garfinkel records that the team discounted this partly because "his dislike for Agnes was evident on other scores" (range 4, l.1983–1994), a piece of reasoning that reads, in hindsight, as motivated. The Appendix, written after the book was already in press, confirms the resident's suspicion was correct: in October 1966 Agnes disclosed to Stoller that she had been secretly taking her mother's Stilbestrol (a synthetic estrogen) since age twelve, financing and later self-filling the prescription, and had never had the biologically intersexed condition originally diagnosed (range 6). Garfinkel's own reflexive comment on learning this is worth quoting directly for what it does and does not concede: the disclosure "turned the article into a feature of the same circumstances it reported, i.e., into a situated report," and he reads the episode as exhibiting "that the recognizedly rational accountability of practical actions is a member's practical accomplishment," and that success at that accomplishment "consists in the work whereby a setting... masks from members' relevant notice members' practical ordering practices" (range 6). This is neither a retraction nor an admission of research misconduct on Garfinkel's part; it is, rather, Garfinkel recuperating the deception itself as a further, second-order instance of his own theoretical apparatus — Agnes's successful deception of the research team is redescribed as one more case of "passing," now practiced on the researchers rather than on family and employers. Whether this recuperation is intellectually satisfying or a convenient absorption of an awkward empirical failure into an unfalsifiable framework is a live question the wiki should record as a position rather than adjudicate (see Reception, Politics, and Ethics, below, and the recommended debate page).

It is worth stating plainly what this episode does and does not establish, in attributed voice. It establishes that the entire medical premise of Chapter 5 — that Agnes was a biologically intersexed male with an endogenous, unexplained estrogen source — was false, and that Agnes deliberately deceived Stoller, Garfinkel, and Rosen (and the endocrinological literature the chapter cites, notably Schwabe, Solomon, Stoller, and Burnham 1962) for at least eight years. It does not, on Garfinkel's own argument, straightforwardly invalidate the sociological analysis of "passing" as continuous practical work — the mechanics of impression management, anticipatory following, casing, and euphemism that Garfinkel catalogs (range 4, l.2279–2493) are independent, in principle, of whether Agnes's underlying physiology was as originally described. But it substantially changes what evidentiary weight the chapter can bear as a study of "natural" ambiguous sex status specifically, since the case's canonical framing — a person managing a genuinely ambiguous biological status — turns out to describe a person managing a wholly self-manufactured one. The wiki's Contradiction and Replication Protocol applies squarely here: the `garfinkel-agnes-study` page's `replication_status` should reflect reanalysis-contested standing, and any claim drawn from Chapter 5 asserting Agnes's biological intersex condition as fact must be corrected or flagged, while claims about Agnes's practical management work may be retained in attributed voice.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

The 1967 volume itself offers direct evidence of Garfinkel's methodological politics: a repeated refusal to treat ethnomethodology as corrective of, or superior to, "practical" or professional sociological reasoning. "Ethnomethodological studies are not directed to formulating or arguing correctives. They are useless when they are done as ironies" (range 1, l.74–76). This stance — refusing to adjudicate professional sociology's rationality against a scientific standard — is itself a considered political-methodological position, not a neutral stylistic tic: Chapter 8 argues at length that treating "scientific rationality" as a norm for judging everyday or professional conduct "multiplies the anomic features of interaction" rather than improving it (range 6). Ethnomethodology, on this evidence, positions itself as descriptively symmetrical between "science" and "common sense," a stance the text states with unusual bluntness: procedures of "water witching, divination, mathematics, sociology" are all addressed under the same policy, that every feature of sense, fact, and method is "the managed accomplishment of organized settings of practical actions" (range 1, l.1693–1719). Whether this radical methodological symmetry is a legitimate bracketing move or itself smuggles a relativism about scientific knowledge is exactly the kind of live dispute the wiki's debates apparatus exists to record rather than settle; a `[[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]]` page is the appropriate home for the "cultural dope" polemic and the broader dispute over whether ethnomethodology is a genuine sociology or a dissolution of sociology's explanatory ambitions.

The ethics record specific to this volume centers overwhelmingly on the Agnes case, and it deserves factual, non-adjudicating statement. Three distinguishable issues are visible in the text itself. First, informed consent and disclosure symmetry: Agnes deceived the researchers about her medical history for the duration of the study and for years afterward, while the researchers subjected her to extensive interviewing, an irreversible surgical intervention (castration and vaginal construction, March 1959), and post-publication scholarly exposure — an asymmetry of vulnerability that the chapter does not fully reckon with even in its own Appendix. Second, the dismissal of the dissenting urological resident's clinical judgment on grounds partly attributed to personal "dislike" for Agnes (range 4, l.1983–1994) raises a documented instance of researchers discounting inconvenient professional skepticism for reasons unrelated to its clinical merits — worth flagging as a case study in confirmation dynamics within a small, invested research team. Third, Garfinkel's own admission that he "passed" with Agnes at points — for instance, fabricating a plausible-sounding physiological explanation involving the "symphasis pubis" and guinea-pig hormone relaxin rather than admit ignorance (range 4, l.2077–2163) — is presented within the text as a further ethnomethodological data point (the researcher's own passing work), but it equally documents an instance of a researcher misleading a study subject to preserve rapport, a research-ethics fact independent of its theoretical redescription. None of this is adjudicated here; it is recorded, in Garfinkel's own words and his collaborator Stoller's, as the documented record of how the case was conducted and later revealed.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

What would sociology look like without Garfinkel specifically, as opposed to without ethnomethodology as a movement? The 1967 volume itself supplies grounds for a qualified answer. Structurally, the intellectual materials were already assembled and available before Garfinkel: Schutz's phenomenology of the natural attitude, Mannheim's documentary method, and Parsons's problem of order were all published, established resources by the mid-1950s, and Garfinkel repeatedly credits them as his own "inexhaustible directives" (range 1, l.107–114) rather than claiming originality for the underlying philosophical apparatus. In that sense, some version of a phenomenologically-informed challenge to structural-functionalism's account of order was arguably coming regardless of any one person — Alfred Schutz's own students and, separately, the broader "interpretive sociology" current (drawing on Weber's *Verstehen*) were already positioned to generate related critiques.

What appears genuinely personal to Garfinkel, on the evidence of this text, is the specific empirical move of *converting* phenomenological description into deliberately breachable, demonstrable, small-scale field trials — the innovation that the "seen but unnoticed" background can be made visible not just through philosophical bracketing but through engineered, repeatable disruptions of ordinary interaction, with observable, patterned, morally-charged reactions as the payoff data. This is not present, in this form, in Schutz, Mannheim, or Parsons; it is a distinctively Garfinkel-and-his-UCLA-circle contribution, developed collectively (per the "Conferences on Ethnomethodology" credit, range 2, l.1797–1846) but organized and published under Garfinkel's name and program. Similarly, the specific coinage "ethnomethodology," the "cultural dope" polemic, the "etcetera principle"/"ad hocing" vocabulary, and the systematic reanalysis-of-quantitative-method critique in Chapters 6–7 read as Garfinkel's own conceptual contributions rather than direct transcriptions of predecessors.

Without Garfinkel, it is plausible that something resembling symbolic interactionism or an expanded [[schutz-alfred]]-influenced phenomenological sociology would have absorbed some of the same critique of structural-functionalism's over-socialized actor — [[goffman-erving]]'s dramaturgical sociology, developing contemporaneously and cited adversarially within this very volume, already supplied an alternative anti-structural-functionalist account of everyday interaction management. But the specific combination this volume displays — breaching as method, indexicality and reflexivity as formal analytic categories, and the radical symmetry thesis about scientific versus common-sense rationality — is plausibly counterfactually fragile: absent Garfinkel's particular synthesis (and the UCLA seminar circle he organized around it), conversation analysis as it actually developed (through Sacks, Schegloff, and their students, all credited collaborators in this volume) would likely have taken a different, less phenomenologically-loaded shape, if it developed via that lineage at all.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

The primary source underlying this analysis is the single ingested edition: Garfinkel, *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice-Hall, 1967). No secondary biographical or critical-reception scholarship on Garfinkel has yet been ingested into this wiki; this section is accordingly limited to what the primary text documents about its own compositional and publication history, plus explicit flags for further ingest.

The volume states that its studies "were written over the last twelve years" (range 1) and that four of its eight chapters were previously published in whole or part: Chapter Two in *Social Problems* (Winter 1964); Chapter Three in *Theories of the Mind* (1962); Chapter Eight in *Behavioral Science* (January 1960); part of Chapter One in an unspecified prior venue (range 1, l.215–233). The book's own internal chronology therefore documents a research program running from at least the late 1950s to 1967, with the volume functioning as a consolidation rather than a single unified composition — a fact the wiki's `sources/` page for this text should record explicitly (`source_type: mixed` is warranted, given the volume mixes previously-published primary studies with new theoretical framing).

One internal inconsistency in the source itself is worth flagging rather than silently resolving: Chapter 5 states Agnes "appeared at the Department of Psychiatry at U.C.L.A. in October, 1958" (range 4, l.113–114) but later states "In November, 1958 Agnes was seen for the first time at U.C.L.A." (l.228) — an authorial or editorial inconsistency in the original text, not an extraction error, and one this analysis has deliberately left unresolved per the source.

A crucial gap for future ingest: this analysis cannot confirm from the ingested source alone the frequently-repeated claim in secondary sociology-of-the-discipline literature that the term "ethnomethodology" itself originated during the jury project (Chapter 4) — the ingested range states explicitly that neither the jury project's year/location nor this origin claim is verifiable within the text as extracted (range 3, coverage note). This should be checked against a proper history-of-the-discipline source before being asserted anywhere in the wiki, including on the `[[ethnomethodology]]` theory page.
