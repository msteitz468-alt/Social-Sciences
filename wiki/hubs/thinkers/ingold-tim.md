# Tim Ingold — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[ingold-tim]]. Built across the *Perception of the Environment* (2000), *Lines* (2007), *Being Alive* (2011), and *Making* (2013) ingests. With this pass the late-career trilogy announced in *Lines*'s 2016 Preface — *Lines* (2007), *Being Alive* (2011), *Making* (2013) — sits complete on this page, all three built on the *Perception* (2000) foundation.

**Analysis standard:** works timeline · concepts matrix · verbatim quotation · empirical base critical · counterfactual genealogy. Primary base for this page: *Perception* (2000), *Lines* (2007), *Being Alive* (2011), and *Making* (2013).

## Formation and Training

From the General introduction to *Perception* (autobiographical, attributed): Cambridge natural-science track; passion for physics cooled into disillusionment with science's military-industrial capture and hierarchical specialization. Tutor suggested anthropology (c. 1967). Motive: close the arts/sciences gap while remaining close to lived experience. He later "despaired" that anthropology fractured along the same humanity/nature fault line. Early conceit of being "the Galileo of anthropology" is disavowed; the bridging ambition remains.

*Lines* (2007 Preface) adds biographical texture Ingold himself treats as formative of the book's project. His father was the mycologist C. T. Ingold, whose "homely science" of riverside fungi Ingold recalls in detail: microscope samples drawn by hand with mapping pen, Indian ink, and Bristol board (Figure 2.1 reproduces one such drawing). Ingold credits this upbringing with his root intuition that organisms are not bounded objects but lines: "the fungal mycelium is a web of linear fibres, radiating in all directions, with no inside or outside, no coherent skin." He asks: "What if we were to take the mycelium as our exemplar of the organism? Arguably, the whole of biological science would be different." A second formative root is his own doctoral fieldwork among Skolt Saami people in the northeast corner of Finland, 1970–71 (16 months), participating in reindeer herding and fishing, collecting material on family, kinship, and domestic life, and following Saami–non-Saami political negotiations. He describes the experience as often lonely, requiring him to "follow my own path and find things out for myself" — the fieldwork in which he says the term "wayfaring" first crept up on him, unnoticed at the time: "a certain manner of carrying on, of combining movement and attention, which I have come to call 'wayfaring'." A third root, less developed on the page, is his lifelong cello playing, feeding the book's interest in music and sound as line-phenomena. His formal Cambridge training in kinship theory (following J. A. Barnes's 1967 article, which drilled the technical term "consanguines" in place of "blood relations") supplies the disciplinary target of the *Lines* Chapter 4 critique of the genealogical chart (see Conceptual Contributions, below).

## The Empirical Base

*Perception* is **not** a field monograph. It synthesizes secondary ethnography, Gibsonian psychology, phenomenology, and archaeological debate. **Strength:** conceptual integration and honest repudiation of Ingold's own earlier dualism. **Limitation:** ethnographic claims are second-hand and date-stamped to cited sources; no new systematic fieldwork dataset in the volume. Circumpolar/hunter-gatherer materials dominate Part I; craft cases (basket, bilum, weaverbird comparison) dominate skill chapters.

*Lines* rests on the same footing, and Ingold is candid about it: it is not a field monograph either, but a wide-ranging comparative synthesis built from published ethnography (Piro and Shipibo-Conibo shamanic writing/song in Peru, per Gow 1990 and Gebhart-Sayer 1985; Inuit wayfaring in Igloolik, per Aporta 2004; Batek root-gathering in Malaysia, per Lye 1997/2004; Foi paths in Papua New Guinea, per Weiner 1991; Orochon reindeer-hunting in Sakhalin, per Kwon 1998; Walbiri sand-drawing in Central Australia, per Munn 1973), art history (Semper, Kandinsky, Klee, Riegl), and philosophy (Bergson, Lefebvre, de Certeau). His **own** primary field material is limited to two brief, explicitly autobiographical illustrations — the Saami reindeer earmark-cutting example (Figure 2.3) and a personally witnessed Finnish–Soviet border fence "herding reindeer along the border... some twenty-five years ago" — both drawn from the same 1970–71 Skolt Saami fieldwork that underwrites *Perception*. The book's evidentiary weight therefore rests on the coherence of its comparative synthesis and its readings of others' ethnography, not on new fieldwork; several of its central examples (Gow's Sangama, Gebhart-Sayer's Shipibo-Conibo pot-painters) are themselves single, decades-old ethnographic accounts, carrying the standard second-hand-ethnography caveat this wiki flags throughout Ingold's work.

*Being Alive*'s empirical base is mixed, closer by chapter to *Lines* than to *Making*: most chapters synthesize others' published ethnography and philosophy rather than new fieldwork. The weather-world chapters (8–11) draw on Richard Nelson's Koyukon ethnography (Alaska), Chris Low's account of Khoisan hunter-gatherers, and a first-hand vignette — Ingold leading Aberdeen students on a stormy beach fieldtrip, used against Christopher Tilley's landscape phenomenology. The naming chapters (12–14) rest almost entirely on Nelson's and Julius Jetté's early-twentieth-century Koyukon corpus — a decades-old, single-ethnographer record carrying this wiki's standard second-hand-ethnography caveat. The Ch. 17 hylomorphism argument and the Ch. 7 ANT/SPIDER fable rest on no new fieldwork at all; they are philosophical constructions (Simondon, Deleuze and Guattari, Latour) illustrated by borrowed cases (Chartres) and, in Ch. 17, Ingold's own Aberdeen kite-making exercise — the same classroom register later expanded into *Making*'s full evidentiary base. The book's own fieldwork thread remains the 1970–71 Skolt Saami material, resurfacing briefly (the herdsman–toggle–lasso example, the "smoke"-dwelling observation from northern Finland). Overall, *Being Alive*'s authority — like *Lines*'s — rests on synthesis and readings of others' ethnography, not a fieldwork dataset of its own.

*Making*'s empirical base is different in kind from *Perception* and *Lines*: rather than synthesizing others' fieldwork, its primary evidence is Ingold's own classroom pedagogy. The book grew directly from the "4 As" (Anthropology, Archaeology, Art, Architecture) course he taught at Aberdeen from 2004 (precursor seminar at Manchester, mid-1990s–1999), combining lectures with practicals and a ten-week student project plus workshops (willow-weaving, an artist's-studio visit, a landscape walk). Chapter-by-chapter, the argument leans on specific pedagogical exercises reported as data: a Week 1/Week 2 "objects" vs. "materials" handling exercise (Ch. 2); a basket-weaving exercise near Aberdeen with craftsperson Stephanie Bunn, in which student weavers found willow "did not want to be bent into shape" (Ch. 2); kite-making and -flying (Ch. 7); string-making from palm-leaf fibres following Willeke Wendrich's (1999) ethnoarchaeological account of Egyptian basketry (Ch. 8); and a handwriting-versus-keyboard note-taking exercise (Ch. 8) — the "4 As" course itself was funded by the Arts and Humanities Research Board (grant B/RG/AN8436/APN14425). Beyond the classroom, the book draws on Ingold's participation in the fieldwork tradition of landscape phenomenology: he and students replicated Christopher Tilley, Sue Hamilton, and Barbara Bender's doorway-frame method (developed at Leskernick Hill, Bodmin Moor) at a ruined croft cottage on the hill of Bennachie, Aberdeenshire, obtaining the opposite result from the original study — the frame restored, rather than dissolved, the phenomenological sense of an interior — which Ingold treats as methodologically instructive rather than a failed replication. His own reindeer-herding fieldwork (Finnish Lapland, cited as Ingold 1993) resurfaces as the source of the herdsman–toggle–lasso example in the correspondence chapter. As with *Lines*, the book's authority rests less on new systematic data than on the coherence of Ingold's synthesis across borrowed philosophical resources (Simondon, Deleuze and Guattari, Heidegger, Bateson, Gibson, Polanyi) and a set of small, richly described, first-person pedagogical vignettes — a different, more autobiographical evidentiary register than either of the earlier two books, and one this wiki flags accordingly: the classroom exercises are illustrations of a theoretical argument, not a fieldwork dataset in the ethnographic sense.

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Celebrated bridge figure across anthropology, archaeology, geography, design. Criticized for soft-pedaling power and history. Autobiographical framing of Cold-War military-industrial science as ethical motive for anthropology is part of the self-presentation. Ch. 21 of *Perception* treats psychic-unity and race historiography carefully as critique of AMH packages — attribute as Ingold's argument, not wiki-voice race science.

*Lines*'s reception, by Ingold's own 2016 account, is a striking case of a book's audience migrating away from its author's home discipline: it was taken up in architecture, design, art, calligraphy, poetry, weaving, music, dance, geography, theology, philosophy, and linguistics, generated two art exhibitions (Edinburgh City Art Gallery, 2012; Pompidou Centre-Metz, 2013), and was translated into French, Spanish, and Japanese — while remaining, in his words, "an outlier" within anthropology itself. Ingold goes further, speculating that *Lines* may mark "the moment at which anthropology and I finally parted company," even as he continues to claim the anthropologist's identity for the book's comparative, critical, open-ended method. This self-assessment is itself a primary datum for the historiography of Ingold's career and for anthropology's own boundary-policing — record as his attributed self-positioning, not as a settled disciplinary verdict.

A distinct, load-bearing feature of *Being Alive* is Ingold's own **explicit self-revision** of "dwelling": he states in the Prologue that the term "carries an aura of snug, well-wrapped localism that seems out of tune with an emphasis on the primacy of movement," and that he "rather regret[s] having placed so much weight on it," preferring thereafter "habitation" and, as the operative term, "wayfaring." This is a corpus-wide correction, not a peripheral aside — the [[dwelling-perspective]] page's 1990s–2000 synthesis should be read alongside it as the point where Ingold himself began favoring wayfaring-first vocabulary, a shift already underway in *Lines* (2007) and completed programmatically here.

Ch. 2's "materials against materiality" argument also stages a **named scholarly dispute**: Ingold reports archaeologist Christopher Tilley responded directly to an earlier draft of the chapter, defending "materiality" as necessary to capture how stones are "given form and significance through incorporation into the social and historical contexts" of human life — a position Ingold quotes before rejecting it as smuggling mind/matter dualism back in. The same disagreement recurs in *Making* (2013), where Ingold again targets Tilley (and Bjørnar Olsen's "ban the word" charge); the dispute is a running disagreement across two books, flagged above as a candidate `debates/` topic rather than a settled verdict.

The closing chapter, "Anthropology is Not Ethnography," revives the Radcliffe-Brown/Kroeber idiographic-versus-nomothetic dispute and Edmund Leach's 1959 attack on Radcliffe-Brown, defending Radcliffe-Brown's comparative ambition (via Whitehead) while rejecting his residual "armchair" comparativism for anthropology as "a study with" rather than "a study of" people. This distinction, sharpened in *Making* Ch. 1 as "knowing from the inside," has been read by some anthropologists as underselling ethnographic fieldwork's evidentiary discipline in favor of a more programmatic anthropology — a live disciplinary reception question, not a resolved one.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Without Ingold, landscape archaeology's taskscape vocabulary would be thinner; one major non-Descola path out of nature/culture dualism would look different; craft and education-of-attention anthropology would still exist (Lave, etc.) but with a weaker phenomenological-ecological bridge. Multi-species turns would still happen; Ingold is a corridor, not the sole cause.

*Lines* extends the [[dwelling-perspective]] developed in *Perception* (2000) from environment/skill onto an explicit theory of the line as such — wayfaring, meshwork, and the threads/traces taxonomy are direct elaborations of dwelling-perspective epistemology ("knowledge... grows as we go along," echoing *Perception*'s claim that surfaces are encountered "in the world, not of it"). Without *Lines*, the "meshwork" vocabulary now common in relational and landscape archaeology, and the wayfaring/transport distinction used across mobility studies, would likely not exist in their current form, or would have arrived via a different route (Deleuze and Guattari's "rhizome" is the nearest rival vocabulary Ingold explicitly positions *Lines* against, without naming it directly in this text). The book itself announces its own sequels: Ingold's 2016 Preface points forward to *Being Alive* (2011) and *Making* (2013, whose closing chapter "Drawing the Line" is a direct continuation of *Lines* Ch. 5–6), plus *The Life of Lines* (2015).

*Being Alive* is the hinge of the trilogy: it supplies [[meshwork]]'s fullest, most explicitly named confrontation with Actor-Network Theory (the ANT-vs-SPIDER fable), coins [[weather-world]] as a rival to "landscape" now current in landscape archaeology and geography, and stages the self-revision from "dwelling" to "wayfaring"/"habitation" that qualifies how [[dwelling-perspective]] should be read from 2011 onward. Without it, the meshwork/network contrast would exist only in *Lines*'s more allusive form; "materials against materiality" — now a standard citation in material-culture theory — would not exist in named form; and "weather-world" and the objections to "soundscape" would have no textual anchor. The closing "Anthropology is Not Ethnography" argument is also the direct ancestor of *Making*'s "knowing from the inside" (Ch. 1) — the two books share the distinction almost verbatim, *Making* extending it into archaeology's "trowel's edge" and teaching itself.

*Making* consolidates the [[dwelling-perspective]] into a fuller, more explicitly programmatic research agenda spanning four disciplines at once (anthropology, archaeology, art, architecture) rather than the anthropology/archaeology-centered dwelling perspective of *Perception* and the line-focused comparative programme of *Lines*. Its critique of [[hylomorphism]] gives landscape and material-culture archaeology (the round-mound argument, the "materiality" polemic) a sharper target than *Perception*'s more diffuse taskscape vocabulary and a direct continuation of *Being Alive* Ch. 2 and Ch. 17's "textility of making" argument, and its correspondence/interaction distinction offers an explicit alternative to "material agency" and actor-network vocabularies circulating in the same literature (Gell, Latour, Malafouris, Barad) without engaging them as a formal debate — the ANT engagement proper belongs to *Being Alive*. Without *Making*, the "knowing from the inside" formulation of participant observation, and the "anthropology of" vs. "anthropology with" distinction now cited across the anthropology-of-art and anthropology-of-architecture literatures, would likely not exist in their current form.

With *Being Alive* now integrated, the late-career trilogy announced in *Lines*'s 2016 Preface — *Lines* (2007), *Being Alive* (2011), *Making* (2013), all built on the *Perception* (2000) foundation — is fully represented on this hub page. (*The Life of Lines*, 2015, remains outside the vault's current holdings and is not claimed as covered here.)
