# Georg Simmel — Deep Analysis

> Thinkers Hub deep analysis. The [[simmel-georg|`thinkers/` summary]] owns overview/contributions/genealogy/critiques/historiography at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: the summary links here by full path `[[hubs/thinkers/simmel-georg|…]]`; this page's `thinker_page` points back to [[simmel-georg]].

**Analysis standard:** interpretive claims in attributed voice; the antisemitism Simmel faced and his own positionality stated factually, neither celebrated nor prosecuted. Built primarily on two ingested Simmel sources — *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* (1900) and [[simmel-sociology-wolff-1950|Wolff's 1950 anthology]] of the *Soziologie* (1908) — plus Wolff's editorial reconstruction of the biography and American reception.

## Formation and Training

Georg Simmel was born in Berlin on 1 March 1858, the youngest of seven children of a baptised-Jewish family; his father, a partner in a well-known chocolate firm, died when Georg was a boy, and a family friend — founder of an international music-publishing house — became his guardian and left him a fortune that underwrote the life of an independent scholar. This detail matters biographically: Simmel never depended financially on the academic advancement the German university system withheld from him for three decades. He entered the University of Berlin at eighteen to read history, switched to philosophy after Mommsen's influence, and named the *Völkerpsychologie* founders **Moritz Lazarus** and **Heymann Steinthal** as his most important teachers, while also studying with Zeller and Harms (philosophy), Bastian (psychology), and Droysen, Sybel, Treitschke, and Grimm (history). He took his doctorate in 1881 with a dissertation on Kant's physical monadology — the Kantian question "how is X possible?" would become the template for his signature sociological question, "how is society possible?"

Simmel was born, in Lewis Coser's phrase, into the position of an intellectual **stranger** — a status he would later theorise. Raised Protestant by baptism in a Jewish family, at home in the Berlin cultural avant-garde but never securely inside the academic guild, he embodied the "unity of nearness and remoteness" his own essay on the stranger names (see [[the-stranger]]).

## Intellectual Genealogy

Simmel's formal teachers (Lazarus, Steinthal) mattered less for doctrine than for the neo-Kantian, folk-psychological milieu they represented; his deepest affinity was with **Wilhelm Dilthey**, who taught at Berlin across Simmel's entire tenure there. Wolff (1950) records the striking fact that Simmel, "characteristically," almost never cited Dilthey despite the evident parallels between Simmel's forms of sociation and Dilthey's *Geisteswissenschaften* — a reticence Wolff reads as symptomatic of Simmel's aversion to placing himself in a school.

Downstream, the genealogy is famously diffuse. Simmel wrote his own epitaph on it: "I know that I shall die without spiritual heirs — and that is as it should be. My estate is like cash distributed among many heirs, each of whom puts his share to use in some trade that is compatible with his nature but which can no longer be recognized as coming from that estate." The "heirs" who took their share include **Georg Lukács** (who called Simmel "the true philosopher of impressionism"), **Ernst Bloch**, **Siegfried Kracauer** (an early monographer), and — decisively for sociology — the American **Robert E. Park**, who audited Simmel's 1899–1900 Berlin lectures and became, more than Albion Small's translations, the conduit of Simmel into the Chicago School. **Max Weber**, who tried and failed to secure Simmel a chair at Heidelberg, absorbed and criticised his account of calculability and rationalisation. As Frisby notes, Simmel "founded no school" — the genealogy runs by capillary diffusion, not descent, which is itself the central puzzle of his counterfactual significance (below).

## Career Phases

**Privatdozent and the blocked chair (1885–1900).** From 1885 Simmel lectured at Berlin as a *Privatdozent*, paid only by student fees, on a remarkable range — logic, Kant, Schopenhauer, Darwin, pessimism, ethics, philosophy of art, social psychology, sociology. His lectures were events; contemporaries describe him "taking his students down an oblique pit into the mine," a "creation-at-the-moment-of-delivery." Yet his advancement stalled. He was widely typed as "an exclusively negative and critical spirit," a reputation he resented; writing to Weber on 18 March 1908 during a failed effort to obtain a Heidelberg professorship, he insisted: "My lectures, as, for many years, all my work, tend exclusively toward the positive… Nevertheless, that opinion has existed for a long time; it is my kismet."

**The sociological decade (1900–1908).** *The Philosophy of Money* (1900) and the *Soziologie* (1908) fall in this middle phase — the former a philosophical sociology of modernity through the single lens of money, the latter the systematic gathering of the *forms of sociation* that make Simmel a founder of the discipline. In 1900 he was made *ausserordentlicher Professor*, an honorary and non-remunerative title he held for fourteen years.

**Strasbourg and the late turn to *Lebensphilosophie* (1914–1918).** Only in 1914, at fifty-six, was Simmel called to a full chair at Strasbourg. The war disrupted teaching; his own stance shifted from an initial nationalist enthusiasm (the 1914 "Germany's Inner Transformation" speech) to a more distanced philosophical reckoning with the "crisis of culture." Dying of liver cancer, he retreated to the Black Forest to finish *Lebensanschauung* (1918), his metaphysical last book, and died on 26 September 1918. His widow recorded that he felt he "had done his essential work."

The through-line is Simmel's method of the essay as a form of cognition: an abstract relational claim, immediately tested against a swarm of concrete illustrations, then qualified. His own prefatory confession to the *Soziologie* is the clearest statement of the epistemic modesty this implies (attributed to Simmel): "nothing more can be attempted than to establish the beginning and the direction of an infinitely long road… it is a characteristic of the human mind to be capable of erecting solid structures, while their foundations are still insecure."

Two mechanics recur and repay close reading. First, Simmel repeatedly **borrows a logical or epistemological structure as a metaphor for a social-psychological mechanism**. Faithfulness he calls "induction by feeling": "At such and such a moment a relation existed. In formal analogy to theoretical induction, feeling concludes that, therefore, the relation also exists at a later moment" — the affect that preserves a bond after its originating motive lapses (see [[faithfulness-and-gratitude]]). Gratitude, correspondingly, is "the moral memory of mankind," the subjective residue of an [[exchange]] that "sinks into the soul" rather than into an external object.

Second, Simmel **separates a relational form from its origin** to expose its autonomy. On authority: "The 'born king' does not need a country, so to speak, he is king; he does not have to become king." On mediation, he translates a social function into an epistemic register: "Reason is everywhere the principle of understanding; on its basis can come together what on that of feeling and ultimate decision of the will is irreconcilably in conflict." And in the triad he insists the leap from two to three is qualitative, not additive: the third can unite, divide, enjoy the others' conflict (*tertius gaudens*), or engineer it (*divide et impera*) — possibilities that simply do not exist in the dyad, whose defining fragility is that "the dyadic element is much more frequently confronted with All or Nothing," dissolving entirely at the death of one member.

His Kant is not merely inherited but contested. Analysing gratitude, Simmel turns on Kant's identification of duty with freedom: "By his bold identification of doing one's duty with freedom, Kant ruled this character of duty out of court, but thereby confused the negative side of freedom with its positive side… Only when we give first are we free" — a sociological case (the irredeemability of the first gift) used to correct a point of transcendental ethics.

## Money as the Purest Form: Value, Exchange, and Objective Culture

*The Philosophy of Money* (1900) is Simmel's other founding move, and its argument runs by a distinct route from the *Soziologie*'s forms of sociation: not "how is society possible?" but how a single social institution — money — condenses the whole structure of modernity. The book has an "analytic" half establishing money's philosophical anatomy (value, exchange, substance) and a "synthetic" half tracing its cultural and psychological effects (freedom, culture, personality, the style of life).

**Exchange as the source of value, not its expression.** Against the classical economists' assumption that objects carry value prior to and independent of trade, Simmel argues the relation runs the other way — value is *constituted* in the act of exchange itself: "Need and enjoyment alone do not comprehend either value or economic life, which are realized simultaneously through the exchange between two subjects each of whom requires a sacrifice by the other… Exchange, i.e. the economy, is the source of economic values, because exchange is the representative of the distance between subject and object which transforms subjective feelings into objective valuation." He draws the analogy to Kant explicitly, converting an epistemological claim into a sociological one: "the possibility of the economy is at the same time the possibility of the objects of the economy" — just as Kantian experience is constituted by the forms of understanding, economic value is constituted by the reciprocal act of exchange, not discovered as a pre-existing property of things. This is the "storey beneath historical materialism" the *Major Works* table above flags: a philosophical grounding of value-theory that both parallels and, Simmel insists, underlies Marx's economic categories.

**Money as the purest form of the means-becoming-end.** Simmel's central structural claim about money is that it is *pure* mediation — a tool so completely emptied of intrinsic content that it can serve as the universal intermediate link in any chain of purposes: "Money is the purest form of the tool… an institution through which the individual concentrates his activity and possessions in order to attain goals that he could not attain directly." Because money has no qualitative content of its own, it can stand for *any* object of desire, which is precisely what allows the general psychological reversal Simmel diagnoses across the book — a means, repeatedly and self-sufficiently pursued, detaches from the ends it was meant to serve and becomes an end in itself. He generalises the mechanism outside economics too, in his account of the origin of aesthetic value: "whereas formerly the object was valuable as a means for our practical and eudaemonistic ends, it has now become an object of contemplation… The whole development of objects from utility value to aesthetic value is a process of objectification." Money's case is the same logical structure at maximum purity and maximum social consequence: the most placeholder-like of objects becomes, for the miser and for modern economic life generally, the most desired object of all.

**Money and individual freedom — a widening but impersonal web.** The synthetic half's most consequential thesis is that money simultaneously **expands** individual freedom and **thins** the personal bonds through which that freedom is exercised. Simmel ties this to a centuries-long parallel movement between the objectification of nature and the individuation of the person: "the accentuation of the one seems to require the accentuation of the other in order to preserve the equipoise of inner life." Applied to the economy: "the personal element increasingly recedes from production, product and exchange. This process, however, releases individual freedom… individual freedom grows to the extent that nature becomes more objective and more real for us… so that this freedom increases with the objectivation and depersonalization of the economic universe." The claim is double-edged and Simmel insists on holding both edges at once: money frees the individual from the older bondage of concrete, personal, particularistic dependence (the serf tied to a specific lord, the guild member fixed to a specific craft-community) — but the price of that liberation is that dependence does not disappear, it *diffuses*: the modern individual depends on an ever-widening, ever more anonymous network of producers, creditors, and exchange-partners, related to money rather than to any of them as persons. Freedom purchased this way is real but impersonal — a freedom of relations emptied of the relations' original content.

**Objective culture, subjective culture, and the tragedy of culture.** The synthetic half closes on a thesis that would become one of Simmel's most influential legacies: the growth of "objective culture" (the accumulating, self-elaborating stock of tools, institutions, sciences, and cultural products a society produces) increasingly outpaces "subjective culture" (any individual's capacity to absorb, master, and be formed by that stock). Frisby's editorial apparatus glosses the terminal statement of the problem, quoting Simmel's own 1911 essay "The Concept and Tragedy of Culture" for its sharpest formulation of a thesis first worked out at length in *Philosophy of Money*: the widening gap between what culture produces and what any subject can inwardly appropriate is, in Frisby's paraphrase, "the peculiar tragedy of culture." Georg Lukács — who named Simmel his "theoretical guide" into questions of reification before breaking with him — read the *Philosophy of Money*'s discussion of objectification as an idealist-metaphysical cousin of Marx's own analysis, framing money's self-sufficiency as "the insoluble opposition between subjectivity and cultural forms, between soul and mind." Simmel's own relation to Marx is best stated cautiously and in attributed voice: the book's chapters bearing most directly on *Capital* — the critique of the labour theory of value and the discussion of the division of labour — do not engage Marx's arguments point by point, and one commentator judges his acquaintance with Marx's text less thorough than Tönnies's; the resemblance is one of parallel structure (a "deepening" of historical-materialist categories from underneath), not demonstrated direct engagement.

**Money, style of life, and the metropolis (attributed from knowledge; the essay itself is not in the ingested source).** Simmel extended the money-and-objectification argument into an explicit sociology of urban experience in the 1903 essay "Die Großstädte und das Geistesleben" ("The Metropolis and Mental Life"), not part of the *Philosophy of Money* text ingested for this hub entry and therefore reported here from established secondary literature rather than verbatim primary quotation. The essay's core claim — attributed — is that the money economy and the metropolis are two expressions of the same underlying form: the "swift and uninterrupted change of outer and inner stimuli" of city life, mediated by a money economy premised on abstract calculability, forces the urbanite into the protective psychological posture Simmel named the **blasé attitude** (*die blasierte Attitüde*) — an evenness of response, a reckoning of qualitative differences in quantitative, monetary terms, that shields the nervous system from overstimulation at the cost of a general dulling of discrimination. Frisby's introduction to the present volume records that this line of Simmel's thought — "Money … objectifies the 'style of life,' forces metropolitan people" into calculative reserve — is continuous with, not separate from, the *Philosophy of Money*'s central argument; the book supplies the philosophical anatomy (objectification, the means-end reversal, the tragedy of culture) that the metropolis essay applies to a single social site. The essay's reception is a documented case of high, delayed influence: Robert Park's transmission of Simmel to the Chicago School fed directly into Louis Wirth's "Urbanism as a Way of Life" (1938) and into the broader tradition catalogued at [[urban-sociology]]; Walter Benjamin's *Arcades Project* reads Simmel's metropolitan dweller as a companion figure to Baudelaire's *flâneur* (Jameson's retrospective judgment, noted in the Historiography section below).

## The Empirical Base

Simmel did no fieldwork, ran no surveys, collected no systematic data. His "evidence" is a virtuosic analogical survey of historical and comparative anecdote marshalled to exhibit a form — Roman consuls and Venetian denunciation culture for the triad, the Frankfurt Jewish head-tax for the stranger, Quaker silent meetings for mass emotionality, the stock exchange as the "nursery" of the [[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]]. *The Philosophy of Money* follows the identical method at book length: the aesthetic-value excursus above is characteristic — a general claim about objectification is established not by data but by a single "very simple observation" generalised across "the sphere of individual psychology to that of the species as a whole," with the author's own caveat that "any quantitative designation in terms of distance is more or less symbolic." This is the deliberate method of [[formal-sociology]], and it is also its most attacked feature. Several traits of the evidentiary style deserve flagging in attributed voice:

- **Psychological substructure asserted, not demonstrated.** Simmel routinely grounds a sociological claim in a confident psychological generalisation with no cited support — e.g., the "two layers" of the soul ("one deeper, hard or impossible to move… the other composed of momentary impulses"). These are theoretical/psychological claims, not findings.
- **Folk and proverbial material used as data.** "If God gives somebody an office, he also gives him the mind necessary for it" is quoted approvingly as evidence for his thesis that latent qualifications exist "in very great diffusion."
- **Impressionistic diagnosis of modernity.** The metropolis essay's mechanism — the city's "swift and uninterrupted change of outer and inner stimuli" forcing a shift from heart to head ("He reacts with his head instead of his heart") — is introspective and observational, without measurement. Simmel's own closing methodological stance is telling: "it is not our task either to accuse or to pardon, but only to understand."

The contrast with the wiki's other classical founders is sharp and instructive: where [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] anchors sociology in moral statistics and the [[social-fact]] and Weber in comparative-historical apparatus, Simmel anchors it in the *form* displayed by illustration. His forms have proven extraordinarily generative precisely as hypotheses; whether the method could ever be *cumulative* is the substance of the [[simmel-scientific-status-debate|scientific-status debate]].

## Reception, Politics, and Ethics

Simmel's career is a documented case of academic antisemitism. His exclusion from a regular chair until 1914 is attributed by Frisby (via Weber and Baumgarten) partly to the pervasive antisemitism of the Wilhelmine academy — Weber "never forgave Dilthey, Rickert and Windelband for concertedly blocking the call to a full professorship for Georg Simmel… because he was a Jew" — and partly to his genuinely unclassifiable style. Wolff (1950) reproduces Aloys Fischer's 1918 obituary, which framed Simmel's achievement in explicitly racial terms ("the Semitic thinker, at whom people like to look askance"), and labels it plainly "a well-known variety of anti-Semitism." When Simmel's son fled Nazi Germany, "practically nothing of Simmel's possessions was salvaged" — the thin archival record that frustrates his biographers is itself a residue of the persecution.

His own positionality bears on the content, and this is where reflexivity is content, not apology. In "The Stranger," Simmel names "the classical example… the history of European Jews" for the stranger-as-trader position, and analyses the medieval Frankfurt Jewish head-tax (*Beede*) as evidence that Jews were taxed "as a Jew," not as individual property-holders. A baptised-Jewish scholar occupying exactly the position of "nearness and remoteness" he theorised, writing an "objective" formal analysis whose paradigm case is the European Jew, invites — without settling — the question of how far the sociology sublimates the autobiography. The passage is recorded here as an interpretive question, not a verdict.

The reception dispute proper was over method. Park and Spykman's call to "return to Simmel" met Pitirim Sorokin's dismissal of his sociology as "pure speculation, metaphysics, and a lack of scientific method"; Leopold von Wiese rejoined in Simmel's defence. The exchange is filed at [[simmel-scientific-status-debate]]. Talcott Parsons then marginalised Simmel in *The Structure of Social Action* (1937), which largely wrote him out of the mid-century American canon — a suppression the 1980s revival reversed.

## Legacy and Counterfactual Significance

Simmel's own "cash among many heirs" metaphor frames the counterfactual precisely: because he built no school, his influence is hard to see yet nearly ubiquitous. Which of his moves were personal and which structural — coming anyway?

Some were plausibly structural. A relational, anti-substantialist conception of society was in the air (Tönnies, the neo-Kantians); had Simmel never written, some version of "society as interaction rather than organism" would likely have arrived. But several specific forms look genuinely personal — that is, unlikely to have been isolated as *forms* by anyone else on his timeline. The stranger as a positive sociological position (not a psychological type), the qualitative dyad/triad break, secrecy as a world-enlarging social achievement, sociability as the pure play-form: these are Simmel's signature abstractions, and the traditions that grew from them (Park's marginal man and the whole Chicago lineage, Caplow's coalition theory, Goffman's information control, network-theoretic *tertius gaudens*) are traceable to specific essays. Remove Robert Park's Berlin audit of 1899–1900, and the transmission belt into American sociology narrows sharply — a contingency of biography, not of intellectual necessity.

The paradox the wiki should preserve is that Simmel's program is officially [[formal-sociology|absorbed]] — there is no living "formal-sociological science" — yet his individual forms are more alive than most named schools. The estate dispersed; the coins still circulate.

## Historiography and Primary Sources

The primary corpus is uneven in translation. *The Philosophy of Money* (Bottomore & Frisby, 1978) and Wolff's *The Sociology of Georg Simmel* (1950) are the load-bearing English sources; Donald Levine's *On Individuality and Social Forms* (1971) extended the accessible canon. Wolff himself judged the existing Simmel literature inadequate to its subject — failing "to convey the uniqueness of his mind" — naming Gertrud Kantorowicz's preface to the posthumous *Fragmente und Aufsätze* as a rare exception. The only contemporary full-length study Wolff knew was Nicholas Spykman's *The Social Theory of Georg Simmel* (1925), whose biographical sketch derived from Simmel's widow; the only formal bibliography was Rosenthal and Oberlaender's 1945 list of 252 items in the *American Journal of Sociology*.

The reputational arc has three phases: enthusiastic early American reception (Small's AJS translations, Park & Burgess, Spykman) → mid-century eclipse (Sorokin's methodological attack; Parsons's marginalisation) → a sustained revival since the 1980s (Frisby's *Sociological Impressionism* and biography, Poggi, Lichtblau, Köhnke's *Der junge Simmel*) that restored *The Philosophy of Money* to the classical canon and recast Simmel as a "sociological predecessor" of Walter Benjamin (Jameson) and the ancestor of the cultural sociology of money (Zelizer, Dodd). A standing methodological caution for any historiography: because Simmel's evidence is illustrative anecdote, his historical examples (Inca administration, the Frankfurt *Beede*, Roman *lex*) should be read as attributed to his argument, never laundered into wiki-voice historical fact.
