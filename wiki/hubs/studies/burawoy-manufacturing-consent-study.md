# Manufacturing Consent — Deep Analysis

Summary page: [[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-study]] · Source: [[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-1979]] · Author: [[burawoy-michael]]

## Question and Intellectual Context

The book opens by inverting a half-century of industrial sociology. From Mayo forward, the field asked why workers restrict output — why they don't work harder. Burawoy, quoting the Lynds' *Middletown* (1929), asks the opposite: "Why do workers work as hard as they do?" — and makes it personal: "What was driving me to increase Allied's profits?... That is the problem I pose." The question presupposes his finding: on the shop floor he observed himself "spontaneously cooperating with management in the production of greater surplus value."

Three contexts frame the study. First, the postwar sociology he attacks: "end of ideology" complacency (Bell, Kerr, Lipset, Parsons, Shils), Mayo's "nonlogical" workers, and an organization theory caught in what Burawoy calls its central paradox — resting on either underlying harmony or the necessity of social control, without reconciling the two (Durkheimian and Weberian inheritances, rarely made to meet). His stated aim is not demolition but rescue: "the rational kernel of plant sociology" incorporated into a Marxist framework. His resolution: "Conflict and consent are neither latent nor underlying but refer to directly observable activities... products of the particular organization of work."

Second, Braverman. *Labor and Monopoly Capital* appeared "while I was struggling to make out at Allied," and Burawoy's approach "has been largely shaped in opposition to many of the dominant themes" of it ([[labor-process-debate]]).

Third, Gramsci. The only Gramsci essay directly on the labor process, "Americanism and Fordism," supplies the epigraph-thesis: in the United States, "hegemony here is born in the factory." Burawoy: "In this study I try to develop and elaborate this suggestive but elusive comment" — consent produced at the point of production, "independent of schooling, family life, mass media, the state, and so forth."

Biographically, the study fuses two formations: Zambian copper-industry research (1968–72) under Jaap van Velsen, carrying the Manchester School's situational analysis into industrial sociology; and a Chicago dissertation committee chaired by William Julius Wilson, with Adam Przeworski's 1973–74 seminar on Marxist theories of the state shaping the internal-state chapters and referee Maurice Zeitlin's 25 pages of critique forcing substantial rewriting.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

Burawoy worked from 2 July 1974 through May 1975 as a "miscellaneous machine operator" in the small-parts department of the engine division of "Allied Corporation," a multinational agricultural/construction-equipment maker. The restudy was serendipity recognized: matching details — machine layout, the Illinois Central line Roy rode from the University of Chicago, old-timers' memory of a "jack shop," the identical union local number — revealed his shop as the "Geer Company" of Donald Roy's 546-page 1945-era dissertation ("Making Out on the Shop Floor," fieldwork October 1944–August 1945 as a radial-drill operator). "To discover what had remained the same on the shop floor and what had changed in the thirty years separating Roy's experience and mine inevitably became one of the central tasks of my study."

The two observers' positions differ instructively. Roy's observation was covert, second-shift, and confined to what he could see — "we therefore learn virtually nothing about Geer Company, the union, other departments, the nature of various markets." Burawoy worked overtly, "with the explicit consent and knowledge of management as to why I was there," with access to managerial records, reconstructing Geer's corporate context through retired managers, Roy's asides, and business journals — anonymity for firm and individuals being the price of access. He declines a "ritual methodological appendix," but addresses the comparison's validity directly: he and Roy occupied "almost identical positions in the labor process," so "the changes I present are 'true' changes and not artifacts of any different orientation" — and he quotes Roy extensively so readers can judge. Machine-tool technology in the department was roughly constant over the thirty years, isolating organizational from technical change.

Two logics defend single-case generalization: "the part as an expression of the totality" (the case contains the essential principles — internal labor market, internal state — of advanced capitalism's labor process) and "generalization by extension from the part to the whole" (situating the case among family, school, state, union, other firms). A quantitative arm — regressions on individual piece-rate output for 185 operators over the first eleven months of 1975 — tests the consciousness argument.

## The Site, Sample, and Data

Geer in 1945 was a small, market-vulnerable jobbing firm ("whistle and whip" days within living memory); Allied, which absorbed it in 1953, was a large integrated corporation whose engine division sold into a captive internal market. The 1974 boom (record farm income; six-to-seven-day weeks; up to eighty hours' overtime; castings shortages from a declining foundry industry) turned into the December 1974 year-end loss, the firing of the general manager, and 1975 retrenchment and layoffs — giving the ethnography a natural experiment in recession (Ch. 8). Data: participant observation across shifts and jobs, Roy's dissertation, managerial records (output, scrap, productivity accounting), union contracts back to 1945, interviews, and correspondence with Roy himself, who confirmed the central comparative finding.

## Findings in Detail

**The game (Chs. 4–5).** "Making out" — collecting the card, negotiating with the scheduling man, squaring the crib attendant (Burawoy's Christmas ham to Harry), depending on truckers and inspectors, beating the rate — dominated shop-floor talk in both eras ("Are you making out?" "Gravy!"). The 1975 apparatus: 100% standard, 125% "anticipated rate," 140% informal ceiling policed by workers *and* management (superintendents returned cards over 140%, instructing operators to bank the excess), "kitties" of banked output, "goldbricking" on impossible rates. Both Roy and Burawoy record the same conversion narrative — initial scorn, then absorption ("He struggled to attain quota 'for the hell of it'" — Roy); Burawoy needed three to four months and found acceptance on the floor contingent on playing well. Foremen expedite and referee rather than police — the "double red card" compensating lost time at 125% is an object of bargaining. Against the entire "autonomous worker games" tradition (Mayo, Roethlisberger & Dickson, Homans, Crozier) and against Castoriadis's reading of games as proto-revolutionary self-organization, Burawoy shows management inside the game — and draws the book's core claim:

> "The very activity of playing a game generates consent with respect to its rules. The point is more than the obvious, but important, assertion that one cannot both play the game and at the same time question the rules... consent rests upon — is constructed through — playing the game. The game does not reflect an underlying harmony of interests; on the contrary, it is responsible for and generates that harmony."

The game obscures (workers inserted as individuals; conflict runs laterally — "Fuck the company, fuck the union, but most of all fuck the truckers because they fuck us all") and secures (output steady within limits set by "minimum wages and acceptable profit margins"). Even accusations that the company is "screwing" workers concern fair conditions for playing — broken drills, missing blueprints — and so "reassert the legitimacy of the rules." Crisis typology: system (profit floor), legitimation (uncertainty too high or too low), motivational (the game loses value).

**Thirty years (Chs. 4, 6–7).** From Geer 1945 to Allied 1975: bargaining moved "out of the shop and into the conference room"; time-study men with stopwatches ("watchdogs of efficiency," fought "as a game, a matching of wits") gave way to office-bound industrial engineers (three rate increases in ten months); hierarchical antagonism was redistributed laterally (understaffed auxiliaries; Roy by letter: "Your point in regard to the big switch of hierarchical conflict to the side of inter-worker competition pleases me immensely"). The **internal labor market** — three wage grades in 1945; U.S. Steel-style job classification from 1947; twelve grades and a full plant-wide bidding system by 1975, with seniority-scaled pensions (1959), supplementary unemployment benefits (1956), vacations, and bumping rights — "internalizes... the competitive individualism of 'free and equal' laborers" and constitutes interests "as those of one individual agent against other individuals rather than those of one class opposed to another class." The **internal state** — union shop, rationalized discipline, grievance machinery — makes the worker an "industrial citizen"; collective bargaining is "another game — this time a game about rules and outcomes of other games"; its relative autonomy protects management "from its tendencies toward arbitrary interventions that would undermine the consent produced at the point of production." Persistent facts cut both ways: union cynicism unchanged since 1945; 6–9% meeting attendance; a 408–307 ratification of a pension-weighted contract the rank and file disliked; over a third of members Black under an all-white union executive (reported grievance, unadjudicated).

**Recession (Ch. 8).** The 1975 downturn tested consent: absenteeism fell ~80% while output barely moved — "coming to work" (coercion-sensitive) is not "working" (consent-governed). Scrap and productivity numbers were partly artifacts of recording politics (each new quality manager clearing the last one's unrecorded scrap; engineers "banking" phantom improvements). The division protected its technical core by externalizing costs to the corporate parent — an option, contra Thompson's organization theory, available only to monopoly-sector firms.

**Consciousness (Ch. 9 + Appendix).** Against Goldthorpe et al.'s imported "orientations to work" (critiqued on four grounds, including reliance on attitudes without behavior — Westergaard: "The 'cash nexus' may snap just because it is only a cash nexus"), Burawoy's regressions found log-seniority and log-experience explained the bulk of explicable output variance (R²=.406; race, age, education, marital status adding ~3.5%). Shop-floor race relations ran through Radcliffe-Brown's joking relationship: prejudice imported as idiom, cooperation determined by production relations ("external consciousness lives on in the joking relationship but only as an idiom"). The Zambian appendix replays the argument comparatively: the colonial color bar, "lashing" as initiation and labor reservoir, Zambianization's contradictions — with Gluckman ("an African miner is a miner"), Epstein, Mitchell, and Kapferer against Bendix- and Bates-style culturalism. Mediation results he labels "suggestive," "no proof."

**Periodization (Ch. 12).** Two ideal types: "Anarchy in the market leads to despotism in the factory... Subordination of the market leads to hegemony in the factory." Marx was right that competitive capitalism would be overthrown, wrong that its overthrow was capitalism's: monopoly capitalism emerged, in which "class struggle was not the gravedigger of capitalism but its savior." The hegemonic organization is not universal (agribusiness; competitive-sector hybrids; Britain/Japan as poles); crisis theory is deflated ("as much a product of the Marxist imagination as of the real world"); socialism is now likelier first in the periphery.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The signature move is **turning the field's anomaly into the explanandum**: output restriction, industrial sociology's puzzle, becomes evidence of a game whose playing produces the real puzzle — effort and consent. Second, **the restudy as time machine**: every theoretical claim about monopoly capitalism is disciplined by a dated, same-site comparison, with Roy quoted at length and even enlisted by correspondence. Third, **first-person implication**: the ethnographer's own making out, his ham for the crib attendant, his ruined pulleys, stand in for the worker's structural position — the book's answer to its question is autobiographical before it is statistical. Fourth, **feudalism, not socialism, as the contrast class** for the capitalist labor process (following Banaji's crystallized feudalism), which lets the labor/labor-power distinction and the obscuring/securing dilemma do the analytic work: "The defining essence of the capitalist labor process is the simultaneous obscuring and securing of surplus value."

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book became the consent-side charter of the [[labor-process-debate]], the standard rejoinder to Braverman's deskilling thesis, and the canonical demonstration that Gramscian [[hegemony]] can be studied ethnographically at the point of production. "Relations in production," the internal labor market/internal state pairing, and the despotic/hegemonic typology structured the comparative program Burawoy pursued in *The Politics of Production* (1985, announced in the book's notes). Methodologically it seeded the [[extended-case-method]]'s sociological formulation — theory-driven ethnography reconstructing Marxist theory against shop-floor anomalies — and its critique of consciousness-first explanations parallels [[willis-learning-to-labour-study|Willis]]'s contemporaneous account of consent produced through workers' own culture.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

- **Burawoy's own caveats:** single case; "meager" evidence on some 1945 contrasts; quantitative mediation "suggestive"; anonymity constraints on verification.
- **Baseline asymmetry:** the 1945 pole rests on Roy's covert, second-shift observation — Burawoy's candor about this does not remove it.
- **Scope:** one monopoly-sector piece-rate machine shop generalized to "the" hegemonic labor process; Burawoy himself lists exceptions (agribusiness, competitive sector, national variation).
- **Gender:** two women in the department; sex excluded from the regressions — the labor-process literature's later feminist turn starts where this book stops.
- **The debate:** Braverman-side and control-side positions (Edwards's bureaucratic control) remain live; see [[labor-process-debate]].

## Current Standing

A classic of the sociology of work and of Marxist ethnography — read as the demonstration that consent is an observable, organized product of the labor process; as a model restudy; and as the hinge between the Manchester School's situational analysis and the extended case method. Its dated findings describe one shop in 1944–45 and 1974–75; its theory remains a contending position, not a verdict.
