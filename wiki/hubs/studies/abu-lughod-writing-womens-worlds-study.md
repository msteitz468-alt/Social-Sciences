# Writing Women's Worlds — Deep Analysis

## Question and Intellectual Context

By the late 1980s, anthropology's [[writing-culture-debate|crisis of representation]] had made classic monographic authority look like a literary-political achievement rather than a transparent window onto "a culture." Feminist anthropology had recovered women's worlds but faced Strathern's charge of an awkward relation between feminism and anthropology — and faced Third World feminist critiques of Western emancipatory projects projected onto "other" women (Ong, Mohanty, Lazreg). Middle East ethnography sat under Orientalist stereotypes of secluded, oppressed Muslim women and under Mediterranean "honor and shame" typologies.

[[abu-lughod-lila|Lila Abu-Lughod]] had already published *[[abu-lughod-veiled-sentiments-1986|Veiled Sentiments]]* (1986): a dual-discourse analysis of [[honor-and-modesty|honor/modesty]] ordinary talk versus [[ghinnawa|ghinnāwa]] poetry of vulnerability among [[awlad-ali|Awlad ʿAli]] Bedouin. *Writing Women's Worlds* (1993) is the companion move: not another ideology-and-poetry analysis, but a **narrative ethnography** that tries to **[[writing-against-culture|write against culture]]** — to refuse the typifying generalization that makes "Bedouin culture" seem homogeneous, coherent, and timeless.

Portions of the introduction had already appeared as "Writing Against Culture" (Fox ed., *Recapturing Anthropology*, 1991) and "Can There Be a Feminist Ethnography?" (*Women and Performance* 5, 1990). The book thus enters as both fieldwork product and programmatic intervention.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How to represent Awlad ʿAli women's lives without essentializing "culture"? | Particular narratives, conversations, songs, arguments | Same Western Desert *najiʿ* / household network as *Veiled Sentiments* (Haj Sagr, Gateefa, Migdim, daughters) | Narrative ethnography; taped talk; life-history event stories; elicited essay (Kamla) | Returns 1986–89 (NEH 1986; Fulbright bulk 1987; Dec 1989 family visit) on 1978–80 base |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** Abu-Lughod reported that in the late 1970s she rarely felt comfortable tape-recording; returns in the mid-to-late 1980s enabled fuller recording of expressive narratives. Migdim refused a formal "life story" request in 1979 and still deflected Western life-story form in 1986 — offering event stories, songs, and arguments instead. Kamla's chapter partly rests on a written essay plus oral parenthetical commentary after high-school graduation. Story selection, she stated, was guided by what excited and concerned the women *and* by salience for Western audiences — a double filter, not a transparent sample of "women's culture."

**Positionality.** Halfie (Arab-American, Muslim-recognized; hosts increasingly focused on part-Palestinian identity); father-mediated 1978 entry; 1989 return with father, Jordanian aunt, and English husband Tim Mitchell as "daughter and new bride" with sheep and bride's postmarital-visit gifts — a patriarchal reconfiguration she analyzed as data. She placed her authorial presence between total erasure and dialogic equal-participant theater.

**Funding and writing sites.** NEH College Teachers Award 1986; Fulbright Islamic Civilization Program and ARCE/Ministry permissions for 1987; transcription and drafting at IAS Princeton (Geertz; Joan Scott Gender Seminar with Butler, Haraway, Keller, Rapp, etc.); Mellon Fellowship at Penn. Santa Fe Fox seminar shaped introduction issues.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The community is continuous with *Veiled Sentiments*: Awlad ʿAli of Egypt's Western Desert coastal strip, Mariut-area *najiʿ* centered on the Haj (Sagr) and his mother Migdim, senior wife Gateefa, other co-wives, daughters including Kamla and Lawz, and surrounding kin. Documentation moments for this book are primarily **1986–89**, with memory depth back through WWII (Alamein, mine deaths, displacement) and the 1960s land claims.

**Data classes:** tape-recorded conversations and stories; songs and poems (transcribed in appendix with the 1986 dialect system); an elicited essay (Kamla); participant observation of births, weddings, land fights, co-wife conflict, schooling gossip. Abu-Lughod censored some sexually crude wedding songs and accepted host censorship of a rhyme disrespectful of the Egyptian president — ethics of publication are part of the method chapter's self-accounting.

### Chapter-by-chapter site content (1980s documentation)

**Ch. 1 Patrilineality — Migdim.** Maternal uncle shelter after father threatened her with a gun over a cousin engagement; eventual marriage to Jawwad via lateral family maneuver ("bread and salt" / divine share framing); seven living children; solitary births and wartime Alamein displacement ("year the Snaagra were wiped out"); contested son/daughter valuation; widowhood after mine-linked brother-in-law death and Jawwad's illness; sons consulted her on household economy; she opposed Lawz's early cousin marriage and sons' polygyny; **summer 1987** land transfer to a neighbor against her ban, nested in Hamid's third-wife politics; settlement crowding and desert reclamation reshaping mobility; shearing songs and hyena folktales; wedding song placing her at the center of sons' sons.

**Ch. 2 Polygyny — Gateefa.** Qur'an 4:3 epigraph; Sagr teased for polygyny and framing monogamy as "Christian"; cousin betrothal of Gateefa; co-wives Azza (town outsider violating respectability norms; long absences) and Safiyya; Gateefa denied "jealousy," claimed anger at unfairness ("Aren't we all the same?"); co-wife care at birth of her ninth daughter; Ramadan fight; isolation without strong natal male backing; folktales of clever vs idle co-wives and sewn-chastity injustice; intimacy growth after separate house move; wedding songs for husband's son by Safiyya.

**Ch. 3 Reproduction.** Postpartum visits and flour-gift gender asymmetries; midwifery (mother-in-law demos; Dhahab's ~1000 births; God-reward frame); infertility "searching" across healers and doctors; son preference vs Gateefa's defense of daughters; evil eye and school critique of child quality; Sagr on sons, inheritance, and mother–son moral tales.

**Ch. 4 Patrilateral parallel-cousin marriage.** Low within-family brideprice with gold investment; father decides under modesty norms yet Gateefa vetoes; cousin-right as claim *and* face-saving bluff; Sabra still unmarried ~1987, refuses polygynous match, welcomes cousin block; henna songs that both glorify and "damn the uncle's son"; wedding shrinkage under religious propriety; Selima wedding honor-cloth performance (~2-minute public defloration ritual as reported); Sabra: blood cloth as most important moment *and* "wrong with the Arabs."

**Ch. 5 Honor and shame — Kamla.** Letter 30 July 1989 announcing engagement to Engineer Ibrahim Saleem (Aisha's brother); post-graduation essay on change; school path (unregistered visitor years, uncles' opposition, secondary school ~3 km with four Bedouin girls, catcalls and false reputation accusations, Sagr's belief in her); agricultural college track forced by uncles; college later blocked by reputation; Egyptian radio melodramas and national culture vs Bedouin modesty; piety and anti-polygyny "order"; authorial pushback on devaluing Bedouin women's struggle; bridal leave-taking songs.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**Load-bearing passage — writing against culture:**

> "in this book I have sought to 'write against culture' by working against generalization. Telling stories, it has seemed to me, could be a powerful tool for unsettling the culture concept and subverting the process of 'othering' it entails."

Analysis: The method is defined negatively (against generalization, against othering) and positively (stories). The stock "Bongo-Bongo are polygynous" joke names the enemy: ethnographic voice that freezes institutions as ethnic properties. The five chapter titles deliberately *look like* that enemy — then fill the form with particulars that break it.

**Load-bearing passage — tactical humanism:**

> "What may be required is a tactical humanism, made politically useful but also limited in its effects by anthropology's location on the side of power in a world organized unequally along lines of 'cultural' difference."

Analysis: She neither rehabilitates classical humanism nor joins pure experimental posthumanism. The adjective *tactical* does the work: temporary, instrumental, self-limiting. Power location remains the frame; writing reform is reform, not revolution (Fox's "shop floor" problem is cited).

**Load-bearing passage — critical ethnography's audience:**

> "My excuse for fixing and making public these stories is the 'higher good' of critical ethnography working in the service of 'writing against culture.' But critical ethnography, as I have described, is a project for the West. What good does it do my subjects?"

Analysis: The book refuses the comfort that better representation automatically helps the represented. Candid everyday stories may be less welcome than formal portraits or high poetry. This anticipates later "ethnographic reserve" in the 2016 *Veiled Sentiments* afterword.

**Chapter rhetoric.** Each chapter opens with a Qur'anic or Prophetic epigraph — framing Islamic textuality as lived moral language, not exterior "Islam" explanation. Stories nest: Migdim's land fight inside Hamid's third-wife politics; Gateefa's co-wife tales inside Sagr's self-justifications; Kamla's essay inside family schooling wars. Songs and poems function as **political speech** in family conflict (continuity with *Veiled Sentiments*' poetry analysis, but here more narrative-embedded).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book became a standard citation for:

- **[[writing-against-culture]]** as named program (often more cited as the 1991 essay than the 1993 book, though the book is the long enactment);
- **feminist narrative ethnography** of the Middle East that refuses both culturalist stereotype and simple liberationist frame;
- **halfie / positionality** ethnography after *Writing Culture*;
- teaching pair with *Veiled Sentiments* (ideology/poetry vs stories against typification).

It densifies [[feminist-anthropology]]'s empirical Middle East strand and supplies concrete counter-cases to Mediterranean honor-and-shame typology and to kinship-type labels ([[patrilateral-parallel-cousin-marriage]]).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

**Internal.** Abu-Lughod's own "For Whom?" and halfie sections are the primary critique: betrayal risk; Western audience; hosts' preference for other genres; limits of textual politics.

**External positions (as field context, not fully ingested secondary literature).** Possible lines: (1) narrative particularism under-theorizes political economy and state; (2) humanist tactics reimport the subject poststructuralism dismantled; (3) focus on one household network limits generalizability (which she would accept as feature, not bug); (4) later "saving Muslim women" debates reframe public stakes beyond this book's audience design.

**Restudy.** Same community returns appear in *Veiled Sentiments* 2016 afterword (2008, 2015) under ethnographic reserve — not a restudy of WWW's findings per se. No replication design applies (`replication_status: not-applicable`).

## Current Standing

A durable method charter for writing against culture and a major case-book of Awlad ʿAli women's lives in the 1980s. Best read with *Veiled Sentiments*: dual discourse of sentiment (1986) plus anti-typification narrative (1993). Concepts [[writing-against-culture]] and [[tactical-humanism]] remain active vocabulary in reflexive and feminist anthropology.
