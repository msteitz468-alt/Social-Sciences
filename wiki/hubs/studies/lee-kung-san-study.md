# The !Kung San (Lee 1979) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[lee-kung-san-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Documentation moment: Dobe 1963–73, not the ethnographic present.

[[lee-richard-b|Richard B. Lee]]'s *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* is the densest single-author synthesis of the population that became modern anthropology's central foraging reference case. It is both a technical ecological ethnography — work diaries, plant assays, birth intervals, homicide codes — and a theoretical statement about the [[foraging-mode-of-production]] at the hinge between postwar cultural ecology and critical-turn Marxism. This page reconstructs the study as conducted, the numbers that traveled into the [[original-affluent-society]] thesis, and the afterlives that made the Dobe [[juhoansi|!Kung]] the terrain of the [[kalahari-debate]].

## Question and Intellectual Context

*(Attributed — Lee 1979, Preface and Introduction.)* Lee opened against a double image of hunters: as evolutionary "living fossils" at the edge of technological simplicity, and as Hobbesian strugglers for whom life was precarious. He was skeptical that a species so successful before agriculture could have had such a miserable baseline. The 1966 *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* symposium — which Lee co-organized with [[devore-irven|Irven DeVore]] — had already broadcast shorter versions of the Dobe numbers and Sahlins's [[original-affluent-society]] slogan. The 1979 book was the full apparatus: fifteen chapters, five appendices, the complete work of a decade and a multi-investigator team.

Two theoretical programs frame the monograph. First, a **uniformitarian** ecological program: population, space, resources, and energy as measurable variables, with etic description preceding emic interpretation. Second, a **Marxist-inflected** analysis of production relations — who owns tools and land, how surplus (if any) is appropriated, how sharing and leveling reproduce a relatively egalitarian order. Lee insisted these were complementary, not alternatives: material constraints first, then social and ideological factors that complete the explanation. The polemical targets include scarcity textbooks, Service-style rigid band models, hunting-as-master-mechanism evolutionism, and (implicitly) any romantic "harmless people" that erases documented violence.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How hard is foraging work? | Camp / forager-day | Dobe dry-season camp, 1964 diary | Input-output time and calorie census | Jul–Aug 1964 (core); multi-season context |
| What do people eat? | Individual / camp | Dobe area | Plant/prey inventories; mongongo grove maps; food weights | 1963–73 |
| How is space owned and used? | Waterhole / n!ore | Dobe, /Xai/xai, /Du/da | Census of camps; ownership interviews | 1964, 1968, 1973 snapshots |
| How is fertility regulated? | Women of reproductive age | 256 women registry; 92 with intervals | Birth histories; nomadic vs sedentary comparison | 1963–73 |
| How violent are the !Kung? | Regional !Kung network | Dobe, Nyae Nyae, /Du/da | Retrospective homicide coding (22 cases) | Events 1920–1955; interviews 1960s |
| How is life changing? | Regional political economy | Cattle posts, border, stores | Observation; income; policy | 1960s–70s |

Fieldwork began with Lee's 1963 search for a site and settlement at Dobe; intensive work 1963–65; returns 1967–69 (including life at /Xai/xai and contact with /Du/da); 1973 return after the research group had expanded. Methods were deliberately quantitative where possible — numbered notebooks filed by topic, work diaries, body weights — but nested in long-term residence and language learning. Lee is frank about observer effects on fights (presence may restrain) and about the sensitivity of homicide interviewing (first solid case after 14 months). Collaborators (Howell, Yellen, Draper, Shostak, and others) supplied demography, ethnoarchaeology, childhood, and life history that the single author synthesizes.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The Dobe area is not "the Kalahari" in general. It is a specific waterhole complex on the Botswana side of the border, with rainfall highly variable (Lee cites swings on the order of ~240–600 mm), dune–molapo topography, and mongongo groves on dune crests. Permanent water is scarce and politically consequential. The San population Lee counted in the hundreds of residents plus visitors; Bantu pastoralists numbered in the hundreds with thousands of cattle by mid-century settlement. Two living-group types structured the sample: **foraging camps** and **cattle-post client groups**. Collapsing them into a single "Bushman" type is a sampling error Lee himself marks.

Data strengths: repeated censuses (1964/68/73), multi-year fertility windows, plant and prey inventories with nutritional assays (Appendices), and a rare forager homicide series with case narratives. Data limits: OCR and original tables show measurement is rough at the edges (Lee labels some production calculations approximate); homicide dating is often approximate; the 1964 diary is one camp one season (a drought year — Lee argues this biases *against* easy affluence); women's work is better on gathering calories than on continuous childcare load (though Ch.11 models carrying costs explicitly).

## Findings in Detail

### Subsistence and the mongongo

*(Attributed — Lee.)* Of **105** edible wild plant species, a minority supplied most vegetable calories; **[[mongongo]]** (*Ricinodendron rautanenii*) stood alone — up to about half of vegetable intake, with kernels on the order of **640–655 Cal/100 g**. Lee maps ~35 groves and tracks collection, transport (kaross loads), cracking (several nuts per minute), camp distribution, and sharing. The epigraph from /Xashe — planting is unnecessary while mongongos abound — is not a joke but a production claim. Hunting supplied roughly **30–40%** of calories, was the major male preoccupation and ritual focus, and showed steep success differentials (a minority of hunters providing most large game) managed by humility rules and **arrow-ownership** meat rights rather than by the shooter's exclusive claim.

### Work and nutrition (the affluent-society numbers)

*(Attributed — Lee 1979 Ch.9–10; refinements of 1968.)* The adjusted subsistence work figure is about **2.4 days per adult per week** obtaining food in the 1964 diary accounting; caloric output about **2,355 Cal** and **~96 g protein** per person per day against a ~1,975 Cal estimated requirement. Gathering returned more calories per workday than hunting. When manufacture, maintenance, and housework are added, total work rises to roughly **40–45 hours per week** — still not a starvation grind, but far from the "two-hour day" cartoon that popular reception sometimes produced. Seasonal weight loss existed but was modest compared with regional farming populations in Lee's comparisons; denser waterhole populations (/Xai/xai) showed more stress. Lee reads Wilmsen's later larger weight-loss figures as evidence of **cash-economy entanglement**, not pure foraging failure — a move that anticipates [[kalahari-debate]] stakes.

### Production and reproduction

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.11.)* Women's gathering supplied on the order of two-thirds of camp food while mothers carried young children on foraging trips — Lee estimates thousands of km of carrying over a child's dependency. Mean birth intervals: about **44 months** for more nomadic mothers vs **36 months** for more sedentary ones in the 1963–73 registry; population-wide intervals shortened across the decade. Lee argues long vigorous lactation (not systematic infanticide) is the main proximate check, and that sedentarization (milk, grains, reduced mobility) shortens amenorrhea — a model for Neolithic fertility rise. He records an "emotional economy" of high parental investment under long spacing, and sharp distress when spacing collapses.

### Space, ownership, leadership

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.12.)* **[[nore|n!ore]]** = named locality around a waterhole (~300–600 km² order of magnitude) with core owners (*k"awsi*). Boundaries are vague by design; access is multiplex (owners, kin, visitors). Land disputes rarely turn violent. Leadership is anti-authoritarian: "each one of us is headman over himself." Spatial organization follows a **concentration–dispersion dialectic** driven by water, food, trade, initiation, and conflict avoidance — not Service patrilocal territories. Pre-contact flexibility is asserted against models that treat fluidity as pure contact degradation.

### Conflict and violence

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.13.)* Against *The Harmless People* and against a pure fear-of-fighting ethnography, Lee documents **81** violence cases across levels (talk / unarmed fight / weapons), **34** unarmed fights during fieldwork years, and **22 homicides** (1920–1955 era series) plus woundings. All killers male; weapons are hunting tools (especially poisoned arrows). Most killings are crimes of passion in public melees; victims are often not principals in the prior argument; **15/22** occur in feud chains; four cases look like collective **executions** of multi-killers. Rate ~**29.3 per 100,000 person-years** by Lee's calculation — high versus many African court series and US domestic rates, but Lee argues foragers still look "harmless" once state warfare is in the comparison. Fission, elder restraint, quiver-hanging peacemaking, intermarriage after feud, and later Tswana courts structure resolution. Fear of fighting, Lee argues, keeps groups smaller and more mobile than food alone requires — conflict as ecological spacing without exclusive territory.

### Change in the 1960s–70s

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.14.)* Donkeys, iron tools, and stores refine foraging before they replace it; **mafisa** cattle clientage and craft sales pull people into cash and hierarchical labor; farming attempts collide with sharing ethics; schooling and Tribal Grazing Land policy threaten n!ore under leasehold enclosure logic; South African border security recruits San trackers and militarizes the frontier. Lee does not freeze 1964 as eternity — the book ends with transformation under way.

Concrete change markers *(attributed)*: by 1968, 58 of 151 men claimed donkeys (~120 San-owned in the area); craft sales to Botswanacraft pulled hundreds of hours from subsistence into cash production; *mafisa* placed San herders in hierarchical dependence on Herero/Tswana cattle owners while still living near wild foods; partial gardens and small herds created contradictions when relatives demanded shares under foraging norms. Lee treats Tribal Grazing Lands Policy as enclosure logic that can convert communal/n!ore access into leasehold real estate San lack capital and schooling to defend. Border fence and South African recruitment of trackers place /Du/da and border San inside a security apparatus — knowledge that later debates about "pristine foragers" must not erase.

### Population, technology, and carrying (supporting apparatus)

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.3, 5, App. E.)* Resident San across mid-1960s–early 1970s censuses run roughly in the high 300s to mid-400s, with visitors and /Du/da expanding a wider frame; ~340 Bantu (1968) with growing herds. Foraging camps average ~19; client groups smaller. Exclusive foraging into the early twentieth century, permanent black settlement from mid-1920s, then cattle/*mafisa*/Tswana courts. Technology: digging sticks, kaross carrying, poison arrows (6–24 h kill), iron from trade/fence wire. Lee elevates **carrying devices** as evolutionarily under-theorized: plant-majority economies require bulk transport. Storage is minimal because sharing and mobility make large stores costly — not because people lack inventiveness. Appendix E's "hand-to-mouth" essay is a origins-of-economy claim, not a technical leftover.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Lee writes as a quantitative ethnographer who still tells case stories (meat insults, divorce arguments, arrow-fight narratives). The rhetoric repeatedly converts moralized images ("precarious," "harmless," "primitive communism") into measurable claims, then returns to political voice in Ch.14–15. Key persuasive devices:

1. **Drought-year diary** — if they manage in a hard year, the scarcity thesis is weaker.
2. **Mongongo as staple** — plants, not charismatic megafauna, carry the economy.
3. **Insulting the meat** — a memorable micro-institution standing for the whole leveling system.
4. **Homicide tables** — refuse romanticization without endorsing Hobbes.
5. **Dual method manifesto** — uniformitarian ecology + Marxist MOP as a package other forager ethnographies often split.

Load-bearing quote (epigraph): "Why should we plant, when there are so many mongongos in the world?" — /Xashe of Mahopa. Lee uses it to reverse the agricultural-progress narrative.

### The Christmas ox (ethnographer as leveled subject)

*(Attributed — Lee 1979 Ch.1; related essay Lee 1969b.)* Early in fieldwork Lee bought a large ox for a Christmas feast, expecting gratitude. The camp responded by **insulting the meat** — declaring the animal thin, worthless, barely edible — until Lee realized he was being subjected to the same humility device used on successful hunters. The episode is pedagogically famous because it turns the ethnographer into data: leveling is not a story about "them" but a practice that polices *any* attempt to convert provisioning into status, including the anthropologist's. It also shows affection and ridicule intertwined — the insult is social inclusion under egalitarian rules, not simple ingratitude.

### Verbatim political ethos

*(Attributed — Lee.)* On headmanship: "Of course we have headmen … each one of us is headman over himself." On poison arrows in fights: an informant explained poisoned (not clean) arrows because "our hearts are hot and we really want to kill somebody with them." On closing the homicide interviews, Old Kashe, asked how many men he had shot: "I never shot anyone… I always missed." Lee places these lines carefully — egalitarianism is real as ideology and practice; lethality is also real; comedy and terror sit in the same toolkit.

### Work diary as designed (not as cartooned)

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.9.)* The 1964 diary recorded a Dobe camp over four weeks in a **drought year**. Lee later recalculated truck-assisted mongongo trips out of the work column. "Work" for subsistence days is food-getting; Table 9.12 then adds manufacture/maintenance and housework to produce the ~42-hour total. Childcare is mostly maternal and treated as a separate load that offsets apparent male surplus in pure subsistence days. Anyone who cites only "two hours a day" without the 1979 full-week table is citing the popularization, not the monograph. Anyone who cites only the full week without the subsistence surplus and plant majority is underplaying Lee's challenge to scarcity.

### Birth-spacing as production–reproduction link

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.11.)* The model is mechanistic before it is cultural: mothers carry children under four on gathering trips; short intervals multiply kg-km of carrying until the body and the schedule break. Registry evidence: nomadic mean interval ~44 months, sedentary ~36; some young sedentary women at !Goshe with ~20–23 month intervals. Lee argues lactational amenorrhea under vigorous on-demand nursing is the main proximate check, not systematic infanticide (contra some Birdsell/Binford-era models). Sedentarization with milk and grains is hypothesized to reduce nursing intensity and raise fertility — a micro-model for Neolithic demographic change. The "emotional economy" section records misery when spacing collapses: production and affect are one system.

### Homicide without Hobbes, without Thomas

*(Attributed — Lee Ch.13.)* The chapter is structured as a double refusal. Against Elizabeth Marshall Thomas's *Harmless People* title and against pure fear-of-fighting ethnography (Lorna Marshall's careful work is quoted then extended), Lee tables 22 killings. Against Hobbesian war-of-all-against-all, he shows most violence is intra-network passion, not stranger war; land fights are rare; fission and later Tswana courts contain escalation; foragers still look better than industrial states once warfare body counts enter the comparison. The collective killing of multi-killer /Twi "like a porcupine" is Lee's most striking image of **embryonic jural capacity** without offices — consensus lethal sanction in a non-hierarchical society. Whether one reads that as proto-state or as the outer edge of leveling is a theoretical choice Lee flags rather than settles.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **[[original-affluent-society]]** — Sahlins's thesis rode Lee's (and McCarthy & McArthur's) numbers; 1979 densified and partly disciplined the cartoon version by adding housework hours and seasonal stress.
- **Hunter-gatherer studies** — Dobe became the default textbook forager; comparative method papers cite Lee as anchor.
- **Gender and gathering** — women's caloric dominance fed [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate]].
- **Demography** — birth-spacing/lactation model entered Neolithic demographic-transition discussions.
- **Political anthropology** — leveling, headlessness, and embryonic "execution" capacity entered egalitarian-society debates ([[egalitarian-society]], [[leveling-mechanisms]]).
- **Archaeology** — Yellen's ethnoarchaeology and Lee's land-use descriptions shaped middle-range models; also cautioned ethnographic analogy.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Low subsistence work effort | 1964 camp diary | Contested (scope of "work"; season; camp choice) |
| Caloric adequacy | I/O weights | Partially supported; seasonal and regional variation stressed |
| Mongongo staple reliability | Grove maps + diet | Robust for Dobe; not universal for foragers |
| Long birth spacing under mobility | 1963–73 registry | Influential; mechanisms debated (fatness vs nursing) |
| Fluid non-territorial bands | Camp censuses | Strongly supported vs rigid Service model |
| Relative isolation of foraging MOP | History Ch.3, 14 | Core of [[kalahari-debate]]; open |
| Low structural violence / no war | Homicide series | Rate high; war absent; comparative framing contested |
| Leveling via meat insult | Ethnographic cases | Widely cited; sometimes over-generalized |

**Revisionist challenge (Wilmsen and others):** Dobe/CaeCae San as long-integrated rural poor, not pristine foragers; iron, trade, and herding deep in time; "foraging" of the mid-20th c. as marginalization. Primary monograph: *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* (1989) — [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]] / [[hubs/studies/wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]]. Lee (and Solway & Lee 1990) reply that majority foraging, drought resilience, and n!ore systems show substantial autonomy even under contact. **This hub does not adjudicate** — see [[kalahari-debate]].

**Affluence debate:** Marshall's thinness/anxiety reports; inclusion of food processing and childcare; whether leisure equals satisfaction. Lee's own 1979 full-work-week figures already complicate the pure leisure myth.

## Current Standing

*The !Kung San* remains required reading for hunter-gatherer ecology and for any serious use of the !Kung as a comparative case. Best practice is to cite it with fieldwork dates, to separate Dobe from Nyae Nyae and from "the San," and to pair it with revisionist historiography and with later Ju/'hoan ethnography (including Lee's own textbook editions). The Studies Hub treats it as a **reference research act**: methodologically exemplary in quantification, theoretically programmatic, and historically entangled with the politics of representing foraging peoples.

### How to cite the numbers without laundering them

| Claim you want | Cite | Do not do |
|---|---|---|
| Modest food-getting labor | Lee 1979 ~2.4 days/week (1964 diary, recalculated) | Cite "2-hour day" without table 9.12 full week |
| Caloric adequacy | ~2,355 Cal + ~96 g protein/person/day | Promote to all foragers / all seasons |
| Plant majority | ~60–70% calories; mongongo up to ~½ vegetable | "Women do all the work / men play" cartoon |
| Full labor load | ~42 h/week incl. tools+housework | Equate with industrial full-time without task structure |
| Birth spacing | ~44 mo nomadic vs ~36 sedentary (1963–73) | Universal "forager fertility" without mobility split |
| Violence | 22 killings series; ~29/100k Lee calc | "Harmless" or "fierce" without the comparative caveats |
| Isolation | majority foraging camps + n!ore into 1960s | Ignore Ch.3/14 contact; ignore dual camp/client structure |

### Counterfactual significance

Without Lee's Dobe program, *Man the Hunter* still happens (Woodburn, Sahlins, Marshall materials exist), but the **quantitative charter** of modern hunter-gatherer economics is thinner. Without the 1979 full apparatus, the 1968 chapter remains a symposium highlight rather than a falsifiable, multi-chapter research act. Without the homicide chapter, textbook egalitarianism stays closer to *Harmless People*. Without Ch.14, isolationist readings of Lee become straw men for revisionists. The study is therefore both the best friend and the best internal critic of the affluent-forager myth it helped create.

What survives most cleanly into current work, even where the isolationist package is contested: (1) plant-majority foraging as the default tropical/temperate pattern; (2) time-allocation as a mandatory method class; (3) leveling as a research problem rather than a slogan; (4) mobility–fertility coupling; (5) the demand that any Pleistocene analogy state its bridging argument. What remains most contested: whether Dobe 1964 is a window onto deep forager structure or a historically specific rural underclass moment. The wiki holds both as positions.

### Reflexivity of the research act

Lee is unusually explicit about researcher impact: trucks, stores, meat-buying, the Christmas ox, expanded research teams, and the 1973 group decision that responsibility "went beyond simply publishing." Ch.14's militarization section places Dobe on a border becoming a counterinsurgency frontier — knowledge production under apartheid-adjacent security politics, not in a vacuum. The study is therefore evidence for forager ecology **and** a document of late-colonial / post-Independence Botswana–Namibia border history. Treating only the ecology half is a reading choice, not the book's structure.
