# The Golden Bough (Frazer) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[frazer-golden-bough-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: summary links here by full path `[[hubs/studies/frazer-golden-bough-study|…]]`; this page's `study_page` points back to `[[frazer-golden-bough-study]]`. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Reconstruct the study *as conducted*, not as advertised: armchair library synthesis, second-hand sources, evolutionary ordering, and uncontrolled case dependence (Galton's problem).

*The Golden Bough* is the emblematic research act of late Victorian armchair anthropology — a book that opens with a single classical riddle (the murderous priesthood of Diana at Nemi) and answers it by assembling a worldwide comparative machine for magic, sacred kingship, vegetation ritual, and the evolution of human thought. Its 1890 first edition already announced a program; the third edition (twelve volumes, 1911–15) and Frazer's own 1922 abridgement (the text underlying this ingest: [[frazer-golden-bough-1922]]) turned that program into a near-encyclopedic monument. The work never rested on participant observation. Its "field" was the Cambridge library; its "informants" were travellers, missionaries, classicists, and folklorists; its ordering principle was unilinear developmental sequence licensed by [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] and [[survivals]]. As a study it founded a generation's comparative religion, stocked literary modernism with dying-god imagery, fixed textbook accounts of [[magic]] for decades — and then became the emblematic target of the fieldwork and particularist revolts that remade [[anthropology]]. This page reconstructs how the study was actually built, what its load-bearing claims say in Frazer's own words, and how those claims have fared.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Frazer framed the entire enterprise as two questions about a single Italian grove. At Lake Nemi in the Alban hills stood the sanctuary of Diana Nemorensis; within it, the priesthood of the *Rex Nemorensis* (King of the Wood) passed only by the sword: a candidate — classically a runaway slave who had first broken a branch from a sacred tree — had to slay the sitting priest and hold the office until himself slain. Ancient opinion identified the fateful branch with Virgil's Golden Bough. Frazer stated the design problem without ambiguity:

> "The questions which we have set ourselves to answer are mainly two: first, why had Diana's priest at Nemi, the King of the Wood, to slay his predecessor? second, why before doing so had he to pluck the branch of a certain tree which the public opinion of the ancients identified with Virgil's Golden Bough?"

The object of the book, he wrote, was "to offer a fairly probable explanation of the priesthood of Nemi" — probability, not demonstration, because no direct record of the rite's origin survived. Foundation myths of Orestes and Hippolytus/Virbius he treated as aetiological, not historical: "stories told to account for Diana's worship at Nemi are unhistorical… myths which are made up to explain the origin of a religious ritual." The true origin, he held, belonged to a "barbarous age" without classical parallel; explanation therefore required comparison with similar customs elsewhere. The methodological charter is explicit:

> "For recent researches into the early history of man have revealed the essential similarity with which, under many superficial differences, the human mind has elaborated its first crude philosophy of life."

That sentence places the book inside late Victorian [[social-evolutionism]] and the British [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion|intellectualist]] program associated above all with [[tylor-edward-burnett|E. B. Tylor]]. Frazer worked as a classicist at Trinity College, Cambridge, who extended Tylor's [[animism]], [[survivals]], and comparative reconstruction of "primitive philosophy" into a vast ritual taxonomy. He drew heavily on Wilhelm Mannhardt's vegetation-spirit research for the European agricultural and fire-festival material that would underwrite the dying-god and corn-spirit chapters. Contemporaries in the evolutionist comparative tradition included [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]], Herbert Spencer, John Lubbock, and J. F. McLennan; the shared assumptions were psychic unity, unilinear stages, and the legitimacy of reconstructing earlier mental states from present "savage" practice and European peasant custom. Against purely theological or purely allegorical readings of myth, Frazer offered a systematic natural history of error — magic as false science, religion as propitiation of personified powers, both as stages on a road toward science. The Nemi riddle is thus both genuine classical puzzle and rhetorical pretext: the grove supplies a literary frame (opening tableau, closing "Farewell to Nemi") within which the real product is a general theory of magic, kingship, and vegetation ritual.

Disciplinary moment matters. The first edition (1890) appears at the end of the Founding Era / turn into the Classical Era of the disciplines — after Tylor's *Primitive Culture* (1871) and Morgan's evolutionary syntheses, before Malinowski's Trobriand fieldwork and Boas's institutional consolidation of particularism. Frazer never joined the fieldwork revolution; the book grew by accretion of library materials through the third edition and the 1922 abridgement even as anthropology was already abandoning the method that made the book possible. The study is therefore both a summit of armchair comparison and a document of its impending obsolescence.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

What the book advertises is a probable solution to Nemi by meeting "comparative conditions." What it actually conducts is a **trait-centred, second-hand, evolutionarily ordered worldwide catalogue** in which the unit of analysis is the *custom, rite, myth, or priesthood type*, never a bounded society studied in context.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why must the King of the Wood slay his predecessor? | Rite / priesthood type | Classical Nemi reports + worldwide regicide and temporary-king cases | Comparative juxtaposition; evolutionary sequence of mitigation | Classical antiquity + 19th-c. ethnography |
| Why must he first pluck the Golden Bough? | Ritual object / myth motif | Virgil, Pliny (Druid mistletoe), Balder myth, European fire-festivals, external-soul tales | Identification by analogy; external-soul logic | Indo-European myth + folklore "survivals" |
| What are the principles of magic? | Thought-principle / charm type | Worldwide image-magic, contagion, weather magic | Analytic reduction to association of ideas | Transhistorical "savage" type |
| How does thought evolve? | Stage of mind (magic / religion / science) | Global comparative sample ordered developmentally | Unilinear staging via [[survivals]] | Evolutionary time (conjectural) |
| What is the dying vegetation god? | Deity / corn-spirit type | Adonis, Attis, Osiris, Dionysus, Demeter; European corn-mothers; fire-festival effigies | Typological amalgamation under vegetation-spirit | Mediterranean antiquity + European peasantry |

**No fieldwork.** Frazer did not visit Nemi as an excavator of ritual practice, interview living practitioners of the customs he catalogued, or control for how missionaries, colonial officers, and travellers produced the reports he used. The "data" arrive pre-filtered through colonial and classical knowledge circuits. Source quality varies from relatively careful early ethnology (e.g. C. G. Seligman's Shilluk materials, which Frazer himself flags as carefully investigated) to travellers' anecdotes, classical scholia, and antiquarian compilations. The book rarely weights sources by reliability; volume and typological fit do most of the work.

**[[comparative-method]] as conducted.** Comparison here means *juxtaposition under a pre-established type*. A Cambodian mystic king, a Shilluk *reth*, a Calicut Samorin, a Babylonian Sacaea mock king, and the Nemi priest are lined up as instances of the same institution (killing or replacing the divine king). European Whitsuntide leaf-men and "Carrying out Death" effigies are lined up as [[survivals]] of the same logic. The method's productive power — it can generate unexpected parallels — is inseparable from its failure modes: decontextualization, scheme-driven selection, and the elevation of analogy to identity.

**Evolutionary ordering is built into the arrangement of evidence.** Frazer does not sample and then discover stages; he arranges materials so that "pure" magic precedes religion-alloyed magic, full regicide precedes temporary kings and animal substitutes, hunting/pastoral animal gods precede agricultural corn-gods, and actual human burning precedes pretence. Mitigation sequences (from killing the king, to killing a substitute, to mock execution, to carnival play) are read as historical development preserved as survivals. That is conjectural history, not independently dated sequence.

**Galton's problem is not controlled.** Cases drawn from historically related Indo-European ritual spheres, Christianized European peasantry sharing ecclesiastical calendars, and colonial-era Africa under overlapping reportage traditions are treated as independent witnesses to universal mental stages. Diffusion, shared descent, and common literary sources (Caesar and Posidonius on Celtic wicker sacrifices; classical school traditions on Adonis) can generate the "same" custom without parallel invention. Frazer's charter invocation of psychic unity licenses treating similarity as evidence of shared stage rather than contact. The comparison page standard of this wiki would require a `galton_note`; Frazer's note, reconstructed, is effectively: *not controlled; similarity of mind is the explanation.*

**What the methods discussion omits.** There is no sampling frame, no inter-coder reliability, no negative cases systematically sought, no separation of etic type from emic category, and no admission that the Nemi solution is underdetermined by the classical evidence alone — the worldwide machine is what makes the solution feel inevitable. When Frazer revises himself (e.g. preferring the purificatory over the solar theory of fire-festivals in later editions; preferring witch-victims over Mannhardt's vegetation-spirit reading of Celtic wicker burnings), the revisions show intellectual honesty but also that the same comparative pile can support rival readings. That underdetermination is a design feature of trait catalogues, not a late discovery.

## The Site / Sample / Data

There is no ethnographic site. There is a **literary type-site** (Nemi) and a **global paper sample**.

**Nemi as frame, not field.** Classical notices of the *Rex Nemorensis*, Diana of the Wood, Egeria, Virbius/Hippolytus, Caligula's interference with a long-tenured priest, and a Greek traveller under the Antonines supply the opening tableau. Frazer does not re-excavate the sanctuary or re-edit the sources as a historian of Roman religion first; he uses Nemi as the puzzle that authorizes the comparative voyage. When he returns in "Farewell to Nemi," the grove has become an elegiac closing device: "The temple of the sylvan goddess, indeed, has vanished and the King of the Wood no longer stands sentinel over the Golden Bough. But Nemi's woods are still green… Le roi est mort, vive le roi! Ave Maria!"

**Evidence classes mixed without hierarchy.** The sample blends:

1. **Classical textual/archival** material (Virgil, Servius, Pliny, Greek and Latin historians, mythographers) — strong on names and literary tradition, weak as direct report of practice.
2. **Second-hand ethnographic** reports from Africa, Asia, the Americas, Oceania, and Australia — depth varies wildly; many are single-anecdote.
3. **European folklore and peasant custom** (fire-festivals, carnival deaths, corn-mothers, need-fire) treated as [[survivals]] of pre-Christian rite — a Mannhardtian archive Frazer expands but does not independently verify in the field.
4. **Myth as ritual script** — Balder, Adonis, Attis, Osiris read as the "book of the words" of magical ceremonies: "A myth is never so graphic and precise in its details as when it is, so to speak, the book of the words which are spoken and acted by the performers of the sacred rite."

**Sample construction is scheme-driven.** Materials enter because they illustrate sympathetic magic, divine kingship, temporary kings, scapegoats, external souls, or vegetation death-and-revival. Counter-examples that would break the type (kingships without fertility bond; magic systems not reducible to association of ideas; dying gods that are not vegetation) are under-weighted. Period ethnonyms and the analytic ranking "savage / barbarous / civilized" are production-context artefacts of colonial knowledge, not neutral descriptors — they structure who counts as evidence of early mind.

**Thin direct evidence, openly admitted in places.** On soul-transmission after regicide Frazer wrote: "the soul of the slain divinity is transmitted to his successor. Of this transmission I have no direct proof except in the case of the Shilluk." That candour is important: the theoretical spine often rests on a few better-documented cases (Shilluk via Seligman; Pliny on Druids; the classical Nemi notices) surrounded by analogical bulk. The bulk creates the impression of proof by saturation.

**Edition as data expansion.** 1890 (2 vols.) → 1900 (3 vols.) → 1911–15 (12 vols.) → 1922 abridgement means the "study" is a moving target. The abridgement preserves the argumentative arc and omits much apparatus; claims about Frazer's final preferences (purificatory fire theory; witch reading of Celtic victims; lightning theory of oak-sky association) must be dated to the later editions reflected in 1922. This ingest's reliability note on [[frazer-golden-bough-1922]] records that condition.

**Sympathetic magic (Ch. 3–4).** Frazer's most durable analytic move is the reduction of magical practice to two principles:

> "IF we analyse the principles of thought on which magic is based, they will probably be found to resolve themselves into two: first, that like produces like, or that an effect resembles its cause; and, second, that things which have once been in contact with each other continue to act on each other at a distance after the physical contact has been severed."

Homoeopathic and contagious branches are comprehended as [[sympathetic-magic]] because "both assume that things act on each other at a distance through a secret sympathy." The intellectualist sting follows: "magic is a spurious system of natural law as well as a fallacious guide of conduct; it is a false science as well as an abortive art." Homoeopathic magic rests on association by similarity; contagious on association by contiguity; "Legitimately applied they yield science; illegitimately applied they yield magic, the bastard sister of science." The pure magician, on this account, assumes order without personified agents — and therefore stands closer to the scientist than to the priest:

> "Wherever sympathetic magic occurs in its pure unadulterated form, it assumes that in nature one event follows another necessarily and invariably without the intervention of any spiritual or personal agency. Thus its fundamental conception is identical with that of modern science."

**Magic, religion, science.** Religion Frazer defines as "a propitiation or conciliation of powers superior to man which are believed to direct and control the course of nature and of human life." That definition implies an elastic, personal cosmos — "directly opposed to the principles of magic as well as of science." In the closing "Farewell to Nemi" he promotes the triad into a history of higher thought:

> "the movement of the higher thought, so far as we can trace it, has on the whole been from magic through religion to science."

Magic trusts human manipulation of an imagined order; when that order is recognized as imaginary, the acuter minds "throw [themselves] humbly on the mercy of certain great invisible beings"; when observation reveals "rigid uniformity," science displaces religion as explanation while recovering magic's faith in regularity — now grounded in phenomena, not "false analogy" of ideas:

> "whereas the order on which magic reckons is merely an extension, by false analogy, of the order in which ideas present themselves to our minds, the order laid down by science is derived from patient and exact observation of the phenomena themselves."

He softens triumphalism: magic, religion, and science are all "theories of thought," and science too may be superseded — "of registering the shadows on the screen - of which we in this generation can form no idea." The three-threaded web (black magic, red religion, white science) images modern thought as mixed fabric whose future colour is unknown.

**The killing of the divine king (Ch. 24–28).** The theoretical core of the Nemi answer is stated as savage reasoning reconstructed by Frazer:

> "The man-god must be killed as soon as he shows symptoms that his powers are beginning to fail, and his soul must be transferred to a vigorous successor before it has been seriously impaired by the threatened decay."

Natural death, on this reconstruction, means the soul has fled or been captured; even catching a disease-weakened soul would plant weakness in the land; killing at full vigour "secure[s] that the world should not fall into decay with the decay of the man-god." The Shilluk supply Frazer's best ethnographic exhibit (via Seligman): the king as reincarnation of Nyakang must not become ill or senile "lest with his diminishing vigour the cattle should sicken… the crops should rot… and man… die in ever-increasing numbers"; sexual failure with many wives is a fatal symptom; old custom was death by immurement (later strangling); royal sons may challenge by night combat. Frazer's bridge to Nemi is explicit: "On the whole the theory and practice of the divine kings of the Shilluk correspond very nearly to the theory and practice of the priests of Nemi, the Kings of the Wood, if my view of the latter is correct." Fixed-term reigns (Quilacare self-dismemberment; Calicut twelve-year combat), temporary and mock kings (Sacaea Zoganes; Siamese ploughing kings), and king's-son sacrifice are arranged as successive softenings of the original rule. Returning to Nemi after the comparative tour, Frazer concludes the King of the Wood was a vegetation incarnation who "had to be killed in order that the divine spirit, incarnate in him, might be transferred in its integrity to his successor," the combat rule testing that strength was still unimpaired.

**Dying gods, corn-spirits, scapegoats.** The middle and later chapters amalgamate Mediterranean dying gods and European agricultural custom under the [[vegetation-spirit]] / [[dying-and-rising-god]] type, with [[scapegoat]] expulsion and "eating the god" as related operations of transferring or assimilating divine life and evil. European spring mummers and "Carrying out Death" are read as survivals: "In most of the personages who are thus slain in mimicry it is impossible not to recognise representatives of the tree-spirit or spirit of vegetation." The programmatic evolutionary claim is that killing the god and believing in his resurrection "originated, or at least existed, in the hunting and pastoral stage… when the slain god was an animal, and… survived into the agricultural stage."

**Balder, mistletoe, and the Golden Bough (Ch. 61–68).** The second Nemi question is answered by identifying the Golden Bough with mistletoe and the king with an oak-spirit whose external life the mistletoe holds. Virgil "definitely describes the Golden Bough as growing on a holm-oak, and compares it with the mistletoe. The inference is almost inevitable that the Golden Bough was nothing but the mistletoe seen through the haze of poetry or of popular superstition." Balder's myth and European fire-festivals are "two broken and dissevered halves of an original whole"; "here, as so often, myth stood to magic in the relation of theory to practice." The load-bearing botanical-theological move:

> "The mistletoe was viewed as the seat of life of the oak, and so long as it was uninjured nothing could kill or even wound the oak…. The pulling of the mistletoe was thus at once the signal and the cause of his death."

Hence the Nemi resolution:

> "As an oak-spirit, his life or death was in the mistletoe on the oak, and so long as the mistletoe remained intact, he, like Balder, could not die. To slay him, therefore, it was necessary to break the mistletoe, and probably, as in the case of Balder, to throw it at him."

[[external-soul]] generalizes the logic: life stowed outside the body for safety; injury to the deposit kills the person; mistletoe "neither on earth nor in heaven" is a safe deposit. Climactic speculation can push further — the priest as Jupiter come down in the lightning to dwell in the thunder-besom, the Golden Bough, on Nemi's oak — but even Frazer marks the limits of occupying "primitive man's standpoint."

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book persuaded as literature and as science-effect, not only as argument. Several load-bearing rhetorical strategies deserve separate analysis.

**1. The riddle frame.** Opening *in medias res* at Nemi with murder, a sacred tree, and Virgilian glamour converts a comparative treatise into a detective story. Every subsequent chapter can be sold as clue-gathering. The closing return to the grove, Angelus bells, and "Le roi est mort, vive le roi!" recycles the divine-king theme as aesthetic closure. Literary modernism did not misread the book in seizing its imagery; the imagery was load-bearing persuasion.

**2. Definition before history.** Chapters 3–4 establish what magic and religion *are* by analytic definition and associationist psychology, then read the world catalogue as illustrations. Once magic is "false science," every charm is already explained in principle; ethnography fills cells in a typology. The cost is circularity: materials that do not fit intellectualist coercion-vs-propitiation are alloyed, exceptional, or late.

**3. Saturation by instance.** Frazer's sentences often march through continents in a single paragraph (image-magic "in ancient India, Babylon, Egypt, Greece, Rome… Australia, Africa, Scotland"). The rhetorical effect is inevitability: *so many* cases cannot be coincidence. Methodologically this is proof by enumeration without base rates, source critique, or independence tests — Galton's problem performed as style.

**4. "The savage" as unified subject.** Reconstructed interiority — what "the savage" thinks about soul-flight, decaying man-gods, or mistletoe — lets Frazer state mechanisms with novelistic clarity. Evans-Pritchard's later gibe that intellectualists argue "If I were a horse" names this move: the anthropologist's guess at primitive philosophy stands in for actors' categories. Frazer sometimes hedges ("to the savage, obvious enough") but the hedge still universalizes.

**5. Survival as time machine.** Present peasant custom is repeatedly read as degraded memory of full rite (pretence of burning → ancient actual burning; carnival death → real regicide). That licenses deep reconstruction from shallow ethnography. It also flattens Christian, seigneurial, and local histories into a single pre-Christian layer.

**6. Self-revision as ethos.** Preferring purificatory over solar theories of fire, or witches over vegetation-victims in Celtic wicker fires, shows Frazer arguing with his earlier editions in public. The ethos is scientific honesty; the structural lesson is that the comparative archive underdetermines theory — a point later critics would turn against the whole enterprise.

**7. Probable explanation, absolute tone.** Officially the book offers "fairly probable" solutions; page by page, "we may infer," "it is natural to conjecture," and "almost inevitable" do the work of certainty. The Nemi solution's chain (oak-spirit → mistletoe as external life → Balder parallel → Golden Bough identification → combat rule as strength test) is only as strong as its weakest analogy, but the chain is presented as cumulative proof.

**8. The melancholy of error.** "Farewell to Nemi" asks whether a "lesson… of hope" can be drawn from "the melancholy record of human error and folly." Progress toward science is affirmed, then shadowed by cosmic heat-death and the provisional status of all theories. That elegiac humanism made the book exportable beyond anthropology — a wisdom book about mind — while insulating evolutionary ranking in high style.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

**Within anthropology and comparative religion.** For a generation *The Golden Bough* organized research and teaching on magic, taboo, kingship, and seasonal ritual. The vocabulary of [[sympathetic-magic]], [[divine-king]], [[scapegoat]], [[external-soul]], and dying vegetation gods entered textbooks and handbooks. The work supports (as historical affiliation) [[social-evolutionism]] and the [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]]; it became, equally, the object against which functionalists and particularists defined themselves. Malinowski's fieldwork program and anxiety-management reading of magic; Radcliffe-Brown's structural-functional presentism; Boasian historical particularism and the critique of unilinear stages ([[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]); Durkheimian insistence that religion is social, not individual philosophy (*Elementary Forms*) — all treat Frazer as predecessor, foil, or both.

**Myth-ritual and the dying god.** The claim that myth is the script of magical rite fed the myth-and-ritual school (and popular comparative religion). The [[dying-and-rising-god]] type shaped early twentieth-century readings of Mediterranean and Christian materials; later specialized scholarship (notably positions associated with Jonathan Z. Smith and others — pending full ingest in this wiki) dismantled the single type as a modern scholarly construct imposed on disparate cults.

**Literary and cultural afterlife.** Eliot, Yeats, and a wide modernist and popular readership absorbed Nemi, the dying god, and the vegetation cycle as imaginative resources. That afterlife is not a side-effect; it tracks the book's rhetorical design. Anthropology's rejection of Frazer's method coexisted for decades with literary and general-educated reverence for the book — a split reception that Stocking and Ackerman (pending full ingest) help historicize.

**Institutional position.** Frazer as Cambridge classicist-anthropologist without a fieldwork school meant influence traveled through print, lectures, and comparative religion curricula more than through a cadre of students trained in his method. trained_under (intellectual debt): Tylor. trained: no major ethnographic lineage comparable to Boas or Malinowski. The genealogy network of this wiki therefore shows Frazer as a node of *conceptual transmission and methodological reaction*, not of field-school descent.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. This is not a modern empirical study subject to statistical replication. Its comparative reconstructions have been continuously reanalyzed; as a *research program* the unilinear intellectualist package is abandoned, while several labels and problem-areas remain live under other theories. Major lines of critique (positions, not wiki-voice verdicts):

- **Methodological particularism (Boas and after).** Similar traits do not license parallel evolutionary stages; each culture's trait complex has a historical trajectory. Trait-lifting erases context that would change meaning. Galton's problem and diffusion were not controlled. This is the central indictment in the [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

- **Source quality and colonial production.** Second-hand missionary and administrative reports encode the reporters' categories; "savage philosophy" often mirrors the library philosopher. Period race language and civilizational ranking are not removable ornaments — they structure the sample.

- **"If I were a horse" intellectualism (Evans-Pritchard and others).** Reconstructing magic as failed physics misses the internal rationality, social embedding, and pragmatic contexts of belief (classic counter-exhibit: Azande witchcraft, oracles, and magic — pending fuller ingest). Malinowski's functional reading (magic as anxiety-handling in uncertain practical enterprises) relocates explanation from origin-in-error to present use.

- **Sociological displacement (Durkheimian and later).** If religion's truth is social, Frazer's individual "primitive philosopher" is the wrong unit. Sacred kingship and seasonal rite become facts of collective representation and classification, not mistaken natural science.

- **Dying-and-rising-god dismantled.** Closer case analysis finds that the unified vegetation-dying-rising type does not survive as a single cross-cultural deity class; "dying and rising gods" as Frazer amalgamated them are argued (by Smith and others) to be a product of comparative method rather than a discovery of it. Individual cults (Osiris, Adonis, etc.) continue to be studied without the master type.

- **Nemi solution as underdetermined chain.** The classical evidence does not independently require oak-spirit + mistletoe + external-soul + Balder parallel. Each link is analogical; the whole is a possible story, not a demonstrated origin. Roman-religion specialists have long preferred more local, historical, and institutional accounts of the Arician cult.

- **Fire-festival and folklore over-reading.** Human-sacrifice inferences from pretence, lots, and effigies are often stronger than the evidence; Christian liturgy, medieval regulation, and regional variation complicate "pre-Christian survival" as a default.

- **What was never "replicated."** No later anthropology has re-run Frazer's worldwide trait catalogue under the same evolutionary rules and confirmed the magic–religion–science sequence or the universal kill-the-king law. Reanalyses dissolve the program into specialized histories (of Roman Diana, of Shilluk kingship, of European calendar custom) that do not reassemble into Frazer's machine.

- **Partial salvage.** Sympathetic magic as a *descriptive* typology of likeness- and contact-based practices; the comparative interest in sacred kingship, scapegoat rites, and seasonal drama; and the historiography of how Victorian science theorized "primitive thought" — these survive without Frazer's ranking. Salvage is not vindication of the original findings.

## Current Standing

*The Golden Bough* stands as a **historical monument** of armchair anthropology and comparative religion, not as a reliable ethnographic encyclopedia or a live general theory of magic and kingship.

**Discarded as research findings:** unilinear magic → religion → science; psychic-unity licensing of independent parallel stages from trait similarity; the single [[dying-and-rising-god]] type as Frazer built it; the Nemi solution (King of the Wood as oak-spirit whose external life is the mistletoe/Golden Bough) as demonstrated history; conjectural mitigation ladders from regicide to carnival; associationist "false science" as the general origin of magic.

**Retained with altered status:** the *labels* [[sympathetic-magic]], [[taboo]], [[scapegoat]], [[divine-king]], [[external-soul]] — often stripped of evolutionary ranking and re-theorized; Nemi and the dying god as literary and historiographic reference points; the book itself as primary evidence for the history of the discipline and for the production of colonial-era comparative knowledge.

**Live programs that are not Frazer's:** cognitive and evolutionary approaches to religion sometimes revisit intellectualist questions (how minds generate supernatural agents and causal intuitions) without his stages or library method; sacred kingship and ritual regicide are studied in regional historical and ethnographic depth (including restudy of cases Frazer used) without the worldwide kill-before-decay law; folklore studies treat calendar custom without default survivalism.

**Pedagogical role.** Still assigned as a landmark to be read critically — for how comparative argument works and fails, for the ethnographic-present and armchair traps, for the difference between a durable *name* for a practice and a validated *theory* of it. In this wiki's terms: a study page and hub entry are mandatory because the work is a reference point; citation of its findings on subject-matter pages must remain attributed and date-stamped as Frazer, never promoted to wiki voice.

## Historiography and Sources

**Primary text trail.** *The Golden Bough: A Study in Magic and Religion* — first edition 1890 (2 vols.); second edition 1900 (3 vols.); third edition 1911–15 (12 vols.); author's one-volume abridgement 1922 (Macmillan). This wiki's ingested text is the 1922 abridgement via a 2009 Lux Occulta Press digital PDF (pdftotext; complete through Ch. 69 "Farewell to Nemi") — see reliability notes on [[frazer-golden-bough-1922]]. The abridgement is Frazer's own reduction of the third edition: argumentative spine preserved, much annotation and bulk example cut. Edition-sensitive claims (self-revisions on fire theory, Celtic victims, oak-sky association) should be tied to the later text.

**Authorial and institutional context.** [[frazer-james-george|James George Frazer]] (1854–1941), Trinity College, Cambridge; classicist by training; no ethnographic fieldwork. Intellectual debt to [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]; vegetation and European peasant materials indebted to Mannhardt. The study is a product of metropolitan library science in a colonial world-system of reportage.

**Secondary scholarship shaping reputation** (named as afterlife; several pending full ingest): Robert Ackerman, *J. G. Frazer: His Life and Work* (1987); George W. Stocking Jr. on Victorian anthropology; Jonathan Z. Smith and related work on the dying-god construct; standard histories of anthropological theory treating Frazer as the high-water mark of classical evolutionist comparison. When those works are ingested, Critiques and Reception sections should be promoted from attributed summary to sourced positions and any live dispute routed to `debates/` (already partly covered by [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]).

**Historiographic caution.** Frazer's reputation has moved through at least three phases: (1) early authority in comparative religion and folklore; (2) mid-century anthropological rejection as the type-case of bad method; (3) late-century partial historicization — still rejected as science of culture, re-read as a major cultural text and as evidence of how disciplines produce knowledge. This hub page belongs to phase (3): reconstruct as conducted, attribute all findings, record the afterlife without either hagiography or pure demolition.
