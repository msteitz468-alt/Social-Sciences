# Ilongot Headhunting, 1883–1974 — Deep Analysis

## Question and Intellectual Context

Rosaldo's opening sentence announces the program flatly: "This book should be read as a demonstration that ethnography stands to gain considerable analytical power through close attention to historical process." The wager is that the two glaring weaknesses of the discipline circa 1980 — synchronic method and the "timeless primitive" it manufactures — are one weakness, and that a single well-worked case can show it.

The intellectual context is triple. First, the exhaustion of the classic monograph: Rosaldo writes that his generation "find[s] it impossible to fit the results of our investigations into the classic ethnographic mold," which either yields "a minuscule variation on... balanced segmentary opposition" or breaks on societies "simply unsuited" to the received toolkit. The [[ilongot|Ilongot]] are the second kind: cognatic, "anarchistic" in authority, lacking segmentary lineages, age-grades, men's houses, dual organization, or marriage rules — none of the institutions "that so often have given ethnographies their classical elegance." Second, the standing ban on history: [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s attack on "pseudo-historical speculations" (quoted at length from 1952) is treated as an "ancestral prohibition" that, by defining history as documentary origin-hunting, "rendered history useless for the study of most so-called primitive societies." Third, the fashionable renewal of timelessness: Eliade's eternal return, [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s "cold" societies, and Sahlins's 1976 restatement of societies "'with' and 'without' history." Rosaldo's verdict is one of the book's most quoted claims: the hot/cold contrast is "an artifact of ethnographic method, not a reflection of the human condition" — it is "surely no accident that historians study so-called hot societies and anthropologists study so-called cold societies."

The book belongs to the same moment as the practice-theoretic turn — Rosaldo cites Bernstein, Giddens, and Raymond Williams as the theoretical base for stressing "not just the given nature of society, but also the ways in which human beings continually construct, manipulate, and even recast the social worlds into which they were born" — and to the anthropology–history rapprochement fostered at the Institute for Advanced Study in 1975–76 (the acknowledgments name conversations with social historians, [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]], [[ortner-sherry|Ortner]], William Sewell, Robert Darnton, and others).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

Thirty months of joint fieldwork with [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] (1967–69, 1974), based in the Kakidugen local cluster of the Rumyad *[[bertan]]*. The design as conducted is a story of two failed designs:

1. **"Alliance and Enmity" (1968).** A conventional synchronic plan to study marital exchange and feuding. It foundered on the *bertan* puzzle: two sociologically disparate kinds of groups — 13 co-residential, ~80% endogamous units of ~165 persons, and 54 dispersed categories that never acted collectively — shared one Ilongot term.
2. **The developmental study (dissertation and after).** Following Fortes (1949), Freeman, and Campbell on cognatic societies, Rosaldo layered four processes (family, household, local cluster, bertan) into composite developmental cycles, modeled on Barton's Ifugao and Kalinga casebooks — cases "robbed of all personal names and reduced to the illustration of elementary structural principles." He later indicts this form: readers cannot tell whether actors recur across cases or which episode came first, which is why "seasoned anthropological readers often skim or even skip such illustrative case materials."

The breakthrough was methodological and dated: **August 1974**, when Rosaldo established a reliable chronology back to about 1920, "and with less certainty to 1883." Its raw material was the genre he had spent 1967–69 transcribing "in uncomprehending boredom" as "disposable texts": Ilongot *tadek* (stories), episodic narratives strung "like beads on a string" by movement through named places rather than by plot. The tearful recitations of place names from the 1945 flight from Japanese troops, which had bored him, turned out to embody "the culturally distinctive sense of history."

Treated properly, the stories became evidence twice over — "documents to assess for their truth value, and cultural forms to appraise for their particular meanings" — following what Rosaldo calls canonical method in anthropology and history (Vansina, Evans-Pritchard, Lewis; "the classic source of course is Thucydides"). Verification proceeded by convergence: multiple versions of an incident compared for factual discrepancy; narratives situated against demography, household composition, residential moves, and written records. The showpiece check: Ilongot stories that the Japanese invaded during planting season "were fully confirmed by reports from the American army."

Two design commitments deserve notice. The **chronological index** (prepared by Amy Burce) makes the book navigable by date as well as by chapter — an artifact of the claim that the analysis is genuinely historical. And the **first-person interventions** are declared "a consciously chosen analytical method, not merely as a literary device": the omniscient-narrator convention misleads wherever the fieldworker did not in fact "hear all major sides of a story."

## The Site, Sample, and Data

The Ilongot at fieldwork numbered ~3,500 upland hunter-horticulturalists (dry rice, sweet potato, taro; deer and wild pig) in the Cagayan drainage of northern Luzon. Subsistence and the sexual division of labor were "much as" William Jones found them in 1908–9 — one of several deliberate continuity checks against the earliest ethnographic witness. The core data: verbatim-transcribed narratives from named (pseudonymous) interlocutors — Lakay, Tukbaw, Insan, Wagat, Tepeg, Disa, and others — plus genealogies, marriage and divorce tallies (e.g., the ten marriages of 1955–58 tracked with their children's 1974 schooling), a 1968 hunting-yield tabulation by age, and colonial-era documents (Jones's letters and diaries via Rideout 1912; constabulary and Bureau of Insular Affairs materials; the hostile 1885 description by Jordana y Morera quoted to exhibit colonial perception).

## Findings in Detail

*(All attributed to Rosaldo; fieldwork 1967–74, narrated events 1883–1974.)*

**The deconstruction of bertan.** The book's namesake analytical result. Synchronic inspection made bertan look like either corporate groups or empty labels; the diachronic view showed "a trajectory from initial residential concentration through later dispersal to final evanescence," with both phases bearing one name because they are one process. Part III radicalizes this: pushed back to 1883–1905, "Rumyad" dissolves into five historically autonomous and at times mutually hostile groups, whose present unity was produced by datable episodes of forced re-concentration — the smallpox epidemics of 1883 and 1889, the 1945 catastrophe, the mid-1950s retreat under Hukbalahap-era pressure. Collective identity is a residue of shared history, not a design of enduring structure.

**Structure inverted.** Rosaldo states the methodological inversion explicitly: he uses "structural summations as an analytical starting point, rather than as an encapsulating conclusion — for I am convinced that structures, in this case developmental cycles, facilitate as much as they constrain human action." Rules "require cultural interpretation — both by Ilongots and by their ethnographer"; hence analysis becomes the study of "struggles among people with different stakes in the outcome of events," and social order emerges "not as the inevitable playing out of underlying principles, but rather as a complex interplay of political processes."

**The feud as engine and archive.** The Butag–Rumyad feud (Part I: stories of 1923–56; the 1969 covenant celebration) is at once the prime mover of marriages and residential moves and the medium of historical consciousness — "the process of feuding is a central moving force for both the conduct and perception of history." Chapter 8 turns Gluckman's functionalist "peace in the feud" on its head: reconstructed over 1890–1928, the feud does not equilibrate; the long view shows "the feud in the peace."

**Cohorts and the 1945 anchor.** Chapter 4 reconstructs the flight from Japanese troops — starvation, disease, and gunfire that killed roughly 35% of the Kakidugen cluster — as the collective memory against which Ilongots date their lives. Chapters 4–6 work out the articulation of life cycle and historical moment: young men take heads and marry "in quick succession, like people walking along a path," so that peer cohorts, bound by mutual envy and shared timing, carry epoch-specific experience. Regularity (the life cycle) and contingency (the epoch) interpenetrate; neither reduces to the other.

**Headhunting historicized.** Raiding is not a timeless custom but a practice with a political history: near-cessation under effective constabulary control by the late 1920s, resurgence amid the 1945 collapse and postwar disorder (Chapter 5 reconstructs raid mechanics, omens and divination, the celebratory songs, and the red hornbill earrings of the "arrived" man), decline again, and definitive moratorium after the September 1972 declaration of martial law, when rumors of firing squads made headhunting "a thing of the past." Rosaldo also debunks the travel-literature staple (from Jenks) that a bride demanded a head as marriage-price.

**Perspectival knowledge.** Because Ilongot life lacked hierarchy "beyond sex and age, no single official version of events is widely accepted, and in this sense their cultural knowledge is deeply perspectival." Divergent tellings are analyzed as data on the tellers' political location — the partisan pole — or on restricted vantage — the soldier-on-the-battlefield pole.

**The ending observed in real time.** The Afterword records the transformation the Rosaldos watched: land parceled into individual shares against Ibaloy, Kallahan, Kankanay, and Ifugao squatters; game depleted; a school opened in 1972; deaths attributed to Ifugao witchcraft driving pig purchases and further land sales; and Radu's household converting to the New Tribes Mission after his seventh child's death. Rosaldo's misreading — "Can anybody be so blind as to believe that their children will never die if they accept the new religion?" — is corrected by Tepeg: with martial law forbidding headhunting, what Radu "sought in the new religion was not the denial of our inevitable deaths, but a means of coping with his grief." The exchange is the book's quiet hinge to *Culture and Truth*'s later argument about grief, rage, and the analyst's positioned understanding.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book opens not with the Ilongot but with **William Jones**, the Fox-descended American ethnographer killed by his Ilongot hosts on April 3, 1909 — and reads Jones's letters (enchantment with the "wildest people," disgust at "beasts than human beings," nostalgia for the closed Oklahoma frontier) as the product of a datable American historical formation, Rough Riders and Wild West shows included. The move is explicitly methodological: "just as I view the Ilongots in historical perspective, I must see myself in the same way." Rosaldo then positions himself — a Vietnam-era, Chicano-identified graduate of the same Harvard — as Jones's structural successor and moral contrast. Reflexivity here is not confession but symmetry: the same historicizing lens applied to observer and observed. The footnote assigning Jones responsibility for his own murder ("Jones's impatience gave way to verbal abuse... and then a threat to imprison an old man") quietly reverses the colonial atrocity framing.

The **three images** — the play, the house, the hidden principle — compress the received view of social structure into a memorable typology whose shared failure is "their failure to notice that human life is both given and actively constructed." The **baseball extended metaphor** (via Gallie and Hexter) models the historical understanding as the fan's double vision: strategic knowledge of the unfolding moment plus a sense of the whole season — exactly what narrative, and only narrative, encodes. And the declaration that "what we have to say is rarely separable from how we say it," with Hayden White's *Metahistory* invoked as the model for a poetics of ethnographic writing, makes this 1980 book one of the earliest programmatic statements of the concern with ethnographic textuality that *Writing Culture* (1986) institutionalized — with Rosaldo among its contributors.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book became a touchstone of the anthropology–history convergence of the 1980s and a standard citation, alongside the practice-theory literature, for treating structure as medium and outcome of action rather than script. Its critique of the ethnographic present and of the "timeless primitive" passed into disciplinary common sense; its demonstration that a nonliterate society's history could be reconstructed with source-critical rigor gave [[ethnohistory]] and [[oral-tradition|oral-tradition method]] a celebrated exemplar. Within Philippine and Southeast Asian studies it stands with Michelle Rosaldo's *Knowledge and Passion* (1980) — the companion account of the emotional idiom of *[[liget]]* — as the core Ilongot corpus.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

No restudy of Ilongot headhunting is possible: the practice ended under martial law, and the Afterword itself documents the society's incorporation. The significant reanalysis is internal to Rosaldo's corpus: "Grief and a Headhunter's Rage" (1989) argues that this book's political-historical account, for all its care, had not conveyed the *force* of bereaved rage that Ilongot men named as headhunting's motive — a limit Rosaldo says he grasped only after Michelle Rosaldo's death in the field (1981). The 1980 monograph is thus bracketed by its author as analytically right and experientially incomplete — itself now a standard teaching case about positioned knowledge.

## Current Standing

Canonical. Read as (1) the exemplary historical ethnography; (2) a precursor text of reflexive anthropology and the Writing Culture moment; (3) the definitive account of Ilongot society at its documented moment. Its claims are period-bound by design — the book's own thesis forbids projecting them beyond 1974.
