# Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[morgan-systems-consanguinity-study]]. Reciprocal hub path: `[[hubs/studies/morgan-systems-consanguinity-study]]`.

**Primary base:** Lewis Henry Morgan, *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity and Affinity of the Human Family]]* (Smithsonian Contributions to Knowledge, vol. 17, 1871), read in the 1997 University of Nebraska Press reprint with Elisabeth Tooker’s introduction. All findings and evolutionary claims below are **Morgan’s (or Tooker’s historiographic)** — attributed. Tables are enormous and layout-noisy; analytic weight rests on prose chapters, especially Part III Ch. VI “General Results.”

## Question and Intellectual Context

Morgan’s problem was twofold and grew while he worked. He began from a **philological-historical** hope: that kinship terminologies, collected as full systems, might prove the **Asiatic origin of the American Indians** and might even aid recognition of language relationships. He also faced an ethnographic puzzle discovered among the **Seneca**: their plan of relationship differed radically from European usage. The research expanded into a world survey of “systems of consanguinity and affinity” and into a **conjectural evolutionary solution** explaining why classificatory systems exist.

Disciplinary moment: founding-era comparative ethnology. Manuscript to Joseph Henry in **1865** — the year of McLennan’s *Primitive Marriage*, Lubbock’s *Pre-historic Times*, Tylor’s *Researches* — revised after reviewers, accepted **1868**, printed **1871** (most expensive Smithsonian volume to that date, Tooker notes). Published just after Lubbock’s *Origin of Civilisation* (1870) and just before Tylor’s *Primitive Culture* (1871). Against sparse prior notices of non-European kin terms, Morgan offered **mass comparison** and a **binary typology**.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How many radical forms of kinship terminology exist? | “System of relationship” of a nation/language | ~139 terminologies (Tooker); six “families” in tables | [[kinship-schedule-method]] + tabulation | mid-19th c. collection |
| Can systems prove ethnic connection / Asiatic origin? | Language “families” (Morgan’s constructs) | Ganowanian vs Turanian type cases (Seneca, Tamil) | comparative identity of radical features | same |
| Why do classificatory systems exist? | Custom sequence (conjectural) | Hawaiian [[punalua|pinalua]] reports; tribal organization | conjectural reconstruction | same |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** Morgan did not sample randomly. He built a **printed schedule** of roughly two hundred kin types, pretested at Tonawanda Seneca, expanded it, and circulated it via missionaries, Indian agents, and foreign correspondents under Smithsonian sponsorship (from 1860). Tooker: North American data strongest (about two-thirds collected by Morgan himself); Oceania and Asia thinner; Central/South America and Africa sparse relative to ambition. Many returns imperfect; Morgan valued complete schedules over many incomplete ones. The schedule is “self-corrective” for reciprocal terms when carefully filled.

**Units of comparison** are period constructs: Aryan, Semitic, Uralian, **Ganowanian** (Morgan’s American Indian stock — [[ganowanian-family|obsolete construct]]), Turanian, Malayan, Eskimo. Tooker maps later renamings: Turanian type ≈ Dravidian; Malayan ≈ Hawaiian. Four-way non-identity: Morgan’s “families” are **not** modern genetic populations, archaeological cultures, or self-conscious peoples.

**Inference styles mixed:** (1) structural typology (descriptive vs classificatory); (2) stability argument (domestic institutions transmitted with blood, more stable than language); (3) conjectural history of customs (promiscuity → communal family → Hawaiian custom → tribal exogamy → pair marriage → property/civilized family); (4) elimination of rival hypotheses for Turanian–Ganowanian identity (borrowing, accident, spontaneous growth, transmission with blood).

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Field.** Morgan’s avid work among Seneca at Tonawanda; *League of the Ho-dé-no-sau-nee* (1851) then a pause, then return via kinship schedules. Western and reservation travels extended North American coverage. **Correspondent science** supplied Europe, Near East, Asia, Pacific — colonial and missionary infrastructures are part of the data’s production context.

**Tables.** About one-third of the volume: parallel columns of kin terms. They are the study’s enduring empirical deposit and also its bulk OCR hazard. Analytic chapters repeatedly send the reader to Table I (descriptive), Table II (Ganowanian), Table III (Turanian–Malayan).

**Gaps Morgan admits.** Mongolian, Tungusian, Australian, and Negroid nations largely absent; he still claims coverage of “eight-tenths and upwards, numerically, of the entire human family” and sufficiency for determining the nature of systems as domestic institutions.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding (Morgan, attributed) | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Only **two radically distinct systems**: descriptive vs classificatory (Celtic vs Seneca-Iroquois as examples) | Global tables; Part I vs II–III | Typology historically foundational; modern formalisms re-subdivide (Murdock etc. not ingested) |
| Descriptive = Aryan–Semitic–Uralian plan: collaterals diverge; primary-term combinations; few specials | Part I schedules | Partial; “Eskimo” type later reclassified differently |
| Classificatory principal form = Turanian + Ganowanian; lower stage = Malayan; third = Eskimo | Parts II–III; General Results | Malayan≈Hawaiian, Turanian≈Dravidian renamings; Eskimo as independent form often accepted as distinct |
| Systems are **domestic institutions** (Prop. II) — indurated, transmitted, resistant | Functional argument + complexity of classificatory plan | Accepted that terminologies are systematic cultural objects |
| Descriptive system follows **pair marriage** / certainty of parentage (Prop. III) | Logical fit under monogamy | Contested as exclusive origin story |
| Classificatory system requires **antecedent customs** (Hawaiian custom, communal family, tribal reform) (Prop. IV) | Hawaiian missionary reports + conjectural series | **Contested/overturned** as universal history ([[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]) |
| Shared American Indian system proves **Ganowanian common origin** (Prop. V) | Identity of indicative features across stocks with unrelated vocables | Kinship plan ≠ language family; construct obsolete |
| Turanian–Ganowanian identity → **Asiatic origin** of American Indians (Prop. VI) | Seneca–Tamil comparison; reject borrow/accident/spontaneous growth | Peopling of Americas settled on other grounds; kinship not primary proof |
| Malayan older; Turanian engrafted; Ganowanian youngest branch after Turanian establishment (Prop. VII) | Stage ordering of forms | Evolutionary ordering abandoned as world history |
| Classificatory terms can **outlive** the customs that produced them | Observation under present pair marriage + surviving terminology | Insight retained in “survivals” debates; mechanism disputed |

### Load-bearing passage (two systems)

> “In a general sense there are but two, the descriptive and the classificatory. Of the first, the Celtic, and of the second, the Seneca-Iroquois is an example. They rest upon conceptions fundamentally different, and are separated from each other by a line so clearly defined as to admit of no misapprehension.”

Rhetoric: **binary clarity** for a mass of table noise. The Celtic/Seneca pairing makes the typology memorable and Euro-American-centered (Europe as descriptive default; Iroquois as classificatory type).

### Load-bearing passage (civilization line)

Morgan maps the two systems onto a near-line between “civilized” and “uncivilized” families and argues that the modern family, growing from **property and inheritance**, underwrites first civilization. This is the hinge where **terminology science** becomes **stage theory** — the move Engels will amplify and twentieth-century anthropology will reject as history while keeping the terminology problem.

### Conjectural fifteen-step series (compressed)

Morgan (crediting a suggestion of Rev. J. H. McIlvaine) orders customs from promiscuity through communal family, Hawaiian [[punalua]] custom, tribal organization as reform banning brother–sister marriage (generating Turanian distinctions), pair marriage, polygamy as limited reform, barbarian/patriarchal households, and finally property-based civilized family that overthrows classificatory systems. He warns there are **no trenchant lines** between stages and that the series is partly hypothetical — then still “reluctantly compelled” to advance sweeping conclusions. That tension is the study’s central rhetorical risk.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Defamiliarize Europe.** By making Seneca the classificatory type, Morgan relativizes English kin terms as one plan among others — founding move of kinship anthropology.
2. **Institutionalize terminology.** “Domestic institution” status elevates word lists to social structure.
3. **Stability > language.** Terms change dialectically within stocks but **system features** persist across stocks with no shared vocables — used to argue blood transmission of institutions.
4. **Elimination argument** for Asiatic origin (four hypotheses) mimics legal brief structure.
5. **Tables as authority.** Page bulk performs scientific seriousness (Smithsonian expense as paratext of credibility).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Founded kinship** as a central, technical anthropological specialty (Tooker).
- **Engels** (*[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*) made Morgan’s ladder world-historical materialism; Marxist canons preserved group-marriage and gens sequences.
- **Linguists** largely rejected kin terms as deep-family proof (Tooker).
- **Peopling of the Americas** was established on archaeological and later genetic grounds independent of Morgan’s kinship argument.
- Modern type names (Dravidian, Hawaiian, Iroquois, Eskimo, Crow, Omaha…) reorganize Morgan’s material under other theories (not yet ingested in this collection).

Canonical **founding study** of comparative kinship. Read for: (1) schedule method history; (2) descriptive/classificatory landmark; (3) Iroquois type case; (4) genealogy of evolutionary and Marxist kinship theory; (5) cautionary case of over-inference from terminology to deep history. *Ancient Society* (1877) elaborates stages — not yet ingested.

## Design and Methods as Conducted — Expanded

### Schedule construction and circulation

Morgan’s instrument was not a short word list. Tooker describes a schedule of some **two hundred kin types**. Morgan’s own Part I introduction narrates: prepare questions describing persons with “particularity”; send schedule and explanatory letter; use returned forms to build tables so that nations appear in parallel columns. He insists every system is “intrinsically difficult until it has been carefully studied,” and that imperfect schedules are worse than fewer complete ones. The schedule’s reciprocal structure (if A is my X, I should be A’s Y) provides internal checks when fillers are careful — a design feature Morgan advertises as self-corrective.

Smithsonian sponsorship (Joseph Henry from 1860) turned a private passion into an institutional data program. The lag from 1865 submission to 1871 publication included Henry’s request to condense, two rounds of reviewers, and three years of printing. Tooker: the most expensive Smithsonian volume to date. Cost and bulk are part of the study’s **rhetoric of completeness**.

### Field and correspondent pathways

Morgan’s North American advantage was personal travel and reservation access, especially Seneca at Tonawanda, after the *League* (1851) ethnography. Tooker notes he then “abandoned ethnographic studies” before returning through kinship. For Asia and the Pacific he depended on American missionaries and other correspondents — **colonial and mission infrastructures** as epistemic pipeline. Geographic unevenness is therefore structural, not accidental: the tables over-represent regions where English-speaking agents could fill schedules.

### What the tables do analytically

Each table freezes a language’s answers to the same genealogical questionnaire. Comparison is then **feature matching**: does father’s brother receive the father term? Are cross-cousins distinguished from parallel cousins? Do collaterals merge into the lineal line? Morgan’s “indicative features” are these pattern diagnostics. When Dakota and Seneca share features but not cognate kin words, he treats the **plan** as inherited independently of the **vocables** — the key move for Prop. V–VI.

### Limits of the design (critical reconstruction)

1. **Genealogical grid imposition.** The schedule assumes English/Latin kin-type geometry is a neutral metalanguage. Where a society does not carve the world that way, fillers may force answers.
2. **Nation/language as unit.** “Ganowanian family” packages diverse Indigenous nations into one stock on kinship-plan evidence alone.
3. **Correspondent error.** Morgan knew imperfect fills were common; quality control was incomplete.
4. **Theory-laden conjectural chapter.** General Results does not fall out of tables alone; the fifteen-step series imports missionary Hawaiian reports and a prior commitment to progress from “barbarism.”
5. **Missing continents.** Australia and much of Africa/Mongolia absent — yet universalist conclusions are drawn.

## Findings in Detail — Expanded

### Proposition I–II: How many systems, and are they institutions?

Morgan’s answer to “how many radically distinct systems?” is **two**. Everything else is stage or variant within classificatory, or the Eskimo third form. Prop. II elevates both systems to **domestic institutions**: once a plan of distinguishing kin is transmitted and fixed, it resists radical innovation and structures family life. The classificatory system, with “upwards of twenty particulars” cohering, is for him an institution “in the highest sense.”

This is a foundational **ontological claim** for anthropology: kinship terminology is not epiphenomenal vocabulary but social structure. Later functionalists and structuralists keep the claim while changing the explanation.

### Proposition III–IV: Origins of each system

**Descriptive** systems, Morgan argues, match the “nature of descents” under **marriage between single pairs**: lineal line, diverging collaterals, numerical degrees. Nature “teaches” this with “unerring certainty” once pair marriage and parental certainty exist. Hence descriptive systems **postdate** monogamous pairing.

**Classificatory** systems cannot be read off “descents as they now exist among civilized nations.” They require other social conditions. Morgan rejects mutual protection alone as sufficient cause; rejects polygamy/polyandria as whole-system explanations (while allowing them limited indicative effects); rejects tribal (gens) membership alone as generative (Seneca examples where tribe and term diverge). True causes must be **antecedent** customs: communal family, brother–sister cohabitation in the Malayan solution, Hawaiian custom, then tribal organization as **reform** producing Turanian/Ganowanian distinctions.

### The Malayan solution and punalua

Morgan treats Hawaiian **pinalua/punalua** reports as non-conjectural ethnographic fact (missionary traces; “antecedent universal prevalence” among Hawaiians — his claim). Under Malayan terminology, primary categories swallow collaterals: wide father/mother/brother/sister/son/daughter classes. He explains this from extended cohabitation rights among brothers and sisters in a communal family. He admits the infinite downward sibling series strains a narrow Hawaiian-custom model and invokes broader “privilege of barbarism.” The Malayan system is “preserved evidence” of a profound primitive state — a classic **survivals** argument.

### Turanian engrafting and tribal reform

Turanian (Tamil type) shares Malayan base but adds distinctions Morgan ties to **exogamous tribal organization** banning brother–sister marriage: sisters’ children become nephews/nieces; father’s sister aunt; mother’s brother uncle. Tribal organization is “the greatest primitive reformatory institution.” Ganowanian matches Turanian’s full form; hence both are “highest” classificatory stage. Malayan cannot be derived from Turanian; ordering is Malayan → Turanian → (Ganowanian split).

### Propositions V–VII: Ethnic history

**Prop. V.** Constant radical features of the American Indian system across nations with unrelated kin words prove, for Morgan, **common source** and continental coevality of the Ganowanian family.

**Prop. VI.** Seneca and Tamil systems identical in radical and minute characteristics. Four hypotheses for joint possession: borrowing, accident, spontaneous independent growth, transmission with blood. He rejects the first three; concludes Turanian and Ganowanian drew from **one parent stock**, Asiatic origin of Ganowanian “to abide scrutiny.” Columbia–Amoor geography is adjunct, not proof.

**Prop. VII.** Malayan older (island stationary); Turanian engrafted after Malayan migration; Ganowanian younger, detached after Turanian system established. Eskimo independent classificatory system — non-connection with the three.

### Seneca-Iroquois type case (Parts II)

As documented in Morgan’s mid-nineteenth-century materials: the Seneca form exemplifies classificatory mergers (e.g., father’s brother under father-class terms; distinctive cross/parallel treatments in his description). He presents it as standard for Hodenosaunee and then tracks “indicative features” across Dakota, Algonkin, Gulf, Cherokee, Prairie, Athapascan, Columbia River, Shoshonee, southwestern village peoples, etc. Society-page claims in this wiki stay in the **past tense of documentation** and link to [[iroquois]].

## Verbatim Passages with Rhetorical Analysis

### On classificatory artificiality under pair-marriage assumption

> “Under the classificatory system consanguinei are not described by a combination of the primary terms, but each and all, however remote in degree, fall under some one of the recognized relationships. The gradus yields to the nexus. … and lastly, the several collateral lines are ultimately merged in the lineal line… The classificatory system becomes, in these several particulars, arbitrary, artificial and complicated.”

Analysis: Morgan’s adjectives “arbitrary, artificial and complicated” are **relative to monogamous descent nature**. The rhetoric sets up the need for a special historical explanation. Twentieth-century formalists flip the valuation: complex merger rules are elegant structure, not barbarous artificiality.

### On systems outliving customs

> “With marriage between single pairs, with the family in a modified sense, with the tribal organization still unimpaired in certain nations and abandoned in others, with polygamy polyandria and the Hawaiian custom either unknown or of limited practice, and with promiscuous intercourse substantially eradicated, the classificatory system of relationship still exists in full vigor… ages upon ages after the sequence of customs and institutions in which it apparently originated have ceased to exercise any influence upon its form.”

Analysis: This is Morgan’s best **anti-naive** observation — terminology is not a simple mirror of current marriage. Ironically, it also undermines easy reading of terms as present social structure, a point later critics use against sociological reduction.

### On property and civilization

Morgan’s claim that property, inheritance, and the “family as now constituted” ground first civilization ties kinship evolution to political economy. Engels will materialize this; Boasians will refuse the package.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications — Expanded Positions

1. **Internal Victorian debate.** McLennan and Bachofen offered rival universal schemes; Engels’s 1891 preface narrates Morgan as victor — a winners’ history.
2. **Data without ladder.** Many anthropologists treated *Systems* as a quarry of facts and discarded the conjectural series (Tooker’s summary of reception).
3. **Linguistic rejection.** Kinship vocabularies did not become a standard comparative-method tool for deep genetic linguistics.
4. **Particularist critique.** No single sequence fits the ethnographic record; “group marriage” cases reanalyzed.
5. **Formal reanalysis.** Later kinship algebra re-describes Morgan’s types without evolutionary stages.
6. **Colonial knowledge.** Schedules extracted Indigenous and colonized peoples’ categories into Smithsonian science under asymmetric power — reflexivity content for any use of the tables.
7. **Marxist preservation.** Engels and later orthodoxy kept the ladder as scientific socialism’s prehistory — a parallel reception track.

## Counterfactual note

Without *Systems*, kinship might still have emerged from British legal anthropology and missionary grammars, but the **global tabular comparison** and the **Seneca type case** gave the field a distinct American empirical charter. Engels without Morgan’s tables would have lacked his strongest non-classical evidence for family evolution.
