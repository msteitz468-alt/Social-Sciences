# Studies Hub — Life and Death on Mt. Everest

> **Summary page:** [[ortner-life-and-death-everest-study]]

Deep analysis of [[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]]'s *[[ortner-life-and-death-everest-1999|Life and Death on Mt. Everest: Sherpas and Himalayan Mountaineering]]* (Princeton, 1999). Companion hub to [[hubs/studies/ortner-high-religion-study|High Religion]].

## Question and Intellectual Context

The book answers a public and an anthropological question at once. Publicly, after the May 1996 Everest storm deaths (eight climbers in commercial and other parties; live satellite drama; ~$65,000 guided clients), who are the usually silent partners of Himalayan climbing — the [[sherpa|Sherpas]] — and what do they and the international climbers think they are doing on lethal walls? Anthropologically, how does one write a long-term **unequal encounter** after the “crisis of representation,” without either dissolving the less powerful into dominant texts or reducing them to pure “resistance”?

Ortner’s answer is a dual history: (1) Sherpas’ changing place *inside* a sport still largely defined by “sahibs”; (2) mountaineering’s place *inside* Sherpa off-mountain agendas — Solu-Khumbu villages, monasteries, Kathmandu. She refuses “impact” as the only verb: mountaineering also supplies means for Sherpas to remake their own society on their own (partly contradictory) agendas. The intellectual neighbors are explicit: Spivak on the subaltern’s speech; Said/orientalism and constructionism; Geertz on purpose and thick description; Sahlins on the structure of the conjuncture; her own *[[ortner-high-religion-1989|High Religion]]* on monastic foundings as the other early-twentieth-century “game.”

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is Himalayan mountaineering as sahib–Sherpa encounter? | Expedition + home community + urban diaspora | 20th-c. expedition literature; Solu-Khumbu; Kathmandu | Historical ethnography of encounter | ~1900s–1990s |
| What do sahibs want? | Sahib culture (classed, gendered) | Hundreds of expedition books/memoirs | Critical reading of dominant texts | Full century |
| What do Sherpas want? | Climbing labor + village/monastery life | Prior field notes 1966–68, 1976, 1979; ~30 Kathmandu interviews 1990; Ang Tharkay & Tenzing autobiographies | Interview + reanalysis of own notes | Field dates as above |
| How does religion intertwine? | Monastic campaign + expedition ritual | High Religion archive + unpublished post-founding material | Historical reconstruction | 1910s–1990s |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** Ortner did not do continuous mountain ethnography on expeditions; the 1990 campaign is urban interviews with climbers/sardars plus gossip. Depth on village religion and inequality comes from earlier Solu (1966–68) and Khumbu (1979) work. The sahib side is almost entirely textual. Autobiographies are mediated (Western co-authors/interpreters); she chooses relatively straightforward use for men and times over full discourse dissection. Field notes are fixed third-person texts — interview “quotes” are often her write-ups. She continues the term **sahib** ironically after Sherpas drop it in the 1970s to keep power asymmetry visible.

**Positionality.** She narrates her own arc: mountaineering nearly invisible in 1960s Solu (border closures; fewer Solu climbers); hostility after *The Man Who Skied Down Everest* deaths and 1979 Khumjung grief; eventual partial “got it” that climbing also stands at a critical angle to dominant culture. She admits counter-positioning against whatever dominant Sherpa image is current (romantic → she stresses conflict; “spoiled” → she stresses dignity and kindness).

## The Site/Sample/Data

- **Mountains / expeditions:** Everest and broader Himalayan 8000m culture; British early period; Swiss/British/American postwar; 1970s Alpine-style; 1980s–90s commercial.
- **Solu-Khumbu:** home region; Khumbu as high source of climbing Sherpas; Solu increasingly labor for Khumbu tourist economy.
- **Darjeeling:** nineteenth–mid-twentieth wage labor and early climbing labor market.
- **Kathmandu 1990:** urban Sherpa community; ~30 climbing interviews.
- **Monasteries:** Tengboche, Chiwong, Devuche, Thami, Takshindo, Serlo; Rumbu (Tibet) until Chinese occupation — Appendix B sketches.
- **Named individuals (illustrative, not full chronicle):** Tenzing Norgay, Ang Tharkay, Ang Rita Sherpa (male multi-summitter), Apa, Pertemba, Ang Phu, Pasang Lhamu, Ang Nyimi, woman climber Ang Rita of Pangboche (distinct person), Tengboche Rimpoche, Zatul Rimpoche, Hillary, Bonington, Messner, et al.

### Serious games (load-bearing definition)

Ortner’s core theoretical passage (Ch.1) packs purpose, discourse, power, and relational difference into one image:

> By the term “games” I mean to capture… that people do not just enact either material necessity or cultural scripts but live life with (often intense) purpose and intention… By the adjective “serious” I mean further to emphasize the constant play of power in the games of life, and the fact that, for most people most of the time, a great deal is at stake… history constructs both people and the games that they play, and in which people make history by enacting, reproducing, and transforming those games.

Ch.6 elaborates: games include purposes, discursive categories, organization and relative power of players; they thrive on difference — here the Sherpa/sahib difference and its permutations. Ch.9: Sherpas always play **several** games at once (sahib mountaineering + games of their own history/politics/desires); prefer **remaking** language to **corruption**.

### Sahib periodization (compressed)

1. **Early British / military-paternalist** — Survey science; discipline; death and “breakdown” as contempt; romanticism and critique of modernity as motives.
2. **Postwar mega / machismo** — scale explosion; competition as masculine essence; body focus; sexualized mountain metaphors; bawdy joking; equality fantasy in some Swiss-style teams vs Hunt hierarchy 1953.
3. **1970s counterculture** — Nepal as magnet; Alpine-style; anti-competition; collective leadership experiments; women enter amid sexism *and* sensitivity discourse.
4. **Mid-1980s+ commercial / yuppie** — paying strangers; guide hierarchy; creature comforts; weak teams; some hardening toward corpses; 1996 as emblem.

### Sherpa labor and identity

Ethnicity vs role: specialized high-altitude “Sherpa” vs local porters (often Tamang) vs “members.” Sardar-mediated hiring; career ladder; zhindak patronage. Cheerfulness and excellence are historically produced, not racial essence — tied to escape from smallness, wage freedom, competitive ethos (*balabenzin*, *tatok*, *chana* among equals). Strikes for pay/equipment *and* respect. By late century: all-Sherpa expeditions; demand for recognition as climbers/members; nonethnic workers called “Sherpas”; tourist-route nouveau riche vs Solu labor internal proletariat.

### Religion on and off the mountain

Monasticism as parallel game: foundings 1916–1920s; campaign against Nupki Gyelwu blood sacrifice; non-killing ethics (Tilman already notes slaughter aversion 1938); monk ideal as emotional self-management; tulku compassion; Lama Gulu’s reincarnation as Tengboche Rimpoche — expedition blessing circuit. On mountains: pollution taboos; big base-camp puja evolution (moral influence *and* staged exoticism); funerals. By century’s end Ortner judges religion largely **monasticized** (married lamas nearly gone in Khumbu; Nyungne widespread; Dumji cleaned; shamans rare or retranslated) even as some monasteries languish and lamas *talok* (fall).

### Death — the “Thick Resistance” payoff

Two Orientalist traps: breakdown → childish; impassivity → fatalism. Ortner: fatalism “is simply wrong.” After deaths, motives for nonreaction include finishing contracted work and religious rules about emotion near the dead, plus monastic letting-go — sometimes refused in spectacular grief. Funerals refuse reduction to pure resistance *or* pure performance *or* pure grief:

> Rather it is crucial to try to understand how Sherpas can be deeply shaped by the mountaineering experience and yet sustain their own structures of intentionality, their own projects in life, and their own ways of enacting those projects.

### Gender and Pasang Lhamu

Shared masculinity is a bonding technology that can exclude women. Sherpa women climbers face household and community resistance; kitchen work may be the step into expeditions. **Pasang Lhamu** (1993) crystallizes gender *with* ethnic/national politics — difference from first-world feminist separatism. Do not merge **Ang Rita** (woman, Pangboche) with male multi-summitter **Ang Rita Sherpa**.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

- **Detective-story realism** against experimental literary ethnography — narrative drive with named expeditions and deaths.
- **Ironic sahib** as sustained analytic tag.
- **Double reading** of every sahib text: orientalism *and* ethnographic recovery.
- **Counter-positioning** against the dominant Sherpa image of the decade — reflexive, declared.
- **Periodization by sahib culture** that never freezes Sherpas as static traditional foil.
- **Internal axes** (big/small, monastic vs popular, gender, Solu/Khumbu) so “resistance” is never only colonial.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Standard citation for Sherpas and Himalayan mountaineering; primary source for Ortner’s [[serious-games]] brand of practice theory in teaching anthologies. Densifies the vault’s [[practice-theory]] and [[agency]] pages beyond *High Religion*’s monastery focus into transnational labor, death, and gender. Feeds feminist and representation debates without closing them.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Holders / texts (as Ortner stages or adjacent) | Claim |
|---|---|---|
| Romantic Sherpas | Early mountaineers; von Fürer-Haimendorf (as Ortner reads him) | Cooperative, cheerful, nonmaterialist essence |
| Spoiled / corrupted Sherpas | Laird 1981; Thompson 1979; Haimendorf 1984; strands of expedition gossip | Tourism/money destroyed honesty, loyalty, culture |
| Virtual / dialogic Sherpas | Vincanne Adams, *Tigers of the Snow* | Identity produced with Western desire; authenticity critique + decline via mimesis cost |
| Ortner remaking thesis | *Life and Death* (1999) | Multiple serious games; reconfiguration not corruption; intentionality not dissolved |
| Fatalism of death | Bruce, Dyhrenfurth lectures, Messner, etc. | Buddhist acceptance of ordained death |
| Ortner anti-fatalism | Ch.5 | Wrong; grief real; impassivity multi-causal |

No experimental replication applies. Later Everest disaster literature and commercial-climbing ethnography can densify or challenge the epilogue’s yuppie sketch.

## Current Standing

Still the major anthropological synthesis of the twentieth-century Sherpa–mountaineering encounter. Best read as a pair with *High Religion* (foundings vs post-founding campaign + climbing labor). Date-stamp: primary interviews stop at 1990; 1996 is frame and literature, not new village restudy.

## Historiography and Sources

Primary: expedition print culture; Ortner field notes (four trips); Ang Tharkay and Tenzing Norgay autobiographies; High Religion oral-history base; secondary ethnography (Fisher, Adams, von Fürer-Haimendorf) treated both as data and as sahib representation. Gaps: limited expedition participant observation; mediated Sherpa “voices” in English print; death statistics second-hand; women’s climbing sample small; Chinese-side and non-Everest Himalaya thinner than Khumbu–Everest core.
