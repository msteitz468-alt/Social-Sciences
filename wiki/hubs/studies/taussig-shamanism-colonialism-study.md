# Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man (Taussig) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-study]]. Source: [[taussig-shamanism-colonialism-wild-man-1987]]. Author: [[taussig-michael]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · nine canonical body sections · attributed voice for all substantive findings.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man: A Study in Terror and Healing* (University of Chicago Press, 1987) is [[taussig-michael|Michael T. Taussig]]'s second major Colombian monograph after *[[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980|The Devil and Commodity Fetishism in South America]]* (1980). Where the devil book asked how folk devilry mediates proletarianisation and [[commodity-fetishism]], this book asks how **colonial terror** and **shamanic healing** share a single historical and imagistic field in southwest Colombia.

The Author's Note states the program without euphemism:

> This book of two parts, terror and healing, takes little for granted and leaves even less in its place. It derives from the almost five years I spent in the southwest of Colombia, South America, from 1969 to 1985, in periods varying from one month to two years.

Subject-matter, he continues, communicates itself:

> in the politics of epistemic murk and the fiction of the real, in the creation of Indians, in the role of myth and magic in colonial violence as much as in its healing, and in the way that healing can mobilize terror in order to subvert it, not through heavenly catharses but through the tripping up of power in its own disorderliness.

Methodologically he joins Walter Benjamin against historicist narcotic:

> The history that showed things "as they really were," he pointed out, was the strongest narcotic of our century. And of course it still is.

Against academic "rituals of explanation" that promise system from chaos without ruffling natural-order imagery, Taussig chooses montage "as I learned that principle not only from terror, but from Putumayo shamanism with its adroit, albeit unconscious, use of the magic of history and its healing power."

Disciplinary location: critical-turn cultural anthropology; medical anthropology of ritual healing; historical anthropology of Amazon rubber; experimental ethnographic writing adjacent to *Writing Culture* (1986) without being a simple member of that cohort. Political location: post-dictatorship Latin American reflection on torture (Timerman, Dorfman, O'Donnell's culture of fear) mounted beside early-twentieth-century Putumayo.

**As conducted, not as advertised.** Part One is not primary eyewitnessing of 1910 killings — it is a theoretically aggressive reading of a mediated archive (Casement's FO-edited realism, Hardenburg's melodrama, company speech, Whiffen's omission). Part Two is multi-year fieldwork with recurrent yagé participation; names sometimes changed (Author's Note); speech in Spanish. The book's "data" for terror are inseparable from the problem of **representation** it theorizes — design and object coincide.

## The Site/Sample/Data

**Putumayo rubber world (historical).** Northern Amazon frontier of Peru/Colombia; Casa Arana and British-registered Peruvian Amazon Company from 1907; rivers Igaraparaná, Caraparaná, Putumayo; stations including El Encanto, Último Retiro, Matanzas. Labor: Indigenous gatherers under debt advances; Barbadian and other employees; *muchachos* as Indian police. Metropolitan audience: London *Truth* 1909, House of Commons blue book 13 July 1913, Select Committee confusion over *conquistar*.

**Healing circuit (ethnographic).** Highland Ingano and related curers (Santiago and Ambrosia Mutumbajoy on the Mocoa; Salvador Moreno on Guamués as regional power); lowland Siona, Cofán, and other forest peoples as plant and *auca*-typed sources of power in highland eyes; poor white colonists (José García and others) as patients and occasional pupils; Capuchin and parish priests as rivals/exorcisers; market herbalists and *magia* book users; closing return to Puerto Tejada / Cauca Afro-Colombian world (Marlene Jiménez, Don Chu Chu).

**Identity cautions.** Huitoto/Witoto appear heavily as **colonial image** (curing song, rubber ethnology, Robuchon photography) — `associated_with` wild-man mythology, not equated with a single self-conscious people, language, or genetic population. *Auca* is a relational savagery label. Four-way non-identity enforced throughout.

### Load-bearing passages (verbatim / near-verbatim from OCR source)

**Space of death as colonial mediator:**

> Whatever the conclusions we draw about how that hegemony was so speedily effected, we would be unwise to overlook the role of terror. And by this I mean us to think-through-terror, which as well as being a physiological state is also a social one whose special features allow it to serve as the mediator par excellence of colonial hegemony: the space of death where the Indian, African, and white gave birth to a New World.

**Colonial mirror (Part One climax):**

> The terror and tortures they devised mirrored the horror of the savagery they both feared and fictionalized.

**Montage as yagé form (Part Two method):**

Taussig defines montage for yagé nights as alterations, cracks, displacements, and swerves all evening long — sudden interruptions that first appear as breaks in meaning-structure but constitute the structure (attributed paraphrase of ch.27 sequence). He rejects Victor Turner's communitas as model: impregnating people with unity may fit some rites, but yagé's playground is self–group flash and sensory pandemonium.

**Closing wild-man thesis:**

Through colonial history, colonizers provided the colonized with the "left-handed gift of the image of the wild man" — a gift whose powers colonizers would be blind to were it not reciprocated in ways that wrest civilization's image-work for healing and disorder (attributed synthesis of ch.28 close).



### Part One — Terror, reconstructed

Taussig opens not in the forest but in modern torture's narrative problem. Jacobo Timerman's *Prisoner without a Name, Cell without a Number* supplies the gaze of those about to die — gazes Timerman cannot and will not fully share. Ariel Dorfman's Chilean *Imbunche* allegory (child's bones broken and resewn by witches; society isolated, senses blocked by fear) names dictatorship's arbitrary order. Miguel Ángel Asturias's Estrada Cabrera Guatemala gives trees with ears and the idiot on the garbage heap as figures of a society where things persecute persons. These are not digressions: they train the reader to see Putumayo through a comparative culture of terror before a single rubber tree is tapped in the prose.

Only then does the book move to the scandal archive. In 1909 London *Truth* published "The Devil's Paradise: A British Owned Congo" on the Arana brothers' domain, forcing Foreign Office action. Roger Casement, already marked by Congo work and claiming Irish historical sympathy as interpretive key, was sent from Rio as consular representative. His Putumayo reports to Sir Edward Grey appeared as a House of Commons blue book on 13 July 1913. Biographer Brian Inglis (as Taussig uses him) notes FO changes that removed "indignant asides" so the report would seem more objective than Casement's manuscript. Taussig reads that style-objectivity as itself political — a production of [[epistemic-murk|epistemic murk]] in the name of dispelling it.

Hardenburg's *The Putumayo: The Devil's Paradise* supplied the scandal's melodrama and much material from short-lived Iquitos papers *La Sanción* and *La Felpa*, which objectified local rumor in print (including early socialist poetry on a front page — the politics of the periphery are not simple). Judge Rómulo Paredes's ~1911 file (~3,000 pages after four months) diagnosed employees' "diseased imagination" that saw Indian attack and conspiracy everywhere while organizing massacre. Taussig's point: the imagination is not epiphenomenal. It is how the labor regime ran.

### Economy, debt, and the stocks

The rubber was low grade; transport expensive; open-market wages would have been high. Official common sense held that labor scarcity plus cost structure "required" coercion. Taussig grants the political economy and then insists it does not explain the **form** of cruelty — target practice on living persons after drinking, petroleum burning "to pass the time," stocks as massive staging of punishment on second-story verandas under the chief's gaze, rape in the stocks, ear-cutting, the "joke" of rifle-to-mouth shooting. These are ritualized art forms, he argues, with deep history in "civilized" values, not spontaneous abandon of civilization.

Debt-peonage vocabulary did ideological work. British Select Committee members stumbled on *conquistar* — company speech for distributing goods against rubber, sounding like military conquest in English. Rocha observed "selling of debts" of Indians spoken as selling of persons — a "deformation of good speech." Father Gridilla (1912) recorded a gatherer who refused goods then demanded a black dog: "If I ask for a black dog you have to give me one." Casement noted advances forced onto people who then "owed." Taussig reads this as Amazonian multilingualism transposing nonmarket gift/force into creditor/debtor capitalist discourse — debt fetishism adjacent to the commodity fetishism of his 1980 book.

*Muchachos* — Indian police of the company — sit at the center of the hierarchy of violence. They break any simple racial binary of white perpetrator / Indian victim without removing company and metropolitan responsibility. Colonial society, Taussig argues, bound itself together through fearful and confusing images of savagery that employees both feared and created through narration.

### Colonial mirror of production

Chapter 6's climax is the mirror thesis. Meaning of terror stories lies "outside" like Marlow's haze, not as a kernel in a shell. Terror thrives on epistemic murk and metamorphosis yet needs hermeneutic violence that flattens contradiction into realist fictions. Colonists' stories of Indian savagery and the tortures they devised mirrored each other. Critical representations could be "complicit with what they opposed" in their imaginative heart — a hard saying aimed at human-rights realism as well as company lies.

Travel ethnology enters as complicity's scholarly wing. Whiffen published on Putumayo peoples without mentioning the torture and killing of Indians he studied; he drank champagne in Europe, signed and tore a note declaring Putumayo paradise, and still omitted atrocity. Father Gaspar's Capuchin exorcisms drew power from the evil they named. Rocha equated prolonged contact with wilderness with becoming savage. The primeval jungle becomes "the colonially intensified metaphor for the great space of terror and cruelty."

### Part Two — Healing as image-work

Part Two opens ethnographically: December 1975, yagé with José García, white pupil of Ingano shaman Santiago Mutumbajoy, among poor white colonists and lowland neighbors. García's biography (born Nariño highlands 1925; to Putumayo foothills 1950; cattle near Santa Marta; yagé career after misfortune) anchors fortune/misfortune as the healing entry-point — not abstract "belief."

**Magical realism** (ch. 8) is claimed as epistemology for colonial Latin America: Carpentier, Bloch, Benjamin — imageric knowing, not genre label alone. **Las tres potencias** (ch. 9) maps racialized spiritual power (Indian, Black, white "potencies") in healing markets and visions. **Wild Woman of the Forest → Our Lady of Remedies** (ch. 10) tracks a gendered metamorphosis of wildness into Marian devotion, with official and oral histories double-visioned — "virginal historiography" as Taussig ironizes Church narrative.

**Wildness** (ch. 11) pries signifier from signified; fertilizes curing; appears as female as well as male; challenges symbolic order's claim to completeness. **Indian fat** (ch. 12) follows Andean fat-stealing and wound-fat lore (Taqui Onqoy memory, *nakaq*, pongo/*wamani*) into magical substance and racialized predation fears — "We are all nakaqs" as modern implication. **Surplus value** (ch. 13) double-reads hacienda maleficio and wage envy through Marxian and magical registers; yagé as labor-control and as critique. **Book of magia** (ch. 15) sets print grimoires and devil pacts beside yagé *pinta*. **Filth and the magic of the modern** (ch. 16) tracks migrants, agribusiness medicine, money/food contrasts on the coast-versus-interior axis.

**On the Indian's back** (ch. 18) is the moral topography chapter: silleros (porters), Capuchin road, conquest labor memory, Siona and other vision narratives of carrying and hierarchy. Soldiers' memories (chs 19–20), everyday toughness/tenderness in the shaman's house (ch. 21), Casemiro's tiger (ch. 22), and priests versus shamans (ch. 23) thicken the frontier as lived by veterans, patients, and churchmen — not only by "the shaman" as hero.

### History as sorcery, envy, whirlpool

Chapters 24–26 are the theoretical ethnography heart of Part Two. History enters curing as active force — preconquest dead, rubber dead, Capuchin crusades — not as inert background. Benjamin's dialectical images (picture puzzles that shock) structure how past rushes into yagé present. Envy is antenna and discourse; yagé nights stage epic theater of purgation (Borbonzay and other cases); uncertainty of accusation is structural. The Whirlpool / Remolino / Puerto Asís material shows white colonists as major clients, role reversals from Capuchin to Indian to colonist, and Dona Pola's death-space — healing market on the frontier of agribusiness and army.

### Montage, healer, Marlene

Chapter 27 equates form and theory. Montage: interruptions and cracks; anti-communitas; Artaud's theater of cruelty; Benjamin allegory vs Romantic symbol; Brecht A-effect as *inconstant* oscillation in and out of self; song as ordered disorder; shaman as colonial wild-man vacuity and Brechtian empty stage on which social contradictions act. Truth as experiment laden with particularity, not deep general under surface particulars.

Chapter 28: folk healers embark on careers by healing themselves — "the cure is to become a curer." Florencio walks the space of death in fever, fails to become healer, contrasts with Santiago's practical anger. Power lies in *differences* of shaman–patient joint construction of "credible impossibility," not in shaman alone. Lévi-Strauss's inversion of psychoanalysis is engaged and displaced. Kantian schematism is read *in vivo* as dialogic image-work. Same death-space that petrifies as culture of terror can incorporate carnival laughter (Turu Mama) as oppositional practice. Cofán left-hand origin story of yagé: God plants hair with left hand; Indians discover properties; God drinks and is undone — indigenous invention inside colonial Christian cosmology.

Chapter 29 returns to Cauca: Marlene Jiménez, murdered with Guillermo; Don Chu Chu's plant bags and limited fame; Putumayo Indian power circulating as dream, plant, and yagé practice among Afro-Colombian and mixed townspeople who may never travel there. The book's two geographies — Putumayo terror/healing and Cauca devil/plantation world of 1980 — close as one national field of colonial image-work.

## Extended Design Critique

What the methods section omits if read naively: (1) OCR and archive mediation mean every "fact" about 1910 is already aestheticized; (2) Taussig's medical training and political arrival narrative (romantic guerrilla-doctor notions redirected into ethnography) shape access; (3) anonymity changes block restudy of named persons; (4) yagé participation ethics are asserted through craft not IRB language; (5) women's voices appear (Ambrosia, Rosario, Marlene, Dona Pola, Wild Woman cycle) but the theoretical "shaman" figure remains largely male in reception. Restudies would need both rubber historiography and contemporary Sibundoy/Putumayo ethnography — neither fully ingested in this wiki yet.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Two-part architecture.** Terror then healing is not chronological progress from bad to good; healing **reactivates** terror's image pool.
2. **Archive as ethnographic object.** Casement and Hardenburg are not transparent windows; their genres (FO realism, melodrama) are part of the culture of terror's metropolitan wing.
3. **Anti-positivist epistemology.** Cross-checking fragments alone can ratify illusory objectivity; myth must be left ambiguous (Conrad vs Casement contrast via Frederick Karl).
4. **Marx and Benjamin.** Commodity/debt fetishism links death-space signification to the 1980 devil book; montage and dialectical image structure presentation.
5. **Magical realism as method.** Carpentier/Bloch/Benjamin used to think colonial Latin American image-politics, not as literary decoration.
6. **Ethnographer in the séance.** Participation produces the montage the book performs — method is mimesis of object.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Became a standard citation for colonial violence as cultural production and for experimental ethnography.
- Supplied portable concepts: [[space-of-death]], [[culture-of-terror]], [[epistemic-murk]], colonial wild man.
- Influenced medical anthropology of shamanism beyond Andes/Amazon (often selectively — "yagé montage" without Putumayo terror).
- Read with *Writing Culture* debates on ethnographic authority; Taussig's path is more Benjamin/Brecht than Clifford/Marcus manifesto.
- Bridges to [[mimesis]] literatures (Adorno/Horkheimer already on wiki; Taussig's later *Mimesis and Alterity* 1993 densifies — not this ingest).

## Current Standing

Still required reading for anthropology of colonial terror and for anyone working Amazonian shamanism in historical depth. Best read as a pair with [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]: devil contracts (Cauca/Bolivia) and wild-man terror/healing (Putumayo) as two fetish complexes of colonial capitalism in the same national field. Gaps for this wiki: primary Casement volumes; June Nash already noted for Bolivia; later Putumayo ethnography; Taussig *Mimesis and Alterity*.



### Comparative placement in the wiki

Within this collection the book sits at a junction:

- **With [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]:** same author, same national field (Colombia), same critical-turn Marxist cultural analysis — but different fetish complex (devil contract / baptism of money / Tio versus wild man / space of death / yagé montage) and different primary methods mix (Cauca primary ethnography + Bolivian secondary synthesis versus Putumayo archival terror + multi-year shamanic ethnography).
- **With [[commodity-fetishism]] and [[mimesis]]:** death-space signification and colonial mirror extend Marxian and Frankfurt-adjacent vocabularies into Amazonian extraction and ritual healing.
- **With [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]:** Whiffen's omission, Capuchin exorcism, and the complicity of critical reportage supply case material for how anthropology and "civilizing" missions participate in colonial image-work.
- **With [[medical-anthropology]] and [[symbolic-healing]]:** yagé nights as montage offer an anti-functionalist, anti-communitas model of ritual efficacy grounded in colonial history rather than homeostatic equilibrium.
- **With [[cauca-valley-afro-colombians]]:** Marlene chapter and earlier devil-book geography show Putumayo power circulating in the cane-valley world without requiring every patient to become an Amazonian ethnographer's "informant."

The book's wager — that healing can mobilize terror to subvert it by tripping power in its own disorderliness — remains its most portable and most contested sentence. Portable because activists and ethnographers of violence reuse it; contested because it can be misread as aestheticizing atrocity or as promising shamanic redemption. Taussig's text supports neither pacifying reading: killing is real; montage is struggle, not catharsis.
