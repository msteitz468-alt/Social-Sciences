# Earth Beings — Studies Hub Analysis

> Summary page: [[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-study]]. Source: [[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015]]. Author: [[de-la-cadena-marisol]].

Deep analysis of Marisol de la Cadena’s *Earth Beings: Ecologies of Practice across Andean Worlds* (Duke, 2015; Lewis Henry Morgan Lectures 2011). This hub reconstructs the study as conducted, works load-bearing passages, and maps the afterlife stakes — without promoting theoretical claims to wiki voice.

## Question and Intellectual Context

De la Cadena set out to co-labor with Mariano and Nazario Turpo of [[pacchanta|Pacchanta]] so that ethnography could register [[earth-beings|tirakuna]] (Ausangate and kin) as participants in politics and conversation without translating them into “beliefs about nature.” The project sits at the intersection of:

- the anthropological [[ontological-turn]] (Viveiros de Castro, Descola, Strathern);
- Andean ethnohistory of [[ayllu]], hacienda, and agrarian reform;
- science-studies / political theory (Latour’s Modern Constitution; Quijano’s coloniality of power; Rancière; Stengers’s [[cosmopolitics]]);
- museum and tourism studies of multicultural indigeneity.

The Morgan Lectures 50th-anniversary framing (Foster/Reichman foreword) explicitly pairs the Turpo co-labor with Lewis Henry Morgan’s joint research with Ely S. Parker — an ancestor figure for collaborative ethnography under asymmetric power.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| Question | How do earth-beings enter Andean politics across land struggle and multicultural tourism, and how can ethnography neither dismiss nor romanticize that entry? |
| Unit | Turpo household/networks; ayllu relational emplacement; documents; state and museum arenas |
| Sample/site | Pacchanta (>4,000 m), Ocongate, Cuzco, Lima; NMAI Washington; Machu Picchu 2001 |
| Method | Co-labor; archive (600+ docs); Quechua–Spanish–English translation chains; stories not chapters |
| Period | Field co-labor 2002–2007; historical narrative ~1904–2014 (mining epilogue) |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** The book begins with Nazario’s death (9 July 2007, Saylla traffic accident) — the co-laborer who would have continued the conversation is gone; the Preface is an ending. Story 1 replaces a conventional introduction with agreements about remembering, translating, and carefully co-laboring. Conversations open with *k’intu* coca blown toward Ausangate: tirakuna are invited into the ethnographic situation. De la Cadena (Lima-born, weak Quechua) contrasts herself with Cuzqueño bilingual anthropologists (Carmona, Flores Ochoa) who also appear in NMAI captions as “shamans.” Access is asymmetric: she can read the archive; she can access earth-beings only through the Turpos.

**Archive.** Mariano kept documents of decades-long struggle; de la Cadena treats them as boundary objects and as an alter-archive that does not reduce to liberal historical inclusion. Nazario once nearly burned papers; papers also lived in *pukara*. The project that began as history-from-documents became a study of excess beyond history.

## The Site/Sample/Data

**Pacchanta / Lauramarca.** High-altitude herding community near Ausangate; formerly within hacienda Lauramarca. Wool for global markets at collapsing prices; tourism later outcompetes wool seasonally (~$400/month high season for Nazario’s agency work vs family peasant income often ≤$600/year — attributed figures).

**Mariano Turpo.** Personero chosen by ayllu late 1940s–early 1950s; yachaq; limited literacy (“I make the letters talk”); *puriq masi* Mariano Chillihuani for reading/writing; networks to lawyers, FTC, Hugo Blanco orbit, police, Presidential Palace. Capture, torture, jail. End of Lauramarca 6 Dec 1969; cooperative afterlife; residual memory uneven (Gow ranks him among major Andean politicians; local forgetting also real).

**Nazario Turpo.** Son; excluded from school despite ayllu fight for teacher; cooperative board president 1980 then exit; *chamán andino* for tourism; NMAI consultant; public death and media (*La República* “Altomisayoq Who Touched Heaven” — a title he refused).

**Earth-beings data.** Not interview-surveyable. Presence via *despachos*, *k’intu*, willakuy landscape signatures, political consequences (hail, crops, mining threat), and refusals to explain.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (with analysis)

**1. Tirakuna definition (Preface).**

> “Among tirakuna, or earth-beings—a composite noun made of tierra, the Spanish word for ‘earth,’ and pluralized with the Quechua suffix kuna—Apu (apukuna is the plural) can be the most powerful in the Andes.”

Analysis: The hybrid Spanish–Quechua coinage already stages partial connection. “Earth-beings” is a **translation**, not a transparent emic label; de la Cadena keeps the hybrid visible rather than purifying into “spirits” or “sacred mountains.”

**2. Cannot know Ausangate (Preface).**

> “I could access tirakuna only through Mariano and Nazario… With my usual epistemic tools, I could not know Ausangate—not even if I got lucky.”

Analysis: Methodological humility is structural, not rhetorical. *Suerte* is not “luck” as English synonym; the refusal to re-explain marks the limit of isomorphic translation.

**3. Ayllu as weaving (Interlude 1 / Oxa).**

> “Ayllu is like a weaving, and all the beings in the world—people, animals, mountains, plants, etc.—are like the threads… a runa is always in-ayllu with other beings—that is ayllu.”

Analysis: Shifts ayllu from property-holding corporation to relational ontology. Political speech *from* ayllu follows: personero is not a liberal signifier standing in for a separated collective.

**4. Modern constitution and coloniality (Story 3).**

De la Cadena places Latour’s partition (nonhumans to science, citizens to politics) with Quijano’s coloniality: liberalism and socialism share the humanity/nature divide that makes earth-beings uncountable — they do not even “count as not counting” (Rancière twisted). Analysis: This is the book’s sharpest political-theoretical move; it explains why left allies and right gamonales can share an ontological exclusion.

**5. Picture is not Ausangate (Story 6).**

Nazario’s correction at NMAI — a photograph is not the earth-being — collapses museum representation into partial connection. Despacho at the museum is representation, not relation. Analysis: Museum collaboration produces new publics (Andean shaman persona) while failing material demands (irrigation letter, *puka kuru* water).

**6. Environment vs tirakuna (Epilogue).**

> “to save the mountain from being swallowed up by the mining corporation, activists themselves—runakuna included—withdrew tirakuna from the negotiation.”

Analysis: Cosmopolitical tragedy/success: the mountain “won,” the earth-being was silenced. Political ontology is “subdued partner” when public concern is captured by ecology/economy frames.


## Design and Methods as Conducted — Expanded

### Co-labor agreements

Story 1 details research agreements with the Turpo family: what would be remembered, how documents would be handled, and what co-labor meant when Mariano “helped [her] think.” De la Cadena names the request selfish: she wanted conceptual partnership, not informant extraction. This cancels the bifurcation that assigns theory to the anthropologist and culture to the local — a standing ontological-turn method claim, here operationalized in Andean fieldwork rather than Amazonian philosophy.

Coca *k’intu* openings are not decorative “rapport.” Mariano and Nazario welcomed tirakuna into conversations about the archive, hoping for good questions, good remembering, and good relations among humans, documents, and earth-beings. The ethnographer’s body (chewing or not) became part of the experimental situation of *suerte*. When Nazario refused to “explain again,” he marked that enough had been said for partial translation — not that the anthropologist had failed a quiz.

### Linguistic and institutional positionality

De la Cadena was born in Lima; Quechua is not native; proficiency is weak. Carmona and Flores Ochoa are southern Andean native bilinguals who appear in NMAI captions as anthropologists who “feel and practice” and, in Carmona’s case, as shaman. The book treats Cuzqueño anthropology’s inclusive otherness (self/other collapse) as another partial connection — not as authenticity ranking that would disqualify the Limeña outsider. Translation remains “tangible condition” for all parties; even bilingual Cuzqueño speech on the Plaza de Armas moves between *señor* and *wiraqucha* with non-isomorphic loads.

### Historical method without historicism alone

Mariano’s *willakuy* is not myth and not professional history. Story 2/4 divide material into what is “historically plausible” to lettered politics and what is earth-being eventfulness that historicism would translate as belief. De la Cadena uses Guha’s “limit” to name excess: the first thing outside which modern politics finds nothing is, from the other side, something real. She refuses Latour’s move of simply extending historicity to nonhumans when the issue is earth-beings that are not historical objects in that sense — preferring **eventfulness of the ahistorical**.

### Networks as data

The study’s “n” is not households surveyed but network nodes: lawyers (triple strategy), FTC and unions, International, police checkpoints and night allies, Hugo Blanco and de la Puente orbits, Radio Santa Mónica, Prom Perú, Núñez del Prado / Yábar mystic tourism, NMAI curators (Her Many Horses), First Lady Karp, World Bank panels, ronda assemblies, municipal candidates (Mescco literacy barrier; Mandura literate mayor). Each node is a partial connection site where earth-beings may be present, translated, or withdrawn.

## The Site/Sample/Data — Expanded

### Hacienda Lauramarca chronology (as of de la Cadena 2015)

Conflict runs from early twentieth-century enclosures through mid-century personero struggle to agrarian reform transfer (6 December 1969) and cooperative dismantling in the 1970s–80s. Scale: large highland property integrating herding and labor extraction; *colonos* in racialized bare-life conditions. Gamonalismo names networked local power; munayniyuq names the will that makes people do what the owner likes — overlapping, not identical. Mariano’s first personero task included school building and produced his first jailing — literacy politics at the start of the grievance walk.

### Landscape signatures

Chaupi Urqu / Yana Machay cave hiding; *pukara* as source of life and paper storage; *inqaychu* as earth-being piece that is also all; *santa tira* at reform ceremony. These are not “ritual settings” appended to politics; they are the places that compose the political event with lawyers and decrees.

### Economic measures (2000s)

Attributed figures: Nazario ~$400/month tourism high season; family wool sale example 130 lbs at 10 soles/lb = 1,300 soles (~$400) at peak, vs 2 soles/lb dry season; tourist overnight group 600 soles (~$200) for nine people two nights; collapsed tourist lodge investment ~5,000 soles; family among larger Pacchanta holdings (~5 masas / ~8 ha unirrigated) still insufficient. Tourism is “desperate attempt at surviving,” not empowerment fairy tale.

## Findings in Detail — Expanded Cases

### Unthinkable Indian leader

Mesa Redonda on *Todas las sangres*: left intellectuals denied indigenous politicians in 1960s Peru; Arguedas dissented. Quijano later coloniality of power may self-critique earlier positions (de la Cadena’s reading). Richard Patch 1958 pejorative framing of personero; Trouillot’s ontology of the unthinkable applied. Mariano’s existence as politician was not only ignored but categorically impossible under lettered hegemony — until translated as peasant leader, which enabled agrarian policy while still excluding earth-beings.

### Lawyers and Ausangate need each other

Story 3’s central empirical-theoretical knot: Mariano bribes, organizes, speaks on Labor Day, *and* emerges with earth-beings. Dual ceremonies at Presidential Palace. Ausangate responds to despacho quality/money; Jesus to justice (Nazario contrast) — different relational grammars, not a single “religion” box. Guerra Ganar history: Ausangate is ayllu, not hacienda. Struggle “not only for land”: land as resource *and* tirakuna.

### Suq’a repatriation comedy

NMAI returns remains inspired by U.S. Native repatriation. Runakuna treat them as *suq’a* — other-era beings, dangerous, tamed by petroleum/gas/masses/holy water — not “ancestors.” Officials see celebration of ancestor return. Equivocation is constitutive; de la Cadena does not claim to unmake it (her own suq’a definition is also translation).

### Rondas and munayniyuq state

Story 7: rondas born locally (Nazario/Octavio narrative); target present munayniyuq (theft, abusive officials), not merely “absent state.” Assembly punishes horse thief against police will; 1989 Alqaqucha attack on Turpo family. Julián Rojo forced return as anti-munayniyuq chief. Mandura defends Ausangate against mining as district mayor; leaves ronda logic when becoming provincial mayor 2010 — partial connection of ayllu practices inside state that can also exit them.

### García and the partition of the sensible

President Alan García’s call to defeat “absurd and pantheistic ideologies” treating mountains as gods — education against superstition aligned with mining. Symmetric opposite of New Age sacred-mountain celebration: both, on de la Cadena’s account, produce earth-beings/nature as complex entities under the nature/belief partition. Disbelief is consequential.


## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

- **Stories not chapters** — refuses social-science exposition that would purify “data” from “theory.”
- **Not only** — recurrent operator of excess (land struggle not only for land; mountain not only mountain).
- **Equivocation comedy** — power-laden misunderstanding staged without claiming to dissolve it.
- **Against recognition utopia** — historical reality of recognition with limited pertinence; epistemic disconcertment (Verran) preferred to same-ing (García’s education-against-superstition).
- **Dual protagonists** — Mariano (land/lettered struggle) and Nazario (tourism/museum) periodize two regimes of indigenous visibility.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact — Expanded

### Within the ontological turn

*Earth Beings* is the principal **Andean political** monograph in the turn’s teaching canon, balancing Amazonian perspectivism and Melanesian partial connections. It shows that “taking seriously” is not only cosmological description but a problem of **state recognition, extractivism, and museum sovereignty**. Stengers’s cosmopolitics becomes ethnographic practice; Viveiros’s equivocation becomes comedy under hierarchical collaboration; Strathern’s partial connections become land-reform and tourism history.

### Adjacent fields

- **Political ecology / extractive studies:** Sinakara case as paradigm of environment-frame victory that silences earth-beings.
- **Latin American cultural studies:** Multiculturalism after Shining Path; Toledo indigenist theater; Karp as neoliberal multicultural broker.
- **Museum studies:** NMAI Our Universes spirituality slot; collaboration frictions (Karp et al. *Museum Frictions* cited in apparatus).
- **Andean ethnography:** Retheorizes ayllu with Oxa; dialogues Allen; refuses espíritu translation for pukara/ruwal (notes).

### What the book does not do

It does not deliver a four-ontology typology (Descola). It does not reduce politics to class alone. It does not claim the anthropologist can enact earth-beings. It does not treat Buen Vivir law as transparent victory — *cosmovivir* / *sumaq kawsay* as uncommons commons is distinguished from developmentalist packaging (Epilogue/notes).

## Reconstructing Fieldwork Conditions

### Arrival and lure

De la Cadena was lured to Pacchanta by Mariano’s knowledge and archive reputation, arriving January 2002 (Story 1 also notes a 2003 first-arrival vignette with Rufino — date tension flagged at extraction; co-labor span is 2002–2007 overall). Tourists were already learning about Nazario’s ability to relate with earth-beings; networks that once carried politics now carried tourism. Mariano died about two years after meeting; Nazario became primary co-laborer and friend until July 2007.

### Domestic and travel conditions

Pacchanta’s cold (>4,000 m) and rheumatic illness (Nazario’s wife’s leg) make wage medicines meaningful. Commute to Cuzco for tourism work is the path of Nazario’s death. De la Cadena’s stays include multi-month Cuzco residence (e.g. August 2006 mining demo). World Bank panel anecdote: Ausangate’s “rage” translated as global warming — another controlled/uncontrolled equivocation in development space.

### Violence and sensitive content

Hacienda narrative includes torture, sexual violence, killing, house burning, children’s deaths (Modesta Condori’s history), cave betrayal. Ronda punishment and rustling attacks. These are political-historical facts in attributed voice, not sensational ethnography. Mining threat is existential for ayllu relational emplacement, not only income.

### What methods omit

No time-allocation tables; no household census; no survey of “belief in Apus.” The book’s refusal of belief as metric is itself a design choice: measuring belief would already enact the modern constitution’s translation. Critics may call this under-evidence; de la Cadena’s answer is that the wrong evidence class would erase the object.

## Rhetorical Architecture of the Book

1. **Preface as ending** — death first; composition without Nazario.
2. **Story 1 method** — introduction by another name.
3. **Interlude 1 + Stories 2–4** — Mariano’s generation: ayllu, land, archive.
4. **Interlude 2 + Stories 5–6** — Nazario’s generation: multiculturalism, museum.
5. **Story 7** — munayniyuq as cross-cutting power concept.
6. **Epilogue** — mining now; cosmopolitical proposal; cosmovivir.

The two-generation structure prevents freezing “Andean ontology” in a single political economy. Mariano’s time: hacienda peonage and reform. Nazario’s time: abandonment, tourism, museum, mining destruction. Same earth-beings, different partial connections.

## Additional Verbatim Analysis

**On belief vs presence (Story 1):**

De la Cadena argues earth-beings are not transported as cultural belief; they are present in ayllu togetherness. Analysis: This is the sharp edge against both leftist “superstition” translation and New Age “sacred mountain” celebration — both treat excess as belief under one Nature.

**On co-labor vs NMAI collaboration (Story 6):**

NMAI hierarchical collaboration produces *chamán* fame and spirituality captions while refusing irrigation as “not the business of multicultural recognition.” Co-labor with de la Cadena aims at conceptual partnership; museum collaboration aims at exhibit content. Same word “collaboration,” divergent politics — the book’s internal comparative method.

**On radical difference (Epilogue):**

> “Radical difference is not to be understood as a quality of isolated indigeneity… rather… a relational condition emerging when (or if) all or some of the parties involved… are equivocal… about what is being enacted.”

Analysis: Blocks essentialist reading of the ontological turn. Difference happens at interfaces with the state, museum, mine, and ethnographer — not in a pure outside.

## Counterfactual and Comparative Notes

Without the Turpo archive, the book might have been pure tourism ethnography. Without Nazario’s museum/tourism path, Mariano’s land struggle might have remained regional peasant history. Without Stengers/Viveiros/Strathern, the politics might have been written as political ecology alone. The conjunction is the contribution.

Comparanda already in vault: Descola’s Achuar socialization of nature (garden/forest kinship templates) is Amazonian ecological-symbolic; de la Cadena’s tirakuna are highland political participants under coloniality of modern politics. **Associated_with**, not identity — different regions, evidence classes, and political stakes.


## Current Standing

Canonical Andean contribution to the ontological turn; the go-to case for “mountains as political beings” in extractive conflict without reducing the claim to New Age or to pure legal personhood of nature. Strength: co-labor + archive + two political generations. Limitation: single community network; Quechua mediated; reception not yet in vault.

## Teaching and Use Notes

Assign Story 1 with Viveiros “controlled equivocation” essay for method. Pair Story 3 with Latour *We Have Never Been Modern* ch. on Constitution and Quijano on coloniality. Pair Story 6 with museum collaboration literature. Pair Epilogue with any extractive-conflict case where “sacred mountain” is the public frame. Always date fieldwork (2002–2007) and refuse ethnographic present.

## Final Analytic Judgment (attributed framing, not wiki verdict)

As of this ingest, *Earth Beings* is best read as **political ontology under partial connection**: it keeps earth-beings from being deleted by modern politics without claiming the ethnographer can stand inside tirakuna practice. Its lasting methodological product is co-labor under translation’s gaps; its lasting political product is the Sinakara lesson that environmental victory can be earth-being defeat. Those products survive even if later critics reject “ontology” as a word.
