# The Gender of the Gift — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub. Summary: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]. Source: [[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988]]. Thinker: [[strathern-marilyn]]. Debate: [[nature-culture-gender-debate]].

**Analysis standard:** design table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine sections. Findings in **attributed voice** (Strathern 1988).

## Question and Intellectual Context

*The Gender of the Gift* asks what happens to Melanesian ethnography — and to feminist anthropology — if the analyst refuses to treat **society vs individual**, **subject vs object**, and **property in labor** as neutral tools. Strathern's early hope for feminist scholarship was that it would change ways of writing about culture and society, not only about women. The book is an experiment that "exploits orthodox anthropological analysis as itself a literary form of sorts. Its style is argumentative."

Three strategic fictions organize the account: **we/they** (West/Melanesia as internal dialogue, not pure reportage); **gift/commodity** (root metaphors after Gregory); **anthropological/feminist** (mutual interrogation without subsumption). The gift is anthropology's long Melanesian entry point; asking about the **gender of the gift** politicizes that entry:

> "To ask about the gender of the gift, then, is to ask about the situation of gift exchange in relation to the form that domination takes in these societies. It is also to ask about the 'gender' of analytical concepts."

Against Highlands "sexual antagonism" models that read male cults as culture dominating female nature; against domestic/public universalism; against Marxist-feminist exploitation readings that smuggle natural proprietorship of labor products.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Melanesian sociality without society/individual hierarchy | Comparative techniques of relation | Hagen + Melanesian literature (Highlands, Massim, Sepik-adjacent cases, Vanuatu, etc.) | Theoretical reanalysis; controlled fiction | Mid-1980s synthesis / 1988 |
| Gender of gift and domination | Exchange, gender imagery, agency | Moka, kula/kitoum, marriage wealth, initiation/semen rites, wok meri | Part 1 critique + Part 2 apparatus | same |
| Feminist vs anthropological frames | Knowledge practices | Western feminist anthropology debates | Ethnography of Western categories (Ch. 1–2) | same |

**Not a single-site monographic ethnography.** Strathern's Hagen fieldwork (from 1964) is the experiential base and recurrent case (domesticity, moka, producer/transactor, women's sanctions), but the argument is regional and comparative. Bibliographic horizon ~1985 ("second wave" monographs by Weiner, Lederman, Munn, Josephides, etc.). Present tense and "we Westerners" are grammatical problems she flags — the account is deliberately partial.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Hagen / Melpa** supplies negatives that structure analysis: no male initiation of the Eastern Highlands type; no indigenous nature/culture contrast of Ortner's kind. Domestic domain produces full persons; childhood is not prolonged dependency into independence. Other cases (Muyuw, Sabarl, Gawa, Sambia, Gimi, Paiela, Trobriands, Daulo *wok meri*, Vanuatu *kastom*) are re-read through secondary ethnography — strength is conceptual synthesis; weakness is dependence on others' monographs and on the we/they fiction's symmetry.

**Load-bearing dividual passage** (see [[dividual]]). **Domination:**

> "Domination is found in particular acts of excess, then; in the Highlands these often involve what we would regard as violent behavior… Domination is a consequence of taking action… When an exchange of perspectives is denied between people who are also partners, when a 'big' man beats a 'small' woman…"

**Paradox of agency:** Melanesian persons as objects of others' regard are celebrated; **as agents** they suffer domination because action must reveal others' demands as its cause — not deprivation of subjectivity in Western property-of-self terms.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Analysis as controlled fiction** — Beer/Darwin, Wagner: proximity to indigenous comprehension via forms that belong to anthropology.
2. **Displacement not replacement** — create analytical space; do not fill an indigenous "lack" of society.
3. **Homology over hierarchy** — plural/singular, one/many/pair; gender as alternation form.
4. **Root metaphors** — commodity vs gift extended from internal Western contrast to external cultural fiction.
5. **Eclipse / transformation / enchainment** — vocabulary to replace alienation/conversion of political economy.
6. **Argumentative density** — anti-summary style forces re-reading; pedagogical cost is intentional.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Became the major Melanesian theoretical text of the late 1980s; constructionist cornerstone against Ortner ([[nature-culture-gender-debate]]); transformed gift theory after Mauss for Melanesia; [[dividual]] entered wider personhood debates and later ontological-turn conversations. Teaching staple for advanced gender and Melanesia courses.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable`. Positions in the afterlife (not fully ingested here):

- **Style:** unreadability / elitism vs principled refusal of easy comparison.
- **Reification of Melanesia:** the we/they fiction can harden into essential contrast.
- **Domination understated?** Political-economy and some feminist readers argue structural inequality is flattened into "acts of excess."
- **Exportability:** dividual outside Melanesia is contested.

## Current Standing

Living classic of critical-turn anthropology. Read as attributed theory + comparative reanalysis. Pair with Mauss *[[mauss-the-gift-study|The Gift]]* and with Ortner/Rosaldo for the feminist debate arc. Later Strathern (*Partial Connections*, *After Nature*) not yet primary-ingested.
