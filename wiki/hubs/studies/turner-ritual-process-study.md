# The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[turner-ritual-process-study|summary]] owns
> question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the
> depth. Reciprocal: the summary links here by full path; this page's `study_page` points back
> to [[turner-ritual-process-study]].

*The Ritual Process* is the book in which a Central African ethnographer turned a theory of one
people's ritual into a theory of society as such. Delivered as the 1966 Lewis Henry Morgan
Lectures and published in 1969, it is [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]'s most cited work and the
source of the two concepts — **[[liminality]]** and **[[communitas]]** — that carried his name
into history, theology, literary criticism, and theatre. It is also his most contested book,
precisely because of the move that made it famous: the leap from a [[ndembu|Ndembu]] seclusion
lodge to the Franciscan Order, the Bengali Sahajiya, and the Haight-Ashbury of 1967. This
analysis reconstructs how the book is actually built — two chapters of close ethnography, then
three of ascending generalization — and where its evidence base changes underneath the argument.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Turner opens on a challenge from the lecture series' namesake. [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry
Morgan]], for all that he "faithfully recorded many religious ceremonies," had "a marked
disinclination to give the study of religion the same piercing attention he devoted to kinship
and politics," and had pronounced "all primitive religions… grotesque and to some extent
unintelligible" (*Ancient Society*, 1877). Turner frames the whole book as an answer: to
"withstand Morgan's casual challenge to posterity" and "demonstrate that modern anthropologists…
can now make intelligible many of the cryptic phenomena of religion."

The deeper question is theoretical and autobiographical. Turner had been "reared in the orthodox
social-structuralist tradition of British anthropology, which… regards a 'society' as a system of
social positions." The book records his break: "like other major insights, the structural
viewpoint has become in the course of time a fetter and a fetish"; "the 'social' is not identical
with the 'social-structural.'" The intellectual moment is the exhaustion of British
[[structural-functionalism]]'s equilibrium models — a dissatisfaction Turner shared with his
teacher [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] (conflict and process) and with [[leach-edmund|Leach]]
(structure as model) — and the search for what lies *beyond* structure that is not merely the
Hobbesian war of all against all.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The book has a two-part architecture that its title ("Structure and Anti-Structure") advertises
but its reception often forgets. Chapters 1–2 are Ndembu ethnography continuous with
*[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]*; chapters 3–5 are comparative theory
built from secondary literature. The change of evidence class between the two halves is the single
most important design fact about the book.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How is a ritual of reproductive crisis (Isoma) organized? | one ritual performance | Ndembu, Mwinilunga | [[participant-observation]] + [[symbolic-exegesis]] | fieldwork 1950–54 |
| What do twins reveal about Ndembu classification (Wubwang'u)? | one ritual + informant texts | Ndembu, Mwinilunga | participant observation + exegesis | 1950–54 |
| What is the social experience of the liminal phase? | cross-cultural comparison | Ndembu + Tallensi, Nuer, Ashanti, monastic orders | [[comparative-method]], secondary sources | trans-temporal |
| How does communitas relate to structure over time? | movements & orders | Franciscans, Sahajiya, millenarian, 1960s counterculture | comparative, secondary sources | trans-temporal |
| What do status-reversal rites do? | calendrical rites | Apo (Ashanti), Holi (India), Samhain/Halloween, Nomkubulwana (Zulu) | comparative, secondary sources | trans-temporal |

The **Ndembu method** (chs. 1–2) is the mature apparatus of *The Forest of Symbols*: read a
[[dominant-symbol|dominant symbol]] at three levels — exegetical, operational, positional (see
[[symbolic-exegesis]]) — and treat the white/red/black color triad as the Ndembu classificatory
rubric. Turner reconstructs the Isoma ritual as staging a passage between a "hot" hole of death
and a "cool" hole of life (he even counts the ablutions — thirteen cool to seven hot, a ~2:1
ratio he reads as "the ultimate victory of life over death"), and the Wubwang'u twin ritual as
dramatizing the paradox of *excess* fertility in a society already strained between matriliny and
virilocality.

The **comparative method** (chs. 3–5) is a different instrument. Here Turner assembles cases from
the published record — [[fortes-meyer|Fortes]] on Tallensi *bakologo* shrines,
[[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] on the Nuer leopard-skin priest, Rattray on the Ashanti
Apo, McKim Marriott on Holi, Norman Cohn on millenarianism, primary religious texts on the
Franciscans and the Sahajiya — and reads them all for a single recurrent form. What the methods
section does not foreground is that the persuasive force of the book's *general* claims rests on
this second-order reading of others' fieldwork, not on Turner's own.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The primary data are Ndembu, from the same 1950–1954 Rhodes-Livingstone fieldwork that produced
*Schism and Continuity* (1957) and *The Forest of Symbols* (1967): a matrilineal, Lunda-speaking
people of some eighteen thousand, documented with unusual density of native exegesis and named
informants. Turner's authority in chapters 1–2 is the authority of long participant observation;
his color-triad and processual readings are checked against multiple informants' interpretations.

The comparative data in chapters 3–5 are of radically mixed provenance and quality: solid
monographic ethnography (Fortes, Evans-Pritchard, Rattray), a contemporary participant account he
partly disputes (Marriott on Holi), historical-textual reconstruction (the Franciscan Spirituals'
Joachimism; the Sahajiya *parakiya* doctrine), and journalism and memoir (Hunter S. Thompson on
the Hell's Angels; "Teddy"'s autobiography of the Vice Lords; press coverage of the hippies). The
1977 Foreword is candid that the concepts had by then been "cited repeatedly by scholars in… 
history, the history of religions, English literature, political science, theology, and drama" —
a reception across evidence classes as heterogeneous as the sources.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book persuades as a text, and its rhetoric is worth reading closely. Turner's definitional
passages are deliberately lyrical where a structuralist would be austere. Communitas is "society
as an unstructured or rudimentarily structured and relatively undifferentiated *comitatus*,
community, or even communion of equal individuals"; liminal persons are "neither here nor there;
they are betwixt and between the positions assigned and arrayed by law, custom, convention, and
ceremonial." He reaches for the philosophers and poets — Martin Buber's "I and Thou" and "Community
is where community happens," William Blake's "winged moment as it flies," Zen's "all is one" — and
for the aphorism: structure and communitas are "immortal antagonists," and "the immediacy of
communitas gives way to the mediacy of structure, while, in rites de passage, men are released
from structure into communitas only to return to structure revitalized."

Three moves are doing the analytic work. First, the **binary table** (chapter 3) that arrays
liminality against structure — transition/state, communitas/structure, equality/inequality,
nakedness/clothing, silence/speech, sacredness/secularity — borrows Lévi-Strauss's form while
insisting, against Lévi-Strauss, that "the whole person, not just the Ndembu 'mind,' is
existentially involved." Second, the **etymological gambit**: communitas as "ecstasy," glossed as
"existence," a "standing outside" one's structural status — a move that converts a felt experience
into a structural position. Third, the **dialectical closing**, where Turner heads off the charge
that he is merely praising fellowship: "Maximization of communitas provokes maximization of
structure, which in its turn produces revolutionary strivings for renewed communitas." The
dialectic is what lets him claim the concept is analytic, not nostalgic — and it is exactly the
move critics call unfalsifiable, since any social fact can be read as a phase of the oscillation.

A revealing instance of Turner arguing with his own sources is his treatment of McKim Marriott's
Holi account. Marriott had framed the festival's inversions as "insubordinate libido" inundating
hierarchy; Turner objects that "it is not the biological drive of 'libido'… but the liberated
experience of communitas," which "involves consciousness and volition, not mere instinct." The
disagreement matters because it shows the book's method at the comparative level: Turner takes
another ethnographer's data and *re-reads* its meaning through his own concept, over the original
author's interpretation.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book's afterlife is the story of a concept escaping its author. "Liminality" and "communitas"
became, within a decade, standard vocabulary far outside anthropology — in ritual and performance
studies (via Turner's own later work and his collaboration with theatre director Richard
Schechner), in the history of religions, in theology (the concepts fit monasticism and pilgrimage
almost too well), and in literary and cultural criticism. Turner's own trajectory ran on from here
into the formal four-phase [[social-drama]] of *Dramas, Fields, and Metaphors* (1974) and the
pilgrimage studies with Edith Turner. Within anthropology the book anchored the processual wing of
[[symbolic-anthropology]] alongside the interpretive wing of [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] and the
classification/pollution wing of [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]] — whose
*[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* pollution hypothesis Turner had already
folded into his account of liminal danger (see [[ritual-pollution]]).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

This is not a hypothesis-testing study, so `replication_status: not-applicable`. Its afterlife is a
history of *critique*, not replication:

- **The scale/overgeneralization charge** is the central and still-open dispute
  ([[communitas-scale-debate]]). Turner reports it himself in the 1977 Foreword: "More than once I
  have been accused of overgeneralizing and of misapplying concepts like 'liminality' and
  'communitas'" — which, critics held, "may adequately describe… phenomena found in preliterate
  societies, but have limited use in explaining sociocultural systems of much greater scale and
  complexity." Turner answers with the adage "the proof of the pudding is in the eating," but also
  concedes ground: modern communitas-seeking groups "have not yet developed a structure capable of
  maintaining social and economic order over long periods."
- **The unfalsifiability charge**: because any social episode can be coded as structure, communitas,
  or the oscillation between them, the dialectic can look like it explains everything and therefore
  predicts nothing.
- **The romantic/normative charge**: communitas is read by some as importing a value-preference —
  a fellowship-nostalgia perhaps deepened by Turner's 1958 Catholic conversion — into what claims
  to be analysis.
- **The Durkheim-redundancy charge**: communitas closely resembles [[collective-effervescence]];
  Turner distinguishes it from Durkheimian solidarity (whose force depends on an in-group/out-group
  contrast, where communitas' boundaries are "ideally coterminous with… the human species"), but
  the family resemblance is close enough to invite the objection that the effervescent assembly had
  been described before.

There has been no restudy of the Ndembu material in the way Freeman restudied Mead's Samoa; the
chapters 1–2 ethnography is generally regarded as sound. The contest is entirely over the reach of
the chapters 3–5 concepts.

## Current Standing

*The Ritual Process* is a modern classic whose vocabulary is now ambient — a rare case of an
anthropological monograph whose key terms are used daily by people who have never read it.
Chapters 1–2 stand as solid Ndembu ethnography of the early-1950s fieldwork moment (see [[ndembu]]
Identity Cautions on the ethnographic-present question). Chapters 3–5 are simultaneously the
book's triumph and its permanent controversy: they gave the human sciences two indispensable
concepts and an argument about their limits that remains unresolved. What survives without
qualification is the *processual* insight — that ritual is a phase of social process, not a static
mirror of structure — and the recognition, against the structuralism Turner was reared in, that
"the 'social' is not identical with the 'social-structural.'"

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text is *The Ritual Process: Structure and Anti-Structure* (Aldine, 1969; Cornell
Paperbacks, 1977, with the important new Foreword in which Turner replies to his critics). It should
be read alongside its companion volume *[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]*
(1967), which supplies the symbol theory and method, and the earlier *Schism and Continuity in an
African Society* (1957), which supplies the [[social-drama]] and the Ndembu village ethnography the
ritual books presuppose. The wiki's Turner coverage is presently two-volume-anchored; the later
performance and pilgrimage works, and Edith Turner's own contribution (already flagged in the books
by Turner's crediting her as a fieldwork collaborator "from the beginning of this venture"), are the
obvious next ingests and would let this analysis do fuller justice to the performance turn the book
set in motion.
