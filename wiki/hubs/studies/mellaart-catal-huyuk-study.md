# Çatal Hüyük (Mellaart 1961–65) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[mellaart-catal-huyuk-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All Mellaart program claims are **attributed voice**. Absolute dates follow Mellaart’s mid-1960s radiocarbon framework unless marked as later revision.

*Çatal Hüyük: A Neolithic Town in Anatolia* (1967) is not a dry site report. It is a **culture-historical synthesis-as-spectacle**: four seasons of area excavation translated into a global public image of the Neolithic — a roof-top town without streets, a quarter of rooms as “shrines,” a goddess who births and rules animals, vultures that clean the dead, kilim walls, and farmers who still hunt aurochs on a plain that did not grow their crops’ wild ancestors. The book made [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] unavoidable. Everything later done at the site — including [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]’s reflexive restudy — is, in part, an argument with this text.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Mellaart’s problem was regional and chronological at once. Postwar Near Eastern prehistory, newly armed with [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]], was dismantling the old story that civilization began only in the riverine Fertile Crescent. Botanists had already relocated early cereals to upland habitats; Levantine and Zagros sites (Natufian, Karim Shahir, Jarmo, Jericho PPNA/PPNB) were pushing food production into the tenth–eighth millennia. Anatolia, Mellaart argued in the Introduction, had been wrongly treated as empty or late. His Konya Plain survey (from 1951) and the 1958 recognition of Çatalhöyük’s Neolithic surface material were meant to put the Anatolian plateau into the multi-foci map of early farming and to show that a settlement of **urban scale** existed in the seventh millennium BC — “three or four millennia before the famous cities of Mesopotamia,” as Wheeler’s preface put the public stake (attributed framing).

The intellectual frame is **culture-history plus Childean Neolithic Revolution**, updated by C-14 and environmental archaeology, not yet processual hypothesis-testing or post-processual reflexivity. Mellaart digs to recover **plans, sequences, art, and lifeways** of a named “civilization,” then generalizes religion and social structure from the decorated buildings he took to be a religious quarter.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is the plan and nature of a large Anatolian Neolithic settlement? | Building / building-level | East Mound area excavations (Levels O–X; densest plans II–VI) | Horizontal clearance of contiguous rooms; limited deep soundings | Field 1961–65; book 1967 |
| How long did each level last? | Building-level | Plaster layers per room; 14 radiocarbon samples (as reported) | C-14 series + annual replaster count as duration check | Levels X–II ≈ 6500–5700 BC (Mellaart) |
| What economic base supported the settlement? | Assemblage / specialist sample | Carbonized plants; fauna; trade materials | Helbaek palaeobotany; Perkins/Ducos fauna; raw-material sourcing | Same |
| What was the religious life? | Decorated room (“shrine”) | ≥40 of 139 living rooms classed as cult | Iconographic and find criteria (see below) | Same |

**Field strategy (as Mellaart states it).** Horizontal digging was preferred to vertical trenching because wall-paintings made deep cuts destructive and because the goal was architectural complexes, not a quick deep sequence. Consequence: **virgin soil was never reached**; a narrow test under Level X showed ≥4 m of earlier deposits, read as courtyard floors at the western edge rather than houses. Building-levels were numbered **from the top (O–X)**; Level VI was split into **VIA and VIB** as two cities. Each building had its own walls (party walls as mutual buttressing); continuous outer faces and roof access structured defence and movement.

**Dating mechanics.** Mudbrick life was estimated from **annual white plaster coats** (locally available *ak toprak*), with extremes of ~100 layers in VIB and ~120 in VII read as long occupations ending in deliberate demolition when walls failed; upper levels VIA–I often show fire destruction while still habitable. Radiocarbon dates “place Catal Hüyük X–II between c. 6500 and 5700 BC in a space of eight hundred years” (attributed, Mellaart 1967). Old wood and reuse (e.g. beams) are flagged as risks. **Later calibrated project chronologies revise absolute dates** — treat Mellaart’s BC figures as 1960s research-front, not current wiki-voice.

**Team and specialists (acknowledgements).** Beyond Mellaart: survey companions Alan Hall and David French (1958); textile work (H. Burnham; Helbaek on textiles); fauna (Dexter Perkins Jr, Pierre Ducos); physical anthropology (Denise Ferembach; notes also via D. Biernoff; L. Angel mentioned as pending); obsidian characterization (J. R. Cann and [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]); flint/obsidian industry (Perry Bialor). Photography and reconstruction drawings underwrite the book’s rhetorical power.

**Stated limits.** Only a fraction of the ~32-acre East Mound was excavated; Mellaart repeatedly calls the excavated zone a **religious quarter**, so social-structure inferences are undersampled by design. Workshops “lay elsewhere on the mound.” West Mound is the later successor settlement, not the main Neolithic focus of the book.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Location and scale (Mellaart).** On the Anatolian plateau ~3,000 ft, ~32 miles SE of Konya, on an old branch of the Çarşamba Çay near Küçükköy. Double mound: East (Neolithic) ~**32 acres**, up to ~**50 ft / 15 m** of deposit; West mound later and smaller. Surface discovery 1958: burned mudbrick, ash, bone, potsherds, obsidian tools from base to summit — material paralleling deep Neolithic Mersin.

**Architecture sample.** Sun-dried mudbrick, reed-and-mud flat roofs, wooden ladders through roof openings (same opening for smoke/entry). No streets: movement on roofs. Built-in platforms, benches, hearths, ovens in standardized arrangements; under-platform burial. Brick sizes tabulated by level; black ash-rich mortar thick in lower levels. Bonded brickwork; foundations of multiple brick courses (stone absent in alluvium).

**Art and cult sample.** Wall-paintings from rubbish under Level X through Level II; plaster reliefs densest in middle levels; portable stone and clay statues; stamp seals; hunting-ritual clay animals with “wounds.” Paintings often **sealed under white plaster** after use — white walls were the normal state; polychrome episodes were intermittent.

**Burial sample.** Secondary burial under platforms (not storerooms/courtyards); no extramural cemetery in the excavated sample (contrast aceramic Hacılar). High counts in long-lived buildings (e.g. 32 individuals in shrine VI.10 across VIA/VIB; 28 in house VI.B.34). Red-ochre and mirror burials preferentially linked by Mellaart to shrines.

**Preservation bias.** Conflagrations preserved carbonized textiles, wood, and offerings *in situ* — a taphonomic gift that magnifies craft richness relative to sites without burning. Perishable wealth is therefore not a simple “more civilized than neighbours” score without that caveat.

### Shrine criteria (load-bearing list)

Mellaart’s recognition criteria (attributed): elaborate ritual wall-paintings; plaster reliefs of deities/animals/heads; cattle horns in benches; rows of bucrania; groups of cult statues; ex-voto figures in walls; human skulls on platforms — “all these features do not occur in normal houses” in combination. He admits borderline cases over earlier shrines where continuity of cult is inferred from later buildings. **This list is the hinge of the restudy**: if the traits can occur in ordinary houses, the dualism collapses.

### Economy (specialist voice)

Helbaek: plain cannot supply wild two-row barley or emmer progenitors; husbandry implies earlier hilly homeland; principal crops emmer, einkorn, naked six-row barley, pea (Levels VI–II abundance). Perkins/Ducos: Konya Plain as rich wild-game niche (aurochs, pig, red deer); domestic sheep/goat from lowest layers; Suberde contrast (Bordaz) shows different hunting emphasis. Mellaart theorizes “conquest of the plain” after agricultural competence, sowing in Çarşamba flood pools.


### Sculpture and portable cult images (Mellaart)

Statues in stone (marble, limestone, alabaster, schist, stalactite) and baked clay; raw materials often non-local. Mellaart rejects a simple aniconic→naturalistic evolutionary sequence within the site. Female images greatly outnumber male; he reads aspects of a single goddess (maiden/mother/death-crone with vulture associations) plus a male son/consort/hunter who fades after Level VI in his sample. The Level II grain-bin group — enthroned goddess on felines giving birth — became the type image of Neolithic “Mistress of Animals.” The four-figure schist plaque from VI.A.30 is offered as possible *hieros gamos* plus mother-and-child narrative. Heirlooms and incomplete figures deposited in shrines complicate simple workshop dating.

### Burial arithmetic and “priestly quarter”

Under-platform secondary burial; no extramural cemetery in the excavated sample (contrast aceramic Hacılar). Mellaart’s vulture-excarnation model links skeletal condition to funerary paintings of vultures and headless corpses (Vulture Shrines VII.8, VII.21). Family accumulations: maximum **32** individuals in shrine VI.10 (VIA+VIB); **28** in house VI.B.34; uneven counts imply not every death entered every building. Preliminary notes (via Biernoff pending Ferembach/Angel): few individuals over ~40 years; women and children outnumber men in the burial sample; no clear violent death or trephination in the series reported; childbirth/fever/pneumonia suggested as main causes — all **provisional**. Red-ochre/cinnabar minority treatment; blue/green painted burials sometimes richer; mirrors and belt-fasteners confined to shrine-linked burials in his tabulation.

### Crafts, fire, and the missing workshops

The VIA conflagration (Mellaart c. **5880 BC**) carbonized textiles, wood, leather, and gifts beneath floors — a preservation spike that makes craft look richer than unburned levels. After the fire, pottery becomes commoner (Level V+); earlier reliance on wood and basketry is inferred. Stamp seals with spirals/meanders may have stamped cloth (dye plants madder, woad, weld named as local). Excavated rooms show domestic tasks but almost no production debris for the hundreds of finished obsidian weapons, cloth, and fourteen cultivated plants Helbaek lists — Mellaart concludes **workshops lay elsewhere** on the mound and that the dig sampled a priestly/religious quarter. Obsidian from Hasan Dağ and other sources; flint imports; copper and lead from Level IX onward with slag; Suberde copper awl as regional parallel. Cann & Renfrew obsidian characterization acknowledged in the apparatus.

### Social inferences (explicitly limited)

Mellaart: ordered conservative society (stereotyped house plans, rebuilds on same footprint); mild inequality by house size and gifts; important position of women given goddess cult and agriculture; full-time specialization without writing or (recovered) music. He flags that only the religious quarter was dug — social structure remains undersampled **by his own admission**.


### Wall-paintings as technique and cycle

Mellaart’s painting chapter is as much a materials science sketch as an art catalogue. Pigments are mineral (iron oxides, azurite, malachite, cinnabar, manganese, galena, soot); plaster is local lake-bed white clay; application is freehand without underdrawing; unfinished panels in shrine A.III.8 show staged textile-pattern execution. He divides subjects into six groups (hands; geometric/textile; animals alone; deities; complex narrative scenes; landscape/architecture). The **whitewash cycle** — paint, use, seal, repaint — means most of a building’s life was visually plain; polychrome episodes were festivals or rites of limited duration. Kilim patterns are argued to copy woven prototypes, pushing Anatolian flatweave antiquity into the late seventh millennium (his claim). Hunting scenes with leopard-skin dancers and the deer hunt cycles are treated as ritual performances, not genre decoration. Vulture-and-corpse panels are the visual backbone of the excarnation theory. The Hasan Dağ landscape, with twin peaks and a town of rectangular houses, is the rare Neolithic “map” that later textbooks never omit — even when they discard Mellaart’s eruption-and-obsidian ideology.

### What the 1967 book does *not* contain

As of Mellaart 1967 the book does not contain: virgin soil; full mound plan; workshop quarter; quantitative spatial statistics; taphonomic formalization of secondary burial; calibrated chronology; aDNA or isotope dietary chemistry; or any systematic comparison that treats Galton-style relatedness among SW Asian sites. It is a **first synthesis at the pause after four seasons**, explicitly hoping for “at least a decade” more work. That unfinishedness is why Hodder’s multi-decade project could reopen the same problems with different epistemology rather than merely add trenches.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. Naming it a “town” / “city” / “civilization.”** The title’s “Neolithic Town” and repeated “city/cities” for each building-level do political work: they elevate seventh-millennium Anatolia to urban status before Mesopotamian orthodoxy. Wheeler’s preface amplifies the C-14-era “fashion” as scientific revolution. The rhetoric is persuasive and **scale-sensitive**: large village vs town is partly definitional.

**2. Shrine percentage as spectacle.** “Out of 139 living rooms… not less than forty and probably more” appear to serve religion. The fraction (~30%) is memorable and drove goddess/matriarchy receptions. It depends entirely on the classification rule.

**3. Art as theology.** Pregnant goddess reliefs, breasts containing animal skulls/beaks, enthroned Level II goddess as “Mistress of Animals” birthing a child, leopard companions, and the schist “hieros gamos” plaque are stitched into a single goddess-with-aspects system (maiden/mother/crone). Mellaart writes with the confidence of a historian of religion reconstructing a pantheon from images. Later readers (including [[cauvin-jacques|Cauvin]]) reuse the archive; critics refuse the pantheon.

**4. Paintings as documentary.** Vulture scenes “document” excarnation; the Hasan Dağ panel “documents” landscape and perhaps eruption-linked obsidian ideology. Treating images as transparent reportage is a 1960s move; post-processual work insists on representation’s autonomy and ritual staging.

**5. Closing genealogy.** The final pages claim Upper Palaeolithic survivals stronger at Çatalhöyük than elsewhere (ochre burial, naturalistic art, hunting rites, dentalium preference) because hunting stayed central — and tip Helbaek’s Anatolia→Europe agriculture path as “our common heritage.” The book ends as origin myth for European civilization via Anatolian farming, not only as site report.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Public and textbook Neolithic.** For decades, “Çatal Hüyük” *was* the illustrated Neolithic: roof-ladders, goddess, bulls, vultures. Wenke & Olszewski, Mithen, and countless surveys still orbit Mellaart’s images even when they cite Hodder’s revisions. The book’s plate program — 15 colour and 121 monochrome plates plus reconstruction drawings — is itself an instrument of persuasion: once the Second Vulture Shrine or the Level II enthroned goddess is seen, abstract debate about “complexity” has a face.
- **Goddess spirituality and second-wave feminist prehistory.** The site became a pilgrimage node; Hodder (1999) later treats goddess tours as one multivocal interest among others — a reception Mellaart’s plates enabled. Matriarchal readings that go beyond Mellaart’s own (already strong) goddess-and-women claims are reception phenomena, not careful restatements of his 1967 prose.
- **Cauvin’s révolution des symboles.** [[revolution-of-symbols]] uses Çatalhöyük as the place where Woman and Bull become “explicit theology,” reading Mellaart’s seventh-millennium art back onto PPNA beginnings (analogy risk noted on [[mother-goddess]] and [[catalhoyuk]]). Without Mellaart’s archive, Cauvin’s Anatolian climax lacks its showpiece.
- **Hodder restudy as method laboratory.** The 1993– project is unintelligible without Mellaart: it reopens the same mound to reverse shrine/house dualism ([[catalhoyuk-shrine-house-debate]]), to install [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]], and to rebuild clay/cattle stories as [[entanglement-theory|entanglement]] rather than goddess cult alone. Hodder’s twelve reflexive steps are a reply to a dig culture of which Mellaart is the local high-water mark of culture-historical confidence.
- **Obsidian characterization.** Cann & Renfrew’s work on Anatolian sources, acknowledged in the apparatus, feeds long-run exchange archaeology and Renfrew’s later trade/interaction models.
- **Europe-from-Anatolia farming narrative.** Helbaek’s claim (as Mellaart closes the book) that Anatolian agricultural development prepared Europe’s Neolithic became a durable textbook pathway — later refined, demic/cultural debates notwithstanding, but historically routed through this monograph’s prestige.

### Comparative place in the 1960s dig landscape

Mellaart digs in the same postwar C-14 generation as expanded work at Jericho, Jarmo, Ali Kosh, and the early PPNB Levant, but his **horizontal art-and-architecture focus** differs from deep-sounding sequence hunting. The choice not to reach virgin soil is the cost of protecting paintings and recovering plan; the gain is the first coherent “townscape” for the Anatolian Neolithic. Compared with later salvage and CRM norms, the 1961–65 project is a research dig with international funding (British Academy, Wenner-Gren, Bollingen, Ny Carlsberg, BIAA, and others named in the foreword) and museum deposit in Ankara — institutional facts that underwrite both quality and the “religious quarter” sampling bias.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

**Position A — Mellaart 1967 (strong form):** A planned Neolithic town with a dense religious quarter; goddess-centred cult in architecturally distinct shrines; secondary burial with vulture excarnation; early metallurgy and textile sophistication; mild inequality; Anatolian agriculture as donor to Europe.

**Position B — Hodder-project contextualism (strong form):** Many “shrine” traits appear in buildings used for ordinary craft and domestic life; inhabitants may not have split ritual/domestic as etic categories; microstratigraphy (e.g. Wendy Matthews) shows toolmaking in elaborately decorated rooms; symbolism is entangled with house maintenance, burial, and cattle histories rather than a separate temple institution. Method: multi-specialist, diary/video, multivocal heritage.

**Position C — Chronological supersession:** Mellaart’s uncalibrated/legacy 6500–5700 BC band is replaced by calibrated sequences placing East Mound occupation roughly in the mid–late 8th to early 6th millennium BCE (research-front; see site page). Plaster-count durations remain heuristic.

**Position D — Goddess critique:** Universal mother-goddess / matriarchy readings over-reach the figurines; alternative models (ancestors, initiation, individual identity, toys) compete; Göbekli Tepe’s male-animal monumentality is often cited as counter-imagery for “the Neolithic.”

**Position E — Career-historiography:** Later controversies around Mellaart (Dorak and related authenticity disputes) affect trust in *uncorroborated personal narratives* but do not automatically void co-documented 1961–65 architecture, paintings recorded in situ, and specialist reports. Reliability is stratified: field photography and multi-witness finds > lone memoir claims.

**replication_status: reanalyzed-contested** — material richness and settlement form largely stand; cult classification, theology, social structure, and absolute dates do not stand as Mellaart wrote them.
