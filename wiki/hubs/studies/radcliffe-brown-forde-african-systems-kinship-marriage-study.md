# African Systems of Kinship and Marriage — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-study]]. Source: [[radcliffe-brown-forde-african-systems-kinship-marriage-1950]]. Companion: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].

**Analysis standard:** design reconstructed · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine sections. All substantive claims in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*African Systems of Kinship and Marriage* (London, for the International African Institute, 1950) is the kinship counterpart to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940). Edited by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] and [[forde-daryll|Daryll Forde]], it gathers a long programmatic Introduction by Radcliffe-Brown and nine condensations of intensive African fieldwork into a single comparative conspectus: how kinship and marriage organize social life among African peoples, and what general structural principles can be abstracted once conjectural history is set aside.

The Preface states the double register of the project. On the scientific side, kinship “looms large” among peoples living in small groups with simple tools; comprehensive obligations of kinship direct activities that industrial societies segregate as political, economic, and religious. On the applied side, peoples in “largely kinbound” societies were reacting to “pressures and incentives from another social world”; “the concomitant of culture contact is social strain.” The volume presents itself as pure comparative sociology *and* as knowledge usable by those managing colonial transition — the same dual address that structured APS a decade earlier.

Intellectual predecessors and targets include: Victorian and early comparative evolutionism (Morgan, Bachofen, Maine-style mother-right/father-right sequences — see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]); colonial court doctrines that treated lobola as “wife-purchase”; pure trait ethnology without structural analysis; and the use of modern English romantic marriage as the civilized standard for African comparison. Positive programme: British [[structural-functionalism]] — social anthropology as comparative natural science of institutions; [[comparative-method|comparison]] plus analysis; classification before laws; synchronic function before diachronic change.

The contributor list is a roll-call of mid-century Africanist social anthropology: [[kuper-hilda|Hilda Kuper]] (Swazi), [[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]] (Nyakyusa), [[schapera-isaac|I. Schapera]] (Tswana), [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]] (Lozi and Zulu), [[richards-audrey|A. I. Richards]] (Central Bantu matriliny), [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]] (Ashanti), Forde (Yakö double descent), [[nadel-s-f|S. F. Nadel]] (Nuba dual descent), [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] (Nuer kinship and local community).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What structural principles organize African kinship and marriage? | Kinship/marriage system as working whole | Theoretical sample across sub-Saharan Africa | Condensed intensive ethnography + editorial structural comparison | Field ~1930s–40s; pub. 1950 |

**As advertised:** general view of nature and implications of kinship in Africa; samples of chief varieties south of the Sahara (explicit exclusions: Bushmen, Negritos, Amhara — no studies available to the editors); theory subordinated to field analysis in the case chapters.

**As conducted (critical reconstruction):**

1. **Editorial pre-structuring.** Radcliffe-Brown’s Introduction is not a neutral abstract of the essays; it supplies the analytic vocabulary (structural principles, corporate unilineal groups, marriage as rearrangement, mother-/father-right as ideal types not evolutionary stages) within which cases are readable as “varieties.”

2. **Condensation genre.** Each chapter compresses monograph-scale fieldwork. Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer essay deliberately omits a full marriage account (already treated elsewhere) to focus on local community. Fortes draws on the Ashanti Social Survey (1945–46) and earlier Rattray material he corrects. Richards synthesizes her Bemba work with secondary sources on other Central Bantu groups.

3. **Colonial production of data.** Fieldwork funded or enabled by colonial institutions, missions, and protectorate administrations (Sudan, Northern Rhodesia, Bechuanaland, Gold Coast, etc.). Culture-contact and labour migration appear as “change” chapters’ edges, but the structural core is often synchronic “as of” the fieldwork moment.

4. **No Galton control.** Cases cluster historically and regionally (Nguni/Sotho; Central Bantu matrilineal belt; West African double descent; Nilotic). Shared Bantu expansions, colonial experience, and ethnographic networks make cases non-independent. The book is a **theoretical sample**, not a survey.

5. **Applied framing without administrative manual.** R-B closes by denying that the anthropologist should tell administrators what to do; the task is “scientifically collected and analysed knowledge that the administrator can use if he likes” — a classic pure/applied split that still places colonial government in the implied audience.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### 1. Method manifesto against pseudo-history

Radcliffe-Brown opens by making African kinship indispensable to any understanding of African social life, then attacks the literature’s “pseudo-historical” explanations — invented unrecorded pasts used to “explain” the present. Authentic history of African institutions, he claims, is mostly unavailable: “We cannot have a history of African institutions.” The alternative is comparison plus analysis:

> The method adopted here is neither that of history nor of pseudo-history but one combining comparison and analysis. Social systems are compared so that their differences may be defined and beneath their differences more fundamental and general resemblances may be discovered. One aim of comparison is to provide us with schemes of classification. Without classification there can be no science.

**What this commits him to:** science as classification of structural types; synchronic wholes; function as contribution to working order. **What it refuses:** conjectural origins of clans, mother-right stages, evolutionary sequences. Later historical anthropology would reply that “we cannot have a history” was a research choice as much as an archive fact — colonial archives, oral history, and archaeology can and do historicize kinship. Within mid-century British social anthropology, the passage is the charter for treating the case essays as type specimens.

### 2. Marriage as structural rearrangement, not purchase

R-B’s definitional move for African marriage is to refuse both romantic English models and commercial “purchase” readings:

> … a marriage is essentially a rearrangement of social structure. What is meant by social structure is any arrangement of persons in institutionalized relationships.

He insists marriage payments are legal instruments transferring rights (mund/manus analogues), multi-directional prestations with symbolic as well as economic weight, and not one-shot sales:

> Some people regard payments of this kind as being a ‘purchase’ of a wife … In South Africa it was at one time held officially that a marriage by native custom with the payment of cattle (lobola) was ‘an immoral transaction’ and not a valid marriage. The Supreme Court of Kenya in 1917 decided that ‘a so-called marriage by the native custom of wife-purchase is not a marriage’. … is the result of ignorance … or of blind prejudice, which is never excusable in those responsible for governing an African people.

**Rhetoric:** the anthropologist corrects the governor and the court. Applied colonial critique and structural definition travel together. The engagement-ring analogy (high value, not a business transaction) is pedagogical rhetoric aimed at metropolitan readers.

### 3. Gluckman’s Lozi–Zulu experiment inside the volume

Gluckman uses the volume’s own comparative space to stage a controlled contrast. He defines a class of Central African systems negatively — “by the absence of a corporate lineage” — and sets Lozi against Zulu. The load-bearing thesis on divorce:

> I have surveyed the literature on many African tribes and affirm tentatively that divorce is rare and difficult in those organized on a system of marked father-right, and frequent and easy to obtain in other types.

Against Loeb’s payment-amount explanation and against a pure economic reading of bridewealth as stabilizer (citing Evans-Pritchard’s critique of that functional claim), he concludes:

> Therefore I suggest that the divorce rate is a reflex of the kinship structure itself. The morals and laws which, as Evans-Pritchard says, sanction the stability of marriage, and the relations between affines which affect it, are aspects of that structure…

**What persuades:** clear binary (corporate lineage / not), vivid divorce contrast (almost unknown in Zululand vs frequent among Lozi), and counterexamples that break the high-payment = stable-marriage correlation. **What remains fragile:** “tentatively”; library limits he admits; “marked father-right” as residual evolutionary vocabulary; later cases of high divorce under patriliny and stable marriage under matriliny that force refinement.

### 4. Fortes’s Ashanti corrective and the “crucial problem”

Fortes writes against a double-descent misreading of Ashanti (*abusua* + *ntoro* as equal matri- and patri-sibs in Murdock’s phrasing). His conclusion:

> The dominant principle of Ashanti kinship is the rule of matrilineal descent. … The chief problem of kinship relations among them is to adjust the jural and moral claims and bonds arising out of marriage and fatherhood to those imposed by matrilineal kinship. … Most, however, try to solve the problem by segregating the respective fields of the conflicting kinship ties. This method of dealing with the problem seems to be gaining ground under modern conditions. … This is the crucial problem of contemporary Ashanti society.

**Rhetoric:** structural dominance claim + process under colonial modernity. Matriliny is not a survival; it is tenacious under European economic and cultural pressure. The “segregation of fields” anticipates later discussions of domestic group flexibility and legal pluralism without abandoning corporate matriliny.

### 5. Typology as knowledge product

Richards’s Central Bantu types and R-B’s four ideal solutions (father-right, mother-right, cognatic, double lineage) turn ethnography into a grid. The volume’s persuasiveness as a handbook rests on this grid-making: a reader can place a new society. The cost is reification — types harden into “systems” that later historical work shows as moving targets.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Teaching canon:** With APS, ASKAM became a standard pair for Africanist and kinship courses in British and Commonwealth universities through the 1950s–70s.
- **Descent-group theory:** Corporate unilineal groups, lineage unity, and polysegmentary systems feed Fortes’s later *Kinship and the Social Order* and textbooks of descent theory.
- **Bridewealth and divorce debates:** Gluckman’s structure-over-amount thesis and R-B’s anti-purchase analysis organize subsequent literature (and its quantitative challenges).
- **Matrilineal puzzle:** Richards and Fortes supply key African cases for why matriliny is analytically “puzzling” under virilocal or father-involving domestic arrangements.
- **Double descent:** Forde (Yakö) and Nadel (Nuba) become type references; Ashanti is *removed* from the double-descent column by Fortes.
- **Companion to political anthropology:** Kinship charter beside APS’s political charter — same institute, overlapping authors, shared structural-functional programme.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

Positions (not adjudicated):

1. **Colonial knowledge / applied anthropology.** The Preface’s culture-contact and administrative audience mark the volume as produced within empire. Decolonizing critiques treat “strain” as euphemism and “samples” as administered peoples. Defenders note R-B’s attack on prejudiced court doctrines and the insistence on indigenous structural rationality.

2. **Ahistoricism.** “We cannot have a history of African institutions” became a methodological target for historical anthropology, oral history, and archaeology. The volume’s synchronic systems understate migration, conquest, slavery, and colonial law as makers of kinship.

3. **Lineage theory overextension.** Gluckman’s corporate-lineage contrast and R-B’s unilineal corporate emphasis were later criticized for over-reading lineage corporateness (including for Nuer — see [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]). Practice theory and network approaches stressed ego-centered kinship and strategic choice.

4. **Gender and voice.** Structural jural analysis centers rights of men and corporate groups; women’s agency, labour, and colonial economy appear unevenly (stronger in Wilson, Fortes domestic sections, Richards residence analysis).

5. **Nadel’s evolutionary residual.** Dual descent as “experiment” with success/failure rankings reimports developmental language R-B elsewhere rejects.

6. **Empirical refinement of Gluckman.** Cross-cultural and regional studies have both supported and weakened simple father-right = stable marriage correlations; bridewealth amount is not irrelevant but is not sufficient.

7. **Replication status overall:** `reanalyzed-contested` — not a single study to replicate, but a programme whose cases and principles were reworked for decades.

## Current Standing

ASKAM remains:

- A **primary source** for mid-century British Africanist kinship and for the named ethnographies.
- A **historiographical monument** of structural-functional comparative method — how a generation classified African social systems.
- Still useful for **concepts** ([[unilineal-descent]], [[double-descent]], [[marriage-payments]], avoidance/joking, corporate lineage) when claims are attributed and dated.
- Not usable as **wiki-voice theory** of how African kinship “is”; systems have changed; models have been contested.

Read with APS for the full IAI charter pair; with Fortes, Gluckman, and later descent/alliance literature for theoretical afterlife; with decolonizing and historical critiques for production context.

### How the volume was assembled

The IAI had already demonstrated the market for a comparative Africanist handbook with APS. ASKAM repeats the formula: short editorial manifesto, multi-author field condensations, explicit dual audience (anthropologists and “those responsible for formulating or carrying out policies of colonial government”). The editors disclaim formal comprehensiveness — Galla-speaking northeast Africa is named as a gap; Bushmen, Negritos, and Amhara are excluded for lack of available studies — while claiming that “the chief varieties of kinship organization occurring in trans-Saharan Africa are illustrated.” That claim is a **theoretical sampling claim**, not a census.

Contributors were not a random draw of Africanists; they were a network already linked by Malinowski/R-B training lines, colonial research institutes (Rhodes-Livingstone, IAI fellowships, Sudan government work), and prior monographs. The volume therefore samples both African kinship *and* a particular research community’s Africa. Later readers who treat the grid of types as the continent’s natural kinds import that sampling frame silently.

### What survived as toolkit

Even where the programme is dead as orthodoxy, several toolkit pieces remain active in attributed use:

1. **Corporate group vs ego-centered network** — the distinction R-B and Gluckman force between exclusive unilineal corporations and cognatic/multi-lineal webs still organizes field description (even when authors reject functional teleology).
2. **Marriage as process and alliance** — multi-stage payments, reverse kinship through children, and inter-group compact language survive in legal anthropology and African family law historiography.
3. **Anti-purchase critique** — the rejection of lobola-as-sale is now near-consensus in scholarly writing (while popular and some administrative discourses still recycle purchase metaphors).
4. **Matrilineal variation** — Richards’s insistence that “matriliny” is not one residential/authority package underwrites all later Central African kinship work.
5. **Double descent as analytical type** — Forde’s Yakö remains a teaching case when double descent is defined carefully (distinct rights in two lines), not as vague “recognition of both parents.”

### What did not survive as theory

- Pseudo-history ban as absolute barrier to African institutional history.
- Confidence that a small set of principles exhausts kinship variation worldwide.
- Simple father-right → low divorce correlations without scope conditions.
- Nadel-style ranking of dual systems as evolutionary experiments.
- The assumption that synchronic “social structure” is the primary scientific object and colonial change a secondary disturbance.
