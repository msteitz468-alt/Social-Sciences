## Question and Intellectual Context

*Evicted* asks how poor Americans lose their homes, who profits when they do, and what forced displacement does to families and cities. [[desmond-matthew|Matthew Desmond]]’s opening intellectual move is diagnostic: American poverty research, he argues, had sorted into structural accounts (discrimination, economic transformation) and individual/cultural accounts (family form, human capital), both of which treated low-income people “as if they lived in quarantine.” Books about single mothers, gang members, or the homeless wrote the poor as “invisible” or “the other America,” the ghetto as “a city within a city.” Missing were the **rich people who wielded enormous influence over the lives of low-income families — who were rich precisely because they did so**. Missing too was the question why bills are so high and where money flows (About This Project, attributed).

Eviction supplied the **relational process**: a struggle that binds landlords and tenants, courts and sheriffs, movers and data vendors. Against a century of progressive focus on jobs, welfare, parenting, and later mass incarceration, Desmond’s Prologue thesis is that “we have failed to fully appreciate how deeply housing is implicated in the creation of poverty.” Nearly everyone in a distressed neighborhood has a landlord.

The book sits in late-contemporary U.S. sociology after [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]]’s underclass program, [[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and Denton’s segregation synthesis, and urban ethnography’s public-sociology turn (Duneier, Anderson, Venkatesh). It does not primarily re-litigate joblessness vs segregation; it inserts **housing markets and forced mobility** as mechanisms that produce the residential instability, material hardship, and neighborhood sorting those literatures describe. Culture-of-poverty framings appear as foils — especially when scarcity-conditioned behavior is misread as autonomous subculture (Notes engagement with Lewis and Stack).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How does eviction work as lived process and market? | Families, landlord businesses | 8 tenant families; landlords Tobin, Sherrena/Quentin; trailer park + North Side | [[relational-ethnography]], [[participant-observation]], digital audio, documents | May 2008–Dec 2009 |
| How common is forced mobility? | Renter households | MARS ~1,100 in-home interviews; 84% RR; citywide weights | [[survey-research]] (~250 items) | 2009–2011 |
| Who appears in housing court and with what outcomes? | Court cases / tenants | Eviction Court Study n≈250; attendance for 1,328 cases over 6 weeks | Court survey + observation | Jan–Feb 2011 |
| Magnitude, disparity, nuisance, property | City / neighborhoods | Court records multi-year; PD nuisance citations; 911; property records | Administrative analysis | multi-year through ~2013 |

**As conducted, not only as advertised.** Desmond moved into Tobin’s trailer park in May 2008 after newspaper reports of possible mass eviction; the mass eviction did not occur, but the park remained a recruitment site for people “getting the pink papers.” Trailer without hot water for months despite landlord knowledge he was writing a book. Fall 2008: moved to North Side rooming house via Officer Woo; landlords Sherrena and Quentin; rent $400. Pitched himself as Sherrena’s “apprentice.” Met Arleen, Lamar, Hinkstons through her; Crystal through Arleen; Vanetta through Crystal. Sat in Room 400; helped move; followed into shelters and abandoned houses; attended church, AA, funerals, births; followed one family to Texas; visited Iowa with Scott.

**Data corpus (About This Project, attributed):** more than 5,000 pages of notes; over 100 recorded interviews; conversations with roughly 30 landlords; later MARS and court study. Quotations from recorder or official documents; names changed. Desmond reports abandoning minimal-intervention policy at least twice; the load-bearing case is advising Sherrena not to call the sheriff on Arleen — Sherrena later said without him she would have done the writ.

**Positionality.** White ethnographer in black North Side: “not uniquely exposed to threats but uniquely shielded from them.” Police softened when he appeared; neighbors protected him; people cleaned up and said “sir.” Gendered suspicion from male partners of women he spent time with. These are validity conditions, not confessions only — they structure what was seen and said.

**Mixed-method loop.** Fieldwork generated questions (especially undercount of informal eviction given folk definitions); surveys and court records established magnitude and disparities; quantitative patterns sent him back to notes for mechanisms. Nuisance analysis used PD citations and 911 data beyond the ethnography’s cast.

**Ethnography as definition and cost.** Desmond’s working definition: ethnography is what you do when you try to understand people “by allowing their lives to mold your own as fully and genuinely as possible” — rapport, long stretches, working and playing alongside, recording until you begin to move, talk, think, and feel something like they do (About This Project, attributed). Notes exceeded **5,000 single-spaced pages**; after drafting, he hired an independent fact-checker; he reports the work was “heartbreaking and left me depressed for years.” Two anonymous “friend” material interventions in the book (U-Haul for Arleen; stove/fridge money for Vanetta) were Desmond himself. These details matter for reconstructing the study **as conducted**: dual loyalty, resource transfers, and emotional load are part of the instrument.

**Why the private rental market.** Desmond argues housing research had over-attended public housing and vouchers while “the private rental market, where the vast majority of poor people lived,” remained poorly known (About This Project, attributed). That is a sample-universe claim: the ethnography’s landlords and MARS’s private-sector renters are not residual to the assisted sector — they *are* the modal poor housing experience.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Milwaukee** as America’s “fourth-poorest city” in Desmond’s framing: fewer than 105,000 renter households; roughly 16,000 adults and children formally evicted yearly (~16 families/day through court). Two ethnographic worlds:

1. **South Side trailer park (Tobin Charney / Lenny / Office Susie):** predominantly white poor and working poor; collection strategies; selective eviction; Common Council deal after political pressure; “Handyman Special” labor; high net income relative to tenant wages (Epilogue comparison, attributed).
2. **North Side inner city (Sherrena and Quentin Tarver):** black landlords, black tenants; ~36 units; Sherrena’s reported ~$20k monthly rent intake, ~$10k monthly net after mortgages and water — “The ’hood is good. There’s a lot of money there.”

**Tenant cases** (pseudonyms; documentation 2008–09): Arleen and sons (serial eviction, welfare, child welfare distrust); Larraine (trailer, disability, food stamps stigma); Scott (nurse → opioid addiction → eviction → recovery/housing); Pam/Ned; Lamar (disabled, work-off-rent painting); Hinkston clan (rat hole, later Tennessee exit); Crystal; Vanetta (prison pathway). **Eight families** as narrative spine; not a probability sample — the ethnography’s leverage is process and mechanism, while MARS supplies citywide rates.

**MARS measurement insight:** tenants often denied being “evicted” unless sheriffs and curb piles were involved; Rose and Tim were court-recorded as evicted after work injury but denied the label. Hence forced-move modules rather than a single “ever evicted?” item. Surveys asking only “Have you ever been evicted?” “vastly underestimate the prevalence of involuntary removal from housing” (About This Project, attributed).

### Housing court as machine (Room 400)

Milwaukee County Small Claims Court Room 400 was “the busiest courtroom in the state” (Ch. 8, attributed). At the back, landlords negotiated **stipulation agreements** (dismiss if tenant catches up) — including cash changing hands in the hallway. Roughly **70% of tenants summoned did not appear** (pattern shared with other major cities; some urban courts ~1 in 10 show). Landlord present + tenant absent → default judgment (three stamps). Non-appearance reasons Desmond lists: work, child care, confusion, indifference, humiliation avoidance.

**Demography of the dockets (attributed):** almost half of formal court-ordered evictions each year in predominantly black neighborhoods; within those neighborhoods women more than twice as likely as men to be evicted. Court study: children in the household almost **tripled** odds of eviction judgment — effect equivalent to falling four months behind in rent. Black women accounted for about half of adults in households appearing in court and 44% of those receiving judgments. Desmond’s compressed gender thesis: “If incarceration had come to define the lives of men from impoverished black neighborhoods, eviction was shaping the lives of women. Poor black men were locked up. Poor black women were locked out.”

**After judgment — Eagle Moving.** Specialized movers; warehouse as “giant stomach”; ~$25/pallet/month; average lot four pallets; ~**70% of confiscated lots discarded** after nonpayment of storage (Ch. 9, attributed). Formal eviction cost structure (~$600 range in claims extraction) makes informal removal attractive to landlords.

### Screening as urban sorting

CCAP (Wisconsin Circuit Court Access) displays eviction records and misdemeanors for twenty years, felonies for decades; dismissed cases appear; removal is effectively impossible (“You probably can’t”). Landlord Training Program (DOJ-funded) preached “Screening, screening, screening.” From thousands of yes/no decisions, Desmond argues, emerges a geography of advantage: landlords as major distributors of who gets which schools and streets. Screening that bans criminality and poverty together draws poor families “shoulder to shoulder with drug dealers” in lenient buildings — concentrating problems at the **address** level, not only the neighborhood (Ch. 7, attributed).

**Core mechanism claim (Epilogue, attributed):** eviction does not merely drop families into a temporary valley; it redirects life paths — possessions lost, jobs jeopardized, government housing assistance blocked by records and debt, worse units and neighborhoods accepted, communities unraveled by turnover. Instability is “not inherent to poverty”; poor families move so often because they are forced to. Between 2009 and 2011 roughly a quarter of moves by Milwaukee’s poorest renters were involuntary; once forced moves are accounted for, low-income households move at rates more like everyone else.

**Process findings from the cases (attributed, ethnographic):**

- **Serial displacement:** Arleen’s sequence (door → Lodge → condemned house → Atkinson → Thirteenth Street → further moves) shows eviction as cascade, not event — welfare letters to old addresses, storage loss, child-welfare anxiety, rent again eating ~entire check.
- **Medical debt and addiction pathways:** Scott’s arc (nurse → Percocet → license suspension → “Fuck it” → junkie → trailer with Teddy → eviction → recovery/housing) ties health systems to housing loss; trailer-park methadone and drug access as environment after screening funnels people to lenient parks.
- **Work-off-rent and informal landlord labor:** Lamar painting for rent credit; “hypes for hire” at $6–10/hr — joblessness statistics can mask cash work for landlords (Ch. 10).
- **Disposable ties after the pink papers:** Crystal–Arleen cohabitation as intense, brittle mutual aid ending in rupture; institutions that manage the poor as meeting grounds for crisis ties (Ch. 12; Notes).
- **Nuisance / 911 trap:** abatement by eviction; domestic violence as common citation category; racial disparity in where citations fall (Ch. 15).
- **Children as eviction risk and eviction victims:** court odds; moms avoiding reporting conditions for fear of retaliation; school churn and “love small” emotional defense under scarcity (Part Three).

**Policy findings framed as positions (Epilogue):** right to counsel would raise appearance of defenses and curb frivolous evictions (South Bronx legal-aid cost-effectiveness example cited); universal vouchers would cover the “unlucky majority” of poor renters without assistance (~67% in 2013 figures Desmond cites); homeowner tax expenditures dwarf housing aid — “If poverty persists in America, it is not for lack of resources.”

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. Open with a child and a door.** The Prologue’s snowball, broken door, and Arleen’s eviction sequence convert a statistical problem into a moral narrative without abandoning rates. Within pages: truck-or-curb, Lodge shelter, condemned house, drug-market complex, Thirteenth Street at 88% of welfare check, then the national and Milwaukee numbers. Rhetoric: particular → general → American story.

**2. Name the missing relation.** Load-bearing About This Project passage:

> “I wanted to try to write a book about poverty that didn’t focus exclusively on poor people or poor places. Poverty was a relationship, I thought, involving poor and rich people alike. To understand poverty, I needed to understand that relationship. This sent me searching for a process that bound poor and rich people together in mutual dependence and struggle. Eviction was such a process.”

This is the charter of [[relational-ethnography]] in the book’s own words: method as theory of the object.

**3. “The ’hood is good.”** Sherrena’s slogan and profit arithmetic reframe the ghetto from residual space of lack into a **site of extraction**. Epilogue escalates to political economy: “Exploitation. Now, there’s a word that has been scrubbed out of the poverty debate.” Poverty as low incomes **and** extractive markets — rent rising with wage gains historically; EITC paying back rent; payday loans for regular expenses. The move licenses policy beyond income supports alone.

**4. Cause, not condition.** The Epilogue’s compressed list of fallouts ends:

> “Eviction is a cause, not just a condition, of poverty.”

Rhetorically this is a one-sentence program statement. Empirically it rests on longitudinal associations (hardship, depression, neighborhood quality, job loss) plus ethnographic process-tracing of how an eviction judgment becomes storage loss, address chaos, missed welfare appointments, and another application denied.

**5. Home and hope / policy crescendo.** Civic Tocqueville and Myrdal on home as base of citizenship; then Gideon/Lassiter contrast for counsel rights; then universal vouchers as “housing program for the unlucky majority.” The book’s public power is this arc from court hallway to constitutional and budget politics (homeowner tax expenditures vs housing aid). These are **positions**, not findings — recorded as such.

**6. Disposable ties as mid-range concept.** Chapter 12 formalizes what the narrative already showed: when kin fail, strangers in poverty’s waiting rooms become intense, brittle infrastructure. Concept work links Stack’s kin world to a harsher contingency regime without denying agency or care. Notes add a methodological point: everyday accounts of kin support vary by mood; giving inflates self-worth and receiving diminishes it, so people overestimate support given and underestimate received — eviction crisis lets the ethnographer compare claimed vs activated support.

**7. Locked up / locked out.** The paired incarceration–eviction thesis is a rhetorical hinge tying *Evicted* to the mass-incarceration literature without reducing housing to a side effect of criminal justice. It genders the urban poverty archive: men’s carceral marking and women’s housing-court marking as dual technologies of containment.

**8. Culture under scarcity.** Notes invoke Maslow and Stack against culture-of-poverty sentience: patterned responses to brutal conditions are misread as autonomous culture; resource-poor institutions (schools) encode disadvantage that then looks like “culture.” Desmond’s Larraine “lobster on food stamps” chapter stages public judgment of consumption to reverse the moral arrow toward structural constraint (Part Three, attributed reading).

**9. Fact-checking and third person.** Choice of novelistic third person with recorder-backed dialogue is a persuasion technology aimed at general readers and a credibility claim aimed at social scientists. Independent fact-checking and the 5,000-page notebook are offered as warrants. Critics may still ask what “witnessed firsthand” means for reconstructed multi-party scenes (e.g. fire night partly reconstructed from interviews and records — Notes).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Public:** Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction (2017); bestseller; classroom and civic staple.
- **Disciplinary:** Re-centered urban poverty on housing courts, landlords, and forced mobility; fed Eviction Lab and related measurement (not yet ingested here).
- **Conceptual exports:** [[disposable-ties]]; [[informal-eviction]] as measurement category; nuisance ordinances as housing-justice issue; revival of “exploitation” in poverty talk.
- **Dialogue with prior programs:** Complements Wilson on concentrated disadvantage by specifying a landlord–tenant mechanism of instability; complements Massey & Denton by showing how forced moves sort families into worse neighborhoods within segregated cities; complements Anderson-type ethnography by dual embedding with landlords and by multi-method rates.
- **Within the underclass debate:** Filed as position 7 on [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]] — housing extraction as relational mechanism rather than a third exclusive primary cause. Desmond’s recovery of “exploitation” language reopens political-economy readings of the slum that culture-of-poverty and pure joblessness frames had sidelined.
- **Methods pedagogy:** Widely used as a case of multi-method design: ethnography that generates measurement (informal eviction), survey that returns mechanism questions to the field, and public narrative that does not drop the rates. [[relational-ethnography]] as named stance travels beyond the book via Desmond & Emirbayer programmatic writing (cited in Notes; not fully ingested).

## Current Standing

*Evicted* remains a charter reference for contemporary U.S. eviction research and a model of public mixed-method ethnography. Within this wiki it anchors the housing–poverty mechanism layer beside Wilson’s joblessness/isolation program, Massey & Denton’s segregation synthesis, Anderson’s street-code ethnography, and (where ingested) Wacquant/Duneier-style urban field studies — partial maps of urban disadvantage that should be read as **linked, not interchangeable**.

What survives most securely as research program (pending replication ingest): (1) forced mobility as a measurable object including informal eviction; (2) landlord–tenant relation as unit of poverty analysis; (3) eviction’s association with hardship and instability; (4) nuisance ordinances as a housing-justice problem. What remains more positional: universal voucher design details, fiscal claims about expanding assistance without new spending, and the strongest causal language (“cause, not condition”) when read as program slogan rather than as a specific identification strategy.
