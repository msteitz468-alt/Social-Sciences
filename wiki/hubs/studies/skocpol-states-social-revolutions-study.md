## Question and Intellectual Context

Theda Skocpol's *States and Social Revolutions* (Cambridge, 1979) asks two linked questions: what *causes* social revolutions — the rare transformations that remake both a society's state and its class structure at once — and what explains the strikingly *different regimes* that emerge from them. The book was written as a frontal assault on the theories of revolution that dominated 1960s–70s social science, and its opening chapter is as much a work of theoretical demolition as of construction.

Skocpol opens by carefully distinguishing her object from adjacent phenomena, a definitional move that does real analytic work throughout the book: "Social revolutions are rapid, basic transformations of a society's state and class structures; and they are accompanied and in part carried through by class-based revolts from below." She sets this apart from mere rebellion ("even when successful, [rebellions] may involve the revolt of subordinate classes — but they do not eventuate in structural change"), from purely political revolution ("transform state structures but not social structures, and... are not necessarily accomplished through class conflict"), and from industrialization or other gradual transformation ("can transform social structures without necessarily bringing about, or resulting from, sudden political" upheaval). Social revolutions are set apart, she writes, "above all by the combination of two coincidences: the coincidence of societal structural change with class upheaval; and the coincidence of political with social transformation." This definitional apparatus is what makes France (1787–1800), Russia (1917–21), and China (1911–49) comparable at all — and, as the Critiques section below discusses, it is also where critics locate the book's most consequential methodological choice.

Skocpol groups her rivals into four families and argues (attributed) that all four share a fatal "voluntarist" or "purposive" image of revolution — grievances or interests generate a mass movement with an ideology and organization, which overthrows the old order and installs a new one:

- **Orthodox Marxism** ([[marx-karl|Marx]]): revolution as the resolution of a forces/relations-of-production contradiction, led by a class-for-itself. Skocpol quotes Marx's 1859 Preface — "At a certain stage of their development the material forces of production... come into conflict with the existing relations of production... Then comes the period of social revolution" — and keeps his class analysis of peasant–landlord relations while rejecting the reduction of the state to class rule.
- **Aggregate-psychological theory** (Ted Gurr, *Why Men Rebel*): political violence from "relative deprivation." Skocpol rejects discontent as an explanation of collective action.
- **Systems/value-consensus theory** (Chalmers Johnson, *Revolutionary Change*): revolution from "dis-synchronization" of values and environment and consequent delegitimation.
- **Political-conflict theory** (Charles Tilly, *From Mobilization to Revolution*): revolution as "multiple sovereignty" arising from mobilization and resources.

Her verdict on the whole field is compressed into a borrowed epigram she makes famous: **"Revolutions are not made; they come"** (quoting the abolitionist Wendell Phillips). No successful social revolution, she argues, was ever the achievement of a movement that set out to make it. Skocpol names three specific defects shared by the existing theories, stated in her own summary of the book's argument: "all existing approaches theorize on the basis of a voluntarist image of how revolutions happen"; "social revolutions cannot be explained without systematic reference to international structures and world-historical developments," yet "existing theories... focus primarily or exclusively upon intranational conflicts and processes of modernization"; and it is "essential to conceive of states as administrative and coercive organizations — organizations that are potentially autonomous from (though of course conditioned by) socioeconomic interests and structures," whereas "currently prevalent theories of revolution instead either analytically collapse state and society or... reduce political and state actions to representations of socioeconomic forces and interests." These three assertions — non-voluntarism, the international/world-historical frame, and state autonomy — are the book's spine; everything in the design and findings below cashes them out against the French, Russian, and Chinese cases.

The intellectual context is the **Critical Turn** and the milieu of [[moore-barrington|Barrington Moore Jr.]], whose *Social Origins of Dictatorship and Democracy* (1966) supplied the comparative-historical model — and whose agrarian-class focus Skocpol both extended and broke from by insisting on the autonomous [[state-autonomy|state]]. Skocpol credits Moore explicitly as the modern exemplar of the method she deploys, noting that comparative historical analysis "is distinctively appropriate for developing explanations of macro-historical phenomena of which there are inherently only a few cases" — "the mode of multivariate analysis to which one resorts when there are too many variables and not enough cases."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The book is a **structural, macro-comparative** analysis built entirely from secondary historiography — not archival research and not fieldwork. Its logical engine is J. S. Mill's comparative method, deployed on a deliberately chosen set of cases.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why do old-regime states break down? | State + landed dominant class | France, Russia, China (+ Japan, Prussia, England contrasts) | Mill's Agreement + Difference | 1787–1917 old regimes |
| When do peasants revolt? | Agrarian class + local community structure | Same six cases | Agreement + Difference | 1789 / 1905–17 / late-imperial China |
| What kind of state results? | Revolutionary state-building process | France, Russia, China | Agreement (convergence) + within-set contrast | 1790s / 1917–39 / 1911–58 |

Skocpol's own methodological statement is explicit about the two Millian logics and how she combines them: "one can try to establish that several cases having in common the phenomenon one is trying to explain also have in common a set of causal factors, although they vary in other ways that might have seemed causally relevant. This approach is what Mill called the 'Method of Agreement.'" The alternative — and, she notes, "taken alone... a more powerful method than the Method of Agreement alone for establishing valid causal associations" — is to "contrast the cases in which the phenomenon to be explained and the hypothesized causes are present to other cases in which the phenomenon and the causes are both absent, but which are otherwise as similar as possible to the positive cases." This is Mill's "Method of Difference," and Skocpol states her design choice directly: "That will be the approach of this book. France, Russia, and China will serve as three positive cases of successful social revolution, and I shall argue that these cases reveal similar causal patterns despite their many other differences. In addition, I shall invoke negative cases for the purpose of validating various particular parts of the causal argument."

Her **Method of Agreement** thus treats France, Russia, and China as positive cases sharing a causal pattern despite their obvious differences (bourgeois-liberal vs. communist, European vs. Asian). Her **Method of Difference** contrasts these against negative cases deployed at specific points in the argument rather than as a single running comparison — England (political but not social revolution), Meiji Japan and reform-era Prussia (reform from above), and abortive 1905 Russia against successful 1917. She defends small-N comparison against Harry Eckstein's demand (in *Internal War*) that valid generalization requires large-N statistics, and distinguishes her causal-comparative approach from the "natural history" school (Edwards, Brinton, Pettee) that merely described a typical revolutionary *sequence* — Brinton's "fever" metaphor being her foil.

Two design choices are load-bearing and contested. First, the cases are chosen as "wealthy and politically ambitious agrarian states" never subjugated by colonialism — a scope condition Skocpol foregrounds. Second, she builds **success into the definition** of social revolution, so that only completed state-and-class transformations count — a move she justifies (successful cases have different structural origins than failures) and critics read as selection on the outcome. She also candidly flags the **non-independence of her cases** (a Galton's-problem concern): they share a world-historical field and are causally linked (the Comintern's influence on China; France as a "world-time" model). See [[comparative-method]] and [[france-russia-china-revolutions]].

## The Site / Sample / Data

Because the "data" are national historiographies, the reliability question is not fieldwork access but *which secondary literatures* Skocpol synthesizes and how she adjudicates their disputes. The endnotes (pp. 293–350) show a dense engagement with the standard mid-century historical scholarship for each case: for France, Cobban, Lefebvre, Palmer, Taylor, Labrousse, Bosher; for Russia, G. T. Robinson, Seton-Watson, Chamberlin, E. H. Carr, Shanin; for China, Skinner, Fei Hsiao-tung, E. P. Young.

She frequently takes an interpretive *position within* a historiographical controversy rather than merely reporting a consensus. On the identity of the Chinese "gentry" — a term she leans on heavily to explain both old-regime resilience and revolutionary breakdown — she states the controversy and her own resolution explicitly: "Some argue that they were those individuals who held official positions and/or Confucian degrees... Others argue that the gentry were basically wealthy families, especially landlords... My view is that old-regime China was an inextricable amalgam of both of these. The dominant agrarian class depended upon the administrative/military backing of, and employment opportunities within, the Imperial state. And ruling dynasties depended upon local class dominants to extend controls over and appropriate resources from the huge, unwieldy agrarian expanse that was China." The definition is not incidental; it is what lets her argue that the gentry could act simultaneously as a class *and* as an arm of the state — the load-bearing ambiguity for her whole account of Chinese old-regime breakdown.

A parallel case is the Russian **obshchina** (peasant land commune), whose legal-communal structure she treats as the pivot explaining why the Russian revolution's agrarian settlement took a leveling, egalitarian form rather than the property-respecting French pattern: unlike French villages, whose common lands existed against a backdrop of private property, in Russia "'all land belonged to God,' and the peasant community as a whole strove to gain access to as much as possible and then distribute it roughly equally to households... all land allocations were only temporary, until the next periodic repartition." She quotes a contemporary source that "the 'land commune, coming alive with quite exceptional force, was undoubtedly the basic ideological kernel of the social mechanism which in fact carried out the agrarian revolution within the peasantry itself.'" These institutional characterizations — themselves contested among specialists in each national historiography — are exactly what a macro-comparativist must take largely on faith from secondary sources, since she does not undertake independent archival verification of any single case.

The evidence class throughout is **textual/secondary**: Skocpol is downstream of the historians, and her macro-claims are only as good as the syntheses she selects. This is the pivot of the historians' critique (below): a comparativist reading national literatures cannot resolve the primary-source disputes those literatures contain, and may harden contested readings into structural "facts."

## Findings in Detail

All findings are **attributed** to Skocpol (1979).

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Old-regime breakdown = international military pressure + dominant-class leverage blocking reform | Comparison of Bourbon France, Manchu China, Romanov Russia vs. Meiji Japan / Prussia | Foundational; broadly influential, contested on ideology/agency |
| France & China fell by *deposition* (dominant-class reaction); Russia by *dissolution* (WWI defeat) | Case narratives (parlements/1787; gentry assemblies/1911; WWI collapse/1917) | Widely cited; historians dispute case specifics |
| Peasant revolt depends on community solidarity + autonomy + relaxed state coercion, NOT grievance/ideology | French village vs. Russian obshchina vs. Chinese marketing community; England/Germany negatives | Influential; challenged by rational-choice and cultural accounts |
| China's peasants lacked autonomous solidarity → revolution required CCP/Red Army from outside | Skinner's marketing communities; Fei on family isolation; Taiping's gentry-dependence | Durable comparative insight |
| Revolutionary outcomes = state-building under international threat, not ideology realized | Convergence of France/Russia/China on stronger centralized states | Signature claim; the main target of the agency/ideology critique |
| The state is potentially autonomous, not reducible to class | Theoretical (Weber, Hintze, Tocqueville); the three cases | Launched "bring the state back in"; debated vs. Marxist relative autonomy |

The **causal argument** (Chs. 2–3) is conjunctural: state breakdown and autonomous peasant revolt are each necessary and neither sufficient. Old regimes broke down where a landed class with extralocal, corporate leverage sat inside a not-fully-bureaucratic state (France's parlements; China's gentry and 1908–10 assemblies) and reacted against modernizing reform, or — as in Russia — where a strong autocracy overrode its weak nobility yet could not modernize its agrarian base fast enough to survive total war. Peasant revolt then transformed class relations where agrarian structure supplied solidarity and autonomy: the French village cohered against seigneurs but respected private property (so dues were abolished, land not widely redistributed); the Russian obshchina, holding that "all land belonged to God," produced a *leveling* redistribution in 1917; the Chinese peasantry, fragmented within gentry-led marketing communities, could not revolt autonomously at all.

The **outcome argument** (Chs. 4–7) is that revolutions are consummated by state-building, and that the *kind* of state tracks how revolutionaries reached the peasantry, not their ideology. France built a professional-bureaucratic state atop a market society; Russia, its Bolsheviks lacking any rural base after autonomous peasant seizures, built a dictatorial party-state and — under international insecurity plus an inherited heavy-industrial base — turned to the Stalinist "revolution from above"; China's Communists, having built the revolution through village-level penetration, produced a relatively decentralized mass-mobilizing party-state with direct ties to the villages ("participatory" vs. Soviet "command" mobilization).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Skocpol's prose persuades by a few repeated maneuvers. The first is the **structural inversion** of common sense, delivered in aphorism: "revolutions are not made; they come." The second is the reframing of the state as an actor in its own right: states are "administrative and coercive organizations... potentially autonomous from... socioeconomic interests and structures," and are "fundamentally Janus-faced, with an intrinsically dual anchorage in class-divided socioeconomic structures and an international system of states." The Janus image does real argumentative work — it lets her keep Marxist class analysis while denying Marxism the state.

A third move is the **disciplining of the cases by the negatives**. The chapter on old regimes is rhetorically anchored by Japan and Prussia: their reform-from-above shows that international pressure alone does not cause revolution, isolating dominant-class leverage as the variable. Lenin's epigraph — "For a revolution to break out it is not enough for the 'lower classes to refuse' to live in the old way; it is necessary also that the 'upper classes should be unable' to live in the old way" — is enlisted for a thoroughly non-Leninist, structural conclusion.

A fourth move, most visible in the outcome chapters, is the **structural rescue of the villain**. Explaining Stalinism, Skocpol explicitly refuses both a purely ideological and a purely personalist account: "There was no abstract, general 'imperative of industrialization' at work here. But there were imperatives of state-propelled, heavy industrialization, undertaken by a regime with a narrow and precarious political basis." She grants that "Stalin's drive to establish and maintain his own personal dictatorship" is "the most straightforward 'explanation'" of the Purges — then subsumes it under the institutional fact that the coercive apparatus already existed from collectivization. This is the book at its most powerful and its most exposed: the same move that makes the outcomes intelligible as structure is what critics call the erasure of agency.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Skocpol is careful, in her international-context argument, to flag the non-independence of her positive cases directly rather than let a reader discover it: she notes that social revolutions occur "within world-historically developing international structures" and that "these phenomena occur in unique world-historical" settings — France's 1789 model was consciously available to later revolutionaries, and the Chinese Communist Party's relationship to the Comintern was a direct historical tie, not an independent replication of a French or Russian "pattern." This is the book's own version of Galton's problem, addressed by argument rather than statistical control: the cases are causally linked through a shared and evolving world-historical field, so their agreement is not simply three independent draws confirming one underlying law but also, in part, one case learning from and reacting to another. See [[comparative-method]].

*States and Social Revolutions* was received almost immediately as a landmark. It won the American Sociological Association's 1980 award for a distinguished contribution to scholarship and became one of the most assigned and most cited works in late-20th-century sociology. Its impact ran on three tracks. It **reset comparative-historical sociology**, modeling rigorous small-N causal comparison for a generation. It **put [[state-autonomy|state autonomy]] at the center** of political sociology, feeding directly into the collective manifesto *Bringing the State Back In* (Evans, Rueschemeyer & Skocpol, 1985), which generalized the book's state-centered analytic into a research program. And it **reframed revolution studies** around structure, states, and international context, displacing the grievance- and value-centered theories of the previous generation. Even scholars who rejected its determinism adopted its questions and its vocabulary.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

The book's afterlife is a series of major, well-defined disputes — recorded here as positions with their evidence.

- **The agency-and-ideology critique (the central one).** William H. Sewell Jr. ("Ideologies and Social Revolutions," 1985) argued that Skocpol's structuralism cannot account for the *content* of revolutions — that ideology is not epiphenomenal decoration but constitutive of what revolutionaries do and of the states they build (the very idea of "revolution" being a cultural creation of 1789). Skocpol replied ("Cultural Idioms and Political Ideologies," 1985), conceding ideology a larger role as "cultural idiom" while defending the structural frame against a return to voluntarism. This exchange is the locus classicus of the [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] in revolution studies.
- **The Marxist critique.** Michael Burawoy ("Two Methods in Search of Science," 1989) contrasted Skocpol's positivist Millian comparison unfavorably with Trotsky's dialectical method, arguing that her focus on the state brackets the class dynamics and internal contradictions that a Marxist analysis foregrounds, and that her method cannot handle the reflexive, theory-laden character of the cases.
- **The historians' objection.** Specialists in each national historiography (and comparativists sympathetic to primary research) argued that macro-causal claims built on selected secondary syntheses harden contested readings — that a comparativist cannot adjudicate the source disputes internal to the literatures she synthesizes.
- **The rational-choice challenge.** Collective-action theorists disputed the treatment of peasant revolt as structurally released rather than strategically chosen, pressing the free-rider problem Skocpol's community-solidarity account brackets.
- **Selection on the outcome.** Building success into the definition of [[social-revolution]] and comparing only completed revolutions invites the objection that the design cannot distinguish causes of revolution from causes of *successful* revolution — a standard methodological worry about the small, outcome-selected sample.
- **Scope, conceded by the author.** In the Conclusion Skocpol herself denies that the France/Russia/China arguments constitute "a 'general theory of revolutions.'" She distinguishes the "classic" imperial-state cases from the colonial and dependent-country revolutions (Mexico, Vietnam, Cuba, Algeria, Angola, Ethiopia) governed by different mechanisms — colonial legacies, foreign patronage, and a post-WWII diffusion of professional militaries that, she argues (citing Katherine Chorley), makes social revolution less likely and coups more frequent.

## Current Standing

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The book remains a living classic and the reference point for comparative-historical revolution studies, but its strong structuralism has been qualified rather than sustained. The "fourth generation" of revolution theory (Goldstone, Foran, and others) restored agency, ideology, leadership, demography, and non-agrarian and negotiated revolutions that the 1979 frame downplayed — a shift the 1989 revolutions, which Skocpol's model did not anticipate, accelerated. What has **survived** is durable: the state-centered analytic and the concept of [[state-autonomy]]; the demonstration that international and geopolitical context is constitutive of domestic upheaval; the insistence that revolutionary *outcomes* be explained by state-building rather than read off ideology; and the model of disciplined small-N comparison itself. The [[structural-theory-of-revolution]] it founded is `status: active` as a tradition even where its determinism has been softened.
