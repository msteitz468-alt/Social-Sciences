# High Religion — Studies Hub Analysis

> Summary page: [[ortner-high-religion-study]]. Source: [[ortner-high-religion-1989]]. Author: [[ortner-sherry|Sherry B. Ortner]].

Graduate-level analysis of Ortner’s historical ethnography of Sherpa temple and monastery foundings and her ethnographic expansion of [[practice-theory]]. All interpretive claims below are **Ortner’s** unless marked as later reception.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Ortner’s surface question is concrete: **why did the [[sherpa|Sherpas]] of Solu-Khumbu found celibate Buddhist monasteries in the early twentieth century**, after centuries of “folk” Tibetan Buddhism in which married village lamas (*lama*) conducted household and temple ritual? She frames the foundings as the **birth of a new institutional form** — full-time celibate monks and nuns, withdrawal from productive labor, monastic upgrading of popular religion — and defers the *effects* of that upgrade to a companion project (cited in-text as Ortner n.d.b).

The deeper question is a practice question: *how did structural and historical forces become intentional human activity with long-term consequences for structure and history?* Ortner situates the book explicitly in the late-1980s practice moment — [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] (1979), [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] (1977), [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (1981), Berger and Luckmann’s triad (“Society is a human product. Society is an objective reality. Man is a social product”) — and against the then-live split between **political-economy** anthropology (external capitalism/colonialism as explanation) and **ethnographic / structural history** (cultural mediation of external forces). She refuses a soft “both/and” synthesis of subjectivism and objectivism; she urges keeping the opposition **inside practice theory as a translation framework** with two moments — objectivist (system/structure constituting or confronting actors) and subjectivist (actors investing the world with order, meaning, and value).

Intellectual neighbors she names include [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]’s *Negara* (fixed ideal reconstructed over centuries) and Sahlins’s *Historical Metaphors* (Cook and the Makahiki script) as historical uses of cultural patterning; her own earlier “key scenarios” work (1970/1973) on Sherpa hospitality; Schieffelin’s cultural scenarios; Turner’s root paradigms. The book is thus both a Solu-Khumbu ethnohistory and a **worked example** of anthropological practice theory after her 1984 review essay “Theory in Anthropology since the Sixties.”

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Element | As conducted (Ortner’s account) |
|---|---|
| Core question | Who built and filled the monasteries, under what constraints and provocations? |
| Unit | Solu-Khumbu Sherpa society across ~early 16th c. settlement → early 20th c. foundings (with Thami celibacy conversion ~1952 as coda) |
| Primary campaign | 1979, five months, Khumbu + Solu + Kathmandu; informants selected for special knowledge of foundings |
| Prior base | 1966–68 general ethnography (17 months); 1976 film/incidental (4 months) |
| Methods | Almost entirely oral history — personal memories and founding stories; documents played a **minor** role (limited access to monastery records; sparse tax lists; difficult Nepal archives) |
| Secondary ethnohistory | Oppitz (migration, chronology); von Fürer-Haimendorf; Sangye Tenzing’s Sherpa religious history (1971); Regmi on taxation; state documents via secondary channels |
| Analytic frame | Historical [[practice-theory]]: contradictions + [[cultural-schema|cultural schemas]] + political economy of state/Raj |

Ortner reports a sharp **methodological contrast** between earlier ethnographic elicitation and 1979 oral history. Questions about “Sherpa custom” often seemed meaningless; expert lamas criticized topic-jumping. A “history of Sherpa religion,” by contrast, made emic sense: Sherpa historical stories cluster around religious foundings and founders’ lives. Tourism, mountaineering, and continuous researcher presence had, she argues, raised collective historical self-consciousness between the 1950s (von Fürer-Haimendorf’s “scant interest in the past”) and 1971 (Sangye Tenzing’s written religious history after multi-year *chayik* collecting).

Writing, not fieldwork, was the harder phase. Assembling multi-version, poorly dated oral narratives into a coherent account of the first two monastery foundings took about two months. Ortner diagnoses the difficulty theoretically: her prior sense of “analysis” was **static** (what things mean and how they hang together) rather than **generative** (how things produce other things) — the problem that practice theory is meant to solve.

**Reliability cautions (as Ortner herself states them):** early history is highly speculative; some texts may be modern compositions; *chayik* are often copies with precision compromised by loss; founding stories are *mythes de référence* as much as chronicles. She uses texts **twice**: for tentative reconstruction *and* for eliciting structural contradictions and cultural schemas.

## The Site/Sample/Data

**Place.** Solu-Khumbu in northeast Nepal: **Khumbu** (highest, coldest, northernmost), **Solu** (lower, warmer, southernmost), **Pharak** between them. High-altitude ethnically Tibetan highlanders (Ortner’s institutional sketch: ~8,500–14,500 ft), one minority within a Hindu kingdom; traditional economy combining agriculture, yak/cow herding, and salt/rice trade with Tibet; private family property in land and animals; patrilineal clans regulating marriage.

**Chronology anchors (Ortner’s approximate chronology, attributed):** ~1480 leave Kham; 1533 into Nepal/Khumbu; 1553 Solu settlement; 1667 Pangboche temple; late-17th-c. noncelibate temple cluster; 1717 submission to Sen king; 1769 Gorkha Kathmandu; 1772 Gorkha acquisition of Solu-Khumbu; 19th-c. Raj/Rana enrichment pathways; **1916** Tengboche; **1923** Chiwong; **1925–28** Devuche nunnery; **~1952** Thami conversion to celibate monastery.

**People (named founders and rivals, as reconstructed).** Early: Lama Sangwa Dorje (Pangboche folk hero), Zongnamba (rival pembu), Dorje Zangbu vs Lama Gombu (Zhung). Twentieth century: Karma, Kusang, Gembu Tsepal (Tengboche lay sponsors); Lama Gulu; Zatul Rinpoche; Sangye (Chiwong); Kusho Tulku; Ngawang Samden (Devuche nuns’ initiative); Tushi Rinpoche (Thami ruling). Emic strata: **[[big-people-small-people|big people / small people]]**; political offices *pembu* / *gembu*; personal-power vocabulary (*tsachermu*, *ongchermu*, etc.).

**Regional representation caveat.** Ortner records divergent ethos portraits: von Fürer-Haimendorf (Khumbu, 1950s) closer to the cooperative mountaineer stereotype; her early Solu work found more conflict and non-cooperation. She later treats the difference as **regional**, not purely observer effect — and refuses a single timeless “Sherpa character.”

### The schema (seven steps)

From the Zhung founding story (Lama Gombu’s attempted murder of Dorje Zangbu; flight; Kutsap Ternga protector; magical contest; temple founding), Ortner extracts:

1. Political and/or fraternal **rivalry**  
2. Struggle; rival gains the upper hand  
3. Hero **departs** and acquires a powerful **protector**  
4. Returns and **defeats** the rival with the protector’s aid  
5. Acquires the rival’s subjects  
6. Rival humiliated, leaves permanently  
7. Hero **founds a temple** (institutionalizes hierarchy of protector above / followers below)

She argues the same plot structures Pangboche tales and is **enacted** (with variable distance — manipulated, driven, or interpretive) in twentieth-century careers: Karma’s Tengboche path after displacement by Sangye; Sangye’s Chiwong path as challenger to Karma. Actors in different positions **read** the schema differently: big people for legitimation; small people for challenge to illegitimate authority; elite children for monastic exit.

### Political economy

The Nepal state, Ortner argues, played a **double game**: support big people as tax agents; curb them as potential rivals and as drivers of peasant flight. Net effect: heighten the big/small contradiction — ascendancy of the big sector *and* chronic weakness of its legitimacy. Nineteenth-century British Darjeeling and trade enrichment pathways, plus Rana-era squeeze, further reconfigure wealth and office. The potato (mid-19th c. staple, per Ortner’s best guess ~1840–1860) buffers taxes and turns more wage income into surplus. These are `contributed_to` conditions for foundings, not sole `caused_by` environmental or colonial determination — cultural mediation remains load-bearing.

### Merit, power, and polyvalent hegemony

Temple founding is, in Sherpa talk Ortner records, **both ego and selflessness** — prestige and merit “more or less in the same breath.” The schema constructs a mode in which power is not merely egotistical and humility not merely impotent (throne-behind-the-throne imagery). Drawing on Lévi-Strauss (mediation) and structural Marxism (legitimation) but insisting on **political ambiguity**, she treats the schema-plus-practices complex as a hegemony that can legitimate dominance *or* teach wit, challenge, and moral personhood depending on reader and circumstance (Foucault’s tactical polyvalence).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Surface mystery → practice theory.** The monastery founding “who and why” is the narrative engine; the four keywords (practice, structure, actor, history) are the analytic chassis.  
2. **Events as symbols.** Foundings move history *and* say something; eventness depends partly on meaning.  
3. **Dual use of folklore.** Neither pure chronicle nor pure myth — reconstruction *and* schema/contradiction elicitation.  
4. **Against soft mediation.** Practice theory as controlling translation, not merging subjectivism and objectivism into mush.  
5. **Middle path on schema strength.** Soft (external model for interest-maximizers) vs hard (deeply embedded identity drivers) vs Ortner’s **variable distance** between actor and culture.  
6. **Valorization expansion.** Classic Weberian legitimation is only the dominant’s form of a wider claim to social value available at every level.  
7. **Subjectivity of bigness.** Feeling big is analytically weighty even when objective status gains lag — a deliberate defense of cultural/subjective analysis against pure political economy.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (with analysis)

**Practice defined (Ch. I).** Ortner writes that any form of human action counts as practice “insofar as the analyst recognized it as reverberating with features of asymmetry, inequality, domination, and the like in its particular historical and cultural setting,” and that “Practice emerges from structure, it reproduces structure, and it has the capacity to transform structure,” with the caveat that “only in historical contexts” is the practice–structure relation “fully played out.” Analysis: this is a **political** definition of practice (Foucaultian breadth of “the political”), not a synonym for “what people do.” It also commits her to history as the privileged laboratory of practice theory — a deliberate step beyond Bourdieu’s more synchronic *Outline*.

**Subject/object cusp (Ch. I close).** “Practice theory always has two moments, one largely objectivist and one largely subjectivist. … This book is situated on this subject/object cusp, and is concerned with these inside/outside translations. The Sherpa monasteries were built by actors who creatively used a world that was using them.” Analysis: the last sentence is the book’s emblem. It refuses both pure victimology (world system writes the Sherpas) and pure voluntarism (founders invent institutions from free will). The monasteries are **joint products** of constraint and creativity.

**Schema enters the event (Ch. VII).** Even when actors are not “enacting” a schema, “others will tend to interpret events as if the actors had in fact been following the schema. … if observers would tend to interpret events in that way, so of course would at least some of the participants. Thus the schema may not begin as part of the events, but insofar as it becomes part of a participant’s interpretations, then it enters the event and begins to shape it.” Analysis: this is Ortner’s anti-overfit clause. Schema is not a hidden code dictating behavior; it is a **public interpretive resource** that can retroactively organize action once participants adopt it — a softer mechanism than Bourdieuian habitus, closer to Sahlinsian structure of the conjuncture.

**Legitimacy as bigness-with-humility (Ch. VII).** Enacting the founding schema “could re-infuse their positions with bigness, insofar as this schema shows the hero to be both powerful and altruistic, capable of protecting others because of his potency, but humble in his desire to serve rather than dominate. This blending of bigness and smallness is, I argued earlier, the essence of ‘legitimacy’ in Sherpa culture.” Analysis: merit and prestige are not rival motives to be disaggregated; the cultural ideal is their fusion. That fusion is why monastery founding works as repair for eroded political legitimacy.

**Valorization umbrella (Ch. VIII).** “I propose using ‘valorization’ as an umbrella term covering all the claims of legitimacy, authenticity, social value, ‘qualification’ … made by actors at any level of society. ‘Legitimation’ is one kind of (claim of) valorization, the kind sought or made by dominant actors…” Analysis: this is the book’s major conceptual extension beyond Weberian legitimation. Small people and monks/nuns are not outside the legitimation problem; they seek **recognition as persons of social consequence** under the same contradiction-system that drives big-people prestige politics.

**Practice question restated (Ch. X).** “Why did the Sherpas found their monasteries? I translated this question into a question of practice: How did various structural and historical forces come together in the actions of particular people at a particular historical moment?” Analysis: the translation *is* the method claim. Narrative ethnohistory is not an illustration of theory applied after the fact; the history *is* the theory’s demonstration.

**Three usages of practice (Ch. X).** Ortner distinguishes (1) routine/everyday practice (internalization and reproduction — e.g. grounding the schema in fraternal, political, and ritual practice); (2) intentional action (structurally constituted projects — the bulk of the founding careers); (3) **praxis** in a Marxist sense — sustained engagement on an alternative logic (Darjeeling wage labor; monastic experience itself) that can reopen structure. Analysis: this triad prevents practice theory from collapsing into either “everything is practice” mush or interest-maximizing intentionalism alone.

**Name/reputation as culturally recognized desire (Ch. X).** Contradictions generate a chronic desire “which they recognize culturally as a desire to make or protect one’s ‘name’ or ‘reputation,’ and which I have glossed as the desire for ‘legitimation.’ … For the small people and the little-big people … the desire was (or came to be) for the right to participate fully in the Sherpa social game.” Analysis: Ortner ties motive to structure without reducing motive to material interest. The egalitarian half of the core contradiction makes exclusion from full participation culturally painful; the hierarchical half makes “name” the recognized remedy.

Load-bearing one-liners (attributed): *“The Sherpa monasteries were built by actors who creatively used a world that was using them.”* · *“A theory of practice is a theory of history.”*

### Soft / hard / middle on how schemas structure

Ortner maps three meta-positions on structure’s grip (Ch. VII):

| Position | Schema relation | Exemplars she associates | Her stance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Soft / external | Schemas as models or resources; actors mainly rational self-interest | Political economists (Wolf, Fox as she cites) | Too thin on cultural constitution of interest |
| Hard / internal | Schemas deeply embedded in identity; actors “do the cultural thing” | Position she attributes to a strong reading of Bourdieu/Geertz | Risks making transformation impossible |
| Middle / variable distance | Some manipulate, some are driven, some follow situational logic, some use schema to interpret others | Ortner’s working position in *High Religion* | Preferred; “enacting the schema” is shorthand, not programming |

This middle position is the book’s distinctive answer to the structure–agency problem relative to both pure political economy and pure culturalism (see [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] Position 12).

### Founding careers as schema mappings (summary)

**Tengboche (1916–19).** Leading lay sponsor Karma: displaced as pembu by younger/rival Sangye → religious departure/meditation → protector network (Lama Gulu; Zatul Rinpoche) → Tengboche as prestige triumph rather than recovery of tax office → Sangye moves out of Zhung as “loser” in the prestige register. Building involves Zatul’s site choice, ramne consecration, multi-year construction.

**Chiwong (1923–~1929/32).** Lay initiator Sangye recruits Kusho Tulku from Thami (celibate prestige + financial inducement); “raid” of experienced Thami monks; unique pledge of lifetime support for fifty monks; schema cast with Sangye as challenger-hero and Karma as reigning rival; competitive “I’ll build a monastery too” fraternal politics.

**Devuche (1925–28).** First nunnery; big-clan and first-monastery sponsors (including long-distance Tsepal from Lhasa); **nuns’ initiative** (Ngawang Samden’s account of night tea-talk → morning petition to Lama Gulu) undercuts pure top-down big-people story.

**Thami (~1952).** Long path from noncelibate temple toward celibacy; collapse when Kusho Tulku leaves for Chiwong; restart after Rumbu vows accumulate; Tushi Rinpoche’s compromise ruling (no eviction of married lamas’ houses) enables conversion — Ortner’s religious-system analogue of gembu overthrow by a newly empowered sector.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*High Religion* became a standard citation for **historical practice theory in anthropology** — the ethnographic counterpart to Ortner’s 1984 periodization of “practice” as the paradigm of the era. It operationalizes cultural schemas in historical time alongside Geertz and Sahlins while adding a durability argument (contradiction-resolution) those works left underspecified. It feeds later debates on hegemony, resistance (Scott, Comaroff, etc., as Ortner cites), and the politics of oral historical consciousness under tourism and research saturation.

The book’s reception also turns on its **genre hybrid**: it is simultaneously (1) a contribution to Himalayan and Tibetan-Buddhist ethnography, (2) a political-economy history of state–elite–peasant relations on a frontier, and (3) a theoretical monograph whose empirical spine is meant to *prove the method*. Readers who want only monastic architecture or only abstract practice theory will find the middle chapters slow; readers who want the practice program *tested* against multi-century material get the book’s distinctive payoff. Ortner’s own later career (*Making Gender*; *New Jersey Dreaming*; the “serious games” formulation) continues the actor/structure program in other fields; *Life and Death on Mt. Everest* (1999) reopens Sherpa wage labor under mountaineering capitalism and is the natural sequel for this vault once ingested.

Within the vault, *High Religion* densifies [[practice-theory]] beyond the Bourdieu/Giddens core (the summary theory page now carries an Ortner expansion section), supplies the major primary base for [[ortner-sherry]]’s Sherpa career phase after the nature/culture essay, introduces [[cultural-schema]] as distinct from (but related to) [[transposable-schemas|Sewell’s schemas]], and puts [[structure-vs-agency-debate|structure vs. agency]] Position 12 on the board as historical-ethnographic practice synthesis. The society page [[sherpa]] and emic stratification concept [[big-people-small-people]] give the subject-matter layer a durable home.

**What the book does *not* settle:** absolute chronologies of early temples; whether potato-driven demography is real (Ortner leaves Oppitz vs von Fürer-Haimendorf open); the full twentieth-century monastic reform of popular religion (companion project); gender as a primary analytic axis equal to big/small and fraternal politics (present at Devuche and marriage squeeze, not the spine).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`not-applicable` as experimental replication. Standing critical angles (record as positions, not verdicts):

- **Oral-history chronology.** Approximate dates and multi-version tales; early history speculative by design.  
- **Schema overfit.** Risk of reading all careers as seven-step plots (Ortner herself stresses variable distance and soft/hard range).  
- **Regional and gender sampling.** Solu/Khumbu differences; women’s lives less central except Devuche and marriage-squeeze analysis.  
- **Companion volume gap.** Monastic reform of popular religion is promised elsewhere; this book is generative history, not full religious sociology of the twentieth century.  
- **Later Ortner.** *Life and Death on Mt. Everest* (1999) and subsequent work re-enter mountaineering labor and gender — not yet ingested; may reframe “small people” wage-labor findings.

## Current Standing

Still a **reference-point historical ethnography** for practice theory and Himalayan anthropology: taught as how to join political economy to cultural analysis without reducing either. The monastery-founding explanation remains Ortner’s attributed reconstruction, not wiki-voice Himalayan history. Best used with date-stamps (1979 fieldwork; 1989 publication) and four-way non-identity caution (Sherpa society ≠ Tibetan Buddhism as a whole ≠ Tibetic language ≠ genetic population).
