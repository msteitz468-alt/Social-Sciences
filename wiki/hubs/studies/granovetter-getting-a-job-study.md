# Getting a Job — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[granovetter-getting-a-job-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Reciprocal: summary links here by full path `[[hubs/studies/granovetter-getting-a-job-study|…]]`.

*Getting a Job* is the full empirical monograph behind the labor-market vignette that made *[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973|The Strength of Weak Ties]]* famous. Where the 1973 article is a portable theory paper with a small original subsample, the 1974 book is an exploratory mixed design — 100 open-ended personal interviews and 182 mail questionnaires of male professional, technical, and managerial (PTM) job changers in Newton, Massachusetts — aimed at reconstructing **how information that facilitates mobility actually travels**. This page reconstructs the study as conducted, not as the two-sentence textbook slogan ("weak ties get you jobs") suggests, and tracks the 1994 Afterword's own reassessment of what survived.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Granovetter opens with a prosaic problem that he treats as theoretically load-bearing: how people find jobs. He argued that both the sociology of [[social-mobility|mobility]] and the economics of labor markets leave a gap at the level of **immediate causes**. Mobility tables (Blau and Duncan and successors) describe flows between categories; motivational studies describe why individuals want to move; neoclassical models assume that when aggregate demand creates vacancies, workers will be matched — without specifying the matching mechanism. He wrote:

> "the actual transmission of information about job opportunities becomes a more immediate condition of mobility than any characteristic of jobs themselves. No matter how great the 'net advantage' to an individual in changing from his present position to some particular new one, he cannot move unless he secures the proper information."

The intellectual targets are precise:

1. **Labor economics' formal/informal dichotomy.** Blue-collar studies from the 1930s onward had already shown formal agencies and ads rarely exceed ~20% of placements, with 60–90% "informal." Granovetter split "informal" into **[[personal-contacts|personal contacts]]** vs **direct application**, arguing the residual category obscured the sociological mechanism.
2. **Neoclassical job-search theory** (Stigler and successors): optimizing search until marginal cost equals expected return. Chapter 1 is largely a demolition of the assumption that PTM matching is primarily *search*.
3. **Modernization/universalism** (e.g. Sjoberg): the expectation that formal, universalistic procedures liberate individuals from particular milieus. Granovetter aligned with organizational ethnography (Selznick, Dalton, Crozier) showing formal systems remain informally constituted.
4. **Jencks et al. *Inequality* (1972).** The 1974 preface treats residual income variation labeled "luck" as partly **structure** — the right contact at the right time — and promises systematic analysis rather than residual mystification.

The Harvard network environment matters: the dissertation was advised by [[harrison-white|Harrison White]]; White's vacancy-chain work (1970) is explicitly engaged in Chapter 4. The book thus sits at the junction of White-school structuralism, labor-market empirics, and what Granovetter would later call the [[new-economic-sociology|new economic sociology]].

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Does "search" describe the process? | Ego | Full sample + interview depth | Self-report search + objective coding | 1969 |

### As conducted, not as advertised

1. **Sole-interviewer exploratory design by principle, not only poverty.** Appendix A reports total budget under $900 and a methodological thesis: for ambiguous subject matter, multi-interviewer hired surveys multiply unaccountable bias; a single researcher-interviewer produces consistent, reportable bias. Mean interview 44 minutes (20–90); open-ended skeleton schedule required because pilot closed-ended instruments elicited only the cultural myth of orderly careers driven by education.

2. **City-directory mobility sample, not a general PTM sample.** Using R.L. Polk 1968–69 Newton directories, Granovetter underlined PTM names on every other page, then selected employer changers and first-time listings (later filtered to changes within five years). Self-employed and family-firm workers excluded (cannot ask how one "found" the job — itself underestimating kin contacts). Cluster sampling into 140 PSUs (avg 3.7 names) for interview efficiency. Letters to 457; yield 100 interviews + 182 mail; response 85.5% / 79.1% among eligible. "Job change" = **change of employer only**.

3. **Male-only, one affluent suburb, white sample.** Females excluded as requiring separate study; sample >99% white — no race contrasts possible in primary data. Newton over-represents Jews (~37%) and college professors (~20% of professionals). These are not bugs to paper over; they bound every generalization.

4. **Supply-side only.** Employers not interviewed. Granovetter flags the missing demand-side story: how many candidates were considered, how unsuccessful candidates were linked, what steps employers took.

5. **Successful matches only.** Only jobs *taken* enter the sample; men who searched hard without moving drop out; information about vacancies not taken is unmeasured. Chain data are successful chains only (biases against long unsuccessful chains).

6. **Tie strength = frequency, post hoc.** Same limitation as the 1973 article: time spent together as crude proxy (often ≥2×/week; occasionally; rarely ≤1×/year), not the full four-dimensional definition later famous from SWT.

### Coding trichotomy (load-bearing)

Granovetter defined:

- **Formal means:** impersonal intermediary — ads, public/private agencies (including "executive search"), university/professional placement.
- **Personal contacts:** someone known in a context *originally unrelated to job search* who informed the respondent or recommended him.
- **Direct application:** approach to a firm without formal or personal intermediary and without a specific opening tip from a contact.

He rejected residual "informal" as theoretically empty. Appendix B coding is conservative: any formal intermediary → formal; personal contact requires a specific opening or a good word to a specific hirer.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Newton, Massachusetts, 1969:** a high-PTM Boston suburb chosen for directory coverage and interview logistics from Cambridge. Fieldwork mostly summer evenings. Pilot N=15 shaped open-ended probing.

**Composition cautions (App. A):** interview subsample reported higher contact use (66%) than mail (51%); early mail responders less contact-using than late/phone — possible overall underestimate of contacts. No important differences by job-start recency (1 / 1–2 / 3–5 years). Professors (43.8% of professionals in sample) used contacts at 77.6% — without them, age/income→contacts associations would be stronger.

**Instruments:** interview skeleton covered work history, how job was found, contact relationship, frequency modes, optional probes on work/social friends; mail questionnaire parallel trichotomy and contact path items (Q6–10). Full letters and schedules are in Appendix C — rare transparency for a 1970s dissertation book.

### Method rates and job quality

Granovetter reported personal contacts as the **predominant** method (~56%), with formal means and direct application each ~18.8%. He stressed preference on both sides of the market: respondents and, per other studies, employers prefer contact-based matching. External tightness/looseness of markets, he argued, may cancel (employers forced to less preferred methods when tight; seekers less motivated when loose), helping explain similar method distributions across economic conditions — a crude hypothesis he marked for further test.

Job quality associations were the persuasive center of the Introduction:

- **Satisfaction:** "very satisfied" 54.2% contacts vs 30.0% formal (p=0.03).
- **Income:** nearly half of contact users above $15k vs under one-third formal and under one-fifth direct (p=0.001).
- **Job origin:** 43.8% of contact-found jobs newly created vs ~22–24% for other methods (p=0.02). System view: highest-salary jobs most contact-filled.

He refused a pure human-capital reading of who uses contacts: religion, ethnicity, and education showed little method association; **age**, **occupation**, and **network position** did. Culture and personality, he argued, affect network position non-systematically; for accepted jobs, **structural factors** dominate method of uncovering.

### The critique of search (Chapter 1)

The empirical surprise — relative to Granovetter's own initial expectation that PTM workers would conduct sophisticated search — is that **search models miss much of the action**:

- 29% denied active search; among contact-finders, only 57.4% searched.
- Contact initiated interaction in 57.9% of contact cases; ~quarter of all contact cases: someone not approached and unsure of interest.
- Search falls with income and with managerial occupation; rises for technical workers and lower prestige colleges.
- Objective coding of whether information "came from search" produces an even steeper income gradient than self-report (Table 11).

The theoretical climax of Chapter 1 names the program that the 1985 essay would expand:

> job-finding is more than a rational economic process — it is **heavily embedded in other social processes** that constrain and determine its course and results.

"Quasi-searchers" (~1/5 of sample: ears open, not actively searching) and "quasi-jobs" (positions that would not exist without the person — half of newly created jobs, on interview impression) further scramble supply-and-demand schedules that treat the market as a well-defined set of seekers and vacancies.

### Contact types (Chapter 2)

Among contact-users, **work contacts dominate** (68.7% vs 31.4% family-social, N=153). Work contacts are rarely also social (88.9% never/rarely saw socially). Family-social use concentrates among the comparatively poorly placed in the metropolitan market: younger workers, first jobs (63.6% family-social), Massachusetts natives, active searchers, and those with unemployment spells. Granovetter warned against reading the work-contact predominance as simple "modernization" without blue-collar comparison data.

How the contact knew of the job: contact was the new employer (33.1%); same firm not as employer (37.7%); business friend of employer (19.9%). Family-social contacts more often transmit by **proximity** than by hiring control — "less inside," potentially less lucrative. Suggestive cases show family-social ties enabling **major occupational change**, where work contacts would recirculate similar work.

### Information flow and weak ties (Chapter 3)

Against Ozga-style diffusion models of market information, Granovetter argued labor information is often **earmarked** for a person and **time-truncated** once a job fills — not spread as a fixed proportion of random contacts. Much transmission is a **byproduct of other social processes**, not search.

The weak-tie argument appears here in full empirical dress (parallel to the 1973 article):

> "there is… a structural tendency for those to whom one is only weakly tied, to have better access to job information one does not already have."

Frequency distribution among interview contact-users (N=54): often 16.7%, occasionally 55.6%, rarely 27.8%. Structure over motivation: rare contacts *more* often put in a good word. Close friends help more under unemployment. Chain lengths are short (N=64); longer chains look more like formal means — more people hear, less influence, worse outcomes. Formal means are "the limiting case of long chains of contacts."

### Vacancy structure (Chapter 4)

Engaging White's vacancy chains, Granovetter coded job origins: direct replacement 44.9%, added on 19.9%, newly created 35.3% (N=272). Contacts excel at filling newly created jobs; half the time the contact for a new job *is* the employer. Mobility is entangled with organizational birth and death of positions, not only person-to-person replacement in a fixed job stock.

### Careers, organizations, comparison, applications (Ch. 5–10)

Chapters 5–6 treat contact acquisition as a **career process**: ties formed years earlier; satisfaction rising with length of acquaintance; Markov models of mobility that ignore contact stockpiles mis-specify dependence. Chapter 7 integrates short-term information dynamics with long-term structure and draws micro-rational → macro-inequality implications (particularism that is rational for participants can produce closed opportunity structures).

Chapter 8 shifts to organizations: interfirm mobility as both product of spanning contacts (sales, purchasing, liaison, professional standards) and producer of cohesion across firm boundaries; conglomerate "impersonality" as network substitution. Chapter 9's comparative evidence is thin; Granovetter's conclusion is carefully **negative** — existing data do not show that his results fail across many economies — not a positive claim of universality. Japan lifetime-commitment stereotypes are critiqued with contact rates still substantial. Chapter 10 applications (computer matching, Black network under-representation and multipliers, training-group composition) are explicitly tentative.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### Move 1: Split "informal"

The book's first technical contribution is taxonomic. By refusing the economists' residual "informal," Granovetter forces attention to **whether a prior personal relationship** mediated the match. Direct application is not network sociology; personal contact is.

### Move 2: "Luck" as structure

The Jencks preface move is rhetorical strategy as much as theory: take a residual that successful people mystify as chance, and show systematic network patterning of income and method. The book never claims contacts eliminate luck; it claims one major component of luck is **position in a contact structure**.

### Move 3: Structure over motivation (the weak-tie punchline)

The finding that rare contacts more often put in a good word is the empirical pearl of Chapter 3. Granovetter uses it to invert the folk theory that close friends help more because they care more:

> "This is a clear indication of the primacy of structure over motivation; close friends might indeed have been more disposed than acquaintances to use influence, but were simply less often in a position to do so."

### Move 4: Embed search in social process

Chapter 1's long engagement with Stigler, Brown, Rees, and Holt is not anti-economics; it is a demand that models treat information as **byproduct** and matching as **embedded**. The 1985 essay and 1995 preface retrospectively name this the [[new-economic-sociology|new economic sociology]] program.

### Move 5: Short chains against perfect markets

The chain-length distribution is used twice: descriptively (useful info travels little social distance) and critically (perfect-market visions of mass media or long diffusion chains do not describe how good PTM jobs fill — and when long chains occur, outcomes look worse).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Immediate:** supplied the empirical depth the 1973 AJS article gestured at; became required reading in stratification and organizations.
- **Program founding:** with Embeddedness 1985, a charter pair for network economic sociology; GAJ is the labor-market exemplar, Embeddedness the general theory.
- **Dialogue partners:** Lin's social-resources theory; Burt's [[structural-holes-theory|structural holes]] (causal agent = hole, not weakness); social-capital bridging measures; organizational recruitment studies.
- **Policy and inequality:** Afterword 1994 recenters the agenda on whether networks **reproduce exclusion** — women's local networks, racial referral gaps, spatial mismatch without network access ("blood from a stone" returns for Black youth contacts).

### Afterword 1994 as reception document

The Afterword is not a restudy of Newton; it is Granovetter's own literature review and agenda reset twenty years on. He judged the **prevalence premise** "clearly sustained" across U.S. and cross-national surveys (Britain GHS contact rates ~30–40%; Watanabe's Tokyo job changers 54.6% contacts; Boxman et al. Dutch top managers 61%), while insisting that **variation is systematic**, not noise. He restated the original weak-tie claim carefully: not that weak ties always pay more, but that structure can outweigh motivation. Bridges & Villemez and Marsden & Hurlbert's null wage effects under controls are met with the argument that controls may proxy the network processes of interest. Institutional cases — strong ties in Japan (Watanabe) and pre-reform China (Bian) — become **scope conditions**, not simple falsifications. The single highest priority he named for future work is how network processes **produce and reproduce inequities** and how that can be discouraged: Weberian closure, extended internal labor markets, ethnic referral efficacy gaps, gendered network composition, and the finding that contacts can yield lower **returns** for Black youth even when used (Holzer; Korenman & Turner). Economics and sociology, he complained, still pass "like ships in the night" — economists elaborating reservation-wage search, sociologists elaborating inequality through networks — with insufficient dialogue on the matching mechanism both need.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

### Author's own limits

Granovetter is unusually explicit: movers-only bias; no employer side; internal markets excluded; frequency proxy; successful chains only; Newton class/region selection; male-only. Appendix A non-response analysis suggests contact rates may be *under*-estimated.

### Light's base-rate objection

If most acquaintances are weak ties, random selection could produce weak-tie predominance. Granovetter notes the objection as inconclusive without baseline network composition — and notes motivation might still favor strong ties under a random model.

### Wage and method reanalyses (1974–94, per Afterword)

Bridges & Villemez (1986) and Marsden & Hurlbert (1988): little residual wage effect of method under controls. Granovetter replies that controls may **proxy** the network processes of interest. Corcoran et al. and Staiger: initial wage advantage that fades. Coverdill: employer-side gains in wage growth/promotion. Lin: weak ties help when they reach **up**. Wegener: status × weak-tie interaction. Watanabe (Japan) and Bian (China): strong ties dominate under institutional constraints — not refutations of structure but scope conditions.

### Comparative and demographic scope

GAJ's male white PTM Newton sample cannot speak to race; Afterword imports PSID, NBER poverty-youth, and gendered network findings to open that agenda. Cross-national contact rates sustain prevalence, not the full Newton coefficient structure.

### Relation to SWT and structural holes

The popular reception often collapses GAJ into SWT. The book is wider: search critique, vacancy structure, career dynamics, organizations, applications. Burt's later argument that **bridging** (holes), not weakness, is the causal agent, reinterprets rather than deletes the GAJ descriptive findings.

## Current Standing

GAJ remains a **founding empirical study** of network labor markets. What survives most cleanly:

1. **Prevalence of contact matching** and the importance of measuring nonsearch.
2. **Information as social byproduct**, not only search product.
3. **Short, influence-laden chains** for good matches among PTM men in this design.
4. The research program linking contacts to **inequality**, which Granovetter himself prioritized in 1994.

What does not survive as an unqualified law: weak ties always produce better wages; contact use always rises with status (often the reverse in later surveys); Newton rates as universal base rates.

Point estimates stay attributed and dated. The study is `partially-supported` in the wiki's replication vocabulary: core mechanisms generative and partly sustained; simple slogan readings not.

## Historiography and Sources

**Primary text ingested:** *Getting a Job*, 2nd ed. (Chicago, 1995) — 1974 body unchanged; Afterword 1994; Appendix D = full 1985 Embeddedness article ([[granovetter-embeddedness-1985]]). PDF → text intake ~94k words / 262 pages (complete).

**Related primary already in collection:** [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973]] (and study/hub). Dissertation *Changing Jobs* (1970) not separately ingested.

**Edition politics:** reprinting unchanged 1974 text with a long Afterword is itself a historiographic choice — exploratory classic preserved; statistical modernization deferred; literature review carries the update burden. The 1995 preface is explicit that Granovetter would analyze the data differently with later multivariate methods but left the original as an exploratory stimulus still useful for undergraduates and researchers — a rare authorial choice that freezes 1969–74 methods as a historical research act rather than retrofitting them.

**Relation of the three Granovetter texts now in the collection.** Order of *reading* for the wiki: (1) GAJ for design and full distributions; (2) SWT 1973 for portable theory and community corollary; (3) Embeddedness 1985 for the general action-theoretic charter. Chronologically the dissertation (1970) precedes both articles and the monograph; the 1973 article published the weak-tie and chain findings before the book expanded the surrounding causal model (search critique, vacancy structure, careers, organizations). The 1985 essay then re-theorized what Ch. 1 of GAJ had already practiced — job-finding as process embedded in other social relations — as a program for markets and firms generally. Citation practice in the wider discipline often collapses all three into "weak ties"; this hub's purpose is to keep the research act of *Getting a Job* visible as a distinct, reconstructable study — with its sample frame, coding rules, biases, and Afterword self-critique intact rather than sloganized.
