# Land Filled with Flies (Wilmsen 1989) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Documentation moment: CaeCae and regional Kalahari as reconstructed to 1989 — not the ethnographic present.

[[wilmsen-edwin-n|Edwin N. Wilmsen]]'s *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* is the densest monographic statement of **Kalahari revisionism**. Where [[lee-kung-san-study|Lee 1979]] built the quantitative spine of the isolationist foraging case, Wilmsen rebuilds the same regional world as a **political economy**: San-speakers as historically co-produced with Batswana, Ovaherero/Mbanderu, and Nama-speaking neighbors, and mid-twentieth-century "foraging" as the livelihood of a **rural underclass** rather than a Paleolithic survival. The book is deliberately **not** a classic camp ethnography; its method is multi-evidence reconstruction under an [[anthropological-political-economy]] program (Wolf's relational totality is the Ch.1 epigraph).

## Question and Intellectual Context

*(Attributed — Wilmsen 1989, Introduction–Ch.2.)* The load-bearing question is political and historiographic: what accounts for peoples labeled "Bushmen" who appear to practice aboriginal foraging **inside modern states**? Wilmsen's answer rejects residual-evolution and isolationist frames. Two metaphors open the book: urban wealth bought by rural degradation (metropolitan "flies" on rural lives), and Zhu cosmology in which Gxanwa eats flies when short of human corpses — inverted as wealth flowing out of Kalahari production.

The polemical targets include:

1. **The Man the Hunter / Harvard Kalahari program** — selection for isolation, uniformitarian Paleolithic modeling, and the packaging of !Kung as threshold-of-Neolithic witnesses ([[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968]], [[lee-richard-b]]).
2. **Forager mode of production models** — Meillassoux, Terray, Godelier, Hindess & Hirst, Sahlins, and field syntheses by Lee, Silberbauer, Tanaka — read as carrying a nineteenth-century pedigree (Tönnies *Gemeinschaft*, Durkheim mechanical solidarity, evolutionary residualism) that must be abandoned for class analysis of noncapitalist Kalahari formations.
3. **[[original-affluent-society]]** — Sahlins's pristine ease and Lee's "abundant food sufficient to needs" as misreading structural scarcity and underclass bodies.
4. **Ethnography as category production** — Bushman/San as colonial-administrative and moral-philosophical categories that authenticate a primitive/civilized opposition (Clifford, Sperber, Wagner, Asad, Althusserian interpellation).

Wilmsen situates his own conversion: 1973 ethnoarchaeology at CaeCae for Paleolithic models → evidence of long complex relations → policy consulting → archives after Jack Parson's question about Bushmen in a modern state.

**Design as conducted, not as isolationist ethnography advertises.** Wilmsen chose CaeCae **because** Lee's Harvard students had worked there — then used that baseline against the isolation narrative. He ranges beyond Zhu to Mbanderu and regional history because interlocutors speak a wider geography. Evidence quality is uneven by chapter: Ch.3–4 are synthetic historiography (dependent on Denbow, Tlou, Parsons, Passarge, trader accounts); Ch.5–7 rest on CaeCae series plus comparative ethnography. Table OCR in the ingested scan is poor for some production tables; load-bearing numbers below are those recoverable from prose and cleaner tables in the primary text.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**CaeCae** (also Onyaiyna in older sources) is a multi-ethnic Ngamiland locality — Zhu homesteads stratified by tenure entitlement, coresident Mbanderu pastoralists, Tawana political horizon — not a pure foraging camp. Wilmsen's strata language: pastoralist / independent / forager / client / reliant. Homesteads 1–2–3 concentrate nqore entitlement, livestock, and wages; excluded homesteads 6–9 show outmigration of employable adults.

Regional sample expands to Dobe-area demography as Wilmsen reads Lee: **43–49% Bantu-speakers** among ~900 mid-century Dobe-area persons — barely half Zhu at the peak "isolated Bushmen" moment. Archaeological sample includes western sandveld and eastern Kalahari Early Iron Age hierarchies (Nqoma, Divuyu, Matlapaneng, Toutswemogala, and related loci) with 14C series as published (treat as **uncalibrated unless stated**; date-stamp Wilmsen 1989 synthesis).

## Findings in Detail

### Theory critique (Ch.1–2)

*(Attributed.)* Categories Bushman/San/forager are products of re-presentation that become indexical signs of independently imagined realities. Two constants across reversed valuations (golden-age ease vs savage brutes): a pure hunters era is imagined as separate, and living hunters represent it. Passarge's 1890s Zhu fieldwork came too late to shape the earlier evolutionary staging; conceptual isolation enabled administrative isolation. Forager appearance = underclass relegation. "Foragers come to class" opens class analysis roughly two millennia after pure-forager relations of production would have been appropriate if the isolation model were true. Ethnographic analogy for Paleolithic reconstruction is rejected.

### Recovered and recorded past (Ch.3)

*(Attributed.)* Developed pastoralism and Early Iron Age settlement structure the Kalahari long before Harvard ethnography; east–west contrasts and trade truncation after ~1000 CE appear in the archaeological synthesis. Nineteenth-century mercantile routes (British, German/Atlantic, Portuguese northern vectors) coalesce on northern Namibia–Ngamiland riches — ivory, feathers, cattle. Orlam/Nama control of corridors, Andersson and other traders, difaqane-era upheaval, and Tawana state formation produce San as producers for wider markets and as tribute/client populations. Remoteness is a **post-merchant-capital product**, not eternal geography. "Bushman" functions as **status** label in historical usage, not only ethnonym.

### Underclass consolidation (Ch.4)

*(Attributed.)* Colonial and Protectorate processes solidify underclass position: British cattle-post clearances of western sandveld (Wilmsen stresses British political interest over ecology alone); Mathiba-era reenserfment; skewed stock confiscations; German–Herero war labor vacuum and refugee influx; Garveyism episode (1920s) as brief international feeler that failed organizationally. Nineteenth-century raiding that transferred exchange value gives way to small-scale theft for immediate meals. Tswana mine migration depended on San cattlepost/field labor remaining behind; San mine registration remained low. Wilmsen rejects Lee's "adjacent stages of precapitalist formations" framing of Zhu–Batswana contact as evolutionary sequencing that softens structural domination.

### Ideology of person and place (Ch.5)

*(Attributed.)* San land relations are as fully developed as neighbors' with significant overlap — undermining inequality-as-absence-of-structure arguments. Following a Gluckman-derived sense of entitlement (obligations among persons regarding things), Wilmsen treats **nqore** as resource-partition units (disagreement among ethnographers on unilineal vs bilateral affiliation: Marshall, Lee, Wilmsen's own bilateral ascription and name-relation mechanisms). **Haro** (hxaro) is exchange practice inside that field, not proof of classless isolation. Marriage, filiation, and multiethnic layered entitlements reproduce differential access. Continuity of multiethnic settlement (including 1904 Mbanderu refugees) is a striking regional feature until commodity capitalism disrupts earlier articulations.

### Production relations (Ch.6)

*(Attributed — CaeCae series.)*

**Vs Lee energetics.** July kcal can match Lee's camp figures while year-round means show huge seasonal range (foragers ~1,408–3,114 kcal/person/day). Vegetables dominate forager budgets (60–98%); status and enhanced means (horses, spears, guns, dogs) produce higher hunting returns at lower frequency for pastoralists. Sharing is politicized within homestead kinship control; instrument-owners own product (arrows **and** guns).

**Concentration.** 1980: 53 Zhu households; 35 (66%) no cattle/goats; 6 (11%) hold 64% of Zhu cattle. Vet chute census: Zhu cattle share 9% (1975) → 14% (1980). Homesteads 1–2–3: order of **45% of population but ~90% livestock and ~88% wages** in the concentration claims. Longest-entitled Zhu enter pastoralism; landless without secure kinship descend.

**Class within Zhu.** Inherited tenure privileges access to hunted meat (rising to ~85% of local Zhu kill for entitled households after consolidation). *Nqorekau* / *xhaiha* leadership extracts surplus labor; challenges from non-entitled ambitious men fail to restructure core power. Class relations are argued to be **inherent** in Zhu social relations as historically formed — colonial asymmetry amplifies but does not invent the structure. Superficial classlessness is an artifact of incorporation as underclass in a wider multi-ethnic formation.

### Exclusion, ethnicity, cash, bodies (Ch.7)

*(Attributed.)*

**Ethnicity as class marker.** Bushman/Basarwa/San labels converge into a monolithic image; ethnicity is a constellation of class markers, not immutable destiny — children of certain intermarriages may escape Sarwa stigma; nqore-secure households can accumulate. Labor-reserve tiers and Remote Area Development categories reproduce subordinate placement.

**Cash poverty.** RIDS poverty datum line (PDL) for standard rural household of five ≈ **P555** (1974); best CaeCae cattle+wage households still ~**50% below** PDL; forager/reliant only ~**33%** of PDL. Local employment thin; wages low versus even other San farm labor comparisons. Food is a small share of cash expenditure; clothing and other needs dominate. Lee's R144 store-food fix for weight loss is rejected: foragers buy little food despite shortfalls; shortfall is structural deprivation, not merely craft diversion.

**Physiology.** Table 7.6 seasonal means: men roughly 49–51 kg, women ~38–40 kg across series; amplitude **3%** (1968–69) vs **6%** (1975–76); max mean weight rose ~1 kg with livestock economy between series. Independent/pastoral Zhu **10% heavier** than foragers; drought losses ~2× for foragers; weight-for-height 75% vs 83% of Bantu. Skinfold losses under 1979 drought larger for foragers. Relief food rapidly raises mean weight. Growth and nutrition literature (Truswell & Hansen; Hausman & Wilmsen; national survey remote-area child undernutrition) is mobilized against genetic-desert destiny. Fertility higher under agropastoral conditions; seasonal conception peaks after high-weight months.

**Direction of intention.** Policy and anthropological fascination with a fixed forager image must be abandoned; class, not ethnicity-as-essence, is the escape path a few already take.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Genre refusal** — "not an ethnography" as method claim: history and political economy first.
2. **Polyphony without dispersed authority** — trader, administrator, and Zhu voices staged so readers can test the radical revision.
3. **Orthographic politics** — Zhu, nqore, haro, Setswana click orthography to reduce exotic obstruction and resist !Kung museum labeling.
4. **Agreement then inversion** — accept Lee's July calories, then deny year-round affluence and isolationist inference.
5. **Body as ledger** — anthropometry as class inscription rather than adaptation narrative alone.
6. **Category destruction** — Bushman/San as class words; abandon category ethnography.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*(Attributed reception history at wiki level of this vault.)* The book became the default citation for revisionist Kalahari positions in teaching and debate literature. It restructured how [[hunter-gatherer]] reference cases and [[ethnographic-analogy]] are policed. It forced isolationist ethnographers to answer on history and political economy, not only ecology. Links forward to Solway & Lee 1990 (not yet primary-ingested) and ongoing open status of the [[kalahari-debate]].
