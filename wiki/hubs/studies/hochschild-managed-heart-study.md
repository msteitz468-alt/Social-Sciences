# The Managed Heart — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[hochschild-managed-heart-study]]. Source: [[hochschild-managed-heart-1983]]. Author: [[hochschild-arlie-russell]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine canonical sections. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] asked how a modern service economy uses workers' capacity to manage feeling, and what happens to the self when that capacity is standardized, trained, supervised, and sold. The problem sits at the intersection of three discourses she found inadequately joined (Ch 1, attributed):

1. **Labor** — Bell on post-industrial "encounter" work; Braverman on deskilling; the eclipse of the assembly-line worker as emblem (fewer than 6% of workers on lines) by face-to-face / voice-to-voice service.
2. **Display** — [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s traffic rules of interaction and [[impression-management]], powerful on the small scene but thin on corporate training, supervision, and profit.
3. **Emotion** — what emotion is and how it can be managed (Appendix A models from Darwin through Freud to Goffman).

Against [[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s [[personality-market]] and great salesroom, she argued the sold smile is not only a white-collar sales problem but a generalizable labor process of feeling. Against Marx on the factory boy's arm as instrument, she placed the flight attendant's induced elation and engineered smile. Against Goffman, she developed **deep acting** as the missing half of display work once employers enter the imagination.

**Formative images (attributed):** Foreign Service childhood and diplomatic smiles "from below" (first-edition preface); Delta Recurrent Training and a pilot's command to "smile like you really mean it," which produced the notebook coinage of "emotional labor" (2012 preface).

**Genre self-description:** the book is "not intended as an empirical report, or not simply as that" — it provides "a set of illustrated ideas about how society uses feeling" (Ch 1, attributed). That self-positioning is load-bearing: the study founds a vocabulary and mechanism, not a probability sample of national emotional labor.

## The Site / Sample / Data

### Private face — Berkeley students (1974)

Illustrations of "will" in feeling talk ("I psyched myself up," "I squashed my anger down"). Notes: exploratory coding found **32% of females and 18% of males** spontaneously mentioned emotion management; weak class indicators suggested more middle-class mention; sex difference held with class controlled (attributed FINDING, exploratory). Four response types: instrumentalists, accommodators, adapters, emotion workers.

### Public front — airlines

Delta Training Center as theater of corporate deep acting: passenger as personal guest; cabin as living room; "as if" techniques; weight and appearance regimes; smile as product quality. Multi-carrier interviews prevent pure Delta-ism while Delta remains the high-demand ideal type. Sexualized advertising and passenger aggression (including racist abuse) enter as labor-process conditions, not side color.

**Training deep acting (attributed FINDINGs):** recruits learn to treat the cabin as "the living room of my own home" and to map passengers onto known kin ("You see your sister's eyes in someone sitting at that seat") so hospitality feeling follows — Stanislavski-style use of personal emotion memory for commercial work. Supervisors and Recurrent Training maintain the feeling rules after Initial Training; advertising archives show decades of selling Southern hospitality and sexualized friendliness as brand. Body rules (weight, smile, age anxiety) are part of the same labor process: the face and body are instruments of the product.

**Access and positionality:** Hochschild observed with management permission; PR officials were assigned to "handle" her; trainers invited her to meals. The study is therefore not a pure worker-standpoint ethnography nor a pure management consultancy — it triangulates both, with open-ended FA interviews and union/assertiveness-training material providing counterweight. Gender of the researcher and of the workforce (women ~85% of FAs) shapes what is visible: sexualization, status shield, and "Woman" as occupational symbol are central, not peripheral.

### Public back — bill collectors

Mini-project only: "soft collect" vs hard escalation; occupational preference for suspicion; status degradation as policy. Soft collect trades an opening gift of trust ("Let's see how we can clear this up"); hard collect renames excuses as lies and may puff the collector's status while deflating the debtor's. Sufficient to establish the **polarity** of feeling demand (toe = enhance customer status; heel = extract payment even if the customer is "wrung dry of self-respect"), not a full ethnography of debt collection.

### Occupational field — Appendix C

1970 Census employed persons 14+: jobs "most calling for" emotional labor = **29,421,055 / 77,308,792 = 38.1%**. Women **55.2%** of employed women; men **27.7%** of employed men in such jobs (attributed). Ch 1 prose rounds to "roughly one-third" of workers and "roughly one-half" of working women.

### Load-bearing definitions (verbatim analysis)

**Emotional labor (Ch 1):**

> This labor requires one to induce or suppress feeling in order to sustain the outward countenance that produces the proper state of mind in others—in this case, the sense of being cared for in a convivial and safe place.

The definition yokes **inner work** (induce/suppress) to **outer countenance** to **other's state of mind**. It is not mere niceness and not mere faking: the product is psychological. The footnote then splits exchange value (labor) from use value (work) — the conceptual hinge of the whole book.

**Exchange vs use value footnote:**

> I use the term *emotional labor* to mean the management of feeling to create a publicly observable facial and bodily display; emotional labor is sold for a wage and therefore has exchange value. I use the synonymous terms *emotion work* or *emotion management* to refer to these same acts done in a private context where they have use value.

Textbook compressions that drop the wage/employer-control criteria lose Hochschild's political economy.

**Transmutation (Ch 6 synthesis):** when emotion management, feeling rules, and gift exchange are directed by paid stage managers and a profit motive is "slipped in under" them, three private elements become public instruments — and the question becomes *whose* instrument feeling is.

**Authenticity scarcity (Ch 9):**

> The more the heart is managed, the more we value the unmanaged heart.

Cultural demand for authenticity is read as a response to corporate deep acting, not only to individual guile (Trilling's sincerity story is revised).

### Human costs and three stances (Ch 9)

Hochschild's load-bearing cost analysis (attributed):

> These are three stances that workers seem to take toward work, each with its own sort of risk. In the first, the worker identifies too wholeheartedly with the job, and therefore risks burnout. In the second, the worker clearly distinguishes herself from the job and is less likely to suffer burnout; but she may blame herself for making this very distinction and denigrate herself as "just an actor, not sincere." In the third, the worker distinguishes herself from her act, does not blame herself for this, and sees the job as positively requiring the capacity to act; for this worker there is some risk of estrangement from acting altogether, and some cynicism about it—"We're just illusion makers."

| Stance | Relation of self to role | Characteristic risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Over-identifies; little "false self" awareness; personalizes company service | Burnout; emotional numbness ("dead words"); dimming of inner signals that interpret the world |
| 2 | Distinguishes self from job but feels guilty/inauthentic about acting | Self-blame; chronic sincerity anxiety |
| 3 | Distinguishes self from act without self-blame; job requires acting | Cynicism; estrangement from acting; under-presence in the role |

She argued the first is potentially most harmful, but **all three improve if workers gain control** over work conditions (union fight over crew size as example of control-for-craft, not only money). Speed-up that keeps the demand for emotional labor while destroying conditions for good deep acting pushes workers from stance 1 or 2 toward refusal — "not present enough." Cheerfulness "in the line of duty becomes something different from ordinary good cheer" even when control is partial (attributed).

**Culture's response:** consumers discount commercialized feeling ("they have to be friendly, that's their job") and hunt residual personal gifts; culture simultaneously elevates spontaneous/"authentic" feeling as the managed heart spreads — Trilling's sincerity→authenticity arc revised so corporate guile, not only individual guile, drives authenticity's scarcity value (attributed).

Workers also **reclaim** meaning backstage (jokes, alternative readings of "girl" and the smile; private re-separation of company anger rules from personal ones). These struggles stay "largely invisible because emotional labor is seldom recognized as labor" (attributed).

### Gender and status (Ch 8)

Women more often market private emotion-management skill because, Hochschild argued, they have less independent access to money, power, and status — feeling becomes a resource offered in unequal exchange (attributed; 1980 earnings comparison in text). Four consequences of subordination (attributed): (1) emotion management as compensatory resource; (2) gender specialization (women more FA-side EL, men more collector-side aggression management), rooted in childhood heart-training; (3) weaker **[[status-shield]]** — others' displaced anger hits women harder; (4) different portions of the managed heart commercialized (women's beauty/charm/relational skill vs men's licensed anger).

**Doctrine of feelings (Dale, via Hochschild):** importance of feelings tracks rank — junior clerk's feelings disregarded; permanent secretary's feelings a principal element of the situation — applied as a gender double standard (attributed). Flight attending is "not the same job for a woman as for a man": women are main shock absorbers and highly visible distillations of middle-class American femininity ("symbolize Woman"), further weakening the shield (attributed). Male FAs (minority; entered after mid-1960s discrimination suits) often receive more passenger deference and are mistaken for managers — fictional authority that becomes interactionally real (attributed). Public-contact status enhancement can sexualize the job; estrangement from sexual attractiveness and motherly care is a gendered human cost. The Delta "balls" joke (woman FA smoking, refusing feminine display) is staged as an estrangement joke about standardized femininity (attributed FINDING).

### Deskilling and speed-up (Ch 6)

Extending Braverman: the "mind" of emotional labor (what mental moves settle an "irate") moves upstairs into manuals and training; workers implement standard deep-acting recipes ("as if cabin is home"; "as if passenger has a traumatic past"). Industry **speed-up** (larger planes, denser seating, Friendship Express-type high-density turns, shortened ground time) can make sincere deep acting impossible — transmutation fails, surface display hollows, slowdown becomes a "war of smiles," workers say "I'm not a robot" (attributed).

### Private system before the market (Part One)

The book's architecture is deliberate: Part One must establish that emotion management is **ordinary social life**, not pathology, before Part Two can show commercialization. Ch 2 treats emotion as a **sense** with a **signal function** (Freud on anxiety extended to emotion generally): feeling reports the relation between what we see and what we expected, and what we are ready to do (attributed; Appendix A elaborates organismic vs interactional models). Ch 3's surface/deep acting and Ch 4's feeling rules make management **techniques under norms**, not mere hypocrisy. Ch 5's gift exchange of feeling — paying respects, bowing, nonpayment and guilt under status inequality — completes a three-part private system: **emotion work + feeling rules + exchange**. Only then does the Part One closer announce the Part Two problem: "what happens when a gift becomes a commodity and that commodity is a feeling" (attributed).

This architecture has rhetorical force: readers who begin at Ch 6's airlines miss why deep acting is not automatically "phony," and why estrangement is a **contextual** judgment (profit systems that refuse to acknowledge costs), not a property of acting as such.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Polar cases:** wallpaper factory boy (physical labor / arm as instrument) vs flight attendant (emotional labor / feeling as instrument) — Marxian estrangement updated for service society.
2. **Toe and heel:** FA friendliness vs collector aggression — same commodity logic, opposite feeling rules; "misfits in each job might do magnificently in the other" (attributed).
3. **Theater analogy inverted:** Stanislavski dignifies deep acting on stage; commercial deep acting lacks artistic autonomy and profit-shares differently. Goffman supplies surface traffic rules; Hochschild supplies the missing deep-acting and employer-profit analysis (Appendix A appraisal, attributed).
4. **Benchmark exaggeration:** Delta chosen *because* extreme — rhetorical honesty that invites misreading as typical.
5. **Genre hybrid:** theory + ethnography + occupational statistics + cultural essay (Trilling, Rousseau, therapies) — persuades by triangulation of illustration rather than single design. Self-label "not simply an empirical report" pre-empts standards it cannot meet while claiming the prior task of naming.
6. **Invisible labor made nameable:** the book's lasting rhetorical achievement is giving ordinary service fatigue a concept that reclassifies it as *labor* — with exchange value, deskilling, speed-up, slowdown, and "breakage"/"bodies" managerial language as evidence of commodity treatment of persons (attributed FINDINGs).
7. **Civility as achievement and cost:** Ch 9 opens by granting emotional labor's public good (strangers might otherwise default to suspicion and anger) before charging the human costs — balances celebration and critique.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Founded **emotional labor** as a research keyword across sociology, gender studies, organizational psychology, nursing, teaching, and care work.
- 2012 preface: ~559,000 web mentions; Grandey, Diefendorff & Rupp: >10,000 academic mentions (half since 2006; 506 in titles) — author's self-report of reception.
- Institutionalization: ASA Sociology of Emotion section (afterword).
- Bridges to comparable worth, burnout, and "emotional proletariat" literatures.
- Program continuation in Hochschild's later books on domestic and intimate commercialization (*Second Shift*, *Time Bind*, *Outsourced Self*) and global care chains (*Global Woman*).

**Afterword (twentieth anniversary) as auto-historiography:** Hochschild maps successor research on consequence (burnout, stress; Steinberg & Jacobs comparable-worth finding that public communication and difficult-client contact are feminized yet underpaid; Erickson testimony on nursing), private-life emotion management, and a new "marketized private life" sector between home and work — paid help under hybrid work/family feeling rules; extreme case of the millionaire's "beautiful, smart" personal-assistant ad (attributed). This afterword converts the 1983 service-labor book into a program charter for intimate and global care commercialization; the wiki treats those extensions as reception/program notes until the later monographs are ingested.

## Current Standing

*The Managed Heart* remains the origin text one returns to when definitional rigor is needed: wage, display, other's state of mind, employer control. Its empirical world (1970s–early 1980s U.S. airlines) is historical; its concepts are live. Best read with Mills and Goffman already in hand, and with later care-work and org-psych literatures as contested descendants rather than simple confirmations.
