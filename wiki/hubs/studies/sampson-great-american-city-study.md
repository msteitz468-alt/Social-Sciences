All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. This hub page is part of the *Great American City* ingest (not deferred): the work founded a multi-decade measurement and theory program for neighborhood context ([[ecometrics]], [[collective-efficacy]]) and remains the central book-length statement of the PHDCN / Chicago Project.

## Question and Intellectual Context

[[sampson-robert-j|Robert J. Sampson]]’s *Great American City* (2012) asks whether **neighborhoods still structure inequality, crime, civic life, altruism, mobility, and elite power** in a world that elite theory and common sense often describe as **placeless** — flattened by globalization, the Internet, and “community liberated” beyond propinquity. The foil is double: (1) placelessness / community-lost narratives (Giddens’s “phantasmagoric” place, Friedman’s “flat” world, Putnam’s bowling alone, Wirth’s urban anonymity recycled for the digital age); (2) the **individualist selection critique** that treats neighborhood associations as composition and choice artifacts, with Moving to Opportunity (MTO)–style experiments cast as the gold standard.

Against both, Sampson advances a thesis of **durable neighborhood differentiation** and a **family of neighborhood effects** mediated by community processes and higher-order city structure. The book is simultaneously intellectual history of the neighborhood-effects idea (from Guerry/Quetelet through the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]], Shaw–McKay [[social-disorganization-theory|social disorganization]], [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]]’s [[concentration-effects]], Massey–Denton segregation, and the experimental turn), a memoir of “big science” urban research, and a theoretically driven empirical map of Chicago. [[wilson-william-julius|William Julius Wilson]]’s foreword positions it as one of the most comprehensive empirical studies in social science and insists Chicago is a **theoretical laboratory**, not a representativeness sample.

The intellectual lineage is Chicago School ecology reworked with multi-level methods: Park/Burgess natural areas and rates; Shaw–McKay ecological stability of delinquency; Wilson’s concentration and isolation; Sampson–Raudenbush **ecometrics** as the measurement philosophy that makes community constructs as rigorous as individual psychometrics.

### Guiding thesis (verbatim load-bearing)

Sampson states the book’s thesis against the placelessness counterfactual:

> the guiding thesis of this book is that differentiation by neighborhood is not only everywhere to be seen, but that it is has durable properties—with cultural and social mechanisms of reproduction—and with effects that span a wide variety of social phenomena. Whether it be crime, poverty, child health, protest, leadership networks, civic engagement, home foreclosures, teen births, altruism, mobility flows, collective efficacy, or immigration… the city is ordered by a spatial logic (“placed”) and yields differences as much today as a century ago… Spatially inscribed social differences, I argue, constitute a family of “neighborhood effects” that are pervasive, strong, cross-cutting, and paradoxically stable even as they are changing in manifest form. (Sampson 2012, ch. 1)

The rhetorical force is cumulative: a walk from the Magnificent Mile through public-housing landscapes; bird’s-eye maps of death, disease, civic events, and networks; comparative notes (Chicago–Stockholm disadvantage–violence); then the claim that globalization has **not** erased neighborhood inequality. “Paradoxically stable even as changing” becomes the master motif of ch. 5 (legacies), ch. 16 (2010 aftermath), and the Gold Coast/Slum revisit.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

**As conducted, not only as advertised.** Sampson’s ch. 4 is unusually candid about “blood, sweat, or tears”: MacArthur Foundation origins (1982 study group; tensions between individual/biological and social-contextual agendas; Wilson/Ohlin-era debates); Felton Earls as PI; scientific directors including Sampson and [[raudenbush-stephen|Stephen Raudenbush]]; single-city choice of Chicago for race×SES variation; contractor collapse; heat-wave disruption; a North Side field lockout; Violence Initiative political scare; planned biology measures dropped. The design compromise — **343 neighborhood clusters** of ~8,000 people (2–3 tracts), with **80 NCs** stratified on SES×race/ethnicity for intensive LCS work, and **all 343** covered by the community survey — reflects both theory (tracts too small, community areas too heterogeneous) and segregation’s empty cells in the 21-stratum cross-classification.

[[ecometrics]] is the load-bearing method commitment: neighborhoods are settings with item-response / multi-level reliability, not residual means of individual traits. SSO and letter-drops supply **independent** behavioral/environmental measures against survey perceptions.

## The Site / Sample / Data

Chicago is both iconic and theoretically selected. Nested units run community areas → NCs → tracts → block groups → face-blocks. The LCS sample (16% European American, 35% African American, 43% Latino; evenly split by gender in the cohort description Sampson gives) was screened from >40,000 households. Over 40% of cohort members moved during follow-up — mobility that becomes data for structural-sorting analyses rather than mere attrition. Destination geocodes extend through the metro area and beyond (including Mexico). Administrative series (homicide, teen births, incarceration, later foreclosures and CHA relocation) give rate outcomes independent of self-report. The 2010 revisit (foreclosures, post-CHA landscape, violence, partial letter-drop replication) tests whether crisis and policy shocks erased the book’s deep structure.

**Sampling-frame content:** a single U.S. city with extreme segregation and a rich Chicago School archive; results travel through theory and partial replications (Stockholm comparison; UK/US replications of CE items Sampson cites), not through a national probability sample of cities.

**What the project measured that typical neighborhood studies omit.** Beyond poverty rates and individual outcomes, the Chicago Project built **parallel ecometric batteries**: survey CE and cynicism; SSO disorder; letter-drop altruism; organizational density (survey services + IRS nonprofits); civic-event time series (with Doug McAdam, *Tribune* coding 1970–2000); KeyNet elite name-generators; interneighborhood migration matrices from geocoded movers. The design’s ambition is to observe the **same places** through many independent instruments so that “neighborhood effect” is not a single regression coefficient.

## Findings in Detail

*(All attributed — Sampson 2012 unless noted; many rest on earlier PHDCN papers the book synthesizes.)*

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status (as of book / subsequent program) |
|---|---|---|
| Neighborhood differentiation is durable; relative ranks of poverty/disadvantage highly stable (e.g. poverty 1960–2000 r≈0.78; disadvantage correlations often >0.9) | Decades of census/admin maps | Widely cited; period- and city-specific magnitudes remain research front |
| [[concentrated-disadvantage]] co-varies with violence, incarceration ecology, compromised wellbeing | PCA disadvantage indices; rate maps | Core of neighborhood-effects and criminology; index composition (esp. race items) debated |
| [[collective-efficacy]] varies widely; higher CE associated with lower violence (e.g. 2 SD CE ≈ 26% lower expected homicide after prior violence) | CS ecometrics + homicide/victimization | Pratt–Cullen meta and replications Sampson cites; reverse causation and selection still argued |
| CE mediates part of disadvantage/instability → violence pathway | Multi-level mediation | Contested magnitudes; central to [[neighborhood-effects-debate]] |
| Broken-windows disorder→crime arrow largely misplaced; observed disorder predicted by structure/land use; CE predicts lower disorder; perceived disorder shaped by race/immigrant composition beyond SSO | SSO + surveys (Sampson–Raudenbush program) | Live in [[broken-windows-debate]]; policy critique of order-maintenance |
| Organizational density (nonprofits/services), not mere membership ties, predicts collective civic events; civic action stable/transformed not simply declined 1970–2000 | CCCP *Tribune* events + NCCS nonprofits | Complements Putnam decline narrative as **community-level** account |
| Behavioral altruism (lost letter; CPR) varies ecologically; sticky over time; predicts wellbeing outcomes with CE and moral/legal cynicism | Letter-drop; CPR data | Methodologically distinctive; smaller n per area |
| Neighborhoods are spatially interdependent (“neighbors of neighborhoods”); spatial lag/multipliers; ecological dissimilarity of black vs white neighborhood environments | Spatial econometrics | Influential for inequality and immigration–crime debates |
| MTO-style experiments understate context: weak treatments relative to concentrated disadvantage/segregation; SUTVA and included-variable issues; destination reproduction | Critical engagement with MTO literature | Central experimental-vs-observational dispute |
| Selection is a social process / **structural sorting** — “neighborhoods choose people” more than pure individual utility | Mobility trajectories; migration networks; disorder-homophily | Theoretical reframing of selection bias |
| Interneighborhood migration and elite (KeyNet) ties form interlocking higher-order structure; CE and org density shape leadership networks | Dyadic migration matrices; KeyNet | Less replicated outside Chicago program |
| 2010 crisis reproduced disadvantage (foreclosures concentrated in black communities; R≈0.86 disadvantage continuity); org density protective; CE still related to later violence; letter returns sticky (R≈0.74) | 2010 field + admin data | Book-specific revisit; durable-inequality claim reinforced |

**Collective efficacy definition and scales (load-bearing).** Informal control vignettes: neighbors likely to act if children skip school/hang out; spray graffiti; show disrespect to an adult; a fight breaks out; the local fire station faces budget cuts. Cohesion/trust items: willing to help neighbors; can be trusted; close-knit; get along; share values. Combined scale with aggregate reliability in the high 0.80s after adjusting individual composition (Sampson 2012 ch. 7, synthesizing Sampson, Raudenbush & Earls 1997).

**Disorder.** Sampson argues designating areas “disordered” can **reinforce** inequality; perceptions are a cultural looking-glass, not raw environment; law-enforcement eradication of disorder is a misplaced policy implication of the broken-windows causal story.

**Legacies of inequality (ch. 5).** Three theses structure the chapter (attributed): (1) dislocations (violence, incarceration, compromised health) are neighborhood-structured; (2) disadvantage is **durable**, especially in black communities (poverty 1960–2000 r≈0.78; 1970–90 r≈0.87; disadvantage often r>0.9; percent black nearly unit-stable; no black→white neighborhood “flips”); (3) only **structural intervention** breaks poverty traps — with caution that interventions like Robert Taylor demolition redistribute rather than dissolve concentrated disadvantage. Incarceration ecology correlates ~0.8 with disadvantage; West Garfield Park vs Clearing-type contrasts illustrate extreme rate ratios Sampson reports.

**Organizations and the “good community” (chs. 8–9).** Civic events 1970–2000 do not show simple Putnam-style collapse at the collective level: civic ~80% of events, protest ~15%, hybrids rising. Organizational service bases and nonprofit density predict action propensity more than individual memberships or dense friend/kin ties. Lost-letter returns (~3,300 drops, ~⅓ returned; rates 0–82% by place) and earlier CPR data supply **behavioral** altruism; with CE and moral/legal cynicism they predict homicide and teen births after controls (infant mortality/LBW weaker after controls in Sampson’s report).

**Spatial logic (ch. 10).** Tobler’s first law operationalized: Moran’s I shows disadvantage, immigration, and crime highly patterned; not everything is spatial. Spatial lag models: crime nearby induces out-migration; violence and control are spatially conditioned; cumulative multipliers mean focal-neighborhood coefficients understate total effects. **Racial invariance**: structural predictors of violence similar across race, but **ecological dissimilarity** means white neighborhoods almost never reproduce the structural circumstances of black neighborhoods — spatial free-riding and middle-class black spatial vulnerability. Immigration concentration is often **protective** against violence in Sampson’s series, against public stereotype.

**MTO and selection (chs. 11–13).** Sampson credits MTO for individual-level randomization against omitted-variable bias but rejects treating it as the threshold test of whether neighborhoods matter. Critiques (attributed): experimental movers are a thin slice of the poverty distribution; treatments shift poverty more than deep disadvantage/segregation; destination clustering reproduces structure; **included-variable bias** (controlling pathways that *are* neighborhood mechanisms) is as dangerous as omitted variables; SUTVA is strained when social ties cross experimental arms. Selection analyses show white/Latino flight patterns, perceived-disorder-linked black outmigration, rare Horatio-Alger upward moves, weak IQ/impulsivity restructuring of flows. Strong thesis: **structural sorting** — neighborhoods choose people.

**Elites (ch. 14).** KeyNet: midlevel leaders; high positional turnover (~60%) but **stable network structure**; poor communities high outdegree (resource dependence); CE → internal cohesion; nonprofit density → brokerage; cohesive leadership → lower homicide/teen births; migration flows predict elite ties.

**2010 (chs. 16–17).** Foreclosure intensity highest in black communities (Washington Park #1 in Sampson’s 2009 filing rates); CHA relocation as Matthew Effect on already-burdened areas; Avalon Park/Chatham as high-CE black middle-class off-diagonals; letter-drop stickiness R≈0.74; Zorbaugh’s Near North transformed (Cabrini) yet “Death Corner” legacies and incomplete mixed-income; theoretical bet that causal forces organizing life chances reside at the social level even when mediated by individual action.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. Opening thought experiment.** The book begins by inviting the reader to imagine a world where distance has died — then systematically falsifies the implication that Chicago would then be a “random swirl.” Rhetoric: concede technology and network community, then show ecological order persists.

**2. “Family of neighborhood effects.”** Sampson expands neighborhood effects beyond a single individual outcome coefficient to rates, processes, networks, and spatial externalities — a definitional move that reorients what counts as evidence.

**3. Ten principles** (ch. 3) — a manifesto bridging Chicago School and contemporary multi-level science: context first; study ecological variation for its own sake with ecometrics; mechanisms over composition alone; life-course neighborhood trajectories; social reproduction; selection as process; extralocal spatial effects; citywide higher-order networks; public-affairs implications; pluralistic evidence/causation.

**4. Project memoir as method.** Ch. 4’s making-of narrative is not color: it argues “data are theory,” exposing how big-science compromises and city politics shape what can be known.

**5. Against experimental hegemony.** Ch. 11’s MTO engagement is rhetorical as well as technical: experiments are valuable but not a gold standard that erases observational multi-level knowledge when treatments are weak relative to the segregation–disadvantage structure.

**6. Strong selection thesis.** “Neighborhoods choose people” inverts the selection-bias critique into a neighborhood effect — the book’s sharpest theoretical punch line of the interlocking-structure section.

**7. 2010 coda.** Crisis and CHA demolition as unnatural disasters that reveal structure rather than erase it; Zorbaugh’s *Gold Coast and the Slum* revisited as continuity-with-transformation.

**8. Looking-glass neighborhood.** Drawing on Cooley’s looking-glass self, Sampson argues shared perceptions (especially of disorder) mediate between individual cognition, community reputation, and durable inequality — a cultural mechanism of reproduction distinct from pure material deprivation.

**9. Pluralistic causation.** The ten principles end with a “pluralistic stance on the nature of evidence and causation,” rejecting a single gold-standard design. Wilson’s foreword amplifies this: Sampson “fully exploits his very rich data sets by taking a pluralistic stance on the nature of evidence to assess causation.”

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Standard reference for **[[collective-efficacy]]** and **[[ecometrics]]** in criminology, urban sociology, and public health.
- Wilson’s foreword amplifies canonical status within underclass / neighborhood research.
- Re-centers Chicago School ecology without nostalgic “urban village” dense-ties romanticism (critiques of that ideal draw on Wilson, Pattillo, Venkatesh, Granovetter as Sampson reads them).
- Policy valence: community-level social infrastructure and structural intervention over pure individual disease models and pure voucher/teardown strategies (Sampson’s closing bets).
- Feeds [[neighborhood-effects-debate]] Position A-style mechanism accounts while complicating simple poverty→outcomes stories with process measures and spatial structure.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Claim | Evidence / locus |
|---|---|---|
| Selection / experimental purists | Observational neighborhood coefficients remain confounded; MTO shows limited or conditional effects | MTO literature Sampson reinterprets rather than concedes |
| Generalizability skeptics | Chicago’s segregation and history are extreme; one-city design | Wilson foreword’s “wrong question” reply; Sampson’s Stockholm/UK citations |
| Culture / ties theorists | CE survey items miss deep culture, gangs, or dense support networks’ dual role | Sampson grants ties are neutral; “negotiated coexistence”; dark side of efficacy |
| Broken-windows defenders | Disorder still matters for fear and crime; order-maintenance worked in NYC | Ongoing [[broken-windows-debate]]; Sampson’s SSO/common-cause reply |
| Measurement critics | Ecological units arbitrary; race-in-disadvantage-index confounds | MAUP discussion; Sampson’s theoretically guided NC construction |
| Afterlife replications | CE items and disadvantage–violence links reappear in other cities/countries with caveats (e.g. Latin America notes Sampson flags) | Book’s own comparative section; meta-analyses cited |

`replication_status: partially-supported` on the summary page reflects a **program** with extensive follow-on research and partial cross-context support, not a single exact replication of every coefficient.

## Current Standing

As of the 2012 synthesis (and the wiki’s ingest of that text), *Great American City* remains the major book-length architecture for multi-level neighborhood research: process over pure composition, ecometric measurement, spatial interdependence, and selection as structure. Live fronts include experimental identification, cross-city transportability, race vs class in disadvantage indices, and the politics of “disorder.” The PHDCN data’s public afterlife continues independent of the book.

**What this wiki page is and is not.** It is a structured reading of the 2012 synthesis for the vault’s two-layer design (knowledge + subject matter). It is not a substitute for the Science/AJS papers’ original coefficients, nor a full historiography of every PHDCN spin-off. Where Sampson cites replications (Stockholm, UK, meta-analyses), those are recorded as **his** citations, not as independently ingested studies.

**Production context.** The PHDCN was “big science” urban research: foundation-funded, multi-investigator, multi-wave, politically exposed (violence research controversies of the 1990s). That scale enabled ecometric depth and multi-instrument triangulation unavailable to single-ethnography or single-survey designs; it also fixed the project in one city and in survey/observational epistemologies that experimentalists still challenge. Sampson’s later Harvard appointment and the book’s University of Chicago Press imprint sit inside the same institutional ecology the Chicago School once defined.
