# Money, Morals, and Manners — Deep Analysis

Summary page: [[lamont-money-morals-manners-study]]. Source: [[lamont-money-morals-manners-1992]]. Author: [[lamont-michele]].

This hub reconstructs *Money, Morals, and Manners* as conducted — design, sample filters, coding, and findings with their evidence base — and situates Lamont’s theoretical moves against [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], rational choice, and automatic hierarchy-from-difference arguments. Claims below are attributed to Lamont (1992) unless marked as design facts or later reception (flagged when not ingested).

## Question and Intellectual Context

Lamont’s problem is deceptively simple: **what makes someone a “worthy person”** for French and American upper-middle-class men who control valued workplace resources? Degrees and seniority matter, she notes, but so do mentors, camaraderie networks, and informal training — access that “largely depends on sharing a valued cultural style.” Managers, she reports from prior literature, favor employees who resemble them culturally and who make others “comfortable.”

The book’s theoretical wager is that these styles can be recovered as **[[symbolic-boundaries]]** and **[[high-status-signals]]** rather than assumed as [[cultural-capital]] in Bourdieu’s sense. Survey instruments that ask about museum attendance or classical composers hard-code high culture into the measurement of status. Open-ended interviews, Lamont argues, let people define the criteria of purity they actually use — and those criteria often turn out to be moral or socioeconomic rather than aesthetic.

Comparative design is not decorative. As a Quebecer who had lived in both France and the United States, she treats the “shock of otherness” as a method: valued traits become salient when two national status cultures are held side by side. France and the United States also differ in formal features of stratification, public sector size, educational centralization, and intellectual public spheres — differences she later mobilizes as remote structural and repertoire factors.

**Prologue as theory-in-miniature.** Nine vignettes cluster into three oppositional worlds: cultural exclusivists (intelligence, refinement, aesthetic sophistication); moralists (honesty, sincerity, anti-climber egalitarianism); and this-worldly success men (money, power, material progress). The groups “live side by side” yet “worlds apart,” largely defining themselves against one another. The rest of the book treats these clusters as national and occupational distributions of boundary patterns, not as personality types.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

### Design summary

| Question | Unit | Sample/site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What criteria define worth for UMC gatekeepers? | Individual evaluative talk | 160 white men, 40×4 sites | Semi-directed interviews + questionnaires | 1987–89 |
| How do France and US differ in boundary salience? | National pattern | Paris+Clermont vs NY+Indy | Paired comparative case study | same |
| How does occupation shape boundaries? | Occupational aggregate | Specialists vs for-profit (4-cell typology) | Cross-tab of coded scales | same |
| Gender difference? | Exploratory | 15 NY-suburb women | Same instrument | same |

### Sampling as actually done

Assistants drew **400–600 names per site** from phone directories on higher-income streets/cities (US census tracts with mean household income >$30,000 in 1980; French cities above national mean income). Female surnames and conspicuously foreign names were filtered. Phone screens established eligibility: male, ages **30–60**, college-educated (US diploma; French first/second/third cycle, grande école, or limited BTS/DUT), majority/native (French colonial-born citizens allowed), with residence stability filters. From the willing pool Lamont **selected** a final occupationally diverse sample to maximize cross-site match — not a pure random final sample. Some Yellow Pages / institutional recruitment, more in France under time pressure. Acceptance rates she reports: Indianapolis 49%, New York 42%, Paris 58%, Clermont-Ferrand 56%.

**UMC definition** is occupational-educational, not self-label: college-educated professionals, managers, and businessmen. She notes the French *cadre* / *bourgeoisie* / *professions libérales* vocabulary does not map cleanly onto American “upper-middle class,” and that Jackman-type surveys show few professionals self-identify as upper-middle class.

### Site pairing rationale

- **Indianapolis ↔ Clermont-Ferrand:** regional centers in large agricultural hinterlands; multinationals (Lilly / Michelin); universities and medical centers; “peripheral” relative to New York / Paris cultural centers. Lamont cautions similarities should not be exaggerated (size, history, manufacturing share).
- **New York suburbs ↔ Paris banlieue:** international cultural centers; suburban sampling for comparable physical settings; named communities listed in Appendix II (e.g. Summit, Merrick; Versailles, Neuilly, Argenteuil).

### Interview and coding

Interviews lasted **1.5–2.5 hours**, tape-recorded, usually opened with catalytic questions about work. Prompts targeted preferred associates, superiors/inferiors, coworker virtues and vices, workplace-valued traits, and child-rearing values. Post-interview leisure and philanthropy questionnaires; ethnographic notes. ~**3,000** transcript pages; 13-page standardized summaries; matrix displays. Each interview coded **four times** by Lamont; dual-coding on 20% of American interviews and 50% of French summaries with high agreement after discussion of deviant cases.

The key operationalization is **three relational 0–5 scales** (moral, cultural, socioeconomic boundary frequency): 0 = frequent anti-boundaries (e.g. anti-intellectualism, antimoralism, antimaterialism); 2 = indifference; 5 = very frequent exclusion on that standard. Ranking is whole-interview and cross-interview, not item counts alone. Appendix IV tables (A.3–A.5) present individual and group rankings; in the ingested epub, table cells are image placeholders — legends and examples survive in text, full cell matrices do not.

### Reflexivity of the interview

Lamont presents a blurred professional and cultural identity (old Peugeot / Renault, young woman assistant professor, foreigner) to reduce demand effects — and reads residual dramatization as itself data on high-status signals. The foreign interviewer prompt made taken-for-granted assumptions more explicit. This is a sophisticated stance, but it does not convert talk into observed hiring behavior; workplace power claims remain inferential.

## The Site/Sample/Data

**Who is in:** white native (or colonial-born French) college-educated men 30–60 in four UMC habitats; intentional overrepresentation of social/cultural specialists (~50%) for Ch. 6 comparisons. American interviewees fall in the top income fifth nationally (as of mid-1980s benchmarks she cites). Sample education: roughly 38–51% beyond BA/license by site; elite school shares higher in Paris and New York than Clermont and Indianapolis.

**Who is out:** women (except 15 exploratory NY interviews); racial/ethnic minorities; non-college men; non-natives; pure probability representation of all UMC occupations. These are not hidden biases — Lamont theorizes them as limits (gatekeeper focus; control of variation). They still constrain generalizability of “French” and “American” patterns.

**Context facts she cites (attributed to secondary statistics):** professionals/managers ~15% of US population by 1987 vs ~9.9% of French active population; French UMC more elite relative to national population (smaller share, larger income ratios manager/worker); French higher education bifurcation (grandes écoles vs universities).

### Moral boundaries (Ch. 2)

Morality remains highly salient for American UMC men — contra narratives of pure moral decline. Honesty ranks first among qualities more often for Americans (57%) than French (35%) in her ranking task. American *phony* (insincere performance without substance) has no clean French equivalent; *social climber* is shared but French *salaud* centers unprincipled sacrifice of others and solidarity breaches. Workplace moral signals diverge: Americans prize friendliness, conflict avoidance, teamwork, flexibility, ambition, competitiveness; French more often valorize brilliance and competence while devaluing raw competitiveness, with more open conflict norms and formality. Religion and volunteerism work as moral high-status signals more readily in the US (philanthropy as prestige) than in France (state/welfare framing of solidarity).

### Socioeconomic boundaries (Ch. 3)

SES boundaries are slightly the most salient overall and **much** more central in American talk. Money as freedom, control, and “comfort level” structures US success stories; French success more often narrates power, fame, glory, and *patrimoine*. French interviewees more often draw **anti-socioeconomic** boundaries (critique of materialism, American money-obsession). Race is less discursively central as an explicit SES boundary in her French material than class/occupation; American discursive egalitarianism coexists with strong money boundaries — a paradox she flags for inequality analysis.

### Cultural boundaries (Ch. 4)

Against the philistines: French cultural exclusivism (refinement, *sens critique*, eloquence, distance from *Français moyen* vulgarity) is sharper. Americans more often center expertise, pragmatism, scientific culture, and purposeful high-culture consumption as status display rather than taken-for-granted lifestyle. Self-actualization bridges moral, cultural, and SES standards. Intellectuals are a larger share of the French sample discourse of valued persons; American anti-intellectualism and cultural laissez-faire make open cultural prestige less legitimate. **POSITION:** cultural capital is powerful but too rigid — underweights sheer intelligence, overweights high-culture familiarity; Bourdieu’s gratuitous excellence model is “unlikely transferable” to the US.

### National synthesis and multicausal explanation (Ch. 5)

Center–periphery differences are small. National differences may be diminishing (French money attitudes softening; US high culture eroding institutionally — DiMaggio via Lamont). Explanation combines:

- **Historical repertoires:** US pragmatism, populism, Americanism (achievement vs egalitarianism); French Republican universalism, cult of Reason, aristocratic refinement, socialist/anarchist anti-profit, Catholic vs Protestant moral-economic couplings.
- **Cultural supply side:** centralized French curriculum and literary culture; US localism and marketable skills; state vs commercial media; French intellectual public sphere vs US university-absorbed intellectuals.
- **Remote structure:** French public sector size and job stability weaken SES boundaries; US mobility and diversity weaken universal high-culture signals and strengthen money as portable status; French relative homogeneity strengthens classical cultural boundaries.

### Internal class boundaries (Ch. 6)

Weber’s economic vs value rationality scaffolds occupational variation. Social/cultural specialists and for-profit workers mutually police each other (anti-materialism vs anti-ideologue realism). Upwardly mobile (~50%) stronger SES boundaries; first-generation less culturally exclusive; multi-generation and traditional French Catholic bourgeoisie most culturally exclusive (reject wealth-based SES prestige while stressing background and cultivation). American specialists appear more marginal in mainstream culture than French counterparts — contributing to US “hegemony” of for-profit socioeconomic standards.

### Inequality and theory (Ch. 7)

**Reproduction without conspiracy:** UMC men mobilize class-typical signals under meritocracy. SES excluders select directly; cultural exclusion may matter more in France (class-unequal eloquence/vulgarity codes); moral signals also class-skewed in US secondary data. **Against automatic hierarchy:** differentiation ≠ domination; only strong, widely agreed boundaries become constraining. US weaker cultural boundaries → less cultural→inequality translation than France.

**Rational choice / Marxism:** interviews show preferences not always economic; rational choice universalizes the culture of upwardly mobile American for-profit men. Resources are not valuable outside cultural valuation (Sewell via Lamont).

**Bourdieu accept/reject:** accepts shared-style reproduction and competition over signal definitions; rejects automatic hierarchy, zero-sum closed fields, moral blind spot, SES-by-definition supremacy, Parisian generalization, habitus-as-proximate-only. Names the alternative the **boundary approach**.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Vignette → typology → ranking.** Prologue dramatizes three worlds; middle chapters fill content; Ch. 5 quantifies relative salience.
2. **Inductive recovery vs a priori high culture.** Methodological polemic against survey cultural capital is also a theoretical claim about what status is.
3. **National patterns as institutionalized boundaries, not modal personality.** Stereotypes become collective boundary-work products.
4. **Conditionals on inequality.** “Only strong boundaries” is the key rhetorical brake against poststructural totalization and against reducing culture to always-already domination.
5. **Respectful dismantling of Bourdieu.** Builds apparatus first, then lists contradictions from her data (morality; Clermont vs Paris; US anti-cultural-goodwill).
6. **Middle-range program.** Ends with propositions and unanswered questions (agency mechanisms; observational workplace studies; gender/race extensions) rather than a closed grand theory.

**Load-bearing formulation (paraphrased close to text):** symbolic boundaries are a **necessary but insufficient** condition for socioeconomic inequality; only when boundaries are **widely agreed upon** do they take on a widely constraining character leading to exclusion, discrimination, overselection, or self-elimination.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Within this vault’s ingested sources, *Money, Morals, and Manners* is the foundation stone for [[symbolic-boundaries]] research. It reopens *[[bourdieu-distinction-study|Distinction]]* for Americanists who found high-culture capital too French or too survey-bound. The boundary approach feeds later evaluation, dignity, and inequality programs (Lamont’s subsequent books and the Lamont & Molnár 2002 review — **not ingested here**; reception claims beyond 1992 should stay attributed and date-stamped when those sources enter the vault).

Impact channels:

- Cultural sociology of inequality (signals as gatekeeping media).
- Comparative sociology (national repertoires + structure without national character psychology).
- Critique of rational-choice universalism from interview evidence.
- Occupational and New Class debates reframed as boundary-pattern variation.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (with analysis)

**On the core objects.** Lamont writes that she scrutinizes “symbolic boundaries—the types of lines that individuals draw when they categorize people—and high-status signals—the keys to our evaluative distinctions.” The parallelism matters: boundaries are *lines* (form, rigidity, exclusion); signals are *keys* (content of evaluation). Much later cultural sociology collapses both into “boundary work”; the 1992 text keeps the pair analytically distinct.

**On unifying prejudices as process.** Regional scorn, puritan-moralism critiques, anti-intellectualism, and anti-materialism “can be interpreted as specific instances of a pervasive phenomenon (i.e., as boundary work) rather than as incommensurable manifestations of national character, political attitudes, regionalism, etc.” This sentence is the book’s anti-tourist-gaze manifesto: the comparative object is pattern of boundary work, not essential French or American souls.

**On moral blind spots in cultural capital theory.** In the Ch. 7 reassessment Lamont holds that “moral boundaries are one of the blind spots in Bourdieu’s theory,” and that “the ‘worldly maximization’ principle should not prevent us from thinking about morality in its own terms.” Rhetorically she refuses both economism and the reduction of morality to “virtue of necessity” among losers. Her men at the top of the ladder still police phonies, climbers, and *salauds* — morality as resource among the successful, not only the downwardly mobile.

**On inequality conditions.** Against poststructuralists who treat difference as automatically hierarchical, she specifies: symbolic boundaries are “a necessary but insufficient condition for the creation of socioeconomic inequality,” and “only strong boundaries can generate inequality.” The words “necessary but insufficient” and “strong” do theoretical work: they keep culture causal without making every classification a domination event. American cultural laissez-faire becomes, in this frame, not merely “weaker taste” but a structural reason cultural boundaries translate less often into objective exclusion.

**On the boundary approach vs cultural capital.** “While cultural capital theory posits that strategies of distinction are mostly organized around tastes and lifestyles, the boundary approach shows that cultural consumption constitutes only one possible type of high status signal.” This is the cleanest statement of her break-without-total-rejection: more comprehensive, “not entirely inconsistent” with Bourdieu, but empirically re-centered.

### Workplace as power medium (Ch. 2–3 detail)

Lamont insists work is “extremely central” for upper-middle-class men — unlike blue-collar “after work” orientations. How they evaluate coworkers and distribute informal resources is shaped by boundary concepts even when they claim pure performance criteria. Drawing on Kanter, she treats trust under uncertainty as regulated by homosocial signals (style, lifestyle, self-presentation), then expands Kanter’s narrow external traits into a full inventory of moral, socioeconomic, and cultural signals. American friendliness/teamwork/conflict-avoidance packages honesty as likability; French brilliance and intellectual honesty package worth as rigor and anti-servility. These are not soft “culture” add-ons: they are the medium through which gatekeeping happens.

### Money’s iron law and French alternatives (Ch. 3 detail)

The “iron law of money” in American talk is not simple greed monologue. Interviewees narrate money as freedom, control, proof of competence, and household “comfort level.” French patterns Lamont reports include more open discussion of power hierarchies, *patrimoine*, and fame/glory, plus stronger anti-socioeconomic boundary talk (money as corrupting; moral and cultural purity incompatible with SES climbing). The elitism paradox: Americans who deny class language still rank hard by money; French who criticize materialism may still rank hard by educational pedigree and social background. Boundary content shifts; ranking does not disappear.

### Cultural exclusion without pure high culture (Ch. 4 detail)

Julien-type cultural excluders in France police vulgarity and *Français moyen* taste; American cultural excluders more often police narrow-mindedness, lack of expertise, or failure to self-actualize. Brilliance (*sens critique*, eloquence) vs expertise/pragmatism is a national contrast inside the cultural standard itself. Self-actualization is analytically crucial because it loads on all three standards — moral growth, cultural sophistication, and success orientation — showing why rigid type boxes need porous edges. Intellectuals appear more central as valued reference persons in French talk; American anti-intellectualism produces reverse discrimination against “ideologues,” which is itself boundary work.

### Multicausal model — what it is not (Ch. 5)

Lamont positions between:

1. **Voluntarist tool-kit** culture theory (agents freely choose repertoires) — she keeps choice but stresses supply-side constraints.
2. **Determinist class–habitus–taste** correspondence — cannot explain national traditions given similar occupations.
3. **National character psychology** — she relocates “national patterns” into institutionalized symbolic boundaries and organizational environments.

Remote factors (public sector, mobility, diversity, inequality) change the *probability* of boundary types; proximate factors (job stability, gender, capital composition) refine them; historical repertoires stock the labels people can reach for. The model is explicitly multicausal and middle-range — not a single master variable.

### Occupational interaction effects (Ch. 6)

The highest-leverage empirical claim is interactional: French social/cultural specialists are not merely “more cultural” in a universal specialist way — they are more cultural *and* less socioeconomic than American specialists, who often absorb economic-rationality yardsticks (regret lucrative paths not taken). French for-profit workers still draw stronger cultural boundaries than American for-profit workers (Freemasonry, philosophical avocations). American for-profit SES exclusivism is stronger than Parisian and slightly stronger than Clermontois. National patterns amplify occupational ones rather than washing them out.

Trajectory findings complicate simple Bourdieuian mobility stories: no relationship between mobility and moral exclusion among her men (contra morality-as-losers’-resource); first-generation men “foot in both worlds,” less cultural exclusion, more transclass “what kind of human being” talk.

### Research agenda Lamont leaves open

She lists needs for: observational workplace research translating traits into profit and promotion; larger-n occupational tests; gender and race/ethnicity comparative boundary work; mechanisms of agency within repertoire-structure constraints; historical change in national boundary patterns (greedy eighties; French commercialization). The book is program-opening, not program-closing.

## Expanded design limitations (as reliability facts)

| Limit | Why it matters for claims |
|---|---|
| Male main sample | Gatekeeper rationale sound; “UMC culture” in title overreaches gender |
| Minority exclusion | Race almost absent as French SES talk may be sampling artifact as well as social fact |
| Specialist overrepresentation | Cultural boundary averages need occupational weighting in mind |
| Final sample matching | Not probability sample for prevalence; good for controlled comparison |
| Interview self-presentation | High-status signals may be performed for Princeton-linked foreign woman |
| Image tables in epub | Exact % cells in A.3–A.5 not recoverable; prose rankings remain |
| Secondary class-culture citations in Ch. 7 | Reproduction mechanism partly rests on literature beyond her interviews |

These limits do not invalidate the comparative pattern findings; they bound their scope. The honest use of the study is as a **charter map of signals and national/occupational pattern hypotheses**, not as a census of French or American values.
