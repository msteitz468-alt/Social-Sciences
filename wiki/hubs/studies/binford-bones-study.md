# Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths (Binford 1981) — Deep Analysis

The book carries an epigraph from [[clarke-david-l|David Clarke]], to whose memory it is dedicated: *"It is the duty of theoretical hypothesis to outrun fact so that speculation overleaps the present information state and points the way; then careful accumulation of tested data will revise the validity of the theoretical position."* Binford's book is the countermove — an argument that until the "careful accumulation of tested data" has its own **method**, the speculation is not a hypothesis but a myth.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Bones* is not the report of an excavation but a demonstration in method, and its question is epistemological: **how does an archaeologist earn the right to convert a pile of animal bone into a statement about how an extinct hominid lived?** The book answers by attacking the reigning conversions — [[dart-raymond|Raymond Dart]]'s [[osteodontokeratic-culture|osteodontokeratic culture]], the "[[hunting-hypothesis|man the hunter]]" reading, and above all [[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]]'s [[home-base|home-base]] / [[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing]] model of early *Homo* — as **modern myths**: stories consistent with the evidence but never independently warranted by it.

The disciplinary moment was [[binford-lewis|Binford]]'s own: two decades after the [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] charter (*New Perspectives*, 1968), the movement's promise to *explain* rather than merely describe had run into a wall Binford diagnosed on the boat home from his French Mousterian fieldwork — "none of these new facts spoke for themselves." The archaeological record is **static and contemporary**; the past is **dynamic and gone**. No amount of pattern-finding closes that gap without a body of **[[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]]** knowledge linking observable dynamics to their static residues. *Bones* is the most sustained application of that program he ever published, and — with [[brain-ck|C. K. Brain]]'s *The Hunters or the Hunted?*, published the same year from independent South African evidence — it founded taphonomically disciplined zooarchaeology.

The polemical target matters to the structure. Binford's charge is that meanings are assigned to the record *by convention* — and that a convention offered by a respected researcher "become[s] integrated into the methodology of the field and become[s] commonly acknowledged conventions for giving meaning to observations." The book's rhetorical engine is the exposure of that laundering process, from [[zhoukoudian|Choukoutien]] cannibalism to Olduvai home bases.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The design is a three-part actualistic bridge, built from present-day observation and then carried onto the deep-time record. What the book advertises — a clean inferential machine — understates how much of the apparatus is Binford's own field labour and how much rests on control data whose limits he himself flags.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What do carnivores do to bone? | bone / assemblage | 12,716 dog-yard bones + 416 wolf-den bones (Anaktuvuk Pass); wolf kills observed 1969–72 | actualistic observation; tooth-mark and breakage typology | present |
| What do humans do to bone? | bone / butchery episode | 122,000+ Nunamiut bones; 400+ butchery episodes, 37 in step-by-step detail; Combe Grenal Mousterian comparison | ethnoarchaeology; cut-mark form-and-placement coding | present / Mousterian |
| Which agent structured a fossil assemblage? | assemblage | Olduvai Beds I–II fauna (Leakey 1971) | MNI standardization; bone-density survivorship regression; R/Q-mode factor analysis | Plio-Pleistocene |

Three design features repay scrutiny. **First**, the human control data are almost entirely **metal-tool** butchery (Nunamiut), while the deep-time comparison is **stone-tool** (Combe Grenal, Mousterian). Binford is explicit that "there is no well-controlled body of data" comparing stone- and metal-tool cut marks on the same population — a candid admission that one leg of the bridge is a model, not a measurement. **Second**, the Olduvai analysis rests entirely on **Mary Leakey's published percentage data** (*Olduvai Gorge, Vol. 3*, 1971), because at the time of writing "no published descriptions of bone breakage, surface modification, or surficial scarring existed for the Olduvai fauna." Binford never handled the Olduvai bone surfaces — a limitation that would become decisive when surface modification later carried the whole debate. **Third**, the density-mediated survivorship correction (a cubic regression of survival percentage on bone density, from Binford and Bertram 1977) does real analytic work: it lets Binford argue that raw comparisons of ravaged assemblages are "a meaningless exercise" because differential destruction masks original composition.

The methods section's key omission, by Binford's own standard, is that the argument's most consequential step — reading his **Factor 1 at Olduvai as a survivorship (destruction) factor rather than a behavioural one** — is an interpretive call about a statistical output, not a demonstrated linkage. He is transparent about this: "It is my opinion that several sites loaded on factor 1 are grouped because they share a pattern derived from bone destruction." The word *opinion* is doing load-bearing work in a book about not smuggling opinion into method.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The **Nunamiut** base is the book's evidentiary bedrock and its most secure element. Binford lived and worked among the Anaktuvuk Pass caribou hunters (1969–1972), personally field-butchering 13 animals under Eskimo supervision, recording bones from over 400 butchering episodes, and observing wolf kills and two wolf dens directly. This is participant actualistic work of a depth rarely matched: he documents the "kootoh" (a tabular limestone hammer), the sequence of marrow-cracking blows to the neck of each long bone, the difference between field-camp "snacking" breakage and standardized residential-camp processing by women concerned to keep bone chips out of the marrow. The wolf-den inventory is equally granular — feeding zone versus defecation zone, 46 scats taken apart for splinter content, pups dragging bones to the lair, the seasonal den/lair distinction his informants drew.

The **comparative** data are thinner and Binford says so. Combe Grenal's fauna was excavated over thirteen years by [[bordes-francois|François Bordes]]'s crew under "traditional procedures" that discarded ribs, long-bone splinters, vertebrae, and most pelvic parts as unsuitable for species identification — so absolute part-frequencies there are unreliable, though percentage-modification data survive. The **Olduvai** data are at one further remove: republished percentages from a 1971 monograph, re-derived into MNI, with OCR-grade uncertainty on the exact figures (the original tables are dense and, in the edition analysed here, partly illegible).

The **control-collection breadth** is one of the book's underrated strengths. Beyond his own wolf observations, Binford assembled comparative animal-structured assemblages from the taphonomic literature: A. R. E. Sutcliffe's excavated hyena lairs in Uganda and Kenya (one near Kajiado containing human crania that Maasai informants traced to bones carried some 2.5 miles from a hospital cemetery — a vivid demonstration of hyena transport range and non-prey scavenging), Hans Kruuk's Serengeti hyena-behaviour data, and Richard Klein's excavated hyena den at Swartklip in South Africa. These let Binford plot **kill-versus-den** and **man-versus-beast** contrasts in anatomical-part representation, and to argue that assemblages long read as human residues (Frison's Glenrock bison, Dart's Makapansgat) fall inside the range animals produce. The breadth matters because it is what converts a wolf study into a general claim about carnivore agency — and it is exactly the deep-time-tropical transfer that the uniformitarian assumption must license.

The sampling frame is itself content, in this wiki's sense. Binford's early-hominid conclusions rest on a **modern Arctic caribou-hunting people** and **modern North American/African wolves and hyenas** standing in for Plio-Pleistocene tropical agents. That transfer is licensed by the [[actualistic-studies|uniformitarian assumption]] — strongest for the mechanical facts (how a jaw crushes cancellous bone, how a hammerstone fractures a shaft) and weakest for the behavioural ones (whether early hominids scavenged as Binford's model implies). The book is on firmest ground exactly where its data are most mechanical and on softest ground exactly where its conclusions are most exciting.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Spiral fracture, "chipped-back" edges, polish, and "pseudo-tools" are routinely produced by carnivore gnawing and trampling | dog-yard/wolf-den samples; Myers et al. 1980 on pre-human (>500 ka to 17 Ma) assemblages | **Confirmed** — a permanent result; single-trait diagnostics of human agency abandoned |
| Cut marks have diagnosable form and placement (skinning / disarticulation / filleting classes) tied to specific butchery acts | Nunamiut metal-tool observation; Combe Grenal stone-tool comparison | **Confirmed and extended** — cut-mark analysis became a core method (Bunn; Potts & Shipman) |
| Differential bone destruction is density-mediated; ravaged assemblages must be corrected before comparison | survival-percentage regression on Alaskan/African samples | **Confirmed** — bone-density studies (Lyman and after) formalized it |
| Brain's Hottentot-goat pattern shows Makapansgat needs no hominid tool-selection | Brain 1967b actualistic control | **Confirmed** — osteodontokeratic culture obsolete |
| Olduvai assemblages are ravaged palimpsests, not "living floors"; Factor 1 is survivorship | factor analysis of Leakey 1971 data | **Partially supported** — palimpsest caution accepted; specific factor reading contested |
| The Olduvai pattern fits hominid **scavenging** of low-utility marrow bones after other predators; **no home bases, no food sharing** | anatomical-part composition; percussion-tool dominance, cutting tools "all but absent" | **Contested / superseded** — cut-mark evidence supports a **mixed / early-access** picture |
| Association of tools and bone does not establish behavioural integration | methodological argument throughout | **Confirmed** — now a discipline-wide commonplace |

The through-line is a single asymmetry: the **taphonomic** findings (rows 1–4) have held up completely, while the **behavioural** conclusion (row 6) has not. Binford's own numbered summary of the Olduvai conclusion is worth stating in his voice (attributed): hominids were "scavenging the consumed kills and scavenging death sites of animals after most of the other predator-scavengers had abandoned the carcass"; the parts taken were "primarily leg bones that appear to have already had the meat removed, or … lower leg bones that had little meat present to begin with"; "the major, or in many cases the only, usable or edible parts consisted of bone marrow"; and "hominids were using hammer tools to break open the leg bones and thereby expose the usable marrow." He then immediately concedes the register: "All of the foregoing statements are essentially post hoc accommodative arguments … As such they are not endpoints of research but should be points of beginning." It is a remarkable moment — the author applying his own solvent to his own conclusion.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

*Bones* persuades as a text, and its rhetoric is built on a repeated three-beat structure: quote a famous interpretation, show the bone modification it rests on is produced by non-human agents, then generalize the failure into a lesson about method.

The **Choukoutien** set piece is the model. Binford quotes the textbook picture — "Peking man" cooking venison over hearths, occasionally eating "one another" — and then narrates Lan-Po's own stratigraphy: after a flood, "cave-dwelling Chinese hyenas … were the first visitors," leaving "a large number of hyena skeletons and a layer of coprolites"; only *then* did "Peking man" arrive. His verdict (attributed): "That man was a cannibal as evidenced by patterns of bone destruction at Choukoutien is a standard part of the myth of the past that is passed on to each student generation." The rhetorical work is done by the sequence — hyenas *first* — which converts a story about human ritual into a story about carnivore denning, without Binford ever having to assert a positive account of his own.

The **Isaac** critique turns on exposing a hidden tautology. Binford reconstructs the inference: "if we accept his convention that an association between stone tools and heterogeneous faunal remains means a base camp, and a base camp means sharing, then all archaeological sites … will confirm Isaac's belief that sharing was the critical factor that made us men." His conclusion: "This type of tautology results from the adoption of a conventional methodology rather than one that demands a well-established understanding of the processes that bring into being the observations." The move is devastating precisely because it does not dispute Isaac's data — it disputes that the data could ever have counted against him.

The **Ardrey** and **Leakey/Lewin** passages are handled by juxtaposition and mockery. Binford quotes each interpreter's confident reconstruction of early tool use and then flattens them into a single genre — "Ardrey believes in the 'killer-apes'; Isaac in a kind of middle-class genteel protohuman who shared his food … for Leakey and Lewin, hominid life was an impoverished projection into the past of Richard Lee's ideas about the !Kung Bushmen" — and reports a student's quip that "the only thing hominids had not yet developed at 2 million years ago was the stock market." The rhetorical claim is that these are all **projections**, differing only in which present the author preferred.

The book's positive rhetoric is its call to method, stated in some of Binford's most quoted lines (attributed): "We desperately need to abandon the technique of evaluating men and adopt the strategy of evaluating ideas." And, on giving up the assumption that hominids authored every associated bone: "We must … give up our anthropocentrism. We must critically seek understanding as to the role of nonhominid agents and conditions as contributors to the geological palimpsests within which the earliest evidence of hominid behavior occurs." The closing peroration names the enemy as a research culture: "the National Geographic approach … the view that progress is made through discoveries (preferably photogenic ones) that are treated as self-evident in their meaning. In fact just the reverse is the case." The book ends by promising a "move from the age of myths to the era of understanding."

One methodological argument is quoted often because it is the book's intellectual keystone: middle-range theory must be **independent** of the theory it tests. Binford's worked example is radiocarbon dating — a method whose warranting theory (isotope decay) "is intellectually independent of arguments that may be made about the age of some remains." An inference machine built out of the very assumptions under test can only confirm them; this is the "fallacy of confirming the consequent" that he charges against the home-base reading.

A subtler analytic move, and one that outlived the scavenging conclusion, is Binford's diagnosis of **configurational inference**: the circularity by which, once a deposit is *stipulated* to be a hominid-formed unit (a "living floor"), any patterning found within it is automatically attributed to hominid behaviour — "a hopeless tautology." He generalizes the charge beyond Isaac to [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael Schiffer]]'s "provenience identification" and to Frison's Glenrock reconstruction, arguing all three assign meaning to an analytic unit before establishing, by independent method, that the unit is behaviourally real. Paired with his notion of the **second-order assemblage** (a set already biased by one round of selection before the archaeologist ever recovers it), this is the book's most transferable methodological gift: a warning that the unit of analysis is itself a hypothesis, not a given.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Bones* reoriented Plio-Pleistocene archaeology. Together with Brain (1981), it made the **formation of a bone assemblage** the first question of any faunal analysis, displacing the prior habit of reading assemblages as direct behavioural records. The convergence of the two 1981 books — Binford from Arctic actualism and Olduvai statistics, Brain from South African cave stratigraphy and the Hottentot-goat control — was widely read as independent confirmation of the taphonomic turn, and is the standard textbook exemplar of the subject (Renfrew & Bahn; Klein).

Its most durable export is the maxim that **association is not integration**, now a discipline-wide commonplace, and the demonstration that spiral fracture and "bone tools" cannot bear the weight Dart, Breuil, and Frison put on them. The cut-mark method it systematized — form, placement, and the skinning/disarticulation/filleting classes — became the operational core of zooarchaeology and the very tool that later qualified Binford's own conclusion. The book also consolidated the vocabulary of [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range research]] for a generation, with *Bones* as its flagship worked case.

The debate it launched — [[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting versus scavenging]] — organized the field for two decades. Binford's strong claim that "the large, highly publicized sites as currently analyzed carry little specific information about hominid behavior" forced the FLK Zinj material back onto the excavation table under new questions.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

The afterlife divides cleanly along the taphonomy/behaviour seam.

**The cut-mark counter-evidence (Bunn; Potts & Shipman; Bunn & Kroll).** The most consequential response came from the very method Binford championed. Binford's own postscripts (added at galley stage, 1980–81) already fold in early findings by Henry Bunn and by Richard Potts and Pat Shipman at FLK Zinj: the bones carried **both** stone-tool cut marks **and** extensive carnivore tooth marks — a multi-agent history that Potts and Shipman summarized as showing "we cannot attribute the observed patterns … solely to hominid activity." Bunn and Kroll (1986) read the cut-mark placement and skeletal-part data as evidence of **early access to fleshed carcasses** (meat, not just marrow) — the opposite of Binford's marginal-scavenging picture. The debate thus shifted from "hunting or scavenging?" to "**early or late access?**", with cut-mark/tooth-mark overprinting order as the discriminating evidence.

**The taphonomic vindication.** On the destructive side, Binford was decisively confirmed. Density-mediated attrition was formalized by R. L. Lyman and others into quantitative bone-density studies; the equifinality of spiral fracture and "pseudo-tools" is now taught as settled. No serious worker reads a spiral fracture as a human signature today.

**The pure-scavenging retreat.** Binford's strongest position — no early access, no meat transport, marrow only — did not survive. Later actualistic and experimental work (Domínguez-Rodrigo and colleagues, 2000s) argued for **primary/early access** at FLK Zinj on the basis of corrected tooth-mark frequencies, midshaft cut marks, and disarticulation signatures — itself a contested position, not field consensus, but one that squarely rejects Binford's 1981 conclusion. The current balance (attributed) is a **mixed economy**: hominids obtained carcasses by both hunting and scavenging, with the timing of access varying by site and period.

**The methodological objections.** [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] (2011) offers the sharpest internal critique: that Binford's Olduvai application did **not meet his own strict formal criteria** for middle-range theory — the Nunamiut-derived propositions were not fully independent of the behavioural questions at issue, and the analysis functioned to *identify ambiguity and generate questions* rather than to *decisively test* between rival hypotheses. Binford himself came to a compatible view in later work, arguing that middle-range research deepens and complicates explanation rather than proving or disproving between hypotheses. Post-processual critics added that the whole program under-theorizes meaning and treats the transfer of present process to deep time as less problematic than it is.

**The data-access limitation.** Because Binford never examined the Olduvai bone surfaces — working only from Leakey's published percentages — his analysis was blind to exactly the evidence class (surface modification) that later carried the debate. This is not a flaw he concealed; it is a limitation the field's subsequent tools exposed.

## Current Standing

*Bones* is a **partially supported** landmark: its method won completely and its headline conclusion lost. What survives is the larger and more important part — the demonstration that a bone assemblage's formation must be established by independent actualistic method before any behaviour is read off it, and the specific taphonomic results (density-mediated attrition, carnivore-produced pseudo-tools, the cut-mark classes) that made that demonstration stick. The 1981 pure-scavenging conclusion is now generally regarded as a **productive overstatement**: it was wrong in its specifics but forced the development of the taphonomic and cut-mark methods that then supported a mixed, early-access picture. In this the book is a near-ideal instance of its own dedication epigraph — a theoretical hypothesis that "overleaped the present information state," pointed the way, and was then revised by the careful accumulation of the very tested data it demanded.

The osteodontokeratic culture is dead; the Choukoutien cannibalism and cave-bear cults are deflated; the home-base/food-sharing model is "in limbo" (Klein); and the maxim that association is not integration is universal. The scavenging hypothesis lives on not as an accepted account of Olduvai but as one pole of a debate the book itself created.

## Historiography and Sources

Primary text: Binford, *Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths* (Academic Press, Studies in Archaeology, 1981); bibliographic record [[binford-bones-1981]]. Read alongside its 1981 twin, Brain's *The Hunters or the Hunted?*, and against its target, Isaac's "The food-sharing behavior of protohuman hominids" (*Scientific American*, 1978). The reception is tracked through Bunn and Kroll (1986), Potts and Shipman (1981), the Domínguez-Rodrigo reassessments (2000s), Klein's *The Human Career* (1999), and Johnson's *Archaeological Theory* (2011) for the methodological verdict. The book's actualistic base is documented more fully in Binford's *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* (1978), which supplies the utility-index and marrow-processing apparatus *Bones* deploys.
