# In Small Things Forgotten — Studies Hub

> Summary page: **[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-study]]**. Source record: **[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996]]**.

Graduate-level reconstruction of [[deetz-james|James Deetz]]'s research program as crystallized in *In Small Things Forgotten* (1977; expanded and revised 1996). The book is not a single dig report; it is a multi-class, multi-region synthesis that became the field's most widely taught charter for ordinary early American material culture — and for reading that culture as **worldview**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Deetz's opening problem is epistemological and political at once: how to recover "simple people doing simple things" when documents privilege philosophy, property, and the literate minority. The title phrase is itself a **[[probate-inventory-analysis|probate]]** formula — "In small things forgotten, eight shillings sixpence" — naming overlooked goods that still had value. Archaeology, Deetz argued, decodes messages in things left behind; historical archaeology does so for literate societies and, in a popular definition he states, for "the spread of European cultures throughout the world since the fifteenth century, and their impact on and interaction with the cultures of indigenous peoples."

The intellectual hinge is **Halloween 1969**, Toledo: introduction to [[glassie-henry|Henry Glassie]] after reading *Pattern in the Material Folk Culture of the Eastern United States*. Deetz calls Glassie his "intellectual mentor." The American Material Culture course (Brown → Berkeley → Virginia) absorbed folk-life structural analysis; by 1975 Deetz wrote the first edition (published 1977). The 1996 revision answers two career moves: research shift from New England to the **Chesapeake**, and the rise of **African American archaeology** as a critical component of the field — presented in separate chapters so the internal logic of African American material culture is not reduced to piecemeal comparison with the dominant group.

Load-bearing passage (program statement after vignettes):

> "Seven Americans engaged in commonplace activities; all in their fashion were communicating with us in a subtle way. In each case, material objects were involved—a house, a gravestone, a set of dishes—and if we could in some way find a way to understand the significance of artifacts as they were thought of and used by Americans in the past, we might gain new insight into the history of our nation."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Dimension | How conducted (Deetz 1996) |
|---|---|
| Unit | Patterned formal properties across artifact classes (symmetry, matching sets, module size, refuse geometry) |
| Regions | New England core (1977); Chesapeake + coastal SC densified (1996) |
| Documents | Probates as folk taxonomies; deeds, pensions, maps, church records; TPQ/TAQ |
| Dating | South [[mean-ceramic-date]]; Harrington/Binford pipestems; known ceramic type series |
| Architecture | Standing houses + earthfast posthole archaeology; focus/visibility language |
| Mortuary | Cemetery seriation with carver identification (Forbes's 127 pre-1800 carvers) |
| African American | Free site (Parting Ways 1975–78) + plantation quarters + shotgun diffusion path (Vlach) + Colono/pipes |

Deetz rejects pure formal typology for European ceramics when potters' own classifications are historically known — "like trying to reinvent the incandescent lamp by candlelight while ignoring the light switch at one's elbow." He adopts Binford's technomic / socio-technic / ideo-technic triad for ceramic function and foodways (Anderson's definition of foodways as a whole system of conceptualization through consumption).

**Survival bias** is method content: museums keep the unusual; the 1798 Direct Tax (via Larkin) shows most American families in one- or two-room houses that have mostly disappeared. Earthfast Chesapeake sites reverse the usual HA visibility advantage — post stains only after plowzone removal.

## The Site/Sample/Data

### New England ceramics and foodways
Three assemblage groups: (1) pre-mid-17th c. plain utility wares; (2) mid-17th–late 18th c. mixed fancy imports + American utility; (3) Revolution–early 19th c. creamware/pearlware dominance. Staffordshire sequence: Whieldon → creamware (1760s) → pearlware (1770s) → modern whiteware (1830s). Early yeoman foodways: ceramics minor except **dairy** (milk pans, crocks); communal trenchers; plates scarce and often display (Flowerdew suspension-hole plate). After c. **1760**: matching plates and chamber pots explode (Plymouth 1760s pit: eight delft plates + eight chamber pots in one short-use deposit vs twenty-one plates total from three long early sites). Inventories: full dish sets by 1770s; tea services by 1780s as re-Anglicization signal.

### Gravestones (c. 1680–1820)
Controlled laboratory: death date ≈ carving date; high spatial variation among part-time rural carvers (~30-mile distributions). Design succession: **death's head → cherub → urn and willow**. Ford-style popularity (battleship) curves; English chronological priority with colonial lag. Inscriptions track secularization (body left behind → soul ascended → memorial formulas). Parallel individualization with ceramics.

### Housing
New England hall-and-parlor, jetty, stone-ender diversity; room-naming shift in probates c. 1660; Glassie grammar and **sixteen-foot** Anglo module. Chesapeake **[[earthfast-construction]]**: posts in ground, tobacco capital saved (Carson et al.; Flowerdew 1695 vignette). Georgian: Foster-Hutchinson house Boston 1688 as early American academic example; bilateral three-part symmetry; hybrids (Mott Portsmouth remodel; Virginia Georgian facade / one-room-depth plan). Refuse: broadcast scatter → deep square pits after ~1750.

### Parting Ways (1975–78)
Four free Black households on 1792 Plymouth grant (New Guinea). Documentary poverty extreme (Howe 1820: $27 personal property). Architecture: **twelve-foot** modules (vs Anglo 16); Turner-Burr two-room plan; mud-wall-and-post feature; West Indian sugar/tamarind jars; clustered settlement; cow's-foot fauna without sawn bone; secondhand fine ceramics (mean ceramic date lag 1794 vs ~1822 occupation). 1978 paved ritual/grave-like deposit (~7,000 intentionally broken wares). Traditional "founders' cemetery" of slate stumps questioned as smallpox burials.

### African American comparative (Ch. 8)
**[[creolization]]** defined; simple [[acculturation]] rejected. **[[shotgun-house]]** path West Africa → Haiti → New Orleans (1810 free people of color) → inland. SC slave houses (Ferguson): trenches, wattle/clay, outdoor cooking suggestion, Europeanizing over time. Kingsmill (Kelso): porches, root cellars ("hidey-holes"), stick-and-clay chimneys (BBC fire experiment). Flowerdew 1830s pier cabin as possible surveillance. Colono ware reattribution (Noël Hume "Colono-Indian" → African production arguments); creole Chesapeake pipes (English tech + African decoration). Porch as West African contribution to mainstream American house (Copley–Pelham 1771 "Peaza" letters).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Vignette pedagogy** — seven opening scenes (Monticello dual pots, Mott facade, Soule cherub, banjo, matching china, earthfast house, probate phrase) preview every chapter's domain.
2. **Structural multi-domain validation** — same opposition (corporate/individual; organic/mechanical; additive/symmetrical) must recur across independent data classes (Glassie three-part bilateral format). This is why later surveys file Deetz under **[[structuralist-archaeology]]**.
3. **Rocket / re-Anglicization metaphor** — colonial culture diverges then is pulled back into a transformed English orbit; Revolution paradoxically on the eve of maximum Englishness of material culture.
4. **Boundary falsifier** — Parting Ways does not fit; therefore Anglo patterns are tradition-bound, not universal progressive refinement.
5. **Creole-language analogy** — Anglo artifacts as lexicon, African American rules as grammar; materials can look "familiar" while masking different perceptions of the world.
6. **Closing culture-shock** — pre-1750 Anglo-Americans would feel as foreign as any "exotic" culture; "borning room" historic-house myth exposed as anachronism; Mayflower Compact vs Declaration as corporate vs individual worldview texts; America not a melting pot (Parting Ways cultural maintenance; Fort Thompson Sioux circular clusters 1957).

Verbatim load-bearing Georgian definition:

> "We might call this new worldview Georgian… Mechanical where the older was organic, balanced where the older had been asymmetrical, individualized where the older had been corporate, this new way of perceiving the world is the hallmark of our third period, which lasts to the present."

Creolization definition:

> "Such a process is known as creolization, the interaction between two or more cultures to produce an integrated mix which is different from its antecedents."

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Pedagogy:** generations of HA students first meet the field through ISTF.
- **Definitional lineage:** Schuyler–Deetz global expansion definition → Deagan → [[orser-charles-e|Orser]] modern-world program ([[global-historical-archaeology]]).
- **Georgian Order:** Leone/Annapolis critical archaeology inherits the pattern language and re-theorizes it as ideology of capitalism and order under slavery (Johnson 2011 textbook map on [[historical-archaeology]]).
- **African American archaeology:** ISTF 1996 mainstreams free and enslaved sites as central, not optional; Colono career continues (Ferguson *Uncommon Ground*; Orser 1996 Ch. 5).
- **Structural/interpretive archaeology:** multi-domain worldview reading as bridge between New Archaeology quantification (South formulas, seriation) and post-processual meaning.

| Critique axis | Strong form of the position |
|---|---|
| Over-coherence | One "worldview" flattens class, gender, ethnicity, and region |

| Ethnicity inference | 12-ft modules, root cellars, Colono attribution underdetermined; ethnographic analogy overstretched |
| Presentism of "Georgian" label | Royal/architectural period name confuses style chronology with cognition |

Deetz's own responses inside the book: multi-domain convergence is the warrant; Parting Ways is the boundary test; he flags Colono African production as "compelling but not universally accepted"; ceramics-as-status gets an explicit caveat from Parting Ways finewares on $8 pensions.

## Current Standing

Still the single most assigned English-language introduction to the *idea* of historical archaeology as ordinary material culture. Specialists treat periodization and Georgian coherence as starting hypotheses to complicate. African American chapters remain assigned even where primary plantation monographs have superseded case detail. Read alongside Glassie (folk housing), Ferguson (Colono/foodways), Leone (critical Georgian Order), and Orser (global HA definitions).

## Reconstructing Parting Ways as Conducted

The research act at Parting Ways is the book's most fully narrated excavation. Documentary fragments establish four men on a 1792 provisional grant of poor land that the town still owns. Military service freed Howe, Quamany, and Turner (Goodwin deserted and remained in partial servitude). Pension inventories make poverty non-negotiable: Howe's 1820 list totals twenty-seven dollars. Archaeology began in 1975 after bicentennial Black-history organizing landscaped the cemetery and reserved the land.

What the dig recovered would look, artifact by artifact, like a poor Anglo-American site — creamware, pearlware, nails, glass. Deetz's interpretive move is to refuse that surface familiarity. The **Turner-Burr** house presents a New England exterior (photograph; small roof chimney) but a ground plan of two contiguous **twelve-foot** squares, built in two stages maybe thirty years apart, first room on an intentional earth platform. A second cellar is also twelve feet square. A rectangular pit twelve by nine feet with corner and mid-side posts and mud walling traces suggests non-standard construction. West Indian sugar jars on a cellar floor link the household to Caribbean (and by extension African) commodity circuits. Faunal remains lack the sawn cuts expected on late-eighteenth-century Anglo sites; cow's feet dominate. Settlement is clustered at the center of ninety-four acres rather than scattered on discrete farmsteads. In 1978 a cobble-paved area yielded roughly seven thousand intentionally broken ceramics and glass vessels — not ordinary trash (no bone, shell, architectural debris) — inviting comparison with African American and West African grave decoration (white objects, water association, perforated vessels).

Deetz is careful about underdetermination: each element alone is "powerfully suggestive" not conclusive; together they compel a reading of African-derived rules applied to Anglo materials. He also weaponizes the site against uncritical status inference: fancy hand-painted creamware on an eight-dollar monthly pension is a secondhand signal, not elite display. The traditional slate-stump "founders' cemetery" is demoted — death dates postdate slate's dominance; smallpox burial groups nearby are a better parallel. The site's theoretical role is boundary: it does not fit Georgian Anglo change, and that non-fit **reinforces** the model by showing where explanation must stop.

## Reconstructing the Georgian Argument as Rhetoric

Chapter 2 states the three periods and names the worldview; chapters 3–6 supply the multi-domain proof structure. The ceramics chapter is the cleanest operationalization: corporate trencher-mates and dairy pans give way to one person / one plate / one chamber pot. The symmetry is not only aesthetic — Deetz says a shared dish is "definitely not" symmetrical in the new order's terms. Gravestones supply independent chronological control (death dates) and an ideo-technic domain (religion → secular memorial). Housing supplies the name "Georgian" and Glassie's three-part bilateral diagram. Refuse pits supply site-formation change simultaneous across density gradients — hard to reduce to pure fashion. Foodways and butchery (chopped → sawn) and furniture (chair multiplication, matched sets) extend the grid. Music (banjo two-finger regional styles vs bluegrass three-finger popular culture) is the folklore parallel to tradition vs horizon.

The causal chapter (end of Ch. 6) is the most vulnerable and most interesting: religious Great-Chain corporate order gives way to secular, scientific, mercantile society in which individuals express status through goods. Glassie is quoted on fashion, social distance, and anonymity. Deetz answers the charge of a-priori pattern imposition by pointing to the number of independent domains and to Parting Ways as a place the pattern **fails**.

## African American Archaeology Program (1996) — Design Honesty

Deetz chooses separate chapters rather than integrated comparison so African American culture is not measured only as deviation from Anglo norms. He restricts intensive discussion to Chesapeake and coastal South Carolina while acknowledging wider geography. Three domains: housing, foodways, ceramics (including pipes). Historical frame: Africans in Virginia before spring 1619 (Thorndale muster: 32 Africans); racial slavery emerges over the century as Christian/heathen yields to Black/white (Morgan). Regional contrast: SC Barbadian founding + Black majority + stronger African retention vs Chesapeake co-residence into the 1680s and Africanisms as **additions** (porches, root cellars) to English earthfast forms.

Shotgun houses are presented as the freest expression (non-slave New Orleans after Haitian free migration). Parting Ways sits on the free end of the continuum; SC early quarters closer to African room sizes; later SC more European; Chesapeake most English-framed. Colono ware: Noël Hume's "Colono-Indian" name; SC reattribution to African makers; chronology inverse to Native population; vessel shapes differ VA vs SC with residential history. Pipes: mold technology English, roulette/decoration African-derived (Emerson); true creole form; complementary distribution with later Colono as social-order transforms. Closing methodological rule: stop hunting isolated ethnic markers; excavate whole sites (Cannon's Point, Parting Ways, Kingsmill) and connect foodways, houses, layout, pottery, pipes.

## What Survived (and What Did Not)

**Survived in teaching and practice:** small-things program phrase; multi-domain thinking; earthfast visibility problem; MCD and pipestem toolkit in pedagogy; Parting Ways as free Black landmark; creolization vs acculturation as the right *question*; porch and shotgun as debate anchors; definitional HA literacy + global expansion wording.

**Survived transformed:** Georgian order without Renaissance mentalité — as consumer revolution, capitalism ideology, or gendered household reorganization in later work.

**Weakened or specialized away:** single three-period clock for all Anglo-America; high confidence ethnicity from single architectural modules; any implication that African American culture is only "visible" when free.

## Counterfactual Significance

Without Deetz (and Glassie behind him), North American historical archaeology still has Noël Hume's artifact guides, South's formulas, and plantation archaeology — but the **public and undergraduate** bridge from sherds to culture change would be thinner. The Georgian vocabulary that critical archaeologists later inverted would lack its most readable popular source. Parting Ways might have remained local memorial history without the multi-domain theoretical frame that made a free Black community the falsifier of an Anglo model.

## Verbatim Passages with Analysis

**On historical archaeology's object (literacy definition):**

> "Historical archaeology studies the cultural remains of literate societies that were capable of recording their own histories. In this respect it contrasts directly with prehistoric archaeology, which treats all of cultural history before the advent of writing, millions of years in duration."

Analysis: This literacy criterion is deliberately institutional (what departments already did) more than theoretical. Deetz immediately supplements it with the popular **global expansion** definition so that forced African relocation and Indigenous contact are not optional add-ons. Later Orser keeps the global move while rejecting literacy alone as the field's essence — a refinement, not a repudiation, of Deetz's dual definition block.

**On Parting Ways as model boundary:**

> "Of course the Parting Ways site does not reflect the changes we have suggested for Anglo-American culture change, nor should it. Yet in its not fitting this pattern, it reinforces it, since it serves to draw a line beyond which explanation cannot and should not proceed."

Analysis: This is sophisticated falsification rhetoric. The Anglo Georgian model is not universal progress; it is tradition-specific. Familiar ceramics "disguised" difference until "different perceptions of the world" were considered — the same structuralist move as the Georgian chapters, inverted.

**On material culture's privilege (closing):**

Deetz argues material culture is the most objective and immediate historical source because it was not produced to persuade future readers the way texts were. The claim is polemical (artifacts are interpretation-laden too) but strategically aimed at historians who treat archaeology as expensive duplication of archives. The Parting Ways and African American chapters are the proof cases: without things, the written scraps cannot recover household rules.
