# The Devil and Commodity Fetishism (Taussig) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]. Source: [[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-1980]]. Author: [[taussig-michael]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · nine canonical body sections · attributed voice for all substantive findings.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*The Devil and Commodity Fetishism in South America* (University of North Carolina Press, 1980; 30th anniversary edition 2010 with new chapter *The Sun Gives without Receiving*) is [[taussig-michael|Michael T. Taussig]]'s bid to make folk devilry answer a political-economic question: **what is the relationship between the image of the devil and capitalist development?**

Taussig's opening aim (1980 preface):

> My aim in this book is to elicit the social significance of the devil in the folklore of contemporary plantation workers and miners in South America. The devil is a stunningly apt symbol of the alienation experienced by peasants as they enter the ranks of the proletariat.

The book joins two cases: Afro-Colombian free peasants and cane cutters of the southern [[cauca-valley-afro-colombians|Cauca Valley]], and [[bolivian-tin-miners|Bolivian tin miners]]. The load-bearing contrast is not "superstition vs science" but **mode of production and mode of evaluation**:

> as peasant cultivators become landless wage laborers, they invoke the devil as part of the process of maintaining or increasing production. However, as peasants working their own land according to their own customs they do not do this…. Whereas the imagery of God or the fertility spirits of nature dominates the ethos of labor in the peasant mode of production, the devil and evil flavor the metaphysics of the capitalist mode of production in these two regions.

Disciplinary moment: critical-turn anthropology (late 1970s–80), Latin American Marxism and peasant studies, cultural studies / "consciousness" over base reductionism (Taussig's 2009 self-placement next to Stuart Hall), and a then-unusual English-language anthropological recovery of Marx's [[commodity-fetishism|commodity fetishism]] as *cultural* critique. Adjacent to [[anthropological-political-economy]] (Wolf, Mintz) but more literary-dialectical and less global-ethnohistorical: Taussig's unit is the **fetish complex** under proletarianisation, not the world-system circuit.

Epigraphs set the program: Benjamin on seizing memory at a moment of danger; Marx on pre-capitalist production for man vs modern production for wealth.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do devil-beliefs mediate capitalist wage labour among newly proletarianised rural workers? | Devil-contract / Tio rite complexes as cultural texts | Southern Cauca Valley (Puerto Tejada region); Bolivian highland tin mines | Historical ethnography + Marxist cultural analysis | Cauca slavery → free peasantry → 1970s agribusiness; Bolivia conquest → tin barons → 1952 nationalisation → post-1964 military |
| What is the social history of the free peasantry that makes devil contracts intelligible? | Land, religion, party war, enclosure | Arboleda estates, *indiviso*, Puerto Tejada | Oral history, regional historiography | 19th–mid-20th c. Colombia |
| How does mine devilry differ from peasant mountain worship? | Spirit-owner / fertility rites | Oruro area mines; Andean peasant communities (via Bastien, Buechler, etc.) | Secondary synthesis (Nash, Rojas & Nash, Costas Arguedas…) | Colonial to 1970s |

**As conducted, not as advertised:**

1. **Asymmetric dual case.** Colombia is primary: Taussig and compañera **Anna Rubbo** spent "almost four years" in and around the southern Cauca Valley from ~1970, "involved in the militant peasant political organization that flourished there in the early 1970s." Bolivia is explicitly secondary: "I have had to rely heavily on the writing of others" (June Nash, Juan Rojas, Bastien, LaBarre, José María Arguedas, Earls…). The comparative architecture is real; the ethnographic depth is not equal.
2. **Not a survey of belief rates.** Taussig argues that collective belief and motif configuration matter more than frequency counts; he also insists contracts "do occur," not only as envy talk.
3. **Dialectical deconstruction as method.** Part I is a primer in reading categories as naturalised social products and in using Marx's commodity analysis against both positivist reification and precapitalist nostalgia.
4. **Anniversary apparatus (2010).** New preface (July 2009) and *Sun Gives* chapter reframe storytelling, Bataillean *dépense*, and neo-liberal afterlives — a self-reanalysis, not a restudy.

**Validity threats Taussig himself surfaces:** risk of precapitalist nostalgia; intellectual criticism can only be "negative"; secondary mine dependence; later self-critique that the 1980 prose was "clear, dry, analytic" omniscient voice that "distances itself from its subject matter."

## The Site / Sample / Data

### Cauca Valley (primary)

- **Population:** Afro-Colombian free peasantry and plantation wage workers; plantation towns around Puerto Tejada.
- **Ecology/economy:** plantain–cocoa–coffee tree agriculture on smallholdings vs expanding sugarcane agribusiness ("Green Monster" in a 1972 peasant broadsheet Taussig quotes).
- **Historical spine:** slavery on Arboleda-type estates; abolition 1851–52 and *concertado* aftermath; free-peasant *indiviso* commons; Liberal/Conservative wars with religious-political Manichaeism; merchant penetration and fences; mid-20th-c. *violencia*; 1970s unions and piecework cane cutting.
- **Data genres:** participant observation; oral histories (named elders — Carbonero, Cambindo, Tomás Zapata, and others); local political documents; plantation work talk; sorcery and curing repertoires; structural analogy tables (baptism of money).

### Bolivian tin mines (secondary)

- **Population:** wage miners and mining communities (Nash's San José and related contexts as cited).
- **Ritual complex:** [[tio|Tio]] ("uncle") / devil as owner of mines and tin; *ch'alla* (Tue/Fri and after accidents); Pachamama sequence; blood sacrifice; anti-Christian interior of the mine.
- **Political spine:** tin barons (e.g. Patiño participation in rites reported); 1952 nationalisation; 1964 military; union as "university"; management economism vs miner "care for the mine."
- **Data genres:** Nash 1972/1976, Rojas & Nash, Costas Arguedas, Bastien mountain-body ethnography, colonial chronicles — **all via Taussig's reading**.

### Sampling frame bias

Foreign anthropologist embedded with left peasant politics; male devil contracts privileged over women's love magic; Bolivia filtered through English/Spanish published ethnography of the 1960s–70s; dual case selected for structural contrast (plantation vs mine under capitalism), not representative Latin America.

### Chapter-level finding chains (attributed)

**Part I — Fetishism as master trope.** Taussig argues that categories we take as natural (labour-time, market value, the commodity as thing) are social products. Marx's commodity — both thing and social relation — yields [[commodity-fetishism]] as critique of capitalist culture: "the animate appearance of commodities provides testimony to the thing-like appearance of persons." The devil beliefs of proletarianised peasants are not pre-scientific error but a **contrast class** that denaturalises our market metaphysics.

**Part II Ch.3–4 — Free peasantry and enclosure.** Slave religion and appropriation of the masters' devil; post-1851 free peasantry tipping the regional balance of power; plantain–cocoa ecology; *indiviso* commons; Liberal freedom theology vs Conservative "conserve" as reenslavement in oral etymology; fences and merchant Puerto Tejada; *violencia* memory. The Green Monster of cane is political-ecological, not only crop choice.

**Part II Ch.5–7 — Devil contract and baptism of money.** Core Cauca ethnography: secrecy, *muñeco*, barren money (luxuries only; *secar*), cane root death, foremen who fire high producers, militants who believe, women's exclusion from production pacts, pollution/*porquería* as person-made filth, house curing as cosmogony. Baptism of money: bill baptised so it returns with increase — folk secret of capital against use-value money. Part II conclusion: these superstitions "endorse systematically the logic of the contradiction between use-values and exchange-values."

**Part III — Mines.** Tio icons (mineral eyes, crystal teeth; Siglo XX erect penis; gringo apparition); *ch'alla* procedure; Pachamama vs Tio; 1952/1964 reorganisation of work groups and rites; peasant mountain-as-body gift cosmology (Bastien) vs mine commodity owner. Problem of evil: preconquest systems lack an almighty evil; Christian devil overlays and is specialised in mines. Conquest iconography: white/rich spirit owners; Inkarri messianism gradients.

**Ch.12–14 + Conclusion.** Devil in mining after conquest, fiercest where exploitation is most intense and return nil. Miners mediate gift→commodity circuits; spirit owner *is* the tin. Peasant rites of production sustain reciprocity and inalienability. Book task: interpret folk magic under loss of means of production. Devil is archsymbol of ambivalence — "not more terrifying than the settlers."

***Sun Gives* (2006/2010).** Keeps 1980 Marxist reading but stresses Bataille: the sun gives without receiving; surplus as *dépense*; stories of extremity as gifts about the gift; devil as Great Imitator of false real transgression. Aftereffect: anthropology stocks "literal" bases of metaphor (once-upon-a-time devil pacts) as its power.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### Dual case as dialectical experiment

Taussig does not claim the same rite in two places. He claims a **homologous structure**: under capitalist wage labour, production metaphysics turn evil, while peasant use-value production remains under God/fertility. The Bolivia case is allowed to be secondary because the *contrast*, not the density of mine ethnography, carries the argument — a design choice that later critics can reverse by reading Nash without Taussig's frame.

### Against utility and limited good

Load-bearing rejection:

> Why is this particular set of ideas, with its pointed meaning and wealth of embedded mythology, chosen in this specific circumstance and time, rather than another set of ideas and practices?

Anxiety-magic and limited-good envy, he argues, presuppose what needs explaining and share an affinity with capitalist epistemology of "usefulness." Magic is instead "poetic echoes of the cadences that guide the innermost course of the world."

### Use-value / exchange-value as ethnographic axis

Taussig imports Aristotle→Marx modes of evaluation and treats Cauca/Bolivia beliefs as mediations of their conflict at the moment of proletarianisation. The baptism-of-money table (child baptism vs money baptism; fertile vs barren money) is structuralist pedagogy. The risk — which *Sun Gives* partly repairs — is reducing narrative extremity to a binary diagram.

### Benjaminian "moment of danger"

Devil-belief as cultural text seized at a historical emergency (Benjamin epigraph) licenses reading folklore as critique rather than survival. 2009 neo-liberal update doubles the move: what was mythologised in 1970 (barren short-term gain) is "now real" under deregulated short contracts.

### Self-critical anniversary voice

The 2009 preface admits the original analytic voice and claims anthropology "makes" culture in writing. That does not invalidate 1980 findings; it reframes them as one storytelling in a chain — *penultimaticity*.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (analysis)

**On the dual contrast (Ch.2).** Taussig states the book's empirical hinge in one paragraph: wage labour invokes the devil; own-plot peasant labour does not; God/fertility spirits vs devil/evil map onto peasant vs capitalist modes. The sentence does double work — it reports a distribution of belief and it installs **mode of production** as the sorting principle. Any later critique that finds devil talk on peasant plots or non-evil mine spirits must either revise the distribution claim or the sorting principle.

**On barren wages (Ch.2 / *Sun Gives*).** The contract's economic theology is precise: short-term monetary gain under wage labour is "more than offset by the supposed long-term effects of sterility and death." *Sun Gives* restates with ethnographic colour — luxuries only; *secar* for trees and livestock. The rhetoric converts a labour-market incentive story into a **reproduction crisis**: capital's fertility sterilises nature's fertility. That is the book's moral-economy kernel; green afterlives in 2009 claim the kernel was prophetic.

**On mediating use- and exchange-value (Ch.2 / Ch.6).** "Following Marx, I call these modes of evaluation use-value and exchange-value…. we will inevitably be led to contrast precapitalist folk mysticism with that form of capitalist mystification Marx sardonically labelled commodity fetishism." The "inevitably" is a rhetorical force-move: once the binary is accepted, devil beliefs *must* be read as mediation. Critics who reject the binary (messy household economies; market peasants) reject the necessity, not merely the ethnography.

**On Tio as tin (Ch.14).** "Whereas the ultimate sacrifice is the miner himself, the spirit owner of the mines is the commodity that the miners excavate: he is the tin." Here Taussig collapses spirit-owner into commodity-fetish — the sculpture *is* the ore's social power made flesh. The line is the Bolivian climax corresponding to Cauca's barren-wage theology. It depends on secondary ethnography for the iconography and on Marxist poetics for the identity claim.

**On magic as social spell (Ch.7).** Paraphrasing Durkheim: "magic is society casting spells on itself." Taussig uses this to refuse Tylor/Frazer instrumental-error theory and to keep magic inside social philosophy — specifically use-value economics' account of commoditisation. The paraphrase is strategic: Durkheimian society-cast-spells + Marxian commodity fetish = the book's method charter.

**On task and danger (Conclusion).** "To interpret the social experience reflected in folk magic as that experience changes with a group's loss of control over its means of production is the hazardous task that I have set out to do." "Hazardous" acknowledges over-reading; "loss of control over its means of production" is the historical filter that makes folklore *about* proletarianisation rather than about timeless Andean or African "superstition."

**On ambivalence (Conclusion).** "This devil is not unambiguously terrifying. He is not more terrifying than the settlers." The sentence blocks both romantic resistance readings and colonial "primitive fear" readings. Evil is comparative and historical, not absolute.

**On Bataille's sun (*Sun Gives*).** "The sun gives without receiving" (and variants) re-keys the 1980 dialectic: expenditure without return, surplus as structural motor, stories as gifts about the gift. The 1980 book is not repudiated; it is re-voiced as insufficiently attentive to *dépense* and narrative form.

## Empirical detail: Cauca historical spine (as Taussig reconstructs)

Taussig's Part II is not only folklore. It is a regional political economy of how a free peasantry was made and unmade:

1. **Slavery and religious appropriation** — slaves and free blacks appropriated the masters' devil as "enemy's enemy"; cofradías/cabildos; feast days and purchase of freedom; Cartagena Inquisition contexts for *brujo* and *palenque* (as he synthesises).
2. **Abolition crisis** — Arboleda-led war against abolition (1851); January 1852 abolition; *concertados* as managed transition; ex-slave refusal of labour; forest *terrajeros*.
3. **Free-peasant ecology** — plantain–cocoa–tobacco on fertile banks; few functioning large estates into late 19th c. because peasants could subsist without hacienda wages.
4. **Party Manichaeism** — Liberal = freedom/Christ; Conservative = "conserve" the evil Spanish law of slavery (oral etymologies he quotes); 1876 war imagery of saints and banners; fear of reenslavement into the mid-20th-c. *violencia*.
5. **Enclosure and town** — *indiviso* commons partitioned; fences; Puerto Tejada merchants; cocoa→coffee→cane; dual proletariat of peasant-proletarians and contractors.
6. **1970s conjuncture** — unions, occupations, piecework, Green Monster broadsheet; Taussig/Rubbo embedded with militant peasant organisation.

This spine is what makes the devil contract *historical* rather than a floating "belief system." The contract appears when free-peasant use-value reproduction is pressured by plantation wage labour — not when "Africans believe in the devil."

## Empirical detail: Bolivian ritual and political spine (as synthesised)

Taussig's Part III layers:

1. **Iconography** — Tio statues; gringo technician face; sexualised power; anti-Christian mine interior.
2. **Procedure** — *ch'alla* inventories (cigarette, alcohol, coca, streamers, llama foetus, cakes); accident-driven blood sacrifice; August/Carnival special rites; pre-1952 white llama gifts involving tin barons (Patiño participation reported via sources).
3. **Political reorganisation** — 1952 nationalisation: theoretical co-ownership vs bureaucratic exclusion; work-group breakup; volume wages; 1964 military; rite suppression; union as "university."
4. **Peasant control contrast** — Bastien mountain-as-body; *aine* reciprocity; inalienable land ideals; misfortune rites on same Tue/Fri calendar as mine devil days — structural echo Taussig exploits.
5. **Historical claim** — mining always had ritual; only after conquest does the spirit of evil dominate; fiercest where return to gods/miners is nil.

Because almost all of this is **Nash-mediated**, the wiki must treat Taussig as theorist of mining culture and Nash (when ingested) as primary ethnographer. Collapsing them launders secondary evidence into primary.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Canonical in economic anthropology and courses on magic/capitalism; often paired with [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study|Mintz]] on sugar and [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] on modes of production.
- Helped naturalise **commodity fetishism** as ethnographic lens in Anglophone anthropology (Taussig's own claim that the concept was then little known in English social science — position, not wiki-voice fact about the discipline).
- Influenced work on moral economy, plantation culture, Andean mining cosmology, and "occult economies" later literatures (not fully ingested here).
- Feeds [[anthropological-political-economy]] from the cultural-Marxist side rather than the world-systems synthesis side.

## Current Standing

Still a **reference-point study** for critical-turn anthropology of capitalism and for teaching fetishism beyond *Capital*'s textbook chapter. Best read as: (1) 1970s Cauca historical ethnography of free peasantry and plantation devil lore; (2) a Marxist-poetic theory of evil under proletarianisation; (3) a secondary synthesis of Bolivian mining rites; (4) a 2010 self-critical rewrite in Bataillean key. Companion gaps: June Nash monographs; Taussig *Shamanism, Colonialism, and the Wild Man*; later occult-economies literature.

### Reflexivity of the research act

Production context: Australian/US anthropologist in Cold War and post-1960s left Colombia; militant peasant organising as access and ethics; English-language academic form that the 2009 preface indicts. Colonial afterlives of "fetish" (de Brosses → Marx → Taussig) are themselves part of the object's genealogy — the book *uses* the colonial word to reverse its charge. Bolivia's people appear mainly through other ethnographers' frames. Gender and Indigenous nationality inside "tin miners" are thinner than the dual-case slogan. These are reliability facts, not reasons to omit the study.
