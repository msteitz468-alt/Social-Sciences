# Coercion, Capital, and European States — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[tilly-coercion-capital-study]]; bibliographic record:
> [[tilly-coercion-capital-1992]]; theory page: [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]]. All
> substantive claims are Tilly's, attributed; the book is secondary synthesis, and its
> historical detail rests on the literature it cites.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Coercion, Capital, and European States* (1990; revised 1992 to run "AD 990–1992") is the
mature statement of the war-centered account of European state formation. Its question: why so
many *kinds* of state across a millennium — empires, city-states, federations, national states —
and why eventual convergence on the [[national-state]]? "Why were the directions of change so
similar and the paths so different?"

The book's lineage is explicit. Dedicated to Stein Rokkan, it aims to "build on the strengths
and correct the errors" of Tilly & co.'s *The Formation of National States in Western Europe*
(1975), whose implicit "new unilinear story — one running from war to extraction and repression
to state formation... was wrong." It began as an escape from a larger cities-and-states project
with Wim Blockmans, delivered as the 1987 Cornell Messenger Lectures, and answers Perry
Anderson's 1974 axiom that "it is the construction and destruction of States which seal the
basic shifts in the relations of production." Chapter 1 measures the argument against four
rival schools — statist (Huntington; Kennedy), geopolitical (Rosenau, Thompson),
mode-of-production (Anderson), and world-system (Wallerstein, faulted for leaving "the nation's
prospering with a wispy national state" unexplained) — and against three admired but
insufficient models: Moore, Rokkan, Mumford.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why variation + convergence in state forms? | states, state system | all of Europe | model (capital × coercion) + comparative narrative | AD 990–1992 |
| How did war build states? | fiscal-military organization | France, Britain, Spain, Prussia, Dutch Republic etc. | secondary fiscal/military histories; troop and debt series | 1500–1980 |
| How did trajectories diverge? | regional political economy | Russia/Poland/Sweden; Venice/Genoa/Dutch Republic; England/France | three-path comparison (Ch. 5) | 990–1914 |
| How does the system behave? | wars and settlements | Levy catalog (119 major wars 1495–1975); Small & Singer; settlement congresses | dataset synthesis; belligerency networks | 1495–1975 |
| Why Third World divergence? | contemporary states | Sivard/Maniruzzaman cross-national data; area cases | hypothesis extension, self-labeled "speculation" | 1945–1992 |

**As conducted, not as advertised**: Tilly is unusually candid about the construction. He
reserves citations "for direct quotations and esoteric or controversial information"; he adopts
deliberate "metonymy and reification" (unitary "rulers" and "states") as the price of
millennial scale; he "make[s] no effort to review alternative theories" beyond Ch. 1's sketch;
and he confesses "my reading of the European past is unconventional, unproved, and riven with
gaps." The compensating device is a **six-point falsifiability checklist** — e.g., the argument
fails if state transformation did not track war, if rulers built states to design and
succeeded, or if the claimed regularities (concentric geography; representative institutions
against rulers' interests; Mediterranean→Atlantic shift; extinction of city-states) collapse.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The "sample" is the entire European state system across a millennium — roughly 200 states in
1490 consolidating to ~25–30 by 1890 — plus the post-1945 world system. The quantitative
skeleton is borrowed: Levy's great-power war catalog (with its ≥1,000 battle-deaths/year
threshold and Eurocentric great-power roster, which Tilly reproduces, critiques as excluding
Ming China and the Mughals, and then defends on military grounds), Small & Singer's Correlates
of War, and Sivard's military-expenditure series. Tilly flags the counts' limits himself
(Thirty Years' War total deaths "may well have topped five million, instead of the two million
sustained by the great powers"). Case narratives rest on named area literatures (Blum on
Russia, Mack Smith on Sicily, Lane on Venice, Parker on Spain, Brewer on Britain, Cumings on
Korea, Anderson on Libya).

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Extraction for war generated durable state structure (treasuries, tax bureaux, debt, courts) | fiscal-military narratives; French/British debt series; "ratchet effect" literature | canonical; mechanism contested by institutionalists |
| Bifurcation of violence: states armed, civilians disarmed; interpersonal violence fell as war intensified | homicide-series and war-frequency literatures | broadly accepted as description; causal weight vs "mentality" (Foucault, Elias) debated |
| Four stages: patrimonialism → brokerage → nationalization → specialization | military-organization histories; mercenary system detail | standard periodization, acknowledged as "cartoon history" fitting Germany better than Holland or Russia |
| Three trajectories converge on the national state because capitalized coercion wins wars | Ch. 5 three-path comparison; Venice/Russia/England contrast | the program's signature claim; tested and partly disconfirmed outside Europe |
| Bargaining over extraction produced citizenship, representation, civilianized government | 1640s crisis; French Revolution direct-rule case; military-share budget tables | widely adopted; Spain/Portugal colonial-revenue deviant case supports it |
| Peace congresses constituted the system; WWII "burst" the settlement system | war-settlement table Westphalia→Korea | descriptive synthesis, standard |
| Post-1945 external coercion supply → military rule, no citizenship loop | Sivard/Maniruzzaman data; ~40% of states under military control in the 1980s | self-labeled "speculation" with named untested rivals; the program's most contested extension |

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book persuades by **aphorism anchored in series data**. The famous formulations — "war made
states, and vice versa"; rulers as "racketeers"; "states that lost wars commonly contracted,
and often ceased to exist" — sit atop tables of troop strengths, war frequencies, and battle
deaths. Two rhetorical devices do heavy lifting: the *wasp-to-locomotive* metaphor (extractive
predators becoming service-bound machines "on the rails of sustenance from the civilian
population"), and the retrospective *anti-teleology tableau* (Charles V's 1555 abdication, when
"nothing then assured the ultimate victory of the national state"). The Elias emendation is a
signature analytic move: the tax/military monopoly "duo... actually forms two voices of a trio.
The missing member, credit." So is the citizenship formulation: "The core of what we now call
'citizenship'... consists of multiple bargains hammered out by rulers and ruled in the course
of their struggles over the means of state action, especially the making of war."

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book became the charter of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]] and the standard
citation for war-centered [[state-formation]]. Its vocabulary — [[capitalized-coercion]],
coercion-/capital-intensive paths, [[national-state]] vs nation-state, [[direct-rule]],
extraction-bargaining, protection racket — organizes comparative politics and historical
sociology syllabi. It complements and contests [[mann-michael|Mann]]'s IEMP program (see the
dialogue note on [[tilly-charles]]) and gave fiscal sociology its most-quoted mechanism.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

- **Tilly's own checklist** invites disconfirmation on six named grounds; he also names the
  expected critics (Strayer: domestic peacemaking; North: property rights; Wallerstein: capital;
  Anderson: nobilities).
- **Extra-European tests** (not yet ingested as primaries): Centeno's Latin America ("blood and
  debt" — limited wars, limited states) and Herbst's Africa (low-density war absent →
  Tilly-mechanism absent) turned the model's failures into findings; Ertman and Downing refined
  the European paths.
- **Internal hedges as positions**: the Ch. 7 mechanism is "speculation" with two untested
  rivals; the typology "captures a significant part of the geographic and temporal variation,
  but not all of it"; the model's application to the Third World risks "intellectual
  colonialism" (Tilly's phrase).
- **Gender**: Tilly's one-line observation that women's near-absence from coercive organization
  "surely helps account for their subordinate position within states" is left as an aside —
  later feminist state theory made the omission itself a critique.

## Current Standing

A living classic: the reference model that subsequent state-formation research confirms,
qualifies, or attacks. Its data horizon (war catalogs to 1975; military data to 1986; text to
spring 1992) and its Eurocentric system rosters are known limits; the mechanism claims remain
the field's common coin.

## Historiography and Sources

Edition ingested: revised Blackwell edition (1992), extending the original *AD 990–1990*
(1990) with the Soviet collapse folded in "in the spring of 1992." The book sits inside
Tilly's own *Studies in Social Discontinuity* series. Prior Tilly programs in this vault
(contention, durable inequality, contentious politics — see [[tilly-charles]]) frame this as
his third major research line; the Rokkan dedication and the 1975 volume are its immediate
ancestry.
