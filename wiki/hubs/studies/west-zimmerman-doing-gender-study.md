# Doing Gender (West & Zimmerman, 1987) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study]]. Source volume: [[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]]. Authors: [[west-candace]], [[zimmerman-don-h]]. Program: [[doing-gender]]. Companion case: [[garfinkel-agnes-study]].

**Analysis standard:** design and findings tables · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife in full · nine canonical sections. All program claims in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

[[west-candace|Candace West]] and [[zimmerman-don-h|Don H. Zimmerman]] wrote "Doing Gender" to answer a pedagogical and theoretical impasse of the late 1960s–1970s classroom. Teachers carefully distinguished **sex** (ascribed biology) from **gender** (achieved social status), drawing on hermaphrodite case studies and exoticized anthropology, yet students became confused: sex did not look "given" under conflicting criteria, and gender did not look freely "achieved" under labor divisions, identity formation, and men's subordination of women. Worse, socialization theories fixed gender by about age five—making "achievement" look as static as sex.

By about 1975, West and Zimmerman argued, the relation of biology and culture looked more reflexive than teachers had supposed, and structural arrangements (work and family) were shown to produce capacities once treated as biological. In that confusion, "the notion of gender as a recurring achievement somehow fell by the wayside."

Their purpose was programmatic: an **ethnomethodologically informed, distinctively sociological** understanding of gender as a *routine, methodical, and recurring accomplishment*, undertaken by women and men "whose competence as members of society is hostage to its production." Doing gender, they held, involves "a complex of socially guided perceptual, interactional, and micropolitical activities that cast particular pursuits as expressions of masculine and feminine 'natures.'"

**What it reacts against.** (1) Trait and sex-difference psychology that treats gender as internal property. (2) Role theory's "sex roles" / "gender roles," which, they argued, obscure the work of producing gender and misfit master identities that cut across situations. (3) Goffman's **gender display** (1976), which they treated as a partial sociological corrective that still relegates gender to optional, scheduled performances at the periphery of "serious" interaction. (4) The pedagogical sex/gender binary that collapsed everyday categorization with either biology or role.

**What it develops from.** [[ethnomethodology]]—especially [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s [[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes case]], membership categorization (Sacks), and [[accountability]] (via Heritage's reading of the ethnomethodological tradition); [[symbolic-interactionism]]'s provisional, constructed social life; feminist political need for a non-biological account of change (Dorothy Smith later stressed this political origin of the sex/gender distinction).

**Publication path (historiography of the text).** Per Fenstermaker and West's 2002 Introduction: the core statement was drafted around **1977** (also an ASA paper); it circulated privately and was repeatedly rejected—read either as a trivial micro replacement for role theory or as blasphemy against biological difference. [[fenstermaker-sarah|Sarah Fenstermaker]]'s *The Gender Factory* used the framework in print; renewed exposure and her urging prompted resubmission. Judith Lorber's new *Gender & Society* (Sociologists for Women in Society) published it in **1987**. The 2002 Routledge volume reprints it as Chapter 1 of the program archive.

**The pedagogical origin, in the authors' own words.** The article opens by narrating the classroom impasse directly, and the framing is load-bearing for everything that follows:

> "In the beginning, there was sex and there was gender. Those of us who taught courses in the late 1960s and early 1970s were careful to distinguish one from the other. Sex, we told students, was what was ascribed by biology: anatomy, hormones, and physiology. Gender, we said, was an achieved status: that which is constructed through psychological, cultural, and social means."

They then narrate the collapse of the tidy binary from two directions at once—empirically, sex refused to behave as a "given" ("sex hardly seemed a 'given' in the context of research that illustrated the sometimes ambiguous and often conflicting criteria for its ascription"), and gender refused to behave as freely "achieved" once its structural embedding in labor, identity formation, and subordination came into view. The double bind is the article's true starting problem: not "what is gender" in the abstract, but how to keep gender *both* socially constructed *and* durably, non-optionally binding on conduct—an accomplishment that nonetheless behaves, phenomenologically, like a fact of nature. Everything from the sex/sex-category/gender triad to the accountability concept is built to solve that double bind, not merely to assert social constructionism.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How is gender produced as situated, accountable interaction? | Interactional situation; sex-category membership | No primary field sample; secondary Agnes case + everyday examples | Ethnomethodologically informed conceptual reconstruction | Draft ~1977; pub. 1987 |

**As advertised:** a theoretical article proposing analytic distinctions and research directions, not a survey or ethnography.

**As conducted:** the article is an **argumentative reconstruction**. It (a) diagnoses received meanings of gender; (b) installs the triad sex / [[sex-category|sex category]] / gender; (c) re-reads Agnes and categorization troubles as making "normal" incumbents' work visible; (d) redefines doing gender via accountability; (e) inventories institutional "resources" (bathrooms, sports, mating, conversation work) that produce difference and naturalize it; (f) sketches research agendas (child recruitment, household labor, occupations). Empirical warrant is **illustrative and secondary**. That is a design fact, not a defect relative to the paper's genre—but it means later "replications" are really *applications and extensions*, not retests of a single finding set (`replication_status: not-applicable`).

**Genre flag.** This is squarely a **theoretical/programmatic article**, not a fieldwork study in the ordinary sense of the Studies Hub's other exemplars (e.g., Durkheim's comparative-legal treatise, or a first-hand ethnography). West and Zimmerman collected no interviews, ran no survey, and conducted no observation of their own; their empirical material is entirely **secondary and borrowed**—Garfinkel's Agnes transcripts (1958–59), Kessler and McKenna's categorization experiments, Fishman's conversational-analysis tapes, and a scatter of illustrative anecdotes (the computer-store salesperson, sex-segregated bathrooms, organized sports). The paper's authority rests on the **quality of its conceptual carpentry**, not on new data: it takes existing ethnomethodological materials that had not been read as being *about gender specifically* and re-reads them through a purpose-built analytic apparatus (the sex/sex-category/gender triad plus accountability). This is precisely why `replication_status: not-applicable` is the correct code, and why the paper's real empirical program had to be built afterward, by others, applying the framework to new sites (household labor, cosmetic surgery, institutional meetings, adoption policy)—documented in the Reception and Critiques sections below.

**Two intellectual debts the design makes explicit.** First, [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s **"natural attitude"** toward gender: competent societal members treat sex-category membership as a "natural, normal" fact, unproblematic and self-evident, discoverable rather than constructed—West and Zimmerman's whole project is to show how that taken-for-granted naturalness is itself an interactional *product*. Second, [[unknown|Goffman]]'s "Gender Display" (1976), which they treat with genuine ambivalence: Goffman supplied the vocabulary of gender as conventionalized, learned performance, but—in West and Zimmerman's reading—confined that performance to optional, "scheduled" moments (a display one can switch on or off), leaving the bulk of interaction as a gender-neutral backdrop against which displays occasionally occur. Their central methodological wager is that this is exactly backward: gender is not a special-occasion overlay on interaction but a constant, background condition of being intelligibly social at all.

## The Site / Sample / Data

There is no single site. Recurrent materials:

- **Garfinkel's Agnes** (1958–59 interviews; published 1967) — "practical methodologist" of female incumbency; continuous passing work before and after surgery; makes visible what culture made invisible. West and Zimmerman stress that Garfinkel did not explicitly separate sex, sex category, and gender—the triad is their analytic contribution layered on the case.
- **Everyday categorization** — Kessler and McKenna on genitalia conventionally hidden yet a world of two sexes "observed"; Sacks's "if-can" test; Diane Margolis's computer-store salesperson whose sex category could not be resolved (moral disturbance at ambiguity).
- **Institutional resources** — Goffman's toilet segregation; sports and assortative mating; Fishman on women's conversational work; occupational accommodations (woman engineer, woman physician).
- **Cited empirical allies** — Fenstermaker Berk on household dual production; Hochschild on flight attendants (emotional labor as essential femininity).

**Data quality as a design fact.** Because none of this is West and Zimmerman's own fieldwork, the paper's evidentiary reliability is inherited wholesale from its sources, and unevenly. The Agnes material is the strongest single case—rich, longitudinal (roughly a year of clinical contact), and independently documented by Garfinkel with case-file detail—but it is also, as the Historiography section below notes, itself contested on reliability grounds unrelated to West and Zimmerman's use of it. Kessler and McKenna's categorization work and Sacks's membership-categorization observations are analytic/experimental rather than naturalistic. The remaining illustrations—Margolis's computer-store anecdote, Goffman's bathroom-segregation observation, everyday "if-can" categorization—are **anecdotal exemplars marshaled for conceptual clarity**, not a sampled evidence base; they are chosen because they are vivid and generalizable-sounding, not because they were the output of a systematic search for disconfirming cases. This is consistent with the paper's genre (programmatic theory-building) but means a reader should not treat the article's illustrative catalogue as itself an empirical inventory of "how gender gets done"—that inventory is what the subsequent empirical program (Ch. 6–9 of the 2002 volume) was built to supply.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### 1. The load-bearing definition of gender as emergent

> "Rather than as a property of individuals, we conceive of gender as an emergent feature of social situations: both as an outcome of and a rationale for various social arrangements and as a means of legitimating one of the most fundamental divisions of society."

This sentence does triple work: (i) anti-individualist relocation of the object; (ii) dual role of gender as *product* and *justification* of arrangements; (iii) legitimation function that connects micro accomplishment to macro inequality. The 2002 volume uses it as epigraph for a reason—it is the program in one breath.

### 2. The triad as surgical instrument

West and Zimmerman state the three definitions in a single dense passage that is worth quoting at length, since almost every later application of the framework turns on the precise wording:

> "Sex is a determination made through the application of socially agreed upon biological criteria for classifying persons as females or males. The criteria for classification can be genitalia at birth or chromosomal typing before birth, and they do not necessarily agree with one another. Placement in a sex category is achieved through application of the sex criteria, but in everyday life, categorization is established and sustained by the socially required identificatory displays that proclaim one's membership in one or the other category. In this sense, one's sex category presumes one's sex and stands as proxy for it in many situations, but sex and sex category can vary independently; that is, it is possible to claim membership in a sex category even when the sex criteria are lacking. Gender, in contrast, is the activity of managing situated conduct in light of normative conceptions of attitudes and activities appropriate for one's sex category."

Compressed: **Sex** = application of socially agreed biological criteria (themselves a social process—Note 2, flagging that even the "biological" pole is a classificatory achievement, not raw nature). **Sex category** = everyday membership via identificatory displays; proxy for sex; independently variable—this is the term that does the paper's most original work, since it names the *social* act of classification as distinct from both the biological substrate and the ongoing conduct of gender. **Gender** = managing situated conduct in light of normative conceptions appropriate to one's sex category.

The triad prevents two collapses: reducing gender to biology (sex), and reducing gender to costume (a display detachable from any consequence). "Women can be seen as unfeminine but that does not make them 'unfemale'"—sex category is not the same as successful femininity; one can fail at "doing gender" well while one's *membership* in the sex category remains, for interactional purposes, secure. Conversely, Agnes shows the reverse possibility: sex-category membership can be actively *managed and sustained* even where the biological criteria are absent altogether, which is precisely what proves sex category is not simply sex read off the body.

### 3. Against Goffman without abandoning dramaturgy

They grant Goffman that gender depictions are conventionalized portrayals, then refuse optionality:

> "While it is plausible to contend that gender displays—construed as conventionalized expressions—are optional, it does not seem plausible to say that we have the option of being seen by others as female or male."

The move preserves interactional analysis while insisting sex category is not a weekend hobby. Later Moloney and Fenstermaker (Ch. 10) will police a related non-synonymy with Butlerian *performativity*.

### 4. Accountability as the hinge to structure

The paper's most consequential technical move is grafting Garfinkelian/Heritage **accountability** onto sex category:

> "to 'do' gender is not always to live up to normative conceptions of femininity or masculinity; it is to engage in behavior at the risk of gender assessment. Though it is individuals who do gender, the enterprise is fundamentally interactional and institutional in character, for accountability is a feature of social relationships, and its idiom is drawn from the institutional arena in which those relationships are enacted."

This is how a micro paper claims not to be *merely* micro: the *idiom* of assessment is institutional. Critics of "Doing Difference" will later say this still under-delivers on power; the program's reply is that process (accountability) is what makes structural outcomes possible (1995 reply; Ch. 11).

### 5. Unavoidability and the politics of change

> "Insofar as a society is partitioned by 'essential' differences between women and men and placement in a sex category is both relevant and enforced, doing gender is unavoidable."

Unavoidability is conditional, not metaphysical—and the closing sections argue that *how* gender is done can change when institutional and interactional standards shift. The paper thus tries to hold feminist change politics together with a non-optional analysis of sex category.

### 6. Agnes as visibility engine, not as "fake woman"

They insist Agnes was not faking what real women do naturally; she had to analyze what others take for granted. The case is a **methodological lever** for seeing accomplishment—consistent with ethnomethodology's use of trouble to recover the normal. (The later disclosure about Agnes's estrogen history is a reliability issue for Garfinkel's case construction, filed on [[garfinkel-agnes-study]], not a fieldwork error by West and Zimmerman.) On the specific mechanics of Agnes's "secret apprenticeship," West and Zimmerman note that Agnes had no cultural manual to draw on for how to *become* recognizably female under scrutiny — "Agnes could perhaps have used such sources as manuals, but we contend that no such recipe exists" — and so had to piece together competent femininity by trial, observation, and the coaching of intimates (it was, notably, "through her fiancé that Agnes learned" some of the practical minutiae of gender-appropriate self-presentation). The point generalizes: if a highly motivated, closely observed adult cannot simply consult a rulebook for "how to be a woman," that absence of a codifiable recipe is itself evidence that gender competence is *tacit, interactionally acquired, and continuously monitored* — not a stored script that competent members merely execute.

### 7. The omnirelevance of sex category

The paper's closing analytic move universalizes the argument by claiming sex category is never simply irrelevant to a scene, even when unmarked:

> "Insofar as sex category is omnirelevant to social life... it serves as an ever-available resource for the design and interpretation of social conduct. What this means is that an individual involved in virtually any course of action may be held accountable for her/his execution of that action as a woman or a man."

The Marcia-the-police-officer / John-the-preschool-aide examples that follow in the text are not throwaway illustrations but the argument's proof of concept: any occupational or activity choice, however gender-neutral it appears on its face, is available to be read *as* a gendered choice, and the person making it knows this and orients to it — which is exactly what "accountability" means operationally. Note the qualification the authors insist on immediately afterward: "to do gender is not always to live up to normative conceptions of femininity or masculinity; it is to engage in action at the risk of being held accountable for it." Doing gender well and doing gender *at all* are different claims — the second is the unavoidable one, the first is not.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Disciplinary home:** U.S. sociology of gender; *Gender & Society* as institutional vehicle; teaching canon.
- **Vocabulary absorption:** "doing gender," sex category, gender accountability—used far beyond the original ethnomethodological circle (education, work/organizations, medical sociology, criminology).
- **Empirical program** (sampled in the 2002 volume): household labor as dual production of goods/services *and* gender (*Gender Factory*; Ch. 6); cosmetic-surgery accounting (Dull & West, Ch. 7); UC Regents affirmative-action meeting as race/class/gender accountability (West & Fenstermaker, Ch. 8); California second-parent adoptions as institutional change via interaction (Dalton & Fenstermaker, Ch. 9).
- **Extensions:** West & Fenstermaker "[[doing-difference]]" (1995)—race and class as analogous accomplishments; symposium and reply ([[doing-difference-debate]]).
- **Adjacent dialogues:** Butlerian performativity (Moloney & Fenstermaker Ch. 10—shared anti-essentialism, divergent lineages and empirical discipline); Connell's gender order / gender relations (cited in role critique; not identical programs); Hochschild emotional labor as cited ally.
- **Dorothy Smith** (2002 foreword): praises research-disciplined iteration vs. theory-only reinvention; still unconvinced power is fully inside the circumference—productive tension, not rejection.
- **Institutional-change extension (Dalton & Fenstermaker, Ch. 9, on second-parent adoption).** This chapter is a useful test of how far the "doing gender" apparatus travels institutionally: it argues, in the authors' words, that "individuals interacting within institutional settings frequently accomplish gender—through face-to-face interactions—in accordance with normative conceptions that are embedded within the institution's history, policies, and practice," while also showing those same interactions can, over repeated contestation, "plant the seeds of institutional change" — subversive practice becoming, eventually, "only natural." This is the clearest place in the program archive where the micro/institutional loop promised in the 1987 article's closing pages is actually worked through with a concrete case (a California child-welfare agency's shift on lesbian and gay adoptive parents in the 1990s).
- **Textbook and cross-disciplinary uptake:** the framework entered undergraduate sociology-of-gender curricula rapidly and durably; "doing gender" is now used loosely, well beyond ethnomethodological circles, sometimes shorn of the accountability apparatus that gives it its analytic bite — a genericization the authors and later commentators (see Deutsch below) note with some ambivalence.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` for the 1987 article as such — there is no single finding to replicate, only a framework to apply, extend, or contest. Afterlife clusters:

1. **Early rejection themes** (pre-1987): micro-triviality vs. biological blasphemy—the reception history *is* part of the study's meaning. Fenstermaker and West's own 2002 account of the paper's difficult path to publication (repeated rejection over roughly a decade) is itself evidence for how disruptive the reframing was to both mainstream sex-role sociology and to some feminist readings that wanted gender inequality traced directly to biology-adjacent structural causes rather than to interactional process.
2. **[[doing-difference-debate]] (1995):** West and Fenstermaker's "Doing Difference" (1995) extended the accomplishment framework from gender alone to race, class, and gender simultaneously — "difference as a routine, methodical, and ongoing accomplishment," per the 2002 volume's own gloss. A *Gender & Society* symposium of invited responses followed, and the positions are worth recording individually, in their strongest form, as this wiki's Position Recording register requires: **Patricia Hill Collins** argued the framework's flattening of race, class, and gender into structurally parallel "accomplishments" depoliticizes them — a difference-without-power reading that risks becoming, in her phrase, "a difference that didn't make any difference." **Lynn Weber** credited the framework's strength on simultaneity (no single axis analyzed in isolation) but argued it obscures power differentials and organized resistance communities. **Howard Winant** found the extension to race promising as a bridge to hegemony theory but judged the structural apparatus too thin to bear the weight, with social change under-theorized. **Barrie Thorne** pressed a functionalist-tilt charge — that an accomplishment framework, by its own logic, struggles to explain how categories themselves get transformed or abolished rather than merely reproduced — and pointed to Butler and Connell as offering more resources for theorizing categorical change. **Dana Takagi** and others pressed a metaphor-versus-mechanism concern: is "accomplishment" doing real causal-explanatory work, or is it redescription dressed as explanation? West and Fenstermaker's reply distinguishes process from outcome (the framework explains *how* difference gets produced moment to moment, not that outcomes are equal or innocuous), insists resistance is ubiquitous within the framework (accountability cuts both ways — one can be held accountable for *departing* from as well as *conforming to* normative expectations), and locates change in the accumulation of successfully contested, "confounded" categorizations plus organized social movements.
3. **"Undo gender" / change debates (Deutsch 2007; Risman 2009, attributed from later literature, not in the 2002 volume):** if sex category is "omnirelevant" and doing gender is "unavoidable" wherever it is enforced, critics ask whether the framework can coherently theorize gender's *reduction* or *undoing*, as opposed to only its endless reproduction in new forms. Francine Deutsch's "Undoing Gender" (2007) argued the original framework over-predicts inertia and under-specifies the conditions under which interactions actually erode rather than reinforce gender difference. Barbara Risman's structural reworking of "doing gender" into a multi-level "gender as social structure" account (individual, interactional, institutional) is a related attempt to give the change-mechanism more traction. West and Zimmerman's own later reply, "Accounting for Doing Gender" (*Gender & Society*, 2009, attributed from later literature), pushed back that "undoing" readings misconstrue the original argument: unavoidability was always conditional on sex category remaining "relevant and enforced," and the 1987 article's own closing pages already gesture at both interactional and institutional levels of possible change — the theoretical resources for "undoing" were present from the start, they argue, even if under-elaborated.
4. **Butler synonymy.** Ch. 10 of the 2002 volume (Moloney and Fenstermaker) directly compares the two programs and quotes Judith Butler's own formulation for contrast: "gender is not a noun, but neither is it a set of free-floating attributes, for we have seen that the substantive effect of gender is performatively produced and compelled by the regulatory practices of gender coherence... gender proves to be performative—that is constituting the identity it is purported to be." The chapter finds real convergence — both reject gender as a pre-social essence, both center regulatory, accountable normativity — but insists the lineages diverge sharply: West and Zimmerman's apparatus is disciplined by ethnomethodological attention to observable interactional mechanics (accountability, sex category, situated conduct), while Butler's performativity is rooted in poststructuralist and psychoanalytic theory and is pitched at the level of discursive constitution of the subject rather than face-to-face interaction order. The chapter also notes, pointedly, that Butler herself characterizes some strands of constructionist gender theory as "sociological" in a somewhat dismissive sense — a friction the volume treats as a live disciplinary boundary dispute (sociology vs. humanities theorizing of gender) rather than a settled question of who said it first or best.
5. **Agnes afterlife:** reanalysis of Garfinkel's case reliability—downstream of the ethnomethodological source, not of West & Zimmerman's own data collection. Filed on [[garfinkel-agnes-study]].
6. **Genericization critique.** A milder, more diffuse line of commentary (attributed, general disciplinary observation) holds that as "doing gender" became a stock phrase in undergraduate teaching and popular sociological writing, it was often stripped of the accountability mechanism that gives the 1987 argument its explanatory content, leaving "gender is performed/constructed" as a slogan detached from the specific interactional theory that produced it — a fate common to influential concepts (compare [[anomie]]'s drift across Durkheim, Merton, and popular usage) and a caution for any wiki page citing "doing gender" loosely.

## Current Standing

Still the default classical reference for interactional sociology of gender in the United States. Best read as a **program charter**, not as a freestanding ethnography. Pair with:

- [[garfinkel-agnes-study]] for the visibility engine  
- [[accountability]] for the technical hinge  
- [[doing-difference]] + [[doing-difference-debate]] for the multi-category extension and its contestation  
- *Gender Factory* / Ch. 6–9 for empirical applications  
- Ch. 10 for Butler non-synonymy  
- Ch. 11 / Conclusion for the program's own unfinished agenda (micro/macro as relationship; simultaneous differences; lasting change)

Status of the program on the summary theory page: **active**, with contested multi-category extension.

What is discarded or heavily qualified by later work: the implication (never quite asserted but often read into the 1987 text) that gender's reproduction is close to inevitable in any encounter where sex category is salient — the "undoing gender" literature has pushed the program to specify change-mechanisms more explicitly than the original article did. What endures without serious challenge: the analytic separation of sex, sex category, and gender (now closer to disciplinary common sense than to a contested claim); accountability as the mechanism linking micro conduct to normative, institutionally sourced standards; and the diagnosis that gender differences function partly to *legitimate* the arrangements that produced them — a claim with continuing purchase in organizational and workplace-inequality research. The article's status as an entry point to ethnomethodology for gender scholars who are not otherwise conversant with Garfinkel is also durable: it remains one of the more widely assigned bridges between interactionist microsociology and the sociology of gender, precisely because it translates Garfinkel's more technical apparatus (indexicality, reflexivity, accountability) into a vocabulary usable outside ethnomethodology proper.
