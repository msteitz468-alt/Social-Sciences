## Question and Intellectual Context

*The Mountain People* asks what happens to human social bonds when a small mountain population is stripped of mobility and hunting, forced into drought agriculture, and starved. Turnbull's answer is absolute in tone: the [[ik|Ik]] he lived among had, he claimed, discarded family, love, law, and ritual as unaffordable luxuries, retaining only individual stomachs and a thin shell of village form. The book is simultaneously a famine ethnography, a human-nature tract, a policy memo, and a confessional — "a voyage in quest of the basic human and a discovery of his potential for inhumanity."

The intellectual contrast with *[[turnbull-forest-people-1961|The Forest People]]* (1961) is structural. Among the [[mbuti|Mbuti]], Turnbull had written of forest benevolence, egalitarian ridicule-as-law, and joyful ritual. Among the Ik he wrote of mountain imprisonment, compound fortification against neighbours, and the death of goodness. He had trained under [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] and made his name as a humanistic popular ethnographer; *Mountain People* weaponises that same literary intimacy — named persons, first-person entanglement — for horror rather than romance. Elizabeth Marshall Thomas had pointed him toward the group; the project was a **third choice** after Congo villager work and Andaman Onge research both failed. That accident of career logistics matters for Position B in the later debate: the ethnographer arrived cold.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What remains of sociality under famine after hunting-ground loss? | Named individuals + village clusters | Pirre (~7 villages), Naputiro, Police Post, Kasilé relief orbit | Residence, Icietot, case narrative | 1964–67 |
| What was the pre-restriction order? | Reconstructed annual cycle | Kidepo, Didinga Mts, Kenya escarpment, Morungole | Elder report + H&G analogy | retrospective |
| How do neighbours structure survival? | Multi-ethnic borderland | Dodos, Turkana, Didinga, administration | Observation + trade/raid politics | same |
| What should the state do? | Policy object "the Ik" | ~2,000 persons (his estimate) | Report to government | end of fieldwork |

**Fieldwork as lived.** Turnbull reached Kaabong via Moroto with Acoli interpreter Martin, learned language from mission boys Peter and Thomas (who revealed the ethnonym **Ik** / language **Icietot**), then climbed to Pirre with a niampara and brother as porters. He built a house near Atum's village and the Police Post — proximity that bought observation and entanglement. American Museum funding and Makerere-brokered restricted-area permission placed him in a militarised drought frontier of cattle raiding, Turkana occupation, and Obote-era politics. Joseph Towles shared "much of the experience" but is deliberately written out of the book.

**What the methods section omits or underplays.** (1) **Third-choice demoralisation** is confessed in Ch.1 but rarely treated as a validity threat in the book's self-presentation of clinical honesty. (2) **Feeding interventions** made Turnbull a food source and political actor — data-generating, not neutral. (3) **Return-visit design**: a second season after harvest tests whether plenty restores sociality; he reports it does not — a powerful claim that depends entirely on his coding of "still selfish." (4) **Towles's absent parallel record** is a structural gap: one collaborator's full experience is withheld by design. (5) **No technical monograph** accompanies this popular book (unlike *Wayward Servants* for the Mbuti), so the scholarly control text never arrived.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The mid-1960s Ik of this book are not a random sample of a timeless tribe. They are **settled mountain farmers** on the edge of a **national park** that had closed their best hunting ground, living through multi-year drought in a **three-state borderland** of herder warfare. Population ~2,000 is Turnbull's figure. Pirre water hole, Morungole slopes, Naputiro ridge villages, Lomej (rejected relocation site), Kasilé relief, and escarpment caves form the spatial grid.

**Key persons** function as recurrent exemplars:

| Person | Role in the text |
|---|---|
| Atum | Central broker, rain-tree fees, political schemer, employer of Turnbull's attention |
| Lolim | Ritual priest; dwindling power; dies after abuse and hunger |
| Kauar | Exceptional kindness; dies on path with mail/food; mocked for feeding children |
| Adupa | ~13, "mad"/kind; tormented; dies after enclosure without food |
| Amuarkuar, Lomeraniang, Loiangorok, Lo'ono | Elders who die "without complaint" or of grief/neglect |
| Nangoli (widow) | Only "real family"; runaway village; love of landscape |
| Nangoli (Lolim's daughter) | "Last Ik who was human"; mad gardener; jail preference |
| Lomongin | Ascendant operator; bond-friend instrumentalism |
| Lokeléa | Topos among Ik; kindness mocked; night song to God |
| Pedo | Risks life for bond friend; father dies of thirst in his absence |

These names are the book's evidence technology: horror and exception travel together through biography, not through tables.

**Residual structure Turnbull still recorded** (critical for Position B): *ao*/*asak*/*odok* architecture; *di* sitting places; *mkungu* and elders; *nyot* bond friendship; age bands; weapons and cattle-holding economy; *abang* gourds and cave stores; di dispute norms; Nangoli family cohesion; Kauar's and Pedo's costly help; hair-teasing as near-affection. Any fair use of the book must hold **generalisation and exception** in the same hand.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. The mirror preface.** Shock vocabulary ("inhuman") is invited then reversed: the reader discovers "oneself." This is literary ethics — and a licence to universalise.

**2. Third-choice clinical voice.** "I neither knew anything nor… did I particularly care… clinical observation was more like it." Honesty about demoralisation doubles as claim to unsentimental accuracy.

**3. Mbuti foil.** Mountains : forest :: imprisonment : benevolence. The earlier book is the control group the Ik fail.

**4. Camp analogies.** Concentration camps / Treblinka / Belsen-Buchenwald body similes place the Ik inside Holocaust memory without the camps' intentional perpetrators — a rhetorical escalation that Position C reads as violence of representation.

**5. Exception-then-demotion.** Hair-teasing is "closest… to affection," then reread as vanity. Kauar is kind, then dies proving kindness is folly. The pattern trains the reader to discount residual sociality.

**6. Policy as ethnography's climax.** Dispersal advice is not an appendix; it is the practical upshot of the lovelessness thesis. Random units of ten make kinship "immaterial" — theory enacted as population engineering.

**7. Civilizational coda.** Ch.12 leaves Uganda for nuclear war, pollution, nursing homes, and "perfect vegetable" futures. The Ik become a prophecy, not a people.

Load-bearing quote (human nature):

> "The Ik teach us that our much vaunted human values are not inherent in humanity at all, but are associated only with a particular form of survival called society, and that all, even society itself, are luxuries that can be dispensed with."

Load-bearing quote (policy):

> "men, women and children could be rounded up at random and should be dispersed throughout the country, in its mountainous regions, in small units of about ten. Age, sex or kinship was immaterial."

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Outside anthropology the book was a bestseller of bleakness — cited in discussions of famine, "breakdown of society," and human nature. Inside the discipline it became a **scandal text**: assigned as much for what not to do as for what it found. It fed later [[crisis-of-representation|crisis of representation]] and research-ethics syllabi alongside Yanomami controversies. Turnbull's reputation, already split between popular gift and scholarly suspicion after *Forest People*, tilted toward the second pole. The Ik entered textbooks as the people without love — a stereotype later ethnography has had to undo.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

See full position map on [[turnbull-ik-representation-debate]]. Summarised afterlife:

- **Accuracy critiques:** undercounted sharing, overgeneralised from famine, selective cases, linguistic/cultural residuals ignored.
- **Method critiques:** demoralisation, intervention effects, single-voice monopoly ("if others interpret more charitably they are lucky"), absent Towles counter-narrative, no technical monograph.
- **Ethics critiques:** camp analogies; extinction hope; dispersal that would kill language and peoplehood; popular packaging of a living minority as inhuman.
