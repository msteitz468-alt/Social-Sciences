# Natural Symbols (Douglas) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[douglas-natural-symbols-study]]. Source: [[douglas-natural-symbols-1970]]. Author: [[douglas-mary]]. Program: [[grid-group-analysis]]. Predecessor study: [[douglas-purity-and-danger-study]] / hub [[hubs/studies/douglas-purity-and-danger-study]].

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Natural Symbols: Explorations in Cosmology* (first Barrie & Rockliff 1970; Pelican revision 1973; this ingest uses the 1996 Routledge edition with new introduction) is [[douglas-mary|Mary Douglas]]'s second program-statement after *[[douglas-purity-and-danger-1966|Purity and Danger]]* (1966). Where the earlier book made dirt, pollution, and [[taboo]] effects of classification and social order ([[purity-pollution-theory]], [[matter-out-of-place]]), *Natural Symbols* asks why some social environments sustain strong ritual and tight category boundaries while others run on anti-ritual, inner experience, and weakly bounded cosmologies — and what that implies for the body as a symbolic medium.

The public occasion was the late 1960s. Douglas dated the initial stimulus to the Dominicans at Blackfriars, Oxford (St Thomas Day lecture, 7 March 1968; published as "The Contempt of Ritual" in *New Blackfriars*), and to the Munro lectures at Edinburgh (May 1968). The 1996 introduction restates the starting problem as student revolt against dead ritual and meaningless forms, post–Vatican II Catholic anti-ritualism (letter vs spirit; hierarchical remoteness), and a broader Western longing for sincerity and equality that still collides with power and wealth as barriers to communication. Douglas doubted the remedy of sweeping away rituals and institutions so people might "speak from the heart": attacking rituals, she argued, attacked the surface.

Intellectually the book sits at the intersection of:
- **Durkheimian classification** ([[durkheim-emile]], [[mauss-marcel]], *Primitive Classification*) — society as prototype of logical relations; the quest for natural symbols becomes a quest for **natural systems of symbolizing**.
- **Bernstein's sociology of speech and control** ([[bernstein-basil]], [[restricted-and-elaborated-codes]]) — positional vs personal family control; restricted vs elaborated codes; later curriculum as boundary system. Douglas called the Pelican book "the other side" of Bernstein's thesis once *Class, Codes and Control* (1971) appeared.
- **Oxford structural anthropology** and high-quality Africanist ethnography (Evans-Pritchard Nilotes; Turnbull Mbuti; Woodburn Hadza; etc.) as test material, not primary fieldwork for this book.
- **[[symbolic-anthropology]]** in the classification/pollution wing (Douglas) rather than Turnerian process or Geertzian interpretation — though [[turner-victor]] and others are interlocutors.

Douglas's own program link (Pelican acknowledgements): *Purity and Danger* had emphasized communication via boundings without facing societies that persist without strongly bounded cognitive categories and that tolerate anomaly more easily. *Natural Symbols* is the answer-book to that self-posed problem.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do social environments pattern cosmology, ritual style, and body symbolism? | Control systems / social environments (not freestanding individuals) | Comparative secondary cases + Western religious sociology | Two-axis typology (grid × group) adapted from Bernstein; long-run match of medium and control | Synthesis 1968–70; rev. 1973; intro 1996 |
| Why anti-ritualism now? | Institutional and class locations of ritual vs anti-ritual | Bog Irish Friday abstinence; student revolt; Catholic reform; sects | Reject deprivation/maladjustment as sole explanation; locate on grid-group and speech-code analogues | 1960s–70s Western cases |
| Do body rules track social control? | Bodily control, trance, waste, spirit/matter | Nilotes, sects, industrial impersonal grid, alienation codes | "Two bodies" correlation; purity rule | Comparative |

**As conducted (not only as advertised):** Douglas did not run a survey or a multi-site restudy. She selected ethnographies she judged high-quality and accessible, plus sectarian and historical cases, and read them through a two-dimensional control model. The Pelican revision is methodologically important: she moved from assigning each society a quadrant toward **scatter patterns** of group pressure and classificatory coherence assessable from ethnography or historical biography. She admitted (1996) that early diagrams now seem complicated and later versions simpler. She also admitted the Bernstein adaptation is crude relative to his subtlety — a "limping parody" when variables are forced from London families onto tribal comparison by stripping industrial division-of-labour effects.

**Assumptions:** (1) Control systems must be legitimated by ultimate principles about man and cosmos (Weberian note). (2) Coding of the medium (speech, ritual, body) interacts with the control system and should match in the long run. (3) Cross-cultural comparison of dogmas without a social-environment theory is subjective; comparison of **environments** is the corrective. (4) Illustrative ethnography is evidence for tendencies, not a full sample of world societies.

**Gaps the methods section does not fix:** Galton-style non-independence of African cases; selection on theoretically convenient cases; secondary use means Douglas's reading of Nilotes or Hadza can diverge from the ethnographers' own frames; industrial "impersonal grid" is sketched rather than measured.

## The Site / Sample / Data

There is no single site. Principal **illustration clusters**:

1. **Western religious sociology:** "Bog Irish" London Irish Catholic fidelity to Friday abstinence vs elite reform anti-ritualism; Exclusive Brethren and other sect trajectories; student revolt and 1968 graffiti; Catholic Incarnation/heresy history as spirit–matter coding.
2. **Africanist secondary ethnography:** Nilotic continuum (Mandari > Nuer > Dinka on grid-group intensity in Douglas's reading); [[hadza]] (Woodburn); [[mbuti]]/pygmies (Turnbull); [[tallensi]]; Central African witch-cleansing; Lugbara cycles; Anuak contrast.
3. **Melanesian and other ego-focused systems:** Garia, 'Are'are, Siuai Big Men; cargo cults; success/luck cosmologies.
4. **Background:** Douglas's own Lele work and *Purity and Danger* body-boundary arguments supply continuity, not new Lele fieldwork for this book.

**Data quality:** secondary and selective by design. Douglas invited anthropologists to spoil her hypotheses with better material. Ethnographic claims on this page remain **Douglas's uses of others**, not wiki-voice findings about those peoples.

### Load-bearing passages (verbatim + analysis)

**On natural symbols (original Introduction):**

> Nature must be expressed in symbols; nature is known through symbols which are themselves a construction upon experience, a product of mind, an artifice or conventional product, therefore the reverse of natural. … even the human physiology which we all share in common does not afford symbols which we can all understand. … This book attempts to reinstate the intuition by following the line of argument of the French sociologists of *L'Année sociologique*. … The quest for natural symbols becomes by the force of this argument the quest for natural systems of symbolizing.

Analysis: Douglas stages a double move. First she grants the learned negative (no isolated natural symbol). Then she reopens the problem at the **system** level: if social relations prototype logical relations, recurring social patterns should yield recurring symbolic patterns. "Natural" is relocated from body-substance to **correlation**.

**On grid (Ch.4):**

> We can therefore take the scope and coherent articulation of a system of classification as one social dimension in which any individual must find himself. I shall call it grid.

Analysis: Grid is not "rules" in the everyday sense alone; it is how much of experience is ordered by a coherent classification available to the person — including competition between systems and narrowing when coherence fails.

**On group (Ch.4):**

> Towards the right he is increasingly under the bond of other people. … I call the tendency towards the maximum personal control the line of group.

Analysis: Group is experienced pressure and mutual claim, not mere demographic size. Zero is solitude/freedom from demands; the rightward pole is bond.

**On two bodies (Ch.5 program):**

> Natural symbols can express the relation of an individual to his society at that general systemic level. The two bodies are the self and society: sometimes they are so near as to be almost fused, sometimes they are almost completely separate.

Analysis: The famous correlation "bodily control = social control" is a research rule of thumb inside this program, not a universal law stated in wiki voice.

**On witchcraft cosmology (Ch.7):**

> There are thus four general characteristics of the witchcraft cosmology: the idea of the bad outside and the good inside, the inside under attack and in need of protection, human wickedness on a cosmic scale, and these ideas used in political manipulation.

Analysis: Local symbols (cats, broomsticks) are demoted to surface variation; social preconditions are elevated. This is classic Douglas: form of social unit before content of belief.

**On anti-ritualism's endgame (Ch.10):**

> Returning to our opening theme, we find that the apparent anti-ritualism of today is the adoption of one set of natural symbols in place of another. It is like a switch between restricted speech codes. Two morals can be drawn… first the duty of everyone to preserve their vision from the constraints of the natural symbols when judging any social situation; second the opportunity of religious bodies to set their message in the natural system of symbols.

> Ears must have walls. … Legitimacy must be clothed in magic, words must be made into things, blocks, hedges, compartments are the condition of knowledge. Thinkers must recognize the destructive lure of the natural system of symbols, equally when it devastates category boundaries as when it wrongfully closes them.

Analysis (continued): The 1968 graffiti reading ("Ears have walls" → "Ears must have walls") crystallizes Douglas's anti-anti-structure stance without simple conservatism: categories are conditions of knowledge, yet natural-symbol systems can also wrongfully close. The book refuses both naive ritualism and naive anti-ritualism. Elaborated-code judgment is required to evaluate social forms; churches are told they are losing the body-symbol game to secular culture while bathing in ethical sensitivity. (Source PDF text layer ends mid-sentence shortly after this synthesis — "While preaching" — so the very last lines of Ch.10 are incomplete in this edition's extract; the two morals and spirit/matter argument are fully present before that cut.)



### Comparative cases (Douglas's secondary uses)

**Bog Irish / Friday abstinence (Ch.3).** Douglas treated London Irish Catholic fidelity to Friday abstinence as a restricted-code allegiance symbol analogous to pork avoidance under Antiochus (1–2 Maccabees): co-ordinate defilement of temple, body, and law. She argued that removing a "meaningless" kitchen symbol does not automatically release charity; elite liturgical reformers may be "colour-blind signalmen" to non-verbal channels. This is the book's signature Western case for anti-ritualism's class location.

**Nilotic continuum (Ch.6).** Drawing on secondary Nilotic ethnography, Douglas ordered Mandari > Nuer > Dinka on grid–group intensity and correlated formality of wrong-doing, possession, and pollution concern. This is a **Douglas ordering for theory testing**, not a substitute for the ethnographers' own analyses ([[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]] on the Nuer; etc.).

**Hadza and Mbuti (Ch.6).** Douglas used Woodburn on the [[hadza]] and Turnbull on the [[mbuti]] as low-grid / low-group or sparse-control contrast cases (menstrual taboo contrast; diffuse symbols; less man-dominated cosmos). Again: attributed comparative use.

**Witchcraft and sects (Ch.7).** Preconditions: sharp external boundaries, confused internal relations, small scale. Four cosmological traits listed above. Exclusive Brethren and similar fellowship structures mapped as good-inside/bad-outside without instituted hierarchy — the social structure "specified for the witchcraft cosmology."

**Ego-focused grid / Big Men (Ch.8).** Garia, 'Are'are, Siuai and analogous success/luck systems: oppressive impersonal or ego-focused rules without strong group solidarity. Douglas hypothesized inherent moral weakness and recurrent millennial or cargo-type breakdowns among those not winning. Student revolt appears here as grid-without-group phenomenology in industrial settings.

**Control of symbols and alienation code (Ch.9–10).** Asceticism increases as grid diminishes; strong grid affirms pomp and organization. Where sub-systems alienate from the whole, a restricted code of alienation despises the body, honors consciousness separately, and intensifies spirit/matter dichotomy — politically a program to free the individual from unwelcome constraints. Orthodox Incarnation (full spirit–matter mediation) is read as the philosophical attitude of social integration; third/fourth-century heresies admitting a gulf are read as detachment/revolt codes (with a caution that official Arianism under Constantius shows codes can flip with institutional power).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Program sequel.** Frames itself as solving *Purity and Danger*'s unfinished problem (weak boundaries, anomaly tolerance).
2. **Public scandal → comparative theory.** Student revolt and Vatican II become cases, not moral panics.
3. **Bernstein as formal engine.** Speech codes and family control supply axes; ritual is declared a restricted code; anti-ritual maps to elaborated-code pressures.
4. **Diagram as argument.** Grid/group figures do persuasive work; Pelican scatter revision is an admission that early hard quadrants over-sold discreteness.
5. **Secondary ethnography as tribunal.** Africanist and sectarian cases "test" the map; readers are invited to spoil hypotheses.
6. **Theological application.** Incarnation vs heresies as spirit–matter coding of society vs alienation — high rhetorical voltage, high risk of specialist pushback.
7. **Double moral ending.** Analytic freedom from natural-symbol constraints **and** pastoral advice to churches — the book addresses anthropologists and religious institutions at once.



### Design critique: what the typology can and cannot do

Grid-group is powerful as a **hypothesis generator** and a **bias detector** against unlocated claims about "modern man," "primitive ritual," or "true religion." It is weaker as a **measurement system** in the 1970 text: operationalization is qualitative reading of ethnography, not scored instruments (later Gross & Rayner and cultural-theory batteries attempt formalization outside this source). Douglas's own move to scatter acknowledges that societies are not pure types.

The Bernstein analogy does double duty: it supplies dimensions **and** a normative sting. Restricted code unites speakers to kin and community; elaborated code enables self-inspection but carries "seeds of alienation." Douglas's long-run counsel — cherish positional forms of control where available; use elaborated code to judge social forms without being trapped by bodily natural symbols — is both analytic and ethical. Readers who want only a value-free typology will find the ending too pastoral; readers who want only pastoral advice will miss the comparative engine.

**Sensitive-content note:** The book discusses witchcraft, millennial violence contexts, and sexual imagery of mystics at ordinary scholarly density — not atrocity documentation. Race and class appear mainly via 1960s US segregation context and speech-code class locations; not race-science.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Immediate follow-up status (Douglas 1996): "mine of ideas" for nearly everything she wrote later; diagrams later simplified.
- Founded [[grid-group-analysis]] as a portable comparative tool beyond anthropology (political science, risk studies, organizational analysis — later literature).
- Paired with *Purity and Danger* as the classification wing of [[symbolic-anthropology]].
- Dialogic counterpart to Bernstein *Class, Codes and Control* (1971).
- Pathway into Douglas & Wildavsky risk work and Thompson/Ellis/Wildavsky cultural theory — **related lineage; do not collapse 1970 axes into later four biases without attribution.**



### Edition trail as reception evidence

The book is not a single frozen text. **1970** launches grid/group with denser diagrams; **1973 Pelican** is Douglas's own first major reanalysis (scatter vs quadrants; Bernstein *Class, Codes and Control* now public as "other side"); **1996** reframes for a generation living after the long aftermath of 1960s anti-structure, claims later diagrams simpler, and situates the book as the quarry for her subsequent oeuvre. Any citation of "Douglas 1970" should note which edition's apparatus is meant when diagram details matter.

Named constructive critics in the Pelican acknowledgements (Horton, Gulliver, Neubauer, Thompson) and Luckmann's encouragement to revise mark an early interdisciplinary reception spanning anthropology, sociology, and religious studies. The later cultural-theory industry (risk, organizational culture, political cultures) is reception by **expansion**, not replication of Ch.6 test cases.

## Current Standing

*Natural Symbols* remains the origin text for grid/group and a major statement on body symbolism and anti-ritualism. Best read as:
1. a **methodological** demand for social-environment variables in comparative religion;
2. a **middle-range** map of cosmology types, not a total theory of culture;
3. a **program bridge** from purity/pollution to later cultural theory — with edition history (1970 / 1973 / 1996) kept visible.

Absorbed vocabulary (grid, group, hierarchy, enclave, bodily control) outruns careful use of the full apparatus. Wiki practice: keep claims attributed; distinguish Douglas 1970 from later cultural-theory restatements; do not promote secondary ethnographic illustrations to wiki-voice findings about named peoples.
