# Pul Eliya — Studies Hub

> Summary page: [[leach-pul-eliya-study]]. Source: [[leach-pul-eliya-1961]]. Author: [[leach-edmund]] · Thinkers Hub: [[hubs/thinkers/leach-edmund]]. Society: [[pul-eliya]]. Concepts: [[pavula]] · [[variga]] · [[land-tenure]] · [[descent-theory]].

Graduate-level analysis of Leach’s 1961 Ceylon village monograph — the research act as conducted, findings with evidence, and afterlife in descent-theory critique.

---

## Question and Intellectual Context

British social anthropology’s most successful mid-century thesis — the theory of [[unilineal-descent|unilineal descent groups]] (Radcliffe-Brown; Evans-Pritchard *The Nuer*; Fortes 1953 summary) — “serves to generate its own antithesis.” *Pul Eliya* is that antithesis as **case history**.

Leach’s dual brief:

1. **Ceylon studies:** replace synthetic NCP village accounts (Ievers 1899; Codrington 1938; Pieris 1956; Ryan’s “Vanniaveva”) with singular particularity — “a case history; it does not refute what has been said before, but it exemplifies past statements in a way that for most readers must be unexpected.”
2. **Technical anthropology:** a small irrigated-rice community with fixed fields; kinship and marriage for land holding and use; “Unilineal descent is not a factor in the situation.”

The Fortes (1953) text he sets as investigation motto asks why descent rather than locality bases corporate groups. Leach’s answer: *in Pul Eliya, locality does* — even while parentage still “fixes one’s place.”

Same-year twin: *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]*. Career contrast (Gudeman & La Fontaine via bibliography apparatus): *Highland Burma* “idealist” vs *Pul Eliya* “materialist.” Continuity claim (Parry & Fuller 1989, not yet fully ingested): verbal categories bind the two monographs more tightly than the labels suggest.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample/site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do kinship/marriage relate to land rights and use? | Village social system | Pul Eliya (146 persons; ≈135 irrigated acres) | Observation + land records + genealogies | 1954 field; ≈1890–1954 records |
| What persists without unilineal corporations? | Property-locality structure | Old Field + gamgoda + freehold/lease expansion | Plot succession; compound maps | same |
| Case history vs ideal type | Particular instance | Not “typical” village by design | Anti-survey for jural facts | same |

**Two classes of social facts.** Statistical facts (birth-rates, production) require survey; jural facts require **particular application**. Thesis: “jural rules and statistical norms should be treated as separate frames of reference, but… the former should always be considered secondary to the latter.”

**Field conditions.** June–December 1954; August 1956 revisit. Leverhulme, Wenner-Gren; Ford for preparation. Yalman’s concurrent longer Dry Zone fieldwork invoked as defence against short-stay criticism. Name code encodes compound of residence/birth and diga/binna. Folded genealogy and field maps are load-bearing apparatus, not ornament.

**As conducted, not as advertised.** The book is relentlessly particular — named plots, named persons, mortgage cascades, faction histories. Leach admits he has not fully escaped the “Frazerian” case-method of preposterous hypothesis + illustration, but claims the volume of case material allows reader scepticism.

## The Site/Sample/Data

[[pul-eliya]]: Dry Zone tank village; main Crown tank + derelict temple tank; ancient hydraulic landscape without claimed social continuity from the Anuradhapura civilisation. Goyigama of one local [[variga]]; marriage field concentrated in neighbouring tanks (Wiralmurippu, Diwulwewa, Bellankadawala, etc.). Compound map (13 groups), Old Field baga layout, *sinakkara* and *badu* expansion fields.

Data classes: observation; Vel Vidane pangu list and paddy census; cadastral categories from ≈1900 survey; oral succession histories; harvest *kayiya* tables; Appendix demography (official census + 1954 composition).

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Locality not descent as corporate base | Absence of lineage corporations; membership = variga + Old Field land + compound; continuity = tank/gamgoda/Old Field | Core citation in descent-theory critique (Kuper et al.) |
| Kinship structure = talk about property | Plot succession; pavula elasticity tracks land/water; purana claims on purchased land | Teaching classic; still contested as over-reduction |
| Brothers rival; affines co-operate | Inheritance co-heirs; *kayiya* affinal recruitment; *massina* vs full-brother cases | Strong micro-finding; generalisation debated |
| Ideal pavula ≠ effective pavula | Charts (Menikrala); faction cases; three-pavula = baga projection | Key anti-lineage move |
| Variga = land-residence corporation under colonial indirect rule | Court history to 1938; endogamy as recognition rule; Arlis admission | Historicises “caste” endogamy |
| Traditional tenure imposes solidarity; freehold brings dissension | Ch. V–VI succession and channel factions | Policy-relevant Ceylon reading |
| Kinship salience varies with scarcity | Old Field vs fishing vs chena | Methodologically sharp; small n seasons |
| UDG structure may be “total fiction” | Speculative extension of method | Polemical; not a demonstrated finding about Africa |

### Load-bearing formulations (verbatim commitments)

On corporate base:

> “Pul Eliya is a society in which locality and not descent forms the basis of corporate grouping.”

On kinship and property:

> “kinship systems have no ‘reality’ at all except in relation to land and property. What the social anthropologist calls kinship structure is just a way of talking about property relations which can also be talked about in other ways.”

On solidarity:

> “‘kinship solidarity’ is here an image of the pattern of landholding and not vice versa.”

On method and descent theory:

> “It might even be the case that ‘the structure of unilineal descent groups’ is a total fiction; illuminating no doubt, like other theological ideas, but still a fiction.”

On structure as statistical:

> “The social structure which I talk about in this book is, in principle, a statistical notion; it is a social fact in the same sense as a suicide rate is a social fact… It is normal rather than normative.”

On economics prior to law:

> “the constraints of economics are prior to the constraints of morality and law.”

On marriage and property:

> “Marriage unifies; inheritance separates; property endures.”

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Antithesis generation.** Poses UDG theory as successful thesis that produces its opposite — frames the book as theoretically necessary, not merely ethnographic.
2. **Ideal type vs case history.** Dismantles Ievers/Codrington/Pieris/Ryan by genre, not only by fact — synthetic “Nuvarakalaviya village” vs singular system.
3. **Statistical vs jural.** Separates frames so equilibrium-legal “structure” cannot absorb economic discrepancy.
4. **Material environment as structured product.** Refuses social/material dichotomy (critiques EP, Fortes, Mitchell, R-B); territory is not introductory backcloth.
5. **Microscopic density as weapon.** Scepticism whether twenty years of elegant structural analyses would survive the same treatment — method as critique.
6. **Highland Burma continuity.** Ideal-schema approach still valued for disposing of equilibrium/consistency fictions; Pul Eliya shifts emphasis toward economic priority and statistical structure.
7. **Anti-typology.** Refuses to offer Pul Eliya as the bilateral counter-type; “sceptical of all typologies.”

---

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **[[descent-theory]] critique:** locality-not-descent and UDG-as-fiction lines enter standard historiography ([[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]]).
- **Property-centred kinship:** becomes a teaching exemplar for economic embedding of kinship.
- **Ceylon / Sri Lanka studies:** local land-tenure and variga analysis for historians of medieval and colonial Dry Zone.
- **Leach corpus:** completes the 1954–61 arc with *Rethinking*; later “materialist” self-label organises reception.

---

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Content |
|---|---|
| Short fieldwork | Six months + brief revisit; Leach anticipates and cites Yalman |
| Over-reduction of kinship to property | Kinship-as-only-property-talk can be read as eliminating meaning systems the book under-documents (religion is thin) |
| Continuity with *Highland Burma* | Idealism/materialism split vs Parry–Fuller continuity via categories |
| Typicality | Explicitly not average village — generalisation is theoretical method, not statistical |
| Later Sri Lankan ethnography | Regional variation and post-1954 change (not yet densely ingested here) |

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — not a failed lab replication but a classic whose theoretical extension remains disputed.

---

## Current Standing

Still a reference point for anti-descent and property-kinship analysis; still read with Fortes 1953 and *Rethinking Anthropology*. The microscopic land-succession chapters remain the evidentiary core; the conclusion’s theological analogy for structural analysis remains the rhetorical high-water mark of Leach’s polemic.
