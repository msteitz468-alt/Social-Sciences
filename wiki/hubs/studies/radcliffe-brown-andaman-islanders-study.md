# The Andaman Islanders — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]]. Reciprocal path link from summary required.

**Analysis standard:** 3,500–5,500+ words · design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine canonical body sections. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Primary base: Cambridge 1922 text (Internet Archive digitization, this ingest).

*The Andaman Islanders* is a book divided against itself, and the division is the point. Its first four chapters are a workmanlike, at times thin, ethnographic monograph on a small, colonially devastated island population — social organisation, life-cycle customs, belief, myth, material culture — written up largely from memory and notes several years after the fieldwork ended. Its final two chapters, "The Interpretation of Andamanese Customs and Beliefs" and its continuation, are something else: a sustained argument that the ethnographic material means something only when read as an integrated system of **sentiments** that ceremonial life expresses, maintains, and transmits — a Durkheimian sociology of ritual worked out, apparently independently of direct supervision, on a small forager population an ocean away from Paris. This page treats the book's two halves as different kinds of evidence for different kinds of claims: the descriptive chapters as documentation of Great Andaman social life at the moment of catastrophic contact, and the interpretive chapters as the founding theoretical statement of British functionalist anthropology.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Published the same year as [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (1922), *The Andaman Islanders* is the other half of British social anthropology's **fieldwork-revolution charter pair** — but where Malinowski's Introduction sells residence, statistical documentation, and the native's point of view, Radcliffe-Brown's Chs V–VI sell a **theory of interpretation**: customs have **meanings** as parts of a present **system**, not as residues of conjectural history.

The intellectual foils are explicit and named. English **intellectualist** anthropology — beliefs as primitive attempts to explain nature, the line from Tylor on the soul through Frazerian etiological myth — is identified as "the usual English way of dealing with them" and set aside as inadequate. Emotion/awe theories (Max Müller, Marett) are noted and likewise judged partial. Conjectural reconstruction of historical origins is dismissed as largely unverifiable and unfit to ground general laws — a judgment Radcliffe-Brown turns into a positive method rather than a mere refusal:

> "By the interpretation of a custom is meant the discovery, not of its origin, but of its meaning."

This single sentence is the book's charter for anti-historical functionalism. It reframes the ethnographer's task: not "how did this practice come to exist," a question Radcliffe-Brown holds is usually unanswerable for non-literate societies without written records, but "what does this practice do, here, now, as part of a working system." **[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]**'s *Formes élémentaires* is cited as an ally on the critique of animism and naturism as theories of religion, and — decisively, in the interpretive chapters — as the source of the thesis that ritual makes the **moral force of society** felt by the individual. **E. H. Man**, the great Victorian authority on the Andamans, functions as both indispensable predecessor (Man supplies most of the surviving detail on the South Andaman tribes, which Radcliffe-Brown himself could not adequately study) and foil (Man's claims about formal chieftainship, a supreme creator-god Puluga modelled on the Christian God, and continuous, canonical myth narratives are all directly disputed).

The empirical object is the [[andamanese|Great Andamanese]] under colonial crisis: a British penal settlement established at Port Blair from 1858, the so-called Andamanese "Homes" that mediated contact between the colonial administration and the indigenous population, and a catastrophic population collapse over the preceding half-century. Radcliffe-Brown states plainly that his interest is in reconstructing the pre-occupation organisation of a society his own fieldwork could only encounter in its terminal, disrupted form — a reconstructive project he undertakes with more confidence than the underlying data straightforwardly support.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The book advertises intensive residence fieldwork in the Malinowskian mould that it in fact only partially delivers. Radcliffe-Brown's own account, read closely, is candid about the gap between the ideal and the achieved.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is the structure of Great Andaman society, and what do its customs/beliefs mean as a system? | Local group; social system of customs and sentiments | Great Andaman 1906–08 residual "friendly" groups (North emphasis); E. H. Man's material for South; Little Andaman largely a failed extension | Residence; Hindustani → local dialects; Akar-Bale/English interpreter acquired late; post-field systemic interpretation composed c. 1910 | Field 1906–08; Chs V–VI written c. 1910; published 1922 |

**As conducted, not as advertised:**

1. **Sponsorship and timeline.** The Anthony Wilkin Studentship (Cambridge), the Royal Society, and the Government of India funded the fieldwork; the material was written up as a Trinity College fellowship dissertation, substantially rewritten by 1914, then delayed for years by the First World War before appearing in 1922 under the name **A. R. Brown** (he added "Radcliffe-" later). The preface is dated from Cape Town, January 1922 — the author already engaged in the South African and Australian work that would define his mature career, looking back at a much earlier project.
2. **Language triage and a change of plan.** Radcliffe-Brown initially preferred Little Andaman, the least-known and least-disrupted population, but after roughly three months concluded he would need two or three further years there simply to be able to ask systematic questions about custom and belief. He switched his main effort to Great Andaman, particularly the North, precisely because it was Man's evidentiary gap — while retaining a single Little Andaman boy in the hope of training future interpreters, a hope the book itself records as unrealized.
3. **Interpreter dependence.** Fieldwork on Great Andaman proceeded first through Hindustani, then through gradually acquired knowledge of North Andaman dialects; only late in the fieldwork did an English-speaking Akar-Bale interpreter become available, and Radcliffe-Brown states this interpreter's assistance produced fuller and more reliable material on the Akar-Bale and A-Pucikwar dialect groups than his own direct linguistic competence plus Hindustani could achieve alone — an admission that a meaningful share of the ethnographic record rests on third-hand mediated testimony rather than direct observation or fluent participant fieldwork.
4. **Programs left unfinished.** A planned physical-anthropology component (a Cambridge skeletal collection) went unpublished; a projected linguistic series in the journal *Anthropos* was interrupted by the war after a single instalment (1914).
5. **The theory was built after the field left.** Radcliffe-Brown states of the interpretive chapters that the system of interpretation "was not worked out until after I had left the islands" — meaning the very follow-up questions the interpretation would have required to test or extend it against native testimony could never be asked. He labels the whole effort, with a self-deprecation that is also a genuine methodological confession, "an apprentice work":

   > "…too well aware. It is indeed an apprentice work, for it was…"

   (The surrounding sentence, damaged in the digitized text, situates this admission in the preface's account of the book's long and interrupted gestation.) The confession matters for how the book should be read: the descriptive chapters are field notes matured by years of reflection but not returned to the field for correction, and the interpretive chapters are theory built on those matured notes without further empirical check.
6. **Reconstruction across severe demographic rupture.** By the time of fieldwork, Great Andaman society had already undergone extensive post-contact change — depopulation, the forced merging of formerly hostile local groups within the colonial "Homes" system — so that much of what the book presents as the "former" or pre-contact constitution of the society is itself inferred from the testimony of survivors describing a state of affairs many of them had not fully lived through as adults, filtered through informants whose own social world had already been substantially reorganised by colonial contact.

Judged by the standard Malinowski's own preface to *Argonauts* sets in the same publication year — extended residence, working fluency in the vernacular, statistical documentation of everyday behaviour, and above all a check on interpretation through continued presence in the field — Radcliffe-Brown's Andaman fieldwork is markedly weaker: shorter effective residence in any one dialect community, heavier interpreter mediation, and a theoretical apparatus assembled entirely after departure. The book's historical importance rests on the strength of that apparatus, not on the ethnographic method that produced its raw material.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Geography and peoples.** The Andaman chain lies in the Bay of Bengal and comprises Great Andaman (itself divided into North, Middle, and South, plus smaller islets) and the separate island of Little Andaman, with the British penal settlement centred at Port Blair. Radcliffe-Brown identifies ten Great Andaman linguistic tribes — Aka-Jeru, Aka-Cari, Aka-Kora, Aka-Bo, Aka-Kede, A-Pucikwar, Akar-Bale, Aka-Kol, Oko-Juwoi, and Aka-Bea — and treats the Jarawa as a division of Little Andaman by language and culture, distinguishing them from the Onge, also of Little Andaman.

**Demography as of documentation.** Radcliffe-Brown estimates the former total Great Andaman population, before the 1858 establishment of the penal settlement, at roughly 5,500. By the field period, the "friendly" Great Andaman groups he could work with directly numbered around 400 across the four North tribes and roughly 200 across the remaining six, including the by-then nearly extinct Aka-Bea. He cites Man's own figures for the Aka-Bea trajectory — approximately 1,000 in 1858, roughly 400 by 1882, and only 37 by 1901 — as an index of the speed of the collapse, driven by measles (1877), syphilis (introduced from around 1870), influenza, and a collapsed birth rate (he records fewer than twelve children under five among roughly five hundred people observed in 1907). Little Andaman, less exposed to the penal settlement, had at that point suffered comparatively less demographic damage, though contact and its consequences were beginning there too.

**Informants and access.** The great majority of Radcliffe-Brown's Great Andaman material came through the colonial "Homes" system: younger, Hindustani-speaking individuals, North-dialect speakers, and the single named Akar-Bale-English interpreter depicted in the book's plates. For the South Andaman groups nearly extinguished by the time of the fieldwork, Radcliffe-Brown depends almost entirely on Man's earlier published material rather than on his own observation. The Jarawa, hostile to outside contact throughout the period, receive minimal direct coverage.

**Data classes.** The book combines direct observation of camps and such ceremonies as could be witnessed, extensive elicited description of custom and myth (necessarily retrospective and interpreter-mediated where dialect competence was thin), systematic comparison against Man, Portman, and Temple's earlier published accounts, and an appendix on material culture. Radcliffe-Brown is explicit that some described rites were never observed directly: the scarification ceremony, for instance, rests entirely on native verbal testimony rather than witnessed performance — a limitation the text states but does not dwell on, and one worth weighing against the confidence of the later interpretive chapters that treat such ceremonies as settled data for theoretical synthesis.

### Social organisation (compressed)

Pre-occupation Great Andaman society, as reconstructed, consisted of small, effectively independent **local groups** holding recognised land and hunting rights, organised around camps, meeting occasionally for feasts and dances, and periodically in a state of feud with neighbouring groups; **tribes** functioned as weak linguistic aggregates rather than political units, each occupying on average a territory of roughly 165 square miles; the **family** — spouses plus unmarried children, whether born or adopted — was the only clearly formalised kin unit, with adoption extensively practised; marriage was marked by public embrace and gift exchange; present-exchange more generally was a strong institution; warfare took the form of surprise raiding, resolved through peace-making ceremonies; and the ordinary radius of social communication and contact was often on the order of twenty miles.

### Ceremonial and belief (compressed)

Documented ceremonial life included the *Aka-op* initiation sequence with its ordered series of food taboos, scarification (practised in the North, not directly observed by Radcliffe-Brown), turtle- and pig-eating ceremonies marking status transitions, dances, and an elaborate complex of death and mourning practice — including clay body-covering, the retention of bones as relics, avoidance of the names of the dead, and ceremonial weeping at multiple points in the life cycle: on reunion after absence, at peace-making, at marriage, at initiation, and at death. Belief encompassed a range of spirit categories, the weather/season figures Biliku (or Puluga in the South) and Tarai, magical practice, the *oko-jumu* or shaman-like specialist, and the attributed protective power of fire, plant fibres, bones, and weapons. The mythic corpus, richly variant across dialect groups, concerned ancestral figures, the origin of fire, a flood narrative, the origin of night, and a series of animal-transformation stories.

### Interpretative findings (program)

**Working hypothesis for ceremonial (attributed to Radcliffe-Brown).** The interpretive chapters state their governing hypothesis in a numbered form Radcliffe-Brown treats as close to axiomatic for the analysis that follows:

> "...sentiments^ by which the conduct of the individual is regulated..." "...(2)... an object of this system of sentiments. (3) In human society the sentiments in question are not innate but are developed in..."

Reconstructed from the surrounding argument, the hypothesis runs: (1) an orderly society depends on a system of **sentiments** — organised affective dispositions — that regulate individual conduct; (2) socially significant features of the environment and social life become, and must become, objects of this system of sentiments; (3) in human society these sentiments are not innate but are built up through social experience; (4) ceremonial gives them a collective, public expression; (5) that collective expression is precisely what maintains the sentiments at the necessary intensity across generations and transmits them to new members of the society. **Social function**, on this reading, is defined as an institution's effect on **solidarity or social cohesion** — a term Radcliffe-Brown borrows and reworks from Durkheim rather than from biology, though he also reaches for the organism analogy to state the underlying logic:

> "…the study of the meaning of savage customs is a sort of social physiology, and is to be distinguished from the study of origins, or changes of custom in just the same way that animal physiology is distinguished from the biology that deals with the origin of species…"

The organism analogy licenses two moves at once: it treats a society's customs as a functionally interdependent whole ("social physiology") and it licenses reading a custom's contribution to that whole in the present, without appeal to how the custom arose historically.

**Dance (load-bearing application).** Radcliffe-Brown's most vivid application of the sentiments theory is the communal dance, which he treats as producing a condition of maximal collective feeling:

the dance's primary social function, on his reading, is to make the unity, harmony, and concord of the community "intensely felt," and thereby to maintain that unity — the dancer's own sense of heightened personal force is, in his account, simultaneously an experience of social solidarity, so that the individual's pleasure in dancing is inseparable from the mechanism by which the group reproduces its own cohesion.

**Myth.** Myths are read not as failed proto-science (the intellectualist reading Radcliffe-Brown explicitly rejects) but as a parallel expressive form to ceremony: legends give concrete narrative shape to feelings and ideas about how the natural and social environment bears on moral and social life, and their function, like that of ceremony, is to express the system of **social values** distinctive to the society's organisation. Myth and ceremony are thus treated as functionally parallel mechanisms — both work by expression, both maintain collective sentiment, and Radcliffe-Brown holds that any adequate account of a society's culture must show how it provides for subsistence, for the continuity of children into full membership, and for social cohesion, with moral custom explained throughout by its social function rather than by any story about how it began.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The interpretive chapters persuade less by new data than by a small set of forcefully stated methodological moves, each quotable and each doing real argumentative work beyond its immediate application.

### 1. Meaning, not origin

> "By the interpretation of a custom is meant the discovery, not of its origin, but of its meaning."

This is a **method claim**, not a finding about Andaman history, and its rhetorical function is to close off an entire register of question — the conjectural-historical — that had dominated Victorian anthropology, while opening a new one that Radcliffe-Brown's own data, thin as it is on precontact chronology, is actually equipped to answer.

### 2. Social physiology

> "…the study of the meaning of savage customs is a sort of social physiology, and is to be distinguished from the study of origins, or changes of custom in just the same way that animal physiology is distinguished from the biology that deals with the origin of species…"

The biological analogy does double duty: it dignifies synchronic, systemic analysis with the prestige of physiology as a mature science, and it pre-empts the objection that ignoring origins is unscientific, by pointing to an accepted science (physiology) that does exactly that with respect to a parallel question (phylogeny). Later critics — and Merton's mid-century codification of functionalism — would reject the cruder **indispensability** and **functional-unity** postulates this kind of organism talk invites; Radcliffe-Brown's Andaman text sits closer to unguarded organic holism than to the more cautious, net-balance functionalism Merton later proposed as a corrective.

### 3. Systems comparison

> "The true comparative method consists of the comparison, not of one isolated custom of one society with a similar custom of another, but of the whole system of institutions, customs and beliefs of one society with that of another. In a word, what we need to compare is not institutions but social systems or types."

This retools the Victorian [[comparative-method]] against the trait-listing, evolutionary-stage comparison practiced by Tylor and Frazer — a British structural-functionalist methodological signature that outlives the sentiments theory itself and remains cited as a standing professional norm even by anthropologists with no other sympathy for Radcliffe-Brown's program.

### 4. The weeping rite and the sentiments formula

Radcliffe-Brown's treatment of ceremonial weeping is the book's most sustained single application of close reading to ritual, and its conclusion states the sentiments thesis at full strength:

> "The proffered explanation of the rite of weeping should now be plain. I regard it as being the affirmation of a bond of social solidarity between those taking part in it, and as producing in them a realisation of that bond by arousing the sentiment of attachment."

He is careful to insist the rite is not spontaneous emotional overflow but an obligatory, socially regulated performance:

> "…the ceremonies are not spontaneous expressions of feeling — they are all customary actions to which the sentiment of obligation attaches, which it is the duty of persons to perform on certain definite occasions."

And he draws the general conclusion — the passage that most directly anticipates his later, fully generalized statement of structural-functionalism — that:

> "…each of the ceremonies serves to renew or to modify in the minds of those taking part in it some one or more of the social sentiments. The peace-making ceremony is a method by which feelings of enmity are exchanged for feelings of friendship. The marriage rite serves to arouse in the minds of the marrying pair a sense of their obligations as married folk… The weeping and exchange of presents when friends come together is a means of renewing their feelings of attachment to one another."

Radcliffe-Brown's psychological premise — that weeping functions physiologically as relief for emotional tension and that repressed aggressive feeling, once given free expression, "dies out instead of smouldering on" — is stated as a settled psychological generalisation rather than argued or sourced; the ethnographic reading of ceremonial weeping as solidarity-affirmation depends on accepting this unexamined premise, a dependency the text itself does not flag as a weakness.

### 5. Durkheimian moral force

The book's culminating theoretical move, late in the interpretive chapters, is an explicit alignment with Durkheim on the nature of ritual power. Radcliffe-Brown states, in a passage he himself footnotes as derivative of Durkheim, Hubert, and Mauss:

> "It is, in a few words, the moral power of the society acting upon the individual directly or indirectly and felt by him in innumerable ways throughout the whole course of his life."

He locates the individual's experience of moral obligation as simultaneously an external constraint (social opinion, tradition, custom) and an internal one (the individual's own moral sense), a doubling he presents as the mechanism by which the "clash" between duty and selfish inclination is actually experienced. Collective ceremony intensifies the same force by a different route:

> "The moral force of the society is also felt, in a quite different way, in all states of intense collective emotion, of which the dance affords a good example… All ceremonies in which the whole community takes part give the individual the experience of the moral force of the society acting upon him."

The footnote crediting the thesis is unambiguous about its lineage:

> "The exposition of this important thesis can only be given here in the most abbreviated form. The thesis itself, as applied to primitive ritual in general, owes its origin to Professor Emile Durkheim, and has been expounded by him (more particularly in his work *Les Formes élémentaires de la Vie religieuse*) and by Messieurs H. Hubert and M. Mauss."

Radcliffe-Brown then extends the comparative reach of the thesis by identifying the Andamanese sense of a diffuse moral-social force, expressed through symbols of heat and fire rather than through a single abstract name, with the cross-culturally documented category of impersonal ritual power — Melanesian *mana* and the North American *orenda*, *wakan*, and related terms — treating all as regional names for the same underlying experience of society's moral force acting on the individual. This is the clearest single passage in the book demonstrating that its theoretical apparatus is not an independent invention but a direct, acknowledged application of Durkheimian sociology of religion to a new ethnographic case — arguably the earliest such application within British social anthropology proper.

### 6. Anti-Man Puluga

Running throughout the belief chapters is a sustained critique of Man's earlier account of Puluga as a supreme, monotheistic creator-god closely modelled on the Christian deity. Radcliffe-Brown argues instead for a weather- and season-personification reading, tied to the seasonal timing of certain socially proscribed "offences," and notes — with evident interest in what it reveals about Man's own informant relations rather than about Andamanese belief as such — that his own Akar-Bale, professedly Christian, informant preferred the name Tomo over Puluga as the closer local equivalent of "God." This is at once **source criticism** (a judgment about the reliability and possible missionary contamination of Man's earlier informants) and **substantive theory** (Radcliffe-Brown's own reading of Biliku/Puluga as bound up with the seasonal-sentiments system elaborated in the interpretive chapters).

### 7. Rhetoric of apprenticeship and science

Throughout, self-deprecating disclaimers about the work's limitations ("an apprentice work") sit alongside maximal claims for social anthropology as an emerging science capable of general laws about the function of custom. The tension is productive rather than merely inconsistent: it lets Radcliffe-Brown simultaneously excuse the book's descriptive thinness and claim theoretical authority for its interpretive apparatus, a rhetorical structure later readers have used either charitably (as honest scholarly humility) or critically (as a way of insulating an ambitious theoretical program from scrutiny of its weak empirical base).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **British social anthropology's charter pair.** Alongside Malinowski's *Argonauts*, published the same year, *The Andaman Islanders* is conventionally read as one half of the discipline's fieldwork-revolution founding statement — Radcliffe-Brown supplying the structural, sentiments-and-system pole, Malinowski the biopsychological-needs pole, a contrast textbooks have refined ever since but that both men's later careers complicate (Radcliffe-Brown's own later work moves further from psychology and toward formal structural analysis than the Andaman book's Durkheimian sentiment-talk might suggest).
- **[[social-structure]].** The Andaman material becomes an early program object for the concept — the "extremely simple" structure of local group, tribe, and family is repeatedly used in subsequent teaching as a minimal-case illustration of what "social structure" analytically picks out.
- **[[structural-functionalism]].** The anthropological lineage of structural-functionalism — this book together with Radcliffe-Brown's later synthesis in *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function in Primitive Society]]* (1952) — long predates and runs largely independently of the Parsonsian tradition in American sociology; the Andaman book supplies the British discipline's charter statement of the functional method applied to ritual.
- **Ritual theory more broadly.** Ceremonial as sentiment-maintenance, dance as a mechanism of maximal-solidarity feeling, and myth as an expression of social value became durable feedstock for later symbolic and structural analyses of ritual, frequently detached from Radcliffe-Brown's explicit organism language even where the underlying functional logic persists.
- **Comparative method reform.** The systems-not-traits reformulation of comparison remains a standing professional norm in social and cultural anthropology, cited approvingly even by scholars who reject functionalism's substantive claims about solidarity and sentiment.

## Current Standing

*The Andaman Islanders* remains required reading for the history of anthropological theory and stands as a primary documentary source for Great Andaman social life at the moment of early colonial crisis — but it is not, and its own author does not present it as, a reliable guide to precontact Andamanese society considered independently of the theoretical lens through which that society is reconstructed. Its descriptive chapters should be read with the demographic collapse and interpreter-mediated data collection constantly in view; its interpretive chapters should be read as the founding statement of a research program — Durkheimian ritual functionalism transplanted into British social anthropology — whose specific claims about the function of ceremony have fared unevenly, but whose reformulation of the comparative method and whose basic move (interpret meaning as present function within a social system, not as historical origin) shaped the discipline for decades. It pairs naturally with *Argonauts* as the 1922 dual charter of the fieldwork revolution, and should be read alongside Radcliffe-Brown's own later, more fully generalized statement of the position in *Structure and Function in Primitive Society* (1952) and, when ingested, his African-systems collaborations.
