# The Art of Not Being Governed — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub. Summary: [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study]]. Source: [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009]]. Thinker: [[scott-james-c]].

All substantive findings below are **attributed to Scott (2009)** unless nested as Scott citing a named source. This page deepens design, evidence, rhetoric, and afterlife; the summary page holds compact findings and standing.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Scott asks how to understand highland social organization, subsistence, ethnicity, and “civilization” status around the padi states of mainland Southeast Asia. The answer inverts the civilizational narrative: hills are not living museums of the pre-state past but **reactive, purposeful statelessness** — “those who got away.”

Disciplinary moment: late-2000s political anthropology / agrarian studies, after *Seeing Like a State* (1998) made [[legibility]] and [[high-modernism]] household terms, and after decades of highland ethnography (Leach, Lehman, Keyes, Michaud) that Scott re-reads as political ecology. Epigraph from Pierre Clastres (*Society Against the State*): peoples “without history” as history of struggle *against* the state. Scott enrolls as a “foot soldier” under Willem van Schendel’s banner of **Zomia studies** and under Jean Michaud’s Southeast Asian massif program.

The book is also a reply to progressive sequences (band → tribe → chiefdom → state; foraging → swidden → wet rice) and to area-studies maps that slice the massif among nation-states and civilizational cores. Global triangulation is explicit: maroons, Cossacks, Berbers, Appalachia, Roman/Han/Ottoman frontiers — always returning to the mainland massif as the largest remaining extrastate region.

**Why the question matters for the disciplines.** State-formation literatures long treated nonstate peoples as residual raw material awaiting incorporation. Peasant-resistance literatures (including Scott’s own earlier work) treated subjects *inside* states. *Art* joins the two by writing a history of the **outside** as a political ecology of distance — continuous with *Seeing Like a State*’s legibility program but inverted: where the modern state simplifies for control, highlanders diversify and disperse for autonomy. The wager is that once the outside is described this way, valley history itself changes: dynastic “golden ages” look like temporary clumps of manpower; collapse and flight become central, not embarrassing footnotes.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why do highland forms look “stateless”? | Hill–valley political ecology of mainland SE Asia | Zomia massif (~2.5M km²; multi-ethnic assemblage) | Comparative synthesis of ethnography + history | ~2 millennia, self-limited to pre-~1945 |
| What is state space? | Appropriation zone / padi core | Alluvial plains (Irrawaddy, Chao Phraya, Mekong, Red River, etc.) | Political ecology; logistics of grain and manpower | Classical through colonial |
| How is ethnicity made? | Named “tribes” and identities | Census categories, frontier polities, oral charters | Radical constructionist reading | Ongoing into nation-state enclosure |

**As conducted, not as advertised:** this is not multi-sited new ethnography. Scott assembles secondary literature and his long SE Asia research program into a single political-ecology thesis. Strength: scale and re-description. Weakness (he and critics own): inherits colonial and classic-ethnographic biases; chronologies and coercion balances are revisable by specialists; functionalism risk when every hill trait is read as escape design.

**Method commitments:** (1) map political space by **friction of terrain** (travel-time), not flat km maps; (2) treat cultivation, settlement, kinship, orality, and ethnicity as having **escape value**; (3) decode civilized/barbarian as **state-subject coding**; (4) insist on **hill–valley coevolution** — neither intelligible alone.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Zomia** (van Schendel’s term; Zo “remote/hills” + Mi “people”): lands above roughly **300 m** from Vietnam’s Central Highlands to NE India; five SE Asian countries + Yunnan, Guizhou, Guangxi, parts of Sichuan; ~**80–100 million** minority peoples; altitudes to >4000 m. Scott confines the west at Naga/Mizo hills and Chittagong Hill Tracts; van Schendel would go farther. Michaud’s massif mapping (via Scott) includes southern/western Sichuan, Guizhou, Yunnan, western/northern Guangxi, western Guangdong fringes, most of northern Burma and adjacent NE India, north/west Thailand, nearly all Laos above the Mekong valley, northern/central Vietnam along the Annam Cordillera, and NE Cambodian fringes — a “pervasive Switzerland without cuckoo clocks” (Gellner phrase on Berbers, applied).

**Altitude as order.** Viewed “horizontally” in Leach’s sense, niches stack: e.g. Hmong often 1000–1800 m with maize, opium, millet. Cultural continuity along altitude bands may exceed proximity to valley peoples thirty miles away. Region knitted not by political unity (absent) but by comparable patterns of diverse hill agriculture, dispersal/mobility, and rough egalitarianism.

**Data provenance:** highland ethnography (Leach on Kachin gumlao/gumsa; Lahu, Karen, Hmong/Miao, Akha, Wa, Yao/Mien, Chin, Palaung, etc. as *cited assemblages*, not identity equations); Chinese frontier historiography (Han expansion, cooked/raw, enter-the-map); SEA comparative state history (Lieberman, Reid, Tambiah, Winichakul); colonial gazetteers (including J.G. Scott / Shwe Yoe, no relation) and missionary archives; logistics estimates (oxcart ~250 km grain self-consumption; ~24 km/day foot travel; effective control radii often cited ~160–300 km depending on source).

**What is missing as primary data:** systematic new surveys; archaeological sequences for highland occupation; genetic population history; quantitative demography of flight. Scott’s evidence class is **textual-comparative and ethnographic-secondary**. That is a feature of the genre (anarchist *history* as re-description) and a standing limit.

**Non-identity hygiene:** Zomia is not a people, language family, or culture. Ethnonyms are political labels under analysis. Scott’s radical constructionism is itself a program under contest. When the book names Hmong, Karen, or Wa, the wiki must keep material culture, language, genes, and self-identification distinct — Scott helps by treating ethnicity as strategy, but readers can still reify his examples.

### Manpower before land (Ch. 3 densified)

Scott’s “first principle” of premodern SE Asian statecraft is **concentration of manpower**, not territory for its own sake. Formula (attributed): political/military supremacy requires superior access to concentrated sedentary populations; before the twentieth century such concentrations typically require irrigated rice. The link is **elective affinity**, not determinism — Hani, Ifugao, and Balinese mountain padi and occasional states without wet-rice cores are noted exceptions. What wet rice uniquely does is make dense, grain-supported villagers easier to mobilize (O’Connor via Scott). Court narratives of luminous attraction of free hillfolk are, Scott holds, “wildly distorted”: war, **slave-raiding**, and forced resettlement of captives by the tens of thousands created “thickly-settled clumps.” Geertz’s Bali dictum — struggle more for men than land — applies to the mainland. Thai titles (Kun Pan, “Lord of a Thousand Men”) and provincial ranks calibrated by musterable heads, not European-style territorial dukedoms; Thongchai’s Siamese attention to summonable manpower over empty sovereignty over land is mobilized as evidence. Power, before modern war technology, “boiled down to manpower.”

### Civilization as subject coding (Ch. 4 densified)

Civilizational discourses (Chinese cooked/raw, valley Buddhist “civilized,” colonial wild/tame) practically mean **governed vs ungoverned**. Ethnicity and “tribe,” Scott argues following Gellner and Lattimore, begin where taxes and sovereignty end. Official narratives rarely entertain voluntary “going over to the barbarians,” yet backsliding and flight are historical commonplaces; Rowe’s claim that more Chinese acculturated to aboriginal life than the reverse is cited for dramatic effect. Catalogue of “primitiveness” = traits that raise friction of appropriation: inaccessible settlement, foraging/swidden, mobility, transient identities, non-subject religious status. The code is agro-ecological: appropriability, not culture essence.

### Peopling, escape agriculture, jellyfish (Ch. 5–6 densified)

Hills are peopled by never-subjects **and** by waves of flight (Han expansion as major long-run driver; Burmese/Thai slave-raiding states; epidemics of crowding; dynastic collapse). **Secondary primitivism** — deliberate return to less state-accessible livelihoods — is rational adaptation, not failure. New World parallels (Clastres, maroons, Siriono) are triangulated: root crops leave-in-ground; defensible out-of-the-way sites; mixed clandestine trade.

**Escape agriculture:** [[swidden]] as friction of appropriation; maize, cassava, yams, sweet potatoes as portfolio options; open commons and staggered fields that defeat cadastre and corvée calendars. Jonsson gradient (via Scott): foraging most appropriation-proof → swidden resistant → fixed wet-rice appropriable and hierarchical.

**Escape social structure:** disaggregation into households; temporary villages; “take me to your leader” with no stable answer. Gellner’s Berber slogan **“Divide that ye be not ruled”**; Yapp’s **“jellyfish tribes”** — amorphous populations without leverage points. States invent chiefs for indirect rule; subjects ignore them (British Chin chief creation; Pau Chin Hau cult repudiating chiefly feasts). Leach’s gumlao/gumsa oscillation is read as a **repertoire** of egalitarian and hierarchical modes invocable by position toward Shan/Burmese state space. Closing image: when threatened, hill societies “retreat, disperse, disaggregate like quicksilver.”

### Orality and ethnogenesis (Ch. 6½–7 densified)

Writing is state memory — lists, charters, fixed claims. Orality travels light, revises genealogy, resists charter capture. “Peoples without history” choose how much history to carry (Lisu/Karen light vs Akha/Kachin deployable depth). Lost-script legends may encode postliteracy as adaptation. **States make tribes**: bounded genealogical total units virtually never exist; “tribe” is a module of rule. Named identities as political projects (Cossack model; Karenni). Porous boundaries, absorption of strangers, dual ritual cycles — identity as **bandwidth of positionality**. Nation-state sovereignty + freehold tenure + valley colonization of hills = the **last great enclosure**, closing the open shatter zone.

### Prophets (Ch. 8 densified)

Archives over-represent rebellion. Some massif peoples (Hmong/Miao, Karen, Lahu) show a vocation for prophets: multiethnic appeal, bridge-burning reorganization, founding of new settlements/identities/petty states. Leaders as local cosmopolitans (multilingual, ex-monks/catechists). Christianity as oppositional modernity (millenarian + not the lowland state religion). Against Hobsbawm-style realist dismissal: failed millennia still reshape groups and hope reservoirs. Prophecy : sangha :: swidden : padi — illegibility analogy.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**Load-bearing passage — maroon thesis (Preface):**

> “I argue that hill peoples are best understood as runaway, fugitive, maroon communities who have, over the course of two millennia, been fleeing the oppressions of state-making projects in the valleys—slavery, conscription, taxes, corvée labor, epidemics, and warfare.”

Rhetoric: reclassifies the entire ethnographic object. Not survival of the archaic but **political flight**. The list of oppressions (slavery, tax, corvée, disease, war) is the negative definition of valley statecraft. “Maroon” imports New World fugitive-slave resonance into SE Asian highland studies — a deliberate comparative provocation.

**Load-bearing passage — conclusion post- thesis:**

> “Hill peoples are not pre- anything. In fact, they are better understood as post–irrigated rice, postsedentary, postsubject, and perhaps even postliterate. They represent, in the longue durée, a reactive and purposeful statelessness of peoples who have adapted to a world of states while remaining outside their firm grasp.”

Rhetoric: serial “post-” negations invert developmental time. “Purposeful” carries the anarchist ethical load; “reactive” keeps coevolution with states. Final label swap — *state-subject* / *not-a-state-subject* for civilized/uncivilized — is the book’s terminological payoff:

> “If they merely substituted ‘state-subject’ for ‘civilized’ and ‘not-a-state-subject’ for ‘uncivilized,’ they’d have it just about right.”

**Friction of terrain as master metaphor.** Scott develops friction of terrain as a “new way of understanding political space”: mountains, swamps, forests, and broken topography multiply travel time and supply cost, placing hard limits on premodern garrisons (seasonal offensives; wet-season breakdown). Standard kilometer maps lie; social maps should enlarge high-friction zones. Distance-demolishing technologies after ~1945 (rail, all-weather roads, telephone/telegraph, airpower, helicopters, IT) so change the strategic balance that Scott warns the book “makes little sense” for late-twentieth-century SE Asia. The warning is both epistemic humility and a rhetorical fence.

**State space as appropriation zone.** Ideal state space is not “civilization” but a zone where subjects and permanent fields are concentrated within cheap extractive reach. Wet rice is the “ultimate state-space crop” because grain stands above ground, ripens roughly together, and supports dense, mobilizable populations. Water transport is the exception that nullifies friction — many large precolonial polities cling to rivers and coasts. Hills are not empty of politics; they are **zones of refuge** where long-term occupation exceeds state capacity.

**Structural rhetoric of the book.** Chapter pairings build a single apparatus of **evasion + prevention**:

| Pair | Evasion (out of state) | Prevention (no state among us) |
|---|---|---|
| Ch.2–3 / Ch.5–6 | Flight from appropriation | Escape agriculture & social structure |
| Ch.4 / Ch.7 | Reject civilizational subject coding | Constructionist ethnogenesis / identity repertoire |
| Ch.6½ | Travel light historically | Oral plasticity vs written charter capture |
| Ch.8 | Millenarian exit | Temporary reaggregation that can become petty state |

Ch. 6½ is numbered as a *half*-chapter to mark orality as hinge between agro-ecology and identity — a formal joke that signals the book’s playfulness with genre while making a serious claim about writing as statecraft software (continuity with *Seeing Like a State* and later *Against the Grain* on lists and ration bowls).

**Anarchist historiography.** The subtitle *An Anarchist History* is not a call for contemporary anarchy but a historiographic stance: write the history of those who *got away*, not only of courts and tax rolls. Clastres’s epigraph equates peoples-without-history’s struggle against the state with Marxian class struggle for peoples-with-history — elevating the highland object to the same moral seriousness as the proletariat of classical narrative. Critics hear romance; Scott’s own pages on highland hierarchy, slave trade, and failed millennia supply the counterweights, unevenly.

**Self-limiting move.** The loud “don’t apply after 1945” is intellectual honesty, a shield against contemporary counterexamples of highland incorporation, and a way to keep the book’s villain (the classical and colonial padi state) historically specific while still speaking to modern enclosure (“last great enclosure”) as the terminus of the story.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Popularized **[[zomia]]** beyond van Schendel’s area-studies proposal; “Zomia studies” became a recognizable conversation.
- Densified [[nonstate-spaces]], [[state-evasion]], [[escape-agriculture]], [[shatter-zone]] as portable concepts.
- Linked modern [[legibility]] program (*Seeing Like a State*) to premodern padi logistics; paved the way for *Against the Grain* (2017) deep-time grain/manpower thesis.
- Forced [[state-formation]] and [[southeast-asian-state-formation]] debates to treat flight, slavery, and shatter zones as constitutive.
- Influenced environmental history, anarchist political theory, and comparative “regions of refuge” work (maroons, borderlands).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

Recorded as **positions**, not wiki adjudication:

1. **Zomia too big / too heterogeneous** — one political ecology cannot unify the massif; boundaries differ (van Schendel vs Scott). Area specialists argue that Hmong highland opium economies, Kachin political oscillations, Karen lowland–highland gradients, and Wa militarized autonomy are not instances of one “escape” type. The strength of the term for teaching and cross-regional talk is also the weakness for local historiography.

2. **Functionalist over-reading** — not every swidden field or oral culture is designed against the state; ecology, markets, and culture have independent weight. If escape value is the only analytic lens, positive preferences (taste, ritual, soil knowledge) disappear. Scott’s elective-affinity language is the softer version; critics target the harder “designed to thwart” phrasing.

3. **Romanticization / anarchist framing** — highland hierarchy, slavery, and raiding under-weighted relative to valley coercion. The book’s ethical energy runs toward the fugitive; predators among hill peoples (slave traders, headmen, warlords) appear but do not organize chapters the way valley slavery does. Comparative “regions of refuge” work since 2009 often rebalances predation.

4. **Constructionist ethnogenesis** — underplays language depth, durable tradition, emic continuity; “states make tribes” as slogan risks cynicism about ethnonational claims (Scott denies denigration and notes institutionalized categories become passionately lived). Linguistic and genetic research — different evidence classes — may show deeper time-depth for some populations than political ethnogenesis alone suggests; the wiki’s four-way non-identity rule applies: do not collapse Scott’s political labels into genes or languages.

5. **Empirical exceptions** — highland states (Nan Chao, Kengtung, Lan-na, Shan sawbwa polities); durable wet-rice in hills (Hani, Ifugao, Bali as Scott’s own exceptions); willing incorporation and cultural attraction that is not pure force; earlier state reaches along trade routes and plateaus than pure “friction” implies. Elective affinity is not universal law — Scott says so; critics say he then writes as if it were.

6. **Secondary-synthesis limits** — not new multi-sited fieldwork; colonial archive bias; missionary and gazetteer sources over-read rebellion and under-read quiet flight. SEA historians (Lieberman tradition and others) may revise chronologies, demographic magnitudes, and the coercion/attraction balance. The book is a re-description engine, not a new primary corpus.

7. **Gender and internal differentiation** — relatively higher status of women is noted as a massif pattern, but gender is not a structural chapter axis; class and rank inside hill societies get less systematic treatment than hill–valley contrast.

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — continuous specialist rework, not lab replication. There is no single “restudy of Zomia” that can confirm or falsify the thesis; instead a literature of partial tests, regional monographs, and theoretical sequels (including Scott’s own *Against the Grain*).

## Current Standing

Still a **major reference point** and teaching text in political anthropology and agrarian studies — the book most students meet when they meet “Zomia.” Best read as a **strong program**: maroon thesis + post- thesis + ethnogenesis constructionism as heuristics that reorganize questions, not as closed total history of the massif. Pair with: Lieberman and SEA historiography; Leach and later highland ethnography; *Seeing Like a State* (1998) for modern legibility; *Against the Grain* (2017) for deep-time grain/manpower extension; van Schendel and Michaud on the massif as research object; regional monographs that test pieces of the thesis without buying the whole package.

What survives most securely even under critique: (1) hill–valley **coevolution** as analytic default — valley states and highland peripheries make each other; (2) **friction of terrain** as political variable — travel-time maps beat blank political maps; (3) civilizational labels as **subject-status codes** — cooked/raw, civilized/barbarian as fiscal-political registration; (4) modern enclosure of the highlands as historically late and technologically enabled — the “last great enclosure” as a name for post-1945 mop-up; (5) the injunction to take **flight and secondary primitivism** seriously as political history, not only as cultural survival.

What remains live and contested: the unity of Zomia; the degree of functional design in escape agriculture and orality; the balance of coercion vs attraction in state peopling; the ethical romance of anarchist historiography. The wiki records these as positions.
