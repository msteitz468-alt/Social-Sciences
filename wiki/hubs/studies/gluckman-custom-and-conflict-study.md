# Custom and Conflict in Africa — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub. Summary: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study]]. Source: [[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-1955]]. Author: [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]].

**Analysis standard:** 3,500–5,500+ words · design-summary table · findings table (finding | evidence base | later status) · verbatim load-bearing passages analysed · the afterlife in full · nine canonical body sections. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Custom and Conflict in Africa* began as sound, not text. In spring 1955 Max Gluckman delivered six lectures on the BBC Third Programme, and when he came to publish them the following year he chose, after "some deliberation," to print them "exactly as I delivered them," refusing even to write a proper introduction or conclusion because doing so would have meant "the writing of a different kind of book." That editorial decision is worth dwelling on, because it shapes everything that follows: the book is deliberately a public argument pitched at an educated general audience, illustrated serially across six African ethnographic domains rather than argued through a single sustained case, and self-consciously undefined at its conceptual core. Gluckman tells his readers plainly in the Preface that he has "not even tried to define the two key concepts of my argument, 'conflict' and 'cohesion', since I hoped their meaning would emerge as I discussed them." This is a study whose method is demonstration by repetition across domains — feud, kingship, family, witchcraft, ritual, and the colonial colour-bar — rather than definitional rigor, and any assessment of it has to take that form seriously as a feature, not an accident.

The thesis is announced in Lecture I by way of T. S. Eliot. Gluckman quotes *Notes towards the Definition of Culture*: Eliot's suggestion that class and region, "by dividing the inhabitants of a country into two different kinds of groups, lead to a conflict favourable to creativeness and progress," and that these are "only two of an indefinite number of conflicts and jealousies which should be profitable to society... so that everyone should be an ally of everyone else in some respects, and an opponent in several others, and no one conflict, envy or fear will predominate." Gluckman converts Eliot's essayistic observation into an anthropological research program: "This is the central theme of my lectures — how men quarrel in terms of certain of their customary allegiances, but are restrained from violence through other conflicting allegiances which are also enjoined on them by custom. The result is that conflicts in one set of relationships, over a wider range of society or through a longer period of time, lead to the re-establishment of social cohesion." The credit line runs in two directions in the Preface: Gluckman also traces the "key idea" to his "first teacher in anthropology," Mrs A. W. Hoernlé, who in Johannesburg in 1931 set him thinking about it while they discussed the Zulu women's rites to the goddess Nomkubulwana — material that becomes Lecture V. The book is dedicated to Hoernlé on her seventieth birthday.

Intellectually, Gluckman writes from inside British [[structural-functionalism]], the program built by Radcliffe-Brown, Malinowski, and their students around the premise that customary institutions exist because of the function they serve for the persistence of the social system. He inherits the vocabulary of function, equilibrium, and social order from that program, and he explicitly names Radcliffe-Brown — "the doyen of anthropology" — as the source of the systematic knowledge of kinship on which he draws. But Gluckman's own move, made across all six lectures, departs from the classical equilibrium reading in one specific and consequential way: where Radcliffe-Brown and Malinowski had tended to treat customary institutions as harmonizing social relations directly, Gluckman argues that custom characteristically *multiplies conflicts* — divides people against one another along several different axes at once — and that social cohesion is the emergent, unintended product of those multiple, crossing divisions rather than of institutions that produce harmony in any single relationship. As he puts it discussing the Nuer, "custom appears to exacerbate these conflicts: but in doing so custom also restrains the conflicts from destroying the wider social order." This is Gluckman's founding move as head of the **[[manchester-school|Manchester School]]** of social anthropology (he was Professor of Social Anthropology at Manchester when the lectures were given): conflict is not the sociological residue that functionalism has to explain away, it is itself the generative mechanism of order. In this sense the lectures anticipate, in miniature and for a lay audience, currents that would later be named **[[conflict-theory|conflict theory]]** and the Manchester School's signature contribution to political and legal anthropology, the **extended-case method**, developed more fully by his students Clyde Mitchell, J. van Velsen, and Victor Turner.

The book's implicit polemical targets are threefold. First, against classical political theory in the Hobbesian vein, which imagines order as either the absence of conflict or its suppression by a sovereign, Gluckman insists — following Evans-Pritchard's description of "ordered anarchy" among the stateless Nuer — that order can be produced by conflict itself, without any centralized enforcing authority. Second, against a purely harmonistic reading of Radcliffe-Brown's functionalism, Gluckman insists that customs which visibly *estrange* people (marital taboos, generational avoidances, witchcraft accusations) are as functionally significant as customs that visibly unite them. Third, against Aristotle's classical distinction between rebellion and revolution — which Gluckman cites directly and credits for seeing "this distinction between rebellions and revolutions" and noting "that rebellions do not attack political authority itself" — Gluckman claims anthropologists "have developed considerably what is implied in this process," extending a philosophical observation into a structural mechanism.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

This is not a fieldwork report; it is a comparative synthesis delivered in a broadcast register, and its "method" has to be reconstructed from what the lectures actually do rather than from any stated methodology. Gluckman is candid about the compositional logic in the Preface: "I have largely used my own research, not because I thought it better than theirs, but because I felt freer to highlight and suppress my own data, to the extent required by broadcasting." This is a significant admission for a work that nonetheless functions as an authoritative synthesis: the selection of illustrative material was governed by the demands of a forty-five-minute radio talk, not by an evidentiary standard for adjudicating between competing interpretations of the ethnographic record.

Each of the first five lectures follows the same design: state the general thesis, ground it in one or two ethnographic monographs (usually not Gluckman's own — Evans-Pritchard on the Nuer, Fortes on the Tallensi, Evans-Pritchard again on the Azande, Hilda Kuper on the Swazi), extend the analysis with Gluckman's own Zulu or Lozi/Barotse material, and close with a modern British or industrial analogy meant to domesticate the African material for a metropolitan audience (trade unions, Parliament, the Ministry of Aircraft Production, the office of the foreman and the shop steward). The sixth lecture breaks this pattern deliberately: Gluckman signals the shift explicitly ("This evening I pass to consider an entirely different kind of social system, that of modern South Africa") and switches from typological demonstration to a single sustained historical-archival case, his own Zululand, read as a limiting instance in which the thesis's scope conditions are tested to destruction.

There is no single "sample" in any survey sense: the data are secondary ethnographic monographs selected for their fit to the thesis, supplemented by Gluckman's own two bodies of fieldwork — Zululand (1936–1938, under the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute milieu, though the fieldwork itself predates Gluckman's directorship of the Institute) and Barotseland (from 1940, including the 1947 return visit he describes for the "You have killed me" installation-speech anecdote). Gluckman is explicit that he selected his own material "not because I thought it better than theirs, but because I felt freer to highlight and suppress" it for broadcasting — a data-selection principle that a listener or reader has no way to audit. The colonial administrative record used in Lecture VI is treated as unproblematic primary evidence (Native Commissioners' reports quoted directly, e.g., "maintaining a kind of passive resistance to my authority"), without methodological comment on the reports' own colonial standpoint — itself a reflexive gap, addressed below.

### The central mechanism, stated and bounded

The mechanism running through Lectures I–V is stated most compactly at the close of Lecture I: "In this way custom unites where it divides, co-operation and conflict balancing each other. At the widest range, cohesion is stated in ritual terms — supported by mystical retribution — where values are unquestioned and axiomatic." Gluckman is careful, at several points, to bound the claim rather than universalize it. Discussing the Nuer he writes: "What emerges, I think, is that if there are sufficient conflicts of loyalties at work, settlement will be achieved and law and social order maintained... provided that there is a general need for peace, and recognition of a moral order in which this peace can flourish." That scope condition — general need for peace, and an accepted moral order — is the load-bearing hinge of the whole book, because Lecture VI is explicitly built to show what happens when it fails.

### Frailty in authority: rebellion, not revolution

Lecture II turns the Aristotelian distinction into a mechanism specific to divine or sacralized kingship. Gluckman argues that because Zulu and Lozi/Barotse kingship was ritually sacred and could only be held by a member of the royal family, discontent with a particular king could never generalize into an attack on kingship as an institution: "Rebels were not seeking to establish a different kind of political society, say, a republic, or even to install a different family on the throne. They were seeking to re-establish the kingship in all its ideals, by making a true prince, with the king-becoming graces, into the king." He extends the analysis structurally with the "device" of delegated blame — subordinate officers absorbing unpopularity so the king himself stays above reproach — illustrated by his own anecdote of a Barotse attendant rebuking Gluckman's carrier-foreman: "You must not tell our lord this. If the carriers know he knows they are short of food they will hate him. You must cut down their food so that they hate you. What do you think he pays you for?" The Barotse maxim he reports, "Every man thinks that the king has only one subject," captures the same structural point: leadership inherently provokes resentment because followers cannot see the competing claims a leader must balance.

### Estrangement in the family: custom builds distance as well as closeness

Lecture III leans heavily on Meyer Fortes's Tallensi material, quoting Fortes's own diagnosis of the father–son taboo: "Perhaps the most extraordinary feature of Tallensi parents' relations with their children is the recognition, in custom and belief, of the latent antagonism behind their mutual identification and comradeship, their devotion and co-operation... The mystical ideas and ritual symbols in which the rivalry between successive generations is clothed form the cultural mechanism by which it is neutralized and made to serve socially useful ends." Gluckman generalizes this into a structural claim about African kinship as a whole: because subsistence societies lack differentiated, single-interest institutions (factories, unions, clubs) to absorb the emotional needs a modern family delegates elsewhere, the wider kinship group must "drive wedges into the family itself" to secure the individual's wider loyalty. He traces this through a comparative account of matrilineal and patrilineal descent (the Roman maxim *mulier et origo et finis familiae est* — "a woman is the beginning and the end of a family" — and the Zulu *izinkomo zizala abantwana*, "cattle beget children") to argue that in both systems the underlying structural link is mother-to-child, but that the *legal* line — to husband or to brother — determines where the conjugal bond is strong (patrilineal, low divorce) or weak (matrilineal, high divorce, the extreme Nayar case via Kathleen Gough).

### Witchcraft as a sociological, not merely intellectual, system

Lecture IV pivots on the distinction between Evans-Pritchard's account of witchcraft's *intellectual logic* (in *Witchcraft, Oracles and Magic among the Azande*) and its *sociology* — where accusations fall. Gluckman's diviner anecdote makes this concrete: on hearing a man accuse his cousin the district governor of bewitching a relative, the old diviner tells Gluckman, "Obviously the accusation is absurd. Why should the governor hate his cousin? The governor possesses political power and has inherited the main family herd. The cousin thinks the governor hates him, because he hates the governor." Gluckman generalizes the pattern: witchcraft accusations are structurally excluded from the closest, legally corporate agnatic bond (Azande witchcraft descends father-to-son, so accusing a fellow noble would indict oneself) and instead attach to structurally ambiguous, in-marrying, or generationally competing relations — the "stranger" wife blamed by her mother-in-law, the sister's son straining against his mother's brother. His fishing anecdote in Barotseland ("He who got fish to-day is a witch!") and the Bemba beehive rule ("to find one beehive in the woods is luck, to find two is very good luck, to find three is witchcraft") illustrate the deeper point that exceptional individual success, in an economy without differentiated channels for ambition, is read as a moral threat to communal equality.

### Rituals of rebellion: the load-bearing concept

Lecture V is the chapter that gave the Manchester School one of its best-known technical terms, and Gluckman states its logic with unusual clarity in discussing the Swazi royal first-fruits ceremony analyzed by Hilda Kuper as "a drama of kingship": "the rites affirm not only the unity of the nation about the kingship, but also all the conflicts around the person of the reigning king: the resentment of his subjects against authority, the jealousy of his brother-princes who covet the throne." He quotes the ceremonial songs directly — "You hate the child king... King, alas for thy fate, / King, they reject thee, / King, they hate thee" — and draws the conclusion that "the acceptance of the established order as right and good, and even sacred, seems to allow unbridled licence, [in] the very rituals of rebellion, for the order itself keeps this rebellion within bounds." His formula for the underlying political logic, restated from Lecture II, is explicit: "Swazi were rebels, never revolutionaries." Gluckman is careful to state the scope condition for this mechanism as well, arguing near the chapter's close that modern industrial Britain has comparatively few rituals of protest precisely because its members hold voluntary, not compelled, membership in most groups, and can exit dissatisfying relationships rather than ritually protest within them — "rites of protest are less developed in those societies where some movement is possible."

### The colour-bar: the limiting case

Lecture VI is the book's methodological pivot, and Gluckman signals its different status directly: unlike the first five lectures, this one takes a single historical system — colonial and Union-era Zululand — rather than a typological survey across tribes. He extends the "peace in the feud" logic to a colonial polity for a time: cross-linking divisions within both the Zulu group (royalist versus collaborationist factions, pagan versus Christian, chiefs favoring Government against subjects who resisted it) and within the White group (missionaries against Boer farmers, Commissioners against Cape Town) produced, for decades, an ill-balanced but functioning cohesion. His summary formula inverts the received cynical reading of colonial administration: "the old Roman maxim, 'divide and rule', is not necessarily the Machiavellian trickery of self-seeking conquerors or rulers. Sociologically, the principle might be stated as, 'divide and cohere'." But Gluckman closes the lecture, and the book, by showing the mechanism's collapse as apartheid is consistently applied: "Hence I've called this analysis 'the bonds in the colour-bar' not because the Africans and Indians of South Africa are chained by discriminatory custom; but because discriminatory custom against the Africans and other coloured groups chains the dominant White group. This dominant group is separated so sharply from the other colour-groups that it loses its ability to manoeuvre and to establish links of friendship with Africans who, in other situations, may well be opponents... If these sorts of links are eliminated, Black will deal with White only as authoritarian ruler and employer, always as an enemy, and never as an ally." This is the book's only unambiguous prediction of systemic breakdown, and Gluckman states his political position plainly beforehand: "I am one of those South Africans who have reacted strongly against the racial discrimination of our country. I claim no prophetic vision in seeing disaster ahead of it."

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Five rhetorical strategies carry the argument across a form (radio talk) that could not use footnotes, tables, or technical apparatus to do the persuasive work a monograph would.

1. **One mechanism, six domains, escalating stakes.** The lectures are sequenced so that the same structural claim — divided allegiances in a narrower range produce cohesion in a wider one — is demonstrated first in a domain with low emotional charge for a British audience (a "primitive" feuding people, seemingly remote), then moves through kingship, family, witchcraft, and ritual, before landing on the colour-bar, the domain of maximal contemporary political stakes for Gluckman's own listeners. The serial repetition builds inductive confidence in the mechanism before it is tested against the hardest case.
2. **Rebellion, never revolution — an Aristotelian distinction domesticating political violence.** By repeatedly distinguishing rebellion (attacks on office-holders that reaffirm the office) from revolution (attacks on the structure of offices itself), Gluckman is able to narrate open violence, feud, regicide, and ritual insult of kings as system-*maintaining* rather than system-*threatening* — a move that does real interpretive work but, as the Critiques section below records, also forecloses the question of when African political systems did undergo genuine structural transformation.
3. **Modern British analogies as domestication.** Trade unions, Parliament, the Ministry of Aircraft Production, the foreman, the shop steward, the hospital ward-sister, the school prefect — Gluckman repeatedly translates African structural positions into familiar British institutional roles ("He is like the Nuer 'man of the earth'"), making the African material legible without (he insists) claiming the cases are identical.
4. **A moral disclaimer at the point of highest stakes.** Only in Lecture VI does Gluckman step outside the analytic voice to state a political position directly — "This doesn't mean that I approve of these conflicts. Indeed, I don't" — a rhetorical signal that the preceding five lectures' structural-functionalist "cohesion" language was not meant to read as approval of the systems it describes, including, by implication, of feud, tyranny, or witchcraft accusation.
5. **The Eliot/Hoernlé double framing.** By opening with T. S. Eliot's essayistic remark on class and regional conflict as socially "profitable" and crediting his teacher Hoernlé's fieldwork insight in the same breath, Gluckman situates anthropological analysis within a wider mid-century intellectual conversation about pluralism and social order, signaling to lay listeners that "conflicting loyalties" is not merely an African curiosity but a general problem of social theory.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Custom and Conflict in Africa* became a standard teaching text for the Manchester School's approach to political and legal anthropology, and remains the most accessible entry point to the concept of **ritual of rebellion**, developed more technically in Gluckman's Frazer Lecture, *Rituals of Rebellion in South-East Africa* (1954), which the book's own reading list cites as its companion piece. The "peace in the feud" formulation and Gluckman's insistence on custom's *cross-cutting* rather than harmonizing character are credited, in the attributed judgment of later commentators on the Manchester School, with helping establish political anthropology and legal anthropology as subfields organized around conflict and process rather than static structure. The lectures are also read as a pivot point in Gluckman's own career and in structural-functionalism more broadly: still recognizably functionalist in its vocabulary of function, order, and equilibrium, but shifting the explanatory center of gravity from harmony-producing institutions (Radcliffe-Brown's classical formulation) to conflict-producing-and-absorbing ones — an emphasis that anticipates, without fully arriving at, later **[[conflict-theory|conflict theory]]** in sociology and the explicitly processual, case-driven method (the **extended-case method**) that Gluckman's Manchester students J. Clyde Mitchell, J. van Velsen, and later Victor Turner would develop out of the same milieu, the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute in Central Africa.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: partially-supported`. The book has drawn sustained critique on several fronts, recorded here as attributed positions rather than adjudicated verdicts.

- **Over-integration / functionalist closure.** The most persistent charge, made by critics of structural-functionalism generally and of Gluckman's "ritual of rebellion" concept specifically, is that the framework treats conflict as *always* ultimately functional for the maintenance of the existing order — a closed explanatory system in which no amount of observed hostility, insult, or violence can, by definition, count as evidence against social cohesion, because any such evidence is simply read as a further mechanism of cohesion. On this reading the theory is difficult to falsify: genuine structural change (revolution, in Gluckman's own terms) would have to be explained by factors the model treats as exceptions rather than as its central object.
- **Ritual of rebellion cannot explain real change.** A more specific version of the same critique holds that by defining "rebellion" a priori as system-maintaining and reserving "revolution" for a category the book barely analyzes, Gluckman's apparatus is constructed so as to make instances of actual structural transformation in African political history invisible or anomalous, rather than illuminating how rebellion sometimes does tip into revolution.
- **Ritual theory after Turner.** Victor Turner, Gluckman's Manchester student and a contributor to the same Rhodes-Livingstone/Manchester intellectual milieu, developed a substantially different account of ritual reversal and status inversion through the concepts of liminality and *communitas* (*The Ritual Process*, 1969), which reframes ritual licence less as a safety-valve for structural hostility and more as the temporary dissolution of structure itself — a position later scholarship treats as complicating, though not simply superseding, Gluckman's ritual-of-rebellion account.
- **Colonial context of Central and Southern African fieldwork.** Gluckman's Zululand and Barotseland research, like that of his Rhodes-Livingstone Institute colleagues, was conducted within and partly enabled by the colonial administrative apparatus he analyzes in Lecture VI — Native Commissioners, magistracies, and the infrastructure of indirect rule. Later historiography of the discipline reads the Rhodes-Livingstone/Manchester School's structural-functionalist framework itself as a product of that colonial research setting, a point sharpened by the fact that Lecture VI's primary documentary evidence is drawn directly from the colonial administrators' own reports, read largely without independent corroboration from Zulu-authored sources.
- **The South African historical trajectory needs political-economy densification.** Gluckman's own account of Lecture VI is explicitly partial — "Zululand is an illustration, not a complete miniature, of modern Africa" — and later scholarship on the political economy of apartheid (migrant labor systems, mineral extraction, the Bantustan system) supplies structural drivers of the colour-bar's entrenchment that Gluckman's cross-linkage analysis, focused on interpersonal and factional allegiance, does not itself provide.
- **The gendered analysis of witchcraft and family estrangement rests on now-dated ethnographic sources.** The comparative kinship and witchcraft material in Lectures III and IV (Schapera on Bechuana marriage, the Nayar material via Kathleen Gough) reflects mid-century ethnographic conventions — including a matter-of-fact treatment of polygyny, bridewealth, and women's rights as objects transacted between men's groups — that later feminist anthropology reads critically, though this line of critique is not yet sourced from ingested works in this wiki.

## Current Standing

The book survives principally as a teaching classic and as the most accessible statement of concepts — "peace in the feud," "frailty in authority," "ritual of rebellion," "divide and cohere" — that did substantial subsequent work in political and legal anthropology, and as the clearest short expression of the Manchester School's distinctive departure from classical structural-functionalism: treating conflict, not harmony, as the generative mechanism of order. What has fared less well is the closed, difficult-to-falsify character of the "ritual of rebellion" formula when pressed to explain genuine structural change, and the book's own candid limits — a broadcast synthesis built for demonstration rather than systematic evidentiary test, drawing on colonial-era administrative sources without extended reflexive scrutiny of their production. It is not a substitute for Gluckman's more sustained monographs (notably *The Judicial Process among the Barotse of Northern Rhodesia*, 1955, published the same year) or for the extended-case studies his students later built on the same Central African fieldwork base.

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text used for this analysis is the 1973 Blackwell reprint of the 1955/56 first edition (the lectures are unrevised from their original broadcast form; a reading list keyed to each lecture was appended). Gluckman names his own most important antecedent publications in the reading list itself, including his Frazer Lecture *Rituals of Rebellion in South-East Africa* (1954) — the fuller technical treatment of Lecture V's central concept — and his APS chapter "The Kingdom of the Zulu of South Africa" (in Fortes and Evans-Pritchard, eds., *African Political Systems*, 1940), the earlier scholarly statement underlying Lectures II and VI.
