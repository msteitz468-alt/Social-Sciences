# The Second Shift — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[hochschild-second-shift-study]]. Source: [[hochschild-second-shift-1989]]. Author: [[hochschild-arlie-russell]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine canonical sections. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

[[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] asked what happens to love, power, and daily life when women enter the paid labor force in large numbers while men, workplaces, and cultural ideals of care change more slowly. Time-use researchers had already measured a "double day." She wanted the **inside** of the extra hours: how each partner feels about contribution and appreciation, how unconscious [[gender-strategy|gender strategies]] organize the evening, and how couples invent stories that make unequal loads livable.

Three discourses she joins (attributed):

1. **Political economy of gender** — deindustrialization and service growth, female employment rise, divorce association, career clocks built for family-free men (Preface: university as workplace with wives as unpaid "social workers").
2. **Time-use demography** — Szalai, Robinson, Walker & Woods: surface of hours without the meanings of twelve minutes with a child.
3. **Microsociology of feeling** — her own [[feeling-rules]] and [[emotion-work]] from *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*, now applied to unpaid domestic regimes rather than commercial service; Swidler's culture-as-strategy toolkit specialized to gender.

**Formative image (Preface, attributed):** nursing an infant during Barrows Hall office hours — the contradiction of career and care made bodily, not abstract.

**Program continuity:** *Second Shift* is the domestic companion to *Managed Heart*'s commercial feeling: both track how modern economies use human capacities for care and display while privatizing the costs.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do dual-earner couples divide and feel about unpaid labor? | Couple / household | 50 two-job couples with children under six | Intensive interviews (often repeated) | 1980–88 main |
| What does the second shift look like in practice? | Domestic evening/weekend | 12 families drawn from the 50 | Home observation, shadowing | multi-year contact |
| How unusual is progressive sharing? | Comparison | Berkeley artisans/students/professionals | Early interviews | late 1970s |
| Who is around the couple? | Care network | Sitters, day-care, teachers, traditional couples, divorcées | Interviews | same window |
| What is the aggregate leisure inequality? | Populations in prior studies | Szalai, Robinson, Walker & Woods, etc. | Secondary synthesis | Appendix |

**Design summary:**

| Element | Detail (as conducted) |
|---|---|
| Screening | 1980 questionnaire to every 13th name on a large urban manufacturing company personnel roster; full-time dual earners with children under six invited for depth work |
| Associates | Anne Machung (co-author); Elaine Kaplan named as research associate |
| Total N | 145 people: 100 spouses in 50 couples + 45 others; ~two-thirds interviewed several times |
| Observation stance | "As unobtrusive as a family dog"; living-room base; followed parents upstairs/down; day-care drop-offs |
| Task inventory | Who cooks, vacuums, plans, notices nail-clipping need, Christmas cards, car oil, etc. |
| Class composition | Disproportionately middle class (46% professional/technical; 12% blue-collar vs much higher national blue-collar share, 1982 BLS comparison in Appendix) |
| Ages | Mean men 33, women 31; 47% one child, 38% two, 15% three |
| Outside help | 79% none regularly (near national pattern) |

**What the design can do:** found a durable vocabulary; show mechanisms of marital accounting; class-contrast cases from warehouse to Silicon Valley. **What it cannot:** national probability estimates of sharing; equal depth for single parents or same-sex couples (noted as important, not core-sampled); clinical child assessment (day-care worker impressions only).

**What methods underplay:** company-roster selection and voluntary depth interviews bias toward couples willing to be watched; observer gender and feminist framing shape rapport (women more often brightened at another session — finding she reports); "myth" and "gratitude" are interpretive reconstructions.

## The Site / Sample / Data

### Time surface (literature)

Hochschild averaged major 1960s–70s studies and reported women worked roughly **fifteen hours longer each week** than men — an **extra month a year** of twenty-four-hour days. Parallel to the wage gap: a [[leisure-gap|leisure gap]] at home. Juster (cited): male slice of second shift ~20% (1965) → ~30% (1981) — slow change.

### Couple core (primary)

Fifty heterosexual married couples with young children; portraits of ten marriages in depth (Holts through Livingstons plus pathway cases). Observation of footsteps, dinner, bedtime, dog care — material culture of inequality (Holt garage elevated to "half" the house).

### Care workers

Baby-sitters and day-care workers often themselves dual-job parents; many welcomed being treated as workers. Channels of communication with parents often blocked by rush and fear of losing clients (Ch. 15, attributed).

### Core concepts (attributed)

- **[[second-shift]]** — unpaid shift after paid work; interviewee metaphor.
- **[[leisure-gap]]** — free-time twin of wage gap.
- **[[stalled-revolution]]** — women changed; workplaces and men lag.
- **[[gender-strategy]]** — plan of action + emotional preparation; three ideologies; surface/deep fractures.
- **[[family-myth]]** — shared story obscuring core tension (upstairs/downstairs).
- **[[economy-of-gratitude]]** — what counts as a gift; dead-letter-office appreciation.

### Aggregate patterns (study)

Women do about two-thirds of daily rigid jobs; multitask more; juggle three spheres (job, children, house) vs men's two more often. Men more control over *when* they contribute ("hold my calls" vs secretary taking calls). Working wives more "mothering the house"; husbands more of home time on preferred child play. Sleep as women's hunger-topic. Over half of working mothers tried to change home roles; ~one-third used supermoming. ~Two-thirds of couples matched ideology; one-third clashed especially on home work. ~One in eight seriously considered divorce; male avoidance of home work common in those cases.

### Men who share vs who don't (Ch. 15)

Sharing not explained by model fathers or childhood chores; recurring theme: disaffiliation from detached/absent/overbearing father plus enough male identification to empathize with mother safely. Paid hours weakly related. Relative earnings: 21% of higher-earning men shared, 30% of equal earners, **none** of men who earned less than wives — "balancing" power via avoiding second shift (theoretical). Wives' invitation nearly necessary; involved fathers elaborate fatherhood; uninvolved fathers imagine wives handle more than they do.

### Urbanizing peasant (Ch. 16)

Working wives analogized to peasants entering modern urban economies — complex motives for paid work, clash of two urgency systems, preservation and loss of domestic traditions across grandmother farm / mother housewife / daughter dual-earner generations (theoretical).

### Case gallery (illustrative mechanisms)

**Nancy and Evan Holt (Ch. 4).** Social worker and warehouse furniture salesman; son Joey. Nancy egalitarian (feminist, doormat-mother cautionary tale); Evan transitional (career fine if she handles family). Task estimates: she 80/90 housework/child care vs his 60/70 claim. Passive refusal of cooking schedule; sex used as leverage against her principles; upstairs/downstairs myth elevates garage and dog to "half"; conflict displaced into Joey's bedtime. Emotion work sustains the myth. Canonical [[family-myth]] and [[leisure-gap]] illustration.

**Frank and Carmen Delacorte (Ch. 5).** Traditional ideology, thin pocketbook; Carmen plays helpless so Frank enters kitchen without losing manhood; inverted public myth that he does little while he does more than many transitional men. Shows traditional couples can share more hours than transitional ones while feeling worse about it.

**Peter and Nina Tanagawa (Ch. 6).** Her salary shames his manhood; gratitude inverted; he "helps" rather than shares; workplace punishes part-time; illness as distress signal. [[economy-of-gratitude]] under female higher earnings.

**Ann and Robert Myerson (Ch. 7).** Silicon Valley antifamily workplace; she quits under cover story; flip-flop between career identification and home realness; she will not fully let him into second shift. Surface egalitarian, deep ambivalence.

**Seth and Jessica Stein (Ch. 8).** Career clockwork as his strategy; scarce gratitude for clashing sacrifices; hired help / "bought father"; emotional separation. Scarcity of gratitude as marital climate.

**Anita and Ray Judson (Ch. 9).** Blue-collar; her job as self-defense and divorce insurance; money–manhood–leisure; public myth vs private ulcer and volatility. Class intensifies economic insecurity of the stall.

**Greg and Carol Alston (Ch. 10).** Equal time ≠ primary parenting; his duration-matching woodwork; Chodorow engaged and amended by [[gender-strategy]]. His/hers of sharing.

**Barbara and John Livingston (Ch. 11).** Supermom/superdad; 50-50 time with her as organizer; birth jealousy vs feeling rule that he should support her work; busyness myth; no time for marriage.

**Michael and Adrienne Sherman / Art Winfield (Ch. 12).** Pathways: **sharing showdown** (leave and return until he shares) vs **natural drift** (primary fatherhood expands to housework fairness). Tokens of "New Man" without a national movement — third-stage economy, second-stage fatherhood (Nash periodization as used by Hochschild).

### Strategies and strains (Ch. 13–14)

Women's strategies: direct change (showdown, crisis, prods, talks); indirect (helplessness, illness, reframing, sex withdrawal); supermoming (~1/3); cutbacks at work/house/marriage/self; seeking help; abandoning personal leisure. Men's resistance: distracted/"playing dumb" tasks; wait-to-be-asked; substitute offerings; needs reduction; praising her organization. Three marital tensions: clashing strategies; shared traditional ideal vs need for her wage; undervaluation of care even when loads equalize (workaholism à deux). Husband sharing associated with happier marriages independent of role ideology (study finding).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**Load-bearing passage — leisure gap / second shift (Ch. 1):**

> Just as there is a wage gap between men and women in the workplace, there is a "leisure gap" between them at home. Most women work one shift at the office or factory and a "second shift" at home.

Rhetoric pairs industrial justice language (wage gap) with domestic time, making unpaid labor legible as inequality rather than love's natural expression.

**Load-bearing passage — stalled revolution (Ch. 2):**

> Women have changed. But most workplaces have remained inflexible… and at home, most men have yet to really adapt to the changes in women. This strain… leads me to speak of a stalled revolution.

Names incomplete transformation without declaring feminism failed; keeps structural and male-adaptation deficits co-present.

**Load-bearing passage — gratitude (Ch. 2):**

> When couples struggle, it is seldom simply over who does what. Far more often, it is over the giving and receiving of gratitude.

Shifts the research object from task lists to moral accounting — the move that makes family myths and strategies necessary tools.

**Load-bearing passage — upstairs/downstairs (Ch. 4):**

> "I do the upstairs, Evan does the downstairs."… the Holts' garage was elevated to the full moral and practical equivalent of the rest of the house.

Comic-material detail does theoretical work: the myth is visible in floor plans.

**Case rhetoric:** novelistic scene openings (mail on the floor, provider chair, bag-lunch moms' group) persuade as texture; Hochschild then names the mechanism. The risk is that readers remember Holts more than N=50 percentages — both layers are intentional.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The concept of the second shift "entered the language" (trade reception cited in later editions: Newsweek novelistic texture; Kuttner in *NYT Book Review* as quintessential domestic bind). Academic uptake in gender, family, work–family, and emotions sociology. Links *Managed Heart* commercialization to unpaid care: [[emotion-work]] under [[feeling-rules]] of equality or shame sustains or challenges the [[stalled-revolution|stall]] at home.

**Program effects (attributed reception pattern):**

- Vocabulary package (second shift, stalled revolution, leisure gap, gender strategy, family myth, economy of gratitude) becomes standard in undergraduate and research use.
- Fatherhood research takes up showdown vs drift and involved-father elaboration of care.
- Policy discourse: family-friendly workplaces, parental leave, care valuation, male participation — Afterword still diagnoses "emotional debt" and medicalization of parental overload as misdirected individual treatments of structural stall (2012-era reflection).
- Comparative family demography later retools "incomplete gender revolution" language (record secondary sources when ingested).

**Relation to *Managed Heart*:** commercial feeling sold for a wage vs domestic feeling managed without wage under unequal marital power; same authorial toolkit (feeling rules, emotion work, gift/gratitude) across public and private.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Content |
|---|---|
| Time-use revisionists | Some later diaries show narrower gaps; method (who drops out of long diaries) contested — Hochschild notes busy-woman dropout risk in Appendix |
| Class and race | Middle-class skew; Black women's distinct marriage/security history appears in Judson chapter but is not a full stratified design |
| Essentialism risk | Balancing and male-resistance typologies can be read as fixed male psychology; Hochschild ties them to structure and ideology |
| Strategy intentionality | "Strategy" may overstate conscious planning despite her emotional-preparation clause |
| Heteronormative core | Same-sex sharing cited from other research; not primary sample |
| Myth epistemic | Observer claims "core truth" behind couple narrative — interpretive privilege |

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — generative program with contested operationalizations, not a failed lab replication.

## Current Standing

Canonical. Still the default citation for unpaid domestic labor after paid work and for incomplete gender revolution. Best used as **mechanism vocabulary + period-stamped time synthesis + rich cases**, not as eternal 15-hour law. Companion books (*Time Bind*, *Outsourced Self*, *Global Woman*) extend the program — queue for further density.
