# Spirits of Resistance and Capitalist Discipline (Ong) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[ong-spirits-of-resistance-study]]. Source: [[ong-spirits-of-resistance-1987]]. Author: [[ong-aihwa]]. Society: [[sungai-jawa]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · nine canonical body sections · attributed voice for all substantive findings.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Spirits of Resistance and Capitalist Discipline: Factory Women in Malaysia* (State University of New York Press, 1987; SUNY series in the Anthropology of Work, June Nash editor) opens with a deceptively local puzzle:

> Why are Malay women workers periodically seized by spirit possession on the shopfloor of modern factories?

[[ong-aihwa|Aihwa Ong]]’s aim is not folkloric salvage. Educated Malays, she notes, treat spirit belief as a cultural relic that “should have been left in departed villages.” She instead argues that spirit attacks speak to **contemporary** experiences of Malay women and families as they move from peasant society into industrial production. The book’s real object is the **relationship among work discipline, sexuality, and cultural change** under simultaneous agrarian transition and export-oriented industrialization in coastal Selangor.

The disciplinary moment is the **critical turn** (late 1970s–80s): feminist anthropology’s second wave; anthropological political economy after Wolf; Foucault’s English reception on power/knowledge and bodies; cultural Marxism and “resistance” studies after Thompson, Scott, and Nash’s anthropology of work. Part I is framed by epigraphs from [[taussig-michael|Michael Taussig]] (1980) on precapitalist meanings at the capitalist periphery and Max Weber on labor as a calling that must be *educated*, not merely incentivized by wages. Ong’s preface explicitly asks whether possession symbolism maps Taussig’s elegant use-value/exchange-value opposition — and prepares a **Malay answer that will refuse that elegance**.

Ong’s culture definition (Ch. 1) situates the project against Geertzian webs-of-significance without power and against syncretic “cultural streams” or great/little tradition dualisms:

> In this book, “culture” is taken as historically situated and emergent, shifting and incomplete meanings and practices generated in webs of agency and power.

[[capitalist-discipline]] is defined operationally as the effect of power exercised in the interests of capitalist production — compliance enforced through cultural forms **inside and outside** the enterprise. That scalar claim is the book’s organizational principle: household, kampung, state, Islamic office, and Japanese corporation are one disciplinary ecology, not nested “levels” with the factory as the only real site of capital.

**As conducted, not as advertised:**

1. **Pseudonym regime.** Sungai Jawa, personal names, and firm codes (EJI, ENI, MUZ) are pseudonyms. The ethical gain is protection; the cost is non-replicability of site restudy by name.
2. **Positionality.** Ong is Malaysian-born Chinese, Columbia-trained, hosted as *kurang Jawa* (“not yet Javanese”) in a Malay-Javanese family — insider to nation, outsider to kampung ethnicity and Islam, woman fieldworker with access to female domestic and factory spaces that male ethnographers might lack.
3. **Multi-method but interpretive center of gravity.** The census and time budgets ground the political economy of Chapters 3–5; the book’s signature claims about possession and resistance are **thick description and hermeneutic argument**, not rate estimates for possession incidence.
4. **National press episodes + local interviews.** Signature possession numbers (40 / 120 / 15 / 21) are largely national FTZ cases Ong assembles; Telok material is managers, nurses, operators, and village talk. The comparative frame strengthens the industrial pattern claim while thinning micro-statistics for Telok alone.
5. **“No final authority.”** Ong presents multi-voiced ethnography — corporate, official Islamic, dakwah, parent, operator — and refuses monologic closure. That rhetorical stance is also a methodological limit: some contradictions are staged rather than adjudicated.

**Validity threats Ong surfaces:** partial/discontinuous consciousness among workers; ethnic structuring of class interest; management medicalization of possession; her own refraction as “multifaceted lens.”

## The Site / Sample / Data

### Mukim Telok and Sungai Jawa

Subdistrict of ~32,000 acres under Malay Reservations, estates, residual forest; five kampung. Commercial-administrative center Kampung Telok: Malay teachers/civil servants; Chinese and Malabari merchants; low-cost housing oriented to palm-oil factory and FTZ. Since 1972, *ketua kampung* appointed via UMNO/District Officer networks; *pembangunan* patronage structures resource flow.

**Land (242 HH, 1979):** average holding just under 3.5 acres; ~24% landless or house-lot only; 27% held 2–5 acres; **54% of acreage controlled by 12% of households**. Islamic inheritance parcellized; civil-servant and rich-villager accumulation for prestige/speculation; *lapar tanah*; squatting and 1979 forced eviction of reserve squatters. Rubber acreage in district collapsed 1966–78 as estates shifted to oil palm; kampung men entered contractor gangs; resident Tamil estate labor became “true rural proletariat” on remnant wages (~M$120/month, Sept 1979).

### Telok Free Trade Zone

Selangor State Development Corporation “rural industrialization”: 50-acre FTZ outside Telok, three Japanese firms — semiconductor assemblers **EJI** and **ENI**, musical-movement **MUZ**. ~2,000 workers total; ~85% female; operators mostly ages 16–25, single, from surrounding villages. Sungai Jawa alone ~400 young women in FTZ (60% at EJI). Recruitment: young single women 16–24, primary education, poor families within ~20 miles; Standard Six preferred as more controllable than Form Five “discontent.”

### State and capital context

EOI after early-1970s oil crisis; 59 industrial estates, 9 FTZs; tax holidays, free capital transfer; ≥40% bumiputra workforce condition. No legal minimum wage; sexual wage imbalance uncorrected; electronics starting wages near agro levels in rural Kuala Langat. High turnover (5–6%/month some firms; 3–4 year typical tenure) disrupted unionization; legal amendments and Registrar practice blocked electronics unions; MAS 1979 ISA imprisonment of unionists shadowed organizing. Peninsula Malay female manufacturing migrants ~1,000 (1970) → ~80,000 by decade’s end (Jamilah Ariffin via Ong).

### Verbatim load-bearing passages

**Discipline defined (preface):**

> If “discipline” is taken to mean the effect of the exercise of power in the interests of capitalist production, then social control can be traced through a variety of cultural forms which enforce compliance and order within and outside economic enterprises.

This sentence licenses the book’s refusal to treat the factory as a sealed labor-process black box. Household *nafkah* authority, UMNO *pembangunan*, religious raids, and corporate *ie* metaphors all count as discipline when they enforce production-compatible order.

**Taussig dialogue (preface / Ch.1):** Ong quotes Taussig’s mediating-image formula for the *hantu*, then refuses the clean use-/exchange-value mapping as sufficient for Malay factory women. The *hantu* may be noncapitalist critique — but also ambivalence between old claims and new capitalist desires, and a contemporary assault on Malay sense of humanity rather than a residual belief.

**Humanity idiom (Ch.9 operator):**

> Justice because sometimes they exhaust us very much as if they do not think that we too are human beings (*manusia*) … so that from time to time we must protest, that they should not rap us too much.

Ong’s analytic move: this is the grain of resistance — **justice as recognition of humanity** under microscope shifts and low wages — not a coherent theory of surplus value. Sporadic protest is “not so much informed by a specific class consciousness as by the felt violation of one’s fundamental humanity.”

**Signature possession statistic (Ch.9):**

> In 1975, forty Malay operators were seized by spirits in a large American electronics plant based in Sungai Way. A second large-scale incident in 1978 involved some 120 operators in the microscope sections. The factory had to be shut down for three days and a spirit-healer (*bomoh*) was hired to slaughter a goat on the premises. The American director wondered how he was to explain to corporate headquarters that “8,000 hours of production were lost because someone saw a ghost.”

The passage does triple work: establishes industrial scale, managerial incomprehension across capital’s cultural distance, and the **costing** of spirit attack in production hours — possession as economic event.

**Management medicalization (ENI):**

> They move from home idleness to factory discipline. The ghosts disturb only the new girls.

Biomedical and transition narratives (no breakfast, lack of sleep → ghosts; third-strike sacking for “security”) compete with workers’ pollution and burial-ground narratives. *Bomoh* photos hung in the factory, *kenduri* served to managers **excluding operators** — ritual management that reasserts hierarchy.

**Conclusion on class sexuality:**

> The hantu symbolism, shifting in and out of their consciousness, spoke not of an ideology of class struggle but of the right to be treated as human beings. Spirit attacks were indirect retaliations against coercion and demands for justice in personal terms within the industrial milieu.

This is the book’s final theoretical compression: [[spirits-of-resistance]] as personal-justice idiom under [[capitalist-discipline]], and “class sexuality” as reformulation still in process — not achieved class consciousness.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Scalar montage.** Chapters oscillate material relations (land tables, wage rates, labor law) and cultural discourse (sexual panics, dakwah, spirit taxonomies). The dialectic is the method.
2. **Against classic proletarianization.** Kuala Langat is not Lenin’s Russia; dispossession feeds state bureaucracy fractions and global corporate labor fragmentation.
3. **Against Thompsonian self-making class culture** as portable model (notes: historical and political limits in Malaysia).
4. **Against Engels teleology** that public industry is first condition of women’s liberation — Ong finds intensified external control as first condition of industrial induction, with less individuation than expected.
5. **Nimble fingers ideology** as racialized-gendered naturalization of cheap assembly labor — not technical necessity alone.
6. **Corporate culture as production of subjectivity** — family metaphors, *bapa angkat*, parents’ days, prayer control — Foucaultian productive power, not only repressive Taylorism.
7. **Multi-voiced refusal of final authority** — rhetorical democracy that also distributes responsibility for interpretation to the reader.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book became a **teaching classic** in feminist anthropology, anthropology of work, and globalization/gender courses. It is routinely paired with Taussig on spirit/capital and with other FTZ ethnographies. Concepts [[neophyte-factory-women]], [[capitalist-discipline]], and [[spirits-of-resistance]] travel as named tools. It anchors Southeast Asian cases in debates often dominated by Latin American or East Asian factory literature.

Impact channels:
- **[[feminist-anthropology]]:** industrial gender, sexuality as contested public arena, critique of teleological liberation-via-wages.
- **[[anthropological-political-economy]]:** multi-scalar capital, NEP/FTZ state, non-classic proletarianization.
- **Resistance studies:** resistance without class ideology; micro-tactics; spirit idiom (Lewis lineage).
- **Foucault in ethnography:** docile bodies and sexualized subjects in actually observed plants.

## Current Standing

Still **canonical** for 1980s industrial ethnography of gender. Use requires:
- **Date-stamp 1979–80** coastal Selangor / Telok FTZ;
- **Attributed voice** for possession-as-resistance;
- **Four-way care** not to equate Malay-Muslim political category, Javanese descent, kampung community, and factory workforce as one identity;
- Awareness that electronics labor regimes, Islamic politics, and Malaysian development strategies have moved since 1987.

## Domestic and Marriage Chapters as Infrastructure (not digression)

Readers sometimes treat Chapters 5–6 as “background” before the factory payoff. Methodologically they are load-bearing. Ong’s argument that discipline is multi-scalar **requires** showing how schooling streams girls into Form Three exit and FTZ entry; how eldest-daughter domestic loads produce dual burdens; how time-allocation fractures kampung “cigar-time” against three-shift clocks; how *lemah semangat* taboos pre-structure who is spiritually vulnerable on night shifts; how marriage strategies under commodity logic raise average first-marriage age (19→22, 1976–80 in Sungai Jawa) and thereby prolong junior female status precisely when wages create partial autonomy.

The Engels challenge lands here as much as on the shopfloor: public industry does not automatically individuate women into emancipated subjects. Peer *malu*, gossip about *bebas*/*sosial* sisters, dakwah models of womanhood, and parental claims on wages pull toward conformity. Spouse-choice autonomy among earners is real (almost all factory women Ong interviewed rejected parental spouse selection — “What if I don’t like him?”) yet coexists with self-enforced unfreedom. Four abortion cases and runaway/incest-refuge episodes (factually sparse in the monograph) mark edges of that autonomy, not its generalization.

Without these chapters, spirit possession looks like a shopfloor oddity. With them, possession is continuous with a wider moral economy of female spiritual vulnerability, sexual surveillance, and incomplete individuation under capital.

## Corporate Culture: Manufacturing Gender Hierarchy (Ch. 7)

Chapter 7 is the book’s labor-process core. Ong records three Japanese plants’ extreme separation of mental and manual labor facilitated by electronics technology; ~85% female workforces; “oriental girl” investment-brochure racism/sexism (“small hands… natural and inherited” bench assembly). Managers embroider “nimble fingers and slow wit”: assembly as knitting; females more patient; men would leave; female labor cheaper in Malaysia than Japan; “fresh” trained female labor highly efficient.

Corporate control repertoire includes Taylorist targets, microscope exhaustion, three shifts, overtime, continual surveillance; dual/temporary labor reserves; family-planning counseling for those who marry on the job; discouragement of married applicants (interacting with protective pregnancy legislation); *bapa angkat* (foster-father) relations and parents’ meetings that extend factory authority into the kampung; company unions and joint consultative committees; village leaders ending a MUZ strike. High turnover (5–6% monthly in some firms) plus legal barriers (under-three-years exclusion from unions; no strike-to-recognize; Registrar refusal for electronics under EIWU) deliver female workers “with little legal defense… to the disposal of international capital” (Ong’s position).

Theoretically Ong pushes past Burawoy/Braverman/Thompson: capital is **culturally embedded**; technology and discursive practices produce **docile bodies and sexualized subjects**; multiple power networks, not a single shopfloor logic, manufacture gender hierarchy. Look East (1982) and Islamic moral vocabularies are recruited into state management of the nascent Malay working class.

## Negative Image and Dakwah (Ch. 8)

Chapter 8 tracks the public production of the [[neophyte-factory-women]] as moral problem. *Minah karan* / Minah letrik media discourse; Bayan Lepas-type scandals; state and academic bio-power framings of female industrial sexuality. Official Islam experienced by young women mainly as legal system (marriage, divorce, inheritance, *khalwat*, *zinah*); understaffed religious offices focus on factory-women concentrations; gendered enforcement sometimes jails the woman when joint fines cannot be paid.

*Dakwah* (ABIM, Darul Arqam, Jamaat Tabligh) demands a new model of Malay womanhood opposed to Western consumer femininity; few factory women adopt voluntary *purdah* robes, but many seek recognition as morally upright Muslims doing *kerja halal*. An EJI operator’s call for religious classes and rules against *kebebasan* to “increase discipline” is Ong’s exhibit of “innocent complicity in greater social control.” An ABIM technician supplies a counter-idiom of *exploitasi* and “revolt behind their back.” The chapter’s structural punchline: factory employment gives economic independence from personal household subordination **and** intensifies contractual dependence on men in industrial hierarchy — many feel unfreedom more sharply at the threshold of emancipation.

## What Survived

- Multi-scalar discipline as ethnographic program — still the book’s most portable design lesson.
- Spirit possession as modern industrial event legible to capital (hours lost, goat on premises, third-strike firing) **and** to moral economy (*manusia*, justice, pollution).
- Neophyte factory woman as contradictory public symbol under EOI — a figure that recurs whenever export assembly recruits young women under sexual double binds.
- Refusal of easy class-consciousness teleology for gendered peripheral labor; ethnic structuring of production conflict as empirical obstacle to class politics.
- Teaching pairing with Taussig’s devil contracts as two critical-turn answers to spirit/capital — Ong’s Malay *hantu* as dialogue partner, not Taussig application.
- Vocabulary: [[capitalist-discipline]], [[spirits-of-resistance]], [[neophyte-factory-women]] remain usable analytic tags when kept date-stamped to 1979–80 Selangor.

## Counterfactual Significance

Without Ong 1987, Anglophone anthropology’s map of global-factory gender would still have Latin American and East Asian cases, but the **Malay-Muslim FTZ** type-case — Japanese corporate culture × Islamic moral surveillance × *hantu* possession × non-classic proletarianization — would be thinner. The book’s distinctive contribution is not “women work in factories” but the **joint** demonstration that spirit attack, sexual panic, and multi-scalar discipline are one research object. That jointness is what the Studies Hub exists to preserve against textbook reductions that quote only the goat and the 8,000 hours.

Had Ong published only a journal article on possession episodes, the village land tables, marriage-age shift, and *khalwat* surveillance might never have entered the same frame — and the multi-scalar thesis would collapse into a shopfloor curiosity. Had she published only a peasant-differentiation monograph without Chapters 7–9, the book would join a crowded NEP rural-sociology shelf without becoming a global-factory teaching classic. The integrated three-part structure (capitalist development and cultural experience; kampung society; neophyte factory women) is itself an argument: you cannot understand the spirits without the land market, the marriage market, and the moral panic.
