## Question and Intellectual Context

Commissioned in spring 1896 by the University of Pennsylvania and Philadelphia's
College Settlement — in the wake of Jane Addams's *Hull-House Maps and Papers*
(1895) — the study was framed by its sponsors as a diagnosis of the "social
problems" of the city's Black population, on the assumption that the Seventh Ward's
condition reflected Black character. [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]], newly returned from
Berlin's historical-economics school and holding the token rank of "Assistant
Instructor," inverted the brief: he treated the "Negro problem" as *"a plexus of
social problems"* to be explained structurally, against the biological and
evolutionary race theory then dominant. *(Fuller reconstruction of the intellectual
context — Du Bois's Berlin training, the American Negro Academy milieu, the
settlement-survey movement — pending the biographical and canon sources below.)*

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Why is the Black Seventh Ward poor and stratified? | Black community | ~9,700 residents, Seventh Ward (of ~40,000 Black Philadelphians) | Door-to-door canvass, schedules, mapping, statistics, history | Fieldwork Aug 1896 – Dec 1897 |

Du Bois and his wife lived on Saint Mary Street inside the ward. He conducted a
**door-to-door canvass** yielding, per the secondary literature, **over 5,000
personal interviews**, using household schedules on occupation, health, education,
family, and religion; he mapped every Black residence, church, and business, and
reconstructed the community's history from archives. Isabel Eaton contributed a
special report on domestic service. *(What the methods actually omit — schedule
wording, response/refusal handling, how the grades were coded, the exact division
of labor with Eaton — requires the primary text and its methodological appendix.)*

## The Site / Sample / Data

The Seventh Ward ran Spruce→South Street and Seventh Street→Schuylkill: white wealth
on the western fringe, a Black elite toward the center, poor residents of both races
on the east. See the subject-matter anchor [[philadelphia-seventh-ward]]. *(Fieldwork
conditions, Du Bois's access as a Black investigator studying a Black community under
white sponsorship, and the sample's construction await primary and biographical
sources.)*

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Black urban poverty is structural (industrial job bars, high rents, slavery's legacy), not pathological | Ward canvass + occupational/rent statistics | Confirmed — foundational to later structural urban sociology |
| The community is internally stratified into four social grades | Ward classification | Widely cited; the moralized language of the lowest grade is contested |
| The lowest grade is the [[submerged-tenth|"submerged tenth"]] (~a tenth; *unverified*) | Ward classification | Concept survives; exact proportion unverified here |
| Employers preferred European immigrants to depress Black wages | Labor-market observation | Confirmed as an early color-line-in-labor argument (per Anderson) |
| The "better classes" owe a duty to the masses | Class analysis | Seed of the 1903 [[talented-tenth|Talented Tenth]] |

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Anderson (2024) highlights Du Bois's mid-study conversion: from imagining
Philadelphia's white elites as uninformed *"benevolent despots"* to concluding
*"There was, however, no benevolent despot"* — the moment structural analysis
displaces reformist optimism. *(A proper rhetorical analysis — how Du Bois's prose,
tables, and maps jointly persuaded, and how his register shifts between clinical
statistics and moral address — requires the primary text for load-bearing
quotation, which this page cannot yet supply.)*

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Now read as the first empirical, statistically grounded community study in American
sociology and a founding text of [[urban-sociology]] and the structural analysis of
racialized poverty. Its survey-plus-mapping method anticipated the
[[chicago-school|Chicago School]] by a generation but "lay dormant" until the 1920s.
Its afterlife is one of neglect then reassessment — see
[[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]].

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — a foundational descriptive community study.
The centenary volume *W. E. B. Du Bois, Race, and the City* (Katz & Sugrue, eds.,
1998) reassessed it as still "vital and relevant," with essays linking Du Bois's
findings to deindustrialization and concentrated poverty. *(The full afterlife —
Morris's canon argument, demographic reanalyses of the ward data, each Katz–Sugrue
essay as a position — is the largest gap on this page and needs the sources below.)*

## Current Standing

Retrospectively canonized as a pioneer of empirical urban sociology; its structural
argument is treated as vindicated. Its exact place in the disciplinary canon, and
the status of its Victorian class vocabulary, remain live.
