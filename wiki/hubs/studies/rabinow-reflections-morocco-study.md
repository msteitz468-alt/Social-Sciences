## Question and Intellectual Context

*Reflections on Fieldwork in Morocco* is the rare ethnographic classic whose object is
ethnography itself. Paul [[rabinow-paul|Rabinow]] published it in 1977 in the University
of California Press's short-format "Quantum Books" series (~160 pp.), a decade after the
1968–69 doctoral fieldwork it narrates, and it arrived as a deliberate provocation to a
discipline that had made fieldwork its sacred rite while refusing to examine it.

Rabinow reconstructs the disciplinary climate that formed him. At the University of
Chicago's graduate anthropology department, "the world was divided into two categories of
people: those who had done fieldwork, and those who had not; the latter were not 'really'
anthropologists, regardless of what they knew about anthropological topics." Mircea
Eliade, encyclopedic in comparative religion, was the standing example of the erudite
non-anthropologist — "his intuition had not been altered by the alchemy of fieldwork."
Yet, Rabinow observed, "I knew of no book which made a serious intellectual effort to
define this essential rite of passage." The two books he names as expressing the ethos
that drew him to the field are diagnostic of the moment: Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of
Scientific Revolutions* (1962), whose "paradigm exhaustion" named a general
dissatisfaction, and [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s *Tristes Tropiques* (1955),
whose *dépaysement* promised "a distancing that would allow one to return more profoundly
home."

The intellectual apparatus Rabinow brings is hermeneutic and phenomenological rather than
positivist. He states it flatly: "The problem of the book is a hermeneutical one, and the
method I employ is a modified phenomenological one." Following [[ricoeur-paul|Paul
Ricoeur]] (*Le Conflit des Interprétations*, 1969), he defines the task as "the
comprehension of self by the detour of the comprehension of the other" — and specifies
that this "self" is "neither the Cartesian cogito nor the Freudian psychological self but
a culturally mediated and historically situated self." Robert N. [[bellah-robert-n|Bellah]]'s
Foreword sharpens the frame, insisting the "self" comprehended is the *cultural* self and
invoking Weber's [[verstehen|value-neutrality]] as a moral norm rather than a positivist
firewall.

The book's polemical target is a "positivistic view of science" applied to anthropology —
"radically inappropriate in a field which claims to study humanity." Against the notion of
fieldwork as a prerequisite *experience* separable from theory, Rabinow argues that
"fieldwork is a distinctive type of cultural activity, and … it is this activity which
defines the discipline." It is the same move that [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s
[[interpretive-anthropology|interpretive anthropology]] made for *culture* — turn it into
a text to be read — pushed onto the *encounter* itself.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

There is no design in the conventional sense: no sample frame, no instrument, no
hypothesis. The "design" is a narrated *sequence of relationships*, each chosen (in the
writing) to isolate the structural position of an interlocutor and what that position
made knowable. The unit of analysis is the **dyadic fieldwork encounter**.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is fieldwork, epistemologically? | the anthropologist–informant dyad | Sefrou + [[sidi-lahcen-lyussi]] | participant observation, narrated reflexively | 1968–69 (written up c.1975–77) |
| How is "data" produced? | successive named informants | Richard → Ibrahim → Ali → Mekki/Rashid → Malik → ben Mohammed | dialogue, elicitation, one paid contract | 1968–69 |
| What does the encounter do to the informant? | the informant's consciousness | Ali, Malik | systematic questioning; the "household economy" exercise | 1968–69 |

Rabinow reads [[participant-observation]] as an oxymoron whose governing term is
*observation*: "the tension between them defines the space of anthropology … this
dialectical spiral is governed in its motion by the starting point, which is observation."
He is explicit that fieldwork is "a dialectic between reflection and immediacy," both of
which are "cultural constructs."

The method's paradigm case is the **household-economy exercise** with his chief informant
Malik. Helping Malik itemize his landholdings produced a quantitative picture at odds with
Malik's self-image as *meskine* (poor, to be pitied). Rabinow's gloss is the book's
methodological core: "The anthropologist creates a doubling of consciousness … the data we
collect is doubly mediated, first by our own presence and then by the second-order
self-reflection we demand from our informants." Malik's own moral-social scheme (*nas
la-bas ʿali-hom*, those for whom things go well, vs. *nas msakin*, those to be pitied) was
not a defective version of socio-economic stratification but a different total order: "Our
objective social science, which treats facts as entities separable from a larger whole, is
real enough for us (perhaps), but it was definitely foreign to Malik."

A crucial and honestly flagged feature of the *conduct* is that the book is retrospective.
Rabinow wrote it roughly five years after the field and revised it two years later, calling
it "a studied condensation of a swirl of people, places, and feelings" in which "some
informants … are collapsed into the figures presented here, and others are left out
altogether." The methodological argument is therefore staged through composited,
edited scenes — a fact that both enables its analytic clarity and limits its standing as a
neutral field record.

## The Site / Sample / Data

Rabinow was attached to the Chicago Sefrou project directed by his advisor Geertz (with
Hildred Geertz and two other young anthropologists studying the walled market town);
Rabinow's assigned task was "the tribal areas surrounding Sefrou in the Middle Atlas
Mountains." He arrived via a charged passage — leaving Chicago two days after Robert
Kennedy's June 1968 assassination, through a Paris just cleared of May '68 students.

**Sefrou** (a Sais-plain oasis town of some 25,000, with a Jewish community documented
since the 9th century that historically brokered Berber–urban exchange) was the base.
Chapter 1 anatomizes the "dying colonialism" through Maurice Richard, the pseudonymous
French hotel owner who arrived in 1950 "a generation too late" to belong to either wave of
settler. Rabinow later recognized that his early aimless conversations with Richard had put
him in "a rather ideal 'anthropological' position": fluent, culturally familiar, trusted
by all sides, unquestionably an outsider. It is the reflexive point in miniature — the
best data came before he knew he was collecting it.

The village of **[[sidi-lahcen-lyussi]]** is the main site, and the book's ethnographic
data on it (thin by design) are dated firmly to 1968–69. It had roughly 900 inhabitants,
half belonging to four saintly lineages descended from the eponymous 17th-century saint,
the rest descendants of Berber refuge-seekers and the locally-named "children of the
slaves." Entry was a political negotiation: opposition centered on Rabinow's association
with Ali (persona non grata), and a rival faction leader engineered a temporary village
boycott by circulating a rumor — via a public "running interpretation" of a routine
gendarme visit — that the government would jail anyone who spoke to the *nazrani*
(Christian) in the saint's village. Rabinow presents this not as an obstacle to data but as
data: evidence of the factionalism structuring the village, and of his own position as a
contested "resource."

The **informant sequence** is the real sample, and Rabinow's Conclusion lays it out as a
typology of marginal intermediaries:

- **Ali**, "an insider's outsider" — a saint's descendant and Aissawa-brotherhood curer,
  ostracized for neglecting his saintly-lineage wife and running a small prostitution ring
  with his friend Soussi. His marginality is precisely what made him a first-rate
  informant: "Ali was more self-reflective about his society and his place in it than most
  other Moroccans I knew."
- **Mekki** and **Rashid** — failure cases: the first imposed by the villagers and unable
  to objectify his culture; the second talented but distrusted and silenced by the
  community.
- **Malik (Abd al-Malik ben Lahcen)** — a former Koranic teacher (*fqi*) on the edge of
  the dominant saintly sub-lineage (his father's exogamous marriage placed him there), who
  negotiated a formal paid contract (five dirhems a session, renewable monthly) and
  supplied most of Rabinow's kinship, irrigation, land, and religion data.
- **Driss ben Mohammed** — the young reformist who *refused* informant status and became a
  friend, providing "dialogue rather than data" and the book's closing meditation.

## Findings in Detail

*(All attributed to Rabinow 1977.)*

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Facts are *made*, not collected ("cannot be … picked up … like rocks") | the household-economy exercise; the whole narrative | near-consensus vocabulary; the constructivist core of reflexive method |
| Data are *doubly mediated* — by the anthropologist's presence and the informant's demanded self-reflection | Malik's "doubling of consciousness" | widely adopted; core of the reflexive turn |
| Fieldwork is intersubjective construction of *liminal* communication; understanding is a "moving ratio" that "never reaches identity" | Ali, Malik, ben Mohammed encounters | influential; extends [[turner-victor|Turner]]'s [[liminality]] to the fieldworker |
| A degree of *symbolic violence* against informants is inherent in fieldwork | the Sultan-exile extraction via Ali | most contested claim; some accept, some reject as overgeneralized |
| Good informants are *marginal self-objectifiers* | Ali vs. Mekki; Malik | echoed in later fieldwork methodology; the "stranger" theme |
| There is no "primitive," only "other men, living other lives"; Otherness is historical, not essential | Conclusion; the ben Mohammed dialogue | broadly absorbed; anti-essentialist consensus |

The two **load-bearing passages** repay quotation. First, on the informant's liminality —
the single most-cited sentence of the book:

> "Under my systematic questioning, Ali was taking realms of his own world and interpreting
> them for an outsider. This meant that he, too, was spending more time in this liminal,
> self-conscious world between cultures. This is a difficult and trying experience — one
> could almost say it is 'unnatural' — and not everyone will tolerate its ambiguities and
> strains."

The claim is double-edged: the informant is not a neutral window onto a culture but is
*changed* by the interrogation, drawn into an artificial second-order consciousness. The
"data" is a joint product of that unnatural state.

Second, on symbolic violence, provoked by Rabinow's trading Ali a ride to his mistress in
exchange for the suppressed account of the 1953 exile of Sultan Mohammed V:

> "My response was essentially an act of violence; it was carried out on a symbolic level,
> but it was violence nonetheless. I was transgressing the integrity of my informants … To
> those who claim that some form of this symbolic violence was not part of their own field
> experience, I reply simply that I do not believe them. It is inherent in the structure
> of the situation."

And the closing formulation, from the ben Mohammed friendship:

> "Our Otherness was not an ineffable essence, but rather the sum of different historical
> experiences. Different webs of signification separated us, but these webs were now at
> least partially intertwined."

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book persuades as a *text*, and its rhetoric is worth reading against its argument.

**The confession turned into epistemology.** Where Malinowski's posthumous *Diary* (1967)
exposed the fieldworker's private squalor as scandal, Rabinow makes the exposure
*argumentative*: every awkward, coercive, or failed encounter is mined for what it reveals
about how knowledge is made. Bellah reads the narrative as a modified hero's journey — the
hero returns "to an even deeper doubt about the very meaning and existence of home than he
had before he set out."

**Structural reading of persons.** Richard, Ibrahim, Ali, Malik, and ben Mohammed are each
read as *positions* in a social field, not as characters. "Just as Richard was situated
between the two French communities, and Ibrahim between the French and local Moroccan Ville
Nouvelle groups, so Ali was situated between the floating population of the medina and his
natal village." The typology of marginal intermediaries is the book's structuralism —
inherited from the Chicago/Geertz milieu even as it is turned reflexively inward.

**Borrowed philosophical authority.** Ricoeur supplies the motto; [[schutz-alfred|Schutz]]
and [[berger-peter-l|Berger]] & [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] supply the "life-world" and
its "thinness" (common sense as "loosely articulated, largely taken for granted, incapable
of withstanding sustained scrutiny"); Geertz supplies [[webs-of-significance|"webs of
significance"]]; Aristotle's *Nicomachean Ethics* frames the ben Mohammed friendship;
Frederic Jameson's *The Prison-House of Language* supplies the "paradigm shift" from
substantive to relational thinking that Rabinow analogizes to anthropology. The
philosophical scaffolding is worn openly, which is itself a rhetorical claim: this is
theory, not mere memoir.

**Against the grand cause.** Rabinow deflates structural-causal generalization ("neo-colonialism
causes rural poverty") as "almost empty" without particular mediations — dramatized by the
finding that a neighboring, equally poor village had actually *prospered* under the French
while Sidi Lahcen declined, because Sidi Lahcen's elders had refused (after WWI) a French
school/market/academy complex fearing loss of religious control over education. The lesson
is methodological: the interpretive particular over the abstract law.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Reflections* became a canonical node in a rapidly consolidating reflexive turn. Alongside
[[crapanzano-vincent|Vincent Crapanzano]]'s *Tuhami* (1980) and Kevin
[[dwyer-kevin|Dwyer]]'s *Moroccan Dialogues* (1982) — a striking Moroccanist cluster — it
moved anthropology from confessional aside to a full **epistemology of fieldwork**. Its
vocabulary (co-constructed data, partial understanding, the fieldworker's positionality)
became standard.

By 1986 the impulse was radicalized and textualized in
*[[clifford-marcus-writing-culture-1986|Writing Culture]]*, where "reflexivity" came to
name the whole crisis of ethnographic authority (see [[writing-culture-debate]]).
Tellingly, Rabinow contributed to that volume ("Representations Are Social Facts") but
turned *critic* — of textualism as a new canon and of the institutional location of the
post-modern moment — drawing on Foucault and Bourdieu. His own 1977 book had kept the
reflexive question dialogical and hermeneutic rather than literary-critical; the drift of
"reflexivity" toward textual self-consciousness is partly a drift *away* from his sense of
it.

The book also stands in an instructive relation to Geertzian
[[interpretive-anthropology|interpretive anthropology]]. Rabinow keeps Geertz's semiotic
culture and "webs of significance" but insists the webs of anthropologist and informant
become "at least partially intertwined" in the encounter — a subtle but real departure from
the interpreter reading a culture-text from outside.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

Not a replicable study; its afterlife is critical.

- **Political economy and institutional critique.** [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] and allied
  critics of the Writing Culture moment argue that reflexive accounts locate the problem of
  ethnographic authority in individual consciousness and *writing* rather than in the
  colonial and professional *power* that structures the encounter — precisely the power
  Rabinow's own "dying colonialism" chapter gestures at but does not theorize as a
  constraint on his method.
- **The narcissism charge.** Later critics (feminist, decolonial, and otherwise) press that
  the reflexive gaze can re-center the anthropologist's experience over the people studied,
  and that "partial understanding" humility can license thin ethnography. Rabinow's book,
  which foregrounds his own consciousness and gives the villagers relatively little
  independent ethnographic weight, is a natural target.
- **The symbolic-violence overgeneralization.** Rabinow's claim that symbolic violence is
  "inherent in the structure of the situation" — and his refusal to believe fieldworkers
  who deny it — is the book's most disputed move: a universal claim asserted from a single
  charged episode.
- **The informants' own charged positions.** The book records, in attributed voice, Malik's
  anti-Black prejudice ("Black was bad … a color worthy of a dog") and Driss ben Mohammed's
  reformist theology that "all Muslims, even the most unworthy … are superior to all
  non-Muslims" as "the fundamental cultural distinction." Rabinow reports these without
  endorsement and largely declines to confront them in the field — a choice that is itself
  part of the reflexive record (the ethics of when the fieldworker argues back), not a
  wiki-voice claim about Moroccan belief. Per the wiki's protocol these are documentation of
  informants' views and, where they concern live disputes, position-recording — not
  findings.

There are no restudies of Sidi Lahcen in the wiki's holdings; the village enters the record
almost entirely through Rabinow's lens.

## Current Standing

*Reflections on Fieldwork in Morocco* is a fixture of graduate methods and history-of-anthropology
teaching, routinely assigned as the prehistory of *Writing Culture*. Its core theses —
data as co-constructed, understanding as partial, reflexivity as an obligation rather than
an indulgence — are now so absorbed as to be nearly invisible as claims. Its strongest
theses (symbolic violence as universal and inherent) remain live. As an ethnography of
*Morocco* it is thin and superseded by Rabinow's own *Symbolic Domination* (1975) and the
wider Moroccanist literature; as an ethnography of *fieldwork* it remains a reference point.

## Historiography and Sources

The episodes are, by Rabinow's own account, retrospective reconstructions with composited
informants, so the book documents an *argument about* fieldwork as much as the fieldwork
itself — a historiographic caution that applies to any use of its scenes as evidence about
Sidi Lahcen. The companion volume *Symbolic Domination: Cultural Form and Historical Change
in Morocco* (University of Chicago Press, 1975) carries the conventional analysis that
*Reflections* brackets, and the two should be read together. Rabinow's Selected
Bibliography names his theoretical foundations — [[berger-peter-l|Berger]] &
[[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]]'s *Social Construction of Reality*,
[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s *Interpretation of Cultures*, [[ricoeur-paul|Ricoeur]],
[[schutz-alfred|Schutz]], Sartre, Duvignaud — and a comparative "Anthropological Fieldwork"
list (Bowen/Bohannan's *Return to Laughter*, Powdermaker's *Stranger and Friend*, Briggs's
*Never in Anger*, Maybury-Lewis, Malinowski's *Diary*, Crapanzano) that maps the
reflexive-memoir genre into which Rabinow deliberately wrote himself.
