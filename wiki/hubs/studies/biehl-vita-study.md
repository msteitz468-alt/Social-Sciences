## Question and Intellectual Context

*Vita: Life in a Zone of Social Abandonment* (UC Press, 2005) asks how a person becomes abandonable in contemporary Brazil — how family, psychiatry, pharmaceuticals, informal asylums, charity politics, and a formal right to health jointly produce what [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]] calls the **[[ex-human]]** — and whether reconstructing one life can make those linkages thinkable. The book is not a community study of "the poor" or a clinic ethnography of "the mentally ill." It is a **dialogic life-history** centered on Catarina (pseudonym), a woman Biehl first met in March 1997 in the infirmary of **[[vita-porto-alegre|Vita]]**, a makeshift asylum in Porto Alegre, Rio Grande do Sul.

Biehl frames three problems crystallized by early conversations with her: how inner worlds are remade under economic pressures; the domestic role of pharmaceuticals as moral technologies; and the common sense that creates a category of unsound and unproductive individuals who are allowed to die (attributed). The intellectual neighborhood is **[[critical-medical-anthropology]]** and the anthropology of suffering after Brazil's redemocratization — adjacent to [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] on **[[everyday-violence]]** and hunger-medicalization in the Brazilian Northeast, and in tension with the interpretive/clinical pole associated with [[kleinman-arthur|Kleinman]] (**[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology]]**, **[[illness-narrative]]**). Biehl is not writing a manifesto of [[militant-anthropology]], but he is not a neutral observer either: he arranges care, facilitates genetic testing, reports to human-rights bodies, and treats Catarina's writing as theory.

The historical moment matters. Brazil's 1988 constitution proclaimed health a right of all; the SUS and psychiatric reform discourse of "citizens burdened by mental suffering" promised deinstitutionalization and community care. *Vita* shows, in Biehl's reading, how that same moment generated **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of social abandonment]]** — places where rights no longer obtain for those who fail the triage of family productivity, pharmaceutical compliance, and model-program eligibility.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** Biehl entered Vita in March 1995 while documenting how marginalized people dealt with AIDS and new control-measure programs; Gerson Winkler (human-rights activist, city AIDS program) and photographer Torben Eskerod accompanied the first visit. Catarina was not the initial object. Only after repeated returns (March 1997; December 1999 photographic fieldwork; 2001–2002 clinical and family reconstruction; last meeting August 2003) did the book become the Catarina study. Consent: oral and written, 1999. Biehl personally conducted and translated the interviews that form the main body; he presents them chronologically, edited for clarity. He reports that everything Catarina said about familial and medical pathways into Vita matched archives and family testimony later found — a verification claim central to the book's epistemic authority (attributed as Biehl's finding).

Methodologically the book is a **hybrid**: institutional ethnography of Vita; multi-site reconstruction of a psychiatric career (Caridade Hospital, São Paulo Hospital, House of Mental Health, general-hospital notes); family ethnography across brothers, ex-husband, children, adoptive patrons; and a collaborative textual practice around the dictionary. Biehl cites Roma Chatterji's "file self" for reading hospital notes as retrieving a patient's voice and its alteration. Theory is integrated into description; there are no free-standing theory chapters. The photographs are not illustration alone — Eskerod's 1995 and 2001 images structure the book's claim that abandonment is visible and still unseen.

What the methods section can understate: the anthropologist as broker (pain creams, special food, Casa da Vida transfer attempt, children's visit, disability-pension plans with Jardim); the dependence on Oscar (resident-administrator) for access and logistics; the political risk of documenting Luchesi-patronized Vita; and the fact that Catarina died mid-project (15 September 2003), so the Postscript is also a death register.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Vita** (founded 1987 by Zé das Drogas after Pentecostal conversion) began as a squat rehab for addicts near downtown Porto Alegre and widened as relatives, neighbors, hospitals, and police left the mentally ill, sick, unemployed, and homeless there. An infirmary opened where the abandoned "waited with death" (Biehl's phrasing). Mid-1990s: ~200 men in recovery, ~200 in infirmary, one bathroom each side, gate between. Staff largely residents who had improved enough to care for others. No city-map presence; public knowledge without remedial program. Class mix: some from working- and middle-class families; others previously institutionalized then dumped.

**City ecology.** Porto Alegre had ~25 "geriatric houses" c. 1976 and more than 200 by Biehl's period, ~70% clandestine, housing the abandoned for welfare pensions (Biehl, citing local reports). After 34 deaths at Santa Luisa (1998), city raids of 200+ services closed few; judges often gave owners "one last chance" (officials Oliveira/Godoy via Biehl). Vita's political clout (Jandir Luchesi) blocked investigation of violation reports; non-registration as elderly health care made formal inspection impossible.

**Catarina as sample of one.** Working-class trajectory: marriage to Nilson, children (Anderson, Alessandra, Ana), repeated hospitalizations from late 1980s, fluctuating diagnoses (psychosis → affective disorder), polypharmacy, house fire/exchange, brothers and adoptive patrons (Urbano/Tamara), last city medical notes December 1995 ("Prescriptions"), then Vita. Brothers Altamir, Ademar, Armando later share cerebellar ataxia; sister Terezinha in a nursing home. The "sample" is not a population draw — it is a pathway selected because her speech and writing opened the machine of abandonment.

**Dictionary data.** Twenty-one notebooks, December 1999–August 2003 (two thrown away by volunteer nurses). Free-verse clusters: dead alive / dead outside, alive inside; pharmacy and rheumatism; divorce and documents; children and brothers; late "Genetics clinic"; refusal of state treatment as "death sentence." Catarina: signification lives in her memory — "With the pen, only I can do it."

### Load-bearing finding 1 — Zones and the ex-human

Biehl writes:

> Vita is a place in the world for ex-humans. I use this concept reluctantly as I try to express the difficult truth that these persons have been de facto terminally excluded from what counts as reality. I first thought of the term "ex-human" as Catarina told me, "I am an ex," and constantly referred to herself as an "ex-wife" and her kin as "my ex-family." … When I say ex-human, I want to highlight the fact that these people's efforts to constitute their lives vis-à-vis institutions meant to confirm and advance humanness were deemed good for nothing… Before biological death, there comes their social death.

What the words commit him to: not Agambenian bare life (language and desire continue), not a soul-less residual, but a form of life produced *inside* the failure of humanizing institutions. The emic "ex-" migrates into an etic analytic. The hesitation ("reluctantly") is part of the claim — philosophical distance is a risk he records against himself.

### Load-bearing finding 2 — Pharmaceutical being

In the hospital archive, Biehl finds that "a pharmaceutically mediated set of signs and symptoms was reported and treated. There was no human voice… Catarina was becoming a pharmaceutical double of herself." Polypharmacy (haloperidol, chlorpromazine, prometazine, biperiden) constitutes the body treated; her "rheumatism" is "relationally and medically engineered" (attributed). Discharge instructions send her "into the world, to medicate herself." Families, model services, and state policy meet in medication — written as "well" in a nonexistent home. This is not only **[[medicalization]]** (social problems → diseases) but a reorganization of *being* around intermittent pharmacotherapy when care has failed.

### Load-bearing finding 3 — Genetics without reduction

MRI shows cerebellar atrophy; molecular testing reveals Machado-Joseph disease (MJD/SCA3) in the brothers; Catarina is later confirmed (as of Biehl 2005). Dr. Jardim: no psychosis or dementia linked to MJD — "your intelligence will be preserved, clean and crystalline." Doctors examining Catarina find her "completely lucid… no mental or other pathology." Biehl treats the diagnosis as a "beacon in the overwhelming disqualification of her as mad" that sheds light on historicity — not as the single cause that would make the social analysis superfluous. Biopsychiatrists could still posit two concomitant processes; Biehl refuses both pure constructivism and pure biologism. Date-stamp: genetic and clinical claims as of the book's collaboration and literature (gene discovery 1994; Jardim's service).

### Load-bearing finding 4 — Dictionary as theory

Catarina: "I write, and you give a way to the words… in all possible ways." Biehl: her subjectivity was "the constant struggle against the intertwining of biology and social death — writing helped her to draw out the best of herself." Literality is the force of the work; words are disjointed objects addressing "the unspeakable of the ordinary." The anthropologist's role is not to supply meaning she lacks but to give *a way to the words* — brokerage of language as ethical and methodological act.

## The Moral Career into Vita (reconstruction)

Biehl's Part Two–Four reconstruction of Catarina's path is the study's spine. Compressed:

1. **Marriage and household labor.** Catarina works as wife and mother in a working-class household; land and property dynamics later favor Nilson (ex-husband) in Biehl's reconstruction of family accounts.
2. **Psychiatric cycling.** Repeated admissions (Biehl tracks Caridade, São Paulo Hospital, House of Mental Health and related services): diagnoses fluctuate from psychosis-type labels toward affective disorder, reflecting broader psychiatric trends, while treatment packages remain heavy antipsychotic polypharmacy. Catarina's reported walking difficulties and "rheumatism" are not medically pursued in the files Biehl recovers.
3. **Family as proxy-psychiatry.** Medication becomes "a family tool: to help with adherence, withdrawal, or oversedation" (House of Mental Health team via Biehl). Household, model service, and state policy meet in the pharmacy.
4. **Economic and legal unmaking.** House exchange/fire; loss of continuous income; divorce signed without her (judge); children placed with others; ID and bank card with adoptive patrons Tamara/Urbano; path into Vita via city-hall pickup and sparse visits.
5. **End of city medical domain.** Last Kunz notes October/December 1995: "Prescriptions." At roughly age 28, alone, without legal rights to her children, without income, overmedicated, losing locomotion — "too late," and no one follows through on a neurological referral (Biehl's synthesis).
6. **Vita.** Infirmary placement among the abandonados; "In my thinking, I see that people forgot me" (Catarina). Care workers Lurdes/Iraci and others mediate daily life; Oscar administers; pharmacy dependence continues.

Brothers later share the disease but are read as physically deteriorating, not mad; they use molecular diagnosis for disability retirement (*desenganar*). Gendered patterning of the same biology is itself a finding (attributed): Catarina's condition was made invisible when she became a "mental case" and castaway.

**Everyday violence without the name.** Part Four's section title invokes ordinary violence; Biehl does not systematically cite Scheper-Hughes there. The vault records adjacency: both document authorized dying in Brazil; Biehl's machinery is family–clinic–pharmacy–informal asylum under formal rights, not the Alto's scarcity-and-motherhood complex.

## Citizenship, Rights, and the Political Economy of Care

Biehl insists that social death and mobilization of life *coexist* in Brazil's political and medical institutions. March 1997 return: "incipient citizenship" along with social death — recovery-area men rehabilitated, some accessing AIDS disability pensions and free antiretrovirals, while the infirmary waited with death and Catarina among them. The 1988 right to health and SUS reform language ("citizens burdened by mental suffering") did not abolish abandonment; they redistributed who could claim care.

Human-rights infrastructure is shown as largely "symbolic" (Oliveira via Biehl): raids after Santa Luisa deaths, few closures, judicial protection of owners' livelihoods, Vita's non-registration blocking inspection, Luchesi's political clout blocking investigation of violation reports. Casa da Vida — a model nonpsychiatric service for homeless people with "mental suffering" — refuses Catarina because policy excludes wheelchairs and she cannot defend herself if hit; the director has no alternative to recommend. Model programs produce success stories of those who can walk out.

Letting people die is economically functional for Vita's donation machine (Oscar via Biehl): radio appeals feature grandparents and the disabled, not drug addicts; the infirmary is the "heart of Vita." By 2003 Biehl observes empty construction façades that attract money, fewer residents equaling fewer problems, and TB deaths investigated only after the fact. The Conclusion's political thesis: notions of universal human rights are "socially and materially conditioned by medical and economic imperatives"; certain rights discourse that generates model programs works by exclusion; "public death remains at the center of various social structures" (attributed).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Single life as structural cut.** Catarina is not representative by sampling; she is a path that intersects family, clinic, pharmacy, genetics, and dump-site. The rhetoric is synecdoche with verification: her story matches the archive.
2. **Dialogic form.** Book progression mirrors joint work; theory is folded into scenes; photographs interrupt and authenticate.
3. **Refusal of pure poles.** Against "only culture" and "only genes"; against rights triumphalism and against fatalism that naturalizes abandonment.
4. **Pharmakon / pharmakos.** Pharmaceutical care as remedy and poison; Catarina as modern scapegoat of household and state triage (attributed theoretical armature drawing on Derrida).
5. **Incipient citizenship with social death.** Recovery-area AIDS pensions and antiretrovirals coexist with infirmary dying — the same political-medical field produces both (attributed).
6. **Death as evidence.** The Postscript's autopsy (intestinal bleeding; hours without assistance) is not melodrama alone; it is the endpoint of the zone's ordinary functioning.
7. **Irony of recognition.** When Catarina finally receives an MJD diagnosis, she meets Vania (sister-in-law who once procured antipsychotics for her) at the genetics office seeking disability papers for Altamir — the same kinship network that managed her as mad now extracts biological truth for men's pensions (Biehl's staged irony).
8. **Common sense.** Geertzian "common sense" is enlisted to name the household criterion of impossibility of care — not culture as total system but the taken-for-granted that makes abandonment thinkable without agents.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Vita* entered medical anthropology as a type case for zones of abandonment, pharmaceuticalization of care, and single-life ethnography as structural analysis. It densifies the critical/political-economic pole of [[medical-anthropology]] with a southern-Brazilian institutional case distinct from Scheper-Hughes's Alto do Cruzeiro (Northeast, infant death, hunger-medicalization) and from Kleinman's clinical-interpretive China/US program. Concepts **[[zone-of-social-abandonment]]**, **[[pharmaceutical-being]]**, **[[ex-human]]**, and **[[social-psychosis]]** are the book's durable exports in this vault. The dictionary practice offers a method exemplar for collaborative textual work with institutionalized persons.

Reception beyond this vault (not yet ingested as secondary literature) has treated *Vita* as a contribution to the anthropology of pharmaceuticals, biological citizenship, and "social abandonment" as a comparative keyword for post-welfare dump-sites. Within the vault's link graph, the book completes a three-node medical-anthropology triangle: **critical ethnography of everyday dying** (Scheper-Hughes), **interpretive clinical anthropology** (Kleinman), and **institutional life-history of pharmaceutical abandonment** (Biehl). It also feeds [[life-history-method]] with a contemporary exemplar that combines archive, family, genetics, and collaborative writing — a different documentary ecology from Thomas–Znaniecki letters or Connell's masculinity interviews.

The photographs by Torben Eskerod matter for reception: they made Vita visually citable in teaching and review, and they bind the book's claim that abandonment is public knowledge without becoming public responsibility. The Postscript's death of Catarina forces readers to treat the ethnography as historically closed for its focal person — not an open-ended "follow-up" community.

Counterfactual significance (analytic, not counterfactual history as wiki fact): without *Vita*, the vault's critical medical anthropology would rest almost entirely on Northeast Brazilian scarcity and maternal practice; the southern institutional-pharmacy-rights configuration would remain a prose footnote. The book is what makes "zone of social abandonment" a first-class concept page rather than a passing phrase.

## Current Standing

Landmark contemporary medical ethnography; primary vault source for zones of social abandonment and pharmaceutical being. Anchors critical medical anthropology beside *Death Without Weeping* and opposite the Illness Narratives program as the third primary medical-anthropology source. Catarina's death (15 September 2003) is part of the study's temporal frame — documentation ends in burial, not in recovery.
