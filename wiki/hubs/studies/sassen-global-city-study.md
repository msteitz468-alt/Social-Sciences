# The Global City (Sassen) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[sassen-global-city-study]]. Reciprocal hub link from the summary.

**Analysis standard:** attributed voice for findings · design and findings tables · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife in full · nine sections. Built from *[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]* (2nd ed. 2001).

## Question and Intellectual Context

Sassen (as attributed throughout this page) opens not from urban theory but from a puzzle in the internationalization literature: if capital, production, and information have become geographically mobile and telecommunications have made distance seemingly irrelevant, why does economic activity keep concentrating in a handful of very expensive, very dense places? Her answer displaces the standard "who rules" question of urban power-structure research with a production question — what must be *made*, and where, for a globally dispersed economy to be coordinated and controlled at all. She states the point of departure directly:

> "The point of departure for the present study is that the combination of spatial dispersal and global integration has created a new strategic role for major cities. Beyond their long history as centers for international trade and banking, these cities now function in four new ways: first, as highly concentrated command points in the organization of the world economy; second, as key locations for finance and for specialized service firms, which have replaced manufacturing as the leading economic sectors; third, as sites of production, including the production of innovations, in these leading industries; and fourth, as markets for the products and innovations produced."

The study is set against several predecessors and rivals it names explicitly, positioning itself in a precise polemical field:

- **"Death of cities" / telecommunications-dissolves-agglomeration discourse.** Sassen frames her whole project as a corrective to "a dominant discourse on globalization, technology and cities which posited the end of cities as important economic units or scales," arguing instead that "the capabilities for global operation, coordination and control contained in the new information technologies and in the power of transnational corporations need to be produced" — dispersal and centralization are not opposites but joint outcomes of the same process.
- **Internationalization research centered narrowly on transnational corporations and large banks.** Sassen wants the analytic unit to include the whole apparatus of accounting, law, advertising, and finance that makes TNC coordination possible, not just the headquarters and balance sheets.
- **Weberian coordination theory and Daniel Bell's postindustrial-society thesis.** She argues explicitly that it is "necessary to go beyond the Weberian notion of coordination and Bell's (1973) notion of the postindustrial society to understand this new urban order," because both assume that as economies evolve past nineteenth-century industrial capitalism, the apex of the social order becomes "pure managerial process" indifferent to content. Against this, she insists global cities "are also particular sites of production" — not just coordination nodes.
- **The older "world city" concept** (Peter Hall 1966; John Friedmann and Goetz 1982; Friedmann and Wolff; Braudel's *supervilles*). Sassen distinguishes her term with care: "When I first chose to use global city (1984) I did so knowingly — it was an attempt to name a difference: the specificity of the global as it gets structured in the contemporary period. I did not chose the obvious alternative, world city, because it had precisely the opposite attribute: it referred to a type of city which we have seen over the centuries." On her own account "most of today's major global cities are also world cities, but there may well be some global cities today that are not world cities in the full, rich sense of that term" — Miami is her example of a city with global-city functions but no deep world-city history.

The naming choice is itself a theoretical move: it commits the study to historical specificity — a *new* kind of urban role tied to the post-1970s reorganization of finance and services — rather than to a transhistorical urban type. Sassen credits Friedmann (1986) with the "nodal points for the coordination of processes" framing but argues this is necessary and not sufficient: global cities are sites of *production*, not only of coordination — specifically the production of specialized services needed to run spatially dispersed corporate networks, and the production of financial innovations and market-making central to the internationalization of finance. "To understand the structure of a global city, we have to understand it as a place where certain kinds of work can get done, which is to say that we have to get beyond the dichotomy between manufacturing and services. The 'things' a global city makes are highly specialized services and financial goods."

A second thematic strand, developed fully in the second half of the book, concerns the social order this new economic role produces: Sassen previews that growth industries "show a greater incidence of jobs at the high- and low-paying ends of the scale than do the older industries now in decline," in contrast to the middle-income concentration of postwar manufacturing employment — the seed of the polarization thesis worked out in Chapters 8 and 9.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The study is not a single-site ethnography and makes no claim to be one. It is comparative economic sociology built from official statistics, industry and trade data, financial-market records, and a substantial secondary literature, supplemented by selective fieldwork-linked material on low-wage, informal, and immigrant labor markets that Sassen or her collaborators accessed through funded research projects and activist contacts rather than through participant observation of her own conducting.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What is the economic order of global control infrastructure? | Industries, markets, cities | New York, London, Tokyo | Comparative structural analysis of FDI, finance, producer-service location | 1970s–1990s (2nd ed.) |
| How do national urban hierarchies reconfigure? | City employment/output shares within national systems | Same triad plus secondary cities (Chicago, Osaka, Nagoya, etc.) | Location quotients; comparative urban-hierarchy analysis | 1980s–1990s |
| What is the social order this growth produces? | Occupations, earnings, urban space, informal work | Same triad | Earnings-distribution analysis; social geography; secondary case material on casualized and informal labor | 1980s–1990s |

The **three-city design is a strategic comparison, not a representative sample.** New York, London, and Tokyo are selected because each anchors a distinct national urban and financial system (the U.S. multi-city hierarchy with New York specialized in international finance and law; the United Kingdom's extreme metropolitan discontinuity around London; Japan's multipolar manufacturing weight in Osaka and Nagoya alongside Tokyo's command concentration), which lets Sassen argue that a *common structural role* recurs across dissimilar national contexts — the logic of a most-different-systems comparison rather than a controlled experiment.

The book's central analytic move, restated across chapters, is that dispersal of routine production and offices and the *concentration* of specialized control and coordination functions are not contradictory trends but joint products of the same underlying process of capital mobility — a paradox Sassen treats as the study's core empirical claim, tested against FDI flows, headquarters location, and the sectoral composition of employment in the triad cities.

**The 2001 redesign.** The second edition is, in Sassen's own description, a "slash and burn" of the first edition's 1980s-vintage empirics: she strips period-specific data and retests the 1991 framework against 1990s evidence, substantially rewrites the chapters on the international financial order, the transnational urban system, and earnings inequality, and adds a new Epilogue mapping the critical literature the first edition provoked. The redesign is explicitly framed as a **retest of the original thesis**, not an independent restudy — Sassen asks whether the 1990s "amplify" the 1980s trends once the analytic optic is in place, and answers in the affirmative throughout. Research assistance is credited for tables (Kathleen Fernicola); the Tokyo material draws on a formal collaboration (Toshio Iyotani, Asato Saito, and colleagues); the London material draws on an LSE-based team; the first edition's international data collection is credited to Zhen Wu (Columbia) and Kiriro Morita (Tokyo University).

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Three cities, deliberately parallel and deliberately different.** Sassen is careful not to claim social-morphological identity across the triad: national systems differ sharply (London's discontinuity with the rest of the UK urban hierarchy; Japan's manufacturing-heavy multipolar system; the United States' distributed multi-city hierarchy with New York carrying the specialized international functions). The comparison's force depends on showing the *same structural role* recurring despite this variation, not on showing three similar cities.

**Data character.** The empirical base is overwhelmingly quantitative and secondary: employment counts by industry and occupation; shares of finance, insurance, and real estate (FIRE) and producer-service employment; foreign direct investment stocks and flows and merger-and-acquisition activity; services-trade statistics (with an acknowledged measurement inadequacy — trade accounting was built for goods, not services); stock-market capitalization and foreign listings; assets under management; international bank rankings; foreign-exchange trading shares; office rents and international property transactions; and household and individual earnings distributions drawn from national surveys (the U.S. Current Population Survey for the earnings-percentile analysis; the UK General Household Survey and New Earnings Survey via Hamnett and Cross 1998 for London). Case material on gentrification, homelessness, and informal work draws on secondary studies rather than Sassen's own fieldwork.

**Access conditions.** The Tokyo chapters depend on Japanese academic and governmental intermediaries — Sassen notes explicitly that in Japan "many of the activities, which in the case of NY and London took place in the private sector, were handled by government agencies," which shapes what data exist and who could supply them. The London material draws on Greater London Council successor research units and LSE-affiliated collaborators. The New York material draws on long-standing U.S. urban-research infrastructure (Conservation of Human Resources Project data, Drennan's work on New York City industries). Low-wage and informal-economy material draws on projects funded to study immigrant women's employment and on contacts with organizations such as Tokyo day-laborer support groups — access channels that shape which informal-sector populations are visible in the book and which are not.

**Undercount as a built-in limitation.** Sassen states plainly that official employment statistics systematically undercount informal and casual work, and builds this limitation into the research design of Chapter 9 rather than treating it as an incidental caveat — the polarization argument is constructed to survive a known undercount of its own low end, which if anything understates the phenomenon being measured.

**Headline social finding (attributed):**

> "Today's leading sectors generate a higher share of high-income jobs and of low-wage jobs.... The overall result is an increased income polarization."

**The inequality-versus-polarization clarification (a load-bearing methodological move in the second edition):**

> "I distinguished between earnings and income inequality on the one hand and polarization on the other. I emphasized that it was not just a matter of growing inequality but also a qualitative transformation in the social forms emerging out of the increased distance between the world of work and home of the new professional strata at the top and the world of work and home of the reconstituted lower-income strata."

This distinction does real argumentative work: it lets Sassen accept the Hamnett-and-Cross finding that most London earnings groups gained in real terms — which on a simple both-ends-must-shrink definition of polarization would refute her — while still claiming polarization in her own, qualitative-transformation sense. It is the study's clearest instance of a thesis being *redefined under critique* rather than abandoned or simply reasserted.

**Informal-economy definition (the operative construct for Chapter 9):**

> "informal work … encompasses work that is basically licit but takes place outside the regulatory apparatus covering zoning, taxes, health and safety, minimum wage laws, and other types of standards."

Sassen treats informalization and casualization as **endogenous** to the advanced urban economy under this new growth complex — generated by the same high-income demand and cost pressures that produce specialized services and financial innovation — rather than as residual, backward, or purely an import carried by immigrant populations from "Third World" economies.

### Employment and earnings across the triad (Chapter 8)

The three-city comparison is built to display a *parallel* structural trend without erasing national difference. Across all three cities manufacturing employment declines (though the timing and depth vary sharply — Tokyo's manufacturing base persists far more than New York's or London's); producer services and finance grow faster than national averages in each of the three cities; finance posts the highest average salaries of any sector, with pronounced gender gaps; professional and lower-end service occupations expand at opposite ends of the distribution while middle-income manufacturing jobs contract. In New York, earnings shares in FIRE and Wall Street exceed employment shares — a gap Sassen uses explicitly to argue against employment-count-only measures of a sector's weight in a city's economy, since a relatively small workforce commands a disproportionate share of total earnings. Tokyo's measured earnings differentials are smaller than New York's or London's, but Sassen reads Japanese secondary research (e.g., Machimura's ward-level income work) as showing compatible spatial polarization tendencies, with subsequent partial reversals in some series — evidence she treats as consistent with a thesis about pressures that national institutional context can mediate rather than eliminate.

The 1990s U.S. earnings data cited in the second edition show the trend sharpening at the national scale — full-time male workers at the 90th percentile saw a 26% real earnings increase from 1979 to 1996, while workers at the 10th percentile lost 21% over the same period — and Sassen reports that the New York region's earnings dispersion, milder than the national trend in the 1980s, became sharper than the national trend in the 1990s.

### Class and spatial polarization (Chapter 9)

Four questions structure the chapter: how the leading, globally oriented sectors articulate with the rest of the urban economy; what social forms this growth promotes; how casualization and informalization fit into that growth; and how immigration recomposes urban labor markets. Sassen tests, and finds each inadequate as a full account, three inherited models of economic articulation: general development theory's assumption of progressive formal-labor incorporation; the export-base/basic-nonbasic multiplier model; and Bell's postindustrial sociology, which expects growth in high-education, equity-expanding service employment. Postwar manufacturing-led growth, on her account, expanded middle classes and formal labor markets through capital intensity, standardization, and suburbanization; the current growth complex is centered on finance and services *sold to firms*, which favors small production scales, low standardization, and casualized employment relations — a structurally different growth logic, not simply a continuation of postwar trends at a slower pace.

**Gentrification** is reconceptualized in the chapter from a house-by-house account of middle-class residential rehabilitation into a spatial component of the broader economic restructuring: as high-income professional employment concentrates, demand for labor-intensive personal and consumer services — restaurants, luxury housing maintenance, boutique retail, domestic and cleaning labor — rises *as a direct function of that growth*, generating low-wage employment alongside high-income employment rather than as an unrelated or residual sector. Downgraded manufacturing — declining unionization, sweatshop production, industrial homework — persists and in places expands within the same cities, coexisting with the high-end service economy rather than being displaced by it.

**Race and nationality** structure differential access to the new high-income occupations: Sassen reports that the minority share of New York's resident workforce rose over the study period even as underrepresentation in managerial and professional employment persisted, and that Black unemployment rates were the highest among the groups compared in her source materials. London's case centers on the salary explosion in the City around the 1986 "Big Bang" deregulation of financial markets alongside documented gentrification and inner-city deprivation; Tokyo's case centers on day-labor markets and debates over an emergent "inner city" — each city instantiating the general pattern through its own institutional and labor-market specifics.

### A new urban regime? (Chapter 10)

The book's closing theoretical claim moves to the scale of accumulation regimes:

> "the global city replaced the industrial/regional complex centered on the auto industry as the key engine for economic growth and social patterning. … It is to emphasize that a whole new arrangement has emerged for accumulation around the centrality of finance in economic growth."

Sassen is careful to distinguish the new high-income professional stratum from C. Wright Mills's power elite: it commands high income without an equivalent structure of ownership and control, yet high-income gentrification and conspicuous consumption secure a form of ideological and spatial alliance functionally comparable to elite consolidation. She frames the closing political question as one of accountability: what happens to democratic and civic accountability when leading economic sectors orient themselves to world markets and to firms rather than to individual citizens or national political constituencies, at the same moment that homelessness, poverty, benefit-less low-wage employment, sweatshops, and industrial homework are all expanding in the same cities?

### The Epilogue as authored self-historiography

The 2001 Epilogue is an unusual document for a study of this kind: Sassen herself maps six clusters of debate the first edition provoked, concedes the underdeveloped treatment of state and politics as a genuine gap, rejects what she frames as straw-man readings of the polarization thesis (a claimed prediction of universal middle-class disappearance, or a claimed prediction that all global cities converge on New York's specific morphology), and argues that the 1990s data amplify rather than complicate the 1980s trends once her analytic categories are applied. For wiki purposes this Epilogue is **primary evidence of how the author narrated her own study's afterlife** — not a neutral or independent meta-analysis of the full critical literature, and it is treated as such throughout the Critiques section below.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Several recurring rhetorical and analytic strategies carry the argument beyond what the data alone would establish.

**1. Naming as theoretical act.** Choosing "global city" over the older "world city" is presented by Sassen as a substantive move, not a stylistic preference: it commits the analysis to contemporary historical specificity — the *particular* structuring of the global in the post-1970s period — rather than to a transhistorical category that would apply equally to Renaissance Venice or imperial-era London.

**2. Production displaces power as the central question.** Reframing "who rules the city" as "what must be produced for control to be possible" opens the service complex, the labor process, and the built environment as objects of analysis in a way that pure elite-power-structure research does not — this is arguably the study's most durable methodological contribution, independent of whether its specific empirical claims hold up.

**3. Against residualizing the low end of the labor market.** Sweatshops, informal work, and low-wage personal services are explicitly linked to *growth* — to high-income consumption demand and to cost pressures generated by high-profit leading sectors — rather than treated as evidence of decline, backwardness, or immigrant "importation" of Third World economic forms. This is a deliberate reversal of development-theory expectations that Sassen states and defends directly, and it is one of the study's more theoretically aggressive claims.

**4. The three cities analyzed as a system, not merely as competitors.** New York, London, and Tokyo are treated as functionally differentiated parts of a single transnational urban system — "these cities do not simply compete with each other for the same business. There is an economic system that rests on the three distinct types of locations these cities represent" — a framing that prefigures the later network-based world-city research (GaWC and successors) even though Sassen's own method does not measure inter-city network ties directly.

**5. Retest as method.** The second edition converts the first edition's critical reception into a research design: rather than defending the original claims unmodified, Sassen restates them as a hypothesis to be tested against 1990s data, and reports (in her own account) that the retest confirms amplification of the original trends — a move that keeps the critical literature inside the book's own argument rather than leaving it as an external commentary.

**Load-bearing counter to the "death of cities" discourse, restated:**

> "my effort was to qualify what was emerging as a dominant discourse on globalization, technology and cities which posited the end of cities as important economic units or scales. … My counter argument was, and remains, that the capabilities for global operation, coordination and control contained in the new information technologies and in the power of transnational corporations need to be produced."

**The regime-scale closing image, restated for its rhetorical weight:** the passage substitutes "arrangement" for "system" and "centrality of finance" for any single named institution, giving the claim a deliberately epochal, Braudelian register that exceeds what the three-city comparative data alone could establish — the empirical chapters supply the evidence, but the closing image is argued at a higher level of abstraction than the design directly supports.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book is widely credited with helping found and organize an interdisciplinary **global-cities research program** spanning urban sociology, economic geography, planning, and political economy. Its core vocabulary — global city, producer services, command functions, transnational urban system — entered graduate training as common reference terms, cited even by scholars who reject significant parts of the substantive argument. Sassen notes that multi-country doctoral and classroom adoption was itself a stated reason for undertaking the labor of the second edition.

The study's reception crossed audiences beyond academic sociology: municipal governments and boosters used the "global city" label as a status marker in inter-city competition for investment, while labor and social-justice movements drew on the polarization thesis as a diagnosis of urban inequality — a dual uptake Sassen herself notes with some ambivalence, since the status-marker use tends to strip out the polarization argument that motivated her own analysis.

Impact on adjacent research programs runs in several directions: it pressures the older world-city literature (Hall, Friedmann) to specify what, if anything, is historically new about the contemporary period rather than resting on a transhistorical urban-hierarchy concept; it pressures Bell's postindustrial-society thesis by showing growth industries generating polarized rather than broadly upgraded occupational structures; and it supplies urban sociology a bridge into globalization debates without dissolving the city into Castells's purely relational "space of flows" — cities remain, in Sassen's framing, specific physical places with specific production requirements, not just nodes in an abstract network.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — this is not a laboratory-style replication failure but a heavily reanalyzed and contested research program, tracked here through Sassen's own 2001 mapping of the critical literature plus attributed replies; the underlying critical works themselves (Hamnett and Cross excepted, engaged directly in the text) are largely not yet ingested into this wiki and should be promoted from summary to sourced position when they are.

Sassen's own Epilogue organizes the critical reception into roughly six clusters, restated here as positions:

1. **Construct and measurement critique.** Critics point to relatively small direct employment shares in finance and to headquarters-count measures that undercut claims of concentrated command functions, and note that a majority of jobs in these cities remain domestically oriented rather than globally oriented — read by critics as evidence against, not for, a distinctively "global" city function.
2. **Digitalization critique.** If electronic trading and telecommunications genuinely dissolve the need for physical proximity, financial and service functions should disperse rather than concentrate — a direct challenge to the book's central paradox claim.
3. **Manufacturing-linkage critique.** Some critics argue producer-service growth requires co-located manufacturing to service, which would undercut claims that producer-service cities can flourish independent of local manufacturing bases — most sharply relevant to the Tokyo case, where manufacturing persistence is itself a stated point of difference from New York and London.
4. **Polarization critique — most directly engaged in the text itself.** **Hamnett and Cross (1998)**, using UK General Household Survey and New Earnings Survey data from 1979–1993, find substantial earnings-inequality growth in Britain and London but argue this does not constitute *polarization* in their preferred sense, because most earnings groups — including the bottom percentile — gained in real terms: "while there has been a growth in earnings inequality, it cannot be said that there has been an increase in social polarization because all groups have gained in real terms." This is a direct empirical challenge to Sassen's polarization language, and her published response is the inequality-versus-polarization distinction quoted above — a redefinition that concedes the raw-income finding while relocating the claim to a "qualitative transformation in social forms" that Hamnett and Cross's income-percentile method cannot directly test.
5. **State and politics critique.** Sassen concedes directly that the treatment of state regulatory action and national politics is underdeveloped relative to the economic analysis — one of the few critiques she accepts without redefining the terms of the dispute.
6. **Convergence critique.** Must all global cities become socially and spatially "like New York"? Sassen rejects this as a straw-man reading of her argument, insisting the claim is about *functional* commonality (command, coordination, production of specialized services), not morphological or social convergence — the Tokyo case is her standing counter-example, since it shares the functional role while diverging sharply in social-political mediation of its effects.

**Elliott (1999) and Drennan et al. (1996), engaged directly in the second edition,** complicate rather than simply confirm the polarization thesis from the labor-market side: using Public Use Microdata Samples from 1950–1990 across 105 U.S. metropolitan labor markets, Elliott finds that the four cities he classifies as global cities (New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, San Francisco) show consistently *smaller* low-income employment rates than metropolitan areas lower in the urban hierarchy, and that low-income employment growth was highest in production centers and lowest in resort areas and global cities — a finding that, on its face, cuts against a strong version of the global-city-disadvantages-the-bottom claim. Sassen's response engages the finding on its own terms rather than dismissing it: "compared to other cities, global city status does not necessarily represent the disadvantage for the bottom stratum that I had postulated in the first edition. It adds a broader context for interpreting what takes place in global cities... even if the distance between them and the top has increased sharply inside global cities." This is a genuine partial concession, narrowing the polarization claim from an absolute-disadvantage claim to a relative-distance claim, distinguishing composition and internal-inequality effects from level effects.

Second-generation multi-city studies extending or contesting the framework in many languages are noted by Sassen as broadly constructive rather than as refutations, though the specific monographs are not ingested in this collection and their positions cannot yet be independently verified here.

## Current Standing

The book remains a canonical reference point in urban sociology and economic geography. Its joint claim — dispersal of routine functions alongside concentration of specialized control functions — remains a default teaching device for explaining continued urban agglomeration in a globalized economy. The magnitude and inevitability of the polarization thesis, and the degree to which national and municipal political institutions mediate it, remain live and contested questions, sharpened rather than resolved by the direct Hamnett-and-Cross and Elliott exchanges recorded above. Empirical measurement in the field has since moved substantially toward firm-network and inter-city transaction data (associated with the GaWC research group and Peter Taylor's world-city-network program) that goes beyond the book's own toolkit of official statistics and industry aggregates — a methodological successor this collection has not yet ingested. This collection holds only the second edition; later Sassen work and the major named critics (Hamnett and Cross excepted) remain to be ingested before the Critiques section above can be fully promoted from author-mediated summary to independently sourced positions.

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text used for this ingest is the Princeton University Press second edition (2001; ISBN 0-691-07063-6); the first edition appeared in 1991. This ingest was made from an epub conversion of approximately 175,736 words, with the body complete through the Epilogue. A significant portion of the study's own historiography is embedded inside the book itself — Sassen's footnoted account of the early-1980s scholars who first identified globalization's urban dimension (Hall, Friedmann and Goetz, Braudel, Castells), and the Epilogue's self-authored map of the critical reception to the first edition. Because the wider critical corpus is large and multilingual, the positions reconstructed on this page are drawn substantially from Sassen's own presentation of her critics plus her attributed replies, with the exception of the Hamnett-and-Cross and Elliott/Drennan exchanges, which are engaged directly from Sassen's quoted and cited material in the second edition itself.
