# Sweetness and Power (Mintz) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]]. Source: [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985]]. Author: [[mintz-sidney-w]].

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · nine canonical body sections · attributed voice for all substantive findings.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*Sweetness and Power: The Place of Sugar in Modern History* (Viking 1985; Penguin 1986) is [[mintz-sidney-w|Sidney W. Mintz]]'s bid to make a single commodity answer a civilizational question: how did sucrose become ordinary, and what does that ordinariness reveal about power, meaning, and the making of modern persons?

Mintz frames the book as a figurative **homecoming**. Nearly his whole professional life had been spent on Caribbean tropical agricultural products and the people who produced them. Because those products were made for Europeans and North Americans, he became interested in how metropolitans became consumers. "Following production to where and when it became consumption is what I mean by coming home."

The intellectual problem is double incompleteness. A Caribbean "insider's" view of imperial networks can share the flaw of an older Europe-centered view that treats the outer world as a remote extension of Europe: "A view that excludes the linkage between metropolis and colony by choosing one perspective and ignoring the other is necessarily incomplete." The book therefore refuses both hub-only market history and rim-only plantation ethnography.

Disciplinary moment: critical-turn anthropology after the peak of timeless community studies; adjacent to [[wolf-eric-r|Eric Wolf]]'s relational world-history program ([[anthropological-political-economy]], *Europe and the People Without History*, 1982 — near-contemporary, not yet the sole source here); engaged with English social history of diet, classical political economy, and Caribbean slavery historiography (Williams, Pares, and others as secondary literature). Mintz is not writing a mode-of-production treatise; he is writing a **commodity biography** that forces meaning and political economy into the same frame.

Personal ground: from January 1948, fieldwork in a Puerto Rican south-coast municipality given over to cane for the North American market; later Jamaica (highland free-village vs plantation; Brown's Town loaf sugars) and Haiti (*rapadou*/*raspadura*). The ethnography supplies the "mystery" of white sugar in the cup versus green cane in the field — and of machetes used in Puerto Rico but manufactured in Connecticut — which the historical chapters then scale up.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How did sucrose become a mass British/European necessity, and what does that show about power and meaning? | Commodity circuit (production + consumption + meanings) | Caribbean production arc; England/Britain as principal consumption case; comparative notes (France, US) | Historical synthesis + ethnographic grounding | ~12th–20th c. for England; production geography from Old World precursors through New World plantations |
| What was the plantation as social form? | Estate/mill complex | Spanish, Portuguese, British Caribbean; 20th-c. Puerto Rico | Ethnohistory + field observation | Early 16th c.–20th c. |
| How do meanings of sugar form under class and power? | Uses, meals, rites, imagery | English/British social history; political pamphlets; budgets | Interpretive historical anthropology | 17th–20th c. climax |

**As conducted, not as advertised:** The book is *not* a year of village ethnography published as history. Mintz confesses late that "there is little [fieldwork] in this book." What is present is long-accumulated Caribbean experience used as epistemic ground and as a standing ethical/analytic orientation (look up and out from local life). The empirical spine is secondary economic and social history plus selected primary documents — duty series, planter pamphlets, Davies and Eden budgets, East India Company tea figures, Torode on jam, Boyd-Orr on diet change, and so on.

**Validity structure Mintz himself marks:**
- Modern consumption series are often "disappearance data," not measured intake.
- National per-capita averages understate class, age, and sex differentials.
- The tempo-of-work → diet link is "difficult or impossible to prove" even as he advances it.
- Britain is the worked case; other European paths (e.g., French lag) are noted, not fully paralleled.

**Method commitments that matter:**
1. Production and consumption co-determine each other ("neatly interdigitated").
2. Demand is historically produced; meaning arises from use.
3. Quantities without use-contexts, or meanings without power, are incomplete.
4. Anthropology of modern food must not drop production the way ethnography of "primitive" food never would.

**Chapter architecture as research design:** Introduction (vantage and mystery of the commodity chain) → Ch.1 Food, Sociality, and Sugar (commensality; starch-core + flavor-fringe; sweetness as predisposition and convention; English exotic→luxury→necessity sketch) → Ch.2 Production (Old World to New World geography; slavery; mill as industry; mercantilism to free trade) → Ch.3 Consumption (five uses; subtleties; tea complex; budgets; quantities; two phases) → Ch.4 Power (inside/outside meaning; extensification/intensification; free trade vs West India interest; drug-foods hypothesis) → Ch.5 Eating and Being (modern desocialized eating; HFCS; anthropology's program). The order is argumentative, not chronological only: meaning theory arrives *after* the empirical consumption series so that definitions are constrained by history.

**What the methods section omits (reconstruction):** Mintz does not publish a formal sample frame for secondary sources, does not re-estimate economic series from primary customs ledgers, and does not offer multi-sited contemporary ethnography of British consumers. The book is synthetic scholarship with ethnographic *ground*, not multi-method fieldwork. Its power is connective: making sugar's boiling-house and sugar's tea-break answerable to one another.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Production sites (ethnographic):**
- **Puerto Rico, from January 1948:** south-coast cane municipality; workers neither farmers nor peasants but propertyless laborers in "factories in the field," buying nearly everything. Harvest labor still largely manual; Mintz likens conditions to an earlier plantation era "only the sound of the whip was missing."
- **Jamaica:** highland Baptist free-village smallholding vs north-coast plantations; Brown's Town Market brown sugar "heads"/loaves for the poor.
- **Haiti:** *rapadou* (Spanish *raspadura*) log-shaped brown sugars for nearly universal poor consumption.

**Consumption "site":** England/Britain as national market and class system — royal and elite uses; middling percolation; working-class tea, treacle, bread-and-spread meals, jam after free trade.

**Data genres:** trade and duty statistics; family budgets; medical and political-economy tracts; cookery and ceremonial history ("subtleties"); literary imagery; technical history of mills and refining; illustration plates (Blake frontispiece; boiling-houses; sugar sculpture; Mexican Day of the Dead sweets) that Mintz captions as evidence of industrial character and residual ceremonial meaning.

**Sampling frame bias (reflexive):** Caribbean sugar is privileged among world sugar regions; England among European consumers; male-authored budget and political sources dominate the secondary base; women's domestic labor appears mainly through secondary social history (time, fuel, breadwinning meat bias) rather than new fieldwork.

**Production geography as "sample" of the complex:** Mintz's Ch.2 is not a single excavation but a succession narrative: cane domestication associated with New Guinea; crystallization and early industry with India; Arab expansion carrying cane and sugar-making across the Mediterranean (Sicily, Spain, Maghrib, islands); relatively brief Atlantic-island phase (Madeira, Canaries, São Tomé); New World dominance after the late sixteenth century; British Caribbean takeoff after Barbados. Each stage is evidenced by secondary synthesis rather than new archival discovery. The point is structural: the same industrial logic of land, labor, capital, and mill recurs and scales.

**Consumption series as "sample" of metropolitan demand:** Illustrative quantities Mintz reports (always through named secondary authorities) include: legal tea imports rising from about twenty thousand pounds (1700) to more than five million (1760) to more than twenty million (1800), with smuggling perhaps matching legal volumes mid-eighteenth century; Eden's 1797 southern family budget implying nearly seventeen pounds of sugar per capita — "startlingly high for the time"; national sugar on the order of 300 million pounds per year in the early 1800s rising toward a billion pounds by 1852 after duty equalization; per-capita consumption moving toward just below ninety pounds by the 1890s and above ninety in 1901; caloric contribution from roughly 2 percent to roughly 14 percent over the nineteenth century as national averages. These figures are load-bearing for the *shape* of the argument (massification, free-trade acceleration), not for clinimetric precision.

**Dual agrarian structure as ethnographic control:** Traditional brown loaf sugars in Jamaica and Haiti function as living controls — poor consumers of coarser sugars with long technical antecedents, including Indian parallels Mintz notes — against which corporate white sugar for export is seen as a different social world, not merely a different purity grade.

### Chapter-level finding chains (attributed)

**Introduction — the mystery of connection.** Mintz argues that technical transformation of substances defines much of the human relation to nature, but *division of labor across space and time* deepens mystery: makers and users unknown to each other. The Connecticut machete anecdote and the white sugar / green cane pairing are pedagogical devices for commodity fetishism without requiring Marxian vocabulary on every page. Caribbean people have been "entangled with a wider world" since 1492; sugar is the staple demand "for most epochs."

**Ch.1 — food, sociality, sweetness.** Traditional diets, Mintz argues (with Rozin flavor-principle apparatus), organize around a starch core made ingestively interesting by small flavor-fringe supplements. Modern high-flesh/fat/sugar regimes invert that pattern. Sweetness is both widespread predisposition and culturally variable practice; sucrose's English path runs exotic → luxury → mass necessity. About a century before he wrote, starch-plus-supplement diets and recurrent hunger still characterized something like 85 percent of world population in his estimate — the British modernization of diet is historically recent and exceptional in scale.

**Ch.2 — production as industry and coercion.** New World plantations from the early sixteenth century, staffed mostly with enslaved Africans, are Europe's overseas experiments. Planters and political economists understood colonial sugar as national wealth: employment of shipping, iron tools, cloth, and "infinite" materials; lower prices than import from rivals. After emancipation, Mintz recounts British West Indian strategies (indenture from India and China; constraints on land and franchise) aimed at preventing freed people from exiting sugar labor or bargaining as metropolitan proletarians could. Sugar is a demographic engine and a labor regime, not only a crop.

**Ch.3 — consumption as use-history.** The five-use schema prevents treating "sugar" as a single demand curve. Decorative subtleties and medicinal prestige matter early; mass food use is late. Tea's victory is political-economic as well as gustatory: colonial production, tax revenue, temperance as factory discipline, economy of dilution versus coffee/chocolate. Working-class substitution of sweetened tea and treacle for milk and beer is documented through Davies and Eden. Free-trade price falls (Mintz: about 30 percent 1840–1850, further 25 percent in the next two decades) enable jam as working-class necessity from about 1870 (Torode). Two phases structure the century: fringe calories with tea (c.1750–1850); deluge and core–fringe erosion (c.1850–).

**Ch.4 — power without conspiracy.** Free-trade industrial interests defeat West Indian preference; cheaper sugar meets factory rhythms already remaking work and meals. Extensification and intensification distribute meaning-making between elite models and proletarian innovation. Outside meaning includes planter ideology, crown revenue, and "civilizing" rhetoric. Drug foods cut the cost of proletarian reproduction. Sugar as "people's opiate" is symbolic success of the system, not only calories.

**Ch.5 — eating and being.** By 1900 sugar is national necessity. Modernity desocializes eating: snacks replace meal grammars; food technologists' "freedom" is narrowed choice under mass production. "We are what we eat" becomes "we are made more and more into what we eat." Anthropology must rejoin production and consumption in the study of modern everyday life or lose purpose. The first sweetened working-class tea prefigures total remaking of economy, self, and commodity meaning.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### Homecoming and dual vantage

Mintz opens by refusing romantic isolation of the field site. The load-bearing sentence is methodological and moral at once:

> Following production to where and when it became consumption is what I mean by coming home.

And:

> A view that excludes the linkage between metropolis and colony by choosing one perspective and ignoring the other is necessarily incomplete.

**Analysis:** The rhetoric converts career narrative into method. "Homecoming" is not nostalgia; it is a claim that the anthropologist of the colony owes an account of the metropolis as consumer. This move founds a generation of commodity studies that treat Nestlé, tea, cotton, or cocaine with the same double optic.

### Factory in the field

> They were wage earners who lived like factory workers, who worked in factories in the field, and just about everything they needed and used they bought from stores.

**Analysis:** The phrase collapses the agriculture/industry binary that still organized much mid-century anthropology (peasants vs proletarians; rural vs urban). Puerto Rican cane workers become the ethnographic proof that plantation agriculture *is* industrial organization — then the historical chapters extend the claim backward to slave mills. Illustration captions of boiling-houses restate "time-conscious, disciplined, industrial character."

### Meaning pair and process pair

Mintz's Ch.4 definitions are the conceptual heart. **Intensification** is emulative/ceremonial percolation (wedding cakes). **Extensification** is mass familiarization plus working-class innovation "at a great remove from the practices of the privileged." **Outside meaning** is what consumption does for rulers — including "pacific domination" through control of foods *and* their connotations.

**Analysis:** The pairs let Mintz hold agency and structure without conspiracy theory. Workers invent meanings; blocs set prices, duties, and availability; both matter. The rhetoric is careful: "There was no conspiracy at work to wreck the nutrition of the British working class…"

### Drug-foods hypothesis

> …the hypothesis offered here is that sugar and other drug foods, by provisioning, sating—and, indeed, drugging—farm and factory workers, sharply reduced the overall cost of creating and reproducing the metropolitan proletariat.

**Analysis:** This is the book's sharpest political-economic claim. It revalues the plantation not only as capital sink/source or market for manufactures but as **wage-goods infrastructure** for industrial Britain. "Drugging" is deliberate provocation — Mintz couples it to calories, habit, and time-saving, not only pharmacology. The hypothesis articulates [[labor-power]] reproduction with colonial agro-industry.

### Closing: commodity and person

> Tobacco, sugar, and tea were the first objects within capitalism that conveyed with their use the complex idea that one could become different by consuming differently.

And the final chord:

> The first sweetened cup of hot tea to be drunk by an English worker was a significant historical event, because it prefigured the transformation of an entire society… What commodities are, and what commodities mean, would thereafter be forever different. And for that same reason, what persons are, and what being a person means, changed accordingly. In understanding the relationship between commodity and person, we unearth anew the history of ourselves.

**Analysis:** Mintz ends as neither pure materialist nor pure culturalist. Identity-through-consumption is historically specific to capitalism's mass luxuries-turned-necessities. The "minor matter" of sugar is allowed, via Ortiz's "favored child of capitalism," to stand for a civilizational transition — a rhetorical escalation that made the book a syllabus staple and a target for specialists who want more numbers and less epitome.

### Anti-grammar of the modern meal

Ch.5 attacks linguistic analogies that treat meals as grammars. Social eating's constraints (shared time, shared dishes, ceremonial invariants) are real; food technologists' ideal of absolute individual preference desocializes ingestion. Mintz sides with Fischler's worry about "gastro-anomie" without abandoning political economy: desocialization is profitable.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Founding genre:** historical anthropology of food / commodity biography. Later sugar, tea, coffee, chocolate, and processed-food histories routinely cite Mintz as charter.
- **Vocabulary uptake:** inside/outside meaning; extensification/intensification; drug foods; factory in the field; production–consumption rejoining as disciplinary program.
- **Bridge position:** readable by historians, anthropologists, and food-studies scholars; sits between [[anthropological-political-economy]] and cultural analysis of everyday life.
- **Pedagogy:** ubiquitous in anthropology of food, Atlantic history, and globalization courses.
- **Adjacent programs:** [[world-systems-analysis]] (core–periphery commodities); Appadurai-era social life of things (not yet ingested here); later fair-trade and critical nutrition studies.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — historical synthesis, not an experimental finding.

**Positions in the afterlife** (as typically framed; full secondary map awaits further ingest):

1. **British exceptionalism / case limits.** The England path is worked densely; French lag and other European paths are sketches. Specialists on other empires revise the consumption chronology without discarding the method. Mintz himself signals the limit when he notes that European sugar consumption was never tied solely to the Caribbean and that worldwide rise continues "without regard to where the sugar comes from."
2. **Gender and domestic labor.** Women's wage work, cooking time, fuel, and intra-household allocation of meat vs sugar appear via secondary sources; feminist food studies have densified what Mintz opened — who sweetens tea, who forgoes meat, who makes jam, who staffs confectionery labor.
3. **Biology vs culture.** Mintz holds sweet preference *and* historical construction; biological anthropologists and pure constructionists pull opposite directions. The book refuses a single reduction: "This idea has little to do with nutrition or primates or sweet tooths, and less than it appears to have with symbols" when he discusses becoming different by consuming differently — a sentence that can be over-read as anti-biology; in context it targets *identity-through-commodities*, not the existence of taste predispositions treated in Ch.1.
4. **Causal weight of drug foods.** Economic historians may re-estimate how much sucrose calories mattered relative to potatoes, wheat prices, or real wages; the hypothesis remains generative. Mintz's own caution that the work-tempo argument is hard to prove is often under-cited by enthusiasts.
5. **Slavery–capitalism link.** Mintz engages Williams-era literature and planter political economy; the wider debate over capitalism and slavery continues independently with new cliometric and archival programs. This hub records Mintz's use, not a wiki-voice verdict on Williams.
6. **Presentism in Ch.5.** The modern-food chapter is essayistic and dated to 1980s concerns (HFCS, snacking, Fischler, Tiger); its program (rejoin production and consumption; resist romantic primitivism in anthropology) outlasts its examples.
7. **Secondary-source dependence.** Production and quantity chapters stand or fall with the authorities Mintz synthesizes. Later revision of a Murphy harvest series or a Burnett budget does not kill the method, but it can revise particular claimed rates of change — hence attributed voice for quantities on the summary page.

**What has *not* happened:** a Mead–Freeman-style restudy that collapses the core claim that British mass sugar consumption was historically produced through colonial production and class-differentiated meaning. Critique has been refinement and extension, not overturning.

Nothing in the primary text is treated here as a failed finding that must be demoted from the study page; critiques refine scope and mechanisms.

## Current Standing

Still the default anthropological citation on sugar and modernity. Concepts remain in active use. The book is stronger as a **method charter** and **interpretive synthesis** than as a final statistical economic history of British diet — a division of labor later specialists have filled. For this wiki, it is the primary densification source for Mintz until *Worker in the Cane* and *Caribbean Transformations* are ingested.

**What survives as research program:**
- Join production and consumption across colony and metropolis.
- Treat food meanings as historically acquired under power (inside/outside; extensification/intensification).
- Read plantations as industrial labor regimes, not only as "tropical agriculture."
- Treat mass luxuries-turned-necessities (drug foods) as infrastructure of industrial personhood.
- Study modern everyday ingestion without dropping political economy or fieldwork strengths.

**What is more dated:** 1980s HFCS examples; some absolute consumption figures pending specialist revision; thin non-British comparison; incomplete gender analysis by later standards.

**Pedagogical standing:** The tea-cup closing remains the most assigned paragraph in food anthropology; the risk is that students remember the epitome and skip the five-use schema, the duty politics, and Mintz's own uncertainty flags.

### Counterfactual significance (brief)

Without *Sweetness and Power*, food anthropology would still have Lévi-Straussian meal structures, ecological calorie studies, and culinary ethnography — but the **default bridge** from plantation slavery to supermarket sucrose would be thinner and later. Wolf's 1982 global ethnohistory might still have carried relational political economy; Mintz specialized that program into edible commodities and everyday meaning. The book's counterfactual weight is genre creation more than a single statistic.

### Reflexivity of the research act

Mintz writes as a white North American man trained in the Steward project, professionally located at Yale then Johns Hopkins, funded by NEH and Wenner-Gren, researching in former plantation societies still organized by sugar capital. He does not center his own positionality in the 1980s reflexive style of *Writing Culture*, but the Introduction's "look up and out from local life" is an ethics of location: refuse to leave the metropolis unexamined. The confession that the book contains little fieldwork is rare honesty; it also marks a limit — meanings of British working-class sugar use are reconstructed from historians, not from Mintz's own contemporary British ethnography. Production-side empathy is thicker than consumption-side ethnography of English tea tables.

### How the book persuades

1. **Career authority:** decades of Caribbean work authorize the homecoming frame.
2. **Anecdote → structure:** machete, *raspadura*, almshouse tea become synecdoches for global circuits.
3. **Definition after evidence:** theoretical pairs arrive in Ch.4 after the reader has lived through uses and quantities.
4. **Anti-conspiracy clause:** disarms reduction to intentional poisoning of the poor while keeping profit struggle central.
5. **Epitomizing close:** Ortiz + tea cup convert a commodity history into a theory of modern personhood — high rhetorical risk, high pedagogical yield.
