# The Anti-Politics Machine (Ferguson, 1990) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]. Reciprocal path link from summary: `[[hubs/studies/ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study|…]]`.

**Analysis standard:** design-summary table · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife as positions · nine canonical sections. Findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

James Ferguson’s *[[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-1990|The Anti-Politics Machine]]* (Cambridge 1990; UMP reprint 1994) asks what the international **"development" apparatus** *does* in Lesotho — not whether development ideology is true or false, and not how to design better projects. The Preface states the dual question structure clearly: a full **genealogy** of how “development” became a central modern value is deferred; the book prioritizes **effects** — how the problematic works in practice in one setting (Lesotho, 1975–84).

Ferguson positions the study against two literatures he finds inadequate. **Liberal / policy** writers treat rural development as a technical-collective enterprise to be improved (Myrdal, Hirschman, some applied anthropologists; Robertson’s planning-as-mankind’s-enterprise). **Neo-Marxist / dependency** writers treat projects as instruments of capital or imperialism that “really” expand capitalism or serve Northern interests. Ferguson argues (attributed) that Lesotho projects characteristically transform *neither* poverty *nor* production relations in the ways either camp assumes; reading them as the historical expansion of capitalism is a mistake for this case. Interests matter, but events are not simple expressions of known interests. Official discourse is not mere mystification: bureaucrats are shaped by a field of acceptable statements.

The constructive model is **Foucauldian**: planned interventions can produce unintended outcomes that crystallize into authorless “strategies” — subjectless constellations of control. Foucault’s prison genealogy (*Discipline and Punish*) supplies the template: the prison “fails” to reform yet succeeds at producing a usable, supervised delinquency. Ferguson’s cardinal principle: **intentional plans are always important, but never in quite the way the planners imagined.** The resultant constellation in Lesotho he names the [[anti-politics-machine|anti-politics machine]].

Disciplinary moment: Critical Turn anthropology; Harvard dissertation (1985) under Sally Falk Moore; southern African labor-reserve political economy (Murray, Spiegel, van Onselen as interlocutors); anthropology of planned development (Brokensha lineage). Adjacent to but not identical with later [[post-development]] (Escobar 1995): shared Foucauldian tools, different political horizon (effects analysis vs. unmaking development as language).

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Lesotho as national setting (as Ferguson states for the period):** small land-locked kingdom surrounded by South Africa; independent 1966 (former Basutoland); ~1.3 million people; ~30,000 km²; ~10% arable land; overwhelmingly rural population concentrated in western lowlands; crops maize/wheat/sorghum; livestock cattle/sheep/goats; primary income for many households migrant wages in South Africa (up to ~200,000 Basotho employed as migrants, GOL/World Bank figures as he cites). Extremely dense multi-donor “development assistance” presence 1975–84 relative to size.

**Thaba-Tseka Project:** mountain-area integrated rural development; joint FAO/World Bank Cooperative Programme mission November 1974 with CIDA; Memorandum of Understanding Lesotho–Canada 8 May 1975; Phase One ~$15 million (CIDA ~$6m, World Bank ~$5.5m, ODM ~$1.5m, GOL ~$2.4m) centered on ~53,000 ha around Thaba-Tseka (then in Maseru district). Livestock/range commercialization, road, regional centre, crops, village development. Phase Two redesign (late 1970s) toward district-scale “integration,” merger with ministry structures, creation of Thaba-Tseka as tenth district. Project “ended of sorts” 1984; 1982 plan revision de-emphasized livestock and abandoned model-district ambitions.

**Mashai / rural sample:** household survey N=73 (1982–83); comparison with Colin Murray’s Ha Molapo material; focus on absentees, migrant laborers, present men (often infirm, laid-off, or visiting miners); agriculture providing roughly one-third of subsistence even in “good” years (as Ferguson reports). Livestock sales at Thaba-Tseka; auction surveys (e.g. ~85% of cattle sold for food need); 1983 drought observations (~40% cattle loss concurrent with *falling* sales relative to 1982).

**Documentary corpus:** World Bank 1975 Country Report (Ch. 2 object); project plans of operations, quarterly reports, organizational charts (multiple competing charts — “era of the two charts”); GOL statistics; secondary southern African historiography.

**Access and positionality:** White American Ph.D. researcher with university affiliation and project-level access; dependent on chiefs, project officers, and local hosts; writing from “academic discourse” that he himself marks as constructed (Ch. 2). The book is revision of a 1985 Harvard dissertation, not contemporaneous “rapid appraisal.”

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### Vivisection, not critique

Ferguson inoculates against the “gotcha” reading:

> To say that “development” is a dominant problematic is, of course, not to suggest that everyone holds the same beliefs about it… If “development” is today from time to time challenged, it is still almost always challenged in the name of “real development.”

And:

> There will doubtless be for some a temptation to read this argument as a critique of “development” ideology, an attempt to refute the “development” picture of Lesotho by showing that it is false… the main thrust of this study is not to show that the “development” problematic is wrong, but to show that the institutionalized production of certain kinds of ideas about Lesotho has important effects…

This is a rhetorical and methodological move: by demoting truth-value as the central question, he makes **effects** the scientific object — “more closely resembles vivisection than critique.” The risk is that readers still recruit the book as a refutation manual; the Epilogue’s refusal of expert prescriptions tries to hold the line.

### Prison → development

Ch.1 and Ch.9 quote Foucault at length on the prison’s “failure” that is success at producing delinquency. Ferguson’s transfer:

> If it is true that “failure” is the norm for development projects in Lesotho, and that important political effects may be realized almost invisibly alongside with that “failure,” then there may be some justification for beginning to speak of a kind of logic or intelligibility… “side effects” may be better seen as “instrument-effects” (Foucault 1979); effects that are at one and the same time instruments of what “turns out” to be an exercise of power.

Then the machine metaphor:

> In this perspective, the “development” apparatus in Lesotho is not a machine for eliminating poverty that is incidentally involved with the state bureaucracy; it is a machine for reinforcing and expanding the exercise of bureaucratic state power, which incidentally takes “poverty” as its point of entry…

> The “instrument-effect,” then, is two-fold: alongside the institutional effect of expanding bureaucratic state power is the conceptual or ideological effect of depoliticizing both poverty and the state… the anti-politics machine.

The SF “anti-gravity machine” analogy makes the political operation vivid: suspending politics from even sensitive political operations under cover of a neutral technical mission “to which no one can object.”

### Quotation marks around “development”

A sustained orthographic politics: “development” in scare quotes throughout, announced in the note on usage, trains the reader to treat the term as problematic object rather than shared goal.

### Ch.2 as sustained close reading

The World Bank chapter is almost a set piece of discourse vivisection: statistics that must exist; remittances minimized; egalitarian peasantry invented from stratified wage access; trade figures rejected when they contradict agricultural-export hopes. The rhetoric is forensic and often sarcastic — effective for teaching, open to charges of piling on a single report.

### Four features of Lesotho-as-LDC (Ch. 2)

Ferguson argues development discourse tends to cast Lesotho as a species of the genus LDC under four headings (attributed):

1. **Aboriginal economy** — “virtually untouched by modern economic development”; poverty explained as primordial backwardness not yet brought into the modern world; “subsistence” contrasted with “modern cash economy” even though almost nobody can subsist on what they grow. Field anecdote: an EEC expert told Ferguson Basotho were reluctant to sell cattle because they did not yet trust paper money.
2. **Peasant society** — egalitarian agricultural producers whose main problem is technical/agricultural, not wage dependence or stratification by mine access.
3. **National economy** — industry, infrastructure, and growth framed within Lesotho’s borders; South Africa treated as accidental neighbor with “ties,” while remittances and migrant employment are minimized or buried.
4. **Governmentality** — the economy imagined as reflex of Lesotho (or colonial) policy and of “development” schemes; problems become points of insertion for agencies (“sectors”), not descriptions of an already regional political economy.

Against this, Ferguson’s academic counter-picture (drawing Ashton, *Encyclopaedia Britannica* 1910, Murray, Wallman, Thompson, etc.): Basutoland/Lesotho had long been a commercial agricultural exporter and then a labor reserve; by independence a cash-crop and labor-export society, not an untouched peasantry. The real twentieth-century transformation was from agricultural-export cash economy to labor-export cash economy — not from non-market subsistence to cash.

### Institutional apparatus (Ch. 3) — design logic

Phase One (from 1975 MoU) concentrated on road access, a regional centre at Thaba-Tseka, livestock/range commercialization (including planned grazing blocks on tens of thousands of hectares), crop switches toward cash crops, and village development. Phase Two “integration” expanded the project toward a mini-state footprint: multiple divisions, district creation, merger with ministry lines, District Development structures. Ferguson’s target-society critique: the design exaggerated peasant isolation and individual farm decision-making, invented technical problems that matched available packages, and treated politics as outside the project’s proper domain.

### Deployment failures that matter (Chs. 6–8) — as encounters, not scorecards

**Livestock:** Five CIDA commercialization conditions and range-block design presupposed legal and social power the project lacked. Grazing association experiments met fence-cutting, arson, court loss (unregistered association without Form C). Mandatory culling against 2–3× carrying-capacity estimates was politically impossible. Auctions existed but commercialized little of the Mystique structure; improved stock/fodder found zero adopters in Mashai by end-1983. Two structural contradictions: target population organized by Mystique + migration; project as instrument of GOL as well as of “development.”

**Decentralization:** Label promised devolution; practice produced centralizing effects — District Coordinator as “District Czar,” ministry budget lines that kept power in Maseru ministries, removal of a Mosotho Acting Director (Jan 1981) in favor of arrangements that kept the programme foreign/non-threatening, health-division vehicle conflicts and National Party use of clinics/ambulance. TTCC dissolved; Director resigned Dec 1981; 1982 revision abandoned model-district ambitions. Ferguson’s “era of the two charts” and bread-crumb/ants-nest metaphors capture organizational dual reality versus plan diagrams.

**Crops and other programs:** Cash-crop switch (maize→wheat/peas) depended on road-as-market fantasy; road also functioned as import channel. Woodlots, land politics, pony incidents, VDC politics, and “popular participation” / DDC collapse show political struggle over land and party structures under technical labels.

### Side effects as state-building (Ch. 9 opening)

Before naming instrument-effects, Ferguson inventories institutional and security side effects of the project era: expanded district administration, roads and services that increase state reach, police/military capacity in the mountains, party structures using project organs. These are not accidental extras but the empirical half of the two-fold effect.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Anthropology of development:** The book became a charter case for studying projects, experts, and encounters as ethnographic objects rather than as policy problems to fix.
- **Concept travel:** [[anti-politics-machine]], [[depoliticization]], [[instrument-effects]] entered critical development studies, political ecology, and policy critique vocabularies — often detached from Lesotho specifics.
- **[[post-development]] adjacency:** Escobar (1995) lists Ferguson among fellow anthropologists of development; the Theory Hub already notes shared object, different punchline (effects vs. unmaking).
- **Political anthropology:** Decentralization-as-centralization and technical cover for state expansion feed postcolonial state ethnography.
- **Economic anthropology:** Bovine Mystique remains a standard counter to “cattle complex” / irrationality explanations and to pure utilitarian commercialization models.
- **Foucault in US anthropology:** One of the cleaner ethnographic operationalizations of apparatus + instrument-effects outside pure cultural studies.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

Positions (not adjudicated):

1. **Case overstretch.** Ferguson himself flags Lesotho’s peculiarities and leaves global generalization to others. Critics who treat “the anti-politics machine” as a universal law of aid ignore that caveat; admirers who only cite Ch.9 miss the empirical burden of Chs. 2–8.
2. **Discourse vs. materialist critique.** Materialist critics of post-development (see position 3 on [[post-development-debate]]) may fold Ferguson into “ideas first” analysis. His reply is already in the book: he rejects pure interest-imputation *and* pure ideology-critique; instrument-effects are structural outcomes, not mental errors.
3. **Conspiracy reading.** He explicitly denies conspiracy (“it really does just happen to be the way things work out”) while explaining replication of failure. Readers who hear functionalism anyway are a documented reception risk.
4. **Agency and resistance.** The book records fence-cutting, office-burning, women’s commercial counter-arguments, party capture, woodlot conflicts — but the machine metaphor can still be read as totalizing. Epilogue’s “They are doing it!” tries to restore popular tactics.
5. **Gender and later labor markets.** Bovine Mystique’s gendered property analysis is strong for 1982–83; later mine layoffs, HIV, and cash-transfer eras (later Ferguson works) may restructure the barrier — not tested in this volume.
6. **Replication status:** `not-applicable` as singular historical case. Later project ethnographies (Mosse, Li, etc.) are not yet ingested here as replications or challenges.

## Current Standing

Still a **teaching classic** and citation engine for anti-politics / depoliticization talk. Empirical claims about Thaba-Tseka and 1980s Lesotho stand as attributed historical ethnography. Theoretical claims about development-as-apparatus are active and contested. Ferguson's later work (*Expectations of Modernity*, *Global Shadows*, *Give a Man a Fish*) repositions him toward modernity, inequality, and distributive politics — not yet in this vault; do not back-project those frames onto 1990 without those sources.

Within this collection, the book densifies the anthropology-of-development node that [[escobar-encountering-development-study|Escobar 1995]] left as a cited neighbor: where Escobar offers a global genealogy of development discourse and a postdevelopment political horizon, Ferguson offers a single-country **vivisection of effects** — how LDC representation, project design, livestock and decentralization encounters, and state expansion cohere as an [[anti-politics-machine]]. Both are required for a non-thin vault coverage of critical development anthropology; neither substitutes for the other.
