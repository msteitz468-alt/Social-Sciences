# Changes in Bodily Form of Descendants of Immigrants — Study Analysis

> **Summary page:** [[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study]] · **Source:**
> [[boas-race-language-culture-1940]] (essay reprint, pp. 60–85) · **Author:**
> [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]

This is the study that let anthropology contest race science with race science's own
instrument. Its interest is threefold: the design (a natural experiment engineered from a
government commission), the rhetoric (a scrupulous refusal to overclaim that has been read
in opposite ways), and an afterlife almost unique among century-old datasets — a 2002
reanalysis of Boas's own numbers that reopened the case and drew a rebuttal within a year.

## Question and Intellectual Context

The **cephalic index** was the load-bearing number of turn-of-the-century physical
anthropology. Deniker, Ripley, and the broader European race-classification tradition
treated the ratio of skull breadth to length as a near-immutable hereditary marker that
sorted humanity into stable "races and sub-races." On that assumption rested both a
science (typological racial classification) and a politics (immigration restriction and
eugenics). The **United States Immigration Commission** — the Dillingham Commission,
convened 1907 amid pressure to restrict southern and eastern European immigration —
entrusted Boas with the anthropometric investigation of immigrants and their descendants.
The commission wanted to know what kind of stock the new immigration represented. Boas
turned the assignment into a test of the premise underneath the question: *does bodily
type reproduce unchanged across the ocean?*

Boas came to it already committed to the environmentalist program he would consolidate in
*[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* (1911). But this was not
armchair argument: it was a commissioned measurement campaign with a sample in the
thousands, and its target was the single trait his opponents held most fixed. See
[[scientific-racism]], [[race]], [[anthropometry]].

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The design's elegance is the **parent–child comparison**. By measuring European-born
immigrant parents against their own US-born children, Boas approximately held hereditary
stock constant and let the American environment be the one thing that varied between the
generations. This is a natural experiment: the "treatment" is being born and raised in the
United States rather than the old country, and the control for ancestry is built into the
family structure of the sample.

- **Sample.** Analyzed by immigrant category rather than pooled — Bohemians (n≈170–416),
  East European Jews ("Hebrews," n≈515–654), Sicilians (n≈188–338), Neapolitans
  (n≈248–367), with additional groups. Keeping the groups separate is what made the
  decisive comparison possible (see below).
- **Measurements.** Head length and breadth and the derived cephalic index; face width;
  stature; with supplementary data on pubertal stage, hair pigmentation, and infant
  swaddling.
- **Statistical apparatus.** Beyond the raw parent–child contrast, Boas ran a
  **time-since-immigration** breakdown (children born <10 vs. >10 years after the mother's
  arrival) and a **regression / "similarity"** analysis partitioning trait variation. He
  reported error margins and used them to rebut the "inadequate sample" objection.

What the methods section understates, by the standards of a modern study, is the
measurement-error structure of a multi-observer field campaign and the messiness of
"immigrant category" as a variable. Boas was alert to the first (he noted that measuring
and recording were done by different people, blunting expectation bias) and exploited the
second rather than hiding it.

## The Sample and Data

The design table that carries the argument is the matrix of increase/decrease in the
American-born generation versus the immigrant parents. Its decisive feature is
**directionality**: East European types (Hebrews, Bohemians) show the cephalic index
*falling* (heads more elongated) in the US-born generation, while South European types
(Sicilians, Neapolitans) show it *rising* (heads more rounded). Representative figures
Boas reported: Hebrew index down ≈1.6–2.0 units; Sicilian index up ≈1.3–1.8. The
time-graded signal — index difference of −0.83 for children born within ten years of the
mother's arrival versus −1.92 for those born later — is the environmental fingerprint Boas
leaned on hardest.

Corroborating data included a separate **Armenian** study (US/European-born Armenians'
heads 6 mm longer and 4 mm narrower than Asia-born Armenians, against a background of
traditional cradle head-shaping), a **Puerto Rico** survey (an observed mean index too
high to be reconciled with any plausible admixture of the long-headed Spanish, African,
and short-headed Taíno sources — "we are compelled to assume" a physiological
modification), and Hrdlička's finding of narrowed face width even in fourth-generation
Americans.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence | Boas's reading | Later status |
|---|---|---|---|
| US-born cranial measures differ from immigrant parents' | Parent–child matrix, all groups | Type is not fixed | Direction accepted; magnitude contested |
| Hebrew index ↓, Sicilian index ↑ (opposite directions) | Group means | Rules out a single stature artifact | Key rebuttal to Auerbach |
| Effect grows with time since immigration | Cohort split (−0.83 vs −1.92) | Environmental, cumulative | Cited as the environmental signature |
| Face width narrows with US residence | Group means; Hrdlička 4th-gen | Cumulative environment | — |
| Pigmentation shows **no** plasticity | Hair-color data | Explicit limit on the claim | Boas's own scope restriction |
| Children of dissimilar parents are more variable | Variability table | Regression to *either* parent, not mid-parent | — |

The conceptual yield is Boas's **genetic type vs. physiological type (ecotype)**
distinction: "the same genetic type may occur in various physiologically conditioned
forms," the physiological differences being of the same magnitude as those used to
distinguish European "races." Crucially he did **not** claim the germ plasm had changed,
and he speculated the same people returned to the old environment might revert.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The study persuades by a rhetoric of restraint. Boas's most-quoted line here is a refusal:
"I find myself unable to give an explanation … all I try to do is prove that certain
explanations are impossible." He chose the deliberately non-committal words "instability
or plasticity of types" to avoid endorsing any one mechanism (selection, growth change, or
hereditary change). He conceded to Backman that rural-to-urban transition might be a
contributory cause and that his own abstract had flagged it. And he closed by *defending*
anthropometry rather than burying it — the method's value, he said, lay precisely in
detecting "the effect of social and geographical environment upon man."

This scrupulousness is why the study has been read in opposite directions. To Boas's
followers it is a model of not overclaiming; to later critics the cautious hedges left the
"plasticity" headline resting on less than the textbook summary implied.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The immediate reception split along the lines of the [[race-and-culture-debate]]:
environmentalists took it as decisive against fixed-type doctrine, race scientists
resisted. Over the following decades it became the standard citation for the claim that
"racial type" is not a stable hereditary essence, feeding the mid-century collapse of
typological race science and the [[nature-nurture-debate]]. It also modeled the tactic
Boas used throughout — beating race science on its own quantitative ground rather than only
on moral terms.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

Contemporary objections were many and Boas answered them in print:

- **Sample inadequate** (Sergi, Fehlinger) — met with error-margin analysis.
- **Observer bias** — met by noting observers did not know what to expect, that measuring
  and recording were separated, and that results ran in *opposite* directions across
  groups.
- **Stature–index artifact** (Auerbach, Schiff) — met by the opposite-direction finding
  (Hebrew length up while width down; Italians the reverse), inconsistent with a single
  correlation artifact.
- **Cradling/swaddling** — met by the Sicilian case (change without swaddling difference)
  and Bohemian face-width change among children who themselves immigrated.
- **Unfalsifiable subtype fitting** (Sergi) — met by the argument that with enough
  postulated subtypes any distribution fits, so the maneuver proves nothing.

The consequential afterlife came decades later. **Corey Sparks and Richard Jantz (2002,
*PNAS*)** digitized and reanalyzed Boas's original data with modern statistics and argued
the environmental effect on cranial form was small and that heritability dominated — a
direct challenge to the received reading. **Clarence Gravlee, H. Russell Bernard, and
William Leonard (2003, *American Anthropologist*)** re-examined both the data and the
reanalysis and defended Boas, arguing Sparks and Jantz had mis-specified the comparison
and understated real environmental effects. The exchange has not been resolved; it is now
itself a teaching case in the reanalysis of historical datasets. The study's
`replication_status` is accordingly **reanalyzed-contested**.

## Current Standing

The weak claim — that head form shows *some* environmental influence, and that the
cephalic index is no stable racial marker — is secure and mainstream. The strong claim —
the magnitude and interpretive weight the textbook "plasticity" story places on Boas's
data — is genuinely open after 2002–2003. That a century-old commissioned survey can still
be reanalyzed into live controversy is the clearest measure of how much intellectual
weight was placed on it.

## Historiography and Sources

The essay circulates most widely in its condensed form in *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|
Race, Language and Culture]]* (1940), pp. 60–85, which combines the 1912 immigrant report
with the 1916 "New Evidence" and 1913 "Influence of Heredity and Environment upon Growth"
papers; the full data lived in the 1912 Columbia University Press report to the Immigration
Commission. The modern controversy is documented in the Sparks–Jantz and Gravlee–Bernard–
Leonard papers, which must be read for the afterlife — the 1940 reprint predates and
contains none of it. For the author's wider program see [[hubs/thinkers/boas-franz|the Boas
Thinkers Hub]].
