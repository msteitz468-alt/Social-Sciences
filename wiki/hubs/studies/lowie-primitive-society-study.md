# Primitive Society (Lowie) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[lowie-primitive-society-study]]. Reciprocal hub path: `[[hubs/studies/lowie-primitive-society-study]]`.

**Primary base:** [[lowie-robert-h|Robert H. Lowie]], *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* (Boni and Liveright, 1920; Liveright Black and Gold Edition, 1947, with new Preface and Corrections and Addenda). Ingested from image-only PDF via OCR (~155k words). All findings and theoretical claims below are **Lowie's (or receptions he reports)** — attributed. The work is a **comparative synthesis of the world ethnographic literature**, not a single-site ethnography; Crow material is one authoritative stream among many, and the book's own opening chapter states the method problem directly: social organization's topics — family, clubs, property, kinship — are "so closely interrelated that none of them can be treated as the basic one from which all others are logically deducible."

## Question and Intellectual Context

Lowie's problem was organizational and polemical at once. By 1920, Boasian anthropology had crystallized its critique of Victorian **unilinear evolution** — analogous, he said in the 1947 Preface, to experimental biologists' protest against extravagant species-development speculation. [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]]'s *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]* (1877) still systematically embodied the older view that social forms march through fixed stages (promiscuity → group marriage → gens democracy → property/state). Lowie set out to replace that ladder for **social organization** — marriage, family, kinship usages, the [[sib]], women's status, property, associations, rank, government, justice — with a historically minded survey of ethnographic diversity.

Disciplinary moment: fieldwork-revolution American anthropology. [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s 1896 Limitations essay and *Mind of Primitive Man* had already attacked stage ranking and race–culture equations; students were flooding the literature with monographs. Heinrich Schurtz's *Altersklassen und Männerbünde* (1902) had shown that age- and sex-based **associations** — clubs, grades, men's houses — play a role earlier writers, concentrated on family and clan, had neglected. Lowie explicitly credits Schurtz with a founding move: "it is to the late Dr. Schurtz that we are indebted for a systematic treatise on all associations... to Schurtz above all others belongs the glory of having saved ethnologists from absorption in the sib organization." Lowie aimed to do justice to **both** Morgan's kinship range and Schurtz's associational range, under a Boasian methodological rule: the ethnologist **must be a historian**; uniformity of culture history cannot be assumed because convenient; diffusion at every level interferes with sequences "inherent in the nature of society."

The book opens, characteristically, not with a thesis statement but with a confession of method: because family, clubs, and property "are so closely interrelated that none of them can be treated as the basic one," Lowie warns "however we commence, there must be constant anticipation and constant cross-referencing, for by the sheer necessities of exposition we are driven to examine fragment after fragment of an organic whole." That warning is itself a quiet rebuke of Morgan's confident architecture — where Morgan built a single ascending structure, Lowie announces from page one that his subject resists any single starting point, "any starting-point will do."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Comparative Scope | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What units actually compose "primitive" society? | Family, sib, association, rank stratum, territorial/political group | Global ethnographic literature, weighted toward North America (Crow, Hidatsa, Pueblo, Plains, Northwest Coast, California, Arctic), with recurrent Australian, Oceanic, African, and Siberian cases | Comparative survey by institution, chapter by chapter | Pre-1920 published ethnography; 1920 synthesis; 1947 corrections |
| Do fixed stage-laws govern these units? | Sequences claimed by Morgan / Schurtz / evolutionists | Side-by-side peoples of similar technological "level" (e.g. Australia vs. Paviotso; Uganda vs. Hawaii) | Distribution, diffusion, limited independent development | Same |
| What is the relation of kinship to property, associations, rank, and justice? | Jural and political institutions | Regional case chapters (Andaman Islands, Banks Islands, Ifugao, Masai, Plains, Polynesia, Melanesia) | Intensive case sketch + cross-case comparison | Same |

**As conducted, not as advertised.** The book is not neutral handbook prose. It is a **persistent critique of Morgan** organized as a chapter-by-chapter demolition of stage packages, interleaved with positive distributional findings. Method commitments (Introduction, restated formally in the Conclusion): reject Pearson-style demand that history become science only when facts fall into formulae; reject the assumption that man developed the same course everywhere; ascertain the course civilization **actually** followed; treat diffusion as a *vera causa* without making it the sole cause; allow independent invention and limited parallelism without "laws regulating the independent reproduction of the same series of stages" (formally abjured in the Conclusion). Lowie is explicit that this is a discrimination, not a wholesale rejection of pattern: he distinguishes sharply "between the theory of independent development, which I have again and again advocated, and a belief in laws regulating the independent reproduction of the same series of stages which I now at the close of my investigation formally abjure."

The Marriage chapter that opens the substantive argument enacts the same method in miniature. Rather than start from a definition of the family and deduce marriage rules from it, Lowie starts from the empirical universality of the incest taboo and works outward: "Within the narrowest family circle sexual relations are universally tabooed. There are no tribes which countenance the mating of parent and child," with the rare royal exceptions (ancient Egypt, Peru) explained not as primitiveness but as "excessive sophistication" among ranked elites who ran out of sufficiently high-status partners — a small but telling inversion of the evolutionary assumption that incest tolerance marks an earlier, cruder stage.

**Evidence class:** ethnographic secondary literature (and Lowie's own fieldwork, chiefly among the Crow, as one source among many). Characteristic failure modes apply: uneven regional coverage, colonial and missionary production of much of the underlying source material, OCR noise in this ingest, and ethnography whose descriptive present is now a century or more stale. Lowie's strength is **comparative architecture and diffusion reasoning**, not primary field reportage for every case he cites.

## The Site / Sample / Data

There is no single site. The "sample" is the world ethnographic library available to an AMNH/Columbia-trained anthropologist c. 1920, with North America (especially Crow, Hidatsa, Pueblo, Plains, Northwest Coast, California, Arctic) heavily weighted; Oceania, Australia, Africa, Siberia also recur throughout. The Sib chapter's working definition shows the comparative method at its most careful: a sib is "most briefly defined as a unilateral kinship group," distinguished from the bilateral family by tracing descent through one parent only — "if a tribe is organized into mother-sibs, every child regardless of sex is considered a member of its mother's sib... if the tribe is organized into father-sibs, every child follows the father's sib." Lowie builds the concept from a deliberately homely analogy — an imagined "Smith sib" of everyone sharing a patronymic — precisely to strip the concept of exotic mystique before applying it comparatively across cases.

The Conclusion's **Mountain Crow life-course** (Strikes-three-men of the Thick-lodge sib, son of Bad-leggings, member successively of the Fox and then Lumpwood societies, initiate of the Tobacco order) is the book's pedagogical type case — a concrete "mottled" affiliation map, not a claim that Crow equals all primitive society. Lowie is careful to generalize outward from it immediately: "the sibless Andamans... status as determined by dietary restrictions... A Banks Islander would be found to belong at once to a sib, a grade in the club, and half a dozen Ghost societies. Among the Vedda territorial grouping would figure prominently, and in Polynesia distinctions of caste would come to the foreground." The multiplicity thesis is thus deliberately cross-checked against several unrelated regions before Lowie will generalize it.

Production context: museum-era salvage and reservation ethnography; comparative reading of missionary, colonial, and scientific reports. Power relations that shaped those underlying sources are not systematically theorized in 1920 terms — a reflexivity gap for the modern reader, not a reason to discard the comparative argument. The associations chapter is explicit that source quality, not just theory, drove Lowie's case selection: he tells readers he is "guided partly by the quality of available literature, partly by the desirability of presenting at least all the main varieties of associational units" — an admission that the sample is a literature-availability sample, not a designed one.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding (Lowie, attributed) | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Primitive society is **mottled diversity**, not dull uniformity of a single sib pattern | Whole-book survey; Crow multi-affiliation illustration; cross-check against Andaman, Banks Island, Vedda, Polynesian cases | **Absorbed** as anti-stage common sense in social anthropology |
| **Bilateral individual family** is universal (present); only escape is flight to remote past | Kin terms both sides; customary law; economic spouse partnership | Broadly retained; form varies; "universal family" debates continue in other registers |
| **Sib** (unilateral unit) has restricted though wide distribution; **family prior** to sib | Sibless simpler cultures (Swanton on North America; global survey); Morgan sampling bias | Absorbed; clan not universal stage |
| No world-historical **group marriage** / strict promiscuity stage in recent millennia | Critique of Morgan's Hawaiian/consanguine terminology packages; Rivers on kinship terminology; cases of sexual license distinguished from group marriage | Stage claim **overturned** as world history; residual debates on specific cases |
| No true **matriarchate**; matriliny ≠ female political dominance; mother-sib not necessary prior to father-sib | Position of Women + History of the Sib chapters | Absorbed |
| **Associations** (age societies, clubs, secret societies) are first-class units, not derivative of sib organization | Schurtz's program extended and corrected via case chapters (Andaman, Australia, Masai, Banks, Pueblo, Crow, Hidatsa) | Absorbed; Schurtz's own unilinear association sequence rejected by Lowie |
| **Incorporeal property** (songs, magical formulas, narratives, carvings) is widely and firmly owned even where material goods are held loosely | Andaman song-copyright; Koryak incantation sale; Kai poem and carving ownership | Absorbed against crude "primitive communism" |
| Rank, government, and justice exist in "rude" cultures; kin and territory coexist as bases of order | Rank, Government, Justice chapters; Maine engaged throughout | Absorbed; later Africanist/political anthropology dense |
| **Diffusion** lays the axe to fixed historical laws; stage sequences become a scientifically worthless "unknowable noumenon" | Conclusion method argument; Omaha sib transmission case; Chinese adoption of Western governmental notions | Core particularist claim; later neo-evolutionists reopened limited sequences without the Victorian ladder |
| Cultural and social complexity provide **no objective criterion of progress**; sociological features are "incommensurable" across societies | Comparison of Andaman bachelor/spinster segregation, Masai age-grading, and modern "cumbersome" order-keeping machinery | Attributed methodological stance; echoed in later cultural relativism, contested by neo-evolutionists on different grounds |
| Civilization is a planless **shreds and patches**, a "hodgepodge" awaiting rational design, not a completed ladder-climb | Conclusion peroration; 1947 clarification | Phrase famous; often misread as pure atomism |

### Load-bearing passage — mottled diversity

> "Primitive society wears a character rather different from that popularized by Morgan's school. Instead of dull uniformity, there is mottled diversity; instead of the single sib pattern multiplied in fulsome profusion we detect a variety of social units, now associated with the sib, now taking its place."

Rhetoric: **visual antonyms** (dull/mottled; single pattern/variety) rebrand the entire comparative field in two sentences. The Crow biography that follows is not decoration — it is the operational definition of mottled diversity (sib + family + band + friendship + graded society + ceremonial order + household), and Lowie immediately generalizes it to the Andaman, Banks Island, Vedda, and Polynesian cases so the reader cannot dismiss Crow as an idiosyncratic outlier.

### Load-bearing passage — the sib defined against Morgan's terminology

> "The sib ('clan' of British anthropologists) is most briefly defined as a unilateral kinship group. The family is bilateral... The sib traces kinship through either parent to the total neglect of the other."

By building the sib concept from a deliberately mundane analogy — English surnames, an imagined "Smith sib" — Lowie strips the term of the exotic, evolution-implying aura it carried in Morgan's *systems of consanguinity* framework, reducing it to a portable comparative tool rather than a rung on a civilizational ladder.

### Load-bearing passage — associations as first-class units

> "Primitive society as pictured by Morgan and his followers is an atomistic aggregate. The tribe consists of units fashioned on a single pattern, the sib concept... In other words, if Morgan were right, individuals in lower cultures would differ from one another socially only as members of this or that sib."

This is Lowie's clearest single statement of what he takes to be Morgan's central error: not merely wrong dates or wrong sequences, but a picture of "primitive" social structure as monotonously single-institution, against which the whole book's cumulative case-by-case demonstration of multiple, cross-cutting affiliations (family, sib, association, rank, band) is aimed.

### Load-bearing passage — abjuring stage-laws

Lowie discriminates independent development (which he advocates) from "laws regulating the independent reproduction of the same series of stages," which he **formally abjures**. Parallelism is of strictly limited scope; diffusion makes fixed sequences an "unknowable noumenon" that is "scientifically worthless." Methodologically this is the Boasian particularist knife: allow recurrence without ladder. He drives the diffusion point home with a linguistic analogy borrowed from his own introductory chapter: when Shoshoni and Greek independently evolve a grammatical dual number, "this is the result of similar classificatory processes, but what is the general import of the isolated resemblance? Precisely nil."

### Load-bearing passage — no objective criterion for social progress

> "The appraisal of sociological features is wholly different from that of technological features of culture. The latter may be rated according to the closeness with which they accomplish known ends; the former have unknown ends or ends whose value is a matter of philosophic doubt, hence they can be graded only on subjective grounds and must scientifically be treated as incommensurable."

This is Lowie's most explicitly relativist methodological sentence, distinguishing technological progress (objectively gradable by efficiency) from social-organizational "progress" (not gradable at all without smuggling in a philosophical value judgment) — the move that licenses his refusal, throughout the book, to rank kinship or political systems on a civilizational scale even where he is willing to note that they differ in complexity.

### Load-bearing passage — mock-law of social evolution

> "Cultures develop mainly through the borrowings due to chance contact. … Hence the specious plea that a given people must pass through such or such a stage in our history before attaining this or that destination can no longer be sustained. … we can act unfettered by the pusillanimous fear of transgressing a mock-law of social evolution."

Political edge: anti-colonial developmental sequencing as well as anti-Morgan science. The "shreds and patches" close converts the same point into cultural self-critique of the West:

> "To that planless hodgepodge, that thing of shreds and patches called civilization, its historian can no longer yield superstitious reverence. He will realize better than others the obstacles to infusing design into the amorphous product; but in thought at least he will not grovel before it in fatalistic acquiescence but dream of a rational scheme to supplant the chaotic jumble."

The line is a **double-edged closer**: it denies Western civilization any privileged endpoint on Morgan's ladder while still leaving room — pointedly, in the closing clause — for deliberate, non-teleological social reform. It is not, on Lowie's own 1947 clarification, a claim that culture is a mere pile of unrelated atoms without any coherence; it is a claim that whatever coherence civilization has was not manufactured by any law of progress and is therefore open to conscious redesign.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Handbook form, polemic spine.** Chapter titles (Marriage, The Family, Kinship Usages, The Sib, History of the Sib, The Position of Women, Incorporeal Property, Associations, Age-Classes, Government, Justice) look like a textbook table of contents; each chapter nonetheless ends by undercutting a stage package Morgan or Schurtz had proposed for exactly that institution.
2. **Schurtz against Morgan, then Schurtz against Schurtz.** Associations rescue non-sib structure from Morgan's single-pattern picture; Schurtz's own sex-psychology and unilinear club-development sequence are then rejected in turn via diffusion reasoning and counterexamples (women's societies, purchase-based rank grades).
3. **Crow as pedagogical hologram.** One life-course (Strikes-three-men) carries the multi-unit thesis better than a table of contents, and Lowie immediately triangulates it against Andaman, Banks Island, Vedda, and Polynesian cases so no single ethnographic stream can be dismissed as anomalous.
4. **The homely analogy as a de-exoticizing device.** The "Smith sib" (English surnames) and the linguistics parallel (Shoshoni/Greek grammatical dual) both work the same way: take a technical anthropological concept, restate it in a familiar Western register, and thereby strip it of the aura of exotic primitiveness that let Morgan treat it as evidence of an earlier evolutionary stage.
5. **Incommensurability pairs.** Australia vs. Paviotso (same "Neolithic" technological level, wholly different social organization); Uganda vs. Hawaii (roughly equivalent technologically, incommensurable politically and legally) — peoples of like "level" that cannot be ordered on one line.
6. **Phrase-making.** "Mottled diversity," "shreds and patches," "peacock theory of primitive man," "mock-law of social evolution" — memetic payloads engineered for particularism, designed to be quotable and to displace Morgan's own vivid stage-names (savagery, barbarism, civilization) from the discipline's working vocabulary.
7. **1947 paratext.** Reception history (Rivers, Kroeber, Sapir, Malinowski) and clarifications become part of the work's meaning for later readers of this edition, and the Corrections and Addenda quietly update terminology (e.g. noting that most anthropologists had by then reverted from "sib" to "clan") without retracting the substantive argument.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Sapir (1920):** first major-scope public work to break definitely with classical evolutionism; clearest expression of the present temper of American anthropology.
- **Rivers:** valuable record; not diffusionist enough.
- **Kroeber:** honesty over Morgan's brilliant illusions; comparative sterility / negativism.
- **Malinowski and international readers:** eulogistic notes; influence beyond the US (Lowie 1947).
- **Morgan admirers:** hot denunciation.
- **Disciplinary role:** charter statement of American social-organization particularism; feeds [[historical-particularism]]; anchors a position on the unilinear-evolution debate over Morgan's stage scheme; standard foil for later neo-evolutionism and for structural-functional kinship studies (which rejected conjectural history for different reasons than Lowie's).

The book's afterlife is inseparable from the **Lowie–Radcliffe-Brown exchange** over kinship classification and method: Radcliffe-Brown pressed for a systematic, structurally comparative kinship anthropology that treated terminologies as expressions of jural and structural principle, where Lowie's comparative practice stayed closer to trait-by-trait distributional history and remained wary of over-systematized structural laws — a continuation, in a new register, of the same suspicion of premature system-building that drove the anti-Morgan argument. The 1947 Preface's own listing of Rivers, Kroeber, Sapir, and Malinowski as respondents is itself part of this afterlife: Lowie curates his own reception into the reissued text, so the 1947 edition doubles as primary source and as historiography of the book's first quarter-century.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Claim (strongest form) |
|---|---|
| **Lowie 1920/1947** | Stage-laws fail; family universal and prior; sib secondary; associations first-class; diffusion plus limited independent development explains resemblance; no objective progress ranking exists for social forms |
| **Rivers** | More diffusionist reconstruction needed than Lowie allows |
| **Kroeber** | Method honest but under-produces broader conclusions; comparative program risks sterility |
| **Radcliffe-Brown (attributed, from later structural-functional kinship studies)** | Kinship terminology and structure require systematic comparative-structural analysis, not only trait-distribution history; the Lowie–Radcliffe-Brown exchange is the discipline's standard reference point for this methodological divide |
| **Neo-evolutionists (White, Steward, and successors, attributed)** | Boasian particularism, of which this book is a charter statement, threw out recurrent developmental pathways along with the discredited Victorian stage-ladder; multilinear evolution later reopened the question of limited, non-teleological sequences that Lowie had formally abjured |
| **Marxist/Engelsian reception (attributed)** | Engels's *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* (1884) had already built directly on Morgan's stage scheme for its own political ends; Lowie's demolition of that scheme is, from this position, read either as an attack on a politically load-bearing framework or as an opportunity for later Marxist anthropology to rebuild materialist social evolution on firmer comparative footing |
| **Structural-functionalists (attributed)** | Present-function analysis of a single society, not comparative historical reconstruction across many, is the proper anthropological program — a different anti-Morgan path than Lowie's, arrived at by rejecting conjectural history altogether rather than by refuting it case by case |
| **Misreaders of "shreds and patches" (attributed)** | Read the closing line as asserting that culture is only unrelated trait atoms; Lowie's own 1947 clarification denies pure atomism while still denying that civilization possesses the organic, law-governed unity Morgan's scheme required |

`replication_status: not-applicable` — synthetic comparative argument, not a single experimental or ethnographic claim series subject to replication in the ordinary sense.

Lowie extended and partly revised his own comparative-historical method seventeen years later in *The History of Ethnological Theory* (1937), which places *Primitive Society* itself as a document in the disciplinary history it narrates — the polemicist becomes, in his own later book, one more position on the map of evolutionist and anti-evolutionist argument.

## Current Standing

What survives: multi-unit social organization as the default expectation rather than the exception; rejection of Morgan's single world ladder; family/clan (sib) non-identity; associations and rank as normal features of non-state and early-state societies; incorporeal property as a real and widely distributed category; political and jural institutions in "simple" societies; diffusion as a standing threat to any fixed stage scheme; and the methodological caution that social-organizational complexity is not the same axis as technological complexity. What is dated: much of the underlying ethnographic detail; some historical reconstructions of sib origins; the vocabulary itself ("primitive," "sib" versus the "clan" the discipline reverted to); thin treatment of the colonial and missionary production conditions of the sources; pre-structuralist kinship formalism later reworked by Radcliffe-Brown's school. Read as the **American charter** of anti-stage social organization, alongside Boas's race–culture program and the later British structural-functional monographs as an alternative, non-comparative-historical path out of Morgan.

## Historiography and Sources

Primary: 1920 body; 1947 Preface and Corrections and Addenda (this ingest), the latter of which records both terminological drift within the discipline (e.g. the reversion from "sib" to "clan") and Lowie's own later second thoughts on specific cases (clan origins, Pueblo clan groupings, Nama Hottentot social organization). Lowie's *History of Ethnological Theory* (1937) and his Crow monographs are not yet ingested — expand reception and fieldwork base when they are.
