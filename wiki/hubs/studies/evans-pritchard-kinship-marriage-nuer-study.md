# Kinship and Marriage among the Nuer (Evans-Pritchard 1951) — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub. Summary: [[evans-pritchard-kinship-marriage-nuer-study]]. All findings **attributed**. Documentation moment: **1930s** Nuerland, not the ethnographic present.

This is the **domestic middle volume** of the Nuer trilogy. *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|The Nuer]]* (1940) built ordered anarchy and segmentary politics while largely bracketing marriage and the village kinship mesh; *Kinship and Marriage* (1951) fills that mesh and makes the classic case for [[ghost-marriage]], Nuer [[levirate]], and cattle [[marriage-payments|bridewealth]]. *Nuer Religion* (1956) later densifies ghosts and spirits that already haunt this book’s marriage rites.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*(Attributed — Preface.)* Evans-Pritchard dedicated the book to [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] and framed it as the second part of a general sociological account begun in *The Nuer*. He had omitted marriage, family, and kinship there because he believed political structure was better inquired into apart from other social relations. The thesis that opens the volume: the network of kinship ties within any Nuer community can **ultimately be reduced to a series of marriage unions**; marriage and the family are the fundamental institutions from which kinship categories derive.

Fieldwork conditions remain those of *The Nuer*: short total time, hostility, reconstruction from testimony. Evans-Pritchard is frank that some forms (frequency of ghost-marriage, some rites) rest on generalization from cases and Nuer statements.

## The Site / Sample / Data

Nuerland of the 1930s — Lou, Leek, eastern Jikany and western groups as contrast sets. Local communities are wet-season **villages** (*cieng*) and dry-season **camps** (*wec*), built of **homesteads** (*gol*: byre + huts). Dominant-clan lineages provide structural spines; many attached lines enter through women, affinity, adoption, and concubinage. Genealogical charts deliberately simplify ghost-marriages as simple legal marriages for readability while EP flags the distortion.

## Findings in Detail

### Ch.I — Local community

*(Attributed.)* Village = corporate political unit *and* dense kinship network: every person can trace *mar* to every other. **Lineage relations** (groups of agnates; *buth* between collateral lineages) ≠ **kinship relations** (person-to-person *mar*). *Mar* becomes *buth* as agnates segment over generations (“Then kinship ceased and there remained only a lineage relationship”). Small lineages exist beyond the dominant clans of *The Nuer*. Case studies (Konye, Nyueny, Yakwac, Kurmayom) show agnatic nuclei with female “points of graft,” matrilocal sons-in-law, Dinka absorption, and natural vs legal kinship from non-simple unions.

### Ch.II — Prohibitions and incest

*(Attributed.)* Wide range: clansfolk, cognates (~six generations in some lines), natural/adoptive kin, certain affines, age-set “daughters.” Spear-names and *cike diel* logic; mother’s clan vs maximal lineage asymmetries. Genitor/pater and captive-Dinka rules create marriage bans via bridewealth debts. Sanctions for *rual* escalate (sausage fruit / sleeping-skin → livestock + leopard-skin chief); children aggravate incest. EP’s analytic order: marriage prohibitions first; incest secondary; wide bans maintain lineage distinctness **and** the local kinship mesh that knits the political structure.

### Ch.III — Marriage

*(Attributed.)*

**Courtship.** Wide premarital sexual freedom oriented toward marriage and children; regional variation (freest Eastern Jikany). Initiation (~14–16) then courtship rivals cattle as male interest.

**Rites.** Dual institution of **cattle + ceremonies** (*larcieng*, *ngut*, *mut*; eastern legal threshold often *mut*). Marriage incomplete until **birth of a child**.

**Bridewealth.** Historical ideal **~40** head; usual **20–30** after rinderpest and colonial constraint on raiding; cannot fall below **~16** basic claims. Family may receive only a minority of total under scarcity (Lou 10/23; Dok 8/22 cases). Functions: temporary scaffolding of non-kin into *ruagh* then *mar*; attaches children as “children of the cattle” so **pater** = name in which cattle paid; lineage exchange of daughters for wives. Bloodwealth parallels (ideal 40; half may marry a wife to the dead).

**Union typology.**

| Form | Legal structure (EP) |
|---|---|
| Simple legal marriage | Cattle + rites; incomplete till child; standard |
| Polygynous complex | Several simple legal marriages, one husband |
| **[[ghost-marriage]]** | Pro-husband marries **in dead man’s name**; ghost = legal husband; *ciekjooka* / *gaatjooka* |
| **[[levirate]]** | Widow remains dead husband’s wife; brother is pro-husband into **existing** family; refuse *kwen* “to marry” |
| Widow-concubinage | Widow with non-kin lover (*ciek ma lak*); original legal family persists |
| Unmarried concubinage | No cattle “on her back” (*ciek ma keagh*) |
| Woman-marriage | Woman as **pater** of wife’s children; rites as male marriage |

Ghost-marriages “must be almost as numerous as simple legal marriages.” Levirate ≠ widow inheritance as new marriage.

### Ch.IV–V — Family and kinship

*(Attributed.)* Dual pull: legal/religious norms toward father’s lineage vs personal affection toward mother’s people. Stereotypes: “wicked” *gwanlen* (FB) vs “good” *nar* (MB). Categories: “bulls” (*tut*) vs *gaatnyiet* (children of girls). Kinship system is an **elastic band** stretched as contacts widen. Age-set supplies parallel *mar rica*. Kinship and political systems are interconsistent but not identical: neighbourhood is lived through kinship categories; lineage system anchors floating domestic process. Supremacy of agnatic principle **enables** family-form variants, Dinka adoption, and easy translation of natural ties into legal ones.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Trilogy division of labor** — politics / kinship / religion as separable abstractions that must recombine.
2. **Simple legal marriage as standard** — other forms approximated to it analytically.
3. **Pater vs genitor** — cattle name vs physiological begetting.
4. **Cattle as jural language** — bridewealth, bloodwealth, and ghost cattle as one system.
5. **Case charts** — villages as proofs that kinship, not pure lineage co-residence, builds community.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Canonical for African marriage ethnography and for teaching ghost-marriage and true levirate. Underwrote mid-century unilineal descent models; later critique of lineage theory re-reads Nuer domestic flux as evidence against over-corporate models ([[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]). ASKAM 1950 chapter is a partial preview of Ch.I without full marriage exposition.
