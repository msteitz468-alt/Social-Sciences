## Question and Intellectual Context

*Middletown* (1929) answers a methodological and political problem of the 1920s United States: how to know a modern industrial community as a **whole**, when social research habitually attacked "problems" (wages, working girls, coal miners) or national aggregates, and when anthropology still meant, for most readers, "curious information about savages." Clark Wissler's Foreword states the wager explicitly: communities are objective realities; anthropology takes the community or tribe as unit; the Lynds attempt "to study ourselves as through the eye of an outsider" — "an experiment not only in method, but in a new field, the social anthropology of contemporary life."

[[lynd-robert-s|Robert S. Lynd]] and [[lynd-helen-merrell|Helen Merrell Lynd]] open Chapter I by refusing both the typical-city fiction and the thesis-driven reform tract:

> "A typical city, strictly speaking, does not exist, but the city studied was selected as having many features common to a wide group of communities. Neither field work nor report has attempted to prove any thesis; the aim has been, rather, to record observed phenomena, thereby raising questions and suggesting possible fresh points of departure in the study of group behavior."

Against piecemeal social-problem attack they cite the emerging recognition that civilization is a continuum. Their positive program is a **total-situation** study of interwoven trends, ordered by six "main-trunk activities" adapted from anthropological culture schemes (they note kinship with Rivers' sixfold groupings and Wissler's culture scheme): getting a living; making a home; training the young; using leisure; religious practices; community activities. The diachronic lever is **1890** — chosen for data availability and because natural gas (struck late 1886) and the subsequent industrial boom mark a break from the quieter county-seat past — against the fieldwork present of **1924–25**.

Institutionally the project sits outside the University of Chicago departmental machine that produced *The Polish Peasant* and *The City*. Funding came from the **Institute of Social and Religious Research**; counsel included Wissler, Ogburn, L. C. Marshall, Lawrence K. Frank, Joseph Chassell, Gardner Murphy. Field staff: Faith Moors Williams (statistics, cost of living, working-class income), Dorothea Davis, Frances Flournoy. The city was not consulted about the project and contributed nothing to its cost. That outsider sponsorship is part of the knowledge's production context: a national research institute studying a Midwestern town that becomes, under a pseudonym, "America."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do interwoven life-activities form the culture of one small American city? | Whole community as system of six activity domains | Mid-sized East-North-Central industrial city (~35k 1920; ~38k 1924 estimate); pseudonym Middletown | Anthropological total-situation + mixed methods | Field Jan 1924–June 1925; baseline 1890 |
| How does cultural change since the gas/industrial boom reorganize classed life-chances? | Business class vs working class as master cleavage | Gainfully employed dichotomized (people- vs things-addressed work); intensive family samples 124 WC + 40 BC | Comparison by class across domains | Same |
| What do people do, not what should they do? | Observed behavior + folk talk as mood evidence | Participation, documents, stats, interviews, questionnaires | Descriptive; no better/worse verdict | Same |

### Selection and the typicality trap

The Note on Method and Chapter II reconstruct selection as a **two-criterion** problem: (1) as representative as possible of contemporary American life; (2) compact and homogeneous enough for total-situation study. Representativeness desiderata included temperate climate, rapid growth, diversified industry (not one-industry), substantial local artistic life, Middle West preference, absence of "outstanding local peculiarities." Homogeneity criteria explicitly included **small Negro and foreign-born populations** so that the staff could concentrate on cultural change rather than "two major variables, racial change and cultural change." Population band 25,000–50,000 (143 Census 1920 candidates); self-contained, not a satellite.

The authors then undercut their own representativeness rhetoric:

> "no claim is made that it is a 'typical' city, and the findings of this study can, naturally, only with caution be applied to other cities or to American life in general."

That sentence is load-bearing for all later "Middletown = America" uses. The design *wanted* common features; it *disclaimed* typicality. Reception often dropped the disclaimer.

### Class as instrument, not discovery alone

Tripartite upper/middle/lower was considered and rejected: vocational stratification fit a culture dominated by getting a living; almost no local "upper class" existed under traditional labels (perhaps eight or nine households). The operational cut:

> "Members of the first group, by and large, address their activities in getting their living primarily to *things*… while the members of the second group address their activities predominantly to *people*."

**Working class** = things (making/handling materials); **business class** = people (selling, promotion, management, services, ideas) — and professionals are folded into business class because business interests "tone" them. Reported 1920-frame magnitudes: roughly **71% working class / 29% business class** among the gainfully employed. The Note on Method is candid that even major life-activities could not be described for "the city as a unit," so the dual class scheme became the principal internal differentiation — "not a complete structural diagram," but believed to indicate outstanding characteristics and regions of movement. Whenever "Middletown" is written as a unit, that is "merely a shorthand symbol" masking individuals.

### Field practice (Note on Method)

Staff opened an office unheralded, presenting themselves as studying "the growth of the city." The Lynds and Flournoy lived in Middletown **January 1924–June 1925**; Davis about a year; Williams five months. No fixed schedules at the outset — six activities first, then schedules framed from observed behavior. Scope narrowed to **1890 and 1924** rather than every intervening year, "not to reveal contrasts but a clearer understanding of past behavior as conditioning present behavior."

Techniques listed:

1. **Participation in local life** — apartments or rooms in private households; dinners alternating manufacturer and laborer; churches, schools, courts, rallies, clubs, card parties; inconspicuous notes then standardized write-ups; "bug on a pin" reduced; interplay of participation and detachment "not at all points… successfully overcome."
2. **Documents** — census, city/county/court/school records; two leading dailies read in detail for 1890–91; current papers clipped for a year and a half; minutes of Board of Education, missionary societies, Ministerial Association, Woman's Club, Library Board, etc.; school exam questions nineties vs today; two detailed diaries 1886–1900 (merchant churchman; young Catholic baker); booster books, directories, surveys.
3. **Compiled statistics** — wages, employment steadiness, accidents, residence near plant, promotion, club and church membership, library circulation, movie attendance, auto ownership, etc.
4. **Interviews** — from street-car chat to four-hour scheduled interviews with six leading Protestant ministers and Y secretaries after months of contact.
5. **Family schedules** — 124 clearly working-class and 40 clearly business-class families; requirements: native-born white Americans, city limits, both parents living together, one or more children ages 6–18; interviews with wives (occasionally husband present). WC nucleus from payrolls of three leading glass/metal plants in three sections; 2–3 hours; completed in over four of five eligible homes where wife was in; nearly all daytime (under-represents employed wives). BC sample **not** as representative of all business-class levels — willing cooperators, appointments, deliberately a prosperous-influential contrast group "direction of change for the rest of the city"; four very wealthy manufacturers purposely omitted.
6. **Questionnaires** — high school upper years (~700–800); **Negro students' answers excluded from tabulations by design** — a major scope limit for race analysis.

Population figure **38,000 for 1924** is the authors' estimate; they reject a higher Census intercensal figure as prolonging a misleading 1910–20 growth rate including suburbs.

### What the design omits or underweights

- **Race** is present in the text (segregation, Klan, Jewish "belonging") but was **designed down** as a variable by city selection and by white-only intensive samples.
- **Men's private speech** is often second-hand (wives, public meetings).
- **Business-class financial intimacy** is limited by reticence the authors themselves note.
- **Politics as power structure** appears in Chapter XXIV but does not organize the book the way class-and-culture does.
- **Anonymity** ("goldfish" privacy in a city of thirty-odd thousand) later collapsed into public identification as Muncie — changing how readers map claims to a real political economy.

## The Site / Sample / Data

[[middletown|Middletown]] in the mid-1920s was an industrial East-North-Central city of roughly thirty-five to thirty-eight thousand, with a pioneer and **gas-boom** past (gas struck late 1886; industrial foundation after gas fades). County seat from 1885. The Lynds sketch a nineteenth-century courthouse-square culture jolted into machine production, retail expansion, and booster civic ideology ("Magic Middletown").

Data classes are plural and unequal:

| Evidence class | Carrier in *Middletown* | Strength | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Observational / participatory | Staff living in town 18 months | Texture of meetings, services, folk talk | Reactivity; selection of attended events |
| Documentary / archival | Papers 1890 vs 1920s; minutes; diaries | Diachronic leverage | Survivorship; booster voice |
| Statistical / institutional | Census, payrolls, tax, membership counts | Magnitudes (cars, turnout, incomes) | Operationalization; OCR-era reprint noise for later readers |
| Interview / schedule | 124 WC + 40 BC wives; ministers | Classed household practices | White nuclear-family filter; BC non-random |
| Questionnaire | HS true-false and activity items | Youth attitudes at scale | Suggestive not proof (authors' note); Negro answers dropped |

The city as documented is not "the Midwest" and not "1920s America"; it is one research construct built under Institute sponsorship with a two-class lens.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

### 1. Anthropology at home

The opening method paragraphs recruit Goldenweiser and the Arunta to license studying corporations, prayer meetings, and Congress as variations on few major activities. Rhetoric: if anthropology can objectify the tribe, it can objectify us. The six-trunk table of contents *is* the argument that Middletown is a culture, not a list of problems.

### 2. Refusal of verdict

Chapter XXIX:

> "Consideration of Middletown in terms of 'good' or 'bad,' 'better' or 'worse,' is, obviously, irrelevant to the purpose of the study… the attempt to reveal interrelations in the maze of interlocked, often contradictory, institutional habits… has led to few general conclusions save as to the inchoate condition of this one small modern community and the extent and complexity of the task confronting social science."

This is both scientific ethos and a shield against reform-society capture. It also leaves readers free to moralize — which popular reception did.

### 3. Escalator and whipstock

The Conclusion's governing image:

> "A citizen has one foot on the relatively solid ground of established institutional habits and the other fast to an escalator erratically moving in several directions at a bewildering variety of speeds."

Paired with Kroeber on the Greek alphabet and the first Packard body delivered with a **whipstock** on the dashboard — invention as twist-and-stretch of the old. Social change ≠ progress; back-eddies (costly home laundry machines undoing centralized laundry drift) count. The Lynds thus smuggle a theory of **uneven institutional tempo** without a formal lag law à la Ogburn (who is thanked in the Preface).

### 4. Class comparisons as drumbeat

Almost every chapter repeats the business/working comparison: rising hour, bathroom, car vs mortgage, college plans, club membership, church ardency, movie attendance. The rhetoric produces a two-nation city inside one "Middletown." That is persuasive and is also how race and ethnicity get sidelined — they appear, but not as the drumbeat.

### 5. Folk talk as evidence of mood, not proof

Chapter I warns that folk talk is not "scientifically valid evidence" but affords "indispensable insights into the moods and habits of thought." The book nonetheless fills pages with quoted boosters, workers, ministers, and Klan speakers. The reader is invited to hear the culture; the authors retain a residual positivist disclaimer.

### 6. Soft numbers and hard scenes

Auto counts and Community Chest tallies sit beside the woman who presses the interviewer to join the Klan against the "Catholic situation," and the capital-city Klan orator on blood mixture and "Christian America." The study persuades as **text** — scene plus table — not as a single regression.

## Domain-by-Domain Texture (as conducted)

What the six trunks actually delivered, beyond the abstract scheme:

**Getting a living.** The longest and densest empirical block. Who earns (sex/age composition; married women's paid work; youth preferred in machine shops; old workers' vulnerability); what people do across hundreds of specialized occupations; the "long arm" into waking hour, night shift, layoff, accident, and promotion odds; the open-shop weakening of unions; advertising and installment credit as engines of the "slope of wants." Money is not only income — it is the language in which housing, marriage timing, and respectability are negotiated.

**Making a home.** Housing stock and utilities as a visible class map; marriage as near-universal destiny with rising divorce and a romance ideology that coexists with thin companionship; child-rearing as transfer of children to extra-home youth culture (high school, car, movies); food and clothing commercialized (bakery bread diffusing working → business class); housework electrified unevenly while the "sacred home" remains a moral slogan.

**Training the young.** Compulsion and enrollment explosion; curriculum shifting toward vocational and civic stamp; teaching force largely young unmarried women, modest credentials, low civic rank; "school life" — basketball cosmos, clubs, fraternities, status criteria — as the real secondary socialization for many adolescents. College aspiration classed.

**Using leisure.** Traditional oratory and serious reading decline relative to passive entertainments; the automobile (6,221 passenger cars by end of 1923 — one per 6.1 persons) competes with bathtubs and mortgages in family budgets; movies run from afternoon to night with attendance multiples of population across the year; radio enters the parlor; clubs multiply (hundreds) while neighborhood and church as leisure bases thin; Rotary-type civic clubs replace older literary-ethical societies as business-class male fellowship.

**Religious practices.** Doctrinal Christian items still widely affirmed on high-school true-false instruments; working-class verbal ardency versus business-class cooler participation; dozens of buildings and denominations; ministers salaried specialists whose prestige trails manufacturers; Sunday morning service competes with the motor outing; national mission budgets can dwarf local charity.

**Community activities.** Government as two-party spoils with collapsed turnout versus 1890s highs; health office part-time and politicized; charity torn between personal-touch ideology and Community Chest war-drive technique; the press as near-universal utility and advertising business; solidarity rituals (boosters, Bearcats) beside cleavages of class, Jewish "belonging," Negro exclusion, and the Klan tornado of the early 1920s.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Middletown* became one of the most widely read American sociological monographs of the interwar years — assigned in colleges, cited in journalism, and absorbed into common speech as a name for "ordinary America." It established the **[[community-study|community study]]** as a recognizable genre: one place, multi-domain, classed comparison, historical baseline, mixed methods.

Relations to neighbors in this wiki:

- **[[chicago-school|Chicago School]] / [[park-burgess-city-study|The City]] (1925):** contemporary urban research, but different institutional home and different master metaphor (total culture vs human ecology / natural areas).
- **[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|Polish Peasant]]:** documentary primary-group depth vs whole-city six-domain breadth.
- **[[family]]:** supplies an industrial-city family case (companionship gap, divorce, classed fertility control) distinct from Comtean theory or peasant family-group documents.
- Later: *Middletown in Transition* (1937) restudies under Depression; Caplow and collaborators' late-twentieth-century Middletown III projects (not yet ingested) make Muncie a multi-wave laboratory.

Conceptually the book's portable tools are the **two-class occupational dichotomy**, the **six activity domains**, and the **uneven rates of change** thesis — all still used as teaching devices even when particulars are dated.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

### Authors' self-limits (in-text positions)

- Not typical; caution on generalization.
- Data gaps and uneven method (Preface); viewpoint developed after 1925 field completion.
- Class scheme not a complete structural diagram; unit labels are shorthand.
- HS true-false "suggestive not absolute proof"; folk talk not scientific proof.
- BC sample skewed prosperous; WC daytime interview bias.

### External critique clusters (reception; not all primary-ingested here)

| Position | Claim | Evidence status in this wiki |
|---|---|---|
| Typicality | Middletown cannot be America | Authors already warned; reception often ignored them |
| Race design | Choosing a low-minority city and white family samples underplays race as organizing principle | Supported by Note on Method + HS exclusion; Klan/segregation chapters show race still erupted |
| Class dualism | People/things cut flattens old middle, foremen, petty owners | Internal to occupational coding debates |
| Power structure | Underplays capital concentration, state, and party machines relative to "culture" | Civic chapter exists; relative weight is judgment |
| Gender | Wives as interview respondents; men's work worlds better seen in plants than domestic interiors | Method note; feminist historiography (future sources) |
| Restudy | Depression and postwar Middletown differ | *Middletown in Transition*; Caplow et al. (queue) |
| Identification | Pseudonym ethics and later Muncie tourism/scholarship | Historiographic |

## Current Standing

*Middletown* remains:

1. The **charter** modern American community study in teaching canons.
2. A **period document** of 1924–25 Midwestern industrial culture — cars, open shop, Protestant churchscape, Klan aftershock, booster civic religion.
3. A **method object**: how totalizing ambition, class dualism, and racial sampling frames produce knowledge.
4. A **laboratory brand**: Muncie as restudy site, independent of any single finding's survival.
