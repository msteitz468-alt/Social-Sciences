## Question and Intellectual Context

*Sex and Temperament in Three Primitive Societies* answers a question that is simultaneously psychological, comparative, and public: **Are the personality traits Euro-Americans call masculine and feminine innate sex-temperaments, or cultural standardisations built around the biological fact of sex-difference?** Mead’s Introduction records that she began sharing “the general belief of our society that there was a natural sex-temperament.” The book is the experiment that, in her account, undid that belief.

The foil is not a single named psychologist (as Hall was for *Coming of Age*) but a whole Western plot: from birth, different behaviour expected of the sexes; courtship, marriage, and parenthood cast as expressions of innate type; discussions of “the position of women” that leave men’s road unexamined. She dismisses the Vaertings’ *The Dominant Sex* as mere sleight-of-hand reversing European dominance rather than opening the design space of temperament allotment. She also sets aside the existence-question for universal sex differences and any “treatise on the rights of women.” The research act is narrower and sharper: how three societies grouped social attitudes toward temperament about sex-difference.

Intellectually the book extends the Boasian and culture-and-personality programme already public in *[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* (1928). Where Samoa tested the universality of adolescent storm-and-stress, *Sex and Temperament* tests the universality of sex-linked personality. Both are natural experiments for audiences of teachers and citizens as well as colleagues. Dedication is to [[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]; the 1934 Hanover Seminar and comments from Ruth Benedict and John Dollard appear in the acknowledgements as the immediate theoretical workshop.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How is temperament conditioned by sex category? | Social personalities of men and women | Three Sepik-region societies | Intensive ethnography + comparison | 1931–33 |
| Same vs different allotment by sex? | Ideal personality patterns | Arapesh; Mundugumor; Tchambuli | Contrast design | same |
| How are patterns moulded? | Childhood to marriage | Child care, initiation, marriage | Developmental ethnography | same |

**Expedition facts (Mead’s acknowledgements):** two years New Guinea with [[fortune-reo|Reo Fortune]]; Mead under AMNH duties financed by the Voss Research Fund (thanks to Clark Wissler); Fortune under Social Science Research Council of Columbia University. Partnership enabled more remote fields; Fortune supplied men’s cults and male-sphere material a woman ethnologist was “practically debarred” from studying alone, analysed the difficult Arapesh language, and took a large share of Mundugumor and Tchambuli ethnography. Consequently Mead treated Arapesh most extensively and gave the other two “only the minimum of ethnological material” needed for the special problem. Arapesh field choice oriented by Briggs of Sydney; Tchambuli by Bateson’s Middle Sepik work; Karawop plantation as Arapesh base; Australian administrative endorsement.

**Design as rhetoric:** the three cases are not a random sample of New Guinea. After Arapesh (both sexes mild) and Mundugumor (both sexes fierce), the party sought a third people “essentially irrelevant to the relations between the sexes”; Washkuk refused; Tchambuli accepted. The book’s structure — long Arapesh, shorter Mundugumor, shorter Tchambuli, then implications — is itself a method fact: depth is unequal by design.

**Colonial conditions as reliability facts:** Mundugumor under full government control ~three years; war, head-hunting, cannibalism outlawed; Mead reconstructs the “mainspring” pattern from vivid memory including children’s participation. Tchambuli renaissance of art under Pax Britannica after earlier Sepik raiding. Present-tense ethnography often means “as recently lived,” not “as of timeless custom.”

## The Site/Sample/Data

### Arapesh (mountain)

Alitoa region: steep gardens, pigs, gift-disguised trade, buanyin competitive partnership as exception, maternal ideal for both sexes. Boy “grows his wife” by feeding. Birth practices involve father’s purification and dual childcare. Childhood continuous nursing, trust training, non-competitive play. Deviants: violent/possessive misfits (Wabe as Western-male ideal made pathetic). Society page: [[arapesh]].

### Mundugumor (river)

Yuat river compounds, rope organisation, same-sex hostility theory, sister-exchange, father–son rivalry, harsh childhood (suckling as struggle, stiff basket), passion without tenderness. Ideal violent for both sexes; mild as enablers. Graphic violence and sexual violence in the record — attributed, not wiki spectacle. Society page: [[mundugumor]] (Biwat).

### Tchambuli / Chambri (lake)

Art-centered men; women fishing and mosquito-bag wealth; patriliny + polygyny + bride-price with women’s economic control; legal male dominance vs emotional male subservience. Unplaced “viriloid” men highly maladjusted. Society page: [[chambri]].

### Load-bearing passage (Ch. 17)

> “We are forced to conclude that human nature is almost unbelievably malleable, responding accurately and contrastingly to contrasting cultural conditions. The differences between individuals who are members of different cultures, like the differences between individuals within a culture, are almost entirely to be laid to differences in conditioning, especially during early childhood, and the form of this conditioning is culturally determined. Standardised personality differences between the sexes are of this order, cultural creations to which each generation, male and female, is trained to conform.”

What the words commit her to: **preponderant** cultural conditioning of personality differences between the sexes as standardized; not a total denial that any biological sex differences exist (she brackets that question); not a claim that every individual is infinitely plastic without residue (she discusses hereditary variation only *after* accepting conditioning’s force). What followers and critics often made of it: a slogan that culture is everything and biology nothing — a stronger thesis than the paragraph’s careful bracketing.

### Three-case summary (her own words, compressed)

- Arapesh: both sexes “maternal in its parental aspects, and feminine in its sexual aspects”; cooperative, unaggressive, responsive.
- Mundugumor: both sexes violent, aggressive, possessive; tenderer sentiments inappropriate in either sex.
- Tchambuli: “genuine reversal of the sex-attitudes of our own culture, with the woman the dominant, impersonal, managing partner, the man the less responsible and the emotionally dependent person.”

Neither Arapesh nor Mundugumor, she notes, *profits by contrast between the sexes*; only Tchambuli uses complementary opposition — which is why the third case isolates sex-specialisation as cultural choice rather than natural complementarity.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Shared starting belief** — she confesses natural sex-temperament belief, then narrates its undoing. Rhetoric of conversion, not of pure outsider critique.
2. **Minimum ethnography for maximum contrast** — especially Parts 2–3; the book is a problem monograph, not three full ethnographies.
3. **Deviant as proof of range** — Wabe, Temos, Ombléan, Tchengokwale, unplaced Tchambuli men: culture selects from a wider human spectrum.
4. **Clothing analogy** — masculine/feminine traits “as lightly linked to sex as… clothing” in some passages: strong constructivist figure.
5. **1950 “too beautiful” defense** — she records the suspicion that three tribes in a hundred miles forming a neat pattern meant she found what she sought; the book’s afterlife includes that charge.
6. **Conclusion as civic address** — Course 1 (clear complementary standardisation), Course 2 (radical single pattern), Course 3 (many temperamental endowments; “whole gamut of human potentialities”). Eye-colour parable ridicules arbitrary birth classifications. Class-for-sex substitution declared no real advance. Final sentence is civic poetry as much as science:

> “If we are to achieve a richer culture, rich in contrasting values, we must recognise the whole gamut of human potentialities, and so weave a less arbitrary social fabric, one in which each diverse human gift will find a fitting place.”

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- Public and textbook charter for **cultural construction of sex roles** in mid-century America.
- Strengthens [[culture-and-personality]] after Samoa; feeds later gender studies vocabularies even when “temperament” drops out.
- Mead 1950: “most misunderstood book” — readers focused on sex differences when she thought temperament was the light; pattern felt “too pretty.”
- 1963 preface reframes for sex-role vs individuality debates of a later American moment.
- Relation to Samoa: same author, same program, different developmental target (adolescence vs sex-personality); Freeman controversy attaches to Samoa primarily but colors overall Mead reputation ([[mead-freeman-debate]]).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. Positions (not adjudicated here):

1. **Confirmation / “too beautiful”** — design after two single-temperament cases sought a third; convenience sample within one region.
2. **Depth imbalance** — Arapesh thick; Mundugumor and Tchambuli thin; Fortune’s unpublished or separate materials not in this volume.
3. **Homogenization** — one temperament per culture, one ideal per sex category; little room for class, age, or historical change inside the type.
4. **Colonial pacification** — Mundugumor “clock” stopped; memory ethnography of violence; present tense ethics.
5. **Ethnonym and restudy** — Chambri, Biwat, and Arapesh literatures after Mead may revise local claims; primary restudies not all ingested in this wiki session.
6. **Biological challenge** — later behavioral ecology / sex difference research challenges strong malleability readings (as positions; not settled in this source).
7. **Gendered access** — strength (women’s lives, children) and limit (men’s cults via Fortune).

## Current Standing

Still assigned as the three-tribe gender classic; best read as **1931–33 Sepik fieldwork + 1935 American argument**, with 1950/1963 self-commentary. Methodologically instructive for comparative design, partner ethnography, and public anthropology. Empirically provisional case-by-case. Concept [[sex-temperament]] remains the right historical label for what Mead claimed to have shown.

## Verbatim passages with analysis (selected)

**On the research problem (Introduction):** Mead insists the study is “very simply, an account of how three primitive societies have grouped their social attitudes towards temperament about the very obvious facts of sex-difference.” Analysis: “very simply” is understatement; the sentence brackets biology and feminism to smuggle a culture-and-personality revolution past readers who would reject a “treatise on the rights of women.”

**On Arapesh maternal men:** “It may be said that the rôle of men, like the rôle of women, is maternal.” Analysis: deliberately yokes Western “maternal” to male role — the shock is the point; she needs English sex-role words to show they can be reassigned.

**On growing a wife:** claim rests on food becoming “flesh and bone of her body.” Analysis: feeding, not begetting or bride-price, as the idiomatic bond — parallel to fatherhood by feeding; kinship substance as nurture.

**On Tchambuli contradiction:** “men are theoretically, legally dominant, but… play an emotionally subservient rôle.” Analysis: blocks the crude “matriarchy” reading the Vaertings offered; power is split across legal and emotional registers.

**On planning (Conclusion):** social production of sex personalities is “a two-edged sword” for Fascist or Communist or liberal programmes. Analysis: 1935 political present; malleability is not automatically emancipatory — Mead’s own warning against reading her as simple liberationist.

**Closing gamut:** “whole gamut of human potentialities… less arbitrary social fabric.” Analysis: Course 3 is not abolition of difference but multiplication of recognised temperaments beyond sex (and class, race, birth accidents). The eye-colour parable has already mocked any single birth-accident classifier.

## Counterfactual significance

Without *Sex and Temperament*, culture-and-personality would still have Samoa and Benedict’s configurations, but the **public American vocabulary for sex roles as cultural** would lack its most portable three-case teaching machine. The risk of the portable form is exactly the “too beautiful” reduction. The book’s deeper contribution is the **deviant** analysis and the **Course 3** horizon — harder to sloganize, more consistent with her claim to care about individual temperament rather than partisan sex interest alone.


## Fieldwork as Conducted — Expanded Reconstruction

### Access, language, and gendered division of labour

Mead’s acknowledgements are a method chapter in miniature. She could not have reached “peoples more savage and more inaccessibly located” alone; Fortune’s partnership reduced costs and opened male cult space. Among Arapesh she was “practically debarred” from men’s cults and confined by precipices to Alitoa while he collected Plains ceremonies and language. Among Mundugumor and Tchambuli “a large share of the ethnographical work fell to him,” which is why her book skims those ethnologies. Any critique of thinness that ignores this division of labour misreads the research act as a solo deep dive it never claimed to be.

Language: Fortune’s Arapesh analysis is credited; Tchambuli work used Middle Sepik orientation and intervening languages in the Washkuk refusal episode. Mead’s Samoan pattern — working in the local language with youth — does not automatically transfer; the New Guinea expedition is multi-site, multi-language, partnered.

### Sampling logic and the “accidental” third case

The third case is narrated as almost accidental: after two single-temperament societies, they wanted any third people for reasons “essentially irrelevant to the relations between the sexes.” District officer Eric Robinson suggested Washkuk or Tchambuli; Washkuk men begged them not to stay (hunting trip, scattered villages); Tchambuli it was. Rhetorically this undercuts “I hunted for a reverse case.” Structurally it still yields the reverse case that completes the design. Both facts belong in the design reconstruction: intention was not to shop for reversal; outcome supplied reversal.

### Child development as the causal engine

Across all three parts, early conditioning is the mechanism of malleability. Arapesh: continuous contact, lip-play, trust, non-competitive play, dual parental care, growth-not-hazing initiation. Mundugumor: pregnancy often unwanted, sex/birth-order survival calculus, stiff carrying basket, suckling struggle, harsh maturity by early teens, kinship taught as sex-split hostilities. Tchambuli: boys excluded from green-room art years, lifelong over-sensitivity; dual socialisation (bought-wife theory vs women’s economic voice). The book’s causal claim is developmental, not merely adult role modeling.

### Named cases as evidence, not ornament

Arapesh: Ombomb–Me’elue–Sauwedjo polygyny cascade; Natun–Nyelahai; Temos; Wabe–Menala; Manum–Silisium clan adultery; Amitoa; Agilapwe; Alis. Mundugumor: Kalektimban, Komedkua (mild enablers); Ombléan (outwits culture); Kwenda. Tchambuli: Yepiwali–Tchuikumban orphan drama; Tchubukéima labour; Tchengokwale midwife; Kaviwon (Luluai’s son). These cases do the work of showing that ideals never fully capture a population — and that cultures still punish or fail to place those who miss the ideal.

## Part Four Implications — Argument Map

Chapter 17 (“The Standardisation of Sex-Temperament”) moves from three ethnographies to generalisation:

1. Traits Westerners call masculine/feminine appear in both sexes across cases.
2. Societies may standardise one temperament for everyone, or allot contrasting temperaments by sex (or age, rank, occupation).
3. Sex-personality specialisation is as arbitrary, historically, as many other birth accidents.
4. Therefore standardised sex differences are cultural creations of early conditioning.
5. Qualification: hereditary individual variation remains a real problem *after* conditioning is grasped — same infant could be developed into participant in any of the three cultures.

Chapter 18 (“The Deviant”) splits physiological handicap from cultural deviance; modern psychiatry, she argues, confuses them. Sex-dichotomy multiplies misfits: every insistence that women follow one feminine pattern “does violence also to the individuality of many men.” America appears as plural contradictory sex-rules plus free intermarriage — everyone believes natural sex behaviour exists, no one agrees what it is. Tchambuli is the ethnographic mirror of American ambiguity: man still defined as head of house while woman is trained to take that position more surely.

### Conclusion — three courses and a horizon

**Course 1:** Standardise complementary antithetical sex personalities; make institutions congruent. Clear; wasteful of gifts; regiments both sexes; millions would “gladly return.”

**Course 2:** Train both sexes to one pattern; abandon artificial European sex-differences as “social fictions.” Associated with “most radical groups.” Contraceptives and declining strength differential (lawsuits replace combat) reduce practical sex differences worth elaborating. But: sacrifice of sex-personality distinctions may sacrifice **complexity**; Arapesh monotone frustrates nonconformists and starves even gifted conformists’ imagination; Mundugumor ceremonial dwindles as women demand initiation; insisting on *no* sex differences can itself standardise; removing minority sex-linked attitudes (e.g. women-for-peace myth) can erase useful diversity of civic attitudes.

**Course 3 (preferred horizon):** Do not choose only between sex-standardisation and abolition. Recognise, train, and place **many divergent temperamental endowments**. Historical paths toward less rigid sex classification: (a) class/rank substituting for sex — frees some women, condemns many of both sexes, “no real advance”; (b) recognising genuine individual gifts in either sex (writing, art) — real distinction for artificial, double recruitment. Educational programme: stop forcing boys to fight and girls to passivity (or all to fight); develop maternal boys and obstacle-fighting girls; many patterns congenial to gifts.

**Eye-colour parable:** educate by eye colour into contrasting personalities — would reduce some sex-group pathologies but remains a “parody” of classifying by birth accident (sex, colour, date of birth, head shape).

**Close:** richer culture requires the “whole gamut of human potentialities” and a “less arbitrary social fabric.”

## Prefaces as Historiography of Reception

**1950:** “most misunderstood book.” Difficulties: talking sex and temperament at once; readers thought pattern “too beautiful” (both mild; both fierce; reverse stereotypes with men “catty… curls… shopping”); material illuminated temperament more than sex differences.

**1963:** American women rely more on sex definition, less on individuality; hope for shift toward shared temperaments; cosmic framing of human differences.

These prefaces are primary for reception history, secondary for 1931–33 ethnography. Fisher 2001 and Bateson 2001 apparatus are further reception layers — POSITION, not findings.

Both **support** cultural plasticity; both are Boasian natural experiments; both write for general readers. Neither is a laboratory demonstration — Mead’s Samoa appendix already admitted the personal equation; *Sex and Temperament* never pretends otherwise.

## Reflexivity of the Research Act

- **Colonial administration** enables and truncates: pacification stops Mundugumor “clock”; Pax Britannica returns Tchambuli refugees and enables art renaissance.
- **Gendered ethnographer role** shapes access; Fortune is not a silent assistant but a co-collector whose materials are selectively narrated.
- **American audience** is built into the conclusion’s Fascist/Communist/liberal planning language — 1935 political present.
- **Period ethnonyms and violence vocabulary** are part of the archive’s politics of representation; wiki voice does not adopt them as neutral descriptors.
- **Collection coverage:** this ingest is the primary text; restudies and Fortune monographs remain gaps.

## What Survived

- Teaching machine of three cases for sex-role plasticity.
- Concept that personality ideals can be same-sex-shared or cross-sex-allotted.
- Warning that malleability is two-edged for social planning.
- Course 3 multi-temperament horizon as ancestor of later individual-difference and anti-essentialist gender talk.
- Deviant analysis linking misfit suffering to cultural selection.

What often did *not* survive in popular memory: the legal/emotional power split at Tchambuli; the complexity-loss critique of Course 2; the eye-colour parable; the insistence that the book is not a rights-of-women treatise.
