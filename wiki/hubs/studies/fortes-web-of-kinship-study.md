## Question and Intellectual Context

*The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi* (1949) is the second part of [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]]'s two-volume analysis of Tale social structure, dedicated "TO MY WIFE AND FELLOW-WORKER SONIA L. FORTES." *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi* (1945) had treated the segmentary lineage system as the permanent large-scale framework of Tale society — what the Foreword to *Web of Kinship* recalls as "an analysis of the warp of the social fabric." The present book turns to "what might be called the woof of the social fabric," starting not from the corporate lineage but from the individual: "Our main interest will be in the standard forms and processes of person-to-person relationships and their connexion with the large-scale framework of the society. Our subject, in other words, is Tale kinship in the narrow sense, viewed in relation to the lineage system." Fortes's question is therefore structural rather than merely descriptive: how does person-to-person kinship — the web spun by marriage and parenthood — articulate with a corporate skeleton already mapped in the companion volume?

The Foreword situates the book against roughly a century of kinship scholarship without attempting to survey it: "It is nearly a hundred years since Bachofen's reconstruction of primordial mother-right and McLennan's theory of exogamy and endogamy upset long-standing beliefs about the nature of the human family. It is exactly a century since Lewis Morgan, who may justly be regarded as the father of the scientific study of kinship in primitive society, first wrote about the Iroquois clan." Fortes declines to adjudicate that literature — the book is "concerned only with kinship in relation to the social structure — that is, in my view, with the fundamental principles of Tale kinship relations as they enter into the organization of collective life" — and this deliberate narrowing of scope is itself a theoretical stance: kinship is to be read structurally, as a set of person-to-person relations coordinated with the lineage system, not as an evolutionary stage or a psychological universal.

The disciplinary setting is British social anthropology at the close of the fieldwork revolution and the opening of postwar structural-functionalism's peak. Fortes names [[malinowski-bronislaw|Bronislaw Malinowski]] and [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]] as the teachers whose working hypotheses were "adapted to the methods of observation available to the anthropologist in the field," and records that while in Taleland he read [[firth-raymond|Raymond Firth]]'s *We, the Tikopia* (1936), calling it "a mine of ideas and a most valuable stimulus to observation." The book is thus explicitly positioned within the emerging structural-functionalist consensus — Radcliffe-Brown's comparative sociology of kinship systems, Malinowski's functionalist attention to the felt texture of domestic life, and Firth's Polynesian comparanda — while remaining, in Fortes's own account, a description-first monograph rather than a general theory of kinship.

The Tallensi had already entered comparative political anthropology through Fortes's chapter in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study|African Political Systems]]* (1940), co-edited with [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]]: a Group B, acephalous society with the Namoo/Talis cleavage and the dual *na'am*/*tendaana* offices already established. *Web of Kinship* does not re-argue that political typology; instead it shows how the same society's domestic and cognatic order completes and counterbalances the corporate lineage system mapped there and in *Dynamics*. Fortes and Evans-Pritchard, working with [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s comparative program, are the two figures most responsible for systematizing what later became known as **descent theory** — the analytic apparatus, tested case by case across sub-Saharan Africa, for explaining political order through segmentary lineage structure. *Web of Kinship* is the volume in which Fortes supplies that apparatus with its necessary complement: an account of what unilineal descent alone cannot organize.

The book supplies the ethnographic and conceptual base for two concepts Fortes did not yet name in 1949 but developed explicitly in later publications: **complementary filiation** (coined in "The Structure of Unilineal Descent Groups," 1953) and the **developmental cycle of the domestic group** (elaborated with Jack Goody's Cambridge group, notably in *The Developmental Cycle in Domestic Groups*, 1958). Neither term appears in the 1949 text itself — the book works with the vocabulary of "cognatic kinship," "agnatic kinship," "the domestic family," and "the lineage system" — but the empirical and analytic groundwork for both is laid here in full, and this analysis flags the anachronism explicitly rather than reading the later terminology back into the 1949 text uncritically.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How does person-to-person kinship organize collective life relative to the corporate lineage system? | Person-to-person kinship ties and the domestic group, articulated to the maximal lineage/clan mapped in *Dynamics* | Taleland (Northern Territories, Gold Coast); intensive work in Tongo and neighbouring settlements; regional population ~35,000 (1931 figure) | Vernacular participant observation; genealogical method; homestead architecture as evidence; N=61 domestic-family sample census (1934); named case histories | Fieldwork from 1934; short revisit 1945; write-up completed 1948; published 1949 |

Fortes and Sonia L. Fortes worked in Taleland from 1934, continuing the fieldwork that had produced *Dynamics of Clanship*. A 1945 revisit, undertaken after the manuscript of *Web of Kinship* was essentially complete, found "no appreciable change" in the social structure as described, though Fortes explicitly anticipated rapid change from war veterans returning with new experience, the expanding Native Administration, schools, and missions — a forward-looking caveat that keeps the ethnographic-present trap in view even as the book's analytic voice remains largely synchronic. The Foreword is explicit about scope: this is not a comprehensive account of Tale family life for its own sake, but kinship *in relation to social structure* — the extraction of "fundamental principles."

Methodologically the book is considerably richer than a kinship-terminology schematic. Five techniques do the empirical work:

- **Architecture as evidence.** Homestead layout — the gateway room, the *zoy* (men's reception area), the cattle-yard, the granaries, and the *dug* (women's apartments) — is read as a material projection of agnatic integration set against matricentral differentiation. Fortes treats the physical plan of a compound as a diagram of its kinship structure, not merely as a backdrop to it.
- **Sample census.** Sixty-one domestic families surveyed in 1934, concentrated in Tongo, ground the book's central claim that the polygynous joint family is a *phase* in a developmental process rather than a fixed household type. This is quantitative in form (a census with countable categories) but small in scale and geographically concentrated — a limitation Fortes does not fully thematize.
- **Developmental "slow motion."** Parent–child relations are traced across the whole life course — from infancy through old age — with attention to the social labels attached to each stage and the reciprocity norms governing it, not only the jural rights nominally in force.
- **Case material.** Named individuals (Kologo and Sayobazaa, Katigoo, Naabdiya, Yindol, Tiezien and Nyaaqzum, and others) illustrate recurrent structural tensions: genitor/pater conflict, lineage fission, first-born tension, the alternate-generation form of the levirate. This is the book's closest approach to Malinowskian "imponderabilia of actual life," embedded within an otherwise structural argument.
- **Field principle.** Extra-clan kinship is mapped as a socio-spatial field — Fortes gives a figure of 134 Tongo men traced by their extra-clan kinship connections in 1934 — congruent with the clanship-field method used in *Dynamics*.

Limits, as the study was actually conducted, are worth stating plainly. The male jural and ritual spheres are documented far more densely than women's everyday voices and perspectives; women appear chiefly as *dug*-heads, as points of connection (mothers, sisters, wives), and as bearers of witchcraft-related uterine ties, rather than as informants whose own account of domestic life is quoted at length. Colonial administration is present as background and as a source of anticipated future change rather than as a primary object of analysis — a contrast with the fuller treatment of "Government" in the APS chapter. And the book presupposes *Dynamics of Clanship* for the full machinery of lineage segmentation; a reader without that companion volume in hand will find the lineage apparatus invoked but not re-derived.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Taleland** in the 1930s: a densely settled population (roughly 170 per square mile, high for the region) of sedentary Mole-Dagbane cereal farmers combining grain agriculture with livestock-keeping, under chronic food insecurity in the dry season. Fortes and Evans-Pritchard had already stressed, in the APS chapter, that Tale society is an equalitarian peasantry without economic classes cutting across lineage lines — no class stratum independent of kinship position. Settlements are aggregations of **homesteads**, each homestead the domicile of a **domestic family**; there is no precise tribal boundary or centralized tribal government, so the analytic population is not "the Tallensi" as a bounded polity but persons whose cognatic web and clanship field fall within Taleland's socio-geographic region — a methodological point Fortes restates from *Dynamics* rather than re-argues.

A note on the analytic distinction that structures the whole book, quoted at length because it is load-bearing for everything that follows: "It is essential to distinguish two kinds of genealogical ties among the Tallensi. There are, firstly, cognatic ties, that is, ties of actual or assumed physical consanguinity and of the social relations entailed by them, which link person to person or an individual to a lineage or one lineage to another in a specifically defined and particular bond. Such ties may be traced through males only, through females only, or through both males and females. Secondly, there are lineage ties, social relations based on the tie of common agnatic descent." The distinction "turns on the principle that lineage ties always unite people in or relate them to corporate groups serving common interests and held together by common values, whereas cognates do not necessarily form corporate groups. Cognates have mutual bonds of sentiment and reciprocal obligations, but not necessarily common interests. All the members of a given lineage have the same agnatic kin and therefore identical lineage ties; but only identical siblings — that is, brothers or sisters by the same parents — have the same cognatic kin." This is the conceptual hinge on which the descent/filiation distinction later turns.

### Complementary filiation avant la lettre

Although the 1949 text never uses the phrase "complementary filiation," its Chapter XII synthesis states the polarity that the phrase would later name. Fortes writes: "the patrilineal principle is the primary factor of stability and continuity in the organization of the family and hence of the entire social structure. Conversely, the recognition of kinship ties through the mother is the primary factor of dispersion and segmentation" — the one a **centripetal**, the other a **centrifugal**, force. Within the cognatic/agnatic frame established in Chapter II, maternal parentage is treated as no mere weak copy of patrilineal descent: the uterine concept *soog* carries its own emotional intimacy, mutual aid obligations, and — distinctively — mystical attributes, including witchcraft potentiality (*tyuk* contamination) transmitted along the female line. Extra-clan matrilateral and sororal ties, with the exception of the symmetrical *soog* bond among uterine siblings, characteristically take the institutionalized form of the **mother's-brother/sister's-son relationship** (*ahab*/*ahay*): gifts, fosterage arrangements, rights of access to land and animals ("hen paths"), ritual shares such as portions of ceremonially cooked meat at the maternal uncle's funeral, and the intermediary role of the *poyasama* in marriage negotiations, glossed by Fortes as "our person and also their person" — a formula that captures the dual, boundary-crossing loyalty structurally required of the position. The father's sister (*ba-pok*), by contrast, is described as a "female father" whose jural force is comparatively limited — a deliberate structural asymmetry that Fortes insists on so that cross-sex parental siblings are not read as interchangeable mirror images of each other.

A remembered native maxim closes the point in emic terms: "It is women who cause kinship to spread widely." Fortes reads this as the Tallensi's own formulation of a structural fact his analysis independently derives — that women, as the vector of cognatic ties beyond the patrilineage, are structurally positioned as the agents of kinship's dispersion under a system that is simultaneously patrilineal in descent and exogamous in marriage.

The 1953 paper "The Structure of Unilineal Descent Groups" would name this centrifugal principle **complementary filiation**: the individualizing tie to the parent through whom descent is *not* reckoned (paradigmatically the mother, in a patrilineal system), understood as structurally complementary to, rather than competing with, agnatic descent. Reading the term back into the 1949 book is useful for locating where the concept's empirical grounding was laid, but this analysis marks the anachronism explicitly: *Web of Kinship* supplies the Tallensi case in full ethnographic and structural detail; the theoretical label and its generalization to a cross-cultural typology of unilineal systems belong to the later paper.

### The developmental cycle of the domestic group

The homestead and the joint family are, in Tale terms, effectively one concept, expressed through the polysemous *yir*/*dug* vocabulary that Fortes also uses for lineage segments — a deliberate ethnographic point about how thoroughly domestic and lineage idiom interpenetrate. Homestead architecture maps the structural cleavage directly: the agnatic core of the *yir* (gateway, men's *zoy*, cattle-yard) stands opposed to the matricentral *dug* apartments organized around each wife. Drawing on the 1934 sample of sixty-one domestic families, Fortes argues that the characteristic observed unit — a **polygynous agnatic joint family** — is not a fixed household type but a phase within a recurrent cycle: expansion through polygynous marriage and the birth of children, internal differentiation along maternal (*dug*) lines even while agnatic unity is nominally intact, eventual fission as adult sons build their own homestead gateways, and reintegration of the resulting units under lineage and leviratic norms when a household head dies. Because "segmentation in the domestic family parallels segmentation in the lineage" — simultaneous in time and complementary in function — the developmental trajectory of any single homestead is legible only against the lineage-fission pattern established in *Dynamics of Clanship*. This is the direct ethnographic root of the later, more general **developmental cycle of the domestic group** framework that Fortes and Jack Goody's Cambridge circle elaborated in the 1950s as a comparative method for household analysis across many societies, well beyond the Tallensi case.

### Parenthood and the generations

Fortes traces parent–child relations as a graded sequence of social labels and reciprocity norms across the full life course rather than as a static jural bundle: an initial phase of nurturance and dependency, a middle phase of parental authority balanced against filial obligation, and — as children mature and parents age — a shift toward reciprocity and eventually generational role-reversal, in which the aging parent depends on the adult child. The moral basis of the parent–child bond is treated as **absolute and non-renounceable**: filial piety is obligatory even where relations are marked by ambivalence, and native theory (the *Yin* concept, first-born taboos observed especially among the Namoo, and reversals of ordinary behavioral norms during funeral ritual) formalizes the emotional charge that biological parenthood carries in Tale social life. The ancestor cult is read as the jural-religious prolongation of this same tie beyond death — ancestors function structurally as parent-images, continuing to make moral demands on their living descendants in a manner continuous with, rather than categorically different from, the demands of living parents. Grandparents, by contrast, function as a structural **foil** to parents: paternal grandfathers retain formal authority as family heads while relating to grandchildren with a familiarity and license for joking that the parent–child relationship strictly forbids — a patterned discontinuity between adjacent generations that recurs across many unilineal-descent societies and that Fortes treats as itself diagnostic of the underlying structure.

### Siblings and marriage

Siblingship (*mabiirat*) carries a baseline of social equivalence that is nonetheless graded internally: full (uterine, *soog*) siblings are structurally closer than paternal half-siblings (*sunzo*), and birth order (*nyeer*) further differentiates status among siblings of either category, with special rules attaching to twins and to classificatory "clan-siblings." Marriage founds the domestic group as a going concern, yet it remains structurally opposed to each spouse's own prior kinship affiliation: sexual and reproductive rights over a wife are described as a **corporate clan interest** rather than a purely conjugal matter, co-wives adjust their domestic arrangements along *dug* lines, and a wife's natal (paternal) family retains ongoing claims on her and, by extension, on her children — claims that surface repeatedly in the mother's-brother material discussed above. Levirate (a widow's remarriage within her late husband's lineage, in an alternate-generation pattern Fortes documents in detail) and a rudimentary sororate articulate the continuity of widow and sibling-substitution norms with the demands of lineage continuity, closing the circle between the marriage system treated here and the corporate lineage system of *Dynamics*.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Five moves organize the book's persuasive architecture, beyond the substantive claims themselves.

1. **The warp/woof metaphor.** Introduced at the seam between the two volumes ("we spoke of the first part of this study as an analysis of the warp of the social fabric. Our task now is to examine what might be called the woof"), the metaphor does real analytic work: it forecloses in advance any reading in which either the lineage system or the domestic-kinship system is treated as more fundamental or as reducible to the other. Warp and woof are equally necessary to the finished cloth; the metaphor commits Fortes to genuine structural complementarity rather than to a hierarchy of levels.
2. **Blood and tissue.** The book's most compact statement of the domestic/lineage relation borrows an organic-analogy idiom close to Radcliffe-Brown's: "Like blood and tissue in the animal organism, they constitute interpenetrating media of Tale social life." Fortes immediately qualifies the analogy by specifying the *functional* division it tracks — "the interests and ends (primarily political, jural, and ritual) subserved by the lineage system differ significantly from those (primarily economic and reproductive) subserved by the family system" — which keeps the organic figure from collapsing into a claim of literal biological determinism.
3. **The field principle**, carried over from the clanship analysis in *Dynamics*: extra-clan kinship, like the clanship field itself, is treated as a set of overlapping, individually centered social fields rather than as a set of bounded, named groups — a move that lets Fortes describe cognatic kinship's diffuseness without abandoning structural rigor.
4. **The moral irreducible.** The book's closing theoretical move, in Chapter XII, is a deliberate refusal of reduction: kinship is not to be explained away as an epiphenomenon of sexual attraction, economic cooperation, or biological reproduction alone. Fortes states that "the essence of a highly elaborated kinship system like that of the Tallensi lies in its function as the primary mechanism through which the basic moral axioms of a society... are translated into the concrete give and take of social life" — a formulation that anticipates the explicitly moral-philosophical register of his later "axiom of amity" (*Kinship and the Social Order*, 1969).
5. **Dialogue with teachers.** The Foreword affirms Radcliffe-Brown's comparative-sociological program and Malinowski's insistence on grounding analysis in the "initial situation" of parenthood, while declining either master's more totalizing claims; Firth's *We, the Tikopia* is credited as a field stimulus and a model for combining structural description with attention to sentiment. This is less a polemic than a quiet positioning of the book within, rather than against, the reigning structural-functionalist consensus.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (analysed)

**On scope (Foreword):** Fortes states he is "concerned only with kinship in relation to the social structure — that is, in my view, with the fundamental principles of Tale kinship relations as they enter into the organization of collective life." *Analysis:* This is not modesty alone; it licenses a structural abstraction that omits a great deal of ordinary domestic ethnography, and it pre-empts a certain kind of critique — that the book gives too little of felt domestic experience — by declaring at the outset that this was never the book's object. Later critics of "structure without sentiment" in African kinship studies must reckon with the fact that Fortes *does* devote whole chapters (V–VIII) to affect and moral tension across the life course; the scope statement narrows the register, not the coverage, of sentiment in the book.

**On the warp/woof relation and the interpenetration of planes (Ch. II):** "Like blood and tissue in the animal organism, they constitute interpenetrating media of Tale social life. Membership of a family and membership of a lineage are equally and often concurrently decisive for the conduct of the individual and for the course of his life. Yet the analytical separation of these two planes of social structure is not entirely artificial." *Analysis:* The passage does two things at once — it licenses Fortes's own analytic procedure (treating domestic organization and lineage structure as separable planes for descriptive purposes) while insisting that this separability is not a native fact but an ethnographer's device, forestalling the objection that the book's two-volume structure imposes an artificial dualism on Tale experience.

**On the centrifugal/centripetal balance (Ch. XII):** "the patrilineal principle is the primary factor of stability and continuity in the organization of the family and hence of the entire social structure. Conversely, the recognition of kinship ties through the mother is the primary factor of dispersion and segmentation." *Analysis:* These words commit Fortes to a functional-equilibrium model in which matrilateral kinship is not a vestigial trace of some prior bilateral system but a structural necessity for any unilineal system that must marry its members out beyond the descent group. Alliance theorists (Lévi-Strauss and successors) would later read broadly similar facts — cross-cousin ties, the mother's-brother relationship — through the lens of marriage exchange between groups; Fortes reads them through filiation to an individual parent and the moral amity that filiation generates, a difference of theoretical architecture, not merely of vocabulary, that structured much of the mid-century descent-versus-alliance controversy.

**On kinship as moral mechanism (Ch. XII):** "the essence of a highly elaborated kinship system like that of the Tallensi lies in its function as the primary mechanism through which the basic moral axioms of a society... are translated into the concrete give and take of social life." *Analysis:* This is Fortes's distinctive theoretical signature — kinship read as ethics enacted rather than as a set of jural rules or an economic arrangement — and it distances the book from both a vulgar biologism (kinship reduced to reproduction) and a narrowly jural formalism (kinship reduced to rights and duties).

**Native maxim:** "It is women who cause kinship to spread widely." *Analysis:* Fortes uses this emic formula to ground, in the Tallensi's own terms, the structural claim that women — as the vector of ties beyond the patrilineage under an exogamous, patrilineal system — are the agents of cognatic dispersion; the maxim functions rhetorically as independent, native corroboration of an analytic conclusion Fortes has already reached by other means.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

Immediate reception placed *Web of Kinship* alongside Firth's *We, the Tikopia* and Evans-Pritchard's *The Nuer* as a model monographic treatment of kinship under British structural-functionalism — precisely the company Fortes's own Foreword invites by naming Firth as a stimulus. Within Africanist anthropology the book, read together with *Dynamics of Clanship*, became a paradigm case for how a single society's political-jural order (agnatic descent, corporate lineages) and its person-to-person kinship order (cognatic ties, the domestic developmental cycle) could be shown as mutually necessary rather than competing frameworks — the empirical demonstration that made Fortes, alongside Evans-Pritchard, one of the two principal systematizers of what came to be called **descent theory**.

**Complementary filiation**, formalized in the 1953 paper that drew directly on the Tallensi material presented here, became standard teaching apparatus for any unilineal system exhibiting strong maternal or affinal ties alongside strict patrilineal descent — cited across the African descent-theory literature as the concept that explained why matrilateral kinship persists and matters even in societies organized around strict patrilineal succession and inheritance. The **developmental cycle of the domestic group**, generalized well beyond the Tallensi case in the Cambridge symposium volume Fortes edited with contributions from Jack Goody and others (1958), became a durable comparative method for household and family analysis across many unilineal and non-unilineal societies alike — arguably the book's most methodologically portable legacy, independent of its specific Tale content.

The book fed later Africanist comparative volumes, including Fortes's own subsequent work on Ashanti kinship, and its findings and framework were folded into Fortes's mature statement of kinship theory, *Kinship and the Social Order: The Legacy of Lewis Henry Morgan* (1969), where the moral-mechanism argument of Chapter XII is developed explicitly into the "axiom of amity" — the claim that kinship relations everywhere carry a normative expectation of mutual aid and moral solidarity ("kinship morality") distinct from, though often entangled with, jural obligation. *Web of Kinship*'s domestic/politico-jural distinction likewise anticipates the more explicit domestic-domain/politico-jural-domain terminology Fortes deployed in his later theoretical writing, though — as with complementary filiation — that vocabulary postdates the 1949 text itself.

## Current Standing

*The Web of Kinship among the Tallensi* stands as a **canonical** African kinship ethnography and the primary published source for Tale domestic and cognatic order, indispensable for the summary pages on [[complementary-filiation|complementary filiation]] and the [[developmental-cycle-of-domestic-group|developmental cycle of the domestic group]], for the patrilineal avunculate as a comparative type, and for the Fortesian synthesis of lineage and kinship more generally. Its descriptive core has held up far better than the broader lineage-theory program it helped found, which subsequent historiography (Leach, Schneider, Kuper) subjected to sustained critique as a *model* — a distinction this wiki treats as load-bearing: the Tallensi data are not thereby discredited, but the generalizing apparatus built on cases like this one has been contested. All claims drawn from the book should be dated to its 1930s–40s documentation moment, and read together with the APS Tallensi chapter and, when available in this collection, *Dynamics of Clanship*.

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text used for this ingest is Fortes 1949 (International African Institute/Oxford University Press; the 1967 Anthropological Publications reprint, authorized by OUP on behalf of the IAI). International African Institute sponsorship situates the book within the colonial-era research infrastructure that funded much of the classic Africanist ethnography of this period — a production context worth noting on the [[tallensi]] society page's own reflexivity treatment, though the present analysis does not have ingested archival material on IAI funding conditions specifically. The companion volume is Fortes 1945, *The Dynamics of Clanship among the Tallensi*; the prior published statement is Fortes's chapter in *African Political Systems* (1940). Intellectual debts are named explicitly in the Foreword: Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown, and Firth. Fortes's own later theoretical development of this material — "The Structure of Unilineal Descent Groups" (1953), the Cambridge developmental-cycle symposium (1958), and *Kinship and the Social Order* (1969) — is referenced above as the concept's forward afterlife but is not yet ingested into this wiki. The critical historiography of the wider descent-theory program to which this book belongs — Leach's and Schneider's critiques, and above all Adam Kuper's *Lineage Theory: A Critical Retrospect* (1982) — is likewise named as the standard secondary reception but remains outside the current collection; when these enter the collection, the Critiques section above should be promoted from attributed summary to fully sourced positions.
