# In the Society of Nature — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[descola-society-of-nature-study]]. Source: [[descola-in-the-society-of-nature-1994]]. Society: [[achuar]]. Thinker: [[descola-philippe]].

**Analysis standard:** design-as-conducted · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine canonical sections. Substantive findings in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Descola’s problem, formulated in the Prefaces and General introduction of *In the Society of Nature* (English 1994; French *La Nature domestique* 1986), is how to analyze one Amazonian society’s **socialization of nature** without accepting either pole of a dualism he diagnoses in the Amazonian literature of his training years: (1) a semiological approach that treats environmental features mainly as props for the internal organization of native discourse; (2) ecological reductionism that treats sociocultural life as epiphenomena of ecosystems. Both monisms, he argues, give practice only a subservient role — either as a decoder of myths and taxonomies, or as pure adaptive function.

The intellectual formation he narrates is explicit. As a philosophy student he had absorbed Althusserian Marxism; ethnology, under [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] and Maurice Godelier, broke that dogmatic frame. From Lévi-Strauss he took the rigor of the “logic of the concrete” and attention to lowly detail; from Godelier, economic anthropology and the techniques of measuring fields and timing tasks learned from Baruya fieldwork. Ecology, in this framing, is a **total social fact**: technical, economic, and religious elements synthesized in a pattern isomorphic with other structures of the social whole (attributed — Preface). The book is bound by monograph form while declaring an anti-dualist ambition: equal heuristic weight to quantitative and qualitative data; no hard line between technical and conceptual determinations.

Disciplinary moment: late 1970s–mid-1980s Amazonian anthropology, post-Meggers/Carneiro pedological and protein debates, Sahlins’s *Stone Age Economics* underproduction thesis circulating, cultural ecology still powerful, structuralism’s nature/culture hinge still ambient. Descola writes from inside the Laboratoire d’Anthropologie Sociale while living the Achuar case that later becomes the “wake-up call” of *[[descola-beyond-nature-and-culture-2005|Beyond Nature and Culture]]*.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do Achuar socialize nature technically and symbolically? | Domestic unit; endogamous nexus | Non-mission Achuar N of Pastaza, Ecuador | Participant observation + quantification + hermeneutics | 1976–79 (core 1976–78) |
| What is the sphere of nature as known/represented? | Territory, landscape, cosmos, beings | Same; maps, taxonomies, calendars | Ethnoecology / ethnoscience | ethnographic present ~1977 |
| How is nature used in house, garden, forest, river? | Household production | Garden surveys; 21 hunters / 84 trips; fishing | Production measurement | 1977–78 |
| What is the “good life” economically? | Labor time; diet; manioc potential | 8 HH, 340 person-days time sample; diet estimates | Time allocation; calorie/protein accounting | 1970s sample |
| Does dualism fit? | Praxis as material+conceptual | Whole monograph | Synthetic interpretation | writing to 1984 / Fr. 1986 |

**What the design omits or brackets (self-declared).** Supralocal production relations and full social reproduction are deferred as a “vaster undertaking.” Mission-contacted Achuar are largely excluded from the ecological sample to hold the system relatively free of national-society constraints — while Descola denies any Amazon refuge is a true isolate free of European technological, epidemiological, and demographic consequences. The household is chosen as analytic pole because Achuar socialize nature primarily in a domestic setting without outside ritual mediators for propitiation.

**Measurement style.** Godelier-influenced: garden areas and plant densities, stump surface loss, clearing times, kill returns by biotope, time budgets by sex and habitat, manioc potential vs actualization. Qualitative counterpart: house architecture and gendered space; myths of Nunkui, Hummingbird, Musach, game mothers, Tsunki; [[anent]]; charms and dream omens.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**People and place.** Achuar as one of four Jivaroan dialect groups (with Shuar, Aguaruna, Huambisa). 1977 estimates: ~4,500 Achuar (~2,000 Ecuador / ~2,500 Peru) of ~80,000 Jivaro; Ecuador density ~0.17/km²; study sector ~0.12/km² over ~9,000 km² with ~1,110 people (attributed). Historical context includes missions, *patrones*, SIL, Salesians, military and oil frontiers; four interaction sectors distinguished; study limited to dispersed non-mission households.

**Social armature conditioning ecology.** Endogamous nexus of 10–15 houses; Dravidian bilateral cross-cousin marriage; uxorilocality; sororal polygyny; levirate; great men as temporary war leaders; endemic feuding with war mortality on the order of half of male deaths and one-fifth of female deaths (attributed). House as rare stability pole. Domestic violence and female suicide recorded as counters to idyllic *shiir waras*.

**Access and positionality.** Classificatory adoption into Achuar kinship (Wisum as first “brother”); dedication to hosts under “imaginary kinship duties.” Funding CNRS via LAS/Collège de France; Lévi-Strauss director. 1978–79 teaching at PUCE Quito. English preface Paris 1993; French text finished early 1984.

**Data strengths and failure modes.** Strengths: multi-year residence; dual quantitative/qualitative design; biotope contrast (riverine vs interfluvial) built into the sample. Limitations: small time-allocation and hunting samples; OCR-era reprint tables sometimes garbled in digital conversions; ethnographic present freezes a late-1970s political ecology; emic cosmology reconstructed from practices, songs, and myths rather than a single native treatise — as Descola himself notes for societies “without coherent canonical theories of the world.”

### Part I — Sphere of nature (detail)

Descola separates, as “author’s artifice,” the environment as known and represented from techniques of use. Part I maps five geomorphological/pedological regions; Achuar relief, water, and soil taxonomies; house and garden siting rules that use pedological knowledge rather than treating soil as a black box. Lexically, **celestial water** (*yumi*) is distinguished from **terrestrial water** (*entza*); cooking water drawn from the river is semantically “celestialized” before fermentation into manioc beer (*nijiamanch*) — a miniature domestication sequence (attributed). Climate is cut by *yumitin*/*esatin* seasons plus micro-seasons; Pleiades (*Musach*) and flood imagery organize a cosmos in which rain can be linked to human actions on liquids. Upstream (*yaki*) and downstream (*tsumu*) name two biotopes Achuar occupy; Descola uses demography and practice to resist pure ecological-optimization stories (riverine density higher, but interfluve not abandoned). Forest structure (stories, *ikiam* typology, habitat markers tied to the ethnonym *achu*) is known in detail without reducing knowledge to utility alone — a direct counter to neofunctionalist readings of ethnoscience.

**Nature’s beings.** Flora and fauna taxonomies (explicit, pragmatic, latent levels) sit beside an **anthropocentric order**: beings differentiated by possession of *wakan*, capacity as *aents* (persons), food taboos, and relations to *iwianch* spirits. This continuum of sociality is the ethnographic material later re-described as animism; in 1986/1994 the metalanguage is still closer to “socialization” and domestic continuum than to four combinatorial cells.

### Part II — House, garden, forest, river (detail)

**House.** Architecture (*naweamu jea* and variants), framework vocabulary, and spatial organization of conjunction/disjunction structure domestic sociability. Time data: men and women spend ~80–90% of a twenty-four-hour day at the house; men five times as long in forest as women; women four to five times as long in garden as men (attributed). The house is placenta-like and river-linked in symbolic topography; funerary and bodily control practices reinforce it as the microcosm of *shiir waras*.

**Garden (*aja*).** Not a European kitchen garden but a clearing won from climax forest (*takamchau*, “unworked,” applies to virgin forest and, metaphorically, to young women not yet socialized into production). Pioneering slash-and-burn; site choice often foraging-informed; three-tier soil knowledge; riverine vs interfluve techniques differ (including maize slash-mulch *utsank* in appropriate soils). Roughly sixty cultigens and more than 150 varieties; manioc densities on the order of thousands of plants per hectare; stump surface can remove ~17–20% of cultivable surface in interfluve clearings versus negligible loss on clean riverine plots (attributed orders of magnitude). Steel axes shorten clearing relative to stone-age baselines Descola models for contrast. Sexual division is complementary and mythically founded (Hummingbird / Wayus; Nunkui origin of plants): men clear; women plant, weed, harvest; each wife is an independent production cell on her own patch — polygyny does not simply lighten each woman’s load (attributed).

**Gardening magic.** Nunkui is the civilizing mother of cultigens; plant *wakan* classes and blood-sucking manioc imagery make the garden dangerous as well as nurturing. Women identify with Nunkui; plants are children under a consanguineal illusion. Descola resists reducing magic to anxiety-relief: the symbolic preconditions are part of the labor process itself. Prestige and complicity help explain huge variation in cultivated area per consumer across households (hundreds to thousands of m²) without mapping that variation onto consumer count alone.

**Forest.** Gathering taps the surface without compensation; hunting is predation whose conditions hunters do not fully control. Technology complex: blowgun (dozens of hours to make), curare and taboos, spears, firearms where present, traps, dogs under female ownership and Yampani patronage. Returns sample (21 hunters, 84 trips, 1977–78): ~14.2 kg live weight per trip average; peccary dominates meat mass; **no biotope advantage** for riverine edible game once dietary taboos are applied — a direct empirical pressure on Gross/Ross-style scarcity narratives for this case (attributed).

**Affinal nature.** Game mothers (Shaam, Amasank, Jurijri) and *amana* (game leaders) are privileged interlocutors; contact runs through [[anent]], charms (*namur*), and *kuntuknar* dreams. Hunting *anent* address animals as *sai* (affine/brother-in-law); woolly monkey and toucan are emblematic affines. Tone is cajoling — opposite of garden kindred speech. War *anent* identify the warrior with big cats (never hunting *anent*); hunting maintains alliance with guardians despite killing, while war breaches alliance. The forest is “an ideally affinal world” that continuously questions social principles (attributed).

**River.** Third space between garden consanguinity and forest affinity: fishing techniques (trap, harpoon and net, line, *masu*/*timiu* poisons) and biotope balance; Timiu–Masu myth; Tsunki marriages following in-law etiquette; river bed as “marital bed.” Fish lack the soul/*anent* density of game — “Tsunki’s cockroaches” in one register — so the river mediates without simply extending either garden or forest logic.

### Good life, under-exploitation, efficiency (detail)

*Shiir waras* combines domestic peace (normative horizon, not universal fact — Descola records beatings and female suicide) with objectifiable diet and labor metrics. Food-procurement average ~206 minutes/person/day (~35% of a notional ten-hour day). Adolescent boys systematically idle until marriage while girls enter garden work early — cultural composition of the workforce, not physical necessity (attributed). Manioc potential: even the most tightly adapted household in Descola’s comparison still leaves ~20% of manioc capacity unused; prestige gardens may cover needs many times over while using a small fraction of capacity; sweet manioc “stored” in the ground partly explains surplus but not its scale.

Diet accounting (Fig. 47-type): **3,408 kcal** and **104.5 g protein** per capita/day on average (hunting ~604.5 kcal / 59 g protein; fishing ~123 / 22; gardening ~2,680 / 23.5), covering energy needs at ~131% and protein at ~381% of Descola’s Lizot-derived safe maxima. Comparative Amazonian rows (Achuar Peru, Aguaruna, Siona-Secoya, Yanomami) show similarly ample Jivaroan diets — sample not framed as freak abundance. Interfluve protein ~76 g vs riverine ~119 g remains abundance vs overabundance. Labor returns: ~1h22 gardening and ~1h28 hunting per person already yield the bulk of calories and protein; hunting is not abandoned despite “suboptimal” marginal utility because of pleasure and taste, not scarce-means optimization (attributed).

**Positions.** Against Gross (1975) protein-limitation as basin-wide absolute: unperturbed societies exploit small fractions of resources, so they cannot be “limited” by protein in the strong sense. Against Sahlins’s DMP chronic underproduction/failed-household generalization: Achuar combine safety margins with underuse without improvidence-driven chronic failure. Against pure least-effort or biotope-compelled intensification: total work quantity stays roughly balanced across habitats and domestic forms.

**Relational schema (summary).** Gardening: two consanguinity relations with the same object-class (Nunkui identification; mother–plant children). Hunting: two affinity relations with two objects (hunter–game as *sai*; hunter–guardians). Language ranks beings by speech and sociability along a continuum — the Conclusion’s alternative to nature/culture dualism.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**Load-bearing program statement (Preface):**

> "I propose to show that it is illusory and useless to draw a hard line between technical and conceptual determinations. I have therefore given equal heuristic weight to quantitative and qualitative data in this analysis of the relations between one society and its natural environment."

This sentence is the book’s method charter. It refuses the division of labor Descola attributes to anthropology’s dual “geometric” and statistical temperaments, and it licenses the monograph’s awkward marriage of tables and myths — a rhetorical risk he flags when noting that quantification and hermeneutics “rarely make a happy match.”

**Dualism trap (General introduction):**

Descola places the ethnographer “along the seam between two worlds” — mathematized *physis* and cosmos that “tells its tale” through those who make it speak. The Amazon is framed as a European naturalist projection (Oviedo to Buffon) that reduced Amerindians to appendages of nature; ecological determinism is cast as Buffon’s heir. The move is polemical: it makes dualism not a neutral analytic but a historically local Western habit — a move later radicalized in BNC’s genealogy of the Great Divide.

**“Society of nature” (Conclusion):**

The English title’s paradox is cashed out: Achuar ecology is “domestic” because nature is governed by the same social relations as the house. Language/speech becomes a discriminator of degrees of sociality among beings, not a wall between culture and mute nature. Under-exploitation is not ignorance or chronic DMP failure but fulfillment of culturally set goals (*shiir waras*) under a social ceiling on labor.

**Anti-functionalist magic.** Gardening magic and *anent* are not reduced to confidence-boosters; they are constitutive of the relations that make gardens and hunts possible as social acts. This is the ethnographic seed of later claims that identification schemas organize practice upstream of “belief.”

**Labor without “work.”** Categories of practice open with a Marxian historicization of labor as category: Achuar lack a vernacular for work-in-general; closest is *takat* — arduous, tool-mediated effort modeled on gardening, closer to Greek *ponos*/Latin *labor* than to abstract labor-power. Hunting and fishing are not *takat* (men may deny fatigue after a hunt while admitting it after ax work); craft is *najana* (transformation of form without essence-change). When rare petroleum wage jobs appear, speakers switch to Spanish *trabajo*. The rhetorical effect is to let Western readers see their own “work” concept as historical while still permitting rigorous time-budget comparison — another dual navigation.

**Diet as polemic instrument.** The calorie and protein tables are not neutral appendix material. By placing Achuar means above Lizot’s Yanomami maxima and alongside other measured Amazonian samples, Descola turns nutrition into an argumentative weapon against hyperdeterministic ecology and against the stereotype of tropical forest as permanent subsistence crisis. Spoilage of smoked surplus is compared to “the same luxury as hyperindustrialized societies” — a deliberate inversion of scarcity rhetoric.

**Title irony.** *In the Society of Nature* / *La Nature domestique* names the result: nature is already social and domestic, not a wilderness awaiting culture. The English “society of nature” and the French “domestic nature” jointly refuse Buffon’s automaton and the empty-conservation fantasy of an uninhabited Amazon.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

1. **Within Descola’s corpus.** The Achuar book is the empirical base without which BNC’s Achuar illustrations lack warrant. BNC does not replace the ethnography; it re-describes it in the metalanguage of [[modes-of-identification]] and [[modes-of-relation]].
2. **Amazonian debates.** Used as a measured counter-case to protein-limitation and crude pedological determinism for at least one low-density Jivaroan sample; comparative tables enroll Aguaruna, Siona-Secoya, Yanomami data.
3. **Ontological turn.** Garden consanguinity / forest affinity became portable images for Amazonian [[animism]] and for debates with [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]’s [[perspectivism]] (see [[ontological-turn-debate]]).
4. **Method exemplar.** Still cited as a rare successful dual commitment to ecological measurement and structural-symbolic analysis in one monographic architecture.
5. **Political ecology.** Later readers mine under-exploitation and indigenous management against empty-wilderness readings of Amazonia; Descola’s own later epilogue (BNC) resists exporting non-Western ontologies as ready-made Northern green politics.
6. **Kinship theory extended.** The garden/forest consanguinity/affinity split became a portable model for how Amazonian societies scale human relational templates to nonhumans — cited in meta-affinity discussions (Fausto, Viveiros de Castro, Sahlins’s later kinship essays) even when those authors reject or rework Descola’s later typology.
7. **Economic anthropology.** Time-allocation and diet tables enter debates on underproduction, “original affluent society” afterlives, and whether tropical forest economies are best read as constrained or as culturally ceilinged.

**What the book does *not* do (boundaries of impact).** It is not a full Achuar political ethnography of the 1980s–2000s mission, federation, and oil eras; not a comparative four-ontology treatise; not a gender monograph centered on domestic violence (though it records the facts). Readers who need those must go elsewhere or wait for other ingests (Taylor; later essays).

## Current Standing

The study remains the principal English-language Achuar ecological ethnography and the fieldwork foundation of Descola’s comparative program. Its quantitative findings are still load-bearing for arguments about Amazonian diet adequacy and labor under-use in low-density forest societies *of this type*; its relational schemas (Nunkui vs game mothers; consanguinity vs affinity) are permanent furniture of Amazonianist theory. Read it as a **1970s documentation event** plus a **1980s theoretical construction**, not as a timeless people-book.

## Historiography and Sources

The book’s French and English lives matter for citation hygiene: *La Nature domestique* (1986) is the composition finished by early 1984; the 1994 English edition (tr. Nora Scott) is the vault’s primary text, with a 1993 English preface. Cambridge Studies in Social and Cultural Anthropology 93 places it in a series then editing “classic” monographic ethnography. Lévi-Strauss’s endorsement on the jacket is both paratext and genealogy marker — the teacher certifying the student’s anti-dualist monographic experiment. Digital reprints (Internet Archive scan used for this ingest) introduce OCR noise on tables; prose-stated numbers should be preferred when digits conflict.
