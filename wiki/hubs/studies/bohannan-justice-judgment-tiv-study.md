## Question and Intellectual Context

*Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv* asks a deceptively simple question — how do the
[[tiv|Tiv]] of central Nigeria handle disputes? — and answers it in a way designed to
expose and disarm a methodological trap that Bohannan took to be endemic to the
anthropology of law. The trap is the smuggling of one society's **folk system** into
another's ethnography under the guise of neutral analysis. Bohannan's target is the word
"law" itself:

> "Law is a rich' and ambiguous word... To lawyers the law is the core of that highly
> refined system of ideas and practices of which the legal institutions of our own
> society consist."

His claim (attributed) is that the lawyer's "law" is not a science but the
English-speaking world's own **folk system** for social action — "a systematization of
ethnographic fact for purposes of social action" — and that to organize Tiv material into
its categories is "the grossly ethnocentric practice of raising folk systems like 'the
law'... to the status of an analytical system." The book is thus simultaneously an
ethnography of a Nigerian people and a manifesto about how ethnographies of law should be
written (see [[folk-system-and-analytical-system]]).

The disciplinary moment sharpens the point. Bohannan trained at Oxford under
[[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]], and he took his model of the folk/analytical
distinction directly from Evans-Pritchard's [[nuer|Nuer]]: the agnatic genealogy is the
Nuer folk system; "a [[segmentary-lineage-system|lineage system based on the principle of
segmental opposition]]" is Evans-Pritchard's analytical rendering of the same field. The
foil, meanwhile, was [[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]], whose *The Judicial Process among the
Barotse of Northern Rhodesia* (1955) had just done the opposite — deploying refined
jurisprudential concepts (the "reasonable man," a Lozi *corpus juris*) as comparative
tools. Bohannan cites Gluckman explicitly and adversarially: "To speak of a *corpus juris*
among Tiv, as Professor Gluckman speaks of it among Lozi, would imply reference to an
analytical system, not a folk system." The confrontation between the two books became the
charter [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]] of [[legal-anthropology]].

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The research is built on the **case method** — the "trouble-case" as the analytic unit,
inherited from Llewellyn and Hoebel's *The Cheyenne Way* (1941) — applied through
[[participant-observation|participant observation]] of two very different tribunals: the
British-imposed **Grade-D Native Authority court** and the indigenous **moot**. Bohannan's
data were the cases themselves, recorded in the field, supplemented by the court scribes'
registers and by colonial district files (some cases reopened from the 1940s).

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How do Tiv settle disputes, in their own terms? | The trouble-case (*jir*) and the moot | MbaDuku Grade-D court + five recorded moots, southern Tivland | Participant observation; case recording; scribe registers; district files | c. 1949–1953 (cases 1949–52) |
| What organizing concepts frame Tiv dispute? | Tiv jural vocabulary (*jir*, *injô*, *ifer*, *tindi*, *tar*) | Native-language usage in and around cases | Semantic/lexical analysis against dictionaries | same |
| How did colonial rule reshape all this? | Court/administration as institution | MbaDuku as lineage segment *and* gazetted district | Documentary (files, gazettes) + observation | 1914–1953 |

What the methods "section" understates is how much of the reading rests on a handful of
intermediaries. Bohannan's grasp of court procedure depended heavily on the scribes,
particularly Akpwe Suye, and on a clerk-assistant, Iyorkôsu, whose interpretations he
sometimes recorded *against his own* (see below). The MbaDuku court itself was barely
literate: of its four *mbatarev* (judges), only one — Huwa — could read, "and even he only
with the greatest difficulty." The tribunal was chaired by Chief Chenge, son of Kyagba,
about sixty during the fieldwork, a former government messenger and blacksmith.

Bohannan is unusually forthright about sampling bias, and it is worth taking his admission
at face value rather than laundering his cases into a representative dataset. Of his 54
marriage-*jir* he writes:

> "I suspect that in my unconscious selection I omitted many of the guardian v. husband
> jir, because most of them were matters of debt and were simple, uninstructive, and
> dull."

He similarly warns that his translations may have *overstated* the witchcraft content of
Tiv testimony: "I have unavoidably given them undue emphasis in the translation... Tiv
would be shocked at the baldness and lack of finesse with which I have expressed them."
Both admissions belong on the reliability ledger: the case corpus is illustrative, not a
sample, and the *tsav*-saturated texture of the moot chapters is partly an artifact of
translation.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The fieldwork centre, **MbaDuku**, is the book's methodological linchpin because it is two
things at once — "the agnatic descendants of a single ancestor, Aduku" and a colonial
"administrative district... gazetted as a Grade-D court," with a chief recognized by
Government, a population around 10,000, and roughly 2 per cent resident strangers. The
whole argument about folk and analytical systems is grounded in this doubling: the same
territory (*[[tar]]*) is a lineage segment in the Tiv folk system and a "clan" district in
the British one.

The court data are quantifiable at the register level: the MbaDuku *jir* completed
**250–300 registered civil cases per year** (September 1950–August 1952), split into
marriage, debt, and appeal, with marriage cases generally the largest class. Jurisdiction
was capped (civil claims ≤ £25; criminal ≤ 3 months / £10), with appeals running Grade-D
("clan") → Grade-C ("sept") → Grade-B Tribal Council (*Jir Tamen*). But Bohannan
repeatedly warns that the registers *misrepresent* the process — they make the *jir* "look
like a mere debt-collection agency," whereas in practice the *mbatarev* wanted the whole
origin-story of a relationship before ruling. The scribes' civil/criminal split, likewise,
"forms no part of the indigenous folk system," even though it "has ethnographic validity"
because the colonial court had by then made it real in Tiv practice.

The moot data are of a different order entirely: **five fully recorded moots**, all in the
Kunav region of southern Tivland, ranging from a father-son marriage dispute (Ornyiman) to
an eight-hour funeral-cum-heart-examination (the death of Gesa, March 1950, nine months
into the fieldwork — Bohannan's "first understood Tiv funeral"). These are dense
eyewitness narratives, and they carry the book's most important — and most
translation-dependent — material.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding (attributed to Bohannan 1957) | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Tiv organize jural life around *jir* (counteraction), not rules; "Tiv have laws but do not have law" | Native-language analysis; case corpus | Confirmed as the book's enduring thesis; contested as *method* (Gluckman) |
| A *jir*'s force rests on concurrence (*lumun*) + community opinion, not "authority"/"enforcement"; imposed decisions (*sa apela*) are the worst thing said of a *jir* | Court observation; Jir Nos. 7, 24, 25 | Widely cited; consonant with later disputing-process work |
| Tiv classify obligations under *debt* (*injô*), collapsing tort and contract | Debt-*jir* corpus (Ch. VI) | Confirmed as a paradigm case of folk-category analysis |
| Wrongs are *ifer* / *kwaghbo* / *kwaghdang*, not "crime"/"sin"; the civil/criminal split is imported | Ch. VII lexical analysis | Confirmed; the three-way scheme flagged by Bohannan as his own etic construct |
| Institutionalized *feud* did not exist; killing led to inter-lineage war, not kin vengeance | Informant testimony; district files | Notable comparative puzzle (vs. Nuer/Bedouin); Bohannan reserved it for a later monograph |
| Courts handle simple disputes between individuals/groups; moots handle complex, mystically-framed disputes within inescapable kin communities | Five recorded moots (Ch. IX) | The book's second major thesis; heavily cited |
| Colonial "pacification" outlawed self right-enforcement and thereby *created* the need the courts met | District files; Conclusion | Confirmed; a standard example of colonial legal transformation |

The **structure of the *jir*** (Ch. IV) is Bohannan's analytical spine. He decomposes a
hearing into acts Tiv name separately — "calling" (*yila*), witnesses and oaths,
"investigating" (*tôv*, the *mbatarev*'s fact-finding), and "finishing" (*kure*) — noting
that "Tiv have no broad, generalizing concept such as 'procedure'." The decisive move is
his reading of concurrence:

> "It is to misunderstand the Tiv view to say that Tiv courts have or have not the
> 'authority' to 'enforce' a 'decision'... If Tiv judges make a settlement in which both
> parties can concur, there is no problem of 'enforcing a decision'."

and, on the pathology of imposed judgment:

> "for a Tiv Native Authority court [to] use its government-backed authority to enforce a
> decision in which the litigants do not concur... is said to have 'heard the jir by
> force'. This is the worst thing that can be said of any jir. It is much worse, in the Tiv
> view, than the acceptance of bribes."

The **debt** analysis (Ch. VI) is the cleanest demonstration of the method. Rather than
sort cases into tort and contract, Bohannan sorts them under *injô*, and defends the choice
programmatically: "I am not saying that Tiv do not 'have' contract; I am merely saying that
they have it like M. Jourdain spoke prose." The **self-help** typology (Ch. VII) — jural
self-help, "self right-enforcement," and "reprisal" — underwrites the striking negative
finding about the [[feud]]:

> "Institutionalized feud did not exist in Tivland... Killings among Tiv did not lead to
> reprisals by kinsmen, but rather to war between lineages. At the end of such a war, the
> score was counted and remembered on each side and, when the next war broke out, a new
> total was added to the old."

The **moot** chapter (Ch. IX) supplies the second thesis and the book's ethnographic
climax. Bohannan's summary formula:

> "The moots are counteractions to complex disturbances in many social relationships, much
> as the courts are counteractions to simple disturbances in single personal
> relationships. Tiv say that courts deal with minor matters. Moots deal with big matters —
> nothing less than the whole social order."

The moot is where *tsav* (witchcraft substance) is adjudicated — precisely what the
colonial court, barred by the witchcraft ordinance, could not touch. Its jeopardy is
collective: "In courts, individuals touch swem; the individual alone is in jeopardy. In
moots the whole community is involved; the whole community shares the danger."

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Bohannan's rhetoric is built to make the reader *feel* the imposition of alien categories
as a distortion. Three moves do most of the work.

First, the **relentless retention of untranslated Tiv words** — *jir*, *tar*, *injô*,
*ifer*, *tindi*, *tsav*, *swem*, *lumun*, *sa apela*, *mimi*, *vough*. Each retained term is
an argument in miniature: it says that no English word fits, and that reaching for one
(law, debt, crime, guilt, truth) would prejudge the ethnography. The glossary is not
apparatus but thesis.

Second, the **explicit self-labelling of his own constructs as analytical**. When he lays
out "the rules that emerge in Tiv marriage cases," he immediately disowns them as folk
fact: "it is an analytical system for determining some of the norms of Tiv marriage. It is
not a folk system either of Tiv marriage institutions or of Tiv 'substantive law'." He does
the same for his three-way scheme of wrongs (*ifer*/*kwaghbo*/*kwaghdang*), calling it "an
analytical formalization... not done by Tiv themselves." The reader is never allowed to
mistake Bohannan's grid for the Tiv's.

Third, the **use of Western jurisprudence as a foil rather than a frame**. He invokes
Austin and Radcliffe-Brown on "sanction" only to reject the concept for African material
("it seems to me to obscure more than it clarifies"), and Gluckman's Lozi only to deny that
Tiv have a *corpus juris*. Even his one borrowed grand theory — Durkheim — is deployed as
analogy, not explanation: "Tiv moots are what Durkheim would have called mechanically
solidary. They repeat themselves. The courts are organically solidary, and form a
hierarchy." He is careful, at the last, to refuse a functionalist reduction of Tiv
religion: "I do not mean to imply that Tiv religion is no more than an idiom for correcting
the community: it is much more."

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

The book landed as a methodological provocation and was received as one. Its positive
legacy is threefold. It **founded the practice** of describing non-Western legal systems in
their own folk categories, making *jir*, *injô*, and the folk/analytical distinction
standard reference points. It supplied, in the **court/moot contrast**, a durable template
for thinking about formal versus informal dispute institutions and about individual versus
collective liability. And it prefigured the **disputing-process turn** associated with
Laura Nader and the 1960s "ethnography of law," which shifted the unit of analysis from
"law" (rules) to "the dispute" (process) — Bohannan's *jir*-as-counteraction is a direct
ancestor of that move. Beyond law, the [[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk/analytical
distinction]] entered the general methodological vocabulary as one statement of the
emic/etic problem.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

The afterlife *is* the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]]. The near-unanimous criticism, as
Bohannan himself summarized it in 1968, was that the book made comparison impossible:

> "The critics, almost without exception, claimed that the material in the book is presented
> in such a way as to make comparison impossible. Hoebel, Gluckman, Ayoub, Nader and some
> others have claimed that the book is not only itself not comparative, but that it does not
> allow other scholars to use the material in comparative problems."

Gluckman's barb — that Bohannan had achieved "cultural solipsism" — became the debate's
catchphrase; Bohannan drily noted it was "a left-handed compliment, which he did not intend
as such." His reply, staged largely in the 1968 preface and in his review of Gluckman's
*Ideas in Barotse Jurisprudence*, has several prongs. He **declined to add** the comparative
chapter critics wanted, judging the polemic more perishable than the ethnography: "I know no
book in which polemic does not age more quickly than factual and interpretative substance."
He **denied the charge of uniqueness**: "My insistence that Tiv ethnography be seen in its
own terms has been read to mean that I said that Tiv ethnography was unique. I find that I
said nothing so absurd as that." He **relocated the dispute** onto the old opposition between
Tylorian comparison (define categories first, then classify and correlate — "turned to
computers") and Boasian comparison (inquire first, then subdivide and find pattern — "turned
to semantics"), insisting "the two need each other desperately."

Most striking is his **self-criticism**, volunteered where the published critics had missed
it. Having argued that only institutions of judgment — not substantive law — are properly
comparable, he confessed that his own marriage chapters had slid into describing substantive
Tiv marriage law: "I am uncomfortable about the chapters on the marriage jir." That is a
rare instance of an author identifying the precise place his book violated its own method.

No **restudy or statistical replication** exists or is possible: this is a single
interpretive ethnography (`replication_status: not-applicable`), and its material — as
Bohannan repeatedly stressed — documents a colonial order that had already vanished by the
mid-1960s. The internal check the book *does* carry is unusual and worth flagging as
reflexive method: Bohannan repeatedly records his own **disagreements with his Tiv
assistant Iyorkôsu** over the motives of the elders (e.g., in the MbaAkanshi and MbaCie
moots), rather than resolving them into a single authoritative reading — a small,
deliberate refusal of ethnographic omniscience.

## Current Standing

*Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv* is a permanent classic of legal and political
anthropology, read on two levels at once: as ethnography of the Tiv at the fieldwork
moment, and as a still-cited statement of the emic/etic and translation problems. Its
substantive claims are held in attributed voice; the comparison question it raised — whether
a legal system described strictly in its own folk categories can be compared with others —
remains formally **open**, though the disputing-process turn partly dissolved the original
terms by changing the unit of study. Bohannan's date-stamped warning governs all use of the
material: the political system he described is "now gone," and "ethnographers cannot go back
again."

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text is the 1957 first edition and the 1968 second impression with its
substantial new preface (the version ingested is the Routledge 2018 reprint,
*African Ethnographic Studies of the 20th Century*, Vol. 8). The essential companions are
Gluckman's *Judicial Process among the Barotse* (1955) and *The Ideas in Barotse
Jurisprudence* (1965), Bohannan's *Kroeber Anthropological Papers* review of the latter, and
the Nader-edited *Law in Culture and Society* (1969), in which Bohannan's fullest reply to
his critics appeared. Background Tiv sources named in the book include Rupert East's edition
of *Akiga's Story* (1939), R. C. Abraham's *The Tiv People* (1940), and the Bohannans' own
*The Tiv of Central Nigeria* (1953) and *Tiv Farm and Settlement* (1954). For the discipline
context see [[legal-anthropology]]; for the intellectual lineage see [[bohannan-paul]] and
[[evans-pritchard-e-e]].
