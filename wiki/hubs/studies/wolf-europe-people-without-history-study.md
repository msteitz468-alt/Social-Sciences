# Europe and the People Without History — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]]. Source: [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982]]. Thinker: [[wolf-eric-r]].

**Analysis standard:** design and findings tables · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine canonical sections. All findings in **attributed voice** (Wolf 1982 unless noted).

## Question and Intellectual Context

[[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] wrote *Europe and the People Without History* against a mid-to-late twentieth-century division of intellectual labor that, in his view, had turned dynamic interconnections into static, disconnected things. Historians, economists, and political scientists took separate nations as units; sociology divided the world into separate societies; anthropology, once interested in trait diffusion, had settled into distinctive culture cases conceived as integrated bounded systems. The Cold War hardened a further reification: a "modern" West, a communist East (a "disease of modernization" in Rostow's phrase as Wolf cites it), and a traditional Third World to be force-modernized — even, in the ghastliest application Wolf records, by "forced draft urbanization" through bombardment (Huntington).

The book's central assertion is stated on the first page of the Introduction:

> "The central assertion of this book is that the world of humankind constitutes a manifold, a totality of interconnected processes, and inquiries that disassemble this totality into bits and then fail to reassemble it falsify reality. Concepts like 'nation,' 'society,' and 'culture' name bits and threaten to turn names into things."

The ironic title — a nineteenth-century phrase Wolf notes Marx and Engels used against some eastern European separatisms — challenges the premise that only Europeans make history and that anthropology's "primitives" are people without history. The 1400 baseline is chosen precisely so that European expansion is seen to encounter already complex, interlocked histories, not empty space or timeless tribes. As the 1997 preface clarifies, "Europe" can also be read as shorthand for the growth of the capitalist [[mode-of-production|mode of production]].

The book is not a world chronicle and not a pure history of capital. Wolf's objective (1997) is to show that societies and cultures cannot be understood until visualized in mutual interrelationships in space and time — locating anthropological subjects in "fields of force generated by systems of power exercised over social labor."

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Scope | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| How did European expansion and capitalism remake global populations anthropology studied as isolated? | Interconnected processes; modes of deploying social labor | Secondary ethnohistory across Americas, Africa, Asia, Europe | [[historical-political-economy]]; three-mode typology; commodity-chain narratives | c. 1400–20th c. |

There is **no primary fieldwork** for this volume. Evidence is synthetic: archaeological trade-good horizons, demography of the Great Dying, company and colonial records as filtered through specialist secondary literature, ethnohistoric reconstructions of Huron, [[iroquois|Iroquois]], Asante, Kongo, Mughal India, etc. Wolf wrote as an anthropologist "rather than as a professional historian" (1997) but insists history matters.

**Method commitments (attributed):**

1. **Political economy** means historically evolving foundations of polities and markets — not only fiscal techniques of market economics (1997).
2. **Mode of production** concepts are "suppositions that orient and guide research," not Systems-Marxist laws of universal generality (1997).
3. **Functionalism** can be useful for non-manifest connections, but configurations do not return to equilibrium; they are marked by tension, contradiction, and fracture (1997).
4. Marxian tools (social labor, mercantile vs industrial capital, long waves, labor theory of value) are "intellectual tools, not ultimate verities" (Afterword).

Structure: Part One (Connections) — Introduction, World in 1400, Modes of Production, Europe prelude; Part Two (In Search of Wealth) — Iberians in America, Fur Trade, Slave Trade, Orient; Part Three (Capitalism) — Industrial Revolution, Crisis and Differentiation, Movement of Commodities, New Laborers; Afterword on society, culture, and power.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The "site" is the globe after 1400 as reorganized by European maritime expansion, with a pre-expansion baseline of Old World and New World networks (trade, empire, pastoral nomadism, tributary civilizations, kin-ordered peripheries). Data quality varies by region with the secondary literature available to Wolf c. 1974–1981. Specialist revision of chapter-level detail is expected and does not automatically falsify the unit-of-analysis argument.

**Production context:** composed while Wolf taught at Lehman College and the CUNY Graduate Center; dedicated to Sydel Silverman; written in the shadow of 1968's call for more history in anthropology (1982 preface) and read after the collapse of "really existing" socialism with a careful "Marxian not Marxist" disclaimer (1997).

## Findings in Detail

**Load-bearing mode definitions (Wolf 1982, Ch. 3):**

> "Each major way of doing so constitutes a mode of production—a specific, historically occurring set of social relations through which labor is deployed to wrest energy from nature by means of tools, skills, organization, and knowledge."

On the capitalist mode:

> "There is no such thing as mercantile or merchant capitalism, therefore. There is only mercantile wealth. Capitalism, to be capitalism, must be capitalism-in-production."

On the tributary mode: primary producers keep means of production; tribute by political/military means; Asiatic and feudal as poles of one continuum (term from Amin).

On kin-ordered mode: labor locked through kinship; "primitive" rejected as empty; chiefdoms reanalyzed via labor command not redistributive typology alone.

**Mode vs market (Ch. 10):** Wolf endorses Mandel's articulated multi-modal system under a capitalist world market and criticizes Frank and Wallerstein for collapsing mode into market and dating world capitalism from the fifteenth century. For Wallerstein (as Wolf reads him), deployment of social labor is secondary. Wolf: capitalist mode late eighteenth century; market escalation is fueled by that mode.

**Afterword synthesis:**

> "There are thus no 'contemporary ancestors,' no people without history, no peoples—to use Lévi-Strauss's phrase—whose histories have remained 'cold.'"

> "'A culture' is thus better seen as a series of processes that construct, reconstruct, and dismantle cultural materials, in response to identifiable determinants."

> "The ability to bestow meanings—to 'name' things, acts, and ideas—is a source of power."



### Part Two — Wealth-seeking ethnohistories (compressed findings, attributed to Wolf)

**Iberians in America (Ch. 5).** Conquest after Tordesillas; the **Great Dying** as demographic catastrophe amplified by social collapse, forced labor, and reorganization of supply — not disease alone as a free-floating natural fact. Silver (Potosí, Mexico) structures the Atlantic and Pacific (Manila galleon) siphon of wealth; constrained official trade coexists with contraband. Labor forms: encomienda, repartimiento, mita; the **hacienda** as sink of land, labor, and credit; *repúblicas de indios* and civil-religious hierarchies as colonial reorganizations of indigenous community — continuous with Wolf's earlier closed-corporate interests. Brazil and Caribbean sugar; pirates, "colonial tribes," and **maroon** communities as margins of the system.

**Fur trade (Ch. 6).** Eurasian and North American trades reorganize [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] labor: family hunting territories, middleman wars, putting-out dependency on European manufactures (cloth, guns). Cases: Abenaki, Huron, [[iroquois|Iroquois]] (trade goods by 1570s; near-total replacement of native manufactures by 1670s in some Onondaga sites — Wolf's archaeological illustration of connection), Great Lakes ethnogenesis (Ottawa–Ojibwa), Midewiwin, Pontiac, Athabascans, Plains horse pastoralists and pemmican economy, Red River Métis, Northwest Coast potlatch still "banking" kinship, Siberian *iasak*. Terminal putting-out: specialization and debt without capitalist purchase of labor-power.

**Slave trade (Ch. 7).** Joint European demand and African supply-side political economy. Volumes and periodization as of Wolf's secondary sources (not updated demography). Mechanisms of enslavement: war capture, judicial sale, pawnship — elite surplus slavery and "big man" accumulation. Regional systems: Gold Coast/Asante and firearms; Oyo, Dahomey, Benin; Niger Delta canoe houses, Efik/Ekpe, Aro; Kongo, Imbangala, Luba–Lunda, eastern ivory/slaving, Bemba. Shatter zones and unitary-process thesis: treating each group as a self-sufficient tribe misreads both past and present. Ties to Williams thesis on profits and industrial capital are recorded as positions in the literature Wolf channels.

**Orient (Ch. 8).** Asian tributary states and long-standing sea-lane commerce; Portuguese factories, Dutch VOC, English Company. Mughal mansabdars, jagirs, zamindars as tributary claims on social labor. Plassey to Company rule: army/bureaucracy; land tenure shifts toward alienable property (Permanent Settlement) while rural "strong men" may keep tributary relations as market entrepreneurs. 1857 rebellion. Opium-for-tea: India as relay in China trade; Pacific extension. India inserted into world capitalism without simple conversion of all producers into free wage labor.

### Part Three — Capitalism (compressed findings)

**Industrial Revolution (Ch. 9).** English textile transformation: capital + labor-power, mechanization, factory; competition with Dutch and Indian textiles; cotton South slavery and Indian Removal as unfree raw-material regimes feeding free-labor factories; Egypt cotton; Bombay industry; railroads and shipping; free wage labor as contingent historical conjuncture, not natural baseline.

**Crisis and differentiation (Ch. 10).** Falling rate of profit and realization crisis as structural models (not literal every crisis); imperialism debates (Hobson–Lenin–Luxemburg) treated critically; differentiation within the mode (long waves, mercantile subordination, labor reserves); differentiation of states. Core theoretical load: Mandel articulation vs Frank/Wallerstein market definition (see Findings table and [[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]).

**Movement of commodities (Ch. 11).** Great Depression of 1873 and British hegemony crisis; regional specialization; plantations vs cash-cropping small farms ("peasants became farmers"); foodstuffs (wheat, rice, meat, bananas); industrial crops (rubber, palm oil); stimulants (sugar, coffee, tea, cocoa, opium); gold and diamonds and southern African transformations. Conclusion: Mandelian multi-modal system under capitalist market dominance.

**New laborers (Ch. 12).** Capital requires wage labor but produces segmented labor markets and a plurality of working classes, not one homogeneous proletariat. Urbanization; migration waves; U.S. immigration; South African mines, compounds, Reserves, color bar; East Indian indenture as "second slavery" (Tinker via Wolf); European subsidized migrants (e.g. Rio Claro Italians); Chinese coolie trade and Singapore; critique of "plural society" models that naturalize ethnic segments. **Race** as exclusionary ascription vs **ethnicity** as segment placement within the labor hierarchy — both historically produced, not primordial. Postwar export platforms noted at the horizon of Wolf's narrative.


## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Irony of the title** — weaponizes a Eurocentric phrase against Eurocentrism; 1997 preface admits some readers missed it.
2. **Billiard-ball metaphor** — names-into-things; East/West/Third World as Cold War instruments.
3. **1400 traveler device** — narrative baseline of interconnection before Europe "orchestrates" global rhythm.
4. **Parsimonious trinity of modes** — explicit anti-classification stance ("utility does not lie in classification").
5. **Commodity chapters as proof structure** — not encyclopedic coverage but demonstration that connection remakes social labor.
6. **Afterword culture theory** — answers charges of economism by relocating meaning inside modes and power, not by abandoning political economy.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Founding charter** of Anglophone [[anthropological-political-economy]] and a bridge to global history.
- **Teaching staple** for critiquing the ethnographic present and bounded culture.
- **Eriksen 2010** situates the book for multi-sited and global anthropology; suggests extensions (e.g. remittances) continuing Wolf's project.
- **Dialogue with** [[world-systems-analysis]], dependency theory, French Marxist anthropology (Meillassoux, Godelier), Mandel, Amin — critical appropriation not discipleship.
- Influenced commodity-chain, plantation, and labor-migration literatures; sits adjacent to Mintz's sugar history (collection-related).



### Reception timeline (attributed positions)

1. **Immediate anthropological uptake (1980s):** The book became a required counterweight to village monographs and culture-and-personality isolation. Programs in historical anthropology and political economy assigned it as charter.
2. **World-systems dialogue:** Agreement on connection and European expansion; disagreement on whether plantations/slavery are "capitalist" by market integration alone. The dispute is structural to late-20th-c. critical social science, not a personal quarrel.
3. **Interpretive / postmodern critique:** Concurrent *Writing Culture* (1986) moment asked for textuality, authority, and voice. Wolf's Afterword is a partial prebuttal — culture as process and naming as power — but does not do discourse analysis of ethnographic texts.
4. **1997 self-positioning:** Wolf answers Systems/Promethean misreadings; reaffirms mode concepts as guides; clarifies title irony; sketches forms of capitalism and culture without rewriting the book.
5. **2010 Eriksen reframing:** Foreword for global/multi-sited anthropology; argues Wolf remains resources for studying glocal connections (including remittances) beyond Wallerstein/Frank dualisms.
6. **Specialist erosion and survival:** Africanist, Americanist, and South Asianist literatures have revised volumes, mechanisms, and demography; the billiard-ball critique and social-labor hinge survive as meta-methodological claims.

## Extended Design Critique — Study as Conducted vs Advertised

**Advertised** (title + packaging): a history of Europe's relation to "people without history," inviting a Europe-centered expansion narrative.

**As conducted:** a three-part argument that (1) social science units of analysis are ideological artifacts of the mid-nineteenth-century split from political economy; (2) a 1400 world already dense with tributary civilizations, mercantile circuits, pastoral empires, and kin-ordered systems must be the baseline; (3) European expansion and later industrial capitalism reorganize those systems through commodities and labor without ever making isolation true.

What the methods section (distributed across preface, Ch. 1, Ch. 3, Afterword) **omits** relative to a fieldwork monograph: sampling frames for secondary sources; explicit inclusion/exclusion rules for cases; quantitative meta-analysis of demography; archival primary research. What it **includes** that monographs omit: intersystemic comparison as the object, not the residual; mode concepts as selection principles for which relations matter.

**Scale:** deliberately macro/global, with meso ethnohistoric set pieces. Risk of ecological fallacy (reading local meanings off global circuits) is real; Wolf's mitigation is the insistence that local groups are already inside fields of force — the micro is not pure. Scale mismatch is a standing lint concern for any page that cites *EPWH* as if it were village ethnography.

**Voice discipline for the wiki:** every regional number and mechanism is Wolf's synthesis of others as of ~1980. Promote to wiki voice only the meta-methodological claims that multiple independent traditions now share (interconnection of the modern world; rejection of pure ethnographic present for contact-era peoples) — and even those stay attributed when they name mechanisms.

### Ch. 1 — Social sciences as false models (detail)

Wolf reconstructs how political economy (wealth of nations, classes, production/distribution within and between polities) was split into specialized disciplines that reified their fragments. Economics and political science professionalized market and state as if separable from historical social labor; sociology inherited Gemeinschaft/Gesellschaft and society-as-bounded-object; anthropology's turn from diffusion to integrated culture cases completed the billiard set. Modernization theory then sorted the billiard balls by color. The political stakes are not abstract: naming the Vietnamese countryside as premodern licensed destruction as development.

This chapter is therefore as much **sociology of knowledge** as global history. It explains why an anthropologist had to write a book that looks like history and political economy.

### Ch. 2 — World in 1400 (detail)

The imaginary traveler device surveys:

- **Physical constants:** east–west mountain chain; dry belt of desert and steppe favoring pastoralism; cultivable "archipelagos."
- **Trade:** dual spheres of local provisioning and elite luxury with political referent (Schneider); long-distance bulk where water transport allows.
- **Pastoral nomads:** protection rents, administrative problems for agrarian states (Lattimore).
- **Near East, Africa, Asia:** Ottoman, West African gold empires (Ghana–Mali–Songhay), East African coast and Zimbabwe, Indian caste as political economy of dominant lineages and little kingdoms, Chinese hydraulic/gentry order and Ming maritime withdrawal, SE Asian inland vs harbor states and Malacca.
- **New World:** Andean verticality (Murra), Inca expansion from Late Intermediate, Mesoamerican postclassic fragmentation, Mississippian and northern woodlands including Iroquoian trajectories.

Closing thesis: the world Europe would reorganize was already a world of linkages; "precontact isolate" is an ethnographic-present artifact.

### Ch. 3 — Modes as instruments not stages (detail)

Wolf inventories Marx's many modes and discards taxonomic ambition. Three modes for parsimony. Explicitly not evolutionary. Explicitly not society labels. Cultural evolution operates on interconnected social fields. Capitalism first in exposition though last historically. Mercantile wealth section is the hinge for the whole book's periodization: trade after 1400 enlarges markets but tributary mode remains dominant until the eighteenth century; merchants buy cheap/sell dear, use political monopoly, create peonage and putting-out, expand slavery — and still do not buy labor-power as capital-in-production.

Chiefdoms: Service's redistributional intermediate type is replaced by inquiry into whether chiefs can break kin-ordered limits. Variation (Northwest Coast tais vs Zulu induna vs Mongol khan) is power potential, not a single stage.

### Afterword — Society and culture rebuilt

Society: changing alignments without fixed boundaries; continuity and consensus problematic, not assumed. Culture: from nation-legitimating integral spirit to fluid processes; Lowie's matter-of-fact usage vs secondary interpretation; hegemony as redundancy across domains; ideology within modes, not pure myth-thinking (contra Lévi-Strauss as Wolf reads him); ecology of collective representations with selection by power; where ideology fails, force.

This is Wolf's answer to the charge that political economy cannot speak culture. Whether it satisfies interpretive anthropology is a position dispute; that he offered a structured answer is a textual fact.


## Current Standing

Still required reading for historical and political anthropology. The interconnection thesis and people-without-history critique remain robust pedagogical tools. Three-mode vocabulary is widely used with caveats about breadth. Mode-vs-market dispute with world-systems remains open. Chapter-level demography and regional narratives should be cross-checked against post-1982 specialist literature. The program claim — power over social labor as hinge for locating "peoples" in history — remains an active research stance.



## Additional Verbatim Passages with Brief Analysis

**On names into things (Introduction):**

> "By turning names into things we create false models of reality. By endowing nations, societies, or cultures with the qualities of internally homogeneous and externally distinctive and bounded objects, we create a model of the world as a global pool hall in which the entities spin off each other like so many hard and round billiard balls."

Analysis: The metaphor does double work — methodological (units of analysis) and political (Cold War sorting). It licenses the rest of the book: if balls are false, webs are true.

**On mode ≠ society (Ch. 3):**

> "The three modes that we employ should not be taken as schemes for pigeonholing societies. The two concepts—mode of production and society—pertain to different levels of abstraction."

Analysis: Prevents the common classroom error of labeling "the Asante = kin-ordered" as a total society essence. A society can host multiple modes; a mode can span societies.

**On Mandel articulation (Ch. 10):**

> "an articulated system of capitalist, semi-capitalist, and pre-capitalist relations of production, linked to each other by capitalist relations of exchange and dominated by the capitalist world market."

Analysis: Wolf's preferred systemic image — hierarchy and heterogeneity without dissolving mode distinctions. This is how fur-trade putting-out and factory cotton can coexist in one historical system.

**On society rebuilt (Afterword):**

> "'Societies' emerge as changing alignments of social groups, segments, and classes, without either fixed boundaries or stable internal constitutions."

Analysis: Aligns Wolf with relational sociology and against organismic functionalism — while still using "function" pragmatically for non-manifest connections (1997).
