# Tsing, *Friction* — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[tsing-friction-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All substantive findings in **attributed voice**. Edition for grounding: French 2020 La Découverte translation (*Friction: Délires et faux-semblants de la globalité*, trans. Pignarre & Stengers, preface Nastassja Martin); line pointers refer to the session cache of that translation, quoted verbatim in French with sense given in English gloss where needed.

*Friction: An Ethnography of Global Connection* (Princeton, 2005) is one of the founding texts of the anthropology of globalization — an ethnography built not around a single bounded community but around the "awkward, unequal, unstable, and creative" places where projects of world-making rub against each other. This page reconstructs the study's design, its central concepts, and the afterlife of the book as method and vocabulary.

## Question and Intellectual Context

Tsing wrote against two mirror-image 1990s stories: a globalization literature that imagined capital, ideas, and people moving in a new era of frictionless, unimpeded flow, and a culturalist countercurrent content to celebrate local autonomy as a self-sufficient answer to globalizing power. Her own account of the book's motivating image is the wheel: "Une roue tourne parce qu'elle rencontre la surface de la route; en l'air, elle ne va nulle part" — "A wheel turns because it meets the surface of the road; in the air, it goes nowhere." Friction, in this sense, is not an obstacle to global connection but its condition of possibility: "La friction est requise pour garder le pouvoir global en mouvement" ("Friction is required to keep global power moving"), and — the line that forecloses any reading of friction as a synonym for resistance — "Friction n'est pas synonyme de résistance. L'hégémonie se fait mais se défait tout aussi bien dans la friction" ("Friction is not synonymous with resistance. Hegemony is made, and also unmade, in friction").

The empirical provocation is Indonesian deforestation under and after the New Order: destruction too rapid and chaotic to be explained by gradual population growth or market expansion; ordinary people — loggers, migrants, officials — sometimes joining forces with distant firms to unmake the very landscapes that sustained them; and, in the same decades, a democratic environmental politics that linked cities and countryside in ways no one had planned. Tsing's stated aim in the book's program is to attend to "la prise opérée par la rencontre" — "the grip exerted by the encounter" (rendered in Tsing's English original as *the grip of worldly encounter*) — rather than to either a triumphalist story of global integration or a romantic story of pure local need.

The book positions itself against several interlocutors at once, staged rather than fully genealogized: globalization theorists narrating a single planetary transition; cultural-studies readings of capitalism (in the Jameson lineage) against social-science efforts (Yanagisako, Mitchell) to locate capitalism's historical specificity; universalist theories of resistance (Hardt and Negri) against situated, place-based accounts of power (Massey, Gibson-Graham); and, in the book's Coda, an explicit dialogue with Pheng Cheah and Neferti Tadiar over whether area-studies particularism can dispense with universal claims at all. Tsing extends her earlier *In the Realm of the Diamond Queen* (1993) argument that even "isolated" Meratus culture was long since made in networks — and attempts the harder reverse move here: showing global forces themselves as aggregates of local/global interaction, not a force descending from outside.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

*Friction* is not a single-site ethnography and does not present itself as one; it is what the wiki labels [[ethnography-of-global-connection|an ethnography of global connection]] — a patchwork design that follows a set of linked "zones of cultural friction" (a mountain forest, a scandal-driven mining company, a national student subculture, a logging campaign) rather than a bounded community observed end to end. This is a design decision, not a fallback: Tsing explicitly renounces holist coverage and asks the reader to accept results that look unlike a classical monograph while remaining ethnographic as a mode of learning.

**As conducted, not only as advertised.** Fieldwork was deep and then deliberately patchy: two years of Meratus immersion (1979–81), followed by shorter return seasons (often North American summers) triangulating Jakarta, provincial towns, and the mountains as funding, health, permits, and teaching allowed — a rhythm the book's methods discussion does not dwell on but which shapes what counts as "current" in each chapter. Access to activists, nature-lovers, donors, and researchers ran through pre-existing affinity chains rather than a constructed sample; by contrast, sustained access inside corporate forestry and mining firms largely failed, leaving frontier capital's interior comparatively thin (see below). Some material was collaboratively produced rather than purely extracted — nature-lover friends sent clippings and wrote short essays for Tsing; Uma Adang's plant-naming sessions are presented as joint list-making, not a neutral inventory, and are explicitly flagged by Tsing as reflecting a single interlocutor's memory at a moment in time, not an authoritative botanical record. Pseudonyms mask private individuals and villages; major public figures and cities are named. Tsing states her own writing position as an "economy of the viewpoint of despair" — placing frontier distress (smoke, poison, confiscation, sensory assault) at the analytic center rather than treating it as an unwritable remainder outside a hybridity-celebrating narrative.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**The Meratus mountains, South Kalimantan.** Highland [[meratus-dayaks|Meratus Dayak]] communities living in patchwork swidden-and-forest landscapes, subject to a regional *orang bukit* ("hill people") stigma from lowland and coastal Kalimantan society, carrying long-standing trade knowledge, and increasingly pressured by 1990s resource-boom incursions — vignettes centered on the village of Kalawan and on Uma Adang, a Meratus woman whose remarks on regional violence and knowledge-making recur through the book. Tsing is careful to distinguish the Meratus case from the ethnically coded Dayak–Madurese violence she reports occurring elsewhere in Kalimantan in the same period — an identity caution the wiki preserves.

**Indonesian nature-lovers (*pecinta alam*).** Student mountaineering and search-and-rescue clubs, descended in part from the Mapala UI lineage, documented through pseudonymous present-tense portraits of individual members (Sri, Budi, Dwi, Hamam in Tsing's Yogyakarta material). Tsing situates the clubs within New Order antipolitical nationalism, consumer adventure culture (including its Marlboro-sponsored forms), and the path by which some members moved from recreational nature-loving into environmental NGO activism.

**Frontier political economy.** Japanese *sōgō shōsha* trading houses and New Order timber concessions from the 1970s onward, the figure of Bob Hasan and APKINDO, and — the book's structural argument about frontier legality — the routine collaboration of legal and illegal actors (concessionaires, officials, small-scale illegal loggers, armed claimants around swallow-nest caves and gold-panning operations) in dismantling prior claims to land. Later material follows the 1997 fires and smoke and a Kalimantan-to-India coal chain documented through a 2000 interview.

**Speculative drama.** The Bre-X Busang gold-mining scandal, read as the paradigm case of what the wiki records as [[economy-of-appearances|the economy of appearances]], set against Freeport as a contrasting case of corporate self-presentation as "civilizing."

**Movements and policy packages.** National environmental organizing under WALHI amid state repression; the Kedung Ombo dam displacement (roughly 30,000 people); the Indorayon pulp-mill lawsuit; the 1986 Manggur logging-protest campaign; the growth of *masyarakat adat* (indigenous customary-rights) organizing and AMAN after 1998; Ford Foundation-funded community-based natural resource management (CBNRM) programs; and the international circulation and backlash around charismatic activist narratives (from Chico Mendes to Chipko, and the countercharge of Sarawak "eco-imperialism").

**Data limits, stated plainly.** Access inside logging and mining firms was thin, leaving the "capital" side of several frontier chapters reconstructed more from documents, press coverage, and outsider interviews than from insider fieldwork; list-making interludes reflect single-interlocutor memory rather than systematic inventory, a limitation Tsing herself flags. This ingest works from the 2020 French translation rather than the English original.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. Friction against the frictionless era.** The wheel-and-road and rubbing-sticks images do double rhetorical work — they explain how motion (global connection) happens at all, and they insist that the specific surface encountered (a particular road, a particular pair of sticks) shapes what kind of motion results. Rubber is Tsing's extended illustration: "Extorqué aux Indigènes américains, le caoutchouc a été volé puis planté partout dans le monde par des paysans et sur des plantations, avant d'être imité et remplacé par les chimistes... C'est à ces vicissitudes que j'ai donné le nom de frictions" — rubber, extorted from Indigenous Amazonians, stolen and replanted worldwide by peasants and plantations, then imitated and displaced by chemists and eventually sport-utility tires; "it is to these vicissitudes that I have given the name friction." By explicitly denying that friction equals resistance, Tsing keeps both the making *and* unmaking of hegemony inside a single analytic — refusing both a pure-domination account of global capitalism and a pure-autonomy romance of local resistance.

**2. Engaged universals as sticky, unfinished links.** Universals are studied, in Tsing's formulation, not "as truths or lies" but as claims that travel and "stick" unevenly: "Les universels engagés voyagent au travers des différences, se chargent de contenu et se modifient chemin faisant... Ils ne réussissent pourtant jamais à remplir toutes leurs promesses d'universalité" — engaged universals travel through difference, take on content, and change en route, yet never fully deliver on their promise of universality. Tsing frames this as a negotiated position between anthropological cultural relativism (which she describes having been trained into, and found an unproductive dead end for talking with non-anthropologists) and an uncritical universalism; she credits Judith Butler's account of the "cultural site of the utterance of universality" and its translation politics, and Ernesto Laclau's account of how particular class claims are made to stand for universal community goals, as the theoretical resources behind the move.

**3. The resource frontier as a traveling, self-consciously unplanned project.** Tsing's frontier chapter explicitly stages the Kalimantan material against the historiography of the North American frontier and against a general theory of "frontières de ressources": "Une zone-frontière est un bord dans l'espace et le temps: c'est le règne du pas encore — pas encore cartographié, pas encore régulé... même dans sa planification, une zone-frontière est pensée comme non planifiée" — a resource frontier is an edge in space and time, the realm of not-yet-mapped and not-yet-regulated, one that is imagined as unplanned even in the moment of its planning. Frontiers, on this account, are not natural edges but "projects for making... geographic and temporal experience," structurally reliant on collusion between "legitimate and illegitimate" actors — soldiers and bandits, gangsters and corporations — that "efface the separation between law and theft, governance and violence, use and destruction."

**4. Performance in its double economic and theatrical sense.** Chapter 2 turns on the pun built into "performance" — financial performance and theatrical performance — with the Bre-X scandal read as an extreme case that reveals the ordinary workings of speculative capital rather than an aberration from them; the [[economy-of-appearances|economy of appearances]] links Canadian junior-mining promoters, Indonesian elite brokers, and regional frontier culture without collapsing the scandal into a single villain's fraud.

**5. Inhabiting the gap rather than resolving it.** Chapter 5's "weediness" thesis holds that the dualisms structuring official land categories — nature/culture, cultivated/wild, "empty" state forest versus settled land — produce illegible zones that are, in practice, exactly where Meratus social and ecological life happens; the method is to enter and describe these gaps ethnographically rather than force the landscape into either expert planning category.

**6. Collaboration without consensus.** Tsing's discussion of the word's double sense — colleagues working toward shared ends versus wartime "collaborators" working with an occupying power — frames Chapters 6–7's account of campaigns that succeed on partial, non-identical understandings among their participants: "Plus elles sont différentes et plus elles doivent chercher à trouver des manières de comprendre la situation qui se recoupent un tant soit peu" — the more different collaborating parties are, the more they must search for ways of understanding the situation that overlap even partially. The 1986 Manggur logging protest is her central case: provincial officials, national capital-based observers, and Meratus leaders told three substantially incompatible stories about the same forest, yet their partial overlap on "community" was enough to halt logging temporarily and draw donor attention — remaking both the landscape and the "real village" for subsequent ecotourism and funding purposes.

**7. Fragments, not a whole pizza.** The Coda states the book's own methodological creed directly: "La globalisation n'est pas livrée sous la forme d'une pizza ronde et entière... Les connexions globales sont faites de fragments... Certains fragments réussissent à se donner l'allure du tout" — globalization does not arrive as a whole, round pizza; global connections are made of fragments, some of which manage to give themselves the appearance of the whole. The book's final lines return to friction directly: "Ce sont des connexions globales fabriquées et embrouillées, en friction. S'intéresser à de telles frictions pourrait cependant bien permettre de rouvrir le mystère de notre époque" — these are global connections that are manufactured, entangled, in friction; attending to such frictions may nonetheless reopen the mystery of our era.

**Rhetorical texture.** Interludes — inventories, a sign-language exchange along the coal chain, lists of nature-lovers' hiking gear — interrupt the monograph's expository register; present-tense ethnographic portraits of individual nature-lovers sit beside numbered analytic conclusions in Chapter 1; the book persuades cumulatively, through the accumulation of awkward, non-generalizable cases, rather than through a single decisive test.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Friction* is a charter text for the concept of **[[friction]]** in the anthropology of globalization and environmental politics, and is frequently credited with naming a distinct genre, **[[ethnography-of-global-connection|the ethnography of global connection]]**, that extended the multi-sited ethnography debates of the 1990s (associated with George Marcus) into a single, sustained study built around a set of linked frictional encounters rather than a set of dispersed field sites loosely tied by a single commodity or idea. It anchors a resource-frontier and political-ecology citation cluster around [[capitalist-frontier|the capitalist frontier]] and later "salvage frontier" scholarship; a speculative-capitalism cluster around [[economy-of-appearances|the economy of appearances]]; and a scale-and-universals cluster around [[scale-making]] and [[traveling-universals|traveling universals]] read alongside geography and science-and-technology-studies work on scale. Within Indonesian studies it is a standard reference for New Order-era student nature-loving, environmental NGO politics, and *adat* land-claim organizing, and is regularly taught alongside other ethnographies of Indonesian resource politics and the post-1998 *reformasi* opening.

The book's reach beyond anthropology proper is part of its reception history: geographers picked up "scale-making" as a term for the practical, contested work of establishing that a claim or process operates at a given scale (local, national, global) rather than treating scale as a preexisting container; political ecologists absorbed "frontier as project" as a corrective to accounts that treat resource frontiers as simply discovered or naturally occurring; and science-and-technology-studies readers engaged the "economy of appearances" alongside performativity accounts of finance. Tsing's later multispecies and Anthropocene-oriented work, *The Mushroom at the End of the World* (2015, ingested as [[tsing-mushroom-2015]] and analyzed at [[tsing-mushroom-study]]), is widely read — by Tsing's own later framing and by reviewers — as extending the frictional, patchwork mode of connection-tracing developed in *Friction* beyond the human, into matsutake mushroom supply chains and disturbed Pacific Northwest forests; the "fragments, not a whole pizza" method of the 2005 Coda anticipates the assemblage-thinking vocabulary (patches, disturbance, contamination as collaboration) that organizes the 2015 book.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: not-applicable` — the book is not a testable-finding study subject to replication in the survey or experimental sense; its afterlife runs through method and concept uptake and through positions in the secondary literature (recorded here, not adjudicated):

1. **Does "collaboration without consensus" understate durable domination?** Some readers have asked whether Tsing's emphasis on productive misunderstanding — especially in the Ford Foundation-funded CBNRM material — risks softening how thoroughly donor agendas can co-opt local campaigns rather than merely being refracted by them.
2. **Generalizability of the frontier and friction concepts.** A standing question is how much of the analysis is bound to Indonesian New Order specificity (its particular legal-illegal collusion, its particular state) versus how portable "friction" and "frontier-as-project" are as general analytic tools — the concepts have in practice traveled widely, which some read as vindication and others as evidence the terms have become loosely elastic.
3. **Relation to multi-sited ethnography.** Commentators have debated whether "ethnography of global connection" names a genuinely distinct method or restates George Marcus's multi-sited ethnography under new vocabulary.
4. **Thinness on capital's interior.** Because sustained access inside logging and mining firms failed, several reviewers have noted that the frontier chapters reconstruct corporate and state actors more from outside documentation than from the kind of close access given to Meratus and nature-lover material — an asymmetry Tsing's own methods discussion partially anticipates but does not fully resolve.
5. **Continuity versus break with *Mushroom*.** Whether *The Mushroom at the End of the World* extends the same analytic program to nonhuman actors or marks a substantive shift toward multispecies ontology is itself a live question in Tsing's reception, not a settled continuity.

No major failed-replication episode attaches to the book; it functions in the field as a method-and-concept charter rather than as a finding subject to confirmation or overturning. A further methodological critique worth recording explicitly, since it bears on how the wiki should treat the book's claims: the patchwork, friction-tracing design that makes *Friction* persuasive as ethnography is the same design that resists systematic causal generalization — Tsing does not claim, and the book's own method disallows, that any single case (Manggur, Bre-X, the Yogyakarta clubs) demonstrates a portable causal law about frontiers, universals, or collaboration in general. Readers who extract "findings" from the book for comparative or causal use should treat each case as an illustration of a conceptual apparatus (friction, engaged universals, collaboration without consensus) rather than as a tested proposition — a distinction some secondary uses of the book blur.

## Current Standing

*Friction* remains widely assigned and cited as a model for studying globalization ethnographically without assuming either frictionless integration or a fully autonomous local outside it. Its most durable portable vocabulary — friction, the frontier as project, the economy of appearances, engaged universals, scale-making, collaboration without consensus, and the "fragments, not a whole pizza" method — continues to organize work in political ecology, resource-frontier studies, and the ethnography of globalization.
