## Question and Intellectual Context

*Analytical Archaeology* asks how a discipline that already manipulates attributes, artefacts, types, assemblages, and cultures can continue to do so as "an intuitive skill — an inexplicit manipulative dexterity learned by rote," and what would be required to turn those tacit operations into **declared models**, **defined entities**, and **central theory**. Clarke's opening diagnosis is not that archaeologists lack data or techniques, but that they lack a scheme of systematic study based on models and rules of procedure, and a body of theory that synthesizes general regularities so that unique residuals can be isolated:

> "Archaeology is an undisciplined empirical discipline. A discipline lacking a scheme of systematic and ordered study based upon declared and clearly defined models and rules of procedure."

The book is therefore a **program charter**, not a site report. It belongs with the late-1960s New Archaeology moment — same year as *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* (1968) — but its temperament is distinct. Where Binford framed archaeology *as anthropology* and process-explanation as the third aim after history and lifeways, Clarke framed archaeology as **itself**: "archaeology is archaeology is archaeology," a discipline with its own data, entities, and processes, which may couple to history and anthropology without becoming their servant. The British setting matters: Cambridge training, Childe's hierarchical culture methodology as both inheritance and target, and a generation newly coupled to systems theory, games theory, set theory, information theory, cultural ecology, locational analysis, and computer-powered statistics.

Clarke positioned the book under Bacon's maxim of "anticipations, rash and premature" — a temporary synthesis that would take generations to mature, and one he closed on Bacon's companion dictum that "truth comes out of error" more readily than out of confusion. He rejected three concurrent responses to the new techniques: nostalgic retreat into historiography; inert encystment within reputations; and mere "lean-to extensions" of new techniques onto a "shabby and already rambling edifice." The demand was congruence: archaeology must redesign itself so that the new couplings change both the techniques and the discipline.

Clarke's own definition of the discipline's object is where the "programmatic" character of the book becomes explicit. His opening chapter, "The nature of archaeology," states flatly: "Archaeology is the discipline concerned with the recovery, systematic description and study of material culture in the past." From this he derives the discipline's autonomy claim — his most quoted single sentence, made "with apologies to Gertrude Stein": "the view taken in this work is that archaeology is archaeology is archaeology... Archaeology is a discipline in its own right, concerned with archaeological data which it clusters in archaeological entities displaying certain archaeological processes and studied in terms of archaeological aims, concepts and procedures." He immediately qualifies the claim rather than treating it as license for isolation: "we fully appreciate that these entities and processes were once historical and social entities but... there is no simple way of equating our archaeological percepta with these lost events." Archaeological attributes, on Clarke's analysis, are the fossilized residue of action: "archaeological attributes equal the results of human or hominid actions, or sequences of actions, and regularities amongst artefact attributes represent regularities of hominid behaviour. The archaeologist is therefore studying concealed and obscure facets of hominid behaviour through the peculiar medium of the fossilized and congealed results of this behaviour, imprisoned in the attributes of ancient artefacts." That formulation — the discipline as the recovery of otherwise-inaccessible behavioural regularities from their material traces — is the epistemological warrant for everything that follows: if the object of study is behaviour that cannot itself be observed, only its patterned residue, then explicit classification and modelling are not optional refinements but the only route to knowledge at all.

Chapman's 1978 Foreword recontextualizes the book after Clarke's death (June 1976). Clarke had planned a shortened, updated second edition with two new chapters; Chapman, lacking usable notes, kept the theoretical spine and concluding chapter and **dropped** first-edition methods chapters 11–13 (computer/mathematical models and spatial analysis) as already developed elsewhere (Doran & Hodson 1975; Hodder & Orton 1975). The 1978 text is thus the classic statement of hierarchical/"culture" theory and systems vocabulary that Chapman thought students still needed — even as the quantitative methods the first edition helped provoke had outrun the book's own methods chapters.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit of analysis | "Sample" / materials | Method | Period of production |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What are archaeology's fundamental entities and how are they related? | Nested material-culture systems | Comparative European and world prehistoric illustrations | Hierarchical polythetic taxonomy | Programmatic synthesis, mid–late 1960s |
| What processes operate on those entities? | System trajectories (ontogeny, diffusion, interaction, selection) | Same + distribution models | Dynamic systems / cybernetic model | same |
| How should research procedure run? | Research cycle as feedback system | General flow-chart (fig. 2) | Three-sphere procedure model | same |
| How should classification work? | Attribute → technocomplex ranks | Beakers, Hallstatt swords, Acheulean, Aurignacian, etc. | Polythetic sets; numerical taxonomy (Sokal & Sneath) | same |

What the "methods section" of a normal study would report as sampling frame is here **illustrative prehistory**: cases are chosen to show regularities (lenticular type-frequency curves, technocomplex-scale labels, ethnological bridging arguments), not to test a single regional hypothesis. Second-edition apparatus adds post-1968 applications (Renfrew, Hodder, Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza, Roper trend surfaces) around Clarke's frame — Chapman/editorial densification, not Clarke's 1968 fieldwork.

**Three elementary models** organize the whole book (Clarke, attributed, ch. 10):

1. **Hierarchical and polythetic model for entities** — attribute → artefact → type → assemblage → culture → culture group → technocomplex.
2. **General dynamic system model for processes** — continuous systems, feedback, variety, constraint, trajectory, equilibrium, dislocation.
3. **Three-sphere feedback model for procedure** — contextual analysis, specific analysis, and hypothesis/model construction continuously recycling against new observations (fig. 2).

Clarke later cautioned that the nested hierarchy is an **arbitrary but useful approximation**: archaeological entities do not really exist in simple hierarchical levels; multidimensional or branching models may fit continuous networked systems better; polythetic fit is approximate, not pure monothetic or pure polythetic. Figure 49's caption states the model's own reflexivity: "A schematic hierarchical model of the major archaeological entities" — schematic, not a claim that the ranks are found rather than made.

**Systems and cybernetics as imported formal language.** Clarke's second model is built almost entirely on W. Ross Ashby's *An Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956), cited on well over a dozen occasions across the book — for the definitions of variety, constraint, and homeostasis; for the "black box" and "incompletely observable box" as models of inference from partial evidence; for the treatment of coupled systems reaching joint equilibrium only "when each is in a state of equilibrium, in the conditions provided by the others"; and for the Markovian/semi-Markovian formalization of system-state transition that Clarke adapts to model cultural-system trajectories. Clarke states the ambition of the borrowing directly: "the concepts of systems theory and cybernetics have been introduced into the... models derived from systems theory and cybernetics have been applied to" material-culture systems (ch. 3) so that culture can be treated formally as "an information system... intercommunicating channels" through which variety — new, alternative, contradictive, or redundant information — is exchanged between coupled subsystems (social, religious, psychological, linguistic, economic, material). The payoff Clarke claims for the cybernetic vocabulary is precision of a kind ordinary prose lacks: "process" stops meaning "change in general" and starts meaning a specified operation on a specified system property (variety, constraint, trajectory, equilibrium, dislocation), each with a stated formal analogue in Ashby.

**The procedure model (fig. 2)** is the least remembered of the three elementary models but the one Clarke treats as methodologically load-bearing: a continuous feedback loop between *contextual analysis* (locating a body of data within its wider physical and cultural setting), *specific analysis* (classification and internal patterning of the data itself), and *hypothesis and model construction* (formal, testable propositions fed back to redirect the next round of contextual and specific analysis). The loop is explicitly anti-linear — data collection does not precede and then yield to interpretation in a single pass; each phase recycles against the others. This is Clarke's answer to the "piling up of data" polemic of the Preface: procedure, not more excavation, is what an undisciplined discipline lacks.

## The Site / Sample / Data

There is no site. The "data" are:

1. **The tacit practice of the discipline** — the entities archaeologists already use without defining them.
2. **Comparative material culture** — pottery, lithics, assemblages, culture maps from European prehistory and selected world cases.
3. **Imported formal languages** — Ashby and Wiener on cybernetics; Bertalanffy on general systems; Sokal & Sneath on numerical taxonomy; information and communication theory; set theory and statistics.
4. **Ethnographic and historical analogies** (ch. 9) — Bantu, Kroeber California, Urnfield–Celtic–Slav–Italic bridging arguments — used as **probabilistic ethnology**, not as proof of identity.

The sampling bias is theoretical: European Bronze/Iron Age and Paleolithic examples dominate; Americanist phase/horizon/tradition vocabulary appears as parallel rather than as the organizing frame (contrast [[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Willey & Phillips]]). The book knows more about classification hygiene than about site formation (Schiffer-era formation processes are noted in second-edition apparatus more than in the 1968 core).

**The ethnology chapter (ch. 9) as bridging apparatus, not evidence.** Clarke's most exposed methodological move is the use of documented ethnic groups to test whether polythetic material-culture patterning tracks anything like a self-conscious people. He works through Urnfield–Celtic–Slav–Italic and Bantu material-distribution cases, explicitly framing the exercise as **probabilistic ethnology**: an affinity threshold (Clarke's own worked figures put strong-affinity technocomplex membership around a 30–60% shared type-family range, with as little as 5% shared *specific*-type identity) used to argue that material affinity groups *sometimes* correlate with independently attested linguistic or historical ethnic groups, without asserting the correlation as general or automatic. This is the chapter that both anticipates and partially licenses the four-way non-identity rule this wiki enforces: Clarke states the caution ("culture ≠ race/tribe/language") in the same breath as he builds bridging arguments that a careless reader could easily convert into an equation. The chapter is best read as a controlled experiment in exactly how far material polythetic classification can be pushed toward ethnic inference before the inference breaks — Clarke's own answer is "some way, cautiously, with independent corroboration," not "not at all" and not "freely."

### Load-bearing definitions (verbatim)

**System:**

> "An intercommunicating network of attributes or entities forming a complex whole."

**Archaeological culture:**

> "an archaeological culture is a polythetic set of specific and comprehensive artefact-type categories which consistently recur together in assemblages within a limited geographical area."

**Assemblage:**

> "An associated set of contemporary artefact-types. To be distinguished rigorously from the loose physical or geographical aggregate."

**Technocomplex:**

> "A group of cultures characterized by assemblages sharing a polythetic range but differing specific types of the same general families of artefact-types, shared as a widely diffused and interlinked response to common factors in environment, economy and technology."

**Polythetic (after Sokal & Sneath):** each individual possesses a large but unspecified number of the aggregate's attributes; each attribute is possessed by large numbers of individuals; **no single attribute is both sufficient and necessary** for membership.

These definitions are the book's most portable product. They rewire Childe's culture-as-people package into a **polythetic material-system package** without deleting the culture unit — a reform, not an abolition, of culture-history's central taxon.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Polemic as threshold.** The preface and ch. 1 do not gently introduce; they pathologize the discipline ("fever," "surgery will prove painful") so that systems vocabulary arrives as therapy.
2. **Autonomy claim.** "Archaeology is archaeology is archaeology" blocks reduction to history or anthropology while still demanding coupling to both. This is the British path: central theory *for archaeology*, not archaeology *as* anthropology alone.
3. **Hierarchy as hygiene.** Technocomplex is invented partly to stop people calling Acheulean-scale packages "cultures." Rank labels are a methodological ethics of comparison.
4. **Polythesis as anti-monothetic reform.** Monothetic key-character types (one necessary-and-sufficient trait list) are exposed as pretence; polythetic sets match how artefacts actually share attributes.
5. **Systems as process language.** Continuity, feedback, variety, constraint, trajectory, equilibrium, dislocation, florescence — a cybernetic thesaurus that lets "process" mean something more specific than "change."
6. **Aphorism.** The wooden-leg line and the engine-numbers/cheese-labels line do rhetorical work textbooks still quote; they separate *techniques* from *theory* and *data accumulation* from *question-driven analysis*.
7. **Baconian humility / hubris.** The book claims prematurity while building a totalizing hierarchy — a tension critics never forgot.
8. **Ethnology as probability, not identity.** Ch. 9's Urnfield–Celtic–Slav arguments use affinity thresholds and historical emergence as **bridges**, not equations — but still risk four-way collapse if readers drop the hedges.
9. **Deferred self-critique.** Clarke does not present the hierarchy as final even within the book's own covers — the caveat that entities "do not really exist" in the clean ranked form the model requires is stated inside the same chapters that build the ranks, a hedge that reads, five years later, as a rehearsal for the harder self-criticism of "Archaeology: the loss of innocence" (1973). That essay — not part of this book's text, cited here only from the bibliography and reception literature — is widely read (attributed to the secondary literature, not verified against its own text in this ingest) as Clarke's own acknowledgment that archaeology's naïve confidence in "facts speaking for themselves" had to give way to an explicit, self-aware theoretical apparatus; *Analytical Archaeology* is the first, most systematized attempt at supplying that apparatus, and the 1973 essay is usually read as its author's mid-course audit of how far the 1968 program had actually succeeded.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Immediate:** Commercial success and scarcity of the first edition (Chapman); polarised as brilliant or baffling; formative for British quantitative and systems archaeology.
- **Parallel charter:** With *New Perspectives* (1968), co-defines Anglophone New Archaeology — Clarke the systems/hierarchy pole, Binford the anthropology/process/hypothesis pole (with much overlap).
- **Vocabulary survival:** Polythetic culture definition; technocomplex; explicit entity hierarchy; systems definition of culture; procedure-as-feedback.
- **Downstream methods:** Numerical taxonomy, seriation formalization, spatial analysis, computer clustering — first-edition methods chapters as provocation even after Chapman cut them.
- **Textbook afterlife:** Johnson and others still use Clarke for systems summaries and the "wooden leg" / "loss of innocence" (1973 essay) pairing.
- **Foil:** Post-processual writers treated Clarke-style systems abstraction as emblem of what meaning-centred archaeology should leave behind — which is itself a form of lasting impact.
- **Chapman's editorial framing as reception evidence.** The 1978 Foreword is itself a data point on how the field absorbed the book: Chapman reports that the first edition was commercially scarce and already treated as a set text within a decade, and chooses to preserve the entity-hierarchy and systems chapters over the original methods chapters precisely because, by 1977, "quantitative methods" had become a mature specialist literature in their own right (Doran & Hodson 1975; Hodder & Orton 1975) while the conceptual apparatus had not been superseded — a judgment that itself shaped which parts of Clarke's program later readers encountered as "the book."
- **Cross-Atlantic comparison.** Clarke and Binford are conventionally paired as co-founders of the New Archaeology, but the pairing obscures a real difference in emphasis this analysis makes explicit: Binford's program (see [[binford-lewis]]) argues from hypothesis-testing and covering-law explanation outward to method, while Clarke argues from classification and systems modelling outward to explanation — a difference in *where the discipline's first move should be* that persisted as a live methodological fault line inside the New Archaeology, not merely as a national-style curiosity.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

*Not a replicable field study; afterlife is theoretical and methodological.*

| Position | Holders / locus | Claim (strongest form) |
|---|---|---|
| Too abstract / unoperational | Many practitioners; textbook lore | Systems vocabulary outruns workable field protocol |
| Hierarchical culture theory masks variability | Later notes (Chapman ch. 9 n.1 framing); social-variability critiques | Mapping hierarchical entities to social groups is unrealistic |
| Inductive classification vs hypothesis-testing | Binford-side critiques | Classification-first programs under-specify testable process questions |
| Do not import Hempel wholesale | Clarke 1972b himself vs Binford/Fritz-Plog/Watson school | Philosophy of science generalized from natural sciences should not be imposed on archaeology |
| Systems homeostasis flattens agency/meaning | Post-processual (Hodder et al.) | Law-like systems models underplay power, symbol, and interpretation |
| Ethnology chapter still risks pots=peoples | Four-way non-identity readers | Even "probable" Urnfield–ethnic bridges need independent linguistic/genetic controls |
| Methods chapters obsolete | Chapman 1978 editorial choice | Quantitative methods better served by specialist handbooks |

No failed replication of "findings" — the work is a research program charter. Elements that "failed" are program ambitions (canonical symbolic models for all archaeology within a generation) rather than empirical results overturned.

## Current Standing

*Analytical Archaeology* remains the classic statement of **hierarchical polythetic culture theory** and **systems framing** in archaeology. Everyday practice still says "culture" and "assemblage" more often than "technocomplex" or "polythetic set," but the book's hygiene rules — do not compare across ranks; do not treat spatial co-occurrence as assemblage; do not equate culture with people/language/race — are still the right defaults when they are remembered. Read as primary source for the British New Archaeology, as still-usable classification theory, and as a documented episode in the discipline's loss of methodological innocence.
