# From Mobilization to Revolution — Deep Analysis

> Summary page: [[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-study]]. Source: [[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978]]. Theory: [[political-conflict-theory]].

## Question and Intellectual Context

Tilly asks how to explain **[[collective-action]]** — its volume, form, violence, and revolutionary extremes — as **political** processes of interest, organization, resource control, and opportunity, rather than as symptoms of social pathology. The book is self-described "stone soup": "Like the down-and-out swindlers of the fable, I boiled up a pot of water, tossed in some pebbles, then invited passersby to add whatever soup makings they could spare" — Michigan seminar memoranda, circulating papers on political conflict and collective violence, NSF France/Britain research, rewritten from four chapters to eight after critical review.

The book's polemical target is named precisely, and it is not a straw man: **the Durkheimian tradition** of treating collective action as a symptom of strain, differentiation, or disequilibrium. Tilly reconstructs it fairly — "a society strained by a continuous struggle between forces of disintegration (notably rapid differentiation) and forces of integration (notably new or renewed commitment to shared beliefs)," yielding "three different kinds of collective action: let us call them routine, anomie, and restorative" — and insists it is the *dominant* paradigm he writes against, not a fringe: "the standard analyses of industrialization, urbanization, deviance, social control, social disorganization, and collective behavior which emerged in the twentieth century all bore the Durkheimian stamp." Its contemporary heirs he names as Samuel Huntington (disorder as mobilization outrunning institutionalization) and Chalmers Johnson (revolution as disequilibrium). Against all of them Tilly proposes not the opposite extreme (pure rational choice) but a **synthesis**: "Why not combine causal models of constraints with purposive models of choices among available courses of action? The synthesis is surprisingly difficult to achieve."

Uncommonly for a formal-model book, Tilly declares his politics openly rather than claiming neutrality: "The tone of later discussions in this book is generally hostile to the collective action of governments and favorable to the collective action of ordinary people; that, too, is a political stance." The intellectual lineage he claims runs through **Marx** (interests and organization rooted in structural position) more than through the Weberian or Durkheimian traditions, though he reads all four classical traditions — Marx, Weber, Durkheim, and Mill — as resources rather than authorities, extracting a usable component from each and discarding the system. The Marxian debt is the deepest: interests are treated as *given by position* in relations of production and power rather than as psychological states, which is exactly what lets Tilly bracket grievance and motive and still explain action. But he refuses the full Marxian apparatus (no theory of history's direction, no privileged revolutionary subject), keeping only the structural account of interest and organization and grafting onto it a Millian, choice-theoretic account of how mobilized groups select among available actions — the "causal models of constraints with purposive models of choices" synthesis that the whole book is built to sustain — a synthesis Tilly concedes is "surprisingly difficult to achieve," and never claims fully to have achieved.

### The two models

The book's analytic core is a pair of linked schematic models, deliberately built as "static" starting points to be complicated later.

The **mobilization model** describes a single contender through five components. Tilly defines each: **interests** ("the shared advantages or disadvantages likely to accrue to the population... as a consequence of various possible interactions with other populations"); **organization** ("the extent of common identity and unifying structure among the individuals in the population" — later glossed as *catness × netness*, the product of shared category and interpersonal network); **mobilization** ("the process by which a group acquires collective control over the resources needed for action"); **opportunity** (decomposed into power, repression/facilitation, and opportunity/threat); and **collective action** itself ("people's acting together in pursuit of common interests"). The title names the causal spine: "Our chief effort... will flow along the lines going from organization to mobilization to collective action to revolution. Especially from mobilization to revolution."

The **polity model** places that contender in a field of power. Its elements are defined with the same precision: a **government** ("an organization which controls the principal concentrated means of coercion within the population"); **contenders** ("any group which... applies pooled resources to influence the government"); the crucial split between a **member** ("any contender which has routine, low-cost access to resources controlled by the government") and a **challenger** ("any other contender"); the **polity** ("the collective action of the members and the government"); and **coalitions**. The model's engine is a permanent struggle over the membership boundary — "all challengers seek, among other things, to enter the polity" and "all members seek... to remain in the polity" — with power operationalized as "an increasing rate of return on expended resources." **Repression** and **facilitation** are defined as the cost side of this economy: "repression refers to the volume of collective action as a function of the costs of producing it, while power refers to the returns."

### The evidentiary base and its declared limits

The models are not free-floating: they sit on a large empirical apparatus that Tilly built with collaborators over the 1960s–70s — "detailed enumerations and descriptions of collective violence in Italy, German[y], France, and England over substantial blocks of time," compiled from "newspapers, archival sources, and historical works," with the **community-population-day** as the elementary unit for individuating discrete violent events. This is quantitative historical sociology in the *event-catalog* tradition, not narrative history and not single-site fieldwork, and the appendices documenting the coding are first-class method statements.

Tilly is candid about the **sampling frame** as a limitation in his own voice. The evidence "comes from the experiences of Western Europe and North America over the last few centuries," which affords rich purchase on "state making, the expansion of capitalism, industrialization, urbanization, electoral politics, and formally organized interest groups" — but at the acknowledged "cost" of excluding "collective action in the absence of a strong state" and "exotic movements such as Melanesian cargo cults." The book, in other words, theorizes contention under the modern national state and says so; whether its models travel beyond that frame is left open. Its self-description as "both a partial synthesis and a proposal for further inquiry" that "does not contain a sustained analysis of a single body of evidence" is an accurate statement of its genre: a theoretical framework illustrated and probed against catalog data, not a monographic case study.

## The Site/Sample/Data

- **France:** long-run collective violence participation; strike–violence relationships; urbanity tables.
- **Britain:** contentious gatherings (meetings, violence, claim-making) with codesheets; electoral brawls as textbook claim–counterclaim.
- **Illustrations:** food riots, poorhouse attacks, Reform-era Hyde Park, women's suffrage/temperance coalitions.

The France and Britain programs are the empirical heart, and they exemplify the book's method: rather than narrating a few revolutions, Tilly and his collaborators built **long-run enumerations** of contentious events — every collective-violence incident or contentious gathering meeting a threshold, coded uniformly across decades or centuries — so that the *changing shape* of contention (its forms, participants, targets, and relation to state-making and capitalism) could be read off aggregate patterns. This is what lets him claim, as findings rather than impressions, that repertoires shift slowly, that violence tracks routine claim-making, and that repressive forces do most of the killing. The design's strength is comparability across long stretches of time; its exposure is to the biases of the sources (press and archival records over-report the visible, the urban, and the violent) and to the difficulty of coding "interests" and "organization" from the outside. Tilly treats the appendices documenting these procedures as integral to the argument, not as back-matter — a mark of the quantitative-historical-sociology genre the book helped define.

The findings hang together as a single displacement: they move the explanation of contention *out* of the psychology of aggrieved individuals and *into* the political relations among organized groups and states. The claim that hardship is not sufficient for violence is the negative half; the mobilization model is the positive half. What makes a grievance into a movement, on Tilly's account, is not the intensity of suffering but the presence of **organization** (a group that is both a recognizable category and a dense network — "catness × netness"), **mobilized resources**, and **opportunity** created by the configuration of power and repression. A quiescent but well-organized population with a favorable opportunity acts; a suffering but disorganized one does not. This is why the book reads as a sustained argument that collective action is *normal politics by other means* rather than a breakdown of the social order — and why its most-cited empirical results (violence as a by-product of routine claim-making; the state as leading perpetrator) are precisely the ones that dissolve the crowd-pathology image.

## Repertoires, Collective Violence, and Revolution

Three of the book's most influential arguments deserve statement in Tilly's own terms.

**Repertoires of collective action.** Contrary to the common shorthand that "repertoires" is purely a *later*-Tilly concept, it is already developed and named here. A population's repertoire is the bounded set of collective-action forms "theoretically available" to it at a given time, and Tilly's arresting analogy is linguistic: "A population's repertoire of collective action generally includes only a handful of alternatives. It generally changes slowly, seems obvious and natural to the people involved. It resembles an elementary language: familiar as the day to its users, for all its possible quaintness or incomprehensibility to an outsider." He models the choice among means (sheer-efficiency, advantage-of-familiarity, flexible-repertoire, rigid-repertoire) and hypothesizes "that the flexible repertoire is the most general case for organized groups" — a bias toward previously-used means, but not closure to innovation. See [[repertoire-of-contention]].

**Collective violence as a by-product.** Tilly's most counter-intuitive empirical claim is that violence is not a distinct psychological category but an outcome of ordinary contention. "Most collective violence... grows out of actions which are not intrinsically violent, and which are basically similar to a much larger number of collective actions occurring without violence in the same periods and settings." The "standard structure" is claim-making: "one group undertakes a large action which directly or indirectly states a claim; a second group challenges that claim; they struggle." And — from his and his collaborators' event-catalogs of France, Germany, Italy, and Britain — the state is the leading perpetrator: "repressive forces are themselves the most consistent initiators and performers of collective violence," with "a division of labor" in which "repressive forces do the largest part of the killing and wounding, while the groups they are seeking to control do most of the damage to objects." This directly inverts the crowd-psychology image of the violent mob and the passive authorities.

**Revolution as multiple sovereignty.** Adopting and generalizing Trotsky's "dual power," Tilly defines a **revolutionary situation** not by grievance or breakdown but structurally, as [[multiple-sovereignty|multiple sovereignty]]: more than one bloc effectively exercising control over part of the state apparatus. Its three "proximate causes" are the appearance of contenders "advancing exclusive alternative claims to the control over the government," "commitment to those claims by a significant segment of the subject population," and "incapacity or unwillingness of the agents of the government to suppress the alternative coalition." Crucially he separates the *situation* from the **revolutionary outcome** (an actual transfer of power), whose proximate causes are "(1) the presence of a revolutionary situation: multiple sovereignty; (2) revolutionary coalitions between challengers and members of the polity; (3) control of substantial force by the revolutionary coalition." And he issues a methodological warning against smuggling in breakdown variables: whether discontent, relative deprivation, or value conflict explains onset "must... be proved and not assumed" — what matters is the population's commitment to alternative claims "regardless of their motives."

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

Tilly's rhetoric is that of a **model-builder who distrusts his own models**, and the tension is deliberate.

- **The reframing opening.** He begins not with theory but with a concrete historical episode (an English popular action) to reset the reader's intuitions away from the pathology frame *before* the argument starts — teaching the reader to see a crowd as claim-making rather than as breakdown.
- **The four-tradition survey.** He catalogs the Marx, Durkheim, Mill, and Weber lineages of thinking about collective action, positioning his own synthesis as selecting from each rather than founding a school — a move that both credits the classics and clears room.
- **Deliberately "primitive" models.** The polity and mobilization diagrams are introduced as avowedly "static" first approximations, to be complicated by time, strategy, and historical variation later. This is a distinctive expository choice: build the crude machine in public, then show where it breaks, rather than present a finished apparatus. It models the fallibilism the preface announces.
- **The causal–purposive straddle.** Following Coleman's distinction between causal and purposive explanation, Tilly refuses to resolve it, insisting his account "straddles" the divide — collective action is both constrained by structural position (causal) and chosen among available options (purposive). This is the book's deepest methodological commitment and the source of both its richness and its irreducibility to a clean model.
- **Confessed fallibilism.** The "stone soup" preface and its admission of remaining "gravel" set a tone of provisional synthesis rather than system — an unusual humility for a founding text, and part of why the book aged into a framework others could rebuild rather than a doctrine to defend or overthrow.

He also **pre-empts the breakdown reading of his own cases**: of an 1866 disturbance he wrote that "some of the demonstrators... were angry, some were drunk, and some enjoyed the rough-and-tumble. But the breaking down of fences and the scuffling with police was a by-product of the play of claim and counterclaim" — conceding the psychological facts while denying they do the explanatory work.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*From Mobilization to Revolution* became a **charter text** of the mobilization/political-process approach that displaced collective-behavior theory in the sociology of social movements. The displacement it helped accomplish was paradigm-scale: the mid-century American sociology of "collective behavior" (Smelser, Turner and Killian, the Chicago crowd tradition) had treated movements, panics, and crazes as a single family of extra-institutional responses to strain — the very Durkheimian lineage Tilly targets. By recasting movements as *organized, interested, strategic politics* continuous with elections and interest groups rather than with panics, Tilly (with the parallel work of McCarthy and Zald on resource mobilization, and later McAdam on political process) moved the field's center of gravity from social psychology to political sociology. That the phrase "resource mobilization" is not actually Tilly's own here — he writes of the "mobilization model" and its components — is a useful reminder that the paradigm label was applied by the secondary literature; the book itself is more idiosyncratic and more historical than the tidy "RM theory" of the textbooks. Its vocabulary — mobilization, polity membership, opportunity, repression/facilitation, repertoires — became standard equipment. Its most consequential single influence is arguably as the **foil** for [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol's *States and Social Revolutions*]] (1979): Skocpol adopts Tilly's stress on organization and resources but rejects the polity model's treatment of the state as an *arena* for contenders, insisting instead on the state as an autonomous actor with its own interests (see [[structural-theory-of-revolution]]). The Tilly–Skocpol contrast — voluntarist mobilization of contenders versus structural state-and-class conjunctures — is one of the defining axes of the comparative-historical sociology of revolution. Downstream, the book's framework fed the "contentious politics" synthesis Tilly built with Tarrow and McAdam (not yet ingested), where mechanisms and processes partly displace the static models used here.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested` — the book is a framework that was extended and contested, not a finding that failed replication.

- **State autonomy (Skocpol).** The polity model treats the government as the prize contenders compete for; Skocpol and the state-centered turn argue this understates the state's independent capacities and interests, which can make revolutions from the top down or through inter-state pressure rather than through domestic mobilization alone.
- **The cultural/framing critique (post-1980s).** The model is resolutely structural — interests derive from position, and meaning-making is thin. The framing and "new social movement" traditions charged that identity, grievance interpretation, and culture do independent work the mobilization model brackets. Tilly's later turn toward repertoires-as-culturally-embedded and toward narrative mechanisms is partly a response.
- **Measurement.** Operationalizing "interests," "organization," and "mobilization" from event catalogs is contestable; the community-population-day unit and newspaper sourcing carry the standard hazards of event data (reporting bias, selection by visibility and violence).
- **Tilly's own revisions.** He never treated the book as final — the "static" models were built to be complicated, and his later work (*The Contentious French*, *Dynamics of Contention*) shifted weight from the polity/mobilization diagrams toward historically variable repertoires and process-tracing. That self-revision is itself part of the book's standing: a framework its own author kept rebuilding.

## Current Standing

*From Mobilization to Revolution* remains a **foundational** text of the political-process paradigm and a standard reference for the mobilization, polity-membership, repertoire, and multiple-sovereignty concepts — even as the field has moved past its static models toward the contentious-politics framework Tilly himself helped build. It is best read today as the **hinge** between the collective-behavior tradition it displaced and the mechanism-and-process sociology that followed: the book where the vocabulary of movements-as-politics was consolidated. Its structural bias (interests from position, thin culture) is the standing limitation, addressed but not dissolved by the framing and cultural turns. Read alongside later Tilly for mature repertoires and state-formation links, and against [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol]] for the state-centered alternative. Its empirical claims about the state's leading role in collective violence, and its structural definition of revolution as multiple sovereignty, have proven among its most durable contributions. `replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`.

## Historiography and Sources

Primary: the 1978 Random House text (OCR ingest, [[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978]]). The book should be read as the mid-point of a long research program, not a standalone monograph: it consolidates a decade of Michigan-based work on French and British contention and is deliberately provisional ("a proposal for further inquiry"). Its proper companions — Tilly's *The Contentious French* (1986), *Popular Contention in Great Britain* (1995), and the McAdam–Tarrow–Tilly *Dynamics of Contention* (2001), where the static models give way to mechanisms and processes — are **not yet ingested**, and the account of the book's afterlife above is correspondingly framed as reception context rather than asserted from those sources. ### Verbatim load-bearing passages

**Multiple sovereignty (Ch. 7):**

> "Multiple sovereignty is then the identifying feature of revolutionary situations. A revolutionary situation begins when a government previously under the control of a single, sovereign polity becomes the object of effective, competing, mutually exclusive claims on the part of two or more distinct polities. It ends when a single sovereign polity regains control over the government."

**Onset recognized by obedience:**

> "The revolutionary moment arrives when previously acquiescent members of that population find themselves confronted with strictly incompatible demands from the government and form an alternative body claiming control over the government, or claiming to be the government … and those previously acquiescent people obey the alternative body. … Multiple sovereignty has begun."

**Repression/facilitation (Ch. 4):**

> "repression is any action by another group which raises the contender's cost of collective action. An action which lowers the group's cost of collective action is a form of facilitation."

**Violence structure (Ch. 6, Hyde Park type-case):** one group states a claim; a second (often repressive specialists) challenges it; they struggle — "the standard structure of collective violence."

**Repertoires as an elementary language (Ch. 5):**

> "A population's repertoire of collective action generally includes only a handful of alternatives... It resembles an elementary language: familiar as the day to its users, for all its possible quaintness or incomprehensibility to an outsider."

These passages are the book in miniature: contention is patterned, learned, and political — a language spoken between organized groups and states — not a spasm of the disordered crowd.
