## Question and Intellectual Context

*Sidewalk* set itself against a policy and a theory at once. In the late 1990s the
governing frame for the New York street was **[[broken-windows|broken windows]]** — James Q.
Wilson and George Kelling's 1982 thesis that visible disorder signals "no one cares" and
breeds serious crime, extended by Kelling and Coles's *Fixing Broken Windows* (1996) into
a program for removing "panhandlers, drunks, addicts… loiterers" and unlicensed vendors,
and operationalized as Giuliani-era quality-of-life policing. [[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]]'s
question was whether the poor, mostly Black men who sold books and magazines on Sixth
Avenue — the human "broken windows" of that theory — actually represented disorder, or
whether the appearance of disorder concealed a working moral order.

His affirmative frame came not from sociology but from the urbanist [[jacobs-jane|Jane
Jacobs]]. *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* (1961) had argued that busy,
mixed-use sidewalks generate their own order through [[public-character|public characters]]
who keep "eyes upon the street" and take "a modicum of public responsibility… without
being hired." Duneier's wager was that Jacobs's public-character role "need not be filled
by conventionally respectable people" — that unhoused vendors also keep eyes on the street.
The book is thus a three-way argument: with Jacobs, against broken windows, and inside the
[[chicago-school|Chicago]] urban-ethnographic tradition Duneier explicitly claims. In the
method appendix he names his forebears — Du Bois, Park, Thomas, Ware, Charles Johnson,
Hughes, Drake, [[whyte-william-foote|Whyte]], Cayton, the Lynds, Becker, [[goffman-erving|Goffman]],
Liebow, Suttles, Gans, [[anderson-elijah|Anderson]] — placing *Sidewalk* as a late entry
in a century-long line of American ethnographies of the urban poor.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The conducted study is far stranger than "an ethnography of street vendors" suggests, and
Duneier's 40-page "A Statement on Method" is unusually candid about it.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Do the street poor build order or disorder? | Public informal economy & moral order of a block | ~3 blocks of Sixth Ave, Greenwich Village; a few dozen regulars | Participant observation as a vendor | 1992–1999 |
| How did this economy come to exist here? | The block's institutional causes | Penn Station, Amtrak suit (D.C.), city council, BIDs | [[extended-place-method]] | 1996–1999 |
| How do "disorderly" encounters actually work? | Single street conversations | Taped vendor–passerby encounters | Conversation analysis | 1996–1999 |

Duneier began, by his own account, "without a precise research question… no theories that
I wanted to test or reconstruct," guided only by a standing theme: "whether and how the
persons I am with are or are not struggling to live in accordance with standards of 'moral'
worth." His colleague Erik Olin Wright named the approach **"diagnostic ethnography"** —
start from observed symptoms, return to the field with new diagnostic tools, reach for
theory last. The method's signatures:

- **The [[extended-place-method|extended place method]].** Duneier distinguishes it from
  Michael Burawoy's extended case method (which, he argues, conflates theory-reconstruction
  with the micro–macro link) and aligns it with George Marcus's multi-sited ethnography. To
  understand the block he followed its causes outward: to Penn Station (where the men had
  lived in the 1980s), to Washington for the Amtrak-lawsuit attorneys, to former councilman
  Edward Wallace (who authored the 1982 vending law), and into the BID offices lobbying to
  restrict vending.
- **Continuous tape-recording.** A recorder ran in a milk crate under his table through his
  days on the block. He argues (attributed): "for a discipline in search of meanings, it was
  well-nigh indefensible that so little attention was paid to exact quotes and quotation
  marks." Verbatim transcription replaced reconstructed dialogue; some men "managed" the
  taping themselves. Crucially, the tapes surfaced patterns invisible in the field — the
  pervasiveness of the "Fuck it!" mentality, the bathroom-exclusion problem — only on later
  review in Madison, Wisconsin.
- **"Checking stuff out."** Modeled on investigative reporting, not standard ethnography:
  cross-checking life stories against relatives, officials, and records. When he checked
  Mudrick's claim that his illiteracy came from witnessing childhood lynchings, the story
  "turned out to be implausible" — which Duneier treats not as invalidating Mudrick but as a
  "representation" revealing the man.
- **The reflexive apparatus.** Real names and the real location (journalist-style, with
  consent); royalties shared with 21 people; the manuscript **read aloud to every subject**
  in a rented hotel room for correction; and a signed **afterword by the informant Hakim
  Hasan**. These are not disclosure footnotes but the study's method.

What the design is *not*: a sample, a survey, or a replicable protocol. `replication_status:
not-applicable`. Its claims to rigor rest on transparency, verification, and verbatim data
rather than on generalizability.

## The Site / Sample / Data

The block held a differentiated informal economy. Written-matter vendors (protected by
**Local Law 33 of 1982**, which lets anyone sell books/magazines without a license) worked
alongside scavengers who supplied inventory from recycling and trash, and support workers:
place holders (who held overnight spots and rented them for $20–50), table watchers (a 50%
cut), movers ($5 a trip), and storage providers. Almost all were African-American men,
mid-thirties to late fifties, most with crack-era jail time, about a third Vietnam veterans;
roughly a third slept on the street. The core informants recur throughout: **Hakim Hasan**,
the book vendor and self-described "eyes and ears of this street" who had read Jacobs;
**Marvin** and **Ron**, magazine scavenger-vendors; **Mudrick**, the illiterate scavenger
who "lays shit out"; **Ishmael**, whose Christmas-week confiscation anchors the regulation
chapters.

Data provenance is heterogeneous and deliberately cross-checked: taped street talk, 20+ paid
life-history interviews ($50/session), Duneier's own vending experience, family interviews
(Ron's sister, Mudrick's daughter, Ishmael's mother), and documentary records (the Amtrak
litigation, City Council legislative findings, court cases against bookstores). The
population was fluid: of sixteen men who had regularly slept on the block, only five still
did so by the writing — most had moved to housed lives while continuing to vend, a datum
Duneier reads as evidence that the economy stabilizes lives over time.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Vendors build a decent moral order; little crime springs from the block | Participant observation, life histories | Widely accepted as ethnographic reading; contested by order-maintenance advocates |
| Sixth Ave is a "sustaining habitat" of complementary elements ([[sustaining-habitat]]) | Comparative analysis (Penn Station vs. Sixth Ave), legal history | Adopted as a concept in urban ethnography |
| Vendors are Jacobs's public characters, respectability notwithstanding ([[public-character]]) | Hakim/Marvin cases | Influential extension of Jacobs |
| Informal control does **not** regulate sleeping, urination, entangling talk | Chapters in Part Three | Accepted; the anti-romantic core of the book |
| Interactional vandalism: tactical seizure of conversational order ([[interactional-vandalism]]) | CA of taped encounters (few cases) | Widely reprinted (Duneier & Molotch); small-n noted |
| Local Law 45 destabilized a working order | Fieldwork + legislative record | The book's "competing legalities" thesis |
| Broken windows conflates physical and social disorder | Textual critique of Wilson & Kelling | Central to the [[broken-windows-debate]]; contested |

**The moral order and the "Fuck it!" mentality.** Duneier's men draw sharp moral
distinctions — vending and scavenging are "an honest living," set against the crimes that
jailed them ("Whatever I do with my money, at least I can say I earned it"). He develops
their own vernacular concept of retreatism ("Fuck it!") and argues the sidewalk is "a social
structure that may encourage or discourage retreatist behavior," pulling men back toward
self-respect through entrepreneurial routine and mentorship.

**The sustaining habitat.** The book's ecological centerpiece (attributed): "what makes an
attribute like food or shelter or trash valuable is not its utility in itself, but its
utility in relationship to other attributes of the setting." Density, donors, free food
(St. Joseph's soup kitchen), sleeping niches, high-quality recyclable trash, sympathetic
residents, and Local Law 33 combined — unplanned — into a habitat. See [[sustaining-habitat]].

**The limits of informal control.** Part Three is the anti-romantic hinge: the same order
does not stop sidewalk sleeping (explained by habitat "embeddedness," against Christopher
Jencks's drugs-vs-lodging inference), public urination (a restroom-access problem structured
by social distance — clean, business-patronizing men like Hakim are admitted; dirty or
homeless-presenting men are not), or men's entangling talk to women.

**Interactional vandalism.** Using conversation-analytic transcripts, Duneier shows a few men
exploit the adjacency-pair structure of talk — pressing questions through the woman's timed
silences ("disaffiliative escalation") until she must be "technically rude" to escape. The
concept (with Harvey Molotch) names a gendered power seized from class subordination and
marks the limit of Jacobs's model, where shared interactional norms cannot be assumed across
race-class-gender divides. See [[interactional-vandalism]].

**Competing legalities and the broken-windows critique.** Before Local Law 45 (1993),
vendors ran a customary spot-ownership system enforced by reputation, where calling the
police on a fellow vendor was taboo ("plantation boy," "snitch"). BID-driven Local Law 45
halved Village vending space and produced extortion (place holders turned enforcers with
blades), attrition of quality-goods vendors, and new police dependence — order-maintenance
producing disorder. On the theory itself, Duneier's critique is precise (attributed): Wilson
and Kelling "used Zimbardo's demonstration with the abandoned cars, which was merely about
physical disorder, to justify a set of ideas about social disorders… social disorder
consists of human beings… capable of thinking and creating meanings." His counter-proposal is
a mirror-image **"fixed windows"** thesis: informal economy and mentorship reintegrate the
marginalized.

**The construction of decency.** The Jane Street contrast case — the white Romps family
selling Christmas trees, offered apartment keys within days, versus Hakim, "as educated,
articulate, and well mannered," never once offered keys — grounds the closing argument:
decency is "achieved in a temporal and spatial process," and the street poor lack the time
and private space to manage what becomes publicly visible. Race interacts with class and
public exposure rather than acting alone.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

*Sidewalk* persuades as a text through three devices. First, **the comparison against
"respectable" analogues**: Duneier insists a "sociological analysis gains power when it takes
up such a challenge, comparing the acts these men engage in with those of other members of
society who are not viewed as 'victims'" — small-business owners who sleep in their shops,
construction workers' sexual banter, bookstore chains that also traffic stolen review copies.
The move refuses to exempt his subjects from ordinary standards *or* to exempt the respectable
from scrutiny.

Second, **verbatim data as authority**. By quoting taped speech exactly, Duneier stakes the
book's credibility on transcription rather than on the ethnographer's memory or voice — a
direct answer to the fabrication anxieties that shadow the genre. The two epigraphs to the
method appendix stage the tension he is working inside: Whyte's claim that "the actual
evolution of research ideas does not take place in accord with the formal statements we read
on research methods," and [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s warning that accounts of technique are
"rationalizations, and we're in the precarious position of providing them."

Third, **turning his own position into evidence**. Duneier reproduces, without softening, a
taped exchange in which two men disparage him with an antisemitic stereotype ("he's a Jew…
they used to taking over"), and glosses it: "participant observers need not be fully trusted
in order to have their presence at least accepted." He similarly reproduces a cart
manufacturer's racial-slur-laden refusal to build Ishmael a vending cart as documentary
evidence of the prejudice the men face. The white, upper-middle-class, Jewish male academic
studying poor Black men makes his own vantage a datum rather than an embarrassment to be
managed away.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Sidewalk* was received as a contemporary classic of urban sociology (Becker: "A masterpiece
of fieldwork"; Sassen, Coles, and others blurbed it) and quickly entered syllabi in urban
ethnography, the sociology of public space, and research methods. Its concepts —
[[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]], [[interactional-vandalism|interactional
vandalism]], the [[extended-place-method|extended place method]] — became teachable
vocabulary. The companion Duneier–Molotch article "Talking City Trouble" (AJS 1999) carried
interactional vandalism into the conversation-analysis and gender literatures. As the leading
ethnographic answer to [[broken-windows|broken-windows]] policing, the book anchors the
[[broken-windows-debate|broken-windows debate]] and is read against the quantitative
challenge (Sampson and Raudenbush's disorder/collective-efficacy work). It extended
[[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]] and the [[chicago-school|Chicago]] line ([[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte]],
Liebow, Gans, [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study|Anderson]]) into the post-industrial
informal economy.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

No restudy of the Sixth Avenue fieldwork exists; as an interpretive single-site ethnography
the book is not the kind of design that replicates. The live positions (recorded, not
adjudicated):

- **Representation defenders.** The apparatus — real names, member-checking, shared
  royalties, an informant afterword — is widely praised as raising ethnography's
  accountability bar; Hasan's published dissent is treated as a methodological innovation in
  informant agency ("a departure from the 'scholar knows best' paradigm").
- **Reflexivity critics.** A white ethnographer's rapport still bounds what can be seen — a
  limit Duneier foregrounds (his admission that trust was sometimes "nothing more than an
  illusion"; the taped antisemitic aside). Hasan himself records the students' challenge:
  "Could a white professor really be trusted to write about black men without succumbing to
  stereotypes?"
- **The ethnographic fallacy.** Stephen Steinberg's charge — that close observation "obscures
  the less visible structures and processes that engender and sustain the behavior" — is the
  standing structural critique. Duneier answers by reading widely in the urban-poverty
  literature ([[wilson-william-julius|W. J. Wilson]], Massey, Jencks, Sassen) and by the
  extended place method itself; whether the answer succeeds is contested.
- **On the substance.** Order-maintenance advocates (Manshel, Klein) hold that unlicensed
  vending is genuinely "antisocial"; the empirical causal question — did broken-windows
  policing cause the crime drop? — remains open, since crime also fell where the theory was
  not applied.

## Current Standing

*Sidewalk* stands as a canonical urban ethnography and the reference ethnographic critique of
quality-of-life policing, and its "Statement on Method" is a methods-course staple in its own
right. Its concepts remain in active use; its reflexive procedures are cited as a model (and a
target) in debates over ethnographic authority. The unresolved questions it leaves —
whether "fixed windows" accommodation scales, whether the reflexive apparatus overcomes or
merely documents the ethnographer's position, whether disorder causes crime — are exactly the
questions that keep it taught.

## Historiography and Sources

Primary: *Sidewalk* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1999), with Ovie Carter's photographs, Hakim
Hasan's afterword, and the "A Statement on Method" appendix — the key document for the study's
conduct. The Duneier–Molotch AJS article (1999) is the peer-reviewed home of the interactional-
vandalism analysis. The book must be read against its interlocutors: [[jacobs-jane|Jacobs]]'s
*Death and Life* (1961); Wilson & Kelling (1982) and Kelling & Coles (1996) on broken windows;
Liebow's *Tally's Corner* (1967) as the explicit precedent for handling intervention and the
informant relationship; and Steinberg's "ethnographic fallacy." Duneier's *Slim's Table* (1992)
and *Ghetto* (2016) frame the career and remain ingestion gaps here.
