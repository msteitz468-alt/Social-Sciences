# Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. The [[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-study|studies/ summary]] owns question/design/findings/impact/critiques/standing at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. All Binford program claims are **attributed voice**; Nunamiut ethnographic facts are dated to the 1969–1973 fieldwork window, never the ethnographic present.

Lewis Binford did not go to Anaktuvuk Pass because he was curious about the Nunamiut. He went because he needed a caribou-hunting people, in an Arctic setting, still using the animal he had spent a year measuring bone-by-bone in a French cave. *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* is the book-length record of that instrumental relationship: five years of fieldwork converted into a set of portable analytic tools — the utility indices — designed to let an excavator read decision-making out of a pile of broken bone. This page reconstructs how the study was actually built: what it measured, on whom, under what constraints, and what happened to its conclusions once other zooarchaeologists began testing them.

## Question and Intellectual Context

The book's origin is not in Alaska but in the Dordogne, and not in 1969 but in a dispute that had been running since the mid-1960s. Binford had accepted **[[bordes-francois|François Bordes]]'** meticulous quantification of Mousterian stone-tool assemblages — four recurring "types" (Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition, Typical Mousterian, Denticulate Mousterian, and the Charentian group) recognized across dozens of French sites — but rejected Bordes's reading of what that patterning *meant*. Bordes held that the four types tracked the presence of four distinct ethnic groups occupying the Combe Grenal region in succession and in alternation; Binford, with his then-wife **[[binford-sally-r|Sally Binford]]**, argued in 1966 that "variation in the structure and function of an archaeological assemblage is directly related to the term, nature, and spatial arrangement of human activities" — that is, the same group could produce different assemblage "types" simply by doing different things at different places (attributed, Binford 1978, quoting Binford & Binford 1966; also *In Pursuit of the Past* 1983, R2-05–R2-21). Binford states the dispute was not, at root, about facts — "we trusted Bordes and had no argument with his typology" (attributed, *In Pursuit* 1983, R2-39) — but about "the appropriate meaning to be given to certain archaeological facts" (Binford 1978, L421–425).

The 1966 position was a claim, not a demonstration. Binford spent 1967 in Bordeaux mapping fauna and stone at Combe Grenal in three dimensions, hoping raw distributional correlation would settle the question. It did not: "none of these new facts spoke for themselves... none of the correlations carried sufficient information to tell us why they were that way" (attributed, *In Pursuit* 1983, R2-42). On the boat home from Le Havre at the New Year of 1969, Binford states he arrived at the formulation that would organize the rest of his career: the archaeological record is *static* and *contemporary* — "my interest is in the past but my observations are on the present" (Binford 1978, L282–296) — so no amount of pattern-finding within the static record alone can certify what dynamic behavior produced it. What was needed was a domain in which the dynamic behavior *and* its static, bone-shaped by-product could be observed simultaneously. This is the seed of what Binford would later name **[[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]]** (a term he first uses explicitly in the book's Conclusions, citing his own 1977 formulation: Binford 1978, L3220).

Binford chose the Nunamiut for three stated reasons: he had already studied reindeer bones from Mousterian sites and the Nunamiut "still hunted the very same animal"; they remained, in 1969, "reported... over 80% dependent for their subsistence on a single species" (Binford 1978, L707–711); and the Arctic environment was "not dissimilar from that of the French occupation sites" he had mapped (attributed, *In Pursuit* 1983, R2-45). The choice of fauna over artifacts as the observational medium was itself argued, not assumed: bones, Binford reasoned, permit "a culture-free taxonomy for tabulating relative frequencies of skeletal elements across sites" in a way that stone tools — freighted with the unresolved question of whether their form reflects "mental templates" — do not (attributed, Binford 1978, L649–673). He had already abandoned lithic use-wear analysis as producing "overwhelmingly... ambiguous results" (attributed, Binford 1978, L458–465).

Binford is explicit that Kuhnian language framed his own self-understanding of the stakes: the Bordes dispute was not an ordinary empirical disagreement resolvable by more hypothesis-testing, because the disagreement was over "the relevance of concepts" themselves — whether the ethnological, Kroeber-and-Kluckhohn definition of "culture" as "historically created designs for living" was an adequate causal bridge between assemblage composition and past behavior (attributed, Binford 1978, L339–420, citing Kuhn on normal science versus crisis periods).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

The published book presents a tightly instrumented research program; the underlying fieldwork was considerably less tidy. Binford conducted **multi-season participant observation among the Nunamiut of Anaktuvuk Pass, 1969–1973**, funded in overlapping and partial ways — the Wenner-Gren Foundation (grant No. 2376), the National Science Foundation, and the Doris Duke Oral History Foundation (which specifically financed 1970 and the winter-1971 season, while the NSF grant covered the main 1971–1972 work) (Binford 1978, L228–234). No single grant covered the whole project, and — as discussed below — none of them adequately covered winter.

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Does anatomical-part utility (meat, marrow, grease) predict which parts hunters transport versus abandon? | Anatomical part / kill-butchering location | 277+ caribou across 111 kill-butchering locations (1969–1973) | Controlled butchering-and-weighing experiments (3 reference animals) → utility indices; compared against field inventories | Seasonal round, 1969–1973 |
| Does a single group generate one assemblage type or many? | Site / seasonal round | ~20+ named site types across spring, summer, fall, winter (Anavik, Mask, Kakinya, Rulland, Bear site, etc.) | Multi-year site survey, excavation, and informant cross-check | 1948–1973 (fieldwork plus reconstructed earlier record) |
| Can a single carcass be traced through multiple independent archaeological deposits? | Individual animal | Billy Morry's 1972 kill-to-consumption sequence (11 caribou) | Real-time observation of one hunter's full decision chain | Spring 1972 |
| Does bone survivorship confound the utility signal? | Skeletal element | Dog-yard assemblages, multiple families | Density/survival-percentage correction applied post hoc to observed MNI | 1969–1973 |

The **utility indices** — the book's durable instrument — were built from three reference animals butchered and weighed under controlled conditions in Albuquerque, not in the field: one wild caribou bull (roughly 3–5 years old, taken August 1969 at Anaktuvuk), one old domestic ewe (about 90 months, poor nutrition), and one domestic lamb (about 6 months, good nutrition, butchered November 21, 1973) (Binford 1978, L746–802). For every anatomical part Binford's crew recorded gross weight and dry-bone weight (bone boiled roughly seven hours in an "antiformin" solution to strip all tissue). From these measurements he derived the Meat Utility Index (MUI), then — recognizing that hunters do not treat each bone as an independent unit but move "anatomical sets," bundles of adjacent parts — the **Modified General Utility Index (MGUI)**, which merges low-value parts into the value of an adjacent high-value part according to an "assimilation" rule (e.g., the low-value metatarsal assimilated to the femur) (Binford 1978, L301–378). Parallel indices tracked marrow (informed by oleic-acid assays commissioned from chemistry graduate student Robert Grange) and bone grease, which Binford further split into a "white grease" scale (articular ends of long bones, most desirable) and a "yellow grease" scale (mandible, vertebrae, ribs — rendered "only when things are bad"), because the Nunamiut themselves partitioned the categories and never mixed the two in storage (Binford 1978, L1900–1932).

Cross-species validation was built into the design: sheep and caribou standardized utility values correlated at r = .86 to r = .96 across the various indices (meat, general, white-grease), which Binford used to argue the indices generalize within the roughly 50–250 lb ungulate size range rather than being an artifact of caribou anatomy specifically (Binford 1978, L1330–1341, L2022–2045).

## The Site / Sample / Data

The fieldwork was not a clean experimental design; it was an accretion of opportunistic and constrained observation, and Binford is unusually candid about both.

**Access and ethics.** Binford's own systematic weighing and dismemberment of animals was regarded by his informants as "something approaching a sacrilege" — killing an animal without sharing it with companions was, in Nunamiut terms, an act of extreme antisocial behavior (attributed, Binford 1978, L946–950). This constrained how the controlled experiments could be run: Binford reports he never personally killed another animal for study after his early sacrilege. The bulk of the quantitative dataset therefore rests not on experimenter-controlled kills but on the accumulated inventory of *Nunamiut* kills, recorded after the fact.

**Named informants and the fieldwork crew.** The 1969 census recorded **126 permanent residents and 4 visiting Eskimos in 21 households** at Anaktuvuk Pass — 17 nuclear-family households, 2 extended-family households, and 2 households of unmarried adults (Binford 1978, L667–679). Binford's crew included Jack B. Bertram, Robert Hitchcock, and Robert Vierra (data organization in Albuquerque); **Johnny Rulland** is named repeatedly as Binford's "constant companion and friend" throughout the fieldwork, and Rulland and his sister **Jane Rulland** jointly demonstrated marrow-cracking technique on 23 long bones in late April 1971 (Binford 1978, L1071–1163). **Homer Mekiana**'s personal diary and trading records — accessed via his son Cyrus Mekiana — supplied the 1949–1950 hunting-record baseline. **Charles Amsden** kept a parallel day-by-day field record. **Dan Witter** recorded the 1971 marrow-cracking demonstration. **Elyjah Kakinya**, described as one of the most famous Nunamiut sheep hunters, supplied pelvis/scapula marrow-bone preference data and recalled making many gifts of sheep meat while living at the Kakinya site. **Simon Paneack** and his son Roosevelt Paneack demonstrated bone-breakage patterns in spring 1971. **Clyde Hugo**'s nine-dog household was monitored for 46 days (June 24–August 7, 1971) to track dog-feeding frequency. **Billy Morry**'s spring 1972 kill of 11 caribou, tracked in real time from kill to consumption, became the book's single most important behavioral control case — Binford diagrams the resulting distribution of bones across at least six separate archaeological loci from that one event (Figure 5.33; Binford 1978, L2313–2332).

**Stated limits.** Binford is explicit, in his own voice, about two structural weaknesses. First: "the data from the Nunamiut, although provocative and informative, are almost certainly not complete or exhaustive in providing clues to the variables to which men respond with varying decision strategies" (attributed, Binford 1978, L264–269). Second, and more consequential: **winter fieldwork was inadequate.** "I was unsuccessful, hence the very short winter chapter. I have, however, visited special-purpose winter locations, and I have even participated in activities in such locations" (attributed, Binford 1978, L3285–3288). This shortfall traces to the funding and permit structure described above — no single grant financed a full winter season, and the book's Chapter 8 ("Winter") is markedly thinner than the spring, summer, and fall chapters, resting more heavily on informant recall of the pre-1950 mobile period than on direct observation of the 1969–1973 fieldwork moment.

**A "Rosetta stone" case.** Among the fall residential locations dating to 1948, Binford found bone caches at the Kakinya and Rulland sites where anatomical parts had been set aside for bone-grease manufacture but the processing was never actually carried out — an accident of history that let him examine, directly, what a grease-destined assemblage looks like *before* the destructive processing step that normally erases the evidence. He calls this "a rare kind of Rosetta stone situation" (Binford 1978, L240–249), and uses it to validate the grease/utility indices as predictors of which parts would have been deleted had the processing gone forward.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Anatomical-part utility (meat, marrow, grease) predicts transport and abandonment decisions ("assimilation" of low-value to adjacent high-value parts) | MGUI/CMGUI built from 3 reference-animal dissections; tested against 111 kill-butchering locations, 277+ caribou | **Contested/reanalyzed** — the modeling framework survives; the naive reading (raw part-frequency curve = direct utility signal) does not (see Critiques) |
| A single group produces many different assemblage "types" through the seasonal round, not through ethnic/cultural difference | Comparative faunal signatures across spring, summer, fall, winter site types (kill sites, hunting stands, residential locations, ice cellars, dog yards) | **Broadly accepted** — became a founding proposition of processual site-formation theory |
| A single carcass can enter six or more separate archaeological assemblages via sequential, independent decisions | Billy Morry's 1972 kill-to-consumption sequence (bones traced to kill site, processing location, drying camp, spring residence, summer residence, at least one summer hunting camp) | **Standard cautionary teaching case** — widely cited as-is |
| Culturally patterned food preference is not a coherent Western-style construct among the Nunamiut; "preference" talk requires season/context specification | Failed direct elicitation attempts; reframed elicitation about size/fatness/condition | **Stands as ethnographic finding**, dated to the fieldwork moment |
| Kill sites and hunting stands are highly redundant/discrete in content; residential sites are flexible in location and highly variable, because they compound multiple strategies over time | Cross-site comparison across the seasonal round | **Broadly accepted**, foundational for site-type recognition in zooarchaeology |
| Bone-mineral density governs differential attrition (dog gnawing, weathering); observed assemblages must be corrected by survival percentages before being read as behavioral | Density-corrected MNI reconstructions across multiple site types | **Vindicated in principle by Binford himself**, but his own correction was later shown by Lyman and Grayson to be insufficiently controlled — see Critiques |
| Variability in Mousterian tool-assemblage "types" need not reflect ethnic/cultural difference, by analogy to the Nunamiut case | Extrapolated from Nunamiut functional-variability findings; cross-referenced explicitly to the Bordes dispute (Binford 1978, L2038–2042) | **Live/attributed** — remains Binford's position in the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]], not a resolved verdict |
| "Culture" as commonly invoked by archaeologists (patterning = shared ideas = ethnic identity) fails as an explanation for the observed Nunamiut variability | Binford's Chapter 9 synthesis, citing Leslie White's "holy water" example and Marvin Harris's "rules for breaking the rules" as foils | **Attributed theoretical claim** — programmatic, not itself an empirical finding subject to replication |

Two findings deserve fuller unpacking because they are the study's most quoted and most contested results.

**The "schlepp effect" / utility-driven culling curve.** Binford reports "a strong relationship... between the frequencies of parts and the MGUI for all assemblages with the possible exception of the data from summer dispersed kills. This relationship is uniform in that it is curvilinear and negative" (attributed, Binford 1978, L490–502): high-utility parts are systematically removed from kill sites (so under-represented there), low-utility parts left behind (over-represented). Binford further modeled a family of curves running from "bulk" strategies (carry away large quantities of high- *and* moderate-value parts, abandon only the very lowest) to "gourmet" strategies (carry only the highest-value parts), and insisted that the *shape* of the curve reflected situational contingencies — transport distance, season, available labor and dogs — "in no way... relevant to differences in the 'culture' or knowledge that the actors bring to concrete situations" (attributed, Binford 1978, L774–828).

**Confounding by consumer demand and dog attrition.** Even within the book, Binford already identifies factors that complicate a naive utility reading. Consumer demand modulates culling conservatism: "the greater the spatial separation between the animals being processed and the anticipated consumer population the less conservative the culling decisions will be" (attributed, Binford 1978, L422–436). Dog-yard assemblages require a density-based survival-percentage correction before they can be compared to human-behavioral models — "the character of the population available for use conditions the actual pattern of use" (attributed, Binford 1978, L594) — and Binford concedes his own bone-density dataset covered only one adult caribou, so his correction could not control for maturational variation in bone density across the population (attributed, Binford 1978, L1007–1027).

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

The book persuades less by narrative than by demonstrated instrument-fit, and three moves carry that persuasion.

The first is the **substitution of an "objective reference dimension" for cultural inference**. Binford states the rationale directly: "if each part is evaluated with respect to (a) the proportion of the total animal represented by the part and (b) the proportion of the gross weight of the part represented by usable meat and fat, we would have an objective reference dimension against which to view manifest decision making" — while immediately hedging, "I am not... suggest[ing] that we should always expect humans to behave in this fashion" (attributed, Binford 1978, L950–957). The hedge matters: the utility index is offered as a *baseline* against which deviation is diagnostic, not as a predictive law. Much of the reception (and much of the later critique) turns on whether readers preserved that hedge or collapsed the index into a direct behavioral prediction.

The second is **repeated cross-validation against independent informant testimony**. Across the fall and summer chapters, Binford's method is consistently to build a statistical cull model from the utility indices, then check the model's fit against what informants independently reported about a camp's history and provisioning — "informants report that this camp was well provisioned from village stores... models pointed to facts that were known ethnographically" (attributed, Binford 1978, L800–836, L913–935). This double-sourcing (quantitative model plus emic report) is presented, and functions rhetorically, as the strongest form of validation the book offers — archaeological signature and ethnographic account converging independently.

The third is **explicit adversarial contrast with rival ethnoarchaeological method**, most pointedly against Yellen's 1977 !Kung San work, which Binford characterizes as content to describe "a single 'typical' Bushman butchering episode" claimed to conform to "the standard pattern": "I must admit that variability is the name of the game in my Eskimo data" (attributed, Binford 1978, L1073–1100). Binford extends the comparison to Navajo (Binford and Bertram 1977) and Alyawara butchering, reporting comparatively low variability among those groups but high variability among the Nunamiut, and concludes there is no universal cross-cultural "method of butchering" — a direct rebuttal, staged through comparative ethnography, of any analogical shortcut from one ethnographically observed group to another. He deploys the identical rhetorical move against Yellen a second time in *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983), rejecting "a simple equation... for relating quantity of waste to duration of occupation" and insisting site function must be independently determined before debris quantity can be interpreted (attributed, *In Pursuit* 1983, R2-89).

The Conclusions chapter closes with the book's most quotable single sentence, an explicit rejection of the inferential chain Binford spent the whole study attacking: "A constant cannot explain variability... there is not the slightest support for the proposition that variability in relative frequencies of similar things at different places is necessarily referable to variability in culture" (attributed, Binford 1978, L2166–2213).

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

*Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* founded **actualistic faunal analysis** as a subfield and supplied [[processual-archaeology|processual archaeology]] with its most durable inferential apparatus. The utility indices — MUI, MGUI, IMGUI, CMGUI, and their marrow and grease analogues — became standard instruments in [[faunal-analysis|zooarchaeology]], routinely applied to assemblages the Nunamiut never touched, from Mousterian cave sites to North American bison kills. The book's central negative finding — that a single ethnic/behavioral system generates enormous assemblage-type variability without any change in "culture" — reframed how the discipline reads intersite variability generally, becoming a load-bearing premise of [[site-formation-processes|site-formation-process]] theory and of [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]] as a named research program (first explicitly labeled as such in this book's Conclusions, citing Binford 1977).

The study also functions as the empirical payoff of an argument Binford had been making since **New Perspectives in Archaeology** (1968): that archaeology needed a testable, non-circular method for assigning meaning to static remains. *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983) restates the whole trajectory for a general audience and makes explicit what the 1978 monograph leaves largely implicit — that the Nunamiut project was never really about the Nunamiut. "My primary interest was in evaluating the utility of certain concepts commonly employed by archaeologists" (attributed, Binford 1978, L2104). Read this way, the book is the second of a three-part arc: the 1968 *New Perspectives* charter calling for explanation; the 1969 recognition, crystallized on the boat home from Bordeaux, that explanation requires an independently justified inferential bridge; and the 1978 construction of that bridge.

Within the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]], the Nunamiut findings are explicitly deployed by Binford as a scale-based counter to Bordes: because five Nunamiut families' residential movements over five years "covered about as much space as is represented by the entire Dordogne area in France" (attributed, *In Pursuit* 1983, R2-71, R2-21), Binford argues Bordes's inference of four distinct ethnic groups from Mousterian tool-frequency variability within that same small region is implausible on settlement-scale grounds alone — a fresh argument the Nunamiut fieldwork made available that the 1966 position paper could only assert in principle.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

`replication_status: reanalyzed-contested`. The utility-index apparatus is a landmark precisely because it has been continuously tested, refined, and partly overturned — the following are positions, not verdicts.

- **Density-mediated attrition (Lyman and Grayson).** R. Lee Lyman and Donald K. Grayson argued, in reanalyses following Binford's own work, that bone-mineral density governs differential survivorship of skeletal parts independently of — and in ways that can mimic or erase — the utility signal Binford was measuring. Because denser, lower-utility parts (e.g., certain marrow-poor articular ends) can survive taphonomic destruction at higher rates than less dense, higher-utility parts, an assemblage's observed part-frequency profile can resemble a utility-driven transport decision when it is in fact substantially a density-driven survivorship artifact (attributed; post-dates the ingested source and is not yet independently ingested in this wiki). This is the single most consequential reanalysis of the book's central instrument: it does not discard the utility indices, but it demonstrates that any application of them to an assemblage must first control for differential bone density before the utility signal can be isolated — a correction Binford himself gestured toward (his own density-survival-percentage adjustments, Binford 1978, L933–954, L2196–2260) but, by his own admission, could not fully execute, since his 1977 bone-density dataset covered only a single adult caribou (Binford 1978, L1007–1027).
- **Index-arithmetic revision (Metcalfe & Jones 1988).** A later revision of the utility-index calculations themselves (Metcalfe & Jones 1988, attributed, not yet ingested) corrected the arithmetic underlying the MGUI family, refining rather than discarding the underlying logic.
- **Funding- and permit-driven sampling gap.** Binford's own stated limitation — the inadequately sampled winter season, a direct consequence of the fragmented, season-by-season funding structure (Wenner-Gren, NSF, Doris Duke Foundation each covering different partial windows) — means the book's seasonal-round argument rests on markedly uneven evidentiary density: strong for spring, summer, and fall; thin for winter. Any claim drawn from the winter chapter should be read with this asymmetry in mind.
- **Equifinality and taphonomic confounds acknowledged within the study itself.** Binford's own text repeatedly documents processes that complicate a direct utility-to-behavior reading — dog gnawing producing "cylinder" bone forms indistinguishable in places from soil-acid diagenesis (Binford 1978, L1174–1178); careful bone-cleaning prior to marrow-cracking leaving striations he flags as likely to be misidentified archaeologically as butchering cut marks (Binford 1978, L1037–1044); grizzly bear feeding producing spiral fractures of the same form long treated as diagnostic of human "crack-and-twist" processing (Binford 1978, L1144–1163). These are Binford's own equifinality warnings, later cited approvingly by his critics as evidence that the interpretive apparatus he built requires exactly the kind of independent control his critics argue he did not always apply.
- **Generalizability of a single ethnoarchaeological case.** Binford states his own limitation plainly — the Nunamiut data are "almost certainly not complete or exhaustive in providing clues to the variables to which men respond" (attributed, Binford 1978, L264–269) — and elsewhere frames the subsistence-security/labor-cost proposition explicitly as an untested candidate "universal law of behavioral economy" rather than an established one (attributed, Binford 1978, L1247–1252). The wider methodological literature on ethnoarchaeology has repeatedly pressed the question of how far a single case (however rich) licenses cross-cultural or cross-temporal generalization — the same caution Binford himself directs at Yellen's single-camp !Kung model.
- **The Mousterian extrapolation remains a position, not a demonstration.** The scale argument against Bordes (Nunamiut mobility area ≈ the Dordogne) is a strong analogical point but not a direct test of what produced Mousterian assemblage variability specifically; it belongs to the [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]] as Binford's position, argued from analogy, not as a wiki-voice resolution of that debate.

## Current Standing

The instruments survive; the naive reading does not. Zooarchaeology continues to use the MGUI family and its descendants as standard tools, but always now paired with an explicit bone-density/survivorship correction — the very control whose absence Lyman and Grayson's reanalysis identified as the study's chief methodological gap. The "single group, many assemblage types" finding and the "one carcass, many assemblages" demonstration (the Billy Morry case) remain uncontested and are taught as foundational to site-formation-process theory. The programmatic claim — that "culture" as conventionally invoked cannot explain the observed variability — remains Binford's attributed theoretical position, influential but not itself an empirical finding subject to replication in the ordinary sense; its force lies in the demonstrated functional account it offers as an alternative, not in falsifying any specific rival account of Mousterian ethnicity. The book is taught simultaneously as a methodological landmark and as a cautionary tale about the taphonomic assumptions embedded in behavioral indices — a dual status that is itself the clearest measure of its standing.

## Historiography and Sources

The primary text is *Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology* (Academic Press, 1978, "Studies in Archeology" series), dedicated to Albert C. Spaulding. The wiki's ingested copy is OCR'd from a severely garbled scan (heavy Spanish-keyboard character substitution throughout), extracted by an eight-range multi-agent partition plus three recovery passes; the book's approximately 90 dense data tables (Tables 1.1 through 9.3) were deliberately not transcribed in the ingest — the concepts they instantiate were captured from surrounding prose instead, per this wiki's table-handling protocol. Binford restates the same research program for a general readership in *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983), whose Chapter 4 ("The Challenge of the Mousterian") and Chapters 5–6 supply the intellectual-biographical context — the Bordes dispute, the 1967 Bordeaux year, the 1969 boat-home formulation, and the choice of the Nunamiut — that the 1978 monograph itself largely omits in favor of direct data presentation. Read together, the two books show a single research act from two distances: the 1983 book narrates the *why*; the 1978 monograph delivers the *how* and the *what*. The major reanalysis literature (Lyman and Grayson on density-mediated attrition; Metcalfe & Jones 1988 on index arithmetic) is named here as the study's most consequential afterlife but is not yet independently ingested into this wiki; when it enters the collection, the Critiques section above should be promoted from attributed summary to sourced positions.
