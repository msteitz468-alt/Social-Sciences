# McIntosh Jenné-jeno Program — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[mcintosh-jenne-jeno-study]]. Reciprocal path link from summary: `[[hubs/studies/mcintosh-jenne-jeno-study|…]]`.

**Analysis standard:** design and findings tables · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife of critique · attributed voice throughout. Built in the same session as the *Ancient Middle Niger* (2005) ingest.

## Question and Intellectual Context

By the mid-twentieth century, West African savanna cities entered world history largely through Arabic geographies and colonial ethnography: towns as late products of trans-Saharan trade, Islam, and state formation (Ghana, Mali, Songhai). Archaeology, when it looked at all, hunted **capitals and citadels** — the monumental signatures that Near Eastern and biblical-urban traditions had taught fieldworkers to expect. Raymond Mauny could walk Jenné-jeno and still feel *en terrain mouvant*, lacking diagnostic urban signatures (McIntosh 2005, reporting Mauny).

Roderick and Susan Keech McIntosh’s 1977 campaign, and the program that followed, reversed the problem. The site was **too big, too early, and too far south** (program slogan via Connah and MacDonald densifications) to be an Arab-trade epiphenomenon. Yet it lacked the palace-temple-citadel package. McIntosh’s 2005 synthesis poses the research question as **City without Citadel**: can urbanism — specialized functions toward a hinterland, heterogeneous corporate population, craft and exchange — emerge and sustain itself for over a millennium **without** coercive hierarchy as the organizing principle?

The book situates that question inside three intellectual moves:

1. **Critique of *ex astra* urban theory** — top-down, state-dependent cityscapes grounded (McIntosh argues) in buried Western values he labels Yahwist / Covenantial Piety, which make Oriental despotism and monumental control the default image of the pre-industrial city.
2. **Complexity / self-organization language** — co-evolution, phase transforms, edge-of-chaos metaphors (Kauffman, Prigogine) applied to landscape and society.
3. **[[heterarchy]]** (Crumley 1987) — horizontal ranking and multiple overlapping authority systems as alternative to nested pyramid hierarchy.

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| When and how did Middle Niger cities form? | Tell stratigraphy | Jenné-jeno units; Hambarketolo; Kaniana; Dia | Stratigraphic excavation + ceramic seriation + ¹⁴C | ~250 BCE–AD 1400 |
| What is the urban form? | Settlement complex | ~70-site Urban Cluster; 1–4 km radii | Surface collection; functional mapping (Clark 2003) | climax ~AD 700–1050 |
| How does city relate to countryside? | Hinterland | Randomized 20–50% surveys Jenné; 50% Dia | Systematic survey vs prior judgmental inventaires | multi-period |
| What environmental conditions enable specialization? | Landscape / climate | Six Middle Niger basins; multi-proxy paleoclimate | Historical ecology; geokistics | Holocene–historic |
| How are specialists accommodated without despotism? | Corporate groups | Archaeological + Mande ethnohistory | Pulse Model (hypothesis); analogy flagged | model + historic |

**As conducted, not only as advertised:** early seasons faced overwhelming surface density and no citadel “anchor” for unit placement; corporate groups (e.g. salt traders) are often **archaeologically invisible** — McIntosh repeatedly warns that specialist undercount biases urban dating and heterogeneity estimates downward. Mande specialist ideology (nyama, nyamakalaw, reputation rules) is used as a **present-to-past analogy generator**, not as proven continuity from the third century BCE; McIntosh himself notes named-group antiquity is unverifiable.

## The Site / Sample / Data

**Jenné-jeno** sits ~3 km southeast of modern Djenné on the Upper Inland Niger Delta floodplain. The main mound exceeds 1 km in length; Hambarketolo links by causeway. Depths reach multi-meter mud-brick wall-melt and occupation deposits — the classic tell problem of sampling a city with small windows.

**Ceramic chronology** (S. McIntosh 1995 via 2005): Phase I/II Fineware ~250 BCE–AD 400; III Painted Ware ~400–900; IV Fine Channeled and Impressed ~900–1400; V under modern Jenné. McIntosh claims one of West Africa’s largest coherent ¹⁴C series for this sequence.

**Dia** provides a peer Urban Cluster: foundation pushed into the early first millennium BCE; five horizons; Fineware longevity differs from Jenné-jeno (not the same chronometer); urban proportions possibly by 500 BCE (McIntosh 2005 summarizing Bedaux et al.).

**Survey data quality hierarchy:** judgmental Méma vehicular and inventaire surveys productive for presence but non-extrapolatable; systematic randomized Jenné and Dia surveys support density, hierarchy, and abandonment comparisons.

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (analysed)

**On discovery and occlusion:**

> “It can be said, without much fear of contradiction, that the ancient Middle Niger is the most recent of the world’s major urban civilizations to be discovered.” (McIntosh 2005, Ch. 1)

Rhetoric escalates stakes: not a regional curiosity but a **world civilization** occluded by theory. The move licenses comparative chapter ambitions and the claim that Middle Niger should retrain global urban archaeology.

**On citadel expectation:**

> “Where is the citadel? Where are the (now-underground) palaces, temples, the residences of the privileged?” (McIntosh 2005, Ch. 4)

The interrogative form converts absence into a research result. Connah’s later caution targets exactly this move: absence of evidence for hierarchy is not positive evidence of heterarchy.

**On urban definition (Trigger expansion):**

McIntosh endorses Trigger’s (1972) city as settlement performing specialized functions for a hinterland, then stresses **qualitative heterogeneity** of corporate population — not elite monuments — as the archaeological recognition problem. That definitional choice makes survey and craft-trace central and palace optional.

**On heterarchy:**

> highly complex society organized horizontally… multiple overlapping and competing agencies of resistance to centralization (paraphrased program language at McIntosh 2005: 187 area; Connah quotes the horizontal formulation)

The political thesis is inseparable from the settlement pattern: clustering as **instrument of resistance**, not failed nucleation.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

1. **Rename the anomaly as the type case** — City without Citadel becomes eponymous, not deficient.
2. **Genealogical unmasking** — Yahwism / Oriental despotism narrative explains why good fieldworkers missed cities; turns theory history into method critique.
3. **Complexity science metaphor** — phase transforms, edge of chaos, self-organization supply a process vocabulary when political hierarchy is denied.
4. **Ethnographic bridge** — Mande specialist corporations and reputation ethics supply the “how peace is kept” mechanism that excavation cannot show; risk of presentism is structural to the argument.
5. **Comparative mirror** — Mesopotamia/Nile/China read *through Middle Niger lenses* so the book exports its anomaly rather than remaining African exceptionalism.
6. **Vibratory / harmonic model** (fig. 6.4) — avoids pure heterarchy vs pure hierarchy binary; nests both as oscillating ideals.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Teaching:** Cambridge Case Studies vehicle placed Middle Niger on standard early-societies reading lists beside Mesopotamia and Oaxaca.
- **African archaeology:** Broke “silent millennia”; forced indigenous-origins consensus against Levtzion-style external stimulus (Connah, Sinclair, MacDonald, Monroe).
- **Complex-society theory:** Lead empirical case for Crumley-style [[heterarchy]] in urban contexts; cited in dual-processual / collective-action prehistories as precedent for non-exclusionary power.
- **Method:** Systematic hinterland survey as mandatory for urban recognition (Trigger’s definition operationalized).

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

**Connah (2016):** Accepts importance of heterarchy; insists many claims rest on **lack of hierarchy evidence**; excavation still small vs site size; premature to deny stratification — especially given gold, later empires, and Ghana’s documentary appearance.

**Handbook refinement (MacDonald 2013):** Dia sequence and Fadugu/Markadugu typology differentiate state-power towns from commercial/spiritual towns that may endure semi-autonomously — nuances the “no state” reading without discarding early urbanism.

**Sample and visibility:** McIntosh’s own warnings about invisible corporations undercut strong negative claims even inside the program.

**Analogy:** Wiki Evidence-Class rules require Mande ethnohistory to stay marked as analogy; deep-time “core rules” are theoretical positions.

**Comparative chapter:** Secondary synthesis of others’ surveys (Adams & Nissen Warka; Chinese regional surveys; Hierakonpolis) — valuable as hypothesis generator, not as substitute for those regions’ primary literatures.

## Current Standing

As of the 2005–2016 densification layer in this vault: chronology, indigenous timing, clustered form, and trade-as-intensifier are **robust program results**. The strong heterarchy / anti-citadel political package remains **productive and contested**. The Pulse Model and self-organizing landscape remain **heuristic frameworks** with partial archaeological ground-truth. Further extensive horizontal exposure at urban sites would be the decisive test Connah demands.

## Extended Design Critique: Sampling the Uncitadelled City

The methodological drama of Jenné-jeno is the mismatch between **city scale** and **window size**. A tell more than a kilometer long, packed with mud architecture that melts into its own matrix, cannot be “opened” like a small village. The first 1977 season had to choose test units without a palace mound or temple platform to serve as the traditional high-value target. McIntosh’s narrative of Konaré’s one-week visit underlines the field pressure: patterns had to be tickled from surface chaos — furnaces, urn burials, round and square houses, tons of potsherds — before excavation could begin.

That pressure shapes what kinds of claims the program can responsibly make. Positive claims about **craft sequence** (iron from lowest levels; copper by mid-first millennium CE; gold before ~850–900; bead imports) rest on stratigraphic association and laboratory work and travel well. Claims about **population** are explicitly labeled guesses. Claims about **absence of elites** are bounded by excavated volume: three decades of work is historically impressive for West African tells, yet still a small fraction of 33 ha plus satellites. McIntosh’s own Chapter 4 discussion of archaeologically invisible corporations (salt, some traders) is the program’s internal critique: heterogeneity will be **underestimated**, and therefore the date and rate of urban emergence may be pushed later than social reality.

The survey program is the necessary counterpart. Trigger’s definition — city as specialist functions toward a hinterland — makes hinterland survey **constitutive of urban recognition**, not optional context. McIntosh’s hierarchy of survey quality (judgmental inventaire < vehicular transect < systematic randomized pedestrian) is as important a contribution as any single find. The 1977–1990s Jenné series and the matched Dia 50% survey allow statements about density, abandonment waves, and the difference between waterway-linear Dia patterning and satellite-orbit Jenné patterning. Prior Méma surveys could show “many sites” without licensing process comparison across basins — a Galton-like problem of non-comparable case construction inside one river system.

## Extended Findings: Chronology, Cluster, and Climate

### Chronological anchors

The ceramic phases remain the teaching spine:

- **~250 BCE:** foundation of Jenné-jeno (Phase I/II Fineware).
- **~AD 400:** transition to Painted Ware; urban prosperity trajectory; metallurgical reorganization (smelting outward, smithing inward).
- **~AD 800:** 33 ha; wall; population guesses enter the 10k–26k band for the 1 km complex.
- **~AD 700–1050:** clustering maximum (Clark).
- **~AD 1000–1100:** nucleation shift — activities concentrate on fewer larger sites.
- **~AD 1400:** abandonment of principal mound; successor Islamic Jenné; Phase V under the modern town.

Dia complicates any single-origin story: foundation into the early first millennium BCE, possible urban proportions by 500 BCE, Fineware lasting ~2,000 years at Shoma (hence **not** a universal Middle Niger clock). Peer urbanism and multiple basins matter for the Pulse Model’s landscape-scale claims.

### Environmental co-evolution

Chapter 2’s historical ecology is not backdrop. The Middle Niger is an **anastomosing** multi-basin system (Upper Delta, Macina, Erg of Bara, Lakes–Niger Bend, Azawad, Méma) with dunes, levees, backswamps, and laterite margins — a mosaic of niches for rice, millet, sorghum, flood-recession (*décru*), fishing, and herding. The Niger is relatively **sediment-poor** and nutrient-depleted compared with pre-Aswan Nile ideals; wealth here is diversity and risk-buffering, not simply silt gift. Paleoclimate phase shifts at multiple timescales (McIntosh’s compiled Phases, ocean and lake proxies) create the “pulse” that the social model names. Geokistics — specialist knowledge of soils, salts, insects, drainage — is how McIntosh bridges environmental description to the claim that specialists see different landscapes.

### The political package

Absence of citadel is elevated to positive theory via three steps:

1. **Definitional** — city = hinterland functions + corporate heterogeneity, not monumentality.
2. **Organizational** — heterarchy and reputation canons replace king/gatekeeper information flow (gatekeepers as “scleroses”).
3. **Spatial** — polynucleation keeps corporate groups separate enough to resist monopoly.

The same package explains **sustainability**: 1,600+ years of urban occupation without the collapse signature McIntosh attributes to fused hierarchy. Chapter 5’s Sustainability Paradox asks how complexity can increase while resilience holds; the answer is pluri-corporate canons and resistance to despotism — until 12th–15th-century reorganization under imperial and climatic pressures. Post-imperial dualism then retains heterarchical local forms under external hierarchy (Mali, Songhai, Ségou, the *dina*).

## Extended Comparative Chapter (Ch. 6)

McIntosh’s comparative method is explicitly counterfactual: **if urban archaeology had begun on the Middle Niger**, would Uruk, Hierakonpolis, and Longshan have been excavated and narrated differently?

**Mesopotamia.** Early–middle Uruk appears “poised” for clustered urbanism (Warka Survey trajectory via Adams & Nissen), then takes a “darker” path toward nucleated, elite-heavy Early Dynastic cities. Recent work on decentralized household economies (Pollock; Wright) and Mashkan-shapir’s surface plan (Stone & Zimansky) supply nested alternatives — a “tapestry of power” rather than pure despotism. Wilkinson’s anastomosing-landscape critique of Wittfogelian irrigation labor as the driver of settlement simplification resonates with Middle Niger fluvial analogies.

**Nile.** Predynastic parallel polities (Hierakonpolis, Naqada, This) show specialization and up-wadi clustering; after unification, the preferred Middle Niger city definition “withers” into Wilson’s civilization-without-cities / Trigger’s dispersed pattern under territorial kingship. Egypt becomes the case of the **aborted** self-organizing city.

**China.** Chang’s network-of-specialized-parts and Ecosocial Interdependence already strained nucleated-city expectations at Shang centers. New surveys (Liu, Underhill, and others as of McIntosh’s 2005 reading) break Zhongyuan core–periphery and reveal early clustered hierarchies — Shandong plans McIntosh says “might have been plucked wholesale out of the Middle Niger floodplain.”

The closing **harmonic model** refuses either/or: hierarchy and heterarchy oscillate and nest. Residual alternatives hide even inside despotic cityscapes. Final rhetorical transfer of authority — “Abana!” — returns celebration of the cities to Mande discoverers rather than only to the foreign archaeologist.

## Fieldwork Phases and Institutional Context

The program is not a single season’s monograph but a **research tradition**:

1. **1977 breakthrough season** — first units and regional survey; Konaré present; surface pattern recognition under extreme assemblage density.
2. **1980s expansion** — deeper sequence control; ceramic typology maturation; Dia exploratory work (1986–87); growing radiocarbon series.
3. **1990s synthesis and hinterland** — systematic survey stages; population and clustering arguments; engagement with complexity theory and Crumley heterarchy.
4. **Late 1990s–2000s Dia international program** — multi-national team horizontal expansion at a peer cluster; forces chronological humility (earlier foundations; Fineware non-transferability).
5. **2005 Cambridge synthesis** — teaching case that freezes a program moment for comparative audiences.

Malian institutional partnership (Direction du Patrimoine; later presidential figure Konaré) is not incidental color: McIntosh’s Ch. 5 closing links deep-time heterarchical political culture to modern decentralization rhetoric. That move is **position**, not archaeological finding — it shows how the program’s political imagination travels into the present.

Funding and laboratory networks (US, UK, France, Senegal, Mali) make the metallurgical and dating claims possible; the book acknowledges scores of specialists. Terracotta looting from the 1930s onward is both a data loss and a public-history driver of outside interest in the mounds.

## What the 2005 Book Is and Is Not

**Is:** programmatic synthesis; best single-volume entry to the McIntosh reading of Middle Niger urbanism; explicit theory of alternative urban pathways; comparative essay.

**Is not:** full multi-volume site report; complete specialist appendices; final word on Dia; resolution of heterarchy debate; substitute for Adams on Mesopotamia or Chang/Liu on China.

Readers using only 2005 should track S. McIntosh 1995 for ceramic/14C detail and later Dia publications for revised early horizons.




## Teaching Use and Misuse

*Ancient Middle Niger* is designed for the Case Studies in Early Societies classroom. Productive uses: (1) contrast with nucleated Near Eastern city chapters in the same series; (2) force students to separate **urbanism** from **state**; (3) practice reading negative evidence critically; (4) map survey quality hierarchies. Misuses: treating 2005 population figures as census facts; equating Mande ethnohistory with first-millennium ethnicity; citing heterarchy as proven rather than argued; ignoring Dia’s chronological challenge to a Jenné-only origin story. Pair with Connah’s caution chapter and a primary ceramic report when possible.


## One-paragraph verdict for vault users

Use this program as the **empirical backbone** for early Inland Niger Delta urbanism and as the **lead teaching case** for heterarchical urban pathways. Treat chronology, clustering, and pre-Islamic interregional exchange as strong attributed findings. Export comparative insights as questions for Mesopotamia, Egypt, and China specialists, not as overrides of those regional literatures.
