# African Political Systems — Deep Analysis

> Studies Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]]. Source: [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940]].

**Analysis standard:** design reconstructed · findings table · verbatim load-bearing passages · afterlife · nine sections. All substantive claims in **attributed voice**.

## Question and Intellectual Context

*African Political Systems* (1940) is the charter comparative volume of British [[political-anthropology|political anthropology]]. Edited by [[fortes-meyer|Meyer Fortes]] and [[evans-pritchard-e-e|E. E. Evans-Pritchard]] for the International African Institute, with a programmatic preface by [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|A. R. Radcliffe-Brown]], it gathered eight condensations of recent African fieldwork into a single argument: African polities can be compared scientifically once political organization is abstracted as a structural problem, and once political philosophy’s normative and conjectural-historical methods are set aside.

The book sits at the intersection of three moments. First, the **[[structural-functionalism|structural-functional]]** program — social anthropology as natural science of social institutions, comparison in place of experiment, classification of types before laws (Radcliffe-Brown). Second, the **fieldwork revolution**’s African turn: Malinowski- and R-B-trained ethnographers producing intensive political ethnographies under colonial governments. Third, **Indirect Rule**: the editors’ note and introduction explicitly address administrators; understanding “native” political systems is framed as both science and policy.

Against what does the volume react? Against armchair political philosophy that invents origins of the state; against untested universal conquest theories of political inequality; against treating “primitive society” as a single residual type. It also reacts, more quietly, against pure culture-trait ethnology: the editors insist culture and social organization must not be confused — identical functions can wear different cultural idioms (mystical values of office among Bemba, Banyankole, Kede, Tallensi).

## Design and Methods as Conducted

| Question | Unit | Sample / Site | Method | Period |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| What are the basic types and principles of African political organization? | Political system (abstracted aspect of social structure) | 8 societies across southern, east, west, central Africa | Condensed intensive ethnography + editorial structural comparison | Fieldwork ~1930s; pub. 1940 |
| How is order maintained with and without government? | Segmentary vs centralized political structure | Group A (5) vs Group B (3) | Comparative typology from case essays | Same |
| What integrates political communities beyond force and interest? | Mystical/ritual values of office and structural nodes | Cross-case in Introduction | Functional abstraction | Same |

**As advertised:** representative sample; advanced field methods; theory subordinated to concise description; contribution to comparative politics.

**As conducted (critical reconstruction):**

1. **Editorial pre-structuring.** The Introduction’s Group A/B split organizes what each essay is asked to do. Group A writers describe government; Group B writers invent answers to “what is political structure without government?” The typology is not a finding that emerges after blind comparison — it is the design frame.

2. **Condensation genre.** Each chapter compresses a monograph’s worth of material. The Nuer essay is explicitly a short version of work already partly published and about to appear as *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]*. Detail, dissent, and awkward cases are under pressure.

3. **Colonial production of data.** Fieldwork funded or enabled by colonial governments (Sudan government for Evans-Pritchard; British African territories for others). Pacification, courts, and Indirect Rule are both *objects* of description and *conditions* of observation. Fortes notes Tale military subjugation c. 1911 and defers full Indirect Rule analysis post-1933.

4. **No Galton control.** Cases are historically and regionally clustered (southern Bantu kingdoms; West African Voltaic and Nupe; Nilotic; Great Lakes). Shared descent and colonial experience make them non-independent for statistical inference. The book is a **theoretical sample**, not a survey.

5. **Radcliffe-Brown’s force definition** supplies the analytic object: political organization = control/regulation of physical force within a territorial framework — covering both “states” and “stateless” systems without the philosophical fiction of the State as sovereign person.

## The Site / Sample / Data

Eight peoples, mapped in the volume:

| Essay | Author | Group | Documentation focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zulu | Max Gluckman | A | Mpande-era kingdom vs European dual authority (field 1936–38) |
| Ngwato | I. Schapera | A | Tswana chiefship, wards, regiments, Protectorate law |
| Bemba | Audrey Richards | A | Matrilineal paramountcy, descent dogma, 1929 ordinances |
| Ankole | K. Oberg | A | Bahima/Bairu stratification, royal kraal, Bagyendanwa |
| Kede | S. F. Nadel | A | Riverain Nupe section; minority state; autonomy claims |
| Bantu Kavirondo | Günter Wagner | B | Logoli & Vugusu; clan order; no tribal government |
| Tallensi | M. Fortes | B | Clanship cleavage; chiefs & *tindaanas*; Earth cult |
| Nuer | E. E. Evans-Pritchard | B | Ordered anarchy; segmentary opposition; feud |

Regional and ecological diversity is real (pastoral kingdoms, matrilineal Central African chiefdoms, river trade polities, dense hoe-farming acephalous systems, Nilotic savannah). What is **not** sampled: Islamic Sudanic empires in depth, coastal mercantile states, Ethiopian highlands, acephalous rainforest systems, women’s political institutions as primary object, urban colonial politics.

## Findings in Detail

| Finding | Evidence base | Later status |
|---|---|---|
| Two main types: Group A (government + stratified power) vs Group B (no government; no sharp rank/wealth) | Editorial synthesis of 8 essays; membership listed | Contested as over-binary; still used pedagogically |
| Only lineage (unilateral corporate descent), not bilateral kinship, creates political corporations | Intro §V; Tallensi & Nuer essays | Contested (practice vs ideology of lineage) |
| Political organization = regulation of physical force | R-B preface | Enduring Weber-adjacent definition in British school |
| Group B order = equilibrium of balanced segments + self-help | Intro; Nuer; Tallensi; Wagner | Core of segmentary model; heavily reanalyzed |
| Mystical values of office integrate beyond utility | Intro §XIII; kingship/Earth cult cases | Influential; functional circularity risks noted later |
| Colonial rule transforms A and B in opposite directions | Intro §XII; several case essays | Productive; colonial “invention of tradition” literature extends it |
| Conquest may produce stratified states (e.g. Ankole pattern) but is not universal origin of inequality | Intro §VIII; Oberg; R-B on Bahima/Bairu | Editors already cautious; later historiography more historical |
| Culture ≠ political structure; same function, different idioms | Intro §II | Enduring analytic hygiene for comparative work |

### Load-bearing case mechanisms (attributed)

- **Zulu (Gluckman):** Nation as allegiance to king + territory; age-regiments; royal ritual; **conflicting loyalties** as check; under European rule, dual authority (chief vs magistrate) and revival of royal symbols.
- **Ngwato (Schapera):** Chief as administrative, judicial, military, ritual apex; ward/*kgotla* structure; regiments; colonial proclamations remaking chiefly powers.
- **Bemba (Richards):** Matrilineal succession; dogma of blood/spirits; headman “herds” people; *bakabilo* ritual specialists can refuse service — mystical check on office.
- **Ankole (Oberg):** Pastoral aristocracy / agricultural commoners as political statuses; tribute; sacred drum cult; succession conflict.
- **Kede (Nadel):** Riverine trade ecology → colonial expansion formula; offices of state; ideology of autonomy within larger Nupe/Fulani/British frames.
- **Logoli/Vugusu (Wagner):** Political unit redefined by function not institution; authority from primogeniture, wealth, *omugasa*, war reputation, ritual, age — not a pyramidal chiefship.
- **Tallensi (Fortes):** No one ruled all Tallensi; major cleavage (Talis/Namoos) structures war alliances; complementary **chiefship (*na’am*)** and **Earth-priestship (*tindaanaship*)**; lineage elders; colonial courts enhance secular chiefly power after 1932.
- **Nuer (Evans-Pritchard):** “Ordered anarchy”; tribe as largest arbitration unit; segmentary fission/fusion; lineage skeleton; leopard-skin chief as mediator not ruler; age-sets non-administrative.

## Analytic and Rhetorical Moves

**1. The typology as discovery claim.** OCR-garbled in the 1950 impression’s text layer but restated clearly: Group A vs Group B. The rhetoric presents the split as something “noted” in the essays, yet the essays were commissioned and written within that contrast. The move is legitimate as **ideal-type comparison** if read as Weberian clarification; it is misleading if read as unsupervised clustering.

**2. Stripping culture to function.** Editors: comparative study must be “on an abstract plane where social processes are stripped of their cultural idiom and are reduced to functional terms.” This is the British SF warrant for equating Bemba royal ritual with Tallensi Earth cult as “mystical values.” It enables comparison and risks erasing historical particularity — the Boasian objection.

**3. Rejection of the State fiction (Radcliffe-Brown).**

> “The State, in this sense, does not exist in the phenomenal world; it is a fiction of the philosophers. What does exist is an organization… The political organization of a society is that aspect of the total organization which is concerned with the control and regulation of the use of physical force.”

This dissolves the “do stateless societies have politics?” pseudo-problem and licenses Group B chapters. It also aligns anthropology with a coercive definition of the political adjacent to Weber, while refusing sovereignty metaphysics.

**4. Mystical values vs utilitarian interest.**

> “Bonds of utilitarian interest between individuals and between groups are not as strong as the bonds implied in common attachment to mystical symbols.”

The closing wager of the Introduction: political dominance over other social ties rests on sacred solidarity. Force is necessary but not sufficient; colonial force fails to generate moral ties. This is both a theory of integration and a quiet critique of pure coercion theories of the state.

**5. Colonial dual transformation.** The Introduction’s opposite effects on A and B is among the book’s sharpest analytic passages: paramount as precarious pivot; segmentary collapse into bureaucracy. It anticipates later “invention of tradition” and “decentralized despotism” arguments without using those vocabularies.

## Reception and Theoretical Impact

- **Immediate:** Became the standard teaching collection for African political systems in British and Commonwealth anthropology departments.
- **Subfield foundation:** [[political-anthropology]] crystallizes as comparative study of government and stateless order, with APS as charter.
- **Conceptual exports:** [[group-a-group-b-political-systems]], [[ordered-anarchy]], [[segmentary-lineage-system]], [[complementary-opposition]], [[mystical-values-of-office]], modern anthropological [[chieftainship]] studies.
- **Manchester and after:** Gluckman’s later conflict/processual school grows from Zulu material and from dissatisfaction with pure equilibrium readings of SF — APS is part of that genealogy.
- **Policy audience:** Cited in Indirect Rule debates; entanglement of anthropology and administration is part of the book’s historical meaning, not an external accident.

## Critiques, Restudies, and Replications

| Position | Claim (strongest form) | Evidence orientation |
|---|---|---|
| A. Program success | APS correctly identified major structural contrast and researchable mechanisms of acephalous order | Generative research program 1940–65 |
| B. Segmentary model overstated | Balanced opposition is ideology or anthropologist’s model more than practice | Later Nuer/Tallensi restudies; [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]] |
| C. Colonial artifact | “Ordered anarchy” and clean segmentary maps partly produced by pacification and administrative need for units | Colonial archives; fieldwork timing |
| D. Binary too coarse | Intermediate forms, ritual chiefship without monopoly of force, colonial chiefs on segmentary bases break A/B | Comparative African history |
| E. Functional teleology | Equilibrium and “function of symbols” explain by assuming the stability they describe | Wider SF critique |
| F. Historical thinness | Conquest, trade, Islam, long-distance state systems underplayed relative to synchronic structure | Historians of Africa |

## Current Standing

- **Unavoidable classic** for history of anthropology and for reading mid-century Africanist ethnography.
- **Pedagogy:** A/B still taught as entry map, usually with immediate qualifications.
- **Research:** Processual, historical, and postcolonial political anthropology have largely replaced APS equilibrium language; case ethnographies remain archival resources.
- **Collection role:** First multi-region comparative African political source in this wiki; pairs with *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]* for Group B depth and with future kinship volumes (e.g. *African Systems of Kinship and Marriage*) for the institutional companion frame.

### Verbatim passage analysis — Group A/B

The Introduction’s core typology (reconstructed from clear restatements where the 1950 impression’s OCR collapses) commits the program to institutional criteria:

> Group A consists of those societies which have centralized authority, administrative machinery and judicial institutions — in short, a government — and in which cleavages of wealth, privilege and status correspond to the distribution of power and authority. … Group B … lack centralized authority, administrative machinery, and constituted judicial institutions — in short which lack government — and in which there are no sharp divisions of rank, status, or wealth.

What the words **commit** the editors to: (1) government as a bundle of three institutional clusters, not a vague “centralization”; (2) stratification **aligned with** power as part of the type, not an optional extra; (3) Group B defined by **absences** plus relative egalitarianism. What followers sometimes made of it: a simple “king vs no king” cartoon, or a developmental ladder from B to A. The editors do **not** rank B as earlier or inferior; they treat both as structural solutions to order. The “primitive states / stateless societies” gloss is presented as optional language for those who define the state by governmental institutions — not as the editors’ preferred theoretical vocabulary (Radcliffe-Brown had already warned that state/stateless labels “do not help us… to solve our problems”).

### Verbatim passage analysis — mystical values

> An African ruler is not to his people merely a person who can enforce his will on them. He is the axis of their political relations, the symbol of their unity and exclusiveness, and the embodiment of their essential values.

This is the book’s anti-Hobbesian and anti-pure-coercion move. Force exists (especially Group A), but **moral axis** language elevates office above the will of the office-holder. The same section denies Europeans mythical warranty: colonial power can replace secular functions and cannot enter sacred precincts. Read against the grain, this also **romanticizes** indigenous legitimacy and underplays coercion internal to African polities — a bias later critics of functionalism and of colonial anthropology would press. Read with the grain, it is a hard claim that political solidarity requires non-utilitarian common symbols, tested by the observation that kingship ritual often outlasts stripped secular power.

### Verbatim passage analysis — Radcliffe-Brown’s force definition

> The political organization of a society is that aspect of the total organization which is concerned with the control and regulation of the use of physical force.

This definition does three jobs. It **includes** feud, self-help, and war as political data (so Group B is not “pre-political”). It **excludes** reducing politics to kinship sentiment or economy alone. It **demotes** the State as sovereign person. The cost is a possible under-theorization of soft power, ideology, and consent — except that the editors reintroduce consent and moral ties through mystical values. The book thus runs two theories of the political in parallel: R-B’s force regulation and Fortes/EP’s symbolic integration. Their joint program is force-plus-sacred-solidarity.

## Extended Case Architectures

### Group A architectures compared

The five Group A essays do not describe the same state form. Gluckman’s Zulu is a militarized nation forged by nineteenth-century expansion, then dismembered and rearticulated under British rule — a **conquest-military** pathway with age-regiments as the administrative-military spine. Schapera’s Ngwato is a **Tswana chiefship** with wards, public counsel, and regimental labor — less empire, more ranked polity under Protectorate law. Richards’s Bemba is a **matrilineal paramountcy** where descent dogma and spirit guardianship of land underwrite office; ritual refusal by *bakabilo* is a structural check unfamiliar in the Zulu material. Oberg’s Ankole is a **stratified pastoral/agricultural kingdom** in which tribute and sacred kingship articulate inequality Oberg links to conquest. Nadel’s Kede is a **specialized riverain minority state** — demographically small, economically strategic, ideologically autonomous within larger Nupe and Fulani frames — showing Group A without demographic majority rule.

Editors’ “government” criterion holds across these variants; the **cultural content** of kingship does not. That is the point of the mystical-values abstraction.

### Group B architectures compared

Wagner’s Logoli and Vugusu lack tribal government; leadership is polycentric and situational (wealth, primogeniture, *omugasa*, warrior fame, ritual, age). Fortes’s Tallensi add a **dual ritual-political polarity** — chiefs and Earth-priests — and a society-wide cleavage that structures war without producing a sovereign. Evans-Pritchard’s Nuer supply the cleanest **segmentary territorial** model and the phrase-making “ordered anarchy.” Group B is thus not one mechanism but a family: clan-law systems, dual ritual office systems, and segmentary pastoral polities. The Introduction’s claim that Group B essays must invent what counts as political structure is autobiographically true of the research process.

### Colonial dual transformation — worked example

Editors argued Group A and Group B respond oppositely to European rule. Case material supports the sketch without exhausting it. Among Zulu and Ngwato, chiefs become agents of magistrates and lose monopoly of legitimate force while sometimes gaining new court powers and cash; royal ritual may revive as identity politics under constraint (Gluckman). Among Tallensi, Fortes records that precolonial absence of anyone who “could call out all the Tallensi” coexists with post-1911 respect for white power and, after 1932, chiefs’ courts that enhance secular authority inside clans while still failing to erase structural cleavages (the Tongo chief still cannot administer all Talis). Among Nuer, government suppression of prophets and of raiding removes the rare supra-segmentary ritual entrepreneurs and bans the self-help that made ordered anarchy work — pushing toward the bureaucratic absorption the Introduction predicts for Group B.

## Empirical Program: Hits and Misses

**Hits (what APS explained that predecessors could not, in its own terms):**

1. **Politics without the State** as a positive structural problem, not a lack.
2. **Comparative African sample** at field-work standard, replacing traveller anecdotes for teaching.
3. **Lineage vs kinship** distinction for political corporations.
4. **Ritual as political infrastructure**, not survivals or “belief” alone.
5. **Colonial transformation** as structural experiment (force monopoly externalized).

**Misses and accumulating anomalies:**

1. **Women’s political roles** largely peripheral in the essays’ political definition (force + office).
2. **Longue durée and regional systems** (trade diasporas, jihads, Great Lakes state systems beyond Ankole sketch) thin.
3. **Internal coercion and exploitation** in Group A sometimes softened by integration rhetoric.
4. **Segmentary maps** cleaner than messy migration, clientship, and multiethnic absorption (Nuer Dinka absorption is in EP’s full monograph more than the APS short chapter).
5. **Intermediate forms** (ritual chiefs, colonial warrant chiefs, federations) overflow the binary.

## What Survived

| APS element | Where it lives now |
|---|---|
| Force definition of political organization | Political anthropology textbooks; dialogue with Weberian state theory |
| Stateless order as analytical object | Anarchy/order debates; some legal pluralism |
| Segmentary opposition toolkit | Even critics use the vocabulary to reject it |
| Mystical/sacred kingship as integration | Ritual and power studies; divine kingship revisits |
| Colonial remaking of chiefs | Historical anthropology; Mamdani-style decentralized despotism debates (later) |
| Multi-case African comparison | Enduring genre (though methods changed) |
| Group A/B labels | Pedagogy more than research frontiers |

What largely **did not** survive unchanged: equilibrium functionalism as explanation; confidence that eight cases reveal “all major principles”; administrative optimism about Indirect Rule informed by anthropology.




### Design ethics and the administrative audience

The Editors’ Note states the book is an experiment in collaborative research and an attempt to focus a major problem of African sociology, and hopes it will be of use to those administering African peoples. That sentence is not a confession of bad faith; it is a **production fact**. Research permissions, funding, and the very existence of “tribes” as administrative units shape the sample. The same note flags the urgency of studying systems “under the influence of European rule” before the opportunity passes — salvage framing applied to political structure. A fair historiography records both the genuine comparative ambition and the administrative entanglement without collapsing one into the other.

### Relation to *The Nuer* (1940)

Evans-Pritchard’s APS chapter is a **condensation** for comparative placement as the extreme Group B type. The full monograph *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]* expands oecology, cattle idiom, structural time, and lineage-political mapping. This hub treats APS as the comparative architecture; the Nuer study page and its hub (where built) own monographic depth. Do not cite the APS short chapter as if it contained the full monograph’s evidence base.
