# Feminist Anthropology — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. The `theories/` summary ([[feminist-anthropology]]) owns core-claims/origins/proponents/critiques/status at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: the summary links here by full path; this page's `theory_page` points back.

[[feminist-anthropology|Feminist anthropology]] is the research program that made the cross-cultural position of women — and, later, the cultural construction of gender — a central anthropological problem. Its charter artifact is *[[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Woman, Culture, and Society]]* (Rosaldo & Lamphere, eds., Stanford 1974), read with Ortner's 1972/74 nature/culture essay and the parallel Reiter collection (1975). This analysis works the **universalist founding phase** as stated in the 1974 volume (now primary-ingested), then tracks the internal demolition of that universalism in the 1980s and what survived into contemporary practice.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Three pressures converge in 1970–74.

**1. Second-wave feminism as research engine.** The editors open from lived politics, not abstract curiosity. The Preface records a Stanford 1971 undergraduate course, "Women in Cross-Cultural Perspective," taught by [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] with Jane Collier and a collective of female graduate students; the lectures became the seed of the book. The Introduction frames the project with [[beauvoir-simone-de|de Beauvoir]] ("Why is Woman 'The Other'?") and [[mead-margaret|Mead]] (activities valued when men do them, devalued when women do):

> "In every known society, the male's need for achievement can be recognized. Men may cook, or weave, or dress dolls or hunt hummingbirds, but if such activities are appropriate occupations of men, then the whole society, men and women alike, votes them as important. When the same occupations are performed by women, they are regarded as less important." — Mead, *Male and Female*, epigraph to the volume

**2. Androcentrism of classic ethnography.** The editors charge that anthropology "followed our own culture's ideological bias in treating women as relatively invisible and describing what are largely the activities and interests of men," with only a "handful of ethnographies that take a woman's perspective" (Kaberry, Landes, Fernea, Strathern, Wolf, etc.). The program is thus a *reflexivity* movement before "reflexivity" was a buzzword: knowledge of society had been produced as if half of social actors did not count.

**3. Rejection of two false explanations.** Against **biological determinism**, the volume holds that biology is interpreted through culture; against **historical matriarchy** (Bachofen, Morgan, some second-wave writers), it follows anthropological consensus that no observed society gives women publicly recognized power surpassing men's — while treating matriarchy myths as *ideology* (Bamberger) rather than usable prehistory. A preferred evolutionary counter-narrative is already present: **Woman the Gatherer** (Linton; Tanner & Zihlman in progress) over exclusive [[man-the-hunter-woman-the-gatherer-debate|Man-the-Hunter]] origin stories.

Intellectual resources: de Beauvoir's existentialist feminism; Lévi-Straussian [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]]; Weber/M. G. Smith on power vs. authority; object-relations psychoanalysis via [[chodorow-nancy|Chodorow]]; Engelsian materialism via Sacks — already a plural toolkit inside one "program."

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The 1974 architecture has two floors: a **universalist explanatory floor** (first three essays) and an **agency-within-asymmetry floor** (the rest of the book).

### Floor 1 — Why secondary status is (claimed) universal

| Claim (attributed) | Mechanism | Carrier text |
|---|---|---|
| Sexual asymmetry is a present universal | Male cultural value + male authority everywhere | Rosaldo & Lamphere intro; Rosaldo overview; Ortner |
| Domestic/public opposition is structural | Mother–child domestic vs linking/ranking public | Rosaldo → [[domestic-public-dichotomy]] |
| Feminine personality is produced by mothering | Personal vs positional identification | [[chodorow-nancy]] |
| Woman is symbolized as closer to nature | Body + domestic role + psyche → intermediate position | [[ortner-sherry]] → [[nature-culture-distinction]] |

Rosaldo's load-bearing formulation:

> "Everywhere, from those societies we might want to call most egalitarian to those in which sexual stratification is most marked, men are the locus of cultural value. Some area of activity is always seen as exclusively or predominantly male, and therefore overwhelmingly and morally important. This observation has its corollary in the fact that everywhere men have some authority over women."

And the power/authority distinction that lets the program *both* assert universal male authority *and* study women's agency:

> "This distinction between power and culturally legitimated authority, between the ability to gain compliance and the recognition that it is right, is crucial to our study of women. … while authority legitimates the use of power, it does not exhaust it."

Rosaldo's conditions for variation (still middle-range useful after universalism falls):

> "women's status will be lowest in those societies where there is a firm differentiation between domestic and public spheres of activity and where women are isolated from one another and placed under a single man's authority, in the home. … perhaps the most egalitarian societies are those in which public and domestic spheres are only weakly differentiated."

Ortner (same volume / 1972) supplies the symbolic mechanism: culture subordinates nature; woman is seen as *closer to* nature (body, domestic role via Rosaldo, psyche via Chodorow), hence devalued — not as identical with nature, but intermediate, mediating, and symbolically ambiguous (witch / goddess).

### Floor 2 — Women as strategists

The second half of the volume refuses the implication that universal asymmetry means women are theoretically uninteresting. [[lamphere-louise|Lamphere]] formalizes strategies by domestic power structure; Collier applies generative political models to Zinacantan women; Stack replaces pathology models of Black families with domestic **networks**; Tanner redefines [[matrifocality]] positively; Wolf's Chinese **uterine family** and Hoffer's **Madam Yoko** show informal and formal female power; Sanday codes public-domain status; Sacks retools Engels; Leis compares Ijaw associations; Denich maps Balkan patricentrism; Bamberger analyzes the [[myth-of-matriarchy]]; Paul and O'Laughlin close with body, work, taboo, and contradiction.

## The Empirical Program

What the 1974 volume *did* that predecessors could not: treat women's goals, strategies, and symbolism as first-class ethnographic objects while still seeking general theory.

**Hits (as the program saw them):**
- Made sexual asymmetry an explicit *explanandum* rather than background noise.
- Separated cultural valuation from on-the-ground power (Ortner's three levels; Rosaldo's power/authority).
- Produced usable middle-range tools: domestic/public variation conditions; Lamphere's strategy typology; Sanday's public-status indices; Tanner's positive matrifocality; Bamberger's myth-as-charter reading.
- Case density across regions (Southeast Asia, China, West Africa, Mesoamerica, Balkans, Amazonia, urban US, comparative Africa).

**Misses / anomalies that accumulated:**
- Ethnographic counter-examples and category critiques (nature/culture not pan-cultural; household *as* polity in many societies).
- Universalist claims outran the sampling frame (WEIRD and salvage ethnography biases).
- Materialist essays inside the same volume (Sacks, O'Laughlin) already pointed to production and property rather than a single symbolic logic — internal pluralism the "universal" brand papered over.

## Method Commitments

- **Fieldwork attentive to women** as informants and actors (not only male gatekeepers).
- **Symbolic/structural analysis** in the early theoretical essays (Ortner, Rosaldo).
- **Psychoanalytic/object-relations** inference from family structure (Chodorow) — high theoretical load, weak cross-cultural sampling as Ortner herself noted.
- **Cross-cultural coding** (Sanday) and **controlled comparison** (Leis on two Ijaw villages).
- **Myth analysis as charter** (Bamberger), not as historical chronicle.
- Later: historicized, anti-universalist comparison; intersection with race/class/colonialism; reflexive attention to the analyst's categories.

## Critiques and Responses

The founding universalism was challenged **from within** within a decade:

1. **Category critique** (MacCormack & Strathern 1980): "nature" and "culture" are historically specific Western concepts; many societies do not think with that opposition, so a universal explanation resting on it is incoherent.
2. **Domestic/public as projection** (Collier & Yanagisako; Strathern): the split is not a cross-cultural given; sometimes the household *is* the political unit.
3. **Self-critique** (Rosaldo 1980): the search for universal *origins* of asymmetry — including domestic/public — was itself a theoretical dead end; analyze historically situated gender systems instead.
4. **Ortner's revision** (1996): male dominance is *pervasive* but not a strict logical universal; categories are historical; thesis holds as strong tendency, not law.

The volume itself already contained the seeds: Hoffer's West African female chiefship challenges a hard domestic/juro-political split; Sacks denies that subordination was always/everywhere the rule; Stack refuses pathology models of non-nuclear families.

## What Survived

Even after the universalist package was dismantled, large pieces remain active vocabulary:

- **Androcentrism as a methodological problem** — still taught as a founding lesson of the discipline's history.
- **Power vs. authority** as a tool for reading women's agency under male-legitimated systems.
- **Domestic/public as a *variable***, not a universal law — Rosaldo's conditions for high/low status travel better than her universal claim.
- **Matrifocality** (positive, non-pathological) and **uterine family** as kinship tools.
- **Myth of matriarchy** as charter analysis rather than lost history.
- **Women as strategists** — the refusal to treat formal exclusion as theoretical uninterest.
- The [[nature-culture-gender-debate]] itself as a teaching case in how programs eat their own foundations.

What did *not* survive as research program: the search for a single pan-cultural symbolic or structural *cause* of female subordination.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Feminist anthropology is **mainstream and transformed**. The field moved from *explaining a universal* to *analyzing gender's cultural specificity*, intersection with race/class/colonialism/sexuality, queer and transgender ethnography, care and reproduction under capitalism, and decolonial critiques of the 1970s charter itself (WEIRD standpoint, salvage sampling, US second-wave framing). Connell's later [[gender-relations-theory|gender relations theory]] and Ridgeway's [[gender-as-primary-frame]] work in sociology are adjacent but distinct lineages. Active frontiers include: reproductive politics; gendered labor in global production; indigenous feminist ethnography; and re-reading the 1974 archive with the category critique already built in.

## Reflexivity

The program's production context — Stanford, second-wave US feminism, a mostly Euro-American contributor list, 1971–73 — shaped both its courage (naming androcentrism) and its blind spot (treating Western analytic categories as pan-cultural). That the founders themselves led the critique is part of the program's intellectual honesty and part of why the 1974 volume remains a *teaching* object rather than a living research platform in its original form.
