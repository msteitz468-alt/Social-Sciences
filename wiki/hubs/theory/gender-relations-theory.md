# Gender Relations Theory (Connell) — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. The `theories/` summary ([[gender-relations-theory]]) owns core-claims/origins/proponents/critiques/status at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: the summary links here by full path; this page's `theory_page` points back.

[[connell-raewyn|R. W. Connell]]'s relational theory of gender turned "masculinity" from a psychological trait or a [[social-role|role]] into an object of structural social analysis. Its signature concept, [[hegemonic-masculinity]], became one of the most-cited ideas in late-twentieth-century sociology. **Both panels of the diptych are now ingested:** *[[connell-gender-and-power-1987|Gender and Power]]* (1987) states the structural theory (labour/power/[[cathexis]]; gender order/regime; [[emphasized-femininity]]); *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995; 2nd ed. 2005) works it out for men (typology + life-history study). Connell & Messerschmidt (2005) remains secondary for the reformulation.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

The program is an answer to two exhausted predecessors and one political moment.

The first predecessor is **sex-role theory**. By the 1970s the standard sociological account of gender ran through the concept of the [[social-role|sex role]] — a fusion, Connell notes, of late-nineteenth-century "sex difference" research with the 1930s sociological concept of role, given its "sophisticated statement" in Parsons's instrumental/expressive division of family roles. Connell's critique is systematic and, in the book, presented as a set of numbered charges: role theory is "logically vague" (the same word covers occupation, life stage, and gender); it "exaggerates the degree to which people's social behaviour is prescribed"; the dramaturgical metaphor fits only where scripts are well-defined, audiences clear, and stakes low — "as a rule" not the case for gender; it produces "categoricalism," collapsing gender into two homogeneous poles and obscuring race, class, and sexuality; and, decisively, it has "a fundamental difficulty in grasping issues of power" and "cannot theorize change as a dialectic within gender relations," treating change as impinging from outside and so remaining "fundamentally reactive." The verdict is quotable:

> "'Sex role' is basically an inappropriate metaphor for gender interactions."

Connell credits Joseph Pleck's *The Myth of Masculinity* (1981) with the most effective *internal* demolition of the "Male Sex Role Identity" paradigm, and notes that the sharpest external break came from the new ethnographic sociology of the 1980s (Messner, Willis, Cockburn) that replaced passive internalization with active construction-in-practice.

The second predecessor is **biological determinism / sociobiology** — "the body is a natural machine which produces gender difference." Connell answers it in chapter 2 with [[body-reflexive-practice]] (analyzed below), rejecting equally the sociobiological body and the purely semiotic body of some constructionism.

The political moment is the confluence, in 1970s–80s Australia, of the New Left, second-wave feminism, and gay liberation. Connell's positive resources come from there and from social theory: **Gramscian hegemony**, the feminist structural theorists **Juliet Mitchell and Gayle Rubin** (Connell credits them with establishing that "gender is an internally complex structure, where a number of different logics are superimposed"), and a broadly [[practice-theory|practice-theoretic]] conception of action drawing on Sartre.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The architecture has four tiers: definition of gender, three structural models, inventory levels (order/regime), and the relational typology of masculinities/femininities.

**Tier 1 — gender as autonomous practice.** From *Gender and Power*: the theory of gender "cannot derive its logic from a source outside itself, whether natural difference, biological reproduction, the functional needs of society or the imperatives of social reproduction." Social practices "do not express natural patterns, nor do they ignore natural patterns; rather, they **negate** them in a practical transformation." From *Masculinities*:

> "Gender is social practice that constantly refers to bodies and what bodies do, it is not social practice reduced to the body."

> "Gender exists precisely to the extent that biology does not determine the social."

Structure is "the intractability of the social world" — limits on freedom and the ability to operate by proxy — modified via practice theory (against Lévi-Straussian structuralism, against pure role voluntarism).

**Tier 2 — three major structural models (*Gender and Power* Ch. 5).**

| Structure | Organizational principle (first approx.) | Content |
|---|---|---|
| **Labour** | separation / division | sexual division of labour; gender-structured production, consumption, distribution; "making of women's occupations"; socio-technical design |
| **Power** | unequal integration | authority, control, coercion; state/business hierarchies; institutional and interpersonal violence; domestic authority |
| **[[cathexis]]** | patterning of attachment | object-choice, desire, desirability; hetero/homosexual production; gender antagonisms; emotional relations of child-rearing |

Connell treats the three as **empirically major**, not necessary or exhaustive; interdependent with **no ultimate determinant**. Unity of the gender field is "historical composition," not functionalist system unity.

**Tier 3 — structural inventories: gender regime and [[gender-order]].**

- **Gender regime** — inventory of an institution (school, workplace, family, state, street).
- **Gender order** — inventory of a whole society; dynamically "the current state of play" in macro sexual politics.
- Complete inventory requires relations *between* regimes (complementary, abrasive, parallel).

**Tier 4 — relational masculinities and femininities (*Gender and Power* Ch. 8; *Masculinities*).**

At whole-society level, forms of femininity and masculinity are "stylized and impoverished"; interrelation centers on "the global dominance of men over women."

| Term | Definition (attributed to Connell) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| [[hegemonic-masculinity]] | configuration that legitimates patriarchy; always relative to subordinated masculinities *and* to women; public face sustains power | 1985/1987/1995 |
| Subordinated masculinity | expelled from legitimacy (paradigm: gay men; also temporary apprenticeship) | 1987/1995 |
| Complicit masculinity | realizes the [[patriarchal-dividend]] without front-line risk | named 1995; mechanism 1987 |
| Marginalized masculinity | class/ethnic subordination relative to dominant-group authorization | developed 1995 |
| [[emphasized-femininity]] | compliance with subordination; accommodating men's interests; mass themes of sexual receptivity / motherhood | **1987 only** (not in *Masculinities*) |
| Other femininities | resistance / non-compliance / strategic mixes | 1987 |

**Fundamental asymmetry:** "There is no femininity that is hegemonic in the sense that the dominant form of masculinity is hegemonic among men." All femininities are constructed in the context of women's overall subordination.

## Key Texts and Proponents

| Text | Year | Move it makes | Reception |
|---|---|---|---|
| Carrigan, Connell & Lee, "Toward a New Sociology of Masculinity" | 1985 | First statement of hegemonic masculinity; breaks with sex-role theory | Founding article of the field |
| [[connell-gender-and-power-1987]] (*Gender and Power*) | 1987 | Full structural theory (labour/power/cathexis); order/regime; emphasized femininity; crisis tendencies; sexual politics | Established Connell as gender theorist; diptych panel 1 (now ingested) |
| [[connell-masculinities-1995]] (*Masculinities*) | 1995 | Typology of masculinities + life-history study + world-historical account | Field-defining; translated widely; Australian "ten most influential" (2003) |

The program originated with Connell and the early collaborators Tim Carrigan and John Lee; its wider community includes Michael Messner, James Messerschmidt, and a large ethnographic literature on sport, schooling, work, and the military.

## The Empirical Program

The framework's own anchor is the [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|life-history study]] of four groups of Australian men, each chosen to probe a crisis tendency in a different structure of the [[gender-order]]. Its results are honestly plural rather than confirmatory: they are used to *display* the typology, not to test it against rivals.

| Claim | Supporting evidence (in the book) | Challenging evidence / limits | Current standing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Masculinities are multiple and hierarchically related | All four life-history groups show distinct configurations | Small purposive sample; single national setting | Widely accepted framing |
| "Protest masculinity" is a marginalized reworking of hegemonic themes under poverty | Ch. 4 unemployed working-class men | Based on 5 core cases | Influential; much cited in criminology |
| Masculine object-choice subverts hegemonic masculinity's heterosexual definition | Ch. 6 "very straight gay" men | Interpretive; 8 cases | Standard reading of subordinated masculinity |
| Hegemonic/complicit masculinity is internally split (dominance vs. expertise) | Ch. 7 professional men | Qualitative | Accepted; feeds later "hybrid masculinities" work |
| A patriarchal dividend makes most men complicit | Income/capital/office indicators | Category "men" contested | Concept widely adopted |

Connell states the external-validity limit himself: "These four studies are not intended in themselves as a map of large-scale change. Their purpose is to illuminate particular situations." The empirical *reach* of the theory, then, rests less on this study than on the thousands of later studies that adopted its vocabulary.

## Method Commitments

The program commits to: **life-history and ethnographic methods** as the way to catch masculinity as configured practice; **structural analysis** (the power/production/cathexis grid) as the interpretive scaffold; and a strong **anti-textualist realism** — life-stories are evidence about lives, not only about narrative: "if the language is all we can see, then we are missing the point of a life-history." Methodologically it also commits to reading any masculinity *relationally* (against other masculinities and against the gender order), which rules out both trait psychology and decontextualized attitude surveys as adequate on their own.

## Major Debates and Rivals

The program was built by beating sex-role theory and biological determinism (above). Its live rivals after 1995 are:
- **Discursive psychology** (Wetherell and Edley) — masculinity as situated repertoire, not stable structure;
- **Hybridization theory** (Demetriou) — hegemonic masculinity as a moving hybrid that absorbs subordinated elements;
- **Psychosocial criminology** (Jefferson) — the concept flattens individual psychic complexity.

These are set out as positions on [[hegemonic-masculinity-debate]]. Connell treats the discursive challenge as a genuine "theoretical impasse" and hopes a "situational approach … connected with the conscious historicity" of good ethnography can reconcile discursive and structural analysis.

## Critiques and Responses

The recurring critiques and Connell's answers:

1. **"It's a fixed type / trait checklist."** Connell: no — it names "a position always contestable," historically mobile; misreadings that reify it violate the definition.
2. **"It over-generalizes 'men' as a shared-interest category."** Connell partly concedes: in the Afterword he stresses that the men who reap the [[patriarchal-dividend|dividend]] and the men who pay its costs (violence, imprisonment) are largely different men, and that men's disadvantages are "broadly speaking, the conditions of the advantages," not offsetting them — yet he refuses to abandon "men" as an analytic category.
3. **"The evidence is thin."** True of the original study (four small Australian groups); the defense is that the study was strategic illustration and the framework's warrant accrued through its adoption across the field.
4. **"Discourse, not structure."** Connell answers that discursive approaches give "no grip on issues about economic inequality and the state."

His formal response to the accumulated critique is the reconstruction with Messerschmidt (2005): retain the concept, add geographic levels (local/regional/global), grant more agency to subordinated and non-hegemonic actors, and strengthen the embodied and psychological dimensions.

## What Survived

Even where specific formulations have been revised, the program's core has been thoroughly absorbed:

- **The vocabulary is now standard.** Hegemonic / subordinated / complicit / marginalized masculinity, [[emphasized-femininity]], the [[patriarchal-dividend]], [[gender-order|gender order / gender regime]], and the labour–power–cathexis grid are used across sociology, criminology, education, health, and IR — often by scholars who dispute the details.
- **"Masculinities" (plural) won.** Defeat of sex-role theory and unitary "male role": masculinity as plural, relational, hierarchical is near-consensus.
- **Institutional gender analysis.** "Gender regime" as state-of-play inventory travels far beyond Connell's school examples.
- **Hybrid masculinities** extend the typology (via Demetriou) to privileged men's selective incorporation of subordinated elements.
- **Transnational turn.** "Transnational business masculinity" and world gender order seed globalization literatures.
- **Body theory.** 1987 negation/practice account + 1995 [[body-reflexive-practice]] feed sport, health, and body sociology.
- **Political grammar.** Men cannot be "liberated" as a dominant group; anti-sexist men's politics is dismantling interest — still the sharpest formulation of that problem.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active`. Connell's framework remains the dominant paradigm in the sociology of masculinities three decades on. Active frontiers: **hybrid and "inclusive" masculinities** (does the softening of hegemonic masculinity in some settings represent real change or a modernized patriarchy — the very question Connell posed about the "Sensitive New Man"?); **masculinities and globalization** (transnational business masculinity, militarized masculinities post-9/11); **digital masculinities** (the online "manosphere" as organized backlash, the political heir of the men's-rights current Connell analyzed); and the ongoing methodological argument over how to hold structural and discursive analysis together. The program's own reflexive lesson — that the study of masculinity is politically charged (Connell's project was attacked as wasteful before it was even published) — remains as relevant as its concepts.
