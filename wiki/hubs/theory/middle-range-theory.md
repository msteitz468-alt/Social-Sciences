# Theories of the Middle Range — Deep Analysis

## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[merton-robert-k|Merton]] formulated middle-range theory as a policy for postwar American sociology amid (1) expanding survey research that risked theory-free fact collection and (2) ambitions for total systems of sociological theory associated especially with [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]. In STSS he expands a 1947 critique of Parsons's statement on the position of sociological theory (ASR 1949 note) and later pieces including the 1957 role-set essay.

The foil is double. Against **all-inclusive systematics**, he argues sociology lacks the cumulative theoretical consolidation that would make Newtonian-style total theory more than premature scaffolding. Against **raw empiricism**, he insists that isolated empirical generalizations are not theories: a theory comprises assumptions from which generalizations are derived. The word "theory," he notes, had become meaningless through diverse referents — working hypotheses, vague speculation, axiomatic systems.

Classical resources: delimited scientific exemplars (Gilbert on magnetism, Boyle on the "sea of air," Darwin on coral atolls) as models of simple seminal images generating testable inferences — not as positivist law-envy, but as proof-by-fruitfulness.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### What middle-range theory is

Merton's definition (STSS Ch. II, attributed):

> theories that lie between the minor but necessary working hypotheses that evolve in abundance during day-to-day research and the all-inclusive systematic efforts to develop a unified theory that will explain all the observed uniformities of social behavior, social organization and social change.

Further commitments he states:

- **Principal use:** guide empirical inquiry.
- **Intermediate** between general theories of social systems (too remote from observed classes of behavior) and detailed descriptions of particulars (not generalized at all).
- Involves **abstractions**, but close enough to data for **propositions that permit empirical testing**.
- Deals with **delimited aspects** of social phenomena (reference groups, social mobility, role-conflict, formation of social norms — as one speaks of a theory of prices or a kinetic theory of gases).
- **Not logically derived** from a single all-embracing theory of social systems, though once developed they *may* be consistent with one.
- **More than** an isolated empirical generalization: a set of assumptions from which generalizations are derived.

### Exemplar logic: relative deprivation

He walks the reference-group / [[relative-deprivation]] idea: people take standards of significant others as basis for self-appraisal. Common sense expects subjective deprivation to track objective loss linearly; the theory predicts comparison-dependent appraisal — under specifiable conditions, those with large losses may feel less deprived than those with smaller losses if surrounding others lost more. Disaster and *American Soldier* materials are used as confirmation patterns (attributed to Merton's synthesis of Stouffer et al.).

### Exemplar logic: role-set

[[role-set]] theory begins with a simple image of how status is organized: a single status (Lintonian tradition) is tied not to one role-partner but to a **complement of role relationships**. This differs from "multiple roles" across a [[status-set]]. The image generates problems of articulating conflicting expectations — a research program, not a verbal category.

### Polarized responses

Merton records **assent** (middle-range as realistic cumulative path) and **rejection** (from total-system first advocates and from those who read middle-range as fragmented empiricism). The 1968 text treats these as ongoing disciplinary politics, not settled verdicts.

## The Empirical Program

| Claim | Supporting materials (as Merton uses them) | Challenging lines | Current standing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Delimited theories guide cumulative inquiry | Reference group, anomie typology, role-set, influentials, scientific ethos in STSS | Can fragment into disconnected specialties | Still default self-description of much empirical sociology |
| Not deduced from total system first | Consistency-without-derivation argument | Parsonsian and later grand-theory renewals | Dual structure of field persists |
| Simple images → testable inferences | RD disaster/military examples; Boyle/Pascal analogy | Post-hoc fitting of "simple ideas" | Heuristic remains standard teaching |

Honest limit: STSS demonstrates the *policy* more than it meta-analyzes failure rates of middle-range programs. Anomie typology's later contestation shows middle-range theories can ossify into textbooks while mechanisms are revised.

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **Total system first** (Parsons and allies) vs middle-range first — see [[structural-functionalism]], [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]]
- **Mills's dual attack** on grand theory *and* abstracted empiricism occupies adjacent space; Merton's third path is not identical with Mills's "classic" craft, nor with Bureau methodism alone
- Later: whether "middle range" dissolved into ordinary specialty theories after Parsonian hegemony ended (absorption vs abandonment)

## Critiques and Responses

**Critique (total theory):** without a general scheme, findings do not cumulate into science. **Merton's response:** premature total schemes produce the appearance of system without testable grip; delimited theories can later be consolidated.

**Critique (fragmentation):** middle-range licenses disconnected empiricism. **Response:** theory requires interrelated propositions and assumptions, not isolated correlations; exemplars show structured research programs.

**Critique (functionalist capture):** middle-range as soft functionalism. **Response:** the policy is methodologically wider than functional analysis (reference groups, science ethos); functional paradigm is one toolkit (Ch. III).

## What Survived

- Training norm: "build testable theory close to a delimited problem"
- Vocabulary: middle-range as contrast class to grand theory in methods courses
- Exemplar theories still taught: [[reference-group]], [[role-set]], strain/anomie typology, [[self-fulfilling-prophecy]], [[scientific-ethos]]
- Institutional settlement of theory + research collaboration (Columbia model generalized)

What faded: the 1950s–60s sense that middle-range was a *party* opposing a *party* of grand theory — after Parsons's decline, the label often names ordinary good practice rather than a movement.
