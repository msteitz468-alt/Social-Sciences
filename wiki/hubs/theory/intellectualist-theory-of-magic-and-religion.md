## Intellectual Origins and Context

The intellectualist theory of magic and religion is the founding-era British answer to a pair of questions that Victorian ethnology and comparative classics could not leave alone: *What is religion, at its minimum?* and *How did human beings first try to explain and control nature?* [[tylor-edward-burnett|E. B. Tylor]] (1832–1917) and [[frazer-james-george|J. G. Frazer]] (1854–1941) gave the most influential answers: magic and religion begin as **theories** — intellectual constructions by rational agents reasoning from limited data — not as social integration (Durkheim), anxiety management (Malinowski), or pure emotional effusion. That thesis is the program's load-bearing joint; everything else (animism, sympathetic magic, the evolutionary sequence, the doctrine of survivals) is scaffolding for it.

**What it reacted against.** Tylor and Frazer wrote against several rivals at once. Against **theological exclusivism**, which treated non-Christian belief as mere idolatry or devil-worship and so blocked systematic comparison. Against **purely allegorical** or euhemerist readings of myth that dissolved ritual into poetry without a developmental story. Against the **ethnocentric dismissal** of "superstition" that refused to treat magic as a coherent (if false) system of thought. And — more quietly — against **polygenist race-science** that would have made non-European minds a lower kind rather than the same kind of mind working with poorer data. Tylor's [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity of mankind]] is the anti-racist half of a package whose other half was a cultural hierarchy of stages; both halves shaped the intellectualist program.

**What it stood on.** The program developed_from three Victorian resources:

1. **[[social-evolutionism|Unilinear social evolutionism]]** — the assumption that culture everywhere ascends a broadly uniform ladder (savagery → barbarism → civilization; animism → polytheism → monotheism; magic → religion → science). Spencer, Lubbock, McLennan, and [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] were building parallel ladders in the same decades; Tylor and Frazer applied the ladder to belief and ritual with unmatched literary force.
2. **British empiricist associationist psychology** — the claim that the mind connects ideas by resemblance and contiguity. Tylor already treated [[magic]] as mistaken association of ideas; Frazer made that analysis the formal architecture of [[sympathetic-magic]] (Law of Similarity; Law of Contact).
3. **Comparative philology, classics, and folklore** — the method of collating variants across peoples and ages. Frazer was a Trinity classicist; the Nemi priesthood (*Rex Nemorensis*) is a classical puzzle solved by ethnological comparison. Wilhelm Mannhardt's vegetation-spirit research supplied the German folkloric engine that Frazer amplified into the [[dying-and-rising-god]] and [[vegetation-spirit]] complexes.

**Disciplinary moment.** The program belongs to the **Founding Era** of anthropology (roughly 1830–1890 for Tylor; Frazer straddles founding, classical, and even the early fieldwork-revolution era without ever doing fieldwork). It is **armchair anthropology** at its apex: the evidence base is travellers', missionaries', colonial administrators', classicists', and folklorists' reports, collated in libraries (Oxford for Tylor; Cambridge for Frazer). No participant observation; no systematic linguistic competence in the peoples described; no sampling frame in the modern sense. The production context is colonial knowledge circuits — the same circuits that supplied the data also supplied the ranking language ("savage," "barbarous," "lowest races") that the program used as analytic categories. That is not an incidental ethical stain; it is part of how the theory's categories were made (see Reflexivity under Critiques).

**Division of labor between Tylor and Frazer.** Tylor founded the intellectualist *religion* thesis: religion as belief in spiritual beings ([[animism]]), inferred by the "primitive philosopher" from dreams and death, elaborated upward into gods, and evidenced by [[survivals]] in modern custom. Frazer founded the intellectualist *magic* thesis and the grand sequence: magic as false science of impersonal forces; religion as propitiation of personal powers when magic fails; science as impersonal order restored on observational ground. Frazer regarded himself as Tylor's disciple; the wiki treats them as co-architects of one program with two main texts.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

All claims below are **attributed** to Tylor, Frazer, or the intellectualist program as a whole. None is wiki-voice fact about what magic or religion "is."

### 1. Intellectual origin (the master claim)

For Tylor and Frazer, magic and religion begin as **attempts to explain and act on the world** — theories of nature and conduct — not primarily as social glue or emotional expression. The agent of origin is a rational individual (Tylor's "primitive philosopher"; Frazer's shrewder mind who eventually notices that magic fails). Society, ritual community, and collective effervescence are secondary in the origin story. This is why Evans-Pritchard later called the tradition **intellectualist**: it reconstructs "If I were a horse" — if *I* (a Victorian rationalist) were a primitive, *I* would invent souls from dreams and death, or invent rain-magic from association of ideas.

### 2. Tylor's animism as minimum religion

Tylor proposed [[animism]] as the "**minimum definition of Religion**": "the belief in Spiritual Beings." He argued that the "primitive philosopher," reasoning from two puzzles — the difference between a living body and a dead one, and the human shapes seen in dreams and visions — inferred that every person has a life and a phantom, combined them into the **ghost-soul**, and then extended soul-belief to animals, plants, objects, nature-spirits, ancestral ghosts, polytheistic gods, and finally monotheism — a single ascending chain. Tylor held that cross-cultural uniformity of soul-belief reflects independent rational inference under [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], not diffusion alone:

> "It is a thin unsubstantial human image, in its nature a sort of vapour, film, or shadow; the cause of life and thought in the individual it animates… capable of leaving the body far behind… continuing to exist and appear to men after the death of that body." (Tylor 1871, Vol. I ch. XI, as cited on the wiki [[animism]] page)

Animism thus does double duty: it is a **definition** of religion (inclusive enough to defeat the claim that "savages" had none) and a **developmental theory** of religion's origin and elaboration.

### 3. Frazer's sympathetic magic as false science

Frazer analysed the principles of thought underlying magic as two:

1. **Law of Similarity** — like produces like; effect resembles cause → **homoeopathic** (imitative) magic.
2. **Law of Contact or Contagion** — things once in contact continue to act at a distance → **contagious** magic.

Both assume action at a distance through "secret sympathy"; together they constitute **[[sympathetic-magic]]**. Frazer held that both are misapplications of the association of ideas, and that magic is therefore a spurious natural law and a fallacious guide of conduct. The canonical formula:

> "magic is a spurious system of natural law as well as a fallacious guide of conduct; it is a false science as well as an abortive art." (*Golden Bough*, ch. 3 §1)

He split theoretical magic (statement of the spurious laws) from practical magic (precepts for ends), and defined [[taboo]] as the **negative side** of practical magic — "Do not do this lest X happen" as the mirror of "Do this that X may happen." Pure magic, he insisted, assumes impersonal, law-like nature without spiritual agency; its fundamental conception is "identical with that of modern science," but its particular laws are wrong. Association applied legitimately yields science; illegitimately, "magic, the bastard sister of science."

### 4. Magic older than religion; the Age of Magic

Frazer argued that magic precedes religion in the evolution of thought, both deductively (association is elementary; personal deities are complex) and inductively (he claimed Australian Aboriginal societies show universal magic and almost no propitiatory religion — a claim later anthropology rejected as description, not only as theory). He conjectured a universal **Age of Magic** analogous to the Stone Age on the material side:

> "just as on the material side of human culture there has everywhere been an Age of Stone, so on the intellectual side there has everywhere been an Age of Magic." (*Golden Bough*, ch. 4)

The causal mechanism for the transition, as Frazer conjectured it: shrewder minds eventually recognise magic's "inherent falsehood and barrenness," confess inability to control nature by spell, and reassign control to stronger unseen personal beings — hence prayer, sacrifice, and dependence. Magic survives under religious conformity as the "Catholic creed" of the masses: a permanent subterranean stratum of belief in sympathetic efficacy.

### 5. Magic → religion → science

Frazer's grand sequence — restated in the closing "Farewell to Nemi" — is the program's most famous architecture. Magic trusts human strength and an imagined manipulable natural order; when that fails, religion throws humanity on invisible spiritual beings; when immutable natural law is recognised by observation, science displaces religion as explanation of nature while reclaiming magic's implicit faith in regularity — now grounded in observation, not false analogy of ideas:

> "the movement of the higher thought, so far as we can trace it, has on the whole been from magic through religion to science." (*Golden Bough*, ch. 69)

Religion, for Frazer, is "propitiation or conciliation of powers superior to man which are believed to direct and control the course of nature and of human life." The decisive contrast: religion assumes conscious personal forces (nature is somewhat elastic under persuasion); magic and science assume unconscious impersonal forces (nature is rigid). When magic deals with spirits, he held, it still *coerces* them; religion *propitiates* them.

### 6. Science itself provisional; the three-threaded web

Frazer did not present science as final dogma. In the same farewell chapter he wrote that science, though the best theory yet, may itself be superseded:

> "In the last analysis magic, religion, and science are nothing but theories of thought; and as science has supplanted its predecessors, so it may hereafter be itself superseded by some more perfect hypothesis, perhaps by some totally different way of looking at the phenomena - of registering the shadows on the screen - of which we in this generation can form no idea." (*Golden Bough*, ch. 69)

And he illustrated the whole history of thought as a **three-threaded web**:

> "we may illustrate the course which thought has hitherto run by likening it to a web woven of three different threads - the black thread of magic, the red thread of religion, and the white thread of science" (*Golden Bough*, ch. 69)

Early thought, he wrote, is a black-and-white chequer (magic mixed with simple observational truths); the middle stretch is deeply crimson with religion; modern thought is a mixed fabric whose future colour — white or red — is unknown. The image does two things at once: it softens the sequence from pure stages into concurrent threads (an internal hedge against crude unilinearism), and it still ranks the threads so that "progress" means more white, less red and black.

### 7. Survivals as the evidential engine

Tylor's doctrine of [[survivals]] — customs carried by force of habit into a new state of society, remaining as proofs of an older condition — is the program's chief **evidential device**. Present "savage" customs and European folklore reconstruct earlier stages of the universal mind. Frazer's entire comparative catalogue of fire-festivals, corn-spirits, [[scapegoat]] rites, and [[divine-king|divine kingship]] runs on survival-reasoning: the pretence of burning a man is a mitigated survival of actually burning him; the Yule log is the indoor counterpart of the midsummer bonfire; mistletoe rules preserve the seat of the oak's life. Without survivals, the evolutionary ladder has no fossils; with them, every anomaly can be read as a milestone — which is also the doctrine's circularity (see Critiques).

### 8. Divine kingship and the dying god (Frazer's applied architecture)

The *Golden Bough* frames itself as an answer to two questions about Nemi: why must Diana's priest slay his predecessor, and why must he first pluck a branch identified with the Golden Bough? Frazer's answer deploys the whole intellectualist machine: the [[divine-king]] embodies the life of the world/vegetation and must be slain when his strength fails so that the divine life may pass undiminished; the Golden Bough is linked to the [[external-soul]] / mistletoe-as-life-seat complex; Adonis, Attis, Osiris, and Balder instantiate the [[dying-and-rising-god]] as vegetation drama. These applied constructs are not separate theories; they are the empirical program *of* the intellectualist sequence — magic and vegetation-logic before (and under) propitiatory religion.

### How the architecture hangs together

Pull any joint and the machine fails. Remove **psychic unity** and cross-cultural similarity no longer licenses parallel stages (Boas's move). Remove the **developmental scale** and survivals become mere persistences with present functions (Malinowski's move). Remove **associationist psychology** and sympathetic magic ceases to be "false science" and becomes social practice, speech-act, or expressive performance. Remove **individual intellectual origin** and Durkheim's social sacred takes the field. The program is tightly coupled — which is why twentieth-century anthropology could dismantle it by attacking single joints rather than refuting every ethnographic citation.

## The Empirical Program

What did the program claim to *explain* that predecessors could not? Three things, on its own terms:

1. **The unity of "superstition."** Image-magic in Scotland and Central Australia, footprint injury, hair-and-nail charms, war-wives' taboos, and weather rites could be brought under two associationist laws rather than left as a heap of curiosities. That classificatory achievement is real even if the "false science" verdict is rejected.
2. **A developmental story of mind.** The Age of Magic / Age of Religion / Age of Science sequence offered a single narrative linking "primitive" rite, ancient polytheism, peasant folklore, and modern science — the narrative Victorian and Edwardian readers wanted.
3. **Classical riddles by ethnological light.** Nemi, Balder, Adonis, the priest-king, the scapegoat — classical and biblical puzzles were re-read as instances of worldwide vegetation and kingship logic.

What it **failed** at, as later work showed: accurate description of living systems of belief (Azande, Trobriand, Nuer); non-circular reconstruction of origins; control for diffusion and shared descent; separation of analytical constructs (e.g. "dying god") from the case materials they claimed to unify; and any account of religion's social production.

**Honest anomalies that accumulated inside the program itself.** Frazer repeatedly revised major interpretations across editions (e.g. solar vs. purificatory theory of fire-festivals; Celtic wicker victims as vegetation representatives vs. witches). He conceded that theories of "primitive man" aspire only to "a reasonable degree of probability." Those hedges did not save the sequence, but they show the program was not simply dogmatic; it was a **catalogue-plus-conjecture** machine that updated conjectures while preserving the intellectualist frame.

## Method Commitments

The program requires a distinctive — and now largely rejected — epistemic package:

1. **[[comparative-method]] on second-hand sources.** Traits are extracted from published reports and arrayed by similarity. Context is stripped so that a custom in Melanesia can illuminate one in ancient Italy. The warrant is psychic unity plus parallel development; diffusion is secondary when the point is stage reconstruction.
2. **No participant observation.** Neither Tylor nor Frazer did ethnographic fieldwork. Authority comes from breadth of reading and classical training, not from residence, language, or long-term observation. This is a method *commitment*, not a mere biographical accident: the program treats the library world-sample as the proper unit.
3. **Survivals as historical fossils.** Present anomalies are evidence of past stages. The method presupposes the evolutionary scale it claims to discover — the circularity functionalists and historians of science later stressed.
4. **Associationist psychology as reverse-engineering tool.** From the form of a rite (imitation; contact), the analyst infers the mental law the "savage" must have been applying. Intention and cosmology are read off structure.
5. **Conjectural history as legitimate science.** Origin stories (first soul-inference; first failure of magic; first priest-king) are offered as probable reconstructions, not as archive-based chronologies. Frazer was sometimes candid about probability ceilings; the genre still filled the books.
6. **Ranking language as analytic vocabulary.** "Lower races," "savagery," "higher thought" are not only period style; they are how cases are ordered on the ladder. Method and colonial hierarchy are fused.
7. **Literary totalisation.** Especially in Frazer, the comparative catalogue is also a *book* — rhetorical cadence, classical frame-tale (Nemi), and farewell meditation are part of how conviction is produced. Classic studies persuade as texts, not only as data (a point the Studies Hub standard insists on).

**What the method can and cannot do.** It can generate typologies (homoeopathic vs. contagious; taboo as negative magic) and can assemble vast dossiers that later specialists mine. It cannot establish causal sequences of origin, cannot validate emic meanings, cannot control Galton's problem, and cannot distinguish independent invention from diffusion without historical work it usually did not do. For Galton's problem specifically: every comparison page in this wiki must state how non-independence is handled; the intellectualist program typically **did not control it** — treat its comparative tables as suggestive collocations, not as independent trials of a stage law.

## Major Debates and Rivals

The program was defined as much by what rose against it as by what it claimed. Blow by blow:

### 1. Durkheimian sociology of religion

Émile Durkheim's *Elementary Forms of Religious Life* (1912) is the structural rival on religion's **origin and nature**. Durkheim rejected animism and naturism as elementary forms; he located religion in the sacred/profane distinction and in collective ritual effervescence, not in the private inferences of a primitive philosopher. The soul, on his account, is a secondary individualization of the clan's totemic principle, not the foundation of religion. Where Tylor said religion is belief in spiritual beings, Durkheim said that definition both misses impersonal sacred forces and misreads social life as bad psychology. The intellectualist program and the Durkheimian program are incompatible origin stories; twentieth-century sociology of religion largely took the social fork.

### 2. Boasian historical particularism and the evolutionism debate

[[boas-franz|Franz Boas]]'s attack on the comparative method (1896 and after) and on racial-developmental hierarchy cut the warrant for parallel stages. Similar traits need not mean similar stages; diffusion and local history can produce likeness; meanings differ even when forms match. The [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]] is the formal locus: intellectualist magic-and-religion theory is a **special case** of the evolutionism Boasians dismantled. Without the ladder, Frazer's Age of Magic and Tylor's animism→polytheism→monotheism chain lose their chronological teeth.

### 3. Malinowskian and Radcliffe-Brownian functionalism

Fieldwork functionalism rejected **conjectural history** and **survivals**. Malinowski re-theorized Trobriand magic as practical and psychological — bridging gaps of uncertainty (open-sea fishing vs. lagoon) — not as failed physics. A custom that looks "irrational" has present functions; labelling it a survival of a vanished stage substitutes origin fantasy for analysis. Radcliffe-Brown redirected "function" toward social structure. The intellectualist "why was it invented?" gave way to "what does it do now?"

### 4. Evans-Pritchard and the rationality debates

E. E. Evans-Pritchard's Azande work and his *Theories of Primitive Religion* (1965) supplied the sharpest **internal** critique of intellectualism. The "If I were a horse" objection: Victorian theorists reconstructed primitive thought by asking what *they* would invent in primitive conditions, thereby projecting their own rationalist psychology. Azande witchcraft, oracles, and magic form a **coherent system** with secondary elaborations that protect the theory from falsification — not a heap of association-errors waiting for science. This is not a defense of witchcraft's truth-claims; it is a demolition of Frazer's error-theory as ethnography.

### 5. Myth-ritual and the dying-god critique

Within classics and history of religions, the [[dying-and-rising-god]] construct came under sustained attack (notably in late-twentieth-century work associated with Jonathan Z. Smith and others): the category was held to blend distinct figures, to over-assimilate Mediterranean and Near Eastern deities to a vegetation template, and sometimes to read Christian dying-rising patterns backward onto earlier materials. The construct's **literary** success (modernist poetry, comparative mythography) outran its **philological** warrant — a split the intellectualist program never fully faced.

### 6. Intramural revisions

Not all pressure was external. Marett's "pre-animism," Lang's high-god arguments, and Frazer's own edition-to-edition reversals (fire-festival theory; witch vs. vegetation readings of Celtic burnings) show a program under empirical and conceptual stress while still committed to intellectualism and evolutionism. The three-threaded web is itself a soft revision of pure successive stages toward concurrent strands with shifting dominance.

## Critiques and Responses

Each major critique as a position; the program's available answers; the balance as of the abandoned status.

**Critique A — Ethnocentric intellectualism ("If I were a horse").**  
*Position (Evans-Pritchard and successors):* Origin stories that impute Victorian inferential habits to non-Western peoples explain the theorist, not the believer.  
*Program response:* Tylor and Frazer claimed psychic unity — the primitive *is* a philosopher, only with fewer facts; the reconstruction is not ethnocentric but humanist.  
*Balance:* The humanist intent is real; the projection problem is decisive for ethnography. Origin-of-religion intellectualism is not revived in mainstream anthropology.

**Critique B — Religion is social, not individual philosophy.**  
*Position (Durkheimian):* Belief in spirits is not elementary; the sacred and the ritual group are.  
*Program response:* Frazer defined religion as propitiation of superior powers and treated social forms as downstream of ideas; Tylor's minimum definition deliberately stayed with belief.  
*Balance:* Sociology of religion largely left intellectualist origins behind; belief-centered definitions persist in some cognitive and philosophical work without Frazer's sequence.

**Critique C — Present function over conjectural origin.**  
*Position (Malinowski et al.):* Survivals are a bad method; living rites do current work.  
*Program response:* Frazer and Tylor never denied present uses, but treated folklore and "superstition" as archives of the past; the reform edge of Tylor's science of culture *wanted* to clear survivals away.  
*Balance:* As historical method, survivals are abandoned; as a reminder that practices outlive meanings, a weak non-evolutionist residue remains in folklore studies.

**Critique D — Comparative method without history (Boas; Galton).**  
*Position:* Similar forms ≠ parallel stages; cases are not independent.  
*Program response:* Psychic unity plus careful collation; Frazer sometimes noted borrowing but built the books on parallelism.  
*Balance:* Uncontrolled worldwide trait-comparison for stage laws is not a live method. Controlled comparison and phylogenetic methods are different enterprises.

**Critique E — Source quality and colonial production.**  
*Position (Stocking and postcolonial critique):* Missionary and traveller reports are biased; ranking language is colonial ideology; armchair totalisation hides uncertainty.  
*Program response:* The authors saw themselves as scientific reformers fighting prejudice *and* superstition; Frazer stressed probability limits in places.  
*Balance:* Intent does not redeem evidence structure. The program is a central case in the history of how empire's archive made theory.

**Critique F — Dying god and divine-king overgeneralisation.**  
*Position (classics / history of religions):* The types do not survive case scrutiny as universal laws.  
*Program response:* Types are ideal-typical comparative constructs; Nemi is explained if the type holds.  
*Balance:* Specific historical problems (including Nemi) are handled with narrower tools; the universal vegetation-king law is not current science.

**Critique G — Magic is not failed science.**  
*Position (Wittgensteinian, symbolic, performative, and many ethnographic accounts):* Magical action is not a botched experiment; it may be expressive, conventional, rhetorical, or embedded in forms of life that do not aim at Frazerian natural law.  
*Program response:* Frazer's "false science / abortive art" and "bastard sister of science" formulae double down on the error-theory.  
*Balance:* Error-theory is dead as a general account; the kinship Frazer saw between magic and science (both assume order) is occasionally reused in philosophy of science *without* his ranking.

The program's best **internal** responses were honesty about uncertainty (Frazer's probability ceiling; the provisionality of science in ch. 69) and classificatory clarity (sympathetic magic's two laws). Neither response answers the social, ethnographic, or historical-method critiques. By mid-twentieth century the program was **abandoned** as a living research agenda, surviving as historiography, literary influence, and stripped vocabulary.

## What Survived

The program is dead; several of its **concepts, questions, and dossiers** are not.

1. **[[sympathetic-magic]] as descriptive typology.** Homoeopathic vs. contagious remains a useful folk-analytic in folklore, history of magic, and some ethnographic description — usually **without** "false science" and **without** evolutionary ranking below religion. The label outlived the verdict.

2. **[[animism]] as a permanent foil — and a revived word.** Tylor's minimum definition still organises textbook chapters ("what is religion?"). The "new animism" (Bird-David, Descola, Viveiros de Castro's perspectivism and related work) **inverts** Tylor: relational ontologies rather than mistaken proto-science. The word survived by changing sides.

3. **The question of magic vs. religion vs. science.** Even when Frazer's sequence is rejected, the **boundaries** among instrumental technique, propitiation, and empirical science remain live problems in anthropology of knowledge, STS, and philosophy. His claim that magic and science share a faith in order while differing in how order is known is still quotable when carefully detached from the ladder.

4. **[[taboo]], [[scapegoat]], [[external-soul]], sacred kingship dossiers.** Comparative religion and political anthropology still meet materials Frazer assembled; they no longer accept his general laws as the explanation. The dossier function of *The Golden Bough* — a searchable museum of rites — outlasted the museum's labels.

5. **Literary and cultural modernism.** Eliot, Yeats, and a wide modernist and popular culture absorbed dying-god and wasteland imagery from Frazerian channels. That is reception history, not scientific survival — but it is why the program remains culturally legible long after anthropologists stopped working inside it.

6. **Tylor's broader bequests that rode with intellectualism.** The definition of [[culture]] as a complex whole acquired as a member of society; the professionalisation of anthropology as a science; the anti-polygenist edge of psychic unity — these lived on after animism-as-origin died. They are adjacent survivors, not proofs that intellectualism was right.

7. **Historiographic centrality.** One cannot teach the history of anthropology of religion without Tylor and Frazer as the position Durkheim, Malinowski, Boas, and Evans-Pritchard defined themselves **against**. Abandoned programs that organised a generation remain load-bearing as *antagonists*.

8. **Cognitive and neo-intellectualist afterlives (partial, non-identical).** Late-twentieth and twenty-first-century cognitive anthropology and some evolutionary approaches again treat religion as involving theoretical cognition, intuitive ontologies, or counter-intuitive agents. These programs sometimes look "intellectualist" from afar but **reject** unilinear magic→religion→science, colonial ranking, and armchair worldwide stage laws. Continuity of *question* (how do minds produce supernatural belief?) is not continuity of *program*. Do not smuggle Frazer back in under cognitive branding.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

**Status: abandoned** as a named research program. No serious contemporary school works "within" Tylor–Frazer intellectualist evolutionism of magic and religion. The status field on the summary page is correct and should stay so until a genuine revival of *the sequence and the method* appears — not merely of isolated terms.

**Where the residue is worked:**

- **History of anthropology** (Stocking, Ackerman, and successors): the program as Victorian science, colonial archive, and literary form.
- **History of religions and classics:** critical dismantling and occasional rehabilitation of specific comparisons; Nemi and dying-god as episodes in method history.
- **Folklore and history of magic:** sympathetic-magic typology; European fire-festival and witchcraft materials Frazer collated, re-analysed with archival methods.
- **Anthropology of religion teaching:** intellectualism as Position One in the theory survey, before Durkheimian, phenomenological, symbolic, Marxist, and cognitive alternatives.
- **Literary studies:** Frazerian modernism and myth criticism.

**Open questions that are *not* Frazerian but neighbor his wreckage:**

- How should comparison of ritual forms proceed after Galton and after the end of unilinear stages?
- What is the right relation among belief, practice, and social form in defining "religion"?
- Can cognitive accounts of supernatural belief avoid the "If I were a horse" trap?
- How should historians use colonial-era ethnographic reports without reinstalling colonial rankings?
- What of Frazer's **provisionality of science** passage — the three theories of thought, the shadows on the screen — when read against later philosophy of science, without his progress myth?

**Reflexivity note (production context).** The intellectualist theory was produced by library philosophers inside imperial Britain, using data extracted under colonial and missionary conditions, and written in a ranking idiom that placed European science at the summit of a universal mental history. Its intellectualism mirrored its authors' class and institutional position: thought explains ritual because thinkers explain the world. Holding that context as *content* — not as a dismissive epithet — is required by this wiki's Reflexivity protocol. The program's abandonment is not only a scientific verdict; it is also a change in who produces anthropological knowledge and from where.
