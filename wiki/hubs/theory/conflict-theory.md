## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Ralf Dahrendorf]]'s *[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]* (German 1957; English author-revised 1959) is a mid-century attempt to **salvage the problem of class** after what he took to be the empirical refutation of Marx's sociological hypotheses and the simultaneous capture of academic sociology by equilibrium and integration imagery. The German preface frames the book against a public that demands ready-made solutions and against sociologists who forge "false currency"; the English preface targets the abandonment of change, conflict, and coercion in total societies for small-system equilibrium analysis. Impulses came from the LSE Thursday Evening Seminar (1952–54) with David Lockwood, and from the Center for Advanced Study Conflict Seminar (1957–58).

Two predecessors dominate. **[[marx-karl|Marx]]** is the rare classical figure who linked "class" to structural self-change rather than to stratification description alone — yet Dahrendorf holds Marx's theory "refuted, but… not… superseded," and sets out to separate the *problem* of class theory from Marx's theory. **[[weber-max|Weber]]** supplies the working categories: power vs authority (*Herrschaft*), and the *Herrschaftsverband* as [[imperatively-coordinated-association]]. Against both the Marxian property definition and the Parsonian integration image, Dahrendorf constructs a coercion-theoretic middle-range program confined (explicitly) to industrial society.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Two faces of society

Dahrendorf's meta-theoretical opening (Ch. V) is the claim that social order has been imagined, from antiquity onward, under two primacies: consensus of values (sociological **"Utopian"**) and constraint/domination (sociological **"Rationalist"**). In contemporary sociology these become **integration theory** and **coercion theory**:

> "For sociological analysis, society is Janus-headed, and its two faces are equivalent aspects of the same reality."

He reduces each to four (deliberately overstated) tenets. Integration: relative stability; well-integrated elements; functional contribution to system maintenance; value consensus. Coercion: ubiquitous change; ubiquitous conflict; elements contribute to disintegration and change; coercion of some by others. Neither model is exclusively valid; exclusive claims (including Parsons's claim that integration theory is the nucleus of general sociological theory) are "utterly unjustified." For explaining conflict-group formation he deliberately employs the "ugly face." See [[two-faces-of-society]], [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]].

The philosophical pedigree is not ornament. Dahrendorf maps Utopian/Rationalist onto Aristotle/Plato, Hobbes/Rousseau, Kant/Hegel, then filters philosophy into science as **heuristic principle** rather than fact-judgment: the sociological Utopian does not claim order *is* value consensus but that it *can be conceived* that way with testable propositions; the Rationalist likewise treats coercion as research assumption. David Lockwood's critique of Parsons — that normative weighting neglects the "substratum of social action" productive of conflict — is cited as touching the core without fully clarifying it. Dahrendorf's clarification: normative and factual levels of structure are not contradictory; the real alternative is stability/integration/values versus instability/conflict/interests. Illustration: introducing personnel managers can be read under integration theory (functional adaptation to "social ethic"); the 17 June 1953 East Berlin revolt documents instability and dissensus that integration theory reduces to "strains," while the coercion model reads conflict as agent of change by disintegration. The wave/quantum analogy for light is his preferred image of coexisting partial theories, each with its realm.

### 2. Authority in imperatively coordinated associations

Under the coercion model, organizations cohere by **enforced constraint**, which implies a differential distribution of power and authority. Following Weber:

> power is the "probability that one actor within a social relationship will be in a position to carry out his own will despite resistance, regardless of the basis on which this probability rests"; whereas authority (*Herrschaft*) is the "probability that a command with a given specific content will be obeyed by a given group of persons."

Authority is role-tied and legitimate; power is personal and factual. The unit of conflict analysis is the **[[imperatively-coordinated-association]]** (*Herrschaftsverband*): state, church, enterprise, party, union, even chess club — any association coordinated by domination and subjection. Within each association, authority is analytically **dichotomous** (plus-side hierarchies of command; minus-side of those who only obey). This is zero-sum for the disruptive face of authority (Mills), without denying authority's integrative face (Parsons). Central thesis:

> "this differential distribution of authority invariably becomes the determining factor of systematic social conflicts of a type that is germane to class conflicts in the traditional (Marxian) sense of this term."

Property is rejected as the sole determinant; authority is "both a more general and a more significant social relation."

Authority relations are specified (five features, Ch. V): super- and subordination; superordinate control via commands/warnings/prohibitions; expectations attached to relatively permanent positions (hence legitimate); specified spheres of control (never generalized control — that would be power); sanctions for noncompliance via legal and quasi-legal norms. **Domination** / **subjection** synonymize participation in vs exclusion from authority. Organization charts show hierarchies only of the *plus-side*; every association also has a *minus-side* of those who merely obey. Empirically the border is hard under division of labor; analytically the dichotomy remains. Two qualifications matter for political sociology: (1) domination in one association does not entail domination in all — unit of analysis is always a **specific association**; (2) total societies usually present a **plurality of competing** dominant and subjected aggregates, so the two-class model applies to associations (including the state as inclusive political association), not to "society" as one big dichotomous cake. "Fifty associations → expect a hundred classes" is the polemical formulation against Marxian polarization of total society.

### 3. Latent interests, quasi-groups, interest groups, class

Differentially equipped authority positions involve **conflicting interests** — formally, to maintain vs to modify the authority status quo — impressed by roles rather than by individual psychology. **[[latent-and-manifest-interests|Latent interests]]** are nonpsychological role expectations; **manifest interests** are articulated programs. **[[quasi-group|Quasi-groups]]** (Ginsberg, specialized by Dahrendorf) are aggregates sharing latent interests — "recruiting fields for groups," theoretical constructions. **Interest groups** organize those interests. **Social class**, in the summary definition:

> "By social class shall be understood such organized or unorganized collectivities of individuals as share manifest or latent interests arising from and related to the authority structure of imperatively coordinated associations. … social classes are always conflict groups."

"Stratum" is reserved for descriptive hierarchical similarity; class is analytical and conflictual.

The formal substance of objective interests is deliberately thin: not "realization of a socialist society" (Geiger's critique of Marx's "true interests" of labor is accepted) but **maintenance vs modification of an authority status quo**. Latent interests are nonpsychological; quasi-groups based on them are "theoretical phenomena" — units constructed for explaining conflict. Empirical conditions of organization (Ch. V) include technical (personnel, charter), political (freedom of coalition), and social (communication, patterned recruitment), plus nonstructural psychological internalization of role interests. The same quasi-group may recruit multiple competing interest groups (rival unions or parties). Whether to keep the word "class" is for Dahrendorf "purely terminological" — he rejects the Hegelian fiction that class is a "historical concept" inseparable from the nineteenth-century proletariat, while retaining the word for pragmatic continuity with the conflict tradition.

### 4. Intensity, violence, pluralism, regulation, change

Once conflict groups form, they engage in conflicts that effect structure changes. What varies is **[[intensity-and-violence-of-conflict|intensity]]** (energy, involvement, cost of victory/defeat) and **violence** (means from debate to force) — independent dimensions. Factors reducing intensity include dissociation of association conflicts, dissociation of conflict types, dissociation of authority and reward scales, and open classes ([[social-mobility]]). Factors reducing violence include organization itself, relative (vs absolute) deprivation, and effective **[[institutionalization-of-class-conflict|regulation]]** (recognition of the opponent, organization, procedural rules; conciliation / mediation / arbitration). Suppression and Utopian "resolution" are distinguished from regulation; ideologies of harmony can *increase* violence. Structure change co-varies: suddenness with violence; radicalness with intensity.

On **functions of conflict**, Dahrendorf uses Coser critically. Coser (following Simmel and Sorel) is granted for integrative and anti-ossification functions of conflict — crisscrossing conflicts that "sew the social system together" — but judged "rather too preoccupied with 'positive' or 'integrative functions.'" From the integration point of view alone, group conflicts remain a nuisance that may "endanger basic consensus." Dahrendorf's preferred formulation (with Dubin): continuing group conflict is "an important way of giving direction to social change." Societies are "essentially historical creatures" and require conflict as motive force. He refuses a categorical split between conflicts "within" and "of" the system (Coser, Marshall, Parsons): if conflict and change are ubiquitous, "the 'system' is no longer the frame of reference." Wage disputes and political conflicts "over the system itself" are both class conflict when they arise from authority distribution in associations. Competition is not excluded from conflict; "established rules" mark regulated conflict, not a separate genus.

**Regulation** (Ch. VI) is neither Utopian resolution nor suppression. Prerequisites: both parties recognize the necessity and reality of the conflict situation and the fundamental justice of the opponent's *right to a case* (not the substance of the opponent's interests); organization of both sides; and agreed procedural rules. Techniques: conciliation, mediation, arbitration. Compulsory settlement and harmony ideologies are rejected as increasing rather than decreasing violence. Absolute deprivation tends toward violence; relative deprivation toward intensity. Open classes (mobility) lower intensity; closed classes raise it. "Classlessness" by permanently alternating chairmanship or extreme mobility is a limiting case of open authority structures, not a Utopian society without domination — the latter is sociologically meaningless if authority is universal.

### 5. Post-capitalist application

Part One documents changes since Marx — [[decomposition-of-capital]], [[decomposition-of-labor]], [[new-middle-class]], mobility, Marshallian citizenship, Geiger's institutionalization of class conflict — that falsify unmodified Marxian polarization without abolishing authority inequality. Part Two applies the formal theory: the enterprise remains an ICA (USSR as USA); industrial democracy regulates industrial conflict; **institutional isolation** confines industrial conflict to industry, yielding two tendentially discrete conflict fronts (industry vs political society). The political **ruling class** core is governmental elites (legislative, executive, judiciary) plus administrative staff and interested parties — bureaucracy always *in* the ruling side, never alone *the* ruling class. Freedom vs totalitarianism is pluralism that accepts conflict vs monism that seeks to eliminate it.

Part One supplies the historical premise. **[[decomposition-of-capital]]**: joint-stock expansion separates ownership from control; Renner's "capitalists without function" yield to "functionaries without capital"; legitimacy of entrepreneurial authority shifts from property toward consensus plus delegated property. **[[decomposition-of-labor]]**: skill stratification replaces the homogeneous unskilled mass Marx expected to polarize; post-1900 rise of semiskilled and renewed demand for high skill undercut the path from homogeneity to revolution. The **[[new-middle-class]]** is "born decomposed" — Dahrendorf splits bureaucrats (hierarchical authority, service class on the ruling side) from white-collar workers (salaried but non-bureaucratic). Mobility and Marshallian citizenship rights further falsify polarization while leaving **power and authority** as the fundamental lasting inequality. Geiger's phrase **institutionalization of class conflict** names the result: recognition, organization, rules, and structural reforms make the market a regulated arena rather than a battlefield — Marx's naiveté, for Dahrendorf, was to assume society could not cope with class conflict except by revolution or abolition.

Industrial democracy (Ch. VII) has five elements in Dahrendorf's reconstruction: organization of conflict parties; collective bargaining as quasi-parliamentary regulation; mediation/arbitration as second line (political not legal conception of conflict); shop councils/stewards (ambivalent dual authority); co-determination (person mobility into ruling quasi-group — latent interests exchange with positions). German co-determination is often "perverted" regulation that blocks channels. Countertrends: oligarchy, wildcats, totalitarianism. The isolation thesis reconciles Burnham (conflict persists *in* industry) with Schelsky (old classes no longer bind whole society) by denying both authors' general validity claims.

The program is **middle-range** and **illustrative**, not a single designed empirical study. Dahrendorf himself caveats that his "theory" lacks full formalization:

> "these suggestions do not display a degree of formalization and rigidity that would warrant calling them a theory."

The numbered summary (end of Ch. VI) still supplies the program's self-image: meta-premises (explain structure change via group conflict under a coercion image of society); concept definitions (authority, ICA, latent/manifest interests, quasi-group, interest group, social class as always conflict groups, intensity/violence, radicalness/suddenness of change); formation model (two aggregates → quasi-groups → interest groups under variable organization conditions); intensity propositions (organization, dissociation of association conflicts, dissociation of group conflicts, authority–rewards dissociation, open classes); violence propositions (organization, relative deprivation, effective regulation); structure-change co-variance with conflict modes. Hits of the program relative to predecessors: it can say why regulated industrial conflict persists without revolution; why managers replace capitalists without abolishing industrial classes; why bureaucracy is powerful yet not alone the ruling class; why totalitarian monism is a distinct intensity regime. Misses and anomalies: borderline staff specialists; co-determination's ambiguous effects; mid-century Western scope; later deindustrialization and financialization outside the illustration set.

### Marx sustained / rejected (Ch. IV checklist)

| Verdict | Element |
|---|---|
| **Sustained** | Primacy of structural-change analysis; conflict groups as forces of change; conflicts traced to structure; formal two-class model (attacker vs status-quo) within associations; conflict definitional of classes |
| **Rejected** | Class struggle as history of all past society; always-revolutionary change and linear intensification; always-violent "class struggle"; property as class determinant; political power necessarily follows industrial power; classes as economic groupings / stratification lines |
| **Supplemented** | Roles and personnel (recruitment; consciousness and culture deferred to later chapters) |
| **Replaced by** | Authority participation/exclusion in ICAs |

This checklist is the program's self-accounting of intellectual debt and rupture — the place where conflict theory claims continuity with Marx's *problem* while changing the *theory*.

## Method Commitments

- **Heuristic dualism:** integration and coercion models as complementary scientific assumptions, not metaphysical essences.
- **Structural roles first:** authority structures analyzable independent of specific personnel; personnel/recruitment/consciousness as second step (Marx supplemented).
- **Middle-range confinement:** industrial society; no claim to a theory of *all* social conflict — only class-type conflicts from ICA authority structures.
- **Science vocabulary:** hypothesis, empirical test, refutation (rejoicing at refuting Marx's sociological hypotheses); Mertonian middle-range orientation.
- **Stratum/class hygiene:** refuse prestige-scale dilution of class (Warner out).

## Major Debates and Rivals

1. **[[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]** — Utopian/integration vs Rationalist/coercion; Dahrendorf's dual-heuristic third position.
2. **Marx sustained vs rejected** — structure–conflict–change sustained; property, universal history of class struggle, revolutionary teleology rejected (Ch. IV checklist).
3. **Class vs stratum** — analytical conflict groups vs descriptive hierarchy (Ch. III dilution critique).
4. **Managerial / new middle class politics** — Burnham, Renner service class, Mills, Dahrendorf's authority criterion splitting bureaucrats from white-collar ([[new-middle-class-politics-debate]]).
5. **Still a class society?** — Schelsky et al. vs Dahrendorf's "naive question": wherever authority is unequal, classes exist; the problem is patterns, not presence.
6. **Ruling class composition** — governmental elites vs industrial managers as core (vs Burnham/Mills as he reads them).

## Critiques and Responses

**Internal early criticisms** (English preface): misuse of "class" label; power/authority definitions; industrial/political separation; ruling-class application; missing large-scale inquiry. Dahrendorf treats them as real soft spots.

**Program-level positions (later literature, not fully ingested here):** authority dichotomies may understate multi-dimensional stratification (status, market class, gender, race, ethnicity); institutionalization may normalize asymmetric power under historically specific postwar settlements; the Janus-head dualism can freeze teaching into two camps rather than integrate practice-theoretic accounts. Dahrendorf's own response pattern in the book is to grant the integration face equal formal status while developing the coercion face for the problems at hand — and to insist conflict regulation, not harmony ideology, is the democratic path.

Value stance is explicit and should not be laundered into wiki voice. On value judgments he "would not hesitate… to express a strong preference for the concept of societies that recognizes conflict as an essential feature of their structure and process," and he treats creativity and development as due "to no small extent" to conflict. The closing of Ch. VIII equates free societies with **pluralism** (accept conflict) and totalitarian societies with **monism** (eliminate conflict). That is liberal political theory as much as sociology — recorded as **position**, and it shapes which variables (regulation, organization rights, electoral exchange) he treats as virtuous reducers of violence.

## What Survived

Even where "Dahrendorfian class theory" is no longer a named research school:

- **Endemic conflict** as a normal feature of social organization, not residual pathology.
- **Authority structures** as generators of opposed role interests in organizations (industrial relations; organizational sociology).
- **Intensity vs violence** and the vocabulary of **regulated** vs suppressed conflict.
- **Pluralism vs superimposition** of cleavages as intensity variables (political sociology of cleavage structures).
- **Institutional isolation** of industrial conflict as a mid-century diagnosis of Western dual fronts (industry vs party politics).
- **Stratum ≠ class** hygiene against prestige-scale "class analysis."
- Teaching dualism of **consensus vs conflict** paradigms — for better or worse, the book's most durable classroom product ([[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]).

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active` as vocabulary and problem-set; the specific ICA-authority reconstruction of *class* competes with neo-Marxian, Weberian multi-dimensional, and cultural/identity-based stratification programs. In political sociology the program's lasting purchase is less "are there still classes?" (Dahrendorf's own answer is yes, wherever authority is unequal — a claim he calls almost tautological once the theory is accepted) than **how conflict is patterned**: plural vs superimposed cleavages, industrial–political dissociation, service-class bureaucracy, democratic regulation vs monist suppression. Industrial-relations research absorbed the institutionalization vocabulary even where it never cited Dahrendorf by name; classroom theory courses absorbed the consensus/conflict dualism even more widely.

Frontiers for this wiki: ingest Coser (*The Functions of Social Conflict*), full Parsons (*The Social System* and later), Lockwood's own essays, and later critiques (including feminist and race-analytic challenges to authority-only class maps); link industrial-democracy findings to postwar labor history and comparative co-determination research; test the isolation thesis against deindustrialization, financialization, and platform labor (beyond Dahrendorf's mid-century cases); densify the political Ch. VIII ruling-class account against Mills's *Power Elite* once that source is ingested. Expand this hub when Coser/Collins enter the collection — the present analysis is Dahrendorf-centered by source constraint, not by field judgment that he alone *is* conflict theory.

**Counterfactual note (genealogy tool, not fact):** without Dahrendorf's 1957/59 statement, postwar sociology still had Coser, Lockwood, industrial-relations institutionalization, and elite theory; what might have been thinner is the *systematic* rebuild of *class* as authority-based conflict groups with an explicit middle-range formation model and a sustained/rejected Marx checklist. The classroom dualism of consensus vs conflict might still have emerged from Parsons-plus-critics, but Dahrendorf gave it its most textbook-ready two-column form.
