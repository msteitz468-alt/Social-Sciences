# Cultural Materialism — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[cultural-materialism]]. Link hub by full path `[[hubs/theory/cultural-materialism]]`.

**Scope.** Primary base for this analysis: [[harris-marvin|Marvin Harris]], *[[harris-rise-anthropological-theory-1968|The Rise of Anthropological Theory: A History of Theories of Culture]]* (Thomas Y. Crowell, 1968). *Rise* is simultaneously a **partisan historiography** of two centuries of culture theory and the **naming statement** of cultural materialism as research strategy. Harris's later *Cultural Materialism* (1979) consolidates infrastructure–structure–superstructure vocabulary and is not yet primary-ingested; where 1979 terminology is mentioned below it is historiographic, not extracted as finding from *Rise*.

All programmatic claims in **attributed voice** (Harris's). Empirical illustrations inside *Rise* are Harris's readings of other people's research, not independent findings of this ingest.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Cultural materialism is a **critical-turn** (late 1960s) American anthropological research program that answers what Harris periodizes as a decades-long **idiographic interlude** in which anthropology abandoned its founding mandate as a nomothetic science of history.

**What Harris says the program reacts against** (*Rise*, Introduction; chs 9–21):

1. **Boasian [[historical-particularism]].** Harris's central historiographic thesis is that Boas and the Columbia school denied historical laws, exaggerated culture's quixotic and irrational ingredients, stressed subjective meaning over objective relations, and "denied the determinism of the material conditions of life" (*Rise*, Introduction — attributed). The particularist interlude, he argues, was not theory-neutral data collection: restricted theoretical frames still produced wide conclusions about history and culture that spread to adjacent disciplines and public culture.

2. **[[culture-and-personality]] and configurationism.** Pre-Freudian, Freudian, and neo-Freudian psychologizing of culture (Benedict, Mead, Kardiner, national-character work) are treated as least suited to explaining institutional regularities; personality, for Harris, is not the origin of those regularities (*Rise*, chs 15–17 — attributed).

3. **[[diffusionism]] (British Egyptocentric; German *Kulturkreis*).** Diffusionist historicism, Harris argues, multiplies historical connections without causal priority on production and habitat (*Rise*, ch. 14).

4. **French [[structuralism]] and British social anthropology.** Lévi-Strauss's mentalism / binary models and British structural-functionalism's solidarity-function analyses are read as idealist or functional non-explanations that reverse or suspend techno-economic priority (*Rise*, chs 18–19 — attributed).

5. **Pure emic "new ethnography" / ethnoscience.** Emic rule-books alone, Harris holds, are a poor guide to historical events and etic regularities (*Rise*, ch. 20).

6. **Dialectical and revolutionary Marxism.** Harris retains Marx/Engels as cultural materialists of "unparalleled importance" but rejects Hegelian dialectic and revolutionary *partiinost* as scientifically unconfirmed (*Rise*, Introduction; ch. 8).

**Positive lineage Harris claims:**

- Enlightenment natural-determinist and proto-techno-environmental strands (Turgot, Millar, Helvétius, etc.) (*Rise*, ch. 2).
- Nineteenth-century evolutionism **critically recovered** — methods and some results retained; racialism, progress-moralism, and non-materialist kinship schemas rejected (*Rise*, chs 5–7).
- Marx/Engels's materialist strategy (especially the *Preface* as cultural selection analogue) **minus dialectic** (*Rise*, ch. 8).
- [[white-leslie-a|White]]'s energetic/evolutionary priorities (formulated strategy "under the pseudonym of evolutionism" but, Harris says, failed on specific cases) (*Rise*, ch. 22).
- [[steward-julian-h|Steward]]'s [[cultural-ecology]] as the leading **concrete application** of materialist principles to differences and similarities (*Rise*, ch. 23).

**Disciplinary moment.** Columbia anthropology, 1960s expansion of funded research and development anthropology; Harris frames macro-theory as urgency against "publishing more and more about less and less" and against applied work without theory of change (*Rise*, Introduction). Cold-war positioning is explicit: "a plague on both your houses" toward party hacks and bourgeois idealism.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Techno-environmental / techno-economic determinism

Harris's load-bearing formulation (*Rise*, Introduction):

> "I believe that the analogue of the Darwinian strategy in the realm of sociocultural phenomena is the principle of techno-environmental and techno-economic determinism. This principle holds that similar technologies applied to similar environments tend to produce similar arrangements of labor in production and distribution, and that these in turn call forth similar kinds of social groupings, which justify and coordinate their activities by means of similar systems of values and beliefs."

**What the words commit.** Priority of technology-in-environment for labor organization, then social grouping, then values/beliefs. Causal arrow runs infrastructure → structure → ideology for research purposes. This is **not** a claim that ideas never feedback; it is a **priority rule** for where to look first when seeking regularities.

### 2. Strategy, not closed physics-style theory

Harris insists cultural materialism is analogous to **natural selection as strategy**: Darwin directed attention to differential reproductive success; researchers still must find particular concatenations of causes in each case. Likewise, cultural materialism assigns priority to material conditions without already stating every law of sociocultural evolution (*Rise*, Introduction; chs 8, 22).

In ch. 22 he restates the strategy as meta-generalization (energy systems as independent variables; other domains dependent), "fully analogous to… the principle of natural selection."

### 3. Decontamination of "materialism"

Harris separates:

| Form | Status for Harris |
|---|---|
| Philosophical/ontological materialism | Left to metaphysics; not required for sociocultural causal priority |
| Dialectical materialism (Marxist-political) | Sub-case of cultural materialism **contaminated** by dialectic and revolution; unconfirmed anthropologically |
| Cultural materialism | Research strategy to be "decontaminated" for science |

He credits Marx and Engels with the breakthrough that social being conditions consciousness ("from earth to heaven") while treating dialectic as "Hegelian monkey" / excess baggage outside mid-nineteenth-century Europe (*Rise*, ch. 8 — attributed).

### 4. Nomothetic science of history

Anthropology "began as the science of history." The early-twentieth-century turn to idiographic uniqueness is, for Harris, a strategic defeat — not a mature specialization. Recovery requires statements of **covariance** (probabilistic predictions/retrodictions about classes of cultures), not only world-historical stage narratives (*Rise*, Introduction; ch. 22).

### 5. White and Steward as incomplete carriers

- **White:** formulates energetic/evolutionary priority but produces "vapid generalizations" and fails to apply the strategy to particular cases; stage summaries lack operational covariance (*Rise*, ch. 22 — attributed).
- **Sahlins & Service** specific/general evolution: Harris rejects phylogeny analogy for culture (no speciation; cultures exchange parts) and rejects "progress" as moral label for thermodynamic efficiency (*Rise*, ch. 22).
- **Steward:** cultural ecology **is** applied cultural materialism — production processes articulated with habitat; techno-environmental variables get priority; social organization and ideology tend to be dependent in large diachronic samples (*Rise*, ch. 23). Multilinear evolution is not a separate method; Steward–White difference is quantitative (scope of regularities), not qualitative.

### 6. Emic and etic

[[emic-and-etic|Emic]] (insider distinctions/rules) and **etic** (observer categories for comparison/measurement) are both legitimate data. Harris's method commitment: emic rules are a poor sole guide to historical events and etic regularities in economic, social, and political subsystems; unrestricted emic ethnography parodies the human condition (*Rise*, ch. 20 — attributed). Etics should not replace emics; both can be studied cross-culturally; ideal/actual ≠ emic/etic.

## The Empirical Program

In *Rise* the "empirical program" is mostly **critical-historiographic**: demonstrate that rival strategies failed to generate viable nomothetic alternatives, and that materialist priorities (when applied — Steward ecology; hydraulic debates; New Archaeology) produce testable hypotheses.

| Claim (Harris) | Supporting studies (as Harris cites) | Challenging studies / problems | Current standing (as of this wiki) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Techno-env. priority yields regularities | Steward band ecology; limited parallels; irrigation/hydraulic debates; settlement-pattern archaeology | Idealists: meaning and structure under-explained; cases with strong ideational drivers | Contested; ecology/archaeology partially absorbed priorities under other labels |
| Emic rules ≠ historical explanation | Bahian fishing; Goodenough Truk case; Bathonga/Maring/Subanum illustrations | Ethnoscience: insider models are the object | Standard metalanguage (emic/etic) survives; priority dispute open |
| White energy law needs covariance form | Harris's own reformulations | White school: general evolution still useful | Energy metrics used selectively; single-curve universalism largely abandoned |
| Steward ecology = applied CM | Steward 1936–55 corpus; Handbook SA; student school | Steward did not self-identify as Harrisian CM | Cultural ecology remains active program; CM label less central |
| Hydraulic / Wittfogel sequence | Millon, Sanders, Adams, Virú, etc. as of 1968 | Failures and regional exceptions Harris himself notes as non-decisive for strategy | Hydraulic hypothesis contested; strategy ≠ single hypothesis (*Rise* closing) |

**Honest limits of *Rise* as empirical charter:** the volume does not itself report new fieldwork. Famous later Harris theses (sacred cow, Aztec cannibalism/protein, Yanomami protein, etc.) are **outside** this source and must be ingested from those monographs before wiki-voice or study pages.

**Closing program statement** (*Rise*, end of ch. 23 — attributed): vindication of cultural materialism does **not** depend on verifying the hydraulic hypothesis or any particular techno-environmental theory; it lies in capacity to generate major explanatory hypotheses testable ethnographically and archaeologically, modifiable if necessary, covering both universal-history features and exotic particularities.

## Method Commitments

1. **Priority rule:** techno-environmental and techno-economic variables first.
2. **Etic operationalization** when seeking regularities; emic data retained as evidence of classification and justification, not as ceiling of explanation ([[emic-and-etic]]).
3. **Covariance / probabilistic formulation** over bare stage lists.
4. **Comparative and ecological analysis** (Steward's three procedures absorbed as materialist method).
5. **Rejection of:** pure idiography; functional "explanation" without causal direction; paradigm frames taken as givens; dialectic as scientific law; progress-as-moral-label for energy efficiency.
6. **Diachronic causal functionalism** (Harris's phrase for materialist sequence) vs synchronic functionalist "vapidity" (*Rise*, ch. 8).

## Major Debates and Rivals

| Rival / debate | Harris's position in *Rise* | Strongest counter-position (record, do not adjudicate) |
|---|---|---|
| Boasian particularism | Premature abandonment of nomothetic science; ideological milieu of professionalization | Boasians: invalid evolutionary comparison and racial misuse of biology were the greater failures; intensive ethnography was necessary |
| Culture and personality | Wrong level for institutional regularities | C&P: personality mediation and meaning are constitutive of culture |
| Structuralism | Mentalist / binary idealism; failed empirical kinship tests (e.g. Purum as Harris reads it) | Structuralists: deep structures of mind explain recurrence better than techno-ecology |
| British SA / structural-functionalism | Solidarity-function without techno-economic causes; colonial practical anthropology | BSA: present structure and function are the proper scientific object; conjectural history failed |
| Dialectical Marxism | Keep materialism; drop dialectic and revolutionary epistemology | Marxists: dialectic and praxis are essential; "decontaminated" CM is bourgeois positivism |
| New ethnography | Emic-only is new old idealism | Ethnoscience: rigorous emic analysis is scientific description |
| White vs Steward | Quantitative scope difference; Steward better on cases; reject phylogeny and "progress" | Sahlins–Service: specific/general dual direction reconciles both |

## Critiques and Responses

**Critique (reductionism).** Techno-economic priority flattens meaning, gender, symbol, and contingency.

*Harris-style response in *Rise*:* priority is strategic, not ontological denial of ideas; ideas justify and coordinate groupings produced under material constraints; multi-factor sketches (e.g. Brazil vs US race relations) show non-monadic explanation (*Rise*, ch. 8).

**Critique (partisan history).** *Rise* caricatures Boas, Lévi-Strauss, and BSA; selective quotation; winners' history for CM.

*Response:* Harris avows critique over encyclopedia (*Rise*, Introduction); readers must treat historiographic chapters as **positions**. This wiki records them as attributed, not as consensus historiography.

**Critique (failed later cases).** Post-1968 empirical CM theses often contested or failed replication.

*Standing:* those theses are not validated by *Rise*; update via Contradiction/Replication protocol when case monographs are ingested.

**Critique (Cold War decontamination).** "Plague on both houses" still centers a scientistic materialism compatible with US developmentalism.

*Reflexivity:* Harris himself ties theory urgency to development programs and funding (*Rise*, Introduction) — production context is content.

---


**Critique (ethnographic thinness of *Rise*).** Because the book is historiography-plus-program rather than a field monograph, its ethnographic illustrations are second-hand and selective. Defenders reply that the point is strategic reorientation of research priorities, not a substitute for case work; the later popular case books are the intended empirical follow-through (still to be audited in this vault).

**Critique (gender and meaning).** Feminist and symbolic anthropologists argued that CM's cascade systematically backgrounds gender as structure, ritual efficacy, and semiotic systems except as epiphenomena. Harris's replies in other works (not *Rise*) often re-translate such domains into techno-environmental functions; whether that translation succeeds is case-by-case and contested.

## What Survived

Even where the **named program** "cultural materialism" is no longer a majority banner:

1. **Emic/etic metalanguage** is standard across anthropology; Harris's etic-priority polemic is one pole of a living method dispute.
2. **Techno-environmental research priority** survives in ecological anthropology, human behavioral ecology, and much archaeological theory (processual and beyond) without always citing Harris.
3. **Stewardian cultural ecology** remains an active lineage Harris claimed as applied CM; the vault already holds [[cultural-ecology]] + Theory Hub from Steward's own text.
4. **Skepticism toward pure emic ethnography and pure structural mentalism** as sufficient for causal history is widely shared even by non-Harrisian scholars.
5. **Infrastructure/structure/superstructure** vocabulary (fuller in 1979) persists in materialist and some Marxist anthropologies.
6. **Public anthropology** of "riddles of culture" (cows, pigs, cannibals) remains Harris's popular afterlife — empirically contested, culturally visible.
7. What largely **did not survive** as orthodoxy: strong techno-economic determinism as sole master key; *Rise*'s totalizing indictment of Boas; dialectic-free Marxism as the discipline's center.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Status **contested** (see summary [[cultural-materialism]]). Who still works in it: residual cultural-materialist and ecological-materialist researchers; some evolutionary social scientists; textbook chapters on theory history. Absorbed: ecological method, emic/etic, materialist hypothesis-generation. Abandoned or marginalized as school: Harrisian CM as comprehensive theory of culture.

### Architecture diagram (Harris 1968 formulation)

Harris's causal priority can be restated without 1979 vocabulary as a three-step cascade:

1. **Technology × environment** → productivity, settlement, labor organization  
2. **Labor / distribution arrangements** → group types (kinship scale, stratification, polity forms)  
3. **Values and beliefs** → justification and coordination of those groups  

The cascade is a **research order**, not a denial that ideology can feedback once established. Harris's multi-factor sketches (e.g. Brazil vs United States race relations in ch. 8) are offered as proof that cultural materialism is not "monadic" single-factor economics: many material variables interact; the contrast is with idealist strategies that reverse the cascade.

### Enlightenment as origin story

Chapter 2 of *Rise* is not antiquarian. Harris argues that the **culture concept**, psychic unity, evolutionary stages, and techno-environmental explanation already appear in Enlightenment authors (Locke, Turgot, Millar, Helvétius, Ferguson, etc.). He attacks Kroeber–Kluckhohn-style chronologies that date the culture concept too late. The point for the program: cultural materialism is a **recovery**, not an invention of the 1960s. Early nineteenth-century "reaction" (degenerationism, theological reaction, racial determinism) and then Boasian particularism **interrupted** a materialist scientific trajectory already underway.

### Racial determinism chapter as negative foil

Chapter 4 ("Rise of Racial Determinism") is load-bearing for Harris's historiography: scientific racism and polygenesis are treated as the catastrophic alternative to culture as lawful, enculturated, material-condition-shaped. Harris's later race–culture separation is continuous with Boasian anti-racism on this point even while he attacks Boasian anti-nomothetic method — a tension the program never fully resolves for critics who see CM as reintroducing evolutionary ranking under thermodynamic dress.

### Evolutionists: methods recovered, results filtered

Chapters 6–7 credit nineteenth-century evolutionists with archaeology and comparative method, survivals, schedules, and statistical inklings (Tylor 1889), while arguing that **none were consistent cultural materialists**. Morgan's scheme is dissected for errors and enduring parts; Bachofen–Maine–McLennan kinship sequences are filtered. Harris's double move: **save the nomothetic ambition and comparative toolkit; discard racialism, progress-moralism, and non-materialist kinship engines**.

### Dialectical materialism chapter: keep base, drop dialectic

Chapter 8 is the program's political-epistemic charter relative to Marxism:

- Engels's graveside claim (Marx as Darwin of social science) is granted in substance for the **materialist strategy**.  
- Unity of theory and practice, *partiinost*, and falsification under class-struggle ethics are rejected as scientifically unacceptable.  
- Marx is read as non-unilinear (Asiatic/ancient/Germanic routes in *Formen*).  
- The 1859 *Preface* is the "principle of cultural selection."  
- Dialectic is the "Hegelian monkey."  
- Engels 1890 Bloch letter ("ultimately" determining) is used against monadic reductionism charges.  
- Morgan/*Origin of the Family* "wedded" cultural materialism to non-materialist prehistory — explaining, for Harris, anthropology's forty-year delay testing the strategy and its reactive posture toward Marxism.

### Historiographic method of *Rise*

Harris states openly that the book is a **critique, not an encyclopedia**, selecting figures by intellectual influence rather than disciplinary identity alone, and that twentieth-century sociology is regrettably omitted for length. That avowal is the warrant for this wiki's treatment: *Rise* is a primary source for **Harris's positions**, not a neutral secondary authority on Boas, Lévi-Strauss, or Radcliffe-Brown. Where the vault holds primary Boas, Steward, White, Lévi-Strauss, etc., those pages control the attributed statements of those programs; *Rise* supplies the cultural-materialist **counter-reading**.

### Case style inside *Rise* (not independent findings)

Illustrations Harris deploys as CM-friendly or CM-critical foils include: Kwakiutl potlatch re-readings (Codere, Suttles, Vayda); Lowie's anti-materialist cases (Chinese milk, Bantu cattle, sacred cows — which Harris later reworks in other books); Bahian fishing captains (emic spots vs etic success); Rappaport Maring pigs/warfare; hydraulic civilization sequences; Teotihuacan settlement work. **None of these become wiki-voice findings from this ingest alone** — they are Harris's citations.



### Statistical nomothetic revival (ch. 21)

Harris treats the Murdock–HRAF statistical cross-cultural tradition as a **partial** return to nomothetic ambition — welcome against pure particularism, but limited by association-without-causation, Galton's problem, taxonomic errors in *Social Structure*, and the "limited possibilities" principle as insufficient substitute for materialist strategy. The chapter positions cultural materialism as needing **more than** correlations: causal priority hypotheses that statistics can test, not replace.

### New Archaeology and the etic turn in prehistory

Near the close of ch. 23 Harris claims that American anthropological archaeology of the 1950s–60s largely conforms to Steward's ecological program — population, settlement, subsistence, habitat — linking archaeology and ethnology under shared **etic** assumptions (Binford cited among others). For cultural materialism this is strategic vindication: deep-time material records answer questions ethnography alone cannot, provided research priorities are techno-environmental rather than ceramic taxonomy alone. Processual archaeology is thus, in Harris's 1968 reading, an ally program — not yet the later post-processual critique.

### Why *Rise* still matters when the school is contested

Even readers who reject Harris's strongest techno-economic claims use *Rise* as a **map of the field's theoretical genealogy**. Its chapter architecture (Enlightenment → race → Spencer → evolutionism → Marx → Boas → Kroeber/Lowie → diffusion → C&P → structuralism → BSA → emics/etics → statistics → CM) became a teaching scaffold for theory courses. The book's durability is therefore double: as program charter for cultural materialists, and as a **polemical syllabus** that forced later historians of anthropology to answer it. This wiki treats both uses: the program page and hub record CM claims; Boas/Steward/White/structuralism primary pages remain authoritative for those programs' own voice.



### Production context and reflexivity (expanded)

Harris wrote *Rise* at Columbia during anthropology's postwar expansion into development consulting and large grants. The Introduction's urgency rhetoric — macro-theory required lest research funding produce trivia, and lest development programs produce "underdevelopment" — places cultural materialism inside the political economy of the discipline, not outside it. The cold-war "plague on both houses" stance (party Marxism vs bourgeois idealism) is itself a mid-century US academic position: it salvages Marx for scientistic use while refusing Communist party epistemology. Critics of CM often reverse this reflexivity: they treat techno-economic priority as continuous with developmentalist and modernization projects that reshaped the Third World under US hegemony. This hub records both the program's self-understanding and that critical frame without adjudicating.
