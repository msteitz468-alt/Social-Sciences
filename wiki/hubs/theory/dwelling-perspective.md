# Dwelling Perspective — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[dwelling-perspective]]. Built same session as *Perception of the Environment* ingest.

**Analysis standard:** claims-vs-evidence matrix · verbatim key passages · empirical program honesty · What Survived · nine canonical sections. Attributed voice throughout for program claims.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Late-twentieth-century anthropology was fractured along biophysical vs sociocultural lines — the same "two worlds" of humanity and nature that [[ingold-tim|Ingold]] treats as the fault line of Western thought (*Perception*, General introduction). Cognitive science and dual-inheritance models offered one reunification path (culture as information analogue of genes). Ingold took another: Gibsonian ecological psychology, developmental systems biology, phenomenology (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty), and Bateson's ecology of mind. His own earlier *Appropriation of Nature* (1986) still worked a dualist "interplay" of social and ecological domains; the 1988 insight that organism and person are one, and the 1990s essays collected in 2000, reverse that architecture.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Building vs dwelling

> "Here, then, is the essence of the building perspective: that worlds are made before they are lived in; or in other words, that acts of dwelling are preceded by acts of worldmaking." (Ch. 10)

> "'We do not dwell because we have built, but we build and have built because we dwell… Only if we are capable of dwelling, only then can we build' (Heidegger 1971…). I take this to be the founding statement of the dwelling perspective." (Ch. 10)

On the building perspective, culture is imposition of design or symbolic meaning on a material substrate (Geertzian webs on a separate plane; Rapoport: space organized cognitively before built). On the dwelling perspective, forms arise within involved activity; the world is the "homeland of their thoughts" (Merleau-Ponty via Ingold); envisioning is itself situated.

### 2. Taskscape and landscape

> "I shall adopt the term 'task'… The entire ensemble of tasks, in their mutual interlocking… [is] the 'taskscape'… the taskscape is to labour what the landscape is to land." (Ch. 11)

> "the landscape is constituted as an enduring record of – and testimony to – the lives and works of past generations who have dwelt within it." (Ch. 11)

> "I argued… that the landscape is not nature; here I claim that the taskscape is not culture. Landscape and taskscape, then, are not to be opposed as nature to culture." (Ch. 11)

> landscape as whole = "the taskscape in its embodied form: a pattern of activities 'collapsed' into an array of features." (Ch. 11)

Temporality is rhythmic resonance of concurrent activity cycles (not metronomic labour-hours). Paths are "the taskscape made visible." Archaeology's object: "the temporality of the landscape."

### 3. Enskilment and affordance

Cultural variation is variation in skill; skills are grown in organism–environment systems. Perception is exploratory pickup of [[affordance|affordances]] (Gibson). Learning is education of attention, not hand-down of representations. Making is growth (weaving paradigm), not hylomorphic transcription.

### 4. Livelihood: trust, procurement, interagentivity

Hunting-as-trust (autonomy + dependency; gift of prey) vs pastoral domination rewrites domestication (Ch. 4). "Nature is a cultural construction" is incoherent (Ch. 3). Social relations are a subset of ecological relations; **interagentivity** with other-than-human persons (Cree, Ojibwa/Hallowell) replaces intersubjectivity limited to humans.

### 5. Wayfinding, globes, senses

Wayfinding through a region vs navigation across a mapped surface (Ch. 13). Globe (from without) vs sphere (from within) topologies of environmentalism (Ch. 12). Vision/hearing hierarchy dismantled via Gibson and Merleau-Ponty (Ch. 14).

### 6. Skill, technology, modernity packages

Technology as modern disembedded concept (Chs. 15–16). Work/leisure and clock-time as commodity overlays on primary task orientation (Ch. 17). "Anatomically modern human" and language-origins as analytic fictions (Chs. 21–22). Craft, song, and imagination replace technology/language/intelligence triad (Ch. 23).

## Method Commitments

Ecological psychology over cognitivist representation; developmental systems over transmissionism; secondary ethnography as philosophical material; archaeology as dwelling; "sentient ecology" / poetics of dwelling against nature-as-spectacle.

## Major Debates and Rivals

- Nature/culture dualism ([[nature-culture-distinction]]) — Descola's four ontologies is a parallel exit route with different architecture.
- Optimal foraging / evolutionary psychology vs enskilment (Ch. 2).
- Building perspective in architecture and cognitive anthropology.
- Practice theory ([[practice-theory]]) — alliance and difference (Lave, Bourdieu cited).
- Ontological turn (Descola, Viveiros) — critical proximity on animism/personhood.

## Critiques and Responses

Power/history deficit charges; second-hand ethnography; falsifiability. Ingold: trust is not arcadian; domination not simply "bad"; commodity perspective overlays rather than abolishes dwelling (Ch. 17).

## What Survived

- **Taskscape** as archaeological keyword.
- Dwelling vs building as pedagogical contrast in environment/architecture.
- Skill/enskilment and education-of-attention vocabulary in craft and learning studies.
- Wayfinding critiques of mental-map orthodoxy.
- Anti-dualist environmental anthropology as a standing option beside Descola-style ontology.
- Influence corridors toward multi-species work ([[multi-species-ethnography]], [[arts-of-noticing]]) without identity.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Active. Later Ingold corpus and critical literature not yet ingested — this hub centers the 2000 charter. Frontiers: political-economy densification; primary ethnographies Ingold cites; post-2000 paleoanthropology response to AMH critique.
