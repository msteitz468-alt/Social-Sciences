## Intellectual Origins and Context

Behavioral archaeology was born not as a rival school imported from outside [[processual-archaeology]], but as a **self-critique from within the New Archaeology’s training pipelines**. [[schiffer-michael-b|Michael B. Schiffer]]’s 1995 personal history is unusually candid about that genealogy. As a UCLA undergraduate (1965–69) he was steered to [[hill-james-n|James N. Hill]], took courses from Sally R. Binford and James Sackett, and heard [[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]] lecture without notes in Haines Hall — “Archaeology as Anthropology” (1962) still semi-opaque until Hill and graduate assistants insisted it was seminal (attributed, Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1). Sally Binford sent him to Paul S. Martin’s NSF Southwest Expedition at Vernon (1968), a field laboratory explicitly designed for hypothesis-testing Binfordian archaeology, with senior staff including Mark Leone and Fred Plog. Vernon is where Schiffer first encountered the gap that would become the program: empty pit-house floors, sherd-color “activity” patterns that evaporated under formation-process scrutiny, and geological processes as sources of variability his teachers had not made first-class (attributed, Ch. 1).

He entered the University of Arizona in 1969 expecting to “become a New Archaeologist, helping to bring to fruition the grand visions of Lewis R. Binford.” Instead, he wrote, much of his graduate and early professional work consisted of “grappling with, and attempting to overcome, the internal contradictions and limitations of processualism,” and the program that resulted — crafted with [[reid-j-jefferson|J. Jefferson Reid]], [[rathje-william|William L. Rathje]], and others — is called behavioral archaeology (attributed, Ch. 1). The term itself first appears in a January 1971 Culbert term paper; the four-strategies matrix is Reid’s spring 1972 classroom insight while TAs for Rathje:

> “archaeology is the study of the relationships between human behavior and material culture in all times and all places.” Also on that paper was a simple matrix that gave behavioral archaeology four interrelated strategies. (Schiffer 1995 Ch. 1)

Reid, Schiffer, and Rathje published the strategies paper in *American Anthropologist* (1975) “without suffering any rejections.” Schiffer’s dissertation (defended 14 August 1973; Raymond Thompson chair) carried the warning not to become “my generation’s Walter Taylor” — the program would have to “bear empirical fruit” rather than remain a methodological manifesto (attributed, Ch. 1). The 1976 monograph *Behavioral Archeology* (spelling with one “a” for Arkansas Survey conventions) opened by demolishing, “for once mincing no words,” Binford’s claim that the archaeological record is a fossilized society — a metaphor Schiffer held responsible for “the appalling state of inference in the hands of processualists” (attributed, Ch. 1).

Two further institutional pressures shaped the object. Rathje’s Projet du Garbage forced a redefinition of archaeology broad enough to count modern material culture as legitimate data. Contract archaeology at the Arkansas Survey’s Cache Project convinced Schiffer that CRM expansion both depended on and could contribute to method and theory — and could insert behavioral-archaeology concerns into applied work (attributed, Chs. 1, 8). Intellectual borrowings include Hempelian law-talk (later treated as an “albatross”), Marvin Harris’s *Nature of Cultural Things* for behavioral units, and Zipf least-effort experiments at Vernon — while the culture concept is deliberately stripped of explanatory work (Ch. 23 §19).

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Disciplinary identity: four strategies

The program’s load-bearing redefinition is Reid’s matrix. Strategies are defined by question type, not by technique (attributed, Schiffer 1976 / 1995 Ch. 6):

| | **Particular (descriptive/explanatory) questions** | **General (law-seeking) questions** |
|---|---|---|
| **Past material culture** | **Strategy 1** — traditional prehistory, classical, historical archaeology as law *users* | **Strategy 3** — general behavioral laws from past remains (time depth for long-term change) |
| **Present material culture** | **Strategy 4** — present behavior from present things (Garbage Project exemplar) | **Strategy 2** — ethnoarchaeology, experiment, “living archaeology” for laws useful in studying the past |

Schiffer insisted the strategies are interdependent: 1 and 4 need laws from 2 and 3; impasses when no law is available generate general questions for 2 or 3; individuals need not master all four, but the discipline must support 2 and 3 if 1 and 4 are to succeed (attributed, Ch. 6). The punchline redefines the field:

> It no longer seems possible to view archaeology as only the study of the past. (Schiffer 1976 / 1995 Ch. 6)

### Systemic and archaeological context

The second architectural piece is the 1972 binary. Schiffer quoted Binford’s 1964 “fossil record of the actual operation of an extinct society” and treated the provenience–use-location identity as a problem, not an assumption (attributed, 1972a / 1995 Ch. 2):

> Systemic context labels the condition of an element which is participating in a behavioral system. Archaeological context describes materials which have passed through a cultural system, and which are now the objects of investigation of archaeologists. (Schiffer 1972a / 1995 Ch. 2)

From the same paper come the refuse distinctions still standard in teaching: **primary refuse** (discarded at use location), **secondary refuse** (transported and discarded elsewhere), **de facto refuse** (usable materials abandoned without normal discard — sensitive to abandonment tempo; Pompeii as catastrophic limiting case). Lateral cycling, recycling, and draw-down sit inside systemic-context life histories before deposition.

### Synthetic model of inference: correlates, c-transforms, n-transforms

Chapter 3 (from *Behavioral Archeology* 1976) formalizes three law types for inference (attributed):

1. **Correlates** — regularities relating behavior and material culture *in systemic context* (what New Archaeology often meant by “archaeological theory”).  
2. **C-transforms** — laws of *cultural* formation processes linking systemic attributes to materials deposited by the cultural system.  
3. **N-transforms** — laws of *noncultural* (environmental) processes that further modify the record.

Schiffer’s deconstruction of the uxorilocality-from-design-elements inference (Hill-type) shows each hidden assumption as a missing transform or correlate — including the usually unstated assumption that pottery is discarded at use locations (attributed, Ch. 3). Correlates alone are necessary but not sufficient.

### Transformation view

By the mid-1970s the program’s view of the record is explicitly transformational (attributed, Schiffer 1983 / 1995 Ch. 18):

> Drawing on the insights furnished by Ascher, several investigators argued that, as a result of formation processes, the archaeological record is a transformed or distorted view of artifacts as they once participated in a behavioral system… The basic practical implication of the transformation view is quite simple: regardless of how much evidence is present, the archaeologist cannot read behavior and organization directly from patterns discovered in the archaeological record. However, because formation processes themselves exhibit patterning… the distortions can be rectified by using appropriate analytic and inferential tools built upon our knowledge of the laws governing these processes.

Four dimensions of transformation recur: formal, spatial, quantitative (frequency), relational. Opposed views named in Ch. 18: **entropy** (record as progressive information loss — Ascher) and **sampling-bias** (Collins/Cowgill-type incompleteness without lawlike transforms). The practical maxim: understand deposit genesis *before* applying behavioral correlates.

### Behavioral ontology: activity, chain, performance

Later chapters thicken ontology. The **activity** is the basic unit: patterned interaction of energy sources (usually people) and other artifacts; “human behavior cannot be defined apart from artifacts” (Ch. 23 §8). Artifacts perform **techno-**, **socio-**, and **ideo-functions** (Ch. 15 / §9). Life histories organize as **flow models** (process groups) or **[[behavioral-chain|behavioral chains]]** (activity-by-activity sequences with seven components including pathways to the record — Ch. 5 / §17). Technical choices affect **performance characteristics**; selection among alternatives entails compromises illuminated by performance matrices (Schiffer & [[skibo-james|Skibo]], Ch. 22 / §§11–15). Culture plays “no explanatory role”; borrowed concepts (power, complexity) must be redefined as activities (Ch. 23 §§19–20).

### Archaeologist as formation process

Principle 25 elevates a methodological reflexivity: the behaviors of the archaeologist — recovery, analysis, publication — are a family of cultural formation processes that create variability in what counts as the record (Ch. 23 §25; Broken K Ch. 21). This is stronger than generic “observer bias”: it treats excavation and laboratory practice as law-governed transforms continuous with past discard.

## The Empirical Program

What the program claimed to explain that predecessors could not: **why archaeological patterns do not map one-to-one onto past activity maps**, and how to reconstruct pathways anyway. Hits and misses as of the 1995 corpus:

| Claim | Supporting studies / cases | Challenging studies / positions | Current standing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Record is transformed, not fossil society | Tucson ethnoarch; Nunamiut (ironically cited by Schiffer against Binford’s earlier principles); Broken K ceramic pathways | Binford rejects “distortion” language while ethnoarch shows low artifact–activity correlation | Transformation vocabulary widely absorbed; “distortion” contested |
| Systemic ≠ archaeological context | Refuse typology applications; abandonment models | Clean binary complicated by reclamation, scavenging, archaeological collection | Standard teaching binary |
| FP identification before social inference | Ch. 18 multi-trace checklist; Broken K reanalysis; Hohokam old wood | Many projects still skip identification; theory–practice disjunction Schiffer himself diagnosed | Best practice in CRM/research; uneven uptake |
| Four strategies legitimate modern material culture | Garbage Project; industrial archaeology | Departmental definitions excluding “garbage” (Arizona episode) | Historical archaeology / modern material culture mainstreamed under other labels |
| Design-element “uxorilocality” needs FP control | Broken K reanalysis: factors track vessels/fragments more than residence | Hill 1970 original; statistical critics who ignored FPs | Residence inference from design elements heavily contested; room function better supported |
| Old wood biases wood radiocarbon | Hohokam chronology (Ch. 16 / FPAR Ch. 11) | Long-count defenders; sample-selection disputes | Old-wood problem standard radiocarbon hygiene |
| Performance characteristics explain temper change | Schiffer & Skibo Archaic→Woodland experiment suite | Residual socio/ideo assignment without techno correlates (Schiffer’s own caution) | Experimental materials archaeology active |

**Broken K (Ch. 21)** is the program’s flagship reanalysis: Hill’s 1970 probability-sampled pueblo as New Archaeology landmark; Schiffer’s ceramic formation-process study argues design-element factors largely track restored/restorable vessels and trash pathways rather than uxorilocal design units; room-function inferences fare better; six ceramic-analysis maxims (segregate vessels vs fragments early; treat recovery as FP) follow. **Dalton (Ch. 7)** applies c-/n-transforms to settlement: Morse’s sedentary-band-per-drainage model revised; known Western Lowlands sites read as base camps/multi-use, not pure kill-butchery loci. **Tucson reuse (Ch. 10)** reports household material-culture dynamics (N=184; sample biases noted) — acquisition mechanisms, developmental cycle, low discard rates for some classes. **Hohokam (Ch. 16)** centers the old-wood problem against phase schemes. **Ch. 22** treats technological change as technical choices under a functional field, with experimental magnitudes (cooling, heating, impact, thermal shock, abrasion, portability) required even when direction of effect is predictable.

Misses and self-limits: Schiffer abandoned scaled reuse research partly for scholarly-identity reasons (looking like a Rathje derivative); Strategy 2 work of the 1970s was, he said, too narrow and rarely lawlike; Ch. 23 §28 admits the program then furnished “no ready-made answers to the ‘big’ questions.”

## Method Commitments

1. **Law-seeking actualism** — ethnoarchaeology and experiment as Strategy 2 producers of correlates and c-transforms; n-transforms often from natural sciences.  
2. **Deposit-level multi-trace identification** — size, density, orientation, use-life, damage, accretions, diversity, reassembly, sediments, ecofacts, geochemistry, context (Ch. 18 family of traces).  
3. **Life-history formalization** — flow models and [[behavioral-chain]] with explicit pathways to the record.  
4. **Refuse typology** — primary / secondary / de facto as analytic, not just descriptive, categories.  
5. **Performance matrices** for technological alternatives (Ch. 22).  
6. **CRM as scientific opportunity** — all contract work depends on and can contribute to method and theory (Ch. 8, 23 §27).  
7. **Uniformitarianism of process** — processes have time-space distributions; *laws* of those processes lack time-space referents among boundary conditions (Ch. 9, 23 §4).

## Major Debates and Rivals

### Inside processualism: social inference without FPs

The Longacre–Hill ceramic-design route to residence rules is the internal rival. Schiffer’s early muted critique (synthetic model; behavioral chains via quotidian activities) became unmuted reanalysis (Broken K). The dispute is methodological more than political: same hypothesis-testing rhetoric, different intervening processes.

### Schiffer–Binford: Pompeii, distortion, middle-range theory

The sharpest external rivalry is with Binford after Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology. Binford’s “Behavioral Archaeology and the ‘Pompeii Premise’” (1981) attacked Schiffer’s distortion language; Schiffer’s 1985 reply and review of *Working at Archaeology* record the clash as **positions**:

- Binford: “I totally disagree with Schiffer’s idea that the archaeological record is a distortion of a past cultural system” (quoted by Schiffer 1985b / 1995 Ch. 19).  
- Schiffer: Binford’s own Nunamiut findings show “little correlation between what is done [at the site] and the artifacts remaining,” contradicting Binford’s earlier direct form-and-composition principle (attributed, Ch. 19).  
- Terminology: Binford uses “middle-range theory” / “formation processes” more or less interchangeably for the whole linking apparatus; Schiffer partitions correlates / c-transforms / n-transforms and treats organizational properties as too coarse without proximate variables (attributed, Ch. 19).  
- Pompeii: Schiffer’s Ch. 20 argues the premise Binford attributes is not his; the “real” Pompeii premise is treating floors as complete systemic inventories interrupted in place; most assemblages are depleted by curate behavior, lateral cycling, draw-down, scavenging, collecting/looting (attributed).

No adjudication here — the debate restructured how both camps talked about middle-range work even when branding stayed separate.

### Archaeology-as-history (Trigger) and cultural materialism (Harris)

Ch. 4 (“Archaeology as Behavioral Science”) answers Trigger’s claim that archaeologists answer historical questions rather than seek general principles. Ch. 17 engages Harris’s cultural materialism: infrastructural determinism and etic behavioral categories, while faulting the artifact gap and eschewal of middle-range apparatus (positions).

### Post-processual and critical fieldwork

Later secondary critiques (e.g. Lucas) place formation-process theory still inside representational models of the record — a different ontology of “trace.” The 1995 corpus does not fully answer that turn; FPAR remains inside a science-of-behavior frame.

## What Survived

1. **Refuse typology and systemic/archaeological context** — teaching and CRM staples even where the program label is rare.  
2. **Formation processes as obligatory topic** — textbooks, CRM scopes of work, and method courses; multi-trace deposit thinking.  
3. **Old-wood problem** — radiocarbon hygiene independent of program brand.  
4. **Life-history / behavioral-chain thinking** — parallel to *chaîne opératoire* but use-to-discard inclusive; feeds modern material-culture and discard studies.  
5. **Performance characteristics / technical choices** — experimental and materials archaeology.  
6. **Modern material culture as archaeology** — Strategy 4 mainstreamed as historical archaeology, contemporary archaeology, discard studies (Garbage Project lineage).  
7. **Archaeologist-as-formation-process reflexivity** — recovery and lab practice as formational, continuous with critical fieldwork themes though different ontology.  
8. **Skepticism toward design-element residence rules** — Broken K afterlife permanently raised the bar for ceramic social inference.

What did **not** fully survive as branded package: the four-strategies matrix as disciplinary constitution; Hempelian law rhetoric; the demand that all social concepts be “behavioralized” as activities; confidence that all biases are lawfully “rectifiable.”

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

On the vault’s densified sources (Schiffer 1995 charter + Schiffer 1987 handbook + secondary textbook reportage), behavioral archaeology is **active as method vocabulary and research practice**, less active as a named school with exclusive journals or departments. Frontiers continuous with the program: geoarchaeology and micromorphology as n-transform identification; genetic and isotopic evidence classes that still require depositional pathway control (four-way non-identity with culture/language/people remains a separate wiki rule); contemporary archaeology and discard studies as Strategy 4 heirs; experimental materials science on ceramics and lithics as Strategy 2; CRM compliance literature as the largest applied audience for FP identification.

**Program brand vs absorbed toolkit.** Schiffer’s 1995 preface already framed late-1970s–1980s “methodological successes” as partial vindication while noting that the social paradigm of behavioral archaeology remained less widely adopted than its formation-process toolkit (attributed). That split has only deepened: refuse types, old wood, life-history pathways, and multi-trace deposit identification travel under CRM manuals and textbook method chapters, while few departments advertise “behavioral archaeology” as a concentration. The four-strategies matrix is historically important as a charter and rarely used as an organizational chart of present research groups.

**Open questions left explicit in Ch. 23 §28.** Building a corpus of well-confirmed behavioral laws sufficient for “big” questions after reformulation; integrating post-processual and symmetrical-archaeology trace ontologies without losing deposit-level discipline; whether performance-matrix technological change can scale beyond well-controlled ceramic temper cases; and how genetic and isotopic evidence classes should be folded into c-/n-transform identification without equating populations with cultures. The program’s anti-culture-as-explanatory stance remains a minority metaphilosophy even among practitioners who use its methods daily.

**Relation to middle-range theory.** In practice, many archaeologists treat Schiffer’s correlates/c-transforms/n-transforms and Binford’s middle-range theory as overlapping answers to the same linking problem. The 1985 exchange shows they are not identical: Binford’s “organizational properties” vocabulary and Schiffer’s proximate-variable demand pull inference in different directions even when both reject naive Pompeii readings. Recording both as positions (not merging them in wiki voice) is part of keeping the dispute productive rather than dissolving it into a single “formation processes = MRT” synonymy. The vault’s [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]] summary and this hub should be read as complementary maps of the same 1970s–80s methodological crisis, not as competing claims that only one label is correct.

**Historiographic value of the 1995 personal history.** Chapter 1 is primary source for program sociology: Vernon as hypothesis-testing lab; the “coconspirators” fantasy with Reid; Thompson’s Walter Taylor warning; identity management around the Reuse Project; refusal to make *Advances in Archaeological Method and Theory* a house organ. Those episodes matter for reflexivity — the knowledge products (refuse types, identification checklists) cannot be cleanly separated from Southwest CRM expansion, NSF field schools, and departmental fights over whether garbage counts as archaeology.
