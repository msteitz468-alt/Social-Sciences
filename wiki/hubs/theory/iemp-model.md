# IEMP Model (Sources of Social Power) — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[iemp-model]]. Grounded in [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]]'s *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|The Sources of Social Power, Volume I]]* (1986). Vols. II–IV not yet ingested — industrial and full theoretical conclusions are provisional here.
>
> **Voice note.** Every programmatic claim is **attributed to Mann**. Historical narratives in Vol. I are secondary-synthesis reconstructions dated to mid-1980s scholarship, not current archaeological findings in wiki voice.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Mann’s preface situates the book as the long afterlife of a 1972 paper, “Economic Determinism and Structural Change,” that “purported not only to refute Karl Marx and reorganize Max Weber” but to sketch a better general theory of stratification and change. Immersion in history then “restored a little respect for the complexity and obduracy of facts” and produced a three-volume plan: agrarian power (Vol. I), industrial power (Vol. II), general theory (Vol. III). The intellectual moment is late Cold War comparative-historical sociology: engagement with [[parsons-talcott|Parsonian]] systems, Marxian mode-of-production narratives, [[world-systems-analysis|Wallersteinian]] world-economy units, neo-evolutionary anthropology, and the state-centered turn of [[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]] and [[tilly-charles|Tilly]].

What Mann *reacts against* is less any single rival than a family of **unitary “society” models**. He lists systems theory, Marxism, structuralism, structural functionalism, multidimensional theory, evolutionism, diffusionism, and action theory as orthodoxies that mar their insights by treating society as an unproblematic totality. In practice, he argues, most accounts take the **nation-state** as their unit — a covert late-nineteenth-century imprint — while anti-state alternatives that equate society with transnational capitalism or industrialism “go too far the other way.” State, culture, and economy “almost never coincide.”

The positive inheritance is neo-[[weber-max|Weberian]]: multi-causal caution, ideal-types for messiness, power as organization, and (later) the switchman/tracklaying metaphor for sources that can reorganize history without being eternal “last instances.” Gellner’s **neo-episodic** view of discontinuous structural spurts supplies the developmental tempo. Logistics and military geography (including Lattimore’s radii) supply measurement tools. The 1984 *AES* essay “The Autonomous Power of the State” freezes the [[despotic-power]]/[[infrastructural-power]] pair that Vol. I deploys throughout imperial and European chapters.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Societies as power networks

Mann’s first foundational statement:

> “Societies are constituted of multiple overlapping and intersecting sociospatial networks of power.”

A society, when defined carefully (after Parsons, stripped of systems metaphysics), is only a network of interaction with some cleavage at its boundaries. There is no whole of which economy, polity, and culture are “levels.” Endogenous/exogenous, evolution-within-system, and action-vs-structure-as-totality dualisms lose their force. This is the architecture under every later case: Mesopotamia is not “a society that became an empire” but shifting overlaps among temple, city, trade, and military networks.

### 2. IEMP as organizational means

Second statement: history and structure are best told through **ideological, economic, military, and political (IEMP)** relationships — simultaneously (1) overlapping interaction networks and (2) **organizations**, institutional means of attaining goals. Primacy is organizational, not motivational: not because humans want “ideology” more than “wealth,” but because particular organizational means can, at times, dictate the form of social life at large.

> “A general account of societies, their structure, and their history can best be given in terms of the interrelations of what I will call the four sources of social power: ideological, economic, military, and political (IEMP) relationships. These are (1) overlapping networks of social interaction, not dimensions, levels, or factors of a single social totality. … (2) They are also organizations, institutional means of attaining human goals. Their primacy comes not from the strength of human desires for ideological, economic, military, or political satisfaction but from the particular organizational means each possesses to attain human goals, whatever these may be.”

This passage is load-bearing for two refusals. First, **factor language** (the endless debate whether “ideas” or “material forces” weigh more) is replaced by organizational morphology: ask what form of cooperation a source can stabilize, over what space, with what logistics. Second, **motivational reduction** (wealth-drive explains economic primacy) is blocked: subsistence drives may be universal constants while alluvial opportunity and organizational capture explain civilization’s location.

Cross-cutting dualisms organize measurement:

| Dualism | Pole A | Pole B |
|---|---|---|
| Reach | [[extensive-power]] — large numbers over far-flung territories, minimal stable cooperation | [[intensive-power]] — tight organization, high mobilization/commitment |
| Form | [[authoritative-power]] — willed commands, conscious obedience | [[diffused-power]] — spontaneous, decentered practices felt natural/moral |
| Stake | Collective power — cooperation capacity | Distributive power — who benefits within cooperation |

### 3. The four means (Ch. 1 + Ch. 16 restatement)

**Ideological power** rests on monopolies of meaning (Weber), norms (Durkheim), and aesthetic/ritual practice (“You cannot argue with a song”). Mann refuses the equation of ideology with false consciousness or pure mask: ideologies “surpass experience” (Parsons) but must be plausible and genuinely adhered to. Two configurations:

- **Transcendence** — sacred authority cutting across existing EMP networks; creates society-like religious/cultural communities from interstitial needs (early civilization cultures; world salvation religions; classical Greece; modern Marxism as secular example).
- **Immanent morale** — intensifies cohesion of an already-established group (class, nation, army); Assyrian, Persian, and Roman ruling-class cultures are paradigms.

**Economic power** organizes extraction, transformation, distribution, and consumption of nature. **Class** is defined as a purely economic power grouping; **social stratification** is any distribution of power; a **ruling class** monopolizes other sources to dominate a state-centered society. Economic organization is a **circuit of praxis**: intensive mass labor + extensive impersonal exchange. Mann refuses a priori production-over-exchange ranking (Marxist vs Polanyi/neo-Weberian); emergence means traders can act back on producers (Phoenicia). Classes are important but **not** the general “motor of history.”

**Military power** is **concentrated coercion**: intensive core of force plus extensive terroristic penumbra. Ancient unsupported march range ~80–90 km limits intensive control. Militarism can build ([[compulsory-cooperation]], corvée, roads) as well as destroy — a claim against pure parasitic militarism.

**Political power** is restricted to **centralized, institutionalized, territorial** regulation — **state** power — plus **geopolitical diplomacy** among states. Political power *heightens boundaries*; other sources may transcend them. Domestically: [[despotic-power]] (what elites can do without routine negotiation) vs [[infrastructural-power]] (penetration and logistical implementation). The main political reorganization of history, Mann argues, is often the **geographical bounding** of social life into more unitary territories, not pure despotic will.

> “Political power is necessarily centralized and territorial, and in these respects differs from the other power sources.”

The **four-not-three** move matters against both Marxist state-as-class-committee reductions and against Weberian treatments that fold military into political. Mann insists military concentrated coercion and political territorial regulation are sociospatially different: the army’s strike penumbra is not the same as the state’s administrative chart. Geopolitics — hegemonic empire over clients vs multistate civilization — is political power organization, not an IR appendix outside “society.”

### 4. Mechanisms: outflanking, promiscuity, interstitial tracklaying

Masses often comply because they are **organizationally outflanked** — embedded in collective and distributive organizations controlled by others — not only because of value consensus, force, or exchange. Sources are **promiscuous**: a military innovation (pike phalanx) draws on morale, economy, and tradition; mono-factorial ranking is a “ritual without hope.” New power often emerges **interstitially** and lays tracks that subsequent history runs on (Weberian switchman / tracklaying vehicles metaphor in Ch. 1).

### 5. Developmental morphology (Vol. I)

- **[[social-caging]]:** most prehistoric peoples evade stable power; alluvial and related cages trap populations into permanent stratification/state/patriarchy.
- **[[multi-power-actor-civilization]]** vs **[[empire-of-domination]]** vs territorial empire (Rome): alternating poles of agrarian high power.
- **[[normative-pacification]]:** ideological regulation of multi-power fields (Christendom; Mann’s reading of caste).
- **[[organic-national-state]]:** early modern fusion of class-as-class with territorial center; constitutional paths more organic than many absolutisms.
- **European dynamism:** accidental conjunction of two macropatterns (eastern political blockage; western agricultural-trading opportunity) acting on patterned European networks — capitalism, organic states, diplomatically regulated multistate civilization — with **Christendom** singled out as necessary for what followed.

**Hits:** organizational measurement of power; state dualism; multi-source European account that refuses single-factor late starts (Protestantism alone, etc.); class *phase* model tied to infrastructures.

**Misses / thin zones (Mann’s own and critics’):** gender and household largely outside the public-sphere frame; non-Eurasian densification lighter; secondary lag on archaeology/linguistics (esp. “Indo-Europeans”); industrial and global twentieth-century tests reserved for later volumes.

### European dynamism worked through (Chs. 12–15)

Mann’s European story is the program’s most ambitious empirical-historical bet. Mid-twelfth-century Europe, he argues, was a **multiple acephalous federation** of villages, manors, and small states bound by the [[normative-pacification]] of Christendom — already intensive in wet-soil agriculture, still behind Asia extensively. Dynamism is **not** a late single cause (towns, double-entry, Renaissance, navigation, science, Protestantism, English agrarian capitalism alone): those “start too late.” Unity and cause must predate them. Comparative Europe-vs-China isolation of variables fails because the cases differ on *all* candidate variables and are not autonomous.

The constructive account:

1. **Christendom** as ideological network — normative pacification, semi-rational/semi-apocalyptic motivation; **necessary** for markets, property norms, and “rational restlessness.”
2. **Small states** — judicial confirmation of custom; later fiscal-military growth under war pressure (~70–90% of English financial resources on military force across centuries).
3. **Military networks** — feudal levy to mixed armies; stratification and surplus for trade.
4. **Economic networks** — village/manor intensity + extensive trade corridors settling into diffuse private-property capitalism.

By 1477 these are becoming multistate capitalist civilization. Overall theory:

> “The European dynamic was the accidental conjunction of two macropatterns… acting on the unique but internally patterned power networks of Europe.”

Two causal directions between state and capital (Ch. 15): (1) **military/political → economic** — geopolitical units spatially shape class circuits of praxis into national segments; (2) **economic → state** — after the Military Revolution, states collaborate with dominant classes into organic unity (Marx’s executive-committee image *in this distributive sense only*). No last-instance ranking of the two directions.

### Class history as infrastructure history (Ch. 16)

Mann’s four-phase class model (latent → extensive → symmetrical → political), introduced for Greece and reused throughout, culminates in a non-Marxist general point: classes have **not** played a uniform role. Sometimes symmetrical class struggle is a local motor (classical polis); sometimes a single extensive political *ruling* class drives development (Rome); sometimes class networks are weak. Class and **nation** share infrastructural preconditions — diffuse message-passing across extensive space — so they co-emerge as restricted “ruling class-nations” long before mass industrial nations. A theory of class must sit inside a theory of world-historical infrastructures (Iron Age farm, hoplite phalanx, galley, alphabet as preconditions for classical struggle).

## Method Commitments

1. **Socio-spatial logistics** over abstract factor scoring.
2. **Historical narrative** when variables cannot be isolated (Europe vs China — too many differences, non-autonomous cases).
3. **Ideal types** of sources and dualisms, applied messily (Weber).
4. **World-historical time:** infrastructures accumulate; location in cumulative power development matters more than static typological labels (“feudalism,” “aristocratic empire”).
5. **Accident + pattern:** “almost weres” (Thermopylae, Poitiers, Cannae resupply, Paul vs Judaean rivals, etc.) could have reversed East→West drift; still Mann claims *partial* patterns exist.
6. **Zigzag** of theory and data until a plausible account of how a society works is established — sociology and history mutually disciplining.

## Major Debates and Rivals

**Vs unitary systems / Parsons.** Society-as-system and cultural last-instance both rejected; “I am a cultural determinist” (Parsons) is a foil in Ch. 15–16.

**Vs [[historical-materialism]].** Rejects class struggle as general motor, economic determination in the last instance, and “transition from feudalism to capitalism” as master frame of the European miracle. Appropriates Marx’s “executive committee” image *only* for 17th–18th c. organic state–capitalist class fusion in one causal direction (economic → state distributive power).

**Vs [[world-systems-analysis]].** Uses Wallerstein 1974 as expansion literature; insists early modern capitalism is *inter-national* (state-mediated, national circuits of praxis), not already a fully transnational world-economy; rejects simple core→strong state functionalism for constitutional England’s infrastructural strength.

**Vs military monism.** Spencerian exaggeration rejected; militarism is one promiscuous source among four, capable of development via compulsory cooperation.

**Vs pure comparative method.** When cases differ on all variables and are geopolitically entangled, comparison cannot isolate the “crucial difference”; historical narrative is primary for Europe’s uniqueness claim.

**Dialogue with Tilly/Skocpol.** Approves Tilly’s two master processes (capitalism expansion + growth of national states and systems of states) for the modern era; situates Skocpol/Hintze dual determinants (class structure + external state system); adds that medieval states were weak and that military-fiscal costs (~70–90% of English financial resources) make purely internal-function theories of the state simplistic.

## What Survived

> “Power is most fruitfully seen as means, as organization, as infrastructure, as logistics.”

That sentence is the program’s portable core. Even where full IEMP historiography is not followed, research communities retain:

1. **[[infrastructural-power]] / [[despotic-power]]** as a standard two-dimensional map of states (political sociology, comparative politics, empire studies).
2. **Multi-source / multi-network** refusal of economy-only or culture-only last instances.
3. **[[social-caging]]** as a portable metaphor in state-formation and early-complexity debates (adjacent to circumscription traditions).
4. **Organizational/logistical** reading of religion (transcendent ecumene) against pure doctrine or pure class-mask accounts.
5. **Organic / coordinating state** language in early modern state formation, in dialogue with Tilly’s capital-coercion map (Tilly *Coercion, Capital* not yet ingested here).
6. The pedagogical clarity of **IEMP** as a checklist against mono-factorial historical explanation.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

The program remains **active** as a reference architecture in historical and political sociology. Frontiers that require later volumes or other sources: industrial class-nation dialectics (Vol. II); fascism and ethnic cleansing; globalization and empire in the long twentieth century; full theoretical closure (Vol. III/IV). Within this vault, densify when Vols. II–IV and Tilly *Coercion, Capital* are ingested; keep case claims date-stamped against post-1986 archaeology and ancient history.
