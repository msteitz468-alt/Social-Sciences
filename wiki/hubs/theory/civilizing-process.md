# Civilizing Process — Deep Analysis

## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[elias-norbert|Norbert Elias]] wrote *Über den Prozess der Zivilisation* in the 1930s and published it in Basel in 1939, as a German-Jewish scholar in exile on the eve of European catastrophe. The work intervenes in three problem-spaces at once.

**First**, the everyday and political meaning of “civilization.” Part One reconstructs how English and French *civilization* and German *Kultur*/*Zivilisation* crystallized different class and national self-images. Elias argues that French/English “civilization” tends to expand outward (accomplishments *and* manners; universalizing self-confidence of colonizing groups), while German *Kultur* demarcates intellectual/artistic products and national particularity—shaped by a politically fragmented Germany whose middle-class intelligentsia opposed courtly French-speaking upper classes. Kant, in Elias’s reading, crystallizes the court vs intelligentsia antithesis as culture vs civilization; 1919 revitalizes German *Kultur* after a war fought against Germany “in the name of civilization.” This is concept-history as [[sociogenesis-psychogenesis|sociogenesis]]: words as crystallizations of shared experience, not detachable mathematical terms.

**Second**, the detachment of psychology from long-run social structure. Elias refuses the image of the adult as a closed, socially unformed subject. The 1968 Postscript names this image *homo clausus*—the absolutely independent closed personality—and argues that it is itself a civilizational product: firmer self-controls experienced as a wall between “inside” and “outside.” Against it he sets the open personality and the [[figuration]].

**Third**, state theory as definition without process. Weber’s [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of legitimate physical force]] is taken as a constitutive feature of the state; Elias’s move is to ask *how* free competition among warriors becomes dual monopolies of force and tax ([[monopoly-mechanism]]), and how that transformation remoulds habitus.

The work also steers methodologically between “staticism” and formless historical relativism: it seeks order, mechanics, and concrete mechanisms of socio- and psychogenetic change without 19th-century automatic progress ideology (a point sharpened in 1968 against both Comtean progress and Parsonian equilibrium).

### Verbatim load-bearing passages (analysed)

**Unplanned yet ordered civilization.** Elias states that civilization is not a product of human *ratio* or calculated long-term planning; nothing indicates purposive education over centuries produced it; it happened largely unplanned, yet not without a specific type of order. The philosophical stake is the general problem of historical change: formations no one intended that are nonetheless structured. He rejects rational/irrational and spirit/nature dualisms as inadequate; social figurations have relative autonomy illustrated by concrete dynamics (feudalization, monopoly formation).

**Order *sui generis* from interweaving.** Plans and actions interweave “in a friendly or hostile way”; from interdependence arises an order more compelling than the will and reason of the individuals composing it—the social order that determines historical change and underlies the civilizing process. Compared to Hegel’s “cunning of reason,” but demystified as figurational dynamics.

**Self-constraint as second nature.** The stability of psychological self-restraint in the “civilized” habitus stands “in the closest relationship” to monopolization of physical force and stable central organs; only with such monopolies do individuals get attuned from infancy to highly regulated self-restraint that becomes automatic “second nature.”

**Pacified spaces.** With monopoly of force, pacified social spaces appear; physical violence is “confined to barracks” and breaks into individual life mainly in war or upheaval; violence becomes more calculable; unarmed people restrain themselves through foresight—self-control imposed by structure.

**Battlefield moved within.** As physical clashes diminish, tensions earlier released man-against-man must be worked out within the person; vertical oscillations of affect reduce; the horizontal fissure between super-ego and unconscious wishes increases. Individual civilizing, like social, has run largely blindly.

**Shame as super-ego conflict.** Shame is habitually reproduced anxiety; superficially fear of degradation, but in adults the feared superiors accord with one’s own super-ego—so conflict is internal. Fear of prohibition takes on the character of shame the more completely external constraints have become self-restraints. Rationalization is the super-ego’s “foreign policy”; advance of the shame threshold its “domestic policy.”

**Figuration program (1968).** The closed personality is replaced by the open personality, oriented toward and dependent on others throughout life. The network of interdependencies is the **figuration**—a structure of mutually oriented dependent people. People exist only in pluralities, only in figurations. Dance analogy: a dance is a mobile figuration of interdependent people; large figurations (states, cities, economic systems) change like dances. Monopoly formation is partly explained by endogenous dynamics of a figuration of freely competing units.

**Hits:** Made manners and the body first-class sociological objects; process-ualized Weber’s monopoly; linked micro affect controls to macro state formation; provided vocabulary against *homo clausus* and static system theory.

**Misses / anomalies:** Eurocentric upper-class documentary base; under-specified non-Western and colonial figurations in the 1939 core; industrial genocide and total war as stress tests of any one-way civilizing story (see [[civilizing-process-debate]]); manners books as ideology vs behaviour.

## Method Commitments

- Long-term process as normal frame (vs homeostatic equilibrium).
- Figuration as unit (vs individual/society substance dualism).
- Documentary series showing standard-shifts on the *same occasions* across centuries.
- Historical narrative of interdependence and competition, not great-man mosaic alone.
- Technical (non-eulogistic) use of “civilization.”
- Interdisciplinary cooperation (history, psychology, sociology) named as requirement in the Preface.

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **vs moral evolutionary civilizationism:** Elias strips progress ideology while retaining directional structural claims.
- **vs Parsonian structural-functionalism (1968):** pattern variables freeze process into dichotomous states; social change as malfunction between equilibria; nation-as-harmonious-system ideal.
- **vs *homo clausus* psychology / epistemology:** closed subject blocks understanding of long-term moulding of drives.
- **Internal reception debates:** teleology, Eurocentrism, violence anomaly, source bias — [[civilizing-process-debate]].
- **Adjacent:** Bourdieu’s [[habitus]] and [[practice-theory]] share durable dispositions and rejection of pure subject/object dualism but different architectures (field/capital vs figuration/monopoly process) — do not equate without argument.

## Extended empirical walk-through (from the book)

### Part One — The words that do work

Elias treats *civilization* and *Kultur* as instruments of group self-interpretation. English/French civilization can name political, economic, religious, technical, moral, *or* behavioural facts; German *Kultur* centres intellectual, artistic, and religious products and draws a line against “mere” political-economic surface (*Zivilisation* as second-rank). Civilization names process and expansion; *Kultur* delimits and particularizes. Neither is a free-floating ideal type: both crystallize class and national situations—German middle-class intelligentsia without a Paris-style central court that could assimilate bourgeois talent; French court society that “colonized” manners. Literary evidence (Werther’s opposition of court ceremony to middle-class inwardness; Knigge; Frederick the Great’s French taste as court standard) supports the class-polemic origin of the antithesis before its nationalization. French *civilisation* is traced through *civilité* and Physiocratic reform discourse; Napoleon’s Egyptian expedition is noted for colonial use of the civilizing watchword. **Implication for the program:** before one studies “the civilizing process,” one must see that the *word* civilization already does political and class work.

### Part Two — Occasions of everyday life

The empirical heart for affect controls is a series of *occasions*: table, meat carving, knife and fork, nose-blowing, spitting, bedroom and nakedness, relations between the sexes, aggressiveness. Elias’s method is to place precepts from different centuries side by side so that the *same* social occasion shows a shifting frontier of the permissible.

**Civilité landmark.** Erasmus’s *De civilitate morum puerilium* (1530) gives *civilité* a sharp stamp; massive reprint and school use mark social need, not mere individual invention. Erasmus still allows some “natural” behaviours later generations find embarrassing—Elias reads modern discomfort at those passages as a *symptom* of the civilizing process (the threshold has moved). Medieval *courtoisie*/*hövescheit* names court-place upper-class self-image with simpler restraint and friend/foe dichotomies; Renaissance advances shame/embarrassment thresholds as *courtoisie* recedes before *civilité*. Later, *civilisation* becomes the broader self-image of Western upper classes.

**Table.** Movement from communal dishes and hand-eating toward individualized plates, forks, napkins; whole animals and carving spectacle recede “behind the scenes”; knife taboos multiply; fork use tracks revulsion standards more than pure technical invention (the 11th-c. Byzantine fork scandal in Venice is structural, not mere ignorance). Reasons given for good behaviour are long *courtois/civil/bienséant* and élite *délicatesse*, not primarily hygiene—hygienic rationalizations often arrive late (spitting series).

**Body and bedroom.** Natural functions, nose-blowing, and spitting show prestige objects (handkerchief) becoming second nature, then medicalized; bedroom and nakedness privatize; adult–child distance grows as children must attain in a few years standards built over centuries.

**Sex and aggression.** Sexual shame frontiers advance; pleasure in open violence declines among upper classes as the monopoly of force removes free warrior outlets—aggression is transformed, not abolished. Knightly “House-Book” scenes illustrate earlier standards of affect display.

**Curve, not jump.** Elias describes a medieval plateau of manners standards, rapid movement in the sixteenth–eighteenth centuries, then a more stabilized modern standard—with class diffusion downward via Church and cheap *civilité* tracts in the eighteenth century. Court cores often need no books; provincial nobles, foreigners, and bourgeoisie are the addressees.

### Part Three — From free warriors to monopolists

Elias refuses pure great-man mosaic history: kings’ personal gifts matter more under free competition of “private enterprise” territorial rule than under later organized monopolies. Primary impulse in the eleventh–thirteenth centuries is land rivalry among warrior families; towns matter mainly as indirect instruments of princely expansion (finance, human resources).

**Monopoly mechanism.** Free competition among many units → elimination contests → restricted competition under monopoly order. “Whoever does not rise, falls back.” Dual monopoly of **taxes** and **instruments of physical force** forms the backbone of the state figuration. Within the monopoly area, no one may use weapons or physical violence without the central ruler’s permission—novel where a whole class once used weapons by means and inclination. Taxation is not invented by a single planner; it arises from interweaving (parallelogram of forces) as feudal *aides* become permanent under war pressure. Military monopoly and tax monopoly reciprocally strengthen each other (e.g. Charles VII after English expulsion).

**Royal mechanism.** Optimal absolutist power when contending blocs (nobility and bourgeoisie; earlier king and Church) are relatively even and highly ambivalent: each needs the centre against the other; the king’s interests are never identical with any single class. Estates assemblies work when compromise is possible; deadlock raises central power. France vs England illustrates different estate balances.

**Courtization.** Absolutist court has a double face: instrument for **controlling** the nobility and means of **sustaining** it. Free knightly life ends; nobles fight as officers in state organization. Versailles: forced presence, surveillance, “I do not know him” as disgrace—court as place of detention for the great. Restricted competition for offices and favours distributed by the money-owning monopolist replaces free warrior competition. Louis XIV keeps family from independent power (apanage lesson).

### Part Four — Theory of the link

Differentiation under competition lengthens chains of interdependence; more people must attune conduct; self-regulation becomes automatic self-compulsion. Traffic analogy: modern city danger is loss of self-control, not primarily armed attack. Super-ego in warrior societies is more diffuse and violence-linked; civilizing produces more even, dispassionate, all-round self-control. National “character” preserves history of nobility–bourgeois struggles; stable super-ego needs security and living standard—classes in permanent starvation/kill danger can hardly maintain civilized restraints (Elias’s own scope condition).

**Conclusion vector.** Interstate monopoly competition continues at a higher scale; civilization is “not yet finished”; human-made fears can be better known and perhaps better steered if unplanned dynamics are understood.

### Postscript 1968 — Reception and program restatement

Elias finds the book still pioneering; critiques Parsons’s pattern variables for reducing process to static dichotomies (affectivity vs affective neutrality) and homeostatic equilibrium models that treat change as malfunction. Twentieth-century sociology, he argues, threw out long-term development with the bathwater of progress ideology, replacing it with Eleatic nation-as-eternal-system ideals. *Homo clausus* blocks understanding; open personality + figuration restore process thought. Dance model teaches mobile interdependence without reifying system above people.




## Conceptual mechanics in finer grain

### Chains of interdependence

Elias’s central structural variable is the *length and density* of interdependence chains. In warrior societies with short chains, the individual can give freer rein to spontaneous affects because survival depends less on continuous attunement to distant unknown others; the cost is exposure to others’ spontaneous violence. As functions differentiate under competition, each person depends on more others for everyday existence; the web of actions must be coordinated more strictly; foresight (connecting past and future, cause and effect) becomes a survival skill. This is not moral improvement rhetoric: it is a claim about selection pressures inside figurations. The same pressure that stabilizes self-constraint also produces new anxieties—fear of social degradation, of faux pas, of the “horizontal fissure” between drive and control.

### External constraint → self-constraint

The conversion thesis is historical, not ontological. External constraint (*Fremdzwang*) never disappears: police, law, and the barracks monopoly remain. What changes is the *balance* and the *automaticity* of control. Childhood training installs a relay-station of social standards so that much restraint is habitual and even unconscious. Elias uses Freudian vocabulary (super-ego, drives) while historicizing it: the agency of conscience is not a timeless structure but a product of specific social monopolies and interdependence forms. The “battlefield moved within” formula captures the psychogenetic cost of pacification.

### Monopoly mechanism vs Weberian definition

Weber defines the state by the successful *claim* to monopoly of legitimate force. Elias accepts the definitional point and asks for the *sociogenesis* of that monopoly: free competition among warriors, elimination contests, dual fiscal-military concentration, private monopoly becoming public. The mechanism is general in form (Elias draws functional analogies to economic competition) but historically specified in European cases. Failure modes—stalemate, apanage fragmentation, estate resistance—are part of the model, not accidents outside it.

### Royal mechanism and ambivalence

The royal mechanism depends on *ambivalence of interests* with lengthening chains: each major bloc needs the centre enough that it cannot destroy the centre without empowering its rival. Absolutism peaks when a weakened nobility balances a rising bourgeoisie. This is figurational power analysis, not a cult of royal genius—though personal capacities matter more in free-competition phases.

### Courtization as remoulding of habitus

Courtization is the micro-political school of civilization for the warrior upper class: continuous observation, exact affect regulation, knowledge of the human terrain, prestige competition without free feud. Elias’s court is both golden cage and workshop of the civilized personality. Later bourgeois codes partly inherit and transform this inheritance; national codes fuse aristocratic and middle-class features differently (England vs United States in Elias’s sketch).

### Anti-linearization

“Spurts and counter-spurts,” diminishing contrasts with increasing varieties, and the insistence that lower strata under direct physical constraint do not automatically acquire stable self-restraints—all block a simple ladder of progress. Colonial “civilizing” is framed as structural incorporation into Western-centred interdependence networks, not as gift of technology. Critics still charge that the residual directional language does progressive work; the debate page records both sides.

## Reflexivity of the program

The 1939 publication date is not incidental. Elias writes a theory of pacification and self-constraint while European monopolies of force prepare industrial slaughter. He neither eulogizes nor simply condemns civilization; he wants more conscious control based on knowledge of unplanned dynamics. The German-Jewish exile position, the delayed Anglophone reception, and the 1968 restatement against both progress ideology and equilibrium nationalism are part of the program’s production context. A Theory Hub analysis that ignored that context would misread both the polemical energy of Part One and the Postscript’s sociology of sociology.




## Worked examples from the manners series (program exemplars)

**Fork and knife.** Elias does not treat the fork as a simple efficiency invention. Resistance and scandal around early fork use, and later naturalization of fork-and-individual-plate dining, illustrate how a technical object becomes a vehicle of revulsion standards: eating with hands or from common dishes becomes “disgusting” only after a new standard is socially installed. Knife taboos (not pointing, not licking, restricted gestures) show weapons-adjacent utensils being civilizationally disarmed at table—parallel to the larger confinement of free violence.

**Spitting and nose-blowing.** Series move from relatively open practices to handkerchief prestige objects to internalized disgust and medical rationalization. Elias stresses that hygienic reasons often appear *late* as rationalizations of thresholds already moved by social delicacy and class distinction. This is methodologically important: actors’ stated reasons are not the full sociogenesis.

**Bedroom and sexual shame.** Privatization of sleep and nakedness, and advancing frontiers of sexual speech and conduct, exemplify the “behind the scenes” movement of animalic functions. The adult–child gap widens: children must be taught advanced standards quickly, making socialization a compressed civilizing process—and a site of pain and “scars” (Part Four).

**Aggressiveness.** Upper-class pleasure in open cruelty and feud declines as the monopoly removes free outlets; war and state violence are reorganized, not eliminated. This finding is the pivot of the violence-anomaly debate: critics point to industrial slaughter; the program distinguishes internal pacification under monopoly from interstate and breakdown violence.

## Program research agenda (what a follower does)

1. Identify a figuration and its power balances and interdependence chains.
2. Track standards of conduct and thresholds of shame/repugnance over time on fixed occasions.
3. Link changes in those standards to monopolies, markets, or other integration mechanisms.
4. Watch for spurts, counter-spurts, and decivilizing breakdowns.
5. Avoid *homo clausus* explanations that start from the closed adult individual.
6. Keep “civilization” technical; state scope conditions (region, class, gender).

This agenda is why the civilizing process counts as a **research program**, not a one-book aperçu: it organizes empirical work across domains (sport, colonial encounters, organizational life, genocide studies, diplomacy) even when practitioners revise Elias’s European narrative.


## What Survived

Even where scholars reject the full teleology charge or the Western narrative arc, the following live on widely:

- **Figuration / interdependence** as anti-atomistic unit of analysis.
- **Process over state** in historical sociology of the state and the body.
- **Shame and manners** as sociological objects tied to power and monopoly.
- **Self-constraint / second nature** as historically variable, not timeless character.
- **Court society** as a type for studying elite observation, etiquette, and dependence.
- **Monopoly mechanism** language in discussions of European state formation (alongside Tilly and others).
- Critique of *homo clausus* as a methodological warning still usable against survey psychologism.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Active figurational and process-sociology research; applications in sport, violence, organizations, international relations, and colonial studies; ongoing debate pages and restudies. Frontiers (for later ingests): full *Court Society*; established–outsiders model; systematic decivilizing-process literature; non-European cases; gendered re-readings of the manners series.
