## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[durkheim-emile|Émile Durkheim]]'s **social realism** is the research program that professionalized French academic sociology by securing for it an object and a method: [[social-fact|social facts]] as realities *sui generis*, to be [[treat-social-facts-as-things|treated as things]] and explained by other social facts. The program crystallizes between the doctoral thesis *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor in Society]]* (1893), the method manifesto *[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|The Rules of Sociological Method]]* (1895; 2nd ed. 1901), and the moral-statistical demonstration *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* (1897). *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-1912|The Elementary Forms of Religious Life]]* (1912) later radicalizes the representational and religious side of the same ontology.

What did it react against? Three rivals dominate the polemical field of the *Rules*:

1. **Utilitarian and contractualist social theory** — [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], classical political economy, and any account that founds society on individual interest or free contract. *Division of Labor* Book I ch. 7 and the concept of [[non-contractual-elements-of-contract]] are the program's first major empirical-theoretical blow (see [[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]]).
2. **Psychological reduction** — explaining collective patterns by individual motives, imitation ([[unknown|Gabriel Tarde]]), race, or an innate progress instinct. *Rules* Ch. V states the hard rule: every time a social phenomenon is *directly* explained by a psychological phenomenon, the explanation is false (attributed).
3. **Encyclopedic system-building and teleological philosophy of history** — Comtean single-Humanity narratives and any sociology that elaborates prenotions (*idola*) instead of observing things. Durkheim keeps Comte's demand for a positive science of society ([[positivism]]) while rejecting Comte's method as *substitute* for comparative demonstration.

In a 1907 letter replying to Simon Deploige, Durkheim self-reported the genealogy of what Deploige called his "social realism": it came from **Comte, Spencer, and Espinas**, not Schaeffle; the maxim that each science explains its own principles came from **Boutroux** (and Aristotle via Boutroux) plus Comtean level-irreducibility; the 1895 lecture course under the influence of **Robertson Smith** was a "watershed" that forced him to restart prior research around religion's capital role (attributed self-report; Lukes volume). That self-report matters historiographically: social realism is not a one-book invention but a program whose method book and whose religious turn are nearly simultaneous (1895).

Institutionally the program rides the Third Republic's demand for a secular civic science of morals — Bordeaux chair, Sorbonne, *Année sociologique* (from 1898). The 1901 Preface of the *Rules* credits the *Année* with making palpable what an "objective, specific and methodical" sociology could be.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Social facts as the object of sociology

*Rules* Chapter I refuses the loose use of "social" for everything that interests society. Eating and sleeping are not social facts in the technical sense. Social facts are **ways of acting, thinking, and feeling external to the individual and invested with coercive power**. Closing definition (Halls/Lukes):

> A social fact is any way of acting, whether fixed or not, capable of exerting over the individual an external constraint; or, which is possible everywhere it is general over the whole of a given society whilst having an existence of its own, independent of its individual manifestations.

Analysis of the words: **exteriority** blocks reduction to private states; **constraint** is the operational mark of that exteriority (sanctions, ridicule, practical impossibility, market ruin). Generality-with-independence is offered as an equivalent formulation: what is general because it presses on individuals, not because it is a mere sum of private acts. Morphological "ways of being" — settlement patterns, political partitions, communication networks — are included as consolidated ways of acting ([[social-morphology]]). Collective **currents** (opinion, suicidogenetic force) count even when not crystallized as rules.

### 2. Treat social facts as things

Chapter II's first and most basic rule:

> The first and most basic rule is to consider social facts as things.

This is an **epistemic attitude**, not a claim that society is a rock. Prenotions — Baconian *praenotiones* / *idola* — stand between the observer and the object; ideology elaborates ideas about law, morality, and the state as if they were the things themselves. The rule demands: proceed from things to ideas; define by external characters; prefer solid, crystallized data (codes, statistics, fixed morphology). The 1901 Preface insists social life is made of **representations** and that "consciousness" names no substance — answering reification charges without surrendering the rule (attributed).

### 3. Association produces a *sui generis* reality

Chapter V's anti-reduction argument uses the living cell: association of elements yields properties not found in the elements taken separately. Society is not the sum of individuals; combination produces a psychical individuality of a **new kind**. Hence the rule: the determining cause of a social fact must be sought among **antecedent social facts**, not among states of individual consciousness. Individuals remain the necessary substratum; they are not a sufficient explanation.

### 4. Cause and function are distinct

Against teleological sociology (purpose as cause), Durkheim holds that the organ is independent of its function: the same institution can change functions without changing nature; facts can survive after utility is lost. Efficient cause and function must be investigated **separately**; function is sought in relation to a **social end**. Needs and desires can hasten or retard development only by acting through efficient causes — they never create from nothing (attributed).

### 5. Normal and pathological relative to type

Chapter III makes the average type of a [[social-types|social species]], at a given developmental phase, the criterion of **health**. Crime is **normal** because no society is free of it and because a crime-free society would require impossible moral homogeneity; crime can even serve moral evolution. Pathological is often a matter of **intensity** (e.g. modern suicide rates), not presence. This is the program's most politically sensitive claim and the one the First Preface anticipates defending.

### 6. Social types via morphology

Chapter IV places **social species** between historians' nominalism (each people unique) and philosophers' monism (one Humanity). Classification proceeds by morphological structure — from the undivided **horde** through compound and polysegmentary forms — not by ideals or civilization-stage labels alone.

### 7. Proof by comparative concomitant variations

Chapter VI: artificial experiment is rarely available; **indirect experiment = comparative method**. Among Mill's canons, **concomitant variations** is the chief sociological instrument. Comparison needs series and interpretation (spurious concomitance is possible). Scopes: within one society, among societies of one species, across species. Comte's special "historical method" is rejected as a *replacement* for comparison. Conclusion: sociology is an autonomous science requiring a specifically sociological culture.

### Conceptual map of the program

```
social facts (exterior + constraint; + morphology)
        │
        ▼
treat as things (discard prenotions; external definition)
        │
        ├── normal / pathological  ←── requires ──→  social types (morphology)
        │
        ├── explain: cause among social facts (inner environment: volume, density)
        │            function relative to social end (cause ≠ function)
        │
        └── prove: comparative method (concomitant variations)
```

The map makes visible why *Rules* is short yet structural: each chapter supplies a necessary hinge. Without types, normality is undefined; without exterior facts, comparison has no object; without separation of cause and function, teleology returns; without comparative proof, the program is only a philosophy of society.

### Rationalism, practice, and the First Preface

Durkheim accepts the label **rationalist**: extend scientific rationalism to human behaviour by showing it reducible to cause-and-effect relations that can be transformed into rules of action for the future. What others call his positivism is, he says, a consequence of that rationalism — not Comte’s or Spencer’s positive *metaphysics*. The First Preface also insists the method is “essentially conservative” in one sense (social facts are not modifiable at will by dialectic artifice) while defending the scientific treatment of crime as normal against the charge of justifying crime. The practical orientation matters for reading *Suicide*’s occupational-group remedy and for later charges that the program smuggles reformist or Third-Republic politics into “health.”

## The Empirical Program

### Empirical sequence across the three monographs

Read as a single research program rather than three free-standing books: *Division of Labor* discovers solidarity types and morphological causation while already practicing external indices; *Rules* freezes the method so that others can repeat and contest it; *Suicide* shows that a limited problem (the rate) can carry the full apparatus of definition, elimination of extra-social causes, typology, and practical implication. *Elementary Forms* then answers the 1895 watershed by making religion the laboratory of collective representations and of the categories of the understanding — without cancelling morphology or the social-fact rule. The program's power is this **method–monograph–application–deepening** cycle; its risk is that later readers take only one book (usually *Suicide* or *Elementary Forms*) and lose the cycle.


What the program explained that predecessors could not (attributed successes):

- **Solidarity without contract** — organic solidarity and non-contractual bases of contract (*Division of Labor*).
- **Suicide as social** — stable rates varying with integration and regulation, not reducible to individual pathology or imitation (*Suicide*).
- **Crime as normal** — shifts pathology talk from presence to type-relative intensity (*Rules*; *Suicide*).
- **Morphological causation** — density/volume as levers of structural change.

Where it failed or accumulated anomalies:

- **Repressive→restitutive legal evolution** as general law — later legal anthropology rejects the simple sequence.
- **Evolutionary ranking of social types** and "lower societies" language — period ethnography and hierarchy.
- **Ecological inference** from rates to individuals — standing statistical critique of *Suicide*.
- **Strong constraint criterion** — not all collective patterns feel coercive; later cultural sociology softens.
- **Economic monocausality rejected, but economic under-theorized** — Labriola review accepts "causes escaping consciousness" yet denies economic foundation as sole key; leaves economic sociology thin relative to Marx.

## Method Commitments

1. **Objectivity as exteriority** — [[treat-social-facts-as-things]].
2. **Definition before explanation** — external characters first.
3. **Type-relative diagnosis** — [[normal-and-pathological]] only after [[social-types]].
4. **Morphological priority in causal search** — inner social environment ([[social-morphology]], [[dynamic-density]]).
5. **Comparative demonstration** — [[comparative-method]], concomitant variations; series not anecdotes.
6. **Auxiliary sciences** — history, ethnography, statistics as instruments, not substitutes for sociological explanation (*Suicide* Preface; subsequent 1903 essay on sociology and the social sciences).
7. **Autonomy of sociology** — not an appendix of psychology, biology, or economics; yet "all sociology is a psychology *sui generis*" (later clarifications).

### Verbatim architecture of observation (Ch. II)

The anti-prenotion argument is not a throwaway Bacon citation. Durkheim claims that reflective thought precedes science and that practical notions about social life are especially seductive because social things are products of human activity and therefore *look like* mere applications of ideas we carry within us. The result is ideology: science proceeds from ideas to things, facts intervene only as examples, and the discipline collapses into an art of prescriptions. Against this he sets three corollaries of treating facts as things (summary of Ch. II structure, attributed): systematically discard all preconceptions; define the group of phenomena by external characteristics common to them; investigate from outside, preferably through the most objective (crystallized) data. The practical upshot for research design is concrete: prefer legal codes, rates, and morphological measures over the sociologist’s sense of what justice or the family “really is.”

### Verbatim architecture of explanation (Ch. V)

Two paired rules close the explanatory chapter:

> The determining cause of a social fact must be sought among antecedent social facts and not among the states of the individual consciousness. … The function of a social fact must always be sought in the relationship that it bears to some social end.

Between them sits the association argument: individuals associated “give birth to a being, psychical if you will, but a psychical individuality of a new kind.” The inner social environment — especially **volume** and **dynamic density** — is the principal region in which to search for those antecedent social causes. Secondary environments (family, occupation, etc.) matter but are subordinate to the general environment of the society. Race and individual constitution are demoted from master keys to, at most, conditions that must themselves be socially mediated.

### Verbatim architecture of proof (Ch. VI)

> We have only one way of demonstrating that one phenomenon is the cause of another. This is to compare the cases where they are both simultaneously present or absent… When… the production of facts is something beyond our power to command… the method used is one of indirect experimentation, or the comparative method.

Durkheim ranks Mill’s canons for sociological use: residues, agreement, and difference are weak given social complexity; **concomitant variations** can prove with fewer cases if the co-variation is interpreted. He rejects the doctrine of a pure plurality of causes that would make the same effect spring indifferently from unrelated antecedents — a doctrine he thinks would make scientific comparison impossible. Comparative sociology, he concludes, *is* sociology itself.

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **[[methodological-individualism-debate]]** — the standing formalization of social-realism vs. individualist explanation.
- **[[structure-vs-agency-debate]]** — constraint and exteriority vs. making history (Marxian constrained agency is a different solution to a related problem).
- **[[durkheim-spencer-contract-debate]]** — non-contractual social conditions vs. industrial-contractual society.
- **[[weber-interpretive-sociology-debate]]** / [[interpretive-sociology]] — subjectively meaningful action vs. exterior things as entry point.
- **[[formal-sociology]]** (Simmel) — forms of sociation vs. social facts as things; Durkheim reviewed Simmel as social philosophy bordering on illegitimate speculation (period reception).
- **Tarde** — imitation vs. *sui generis* collective forces.
- **Seignobos (1908)** — whether historical facts are comparable; documents and participant motives vs. institutional comparison.
- **Marxism (Labriola review)** — shared hostility to idealist history; dispute over economic primacy and religion's primitiveness.

## Critiques and Responses

**Reification.** Critics charge a hypostatized Society or group mind. Durkheim's 1901 and letter-form responses: no substantial soul; social life is representations; *sui generis* means synthesis by association, not a ghostly substance. Whether this fully answers the charge remains a **position**, not a wiki verdict.

**Anti-psychology.** Misreaders hear elimination of mind. Reply: social facts are mental *sui generis*; psychology is necessary preparation but not the focal explanation.

**Authoritarian implication of "constraint."** Softened by the claim that constraint is often unfelt when conformity is willing, and that the rule is methodological indication of exteriority.

**Conservative reading of normality.** Declaring crime normal and the method "essentially conservative" (First Preface) can sound like apology for the status quo; Durkheim's reformism (*Suicide*'s occupational groups; moral individualism) complicates that reading without cancelling the tension.

**Ethnographic and statistical base.** Second-hand ethnography and 19th-c. moral statistics are period instruments; later science revises numbers and rejects racialist asides. Findings that depend on those bases must stay attributed and dated.

## What Survived

Even where strong social ontology is qualified, large parts of the program live on:

- **Disciplinary identity** — sociology as study of an order not identical to psychology or biology.
- **"Social facts" vocabulary** — often loosened to "social construction" or "institutions," but the exteriority intuition remains.
- **Function vs. cause** — absorbed into functional analysis and then into critiques of functionalism; the *separation* remains useful.
- **Anomie, solidarity, integration/regulation** — permanent teaching concepts (from *Division*/*Suicide*).
- **Comparative covariation** — ancestor of quantitative comparative sociology (design standards raised).
- **Morphology → densification/urbanization** — variables without the word "morphology."
- **Collective representations / categories** — bridge to cultural sociology and sociology of knowledge (*Elementary Forms*).
- **Professional form** — limited-problem monographs + journal (*Année*) as model of cumulative science vs. system philosophy.

What largely did **not** survive intact: the evolutionary species ladder as moral ranking; crime-as-normal as a popular slogan; the claim that psychology can never directly explain any social phenomenon (contemporary multilevel models allow micro foundations under macro constraints).

What survived in **disguised form** is as important as what kept Durkheim’s labels. Mid-century American structural-functionalism (Parsons, Merton) took function/cause separation and system needs while often dropping exterior “thing” rhetoric and the hard anti-psychology rule. British social anthropology (Radcliffe-Brown) took social structure and function with a Durkheimian debt explicit in places. Late-twentieth-century cultural sociology reopened collective representations without always accepting morphological primacy. Critical theory’s interest in social pathology (Honneth and others — secondary to this wiki’s primary base) reactivates the normal/pathological problem on different grounds. In each case the program’s **problems** outlived its **package**.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active` as **ancestor program**. Few researchers call themselves "social realists" in Durkheim's sense; many work inside problems he posed: emergence and multilevel causation; institutions as constraints; measurement of integration and regulation; cultural codes as collective representations; the politics of "pathology" and critique.

Open frontiers visible from the ingested primaries (not wiki verdicts): how to combine exterior constraint with interpretive access to meaning; how to identify causes beyond concomitant variation; how far religion remains the matrix of categories; how occupational and secondary groups map onto contemporary precarious labor; how the normal/pathological distinction relates to critical theory without smuggling ideals.

### Lukes’s live questions (secondary map, 2013)

Steven Lukes’s Introduction (attributed secondary) lists questions the *Rules* still poses without settled answers: methodological individualism vs. emergence; objectivity’s kind and degree; operationalization of theoretical concepts; whether “normal/pathological” belongs in social science and how it relates to critique; relevance of biological evolution to social evolution; construction of typologies; causation and confirmation/refutation in social science; how far to endorse the Marxist-sounding claim that social life must be explained by profound causes escaping participants’ consciousness. This wiki records those as **open research frontiers**, not as verdicts.
