# Ethnography of Speaking — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[ethnography-of-speaking]]. Built primarily from *[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations in Sociolinguistics]]* (1974). All programmatic claims in **attributed voice**.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[hymes-dell|Dell Hymes]] named the **ethnography of speaking** in 1962 and broadened the frame to **ethnography of communication** in the 1964 Gumperz & Hymes AAA collection. The Critical-Turn context was dual: (1) American structural linguistics had achieved autonomy of form at the cost of sociological blindness; (2) Chomskyan generative grammar had revitalized "competence" and "creativity" while defining theory for an ideal speaker-hearer in a perfectly homogeneous community, free from limitations of actual use.

Hymes's reaction is not anti-formal. He grants that structural and generative advances were historically necessary. His claim is that their methodological canons, when universalized, **close** the study of social realities of language. In *Foundations* he adapts Sapir's 1938 "Why cultural anthropology needs the psychiatrist" move into "Why linguistics needs the sociologist," and quotes Sapir's 1929 hope that linguists will not "stand aloof behind a tradition that threatens to become scholastic when not vitalized by interests which lie beyond the formal interest in language itself."

Intellectual debts he names: [[sapir-edward|Sapir]], Jakobson (functions and components of the speech event), Burke (dramatism; action vs motion; multi-function language), Cassirer, Marx (fetishism analogy for language abstracted from social roots), and Chomsky (critical engagement). Special institutional debt to [[goffman-erving|Goffman]], the Center for Urban Ethnography, and the SSRC Committee on Sociolinguistics. Roots also run through Boas, Prague and Firthian process linguistics, and Malinowskian context of situation.

What the program **reacted against**: (a) correlational "language and society" that leaves both departments unchanged; (b) semiotic/pragmatic programs that stay too abstract to force ethnography; (c) theory-first strategies that postpone description of communities' ways of speaking; (d) the equation of speech community with "a language."

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Three themes of *Foundations*

Hymes states three interconnected themes as fundamental to sociolinguistics:

1. There is a **mode of organization of language** that is part of the organization of **communicative conduct** in a community, requiring a corresponding new mode of description.
2. Recognition of that mode makes the study of language **multidisciplinary** — linguistics indispensable, but so are sociology, social anthropology, education, folklore, and poetics.
3. Study of that organization forces reconsideration of the **bases of linguistics itself** — ultimately, reconstruction of linguistics as sociolinguistics.

### Frame: ethnography and communication, not linguistics and language

> "It is not that linguistics does not have a vital role. … It is rather that it is not linguistics, but ethnography, not language, but communication, which must provide the frame of reference within which the place of language in culture and society is to be assessed."

The term "ethnography of communication" is intended to indicate necessary **scope** (patterned complexity of communicative conduct) and **basis** (ethnographic investigation of a community or network). Fresh data are needed on language in contexts of situation; patterns proper to speech activity escape separate studies of grammar, personality, social structure, and religion.

### Units

| Unit | Definition (Hymes) |
|---|---|
| [[speech-community]] | Community sharing knowledge of rules for the conduct and interpretation of speech (form + patterns of use) |
| Speech situation | Contexts in which speech may occur (not mere activity labels) |
| [[speech-event]] | Activities governed by rules/norms for use of speech |
| Speech act | Minimal unit; not identical with sentence form |
| Ways of speaking / speech economy | Primitive terms for repertoire organization |

### SPEAKING architecture

The [[speaking-model|SPEAKING]] mnemonic groups a larger etic inventory: Setting/Scene, Participants, Ends, Act sequence, Key, Instrumentalities, Norms, Genre. Hymes insists the mnemonic "has nothing to do with the form of an eventual model and theory." Rules are **relations among components**; which components define events differs by society. French **PARLANT** regrouping is offered to reduce ethnocentrism of the English acronym.

### Communicative competence

Against Chomsky's reduction of competence to systemic grammatical knowledge of an ideal speaker-hearer, Hymes distinguishes four aspects:

- **(a) systemic potential** — what the formal system allows (Chomsky's focus)
- **(b) appropriateness** — suitability/effectiveness in context
- **(c) occurrence** — whether and how far something is done
- **(d) feasibility** — practicability given means and processing

Creativity requires appropriateness rules; silence and "should not speak" are competence, not mere performance deficit. Performance is to be reclaimed as **accomplishment** (genre, style, responsibility), not residue.

### Socially constituted linguistics

Hymes distinguishes three orientations of work called sociolinguistic:

1. **The social as well as the linguistic** — applied/social-problem work that does not challenge existing linguistics.
2. **Socially realistic linguistics** — community data challenging linguistics on "variation" and "validity" (Labov as exemplar).
3. **Socially constituted linguistics** — the radical program: social function gives form to how linguistic features are encountered; begin with social functions; reverse structuralist isolation of referential structure.

The third orientation is the program's theoretical core. Its explanatory adequacy is **complementary** to Chomsky's: not from speech toward innate universals alone, but from common human means toward what particular communities have made of them.

### Seven slogans

If sociolinguistics is identified with socially constituted linguistics, Hymes offers seven themes: theory of language entails organization of speech not just grammar; foundations entail function not just structure; speech communities are organizations of ways of speaking; competence is personal ability not just grammatical knowledge; performance is accomplishment not just processing; languages are what users have made of them; liberté, égalité, fraternité of speech are social achievements, not postulates from "Language."

### Self-liquidation

> "The final goal of sociolinguistics, I think, must be to preside over its own liquidation."

Hybrid names mark disciplinary gaps. Success would make social meaning ordinary in linguistics (as semantics once migrated), not a permanent border specialty.

## The Empirical Program

*Foundations* is deliberately idea-heavy. Hymes hopes there is "room in the study of language for both" ideas and data. The empirical demand is programmatic:

- Describe a community's sociolinguistic system as one describes a language or culture.
- Prefer description and taxonomy now over premature general theory ("toward toward a theory").
- Use juxtaposition of ethnographies to show that communities differ in ways of speaking (valuation of speech, quantity, silence, gender scenes, music–speech configurations).
- Formalize rules of co-occurrence and alternation (Ervin-Tripp) for comparison.
- Note HRAF-era neglect of speaking as a systematic topic; build etics of speaking.

Illustrations (heuristic, not findings of a single study): Wishram networks and scenes; Paliyan silence; Chinookan speech/silence; Gbeya/Anang/Araucanian speech valuation; bilingual classrooms; folklore performance events; pidgin/creole processes generalized as function change.

**Hits:** Created durable descriptive vocabulary; licensed generations of speech-event ethnographies and classroom studies; linked folklore performance to linguistic theory.

**Misses / anomalies:** Large-scale variationist quantification developed partly on a separate track (Labov's "socially realistic" path); conversation analysis developed its own units; the self-liquidation of the sociolinguistics label did not occur; SPEAKING often checklist-ized in teaching.

## Method Commitments

See [[ethnography-of-communication]]. Commitments in brief:

- Community/network as frame, not pre-given language.
- Keep message form and context together.
- Nested situation/event/act analysis.
- SPEAKING as heuristic relations among components.
- Emic validation under etic grids.
- Multidisciplinary dependence (not amalgamation without recast).

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **[[communicative-competence-debate]]** — Hymes vs Chomsky on the object of linguistic theory.
- **Correlation vs integrated description** — Hymes rejects gross language–society correlation as missing "structure as organization of diversity."
- **New discipline vs recast** — prefers merger of recast linguistics and ethnography over a permanent third field.
- **Cartesian vs Herderian isolation of language** — both, Hymes argues, isolate "a language"; sociolinguistics responds by shifting to persons and ways of speaking (Ch. 5).
- **Performance** — folklore/ethnography's positive performance vs Chomsky's residual performance.

## Architecture detail: from components to functions

Hymes's Ch. 1 four-aspect framework ties components of events, relations among components, capacity/state of components, and activity of the whole. Jakobson's component inventory is a starting point, not a closed list. Message form must stay with context — Bloomfieldian abstraction from situation is a methodological error for this program. **Functional relativity** of languages (differential roles of speech even among monolinguals) is prior to Whorfian grammatical relativity: the Whorf question, Hymes argues, requires asking how language functions in communicative economies, not only how grammar segments experience.

Ch. 2 develops **repertoire** as more general than bilingualism: switching can be of styles, not only languages; communities differ in speech load and in whether phatic communion or silence is valued (Paliyan silence is structural, not mere absence). Bernstein's elaborated/restricted dimensions are generalized as taxonomic axes that may sort separately cross-culturally — not only as British class codes. Mead's speech-type sketches (Arapesh personal, Iatmul positional, etc.) become cross-cutting dimensions, not closed types.

Ch. 3–4 push the institutional argument: American structuralism assumed uniform language/community without methods for testing full-range use (Bloomfield's White-Thunder sketch as caution). The same formal system can enter different sociolinguistic systems; potential equality of languages differs from actual inequality of communicative resources. Century trajectory of description (phonology → morphology → syntax → semantics → sociolinguistic description) places ethnography of speaking as next frontier. Structure–freedom arc: Saussure at the word, Chomsky at the sentence, ethnography of speaking at the **speech act**. Social anthropology reciprocally needs linguistic content (gossip, address, interrogative behavior, kinship as speaking problems).

Ch. 5–7 apply the program: bilingual education must start from speech event, repertoire, and use-defined community; Cartesian and Herderian traditions both isolate "a language." Folklore contributes performance and genre; five challenges to Chomsky from folklore (performance as positive competence; differentiated knowledge; creativity as appropriateness/familiarity as well as novelty; start from acts/genres; continuum of stylization). Poetics via Burke: symbolic competence beyond technical grammar; performance as accomplishment.

Ch. 8–10 reverse the external critique: foundations of linguistic theory are equally in social life and ethnography; referential and stylistic functions are elementary; grammar that explains rare jumbles but not common mistakes is incomplete; acceptability has a social dimension; context-sensitive grammar requires prior social analysis; "key" (mock vs face-value) and reciprocal vs reflexive role construal show conversation is needed for syntax/semantics. Object of study becomes **means of speech**, not "a language." Three unities of language study: origin in the past; structure in the present; unity in the future under emerging world society.

## What Survived

1. **[[communicative-competence]]** — durable keyword in linguistic anthropology, applied linguistics, and education (often thinned pedagogically).
2. **Speech-event analysis** and **SPEAKING** teaching heuristic — still standard in linguistic anthropology courses.
3. **Speech community** as rule-sharing unit — contested but central; resists pure mutual-intelligibility definitions.
4. **Interactional and educational ethnography** of classrooms and institutions (Philips lineage and after).
5. **Performance** bridge between folklore and linguistic anthropology.
6. **Critique of autonomous linguistics** as cultural common sense in linguistic anthropology even where generative work continues elsewhere.
7. Vocabulary of **ways of speaking**, **repertoire**, **key**, **genre** as cross-field tools.
8. **Rules of co-occurrence and alternation** (via Ervin-Tripp) as bridge between ethnography and formalization.
9. Permission structure for treating **silence**, **channel**, and **non-referential** functions as first-class data.

What did **not** fully survive as Hymes hoped: liquidation of the "sociolinguistics" hybrid into ordinary linguistics; unification of Labovian, ethnographic, and CA tracks under one socially constituted theory; universal adoption of formal sociolinguistic description as the standard mode of grammar-writing; the "socially constituted linguistics" brand as a department-level identity.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active` as research program vocabulary and method, partly **absorbed** into linguistic anthropology's default toolkit. Frontiers that continue Hymes's problems without always using his labels:

- **Interactional sociolinguistics** and discourse analysis of institutional talk
- **Language socialization** (acquisition of communicative competence as cultural process)
- **Raciolinguistics** and unequal competence opportunities (his liberation stake, re-theorized)
- **Multimodal communication** (channels beyond speech — his "communication" scope)
- **Decolonizing language documentation** (community frames vs salvage grammar alone)
- **Digital speech communities** and mediated publics (strain local-unit assumptions)
- **Performance studies** after the Bauman–Hymes bridge
- Interface work between **formal pragmatics** and ethnography (local convergences without full synthesis)

The standing test Hymes set remains: can one describe a community's ways of speaking with the same seriousness that one describes its grammar — and can linguistics accept that description as internal to its foundations?

## Reflexivity (program production)

The ethnography of speaking is a product of U.S. Critical-Turn anthropology and linguistics: SSRC committee infrastructure, urban ethnography centers, AAA publication vehicles, and a polemic against generative hegemony in departments. It carries Boasian Amerindian philology forward while criticizing scholastic formalism. Applied stakes (bilingual education, classroom equity) are not afterthoughts — Hymes treats scientific and social concerns as convergent. Later pedagogical capture of communicative competence in testing regimes is a reception irony: a concept built against ideal-speaker standards can be re-standardized. The program's comparative illustrations often draw on salvage-era and colonial-era ethnographies; the method's ethics inherit those archives even when the theory is emancipatory in intent.
