## Intellectual Origins and Context

Harold Garfinkel presented *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967) as the product of twelve years of work whose "inexhaustible directives" he attributed to two decades spent with the writings of [[parsons-talcott]], [[schutz-alfred]], Aron Gurwitsch, and Edmund Husserl. Garfinkel was explicit that this was not a repudiation of Parsons: he called Parsons's work "awesome for the penetrating depth and unfailing precision of its practical sociological reasoning on the constituent tasks of the problem of social order and its solutions." Ethnomethodology begins, on Garfinkel's own account, inside the Parsonian "problem of order," not outside it — the question of how social order is possible at all, the question that organizes [[structural-functionalism]] from Parsons's *The Structure of Social Action* onward. What Garfinkel changed was not the question but the site at which its answer was sought.

That relocation came from [[schutz-alfred]]'s phenomenology of the natural attitude — the "world known in common and taken for granted," the "attitude of daily life" as a domain with its own presuppositions, distinct from and irreducible to the "attitude of scientific theorizing." Garfinkel adapted Schutz's descriptions of background expectancies, the documentary method (itself borrowed by Schutz's tradition from Karl Mannheim), and the phenomenological analysis of indexical expression, and turned them from resources for describing lay cognition into a research program for empirical sociology. The debt to Husserl and Gurwitsch is narrower and more technical: it supplies the vocabulary of indexicality (expressions whose sense cannot be fixed without knowing the speaker's biography and the occasion of use) that Garfinkel took from logicians' treatments of "indexical" and "occasional" expressions (Husserl via Marvin Farber's exposition, Bertrand Russell, and Nelson Goodman) and redirected toward ordinary talk.

Ethnomethodology's founding gesture is a explicit substitution for a Durkheimian premise. Where Durkheim's dictum treats "the objective reality of social facts" as sociology's fundamental principle — social facts as things, external and constraining — Garfinkel proposed that "the objective reality of social facts as an ongoing accomplishment of the concerted activities of daily life" is the fundamental phenomenon for a practical sociology, with the "ordinary, artful ways of that accomplishment being by members known, used, and taken for granted." This is a reacted-against relationship in the strict sense used throughout this wiki: ethnomethodology does not deny that social facts appear solid, external, and constraining to the people living among them — it takes that very appearance of solidity as the thing requiring explanation, and locates the explanation in members' unnoticed practical work rather than in an ontology of facticity itself.

The disciplinary moment was the high tide of structural-functionalism and the survey-research consensus of the postwar-expansion era: large-scale, rule-governed, variable-based models of action (Parsons's normative order, quantitative attitude and selection studies, actuarial reading of records) were the dominant idiom of American sociology. Garfinkel's program took shape as sustained, close-range engagement with exactly this idiom's working materials — jury deliberation, psychiatric clinic records, an attitude survey's coding sheets, a "one-price" market norm, a person's claim to a sex status — rather than as an alternative grand theory competing with Parsons's for the same explanatory territory. This is part of why ethnomethodology has always sat awkwardly next to [[symbolic-interactionism]] and [[interpretive-sociology]], both of which it resembles in emphasizing meaning and situated action but from which it differs in refusing to treat members' meanings as data to be reported rather than as accomplishments to be studied as accomplishments.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

Garfinkel's own formal definition, stated in Chapter One: "I use the term 'ethnomethodology' to refer to the investigation of the rational properties of indexical expressions and other practical actions as contingent ongoing accomplishments of organized artful practices of everyday life." Every subsequent concept in the book elaborates one clause of this sentence.

**Accountability.** Garfinkel held that "the activities whereby members produce and manage settings of organized everyday affairs are identical with members' procedures for making those settings 'account-able.'" He defined accountable as "observable-and-reportable, i.e. available to members as situated practices of looking-and-telling," an "endless, ongoing, contingent accomplishment" carried out by parties whose "competence" in that accomplishment — their skill with, knowledge of, and entitlement to the detailed work — is "obstinately depend[ed] upon, recognize[d], use[d], and take[n] for granted" by co-participants. This is a strong identity claim: not that accountability is a byproduct of orderly action, but that producing order and rendering it accountable are the same activity described twice. [[accountability]] is thus not a synonym for "explanation" in the ordinary sense; it names the reflexive, in-the-doing property by which any setting is simultaneously enacted and displayed as a recognizable setting.

**Reflexivity of accounts.** Garfinkel named "the 'reflexive,' or 'incarnate' character of accounting practices and accounts" as the crux of the accountability thesis: members' accounts are "reflexively and essentially tied for their rational features to the socially organized occasions of their use for they are features of the socially organized occasions of their use." He was careful to specify that members are, in a precise technical sense, "not interested" in this reflexivity as a topic — it is not that they fail to notice it through carelessness, but that treating it as an unalterable, unavoidable condition of inquiry rather than an object of inquiry is itself constitutive of practical action. [[reflexivity-of-accounts]] therefore names a structural feature of accounts, not an error correctable by better description.

**Indexicality.** The first of Garfinkel's three "constituent problematic phenomena" of practical action is "the unsatisfied programmatic distinction between and substitutability of objective (context-free) for indexical expressions." He argued that the professional-scientific program of replacing indexical expressions with objective ones "remains programmatic in every particular case" — never actually completed, only ever achieved "for all practical purposes." This is the theoretical hinge of the whole book: if the objective/indexical distinction is itself always only a practical, situated accomplishment, then [[indexicality]] cannot be treated as a removable defect of ordinary language that rigorous method eventually eliminates. Garfinkel's third constituent phenomenon — "the analyzability of actions-in-context as a practical accomplishment" — pushes this further, holding that "no concept of context-in-general exists" and "every use of 'context' without exception is itself essentially indexical."

**Practical action and "for all practical purposes."** Garfinkel's recurring formula — rational-adequacy-for-all-practical-purposes, scientific-for-all-practical-purposes — names the register in which [[practical-action]] achieves whatever adequacy it has: not by rule-compliance or correspondence to an independent state of affairs, but by a socially organized, situated, and endlessly revisable "good enough" standard. His analysis of the Suicide Prevention Center staff conducting "psychological autopsies" illustrates this: investigators, he argued, "make do" with whatever remains are at hand — "whatever is there is good enough in the sense that whatever is there not only will do, but does."

**Membership and the etcetera principle.** Garfinkel used "members" as a technical term for competent parties whose entitled, presupposed knowledge of a setting is a condition of their being able to act intelligibly within it — a member "must at the outset 'know' the settings in which he is to operate if his practices are to serve as measures to bring particular, located features of these settings to recognizable account." Deriving from a reanalysis of a UCLA clinic-coding study, he named the "etcetera principle" cluster — "et cetera," "unless," "let it pass," "factum valet" — coining the term "ad hocing" for the practices by which any set of instructions is fitted to actual particulars: "For convenience let me call these 'ad hoc' considerations, and call their practice 'ad hocing.'" Crucially, Garfinkel argued ad hocing is not a remediable imperfection to be minimized by better-written rules but is "required if the researcher is to grasp the relevance of the instructions to the particular and actual occasions they are intended to analyze" — he explicitly rejected the "prevailing view" that good coding practice should minimize it. [[etcetera-principle]] and [[membership]] are thus jointly load-bearing: competent membership just is knowing how, and when, to invoke the etcetera clause.

**The documentary method of interpretation.** Adapted explicitly from Karl Mannheim's essay "On the Interpretation of Weltanschauung" (in *Essays on the Sociology of Knowledge*), the [[documentary-method-of-interpretation]] names a circular interpretive procedure: "The method consists of treating an actual appearance as 'the document of,' as 'pointing to,' as 'standing on behalf of' a presupposed underlying pattern. Not only is the underlying pattern derived from its individual documentary evidences, but the individual documentary evidences, in their turn, are interpreted on the basis of 'what is known' about the underlying pattern. Each is used to elaborate the other." Garfinkel's most consequential extension of Mannheim was to argue that this method is not confined to lay or "soft" interpretive procedures — it underlies rigorous survey research, questionnaire coding, and even the reading of statistical "results" as sociological "findings": "statistical measurements are most frequently used as indicators, as signs of, as representing or standing on behalf of the intended findings, rather than as literal descriptions of them." This claim — that the documentary method is not a lay deficiency to be professionalized away but the condition of interpretation as such, professional included — is the single most far-reaching theoretical move in the book, since it denies method-based sociology any epistemically privileged escape from the phenomena ethnomethodology studies.

**Background expectancies.** Following Schutz, Garfinkel held that members use "background expectancies as a scheme of interpretation" that renders appearances recognizable as familiar events, while being unable to articulate what those expectancies consist of: "Demonstrably he is responsive to this background... When we ask him about them he has little or nothing to say." He further held that familiar scenes are "moral facts," not merely factual ones: "For members not only are matters so about familiar scenes, but they are so because it is morally right or wrong that they are so." [[background-expectancies]] therefore carries a normative charge from the outset — breaching them is not merely informative but sanctionable.

**The "cultural dope" critique.** Garfinkel's polemical target throughout is what he called the "cultural dope" (and, in psychology, the "psychological dope"): "the man-in-the-sociologist's-society who produces the stable features of the society by acting in compliance with preestablished and legitimate alternatives of action that the common culture provides." He argued that standard sociological models portray members' common-sense rationality as epiphenomenal to rule-following, and that his breaching demonstrations forced members' active, ongoing judgmental work into visibility precisely because breaches presented practical problems that could not be solved by rule-compliance alone. Programmatically, Garfinkel refused to explain the rational properties of members' inquiries "by members' compliance to rules of inquiry," treating logic, methodology, and "adequate demonstration" as themselves "glosses for organizational phenomena" rather than invariant categories imposed from outside — a refusal he stated as an explicit "leading policy."

**Passing and the managed achievement of status.** In the analysis of Agnes (garfinkel-agnes-study), Garfinkel generalized accountability and indexicality to the ascriptive category "normal sexed person" itself, arguing that sex status, ordinarily treated by "normals" as a natural fact fixed by birth, is instead "an ongoing, practically managed achievement" — continuously produced and re-produced through witnessable talk and conduct. His insistence on the active verb form — "it would be incorrect to say of Agnes that she has passed... she is passing" — extends the accountability thesis from settings in general to embodied identity claims in particular.

## The Empirical Program

Ethnomethodology's empirical program consists overwhelmingly of small-N demonstrations designed to make ordinarily invisible background work visible by disrupting it, plus close reanalyses of existing professional and institutional records. What this program explained that Parsonian theory and survey-based sociology could not was the *mechanism* by which normative order appears solid: rather than positing internalized norms as a black box producing conformity, Garfinkel produced repeated, textured evidence that conformity is actively, moment-to-moment accomplished, and that its accomplishment becomes visible exactly at the point where it is disrupted. The bargaining-for-standard-priced-merchandise demonstration is instructive here precisely because it complicated the program's own expectations: Garfinkel had predicted, following [[parsons-talcott]]'s account of the "institutionalized one price rule" as fully internalized, that breaching it would produce shame and anxiety in sales staff. Instead, discomfort was concentrated in students (the breachers) rather than salespersons, was heavier in the single-trial than the six-trial condition, and declined with repetition — leading Garfinkel to concede that "sales persons can be dismissed as either having been dopes in different ways than current theories of standardized expectancies provide, or not dopes enough." This is a genuine empirical embarrassment for the strong version of Garfinkel's own thesis (that background norms are rigidly enforced), honestly reported rather than explained away.

Where the program succeeded most clearly was in showing that professional, "rigorous" procedures rest on exactly the same documentary and ad hoc work as lay reasoning. The UCLA clinic-coding reanalysis found that graduate coders, even when authorized to make inferences, obtained usable data on only a fraction of scheduled items (18 of 47 "history of contact" items reached 90%+ coverage across 661 cases) and required unarticulated background knowledge of clinic organization simply to apply the coding instructions — a finding Garfinkel used to argue that a claim to disinterested, actuarial description is itself a documentary accomplishment. Chapter 7's reanalysis of Katz & Solomon's (1958) and Myers & Schaffer's (1954) published data — recomputing chi-square comparisons using correct "in/out" population logic rather than the published "in-in"/"out-out" comparisons — actually reversed and strengthened prior published findings, a rare case of an ethnomethodological argument yielding a directly falsifiable, and confirmed, statistical correction.

The program's methodological limits are equally clear in the record. The breaching demonstrations were, by Garfinkel's own description, not controlled experiments: "my studies are not properly speaking experimental. They are demonstrations, designed, in Herbert Spiegelberg's phrase, as 'aids to a sluggish imagination.'" Sample construction was informal and uneven (23 students reporting 25 encounters in one demonstration; 33, then 49, then 68/67, then 247, then 79 in others), coding of reactions was qualitative and investigator-supplied, and no comparison or control conditions were run. The correlation Garfinkel reported in the medical-school "fake applicant" interview (r = .026 rising to r = .751) was explicitly flagged by Garfinkel himself as illustrative rather than a finding — "Under no circumstances should they be considered as findings" — a rare and notable instance of a researcher pre-empting the misuse of his own numbers, but also an acknowledgment that the empirical base for the program's central claims rested on demonstration rather than measurement.

The Agnes case is the program's most consequential and most troubled anomaly. The February 1967 Appendix — added after the book was already in press — recorded that Agnes had disclosed to Robert J. Stoller that she had never had the biologically intersexed condition originally diagnosed: from age twelve she had been secretly taking Stilbestrol stolen from her mother's post-panhysterectomy prescription, successfully feminizing herself without any endocrine anomaly. The entire medical premise underlying Chapter 5's analysis — Agnes as a "biologically defective male" whose passing work was framed against a genuine physiological ambiguity — was constructed on a deception the research team had actively discounted evidence for (a UCLA urological resident's contemporaneous suspicion of an exogenous estrogen source had been dismissed, partly, Garfinkel noted, because "his dislike for Agnes was evident on other scores"). Garfinkel's own retrospective comment treated the disclosure as a further exhibit of ethnomethodological phenomena rather than as a design failure: the news, he wrote, "turned the article into a feature of the same circumstances it reported, i.e., into a situated report," exhibiting both that "the recognizedly rational accountability of practical actions is a member's practical accomplishment" and that the success of that accomplishment consists in masking, from the researchers themselves, their own practical ordering of the evidence. Whether this reflexive recuperation rescues the chapter's analytic content or simply names, without resolving, a genuine failure of evidential diligence is a live question the wiki should not adjudicate; both readings are defensible and are recorded on the debates page below.

## Method Commitments

Ethnomethodology's method commitments follow directly from its central claims about indexicality and the documentary method, and they are unusual among the theoretical programs treated in this hub for being stated as explicit, numbered "policies" rather than left implicit. Garfinkel's Policy 1 asserts a radical methodological symmetry: "any occasion whatsoever" — including "water witching, divination, mathematics, sociology" — is to be studied under the same policy, that "every feature of sense, of fact, of method... is the managed accomplishment of organized settings of practical actions." This commits the program to treating scientific method itself as an object of the same kind of analysis it applies to lay reasoning, rather than as an epistemically privileged vantage point exempt from it. Policy 3 states a refusal "to assess, recognize, categorize, [or] describe" the rational properties of practical activities "by using a rule or a standard obtained outside actual settings" — method must be internal to the setting studied, not imported from an external normative theory of rationality. Policy 4 holds that "any social setting be viewed as self-organizing" with respect to its own intelligibility.

Operationally, the program's characteristic technique is the breaching demonstration: deliberately disrupting a background expectancy (feigning ignorance of common-sense meaning, acting as a "boarder" in one's own home, distrusting a conversational partner, bargaining over a fixed price) without disclosing the disruption as an experimental manipulation, and observing the affective and interpretive work participants perform to restore normal appearances. Garfinkel was explicit that these are "demonstrations," not controlled experiments, designed as "aids to a sluggish imagination" rather than as hypothesis tests in the conventional sense — a methodological self-description that later critics would treat as either an honest disclaimer or, less charitably, as an evasion of standards the program should be held to.

A second method commitment, developed most fully in the clinic-records and jury-selection chapters, is reanalysis of existing institutional documents and prior published quantitative studies from within the documentary-method framework: reading records not as transparent windows onto events but as documents whose sense is jointly constituted by the reader's situated, competent membership knowledge. This licensed Garfinkel and [[bittner-egon]]'s methodological critique in Chapter 7 — the argument that quantitative selection studies conflate "predictive criteria" with actual "selection criteria," an argument Garfinkel and Bittner explicitly analogized to Robinson's critique of ecological correlations.

## Major Debates and Rivals

Ethnomethodology's most consequential rivalry is with the Parsonian [[structural-functionalism]] it emerged from and against. Both programs address the Hobbesian problem of order; both take normative order as real and needing explanation. They diverge sharply on where the explanatory work is done: Parsons located the answer in internalized normative patterns operating largely beneath actors' awareness, coordinated at the level of the social system; Garfinkel located it in members' continuous, situated, artful accomplishment of accountable order, moment to moment, at the level of practical activity. Garfinkel's bargaining demonstration is itself a direct empirical challenge to a specific Parsonian claim (the "institutionalized one price rule" as a fully internalized constituent of the institution of contract) — one of the few points in the corpus where ethnomethodology directly tested, and found only partial support for, a named Parsonian proposition.

A second major point of contest is internal to interpretive sociology: Garfinkel's explicit engagement with [[goffman-erving]]'s dramaturgical framework in the Agnes chapter. Garfinkel granted Goffman's descriptive acuity about impression management but argued that Goffman's episodic framing could not handle Agnes's non-episodic, continuously developmental passing occasions, and — the sharper point — that Goffman's model of a calculating, deliberately strategic actor conflicts with the sociological requirement that members must treat most matters in a trusting, unreflective, taken-for-granted manner for mutual judgments of normality and rationality to hold at all. Where [[symbolic-interactionism]] (of which Goffman's work is a variant) treats meaning as constructed through interaction episodes that actors more or less consciously manage, ethnomethodology treats the more fundamental phenomenon as the mostly unnoticed background work that makes strategic, episodic self-presentation possible in the first place.

Ethnomethodology's relationship to [[interpretive-sociology]] and to [[sociology-of-knowledge]] (via Mannheim and, more broadly, to [[social-construction-of-reality]]-type arguments) is one of partial overlap and methodological divergence: all three traditions treat meaning and social reality as produced rather than simply given, but ethnomethodology alone insists on studying the *mechanics* of that production at the level of situated, observable conduct, refusing the move (which Garfinkel treats [[social-construction-of-reality]]-style arguments as prone to) of stating the constructedness of social reality as a general theoretical thesis rather than demonstrating it occasion by occasion. This methodological insistence is at the center of the `ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate`: whether ethnomethodology is a genuine alternative research program capable of cumulative findings, or a perpetual metacommentary on other sociologies' unexamined assumptions, incapable of generating positive results of its own. Garfinkel's own Chapter 7 reanalysis — which corrected and reversed prior published quantitative findings using conventional statistical technique applied more carefully — is the strongest piece of evidence on the "genuine research program" side of that dispute; the small, uncontrolled, investigator-coded breaching demonstrations are the strongest piece of evidence on the "metacommentary" side.

## Critiques and Responses

The most persistent critique of ethnomethodology, both at the time and since, is that its demonstrations lack the controls, sample sizes, and coding rigor conventionally required of empirical sociology, and that its central claims — that all order is accomplishment, that documentary interpretation underlies all fact-finding, professional included — are stated at a level of generality that resists disconfirmation. The number of participants across Garfinkel's own demonstrations is small and unevenly reported (from ten subjects in the fake-counselor study to 247 in the ticktacktoe demonstration), coding of participants' reactions is investigator-supplied rather than independently validated, and Garfinkel's own disclaimer that the studies are "demonstrations" rather than experiments concedes much of this ground pre-emptively. Garfinkel's response, evident throughout the text, is that this is precisely the point: conventional experimental controls presuppose the very "scientific rationalities" (Chapter 8's fourteen rationalities, of which only four are peculiar to scientific theorizing) that ethnomethodology argues cannot be imposed on everyday conduct without generating spurious findings of irrationality — imposing them here would defeat the object of showing how *ordinary* practical reasoning, not idealized reasoning, actually operates.

A second major critique concerns obscurantism of exposition: Garfinkel's prose is dense with neologism and hedge ("accountable-for-all-practical-purposes," "membershipping," "ad hocing"), a style critics have charged obscures rather than clarifies the program's actual empirical content and has made ethnomethodology harder to assess, teach, and build on than its underlying findings might otherwise warrant. Garfinkel's defenders — most consequentially his students Harvey Sacks, Emanuel Schegloff, and Gail Jefferson, who built conversation analysis as a far more procedurally disciplined and cumulative research tradition directly out of ethnomethodological premises — effectively answered this critique not by defending the prose but by developing a more rigorously transcribed, sequentially analyzed empirical method that retained the core claims (indexicality, accountability, members' methods) while abandoning the demonstration format's looseness.

The Agnes case generates its own distinct critique, sharpened by the 1967 Appendix's disclosure: that the research team discounted a contemporaneous dissenting clinical opinion (the UCLA urological resident's suspicion of exogenous estrogen) partly on grounds unrelated to the medical evidence itself ("his dislike for Agnes was evident on other scores"), and that roughly seventy hours of conversation with Agnes nonetheless failed to surface — by Garfinkel's own accounting — basic facts about her biography, including the deception itself, her mother's possible knowledge, and other matters he explicitly listed as unexamined. Whether this reflects an unavoidable limit of any single-subject, trust-dependent research relationship, or a more specific failure of evidentiary diligence by the research team, is contested; Garfinkel's own retrospective framing (treating the disclosure as itself an exhibit of ethnomethodological phenomena rather than as a validity threat to the chapter's specific claims) has been read by some commentators as an elegant reflexive recovery and by others as a way of converting a research failure into a theoretical asset without conceding error.

A further critique, directed at Chapter 8's argument that scientific rationality should not be imposed as a standard on everyday conduct, holds that this position risks a thoroughgoing relativism that leaves ethnomethodology unable to say that any account of a setting is better or worse than any other — a charge Garfinkel's response (that the fourteen rationalities are to be treated as "empirically problematical material," data to be explained rather than a standard for judgment) answers only by relocating, not resolving, the question of evaluative standards.

## What Survived

Ethnomethodology's most consequential afterlife is conversation analysis, developed by Harvey Sacks (a named collaborator credited in Garfinkel's own preface as a source of "extraordinary writings and lectures" that served as "critical resources"), together with Emanuel Schegloff and Gail Jefferson, directly out of the program's core premises — that ordinary talk is orderly at a level members produce and orient to but do not typically articulate, and that this order is available to rigorous, procedurally disciplined empirical study through close transcription and sequential analysis. Conversation analysis retained ethnomethodology's central commitments (accountability, indexicality, members' methods) while replacing the demonstration format with a far more cumulative and replicable analytic apparatus, and it has become, in institutional terms, more durable and more widely applied across linguistics, communication studies, and sociology than ethnomethodology proper.

A second major line of survival is "studies of work": the tradition, descending directly from Chapters 6 and 7's analyses of clinic records and selection studies, of examining the situated, practical competencies embedded in professional and technical work (laboratory science, mathematics, air-traffic control, and other settings) as accomplishments requiring the same close attention Garfinkel gave to jurors' reasoning and clinic coders' inference. This tradition carried forward the documentary-method thesis that professional rigor does not escape situated, documentary interpretation but is itself an accomplishment of the same general kind.

Beyond these two direct descendants, several of Garfinkel's individual concepts have been absorbed into wider sociological and social-scientific vocabulary largely detached from the full ethnomethodological program: [[accountability]] and [[reflexivity-of-accounts]] circulate broadly in science studies and organizational sociology; [[indexicality]] is a standing term in linguistic anthropology and pragmatics, often via routes independent of Garfinkel's specific argument about the professional/lay distinction; the [[etcetera-principle]] and the general insight that rule-application always requires unstated, ad hoc judgment has become close to common sense within qualitative methodology generally, even among researchers who would not identify as ethnomethodologists. This is the characteristic pattern of an "absorbed" program's vocabulary outliving the program's institutional identity as a distinct research community — though, unlike the status label `absorbed` used elsewhere in this wiki for programs whose research communities have effectively dissolved, ethnomethodology and conversation analysis retain active, self-identified practitioners and dedicated venues, which is why this page's frontmatter records `status: active` rather than `absorbed`.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Ethnomethodology continues as an active, if institutionally modest, research tradition, most visibly through conversation analysis and studies of work, both of which maintain dedicated journals, conferences, and training lineages distinct from mainstream sociology departments. Contemporary applications extend Garfinkel's original settings (clinics, juries, ordinary conversation, embodied identity claims) into technologically mediated and professional-scientific settings: human-computer interaction, workplace studies of technical and scientific practice, and categorization work in institutional settings continue to draw on the accountability and documentary-method theses.

Open questions the field continues to work include: how far the documentary-method thesis (that professional science is not epistemically exempt from documentary interpretation) can be pressed without collapsing into an unworkable relativism about scientific knowledge claims; whether ethnomethodology's demonstration-based empirical style can or should be reconciled with more standard replicable methodology, or whether doing so would defeat its purpose; and how the program's founding case studies — Agnes above all — should be re-read in light of what is now known about their evidentiary limits, a question this wiki routes to the `ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate` and to the debate page treating the Agnes disclosure specifically, rather than adjudicating in wiki voice.
