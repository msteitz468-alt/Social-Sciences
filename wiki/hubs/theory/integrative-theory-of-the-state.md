# Integrative Theory of the State — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. The [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]] summary owns
> core-claims/origins/proponents/critiques/status at normal resolution; this page owns
> the depth. Reciprocal: the summary links here by full path; this page's
> `theory_page` points back to [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]].

All programmatic claims below are in **attributed voice** — this is
[[service-elman-r|Elman Service]]'s theory, stated in
*[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization: The Process of
Cultural Evolution]]* (1975), not the wiki's finding. The book's own evidence is
mid-century secondary scholarship, superseded on many empirical points.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Service's book was written at the hinge of the critical-turn era, as the neo-evolutionary
program he and [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] had built in *Evolution and Culture* (1960)
came under fire from every direction — Marxist political economy on one side, the
symbolic/interpretive turn and the coming post-processual critique on the other. Its
polemical target, however, was older and more specific: the **Morgan–Marx–[[engels-friedrich|Engels]]
theory of the state** as an organ of class repression, in its mid-century
anthropological revivals by V. Gordon [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]], Karl Wittfogel, and —
closest to home — Service's own friend and Columbia colleague [[fried-morton-h|Morton
Fried]], whose *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|Evolution of Political
Society]]* (1967) had derived the state from unstable [[stratified-society|stratification]].

Service's opening move is autobiographical and disarming. He tells the reader he
"simultaneously discovered anthropology and Marxism in the late 1930s," was formed by
[[white-leslie-a|Leslie White]] and [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]], and had
"eventually became dissatisfied with Marxism" while keeping its questions. The book is
that dissatisfaction worked out at monograph length. Crucially, Service reports that the
argument changed under his hands:

> "When I began work, I accepted the usual formula that equates the rise of
> civilization with the origin of the state, which in turn is defined by the presence
> of repressive controls based on physical force… But I have found that these" cases do
> not bear it out.

The intellectual context that makes the theory legible is therefore a *reversal
narrative*: a Marxist-trained evolutionist reporting that his own comparative data
refused the class-conflict story and forced an integrative one. That framing is both the
theory's rhetorical engine and, for critics, its vulnerability.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The architecture rests on a distinction Service had built earlier: the
[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|band–tribe–chiefdom]] sequence, in which the
[[chiefdom]] is a **[[redistribution|redistributional]]** society with a permanent
central agency of coordination but no monopoly of force. *Origins of the State* adds the
top rung — the [[the-state|state]] as "the power of force in addition to the power of
authority" — and asks how the leap from theocratic chiefdom to force-wielding state
happened. The answer is assembled from six load-bearing claims.

**1. The definitional wedge: authority vs. force.** Service, following Hannah Arendt,
separates *authority* (obedience without coercion, grounded in legitimacy and benefit)
from *force* (physical coercion). Arendt's line — "where force is used, authority itself
has failed" — is the hinge of the whole book: it lets Service treat coercion as a
*symptom of breakdown* rather than the essence of governance.

**2. The three functions.** All political systems, egalitarian or hierarchical, must
perform **reinforcement** (deterring deviance), **leadership** (organizing action), and
**mediation** (settling disputes). By defining the political in terms of recurrent
*problems* (a principle he borrows from Walter Goldschmidt) rather than institutions,
Service can compare a !Kung camp with pharaonic Egypt on the same axis.

**3. Government legitimizes itself.** The book's thesis sentence:

> "primal government worked to protect, not another class or stratum of the society, but
> itself — legitimizing itself in its role of maintaining the whole society. The two
> basic strata were the governors and the governed; and the governors created
> themselves."

This is the precise inversion of Engels. There is a stratification — governors vs.
governed — but it is *political office*, not economic ownership, and it is
self-generated by the success of coordination, not imposed to defend prior property.

**4. The redistributive engine.** Political power "organized the economy, not vice
versa. The system was redistributive, allocative, not acquisitive." The big-man amasses
a "fund of power" (Malinowski's phrase) by redistributing well; "a leader is created by
his followers… by their appreciation of his exemplary qualities." Success "snowballs" —
visible redistributive efficiency breeds more specialization, which needs more
coordination. Primogeniture then freezes personal charisma into hereditary office.

**5. Theocracy as the integrative solvent.** Chiefdoms are "typically, perhaps
universally, theocracies." Priestly control of supernatural sanction — not police — is
"by far the most direct and pervasive cause of the 'consent of the governed.'" The first
formal public law is *lèse-majesté*, protecting the ruler's sacred authority. Service
quotes Hocart to collapse the sacred/secular divide: "Temples are just as utilitarian as
dams and canals… dams and canals are as ritual as temples."

**6. Bureaucracy as creator, not resultant.** The move that most annoys his opponents:

> "Rather than the mere resultant of causes, this centralized bureaucracy was itself the
> creator of many new aspects of culture, in religion, art, literature, economics,
> technology, and warfare."

Where conflict theory treats the state as a *structure* erected by economic forces,
Service treats it as an *agent* generating its own economy, ideology, and technology —
an "orthogenetic" process.

The conceptual keystone binding these is **"governance by benefit"**: "a universal in
the formation of all persevering power relationships." Even circumscription and external
threat operate through it — they make the benefits of membership obvious, because the
alternative (leaving, revolting) is worse.

Service is essentially the sole proponent-author; the theory has no research community of
the kind that carries [[structural-functionalism]] or processualism. That is itself
analytically important (see Current Status): it lives on as a *position* to argue with,
carried by textbooks and by its opponents, more than as a program with practitioners.

## The Empirical Program

Service ran the theory against fifteen cases in two tiers. The honest scorecard is mixed,
and Service is unusually candid about the weaknesses.

| Claim | Supporting cases (Service's reading) | Challenging cases / problems | Current standing |
|---|---|---|---|
| State arose from benefit, not coercion | Egypt "super-bureaucracy"; Cherokee bureaucratic law; Ankole drum-cult redistribution | Zulu/Ankole/Nupe conquest states — Service must classify these as *secondary* | Largely rejected as universal; retained as one pathway |
| Civilization precedes urbanism | Egypt, Indus, lowland Maya, coastal Peru | Sumer, Tiahuanaco (urban, warlike) | Broadly accepted that urbanism ≠ civilization; multi-path |
| Irrigation is permissive, not causal | Peru (Lanning: canals under chiefdoms centuries before states); Mesopotamia/China (politics builds canals) | — | Wittfogel hydraulic-despotism largely abandoned; Service's side prevailed here |
| Coercion is symptom, not origin | Old Kingdom Egypt; Indus "priestly state" | Sumerian fortifications, forced urban relocation (Adams) | Rejected; early states show real coercion |
| Class = office, not ownership | Egypt ("stewardship, not ownership"; no merchants) | Mesopotamia's developing property/markets | Contested; "office vs ownership" remains a live analytic |

The program's most successful stretch is the **anti-hydraulic** case: Lanning's finding
that North-Coast Peruvian canal systems reached full development under chiefdom authority
long before any state, plus the Mesopotamian and Chinese evidence that political
centralization built the canals rather than vice versa, genuinely damaged Wittfogel's
thesis, and that verdict has largely held.

The program's **weakest joint** is the pristine/secondary firewall. Service's cleanest
"benefit" cases are the archaic civilizations he knows only archaeologically (so cannot
observe coercion directly), while his cases of observable state-formation — Zulu,
Ankole, Nupe, Ashanti, Kongo, Tahiti — are conquest- and contact-driven, and he must
quarantine them as *secondary* to protect the theory. Critics note the circularity: the
cases where we can watch a state form look coercive; the cases that look benign are the
ones where coercion would leave the least trace.

An accumulating anomaly Service half-registers is **the Zulu itself**. His own chapter
concedes Shaka ruled by terror; he rescues the integrative reading by relocating the
"true state" to Mpande's peaceful reign a generation later, under
politico-military circumscription. It is a defensible move, but it shows the theory
absorbing a coercion-first case by redescription.

## Method Commitments

Comparative neo-evolutionism, with three explicit commitments. First, the **"our
contemporary ancestors" method** — using ethnohistoric societies as analogues to
interpret archaeological ones — which Service defends against the Boasian prohibition
while warning against its abuse (he singles out mechanical use of the HRAF and
*Ethnographic Atlas* on already-acculturated societies). Second, **problem-based rather
than institution-based comparison** (Goldschmidt), which licenses the wide net. Third, a
**revision of his own 1962 scheme for archaeology**: because the chiefdom–state boundary
is defined by institutionalized repressive force, and force leaves little unambiguous
archaeological trace, Service argues the boundary is "archaeologically invisible" and
prefers to speak of "archaic civilizations" rather than "states" for prehistoric cases —
criticizing Sanders and Price for over-reading "state." This is methodologically honest
and, ironically, concedes that his central variable (coercion) is the hardest one to see
in exactly the cases he most wants to call benign.

## Major Debates and Rivals

The theory is one corner of the classic neo-evolutionary triangle of state-origin
theories, and the whole of *Origins of the State* Chapter 16 ("Conclusions I,
Negative") is a systematic demolition of the rivals — see
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]] for the full position map:

- **vs. conflict/class (Engels, Fried, Adams):** Service argues there is "absolutely no
  evidence" of private-property capitalism, class uprisings, or rulers' force against the
  masses in his cases; stratification is political office. He even turns Fried's own line
  — "stratification is a provoker of war," not the reverse — against the conquest school.
- **vs. hydraulic despotism (Wittfogel):** rebutted empirically (above).
- **vs. conquest/circumscription (Spencer, Oppenheimer, Carneiro):** war amplifies an
  already-existing state; circumscription is a real *enabler* (vindicated for Egypt and
  Peru) folded into "governance by benefit," not a sufficient cause.
- **vs. Service's own rival, Fried:** the [[service-fried-political-typology-debate|
  typology debate]] is about stage labels; *this* debate is about the *cause* of the
  state, where Service (benefit) and Fried (stratification/repression) are furthest apart.

## Critiques and Responses

The standing critiques cluster in three families. **(1) It launders coercion.** A theory
that finds no exploitation at the birth of the state can be heard as ideologically
consoling — the mirror image of the Marxism Service left. He pre-empts this reflexively
(Ch. 16's "Radicals and Conservatives" section notes that Weber's anti-Marxism and every
inequality theory carries a politics), but pre-emption is not refutation. **(2) The
pristine/secondary firewall is convenient.** As above, the benign cases are the
unobservable ones. **(3) The typology reifies.** Later archaeology (e.g.
[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]], citing McGuire)
treats band/tribe/chiefdom/state as static descriptive types "poorly suited to origin
analysis," preferring corporate-vs-network strategies, heterarchy, and multi-causal
regional pathways. [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]] (2021)
reject the origin question itself.

Service's live responses (in the book) are strongest on the empirical rivals and weakest
on the meta-critique: he can show Wittfogel and simple conquest theory fail his cases,
but he cannot show that "benefit" is the *only* reading of cases where the losers left no
records.

## What Survived

The program as a *program* is dead — it never had practitioners beyond Service. But four
things survived it:

1. **The redistributional chiefdom** as a working concept, now detached from the
   integrative theory and used across archaeology (Earle and others) — see [[chiefdom]].
2. **The anti-hydraulic verdict.** Wittfogel's despotism-from-irrigation thesis is
   effectively finished, and Service's Lanning-based argument is part of why.
3. **"Office vs. ownership."** The insistence that early-state "class" may be political
   office rather than economic ownership remains a live analytic distinction, echoed in
   debates over Egyptian and Andean political economy.
4. **The reversal itself as a permanent option.** "Does inequality make the state, or
   does the state make inequality?" is now a standard framing, and Service's answer —
   integrative organization first, inequality as by-product — is the canonical statement
   of one horn of it, taught opposite Fried and Carneiro.

The [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]] (from Sahlins & Service 1960, redeployed in the
epilogue's "the first classic civilizations… did not fall; they were pushed") also
survives as a core–periphery/marcher-state heuristic.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`contested`. No one runs an "integrative theory" research program, but the theory is
undead in the way influential wrong-or-half-right theories are: it is the fixed
reference point for the benefit side of every textbook treatment of state origins, and
its vocabulary (redistribution, chiefdom, governance by benefit) is common currency.
Contemporary frontiers have moved past the benefit-vs-coercion binary toward
collective-action and fiscal-sociology models of early states (why some early states
were more "autocratic" and others more "collective/corporate"), which in a sense
vindicate Service's intuition that *how the polity funds and legitimizes itself* — not a
single class-conflict switch — is the right variable, while rejecting his claim that the
answer was benign integration everywhere. The theory's most durable legacy may be
methodological humility it did not intend: by conceding that coercion is
archaeologically near-invisible, Service exposed exactly why the origin-of-the-state
debate stays [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate|open]].
