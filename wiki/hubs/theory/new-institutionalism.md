# The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[new-institutionalism]]. Reciprocal link from the summary points here by full path `[[hubs/theory/new-institutionalism|…]]`.

**Analysis standard:** verbatim passages · claims-vs-evidence matrix · honest empirical program · **"What Survived"** · nine canonical sections. All programmatic claims in **attributed voice**. *Built from the 1991 Powell & DiMaggio anthology, which reprints the charter papers and stages the program's first decade of self-criticism; later developments (Scott's Institutions and Organizations, the world-society program, the institutional-logics literature) await ingest and appear only as trajectory.*

## Intellectual Origins and Context

The editors assign the program a precise birth date. "If, in retrospect, one could assign a birth date to the new institutionalism in organizational studies, it would have to be 1977, the year in which John Meyer published two seminal papers, 'The Effects of Education as an Institution' and 'Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony' (with Brian Rowan)." By 1985, when [[zucker-lynne|Lynne Zucker]] convened a small conference at UCLA, enough scholars were "intrigued by the effects of culture, ritual, ceremony, and higher-level structures on organizations" for "neoinstitutional theory to be named and reified." The 1991 volume grew out of a successor conference at the Center for Advanced Study in the Behavioral Sciences.

Two lineages converge. The first is organizational: the "old institutionalism" of Philip Selznick's TVA study (1949) and *Leadership in Administration* (1957). [[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio]] and [[powell-walter-w|Powell]] are careful about the inheritance — "Neoinstitutionalism traces its roots to the 'old institutionalism' of Philip Selznick and his associates, yet diverges from that tradition substantially." Both share "a skepticism toward rational-actor models of organization," view institutionalization as a "state-dependent process" limiting rationality, and "promise to reveal aspects of reality that are inconsistent with organizations' formal accounts." But their Table 1.1 codifies fourteen divergences, the load-bearing ones being: conflicts of interest were *central* to the old and are *peripheral* to the new; the old located institutionalization in the *organization* (Selznick's unique organizational "character"), the new in the *field or society*; the old's cognitive currency was "values, norms, attitudes" acquired through socialization and commitment, the new's is "classifications, routines, scripts, schema" carried by "habit, practical action." Where Selznick found irrationality in the "shadowland of informal interaction" subverting formal purpose, "the new institutionalism, by contrast, locates irrationality in the formal structure itself, attributing the diffusion of certain departments and operating procedures to interorganizational influences, conformity, and the persuasiveness of cultural accounts, rather than to the functions they are intended to perform."

The second lineage is a general-theoretical one the editors call the **cognitive turn**: "a dramatic transformation in the way in which social scientists have come to think about human motivation and behavior… from Parsonsian action theory, rooted in Freudian ego psychology, to a theory of practical action based in ethnomethodology and in psychology's 'cognitive revolution.'" Their reading of [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] is surgical: the middle Parsons of the general theory of action made three "reductive moves" — dismissing culture as an external object of orientation in favor of an internalized personality element, shifting from the cognitive to the evaluative aspects of culture, and conflating cognition and cathexis — so that "Parsons' break with utilitarianism is incomplete," action remaining "the quasi-intentional pursuit of gratification." The repair came from elsewhere: [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s [[ethnomethodology]] (norms as "cognitive guidance systems" externalized in accounts, closer to cognitive psychology's "scripts" than to Parsonsian values); Berger and Luckmann's *Social Construction of Reality* ("How is it possible that subjective meanings become objective facticities?"; institutions as "a reciprocal typification of habitualized actions by types of actors" that "control human conduct… prior to or apart from any mechanisms or sanctions specifically set up to support them"); the Carnegie school (Simon, March: bounded rationality, routine, the garbage-can model — "from commitment to routine, from values to premises, from motivation to the logic of rule following"); [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s structuration and practical/discursive consciousness; Goffman's and Collins's Durkheimian interaction ritual; and — the editors' strongest claim of affinity — [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]: "Within the broader field of social theory, we come closest to a genuine alternative to Parsons' version of role theory in Pierre Bourdieu's (1977) theory of the habitus," a "system of 'regulated improvisation'" internalizing past experience through shared typifications, "knowledge without concepts."

The editors are equally frank about what this microfoundation does *not* yet deliver: why actors work so hard to sustain shared images of reality; how microprocesses aggregate to macro order; and "what place do intentionality and interest have in the institutional order?" — "These problems have not been solved; nor, perhaps, are they likely to prove soluble within the framework of neoinstitutional theory."

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The program's architecture as staged in the volume has four strata.

**1. Rationalized myths and decoupling ([[meyer-john-w|Meyer]] & [[rowan-brian|Rowan]] 1977).** The founding move inverts the Weberian story of bureaucracy. Formal structures arise "in highly institutionalized contexts"; organizations "are driven to incorporate the practices and procedures defined by prevailing rationalized concepts of organizational work," and doing so "increase[s] their legitimacy and their survival prospects, independent of the immediate efficacy of the acquired practices and procedures." The generative elements are **[[rationalized-myth|rationalized myths]]**, with "two key properties. First, they are rationalized and impersonal prescriptions that identify various social purposes as technical ones and specify in a rulelike way the appropriate means to pursue these technical purposes rationally. Second, they are highly institutionalized and thus in some measure beyond the discretion of any individual participant or organization." Because ceremonial conformity "often conflicts sharply with efficiency criteria," organizations resolve the tension by **[[decoupling]]** (Proposition 4: "elements of structure are decoupled from activities and from each other") and by a **logic of confidence and good faith**: "Hospitals treat, not cure, patients. Schools produce students, not learning." Six numbered propositions formalize the argument; institutionalized organizations "seek to minimize inspection and evaluation" (Proposition 6).

**2. Isomorphism in organizational fields ([[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio]] & [[powell-walter-w|Powell]] 1983).** The paper reopens [[weber-max|Weber]]'s [[iron-cage]]: "the causes of bureaucratization and rationalization have changed… bureaucratization and other forms of organizational change occur as the result of processes that make organizations more similar without necessarily making them more efficient." The unit is the **[[organizational-field]]** — "those organizations that, in the aggregate, constitute a recognized area of institutional life" — which becomes structured through a four-part structuration process (interaction, domination structures, information load, mutual awareness). Within structured fields, three mechanisms of **[[institutional-isomorphism]]** operate: "(1) coercive isomorphism that stems from political influence and the problem of legitimacy; (2) mimetic isomorphism resulting from standard responses to uncertainty; and (3) normative isomorphism, associated with professionalization." Twelve hypotheses (six organizational-level, six field-level) are stated and explicitly flagged as untested: "the ultimate value of our perspective lies in its predictive utility."

**3. Microfoundations ([[zucker-lynne|Zucker]] 1977; [[jepperson-ronald|Jepperson]] 1991).** Zucker redefines [[institutionalization]] as "a variable, with different degrees of institutionalization altering the cultural persistence which can be expected," measured through the ethnomethodological criteria of objectivity and exteriority, with "office" and "personal influence" as poles. Jepperson supplies the program's most careful conceptual apparatus: "An institution represents a social order or pattern that has attained a certain state or property… Institutions are those social patterns that, when chronically reproduced, owe their survival to relatively self-activating social processes." His negative definitional work — institutionalization is *not* legitimacy, *not* formal organization, *not* any contextual effect, *not* "culture" — and his taxonomies (three carriers: formal organization, regimes, culture; four change types: formation, development, deinstitutionalization, reinstitutionalization) discipline a vocabulary the program had used loosely. His aphorism condenses the action contrast: "one enacts institutions; one takes action by departing from them, not by participating in them."

**4. Societal architecture ([[scott-w-richard|Scott]] & Meyer; Jepperson & Meyer; [[friedland-roger|Friedland]] & [[alford-robert-r|Alford]]).** Scott and Meyer's **[[societal-sector]]** concept and technical/institutional environment cross-classification scale the analysis up from fields to functionally (not geographically) bounded sectors. Jepperson and Meyer derive organizational structure from polity form (liberal, statist-society, segmental, corporatist), with the striking formulation that "formal organizing is a sort of manic outburst of rationality created under considerable competitive urgency and, for the same reason, unlikely to work as chartered." Friedland and Alford's **[[institutional-logics]]** give the program its societal content: "Each of the most important institutional orders of contemporary Western societies has a central logic — a set of material practices and symbolic constructions — which constitutes its organizing principles" — accumulation/commodification (capitalism), rationalization/regulation (state), participation (democracy), community/loyalty (family), truth (religion). Because these orders are "interdependent and yet also contradictory," "institutional contradictions are the bases of the most important political conflicts in our society."

## The Empirical Program

What the program explained that predecessors could not: organizational similarity without efficiency, and structure without function.

| Claim | Supporting studies (in the volume) | Challenging/complicating evidence | Standing as of 1991 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adoption shifts from technical to institutional predictors as a field structurates | Tolbert & Zucker 1983 (civil-service reform: city characteristics predict early adoption, institutional definition predicts late); M. Meyer et al. 1985 (urban fiscal agencies) | Scott (ch. 7) notes the inference is contestable — later adopters may face different internal problems | Supported but methodologically contested |
| Degree of institutionalization raises transmission, maintenance, resistance to change | Zucker's three autokinetic experiments (180 subjects; office > organizational context > personal influence on all three outcomes; e.g. transmission coefficients .94/.88/.43) | Laboratory scope; single study | Supported experimentally, unreplicated in the volume |
| Institutional endorsement raises survival | Singh, Tucker & House 1986 / ch. 16 (Toronto VSSOs: community-directory listing, charitable registration, board size all significantly reduce death rates) | Ch. 16 also finds density effects *opposite* in direction to prior ecological findings, and the favorable OFY shock *raised* death rates | Core survival claim supported; surrounding dynamics anomalous |
| Fields homogenize as they structurate (the "master hypothesis") | DiMaggio & Powell 1983 hypotheses; East Asia within-country isomorphism (ch. 15) | Scott & Meyer: centralized authority can create diversity by design; Powell ch. 8: heterogeneity sources are systematic | Contested inside the program |
| Institutional ≠ technical environments (dichotomy) | Meyer, Scott & Deal 1981 (schools) | Orrù, Biggart & Hamilton: institutional arrangements operate "at the very core of market-regulated, technically dominated environments"; Powell: the dichotomy was misread as kinds of organizations | Abandoned as dichotomy, retained as dimensions |
| Field construction is a professional project driven by interests | DiMaggio ch. 11 (US art museums 1920–40: Carnegie Corporation supplied >80% of foundation arts funding; 167 museums by 1930, 387 by 1938); Galaskiewicz ch. 12 (Twin Cities Five Percent Club); Brint & Karabel ch. 14 (community-college vocationalization); Fligstein ch. 13 (diversification driven by presidents' backgrounds, then by industry-level diffusion) | These cases were assembled partly *against* the passive-conformity reading | The program's growth edge |

Fligstein's chapter deserves note as the volume's most fully realized quantitative study: 100 largest US firms at seven time points 1919–1979, strategy coded product-dominant/related/unrelated, logit models showing sales-and-marketing presidents predicting related diversification only in 1919–39 (agency), with the industry-level percentage of prior adopters predicting later shifts (institutionalization) — a designed test of agency-then-diffusion sequencing.

## Method Commitments

The program is committed to institutional measurement without a single method. The volume contains laboratory experiments (Zucker), quarterly time-series and event-history models (Singh, Tucker & Meinhard, with candid multicollinearity self-critique), logit panel models (Fligstein), archival-historical case studies (DiMaggio, Galaskiewicz, Brint & Karabel), and comparative institutional description from national directories (Orrù, Biggart & Hamilton). Powell's closing methodological position is pluralist by design: the field needs both "detailed ethnographic studies that reveal how institutional practices come to be legitimated" and "large-scale, longitudinal studies that explore the staying power of institutional arrangements." Jepperson adds the discipline that institutional arguments require "microtranslation" of higher-order effects into lower-level process statements — a test he concedes early institutionalism (Durkheim, Parsons, culture-and-personality) failed.

## Major Debates and Rivals

**Against population ecology.** DiMaggio and Powell distinguish institutional from competitive isomorphism (Hawley, Hannan & Freeman) and reject "natural selection" accounts of similarity; by 1991 the editors describe a rapprochement — ecologists "now emphasize institutional factors and disavow Panglossian models of organizational evolution," and ch. 16 formalizes the synthesis, premised "firmly in the fundamental complementarity of the two approaches."

**Against rational choice and the new institutional economics.** Friedland and Alford: "Without priced commodities, the postulate of rational utility maximization quickly becomes tautological, a liberal trope, not subject to falsification. Economics cannot have a theory of utility formation." Jepperson is subtler, resisting the simple opposition: "self-proclaimed rational-choice arguments often feature institutional constraints… as central causes, and institutional arguments often invoke adaptive responses." See [[rational-choice-theory]].

**Against state-centered theory.** Friedland and Alford's critique of Skocpol: "these are not factors which explain autonomous state action; they are aspects of autonomous state action. A set of assumptions is transformed into a theory."

**Against network reductionism.** Their critique of Granovetter's [[embeddedness]]: "social networks per se do not have any content and as such do not entail interests, values, motives, beliefs… it will be impossible to explain what kinds of social relations have what kind of effect."

**Within the program.** The power/agency dispute is documented on [[institutionalism-power-and-agency-debate]] — Scott vs. the master hypothesis, Powell's self-critique, Fligstein's power-based fields, Brint & Karabel's "silences," Galaskiewicz's purposive institution-building, Zucker's microfoundational dissent, and the divergence between Meyer/Rowan's loose coupling and both Zucker (expects substantive internal change) and DiMaggio/Powell (predict homogeneity, not decoupled diversity).

## Critiques and Responses

The deepest external charge — that the program describes conformity but cannot explain origins, change, or resistance — was internalized rather than rebutted. Powell's ch. 8 concedes three faults in his own 1983 paper: the market/institution contrast is overdrawn ("it is too easy to call one form of exchange economic and another social" — quoting Braudel); the loose-coupling insight was misread as making institutionalized organizations "relatively passive, inefficient manipulators of symbols rather than substance"; and the literature "suggests a static, constrained, and oversocialized view of organizations." His repair kit: four avenues of institutional reproduction (power, complex interdependencies, taken-for-granted assumptions, path dependence — the last importing Arthur/David lock-in economics and Levitt & March's "competency traps") and six systematic sources of field heterogeneity. The editors likewise retract the early claim that ceremonial spending is "pure cost": legitimacy can be income-producing, and "the key thrust of institutional analysis is neither to expose the inefficiency of organizational practices nor to celebrate the triumph of institutional arrangements."

A second critique — US-parochialism — is half-acknowledged: the East Asia chapter is framed as demonstrating that the theory travels, but its authors turn it against the program's own technical/institutional dichotomy: "It is not despite their institutional isomorphism that Japanese enterprise groups are economically fit, but because of the incorporation of institutional elements in their organizations that they are so successful."

A third, from the Marxian side, is registered by the editors themselves in a remarkable footnote: institutionalism and Marxism share terrain (Gramsci's hegemony, Mann on class consciousness), but the dialogue "has not yet taken place" — Marxists treat institutional processes as false consciousness; institutionalists neglect "the exercise of power… and patterns of inequality of power."

## What Survived

Judged from within this collection: the **[[organizational-field]]** concept became the mesolevel workhorse of American sociology — [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] (Fligstein & McAdam 2012, separately ingested) is its direct descendant, built precisely on the power-based reading Fligstein stakes out in ch. 13. **[[institutional-isomorphism]]** and its three mechanisms became textbook vocabulary far beyond organization theory (cf. Tilly's [[organizational-emulation]] mechanism in the inequality literature). **[[institutional-logics]]** outgrew its chapter to become a named research program. **[[decoupling]]** and **[[rationalized-myth|rationalized myths]]** remain the standard analysis of policy–practice gaps. Jepperson's definitional apparatus quietly became the field's conceptual hygiene. What did *not* survive intact: the technical/institutional dichotomy (retracted within the volume itself), the strong homogenization prediction (qualified into scope conditions), and the early actor-less framing (reversed by the institutional-entrepreneurship literature the volume's own empirical section inaugurated).

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

As of the ingested source (1991), the program was consolidating while pivoting: from demonstrating institutional effects to explaining institutional construction and change; from the US nonprofit/public sector to markets and non-Western economies; from actor-less diffusion to interests, power, and entrepreneurship. The volume's declared frontiers — micro–macro linkage, institutional origins, cross-national variation in organizing — map directly onto the subsequent careers of its contributors. Within this wiki, the nearest ingested successors are [[strategic-action-fields-theory]] and [[new-economic-sociology]]; the world-society strand (Meyer) and the mature institutional-logics literature (Thornton, Ocasio, Lounsbury) are not yet in the collection — flag for acquisition.
