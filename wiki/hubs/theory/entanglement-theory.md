## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]’s *[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* (Wiley-Blackwell, 2012) is a programmatic archaeology of human–thing relations written after three decades of post-processual and reflexive-field work, much of it organized through the long-running project at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]]. Where *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999) had answered the charge that interpretive archaeology lacked a field methodology — with [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge|interpretation at the trowel’s edge]], diaries, multivocality, specialist recursion — *Entangled* shifts the theoretical target from **how archaeologists know** to **what humans and things do to each other** once knowledge-construction is granted.

Hodder situates the book inside a broad “return to things” across the humanities and social sciences: Appadurai’s social life of things and [[cultural-biography-of-things|Kopytoffian biographies]]; materiality studies (Miller, Meskell, Keane, Pels); Nicholas Thomas on colonial object entanglements; Bill Brown’s literary “thing theory”; Latour’s symmetrical anthropology and Actor-Network Theory; Heideggerian equipment and jug/bridge “gathering”; cognitive and extended-mind archaeology; Behavioral Archaeology’s behavioral chains; dual-inheritance and niche-construction evolutionary models. His recurring criticism is that, protestations aside, most of these approaches still ask **what things can do for humans** — they break things into the aspect useful to the study (symbol, labor, shiny attraction, killing efficiency, network link) and leave object chemistry, decay, engineering, and archaeometric materials under-engaged.

The pedagogical opening is a reconstructed Mesolithic Lepenski Vir scene into which Hodder inserts an absurd concert piano. As long as things are props for a hunter-gatherer way of life, the gaze stays on society. The piano forces a shift:

> "So, subversively and subtly, the focus has changed from how things make society possible to the thing itself and its multiple connections. The gaze shifts to look more closely, harder at the thing, to explore how society and thing are co-entangled."

That shift — from props to co-entanglement — is the book’s first move. The second is genealogical: mature [[post-processual-archaeology]] had secured meaning, context, and reflexivity; *Entangled* rebalances toward **objectness** without reverting to naive materialism or covering-law processualism. The third is synthetic: supposedly opposed traditions (ANT, HBE, evolutionary archaeology, practice theory, complex systems) can each contribute to analyzing entanglement if selectively reworked — a synthetic ambition Hodder admits is achieved by taking what serves his purpose.

Written on leave at Magdalen College Oxford, the Fondation Maison des Sciences de l’Homme (Paris), and Koç University’s Research Center for Anatolian Civilizations, the book is also a product of the Çatalhöyük laboratory’s multi-specialist materials knowledge (clay, phytoliths, residues, fauna) — production context that makes certain TT/TH strings visible that armchair materiality theory often misses.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Themes about things (Ch. 1)

Hodder lays four standing themes before any formal definition:

1. **Things are not isolated** — artifacts fit into each other (wall/roof, sail/mast); even “natural” things participate in ecosystems; pure isolation is hard to find once humans exist at scale.
2. **Things are not inert** — matter has charges, viscosity, decay, hydration; “vibrant vitality” (Bennett); flows of matter, energy, and information (Deleuze & Guattari, Ingold) rather than fixed substances.
3. **Things endure over different temporalities** — geological, artefactual, residual spans exceed individual lives, producing *apparent* durability that still requires work.
4. **The forgetness of things** — connectedness (car to Minnesota ore, Detroit, Iraqi oil; watch to deep history) is routinely backgrounded until breakdown.

A **thing** is an entity with presence — a temporary configuration bundling flows — preferred over “object” because of gathering (Heidegger’s jug and bridge) and because entity boundaries track use (the computer as box vs as global energy–information system). Humans are also temporary bundles, but a particular kind of thing whose nervous system requires external activation. Knowing things is multiple (Shang oracle bones: divination → medicine → script discovery → looting → archaeology). Yet things also **make us**: objectness as “stand-in-the-wayness.”

### Dependence and dependency (Ch. 2)

The load-bearing distinction is stated in full:

> "I shall distinguish two forms of dependence in this book. The first and more general focus on dependence (and for the plural I shall use dependences) recognizes that the human use of things is enabling. … I use the term dependence here … in the sense of ‘reliance on’. But the word also has another inflection, as in the phrase ‘it depends’. … But dependence also often leads to a second focus that I shall term dependency … Dependency involves some form of constraint, as is seen in various dependency and co-dependency theories from World Systems Theory (Wallerstein 1976) to psychology (Rice 1998)."

Physical, economic, social, and psychological dependencies arise in **interaction**, not as essences of objects: whisky bottle for the recovering alcoholic, ash-tray for the smoker, Imelda Marcos shoes, crucifix for the priest, Roman coins for the archaeologist. The same table of “everyday objects” is background for one person and foreground compulsion for another. Ownership and identification draw humans toward things while denial and forgetness push away. Master–slave co-constraint is the HH analogue: each side’s livelihood is caught in dependency on the other.

Hodder also sketches a **hierarchy of HT dependence**: practical/sensual engagement (hand from flame; body on bicycle) → reflective thought about feelings and behaviors → thought about thoughts (including the book’s own meta-reflection). He flags dangers of level-models (cultural variation in human/non-human boundaries; artificial separation of experience) but keeps the device to clarify reflective vs non-reflective relations — linking Pitt-Rivers’s “automaton mind,” Mauss/Leroi-Gourhan/Bourdieu *connaissance* vs *savoir faire*, and finer archaeological distinctions (Pelegrin, Wynn, Bekaert). Ch. 2 then surveys the consensus that “Things R Us” (materiality, phenomenology, cognition, practice, extended mind) and finds it still **anthropocentric** and shy of materials analysis — the gap that licenses Chapters 3–9.

### Thing–thing and thing–human dependence (Ch. 3–4)

TT dependence: fire tool-chains; plaster/clay sequences at Çatalhöyük; wheel and car part assemblies; heterogeneous nodes of matter–energy–information; non-redundant pins that drop whole systems. [[affordance|Affordances]] (Gibson via Knappett) point both ways; mutual fit can tip into unequal **dependency** (deeper ploughing depletes nutrients). Behavioral chains and *chaînes opératoires* sequence connections through procurement–manufacture–use–maintenance–discard.

TH dependence: things fall apart and require human care. Unfired mudbrick walls at Çatalhöyük appear permanent only through modern conservation chemicals; ancient inhabitants faced the same falling walls. Things have **primary agency** from vibrancy and lives of their own, not only secondary agency delegated by humans (contra pure Gell/Latour readings). Domestication is co-dependency: non-shattering rachis traps farmers into labor; sheep/goat care traps humans into pens, wolves, distances, milk/dung/wool investments. Refuse buildup forces organized disposal. Society depends on the **work** that produces the appearance of stability — not on inert durability alone.

### The formula (Ch. 5)

> Entanglement = (HT) + (TT) + (TH) + (HH)

HH is often taken for granted by social science; Hodder includes it but spends the book rebalancing toward TT and TH. He rejects reducing entanglement to network, meshwork, material engagement, equipmental totality, or chain alone — those tools help but underplay **sticky entrapment**, historical debt, and independent material process (Pleistocene ice melt; salts on church stone). Path dependence (Pierson, David) is close kin: exit costs rise; linked infrastructure locks paths; entanglement adds practical material interlacing and heterogeneous values.

Entanglements are **not cultures or systems**: they may be partial, local, or hyper-connected; open-ended; heterogeneous (beliefs and bricks in one web). Degrees matter — sailboat pin vs rowing eight; cereal vs early sheep; pebble vs serpentine bead — but semiotic thickness can invert material simplicity. Cores and peripheries of entanglement; forgetness as social “invisibility” of domesticates until other strands shift.

### Fittingness (Ch. 6–7)

[[fittingness]] joins functional [[affordance]] to acceptability/coherence (abstraction, metaphor, mimesis, bodily resonance). Nested fit (Porter’s mutually reinforcing activities; van der Leeuw paste spaces). Sound symbolism and Bourdieu redirected toward bodily coherence with affordances. Evolutionary claim: traits persist by **fittingness within taut entanglements**, not solely reproductive fitness or replicative success. Piano absent from Mesolithic because the whole industrial–musical entanglement is absent; wheel fails without draught animals and roads. Çatalhöyük pottery: undecorated cooking pots fitting with sheep/goat fat processing and separation from wild-animal history symbolism; unique face pot (human faces without eyes + bull heads) never copied — wrong medium; later West Mound decoration proliferates when houses, hearths, and productive display reorganize.

### Contingency and directionality (Ch. 8)

“Things happen” — unruliness from mismatched temporalities; open complex systems; catalysis and untying. **Irony of entanglement:** transformative side wins long-term, but entrapment directs change locally and globally **toward greater entanglement**. Sunk costs and Concorde fallacies; ship-momentum metaphors; difficulty of going back (Natufian after Younger Dryas; Çatalhöyük after wild-cattle decline — cannot simply return 400–500 years to clay balls, smaller houses, pre-pot cooking). Rate-of-change paradox: more complex entanglements can change *faster*, not only more rigidly.

### Temporality, structure, and the “revenge of the blob” (Ch. 10)

Hodder argues entanglement is **fundamentally about time**. Network and systems analysis track links and feedback; entanglement adds temporal weight — debts, duties, investments, schedules, bottlenecks, waiting for things to happen. Etymology is enlisted: *depend* as “hang down” (pendulum) — nodes weighed down by commitments. House purchase is the micro-example: mortgage choice and roof repair lean upstream and downstream; structure is not only rules and codes but **emergent webs of dependence** with duration. Unacknowledged conditions and unintended consequences (Bourdieu, Giddens) move from margin to center: messiness is constitutive (Thomas’s colonial “practically muddled” symbolic world).

On the charge that the theory is special pleading by archaeologists who professionally deal with material “blobs,” Hodder answers that global warming, oil limits, terrorism’s brute force, and nostalgic desire for tactile things in virtual lives all force a wider “return to things.” He denies material determination: starting point remains human need, dialectically awakened by things; entanglements are **heterogeneous**, not reducible to practical necessity alone.

### Ethics (Ch. 10)

Object nature of things is ethically load-bearing: pure semiotics undermines things’ power to entrap the vulnerable (AIDS, chains, child-rearing, global agriculture — his list). Species-level dependence on made things has one long intensifying movement since the first tool and fire. “Sustainable” fixing often adds regulation and further entanglement. Moral horizon Hodder sketches (not wiki-voice fact): possibly changing what it is to be human — a position, not a finding.

**Honest hits:** The program organizes heterogeneous evidence (geoarchaeology, fauna, pottery residues, architecture, symbolism) under one dependence language better than meaning-only or efficiency-only frames. The clay extraction “hole” and reed/*Phragmites* feedback is a strong multi-proxy illustration of TT/TH cascade. The face-pot vs West Mound decoration contrast is a crisp negative case for transmission-alone stories: creativity without fit does not copy. The “cannot go back” reconstruction after wild-cattle decline (pots, house size, obsidian routes, history-making) is a historically specific path-dependence narrative of the sort archaeology can actually argue.

**Worked sequence — pottery and houses (Ch. 7, condensed):** Early thick organic-tempered pots scarce and little used over fire; cooking via heated clay balls. Level VII: cooking pottery rises with sandier bricks, new clay sources/off-site brick production, mineral-inclusion fabrics, residues of sheep/goat products. Cooking pots multi-task (Atalay ethno/experimental comparison: balls need continual monitoring; pots act as more autonomous “delegates”). Decoration stay off cooking ware while wild animals dominate symbolism and burial platforms. One face pot (eyeless human faces + bull heads) appears in midden, never transmitted. Later/West Mound: larger multi-room houses, central hearth, serving/display pottery, adult burial shifts, less house-on-house history repetition — decoration proliferates for centuries. Continuities of tradition exist, but Hodder locates the selector in co-fitting of house, hearth, economy, and medium — not in reproductive success of potters.

**Honest misses / limits:** HH and power are thinner than HT/TT; modern analogies risk presentism; “degree of entanglement” resists clean measurement; ethical species-narrative outruns the case studies. Causal confidence on wild-cattle landscape availability is explicitly uncertain in the book. Replication status of particular Çatalhöyük co-fitting chains belongs on site and specialist literature pages as those sources are ingested.

## Method Commitments

1. **Symmetry without erasure of objectness** — take things seriously without pure Latourian dissolution of material constraints.
2. **Chain and life-history methods** — operational chains, behavioral chains, [[life-history-method]], type sequences, legacies.
3. **Tanglegrams** — arrows as “depends on”; one-way enabling vs two-way co-dependence; filled rectangles for human-dependent nodes; partial by design.
4. **Archaeometry inside theory** — clays, tempers, residues, phytoliths, micromorphology as theoretical evidence, not appendices.
5. **Open systems** — diagrams are schemas; unforeseen variables always possible.
6. **Fittingness analysis** — ask why a trait was copied or not given the whole web (face pot vs West Mound ware).
7. **Long-term and macro** — rework evolutionary and complex-systems tools for directionality and rate-of-change without reducing to reproductive success.

### Worked method: the Çatalhöyük clay tanglegram (Ch. 9)

Hodder and Chris Doherty’s Figure 9.2 is the book’s flagship method demonstration. Starting from clay for houses, threads pull in midden, mortar, painting, pigment landscape, burial, dogs, groundstone, cooking balls, wood, memory-making heads, and more. Conventions:

- **One-way arrow** — often enabling dependence (midden depends on clay spread; painting depends on house, not vice versa).
- **Two-way arrow** — co-dependence often constraining (houses ↔ middens: refuse constitutes middens but builds up against walls; cooking ↔ clay balls; groundstone ↔ landscape of trees and rock).
- **Filled rectangles** — nodes with marked human dependence; open circles for more self-regenerating landscape nodes (still affected by humans in degree).
- **Heterogeneous reasons** — house depends on heads for memory *and* on mudbrick for fabric *and* on mats for cleanliness; no a priori causal hierarchy.

Quantitative landscape punchline: East Mound volume ~750,000 m³ / ~1.13 million metric tons; clay volume estimate ~675,000 m³; extraction pits ~1 m deep imply an area on the order of an 800 m square — a “hole” later partly built over by the West Mound, altering water flow, colluvium, and (with water-filled pits) *Phragmites* proliferation and further human intervention. Method moral: follow TT/TH strings and “a very large part of the whole social and material system gets drawn in.” Caveat: the diagram is partial by design; fire-lighting schemas already showed open systems never list all variables.

Adjacent methods Hodder folds in: spatial mapping of associations; operational chains and life histories; type sequences as battle-ship/spindle curves reinterpreted through web costs of going back; origins-of-agriculture narratives as long-term entanglement rather than single invention events.

## Critiques and Responses

### External positions as represented in the book

- **Network is enough (ANT):** Hodder replies that network/mesh underplays dependency’s stickiness and material processes that are not only mediations.
- **Fitness is enough (evolutionary archaeology/HBE):** Replies that reproductive/replicative success, when present, are *explained by* fittingness in webs, not the other way around.
- **Phenomenology of landscape (Tilley strand):** With Barrett and Ko, Hodder marks some modern-embodiment-as-past-experience claims as un-Heideggerian — a side skirmish about authentic equipmental worldhood.
- **Human sociality is primary (HH-first social science):** Ch. 10 considers turning the book around to start from HH; rejects reducing material strings to metaphors for human relations alone.

### Self-critique (Ch. 10)

Hodder lists what the scheme leaves awkward: HH underplay; power and agency thinner than material strings; tension between “objective” tanglegram and positioned interest; reductive clarification vs full complexity; quantification vs context; definition of thing. He ends by embracing **messiness** as feature: lives are struggle in webs of demand and potential, often unclear.

> "But the loose ends can never be neatly tied up – if they could it would be possible for there to be an end to entanglement. But humans cannot exist without things."

### Ethical critique risk

The species-level story of ever-increasing entanglement can be read as totalizing or fatalistic. Hodder’s own response is dual: short-term fixing is the human impulse *and* may worsen the trap; recognition raises ethical stakes without delivering a policy recipe. Treat as **position**, not finding.

## What Survived

Even where “entanglement theory” is not named as a school, the following have durable afterlives in archaeology and material-culture studies (as framed from the 2012 charter and vault context):

1. **Dependence/dependency language** for human–thing lock-in (domestication, infrastructure, maintenance).
2. **Tanglegram / thread-tracing** as a heuristic alongside *chaîne opératoire* and behavioral chains.
3. **Fittingness** as a middle path between pure meaning and pure optimality for trait persistence.
4. **Object-side insistence** that archaeometry and decay belong in theory, not only specialist appendices — continuous with reflexive multi-specialist fieldwork at Çatalhöyük.
5. **Path dependence and “digging holes”** metaphors for irreversible investment in prehistory (Natufian, farming, urban metabolism).
6. **Bridge role** between interpretive archaeology and evolutionary/complex-systems toolkits without full conversion to either.

What is less secure: the four-term formula as liturgy; the species-ethical climax; any claim that entanglement replaces rather than re-describes neighboring frameworks.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

**Status:** active research program (2012 charter). In this vault, densified from one primary text; reception literature, later Hodder restatements, and critical responses remain acquisition targets.

**Frontiers suggested by the book itself:**

- Fuller HH and power analyses inside entanglement frames (inequality of who is trapped by which strings).
- Comparative tanglegrams across scales (forager vs Neolithic vs industrial) without collapsing to teleology.
- Formal links to path-dependence economics and complex-systems metrics without losing historical specificity.
- Independent reanalysis of Çatalhöyük cattle–pottery–house co-fitting claims as more specialist volumes are ingested.
- Colonial and global-south entanglements (Thomas strand) with equal object-side rigor.
