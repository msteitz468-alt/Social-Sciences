## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[omi-michael|Michael Omi]] and [[winant-howard|Howard Winant]] published the first edition in 1986, amid exhaustion of civil-rights-era liberal race sociology and the rise of neoconservative “colorblind” politics. The third edition (2015) is not a paint job: the authors describe needing to knock walls down after two decades of demographic change, evisceration of early postwar civil-rights legacies, colorblind/neoliberal hegemony, and the Obama years.

The program’s foil is threefold (Part I):

1. **[[ethnicity-paradigm|Ethnicity paradigm]]** — race as culture/assimilation/inclusion into a universal nationality (Chicago ethnicity theory, postwar liberalism, Glazer–Moynihan, and neoconservative successors).
2. **Class paradigm** — market, stratification, and class-conflict reductions of racism to economics (from neoclassical “taste for discrimination” through Wilson-type underclass frames to Marxist race/class debates).
3. **Nation paradigm** — race as peoplehood/nationalism (white nation-building; black, Chicano, and Third World nationalisms; internal colonialism).

Each paradigm contributes, they argue, and each fails as a total theory of race. Against biological race science and against treating race as mere illusion, they insist race is a fundamental, ongoing sociohistorical process.

They also write against classical social theory’s relative silence on race (Marx, Durkheim, Weber focused on European feudalism→capitalism) while recovering black intellectual traditions (Du Bois and others) that treated race as central.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Race: not essence, not illusion

Race is “making up people” (Hacking) but not benign othering alone — it organizes domination and resistance. Social construction is widely accepted yet insufficient as a stopping point: race includes technologies of exploitation *and* of resistance. In the U.S., they treat race as a **master category** historically shaping class definition, consciousness, and organization — without reducing class (or gender) to race.

> “Historically in the United States, race has provided a master category for understanding the definition of class and the patterns of class consciousness, mobilization, and organization.”

### Racialization

> “We define racialization as the extension of racial meaning to a previously racially unclassified relationship, social practice, or group.”

Core constitutive element: imparting social and symbolic meaning to perceived phenotypical differences. Operates at macro and micro scales; state classifications (census, law) and group self-identification from below both matter.

### Racial projects

Racial meaning sits at the **crossroads of social structure and cultural representation**. A [[racial-project|racial project]] is simultaneously an interpretation of racial dynamics and an effort to reorganize resources along particular racial lines. Projects operate across scales and “travel”; competing projects maintain or challenge the racial system.

### Racial formation

> “Racial formation, therefore, is a synthesis, a constantly reiterated outcome, of the interaction of racial projects on a society-wide level.”

Formation is process, not a one-time event: categories are created, inhabited, transformed, and destroyed.

### Racism beyond intent

Racism is not reducible to individual hate. Post–civil rights formations can produce domination “without perpetrators” in the classical sense; structures and projects matter. (Formal “racist project iff…” criteria are flagged incomplete in OCR of mid-chapter pages — do not invent a formula not cleanly recovered.)

### The racial state

Race is preeminently political. The state defines race (census, law, rights) and is always a racial state — terrain of despotism and democracy, hegemony and war of position (Gramscian vocabulary). Movements and the state co-produce racial trajectories.

### Colorblindness as hegemonic project

In the early 21st century U.S., they argue, the hegemonic concept of race is colorblind ideology: race-consciousness is cast as itself racist; civil rights goals are declared achieved by ignoring race. Colorblindness underwrites and converges with neoliberalism. Obama heightens rather than resolves the tensions of colorblind hegemony.

## Method Commitments

- Paradigm critique (intellectual history + policy orientation)
- Concept construction (formation, project, racialization)
- Historical periodization of racial politics (trajectory)
- Gramscian hegemony / rearticulation
- State-centered political sociology of race
- Engaged anti-racist scholarship (authors’ stated commitments)

## Major Debates and Rivals

1. **Ethnicity vs formation** — assimilation/inclusion vs durable racial domination.
2. **Class vs race primacy** — reduction vs co-constitution (race as master category in U.S. without erasing class).
3. **Nation/peoplehood** — white nationalism vs oppressed nationalisms vs formation’s multi-project view.
4. **Colorblindness** — anti-racist achievement vs hegemonic containment of radical democracy.
5. **Intersectionality** — third edition acknowledges race/class/gender; residual tension over “master category” language.

## Critiques and Responses

- Overbreadth of “racial project.”
- U.S. provincialism vs global race (Winant’s later work partially addresses — not ingested).
- Relation to CRT and Bonilla-Silva’s color-blind racism (parallel programs).
- Whether Obama era disproves or confirms colorblind hegemony (authors: heightens tensions).

Authors respond by treating colorblindness as *verisimilitude-dependent* hegemony — a “rude beast” that works only insofar as it can claim civil-rights success — and by insisting race remains a political terrain of rearticulation (“Can it happen again?”).

## What Survived

- **Racial formation** and **racial project** as standard sociology vocabulary.
- Critique of ethnicity-only race theory.
- **Racialization** as process term.
- Colorblindness as object of critical race sociology (multi-author field).
- Teaching of race as structure + representation, not prejudice alone.
- Linking of neoliberalism and racial politics.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

**Active.** Core program still organizes U.S. race courses and research. Frontiers: comparative/global racial formation; digital racial projects; refined intersectional integration; empirical measurement of projects; rearticulation after Trump-era open racial politics (post-dates 2015 edition).

Collection: only 3rd ed. OCR ingested; 1st/2nd eds., Bonilla-Silva, CRT monographs, Winant *World Is a Ghetto* not yet ingested.
