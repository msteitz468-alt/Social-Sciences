## Intellectual Origins and Context

Critical Theory is the intellectual program of the [[frankfurt-school|Institut für
Sozialforschung]], named and defined by [[horkheimer-max|Max Horkheimer]] in his
1937 essay "Traditional and Critical Theory." It is best understood as **Western
Marxism after the failure of the revolution** — an attempt to retain Marx's
critique of capitalism, ideology, and reification while abandoning economic
determinism, the vanguard party, and (increasingly) the proletariat as the
guaranteed agent of history.

Three defeats set its problem. First, the failure of the 1918–19 German revolution
and the *non*-arrival of proletarian revolution in the advanced West, which
discredited orthodox Marxism's economic prophecy. Second, the rise of fascism —
which the working class did not prevent and in part supported. Third, the discovery,
in American exile, of an *administered* mass society in which affluence and the
[[culture-industry]] integrated opposition without terror. Against this backdrop the
program reacted against three things at once: **positivism** (as the affirmative
acceptance of the given), **orthodox/Soviet "scientific" Marxism** (as itself a
species of the instrumental thinking it should oppose), and the **reduction of
reason to instrumental calculation** in modern philosophy and science.

Its resources were correspondingly plural: Hegelian dialectics (the "power of the
negative," determinate negation), Marx (commodity fetishism, reification, ideology
critique), Freud (the instinctual economy, repression, projection),
[[weber-max|Weber]] ([[rationalization]] and the [[iron-cage|iron cage]]), and
Husserl's critique of Galilean science. Lukács's *History and Class Consciousness*
(1923) — reification as the master category of capitalist society — is the immediate
predecessor.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

Six load-bearing claims, worked through, all attributed.

**1. Theory is not neutral; critical theory is reflexive and emancipatory.**
Horkheimer's founding distinction (1937): "traditional" theory models itself on
natural science, takes the categories of the existing order as given, and thereby
reproduces that order; "critical" theory reflects on its own social conditions of
production and is oriented to human emancipation. [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]] radicalizes
this: critical theory "refuses to accept the given universe of facts as the final
context of validation," measuring what is against "its arrested and denied
possibilities."

**2. The critique of [[instrumental-reason|instrumental reason]].** Reason has been
reduced to the calculation of means, indifferent to ends. In *Dialectic of
Enlightenment*: "power and knowledge are synonymous"; reason becomes "the mere
instrument of the all-inclusive economic apparatus." Horkheimer's *Eclipse of
Reason* (1947) names this "subjective reason," the atrophy of an objective reason
that could still ask about ends.

**3. The [[dialectic-of-enlightenment]] thesis.** "Myth is already enlightenment,
and enlightenment reverts to mythology." Enlightenment carries [[domination-of-nature|domination]]
in its structure from the start and, carried to its end, turns against the subject
and lapses into a new barbarism. This is the program's philosophy of history and its
most contested move.

**4. The integration/containment thesis.** Advanced industrial society "contains"
qualitative change: the [[culture-industry]] manufactures conformity and
[[false-needs]], so domination operates through "affluence, technology, and
administered satisfaction rather than overt coercion" (see [[one-dimensionality]],
[[repressive-desublimation]]).

**5. Fascism as the limit-case, not the antithesis, of Enlightenment.** In the
"Elements of Anti-Semitism" excursus, anti-Semitism's "'irrationalism' is deduced
from the nature of the dominant *ratio* itself"; [[false-projection]] is the paranoid
inversion of [[mimesis]], the mimetic impulse civilization represses.

**6. The crisis of the revolutionary subject.** With the working class integrated,
critical theory retains "no demonstrable agent of change" and is "thrown back to a
high level of abstraction" (Marcuse; see [[revolutionary-subject-debate]]).

The conceptual scaffolding holding these together is the pairing of
[[instrumental-reason]] with its repressed other, [[mimesis]], and the method of
**determinate negation** — rejecting false absolutes without erecting a new one.

## The Empirical Program

Critical Theory's "empirical" work is diagnostic-interpretive rather than
hypothesis-testing: readings of culture, language, the instincts, science, and myth
deployed to expose domination concealed as rational necessity. Its illustrations —
Hollywood cinema, commercial radio, jazz, opinion polling, Sade's novels, the
Odyssey, Nazi propaganda — are marshalled for the argument, **not offered as
independent findings**; the school is in principle hostile to isolating "facts" from
the social totality that produces them.

The exception is the Institut's genuinely empirical strand: the *Studien über
Autorität und Familie* (1936) and, in exile, the *Studies in Prejudice* series
culminating in *The Authoritarian Personality* (1950), which operationalized
authoritarian dispositions via the F-scale. This strand sits in tension with the
philosophical critique of positivist social science and was itself criticized on
sampling and method grounds.

## Method Commitments

- **Dialectical, "negative" thinking** — holding "what is" against "what could be";
  the refusal of premature positivity. Adorno's *Negative Dialectics* makes this
  programmatic: thought must resist the "identity thinking" that subsumes the
  particular under the concept.
- **Determinate negation** — rejecting false absolutes ("idols") without collapsing
  into dogmatic rigorism or empty skepticism; "dialectic interprets every image as
  writing," reading the confession of its own falsity out of each.
- **Immanent critique** — measuring a society or a work against its own unrealized
  possibilities, rather than against an external standard.
- **Reflexivity** — the theory's constitutive awareness of its own social conditions.
- **Interdisciplinarity** — philosophy, sociology, economics, and psychoanalysis
  fused, on the premise that no single discipline grasps the totality.
- **The essay and the fragment as forms** — *Dialectic of Enlightenment* is
  deliberately "fragmentary," a form the authors defend as itself resistant to
  systematizing, identity-thinking reason.

## Major Debates and Rivals

Against **positivism** and empiricist social science: the running polemic that "the
worship of facts" is the affirmative acceptance of the given (later the German
*Positivismusstreit*, Adorno vs. Popper, 1961). Against **orthodox Marxism**: the
charge that Soviet "scientific" Marxism reproduces instrumental reason and treats
necessity as permanent — Adorno and Horkheimer argue socialism "held on all too
surely to the legacy of bourgeois philosophy" by taking the domination of nature as
an unrevisable basis. Against **Heidegger** and the "jargon of authenticity": the
rejection of an ontology of Being as ideological consolation. And the decisive
**internal** debate — Habermas vs. the first generation — treated below and on
[[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]].

## Critiques and Responses

Each critique as a position (attributed):

- **Performative contradiction (Habermas).** By indicting reason *as such* as
  instrumental, *Dialectic of Enlightenment* undermines the rational ground of its
  own critique. Habermas's response is reconstructive, not dismissive: distinguish
  instrumental from **communicative rationality** and locate emancipation in
  uncoerced understanding. This is the major internal reconstruction and the reason
  the program survived into a second generation.
- **Totalizing pessimism / over-integration.** The system is depicted as seamlessly
  closed, with no agent of change — a functionalism of domination. Marcuse's own
  post-1968 writings (*An Essay on Liberation*) relax the closure; the first
  generation largely did not.
- **Élitism.** The theorist decides which needs are [[false-needs|false]] and
  dismisses popular culture (Adorno on jazz) from a mandarin height.
- **Empirical thinness / method.** The cultural diagnoses rest on illustration, not
  systematic evidence; *The Authoritarian Personality* was faulted on sampling and
  acquiescence bias.
- **History over-reach.** The line from Odysseus through positivism to Auschwitz is
  too total to discriminate among historically distinct rationalities.

## What Survived

Even where the first generation's system is treated as a period piece, its concepts
are pervasive:

- **The [[culture-industry]]** and [[pseudo-individualization]] are foundational to
  media and cultural sociology, even for scholars who reject Adorno's verdict; they
  read as newly apt for algorithmic recommendation and platform monopoly.
- **The critique of [[instrumental-reason|instrumental]]/[[technological-rationality|technological
  rationality]]** underwrites the critical philosophy of science and technology and
  much ecological political theory (the [[domination-of-nature]] as prescient of
  environmental critique).
- **Habermas's communicative turn** carried the program into discourse ethics,
  deliberative democracy, and the theory of the public sphere — the largest living
  branch.
- **The "critical" prefix** now spans critical race theory, feminist theory, and
  postcolonial theory, generalizing the reflexive/emancipatory stance far beyond the
  original Marxist frame.
- **Axel Honneth's recognition theory** reconstructs the program a third time around
  intersubjective recognition rather than reason or labor.
- **Adorno's aesthetics and negative dialectics** remain live in philosophy and art
  theory as a model of non-dominative, "mimetic" cognition.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active`. Critical Theory runs into a third and fourth generation at the
reconstituted Frankfurt Institut (Honneth and successors) and, more diffusely,
across the humanities and social sciences worldwide. Active frontiers: recognition
and its critics; the theory of the public sphere under digital media; the revival of
the [[culture-industry]] critique for the platform economy; critical theory of
technology and of climate; and continued contest over whether the first generation's
totalizing critique of reason or Habermas's communicative reconstruction is the more
defensible foundation (see [[dialectic-of-enlightenment-debate]]). This wiki's
coverage rests on two first-generation primary texts —
*[[horkheimer-adorno-dialectic-of-enlightenment-1947|Dialectic of Enlightenment]]*
and *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* — and will deepen as
Horkheimer's essays and Habermas are ingested.
