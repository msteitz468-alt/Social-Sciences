# Practice Theory — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. The [[practice-theory|theories/ summary]] owns core-claims/origins/proponents/critiques/status at normal resolution; this page owns the depth. Reciprocal: summary links here by full path; this page's `theory_page` points back to [[practice-theory]]. Primary base: [[bourdieu-pierre|Pierre Bourdieu]]'s *[[bourdieu-outline-1977|Outline of a Theory of Practice]]* (French 1972; Nice English 1977) — the **founding statement**, now ingested — and *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]* (French 1980; Nice English 1990), its expanded reworking. All programmatic claims are in **attributed voice** — this is theory, not established finding. Parallel program now primary-ingested: [[structuration-theory|Giddens structuration]] (*[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|Constitution]]*, 1984). > **Outline (1972) vs Logic (1980) — provenance note.** The two books share most of the apparatus; *Logic* is the rewrite of *Outline*, "carrying the argument further" (translator's foreword) on practical logic and symbolic capital. Where this page cites the canonical formulations, the concept *originates in Outline*: the habitus definition (Outline ch. 2, keyed to "environment" rather than "conditions of existence"), [[doxa]]/orthodoxy/heterodoxy and the theory of symbolic power (Outline ch. 4), [[symbolic-capital]] and the modes-of-domination thesis (Outline ch. 4), the [[strategy-vs-rule|strategy-not-rule]] critique and "the fallacies of the rule" (Outline ch. 1). The English 1977 *Outline*, not a straight translation of the 1972 French, already incorporates Bourdieu's mid-1970s revisions.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Practice theory, in the form Bourdieu elaborates in *The Logic of Practice*, is a product of the **Critical Turn** (1968–1990) that rewrote both sociology and anthropology after the peak of [[structural-functionalism]] and high structuralism. Bourdieu situates himself in a French intellectual field structured by the confrontation of **Lévi-Strauss** and **Sartre** — a generation turning to the social sciences, he recalls, to reconcile scientific vocation with ethical-political vocation as "militant craftsmanship, as remote from pure science as from exemplary prophecy." The program is not a free-floating epistemology: it was forged in colonial Algeria, statistical survey work at the Algiers Institute of Statistics, and long ethnographic engagement with [[kabyle|Kabyle]] society under conditions of war and independence struggle.

Bourdieu's own intellectual itinerary is part of the program's content. He describes the 1963/1970 Kabyle-house article published in honour of Lévi-Strauss as "perhaps the last work I wrote as a blissful structuralist." Progress in the social sciences, he holds, "presupposes progress in our knowledge of the conditions of knowledge" — hence the book's persistent return to objects already treated in *Outline of a Theory of Practice* and secondarily *Distinction*, each doubling-back an opportunity to objectify more completely the objective and subjective relation to the object. Theoretical theses, he insists, would "lose [their] meaning and [their] effectiveness" if dissociated from the practice that produced them and left "in the unreal, neutralized mode of existence which is that of theoretical 'theses'."

The program **reacted against** a short list of load-bearing alternatives:

1. **Objectivism / structuralism.** Social physics that establishes regularities independent of consciousnesses, then mistakes the model constructed *to account for* practice for the *principle of* practice. Structuralist kinship formalisms (the "algebra of kinship"), synoptic calendars, genealogies, and maps are the privileged instruments of this error.
2. **Subjectivism.** Sartrean philosophies of pure consciousness (each action an antecedent-less confrontation of subject and world); phenomenological social science that describes the taken-for-granted without stating its conditions of possibility; rational-actor economics that universalizes a "rational" habitus available only to those with the requisite capitals.
3. **Legalism.** The language of rule, norm, prescription, and jural custom as the explanatory principle of kinship, succession, land transfer, and honour — "the obstacle par excellence to the construction of an adequate theory of practice."
4. **Economism narrowly construed.** The reduction of all interest to monetary profit, which cannot grasp symbolic capital, gift exchange, or the economy of honour.

Against Marx-as-economism, Bourdieu credits **Weber** with helping him to a "generalized materialism" that carries materialist thought into areas Marxist materialism abandons to spiritualism — the economy of symbolic capital, exchanges between sexes and generations. Against Lévi-Strauss's universal logical categories of the unconscious mind, he asserts schemes of thought formed through the dialectic of social structures and structured, structuring dispositions. Against Sartre, he insists on durable dispositions and "meaning-made-body." The program thus sits at the hinge of [[structure-vs-agency-debate|structure vs. agency]]: neither pure structure nor free agency, but the relation between objective structures and incorporated dispositions.

Colonial production context is not external decoration. Bourdieu first excluded Kabyle ritual as an object likely to reinforce racist representations; then, from 1958, he tried "to retrieve it from the false solicitude of primitivism and to challenge the racist contempt which, through the self-contempt it induces in its victims, helps to deny them knowledge and recognition of their own tradition." Seed-jar photographs of high quality without flash, he notes, were possible because the French army had destroyed the house's roof when expelling its occupants — a flag of "misplaced" *libido sciendi* in wartime ethnography. Every genuine sociological undertaking, he claims, is inseparably a **socio-analysis**.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The architecture is a set of interlocking concepts that Bourdieu holds must be thought together. None is freestanding.

### 1. The double break (objectivism and subjectivism)

Bourdieu argues that of all oppositions artificially dividing social science, "the most fundamental, and the most ruinous, is the one that is set up between subjectivism and objectivism." Both modes of knowledge are indispensable; neither can reduce social science to social phenomenology or social physics. Moving beyond the antagonism while preserving gains from each requires making explicit the presuppositions they share *as theoretical modes of knowledge*, both equally opposed to the practical mode of knowledge underlying ordinary experience of the social world.

Social science must not only, as objectivism would have it, break with native experience and native representation of that experience, but also, by a **second break**, call into question presuppositions inherent in the position of the "objective" observer — who tends to bring into the object the principles of his relation to the object. Taking a viewpoint on practice constitutes it as object and alters it: "A much more fundamental alteration … is performed on practice by the sheer fact of taking up a 'viewpoint' on it and so constituting it as an object (of observation and analysis)." The aim is not rehabilitation of practice against theory, but "to bring to light the theory of practice which theoretical knowledge implicitly applies and so to make possible a truly scientific knowledge of practice."

### 2. Habitus

[[habitus]] is the load-bearing concept. Bourdieu defines it as systems of durable, transposable dispositions:

> "the conditionings associated with a particular class of conditions of existence produce habitus, systems of durable, transposable dispositions, structured structures predisposed to function as structuring structures, that is, as principles which generate and organize practices and representations that can be objectively adapted to their outcomes without presupposing a conscious aiming at ends or an express mastery of the operations necessary in order to attain them."

Several corollaries follow, each attributed to Bourdieu:

- **Regulated improvisation without rules.** Practices generated by habitus are "objectively 'regulated' and 'regular' without being in any way the product of obedience to rules," and "can be collectively orchestrated without being the product of the organizing action of a conductor."
- **Infinite yet limited generative capacity.** Habitus makes possible the production of all thoughts, perceptions, and actions inherent in the particular conditions of its production — *and only those*. The concept aims to transcend the antinomies of determinism/freedom, conditioning/creativity, consciousness/unconscious, individual/society.
- **Virtue made of necessity.** The most improbable practices are excluded as unthinkable by immediate submission to order that inclines agents "to refuse what is anyway denied and to will the inevitable."
- **History producing more history.** Habitus is "a product of history" that "produces individual and collective practices — more history." Past experiences deposited as schemes guarantee correctness of practices "more reliably than all formal rules and explicit norms."
- **Two states of the social world.** Practices cannot be deduced from present conditions alone or past conditions alone; they require relating the social conditions in which the habitus was constituted to those in which it is implemented — the interrelationship the habitus performs while concealing it.
- **Class and homology.** A social class (in-itself) is simultaneously a class of similar conditions of existence and a class of individuals having the same habitus; singular habitus within a class are structural variants united in homology.
- **Hysteresis.** When dispositions are ill-adjusted to present objective chances (Marx's Don Quixote), the past in false anticipation of the future is negatively sanctioned — the clearest case of habitus out of phase with conditions.
- **Objective meaning without subjective intention.** Habitus is "the source of these strings of 'moves' which are objectively organized as strategies without being the product of a genuine strategic intention."

Habitus enables institutions to attain full realization: "it is through the capacity for incorporation … that the king, the banker or the priest are hereditary monarchy, financial capitalism or the Church made flesh."

### 3. Practical sense and the logic of practice

[[practical-sense|Practical sense]] (*sens pratique*) is quasi-bodily involvement in the world without representation of body or world. Bourdieu's preferred analogy is sport's "feel for the game" — almost miraculous encounter between habitus and field:

> "Practical sense is a quasi-bodily involvement in the world which presupposes no representation either of the body or of the world, still less of their relationship. … A particularly clear example of practical sense as a proleptic adjustment to the demands of a field is what is called, in the language of sport, a 'feel for the game'."

Practice, he holds, "has a logic which is not that of the logician." One must not ask of it more logic than it can give. The principle of the **economy of logic**: no more logic is mobilized than practice requires; generative schemes are interchangeable; products are systematic but with approximate, fuzzy coherence that cannot withstand logical criticism. "It is because agents never know completely what they are doing that what they do has more sense than they know."

**Time** is constitutive. Practice unfolds in time and has irreversibility, rhythm, tempo, and directionality that synchronization destroys. Gift exchange and honour jousts derive properties from construction in time that detemporalizing science has least chance of reconstituting. "To reintroduce uncertainty is to reintroduce time … substituting the dialectic of strategies for the mechanics of the model, but without falling over into the imaginary anthropology of 'rational actor' theories." Instruments of totalization — calendar, genealogy, map — create *ex nihilo* relations of simultaneity and symmetry among reference-points never brought face to face in practice. The generative formula that reproduces essential features of practices treated as *opus operatum* is not the generative principle of practices, the *modus operandi*.

### 4. Doxa, hexis, illusio

[[doxa]] is not a state of mind but a state of the body: "the relationship of immediate adherence that is established in practice between a habitus and the field to which it is attuned, the pre-verbal taking-for-granted of the world that flows from practical sense." Practical faith is the condition of entry every [[social-field|field]] tacitly imposes — undisputed, pre-reflexive compliance with the field's fundamental presuppositions.

[[hexis|Bodily hexis]] is "political mythology realized, em-bodied, turned into a permanent disposition, a durable way of standing, speaking, walking, and thereby of feeling and thinking." What is learned by body is not something one *has* but something one *is*: "The body believes in what it plays at: it weeps if it mimes grief." Among the Kabyle as Bourdieu documented them, male/female opposition was realized in posture — *lah'ia* (modesty) orienting the female body downward/inward, *nif* (point of honour) asserted upward/outward toward other men.

[[illusio]] is investment in the game: in social fields that are games "in themselves" and not "for themselves," "one does not embark on the game by a conscious act, one is born into the game, with the game," and the relation of investment is more total for being unaware of what it is.

### 5. Capitals, misrecognition, symbolic violence

Bourdieu generalizes the economy of practices: "There is an economy of practices, a reason immanent in practices, whose 'origin' lies neither in the 'decisions' of reason understood as rational calculation nor in the determinations of mechanisms external to and superior to the agents." The economy described by economic theory is a particular case of a universe of economies — fields of struggle differing in stakes and forms of capital.

[[symbolic-capital]] is "denied capital, recognized as legitimate, that is, misrecognized as capital" — credit in the broadest sense, an advance the group's belief grants. [[misrecognition]] is not a cognitive error to be corrected from outside and then discarded; it is part of the full truth of the relations that depend on it. Gift exchange is the paradigm: "the functioning of gift exchange presupposes individual and collective misrecognition of the truth of the objective 'mechanism' of the exchange"; the counter-gift must be deferred and different; the interval is an instrument of denial allowing subjective and objective truths to coexist.

[[symbolic-violence]] is "censored, euphemized, that is, misrecognizable, recognized violence" — gentle, invisible, "chosen as much as undergone." In pre-capitalist economies especially, relations of domination can only be set up and maintained through strategies that must be disguised if they are not to destroy themselves by revealing their nature. Soft domination is costly: without objective mechanisms that automatically reproduce the order, dominant agents must work daily, personally, to produce and reproduce conditions of domination. Modes of domination differ by degree of **objectification of capital** — personal, interactional domination versus domination mediated by market, school, and law.

[[cultural-capital]] becomes capital only when inserted into objective relations between the system of economic production and the system producing producers (educational system and family). [[social-capital]] is effective possession of a network of kinship or other relations capable of being mobilized — "Practical kin make marriages; official kin celebrate them."

### 6. Strategy, not rule

The movement from rule to strategy situates analysis at the point of generation of practice. Marriage strategies are inseparable from inheritance, fertility, and educational strategies — the whole set of strategies for biological, cultural, and social reproduction. Agents "obey the impulses of feeling or the injunctions of duty more than the calculations of interest, even when, in doing so, they conform to the economy of the system of constraints … of which their ethical and affective dispositions are the product." Social science makes greatest use of the language of rules precisely where it is most inadequate — in formations where, because objective conditions are constant, practices are largely entrusted to the automatisms of the habitus.

## The Empirical Program

Practice theory's honest empirical record in this source is **Kabyle and Béarn ethnography** plus Algerian survey work — not a multi-case comparative program. Book I states the apparatus; Book II claims to show that the same generative schemes regenerate matrimonial strategies, gift and honour logics, agrarian calendar, sexual division of ritual labour, and the inverted world of the house. What the program explained that rule-and-structure predecessors could not, on Bourdieu's account:

- **Why "preferential" parallel-cousin marriage is often rare yet ideologically central** — a forced choice presented as ideal ("making a virtue of necessity"), not a jural rule or structural algebra; statistics he cites (e.g. Aghbala: ~4% parallel cousin among first marriages of 218 men) underwrite the claim that ordinary marriages within practical kinship predominate.
- **Why gift exchange requires time** — counter-gift deferred and different; interval as instrument of collective misrecognition; tempo as strategic resource.
- **Why kinship looks like a rule-system only to the totalizing observer** — official vs practical kinship; "Practical kin make marriages; official kin celebrate them"; genealogical diagrams put abandoned roads and working paths on the same footing.
- **Why honour expenditures look "economically absurd"** — comprehensive balance-sheet of symbolic profits; defence of symbolic capital can be ruinous in material terms yet rational in the economy of honour.
- **Why ritual is systematically fuzzy yet effective** — economy of logic; scheme transfer across house, day, year, body, marriage, craft; rites as practical mimesis, not logical operations.

Anomalies and limits visible even within Bourdieu's own apparatus: the synoptic illusion he criticizes is hard to avoid when writing a book; Kabyle "hyperbolic realization of male fantasies" is both empirical claim and socio-analytic mirror — risk of over-generalizing from a case chosen for theoretical clarity; Béarn material functions as reverse test of familiarization, not independent multi-site confirmation; *Distinction*'s French class analysis (not yet ingested) is needed before cultural-capital claims can be weighed as findings rather than theoretical extensions.

## Method Commitments

Bourdieu's method is simultaneously ethnographic, statistical, and epistemological.

1. **Objectify the objectifying subject.** Progress requires knowledge of the conditions of knowledge — the scholastic point of view, social distance from necessity, wartime and colonial position of the fieldworker. Every genuine sociological undertaking is a socio-analysis.
2. **Double break.** Break with native experience *and* with the observer's theoretical relation to the object. Retain structuralist tools of objectification without reifying models as generators of action. "If it is to be more than the projection of personal feelings, social science necessarily presupposes the stage of objectification."
3. **Refuse the rule as explanatory principle.** Move from rule to strategy; treat native legalistic vocabulary as data about official truth and second-order strategies of conformity, not as the engine of practice. "Appeal to the 'rule', that last resort of ignorance, makes it possible to avoid drawing up the complete balance-sheet of material and symbolic costs and benefits."
4. **Recover the time of practice.** Tempo, irreversibility, and interval are constitutive. Detemporalizing instruments (calendar, genealogy, map, diagram) must be used with knowledge of the theorization effect they produce.
5. **Think practices as economies of multiple capitals.** Abandon economic/non-economic dichotomy; treat all practices — including those experienced as gratuitous — as economic practices aimed at maximizing material or symbolic profit within field-specific stakes.
6. **Read the body.** Hexis, posture, gait, and inhabited space are primary archives of classification schemes. "Inhabited space — starting with the house — is the privileged site of the objectification of the generative schemes."
7. **Hold double truth.** Subjective experience and objectivist reconstruction must both enter the model; objectivism's curse is that it cannot include the *illusio* and collective denial against which it won its truth. Misrecognition of exploitation is part of the full truth of kinship and class relations that can exist only as misrecognized.

Epistemically, Bourdieu holds that the most coherent model accounting for observed facts is not the principle of the practices it explains; diagrams are valid as logical models and become false when treated as real principles of practices. The logic of practice "can only be grasped through constructs which destroy it as such" unless one considers the effects of instruments of objectification.

## Major Debates and Rivals

Practice theory was defined as much by its rivals as by its positive apparatus. The formal locus for the structure/agency problem is [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

**Structuralist objectivism (Lévi-Strauss and structuralist kinship).** Bourdieu keeps the breakthrough of objectification and rejects the identification of *opus operatum* with *modus operandi*. Parallel patrilateral cousin marriage appears as "a sort of scandal" only within anthropological taxonomies that take exogamy and alliance algebra as universal; he argues one must question the categories producing the unthinkable rather than treat the practice as exception. Genealogies, synoptic calendars, and structural house analyses are instruments that create logical relations never faced in practice. His own 1963 house article stands as internal document of the break.

**Sartrean subjectivism and phenomenology.** Sartre refuses durable dispositions; each action is antecedent-less. Phenomenology describes the taken-for-granted but "excludes the question of the conditions of possibility of this experience, namely the coincidence of the objective structures and the internalized structures." Habitus is Bourdieu's answer to both pure freedom and pure description without genesis.

**Rational-actor / economistic models.** The "rational" habitus is product of particular economic conditions defined by possession of economic and cultural capital required to seize opportunities theoretically available to all. Reasonable actions without reasoned design cannot be understood if the only alternatives are rational calculation and mechanical reaction. Weber's pure model of rational action is not an anthropological description of practice.

**Legalism and "situational analysis."** Efforts to correct structuralism via "context" or "situation" (e.g. Gluckman/Van Velsen; Leach on free choice within systems) remain, Bourdieu argues, locked in the framework of rule and exception. Needham's questions on the language of law are endorsed; Barth and Murphy & Kasdan on parallel-cousin function are faulted for explaining strategies by effects (fission/fusion as "functions") as far from practice as invoking the rule.

**Marxist structuralism (agents as Träger).** Ignoring the dialectic of objective and incorporated structures yields "fetishism of social laws" — Capital or Mode of production as entelechy, history as process without a subject. Bourdieu's generalized materialism is meant to keep capital analysis while refusing agentless structure.

**Functionalism (adjacent rival).** Parsons and [[structural-functionalism]] made order the explanandum via internalized value-orientations; Bourdieu's habitus answers a similar problem of regular practice without conscious rule-following, but through classed conditions of existence, bodily incorporation, and struggle over capitals rather than institutionalized common values and equilibrium. The programs are rivals more than kin, despite surface resemblance on "internalization."

## Critiques and Responses

Critiques below are stated as **positions**; responses are those Bourdieu anticipates in *Logic* or that follow from his apparatus. Secondary critique literature is not yet systematically ingested — expand when those sources arrive.

**Determinism / over-socialization.** Charge: habitus as product of conditions that tends to reproduce those conditions leaves no room for genuine agency, creativity, or historical novelty; "virtue made of necessity" looks like resignation theorized. Bourdieu's response in the text: infinite yet strictly limited generative capacity; regulated improvisation; strategies coping with unforeseen situations; hysteresis as systematic source of misadaptation, revolt, and resignation; personal style as structural variant within homology. Whether this dissolves the charge or relocates freedom inside determination remains contested.

**Residual structuralism.** Charge: generative schemes, homologous oppositions (male/female, wet/dry, inside/outside), and scheme transfer across domains reinstall a structuralist combinatorics under another name. Bourdieu's response: the generative formula of the *opus operatum* is not the *modus operandi*; practical schemes are bodily gymnastics, not axioms; fuzzy polythesis sacrifices logical rigour; the theorization effect produces forced coherence. The Kabyle material's systematicity is both the program's strength and the point of greatest residual-structuralist risk.

**Cryptic economism.** Charge: "economy of practices," maximization of material or symbolic profit, and conversion of capitals reduce culture to interest under another vocabulary. Bourdieu's response: economism recognizes only monetary profit; symbolic capital is misrecognized capital; agents obey feeling and duty more than calculation even when conforming to the economy of constraints; the denied truth surfaces openly only in crises. Critics reply that "interest" remains the master category.

**Kabyle exceptionalism / ethnographic present.** Charge: a mid-twentieth-century, partially idealized Kabyle case is made to carry a general theory of practice; colonial and wartime conditions of production are acknowledged but may still shape the construct. Bourdieu's response: socio-analysis is built into method; Béarn reverse test; Algeria statistics; ritual studied against primitivism and racist contempt. The documentation dates and colonial context must stay on every claim drawn from the ethnography (see [[kabyle]]).

**Gender and the "hyperbolic" male world.** Bourdieu treats Kabyle space as hyperbolic realization of male fantasies and claims anthropology thereby forces discovery of a collective unconscious haunting (male) anthropologists. Feminist positions have both used symbolic violence/hexis for analysis of gendered domination and criticized the male-centered optic and the risk of naturalizing the symbolic order described. *Masculine Domination* (later; not this source) is the program's direct extension — not yet ingested.

**Illusio and circularity.** Charge: if agents' belief is required for the game and the game produces belief, explanation risks circularity. Bourdieu's response: practical faith is field entry condition produced by selection and shaping of entrants; primary belief is product of serial constraint when practices show little dispersion (J-curve); breaks take sudden collective form when circular control loses efficacy (e.g. urbanization collapsing the fiction of the religion of honour).

## What Survived

As a named research program with a single architect, practice theory remains active rather than dead — but its concepts have also been **absorbed** into vocabularies that no longer carry the full apparatus. What lives on in current work:

- **[[habitus]]** — durable dispositions linking classed past to present practice; standard concept in sociology of culture, education, inequality, and migration studies, often stripped of field and capital.
- **[[cultural-capital]], [[social-capital]], [[symbolic-capital]]** — the capital metaphor generalized beyond economics; educational qualifications as objectified cultural capital; social capital as mobilizable networks. Heavily used, frequently loosened from misrecognition and field specificity.
- **[[social-field|Field]]** — relatively autonomous games with specific stakes and forms of capital; organizational and cultural sociology.
- **[[practical-sense|Practical sense]] / feel for the game** — practice-oriented ethnography, organizational studies, and skill research.
- **[[symbolic-violence]] and [[misrecognition]]** — inequality research, education, gender, colonial and postcolonial studies; the claim that domination works through recognition/misrecognition as much as force.
- **Strategy over rule in kinship and exchange** — transformation of kinship anthropology after the structuralist moment; gift theory after Mauss via time and misrecognition.
- **Reflexive objectification of the scholarly gaze** — the scholastic point of view as epistemic problem; distinct in genealogy from Gouldner's [[reflexive-sociology]] though convergent in name and concern.
- **The structure–agency problem restated** — habitus as a major candidate solution in [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]; rivals include Giddensian structuration, interactionism, and rational choice.

What did **not** fully survive as orthodoxy: the claim that a single generative scheme-set regenerates an entire cultural universe (Kabyle-style total homology); the tight coupling of all concepts in one system (users cherry-pick habitus or capital); the strong thesis that all practice is economic in the generalized sense (resisted in moral sociology and some anthropology).

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

`status: active`. Practice theory remains a live research program in sociology of culture, education, stratification, anthropology of practice, and adjacent fields (organizational studies, geography, public health). The vocabulary is more widely absorbed than the full program; many citations of "habitus" or "cultural capital" do not deploy field, hexis, or the double break.

**Open frontiers** (relative to this wiki's ingested base):

1. **Multi-case testing** beyond Kabyle/Béarn/French class — when does hysteresis dominate reproduction? When do fields autonomize enough for illusio to detach from kinship honour economies?
2. **Gendered habitus and symbolic violence** — extension and feminist critique; *Masculine Domination* and later gender sociology not yet ingested.
3. **Institutional objectification** — school, state, and market as automatic reproduction mechanisms vs. elementary personal domination; links to state theory and bureaucracy.
4. **Relation to parallel practice programs** — Giddens, Ortner, Schatzki, STS practice theory: convergences and incompatibilities once those sources are ingested.
5. **Computational and experimental operationalizations** of habitus and field — measurement constructs and their validity threats.
6. **Colonial and postcolonial re-readings** of the Algerian archive — production context as continuing reliability fact for the empirical program.
