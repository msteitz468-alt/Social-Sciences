## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[fligstein-neil|Neil Fligstein]] and [[mcadam-doug|Doug McAdam]] narrate *[[fligstein-mcadam-theory-of-fields-2012|A Theory of Fields]]* as the end of a long joint journey. In the late 1980s at the University of Arizona, newly tenured, they found that McAdam's civil-rights / *Freedom Summer* work and Fligstein's *Transformation of Corporate Control* were "working on the same generic sociological problem": strategic action and stability/change in emerging or established "fields." A feverish 1990 draft (~150 pages), written as Fligstein left for Berkeley, remains "remarkably similar in substance and spirit" to the 2012 book. Between draft and book, ideas circulated in a "Political–Cultural Approach to the Problem of Strategic Action," Fligstein's social-skill papers (*American Behavioral Scientist*; *Sociological Theory* 2001), McAdam's 1999 *Political Process* introduction, graduate teaching, and a 2011 *Sociological Theory* co-authored précis.

The intellectual moment they address is the post-1980s proliferation of mesolevel order concepts across balkanized subfields: organizational fields (DiMaggio & Powell), sectors (Scott & Meyer), markets as constructed orders (Fligstein), social movement industries (McCarthy & Zald), policy domains (Laumann & Knoke), Bourdieuian fields, network "fields," historical-institutionalist institutions. Scholars in social movements, organizations, economic sociology, and political science institutionalism were discovering, they argue, a common foundational problem of collective strategic action — how actors cooperate amid conflict, set rules of the game, and stabilize winners and losers — while disciplinary specialization blocked synthesis.

They state the problem of mesolevel order as *the* central problem of a social science concerned with collective action:

> "The problem of mesolevel social order and the creation of strategic action fields is the central problem of a social science interested in how people engage in collective action, how they construct the opportunity to do so, the skills they bring to the enterprise, how they sometimes succeed, and if they do succeed, how they seek to stabilize and maintain the resulting order."

That sentence is the program's mission statement. Agency–structure dualisms (Giddens, Sewell) and micro–macro links (Alexander, Coleman) are to be pushed forward not by another grand dualism but by a **mesolevel theory of action** — action between and within organized groups. Stability is "relative" and requires constant work; change is "always going on" to a degree.

They position legitimate alternatives as new institutional theory, [[structuration-theory|Giddens's structuration]], and especially Bourdieu's habitus/field/capital — each admired and borrowed from, each judged "inadequate" for explaining structure *and* sources of change and stability in modern institutional life. They also write against (1) pure rational-actor microfoundations, (2) Wrong's "oversocialized" actor, (3) movement-centric social-movement theory that says little about order, and (4) "fieldcentric" case studies that ignore the lattice of other fields. The program is thus a *political-cultural* synthesis: power and interests plus meaning and identity, at the mesolevel, with mandatory macro embedding.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Three components

Fligstein and McAdam open with three components:

1. **[[strategic-action-field|Strategic action fields]]** as mesolevel social orders are "the basic structural building block of modern political/organizational life in the economy, civil society, and the state."
2. **Any field is embedded** in proximate and distal fields, and **states are systems of SAFs**; opportunities and crises typically arrive through these ties.
3. **Strategic action** blends material and "existential" considerations; a microfoundation in the **existential functions of the social** grounds **[[social-skill]]** — "the capacity for intersubjective thought and action that shapes the provision of meaning, interests, and identity in the service of collective ends."

### Seven elements

| # | Element | Core commitment (attributed) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Strategic action fields | Constructed arenas; shared ≠ consensual understandings |
| 2 | Incumbents, challengers, IGUs | Power structure + administrative reproduction machinery |
| 3 | Social skill + existential function | Meaning-making capacity and species need for membership |
| 4 | Broader field environment | Nesting, dependence, density → stability/vulnerability |
| 5 | Exogenous shocks → mobilization | Usually inter-field; three SM-style mechanisms |
| 6 | Episodes of contention | Sustained innovative conflict under uncertainty |
| 7 | Settlement | New or refurbished rules/norms; return of positional order |

### The SAF as construct

A strategic action field, they write, is:

> "a constructed mesolevel social order in which actors (who can be individual or collective) are attuned to and interact with one another on the basis of shared (which is not to say consensual) understandings about the purposes of the field, relationships to others in the field (including who has power and why), and the rules governing legitimate action in the field."

Three construction respects: membership by **subjective standing** more than objective lists; **shifting boundaries** with issues (the 2,500 U.S. colleges as many comparator fields until a tax bill unites them); understandings fashioned over time. Against the lump concept "institutional logic," they split **four** understandings: (1) what is at stake; (2) who has power; (3) rules/tactics; (4) **positional interpretive frames** (incumbent self-serving vs challenger oppositional). This is a deliberate anti-consensus move: "Constant low-level contention and incremental change are the norm in fields rather than the image of routine reproduction that tends to define most versions of institutional theory."

**Incumbents** wield disproportionate influence; field structure and rules favor them. **Challengers** occupy lesser niches, can articulate alternatives, but usually conform grudgingly — "taking what the system gives them" — until openings appear. Terminology is acknowledged as Gamson (1975) via social-movement theory. **[[internal-governance-unit|Internal governance units]]** oversee compliance and smooth reproduction *inside* the field (distinct from external state structures); despite neutral rhetoric, they "reinforce the dominant perspective and guard the interests of the incumbents." IGUs free incumbents from day-to-day field management, naturalize rules (information, standards), and liaison to external fields — hence conservative forces in conflict. The authors flag IGUs as one of their more distinctive contributions.

### Rhetoric of anti-consensus institutionalism

The load-bearing polemical passage of Chapter 1 targets the "taken for granted" image:

> "One of the key differences between our perspective and most versions of institutional theory is that we see fields as only rarely organized around a truly consensual 'taken for granted' reality. … Constant low-level contention and incremental change are the norm in fields rather than the image of routine reproduction that tends to define most versions of institutional theory."

What the words commit the program to is not "institutions don't reproduce," but that **reproduction is work** — constant adjustment, interpretation of others' moves, and opportunistic marginal improvement by the less powerful. Settled fields still sit on a **continuum of consensus**, from rare high agreement to conflictual-but-stable structure; open conflict is the continuum's outer edge where "the very existence and structure of a strategic action field is up for grabs." Critics of institutionalism will hear an ally; strong institutionalists will hear a straw man of their best work (which already theorizes contradiction and institutional entrepreneurship). The program's wager is that making jockeying the *default image* changes research questions — toward sequences of moves, not only isomorphic diffusion.

### Hierarchy versus coalition as structural principles

A less advertised architectural choice: order in a field is not only "more or less consensus" but tends toward **hierarchy** (concentrated resources → domination) or **coalition** (roughly equal endowments → bargained division of spoils) depending on founding conditions. Both stabilize; both can embed material *and* existential payoffs (shared identity as glue of coalitions). This is their answer to the false choice between pure conflict and pure legitimacy models of field order.

### Microfoundation: existential function and social skill

Chapter 2 rejects leaving behavioral assumptions implicit. They sketch an evolutionary story: for most of hominid history sociability was primarily material; around 45,000–50,000 years ago symbolic culture (burial, art, ornament) marks the rise of collaborative meaning-making — the **existential function of the social**. Language and self-as-object (Mead) enable role-taking and skill but also existential fear. Material and existential motives are "inextricably linked"; they reject Marxian reduction of religion/meaning to pure material interest. **Dark side:** meaning projects often triumph through conflict with out-groups; skilled actors can valorize destruction (Hitler as extreme case of skill).

**Social skill** (Ch. 2 formal definition): ability to induce cooperation by appealing to and creating shared meanings and identities; empathetically reading others' situations and framing courses of action. Rooted in symbolic interactionism (Mead, Goffman). Skilled actors keep goals somewhat open-ended and "take what the system will give," unlike ideal-type rational actors with fixed preferences. Skill is a property of individuals but *use* depends on position; all need some skill; some are better. Skill × field state: premium in emergence and crisis; in settled fields resources often decide among equally skilled actors.

Social skill, they argue, solves three problems at once: (1) a microfoundation where collaborative meaning-making is primary and material/existential motives are entangled, not a pure rational calculus; (2) affiliation with collectives as an end-in-itself (contra free-rider models that need selective incentives to explain membership); (3) a shift from self-interested structural reaction — including, they say, Bourdieu's habitus as the most sophisticated such link — toward skilled *contributions* to emergence, maintenance, and transformation. Whether that reading of habitus is fair is a live position dispute; the programmatic force is clear: **keep skilled actors in the model without making them unconstrained entrepreneurs.**

Tactics of skill (Ch. 2) include agenda-setting, framing, brokerage across groups, and providing identities that make cooperation sensible. Scope claim: the same skill/fields apparatus covers institutional politics, religion, social movements, markets (firms and governments), and nonprofits — wherever actors build institutions for mixed existential and material stakes.

### Macro: embedding and the state

Chapter 3's macroperspective is "even more central" than the microfoundation, they claim. **Fieldcentric bias** — studying a field's internals while ignoring external SAFs — truncates analysis. Three embedding dimensions:

1. **Russian-doll nesting** — fields inside fields (firm: plant → division → HQ).
2. **Power relation between fields** — dependent, interdependent, or unconnected.
3. **Density of ties** — latticework vs isolate.

Nearness is **social**, not geographic. Vulnerability is not mere tie count: resource dependence and availability of alternative partners decide whether dense ties stabilize or transmit crisis (1980s S&L collapse relatively contained; 2007–08 housing crisis widely coupled). Formal organizations are a rigid, legally bounded *kind* of field — nested bureaucratic SAFs with high resource dependence, still politically dynamic (profit centers; military under unrest).

The **state** is not a unitary actor but a dense system of SAFs (agencies, courts, parties, legislatures). State fields found, certify, stabilize, and transform nonstate fields; nonstate fields also support or undermine states (elite defection; legitimation crises). Certification and legal rules are key state services; "the state" as ad hoc residual in org/SM theory is rejected.

### Dynamics: emergence, settlement, crisis, re-settlement

Chapter 4 models: **emergence** (competing cultural projects; hierarchy vs coalition shaped by resource endowments at founding); **reproduction** (default; IGUs + external allies; incumbent care, challenger patience); **rupture** (usually exogenous shocks from proximate fields — "rolling turbulence"; occasionally endogenous tipping); **[[episode-of-contention]]** (collective attribution of threat/opportunity; appropriation of organization; innovative action; sustained uncertainty); **re-settlement** (incumbent restoration via thicker external ties, or challenger coalition plus state ratification). Fundamental change = reordering major field aspects; piecemeal change is ubiquitous; authors note the dichotomy may be better as continuum (Ch. 7).

## The Empirical Program

Chapter 5 offers two long illustrations — not freestanding primary datasets for society pages in this vault, but tests of whether one apparatus can span "political" and "economic" domains.

### Civil rights / racial politics (1932–1980, with deep prehistory)

Authors reject a narrowly movement-centered CRM narrative. They reconstruct **national racial politics** and **Jim Crow as a dense system of fields** (with IGUs; cotton economy buttress; Southern congressional incumbents), not "white supremacy" as itself one SAF — a useful demonstration of their rule that principles spanning fields are not fields. Founding U.S. settlement subordinated African Americans; Compromise of 1876 denationalized the "Negro question." Depression and Cold War supply exogenous shocks (party, Court, Great Migration; geopolitical renationalization of race). The civil rights movement escalates an **episode of contention** already structured by those shocks; social skill appears in coalition and framing (including the "big five" organizations).

A signature micro-example of their crisis mechanisms is the Rosa Parks arrest: countless prior arrests did not produce national crisis; this one was *attributed as opportunity*, organizationally appropriated, and turned into innovative collective action — otherwise "no crisis or sustained episode of contention is likely to develop." Settlement terms, Dixiecrat "revenge" and New Deal electoral rupture, Black Power internal rupture, and 1970s institutionalization/spillover into other fields complete the arc. Postscript: existential as well as material motives in racial politics — the microfoundation is not decorative.

### Mortgage market / MBS (1969–2011)

Dominant S&L field under New Deal settlement (FHA, FSLIC, "3-6-3" banking) is destabilized by GSE/MBS invention, stagflation, deregulation (Garn–St. Germain), S&L crisis. A new settlement around securitization and an "industrial model" of mortgage production (Countrywide as entrepreneurial figure) expands, then crises via leverage, prime-supply peak, subprime, rating downgrades, GSE takeover. Spillover through ABS, CDO/CDS, AIG illustrates **inter-field coupling** — the theoretical moral of Ch. 3. Authors note policy misreads of field interdependence (e.g. Bernanke).

The mortgage case is also their comparative lesson on **tie density**: the 1980s S&L collapse, they argue, was relatively insulated; 2007–08 was not, because tight coupling and few alternative partners transmitted crisis. That is Ch. 3 theory wearing empirical clothes — and a claim later financial sociology can support, qualify, or challenge without abandoning field language.

| Claim | Supporting illustration | Challenging / open | Current standing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exogenous shocks from proximate fields drive most crises | Cold War + Depression → CRM opening; finance coupling → 2008 | Endogenous paths underplayed? | Core program claim; needs more comparative cases |
| Same SAF apparatus spans movements and markets | Parallel Ch. 5 structure | Domain specialists may reject unification | Program bet; contested by subfield autonomy |
| Social skill decides in flux | Movement entrepreneurs; Countrywide | Post-hoc skill attribution risk | Method Ch. 6 cautions; still soft measure |
| IGUs stabilize incumbent order | Southern Congress / field associations; finance governance | IGU vs incumbent firm boundary fuzzy | Distinctive but under-operationalized |
| State as multi-field system | Courts, parties, GSEs, Fed | Risk of "state did it" redescription | Aligns with historical institutionalism |

Honest limit: both cases are U.S. twentieth-century; generality for "modern political/organizational life" is asserted more than comparatively demonstrated in this book.

## Method Commitments

Chapter 6 is a research program, not a methods manual. The authors organize inquiry by **three field states** — formation/emergence, stability/reproduction, rupture/crisis/re-settlement — each with distinctive research questions. Most fields, they claim, emerge without intentional public design; intentional design is the special case.

**Existence of a SAF:** mutual orientation among actors; not every objective category is a field. Membership should be drawn relatively **narrowly** (subjective standing), while still tracking crucial external resource ties — IGUs and state regulators create membership ambiguity. Network maps that omit culture and state players mis-specify the field. Emerged fields show the four structural understandings; shared account of the *terrain* matters more than shared belief that the *spoils* are legitimate (challengers can agree on the map while rejecting the justice of the distribution).

**Field state** inferred from forms of strategic action and historical sequence; crisis = incumbent non-reproduction + uncertainty + innovative action. Hallmark of a true episode: "heightened interaction involving the use of innovative and previously prohibited forms of collective action." If challengers stay in proper channels, no sustained episode. Innovative action typically comes first from challengers.

**Entrepreneurship** is a role under field conditions, not a personality type; do not overestimate skill relative to resources (Ganz/Chavez UFW caution — resource valuation and organization, not pure charisma). Philosophies of science: positivist covering laws (with propositions about when states facilitate settlements, etc.) and realist case reconstruction both licensed; pure empiricism/technique-as-theory rejected. Observation is theory-laden; the SAF frame is offered as a better theory-ladenness. Formal state certification is a useful empirical marker of organized SAFs. Two arenas for studying the state: state fields themselves, and state intervention in nonstate fields — never treat "the state" as an ad hoc residual in organizational or movement narratives.

## Major Debates and Rivals

| Rival | Authors' engagement (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Bourdieu field theory | Intense sympathy; power and capital acknowledged; reject habitus as sufficient agency model; add exogenous shocks, SM contention, IGUs, skill microfoundation |
| New institutionalism | Borrow constructionism; reject strong taken-for-granted consensus and rare intentional change |
| Giddens structuration | Share agent–structure problem; prefer mesolevel fields as the push-forward |
| Network analysis | Useful method; not a theory of fields; omits culture and often the state |
| Rational choice | Underspecifies meaning, identity, and affiliation-as-end; free-rider problem mis-set |
| Social movement theory | Source of incumbent/challenger, framing, opportunity, episode language; too movementcentric for general order theory |
| Unitary state models | State as SAF system; mutual dependence with nonstate fields |

Standing open debates for the vault: How distinct is SAF from *champ*? Can skill be measured non-circularly? When is field-boundary drawing scientific vs narrative convenience?

## Critiques and Responses

**From within the book (self-critical):** fundamental vs piecemeal change may be too sharp (continuum); possible overreach claiming one theory across all institutional arenas; inter-field linkages still under-studied even by their own lights (Ch. 7 open questions).

**Predictable external critiques (positions, not vault verdicts):**

1. **Rebranding.** SAF restates institutional and Bourdieuian field theory with new labels. *Response (authors):* list of novelties — existential microfoundation, social skill, inter-field environment, hierarchy/coalition, IGUs, incremental dynamism + rupture, states as field systems.
2. **Operationalization.** Subjective membership and skill invite researcher discretion. *Response:* Ch. 6 mutual-orientation tests, certification markers, historical sequences; collaborative program invited.
3. **U.S. cases.** Illustrations may not travel. *Response:* theory claims generic structure of any SAF; comparative research called for.
4. **Agency romanticism.** Skill can become hero narrative. *Response:* skill always positional; resources and allies remain decisive; dark-side meaning projects included.

## What Survived

Regardless of whether "SAF theory" remains a named school:

- **Field language** for markets, movements, and policy domains as parallel objects is now normal in economic and organizational sociology.
- **Incumbent/challenger + settlement/episode** vocabulary continues in contentious politics and institutional change research.
- **Inter-field embedding** (against fieldcentric case studies) is a durable methodological demand — reinforced by financial-crisis scholarship after 2008.
- **Social skill / institutional entrepreneurship** language persists, with ongoing debate about measurement and hero narratives.
- **IGU-like** attention to associations, standards bodies, and rating agencies as field infrastructure (even when not using the acronym).
- Productive tension with Bourdieu keeps both programs honest: capital topology vs shock-and-skill dynamics.
- The **four understandings** split (stakes / positions / rules / frames) is a portable analytic checklist even for researchers who never adopt the SAF brand — a practical alternative to invoking a single "logic."
- Teaching canons in economic sociology and political sociology often now present markets and movements as field-like objects; *Theory of Fields* is one of the few monographs that *forces* the comparison in one cover.

What did *not* fully survive as a sealed package: the evolutionary story of the existential function (45–50k BP symbolic explosion) is rarely the citation people take; the full seven-element system is often cherry-picked; "IGU" as acronym is less common than the underlying organizational facts. Survival is modular, which is typical of successful middle-range packages.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

The program is **active** as a synthesis frame. This vault holds the 2012 programmatic book; precursors (Fligstein 1990/2001; McAdam 1982/1988/1999; *Dynamics of Contention* 2001) remain thin or secondary. Ch. 7's collaborative agenda still describes live fronts: mapping inter-field networks systematically; measuring skill without tautology; specifying when hierarchy vs coalition emerges; treating change as continuum; testing non-U.S. and non-modern cases carefully.

### Seven novelties (authors' self-inventory)

In "So What Is New Here?" they list: (1) existential microfoundation; (2) social skill; (3) fields embedded in networks of fields; (4) hierarchy vs coalition; (5) IGUs; (6) incremental dynamism *plus* rupture (not only one or the other); (7) states as systems of fields. Whether each is "new" versus "recombined" is a historiography question; as a checklist of what any rival must answer, the list is clear.

They also diagnose why social science accumulates knowledge poorly: Kuhnian disciplinary fields, career incentives, and Weberian specialization — the disciplines *are* fields, so balkanization is itself a field outcome. The "surprising discovery of fields" across economics, political science, and sociology is their evidence that the object is real enough to force multiparadigm attention; economics as incumbent discipline shapes which field theories gain authority. The closing invitation is collaborative general field theory rather than another sealed school. For this vault, next thickening steps are primary ingest of Fligstein's market monographs, McAdam's *Political Process* and *Freedom Summer*, and *Dynamics of Contention* — so that SAF claims can be checked against the empirical programs that generated them, not only against the 2012 synthesis.
