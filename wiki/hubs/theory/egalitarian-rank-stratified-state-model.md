## Intellectual Origins and Context

[[fried-morton-h|Morton H. Fried]] published *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* in 1967 in the Random House Studies in Anthropology series he co-edited with Marvin Harris. The book is an essay in **political anthropology** that deliberately runs against the mid-century grain: behavioral and systems analysis, restriction to the directly observable, and **synchronic functionalism**. Fried expected evolutionary generalization about polity to strike colleagues as quixotic or antediluvian. He wrote, he says, from ignorance of any modern attempt to link subdisciplinary contributions into a unified theory of the emergence of ranking, social stratification, and the state — and offered his theory as a **target** to attract fire and better aim.

The strategic context is the US neo-evolutionary revival after [[white-leslie-a|White]] and [[steward-julian-h|Steward]], concurrent with [[service-elman-r|Service]]’s *Primitive Social Organization* (1962; 2nd ed. 1971) and Sahlins & Service’s *Evolution and Culture* (1960). British political anthropology’s charter (*African Political Systems*, 1940) had classified African systems as Group A/B without an evolutionary ladder of ranking and stratification. Political science was expanding “the political” into “nooks and crannies” of all social systems (Almond & Coleman) and sometimes abolishing “the state” as a useful term. Fried’s four standing questions refuse that dissolution: how social relations became complex; how invidious distinctions arose within a single society; how exploitation developed; how the political state came to be. Behavioral approaches may illuminate parts; **basic answers, he held, will be evolutionary**.

Definitionally, Fried opens with a toolkit — culture as conventional responses primarily by symbolic learning; society (Aberle et al.); social control; sanctions vs reinforcements; **authority** (channeling behavior without threat of sanctions) vs **power** (with threat/use of sanctions); custom vs **law** (Austinian rule enforced by sanctions from a determinate locus of power); political organization vs government; **legitimacy** as ideology blended with power. These definitions are tools tested by utility, not hypostatized truths — and they are load-bearing for where “law” and “state” appear in the sequence.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### The cut of the typology

Fried’s sequence is not Service’s mode-of-integration ladder. It is a cut on **two axes**:

1. **Status scarcity** — are there as many valued prestige positions as capable persons, or fewer?
2. **Resource access** — do adults of same sex and equivalent age share equal rights of access to **basic resources** (capital-like ultimate sources of livelihood), or not?

| Level | Status axis | Resource axis | Stability (attributed) |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[egalitarian-society]] | Prestige positions ≈ capable persons | Open access to basic resources | Baseline simple form |
| [[rank-society]] | Prestige positions **scarce** | Still open (or not systematically privileged) access to basic resources | Relatively stable |
| [[stratified-society]] | (often ranked as well) | **Differential** access to basic resources | Unstable |
| [[the-state]] | Hierarchy institutionalized | Stratification maintained by specialized power | End form of the sequence |

**Verbatim load-bearing definition — egalitarian society:**

> “An egalitarian society is one in which there are as many positions of prestige in any given age-sex grade as there are persons capable of filling them. … An egalitarian society does not have any means of fixing or limiting the number of persons capable of exerting power.”

Analysis: Fried does **not** claim biological or role equality. He claims the social structure does not **ration** prestige offices or freeze who may lead. Leveling mechanisms and ephemeral ability differences keep hierarchy from crystallizing. Age and sex remain universal status differentiators inside “egalitarianism.”

**Verbatim load-bearing definition — rank society:**

> “A rank society is one in which positions of valued status are somehow limited so that not all those of sufficient talent to occupy such statuses actually achieve them. Such a society may or may not be stratified. … Ranking can and does exist in the absence of stratification.”

Analysis: The unusual move is the **decoupling of prestige from class**. Sumptuary goods and Bohannan-style spheres of exchange prevent prestige accumulation from converting into privileged claims on strategic resources. This is why NW Coast potlatch societies can be rank without being stratified in Fried’s sense — a direct challenge to treating all hereditary inequality as class.

**Verbatim load-bearing definition — stratification:**

> Members of the same sex and equivalent age status “do not have equal access to the basic resources that sustain life.”

Analysis: Stratification is **economic access**, not occupational prestige scales or Weberian status honor. Exploitation appears when impeded-access persons buy access with labor or produce. Famine behavior differs: prestratified hoarders must flee neighbors’ claims; stratified shortage raises prices and enables withholding.

**Verbatim load-bearing process claim — instability:**

> “Whereas the rank society, that is, a society with ranking but not stratification, is relatively stable, provided that it is not unbalanced by pressures emanating from more highly organized societies, the stratified society is by nature unstable. It must give way fairly rapidly to a more complex system of political organization or return to a lower level of organization… Should it move successfully on to the higher level of organization, its form will be transmuted into that of a state.”

Analysis: The stratified nonstate form is a **theoretical bridge**. Fried admits it is almost unfindable ethnographically. That admission is both honesty and vulnerability: critics can say the stage exists to connect rank to state.

**Verbatim load-bearing definition — the state:**

> “A state is better viewed as the complex of institutions by means of which the power of the society is organized on a basis superior to kinship.”

And:

> “The state, then, is a collection of specialized institutions and agencies, some formal and others informal, that maintain an order of stratification.”

Analysis: Against Hoebel’s “where there is political organization there is a state” and Almond & Coleman’s functional universalism, Fried insists the state is a **historical product** whose fountainhead is force and power, whose heart is **defending differential access to basic resources**. Kinship can handle egalitarian and rank control; stratification creates pressures kinship and ideology alone cannot contain. Legitimacy blends ideology with power but never substitutes for organized force.

### Economy parallel to the political sequence

Fried pairs each political level with a characteristic mode of economic integration (attributed, in dialogue with Polanyi and Sahlins):

- **Egalitarian:** reciprocal economy; **generalized reciprocity** at the core — vague obligation, no required equivalents or time limits; “theft” is hard to conceptualize; balanced and negative reciprocity peripheral. Open access to basic productive resources (Eskimo *res nullius* until taken; !Kung nominal territory that does not block veldkos access). Hunter prestige does not convert into political power — !Kung arrow-ownership randomizes who “owns” the kill.
- **Rank:** reciprocity continues, but **redistribution** becomes the major integrative process — goods flow into and out from a finite center at the pinnacle of the rank hierarchy (or a component network). Signature formula: chiefs are “**rich for what they dispensed and not for what they hoarded**.” NW Coast “owners” are administrators of kin-group wealth (Drucker), not individual capitalists. Fiji paramount as redistributor (Sahlins).
- **Stratified / state:** differential access to capital-like basic resources; exploitation as purchase of access with labor/produce; famine enables price and withholding; fiscal extraction (tax, levy, labor conscription) as state attribute.

### Leadership, law, and force across levels

**Egalitarian leadership (six principles, attributed):** rests on authority not power (with exceptions); transient among competent persons; shifts with situations more than persons; power limited to exceptional small groups and vanishes as scope widens; authority also declines with expansion; denser/more productive regimes allow more extensive leadership. Typical form: “If this is done, it will be good,” often performed by the initiator. Rejects state-as-family / *patria potestas* origin stories.

**Law:** appears only with determinate locus of power. Simple egalitarian societies have **social control without law** (Eskimo community killing of recidivist homicide lacks legitimacy for Fried — killers risk vengeance). Australian *djugururu* (“the way”) is ideology, not validated law. Rank societies still lack law in this sense (vs Pospisil on Kapauku); repair processes, ridicule, sorcery, big-man personal power substitute. The state brings formal norms with sanctions, courts, and **intrusion into kinship** — members of a common kin group can accuse one another before an external force.

**Force:** egalitarian and rank control lack specialized armies, police, constabularies. The state claims **paramountcy in naked force** — warfare and killing often monopolized; concern may invade the narrowest kin groups (even suicide prohibitions). Stratified nonstate society that lacks such mechanisms “courts disaster.”

### Primate limiting conditions and the sharing hinge

Fried treats nonhuman primate political-like elements as elementary limiting conditions, not templates. Dominance is clearest in rhesus, weakest among chimpanzees and gorillas; leadership is mostly initiating movement; **no sexual division of economic labor** and almost **no food sharing**; territorial ranges/cores with mostly peaceful meetings. The qualitative gap to human society is division of labor, sharing, and specified relationship types. The **paramount invention** leading to human society, Fried argues (with Sahlins and Service), was **sharing** — underwriting division of labor, reducing individual dominance hierarchy, substituting symbolic prestige and categories (e.g. incest categories). Early bands rarely exceeded ~50 until Upper Pleistocene, repeatedly fissioning.

### Pristine and secondary

**Pristine:** autochthonous growth of specialized control from stratified society in a political vacuum — no colonial pressure, no constitutions to copy, only lineage heads, temporary war chiefs, big-men. Power may outrun consciousness of its holders. Each pristine state traversed stratification-without-stateship. Neighbors develop in tandem; conquest theories fit **after** the state is virtually born (superstratification), not as origin of pristine formation.

**Secondary:** pre-existing states supply stimuli or models; processes differ; Crete, Greece, Rome, western/northern Europe classed secondary; all contemporary states secondary. Colonialism as modern analogue.

### Causal architecture (origins)

**Egalitarian → rank (attributed emphasis):** demography and redistribution primary; domestication associated but ranking rare without it (secondary exceptions); rank often emerges unnoticed; chiefs/big-men work harder and give more — not managerial necessity of pure hunting-gathering.

**Origins of stratification — provisional positive list:** population pressure; postmarital residence shifts; contraction/alteration of basic resources; subsistence/technological/market shifts; managerial roles from social/ceremonial maturation.

**Explicitly rejected as initiators:** warfare and slavery. War is provoked by stratification; slavery *is* a form of stratification (Nieboer compulsion criterion; NW Coast captives ≠ economic base of stratification). Wittfogel hydraulic “managerial revolution”: no necessary link; China chronology may reverse the story; managerial irrigation can create labor-for-managers paths but is not universal key.

**Hits:** portable vocabulary for prestige vs class; refusal to universalize the state; pristine/secondary distinction; clear process claim about stratified instability; serious engagement with Service without mere duplication.

**Misses / anomalies:** stratified nonstate almost never observed; population-pressure archaeology still open (Fried acknowledges Adams); OCR-poor or thin treatment of some war passages in the ingested text; secondary ranking and secondary tribes multiply exceptions; China/Indus pristine lean is explicit personal judgment.

### Worked illustrations (attributed deployment)

**Egalitarian type-cases.** Eskimo (no exclusive land/game rights); !Kung (headman as corporate charter, not hierarchy; arrow ownership); Montagnais-Naskapi via Leacock vs Speck (family hunting territory as fur-trade adjustment, not pristine private property in basic resources); Australian groups (ideology of “law” without permanent legislative/judicial offices). Habitats of living simple societies are often **marginal** — Fried warns that Pleistocene simple societies frequently occupied richer environments; demographic capacity and political possibility differ.

**Rank type-cases.** NW Coast redistribution and captive “slavery” that does not engage basic-resource access as stratification; Tikopia chiefs and ritual-economic taboo without force (Firth); Siuai big-men (Oliver/Sahlins); Choiseul coresidence and agnatic primacy as seed of stratification; Fiji *kerekere* and rank asymmetry in demands; Camayura as egalitarian organization with ranked chief functions; Mandari and Tallensi ritual-political fusion; Kachin *mayu-dama* and gumsa materials for residence-rule breach paths toward stratification.

**Stratified / state.** Tikopia land-tenure narrowing under population pressure; Kachin gumsa as secondary stratification path; Mesopotamia lean for earliest priority; Egypt; Indus and Yellow River as possible pristine (Fried’s personal lean); Crete, Greece, Troy, Persia, Rome, western/northern Europe as secondary. Speculative lost pristine states in sub-Saharan Africa. No pristine stratified-nonstate field site for direct research.

### External relations and the Service dispute

Fried disputes Service’s claim that “external polity of tribes is usually military only.” For rank societies, he argues, feasts, ceremonies, and hospitality are as frequent or more so than war, and **ranking reaches its apogee as organizer** in those events — warfare produces nothing like that organizational intensity. He hypothesizes (unproven) that war intensity correlates with population pressure on resources more than with the mere significance of ranking. Military status tends to depend on prior general status, not to originate political power. At the stratified level, conversely, stratification **provokes** war and enhances military status rather than war initiating stratification.

## Method Commitments

1. **Evolutionary questions require evolutionary answers** — synchronic functionalism cannot explain origins of ranking, exploitation, and the state.
2. **Definitions as tools** — utility, not essence; polarize continuous phenomena (custom/law) for analysis.
3. **Preserve nonstate/state dichotomy** — against functional universalism of “the political.”
4. **Access over emic property ideology** for basic resources — production/distribution behavior over group “attitudes” as sole criterion (vs Herskovits).
5. **Conjectural reconstruction legitimate** — bans that kill evolutionary biology kill this project too.
6. **Flag secondary situations** — contact composites, colonial tribes, fur-trade territories must not be read as pristine.
7. **General vs specific evolution** (Sahlins–Service) for shared state core vs local divergence.
8. **Separate observation from informant idealization** (Warner/Drake/Blair type caution).

## Major Debates and Rivals

### Service band–tribe–chiefdom

See full [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]]. Core tensions: integration vs access axes; tribe as stage vs cul-de-sac; chiefdom vs rank/stratified; military vs feast external polity; where the state begins.

Fried (attributed): no absolute necessity for a Sahlins–Service tribal stage between single settlement and complex state; city-state-like units (Jericho, Mesopotamian/Chinese/Indian village→city paths) imaginable; **chiefdom bears the evolutionary weight** in their scheme; tribes and chiefdoms are not the same. He praises the Service–Sahlins sodality analysis as intellectually serious, then demotes tribe: most tribes are secondary/colonial creations; linguistic and inventory criteria fail to bound them; Morgan’s classic tribe criteria are skewed by secondary situations; tribal borders are no clearer than band borders; “tribalism” is an evolutionary cul-de-sac relative to the questions of ranking and stratification.

Service’s program, by contrast, keeps tribe as a general level defined by pan-tribal associations and treats chiefdom as the ranked redistributional stage without Fried’s intermediate “stratified nonstate” construct. The two programs share redistribution and neo-evolutionary ambition; they disagree on **which intermediate cut does the explanatory work**.

### Universal law / universal state

Against Hartland (law swallowed into custom), Malinowski-near-universalizers, Hoebel (law and state universal), Almond & Coleman (functions imply structures in nooks and crannies), Easton-type behavioral redefinitions: Fried keeps **determinate locus of power** for law and **superior-to-kinship power apparatus** for the state.

### War and conquest theories of stratification/state

Nineteenth-century Spencer/Gumplowicz lines: Fried opposes war as *initiator* of stratification; conquest fits after state/stratification already in place for pristine sequence (superstratification).

### Hydraulic / managerial theories

Wittfogel summarized and limited: managerial irrigation can be a path; not necessary; chronology may reverse; Adams rejects extracultural population/irrigation as independent causes — Fried keeps demography as open archaeological question while retaining it on the provisional list.

### Engels / historical materialism

Fried uses “exploitation” in a conventional Marxist sense when describing stratified pressures, and frames origins partly as conversion of basic resources from communal to private property. He is **not** simply restating Engels’s gens→state pathway; the intermediate rank form and pristine/secondary cut are anthropological neo-evolutionary architecture. Dialogue possible with [[historical-materialism]] and [[engels-friedrich]] without identity.

## What Survived

1. **Pristine / secondary (primary / secondary) state formation** — among the most durable exports; standard in archaeology and comparative politics of early states.
2. **Ranking vs stratification distinction** — prestige inequality without class-like resource monopoly remains a teaching and research useful type (NW Coast, parts of Polynesia/Melanesia as classic illustrations).
3. **Refusal of universal state** — political anthropology’s charter interest in order without the state is reinforced, not dissolved, by Fried’s evolutionary framing.
4. **Unstable stratified bridge** — even when not taken as a rigid stage, the idea that differential resource access generates pressure for specialized coercion remains widely usable.
5. **Critique of “tribe”** — colonial construction and terminological ambiguity of tribe became mainstream; Service’s sodality insight often kept without the stage.
6. **Textbook pairing with Service** — generations learned political evolution as Service *or* Fried *or* a blend; the debate itself survived as pedagogy.
7. **Definitional care** — power/authority, custom/law, political organization/government remain useful teaching pairs even when not cited to Fried.

What largely **did not** survive as live unitary program: the four-step ladder as mandatory sequence for all societies; confidence that living foragers are transparent windows on Pleistocene egalitarianism without heavy caveats; pure population-pressure stories of pristine stratification.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

**Status: absorbed.** Neither Fried 1967 nor Service 1962/1971 is a dominant research design; both are reference points. Frontiers that still speak Fried’s language (often without the ladder):

- Archaeological primary vs secondary state formation and peer-polity interaction
- Heterarchy and intermediate complexity (where rank and stratified cuts are reworked)
- Forager inequality and “egalitarian” revisiting
- Slavery and captive incorporation vs class stratification
- Colonial secondary state formation and “tribal” encapsulation
- Dialogue with Weberian monopoly-of-force definitions and Engelsian class-organ theories without collapse into either

### How the program teaches

In mid- and late-twentieth-century curricula, EPS often appeared beside Service’s PSO as the two neo-evolutionary maps of “primitive” political organization. Students learned to ask: Is this inequality about **who may hold honor** or about **who may work the land**? Is this polity a state because it has chiefs and courts, or only when power sits **above kinship** and defends **differential access**? Those pedagogical questions outlasted the confidence that all societies climb the same four rungs. Archaeological field schools still use primary/secondary state language; ethnography courses still mark foragers as “egalitarian” while footnotes pile up about gender, storage, and delayed-return economies.
