## Intellectual Origins and Context

Processual archaeology emerged from a specific, dated methodological crisis inside American archaeology of the late 1950s and early 1960s, not from a single manifesto handed down whole. [[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]] later recalled that his cohort "began our careers in archaeology in the late 1950s and early 1960s" trained in "rather traditional ways," where "archeological methodology was almost exclusively concerned with questions of chronology" — seriation and artifact-form change substituting for stratigraphic control (Binford 1983). The advent of radiocarbon dating displaced chronology as archaeology's central preoccupation and, Binford argued, freed a generation to ask what else artifacts might reveal beyond age and sequence (Binford 1983).

The immediate polemical target was [[culture-historical-archaeology]] as codified by Willey and Phillips (1958), whom the *New Perspectives* editors quote as holding that archaeology "lacks a systematic body of concepts and premises constituting archaeological theory" and stands in "a particularly close and, so far as theory is concerned, dependent relationship to anthropology" (Willey and Phillips 1958, quoted in S. R. Binford and L. R. Binford 1968). Binford's programmatic chapter, "Archeological Perspectives," opens by cataloguing three widely accepted aims of the field — reconstruction of culture history, reconstruction of lifeways, and delineation of cultural process — and by documenting "increasing despair over the feasibility of achieving" the third (Binford 1968). His genealogy of failure runs through the field's oldest unsolved technical problem: distinguishing homologous from analogous cultural similarities in trait-based classification, a puzzle he traces from Tylor (1896) through Graebner (1911), Lowie (1912), and McKern's Midwestern Taxonomic System, concluding that archaeologists "have continued to formulate reconstructions using the procedures set forth by Tylor and Graebner on a common sense level" while remaining, as he put it, "deliciously unaware" of the field's own unresolved homology problem (Binford 1968).

A more proximate precursor is [[taylor-walter-w|Walter W. Taylor]]'s [[conjunctive-approach]] (1948), which the volume's editors invoke via Taylor's own definition — archaeology "as an autonomous discipline... consists of a method and a set of specialized techniques for the gathering or 'production' of cultural information" (Taylor 1948, quoted in Binford and Binford 1968). Binford later credited Taylor with recognizing that reconstruction rests on inference and with naming that inferential procedure "the conjunctive approach," but faulted him for offering "no practical guidelines for making or verifying accurate inferences" (Binford 1983) — the gap processual archaeology set out to close. European antecedents are also on record: A. R. Tallgren's 1937 complaint that archaeology "has got into a cul-de-sac" of "comparison of forms and systematization" that fails to elucidate "the organic structure of the period... its social systems, economic and social history" is cited approvingly by Binford as an early, parallel diagnosis of the same disease (Tallgren 1937, quoted in Binford 1968).

Institutionally, the program's charter document grew out of a symposium, "The Social Organization of Prehistoric Communities," at the 64th AAA meeting in Denver, November 1965, organized by editors Sally R. and Lewis R. Binford after a conversation with Stuart Struever and William Longacre (S. R. Binford and L. R. Binford 1968). The editors were explicit — and this matters historiographically — that the participant list "was not exhaustive of those engaged in new kinds of research and should not be taken to represent any 'school' of archeological thinking" (Binford and Binford 1968). The label "New Archaeology" that stuck to the movement was, on Binford's own later account, coined by opponents, not by its practitioners: "It was our 'opponents', not we, who coined the term 'New Archaeology'; Robert Braidwood went so far as to call it a religion" (Binford 1983).

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The program's foundational move was redefining archaeology's aim. Accepting Spaulding's minimal 1960 definition of archaeology as "the study of the interrelationships of form, temporal locus, and spatial locus exhibited by artifacts," the *New Perspectives* editors specified the discipline's aim as "the explanation of the observed interrelationships... an explanation of the order we observe in the archeological record" (Binford and Binford 1968) — displacing description and chronology-building with explanation as the discipline's telos. Archaeological theory was correspondingly redefined as "propositions and assumptions regarding the archeological record itself — its origins, its sources of variability, the determinants of differences and similarities" — theory about the record, not theory merely borrowed wholesale from ethnology (Binford and Binford 1968).

Binford's most quoted formulation states the epistemological break directly:

> "If we hope to achieve the aim of reconstructing culture history, we must develop means for using archeological remains as a record of the past and as a source of data for testing propositions which we set forth regarding past events, rather than as a record we can read according to a set of a priori rules or interpretive principles." (Binford 1968)

And, still more compactly:

> "Facts do not speak for themselves, and even if we had complete living floors from the beginning of the Pleistocene through the rise of urban centers, such data would tell us nothing about cultural process or past lifeways unless we asked the appropriate questions." (Binford 1968)

What these sentences commit the program to, read closely, is narrower than the slogan's later fame suggests. Binford is not claiming that data are meaningless without theory in some generic hermeneutic sense; he is making a specific methodological claim against traditional inference-by-analogy: that "fitting archeological remains into ethnographically known patterns of life adds nothing to our knowledge of the past" because it "denies to archeology the possibility of dealing with forms of cultural adaptation outside the range of variation known ethnographically" (Binford 1968, citing S. R. Binford 1968). The positive program that follows is hypothesis-testing: "It is the testing of hypotheses that makes our knowledge of the past more certain" (Binford 1968), replacing what he called the traditional archaeologist's reliance on "the competence and honesty of the person offering the reconstruction" (Thompson 1956, quoted in Binford 1968) as the only available warrant for an interpretation.

A second load-bearing claim treats culture as a systemic, adaptive phenomenon rather than a normative trait-complex defining "a people." [[binford-sally-r|Sally R. Binford]]'s Near Eastern Mousterian paper states the alternative starkly: "assemblage variability does not necessarily indicate either fixed local traditions (style) or evolutionary change within cultural systems. The alternative of functional variability must be considered" (S. R. Binford 1968) — a direct rejection of "prehistorians view[ing] flint industries 'evolving' through time, as though they contained genetic materials and were capable of mutation" (S. R. Binford 1968). William Longacre, drawing explicitly on "L. R. Binford (1962, 1965)," adopted "a perspective which views culture as a systemic whole composed of interrelated subsystems, such as the social system, the technological system, the religious system" (Longacre 1968) — the systems-theory vocabulary that gave the program its characteristic idiom of "arguments of relevance," "cultural process," and "processual archaeology" itself, a term used in editorial framing as early as 1968: "Archeology shares with other anthropological sciences the aim of explaining differences and similarities among cultural systems. We are, therefore, concerned with cultural theory and processual arguments" (Binford and Binford 1968).

By 1983 Binford added a decisive architectural refinement that sharpened, and partly repudiated, the 1960s program's confidence: the archaeological record is "here with us in the present... contemporary, and any observations that I, as an archaeologist, make... are contemporary observations. My interest is in the past but my observations are on the present" (Binford 1978; restated in Binford 1983). From this followed the demand for **middle-range theory**: independently warranted "linking arguments" connecting static material patterning to past behavioral dynamics, developed and tested "in intellectual contexts divorced from the theories about past behavior which we seek to evaluate" (Binford 1983). Binford was explicit that this was not a return to naive empiricism: "this is not a blind appeal for empiricism" but a search for "relevant experience" against which interpretive tools could be tested (Binford 1978).

## The Empirical Program

Judged on its own declared terms, the program's empirical record is genuinely mixed, and Binford's later writing is unusually candid about the mixture — a rare case of a research program's founder auditing its failures in print.

**What it explained that culture-historical practice could not.** The *New Perspectives* case studies demonstrated, for the first time systematically, that quantitative attribute-clustering and multivariate statistics could extract organizational hypotheses from a single site's internal patterning rather than merely placing sites in a regional chronology. Deetz's four-level behavioral model (individual attribute patterning → minimal-group patterning → community patterning → society-level settlement patterning) gave the program an explicit analytic scaffold (Deetz 1968). Longacre's ceramic design-element regression at Carter Ranch Pueblo — built on the premise that "if there were a residence rule which led to related females living in the same locale... then ceramic manufacture and decoration would be learned and passed down within the context of this residence unit" (Longacre 1968) — and Hill's Broken K room-function study, which explicitly refused to "terminate the analysis" at plausible ethnographic parallel and instead derived sixteen independently testable propositions (Hill 1968), together modeled a discipline that tested rather than merely asserted its inferences. Cowgill's computer-assisted, city-scale tract analysis of Teotihuacan showed the same logic could scale from a pueblo room to an early urban center (Cowgill 1968). S. R. Binford's factor-analytic decomposition of Mousterian tool-kits into functional clusters (a hunting/butchering kit, a base-camp secondary-tool kit, a fine-cutting/food-preparation kit) gave the Paleolithic a functional alternative to Bordes's ethnic reading of assemblage variability (S. R. Binford 1968).

**Where it overreached.** Binford's own retrospective judgment on the program's central Southwestern case studies is unsparing. He later concluded, from his own Nunamiut fieldwork, "not that my functional arguments about Mousterian variability were correct, but rather that archaeological methods for inference are in general very inadequate" (Binford 1983) — a direct admission that the confident 1960s inferential apparatus had outrun its evidentiary warrant. He generalized the critique into a standing methodological indictment: "criteria based on similarity are insufficient to demarcate the boundaries of past cultural systems... Simply to group things which are similar ensures that we will never see a system in realistic terms" (Binford 1983). Longacre himself performed a documented act of self-revision, writing that he had "been able to refine my thoughts regarding the difficulty if not the impossibility of getting at descent using archeological data" (Longacre, quoted in the *New Perspectives* claims record) — a retraction of the stronger "localized matrilineal descent groups" claim advanced in the original 1968 paper. The underlying problem, as Binford diagnosed it by 1983, was that "current conventions" for identifying base camps, activity areas, and social units "stem largely from wishful thinking" and that "plausibility is not a substitute for evidence" — a self-critique of the very Southwestern residence-inference program he had helped launch (Binford 1983).

The 1960s positivism itself proved unsustainable in its strongest form. Spaulding's contribution to *New Perspectives* had committed the program to a strict Hempelian covering-law model of explanation — "there is only one kind of serious explanation, the nomological or covering-law explanation" (Spaulding 1968) — and Binford's own 1968 Post-Pleistocene Adaptations paper aspired to exactly this kind of law-governed demographic model of agricultural origins. By 1983 Binford judged that paper an "explanation sketch" in Hempel's own weaker sense, one that was simply "wrong about what the past had been like," and traced its flaw to an unexamined residual "Garden of Eden" assumption inherited from the very theories it meant to displace (Binford 1983). More broadly, he came to see 1960s "New Archaeology" programmatics as "perhaps ill-timed because tactics were sold without a clear teleology/goal," conceding "some negative reactions were justified" (Binford 1983) — while still insisting that "deep concern for and experimentation in epistemological strategies is a key to the growth of archaeological science" (Binford 1983).

## Method Commitments

The program's methodological core, stated most crisply in 1968, was problem-oriented research design coupled to explicit hypothesis-testing: propositions are formulated, then tested against independent data, rather than plausibility being treated as sufficient warrant. Quantification and multivariate statistics (factor analysis, multiple regression, chi-square tests of association) were the preferred instruments — used by Deetz for attribute clustering, Longacre and Hill for room- and design-based social inference, S. R. Binford for Mousterian tool-kit decomposition, and Cowgill for Teotihuacan tract analysis (all in Binford and Binford 1968). Ethnographic analogy was retained but reframed: no longer an endpoint of interpretation but a source of testable propositions, subject to what Hill called the "independent-data rule" — a proposition generated from ethnographic parallel must be tested against archaeological data not used to generate it, on pain of circularity (Hill 1968).

By the 1970s and fully in 1983, this apparatus was supplemented — and in Binford's own account, largely displaced in importance — by [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]] built through [[ethnoarchaeology]] and [[actualistic-studies]]. Binford explicitly borrowed the geological principle of uniformitarianism as epistemic warrant: "We must therefore proceed along the research path forged quite eloquently by our sister discipline geology in its adoption of the proposition of uniformitarianism. Is the formation of archaeological remains as a by-product of adaptive behavior a process that is operative in the contemporary world? Can we directly experience this process?" (Binford 1978). His fieldwork among the [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] (1969–1973) operationalized this through the Meat, Marrow, and Grease Utility Indices, built from controlled butchering of a caribou and two sheep, and through direct observation of transport, storage, and discard decisions (Binford 1978). The resulting "schlepp effect" logic — that low-utility anatomical parts are left at kill sites while high-utility parts are transported away, producing an archaeological assemblage that is "an inverse, decision-filtered image of what was consumed elsewhere" (Binford 1978) — became a template method for [[faunal-analysis]] generally. Binford also insisted on rigorous [[formation-processes|formation-process]] and [[taphonomy|taphonomic]] controls, crediting C. K. Brain's work on Hottentot goat remains and hyena-den assemblages as demonstrating that "natural/mechanical (non-cultural) processes could account for much of the Makapansgat pattern Dart had attributed to hominid behavior" (Binford 1983) — a caution he generalized into skepticism about "living floor" and "home base" claims for early hominid sites (Binford 1983).

## Major Debates and Rivals

**The Bordes–Binford Mousterian debate.** See [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]. [[bordes-francois|François Bordes]] had developed a rigorous, quantitative classification of Middle Paleolithic assemblages into four recurring types — Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition, Typical Mousterian, Denticulate Mousterian, and the Charentian group — and read their alternation and co-existence at sites like Combe Grenal as evidence of distinct, persisting "cultures" or ethnic traditions (Binford 1983, summarizing Bordes). Binford would later credit Bordes's pattern-recognition work as genuinely "unprecedented" in its accuracy, but argued it remained "integrated within an older view of culture and the past compatible with a biological model: the tree of life" (Binford 1983). In 1966, with Sally Binford, he proposed instead that "variation in the structure and function of an archaeological assemblage is directly related to the term, nature, and spatial arrangement of human activities" — i.e., functional/activity variability, not ethnic identity, could produce Bordes's observed patterning (Binford and Binford 1966, cited in Binford 1978). Binford spent a year (1967–1968) in Bordeaux working directly with Bordes on the fauna and spatial distributions at Combe Grenal, but concluded the exercise "increased the scope of the problem without reaching any solutions" — "none of these new facts spoke for themselves" (Binford 1983). This impasse was the direct proximate cause of the Nunamiut fieldwork: on the boat home from Le Havre, Binford reasoned that the archaeological record's static, contemporary character meant no purely archaeological resolution to the Bordes dispute was possible without independently developed inferential methods (Binford 1983). By 1983 his own verdict was that the dispute had never really been about facts — he "trusted Bordes and had no argument with his typology" — but about "the meaning of the patterning," and that neither side's method could adjudicate the question without a theory of formation processes neither yet possessed (Binford 1983). Using Nunamiut settlement-scale data (five families' residential sites over five years covering roughly as much territory as the entire Dordogne), Binford argued Bordes's inference of four distinct cultural groups from tool-kit differences was empirically implausible at that geographic scale (Binford 1983).

**Processual vs. post-processual archaeology.** See [[post-processual-archaeology]] and [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]. By the early 1980s [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] and colleagues at Cambridge mounted a sustained critique charging that processual practice had side-stepped the theory-ladenness of observation itself, that its universal "field packages" for excavation and interpretation smuggled in unexamined theoretical assumptions, and that meaning, agency, and the politics of interpretation were being excluded by fiat (documented in Hodder 1982, cited in Binford 1983's notes). Binford's own account of an encounter with Hodder's students at Cambridge is notably combative: he characterizes their tactics as including "fallacious 'when did you stop beating your wife' argument forms" and "the fallacy of affirming the consequent" (Binford 1983), and groups Marxists, structuralists, and other critical-theory-inflected archaeologists together as "social philosophers" who, he argued, advance preferred positions through "post hoc accommodative arguments" and "tautological observational languages" rather than through appeal to an independently warranted empirical method (Binford 1983). Binford's counter-position was not a rejection of theory as such — "philosophy without science is simply culture and science without philosophy is sterile convention" (Binford 1983) — but an insistence that positions be evaluable against "the properties of the external world" via a scientifically constructed observational language, on pain of "no objectivity" and "merely the fascination of endless debate" (Binford 1983).

**Internal splits Binford disowned.** Binford was explicit and public in dissociating himself from the post-1960s proliferation of "ever-newer archaeologies" — Behavioral Archaeology, Social Archaeology, Astroarchaeology — writing that "they do not concern serious proposals within a scientific discipline, but rather a form of sociological posturing... this is an aspect of the New Archaeology from which I should like to dissociate myself" (Binford 1983). He similarly noted that after 1968 he "stood on the sidelines and watched some of the themes and arguments I or my colleagues had introduced picked up and sometimes taken in directions I really did not think they ought to go" (Binford 1983), while occupying himself with the methodologically oriented Mousterian and Nunamiut research that became his real, self-described lifelong project: "those ultimate concerns have been to explain the origins of agriculture and variability in the Mousterian... I have really worked on no other problems in my entire career" (Binford 1983).

## Critiques and Responses

Beyond the post-processual challenge, three critique lines recur through the record. First, the **circularity charge** against the flagship social-inference case studies: Hill himself named the problem when attacking the common practice of asserting functional identity between prehistoric and ethnographic room types on the basis of formal similarity alone, quoting E. Bright Wilson's warning that "plausibility is not a substitute for evidence" and that social-science practice had allowed "an abbreviation of the scientific method in which this step [testing] is simply omitted" (Hill 1968, quoted approvingly by Binford as a founding methodological principle — and later turned by critics, and eventually by Binford himself, against the program's own Southwestern case studies). Second, the **positivism-overreach charge**: Spaulding's strict Hempelian covering-law commitment and the program's early confidence that patterned material remains directly evidenced systemic cultural process proved too strong; Binford's later self-assessment that "New Archaeology programmatics were perhaps ill-timed because tactics were sold without a clear teleology/goal" and that "some negative reactions were justified" (Binford 1983) is itself the clearest surviving statement of this critique, offered by the program's own founder. Third, the **formation-process gap**: Binford's mature critique of "living floor" and simple density-based "home base" inferences in East African early-hominid archaeology — a domain outside the program's original American core — generalized his Mousterian-era lesson that assemblage content cannot be read directly without independently secured knowledge of the processes (natural, carnivore, and human) that produced it (Binford 1983, drawing on C. K. Brain's taphonomic work).

Binford's response to all three lines was not retreat but redirection: rather than abandoning the explanatory ambitions of 1968, he relocated the discipline's most urgent work one level down, from theories of past cultural process to theories of *how the present record could license any inference about process at all* — the middle-range program. His stated position by 1983 was that "we need to concentrate on the development of middle-range theory... Only if reliable answers [to 'What was it like?' and 'What does it mean?'] are available might work on the question 'Why did it happen?' be profitable" (Binford 1983).

## What Survived

Even where the program's specific 1960s conclusions did not hold, several of its structural commitments persisted and, in Binford's later formulation, became more rigorous rather than less. [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|Middle-range theory]] and [[ethnoarchaeology]] as independent research programs — built from the Mousterian impasse and matured through the Nunamiut fieldwork — remain active method in zooarchaeology, lithic analysis, and settlement studies; the Meat/Marrow/Grease Utility Indices Binford constructed from controlled butchering experiments are still cited as standard tools (Binford 1978). [[actualistic-studies|Actualistic and formation-process research]], catalyzed by the Bordes dispute and by C. K. Brain's taphonomic work, is now baseline practice across the discipline, including in fields (early-hominid archaeology, faunal analysis) well outside processual archaeology's original American Southwest/Near East case-study base. The insistence on **explicit, testable propositions** rather than plausible narrative — Hill's independent-data rule, in particular — survives as a general methodological norm even among archaeologists who reject the program's positivist self-description; quantification and multivariate analysis of assemblage variability remain routine. Systems-theory vocabulary (activity areas, settlement systems, adaptive systems) persists as working language even where its strong 1960s ontological commitments (culture as a bounded, functionally-integrated system) have been abandoned or heavily qualified. And the program's central epistemological insight — that "the archaeological record is contemporary" and that any inference from static material patterning to past behavioral dynamics requires an independently warranted "linking argument," not a simple reading of the record — is now widely accepted across theoretical camps, including by many post-processual archaeologists who reject Binford's positivist framing of it. In this sense processual archaeology exemplifies `status: absorbed` in one dimension (the strict covering-law positivism) while remaining genuinely `status: active` in another (its methodological apparatus and its problem-orientation).

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

Processual archaeology no longer names a unified school; "New Archaeology" is now purely historiographic, a label Binford himself noted was coined by critics (Binford 1983). "Processual" survives as a family label for approaches committed to explicit hypothesis-testing, systemic/ecological explanation, and middle-range grounding, coexisting — sometimes in explicit synthesis, sometimes in continued tension — with post-processual, cognitive, and evolutionary-ecological approaches. Binford's own late-career research agenda (visible in the 1983 *In Pursuit of the Past* chapters on agricultural origins and social complexity) shows the program's explanatory ambitions redirected toward demographic/ecological "packing" models of subsistence change and toward reworked, more cautious accounts of the rise of social complexity — explicitly rejecting his own earlier "Garden of Eden" and leisure-time explanatory sketches, rejecting redistribution-based (Sahlins) and simple intensification-for-surplus models of chiefdom formation as unsupported ("archaeologists still do not know what causes complex societies, what brings them into being" — Binford 1983), and proposing Melanesian-style "big-man" competitive-alliance systems as a more promising model for the origins of social ranking than Sahlins's redistributive-chief model. Open research fronts the program bequeathed the discipline include: the continued unresolved status of the Bordes–Binford Mousterian debate itself (Binford's own final verdict was that the discipline still lacked adequate methods to resolve it, not that his functional reading had been vindicated); the ongoing development of formation-process and taphonomic method in human-origins research; and the general problem — still explicitly programmatic rather than solved — of building middle-range theories adequate to license inferences about social organization, as opposed to subsistence and site-formation, from the material record.
