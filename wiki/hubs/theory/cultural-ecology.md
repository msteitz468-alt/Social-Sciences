# Cultural Ecology — Deep Analysis

> Theory Hub deep analysis. Summary: [[cultural-ecology]]. Link hub by full path `[[hubs/theory/cultural-ecology]]`.

**Scope.** Primary base: [[steward-julian-h|Julian H. Steward]], *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change: The Methodology of Multilinear Evolution]]* (University of Illinois Press, 1955; 1973 paperback reprint used for OCR). The volume is an essay collection: most chapters revise earlier papers; Chapters 2, 5, and 6 were newly written for coherence. Closely coupled apparatus treated here as one research program: [[multilinear-evolution]], [[cultural-ecological-method]], [[cultural-core]], [[culture-type]], [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]], [[culture-area]] (as foil), and type cases ([[patrilineal-band]], [[great-basin-shoshoneans]], [[carrier-indians]], irrigation civilizations, Puerto Rico).

All programmatic claims in **attributed voice**. Secondary reception (cultural materialism, processual archaeology, political ecology) is noted only as historiography, not as findings of this source.

## Intellectual Origins and Context

Cultural ecology is a **postwar-expansion** research program that answered a double deadlock in American anthropology.

**What it reacted against:**

1. **Victorian unilinear [[social-evolutionism]].** Morgan, Tylor, Spencer, and allies arrayed societies on a single ladder. Steward held the ladder inadequate: categories such as "savagery" were too coarse; alleged universal priorities (e.g. matriarchy before other forms) failed restricted tests. He also noted that Marxist/Communist adoption of Morgan had damaged the word "evolution" itself — a Cold War-era sensitivity about labels, not a rejection of generalization.

2. **Boasian [[historical-particularism]] and [[culture-area]] relativism.** Steward accepted diffusion, historical uniqueness, and the critique of naïve comparison, but argued that exclusive focus on area uniqueness and total trait inventories forgoes **regularities** — independent functional parallels. Culture-area taxonomy, he held, is natural-history classification suited to contrasts, not to cross-cultural science of process. Formal-only comparison (unlike gods, unlike architecture) yields relativism even when **functions** (e.g. theocratic sanction of class) recur.

3. **Chicago [[human-ecology]] / social ecology.** Steward carefully distinguished cultural ecology from biological ecology and from Park–Burgess–McKenzie human ecology. The latter, he argued, treats the human community as an end in itself with tools borrowed from plant succession (competition, invasion, climax). Cultural ecology's object is **culture's adaptation**, not the community as biotic assemblage. Man introduces **super-organic culture** into the web of life; culture is not genetically derived.

4. **Whitean / Childean universal evolution (as sole master key).** Steward praised the quest for laws of culture-in-general (White's energy levels; Childe's Darwinian formula transferred to culture) but insisted such laws explain *that* culture changes, not the characteristics of particular cultures — as natural selection does not explain a single species without local history. Multilinear evolution is the third path: limited, empirical parallels.

**Disciplinary moment.** Mid-century US anthropology: neo-evolutionary revival; large comparative projects; Steward's own Great Basin ethnography (1930s), band essay (1936), Handbook of South American Indians editorship, and Puerto Rico community study project. Archaeology's developmental sequences (Andean, Mesoamerican, Old World) supplied the irrigation trial formulation. Early radiocarbon revision already appears in Steward's chronological caveats.

**Partial inheritances.** Interest in parallelism inside the Boas school (Steward notes it); Kroeber on limited independent culture-form types; Wittfogel on irrigation and polity; Redfield's folk society re-read as an integrational **level**.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

The architecture is a set of interlocking operational concepts, not a single master narrative.

### 1. Multilinear evolution as methodology

Steward defined [[multilinear-evolution]] as "essentially a methodology based on the assumption that significant regularities in cultural change occur," empirical rather than deductive, dealing "only with those limited parallels of form, function, and sequence which have empirical validity." No a priori world scheme. "What is lost in universality will be gained in concreteness and specificity." Evolution, for him, means interest in recurrent forms, processes, and functions — synchronic or diachronic — whether few or many cultures are embraced.

### 2. Cultural ecology as creative process

[[cultural-ecology]] studies how culture adapts to environment through technology and subsistence behavior. It is not environmental determinism: environment is relevant only as culturally utilized; some adaptations **require** narrow social patterns, others allow **latitude** (Carrier case). The method has three procedures (see Method Commitments).

### 3. Cultural core vs secondary features

The [[cultural-core]] is "the constellation of features which are most closely related to subsistence activities and economic arrangements," including social, political, and religious patterns empirically tied to those arrangements. Secondary features vary more by diffusion and innovation and produce outward distinctiveness among cultures with similar cores. This is the program's load-bearing analytic cut — and its main vulnerability to critique (what is "core" by fiat?).

### 4. Culture type vs culture area

A [[culture-type]] is defined by selected, functionally interrelated features (same interrelationship in each case), not total element content. Steward's terminological pair:

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Culture area type | Locally distinctive total system |
| Cross-cultural type | Same type in historically unrelated areas |
| Uniformities | Similarities within one area/co-tradition (historical) |
| Regularities | Similarities recurring in separate traditions (causal) |

Only independent comparison, he argued, can stipulate causal interrelation.

### 5. Levels of sociocultural integration

[[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]] (family / folk-multifamily / state-national, with intermediates such as lineage and clan) are operational, not Morgan–Tylor stages. They enable comparison of like with like and analysis of multilevel systems (nations with subcultures; empire layered over folk communities). Sequence is not mandatory — composite bands may "skip" lineage level.

### 6. Hunting-and-gathering is not one stage

"Savagery" fails as a type: [[patrilineal-band]], composite multi-family bands, dispersed family groups (Shoshoni, Eskimo), and California tribelets arise from different ecological adaptations. This single move dismantles both unilinear "stage of savagery" and lazy lumping of all foragers.

## The Empirical Program

| Ecological latitude allows diffusion of social forms | Carrier + Sekani contrast (Ch. 10) | Original baseline uncertain | Case for non-determinism |
| Parallel irrigation-civilization sequence | Peru, Mesoamerica, Mesopotamia, Egypt, China (Ch. 11) | Explicitly trial; Mesoamerican irrigation timing caveat; militarism–population unresolved | Wittfogel-adjacent; heavily revised by later archaeology |
| Industrial national society = multilevel subcultures | Puerto Rico tobacco/coffee/sugar/upper class (Ch. 12) | Project-based; mid-century political frame | Community-study lineage |

**Quote — multilinear definition (Ch. 1):**

> "Multilinear evolution is essentially a methodology based on the assumption that significant regularities in cultural change occur, and it is concerned with the determination of cultural laws. Its method is empirical rather than deductive. … it deals only with those limited parallels of form, function, and sequence which have empirical validity. What is lost in universality will be gained in concreteness and specificity."

**Analysis:** The sentence structure does the political work: "evolution" and "laws" are retained, then immediately restricted by "methodology," "empirical," and "limited." The program claims scientific status without physics-style absolute universals (Ch. 11 methodological assumptions).

**Quote — cultural core (Ch. 2):**

> "Elsewhere, I have offered the concept of cultural core — the constellation of features which are most closely related to subsistence activities and economic arrangements. The core includes such social, political, and religious patterns as are empirically determined to be closely connected with these arrangements. … secondary features … give the appearance of outward distinctiveness to cultures with similar cores."

**Analysis:** "Empirically determined" is the escape hatch against tautology — and the burden of proof. Followers who treat core as *a priori* economic base drift toward a different program (cultural materialism). Steward's wording keeps core as research finding within each analysis.

## Method Commitments

[[cultural-ecological-method]] — three procedures:

1. **Technology–environment.** Exploitative/productive technology relative to environment (subsistence devices in simple societies; agriculture/herding/manufacture; in industrial worlds capital, credit, trade). Relevant environmental features depend on culture (water-hole spacing; game habits; fish runs).
2. **Exploitative behavior.** Behavior patterns of using that technology in that area (seed-gathering fragmentation vs cooperative hunting surrounds).
3. **Effects on culture.** Extent to which those patterns shape other aspects of culture — holistic for the core.

Additional commitments: typology before law (Ch. 11: typology → causal interrelation → independent recurrence); form **and** function; procedures scaled to [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]]; diffusion as non-explanation of origins (acceptance of traits still needs cause-effect analysis).

## Major Debates and Rivals

- **[[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]** — Position 4 densified: Steward vs unilinear vs particularist vs White universal.
- **[[historical-particularism]]** — complementary history vs exclusive program; culture area as foil.
- **[[human-ecology]]** — urban/community ecology vs culture-explanatory ecology.
- **Environmental determinism** — Steward's standing denial; latitude cases (Carrier) as internal evidence.
- **Wittfogel / hydraulic thesis** — adjacent on irrigation; Steward's trial formulation is broader developmental sequence, not identical to Wittfogel's oriental despotism thesis (Wittfogel cited as type illustration).
- **Speck vs fur-trade territories** — composite-band chapter takes the trade-origin side for family trapping territories.

## What Survived

1. **Cultural ecology** as named method and research style in ecological anthropology.
2. **Cultural core** vocabulary (even when redefined).
3. **Multilinear** as teaching contrast to unilinear and universal evolution.
4. **Forager diversity** — not one "hunting stage" — now commonplace.
5. **Band typology debates** (patrilineal vs composite; fur-trade territories).
6. **Multilevel analysis of complex societies** (subcultures under national institutions) — community-study and later political anthropology.
7. **Archaeological interest** in parallel pathways and ecological constraint (processual reception — secondary).
8. **Rejection of pure diffusion as origin explanation** while retaining history — standard methodological hygiene.

Absorbed into: cultural materialism (Harris — different ontology of "core"); processual archaeology; later political ecology (often as foil); this wiki's evidence-class and non-identity rules (ecology contributes, rarely sole-causes).

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

As of the mid-1950s source horizon, the program is active and ambitious (trial laws of early civilization; national subcultures). In later disciplinary history (not primary here), cultural ecology's name persists while many specific regularities were revised by denser ethnography, archaeology, and feminist/postcolonial critique. Live frontiers that still speak Steward's language without his full apparatus: niche construction; resilience and socio-ecological systems; comparative pathways of inequality and state formation; multi-sited analysis of national and global institutions shaping local cores.

## Detailed Case Architectures

### A. Great Basin family level (Ch. 6)

Steward's Great Basin analysis is the empirical heart of the family-level concept. The argument chain is ecological:

1. Arid physiography and climate produce sparse biotic resources; large game is limited; small game and insects matter more; **pinon** is the principal plant staple but spatially and annually unreliable.
2. Seed and root gathering, largely by women working alone or in small groups, creates competition rather than cooperation at scale — a premium on **family separatism**.
3. Resource unpredictability yields an unpredictable seasonal itinerary; winter pinon concentrations are temporary, not corporate villages.
4. Therefore the nuclear family is the primary unit of production and consumption; multipattern political integration remains weak; "chiefs" clear information more than they command.
5. Property is justified by work and habitual use, not exclusive group territory — exclusive local-group land would threaten survival when resources fail locally.
6. Rabbit and antelope drives show that higher integration is **possible** but **temporary** and task-specific (rabbit boss; antelope shaman) — not a permanent band structure.
7. Post-contact **predatory bands** after horses and settler pressure are a different culture type by form and function; they never organized all people; treating them as aboriginal would misread the family-level baseline.
8. Owens Valley is the exception that tests the rule: better resources support more permanent settlement and band-like organization.

**Theoretical yield:** same *level* as Eskimo family organization, different *type*; culture-area labels miss the ecological configuration; post-contact fragmentation is not aboriginal family-level organization. Steward also used Western Shoshoni ease of certain forms of acculturation (absence of aboriginal band institutions) as a levels-and-acculturation illustration (Ch. 3) — with race barriers still limiting full assimilation in his mid-century account.

### B. Patrilineal band (Ch. 7)

The patrilineal band is Steward's best-known culture type. Core features: localized patrilineal (typically exogamous) hunting group with territorial association; relationships among hunting economy, descent, marriage, and land tenure. Slightly higher integration than Great Basin families.

**Causal ecology (Steward):** not similarity of total environment — cases span desert, rainforest, and cold rainy coasts — but similarity of **subsistence problem**: game in small nonmigratory groups such that collective localized hunting pays and male-linked land inheritance stabilizes access. Population density and transport poverty keep groups small enough that kinship reckoning and exogamy remain workable. Technology varies in detail; the sociological effect of hunting is what is held constant.

**Negative claims:** pure diffusion cannot explain the type's multi-continental distribution; "archaic heritage" is not an explanation; identity of exploitative patterns matters more than identity of total environment or total technology inventory.

**Methodological reflexivity:** Steward rejects both pure culture-history (origins as borrowed traits) and holistic "all features are equal cause and effect." The cultural core is the causal package; secondary features may differ.

### C. Composite hunting band (Ch. 8)

Composite bands integrate many **unrelated** nuclear families by constant association and cooperation rather than unilineal kinship. Same integration *level* as patrilineal bands, different *type*.

**Ecology:** low density + primary reliance on large herds of **migratory** game (caribou, musk ox) enables seasonal aggregates of hundreds in which relationship knowledge is lost and band exogamy becomes unnecessary. Steward's causal formula: band is composite rather than patrilineal if (1) chief food is large-herd game, or (2) social practices temporarily introduce unrelated families into a patrilineal band.

**Historical change:** family trapping territories (beaver) coexisting with band ownership of large-game ranges developed, he argued, in response to the **fur trade** (market + steel goods), against Speck's aboriginal-territory thesis. As fur dependence grew, bands fragmented into families linked to Canadian national society via trader, missionary, and officials — a multilevel national integration story that prefigures Ch. 4 and the Carrier chapter.

### D. Lineage to clan — Southwest (Ch. 9)

Steward treats lineage and clan as **levels** and processes, not a single culture type and not unilinear stages. Definitions: lineage (traceable kin), localized lineage, clan (identity retained with culturally constructed exogamy "fiction," functions surrendered into larger systems).

**Paths:**

- Southern California: localized patrilineal lineage as fundamental unit under patrilineal-band ecological conditions; multiclan villages under contact/density conditions.
- Yuman: horticulture + warfare path from patrilineal bands toward clans; density alone insufficient (California vs Colorado River contrast); male economic weighting precedes clans.
- Basket Maker–Pueblo: archaeology (kiva-room ratios; house clusters) read as lineage amalgamation into communal houses; village concentration sometimes nonecological (defense); matriliny via female land/house ownership and field distance — a different path than California patrilineality.
- Five-process formulation of lineage-to-clan transformation (local processes rather than single diffusion origin of "the clan").

**Negative claim:** diffusion of arbitrary features (e.g. totem names) does not prove unilateral origin of the institution.

### E. Carrier latitude (Ch. 10)

The Carrier case is the program's anti-determinism exhibit. Western Athabaskan salmon-surplus ecology **permits** Northwest Coast moiety-potlatch patterns to diffuse; Sekani lack of surplus blocks adoption. Fur trade later dissolves moiety patterns toward individual territories. Ecology poses an **open question**: when technology and environment allow latitude, cultural-historical factors decide which option is taken. Steward's evaluative language ("shabby imitation") reveals mid-century center-periphery hierarchy in comparative style even as the structural argument is about surplus and possibility.

### F. Irrigation civilizations — trial formulation (Ch. 11)

Steward's most ambitious diachronic claim: five arid/semiarid cradles (Northern Peru, Mesoamerica, Mesopotamia, Egypt, China) show parallel developmental sequences because pre-iron exploitation of irrigable environments entailed similar solutions. Environments **facilitated** growth (digging-stick and irrigation farming) rather than stimulating via hardship. Tropical forests, northern hardwoods, and sodded plains were hard to exploit without iron.

**Eras by organization, not metals:**

| Era | Organizational characterization (Steward) |
|---|---|
| Pre-Agricultural / Incipient Agriculture | Food production begins |
| Formative | Basic technologies; folk culture; local theocracy; local irrigation control by religious leaders; small communities → multicommunity states; limited raid warfare |
| Regional Development / Florescence | Theocratic states; arts peak; irrigation enlargement; militarism relation to population **unresolved** across centers |
| Cyclical Conquests | Conquest empires; peak; rebellion; "dark ages"; urbanization as imperial integration |

**Methodological frame:** three requirements for regularities — typology; causal interrelation (synchronic/sequential); independent recurrence as "law." Diffusion of particulars does not explain cores. Formulations are tentative; "facts" only via theories. Carbon-14 revision: food-production potential realized relatively quickly (~3,000 years to irrigation-based empires in both hemispheres under the technology's sociocultural limits). Iron Age transforms forest zones more than irrigation cores; New World sequence cut short by European conquest.

**Internal honesty:** Mesoamerican irrigation may be late; Yucatan/SE Asia possibly excluded; militarism–irrigation–population relation "not clear." This is science-as-trial-formulation, not a closed hydraulic law.

### G. Puerto Rico — complex contemporary society (Ch. 12)

Puerto Rico applies levels and subcultural cores under industrialization and U.S. sovereignty:

| Subculture | Productive arrangement | Steward's cultural sketch |
|---|---|---|
| Tobacco small farmers | Cash crop, nuclear family | Mobility values; fluid class; Independentista politics; Protestant penetration |
| Coffee hacienda workers | Capital barriers, paternalism | Landlord dependency; traditional family; unorthodox saint cult; least value on individual education/services |
| Sugar proletariat | Corporate/government monocrop | Fixed wages; unions; matrifocal family patterns; residual witchcraft/saints in profit-sharing variants |
| Upper class | U.S. commercial allegiance | Extreme Americanization |

**Conclusions Steward draws:** similarities to U.S. counterparts among tobacco farmers and sugar workers come less from direct borrowing than from industrial trends introducing North American-type national institutions; goods, sports, and films enter differently patterned local subcultures; industrial goals mediated by the U.S. would be much the same under another sovereignty; resulting insecurity produces **cultural** rather than overtly political nationalism. National common denominators (Hispanic heritage, language, Catholicism, compadrazgo) are class-differentiated in depth — not a single tribal-style "national culture."

## Architecture: How the Pieces Lock

```
multilinear evolution (methodology: limited regularities)
        ↑ supplies types for comparison
cultural ecology (method: tech–env–behavior → core)
        ↑ defines
cultural core + levels of integration
        ↑ compose
cross-cultural culture type
        ↑ opposed to / complementary with
culture area (historical uniformities)
```

Without ecology, multilinear evolution is empty typology. Without multilinear restriction, ecology slides toward universal stages or determinism. Without levels, complex nations cannot be compared to "tribal" ethnography without category mistakes. Without core/secondary cut, total-culture uniqueness dissolves all parallels.

## Rhetoric and Genre

*Theory of Culture Change* is a **method manifesto with case appendices**, not a single ethnography. Steward's rhetoric repeatedly:

- Keeps "science," "law," and "evolution" while narrowing their scope.
- Frames opponents as extremes (relativism denies regularities; unilinear overclaims; universalism is too abstract).
- Uses "trial formulation" to license bold comparative archaeology without finality.
- Condenses his own monographs (Basin-Plateau; band paper; Puerto Rico project) into type cases for a reading audience of theorists and archaeologists as much as ethnographers.

The American Antiquity blurb reprinted on the paperback (back matter) positions the book for prehistorians as much as ethnologists — correctly reading Steward's intent that archaeology and ethnology share developmental typology.
