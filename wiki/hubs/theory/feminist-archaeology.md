## Intellectual Origins and Context

By the mid-1980s, [[gero-joan-m|Joan Gero]] and [[conkey-margaret-w|Margaret Conkey]] argued, archaeology had “lagged far behind” sister social sciences in placing women as sociocultural subjects on the analytical stage. Their preface to *Engendering Archaeology* opens with that diagnosis:

> "A volume using explicitly feminist social theory in archaeological research is long overdue. By the mid 1980s it was no secret that archaeology was lagging far behind our sister disciplines in getting even exploratory research about women onto our analytical and interpretive stages, where women are explicitly sought as sociocultural subjects."

The lag was not for want of a program statement. Conkey and [[spector-janet|Janet Spector]]’s “Archaeology and the Study of Gender” (1984; circulating in manuscript earlier) had been recognized as “compelling and timely.” Yet when Basil Blackwell’s Social Archaeology series (general editor [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]; Conkey herself an advisory editor) approached Gero and Conkey about three years later, they found “simply no archaeological literature to cite as contributions, nor… any defined circle of experts.” Androcentric assumptions, they charged, still structured hypothesis formulation; prehistory remained “largely unchallenged male territory,” populated — when sexed at all — by presentist gender roles: “women quietly fulfilling domestic obligations; men visibly taking on the rest.”

That vacuum sat between two foils the editors carefully distinguished: (1) a “tiny smattering” of professional work that asked whether women’s status was “high” or “low,” and (2) popular, “essentially unsubstantiated” Neolithic-goddess literature outside the professional canon. Feminist questions about origins of the [[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labour]], women and state formation, and colonial transformations of gender relations had been framed in sociocultural and historical literatures while practicing archaeologists, the editors claimed, were neither addressing nor framing them.

The institutional response was collective and deliberately hard-case. From 1986 Gero and Conkey organized a research conference emphasizing **prehistoric** analyses rather than ethnoarchaeological or historic ones, on the grounds that prehistory presented the “greatest methodological challenges to our androcentric thinking.” The “Women and Production in Prehistory” meeting was held at the Wedge Plantation, Georgetown, South Carolina, April 1988, with Wenner-Gren and NSF support. Discussants included [[moore-henrietta-l|Henrietta Moore]], Irene Silverblatt, and Peter White. Updated versions went to a 1989 SAA symposium (Atlanta; discussants Tom Patterson and Linda Cordell). The 1991 volume is thus a conference product, not a spontaneous multi-author manifesto — analogous, in organizational form, to *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* for [[processual-archaeology]], though aimed at a different problem.

Intellectually, the program drew on three decades of second-wave feminist scholarship: [[feminist-anthropology]] (Rosaldo & Lamphere 1974; Reiter 1975; MacCormack & Strathern 1980; Moore 1988), women’s history (Scott; Gordon, Buhle, Dye), feminist philosophy of science (Keller; Longino; Hartsock as engaged by Wylie), and, within archaeology, the critical-turn openings of [[post-processual-archaeology]] toward social and symbolic theory. Conkey and Gero explicitly refuse to treat gender theory as a static “quick study” to be borrowed: archaeologists must develop their own conceptual frameworks. They also name professional sociopolitics — woman-at-home ideology in the discipline (Gero 1985), discrimination statistics (Kramer & Stark 1988) — as part of the knowledge-production problem, not external noise.

## Core Claims and Conceptual Architecture

### Dual object

The volume title’s aim is stated without equivocation:

> "The title of this volume is Engendering Archaeology: Women and Prehistory; the chapters are intended to contribute to the study of gender systems in both prehistory and the practice of archaeology."

Gender is thus simultaneously a property of past social life and of present disciplinary practice. Focusing on women is framed as an **entry point**, not a claim that only women have gender:

> "Although both men and women have gender, we have chosen to focus on women as a means to engender the past because we believe that to take one - and one previously neglected - sex/gender as an entry point into the study of past human societies is particularly effective, challenging, and supported by a now substantial interdisciplinary literature."

### Three aims

Conkey and Gero define three program aims: (1) continue exposing gender bias across assumptions, concepts, models, evidence standards, and received knowledge; (2) “find” women and identify participation in gender relations, ideologies, and roles; (3) problematize underlying assumptions about gender and difference. The third aim blocks reduction of the program to recovery archaeology alone.

### Anti-add-women-and-stir

Structuring biases, they insist, do not change “merely by the addition of ‘new data’ and of ‘case studies’ about women.” Feminist critique of methodologies and of the category “woman,” plus new conceptual categories for gender groups, relations, and meanings, are required. The volume is self-described as “transitional,” with only “glimmerings” of a more radically feminist archaeology (Conkey and Gero 1988).

### Construction over determinism

> "Underlying the framework for engendering archaeology is a rejection of the biological determinism that is implicit in many models of sex role differentiation… In contrast… conceptualizations of gender as culturally and socially constructed, as historically and culturally contingent…"

Gender may be treated as a “primary structuring principle,” yet Conkey and Gero immediately caveat variability: multi-dimensional gender structures relations so diversely that “we have to be more impressed by the variability than the homogeneity,” and must not foreclose prehistoric possibility.

### Gender attribution is not the gate

A load-bearing methodological claim: the main perceived barrier — secure gender attribution of tools, hearths, ceramics — is refused as a prerequisite.

> "we do not see that a feminist approach to archaeology is dependent upon some sort of methodological breakthrough that will suddenly render women (and even men) “archaeologically visible”. Being able to “assign” certain activities or material culture to males and/or females is not the goal; it is not an end nor is it the means… we refuse to feel limited by the notion that we must provide gender attributions and must do so with a certain “fixity”."

Consequently:

> "there can be no archaeology of gender that is limited to and defined by gender attributions. Archaeologists committed to archaeologies of gender need not be “professional magicians” who can render visible the “invisible” women of the past…"

Engendering becomes understanding how gender “works” (ideology, roles, relations, meanings) — conceptual before, or without, complete sex-assignment of the inventory. A related parity argument undercuts the charge that gender is less “real” than favored processual abstractions: no archaeologist has “seen” a subsistence-settlement system, whereas men and women are known to have existed in prehistoric societies.

### Wylie’s epistemology

[[wylie-alison|Alison Wylie]]’s chapter supplies the volume’s philosophy-of-science spine. She poses two questions: why no archaeology of gender paralleling other social sciences? and what are the prospects now? The commonplace answer — methodological inaccessibility — she labels unsatisfactory. In other fields, the tangible presence of women challenged androcentric theory; in archaeology, identification of women is said to be inherently problematic and to await theory from disciplines with “direct access.” Wylie argues that account fails even though material constraints are not trivial: the gap is better explained by androcentric frameworks, essentialist assumptions, and — under [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] — exclusion of “ethnographic”/internal social variables from the scientific core of eco-system explanation (her reading of Binfordian priorities). She maps stages of feminist inquiry familiar from other disciplines (critique of androcentrism → reclaim subjects → rework categories and theory) and argues that feminist philosophy of science (empiricist, standpoint, and postmodern strands in productive tension) shows gender research can be epistemically rigorous. Prospects as of the early 1990s she judges good; pluralism, not a single method package, is the warranted stance.

### Moore’s closing constraint

[[moore-henrietta-l|Henrietta Moore]]’s epilogue refuses closure. There is no single narrative of women and production in prehistory. Once feminists make women visible, the object slips: research becomes about the conceptual basis of the social sciences, not an unproblematic category “woman.” Doing feminist archaeology means recognizing and theorizing gender as a structuring principle in the record and in practice — **not** merely identifying women or their activities.

## The Empirical Program

The volume’s honesty test is whether gender analysis changes **what** is asked of standard data classes. Cases (all attributed to chapter authors):

| Claim domain | Supporting case (attributed) | Challenge / limit | Standing in volume |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gender as agency in deep prehistory | Conkey Magdalenian contexts of action/power; Cueto de la Mina | Not “find the women” with fixity; UP social-life data thin | Programmatic demonstration |
| Households as gendered | Tringham Opovo architecture; “faces” vs faceless blobs | Household concept itself contested | Method reorientation |
| Food, space, state | Hastorf Sausa/Wanka paleoethnobotany + isotopes; Inka *mit'a* / *chicha* | Small isotope n; gender↔food labeling caution | Strong multi-proxy case |
| Women as lithic producers | Gero Huaricoto EIP flake dominance; ethno + experiment critique | Individual tool sexing still hard; model of minimum female participation | [[genderlithics]] charter |
| Pottery and women’s labor | Wright Harappan/South Asia production organization | Against universal state-subordination models | Challenges grand narrative |
| State demand on women’s craft | Brumfiel Aztec cloth, spindle whorls, griddles | Ethnohistory not transparent “model of” past | Ethnohistoric-archaeological braid |
| Women and plant domestication | Watson & Kennedy Eastern Woodlands | Against textbook DoL and “shaman cultivator” innovation-to-men | Explicit POSITION for intentional women cultivators |
| Shellfishing engendered | Claassen SMA; ethnographic women shellfishers | De-gendering in archaeological prose | Status/ideology of shellfishing |
| Women’s fixed production facilities | Jackson Mono acorn mortars, K-sites, matrilocality | Analogy precarious (author notes) | Property + surplus negotiation |
| Art and power narratives | Handsman [[lepenski-vir]] exhibit/Srejović critique | Interpretive, not re-excavation | Discourse as productive of inequality |
| Representation vs economic texts | Pollock Sumerian literary/economic/iconography/Royal Cemetery | OCR-truncated opening; later copies of “early” texts | Multi-media gender images |
| Feminist narrative form | Spector Dakota awl | Narrative is POSITION reconstruction | Method of writing as feminist tool |

**What the program explained that predecessors did not:** why “neutral” lithic, shell, plant, and household analyses silently reproduced modern sex-role ideology; how state formation and tribute can reorganize women’s labor without “finding” a female skeleton for every spindle whorl; how exhibit and textbook discourse are data for the sociology of archaeological knowledge.

**Where it remained provisional:** secure sex-attribution of many artifact classes; deep Paleolithic gender systems beyond conceptual reorientation; generalizing from ethnohistoric cases (Aztec, Mono, Dakota) to prehistory without androcentric ethnographic filters.

## Method Commitments

1. **Gender theory first** — questions and categories before, or without, complete attribution.
2. **Multi-proxy cases** — architecture, food chemistry, paleoethnobotany, lithics, ceramics, textiles, imagery, ethnohistory.
3. **Analogy under suspicion** — ethnography and ethnohistory must themselves be scrutinized for androcentrism (Conkey & Gero; Jackson; Brumfiel).
4. **Minimum-participation strategies** where attribution is weak (Gero’s habitation + local raw material + expedient flake criteria for ~90% of lithic record).
5. **Writing form as method** — Spector’s task differentiation and narrative; Handsman’s critique of exhibit naming; rejection of periodizations that homogenize male experience as pan-human (Conkey & Gero).
6. **Pluralism** — Wylie’s epistemic stance; volume “tensions” and “pluralities” as features, not bugs.
7. **Reflexivity** — who digs, who knaps experimentally, whose art is named, who is discussed as producer.

## Gilchrist 1999 — Post-Charter Synthesis (attributed)

*(Primary densification from [[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gilchrist, *Gender and Archaeology*]] 1999. Complements the 1991 multi-author charter above.)*

### Equality to difference

Gilchrist argues that gender archaeology’s ~15-year arc (to 1999) tracks feminist waves: second-wave concerns with patriarchy, [[androcentrism]], and equality of recognition produced the visibility project; third-wave pluralism, cultural/symbolic approaches, and the imperative of **difference** (between and among men and women of contrasting sexualities, ethnicities, classes) reorient the field toward identity, the body, sexuality, and masculinity. Not all gender archaeology is feminist or done by women, but the relationship is symbiotic. She distinguishes Americanist streams (labour, prestige, processual roots — Gero & Conkey 1991 as emblem) from European streams more focused on identity and cultural construction (Moore & Scott 1997).

### Method without a template

> The book has not set out to provide a template or exemplar for how to do gender archaeology… there is no single method or means of doing gender archaeology, and the interrogation of gender in antiquity will never be complete… the imperative exists to do gender archaeology well.

Pluralism without relativism: cite [[wylie-alison|Wylie]] on rigour; prefer multi-source designs that reveal ambiguity and contradiction; critical analogy; precise definitions of sex, gender, sexuality; suspicion of essentialist and cross-cultural stereotypes. [[gender-attribution]] remains useful for visibility but is not the sole gate — technology relations and representation matter; visibility must extend beyond the sexual division of labour to **embodiment**.

### Body, queer theory, science

Gilchrist imports [[butler-judith|Butler]]’s performativity and queer theory’s challenge to binary categories, while insisting queer archaeology should disrupt heteronormative protocols rather than “make gays or lesbians visible” in antiquity. Social constructionism is not absolute: cognitive science and medical history (Laqueur’s one-sex→two-sex model; sex as spectrum) require engagement with science rather than retreat. Multiple genders (two-spirit, hijra, Byzantine eunuch — as she reviews) and mortuary sex/gender mismatches pressure binary attribution. Masculinist studies are welcomed if they challenge essentialism of both sexes rather than restore male-only visibility.

### Contested garden (empirical densification)

Chapter 6’s later medieval English castle case — the **[[contested-garden]]** — is Gilchrist’s signature application: against the castle as pure masculine warrior bastion, she reconstructs elite female seclusion, private gardens, chapels, and chambers through access analysis + documents + literary metaphor (*hortus conclusus*, *Roman de la Rose*). Artefact-based room gendering fails; seclusion can mark **prestige** (king, monastics, noble women), not only subordination; masculine and feminine are embodied sensorily but not as exclusive domains. The case operationalizes [[gender-lifecourse]] and phenomenological space for historical archaeology.

### What the synthesis adds to What Survived

Adds to the 1991 charter’s surviving toolkit: explicit second→third wave periodization; US/Europe tradition split; lifecourse and queer-disruption program statements; medieval multi-source exemplar; glossary-level definitions ([[androcentrism]], gender attribution, performance) that stabilize teaching vocabulary. Still outstanding for this hub: Conkey & Spector 1984 primary text; Spector 1993; later intersectional/queer handbooks; Gilchrist’s nunnery monographs.

## What Survived

- **Gender archaeology** as a normal research domain in handbooks, journals, and curricula — the 1980s “no literature” vacuum closed.
- **Androcentrism critique** as standard reflexive tool (Man the Hunter / Man the Toolmaker / woman-at-home ideology).
- **Gender attribution is not the gate** — still cited against “show me the sex of the knapper” dismissals.
- **Domain-specific programs:** genderlithics lineage; gendered household archaeology; food/gender/state multi-proxy designs; critique of de-gendered subsistence language.
- **Narrative and exhibit politics** as legitimate archaeological analysis (Spector; Handsman).
- Vocabulary: *engendering*, *archaeology of gender*, *gender archaeology*, task differentiation, households with faces.
- Institutional: gender sessions at SAA/EAA; later queer and intersectional extensions that the 1991 volume only glimmers toward.

What did **not** survive unchanged: early-second-wave residual essentialism (already under pressure in Moore’s epilogue); any hope of a single feminist method package; the idea that post-processualism alone would deliver gender analysis without explicit feminist theory.

## Current Status and Research Frontiers

As of Gero & Conkey 1991 (and as of vault coverage 2026-07-10), feminist/gender archaeology is an **active and mainstream** research domain, with research frontiers (not yet primary-ingested here) in queer archaeologies, masculinities, intersectional race/class/colonial analyses, and aDNA-informed kinship that must still obey the four-way non-identity rule (genes ≠ language ≠ culture ≠ people). The 1991 charter remains the historical hinge: the moment Anglophone archaeology acquired a multi-author empirical demonstration that gender was researchable. **Gilchrist 1999 synthesis now primary-ingested** (this section). Still outstanding: Conkey & Spector 1984 primary article; Spector’s 1993 book *What This Awl Means*; later synthetic handbooks; Gero’s full socio-politics corpus; Gilchrist’s medieval nunnery monographs.
