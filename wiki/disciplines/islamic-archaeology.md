# Islamic Archaeology

Subfield of [[archaeology]] concerned with the material culture of Muslim societies and of the Islamic faith as a structuring force on life — from the seventh century CE to the present, across a near-global range. Parent: [[archaeology]]. Closely adjacent to Islamic art history, Islamic history/textual studies, and the anthropology of Islam.

## Scope

Objects, buildings, settlements, landscapes, burials, diet, dress, trade goods, and everyday assemblages produced or used in Muslim contexts. Insoll (1999) frames the field’s object as both **Islam** (a cross-cultural religious superstructure) and **Muslims** (diverse local practices and material substructures) — not a single essentialized “Islamic civilization” style-package.

## Historical Development

- **Art-historical and monument tradition.** Grabar and others (as Insoll summarizes) long treated Islamic archaeology as chronological/spatial authentication of monuments and high-status objects; cataloguing and collecting dominated over social interpretation (attributed, Insoll 1999 citing Grabar 1971, 1976).
- **Institutional separation.** Specialists often housed in area/Islamic-studies units rather than mainstream archaeology departments — language skills concentrate, but theoretical exchange with [[processual-archaeology|processual]] and [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] programs lagged (attributed, Insoll 1999).
- **Social-archaeology program.** *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]* (Insoll 1999) argues for multi-disciplinary, multi-scalar work that treats religion as a way of life visible in domestic space, diet, magic, warfare, and settlement form — not only mosques and luxury arts. Homology claimed with European medieval archaeology’s earlier self-examination.
- **Living religion.** Continuity of core doctrines since early Islam is treated as an interpretive advantage (and a sensitivity burden) relative to prehistoric religion archaeology (attributed, Insoll 1999).

## Theoretical Landscape

Programmatic frame in this vault (all attributed, Insoll 1999):

1. **Superstructure / substructure.** Islam as a uniform superstructure of fundamentals of belief with a diverse substructure of practices, cultures, and material manifestations — so Muslim communities should be archaeologically recognizable, yet manifestations will be extremely diverse.
2. **One Islam, not only local “Islams.”** Against pure regional fragmentation; flexible thought/practice and local assimilation are accepted inside one faith that can still leave shared material signatures.
3. **Ideals vs realities.** Qur’an, hadith, and shari’ah guide interpretation but are not perfect manuals of past practice; individuals are selective; non-observance is expected; complete “ideal suites” of Islamic material culture are unlikely ever to appear in totality; exceptions may outweigh orthodox residue (grave goods, alcohol, misaligned mosques, non-courtyard houses).
4. **Dual analytical scales.** Immutable structuring principles (e.g. obligation of prayer → mosque; privacy/gender segregation → domestic form; burial orientation ideals) vs cultural diversity (regional styles, fashions, pre-Islamic heritage).
5. **Annales mix-and-match.** Longue durée ≈ Islamic structuring codes; conjonctures/événements ≈ regionalism and fashion — heuristic, not dogmatic Braudel.
6. **Anti-essentialism.** Prefer “Muslim” over “Islamic” as a descriptive term when flexibility and individual diversity matter; reject pure-Arab-heartland vs impure-periphery hierarchies; reject Hollywood “ideal Muslim” stereotypes.

### Programmatic critique of the field (Insoll 1999)

| Defect (attributed) | Content |
|---|---|
| Institutional isolation | Specialists in area/Islamic units, pigeonholed as “exotic,” cut off from mainstream theory |
| Art-history subservience | Grabar’s early authentication/dating/localization program; cataloguing over interpretation |
| Top-heavy elite bias | Great mosques, palaces, lustre/rock-crystal/ivory; silent masses under-studied |
| Neglected evidence classes | Fauna, botanicals, everyday pottery, economic/demographic/landscape archaeology |
| Superposition neglect | Islamic levels rushed or discarded to reach “fashionable” earlier deposits |
| Theory lag | Self-examination done in medieval European archaeology and in Islamic anthropology (Geertz, Gellner, Gilsenan) largely bypassed Islamic archaeology |

### Recognition domains (structuring principles)

| Domain | Ideal signatures (Insoll 1999) | Diversity / exceptions |
|---|---|---|
| [[mosque]] | Qibla orientation; mihrab; prayer-space scales (masjid, jami‘, musalla); often sterile deposits | Ephemeral/nomad prayer grounds; conversion/qiblarization; regional styles; modern conversions hard to read |
| Domestic / [[household]] | Privacy, purdah/gender domains, dual male/female communal spheres; courtyard package as stereotype | Plan alone insufficient; pre-Islamic privacy features; tents, tower houses, diaspora terraces |
| Diet | Halal/haram/makruh rules; four faunal questions (pig/dog absence; butchery; slaughter ages; herd composition); alcohol avoidance | No Muslim cuisine; regional food fashion; exceptions (alcohol, residual pig); botanical crop association ≠ “Muslim identity” of species |
| Burial | Right side, face to qibla; few grave goods; single interments / gender segregation | Above-ground tomb diversity huge; grave goods and communal graves occur; Renfrew-style separation of burial from religion rejected |
| Settlement | Minimum: mosque + Muslim burials; public/semi-private/private domains; mix-and-match not orientalizing “Islamic city” isnad | Created (amsar) vs adapted cities; Bacharach power-locus phases; villages/camps understudied |
| Art / trade | Surface decoration, calligraphy, coinage reforms; trade as bridgehead of Islamization in some regions | “Art of Muslims” ≠ unitary Islamic art; figural practice continues; Scandinavia silver without conversion |



## West African Case Densification — Gao (Insoll 1996)

*[[insoll-islam-archaeology-history-gao-1996|Islam, Archaeology and History: Gao Region]]* supplies the vault’s first **regional excavation case** for Islamic archaeology beyond the 1999 program book:

- Multi-domain material criteria (mosque, burial/stelae, rectangular/fired-brick architecture, imports) applied to [[gao-ancien]] / [[gao-saney]].
- **Islamized quartier** vs surrounding non-/less-Islamized settlements — diversity inside one entrepôt.
- Trade (trans-Saharan + inter-regional) as primary Islamization vector; staged conversion (Fisher model applied).
- Explicit four-way caution on Songhay/empire labels.

Study: [[insoll-gao-region-study]]. Coverage for West African Islamic archaeology: now **moderate** (still thin for East Africa/Swahili and Maghreb excavation monographs).

## Methods

Standing architecture and epigraphy remain central; Insoll stresses underused classes — [[faunal-analysis]], [[paleoethnobotany]], everyday ceramics, landscape/settlement archaeology, ethnohistoric analogy carefully flagged. Recognition criteria above are **ideals against which diversity is measured**, not universal checklists. Living religion supplies multi-disciplinary privilege (anthropology, history, sociology) and ethnohistoric analogy — hypothesis-generating, not proof. Developmental lag: early conquest material culture need not appear fully fledged (e.g. Spain 711 → Córdoba mosque 785–6); ninth century flagged as a possible turning point for recognizable Islamic features.

## Current State

Vault coverage begins with Insoll 1999 as the social-archaeology charter. Field has developed substantially since (heritage politics, urban CRM, scientific analyses, regional schools) — **not yet ingested**. Collection coverage: **weak**.

## Reflexivity

Western non-Muslim scholarship on Islam is historically entangled with orientalism and contemporary media stereotyping (terrorism/intolerance frames); Insoll argues the past is common property and that Muslim colleagues supported non-Muslim study, while insisting on sensitivity. Top-heavy research designs reproduce elite bias. Gender: the 1999 synthesis itself notes masculine emphasis in sources and monuments. National and confessional appropriations of Islamic heritage are live political stakes.
