# Organizational Sociology

Organizational sociology studies formal organizations — firms, agencies, hospitals, schools, museums, voluntary associations — as social forms in their own right: how they are structured, why their structures take the shapes they do, and how they relate to the technical and institutional environments that surround them.

## Scope

The subfield's object is the **formal organization** as a unit of sociological analysis distinct from both the individuals who compose it and the broader society that contains it: the table of organization, the relational networks and boundary-spanning exchanges that generate coordination problems, and — the specifically institutionalist emphasis — the symbolic and legitimating environment ("rationalized myths," professions, the state, law) in which organizations are embedded. Organizational sociology sits at the boundary of sociology and management/organization studies, sharing subject matter but privileging causal accounts pitched at the field, sector, or societal level rather than the level of managerial technique.

## Historical Development

[[weber-max|Weber]]'s theory of bureaucracy — bureaucratization driven by market competition among capitalist firms, competition among states, and demands for equal legal protection — supplies the classical starting point, later restated and revised in [[new-institutionalism]] (see [[iron-cage]]). In the **Fieldwork Revolution** and **Postwar Expansion** eras, Philip Selznick's "old institutionalism" (*TVA and the Grass Roots*, 1949; *Leadership in Administration*, 1957) analyzed organizations as embedded in local communities, co-opting external constituencies and developing distinctive organizational "character," while the Carnegie School (Herbert Simon, James March, Richard Cyert) developed a cognitive, bounded-rationality account of organizational decision-making (satisficing, the "garbage-can model") that displaced earlier rational-actor assumptions. The 1960s–70s saw contingency theory, resource-dependence theory, and population ecology (Hannan and Freeman) treat organizational structure and survival as shaped respectively by task environment, resource dependencies, and competitive selection among organizational populations.

The **new institutionalism** dates its own birth, by its own account, to 1977 — the year [[meyer-john-w|John Meyer]] published "The Effects of Education as an Institution" and, with [[rowan-brian|Brian Rowan]], "Institutionalized Organizations: Formal Structure as Myth and Ceremony." Meyer and Rowan argued organizations adopt institutionalized practices to gain legitimacy and survival, independent of technical efficiency, and cope with the resulting tension through [[decoupling]] and a "logic of confidence." [[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio]] and [[powell-walter-w|Powell]]'s 1983 "The Iron Cage Revisited" (ch. 3 of [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]]) extended this into a theory of [[institutional-isomorphism]] — coercive, mimetic, and normative mechanisms making organizations in a field more similar without necessarily making them more efficient. By 1985 [[zucker-lynne|Lynne Zucker]]'s UCLA conference had, per the volume's own account, brought together enough scholars for "neoinstitutional theory to be named and reified," feeding into the 1991 anthology this page draws on for its **critical-turn/contemporary**-era coverage.

## Theoretical Landscape

[[new-institutionalism]] is the subfield's dominant contemporary program in this collection, itself explicitly built in reaction against Parsonsian normative action theory (see [[parsons-talcott]]) and toward ethnomethodological and phenomenological "practical action" (see [[garfinkel-harold]], [[institutionalization]]). It stands alongside — and, per Fligstein's chapter in the same volume, in explicit tension with — power-based accounts of the [[organizational-field]] (see [[fligstein-neil]], [[strategic-action-fields-theory]]), and, in plain text, **population ecology** (competitive isomorphism), **resource-dependence theory**, and **transaction-cost economics** as rival or complementary lenses on organizational structure and change. [[rational-choice-theory]] and [[new-economic-sociology]] (embeddedness; see [[embeddedness]]) supply adjacent accounts of economic organization; Friedland and Alford's "institutional logics" concept (ch. 10 of the 1991 volume) offers a further synthesis attempt, arguing distinct societal sectors (market, state, family, religion) carry distinct organizing logics.

## Methods

As evidenced in the 1991 volume: comparative historical/case-study analysis (Selznick-style organizational ethnography; DiMaggio's study of US art museums 1920–1940; Galaskiewicz on Minneapolis corporate philanthropy); event-history and diffusion analysis (Tolbert and Zucker on municipal civil-service reform); archival and quantitative studies of large-firm diversification (Fligstein); cross-national comparative studies of intercorporate networks (Orrù, Biggart, and Hamilton on Japan, Taiwan, Korea); and a laboratory experiment operationalizing institutionalization as a manipulated variable (Zucker's autokinetic-effect studies — see [[institutionalization]]).

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Organizational sociology overlaps heavily with **economic sociology** (embeddedness, markets as social constructions), **management and organization studies** (shared subject matter, differing units and levels of analysis — organizational sociology privileges field/sector-level explanation over managerial technique), and **political sociology** (the state as a source of coercive isomorphism and a shaper of organizational fields; see Fligstein's tripartite institutional spheres of organization, field, and state).

## Current State

As of the 1991 volume, the field registers a partial "rapprochement" between institutionalism and population ecology — institutionalists more willing to acknowledge competition and selection, ecologists more willing to admit institutional factors into survival models — alongside live internal critique (Fligstein; Brint and Karabel) that institutionalist accounts had "overestimated the role of norms... and underestimated the relative power of actors." This wiki's coverage of the subfield rests so far on a single major anthology; the state-of-the-field summary above should be read as that anthology's own 1991 self-assessment, not an independently audited survey of the discipline since.

## Reflexivity

The empirical base assembled in [[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]] is heavily **US-centric** — art museums, community colleges, corporate philanthropy, municipal civil service, and the 100 largest US firms are the volume's principal cases — with the Orrù, Biggart, and Hamilton chapter on Japan, Taiwan, and Korea a partial exception. The volume's own introduction concedes this: the new institutionalism's claims about "modernized society" and organizational fields were developed chiefly from American organizational data, a sampling-frame fact that bears on how far the theory's generalizations should travel.

## Collection Coverage Note

Moderate. One major anthology — Powell and DiMaggio (eds.), *The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis* (1991) — ingested 2026-07-10, giving strong coverage of the new-institutionalist program (Meyer and Rowan; DiMaggio and Powell; Zucker; Jepperson; Scott and Meyer; Friedland and Alford) and partial coverage of rival power-based (Fligstein), population-ecology, and cross-national comparative (Orrù, Biggart, and Hamilton) perspectives. Selznick-era old institutionalism, contingency theory, and resource-dependence theory are covered only secondhand, through this volume's own historiography of "old vs. new" institutionalism — primary sources for those traditions are not yet ingested.
