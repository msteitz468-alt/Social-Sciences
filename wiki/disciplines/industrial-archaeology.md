## Scope

Industrial archaeology studies the physical remains of industrialization — factories,
mills, mines, and the settlements and infrastructure built around them — as material
evidence of production, labor, and technological change.

## Historical Development

Symonds and Casella trace the field's name to Michael Rix, who popularized the term
"industrial archaeology" in a 1955 article in *The Amateur Historian*. The field's
early character was shaped by volunteer and extramural (adult-education) origins,
characterized in the chapter with the phrase "history with gumboots" — fieldwork
conducted largely outside professional academic archaeology. R. A. Buchanan supplied an
influential definition of the field in 1978. Professionalization followed in 1973 with
the founding of the Association for Industrial Archaeology (AIA) and TICCIH (The
International Committee for the Conservation of the Industrial Heritage), alongside the
National Record of Industrial Monuments (NRIM) (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 9).

## Theoretical Landscape

Richard Gould is quoted characterizing the field's lack of an articulated intellectual
or theoretical tradition as its "Achilles heel" — an attributed critique of industrial
archaeology's historically descriptive, monument-recording character rather than a
settled judgment. Mary Beaudry and Stephen Mrozowski's work at the Boott Mills complex
in Lowell, Massachusetts, is credited with broadening the field's object of study from
industrial workplaces alone to workers' domestic settlements, integrating labor history
and household archaeology into what had been a more narrowly technological subfield.

## Methods

Methods include standing-building and machine survey, oral history, and documentary
research into company and lease records.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Industrial archaeology overlaps with [[historical-archaeology]], labor and social
history, and [[household-archaeology]] following the Boott Mills broadening; it also
intersects with heritage and conservation practice through bodies such as TICCIH.

## Current State

Donald Hardesty's work on Western mining landscapes is cited as extending industrial
archaeology into extractive-industry settlement archaeology. The Alderley Sandhills
project (Hagg Cottages, combining excavation with oral history) and a case study of the
John Watts cutlery works in Sheffield — an eighty-nine-room complex documented through
combined machine survey and lease-and-land records — are cited as detailed
site-specific applications. Jan Nijhof's work on the Ruhr is cited for its attention to
a multiethnic industrial settlement described in the source as a "cité-German" pattern.
The chapter identifies three current thematic foci for the field: globalisation,
community heritage, and identity (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 9).

## Reflexivity

The field's volunteer and extramural origins shaped its long-standing descriptive,
monument-inventory character, which Gould's "Achilles heel" critique frames as a
deficit relative to theoretically self-conscious archaeology. The Boott Mills
broadening toward workers' settlements is itself a reflexive move within the field,
shifting attention from industrial technology and elite ownership toward labor and
domestic life.
