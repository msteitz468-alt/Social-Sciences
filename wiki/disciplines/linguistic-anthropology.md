# Linguistic Anthropology

## Scope

Linguistic anthropology is the subfield of [[anthropology]] that takes **language** as its object — both as a formal system in its own right and in its relations to thought, culture, society, and history. In the North American four-field tradition it grew directly out of the [[boas-franz|Boasian]] program of documenting Indigenous American languages, and it retains anthropology's comparative, holistic, and reflexive orientation. As [[sapir-edward|Sapir]] framed it in *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]* (1921), the discipline studies "what language is, its variability in place and time, and its relations to other fundamental human interests — thought, history, race, culture, art."

## Historical Development

- **Classical era (1890–1920).** [[boas-franz|Boas]] establishes the descriptive study of unwritten languages as a core anthropological task (*Handbook of American Indian Languages*, 1911), insisting each language be described in its own terms rather than through Indo-European grammatical categories, and arguing for the independence of race, language, and culture. See [[race-and-culture-debate]].
- **Fieldwork revolution (1920–1945).** [[sapir-edward|Sapir]]'s *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]* (1921) gives the field its classic synthetic statement and the concept of **[[linguistic-drift]]**. American structural linguistics consolidates around the Sapir (mentalist/patterning) and Bloomfield (behaviorist) poles. The [[linguistic-typology|conceptual typology of languages]] and the analysis of [[phonetic-pattern|phonetic patterning]] date from this period. In British social anthropology, [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Coral Gardens]]* Vol. II (1935) states an **ethnographic theory of language**: speech as cultural mode of action within the [[context-of-situation]]; meaning as function; corpus of garden terminology and magical formulae as required ethnographic product — complementary to American pattern linguistics.
- **Later developments (now partly ingested).** **[[linguistic-relativity]]** (Sapir 1929 seed; [[whorf-benjamin-lee|Whorf]] 1930s–41 primary in *[[whorf-language-thought-reality-1956|LTR]]*) is the American language-and-thought charter: grammar as shaper of [[habitual-thought|habitual thought]]; [[standard-average-european|SAE]] vs Hopi; [[cryptotype|cryptotypes]]; debate still open ([[linguistic-relativity-debate]]). **Critical Turn — ethnography of speaking / sociolinguistics:** [[hymes-dell|Dell Hymes]], *[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations in Sociolinguistics]]* (1974), founds the research program of the **[[ethnography-of-speaking]]** / method **[[ethnography-of-communication]]**, units [[speech-community]], [[speech-event]], [[speaking-model|SPEAKING]], and **[[communicative-competence]]** against Chomskyan ideal-speaker linguistics ([[communicative-competence-debate]]). Firthian contextual linguistics as a named British school remains thin.
- **Contemporary — language ideology program (1990s–2000s).** [[silverstein-michael|Silverstein]]’s 1979 definition of linguistic ideologies; AAA sessions and *Language Ideologies* (Schieffelin, Woolard & Kroskrity 1998); SAR seminar 1994 → [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|Kroskrity (ed.), *Regimes of Language* (2000)]]. Central concept [[language-ideology]]; toolkit [[iconization]] / [[fractal-recursivity]] / [[erasure-semiotic|erasure]] (Irvine & Gal); [[monoglot-standard]]; [[language-regimentation]]. Links microanalysis to political-economic macroprocesses (Kroskrity’s program claim).

## Theoretical Landscape

The founding commitments on the American side are Boasian: descriptive rigor on each language's own terms, [[historical-particularism|historical-particularist]] reconstruction of language history, and the non-correspondence of language, race, and culture. Sapir adds a strong emphasis on **pattern** — that each language is an internally coherent formal system — and on directional historical change ([[linguistic-drift]]). Whorf radicalizes the language–thought link into a contested research program ([[linguistic-relativity]]; Theory Hub: [[hubs/theory/linguistic-relativity]]). On the British ethnographic side, Malinowski (1935) prioritizes **pragmatic function** and cultural context over formal pattern or historical reconstruction as the anthropologist's linguistic task — language cannot be a third anthropology separate from culture. Hymes (1974) reconstructs the Critical-Turn program: **socially constituted linguistics** via ethnography of speaking; Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/ethnography-of-speaking]]; Thinkers Hub [[hubs/thinkers/hymes-dell]].

## Methods

Descriptive fieldwork on unwritten languages, phonetic analysis (the [[phonetic-pattern|phonetic pattern]] of a language), and [[historical-linguistics|historical-comparative reconstruction]] (phonetic law, linguistic stocks, genetic relationship) are core American methods; the anthropological [[comparative-method]] frames cross-linguistic classification ([[linguistic-typology]]). Malinowski adds: long residence language learning; **corpus inscriptionum** woven into continuous ethnographic narrative; interlinear fixed-equivalent translation plus free translation justified by situation; fourfold commentary on ritual speech ([[participant-observation]], [[malinowski-coral-gardens-study]]). Hymes adds **[[ethnography-of-communication]]**: community as frame; nested situation/[[speech-event]]/act; [[speaking-model|SPEAKING]] component analysis; rules of co-occurrence and alternation.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Linguistic anthropology sits at the boundary of [[anthropology]] and general linguistics, sharing objects with historical linguistics, phonetics, and (later) sociology of language. Its distinguishing commitment is anthropological: language studied as part of culture and as evidence for culture history, without being reduced to it. Malinowski's program is continuous with social anthropology of institutions ([[magic]], gardens, law); Sapir's with general linguistics and culture-and-personality.

## Current State

Coverage is **moderate** with several primary poles: [[sapir-language-1921|Sapir 1921]] (pattern, drift, typology); [[malinowski-coral-gardens-1935|Malinowski 1935 Vol. II]] (context of situation, pragmatic meaning, magical language); [[whorf-language-thought-reality-1956|Whorf LTR 1956/2012]] (linguistic relativity, SAE/Hopi, cryptotype); [[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Hymes 1974]] (ethnography of speaking; communicative competence); *[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972) and [[bauman-sherzer-explorations-ethnography-speaking-1974|Bauman & Sherzer 1974]] (empirical ethnography of speaking); [[kroskrity-regimes-of-language-2000|*Regimes of Language* (2000)]] (language-ideology program charter volume — Silverstein, Irvine & Gal, Kroskrity, Schieffelin, et al.); and — at a further contemporary edge — [[everett-dont-sleep-2008|Everett 2008]] on [[piraha|Pirahã]] (culture→grammar challenge to [[universal-grammar|UG]]; [[piraha-recursion-debate]]). Remaining gaps: Boas's linguistic writings as such, Bloomfield, Firth, neo-Whorfian experimental literature, Woolard monographs, *Language Ideologies* (1998) full volume, Labov *Inner City*, Gumperz *Discourse Strategies*, and primary generative/Pirahã technical sources.

## Reflexivity

The subfield's American foundational corpus is entangled with the salvage-ethnography moment: American Indigenous languages were documented as "vanishing," under conditions of dispossession, by scholars (Boas, Sapir) who were also arguing against the racial hierarchies of their day. The framing of languages as "primitive" vs. "civilized" was explicitly rejected by this same tradition — Sapir insisted that so-called primitive languages are often more, not less, formally elaborate than European ones. Malinowski's Trobriand linguistics is colonial-era Melanesian ethnography: immobilising speech under Australian administration, with period language ("savage tribe") and reverse of missionary translation aims as his explicit contrast.
