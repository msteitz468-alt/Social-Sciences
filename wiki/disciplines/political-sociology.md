# Political Sociology

## Scope

**Political sociology** is the [[sociology|sociological]] study of power, domination, the state, political leadership, and the relation between social structure and political order. It differs from political science by treating political institutions as *social* forms — asking not what constitutions prescribe but how power is actually held, legitimated, and organized, and how it is embedded in class, status, and belief. Its defining objects in this wiki are [[the-state|the state]], [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]], [[bureaucracy|bureaucracy]], and political leadership. As a named strand it nests under [[sociology]] alongside [[social-psychology]].

## Historical Development

### Founding Era (to c. 1890)

The **conflict tradition** originates with [[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]]: the state as an instrument of class domination arising with private property and class society (*[[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848|Communist Manifesto]]*; see [[historical-materialism]]). Marx’s *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (1852; study [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]]) supplies the tradition’s classic **case analysis** of class fractions, [[bonapartism]], and [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state|relative autonomy of the executive]] in France 1848–51. Engels’s *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]* (1884/1891) supplies the tradition’s classic **origin theory** of the state — gens dissolution, territorial membership, special public force, class organ with relative-independence exceptions, [[withering-away-of-the-state]] horizon ([[state-formation]]). [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]] founds a comparative **political sociology of democracy** — the democratic social state, [[equality-of-conditions]], administrative centralization, and the [[tyranny-of-the-majority]] / [[democratic-despotism]] as sociological tendencies rather than legal facts (*[[tocqueville-democracy-in-america-1835|Democracy in America]]*; analyzed as [[tocqueville-democracy-study]]).

### Classical Era (c. 1890–1920)

The subfield consolidates. [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]] develops an **elite-theoretic** account of political power — the [[circulation-of-elites|circulation of elites]], [[lions-and-foxes]], [[demagogic-plutocracy]] — locating rule in psychology and elite renewal rather than in legitimacy or class (*[[pareto-mind-and-society-1935|The Mind and Society]]*; [[paretian-sociology]]).

[[weber-max|Max Weber]]'s *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]* (1919) is the subfield's founding synthesis. It supplies: the definition of [[the-state|the state]] by its [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force]]; the three pure types of [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]] (traditional / [[charismatic-authority|charismatic]] / legal-rational); the analysis of [[bureaucracy|expert officialdom]] and its separation from the means of administration; the comparative sociology of parties and [[plebiscitary-leader-democracy|plebiscitary leader-democracy]]; and a normative political ethics built on the contrast between the [[ethic-of-ultimate-ends|ethic of ultimate ends]] and the [[ethic-of-responsibility|ethic of responsibility]]. It extends the *[[calling-beruf|Beruf]]* motif of *[[weber-protestant-ethic-1905|The Protestant Ethic]]* from economic into political life.

### Later eras

Mid-century power-structure analysis: Mills *[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]* (1956) — see Postwar Expansion. Still queued from primary ingest: full Weber ES composite beyond Part I, Michels's oligarchy thesis, Schumpeter's competitive-elitist democracy, Dahl and pluralist primaries, Domhoff, and contemporary political sociology.

**Mann / IEMP (1986, attributed).** [[mann-michael|Michael Mann]]'s *[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]* (1986) supplies a neo-Weberian organizational multi-causal program for political sociology: societies as overlapping [[power-networks|power networks]], not unitary systems; four sources of social power — ideological, economic, military, political ([[iemp-model|IEMP]]) — as organizational means rather than levels of a totality; political power restricted to centralized, territorially bounded regulation (state power) and dualized as domestic ([[despotic-power|despotic]] vs [[infrastructural-power|infrastructural]]) plus geopolitics. Separates military from political power against the classical triad that folds force into the state. See [[the-state]], [[state-formation]].

## Theoretical Landscape

Three classical programs stand side by side (attributed voice): the **Marxian** class theory of the state ([[historical-materialism]], thickened by the *Brumaire* on [[bonapartism]] and [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]]); **Weberian** legitimate-domination and the sociology of bureaucracy and leadership; and **elite theory** ([[paretian-sociology]], [[circulation-of-elites]]). Tocqueville's comparative democratic analysis sits partly athwart these. Weber's realist, leader-selection view of democracy ([[plebiscitary-leader-democracy]]) anticipates later competitive-elitist democratic theory; the Marx–Weber contrast over the autonomy of the political is the field's central founding tension ([[relative-autonomy-of-the-state-debate]]).

**Mann (IEMP, attributed).** [[mann-michael|Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|SSP I]]*, 1986) extends the multi-causal Weberian line against both Marxian last-instance primacy and neo-Weberian "dimensions of a unitary society": the [[iemp-model|IEMP model]] treats ideological, economic, military, and political organizations as overlapping networks whose relative organizational reach varies historically; rejects class as general motor and "society" as closed unit. Dense concepts: [[infrastructural-power]], [[despotic-power]], [[social-caging]], [[organic-national-state]].

### Postwar Expansion

[[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]] (*[[mills-power-elite-1956|The Power Elite]]*, 1956; study [[mills-power-elite-study]]) supplies the subfield's principal mid-century **power-structure** analysis of the United States: the [[power-elite]] as occupants of corporate, military, and political command posts; rejection of simple Marxian "ruling class" and of the [[theory-of-balance]] as a master model; [[middle-levels-of-power]] vs top; [[military-ascendancy]]; [[mass-society]] (public vs mass); [[organized-irresponsibility]] and [[higher-immorality]]. Standing dispute: [[power-elite-vs-pluralism-debate]]. Completes the stratification trilogy with *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951).

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Ralf Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict in Industrial Society]]*, 1959) supplies the subfield's principal postwar **[[conflict-theory|conflict-theoretic]]** reconstruction of class and the state: political society as an [[imperatively-coordinated-association|imperatively coordinated association]]; the ruling class as governmental elites plus interested parties and administrative staff; bureaucracy as service class *in* but not alone *the* ruling class; political democracy as institutionalized regulation of political conflict; freedom vs totalitarianism as pluralism vs monism toward conflict; and the **institutional isolation** of industrial from political conflict in "post-capitalist" societies. The program competes with both Marxian property class theory and integration-only models of order ([[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]]).

[[lipset-seymour-martin|Seymour Martin Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, 1960; study [[lipset-political-man-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/lipset-political-man-study]]) supplies the subfield's principal mid-century **comparative survey synthesis of democratic conditions**: stable democracy as cleavage *plus* consensus; [[economic-development-and-democracy]] (development raises chances of sustained democracy — [[modernization-theory]]); [[legitimacy-and-effectiveness]]; [[working-class-authoritarianism]] and multi-class fascism bases; electoral [[democratic-class-struggle]] and [[cross-pressures]]; union oligarchy as private-government problem; [[end-of-ideology]] postscript. Debates: [[economic-development-democracy-debate]], [[working-class-authoritarianism-debate]], [[end-of-ideology-debate]]. Complements Mills (power structure) and Dahrendorf (authority-class conflict) with cross-national electoral and regime-stability analysis.

### Knowledge, ideology, and scientific politics (Mannheim)

[[mannheim-karl|Karl Mannheim]] (*[[mannheim-ideology-and-utopia-1936|Ideology and Utopia]]*, 1929/1936) supplies a classical **[[sociology-of-knowledge]]** program for political sociology: why a science of politics is blocked under rationalistic culture; five ideal-typical styles of thought (bureaucratic conservatism, historical conservatism, liberal-democratic bourgeois, socialist-communist, fascism) with distinct theory–practice meanings; synthesis of partial perspectives as problem; the [[free-floating-intelligentsia|socially unattached intelligentsia]]; [[ideology]]/[[utopia]] pairing. Claims remain attributed; see also [[relationism-vs-relativism-debate]].

## Methods

- Weber: the [[ideal-type|ideal type]] (pure types of domination) plus historical-comparative reconstruction — *[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]*.
- Tocqueville: comparative institutional and travel observation — [[tocqueville-democracy-study]].
- Pareto: the [[logico-experimental-method|logico-experimental method]] — residues/derivations and social equilibrium — [[pareto-mind-and-society-1935]].
- Marx & Engels: historical-materialist class analysis (programmatic in the *Manifesto*; case analysis in the *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|Eighteenth Brumaire]]*; origin theory in *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*).
- Lipset: comparative aggregate indicators + secondary multi-country survey analysis + historical and organizational cases — *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Overlaps political science (shares the object, differs in treating power as socially embedded rather than constitutionally defined), economic sociology (the state–economy boundary; Weber's administration/production analogy to Marx), and the sociology of religion (charisma migrates from religious to political authority). Boundary with history: ideal-typical/comparative analysis vs. concrete narrative.

Racial politics strand now includes [[racial-formation-theory]] (Omi & Winant — racial state, trajectory of racial politics, colorblind hegemony).

## Current State

**Contentious politics program (ingested):** McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly *[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* (2001) supplies the vault's mechanism–process synthesis for movements, revolutions, nationalism, and democratization — [[contentious-politics-theory]]; Hub [[hubs/theory/contentious-politics-theory]].

## Current State

Classical and mid-century foundations now include Mills, Dahrendorf, and Lipset. **Contemporary civic-engagement program:** [[putnam-robert-d|Putnam]] *[[putnam-bowling-alone-2000|Bowling Alone]]* (2000) — [[civic-social-capital]], [[political-participation]] trends, democracy outcomes (Section IV), [[bowling-alone-debate]]. **Field-theoretic synthesis:** [[fligstein-neil|Fligstein]] & [[mcadam-doug|McAdam]] *[[fligstein-mcadam-theory-of-fields-2012|A Theory of Fields]]* (2012) — [[strategic-action-fields-theory]]; state as system of SAFs; civil-rights illustration of [[episode-of-contention]] and [[field-settlement]] (McAdam lineage). Further fronts (state capacity monographs, *Dynamics of Contention* primary, third-wave democratization, populism after the 1990s) still require sources not yet fully ingested.

## Reflexivity

Political sociology's founding texts are politically engaged, not neutral: Marx and Engels wrote as revolutionaries; Tocqueville as a liberal aristocrat anxious about mass democracy; Pareto's *Trattato* polemicizes against "pluto-demagogic" democracy and carries dated race/sex illustrations; Weber lectured *Politics as a Vocation* amid the German Revolution of 1918–19 and advocated a plebiscitary presidency — a stance later read (contestedly) as prefiguring *Führer* politics (see [[plebiscitary-leader-democracy]]). Lipset writes as a Cold War democratic partisan — democracy as "the good society itself in operation" — with Western industrial democracies as the evaluative center and communism/nationalism framed as system challenges. Each program's account of "legitimate" power reflects its author's political situation; the subfield's own categories (legitimacy-as-belief, elite, class, development) are theoretical commitments, not neutral descriptions.
