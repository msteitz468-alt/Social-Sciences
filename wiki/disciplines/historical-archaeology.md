# Historical Archaeology

Subfield of [[archaeology]] concerned with periods and places for which documentary records exist, and — in its influential North American formulation — with the **modern world** produced by European expansion, capitalism, and global interconnection. Parent: [[archaeology]]. Named research program densifying the modern-world definition: [[global-historical-archaeology]] ([[orser-charles-e|Orser]] 1996).

## Scope

Text-aided archaeology of recent centuries in the Americas; in Europe often continuous with medieval and post-medieval archaeology; globally, the archaeology of colonial, capitalist, and modern social formations.

[[orser-charles-e|Orser]] (1996) argued the proper object is the **creation and re-creation of the modern world**, not isolated historic sites. With Fagan (1995) he held HA has been defined three ways — as a **time period**, as a **research method**, and as the study of the **modern world** — and that only the last "unlocks the power of the field." His preferred definition: "a multi- and interdisciplinary field that shares a special relationship with the formal disciplines of anthropology and history and seeks to understand the global nature of modern life." Literacy alone does not define the field; modern times ~1492–present are structured by the [[four-haunts]].

## Historical Development

- **Precursors:** antiquarian and classical/medieval text-aided traditions ([[classical-archaeology]]; European post-medieval archaeology).
- **North American professionalization:** mid-20th c.; Society for Historical Archaeology (1967); Colonial Williamsburg and related reconstruction archaeology (Noël Hume); pattern and "small things" approaches (Deetz). Early equation with colonial sites (Setzler 1943; Harrington 1952) naturalized "colonial archaeology" as HA's companion.
- **Processual uptake:** New Archaeology applications to historic sites; documents as middle-range evidence (Johnson 2011 summary below); Binford (1977) hoped HA would lead theory — Orser (1996) judged that prophecy unfulfilled.
- **Theory crisis (1980s):** Orser's "wells" metaphor — HA digs physical wells eagerly but neglects theoretical ones; 1987 SHA plenary (Honerkamp, Cleland) diagnosed an atheoretical field / theoretical angst; residual "junior varsity" status.
- **Critical / global turn (1970s–1990s):** archaeology of capitalism (Leone, Annapolis); ethnicity and identity (Deagan, St Augustine); African-American archaeology (Fairbanks onward); resistance and colonial entanglement; Orser's **[[global-historical-archaeology]]** charter (1996) and Plenum series — mutualism, four haunts, "think globally, dig locally"; reattach Europe and look beyond Europe.
- **Contemporary:** contemporary archaeology, conflict archaeology, further decolonizing and Indigenous collaborations — beyond 1996 charter's horizon.

## Theoretical Landscape

Spans [[processual-archaeology|processual]] pattern studies, critical/Marxian archaeology of capitalism, [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] meaning and identity work, and Orser's mutualist global program ([[four-haunts]], [[mutualism-historical-archaeology]]). Links to [[world-systems-analysis]] (via Wolf-type interconnection and capitalism literature), [[postcolonialism]], [[acculturation]] critiques (Quimby–Spoehr foil), subaltern speech under unequal power. Institutional split with British **post-medieval archaeology** is real but, Orser argued, should dissolve when both study four-haunt modern-world formation (Bartlett 1993 on medieval European "incipient" haunts as bridge).

## Methods

Stratigraphic and open-area excavation adapted to historic sites; ceramic type collections; probate and estate-document correlation; landscape and spatial analysis; increasingly scientific materials analysis. Orser stresses multi-scalar "think globally, dig locally."

## Current State

Central rather than marginal in Anglophone theoretical debate (Johnson 2011 assessment). Vault primary depth now anchored by Orser 1996 and Deetz *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* (1996 ed.); Leone monographs and SHA literature still outstanding.

## Reflexivity

Historical archaeology has been accused of serving patriotic reconstruction, tourist heritage, and elite house museums; critical practitioners reoriented it toward slavery, inequality, and colonial violence. Orser's 1492 modern-world start both enables global comparison and risks Eurocentric periodization. Case choices (Palmares, Gorttoose) are politically charged commemorative landscapes.


## Prior Textbook Coverage (Johnson 2011)

*(Attributed to Johnson 2011, Ch.11, via former concept-page summary — now folded here to keep the slug unique.)*

**Definitional range.** Historical archaeology is the archaeology of societies with documentary records. In North America the term narrowed to the post-Columbian, colonial, and industrial past; in Europe it may extend to any text-aided period from the classical world onward. The historic/prehistoric boundary is a disciplinary convention tied to the local documentary horizon.

**Documents as evidence.** Two traditions: (1) Binford-style middle-range use of documents to test general propositions (e.g., port books vs. container finds); (2) postprocessual contextual use for experience, gender, and meaning. Deagan's quantitative ethnicity indices at St Augustine exemplify one operational strand.

**Institutional home.** North American archaeology sits with anthropology; much European archaeology with history — shaping historical archaeology's theoretical home (see [[archaeology]]).

**New Archaeology uptake.** 1960s–70s pattern-seeking on historic sites; Binford (1977) held historical archaeology should provide strong tests of general archaeological ideas.

**Landmark programs (textbook map).** Kathleen Deagan, St Augustine (ethnicity measures); Mark Leone, Annapolis/Chesapeake — archaeology of capitalism and the "Georgian Order" (order/symmetry/segregation across architecture, gardens, pottery, landscape; Glassie–Deetz lineage; ideology of liberty vs. slave society); Barbara Voss on Californio identity; archaeologies of resistance in institutional contexts.

**Prominence.** Johnson's assessment: historical archaeology moved "from the margins ... to centre stage" in the ~20 years before 2011, with theoretically current work on gender/identity, politics, agency, materiality. Prehistory's privilege as "pure" archaeology partly reflects historiographical bias and New Archaeology's long-term process focus.

## Insoll 1999 — Islamic Periods as Historical Archaeology

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]*.)* Positions [[islamic-archaeology]] as text-aided, multi-disciplinary historical archaeology of a living religion, homologous with European medieval archaeology's earlier isolation-from-theory problem. Developmental conquest archaeology, coinage reform sequences, and funerary epigraphy are classic historical-archaeology problems. Historians often ignore material culture while archaeologists ignore texts. Superposition neglect of Islamic levels under "fashionable" earlier deposits is a field-practice pathology of historical layers.

## Deetz 1996 — Small Things Program

*(Attributed — [[deetz-james|Deetz]], *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]*, expanded ed. 1996.)*

**Definitions.** Deetz states a literacy-based definition (cultural remains of societies capable of recording their own histories) and a popular global definition (archaeology of European cultural spread since the fifteenth century and interaction with indigenous peoples). He insists historical archaeology must be global because post-fifteenth-century sailing contact was historically unprecedented; African American, Asian American, and post-contact Native American archaeology are integral, not optional. English counterpart work is **postmedieval archaeology**.

**Ordinary material culture.** Program center is the "small things forgotten" of probate inventories — ceramics, houses, graves, refuse — complementary to documents that miss everyday meals and thought. Methods pedagogy: TPQ/TAQ, South [[mean-ceramic-date]], Harrington/Binford pipestems, [[probate-inventory-analysis]] as folk taxonomy, standing architecture plus excavation.

**[[georgian-worldview]].** Three Anglo-American periods (to ~1660; ~1680–1760 folk/regional; ~1760+ Georgian re-Anglicization). Multi-domain cognitive shift (order, symmetry, individuation) across houses, ceramics, gravestones, refuse. Boundary case: [[parting-ways|Parting Ways]] free Black community does not fit Anglo rules.

**African American archaeology.** [[creolization]] (not simple [[acculturation]]); [[shotgun-house]] path; slave housing continuum; [[colono-ware]] and creole pipes; foodways (Cannon's Point via Otto).
