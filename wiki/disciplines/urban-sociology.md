# Urban Sociology

## Scope

Subfield of [[sociology]] treating cities, [[urbanization]], and urban social organization — neighborhoods, natural areas, institutions (press, settlements), mobility, and urban social problems. Charter American program: *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925) and the [[chicago-school|Chicago School]].

## Historical Development

### Classical-era precursor (Du Bois)

Before the Chicago program, [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]]'s
*[[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|The Philadelphia Negro]]* (1899, fieldwork
1896–97) carried out the first empirical, statistically grounded community study
in American sociology — a door-to-door canvass, social mapping, and descriptive
statistics of the [[philadelphia-seventh-ward|Seventh Ward]] — arguing racialized
urban poverty structurally rather than culturally. Its methods "lay dormant" until
the 1920s Chicago School and it was long denied canonical founder status (→
[[du-bois-sociological-canon-debate]]); it is now widely read as a founding text of
the subfield.

### Fieldwork Revolution (institutional founding moment)

Park, Burgess, McKenzie, and Wirth at Chicago framed the city as laboratory: [[human-ecology]], [[concentric-zones]], [[natural-area|natural areas]], [[social-disorganization-theory|social disorganization]], community organization, and a classified urban bibliography. Related documentary strand: [[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|Thomas & Znaniecki]] on immigrant primary groups. Parallel classical resource: [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] on the metropolis (via Park).

### Later trajectories (partial coverage)

Shaw & McKay delinquency ecology; Wirth 1938 urbanism; mid-century variable sociology; urban political economy; ethnography — still largely not ingested. **Ingested contemporary strands:** (1) [[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]], *[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]* (1987) — [[urban-underclass]], [[social-isolation]], [[concentration-effects]], [[male-marriageable-pool-index]], universalist policy agenda ([[wilson-truly-disadvantaged-study]]; [[hubs/studies/wilson-truly-disadvantaged-study]]); (2) [[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Denton]], *[[massey-denton-american-apartheid-1993|American Apartheid]]* (1993) — [[residential-segregation]], [[hypersegregation]], fair-housing policy failure ([[massey-denton-american-apartheid-study]]); (3) [[sassen-saskia|Sassen]], *[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]* (1991/2001) — [[global-city-theory]], [[producer-services]], [[spatial-polarization]], transnational urban networks ([[sassen-global-city-study]]; [[hubs/studies/sassen-global-city-study]]). Debates: [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]; [[global-city-model-debate]]. **(4)** [[wacquant-loic|Wacquant]], *[[wacquant-body-and-soul-2004|Body & Soul]]* (2004) — carnal ethnography of [[woodlawn-boys-club|Woodlawn Boys Club]] (1988–92); gym as island of order vs street; underclass/disorganization critique; [[carnal-sociology]] ([[wacquant-body-and-soul-study]]; [[hubs/studies/wacquant-body-and-soul-study|Studies Hub]]). **(5)** [[desmond-matthew|Desmond]], *[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]* (2016) — [[relational-ethnography]] of landlords and tenants in Milwaukee; [[eviction]] as poverty-creating process; MARS forced-mobility measurement ([[desmond-evicted-study]]; [[hubs/studies/desmond-evicted-study|Studies Hub]]). **(6)** [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]], *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (2012) — PHDCN / Chicago Project multi-method program; [[collective-efficacy]], [[ecometrics]], durable [[concentrated-disadvantage]], structural sorting, and multi-outcome [[neighborhood-effects]] ([[sampson-great-american-city-study]]; [[hubs/studies/sampson-great-american-city-study|Studies Hub]]; debate [[neighborhood-effects-debate]]). Wilson wrote the foreword, positioning GAC as a landmark empirical neighborhood-effects study.

## Theoretical Landscape

[[human-ecology]]; [[social-disorganization-theory]]; Chicago school case tradition; Wilson structural underclass / neighborhood isolation program; segregation/underclass spatial structure (Massey & Denton); [[global-city-theory]] (Sassen — cities as production sites for global control infrastructure); housing-market / eviction-as-mechanism program (Desmond); Sampson/PHDCN **collective-efficacy** and **theory of context** (process mediation, spatial interdependence, selection as structural sorting — “neighborhoods choose people”); [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]; [[global-city-model-debate]]; [[neighborhood-effects-debate]]; [[broken-windows-debate]].

## Methods

[[ecological-method]]; community monographs; maps/rates; [[segregation-indices]]; [[housing-audit-study]]; multi-level neighborhood-effects models; **[[ecometrics]]** and **[[systematic-social-observation]]** (Sampson–[[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]] PHDCN program); community survey + key-informant networks + longitudinal cohorts; comparative urban economic analysis (FDI, finance, producer services, earnings); [[relational-ethnography]] + survey/court mixed designs (Desmond).

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Geography and planning; criminology; social work/settlements; anthropology of the city; social psychology of urban contacts; demography (segregation measurement); civil-rights law / housing policy; economic sociology and international political economy (global cities); developmental psychology and public health (PHDCN child-cohort design).

## Current State

Active worldwide (inequality, segregation, global cities, neighborhood effects, network/informational urbanism, housing/eviction, organizational brokerage of urban social capital, low-income family formation). Collection now holds the 1925 Chicago charter, Wilson 1987 underclass charter, the 1993 Massey–Denton segregation synthesis, Sassen 1991/2001 global-city charter, Castells *Rise of the Network Society* (space of flows / informational city), Anderson *Code of the Street* (1999), Bourgois *In Search of Respect* (1995/2003 — East Harlem crack economy / [[street-culture]]), Whyte *Street Corner Society*, Desmond *Evicted* (2016), Small *Unanticipated Gains* (2009) on organizational embeddedness of networks / neighborhood buffering via childcare centers, Sampson *Great American City* (2012) on PHDCN ecometrics / collective efficacy / structural sorting, **and** Edin & Kefalas *[[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Promises I Can Keep]]* (2005) on multi-neighborhood ethnography of unmarried motherhood and the high marriage bar in Philly/Camden ([[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]); still thin on Wirth 1938, Shaw & McKay primary monographs, Wilson’s later monographs, *Streetwise* (1990), *The Informational City* (1989), Eviction Lab follow-ons, and world-city network measurement literature.

## Reflexivity

Progressive-era reform adjacency; race and immigration frames; ecological naturalism; knowledge of "disorganized" areas produced for academic and control audiences. Massey & Denton reverse ecological naturalization: the black [[ghetto]] is manufactured and maintained, not a natural area outcome. Wilson’s underclass vocabulary and “social isolation” frame risk pathologizing readings if detached from his structural labor-market argument — a reception problem he addresses in the 2012 Afterword. Sassen’s global-city knowledge is produced largely from Northern financial centers and official statistics; dual reception (mayoral status claims and justice critiques). Castells’s Vol. I urbanism is G-7/advanced-economy weighted with selective mega-city cases; morphology can crowd out street-level ethnography. Sampson’s Chicago “truth spot” strategy is theoretically driven rather than sample-representative (Wilson’s foreword agrees); critiques of single-city generalizability are answered in GAC with national tract comparisons (disadvantage inertia r>0.9 matching Chicago) but remain live in [[neighborhood-effects-debate]].
