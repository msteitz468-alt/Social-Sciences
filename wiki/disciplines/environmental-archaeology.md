# Environmental Archaeology

The study of past human–environment relationships through the physical, biological, and geological traces of the environments people lived in. It sits at the intersection of [[archaeology]], the Quaternary earth sciences, and palaeoecology, and supplies the wiki's **environmental evidence class** — the independent chronologies (ice cores, tree rings, varves) and biotic proxies (pollen, insects, molluscs, diatoms) against which social and archaeological narratives are tested.

## Scope

Environmental archaeology claims as its object the reconstructed past environment: climate, vegetation, fauna, soils, hydrology, and their transformation by and of human societies. It is distinguished from climatology or geology by its focus on the human timescale and the human role. As synthesized by [[roberts-neil|Neil Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]), the field's organizing problem across the [[holocene|Holocene]] is that the "natural" and "cultural" components of environmental change become progressively harder to separate, so that later-Holocene questions — is a given episode of erosion, eutrophication, or [[desertification]] natural or cultural? — are its characteristic form.

## Historical Development

Roots lie in early-20th-century Scandinavian [[pollen-analysis|pollen analysis]] (Lennart von Post, later Iversen and Faegri) and the [[blytt-sernander|Blytt–Sernander]] climatic periodization of the European postglacial. The **fieldwork-revolution** and **postwar-expansion** eras saw Grahame Clark's economic prehistory (Star Carr) and Eric Higgs' palaeoeconomy school integrate biological evidence into archaeology; the **radiocarbon revolution** (from 1949) gave the field its independent absolute chronology. The **critical turn** and **contemporary** eras added high-resolution ice-core, speleothem, and lake-sediment records and computational palaeoecology.

## Theoretical Landscape

The field has repeatedly negotiated [[environmental-determinism|environmental determinism]] — the temptation to read social change directly off climatic change — against possibilist and social-agency positions. Processual archaeology's systems thinking made environment central; post-processual critique insisted environment is culturally mediated. Roberts models a cautious middle: environment as constraint and contributor rather than sole cause.

## Methods

See [[pollen-analysis]], [[radiocarbon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]], [[varve-analysis]], [[ice-core-analysis]], [[diatom-analysis]], [[coleopteran-analysis]], [[mollusc-analysis]], [[isotope-analysis]], [[zooarchaeology]], and [[paleoethnobotany]]. Multi-proxy reconstruction and independent cross-dating are the field's methodological signature.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Overlaps Quaternary science, physical geography, palaeoclimatology, and [[biological-anthropology]]; Roberts frames the synthesis as "above all a work of geography." Boundary disputes concern whether environmental archaeology is a service discipline (supplying dates and proxies) or an interpretive one in its own right.

## Current State

Active fronts: abrupt-climate-change events (e.g. the 8.2 ka and 4.2 ka events), the [[anthropocene]] debate over when human impact became geologically dominant, and integration with [[ancient-dna|ancient DNA]] and isotope data on diet and mobility.

## Reflexivity

The evidence base is spatially uneven — Northern Hemisphere, and especially northwest European, records dominate because that is where the methods were developed and the lakes and bogs that preserve pollen are abundant; tropical and Southern Hemisphere Holocene records are thinner. Proxy records carry taphonomic and calibration biases. The field's older "climate-drives-culture" narratives are a standing reflexive concern (see [[environmental-determinism]]).
