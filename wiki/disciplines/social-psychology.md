# Social Psychology

## Scope

**Social psychology** studies the mutual constitution of individual and society — how mind, self, and conduct arise within social interaction, and how social order is enacted through interacting individuals. It straddles [[disciplines/sociology|sociology]] and psychology; this wiki's coverage is of the **sociological** wing, whose classical foundations are [[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]]'s *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]* (1902) and [[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]]'s lecture program (posthumously *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]*). Mead reframed the field's standpoint: against a social psychology that "dealt with various phases of social experience from the psychological standpoint of individual experience," he proposed "dealing with experience from the standpoint of society... from the standpoint of communication as essential to the social order" (attributed).

The subfield's object is thus neither the isolated psyche nor the macro-structure, but the **[[the-social-self|social self]]** and the interactional process — for Cooley the [[looking-glass-self|looking-glass self]] and personal ideas; for Mead [[gesture|gesture]], [[significant-symbol|significant symbol]], [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|role-taking]], the [[generalized-other|generalized other]], and the [[the-i-and-the-me|"I"/"me"]] dialectic; for Thomas & Znaniecki (*[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|Polish Peasant]]* Vol I) [[attitude-and-value|attitudes and social values]], the [[definition-of-the-situation]], and [[personal-documents-method|personal documents]] as data for social psychology defined as the science of attitudes.

## Historical Development

- **Classical era.** The sociological social psychology of [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] and his contemporary [[cooley-charles-horton|Charles Horton Cooley]] (*[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]*, 1902) forms in the American classical era from c. 1900 (Cooley at Michigan; Mead's Chicago lecture course from 1900); Mead's lecture course "Social Psychology" (from 1900) is its seedbed. Its roots are [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophy and German psychology (Wundt's [[gesture|gesture]] concept, refracted through Mead's critique of parallelism).
- **Fieldwork Revolution / classical documentary arm.** [[thomas-william-isaac|Thomas]] and [[znaniecki-florian|Znaniecki]] (*[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|Polish Peasant]]*, 1918–20; Vol I ingested) supply the empirical documentary arm: attitude–value schema, definition of the situation, four wishes, family letter series. The Chicago tradition of urban ethnography (Park, Burgess) continues that line; Ellsworth Faris institutionalized "Introduction to Social Psychology" in the sociology department from 1920.
- **Critical Turn.** Mead's student **Herbert Blumer** coins **[[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]** (1937) and consolidates it as the field's dominant sociological program; Goffman's dramaturgy and labeling theory branch from it. [[hochschild-arlie-russell|Hochschild]] (*[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|The Managed Heart]]*, 1983) extends the interactionist concern with display into a sociology of **[[emotional-labor|emotional labor]]**, [[feeling-rules]], and the commercialization of feeling — microsociology of emotion under service capitalism (Studies Hub [[hubs/studies/hochschild-managed-heart-study]]). *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|The Second Shift]]* (1989) carries the same feeling-rules / emotion-work toolkit into unpaid domestic regimes: [[second-shift]], [[gender-strategy]], [[family-myth]], [[economy-of-gratitude]], [[stalled-revolution]] (Studies Hub [[hubs/studies/hochschild-second-shift-study]]).
- **Postwar small-group program.** [[homans-george-c|George Homans]] (*[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*, 1950) builds an inductive [[small-group-theory|theory of the small group]] from industrial and field cases (activity–interaction–sentiment; external/internal systems) — sociological social psychology of face-to-face groups, concurrent with Bales’s lab IPA and distinct from later Homans exchange theory (1961).
- **Postwar theoretical bridge.** [[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (*[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|Social Construction of Reality]]*, 1966) explicitly build a bridge from sociology of knowledge to social psychology: Part Three treats [[primary-socialization]], [[secondary-socialization]], identity, and [[plausibility-structure|plausibility structures]] as the subjective side of the societal dialectic, drawing heavily on Mead while embedding socialization in [[institutionalization]] and [[legitimation]].

## Theoretical Landscape

The sociological wing is organized chiefly around [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]] and its Meadian foundations in [[social-behaviorism|social behaviorism]]; it stands opposed to both **Watsonian behaviorism** (which denied mind) and the **individualistic** social psychology that derives society from pre-social selves. A standing internal fault line — the sociological vs. the psychological (experimental) traditions of "social psychology" — divides the field institutionally. Berger & Luckmann’s [[social-construction-of-reality]] program is a major postwar *theoretical* articulation of mutual constitution (individual and society) that is not identical to Blumerian interactionism but shares Meadian internalization premises.

## Methods

Close observation of everyday interaction (participant observation, life history), against the variable-analytic survey method; in Mead's own program, the analysis of the social act "from the outside to the inside." The psychological wing (experimental) is a coverage gap here.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Social psychology is the contested borderland between sociology and psychology; the same name denotes two partly separate enterprises. Its Meadian wing also borders [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] philosophy (the theory of the act) and, through communication theory, links to later work (Habermas's reading of Mead as the origin of communicative action).

## Current State

The sociological wing remains active through symbolic interactionism and the sociology of everyday life, emotion, and identity. This wiki's coverage holds Cooley, Mead, Thomas–Znaniecki Vol I, Blumer (*Symbolic Interactionism*), Goffman's *[[goffman-presentation-of-self-1956|Presentation of Self]]*, *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]*, and *[[goffman-stigma-1963|Stigma]]*, Homans *[[homans-human-group-1950|The Human Group]]*, and Hochschild *[[hochschild-managed-heart-1983|Managed Heart]]* + *[[hochschild-second-shift-1989|Second Shift]]*. Still lacks Homans *Social Behavior* (exchange); full labeling-theory monographs; the psychological/experimental small-group tradition (Bales, Sherif, etc.).

## Reflexivity

The subfield's founding text is an editorial reconstruction (see [[mind-self-society-authorship-debate]]): the "Mead" who founded sociological social psychology reaches the discipline through a book he did not write, systematized by his editor Morris and canonized by his student Blumer. The gap between the archival Mead and the textbook Mead is a live historiographical concern.
