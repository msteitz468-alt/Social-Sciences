# Sociology of Culture

The subfield of [[sociology]] concerned with culture — systems of meaning, symbols, values, tastes, and practices — both as an object of study and as an explanation of social action. This overview page is scaffolded from [[swidler-talk-of-love-2001|Swidler's *Talk of Love*]] (2001), a programmatic intervention in the field; much adjacent material lives on the [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], [[practice-theory]], and taste/[[cultural-capital]] pages already in the wiki. Also called **cultural sociology**, though some authors distinguish the two (see Scope).

## Scope

The subfield claims culture as its object under a definition that has itself shifted. [[swidler-ann|Swidler]] (attributed) notes that since [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s 1973 work the field moved away from two older definitions — culture as "the entire way of life of a people" (including material artifacts) and Goodenough's culture as "everything one needs to know to function as a member of a society" — toward defining culture as **publicly available symbolic forms** through which meaning is experienced and expressed (Geertz: "an historically transmitted pattern of meanings embodied in symbols"). The field's distinctive contemporary problem, on Swidler's account, is causal: since "cultures are complex and contradictory," describing what distinguishes one culture from another must be supplemented by an account of intra-cultural variation in *use*.

## Historical Development

- **Classical roots (classical era).** [[weber-max|Weber]]'s interpretive sociology of religious ethics and [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]'s sociology of collective representations are the twin sources; both treated meaning as sociologically consequential.
- **Postwar (postwar expansion).** [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]] absorbed Weber's ideas into a theory of internalized **values** as the cultural component of the social system — the settlement against which later cultural sociology defined itself.
- **The interpretive turn (critical turn).** [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s semiotic, meaning-centered concept of culture and thick description reoriented the field in the 1970s toward interpretation. [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s [[practice-theory]] linked culture to class reproduction through [[habitus]] and [[cultural-capital]].
- **Contemporary.** A pragmatist, action-centered strand — Swidler's [[culture-in-action|toolkit theory]], DiMaggio on culture and cognition, Lamont on symbolic boundaries — treats culture as repertoire and asks how it is put to use; alongside "production of culture" and cultural-consumption programs.

## Theoretical Landscape

Major frameworks in the wiki: [[practice-theory]] (Bourdieu — culture as embodied disposition and capital), [[culture-in-action]] (Swidler — culture as toolkit and strategies of action), and the older [[structural-functionalism|Parsonsian values]] model now largely superseded. Live tensions: culture as coherent system vs. as fragmentary repertoire; meaning vs. practice; culture as constraint vs. culture as resource.

## Methods

Ethnographic and in-depth [[participant-observation|interview]] methods (Swidler's love-talk study), content/discourse analysis, survey measurement of values and taste (Bourdieu's *Distinction*), and comparative-historical analysis. A standing methodological concern is that self-report about culture (interview talk) is not behavior — Swidler treats expressed ideas as "possibilities in a repertoire," not as typical conduct.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

The subfield borders cultural anthropology (shared debt to Geertz; shared object, different scale and method), the sociology of knowledge, social psychology and cognition, and the sociology of religion. The boundary with anthropology is porous at the level of theory (Geertz, Bourdieu cross both) but sociology tends toward larger samples, survey scale, and explicit links to stratification.

## Current State

Active research fronts include culture and cognition, cultural repertoires and symbolic boundaries, the measurement of meaning, and the culture–inequality link. The field's self-described central open problem (Swidler) is the "anchoring" question: whether and how some cultural elements organize or control others.

## Reflexivity

The subfield's definition of its own object is contested and historically moving (the "culture" of 1950 ≠ 1973 ≠ now) — a live instance of the wiki's construct-dating concern. Its empirical base has historically over-sampled Western, and often middle-class, populations (Swidler's own prototypical-not-typical suburban sample is a case in point), so generalizations about "American culture" or "modern selfhood" carry sampling-frame limits that are content, not caveat.
