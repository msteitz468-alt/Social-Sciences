# Archaeology

The discipline that reconstructs past human life from material remains. In this vault's four-field framing it sits beside and within [[anthropology]]. Related: [[archaeogenetics]], [[biological-anthropology]].

## Scope

Deep-time and historical material culture; sites, landscapes, objects, and (increasingly) biomolecules. Distinct from textual history where texts exist, but often joined to it in historical archaeology.

**On the nature of theory itself** (attributed, [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson 2011]]): Johnson argues **all archaeological practice is theory-laden** — even ostensibly atheoretical, purely descriptive, "common sense" writing rests on implicit theoretical assumptions ("common sense is not enough"). He offers four justifications for engaging theory explicitly: archaeologists must justify what they do; must be able to evaluate one interpretation against another; should be explicit about their assumptions; and should recognize that everyone uses theory whether or not they acknowledge it. Johnson's own preferred working definition of "theory" — **"the order you put facts in"** — is chosen for accessibility and for allowing diverse theoretical viewpoints to coexist; see [[archaeological-pragmatism]] for how this sits alongside narrower (positivist/middle-range) and broader (postprocessual/critical-theory) definitions.

## Historical Development

- **Precursors / founding:** [[antiquarianism]] (text-aided and text-poor); [[classical-archaeology]] as early historical archaeology; Scandinavian [[three-age-system]] ([[thomsen-christian-j|Thomsen]], [[worsaae-jens-j-a|Worsaae]]).
- **19th c. evolutionary program:** [[nineteenth-century-evolutionary-archaeology]] ([[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] *Pre-Historic Times* synthesis; colonial applications) — **not** later Dunnell [[evolutionary-archaeology]].
- **Fieldwork revolution / interwar:** [[culture-historical-archaeology]] — Childe *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]* (1929) as English charter for [[archaeological-culture]]; *[[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958|The Dawn of European Civilization]]* (1925; 6th ed. 1957/58) as continent-scale narrative under revised *[[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]*.
- **Postwar American formalization:** [[willey-gordon-r|Willey]] & [[phillips-philip|Phillips]] *[[willey-phillips-method-theory-1958|Method and Theory in American Archaeology]]* (1958) — archaeology as anthropology; [[culture-historical-integration]] / [[processual-interpretation]] levels; component–phase–horizon–tradition units; New World stages ([[lithic-stage]]…[[postclassic-stage]]).
- **Postwar:** radiocarbon ([[radiocarbon-dating]]); [[taylor-walter-w|Taylor]] *[[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948|A Study of Archeology]]* (1948) — conjunctive approach (proto-processual critique); **[[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology / processualism]]** — *[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives in Archeology]]* (Binford & Binford eds., 1968) as American public charter; British systems charter [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]] *[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* (1968/1978) ✅ (polythetic hierarchy, [[technocomplex]], archaeology-as-autonomous-discipline); [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]] / [[ethnoarchaeology]] synthesis in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (Binford 1983); post-processual reactions — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Hodder & Hutson *Reading the Past*]] (1986; 3rd ed. 2003) ✅, the textbook charter of [[contextual-archaeology|contextual]]/interpretive method; calibration collapses short Orientalist chronology ([[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew *Before Civilization* 1973]] ✅); founding method text [[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Libby *Radiocarbon Dating*]] (1952/55) ✅.
- **Postwar British / economic turn:** [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* (1939; rev. 1957) reframes archaeology's aim as the ecological and economic reconstruction of prehistoric life ([[economic-prehistory]]) — "the modern prehistorian approaches his subject in the spirit of an ecologist" — a bridge from culture-history toward processualism. The same program is scaled to a single-frame **[[world-prehistory]]** in *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961) — first modern-style global prehistoric textbook, with full chapters on the New World and Australasia and cautious early-[[radiocarbon-dating]] alignment of regional sequences.
- **Critical Turn / Contemporary — interpretive reaction:** [[post-processual-archaeology]] — *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Shanks & Tilley, Social Theory and Archaeology]]* (1987) as critical/metatheoretical charter (theory-as-practice; [[logic-of-contingency]]; material culture as signifying practice; archaeology as politics); [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999) as field-method charter (interpretation at the trowel's edge; reflexive excavation at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]]); debate [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].
- **Critical Turn — feminist / gender archaeology:** *[[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991|Engendering Archaeology]]* (Gero & Conkey, eds., 1991) multi-author charter for [[feminist-archaeology]] (Conkey & Spector 1984 agenda; Wylie epistemology; genderlithics, households-with-faces, food/gender cases); Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/feminist-archaeology]].
- **Contemporary — human–thing entanglement:** [[entanglement-theory]] — Hodder *[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* (2012); Theory Hub [[hubs/theory/entanglement-theory]]; concepts [[entanglement]], [[dependence-dependency]], [[fittingness]].
- **Contemporary:** aDNA revolution ([[archaeogenetics]]); Bayesian chronologies; landscape and environmental archaeology; heritage multivocality and digital archaeology.
- **Contemporary — world religions / Islamic archaeology:** [[islamic-archaeology]] enters the vault via Insoll *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]* (1999) as a social-archaeology program for a living world religion — mosque, domestic, diet, burial, settlement, art/trade — against art-historical isolation and top-heavy elite bias; method frame [[archaeology-of-religion]].
- **Contemporary — African complex-society synthesis:** Connah *[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* (3rd ed. 2016) is the vault’s continent-scale archaeological review of **urbanism and state formation** across ten African regions, stressing local productive-land control, trade as intensifier, and visibility/research bias (stone ruins vs organic architecture; savanna vs rainforest surveyability). Fills a major subject-matter gap (Africa south of Egypt had been thin relative to Near East/Mesoamerica).

## Methods

Stratigraphy and typology (as method rules in Childe's Preface); [[radiocarbon-dating]] (founding monograph [[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Libby 1952/55]]; [[libby-willard-f|Libby]], [[johnson-frederick|Johnson]] ch. VII); [[ancient-dna]] / [[admixture-statistics]]; survey and excavation standards. Field-method now anchored by [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s *[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from the Earth]]* (1954): [[stratigraphic-excavation]], the [[box-grid-excavation|box-grid system]], [[seriation]] and [[typological-method]] as relative-dating tools, resting on the [[law-of-superposition]]. **Critical history of field method:** [[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] *[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches to Fieldwork]]* (2002) reads the shift from grid-and-section to [[open-area-excavation|open-area]] / [[single-context-recording|single-context recording]] and the [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] as changing conceptions of the [[archaeological-record]], not neutral progress (see [[stratigraphic-recording-debate]]). **Reflexive method layer:** [[hodder-ian|Hodder]] *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999) — [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]], recursive specialist–excavator interaction, diaries/video, multivocal digital presentation ([[reflexive-fieldwork]]). **Integrative method layer (Americanist):** [[archaeological-phase]], [[archaeological-component]], [[archaeological-horizon]], [[archaeological-tradition]] as formalized in Willey & Phillips 1958.

**Textbook methods scaffold (Renfrew & Bahn 2012):** *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice]]* (6th ed.) supplies the vault's broadest single-source survey of method families — [[archaeological-survey]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]], [[geophysical-survey]], [[underwater-archaeology]], [[absolute-dating]] / [[relative-dating]] suite (including [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[potassium-argon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]]), [[experimental-archaeology]], [[use-wear-analysis]], [[residue-analysis]], [[paleoethnobotany]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[bioarchaeology]], [[public-archaeology]], and [[cultural-resource-management]] — plus [[social-archaeology]] and [[cognitive-archaeology]] interpretive frames (attributed textbook synthesis; not a substitute for primary method monographs already ingested).

**Institutional split** (attributed, Johnson 2011): [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson]]
notes archaeology's disciplinary home differs by national tradition — housed within
anthropology departments in North America (the "four-field" model), but institutionally
allied with history in much of Europe. He also notes **[[historical-archaeology]]** —
the archaeology of the post-medieval/colonial/capitalist world, documentary and material
evidence combined — has moved toward the centre of Anglophone theoretical debate rather
than remaining a marginal specialism.

## Current State

Active fronts: aDNA–archaeology integration; radiocarbon precision; decolonizing heritage; climate–culture models. Collection has only begun.

## Reflexivity

Archaeology's culture maps underwrote nationalist prehistories; museum collecting and colonial excavation shape the archive. Culture = people is a methodological assumption with political history — always attributed and cautioned. [[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] (2002) adds that the discipline's *fieldwork* history is itself not neutral progress but a series of theory-laden shifts, and that routine practice (drawing a context, splitting a site report, naming a culture) silently encodes conceptions of the [[archaeological-record]]; area/period specialisms are contingent products of culture-history, not natural divisions.
[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson (2011)]] adds (attributed) that archaeological
interpretation does not occur in a political vacuum: site-selection decisions, funding
choices, and interpretive framing (e.g., of Roman "Romanization") reflect present-day
social and political context, often without conscious bias — a position he extends into
current debates over multiculturalism, diversity, and inclusion legislation shaping
whose pasts get excavated, funded, and narrated, and into the archaeology-and-politics
material recorded on [[indigenous-archaeology]], [[postcolonialism]], and
[[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]].
[[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan (1987/2004)]] documents two American cases where
racial ideology and disciplinary authority, not evidence, set the terms of
prehistory: the nineteenth-century [[moundbuilder-myth]] (the belief that Native
ancestors could not have built the mounds — empirically refuted by Cyrus Thomas
under Powell, 1882–89) and the early-twentieth-century [[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]]–Holmes
"no early man" orthodoxy, broken only by the 1927 [[folsom-site|Folsom]] find
(attributed).

## The theoretical landscape after post-processualism (Preucel & Mrozowski 2010)

*(Attributed — Preucel & Mrozowski, "The New Pragmatism," editors' introduction to [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)*
The editors survey Anglo-American archaeological theory since the 1960s and argue it is consolidating
around a **"[[archaeological-pragmatism|new pragmatism]]"** — a problem-driven, politically-engaged
practice holding that "archaeology is irreducibly both [an objective science and a social practice]
at the same time." Their genealogy: the [[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]] (Binford; Clarke)
and its internal disputes (Fritz & Plog on hypothetico-deductive explanation; Schiffer's site-formation
"transforms" vs. Binford's "Pompeii premise"; behavioral archaeology; the analogy debate); the
[[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] turn (Hodder; Shanks & Tilley) toward meaning, agency,
and history; parallel Marxist, [[feminist-archaeology|feminist]] (Conkey & Spector 1984), evolutionary
(Dunnell's selectionism), cognitive-processual (Renfrew), [[postcolonialism|postcolonial]], and
[[indigenous-archaeology|indigenous]] currents.

A second thread is **disciplinary anxiety** over the "archaeology is anthropology" thesis and the
Boasian **four-field** model (Segal & Yanagisako's *Unwrapping the Sacred Bundle*; Hodder's "why are
there only four fields?"; the 1998 Stanford department split along positivist/postpositivist lines).
The editors also foreground **[[materiality]]** (three approaches — material culture / material
language / material network) and the "**Three R's**" — repatriation, reconciliation, restitution
(the Burra Charter, Nunavut, NAGPRA, South Africa's Truth and Reconciliation Commission, the
Euphronios krater) — as evidence that the past is now "an increasingly contested political terrain."
They single out **[[historical-archaeology]]** as "a healthy model for contemporary archaeology." See
[[practice-theory]], [[heritage]], [[social-memory]]. Primary modern-world charter now ingested: [[orser-charles-e|Orser]] *[[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996|A Historical Archaeology of the Modern World]]* (1996) — [[global-historical-archaeology]], [[four-haunts]], [[mutualism-historical-archaeology]].

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]]; mid-2000s textbook synthesis.)*

Wenke & Olszewski frame contemporary [[archaeology]] as oriented to the **scientific method**—proposing, testing, comparing, and refining explanations of cultural variability—rather than relic-hunting as in Belzoni/Schliemann-era practice. They locate modern foundations in the Enlightenment combination of method, determinism, materialism, and evolutionism, and treat the discipline as in an **identity crisis** over whether the past can be “explained” versus only interpreted.

As of their 2007 synthesis they map three poles: (1) [[processual-archaeology|processual]]/New Archaeology (post-~1960; [[binford-lewis|Binford]]; mathematics, [[cultural-ecology]], systems, evolutionary science); (2) [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processualism]] (Shanks & Tilley: past as multivalent “text”; Hodder’s culture/agency/meaning critique; Trigger’s four tenets of non-objectivity, resistance of belief to data, holism, science-as-social); (3) their own **holistic / “cognitive processual”** stance (Trigger; Renfrew & Bintliff), combining scientific rigor with non-scientific interpretation of ideology—contested by both extremes. They insist archaeology is **not a predictive science**, that there is no single powerful comprehensive explanation of the past, and that industrial destruction is erasing the record faster than theory/method can compensate.
