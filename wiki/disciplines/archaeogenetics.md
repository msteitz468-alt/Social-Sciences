# Archaeogenetics

## Scope

Archaeogenetics (also *paleogenomics* or *genetic prehistory*) studies the human
past through genetic variation — increasingly through **ancient DNA** recovered
from skeletal remains, analyzed at whole-genome scale. Its object is population
history: the splits, migrations, expansions, and above all the *mixtures* that
connect ancient and living peoples. It sits at the intersection of
[[biological-anthropology]], archaeology, historical linguistics, and population
genetics.

## Historical Development

- **Precursor phase (blood groups → single loci, 1960s–2000s).**
  [[cavalli-sforza-luigi-luca|Cavalli-Sforza]] founded the field on the bet that
  past migrations could be read from *present-day* variation, first via
  blood-group proteins, later mitochondrial DNA and Y-chromosome markers. Reich
  (2018) treats this as the field's "dark ages": suggestive but underpowered.
- **The ancient-DNA revolution (2009–).** The ability to extract genome-wide DNA
  from ancient bone — enabled by next-generation sequencing and by
  [[paabo-svante|Pääbo]]'s laboratory's capture methods — transformed the field.
  The first five ancient human genomes appeared in 2010; whole-genome aDNA "went
  into hyperdrive" in 2015 (Reich 2018).

## Theoretical Landscape

The field's unifying empirical claim (attributed to Reich and colleagues) is the
**recurrence of population mixture**: present populations are blends of past
populations that were themselves blends. This has reopened **migrationism** in
archaeology after decades in which processual archaeology minimized migration as
an explanation ("pots are not people"). See [[demic-diffusion]],
[[ghost-population]].

## Methods

[[ancient-dna]] (recovery and authentication), [[admixture-statistics]]
(*f*-/*D*-statistics, admixture graphs), [[principal-component-analysis]], and
model-based clustering. Chronology depends on associated radiocarbon dates —
carry calibration status and lab uncertainty.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Archaeogenetics has an uneasy, sometimes triumphalist relation to archaeology:
Reich (2018) claims genetics has "surpassed" archaeology's traditional toolkit
for reconstructing *what happened*, while conceding archaeologists remain better
placed to explain *why*. With historical linguistics it shares the fraught
project of correlating language spread with population movement (e.g.
Indo-European). The **four-way non-identity** (genes ≠ language ≠ material
culture ≠ self-conscious people) is the standing methodological caution.

## Current State

As of Reich (2018): explosive data growth (his laboratory alone had >3,000
ancient samples by August 2017), with data production outpacing publication.
Active fronts include the peopling of Africa, Asia
([[austronesian-expansion]], [[bantu-expansion]]), and the Americas
([[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]), and the genomics of social inequality
([[sex-biased-admixture]]).

## Reflexivity

The field is young, fast-moving, and prone to press-release inflation of
single-study claims; supersession is expected and every claim must be
date-stamped. It is also politically charged: aDNA speaks directly to origins,
migration, and difference, making it vulnerable to nationalist and racial
misuse — the subject of [[race-and-genomics-debate]]. Sampling is uneven (better
DNA preservation in cold climates biases coverage toward Eurasia; hot regions
like the Near East and sub-Saharan Africa are underrepresented).
