## Scope

Urban archaeology studies the material remains of cities, addressing two practical
challenges the chapter's authors identify as distinctive to the subfield: the vast
scale of living, continuously occupied urban sites, and the heavily disturbed
stratigraphy produced by centuries of rebuilding, utility installation, and
redevelopment. A large proportion of urban archaeological work is generated as
commercially commissioned rescue excavation and circulates as unpublished "grey
literature" rather than conventional academic publication (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 6,
O'Keeffe & Yamin).

## Historical Development

O'Keeffe and Yamin frame the historical interpretation of cities through a contrast
between "city-as-theatre" — cities designed and read as spaces of deliberate visual and
civic display, exemplified by Renaissance planning, Pope Sixtus V's redesign of Rome,
the construction of star-fort fortifications, and Craig Koslofsky's work on the
introduction of street lighting as a technology of urban order and visibility — and
"city-as-non-theatre," the hidden, informal, non-monumental spaces of urban life that
official design did not anticipate or intend to display (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 6).

## Theoretical Landscape

The Archaeology in Annapolis project, associated with [[leone-mark-p]] and
[[historical-archaeology]]'s critical tradition, is identified as the best-known
critical urban archaeology programme, using [[marxist-archaeology|Marxist]] and
ideology-critical approaches to interpret Annapolis's colonial urban landscape.

## Methods

Methods include urban rescue/salvage excavation under commercial and heritage-management
constraints, synthesis of unpublished grey literature, and [[documentary-archaeology]]
combining property, court, and municipal records with excavated evidence.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Urban archaeology overlaps with [[historical-archaeology]], [[heritage-management]] (as
much of its fieldwork is rescue-driven), and urban history.

## Current State

The Five Points/Courthouse Block excavation in New York (see [[five-points-new-york]])
and the African Burial Ground in New York City are cited as major American urban
excavations recovering the material lives of marginalized urban populations —
immigrant, working-class, and enslaved and free Black New Yorkers, respectively. The
Philadelphia President's House excavation, and specifically the discovery of an
icehouse associated with the site's history of enslaved labor, is discussed as an
episode where urban excavation intersected directly with contested public memory of
slavery at a national historic site. Alan Mayne's concept of the "imagined slum" is
cited as a critique of how urban reformers' and later historians' textual
representations of poor urban districts diverged from, and often distorted, the
material and social reality those districts' archaeology recovers (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 6).

## Reflexivity

O'Keeffe and Yamin's chapter includes its own reflexive critique of the subfield's
literature, noting a predominantly Western focus and the near-absence of Islamic
urbanism from the comparative record the chapter surveys — a gap the authors flag
explicitly rather than one this wiki asserts independently. Mayne's "imagined slum"
critique similarly cautions that documentary representations of urban poverty carry
their own ideological freight, which urban archaeology's material evidence can
complicate but not simply correct.

## Collection Coverage Note

Coverage of urban archaeology in this collection is weak, resting on a single handbook
chapter (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 6:
