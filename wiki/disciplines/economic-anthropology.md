# Economic Anthropology

## Scope

Economic anthropology studies how human societies — especially non-market, tribal, and peasant societies — organize the **provisioning of material life**: production, distribution, exchange, and consumption. Its distinctive claim, in the substantivist tradition, is that "the economic" is not a self-evident, universal domain of scarcity-driven rational choice but a **category whose form varies with the society** — in [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s phrase (*[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]*, 1972), "a category of culture rather than behavior, in a class with politics and religion rather than rationality or prudence" *(attributed)*. The field's central methodological quarrel — whether formal economic theory travels across cultures — is the [[formalist-substantivist-debate|formalist–substantivist debate]].

## Historical Development

- **Classical-era roots.** The field's charters are ethnographic: [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts of the Western Pacific]]* (1922), which demolished "Primitive Economic Man" and described the [[kula]] ring, and [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]]'s *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (1925), which theorized [[gift-exchange]] and [[total-prestation]] as total social phenomena.
- **Fieldwork-revolution founding monograph.** [[firth-raymond|Firth]]'s *[[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study|Primitive Polynesian Economy]]* (1939, primary-ingested) is the first full-scale ethnographic economy framed directly against formal economic theory (Robbins, Keynes, Hicks): no money, prices, or wage labour on [[tikopia]], yet scarcity, choice, and capital in modified form. It adopted Hicks's "substantial vs. formal" categories (the debate's future vocabulary), coined [[spheres-of-exchange]], refined [[reciprocity]] quantitatively, forecast "economic anthropology" as a named specialism, and proposed a six-index typology of primitive economies *(all attributed)*.
- **Postwar expansion — substantivism.** [[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]] and the "Columbia group" (George Dalton, Conrad Arensberg) built [[substantivism]]: the economy as an **instituted process** [[embeddedness|embedded]] in social relations, integrated by [[forms-of-integration|reciprocity, redistribution, householding, and exchange]]. See [[substantivism]].
- **Critical turn — the debate and its dissolution.** The [[formalist-substantivist-debate|formalist–substantivist debate]] ran through the 1960s and was largely abandoned rather than settled by the 1970s, as Marxist (Meillassoux, Godelier) and later culturalist approaches reframed the questions. Sahlins' *Stone Age Economics* (1972) is a late, influential substantivist statement, extending the program to hunter-gatherer affluence ([[original-affluent-society]]), the [[domestic-mode-of-production|domestic mode of production]], and the [[reciprocity]] typology.

## Theoretical Landscape

Three broad positions have organized the field: **[[substantivism]]** (the economy is instituted and embedded; formal economics is culture-bound); **formalism** (economizing under scarcity is universal; marginalist tools apply everywhere — Herskovits, Firth, Cook); and **Marxist economic anthropology** (production and its social relations, modes of production, and surplus extraction — Meillassoux, Godelier), on which Sahlins draws selectively in theorizing the [[domestic-mode-of-production]]. Current work has largely moved to the anthropology of money, debt, credit, and value.

## Methods

Ethnographic [[participant-observation]]; household **time-allocation and work-effort studies** (McCarthy & McArthur in Arnhem Land; Lee's !Kung day-diaries; Pospisil's Kapauku records) that quantify labor input against output; [[comparative-method|controlled comparison]] across cases; and the reanalysis of others' ethnographic and statistical data (Sahlins' method in *Stone Age Economics*, e.g. his rework of [[chayanov-a-v|Chayanov]]'s Russian peasant figures).

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Economic anthropology borders **economics** (with which the formalist–substantivist debate is a boundary dispute over whether marginalism is universal), **[[economic-sociology]]** (Polanyi's [[embeddedness]] migrated there via Granovetter), and **[[political-anthropology]]** (chiefship, the [[big-man]], and redistribution link economy to power). Its evidence is chiefly ethnographic, with archaeological and historical material used comparatively.

## Current State

Frontier capitalism and speculative performance: [[tsing-friction-study|Tsing *Friction*]] ([[capitalist-frontier]], [[economy-of-appearances]], commodity-chain friction). Salvage / peri-capitalist supply chains: [[tsing-mushroom-study|Tsing *Mushroom*]] ([[salvage-accumulation]], gift–commodity toggle, [[open-ticket-oregon]]).


Within mainstream anthropology the formalist–substantivist question is dormant; active fronts include the anthropology of debt and money, value theory, financialization, and the ethnography of markets. Polanyi's stock rose after 1990 in critical political economy.

### Critical-turn commodities and things-in-motion (1986)

*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* (Appadurai ed., 1986) reoriented the field toward **things-in-motion**, comparative [[commoditization]], [[regimes-of-value]], and the politics of demand — deliberately blurring overdrawn gift/commodity dualisms and expanding the commodity beyond a capitalism-only product. Charter essays by [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] and [[kopytoff-igor|Kopytoff]] ([[cultural-biography-of-things]], [[singularization]], [[object-biography]]); cases span Melanesia, India, Varna, medieval relics, carpets, qat, French and Indian cloth. Complements production-centered Marxist economic anthropology with a trajectory/demand focus *(attributed program)*.

## Reflexivity

The field's foundational data are largely **salvage-era and mid-century ethnography**, gathered under colonial conditions and selecting for the "untouched" — a sampling frame that shapes every generalization about "primitive" economies (Sahlins himself flags that surviving hunter-gatherers are "displaced persons" in marginal refuges). The formalist–substantivist debate was, as Sahlins argued, partly ideological: "formal economics flourishes as ideology at home and ethnocentrism abroad" *(attributed)*. The very object "the economy" is at issue, so the discipline's categories are unusually reflexive. SLOT’s ethnohistory framing (Farriss) adds the system-vs-process problem when reconstructing past regimes of value from mute or partial sources.
