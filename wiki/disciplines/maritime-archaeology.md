## Scope

Maritime archaeology studies human interaction with maritime, riverine, and lacustrine
environments through their material remains — ships and shipwrecks, ports, maritime
infrastructure, and submerged landscapes. Flatman and Staniforth's chapter distinguishes
several overlapping terms in circulation: "maritime archaeology" (the broadest,
encompassing all human interaction with the sea), "underwater archaeology" (defined by
the excavation environment rather than subject), "marine archaeology," and "nautical
archaeology" (focused specifically on vessels and seafaring technology) (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 10).

## Historical Development

George Bass's excavation of the Bronze Age wreck at Cape Gelidonya in 1960 is credited
as the first fully recorded underwater excavation conducted to the standards of
land-based archaeology, and is treated as the field's founding moment. Keith
Muckelroy's work, notably on the Kennemerland wreck, developed a systematic account of
wreck-formation processes — the physical, chemical, and cultural processes by which a
shipwreck is transformed from a functioning vessel into an archaeological deposit —
that became foundational to the subfield's method. Richard Gould's program of
"shipwreck anthropology" pushed the field toward using wrecks as evidence for the
societies that built and sailed them, not merely as engineering artifacts (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 10).

## Theoretical Landscape

The chapter's authors characterize maritime archaeology as having been dominated by
[[processual-archaeology|processual]] approaches, with formation-process analysis
(Muckelroy) as the field's central intellectual export. Robert McGhee's critique,
discussed below, is an explicit call for the field to engage more with
post-processual and postcolonial concerns.

## Methods

Core methods include underwater excavation and survey, formation-process analysis, and
[[documentary-archaeology]] used to cross-read ships' logs, insurance records, and
company archives (notably for East India Company vessels) against the excavated
remains.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Maritime archaeology sits within [[historical-archaeology]] and [[archaeology]] more
broadly, sharing method and theory debates with land-based historical archaeology while
being methodologically distinguished by its underwater recovery environment.

## Current State

Flatman and Staniforth discuss a cluster of well-documented European East India Company
wrecks — including the VOC ships *Batavia*, *Amsterdam*, and *Witte Leeuw*, and the
*Zeewijk* — as a mature body of case material combining documentary company records
with excavated cargo and hull evidence. Robert McGhee argued that the field "has not
problematised empire" — that is, that maritime archaeology has extensively documented
European trading-company shipping without adequately interrogating the colonial and
imperial relations those voyages served, an interpretive critique attributed to McGhee
rather than a wiki-voice verdict. The authors identify a corresponding evidentiary gap
around the slave trade, noting comparatively few excavated slave-ship sites — the
*Henrietta Marie* and the *James Matthews* are cited as exceptions. Port Royal (Jamaica),
Hoff's Store, and the Red Bay Basque whaling site are cited as further significant
excavated sites (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 10).

## Reflexivity

The field's wreck record is entangled with nationalism: high-profile wrecks such as the
*Mary Rose*, wrecks from the Falklands conflict, and the Swedish warship *Vasa* have
been mobilized as national heritage objects, shaping funding, public interest, and
interpretive framing in ways the chapter's authors treat as a standing methodological
concern. McGhee's critique of the field's inattention to empire, and the noted
under-representation of slave-ship archaeology relative to European merchant and naval
wrecks, are both flagged in the source as evidence of a research-focus imbalance rather
than a natural distribution of surviving sites.
