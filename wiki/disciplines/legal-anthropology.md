# Legal Anthropology

## Scope

Legal anthropology is the ethnographic and comparative study of law, dispute, order, and
social control — especially in societies without state courts, written codes, or a
professional legal class. It asks whether "law" is a universal or a culturally specific
folk category, how disputes are processed where no coercive state monopoly exists, and how
colonial and postcolonial regimes reshaped indigenous jural institutions. It overlaps
heavily with [[political-anthropology]] (order, authority, force) and draws on the same
mid-century African archive.

## Historical Development

An armchair-comparative precursor runs from Henry Maine (*Ancient Law*, 1861) through the
evolutionary jurists. The modern subfield is a product of the fieldwork era and postwar
expansion: [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]]'s *Crime and Custom in Savage Society*
(1926) argued that reciprocity, not codes and courts, underwrites order among the
[[trobriand-islanders|Trobrianders]]; Llewellyn and Hoebel's *The Cheyenne Way* (1941)
founded the **case method** (the "trouble-case" as the unit of analysis). The subfield's
defining mid-century moment is the confrontation between two African ethnographies:
[[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]'s *The Judicial Process among the Barotse* (1955) and
[[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]]'s *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice and Judgment
Among the Tiv]]* (1957).

## Theoretical Landscape

The organizing dispute of the field is over **method and comparison** — whether other
legal systems should be described in their own **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk
categories]]** (Bohannan's *[[jir]]*) or translated into the categories of Western
jurisprudence to make them comparable (Gluckman's use of "reasonable man," "law," etc.).
This is the [[bohannan-gluckman-debate]], the field's charter debate and a local instance of
the wider emic/etic and translation problems. A second axis contrasts **rule-centered**
views of law (law as a body of rules) with **process-centered** ones (law as dispute-
processing and counteraction) — Bohannan's *[[jir]]* as "counteraction" is a strong
process-centered statement.

A third, interpretive position enters with [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]'s
*[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]* essay "Local Knowledge: Fact and Law in
Comparative Perspective" (Storrs Lectures, 1981). Geertz argues (attributed) that law is "part
of a distinctive manner of imagining the real" — a culturally specific **[[local-knowledge|legal
sensibility]]** — and that comparative law should be **cultural translation** rather than
institutional taxonomy or a search for universals. He explicitly rejects *both* the
rule-centered (Pospisil) and process-centered (Malinowski) paradigms as, respectively,
"overautonomous" and "overpolitical," and distances his approach from the field's "social
control" classics (Malinowski, Llewellyn & Hoebel, Gluckman, Bohannan), praising instead Sally
Falk Moore and Lawrence Rosen. His comparison of the Islamic *haqq*, Indic *dharma*, and
Malay/Indonesian *adat* idioms shows three distinct ways of fusing "is" and "ought" that the
modern West separates as fact and law — and he adopts "**legal pluralism**" for the coexistence
of unreconciled sensibilities within one polity (case studies: pre-1974 Ethiopia; post-colonial
Indonesia). This carries the field's charter emic/etic dispute into an interpretive register:
against a jurisprudence that requires prior consensus (Grant Gilmore), Geertz holds that law is
constitutive of social life and must "compass dissensus."

## Methods

The distinctive methods are the **case / trouble-case method** (extracting recorded
disputes as the analytic unit), its later elaboration as the [[extended-case-method|extended-case
method]] (Manchester School), and [[participant-observation|participant observation]] of
courts and moots. Bohannan's Tiv material is built from close records of Grade-D court
sittings and of in-lineage moots.

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Legal anthropology sits between [[political-anthropology]], the sociology of law, and
comparative jurisprudence. Bohannan insisted the boundary with jurisprudence be policed:
the lawyer's "law" is itself a **folk system**, not a neutral analytical science, and must
not be used as a *deus ex machina* to organize other peoples' material.

## Current State

The mid-century comparison debate opened onto processual and dispute-centered approaches
(Nader, disputing-process studies), **legal pluralism**, and the anthropology of human rights
and the state. Geertz's *[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]* essay (1983) is the
interpretive turn's charter statement for the field — recasting comparison as cultural
translation and law as [[local-knowledge|legal sensibility]] — though Geertz notes the
"interpretive turn" had "not as yet had very much influence in legal studies," constrained by
"the strong 'how-to' bias of practiced law."

## Reflexivity

The field's classic archive is colonial: the "courts" Bohannan describes are British-imposed
Grade-D Native Authority courts, and the administration that outlawed Tiv self-help created
the demand its courts then met. The founding texts thus document colonial legal
transformation as much as indigenous law — a production context that shaped the very object
of study.

priority gaps.

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 1.)* Early study of [[primitive-society]] was treated as a branch of **legal studies**, not natural history: many key authors (Bachofen, Kohler, Maine, McLennan, Morgan) were lawyers; marriage, family, private property, and the state were legal questions; Roman law was the shared case-study.
