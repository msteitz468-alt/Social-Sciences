# Medical Anthropology

## Scope

Medical anthropology is the subfield of [[anthropology]] that studies health, illness, the body, and
healing as social and cultural phenomena — how sickness is experienced and explained, how healing systems
(biomedical and other) are organized, and how social, economic, and political conditions produce disease
and distribute suffering. It spans all four fields in principle (biological anthropology's disease ecology;
the ethnomedicine of cultural anthropology; the [[linguistic-anthropology|discourse]] of the clinic) but
is centered in sociocultural anthropology. Its distinctive commitment is that **[[methods|how a body comes
to be sick, named, and treated]]** is content, not background.

## Historical Development

- **Precursors and postwar expansion (to 1968).** Early disease-ecology and ethnomedical work (W.H.R.
  Rivers; culture-and-personality studies of illness) and postwar international-health programs gave the
  subfield an applied, cross-cultural base. The institutional origin was explicitly applied: a 1950s–60s
  public-health-oriented cohort (Benjamin Paul, George Foster, Charles Erasmus, Hazel Weidman) critiquing
  public-health specialists' "cultural naivete." In 1957 Ben Paul could list 49 American anthropologists
  with public-health experience; by the early 1990s the Society for Medical Anthropology had over 1,700
  members ([[good-byron|Good]] 1994). The journal *Culture, Medicine and Psychiatry* was founded in 1977.
- **Critical turn (1968–1990).** The subfield names and consolidates itself. Arthur Kleinman's work on
  "explanatory models," illness/disease distinction, and somatization built an interpretive, clinically
  engaged medical anthropology. Against and beside it, a **[[critical-medical-anthropology]]** current
  (Baer, Singer, Frankenberg, Taussig) recast sickness through political economy. The **[[the-mindful-body|"mindful
  body"]]** program of [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] and Margaret Lock (1987) reoriented the field
  toward embodiment and power.
- **Contemporary (1990–present).** Global health, the anthropology of biomedicine and pharmaceuticals,
  **[[structural-violence|structural violence]]** and health inequality ([[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]],
  whose *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* made
  [[tuberculosis]] and [[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]] among the poor the paradigm cases of
  **[[embodiment]]** via **[[biosocial-analysis]]**), and the anthropology of suffering. [[biehl-joao|João Biehl]]'s
  *[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]* (2005) names **[[zone-of-social-abandonment|zones of social abandonment]]** and
  **[[pharmaceutical-being]]** as forms of care and exclusion under democratization and the right to health.

## Theoretical Landscape

The field runs between an **interpretive/clinical** pole (meaning, explanatory models, the phenomenology
of illness) and a **critical/political-economic** pole (**[[critical-medical-anthropology]]**), with the
**[[the-mindful-body|mindful body]]** framework a bridging synthesis. The interpretive pole is now
represented in the vault by **[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology]]** — [[kleinman-arthur|Kleinman]]'s
program of the [[illness-disease-distinction|illness/disease distinction]], [[explanatory-models]],
[[illness-narrative|illness narratives]], and the [[explanatory-models-interview|mini-ethnography]]. Key
concepts developed or annexed here include [[nervoso|idioms of distress]], [[somatization]],
[[neurasthenia]], medicalization, embodiment, and structural violence.

The field's most-cited internal map is [[good-byron|Byron Good]]'s four-approach taxonomy
(*[[good-medicine-rationality-1994|Medicine, Rationality, and Experience]]*, 1994, attributed):
(1) an **empiricist/rationalist** tradition treating illness representations as folk beliefs
(Health Belief Model, illness-behavior models, ecological-biocultural adaptationism); (2)
**cognitive** anthropology (ethnosemantics, schema/prototype theory); (3) the
**[[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology|meaning-centered/interpretive]]** tradition; and (4)
**[[critical-medical-anthropology]]**. Good's own program adds the epistemological ground: the
"problem of belief" — describing others' medicine as belief and biomedicine as knowledge — as
the field's founding error.

## Methods

Grounded in [[participant-observation]], extended to the clinic and hospital (clinical ethnography),
illness-narrative and life-history collection, and — in its critical wing — explicitly engaged forms (see
[[militant-anthropology]]).

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

Medical anthropology overlaps medical sociology (the sociology of health and illness), public health and
epidemiology (which it often critiques for naturalizing social causes of disease), and bioethics. A
recurring boundary dispute concerns whether the anthropologist's role is to *serve* biomedicine (applied
medical anthropology) or to *critique* it (critical medical anthropology). A related quarrel with
epidemiology and public health — whether disease patterns are best explained by culture and behavior or by
structural inequality — is recorded at [[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]].

## Current State

Active fronts include global health and humanitarianism, the anthropology of pharmaceuticals and
biotechnology, mental health across cultures, and the political economy of epidemics. Body and tissue
markets — organs, gametes, cell lines, genetic material — form a dense contemporary cluster: multi-author
work such as *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* (Scheper-Hughes &
Wacquant eds., 2002; orig. *Body & Society* 7.2–3, 2001) gathers critical medical anthropology and adjacent
sociology around **[[body-commodification]]**, with [[lock-margaret|Margaret Lock]] on tissue alienation and
immortalized cell lines, [[cohen-lawrence|Lawrence Cohen]] on Indian kidney biopolitics, and
[[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]]'s multi-sited Organs Watch project on the global
[[human-organs-trade|organs trade]].

## Reflexivity

The subfield is internally divided over its own politics: whether medical anthropology should be a handmaiden
to clinical medicine and international health, or an adversarial critic of biomedical power and of the social
production of disease. Scheper-Hughes's **[[militant-anthropology]]** stakes the strongest version of the
latter position (see [[militant-anthropology-debate]]).

## Collection Coverage Note

Coverage is **moderate** — the interpretive pole is now strongly represented by Kleinman's founding trilogy
(*[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* 1980, *[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking
Psychiatry]]* 1988, *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]* 1988 — with a
[[hubs/thinkers/kleinman-arthur|Kleinman Thinkers Hub]]), alongside the critical pole (Scheper-Hughes,
*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]*), Biehl (*[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]*),
and Farmer (*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]*). Body-commodification and
organs-traffic coverage is strengthened by *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying
Bodies]]* (Lock, Cohen, Scheper-Hughes, and others; Organs Watch as activist multi-sited research). The broader
ethnomedical and global-health literatures remain priorities for ingest.
