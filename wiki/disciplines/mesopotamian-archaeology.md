# Mesopotamian Archaeology

Subfield of [[archaeology]] concerned with the material past of Mesopotamia (modern Iraq
and adjoining parts of Syria, Turkey, and Iran) — the setting of several of the first
farming societies, cities, writing systems, and empires. The wiki's account of the field
as a *discipline* (its history, methods, politics, and future) rests on
[[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Roger Matthews's]] critical guide (2003), the only
book-length reflexive treatment of Mesopotamian archaeology as such. Distinct from
**Assyriology** (the text-based study of cuneiform sources), with which it stands in a
long and contested relationship.

## Scope

Matthews (attributed) frames the discipline as organized around two non-exclusive
paradigms: **culture history** (predominantly European) and **anthropological
archaeology** (predominantly North American), noting that "Mesopotamian archaeologists are
seldom explicit about theoretical stances." His own position rejects choosing between
them: "Without culture history we lack the spatial and chronological coordinates within
which to situate the specifics of economics and society that only anthropological
approaches can apprehend." The discipline's object is uniquely suited, in his account, to
two of archaeology's "big questions" (after Gamble) — the origins of *urbanism/civilization*
and of *literate complex society*.

The very term **"Mesopotamia"** is contested. Zainab Bahrani (attributed) argues its use
since the mid-19th century "serves a political end in expropriating the past of Iraq … for
the European classical tradition," dissociating the ancient past from the region's modern
Islamic-era inhabitants; some scholars now prefer "Southwest Asia" / "Western Asia" as more
neutral. J. J. Finkelstein showed the Akkadian *māt bērītim* originally meant only "the
midst of the river" (the Euphrates bend), the modern all-encompassing sense being a later
Aramaic/Hellenistic-Roman extension.

## Historical Development

- **Antiquarian precursors:** Rabbi Benjamin of Tudela (12th c.); Pietro della Valle's 1625
  collection of inscribed bricks; Niebuhr's 1766 mapping of Nineveh; Claudius Rich's
  1811/1820 surveys. Ancient Mesopotamians themselves practiced a proto-archaeology
  (Nabonidus excavating millennium-old buildings at Ur and Sippar).
- **Museum-driven origins (1842–1880s):** Botta's 1842 trenches (Khorsabad) and Layard's
  1845 discovery of Assyrian palaces at Nimrud — excavation entangled from the start with
  British Museum / Louvre competition and a biblical-classical framework. Hormuzd Rassam
  ("the first Iraqi archaeologist," per Reade) dug Nineveh, Nimrud, Babylon, and beyond
  under Ottoman firman. Matthews (attributed): early Western excavation "was at one level an
  episode of loot-grabbing."
- **The German stratigraphic tradition (1899–1917):** Robert Koldewey at Babylon and Walter
  Andrae at Assur — Andrae's Ištar Temple sounding is credited as the first true
  stratigraphic excavation in the region, decades ahead of general practice.
- **Nation-building era (post-WWI):** British Mandate Iraq; Gertrude Bell heads the Iraqi
  Department of Antiquities and founds the Iraq Museum. Matthews (attributed): the new
  "largely artificial" Middle Eastern states were "in need of national identities," and
  archaeology "was directed towards such an end." 1930s Iraqi antiquities-export laws pushed
  many foreign teams (Woolley, Mallowan, the Chicago group) into Syria.
- **Anthropological turn (1947–):** [[braidwood-robert|Robert Braidwood's]] Iraq-Jarmo
  project imported an economic/ecological "idea archaeology" and the "hilly flanks" model of
  farming origins; [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert Adams]] built [[survey-archaeology|settlement
  survey]] into the region's signature method.
- **Fieldwork on ice (1979–):** the Iranian Revolution (1979) and Iraq's invasion of Kuwait
  (1990) halted almost all fieldwork in Iran and Iraq; as of Matthews's writing (2003) the
  discipline had been "on ice for over a decade," with activity shifted to Syria and Turkey
  (100+ projects in Syria in 2001; twice that in Turkey).

## Theoretical Landscape

The general archaeological sequence — culture history → economic archaeology → New
(processual) archaeology → interpretive/post-processual — is unevenly absorbed here. Ian
Morris (attributed) has "rekindled" the culture-history debate, arguing 1960s critiques were
naive and that recent culture history shares post-processual concerns with meaning and
context; Susan Pollock's charge that the field remains "starkly atheoretical" is itself
contested. Named framing devices Matthews engages include the [[world-systems-analysis|Uruk
world-system]] ([[algaze-guillermo|Algaze]]) and its [[distance-parity|distance-parity]]
challenger ([[stein-gil|Stein]]) — see the [[uruk-world-system-debate]].

## Methods

Signature methods: [[stratigraphic-excavation|excavation]] of deep mud-brick tells (where
Matthews argues single-context planning developed for European urban sites needs adapting,
and champions the hand-pick and micromorphology); [[survey-archaeology|regional settlement
survey]] (the Adams tradition — the discipline's most distinctive contribution);
[[seriation|ceramic seriation]] and type-site chronology (Hassuna, Samarra, Halaf, Ubaid,
Uruk, Jemdet Nasr, agreed at the 1929 Baghdad / 1931 Leiden conferences); and scientific
dating ([[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]]). The **text–artifact relationship** is the
discipline's defining methodological problem — see
[[archaeology-assyriology-text-object-debate]].

## Relations to Adjacent Disciplines

The boundary with **Assyriology** is the load-bearing one: whether archaeology is a
"handmaiden" supplying context for the texts, or an independent evidence class with its own
authority. Matthews (with Van De Mieroop, Moreland, Morris) argues for parity — texts are
themselves "archaeological artefacts with meaningful contexts." Adjacent also to
Near Eastern history, [[archaeogenetics]], environmental archaeology, and art
history (Bahrani).

## Current State

As of 2003 the field's Iraqi heartland was inaccessible; research had concentrated on the
Neolithic (Çatalhöyük, Göbekli Tepe), on survey and household-scale questions, and on the
peripheries (Syria, Turkey). Matthews flags an institutional crisis at home: the ancient
Near East had all but vanished from UK and French school curricula. Written on the eve of
the 2003 Iraq war, his "Futures" chapter warns of imminent site destruction (the Assur dam)
without yet registering the looting catastrophe that followed.

## Reflexivity

Reflexivity is unusually central to this field and to Matthews's book. The discipline was
born of **imperial and museum competition** and read the region through a biblical-classical
lens; Matthews invokes Said, quoting that an "entire past was being kidnapped as part of
building the Orientalist discourse," and Bahrani's charge that the image of Mesopotamia "was
necessary for a march of progress from East to West … explicitly Eurocentric and
imperialist." He positions himself openly — "a Mesopotamian archaeologist of west European
origin," "an institutional, male, white Westerner" — and stakes out a measured counter-view,
wanting to "bring in from the postmodern cold" the 19th-century excavators (quoting Seton
Lloyd) while accepting the critique's force. He amplifies the **postcolonial critique of
practice**: the *Adumatu* editorial (Al-Ansary) asking "why should foreign expeditions impose
their cultural and epistemological orientations on our archaeological sites?", Kohl on the
"Orientalist mode," and Redman on the unsustainability of Western-directed fieldwork; he
calls for host-country academics at every level and criticizes Western archaeologists'
unwillingness to learn Arabic and Turkish. On gender, he endorses correcting the field's
"masculinist bias" (Pollock, Bahrani) — and recovers the mid-century "inverted archaeology"
memoirs by archaeologists' wives (Agatha Christie, Mary Chubb) as an unofficial genre.
Shanks and Tilley, quoted approvingly: "Archaeology, as cultural practice, is always a
politics, always a morality."
