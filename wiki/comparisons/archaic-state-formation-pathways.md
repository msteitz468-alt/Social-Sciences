# Archaic State-Formation Pathways (Service 1975)

A comparison drawn entirely from [[service-elman-r|Service]]'s
*[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]* (1975), reading
his fifteen cases through his [[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative theory]].
All claims are **attributed to Service** and rest on mid-century secondary evidence
superseded on many points; the table records his readings, not current archaeology.

## Two classes of case

- **Archaic (pristine) civilizations**, known archaeologically:
  [[teotihuacan|Mesoamerica]], Peru, [[uruk|Mesopotamia]], Egypt,
  [[harappan-civilization|Indus]], [[shang-civilization|China]].
- **"Modern primitive states"**, known ethnohistorically and (Service stresses)
  secondary and contact-shaped: [[zulu|Zulu]], [[banyankole|Ankole]], Nupe,
  [[ashanti|Ashanti]], Kongo, Cherokee, [[hawaiians|Hawaii]], Tahiti, Tonga.

## The pristine cases (attributed to Service)

| Case | Urbanism | Coercion at origin | Integrative mechanism Service emphasizes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mesopotamia (Sumer) | High; walled cities | Warfare/kingship *later* (Sargon) | Ubaid temple as sanctuary + storehouse + redistributive center; trade + craft specialization; urbanism a defensive response |
| Egypt | Low; no true cities until late 2nd millennium | Peaceful Old Kingdom; no nomadic threat | "Super-bureaucracy" — treasury, corvée, craft guilds; royal monopoly of trade; rank as *stewardship, not ownership*; circumscription by desert |
| Indus (Harappa) | Planned twin cities, unfortified | No clear evidence of war/coercion | "Priestly state" like Egypt's Old Kingdom (McNeill/Piggott); Malik: a chiefdom, seedbed of caste |
| China (Shang–Chou) | Less urban early | Ecological polarization; later marcher-state war | Hereditary theocracy/ramage + ancestor worship; "mandate of heaven" as integrative ideology |
| Mesoamerica | Highland urban (Teotihuacán); lowland non-urban (Maya) | Warfare in collapse, not origin | Redistribution across symbiotic ecological regions; centralization and redistributive efficiency reinforce each other |
| Peru | Late, trade-route highland centers (Huari/Tiahuanaco) | Warfare *after* monumentality | Canals built *under chiefdom* authority centuries before states (Lanning); highland centers control cross-zone trade |

## Cross-case patterns Service draws

1. **Civilization precedes empire and urbanism.** In every case theocratic
   redistribution, craft specialization, and monumental works precede military empire;
   "urbanism is not necessarily the concomitant of a developing civilization."
2. **Two structural styles.** An "Egyptian style" (spread-out, peaceful, low-urban,
   bureaucratic — Egypt, Indus, coastal Peru) versus a "Sumerian style" (walled,
   warfare-driven urbanism — Sumer, Tiahuanaco).
3. **Population threshold → impersonal controls.** Growth past a point "requires more
   and more authoritative controls of the more impersonal kind" — the bureaucratic
   engine of the integrative theory.
4. **Marcher-state advantage.** Dynastic renewal comes disproportionately from
   less-specialized frontier polities (Upper Egypt's Thebes, Sargon, the Chou and
   Ch'in, the Aztecs) — an application of the [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]].
5. **Circumscription as enabler, not cause.** Geographic boundedness (Egypt, Peru)
   and persistent external threat (Mesopotamia, north China) make membership's benefits
   obvious and strengthen integration — Carneiro's factor folded into "governance by
   benefit."

## The secondary cases as analogy

The ethnohistoric states illustrate the chiefdom→state transition in observable time:
Zulu (Mpande's peaceful statecraft under politico-military circumscription, not
Shaka's terror); Ankole (clientship and the royal-drum cult); Ashanti (confederative
growth and the Golden Stool); Hawaii (the 1819 secularizing "cultural revolution").
Service reads each against the "great man vs. the invisible hand" question, siding with
a structural/evolutionary process that still leaves room for individual political
skill. Because all are contact-shaped secondary states, he treats them as
hypothesis-generating analogies for the pristine cases, not as independent evidence —
see the `galton_note`.

## Standing

The comparison is the empirical spine of the
[[integrative-theory-of-the-state|integrative theory]] and a primary document in the
[[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. Its case readings are
attributed and dated; current archaeology revises many (Harappan state-vs-chiefdom
status, Andean and Chinese chronologies, calibration) — see
[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]] for a
later multi-causal survey of the same six pristine areas.

### Counter-reading — Yoffee 2005 (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*) rejects the Service-style packaging of pristine civilizations into a single integrative pathway and stage sequence. He stresses **city-state** multiplicity within civilizations, limits on despotism, and non-state trajectories (Chaco, Cahokia) that Service’s archaic table does not center. Use this comparison as **Service’s map**, not as wiki-voice pathway law; pair with [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]] and Trigger’s [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]].

## Adams layer — Mesopotamia ↔ central Mexico (1966, attributed)

A separate two-case controlled comparison predates Service: [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]], *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* ([[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|study]]). Adams does **not** score six pristine civilizations; he argues southern Mesopotamia and central Mexico are **slightly variant examples of one multilinear processual pattern** (fundamental similarity without one-for-one formal identity).

| Dimension | Adams (1966, attributed) |
|---|---|
| Cases | Mesopotamia (Protoliterate → Akkadian); central Mexico (Teotihuacan → Aztec) |
| Method | Homotaxial phase correlation under [[multilinear-evolution]]; contextual synthesis |
| Sequence | [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]] (theocratic → militaristic → conquest / trade-and-tribute states) |
| Homotaxial pairs | [[uruk]] ≈ [[teotihuacan]]; Early Dynastic ≈ end Tula; Akkadian ≈ [[aztec-empire]] |
| Growth curves | Style/monumentality = "step"; social differentiation = "ramp" (Mesopotamia ~A; Mexico pulsed ~B) |
| Surplus | Gross deployable wealth, institutionally mobilized (Polanyi/Orans) — not per-capita efficiency prime mover |
| Stratification | Corporate kin land → private elite estates; class as objective access without required class-consciousness |
| Imperial termini | Both Akkadian and Aztec as loosely knit trade-and-tribute systems, not full modern "empires" |
| Differences | Mexican fragmentation between peaks; markets hyperdeveloped in Mesoamerica vs redistributive Mesopotamian composite communities; managerial vs more predatory elite styles at terminal points |

Use Service's table for six-case integrative readings; use Adams for the classic Old World / New World urban parallel and the ramp/step + theocratic→militaristic apparatus.
