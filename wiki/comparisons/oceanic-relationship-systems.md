# Oceanic Systems of Relationship (Rivers' comparison)

## What Is Compared

The comparison at the heart of [[rivers-melanesian-society-study|Rivers' *The History of
Melanesian Society*]] (1914): the systems of relationship (kinship terminologies) of some
two dozen Melanesian and Polynesian communities, compared along two axes Rivers deliberately
separated — the **morphological** (structure: which relatives are merged or distinguished,
and by what principles) and the **linguistic** (the actual terms, treated as distributional
facts). The comparative tables of Volume I are the evidentiary base, drawing on Rivers' own
[[genealogical-method|genealogical]] fieldwork plus published sources (Gray, Ray, Wheeler,
Peekel) for Aniwa, Tanna, the Shortlands, and New Ireland.

## Dimensions and Findings (attributed)

- **Diversity.** No two Oceanic systems are exactly alike; complexity and richness of
  terminology vary enormously (Pentecost, inland Fiji, and Buin richest; Polynesia and
  Eddystone simplest).
- **Resemblance tracks structure, not race or geography.** Widely separated peoples (coastal
  Viti Levu, southern New Hebrides, Guadalcanar) resemble one another more than they resemble
  their near neighbours — from which Rivers inferred that terminology depends on social
  structure, above all **forms of marriage** ([[cross-cousin-marriage]] and the anomalous
  gerontocratic marriages), not on physical type.
- **Terminology encodes marriage and function.** Special terms track special social functions
  attached to kin (e.g. the mother's brother), and their presence or fossilized survival is
  read as evidence for present or past marriage practice.
- **Uniformity vs. diversity as a stratigraphic signal.** In the linguistic comparison,
  Rivers read terms showing wide *uniformity* across otherwise diverse Melanesian languages as
  a later **introduced** stratum, and terms showing great *diversity* as the older aboriginal
  layer — the pivot to his [[diffusionism|diffusionist]] reconstruction.

## Method

A [[comparative-method|cross-cultural comparison]] organised by kin-type category across
societies (a wide-format typological table), with correlations drawn between terminological
features and marriage/social-organisation types. The [[genealogical-method]] supplied the raw
pedigrees; Rivers graded each society's evidence by informant and linguistic medium.

## Galton's Problem

See the `galton_note` above: Rivers' comparison is a rare early case where the non-independence
of cases is not a methodological embarrassment to be controlled but the **explicit object** of
study — the resemblances are the evidence for historical connection. This is also its
vulnerability: because every resemblance is read as descent or diffusion, the scheme resists
the kind of test that independent-case comparison allows.

## Standing

The comparative *data* remain a valued early corpus of Oceanic kinship; the *historical
inferences* built on them belong to the abandoned diffusionist program. Later comparative
kinship theory (Lévi-Strauss' alliance theory) and evidence-based Oceanic migration studies
(historical linguistics, and ancient-DNA population genetics as of the 2010s aDNA revolution) reworked the same material on other foundations.
