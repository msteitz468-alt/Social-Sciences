# France, Russia, and China: Old-Regime Breakdown, Peasant Revolt, and Revolutionary State-Building

The controlled comparison at the heart of [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]]'s *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979). France, Russia, and China are the **positive cases** (social revolutions occurred); England, Meiji Japan, and Prussia/Germany are **negative/contrast cases** (comparable international pressure, but reform-from-above or non-social political revolution instead). All claims below are **attributed** to Skocpol. Method: J. S. Mill's Method of Agreement (across the positive cases) plus Method of Difference (positive vs. negative); see [[comparative-method]] and the [[structural-theory-of-revolution|structural theory of revolution]].

## Dimension 1 — Old-regime state and dominant-class leverage (cause of breakdown)

| Case | State form | Landed dominant class | Extralocal political leverage over the state | Breakdown pathway |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| France | Proto-bureaucratic Bourbon absolutism, "multiply layered" (venal office, parlements, provincial estates) | "Proprietary" landed-commercial class, entrenched *within* the state | **High** — parlements could block tax/reform; officers reluctant to repress | Deposition: dominant-class reaction (1787–89) paralyzed the administration |
| China | Proto-bureaucratic Manchu empire; gentry an amalgam of landlords + degree-holding officials | Gentry, locally hegemonic; gained corporate voice via 1908–10 assemblies | **High (rising)** — de facto provincial control + new assemblies | Deposition: gentry/provincial reaction to centralizing reform → 1911 collapse, warlordism |
| Russia | More genuinely bureaucratic/militarized autocracy (Petrine) | Weak, state-dependent nobility (scattered estates, mandatory service, indebted) | **Low** — no corporate leverage; could not block the 1861 Emancipation | Dissolution: total-war (WWI) defeat destroyed the state (1917) |
| *England (contrast)* | No standing army/royal bureaucracy in localities | Landlords own ~⅔ of land; rule localities as gentry (JPs, militia) | Landlords ARE the local state | Political (not social) revolution via intra-elite civil war |
| *Japan (contrast)* | Tokugawa baku-han; samurai a salaried administrative corps separated from land | No politically powerful landed class | None (landlords excluded from extralocal power) | Reform from above: Meiji coup by outer-han samurai (1868) |
| *Prussia (contrast)* | Highly bureaucratic-military Hohenzollern state | Junkers: total *local* control, but only as disciplined individuals in the state | Local only — no corporate state-level leverage | Reform from above (1807–15); army stays reliable |

**Agreement across positive cases:** international military pressure from more-developed competitors met a state that could not reform fast enough. **Difference from negatives:** where the landed class held *extralocal, corporate* leverage within a *not-fully-bureaucratic* state (France, China), reaction broke the state; where it did not (Japan, Prussia), crisis was resolved from above. Russia is the intermediate case — a strong-enough autocracy that overrode its weak nobility, but whose backward agrarian economy left it unable to withstand total war.

## Dimension 2 — Agrarian structure and peasant insurrectionary capacity

| Case | Peasant landholding | Community solidarity / autonomy | Local sanctioning machinery | Result on state breakdown |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| France | Owns/works most land under seigneurial claims | Village community, self-governing; cohered against seigneurs (but respects private property) | Royal officials, not seigneurs | Autonomous antiseigneurial revolt (1789); dues abolished, private property spared |
| Russia | Communal tenure (obshchina), periodic repartition; "all land belonged to God" | Very strong; sovereign village assembly; no legitimation of private property | Tsarist bureaucracy, not landlords | Autonomous, *leveling* land seizure (1917); estates and rich-peasant farms redistributed |
| China | Owns/works most land; rents to gentry | **Weak** — "no peasant community"; marketing community led by gentry (Skinner; Fei) | Gentry-dominated (clans, militias) | No autonomous revolt possible; required CCP/Red Army to organize peasants from outside |
| *England* | Landlords own ~⅔; peasantry polarized (yeomen vs. laborers) | None strong | Landlord-controlled | No peasant revolt; social structure untransformed |
| *Germany (E. of Elbe)* | Junker estates; peasants apart, no open-field community | None strong | Junker = local state agent | No east-Elbian peasant revolt; Prussian army stays intact → 1848 crushed |

**The decisive interaction:** structural capacity for revolt (solidarity + autonomy) becomes *actual* revolt only when centralized state coercion breaks down — neither factor alone suffices.

## Dimension 3 — Revolutionary outcome (type of state built)

| Case | New regime | Driving conditions | Peasant incorporation |
|---|---|---|---|
| France | Professional-bureaucratic, centralized state (Napoleonic); private-propertied market society | War + Jacobin/Napoleonic consolidation; market economy left intact | Peasants left as propertied smallholders; not incorporated into a party |
| Russia | Dictatorial party-state; state-propelled heavy industrialization; Stalinist "revolution from above" | International insecurity + inherited heavy industry + a hostile subsistence peasantry the regime could not reach except by force | Coerced (collectivization); exaggerated inequalities |
| China | Mass-mobilizing party-state, relatively decentralized; agrarian/rural emphasis | CCP built through village-level penetration; direct political ties to peasant villages | "Participatory mobilization" (vs. Soviet "command mobilization," Bernstein); more local participation |

**Agreement:** all three built states more centralized, bureaucratic, mass-incorporating, and internationally powerful than the old regimes — *regardless* of ideological direction (pro- or anti-capitalist). **Difference:** the type of state tracked how the revolutionaries reached the peasantry, which tracked back to Dimension 2's agrarian structures — not to the professed ideology.

## What the comparison establishes

Skocpol's controlled comparison is the empirical spine of the [[structural-theory-of-revolution]]: it argues that a small set of structural variables — state bureaucratization, dominant-class leverage, international pressure, and agrarian class structure — predict both *whether* a social revolution occurs and *what kind of state* results, without recourse to revolutionary ideology or actors' intentions. Its contested points (agency, ideology, secondary sources, selection on outcome) are recorded on the [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|study page]] and in [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].
