# Modjokuto vs Tabanan — Economic Modernization Paths

[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]’s controlled two-town comparison in *[[geertz-peddlers-princes-1963|Peddlers and Princes]]* (1963). Source study: [[geertz-peddlers-princes-study]]. Design principle: **similarity amid difference**.

## Cases

| | Modjokuto (Java) | Tabanan (Bali) |
|---|---|---|
| Character | Market town; bazaar tradition strong | Former court town; aristocratic/state tradition strong |
| Fieldwork | 1952–54 (+ short return) | 1957–58 |
| Population (Geertz) | ~24,000 (~2,000 Chinese) | ~12,000 (~826 Chinese) |
| Innovative class | Self-made reformist-Muslim peddlers → shop/factory | Nobles redirecting political loyalty → enterprises |
| Economic mode | [[bazaar-type-economy]] → [[firm-type-economy]] | Rural *seka* / pluralistic collectivism + aristocratic firm-type |
| Ethos (Geertz) | *Homo economicus* of the market | *Homo politicus* of the palace |
| Historical root of leadership | North-coast Islamized commercial elite of 1910s–20s | Hinduized, caste-insulated pre-Revolutionary political elite; 1906 conquest as delayed shock |
| Ideology | Islamic reform / puritan excellence | Catholic, restorationist aristocratic excellence |
| Organizational strength | Bazaar business orientation; individual shrewdness | Mass mobilization; *seka*; vertical loyalty |
| Organizational defect | Atomism; firms fail to grow | Politicization; welfare pressure; firms fail to rationalize |
| Urbanization | Advanced commercial urbanization (facilitates and distorts) | Weaker; half-urbanization risks |

## Shared frame

Both towns: fertile irrigated-rice hinterlands; administrative–commercial–educational capitals; East/West and traditional/modern contact arenas; pretake-off Indonesia (Geertz’s 1952–59 “now”). Narrowly economic process Geertz treats as **nearly identical**: clear a field for economic rationality + construct a normative economic ethic. Social reorganization **differs**.

## Geertz’s six synthetic propositions

*(All attributed, Conclusion 1963.)*

1. Entrepreneurship occurs in a fairly well defined, socially homogeneous group.
2. That group crystallizes from a long extra-village, interlocal traditional formation (bazaar network vs court network).
3. The larger parent group is undergoing radical change in its relation to the wider society — innovation as response to status insecurity / disarray of status demarcations.
4. On the ideological level the innovative group conceives itself as vehicle of religious/moral excellence within a wayward community.
5. Major innovations and innovational problems are **organizational rather than technical**.
6. Function of the entrepreneur in pretake-off societies: adapt **customarily established means to novel ends** (Janus-faced bridge; transitional institutions).

## End-state (Geertz)

Both groups seized pre-Revolutionary elements facilitative of modernization and entered a “no man’s land of transition.” Renovation of usable past is insufficient for later phases: “the mentalities of peddler and prince must both be abandoned for that of the professional manager” *(attributed)*.

## Policy implication (Geertz’s position)

National planning should search for local **growing points** and differentiate by class/town/region rather than monistic hypernational crash programs — while still requiring central planning capacity (not grass-roots laissez-faire romanticism).

## Limits

Galton problem: shared nation and colonial history. Weakness of state tradition in Modjokuto and of bazaar tradition in Tabanan leaves the dual-vigorous case (e.g. Jogjakarta, per Geertz’s footnote) untested. Post-1959 national politics outside frame. Chinese commercial role and national capital constraints limit generalizing from local innovators alone.
