# Hunter-Gatherer Band Organization (cross-case)

A cross-case comparison drawn from Part III of *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the
Hunter]]* (1968), the volume's HRAF-style comparative payload on how foraging groups are
organized. Its collective import was the [[patrilocal-band-model-debate|challenge to the
patrilocal band model]].

## Dimension compared

Band **composition** (agnatic vs. bilateral), **size**, **territoriality**, and **residence
rule**, across the volume's ethnographic cases.

## The cases

*(Attributed to each fieldworker; dated to their fieldwork moments.)*

| Case | Group/"band" | Composition | Territory? |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[juhoansi]] (Lee/Marshall) | camp ~25, fluid | bilateral; Lee avoids "band" | non-exclusive |
| [[hadza]] (Woodburn) | camp ~18 adults, highly variable | bilateral, matrifocal | none asserted |
| [[mbuti]] (Turnbull) | net-hunting band, in flux | by "dissent," age, not descent | loosely based |
| [[netsilik]] (Damas) | winter village ~100 | bilateral, agnatic emphasis; virilocal extended | seasonal |
| Dogrib (Helm) | regional / local / task groups | bilateral primary linkage | range-based |
| Birhor (Williams) | "lineage-band" ~25 | flexible; "patrilocal... less misleading" | seasonal |
| Australia (Hiatt/Meggitt/Pilling) | community of 2–12 patriclans | not the single-clan horde | heartlands, vague limits |

## Patterns and findings

*(Attributed.)* Across the cases the recurring pattern is **fluid, often bilateral,
non-exclusively-patrilocal** composition, with a modal local-group/camp size of roughly
**15–25 adults** noted independently by Binford, Woodburn ([[hadza|Hadza]]), and Lee
([[juhoansi|!Kung]]). The term "band" itself was contested as a valid cross-cultural unit:
Damas reported it had been applied to groups as disparate as the 25-member Birhor and the
300–400-member Peel River Kutchin. Even *within* the single Eskimo culture area, Damas showed
settlement types ranging from 300-person whaling villages to tiny mobile Caribou Eskimo
groups — a caution against one "hunter-gatherer" social type. A few cases (Ona, Kaiadilt,
Birhor) showed more patrilocal-looking organization, keeping the classical model alive as a
*variant*, not the norm.

## Galton's problem

**Not controlled.** Helm and others analogized directly across Dogrib, Nambikwara, !Kung,
Hadza, and Arnhem Land on shared "bilateral primary linkage" without addressing whether these
regionally dispersed cases are historically independent. The convergence is best read as
**suggestive parallel adaptation** to the demographic and ecological conditions of low-density
foraging, not as statistically independent confirmation. Sharp warned that "the smallness of
our sample" and "the lack of situational material" limit any typological claim, and that some
populations are better described as overlapping "ego-centered set[s] of societies" than as
bounded bands.

## Bearing on theory

The comparison is the empirical basis of the [[patrilocal-band-model-debate|patrilocal
band-model debate]] and a standing qualification on the [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] and on
[[composite-hunting-band|Steward's/Service's band typologies]].
