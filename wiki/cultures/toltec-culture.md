# Toltec Culture

The Toltecs, ruling from their capital [[sites/tula|Tollan (Tula)]] in Hidalgo, are
identified in post-Conquest Nahuatl annals as one of four successive "unifying forces"
in pre-Spanish Mexican history — Olmec, Classic Teotihuacan, Toltec, and Aztec (Coe &
Koontz 2013). The annals depict the Toltecs ("the artificers") as ruling northern and
central Mexico, the Guatemalan highlands, and the Yucatan Peninsula, and virtually every
later Mexican and Maya dynasty claimed Toltec descent.

## Definition and Construct History

"Toltec" is a composite construct resting on two evidence classes that must be kept
separate: a body of semi-legendary post-Conquest annals (Nahuatl and Spanish
ethnohistorical sources) narrating a dynastic history, and the archaeological record at
[[sites/tula]] and related sites. The annals themselves describe Toltec society as
composed of two groups: the Tolteca-Chichimeca, probably Nahua speakers and the
dominant political group, and the Nonoalca, described by some scholars as sculptors and
artisans from Puebla and the Gulf Coast brought to build Tula's monuments (attributed,
per Coe & Koontz).

**The annals-derived narrative should be treated as attributed narrative, not
wiki-voice fact.** Per the Jiménez Moreno reconstruction as presented by Coe & Koontz,
the Tolteca-Chichimeca under the ruler Mixcoatl entered civilized Mexico via northern
Jalisco/southern Zacatecas in the early ninth century AD, settling first at Colhuacan
in the Valley of Mexico. Mixcoatl's son Topiltzin, born in the year 1 Reed (AD 935 or
947 per the annals), was later identified — "to the confusion of modern scholars," per
the source — with the Feathered Serpent deity Quetzalcoatl; post-Conquest sources
describe him as fair-skinned, with long hair and a black beard. Topiltzin Quetzalcoatl
is recorded as having moved the Toltec capital from Colhuacan, via Tulancingo, to Tula.

The annals recount a struggle between Topiltzin's peaceful Feathered-Serpent cult
(which opposed human sacrifice) and devotees of Tezcatlipoca ("Smoking Mirror"),
patron of the warrior orders; Topiltzin and his followers were forced to flee Tula,
"perhaps in AD 987," in a defeat Nahuatl poetry frames as engineered by Tezcatlipoca
through trickery. The legend of Topiltzin's departure has two endings recorded in the
sources: self-immolation at the Gulf coast, becoming the Morning Star; or departure
eastward on a serpent raft with a promise of return — the version the Spanish claimed
was known to Motecuhzoma Xocoyotzin and invoked around the time of the Conquest (see
[[cultures/aztec-empire]]).

**Historicity is a live dispute, not settled fact.** Coe & Koontz note Maya accounts of
a Mexican conqueror "Kukulcan" ("Feathered Serpent") arriving from the west (perhaps AD
987) and subjugating Yucatan, which they treat as possible historical corroboration of
the Topiltzin legend, alongside archaeological evidence of a seaborne Toltec-linked
intrusion establishing a "Mexican" period centered on [[sites/chichen-itza]] in the
late tenth century. Against this, archaeologist/ethnohistorian Susan Gillespie is cited
as having "marshaled impressive evidence that much of [Topiltzin's] 'history' was
fabricated in the early Colonial period" — raising the possibility "there may never have
been such a personage as Topiltzin Quetzalcoatl." Coe & Koontz state that Gillespie's
argument does not affect the separate, independent material evidence for Toltec traits
at Chich'en Itza — the two evidence classes (annals-narrative and archaeological
material culture) can diverge without one invalidating the other.

With the Tezcatlipoca faction dominant, the annals describe the Toltec state reaching
its greatest territorial extent. The last ruler, Huemac ("Big Hand"), presided over
Tula's end amid droughts and factional conflict between the Tolteca-Chichimeca and the
Nonoalca; he transferred his capital to Chapultepec in 1156 or 1168 and committed
suicide there. The remaining Tolteca-Chichimeca abandoned Tula roughly fifteen years
later, moving south to the Valley of Mexico and Cholula, triggering what Coe & Koontz
call the "Toltec diaspora" — refugee groups across highland and lowland Mexico and
Guatemala subsequently claimed Tula ancestry.

## Material Signature

Archaeological Tula (see [[sites/tula]] for detail) provides an independent check on the
annals: monumental architecture (Pyramid B's atlantean warriors and Feathered Serpent
columns, chacmools, the Palacio Quemado), warrior-order iconography (jaguar, coyote,
eagle bas-reliefs), long-distance trade goods (Tohil Plumbate and Papagayo Polychrome
pottery, Southwestern turquoise), and evidence of large-scale obsidian craft production.
Notably, Tezcatlipoca — dominant in the annals' narrative — is essentially absent from
the excavated material record, an unresolved tension between the two evidence classes.

## Linguistic Signature

The Tolteca-Chichimeca are presumed Nahua speakers (attributed, not directly evidenced
by inscriptional material at Tula); the Nonoalca's linguistic affiliation is not
established in the source.

## Genetic Signature

No aDNA or biodistance study is cited for Tula's population (as of Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Distribution and Chronology

Toltec-linked material culture and annalistic claims of Toltec descent extend from
central Mexico south into the Guatemalan highlands and, via the contested connection
discussed below, into Yucatan. See [[sites/tula]] for the site's internal occupation
sequence (Preclassic villages through the Tollan-phase apogee).

## Lifeways

Reconstructed largely from the Tula excavations: a stratified, militarized urban
society organized around warrior orders (coyote, jaguar, eagle), large-scale
obsidian-tool production, agriculture dependent on small-canal irrigation at the edge of
Mesoamerica's viable farming zone, and long-distance trade reaching the Gulf Coast,
Pacific coast Central America, and the US Southwest (turquoise).

## Identity Cautions

"Toltec" names an annals-derived dynastic history that is semi-legendary in its
particulars (Mixcoatl, Topiltzin Quetzalcoatl) and an archaeological culture (Tula's
material record) that agrees with the annals in general outline (a Post-Classic
Nahua-affiliated state, later destroyed) but diverges in specifics (the near-total
absence of Tezcatlipoca imagery despite his narrative prominence). Treat the annals
and the archaeology as two separate evidence classes; do not silently merge them into
one uncomplicated "Toltec people."

## Historiography

The identification of "Tollan" with the Hidalgo site of Tula was itself a 20th-century
scholarly achievement (Jiménez Moreno, late 1930s), following decades of confusion in
which the generic sense of "Tollan" ("civilized capital") led scholars including George
Vaillant to equate the Toltec capital with Teotihuacan. Gillespie's critique of
Topiltzin's historicity (cited by Coe & Koontz) represents a significant late-20th-century
revisionist current; the resulting historiographical position is recorded, not
adjudicated, on this page — see [[debates/toltec-chichen-itza-connection-debate]] for
the related dispute over the Tula–Chich'en Itza connection.
