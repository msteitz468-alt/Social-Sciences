# Hongshan Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Hongshan culture, dated ca. 4500–3000 BCE per [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu
& Chen (2012)]], is distributed over a broad region of the Liao River system in
Northeast China. It succeeded the Zhaobaogou culture (ca. 5200–4500 BCE), which in
turn succeeded the early-Neolithic [[xinglongwa-culture]] in the same region. Nearly
100 Zhaobaogou sites and more than 500 Hongshan sites have been reported, though only
a few dozen have been excavated. `construct_status: robust` as a named archaeological-
culture construct well established in the literature — Liu & Chen never equate
Hongshan with a language or genetic population, and this page follows the wiki's
four-way non-identity rule accordingly.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** Population is reported to have grown steadily
from Xinglongwa through Zhaobaogou, increased rapidly during Hongshan, then declined
sharply by the succeeding Xiaoheyan culture (ca. 3000–2200 BCE). Table 6.1 in the
source reports lithic-assemblage composition (microliths, grinding stones, knives)
from seven Hongshan sites: Baiyinchanghan, Nantaizi, Xishuiquan, Gacha, [[niuheliang]],
Shuiquan, and Erdaoliang; grinding-stone proportions vary widely across these sites
(5–57 percent). More than 250 Hongshan jade objects have been excavated or collected
from nearly 30 sites, including animal figurines (dragon, pig-dragon, turtle, bird),
human figurines, *bi* discs, axes, adzes, awls, *jue* earrings, hook-cloud ornaments,
tubes, and beads. Recent provenance research (Wang, S. et al. 2007) indicates most
Hongshan jade (nephrite) matches the Xiuyan jade mines on the Liaodong Peninsula, some
300 km from [[niuheliang]], suggesting a regional exchange network supplied the raw
material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

Hongshan settlement pattern comprises two site types: ceremonial centers — only four
locations or clusters within the Daling River system ([[niuheliang]], Dongshanzui,
Hutougou, and at least seven locations in the upper Laohushan River Valley) — and
ordinary villages of semi-subterranean houses arranged in rows, with nearby cemeteries
in which only small numbers of burials show stone-slab construction or jade.

**Linguistic.** [[unknown]] — no linguistic affiliation is proposed for Hongshan
populations in this source.

**Genetic.** [[unknown]] as of this source. Isotopic dietary data from Xinglongwa-site
human bone (N=7 early-period, N=1 Hongshan-period samples) suggest a trend toward
increased C4 (millet) intake into the Hongshan period, but Liu & Chen caution the
sample sizes are too small to generalize.

## Distribution and Chronology

Hongshan sites are distributed over a broad region of the Liao River system,
Northeast China. Ceremonial construction at [[niuheliang]] dates to the late Hongshan
period, ca. 3650–3150 BCE; a radiocarbon date from Dongshanzui gives 5485 ± 110 cal.
BP (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Hongshan culture collapsed by roughly 3000 BCE.

## Lifeways

Li Xinwei (2008) and other scholars argue the Hongshan people were agriculturalists,
citing settlement density, domesticated pig and millet remains, harvesting-knife
typology, house and pottery similarities to Yellow River agricultural cultures, and a
cosmology "focused on assuring abundant harvests of cereals" — described by Liu & Chen
as "the consensus of archaeologists." Liu & Chen (2012) themselves assess the
subsistence evidence as mixed: flotation samples from Hongshan-period deposits at
Xinglonggou show a floral assemblage dominated by nuts and fruits (acorn, hazelnut,
Manchurian walnut; Zhao Z. 2004a), and microliths remain common. Their conclusion:
"the Hongshan people are likely to have relied on broad-spectrum subsistence
strategies, although with agriculture playing a much more important role than ever
before" — an attributed synthesis, not a settled fact.

Liu & Chen's social-complexity synthesis for Hongshan (explicitly attributed
interpretation) holds: (1) a settlement hierarchy exists, but with no evidence of a
centralized polity across the whole Liao region; (2) functional segregation between
ritual centers ([[niuheliang]], Dongshanzui) and villages intensified in the late
Hongshan period; (3) monumental architecture (cairns, altars, the Goddess Temple at
[[niuheliang]]) implies labor mobilization and management beyond the individual
community; (4) jade craft specialization required procurement, standardized
manufacture, and elite-managed distribution; and (5) ritual paraphernalia (figurines,
masks) grew from small domestic-scale objects, as at [[xinglongwa-culture]] and
Zhaobaogou, to large public-ritual-scale objects, indicating a broadened ritual
context moving from household to regional scale (Chen, X. 1990; Li, X. 2008; Shelach
1999). Small chert drills and scrapers found in cairns and pits near ritual
structures at [[niuheliang]] suggest, per Liu, L. (2003), that elites "may have been
involved, to a certain extent" in jade production, though the authors note "more
evidence is needed to test this proposition." Liu & Chen state it remains "unclear...
what forms of regional level sociopolitical organization may have been involved in
the construction of ritual landscapes on such a magnificently extensive scale" —
an explicitly flagged open question, not a settled finding.

Shelach-Lavi ([[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]]) adds that
the Niuheliang complex comprises more than ten related sites within roughly 50 km²; its
Goddess Temple is about 25 m long and 2–9 m wide, set on an earthen platform of roughly
3 ha, and yielded life-size clay sculptures including a female figure (Barnes and Guo
1996; Liaoning 1997). He also cites a settlement-hierarchy interpretation (Drennan and
Peterson 2006) applying Liu's (2004) middle-Yellow/Wei-River clustering model to
Hongshan: clustering of smaller sites around one or two large ritual centers is read as
evidence of political integration transcending the individual village — an attributed
interpretation, not a settled finding, paralleling the "unclear...regional-level
sociopolitical organization" caveat Liu & Chen themselves raise above.

## Relations and Successions

Hongshan `preceded_by` the Zhaobaogou culture (ca. 5200–4500 BCE), itself preceded by
[[xinglongwa-culture]]; Zhaobaogou is not yet a standalone wiki page. Hongshan
`followed_by` the Xiaoheyan culture (ca. 3000–2200 BCE), which shows population
decline, a shift from agriculture toward pastoralism, more mobile residence,
abandonment of ritual sites, and cessation of public architecture and jade
manufacture — also not yet a standalone page. Hongshan is `analogous_to`
[[dawenkou-culture]] and the Daxi-culture elite-jade-ritual pattern (per Liu, X.
2004), and is `evidenced_by` [[niuheliang]].

**On collapse and climate.** Multiple paleoclimate studies (An, Z. et al. 2000;
Schettler et al. 2006) date a summer-monsoon weakening or minimum to ca. 2950 BCE
(Lake Sihailongwan sediments), and several researchers (Jin, G. 2004; Li, X. 2008)
link Hongshan's collapse to this drought episode (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Following
the Evidence-Class Framework's climate rule, this proxy synchronicity licenses at most
`contributed_to`, not `caused_by`: Song Yuqin (2002) argues excessive farming caused
desertification in this fragile landscape, contributing to agricultural decline, and
Li Xinwei (2008) argues collapse was partly attributable to an elite-response
mechanism — rather than developing agricultural technology to cope with environmental
pressure, elites intensified investment in ritual architecture and jade production, a
response Liu & Chen characterize as "desperate measures taken against environmental
uncertainties, signaling the eve of Hongshan complex society's final collapse."
This is an attributed causal thesis naming a stated social mechanism (elite ritual
overinvestment displacing adaptive agricultural response), not a wiki-voice verdict,
and climate alone is not treated as sole cause.

## Identity Cautions

Hongshan is an etic archaeological-culture construct defined by settlement pattern,
mortuary practice, and jade craft distribution. It is not equated by Liu & Chen with a
language, ethnic group, or genetic population. Claims linking Hongshan material
culture to later peoples or language families are not made in this source and should
not be inferred.

## Historiography

Whether Hongshan communities were agriculturalists remains debated even within the
"consensus" view Liu & Chen describe; the authors' own broad-spectrum-subsistence
reading is presented as a qualified revision. The collapse-by-climate thesis is an
active, multi-author explanatory program rather than a single settled account. See
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source discussion; [[niuheliang]]
carries the fullest single-site treatment of Hongshan ceremonial architecture.
