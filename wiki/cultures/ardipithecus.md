## Definition and Construct History

*Ardipithecus* is a late-Miocene–early-Pliocene fossil genus known from the **Middle Awash** region of northern Ethiopia, treated in [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012|Tattersall, *Masters of the Planet* (2012)]] as the best-skeletonized among the “earliest hominid” contenders — and also as one of the most anatomically problematic.

Two species appear in Tattersall’s account:

| Species | Horizon (as of Tattersall 2012) | Material character |
|---|---|---|
| *Ardipithecus kadabba* | ~5.8–5.2 Ma, nearby localities | Miscellaneous materials; association **less secure** than [[orrorin]] |
| *Ardipithecus ramidus* | 4.4 Ma deposits at **Aramis** | Fragments reported **1994**; nearly complete crushed skeleton published **2009** after >12 years of restoration (“**Ardi**”) |

*Ar. kadabba* (named 2001): canines nearly rivaling female chimpanzees though less pointy; **thin** molar enamel; a **5.2 Ma toe bone** strongly curved but articulating like later hominids (taken as bipedality evidence); upper limb more apelike; wooded environment (as of Tattersall 2012).

*Ar. ramidus* is the species for which Tattersall works through the skeleton claim by claim (below). His overall phylogenetic position: **if Ardi is a hominid**, it is **not** on the direct line of later hominids — “a late representative of an early branch off the hominid tree,” anatomically bizarre relative to better progenitor candidates slightly later (attributed thesis, Tattersall 2012). He further argues that *Australopithecus anamensis* **convincingly sidelines** the only slightly earlier *Ar. ramidus* as a potential **direct ancestor** of later hominids (as of Tattersall 2012; see [[australopithecus]]).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Cranium and dentition (*Ar. ramidus*, as of Tattersall 2012).** Virtual skull reconstruction: braincase **300–350 cc** (~chimpanzee size); body bulk ~small chimp, ~**110 lb**. Essentially **apelike skull proportions** despite some facial reduction. Modest molars with enamel **thicker** than the original fragmentary sample; canines **much smaller** than *Ar. kadabba*; **no premolar honing**. Foramen magnum **somewhat forward/downward**.

**Forelimb.** Arm and hand **highly arboreal climbing-adapted**; **lack knuckle-walking features** of chimpanzees and gorillas. That absence creates a phylogenetic puzzle if the molecular tree places humans closer to chimps than to gorillas: a shared knuckle-walking last common ancestor would imply loss of knuckle-walking after the chimp–human split, with possible telltale signs expected in an early ancestor — signs Ardi does not show (as of Tattersall 2012).

**Pelvis and bipedality (contested reconstruction).** The pelvis is **badly crushed**. As reconstructed by the discovery team, shorter iliac blades and a large anterior spine suggest some upright walking. Tattersall counters that *Oreopithecus* also showed these features and that they may associate more with **habitual upright posture in trees** than with ground walking (attributed critique, Tattersall 2012).

**Foot.** Ardi’s foot is a **long, curving tree-climber foot** with a **divergent great toe** for grasping — **not** the in-line big toe of the later hominid ground-walking foot; ill-fitted for terrestrial striding. The discovery team denies suspensory tendencies despite body-weight challenges for that reading (as of Tattersall 2012).

**No genetic, linguistic, or material-culture signature** is reported for the genus in this source.

## Distribution and Chronology

Ethiopian Middle Awash / Aramis complex, ~**5.8–4.4 Ma** across the two species (as of Tattersall 2012). Wooded paleoenvironments. Tattersall notes Middle Awash teams sometimes privilege **age order** over anatomy under a linear-evolution model transforming *Ardipithecus* eventually into *H. sapiens* — a frame he rejects in favor of a bushy tree with side branches (attributed; see also his treatment of *Australopithecus garhi* and Bouri).

## Lifeways

Inference only: climbing-adapted forelimb and grasping foot point to substantial **arboreality**; dental morphology of *Ar. ramidus* (reduced canines, modest molars, no honing) is consistent with a generalized plant diet rather than a specialized hard-object or shearing complex. No tools. Not an obligate bipedal ground forager in Tattersall’s reading.

## Relations and Successions

- `associated_with` other earliest-hominid candidates: [[sahelanthropus]], [[orrorin]].
- Chronologically before definitive bipedal [[australopithecus]] (*A. anamensis* ~4.2 Ma; *A. afarensis* at [[hadar]], [[laetoli]]).
- **Tattersall’s phylogenetic position (attributed):** if hominid, then **side branch**, not direct ancestor; *A. anamensis* sidelines *Ar. ramidus* as direct progenitor of later forms.
- Helps set his broader pattern of **remarkable early hominid diversity** until *H. sapiens* is left alone (attributed).
- Knuckle-walking absence bears on models of the last common ancestor with chimpanzees ([[biological-anthropology]], [[bipedalism]]).

## Identity Cautions

*Ardipithecus* is a **morphological genus construct**, not a people, language, or genetically sampled population. “Ardi” is a specimen nickname. **Hominid status, bipedality, and ancestral role are all contested** in Tattersall’s account: crushed pelvis reconstruction, grasping foot, and arboreal forelimb undercut a simple ground-biped reading; linear Middle Awash narratives that make Ardi an ancestor of *Homo* are positions, not wiki-voice fact. Association of *Ar. kadabba* elements is less secure than for *Orrorin*’s sample (as of Tattersall 2012). Four-way non-identity enforced.

## Historiography

Fragments 1994 → skeleton 2009: a long restoration cycle that made *Ar. ramidus* a public and scientific landmark. Tattersall’s 2012 treatment is **skeptical of the discovery team’s biped/ancestor package** while granting the skeleton’s importance for documenting early diversity and non-knuckle-walking anatomy. Comparative use of *Oreopithecus* (insular Miocene form with uprightness and claimed precision grip) is his caution against treating pelvic/iliac features as exclusive ground-walking signatures.
