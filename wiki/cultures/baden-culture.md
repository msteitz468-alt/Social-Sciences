# Baden Culture

Central European late Neolithic / Copper Age (Balkan Early Bronze Age terminology) archaeological culture of the Carpathian Basin and adjacent regions, treated by [[sherratt-andrew|Andrew Sherratt]] as the classic temperate-European **settlement-and-consumption horizon** of the [[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]] (SPR). Account here is densified from *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]* (1997 essays 1981–95); absolute dates follow Sherratt’s essay horizons, not post-1997 consensus alone.

## Definition and Construct History

**Baden** is a conventional material-culture label (pottery, settlement forms, burial practices) spanning Boleráz early phases through classical Baden. Sherratt (1981/1997) groups it with **Cernavodă** and **Ezero** as an “instant transformation of culture and settlement-pattern” inside the Carpathian arc when secondary-products features and elite consumption packages arrive — the **Baden–Cernavodă–Ezero complex** (Bronze Age nomenclature in Balkan usage). The construct is **etic**: style and settlement horizons, not a self-named people.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Sherratt 1981–97, attributed):**

- **Wheeled-vehicle iconography:** four-wheeled wagon drinking-cups from **Budakalász** and **Szigetszentmárton**; early Baden wagon-vessel with ox protomes at **Radosina**; 14C horizons he cites ~3400–3100 BC for key pieces.
- **Paired draught / cattle:** double bovid burials (e.g. **Alsónémedi**); 1997 note that some pairs are cow-and-calf rather than prized draught oxen — still traction-package signals when read with wagon models.
- **Drinking services:** multi-handled liquid-serving vessels as sets; association of paired-cattle burials with drinking-sets resembling Anatolian metal vessels — “paired draught … associated with new, exotic consumption-habits” (Sherratt).
- **Settlement:** radical reorganisation relative to mature Neolithic tell/horticultural patterns on the Plain (dispersal, fortification/spacing shifts in his Hungarian synthesis).
- **Metallurgy context:** Baden sits after SE European Copper Age elite networks; continuity into later Fahlerz horizons in NW Basin readings (Sherratt 1976/1997).

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown from this source. **Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people.**

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Core:** Carpathian Basin (Hungary, adjacent Slovakia, Austria, northern Balkans).
- **Horizon logic (Sherratt 1981):** traction-complex horizon aligns major transitions — Baden in central Europe; EBA further SE; Tripole–Usatovo; NW Middle Neolithic passage-grave period; SW Chalcolithic — roughly mid/late fourth millennium BC package integration.
- **Asynchrony:** SE Europe (incl. Baden complex) more coincident secondary features; North European Plain elaborates later toward Corded Ware (~500 years lag in his framing).

## Lifeways (as evidenced / argued)

Sherratt reads Baden as plough/cart-using, livestock-intensive farming with **elite liquid consumption** (alcohol package more than milk for upper/metal vessel models after his 1997 self-correction). Livestock mobility and exchange integrate the Plain into longer Turkey–Denmark axes that later become Reinecke Bronze Age routes. Not pure pastoral nomadism — mixed secondary-products farming with new prestige media.

## Identity Cautions

- Baden pots and wagon-cups are **not** an ethnic or linguistic people.
- “Instant transformation” is Sherratt’s historical model of package impact, not proven mass migration in every micro-region.
- Balkan “Early Bronze Age” vs central European “Copper Age/late Neolithic” labels for the same horizon — periodization is regional terminology, not different processes.
- Wagon-cup = model/representation of vehicles + drinking form; do not collapse icon into everyday transport statistics.

## Historiography

Culture-historical type naming (Baden bei Wien tradition); Sherratt’s SPR reframing (1981+) makes Baden the **settlement-horizon analogue** he once wrongly sought at TRB beginnings (self-correction 1997). Whittle 1996 (below) accepts wheeled transport and secondary-products *accentuation* while reading Baden cattle-keepers as continuity of hospitality/animal metaphors—**not** a newly ranked society—and rejects single kurgan/steppe conquest as the SE “end.” Gimbutas wave models and later aDNA steppe debates (see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]) re-read Baden/Vučedol packages under different equations — keep economic-consumption and community readings separate from language/people identity claims. Post-1997 residue dairying and refined 14C sequences may retune milk timing and absolute phases without erasing the traction/consumption package thesis.

## Whittle densification (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Ch. 5. Mid-1990s cal BC; pre-aDNA. Baden = etic material-culture construct; ceramic style ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes; IE arguments kept separate from pottery maps.)*

### Alsónémedi double human–cattle burials (~3500 BC as of Whittle 1996)

Alsónémedi (SE of Budapest), Baden/Pécel cemetery ~**40** people: **grave 3** woman 30–35 + man 35–40 with two cattle (mature cow ~8 yrs + young possibly male ~1.5 yrs) facing each other; humans crouched facing each other behind animals; copper bead, divided bowl, high-handled jugs. **Grave 28:** cow ~6 yrs + calf 10–12 mo facing; man ~40 behind calf; vessel set (divided bowl, jug, cups, red-painted ladle).

**Whittle’s theory reading:** double animal burials are a new formality beyond food/token parts; cattle of long-domesticated stock used for secondary products/uses (milk, traction); mature cow + young bull as traction pair; drinking set may formalise drinking ritual; humans defined in relation to animals—possible new ownership attitudes and interwoven human/animal fertility (**attributed**; dialogues with [[sherratt-andrew]] secondary-products framing).

### Cart models and distribution

**Budakalász** Baden cemetery ~**400** graves: grave 3 cattle pair + human couple; grave **177** cenotaph with footed goblet and four-wheeled high-sided clay cart model vessel (red paint, zigzag). **Szigetszentmárton:** complete four-wheeled cart-cup model in adult grave with 7 further vessels. Boleráz phase earliest claimed models: Boglárlelle (waggon superstructure minus wheels), **Radosina** (Slovakia). Cart cup-models hailed as wheeled transport arrival in the N Carpathian basin; developed first in Mesopotamia; Baden models likely reflect **contemporary local vehicles**. Baden pottery handled jugs/cups part of a wider repertoire Carpathian basin–Bulgaria–N Greece–Aegean from ~**3500 BC**. Baden succeeds Bodrogkeresztúr/late Hunyadi by ~3500 BC across Hungary into W Romania, E Austria, S Slovakia; occupation often **pits only**.

N Hungary Center-group Baden cremation urns in large anthropomorphic form (e.g. Center near Ózd)—Whittle: harks back to much older traditions; burial-ground tradition already old by Baden, continuity with Tiszapolgár/Bodrogkeresztúr cemetery practice. Handled wares may imitate N Aegean metal vessels (e.g. Troy II); fermented-drink package hypotheses noted but Whittle already argued fermented drinks likely much earlier (Chs. 3–4).

### Secondary products as acceleration, not sole revolution

Whittle finds Sherratt’s secondary-products model attractive but stresses difficulties of a single later-4th-mill. Near Eastern package: wheeled vehicle the most convincing innovation (Uruk pictograms first half 4th mill.; Baden cart models **3500–3300 BC**—rapid diffusion); plough/ard and milk/wool have earlier claims in places; secondary products horizon = **acceleration/accentuation within changing residence—not sole cause**. Mature cattle/sheep age patterns at earlier SE sites (Ovčarovo, Golyamo Delčevo, etc.) may already show secondary uses before the 4th mill. (**position** modulating SPR).

### SE European “accents of change” and Whittle’s multi-causal thesis

Most tells abandoned (many permanently); settlements smaller/more dispersed; hilltop and cave occupations; painted pottery and copper tools replaced by plainer vessels and eventually arsenical alloying; Black Sea coast cemeteries/tells largely lapse; small mound (kurgan) burials appear as far W as the Hungarian plain. Bodrogkeresztúr between Tiszapolgár and Baden as chronological counterpart to the southern “transition”; Baden tumuli on the plain (to Szolnok) into W Romania—simple poorly furnished ochre burials, poorly dated; surveys (e.g. Dévaványa) complementary Baden and tumuli distributions. Gomolava later levels pits (Baden, Kostolac, Vučedol); Coțofeni correlates Baden/Ezero.

**Against single explanation** uniting Balkan collapse + steppe horse-rider incursions + PIE dispersal as warlike mobile takeover (**position**): change neither synchronous nor identical regionally; much change internal “structural reordering”; SE Europe influenced from outside (SW Asia as much as steppes); IE language may have spread **after** changes underway, as lingua franca of mobility/exchange, not as primary cause via mass migration; limited steppe movement more opportunistic infill than initial cause. In closing synthesis: **Baden cattle-keepers** exemplify wider contacts (wheeled vehicles) yet **older hospitality/animal-metaphor traditions—not a newly ranked society** (**position**). Alsónémedi/Budakalász keepers may not have spoken only one language; predecessors would recognise drinking vessel sets and cemetery burial—change as **inflection of existing root**, not total rupture.

Bronocice (TRB–Lublin-Volhynian–Baden sequence) is noted in later ranges for a decorated pot appearing to show a four-wheeled waggon and Baden-phase ox horncore suggesting traction—associated_with wheeled transport evidence, not Baden ethnicity.

### Identity cautions (Whittle layer)

Archaeological cultures (Vinča, Baden, Sredny Stog, Yamnaya, Cucuteni–Tripolye) treated as material-culture constructs; ceramic style ≠ political tribe; horse/waggon packages not equated with ethnic “peoples” without argument.
