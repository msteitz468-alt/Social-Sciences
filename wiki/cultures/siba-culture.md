# Siba Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Siba culture is a Bronze Age archaeological construct of the Hexi corridor (Gansu), radiocarbon
-dated ca. 1900–1500 BCE and largely contemporary with [[qijia-culture|Qijia]]. Only a handful of
sites have been excavated — Huoshaogou (Yumen), Donghuishan (Minle), and Ganguya (Jiuquan) — all
burial sites; the residential settlement pattern is poorly known (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Typological analysis suggests Siba pottery derived mainly from the Machang variant
of Majiayao culture via an intermediate phase, with additional influence from Qijia to the east and
interaction with the later Kayue culture (ca. 1600–700 BCE) to the southeast. Siba metal
composition (mainly tin bronze and arsenical copper/bronze) differs from Qijia's (mainly pure
copper/tin bronze) but resembles that of Tianshanbeilu in eastern Xinjiang; arsenical-copper
technology is associated with the Urals and tin bronze with the Altai/eastern Kazakhstan region,
evidence cited (Mei 2003) for Siba (together with the Xinjiang sites) having played an intermediary
role linking the Eurasian steppe metallurgical tradition to Qijia (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

**Linguistic.** No linguistic data reported; `language_affiliation: [[unknown]]`. Some scholarship
has associated Siba-region populations with the historically-attested Qiang people — this is an
**ethnographic/historical analogy**, not a demonstrated linguistic or ethnic identity, and is
flagged here rather than asserted; the source does not itself make this equation directly, and no
independent evidence class in this source (material, genetic) tests it.

**Genetic.** A physical-anthropological study of Donghuishan/Huoshaogou remains (Zhu 1998) found a
Mongoloid population closest to East Asian types, consistent with the Qijia physical type (as of
Liu & Chen 2012) — an `associated_with` relationship between skeletal samples, not an equation of
cultures or peoples.

## Distribution and Chronology

Concentrated in the Hexi corridor of Gansu, overlapping geographically and temporally with Qijia to
the east. One arsenic-bronze item recovered at [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] points to contact
between Erlitou and Siba territory in Gansu (as of Liu & Chen 2012), evidence of long-distance metal
exchange networks reaching the Central Plain.

## Lifeways

Evidenced almost entirely from burial contexts: bronze/arsenical-copper metalworking, mortuary
practice; residential/subsistence evidence is not well documented in the excavated record (as of
Liu & Chen 2012).

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by`: Machang-variant Majiayao (ceramic ancestry, via an intermediate phase).
`associated_with` [[qijia-culture|Qijia]] (contemporary, ceramic and metallurgical influence both
ways) and Tianshanbeilu (metal-composition resemblance, positioning Siba as a link in the
steppe-to-Qijia metal-transmission chain). `succeeded_by`/overlaps with the Kayue culture to the
southeast.

## Identity Cautions

Siba is an etic archaeological construct defined from a small burial-site sample; no linguistic or
firsthand ethnic-identity evidence exists for its population. Any association with the later,
historically-documented Qiang people is an analogy only, not asserted by this source and not to be
read as an identity claim — the four-way non-identity rule (culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population
≠ named people) applies in full: the physical-anthropological data speak only to skeletal
population affinity, not to language or ethnonym.

## Historiography

Defined through mid-to-late-20th-century Chinese excavation of a small number of Gansu Hexi-corridor
burial sites; Liu & Chen (2012) synthesize the ceramic-typological and metal-compositional evidence
positioning Siba within the broader Northern Zone/Eurasian-steppe metal-exchange network that also
included Qijia, Zhukaigou, and Lower Xiajiadian.
