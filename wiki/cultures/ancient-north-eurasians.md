## Definition and Construct History

The Ancient North Eurasians (ANE) began as a [[ghost-population]] — a lineage inferred by Reich's team from statistical mixture-modeling of living populations' genomes before any matching ancient skeleton was known. Reich proposed that a population existed more than 15,000 years ago in northern Eurasia, some of whose descendants migrated east (contributing to Native Americans via Beringia) and some west (contributing to Europeans) (as of Reich 2018). The inference was confirmed in late 2013 when Eske Willerslev and colleagues published genome-wide data from a boy who had lived at the Mal'ta site in south-central Siberia around 24,000 years ago; his genome matched the predicted population and became its "type specimen." Reich states that, judged by the standards contemporary race-thinking applies to living continental populations, the Ancient North Eurasians would "without a doubt" have been classified as a distinct race had they lived today (attributed, Reich 2018) — a claim made to illustrate the instability of race categories under deep-time genetic analysis, not to endorse the category.

## Genetic Signature

Analysis of the Mal'ta genome indicated Native Americans derive about one-third of their ancestry from Ancient North Eurasians, with the remainder from East Asian-related sources (as of Reich 2018). Iosif Lazaridis, working in Reich's lab, used the Mal'ta genome to resolve a Four Population Test anomaly and identified a further, still-deeper ghost lineage he named "Basal Eurasian" — the deepest split among lineages contributing to non-Africans. The earliest known example of the "classic" European blond-hair mutation was found in an Ancient North Eurasian individual from the Lake Baikal region, dated to roughly 17,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Reich states that serial-founder models predicting minimally mixed post-establishment population structure are rendered untenable by ancient DNA: much of today's population structure was forged recently, within roughly the last 5,000 years, through mixture events in which Ancient North Eurasian ancestry was a repeated contributor to both Europeans and Native Americans.

## Distribution and Chronology

Reich's team places the Ancient North Eurasians on the lineage leading to West Eurasians despite the population's Mal'ta type specimen having lived geographically closer to East Asia; Reich links this position to the geographic distribution of Upper Paleolithic stone-tool technology (attributed, Reich 2018). More than half of the world's population today carries between 5 and 40 percent Ancient North Eurasian ancestry, reflecting the population's dual eastward (into the Americas) and westward (into Europe) contribution.

## Lifeways

Lifeways are evidenced only indirectly, through the Mal'ta boy's burial context in south-central Siberia around 24,000 years ago; no broader material-culture assemblage is treated here as diagnostically "Ancient North Eurasian" in the way an archaeological culture would be defined by artifact typology.

## Relations and Successions

The Ancient North Eurasians contributed ancestry to both [[western-hunter-gatherers]] (via later mixture on the western end of their range) and, further east, to the ancestors of Native Americans; they are also linked to the ancestry mixture that later formed steppe pastoralist populations `associated_with` [[yamnaya-culture]]. The population is `descends_from` no currently identified ancestral population in the source material and is treated as a reconstructed ghost lineage whose only direct genetic sampling point is the Mal'ta individual.

## Identity Cautions

"Ancient North Eurasian" is a statistically reconstructed genetic population — a [[ghost-population]] inferred from admixture patterns in living and ancient genomes before being anchored by a single ~24,000-year-old individual. It is not a documented archaeological culture with an artifact assemblage, nor a linguistic community, nor a self-conscious people; the "type specimen" language borrowed from archaeology should not be read as implying the Mal'ta boy represents a demographically or culturally uniform population. Reich's remark that the population would have been called a "race" is an illustration of the fluidity of racial categories over deep time, explicitly not a claim that the ANE were a bounded, self-identifying human group.

## Historiography

The Ancient North Eurasians are a rare case in which a ghost population predicted from statistical genetics was subsequently confirmed by a matching ancient genome (Willerslev's 2013 Mal'ta publication), rather than the reverse order in which an ancient sample is sequenced first and its genetic affinities worked out afterward. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]] for the full account.
