# Jōmon Culture

Japanese postglacial archaeological culture named for **cord-marked** (*jōmon*) pottery — world’s earliest pottery tradition in Mithen’s account — combining sedentary villages with wild woodland and coastal resources for millennia without full rice agriculture. Account follows [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]].

## Definition and Construct History

*Jōmon* means “cord-marks,” the decoration technique on pottery. Edward S. Morse’s 1877 Omori shell-midden excavation trained Japanese students and launched recognition of Jōmon pottery, dwellings, and artefacts. Six cultural phases run from **Incipient** through **Final** Jōmon, ending ~500 BC when rice farming arrives at scale. Natsushima provided the first radiocarbon dates for Japanese prehistory (~9000 BC). The construct is **etic** and pottery-defined; it is a textbook case of [[complex-hunter-gatherers]].

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Mithen 2003):**

- **Pottery:** earliest in the world — Fukui Rockshelter shards at 13,000 BC; Odaiyamamoto residues 14,500 BC (challenging acorn-storage-only invention theories because Honshu then had only sparse pine/birch); later ornate sculptural pottery after ~3000 BC.
- **Other precocities:** ground axes; lacquer (Kakinoshima comb 7000 BC).
- **Settlement:** Initial Jōmon sedentism from ~9500 BC — substantial huts, grinding stones, acorn storage pits (Uenohara ~9200 BC narrative); egalitarian dwelling similarity.
- **Subsistence tech:** thousands of oval boar/deer pit-traps (Tama Hills 10,000 pits / 30 km²); Early baited pits, Later linear drive arrangements; shell middens becoming huge horseshoe mounds; migratory salmon as possible complexity underwriter (NW Coast analogy — attributed).
- **“Wild gardening”:** forest management/cultivation intensifies at apogee ~3000 BC without replacing wild-food dependence until rice.

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown securely. Mithen reports beliefs that Ainu are cultural and biological descendants of Jōmon and that most modern Japanese descend from rice-bringing immigrants from China/Korea — **positions as of 2003**, not four-way identity claims.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Geography:** Japanese archipelago; colder north (northern Honshu, Hokkaido) retains Jōmon lifeways longer because rice grows poorly.
- **Phases:** Incipient → Initial (~9500 BC sedentism) → … → Final ends ~500 BC.
- **Environment:** Holocene warmth/rainfall enables Initial sedentary villages (Jericho parallel for timing, not economy).

## Lifeways (Settlement and Subsistence)

Year-round or multi-season villages on **wild** woodland, coastal, and ocean resources; extensive pottery use; storage; trap hunting; later ornate ceramics and midden monumentalization. Mithen stresses the contrast with Jordan valley: Jōmon remained entirely reliant on wild foods while using pottery extensively. Rice farming ~500 BC replaces the lifestyle at scale in suitable climates.

## Relations and Successions

- **Parallel to:** other complex foragers (Ertebølle, Natufian, NW Coast analogies).
- **Adjacent to:** Yangtze [[rice-domestication]] world for thousands of years before adoption.
- **Succeeded by:** rice-farming economy of continental derivation (as of Mithen); northern persistence.
- **Not identity with:** modern Japanese nation or Ainu without explicit, attributed biological/cultural arguments.

## Significance

Jōmon is the premier demonstration that **pottery, sedentism, and social complexity do not require agriculture**, and that the world’s earliest ceramics need not track cereal farming. It anchors comparative [[complex-hunter-gatherers]] theory and East Asian Holocene sequences.

## Identity Cautions

- Cord-marked pottery tradition ≠ single ethnicity, language, or gene pool across 10,000+ years.
- Phase labels are archaeological, not emic.
- Ainu–Jōmon and immigrant–Yayoi narratives are historically and politically loaded — record as positions with dates.
- Four-way non-identity enforced.

## Historiography

Morse 1877; Japanese institutional archaeology and development-led excavation (Imamura Kirigaoka traps); radiocarbon revolution (Natsushima); deep pottery dates continually pushed earlier (Odaiyamamoto 1999). Mithen 2003 popularizes the complex-forager reading for a global audience; post-2003 aDNA has transformed ancestry debates — update from later sources with date-stamps.
