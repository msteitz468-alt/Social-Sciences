## Definition and Construct History

This page describes "San"/"South African Forager" ancestry as a genetic-lineage construct discussed in David Reich's [[reich-who-we-are-2018|Who We Are and How We Got Here]] (2018) — it is **not** an ethnographic or unified-people page, and it does not describe a single self-conscious group. Reich's account draws on genome-wide comparisons among southern and eastern African hunter-gatherer populations, including present-day San groups and ancient DNA from southern Africa.

San hunter-gatherers of southern Africa were long assumed — a "commonly held view" in Reich's phrasing — to derive from a lineage that branched off before all other modern human lineages separated from one another (as of Reich 2018). Reich reports that this view has been complicated by subsequent findings (Pontus Skoglund and colleagues): the San share more mutations with eastern and central African hunter-gatherer populations than with West African populations such as the Yoruba, suggesting that West Africans in fact carry more ancestry from an early-splitting lineage than non-Africans generally do — a finding that revises rather than simply confirms the "San as earliest-branching" narrative (as of Reich 2018).

## Genetic Signature

Reich's account states that some pairs of African populations — San hunter-gatherers and Yoruba are the example given — have been isolated from one another longer, as measured by mutation density between their genomes, than any pair of populations found outside Africa; divergence times for some sub-Saharan African population pairs are reported on the order of up to roughly 200,000 years, reflecting Africa's much greater time-depth of population structure relative to the rest of the world (as of Reich 2018). San/"South African Forager" ancestry today is largely restricted to southernmost Africa and constitutes nearly all of the ancestry of present-day San foraging populations (as of Reich 2018).

Ancient genomes from Malawi, dated to roughly 8,100–2,500 years ago, show a population carrying about two-thirds South African Forager-related ancestry — evidence that this ancestry lineage was once distributed far more broadly across the continent than its present southernmost-Africa restriction would suggest (as of Reich 2018).

## Distribution and Chronology

Present-day distribution is confined to southernmost Africa. Reich attributes the contraction of this lineage's range to at least four later agricultural/language expansions across Africa over the last few thousand years — [[bantu-expansion|Bantu]], Nilo-Saharan, Afroasiatic, and Khoe-Kwadi — which he describes as having "thrown a veil" over deeper African population history by overlaying and absorbing older forager populations across most of the continent (as of Reich 2018).

## Lifeways

This page addresses only the genetic-ancestry construct as reported by Reich; it does not attempt an ethnographic account of San social organization, subsistence, or belief, which belongs on society-layer pages outside the scope of this genetic source and are not created in this ingest.

## Identity Cautions

"San"/"Khoe-San"/"Bushmen" are contested ethnonyms in the anthropological literature with their own naming politics, not addressed by this genetic-ancestry source. As used on this page, "San"/"South African Forager" refers strictly to a genetically defined ancestry lineage identified through mutation-sharing comparisons in Reich's account — it should not be read as a claim about a single self-conscious people, a shared culture, or a shared language across all populations carrying this ancestry signature. Deep genetic divergence is a population-genetic fact about ancestry lineages; it does not by itself imply cultural, linguistic, or political unity, either historically or today. `construct_status: contested` reflects both the revision of the "earliest-branching San" narrative described above and the unresolved question of how far this ancestry lineage's original range extended.

## Historiography

The "San as earliest human lineage" framing common in earlier population-genetics literature has been revised by later work (Skoglund and colleagues, cited in Reich 2018) showing that other African populations, including West Africans, also carry substantial ancestry from early-diverging lineages — a shift Reich presents as part of a broader pattern of "genetic sharing" among structured ancient African populations replacing simpler branching-tree narratives (as of Reich 2018).
