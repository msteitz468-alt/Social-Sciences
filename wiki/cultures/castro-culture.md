# Castro Culture

Fortified-settlement (castro) culture of northwest Iberia (Galicia, northern
Portugal, Asturias).

## Definition and Construct History

"Castro culture" (from *castro*, Portuguese/Galician for a defended hilltop settlement) names the archaeological horizon of thousands of fortified hilltop settlements densest in Galicia and spanning from Coimbra to Santander. Cunliffe (2001) divides its occupation into three phases: (1) c. 1000–c. 500 BCE, covering the end of the region's widespread Atlantic Bronze Age maritime exchange as emerging Phoenician/Punic trade contacts begin; (2) c. 500–c. 140 BCE, a period of intense regional cultural development that absorbs Punic and Celtiberian influence while remaining culturally distinct; (3) from 138 BCE, Roman military conquest and provincialization (Decimus Brutus's campaign 138–136 BCE; Caesar's expedition 61–59 BCE capturing Brigantium/La Coruña; conquest completed under Augustus's Cantabrian Wars 29–19 BCE). The construct is defined by the shared settlement form (fortified hilltop enclosure) and associated material culture across a large region rather than by a single type-site; `construct_status: conventional` reflects that it is a recognized regional archaeological unit, though Cunliffe (2001) treats its internal social coherence — not just its shared architecture — as itself an interpretive claim.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material:** Castros share hilltop location, multiple defensive walls, densely ordered internal settlement, and (in the pre-Roman phase) circular houses. Major excavated examples include Citânia de Briteiros, Citânia de Sanfins, and Sabroso (near Guimarães, northern Portugal), Monte Tecla (near Pontevedra, Galicia), and the smaller Castro de Baroña. Distinctive material culture includes elite gold ornaments (bracelets, earrings, torcs) whose regionally varying styles Cunliffe (2001) reads as marking distinct ethnic/social groups — the torc, on his interpretation, functioned "to define membership of a discrete social entity," with its value resting in social restrictions on use as much as in metal or craftsmanship; stamped/incised pottery; verraco stone quadrupeds (probable territorial/pastoral markers); monumental warrior statues (short-sleeved shirt, kilt, circular shield, dagger); and subterranean structures interpreted, with a range of proposed functions (ritual/medical use, warrior initiation), as sauna baths — Strabo (*Geog.* 3.3.6) describes peoples near the Duro living "after the manner of the Laconians," using anointing-rooms twice daily and vapour baths from heated stones, possibly referring to these structures. Maritime trade with the south is attested by scattered Greek and Ibero-Punic coins, Punic and Greek pottery, and rare glass, though volume was slight and penetrated little inland; Cunliffe (2001) suggests perishable goods (fabrics, perfumes, spices) may account for an archaeologically invisible bulk of exchange. Distinctive fibulae with upturned, disc-ended feet link north-western Iberia and Aquitania, with rare examples (possibly local copies) reaching Mount Batten (Devon) and Harlyn Bay (Cornwall) — Cunliffe (2001) speculates, as an explicitly hedged suggestion, that such long-distance exchanges of a dress-fastening type might have involved the movement of brides.

**Linguistic:** Cunliffe (2001) suggests the Atlantic-facing west and north coast of Iberia, of which the castro zone is part, developed "quite possibly, from an early date," a version of the "Celtic" language, in contrast with the distinct "Iberian" culture and language of the peninsula's Mediterranean-facing south/east coast. This is an attributed interpretive claim, part of the broader contested Celticity debate over Atlantic language spread; see [[celticity-debate]]. No linguistic evidence proper to the castro region specifically (inscriptions, glossed names) is extracted here beyond this general claim.

**Genetic:** Not defined; *Facing the Ocean* (2001) is a pre-aDNA source and makes no genetic-population claims for the castro region.

## Distribution and Chronology

The castro zone covers north-western Iberia (Galicia, northern Portugal, Asturias), with settlement density peaking in Galicia. Under Rome, hilltop castro settlements did not disappear but evolved into planned native towns: Citânia de Sanfins (Portugal), over 15 hectares with a regular street grid, comprised insulae subdivided into roughly 200–300 m² plots housing extended-family units; assuming 10–20 people per family, Cunliffe (2001) estimates the settlement's population could have reached roughly 3,000. Citânia de Briteiros had a large circular meeting-house (11 m diameter) with stone benches, possibly for an assembly of elders, and remained occupied into the late 4th century CE. The Roman port of Flavium Brigantium developed at La Coruña on a natural harbour; the nearby castro of Elviña was occupied from the 3rd century BCE to the 1st century CE, and Greco-Italian amphorae found there (2nd–1st century BCE) show long-distance trade predated the Roman conquest. The Roman lighthouse Torre de Hércules, built on the same promontory, survives incorporated into the present-day structure.

Roman gold mining at Las Médulas (Asturias) — described by Pliny the Elder, procurator of Citerior province c. 72–74 CE, and archaeologically attested via hydraulic *ruina montium* mining (tunnelling beneath alluvial gold-bearing terraces, then flushing with reservoir/aqueduct water through heather-lined channels to trap gold flakes, with over 230 individual mining sites identified by survey) — used many technical terms that, per Cunliffe (2001), "are evidently native words reflecting a continuity of tradition which must go back to Bronze Age times, if not before," implying substantial continuity of indigenous mining knowledge across the Roman conquest.

## Lifeways

Beyond settlement and metalworking, the castro region shows strong evidence of persisting pre-Roman lineage-based social organization well into the imperial period: over 300 inscriptions naming clans (*gentes*, *gentilitates*) in north-west Iberia, together with the persistence of *hospitium* (formal inter-community hospitality agreements recorded on inscribed tesserae, e.g. from Asturica Augusta, 27 CE) into the 2nd–3rd centuries CE, are read by Cunliffe (2001) as "a firm indication that pre-Roman social systems and traditions remained strong centuries after the conquest" — he further argues the very urban plan of Citânia de Sanfins reflects this lineage system.

`succeeded_by`: direct Roman military conquest and provincial administration (Decimus Brutus 138–136 BCE; Caesar's expedition 61–59 BCE; completed under Augustus's Cantabrian Wars 29–19 BCE), though — per the Lifeways/Historiography sections above — indigenous social structure persisted well past formal conquest rather than being replaced by it.

## Identity Cautions

"Castro culture" names a shared settlement form (the fortified hilltop enclosure) and associated material assemblage across a large and internally diverse region — it does not denote a single self-conscious people, a single language, or a genetically defined population. Cunliffe (2001) explicitly frames an interpretive question that the wiki records as open rather than resolved: whether the persistence of native social structures and settlement forms under Rome reflects deliberate rejection of romanization or simply the passive effect of remoteness from Roman administrative interest. He leans toward deliberate rejection in a comparable British case (the Dumnonii of south-west Britain — "looks far more like the deliberate rejection of Roman values by the tribe") but explicitly calls the Iberian case "unclear," so no wiki-voice conclusion is drawn here. The regional variation in torc styles that Cunliffe (2001) reads as marking distinct ethnic/social groups is his interpretation of an artefact-style pattern, not direct textual or genetic evidence of separate peoples — an `associated_with`, not an identity claim.

## Historiography

Cunliffe (2001) explicitly rejects a "backwardness born of geographical isolation" reading of the castro region's persistence of indigenous social structure through two centuries of Roman contact, arguing instead — as an interpretive claim, not settled fact — that the evidence better supports "internal vigour," with physical/oceanic remoteness perhaps reinforcing rather than causing a deliberate cultural distinctiveness. This is a revisionist move against an older diffusionist/culture-historical framing that would treat persistence of "primitive" material culture as evidence of isolation-driven stagnation; Cunliffe's reading instead credits agency to the castro communities themselves. Evidence quality is uneven: excavated settlement sequences (Citânia de Briteiros, Citânia de Sanfins) offer good structural and chronological control, while much of the elite-status interpretation (torc symbolism, sauna-bath function, gentilitas inscriptions as evidence of lineage continuity) rests on inference from artefact distribution and epigraphy rather than excavated behavioural contexts.
