# Inca Empire

## Definition and Construct History

**Tawantinsuyu** ("The Four Parts Together") was the Inca imperial polity at its early-16th-century height — by Moseley's synthesis the largest native state in the Western Hemisphere and the largest ancient empire south of the equator ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], attributed). [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (2002) likewise treats it as the largest polity ever created in the native Americas and defines *empire* as an extensive core polity controlling a range of other societies — political, military, or economic dominion that may be remote or immediate ([[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]], attributed).

The term **Inca** properly names a small closed ethnic nobility who ruled as governing elite. Moseley puts the kindred on the order of fewer than ~40,000 over subject populations on the order of ten million; D'Altroy's expansion sketch has a society of roughly ~100,000 asserting rule over ~10–12 million in a few generations after regional dominance around AD 1400 (both attributed; demography is order-of-magnitude, not census). Subjects were *not* "Incas." The empire amalgamated on the order of eighty or more provinces from conquered ethnic polities; ethnic separatism made conquest relatively easy and consolidation hard (Moseley; D'Altroy).

Radiocarbon and ceramic work place crystallization of distinctively imperial Inca styles shortly after AD 1375 (as of Scarre ed. 2013); local Cuzco-basin origins reach into the post-[[wari-culture|Wari]] landscape. D'Altroy stresses that pre-imperial (Killke) society is poorly known because royal narratives became grand epics and imperial building erased earlier settlements; several hundred Killke sites within ~60 km of Cuzco form a settlement hierarchy from ~AD 1000–1200, with relatively low defensive posture in the basin core (as of D'Altroy 2002). Ethnohistoric dynastic lore (Pacariqtambo origins, Pachakuti-generation expansion) is ideological charter as well as historical claim — mythic and archaeological timelines both recorded, not adjudicated.

D'Altroy surveys the extreme malleability of Tawantinsuyu in modern and colonial characterizations — benevolent monarchy, primitive communism, feudal society, Asiatic despotism, territorial empire — and notes that some modern scholars doubt an "empire" existed at all, seeing only an ethnic patchwork (position recording; not wiki-voice verdict).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Imperial masonry: ashlar (rectangular, mortarless freestanding walls) and polygonal (terraces, solid masses); D'Altroy synthesizes craft studies (Protzen, Lee) on pounding and scribe-and-cope fitting. [[cuzco]] as sacred-administrative navel; Qorikancha ("Golden Enclosure"/Temple of the Sun) as Inti cult focus; [[saqsawaman|Saqsawaman]] as cyclopean ceremonial-fortress complex above the city. Road network on the order of ~40,000 km with *tampu* and *chaski* relays (D'Altroy citing Hyslop; Cieza); thousands of provincial installations; *qollqa* storehouses as staple-finance infrastructure. Royal estates ([[machu-picchu]], [[ollantaytambo]], Pisac, Yucay, Chinchero) as country seats and productive holdings of rulers and [[panaqa]], not "last refuges." *Mallki* (ancestor mummies) retained political personhood; [[panaqa]] managed estates of deceased rulers under [[split-inheritance]].

**Linguistic.** Quechua (Runa Simi) as imposed administrative lingua franca across scores of native languages (Moseley 2001; D'Altroy 2002). Four-way non-identity: Quechua spread ≠ single people ≠ genetic population ≠ "Inca" ethnicity.

**Genetic.** Not addressed in ingested Moseley/Scarre/D'Altroy material — [[unknown]].

## Distribution and Chronology

Four *suyu* radiated from Cuzco: Chinchaysuyu (north — most populous, most provinces), Antisuyu (eastern montaña; limited jungle penetration), Kollasuyu/Collasuyu (south — Titicaca, altiplano, Chile/Argentina highlands), Cuntisuyu (southwest; smallest). Late Horizon conventionally AD 1476–1533 in Rowe-derived schemes ([[andean-chronology]]); Moseley and D'Altroy both emphasize longer local Inca development before rapid expansion. D'Altroy notes Cabello's conventional sequence (roughly 1438–1526 for the imperial phase) may be late relative to radiocarbon that pulls some expansion into the earlier fifteenth century (as of D'Altroy 2002 — contested chronology).

**Expansion narrative (ethnohistory, D'Altroy 2002 composite):** after Wari/Tiwanaku decline, Incas among several southern highland groups; regional dominance ~1400; imperial launch associated with Pachakuti (Inka Yupanki) after the Chanka war cycle (authenticity debated — Duviols and others skeptical); Thupa Inka Yupanki and Wayna Qhapaq continue expansion; Chimor ([[chimu-culture]]) falls in the Thupa generation; final dynastic war between Waskhar and Atawallpa. D'Altroy presents Pärssinen-style phase maps as guides, not gospel; earliest imperial stages may never be authoritatively resolved from sagas alone.

**Spanish conquest sequence (D'Altroy 2002 / Moseley 2001, attributed):** Pizarro's force (D'Altroy: 168 at [[cajamarca]]) seized Atawallpa 15–16 November 1532 amid an army reported at tens of thousands; ransom over ~eight months; execution 26 July 1533; Spanish–native alliances (e.g., Wanka/Xauxa) and entry to Cuzco 15 November 1533; Mango Inka co-rule then revolt; 1536 sieges; neo-Inca Vilcabamba resistance ~36 years until Thupa Amaru's death 24 September 1572. D'Altroy frames early Spanish military success as multi-causal (arms, horses, tactics, alliances, faction, bravado) — **not** disease alone as the explanation of battlefield outcomes; colonial demographic collapse (~half population in Cook's estimates as cited) combines pestilence with war and forced labor.

## Lifeways

**Folk base scaled to empire** (Moseley's "Inca model of statecraft"; D'Altroy Ch. 8–11): [[ayllu]] corporate kin communities; [[dual-organization|hanan/hurin]] moieties and *saya* partitions; reciprocity (*ayni*, *minka*; [[mita-labor|mit'a]]); ancestor and [[huaca]] cult; *kuraka* hereditary lords. Imperial instruments: decimal administration (*chunka* 10 → *hunu* 10,000 household units — ideal, unevenly applied; absent as regular native hierarchy in far Chile/Argentina per D'Altroy); three labor axes (agricultural production for state/Sun, mit'a service, textile obligations); staple finance via *qollqa* vs wealth finance (fine cloth/*qompi*, metals); [[mitmaq]] resettlement; specialized statuses [[yanakuna]] (lifelong retainers) and [[aqllakuna]] (Chosen Women); elite education/hostage of conquered heirs at Cuzco; roads and *chaski*. Economy without money: labor and cloth as media of obligation. [[khipu]] for census and accounts (*khipu kamayuq*).

**Politics of blood (D'Altroy Ch. 5):** Sapa Inca as living deity and government head; royal women (*qoya*); mummies and *wawqi* statues in council and ceremony; ten royal [[panaqa]] at conquest under [[split-inheritance]] (new ruler takes office; deceased's estates stay with his descent corporation — theory and practice both contested in the literature D'Altroy reviews). Alternative models of government (monarchy, dual rule, diarchy, "snapshot" of factional balance) recorded as positions.

**Ideology (D'Altroy Ch. 7):** Inti/Sun, Wiraqocha, Thunder, Moon, Pachamama and regional oracles (e.g., [[pachacamac]]); lunar ritual calendar; [[zeqe-system|zeq'e (ceque) system]] of ~400+ *wak'a* on radiating lines from Cuzco (Polo; Bauer); [[qhapaq-ucha]] state pilgrimage/sacrifice including high-peak archaeology (Ampato, Llullaillaco — as of D'Altroy 2002).

**Provincial rule (D'Altroy Ch. 10–11):** mixed strategy — more direct/territorial in the central highlands, more hegemonic/indirect on much of the north coast and far north/south; umbrella of Inca supervisors over hereditary ethnic lords, not a fully autonomous bureaucracy; [[huanuco-pampa|Huánuco Pampa]] as excavated provincial-center exemplar (plaza, *kallanka*, *usnu*, storehouses). Comparative frame: hegemonic ↔ territorial continuum (Luttwak/Hassig vocabulary applied in D'Altroy 1992/2002); Incas as counter-example to "complex core / simple periphery" (conquered Chimor more urbanized than Cuzco heartland in key respects).

Ecological frame (Moseley dual trajectory, still useful backdrop): **Arid Montane** agropastoral verticality ([[vertical-archipelago]]) climaxing in Inca Cuzco vs **Maritime-Oasis** coastal trajectory climaxing in Chimor/[[chan-chan|Chan Chan]] before conquest.

## Relations and Successions

Incorporated [[chimu-culture|Chimor]] and earlier coastal and sierra polities; reused Tiwanaku prestige in Kollasuyu ideology (attributed). Preceded in the Cuzco basin by Killke/Lucre phases and broader post-Wari fragmentation. Spanish conquest ends independent rule; colonial *mita* and *reducción* transform Inca labor and settlement drafts; Taki Onqoy and Inkarri myth mark colonial afterlives (D'Altroy Ch. 13, attributed).

## Identity Cautions

"Inca empire" is a political-administrative and material-culture construct. *Inca* ≠ all subjects; Quechua lingua franca ≠ ethnolinguistic homogenization; imperial style ≠ single genetic population. Archaeological "Inca" horizons and ethnohistoric "Tawantinsuyu" must be linked with `associated_with` arguments, not silent identity. Four *suyu* names are administrative geography, not ethnic essences. Canonical wiki slug: **`inca-empire`** (also linked historically as "Inka Empire" in some textbook pages — same polity).

## Historiography

Classic chronicles (Cieza de León, Cobo, Guaman Poma, Betanzos, Sarmiento, Sancho, Pedro Pizarro) vs archaeological revision (Rowe horizon scheme; Bauer/Covey on early Inca and zeq'e; Morris on Huánuco Pampa; D'Altroy on provincial power and comparative empire). D'Altroy (2002) is the major single-author ethnohistoric–archaeological synthesis densifying this page beyond Moseley: empire theory (hegemonic–territorial; staple finance), Cuzco politics ([[panaqa]], [[split-inheritance]]), ideology ([[zeqe-system]], [[qhapaq-ucha]]), social hierarchy ([[aqllakuna]], [[yanakuna]]), militarism, provincial administration, and invasion aftermath. Method stance: balance elite and subject perspectives; treat history and archaeology as complementary; keep saga limitations visible. First edition (2002/2003) ingested — not the 2014 second edition. Moseley 1992/2001 remains the deep-time Andean ecological synthesis (Inca as interpretive model read backward — risk of presentism on Preceramic/EIP pages).
