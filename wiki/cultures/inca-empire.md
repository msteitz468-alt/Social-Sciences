## Definition and Construct History

**Tahuantinsuyu** ("Land of the Four Quarters") was the Inca imperial polity at its early-16th-century height — by Moseley's synthesis the largest native state in the Western Hemisphere and the largest ancient empire south of the equator, extending more than 5,500 km down the Andean Cordillera and over dry coast and Amazonian flanks ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], attributed). The term **Inca** properly names a small closed ethnic nobility (Moseley: fewer than ~40,000 kindred) who ruled as governing elite over subject populations numbering on the order of ten million — subjects were *not* "Incas" ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001]], attributed). The empire amalgamated more than eighty administratively distinct provinces from conquered *señoríos* and *parcialidades*; ethnic separatism made conquest relatively easy and consolidation hard.

Radiocarbon and ceramic work place crystallization of distinctively imperial Inca styles shortly after AD 1375 (as of Scarre ed. 2013); local Cuzco-basin origins reach into the post-[[wari-culture|Wari]] landscape (Covey 2006, via Scarre). Moseley treats ethnohistoric dynastic lore (origin centers, Pachacuti-generation expansion) as ideological charter, not transparent chronology — mythic and archaeological timelines both recorded, not adjudicated.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Imperial masonry: ashlar (rectangular, mortarless freestanding walls) and polygonal (terraces, solid masses). Cuzco as sacred-administrative navel; Coricancha ("House of the Sun") as Inti cult focus; Sacsahuamán as cyclopean acropolis (corvée labor at scale — Moseley cites multi-year drafts). Road network ~30,000–40,000 km with *chaski* relay posts; *qollqa* storehouses as staple-finance infrastructure. Royal estates (Machu Picchu, Ollantaytambo) as country seats, not "last refuges." *Mallqui* (ancestor mummies) retained political personhood; panaka (royal descent corporations) managed estates of deceased rulers.

**Linguistic.** Runa Simi/Quechua as imposed lingua franca for administration across linguistic diversity (Moseley 2001). Four-way non-identity: Quechua spread ≠ single people ≠ genetic population ≠ "Inca" ethnicity.

**Genetic.** Not addressed in ingested Moseley/Scarre material — [[unknown]].

## Distribution and Chronology

Four *suyu* radiated from Cuzco: Chinchaysuyu (north — coast and highlands, major canal polities), Antisuyu (eastern montaña; limited jungle penetration), Collasuyu (south — Titicaca, altiplano, Chile/Argentina highlands), Cuntisuyu (southwest desert/*lomas*). Late Horizon conventionally AD 1476–1533 in Rowe-derived schemes ([[andean-chronology]]); Moseley emphasizes longer local Inca development before blitzkrieg expansion and the 1470s conquest of Chimor ([[chimu-culture]]).

**Conquest sequence (Moseley 2001, attributed):** Old World epidemic disease (esp. smallpox) reached the Andes before Pizarro, killing Wayna Capac (d. ~1526) and much of the elite; civil war between Huascar (Cuzco faction) and Atahualpa (Tumibamba/north faction); Pizarro's ~260 men (62 horse) seized Atahualpa at Cajamarca 1532 after a false peaceful meeting; ransom then garrote; Spanish alliance with Cuzco faction entered the capital. Disease and civil war are contributing factors, not sole causes, of imperial collapse.

## Lifeways

**Folk base scaled to empire** (Moseley's "Inca model of statecraft," Ch. 3): [[ayllu]] corporate kin communities; [[dual-organization|hanan/hurin]] moieties; reciprocity (ayni; [[mita-labor|mit'a]]); ancestor/*huaca* cult; *kuraka* hereditary lords. Imperial instruments: decimal administration; three labor taxes (agricultural thirds, mit'a service, textile tribute); staple finance via *qollqa* vs wealth finance (fine cloth/*qompi*, metals); [[mitmaq]] resettlement of loyal and hostile communities; elite education of conquered heirs at Cuzco; roads and *chaski*. Economy without money: labor and cloth as media of obligation. [[khipu]] for census and accounts (*quipukamayoq* specialists).

Ecological frame: **Arid Montane** agropastoral verticality ([[vertical-archipelago]]) as highland trajectory climaxing in Inca Cuzco, paired against **Maritime-Oasis** coastal trajectory climaxing in Chimor/[[chan-chan|Chan Chan]] before Inca conquest (Moseley dual-trajectory thesis, attributed).

## Relations and Successions

Incorporated [[chimu-culture|Chimor]] and earlier coastal and sierra polities; reused Tiwanaku prestige in Collasuyu ideology (attributed). Preceded in the Cuzco basin by Killke/Lucre phases and broader post-Wari fragmentation (Moseley Ch. 9). Spanish conquest ends independent rule; colonial *mita* transforms Inca labor drafts.

## Identity Cautions

"Inca empire" is a political-administrative and material-culture construct. *Inca* ≠ all subjects; Quechua lingua franca ≠ ethnolinguistic homogenization; imperial style ≠ single genetic population. Archaeological "Inca" horizons and ethnohistoric "Tahuantinsuyu" must be linked with `associated_with` arguments, not silent identity. Four *suyu* names are administrative geography, not ethnic essences.

## Historiography

Classic chronicles (Cieza de León, Cobo, Guaman Poma) vs archaeological revision (Rowe horizon scheme; Covey on early Inca; D'Altroy on empires). Moseley 1992/2001 is a major single-author synthesis stressing ecology, folk institutions, and Inca-as-interpretive-model read backward onto prehistory — a method that risks presentism when applied to Preceramic and EIP societies (flag on those pages).
