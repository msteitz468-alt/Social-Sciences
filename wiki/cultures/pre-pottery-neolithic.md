# Pre-Pottery Neolithic

Fertile Crescent early Neolithic tradition defined by village life, cult architecture, and plant/animal management **without pottery** — Kenyon’s analytical split of Childe’s “Neolithic package.” Account below follows [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] (dates BC as he presents them).

## Definition and Construct History

**Kathleen Kenyon**, excavating [[jericho|Jericho]], found houses, burials, and a “Neolithic” lifestyle without pottery and coined **Pre-Pottery Neolithic (PPN)**; **PPNA** for the first village phase lasting no more than ~1,000 years after Holocene onset, then **PPNB** for later rectangular plastered architecture. The construct challenged Gordon Childe’s indivisible Neolithic package (pottery + farming + villages together). Subsequent work from Netiv Hagdud to WF16 confirmed Kenyon’s PPNA package broadly while decentralizing Jericho as sole origin (Mithen: West Bank and northern Fertile Crescent excavations reframe the Jordan valley as possibly peripheral to key ideological developments).

**Etic culture-historical construct** — not an ethnonym.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Mithen 2003):**

| Domain | PPNA | PPNB |
|---|---|---|
| Architecture | Circular/sunken mud-brick or stone dwellings (~5 m); Jericho tower/wall unique | Rectangular multi-room / two-storey plaster-floor houses; household storage |
| Lithics | el-Khiam points (multi-function) | Later Neolithic point types; craft specialization |
| Burial / ritual | Under-floor burials; skull removal continuing Natufian practice | Plastered skulls (Jericho); Kfar HaHoresh; Nahal Hemar; monumental cult (Göbekli T-pillars PPNA; Nevali Çori PPNB) |
| Economy | Wild game + cultivated wild or possibly domestic cereals; diverse plants | Domestic sheep/goats (e.g. Beidha); cereals; trade (obsidian, shells) |
| Pottery | Absent (definitional) | Absent until Pottery Neolithic successors |

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown from this evidence class. Do not equate PPN with later Semitic, Indo-European, or any aDNA cluster.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Geography:** Levantine corridor (Jericho, Netiv Hagdud, Gilgal, WF16, Dhra’, Zad), Middle Euphrates (Mureybet, Jerf el Ahmar, [[abu-hureyra|Abu Hureyra]] farming return), southeast Anatolia ([[gobekli-tepe|Göbekli Tepe]], Nevali Çori, Çayönü, Aşıklı), Cyprus (PPNA/PPNB artefacts in wells; circular architecture persisting), path toward central Anatolia ([[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]]).
- **Chronology (Mithen 2003):** Holocene warming soon after 10,000 BC; PPNA villages by ~9600 BC (Jericho ~50 dwellings/>500 people growing toward ~1,000); rectangular PPNB after ~9000 BC; ‘Ain Ghazal growth then decline by ~6300 BC; Cyprus farming ≥2,000 years earlier than once thought via Mylouthkia wells.

## Lifeways (Settlement and Subsistence)

**PPNA:** small circular dwellings, under-floor burials, skull rituals, hunting plus plant cultivation short of full package identity with later farmers. Jericho largest known but not sole origin.

**PPNB:** family as key social unit (Flannery’s circular→rectangular argument via Mithen); indoor craft/storage differentiation (Beidha); goat herding replacing gazelle dominance; craft specialists and long-distance materials; Mithen’s interpretive claim that thick-walled towns produce anonymity relative to open forager villages.

**Collapse / transformation:** PPNB towns of the Jordan valley — complete economic collapse narrative (soil exhaustion, erosion, infant mortality, return to hamlets); replaced by [[pastoralism|nomadic pastoralism]] continuing in Mithen’s present-day framing. Northern sites show different trajectories (cult buildings, craft towns).

**Cyprus:** boat-borne farmers with seed and livestock from western Syrian PPNA traditions (Peltenburg via Mithen); circular dwellings long after mainland went rectangular.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] and other Epipaleolithic foragers; Younger Dryas stress as context for cultivation trajectories.
- **Enables:** Pottery Neolithic; agro-pastoral expansion onto Iranian plateau after ~8000 BC; European farming package ultimately derived from this western Asian sphere ([[farming-spread-europe-debate]], [[early-european-farmers]]).
- **Compared with:** independent [[rice-domestication|rice]], [[maize-domestication|maize]], New Guinea [[horticulture]] origins — same era class, different packages.
- **associated_with** (not identity): coastal Syrian PPNA and Cypriot farmers (Peltenburg).

## Significance

PPN is the primary archaeological demonstration that **villages, ritual monumentality, and plant/animal management can precede pottery**, fracturing the classical Neolithic package. It anchors global debates on why farming began and how ideology (Göbekli) relates to economy.

## Identity Cautions

- **Etic construct** with regional facies (PPNA/PPNB; local site clusters) — not one people.
- **Four-way non-identity:** PPN material culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ “first farmers” continuous into historic nations.
- Kenyon’s labels are excavation-derived analytical phases, not self-names.
- Collapse narratives for Jordan valley PPNB should not be universalized to all Fertile Crescent PPNB.

## Historiography

Construct born at Jericho under Kenyon; expanded by West Bank PPNA projects, Euphrates salvage (Mureybet, Abu Hureyra), Anatolian cult archaeology (Schmidt at Göbekli), and Cyprus wells. Mithen 2003 is a popular-academic synthesis of early-2000s research — absolute dates, genetic ancestry, and some site interpretations have been revised since; keep claims attributed and date-stamped.

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* (Chicago 1988), Ch. 2 “The Time of Settlement (ca. 9000–6000 B.C.)” and opening of Ch. 3.)*

**Settlement ecology and catchment.** Nissen argues Neolithic sites were not random: optimal locations lay where varied sustenance was available nearby for as long and regularly as possible; camps on borders between small ecological units minimize travel while maximizing variety and season length — favoring differentiated (hilly/mountainous) relief. That matches early permanent/temporary settlement distribution: never on the great alluvial plains of southern Iraq (Babylonia) or Khuzistan (Susiana). Pre-pottery and earliest pottery Neolithic sites in the Zagros (e.g. Tepe Guran, Tepe Sarab, Ganj Dareh, Qabat [[jarmo|Jarmo]], Qabe Rostam) sit in valleys or valley exits to smaller plains as **isolated settlements far from peers**. Beidha (SE of the Dead Sea) exemplifies multi-ecotope access on a plateau between hills/desert and the Wadi Araba rift, with mixed production and foraging. Bouqras on the central Euphrates shows that even less differentiated hinterlands still used river, terrace, and hinterland for varied food-gathering (as of Nissen 1988).

**Early villages and mixed economy.** Permanent settlements without pottery were first recognized at Jarmo (eleven aceramic layers beneath limited simple pottery, high share of produced food). Simple storage pits in several prepottery settlements mark beginnings of stationary storage, later improved by clay plaster and large fired vessels. Nissen frames the “beginnings of permanent settlements” as a long period when cultivation and husbandry reduced — but did not soon replace — hunting/fishing/gathering, because production remained too unreliable; improved storage was more crucial than grain reserves for making extensive cultivation sensible. Domestication of cereals via selection of firm rachis and free-threshing traits; PPN bone assemblages with sex/age concentrations indicate control beyond pure hunting. Cultivation stayed climatically precarious; a mixed economy with gathering as buffer was essential for a very long time — people remained tied to multi-ecotope catchments even as production grew. Large hinterlands and terrain barriers kept settlements far apart, so neighborly contacts and **settlement systems cannot be expected** in this period; absence of systematic mutual connections is not only due to fragmentary finds (as of Nissen 1988).

**Social undifferentiation cautions ([[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] partial exposure).** At Çatalhöyük, excavations uncovered ~440 m² of densely packed standard-size houses with roof access and no ground-level doors; lack of significant house-size differences and absence of freestanding communal facilities suggest a socially undifferentiated community — but Nissen stresses **only about one-eighth of the settlement was excavated**, and grave finds point to social differences (few burials with opulent goods; houses with rich graves do not differ from others). Settlements generally have rarely been excavated enough to determine social differentiation from layout, house-size spread, or find distribution; generalizations are impossible. Bull-skull/horn rooms may indicate bull cult or totem animals, but cult rooms connect to living quarters — no separate freestanding temples as later temple prototypes. Nissen groups Çatalhöyük with [[jericho|Jericho]] as special early “town”-like extremes with unusual density-management problems, but only to a limited extent; further political-organization development did **not** lead via Jericho or Çatalhöyük, but via slow structured relationships between settlements and settlement systems (as of Nissen 1988). Pottery’s first appearance is not treated as a fundamental civilizational break (no accompanying change in architecture or subsistence); like writing later, pottery’s importance for modern chronology exceeds its status as an indicator of far-reaching social change.

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]*.)*

- **PPNA not one culture:** three regional cultures from [[khiamian|Khiamian]] — [[sultanian-culture|Sultanian]] (south), [[aswadian-culture|Aswadian]] (Damascus), [[mureybetian-culture|Mureybetian]] (middle Euphrates) — in the [[levantine-corridor|Levantine corridor]] (~9500–8300 BC). Kenyon’s PPNA was a chronological horizon at [[jericho|Jericho]].
- **PPNB phases (Cauvin):** aceramic culture ~8600–7000 BC; Early 8600–8200 (northern Syria from Mureybetian + SE Anatolia); Middle 8200–7500 (southern Levant expansion; dry-farming zone); long Late to pottery. Traits: naviform lithics/weaponry, rectangular architecture, herding + perfected cultivation, [[skull-cult|skull cult]].
- **~8000 BC reunification (Postscript):** middle PPNB simplifies and reunifies domesticate packages, architecture, technology across Levant (except central Anatolia); PPNA becomes more chronological than cultural label.
- **Anatolia:** neolithisation of Taurus/SE Anatolia (Çayönü, [[cafer-hoyuk|Cafer Höyük]], [[nevali-cori|Nevalı Çori]], [[gobekli-tepe|Göbekli]]); Aşıklı fully cultivated without herding (herding later in Anatolia).
- **Diffusion:** “Great Exodus” — Mediterranean littoral, [[khirokitia|Cyprus]] (chronology corrected earlier after Halula cattle ~7700 BC), Jezirah/desert sedentary villages, pastoral nomadism.
- **Symbols:** Goddess/Bull system; southern plastered skulls, masks, monumental statues; Göbekli T-pillars enrich animal repertory (Postscript).
