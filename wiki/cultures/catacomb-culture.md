# Catacomb Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The **Catacomb culture** is an etic Middle Bronze Age construct of the Pontic steppes named for its characteristic **catacomb** (side-chamber) grave form, succeeding and fragmenting out of the [[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]] horizon. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] (*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, 2007) places Yamnaya fragmentation into **Catacomb (Don–Kuban)** and **Poltavka (Volga–Ural)** around **~2800 BCE**, with late PIE no longer a unity by ~2500 BCE. Claims density on Catacomb *per se* in this source is thinner than for Sintashta or Sredni Stog: Catacomb appears mainly as Yamnaya’s western successor, a wagon-grave partner culture, a ceramic/metal donor to Abashevo, and a problematic candidate in the **Greek origins** discussion. Still a conventional archaeological culture, not a self-named people.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Mortuary / vehicles:** Aleksandr Gei (2000) counted **257 Yamnaya and Catacomb** wagon and cart burials in the Pontic-Caspian steppes, radiocarbon ~**3100–2200 BCE**; vehicles in <5% of graves; largest cluster **120 in the Kuban** (mostly Novotitorovskaya); wheels usually disassembled at pit corners; ten whole wagons: fixed axle, revolving 50–80 cm multi-plank wheels, bed ~1×2–2.5 m, gauge 1.5–1.65 m, painted reed-mat tilt.

**Ceramic and metal influence north:** Abashevo ceramics showed increasing decorative influences from steppe Catacomb traditions — horizontal line-and-dot, horizontal fluting; **shell tempering**; some Abashevo metal types (waisted knives) copied Catacomb and Poltavka types. Anthony: Abashevo as conduit of steppe customs north into the forest-steppe, feeding [[sintashta-culture|Sintashta]] origins.

**Ingul late Catacomb (~2500–2000 BCE):** Anthony notes Mycenaean Shaft Grave parallels (cheekpieces, socketed spearheads, death masks) that are hard to source specifically — suggestive but not a demonstrated migration trail.

**Linguistic:** Greek is “the only major post-Anatolian branch that is difficult to derive from the steppes” (Anthony). Pre-Greek likely split from later IE dialects, sharing traits with Armenian/Phrygian (SE Europe before 1200 BCE) and pre-Indo-Iranian; **early western Catacomb** would fit eastern SE-European / western steppe border requirements — but “it is impossible, as far as I know, to identify a Catacomb-culture migration that moved directly from the western steppes into Greece.”

Precise internal periodization and type-site hierarchy are not detailed in the extracted Anthony claims beyond the Yamnaya-succession and contact roles above.

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Continuity with late Yamnaya mobile pastoralism is implied by shared wagon-grave technology and steppe distribution; Anthony’s dense economic description focuses on Yamnaya and later Sintashta more than on Catacomb daily settlements. Wagon graves mark elite or special burials (<5% of graves), not universal transport in every household.

## Relations and Successions

- `succeeded` early/middle [[yamnaya-culture]] on the western/central Pontic steppe as Yamnaya fragments.
- `concurrent_with` Poltavka on the Volga–Ural as eastern Yamnaya continuation.
- `associated_with` Abashevo ceramic and metal influence → Sintashta genesis pathway.
- `associated_with` (weakly, attributed) Pre-Greek problem — fit without proven migration.
- Wagon package shared with Yamnaya; Kuban Novotitorovskaya concentration.

## Identity Cautions

- Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people. Catacomb ≠ “Greeks” or any named ethnos.
- Do not treat Shaft Grave formal parallels as proof of Catacomb migration to Mycenae.
- Wagon-grave counts combine Yamnaya **and** Catacomb — disaggregate carefully when citing Gei 2000 via Anthony.
- This page is densified from relatively **sparse** Anthony 2007 targeting; fuller Catacomb monographs would thicken chronology and regional variants.
