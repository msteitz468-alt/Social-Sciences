# Ertebølle Culture

Late Mesolithic archaeological culture of Denmark (and related southern Scandinavian coasts), type-named from the **Ertebølle shell midden** — classic [[complex-hunter-gatherers|complex hunter-gatherers]] with pottery, marine diets, cemeteries, and frontier contact with [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] farmers. Account follows [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]].

## Definition and Construct History

The Ertebølle midden (Jutland) — ~20 m wide, metres thick, >100 m long — gave its name to the last Mesolithic peoples of Denmark (National Museum usage in Mithen’s framing). Søren Andersen re-excavated in 1983; Victorian John Lubbock had visited Danish *køkkenmøddinger* in the 1860s, already inferring year-round coastal residence from swan bones, antlers, and young mammals. Danish shell-midden research since the 1850s preceded British Mesolithic lifestyle archaeology (Clark/Star Carr). **Etic construct.**

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Mithen 2003):**

- **Middens:** massive oyster and shell accumulations; chemical/bone evidence of heavy marine and freshwater diet vs game ornaments.
- **Pottery:** coarse pointed-bottom bowls and flat cooking dishes — plain, thick, uneven walls — distinct from Nea Nikomedeia fine farming wares; pottery among foragers as a change marker with ground stone axes.
- **Boats/fishing:** Tybrind Vig lime dugouts, leisters, ash paddles with geometric designs as identity/territory signals.
- **Mortuary:** Vedbæk/Bøgebakken graves (e.g. mother and newborn on swan’s wing); regional late Mesolithic violence signatures (Skateholm and northern cemeteries).
- **Inland logistics:** Ringkloster winter fur-trade camp (pine marten etc.) exchanging with coast for oysters, dolphin, amber — only known inland Mesolithic settlement in Denmark after 150 years of research (as of Mithen).

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown; do not equate with historic Danes.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Core:** Danish coasts and lagoons; related late Mesolithic marine economies in southern Scandinavia; Portuguese estuary middens compared as analogues (Tagus/Sado).
- **Frontier:** northern German contact with LBK farmers ~4400 BC (fur for polished axes; intermarriage scenarios).
- **End:** Danish/Swedish complex forager communities collapse under competitive ethic from LBK contact and “seepage of youth,” becoming farmers around **3900 BC** (Mithen’s synthesis); early Scandinavian farmers differ from LBK — cattle, ephemeral dwellings, continued shell middens, long barrows inspired by LBK long houses.

## Lifeways (Settlement and Subsistence)

Sedentary or multi-season coastal residence; marine protein emphasis (possible parasite/health costs); pottery cooking; trapping and exchange; identity marking via clothes, harpoons, axe shapes in a late Mesolithic world of fractured cultural uniformity versus early Star Carr–era pan-northern similarity. Rowley-Conwy (via Mithen): decreasing salinity may have abandoned shell mounds and switched economy toward farming — environmental push alongside social frontier pull.

## Relations and Successions

- **Contact:** [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] and broader [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].
- **Comparative:** other complex coastal foragers ([[jomon-culture|Jōmon]], Téviec/Hoëdic, NW Coast analogies).
- **Historiography link:** Victorian Lubbock as proto-archaeozoologist of middens.

## Significance

Ertebølle is the European type-case for **pottery-using, sedentary marine foragers** who meet farming without being “simple” precursors — central to debates on whether Mesolithic people were replaced, converted, or hybridized.

## Identity Cautions

- Shell-midden culture ≠ ethnic “Danes” or a single tribe.
- “Last of the Mesolithic peoples” is a narrative phrase, not a genetic endpoint.
- Four-way non-identity: material Ertebølle ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ self-conscious people.
- Frontier marriage/trade scenes in Mithen include **narrative reconstruction** — separate from excavated facts.

## Historiography

1850s+ Danish kitchen-midden committees; Lubbock 1860s visits; National Museum culture naming; Andersen excavations; Rowley-Conwy economic models; Mithen’s comparative late Mesolithic chapter set. Absolute phase boundaries refined in specialist literature beyond Mithen 2003.
