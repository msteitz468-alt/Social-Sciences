# Ertebølle Culture

Late Mesolithic archaeological culture of Denmark (and related southern Scandinavian coasts), type-named from the **Ertebølle shell midden** — classic [[complex-hunter-gatherers|complex hunter-gatherers]] with pottery, marine diets, cemeteries, and frontier contact with [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] farmers. Account follows [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]].

## Definition and Construct History

The Ertebølle midden (Jutland) — ~20 m wide, metres thick, >100 m long — gave its name to the last Mesolithic peoples of Denmark (National Museum usage in Mithen’s framing). Søren Andersen re-excavated in 1983; Victorian John Lubbock had visited Danish *køkkenmøddinger* in the 1860s, already inferring year-round coastal residence from swan bones, antlers, and young mammals. Danish shell-midden research since the 1850s preceded British Mesolithic lifestyle archaeology (Clark/Star Carr). **Etic construct.**

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Mithen 2003):**

- **Middens:** massive oyster and shell accumulations; chemical/bone evidence of heavy marine and freshwater diet vs game ornaments.
- **Pottery:** coarse pointed-bottom bowls and flat cooking dishes — plain, thick, uneven walls — distinct from Nea Nikomedeia fine farming wares; pottery among foragers as a change marker with ground stone axes.
- **Boats/fishing:** Tybrind Vig lime dugouts, leisters, ash paddles with geometric designs as identity/territory signals.
- **Mortuary:** Vedbæk/Bøgebakken graves (e.g. mother and newborn on swan’s wing); regional late Mesolithic violence signatures (Skateholm and northern cemeteries).
- **Inland logistics:** Ringkloster winter fur-trade camp (pine marten etc.) exchanging with coast for oysters, dolphin, amber — only known inland Mesolithic settlement in Denmark after 150 years of research (as of Mithen).

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown; do not equate with historic Danes.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Core:** Danish coasts and lagoons; related late Mesolithic marine economies in southern Scandinavia; Portuguese estuary middens compared as analogues (Tagus/Sado).
- **Frontier:** northern German contact with LBK farmers ~4400 BC (fur for polished axes; intermarriage scenarios).
- **End:** Danish/Swedish complex forager communities collapse under competitive ethic from LBK contact and “seepage of youth,” becoming farmers around **3900 BC** (Mithen’s synthesis); early Scandinavian farmers differ from LBK — cattle, ephemeral dwellings, continued shell middens, long barrows inspired by LBK long houses.

## Lifeways (Settlement and Subsistence)

Sedentary or multi-season coastal residence; marine protein emphasis (possible parasite/health costs); pottery cooking; trapping and exchange; identity marking via clothes, harpoons, axe shapes in a late Mesolithic world of fractured cultural uniformity versus early Star Carr–era pan-northern similarity. Rowley-Conwy (via Mithen): decreasing salinity may have abandoned shell mounds and switched economy toward farming — environmental push alongside social frontier pull.

## Relations and Successions

- **Contact:** [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] and broader [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].
- **Comparative:** other complex coastal foragers ([[jomon-culture|Jōmon]], Téviec/Hoëdic, NW Coast analogies).
- **Historiography link:** Victorian Lubbock as proto-archaeozoologist of middens.

## Significance

Ertebølle is the European type-case for **pottery-using, sedentary marine foragers** who meet farming without being “simple” precursors — central to debates on whether Mesolithic people were replaced, converted, or hybridized.

## Identity Cautions

- Shell-midden culture ≠ ethnic “Danes” or a single tribe.
- “Last of the Mesolithic peoples” is a narrative phrase, not a genetic endpoint.
- Four-way non-identity: material Ertebølle ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ self-conscious people.
- Frontier marriage/trade scenes in Mithen include **narrative reconstruction** — separate from excavated facts.

## Historiography

1850s+ Danish kitchen-midden committees; Lubbock 1860s visits; National Museum culture naming; Andersen excavations; Rowley-Conwy economic models; Mithen’s comparative late Mesolithic chapter set. Absolute phase boundaries refined in specialist literature beyond Mithen 2003.

### Sherratt densification — conversion and TRB interface (1990/95)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]* Ch.13–14.)* Sherratt does not always name Ertebølle in every slice, but his **outer Europe / Baltic Sub-Neolithic** and **[[funnel-beaker-culture|TRB]] fusion** models supply the conversion layer for dense forager coasts meeting farming: megalithic and long-mound monumentality as **instruments of conversion** recruiting Mesolithic communities into farming social worlds; TRB beginnings as loess Neolithic × indigenous North European Plain fusion (not pure continuity, not pure southern wave). Pair with Mithen’s 3900 BC farming transition above — independent syntheses, both attributed. See [[megalith]], [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].

## Whittle densification — forager neighbours, Strøby Egede, Oronsay, TRB transition (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 6–7 and synthesis Ch. 10. Mid-1990s cal BC; **pre-aDNA**. Ertebølle evidence tends to colour interpretations of other forager coasts — do not equate all Atlantic/British late Mesolithic with Baltic shell-midden density.)*

### Forager neighbours of the LBK longhouse world

Whittle treats Baltic–Scandinavian coastal foragers as **neighbours** of the [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] woodland longhouse world, not as passive empty landscape. Ertebølle–**Ellerbek**–Lietzow complex: well-established Baltic–Scandinavian coastal foragers in the fifth millennium BC; also coastal-zone W Netherlands, Britain/Ireland, Brittany; inland Alpine foreland, parts of N European plain, many parts of Britain — **not all identical**. From mid sixth millennium, Ertebølle phase enhances **radiating mobility**; middle–late Ertebølle (≈fifth millennium) large numerous coastal sites as main residential zone + some inland sites. Coastal pathway (later sixth millennium): foragers around Baltic, S Scandinavia, Netherlands, perhaps Brittany/parts Britain–Ireland more numerous; continuous coastal use; not far from northern LBK limits.

If one concentrates on diet and shelter technology, forest farmers and coastal foragers look different, but Whittle suggests **lifestyles and values may have had many similarities** — neither an accumulation ethic; attachment to place/continuity; community of the dead basic. “Longhouses and lagoons”: forest-farmer longhouse settlements as “artificial lagoons of productivity” in a woodland sea; Wittmar and Skateholm II rites only ~350 km apart rather similar. Stable frontier with Ertebølle–Ellerbek; much lifestyle convergence despite archaeological labels (Ch. 10 synthesis).

### Settlement system and technology (as of Whittle 1996)

**Tybrind Vig** (submerged Fyn NW shore): dugout canoe ~10 m, charcoal fire for night eel fishing with double-headed prongs; long fifth-millennium use; wide marine/terrestrial resources; food-crust fish + dry-land plants; multi-season presence; elegant tech — flint core axes, plaited rope/plant-fibre fabric, wooden arrows/bows, bone hooks, wooden leisters, brown-patterned ash paddles.

Large coastal/shell-midden bases: Ertebølle mound ≥140×20–30 m up to 2 m high (+ smaller); Bjørnsholm–Ale strip up to 500×30–50 m, ~8 km apart on Limfjord; specialised camps (Aggersund swan-fowling; Ølby Lyng seals; Ringkloster inland pig-hunt ~15 km from Norsminde fjord sites — meat processed, back ends removed). Shell middens as special gathering/feast/ritual places vs permanent bases remains debated; enduring stability of coastal-zone concentration with anchors of special places.

### Mortuary: Skateholm, Vedbæk, Strøby Egede

**Skateholm** (S Sweden): sixth–fifth millennia shallow coastal lagoon; Skateholm II earliest known, inundated ~5000 BC; attachment to place expressed in burial ground beside settlement — new vs earlier circulation of human bone among living. Skateholm II: >20 graves; men, women, children, domesticated dogs; mostly single; gendered goods; red deer antlers, red ochre; LBK-type stone adze = early contact/exotics. Whittle: burial ground partly **redefinition of local identity** vs new southern developments. Skateholm I (fifth millennium): ≥65 graves (57 human with 62 individuals, 8 dogs); goods quality/quantity decline through time; dogs mostly separate canine area.

**Vedbæk Bøgebakken:** >20 people in <20 graves by estuary occupation; triple grave infant between man and woman — bone point in man’s chest; severe injuries also at Skateholm and Tybrind Vig — suggested killing/murder and rising tensions; Whittle: moot without enough earlier skeletons for comparison.

**Strøby Egede** (Zealand E coast): one large collective grave (others maybe originally present) — **8 people**: 4 females heads one end (infant, child, young woman, mature/old woman buried first); 4 males heads other end (child, infant, man with infant in right arm last); beads/blades with children and young woman; old woman flint knife; man 5 blades + incised antler axe; red ochre thickest round children. Møllegabet: young man partial remains in burnt dugout canoe stem staked near water, elm bark cover; healed severe head wound. Late unfurnished midden burials including Ertebølle itself — important for end-sequence modes.

### Oronsay (comparative late Mesolithic — not Ertebølle, Whittle’s parallel)

**Oronsay** (Inner Hebrides): ≥6 late Mesolithic shell middens on raised beaches; limpets (scallops/crabs), saithe inshore fishing, grey/common seals, seabirds; deer/pig bones possibly meat from mainland/Jura; otolith seasonality suggests different middens peak mid-summer through late autumn/winter; hearths, stake holes for flimsy shelters/drying racks, no substantial structures. Whittle: **repeated seasonal visits in a regional mobile system** more likely than year-round Oronsay base; isolated human bone (teeth, feet, hands, skull) — ancestors circulating with the living? Flag: Oronsay is **British late Mesolithic**, not Danish Ertebølle — Whittle uses it for comparison of coastal midden mobility and ancestor-bone practice, not identity with Ertebølle culture. Brittany Téviec/Hoëdic: possibly **radically smaller more mobile population than Baltic**, important for different innovation-acceptance conditions.

### Transition to TRB / early Neolithic (Whittle’s positions)

Ertebølle–TRB transition hard in middens; **Norsminde** shell midden continues early Neolithic — hunt/fowl/shellfish, less/no fish, + domesticates/cereals, new pot/tool styles; ¹⁴C imprecise allows continuity or brief abandonment. Loddesborg Scania: Ertebølle and TRB pots same undisturbed layer, former with cereal impressions. Satrup Moor Ellerbek may know cereals/domestic cattle/pig; early TRB Siggeneben-Süd still diverse mixed economy. Ch. 7: continuity of population from Ertebølle to early TRB (ENI) in Denmark and southern Sweden; regional early pottery styles match regional Ertebølle material culture; coastal use, shell middens (e.g. Norsminde), inland hunting (Muldbjerg), sealing (Hesselø), early TRB fishing weirs. Isotope analysis suggesting decisive shift to mainly terrestrial diet vs Ertebølle marine diet (Vedbæk) treated as **preliminary** and open to suspicion (as of Whittle 1996).

At least five transition situations (not one model): (1) **main Ertebølle** — coexistence, independence, cultural resistance, then swift change: new resources without abandoning traditional ones, some new site locations, **no dramatic longhouse life**, striking new material media/gatherings/ceremonies; (2) southern Ertebølle–Ellerbek — more gradual partial new subsistence first; (3) W Netherlands gradual; (4) Brittany sparse mobile population may fuse with outsiders; (5) Britain/Ireland — sea transport of seed corn and sheep/goats required. Consensus swing to forager continuity/indigenous transformation Loire→lower Vistula, but Whittle warns against stereotyped single transformation — **many transitions**. Danger of over-reading Ertebølle density onto all NW forager coasts.
