# Ancestral Pueblo (Anasazi) Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Ancestral Pueblo** is the modern name for the maize-farming, masonry- and adobe-building
tradition of the northern [[hohokam-culture|Southwest]] (Colorado Plateau and Four Corners),
ancestral to the historic Pueblo peoples. [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]]
uses the older term **"Anasazi"** (from Navajo, variously glossed "the old people" / "ancient
enemies"; proposed by A. V. Kidder) throughout — recorded here as his dated period term; this
wiki uses **Ancestral Pueblo**, now standard because Pueblo descendants reject "Anasazi."

The developmental sequence follows the **Pecos classification** (Kidder, 1927 Pecos
Conference): Basketmaker II–III → Pueblo I–V, a *developmental* not strictly chronological
ladder, criticized as "too linear" for the region's real diversity. Southwestern chronology is
unusually precise because of **dendrochronology** (A. E. Douglass bridged historic and
prehistoric tree-ring sequences in 1929) — tree rings supply both fine calendar dates and
drought reconstruction.

`construct_status: conventional` — an etic construct ancestral to *several* Pueblo peoples
speaking unrelated languages (Hopi, Zuni, Keresan, Tanoan); it is not one people or one
language.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** A shift, c. AD 700–900, from pithouse villages to
above-ground masonry/adobe roomblocks, with the old pithouse persisting as the **kiva**
(subterranean ceremonial chamber). Maize-beans-squash agriculture (dry, floodwater, and some
water-control farming); painted pottery; great kivas; and, in the [[chaco-canyon|Chaco]]
florescence, monumental multi-story **great houses**, a **road network**, and turquoise
craft. Archaeoastronomy (solar/solstice alignments) is documented at Hovenweep and argued at
Chaco (Casa Rinconada, Pueblo Bonito).

## Distribution and Chronology

Centered on the San Juan Basin and Colorado Plateau. Two contrasting florescences of AD
900–1150: the nucleated, hierarchical, regionally integrated **[[chaco-canyon|Chaco
phenomenon]]** and the community-scale **[[mesa-verde|Mesa Verde]]** region. By **AD 1300 the
entire San Juan drainage was depopulated** ("the abandonment"), its people migrating south and
southeast to the lands of the historic Hopi, Zuni, and Rio Grande pueblos, aggregating into
large multi-storied pueblos (some 1,000+ rooms) — where, by Spanish contact (1540), kachina
ritual is archaeologically recognizable.

## Lifeways

Fagan tracks a long-running debate on the pithouse-to-pueblo shift and settlement aggregation:
Julian Steward's (1955) ecological clan-origin model (later revised — dispersed rancherias were
more widespread than Steward thought, and population pressure rather than "defense" is the more
likely driver of aggregation); Fred Plog's population-growth-and-dispersal model; and the
"abandonment controversy" (early raiding/warfare/epidemic theories now largely displaced by a
**drought-cycle** explanation grounded in tree-ring data, itself stated as provisional).
Fagan cautions that apparent "abandonments" may partly reflect decentralization and reduced
labor coordination rather than sheer depopulation.

## Relations and Successions

- **Descended from (argued):** the Southwestern Archaic (Oshara tradition, per Irwin-Williams).
- **Descends to:** the historic Pueblo peoples — a continuity Pueblo communities themselves
  affirm; hence the terminology shift from "Anasazi."
- **Contrasts with:** [[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] (desert irrigation) and
  [[mogollon-culture|Mogollon]] (mountain pithouse-to-pueblo, Mimbres pottery).

## Identity Cautions

- **"Anasazi" is contested.** Use Ancestral Pueblo; record "Anasazi" only as historical/period
  usage.
- **Multiple descendant peoples.** The construct is ancestral to several linguistically distinct
  Pueblo groups — not a single ethnos.
- **Defensive architecture ≠ warfare.** At Mesa Verde, Fagan notes apparent defensive features
  but states "there are no signs of warfare in the archaeological record."

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], resting on the Pecos classification and
dendrochronology. The pithouse-to-pueblo, aggregation, and abandonment questions are the central
debates; NAGPRA-era terminology ("Ancestral Pueblo" over "Anasazi") postdates this 1991 text.
Densify from later Chaco, Mesa Verde, and Pueblo-descendant collaborative work when ingested.
