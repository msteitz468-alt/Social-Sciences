# Erligang Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Erligang culture (ca. 1600–1400 BCE) is defined from the material sequence at
[[zhengzhou]], where it names the archaeological horizon that emerged during [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]]
Phase IV, marked by two new walled settlements — Yanshi and Zhengzhou — replacing Erlitou as the
dominant centers of the middle Yellow River region. As reported by Liu & Chen (2012), Zhengzhou
was more than four times the size of Erlitou and became the primary center, with Yanshi as a
secondary center.

Liu & Chen deliberately separate the **political** label from the **material-culture** label for
this horizon: "We prefer the term 'Erligang state' when referring to the political dimension of
Erligang culture, but follow the conventional use of 'early Shang' in the Chinese archaeological
literature when describing relevant material remains" (as of Liu & Chen 2012). This wiki follows
that distinction — "Erligang culture/state" names the archaeological/political construct built
from excavated remains; "early Shang" is the conventional label used elsewhere in Chinese
archaeology for the same material horizon, and neither should be read as a settled identification
of the Zhengzhou/Yanshi sites with a specific historically-named dynastic capital (see Identity
Cautions).

## Material Signature

At least three tiers of political hierarchy are archaeologically visible within the Erligang core
area, and Erligang material remains spread over a much greater region than Erlitou's; unlike most
Erlitou regional centers, most Erligang regional centers were fortified, which Liu & Chen read as
evidence of intensified warfare (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Diagnostic material includes:

- **Zhengzhou itself**: a walled city of ~25 km² (rectangular inner city ~300 ha, roughly round
  outer city ~18 km²); bronze foundries (Nanguanwai, Zijingshan) with hard evidence of
  ritual-vessel casting, three bronze hoards (28 bronzes, >500 kg total, including a 100 cm,
  86.4 kg fangding tetrapod — a vessel type new at Zhengzhou); two inscribed animal-bone
  fragments stylistically similar to later Shang oracle-bone script (see [[zhengzhou]] for detail).
- **Fortified outposts**: Panlongcheng (south), Dongxiafeng, Nanguan, Donglongshan (west),
  Daxinzhuang/Qianzhangda (east) — several continuing earlier Erlitou-period regional centers now
  refortified.
- **Bronze ritual-vessel production and redistribution**, which Liu & Chen model as the core
  mechanism of a tributary core-periphery political economy: peripheral centers supplied raw and
  exotic materials (metal ores, salt) as tribute, and the Zhengzhou/Yanshi core redistributed
  restricted prestige goods — above all bronze ritual vessels — to regional elites (as of Liu &
  Chen 2012).

No linguistic or genetic evidence for Erligang-affiliated populations is reported in this source;
those evidence classes are silent here.

Radiocarbon dates place the Erligang (early Shang) state at c. 1600 BCE, close in time to the
latest Erlitou Phase III dates (as of [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]], both based on small C-14 samples). Shelach-Lavi and Jaffe (2013), cited in
Shelach-Lavi (2015), argue that Erligang — rather than Erlitou — marks the "qualitative,
step-like" sociopolitical change toward statehood: its bronze vessels (e.g., a 100 cm, 86.4 kg
fangding) dwarf Erlitou-period production, and Zhengzhou's fortifications and settlement scale
lack a clear Erlitou-period precedent of comparable magnitude — a position bearing directly on
[[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]].

## Distribution and Chronology

Erligang expansion reached south to Panlongcheng (Hubei), west to Dongxiafeng/Nanguan/Donglongshan,
and east to Daxinzhuang/Qianzhangda, radiating out from the Zhengzhou/Yanshi core in the middle
Yellow River valley. Around 1400 BCE the expansion ceased: Zhengzhou and many regional centers
were abandoned, ending the centralized political-economic system. Three bronze hoards buried in
haste at Zhengzhou near the end of the Upper Erligang phase are interpreted by Liu & Chen as
safe-keeping caches during unrest, analogous in function to the later Western Zhou Zhouyuan
hoards (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

Following Zhengzhou's collapse, a "middle Shang" phase (ca. 1400–1250 BCE) saw smaller successor
centers — Xiaoshuangqiao (150 ha, near Zhengzhou) and Huanbei Shang City (a 470 ha enclosure on
the Huan River, opposite [[anyang|Yinxu]]) — with peripheral regional centers continuing amid
political instability and, per Liu & Chen, factional competition. Liu & Chen frame the whole
Erlitou-to-middle-Shang sequence as a first cycle of political centralization (Erlitou, then
Erligang/Zhengzhou) followed by decentralization (middle Shang) — a cycle they note recurred
repeatedly across later Chinese dynastic history (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Lifeways

As evidenced at Zhengzhou: intensive bronze-ritual-vessel casting and bone-working (including,
per the skullcap finds, processing of human bone), fortification-building at a scale implying
large corvée labor mobilization, and a settlement hierarchy of at least three tiers. Warfare
appears intensified relative to the preceding Erlitou period, inferred from the shift to fortified
regional centers.

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by`: [[erlitou-culture]] — Erligang emerges during Erlitou Phase IV; several Erligang
regional centers directly continue earlier Erlitou-period sites (Panlongcheng, Dongxiafeng,
Nanguan, Donglongshan), now refortified.

`succeeded_by`: [[shang-civilization]] — Erligang material culture is conventionally labeled
"early Shang" in Chinese archaeology; the middle-Shang successor centers (Xiaoshuangqiao,
Huanbei) lead toward the later Anyang/Yinxu Shang sequence.

`associated_with`: no scholarly consensus exists on which historical dynastic capital names (e.g.,
Ao vs. Bo for Zhengzhou; Xiang vs. Yin for Huanbei) correspond to which excavated Erligang/middle-Shang
sites (as of Liu & Chen 2012) — the dominant view among many Chinese archaeologists reads the rise
of Yanshi Shang City, in the late-Erlitou-Phase-IV heartland, as the historical event of Shang's
conquest of Xia (Du & Wang 2004; Zhongguo Shehui Kexueyuan 2003b), i.e. Erlitou = Xia, Yanshi =
succeeding Shang — a live position, not settled fact; see [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]] and
[[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]].

## Identity Cautions

"Erligang culture" (material-culture label) and "Erligang state" (political label) are Liu &
Chen's own terminological distinction and should not be collapsed. Neither label, nor the
conventional "early Shang" gloss, constitutes a demonstrated identification with a specific named
historical dynastic capital — which historical capital name attaches to which excavated site
remains disputed. The culture is defined purely on material/settlement evidence; no linguistic or
genetic data are available to test any correspondence with a self-identified people.

## Historiography

The Erligang sequence was defined through excavation at Zhengzhou beginning in 1950 and refined
through subsequent stratigraphic and ceramic-seriation work; Liu & Chen (2012) synthesize this
record and introduce the "Erligang state" / "early Shang" terminological distinction used above,
explicitly to avoid over-committing archaeological description to unproven textual-dynastic
identifications.

## Chang 1980 densification (attributed)

[[chang-kwang-chih|Chang]] (*[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*, 1980) names
the **Erh-li-kang phase** from the Cheng-chou type site and treats it as the Shang capital form
**ancestral** to dynastic An-yang: hang-t'u walls, Loehr I–III bronzes, northeast palace sector,
workshop ring. He maps Erh-li-kang–phase and dynastic-phase sites across north, east, south, and
west China (including [[panlongcheng|P'an-lung-ch'eng]]), leaving colonial, peer-interaction, and
resource-outpost readings open. Sequence (attributed): Dynastic An-yang ← Erh-li-kang ← Erh-li-t'ou
(not Shang) ← Honan Lung-shan. Absolute mid-second-millennium dates "(as of Chang 1980)" are
provisional. Later Liu & Chen separate "Erligang state" (political) from "early Shang" (material
gloss) more sharply; Chang's 1980 prose often still says "Shang" for Erh-li-kang material while
excluding Erlitou.
