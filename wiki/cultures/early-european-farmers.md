## Definition and Construct History

"Early European farmers" denotes the genetically defined population descended predominantly from Anatolian-related immigrants who spread farming into Europe beginning roughly 9,000 years ago. Genome-wide ancient DNA (2015–2016, Reich's lab with Ron Pinhasi, using petrous-bone extraction and Matthias Meyer's DNA-enrichment method) established that the first farmers of Europe — sampled in Germany, Spain, Hungary, and Anatolia itself, dated 8,800–4,500 years ago — were genetically similar to present-day Sardinians, retaining at least 90 percent ancestry from the immigrant Anatolian source, with minimal admixture from resident hunter-gatherers (as of Reich 2018). This finding directly falsified Luigi Luca Cavalli-Sforza's earlier "demic diffusion" model, which had proposed a smooth southeast-to-northwest gradient of increasing hunter-gatherer ancestry produced by progressive farmer-forager mixing as agriculture spread (see [[demic-diffusion]]); the actual highest-surviving proportion of Anatolian-farmer ancestry today is in Sardinia, not southeast Europe as Cavalli-Sforza's model predicted, and John Novembre and colleagues showed from 2008 that the clinal gradients underlying the older model can arise as a mathematical artifact of the clustering technique used, independent of any true migration gradient.

## Material Signature

This page treats "early European farmers" as a genetic population rather than an archaeological culture defined by artifact typology; the associated Neolithic material record (LBK and related farming-package assemblages) is not detailed here and belongs on the relevant archaeological-culture and period pages.

## Genetic Signature

Peloponnese/Crete farmers dated to roughly 6,000 years ago, who practiced pre-pottery farming, may derive part of their ancestry from a different, more Iranian-related Anatolian source population than the farmers who spread across the rest of Europe — a live possibility, attributed to Reich (2018), not settled fact. Later European farmers, dated 6,000–4,500 years ago, show roughly 20 percent additional hunter-gatherer ancestry not present in the earliest farmer populations, indicating substantial admixture with resident [[western-hunter-gatherers]] began only after a roughly two-thousand-year delay following initial farmer arrival. Reich states that light skin in Europe largely owes its origins to these migrating farmers, who mostly carried light-skin alleles but dark hair and brown eyes, contrasting with the dark-skinned, blue-eyed hunter-gatherer populations they encountered (attributed, Reich 2018). Joachim Burger's 2009 mitochondrial DNA study found early European farmers carried mitochondrial haplotypes largely distinct from preceding hunter-gatherers and instead similar to present-day southern Europe and the Near East, indicating minimal descent from local foragers.

## Distribution and Chronology

Early European farmer ancestry spread from Anatolia and the Near East into Europe beginning after roughly 9,000 years ago, reaching Germany, Iberia, and Hungary by 8,800–4,500 years ago. The Funnel Beaker culture, arising near the Baltic Sea after roughly 6,300 years ago, represents a case of northern hunter-gatherer populations adopting farming gradually over more than a thousand years while retaining hunter-gatherer genetic and cultural elements before being genetically overtaken by farmer-related ancestry by 6,000–5,000 years ago. Farmer-descended populations were themselves later substantially displaced or admixed by steppe-derived populations after roughly 5,000 years ago: British/Irish Bronze Age skeletons following the Beaker period had at most about 10 percent ancestry from the islands' first farmers, with roughly 90 percent from Bell-Beaker-associated continental populations — a population turnover Reich characterizes as at least as dramatic as the Corded Ware replacement further east (as of Reich 2018).

## Lifeways

Lifeways are evidenced through the archaeological Neolithic farming package (agriculture, sedentism) associated with the population's Anatolian origin; specific subsistence and settlement detail is treated on relevant Neolithic period and site pages rather than here.

## Relations and Successions

Early European farmers are `preceded_by` [[western-hunter-gatherers]] and are themselves substantially displaced or admixed by later steppe-derived populations, `associated_with` [[yamnaya-culture]], [[corded-ware-culture]], and [[bell-beaker-culture]]. Colin Renfrew's 1987 Anatolian hypothesis proposed that Indo-European languages spread together with this farming population beginning after 9,000 years ago (a position); Reich argues ancient DNA evidence of a later, demographically substantial Corded-Ware-associated migration after 5,000 years ago undermines this hypothesis by showing that large-scale later migrations were demographically possible after all, favoring instead a modified steppe hypothesis, though the location of the original Indo-European homeland remains open (see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

"Early European farmers" is a genetically defined population inferred from ancient DNA comparison against Anatolian and hunter-gatherer reference genomes; it is not itself a named archaeological culture with a fixed artifact typology, nor a linguistic community, nor a self-conscious ancient people. The population's Anatolian-farmer genetic signature should not be equated with any specific Neolithic language or with any single archaeologically defined culture — the relationship between this genetic population and the archaeological Neolithic "package" (pottery styles, settlement forms) requires separate argument on a case-by-case basis, not automatic identity.

## Historiography

The genetic dissection of early European farmer ancestry, and its falsification of Cavalli-Sforza's demic-diffusion model, is a signature result of the 2010s ancient-DNA turn in archaeogenetics, driven by petrous-bone extraction methods that dramatically improved DNA yield from ancient skeletal remains. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]] for the full account.
