# Shijiahe Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Shijiahe culture (2500–2000 BC) succeeds Qujialing culture (3400–2500 BC) on the Jianghan Plains of the middle Yangzi River region, an area bounded by the Dabie Mountains to the east, Lake Dongting to the south, the Three Gorges to the west, and the Nanyang Basin to the north (Liu & Chen 2012). Qujialing and Shijiahe together comprise nearly 2,000 identified sites, with at least nine walled sites among them. The type site, Shijiahe (Tianmen, Hubei), is the largest, at ~120 ha core area.

## Material Signature

Walled sites in the Qujialing–Shijiahe sequence were built by digging wide moats and piling the excavated soil into walls, sited at the juncture of high ground and floodplain, with residential areas on the high ground inside. Most walls date to the late Qujialing era; sites were generally abandoned after the middle Shijiahe stage. Sizes range 7.8–120 ha. The Shijiahe site cluster itself comprises 40+ sites across 8 km² between two rivers: it began as a small Daxi-period village, grew into a large walled settlement during the late Qujialing period, and was abandoned in the late Shijiahe period. Its core (~120 ha) is enclosed by earth walls, with a smaller moated enclosure (up to 100 m wide) in the northeast (Wang 2003; Zhang, C. 2003).

Craft-production areas at Dengjiawan and Tanjialing yielded houses, pits, ritual areas, over 100 tombs, and two pits containing thousands of small clay figurines (birds, fish, chickens, dogs, sheep, turtles, pigs, elephants, tigers, and humans holding fish), interpreted as production waste. Tens of thousands of red pottery cups were recovered at Sanfangwan; a jade/stone workshop with hundreds of spindle whorls was found at Luojiabailing; specialized pottery production is indicated at Xiaojiawuji, where early Shijiahe burials contained over 100 identical pottery vessels.

During the late Shijiahe phase (ca. 2200–2000 BC), urn burial with jade grave goods was practiced; at Xiaojiawuji, jade counts per burial ranged from none to several dozen, with the wealthiest urn burial (W6) holding 56 jade items, apparently made locally from similar raw material. Shijiahe jade forms — anthropomorphic heads, cong tubes, birds, eagles, dragons, cicadas, and phoenix motifs — mostly parallel jade forms from the contemporary [[liangzhu-culture]] far to the east; Liu and Chen (2012) read this parallel, together with Shijiahe's outer-wall/inner-enclosure site plan and craft specialization (both resembling the Mojiaoshan cluster), as suggesting "a strong cultural influence from the east" — an attributed `associated_with` relationship between two independently developing regional traditions, not a claim of shared identity.

## Distribution and Chronology

Ca. 3400–2000 BC (Qujialing phase 3400–2500 BC, Shijiahe phase 2500–2000 BC) on the Jianghan Plains, middle Yangzi. Shijiahe is `preceded_by` [[daxi-culture]] via the intervening Qujialing culture, and ended around 2000 BC with the abandonment of the large earth enclosures and a shrinkage in settlement size and number.

## Lifeways

Craft specialization (pottery, jade, clay figurines) at named production centers, walled/moated settlement organization, and status-differentiated urn burial with variable jade grave-good counts are documented for the Shijiahe phase.

## Relations and Successions

Unlike [[liangzhu-culture|Liangzhu's]] complete disappearance, Shijiahe's ending shows a population decrease rather than total collapse, and some late sites exhibit Central-Plain Longshan material-culture traits. Some scholars (Zhang, C. 2003) attribute the Shijiahe decline to southward expansion of the Longshan culture producing intergroup warfare — an attributed, contested causal claim. Liu and Chen's broader framing situates Longshan, [[liangzhu-culture|Liangzhu]], [[taosi]], and Shijiahe together as hierarchically organized polities competing regionally through walled centers, prestige-goods exchange, and intergroup warfare, with political control, ritual power, and material wealth concentrated in small elite groups — a chiefdom-type/proto-state framing they distinguish from a settled "state" classification (see [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]]). Liu and Chen further note that Taosi and Liangzhu, "the most developed complex societies" of the period, both formed highly stratified political systems with ritually legitimated elite status yet both vanished from the archaeological record by the end of the third millennium BC — while Shijiahe's ending was comparatively gradual.

## Identity Cautions

Shijiahe (and its Qujialing antecedent) is an archaeological-culture construct defined by settlement morphology, ceramic and jade assemblages, and craft-production evidence at excavated sites. It is not evidence of a self-identifying people, a language, or a genetic population. The "strong cultural influence from the east" (Liangzhu) claim is an attributed material-parallel argument, not an assertion of population movement or shared ethnic identity — no genetic or linguistic evidence for such movement is reported in the source consulted.

## Historiography

Qujialing and Shijiahe are established regional constructs in the middle-Yangzi Neolithic sequence, extensively documented through decades of excavation on the Jianghan Plains; the culture is a central case in ongoing debates over Late Neolithic Chinese social complexity and its relationship (or lack thereof) to state formation.
