# Oneota Culture

Late prehistoric Upper Midwest archaeological construct (Minnesota, Iowa, Wisconsin, Illinois and related areas) treated in [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner 2021]] Ch. 7. **Etic** — not an ethnonym.

## Definition and Construct History

Oneota labels late prehistoric **village-farming** traditions of the Prairie Peninsula / Upper Midwest, contemporaneous with and interacting with Mississippian and other neighbors. Part of Milner’s northern sedentary-villager mosaic with [[fort-ancient-culture|Fort Ancient]], Monongahela, and Owasco–Iroquois.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Milner 2021):**

- Heavy maize reliance; permanent villages; settlement clumping; often palisades.
- Obtained Mississippian objects that sometimes traveled far; shared some mortuary practices across cultural lines (e.g. pots and mussel-shell spoons with the dead among culturally distinct Illinois groups).
- Few large platform mounds; small earth piles / limited ranking in many mortuary contexts.
- **[[norris-farms-36|Norris Farms No. 36]]** (~1300 CE): well-documented Oneota-related cemetery with high violent-trauma rates — key bioarchaeological case for late prehistoric warfare.

## Distribution and Chronology

Upper Midwest late prehistory into contact. Affected by the same fourteenth-century climate unpredictability and conflict that emptied much of the midcontinental “Vacant Quarter” and dissolved northward Mississippian expansions (Milner synthesis: climate volatility + social forces, not cold alone).

## Lifeways

Agricultural villagers often classified as **tribes** without permanent inherited leadership (Milner); decisions by consensus with situational influence. Intergroup violence significant in some regions (Norris Farms).

## Relations and Successions

- Interaction with [[mississippian-culture]] (objects, ideas).
- Parallel to [[fort-ancient-culture]].
- Post-contact transformations into historic nations — ethnic equations require separate attributed argument.

## Identity Cautions

Do not equate Oneota material culture with a single historic nation or language family without attributed argument.

## Historiography

Culture-historical construct; bioarchaeology and settlement archaeology densify conflict and interaction narratives.
