## Definition and Construct History

"Early Harappan" is the label Gregory L. Possehl (2002) applies to Stage Three of his
[[indus-age|Indus Age]] periodization, spanning roughly 3200–2600 BCE. It comprises
four regional archaeological phases treated by Possehl as broadly contemporaneous
rather than as sequential stages of one another:

- **Amri-Nal** — Sindh, Baluchistan, and north Gujarat; first defined following
  excavations by N. G. Majumdar (December 1929–January 1930), with definitive
  excavation by Jean-Marie Casal (1959–1962) at the type site of Amri; the Nal
  ceramic component was explored separately by Aurel Stein and B. de Cardi in
  southern Baluchistan.
- **Kot Dijian** — northern Sindh and beyond; defined by F. A. Khan's excavations at
  [[kot-diji|Kot Diji]] (1955).
- **Damb Sadaat** — central Baluchistan and the Quetta Valley, including the sites of
  Quetta Miri and Mundigak (Helmand drainage, Afghanistan).
- **Sothi-Siswal** — the eastern region, following the Sarasvati/Ghaggar-Hakra
  drainage into Punjab and Haryana, with one outlying site (Nawanbans) in the
  Ganga-Yamuna doab; attested at [[kalibangan|Kalibangan]] and [[rakhigarhi|Rakhigarhi]] prior to their Mature Harappan occupations.

Possehl states explicitly (drawing on general anthropological archaeology) that a
"phase" of this kind functions much like the older notion of an "archaeological
culture," while flagging that the discipline now recognizes a gap between lived
culture and archaeological reconstruction from material remains — the basis for this
page's `construct_status: conventional`: the Early Harappan grouping is a widely used,
serviceable analytic label, but not a claim about a bounded, self-conscious ancient
people.

The Early Harappan is marked, per Possehl, by geographical expansion (onto the Potwar
Plateau, into Indian Punjab, Haryana, Rajasthan, western Uttar Pradesh, and Gujarat)
but a "minimum of technological change" relative to the preceding Stage Two. Chronology
throughout rests on radiocarbon series; Possehl does not state calibration status
explicitly in the ranges consulted for this ingest, and dates should be treated as
approximate pending cross-check against calibrated series elsewhere in the source.

## Material Signature

- **Amri-Nal**: fine wares, light red/buff fired, red or buff slips often with black
  paint, geometric designs early and curvilinear later; open bowls, jars, and tall
  vases with featureless rims. White paint over a black slip is a hallmark shared with
  Nal ware and characteristic of the Early Harappan generally. Settlement data: 164
  known sites, average size 3.67 ha, estimated total settled area 610.56 ha. Possehl
  interprets the Amri/Nal ceramic split as evidence of a seasonal transhumance
  pattern — summers in the highlands (Nal-style ceramics), winters in the Indus
  floodplain (Amri-style) — an attributed interpretation, not a settled finding.
- **Kot Dijian**: a distinct ceramic assemblage from Amri-Nal, though sharing some
  simple forms; 111 known sites, average size 6.31 ha, estimated total settled area
  700.06 ha.
- **Damb Sadaat**: slipped surfaces pared to thin walls; "Quetta Ware" (painted, the
  most characteristic type) and Faiz Mohammad Grey Ware (double-fired, oxidizing then
  reducing); 37 known sites, average size 2.64 ha, estimated total 97.54 ha.
- **Sothi-Siswal**: 165 known sites, average size 4.28 ha, estimated total 705.87 ha;
  two sites exceed 20 ha.
- **Gujarat expansion**: sites including Dholavira, Surkotada, Moti Pipli, the Santhli
  sites, and Nagwada show ceramics blending Amri-Nal and Kot Dijian features; Padri,
  Somnath, and Loteshwar show even earlier, distinct pre-Early-Harappan pottery
  traditions, flagged by Possehl as an open research question.

Possehl reports little evidence for social differentiation, craft or career
specialization, or public/political-ideological architecture during the Early
Harappan — a marked contrast with the [[harappan-civilization|Mature Harappan]] that
follows, and used by him as the developmental baseline against which the
transition to urbanization is measured. Population nonetheless grows steadily from
the beginnings of food production through the "threshold of civilization."

## Distribution and Chronology

See the Indus Age stage table on [[indus-age]] for the full periodization context.
Radiocarbon evidence from [[kalibangan|Kalibangan]] places the earliest Mature
Harappan dates in the region at approximately 2600 BCE, marking the transition out of
the Early Harappan stage at that site.

## Lifeways

Subsistence during this stage continued the food-producing economy documented at
[[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] (barley- and wheat-based agriculture, pastoralism), without the
urban infrastructure, standardized weights, or writing system that mark the Mature
Harappan. Settlement was regionally dispersed across the four phases above, with
population growing steadily but without evidence of the political-ideological
apparatus Possehl later attributes to the Mature Harappan (see [[harappan-civilization]]).

## Relations and Successions

`succeeded_by: [[harappan-civilization]]`. Possehl documents a marked discontinuity at
the Early Harappan/Mature Harappan boundary: settlement-abandonment data from
Cholistan show 33 of 37 Kot Dijian sites abandoned, with 132 of 136 Mature Harappan
sites founded on virgin soil (a similar, less dramatic pattern appears in Sindh) —
interpreted by Possehl as evidence of deliberate resettlement rather than continuous
reoccupation of Early Harappan places. An unusual, temporally clustered burning
horizon is attested at [[kot-diji|Kot Diji]], Gumla, Amri, and Nausharo specifically at
this transition; Possehl reasons (without asserting warfare, which he calls an
"outmoded" default explanation) that a pattern clustered in time across sites suggests
"a common cause," while S. P. Gupta is recorded as favoring natural calamity for at
least the Kalibangan disruption.

Shaffer and Lichtenstein (1989), extensively quoted by Possehl, propose that the
Mature Harappan arose around 2600–2500 BCE as a rapid **fusion** of Early Harappan
groups they label (using their own interpretive terminology) "Bagor," "Hakra," and
"Kot Diji" — a fusion they estimate as possibly complete within roughly a century.
Possehl states plainly: "we do not know what these archaeological assemblages
represent in terms of ethnicity" — Shaffer and Lichtenstein's "ethnic groups" language
is their own interpretive label, not an established ethnic-identity claim, and is
recorded here via `associated_with`, never as settled identity.

## Identity Cautions

Possehl explicitly warns against "Kot Diji people" or "Amri-Nal folk" style reasoning
that infers a bounded, self-conscious ancient people directly from an archaeological
assemblage — citing Fredrik Barth's (1969) work on ethnic boundaries as the
theoretical basis for treating material-culture zones and peoplehood as distinct
questions. The four regional Early Harappan phases are material-culture and
ceramic-distribution zones, not documented ethnic groups, and their relationship to
any single ancient language, self-identified people, or genetic population is
unestablished. As Possehl puts it regarding the diversity underlying the Mature
Harappan's emergence: "there was a diversity of peoples in the Greater Indus region
at the time," but the four phase-assemblages should not be read as a one-to-one map
of that diversity.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Regionalization Era, Kot Diji, Harappa Period 2

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998, following [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]]’s Indus Valley Tradition framework; see also [[indus-valley-tradition]], [[indus-age]].)*

**Regionalization Era and “Early Harappan.”** In the Shaffer/Kenoyer scheme, the later **Regionalization Era** (commonly Early Harappan; Kenoyer’s tables place Regionalization / Early Harappan ca. **5000–2600 BCE**, with dense late phases ca. **3300–2600 BCE**) is **formative urbanism**: walled settlements, specific painted pottery and ornaments, seals and **rudimentary writing**, expanded trade — the initial step toward Integration-era cities. This is a longer, process-oriented envelope than Possehl’s Stage Three (3200–2600 BCE) alone; the two schemes are compatible as alternative periodizations, not competing ethnic labels.

**Shaffer’s Indus Regionalization phases (Kenoyer’s account).** Within the Indus Valley proper, Shaffer identifies four important cultural phases: **Balakot, Amri, Hakra, and Kot Diji** (highland-related Mehrgarh phases grouped with a Baluchistan tradition). **Amri Phase** (type-site Amri, Sindh): southern Pakistan, possibly to the Rann of Kutch. **Kot Diji Phase** (type-site near Mohenjo-daro): larger area — northern Indus and Saraswati plains plus parts of Baluchistan; both late Regionalization ca. 3300–2600 BCE before large urban centers. Amri and Kot Diji contribute styles later incorporated in the Integration Era. **Identity caution remains absolute:** these are archaeological phases / material-culture zones, not documented peoples, languages, or genetic populations (same four-way non-identity rule as Possehl’s warning against “Kot Diji people” reasoning above).

**Four interaction spheres.** Kenoyer documents four major Regionalization exchange spheres: (1) southern Indus plain + western highlands; (2) Kutch, Saurashtra, mainland Gujarat; (3) northern Indus and Saraswati valleys; (4) highland valleys of Baluchistan and Afghanistan — networks expanding from the seventh millennium; crossroads villages later becoming cities.

**Harappa Period 2 (2800–2600 BCE) as early urban core.** At [[harappa|Harappa]], Kenoyer’s HARP synthesis treats Period 2 / Kot Diji Phase as urban formation: settlement grows to ~**25 ha**, grid layout with large N–S (and presumably E–W) streets, raw materials (carnelian, chert, copper, shell) flowing into workshops, ceramic production continuity in one kiln area across centuries. Comparable planning appears at Kalibangan, Rehman Dheri, Nausharo, and Kot Diji. **Mold-made bricks of standard 1:2:4 proportions** first appear in the Kot Diji Phase (house and wall sizes); kiln-baked bricks follow ca. 2600 BCE. Early Indus pottery graffiti / “Early Indus scripts” on Period 2 levels (and Nausharo) include symbols identical to later Indus signs — precursors to, not yet, the full Integration-era seal script (see [[indus-script]]). Square steatite seals with perforated bosses appear at Rehman Dheri and Harappa as status markers; geometric clay/bone/ivory seals are common Regionalization types.

**Walls, drains, limited warfare evidence.** Village and town walls (sun-dried brick or stone) control access and monitor goods movement; platforms protect against floods. At Kalibangan, first evidence for latrines, drains, and washing areas anticipates Mature Harappan urban infrastructure. Kenoyer reports **no massive destruction levels or slaughtered inhabitants** at Regionalization sites; Kot Diji ash layers and Nausharo burning could be accidental; ceramic continuity argues against wholesale population replacement; copper weapons exist but no warrior/captive imagery (contrast Mesopotamia/Egypt); Harappa shows no occupational discontinuity between early settlement and Harappan Phase.

**Hierarchy without elite burials.** Multi-mound segregation (Amri, Kot Diji), house-size hierarchy (Mehrgarh, Nausharo), craft specialization, and regional artifact styles are Kenoyer’s evidence for class differentiation despite the absence of great-wealth burials (wealth kept in circulation — pattern continuing into urban cemeteries). Shared horned-headdress motifs on pottery and terracotta cakes (e.g. Kalibangan Period I) he reads as pre-urban shared beliefs aiding later Integration.

## Historiography

The Early Harappan phases were defined piecemeal across the twentieth century by
different excavators working in different regions — Majumdar and Casal at Amri, F. A.
Khan at Kot Diji, B. B. Lal and B. K. Thapar at Kalibangan, and others — rather than
as a single coordinated research program, which is part of why Possehl treats the
resulting four-phase scheme as a convenient but provisional analytic grouping rather
than a discovered ancient reality. Kenoyer’s synthesis re-embeds the same regional
assemblages in Shaffer’s **Indus Valley Tradition / Regionalization Era** vocabulary
and densifies them with HARP stratigraphy at Harappa (Period 2 urban core, early
script, brick standards). Excavation quality varies considerably across the
constituent sites and seasons; see the individual site pages ([[kot-diji]],
[[kalibangan]]) for excavation-specific reliability notes. Primary sources for this
page’s Kenoyer material: [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]]; Possehl baseline:
[[possehl-indus-civilization-2002]].

## Wright (2010) — Ravi/Hakra/Kot Diji/Amri/Sothi-Siswal phase chronology, "emerging polities," and Rehman Dheri's indigenous continuity

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Ch. 4, "An Era of Expansion and Transformation.")* Wright, drawing on and simplifying Jim Shaffer's (1992) "tradition/era/phase" terminology, divides the Pre-urban alluvial-plain sequence into regional phases with approximate radiocarbon-based date ranges: on the **Upper Indus**, Ravi (3300–2800 BCE) followed by Early Harappan/Kot Diji (2800–2600 BCE); on the **Lower Indus**, Amri (3300–2600 BCE) and Hakra (3500–3000 BCE) followed by Early Harappan/Kot Diji (3200–2600 BCE); in **Ghaggar-Hakra/Cholistan**, Hakra (3500–3000 BCE) followed by Early Harappan/Kot Diji (3000–2600 BCE); and in **Ghaggar-Hakra/Northwest India**, Early Harappan/Kot Diji and **Sothi-Siswal**, both roughly 3200–2600 BCE. She reports that Ravi ceramics closely resemble but are typologically distinct from contemporary Hakra wares in Cholistan (differing in vessel shape, rim form, temper, and production technique — Ravi hand-built versus some Hakra wheel-made; Kenoyer and Meadow 2000; Mughal 1997).

**"Age of Emerging Polities."** Wright proposes her own analytical framing term, "emerging polities," for this Pre-urban stage — explicitly her own framework rather than a finding — characterized by (1) multiple coexisting political units with no single dominant group; (2) active exchange networks in nonlocal raw materials, analogous to "peer polities" documented in other world regions; (3) increasing economic and social complexity (settlement growth in number and size, new technologies); and (4) increasing material-culture and site-planning uniformity. At Harappa itself, she reports continuous occupation from the Ravi phase (settled area 7–10 ha) through the Kot Diji phase (expanded to roughly 26 ha) to peak Urban-period extent (150+ ha), with a massive mudbrick perimeter wall, platforms, and early grid-oriented street planning already appearing in the Kot Diji phase — evidence, in her reading, of large-scale community labor and standardized measurement systems well before full urbanization.

**Rehman Dheri's indigenous continuity.** At [[rehman-dheri|Rehman Dheri]] (roughly 500 km from [[shortugai|Shortugai]], near the confluence of the Indus and Zhob rivers) — a roughly 22-hectare site with an enclosure wall (Durrani 1988) and a main street axis established from its earliest levels — Wright reports that Kot Diji-style ceramics, elsewhere a Pre-urban marker, continued to be produced there until at least 2300 BCE, with the new ceramic types associated with the Mature Harappan largely absent. She interprets this as evidence that the region retained an indigenous cultural tradition rather than being eclipsed by the expanding Indus Civilization, even though beads (lapis lazuli, turquoise) and figurines at the site attest ongoing Central Asian connections. She frames Rehman Dheri, together with the [[kulli-culture|Kulli]] domain of southern Baluchistan and the Kachi Plain settlements around Mehrgarh/Nausharo, as "boundary" or "edge" cultures not fully incorporated into the Indus Civilization but engaged in negotiated affiliation with it — a picture of the mature civilization as an expansive but non-uniform political and economic sphere, which she explicitly contrasts with Possehl's (2003) alternative suggestion that Kulli may simply be a local Indus variant rather than a genuinely separate culture.
