# Chifumbaze Complex

## Definition and Construct History

The **Chifumbaze complex** is Phillipson’s preferred archaeological label for the early Iron Age farming/metal-using package of eastern, central, and southern Africa — pottery traditions, iron metallurgy, and food production that appear in the first millennium CE. Facies names in the literature (Urewe, Kwale, Nkope, Gokomere, and others) are regional expressions of this complex (as of Phillipson 2005).

**Four-way non-identity (mandatory):** Chifumbaze is a **material-culture complex**. [[bantu-expansion|Bantu]] is a **language-family** construct. Correlation between early Bantu-speaking communities and Chifumbaze archaeology is an **argued association** in Phillipson’s synthesis (and in much of the secondary literature), not an identity of pots = people = languages = genes. State associations with `associated_with` language only.

## Material Signature

- Early iron-working (smelting and forging) co-occurring with farming in much of sub-equatorial Africa — Phillipson emphasises that south of the Sahara there was generally **no distinct Bronze/Copper Age** before iron (as of Phillipson 2005).
- Characteristic pottery facies (Urewe near the Great Lakes; Kwale coastal/eastern stream; Nkope–Gokomere and related inland/southern facies — names as used by Phillipson).
- Farming package including cereals and domestic animals where preserved; exact crop lists are regional.

## Distribution and Chronology

From Great Lakes / western stream areas into eastern and southern Africa during the first millennium CE, with accelerated change and pottery-style breaks around the eleventh century in many easterly regions (Luangwa tradition replacing Chifumbaze facies in much of Zambia/Malawi), while some westerly areas show greater continuity into later centuries (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Lifeways

Mixed farming and metallurgy; settlement density and political centralisation vary by region and century. Second-millennium successors include Mapungubwe–Great Zimbabwe sequences in the south and interlacustrine/savanna kingdoms further north — not all are “Chifumbaze” as a ceramic label (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Relations and Successions

- `associated_with` (argued) early stages of the [[bantu-expansion]] as an archaeological–linguistic correlation.
- Succeeded or transformed into later regional traditions (Luangwa, Leopard’s Kopje, Zimbabwe Tradition, etc.).
- Links to sites densified from later synthesis: [[mapungubwe]], [[great-zimbabwe]] (post-Chifumbaze florescence).

## Identity Cautions

Do not write “the Chifumbaze people” as an ethnonym. Do not treat Bantu languages, Chifumbaze pottery, iron technology, and genetic ancestry as interchangeable labels. Phillipson’s own linguistic chapter stresses contribution of Bantu comparative studies **alongside** archaeology, not replacement of one by the other (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Bellwood 2005 — comparative farming-dispersal frame

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter|Bellwood]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* ch. 5; archaeological dates **as of Bellwood 2005** / Phillipson citations he uses.)*

Bellwood adopts the Chifumbaze complex (named for early eastern African pottery styles; Phillipson 1993) as the **archaeological face** of early Iron Age agricultural expansion that he `associated_with` (argued correlation, not identity) early [[bantu-expansion|Bantu]] dispersal under the [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]].

**Stylistic sequence he reports.** Urewe oldest (~500–200 BC, west of Lake Victoria, mid/late 1st millennium BC horizon), then Kwale (Kenya), Nkope (Malawi/Zambia), Matola (Mozambique, 1st c. AD), related Early Iron Age mid-1st millennium AD in South Africa. Horizontal zones of incised/stamped decoration. Main burst may occupy only a few centuries rather than a full millennium (Phillipson 1993, 2003 as of Bellwood 2005).

**Economy package (established phases).** Cattle, sheep, goats, chicken; sorghum, pearl and finger millet; widespread iron working (rapid forest clearance); village-based coastal settlements, more pastoral inland south; high cultural homogeneity of the agro-pastoral package; some Stone Age incorporation inland (e.g. Nkope Zambia). Phillipson believes the package was probably present from Urewe onward (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Indian Ocean / SE Asian crops.** Mid-1st millennium AD Kenyan coast in Indian Ocean trade networks — possible role in arrival of bananas, taro, and greater yam. Banana phytoliths in Cameroon ~500 BC (Mindzie et al. 2001) already raise earlier Indian Ocean possibilities (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Boundary with non-Chifumbaze pottery.** SW of Kalahari / coastal SW Africa: pointed-based pottery ~2000 years ago and sheep herding ~1600 years ago **without** agriculture and **without** Chifumbaze affinity — indigenous Khoisan adoption (Sadr; Bousman), not Chifumbaze demic expansion (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Stream debate.** Huffman/Denbow emphasize secondary Kalundu / Western Stream from lower Congo–Angola after AD 100 meeting eastern Chifumbaze facies in SE Africa; Bellwood notes mapping of dual archaeological origins onto Western vs Eastern Bantu languages remains an “area of great uncertainty.” See [[bantu-expansion]] for full linguistic and genetic layers.

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — Peter Mitchell in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]] Ch. 45; also de Maret Luangwa note; Schoeman LFC terminology. **as of 2013**.)*

### Early Farming Communities terminology

Mitchell prefers **“Early Farming Communities” / “agropastoralists”** over “Early Iron Age,” arguing the latter overemphasizes iron and treats societies as static blocks rather than dynamic entities; first-millennium AD Bantu-speaking cultivators-herders-smelters transformed south-central and southern Africa, ancestral to second-millennium farmers (Pikirayi, Schoeman chapters). Schoeman similarly prefers **Late Farming Communities** over Late Iron Age for second-millennium southern Africa.

### Chifumbaze Complex definition (Mitchell summarizing Phillipson/Huffman)

Most Early Farming Community “thick” wares share style with Great Lakes **Urewe** ceramics, grouped by Phillipson (1977) as the **Chifumbaze Complex** (**Kalundu** and **Urewe** traditions; Urewe’s **Nkope** and **Kwale** branches). Huffman’s multidimensional ceramic analysis plus Eastern Bantu language affiliations of Shona (from Happy Rest/Kalundu) and Swahili (from Tana/Kwale) lead Huffman (2007) to argue Kalundu and Urewe makers **spoke Eastern Bantu**; Naviundu Tradition ceramics linked to Western Bantu. **Ceramic style ≠ tribe** (Hall 1983 caveat recorded by Mitchell); no necessary correlation with social/political entity.

### Central Cattle Pattern debate

Mitchell presents Huffman’s **Central Cattle Pattern (CCP)** as dominant model for Eastern Bantu village organization: cattle byres central; leading man at apex; bridewealth, ancestors, court politics; traced from ethnographic present through second into first millennium. Critics (Hall 1986; Badenhorst 2008–10) argue cattle were too few for bridewealth/politics early on, byres not exclusively cattle, equifinality of functional layout, and possible matriliny under hoe/caprine regimes; Huffman defends CCP via ethnohistoric consistency. **Positions recorded.**

### Expansion, trade, Bambata, subsistence limits

Farming spread by demographic increase plus forager assimilation (Vansina 1995), not pure forager uptake without migration (pace Robertson 2000); often “hopping” favorable locations then infilling. Early second-millennium replacements: Nguni and Sotho/Tswana languages from eastern Africa; north of Zambezi, 11th-c. **Luangwa** expansion of matrilineal Western Bantu replaces Kalundu ceramics (de Maret/Mitchell).

Late first-millennium specialization and **Indian Ocean** engagement (glass beads, marine shells; Schroda ivory-working) critical to complexity trajectory toward [[mapungubwe]]/[[great-zimbabwe]] (Mitchell).

**Bambata pottery debate:** traditionally hunter-gatherers/herders (Sadr 2008); Huffman (2005) identifies Bambata as earliest southern African Kalundu—dispute open. Cereal agriculture ecological limits: ≥350 mm summer rain, ≥500 mm annual, nocturnal ≥15°C—restricting settlement to miombo/savannah, attenuating along east coast to ~33.5°S. Caprines more numerous early; cattle primacy from 8th c. (not invariable); Huffman phytoliths vs Badenhorst dung-bias dispute on early cattle.

Research coverage uneven: South Africa best served; little Namibia; none Angola (Mitchell).
