# Great Basin Desert Archaic

## Definition and Construct History

The **Desert Archaic** (or **Desert West**) is the long-lived hunter-gatherer adaptation of
the Great Basin — the ~400,000 sq mi of internal drainage between the Rockies and the Sierra
Nevada — persisting from the Early Holocene to European contact, **at least 10,000 years**. As
presented by [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], it is known above all from
**stratified dry caves** (Danger, Hogup, Lovelock, Hidden Cave) whose desiccated deposits — down
to hundreds of preserved **coprolites** — document actual ancient meals.

The concept has a contested history. **Jesse Jennings**, excavating Danger Cave and drawing on
Julian Steward's ecological work, formulated the **"Desert Culture"** — a stable, multi-millennial
adaptation. Critics (Baumhoff & Heizer 1965) found the Danger Cave sequence too incomplete for
such a generalization and objected to the assumed climatic/cultural uniformity across so large an
area; the concept was eventually abandoned, even by Jennings, and recast as the **Desert (or
Western) Archaic**, aligned with the continent-wide Archaic stage. `construct_status: contested`
accordingly.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** A broad-spectrum foraging toolkit — milling stones (manos and
metates), basketry, netting, duck decoys (Lovelock Cave), projectile points seriated through
time (Thomas's Gatecliff/Elko/Rosegate/Desert series) — geared to a diet dominated by seeds,
piñon pine nuts (a storable staple harvested on multi-year abundance cycles), marsh plants
(pickleweed, cattail), waterfowl, fish, and game (deer, pronghorn, mountain sheep, rabbit). The
bow and arrow arrives late; pottery only after c. AD 1000.

## Distribution and Chronology

Fagan gives a four-period eastern/northern sequence (Jennings 1986): Paleo-Indian (before
7000 BC), Bonneville (9000–7500 BC), Wendover Early Archaic (7500–4000 BC, heavy marsh-plant
reliance), and Black Rock (4000 BC – AD 500, rising population, small maize/pottery appearing c.
AD 400–500 from the Southwest without displacing foraging). The western sequence (Elston) runs
Early / Middle / Late Archaic, the Middle Archaic marked by Lovelock Cave's lakeside adaptation.

## Lifeways

Great Basin foragers took a very broad resource range rather than specializing, because plant
food could vary as much as six-fold between wet and dry years; some groups "managed" their
environment by burning to boost seed grasses, tobacco, and game. Fagan records a genuine debate
over the **Lovelock lakeside adaptation** — a "limnosedentary" reading (Heizer & Napton: a
near-sedentary lakeshore life, coprolites showing ~90% marsh-and-lake diet) versus a
"limnomobile" reading (marsh resources too costly to process to anchor sedentism). David Hurst
Thomas's Hidden Cave work concluded both extremes were too simple — the occupants were both
foragers and collectors adapted to widely separated, storable but variable resources.

## Relations and Successions

- **Contributed to:** the [[fremont-culture|Fremont]] farming-foraging culture, which grew from a
  Desert Archaic base after AD 400.
- **The Numic spread (debated).** Historic Great Basin peoples were **Numic** (Uto-Aztecan)
  speakers (Shoshone, Paiute, Ute, Comanche). Whether Numic speakers expanded *into* the Basin
  within the last ~1,000 years (Lamb 1958; Bettinger & Baumhoff's competitive-strategy model) or
  developed *in situ* is unresolved — a standing "Numic spread" problem.

## Identity Cautions

- **Don't use historic foragers as a timeless blueprint.** Madsen and others warn against reading
  the ethnographic Great Basin straight back onto deep prehistory.
- **Independent culture area.** Radiocarbon dating showed many "Southwestern" traits actually
  derived from *earlier* Great Basin cultures — the Basin is not a mere periphery of the Southwest.

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]]; the Desert Culture debate, the
limnosedentary/limnomobile dispute, and the Numic spread are the central issues. Great Basin
research shifted from single dry-cave sequences to valley-wide settlement-subsistence studies
(O'Connell, Thomas) and evolutionary-ecology/optimal-foraging modeling. Densify from later Basin
survey work and Numic-spread genetics when ingested.
