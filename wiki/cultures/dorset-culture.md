# Dorset Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Dorset** (named from Cape Dorset, Baffin Island) is the late **Paleo-Eskimo** culture of
the eastern North American Arctic, developing out of **Pre-Dorset** around 550 BC and lasting
until roughly AD 1100 (later in refugia). As presented by
[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], it descends from the **Arctic Small Tool
tradition (ASTt)** — the microblade-using toolkit that reached Alaska c. 2000 BC and, in its
Independence I and Pre-Dorset stages, was "the first human settlement of the Eastern Arctic."
Scholars group Pre-Dorset and Dorset as "Paleoeskimos," distinct from the later
"Neoeskimos" ([[thule-culture|Thule]]/Inuit).

`construct_status: conventional`. Fagan's period term "Eskimo" is his stipulated usage, not a
neutral ethnonym; and any equation of Dorset with a historic language or people is a claim,
not a fact — enforce the four-way non-identity rule (later ancient-genome work, as of
Raghavan et al. 2014, in fact distinguishes Paleo-Inuit populations sharply from the
Thule/Inuit who replaced them).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** Rectangular semi-subterranean houses; triangular sealing
points; a ground-slate industry; distinctive harpoon-head forms (used as a seriation clock);
and a celebrated **art tradition** — lifelike carvings in antler, bone, ivory, and soapstone,
over half of them human figures or polar bears, with strong shamanistic and funerary
overtones (masks, amulets, dolls at Button Point). Notably, Dorset **lacked** dog sleds, bows
and arrows, and float-and-toggle whaling gear, hunting seals and small sea mammals at close
range with thrusting harpoons — an apparent technological narrowness beside its artistic
richness. Permafrost preservation makes Dorset far better documented than warmer-climate
Pre-Dorset.

## Distribution and Chronology

From the Foxe Basin core across the Canadian Arctic archipelago, Labrador, Newfoundland, and
Greenland. Fagan follows Maxwell's Early/Middle/Late/Terminal stages: Middle Dorset retreat
from parts of the High Arctic during cooling (Port aux Choix, Newfoundland — a Middle Dorset
harp-seal-hunting village, one midden 98% harp seal); a Late Dorset artistic "explosion" and
re-expansion into the High Arctic after c. AD 500 (Button Point possibly a ceremonial center).

## Lifeways

Sealing (at winter breathing holes) was the staple, supplemented by caribou, musk ox, walrus,
and small game. Fagan (following Maxwell) reads the rich Late Dorset art and possible
ceremonial centers as evidence of a shamanistic ritual life integrated across a wide network.
He asserts in his own voice that "some of the fundamental spiritual beliefs of modern Inuit
groups have their roots in the… Dorset people" — recorded here as an **attributed** continuity
claim, not wiki-voice fact, and one the four-way non-identity rule flags.

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from:** Pre-Dorset / the Arctic Small Tool tradition (Paleo-Eskimo).
- **Succeeded by / displaced by:** [[thule-culture|Thule]] — Dorset "reached its climax by AD
  1000" and "a century later had all but vanished." Fagan questions the "conventional"
  superior-technology takeover story: there is little evidence of sustained Dorset–Thule
  contact, though Thule borrowed some Dorset traits (snow knives, soapstone use). The two may
  have coexisted for two or three centuries.
- **Possible late survival (unresolved):** the isolated **Sadlermiut** of Southampton Island
  (extinguished by epidemic in 1902–3) yielded both Dorset and Thule artifacts — "a mystery";
  do not equate them with Dorset.

## Identity Cautions

- **Paleo-Eskimo ≠ Inuit.** Dorset is a Paleo-Eskimo culture; the Thule/Inuit who replaced it
  are a separate tradition (a distinction later genetics sharpened).
- **"Eskimo" is Fagan's period term** — recorded as such, not endorsed as current usage.

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], synthesizing Maxwell, McGhee, and
Fitzhugh; chronology rests on radiocarbon plus harpoon-head seriation and raised-beach
sequences. The Dorset–Thule transition and the fate of the Dorset people are the central
questions; post-1991 ancient-genome work (as of Raghavan et al. 2014) resolved much of the
population question in favor of near-total replacement. Densify when Arctic ancient-genome
sources are ingested.
