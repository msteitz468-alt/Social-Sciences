## Definition and Construct History

Moche (Mochica) names the art style, monumental adobe tradition, and associated material culture of Peru’s north coast in the Early Intermediate Period. As of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], Moche was "the first archaic state to arise in the northern pole of Andean civilization," "perhaps akin to a confederacy," uniting fragmented canal-based *parcialidades*. Larco Hoyle’s sequence: Cupisnique → Salinar → Virú/[[gallinazo-culture]] → Moche (five phases by spout form). Corporate style formation follows corporate body formation: late Gallinazo and early Moche are basically indistinguishable except for corporate art and ideology; kuraka coalitions of Moche and Chicama adopted Moche style as hallmark.

Scarre ed. 2013 places Moche 200 BC–AD 650 with drought/El Niño contraction and successor sites to ~AD 700–800. Moseley’s Ica-gauged EIP is ~200 BC–AD 600; he flags major upheaval "shortly before AD 600" for the following chapter.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Capital (Cerro Blanco):** Huaca de la Luna (smaller, southern, white-hill flanks) and Huaca del Sol (nearer river) ~500 m apart express consolidated, potentially dual power. Sol: today 340 × 160 m, >40 m high — largest solid-adobe structure ever in the Andes; less than half survives (Spanish diverted Río Moche to mine tombs); original giant cross plan suggested; interpreted as imperial palace and mausoleum. Construction: >100 million bricks; segmented construction; >100 makers’ marks (= communities supplying workers); multi-stage use from Phase I burial near base to Phase IV couple atop last stage. Luna: three interconnected platforms, polychrome murals (Ayapec with marked canines; spiders; anthropomorphs; captive warriors); southern platform over rock outcrop as mountain-top sacrifice stage; Uceda excavations — >dozen mature male corpses dispatched by blows, cast onto El Niño–muddied ground; primarily huaca adoratorio vs Sol as huaca sepultura — tempting dual organization at apex (Moseley). Between huacas: walled extended-family compounds, craft and noble residences; site ~1 sq. km, deposits to 7 m.

**Art and ideology (Donnan via Moseley):** fine arts rationalize kuraka rule; multi-piece molds for stirrup-spout vessels; five spout forms = five phases; naturalistic portrait heads and fine-line painting (from Phase III). After ~AD 300, corpus revolves around fewer than two dozen themes (scripture-like). **Presentation/sacrifice theme:** ritual combat among elite kuraka aiming to capture not kill; nude parade; throat-cutting; blood in goblet to warrior priest — matched by [[sipan|Sipán]] tombs (Alva) and Jequetepeque priestess graves (Donnan). Murals of captive parade at Luna and Huaca Cao Viejo (El Brujo); sacrifice at Panamarca (Nepeña). Burial theme; skeletal figures with possible mallquipavillacs. Emblematic **mace-headed pike** continues from Gallinazo. Arts never depict peasant farming; bloodshed rituals probably to placate Pacha Mama on ceremonial calendar (Moseley).

**Agriculture:** standardized fields and supervisory adobe platforms around metropolis; field work as peasant tax; commoners and kurakas as distinct classes (Moseley).

**Linguistic / genetic:** none in sources; osteology at Luna addresses manner of death, not population genetics.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Land:** Vicús (central Piura) to Río Huarmey; **sea:** island presence to Chincha Islands. Best case for unified state: Cerro Blanco–based, Chicama south through smaller valleys to Huarmey, Phase III especially IV; provincial centers Huancaco (Virú), Pampa de Los Incas (Santa), Panamarca (Nepeña). Casma skipped; limited Huarmey. Northern valleys: Sipán Phase III powerful señorío not lockstep with Cerro Blanco; Jequetepeque Phase IV largely absent despite Chicama predominance; lack of intrusive southern-style admin centers → Moseley’s impression of largely independent northern señoríos in **loose confederation** via kuraka ideology. Forced conquest vs voluntary conversion debated; arts show battles in uninhabited desert not settlements; southern realm pukaras rare ("Pax Moche" for commoners).

**Precursors:** Salinar (Cerro Arena nascent urbanism; metallurgy prerogative); Gallinazo valley consolidation, mold adobes, Gallinazo Group ~30,000 rooms. Fortifications/pukaras increase with filled land and drought (Christ–AD 200); earliest defenses in smaller southern valleys (Casma, Nepeña, Santa).

**End:** Scarre — drought AD 562–594 + El Niño flooding → contraction and ideology recast; successor sites Galindo, San José de Moro, Pampa Grande. Moseley — nature "turned pernicious" shortly before AD 600 (detail in next chapter range).

## Lifeways

Canal irrigation, adobe corporate architecture, wealth-finance monopoly of fine arts by stratified kuraka (warrior priest and presentation priestess as demigods via special creation myths). Elite exchange with Recuay (Callejón); Cajamarca ceramics at lower Jequetepeque sites. Mit’a-like modular construction continuity from Gallinazo/Preceramic.

## Relations and Successions

- From [[gallinazo-culture]] via kuraka coalition and new corporate ideology.
- Political organization contested: unified state vs coeval northern/southern states vs confederation (Scarre; Moseley leans confederation for north + stronger southern integration).
- Succeeded after Middle Horizon-era decline by Lambayeque polities and later [[chimu-culture]] in same heartland.
- Contrasts with [[nazca-culture]] confederacy/maximal-ayllu model (Silverman via Moseley) and with conservative altiplano ideology at Tiwanaku (Staff God continuity vs coastal secular kuraka focus).

## Identity Cautions

Moche ≠ single ethnic group ≠ single language ≠ necessarily single state. Sipán "Warrior Priest" matches an office in iconography, not a personal name or dynasty in wiki voice. Presentation Theme confirmation is about ritual practice, not genetic uniformity.

## Historiography

Larco Hoyle phasing; Donnan thematic analysis; Alva Sipán; Uceda Luna; Bourget skeletal sacrifice (Scarre). Debate shifted toward valley-to-valley diversity. Collection: Scarre + Moseley 2001; primary monographs not fully ingested.
