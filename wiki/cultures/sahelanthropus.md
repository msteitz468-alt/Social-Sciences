## Definition and Construct History

*Sahelanthropus tchadensis* is the formal name of a close-to-seven-million-year-old fossil taxon discovered in 2001 in Chad (central-western Africa, west of the East African Rift), introduced in [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012|Ian Tattersall’s *Masters of the Planet* (2012)]] as one of the late-Miocene contenders for “earliest hominid” status. The informal specimen name is **Toumaï** (“hope of life”). Published material reported by Tattersall consists of a **badly crushed cranium** plus **partial mandibles** (as of Tattersall 2012).

Until near the turn of the twenty-first century, Tattersall notes, the known hominid fossil record extended only about three to four million years; *Sahelanthropus* belongs among the post-1990s finds that push claimed bipedal hominids back toward the molecular-clock window for the human–chimpanzee split (typically estimated ~5–7 Ma; paleontologists often find molecular ages a bit low — as of Tattersall 2012). [[tattersall-ian|Tattersall]] frames recognition of the earliest hominids as a standing problem: thick molar enamel and canine reduction (loss of upper-canine honing on the lower premolar) are perennially used as hominid markers but also occur in Miocene apes, so the field has returned full circle to claimed **ground bipedality** as the most notable common criterion among “earliest hominid” candidates (attributed, Tattersall 2012).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Morphology (as of Tattersall 2012).** Toumaï combined a **small apelike braincase** with a **large flattish face** unlike younger hominids and living apes. Dental traits reported: molars with **moderately thick enamel**, **reduced canines**, and **no lower-premolar honing**. The load-bearing bipedality claim rests on the **foramen magnum**: described as shifted underneath the skull so as to face largely **downward** (consistent with upright carriage of the head).

**Bipedality contested.** Because the skull is crushed, orientation of the foramen magnum has been disputed. A **CT virtual reconstruction** gave the discoverers grounds for a **plausible biped** reading; **skeptics remain**. Tattersall’s own judgment: final settlement requires a **body skeleton**, not cranium alone (attributed, Tattersall 2012).

**No genetic signature** is reported. **No linguistic** evidence exists or is claimed. Material culture is **not** attributed to this taxon.

## Distribution and Chronology

Known (as of Tattersall 2012) from Chad deposits dated near **~7 Ma**. Geography matters historiographically: discovery **west of the Rift** challenged Rift-centric sampling narratives of early hominid origins. Associated fauna, Tattersall reports, suggest a **well-watered environment with nearby forest** — consistent with his broader claim that all known “very early hominid” fossils come from **thickly wooded or mixed habitats**, not from a world forced into bipedality by wholesale grassland conversion (as of Tattersall 2012).

## Lifeways

Tattersall’s ecological inference (attributed): *Sahelanthropus* was **at least a part-time biped** that subsisted on a **fairly generalized plant-based diet** (fruit, leaves, nuts, seeds, roots), plus insects and small vertebrates. This is paleoecological reconstruction from associated fossils and dental morphology, not observed behavior. No stone tools, hearths, or other archaeological signature are linked to the taxon in this source.

## Relations and Successions

- `associated_with` the problem of recognizing earliest members of the human family (Hominidae / “hominids” in Tattersall’s usage — see his terminological note on hominid vs. hominin).
- Chronologically precedes [[orrorin]] (~6 Ma), [[ardipithecus]] (*Ar. kadabba* 5.8–5.2 Ma; *Ar. ramidus* 4.4 Ma), and the definitive bipedal package of [[australopithecus]] (especially *A. anamensis* from ~4.2 Ma).
- **No** secure `descends_from` or `preceded_by` chain is asserted: Tattersall treats early putative hominids as a bush of candidates, not a ladder. Multiple ape lineages may have stood upright; only one was the progenitor of later hominids (attributed thesis).
- Dental “hominid markers” (thick enamel, reduced canines) are explicitly **non-unique** — matched outside Hominidae (e.g. Miocene forms such as *Oreopithecus*) (as of Tattersall 2012).

## Identity Cautions

*Sahelanthropus* is an **etic paleontological taxon** defined from fragmentary, crushed cranial/mandibular material — **not** a people, language, archaeological culture, or genetically sampled population. “Toumaï” is a specimen nickname, not an ethnonym. **Hominid status is contested**: bipedality is inferred from foramen-magnum orientation on a deformed skull; without postcrania the claim remains open (as of Tattersall 2012). Do not equate this taxon with later [[australopithecus]] or with living human groups. Four-way non-identity: fossil morphology ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people.

## Historiography

*Sahelanthropus* enters the literature only in 2001–02, decades after the classical australopith and *Homo erectus* discoveries that structured twentieth-century paleoanthropology (Tattersall notes the fossil record was discovered roughly **inverse** to geological age: Neanderthals mid-nineteenth century, *H. erectus* later, australopiths later still). The Chad find expanded geography beyond the East African Rift and chronology toward the molecular split window. Debates over CT reconstruction vs. crushing artifacts, and over whether bipedality is the right earliest-hominid criterion, place *Sahelanthropus* squarely in the live “who counts as earliest hominid?” dispute rather than as a settled ancestral node.
