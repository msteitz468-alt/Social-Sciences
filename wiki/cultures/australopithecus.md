## Definition and Construct History

Leakey & Lewin (1977) group the genus into two forms sharing a common ancestor with the Homo lineage (per the book, [[ramapithecus]] — a claim since superseded): a gracile species, *Australopithecus africanus*, and a robust species, *A. boisei* in East Africa (with a southern African form separately named *A. robustus*) (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). The type discovery was the "Taung baby": late in 1924 a miners' explosion at a limestone quarry near Taung, on the South Africa/Botswana border, exposed the fossilized skull of an infant, sent to anatomist [[dart-raymond]] at the University of the Witwatersrand. Dart identified the skull as showing a relatively large brain for a non-human primate, hominid-like teeth and facial proportions, and a skull-to-spine angle consistent with upright posture, publishing the identification in *Nature* at the start of 1925.

Dart's identification was, per the source, largely ignored or scorned by much of the British, French, and German scientific establishment for two decades, in part because the Piltdown Man forgery — "discovered" in England in 1912 and not exposed as a hoax (a modern human skull combined with an orangutan jaw) until roughly forty years later — had lent spurious support to a rival "big brain first" model of human evolution incompatible with Taung's small-brained, upright-postured anatomy. Within twenty years of Taung, further "ape-men" fossils were recovered at four more southern African sites by Dart and others, including Robert Broom, John Robinson, and later Philip Tobias. Taung, Sterkfontein, and Makapansgat yielded the gracile form (*A. africanus*, named by Dart); two other southern African sites yielded the larger, stockier robust form, named *A. robustus* as the southern African equivalent of East Africa's *A. boisei*.

The East African robust lineage's most famous specimen — a skull found by [[leakey-mary]] at [[olduvai-gorge]] on 17 July 1959, while [[leakey-louis]] rested in camp with flu — was originally named *Zinjanthropus boisei* and had a cranial capacity of about 530cc versus roughly 1400cc for modern humans; it is now classified as *Australopithecus boisei* (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). A separate jaw found by Kamoya Kimeu at Lake Natron showed worn premolars, suggesting a rougher, grittier diet, and small incisors relative to the rest of the dentition.

## Material / Anatomical Signature

Australopithecine thigh bones show a smaller femoral head and a longer, flatter femoral neck than in *Homo*, which Leakey & Lewin interpret as evidence of less mechanically effective bipedal walking than modern humans (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977); the authors nonetheless state the genus was "quite definitely" composed of habitual upright walkers, not knuckle-walkers. At most southern African sites other than Taung, particularly younger deposits, excavators found stones apparently shaped as crude tools for crushing or scraping, but this material was not treated by the authors as evidence of a developed stone-tool industry belonging to the genus.

Raymond Dart separately proposed an "osteodontokeratic culture" hypothesis: that *Australopithecus* at Makapansgat used bone, tooth, and horn implements — jaws as saws, limb bones as clubs — tied by Dart to an alleged carnivorous, even cannibalistic, way of life (attributed, Dart). C.K. (Bob) Brain's experimental taphonomic studies of bone weathering and scavenger damage are presented in the source as having "effectively demolished" this hypothesis, showing the Makapansgat bone assemblage matched ordinary leftovers from leopard and hyena kills rather than a deliberate tool kit; the five main southern African australopithecine sites are accordingly interpreted in the source as scavenger-lair cave deposits, not hominid living or home-base sites. This bone/tooth/horn material is `associated_with` *Australopithecus* only through cave co-deposition, never as an intentional industry the genus manufactured — the osteodontokeratic refutation is the source's own worked example of why species and tool-industry claims must be kept separate.

## Distribution and Chronology

Robust australopithecines at Lake Turkana (East Africa) were, per the source, substantially stockier than their southern African counterparts, indicating regional variation within the robust lineage. Dating of the southern African cave hominid levels rested, as of 1977, on faunal correlation — comparing the evolutionary stage of associated animal fossils — which the authors call "capricious" and explicitly "not firm"; estimates ranged from just over one million to nearly three million years, with Kromdraai the youngest site and Makapansgat the oldest. Both major species are described as disappearing from the fossil record around one million years ago, though the authors note it "cannot be ruled out" that small, isolated populations of *A. boisei* survived to as recently as roughly 300,000 years ago — explicitly labeled a speculative "tantalizing possibility," given the lack of well-excavated sites spanning that intermediate range (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977).

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Leakey & Lewin's ecological reading (attributed) treats the two species as occupying distinct dietary niches despite both walking upright: *A. boisei* is read as a specialist, heavy-jawed root- and seed-eater living in troops of roughly twenty, likened by the authors to a warthog's feeding niche; *A. africanus* is read as a more omnivorous, mobile forager using (but, per the source, not manufacturing) tools, living in larger social groups and occupying an ecological niche the authors liken to that of modern baboons. The authors propose *A. africanus* went extinct somewhat earlier than *A. boisei*, possibly displaced by competition from emerging baboon populations and from [[homo-erectus]] — but the source is explicit that "there is no evidence" the australopithecines were hunted to extinction by *Homo erectus*, flagging this as an unsupported popular notion rather than a documented cause.

## Relations and Successions

Per Leakey & Lewin (1977), *Australopithecus* shares a proposed common ancestor with the *Homo* lineage in [[ramapithecus]] — a descent claim now superseded along with the Ramapithecus construct itself (see [[ramapithecus]]). At [[olduvai-gorge]], the source reports *A. boisei*, *A. africanus*, and [[homo-habilis]] as having occupied the lake shore contemporaneously, and more broadly the authors' Koobi Fora thesis holds that around 2.5 million years ago, four distinct hominid types coexisted by the lake margin, differing in stature and especially in behavior (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). *Australopithecus* is `associated_with` but not identical to [[homo-habilis]] and [[homo-erectus]], which the source treats as contemporaries rather than descendants for at least part of the overlap period — an explicit break from a simple linear "ladder" model of human evolution.

## Identity Cautions

"*Australopithecus*" denotes a genus defined by skeletal and dental morphology across multiple southern and East African fossil populations; it names neither a single population, a language, nor a self-conscious people, and the gracile/robust split (and the further southern-African/East-African regional split within the robust form) indicates real morphological diversity within the genus, not a single uniform "australopithecine" type. The osteodontokeratic-culture episode is a caution against reading co-deposited bone material as evidence of manufactured technology absent taphonomic controls.

## Historiography

Dart's 1925 identification of the Taung skull was resisted for roughly two decades by the European scientific establishment, a resistance the source attributes partly to the Piltdown forgery's support for a "big-brain-first" evolutionary model that Taung's small-brained, upright anatomy contradicted — an instructive case of a fabricated primary specimen (Piltdown) actively delaying acceptance of a genuine one (Taung). Bob Brain's later taphonomic refutation of Dart's own osteodontokeratic-culture hypothesis is presented in the source as a methodological turning point, demonstrating experimentally that scavenger and weathering processes could produce assemblages easily mistaken for deliberate tool kits — a caution the wiki carries forward in keeping species pages and tool-industry claims separate throughout this ingest. Philip Tobias reported the recovery of a partial cranium (a possible early *Homo* specimen) in southern Africa in the fall of 1976, noted in the source as a then-recent development still being assessed (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977).

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein's synthesis substantially expands and updates the Leakey & Lewin layer above. He treats
the australopithecines as **"bipedal apes rather than primitive humans"**: bipedal (the trait
that "guarantees their hominid status") but small-brained (~400–550 cc), markedly sexually
dimorphic, maturing at an apelike rate, with **no archaeological evidence of technology** ("tool
use was probably no more developed than among chimpanzees"). He recognizes at least **seven
species across one to three genera** (*Ardipithecus*, *Australopithecus*, ***Paranthropus***):
*A. ramidus* (4.4 Ma), *A. anamensis* (the oldest with conclusively demonstrated bipedalism,
~4.2–3.9 Ma), *A. afarensis* (Hadar/[[laetoli]], 3.4–2.9 Ma; the Laetoli footprints), *A.
africanus* ([[sterkfontein]], Taung), *A. aethiopicus* (the "Black Skull" KNM-WT 17000, ~2.5 Ma),
and the **robust australopithecines *P. robustus* and *P. boisei*** — small-bodied but with a
hugely enlarged masticatory apparatus, extinct 1.2–0.7 Ma. Klein adopts Broom's *Paranthropus*
for the robusts, reflecting their status as a specialized side-branch. Isotope/strontium data
suggest *P. robustus* had a "catholic diet," not strict vegetarianism. He argues **_Homo_ made
most or all of the Oldowan tools** found alongside *Paranthropus* (e.g. at [[swartkrans]]), so
the robusts made few if any. Phylogeny is explicitly unresolved — rival equally-parsimonious
cladograms disagree on whether *A. africanus* lies on the *Homo* line. Southern-African cave
breccias were historically dug with dynamite and can only be faunally dated — an excavation-quality
caution. See [[oldowan-industry]], [[bipedalism]], [[cladistics]].
