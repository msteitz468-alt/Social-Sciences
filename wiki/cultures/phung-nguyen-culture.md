## Definition and Construct History

Phung Nguyen is the conventional Vietnamese archaeological phase for late Neolithic (pre-bronze or essentially aceramic-bronze) farming communities of the Bac Bo middle country — elevated terrain above the Red–Black confluence and adjacent stream systems — dating from the late third to mid second millennium BC (as of Higham 2002; R2-040). It is the first stage of the locally continuous sequence Phung Nguyen → Dong Dau → Go Mun → [[dong-son-culture]] that Vietnamese scholars use to argue indigenous cultural continuity into the Iron Age (Higham 2002; R4-005, R4-006).

Sites are typically 1–3 ha on elevated terrain near stream confluences; stratigraphy is shallow; Co Loa’s initial settlement is placed ~2000 BC within this frame (R2-040). Higham reports no bronze from classic Phung Nguyen contexts: the industry is stone-adze heavy (777 quadrangular vs 4 shouldered among counted specimens; 1,138 adzes or fragments total), with chisels, whetstones, nephrite rings, spindle whorls, and bone harpoons (R2-040).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Diagnostic package: black pattern-incised pottery with S-meander and “geometric asymmetry” designs paralleling Yunnan Neolithic Baiyancun/Dadunzi (R2-041); abundant quadrangular stone adzes; nephrite jewellery; late Phung Nguyen nephrite workshop at Trang Kenh ~1650–1500 BC; Lung Hoa deep ledge graves; *ge* halberd and *yazhang* jade blades matching southern China and Sanxingdui/Erlitou — continued contact with Yangzi and Huanghe complex societies even late in Neolithic expansion (Ha Van Tan 1993 via Higham; R2-041; R3-006). Dong Dau basal material is ascribed to the final Phung Nguyen sub-phase; Dong Dau pottery retains Phung Nguyen stylistic origins while developing a vigorous local bronze industry that copies many Phung Nguyen stone forms in metal (Trinh Sinh 1977 via Higham; R3-031).

**Language.** No inscriptions. Higham’s (2002) language-farming model proposes mainland expansion of early Austroasiatic speakers along river routes including the Red River to Phung Nguyen, parallel to Austronesian maritime expansion, citing cognate rice vocabulary from Munda to Vietnamese (R2 claims synthesis; R1-029). This is an **attributed macro-historical hypothesis**, not a demonstration that Phung Nguyen sites = Austroasiatic speech communities. Use `associated_with`, not identity.

**Genetics.** Not addressed (as of Higham 2002).

## Distribution and Chronology

Concentrated in Bac Bo middle country where prehistoric delta flooding pushed early settlement onto elevated terrain; later expansion into upper and middle delta accompanies the Dong Son agricultural intensification (Gourou climate context via Higham; R4-004). Coastal Hoa Loc metallurgy equates with latest Phung Nguyen inland sub-phase as sea level fell (R3-034). Mekong tributary survey (1990–1) found small mounds with the same black pattern-incised pottery as Baiyancun and Phung Nguyen — a continuum without major excavated, securely dated anchors (excavation lacuna; R2-042).

Chronological placement late 3rd–mid 2nd millennium BC (Higham 2002) sits within his broader five-phase framework: Neolithic rice agriculture originating middle Yangzi 8000–6000 BC, reaching Red River/Khorat/Central Thailand 2500–2000 BC (R1-029). Shang jades/bronzes in late Viet Nam/Lingnan contexts mark the transition toward local bivalve-mould bronze casting (R3-006).

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Farming communities with heavy investment in stone woodworking tools (adze counts), spinning (spindle whorls), and nephrite ornament production/exchange. Rice agriculture is inferred from the regional Neolithic model and later Dong Dau rice evidence more than from dense Phung Nguyen palaeobotanical reports in Higham 2002 — treat plant domestication claims as regional synthesis, not site-by-site proof. Settlement size (~1–3 ha) is two orders of magnitude smaller than later Dong Son Co Loa (~600 ha outermost ramparts — “about 200 times the area of Phung Nguyen”; R4-008).

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by` long-established Bac Bo hunter-gatherer sequences ([[hoabinhian]] and related coastal groups). `succeeded_by` Dong Dau (Early Bronze Age, local casting, pediform axe beginning) → Go Mun (intensified bronze; pottery motifs Ha Van Tan sees as models for Dong Son drum decoration; R3-033) → [[dong-son-culture]]. Vietnamese scholars stress decorative continuity from Phung Nguyen pottery to Dong Son bronzes against earlier European exotic-origin models (R4-006). Shang-period exotic jades mark exchange with Chinese early states without equating Phung Nguyen communities with Chinese polities (R3-006).

## Identity Cautions

Phung Nguyen is a **material-culture / phase construct**, not a people, language, or genetic population. Do not equate:

1. Phung Nguyen pottery–stone assemblage  
2. early Austroasiatic speech (Higham’s farming-language model)  
3. later Vietnamese ethnonym or Lac/Viet polities of Chinese texts  
4. any aDNA cluster (absent as of Higham 2002)

Jade and ceramic parallels with Yunnan and the Central Plains indicate **exchange and contact**, not colonisation or ethnic identity. Co Loa’s later Dong Son-period ramparts are not Phung Nguyen urbanism.

## Historiography

Defined within Vietnamese prehistoric systematics under the Institute of Archaeology tradition that succeeded EFEO fieldwork; Higham 2002 synthesises that sequence for English-language readers and embeds it in a Yangzi-origin Neolithic expansion narrative. The continuity Phung Nguyen → Dong Son is both an archaeological seriation and a nationalist historiography of indigenous Vietnamese origins — Higham records the continuity claim without reducing the archaeology to propaganda (see [[dong-son-culture]] Historiography).
