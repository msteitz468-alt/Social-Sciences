# Celtic Languages

## Definition and Construct History

The Celtic branch of [[indo-european-languages]], reconstructed comparatively and attested in Continental epigraphy (Celtiberian, Gaulish, Lepontic, and poorly known Middle Danube Celtic) and in Insular medieval literatures (Irish, Welsh, and related). Cunliffe (1997) Ch. 2 summarizes Celtic’s place in IE, the imperfect mapping from language to classical ethnonyms and archaeological cultures, and the historiography of Celtic linguistics.

Cunliffe reports that Celtic is closest to the Italic languages; some linguists postulate a common Italo-Celtic origin broadly parallel to other major ancestral groups. Absolute dates for Celtic’s distinction are debated: an extreme view places main Celtic-speaking groups in place by c.3000 BCE; a more conventional view has Italic and Celtic separating and developing in their later regions c.1300–800 BCE before outward movements of Celts and Latins (R1-048). Absolute linguistic dating is contested under this wiki’s Chronological Uncertainty Protocol; these figures are **attributed** to the debate Cunliffe reports, not wiki-voice chronology.

**Construct status:** robust as a comparative language family; contested in its equation with classical *Keltoi*/Gauls or with [[la-tene-culture]] packages. Cunliffe’s load-bearing methodological claim: if a Celt is defined as a speaker of Celtic, linguistics **uncouples** “Celt” from archaeologically defined La Tène — the language was spoken considerably earlier and over a more extensive area than La Tène culture (R1-060, R5-042).

Source densification: [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]]; specialist linguistic monographs remain outstanding for deeper phonology and reconstruction.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

### Linguistic

**Continental Celtic** (as Cunliffe summarizes). Known from place and personal names, coins, and relatively few inscriptions — three, possibly four languages (R1-050):

| Variety | Zone (Cunliffe 1997) | Notes as presented |
|---|---|---|
| Celtiberian | Centre, west, north of Iberia | Earliest inscriptions third century BCE; Greek writers show Celts there earlier; Botorrita bronze (decipherment announced 1971) key text (R1-050, R1-057, R3-065) |
| Gaulish | Gaul, extensively first century BCE | ~100 inscriptions; broadly Brythonic under dialect variety, though classificatory value of that term doubted (R1-050, R1-059) |
| Lepontic | NW Po Valley / Alpine lake zone | ~70 inscriptions; earliest sixth century BCE (possibly earlier) — pre-dates historic early-fourth-century Alpine migration package (R1-050, R1-058, R2-052) |
| Middle Danube Celtic | Middle Danube | Differs from western Celtic; little known of structure/date; many names no later than first century BCE (R1-050) |

**Insular Celtic.** Conventionally divided into Q-Celtic/Goidelic (Ireland, Isle of Man, western Scotland) and P-Celtic/Brythonic (basis of modern Welsh, Cornish, Breton) (R1-051). Long-established model: Q older, adopted early by Atlantic communities; later P-Celtic (basis of Gaulish) overtakes Britain; Q reintroduced to Scotland/Man by Irish movements third–fourth centuries CE; P reintroduced to Brittany by SW British settlers fifth century CE. Cunliffe’s position: recent work suggests this Q/P invasion model is a considerable oversimplification — P-Celtic place-names recorded in Ireland; Breton better as native Armorican P-Celtic under Latin, reinvigorated by British settlers’ related speech; Q/P division may be less significant than once believed (R1-052). On Breton specifically he records Loth/Jackson (reintroduction from Britain) vs Falc’hun (substantial pre-Roman indigenous Celtic survival with British reinforcement) (R5-039; also R3-080).

**Geographic structure (Cunliffe).** A central Celtic linguistic zone (Gaul, British Isles, possibly eastern Celtic province) vs a periphery (Ireland, Iberia, Lepontic) with somewhat different Celtic versions; two peripheral areas Celtic-speaking by the sixth century BCE, with strong possibility Ireland was as well (R1-060). Original ancient extent: a broad swath from south-western Iberia through Gaul and the Alpine region into the Middle Danube; Galatians introduced Celtic into central Asia Minor, still recognizable in the fourth century CE according to St Jerome (R1-049). St Jerome also notes similarity between Treveri speech and Galatian, implying continental survival into the Migration Period in places (R3-080).

**Iberian toponymy.** SE non-Indo-European place-names (-il(t)i / -il(t)u) vs widespread -briga names marking Celtic-speaking areas; Lusitania/Gallaecia archaic Celtic forms; differences east/west of an Oviedo–Mérida line — all broadly Celtic IE family as Cunliffe reports (R3-064). Most surviving Iberian Celtic as older Q-type implying early penetration; some P-type -dunum names mainly in Catalonia taken by some as a second influx — Cunliffe treats such invasionist chronologies as oversimplifications; language shows wide Celtic speech without a convincing chronology or population-movement model (R3-065).

**Homeland and spread models (attributed).** Linguistic consensus origin in the Rhine–Danube zone in the late second / early first millennium BCE is, for Cunliffe, consistent with archaeology; Late Bronze Age intensification of production/exchange, population growth, and settlement stability as optimum conditions for major language groups crystallizing (R5-043). He rejects both (a) pre-1960s major-migration answers and (b) pure in-situ evolution of western Celtic from indigenous IE as unacceptable generalizations (R3-081). For Atlantic Celticization he proposes long reciprocal exchange c.1300–600 BCE as a context in which more archaic Celtic could spread from a west-central European homeland without invasion as *deus ex machina* — possibly via mobile pyrotechnical/religious specialists carrying Celtic as lingua franca and caste language (plausible, hard to prove) (R4-018, R5-043). If Celtic was Atlantic lingua franca, inland Celtiberian and Lusitanian developments might radiate from western Iberian coasts — citing Herodotus that Celts were the most westerly Europeans next to the Cynetes (R4-019). For Golasecca/Lepontic: language closely related to Celtic from at least the sixth century, generally assumed much earlier given no cultural break; language similarity both sides of the Alps facilitated intercourse without equating culture = language = people (R2-052).

**Historiography of the discipline.** Edward Lhuyd (*Archaeologia Britannica*) late seventeenth/early eighteenth century noted close relationships among Gaulish, Irish, and British without systematizing Q/P (R1-053); Lhuyd already linked language to colonization waves (R1-054). Broader IE frame: Scaliger; Sir William Jones (1796) including Celtic; Schleicher’s tree with Celtic among fundamentals (R1-055). Most influential Insular folk-movement model after Lhuyd: Sir John Rhys, *Early Britain: Celtic Britain* (1882) — Goidelic then Brythonic waves (R1-056). Gaulish surveys: Evans 1967, Whatmough 1970 (R1-059). Iberian Celtic boosted by Botorrita and work of Tovar and others (R1-057).

### Material

Language is not read directly from pots. Associations with [[hallstatt-culture]] / [[la-tene-culture]] / [[celtiberian-culture]] are inferences. Cunliffe stresses incomplete overlap: Celtic speech by sixth century BCE over wide if discontinuous western Europe; La Tène material reflects a different three-to-four-century complex (R5-042). Iberia: Celtic spoken and classical Celtiberians named, yet material culture “differed markedly from that of the La Tène type” (R3-058). Atlantic façade: highly individual regions within a broad Celtic-speaking zone, maritime exchange waxing and waning, east–west contacts to the La Tène heartland superimposed (R4-032, R3-077).

## Distribution and Chronology

**Ancient (Cunliffe 1997).** SW Iberia through Gaul, Alps, Middle Danube; Lepontic in NW Italy by sixth century BCE; Galatia (Asia Minor); Britain and Ireland with Celtic established by sixth century BCE on his reading, though never called “Celts” by ancient authors (R1-049, R3-079). Greatest territorial co-occurrence with classical “Celtic world” rhetoric in the third century BCE (R5-023). Under Rome, Celtic personal names and rare curse texts (Bath; Amélie-les-Bains; Chamalières) into third–fourth centuries CE do not securely measure spoken Celtic vs vulgar Latin; outside intense romanization Celtic remained dominant (R5-038). By AD 400 Cunliffe maps Celtic languages surviving mainly in the Atlantic zone (R5-047).

**Post-Roman movements.** Irish colonies (Déisi in SW Wales, Llŷn, Dál Riata) bring distinct Celtic dialects fourth–fifth centuries; British migration to Armorica; Anglo-Saxon and Frankish pressure shrinks continental and SE British Celtic; Latin basis of French except Brittany (R5-039).

**Modern survivals.** Ireland, Scotland (Gaelic), Isle of Man (Manx), Wales, Brittany, Cornwall (revived), with Galician Atlantic Celticism as cultural rather than continuous linguistic survival in Cunliffe’s closing remarks (R5-040, R3-077). Not continuous political “Celtic states.”

**Possible linguistic divergence with Mediterranean contact (750–450 BCE).** Cunliffe suggests Celtiberian retained earlier structure while West Hallstatt developments began a path toward Brythonic/P-Celtic (R5-044) — attributed hypothesis, not established fact.

## Identity Cautions

- Speaking Celtic ≠ being called *Keltoi*/Gaul by Greeks and Romans ≠ using La Tène material culture ≠ belonging to a genetic population.
- No ancient author described the inhabitants of Britain and Ireland as Celts (R3-079).
- Lepontic predates the historic Po Valley migration narrative (R1-058) — early Celtic speech ≠ identity with later classical “Celts.”
- Q/P invasion models and Iberian two-wave models are oversimplifications on Cunliffe’s reading (R1-052, R3-065, R3-066).
- Modern Celtic languages do not prove Iron Age political unity or continuous self-conscious pan-Celtic ethnicity (R5-041).
- Glottochronology and absolute linguistic dates are contested; prefer relative sequence plus independent archaeological/historical anchors.

## Historiography

Cunliffe’s Ch. 2 is a secondary synthesis, not primary comparative reconstruction. He traces: Lhuyd and the birth of Celtic comparative awareness; Jones/Schleicher IE framing; Rhys invasion models; Continental epigraphy (Evans, Whatmough; Botorrita); art-history and archaeology drawn into a “single study” of Celtic culture after Jacobsthal 1944 (R1-053–R1-060, R1-080). Invasionist circularity — language implies migration implies material culture implies language — is explicitly criticized for Iberia, Britain, and Ireland (R1-038, R1-039). Post-1960s Spanish work rejecting monocausal Celtiberian invasionism for élite assimilation of selected elements is reported sympathetically (R3-066, R3-067). Chapman 1992 is flagged as the sharpest challenge to the whole “Celt” concept (R5-048).
