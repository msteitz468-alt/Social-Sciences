# Howiesons Poort Industry

## Definition and Construct History

**Howiesons Poort** (often Howieson’s Poort) is a southern African lithic industry/facies within the broader Middle Stone Age / mode-3–5 continuum, famous for **backed bladelets and geometric microlithic forms** that prefigure later mode-5 technology. [[phillipson-david-w|Phillipson]] treats it as a key illustration that sophisticated composite-tool technology and other “modern” cultural markers appear in sub-Saharan Africa **well before** their relatively sudden appearance in Europe (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Material Signature

Backed microliths/bladelets suitable for hafting; part of the broader African trajectory Phillipson describes from mode-3 prepared cores toward mode-5 microlithic composite tools (as of Phillipson 2005; see [[lithic-technological-modes]]). Specific type lists and raw-material preferences are site-level and should be densified from excavation reports.

## Distribution and Chronology

Southern Africa, with classic stratified sequences at coastal and near-coastal sites including [[klasies-river]] and related MSA caves. Absolute ages often depend on luminescence and uranium-series methods because much of the span is beyond reliable radiocarbon; Phillipson flags many ≥40 ka 14C ages as **minimum** rather than absolute (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Lifeways

Hunter-gatherer; Phillipson links backed microliths continent-wide to probable arrow tips/barbs and bow-and-arrow hunting (especially with poison) as a revolutionary technology — a general mode-5 argument that includes but is not limited to Howiesons Poort (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Relations and Successions

- Embedded in southern African MSA sequences that also include sites such as [[blombos-cave]] (symbolic/engraved materials — Phillipson is cautious about over-reading single-site “firsts”).
- Temporal priority of African microlithic/composite technology relative to European Upper Palaeolithic is a load-bearing claim of Phillipson’s Ch. 4 synthesis (as of Phillipson 2005).
- Industry ≠ population: do not equate Howiesons Poort makers with any modern ethnolinguistic group without independent argument.

## Identity Cautions

Construct is a **facies/industry label**, not a people. “Behavioral modernity” packages that use Howiesons Poort as a trait list are theoretical frames — keep attributed (see [[behavioral-modernity]], [[recent-african-origin]]).

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Marlize Lombard (Ch. 26), Lyn Wadley (Ch. 25), Christian Tryon (Ch. 8), Lawrence Barham (Ch. 24) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**.*

Lombard reports the Howiesons Poort as currently the **best-understood southern African MSA phase** for lithic technology, palaeoenvironment, subsistence, and cognitive complexity, with ages ~**59–65 kya**, **post-dating** the reinstated [[still-bay-industry]] (~70–75 kya). She places HP among four techno-complexes (Klasies River, Mossel Bay, Still Bay, Howiesons Poort) showing subtle internal variation with **technological continuity** — HP groups used techniques already part of the repertoire ~110 kya — dispensing with notions of stagnant MSA technology/behaviour between ~120 and 60 kya. Production of backed blade artefacts largely stopped after ~59 kya; older claims that post-HP “reverted” to Mossel Bay type (territorial displacement/behavioural reversal) are, she argues, overemphasized; [[sibudu-cave]] shows technological/behavioural flexibility comparable to recent hunter-gatherers.

Multi-stranded evidence from HP contexts at Sibudu and Umhlatuzana (60–64 kya) indicates **stone-tipped arrows** (and by implication bows) — currently the most direct MSA evidence for mechanically projected weaponry (Lombard). Direct evidence for ochre-enriched **compound adhesives** for hafting begins ~70 kya at Sibudu and continues to final MSA ~35 kya; replication studies imply deliberate chemical/thermal transformations (Wadley et al. via Lombard). Tryon (Ch. 8) notes particular progress combining residues, microwear, and experiment on Howiesons Poort, Still Bay, and other MSA tools in South Africa for hafting; his inventory figure uses a Howiesons Poort backed piece from [[klasies-river]] as the type exemplar. Wadley (Ch. 25) cites repeated Howiesons Poort **engraved ostrich eggshell** at Diepkloof (>60 kya) as an early repeated cultural item (single-site tradition), and treats MSA points/segments as weaponry via Lombard’s residue/use-trace work.

Barham (Ch. 24) situates Howiesons Poort in the behavioural-modernity debate: Mellars (2005) even traced European Upper Palaeolithic origins to Howiesons Poort makers ~65–60 kya (extreme diffusionism Barham historicizes). Industry ≠ people: do not equate HP makers with any living ethnolinguistic group; symbolic packages 120–60 kya are argued (d’Errico & Vanhaeren via Barham) as **episodic not cumulative**.
