# Thule Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Thule** (pronounced "Tooley," from the site in northwestern Greenland) is the
whale-hunting Arctic culture **ancestral to the historic Inuit**, rooted in the Bering Strait
Neoeskimo sequence (Old Bering Sea → Birnirk / Punuk) and spreading rapidly **eastward across
the Canadian Arctic and Greenland around AD 1000**. As presented by
[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], its three defining innovations — the
**dog sled, the umiak (large skin boat), and the kayak** — gave far larger hunting ranges,
heavier transport, and open-water whaling, making it "infinitely more efficient" than the
[[dorset-culture|Dorset]] culture it displaced. Fagan calls its bearers "Neoeskimos," against
the Paleo-Eskimo Pre-Dorset/Dorset.

`construct_status: conventional`. Fagan's own culture→language inference ("perhaps speaking an
archaic form of Inupiat") is hedged and should stay attributed; the Thule-to-Inuit ancestry is
well supported and later confirmed by ancient-genome work (as of Raghavan et al. 2014).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** Toggling harpoons with sealskin floats; polished slate; the
dog sled, umiak, and kayak; large semi-subterranean winter houses of stone, sod, and whale
bone (typical unit 4–6 houses, ~20–25 people), with deep entrance tunnels for heat retention;
summer skin tents (stone tent rings — "the most ubiquitous archaeological sites in the Eastern
Arctic today"). Pre-contact **iron** (meteoritic Cape York iron; native copper; some
Amur-region and, later, Norse iron) was worked by cold-hammering — McCartney's
"epi-metallurgy." Thule art was **modest** (simple engravings) — the inverse of Dorset's rich
carving.

## Distribution and Chronology

Alaskan Thule roots run back to the Old Bering Sea stage (c. 200 BC – AD 800) on St Lawrence
and neighboring islands, through the whale-hunting **Punuk** and sealing **Birnirk** stages.
The eastward migration (c. AD 900–1000, dated insecurely — radiocarbon is unreliable here, so
harpoon-head seriation is used) reached the Canadian Arctic and Greenland by the 13th century.
**Classic Thule** (AD 1100–1400, bowhead-whaling winter settlements) gave way to a
**Postclassic** phase as the **Little Ice Age** (c. AD 1400–1600) heavier pack ice ended
High-Arctic whaling, forcing a shift to sealing, caribou, and fishing and the abandonment of
much of the High Arctic — the context, Fagan suggests, in which the historic Inuit groups
(Copper, Netsilik, Iglulik) emerged.

## Lifeways

The **bowhead whale** was central where available (one whale yielding ~15,000 kg of meat/skin
and ~9,000 kg of blubber, plus bones for house rafters), taken by umiak crews with toggling
harpoons and floats. Outside whaling zones, Thule exploited a wider prey range than Dorset —
caribou, walrus, ringed seal, fish, musk ox, birds — thanks to superior technology and
transport. The **umialik** (whaleboat captain) was an influential, wealthy leadership role;
Fagan (following Maxwell) reads Thule society as more cooperatively organized and capable of
concerted action than the "more loosely knit" Dorset groups (an attributed comparative
judgment).

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from:** the Alaskan Birnirk and Punuk cultures (Old Bering Sea lineage).
- **Displaced:** [[dorset-culture|Dorset]] — mechanism debated (superior technology and
  mobility; competition over shrinking ring-seal ice; possibly introduced disease). Migration
  drivers are also disputed — McGhee's whaling-follows-warming model (the Medieval Warm Period)
  vs. Stanford's sealing-driven model.
- **Descends to:** the historic **Inuit** encountered by European explorers after the 16th
  century; Norse trade (iron, copper) reached Thule via Greenland from c. AD 1000.

## Identity Cautions

- **Culture→language inference.** Fagan's "archaic Inupiat" attribution is hedged; keep it
  attributed.
- **"Eskimo/Inuit" usage** is Fagan's stipulated 1991 convention (Eskimo = Alaskan/western,
  Inuit = Canadian/eastern), not the field's settled terminology.

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]]; the Eastern Arctic Thule culture was
first defined by the Danish Fifth Thule Expedition (Mathiassen, Birket-Smith, Rasmussen,
1921–24). Migration timing and route, and the Dorset-displacement mechanism, are the central
debates; the Thule-to-Inuit ancestry was later confirmed by ancient-genome work (as of
Raghavan et al. 2014). Densify from Arctic ancient-genome and site reports when ingested.
