## Definition and Construct History

"Ancestral South Indians" (ASI) is a name coined by David Reich and colleagues, alongside [[ancestral-north-indians|ANI]], for the second of two highly differentiated ancestral populations whose mixture produced the ancestry of most present-day Indian groups (as of Reich 2018). The framework dates to a 2009 Reich-lab paper and was revised in 2016. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]].

Like the ANI, the ASI is a [[ghost-population]]: inferred statistically from admixture patterns in present-day genomes rather than sampled directly from an ancient South Asian burial (as of Reich 2018).

## Genetic Signature

In the original 2009 formulation, the ASI were described as descending from a population not closely related to any present-day population outside India (as of Reich 2018). The Andamanese of Little Andaman Island — an isolated population with a census size under 100 and no detectable West-Eurasian-related ancestry — were used as a proxy reference for the pre-mixture South Asian lineage; Reich's collaborators' earlier mitochondrial and Y-chromosome study (Singh and Thangaraj) had found the Andamanese genetically separated from mainland Eurasian populations for tens of thousands of years (as of Reich 2018).

The 2016 revision by Lazaridis and Patterson found the ASI were themselves a mixture of roughly 25% ancient-Iranian-farmer-related ancestry and roughly 75% ancestry from a previously established South Asian hunter-gatherer population (related distantly to the Andamanese, given as plain text — not identical to them), overturning the earlier assumption that the ASI carried no West Eurasian ancestry at all (as of Reich 2018).

Before mixing with the ANI, the ANI and ASI were estimated to be as genetically differentiated from each other as present-day Europeans and East Asians (as of Reich 2018).

## Distribution and Chronology

ANI-ASI admixture across Indian groups has been dated by Priya Moorjani's method (contiguous-ancestry-segment length, calibrated against known recombination rates) to between roughly 4,000 and 2,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Because ASI ancestry correlates strongly with Dravidian-speaking groups today, Reich suggests the formation of the ASI population itself — via Iranian-farmer/South-Asian-hunter-gatherer mixture — may have been the process that spread Dravidian languages across South Asia (as of Reich 2018). This mixture appears to have been substantially curtailed by the later onset of strict endogamy within caste-structured jati groups (see [[caste]]), which limited further genetic exchange between highly ANI- and highly ASI-weighted lineages.

## Lifeways

As with the ANI, Reich's account attaches no independent, directly-excavated material-culture record to the ASI; it is an inferred ancestry component rather than an archaeologically documented population.

## Relations and Successions

The ASI is `associated_with` (not identical to) the spread of Dravidian languages in South Asia and, more distantly, with the Andamanese lineage as a proxy for its pre-mixture hunter-gatherer component — an association, not an identity claim (as of Reich 2018). Reich's team modeled Indian genetic variation as forming a gradient, the "Indian Cline," on a principal-component-analysis plot running toward West Eurasian populations, interpreted as evidence of two-way mixture between a West-Eurasian-related population (the ANI) and this distinct, highly divergent South Asian population (the ASI) (as of Reich 2018). See [[ancestral-north-indians]].

## Identity Cautions

ASI is a genetic-ancestry construct, not a self-conscious people, an archaeological culture, or a language group; its correlation with Dravidian speech is an attributed inference from present-day ancestry-language correlation, not an established identity (four-way non-identity). The "Andamanese-related" characterization of the ASI's hunter-gatherer component is likewise a distant statistical proxy relationship, not a claim that South Asian mainland populations descend directly from the historically documented Andaman Islanders.

## Historiography

The ASI concept was introduced alongside the ANI in 2009 and substantially revised in 2016 following broader ancient-DNA sampling; the revision reversed the original assumption of "no West Eurasian ancestry" for the ASI once Iranian-farmer-related ancestry was detected within it (as of Reich 2018). The methods used to detect and date ANI-ASI admixture — allele-sharing comparisons and ancestry-segment-length dating — were, per Reich, originally developed specifically to study Indian population history and only later (in 2010) applied to demonstrate Neanderthal-modern human admixture, reversing the more familiar historiographic assumption that Indian genetics was a downstream application of methods developed for European/Neanderthal questions (as of Reich 2018).
