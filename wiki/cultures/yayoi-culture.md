## Definition and Construct History

Yayoi is the conventional archaeological label for the rice-farming, metal-using
culture of Japan that succeeded [[jomon-culture]]. The name derives from the Yayoi
district of Tokyo, where diagnostic pottery was first identified. The source
([[scarre-human-past-2013]]) divides Yayoi into three phases: Early (300–100 BCE),
Middle (100 BCE–100 CE), and Late (until 300 CE), though it explicitly notes that "the
critical issue of the origins of the Yayoi has not been completely resolved, even if
the main points are evident." At Itazuke (northern Kyushu), Early Yayoi-style
potsherds occur in association with Yusu ware, which is conventionally assigned to
late Jomon but which "there is a school of thought" instead assigns to Yayoi proper —
a named, unresolved classificatory dispute over where the Jomon/Yayoi boundary falls.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material:** rice cultivation established via wooden and later iron agricultural
tools; bronze and iron metallurgy; irrigated rice fields at Itazuke dated to c.
500–300 BCE; increasing material contact with China and Korea. Rice farming spread
from Kyushu to northern Honshu within two to three centuries of its introduction.
Yoshinogari (Kyushu), a 25-hectare Late Yayoi settlement, has a defensive ditch,
watchtowers, and a 40×26 m mounded tomb with ritual pottery suggesting an emergent
elite — details from the Chinese text *Wei zhi* describing Yayoi Japan "appear to have
been confirmed by archaeology" at this site, an instance of independent convergence
between textual and material evidence.

**Genetic (attributed):** physical-anthropological evidence shows Yayoi skeletal
remains taller than Jomon counterparts, with differently shaped heads; this is
interpreted as supporting substantial immigration accompanying or following the
establishment of Yayoi rice farming. Settlement-size-based population estimates are
likewise read as indicating "a considerable degree of immigration" (bioarchaeological
interpretive claim, per [[scarre-human-past-2013]]).

**Linguistic:** the wiki records no independent linguistic-reconstruction argument
from this source beyond the general association of the rice-farming influx with
incoming Japonic speech; this affiliation is argued, not demonstrated by linguistic
data in the extracted material, and should be treated as a hypothesis pending
independent linguistic evidence.

## Distribution and Chronology

Centered on Kyushu, from which rice farming and the associated material package spread
to northern Honshu within two to three centuries. The Ainu of Hokkaido are described
in the source as "in all probability" descendants of persistent Jomon-derived
foragers who did not adopt Yayoi rice farming, attributed to Hokkaido's climate.

## Lifeways

Rice-farming with irrigated fields (evidenced at Itazuke), bronze and iron tool and
weapon manufacture, and growing external contact with the Korean peninsula and China.
Settlement scale and defensive works (ditches, watchtowers) at Yoshinogari, together
with a large mounded elite burial, indicate emergent social stratification by the Late
Yayoi phase.

## Relations and Successions

Preceded by [[jomon-culture]], whose foragers (per Bleed and Matsui 2010, as cited in
[[scarre-human-past-2013]]) had already begun managing wild plants such that rice
cultivation "fitted readily into their patterned subsistence" on introduction. Yayoi
is succeeded by the Kofun period / Yamato state. Contemporary developments in Korea
are recorded on the Chulmun culture material from the same source range; rice
cultivation reaching Korea (from roughly the early 2nd millennium BCE, possibly late
3rd) and Japan are treated by the source as connected episodes of the same
rice-farming expansion, `associated_with` rather than identical processes.

## Identity Cautions

Yayoi is a material-culture construct (pottery style, tool kit, settlement pattern),
not directly a language or a population. The wiki's four-way non-identity rule
applies with particular force here: the bioarchaeological finding of taller,
differently-shaped Yayoi skeletons is genetic/physical evidence bearing on
*population* history; the presumed shift toward Japonic speech is a *linguistic*
hypothesis; "Yayoi culture" itself is a *material* label. None of these three is
identical to the others, and the source itself flags the origins question as
unresolved. Any statement equating "Yayoi" with a single incoming population or a
single language should use `associated_with`, never identity. The Ainu-as-Jomon-descendant
claim is likewise an ethnic-continuity inference from archaeological/foraging
continuity, not a settled genetic or ethnic identity, and is recorded here as
attributed, not wiki-voice fact.

## Historiography

Extracted from [[scarre-human-past-2013]] (Scarre, ed., *The Human Past*, 3rd ed.,
2013), drawing on Bleed and Matsui (2010) on Jomon plant/animal management, Yoshizaki
(1997) on rice-associated Late Jomon pottery, and Crawford and Takamiya (1990) /
Barnes (1993) on pre-agricultural plant cultivation. The origins question — how much
of Yayoi represents in-situ Jomon adoption of farming versus substantial immigration
from the Korean peninsula — is explicitly framed by the source as unresolved, not a
settled consensus.
