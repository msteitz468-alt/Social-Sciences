## Definition and Construct History

"Neanderthal" originated as a skeletal-morphology taxon: the type specimen was found in 1856 by miners in a limestone quarry in Germany's Neander Valley. Neanderthals are classified as "archaic" because their skeletal morphology falls outside the range of present-day human variation. Reich reports they dominated the European landscape from around 400,000 years ago and had brains slightly larger on average than modern humans (attributed, Reich 2018). Genetic data place the Neanderthal–Denisovan split at 470,000–380,000 years ago and their shared split from the modern-human lineage at 770,000–550,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). The population's genetic identity was established in 2010 when Svante Pääbo's team at the Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology (Leipzig) sequenced the first whole Neanderthal genome, from three ~40,000-year-old bones from Vindija Cave, Croatia, containing as much as 4 percent archaic human DNA amid bacterial and fungal contamination.

## Material Signature

Neanderthals made stone tools using the Levallois technique, which Reich reports required cognitive skill and dexterity comparable to Upper Paleolithic modern-human toolmaking (attributed, Reich 2018). Excavation evidence at [[shanidar|Shanidar Cave]], Iraq, including nine deliberately buried skeletons — among them a half-blind elderly man who could not have survived without care — has been read as evidence Neanderthals cared for their sick and elderly. Possible symbolic behavior is evidenced by eagle-talon jewelry at Krapina Cave, Croatia (~130,000 years ago), and stone circles at Bruniquel Cave, France (~180,000 years ago). Neanderthals largely disappear from the archaeological record above the ash layer of a supervolcanic eruption near Naples ~39,000 years ago, alongside many modern-human archaeological cultures, suggesting a compounding climate/competition crisis (attributed, Reich 2018).

**(As of Stringer & Gamble 1993 — anatomical/archaeological layer, pre-nuclear aDNA.)** *[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993|In Search of the Neanderthals]]* synthesizes classic Neanderthal morphology as specialized relative to African and Far Eastern contemporaries: long low vault; midfacial prognathism with large nose; inflated (non-hollowed) cheeks; large front teeth with heavy wear and frequent molar taurodontism; long thin superior pubic rami; stocky cold-adapted body proportions (short distal limbs, barrel chest). They report classic western Neanderthals ~120–35 ka; total known remains then under ~500 individuals (nearly half children). Main skeleton+tool co-occurrence zones they emphasize are SW France and the Middle East ([[tabun]], [[shanidar]], [[kebara]], Amud). On behaviour they argue scant structured living space, and absence until the Upper Palaeolithic of art/ornament/display, is not merely preservational; they treat many purported Middle Palaeolithic “burials” as complete skeletons whose survival may track carnivore cave-use more than symbolic burial (Gamble’s model, attributed). Late Neanderthal industries they associate with imitation of incoming Modern technology ([[chatelperronian]] as possible acculturation) rather than independent invention of the Upper Palaeolithic package — a **position** of their replacement program, not wiki-voice fact.

## Genetic Signature

Neanderthal and modern-human mitochondrial lineages shared a common maternal ancestor 470,000–360,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Using the Four Population Test (see [[admixture-statistics]]), Reich's team found Neanderthals equally closely related to Europeans, East Asians, and New Guineans, but closer to all non-Africans than to sub-Saharan Africans — a result with a probability of chance occurrence of less than one in a quadrillion. Initial admixture into non-African ancestors was dated to 86,000–37,000 years ago via segment-length analysis, later refined to 54,000–49,000 years ago using an ancient Siberian modern-human genome (as of Reich 2018). Present-day non-Africans carry roughly 1.5–2.1 percent Neanderthal ancestry, higher in East Asians than Europeans; Reich attributes the European deficit to dilution from an early-splitting non-African lineage entering with farmers. Ancestry is unevenly distributed across the genome — absent across more than half of it, exceeding 50 percent in some regions — a pattern Sriram Sankararaman's mapping work links to ongoing natural selection against Neanderthal DNA, especially near the X chromosome and male-fertility genes; Reich attributes this to Neanderthals' small population size and long separation time from modern humans (attributed, Reich 2018). Reich argues the most likely geographic locus of the admixture persisting in living non-Africans was the Near East, where Neanderthals and modern humans traded regional dominance at least twice between 130,000 and 50,000 years ago. Neanderthal genomes also show a possible few percent of ancestry from an early-diverging modern-human lineage, a finding Reich states he and other geneticists remain "still on the fence" about.

## Distribution and Chronology

Neanderthals evolved in Europe by around 400,000 years ago and are documented across Europe, the Near East, and central Asia (Shanidar, Iraq; Denisova Cave, Siberia, where a Neanderthal toe-bone genome was also recovered). They went extinct in their last known western European holdout between roughly 41,000 and 39,000 years ago, within a few thousand years of modern-human arrival there (as of Reich 2018).

## Lifeways

As evidenced by excavation and genetics: Neanderthals used Levallois-technique stone tools, possibly cared for injured and elderly group members, and, at some sites, engaged in behavior read as symbolic (jewelry, structured cave features). A 2014 analysis of a modern-human individual from Peștera cu Oase, Romania (~40,000 years old), found 6–9 percent Neanderthal ancestry in segments so large they imply a Neanderthal ancestor within six generations; Reich's team treats this individual's lineage as an evolutionary dead end with no descendants among people living today.

**(As of Stringer & Gamble 1993.)** They report care for injured individuals as widely accepted; height estimates ~169 cm males / ~160 cm females (European formulae), with Middle Eastern individuals slightly taller (Amud ~179 cm; smallest females at La Ferrassie and Shanidar ~155 cm). Speech capacity was contested: Crelin & Lieberman once argued severe restriction, later modified; the [[kebara]] hyoid is virtually modern-shaped, but experts disagreed whether that implies modern vocal skill. On replacement mechanism they reject colonial “genocide” metaphors (Wells’s *Grisly Folk*): Stringer argued Neanderthals “went with a whimper rather than a bang”—gradual displacement toward less favourable environments via population growth and resource competition, possibly limited gene flow into a larger modern pool (attributed position; pre-aDNA consensus later revised by nuclear admixture results — see Genetic Signature and Historiography).

## Relations and Successions

Neanderthals interbred with the ancestors of present-day non-Africans, `associated_with` the admixture event dated to the Near East roughly 50,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Erik Trinkaus argued skeletal remains from Oase Cave were morphologically intermediate between modern humans and Neanderthals — a position Reich treats as suggestive but not decisive absent genetic confirmation (a live position, not settled fact). Laurent Excoffier proposed low hybrid fertility explains the low surviving Neanderthal ancestry percentage; Reich states he initially favored a social/behavioral explanation (assortative mating) but that subsequent evidence supported Excoffier's hybrid-infertility mechanism. Homo heidelbergensis has been proposed by Reich as a plausible common ancestor of modern humans, Neanderthals, and Denisovans (a speculative position). The population is closely `associated_with` [[denisovans]], with whom Neanderthals shared a more recent common ancestor than either shares with modern humans.

## Identity Cautions

"Neanderthal" here denotes a genetically and morphologically defined archaic-human population, not a language, a self-conscious people, or a single uniform group — sampled Neanderthal genomes (Vindija, Siberian toe bone) show internal structure and geographic variation. The population's species status relative to modern humans is scientifically contested: Reich notes that confirmed interbreeding undermines species definitions premised on reproductive isolation, and treats the boundary as an open category question rather than settled fact. Genetic ancestry proportions describe present-day statistical admixture, not identity between Neanderthals and any living population or "race."

## Historiography

Reich began his own apprenticeship in ancient DNA in 2007 working with Svante Pääbo on the Neanderthal and Denisova genome projects; he and applied mathematician Nick Patterson joined Pääbo's consortium as specialists in population-mixture statistics. Reich reports entering the project with a strong prior bias against interbreeding, shaped by training under David Goldstein, himself a student of Luca Cavalli-Sforza's strict out-of-Africa model. Pääbo's own earlier research — 1987 mitochondrial work locating the deepest human lineages in Africa, and his 1997 study placing Neanderthal mtDNA outside modern human variation — had reinforced the pre-2010 consensus against Neanderthal interbreeding. Reich frames the confirmed admixture findings as producing a "mostly out-of-Africa" synthesis superseding both strict multiregionalism (the position that modern populations descend substantially from local archaic humans) and strict replacement models. Graham Coop raised, and Reich's team later argues it ruled out via a high-quality Siberian Neanderthal genome, the alternative possibility that the detected admixture came from an unknown archaic population only distantly related to Neanderthals. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]] for the full account.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]]; mid-2000s textbook layer—pre- and early-aDNA consensus; do not overwrite Reich-layer genetics above.)*

**Discovery historiography (as of W&O 2007):** Neandertal child’s skull near Liège (1829–30); Gibraltar quarry skull 1848; 1856 Neander Valley skullcap/limbs initially dismissed (Virchow: deformed human; others: Celt, rickets, idiot, Cossack); Spy 1886 partial skeletons associated with tools and extinct fauna turned opinion toward ancient men. Schwalbe (1906) proposed pithecanthropine → Neandertal → modern succession.

**Biology and range:** ~130–30 kya mainly Europe and western Asia; traits listed include receding/absent chin, large cheekbones/continuous brow ridges, midface prognathism, short powerful stature with thick curved long bones, cranial capacity in modern range (slightly larger average for Classic western type). 1957 conference revised early anti-evolutionary hostility (Straus & Cave “New York subway” thought experiment).

**Culture and debates they report:** Mousterian industry (Bordes’s four facies as cultural “tribes” vs Lewis & Sally [[binford-lewis|Binford]] functional task kits; Dibble scraper-reduction model). Speech anatomy contested (Lieberman & Crelin vs Frayer & Wolpoff). Purported burials (La Chapelle, La Ferrassie, Shanidar pollen, etc.) equivocal—positions range from no intentional burial to hygiene-only (Klein) to rare burial. Care for impaired individuals accepted; high young-adult mortality (Trinkaus). Best test case for Replacement vs Multiregional Continuity given abundant fossils and >100 years of research—as of their 2007 synthesis, before full aDNA resolution.

### Gamble — *Timewalkers* (1994)

[[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] treats European Neanderthals as the type **Ancients** in an Ancients/Pioneers/Moderns colonization scheme (*[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* ch. 8). He sides with replacement of Ancients by Moderns (his "Gardeners" camp) while insisting replacement can be competitive/behavioral without documented conflict; records Piltdown unmasking and Straus & Cave’s La Chapelle reexamination as rehabilitating Neanderthal anatomy against the Boule/Wells "hunched" image; surveys Stringer vs Brace/Wolpoff on European ancestry, climatic-adaptation hypotheses (Wolpoff, Trinkaus, Beals), and language/voicebox debate (Lieberman vs Kebara hyoid) — all as **positions as of 1994**, pre-nuclear aDNA (attributed). Links the fate of Neanderthals to the later **[[global-colonization]]** burst of Moderns without treating replacement as teleological design to fill the world ([[exaptation]] framing). See [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

### Stringer & Gamble — *In Search of the Neanderthals* (1993)

*Source: [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]. Pre-nuclear aDNA horizon — do not overwrite Reich-layer genetics above; this is a dated research-program layer.*

**Program and frame.** [[stringer-chris|Chris Stringer]] and [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] integrate fossil anatomy with Palaeolithic archaeology under an **Ancients / Moderns** analytic split: Neanderthals and other pre-modern contemporaries = Ancients; anatomically modern successors (European Cro-Magnons as type case) = Moderns. They note Moderns “literally behaved like Neanderthals for very long periods,” separating skeletal modernity from behavioural modernity ([[anatomically-modern-humans]], [[behavioral-modernity]]). Their core thesis — Recent African Origin / population replacement of Neanderthals by African-derived moderns, with limited if any lasting regional continuity — is a **position** in the [[modern-human-origins-debate]], not a wiki-voice finding. They explicitly contrast it with [[multiregional-evolution]] (Weidenreich → Coon, Brace, [[wolpoff-milford|Wolpoff]]): multiregionalists held Neanderthals evolved into modern Europeans; replacement proponents treated them as an evolutionary dead end.

**Discovery historiography (as of S&G 1993).** Feldhofer Cave 1856 as the start of palaeoanthropology as a field (Engis 1830 and Gibraltar 1848 recognized only later); William King 1864 names *Homo neanderthalensis*. Rudolf Virchow and many pre-WWI scholars treated the fossils as diseased or too specialized for ancestry of living (especially “advanced white European”) humans. Marcellin [[boule-marcellin|Boule]]’s La Chapelle monographs (1911–13) fixed a stooped, ape-like reconstruction S&G call inaccurate and racist in its comparative framing; they nonetheless hold separate-species status themselves “for entirely different reasons than Boule’s” — specialization of the fossil morphology, not simian characters. Popular images (Wells’s *Grisly Folk*, Quennell textbooks, caveman props) tracked expulsion from ancestry; later fiction (Golding, Auel) tracked post-Boule rehabilitation.

**Chronology and geography (as of 1993).** They place Neanderthals ~230–30 ka, confined to Eurasia from Uzbekistan (Teshik Tash) to Iberia, south no further than the northern Mediterranean / “gates of Jerusalem”; not Africa, India, or eastern Asia south of the great mountain chains. Geographic limits set by interwar Mount Carmel (Garrod et al.), Crimea, and Central Asia (Okladnikov). Most palaeoanthropologists, they report, derived Neanderthals from European middle Pleistocene ancestors (late *H. erectus* / *H. heidelbergensis* / “archaic *H. sapiens*”), with early characters by the end of the middle Pleistocene (Ehringsdorf ~230 ka as a minimum split age if the modern lineage also splits then).

**Middle East corridor.** S&G make the Levant the load-bearing test case: Middle Palaeolithic fossils divide (with few exceptions) into Neanderthal-like ([[tabun]], [[shanidar]], Amud, [[kebara]]) and modern-looking ([[skhul]], [[qafzeh]]). TL/ESR results they report place Skhul/Qafzeh moderns ~100–90+ ka — older than Kebara Neanderthal ~60 ka — overturning progressive Tabun→Skhul succession models. Three field options they record: (1) overlap zone without proven contemporaneity; (2) moderns first, cold-adapted Neanderthals entering ~70 ka and possibly displacing early moderns; (3) single highly variable regional *H. sapiens* population. Western Europe they call a cul-de-sac; the Middle East a corridor/crossroads and springboard for later global Modern dispersals. See site pages and [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

**Behaviour and late fate (attributed positions).** They argue Modern behaviour was of “a completely different order of complexity,” so replacement is the only plausible explanation given no gradual change from one way of life to the other (position). Aurignacian chronology they use as proxy for Cro-Magnon entry: >40 ka centre/east Europe, then Iberia, ~36 ka southern Germany, ~34 ka SW France — Neanderthals unchallenged on home ground until after ~45 ka. Chatelperronian and related late industries they read as imitation of incoming technology, not independent UP invention. Ezra Zubrow’s modelling (via S&G) and Stringer’s “whimper not bang” displacement language reject genocide metaphors while still asserting demographic replacement.

**Genetics as of 1993 (superseded).** They cite Cann–Stoneking–Wilson 1987 mtDNA (“Eve” ~200 ka African, dispersal ~100 ka) and Cavalli-Sforza nuclear clustering as independent support for Out of Africa 2, and record multiregional attacks on mtDNA calibration and PAUP. **This pre-aDNA genetic layer is historical only.** Nuclear Neanderthal genome results (2010 onward; Reich 2018 layer above) established low-level admixture into non-Africans, producing a “mostly out-of-Africa” synthesis that neither pure multiregional continuity nor total-replacement-without-gene-flow 1993 models fully anticipated. Date-stamp all 1993 genetic claims; do not promote them to wiki voice.

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; a paleontological/archaeological
layer, as of Klein 1999, predating the nuclear-aDNA admixture results in the Reich layer above.)*

Klein treats Neanderthals as a **separate species, *Homo neanderthalensis***, physically
**replaced** by intrusive modern humans rather than ancestral to living Europeans — arguing
fossils and archaeology indicate they "rarely if ever interbred." (Post-2010 aDNA has since
established low-level admixture; do not read Klein's no-admixture position as current.) More
than 275 individuals from 70+ sites are known; discovery history runs Engis (1829–30),
Gibraltar (1848), the 1856 Neander Valley type find, Spy (1886). Diagnostic features: a low
globular braincase with occipital "bun" and double-arched browridge, midfacial prognathism,
and a large mean endocranial capacity of ~1,520 cc (1,245–1,740 cc). Klein/Trinkaus read the
extreme postcranial robusticity and short distal limbs as **cold adaptation** (Bergmann/Allen),
implying reliance on physiology more than culture relative to the Inuit. Feldhofer mtDNA
(Krings et al. 1997) put the Neanderthal–modern split at ~690–550 ka (as of Klein 1999).

Klein's signature claim is behavioral: the **Mousterian/Middle Stone Age "sameness"** — rare
formal bone tools, near-absent art and ornament, minimal raw-material transport, no clear
structures, low site density — contrasts so sharply with Upper Paleolithic diversity that he
reads it as "an underlying biological contrast in basic cognitive capacity," i.e. Neanderthals
were "behaviorally inferior" and this "may have been rooted in their biological makeup." He is
skeptical of Neanderthal symbolism: the Shanidar "flower burial" pollen may be rodent-intrusive,
the Teshik-Tash/Guattari "ritual" cases fail on inspection, and burial may be simple corpse
disposal (see the Neanderthal-burial dispute; [[shanidar]], [[la-chapelle-aux-saints]]). He
does accept **care for the injured/elderly** (Shanidar 1, the La Chapelle "old man") as the
clearest inferable non-material behavior — while noting maximum life expectancy only in the late
30s–mid 40s and high rates of childhood enamel hypoplasia. The Neanderthal-made
[[chatelperronian]] (bone tools, ornaments at Saint-Césaire, Arcy-sur-Cure) he reads
as acculturation from Aurignacian moderns, "a major puzzle" for closure on Out of Africa 2.
See [[mousterian]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[behavioral-modernity]].

### Mithen — *The Prehistory of the Mind* (1996)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], [[mithen-prehistory-of-the-mind-1996]]; pre-aDNA; European-weighted.)* Neanderthals are grouped with *H. erectus*, archaic *Homo*, and *H. heidelbergensis* as **Early Humans** sharing a generic Phase-2 ([[cognitive-fluidity]]) architecture: powerful but **isolated** [[specialized-intelligences]] (technical — handaxes, Levallois skill modern students rarely match; natural-history — glacial survival and colonization; social; linguistic as **social-only** language). Paradoxes: large brains (~1200–1750 cc, same range as AMHs) and high technical skill **without** art, complex multi-material technology, purpose-dedicated tool diversity, or probable religious behaviour — Mithen’s dilemma “what was all the new brain processing power for?” before the cultural explosion. Consciousness (via Humphrey) is argued to be trapped in the social chapel during skilled toolmaking/foraging. Under more adaptive stress than UP moderns, they produced no art: capacity argument, not leisure. Difference from *H. erectus* is mainly linguistic capacity, not full fluidity. **Do not** equate this 1996 cognitive profile with later symbolic claims (ornament, Bruniquel, etc.) — record as dated position; identity cautions and Reich-layer genetics above still govern population definition.

### Gamble — *The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]; social-network layer, **as of Gamble 1999**. Pre-nuclear aDNA; do not overwrite Reich-layer genetics above. Related industries: [[mousterian-industry]], [[chatelperronian-industry]], [[aurignacian-industry]]. Debate homes: [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]].)*

**Not Bordes's tribes.** Gamble rejects reading Mousterian facies as five sedentary Neanderthal **tribes** / ethnic cultures (Bordes); variability is pursued via mobility, raw material, *chaîne opératoire*, and settlement (Turq on Quina as non-culture; Mellars chronology) — see [[mousterian-industry]] and the debate page.

**Intimate / effective networks; weak extended.** Neanderthal society, for Gamble, was founded on **intimate** (~5) and **effective** (~20) personal networks negotiated through emotional and material resources in co-presence. **Extended networks** remained largely **unnegotiated and undeveloped**; life was still **very local and usually immediate**. Skill transfer enabling continental occupation worked by individuals negotiating effective networks more intensively via emotion, materials, and gesture embedded in temporal rhythms — not via external symbol systems inscribed on landscape or objects at gatherings (attributed). Co-operation was routine negotiation within intimate/effective networks, not an external organizing principle requiring symbolic language to stretch relations across time/space. Social occasions and built "places" (hearths, pits, postholes, architecture) are judged lacking relative to later UP; gatherings depended neither on language nor personification of objects/locations in the strong UP sense (position).

**Landscape of habit.** Neanderthals lived in **landscapes of habit**, not a full **social landscape**: dimensions traced by raw-material transfers (majority <20 km; furthest bulk typically 80–100 km; longer NC vs SW tracks under continentality); no non-stone materials transferred any distance; Geneste's Eemian watershed allows some post-interglacial territorial extension but still small habit-scale chaînes. They constantly renewed lithic technology — carrying tools as extensions of embodied gestures — but did not stock locales with caches; modern humans (in this model) move social occasion to social occasion, Neanderthals at most gathering to gathering (Kuhn via Gamble). Grotte Vaufrey star-pattern transfers; MP pattern closer to low-density wheel/star-burst network than seasonal circular round; individuals did not carry bulk >20 km (attributed).

**Burial geography.** Complete/near-complete Neanderthal skeletons concentrate in SW France and Israel (Spy certain in NW; Kiik-Koba probable NE Crimea); elsewhere highly fragmented. French Interpleniglacial sample coincides with extreme rarity of carnivores in rock shelters; SE/ME fragmentary remains (Vindija, Krapina) with abundant bear/hyena. Gamble: complete bodies may reflect **carnivore non-disturbance** (no denning/cub-rearing in those shelters) rather than deliberate burial alone; Guattari skull = hyena den (Stiner); Hortus resistant parts with leopard/bear (Defleur via Gamble; continues his 1986a line) (attributed). Later UP open-air and multiple burials contrast with Neanderthal burial as *adieu* rather than *au revoir* (extended-network ancestors) — Gravettian is the key period for European UP burial elaboration (Gamble Ch.7 position).

**Two social systems at the transition.** Innovative rhythms yield system integration via symbolic resources beyond co-presence limits, selectively before 33 Kyr and structurally after. Two different social systems — **Neanderthal and Cro-Magnon** — based on distinct uses of emotional, material, and symbolic resources; pure population-replacement portrayal misrepresents transition subtlety. Human **agency** creates the immediate taskscape irrespective of fossil species name-tag (Gamble position). Faunal evidence (Mauran MP bison vs Abri Pataud Aurignacian reindeer) does **not** show very different organizational hunting capabilities; the load-bearing contrast is network resources and release from proximity after ~33 Kyr, not "stomach-led vs brain-dead" caricatures (closing manifesto). Chatelperronian co-residence/imitation positions and Iberian late Mousterian refugia are recorded on [[chatelperronian-industry]], [[aurignacian-industry]], and [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]. Nuclear-aDNA horizon as of 2012 — densifies behaviour, genome results, and extinction narrative; does not overwrite Reich 2018 refinements above.*

**Anatomy and drift.** Weaver, Roseman, and Stringer argue cranial differences between Neanderthals and modern humans can be explained largely by **genetic drift** after ~400 ka separation rather than special adaptive accounts alone: “we drifted—rather than were driven—apart” (as of Stringer 2012). Semicircular-canal morphology makes Neanderthals the odd ones out relative to *H. erectus* and early moderns (Spoor et al. via Stringer); European candidates (Steinheim, Reilingen) approach the Neanderthal pattern. Synchrotron dental work (Smith, Tafforeau, Hublin, Stringer et al.) finds early moderns ([[skhul]], [[qafzeh]]) matured at slow recent-human rates while Neanderthals grew somewhat faster (Engis ~3, Scladina ~8, Le Moustier ~12 actual years vs modern-pattern expectations ~4, 11, 16), with thinner molar enamel.

**Behavioural revision (as of Stringer 2012).** Against the older “behaviourally inferior” caricature, Stringer synthesizes:

- **Marine and plant resources:** Gibraltar excavations (Stringer with Barton and Finlayson from 1994) yield tools, hearths, food debris, and strong evidence for shellfish and seal exploitation; mussels baked in dying embers while much land game may have been eaten raw. Plant processing (grains, tubers) is evidenced from site debris and dental calculus.
- **Trauma and care:** Berger & Trinkaus high head/neck trauma (“rodeo rider”) pattern; Stringer adds interpersonal violence as at least an additional factor. Shanidar 1’s long survival with severe disability implies group assistance; Shanidar 4 “flower burial” pollen is reinterpreted (Redding/Sommer) as rodent flower-head caches. Stringer accepts **some** later-Neanderthal ritual burial (including infants with simple grave goods) against Gargett’s total denial, while demoting romantic flower-people imagery.
- **Pigments and shells:** European groups used dark pigments (manganese dioxide, pyrite); Cueva Antón and Cueva de los Aviones (Zilhão) yield pigmented shell pendants — Aviones ~50 ka argued too old for Cro-Magnon influence. Stringer treats this as strong evidence some Neanderthals expressed themselves symbolically on a par with many MSA Africans.
- **Music rejected (for now):** Divje Babe “flute” rejected; carnivore punctures preferred until confirmatory finds.
- **Diet isotopes:** Richards & Bocherens collagen C/N place sampled Neanderthals as top predators (large herbivores), with southern coastal supplementation (shellfish, seals, occasional dolphin).

**aDNA (~2% admixture; FOXP2 / MC1R).** First Neanderthal mtDNA from the 1856 Neander Valley skeleton (1997); by Stringer’s writing, 20+ fossils had yielded mtDNA. Draft autosomal genome (predominantly three Vindija ~40 ka females; also El Sidrón) largely confirmed Out of Africa and ~350 ka separation, but non-African moderns (Europe, China, New Guinea) lay slightly closer to Neanderthal sequence than Africans — estimated **~2% Neanderthal genetic input** overall (as of Stringer 2012), surprisingly high to pure-RAO adherents. Yotova et al.: non-African X-chromosomes up to ~9% Neanderthal-derived at one locus. Stringer notes the ~2% is not the same 2% across French, Chinese, and New Guinean samples (Currat & Excoffier: ineffective-hybridization ceiling). He prefers possible earlier/warmer admixture (e.g. Skhul–Qafzeh / Tabun-era Levant ~120 ka) over only late European contact; early Cro-Magnon linear physiques and European light-skin gene ages (some <15 ka) do not match simple “cold-adapted Neanderthal gift” narratives. El Sidrón / Monti Lessini **MC1R** mutations imply red hair and pale skin, but the Neanderthal MC1R variant is **distinct** from modern European red-hair variants. Human-form **FOXP2** confirmed in carefully screened El Sidrón individuals after contamination concerns about early drafts; Stringer holds this does **not** prove fully modern language — only that biology does not deny speech potential. Six complete Neanderthal mtDNA genomes differ at only 55 of >16,000 base pairs; effective female population size estimates as low as ~3,500 across Europe and western Asia. Lalueza-Fox et al. on the El Sidrón “family”: three identical male mtDNAs and three different female sequences — if a family, implies **patrilocality**. Microcephalin D-variant-from-Neanderthals hypothesis fails: Vindija had the ancestral “African” MCPH1 form.

**Châtelperronian.** Saint-Césaire skeleton associated with Châtelperronian industry: Klein and Stringer expected Neanderthal makers capable of UP-style tools, not morphological intermediates proving Neanderthal Phase/[[multiregional-evolution]]; skeleton identified as rather typical Neanderthal. [[wolpoff-milford|Wolpoff]] argued transitional features overemphasized. Once Neanderthal nature accepted, supported Replacement in W Europe: Châtelperronian ~35 ka ¹⁴C as local Neanderthal strand parallel to Aurignacian moderns ~40 ka. Grotte du Renne (Arcy): Neanderthal remains with Châtelperronian tools plus pierced teeth and worked bone/ivory also characteristic of Aurignacian — four competing readings (mixing; trade/scavenge; acculturation; independent Neanderthal “modernization”). Recent ¹⁴C suggests Châtelperronian deposits at Arcy suffered disturbance/mixing, but other Châtelperronian sites also show “advanced” tools/pendants.

**Extinction and demography.** Cultural breakthroughs (burial, blades, hafting, pigments, Spanish shell cosmetics) occurred, yet within ~20 ka Neanderthals were extinct: glacial attrition plus modern-human arrival kept densities too low for cumulative cultural takeoff (as of Stringer 2012). Caspari & Lee: only European Cro-Magnons show high old:young adult ratios (~4× Neanderthal); Skhul/Qafzeh matched Neanderthal low middle/old-age survivorship — implicating cultural/social factors, not pure biology. Brennan: childhood enamel hypoplasia far more common in Neanderthals. Southern Iberia among last Neanderthal outposts; Gibraltar route of modern entry lacks good evidence. Species taxonomy: morphological differences comparable to distinct primate species, yet hybridization possible; Stringer resists lumping Neanderthals into *H. sapiens* because that would force contradictory character lists (vault shape, brow, chin, suprainiac fossa, inner ear, pelvis).

See [[recent-african-origin]], [[assimilation-model]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[denisovans]], [[ancient-dna]].

### Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*Source: [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]. Synthesis horizon **as of Tattersall 2012** — nuclear aDNA then incomplete; do **not** overwrite Reich 2018 / later refinements above. All claims attributed; genetics date-stamped.*

**Discovery and species status.** *Homo neanderthalensis* was the first extinct hominid species discovered and named (mid-19th c.); Tattersall holds they were not direct human precursors and that field agreement treats them as a distinctive species with surprisingly little disagreement over which fossils count as Neanderthal (**thesis**/reported consensus, Tattersall 2012). Full-fledged Neanderthals are, he argues, unlikely before ~250 ka; earliest distinctive fossils include Biache-St-Vaast ≥170 ka (MIS 6), with possible earlier incomplete forms at Reilingen and Steinheim (~MIS 8 ~250 ka). He frames a Middle Pleistocene European “complex minuet” among species, with *H. heidelbergensis* coexisting until large-brained Neanderthals alone held the subcontinent by Biache times (attributed).

**Range and cold-adaptation critique.** Over ~200 kyr Neanderthals ranged Gibraltar/Israel to Finland (warm interludes), Arctic-proximate Russian tool sites ~31–34 ka (species assignment *assumed* from tools — **industry ≠ fossil identity**), Wales to Uzbekistan, and a nondescript Altai bone with Neanderthal genetic signature (as of Tattersall 2012). Occupation contracted/expanded with climate (e.g. Mediterranean fringes ~70–60 ka cold; far north/central Europe in warm MIS 3). Against the older “cold-adapted vs tropically adapted sapiens” contrast, Tattersall finds little support in nasal anatomy or limb proportions (said to resemble intensive modern foragers of varied backgrounds); cultural accommodation across many climates is his preferred reading; extreme cold without clothing would demand unworkable fat mass for a hunting lifestyle (**thesis**, Tattersall 2012). A pigmentation-gene variant (two DNA samples) suggested pale skin and red hair **not** matching modern redhead variants — emblematic, for him, that Neanderthals should not be read as less-successful versions of us (as of Tattersall 2012).

**Anatomy and development.** Shared body plan: long low vault with side-bulging and occipital protrusion; faces hafted forward of vault, large noses with unusual internal bony structures, retreating cheekbones — vs modern lightly built globular vaults and tiny retracted faces. Postcranially: thick-walled long bones, large joints, funnel-shaped thorax to broad flaring pelvis vs modern barrel torso; stiffness and hip rotation imply a different gait; robustness implies high strength and metabolic demand. Dental ultra-high-resolution work (as reported): developmental period extended vs earlier *Homo* but **shorter** than modern humans (e.g. upper M3 onset <6 years); shorter parental dependence and faster sexual maturity, coinciding with developmental-gene differences in genome analyses (as of Tattersall 2012). Imaging: many face differences established at birth; both born with longish skulls; moderns achieve globularization in the first year — researchers’ speculation of internal reorganization for symbolic cognition is **position**, not Tattersall’s primary claim.

**Genetics (2012 horizon).** 1997 Feldhofer mtDNA fell well outside modern variation (~26 differences vs ~8 among modern population pairs / ~55 humans–chimps); subsequent mtDNA across the range: low diversity (small populations), Neanderthals cluster together, no detectable contribution in extensive modern European mtDNA samples (as of Tattersall 2012). 2010 draft nuclear genome from three Vindija ~40 ka bones: after heavy algorithmic processing (“with which not everyone is entirely happy”), reported Neanderthals closer to present Eurasians than to sub-Saharan Africans → ~**1–4%** gene flow into non-African ancestors, oddly one-way Neanderthal → moderns (**FINDING as reported**, German group via Tattersall 2012). Tattersall treats species as “leaky vessels” among close Pleistocene relatives but argues skull-structure difference exceeds lion/tiger or hamadryas/gelada pairs → probability of **evolutionarily significant** interchange is negligible; claimed hybrids (Lagar Velho, Peștera cu Oase) he reinterprets as unusual modern humans; archaeology signals little cultural intermixing (**thesis**). Microcephalin rapid-spread ~37 ka introgression hypothesis he leaves premature (as of 2012). **Later Reich-layer percentages and maps supersede this horizon’s nuclear picture** — keep both dated.

**Diet and lifeways.** Thin on the ground; heavy dependence on animal fats/proteins in seasonal environments, flexible foragers. Italian adjacent sites: scavenging-like pattern ~120 ka vs ambush hunting of prime-age prey ~50 ka. Stable N isotopes: Neanderthals as top predators; St.-Césaire very late individual above local hyenas → mammoth/rhino specialization claim (French 2005 team via Tattersall). Routine fire/hearths; plant microfossils from dental plaque at Shanidar (~46 ka) and Spy (Belgium) show wide plant variety, some cooked — N isotopes miss non-protein plants; essentials of modern hunting-gathering established by Neanderthal times (attributed synthesis). Shanidar aged male with withered arm: social-support speculation. Domestic space typically little activity zoning vs symbolic modern sites. Group size: El Sidrón twelve; Abric Romaní ~8–10 (as of Tattersall 2012).

**El Sidrón (~50 ka).** ~1,800 fragments = 12 individuals (6 adults, 3 adolescents, 2 juveniles, 1 infant) in a collapse after death; cutting/percussion consistent with defleshing; Spanish team: massacre and **survival cannibalism** under stress (abundant enamel defects), contrasted with “gastronomic” Gran Dolina habit; skeptics: postmortem defleshing ≠ cannibalism. mtDNA: three adult males same lineage, each female different → male philopatry / female transfer as **potential** inference (Tattersall flags anthropomorphizing risk). Birth interval ~3 years conjecture from child ages; raw-material territorial conflict as speculative raid trigger.

**Burial.** Tattersall middle position: Neanderthals invented burial (explains intact remains); no really convincing modern-style ritual/grave goods; deep empathy “close to certain”; afterlife belief (would demand symbolic cognition) “far less probable” (attributed).

**Materials / Mousterian.** By diagnostic Neanderthal times, prepared-core [[mousterian-industry]] was entrenched; in Europe “virtually synonymous” with *H. neanderthalensis*, but **very similar kits** produced by other hominids in N. Africa and Levant (**industry ≠ species**). Continuum of forms via resharpening of superior materials; good stone transported many miles; resin hafting; bone/antler rarely used as tools (La Quina ~50 ka bone retoucher from hominid cranium as rare case). Skillful but “pretty stereotyped” craftsmanship; monotony over vast area.

**Cognitive contrast (core thesis).** Nothing in the technological record suggests symbolic thinkers “in the way that we are” — “like their ancestors, only more so. We are not. We are symbolic” (**thesis**, Tattersall 2012). Stone tools are poor proxies for symbolic thought; from the whole Neanderthal time–space expanse, nothing both confidently associated and unambiguously modern-cognitive as *routine* practice. Cro-Magnons enter Europe ~40 ka with Upper Paleolithic art (Lascaux, Chauvet, Altamira; Vogelherd horse ~34 ka) and higher site densities; Neanderthals disappear within ~10 kyr of contact (or already declining regionally — reported alternative). Châtelperronian (~36–29 ka) hybrid industry: traditional acculturation reading contested; Grotte du Renne / St.-Césaire associations questioned by mixing studies (as of 2012). Language gulf: Cro-Magnons almost certainly linguistic; Neanderthals likely not — FOXP2 shared human form is necessary not sufficient (developed further in his language chapter; pointer [[behavioral-modernity]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]] without rewriting those pages). Injustice, he insists, to treat Neanderthals as unsuccessful versions of ourselves.
