# Dawenkou Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Dawenkou culture, dated ca. 4300–2600 BCE per
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]], succeeded the Beixin culture
(ca. 5300–4300 BCE) in the lower Yellow River region of Shandong; its type site is
Dawenkou in Tai'an. Early Dawenkou was centered around the Taiyi Mountains, later
spreading into Shandong, northern Jiangsu, northern Anhui, and eastern Henan. Roughly
550 Dawenkou sites are known in Shandong, of which more than 20 have been excavated.
`construct_status: robust` — Liu & Chen never equate the culture with a language or
people.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** Agriculture — millet, rice, and soybean remains
— was well established, alongside domesticated pigs and dogs; pig heads and mandibles
were often used as mortuary offerings. Knives and sickles were common in tool
assemblages, while grinding stones (found at 21 or more sites, mainly Jiaodong-
peninsula shell middens and sites near the Taiyi Mountains) suggest continued nut and
cereal processing. Number of recorded sites in Shandong grew sharply through the
period: from 2 (Beixin) to 27 (late Dawenkou) to 463 (Longshan) in a southeastern
Shandong regional survey, and from 547 (Dawenkou) to 1,492 (Longshan) province-wide —
cited by Liu & Chen as evidence of substantial population growth. Salt-making
evidence near Bohai Bay is traced to Neolithic times: roughly 15 Dawenkou coastal
sites (4500–3000 BCE) in poor-soil, non-agricultural terrain show ash-filled burnt
pits interpreted as salt-making facilities, with non-local jade, stone, and painted
pottery (e.g., at Wucun, Guangrao County) indicating exchange networks.

More than twenty pictographs (eight types) have been found on *dakouzun* urns of the
late Dawenkou period; carved multi-character pottery sherds at Dinggong (Zouping,
Shandong) and Longqiuzhuang (Gaoyou, Jiangsu) date to ca. 2000 BCE. Scholars dispute
whether these pictographs and inscriptions constitute writing comparable to
oracle-bone or bronze script, or are instead non-linguistic signs or marks; attempts
to decipher the Dinggong inscriptions have reached no consensus (Feng, S. 1994;
Keightley 2006; Shandongsheng 2005; Yang, X. 2000) — a live position, not a settled
finding.

**Linguistic and genetic.** [[unknown]] — no linguistic or genetic-population
affiliation is claimed for Dawenkou populations in this source.

## Distribution and Chronology

Early Dawenkou was centered around the Taiyi Mountains in Shandong; the culture later
spread into northern Jiangsu, northern Anhui, and eastern Henan. Late Dawenkou (3000–
2600 BCE) was succeeded by [[lungshan-culture|Longshan culture]] (2600–1900 BCE) in
the lower Yellow River (Haidai) region.

## Lifeways

Most excavated Dawenkou sites are cemeteries; tombs are arranged in rows and clusters,
likely reflecting kin groups. At the type site, early-phase tombs are small with few
grave goods, while late-phase tombs show emergent hierarchy: some tombs (including
those of juveniles) are much larger, with far more elaborate goods — large tombs held
100–200 grave goods versus few in small ones. High-status or ritual items (turtle
shells, ivory or bone carved cylinders, jade, black pottery goblets, pig mandibles)
concentrate in the richer tombs. At Lingyanghe, 663 drinking goblets were found across
45 tombs (45 percent of all grave goods there); Tomb M25 alone held more than 30
goblets among 73 vessels, with a male occupant. Liu & Chen (citing Fung, C. 2000; Liu,
L. 2004; Underhill 2000) hold that "the development of mortuary stratification was
closely associated with the emergence of social hierarchy within kin-based
communities," with men generally holding higher status than women (with exceptions)
— an attributed interpretive claim inferred from differential burial treatment, not
a brute fact.

Dawenkou funerary ceremonies were directed mainly toward individual, especially male,
ancestors; Liu & Chen suggest such individually oriented ritual "may have in turn
stimulated the development of social hierarchy," an explicit contrast with the more
group-oriented [[yangshao-culture]] mortuary pattern. At Yuchisi (Mengcheng, Anhui;
2800–2600 BCE), a late Dawenkou village yielded houses with unusually large numbers
of ceramic vessels.

Shelach-Lavi ([[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]]) describes
grave M10 at Dawenkou (4.2 × 3.2 m, with a secondary ledge and wooden coffin) as
belonging to an elderly female, containing ninety-three vessels along with a large jade
axe, jade ornaments, and four pig mandibles — among the richer known individual burials
of the culture. He also frames the Shandong Longshan mortuary tradition as a direct
continuation of Dawenkou practice, with rich/poor grave disparity increasing further
into the Longshan period, and drinking cups and wine vessels in late-Dawenkou and
Longshan graves associated with feasting (see [[lungshan-culture]]).

## Relations and Successions

Dawenkou is `preceded_by` Beixin culture (ca. 5300–4300 BCE, not yet a wiki page;
sedentary villages up to 10 ha, ground tools, millet, pig/dog domesticates, minimal
social hierarchy, gender-based rather than status-based division of labor in
burials) and, at a further remove, follows the regional early-Neolithic
[[houli-culture]] tradition. It is `succeeded_by` [[lungshan-culture]] (2600–1900
BCE), whose decline around 2000 BCE was marked by a sharp drop in settlement numbers
and the disappearance of most regional centers, though some (e.g., Chengziya)
persisted into the succeeding Yueshi culture. Dawenkou is `contrasts_with`
[[yangshao-culture]] in its individually-oriented, male-ancestor-focused mortuary
ritual, and `analogous_to` [[hongshan-culture]] in developing elaborate elite jade
and ritual practice within the same broad Middle Neolithic horizon (per Liu, X. 2004
and Liu & Chen's synthesis of Chang's "Chinese Interaction Sphere" concept).

## Identity Cautions

Dawenkou is an etic archaeological-culture construct defined by ceramics, mortuary
pattern, and settlement distribution. Liu & Chen do not equate it with a language,
ethnic group, or genetic population. The *dakouzun* pictographs and Dinggong/
Longqiuzhuang inscriptions are explicitly not treated by the authors as evidence of
a developed writing system tied to a known language — most scholars (following Boltz
1986; Keightley 2006) regard comparable late-Neolithic marks as pictographic or
schematic clan emblems ancestral to later Shang inscriptions, "unlikely to have
intended to write the name of the clan" and lacking assessable phonetic values;
Liu & Chen describe these as, at most, possible "proto-writing," not a decipherable
linguistic system.

## Historiography

Dawenkou's mortuary stratification is a key data point in debates over the origins
of social hierarchy in Neolithic China, and its salt-making and exchange evidence
feeds broader arguments about early economic specialization. The dakouzun/Dinggong
inscription debate remains genuinely unresolved among specialists. See
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source discussion.
