## Definition and Construct History

"Ancestral North Indians" (ANI) is a name coined by David Reich and colleagues (with Nick Patterson and collaborators) for one of two highly differentiated ancestral populations whose mixture, modeled from genome-wide data, produced the ancestry of most present-day Indian groups (as of Reich 2018). The ANI/[[ancestral-south-indians|ASI]] framework was first proposed circa 2009 and substantially revised in 2016 by Iosif Lazaridis and Nick Patterson working in Reich's lab. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]].

The ANI is a [[ghost-population]] in the technical sense used by Reich's group: a population inferred statistically from the admixture signal in present-day and ancient genomes, not directly sampled from an ancient burial (as of Reich 2018).

## Genetic Signature

Before mixing with the [[ancestral-south-indians|ASI]], the ANI and ASI were estimated to be as genetically differentiated from one another as present-day Europeans and East Asians are today (as of Reich 2018). The original (2009) formulation described the ANI only as related to Europeans, Central Asians, Near Easterners, and peoples of the Caucasus, with no claim about a specific homeland or migration route (as of Reich 2018).

The 2016 revision (Lazaridis and Patterson) found the ANI were themselves a mixture of roughly 50% ancestry related distantly to the [[yamnaya-culture]] steppe population and roughly 50% ancient-Iranian-farmer-related ancestry (as of Reich 2018). Reich frames this as part of a parallel two-stage migration model across Eurasia: an early Holocene farmer migration (Iranian-related, into South Asia; Anatolian-related, into Europe) followed by a later steppe-pastoralist migration after roughly 5,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018).

Six Brahmin groups analyzed showed a higher ratio of steppe-related to Iranian-farmer-related ancestry than the general ANI-ASI mixture model predicted, which Reich interprets as internal ANI substructure preserved by caste endogamy, and offers as further evidence that Indo-European culture — not language alone — was spread by steppe-derived peoples (as of Reich 2018).

ANI ancestry contribution to present-day India is sex-biased: the male (Y-chromosome) line carries disproportionately more ANI-derived ancestry than the maternal (mitochondrial DNA) line, which remains overwhelmingly ASI-derived even in northern India — indicating ANI-descended males had disproportionate reproductive success (as of Reich 2018). See [[sex-biased-admixture]].

## Distribution and Chronology

Priya Moorjani, in Reich's lab, dated ANI-ASI admixture across Indian groups to between roughly 4,000 and 2,000 years ago, using the length of contiguous ANI/ASI ancestry chromosome segments broken up by recombination since mixture (as of Reich 2018). Indo-European-speaking Indian groups show on average more recent mixture dates than Dravidian-speaking groups, a pattern Reich attributes to multiple successive waves of ANI-related mixture spreading south over time (as of Reich 2018).

West-Eurasian-related (ANI) ancestry proportions across Indian groups today range from about 20% to 80%, correlating with both language (more in Indo-European speakers) and traditional caste status (more in higher-status groups, e.g. Brahmins compared to co-linguistic neighbors of lower status) (as of Reich 2018).

## Lifeways

The ANI is a statistically inferred ancestry component, not a sampled archaeological population with directly documented material culture; Reich's account attaches no independent lifeways evidence to the ANI beyond what is inferred from its constituent steppe- and Iranian-farmer-related source populations (as of Reich 2018).

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by` [[yamnaya-culture]] (partial, steppe-related component only) — associated_with, not identity: the ANI is modeled as roughly half steppe-related and half Iranian-farmer-related ancestry, not as a lineal continuation of Yamnaya. The ANI is `associated_with` the spread of [[indo-european-languages]] in South Asia: Reich infers that groups with more ANI ancestry tend to speak Indo-European languages while groups with more ASI ancestry tend to speak Dravidian languages, and suggests the ANI probably spread Indo-European languages, though this is an attributed inference from correlation, not a demonstrated identity between the genetic population and the language family (as of Reich 2018).

The ancestry of the Indus Valley Civilization remains unresolved in Reich's account: as of his writing, three competing hypotheses were still live — unmixed Iranian-farmer descendants speaking early Dravidian, an ASI-like population, or an ANI-like population — pending ancient DNA from Indus Valley sites (as of Reich 2018).

## Identity Cautions

ANI is a genetic-ancestry construct inferred from statistical modeling of present-day and some ancient genomes; it is not a self-conscious people, an archaeological culture with directly excavated remains, or a language group. Its association with Indo-European speech and with steppe-descended peoples is an attributed inference, not an established identity (four-way non-identity: culture/language/genes/people must not be conflated). The politically fraught "Aryan invasion theory," historically appropriated by European (including Nazi) and Indian nationalists, is a separate and older narrative that Reich explicitly distinguishes from the cautious ANI/ASI admixture model; his account notes that this political history has suppressed open scholarly discussion of Indo-European migration into South Asia (as of Reich 2018).

## Historiography

The ANI/ASI framework originated in a 2009 Reich-lab paper and was substantially revised by Lazaridis and Patterson in 2016 after wider ancient-DNA sampling of Eurasia became available, replacing the earlier assumption that the ASI carried no West Eurasian ancestry (as of Reich 2018). Reich's collaborators Lalji Singh and Kumarasamy Thangaraj initially objected to "West Eurasian mixture" framing and proposed "genetic sharing" instead, to avoid implying a directional migration; Reich states the whole-genome data nonetheless demonstrated real, profound admixture (as of Reich 2018).
