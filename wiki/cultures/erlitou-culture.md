# Erlitou Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The **Erlitou culture** names the material assemblage of the first archaeologically demonstrable
state-level society in the Central Plain of China, defined from the type site of
[[erlitou|Erlitou]] in Yanshi County, western Henan, discovered in 1959 by Xu Xusheng while he was
searching for a Xia-dynasty capital recorded in later texts. More than 300 sites sharing Erlitou
material characteristics are known across the middle Yellow River valley. Liu & Chen (2012) adopt
a chronology of **c. 1900–1500 BCE** for the book, while noting a competing, narrower AMS-based
range of c. 1750–1530 BCE derived from carbonized-seed dates at Xinzhai and Erlitou (Zhang et al.
2007) against which earlier Huizui dates conflict — the dating is genuinely disputed (as of Liu &
Chen 2012).

Whether the Erlitou culture corresponds to the **Xia dynasty** of later textual tradition is a
long-standing dispute; Liu & Chen decline to make the identification. See
[[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]].

The type site is occupied c. 1900/1850–1550 BCE across four phases, reaching its maximum
extent and building activity in Phases II–III; some recently published radiocarbon dates push
the culture's beginning into the eighteenth century BCE ([[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]]). Whether Erlitou crossed a
qualitative "state" threshold is itself contested: against Liu & Chen's reading above,
Shelach-Lavi (2015) argues the site's scale, "palace" size, and burial wealth are not clearly
exceptional relative to Late Neolithic centers such as Taosi, and identifies the following
[[erligang-culture|Erligang]] period, not Erlitou, as where the step-like sociopolitical change
he sees actually occurs — recorded as a position on [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]] rather than
adjudicated here.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Erlitou is defined by the first appearance of **piece-mold cast bronze ritual
vessels** (*jue*, *jia*, *he*) — the sole such evidence among contemporary Chinese sites and the
technical ancestor of the later Shang bronze tradition — alongside turquoise-inlaid bronze
plaques, jade *ge* and *zhang* blades, and a distinctive fine grey/black ceramic assemblage. More
than a hundred metal items (copper and various copper-tin, copper-lead, and arsenical-copper
alloys) have been recovered; one arsenic-bronze item points to contact with the
[[siba-culture|Siba culture]] in Gansu.

**Linguistic.** More than forty glyphs occur on Erlitou pottery, some resembling later oracle-bone
forms. Liu & Chen and others do **not** treat them as a writing system; their status is contested.
No text identifies the polity by name.

**Genetic.** Not systematically addressed by Liu & Chen (2012).

## Distribution and Chronology

The Yiluo Basin core shows a **three-tiered regional settlement hierarchy**: [[erlitou|Erlitou]]
itself (a 300 ha primary center) plus more than 200 smaller sites across an ~860 km² survey
region. Two regional variants are distinguished — Erlitou (Henan) and Dongxiafeng (Shanxi) — with
scattered Erlitou components near the middle Yangzi and the Dan River marking southward expansion.
Unlike the preceding Longshan pattern of many small competing walled polities, the Erlitou primary
center was itself **unfortified** while peripheral centers (Dashigu, Wangjinglou) were walled —
which Liu & Chen read as a shift to a monocentered political system.

## Lifeways

The culture is defined chiefly by its political and craft economy rather than its subsistence
base: attached specialist production of prestige goods (bronze, turquoise, jade) clustered near
the Erlitou palatial zone, supplying an elite whose authority was expressed through monopolized
ritual-vessel casting. Liu & Chen argue Erlitou expansion into its periphery (Dongxiafeng, Nanguan,
Donglongshan, and south to [[panlongcheng|Panlongcheng]]) was driven by procurement of **metals
and salt**, establishing a hegemonic core–periphery system with a heterarchical dimension
(independent hinterland craftsmen pursuing status through skill) — an attributed interpretation.

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from:** local Central Plain Longshan / Xinzhai-phase antecedents.
- **Succeeded by:** the [[erligang-culture|Erligang culture]] ("early Shang"), whose walled cities
  at [[zhengzhou|Zhengzhou]] and Yanshi arose during Erlitou Phase IV. Many Chinese archaeologists
  read the rise of Yanshi Shang City as the archaeological trace of Shang's conquest of Xia.
- **Associated with (not identity):** the historical Xia dynasty — a *claim* requiring argument,
  recorded at [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]], not asserted here.

## Identity Cautions

Erlitou is an **etic archaeological construct** defined by a material assemblage. It is not, in
itself, the "Xia dynasty," a language, or a genetic population. Liu & Chen argue the Erlitou
polity's initial population likely coalesced from discrete migrant kin groups **without** a
pre-existing overarching ethnic identity, rather than representing a bounded people called "Xia" or
"Shang." Pots, palaces, and dynastic names are three different things.

## Historiography

Erlitou has been read through successive lenses: as the Xia capital (the dominant view in Chinese
archaeology), through Xia Nai's Childe-derived four-criteria test for civilization, and through the
anthropological "social archaeology" of settlement hierarchy that Liu & Chen (2012) adopt, by which
Erlitou qualifies as a **state** (a professional ruling class over a commoner class, with a
multi-tier settlement hierarchy) regardless of its dynastic name. Sarah Allan (2007) and Yoffee &
Li (2009) offer parallel models in which a shared elite religious/value culture crystallizing at
Erlitou constitutes "civilization." (Source: [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]].)

**Chang 1980 (attributed).** *[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]* places
Erh-li-t'ou **outside Shang**, between Honan Lung-shan and Erh-li-kang, and treats it as the
archaeological correlate of **Hsia** in a partly horizontal Three Dynasties model — see
[[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]].
