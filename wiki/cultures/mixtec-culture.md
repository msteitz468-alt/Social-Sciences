# Mixtec Culture

The Mixtec homeland, per Ignacio Bernal (quoted by Coe & Koontz 2013), was "a
succession of very small, rather prosperous valleys surrounded by large areas of nearby
desert," in the rugged mountains of western and northern Oaxaca.

## Definition and Construct History

Ronald Spores's (Vanderbilt University) archaeological survey found Mixtec
Classic-period settlements on hilltops shifting, by the Post-Classic, down into
valleys; per John Pohl and Bruce Byland, these were organized into multiple independent
kingdoms/polities with no large-scale political integration or large cities. Scarce
arable land drove endemic warfare along kingdom borders.

Four pre-Conquest Mixtec codices survive — folding, gessoed-deerskin books produced late
in the Post-Classic for the nobility. Via Alfonso Caso's research they extend Mixtec
history further back than any other non-Maya Mexican annals; Emily Rabin's analysis
established that the codices cover a roughly 600-year span beginning about AD 940. Jill
Furst (quoted/paraphrased by Coe & Koontz) describes the codices as concerned with
"historical events and genealogies... records of births, marriages, offspring, and
sometimes the deaths of native rulers, and their conflicts to retain their lands and
wars to extend their domains" — the exception being the obverse of the Vienna Codex, a
land document beginning in a mythical "first time" (ancestral Mixtecs emerging from a
tree at Apoala) that establishes lineage rights to rule via divine/ancestral sanction.

## Material / Linguistic Signature

Mixtec codex dates use only the 52-year Calendar Round (year sign + Year Bearer +
260-day count date); reading order varies by codex (right-to-left or left-to-right,
with red horizontal guidelines). Individuals are identified by their 260-day birthdate
plus an iconic "nickname" rather than portraiture, with costume conveying office and
ceremonial detail.

Coe & Koontz assess Mixtec writing as comparatively rudimentary next to Zapotec (its
likely source) and Maya script, arguing this was a deliberate strategy enabling
cross-language legibility — Chocho, Zapotec, and Nahua speakers could read it without
knowing Mixtec. The associated "Mixteca-Puebla" art style's origins are, per Coe &
Koontz, "still hotly debated" — **flagged here as a live scholarly dispute, not
resolved by this source** — and the style achieved wide Mesoamerican distribution by
the fourteenth century, exceeding the earlier Toltec symbol system's reach; it reached
as far north as Cholula (see [[concepts/postclassic-stage]]).

## Distribution and Chronology

Mixtec expansion over the Mixteca and most Zapotec territory came through both
conquest and strategic marriage; shared oracular shrines and cult practices helped
manage internecine rivalry among competing lords, and by the Conquest the nobility
traced descent from a shared set of ancestors via a common heroic saga.

Per the codices, the Post-Classic Mixtec political landscape traces to the fall of
"Hill of the Wasp" (Yucu Yoco), a Classic-period hilltop capital in the southern
Nochixtlan Valley, in the late tenth century, followed by a sixteen-year, three-faction
conflict called the "War of Heaven," ending with the founding of the first Tilantongo
dynasty ("Black Town"), which jointly ruled with "Xipe Bundle" until its own fall. The
culture hero 9 Wind is credited with a role in this narrative.

The best-documented figure is 8-Deer "Jaguar Claw," of Tilantongo's second dynasty,
active in the Calendar Round AD 1063–1115. He attacked "Red and White Bundle" and
sacrificed its lord and Lady 6-Monkey; in 1097 he traveled to Tollan and was invested
with the Toltec nose-button by the Toltec king "4 Jaguar," likely marking his
accession — Coe & Koontz liken this Toltec investiture to the pope crowning the Holy
Roman Emperor (attributed analogy). He married five princesses, some from towns he had
subjugated, and was ultimately killed by Lady 6-Monkey's son, whose own daughter later
married into 8-Deer's descendant line. 8-Deer's exploits legitimized several Mixtec
royal houses into the Conquest era.

By c. AD 1350, Mixtecs infiltrated the Valley of Oaxaca chiefly via royal marriage; by
the Conquest, nearly all Zapotec sites were Mixtec-occupied. The Tomb 7 treasure at
Monte Albán (mid-14th century) — gold (lost-wax cast) and silver objects, turquoise
mosaics, rock crystal, amber, jet, coral, and pearl jewelry, and carved jaguar bone — is
attributed to Mixtec goldsmithing and turquoise-mosaic craftsmanship. Alfonso Caso's
team identified the central Tomb 7 skeleton as male, but other evidence (a weaving kit
with battens, picks, spindle whorls, and bowls) suggests strong female associations; Coe
& Koontz conclude there is "little firm evidence" the skeleton was female but suggest
the figure was likely "a female deity impersonator," noting both Mixtec and Aztec
cultures had powerful political/ritual posts linked to female deities — **this
attribution is contested and recorded as a position, not settled fact.**

Zaachila, a Valley town divided between Zapotec and Mixtec descendants, had a Zapotec
king but Mixtec material culture; its tombs yielded Mixtec-style treasure comparable to
Tomb 7, including a painted cup with a three-dimensional blue-hummingbird figure.

## Lifeways

Endemic warfare along kingdom borders (per Spores/Pohl/Byland); a nobility legitimized
through genealogical descent narratives, marriage alliance, and material display
(goldwork, turquoise mosaic); shared oracular shrines used to manage rivalry among
competing lords.

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` the Zapotec (via conquest, marriage, and shared sites — see
[[sites/mitla]]); despite being targeted by Aztec expansion, the Mixtecs allied with the
Zapotecs and were never fully conquered by the [[cultures/aztec-empire]].

## Identity Cautions

"Mixtec" here denotes both a linguistic/ethnic identity and the codex-documented
political history of the Mixteca region; the four-way non-identity rule applies to the
"Mixteca-Puebla" art style specifically — its wide geographic distribution reflects
style transmission across language and polity boundaries, not a single unified Mixtec
political entity.

## Historiography

Alfonso Caso's codex research and Tomb 7 excavation are foundational; Ronald Spores's
settlement-pattern survey and the codex analyses of Emily Rabin and Jill Furst represent
later 20th-century scholarship extending and complicating the picture. The origins of
the Mixteca-Puebla style remain actively debated.

## The Cloud People (Flannery & Marcus, eds., 1983)

[[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983) treats the Mixtec as one half of a [[divergent-evolution|divergent evolution]] from a common proto-Otomanguean ([[otomanguean-languages]]) ancestor shared with the [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec]]. Its principal Mixtec contributors are [[spores-ronald|Ronald Spores]] (archaeology and ethnohistory of the Mixteca Alta) and [[smith-mary-elizabeth|Mary Elizabeth Smith]] (the [[mixtec-codices|codices]]).

- **Slower Formative trajectory.** [[spores-ronald|Spores]] argued the Nochixtlán Valley/Mixteca Alta lagged the Valley of Oaxaca in Formative complexity; no Mixteca site before the Terminal Formative approaches [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]] in scale. [[huamelulpan|Huamelulpan]] and [[monte-negro|Monte Negro]] are the region's early ceremonial centers; **Yucuita** became its first urban center in the Ramos phase (ca. 200 BCE–300 CE).
- **Origins of stratification.** Spores hypothesized (attributed) that Mixtec social stratification arose internally from religious specialists converting supernatural authority into control of goods and labor — not from occupational specialization or external conquest. Sixteenth-century sources document two hereditary strata (rulers/*yya*, nobles/*tay toho*) over commoners, plus landless *terrazgueros* (*tay situndayu*).
- **The codices.** [[smith-mary-elizabeth|Smith]] read Mixtec writing as a "limited"/logographic system recording chiefly personal and place names, with narrative carried pictorially; she argued Codex Selden derives from Magdalena Jaltepec in the Nochixtlán Valley, and that each codex records a parochial dynastic point of view (150 of Selden's 204 named persons appear in no other manuscript).
- **The "Mixtec impact" question.** Whether Late Postclassic Monte Albán V marks a Mixtec population in the Valley of Oaxaca or Zapotec continuity with elite intermarriage is the volume's central staged dispute — see the [[mixtec-impact-valley-of-oaxaca-debate|Mixtec-impact debate]].

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — IIIa pressure / later alliances

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]]. Light note — four-way non-identity: Mixtec speakers ≠ archaeological Monte Albán culture ≠ genetic population.)*

**Original occupants vs later entrants.** Marcus & Flannery argue archaeological and ethnohistoric research shows **Zapotec as original occupants** of the Valley of Oaxaca while **Mixtec speakers entered later**, often through arranged marriages with Zapotec nobles; most Mixtec speakers lived in mountain valleys north and west of Etla.

**Monte Albán IIIa pressure.** By Period IIIa the Zapotec were losing outer Monte Albán II provinces ([[monte-negro|Monte Negro]] largely abandoned; Quiotepec eventually given up). Mixtec were building major centers (e.g. **Yucunudahui**); authors state they “probably had designs on the Valley of Oaxaca” and would later establish a foothold — if Etla’s demographic decline partly reflects vulnerability, Mixtec are the “most clear and present danger.” Period IIIa Zapotec stop expanding outward and solidify valley control via diplomacy and warfare; defense priority rises (38 fortified/defensible sites).

**Later alliances.** Ethnohistory they use includes Cociyoeza’s alliance with Mixtec of Achiutla against Aztec Ahuitzotl; succession practice includes strategic Mixtec marriages; later history includes Mixtec–Zapotec marriage alliances bringing foreign nobles into the western valley. Aligns with the Cloud People Mixtec-impact debate without resolving it.
