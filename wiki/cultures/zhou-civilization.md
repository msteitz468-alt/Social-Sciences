# Chou (Zhou) Civilization

## Definition and Construct History

**Chou** (pinyin *Zhou*) names the North China civilization that occupied the historical
stage from the Conquest of [[shang-civilization|Shang]] until the Ch'in (Qin) unification of
221 BCE. As presented by [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|Chang (1963)]], Chou is
conventionally divided into **Western Chou** (capital in the Weishui valley near Sian) and,
from 771 BCE, **Eastern Chou** (capital moved to Lo-yang), the latter subdivided into the
Ch'un-ch'iu (Spring-and-Autumn) and Chan-kuo (Warring States) periods. Chang used round
figures — ~1100 BCE Conquest, 770 BCE start of Eastern Chou, ~450 BCE start of Warring
States — noting the Conquest date is disputed among ten proposed values (traditional 1122
BCE; others from 1116 to 1027 BCE).

`construct_status: conventional` — "Chou" is a dynastic-historical label mapped onto material
assemblages; do not equate it with a single genetic population. Its script is ancestral to
Chinese writing, but culture, language, and population remain distinct claims.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Continuity from Shang (Chang's thesis, attributed).** Pre-Conquest Western Chou is
archaeologically a near-blank hiatus; Chang concluded that after the Conquest, Chou was "a
linear descendant of the Shang civilization" with "no major discontinuity," and read the
Conquest itself as an **internal power struggle within a shared cultural sphere**, not a
conquest by an alien people. On this basis he explicitly refuted Wang Kuo-wei's claim that
feudalism and the *tsung-fa* kinship system were post-Conquest Chou inventions, arguing
oracle-bone evidence shows these institutions in rudimentary form already in the late Shang.

**Bronze and the iron transition.** Bronze was the sole significant artifact material through
Shang, Western Chou, and early Eastern Chou (c. 1700–500 BCE), reserved for ceremonial
vessels and weapons. Iron became significant only at the end of the Ch'un-ch'iu / beginning of
the Chan-kuo period; China notably developed **cast iron** (pig iron) early — metallographic
study of Warring-States iron implements found them all cast. Early iron plows (e.g. the
V-shaped pieces from Ku-wei-ts'un, Hui Hsien) were "highly primitive"; cattle-drawn
deep-plowing plows do not appear archaeologically until the middle Western Han.

**Bronze-style periodization.** Chang recorded the parallel schemes Kuo Mo-jo and Bernhard
Karlgren independently derived c. 1930 from bronze typology and inscriptions (Kuo's
Beginnings / Florescence / Decadence / Renaissance / Decline; Karlgren's Archaic / Middle
Chou / Huai). Chang's own restudy argued early Western Chou bronze art was "a continuation of
the Yin bronze art," disputing the significance of Karlgren's "Yin-Chou" subdivision, and
rejected Karlgren's contact-with-alien-peoples explanation for the Middle Chou stylistic
break in favor of internal development.

## Distribution and Chronology

The chapter's evidence is dominated by **Eastern Chou walled city sites**, each a state
capital: Lo-yang (Royal Chou), Hsin-t'ien (Jin, near Houma), Wo-kuo (Jin), Han-tan (Zhao),
Hsia-tu (Yan), and Lin-tzu (Qi). Chang traced six constants across Shang-through-early-Han
cities (walled; stamped-earth walls; rectangular; cardinal orientation; earthen platforms
under important buildings; specialized internal quarters) and a **layout change from late
Ch'un-ch'iu**: from a simple aristocratic center plus external quarters (the Shang / early
Chou pattern of Cheng-chou, Anyang, Hsin-t'ien) to enclosing industrial, commercial, and
residential quarters within an additional larger wall — a triple spatial division of
aristocracy / industry-commerce / farming.

## Lifeways

Burial custom shows Yin–Chou constants (vertical pits, wooden coffins and chambers, a central
waist-pit with dog sacrifice, extended posture, grave goods scaled to family means) and
Eastern Chou changes (cave-chamber tombs and hollow-brick chambers late in the Warring
States; burial mounds becoming general only from Eastern Chou; flexed posture, present
throughout Chou, becoming numerically significant only in the Chan-kuo). Chang argued the
period's two interlinked trends were the **territorial expansion of Chinese civilization** and
the **accelerating growth of internal society, economy, and political organization** —
distinguishing cultural influence, political control, and full assimilation as three
different relationships he judged "in most cases impossible to recognize" archaeologically.

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from:** [[shang-civilization|Shang]] (linear cultural descent, per Chang).
- **Produced:** the conditions for Ch'in unification (221 BCE) — for Chang a *political
  followup* to a cultural unification already underway by late Eastern Chou.
- **Radiated into:** South China (the Ch'u, Wu, and Yüeh states of the Yangtze) and, via those,
  Szechwan and Yunnan — Chang's second and third "waves" of northern influence (see
  [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]). The southern Ch'u kingdom is treated by Chang as
  "basically derivative of the Chou civilization" acculturated onto a native Geometric base,
  yet politically and culturally the most powerful state of South China.

## Identity Cautions

- **Dynastic label, not an ethnos or a genetic population.** Chang treated Western-Hsia /
  Eastern-Yih ethnic framings as geographic variants of "the same Chinese people," and
  cautioned that northern-frontier material with steppe affinities marks a way of life, not a
  demonstrable foreign ethnicity.
- **"Conquest" ≠ population replacement.** Chang's reading of 1122/1100 BCE as an intra-cultural
  power struggle is an attributed interpretation, not a settled fact.

## Historiography

From Chang's [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]] synthesis, whose Chou chapter leans
more on excavated city walls, bronzes, and tombs than on the rich transmitted textual record
(which Chang deliberately underused). Chronology rests on bronze typology and a disputed
traditional Conquest date rather than radiocarbon. Densify from Zhouyuan, Fengxi, and
Marquis-of-Jin cemetery reports and from the later bronze-inscription scholarship when
ingested.

## Liu & Chen (2012) note — proto-Zhou as a peripheral late-Shang neighbor

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 10, "The Late Shang Dynasty and Its Neighbors, 1250–1046 BC." This
chapter treats the Zhou only as a peripheral neighbor of the late Shang, not as a civilization
in its own right — the source's fuller Zhou treatment lies outside the material available to
this ingest; keep this note brief pending a future ingest densifying Zhou proper.)*

"Proto-Zhou" sites are recorded in the Wei River region (Shaanxi), part of the "Western Land"
(*xitu*) settlement cluster alongside the Laoniupo site cluster. Liu & Chen state that
Laoniupo's sudden decline after its Phase IV "was most likely caused by the intervention of the
Zhou, who finally conquered Shang" (Liu, S. 2001) — the chapter's only reference to the
conquest event itself, given without an independently argued date in this range; the
conventional 1046 BC end date for late Shang (matching this page's `date_start`) is used as a
period boundary rather than defended here. Some Dayangzhou (Xin'gan, Jiangxi) bronzes of the
late Shang period are classified stylistically as "proto-Zhou style," alongside Shang,
indigenous, and mixed styles — evidence of stylistic contact rather than Zhou political
presence that far south.

## Shelach-Lavi (2015) — Western/Eastern Zhou archaeology, ritual reform, and "globalization"

*(Attributed — [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]], ch. 10.)*

Shelach-Lavi treats the Zhou conquest of Shang at the **Battle of Muye**, traditionally dated
1046 BCE, as the date now generally accepted following the Xia-Shang-Zhou Chronology Project.
The Western Zhou royal domain was centered on **Hao** (near modern Xi'an), with a secondary
capital at **Chengzhou/Luoyi** (near modern Luoyang); in 771 BCE the Wei River basin capital was
overrun by an alliance of disaffected regional lords and Quanrong invaders, ending Western Zhou
rule there and beginning the Eastern Zhou, centered on Chengzhou. Eastern Zhou Wangcheng (near
Luoyang) enclosed only ~9 km², small relative to later Warring States capitals — read by
Shelach-Lavi as reflecting the diminished political weight of the royal house after 771 BCE.

At **Zhouyuan**, the Fengchu palace complex (dated to the late predynastic/early Western Zhou
period) stands on a 1.3 m pounded-earth platform, covers ~1,500 m², and is symmetrically
arranged on a north–south axis. **Grave M18** at Zhougong Miao (a proposed Zhou royal cemetery
north of Mount Qi) has four descending ramps (*mudao*), a feature associated with royal burial,
but is markedly smaller than the Shang royal tombs at Xibeigang — Shelach-Lavi reads the
disparity as consistent with a still-developing, less monumentalized royal mortuary tradition
under the early Zhou.

Jessica Rawson's (1999) identification of a **"late Western Zhou ritual reform,"** beginning
between the mid-10th and mid-9th centuries BCE, is reported as Rawson's finding (as of
Shelach-Lavi 2015 relying on Rawson 1999): Shang-style alcohol vessels disappear from ritual
sets in favor of food vessels (*ding*, *gui*), standardized under sumptuary rules known as *lie
ding*. Shaughnessy (1988) is cited for the finding that Shang chariots were used mainly for
display and hunting rather than warfare, and that their military potential was not realized
until roughly the later Western Zhou.

At the **Liulihe cemetery**, excavators divided burials into an area associated with Shang
descendants/local population and an area associated with Yan-lineage Zhou descendants; Jaffe
(2013), cited by Shelach-Lavi, disputes this clean two-population reading, arguing mortuary
practice at Liulihe instead negotiated social identity between local and incoming populations —
an attributed interpretive dispute, not settled here.

For **Eastern Zhou**, Falkenhausen (1999) argues that the modern archaeological practice of
defining separate "state cultures" (Jin culture, Qi culture, Qin culture, etc.) is a manufactured
construct that obscures the more salient process of increasing elite **cultural convergence**
across the Zhou sphere alongside resurgent local/particularistic identity — a caution directly
relevant to this page's `construct_status: conventional` and to the four-way non-identity rule
(a shared "Zhou culture" label should not be read as a single self-conscious ethnos). Wu Hung
(1999) is cited for a parallel stylistic argument: Zhou/Warring States public architecture shifted
from a "two-dimensional," self-contained Western Zhou tradition to a "three-dimensional"
monumental style in the Warring States period. Shelach-Lavi draws his own explicit — and
flagged-as-his-own — analogy between this Eastern Zhou elite convergence-plus-local-resistance
pattern and modern globalization, while noting the causal drivers differ (ancient: political-military
expansion; modern: international economic processes and migration).
