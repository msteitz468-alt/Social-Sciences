## Definition and Construct History

"Elam" and "Elamites" denote the polities and population of southwest Iran centered on
Susa (the lowland capital) and Anshan (a highland center roughly 500 km to the
southeast), attested across three widely separated dynastic phases from the mid-third
millennium into the mid-first millennium BCE, and absorbed into the Achaemenid Persian
empire under Cyrus II (see [[achaemenid-empire]]). Van De Mieroop 2016 devotes an entire
section to what he treats as the best-evidenced episode, the Middle Elamite kingdom
(ca. 1400–1100 BCE), while cautioning that even this best-documented period leaves "our
grasp on it... limited": reconstruction rests almost entirely on royal building
inscriptions — which supply approximate territorial extent and genealogy, with
scholarly disagreement even on king sequencing — plus occasional Babylonian-side
references to war and marriage. No Elamite royal archives survive, and no genuine
Elamite-side letters are known (only late, possibly fictional copies).

Elam's political unity was broken by Hammurabi's eighteenth-century-BCE defeat of Elam
and was not restored, per Van De Mieroop 2016, until roughly 1400 BCE — though rulers at
Susa styled themselves "King of Susa and Anshan" (in Akkadian) or "King of Anshan and
Susa" (in Elamite) as early as 1500 BCE. Van De Mieroop 2016 explicitly flags this
titulary as possibly aspirational rather than descriptive: "This may have been a
fiction... we have no evidence of control outside the Susiana plain."

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** The type example of Middle Elamite royal building is Al-Untash-Napirisha
(modern Chogha Zanbil), a wholly new city built by Untash-Napirisha in the late
fourteenth century BCE, centered on a massive ziggurat dedicated jointly to Napirisha
(the chief Elamite god) and Inshushinak (Susa's patron deity), inside a double-enclosure
complex with secular buildings. Van De Mieroop 2016 describes it as "the best-preserved
ziggurat in the Near East," owing to construction quality — a two-meter-thick baked-brick
casing over a sun-dried core, with dedicatory inscribed bricks every tenth course. The
city did not survive as a capital past its founder's death, reverting to secondary
status behind Susa — a pattern Van De Mieroop 2016 notes recurs across the Late Bronze
Age (compare Dur-Kurigalzu and Kar-Tukulti-Ninurta).

**Linguistic.** The four-way non-identity distinction is directly evidenced in Elam's
own record: the official administrative language shifted from Akkadian (dominant in
earlier Elamite history) to Elamite after ca. 1400 BCE — a datable, documented
language-policy change tied to dynastic and political shift, not a fixed ethnic marker.
Elamite gods correspondingly displaced Mesopotamian deities in the OFFICIAL cult only
after 1400 BCE, though Mesopotamian worship continued unofficially. Elamite is a
language isolate; it is not genetically related to Akkadian (Semitic), nor to the later
Old Persian of the Achaemenid dynasty (Iranian, Indo-European) — even though Elamite
served as the Achaemenid empire's primary working administrative language (the
Persepolis Fortification and Treasury archives are overwhelmingly Elamite-language) and
appeared, alongside Old Persian and Akkadian, on the Bisitun trilingual inscription,
carved in Elamite first.

**Genetic.** [[unknown]] — no archaeogenetic material appears in the ingested source.

Van De Mieroop 2016 stresses that post-1400 BCE Elam was internally two-tiered rather
than a homogeneous bloc: it joined Susiana-lowland populations with strong Mesopotamian
cultural influence to Anshan-highland populations that preserved distinct local
traditions, "genuinely different cultural traditions" in both language use and
religious practice — an explicit caution against treating "Elamite culture" as a single
undifferentiated identity.

## Distribution and Chronology

Three dynastic phases are attested (Van De Mieroop 2016, §9.3):

1. **First dynasty (to ca. 1400 BCE)** — only five known ruler names; VDM is uncertain
   whether it constitutes a true unified dynasty at all, and there is near-total lack of
   evidence for real territorial reach beyond Susiana, possibly not even full control
   there. The best-attested act is Tepti-ahar founding Kabnak (modern Haft Tepe),
   possibly after losing Susa itself — though Van De Mieroop 2016 notes "no clear
   evidence for this."
2. **Second (Igi-halki) dynasty, roughly two centuries** — an unusual alternating
   succession between two branches of Igi-halki's descendants. Untash-Napirisha founded
   Al-Untash-Napirisha (above). Late in this dynasty, Kidin-Hutran III twice invaded
   Babylonia (taking Nippur, then attacking Isin) while Babylon was under Assyrian
   puppet rulers installed by Tukulti-Ninurta I; he died soon after Tukulti-Ninurta's own
   death (1197 BCE), coinciding with an Elamite dynastic change.
3. **Third dynasty (Shutruk-Nahhunte and descendants, early-to-mid twelfth century BCE)**
   — Elam's territorial and political peak, and the "sack of Babylon" dynasty (below).

**The 1158/1155 BCE sacks of Babylon and the monument removals.** Shutruk-Nahhunte —
married to a daughter of the Kassite king Meli-Shipak (1186–1172) — invaded Babylonia in
1158 BCE, sacked "700 cities" per his own inscription, deposed the Kassite king (see
[[kassites]]), and carried off major Babylonian monuments to Susa, including the stele
of Naram-Sin (captured at Sippar) and the Law Code of Hammurabi, inscribing his own
Elamite-language dedication to Inshushinak on them; he also removed objects from his own
kingdom's cities (including Al-Untash-Napirisha), asserting supremacy over Elamite
subjects as well as conquered Babylonians. This is why these iconic Old Akkadian and Old
Babylonian monuments were excavated at Susa by French excavators around 1900 rather than
in Mesopotamia. His son (and possible assassin), Kutir-Nahhunte, invaded Babylonia again
in 1155 BCE, ransacked further cities, took Marduk's cult statue to Susa, and ended the
Kassite dynasty outright — but did not himself take the Babylonian throne; a non-Kassite,
non-Elamite "second Isin" dynasty succeeded instead. His brother and successor,
Shilhak-Inshushinak, claimed further raids into Babylonia and Assyria, and control east
of the Tigris as far as Nuzi — Elam's territorial peak, exploiting the simultaneous
Kassite collapse and Assyrian weakness. Babylonian resurgence under Nebuchadnezzar I
(second Isin dynasty, 1125–1104 BCE) recovered Marduk's statue from Susa and ended
Elamite prosperity; Elam then disappears from the surviving record for roughly three
centuries.

**Debate — why did Shutruk-Nahhunte carry the monuments to Susa?** Van De Mieroop 2016
stages three (or four) positions, none adjudicated: (a) simple trophy collection,
analogized to Nazi art looting in the Second World War (Carter and Stolper 1984); (b) a
legitimation display — Susa staking a claim as a major city, or the Elamite king
positioning himself as rightful heir to the Kassites (Carter 1992); (c) protective
removal, ironically exposing the objects to later violence when Ashurbanipal sacked Susa
in 646 BCE (Harper 1992); and (d) Van De Mieroop's own preferred reading, attributed to
Bahrani 1995 — that abducting royal monuments was a routine Near Eastern act of war
because such images were understood to still embody the depicted ruler's presence and
power (Naram-Sin "still exercised his military prowess in it," Hammurabi "still a great
king of justice through his law code"), making the re-inscription an act of usurping the
represented rulers' ongoing authority rather than mere trophy-taking. These positions are
recorded here per the wiki's protocol; none is adjudicated in wiki voice.

**Later history.** Elam re-enters the record backing anti-Assyrian Babylonian and
Chaldaean claimants (supporting Mushezib-Marduk's coalition against Sennacherib;
capturing and "disappearing" Sennacherib's son Assur-nadin-shumi in 694 BCE; backing
Shamash-shuma-ukin's 652 BCE revolt against Assurbanipal — see [[assyrians]]). Elam was
never itself annexed by Assyria but was progressively weakened by punitive campaigns,
culminating in Assurbanipal's sack of Susa in 647 BCE, explicitly motivated by revenge
for Elam's support of his brother's rebellion. This post-647 weakening is cited by Van De
Mieroop 2016 as one condition enabling subsequent Median expansion into the Zagros (see
[[medes]]). Elam ultimately lost independence to Cyrus of Persia, not to Assyria or
Babylonia, becoming the Achaemenid heartland-adjacent region and the source of the
empire's chief administrative language (see [[achaemenid-empire]]).

## Lifeways

Evidence for everyday Elamite society is thin relative to royal-inscription evidence:
Van De Mieroop 2016's account of Elam is overwhelmingly a political and dynastic
narrative reconstructed from building inscriptions and Babylonian-side references, not a
social or economic description. The one clearly evidenced social dynamic is
lowland/highland cultural heterogeneity (above) — Susiana-plain populations under heavy
Mesopotamian influence versus Anshan-highland populations retaining distinct local
religious and linguistic traditions, unified only at the level of royal ideology.

## Identity Cautions

Elam is not a single ethnic or linguistic bloc but a polity whose official language,
official cult, and territorial reach changed over thirteen centuries — Akkadian-official
Elam before ca. 1400 BCE is not the same linguistic-political entity as
Elamite-official Elam after that date, even though both are conventionally called
"Elamite." The royal title "King of Susa and Anshan" is documented by Van De Mieroop 2016
as possibly aspirational propaganda rather than descriptive fact for the earliest
attested holders — titulary is not proof of territorial control. Later, the Achaemenid
Persian dynasty's adoption of the "King of Anshan" title and Elamite as an
administrative language does NOT make the Persians Elamite: Van De Mieroop 2016 is
explicit that the Achaemenid population was "an Iranian people that spoke an
Indo-European language," ethnically and linguistically distinct from the Elamites whose
political geography and administrative apparatus they inherited (see
[[achaemenid-empire]]).

## Historiography

Elam's historical record is asymmetric and externally mediated for much of its span: no
Elamite royal archives survive, and its best-documented period (Middle Elamite, ca.
1400–1100 BCE) is reconstructed almost entirely from royal building inscriptions (with
scholarly disagreement on king sequencing) and Babylonian-side references. Elam's
absence from the Amarna international correspondence is attributed by Van De Mieroop
2016 to timing (Elam's major political rise came after the Amarna archive's period) and
geographic distance from Syria — not to Elam's insignificance. The nineteenth-to-
twentieth-century French excavation of Susa recovered Mesopotamian royal monuments
(Naram-Sin's stele, the Hammurabi Code) whose presence there is explained only by the
1158 BCE looting campaign documented above — a case where excavation findspot and
textual/historical reconstruction depend on each other.

## Nissen densification (1988) — Susiana / proto-Elamite and Akkad–Elam

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* (Chicago 1988). Nissen writes “Susiana” / “proto-Elamite” for the early phases; later “Elam” appears in Akkad-period campaigns. Four-way non-identity: Susiana material horizon ≠ Elamite language ≠ genetic population ≠ later Middle Elamite polity.)*

**Late Uruk package in Susiana.** Physically similar alluvial plain (larger dry-farmable share; never marshland except south); continuous densification in Middle–Late Susiana with complex settlement systems/centers while Babylonia was still water-logged and isolated — both parts of a greater unity until the next phase. Unlike Babylonia’s slow Ubaid painted → Uruk unpainted wheel-pottery transition, Susiana **jumps** from richly painted Late Susiana to unpainted Uruk-type wheel pottery with no transition — Nissen suggests the pottery developed in Babylonia and was taken over. Susiana also takes almost all other Babylonian developments: mass bevel-rimmed bowls, tally stones, sealed balls, both cylinder-seal groups, numerical tablets, and writing — same materials/technique/elements but **different symbols and most probably a different language** (undeciphered proto-Elamite). Seal themes are wider (siege/conquest, building erection). Commonality more in economic/control organization (including ration containers); cultural traditions largely untouched (as of Nissen 1988).

**Jamdet Nasr–ED I divergence.** Strong mutuality only during Late Uruk. From Jamdet Nasr, Susiana abandons Babylonian-pattern ration distribution (no BRB successors / no mass-produced pottery types) while Babylonia continues mass production. Proto-Elamite writing remains static then ceases while Babylonian writing advances; no plano-convex brick. By ED I-time Susiana returns toward pre–Late Uruk-style economic/cultural life; painted pottery art returns. Nissen explains the collapse of the adopted package by a rising organizational threshold: as Babylonian forms specialized to alluvial scale, structures lost meaning for Susiana conditions — exemplifying Early Period linkage of interconnected area size to organizational complexity. Tepe Malyan (later Anshan) shows early close similarity to Susa, suggesting possible political affiliations (as of Nissen 1988).

**ED and Akkad–Elam.** Susiana settlement decline from the Uruk period reached ED II with few inhabited places besides Susa; late ED Babylonian influence rises again; by late ED III and especially the Akkad Dynasty, writing is retaken from Babylonia — language and writing both (Sumerian then Akkadian texts at Susa), with some Elamite texts showing local language could be transcribed. Akkad-era Elam/Susa as permanent foreign possession with Susa ruler as servant of Akkad may also describe late ED. Under Akkad: Rimush records a victory over Elam; Manishtushu a victory over Anshan; Naram-Sin early campaigns against Elam and Magan. End of Ur III: Elamite invasion conquers Ur and abducts the last ruler — one factor among multiple (as of Nissen 1988). Language of administration ≠ ethnic identity of population.
