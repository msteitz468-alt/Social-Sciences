# Linear Pottery Culture (LBK)

Central European early Neolithic archaeological culture (*Linearbandkeramik*, LBK) defined by **band-decorated pottery**, timber long houses, and loess-oriented farming with cattle emphasis. Naming ancestor of Childe’s [[danubian-i-culture|Danubian I]] discussions; modern LBK is the operational construct in [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]].

## Definition and Construct History

By **5500 BC**, Mithen states, a new farming culture emerged from the fringes of the Hungarian plain — the Linearbandkeramik — and spread with astonishing speed east into Ukraine and west across Poland, Germany, the Low Countries, and eastern France. Defined by band-decorated pottery, three-aisled timber long houses with wattle-and-daub, and cattle-rich mixed farming. Culture-historical archaeology (including Childe’s Danubian sequence) earlier mapped the same phenomenon; LBK is the standard later label. **Etic construct.**

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Mithen 2003):**

- **Pottery:** linear band decoration (definitional).
- **Houses:** long houses ~12 m (sometimes much larger); three-aisled posts; household as key social unit; abandoned intact when household dies → low mounds (“dead” houses matching “dead” families); floors often destroyed by later ploughing.
- **Uniformity:** near-identical houses from Cuiry-lès-Chaudardes (Paris basin) to Miskovice (Czech Republic) nearly 1,000 km and centuries apart — frontier mentality explanation (Mithen).
- **Economy:** fertile-soil farming; cattle emphasis; polished stone axes as frontier exchange goods.
- **Burial:** village-adjacent cemeteries; often only tooth enamel survives; ages/sexes together; axes/adzes/arrows/shells with men, grinding stones/awls with women; little evidence of wealthy individuals or elaborate ritual.

**Linguistic:** Renfrew’s claim that proto-Indo-European spread with Neolithic farmers is a **linked debate position**, not a property of LBK pots.

**Genetic:** classical blood-group wave-of-advance (Cavalli-Sforza) vs mtDNA “tiny ripple” (Sykes et al.) as of Mithen 2003 — see debate page; not LBK = one gene pool.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Origin zone:** Hungarian plain fringes ~5500 BC.
- **Spread:** ~25 km per generation westwards by filling fertile soils then leap-frogging poorer ones (Mithen); east to Ukraine.
- **Frontier:** contact with late Mesolithic foragers including [[ertebolle-culture|Ertebølle]]-related groups ~4400 BC (exchange, intermarriage scenarios).

## Lifeways (Settlement and Subsistence)

Village farmers on loess; household production; polished-axe exchange at forest frontiers; cemeteries without strong hierarchy signals in Mithen’s sketch. Relationship to indigenous Mesolithic people is the core of [[farming-spread-europe-debate]] (Zvelebil indigeneity thesis vs migration models).

## Relations and Successions

- **Upstream:** Greek/Balkan early farming from western Asia (boats with seed-corn and livestock ~7500 BC).
- **Parallel label:** [[danubian-i-culture]] (Childe 1929 culture-historical form).
- **Broader genetic/cultural construct:** [[early-european-farmers]] in later archaeogenetic literature — do not silently equate without attribution.
- **Downstream influence:** Scandinavian early Neolithic long barrows said to echo LBK long houses (Mithen).

## Significance

LBK is the type-case of rapid temperate-European agricultural expansion and the empirical core of demic vs cultural diffusion arguments for the European Neolithic.

## Identity Cautions

- Pottery+long-house package ≠ single ethnicity, language, or migration event.
- Zvelebil’s indigenous-descendant claim and immigrant-wave claims are **positions** — both can be partly true regionally.
- Four-way non-identity strict.
- Do not merge LBK with all “first farmers of Europe” (Greece and Mediterranean Cardial differ).

## Historiography

Childe Danubian I; mid-20th-c. LBK systematics; processual settlement archaeology; Zvelebil Mesolithic continuity school; Cavalli-Sforza genetic geography; Sykes mtDNA critique; Renfrew language-farming hypothesis — all refracted through Mithen 2003’s popular synthesis. Post-2010 aDNA transformed the debate — densify from later sources with explicit date-stamps; do not overwrite this page’s 2003 horizon silently.

### Sherratt densification (1997)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]], Ch.2–3, 10–12. )* Early loess/moist-soil horticultural restriction; groundwater fixed-plot models; later secondary-products horizons restructure settlement beyond primary LBK/AVK village patterns on the Hungarian Plain (AVK split, regional styles, Bükk trade). Bandkeramik opium-poppy associations appear in Ch.16 intoxicants discussion — botanical claims as of his cited evidence. Culture ≠ people/language/genes.

### Renfrew 1987 — rapid farming transformation in the wave of advance

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]*).* LBK figures as a **rapid** regional transformation within the European farming wave of advance (contrast slow Scandinavian neolithic). One of several named early-farming cultural transformations he maps onto a speculative linguistic genealogy under the [[anatolian-hypothesis]] — association only, not culture=language identity. Demic preference over pure mesolithic creation of LBK (contra Zvelebil-type Position B on [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]).

## Bellwood densification — *First Farmers* (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; chronologies and isotopes as of that synthesis. Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes.)*

### Chronology, package, and dispersal signature

- After ~**5400 BC**, following ~**600-year standstill** on the W edge of the Hungarian Plain, LBK (= Danubian / Linear Bandkeramik) pioneer farmers spread up the Danube and through Europe N of the Alps to the Rhineland and Paris Basin; rapid — “perhaps 200 years from Slovenia and Poland to the Paris Basin” (attributed).
- **Package:** timber longhouse settlements; incised pottery; fertile loesslands and floodplain-edge permanent villages; economy of hulled emmer/einkorn, broomcorn millet, cattle, sheep, goats, pigs in small forest clearings. Origins at SE end of distribution (attributed).
- **Homogeneity signature:** remarkable early homogeneity in pottery decoration and longhouse construction → dispersal signature; homogeneity breaks up with settling-down and decay of long-distance colonizer networks (attributed; colonization-reading also cites Childe 1956).
- **Longhouses:** Bellwood ethnographic analogy — may reflect non-lineage pioneer societies oriented to labor acquisition not land (Than of W Borneo analogy) (thesis).

### Mobility, frontiers, and Mesolithic relations

- **Price et al. 2001 (Sr isotopes):** Rhineland LBK teeth/bones — many people spent childhoods in different environmental chemistries than burial places → mobility (finding as of Bellwood 2005).
- **Bentley et al. 2003:** Sr isotope analysis female skeletons LBK Rhineland — non-local females (possibly Mesolithic) from uplands into riverine LBK farming landscape (finding).
- **Jones 2001:161:** ancient bone from LBK contexts France/Germany — mtDNA lineages of SW Asian origin = direct evidence some female Neolithic movement *(as of Bellwood 2005; pre-genome-wide aDNA)*.
- **Keeley; Keeley & Cahen 1989:** edges of LBK in Low Countries — longhouses fortified by ditches/palisades; no-man’s-land between Mesolithic and LBK in Belgium Meuse valley; Talheim massacre of 34 LBK people; Keeley: interactions “at best chilly and at worst violent” (attributed).
- **Gronenborn 1999:** some lithic continuity in blade manufacture; large-blade tools/sickles largely replace Mesolithic bladelets/burins; little interaction evidence except peripheral W/N parts (attributed).
- North of LBK distribution, spread slowed: entrenched Mesolithic on N European alluvial plain / Baltic / North Sea + less favorable soils/climate (attributed).
- **Zvelebil & Rowley-Conwy 1986; Zvelebil 1998:** availability–substitution–consolidation model; Ertebølle frontier with farmers ~4800–4000 BC, then TRB (Funnel Beaker) agriculture; Ertebølle had pottery ~4500 BC, imported LBK adzes, almost no agricultural products except occasional goat (attributed).
- **Bellwood thesis:** TRB derives from earlier LBK (Lengyel variant of later LBK); early TRB homogeneity hints dispersal not piecemeal Mesolithic origin; becoming Neolithic in N Europe “essentially driven by an LBK-TRB cultural phylogeny rather than by a native Mesolithic one” (attributed).

### Historiographic placement

Bellwood treats LBK as a **cultural replacement** episode with high selectivity of loess territory and strontium mobility as colonizing demography — core empirical case for demic strength of the European Neolithic advance under [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] and [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]. Parallel densification of the genetically defined [[early-european-farmers]] population (Reich 2018 horizon) is a separate evidence class — do not silently equate LBK pots with EEF genomes or with Indo-European. Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Gimbutas densification — *Civilization of the Goddess* (1991)

*(All claims in this section attributed to [[gimbutas-marija]], *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* 1991. FLAG source: peaceful unfortified Old Europe → fortification as Kurgan-wave response, and matrilineal stem-family readings of longhouses, are **theses**. Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people. Links: [[old-europe]], [[kurgan-hypothesis]], [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]], [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture]], [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].)*

### Naming, origin, and package (as of Gimbutas 1991)

Gimbutas reports LBK as the best-known central European culture from France to Romania; also Bandkeramik / Linear Pottery. Childe (*Dawn*, 1925) called it “most classically Neolithic in the ancient world” and “Danubian I” — she notes the Danubian label is problematic because the culture is not Lower Danube. “Linear bands” misdescribes pottery that also carries spirals, snakes, meanders, rectangles, V’s, chevrons, breasts, horns, zoomorphic reliefs; fabric/symbolism similar to late Starčevo (Körös) but generally **lacks painted ware**.

**Origin (thesis + affinities):** close Starčevo (Körös)–LBK affinities “firmly suggest actual movement of people from the southeast”; oldest LBK on the Middle Danube developed ~**5600–5400 B.C.** directly out of Starčevo (Körös); physically generally related to small gracile Balkan Mediterraneans; ~**500 years** elapsed before Balkan Neolithic/agriculture entered temperate central Europe. Diffusion west to E France/Netherlands and north to lower Oder (~1,000 km Hungary–Holland) in a relatively short time given ceramic/lithic similarity; 6th-millennium climate warmer; light mixed-oak forests; settled almost exclusively on **loess** plains with little Mesolithic competition; emmer principal crop plus other cereals, legumes, opium poppy, flax; cattle prime livestock; hunting marginal; some villages occupied 400–500 years.

### Tools, settlement, longhouses, cemeteries (material inventory)

Ground stone adzes and shoe-last celts; flint industry continuing Starčevo traditions; specialized flint mining (e.g. Jurassic flint at Olszanica, S Poland); Bükk/Tatra obsidian. Settlement cells of 8–10 sites; S Poland Dlubnia density 20 sites/25 km²; villages she sizes from 125 to >500 ha (note: scale figures as in her text — reconcile carefully with later settlement archaeology); largest perhaps 500+ inhabitants, normally **5–8 houses** ~20 m apart; **no defensive palisades or ditches in the early period** (fact-register as she reports; contrast later fortification thesis).

**Longhouses:** characteristic timber longhouses excavated in Czechoslovakia, Poland, Germany, Holland, France; no Starčevo prototypes — local development assumed; Bylany (Soudský) 108 dwellings identified, 5–8 simultaneous; large structures sometimes **45 m** long with fence/gate corrals; 5.5–7 m wide; five post rows; later Stroked Pottery and Rössen trapezoidal plans. Cemeteries 100–500 m from settlements, inhumation and cremation, some >100 graves; goods include pots, celts, arrowheads, palettes, *Spondylus* ornaments. Later densification (Ch. 9): ~20 LBK cemeteries France–Czechoslovakia, 20–>200 graves (Elsloo 113; Flomborn 85; Aiterhofen 160+69 cremations; Nitra 72); clusters of kin; no spatial rich/poor hierarchy in her reading. Nitra egg-shaped tombs c. 5000 B.C. fit her broader “egg/tomb-as-womb” burial ideology (**thesis**). Trade 5500–4500 B.C.: marble bracelets in LBK (Stroked Pottery) graves in Bohemia and central Germany; shells/marble up the Danube.

### Chronology phases and eastern/northern relations

She subdivides LBK: **early** (2nd half 6th mill.) spiral-meander-chevron; **middle** (end 6th–early 5th) “musical note” decoration — maximum expansion; **late** (to mid-5th) stroked decoration. Middle-period sites N of Carpathians in Dniester/Siret/Prut/Bug; fused with/influenced Dniester-Bug; in Moldavia/W Ukraine **LBK substratum for Cucuteni (Tripolye)**. End 5th mill. (4300–4200) central LBK areas colonized by Lengyel and Tiszapolgar from south, “probably as a chain reaction to the first Kurgan wave” (**thesis**). Western descendants continued almost a millennium to mid-4th millennium (Hinkelstein, Grossgartach, Rössen, Epi-Rössen; Paris basin Villeneuve-Saint-Germain; Hainaut Blicque; Post-Rubané). Northern central Europe (Holland–Poland) transformed via late LBK and Lengyel influence/colonization second half 5th mill., yielding TRB/Funnel Beaker; TRB beginning she links to first Kurgan infiltration into the Danubian basin and Lengyel expansion ~4300 B.C. as chain reaction (**thesis**).

### Social structure reading (thesis)

LBK hamlets 2–6 houses, ~30-year house life; large/medium/small houses; very long solid houses (Bylany, Olszanica, Aldenhoven) with solid NW ends and richer decorated pottery — interpreted by some as clubhouse/big man/chief/priest houses. **Gimbutas:** no chief’s houses in LBK or Old Europe; long-house = matrilineal **stem-family** core directing religious/social/economic life; van de Velde pottery assemblages homogeneous over ~30 years → matrilineal stem family (women potters) in her citation. **Do not promote** matriliny or absence of hierarchy to wiki voice.

### Demise / fortification shift and Kurgan Wave 1 (Gimbutas thesis)

End 5th–c. 3500 B.C.: profound changes — settlements outside valley bottoms, **palisades of tree trunks** — signaling, for her, a shift from peaceful unfortified lifestyle to one requiring protection against **invasion** (**thesis**; load-bearing Old Europe peace package). Under [[kurgan-hypothesis]] Wave 1 (c. 4400–4300): LBK among groups most affected; “Emergence of Kurgan Elements in the Milieu of the LBK Culture” — chain of **hill forts** on high riverbanks in Middle/Upper Danube basin (Hungary, Austria, W Slovakia, Moravia, Bohemia, Bavaria), new in European prehistory, contemporary with late Lengyel and Rössen or immediately following (C-14 frame 4400–3900). Pit dwellings contrast solid LBK/Lengyel longhouses; solar-decorated Furchenstich pottery; Rössen as “mixed culture” / LBK “with oriental elements”; Goldberg fortified hilltop; Wahlitz 2 ha ditched hilltop (cal ~4380–3950 B.C.). Baalberge single graves under round barrows contrast local collective burial. Violence: Goldberg spearpoint skull wounds; **Talheim** 34 murdered men/women/children — she places massacre after early 5th mill. LBK dates, probably Rössen period in her sequence (Bellwood/Keeley layers above date Talheim in LBK frontier violence without requiring Kurgan agents).

Not all of central Europe converted after Wave 1 in her synthesis: **Cucuteni, TRB, and western LBK continued** through the first half of the 4th millennium or longer; increased “Kurganization” second half 4th mill. **Competing explanations** for fortification, Talheim, and post-LBK change (indigenous conflict, climate, Mesolithic frontier violence, multi-causal collapse) live on [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]] and [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] — record Gimbutas’s invasion-response reading as one position.

### Identity cautions (Gimbutas layer)

- LBK pots + longhouses ≠ Indo-European speech or Kurgan ethnicity.
- “Peaceful early / fortified late because of Kurgan Wave 1” is a causal thesis, not a finding.
- Matrilineal stem-family longhouse reading is interpretive.
- Physical-type (gracile Mediterranean) claims are dated physical anthropology as of 1991.

## Barker 2006 densification (*The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*, Ch. 9)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006. Genetics/isotopes date-stamped **as of Barker 2006 / mid-2000s** — later aDNA may supersede.)*

### Expansion, package, and husbandry

- **Scale and tempo:** Agricultural frontier stayed within middle Danube/Carpathians ~1000 years; then c.5500 BC extraordinary rapid expansion of Early Neolithic Linearbandkeramik (LBK) pottery sites from N Hungary to Baltic and Paris basin to Bug/Dniester, generally on loess soils; thousands of sites.
- **Settlement form:** Thatched timber-post longhouses mostly 15–30 m long, 6–7 m wide (e.g. Geleen, Elsloo, Sittard, Bylany); extended-family households; tripartite space theories (living/animals/storage); cemeteries with age/gender-marked grave-goods. Farms, hamlets, small villages as house clusters on shared orientation, not planned street agglomerations (Lüning 1988 via Barker).
- **Husbandry adapted to damp late-Atlantic forest:** Grindstones, sickles, shoe-last adzes; cereals esp. emmer and barley, also einkorn, bread wheat, spelt; legumes vetch/pea/bean; flax, poppy; little reliance on wild plant foods; some hunting/fishing but fauna dominated by domestic cattle, pigs, sheep — **cattle most frequent**; ceramic sieves for cheese-making (Bogucki 1984); possible cattle castration and blood drinking.
- **Settlement islands:** LBK sites in distinct enclaves along smaller tributaries of major rivers (Poland, Aisne, Aldenhovener Plateau) not uniform landscape fill; crops in small fields; stock moved seasonally alluvium↔loess plateau. Early LBK often seasonally mobile ‘tethered mobility’ (Whittle 1996); wells for stock watering. Loess-fringe sands/clays (e.g. Brześć Kujawski): less substantial structures, mix of foraging and small-scale farming akin to Körös.
- **Swidden model rejected:** [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] linked LBK to loess open corridor; J. G. D. Clark (1952) attributed rapid colonization to swidden. Critiqued: many LBK sites show repeated rebuildings; Rowley-Conwy (1981b) — swidden suited to tropics not European loess; fertility maintainable with rotation/manuring. Archaeobotany: permanent fields with hedges (later LBK Netherlands); intensive autumn-sown garden cultivation (Bogaard 2004) implying labour commitment and crop–stock integration.

### Demic colonization vs acculturation (positions as of Barker 2006)

- **Long-dominant demic explanation:** Neolithic colonization from Balkan Early Neolithic (sparse pre-LBK loess foragers + sudden LBK appearance) (Bogucki 2000 via Barker). Barker: demographic case for Balkan population stress/budding never satisfactorily made; marked differences Balkan EN vs LBK settlement/material culture/subsistence unexplained. Rapid LBK cultural explosion and discontinuous enclaves fit poorly with Ammerman–Cavalli-Sforza ‘[[demic-diffusion|wave of advance]]’. Some propose ‘leap-frog colonization’ — deliberate long-distance moves to favourable patches (Lüning & Stehli 1994).
- **Indigenous / acculturation + mid-2000s proxies:** Increasing evidence of mobile Late Mesolithic seasonal use of loess pre-LBK; earliest LBK more mixed foraging–farming and lithic similarity to pre-LBK foragers; Mesolithic/EN palynological signatures fundamentally same (as Balkans). Acculturation proposed N of Körös and SW Germany/France. La Hoguette: Mesolithic foragers first develop pottery and acquire sheep from Mediterranean forager-farmers, then LBK pottery and cattle from east. Foragers and farmers coexisted centuries in upper Rhône (Perrin 2003). **Sr isotopes:** high non-local female incidence at SW German LBK sites — perhaps marriage partners from foraging communities (Bentley et al. 2003). **Most convincingly (Barker’s wording): aDNA from LBK skeletons Germany/Austria/Hungary indicates most LBK people genetically identical to preceding Mesolithic/Palaeolithic central European population, not incomers (Haak et al. 2005)** — **as of Barker 2006**; do not promote to current genetic consensus.
- **Whittle / Bogaard process readings:** Whittle (1996): adoption of longhouse architecture and domesticates enhanced rather than altered existing cooperation/sharing values; major shift was longhouse life. Bogaard (2004): autumn sowing season conflicted with collecting storable wild plant foods and prime ungulates, so farming rapidly displaced foraging among LBK communities rather than long small-scale coexistence.
- **Violence and successors:** LBK violence rare except late LBK mass grave ~35 at Talheim (Germany) — skulls with shoe-last celt impacts (Wahl & König 1987); hard to argue forager ambush. Late LBK northern/NW margins: ditch and palisade earthworks — some ritual, many likely defensive. Fifth millennium BC: LBK replaced by regional groups (SBK W Germany; Lengyel E Germany/Poland/Czech) with continued longhouses + elaborate ceremonial enclosures and burial monuments.

**Identity cautions (Barker layer):** LBK pottery + longhouses ≠ demic identity with Balkan colonists; Haak 2005 reading is mid-2000s aDNA; leap-frog vs wave vs acculturation remain positions on [[farming-spread-europe-debate]] / [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]]. Source: [[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006]].

## Bradley 1998 — symbolic dimension of the house (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|Bradley, Significance of Monuments]]* (1998, Ch. 3) argues that culture-historical and economic settlement archaeology underplayed the **symbolic life-cycle of LBK houses**:

- Timber longhouses often used ~20–27 years despite massive construction; little repair/fire destruction → abandoned while sound; “living” and “dead” houses co-present on multi-phase sites (Köln-Lindenthal, Bylany, Langweiler 9, Merzbach region, Laurenzberg 7, Olszanica, Brześć Kujawski).
- Adult burial in flat cemeteries near some settlements (individual commemoration) parallel to but distinct from household-scale house histories; children’s bones mainly in buildings, not abandonment deposits (Veit via Bradley).
- Late LBK: enclosure development including Belgian interrupted-ditch/palisade enclosures with houses inside (Keeley & Cahen) — prototypes for later [[causewayed-enclosure]] circuits.
- Beyond the loess, long-mound and enclosure monumentality transforms these domestic templates ([[death-of-the-house]]); complementary distributions of longhouses / long mounds / megaliths (secure coincidence mainly Kujavia).
- Agricultural frontier ~5000–4000 BC: loess longhouse zone vs monument-rich margins where first major monuments appear as agricultural resources are newly employed (Sherratt 1990 via Bradley).

Construct caution: Bradley does not equate LBK ethnicity with northern long-barrow builders — morphological and symbolic association, not identity.

## Whittle densification (1996) — longhouse lives and indigenous origins

*(Attributed — [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, esp. Ch. 6. Chronology mid-1990s cal BC; **pre-aDNA**. Position on [[farming-spread-europe-debate]], not wiki-voice demography.)*

### Chronology and distribution (as of Whittle 1996)

- LBK ~**5500 to after 5000 BC**, then post-LBK regional successors (Lengyel, Rössen, Stichbandkeramik, etc.) to before ~4000 BC.
- Range: Transdanubian Hungary to S Netherlands, E Belgium, Paris-basin valleys; Slovakia, Czech Lands, N/E Austria, S and parts of central Poland; Germany N toward Braunschweig/Magdeburg; northern limits roughly loess / N European plain edge with Polish outliers (Kujavia, Chełmno-land, near Szczecin).
- Typical siting: fertile soils near water; settlement “cells” with gaps; limited woodland inroads in pollen (Aldenhoven example). Early material uniformity (houses, adzes, pottery) then later regionalisation of pottery styles.

### Settlement and [[longhouse]] architecture

- Three-aisled timber longhouses; individual houses may last about a generation and be replaced nearby; some large (~25 m) from early on, spaced tens of metres apart.
- Exemplars in his synthesis: Bylany, Langweiler 8 phasing, Frankfurt–Niedereschbach earliest house, Erkelenz–Kückhoven wooden wells, Olszanica (obsidian/polished stone distributions around houses), Darion enclosure, Merzbachtal settlement development.
- Whittle reads longhouses as **social creators** of aggregation and cooperation, not mere colonist shelters; major lifestyle shift = longhouse life in small groups; domesticates and architecture **enhance** existing sharing values rather than wholly replace them.

### Origins: colonisation orthodoxy vs indigenous transformation

- **Orthodoxy he reports:** clean break with thin inland foragers; demographic overflow from SE Europe / Hungarian plain; rapid ¹⁴C expansion; cultural uniformity; empty fertile woodland niches.
- **His alternative:** acculturation/enculturation of **indigenous inland foragers** under contact with southern/eastern Neolithic communities; flint blade traditions continuous; physical anthropology “compatible with continuity” (DNA as future research only — **as of 1996**).
- Contact markers: Limburg / La Hoguette-type pottery; late Körös expansion intensifying interaction; cattle and cereals taken up to underpin cooperative ethics.

### Burial, violence, and enclosures

- Cemetery example: Elsloo grave assemblages.
- **[[talheim|Talheim]]** mass grave (reconstructed body positions) as evidence of lethal conflict within the longhouse world — held beside, not erased by, the sharing-ethic thesis.
- Late enclosures (e.g. Künzing–Unternberg, Lengyel/SBK roundels) not automatically defensive forts in his reading; ritual/aggregation readings remain open.
- Post-LBK: [[lengyel-culture]] (Brześć Kujawski, Oslonki), Rössen (Inden), Passy burial structures; northern forager neighbours ([[ertebolle-culture]], Skateholm, Oronsay) in long coexistence before fifth-millennium convergence.

### Identity cautions (Whittle layer)

LBK pottery + longhouses ≠ single ethnicity or language. Indigenous vs demic origin is a **position** dated to 1996; later aDNA (EEF) densifies Position A strength — keep both on the debate page. Source: [[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]].
