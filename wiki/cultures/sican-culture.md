## Definition and Construct History

**Sicán** (also Lambayeque in regional usage) names the post-Moche north-coast cultural and political formation centered in the Lambayeque–Leche drainages from roughly the late first millennium AD. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) correlates ethnohistoric **Naymlap** lore with the early Sicán Phase (~AD 700–900) and treats Middle Sicán (~AD 900–1100) as the florescence of a corporate style and multi-city confederation later absorbed by [[chimu-culture|Chimor]]. The construct is archaeological (ceramics, metallurgy, platform centers, burial practice) plus ethnohistoric dynastic narrative—not a self-documented ethnonym recovered from writing.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Style synthesis (as of Moseley 2001):** After Pampa Grande’s abandonment, Cajamarca highlanders push lower slopes; Huari iconography is adopted; synthesis with older Moche and local innovation produces Sicán style—paddle-stamped ornament, black ware, double-spouted libation vessels increase; little early monumental construction then Middle Phase crystallization.

**Sicán Lord:** Central male figure with wings on shoulders, beak-like nose, sometimes talons; flight attitude on double-headed serpent (old Moche sky motif)—strong iconographic candidate for Naymlap (attributed). Conspicuously **excised** from late-phase iconography after Batán Grande’s AD 1100 conflagration.

**Centers:** Many Lambayeque cities (Chotuna, Cinto, Túcume, Juayanca) arise—allegorically 12 Naymlap heirs / confederation of local city-states (archaeology/ethnohistory suggest ~ten; others in Zaña). Inter-valley canals (e.g. Taymi from Cinto toward Túcume) used Naymlap-descent kinship charter for cooperation; Zaña–Jequetepeque link incomplete—technologically feasible not always politically attainable.

**Chotuna:** Occupied early Sicán into Late Horizon (Donnan); ancestral confederation bond rather than political capital; catastrophic flooding ~AD 1100 removed much early construction; Huaca Gloria small ornate platform tempting as Naymlap shrine (double-headed serpent friezes; Sicán Lord absent).

**Batán Grande:** Pre-eminent center to end of Middle Sicán; Sicán Precinct civic core ~4 km² with >dozen truncated pyramids (Huaca Corte ~250 m square); architectural carryovers from Pampa Grande (perpendicular ramps, colonnades, chamber-and-fill, marked adobes); flexed seated burials; tens of thousands of graves—“perhaps more buried than resident,” religious capital “similar to today’s Vatican” (Shimada via Moseley, attributed). Elite tombs: gold/silver, Spondylus, turquoise, lapis, emerald, human sacrifices; stacks of *naipes* (I-shaped copper “axe money,” 5–7 × 3–5 cm)—Batán Grande likely mint; arsenical copper industry from ~AD 850; metallurgy + seafaring trade key to florescence; Chira and Tumbes enter Andean cultural fold under this commerce.

**AD 1100 crisis and Túcume:** Severe El Niño then generations of drought; Batán Grande not rebuilt—wood/brush piled against pyramids, city burned and abandoned (like Pampa Grande and Cerro Baúl final rites); Moseley reads rejection of false divine intermediaries. Power shifts down the Leche Valley to Túcume/Túcume Viejo; Huaca Larga among continent’s largest platforms; remains major after Chimor conquest; may later have joined Incas against Chimor (as of Moseley 2001).

## Distribution and Chronology

Core: Lambayeque, Leche, and neighboring north-coast valleys; commercial reach toward Ecuador (*Spondylus*, copper exchange media). Early Sicán ~AD 700–900; Middle ~AD 900–1100; late phase after Batán Grande torching; Chimor incorporation generations before Minchançaman’s final expansion; Inca Late Horizon administration of residual centers.

**Naymlap narrative (ethnohistory, compressed):** Lord lands with balsa flotilla, wife, greenstone idol, 40 officials; builds court at Chot (=? Chotuna); fakes winged ascent at death; 12 sons found settlements; last ruler Fempellec tempted to move idol → 30 days rains/floods, famine, pestilence, cast to sea; interregnum then Chimor governors before Incas. No archaeological evidence of seafaring foreigner *incursion* though later Ecuador commerce (as of Moseley 2001).

## Lifeways

Irrigation agriculture coordinated by multi-city canal politics; elite metallurgy and long-distance shell/copper trade; seated flexed burial (post-Moche beliefs); confederation ideology via shared descent lore; wealth finance through precious metals and *naipes*.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** Moche Phase V capital at Pampa Grande and fragmented señoríos.
- **Influenced by:** [[wari-culture]] iconography; Cajamarca highland interaction; Moche substrate.
- **Succeeded/absorbed by:** [[chimu-culture]] (craft transfer of ~10,000 northern specialists to [[chan-chan]]); later [[inca-empire]].
- **associated_with:** Naymlap lore; Chimor dual-dynasty pairing with Taycanamu.

## Identity Cautions

Sicán/Lambayeque is a material-political construct and regional label—not proven ethnolinguistic identity. Sicán Lord ≠ historically documented individual; Naymlap is compressed lore. Do not equate Batán Grande’s burial population with a single genetic people. Chimor incorporation is political succession, not cultural erasure of all local practice.

## Historiography

Shimada’s Batán Grande work and Donnan’s Chotuna excavations (via Moseley) anchor the archaeological narrative; Heyerdahl/Sandweiss at Túcume for post-1100 sequence. Ethnohistoric–archaeological correlation of Naymlap is Moseley’s synthesis, open to revision. Source: [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001]].
