# Yangshao Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Yangshao** (Wade-Giles *Yang-shao*) names the painted-pottery Neolithic of the middle
Huanghe (Yellow River) valley, defined from the type site Yang-shao-ts'un in western
Henan, excavated in 1921 by the Swedish geologist J. G. Andersson (the first painted-
pottery assemblage recovered in China). As presented by [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|Chang
(1963)]], Yangshao is the earlier of two developmental "stages" of the North China
Neolithic — the stage of "primary village-farming efficiency" — preceding the
[[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]] stage of "stratified village-farmers." Chang noted that
the type site itself probably dates to the very *end* of the Yangshao stage, but retained
the name by convention.

Chang's 1963 account is a **pre-radiocarbon relative chronology**: it carries no absolute
dates, sequencing Yangshao stratigraphically (above Palaeolithic/Mesolithic, below
Lungshan) and by ceramic seriation. The absolute dates in the frontmatter above are later
radiocarbon consensus, not Chang's. `construct_status: conventional` — Yangshao is an etic
archaeological construct, not an observed people; Chang did not equate it with any language
or population, though he tied the "earliest farmers" to a physical-anthropological
"Mongoloid" identification in the dated 1960s idiom of his sources (flagged; see Identity
Cautions).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Chang 1963).** Painted pottery — brick-red or yellowish-brown, burnished,
painted in red or black; cord-, mat-, and basket-impressed cooking and storage wares;
foxtail millet (*Setaria italica*) as the staple, with broomcorn millet, kaoliang, and
some rice recorded; domesticated dogs and pigs (most common), some cattle and sheep/goats;
a half-cut silkworm cocoon at Hsi-yin-ts'un, southern Shansi (evidence Chang read as
silkworm raising); hoes, spades, polished stone celts, and polished/chipped stone
sickle-knives; hunting and fishing gear (bone points, harpoons, hooks, net-sinkers), with
fish motifs abundant on Weishui-valley pottery.

**Two ceramic subhorizons (Chang's revision, attributed).** Against Andersson — who held the
Kansu (Gansu) and Central Plains painted-pottery groups to be strictly contemporary —
Chang argued for two *chronologically sequential* subhorizons: an earlier **Chungyuan**
(Central Plains) subhorizon (wide black bands, simple curving motifs, much blank ground)
and a later **Kansu** subhorizon (denser, thin-line designs, common interior painting),
itself subdivided into a Pan-shan and a later Ma-ch'ang stage. He supported the sequence
with the stratified site of Ma-chia-yao, where Kansu-type sherds overlay Chungyuan-type
sherds.

**Genetic / linguistic.** Not addressed. Enforce the four-way non-identity rule: Yangshao is
pots, not a language, a genetic population, or a self-conscious people.

## Distribution and Chronology

Earlier Yangshao phases cluster in the Huanghe, Fenhe, and Weishui valleys (southern
Shansi, western Henan, central-eastern Shensi); later phases expand into eastern Kansu,
central Shansi, and northern Henan. Chang argued Yangshao farmers developed *slowly* within
this [[nuclear-area|North China nuclear area]] and did not expand "explosively" outward
until the succeeding Lungshanoid stage.

Within the Chungyuan subhorizon Chang distinguished an earlier **Pan-p'o type** (near Sian;
less painted pottery, simpler realistic designs, round-bottomed bowls) from a **Miao-ti-kou
type** (western Henan; more and more complex painted decor), with Pan-p'o stratigraphically
earlier.

## Lifeways

Chang overturned an earlier scholarly consensus that Yangshao villages were "large,
sedentary communities": from site density, thin single-period deposits, and survey reports
near Sian, he argued the Yangshao settlement pattern was **shifting and repetitive**, tied
to slash-and-burn cultivation — villages as self-sufficient "little communities" (invoking
Redfield) linked by lineage fission, trade in seashells and jade, and a shared decorative-
art horizon-style. The [[banpo|Pan-p'o]] village — dwelling area, an eastern kiln quarter,
and a northern cemetery, with infants urn-buried between houses — is the fullest excavated
example.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** the North China Mesolithic microblade tradition, which Chang argued was
  the probable local forerunner of the North China Neolithic (an in-situ-continuity claim,
  attributed).
- **Succeeded by:** the [[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]] horizon, via the transitional
  "Miao-ti-kou II" (proto-Lungshan) complex — for Chang, a developmental transition within a
  single sequence, not a change of "culture."
- **Associated with (not identity):** the Kansu "eneolithic" painted-pottery traditions,
  which Chang argued were *derivative* of and *later* than the nuclear-area Yangshao — a
  direct reversal of Andersson's ex-oriente-lux ordering (see
  [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

- **Etic construct, not a people.** "Yangshao" labels an assemblage; it names neither a
  language nor a genetic population nor a self-identifying group.
- **Dated race terminology.** Chang's identification of the earliest farmers as "Mongoloid"
  reflects 1920s–1960s physical-anthropology vocabulary carried over from his sources;
  recorded here as an attributed, superseded usage, not a wiki-voice fact.
- **The type site is late.** Yang-shao-ts'un dates to the end of the stage it names — the
  label is a convention, not a claim about the site's priority.

## Historiography

Recorded from Chang's [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]] first-edition synthesis
— a pre-radiocarbon, relative-chronology account built from published PRC excavation reports
that Chang could not verify in the field. Later radiocarbon dating anchored Yangshao to
roughly 5000–3000 BCE and the multi-regional turn in Chinese archaeology complicated Chang's
single-nuclear-area origin story. Densify from post-1963 Banpo and Jiangzhai reports and
later ancient-DNA studies (date-stamp each "(as of Author Year)") when ingested.

## Bellwood densification — *First Farmers* (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005, ch.6. Yangshao = Yellow River **millet** Neolithic tradition, not Yangzi rice — keep packages separate. Culture ≠ language ≠ genes.)*

### Definition and succession

- **Yangshao** (post-**5000 BC** middle Yellow River and tributaries Hebei→Gansu) = direct descendant of **Peiligang**; “major (if not the major) direct ancestor of the Han Chinese cultural and linguistic tradition” (Bellwood thesis — **attributed association**, not four-way identity) (attributed).
- Sites large: Banpocun, Jiangzhai, Beishouling 5–6 ha (vs smaller Peiligang average); village plans, cemeteries, pottery kilns → complex possibly ranked societies; notation on pot rims possibly ancestral to Shang Old Chinese script (~1300 BC) (attributed findings as of 2005).
- Peiligang–Cishan continuum Yellow River ~6500–5000 BC precedes Yangshao; foxtail and broomcorn millets on loess from ~6500 BC (attributed). Rice at Jiahu (Huai, Peiligang-related) shows rice–millet overlap — two completely separate Chinese agricultural origins “counterindicated” (Cohen 2003 via Bellwood); see [[rice-domestication]].

### Chang historiography (Bellwood’s reading)

- Early Chang (1960s) Yangshao/Longshan heartland Zhongyuan + Lungshanoid secondary dispersal correct **in spirit** (Bellwood).
- Anti-diffusionist 1977–86 editions treated Lungshanoid as interaction/convergence.
- Late Chang (1996 with Goodenough) returning toward Yangzi-linked population dispersal for S China/Taiwan Neolithic (attributed).
- **Bellwood thesis:** Late Neolithic China “cozy club of cultural interaction” ~3500 BC because cultures already closely related with ultimate common regional origin at Neolithic beginning ≥6500 BC — not one small ancestral society but high communication among related ethnolinguistic populations in heartland (attributed).

### Language program (positions)

Bellwood prefers Janhunen’s **Yellow River / Yangshao** homeland for Sino-Tibetan among contested options (Peiros N South Asia; van Driem Sichuan; Matisoff Himalayan Plateau) — based on farming/language reasoning + unbroken Neolithic→Chinese historical continuity central China, with strong rice cultivation reconstructions in the family lexicon (positions as of 2005). Record as [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] linkage, **not** culture = language.

### Identity cautions restated

- Yangshao material culture ≠ Han ethnicity ≠ Sino-Tibetan ≠ aDNA cluster without separate argument.
- Millet Yellow River package ≠ Yangzi rice package, though interaction and Jiahu overlap complicate dual-origin stories.
- Skeletal claims (Upper Cave not ancestral modern E Asians; fully Mongoloid in early Neolithic Jiahu, Dawenkou, Jiangzhai — Kamminga & Wright, Brown, Chen & Zhang via Bellwood) indicate “considerable Chinese Neolithic population replacement” while genetics “seems to differ” — debate continues *(as of Bellwood 2005)*.

Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Liu & Chen (2012) densification — radiocarbon-anchored regional record

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 6, "Emergence of Social Inequality — The Middle Neolithic (5000–3000
BC)." Radiocarbon-anchored throughout; supersedes Chang's 1963 pre-radiocarbon relative
chronology for the sites below.)*

By the late 1990s more than 5,000 Yangshao sites had been identified and roughly 100
excavated, spanning Hebei, Henan, Shanxi, Shaanxi, and part of Inner Mongolia; settled
farming of millet and rice, with pig and dog, was well established, and settlement sizes
ranged from a few hundred m² to over 100 ha. Liu & Chen distinguish three sub-phases: Early
(Banpo, c. 5000–4000 BC), Middle (Miaodigou, c. 4000–3500 BC), and Late (c. 3500–3000 BC).

**Jiangzhai (early Yangshao, Lintong, Shaanxi).** A 5-ha settlement with a central plaza
ringed by houses whose doors faced the plaza, a ditch-enclosed residential core (~2 ha),
and burials clustered outside the ditch in groups apparently correlated with residential
sectors. Grave goods were mostly utilitarian with no obvious difference between graves —
Liu & Chen read this as "suggesting a relatively egalitarian society." Population is
estimated at 75–125 (Zhao C. 1995, 1998). **[POSITION]** Two readings of Jiangzhai's
residential sectors compete: an earlier Chinese scholarly interpretation held the community
was matrilineal, with small/medium houses for clan members and large houses for chiefs or
"secret social groups" (Xi'an et al. 1988; Yan, W. 1989b); Lee (1993), from spatial analysis,
instead argues the sectors reflect "hierarchical nesting" (nuclear family → household →
household cluster/lineage → village), a segmentary-organization principle typical of tribal
societies generally, not evidence of matriliny specifically.

**Xipo (middle/Miaodigou-phase, Zhudingyuan survey area, western Henan).** A 40-ha central
place in a three-tiered regional settlement hierarchy (Ma, X. et al. 1999; Wei, X. and Li
2003). Ma, X. (2005) argues households in Xipo's medium rammed-earth buildings — some floors
plastered with cinnabar, associated with pig-dominated faunal remains suggesting ritual
feasting — were "emergent elite families... competing with one another for prestige and
status"; the largest building (F105, 204 m² room area) was built atop these medium houses,
which Liu & Chen read as possibly "manifest[ing] the dominance of some elite groups, resulting
from factional competition within this community" (attributed interpretive claim, not brute
fact). Of 34 excavated Xipo cemetery burials, tomb size did not consistently track grave-good
wealth (the two largest, M27/M29, held no jade; the jade-richest, M11, was a child's), though
larger tombs held more goods on average. Liu & Chen conclude Xipo "apparently imply[s] that
social hierarchy emerged there, representing the first complex society in the Yangshao
culture."

**Late Yangshao regional centers and conflict.** Dadiwan (Gansu), first settled c. 5800 BC,
reached 50 ha by the late phase; its largest house, F901 (290–420 m², multiroomed, with a
2.5-m-diameter central hearth and an associated ~1,000 m² plaza), is read by Liu, L. (2004) as
"a central place for activities of regional communities," probably feasting and redistribution
implying supracommunal leadership. By contrast, Xishan (25 ha, near Zhengzhou, Henan) was a
walled settlement whose ash pits contained adult skeletons "in postures of struggle" mixed with
animal bone, alongside nonlocal Dawenkou/Qujialing-style ceramics read as migration-linked; Liu,
L. (2004) interprets this pattern as evidence "intergroup conflict played an important role in
the development of this settlement pattern" (attributed).

**Ritual and mortuary contrast with Dawenkou.** Ancestor worship and shamanism (*wu*) are
identified by multiple scholars (Chang 1995; Keightley 1985; Liu, L. 2000a) as the region's
major inferred Neolithic religious forms (attributed — names mechanism and meaning). Early
Yangshao cemeteries (e.g., Longgangsi) show a group-oriented ancestral cult with no individual
ritually distinguished, continuing the earlier Peiligang-period pattern. At Shijia (middle
Yangshao), secondary collective burials show consanguineally related individuals interred
together in a patrilocal community, with a sex ratio strongly biased against females
especially among older individuals (Gao, Q. and Lee 1993); Liu, L. (2000a) reads the female
exclusion from ancestral-cult burial as marking "a starting point... when people began to
treat some of their dead forebears differently," probably reflecting reduced economic/political
standing of women even while the broader society remained egalitarian — a single-study
inference, attributed. At **Xishuipo** (Puyang, Henan), Burial M45 held an adult male flanked
by large mollusk-shell mosaics (a dragon, a tiger, and a "Big Dipper" formed of human tibias
and shells); Feng, S. (1990) reads the arrangement as an early lunar-mansion astronomical
scheme, while Chang (1988) reads the occupant as a *wu* (shaman) whose animal images represent
spirit helpers — Liu & Chen note the two readings are not contradictory, since *wu* were held
to be "rationally knowledgeable about the supernatural world where they traveled in their
ecstatic state" (**[POSITION]**, both attributed). Despite its ritual elaboration, M45 held no
personal-wealth markers. Liu & Chen's broader comparison: Dawenkou mortuary ritual grew more
individually and economically elaborate over this period, whereas most Yangshao communities
practiced comparatively moderate, group-oriented burial — a contrast the wiki records via
`contrasts_with: [[dawenkou-culture]]`.

**Shelach-Lavi (2015) addendum.** Isotopic analysis of human bone from Jiangzhai, Shijia,
and Dadiwan level 2 shows millet contributing up to 80 percent of diet by the Yangshao
period (as of Shelach-Lavi 2015) — a marked increase over the lower millet-reliance
signals reported for the earlier [[xinglongwa-culture]] and Dadiwan's earliest phase.
Shelach-Lavi ([[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]]) also notes
Xishan (late Yangshao, Henan, 25 ha) was enclosed by a circular ditch and a pounded-earth
wall originally 3–5 m wide and 2.5 m high, roughly 200 m in diameter, with two gated
entries — a fuller description of the fortification already linked above to intergroup
conflict (Liu 2004). He additionally offers two non-adjudicated readings of increased
manual-labor skeletal indicators from late Yangshao through Longshan times (2800–1900
BCE): they may reflect either agricultural intensification or increased elite-driven
labor extraction under growing stratification — recorded here as an open, attributed
question rather than a resolved finding, alongside the Xipo/Xishan stratification
material above.

Four-way non-identity: none of the above equates Yangshao material culture with a language,
genetic population, or self-conscious people; the matrilineal/hierarchical-nesting dispute
over Jiangzhai and the astronomer/shaman dispute over Xishuipo M45 are recorded as live
positions, not resolved wiki-voice facts.

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 6; research-history densification.)*

- **Historiography.** Andersson 1921 Yang-shaocun → Yangshao painted pottery, millets, dog/chicken/pig; early 14C put Yangshao mid-fourth millennium; until late 1960s prehistory of Chinese agriculture ≈ Yangshao c.3500–2500 BC + Longshan c.2500–1500 BC (Chang 1976 frame via Barker). Research since pushed millet and rice farming to Holocene beginnings; Yangshao now dated **c.5000–3000 BC** (not 3500–2500) (attributed).
- **Village scale.** Banbo, Jiangzhai much larger than Peiligang; Jiangzhai ~100 houses in circle around courtyard, ditch+palisade; slow wheel pottery, craft specialization, hemp and silkworm; millet cultivation and pig breeding mainstay; dog, chicken, water buffalo, small numbers sheep/goats/cattle; activity zoning; lineage/clan differentiation in cemeteries (K.-C. Chang 1986 via Barker) (attributed).
- **Trajectory claim (attributed).** Barker (via Chang): central Huanghe valley shows beginnings of social stratification, technological development, economic intensification, and demographic expansion culminating in the Shang state — a teleological research narrative, not wiki-voice necessity (position).
- **Antecedent Peiligang.** Barker situates Yangshao after Peiligang-type mid-seventh-millennium millet villages (foxtail + broomcorn; C4 isotopes; pigs/dogs/chickens) on loess foothills — permanent terrace fields more likely than swidden (Rowley-Conwy via Barker). Jiahu (southern edge) grows millet **and** rice — mid-Holocene less N–S differentiation (attributed).
- **Identity caution.** Yangshao remains an archaeological-culture construct; not language, genes, or people. Cross-links: [[millet-domestication]], [[rice-domestication]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[barker-graeme]].
