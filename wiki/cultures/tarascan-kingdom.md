# Tarascan Kingdom

The Aztecs called Tarascan territory "Michoacan" ("place of the masters of fish"),
centered on fish-rich Lake Pátzcuaro in western Mexico; the Tarascans' self-designation
and their language's name is Purépecha. Little field archaeology has been done in
Michoacan; the principal source is the ethnohistoric *Relación de Michoacán*, an early
Spanish translation of Tarascan-language document(s) (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Definition and Construct History

In the Late Post-Classic the Tarascan state bordered Aztec-controlled territory to the
south and west and Chichimeca territory to the north. "Pure" Tarascans made up only
about 10 percent of an ethnically mixed population, with many subjects being Nahuatl
speakers. Per the *Relación*, the dominant "Chichimec" ethnic group within Michoacan —
probably semi-barbaric Tarascan-speakers calling themselves Wakúsecha — settled on
islands in Lake Pátzcuaro; their first capital, Pátzcuaro, was "founded" c. AD 1325
under the hero-chief Taríakuri, from which they imposed language and rule on other
groups.

## Material / Linguistic Signature

Purépecha is a linguistic isolate per Coe & Koontz — unrelated to any other known
language. The Tarascans eventually conquered all of present-day Michoacán and built
fortified frontier outposts; the capital moved from Ihuátzio to Tzintzúntzan, the seat of
power when the Spanish arrived in 1522.

## Distribution and Chronology

The Tarascan king, the *Kasonsí*, based at Tzintzúntzan, served as war chief and supreme
judge, with subordinate rulers at Ihuátzio and Pátzcuaro plus four boundary princes; the
court included specialized occupational officials (masons, drum-makers, doctors,
obsidian knappers, anglers, silversmiths, cup-decorators) as well as a zookeeper and a
head war-spy. Per the *Relación*, *Kasonsí* funerals involved elaborate rites and the
sacrifice of attendants — seven palace women (including a "keeper of gold and turquoise
lip-ornaments," a cook, a wine-bearer, and a "keeper of his urinal") and forty male
attendants, including the physician who had failed to cure him.

Unlike the Aztec, but like the late pre-Conquest Maya, the Tarascan priesthood was not
celibate; priests wore a tobacco-gourd container on their backs, and a Supreme High
Priest headed a ranked priestly hierarchy.

## Lifeways

Coe & Koontz describe Tarascan state religion — likely codified within the last ~150
pre-Conquest years — as atypically un-Mesoamerican: no Tlaloc-equivalent rain god, no
Feathered Serpent, and notably absent 260-day count/divinatory calendar use, though an
approximate 18×20-day solar year plus 5 extra days was used; ball courts were rare and
apparently absent at Tzintzúntzan itself.

Per Helen Perlstein Pollard, the Tarascan cosmos comprised three parts: sky (associated
with eagles, hawks, and falcons, and with the Wakúsecha elite), earth (a
four-directional goddess), and the Underworld (death and burrowing creatures). The sun
god Kurikaweri — also a war god and the Wakúsecha's tribal deity, embodied by the
Kasonsí — demanded large firewood offerings implicated in Michoacan deforestation; his
consort Kwerawáperi was an earth-mother creator deity controlling life, death, rain, and
drought; their offspring Xarátenga was the moon/sea goddess of the western domain, able
to take the form of an old woman, coyote, or owl.

Young Wakúsecha aristocratic women received communal education as ceremonial "wives" of
Kurikaweri, usually later married to army officers; there was no formal male education
(fathers trained sons by trade).

The final capital Tzintzúntzan sits on a terraced slope above Lake Pátzcuaro's
northeast arm; an enormous platform (1,440 ft/440 m long) supports five "yákatas"
(rectangular stepped pyramids joined via stepped passageways to round stepped
pyramids), faced in fitted volcanic-stone slabs that Coe & Koontz compare to Inca
masonry; excavated yákatas contained richly furnished burials, interpreted as
Kasonsí/retainer tombs.

## Relations and Successions

Coe & Koontz suggest the Tarascans may have taken over parts of the former
Toltec-controlled northern trade routes — notably Southwestern turquoise commerce —
after [[sites/tula|Tula]]'s fall; Tarascan craftsmen are noted for paper-thin
gold/turquoise-inlaid obsidian earspools/labrets and bimetallic gold-and-silver work.
The Tarascans were persistent rivals of the [[cultures/aztec-empire]]: Aztec ruler
Axayacatl (1469–1481) subdued Tlatelolco but failed against the Tarascan kingdom of
Michoacán.

## Identity Cautions

Tarascan/Purépecha political identity, language (an isolate), and state religion are
each distinctive and should not be assimilated to the broader Mesoamerican pattern
represented by the Aztec and Toltec material on this wiki; Coe & Koontz explicitly flag
Tarascan religion as atypical for the region.

## Historiography

Reconstruction rests heavily on a single ethnohistorical source, the *Relación de
Michoacán*, given the paucity of field archaeology in the region; Helen Perlstein
Pollard's work on Tarascan cosmology supplements this.
