# Funnel Beaker Culture (TRB)

North and north-central European Neolithic archaeological culture named from **funnel-necked beaker** pottery (German *Trichterbecherkultur*, **TRB**). In [[sherratt-andrew|Sherratt]]’s *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997|Economy and Society in Prehistoric Europe]]* program, TRB is the classic **outer-margin** farming world that receives the plough and related secondary-products features asynchronously — not the simultaneous Baden-style settlement revolution of the Carpathian arc.

## Definition and Construct History

TRB is a broad etic culture-historical label covering regional groups from the Netherlands and Denmark through northern Germany and Poland into parts of the North European Plain and related loess margins. Sherratt (1984, 1997) rejected both pure local Mesolithic continuity and a simple fresh southern agricultural wave as sole origins: he reads TRB as a **farming pattern uniting the North European Plain and northern loesslands** that originates from **interface interaction** between loess Neolithic and indigenous northern populations.

**Self-correction (Sherratt 1997 on Ch.6):** He originally sought a major plough-related settlement-pattern horizon at TRB *beginnings* analogous to [[baden-culture|Baden]]. He later abandoned that: TRB start = loess Neolithic × indigenous fusion; first plough after ~3500 BC associated with **little** settlement change; radical settlement change only after ~3000 BC (toward Corded Ware patterns).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (Sherratt-attributed evidence for SPR / traction within TRB):**

- **Bronocice** (SE Poland): four-wheeled vehicle representation on pottery (Phase III ~3500–3100 BC in his table; related phase V horn-core rope-compression marks ~3000–2700 as traction signal in later notes).
- **Krężnica Jara:** yoked-oxen handle (SE TRB).
- **Danish plough-marks:** sequence under barrows/passage graves suggesting ~3500 BC plough horizon (EN C/Fuchsberg mid-4th mill.; MN passage graves; none under earliest long-mounds) — matches Hungarian/Polish iconic traction evidence 3500–3000 BC.
- **Funnel-necked beaker** itself: appears in his “linkup phase” drinking-vessel chain narratives with copper-contact horizons.
- **Monumentality:** TRB regions participate in NW European megalithic / long-mound traditions — see [[megalith]], conversion thesis.

**Linguistic / genetic:** unknown here. Four-way non-identity enforced.

## Distribution and Chronology

Approximate span early fourth to late third millennium BC (regional). Geographic core: North European Plain and southern Scandinavia, with SE Polish TRB (Bronocice) bridging toward Carpathian packages. Sherratt stresses **asynchrony** of secondary products: plough mid-TRB before other features; many northern package elements with Corded Ware; wool rare long in the far west; wheeled vehicles lag plough in NW Europe.

## Lifeways

Early TRB as mixed farming after forager–farmer interface; Kruk’s Little Poland sequence (via Sherratt 1980): Danubian floodplain horticulture → fourth-millennium TRB shift to interfluves/higher ground — not simple slash-and-burn on unlimited forest. Plough opens heavier/middle-range soils without immediately rewriting settlement hierarchy the way Baden does further SE. Livestock and lithic exchange intensify in later fourth–third millennia (obsidian, banded flint mines such as Krzemionki in the wider northern network).

## Identity Cautions

- TRB is a **pottery-and-practice construct**, not a tribe or language.
- “Funnel Beaker people” in popular writing is a reification — use culture/horizon language.
- Do not equate TRB beginnings with the plough (Sherratt’s own retracted error).
- Danish Ertebølle → TRB transition is multi-causal (contact, ecology, social recruitment); Sherratt’s conversion rhetoric is one model among others (Mithen, Rowley-Conwy, etc.).

## Historiography

Culture-history TRB taxonomy (northern European scholarship); New Archaeology demography and Kruk settlement models; Sherratt SPR (1981) and 1997 self-corrections; Renfrew megalith/territorial models; later aDNA farmer–forager debates re-open population questions without collapsing them into the ceramic label. Chapman’s autonomist critiques of SPR (wheels/ploughmarks/wool) partly answered on TRB evidence in Sherratt 1987.

## Whittle densification — settlement, monuments, forager interface (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 6–7 and synthesis. Mid-1990s cal BC; **pre-aDNA**. TRB = etic ceramic/practice construct, not a people or language.)*

### Origins interface (Kujavia and the north)

Whittle leaves TRB origins open between indigenous Mesolithic people and offshoots of the “Danubian” tradition (late [[lengyel-culture|Lengyel]] Brześć Kujawski ¹⁴C overlaps early TRB in Kujavia). Early TRB in Kujavia largely sandy/morainic zones complementary to black-soil islands of Danubian tradition (Sarnowo ~20 km SW of Brześć). Possible chronological overlap so a **new cultural tradition imitated the longhouse idea while longhouses still existed** (Kujavia elongated trapezoidal/triangular earthen mounds with small stone kerbs covering broad-end individual or few burials — Sarnowo and others); charcoal ¹⁴C confusion caveat. Early TRB mounds at Sarnowo and Wietrzychowice important date markers for the early long-mound horizon; claimed ardmarks under barrow 8 at Sarnowo on sandy soil near a stream — not the most obvious place for sustained cereal cultivation (attributed caution).

Ch. 10 synthesis: LBK–Ertebølle convergence → northern TRB; southerly TRB less clear (dispersed inland foragers). Continuity of population from [[ertebolle-culture|Ertebølle]] to early TRB (ENI) in Denmark and southern Sweden; regional early pottery styles match regional Ertebølle material culture. Recurrent northern Neolithic pattern: dispersed settlement, woodscape mobility, limited transient clearances, herding, hunting, small-scale cultivation; **substantial domestic houses infrequent** (TRB, Michelsberg, N Chasséen, British earlier Neolithic) — contrast Alpine foreland waterlogged settlements. Not inexorable population growth/intensification. Cattle especially valued (enclosure ditches; later Globular Amphora animal burials).

### Settlement scale and aggregation (as of Whittle 1996)

Large TRB sites like **Gródek Nadbużny** (~10 ha on Bug; pits, possible huts, work areas; cattle dominant; multi-source flint including Krzemionki banded flint; Volhynian copper ores) lack defining boundaries/clear plans; Whittle suggests large sites spaced across landscape (cf. Tripolye) may have been **points of reference and venues for periodic gatherings** by more dispersed populations — not steady expansion of population/agricultural economy after Danubian settlements.

**Bronocice** on a loess ridge above the Nidzica: up to ~50 ha through a long TRB–Lublin-Volhynian–Baden sequence (TRB first phase ~5 ha; later TRB expansion ~18–26 ha; Baden contraction ~17 ha); Lublin-Volhynian oval ditch ~170×210 m; decorated pot from last TRB phase appears to show a four-wheeled waggon; ox horncore from Baden pit suggests traction; TRB pot handle from Krężnica Jara modelled as yoked cattle pair — better evidence for changing animal uses closer to the Carpathian basin.

Kujavia: TRB sites more dispersed onto sandy/morainic soils; few domestic structures; notable burial mounds; cattle dominant. Globular Amphora usually after or parallel to late TRB, but Kujavian evidence (e.g. Krusza Zamkowa) cited for contemporaneity — Globular Amphora representing Danubian tradition more than TRB in one reading — still dispersed settlement, **abandonment of longhouse pattern**, landscape framed by burial sites/monuments.

Banded flint from **Krzemionki** (Holy Cross hills): sustained exploitation began in TRB, greatest extraction in Globular Amphora; found up to ~400 km from source; Whittle argues against increasingly specialised controlled production — open access, gift exchange, axe as badge of communal membership and hospitality capacity. Copper made in Alpine region and SE TRB area, moved irregularly beyond production zones. Northern TRB pottery: wider formalised serving forms; fermented drinks possible; food/drink offerings to spirits at ancestral monuments; pots as promoters of uniformity in mobile lifestyles.

### Monuments and enclosures

Long form dominated the north European plain; Dutch hunebedden and German/Polish Grosssteingräber vary mound/kerb/chamber combinations; massive external pottery depositions (e.g. >1000 pots at Emmeln 2; ~650 at Havelte D53) — offerings marking exits of souls/spirits rather than only human needs; Globular Amphora and [[corded-ware-culture|Corded Ware]] burials later inserted into existing monuments. Danish sequence: early mounds over timber/stone graves → ENII dolmens → early MNA passage graves in flourish then few new builds; passage graves largely in round mounds — longhouse idea “must therefore in some sense have faded.” Shell middens (Ertebølle elongated mounds with inserted burials; Bjørnsholm long barrow near midden) another possible source for long-mound idea alongside longhouse memory.

Middle Neolithic ditched enclosures discontinuous from central-west France to southern Scandinavia; SE TRB enclosures more often uninterrupted; Michelsberg/TRB common but none so far in Dutch TRB west group (as of Whittle); Denmark ~30 known. Possible source: later LBK and earlier Lengyel enclosure tradition kept alive as cultural memory; alternative contemporary occupation-space models. After long debate on TRB–Corded Ware/Single Grave overlap, favoured model is succession (Danish MNB late phase); successive chronology supports **continuity rather than population movement** as primary explanation (Whittle’s position — pre-aDNA).
