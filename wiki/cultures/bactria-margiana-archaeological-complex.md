# Bactria–Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC)

## Definition and Construct History

A Bronze Age archaeological culture (also "Oxus Civilization") of the
Bactria–Margiana region of Central Asia (modern Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan,
northern Afghanistan). Documented here only as it bears on the Indus, from
[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|Wright (2010)]] (drawing on Possehl 2002, Kohl 2007,
J.-F. Jarrige, Hemphill). An **etic construct**; densify from Central Asian
sources when ingested.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** A distinctive assemblage — including round/compartmented stamp
seals, monumental architecture, and characteristic metalwork — appearing at
Indus and Baluchistan sites especially in the late third / early second
millennium BCE. Kohl (2007) and J.-F. Jarrige suggest BMAC origins may span a
wider region including eastern Iran and Balochistan.

**Linguistic / Genetic.** Unknown. An Indo-Iranian linguistic association is
often proposed but is not demonstrated; do not equate the BMAC material culture
with a language or population.

## Distribution and Chronology

BMAC material reaches the Indus world c. 2300–2000 BCE: at [[shortugai|Shortugai]]
(Afghanistan) Indus material is fully replaced by BMAC material around 2200 BCE;
BMAC-associated Period VIII cenotaphs appear at [[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] and the
new site of Sibri; round BMAC-motif seal impressions occur at
[[nausharo|Nausharo]], southern Baluchistan (Kulli sites), eastern Iran, and —
notably — as far east as Gilund in Rajasthan (Shinde et al. 2005), "significantly
expand[ing] the geographic picture" (as of Wright 2010).

## Identity Cautions

Wright (attributed) argues the appearance of BMAC material at Indus sites
reflects **small-scale interaction of key objects across a broad zone, not a
large population migration** — and cites Hemphill and Christensen (1994) that
there is no skeletal evidence for "massive population replacement" of Indus
Valley populations. This bears directly on the four-way non-identity rule: BMAC
pots at Indus sites are `associated_with`, not evidence of an incoming people,
language, or genetic population. (As of Wright 2010; this pre-dates the
ancient-DNA literature on Indo-Iranian movement — date-stamp and update via the
Contradiction and Replication Protocol when aDNA sources are ingested.)
