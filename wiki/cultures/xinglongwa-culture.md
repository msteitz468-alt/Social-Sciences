# Xinglongwa Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Xinglongwa culture, dated ca. 6200–5200 BCE, represents the early stage of Neolithic
development in Northeast China, per [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen
(2012)]]. It comprises roughly 100 known sites in the hilly Liaoxi region (southeastern
Inner Mongolia, western Liaoning, northern Hebei). The type site, Xinglongwa itself, is
a 3-hectare moated settlement with 170 excavated houses spanning three occupation
phases. `construct_status: robust` — a regionally well-defined etic archaeological-
culture construct, not equated by the source with a language or genetic population.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** Xinglongwa sites are characterized by
rectangular subterranean houses arranged in rows, 50–80 m² (the largest reaching 140
m²), with some settlements surrounded by ditches; storage pits occur both inside and
outside houses, and indoor burials are occasionally found beneath house floors. Tomb
M118 at Xinglongwa held the most unusual mortuary offerings: an adult male buried with
two pigs and numerous ceramic, lithic, bone, shell, and jade artifacts, including 715
microblades. At Xinglongwa, two complete adult pig skeletons (male and female), legs
apparently bound, were found within a human burial — the earliest known instance of
complete pigs in human burial in China, later becoming widespread as animal sacrifice
in the north China Neolithic (Yang & Liu 1997). Interpretation of this joint
human-pig burial is disputed: Yang & Liu (1997) suggest wild-pig totemism, while
others (Luo 2007; Yuan & Yang, in press) argue it reflects a special human-pig
relationship distinguishing pigs from other wild animals. Coefficient-of-variation
analysis of pig tooth measurements (Table 4.1, Liu & Chen 2012) shows Xinglongwa
assemblages far more variable than modern wild boar populations, suggesting mixed
wild/domestic/hybrid pig populations.

Isotope analysis (N=7 individuals, Zhang, X. et al. 2003) of human bone from
Xinglongwa found that roughly 85 percent of diet derived from C4 plants; residue
analysis of grinding stones at Baiyinchanghan and Xinglonggou identified yams, acorn,
and grasses including domesticated millet, mostly interpreted as processing wild
plants. At Xinglonggou, millet accounted for only 15 percent of flotation-recovered
seeds during the Xinglongwa period, versus 99 percent in Lower Xiajiadian Bronze Age
deposits (4000–3500 cal BP) at the same site — evidence of millet's minor early
dietary role.

Xinglongwa culture shows the region's earliest ritual figurations: clay and stone
human figurines (predominantly depicting female bodies), jade and stone ornaments
(cicada and bear shapes), and face masks made of shell, stone, and human skullcap,
found in household deposits and burials.

## Distribution and Chronology

Roughly 100 sites are known across the hilly Liaoxi region. Xinglongwa culture was
succeeded by the Zhaobaogou culture (ca. 5200–4500 BCE) and then the [[hongshan-
culture]] (ca. 4500–3000 BCE) in the same Liao River system, per Liu & Chen's regional
sequence; population is reported to have grown steadily from Xinglongwa through
Zhaobaogou, then increased rapidly during Hongshan before declining sharply in the
succeeding Xiaoheyan culture (ca. 3000–2200 BCE).

## Lifeways

Liu & Chen (2012) conclude that, despite the presence of large sedentary settlements,
Xinglongwa subsistence followed a broad-spectrum strategy emphasizing hunting and
gathering, with only minor components of food production. Liu & Chen (citing Liu, L.
2007 and Li, X. 2008) interpret Xinglongwa household ritual practice — figurines,
indoor burials — as a possible social response to the scalar and economic stresses of
transitioning from mobile to large sedentary communities. Archaeological/genetic
evidence (per Luo 2007, as cited in Liu & Chen 2012) supports multiple independent
centers of pig domestication in China, with Xinglongwa culture sites in Northeast
China representing one such center, alongside [[jiahu]] (north-central) and Kuahuqiao
(central Yangzi), each showing locally distinct pig morphology.

## Relations and Successions

Xinglongwa `succeeded_by` the Zhaobaogou culture (ca. 5200–4500 BCE) and then
[[hongshan-culture]] in the Liao River system — these successions are temporal and
material, not claims of ethnic or linguistic continuity. Xinglongwa is `analogous_to`
[[cishan-culture]], [[houli-culture]], and [[peiligang-culture]] as broad-spectrum
early-Neolithic north China economies with only minor food production.

## Identity Cautions

Xinglongwa is a material-culture construct defined by settlement form, mortuary
practice, and artifact assemblage. Liu & Chen do not equate it with a language, an
ethnic group, or a genetic population. Isotope dietary data (C4 plant intake) speaks
to subsistence, not to population ancestry, and should not be conflated with a
genetic-population claim.

## Historiography

Xinglongwa's household ritual assemblage (figurines, masks) is treated by Liu & Chen
as the region's earliest evidence of such practice, feeding into the later, larger-
scale ceremonial architecture of [[hongshan-culture]] (see [[niuheliang]]). See
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source discussion.
