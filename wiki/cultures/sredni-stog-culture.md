# Sredni Stog Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The **Sredni Stog culture** is an etic archaeological construct for Eneolithic material assemblages of the Ukrainian (Pontic) steppes, named from the type-site island in the Dnieper Rapids excavated in 1927. D. Ya. Telegin (1973) first mapped the culture as a regional package — about **150 sites** from the Ingul west of the Dnieper to the lower Don — and this grouping became Marija [[gimbutas-marija|Gimbutas]]’s archaeological foundation for Indo-European “Kurgan” steppe pastoralists. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]], in *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* (2007), retains Telegin’s grouping against Yu. Rassamakin’s proposed split, arguing Telegin’s documentation is stronger. Archaeological cultures are analytical packages of co-occurring traits, not self-identified peoples (`associated_with`, not identity).

**Gimbutas 1991 — Sredniy Stog II as Kurgan I package (attributed).** In *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]*, Gimbutas treats **Sredniy Stog II** (her spelling) as the Lower Dnieper face of Kurgan Wave 1 / early Yamna expansion. At the type site she reports Dnieper-Donets (Sredniy Stog I) overlain ~4500 B.C. or earlier by Sredniy Stog II: single burial in cairn-covered shaft/cist graves; supine contracted or extended; flint daggers, arrowheads, spearpoints, pointed-base beakers; shell-tempered pots with stamped/pitted/cord solar motifs vs earlier vegetal temper; skeletal type she describes as dolichomesocranial, taller, more slender than robust Cro-Magnon Dnieper-Donets predecessors. She holds local evolution cannot account for the abrupt burial, ceramic, weapon, and physical-type changes — complex is **extension of Volga pastoralists into the Dnieper basin** at the end of Khvalynsk (Gimbutas 1991). Weapon kit she calls prototypical IE weaponry usable from horseback (long flint daggers to 22 cm, bifacial spearpoints, triangular arrowheads, narrow-butted axes); rich male graves with shell belts, copper beads, spiral arm rings; copper via barter from Ai-Bunar through Cucuteni. Dereivka: bones of 55 horses = 63% of domesticated animal remains; antler cheek pieces; tombs with sacrificed horses and dogs; man+woman (widow?) and man+child double graves frequent; calibrated C-14 second half 5th mill. (her lab codes UCLA 1466, Kiev 466, Kiev 465). Suttee-type double graves also at Yama and Aleksandriya (Lower Dnieper, Sredniy Stog II). **These are her correlations** of Sredniy Stog II with Kurgan I and early Yamna — `associated_with` her IE equation, not identity of culture with language, people, or genes. See [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]], [[yamnaya-culture]].

Anthony treats the **Suvorovo–Novodanilovka** complex (~35–40 cemeteries, most with fewer than ten graves; ~4300/4200–before ~3900 BCE) as the **chiefly elite within Sredni Stog** (Telegin’s original position), not a separate culture — involved in raiding and trading with the lower Danube during Tripolye B1, just before the collapse of “Old Europe.” Gimbutas 1991 independently highlights Suvorovo (Moldavia) and Casimcea (Dobruja) horse-head scepter chieftain graves with presumed suttee as first IE ideology in east-central Europe — layered with, not overwritten by, Anthony’s elite-within-Sredni-Stog reading.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Anthony 2007):** Round-bottomed, **shell-tempered** pottery (contrasting with Dnieper-Donets II flat-based, sand-tempered wares); cord decoration appears in later phases. Burial shift from DDII communal multilayer pits to **individual single graves**, some with stone circles and low stone or earth mounds — Anthony’s candidates for the **earliest kurgans** in the steppes (e.g. Kvityana, Maiorka). Standard posture on the **back with raised knees**, ENE orientation — a package Anthony sees as copying eastern ([[khvalynsk-culture|Khvalynsk]]) practice. Polished **horse-head and eared stone maces** with older steppe prototypes (DDII, Khvalynsk, Varfolomievka) appear both in Sredni Stog contexts and in Tripolye B1 villages (Fitioneşti, Fedeleşeni horse-heads; Obarşeni, Berezovskaya GES eared), often with shell-tempered **Cucuteni C** ware — Anthony reads them as steppe symbols of power and likely steppe presence inside farming villages.

**Linguistic:** No texts. Anthony’s theoretical assignment places archaic PIE among Pontic-Caspian steppe tribes around 4000 BCE, including Sredni Stog; the Suvorovo move into the lower Danube ~4200 BCE is his candidate historical event separating **Pre-Anatolian** dialects from the steppe community. This remains an attributed model under [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]], not a material reading of language.

**Genetic:** Not treated in Anthony 2007 for this culture as such.

## Distribution and Chronology

| Phase (Anthony) | Approximate dates (cal BCE) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Early Sredni Stog (I; no cord decoration) | from ~4400 | Contemporary with Tripolye B1 crisis |
| Dereivka settlement (IIa) | 4200–3700 | Ten radiocarbon dates at [[dereivka]] |
| Late Sredni Stog (IIb) | to ~3600–3300 (Petrovskaya Balka); some Dnieper locales to ~3400 | Final Eneolithic with Mikhailovka I, Post-Mariupol/Kvityana, Repin, late Khvalynsk |

Geographic span: Ukrainian steppes from the **Ingul** west of the Dnieper to the **lower Don**. Dereivka (three excavated Sredni Stog structures; horses 63% of animal bones) is the best-known settlement. Late Sredni Stog is one of Anthony’s **five Final Eneolithic** Pontic-Caspian cultures (~3800–3300 BCE), with shifting borders and interpenetration.

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

At [[dereivka]], V. I. Bibikova counted **2,408 horse bones** (MNI ≥51) — more than half the mammals butchered, ~9,000 kg of meat; horses **63%** of mammal NISP; cattle 16%, red deer 10%. Anthony links the doubling of horse bones relative to DDII to colder climate ~4200–3800 BCE and advantages of maintaining domestic horses. Predecessor DDII settlements around the Rapids already mixed stockkeeping with fishing (¹⁵N often >50% fish meat) and horse hunting; Sredni Stog continues a herding-plus-hunting economy without the towns, ploshchadka houses, female figurines, or painted ceramics of neighboring [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture|Cucuteni-Tripolye]]. Anthony models Tripolye B–Sredni Stog relations as partly **mutualist** (alliances, gifts, possible herd management) while arguing mounted raiding also intensified frontier conflict.

## Relations and Successions

- `preceded_by` local DDII forager–early herder communities; Telegin’s DDII vs CT contrast: communal cemeteries, no female figurines, bark huts, little grain, different pottery — “fundamentally different people and almost certainly spoke different languages” (Anthony 2007, attributed).
- `associated_with` eastern contribution: Anthony argues **Volga / Khvalynsk-related** immigrants helped instigate the DDII→Sredni Stog shift in funeral custom and pottery (“Perhaps they arrived on horseback”).
- `associated_with` [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture]] frontier: Cucuteni C ware, maces, fortification spike in Tripolye B1; Chapaevka (Tripolye B2/C1, ~3700–3400 BCE) as earliest known Tripolye cemetery burial — gradual Dnieper-frontier assimilation with late Sredni Stog.
- `associated_with` Suvorovo–Novodanilovka elite complex and lower-Danube raiding/trading before Old Europe’s collapse.
- `followed_by` / contributes to late Final Eneolithic package; Yamnaya horizon after ~3300 BCE reorganizes the steppes — do not equate Sredni Stog with Yamnaya.

## Identity Cautions

- Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people. Sredni Stog is a **material-culture construct** (Telegin 1973; retained by Anthony 2007; absorbed into Kurgan Wave 1 by Gimbutas 1991 as **her** package).
- Gimbutas’s broad “Kurgan culture” absorbed Sredni Stog into an invasion narrative and equated Sredniy Stog II with Volga pastoralist extension + early Yamna/Kurgan I; Anthony keeps a narrower steppe EBA core, treats Suvorovo–Novodanilovka as elite within the culture, and rejects Hun-style nomadic-army analogies for Eneolithic warfare (seasonal tribal raiding, not cavalry armies). Layer both; do not merge.
- The famous Dereivka “cult stallion” bit-wear claim was **retracted** as a Scythian intrusion (see [[dereivka]], [[bit-wear-analysis]]) — it does not date Eneolithic riding at the site. Gimbutas 1991 still cites Dereivka horse bones, cheek pieces, and horse/dog sacrifice as riding/cult evidence within her Kurgan package; that inventory is her position, qualified by the later retraction for the cult stallion specifically.
- Dates and internal phases: Anthony’s 2007 synthesis (calibrated radiocarbon; human-bone dates require freshwater-reservoir caution) is the primary vault chronology; Gimbutas’s mid/late 5th-mill. Sredniy Stog II figures are her wave frame.

## Historiography

Primary vault sources for this construct’s steppe-hypothesis role: (1) **Gimbutas 1991** — Sredniy Stog II as Lower Dnieper Kurgan I / Volga-pastoralist extension, horse cult, Wave 1 weapon kit, Dereivka inventory (*[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991]]* Ch.10); (2) **Anthony 2007** — Telegin’s site maps and phasing retained, Bibikova’s faunal work, debates with Rassamakin, Levine, Häusler, and Gimbutas; Suvorovo–Novodanilovka as elite within culture. Earlier Soviet definitions (Telegin) are reported via Anthony. Horse-economy and riding claims for Dereivka must be separated: settlement dates and horse-bone abundance remain, but the bit-wear stallion does not.
