# La Tène Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Later Iron Age archaeological culture of temperate Europe, named for the lakeside site of [[la-tene]] (Lake Neuchâtel, Switzerland). Defined by weapon, ornament, and art-style packages (La Tène A–D). Cunliffe (1997) ties La Tène material horizons to the era of historically attested [[celtic-migrations]] (c.400–200 BCE) and to the developed [[oppida]] world of the last two centuries BCE — **without equating style with ethnicity**.

**Construct history (as Cunliffe historicizes it).** Discovery at La Tène (1857) and comparison of Champagne graves with the cemetery in the ruins of Etruscan Marzabotto (1871 International Congress, Bologna — Gabriel de Mortillet and Emile Desor) fixed La Tène-type finds as the material culture of Celts who crossed the Alps “just as Livy and Polybius had described.” Hans Hildebrand (1872) systematized Early Iron Age = Hallstatt / Later = La Tène, the latter regarded as archaeological expression of the Celts. Otto Tischler’s Early/Middle/Late (I–III) and Paul Reinecke’s La Tène A (fifth-century Bavarian tumulus phase as formative **pre-migration** phase) then B–D underpinned twentieth-century studies. Déchelette’s *Le Second Age du fer* (1914) was the first European-wide La Tène synthesis; Jacobsthal’s *Early Celtic Art* (1944) fixed art-style labels (Early Style, Waldalgesheim/Vegetal, Plastic, Sword styles) that Cunliffe treats as convenient but classificatory rather than explanatory.

Cunliffe’s methodological core: if a Celt is one who spoke Celtic, linguistic studies **uncouple “Celt” from archaeologically defined La Tène culture** — Celtic language was spoken considerably earlier and over a more extensive area than La Tène material. Iberia crystallizes the point: classical Celtiberians, Celtic speech, and social systems resembling temperate-European Celts coexist with material culture markedly **not** of La Tène type (see [[celtiberian-culture]], [[celticity-debate]]).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material signature (attributed Cunliffe 1997).**

- **Warrior package.** Iron swords in iron/bronze/wood/leather sheaths as warrior symbol (elaborate sheath decoration; blade stamps); throwing and thrusting spears with great spearhead variety — the La Tène type-site assemblage includes serrated, flame-shaped, hollow-ground, and notched forms matching Diodorus’s description of flesh-tearing spears. Shield as normal protection; sometimes helmets. Cunliffe notes Polybius’s report that Celtic swords at Telamon bent may partly confuse battle damage with deliberate **ritual bending** before deposition.
- **Vehicles.** From the fifth century, aristocracy favours **two-wheeled** funerary carts (vs Late Hallstatt four-wheeled). Light war chariot (iron-tired spoked wheels ~0.9 m, two ponies, open front/back) is a specialized version of that two-wheeler; ultimate inspiration may be Etruscan; funerary vehicles more likely parade/status vehicles than war chariots (though convertible).
- **Art.** Highly distinctive [[celtic-art]] develops in the Early La Tène zone (Marne–Moselle–Bohemia): Early Style in fifth-century elite burials of Moselle and Marne, modelled on imported Mediterranean wine kit but locally manufactured (beaked flagons at Kleinaspergle, Dürrnberg, Basse-Yutz); fourth-century **Vegetal Style** (Marne, Switzerland, Hungary, Po Valley — Cunliffe prefers this label over “Waldalgesheim master”); third–early second centuries Hungarian Sword Style (dragon-pairs), Swiss school, Free Graphic / Free Plastic styles on warrior and women’s ornaments. Broad cultural similarity over vast tracts coexists with dazzling decorative inventiveness; technology of construction is distinct from pure decoration.
- **Craft and iron.** Ironworking from eighth-century introduction; late La Tène double-pyramidal and sword-shaped iron bars; layered steel edges on swords; lost-wax casting (e.g. Gussage All Saints); coral and red glass insets; later enamelling.
- **Later landscape.** From c.200 BCE production intensification and large nucleated defended settlements ([[oppida]]); coinage from early third century BC as gift/clientage medium more than capitalist commerce (Cunliffe).

**Linguistic signature.** Overlap with Celtic speech is incomplete in time and space. Galatians in Asia Minor maintained Celtic ethnic/linguistic identity for centuries with **almost no** distinctive La Tène metalwork (four bracelets, twenty brooches total from Asia Minor in Cunliffe’s count) — elite dominance without material ethnic symbols. Atlantic zones show Celtic speech with selective La Tène adoption.

**Genetic signature.** Not treated in Cunliffe 1997.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Early La Tène (fifth–early fourth centuries).** Three power/innovation zones after the Late Hallstatt core’s decay:

1. **Moselle–Rhine confluence** — dense Etruscan bronzes (Vulci beaked flagons, stamnoi), local gold-work, selective adaptation of Etruscan motifs into new art; by century’s end richer and more innovative than the Neckar.
2. **Marne valley** — intense occupation; aristocrat typically in pit with two-wheeled vehicle, harness, drinking set; fewer southern luxuries than Moselle but stable culture and major art role (Somme-Bionne, etc.).
3. **Bohemia (via Dürrnberg/east Alpine–Adriatic axis)** — elite burials later fifth century with Etruscan wine gear; Závist cult centre continuity from Hallstatt.

Subsidiary node: **Bourges** continuing Late Hallstatt trade (Massiliote amphorae, Attic wares) into Early La Tène, sited for Atlantic corridor control. Eastern salt: Hallstatt mines from c.1000 BCE and Dürrnberg from c.600, both ceasing c.400 BCE; Dürrnberg prospers early fifth century with elite two-wheeled vehicle graves.

Cunliffe’s brackets: La Tène A c.450/440–c.370/350 BCE (with possible ~generation overlap, earliest La Tène from c.480 preferred on balance).

**Migrations horizon (c.400–200 BCE).** Homelands of Marne, Moselle, and Bohemia contribute populations through Alpine passes to the Po Valley; archaeology of Boii (cremation; Bohemian traits) vs Senones/Cenomani (Marne-like styles) offers attributed confirmation of classical tribal settlement accounts without collapsing culture = people. Eastward spread fills northern Transdanubia to the Great Hungarian Plain and Transylvania; sparse La Tène objects in Thrace/Asia Minor need not map settlement density. Fourth-century movements highly complex (folk migration ↔ exchange of wives/warriors ↔ indigenous style adoption). Third century: huge geographic sweep of similar art and burial behaviour as fluidity of ideas partly driven by population flux; possible westbound returns to Champagne and Languedoc/Toulouse.

**Later La Tène (c.200 BCE–Roman conquest).** Intensification, oppida, Roman wine (Dressel 1 amphorae), coinage, gradient of social zones from Mediterranean-adjacent oligarchies to prestige-burial peripheries — see [[oppida]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[urbanization]], [[celtic-migrations]].

**Atlantic and Britain.** Selective adoption: Early La Tène vehicle-burial rite appears in Lower Seine, Ardennes, Haine, and Yorkshire — population movement possible but not required (local elite fashion / diplomatic gifts). Armorica largely lacks Ha C items; develops distinctive stamped pottery and later ceramic art. Eastern Britain copies Vegetal Style warrior gear, mostly river dedications. Ireland: significant Ha C participation then long relative isolation (Cunliffe: “backwater for almost half a millennium”).

## Lifeways

**Early La Tène warrior aristocracies.** Differ from Late Hallstatt chiefdoms: geographic foci barely overlap; fighting weapons frequent vs Hallstatt hunting/display weapons; two-wheeled funerary carts; shared elite burial with wine-drinking equipment from/inspired by Mediterranean *symposion* kit. Cunliffe theorizes that northern-periphery raiding, once feeding the core prestige-goods system, becomes embedded after west Hallstatt chiefdoms collapse — generating “one of the defining characteristics of early Celtic society.” Overpopulation (preferred classical cause) plus raid-and-retainer structure set the migrations stage.

**Feast, clientage, potlatch (later classical ethnography applied carefully).** Poseidonian feast as hierarchy definition; hero’s portion; potlatch-like wealth dispersal (Louernius); multi-level clientage in first-century BCE Gaul (Caesar). These are **textual** models for historical Celts, not automatic readings of every La Tène assemblage.

**Third–early second centuries.** Small communities with mobile warrior menfolk; some archaeologists call this democratization; swords and women’s ornaments carry metalwork development.

**Post-200 BCE.** Raid obsolescence near Roman frontiers; conspicuous giving/consumption and control of commodities (esp. wine) through oppida and coinage replace raid prowess as elite display (Cunliffe’s synthesis).

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by** late [[hallstatt-culture]] in many zones; Ha→LT transition is core decay + northern periphery flourish (“mushroom ring”), not simple renaming.
- **Overlaps imperfectly with** classical Celts/Gauls/Galatians and with [[celtic-languages]] — see [[celts]], [[celticity-debate]].
- **Art and Mediterranean models:** [[celtic-art]]; wine-kit imports and prestige monopoly: [[prestige-goods-economy]].
- **Migration-era expansion and later contraction under Rome:** [[celtic-migrations]].
- **Late nucleated centres:** [[oppida]]; wider urbanization debates: [[urbanization]].
- **Iberian contrast case:** [[celtiberian-culture]] — dissociation of La Tène package from Celtic speech/identity.

## Identity Cautions

- La Tène art ≠ Celtic speech ≠ genetic people ≠ classical ethnonym. Cunliffe’s closing retrospect: language and La Tène material/art are different attributes, by no means coterminous.
- Extreme paucity of La Tène material in Greece and Asia Minor — where Celts were historically active — is Cunliffe’s standing warning that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence; Galatia shows the inverse (identity without La Tène package).
- Eastern and Atlantic regions show **selective** adoption; Celtiberia shows Celtic speech without La Tène material dominance.
- Archaeological “celticization” in Cunliffe’s prose is a cultural-process label, not four-way identity.
- Ulster Cycle material culture is a “window on the Dark Ages,” not on La Tène (Mallory via Cunliffe) — do not back-project medieval Irish epic kit onto the continental Iron Age.

## Densification from Cunliffe, *Facing the Ocean* (2001)

Cunliffe (2001) reiterates that "Hallstatt"/"La Tène" is an artificial nomenclature carrying no implication of ethnicity or cultural continuity/dislocation, restating this page's construct-history caution from a second, later synthesis. He adds detail on La Tène A elite formation in the Marne/Moselle (5th century BCE): new power centres with two-wheeled vehicle burials, warrior gear, and the new decorative style later termed "Celtic Art," partly inspired by Greek/Etruscan motifs reaching the zone via a Moselle–Etruscan trading axis (Vulci beaked flagons) that Cunliffe suggests eclipsed Massalia-routed Greek influence around 500 BCE (Cunliffe 2001; see [[hallstatt-culture]]).

He also documents cross-Pyrenean La Tène linkages bearing on the culture's southern edge: the antennae-hilted sword is shared as elite-warrior weaponry across Galicia, Celtiberia, and communities north of the Pyrenees (Aquitania, Languedoc), with close stylistic overlap between Celtiberian and southern French forms — read as evidence of long-term elite exchange across the western Pyrenees passes rather than migration (Cunliffe 2001; see [[celtiberian-culture]]). By contrast, direct exchange evidence between the Celtiberian zone and the Atlantic-facing NW Iberian communities is "surprisingly sparse" in his review, suggesting the Celtiberian interior remained relatively isolated from the Atlantic seaways (Cunliffe 2001).

## Historiography

Franks (“Late Keltic,” 1863), Keller’s lake-village framing of La Tène, the 1871 Marzabotto equation, Hildebrand/Tischler/Reinecke periodization, Déchelette synthesis, Jacobsthal art styles, and invasionist then anti-invasionist readings structure the field. Since c.1970 Cunliffe notes a shift toward economic and world-systems models (coinage, oppida, pan-European Celt image as political metaphor). Cunliffe 1997 ([[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]]; [[cunliffe-barry]]) is a pan-European synthesis that keeps the La Tène package as archaeologists’ “Celtic” material culture while insisting on dissociation from language and classical ethnonym where the evidence demands it. Absolute dates and regional sequences have continued to refine since 1997; the attributed social narrative (Hallstatt collapse → Early La Tène periphery → migrations → oppida/Roman periphery) remains this source’s frame. [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001]] (Ch. 8) is a second, independent Cunliffe synthesis reinforcing the construct-history caution and adding Iberia-facing cross-Pyrenean detail (see Densification section above).
