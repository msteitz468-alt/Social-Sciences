# Sinagua Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Sinagua** is an archaeological culture of north-central Arizona, named and defined by **Harold S. Colton** (Museum of Northern Arizona). *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* Chapter 4 reviews the construct’s history: Colton formed his notion of **Southern Sinagua** partly on Verde Valley sites; tourist destinations such as Tuzigoot, Montezuma Castle, and Montezuma Well have been attributed to Sinagua in public interpretation. `construct_status: conventional` — an etic culture-historical unit, not an observed people.

Colton’s sketch (as summarized by Whittlesey, attributed): upland and riverine groups in separate zones ~AD 500–1125; after ~**1125**, pueblo-building uplanders argued to displace riverine Hohokam (Northern Sinagua migration south); after ~1300 upland abandonments; possible continued movement toward the Gila–Salt — **migration hypotheses, not established facts**.

**Albert H. Schroeder** reworked Sinagua relations and advanced the broader **Hakataya** construct, including arguments linking some material patterns toward historic Yavapai — another attributed equation requiring four-way non-identity care. Later ecological and CRM research (Pilles, Fish, McGuire, ASU/ASM programs) complicated monothetic labels.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of LVAP 1997 research-history synthesis):** paddle-and-anvil and other ceramic traditions in regional comparisons; masonry pueblos and field-house patterns in Verde and Flagstaff country; interaction with [[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] red-on-buff spheres and plateau white wares. LVAP Classic site [[roadhouse-ruin|Roadhouse Ruin]] shows almost no local decorated tradition; Lone Juniper includes a feature discussed in Sinagua-related terms (Ch. 13) — association, not identity.

**Linguistic / genetic:** silent in this source.

## Distribution and Chronology

Literature core: Flagstaff / San Francisco Peaks; extension into middle and upper Verde. Lower Verde is a **contact and joint-use** zone in LVAP framing rather than classic Colton heartland. Chronology in older literature often borrowed Phoenix Basin or middle Verde phase sequences — a problem LVAP’s own dating program was designed to reduce for the project area.

## Lifeways

Farming (irrigation and dryland), hunting, and exchange within the Transition Zone. Whittlesey et al. emphasize mobility, multi-tradition landscapes, and indigenous riverine occupations over simple colonization-from-Phoenix narratives when discussing Verde prehistory as a whole.

## Relations and Successions

- `associated_with:` [[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] — ceramics, ballcourt-period interaction, contested “Southern Sinagua as local Hohokam expression” position recorded as a *position*, not fact.
- `associated_with:` plateau puebloan traditions (architecture, white wares).
- Historic [[yavapai|Yavapai]] / [[western-apache|Western Apache]] occupation of overlapping geography: **not** descent without independent argument (Schroeder-style links remain attributed hypotheses).

## Identity Cautions

Sinagua ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ self-name. Verde sites labeled Sinagua in older literature may conflate local traditions with Flagstaff-centric types. Prefer `associated_with` when linking to Hohokam or historic peoples.

## Historiography

Colton MNA program; Schroeder Hakataya; ecological turn of the 1970s; CAP/CRM surveys; LVAP Ch. 4 as mid-1990s state-of-research snapshot for central Arizona. Salado (Tonto Basin) is a related labeling problem treated in the same chapter’s Tonto research history — keep Salado distinct unless a source argues association.
