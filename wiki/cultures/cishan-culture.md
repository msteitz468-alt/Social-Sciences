# Cishan-Beifudi Culture

## Definition and Construct History

[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]] jointly label the early
Neolithic material found on river terraces east of the Taihang Mountains as the
"Cishan and Beifudi cultures," an etic archaeological construct spanning several dozen
sites, ca. 6500–5000 BCE. The two type sites are Cishan (Wu'an, Hebei) and Beifudi
(Yixian, Hebei). `construct_status: robust` regionally, though the joint "Cishan-
Beifudi" label reflects the authors' own combined treatment of two related but
distinct site assemblages rather than a single, uniformly named tradition elsewhere
in the literature; this naming ambiguity is noted here rather than resolved.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** At Cishan, excavation of 2,579 m² revealed a
settlement containing 474 pits, two house foundations, and roughly 2,000 artifacts.
The ceramic assemblage was dominated by the flat-bottomed *yu* pot and the *zhijia*
support. Grinding stones (*mopan* slabs and *mobang* handstones) made up 12.5 percent
of 880 stone tools, while only six sickles (0.6 percent) were recovered. Only pig and
dog were confidently identified as domesticated among 23 identified animal species.
Forty-five clusters of grinding-stone/cooking-vessel tool assemblages were found in
open areas, interpreted via ethnographic analogy to Australian Aboriginal grinding
practices (Pedley 1992) as collective food-processing loci. At Beifudi, Phase I dates
to 6000–5000 BCE; Section I (residential) had 14 subterranean houses (6.5–15 m²) and
34 pits, while Section II was a 90 m² open plaza with artifact caches; no animal
bones or seeds were recovered there, but abundant wild walnuts and acorns were
present, and ceramic face masks were found.

The traditional interpretation of Cishan as a site of intensified millet production
rested on a reported ~80 storage pits with thick millet deposits (Tong 1984; ~50,000
kg reported by Sun et al. 1981), but Liu & Chen (2012) contest this: no carbonized
millet was actually preserved (the claim rests on phytolith and starch analysis), some
pits held pig and dog skeletons beneath the reported "millet layers" inconsistent with
grain storage, and the extremely low sickle count makes the claim for intensive millet
production questionable. Phytolith analysis of Cishan soil samples (Lu, H. et al.
2009) suggested even earlier dates for millet domestication (broomcorn millet at
10,300 cal BP, foxtail millet after 8,700 cal BP) than previously thought — a live
methodological dispute: Liu & Chen report this result "has been questioned" by Chinese
archaeologists for its early dates, unsystematic sampling (soil from pits without
controlled excavation), and AMS dates not derived from carbonized seeds. An early
claim of chicken remains at Cishan (ca. 6000 BCE) is likewise noted by Liu & Chen as
"not accepted by many archaeologists."

## Distribution and Chronology

Several dozen sites are known on river terraces near the mountain ranges on the
eastern piedmont of the Taihang Mountains, in the modern province of Hebei. The
culture falls within the broader north China early-Neolithic monsoon-favorable window
(monsoon maximum in eastern China ca. 9000 cal BP, as of An, Z. et al. 2000), during
which [[xinglongwa-culture]], [[houli-culture]], and [[peiligang-culture]] also
flourished.

## Lifeways

Liu & Chen (2012) conclude that Cishan was likely a year-round sedentary village
practicing broad-spectrum subsistence — hunting, fishing, nut collecting, animal
husbandry, and some millet cultivation — while Beifudi functioned as a seasonal
nut-collecting camp visited by part of the same or a related population, applying
Schiffer's (1976) refuse-deposition model (primary, secondary, and de facto refuse)
to the settlement-system interpretation. High nut remains (wild walnuts, hazelnuts,
hackberry) at Cishan, combined with the low sickle count, support a nut-based
broad-spectrum economy over the older millet-intensification narrative.

## Relations and Successions

Cishan-Beifudi is `analogous_to` [[xinglongwa-culture]], [[houli-culture]], and
[[peiligang-culture]] as early-Neolithic north China cultures sharing a broad-spectrum
subsistence pattern with only minor food production, per Liu & Chen's cross-regional
synthesis. Precise predecessor and successor cultures are not specified in the
material reviewed; both fields are marked [[unknown]] pending further ingest.

## Identity Cautions

Cishan-Beifudi is an etic material-culture construct built from ceramic and lithic
assemblages and settlement remains. Liu & Chen do not equate it with a language, an
ethnic group, or a genetic population. The joint "Cishan-Beifudi" label itself should
not be read as asserting the two site clusters represent a single self-conscious
community — the source's own settlement-system model (sedentary village vs. seasonal
camp) treats them as functionally distinct occupations of a shared regional tradition.

## Historiography

The Cishan millet-storage narrative is a long-standing and still-contested claim in
Chinese Neolithic archaeology; Liu & Chen (2012) explicitly revise the older reading
toward a nut-based broad-spectrum economy, while flagging the 2009 phytolith
redating of millet domestication as an unresolved methodological dispute. See
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source discussion.
