## Definition and Construct History

Indo-European is a language family comprising most languages of Europe, Iran, and the Indian subcontinent, first recognized as a coherent family on structural-comparative grounds. Reich (2018) records the field's founding observation: in 1786, William Jones, a judge in British Kolkata, noted the structural affinity among Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin and concluded that "no philologer could examine them all three, without believing them to have sprung from some common source" — the founding observation of comparative Indo-European linguistics ([[reich-who-we-are-2018]]).

Anthony (2007) adds Jones’s later inclusion of Persian, Celtic, and German; stresses that IE languages uniquely share a constellation of noun declension (cases × three genders), verb conjugations, stem alterations, and endings proving genetic descent from one language; and rejects convergence-only explanations (Trubetzkoy-inspired): modern convergence studies accept common descent, and creole-typical features are absent from PIE and its daughters. IE vocabularies and grammars **replaced** competing languages rather than creolizing with them (superficial borrowing ≠ creolization) (Anthony 2007).

`construct_status: robust` applies to the *linguistic family itself* — its internal relationships are established by conventional historical-linguistic method (cognate sets, sound correspondences) independent of any archaeological or genetic argument. What remains genuinely contested is the *homeland and dispersal mechanism* — see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]].

## Linguistic / Genetic / Material Signature

**Linguistic:** The family's internal structure (its branches, including the early-diverging Anatolian branch containing Hittite) is established by comparative method independent of archaeology or genetics. Anthony reconstructs ~1,500+ unique PIE roots (total terms much greater) and a five-term **wagon semantic field** (*kwékwlos ‘wheel’; *rot-eh₂-; *aks-/*h₂eks- ‘axle’; *h₂ih₃s- ‘thill’; *wégheti ‘convey in a vehicle’) plus wool/*HwlHn- and a rich pastoral/kinship lexicon (Anthony 2007).

David Anthony's argument, as reported by Reich and now read in primary form (2007), rests on shared Indo-European vocabulary for wagon technology — words for axle, harness pole, and wheels — present across most branches but absent (as a full package) from the earliest-diverging Anatolian branch for the later o-stem wagon set. Anthony reads this as implying that most Indo-European languages descend from a population using wagon technology no earlier than roughly 6,000 years ago / after ~4000–3500 BCE, which would rule out the much earlier (9,000–8,000-years-ago) Anatolian farming expansion as the source for those branches (Anthony 2007; per Reich 2018).

**Genetic:** Reich states plainly that "DNA cannot of course reveal what languages people spoke. But what genetics can do is to establish that migrations occurred" (as of Reich 2018) — genetics is circumstantial evidence for a dispersal *mechanism* (population movement), never direct evidence of language. The steppe migration into Europe after roughly 5,000 years ago (see [[yamnaya-culture]], [[corded-ware-culture]]) and a parallel steppe-related migration into South Asia, contributing to [[ancestral-north-indians]] ancestry, are the two population movements Reich treats as plausible vectors for Indo-European dispersal into Europe and South Asia respectively (as of Reich 2018).

**Material:** No independent material signature is claimed for the language family as such; material-culture correlates (Yamnaya, Corded Ware, Bell Beaker) are `associated_with` the proposed dispersal, never identified with the language family. Anthony’s four-way non-identity is explicit: material culture often has little relationship to language; persistent frontiers are a proposed exception (Anthony 2007).

## Distribution and Chronology

Modern distribution spans most of Europe, Iran, and South Asia (~three billion speakers as Anthony notes for the modern period); the disputed homeland and initial dispersal date are the subject of [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]].

### Anthony 2007 — phased PIE chronology (attributed)

Reconstructed PIE, though fragmentary and dictionary-like, refers to a real spoken language; its grammar looks homogeneous enough that PIE as a single language probably lasted less than a thousand years of change (Anthony assigns a nominal ~2,000-year lifetime for archaeological targeting, likely too long). Terminal date should lie near the birth of the oldest daughters; by ~**2500 BCE** reconstructed PIE was a **dead language** — later IE languages developed from intermediate dialects, not from PIE itself (Anthony 2007).

| Stage (Anthony 2007) | Approx. dates | Character / daughters |
|---|---|---|
| **Archaic PIE** | before ~4000 BCE | Partly preserved only in **Anatolian**; laryngeal-rich phonology; Pre-Anatolian separation ~4500–3500 BCE (Melchert & Lehrman ~4000 BCE); may predate wagons |
| **Early PIE** | ~4000–3500 BCE | Partly preserved in **Tocharian**; Don–Volga–Ural region in Anthony’s steppe model |
| **Late PIE** | ~3500–3000 BCE (heart 4000–3000; ends by 2500) | Full **wagon/wheel vocabulary**, o-stems; expands with [[yamnaya-culture]] from ~3300 BCE; source of Italic/Celtic and remaining core |
| **Terminal / dialect scatter** | by ~2500 BCE | Pre-Greek, Pre-Indo-Iranian, etc.; PIE no longer a unity |

**Birth/death window:** *terminus post quem* ~4000–3500 BCE (wool and wheels); *terminus ante quem* ~2500 BCE (historical early IE languages and late-branch chronologies); maximum archaic-to-late window ~**4500–2500 BCE** (Anthony 2007). Heart of the period probably 4000–3000 BCE (Anthony 2007).

### Anatolian split and early written record (Anthony 2007)

- Oldest written IE languages: **Anatolian** branch (Hittite, Luwian, Palaic).
- Hittite/Luwian names in Assyrian Kanesh *karum* records ~1920–1750 BCE; Hittite speakers in Anatolia by ~1900 BCE; empire ~1650–1600 BCE; Hattušas burned ~1180 BCE — Hittite then quickly disappeared (apparently only the ruling élite spoke it); Luwian continued more widely.
- Anatolian lacks later IE innovations: only two verb tenses (present/past) vs up to six elsewhere; animate/neuter without feminine; no dual; Lehrman identified ~ten traits as post-Anatolian-split innovations in the rest of PIE.
- Laryngeals (*h₁, *h₂, *h₃) preserved clearly only in Anatolian — confirming Saussure and dating Pre-Anatolian split when laryngeal-rich phonology still characterized archaic PIE.
- Anthony prefers “**archaic Proto-Indo-European**” over Sturtevant’s “Indo-Hittite” label for the Anatolian source stage.
- Early Hittite and Palaic speakers as **intruders** among non-IE Hattic speakers who already had cities, literacy, and kingdoms (heavy Hattic loans for throne, lord, king, queen, priests).

Archaeologically, Anthony links Pre-Anatolian detachment to westward steppe movements into Old Europe ~**4200–3900 BCE** (Suvorovo-type), **before** wheeled vehicles in the steppes — consistent with Anatolian’s incomplete share of the later wagon package (Anthony 2007).

### Branch order Anthony proposes (attributed)

Drawing on Ringe–Warnow-type cladistics and archaeological migration sequence:

1. **Pre-Anatolian** — oldest split (before ~4000 / ~4200–3900 BCE west).
2. **Pre-Tocharian** — early eastward detachment (Repin→[[afanasievo-culture]] ~3700–3300 BCE).
3. **Pre-Italic / Pre-Celtic** — leave before satəm and ruki innovations; western Yamnaya Danube/Carpathian ~3100–3000 BCE as incubation (Hungarian Yamnaya; Bell Beaker Csepel as bridge for Celtic path — Anthony’s proposal).
4. **Pre-Germanic** — uncertain cladistically; Anthony’s guess: Usatovo → late TRB (Dniester–Vistula) → early [[corded-ware-culture]] medium.
5. **Pre-Greek** — late daughter; Mycenaean Greek clear by 1450 BCE; Shaft Graves ~1650 BCE; Pre-Greek separation minimally ~2400–2200 BCE; shared package with Indo-Iranian (augment, mediopassive -i, bow vocabulary, deity names, dual meters) implying neighboring late dialects; Greek left before satəm/ruki. Hardest major post-Anatolian branch to derive directly from steppes (Catacomb eastern-border suggestion without proven migration into Greece).
6. **Pre-Indo-Iranian** — late; Old Indic distinct by ~1500 BCE (Mitanni Indic names/gods ~1500–1350 BCE); Common Indo-Iranian ≥1700 BCE; Proto-Indo-Iranian ≤2000 BCE; Pre-Indo-Iranian as eastern late PIE dialect ~2500–2300 BCE; archaeological path late Yamnaya/Poltavka → [[sintashta-culture]] ~2200–2000 BCE.
7. **Baltic / Slavic** — satəm/ruki group with Indo-Iranian; Anthony associates middle Dnieper dialects and Corded Ware northern medium.

**satəm** group (Indo-Iranian, Slavic, Baltic, Albanian, Armenian, perhaps Phrygian): *k- before front vowel → š/s; *kw- → k-; **ruki**-rule shared by Indo-Iranian, Baltic, Slavic. Italic, Celtic, and Tocharian left before these innovations (Anthony 2007).

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Not applicable as a population — this is a language family. Reconstructed late PIE vocabulary (Anthony 2007) points to speakers who were farmers and stockbreeders, with patrilineal/patrilocal kinship terms (*dyew pter Sky Father; *weik-potis chiefs), guest-host and patron-client institutions, cattle-centered ritual (despite sheep wealth, explained by milk yield), and wagon-based mobility among Yamnaya-associated groups. Lifeways in detail belong to the specific archaeological cultures ([[yamnaya-culture]], [[corded-ware-culture]], [[bell-beaker-culture]]) proposed as carriers. Archaeology shows more variable gender in actual kurgan practice than the homogeneous patri-centered kinship lexicon alone would imply (Anthony 2007).

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` [[yamnaya-culture]] and, via it, [[corded-ware-culture]]: Reich (2018) concludes that the genetic evidence "tips the scales" toward some version of the steppe hypothesis for Indo-European dispersal into Europe, while noting the original homeland question remains unresolved. Anthony (2007) supplies the archaeological-linguistic package linking late PIE to the Yamnaya horizon and sequenced out-migrations. Reich additionally offers his own speculative inference (explicitly marked as his own, "suggests to me," not an established finding) that the original Indo-European-speaking population may have lived south of the Caucasus, in the Iran/Armenia region, later splitting into a steppe-bound branch (mixing with steppe hunter-gatherers to form the Yamnaya) and an Anatolia-bound branch (ancestral to Hittite speakers) (as of Reich 2018). This speculative homeland proposal is recorded here as attributed and should not be promoted to wiki voice; it is in tension with Anthony’s north-of-Caucasus placement.

`associated_with` [[ancestral-north-indians]] for the Indo-Aryan branch: the 2016 Lazaridis/Patterson finding that ANI ancestry is roughly 50% steppe-related is treated by Reich as a genetic parallel supporting steppe-linked spread of Indo-European speech into South Asia as well as Europe (as of Reich 2018).

## Identity Cautions

A language family is not a population, a genetic lineage, or an archaeological culture. The Indo-European family's internal structure is secure historical linguistics; the claim that any specific ancient population (Yamnaya, Corded Ware) *spoke* an Indo-European language is an attributed inference from migration timing and comparative vocabulary, not a direct reading of any surviving text from that population. "Indo-European ancestry" is not a coherent phrase under this wiki's four-way non-identity rule — ancestry belongs to genetic populations, not to languages; use `associated_with` between a proposed archaeological/genetic carrier population and the language family, never an identity link. Anthony’s “Aryan” is properly an Indo-Iranian ritual-linguistic label, not a racial category (Anthony 2007).

## Historiography

Two competing dispersal hypotheses are recorded in full on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]: Colin Renfrew's Anatolian-farming-dispersal hypothesis (1987) and the steppe/Kurgan hypothesis associated with Marija Gimbutas, J. P. Mallory, and David Anthony. Primary Anthony (2007) is now ingested: wool/wheel termini, phased archaic/early/late PIE, Pontic-Caspian homeland, franchising expansion model. Reich (2018) reports that the Anatolian hypothesis "has lost its best evidence" given post-2015 ancient-DNA documentation of a large, demographically real steppe migration after roughly 5,000 years ago — a migration whose sheer scale Renfrew's model had treated as implausible. Reich further connects 19th–20th-century "Aryan purity" ideology, which fueled nationalist and Nazi racist claims, to a misreading of Indo-European origins as evidence of a pure ancestral people; he argues the genetic findings undermine this ideology directly, since the Yamnaya themselves were formed by mixture, as were the Corded Ware and Bell Beaker populations that followed (as of Reich 2018). See [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] for the recorded political misuse across Nazi and Hindutva variants, framed as positions rather than adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Renfrew 1987 primary layer

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]*).* Renfrew accepts the family as real (not pure Trubetskoy convergence) and re-dates primary European dispersal to the **Neolithic farming expansion** from Anatolia via first Greek farmers ([[anatolian-hypothesis]]). He surveys decipherments and branches (Hittite/Anatolian, Linear B Greek, Tocharian *centum* in the east, Mitanni/Kikkuli horse treatise, non-IE Basque/Etruscan/Iberian) as materials for a processual account of replacement. Celtic languages are treated as long local continuity after early western farming IE, not Hallstatt/La Tène folk arrivals. Indo-Iranian is left dual (early farming Hypothesis A vs later mounted élite Hypothesis B). **Four-way non-identity** is his own methodological rule as much as the wiki's: IE names a language family, not a race or a pottery package. See [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] Position B for the full case and later aDNA assessments.

## Cavalli-Sforza synthesis (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000)

*Genes, Peoples and Languages* ch. 5 reviews the IE homeland as highly variable in the literature (Germany to Caucasus, Baltic to Suez). Two poles:

1. **Gimbutas Kurgan** — origin north of the Black Sea; horse, chariot, warrior kurgans; dates Cavalli-Sforza treats as roughly 3,000–3,500 BCE (vindicated, he says, by later excavations). Linked by him to European **PC3** gene geography.
2. **Renfrew farming / Anatolian** — IE spread with Neolithic farmers; demic agriculture as language vehicle; chronology ~9,500–10,000 ya vs linguistic estimates nearer ~6,000 ya / Gimbutas 5–5.5 kya tension noted.

Cavalli-Sforza (with Piazza) proposes a **dual-wave reconciliation**: primary Anatolian/farming layer and secondary Kurgan/proto-IE pastoral layer — languages developing on the steppe from an earlier farmer-related stock then spreading with pastoralists. He also discusses quantitative IE trees from Dyen–Kruskal–Black lexicostatistical data and critiques late-branching Celtic results from Warnow et al. **Four-way non-identity:** all homeland models are linguistic-archaeological-genetic *associations*, not equations of pots, genes, and speakers. See [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]], [[demic-diffusion]], [[renfrew-colin]], [[cavalli-sforza-genes-peoples-languages-2000]].
