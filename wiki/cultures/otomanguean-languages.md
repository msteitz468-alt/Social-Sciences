# Otomanguean Languages

## Definition and Construct History
A large Mesoamerican language family including Zapotecan and Mixtecan. Its comparative reconstruction (Rensch, Longacre, Swadesh and others) underpins the "common origin" argument of [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983), where [[marcus-joyce|Joyce Marcus]] used the family tree and glottochronological divergence estimates to model when proto-Zapotec and proto-Mixtec separated ([[divergent-evolution]]).

## Linguistic Signature
Tonal languages with reconstructible proto-vocabulary; Marcus (1983) reported glottochronological estimates placing Zapotecan–Mixtecan separation in the range of the fourth millennium BCE — **flagged: glottochronology-based absolute dates are contested**; the relative branching sequence is far more secure than any absolute date.

## Distribution and Chronology
Southern and central Mexico, centered on Oaxaca, Puebla, and Guerrero.

## Lifeways
As a language family the construct carries no direct lifeways evidence; the reconstructed proto-Otomanguean vocabulary (Flannery & Marcus 1983 via 16th-century Zapotec and Mixtec dictionaries) suggests shared concepts — a "vital force," four-quarter cosmology, and bilateral kinship — but these are linguistic-paleontology inferences, not observed practices. Subsistence and social organization belong to the associated archaeological cultures, not the language family itself.

## Relations and Successions
Major branches include Zapotecan (Zapotec, Chatino), Mixtecan (Mixtec, Cuicatec, Trique), Otopamean, Popolocan, Chinantecan, and Chiapanec-Mangue. In the [[divergent-evolution]] argument the Zapotecan and Mixtecan branches `descends_from` a common proto-Otomanguean stem; their speakers are `associated_with` (not identical to) the [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec]] and [[mixtec-culture|Mixtec]] archaeological sequences.

## Identity Cautions
Language family ≠ archaeological culture ≠ people: the association of proto-Otomanguean speech with the Oaxaca preceramic and Formative record is an argued hypothesis of the Cloud People program (`associated_with` [[zapotec-civilization]], [[mixtec-culture]]), not an observed identity.

## Historiography
Documented here via Flannery & Marcus 1983; the family's specialist literature is not yet in the collection.
