# Homo naledi

A small-brained hominin known from the **Rising Star cave system**, South Africa,
described here from [[fagan-durrani-people-of-the-earth-2022|Fagan & Durrani (2022)]]
(attributed). Its interest lies in a **mosaic morphology** and a **surprisingly recent
date**: it lived alongside early *[[homo-sapiens|Homo sapiens]]* despite retaining
archaic features, complicating any tidy ladder of human evolution.

## Definition and Construct History

Discovered in 2013 (the Dinaledi Chamber) by a team led by Lee Berger, using slender
"underground astronaut" excavators to reach a near-inaccessible chamber; named in 2015
(Berger et al.) and dated in 2017 (Dirks et al.) to ~335,000–236,000 BP. A second
assemblage (the Lesedi Chamber) followed. The recovery — hundreds of bones from multiple
individuals in a deep, hard-to-reach chamber — and the team's rapid, open-access
publication strategy both drew controversy (attributed — Fagan & Durrani 2022).

## Anatomical Signature

A mosaic: small brain (~465–610 cc), curved fingers and other climbing adaptations, paired
with *Homo*-like hands, wrists, feet, and teeth. `construct_status: contested` because the
combination of primitive brain size with a late date is hard to place on existing
phylogenies. No ancient DNA has been recovered.

## Distribution and Chronology

Known **only** from the Rising Star system near Johannesburg, ~335,000–236,000 BP —
contemporaneous with early *H. sapiens* elsewhere in Africa (e.g. Jebel Irhoud). No wider
distribution is documented.

## Lifeways

Little can be said. No stone tools are securely associated with the remains, and the
assemblage is a death context, not a settlement, so subsistence and social life are
essentially undocumented. The much-discussed possibility that the bodies were **deliberately
placed** in the chamber (which, if true, would imply complex mortuary behaviour in a
small-brained hominin) is contested and not established.

## Relations and Successions

Phylogenetic position unresolved; not established as ancestor or descendant of any named
taxon. Best treated as a distinct late-surviving small-brained *Homo* lineage rather than
slotted into a sequence.

## Identity Cautions

The much-publicized claim of **deliberate body disposal / "burial"** in the chamber is
**contested**, not established — Fagan & Durrani record it as an interpretation, not a
finding. The single-locality record means "*Homo naledi*" picks out an assemblage, not a
known range or population history.

## Historiography

An instance of the broader lesson that human evolution is bushy, not linear (cf.
[[modern-human-origins-debate]]). Claims here rest on one excavation project's rapid
publications; treat the disposal interpretation and phylogenetic placement as open. Fagan
& Durrani's account is a textbook summary of primary reports (Berger et al. 2015; Dirks et
al. 2017).
