## Definition and Construct History

**Pukara** (Pucará) names the Early Intermediate Period political and cultural formation that brought a degree of political integration to the **north** end of Lake Titicaca, within the southern demographic pole of Andean civilization. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) places its flowering after earlier Yaya-Mama ideological integration and before southern Tiwanaku’s rise: “The flowering of Pukara brought a degree of political integration at the north end of the lake, but the site succumbed to drought during the first centuries AD.” The construct is archaeological (type site, ceramics, corporate art)—not a self-named people in contemporary texts.

Note: Spanish/Quechua *pukara* also means hillfort/redoubt across the Andes (EIP coastal bastions; LIP altiplano walled settlements). This page is the **Titicaca EIP culture/site complex**, not every fortified hill named pukara.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material:** Type-site centered political integration at the north lake; corporate style and monumental investment sufficient for Moseley to treat it as a peer precursor to Tiwanaku’s southern trajectory. Detailed architectural inventories are thin in the 2001 synthesis chapter used here—densify from specialist monographs when ingested.

**Linguistic / genetic:** [[unknown]]. Four-way non-identity: Pukara culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ continuous ethnic identity into LIP Aymara kingdoms.

## Distribution and Chronology

North Titicaca Basin (Peru/Bolivia borderlands). EIP chronology; ends under early centuries AD drought while contemporary southern settlement weathered dry times and matured into [[tiwanaku-culture]] (as of Moseley 2001). Sprawl around Titicaca as southern demographic pole frames both Pukara and Tiwanaku.

## Lifeways

High-altitude agropastoral basin adaptation (implied by regional context); political integration above pure ideological (Yaya-Mama) coordination. Specific crop/herd mixes and household economy require specialist sources beyond Moseley’s brief EIP close.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** Yaya-Mama ideological integration across the basin.
- **Succeeded by / contrasted with:** Southern Tiwanaku urbanization, ridged fields, and eventual empire; Pukara does not simply “become” Tiwanaku—different lake ends, drought differential outcomes (as of Moseley 2001).
- **Later:** LIP Aymara señoríos and Inca conquest of the lake region reorganize the same landscape under different constructs—no automatic ethnolinguistic continuity.

Moseley also notes major centers from Pukara onward turned hinterlands into agrarian parklands as a long-term highland pattern of state investment (mit’a/land reclamation theme)—a generalizing claim, attributed.

## Identity Cautions

Do not confuse **Pukara culture** with **pukara fortifications** elsewhere (Santa Valley EIP; LIP Pukara de Juli, etc.). Do not equate Pukara with Tiwanaku, Aymara, or Inca. Drought end is Moseley’s climatic contribution claim—record as attributed concurrent factor.

## Historiography

Brief treatment in Moseley Ch. 7 as setup for Tiwanaku; earlier Andean literature (Rowe, Kidder, and later Titicaca projects) defines the ceramic/architectural complex more fully—not yet ingested here. Source for this densification: [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001]].
