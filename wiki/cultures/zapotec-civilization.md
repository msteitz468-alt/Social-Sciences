# Zapotec Civilization

## Definition and Construct History

"Zapotec civilization" designates the archaeological sequence of the Valley of Oaxaca centered on [[sites/monte-alban|Monte Albán]] and its Preclassic precursor San José Mogote. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], San José Mogote remained the Valley's leading center through the end of the Middle Preclassic; Kent Flannery and Joyce Marcus recovered a bas-relief threshold stone there depicting a dead captive with a name-glyph ("1 Eye") in the 260-day calendar, treated as a possible precursor to the Danzantes carvings and among the oldest writing in Mesoamerica. Monte Albán was founded near the close of the Middle Preclassic, c. 500–450 BCE, replacing San José Mogote; Coe & Koontz characterize the founding as "a rapid, deliberate episode" (attributed). No evidence of major population displacement in central Oaxaca is recorded until c. AD 900, supporting continuity of Zapotec-speaking occupation across the whole sequence — an inference resting on the four-way non-identity caution below.

## Material, Linguistic, and Genetic Signature

- **Material**: fine gray-clay pottery with bridge-spouted vases and hollow tripod bowls (Monte Albán I, c. 500–150 BCE); the Danzantes corpus (~300 known bas-relief figures of contorted nude male figures with Olmecoid facial features, interpreted — attributed — as depictions of slain chiefs or captives, some showing sexual mutilation and one a severed head); Building J's conquest-record slabs (Monte Albán II, c. 150 BCE–AD 150); over 170 subterranean masonry tombs distributed across the site, the finest with corbeled vaults, ante-chambers, and murals (Tomb 104, Tomb 5 at Cerro de la Campana); urn figures representing a Classic-period ancestor cult.
- **Linguistic**: a logo-syllabic script of 60–80 non-calendrical glyphs, spanning Monte Albán I through the Epiclassic, remains **undeciphered**. Alfonso Caso (see [[thinkers/caso-alfonso]]) established the Zapotec Calendar Round (Year Bearer days falling in the 2nd, 7th, 12th, and 17th positions of the 20 named days), later refined by Javier Urcid. The Zapotecs never adopted the Maya Long Count, so inscribed Calendar-Round dates cannot be fixed to an absolute year without independent anchors; Urcid further cautions that most Zapotec monuments were reused or moved (disrupting original inscriptional context) and that many 260-day-count notations once read as chronological markers are actually calendrical personal names.
- **Genetic**: not addressed in this source; "[[unknown]]".

## Distribution and Chronology

Centered on the Valley of Oaxaca (>1,000 settlements at Monte Albán's Classic apogee, per Coe & Koontz), with an enclave community ("Oaxaca ward"/barrio) resident at [[sites/teotihuacan]] from early in that city's history, and reciprocal Zapotec cultural presence maintained there. Late Preclassic population estimates (Richard Blanton and Stephen Kowalewski) run 10,000–20,000 at Monte Albán; Classic-period population reached an estimated ~25,000 at apogee.

## Lifeways

By late Middle Preclassic the Valley's Zapotec-associated communities practiced irrigation, including canal-fed hillside terracing at Hierve el Agua. Classic Monte Albán supported an ancestor-veneration cult centered on urn figures representing deified ancestors, with named deities drawn from an "old dictionary" of Zapotec religious terms (Lightning God Cociyo, Maize God Pitao Cozobi, Feathered Serpent, Bat God, Old Fire God, and possibly a Water Goddess).

## Relations and Successions

Monte Albán II's Building J bears more than 40 inscribed conquest slabs — upside-down defeated-ruler heads paired with place-glyphs given phonetically/rebus-style, plus calendrical notation — correctly interpreted by Alfonso Caso as records of town conquests, confirmed in the Zapotec language. Astronomer Anthony Aveni and architect Horst Hartung identified an alignment of Building J with the star Capella. The Late Preclassic–Classic transition was peaceful; some Monte Albán II burial-cult elements (potstands, painted stucco) arrived from the Maya area but Maya influence ceased at the start of the Classic proper, `associated_with` a subsequent, and much stronger, [[sites/teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] influence on Classic Monte Albán frescoes and material culture — Coe & Koontz note it has "even been suggested that Teotihuacan directly controlled Monte Albán at some point during the Classic period, although the evidence for this is thin" (a weakly evidenced **[POSITION]**, not adjudicated). Post-Classic Mixtec groups reused old Zapotec tomb sites as consecrated ground; Zapotec polities persisted into the Post-Classic (e.g., Zaachila) alongside growing Mixtec cultural influence.

## Identity Cautions

"Zapotec civilization" is an archaeological-culture label defined primarily by shared material culture, an undeciphered script, and continuity of occupation in the Valley of Oaxaca. The equation of this construct with "Zapotecan-speaking peoples" is an attribution made explicitly by [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz]] on linguistic-historical grounds (the later attested Zapotec language of the Valley), not a claim independently verified by genetic or unbroken textual evidence for the deep Preclassic and Classic phases. No genetic population data are cited in this source; treat "Zapotec" here as `associated_with`, not identical to, any single self-conscious ethnic group across the full 500 BCE–AD 900 span.

## Historiography

Alfonso Caso's excavations (see [[thinkers/caso-alfonso]]) established the Monte Albán sequence (Danzantes, Building J, the Calendar Round) still used as the field's chronological backbone; Javier Urcid's epigraphic work has revised readings of Zapotec script and monument reuse. Kent Flannery and Joyce Marcus's work at San José Mogote extended the sequence into the Preclassic. See [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013]].
