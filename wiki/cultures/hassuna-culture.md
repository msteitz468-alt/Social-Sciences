# Hassuna Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Hassuna culture is named for its type site, Tell Hassuna, 35 km south of Mosul in northern Iraq, excavated 1943–44 by the Iraqi Directorate of Antiquities under Seton Lloyd and Fuad Safar. [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] places it at the start of his six-stage relative chronology of Mesopotamian proto-history, c. 5800–5500 BCE — a chronology he himself flags as "somewhat artificial," since culture areas shift between periods and "cultures long thought successive are in fact contemporaneous or at least overlapping." Roux does not specify whether these dates are calibrated; treat as uncalibrated conventional archaeological dates pending recalibration.

The Hassuna type-site sequence begins with a lowest level on virgin soil showing coarse pottery and stone tools attributed to a Neolithic farming community living in huts or tents (no building traces survive), overlain by six successive layers of increasingly large and well-built houses.

## Material Signature

Architecturally, later Hassuna houses had six to seven rooms arranged in two blocks around a courtyard (living quarters plus kitchen/storage), pressed-mud walls, clay-and-straw floors, large sunken unbaked-clay grain bins, and domed *tanur*-type bread ovens. Material culture included mortars, flint sickle blades, stone hoes, clay spindle whorls, and crude clay female figurines (seated, naked).

Pottery divides into two phases (per Roux): "archaic" ware (levels Ia–III — coarse undecorated jars, burnished bowls, sparse red-painted geometric bowls/jars) and "standard" ware (levels IV–VI — the same forms but with thicker matte brown paint and more extensive, skilled decoration, some incised). Roux reports that archaic Hassuna pottery shares traits with deep levels at Turkish (Sakçe Gözü, Mersin), Syrian (Karkemish, 'Amuq), and Palestinian (Megiddo, Jericho) sites, while the standard ware "seems to have developed locally," within a relatively small area of northern Iraq.

At Yarim Tepe mound 1 (near Tell 'Afar), 13 levels of Hassuna-affiliated occupation include square and round houses, flint and obsidian tools, copper ore and copper/lead ornaments, small clay figurines, and small perforated stone/clay discs. Some scholars interpret the latter as proto-stamp-seals (an ancestor of the cylinder seal); Roux notes other scholars regard them merely as amulets or ornaments — a minor, genuine scholarly disagreement, not adjudicated here.

Umm Dabaghiya (48 km south of Yarim Tepe; excavated by Diana Kirkbride, 1971–73) was a small trading-post settlement handling onager and gazelle hides from desert nomads, related to but possibly older than archaic Hassuna pottery. Distinctive features include clay-slab floors (a precursor to moulded brick), gypsum-plastered and red-painted walls with fresco fragments (an onager hunt, a spider, possibly flying vultures), carved alabaster bowls, and "applied decoration" pottery (figurines attached to vessels before firing). Roux notes Umm Dabaghiya's plastered floors and arrowheads point stylistically west toward Syria (Buqras, Ras Shamra, Byblos), while its frescoed red walls recall contemporary Çatalhöyük in Anatolia — flagged as cross-regional stylistic influence, not a migration or identity claim. Other Hassunan-affiliated sites named by Roux: Tell Sotto, Kül Tepe (near Yarim Tepe), and mound 2 at Tulul ath-Thalathat.

## Distribution and Chronology

Hassuna ware and its "standard" successor are attested at Tell Hassuna itself, at Nineveh's lowest levels, at Matarrah (south of Kirkuk), at Shimshara on the Lower Zab, and across the 13 levels at Yarim Tepe mound 1. Distribution is concentrated in a relatively small area of northern Iraq, though archaic-phase affinities extend the comparative horizon into Anatolia, Syria, and Palestine.

## Lifeways

Subsistence rested on farming (evidenced by grain bins, sickles, and hoes) supplemented by animal husbandry implied by the material assemblage. Burial practice was inconsistent by age class: children were buried in jars inside houses with small grave goods (cups, pots), while adult burial customs varied — bodies piled in room corners, deposited in clay bins "without ceremony" (Roux's phrasing), or placed in cist graves without goods.

## Relations and Successions

Roux frames the Samarra culture explicitly as "a derivative of the Hassuna culture," in contrast to the Halaf culture, which he treats as having no local Mesopotamian ancestor. Hassuna therefore anchors the beginning of Roux's proposed ceramic-sequence chain: Hassuna → [[samarra-culture]] → (via Choga Mami Transitional ware) → Eridu/Hajji Muhammad wares → [[ubaid-culture]].

## Identity Cautions

Roux reports skull morphology from Hassuna (as at Byblos and Jericho) as belonging to "the large-toothed variety of the long-headed Mediterranean race," and interprets this as suggesting "unity of population throughout the Fertile Crescent in late Neolithic times." This is a physical-anthropology/race-typology claim drawn from older literature (as of Roux 1992) and must not be read in wiki voice as an established genetic or ethnic finding: a shared cranial type does not by itself establish a shared culture, language, or genetic population. The four-way non-identity principle applies in full — "Hassuna culture" names a material-culture horizon (pottery, architecture, tool kit), not a self-conscious people, a language, or a genetic population, and none of those should be silently equated with it.

## Historiography

Known to the wiki via [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], writing from the state of archaeological knowledge as of the early 1990s and citing excavations from the 1940s (Lloyd and Safar at Tell Hassuna) through the 1970s (Kirkbride at Umm Dabaghiya). Excavation quality varies by site and era; later specialist reports and post-1992 reanalysis are outstanding for this page's densification.
