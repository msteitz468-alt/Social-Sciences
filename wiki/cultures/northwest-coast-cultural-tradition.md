# Northwest Coast Cultural Tradition

## Definition and Construct History

The **Northwest Coast cultural tradition** is the archaeology of the forested Pacific littoral
from the Copper River (Alaska) to the Klamath River (northern California), home at European
contact to some of **the most socially complex hunter-gatherer societies in the world** —
ranked, sedentary, wealthy fishing peoples (Tlingit, Haida, Nuu-chah-nulth/Nootka, Coast
Salish, Makah). As presented by [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], following
Carlson's Early/Middle/Late Period scheme, the tradition shows continuous cultural elaboration
from before 8000 BC, becoming recognizable by c. 3500 BC and reaching its ethnographically
documented florescence in the Late Period (from c. AD 500).

`construct_status: conventional` — an archaeological tradition spanning **many unrelated
language families**; it is not one people. Fagan repeatedly and explicitly reads the historic
ethnographies back onto the archaeological record (an ethnographic-analogy method he flags
himself), because few sites preserve the stratigraphic/organic data to document the emergence
of complexity directly. This makes it a
[[complex-hunter-gatherers|complex-hunter-gatherer]] case study par excellence.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** A simple polished-stone/shell/antler toolkit producing an
elaborate cedar material culture — **plank houses, dugout canoes, and monumental carved art**
(commemorative and heraldic poles). Salmon (five species), halibut, herring, and sea mammals
supported dense, relatively sedentary settlement, especially north of the Columbia. Status
markers — labrets, skull deformation, differential grave goods — appear from the Middle Period.
Wet sites ([[ozette|Ozette]], Hoko River) preserve otherwise-invisible basketry, cordage, and
wooden gear.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Early Period** (before 8000 BC – 3500 BC): several maritime hunter-gatherer traditions
  known mainly from stone tools (Namu, Queen Charlotte Islands maritime adaptation by c. 5000
  BC).
- **Middle Period** (from 3500 BC): greater homogeneity; obsidian trade; the appearance of the
  historic culture's diagnostic emphases on **status, wealth, and ceremonial**; planked houses
  "probably" in use; the **Marpole** culture (c. 400 BC – AD 400, Strait of Georgia) shows clear
  status differentiation in graves and cranial deformation.
- **Late Period** (from c. AD 500): economic specialization, high population density, and the
  sophisticated social/political organization documented ethnographically — including the
  [[potlatch]].

## Lifeways

Fagan frames the **emergence of complexity** as the tradition's central problem, and records a
genuine scholarly disagreement (attributed positions):

- **Fladmark / Ames:** efficient exploitation of salmon runs *required* centralized authority —
  prominent individuals managing territories, coordinating extended-family labor, and
  redistributing food.
- **Croes & Hackenberger (1988):** intensified fishing did *not* itself require centralized
  authority; rather, resource depletion (overexploited shellfish) plus rapid growth of extended
  families led to **ownership of well-defined territories**, and differential access to fisheries
  produced wealth and rank — with social ranking and ritualized redistribution (potlatches) as
  *adaptive responses* to food-supply fluctuation, not prerequisites.

Many Late Period societies can be classed as **chiefdoms** (powerful clan leaders) or,
alternatively, "Big Man" polities (Johnson & Earle) — chiefly authority resting on attracting
and retaining followers through control and redistribution of resources.

## Relations and Successions

- **Descends to:** the historic Northwest Coast peoples (Tlingit, Haida, Nuu-chah-nulth, Coast
  Salish, Makah) — Marpole, for instance, is held by most authorities to have developed into
  Coast Salish culture.
- **Analogous to:** the [[chumash|Chumash]] of the Santa Barbara Channel and the affluent
  foragers of the Eastern Woodlands — all read by Fagan as reaching "about the limit of social
  complexity possible without agriculture" in unusually rich environments.

## Identity Cautions

- **Many peoples, many languages.** The tradition is not one ethnos; enforce four-way
  non-identity.
- **Ethnographic analogy, flagged.** The Late Period picture leans heavily on 18th–19th-century
  ethnography read back onto the archaeological record — a hypothesis-generator, not direct
  evidence, as Fagan himself acknowledges.

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]]; the "origins of complexity" debate
(management-first vs. ownership-first) is the live methodological issue, tested best at the few
stratified, organically preserved sites ([[ozette|Ozette]], Hoko River). Densify from later
Northwest Coast archaeology and descendant-community collaborative work when ingested.

**Boas's founding characterization (1909, via [[boas-kwakiutl-ethnography-1966|Codere ed. 1966]], attributed).** Boas described the North Pacific Coast tribes (Juan de Fuca Strait to Yakutat Bay) as linguistically and physically diverse yet culturally "one of the best defined cultural groups of our continent," explaining both isolation and internal interchange by fjord-and-pass geography, and listing seven language families north to south (Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian, Wakashan, Salishan, Chemakum, Chinook), with a tentative genetic grouping of Tlingit–Haida and Kwakiutl–Salishan–Chemakum. His comparative claims — [[numaym]]-like village units underlying Coast Salish and (overlaid by totemic divisions) northern organization; an 18th-century southward spread of the winter-ceremonial complex to Tsimshian/Haida neighbors; the c. 1860 adoption of the northern carving style by the Kwakiutl — are early evidence-based regional-tradition arguments, oral-tradition-dated and attributed. He also flagged the unit-assumption himself: the coast is "generally treated as though it was a unit" (1935), a caution this page inherits.
