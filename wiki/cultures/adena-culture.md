# Adena Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Adena** is the conventional archaeological construct for **Early Woodland burial-mound** societies of the Ohio Valley and neighboring Eastern Woodlands. Named from an Ohio-region type context; an **etic** material-culture and mortuary label, not an ethnonym. Account densified from [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner, *The Moundbuilders* (2021)]] Ch. 4.

Milner places Early Woodland societies as appearing by the opening centuries of the **first millennium BCE** and lasting roughly **500 or more years**, with Middle Woodland following in the Midwest and Southeast for about the next half-millennium (ending ~400 CE or a bit later in those regions). Local timing varies by centuries.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Milner 2021):**

- **Burial mounds** as the signature architecture: earthen piles covering graves, often with log tombs or prepared burial facilities; some mounds built by stacking successive cemetery episodes (Milner box: **Robbins**, Kentucky — stacked cemeteries).
- Part of a first-millennium BCE Eastern Woodlands package of elaborate burial mounds, special treatment of key persons, and early earthworks — continuous with, but not identical to, later [[hopewell-culture|Hopewell]] display.
- Residential archaeology thinner than mortuary: settlements generally small and dispersed relative to later Mississippian towns.
- Exchange of valued materials present but less spectacular than the Hopewell peak (densify per specific artifact claims in source).

**Linguistic / genetic:** Not definitional. Do not equate Adena with a single people or language.

## Distribution and Chronology

Ohio Valley core with related Early Woodland mound traditions across adjacent Eastern Woodlands. Period boundaries are conventional and locally variable (Milner chronological caveat).

## Lifeways

- Mixed **foraging and native-plant gardening** within the [[eastern-agricultural-complex|Eastern Agricultural Complex]] pathway — not maize-staple agriculture.
- Mortuary ceremonialism and mound construction as major social investments.
- Social ranking, where visible, is primarily **mortuary/situational** rather than everyday residential elite architecture (Milner’s broader Early–Middle Woodland reading: selective status in death without clear hereditary-chiefdom organization of daily life).

## Relations and Successions

- Preceded by Late Archaic sedentary foragers and early mound traditions ([[watson-brake]], [[poverty-point]]).
- Textbook pairing with [[hopewell-culture|Hopewell]] Middle Woodland ceremonialism; Hopewell elaborates exchange and geometric earthworks.
- Long-run successor complexity after Late Woodland: [[mississippian-culture]] platform-mound [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]].

## Identity Cautions

Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people. “Adena” is a modern label for patterned material and mortuary practices. Mound-building does not by itself prove state or Servicean chiefdom organization (Milner 2021 on the nature of mound-building societies).

## Historiography

Nineteenth-century mound exploration; twentieth-century culture-historical naming; modern radiocarbon recalibration revises boundaries. Milner 2021 synthesizes for a general archaeological audience with Midwest fieldwork emphasis.
