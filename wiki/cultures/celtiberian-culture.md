# Celtiberian Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Celtiberian** names Iron Age communities of interior Iberia — homeland focused on the north-eastern peninsula from the southern Ebro flank to the Eastern Meseta — combining **Celtic linguistic affiliation** with Iberian and Mediterranean contacts and a material culture that **differs markedly from [[la-tene-culture|La Tène]] type**. Cunliffe (1997) ch. 7 is the wiki’s primary synthesis of classical notices and archaeology.

Cunliffe frames Iberian Celticity as bringing general [[celticity-debate|Celticity]] issues into focus: classical writers refer to Celtiberians; Celtic was spoken in some areas; social systems resembled historical Celts of temperate Europe; yet material culture is not La Tène — therefore **La Tène material culture must be dissociated from the concept “Celtic”** (Cunliffe 1997). “Celtiberian” is itself a classical compound label (Diodorus/Poseidonius: fusion of Celts and Iberes after long bloody wars); Martial (born Bilbilis AD 40) proclaimed pride in descent “from Celts and Iberians.”

**Construct history of “arrival” models (attributed Cunliffe):**
- Nineteenth-century megalith links; 1920s–30s Urnfields-as-Celtic; two-wave (~1000 + sixth century) or single eighth-century invasion models (most influentially Bosch-Gimpera, “Two Celtic Waves in Spain,” 1939).
- Undermined when Celtiberian zone and Iberian Urnfields shown exclusive and Urnfield-area language non-Indo-European.
- Unconvincing La Tène Celtic-incursion models for explaining the culture.
- **Current Spanish work** (as of Cunliffe 1997): rejects monocausal invasionism for indigenous elite assimilation of selected cultural elements.

**Spanish current model Cunliffe presents:** Atlantic Bronze Age “proto-Celtic” substratum already absorbing linguistic/ideological traits common to historical Celts (initial-P names related to Lusitanian; circular houses; excarnation; natural-feature sanctuaries); in Iberian Mountains/Eastern Meseta this develops into historical Celtiberian culture by absorbing Mediterranean-fringe influences, then spreads N and W; extreme NW **Castro culture** remains largely “untainted” proto-Celtic. Classic Celtiberian as proto-Celtic + Mediterranean overlay **parallels** the process behind [[la-tene-culture|La Tène]] emergence north of the Alps — analogy of process, not identity of peoples.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

### Linguistic (separate evidence class)

- **Celtiberian** is one of three or four Continental Celtic languages (with Gaulish, Lepontic, poorly known Middle Danube Celtic) — known from place/personal names, coins, and inscriptions; earliest Celtiberian inscriptions third century BCE, but Greek writers place Celts in Iberia at least two centuries earlier (Cunliffe 1997).
- **Toponymy:** SE non-IE place-names ending *-il(t)i / -il(t)u* ≈ Iberian cultural region; much of the rest *-briga* names ≈ Celtic-speaking area; sub-zones include Lusitania/Gallaecia archaic Celtic forms; differences east/west of an Oviedo–Mérida line; all broadly Celtic IE family.
- **Q-Celtic character** of most surviving Iberian Celtic often taken to imply early penetration; some P-Celtic *-dunum* names mainly in Catalonia taken by some as second Pyrenean influx — Cunliffe: oversimplifications; language shows Celtic spoken widely but **gives no convincing chronology or population-movement model**. Neolithic food-producer spread, successive prehistoric waves, and limited NE elite-language spread all proposed — “we do not know.” By fourth century BCE a Celtiberian spoke a language with much in common with western/central European Celtic (e.g. Botorrita metal plaques; decipherment announced 1971).
- **Atlantic lingua-franca hypothesis** (Cunliffe): if Celtic was the lingua franca of Atlantic seaways, inland Celtiberians (and Lusitanians) may have developed their language from western Iberian coasts — consistent with Herodotus placing Celts as most westerly Europeans next to the Cynetes — without requiring the 400–200 BCE [[celtic-migrations|migration]] package.

### Material

**Homeland package (Cunliffe 1997):** harsh Meseta pastoralism with transhumance; elite rich graves with short swords, spears, round shields; principal rite urned cremation (Urnfield-derived); Tartessian-derived painted pottery; two-part spring fibulae; belt hooks; short antennae-hilted iron sword as northern development spanning Celtiberia, Ebro, Languedoc, Aquitania.

**Mediterranean/Iberian stimuli from sixth century:** by fourth century rotary querns and potter’s wheel; Celtiberian script (from Iberian) by third century; large **oppida-like settlements** about the same time or little later (Iberian inspiration). Distinctive Celtiberian silver Meseta and gold NW **torcs** and horse-and-rider fibulae.

**La Tène contacts:** scatter of La Tène artefacts (e.g. third-century weapons burial at Quintana de Gormaz with dragon-pair scabbard) shows northern contacts via **gift exchange without deep cultural takeover**. Possible limited La Tène Celtic movements as raiders/settlers/mercenaries (war bands with Cimbri 104 BCE; Caesar notes 6,000 Gauls including cavalry and Ruthenian archers to Lérida 49 BCE) — contribution to Celtiberian culture **minimal** (Cunliffe).

**Classical warrior kit stereotype (Diodorus et al. via Cunliffe):** mixed shield types (light Celtic-type and Greek round); two-edged swords shorter than the temperate Celtic great sword but of excellent iron; carnyx-like war trumpet — baked-clay wolf’s head mouth from Numantia as parallel to temperate Celtic carnyces.

### Genetic

Not treated in Cunliffe 1997. Do not equate Celtiberian culture, Celtiberian language, or classical ethnonym with a genetic population.

## Distribution and Chronology

### Iberian peninsula geography (setup)

Cunliffe sketches the peninsula as a plain tilted west; mountainous Mediterranean east/south vs lower Atlantic west; Pyrenees/Cantabria isolate from Europe; metal-rich “pyrite arc” Sierra Morena to Galicia/Cantabria. Three broad cultural zones relevant here:

1. **Mediterranean coastal Iberia** — Phoenician Gadir from eighth century; orientalizing; Greeks (Emporion etc.); **Iberian culture** eighth–mid-third century: nucleated Mediterranean-style cities, distinctive art, state-level complexity by fourth century, Iberian script, hoplite-like forces with *falcatas* sought as mercenaries; Barcid then Roman conquest (Cádiz 206 BCE). **Not** Celtiberian.
2. **Atlantic Iberia / NW Castro culture** — defended hilltop *castros*, decorated pottery, stone sculpture; among last to submit to Rome (first century BCE); indigenous development rooted in second millennium; later Meseta links; Decimus Brutus campaign 138–136 BCE catalyses urban traits in favoured settlements. Structural similarities to northern Atlantic (round houses, *chevaux de frise*) of uncertain significance. Castro ≠ automatically “Celtic people.”
3. **Interior Meseta / Celtiberian core** — indigenous Celtic-speaking groups developing Celtiberian culture.

### Expansion of Celtiberian culture

Mapped by *-briga* and *Seg-* place-names; grave evidence of westward extension to Vettones fifth century and to Estremadura/far west (surname *Celtius*); Pliny: Celtiberians to Baetica as *Celtici* (Peñaflor = Celti); *Celti praestamarici* to N Portugal, *Gallaeci* to Galicia, *Galli* to Ebro. **Ethnic rather than tribal designations** suggest **military elites taking over peripheral power structures** more than mass population movement (Cunliffe). Raids into advanced Iberian zones employed Celtic mercenaries (Osuna relief elongated La Tène-type shield; Liria pot).

### Classical chronological notices

- Herodotus (mid-fifth century BCE): Celts as most westerly Europeans next to Cynetes, beyond Pillars of Hercules.
- Eratosthenes (third century): greater Celtic prominence than two centuries earlier.
- From early second century BCE: Celtiberes/Celtiberi frequent in Roman conflicts.
- Second Punic War entry 218 BCE: Celtiberians heavily used as Carthaginian mercenaries and unreliably by Romans.
- **~70-year war of attrition:** Tiberius Gracchus claimed destruction of 300 Celtiberian hill towns in 179–178 BCE; after 154 revolt of Celtiberians and Lusitani, bitter fighting under Lusitanian leader Viriathus; final stage from 143 culminating **133 BCE destruction of Numantia** after long siege; survivors sold into slavery — effective end of organized Celtiberian resistance though full Roman control of Iberia took another century+. Fall of Numantia (with Pergamene bequest the same year) among events establishing Rome as world power (Cunliffe retrospect).

### Structural periodization (Retrospect)

Cunliffe’s 750–450 BCE model pairs two innovating Celtic-speaking cores: **West Hallstatt** (Rhine–Danube) and **Celtiberian Meseta** élite learning from SE Spanish Iberian culture under Greek/Etruscan/Phoenician influence — cores facilitated (not simply caused) by Mediterranean market access, largely isolated from each other; possible linguistic divergence with Celtiberian retaining earlier structure while West Hallstatt zone begins path toward Brythonic/P-Celtic (R5-044).

## Lifeways

*(Attributed classical ethnography via Cunliffe 1997, plus archaeological settlement.)*

- **Economy:** Meseta pastoralism with transhumance; agrarian mix; Vaccaei communal land reallotted yearly (classical notice).
- **Settlement:** defended hill towns / oppida-like nucleations; Los Cogotas (Ávila) — defences enclose ~14.5 ha, only upper enclosure intensively occupied; not the largest oppidum in the Amblés Valley.
- **Warrior ethos:** warlike yet hospitable (strangers under divine protection); support of chief in battle demanded — to survive him criminal; glorious death in battle; possible excarnation (souls of battle-dead eaten by vultures go to heaven) — classical colour, not ethnographic present.
- **Hospitality as social bond** (Celtic leitmotif): Romans allegedly abused hospitality rules to trick/slaughter Lusitanians and Celtiberians; Mithridates later massacred Galatian tetrarchs under similar abuse — Mediterranean realpolitik vs hospitality norms (Cunliffe).
- **Mercenary labour markets:** Carthaginian and Roman recruitment from Second Punic War onward.
- **Local colour in classical sources:** wine with honey; urine tooth-cleaning — ethnographic stereotype, not wiki-voice custom.

## Identity Cautions

1. **“Celtiberian” is a classical compound** — fusion narrative is Diodorus’s ethnography, not a modern ethnohistory verdict.
2. **Language ≠ material culture ≠ people ≠ genes.** Celtiberian speech does not imply La Tène material; La Tène dragon-pair scabbards do not make a site “migrant Celt.”
3. **Iberian (coastal) ≠ Celtiberian (interior).** Different scripts, settlement traditions, art, and classical ethnonyms.
4. **Castro culture ≠ Celtiberian core.** NW hillfort zone has its own indigenous trajectory; “Celtic” labels here are especially slippery.
5. **Elite-dominance expansion** of *Celtici/Gallaeci* labels is not mass folk migration of the Po Valley type.
6. **No genetic signature** in the present source; do not back-project modern DNA claims onto Cunliffe’s 1997 frame.
7. Martial’s self-ascription is classical ethnonym identity (Celt + Iberian), not archaeological culture equation.

## Historiography

- **Classical production:** Roman writers came to respect Iberian Celts as fearless warriors with awe-inspiring cavalry and charismatic leaders (Viriathus), bound by sacred hospitality; “Noble Savage” caricature nonetheless tracks deep social features — valour and hospitality plus language allowed classical authors to treat Celtiberians and temperate European Celts as **one people** (Cunliffe’s closing judgment on classical equation vs material non-identity).
- **Modern archaeology:** invasionist two-wave models (Bosch-Gimpera) → rejection when Urnfield/Celtiberian distributions shown exclusive → indigenous elite-assimilation models in Spanish research (as of 1997).
- **Celtic studies:** Iberia as the standing counter-example to equating “Celtic” with La Tène — load-bearing for [[celticity-debate]] and for Cunliffe’s multi-thread definition of [[celts]].
- **Evidence quality:** epigraphy (Botorrita) strong for language; hillfort excavation uneven; classical war narrative densest for second-century Roman wars; pre-Roman social structure heavily filtered through enemy ethnography.
- **Collection coverage:** [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]] only so far; specialist Iberian Iron Age monographs and primary Botorrita editions not yet ingested.
