# Atlantic Bronze Age

## Definition and Construct History

"Atlantic Bronze Age" is an etic archaeological label for the shared metalworking and exchange zone running the length of the Atlantic façade — from southern Iberia to northern Scotland — during the second and first millennia BCE. Cunliffe (2001) treats it as the terminal phase of what he calls the "first cycle" of Atlantic maritime interaction, a system he periodizes as running from the Mesolithic to c. 800 BCE and characterized by shared belief systems (megalithic collective tombs, stone alignments, astronomical knowledge), gift-exchange networks moving axes and gold, and — on his own reading, explicitly hedged as speculative — the eventual spread of a maritime lingua franca that by the first millennium BCE had become an early form of "Celtic," an Indo-European branch (Cunliffe 2001; his own words: "For convenience we can refer to the period up to about 800 BC as the first cycle of the Atlantic system"). The construct is defined almost entirely by artefact typology and deposition pattern (metalwork hoards, shipwreck cargoes, sword/axe/spearhead typologies) rather than by any single excavated settlement sequence, so no type-site is assigned; construct status here follows the wiki's `conventional` designation — the zone is a recognized analytical unit in the literature but its coherence as a single interacting "culture" (as opposed to a family of regionally overlapping exchange networks) is itself part of what Cunliffe is arguing for, not a settled fact.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material:** The construct rests on convergent metalwork typology and deposition practice across the whole Atlantic seaboard. Diagnostic material includes: socketed axes (Armorican hoards alone have yielded 315 hoards totalling roughly 38,000 axes, an estimated 5–6 tonnes of lead, 2 tonnes of tin, and 7–8 tonnes of copper in circulation); carp's-tongue swords (named for find-sites at Vénat, Huelva, and Monte Sa Idda in Sardinia, with local Iberian copying attested at Ronda and Peña Negra); Irish-type basal-looped spearheads reaching France and Huelva; Ballintober, Hemigkofen, and Gündlingen sword types marking successive phases of Britain/Ireland–Continental sword exchange (12th–7th centuries BCE); bronze cauldrons and flesh-hooks (originating in Ireland in the 10th century BCE, concentrated in Ireland, south-east Britain, Brittany, and north-west Iberia) read by Cunliffe (2001) as material markers of an elite communal feasting complex; and gold ornament types (lunulae, probably of Irish manufacture, found also in Cornwall and Brittany; Berzocana-type neck rings from south-west Iberia found in Armorica and Ireland, moving opposite Irish gold bar torcs and discs). Most of this metalwork was recovered from deliberate deposition — burial, or "watery" contexts (rivers, lakes, bogs, the sea) — rather than settlements or cemeteries; Cunliffe (2001) argues this ritual-selection bias does not seriously distort the broad distribution patterns used, together with radiocarbon dating, to reconstruct the exchange networks over time.

**Linguistic:** Cunliffe's own position — stated as an interpretive claim, not a wiki-voice fact — is that a maritime lingua franca developed across the Atlantic network and had, by the first millennium BCE, become an early form of "Celtic." This is his contribution to the live, contested Celticity debate over whether an "Atlantic Celtic" language spread by sea-lane contact rather than by inland Indo-European migration; see [[celticity-debate]]. The wiki does not assert any language affiliation for the Atlantic Bronze Age in its own voice.

**Genetic:** Not defined; *Facing the Ocean* (2001) is a pre-aDNA source and makes no population-genetic claims about the Atlantic Bronze Age zone.

## Distribution and Chronology

The zone runs the length of the Atlantic seaboard from southern Iberia (the Iberian pyrite belt, Sierra Morena) through Galicia and Asturias, Armorica (Brittany), south-west Britain (Cornwall, Dartmoor), Wales, and Ireland, to northern Scotland and the Northern Isles. Copper exploitation began earliest in south-west Iberia (local use from c. 3000 BCE, predating Beaker pottery); Irish extraction opened at Ross Island, Co. Kerry, c. 2400–2000 BCE (fire-setting, stone hammers, bowl-furnace smelting of arsenical ore, associated with Beaker pottery); Mount Gabriel (Co. Cork) operated c. 1700–1500 BCE (estimated 1.5–26.5 tonnes total copper output); roughly thirty Welsh mining sites are known, four radiocarbon-dated to the earlier second millennium BCE, including the deep tunnel/shaft workings at Great Orme's Head. Local bronze production reached even the most remote Atlantic communities: crucibles and moulds for casting swords, spearheads, and rings occur at Dun Aonghasa (Aran Islands, 1300–800 BCE) and at Jarlshof (Shetland, mould fragments for eight socketed axes, seven swords, an Irish-type pin, a gouge, and rods). Two Late Bronze Age shipwreck-debris scatters off southern Britain — 189 bronzes at Langdon Bay near Dover and 7 bronzes off Moor Sand, Devon — are both of northern-French origin, with some Langdon Bay pieces cut up for packing, indicating cross-Channel shipment of scrap bronze for recasting.

By the 10th century BCE the system's southern end was drawn into Mediterranean contact: the Huelva hoard (over 400 artefacts dredged from the River Odiel in 1923 — 78 swords, mostly carp's-tongue type; 22 short swords/daggers; 88 spearheads, some Irish-made; east Mediterranean-origin elbow fibulae; helmet fragments), radiocarbon-dated to the mid-10th century BCE, is generally read as a votive deposit rather than a shipwreck or grave assemblage, consistent with the wider Atlantic rite of depositing bronze in watery contexts; Cunliffe (2001) interprets the Huelva/Odiel estuary as a symbolically "liminal" gateway between the metal-rich hinterland and the sea. Probably-Cypriot "fibules coudées," articulated roasting spits, and bronze feasting vessels reached Iberia in this "pre-colonial" period, likely via early Phoenician exploration (see [[phoenician-expansion]]); the roasting spit was subsequently absorbed into Atlantic material culture across western Iberia, western France, and eastern Britain. Cunliffe (2001) frames the whole first-millennium-BCE system as three interacting cultural zones — Atlantic, Rhine-Danube, and Nordic — with Britain occupying the interface and mediating Irish exports (e.g., gold hair-rings reaching the Seine/Lower Rhine).

The system did not decline uniformly. By the 7th–6th centuries BCE Britain (and, less so, Ireland) had reoriented more toward northern Europe than the Atlantic circuit, with Britain possibly acting as middleman for Irish metal reaching the Continent; after c. 600 BCE Ireland entered several centuries of isolation from both British and Atlantic developments, and after the mid-5th century Britain too lost its European network connections, while Brittany retained links via the Garonne–Loire corridor. The mass deposition of tens of thousands of socketed axes in Armorica in the 7th century BCE is read by Cunliffe (2001) as possibly marking "the cataclysmic end of a long-established and deeply rooted system." He periodizes the whole span as his "first cycle" of the Atlantic system (Mesolithic–c. 800 BCE), succeeded by a "second cycle" (c. 800 BCE–c. 400 CE) dominated by Phoenician and then Roman Mediterranean demand — see [[phoenician-expansion]] and [[tartessos]] — and eventually a "third cycle" running from the post-Roman period to 1500.

## Lifeways

Metalworking, exchange, and votive deposition dominate the visible record. Elite display took markedly different regional forms: in Wessex and Armorica, elites used rich single burials for conspicuous consumption (see [[wessex-culture]]); in Ireland, by contrast, elites apparently did not use burial for status display at all, instead depositing very large quantities of luxury metalwork — axes, halberds, daggers, spears, gold lunulae, earrings, and discs — directly into hoards or watery contexts, which Cunliffe (2001) interprets as votive offering to deities rather than accompanying the dead ("the Irish elite may have chosen to display their status by making offerings of conspicuous wealth direct to the deities rather than indirectly, accompanying their dead"). Cross-regional elite gift exchange is attested archaeologically: gold-pin-inset dagger hilts at a dozen Breton sites and two Wessex sites (including Bush Barrow), and a comparable gold box from La Motta (Lannion) read as a British product found in Brittany — evidence, on Cunliffe's reading, of reciprocal elite exchange rather than one-way tribute. A shared feasting complex (cauldrons, flesh-hooks, roasting spits) is argued to have reaffirmed social hierarchy across Ireland, Britain, Brittany, and north-west Iberia, a practice Cunliffe (2001) connects — as analogy, not proof — to later classical descriptions of the Celtic feast and to early Irish vernacular literature. The function of the Armorican socketed-axe hoards is itself disputed: one position holds they served as a form of currency; an alternative, which Cunliffe (2001) finds "perhaps more plausible," holds they were made specifically for deposition, with increasing lead content marking a shift from functional to symbolic objects as part of an escalating pattern of religious propitiation.

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by`: [[bell-beaker-culture]] — the Bell Beaker exchange networks (Maritime Beaker pottery, callais beads, Palmela points) that linked the Tagus, Morbihan/Brittany, and the Golfe du Lions in the third millennium BCE fed directly into the metal-exchange infrastructure the Atlantic Bronze Age inherited and intensified.

`succeeded_by`: in north-west Iberia, [[castro-culture]] directly continues the Atlantic Bronze Age's maritime and metallurgical traditions — Cunliffe (2001) periodizes castro-culture's earliest phase (c. 1000–c. 500 BCE) explicitly as covering "the end of the period of widespread maritime contact" through emerging Phoenician/Punic trade. Elsewhere on the Continent the Hallstatt culture (no wiki page yet) represents the succeeding elite-exchange horizon that reoriented British and Irish metal networks northward and eastward in the 7th–5th centuries BCE.

`associated_with`: [[tartessos]] (the Guadalquivir-valley polity through which Atlantic bronze intersected Mediterranean silver demand) and [[phoenician-expansion]] (the Mediterranean commercial system that, per Cunliffe's own explicitly hedged and "difficult to test" hypothesis, may have driven the observed increase in Atlantic bronze production and circulation in its final centuries). The relation to [[world-systems-analysis]]/[[core-periphery]] is Cunliffe's own theoretical vocabulary: he describes the Mycenaean Aegean of the third–second millennia BCE as an "innovating and consuming core" to which the rest of Bronze Age Europe, including the Atlantic zone, "readjusted" as periphery, and holds that the Mycenaean collapse of the 12th century BCE sent shockwaves through the Atlantic exchange infrastructure before it re-formed.

## Identity Cautions

"Atlantic Bronze Age" names a shared metalwork-and-exchange horizon, not a single people, language, or polity. The construct says nothing about genetic population structure (pre-aDNA source) and its linguistic claim — that an "Atlantic Celtic" lingua franca emerged from this network — is Cunliffe's own contested thesis, argued jointly with linguist John Koch, and disputed by scholars who favor an inland/Continental Indo-European spread model; see [[celticity-debate]]. Do not read shared metalwork typology as evidence of a single self-conscious ethnic identity: Cunliffe (2001) himself stresses that the system was a patchwork of overlapping, mostly local/regional down-the-line exchange networks (illustrated by the single-looped palstave type concentrated in the Lower Douro valley) rather than one integrated society, with only a thin layer of prestige-goods exchange operating at the long-distance, cross-regional scale.

## Historiography

Cunliffe (2001) writes explicitly against an older culture-historical tendency to read Atlantic Bronze Age metalwork distributions as evidence of migration or invasion (he makes the same move rejecting the "Beaker Folk" migration model for the preceding period), instead framing the material as evidence of exchange, gift-giving, and votive practice within a longue-durée system of maritime contact — a processual-to-post-processual interpretive stance that treats deposition itself as meaningful ritual behaviour rather than incidental loss. Evidence quality varies sharply by region and artefact class: most metalwork comes from antiquarian dredging and hoard finds (e.g., the Huelva hoard, recovered in 1923) rather than controlled excavation, so contextual information is often minimal; shipwreck assemblages (Langdon Bay, Moor Sand) and mining sites (Ross Island, Mount Gabriel, Great Orme's Head) offer better-controlled modern excavation contexts. A live methodological debate (noted but not adjudicated here) concerns whether Mediterranean demand for metal directly drove the observed intensification of Atlantic bronze production — Cunliffe himself calls this "difficult to prove." Collection coverage: this page rests on a single secondary synthesis (Cunliffe 2001); no primary excavation reports or aDNA studies of the region have yet been ingested.
