## Definition and Construct History

Austroasiatic is one of the four major language groupings recognised in Southeast Asia in
the standard mid-twentieth-century classification Higham (1989) follows (Lebar et al.
1964: Sino-Tibetan, Tai, Austroasiatic, and Austronesian). It is defined by the
**comparative method** — regular sound correspondences establishing descent from a common
ancestor — not by any material culture, genetic population, or self-conscious people. Its
principal mainland Southeast Asian members are **Mon** and **Khmer** (the Mon-Khmer
branch) and **Vietnamese** (Vietic); the family also extends westward into eastern India
(e.g. Munda languages, outside Higham's scope). Mon's affinities lie with Khmer and
Vietnamese; the family is described by Higham as widely distributed and of considerable
time-depth on the mainland.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

Only the **linguistic** signature is directly evidenced, and it must be kept separate
from other evidence classes:

- **Linguistic.** The epigraphic record ties specific languages to specific early
  polities: early Chao Phraya valley inscriptions are in **Mon** (the vernacular of the
  Dvaravati [[mandala|mandalas]] — a single-line 7th-century Lopburi inscription, and an
  8th–9th-century one recording gifts to a Buddhist monastery); Tonle Sap and middle
  Mekong inscriptions are in **archaic Khmer** (the vernacular of Zhenla), used bilingually
  alongside Sanskrit, the ritual and elite register. North of the Ca River no early
  inscriptions survive, but the probable prehistoric language was Vietnamese.
- **Genetic / material.** Not addressed by Higham (1989), which predates archaeogenetic
  work. No archaeological culture on this wiki should be silently equated with the family.

## Distribution and Chronology

Higham reports evidence that Austroasiatic languages **preceded Tai** in the uplands where
Khmer, Cham, and Vietnamese are now spoken. His examples are folk-linguistic and
attributed: the Khmu of upland central Laos are called "older brothers" by the Tai-speaking
Lao; the Yumbri, a surviving hunter-gatherer group of upland northern Thailand, are probably
also Austroasiatic-speaking. Most inhabitants of the Truong Son cordillera speak Khmer-affinity
languages. The **Tai** languages, by contrast, are argued (citing Bayard 1979) to have a
mainland time-depth of less than a millennium in the Mekong and Chao Phraya valleys, entering
from southern China — on Bayard's reconstruction, small groups of warrior nobles moving into a
Chao Phraya valley previously occupied by Mon (Austroasiatic) speakers.

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` — not identical to — the Dvaravati and Zhenla/Angkorian polities via
their vernacular inscriptions. The **Cham** language of the Champa/Linyi mandalas is
**not** Austroasiatic but Austronesian (see [[austronesian-expansion]]); Higham stresses
it is "quite distinct" from the Vietnamese and Mon-Khmer languages. Language replacement
is documented as a real process: Vietnamese displaced Cham along the central Vietnamese
coast through military expansion, and Tai later superseded Mon in the Chao Phraya.

## Identity Cautions

The family's boundaries are **linguistic only**. Higham warns that language distribution
is "both a lead and a pitfall" for prehistorians — a caution against reading modern
language maps back onto prehistoric population movements. Per this wiki's four-way
non-identity rule: an Austroasiatic language is not an archaeological culture (do not
equate it with the Hoabinhian, Phung Nguyen, or [[dong-son-culture]]), not a genetic
population, and not a single self-conscious people. Any such equation is a claim requiring
argument and attribution.

## Historiography

The classification Higham uses is the standard structural one of Lebar et al. (1964);
absolute dating of language spread (glottochronology) is contested and Higham prefers
relative sequence plus independent epigraphic anchors. Homeland and time-depth arguments
for the family remain live in historical linguistics.
