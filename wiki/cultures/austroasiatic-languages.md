## Definition and Construct History

Austroasiatic is one of the four major language groupings recognised in Southeast Asia in
the standard mid-twentieth-century classification Higham (1989) follows (Lebar et al.
1964: Sino-Tibetan, Tai, Austroasiatic, and Austronesian). It is defined by the
**comparative method** — regular sound correspondences establishing descent from a common
ancestor — not by any material culture, genetic population, or self-conscious people. Its
principal mainland Southeast Asian members are **Mon** and **Khmer** (the Mon-Khmer
branch) and **Vietnamese** (Vietic); the family also extends westward into eastern India
(e.g. Munda languages, outside Higham's scope). Mon's affinities lie with Khmer and
Vietnamese; the family is described by Higham as widely distributed and of considerable
time-depth on the mainland.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

Only the **linguistic** signature is directly evidenced, and it must be kept separate
from other evidence classes:

- **Linguistic.** The epigraphic record ties specific languages to specific early
  polities: early Chao Phraya valley inscriptions are in **Mon** (the vernacular of the
  Dvaravati [[mandala|mandalas]] — a single-line 7th-century Lopburi inscription, and an
  8th–9th-century one recording gifts to a Buddhist monastery); Tonle Sap and middle
  Mekong inscriptions are in **archaic Khmer** (the vernacular of Zhenla), used bilingually
  alongside Sanskrit, the ritual and elite register. North of the Ca River no early
  inscriptions survive, but the probable prehistoric language was Vietnamese.
- **Genetic / material.** Not addressed by Higham (1989), which predates archaeogenetic
  work. No archaeological culture on this wiki should be silently equated with the family.

## Distribution and Chronology

Higham reports evidence that Austroasiatic languages **preceded Tai** in the uplands where
Khmer, Cham, and Vietnamese are now spoken. His examples are folk-linguistic and
attributed: the Khmu of upland central Laos are called "older brothers" by the Tai-speaking
Lao; the Yumbri, a surviving hunter-gatherer group of upland northern Thailand, are probably
also Austroasiatic-speaking. Most inhabitants of the Truong Son cordillera speak Khmer-affinity
languages. The **Tai** languages, by contrast, are argued (citing Bayard 1979) to have a
mainland time-depth of less than a millennium in the Mekong and Chao Phraya valleys, entering
from southern China — on Bayard's reconstruction, small groups of warrior nobles moving into a
Chao Phraya valley previously occupied by Mon (Austroasiatic) speakers.

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` — not identical to — the Dvaravati and Zhenla/Angkorian polities via
their vernacular inscriptions. The **Cham** language of the Champa/Linyi mandalas is
**not** Austroasiatic but Austronesian (see [[austronesian-expansion]]); Higham stresses
it is "quite distinct" from the Vietnamese and Mon-Khmer languages. Language replacement
is documented as a real process: Vietnamese displaced Cham along the central Vietnamese
coast through military expansion, and Tai later superseded Mon in the Chao Phraya.

## Identity Cautions

The family's boundaries are **linguistic only**. Higham warns that language distribution
is "both a lead and a pitfall" for prehistorians — a caution against reading modern
language maps back onto prehistoric population movements. Per this wiki's four-way
non-identity rule: an Austroasiatic language is not an archaeological culture (do not
equate it with the Hoabinhian, Phung Nguyen, or [[dong-son-culture]]), not a genetic
population, and not a single self-conscious people. Any such equation is a claim requiring
argument and attribution.

## Historiography

The classification Higham uses is the standard structural one of Lebar et al. (1964);
absolute dating of language spread (glottochronology) is contested and Higham prefers
relative sequence plus independent epigraphic anchors. Homeland and time-depth arguments
for the family remain live in historical linguistics. The 1989 volume treats Austroasiatic
largely as an epigraphic and modern-distribution fact for historic polities; the 2002 volume
adds an explicit **Neolithic farming-dispersal hypothesis** (below) that must remain
attributed and must not collapse language, material culture, and genes into one identity.

## Higham 2002 update — farming expansion model

*(Attributed — [[higham-charles|Higham]], *[[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002|Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia]]* 2002, Ch. 3 Neolithic Settlement summary and Ch. 8 Structure of the Past. This is a **theoretical/position** package, not wiki-voice fact. Enforce four-way non-identity throughout.)*

### Linguistic framing Higham endorses

(ATTRIBUTED) Higham links Yangzi agricultural origins to language distribution. Austroasiatic extends from eastern India to Vietnam and the Nicobars (patchy under later Thai, Burmese, and Austronesian overlays). Structural parallels between Nicobarese and Austronesian suggest common origin (Reid 1994 via Higham); oldest Austronesian on Taiwan (standard Austronesian historical-linguistics baseline as he presents it). Blust (1996 via Higham) proposed that a common ancestral language of early rice cultivators could explain both distributions — Austroasiatic into Southeast Asia and India from the upper Yangzi via rivers, Austronesian maritime expansion from Taiwan.

### Archaeological expansion package (associated_with, not identity)

(THEORETICAL — Higham 2002) Higham proposes a **mainland expansion of early Austroasiatic speakers** paralleling Austronesian maritime expansion: agricultural village communities expanding downstream to Hemudu and southward to Shixia; in Yunnan Baiyancun (initial settlement 2400–2100 BC); and down the Red, Mekong, and Chao Phraya rivers to Phung Nguyen, Ban Phak Top, and Non Pa Wai. He notes **cognate words for rice and aspects of its cultivation** from Munda languages west to Vietnamese — linguistic evidence for a shared rice vocabulary, not proof that any single site "is" Austroasiatic.

(FINDING package he treats as supportive) Agricultural settlements in the lower Red River, Mekong tributaries, and Chao Phraya; dates roughly the same, none earlier than ~2300 BC on the Khorat Plateau (Ban Chiang AMS rice-chaff Neolithic 2190–1880 and 2050–1500 BC); shared pottery decoration, extended inhumation, rice plus stock. Pan-Southeast Asian farming settlement with shared decorative pottery styles from Central Thailand to Yunnan and Bac Bo is placed within **2500–2000 BC** (needing more dates). Few inland Hoabinhian sites post-date 2300 BC; possible interaction with coastal foragers ([[nong-nor]] ~2450 BC no agriculture/dog; [[khok-phanom-di]] from ~2000 BC).

### Explicitly provisional — advanced for critical review

(POSITION — Higham 2002, reflexivity) The proposed intrusive movement of rice farmers "stands as a new model, and is just as subject to intensive and critical scrutiny as its predecessor." Higham acknowledges it may not gain general acceptance (resembles Heine-Geldern language/material-culture migration accounts; languages and styles can change for other reasons), but claims it harmonises better with available evidence than any other and aligns with Renfrew's (1987) Indo-European language-spread model. He judges it more convincing than the unsuccessful local rice-transition search of fifteen years prior (Spirit Cave, piedmont, coastal [[khok-phanom-di]] models — all "foundered for lack of supportive evidence").

### Proposed tests (as of 2002)

(ATTRIBUTED) Ancient DNA linking Non Pa Wai/Ban Kao settlers to Yangzi; dog first appears with agricultural settlements and cranial affinity to wolf (Higham et al. 1980), with no native Southeast Asian wolves — likely introduced; DNA might link dogs to Chinese wolf. White's (1997) earlier Kumphawapi agriculture, if archaeologically validated, would push intrusion earlier without overturning the model.

### Four-way non-identity (mandatory)

Do **not** equate:

| Class | What Higham 2002 associates | What must not be asserted |
|---|---|---|
| Language family | Austroasiatic (Blust/Reid framing) | = Phung Nguyen or any site culture |
| Material culture | rice + stock + shared pottery styles ~2500–2000 BC | = speakers of a named modern language |
| Genetic population | hypothesized Yangzi–SE Asia continuity (untested in source) | = Austroasiatic speakers as a biological race |
| Self-conscious people | not claimed | prehistoric "Austroasiatic people" as ethnonym |
