## Definition and Construct History

Leakey & Lewin (1977) narrate *Homo erectus* as the endpoint of a pattern of hominid diversification followed by drastic pruning that the authors treat as a recurring theme of human evolution: after the diversification of the [[ramapithecus]] stock (per the source's now-superseded account) into multiple lines, and after the multi-species coexistence documented at [[koobi-fora]] and [[olduvai-gorge]], by roughly three-quarters of a million years ago only a single hominid type is reported to have remained — *Homo erectus* (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). The taxon's naming history predates the book: Eugène Dubois recovered *Pithecanthropus erectus* fossil remains (a skull cap and femur) in Java between 1891 and 1898, using a genus name suggested by Ernst Haeckel in 1886; this Java material, along with Chinese "Peking Man" fossils, anchored the species outside Africa well before the East African discoveries the book centers.

A key African specimen reported in the source: in 1975 a member of the East Turkana Research Group found what the authors describe as the oldest and most complete *Homo erectus* cranium then known in Africa, with an estimated cranial capacity of about 900cc, dated to approximately 1.6 million years and described as similar in form to the Peking Man cranium (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). At [[olduvai-gorge]], a second, distinctive tool tradition — the Acheulian industry, hallmarked by the hand ax — is stated to have been made "apparently" by *Homo erectus*; the source reports the Developed Oldowan and Acheulian industries coexisted at Olduvai for roughly half a million years before the Developed Oldowan disappeared around one million years ago.

## Material / Anatomical Signature

The East Turkana cranium's ~900cc capacity is reported alongside a broader brain-size series presented elsewhere in the source (illustration captions): *Australopithecus* forms roughly 400–500cc, *Homo habilis* roughly 750cc, early *Homo erectus* roughly 900cc, and late *Homo erectus* roughly 1,100cc, against Neanderthal (~1,500cc) and modern *Homo sapiens* (~1,400cc) (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). The authors note a brain-size threshold of roughly 700cc has conventionally, if "somewhat arbitrar[ily]," been used as an informal marker for assigning fossils to the genus *Homo*.

The Acheulian hand-ax industry is `associated_with` *Homo erectus* in the source but is explicitly treated as a separate object of description from the species' anatomy — the authors state the makers were "apparently" *Homo erectus*, marking an inference rather than a directly observed link between a specific tool assemblage and a specific fossil. Claims of *Australopithecus* fragments from Java and China are noted in the source but described as too poor to permit confident interpretation, underscoring that the East Asian and Southeast Asian fossil record at this period is treated as *Homo erectus* territory, not shared with australopithecines.

## Distribution and Chronology

*Homo erectus*, like [[ramapithecus]], is reported to have occupied Europe and Asia as well as Africa — a geographic breadth the authors contrast with the australopithecines' apparently Africa-restricted diversification episode. Leakey & Lewin (attributed) describe bands of *Homo erectus* crossing from Africa into Asia around one million years ago via a narrow strip of arid land joining the continents, a dispersal the authors frame as driven by an internal "spirit of adventure" and food-sharing social organization rather than by climate pressure or aggressive territorial expansion — "the fundamental reason for their success in globetrotting was within themselves, not outside" (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977, attributed; note this reading is an interpretive thesis of the authors, not a documented behavioral finding). The East Turkana cranium anchors the African record at about 1.6 million years ago; the Java (Dubois) and China (Peking Man) material extends the species' known range across East and Southeast Asia by a date not precisely stated in the extracted material.

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Beyond the dispersal narrative above, the source's direct evidentiary claims about *Homo erectus* lifeways are limited chiefly to the Acheulian hand-ax association at Olduvai and the East Turkana cranial material; the richer behavioral reconstruction offered in the source (home bases, food sharing, sex-differentiated foraging) is explicitly built around the earlier *Homo habilis* "camp" scenario (see [[homo-habilis]]) and is not re-applied to *Homo erectus* in the extracted claims. The authors explicitly state there is no evidence supporting the popular notion that australopithecines went extinct because they were preyed upon by *Homo erectus* — flagging this as an unsupported idea rather than a documented predator–prey relationship (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977).

The book's chapter 6 does, however, add two lines of behavioral evidence for the *Homo erectus* grade. First, **built shelters**: at **Terra Amata** (a hillside site at Nice, southern France, excavated by Henry de Lumley), the authors report a camp of roughly four hundred thousand years ago whose occupants built shelters by driving wooden posts into the ground, braced with stones and possibly covered with skins or branches, and reoccupied the living floor repeatedly at apparently yearly intervals; the authors guess the occupants were *Homo erectus* (attributed, Leakey & Lewin 1977). Second, **the control of fire**: the source associates the deliberate use of hearth fire with *Homo erectus*, arguing that fire's warmth and light — extending the day and providing a social focus for "an already very social animal" — became increasingly important as *Homo erectus* bands moved northward into colder climates (attributed). These are read by the authors as further evidence of the home-base, socially-cooperative pattern central to their [[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing]] thesis, not as evidence of aggression.

## Relations and Successions

*Homo erectus* is presented as the sole hominid lineage surviving the pruning of the earlier multi-species diversification documented at [[koobi-fora]] and [[olduvai-gorge]], succeeding [[homo-habilis]] within the *Homo* lineage. The source proposes *Homo erectus* may have contributed to competitive pressure on late-surviving *Australopithecus africanus* and *A. boisei* populations (alongside emerging baboons), though again explicitly not through predation. Chapter material outside this range's assigned extraction (flagged by another extractor) covers *Homo erectus*'s further relationship to Neanderthals and the species' role in the "From Africa to Agriculture" narrative arc of the book's chapter 6; that later dispersal-and-succession detail is not duplicated here.

## Identity Cautions

"*Homo erectus*" as used in the source spans fossil material from Java, China, and East Africa treated as a single species on cranial-morphology grounds; no genetic or linguistic evidence links these geographically dispersed populations in the source, and the "spirit of adventure" dispersal narrative is an explicitly interpretive, attributed thesis about motivation, not a documented behavioral fact. The Acheulian tool tradition is `associated_with` the species via inferred authorship ("apparently"), not directly observed manufacture linked to a specific skeleton.

## Historiography

*Homo erectus* research predates the Leakeys' East African program by nearly a century, rooted in Eugène Dubois's 1891–98 Java expedition and complicated for decades by the Piltdown Man forgery (exposed roughly forty years after its 1912 "discovery"), which the source elsewhere describes as having reinforced a discredited "big-brain-first" model of human evolution (see [[australopithecus]]). The 1975 East Turkana cranium is presented in the source as a recent (pre-publication) addition strengthening the African evidentiary base for the species, dated via potassium-argon methods to about 1.6 million years. The book's dispersal narrative — crediting *Homo erectus*'s spread from Africa to an internal social/behavioral drive rather than external pressure — is an authorial interpretive thesis (attributed, Leakey & Lewin 1977) that should be read alongside the food-sharing framework developed for [[homo-habilis]] on [[food-sharing-hypothesis]], of which it is an extension.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]]; mid-2000s textbook synthesis.)*

W&O treat humans ~1.5–0.5 mya as *Homo erectus* for narrative convenience while noting the taxon is contested (possible restriction to East Asian forms vs African *Homo ergaster*; questions of stasis and European presence). Timeline anchors: *H. erectus* grade and Acheulian hand-axes ~1.8 Ma; out-of-Africa migration ~1.7 Ma in their overview.

**Anatomy (as they describe):** average just over five feet; cranial capacity ~850–1,300 cm³; low foreheads, large brow ridges, thick cranial bones, almost no chin. **KNM-WT 15000** (Nariokotome boy, West Turkana, ~1.6 Ma; Richard Leakey group 1984): ~1.6 m tall at ~11 years, cranial capacity 880 cm³—body form already modern-like. **Zhoukoudian:** >40 individuals, >100,000 tools; Wu & Jia summarize uppermost layers ~230 kya, earliest ~700 kya; mean brain ~1,040 cm³; shovel-shaped upper incisors used by some as continuity to modern Chinese populations (continuity claim attributed, contested under replacement models).

**Culture:** Acheulian hand-axes in East Africa >1 Ma vs earliest European Acheulian ~600 kya; economy disputed—hunter readings vs scavenging/opportunistic hunting ([[binford-lewis|Binford]] on Olduvai/Olorgesailie). W&O stress cultural absences vs later humans: no secure figurines, wall painting, rock carving, or elaborately non-utilitarian stone tools; no intentional burials securely attributed. Java Dubois finds re-dated (de Vos et al.) to ~1.7 mya with uncertainty of findspot/sediment association and no good tool association.

### Stringer & Gamble — *In Search of the Neanderthals* (1993)

*(Attributed — [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]], [[stringer-chris]], [[gamble-clive]]; as of 1993.)*

**Out of Africa 1:** *Homo erectus* expands from Africa into Eurasia as the first major hominin dispersal (distinct from later modern-human Out of Africa 2). Authors cite African early *erectus* (including WT-15000 / Nariokotome context) and Asian material; Far Eastern *erectus* is read by the replacement camp as more isolated than multiregionalists allow (POSITION on [[modern-human-origins-debate]] / [[multiregional-evolution]] vs [[recent-african-origin]]).

**Relation to Neanderthals and moderns:** European archaic sequences (Swanscombe–Steinheim–Arago–Petralona–Atapuerca grades as they narrate) are treated as leading toward [[neanderthals]], while African archaics lead toward [[anatomically-modern-humans]] — a geographic-divergence reading, not multiregional continuity from regional *erectus* to local moderns (attributed). Do not equate *Homo erectus* with a people, language, or single culture.

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein distinguishes African **_Homo ergaster_** (early African *erectus*-grade) from the
classic East Asian *Homo erectus*, treating the branching rather than a single worldwide
species. The Nariokotome/Turkana Boy skeleton (KNM-WT 15000, West Turkana, ~1.6 Ma) shows an
already modern-like body build and tall stature by adolescence with a cranial capacity ~880 cc.
Klein credits *H. ergaster/erectus* with the **first dispersal of *Homo* out of Africa** ("Out
of Africa 1") by ~1.7 Ma — anchored at [[dmanisi]] (Georgia) and the Javanese and
[[zhoukoudian]] (Chinese) records — and with the **[[acheulean-industry]]** (bifaces/handaxes),
which appears ~1.7 Ma and postdates the earlier [[oldowan-industry]]. On **fire**, Klein is
cautious: burned bones at Swartkrans Member 3 are contested incipient evidence, with no secure
hearths early on (see [[control-of-fire]]). *H. erectus/ergaster* subsistence (hunting vs
scavenging) he leaves genuinely open. The lineage grades, via archaic
[[homo-heidelbergensis]]-grade populations, toward the [[neanderthals]] in Europe and modern
humans in Africa. Industry is kept separate from taxon throughout. See [[encephalization]].

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012.*

**World at ~200 ka (sketch).** Stringer places late *H. erectus* as still surviving in parts of Asia while other lineages diverged: East Africa shows the first known signs of the modern human pattern; Europe is already on a Neanderthal trajectory; Narmada (India) may represent an erectus or heidelbergensis descendant; China occupied by descendants of erectus or heidelbergensis; Ngandong dating uncertain for late Javan erectus; [[homo-floresiensis]] long established on Flores (as of Stringer 2012). SE Asian island connections among erectus descendants (and Hobbit-like relicts) would have been disrupted by sea-level change.

**Dmanisi and Out of Africa 1.** Dmanisi (Georgia) ~1.75 Ma — five small-brained skulls, jaws, skeleton parts, basic volcanic-rock tools — challenges the view that the first out-of-Africa move required larger brains, better tools, or major behavioural upgrades (none evidenced there). Rosas and Bermúdez de Castro noted jaw resemblances to early East African erectus and later Chinese erectus. Combining Dmanisi primitiveness with Liang Bua, some (Dennell, Roebroeks, others) argue a widespread Eurasian pre-erectus phase ~2 Ma now only sparsely sampled — an alternative to classic Out of Africa 1 orthodoxy that Stringer records without fully endorsing (position in [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

**Brain, growth, cooperative breeding.** Birth-canal brain-size threshold ~500 cc must have been reached in *H. erectus* time, implying extended post-birth immaturity vs apes; estimated molar eruption ~5, 9, 15 years vs modern ~6, 12, 18. Brain-size increase and dental reduction began in erectus, developed by heidelbergensis, and reached near-modern levels in Neanderthals. Sarah Blaffer Hrdy (via Stringer): large-brained dependent infants by erectus time already required alloparents; cooperative breeding enabled still larger brains (attributed theory). Semicircular canals in *H. erectus* and early moderns more like living humans; Neanderthals the odd ones out.

**Multiregional continuity rejected (as of Stringer 2012).** Classic Multiregional claims that regional skeletal feature complexes link erectus fossils >1 Ma in each region to living peoples in the same regions are treated as comprehensively falsified by the evidence he presents — Africa’s special role and recent modern origin stand; any residual archaic input is occasional hybridization (Neanderthals, Denisovans, surviving SE Asian erectus, perhaps floresiensis), not continuous multiregional conversion of local erectus into local moderns. See [[recent-african-origin]], [[multiregional-evolution]], [[wolpoff-milford]].

**Identity cautions.** Taxon ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ people; industry associations remain `associated_with`.

### Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*(Attributed — [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]; as of Tattersall 2012.)*

**Taxonomy and African *ergaster*.** Tattersall prefers *Homo ergaster* for early African forms (name from a 1.5 Ma East Turkana mandible, 1975), keeping classic East Asian *H. erectus* (Dubois’s Trinil *Pithecanthropus*; long low vault, strong brow, regional characters) partly separate from a Mayr-style worldwide middle grade. Iconic African cranium KNM-ER 3733 (~1.8 Ma, East Turkana): face not strongly projecting, somewhat protruding nose, vault ~850 cc — “anticipated what was to come” and first clearly deserved *Homo*. Turkana Boy KNM-WT 15000 (~1.6 Ma): nearly complete adolescent; dental increments imply ~8 years despite eruption/bone age like a modern ~12-year-old — fast, more apelike developmental schedule; long legs, near-modern proportions, **obligate** not merely facultative biped; East Turkana footprints ~1.5 Ma confirm long stride and basically modern foot. No convincing morphological intermediate between australopith/“early *Homo*” and the Boy (saltational/regulatory-change thesis). Rapid *ergaster*/*erectus* development: Java juvenile ~1 year already ~72–84% of adult brain average; Boy died with ~880 cc near adult size. Broca’s area on endocast does not prove speech; thoracic vertebral canal average-primate (not expanded like moderns/Neanderthals) — POSITION against peripheral speech fine-control. Cognition: larger absolute brains than bipedal apes but not junior-league moderns; no archaeological symbolic manipulation across long tenure.

**Out of Africa 1 and diversity under the label.** Initial Eurasian exit ~1.8 Ma (or earlier) in archaic archaeological context — not enlarged brain or improved toolkit (see [[dmanisi]]). Java dates ~1.8–1.6 Ma jointly make early exit hard to dispute (as of 2012). Morphological diversity under *H. erectus* ~1 Ma (Olorgesailie <800 cc; Buia 750–800; Daka 995; Olduvai Bed II 1067) differs from Java type and from each other. Brain enlargement independently in ≥3 lineages (Java erectus, Neanderthals, sapiens precursors); large brain necessary but not sufficient for modern cognition. First *H. ergaster* kept Oldowan-like tools for hundreds of millennia (reuse existing toolkits / accommodate rather than invent — see [[exaptation]], [[oldowan-industry]]); [[acheulean-industry]] handaxes appear after *ergaster* (~1.78 Ma earliest Kenya as of 2012).

**Fire and diet edges.** Early fire evidence for *ergaster* almost entirely circumstantial (as of 2012); high-quality animal diet and expensive-tissue arguments are reported as literature positions. Identity: industry `associated_with` taxon; African ergaster-grade ≠ single worldwide people or language.
