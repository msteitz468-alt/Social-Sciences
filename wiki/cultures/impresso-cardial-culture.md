# Impresso–Cardial Culture

Early Neolithic archaeological complex of the **central and west Mediterranean**, defined by **impressed and Cardial (cockle-shell) decorated pottery** and associated early farming practices along coastal and hinterland routes. **Etic construct.**

## Definition and Construct History

Standard label for the Mediterranean early Neolithic pottery horizon (Impressed Ware / Cardial). Whittle (*[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]*, Ch. 8) supports a **lower chronology** for much of the west Mediterranean and a **central role for indigenous population** in many areas — a **position** within [[farming-spread-europe-debate]], not wiki-voice consensus. He treats the complex as strongly **regionalised from the outset** (Uzzo ≠ eastern Sicily; Curinga ≠ Tavoliere; scratched Matera styles; trichrome climax in N Apulia), contrasting early [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] ceramic uniformity and thereby weakening colonisation-by-common-style arguments (attributed).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material:** Cardial/impressed ceramics; mixed use of domesticates and wild resources; cave and open sites; coastal orientation early on. Resource bases vary: coastal lagoon/fishing/shellfish; open sites (e.g. Le Baratin, Portiragnes, Leucate underwater); upland caves with hunting principal and later herding; inland Massif Central users continuing largely as foragers (Roucadour wild-dominated — as of Whittle 1996 synthesis).

**Linguistic / genetic:** No identity equations. Demic (Zilhão-style maritime colonisation) vs indigenous selective adoption remain rival positions. Whittle **rejects pure demic package** as the primary west-Mediterranean engine (position, mid-1990s; pre-aDNA).

## Distribution and Chronology

*(Attributed — Whittle 1996, Ch. 8; mid-1990s radiocarbon reliability caveats.)*

- **Lower chronology lean:** Whittle accepts a **lower start** for southern French and eastern–southern Spanish Impressed/Cardial (**soon after ~6000 BC, no earlier**), citing shell-sample problems, lab variation, and sample–context association; early seventh-millennium claims (Cap Ragnon, Île Riou; Dehesilla, Nerja, Cova Fosca non-cardial horizons) are doubted. Castelnovien “sheep ~7000 BC” cases (Châteauneuf, Gazel, Dourgne, Gramari) treated as suspect (disturbance/intrusion).
- **Staged sequence (his tentative map):** later rather than earlier seventh millennium indigenous change in southern Italy and eastern Sicily; western Sicily late seventh; central Italy ~6000 BC; then S France (with contact into central-west France), E/S Spain, S Portugal; Liguria ~6000 BC but Po valley only later sixth; Sardinia/Corsica Impressed elements through sixth; Malta from ~5000 BC; many inland areas still sparse by end of sixth; N African coast largely foragers to ~5000 BC.
- **Southern Italy reported orthodoxy:** limited seaborne colonisation from across the Adriatic as Balkan/Greek offshoot is the **consensus model he reports** (paralleling Crete/Cyprus); open occupations and ~500+ Tavoliere *villaggi trincerati* (Passo di Corvo etc.) mark densest settlement signal — Whittle still prefers mobility/aggregation readings of enclosures as unfinished projects of coming and going, not automatic sedentary colonist villages.

## Lifeways

### Grotta dell’Uzzo (type sequence for indigenous continuity)

*(Attributed — Whittle 1996.)* Long post-glacial occupation (oak-woodland hunting, birds, shellfish, plants; several cave burials). From ~**7000 BC** intensified sea fishing (including whales — stranded or possible offshore boat forays). Later seventh millennium: domesticates in **small quantities** — pigs (possibly indigenous stock), sheep/goats (not native), cattle; emmer, einkorn, lentils; after 6000 BC also barley, other wheat, beans. Early impressed clay containers, later painted bands. Whittle: colonisation of such a long-held shore is “inherently unlikely”; “highly probable” same people continued; gradual change = **broadening of resource base**, not abrupt package substitution — **indigenous continuity** exploiting long-range waterborne contact. He flags that full publication had not yet demonstrated continuity end-to-end (Neolithic mainly from cave front; transition dates extrapolated).

### Sea-borne acculturation (primary mechanism — position)

Whittle’s core Ch. 8 claim: primary means of change throughout the central and west Mediterranean was **acculturation** via **sea-borne** transmission of contacts, ideas, and resources, with indigenous transformation at different rates — **not** demic wave-of-advance alone. Process analogous (in his reading) to sixth-millennium inland forager → LBK transformation plus surrounding forager convergence. S France/E Spain preferred model: late start ~6000 BC; indigenous adoption of pottery **plus** addition of herding and plant cultivation to wide-ranging hunting/collecting. Before 5000 BC the label “farmers” applies nowhere region-wide in his vocabulary; adoption is **piecemeal**, community-by-community; no convincing region-wide resource-stress model. Pottery initially for serving food and **drink** in special as well as mundane contexts; integration/cooperation via sharing, not clearly competitive display. Portuguese estuarine middens (Tagus, Sado, Mira) as long-lived forager bases; colonisation-into-empty-niches vs gradual indigenous pottery/sheep adoption remain rival models he records. Best colonisation case in the basin might be Sardinia/Corsica (empty niche + seafaring) — still open.

## Relations and Successions

- **associated_with:** Mediterranean early farming, later Chasséen and Iberian sequences ([[los-millares]] horizon is later Copper Age place-making, not Cardial).
- Contrasts with loess [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]] inland package.
- **challenges:** pure [[demic-diffusion]] / wave-of-advance as sole European engine (Whittle position; see also [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

Cardial pots ≠ Cardial people. Flooded coastal farmsteads (Zilhão) vs cave continuity (Lewthwaite/Rowley-Conwy type arguments) are competing readings of the same sites. Regional style diversity from the outset undercuts treating Impressed Ware as a single demic ethnic package. Pre-aDNA genetic demography is outside Whittle’s evidence class (**as of 1996**).

## Historiography

Mediterranean Neolithic research traditions; Zilhão colonisation vs selective adoption debate; densified here via *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]* Ch. 8–10 (mid-1990s synthesis; lower chronology + sea-borne acculturation program).
