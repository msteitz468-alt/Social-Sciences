# Samarra Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Samarra culture is named for Samarra ware, first discovered in 1912–14 in a prehistoric cemetery beneath the medieval city of Samarra. [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] reports that it was "long thought" the Samarra painters came from Iran, but "we now know" the ware was indigenous to Mesopotamia, belonging to a culture that flourished in the middle Tigris valley in the second half of the sixth millennium BCE — an explicit revision of an earlier diffusionist assumption. In Roux's six-stage relative chronology the Samarra period runs c. 5600–5000 BCE (approximate, uncalibrated as given).

The culture was substantively "revealed in the 1960s" through Iraqi excavations at Tell es-Sawwan, a large mound on the left bank of the Tigris, 11 km south of Samarra.

## Material Signature

Samarra ware has a pale surface bearing geometric bands or figural motifs (humans, birds, fish, antelope, scorpions) in red, dark brown, or purple; Roux describes it as showing "extraordinary impression of movement" (his own aesthetic judgment, attributed).

At Tell es-Sawwan, inhabitants used Hassunan-type tools but were, per Roux, the first in the sequence to practice primitive irrigation agriculture, using Tigris floodwater to grow wheat, barley, and linseed. The village had a defensive ditch some 3 m deep plus a thick buttressed mud-brick wall; large regular multi-room houses with courtyards were built of "cigar-shaped" mud bricks — an advance over pressed mud — with gypsum- or clay-plastered floors and walls, and large empty "granary"-type buildings (an interpretive label, attributed to the excavators).

Burial practice at Tell es-Sawwan: adults were buried contracted and wrapped in bitumen-coated matting; children were buried in jars or bowls under house floors. Grave goods included fine translucent marble vessels and alabaster or terracotta female (occasionally male) statuettes, some with "coffee-bean" eyes and pointed heads similar to later Ubaid figurines, others with shell- or bitumen-inlaid eyes that Roux (quoting an unnamed source) calls "astonishingly reminiscent of much later Sumerian technique."

## Distribution and Chronology

No site comparable in scale to Tell es-Sawwan has yet been found. Samarra pottery — as copies or imports — is documented at Baghuz on the middle Euphrates and at Chagar Bazar in the central Jazirah, and along the Tigris valley from Nineveh to Choga Mami on the Iraqi-Iranian border. At Choga Mami, canal irrigation was practiced, coffee-bean-eyed statuettes recur, and Samarra ware locally evolved into "Choga Mami Transitional" pottery, which Roux compares to the Eridu and Hajji Muhammad wares of southern Iraq — themselves early forms of Ubaid pottery. Roux proposes this Choga Mami Transitional link as a possible ceramic-sequence connection between Samarra and Ubaid material culture.

## Lifeways

Subsistence combined irrigation-fed cereal and flax cultivation with the broader Hassunan farming toolkit. Village defense (ditch and wall at Tell es-Sawwan) and substantial storage architecture suggest organized, settled communities managing surplus.

## Relations and Successions

Roux frames the Samarra culture as "a derivative of the Hassuna culture," explicitly contrasted with the Halaf culture, which he treats as having no local Mesopotamian ancestor. He also poses, as an open and explicitly speculative question rather than an assertion, "Could the Samarran folk be the ancestors of the 'Ubaidians' and even perhaps of the Sumerians?" — flagged here as a position, not a finding; it is a candidate for a `debates/` page on Mesopotamian cultural continuity/ethnogenesis should the wiki develop one. Roux draws a broader ceramic-sequence hypothesis (detailed on [[ubaid-culture]]) in which Ubaid ware derives from Hajji Muhammad ware, which derives from Eridu ware, which in turn appears to derive from — or at least share common ancestry with — Samarra ware.

## Identity Cautions

Elsewhere in his discussion of Sumerian origins, Roux explicitly warns against equating the Samarrans with the Sumerians or 'Ubaidians "on the sole basis of their pottery" — a direct statement of the pots-≠-people caution this wiki enforces as the four-way non-identity rule. "Samarra culture" designates a material-culture (ceramic and architectural) horizon; it carries no established linguistic or genetic identity, and any proposed continuity with later populations (Ubaidians, Sumerians) is, by Roux's own framing, an open and speculative question, not an equation to be asserted.

## Historiography

Known to the wiki via [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]. The culture's definition rests substantially on 1960s Iraqi excavation at Tell es-Sawwan, superseding an earlier (pre-1960s) diffusionist assumption of Iranian origin. Later specialist reports and post-1992 reassessment are outstanding for this page's densification.
