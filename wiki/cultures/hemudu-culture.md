# Hemudu Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Hemudu culture (ca. 5500–3300 BC) is defined at the Ningshao Plain along the Yao River, Zhejiang province, on the lower Yangzi, and is documented primarily through the type site of Hemudu and the extensively excavated site of Tianluoshan (Liu & Chen 2012). The construct is well established in Chinese Neolithic archaeology as a distinct regional tradition characterized by waterlogged-preservation-enabled wooden architecture and an unusually rich organic record.

## Material Signature

Hemudu is best known for pile-dwelling wooden architecture built with mortise-and-tenon joinery, an advanced carpentry technique preserved by waterlogged site conditions. Sites have yielded abundant rice remains spanning both wild and domesticated morphology. Liu and Chen (2012) characterize the subsistence economy as "rather egalitarian... with little evidence of social stratification," and note the diet remained predominantly reliant on wild foods even after domesticated rice cultivation was established — a case where domestication and dietary dependence did not move in lockstep.

A recurrent bird-on-altar motif with possible sun/moon symbolism appears in Hemudu iconography and is noted by Liu and Chen as echoed later in Liangzhu jade and pottery imagery (Wu 1985; Ren 1996; Yang 2000) — flagged here as a material-motif continuity, not an identity claim between the two cultures.

## Faunal Signature: Water Buffalo Non-Domestication

A long-standing assumption that Hemudu inhabitants had domesticated the water buffalo has been revised by aDNA and zooarchaeological analysis: Liu, L. et al. (2004) and Yang, D. et al. (2008) found the water buffalo remains at Hemudu were **not domesticated**, contrary to earlier belief (as of Liu & Chen 2012). This is recorded here in attributed voice as a genetics/zooarchaeology-based revision of an older material-culture inference — an instance of the four-way non-identity principle applying even within a single evidence class (morphological vs. genetic evidence of domestication status).

## Distribution and Chronology

The culture is dated ca. 5500–3300 BC in the Ningshao Plain/Yao River area of Zhejiang. It follows the Kuahuqiao culture regionally (not yet a wiki page) and its later phase is contemporary with, and in some readings ancestral to, developments feeding into the subsequent Liangzhu-era lower-Yangzi sequence.

## Lifeways

Hemudu communities practiced pile-dwelling construction, rice cultivation (with substantial continued wild-food reliance), and did not domesticate the local water buffalo despite proximity and later assumptions to the contrary. Liu and Chen's "rather egalitarian" characterization is an attributed social-stratification assessment based on burial and settlement evidence, not a wiki-voice universal claim about all Hemudu communities across its full duration.

## Relations and Successions

Hemudu `contrasts_with` the subsequent Songze culture of the Lake Tai region, which Liu and Chen explicitly frame against Hemudu's egalitarian pattern (Songze's Dongshancun cemetery shows marked grave-good disparities by contrast). Wang, H. and Liu (2005) propose that the late Hemudu dispersal is linked to the initial phase of Austronesian seafaring expansion — this is recorded here as an **attributed position**, associating (not identifying) an archaeological culture with a later linguistic-population phenomenon; the four-way non-identity rule applies directly, since Austronesian is a language-family designation, not an archaeological-culture or genetic-population label coextensive with Hemudu.

## Identity Cautions

Hemudu is an archaeological-culture construct defined by material remains (architecture, ceramics, faunal/floral assemblages) at specific sites. It is not evidence of a self-identifying people, and the water-buffalo case illustrates how morphology-based domestication inferences can be overturned by later genetic evidence. The proposed Hemudu–Austronesian-dispersal link (Wang & Liu 2005) is a contested, single-line-of-evidence hypothesis linking a material culture to a much later linguistic phenomenon across a broad geography and time depth; it is recorded via `associated_with`, not as an identity claim, and should be read alongside the broader Neolithic-to-Austronesian dispersal debate flagged on other south-China/Taiwan Neolithic material (Keqiutou, Dabenkeng, Xiantouling — prose-only in this wiki pending dedicated pages).

## Historiography

Hemudu has been a reference site for lower-Yangzi rice-farming and wooden-architecture studies since its excavation; Tianluoshan has substantially extended the record through modern archaeobotanical and zooarchaeological methods, producing the water-buffalo non-domestication revision cited above.
