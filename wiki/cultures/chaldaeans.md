## Definition and Construct History

"Chaldaeans" is the conventional label for the Eleventh, last, and Neo-Babylonian
dynasty of Babylon (626–539 BCE), founded by Nabopolassar. Per
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], the label itself is questionable: "the Eleventh
and last dynasty or, as we call it, the Chaldaean (perhaps wrongly) or Neo-Babylonian
dynasty." Nabopolassar is often held to have belonged to the Kaldu tribe — one of
several Aramaean-affiliated tribal groups (alongside the Litaû, Puqudû, and Gambulû)
that had settled southern Iraq some three centuries earlier (see [[aramaeans]]) — but
Roux does not treat this tribal identification as fully secure, and the wiki records
`construct_status: contested` accordingly. The dynasty's political and cultural
achievement — the revival of Babylon as an imperial capital — is far better documented
than the ethnic label attached to it.

## Material / Linguistic / Textual Signature

- **Textual**: an unusually well-documented dynasty by ancient Near Eastern standards.
  The Babylonian Chronicles (a contemporary cuneiform annal series beginning in 616
  BCE) provide near-exact regnal and campaign dating, cross-checkable against
  Herodotus, the Nabonidus Chronicle, the Cyrus Cylinder, and biblical text — a rare
  case of multiple independent textual lines converging on a single high-confidence
  chronology.
- **Material**: the Neo-Babylonian rebuilding of [[babylon]] itself (Ishtar Gate,
  Processional Way, the Southern Citadel palace, Etemenanki) is the dynasty's
  principal material signature; see that page for detail.
- **Linguistic**: royal and administrative texts remained in Akkadian, but Aramaic
  continued its centuries-long displacement of spoken Babylonian across this period.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Nabopolassar (626–605 BCE).** Nabopolassar seized Babylon in 626 BCE amid Assyrian
civil war and resisted repeated Assyrian reconquest attempts, controlling all of Sumer
and Akkad by 616 BCE. In 614 BCE he allied with the Median king Cyaxares (Babylonian:
Umakish-tar) after the Medes sacked Assur, an alliance later sealed by the marriage of
Nabopolassar's son Nebuchadrezzar to Cyaxares' daughter Amytis. The combined
Babylonian-Median force took [[nineveh]] after a three-month siege in summer 612 BCE
(see that page for detail); a residual Assyrian state under the non-royal officer
Ashur-uballit held out at Harran until 609 BCE. Nabopolassar's own inscription frames
Assyria's destruction as retribution: "I slaughtered the land of Subarum (Assyria)…
The Assyrian, who since distant days had ruled over all the peoples… his yoke I threw
off." The Babylonians did not occupy or rebuild devastated Assyria, focusing instead on
the religious and cultural revival of southern Mesopotamia and on securing Syria and
the Taurus frontier, since Babylonian trade now ran overwhelmingly through the west.

**Nebuchadrezzar II (605–562 BCE).** As crown prince, Nebuchadrezzar defeated an
Egyptian garrison at Karkemish (May–June 605 BCE), opening Syria-Palestine to Babylon;
he was crowned king that September after a forced 23-day march home following his
father's death. His Levantine campaigns included tribute collection from Damascus,
Tyre, Sidon, and Jerusalem and the destruction of Ascalon (604 BCE); an indecisive
battle against Egypt (601 BCE); and two captures of Jerusalem — the first (16 March 597
BCE), deporting King Jehoiakin and 3,000 Jews and installing the puppet king Zedekiah;
the second and final capture, by storm on 29 July 587 BCE after an 18-month siege,
in which Zedekiah's sons were killed before his eyes, Zedekiah himself blinded and
deported, Solomon's Temple burned, the walls broken down, and a mass deportation — "the
Babylonian captivity" — carried out. Roux frames the parallel explicitly: "Thus 135
years after Israel, 'Judah was carried away out of the land.'" Tyre was besieged for 13
years under Nebuchadrezzar, and a fragmentary tablet alludes to an uncertain, likely
unsuccessful, campaign against Egypt in 568 BCE. Nebuchadrezzar also mediated the 585
BCE "Battle of the Eclipse" peace between Media and Lydia, fixing their frontier at the
Halys River — evidence of substantial Neo-Babylonian regional diplomatic weight beyond
conquest. He died of illness in early October 562 BCE after a 43-year reign.

**Succession crisis and Nabonidus (562–539 BCE).** Nebuchadrezzar's son Amêl-Marduk
(Evil-Merodach) reigned two years before being assassinated by his brother-in-law
Neriglissar (r. 559–556 BCE); Neriglissar's young son Labâshi-Marduk was tortured to
death by conspirators nine months into his reign; the conspirators elevated Nabonidus
(Nabû-na'id) in June 556 BCE.

## Nabonidus — Source-Reliability Caution

Nabonidus (r. 556–539 BCE) is the dynasty's most contested figure and requires careful
handling. He was the son of a Babylonian noble and a long-lived Sin-priestess mother
who received a royal burial at her death (547 BCE, aged 104); he held high office under
Nebuchadrezzar and Neriglissar before his own accession. His devotion to the moon-god
Sin — restoring the E.hul.hul temple at Harran and temples at Ur — and a long personal
absence in Arabia made him deeply unpopular with Babylon's Marduk priesthood.

The principal hostile source, the **"Verse Account of Nabonidus,"** is an explicitly
post-conquest polemical pamphlet accusing him of madness, lying about victories, and
"heresy" (worshipping "a deity which nobody had ever seen in the country"). Roux argues
this polemic — via a probable confusion of names — is the likely source of the
"Nebuchadrezzar's madness" story later told in the Book of Daniel, with echoes in the
Dead Sea Scrolls. Roux's own historiographic judgment is double-edged: "even the most
cautious of modern historians are obliged to admit that they contain a spark of truth,"
but to conclude Nabonidus wanted to replace Marduk with Sin "is perhaps going too far" —
Roux reads Nabonidus's extensive temple-building (which included Marduk's own temple)
and his antiquarian excavation of foundation deposits as reflecting genuine reverence
for Sumero-Akkadian tradition, earning him the nickname "the royal archaeologist,"
though Roux stresses "neither his aims nor his methods had anything to do with
archaeology" in the modern sense — his activity belongs instead to a broader
Neo-Babylonian/Achaemenid-period antiquarian passion (chronicle-copying, king-list
compilation, antiquities collecting). Woolley's excavations found a "private museum" of
mixed-period antiquities in the palace of Nabonidus's daughter, the Sin priestess
En-nigaldi-Nanna, at Ur — direct material evidence of this antiquarian culture.

Roux argues, as his own causal interpretation, that it was Nabonidus's 553 BCE
imposition of closer royal control over temple business (appointing royal officers over
Uruk's E-Anna temple to supervise tithes and taxes) — "more than the king's 'heresy'" —
that alienated the priesthood and threw it toward Cyrus. Nabonidus's roughly decade-long
absence in Arabia (based at the oasis of Temâ from perhaps his 7th to 11th regnal
years) is itself a live, unresolved question: Roux surveys explanations — control of
Arabian trade routes and the cult of Sin at Temâ (his preferred reading), the official
"Harran inscription" account of a voluntary departure amid civil war and famine, or
political enemies preventing his return — and states plainly that none fully accounts
for so long an absence. During this absence the Babylonian New Year Festival could not
be celebrated for years.

## The Fall of Babylon (539 BCE)

Cyrus II of Persia (throne from 559 BCE) united the Median and Persian kingdoms after
defeating his grandfather Astyages of Media (550 BCE) and went on to conquer Lydia (547
BCE) and the Ionian Greek cities, building an empire stretching roughly 5,000 km within
a decade. In autumn 539 BCE, per the Nabonidus Chronicle, Cyrus defeated Akkad's army at
Opis; Sippar fell without battle; Gobryas (Gubaru), governor of Gutium, defected and
entered Babylon with Cyrus's forces without a fight; Nabonidus was arrested on his
return to the city; Cyrus himself entered Babylon shortly after, proclaiming a "state of
peace." Formal Persian occupation is dated from 12 October 539 BCE. Belshazzar
(Bêl-shar-usur), Nabonidus's son and regent, was killed at Opis. Unlike Nineveh, Babylon
itself **was not destroyed** — Cyrus preserved the city, restored captured gods
(including Assyrian gods earlier taken by the Medes) to their shrines, and presented
himself in the Cyrus Cylinder as Marduk's chosen successor.

## Lifeways

The Chaldaean/Neo-Babylonian state, as documented by [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]],
concentrated its energies on the religious and cultural revival of southern Mesopotamia
rather than the reconquest of devastated Assyria. Its wealth rested on control of the
western trade routes (all Babylonian commerce now ran through Syria-Palestine), on an
intensive temple economy (see [[temple-economy]] and [[babylon]]) in which institutions
like the E-Anna of Uruk operated as major landowners, employers, and traders, and on a
silver-standard credit economy served by banking families such as the Egibi of Babylon.
Nebuchadrezzar II's building programme at [[babylon|Babylon]] — the walls, the Ishtar
Gate, the Processional Way, Etemenanki, and Esagila — is the fullest material expression
of the dynasty's cult-centred wealth. Religious life centred on Marduk and the annual
[[mesopotamian-religion|Akitu festival]], whose interruption (under Nabonidus's Arabian
absence) was itself a political crisis.

## Relations and Successions

- **`associated_with`:** the [[aramaeans|Aramaean]]-affiliated Kaldû tribes of southern
  Iraq (Nabopolassar's reported origin) — a tribal, not cleanly ethnic, connection.
- **`succeeded`:** the [[assyrians|Neo-Assyrian empire]], destroyed 612 BCE by the
  Nabopolassar–Median alliance; the Chaldaeans inherited the Assyrian west but not its
  ruined heartland.
- **Rivals/contemporaries:** the Medes (allies under Cyaxares, later absorbed by Persia),
  Egypt (defeated at Karkemish 605 BCE), and Lydia.
- **`succeeded_by`:** the [[unknown]] Achaemenid Persian empire under Cyrus II (539 BCE),
  which preserved [[babylon|Babylon]] and continued its administrative traditions.

## Identity Cautions

"Chaldaean" is a political-dynastic label of debated adequacy (Roux's own hedge,
"perhaps wrongly"), rooted in one tribal-affiliation claim (Nabopolassar's reported
Kaldu origin) rather than a demonstrated ethnic or genetic population distinct from the
wider Aramaean-affiliated tribal milieu of southern Iraq. Do not read "Chaldaean" as
naming a genetically or linguistically distinct people from the Aramaeans generally;
treat the political dynasty and any ethnic-tribal substrate as separately evidenced.

## Historiography

The Neo-Babylonian period is unusually well triangulated: the Babylonian Chronicles
(contemporary), the hostile Verse Account (post-conquest polemic), Herodotus (later
Greek eyewitness/secondhand tradition), the Nabonidus Chronicle, and the Cyrus Cylinder
together allow cross-checking rare for this era — but the Nabonidus material
specifically shows how a single hostile pamphlet can shape both ancient and later
(biblical) tradition. Treat any characterization of Nabonidus as "mad" or "heretical" as
inherited from a source with an explicit political interest in his downfall.

**Collection coverage note**: moderate for the dynasty's political/military narrative
(strong primary-source triangulation per Roux's synthesis); weak for
Chaldaean/Kaldu tribal ethnography specifically, and for Neo-Babylonian economic and
temple-institution history beyond what is summarized on [[babylon]].

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] confirms and
extends the Roux-derived account above with an explicit structural framing of the
Neo-Babylonian empire and a sharper propaganda-critical reading of its final years.

**"Chaldaean" as a political/tribal, not cleanly ethnic, label.** Van De Mieroop uses
"Chaldaean" consistently as a designation for a set of southern-Babylonian tribal
leaders and dynasties who repeatedly contested the Babylonian throne — Marduk-apla-iddina
II (Merodach-baladan), Mushezib-Marduk, and ultimately Nabopolassar — rather than as a
linguistic or genetic category, directly reinforcing this page's `construct_status:
contested` and the caution against reading "Chaldaean" as a bounded ethnic population.
Chaldaean leaders repeatedly formed the core of anti-Assyrian coalitions sheltering in
the militarily inaccessible southern Babylonian marshes; Nabopolassar himself — a
Chaldaean and former Assyrian official at Uruk — is recorded as having masked his origin
by claiming to be "the son of nobody" on assuming the throne in 626. (Attributed;
Van De Mieroop.)

**The Neo-Babylonian empire as Assyria's administrative successor.** Van De Mieroop
frames the Nabopolassar–Nebuchadnezzar II–Neriglissar–Nabonidus sequence (626–539) not
as a wholly new administrative system but explicitly "as the successor to Assyria" —
continuity of scribal and administrative practice is directly attested at Dur-Katlimmu,
where Assyrian-trained scribes continued their work under the new Babylonian overlord.
He flags an important evidentiary asymmetry, however: unlike Assyria's, Neo-Babylonian
royal archives are not well preserved, so detailed provincial administration is largely
unknown — an absence that should be read as an evidentiary gap, not as evidence the
Neo-Babylonian system was administratively different from its Assyrian predecessor.
Nebuchadnezzar II's reputation as the dynasty's great military leader is flagged by
Van De Mieroop as probably "exaggerated by the nature of the documentation available":
unlike the Assyrians, the Babylonians did not use annals or reliefs to advertise
campaigns, so the real military accomplishments of later kings (Neriglissar's
annexation of Cilicia, Nabonidus's Arabian conquests) are comparatively
under-documented. (Attributed; Van De Mieroop.)

Continued deportation policy under Nebuchadnezzar II — the 597, 587, and 582
deportations of Jerusalem/Judah populations (Jehoiachin and 3,000 leading citizens in
597; a larger deportation after the 587 siege; a third ordered in 582 after governor
Gedaliah's assassination) — is read by Van De Mieroop as evidence Babylonian imperial
rule may in some respects have been "more oppressive" than Assyria's: unlike Assyria's
redevelopment of Samaria, Babylon did not redevelop the conquered southern Levant,
leaving it "uninhabited and the cities in ruin" — attributed by Van De Mieroop to the
self-sufficiency of the Babylonian heartland reducing any incentive to invest in the
periphery. The resulting capital was markedly multi-ethnic (Syro-Palestinian,
Phoenician, Elamite, Persian, Median, Ionian, Cilician, Egyptian communities organized
in named quarters/villages, retaining native names, gods, and languages across
generations while intermarrying) — "there was no cultural isolation, it seems."
(Attributed.)

**The Cyrus Cylinder as propaganda, read critically.** Van De Mieroop treats the Cyrus
Cylinder's presentation of Nabonidus — as having "plotted against the worship of
Marduk," ruined Babylon's people, and imposed corvée, prompting Marduk himself to "seek
a replacement" and "take the hand of Cyrus" — explicitly as Persian-commissioned
propaganda rather than a neutral account, directly reinforcing this page's own
Nabonidus source-reliability caution above. He further notes that the Cylinder's own
internal logic (its passage on returning displaced gods and peoples to their homes east
of the Tigris) actually "casts suspicion on Cyrus's claim that Babylon's conquest was
unopposed" — the conquest likely did harm the population, contrary to its own framing
as a bloodless liberation. Van De Mieroop also explicitly disputes the popular modern
reading of the Cylinder as "the earliest declaration of human rights" (the basis for a
facsimile displayed at the UN): "its actual content is less exciting," and the claim
that Cyrus was "an enlightened promoter of human rights... is contested" among
scholars — Persian policy is better read as pragmatic accommodation than principled
tolerance. (Position, explicitly attributed to Van De Mieroop's own critical stance.)

Administrative continuity persisted into Persian rule for decades: known archives (e.g.
the Egibi banking family) show no break in bureaucratic practice for roughly 55 years
after 539, only the royal name in date formulae changing; Cyrus made his son Cambyses
king of Babylon while continuing ritual participation in the Akitu New Year festival
himself — an explicit accommodation strategy.

**The 484 BCE revolt as the real rupture point.** Van De Mieroop identifies the true
break in old Babylonian civic continuity not at the 539 conquest but at the **484 BCE
revolt under Xerxes**: two rebels, Bel-shimanni and Shamash-eriba, each briefly
proclaimed "king of Babylon" in northern Babylonian cities; the revolt was crushed
within weeks or months, but its suppression destroyed the old temple-linked Babylonian
families' economic power (their archives cease at this point) and replaced them with
newcomer managerial families — a precursor to the later Murashu archive pattern at
Nippur. Xerxes subsequently split Babylonia into two satrapies (Babylonia proper and a
new "Beyond the River," probably capitaled at Damascus). Van De Mieroop treats 539 as
ending Neo-Babylonian imperial *independence* but explicitly not Chaldaean/Babylonian
cultural or economic continuity, which persisted through 484 and only ruptured there.
