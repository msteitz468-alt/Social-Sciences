# Lapita Cultural Complex

## Definition and Construct History

**Lapita** is the archaeological construct for the early Oceanic cultural complex defined
especially by its distinctive **dentate-stamped pottery**, and associated (as a historical
argument, not identity) with the rapid spread of Austronesian-speaking communities into
Remote Oceania. In *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the
Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* (1984), [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] treats Lapita — and
particularly the **Eastern Lapita** sequence in the Fiji–Tonga–Samoa region — as the
immediate cultural foundation from which **[[ancestral-polynesian-society|Ancestral
Polynesian Society]]** (APS) emerged over some 1,500 years of local adaptation.

**Construct history (as of Kirch 1984).** Lapita sherds were first reported by Father Otto
Meyer (1909, Watom, New Britain). McKern excavated decorated Lapita on Tongatapu and ‘Eua
(1921) without grasping Polynesian-origin significance until Gifford and Shutler (1956)
excavated Site 13 at Lapita, New Caledonia, and linked Watom, Site 13, Tonga, and Fiji —
giving the complex its name. Kirch (after Golson 1971; Kirch 1978a, 1981a) uses a
**Lapitoid Ceramic Series** concept emphasizing both horizon and tradition aspects of
spatial and temporal ceramic variability. Golson (1972a:10) is cited: Lapita sites provide
evidence of a community of culture straddling the traditional Melanesia–Polynesia boundary
and antedating its appearance.

The construct is a **material-culture complex**, not an ethnonym; do not equate Lapita pots
with a single “Lapita people,” language, or genetic population without explicit argument
and `associated_with` framing.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

*(Attributed — Kirch 1984 Ch. 3, summarizing then-current research.)*

### Material package

- **Ceramics:** minimally defined by characteristic dentate-stamped pottery; vessels
  well-fired, often calcareous-sand tempered, paddle-and-anvil earthenware. Mead et al.
  showed Lapita design is built from a small set of design elements combined by
  syntactical rules into about 150 motifs; a shared motif subset across the full geographic
  extent testifies to cultural continuity (Green 1978).
- **Portable assemblage:** stone and shell adzes; chert/obsidian flake tools; coral and
  sea-urchin abraders; shell scrapers/peelers; slingstones; simple fishhooks; tattooing
  needles; awls; spear points; shell ornaments (rings, bracelets, beads). Green showed the
  early Polynesian adz kit developed gradually from Lapita prototypes.
- **Settlement features:** sites range ~800–10,000 m² (Green 1979a); basin-shaped earth
  ovens, pits, postmoulds of permanent structures.
- **Economy:** broad-spectrum — horticulture, animal husbandry (pig, dog, chicken in the
  colonizing package), and marine exploitation. Pioneering communities depended heavily on
  marine resources while terrestrial systems established; early sites show unusually large
  shellfish and high species diversity (Poulsen Tongatapu; Swadling Reef Islands; Kirch and
  Yen Tikopia early levels).
- **Exchange:** Ambrose and Green (1972) linked Ambittle and Gawa obsidian to Talasea (New
  Britain) — ~2,000 km to Gawa (Santa Cruz). Subsequent work showed chert, metavolcanic
  adzes, oven stones, and pottery also moved between Lapita communities. Green (1976b)
  suggested the original Lapita adaptation was to a complex continental-island environment
  with wide resource ranges assemblable in individual communities through exchange. Wide
  exchange implies complex social organization that need **not** have been hierarchical —
  possibly horizontal areal networks (Schwartz 1963); Friedman’s hierarchical
  prestige-goods model remained untested as of 1984. Kirch: do not assume ancestral Lapita
  societies were less complex than Polynesian/Melanesian descendants — change was
  **transformation**, not unilinear progress to greater complexity.

### Linguistic association

Most culture-historians (as Kirch reports: Shutler and Marck 1975; Pawley and Green 1975)
associate dispersal of **Eastern Oceanic** languages with the Lapita Cultural Complex.
Eastern Lapita in Fiji–Tonga–Samoa is associated with **Proto-Central Pacific**, which
diverged into Proto-Fijian and Proto-Polynesian over several hundred years after initial
settlement. Proto-Austronesian lexical reconstructions (boats *wangka, outriggers *katiR,
taro, yam, fishhooks, etc.) indicate watercraft, horticulture, and advanced fishing by
perhaps 6000 BP — pre-adaptations carried into Oceania. These are **historical
associations**, not proof of culture = language.

### Genetic signature

Not part of the 1984 definition. Later aDNA/commensal studies (as of post-Kirch 1984 literature) are outside this source.

## Distribution and Chronology

*(Absolute dates as of Kirch 1984 / sources he cites; later radiocarbon programs revise
many figures.)*

- **Span:** Bismarcks through Santa Cruz, New Hebrides (Vanuatu), and New Caledonia to
  Fiji, Tonga, and Samoa; complex spans c. **1600 BC to 500 BC** (Green 1979a as cited).
- **Western colonization of Samoa–Tonga:** Lapita-pottery makers first colonized tropical
  western archipelagos of Samoa and Tonga about **1500 BC** and would become the “first
  Polynesians” (Kirch Ch. 2).
- **Fiji–Tonga–Samoa triangle:** earliest reliable dated Lapita site Natunuku (Viti Levu),
  **3240 ± 100 BP**; by end of second millennium BC permanent colonization of Tongatapu and
  Samoa clear; Kirch judged all three archipelagos plus probably Futuna and ‘Uvea settled
  by **1000 BC**.
- **Andesite line:** entering Fiji–Tonga–Samoa, pioneers crossed into truly oceanic
  conditions — depauperate biota; restricted rock types for tools (Green 1974b).

### Western vs Eastern Lapita split

Despite two-way voyaging capability, Green’s motif analysis revealed a fundamental
distinction between **Western** and **Eastern Lapita** (Fiji, Tonga, Samoa). After initial
settlement the Eastern group became effectively isolated from the Western counterpart
across the ~**850 km** gap between Fiji and the New Hebrides. It is this **Eastern Lapita
population** which, over ~1,500 years of local adaptation, gave rise to APS.

### Eastern Lapita ceramic sequence (Green)

| Phase | Character (as of Kirch 1984) |
|---|---|
| Early Eastern Lapita | Dentate-stamped decoration; wide vessel range |
| Late Eastern Lapita | Loss of early forms/dentate decoration; frequent notched rims |
| Polynesian Plain Ware | Undecorated simple bowls, often crude |
| Pottery abandonment | Completely abandoned in Polynesia c. AD 300 |

Explanations for pottery loss remain speculative (as of 1984); absence of clay/temper can be
ruled out on high islands; change may involve shifting ritual/exchange roles of pottery and
food-preparation methods (Irwin 1981). Viti Levu’s ceramic sequence parallels Polynesian
islands through Late Eastern Lapita, then **diverges** — later Fijian culture-history is
separate from Polynesia (Frost 1979).

Green (1979b) argues some Lapita decorative motifs can be traced into ethnographically
documented barkcloth and tattooing designs; tattooing chisels occur in Tongan sites
(Poulsen 1968).

## Lifeways

Broad-spectrum horticulture–husbandry–marine economy; permanent structures; long-distance
exchange in Western Lapita; Eastern Lapita adaptation to oceanic island constraints with
declining inter-region exchange and increasing self-sufficiency toward APS. Ranked
[[ancestral-polynesian-society|APS]] social forms (hereditary *‘ariki, conical clan) are a
**later** Eastern Lapita / post-Lapita development in Kirch’s model — not a Lapita given,
though Lapita social complexity should not be assumed “simple.”

## Relations and Successions

- `associated_with:` Oceanic / Eastern Oceanic Austronesian languages; Proto-Central
  Pacific in the Eastern Lapita triangle (argued association).
- `preceded_by:` Near Oceanic pre-Lapita traditions.
- `succeeded_by:` APS / Polynesian Plain Ware sequences in West Polynesia; diverse
  post-Lapita Melanesian cultures; divergent post-Late-Eastern-Lapita Fiji.
- Polynesian origins **traced to** Lapita (Green 1978, 1979a as cited) as first major
  intrusion into deeper Oceania beyond New Guinea and immediate neighbours — not as racial
  “wave” identity.

## Identity Cautions

- **Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people.** Lapita is an etic archaeological construct.
  Equations to “Lapita people,” Proto-Oceanic speakers as identical entities, or genetic
  populations require separate argument and attribution.
- “First Polynesians” in Kirch’s phrasing is a culture-historical claim about Eastern
  Lapita descendants becoming Polynesian in West Polynesia — not a timeless ethnonym.

## Historiography

Primary coverage here from Kirch 1984 (esp. Ch. 3), itself synthesizing Green, Golson,
Pawley, Poulsen, Ambrose, Mead et al., and others. By ~1980 (as Kirch reports) consensus
shifted from ≤~1,000 years Polynesian settlement to a time-scale exceeding three millennia
at least in western archipelagos, with single common origin and affinities to eastern
Melanesian materials. A full Lapita literature review (Spriggs, Sheppard, Sand, modern
commensal genetics and revised radiocarbon chronologies beyond the 1984 horizon (as of Kirch 1984) is not yet ingested; densify when
Pacific handbooks or *On the Road of the Winds* are read.

## Update: *On the Road of the Winds* (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]], Ch. 4, synthesizing research to ~2016;
these figures revise the 1984-era dates above.)*

- **Origin and emergence.** Lapita is now read as **intrusive**, not an in-situ Near Oceanic
  development: a fusion in the Bismarck Archipelago, ~**1410–1290 cal BC** (AMS on plant materials,
  avoiding marine-reservoir shell error; Denham, Ramsey, and Specht 2012), of intrusive
  Austronesian-speaking immigrants (ultimately from Taiwan/ISEA) with resident Papuan-speaking
  populations. Kirch adopts **[[green-roger|Green]]'s [[triple-i-model|Triple-I model]]**
  (Intrusion / Innovation / Integration) — dentate-stamping itself is treated as a Near Oceanic
  *innovation*, not an import. See [[express-train-vs-entangled-bank-debate]].
- **Rapid spread.** >230 sites, Bismarcks to Samoa; the full Near-to-Remote expansion (to
  Tonga/Samoa) accomplished in perhaps two centuries — Kirch calls it "one of the most rapid
  population expansions... known anywhere in world history."
- **Five provinces** (cumulative scholarship): Far Western (Bismarcks; Summerhayes 2000), Western
  (SE Solomons–Vanuatu; Green 1979), Southern (New Caledonia–Loyalties; Kirch 1997), Eastern
  (Fiji–Tonga–Samoa; Green 1979), and South Papuan (Caution Bay; David et al. 2011).
- **Revised chronology** (calibrated; type sites): Bismarcks 1410–1290 BC; Reef–Santa Cruz
  (Nenumbo, RF-2) not before ~1050 BC; [[teouma|Teouma]] cemetery ~970–800 BC; Nukuleka
  (Tongatapu) 888 ± 8 BC (U/Th on coral abraders, Burley et al. 2012); Mulifanua (Samoa) ~930–800 BC.
- **aDNA and the four-way non-identity.** Teouma/Talasiu genomes (n=4, all female) show
  Taiwanese/Philippine affinity with **no detectable Papuan ancestry** at initial Remote Oceania
  settlement — "the Papuan genetic signature appeared... only subsequent to initial settlement"
  (**as of Skoglund et al. 2016**); cranial morphology concurs (Valentin et al. 2016). Y-chromosome
  data show a Near Oceanic–origin dominant Polynesian haplotype (Kayser et al.), reconciled with
  the mtDNA "Polynesian motif" via a matrilocal-recruitment model. Lapita (culture),
  Oceanic/Austronesian (language), and this genetic signature remain **`associated_with`**, not
  identical — Kirch states the equation as well-supported *for Lapita specifically* while noting
  archaeologists' general caution about equating pots with people.
- **Type-site discovery narrative** (Ch. 1): sherds first noted by Meyer (1909, Watom) and McKern
  (1920, Tonga, misidentified) but not understood until Gifford and Shutler excavated the Lapita
  type site (New Caledonia) in 1952 and radiocarbon-dated it (2800 ± 350 BP).

Key associated sites now on the wiki: [[talepakemalai|Talepakemalai]] (Mussau — largest Lapita
site, waterlogged stilt-house preservation) and [[teouma|Teouma]] (Vanuatu — the Lapita cemetery
supplying the aDNA).

## Reflexivity

“Lapita” is an archaeological label that can silently become a people. This page keeps
culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ polity distinct. Colonial and missionary-era ethnography of
descendant societies is a separate evidence class from Lapita archaeology. Absolute dates
and settlement windows are **1984-era** positions subject to supersession.
