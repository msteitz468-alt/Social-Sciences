# Still Bay Industry

Etic **Middle Stone Age** techno-complex of southern Africa, best known from sites such as [[blombos-cave|Blombos Cave]]. In *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|The Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology]]* (2013), Marlize Lombard (Ch. 26) reports reinstatement of the Still Bay with ages converging at about **70–75 kya**, **pre-dating** the [[howiesons-poort-industry|Howiesons Poort]] (~59–65 kya as of that synthesis).

## Definition and Construct History

Still Bay is a **material-culture construct** (bifacial points, associated MSA technologies), not a people, language, or genetic population. Lombard places it among four southern African techno-complexes (Klasies River, Mossel Bay, Still Bay, Howiesons Poort) that show technological **continuity with internal variation** rather than a stagnant MSA (Lombard Ch. 26, attributed; as of handbook ~2012).

## Material Signature

- Bifacial points and related Still Bay lithic package (site-level detail densified on [[blombos-cave]], [[sibudu-cave]]).
- Associated behavioral-complexity evidence in the same time window (attributed to specialists via Lombard): bone tools at Blombos (~72–80 kya); personal ornaments (*Nassarius* beads ~72 kya); heat treatment and compound adhesives discussed for related MSA contexts (see [[behavioral-modernity]]).

## Distribution and Chronology

Southern Africa, late Pleistocene MSA. Chronology is OSL- and stratigraphy-led and **date-stamped to the handbook’s ~2012 horizon** — later publications may refine ages.

## Lifeways

Hunter-gatherer MSA economies; specific subsistence and cognition arguments are attributed on site and concept pages (Lombard Ch. 26).

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by` earlier MSA substages; `succeeded_by` [[howiesons-poort-industry]]. Continuity arguments challenge models of post-Howiesons Poort “behavioural reversal” (Lombard, attributed).

## Identity Cautions

Still Bay ≠ San/Khoekhoe; ≠ *Homo sapiens* as a whole; ≠ language family. Link only as `associated_with` when bridging evidence classes.

## Historiography

Reinstatement after periods of definitional doubt is part of early-21st-century southern African MSA research (Henshilwood, Villa, Jacobs, Wurz via Lombard 2013). Source: [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]].

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Marlize Lombard (Ch. 26), Christian Tryon (Ch. 8) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**. Frontmatter chronology already from this source; this section densifies technique and site associations without re-counting the ingest.*

Lombard reports reinstatement of the Still Bay with ages converging ~**70–75 kya**, pre-dating Howiesons Poort (~59–65 kya). Among the four southern African techno-complexes (Klasies River, Mossel Bay, Still Bay, Howiesons Poort), she argues technological continuity and internal variation rather than a stagnant MSA. Still Bay time-window finds she affirms include the MSA **bone-tool industry** at [[blombos-cave]] (~72–80 kya: awls, polished points, one tanged) and bone points at [[sibudu-cave]] (64–61 kya, one possible arrowhead); the region’s oldest well-supported beads are *Nassarius kraussianus* from Blombos ~72 kya; six *Afrolittorina africana* shells (three perforated) at Sibudu ~71 kya may also be beads. Blombos engraved ochre nodules (~72–100 kya) belong to a geometric engraving tradition cited also at Pinnacle Point, Klein Kliphuis, Klasies bone, and Diepkloof eggshell.

Tryon (Ch. 8) notes soft-hammer evidence includes ochre/haematite traces on ~70 ka Still Bay points at Sibudu, and that **pressure flaking** earliest appears for final shaping of Still Bay points >70 ka (Mourre et al. 2010). Residue/microwear/experiment progress on Still Bay and HP hafting is flagged as a methodological advance. His inventory figure uses a Still Bay point from Blombos as the type exemplar. Four-way non-identity: Still Bay is a lithic construct, not a people, language, or genetic cluster of living southern Africans.
