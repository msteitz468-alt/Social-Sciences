# Nok Culture

Central / northern Nigerian archaeological culture known especially for terracotta sculpture; treated only **briefly** in [[connah-graham|Graham Connah]]’s *[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]* (3rd ed. 2016). **Etic construct** — culture ≠ language ≠ people. This page is densified honestly from a thin Connah 2016 footprint; deeper Nok chronologies, settlement systems, and iron-smelting debates in the specialist literature are outside this ingest.

## Definition and Construct History

In Connah 2016, “Nok” appears mainly as a **named node in a West African savanna ceramic/figurine craft tradition**, not as a fully developed case study of urbanization or state formation. Ch. 6 situates high-quality first-millennium AD savanna pottery and animal/human figurines (especially Chad basin and Inland Niger Delta) within a longer regional tradition “of which the mainly first-millennium BC terracottas from Nok in northern Nigeria are perhaps the most remarkable examples.” Nok is listed among map sites in the West African savanna discussion but is **not** used as an explanatory case for forest-state origins in Ch. 7 (where early iron/food-producing references for forest fringes point instead to Kintampo, Hani/Bono Manso iron dates, Nsukka/Cameroon iron, and Iwo Eleru).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (Connah 2016):**
- **Terracottas** — mainly first-millennium BC; presented as outstanding products of savanna ceramic craftsmanship and figuration.
- **Iron** — Connah reports iron in West Africa by the mid-first millennium BC (and copper smelting by a similar date) as a regional technological background; he does not develop a site-by-site Nok iron sequence in the synthesis chapters densified here.
- Settlement layout, subsistence package, and burial programs for Nok itself are **not detailed** in the Connah 2016 claims extracted for this wiki densification.

**Linguistic / genetic:** Not established by this source. Do not equate Nok terracottas with any modern ethnic group or language family.

## Distribution and Chronology

- **Distribution (as named):** Nok in northern Nigeria, within the broader West African savanna ceramic tradition that also includes later Chad basin and Inland Niger Delta figurines.
- **Chronology (as given):** “mainly first-millennium BC” for the terracottas. Connah’s wider West African iron horizon is “by mid-first millennium BC.” Absolute start/end of a “Nok Culture” package, phase subdivisions, and radiocarbon debates standard in specialist Nok research are **not** laid out in the 2016 passages used here.
- **Thinness flag:** No Connah 2016 claim set in this ingest develops Nok settlement hierarchies, urban claims, or state-formation arguments.

## Lifeways

**Not reconstructed in Connah 2016 for Nok specifically.** Indirect regional context only: savanna craft specialization (pottery, figurines, iron, copper); food-production and surplus economies discussed for other savanna and forest-fringe cases (Jenné-jeno, Chad basin, forest yam–oil palm systems) without equating those packages to Nok.

## Relations and Successions

- Placed as an **early remarkable instance** of a craft tradition that continues in later savanna ceramic and figurine production—not as a documented political ancestor of second-millennium forest city-states (Ife, Benin, Akan, etc.).
- Book plan (Ch. 1) lists `nok-culture` among type-site targets for West African chapters, but the actual narrative load falls on other sites.
- **Preceded by / succeeded by:** [[unknown]] in this source.

## Identity Cautions

- **Culture ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes.** Terracotta style labels do not identify ethnic or linguistic groups.
- Do not project modern Nigerian ethnolinguistic maps onto first-millennium BC Nok finds without separate argument.
- Do not use Connah’s brief terracotta mention to claim Nok as a primary case of African urbanism or state formation—he does not make that claim.

## Historiography

Within Connah 2016, Nok functions as a **craft-historical landmark** in the West African Iron Age / ceramic narrative, not as a full comparative city/state case. Specialist Nok archaeology (extended radiocarbon series, iron-smelting furnaces, settlement surveys, looting/heritage debates) largely post-dates or lies outside the synthesis depth Connah gives this label. ## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — Detlef Gronenborn in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]] Ch. 58; thin densification—handbook mentions Nok as chronological **parallel** for complexity, not as full site monograph. **as of 2013**.)*

### Chad Basin parallel (mid-first millennium BC)

First complex settlement systems west of Lake Chad mid-first millennium BC (extensive nuclear sites, possible lake-transgression congestion). **Complexity also emerges then in central Nigeria with the Nok Culture** (Gronenborn 2013). Permanent villages with mixed pastoralism/horticulture by ~AD 800 at Kursakata/Mege (regional sequence, not Nok itself).

This placement treats Nok as a **marker of early Iron Age / complex settlement timing** in central Nigeria alongside Chad Basin developments—not as a developed case of urbanization or state formation in the handbook chapter. Do **not** promote Nok to primary state-formation exemplar from this mention alone (aligns with thin Connah 2016 craft-landmark treatment above).

### Central Sahel statehood conclusions (context)

Gronenborn: traditional historiography saw central Sahel state landscape emerging via southbound Islam and associated trade (Bovill 1970). External influences cannot be neglected, but **internal developments** increasingly regarded as equally significant; mid-1st-millennium BC walled settlements and possible hierarchical structures in first millennium cal. BC–early AD **challenge external-origin-only models**. Nok’s chronological co-occurrence with early Chad Basin complexity is part of that internal-deep-time argument package—not evidence that Nok *caused* Kanem-Borno or Hausa city-states.
