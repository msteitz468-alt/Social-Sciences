## Definition and Construct History

Dvaravati is Higham’s (2002) label for the early historic Buddhist (and partly Vaishnava) cultural–political complex of the Chao Phraya and associated flood plains of central Thailand. The name is epigraphic/numismatic: two silver medallions under a Nakhon Pathom sanctuary proclaim “the meritorious work of the King of Sri Dvaravati” in 7th-century south Indian characters; coins from Muang Dongkorn also refer to the King of Dvaravati. *Dvaravati* means “which has gates” (as of Higham 2002; R5-033).

Higham characterises early agrarian states of mainland Southeast Asia as firmly based on local traditions under leaders who adopted aspects of Hinduism/Buddhism and Sanskrit names — an “Indic veneer to an essentially indigenous growth of civilisation” exemplified by Angkor, Dvaravati, and Champa (R1-033; R5-062). Dvaravati is thus filed as a culture/complex with political claims, not as proof of a single ethnic kingdom.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Major centres share moat and bank of irregular plan (oval/sub-rectangular), usually stream-fed — markedly different from rectangular Mekong Valley geometry. Religious buildings of laterite/brick include stupas and caityas inside and outside moats, with stucco decoration (R5-035).

Key centres (Higham synthesis; R5-036):

- **U-Thong** — oval moat 1,690 × 840 m; mostly Buddhist structures; Harshavarman lion-throne copper inscription; often treated as early royal capital.
- **Nakhon Pathom** — largest (~3,700 × 2,000 m); Dvaravati king medallions; royal-symbol terracotta trays (possible investiture).
- **Ku Bua** — 2,000 × 800 m; many religious structures; stucco of royalty, musicians, bound prisoners, possible foreign traders.
- **Sri Thep** — moats enlarged to 4.7 km²; 6th-c.+ occupation; Buddhism and strong Hinduism; strategic Mun/Chenla contact.
- **Bang Pakong corridor** — Muang Phra Rot, Dong Si Mahosod control Gulf–Tonle Sap route; Ban Di Lung specialised iron smelting 6th–7th c. (Suchitta 1985).

**Formation continuity.** Chansen six phases: prehistoric 800 BC–AD 200/250 through Oc Eo-parallel material (tin amulets, stamps, bronze bells, stone moulds) to period 5 circular moat and Dvaravati-style pottery — continuity to 7th-c. establishment (Bronson & Dales; R5-034). Ban Tha Kae and [[ban-don-ta-phet]] show Iron Age Indian glass/agate/carnelian and later Oc Eo parallels (R5-032). Geographic setting: rice plains, ore-poor but nodal for India via Three Pagodas Pass; Roman coin of Victorinus (AD 268–70) at U-Thong (R5-031).

**Language.** Mon indigenous language in inscriptions; Sanskrit sovereign names; Buddhism and Vishnu worship (R5-033). Higham does **not** equate Mon language, Dvaravati material culture, and a single ethnic “people.”

**Genetics.** Not addressed.

## Distribution and Chronology

Chao Phraya, Mae Khlong, Ta Chin, Bang Pakong, and Pa Sak flood plains; late prehistoric–early historic higher sea level meant now-inland sites had more direct maritime access (R5-031). Upper Mun and Chi valleys show parallel Dvaravati-related monuments (Muang Fa Daet ~171 ha; Muang Sema rival size), while middle/lower Mun is more integral with Chenla brick shrines — Higham hypothesises possible prehistoric roots in Khmer speakers middle/lower Mun vs Mon speakers upper Mun/Chi (**hypothesis, not proven identity**; R5-040).

Political chronology: mid-7th-c.+ large moated Buddhist centres; mid-7th-c. U-Thong copper inscription of Sri Harshavarman, grandson of Ishanavarman (unclear if same as Ishanapura’s king); Sri Thep accession text invoking Bhavavarman comparison (R5-037). Later sources name Lavo/Lopburi rather than Dvaravati (R5-039).

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Agrarian rice economy on flood plains; specialised iron smelting at Ban Di Lung; craft and trade continuity from Iron Age coastal exchange. Ruling-class conflict is evidenced iconographically (bound prisoners at Ku Bua) and by army/prisoner themes at U-Thong and Nakhon Pathom (R5-039). Religious economy centres on Buddhist foundations with Hindu practice also present (especially Pa Sak/Bang Pakong and Sri Thep).

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by` Iron Age Chao Phraya trading communities. `associated_with` Funan/Oc Eo material parallels at Chansen and Ban Tha Kae, and with Chenla/Mun political geography via inscriptions and monument styles — not conquest identity. Chinese and Funan texts describe Gulf of Siam maritime hegemony (Fan-man of Funan; Dunsun as ocean mart) as a prior coastal order (locations often uncertain; R5-038). Higham’s five-phase framework places Dvaravati among early states by AD 100–200 onward with Indic veneer (R1-029).

## Identity Cautions

Keep separate:

1. **Archaeological complex** — moated irregular towns, stupas/caityas, Dvaravati art style  
2. **Numismatic/epigraphic “King of Sri Dvaravati”** — may name a paramount or a style of kingship, not proven unitary territorial state  
3. **Mon vernacular** — language of some inscriptions, not a census of all inhabitants  
4. **Any Thai or Mon modern ethnonym** projected backward  

Higham explicitly: existence of a state encompassing the entire Chao Phraya–Bang Pakong–Mae Khlong region “while feasible, is not yet based on any firm evidence”; truth may be periods of unity under vigorous rulers punctuated by fragmentation, as in the Mekong Valley (R5-039). Quaritch Wales’s Western/Central/Eastern groups are geographic, not assumed political units (R5-035). Khorat founding folklore is a “small thread” only (R5-041).

## Historiography

Colonial and mid-20th-century scholarship (Quaritch Wales and others) often assumed a Dvaravati kingdom with capital first at U-Thong then Nakhon Pathom and expansion into Mun and Chi valleys. Higham 2002 retains the name for a real archaeological and epigraphic phenomenon while refusing unproven unitary-state and expansion theses. Fits his broader anti-Coedès revision: Indianisation as selective adoption with adaptive potential, not forced blotting-paper absorption (R5-055, R5-062).
