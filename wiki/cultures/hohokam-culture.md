# Hohokam Culture

## Definition and Construct History

**Hohokam** (a Pima/O'odham word usually glossed "those who have gone") is the
archaeological culture of the Gila and Salt River basins of southern Arizona,
distinguished by **canal irrigation**, red-on-buff pottery made by the paddle-and-anvil
technique, ballcourts, platform mounds, and cremation burial. As presented by
[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], it is one of the four major traditions
of the prehistoric [[ancestral-pueblo-culture|Southwest]] (with Anasazi, Mogollon, and
Patayan), developing in the lower Sonoran desert from about AD 400 and reaching its apogee
in a "Classic period" c. AD 1100–1450.

`construct_status: conventional` — Hohokam is an etic archaeological construct; Emil Haury
and others argued the historic **Pima (Akimel O'odham)** descend from the Hohokam, but this
is an argued continuity, not an established identity (enforce the four-way non-identity
rule). Chronology rests on ceramic seriation and radiocarbon, not the tree-ring precision
available on the Colorado Plateau.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Fagan 1991).** Canal-irrigation agriculture on the Gila/Salt terraces;
red-on-buff pottery; large pithouses giving way to above-ground adobe in the Classic
period; **ballcourts** (floors excavated ~0.5 m below the desert surface) and clay-capped
**platform mounds** that Haury compared to Mesoamerican forms; extensive trade in marine
shell (Strombus trumpets, bracelets, beads), copper bells, and macaws reaching "far into
Mexico." A later **Lower Sonoran Agricultural Complex** (heat-tolerant, irrigation-dependent
crops — sieva beans, cushaw squash, cotton) arrived from Mexico c. AD 300–500 and was
adopted by the already-irrigating Hohokam.

## Distribution and Chronology

The Hohokam interaction sphere is defined by ballcourt distribution — from Gila Bend in the
west to Point of Pines in the east, Tucson in the south to Flagstaff in the north — enclosing
considerable cultural diversity of farming communities. [[snaketown|Snaketown]], the type
site on the Gila, went through seven phases (earliest c. AD 500) and declined and was
abandoned in the 15th century. At later sites such as Casa Grande (Arizona — **not** Casas
Grandes/Paquimé in Chihuahua) Anasazi influence appears mixed with Hohokam material.

## Lifeways

Hohokam society was organized around a settlement hierarchy of large canal-irrigating
villages and smaller rancherias. Fagan records the migrationist model (Haury 1976: exotica,
ballcourts, and platform mounds introduced by Mexican immigrants) as **superseded** by "more
recent fieldwork" (Fish and Fish 1977; Wilcox 1985) supporting indigenous roots in the
Cochise Archaic — Hohokam as a local culture gaining complexity over time. Whether the formal
post–AD 1000 trade network reflects a shared religious/belief system is left open (attributed,
speculative).

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from (argued):** the Southwestern Archaic (Cochise tradition) — an indigenous-
  origin reading that replaced Haury's earlier Mexican-migration model.
- **Associated with (not identity):** Mesoamerica, via ballcourts, macaws, copper bells, and
  the Lower Sonoran crop complex — trade and stimulus, not colonization; Fagan flags the
  ballcourt–Mesoamerica link as explicitly uncertain.
- **Contrasts with:** [[ancestral-pueblo-culture|Anasazi]] (masonry pueblos, dry/floodwater
  farming, dendro-dated) and [[mogollon-culture|Mogollon]] (mountain pithouse-to-pueblo).

## Identity Cautions

- **Culture ≠ people.** The Pima-descent claim is argued, not demonstrated.
- **Not a Mesoamerican colony.** Mesoamerican traits are `associated_with`, evidence of
  exchange, not political incorporation.

## Lower Verde / Transition Zone (as of Whittlesey et al. 1997)

*[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* (LVAP) densifies Hohokam research outside the Phoenix Basin core. Key attributed points:

- **Regional models.** Gladwinian expansion, [[hohokam-core-periphery-model|core–periphery]], and regional-system frameworks all assume a Gila–Salt center and peripheral colonization/dependency; Whittlesey (Ch. 15) argues these heuristics fail for central Arizona given diversity among “peripheries” and weak economic core-dependency evidence.
- **Lower Verde chronology (Deaver):** Period 1 ~AD 700–950; hiatus ~950–1000; Period 2 ~1000–1125; Classic/Period 3 ~1125–1360 in project contexts — local radiocarbon/archaeomagnetic grounding rather than pure Phoenix borrowal.
- **Trait attenuation:** [[scorpion-point-village|Scorpion Point Village]] buff ware ~4.2% of ceramics; small informal ballcourts; courtyard groups present; cotton ubiquity high enough to suggest exchange production — “Hohokam” participation without monothetic core copies.
- **Classic:** [[roadhouse-ruin|Roadhouse Ruin]] masonry compounds with almost no local decorated pottery; project-area Classic peak population modeled ~750 (Ciolek-Torrello).
- **Agriculture:** Van West & Altschul model Horseshoe Basin irrigation/carrying capacity (see [[lower-verde-archaeological-project-study|LVAP study]]).

`associated_with` (not identity): [[sinagua-culture|Sinagua]] labels in the Verde; later [[yavapai|Yavapai]] and [[western-apache|Western Apache]] historical occupation of the same valleys.

## Historiography

From [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]], a dated first-edition synthesis;
Emil Haury's Snaketown excavations (1934–35, 1964–65) anchor the sequence. The
migration-vs-indigenous-origins question and the degree of Mesoamerican influence remain
central. [[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Whittlesey et al. 1997]] supplies a CRM-era
critique of core–periphery and a Transition Zone case study (as of Whittlesey et al. 1997);
later genetic and canal studies still needed for further densification.
