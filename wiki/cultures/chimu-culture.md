## Definition and Construct History

**Chimor** (Chimú) is the Late Intermediate Period coastal state governed from [[chan-chan]] in the Moche Valley—the second-largest native South American state documentable by both archaeology and ethnohistory (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]). At its height it embraced ~1,000 km of Pacific coast (~two-thirds of irrigated desert land and, by inference, coastal population), synthesizing the Maritime-Oasis lowland trajectory and integrating the northern demographic pole. Moseley frames Chimor/Chan Chan as the evolutionary climax of lowland development, geopolitically pitted against highland [[inca-empire|Incas]]/[[cuzco]] until protracted drought tipped the balance and Cuzco prevailed (attributed dual-trajectory climax).

Spanish writers recorded dual founder dynasties: **Taycanamu** at Chan Chan (centralized-rule lore) and **Naymlap** of Lambayeque (confederacy lore later incorporated)—“dynasty” is misleading; Moseley prefers dual rulerships compressed mythically into 10–12 generations spanning many archaeological centuries (as of Moseley 2001). See [[sican-culture]] for Naymlap/Sicán archaeology.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Capital [[chan-chan]]:** Northern city wall brackets ~20 km² of valley mouth; dense civic center ~6 km². Class/occupation by architecture: majority craftsmen/women in cane patio/irregular rooms (wood, lapidary, metallurgy, weaving)—estimated ~26,000 craftsmen/women at height + ~3,000 serving royal enclosures; farmers and fishermen excluded—city exclusively for rule/governance; urban elite minority ≤~6,000 in lesser-noble compounds and palatial enclosures (as of Moseley 2001).

**Ciudadelas:** ~9–11 monumental enclosures roughly matching 9–11 pre-Inca rulers plus evidence of razed/buried compounds. Sequence: early multi-compound complexes (Tello, Uhle); middle phase c. 1125–1350 standardization; late phase c. 1350–1470 four highly repetitive *ciudadelas*; pairing may reflect dual rural and moiety organization splitting the metropolis east/west. Final pair Rivero and Tschudi—Tschudi yielded rare Chimu-Inca ceramics, candidate for Minchançaman’s court. Thick three-story walls in [[mita-labor|mit’a]] segments; single-file northern gateway; northern/central sectors with friezes, ramps, *audiencias*, warehouses (wealth finance); southern retainer cane quarters.

**Audiencias:** U-shaped offices (~4 m square, niches, gabled roofs) of god-kings and nobility—recalling Initial Period U-shaped centers but housing rulers not gods. Sea creatures and maritime adobe friezes dominate imperial interiors; sea as primary deity; tide-pulling moon considered more potent than sun—elaboration of Moche V maritime themes after drought (as of Moseley 2001).

**Huacas sepulturas:** Multi-story burial platforms in high-walled courts; late Tschudi-type cells around T-shaped chamber for principal corpse; looters’ dregs and Spanish docs show Spondylus/Conus, llama bones, juvenile human sacrifices (largely young women), fine textiles, precious metal. Each late platform as mausoleum of the ruler who built the compound—apex of Chimor “glued together by corpses and ancestor veneration” as with the [[ayllu]] (as of Moseley 2001). Wooden *ciudadela* models with processions to mummy bundles.

**Sicán/Lambayeque relationship:** After Pampa Grande’s end, [[sican-culture|Sicán]] synthesized Moche, Huari, and local styles (Sicán Lord icon; Batán Grande religious-funerary capital; Túcume after AD 1100 conflagration). Chimor incorporated Lambayeque generations before Minchançaman’s final expansion; ~10,000 skilled northern metallurgists and craftspeople brought into Chan Chan; provincial centers (e.g. Farfán) with *audiencias*; later, governors ruled through local kuraka without royal burial platforms (kings monopolized platform burial) (as of Moseley 2001).

**Linguistic / genetic:** Not established — [[unknown]].

## Distribution and Chronology

**Staged expansion (lore + archaeology):** Taycanamu arrives by boat, settles Moche Valley; son subjugates lower drainage; grandson Nancenpinco consolidates upstream then first external expansion Santa to Jequetepeque; 5–7 unnamed rulers; Minchançaman second expansion Chillón through Tumbes, defeated by Incas, taken to Cuzco. Alternate: General Pacatnamu subdues Jequetepeque, builds Farfán. Archaeology: first stage composite incremental conquests over generations; Lambayeque incorporated generations before Minchançaman; after AD 1100 floods rendered reclamation inoperative, lords turned to obtaining land by force (as of Moseley 2001).

**Water crisis:** City growth tied to walk-down wells and canal-recharged aquifer; AD 1100 El Niño damaged city and Moche irrigation; failed >70 km Chicama inter-valley canal (runs uphill at active fault); sunken gardens fell far short of replacing lost irrigated land; late prehistoric coastal population decline (as of Moseley 2001).

**Inca conquest and dismemberment c. AD 1470:** Bitterly contested; Incas exiled hostile groups, carved nation into independent señoríos loyal to Cuzco; Chan Chan artisans moved to Cuzco; at least one violent coastal revolt; smallpox and first New World pandemics further ravaged survivors—conquistadors met fragmentary dynastic accounts. Last great enclosure Chiquitoy Viejo built under Inca aegis south Chicama controlling the road to the former capital. Ethnohistory: close Inca–Ichma military alliance (central coast, [[pachacamac]]) for attacking Chimor (as of Moseley 2001).

## Lifeways

Intensive irrigation agriculture across desert valleys; wealth finance via craft specialization and storage of elite goods; staple support for mit’a and retainers. Maritime religion and fishing hinterland economies (excluded from capital residence). Dual organization at capital and kuraka dual rule at lower scales. Corporate Chimu style after Galindo abandonment and Huari–local melding.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** Moche (especially Phase V Galindo enclosure as early huaca-sepultura pattern); Sicán/Lambayeque confederation.
- **Peer rival:** Highland Incas—drought-era demographic/resource asymmetry favors sierra (as of Moseley 2001).
- **Succeeded by:** Inca provincial order; craft transfer to [[cuzco]]; residual señoríos.
- **associated_with:** [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] dual-trajectory climax; [[sican-culture]]; [[chan-chan]]; [[pachacamac]] (Ichma alliance).

## Identity Cautions

Chimú/Chimor is a polity and material-culture construct. Do not equate the state with a single language or genetic population. Sicán/Lambayeque and Chimor are related successive/overlapping north-coast formations—not identity. Taycanamu/Naymlap narratives are compressed ethnohistoric lore, not year-by-year chronicles.

## Royal Ciudadelas as "Houses" (Moore 2005, attributed)

[[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore (2005)]] argues the ten Chan Chan ciudadelas materially qualify as [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s "noble [[house-society|houses]]": bounded corporate space, accumulated storage wealth, and perpetuated identity through burial and sequential rebuilding. He reads this as evidence of a Chimú polity in transition from kin-based toward territorial-based social membership, cut short by Inca conquest, and attributes the ciudadelas' massive walling to "an ideology of separation" marking the king as a distinct order of being (Moore 1996b, 2004, as reported in Moore 2005). See [[chan-chan]] for the ciudadela-by-ciudadela evidence and the Rowe (1995) vs. Day/Conrad/Moore/Mackey occupation debate.

Litter-bearing and ramp-based (rather than stair-based) royal movement is attested in Chimú ethnohistory: a litter-bearer noble from the Changuco valley testified he served king Chimu Cápac (Rostworowski 1990, via Moore 2005); litter-bearing by North Coast lords persisted into the Colonial period until prohibited by the Spanish (Netherly 1976). Chimú royal compounds used ramps rather than stairs, consistent with litter-borne rulers (attributed).

An intrusive Chimú burial at Huaca de la Luna (Moche site), excavated under Santiago Uceda (1999), yielded carved miniature wooden funerary models (*maquetas*) depicting a plaza ceremony, two funeral corteges, and two sacrificial processions. Uceda (1999) interpreted the plaza-ceremony scene — mummies venerated with chicha and music — as analogous to Inca practice of periodically feting royal mummies years after death; Moore (2004) disagrees, arguing the ceremony likely occurred soon after death because Chimú tombs were permanently sealed and mummies irretrievable. Recorded as a timing dispute, not adjudicated. Musicians in the model (drummers, horn players, a possible "conductor") flank the plaza's corners, while the funeral corteges themselves are silent — paralleling a noisy-fiesta/silent-cortege contrast also documented in Inca royal funerals (Moore 2005, attributed).

## Historiography

Chan Chan–Moche Valley Project and Moseley’s long engagement shape the ciudadela, craft-demography, and hydraulic narratives. Dual-dynasty lore vs radiocarbon/architectural stages is a classic ethnohistory–archaeology tension. Dates for Lambayeque incorporation and Túcume’s Chimor conquest have gaps/OCR issues in some printed lines—prefer archaeological stages over single lore years.
