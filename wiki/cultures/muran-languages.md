# Muran Languages (Mura–Pirahã)

**Muran** (Mura–Pirahã) is a small language grouping of the Madeira basin in Amazonas, Brazil. Its only living representative is **[[piraha|Pirahã]]**; the closely related **Mura** proper is no longer spoken (its former speakers now speak Portuguese). [[everett-daniel|Everett]] treats Pirahã and Mura as "two very similar dialects of a single language," and the surviving language as **a language isolate in practice** — not demonstrably related to any other living language.

> **Four-way non-identity.** Muran is a **linguistic** construct. It is not the same object as the [[piraha|Pirahã people]] (an ethnographic community), a genetic population, or any archaeological culture of the Maici. Everett's suggestion that the Pirahã may not be autochthonous rests on a single Tupi-Guarani loanword (*paguacu*, a monkey species) and is his own inference, attributed and not established.

## Definition and Construct History

Early explorers knew the group as **Mura-Pirahã**, two dialects of one language. The ethnologist Curt Nimuendajú (obs. 1922; "The Mura and Pirahã," *Handbook of South American Indians*, 1963) grouped the Pirahã as "the least acculturated Mura tribe." SIL missionary-linguists **Arlo Heinrichs** and **Steve Sheldon** produced the first modern phonemic and lexical description from the 1960s; Everett (from 1977) extended and partly revised it. The classification as **isolate** is Everett's: he calls Joseph Greenberg's assignment of the group to Macro-Chibcha "nearly impossible to evaluate," and notes there is "no evidence that the Pirahãs have ever given up one of their own words to borrow a word from another language" — a resistance to borrowing (attributed) that complicates external classification.

## Linguistic Signature

All claims below are **attributed to Everett** and are the object of [[piraha-recursion-debate|active dispute]]; they are single-observer analyses, not settled typological facts.

- **Phonology.** One of the smallest phoneme inventories known: men's speech 3 vowels (i, a, o) + 8 consonants = **11 phonemes** (tying Rotokas and Hawaiian); women's speech 10 (h absorbs s). Every vowel bears high or low **tone** (minimal pair *bagiái* "friend" / *bágiái* "enemy"). Words are correspondingly long. Everett argues the small inventory is possible because prosody (tone, stress, syllable weight) carries heavy load — and links it (attributed) to the discourse channels below.
- **The five channels of discourse** (Everett borrows [[hymes-dell|Hymes]]'s "channel" terminology): normal, **whistle** (men, hunting), **hum** (privacy, mothers to infants), **musical/sung** (new/important information, spirit talk, dancing), and **yell** speech (distance, rain). Prosodic information survives when segments are stripped away, enabling whistle and hum communication.
- **Morphology and syntax.** OV word order; verbs can carry up to sixteen suffixes (up to 2¹⁶ possible forms); three evidential suffixes (hearsay / observation / deduction) at the verb's end. Exocentric **spatial orientation** ("upriver/downriver" rather than left/right) — Everett applies [[unknown|Levinson]]'s endocentric/exocentric typology.
- **Reported absences.** No grammatical number/plural (Everett cites Corbett's survey calling this unique); no number words or counting (**anumeric**); no colour lexemes (only descriptive phrases: "it is blood" = red); no quantifiers (*all/every*); no perfect tense; and — the flashpoint — no sentential [[recursion]] (embedding), which Everett argues is expressed instead by juxtaposed independent sentences (parataxis). Everett stresses recursion *does* appear in Pirahã **discourse/story structure** even while (he claims) absent from sentence grammar.

## Distribution and Chronology

Confined to the Maici River (~50 miles from mouth to the Transamazon crossing). Mura proper ranged more widely in the Madeira basin historically but underwent language shift to Portuguese. Pirahã shows **no shift** — the community is uninterested in Portuguese — so the language's endangerment tracks the [[piraha|people's]] demographic fragility, not competition from a national language.

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

As a linguistic construct, Muran carries no lifeways of its own; the lifeways of its speakers are documented on the [[piraha|Pirahã]] society page (foraging economy, riverine settlement on the Maici, the discourse channels used in hunting and privacy). The language's features that most directly index lifeway — whistle speech for hunting, the "spear fish / pull out fish by hand" verb specificity, exocentric river-based orientation — are recorded under Linguistic Signature above and on [[piraha]].

## Relations and Successions

Pirahã and **Mura** proper are treated by Everett as dialects of one language (`associated_with`, not identity, with any people or gene pool); Mura proper underwent language shift to Portuguese, so the family has no living successor beyond Pirahã. External genetic relationships are undemonstrated: Everett rejects Greenberg's Macro-Chibcha assignment and treats the surviving language as an **isolate**. No `descends_from` claim to another family is asserted.

## Identity Cautions

"Muran" and "Pirahã" as language labels do not entail a bounded people or a genetic lineage; the extinction of Mura *proper* is language shift, not the disappearance of descendants. The isolate classification is a statement about *demonstrable* relatedness given current evidence, not a claim of primordial uniqueness.

## Historiography

The description rests overwhelmingly on Everett; independent verification is partial (Ladefoged on stress; Peter Gordon on numerical cognition; Corbett cited on grammatical number). Everett's wife Keren Everett reached partly divergent conclusions on Pirahã prosody, and the recursion analysis is formally contested in the post-2008 technical literature (not yet ingested here) — see [[piraha-recursion-debate]].
