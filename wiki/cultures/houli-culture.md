# Houli Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Houli culture, dated ca. 6500–5500 BCE per [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu &
Chen (2012)]], comprises about a dozen known sites on floodplains along the northern
piedmont of the Taiyi Mountains in Shandong. Key sites include the type site Houli
(Linzi), Xiaojingshan and Xihe (Zhangqiu), Qianbuxia (Weifang), and Yuezhuang (Jinan).
`construct_status: robust` as a regionally-defined etic material-culture construct.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** Xiaojingshan (12 ha), the only ditched Houli
site known, had at least 40 house foundations and around 30 tombs arranged in three
clusters. Xihe had 19 houses of two types: a large, well-built form (25–50 m²) and a
smaller, apparently unfinished form (10–20 m²). Grinding stones dominate lithic
assemblages at Yuezhuang (77 percent, N=136) and Xiaojingshan (57 percent, N=46).
Carbonized millet and rice grains were recovered at Yuezhuang — the earliest such
rice remains known in the Yellow River region (as of Crawford et al. 2006). Stable
isotope analysis of human bone from Xiaojingshan (as of Hu, Y. et al. 2006) found
that millet, a C4 plant, contributed only about 25 percent of diet. A cave site,
Bianbiandong (Yiyuan, Shandong, ca. 11,000–9,600 cal BP), yielded pottery sherds (bo
bowls, fu cauldrons) similar to Neolithic Houli-culture wares (8,500–7,500 cal BP),
suggesting a possible antecedent link, though this is not treated as an established
continuity in the source.

**Linguistic and genetic.** [[unknown]] — no linguistic or genetic-population
affiliation is claimed for Houli in this source.

## Distribution and Chronology

About a dozen sites are known on Shandong floodplains along the northern piedmont
of the Taiyi Mountains. Houli falls within the broader north China early-Neolithic
window associated with the eastward advance of the Holocene monsoon maximum (as of
An, Z. et al. 2000), alongside [[xinglongwa-culture]], [[peiligang-culture]], and
[[cishan-culture]].

## Lifeways

Liu & Chen (2012) conclude that, given the lack of agricultural implements at Houli
sites, millet and rice farming could not have played a major economic role in the
culture's economy. The comparatively higher grinding-stone frequency at Houli sites
relative to Cishan-Beifudi is attributed by the authors to greater local availability
of oak-forest (*Quercus*) resources for acorn processing. Liu & Chen suggest that high
degrees of sedentism were likely present in Houli culture, but that logistic
residential mobility was also practiced.

## Relations and Successions

Houli is `analogous_to` [[xinglongwa-culture]], [[cishan-culture]], and
[[peiligang-culture]] in its broad-spectrum, low-food-production early-Neolithic
economy. Its relationship to the later Beixin and [[dawenkou-culture]] sequence in
Shandong is not detailed in the material reviewed here and is marked [[unknown]]
pending further ingest; Beixin culture (ca. 5300–4300 BCE) is Dawenkou's direct
predecessor per the source but is not yet a wiki page.

## Identity Cautions

Houli is an etic material-culture construct defined by settlement remains, ceramics,
and lithic assemblages. Liu & Chen do not equate it with a language, ethnic group, or
genetic population. The early rice and millet remains at Yuezhuang document plant
use, not agricultural dependence or ethnic identity — the source explicitly argues
against reading these finds as evidence of a farming economy.

## Historiography

Houli's early rice find at Yuezhuang is notable within Chinese Neolithic archaeology
as the earliest known in the Yellow River region (as of Crawford et al. 2006), though
Liu & Chen caution against overreading this into an agricultural-economy claim given
the isotope and tool-assemblage evidence. See
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source discussion.
