# Lungshan (Longshan) / Lungshanoid Cultures

## Definition and Construct History

**Lungshan** (Wade-Giles; pinyin *Longshan*) was first identified in 1928 by G. D. Wu at
Ch'eng-tzu-yai in Shandong, near Lung-shan-chen (the name source): thin, lustrous black
pottery, rectangular polished stone tools, scapulimancy, ring-footed and tripod vessels,
and stamped-earth (*hang-t'u*) village walls, contrasting sharply with painted-pottery
[[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]]. The 1931 excavation of Hou-kang near Anyang gave the first
stratigraphic sequence — Yangshao below, Lungshan above, historic Yin-Shang on top.

**"Lungshanoid" is [[chang-kwang-chih|Chang]]'s coinage (1959, attributed).** In the 1963
book Chang used it for a broad **horizon** — the proto-Lungshan complex plus the various
regional Lungshan phases — emphasizing horizon-wide similarity with regional variation. He
tied the horizon to the transitional **"Miao-ti-kou II"** (proto-Lungshan) assemblage first
recognized in 1956–57 during Yellow River reservoir salvage work, where a proto-Lungshan
layer overlay Yangshao remains. For Chang this dissolved the old idea of Yangshao and
Lungshan as two separate "cultures" of different origin (the "Two Culture Theory") into two
developmental **stages** of one sequence.

`construct_status: contested` — Chang's single-horizon "Lungshanoid" framing was central to
his monocentric model of Chinese origins, which later multi-regional archaeology substantially
revised; "Longshan" today usually denotes a family of related but partly independent regional
Late Neolithic cultures. This account is pre-radiocarbon relative chronology; frontmatter
absolute dates are later consensus, not Chang's.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Chang 1963).** Four proposed horizon-marker features: (1) varied tripod and
ring-foot pottery plus scapulimancy, implying ritual elaboration; (2) sharp vessel-body
curves (vs. Yangshao roundness); (3) cut-out ring-feet; (4) decline of painting and rise of
incision, combing, and checker-impression, with gray the predominant color and wheel-marks
and fine black ware present regionally but not universally. Quantitative sherd tabulation
across Miao-ti-kou I (Yangshao) → Miao-ti-kou II (proto-Lungshan) → San-li-ch'iao (Lungshan)
shows painted ware dropping from ~14% to under 1% to nil while gray and fine gray/black wares
rise. Stone tools become characteristically asymmetric-edged and rectangular in cross-section;
semilunar and double-holed sickle-knives appear. Scapulimancy (scraped and burned cattle,
deer, and occasionally sheep scapulae) becomes newly important across many provinces.

**Genetic / linguistic.** Not addressed. Enforce the four-way non-identity rule.

## Distribution and Chronology

Lungshanoid assemblages are recorded across Shensi, Shansi, Henan, Shandong, Hebei, Liaoning,
Hubei, Anhwei, Kiangsu, Chekiang, Fukien, Taiwan, and Kwangtung — the middle and lower
Huanghe, the Pohai Bay coasts, the middle and lower Yangtze, and the southeastern coast.
Chang distinguished at least two chronological strata: an earlier, widespread proto-Lungshan
phase (ting/kui tripods, some surviving painting, cut-out pedestals) followed by divergent
regional later developments (the Pohai-coast "mature" Lungshan of Ch'eng-tzu-yai and
Liang-ch'eng-chen; the western/northern Honan phase with painting gone and li/ting tripods
elaborated; southern Huaihe and Yangtze-delta continuations). He flagged this alignment as
provisional and "worthy of a test in further investigations."

## Lifeways

Chang read the Yangshao→Lungshanoid transition as a shift from shifting/repetitive settlement
toward **permanent, larger, longer-occupied villages** — some with stamped-earth defensive
walls (Ch'eng-tzu-yai, Hou-kang) — and inferred that **warfare "must have been common,"**
citing walls and a Hantan well full of skeletons "presumably thrown there after a village
raid." His summary Table 5 (attributed, several items self-flagged uncertain) posed the
horizon as marking craft specialization (wheel-made pottery, occupational scapulimancy),
sharper status differentiation (ranked and prone burials, isolated jade concentrations),
institutionalized ancestor worship succeeding the earlier fertility cult, and the beginnings
of fortification and organized violence — the social scaffolding, in his model, from which one
"local state eventually succeeded in expansion and conquest" as the Shang.

Rice and wheat were added to millet at Lungshanoid sites; Chang held that rice was
domesticated in the south and carried back north by expanding Lungshanoid farmers (see
[[rice-domestication]]).

## Liu & Chen (2012) densification — competitive regional polities, 2600–1900 BC

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 7, "Rise and Fall of Early Complex Societies: The Late Neolithic,
3000–2000 BC." Radiocarbon-anchored; distinguishes a lower/eastern (Haidai/Shandong) Longshan
from a middle Yellow River Longshan, both discussed here as regional variants of the same
horizon-level construct — reinforcing `construct_status: contested` above.)*

**Lower Yellow River (Haidai) Longshan, 2600–1900 BC.** Succeeded the late Dawenkou culture
(3000–2600 BC) in Shandong, eastern Henan, northern Anhui, and northern Jiangsu. Recorded site
counts in Shandong rose from 547 (Dawenkou) to 1,492 (Longshan); a southeastern-Shandong
regional survey shows growth from 2 sites (Beixin period) to 27 (late Dawenkou) to 463
(Longshan). About twenty walled Longshan-period sites are reported in Shandong (seven
confirmed by excavation), regional centers spaced roughly 30–50 km apart — a pattern Liu & Chen
(citing Liu, L. 2004; Underhill 1994; Underhill et al. 2008) read as indicating "a competitive
relationship between regional polities." Two major centers, **Liangchengzhen** (272.5 ha) and
**Yaowangcheng** (367.5 ha), each show four-level settlement hierarchies with differing
nucleation patterns "attributable" (Underhill et al. 2008) to diverse elite-control strategies;
Liangchengzhen shows evidence of stone-tool and possible jade/eggshell-pottery production, which
Liu & Chen suggest partly drove its population nucleation.

**Salt and exchange.** Roughly fifteen Dawenkou-period (4500–3000 BC) sites near Bohai Bay, sited
on poor agricultural soil, show ash-filled burnt pits interpreted as salt-making facilities, with
nonlocal jade/painted pottery indicating exchange; by the Longshan period four walled inland
sites (Chengziya, Dinggong, Tonglin, Bianxianwang) were spaced equidistantly, with small
seasonal coastal salt sites possibly affiliated to them — Liu & Chen suggest competition among
walled centers "may have related to the control of natural resources (such as salt) for trade."

**Mortuary feasting and proto-writing.** Dawenkou mortuary practice already featured elaborate
feasting goods (jades, ivory, fine pottery, pig mandibles, deer tusks; at Lingyanghe, 663
drinking goblets across 45 tombs); Liu & Chen (after Fung 2000; Keightley 1985; Underhill 2000,
2002) read this as evidence that "feasting was an important part of mortuary practice, and
social stratification was explicitly expressed in mortuary display," continuing into Longshan
elite tombs (black eggshell-pottery goblets, jades, alligator-skin drums). **[POSITION]** More
than twenty pictograph types on late-Dawenkou *dakouzun* urns, and carved multi-character
pottery sherds at Dinggong and Longqiuzhuang (c. 2000 BC), are disputed as either genuine writing
comparable to oracle-bone/bronze script or non-linguistic marks; attempts to decipher the
Dinggong inscriptions have reached no consensus (Feng, S. 1994; Keightley 2006; Yang, X. 2000).

**Decline.** Haidai Longshan declined around 2000 BC (sharp drop in settlement numbers,
disappearance of most regional centers, though some, e.g. Chengziya, persisted into the
succeeding Yueshi culture); Liu & Chen state the causes "are still unclear," likely both natural
and human, "unlikely to have been a single catastrophic event."

**Middle Yellow River Longshan (Henan, southern Hebei, southern Shanxi, central Shaanxi).**
Divided into early/Miaodigou II and late phases with 10+ regional ceramic variants (*leixing*);
national survey data show site counts in Henan/Shanxi/Shaanxi rising from a combined Yangshao
total of 3,556 to a Longshan total of 4,302. At least nine fortified regional centers (≤50 ha,
near-equidistant spacing) are documented in Henan and southern Shanxi (Liu, L. 2004), read by
Liu & Chen as again "suggesting competitive relationships among the polities." Two sites in this
zone, [[wangchenggang]] and [[taosi]] (both new pages from this ingest, not owned here), anchor
the debate — noted separately in the brief as belonging to another agent — over whether Late
Neolithic Longshan-horizon societies had crossed the threshold into "early states"; Liu & Chen
describe this as "a matter of debate" and decline to adjudicate it themselves, prioritizing
reconstruction of "how these complex societies developed, operated, and changed" over the
state/non-state label.

Four-way non-identity: none of this material equates the Longshan construct — already flagged
above as `construct_status: contested` — with a language or genetic population; the
proto-writing dispute over Dawenkou/Dinggong marks is recorded as a live position, not resolved.

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from:** [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]] via the Miao-ti-kou II proto-Lungshan
  transition (a developmental continuity, per Chang).
- **Produced / preceded:** [[shang-civilization|Shang]] civilization — Chang argued the
  Honan-phase Lungshanoid was the direct substratum of Shang, with "no major break."
- **Associated with (not identity):** regional Neolithic traditions from Liaoning to the
  Yangtze delta that Chang read as extensions of a single radiating horizon — a reading later
  archaeology challenged (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

- **Etic construct.** "Lungshanoid" is Chang's analytic horizon-label, not a people or a
  polity; later scholarship treats "Longshan" as multiple partly independent regional cultures.
- **Load-bearing for a superseded thesis.** The horizon's coherence was integral to Chang's
  monocentric nuclear-area diffusion model; read it as an attributed 1963 framework.

## Historiography

From Chang's [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]] synthesis. The "Lungshanoid
expansion" was the book's signature contribution and its most consequential claim — and the
one most thoroughly revised, first by Chang himself (toward a regional "interaction sphere")
and then by the multi-regional consensus of later Chinese archaeology. Densify from
radiocarbon-anchored regional Longshan reports and Taosi/Liangzhu material when ingested.
