# Pengtoushan Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Pengtoushan culture designates the earliest recognized Neolithic material assemblage in the middle Yangzi River region, dated ca. 7000–5800 BC. Liu and Chen (2012) state: "The earliest Neolithic remains in the middle Yangzi River are defined by the Pengtoushan culture (ca. 7000–5800 BC)." The construct rests on roughly 15 known sites clustered around Lake Dongting, Hunan, north of the Lishui River. The type site, Pengtoushan (3 ha), and the associated site of Bashidang (3.7 ha), both in Lixian county, Hunan, anchor the sequence (Liu & Chen 2012, citing excavation reports).

## Material Signature

Pengtoushan is defined archaeologically by fiber-tempered pottery containing carbonized rice grains, though organic preservation across the culture's sites is generally poor owing to acidic soils (Liu & Chen 2012: "Pengtoushan is well known for showing carbonized rice grains in its fiber-tempered pottery"). Excavation at Pengtoushan itself (400 m²) revealed six houses, 15 pits, and 21 burials with little evidence of spatial planning. At Bashidang, excavation (1,200 m²) uncovered 24 pile or ground-level dwellings, 98 secondary burials, 80 pits, and roughly 10,000 rice grains alongside abundant wild plant and animal remains — none of the recovered mammal remains have been confirmed as domesticated. Rice grains at Bashidang have been identified by some researchers as a domesticated form, but Liu and Chen caution this claim rests on grain morphology "not based on characteristics of the rachis," and that "further study... is warranted" (citing Crawford and Shen 1998).

Bashidang is additionally notable for an earthen wall-and-ditch enclosure (ditch depth to 1.7 m, width to 5.7 m) bordering two sides of the settlement, with a river forming the other two boundaries. Liu and Chen describe this feature as showing "little effectiveness for defense," interpreting it instead as flood control; they nonetheless characterize it as "the earliest currently known fortification in ancient China" in a purely structural (not functional-defensive) sense, reading its construction as evidence of both high sedentism and a communal social response to monsoon-flood stress (Liu & Chen 2012).

## Linguistic and Genetic Signature

No linguistic or genetic evidence is associated with the Pengtoushan construct in the source consulted; both fields are recorded as `[[unknown]]` pending future study. The construct is defined purely by material culture (pottery, architecture, subsistence remains) — see Identity Cautions below.

## Distribution and Chronology

Sites cluster in the Lake Dongting basin of Hunan province, dated ca. 7000–5800 BC by radiocarbon and ceramic seriation. The Pengtoushan tradition is succeeded locally by the Lower Zaoshi culture (ca. 5800–5500 BC), and Liu and Chen (following Jiao 2006) suggest Pengtoushan-tradition traits reached as far as Jiahu on the Huai River and Kuahuqiao in the lower Yangzi, which they read as reflecting demographic expansion linked to the broader development of rice farming across the region.

## Lifeways

Subsistence combined wild plant and animal exploitation with early rice use; the domestication status of Bashidang's rice remains uncertain pending rachis-based analysis. Settlement involved semi-sedentary to sedentary occupation (pile and ground-level dwellings), small-scale mortuary practice (simple secondary burials, no elaborate grave goods reported), and communal earthwork construction (the Bashidang enclosure) directed at flood mitigation rather than warfare.

## Relations and Successions

Pengtoushan is `preceded_by` undocumented pre-ceramic Lake Dongting occupations and `succeeded_by` the Lower Zaoshi culture (not yet a wiki page). Liu and Chen argue that population growth associated with this sequence is more plausibly attributed to increased sedentism from broad-spectrum subsistence than to rice cultivation alone, while acknowledging that rice farming later "unquestionably contributed to further population growth" (Liu & Chen 2012) — an attributed causal claim, not a wiki-voice finding. The culture is `analogous_to` other early Yangzi/Huai fiber-tempered pottery traditions (Jiahu, Kuahuqiao) without asserting shared identity.

## Identity Cautions

Pengtoushan is an archaeological-culture construct defined by pottery, architecture, and rice-associated subsistence evidence — it is not evidence of a self-conscious people, a linguistic group, or a genetic population. No language affiliation or genetic population has been argued for Pengtoushan in the source consulted; any future claim linking the culture to a language family or genetic lineage would require independent argument and an `associated_with` link, not identity.

## Historiography

The culture is a well-established regional sequence in Chinese archaeology, defined through decades of excavation at Pengtoushan and Bashidang; Liu and Chen (2012) synthesize it as part of the broader Early Neolithic middle-Yangzi rice-origins research program (see [[rice-domestication]]).
