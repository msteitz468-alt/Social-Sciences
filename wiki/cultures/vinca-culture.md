# Vinča Culture

*(As treated in Childe 1929 *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|Danube]]*.)* Balkan Neolithic tell culture; type site Vinča near Belgrade. Childe's **Vinča I/II** sequence anchors his "first Neolithic civilization on the Middle Danube" and later painted-pottery / Copper Age discussions.

## Definition and Construct History

Defined from stratified tell material: pottery, figurines, anthropomorphic lids, celts, settlement depth. Childe treats Vinča I as part of a SE Neolithic package linking the Danube to the Aegean/Anatolian world (colonist fishers in his reading — not mere trade, not simply a daughter of Troy II).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as Childe):** tell stratigraphy; figurines; anthropomorphic lids; various ceramic forms; later Vinča II with Adriatic and related contacts (Butmir, Cséka/Tordos as he links them).

**Linguistic / genetic:** none in source.

## Distribution and Chronology

Middle Danube / northern Balkans; Childe argues Vinča I does not extend north of the Belgrade region in the same form — north of that, Danubian I takes over. Absolute dates: 1929 relative only; he gives Vinča high antiquity relative to some German authorities (Epilogue notes the dispute).

## Lifeways

Tell settlement; mixed farming/fishing package as Childe reconstructs from finds.

## Relations and Successions

SE origin narrative; synchronisms and "relations" with Aegean and with [[danubian-i-culture]] to the north; later Copper Age and painted-pottery overlays.

## Identity Cautions

Etic archaeological culture; not an ethnonym; not a language or genetic population. Modern Vinča absolute chronology and social interpretations diverge from 1929.

## Renfrew's recalibration and independent metallurgy (Before Civilization, 1973)

*(Attributed, as of Renfrew 1973 — see [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973]].)* Where Childe
(1929) derived Vinča from Aegean/Anatolian colonists, [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] used
calibrated [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] dates to place Vinča centuries **earlier** than
its supposed sources — uncalibrated dates of 4400–3600 b.c. calibrate to ~5300–4000 BCE, a
"yawning millennium" beyond Milojčić's short-chronology estimate of 2700–2000 BCE (which
Milojčić defended by rejecting radiocarbon; see [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]).
Renfrew argued Balkan **copper metallurgy was very likely invented independently** in the
Balkans, not diffused from the Near East: a locally documented sequence (native copper at
Cernica, hot-working, open-mould cast shaft-hole axes in the Gumelnitsa culture before 4000
BCE per J. A. Charles) and the **Rudna Glava** mine shafts (eastern Serbia, excavated 1970;
Vinča-period pottery) — "the earliest mines anywhere in the world, on the basis of present
knowledge." Independent stratigraphic support came from [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate|Sitagroi]].

On social organization, Renfrew read Vinča/Gumelnitsa society as **basically egalitarian** —
no chiefly display goods or monumental territorial architecture — an explicit contrast with his
[[maltese-temples|Maltese]] [[chiefdom]] case. On the **Tărtăria tablets** and other incised
Balkan signs (offered by Milojčić as a Sumerian synchronism), Renfrew held the find-context was
possibly intrusive and the signs an **independent local sign-system, not "writing"** and not
evidence of contact — a deliberate refusal to conflate a material symbol-set with a script or
language (a model instance of the four-way non-identity caution). Marija Gimbutas's reading of
individual deities among the figurines he found partly "convincing" but the "Great Earth Mother"
interpretation "incautious."

## Gimbutas densification — *Civilization of the Goddess* (1991)

*(All claims in this section attributed to [[gimbutas-marija]], *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* 1991, as of that synthesis. FLAG source: Goddess typology, peaceful/matristic social reading, and Kurgan-wave demise are **theses**, not wiki-voice findings. Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people. Cross-links: [[old-europe]], [[kurgan-hypothesis]], [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]].)*

### Chronology and formation (as of Gimbutas 1991)

Gimbutas places Vinča formation around **5400–5300 B.C.** as ceramic and other innovations on a **Starčevo continuum** (Bulgarian and Adriatic influences on final Starčevo also stimulate Butmir, Tisza, and Lengyel in her Ch. 3 framing). Dendro-calibrated radiocarbon: culture roughly **5400–5300 to 4300–4100 B.C.**; **Early Vinča** c. 5400–4900; **Late/Classical** c. 4900–4300 (rarely later than 4300). She rejects Vasić’s 1930 Aegean-colony / 2nd-millennium dating and endorses an **indigenous** Balkan development with ~1,000 years of continuity, accelerated by metallurgy and population increase; settlements nearly doubled over the millennium in her account. Eponymous tell 14 km east of Belgrade: Miloje Vasić excavations 1908–1934, ~7 m stratified Vinča over Starčevo, ~10 habitation horizons. Distribution: central Balkans to Banat, NE Bosnia, W Bulgaria, SW Romania — largely former Starčevo territory; nearly **650** sites known (Chapman 1981 via Gimbutas).

### Settlement, material culture, and copper (as of Gimbutas 1991)

Settlements larger than Starčevo; unenclosed along rivers; larger open sites **25–30 ha**, up to **80 ha** (Selevac) and **100 ha** (Potporanj at Vršac). Multi-generation occupation (500–600 years) on high-arable soils forms tells of **2–6 ha** — she evaluates even these as larger than Near Eastern Early Dynastic towns (evaluative thesis). Population ranges she cites: small ≤200, medium ≤500, largest **1,000–2,500**. Villages planned: leveled ground, terraced slopes, roughly parallel houses, streets 2.5–3.5 m. Early houses: one large room + antechamber or two rooms ~8 × 3–4 m; mid-Vinča 3–5 rooms; largest to ~20 m. Timber posts/split-plank frames, plaited twigs, thick clay plaster; Anza (her excavation) early floors lime-plastered over logs/stones; Late Vinča Divostin house groups of twos/threes spaced 26–60 m.

**Pottery (fact-register as she reports):** black-burnished fine wares (biconical vessels, carinated/flat dishes, zoomorphic and ornithomorphic vases, high-footed forms); channeling by burnishing; black-topped ware by inverted-rim firing. Emergence of black burnished pottery ~5400–5300 B.C. she attributes to **Karanovo (Bulgarian) influence**, not Anatolian immigration — transition documented at Anza c. 5300–5000 B.C. Graphite-painted Karanovo wares also exported into Vinča in the 5th millennium (as of her metallurgy/trade chapters).

**Trade and mining:** Upper Tisza obsidian; Adriatic/Aegean alabaster, marble, *Spondylus*; cinnabar from Šuplja Stena; copper mining no later than **5000 B.C.** **Rudna Glava** (~140 km east of Belgrade): among earliest copper mines; ~30 shafts excavated 1968–1979 (Jovanović); malachite/azurite; stone mauls and antler picks; fire-and-water cracking; Vinča pottery caches date Early→middle through Late Vinča; ore to Morava-basin villages. Aligns with Renfrew’s independent-Balkan-metallurgy frame above on the **mine as early**, while her Goddess-as-patroness-of-metallurgy reading of Bird Goddess symbols on a Rudna Glava lamp is a **separate interpretive thesis**.

### Temples, figurines, and “sacred script” (theses + find inventory)

House and “temple” architecture are closely related in her account, so recognition is difficult; multi-roomed buildings with walls painted red/blue/white (chevrons, meanders) stand out. At least four settlements (Vinča, Parța, Jela, Kormadin): **bucrania** (clay-coated ox skulls; blue muzzles, red forehead triangle) on columns/walls. **Parța** (SW of Timișoara): stacked “temples” with altars, offering tables, mobile hearths, monumental statues, bucranium columns, double two-headed goddess statue (final report not yet out as of 1991). Anza “destroyed temple” assemblage she cites: 92 cm Bird Goddess vase, 60 cm waterbird vase, life-sized pig sculpture.

**Sculptural corpus:** thousands of clay sculptures; type site alone nearly **2,000** figurines and anthropomorphic vases (630 published by Vasić vol. 3); also marble, alabaster, opalite; monumental life-sized masks in southern Yugoslavia; owl-faced lids for large biconical ritual containers. **Gimbutas’s divine typology (thesis):** Bird Goddess as primary (beaked masks, V’s, chevrons, meanders); also Snake Goddesses, “Madonnas” (mother with bear/bird mask and baby); ~20% zoomorphic and masked male; centaurs (masked human head on bull body) in southern Yugoslavia. Classical masks pentagonal with semicircular eyes; later almond-shaped eyes. Early Vinča: many schematized miniature inscribed figurines as ex-votos. Aesthetic claims equating torsos with Classical Greek sculpture are evaluative thesis only.

**[[old-european-script]] (thesis):** First finds at Turdaș (Tordos) 1874; Tărtăria plaques 1961 from early Vinča sacrificial pit — she rejects Hood et al.’s “uncomprehending imitation” of Sumerian. Main concentration of inscribed objects in Vinča & Tisza basins (plus Karanovo and others); Winn (1981) catalogued **210** recognizable Vinča signs from 29 sites. She argues common use c. **5300–4300 B.C.** as **sacred** writing on religious objects (“communication between individuals and deities”), not commercial administration; script “virtually disappeared” with disintegration of Karanovo, Vinča, and related groups c. **4300–4000 B.C.** following steppe pastoralist infiltration in her model. Renfrew’s “independent local sign-system, not writing” remains the competing position on this page — do not promote either reading to wiki voice.

### Demise and Kurgan Wave 1 (Gimbutas thesis)

Under the [[kurgan-hypothesis]] as stated in *Civilization of the Goddess*, **Wave 1 (Kurgan I)** c. **4400–4300 B.C.** is **catastrophic** for Varna littoral, Karanovo-Gumelniţa, **Vinča**, and Lengyel (Cucuteni survives). She claims **no habitation continuity on the Vinča mound after c. 4300 B.C.**; temples, figurines, and exquisite pottery gone by end of 5th millennium. Disruption of Varna, Karanovo, and Vinča triggers a dislocation cascade into Yugoslavia, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, as far as Upper Danube, Upper Elbe, Upper Oder: Vinča elements into western Hungary (“Balaton” complex) and Croatia/Bosnia/Slovenia (“Lasinja”); Lengyel migrates W/N; caves and high defensive locations occupied under stress. Discontinuity plus large-scale N/NW population shifts are, for her, indirect evidence of catastrophe **not** explained by climate change, land exhaustion, or epidemics; direct evidence she cites is single male barrow burials and the broader Kurgan trait complex. **This invasion/demise package is a live position in [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]] and [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]** — record it as attributed, not as settled history. Compare Anthony’s steppe-frontier models and Renfrew’s non-Kurgan frames elsewhere in the vault.

### Identity cautions (Gimbutas layer)

- Vinča material culture ≠ “Old Europeans” as ethnicity, language, or genetic population.
- “Bird Goddess” / Goddess-civilization readings are **her** interpretive system (see debate page).
- Inscribed signs ≠ proven logo-syllabic script or non-IE language identity.
- Physical-type claims (gracile Mediterranean continuous from Starčevo) are dated physical anthropology as of 1991, not modern aDNA.

## Historiography

Type-site name remains standard; Childe's Aegean-colonisation emphasis is one 1929 position among later autochthonous and network models. Renfrew's 1973 independent-invention reading is now the mainstream frame for metallurgy chronology; Gimbutas 1991 supplies the fullest vault densification of settlement scale, figurine/temple inventory, Rudna Glava, and the Kurgan-wave end narrative — all recorded in attributed voice. Whittle 1996 (below) adds anti-elite community/aggregation-dispersal readings and non-synchronous end chronology against single kurgan shockwaves. Primary sources on this page: [[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929]], [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973]], [[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991]], [[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]].

## Whittle densification (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 1, 4–5. Mid-1990s cal BC; pre-aDNA. Vinča is a **material-culture construct**—ceramic style ≠ political tribe ≠ language ≠ genes.)*

### Extent, duration, settlement diversity (as of Whittle 1996)

Whittle places Vinča from mid **6th** to mid **5th** millennium BC or later, across Serbia/Bosnia/Croatia/W Romania: more numerous and more widely spread sites than Starčevo in many areas. Vinča tell continued (~**6 ha**, ~**7 m** Vinča deposits, main phase earlier Vinča); new tells e.g. Gomolava; large flat sites **Selevac** (~50+ ha, c. 5100–4400 BC, max ~3 m buildup, shifting foci), Potporanj; many smaller occupations. By mid 5th mill. late Vinča / Krivodol–Bubanj Hum often **reversion to dispersed smaller units**. Only two known burial grounds in his account (Gomolava intramural; Botoš separate). Plain dark fine pottery; copper from **Rudna Glava** etc. Expansion not everywhere (middle Morava little increase; Anza ends ~5000 BC). He notes fewer Balkan scholars by the mid-1990s explained Vinča by southern population arrival—yet an “enormous disposition to believe in colonisation” still shaped SE European narratives (**historiographic position**).

### Selevac and Opovo — aggregation/dispersal, not elite households

**Selevac (POSITION/FINDING):** series of shifting smaller foci (perhaps 3–15 ha) within ~50 ha; four building phases of rectangular post-and-daub, often deliberate burning; structures more solid over time; early vertical replacement then later horizontal displacement—may show **reluctance for a fixed long-term place**; abundance of material culture as social relationships (exchange, gift, hospitality) consolidating community; large sites may draw occupants from a wide area with multiple allegiances. Against Tringham-style **autonomous competing households** fissioning into late Vinča pattern: house≠household equation weak; later Divostin still ordered freestanding houses with common style/orientation (**position**).

**Opovo** (Banat wetlands): large low mound ~200 m diameter, 3 main levels, 2+ centuries later Vinča ~**4700–4500 BC**; houses copy prior orientation/plots; most burned—perhaps deliberate but communally sanctioned; domesticates + cereals + much wild game; little copper; possibly seasonal; reflects less stable later Vinča dispersal.

### Tărtăria and figurines

Tărtăria pit into earlier Vinča level: 26 clay figurines, 2 alabaster figurines, clay “anchor,” *Spondylus* bracelet, **3 unbaked inscribed clay tablets**, + burnt broken disarticulated adult male bones. Whittle allows shaman/spirit-medium burial claims but also foundation offering / ancestor-related readings; similar signs on Vinča pot bases (e.g. Divostin). Distinctive mask-like triangular faces, large eyes, body decoration, often seated figurines—**principal context domestic**; large figurines Predionica. Compare Renfrew’s “independent local sign-system, not writing” and Gimbutas’s sacred-script thesis already on this page—**no wiki-voice adjudication**.

### Copper and early metallurgy (aligns with Renfrew independence frame)

Simple 6th mill. beads/hooks (Vinča, Selevac, etc.); slag Anza IV ~**5000 BC** confirms smelting; **Rudna Glava** (NE Serbia, later Vinča pottery) among extraction sites with Ai Bunar and Prljusa–Mali Sturac. Gold rare in the Vinča area relative to Varna. Whittle rejects generalised “centralised cult in Vinča” and Copper Age hierarchy packages as over-general (**position**).

### Ceramic identities without political unity

Early 5th mill. mosaic including large Vinča shifts by late Copper Age to fragmented late Vinča / Krivodol–Bubanj–Salcuța beside the Karanovo VI–Gumelnița bloc. Conventional tribe/political-unity reading of ceramic distributions **rejected**; better as shared identity ideas without political unity; goods cross “cultural” boundaries (**position**; four-way non-identity).

### End sequence and non-synchronous change (Ch.5)

Radiocarbon may put widespread Vinča end **earlier than** the Karanovo VI complex, **before 4000 BC**; successor Salcuța IV–Bubanj Hum–Krivodol spans end Copper Age into the transition; hilltop occupations (Bubanj, Hum, Krivodol); handled jugs/cups + graphite decoration; continues later Vinča dispersal. Gomolava later levels pits (Baden, Kostolac, Vučedol). **Late Vinča settlement-pattern alterations precede Usatovo–Horodiștea by up to a millennium**—external-stimulus explanations that assume a narrow successive horizon are weakened; internal-factor explanations fit gradual/varying-rate transformations (**position** against Gimbutas-type synchronous Wave 1 catastrophe as sole driver). In his synthesis, abandonments (Selevac, Opovo, Hungarian plain Tisza orbit) may show aggregation points **falling away successfully**, not household competitive rise; weak evidence for intensification/marked differentiation (**position**).
