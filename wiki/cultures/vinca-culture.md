# Vinča Culture

*(As treated in Childe 1929 *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|Danube]]*.)* Balkan Neolithic tell culture; type site Vinča near Belgrade. Childe's **Vinča I/II** sequence anchors his "first Neolithic civilization on the Middle Danube" and later painted-pottery / Copper Age discussions.

## Definition and Construct History

Defined from stratified tell material: pottery, figurines, anthropomorphic lids, celts, settlement depth. Childe treats Vinča I as part of a SE Neolithic package linking the Danube to the Aegean/Anatolian world (colonist fishers in his reading — not mere trade, not simply a daughter of Troy II).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as Childe):** tell stratigraphy; figurines; anthropomorphic lids; various ceramic forms; later Vinča II with Adriatic and related contacts (Butmir, Cséka/Tordos as he links them).

**Linguistic / genetic:** none in source.

## Distribution and Chronology

Middle Danube / northern Balkans; Childe argues Vinča I does not extend north of the Belgrade region in the same form — north of that, Danubian I takes over. Absolute dates: 1929 relative only; he gives Vinča high antiquity relative to some German authorities (Epilogue notes the dispute).

## Lifeways

Tell settlement; mixed farming/fishing package as Childe reconstructs from finds.

## Relations and Successions

SE origin narrative; synchronisms and "relations" with Aegean and with [[danubian-i-culture]] to the north; later Copper Age and painted-pottery overlays.

## Identity Cautions

Etic archaeological culture; not an ethnonym; not a language or genetic population. Modern Vinča absolute chronology and social interpretations diverge from 1929.

## Renfrew's recalibration and independent metallurgy (Before Civilization, 1973)

*(Attributed, as of Renfrew 1973 — see [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973]].)* Where Childe
(1929) derived Vinča from Aegean/Anatolian colonists, [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] used
calibrated [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] dates to place Vinča centuries **earlier** than
its supposed sources — uncalibrated dates of 4400–3600 b.c. calibrate to ~5300–4000 BCE, a
"yawning millennium" beyond Milojčić's short-chronology estimate of 2700–2000 BCE (which
Milojčić defended by rejecting radiocarbon; see [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]).
Renfrew argued Balkan **copper metallurgy was very likely invented independently** in the
Balkans, not diffused from the Near East: a locally documented sequence (native copper at
Cernica, hot-working, open-mould cast shaft-hole axes in the Gumelnitsa culture before 4000
BCE per J. A. Charles) and the **Rudna Glava** mine shafts (eastern Serbia, excavated 1970;
Vinča-period pottery) — "the earliest mines anywhere in the world, on the basis of present
knowledge." Independent stratigraphic support came from [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate|Sitagroi]].

On social organization, Renfrew read Vinča/Gumelnitsa society as **basically egalitarian** —
no chiefly display goods or monumental territorial architecture — an explicit contrast with his
[[maltese-temples|Maltese]] [[chiefdom]] case. On the **Tărtăria tablets** and other incised
Balkan signs (offered by Milojčić as a Sumerian synchronism), Renfrew held the find-context was
possibly intrusive and the signs an **independent local sign-system, not "writing"** and not
evidence of contact — a deliberate refusal to conflate a material symbol-set with a script or
language (a model instance of the four-way non-identity caution). Marija Gimbutas's reading of
individual deities among the figurines he found partly "convincing" but the "Great Earth Mother"
interpretation "incautious."

## Historiography

Type-site name remains standard; Childe's Aegean-colonisation emphasis is one 1929 position among later autochthonous and network models. Renfrew's 1973 independent-invention reading is now the mainstream frame; both are recorded here in attributed voice.
