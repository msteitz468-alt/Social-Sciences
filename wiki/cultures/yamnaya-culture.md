## Definition and Construct History

The Yamnaya culture (Russian: "pit-grave culture"; also Yamna) is an archaeologically defined **horizon** of Pontic-Caspian steppe pastoralists of the Early Bronze Age, named for its characteristic burial form: pit graves under earthen mounds (kurgans), often with ochre, and sometimes containing wagons, carts, and animal sacrifices. It is defined by shared mortuary and material traits, not by a self-identified ethnic label.

**Anthony archaeological layer (as of Anthony 2007).** In *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, [[anthony-david-w|David W. Anthony]] dates the Yamnaya horizon about **3300–2500 BCE** and argues it crystallized when ox-drawn wagons became normal steppe equipment (roughly 3300–3100 BCE) under a drier climate (pollen cores ~3500–3000 BCE). Combined with horseback riding, wagons enabled a more mobile pastoralism that exploited interfluvial deep steppes; disputes over pastures, Anthony argues, produced new social rules and a distinctive Yamnaya identity expressed archaeologically across the Pontic-Caspian belt. He prefers the Western term **"horizon"** over a rigid cultural-historical community label: shared lifestyle over five Final Eneolithic local cultures, without equating pots with a people or a language (Anthony 2007). He retains only the EBA Pit-grave/Yamnaya steppe core of Gimbutas’s broad “Kurgan culture,” rejecting her later expansion of that label to Maikop and many European Late Neolithic cultures (Anthony 2007).

Most archaeologists, Anthony reports, accept Merpert’s judgment that the **oldest Yamnaya variants** appeared in the driest **Volga–Don steppes**, with ceramic ancestry in late [[khvalynsk-culture|Khvalynsk]] / Repin traditions (A1 Repin-like; A2 late Khvalynsk-like, both shell-tempered); contact with late [[maikop-culture|Maikop]]–Novosvobodnaya on the lower Don (e.g. Konstantinovka) may have stimulated Repin → early Yamnaya, including wagons (Anthony 2007). From roughly 210 Yamnaya radiocarbon dates, Telegin et al. place the earliest substantial interval at ~**3400–3200 BCE** across NW Pontic, lower Dnieper, Donets, lower Don, middle Volga, and Kalmyk steppes — a rapid spread Anthony reads as competitive advantage aggressively exploited (Anthony 2007).

**Reich genetic layer (as of Reich 2018) — keep distinct.** Ancient-DNA analysis by Reich's lab (led by Iosif Lazaridis) established that Yamnaya individuals carried ancestry not previously present in central Europe: an approximately one-to-one mixture of an Armenian/Iranian-related southern population and an eastern-European-hunter-gatherer population, which Reich attributes to a migration across the Caucasus isthmus roughly 7,000–5,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Ancient DNA from the northern Caucasus, including from the earlier Maikop culture (which culturally influenced the Yamnaya — wagons, kurgan-building, and possible Uruk-Mesopotamian trade contact), carries this same southern ancestry component, per Haak, Krause, and colleagues (as of Reich 2018). This genetic formation story is independent of Anthony’s 2007 archaeological package; layer them, do not merge.

## Material / Genetic / Linguistic Signature

### Material (Anthony 2007)

Shared early Yamnaya archaeological traits, as Anthony defines the horizon, include:

- shell-tempered, egg-shaped pots with everted rims, comb stamps and cord impressions;
- tanged bronze daggers; cast flat axes; bone pins;
- **supine-with-raised-knees** burial posture; ochre staining of grave floors; usually NE–E orientation;
- funeral sacrifices of **wagons, carts, sheep, cattle, and horses**;
- kurgan mounds, including first cemeteries in the **deep steppes** between major valleys (property claims on plateau pasture; Shilov’s deep-steppe sample shows Yamnaya as the earliest graves in those locations) (Anthony 2007).

**Wagons.** Wagons probably entered the Pontic-Caspian steppes ~**3500–3300 BCE** (via Europe or late Maikop from Mesopotamia), a couple of centuries before the horizon’s archaeological peak; secure external anchors include the Bronocice wagon image ~3500–3300 BCE and Baden/Novosvobodnaya (Starokorsunskaya) roughly the same age. Oldest excavated steppe wagon graves radiocarbon ~3100–3000 BCE (Ostannii; Bal’ki), unlikely to be the absolute first. Gei (2000) counted **257** Yamnaya and Catacomb wagon/cart burials ~3100–2200 BCE (<5% of graves; largest cluster 120 in the Kuban, mostly Novotitorovskaya). Ten whole wagons: fixed axle, revolving multi-plank wheels 50–80 cm, bed ~1×2–2.5 m, gauge 1.5–1.65 m, often painted reed-mat tilt (Anthony 2007). Anthony’s synthesis: slow solid-wheeled ox wagons with reed-mat roofs enabled multi-week deep-steppe migrations; combined with horseback transport they “domesticated” the open steppes for year-round pastoral mobility (Anthony 2007).

**East–west split.** Anthony contrasts:

| Dimension | Eastern Yamnaya (Volga–Ural–N. Caucasus steppe) | Western Yamnaya (S. Bug–lower Don / Ukraine) |
|---|---|---|
| Settlements | Essentially none east of the Don to the Ural despite hundreds of kurgan cemeteries — best explanation: much of life spent in wagons | Settlements known west of the Don (e.g. Mikhailovka II–III; Liventsovka, Samsonovka; some fortified) |
| Herds | Sheep-dominant high-mobility strategy | Cattle dominate (Mikhailovka II–III: cattle ~60% vs sheep ~29%) |
| Grain | No cultivated grain imprints in eastern pots | Grain imprints present in western pottery |
| Burial sex bias | ~80% male on lower Volga | Less extreme male bias; Manych/Kuban-Azov nearer parity |
| Linguistic correlate (Anthony’s reading) | Loss of many cereal-agriculture cognates in eastern IE branches | Better preservation of cereal cognates in western IE; more female-inclusive myth near Tripolye figurine zone |

What spread with the horizon west, Anthony argues, was less the eastern sheep-mobile **economy** than **institutions**: guest-host agreements, patron-client contracts, and ancestor cults — some eastern chiefs may have moved west but added cattle and used fortified home bases (Anthony 2007).

**Guest-host (*ghos-ti-*).** Anthony links English *guest*/*host* (and cognates) to a late PIE reciprocal, oath-bound hospitality institution useful for regulating mobile herders moving through others’ territory; he treats it as among the critical identity-defining innovations that may have spread with the Yamnaya horizon. Apparent absence of the root in Anatolian and Tocharian supports late development tied to early Yamnaya migratory behavior (Anthony 2007).

**Dairy and diet.** East of the Don, no grain imprints; **0 caries** in 428 adult Yamnaya–Poltavka teeth from Samara; Chenopodium/amaranth phytoliths on Manych grave floors; peak childhood cribra orbitalia on the middle Volga consistent with dairy-rich diets or animal-contact parasites. Anthony reports genetic research placing the lactose-tolerance mutation west of the Urals ~4600–2800 BCE (Late Eneolithic–Yamnaya) (as of Anthony 2007 — date-stamp; later aDNA refined this field). Cattle prominence in PIE myth/ritual, despite sheep wealth, he explains by milk yield: cow > mare > goat (citing Vainshtein) (Anthony 2007).

**Demography and hierarchy.** Mallory’s review of 2,216 Yamnaya graves: median kurgan <3 Yamnaya graves; ~25% single grave; children never alone in the central grave; lower Volga ~80% male; females can occupy central graves (~1/5 on Volga). Class-1 kurgans ≥50 m diameter required >500 person-days; size and grave wealth imperfectly correlated; richest objects often metal (e.g. Kutuluk copper club; Utyevka I gold rings and iron-headed pin under a >100 m kurgan). Episodic cemetery use: many multi-dated cemeteries show statistically indistinguishable kurgan pairs — built rapidly in clusters then abandoned for centuries. Golyeva: EBA Yamnaya kurgans on pristine soils; many MBA Catacomb kurgans on already overgrazed soils (Anthony 2007).

**Metallurgy and stelae.** Local “clean” copper (Krasnodar–Kargaly azurite/malachite); forms often imitate Novosvobodnaya; early iron experiments (Utyevka pin head; Gerasimovka iron-bladed knife ~2500 BCE) long before Hittite/Near Eastern iron ages. ≥300 anthropomorphic stone stelae in Yamnaya/Catacomb North Pontic graves (half between South Bug and Ingul), expanding after ~3300 BCE from Kemi-Oba Crimean practice; Anthony notes striking parallels with contemporary Tuscany/Italian piedmont stelae and a Troy I fragment — linked by him to invigorated Black Sea / eastern Mediterranean maritime trade concurrent with Yamnaya land expansion (Anthony 2007).

**Ritual horses.** Horse sacrifice can be extraordinary: Catacomb-period Tsa-Tsa k.1 gr.5 (~2500 BCE) had forty horse skulls in two rows over an adult male — Anthony estimates ~8,000 kg meat for a vast funeral feast (Anthony 2007). Larger steppe horses appear in Baden, Cernavoda III, and Cham sites ~3300–3000 BCE, which Anthony reads as probably imported from the steppes (Anthony 2007).

**Expansion mechanism (Anthony).** Subsequent spread across the Pontic-Caspian steppes was **not primarily through warfare** (minimal evidence), but because sharing mobility institutions made people potential allies while non-participants became cultural Others; larger herds increased prestige, loans, feast sacrifices, and bride-prices (Nuer-like competitive dynamic) (Anthony 2007).

### Genetic (Reich 2018 — layered, not overwritten)

Reich states that the single most important source of ancestry across northern Europe today is the Yamnaya or closely related groups (as of Reich 2018). Ancient DNA further shows the Yamnaya carried early strains of *Yersinia pestis* (the Black Death bacterium) — Willerslev, Rasmussen, and Kristian Kristiansen found the pathogen endemic on the steppe in seven of 101 tested ancient samples; the early strains lacked flea-borne (bubonic) transmission genes but carried genes for pneumonic (respiratory) spread (as of Reich 2018). Reich raises, as an unestablished hypothesis, that steppe-carried plague may have helped clear the way for the subsequent Corded Ware expansion by analogy to post-1492 Native American epidemic collapse.

### Linguistic (inference only)

No direct linguistic evidence survives from Yamnaya remains. Anthony (2007) **associates** the horizon with classic / late PIE (wagon vocabulary, guest-host, pastoral lexicon) and its early dialect scatter; Reich (2018) uses migration timing as circumstantial support for a steppe-linked dispersal mechanism. Neither is a direct reading of a Yamnaya text. See [[indo-european-languages]] and [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]].

## Distribution and Chronology

**Anthony 2007 chronological frame:**

| Phase (Anthony) | Approx. dates | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Wagon entry to steppes | ~3500–3300 BCE | Before full horizon |
| Early Yamnaya rapid spread | ~3400–3200 / 3300–3100 BCE | Horizon crystallizes across Pontic-Caspian |
| Danube folk migration | ~3100–2800 BCE | Western Yamnaya stream into lower Danube / Carpathian Basin |
| Late Yamnaya fragmentation | ~2800–2500 BCE | → [[catacomb-culture]] (Don–Kuban), Poltavka (Volga–Ural); late PIE no longer a unity |

Reich (2018) reports the Yamnaya culture emerged on the Eurasian steppe around 5,000 years ago (~3300 BCE) and spread from the Hungarian plain in the west to the foothills of the Altai Mountains in the east — a distributional frame compatible with Anthony’s archaeological horizon plus the earlier Repin→[[afanasievo-culture]] long-distance eastward movement Anthony dates ~3700–3400 BCE (pre- or para-Yamnaya, not the classic horizon itself).

**Western contact cases 3300–2800 BCE (Anthony).** Three archaeological cases of western Pontic steppe contact where Anthony thinks language expansion probably occurred: (1) **Usatovo**–late Tripolye integration on the upper Dniester/Prut (steppe-origin patrons, not a Tripolye migration into the steppe; shares kurgan/painted-skull customs with Yamnaya/Afanasievo but not counted as Yamnaya because of Tripolye integration); (2) massive Yamnaya **Danube valley folk migration** 3100–2800 BCE (stream lasting ~300 years; origin east of Usatovo — South Bug–Ingul–Dnieper, not lower Dniester Yamnaya which mostly postdates 2800 BCE); five targeted clusters (Varna plain; Maritsa uplands; NW Bulgaria; Iron Gates/Serbia; **eastern Hungary** largest — Ecsedy ≥3,000 kurgans over ~6,000–8,000 km²); intrusive funeral package (kurgans 15–60 m, supine raised knees, ochre, Plachidol wagon-wheel grave, earliest carbonized hemp seeds at Gurbănești); (3) Yamnaya expansion toward [[corded-ware-culture]] on Dniester headwaters with hybrid border culture near the middle Dnieper (Anthony 2007).

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

**Anthony 2007:** First Eurasian steppe herding economy requiring regular **year-round seasonal pasture moves**; ox-wagons carried tents, water, and food into deep steppes far from river valleys; horseback riding enabled scouting and large-scale herding. Shishlina’s Kalmyk botanical data: seasonal moves between valley-bottom pastures (all seasons) and plateau pastures 15–50 km away (spring/summer). Of 2,096 graves Shilov reviewed, only 15.2% had domestic animal sacrifices — sheep/goat 65%, cattle 15%, horse 8%, dog 5%. Anthony rejects the model that Eurasian nomadic pastoralism requires agricultural states: if “nomadic pastoralism” means high residential mobility pastoral economy, it appeared with Yamnaya **before** states (Anthony 2007).

**Reich 2018:** Pastoral economy centered on sheep and cattle herding; wagon-based mobility across open grassland; minimal permanent settlement structure relative to burial monumentality.

## Relations and Successions

`preceded_by` late [[khvalynsk-culture]], Repin, and related Final Eneolithic steppe groups (Anthony’s ceramic and chronological sequence); genetically, Reich’s EHG + Caucasus/Iranian-related mixture (see [[ancient-north-eurasians]]). `succeeded_by` [[catacomb-culture]] and Poltavka on the steppes (Anthony ~2800 BCE fragmentation); in Europe, `associated_with` (not identical to) [[corded-ware-culture]]: Reich's lab found that individuals buried with Corded Ware pottery in Germany derived roughly three-quarters of their ancestry from Yamnaya-related groups, and Reich states the genetic data settled a long archaeological dispute by showing Corded Ware to be, "at least in a genetic sense," a westward extension of the Yamnaya (as of Reich 2018) — see the Reflexivity note on [[corded-ware-culture]] for why this finding is politically fraught.

Anthony’s **migration cascade** (attributed sequence, not wiki-voice identity of language with culture):

1. Repin → [[afanasievo-culture]] ~3700–3500 BCE ≈ Pre-Tocharian separation (Mallory & Mair accepted as likely);
2. early Yamnaya spread ~3300 BCE scatters late PIE;
3. western Yamnaya Danube/Carpathian migration ~3100–3000 BCE incubates Pre-Italic / Pre-Celtic;
4. contact with Corded Ware ~2800–2600 BCE for northern IE (Germanic path via Usatovo–TRB–Corded Ware is Anthony’s guess);
5. late Yamnaya/Poltavka → [[sintashta-culture]] ~2200–2000 BCE for Indo-Iranian precursors (Anthony 2007).

The Yamnaya are `associated_with` — not identified with — the spread of [[indo-european-languages]]. A 2016 reanalysis by Lazaridis and Nick Patterson found that Ancestral North Indian ([[ancestral-north-indians]]) ancestry is itself roughly 50% steppe-related and 50% ancient-Iranian-farmer-related, a genetic parallel Reich treats as consistent with steppe-descended groups spreading Indo-European speech to both Europe and South Asia (as of Reich 2018).

## Identity Cautions

"Yamnaya" is an archaeological-culture / horizon label describing a shared mortuary and material pattern with a documented genetic signature (post-2015); it is not a self-conscious ethnic group, a nation, or a language directly attested in the record. Anthony’s own four-way non-identity stance: archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people (Anthony 2007). The proposal that Yamnaya population movement and institutional franchising carried Indo-European speech is Anthony’s and (on genetic demography) Reich’s attributed interpretation — see [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]. Do not read "Yamnaya ancestry" in a later population as evidence of Yamnaya self-identification or cultural continuity beyond the specific traits documented. Grain-imprint and settlement asymmetries show that even within the horizon, eastern and western economies differed sharply.

## Historiography

**Anthony primary (now ingested).** *The Horse, the Wheel, and Language* (2007) is the landmark archaeological-linguistic synthesis of the Pontic-Caspian / Yamnaya case: material package, wagon and wool chronological termini for late PIE, guest-host and patron-client institutions, east–west economic split, Danube and Usatovo western contacts, and a franchising (not invasion) model of IE expansion. Pre-nuclear-aDNA: Anthony wrote before Haak/Allentoft/Reich 2015–2018 demography. Absolute chronology uses calibrated radiocarbon with Anthony’s provisional freshwater-reservoir correction for fish-eating steppe populations.

**Reich on Anthony’s migration stance (as of Reich 2018).** Reich reports that prior to 2015 ancient-DNA findings Anthony was a leading proponent of Yamnaya *cultural* transformation but argued that its spread was driven by imitation and proselytization rather than mass migration. That characterization applies to Anthony’s *mechanism* emphasis (franchising, institutions, language shift under prestige) more than to a total denial of demographic movement — Anthony 2007 already describes major folk migration into the Danube and demographically real streams, while insisting western IE spread was not a coordinated military “invasion.” Post-2015 aDNA (Haak, Lazaridis, and colleagues) established large-scale genetic replacement for the Yamnaya-to-Corded-Ware transition specifically — a demographic scale Anthony’s 2007 book could not yet cite. Keep the layers distinct: Anthony supplies archaeology + linguistic chronology + institutional model; Reich supplies nuclear aDNA demography.

**Earlier layers.** Sherratt (Pit-Grave / steppe essays) treats cart, horse, and secondary-farming features and Pit-Grave networks as transmission/contact, **not** identity with a people-language. Kristiansen & Larsson (2005) group Yamna with third-millennium tumulus/individual-burial mobile-pastoral steppe cultures and provisionally link PIE to ranked societies “most probably of the third millennium BC” — pre-aDNA, tentative.
