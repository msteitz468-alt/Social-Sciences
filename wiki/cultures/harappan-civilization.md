# Harappan Civilization

## Definition and Construct History

The **Harappan** (Indus Valley) tradition is the archaeological construct for South Asia’s first urban [[civilization|civilization]] complex. As presented by [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]], the chapter follows Possehl’s chronology: Mature Harappan “state” c. 2600–1900 BCE within a longer sequence c. 3200–1000 BCE. The mature phase lasted only about five to six centuries. The script remains undeciphered; as Possehl is quoted, scholars “do not know what these people called themselves or their cities… We have no king lists, no internal chronology.” Modern discovery dates to early twentieth-century work (Marshall and others).

Wenke & Olszewski treat Harappan culture as a pre-industrial [[pristine-state|pristine]]/primary-state case whose major evolutionary dynamics appear primarily self-contained, with only minor outside influences—hence useful for comparative [[state-formation|state formation]]. Childe is quoted as epigraph: India in the third millennium BCE confronts Egypt and Babylonia with a thoroughly individual, independent civilization, technically the peer of the rest and deeply rooted in Indian soil.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007).** Fired-brick urban planning, citadel/high-mound and lower-town layouts, municipal drains and bathrooms, standardized chert weights (binary and decimal systems) and a length unit of about 33.5 cm, steatite seals with short inscriptions, craft production (beads, bronze, ivory, pottery), and relatively muted mortuary wealth differentials compared with Egypt or Mesopotamia. [[mohenjo-daro]] is described as covering at least 2.5 km² with perhaps ~40,000 inhabitants; a Great Bath (~12 × 7 m, 2.5 m deep, bitumen-lined) anchors the western raised mound. Script inventory is estimated at ~350–425 unique symbols—consistent with a logosyllabic rather than alphabetic system (Parpola: not related to any known system; language-family affiliations not demonstrated). Seals are often short (five–six signs), right-to-left, sometimes boustrophedon; writing appears relatively suddenly in full form (preservation bias possible).

**Linguistic.** Undeciphered. Do not equate Harappan material culture with Dravidian, Indo-European, or any single language family; proposals remain unproven in the textbook presentation.

**Genetic.** Not used as identity criteria in this source; four-way non-identity applies.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Setting.** Wenke & Olszewski emphasize two rivers—the Indus and the now-extinct Ghaggar-Hakra (linked in later tradition to the Saraswati). Mughal argued the Ghaggar-Hakra supported many communities, including two cities as large as Mohenjo-daro and Harappa. Agriculture relied mainly on river irrigation/flood inundation; rivers were more unpredictable than the Nile; the zone is circumscribed by western highlands, Himalayas, Arabian Sea, and Great Indian Desert—analogous, in textbook framing, to Egypt and Peru. Flam’s Indus-course model places Early/Mature Mohenjo-daro ~25 km southeast of the then Sindhu Nadi course (outside the worst flood zone); an eastward shift late in the third millennium BCE is linked to flood exposure and possible relocation.

**Neolithic background.** Mehrgarh (Kachi Plain) shortly after 7000 BCE: multi-roomed mudbrick, cereal farming plus hunting, pottery by ~5500 BCE; Meadow on sheep/goat/cattle and early domesticated zebu (*Bos indicus*); copper, turquoise, lapis, marine shell; southern Asian cotton domestication ~7000–5000 BCE (timing/location unclear). DNA cited via Loftus et al. for independent zebu domestication.

**Early Harappan (~3200–2500 BCE).** Continuity in subsistence and local trade items, discontinuity in urbanism, stratification, and specialization. Possehl lists 463 sites, mostly ~5 ha with a few 22–30 ha; population more than tripled 3200–2600 BCE; little public architecture (only 3/463 pre-urban sites walled). After ~2600 BCE, citadels, warehouses, and residential wealth differentials appear.

**Mature Harappan (after ~2600 BCE, ~600-year span).** Three huge communities ~80–85 ha—Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, and Ganwariwala—evenly spaced in locational terms; >800 sites over ~1.25 million km²—larger, as stated, than the first Egyptian state or Sumer + Akkad + Assyria combined. Miller: not strictly riverine—settlements in many zones, river clustering later. Population early second millennium ≥~200,000. Some scholars argue a rapid Early→Mature transition (~100–150 years); others dispute this. Advanced town planning and municipal water/sewage are described as unequaled until the Romans; cultural/political influence over nearly 1.3 million km² with high material homogeneity relative to Mesopotamia; little archaeological display of extreme wealth inequality.

**Decline (~1900–1000 BCE).** Style heterogeneity, “degraded” art/architecture; a score of skeletons in Mohenjo-daro streets once read as invasion (Kennedy rejects Wheeler’s massacre reading). Competing explanations recorded as positions: Raikes (tectonic damming/floods); Flam (river-course shift); Possehl (African millets drawing populations south); Thapar multi-factor (tectonics, deforestation/overgrazing, marine regression stranding ports); Aryan/Indo-European invasion from Rigvedic tradition—many now treat language/crop spread as less catastrophic (Renfrew: Indus people may already have been Indo-European speakers—position, not wiki fact). Textbook synthesis: multi-causal gradual abandonment. After collapse, power centers shifted toward the Ganges after ~1100 BCE, with Harappan cultural legacies in later metallurgy, architecture, pottery, and agriculture.

## Lifeways

**Subsistence (Mature).** Cereals—particularly barley, also wheat—as economic basis; dates, melons, sesame, peas, mustard; cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, domestic fowl; buffaloes, camels, asses, dogs, cats kept; horse rare until the end of the period (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007).

**Economy and craft.** Strong possibility of administered, noncapitalistic redistribution rather than free-enterprise peasant marketing. Urban craft specialists (goldsmiths, potters, bead-makers, seal-cutters, brick masons). Lothal (~250–300 m): dock-like tank (Rao; function disputed by Possehl), bead factory, bronze/ivory; Possehl reads it as a frontier entrepôt processing hinterland raw materials for Harappan cities; trade also with hunter-gatherers (knives, pottery, steatite beads vs stone, meat, beeswax, wood). Standardized weights/measures imply administered exchange. Miller notes remarkable lack of evidence for external trade in finished Harappan goods relative to Mesopotamia; colonies reported in Afghanistan and Oman.

**Political organization (contested).** If three or more administrative levels define a state, Harappan was a state; settlement hierarchy supports that reading. Service and others called it a [[chiefdom]] for limited monumental architecture, weak wealth differential, and militarism—Jacobson notes newer evidence (fortification, stratification, writing, hierarchy) challenges that. Shaffer: urban literate culture without the usual hereditary-elite / centralized-state / warfare package. Kenoyer: competing elite classes (merchants, ritual specialists, resource controllers) sharing ideology and economy rather than one absolute ruling group. Piggott’s 1950 “rigidly stratified, centralized, authoritarian” image was recycled for decades despite contrary evidence (Shaffer via W&O)—received-wisdom under data scarcity.

**Foreign trade stimulus.** Wenke & Olszewski suggest foreign trade (minerals/metals via highlands; Persian Gulf links to Mesopotamia; Magan/Meluhha possibly Indus ports) may have been an unusually powerful developmental stimulus; Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, and Lothal optimally spaced as “central places”; takeoff coincides with some burned-building evidence of regional warfare. Yet overall summary also states long-distance trade volume was low and mainly luxuries—little importance to complexity evolution; population growth slow; no agricultural pressure until long after collapse. Mesopotamian elements present (stone boxes, dice, faience, wheeled vehicles, shaft-hole axes, motifs), but commerce was one-sided (little SW Asian material in the Indus).

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** highland village-farming spread into the Indus floodplain after ~5000 BCE; discontinuous evolution—initial complexity moves stalled while peripheries became cores (Mesopotamia parallel); not automatic outcome of productive farming plus ecology (as of W&O).
- **Associated with (not identity):** Mehrgarh Neolithic package; Ghaggar-Hakra settlements; possible Magan/Meluhha trade names.
- **Compared with:** Egypt (less mortuary inequality; different urbanism); Mesopotamia/[[uruk-culture]] (weaker palaces/tombs; stronger planned sewers; one-sided trade); China/[[shang-civilization]] (all three as riverine primary-civilization cases in the textbook).
- **Succeeded by:** post-urban regional diversity; Ganges-centered cities and state-level systems after ~1100 BCE incorporating Harappan elements (textbook narrative).

## Identity Cautions

- **Etic construct.** “Harappan” / “Indus civilization” are archaeological labels; no secure endonym.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Archaeological Harappan ≠ single ethnos ≠ single language ≠ genetic population. Kenoyer (via W&O): early interpretations treated Indus civilizations as one ethnic group; evidence suggests mosaics of ethnic groups, languages, and adaptations—like Mesopotamia.
- **Script ≠ language proven.** Dravidian/Elamite-family proposals unproven; do not smuggle later Sanskrit/Aryan ethnonyms into Mature Harappan identity.
- **Ghaggar-Hakra / Saraswati.** River name in later texts is not an ethnonym for Harappan people.
- **State vs chiefdom** and **decline causes** are open scholarly positions, not settled wiki-voice facts.

## Historiography

Textbook entry point only (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007). Long neglected relative to Egypt/Mesopotamia for lack of pyramids/palaces and later maturity; critical phases buried under alluvium and the water table—knowledge “in some ways less than that of Egypt and Mesopotamia.” Discovery/excavation history from Marshall onward; Piggott stereotype vs Shaffer/Kenoyer/Possehl reanalyses. Daniel Miller: by 4000 BCE communities across Pakistan traded marine shell and lapis, showed architectural planning, and produced competitive regional pottery styles; several Indus-plain and eastern Iranian regions moved toward complexity before the Indus plain became the center; Mature Harappan changes after ~2600 BCE had indigenous revolutionary character. Specialist monographs, script work, and post-2007 aDNA are outstanding for later densification.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Elman Service (1975) read Harappan political organization as a "priestly state," unfortified and lacking clear evidence of coercive force — resembling Egypt's Old Kingdom rather than Sargon's military empire — against Woolley's conquest-state reading and Childe's merchant-class reading, which Service called "an unwarranted inference." He reported Malik's argument that the Indus system was a chiefdom rather than a state, and speculatively linked its long-lived chiefdom organization to the later crystallization of the Hindu caste system via endogamy and craft/regional specialization — an alternative Service judged more "functional-historical" than the standard racial-conquest account of caste origins. Following Malik, he held the civilization's demise (as of Service 1975) reflected political decay and loss of central authority rather than climatic cataclysm or invasion, and — against Childe — that Indus civilization preceded urban demography rather than resulting from it. See [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]], [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]], and [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. (Source: [[service-origins-state-1975]].)

## Possehl, *The Indus Civilization* (2002)

[[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl (2002)]] gives the fullest specialist reframing of the culture yet ingested here: he argues explicitly for "archaic sociocultural complexity... without the state," classifying the civilization as "more corporate than exclusionary," a heterarchy of councils rather than a kingship (see [[indus-polity-debate]]) — a position, not a consensus finding. He lists "myths to discard": no proven priest-kings; five great cities, not the traditional twin-capital model — Mohenjo-daro, [[harappa|Harappa]], [[dholavira|Dholavira]], [[ganweriwala|Ganweriwala]], [[rakhigarhi|Rakhigarhi]]; non-defensive "citadels" (retaining walls for elevated platforms, not fortifications); unconfirmed "granaries"; and a civilization that was regionally diverse in material culture, not uniformly a baked-brick tradition — organized into his "Domains" (Sindhi, Kulli, Sorath, Eastern, Northwestern, Punjab, Cholistan). He gives the civilization's extent as **over 1,000,000 km²**, a larger figure than the ~680,000 km² cited elsewhere in this wiki's frontmatter; the discrepancy likely reflects different area definitions (core distribution vs. total contact zone) and should not be silently resolved. Possehl periodizes the whole span from early food production to the Iron Age as the "[[indus-age|Indus Age]]," and frames the civilization's end (~1900 BCE) as internally-driven transformation, not collapse or conquest (see [[deurbanization-of-the-indus]]). He preserves, without endorsing, Marshall's 1931 speculation that Harappan speech belonged to the Dravidian group — filed `associated_with`, not identity, per the four-way non-identity rule. See also [[possehl-gregory-l]], [[indus-script]], [[middle-asian-interaction-sphere]].

## Kenoyer, *Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization* (1998)

[[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] (1998) is the craft- and urban-infrastructure specialist synthesis companion to Possehl 2002. He adopts [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]]'s **[[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]]** multi-era frame (foragers → regionalization → integration/Harappan Phase ~2600–1900 BCE → localization) rather than treating Mature cities as an isolated spike. Settlement scale is given as on the order of **~1,500 sites over ~680,000 km²** (Kenoyer's figure; contrast Possehl's >1,000,000 km² extent claim — different definitions). Core descriptive emphases: planned multi-mound cities with wells, bathing rooms, and drains; **no** Mesopotamian-style palaces, temples, elite wealth burials, long texts, or warfare iconography; multi-elite political model (merchants, landowners, ritual specialists) framed as city-states rather than valley empire ([[indus-polity-debate]]); script as **undeciphered** but analyzable through use-contexts ([[indus-script-decipherment-debate]]); craft specialization (seals, stoneware, long carnelian beads, standardized weights) as status and control technology; decline as **Localization Era** continuity without invasion ([[indus-transformation-debate]]). Field base is the Harappa Archaeological Research Project (HARP, from 1986; co-directed with [[meadow-richard|Richard Meadow]] and others), with comparative material from [[mohenjo-daro]], [[chanhudaro]], [[nausharo]], [[allahdino]], [[miri-qalat]], [[lothal]], [[dholavira]], and [[mehrgarh]]. Exhibition catalogue objects (seals, [[priest-king-statue]], jewelry hoards) are primary illustration sources. Pre-aDNA (1998). See [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]], [[craft-specialization]], [[great-bath]], [[standardized-weights]].

## Wright, *The Ancient Indus* (2010)

[[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]] (2010) is the third major single-author Indus synthesis ingested here, and the most explicitly organized around **political economy** — the integration of agropastoral and craft-producing economies, landscape construction, urban form, and social difference. Distinctive emphases (all attributed to Wright): (1) A "**transformative technological style**" (after H.M.L. Miller) unifying Indus craft and city-building — artisans broke down and reconstituted raw materials (grinding stone, heating carnelian, vitrifying steatite) and analogously "metamorphosed" the natural landscape into humanized urban form (built platforms aligned to the Kirthar range and fixed stars at Mohenjo-daro). (2) A **differentiated-society** thesis: against D. Miller's (1985) reading of Indus homogeneity as concealed inequality, Wright argues status differences were *visible* in the differential value of artifacts, amenities, and architecture — house scale correlates with statuary, hoards, and rare-material access (see [[indus-polity-debate]], [[gender-in-the-indus-civilization]]). (3) A **city-state with "vertical integration"** political model (after Kenoyer 1997a, [[trigger-bruce|Trigger]] 2003, Charlton & Nichols 1997), with council-based governance and integration across great inter-city distances maintained chiefly by standardized material culture and trade rather than coercion; no centrally controlled irrigation (refuting a Wittfogel hydraulic-despotism reading). (4) An **"emerging polities"** framing of the Pre-urban period (peer-polity-like units linked by exchange), building on the [[mehrgarh|Mehrgarh]] Neolithic sequence and Ravi/Kot Diji phases. (5) **Uneven transformation** at the end (c. 1900 BCE): regional, "not wholly ecological," with a live dispute against Shaffer & Lichtenstein over Post-urban→PGW continuity (see [[indus-transformation-debate]]). Wright reaffirms Kennedy (2000) against the Aryan-invasion/massacre reading ([[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]]), and enforces four-way non-identity on BMAC contact (small-scale object exchange, not migration — [[bactria-margiana-archaeological-complex]]). Field base: the Beas River regional survey near Harappa and long involvement with HARP. New pages this ingest feeds: [[shortugai]], [[rehman-dheri]], [[sutkagen-dor]], [[sotka-koh]], [[balakot]], [[kulli-culture]], [[bactria-margiana-archaeological-complex]]. Synthesis horizon ~2009, pre-Rakhigarhi-aDNA. See [[wright-ancient-indus-2010]], [[wright-rita-p]], [[craft-specialization]], [[standardized-weights]], [[meluhha]].

## Potts densification (1997): Mesopotamian trans-shipment evidence and the Meluhha question

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts, Mesopotamian Civilization 1997]], reasoning from Mesopotamian-side textual and material evidence toward the Indus/Harappan civilization; treat as evidence about contact and trans-shipment, never as evidence internal to the Indus record itself. See [[meluhha]], [[mesopotamian-trade]].)*

**Zebu cattle.** Faunal and figurine evidence from Mehrgarh (R.H. Meadow) and Shahr-i Sokhta (Compagnoni and Tosi) shows *Bos indicus* (zebu) rising from 4% to 65% of the faunal assemblage at Mehrgarh across the aceramic-to-mid-ceramic Neolithic (ending c. 5000 BCE), developed there from a locally distinct wild form (Meadow 1984) — establishing a South Asian/Baluchi origin for the species. Potts revises earlier scholarship (Van Buren 1939) that had misidentified numerous Ubaid-period and later Mesopotamian terracotta/glyptic bovine figures as zebu; most, he argues, are actually *Bos taurus* or *Bos primigenius*. The first clear zebu representations in southern Mesopotamia occur only in the early 2nd millennium BCE (an Isin-Larsa/Old Babylonian cylinder seal from [[ur]]; a stamp seal from Latifiya; a terracotta plaque from Tell Ischali); the zebu reached the Oman peninsula (Magan) by c. 2400/2300 BCE and appears on "Persian Gulf" stamp seals from Bahrain by the late 3rd millennium. Its transmission route to Mesopotamia — via Magan/Ur III contact, Dilmun trade, or overland Iran — is unknown, but its South Asian origin is not in dispute (attributed, position on route only).

**Water buffalo.** The water buffalo (*Bubalus bubalis*) first appears in Mesopotamian iconography on cylinder seals from the last third of Sargon of Akkad's reign (Boehmer 1975), including one belonging to a scribe of Enheduanna; it appears on seals of high Akkadian officials but disappears from the record at the end of the Old Akkadian period, not reappearing until the Sasanian era. R.M. Boehmer (position, followed by J.S. Cooper, B. Lion, and W. Heimpel) suggested it was introduced as a gift or tribute from Meluhha to Sargon, and that Mesopotamian herds died out after the Guti invasion/fall of Akkad. At Mehrgarh water buffalo were rare in the aceramic/early ceramic Neolithic (Meadow 1982); the animal appears on only 12 of Mohenjo-Daro's Harappan stamp seals (0.9%) versus 44 for zebu (3.1%) (Franke-Vogt 1991).

**Ivory.** Ivory imports into Mesopotamia are traceable from late Early Dynastic pre-Sargonic Lagash texts (figurines), through the earliest attestation of raw-ivory import (Gudea/Ur-Baba era; Heimpel 1987), to substantial Ur III quantities recorded at Ur (multiple texts totaling tens of kilograms). D. Collon (1977) concluded the ivory always derived from the Indian elephant (*Elephas maximus*), with elephants themselves imported only intermittently; the earliest confirmed elephant representation in Mesopotamian glyptic is a late Old Akkadian cylinder seal from Tell Asmar.

**Carnelian and the identification of Meluhha (position — see [[meluhha]]).** Potts identifies the primary carnelian source as the Ratanpur metamorphic beds of southern Gujarat (Asthana 1993), which still supply "the great majority of the chalcedony varieties used in the world" (Tosi 1980). Long barrel-cylinder carnelian beads of Harappan type are documented from the Royal Cemetery at [[ur]], Early Dynastic Kish, and Old Akkadian graves at Ur; etched barrel-cylinder beads believed Harappan were found at Tello in a Gudea/Ur III context. Citing the hymn *Lugal-e*, Ninurta received both lapis lazuli and carnelian from Meluhha in the time of Gudea (Heimpel 1993); later "Lipshur litanies" call Meluhha "KUR na4gug(-samtu)," land of carnelian (Leemans 1960). W. Heimpel (1993) takes this as a clear indication that "Meluhha" designated the Harappan civilization, or at least the Gujarat carnelian-supplying region — an interpretive, contested identification (`associated_with`, not settled identity) rather than established fact.

**Pingree's calendrical-convergence argument against an early end to contact (position).** Potts cites D. Pingree's documentation of "a remarkable congruence... between the early Vedic system of calendrics and Mesopotamian menology" (Pingree 1982) and the survival of *Summa alu*-series omen formulations in later Pali literature reaching northwest India during the Achaemenid period (Pingree 1992). He deploys this evidence explicitly against A.L. Oppenheim's earlier view (1954) that Mesopotamia–Meluhha contact effectively ended by the close of the Old Babylonian period, a view Oppenheim attributed to an "Aryan invasion" collapsing the Harappan civilization — a causal model Potts treats as now outdated. Potts frames the Mesopotamian archaeological/textual record's apparent sparseness on later Meluhha contact as potentially misleading given this astronomical/calendrical evidence, invoking "absence of evidence is not evidence of absence." This is recorded here as a live position on the duration and channels of Mesopotamia–South Asia contact, not as a settled finding about Harappan (post-urban) society itself.
