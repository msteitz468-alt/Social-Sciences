# Hittite Empire

## Definition and Construct History

The **Hittite Empire** is the Late Bronze Age Anatolian great power centered at [[hattusa]] (Boğazköy), known from its own cuneiform archives and from Egyptian, Assyrian, and Levantine correspondence. Unlike purely ceramic "cultures," it is textually self-documenting as a kingdom/empire — still not identical to a single genetic or linguistic population across its territory.

[[cline-eric-h|Cline]] (2014) treats the Hittites as one of the LBA "Great Powers," vassal overlord of [[ugarit]] and north Syria after Suppiluliuma I, rival then treaty-partner of Egypt, and a major casualty of the [[late-bronze-age-collapse]].

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

- **Material:** capital fortifications and temples at Hattusa; imperial art and seals; provincial archaeology.
- **Linguistic:** Hittite/Nesite archives; Luwian hieroglyphic and other languages.
- **Textual identity:** kings' names and treaties (e.g., with Egypt after Kadesh).

## Distribution and Chronology

Central Anatolia core; influence into western Anatolia, north Syria, and intermittently beyond. Collapse of the central empire early 12th century BCE; successor "Neo-Hittite" states continue in southeast Anatolia/north Syria.

## Lifeways

Agrarian base; imperial administration; chariot warfare; diplomacy within the LBA great-power club (Egypt, Mitanni, Assyria, Babylon, etc.).

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` [[ugarit]] (vassalage); conflicts and treaties with Egypt; wars with Mitanni and Assyria. End tied to [[late-bronze-age-collapse]] without equating all Anatolian change to [[sea-peoples]].

## Identity Cautions

Hittite kingdom ≠ all Anatolia; Luwian ≠ Hittite; Neo-Hittite Iron Age states are successors, not simple continuity of the empire.

## Historiography

Rediscovery via Boğazköy archives transformed Near Eastern history; collapse causation remains debated ([[late-bronze-age-collapse-causes-debate]]).

## Additional Material (from Roux, Ancient Iraq)

Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], the Old Hittite Kingdom crumbled after Mursilis I's sack of Aleppo and then [[babylon|Babylon]] (c. 1595 BCE, ending the First Dynasty of Babylon and opening the [[kassites|Kassite]] period there); Mursilis I was assassinated in 1590 BCE, and his successors abandoned Hittite claims south of the Taurus for a period of internal weakness.

Hittite power was rebuilt from 1380 BCE by **Suppiluliumas**, who conducted two Syrian campaigns across the Euphrates, plundered Washukkanni (the Mitannian capital — see [[hurrians]]), captured Qadesh, subjugated Nuhashshe and Qatna, and brought [[ugarit]] and Alalah under Hittite suzerainty, installing his own sons at Aleppo and Karkemish. He died in 1336 BCE with Hittite control extending to Damascus, while Assyria rose in this same period as the "second great power" (see [[assyrians]]). His successor Mursilis II (1335–1310 BCE) suppressed the Syrian revolts that followed Suppiluliumas's death.

The **Battle of Qadesh** (1300 BCE) between the Hittite king Muwatallis and Egypt's Ramesses II was inconclusive; the resulting peace treaty between Hattusilis III and Ramesses II survives in both Egyptian and Hittite/Akkadian versions and was sealed by a royal marriage — one of the best-attested diplomatic settlements of the Late Bronze Age.

The Hittite kingdom centered at Boğazköy (Hattusa) collapsed under invasion around 1200 BCE, linked by Roux to Thraco-Phrygian pressure; the archive record at the capital ends around 1190 BCE. Successor **"Neo-Hittite" states** — Luwian-speaking, using hieroglyphic Luwian script distinct from cuneiform Hittite — flourished in the Taurus and north Syria region from the 10th through 8th centuries BCE, before being absorbed by Assyria between 745 and 708 BCE, with some falling earlier to the [[aramaeans|Aramaeans]]. Roux stresses that "Neo-Hittite" is "no more than a convenient appellation": the break between the Hattusas-based empire and its successor states is political and linguistic, not a claim of simple ethnic continuity, and the Luwian-speaking population of these later states is linguistically (and possibly demographically) distinct from the population of the earlier cuneiform Hittite state. Source: [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] (sources_ingested: 2; last_updated: 2026-07-10).

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] adds current
consensus framing on the New Kingdom's governing structure and on the Neo-Hittite
successor states.

**New Kingdom vassalage structure.** Van De Mieroop explicitly compares (with an
explicit hedge — "often compared to") Hittite New Kingdom political organization to
medieval European vassalage: the Great King directly ruled the Anatolian core while
controlling Anatolia and Syria mainly through oath-bound vassals who could and did
switch allegiance; Hittite princes directly administered strategically important
centers (Carchemish, Aleppo), while other regions (Ugarit, Emar/Astata) retained their
own indigenous dynasties under Hittite suzerainty. He notes this structure paralleled
[[hurrians|Mitanni]]'s own — both let subject populations keep their own culture and
political hierarchies rather than pursuing administrative unification, in contrast to
the direct-provincial-annexation model the [[assyrians|Assyrians]] later developed.
(Attributed; Van De Mieroop.)

**The Ahhiyawa/Mycenaean equation.** Van De Mieroop reports, with an explicit hedge,
that "a scholarly consensus is growing" that Ahhiyawa in Hittite texts refers to the
Mycenaean Greeks (Homer's Achaians) — based on the name's similarity and Hittite
textual evidence of Mycenaean political/military interaction with western Anatolia.
He notes that Tudhaliya IV's treaty with Amurru originally listed the "King of
Ahhiyawa" among the recognized Great-King equals (alongside Egypt, Babylonia, and
Assyria) before that phrase was physically erased from the tablet — an unresolved,
textually visible retraction of status. This equation is recorded here as a growing
but not unanimous position, not a settled identification.

**Neo-Hittite continuity.** Van De Mieroop supplies a specific transmission mechanism
for the continuity already noted above (per Roux) between the Hittite empire and its
Iron Age successor states: Carchemish had served as the seat of a Hittite viceroy
(often a royal prince) since the 2nd millennium; when the central Hittite state
collapsed, **Kuzi-Teshub**, son of the last attested Carchemish viceroy, declared
himself "Great King of Hatti" and filled the resulting power vacuum in northern Syria.
His realm soon fragmented into successor kingdoms along the Euphrates, the most
prominent being **Melid** (near modern Malatya), whose rulers claimed descent from
Kuzi-Teshub. Van De Mieroop treats "Neo-Hittite" ("Hatti" in Assyrian usage) as a
modern/Assyrian-derived label rather than a self-designation, and reads the Neo-Hittite
states' script (Anatolian hieroglyphs), language (Luwian, an official Hittite-era
language), and political ideology (rulers presenting themselves as descendants of
Hittite kings) as evidence of genuine continuity — while stressing, consistent with
this page's existing Identity Cautions, that this is political and linguistic
continuity through specific dynastic transmission (Carchemish → Kuzi-Teshub → Melid),
not identity between the "Neo-Hittite" label and any bounded ethnic or genetic
population. Elite Neo-Hittite wealth is inferred largely from citadels, palace
architecture, and orthostat relief carving in a distinctive style the Assyrians
themselves admired and imitated (calling porticoes "in the style from Hatti") — an
inference Van De Mieroop flags explicitly as resting on material evidence alone, since
"there are no written records to demonstrate how the elites ruled." The Neo-Hittite
states were absorbed piecemeal by Assyria: paying tribute under Shalmaneser III (9th
century), then gradually incorporated as provinces under Tiglath-pileser III (mid-8th
century), with Urartu and, in some cases (e.g. Hamath by the 8th century), Aramean
political takeover intervening in the interim.
