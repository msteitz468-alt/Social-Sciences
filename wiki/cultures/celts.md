# Celts

## Definition and Construct History

**"Celt" is a contested multi-layer construct**, not a single archaeological culture, language, genetic population, or self-conscious people across all times and places. Cunliffe (1997) treats three partially overlapping streams that must **not** be silently equated (four-way non-identity — see [[celticity-debate]]):

1. **Classical ethnonyms** — Greek *Keltoi*, Latin *Celtae*, and related *Galli*/*Galatae* used by Mediterranean writers for communities north of the Alps and in parts of Iberia and Asia Minor.
2. **[[celtic-languages]]** — a branch of Indo-European (close to Italic in many reconstructions).
3. **Material packages** — especially [[hallstatt-culture]] and [[la-tene-culture]] of temperate Europe, plus regional variants such as [[celtiberian-culture]].

Cunliffe’s methodological stance (Ch. 1–2) is that Greco-Roman texts are partial, manipulative, and stereotyped, yet too rich to discard if read critically; that modern “Celtomania” and total terminological nihilism are both sterile extremes; and that real threads remain — peoples named Celts/Gauls/Galatians in contemporary sources, a living and historically reconstructible Celtic language, distinctive material culture and art, and Insular vernacular traditions — while presentist distortion must be guarded against (R1-045, R1-046, R1-087).

He frames the “concept of the Celts” as having **many realities** born of different disciplines: a classical literary/sculptural “reality”; eighteenth–nineteenth-century Celtic linguistics plus Insular vernacular literature; the archaeological record of weapons, settlements, and economies; and, from the 1930s–40s, Celtic art as a bridge among them — each valid within its parameters yet producing different images (R1-047). In the Retrospect he argues that “Celticness” has existed only through observers’ interpretations, from Polybius to Yeats, and that whether first-millennium BCE lineages felt a shared international Celticness “will always remain in doubt” (R5-041). His presentist definition of modern Celtic identity is self-ascription: “Perhaps the only real definition of a Celt, now as in the past, is that a Celt is a person who believes him or herself to be Celtic” (R5-040).

Within Cunliffe’s long chronological frame, communities speaking Celtic and known to contemporary writers as ethnically distinctive “Celts” or “Gauls” emerge inside a “middle cycle” of European development he dates c.1300 BCE–AD 400 (R1-081). Social and economic processes detectable already in the Urnfield continuum of central Europe c.1300–1000 BCE create, he argues, a structure largely maintained for the next half-millennium, by which time Greek historians identify Celts in the region — a structural backdrop, not automatic ethnic identity of pots with people (R2-007).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

### Material

The conventional archaeological package associated with “historic Celts” in culture-historical scholarship is [[la-tene-culture]], named for the type-site [[la-tene]] (Lake Neuchâtel). Cunliffe historicizes the equation: only in 1871, when Champagne and La Tène assemblages were matched to burials at Etruscan Marzabotto, could historic Celts “confidently be identified” archaeologically; thereafter La Tène-type finds were widely treated as “the material culture of the Celts” and as a dating horizon tied to classical chronology (R1-036, R1-070). Hans Hildebrand’s 1872 division of the Iron Age into earlier Hallstatt and later La Tène cemented the constructs, with La Tène regarded as the archaeological expression of the Celts (R1-071). Cunliffe himself insists the equation is incomplete: La Tène reflects one cultural complex’s emergence, expansion, and evolution over only three or four centuries, whereas Celtic languages were spoken earlier and more widely (R1-060, R5-042). Iberia is his sharpest test case — classical Celtiberians, Celtic speech, and social systems resembling temperate European “Celts,” yet material culture markedly different from La Tène type — so La Tène must be dissociated from the concept “Celtic” (R3-058). Extreme paucity of La Tène material in Greece and Asia Minor, where Celts were historically active, is for him a salutary warning that absence of evidence is not evidence of absence (R1-037).

Earlier Iron Age background is [[hallstatt-culture]] (type-site [[hallstatt]]): Ramsauer’s 1846–63 cemetery excavations (~1000 graves, mainly seventh–sixth centuries BCE) were conventionally called “Celtic”; later seriation (Tischler, Reinecke) underpinned twentieth-century periodization (R1-068, R1-072). Elite Hallstatt packages (long swords, horse trappings, vehicle burials) mark graded aristocracy and Mediterranean/eastern contacts, not language identity (see [[hallstatt-culture]]).

[[celtic-art]] (Jacobsthal’s Early Style, Waldalgesheim, Plastic, Sword styles; Franks’s earlier “Late Keltic” appreciation) is treated by Cunliffe as a bridge among classical vision, vernacular mystery, and mundane archaeology — classificatory styles rather than explanation of ethnicity (R1-078, R1-079, R1-080). Later Iron Age nucleated defended settlements ([[oppida]]), coinage, and production intensification after c.200 BCE are, in his model, partly catalysed by Roman economic demand on the barbarian periphery (R5-018, R5-046).

### Linguistic

See [[celtic-languages]]. Cunliffe’s synthetic claim: if a Celt is one who spoke Celtic, linguistic geography **uncouples** “Celt” from archaeologically defined La Tène — the language was spoken considerably earlier and over a more extensive area (R1-060). Classical ethnonyms and Celtic speech only partially coincide (e.g. no ancient author called the inhabitants of Britain and Ireland “Celts,” yet Celtic language was established there by the sixth century BCE on Cunliffe’s reading; Pytheas places Pretanic islands north of the land of the Celts and does not treat Pretani as Celtic — R1-005, R3-079).

### Genetic

Not addressed in Cunliffe 1997 (pre-nuclear aDNA synthesis, as of Cunliffe 1997). Do not invent genetic Celts from this source. Later archaeogenetic claims require independent, date-stamped sources.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Classical ethnonym geography (as Cunliffe presents it).** Fourth- and third-century BCE movements thrust warriors and sometimes migrating communities into Italy, Greece, and Asia Minor: Roman conflict from advance toward Rome soon after 400 BCE to Telamon (225 BCE); Greek confrontation around the invasion of 280–278 BCE culminating at Delphi; Galatians settled in Asia Minor after migration from Europe (R1-007). Celtic mercenaries appear with Iberians in the Sparta–Thebes war of 367 BCE after pressure from c.390 BCE (R1-001). Third century BCE marks the greatest extent Cunliffe assigns to the “Celtic world”: Celtiberians in north-central Spain; Celtic language to Ireland; La Tène among Insular communities; Po Valley and Galatia; Black Sea shores; Carpathians/southern Poland; Delphi, Egypt mercenaries, Alexander’s Babylon (R5-023). Asia Minor Galatians (Tolistobogii, Tectosages, Trocmi) are treated as migrant populations seeking land (half non-combatants among ~20,000), not only raid warriors (R2-068); eastern movers may have been of mixed ethnicity yet are “legitimately” considered here because contemporaries recognized them as Celts/Gauls/Galatians and some spoke Celtic (R4-036).

**Archaeological packages.** Hallstatt C–D floruit roughly mid-first millennium BCE in the Rhine–Danube zone; La Tène from fifth century BCE through Roman conquest; Iberian Celtiberian developments parallel but materially distinct (R3-067, R5-044). Atlantic façade: long reciprocal exchange with west-central Europe c.1300–600 BCE, then slackening and migratory disruption — cultural convergence without requiring invasion for archaic Celtic speech, on Cunliffe’s thesis (R4-018).

**Roman conquest and survival.** Second Punic War and aftermath shift the balance: Celtiberians/Lusitani to Numantia 133 BCE; Po Valley campaigns; Galatia 189 BCE; southern Gaul annexed; long interface Pyrenees–Illyria (R5-023). After c.200 BCE Cunliffe models concentric peripheries of decreasing complexity from the Mediterranean: oligarchies/market zones near the frontier; kingdoms on [[oppida]]; prestige-burial zones; simpler northern zones classical writers call “German” — the Rhine Celt/German divide is for him a convenient commentator construct, not a deep ethnic line before the mid-first century BCE (R5-025, R5-026). By end of first century CE, Celtic lands south of Rhine–Danube under Rome; Bohemia/Moravia germanized; only N/W Scotland and Ireland free of romanization; survival of “Celtic culture” tracks intensity of romanization in core / peripheral / free zones (R5-037). Vernacular Celtic languages and literary traditions survive on the Atlantic fringe into the medieval and modern periods (R5-039, R5-040, R5-047).

**Four-phase periodization (Cunliffe’s Retrospect).** (1) Formative Late Bronze Age c.1300–750 BCE; (2) early chiefdoms 750–450 BCE as Mediterranean economy impinges; (3) systems-collapse and diaspora 450–200 BCE creating the warrior society that gave the classical world its “northern barbarian” stereotype and archaeologists a material culture/art called “Celtic”; (4) Roman Mediterranean culture spreads via trade, army, then provincial administrators until only isolated Atlantic-fringe communities remain free (R5-045, R5-047).

## Lifeways

**Classical ethnography (attributed, agenda-laden).** Plato’s *Laws* presents Celts as warlike and hard-drinking — Cunliffe allows possible factual basis rather than pure stereotype (R1-002). Aristotle’s hardy northern package (harsh child-rearing, warlike irrationality, hospitality rules, scant attention to women) is judged a compound of scraps within a vision of how barbarians ought to be (R1-003, R1-004). The fourth–third-century “schoolboy sketch” of wild fearless warriors, unreliable allies, human sacrifice, and cannibalism is read as standard “enemy from without” rhetoric with elements of truth from two centuries of conflict (R1-008, R1-009). Historians (Polybius, Livy, Pausanias) cast Celts as the antithesis of rational state order yet as worthy opponents of savage nobility (R1-010). Poseidonius (c.135–c.50 BCE) supplies the principal ethnographic tradition (via Strabo, Diodorus, Athenaeus; possibly Caesar Book VI): Stoic soft primitivism, “rosy glow,” Druids as natural/moral philosophers, accommodation even of headhunting (R1-014–R1-017). Caesar reports factions in every state/village/household; Tacitus, shift from kings to rival chiefs — comments Cunliffe reads as implicit justification for conquest, while Tacitus also uses Britons/Germans as allegory against Roman decadence and puts Calgacus’s “desolation and call it peace” in *Agricola* 30 (R1-018–R1-021). Cunliffe cautions that any reconstruction of Celtic social systems is biased by Greco-Roman prejudices and by the narrow time span and geography of sources; outline social articulation is possible but no general scheme applies to all Celtic societies at all times (R2-076, R3-035).

**Insular vernacular analogues.** Irish Ulster Cycle (esp. *Táin Bó Cúailnge*) scenes of feasting, hero’s portion, chariot warfare, single combat, severed heads, fosterage, and raiding look familiar from Poseidonius and Caesar — but Cunliffe rejects simple “window on the Iron Age” readings: Kenneth Jackson’s 1964 thesis is influential yet the cycle is composed by Christian monks; Mallory’s material-culture study finds Dark Age silver not La Tène; Ireland lacks continental-style large slashing swords and war-chariot fittings; the epic core may be continental tradition woven into indigenous tales (R1-061–R1-066). Old Irish law texts (seventh–eighth centuries CE origin) are at best a “distant and distorted echo” (R1-067).

**Economy and political form (late period).** Agrarian base, craft specialization, clientage, warfare, and prestige exchange ([[prestige-goods-economy]]) structure much of Cunliffe’s narrative. After c.200 BCE he argues Roman demand for slaves and markets for wine/surplus catalyses production intensification and [[oppida]] urbanization on the periphery (“Trade preceded the flag”) (R5-004, R5-018). Three broad social zones in the second/first centuries BCE: core tribes/early states with elected magistrates; intermediate kingdoms with large oppida; periphery still expressing status via sumptuous burial; older systems beyond to Rhine and in much of Britain/Ireland (R5-022). Coinage articulates gifts, dues, and exchanges as tribes transform toward urban-based states (R5-046). Religious specialists and sanctuaries including [[druids]] belong on those concept pages.

**Migrations.** See [[celtic-migrations]]: Po Valley, Balkan/Greek raids, Galatians, and elite expansions (e.g. Celtiberian -briga / ethnic designations as military élites more than mass movement — R3-071) are historical processes, not proof that all Celtic speakers migrated from a single homeland.

## Identity Cautions

- Classical “Celt/Gaul/Galatian” is a **Mediterranean label**, not a transparent self-ethnonym for all Iron Age Europeans; ethnonym inventory includes Pretani (not equated with Celts by Pytheas), Britons, Celtiberians, Galatians (R1-086).
- La Tène art style ≠ Celtic speech ≠ classical ethnonym ≠ genetic population (R1-060, R3-058, R5-042).
- No ancient author called Britain/Ireland’s inhabitants Celts (R3-079); language presence and ethnonym are separate claims.
- Iberia: language + classical Celtiberian + social resemblance without La Tène package (R3-058, R3-074).
- Eastern “Celts” of the diaspora are likely mixed ethnicity under classical labels (R4-036).
- Celt/German Rhine divide is a classical construct; graded material, social, and linguistic continua (R5-025).
- Modern Celtic national identities (Irish, Welsh, Breton, Galician, etc.) are historically layered reworkings and revivals; Cunliffe leaves survival-vs-revival to anthropologists and defines modern Celts by self-belief (R5-040).
- Antiquarian and Romantic “Celts” (Stukeley Druids at Stonehenge; megaliths as Celtic; Ossian; Gorsedd; nationalist heroes Boudica/Vercingetorix/Ambiorix/Viriathus) are historiographic objects, not Iron Age evidence (R1-026–R1-031).
- Vernacular literature is not direct ethnography of the La Tène period (R1-064–R1-066).

## Historiography

Cunliffe 1997 Ch. 1–2 is itself a historiography of the Celts as a European idea:

1. **Classical vision** — military experience, visual propaganda (Civitalba; Attalid Dying Gaul and Pergamene monuments equating Gauls with Titans/external chaos), Poseidonian soft primitivism, Caesar/Tacitus agendas (R1-011–R1-021). Once created, Cunliffe holds, this vision “has remained in the consciousness of all subsequent observers” (R1-013).
2. **Oblivion and awakening (c. fourth–seventeenth centuries CE)** — medieval manuscript survival of classical texts; print revival (*Agricola* c.1470; Caesar Venice 1511) feeding national prehistories (Camden’s *Britannia* 1586; Le Fevre 1532) (R1-023, R1-024).
3. **Noble Savage and Celtomania (c.1700–1870)** — New World analogies; Stukeley; Pezron; Macpherson’s Ossian; Iolo Morganwg; Breton megaliths as Celtic; Reinach’s wry summary of Celtomane beliefs (R1-025–R1-030). Cunliffe: mixed facts and fantasies, a necessary stage, yet “much of the romantic nonsense… still persists” (R1-029).
4. **Nationalism and state archaeology** — Boudica, Vercingetorix, Ambiorix, Viriathus as freedom symbols; Napoleon III’s Alesia/Bibracte programme and *Histoire de Jules César*; Musée des Antiquités Nationales 1863; pan-Celtic congresses and Breton separatism; 1949 Alesia allegory; EC-era Venice 1991 “Celts, the Origins of Europe” and Mitterrand’s Bibracte (R1-031–R1-035, R1-042–R1-044). Post-1970 shift toward economic/world-systems models and oppida/coinage research is for Cunliffe partly appropriate to European Community politics (R1-043).
5. **Archaeological equation 1870–1970** — 1871 Bologna = La Tène as Celts; invasionist models (Hawkes hillfort waves; Bosch-Gimpera “Two Celtic Waves”) until 1960s rethinking; *celticus domesticus* image under war-era settlement archaeology (R1-036–R1-041, R1-070–R1-074).
6. **Art and “coming of age”** — Franks 1863; Jacobsthal 1944 as the moment Celtic studies “came of age” for Cunliffe (R1-075–R1-080).
7. **Late twentieth-century contestation** — Chapman 1992 *The Celts: The Construction of a Myth* flagged as the most challenging work on validity of the concept (R5-048); Cunliffe’s own middle path between New Celtomania and nihilism (R1-046).

Reflexivity is built into Cunliffe’s programme (Jacquetta Hawkes: each generation gets the archaeology it deserves — R1-045). Collection coverage for this wiki rests on Cunliffe 1997 as the first major Celtic synthesis ingested; specialist linguistics, aDNA, and later sceptical monographs remain outstanding.
