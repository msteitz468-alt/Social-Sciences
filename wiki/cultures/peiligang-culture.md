# Peiligang Culture

## Definition and Construct History

Peiligang culture is an etic archaeological-culture construct naming more than 120
sites distributed across the middle Yellow River valley in Henan, dated approximately
7000–5000 BCE, as described in [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]].
The largest and most extensively studied sites are [[jiahu]] (Wuyang, ~5 ha) and Tanghu
(Xinzheng, ~30 ha), both interpreted as likely year-round settlements. `construct_status:
robust` — Peiligang is a well-defined regional material-culture construct with internal
settlement-type variability, not equated by the authors with a single society, language,
or genetic population.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Liu & Chen 2012).** Grinding stones and sickles account for 14–39
percent of stone toolkits across Peiligang sites. Use-wear and residue analyses at
Jiahu, Shigu, and Egou indicate sickles were used for cutting reeds and grasses and
possibly harvesting millet, as well as collecting fruit and nuts, while grinding stones
were used mainly to process acorns, along with beans, tubers, and millet. Millet starch
residue on grinding stones from Egou corroborates macrobotanical millet finds (Liu, L.
et al. 2010a, as cited in Liu & Chen 2012); starch grains identifiable as *Dioscorea*
sp. (yam) were also recovered on grinding slabs at Egou (8,500–7,000 cal BP). At Jiahu,
domesticated rice contributed only about 10 percent of plant macroremains (as of Zhao,
Z. and Zhang 2009), and the farming-tool proportion of the toolkit rose across three
occupation phases, from 6.8 percent (Phase I) to 11.4 percent (Phase II) to 53.4
percent (Phase III).

**Linguistic.** [[unknown]] — Liu & Chen do not propose a linguistic affiliation for
Peiligang populations; no linkage to a named language family is made in this source.

**Genetic.** [[unknown]] as of this source — stable isotope dietary analysis (Hu, Y.
et al. 2006) at Jiahu indicates a diet predominantly of C3 plants and C3-feeding
animals, but this is dietary, not population-genetic, evidence and is not treated by
the source as establishing genetic-population identity.

## Distribution and Chronology

Peiligang sites span the middle Yellow River valley in Henan, with over 120
identified. [[jiahu]] shows three occupation phases spanning roughly 7000–5500 BCE.
Liu & Chen (2012) propose a two-tier settlement model: alluvial-plain sites (larger,
with thicker deposits and more elaborate assemblages, interpreted as showing higher
sedentism and social complexity) versus hilly sites (smaller, with thinner deposits,
likely seasonal camps). The Tieshenggou site cluster in western Henan (upper Wuluo
River) illustrates this variability: a full-coverage regional survey located five late
Peiligang sites (ca. 5500–5000 BCE) spanning about 16 km, small and thinly deposited,
interpreted as seasonal task-group camps, while the nearby Wayaozui site had
high-quality thin black ware and lacked grinding stones and microliths — evidence, per
Liu & Chen, that site function varied significantly across ecological settings within
a small region.

## Lifeways

Peiligang has "long been regarded as having achieved a considerable level of
agriculture," but Liu & Chen (2012) argue the archaeological record instead indicates
a broad-spectrum subsistence strategy in which food production was only a minor
component. At Jiahu, a bioarchaeological study (Smith, B.L. 2005) found general health
indicators (life span, dental caries, anemia, periosteal reactions) worse than the
contemporary [[xinglongwa-culture]] population but better than the fully agricultural
[[yangshao-culture]] population at Shijia (ca. 4300–4000 BCE). At Jiahu, hunting and
fishing tools cluster with male burials, and grinding stones with female burials
across three contemporary cemeteries — a division of labor pattern the source treats
as attributed inference rather than established fact. Liu & Chen (citing Liu, L.
2004) characterize Jiahu society as broadly egalitarian, with some individuals
achieving elevated status through special, likely ritual, abilities, evidenced by
richer burials containing ritual paraphernalia. Jiahu also yielded elaborate burials
with turtle shells (used as rattles) and flutes made from red-crowned crane ulnae,
described by the source as the earliest known playable multinote musical instruments;
some turtle shells bear incised signs that anticipate but do not constitute later
Shang oracle-bone inscriptions (as of Li, Xueqin et al. 2003).

## Relations and Successions

Peiligang `preceded_by` an unresolved late-Pleistocene microlithic tradition —
Liu & Chen (2012) note that the site of Lijiagou helps establish a temporal link
between this tradition and early Peiligang (ca. 9000/8500–7000 cal BP) in the same
region, but flag that Lijiagou's pottery style differs notably from Peiligang's,
an unresolved research question. Peiligang `succeeded_by` [[yangshao-culture]] in
the middle Yellow River sequence; early-Yangshao mortuary patterns (e.g., at
Longgangsi) are described by Liu & Chen as continuing a group-oriented ancestral-cult
pattern first seen at the Peiligang-period Shuiquan site. Peiligang is treated as
`analogous_to` other early-Neolithic north China cultures ([[cishan-culture]],
[[houli-culture]], [[xinglongwa-culture]]) in subsistence pattern (broad-spectrum
economies with minor food production) but not as materially identical to them.

## Identity Cautions

Peiligang is a material-culture construct defined by ceramics, tools, and settlement
pattern. It is not equated with a language, an ethnic group, or a genetic population
by Liu & Chen (2012); no such equation is asserted in the source material reviewed.
Following the wiki's four-way non-identity rule, any future linkage of Peiligang to
a language family or genetic population must be attributed and flagged, not asserted
in wiki voice.

## Historiography

Peiligang and its type site were long treated in Chinese archaeology as evidence of
"considerable" early agriculture; Liu & Chen (2012) explicitly revise this consensus,
arguing the broad-spectrum-subsistence reading is better supported by tool-frequency
and residue data. See [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012]] for full source
discussion; [[jiahu]] carries the fullest single-site treatment.
