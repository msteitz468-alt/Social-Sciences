# Sumerians

**"Sumerian"** designates the population of southern Mesopotamia (roughly Nippur to the
Gulf) whose language, Sumerian, is attested in cuneiform from the late Uruk period
(c. 3300 BCE) onward. Following [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], this page treats
"Sumerian" strictly as a **linguistic** label, not an ethnic, racial, or genetic one —
see Identity Cautions below, which are load-bearing for this page and for the wiki's
four-way non-identity rule generally.

## Definition and Construct History

The name derives from the ancient toponym Sumer/Shumer, written in cuneiform
KI.EN.GI. At the dawn of Mesopotamian history (Roux 1992) three ethnolinguistic groups
coexisted in the region: Sumerians (dominant in the far south), Semites (dominant in
central Mesopotamia, "Akkad" after c. 2400 BCE; see [[akkadians]]), and a small, diffuse
group known only from a handful of non-Sumerian, non-Semitic personal and place names.
Roux states explicitly that the *only* reliable criterion distinguishing these groups
was language — not politics, technology, art, or religion, which all three shared. "The
Sumerians," in Roux's own words, were "as we all are, a mixture of races and probably of
peoples"; their civilization blended foreign and indigenous elements, and their
language belonged to a widespread but ill-defined typological family. He quotes an
unnamed "brilliant orientalist" that "the much discussed problem of the origin of the
Sumerians may well turn out to be the chase of a chimera."

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Sumerian material culture is continuous with the preceding [[uruk-culture]]
horizon — mudbrick temple architecture, cylinder seals, wheelmade pottery, monumental
sculpture — with no attested break marking an ethnic replacement (see
[[uruk-culture]] for the Roux material-continuity argument).

**Linguistic.** Sumerian is an **agglutinative isolate**: it has no known living or
dead relative and, per Roux, belongs typologically (not genetically) to a broad
category of agglutinative languages found "from Hungary to Polynesia" — a typological
comparison only, not evidence of common origin. Unlike the Indo-European or Semitic
cases, where shared language licenses an inference about shared origin/homeland,
Sumerian offers no such clue. Sumerian literature's setting — rivers, marshes, reeds,
tamarisks, palms — reads as indigenous to southern Iraq and gives no hint of an
outside homeland (attributed, Roux 1992).

**Genetic.** No genetic-population claim is defensible from this source. Roux reports
(and this page reproduces only as documented period historiography, explicitly
flagged, never as fact) the physical-anthropological literature available to him:
skulls from Sumerian graves showing a mix of dolichocephalic and brachycephalic types,
read as a blend of "Armenoid" and "Mediterranean" physical types (the latter "somewhat
predominant"). Roux states in his own voice: **"there is no such thing as a Sumerian
'race' neither in the scientific nor in the ordinary sense of the term."** He further
argues that the conventional "Sumerian" facial features seen on Mesopotamian monuments
(big fleshy nose, huge eyes, thick neck, flat occiput) are a stylistic artistic
convention, not evidence of a physical type — the same features appear on statues of
individuals bearing Semitic names from the largely Semitic city of [[mari]], while more
"realistic" portraits (e.g. of Gudea of [[lagash]], himself Sumerian-speaking) show
different features. Roux uses this to argue that physical typology is simply useless
for adjudicating the Sumerian question. (as of Roux 1992; superseded methodologically —
no modern aDNA study is cited or implied here.)

## Distribution and Chronology

Sumer proper covered roughly 30,000 km² (comparable to Belgium), concentrated along the
Euphrates and its irrigation canals, with a linguistic frontier against Semitic-speaking
Akkad falling "somewhere between [[kish]] and [[nippur]]" (Roux 1992). Chronologically,
Sumerian-speaking political dominance runs from the Early Dynastic period (c. 2900 BCE)
through the fall of the Akkadian empire, and is reasserted once more — for the last time
— under the Third Dynasty of Ur (Ur III, c. 2112–2004 BCE) before permanent Semitic
(Akkadian and Amorite) political dominance of Mesopotamia for roughly the following
1,500 years (Roux 1992).

## Lifeways

**Early Dynastic Sumerian society** (per Roux's synthesis of Presargonic tablets,
chiefly from Girsu/Lagash and Shuruppak) divided into three broad layers, with the
ruler atop the structure: (1) **slaves**, mostly war captives or foreign kidnap
victims, never very numerous; (2) landless peasants and workers dependent on temple or
palace; (3) landowning **"freemen,"** ranging from artisans to members of the royal
family. Roux explicitly revises an older scholarly model in which all city-state land
was assumed to be temple property and all inhabitants temple dependents — a view that
arose from the chance survival of an unusually large archive from the Baba temple at
Girsu; modern reappraisal of Presargonic tablets shows a mixed economy of temple-land,
palace-land, and private land coexisting (see [[temple-economy]]). Temple land
(roughly a third of arable land around a city) was itself subdivided into "land of the
Lord" (feeding priests/officials), "food land" (allotted, revocable, not full
ownership), and "plough land" (let to tenants for a harvest share). Occupational
specialization was extensive — tablets from Girsu and Shuruppak record separate
shepherds for male and female donkeys, fishermen divided by water type, even a
"corporation" of snake-charmers with its own chief — under a bureaucracy of scribes,
controllers, overseers, and a high priest (*sanga*).

**Metalworking.** The Early Dynastic period, evidenced above all by the Royal Cemetery
finds at [[ur]], represents (per Roux) a peak of Sumerian goldsmithing and metalworking
skill: Sumerian craftsmen pioneered or advanced *cire perdue* (lost-wax) bronze casting
and repoussé work in precious metals, a level of craftsmanship Roux calls "unequalled
in any other contemporary civilization" (attributed superlative judgment). Raw metal
was imported and paid for with cereals, hides, wool, textiles, manufactured goods, and
bitumen.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** [[uruk-culture]] and, at greater time-depth, the [[ubaid-culture]]
  (per the "already-present" position in the Sumerian problem debate below).
- **Succeeded (politically) by:** Sargon of Akkad's dynasty ([[akkadians]]), then, after
  the last Sumerian-led revival, by [[amorites|Amorite]] successor states (Isin,
  Larsa) — see below.
- **The "Sumerian renaissance": Ur III and its Isin aftermath.** Roux frames the Third
  Dynasty of Ur (c. 2112–2004 BCE, "Ur III" or "Neo-Sumerian") as "one of the most
  brilliant periods" of ancient Iraqi history and an artistic/literary renaissance —
  but also, implicitly, the **last Sumerian-led major state** before permanent Semitic
  political dominance. Shulgi (Ur-Nammu's son) founded the scribal schools of Ur and
  Nippur "to which we owe so many masterpieces of Sumerian literature." After Ur III's
  fall (2004 BCE), the following Isin-dynasty kings were themselves Semitic/Akkadian by
  origin (e.g. Ishbi-Irra, a general native to [[mari]]) — yet these kings
  "considered themselves the true successors of the Sumerian kings of Ur," adopted Ur
  III royal titles, were deified in the Ur III manner, and used Sumerian exclusively in
  official inscriptions. Nearly all surviving great works of Sumerian literature from
  Nippur's temple "library" were in fact composed or copied under this Semitic Isin
  patronage. Roux's own analogy, offered as interpretive framing rather than settled
  fact: **"Sumer in those days was like the declining Roman empire where everything was
  Latin, save the emperors."**

## Identity Cautions — the "Sumerian Problem"

This page enforces the wiki's four-way non-identity rule with unusual explicitness,
because Roux himself makes the methodological point in his own voice (quoted above):
language is the *only* reliable criterion separating "Sumerians" from their neighbors;
culture, technology, religion, and (per Roux) physical type were shared or
indistinguishable across the region's linguistic groups. "Sumerian," strictly, means
"Sumerian-speaking" and nothing more — it is not a claim about race, a self-conscious
political nation, or a genetically distinct population.

**The Sumerian Problem (open debate — see [[sumerian-problem-debate]]).** The origin of
Sumerian-speakers in Mesopotamia is a live, unresolved scholarly dispute with at least
two named positions:

1. **Migration-during-Uruk:** Sumerians (or Sumerian-speakers) arrived in Mesopotamia
   during the Uruk period, into an already-populated land.
2. **Already-present-since-Ubaid:** Sumerians, or their linguistic ancestors, were
   already present in southern Mesopotamia by Ubaid times at the latest — i.e., they
   may have "always" been in Iraq.

Roux states his own leaning toward position (2) but frames this explicitly as his
personal view within an open debate, not as settled fact. Arguments he gives against
migration-during-Uruk: the first appearance of Sumerian writing at the *end* of the
Uruk period does not prove the language was not spoken earlier; non-Sumerian/non-Semitic
place-names do not necessarily indicate a displaced prior population; the Uruk-period
pottery change likely reflects mass production rather than invasion; and postwar
excavations across Turkey, Iran, Baluchistan, Afghanistan, and Central Asia found
nothing resembling Uruk/Jemdat Nasr material culture, nor any Sumerian inscriptions,
abroad (used by Roux as negative evidence against an external migration route). Tracing
material continuity backward, Roux links Uruk/Jemdat Nasr Sumerian material culture to
antecedents in northern Iraq in the 6th–5th millennia BCE (Choga Mami links the Samarra
culture to the early Ubaid stages at Eridu and Hajji Muhammad) — but he explicitly
**warns against equating the Samarrans with the Sumerians or Ubaidians "on the sole
basis of their pottery,"** naming the pots-≠-people trap directly.

**Pots, genes, and language are not interchangeable.** Consistent with the above, do
not read any archaeological "Sumerian" material-culture horizon as proof of linguistic
or genetic Sumerian identity, and do not read the (dated, rejected-by-Roux-himself)
racial-typology literature reported above as a finding about a real population. The
only load-bearing identity claim this page endorses in wiki voice is linguistic:
Sumerian-speakers are attested from the late Uruk period, dominant in the far south of
Mesopotamia at the dawn of the historical record, and politically eclipsed (though not
culturally erased) by Semitic-speaking dynasties from the Akkadian period onward.

## Historiography

Roux's synthesis is a single-author narrative history (3rd ed. 1992); his treatment of
the Sumerian Problem is itself a documented historiographic stance within an
unresolved debate, and his reporting of early-20th-century physical-anthropological
racial typology is presented and then explicitly refuted in his own text — reproduce
that refutation, never the typology, as this page's authoritative content. See
[[sumerian-problem-debate]] for the debate page recording both positions in full, and
[[akkadians]] for Roux's parallel and equally explicit rejection of a "Semitic race."

## Social Structure (Postgate 1994)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]] reconstructs Sumerian (and, by the
Old Babylonian period, broader southern Mesopotamian) social organization directly from
legal, administrative, and lexical texts rather than narrative sources — attributed
throughout, since it rests on documentary inference rather than convergent evidence.

**Clan and household.** Scattered third-millennium evidence attests **clan or tribal
groupings** (Sumerian *im-ru-a* / *im-ru*) within long-settled urban communities, larger
than the individual family: Gudea's inauguration labor for the E-ninnu temple at Girsu
was drawn from three *im-ru-a* groups, each carrying a symbolic standard (*šu-nir*)
named for a major deity; an Early Dynastic Shuruppak text records "539 boys from 7
clans," implying each clan comprised several families. Postgate flags as an open
question whether clan affiliation appearing more often in earlier documents reflects a
real historical decline in clan importance or merely a documentary artifact of later,
narrowly legal record-keeping. By Old Babylonian times the normal residential and
social unit was clearly **patrilineal and patrilocal**: men were identified by
patronymic (a naming practice Postgate compares to "Russia and the Near East today"),
and producing a son/heir carried strong social and religious weight (old-age support;
appeasing the parent's spirit after death via the *kispum* libation rite). Postgate,
following Laslett (1972), draws a foundational distinction between the **family**
(members related by blood/marriage, inherently vague in scope) and the **household**
— a "co-resident domestic group" with comparatively precise limits at any given
moment — and treats the household as the basic unit of Sumerian and Babylonian social
and economic life (see [[household]]).

**Marriage law.** Old Babylonian marriage was fundamentally a bond between kin groups,
not simply individuals, reconstructed by Postgate as a four-stage process: betrothal;
reciprocal exchange payments (*terhatum*/*kirrum* from the groom's side, *níg.dé.a* from
the bride's); the bride's physical move to the father-in-law's house; and the
establishment of co-habitation. Legally, marriage required **both** a contract
(*inim-kešda*/*riksatum*) and a feast (*kirrum*) — per the Ešnunna code (§§27–8),
cohabitation for a year without these formalities did not make a woman a wife, while
performing them did, even absent cohabitation. A long historiographic debate over
whether the groom-side payment (*terhatum*) constituted "*Kaufehe*" (purchase-marriage)
is recorded by Postgate as a live scholarly dispute; he leans toward "no," since the
payment was often returned at the wedding's conclusion, characterizing it instead as "a
cautionary down-payment or earnest of good faith." Divorce required the spoken
formula "You are not my husband"/"You are not my wife," but the consequences were
**markedly asymmetric by gender**: per the lexical text *ana ittišu*, a wife who
rejected her husband was to be drowned, while a husband rejecting his wife paid a fine
of half a mina of silver — a contrast Postgate notes "remains just as stark" even
though these were nominally contract terms the woman's family had itself agreed to. He
argues actual practice was probably less harsh than the formal penalties suggest, since
divorce required serious cause and unreasonable divorce carried social stigma.

**Childlessness, concubinage, and the *nadītum*.** A documented legal solution to
childlessness was for the wife to supply a slave-girl to bear children on her behalf;
such children counted legally as "her sons" (Code of Hammurapi §163) — a practice
Postgate compares to the biblical Sarah/Hagar narrative. Whether a wife had borne sons,
including via a slave surrogate, significantly affected her protection in divorce,
since sons linked her permanently to the patrilineal family regardless of the parties'
wishes. The **nadītum**, a special class of priestess, could marry but was not
permitted to cohabit with her husband; if she had provided a slave-girl who bore him
sons, he could not additionally take a concubine, while if she had not, he could —
though the concubine could never be raised to the nadītum's status (CH §§144–5).
**Monogamy** (one recognized wife at a time) was the norm across second- and
third-millennium evidence, with narrow exceptions (CH §148 permits a second wife if the
first is incapacitated by illness; certain nadītum cases). Slave-girl concubinage was
legally distinct from and tolerated alongside formal monogamous marriage; CH §§170–4
permit, without requiring, children of such unions to inherit from the paternal estate.

**Domestic slavery.** Domestic slaves, male and female, were regular members of
Mesopotamian households, listed as property in inheritance divisions and frequently
sold, but Postgate's evidence indicates the practice's actual scale was **limited**:
"one or more slaves are found in all partitions of sizeable estates," but households
with as many as ten of each sex, or fourteen male and six female slaves, were rare or
occasional. Slaves were acquired through war, purchase, or (rarely) birth into slavery
within the household (*wilid bītim*, "house-born slave"); runaway slaves were pursued
via public herald announcements and a distinctive hairstyle/mark, removable only by
formal manumission.

**Women outside the patrilineal household.** Widows who did not return to their
father's house, women who left "after the man of her heart," prostitutes associated
with public urban space, and "ale-wives" are poorly documented precisely because the
legal record by nature focuses on property-holding family units — Postgate flags this
as a structural documentary silence, not evidence of these women's rarity or
insignificance.

**Four-way non-identity, in Postgate's own words.** Independently of Roux, Postgate
states the wiki's four-way non-identity principle explicitly and sharply, warning
against ethnic/migration speculation about "who were the Ubaid people" and where the
Sumerians "came from." He quotes and criticizes older scholarship's claims that
material-culture change "betray[s] the intrusion of a forceful and heterogeneous ethnic
group," and that "the Semites probably infiltrated into Southern Mesopotamia," as
going "beyond what can reasonably be deduced from the archaeological evidence."
Postgate further stresses that, historically, intrusive ethnic groups have rarely
imposed their own language on an existing population — in the region's history, only
Aramaic and Arabic did so — so the eventual dominance of Sumerian in the south does
**not** straightforwardly imply an originating ethnic migration or replacement. He
makes the same point regarding the Sumerian/Semitic-Akkadian north–south linguistic
divide centered on Kish: "We should not conclude that the north–south opposition was
caused by a linguistic or ethnic difference, since it is clear that speakers of the two
languages were peacefully integrated in both regions." Both pots-≠-people and
language-≠-ethnicity claims here are the direct textual basis for the wiki's four-way
non-identity rule, independent of and consistent with Roux's parallel treatment above.
(As of [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] states in his own voice the position Roux and Postgate anticipate above, and extends it into an explicit denial of "ethnic conflict" as a historiographic category for Early Dynastic Babylonia. He treats "Sumerian" purely as a linguistic category recoverable from onomastic (personal-name) evidence, not as a marker of ethnic origin or migration: "Personal names again are the prime marker of this linguistic multiplicity... the names of people are probably a better indicator of the language they spoke [than the language a text is written in]." This is because scribal practice tracked genre and training, not native tongue: "The scribes of Abu Salabikh, around the year 2500, bore Semitic names but wrote almost exclusively in Sumerian... A text written in Sumerian is thus not evidence that the author's mother tongue was Sumerian." Notably, Van De Mieroop does **not** frame a "Sumerian problem" (autochthonous-vs.-migrant origins) as a live ethnic-origins debate in the way older treatments (including Roux) do — he brackets the origins question and treats the linguistic evidence on its own terms.

Early Dynastic Babylonia, per Van De Mieroop, was linguistically mixed from very early — Sumerian names predominant in the south, a Semitic dialect ("proto-Akkadian") predominant in the north — but he explicitly **denies** that this linguistic division tracked or produced ethnic hostility: "This distinction did not lead to ethnic conflict, as has sometimes been argued. Members of the two linguistic groups lived side-by-side. Politically, Early Dynastic Babylonia was divided; culturally it was not." This directly qualifies any older "Sumerian vs. Akkadian/Semitic ethnic rivalry" framing that period historiography (including some of Roux's own reported-and-refuted material above) might suggest — political fragmentation among city-states, not ethnic division, was the operative reality. He records the identical pattern for the multilingual Habur region (Semitic- and Hurrian-speakers): "This linguistic heterogeneity did not lie at the basis of any social or ethnic conflict to our knowledge" — a consistent authorial position that linguistic diversity does not automatically produce ethnic conflict in the 3rd-millennium Near East, reinforcing this page's existing four-way non-identity treatment and the [[sumerian-problem-debate]].

Van De Mieroop's framing of Early Dynastic Babylonia's political structure is one of competing, politically autonomous city-states (Kish, Uruk, Lagash, Ur, and others) sharing a single cultural and scribal tradition rather than a "Sumerian" or "Akkadian" political bloc — consistent with, and reinforcing, the city-state material already on this page and on [[uruk-culture]]. (as of [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]])
