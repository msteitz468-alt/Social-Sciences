# Fort Ancient Culture

Late prehistoric archaeological construct of the middle Ohio Valley (Ohio, Kentucky, West Virginia), treated in [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner 2021]] Ch. 7. **Etic** material-culture label — not an ethnonym.

## Definition and Construct History

Named from the Fort Ancient earthwork/site in Ohio; denotes **agricultural village** societies of the late prehistoric Ohio Valley, distinct from classic southern [[mississippian-culture|Mississippian]] platform-mound [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] though interacting with them. Part of a northern Eastern Woodlands mosaic with [[oneota-culture|Oneota]], Monongahela, and Owasco–Iroquois traditions (as of Milner 2021).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Milner 2021):**

- Permanent villages with heavy **maize** reliance (~from 1000 CE; beans after ~1300 in the northern pattern); often wooden **palisades**.
- Architecture and artifacts show **mixed cultural backgrounds** in some middle Ohio River communities (Mississippian interaction).
- Few large platform mounds relative to southern chiefdoms; limited mortuary ranking compared with [[cahokia]] or [[moundville]].

## Distribution and Chronology

Middle Ohio Valley from roughly the start of the second millennium CE through contact. Fourteenth-century climate stress (drought, transition toward Little Ice Age) worsened already tense intergroup relations across the northern woodlands (Milner synthesis).

## Lifeways

Sedentary maize farmers in clustered villages; consensus-oriented political organization often labeled “tribal” — **permanent inherited leadership notably lacking** (Milner position), with context-limited influence of senior or charismatic individuals. Obtained Mississippian objects and shared some mortuary ideas across cultural boundaries.

## Relations and Successions

- Neighbor/interactant of Mississippian worlds — objects, ideas, mixed communities.
- Parallel to [[oneota-culture]] as northern villager tradition.
- Convenient north-tribe / south-chiefdom contrast **masks overlap** in settlement size and everyday life (Milner).

## Identity Cautions

Culture ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Do not equate Fort Ancient pottery with a single historic tribe.

## Historiography

Culture-historical naming; modern settlement and interaction studies densify Mississippian–Fort Ancient contact.
