## Definition and Construct History

**Tiwanaku** (Tiahuanaco) designates the altiplano metropolis ~15 km east of Lake Titicaca and the expansive political-religious formation that integrated the southern demographic pole of Andean civilization during the later Early Intermediate Period and Middle Horizon. The construct is defined by monumental masonry, stelae, corporate ceramics/textiles, ridged-field agroscape, and caravan/colony distributions—not by a self-named ethnic ethnonym recovered in texts. Spanish awareness begins with Cieza de León (1549). Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) calls it the highest capital of an ancient empire the world has known and stresses political **longevity** beyond other Andean empires of its era while Moche and Nazca fell and [[wari-culture|Huari/Wari]] arose in Peru.

Five-phase occupation (as of Moseley 2001): radiocarbon back to ~400 BC; unpretentious until large architectural/agrarian projects in **Phase 3** (c. AD 100–375); **Classic Phase 4** (c. AD 375–600/700) regional hegemony and hierarchy of administrative centers at the south end of the lake; **Phase 5** far-flung colonies and caravanserai into the Chilean altiplano, with a managerial/political tightening after the AD 562–94 drought; decline with protracted drought ~AD 1100 and collapse shortly thereafter.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Urban core:** Monumental edifices and stelae once encircled by a shallow moat; beyond, up to ~10 km of adobe residential compounds (as of Moseley 2001).

- **Akapana:** Largest structure, southernmost great Andean platform—>15 m high, ~200 m on a side, T-shaped (widest east); summit sunken court amid stone buildings; six terraced steps (basal cyclopean sandstone, higher fine andesite); complex drainage so water cascaded like a moisture-giving *apu*; cache of deliberately smashed kero beakers; dozens of dismembered male bodies near basal terrace—apparent sacrifices.
- **Semi-subterranean court** (~30 m square, west of Akapana): tenoned heads; stelae including the Bennett monolith (largest known Andean stela)—Classic-style figure with kero and baton. Thunderbolt Stela half at the city, half at the north end of the lake—Moseley reads imperial policy of holding subject peoples’ sacred/ancestral objects **hostage** at the capital.
- **Kalasasaya:** Monolithic stairs/gateway; summit court with Ponce monolith; framed by one-room buildings; >100 m square; H-type masonry with gargantuan vertical slabs.
- **Putuni:** Interpreted as palace compound of rulers; finely carved water conduits—functional plus ritual water manipulation.
- **Pumapunku** (~1 km away): Finest andesite/sandstone masonry; 5 m high, 150 m square; megalithic facade with portals carved from single multi-ton andesite slabs; unexcavated sunken court behind.

**Gateway of the Sun and [[staff-god]]:** Decorated lintels show paired beings converging center in *tinku* fashion. Gateway of the Sun: frieze converges on forward-facing Gateway God on triple-tiered platform; headdress with 19 ray projections; two vertical staffs terminating in condor heads—Moseley reads resurrection/revitalization of the Chavín Staff God, “supreme synthesizer” of [[dual-organization]]; winged attendants with staffs of office; inferred focal point of tinku rituals uniting halves of society (as of Moseley 2001). Overview contrast (attributed): Tiwanaku/altiplano more conservative/traditional ideology vs Moche coast more secular/kuraka-oriented.

**Linguistic:** No secure language ID from the archaeological construct. LIP debate whether Aymara was always indigenous to the lake region or entered after Tiwanaku’s fall remains open (as of Moseley 2001)—do not equate Tiwanaku = Aymara.

**Genetic:** Not addressed with ancient DNA in this source (as of Moseley 2001). Chen Chen/Moquegua cemeteries show kin clustering and shared cranial deformation read as altiplano colonist osteology (Goldstein via Moseley)—osteological affinity, not population-genetic identity.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Heartland agrarian base:** Little hillside terracing interest; reclamation focused on **ridged (raised) fields** in flat lake-margin wetlands requiring high water table—most expansion after AD 200 as drought gave way to abundant rainfall. Pampa Koani hierarchy: major centers Pajchiri (north) and Luqurmata (south); smaller centers and farmstead mounds among fields. Estimated ~100,000 ha of ridge fields in production including ~75 km² wetlands on Pampa Koani; experimental rebuilds yield double+ plowed fields via frost mitigation and ditch-muck fertilizer; two harvests/year vs one for rainfall farming; lake levels historically fluctuate up to ~4 m/decade—delicate technology (as of Moseley 2001).

**Political models:** Agropastoral economy with llama caravans deep into Chile. Artifact distribution interpreted variously as centralized Inca-like organization vs segmentary maximal/mountain [[ayllu]] with internal hierarchies (field debate). San Pedro de Atacama (~800 km): among thousands of burials <2 dozen with fancy Tiwanaku goods—local people who trekked without political involvement. Azapa: minority interments may be local agents. Island of the Sun wrested from local señorío under imperial shrine management. Possible Cochabamba enclaves. West: mitmaq-like colonization in Moquegua arid sierra.

**Moquegua colony sequence (as of Moseley 2001):** Mid-valley first irrigated by Huaracane; modest Phase IV Omo settlers ~AD 550–600 (~500 rectangular cane rooms; chicha brewing with portrait-head keros; open undefended sites). Phase 5 **Chen Chen** phase: large settler influx, long corporate canals, hierarchical province, crops for local use and presumed export to Tiwanaku ~300 km away; only Tiwanaku platform mound outside Titicaca Basin at new Omo (120 m, three tiers, sunken court; local-stone stela head). Violent end—canals destroyed, buildings razed, cemeteries violated (dating vague—possibly linked to Huari Baúl retreat or later revolt). **Tumilaca** post-state: local wares, fortification, contraction, then irrigated terracing up-valley; coastal Chiribaya style perpetuates Tiwanaku legacy after imperial collapse. Moseley credits Huari’s brief intrusion with an agrarian legacy restructuring Moquegua sierra centuries later.

**Collapse (~AD 1100):** Drought dropped lake ~12 m and water table proportionally, creating dry standing ridged fields; populations dispersed to high-mountain rainfall farming; long-term Titicaca demographic decline (as of Moseley 2001). Capital abandonment may have involved intentional destruction (Akapana upper terrace walls; Pumapunku blocks pried up); Moseley “suspects but cannot prove” commoner uprising against divine-right lords. Later haphazard repairs (Gateway of the Sun moved to incongruous Kalasasaya position); numerous Inca artifacts lead Moseley to suspect Tahuantinsuyu masters resurrected ruins as a park validating lore (giants; Viracocha fashioning humans from lake clay at Tiwanaku)—speculative, attributed.

## Lifeways

True agropastoral state: ridge-field farming of wetlands + camelid herding + caravan trade. Intensive in the heartland; **extensive** (nodal) at distant frontiers—isolated nodes of state authority among local populations (e.g. northern Titicaca Basin) perpetuating ethnic heterogeneity arising from folk economic archipelagos (as of Moseley 2001; see also [[vertical-archipelago]]). Phase 5 shifted from more ideological to more political integration with strong sectarian concerns over agricultural management.

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** [[pukara-culture]] political integration at north Titicaca (succumbed to early centuries AD drought); Yaya-Mama ideological integration.
- **Peer:** [[wari-culture]] — shared Staff God vocabulary; Moquegua frontier coexistence and possible conflict.
- **Succeeded by:** Combative LIP Aymara señoríos (Colla, Lupaka, etc.) that Cuzco played off and conquered piecemeal; Inca resettlement from pukaras to lowlands; ritual reuse of the capital under [[inca-empire]].
- **Contrasts with:** Coastal Moche intensive state ideology; Maritime-Oasis trajectory culminating later in [[chimu-culture]].

## Identity Cautions

Tiwanaku architecture, art, and colonies are **not** a language, a genetic population, or a continuous ethnic nation into the LIP. Aymara kingdoms after collapse are successor political landscapes, not proven ethnolinguistic identity of the classic capital’s residents. Staff God continuity with Chavín and Wari is iconographic/ideological association, not political succession. Hostage stelae policy (if accepted) implies multi-community empire, not cultural homogeneity.

## Historiography

Multi-national projects and excavators of the capital and Moquegua (Goldstein and others via Moseley) shape the five-phase and colony narrative. Paired Wari/Tiwanaku characterizations (Kolata, Stanish, etc.) are interpretive. Climate–collapse linkage is strong in Moseley’s 2001 synthesis but must not be written as sole environmental causation; intentional destruction and Inca “park” hypotheses are explicitly speculative in-source.
