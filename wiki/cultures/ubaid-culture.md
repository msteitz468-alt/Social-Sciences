# Ubaid Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Ubaid culture takes its name from the small mound al-'Ubaid near Ur, excavated in the 1920s. Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], it is significant as the first proto-historic culture to span continuously from the Jazirah to the Tigris-Euphrates delta. In Roux's six-stage relative chronology the "Ubaid period" proper (Ubaid 1 and 2 included) runs c. 5000–3750 BCE; the transition from Halaf material culture occurred in northern Mesopotamia between 4500 and 4300 BCE, without a sharp material break — Roux argues this continuity "excludes a conquest... by 'Ubaidians' coming from the south," favoring peaceful infiltration or local adoption of a southern culture after prolonged contact.

Southern Iraqi occupation predating the full Ubaid period was demonstrated by 1946–49 excavations at [[eridu]] (Abu Shahrain, 19 km SW of Ur) by Seton Lloyd and Fuad Safar: seventeen superimposed temple levels beneath a later ziqqurat built by Amar-Sin of the Third Dynasty of Ur (2046–2038 BCE). The lowest temples (levels XVII–XV) are small one-room buildings with altars and offering tables, associated with "Eridu ware" (elaborate dark-brown geometric designs related to Choga Mami Transitional ware). The middle temples (XIV–XII) are associated with "Hajji Muhammad ware" (crowded designs, "reserve slip" decoration), matching pottery found in 1937–39 by German archaeologists at Qal'at Hajji Muhammad near Uruk and also at Ras el-'Amiya (8 km north of Kish). The upper temples (XI–VI) are associated with standard Ubaid ware; temples VI–I belong to the early Uruk period. Roux notes these four ceramic phases are now commonly labeled Ubaid 1 (Eridu), 2 (Hajji Muhammad), 3, and 4.

Excavation at Tell el-'Oueili near Larsa (French, soundings 1981 and 1983) revealed 20 occupation levels: levels 1–8 carry Ubaid 4/3/2 pottery, levels 8–11 carry Ubaid 1 (Eridu) ware, and levels 12–19 revealed a previously unknown pottery type ("Pre-Ubaid"/"Ubaid Zero") with Samarra-ware affinities, plus cigar-shaped mud bricks (in a level-12 wall) resembling Tell es-Sawwan brick technology. Below level 20, further occupation was visible in the water table but unexcavatable. This pushes southern Mesopotamia's settlement history further into the sixth millennium than previously documented; as Roux puts it, "no one knows how far back... the roots of this modest South Mesopotamian village go."

## Material Signature

Standard Ubaid ware is typically an overfired buff-to-green clay with matte dark-brown or bluish-black paint in partial-vessel decoration (triangles, striped or cross-hatched bands, wavy lines) — Roux assesses it as showing "a lack of imagination" compared to Halaf ware (an aesthetic judgment, attributed). Some vessels were made on a slow wheel or "tournette"; spouts and loop-handles appear for the first time. Diagnostic forms include the bell-shaped bowl, the basket-handled jar, the cream bowl with a pouring lip, and the "tortoiseshell" lenticular spouted vessel. The ware is found across nearly all southern Iraqi sites and many northern ones (a notable exception being Kish).

In southern Iraq, stone was rare, so most tools and objects were made of terracotta (mullers/"nails," sickles, spindle whorls, loom-weights, net-sinkers, sling pellets, model axes/adzes/knives). Standing female figurines of the "Mother Goddess" type (lizard-like head, bitumen hair-coil, coffee-bean eyes recalling Tell es-Sawwan and Choga Mami figurines) are, per Roux, "erroneously" labeled as such — an explicit caution against over-interpretation, paralleling his caution on Halaf figurines. Houses combined reed-mat/wood-pole construction (comparable to modern Basrah vernacular building) with pressed-mud and mud-brick structures. Eridu's Ubaid temples were large mud-brick buildings with an oblong cella and corner rooms, a podium for a god's statue, a brick altar, and external buttresses and niches, raised on mud-brick platforms that foreshadow later ziqqurats.

Secular architecture at Tell el-'Oueili's upper (late Ubaid) levels included large, carefully built mud-brick houses with open spaces between them; one house had many small square cavities, interpreted (attributed) as possible granary infrastructure. Tell Madhhur in the Hamrin basin (British excavation, 1977–80) yielded what an excavation-report assessment (quoted by Roux without a named source) calls "one of the best preserved prehistoric buildings ever found in Mesopotamia" — a house on the standard Ubaid "tripartite plan" (central cruciform hall with side rooms), its walls surviving over six feet high, destroyed by fire that preserved its contents in situ: pottery, agricultural and household tools, thousands of clay sling-bullets, but — notably — no baked-clay sickle.

Northern Ubaid-period sites differ markedly from the south: no reed habitations (construction is entirely in brick), stone is commonly used, and stone stamp-seals — rare in the south — are common, bearing figural or mythic scene designs. At Tepe Gawra, the leading northern site of this period, level XIII shows an "acropolis" of three large painted-wall temples closely matching Eridu's temples, but two tholoi persist alongside them (a Halaf architectural holdover), as do Halaf-style seated painted female figurines.

## Distribution and Chronology

The Ubaid culture's overall span "lasted at least a thousand years" (Roux), spreading over the cultivable Mesopotamian plain except the middle Euphrates and lower Tigris valleys, bounded by the Taurus and Zagros ranges but not stopped by them commercially. Evidence of long-distance trade includes widespread obsidian in the south; gold and amazonite (from India) at Ur; Ubaid pottery found at Ras Shamra on the Syrian coast and on the Arabian Gulf shore. Settlements communicated via waterways, evidenced by clay boat models found at Eridu and Ur.

## Lifeways

Southern subsistence relied on barley, date-palms, and other crops, with livestock nearly exclusively zebu cattle and pigs (fed on aquatic plants); reed-cutting used baked-clay sickles. Burial customs differ sharply by region: at Eridu, adults and children alike lie supine on potsherd beds in mudbrick-lined cist graves in an extramural cemetery; at Tepe Gawra, by contrast, only one such burial is known — most graves there are simple pits around houses with flexed on-side burial, and children are buried in urns. Roux interprets this contrast as evidence that "bearers of the Ubaid culture were in the minority in the north," with descendants of "the Halafians" still forming a large population share there, "whereas the south was entirely 'Ubaidian'" — an explicit population/culture-mixture interpretation that uses "Ubaidian" and "Halafian" as population labels derived directly from pottery-culture names. This is flagged here as a textbook case of the etic-construct-vs-people conflation the wiki must guard against, even though Roux is elsewhere reasonably careful to hedge such claims.

Continuity of religious tradition at Eridu is argued from architectural continuity across the temple sequence from the mid-sixth millennium into historical times, reinforced by two later Ubaid-period shrines found near the "White Temple" of Anu at Uruk.

## Relations and Successions

Roux draws an explicit ceramic-sequence genealogical hypothesis: "the Ubaid ware... derives from the Hajji Muhammad ware, which derives from the Eridu ware, which in turn appears to derive from, or at least to share common ancestors with, the Samarra ware." This chain — Samarra → Eridu → Hajji Muhammad → (standard) Ubaid — is represented here via `preceded_by`, understood strictly in the material-culture (ceramic-sequence) sense, never as an ethnic or linguistic identity claim. Roux notes the ultimate origin of Ubaid culture is itself disputed: "whether imported from south-western Iran or, as is increasingly probable, developed locally" — an open question with a stated current leaning toward local development, suitable for a `debates/` page or at minimum flagging as a live position here.

Ubaid material culture is directly succeeded by [[uruk-culture]]; Roux argues (citing site stratigraphy at Eridu, Uruk, and Ur) there is "no clear-cut break between the Ubaid culture and the Uruk culture and no sign of armed invasion and destruction" — new Uruk-period temples were built directly over Ubaid-period ones, on the same plans and in the same materials, arguing continuity rather than replacement.

## Identity Cautions

"Ubaid culture" designates a material-culture (ceramic and architectural) horizon spanning a thousand years and a large geographic range; it is not a self-conscious people, a language, or a genetic population. Roux himself states the pots-≠-people caution explicitly elsewhere in his text, warning against equating Samarrans with 'Ubaidians or Sumerians "on the sole basis of their pottery." His own north/south "bearers of the Ubaid culture" language (above) shows how easily even a careful author can slip toward treating a ceramic label as a population label — the wiki flags this explicitly rather than repeating it in wiki voice. Any claim linking Ubaid material culture to a specific language (proto-Sumerian or otherwise) or genetic population is, per the collection to date, unestablished and must be attributed and hedged.

## Historiography

Known to the wiki via [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], drawing on excavations spanning the 1920s (al-'Ubaid), 1937–39 (Qal'at Hajji Muhammad), 1946–49 (Eridu, Lloyd and Safar), and 1977–83 (Tell Madhhur; Tell el-'Oueili). The origin question (local development vs. Iranian import) remained explicitly open as of Roux's writing; later specialist work and post-1992 reassessment are outstanding for this page's densification.

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* (Chicago 1988), chiefly Ch. 3.)*

**Regional horizon, not migration.** Nissen argues that under a new production technique, earlier more localized pottery decorations changed so uniformly that the “Ubaid horizon” could easily be misread as the spread of Ubaid painting from one place by migratory movement. He rejects that reading: the basic trend “cannot be put down to the migration of a particular population group,” but is rather the visible sign of the introduction and spread of a new technical device — the **pivoted work surface (turnette)**. Ubaid-style all-round painting (bands, garlands, wavy lines) still spread over most of the Near East with greater or lesser intensity at the period’s end, showing continued commonalities even as regional settlement trajectories diverged (as of Nissen 1988).

**Turnette and potter’s wheel.** At the end of the Halaf-related horizon Nissen identifies the turnette (pivoted working surface; sometimes erroneously called a “slow rotating wheel”): it rotates only while turned, lacks bearings, but eases vertical shaping and especially painting by rotating the vessel. Concentric-band painting characteristic of the Ubaid period (named for Tell al-Ubaid, southern Iraq) follows as a technological consequence — pressing a color-soaked brush against the rotating pot — so production and painting take a fraction of prior time. Decorated-ware proportion rises beyond former luxury levels; professional potters with divided procedures among workers certainly existed by no later than this stage. The next innovation — setting the wheel’s axle in bearings, creating an **actual potter’s wheel** — he dates probably to the **end of the Ubaid period**, aiming to simplify production, with major effects on process and pottery appearance that belong chronologically to the following Uruk period. Unlike the turnette, the true wheel “could only make a breakthrough in the more highly developed regions.” Post-Ubaid pottery differences (clay consistency for the wheel, additives such as ferric oxide altering fired color, near-cessation of painting, more specialized shapes) are **not**, Nissen argues, a population change (as of Nissen 1988).

**Southern densification context.** During Middle–Late Susiana (= Ubaid-time Babylonia in Nissen’s parallel), Susiana already developed complex settlement systems and centers while Lower Mesopotamia remained at widely separated isolated settlements in water-logged conditions — both still parts of a greater unity. Occupation of [[uruk]] from at least the Ubaid period onward provides chronological sequence information that overshadows other Babylonian evidence. An Ubaid-period shrine already stood on the western high terrace at Uruk (later Anu/Kullaba area) — Nissen treats the western area as the kernel of the great settlement, older than Eanna’s large Late Uruk buildings. Mid-fourth-millennium climatic change and receding waters then opened Lower Mesopotamia to denser settlement than prior Susiana, shifting the main arena of subsequent developments (including the [[uruk-culture|Uruk]] “early high civilization” package) to Babylonia (as of Nissen 1988). Four-way non-identity: Ubaid ceramic/architectural horizon ≠ ethnos ≠ language ≠ genetic population.

## Potts densification (1997)

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts, Mesopotamian Civilization 1997]], drawing chapters I–II.)*

**Ubaid 0 at Tell Oueili and the pre-Eridu horizon.** Pre-Ubaid occupation in southern Mesopotamia went undetected for decades owing to heavy alluvial sediment burial — up to c. 5 m of accumulation over the past 6,000 years across the plain, and 2.5 m at Hajji Muhammad alone (per Huot 1989). A 1983 deep sounding by the French expedition at Tell Oueili (WS 460, "Tell Awayli," near Larsa) uncovered more than 5 m of pre-Eridu remains above the water table, designated "Ubaid 0" (Huot 1989; Calvet 1989) — the earliest then-known evidence of alluvial human occupation, though not necessarily the very first settlement there. The material shows close affinities to sites in eastern, central, and northern Mesopotamia (as of J. Oates 1987).

**Choga Mami Transitional (CMT) ware.** J. Oates (1987) identifies "Choga Mami Transitional" pottery — an "interface" style associated with the Mandali region and the eastern alluvium edge — as closely allied with the Ubaid 0 material; CMT sherds appear in the Tell Oueili sequence (Calvet's 1981 and 1983 soundings, area Y27 and X36), judged by Oates contemporary at latest with Eridu levels 19–16 (Ubaid 1) and likely extending earlier, with cigar-shaped bricks paralleling Choga Mami and the Samarran site Tell es-Sawwan. Choga Mami itself has yielded the earliest evidence in greater Mesopotamia for canal irrigation, dated to roughly 6000 BCE (Oates and Oates 1977) — some authorities hold irrigation was a precondition for southern colonization, a hypothesis Potts goes on to complicate (see [[irrigation]], [[agricultural-intensification]]).

**Northern connections: einkorn and Hassuna "Soft Ware."** J.-L. Huot reports smooth yellow-brown "Soft Ware" sherds at basal Tell Oueili levels resembling Hassuna pottery from northern Iraq (Courtois and Velde 1989). More significantly, einkorn wheat (*Triticum boeoticum*) — a cereal traditionally associated with the dry-farming, mountainous/piedmont zone of northern Mesopotamia, poorly salt-tolerant and requiring extra hulling — appears in these same early Oueili contexts (Neef 1989). J. Oates (1987) further links the Oueili pottery to the Samarran assemblage at Tell es-Sawwan and to Halaf material there, indicating north–south contact. J.-D. Forest (position) argues CMT represents acculturation of Samarrans from central Mesopotamia who moved south under Halaf pressure, carrying irrigation technology (attested at Tell es-Sawwan) and a Samarran ceramic tradition that, modified through contact with early Ubaid settlers, produced CMT.

**Arabian bifacial tradition and hunter-gatherer infiltration.** Western Iraqi desert sites along the Wadis Farukh, Ghadaf, Ubaidh, and Khirr (draining into the Euphrates basin) yield flint tools characteristic of the late Pre-Pottery/early Pottery Neolithic Levant, suggesting eastward infiltration of hunters and pastoralists from at least 6000 BCE (Zarins 1990). The Rub al-Khali Holocene playa lakes and much of northeastern Arabia are characterized in the 5th–4th millennia BCE by the "Arabian bifacial tradition," marked by barbed/tanged bifacially-flaked arrowheads (Potts 1990), whose users practiced mixed herding (sheep/goat), hunting, gathering, and possibly cereal cultivation (Potts 1993, 1994). Identical bifacial material has been found at Ur ([[ur]]; Woolley 1955), near [[eridu]] (H. Wright survey), at Tell Oueili (Inizan and Tixier 1983), and at Tello (Cauvin 1979); finds previously mis-identified as "Middle Palaeolithic" from southern Iraq and Qatar are now understood to be of Holocene date (Zarins 1992).

**Aurenche's pre-ceramic link hypothesis.** Olivier Aurenche (1987; position) points to Tell Rihan III in the Hamrin (pre-ceramic, circular semi-subterranean houses, c. 6500 BCE) and the contemporary Choga Banut in Khuzistan as evidence of continuous settlement from the aceramic Neolithic along the eastern alluvium/piedmont fringe, arguing a similar (as-yet undiscovered) pre-ceramic occupation likely existed in the western alluvium as well. He proposes Tell Rihan III as a link between the Zagros Neolithic heartland and the southern alluvium via the Hamrin, attributing the apparent chronological gap to Ubaid 0 at Tell Oueili to incomplete research rather than a genuine settlement hiatus.

**Sea-level and climatic explanations for early settlement (position, on [[sumerian-problem-debate]]).** P. Sanlaville's (1989) sea-level reconstruction for the Arabian Gulf holds that sea-levels were c. 15–20 m lower than today at the start of the Ubaid period (c. 5500 BCE), reaching roughly modern levels only by its end (c. 3800 BCE), then rising to a maximum c. 1–2 m above modern sea-level around 3500 BCE. H.J. Nissen (1988a, 1988b) had argued sea-levels up to 2.75 m higher than today made much of lower Mesopotamia uninhabitable through the Ubaid period, with a recession c. 3500 BCE removing surface water and enabling Uruk-period settlement expansion (Adams and Nissen 1972). Oates (1991, 1993) challenges this, and Sanlaville's data directly contradict it: Ubaid-period sea-levels were in fact lower than today throughout, removing sea-level as an explanation for any earlier absence of settlement. Potts's own synthesis (attributed) instead credits climatic conditions — the early-to-mid-Holocene northward displacement of the summer monsoon (c. 7th–4th millennium BCE) — with periodically making southern Mesopotamia wetter and richer in game and plant resources, drawing hunter-gatherer-herder populations from northern Arabia. Potts argues the resulting picture is one of a multi-origin, multilingual founding population — CMT/irrigation-bearing groups entering from the east, einkorn/Hassuna-related groups from the north, and Arabian-bifacial-tradition hunter-gatherers from the south/west — co-existing at the outset of Mesopotamian settlement, a diversity he suggests may itself help explain the Sumerian/non-Sumerian place-name split (see [[sumerian-problem-debate]]) without requiring Nissen's sea-level recession.

**Locally available lithic raw material.** At the Ubaid 3–4 site of Tell el-Saadiya (Hamrin basin), most chipped stone (flint, small obsidian, rare jasper) appears imported, but locally available quartzite was used for choppers (Kozlowski 1987). At Tell Oueili, a c. 45 m² workshop area (excavated 1981 by Inizan and Tixier) shows glassy quartz was worked from locally-picked-up pebbles, with only obsidian imported (Inizan and Tixier 1983).

**Clay sickles — reed-cutting hypothesis (position).** Long assumed to be cereal-harvesting tools, Ubaid-period clay sickles from Tell Oueili have been the subject of microwear analysis (Anderson-Gerfaud 1983) suggesting they could have been used to cut reeds instead; Benco's (1992) study of their distribution proposes reed-cutting may in fact have been their primary function. Clay sickles nearly identical to Ubaid forms continued in use into the Late Uruk period (Finkbeiner 1985), where the same functional question applies (see [[uruk-culture]]).

## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]], Ch.4.)*

**Eridu temple sequence and continuity.** Matthews reports the [[eridu]] temple sequence (excavated late 1940s by Lloyd and Safar) as superimposed temples spanning roughly 1,500 years, each rebuild larger and grander than the last, probably dedicated to Enki — massive mud-brick platforms, a central open space with altar, niches/buttresses, and cardinal-point orientation. He notes that continuity of the temple's location, together with Sumerian tradition naming Eridu a source of kingship and one of the first post-flood cities, "argue persuasively for cultural and possibly ethnic (Sumerian?) continuity" of south Mesopotamian occupation — a claim Matthews himself flags with a question mark, recorded here as an attributed hypothesis, not an identity claim (four-way non-identity rule).

**Stein's "agricultural bank" model.** [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]] (1994b) proposed that Ubaid temples functioned as agricultural "banks" or buffers, with chiefly power grounded in control and redistribution of local surplus rather than display of exotic goods: "the close association between irrigation, socio-economic differentiation, and the appearance of temples suggests that ritual elaboration played an important role in generating and maintaining these economic differences... locally-based, ritually generated staple finance resulted in the peaceful spread and long-term stability of small scale chiefly polities" (Stein 1994b: 44). Matthews adds a methodological caution (via Pollock 1999) that ritual food/textile-processing debris at Eridu and Tepe Gawra may be archaeologically indistinguishable from ordinary domestic activity despite differing social meaning.

**Complication: Tepe Gawra elite tombs.** Occasional lapis lazuli/carnelian beads at Tepe Gawra level XIII — precursor to lavish later tombs at level X and above — suggest some elite/exotic wealth display, complicating Stein's low-key, quasi-egalitarian Late Ubaid model. Matthews notes drily that "the discovery of a single wealthy tomb could overturn a theory overnight," treating the Gawra tomb sequence as a live `challenges` relation to Stein's staple-finance model rather than a settled refutation.

**Berman vs. Pollock on Susiana pottery.** Judith Berman's (1994) neutron activation analysis of Susiana (SW Iran) Ubaid pottery found plain red wares produced locally at household/small-site scale, black-on-buff wares produced at larger village workshops, and elaborately decorated Susa Necropole vessels made from diverse clay sources, possibly transported as elite burial gifts. Berman read this pattern as centered on private food consumption, with simple repetitive decoration reinforcing shared group identity rather than elite-status display (Berman 1994: 29). Susan Pollock's (1983) contrasting reading of Terminal Ubaid (Susa A) painted pottery holds instead that stylistic complexity in high-class painted vessels correlates with, and indicates, elite production and use within a stratified social context — a minor but direct disagreement over the same evidence, recorded here as a position pair rather than adjudicated.

**Nissen's warning against pots-as-migration.** Matthews relays [[nissen-hans-j|Hans Nissen's]] explicit caution against reading the ceramic uniformity of the "Ubaid horizon" as evidence of a migratory spread of population: "the impression could easily arise that this 'Ubaid horizon' was the external indication of the spread of the 'Ubaid' method of painting to the whole of the Near East from one place as a result of a migratory movement" (Nissen 1988: 46) — a direct invocation, in the source itself, of the four-way non-identity rule against equating a pottery horizon with a population movement. Nissen further links the late-5th-millennium decline of painted-pottery decoration to a shift toward a stratified society in which status could no longer be adequately signaled through painted pottery (Nissen 1988: 63).
