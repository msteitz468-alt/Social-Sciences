## Definition and Construct History

**Wari** (also spelled **Huari** in older and some Andean literature, including Moseley’s prose) names the highland Middle Horizon polity and material-administrative horizon anchored at a capital of the same name on the Ayacucho plateau of the central Peruvian sierra. As an archaeological-culture/polity construct, it designates shared architecture, corporate arts, provincial compounds, and iconography—not a demonstrated single ethnic or linguistic people. Moseley (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|2001]]) treats “Huari” as the political and religious formation that, from Huarpa beginnings, packaged terrace-maize intensification with hierarchical organization, reciprocity ideology, and a revitalized [[staff-god]] cult, then extended state “tendrils” to disparate highland and coastal nodes. This page uses the vault-canonical slug [[wari-culture]]; Huari is the alternate spelling once noted here.

Field debate (recorded as positions, not adjudicated): some scholars read Middle Horizon highland remains as a **religion** practiced at confederated regional centers; others as an Inca-like **militaristic empire**. Moseley favors a middle ground—economic innovations wrapped in religious fundamentalism spreading to political nodes (as of Moseley 2001).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material**: The capital (2,800 m, ~25 km north of modern Ayacucho) grew from humble Huarpa origins into a ceremonial/elite center; by AD 600 corporate arts reached coastal Ica (Moseley’s Middle Horizon marker). Surface ruins include extensive irrigation and underground water conduits; a terraced urban core; massive walls compartmentalizing the city into irregular sections—emphasis, Moseley argues, on **segregation** by kin, class, rank, and occupation rather than integration. Compounds often 40–100 m on a side, two–three stories, rectangular patios with elongated galleries (compared to Marcahuamachuco gallery tradition). Craft specialization (ceramics, jewelry, projectile points); feasting enclosures with serving bowls and libation cups. Cheqo Wasi: looted subterranean megalithic elite tombs with gold and luxury goods—possible potentate tombs (Moseley/Isbell synthesis, attributed). Long-distance imports include Ecuadorian *Spondylus*, coastal shells, Cajamarca pottery, distant minerals, copper/silver/gold. Population estimates before abandonment ~AD 1000 range ~10,000–35,000 (as of Moseley 2001).

**Okros style** packages early political expansion: many Huarpa settlements (including Nawinpukio) abandoned; people relocated toward Huari and Conchopata; local traditions plus Nazca design elements; major Tiwanaku ritual art and architecture elements reached Huari/Conchopata in Okros times—by uncertain means (emissaries between capitals, or via the Cerro Baúl colony in Tiwanaku’s Moquegua territory) (as of Moseley 2001).

**Staff God / agrarian ideology**: Moseley argues Ayacucho “savants” appropriated Tiwanaku Gateway of the Sun staff-bearing icons: winged single-staff figures became secondary “angels”; the double-staffed Gateway God became an agrarian almighty with maize cobs on rayed headdress/staffs—read as resurrection of the old Chavín [[staff-god]] for a fundamentalist ideology propagating terraced maize political economy. Ritual intoxication ceremonies with smashing/interment of vessels (keros at Cerro Baúl; large chicha jars/urns in caches at Conchopata, Huari, Pacheco/Nazca); each hoard commissioned for a single ceremony; at Conchopata young women sacrificed adjacent to one ceramic cache (as of Moseley 2001). A rectangular sunken court of finely cut stone at the capital (initial construction carbon AD 580 ± 60; middle floor AD 720 ± 60) was carefully entombed under later building—Moseley considers the form and associated ritual borrowed from [[tiwanaku-culture]].

**Linguistic**: No linguistic evidence for Wari populations is presented in the ingested sources; the state should not be equated with any specific language or language family on material evidence alone.

**Genetic**: No population-genetic data in the ingested sources — [[unknown]].

## Distribution and Chronology

**Chronological frame (as of Moseley 2001):** Middle Horizon technically AD 600–1000 in the Ica sequence, but “set in motion” by a 25–30% rainfall plunge AD 562–594 reconstructed from Quelccaya ice cores—the most severe Andean rainfall abnormality of the last 1,500 years aside from Little Ice Age precipitation increase (attributed climate synthesis). Moseley frames the horizon as punctuated change: old empires withered, new ones arose; people on the move; linguists see shifts in indigenous language/dialect distributions.

**Heartland and sierra nodes:** Capital at Huari; hinterland administrative centers typically open flatland rectangular enclosures (25–800 m on a side) with courts, high frequencies of serving vessels (corporate feasting/drinking), food prep and storage, elite quarters; long cell-like buildings may be barrack-like [[mita-labor|mit’a]] labor quarters; style/layout suggests Huari architect supervision; elite Huari ceramics + local utilitarian wares—impression of local folk and nobles with a small Huari overlord contingent. **Pikillaqta** near [[cuzco]] is the largest and southernmost highland center; state-built centers string major sierra basins including Cajamarca for >1,000 km northward. **Jincamocco** (Carahuarazo, route Huari–Nazca): cooperative terrace transformation—locals moved downslope for maize; no fortification. **Viracochapampa** (<3 km from Marcahuamachuco): second-largest Huari compound; no fortification or conflict evidence with that northern capital.

**Cerro Baúl (Moquegua):** Possible early exception to non-militaristic spread—mesa-top colony (2,300 m) in Tiwanaku’s Moquegua province; Huari seized the pinnacle (sacred *apu* today) and colonized Cerro Mejía behind it; ~10 km contour canal irrigating mountain terraces; elite patio groups; D-shaped ritual centers per capital canons; Okros pottery early; persisted to ≥AD 900; abandonment with vessel-smashing and roof-torching; coexistence with neighbors for centuries; thereafter frontiers pulled back to the Sihuas drainage (as of Moseley 2001; Williams excavations).

**Coast:** Presence more **ideological** than architectural—rare enclosure centers; some authorities argue invasion of southern oases; alternatively long-standing verticality (Ica–Nazca–Ayacucho) formalized without force. Late dual-staff mural at Huaca de la Luna “very likely the Huari rayed deity” (Moseley). At [[pachacamac]], Middle Horizon tomb accompaniments show Huari influence without skeletal evidence of Ayacucho colonization or highland administrative centers (as of Moseley 2001).

Collapse/abandonment of the capital around AD 1000 is undated in detail and not fully understood in Moseley’s account; both Wari and Tiwanaku religious-political faith are said to have been undermined as multi-century drought deepened (contributing/concurrent factors—not sole environmental causation in wiki voice).

## Lifeways

Early Intermediate sierra farming was mostly high-elevation potato/tuber rainfall agriculture; Huarpa among the first to terrace and irrigate inclined terrain. Isbell’s work (via Moseley) stresses an innovative Huarpa system at Huari—high-altitude water source, long primary contour canal, extensive terraces—as economic “pre-adaptation” to the great drought, opening under-exploited *quichua* maize lands. Slope reclamation remained a major highland expansion means long after rains returned (as of Moseley 2001). Hierarchy and reciprocity are emphasized as organizational wrappers of the terrace package. Mit’a-like modular labor is inferred at provincial compounds (barrack rooms, feasting/storage).

## Relations and Successions

- **Preceded by:** Huarpa señorío (~AD 200–600), Nazca-influenced ceramic sphere, Nawinpukio as modest capital laying agrarian/political foundations (as of Moseley 2001).
- **Peer/competitor:** [[tiwanaku-culture]] — iconographic transfer of Staff God/sunken-court ideology; physical confrontation/coexistence at Moquegua (Baúl vs Omo/Chen Chen); complementary intensive vs extensive models of [[state-formation]].
- **Coastal interaction:** Ideological influence on Moche late Phase IV/V iconography; Huari-influenced tombs at Pachacamac; stylistic elements littoral to Lambayeque.
- **Succeeded by:** LIP polities; near Cuzco, Huari’s Pikillaqta and later Chokepukio/Lucre arts are among local foundations for early Inca elaboration (Killke/Lucre), not direct political descent of [[inca-empire]] from Wari (associated_with, not identity).

## Identity Cautions

“Wari/Huari” is a material-administrative construct. Do not equate pots, patio groups, or Staff God imagery with a single language, genetic population, or self-conscious ethnicity. Provincial centers may reflect small overlord contingents among local populations (extensive/nodal organization) rather than mass Ayacucho colonization—except where osteology and architecture argue otherwise (e.g. Baúl debate). Tiwanaku-derived icons at Wari sites are **associated_with** shared religious vocabulary, not proof of Tiwanaku political rule over Ayacucho.

## Historiography

Investigations of the capital headed by Luis Lumbreras and later William Isbell (as cited by Moseley). Textbook paired-empire models (Schreiber 1992; Janusek 2008; Kolata 1996; Stanish 2011 “militant vs religious” shorthand) remain interpretive readings. Primary densification here from [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001]] (Ch. 7–9) plus earlier handbook synthesis [[scarre-human-past-2013]]. Post-2001 Cerro Baúl and Ayacucho fieldwork may revise scale, militarism, and collapse chronology—date-stamp Moseley claims.
