# Inka Empire

## Definition and Construct History

The **Inka Empire** of Tawantinsuyu (“the four parts together”) is the Late Horizon Andean imperial polity centered on Cuzco. As presented by [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]], it expanded within ~87 years (Late Horizon ~AD 1476–1532) from Cuzco to control territory from Colombia to central Chile, Pacific to eastern jungles, with perhaps 10–12 million people under a single royal lineage—the largest pre-Columbian New World polity.

Wenke & Olszewski state that the Inka Empire was the only one of the six “primary” ancient civilizations (as framed in the book) that did **not** develop a written language, yet achieved imperial scale. When Europeans reached the Andes they found a rigidly stratified class society under a supreme monarch, organizationally familiar despite cultural strangeness (including ritual human sacrifice). Early Mesoamerican states, compared with Inka, Sumerian Mesopotamia, Old Kingdom Egypt, and others, illustrate that factors producing early civilizations operated similarly around the world at different times (textbook comparative premise).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material / administrative (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007).** ~40,000 km paved road network with causeways, wind walls, suspension bridges, storehouses, and relay runners reputed to move messages ~2,400 km in five days; palaces, temples, granaries, forts; rural stone/adobe houses for most people; decimal taxpayer units; *ayllu* kin groups holding land in common with reciprocal labor; labor tribute (*mit’a*-style work on state plots/specialist service); **kipu** (knotted strings) with a hereditary accountant class for taxes, transactions, and census—judged adequate for storage/retrieval when backed by bureaucracy, though weaker for religious-philosophical texts than Mesopotamian writing.

**Economy package.** Integrated fishing, herding, farming, taxation, textiles, and metallurgy; staples maize, beans, potatoes, quinoa, oca, peppers; storage including *chuño* (freeze-dried potato), jerky, and *chicha* beer. Hastorf and Johannessen argue Inka and predecessors converted maize into prestige *chicha* used in imperial feasts that reinforced elite–peasant indebtedness within national religion and class hierarchy. Murra’s **ecological complementarity**—integrating multiple environments—is presented as a major Andean civilizational achievement supporting vast populations and high productivity.

**Not present.** No Old World-type wheeled chariots/wagons; no alphabetic/cuneiform-style writing (kipu compensation). Llamas insufficient as draft animals versus oxen/horses (textbook constraint on development).

**Linguistic / genetic.** Quechua as imperial language does not equal ethnic homogeneity of all subjects; four-way non-identity applies.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Andean setting.** Andes rise so sharply from the Pacific that only a thin coastal strip (<~60 km at widest) separates mountains from sea; rain-shadow desert (rain once or twice every five years in many places). Coastal habitation depended on ~50 small rivers; marine resources extraordinarily rich from upwelling (anchovies → food chain). El Niño can collapse marine food chain ~yearly historically every 2–7 years. After ~4000 BCE highland foragers succeeded by farmers of potatoes, maize, quinoa and herders of llama and alpaca; eastern montaña/Amazon sources of products (e.g., feathers) but not directly controlled by Andean polities.

**Deep formative sequence (context for Inka).** Late Preceramic (~3200–1800 BCE) and Initial Period (~1800–900/600 BCE); El Paraíso U-shaped mounds (~58 ha); Norte Chico monumental sites; Chavín Horizon after ~900 BCE (art spread analogous to [[olmec-culture]] style horizons); Early Intermediate (Moche et al.) A.D. 1–600 with militaristic states, population estimates rising to ~4–5 million; Middle Horizon (~AD 600–1000) Wari (~15 km² capital; coast–highland operation Cajamarca to Sicuani; major roadways may date here) and Tiwanaku (planned capital; state pop. perhaps ~100,000; raised-field potatoes + llama caravans); Late Intermediate ≥7 power centers including Chimú at Chan Chan (~11 km², 10 major compounds).

**Inka expansion narratives (positions).** Spanish/native oral accounts emphasize rapid 15th-century conquests after ~1438 Chanka war. Alan Covey proposes earlier Inka regional domination after Wari fall (~AD 1000–1200) via Cuzco Basin control, marriage alliances, agricultural intensification, and population in-migration, with later militarism.

**Spanish conquest.** Pizarro at Cajamarca 15 Nov 1532: after a chaplain’s religious-political harangue, Atawallpa discarded a breviary; Spanish attacked, captured the king, extracted gold/silver ransom, then executed him. Population thought to have fallen from 10–12 million to ~6 million within 50 years from disease, war, and irrigation destruction (as of W&O 2007).

## Lifeways

Stratified monarchy; decimal administration; *ayllu* reciprocity and *mit’a* labor tribute; feasting and *chicha* politics; road and storehouse logistics; multi-environmental production (coastal fish, highland tubers, maize prestige economy). Most people rural; public works absorbed labor and signaled state power. W&O’s framing thesis for ancient civilizations: social institutions permitted “pervasive exploitation of the many by the few” and made that exploitation a religious and civil virtue—applied comparatively, including Andes.

## Relations and Successions

- **Developed from / associated with:** Wari roads and administrative precedents; Chimú and other Late Intermediate competitors; deeper Formative monumental and ritual traditions (Chavín, Norte Chico, El Paraíso orientations toward astronomical events as possible deep roots of Inka astrocosmology—Quilter via W&O).
- **Compared with:** only primary civilization without writing; Mesoamerican states ([[aztec-empire]], [[classic-maya]]); Old World primary states; ecological complementarity vs. more homogeneous Nile resource base (Egypt partial exception).
- **Multi-causal rise (W&O conclusion):** rich maritime + agricultural resources essential; Carneiro’s warfare–population–circumscription model “particularly applicable,” but sustained warfare seems more important *after* monumentality, regional economic coordination, craft specialization, and great religions already appeared; geographic isolation from other Old World-style state systems and lack of draft animals constrained development.

## Identity Cautions

- **Empire ≠ single people.** Tawantinsuyu incorporated multiethnic subjects under a royal lineage; “Inka” as ethnic self-name is narrower than the imperial construct.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Quechua language, genetic populations, archaeological horizons (Wari, Chimú, Inka), and political subjects are distinct analytical classes.
- **Kipu ≠ full writing.** Do not smuggle Mesopotamian literacy assumptions into “civilization” checklists as universal necessities—Inka is the textbook counterexample.
- **Expansion chronology** (rapid 15th-c. vs. Covey’s earlier Cuzco Basin domination) remains a position pair.

## Historiography

Textbook Andean chapter (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007) combining ethnohistory (Spanish and native accounts), archaeology of predecessors, and Murra ecological complementarity. Spanish conquest narratives are colonial-source-laden. Specialist archaeology of Cuzco Basin, kipu studies, and post-2007 bioarchaeology outstanding for densification.
