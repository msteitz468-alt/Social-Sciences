# Amorites

The Amorites (Sumerian MAR.TU; Akkadian *Amurrum* or *Tidnum*) were, in [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]]'s account, West Semitic-speaking populations originating in the land west of the Euphrates — roughly modern Syria, including the Palmyra desert, the Orontes valley, and the Mediterranean-bordering mountains. Sumerians and Akkadians applied the term specifically to nomadic tribes grazing flocks in the Mesopotamian steppe, distinct from the region's settled town- and village-dwelling population. Amorite political actors became the dominant force in Mesopotamian government after the fall of [[ur|Ur III]] (2004 BCE).

## Definition and Construct History

"Amorite" is, like "Akkadian" and "Semitic" generally, fundamentally a linguistic and lifestyle-based label rather than a bounded ethnic or biological population (see [[akkadians]] for the fuller discussion of the "Semitic race" historiography Roux rejects). Roux explicitly critiques an older "classical theory" of Semitic origins, which held that Semites originated as nomads in the central Syro-Arabian desert and radiated outward in successive waves — Akkadians into Mesopotamia in the 4th millennium BCE, Western Semites (Canaaneo-Phoenicians, Eblaites, Amorites) into Mesopotamia and Syria-Palestine in the 3rd–2nd millennia, Aramaeans in the 12th century BCE, Nabateans and pre-Islamic Arabs from the 2nd century BCE, and Muslim Arabs in the 7th century CE.

Roux's counter-position: the central Arabian desert was **not** the diffusion center. It was likely uninhabited true desert between the Palaeolithic (when it was savanna) and the 1st millennium BCE, and long-range nomadism required domesticated camels, which were not widespread in the Near East until roughly the 12th century BCE. Before that, nomads rode asses, kept sheep, and were confined to the grassy steppe between the Tigris-Euphrates and the Zagros/Taurus/Lebanon foothills — in constant contact with sedentary agriculturalists, not isolated in a desert interior. Roux's model treats sedentarization as a slow, continuous process of short-range movement within the Fertile Crescent's periphery (steppe to irrigated land), not a single outward radiation from a central desert homeland; he states the Fertile Crescent itself, not the interior desert, is "the true homeland of the Semitic-speaking peoples," present there since prehistory. This is presented here as a position within a live historiographic dispute, not settled fact — cite it as Roux's argument against the "classical theory," not as wiki-voice consensus.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

- **Textual/literary**: Amorites (as nomadic tribes) are attested from the Early Dynastic period onward. Sumerian literature preserves an explicit ethnic caricature, viewed by Sumerians with what Roux calls "disgust and contempt": "The MAR.TU who know no grain... who know no house nor town, the boors of the mountains... who digs up truffles... who does not bend his knees (to cultivate the land), who eats raw meat, who has no house during his lifetime, who is not buried after his death," alongside a passage describing an Amorite failing to recognize a wheat/grain confection. This is quoted as a documented period prejudice and literary stereotype — **not** an endorsed ethnographic description of actual Amorite lifeways.
- **Historical/political**: Shar-kali-sharri, the last major Akkad-dynasty king, named a regnal year for a victory over the MAR.TU "in mount Basar" (Jabal Bishri, between Palmyra and Deir-ez-Zor); Amorite prisoners of war are referenced in Shulgi- and Amar-Sin-era Ur III texts. In his 4th regnal year, Shu-Sin of Ur III built a defensive fortress "which keeps away Tidnum" between Mari and Ur — an explicit sign of rising Amorite pressure on the western frontier.
- **Genetic**: no genetic-population evidence is presented in this source; the construct rests on language, lifestyle (pastoral/semi-nomadic vs. sedentary), and textual attestation (as of [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]).

## Distribution and Chronology

During the reign of Ibbi-Sin (Ur III's collapse), Amorites broke through Ur's defenses in his 5th regnal year and ravaged the country. Ishbi-Irra — an Akkadian general native to Mari — reported that MAR.TU forces had cut all roads to Ur and threatened Isin and Nippur, and requested (and received) command of the region's defense. Ibbi-Sin eventually defeated the MAR.TU militarily but faced famine and internal insubordination regardless. Roux's own causal analysis: while the Elamites delivered the final blow — sacking Ur in 2004 BCE — "the secession of entire provinces, the revolt of Ibbi-Sin's officials and the Amorite invasion were the real causes of the Sumerian defeat." He marks this, in his own words, as "the end of the Sumerian nation and type of society," after which Semites (Akkadian and Amorite) held Mesopotamian government "for nearly fifteen hundred years." (as of [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]])

In the following Isin-Larsa period (c. 2000–1800 BCE), rulers were either native Akkadians or Western Semites — "Amorites in the broad sense." Roux flags the received characterization of these rulers as "allegedly uncouth bedouins" and reports that, whatever their origin, they assimilated Sumero-Akkadian culture "with remarkable ease and rapidity": adopting Akkadian for writing (their own Semitic dialects made this straightforward), continuing Sumerian religion, mythology, and epic largely unaltered, and producing art essentially continuous with Ur III work. Roux's summary judgment: "the civilization created by the Sumerians outlived them."

Roux attributes several structural and economic changes to the Amorite-dynasty period: dissolution of the old city-state structure into larger territorial kingdoms; a shift from temple/palace land ownership toward private large landed estates worked by tenant farmers; the rise of craft workshops and merchant associations (*karum*) operating with state capital but private profit-sharing; the reduction of temples to (in Roux's own phrase) "land-owners among other land-owners, tax-payers among other tax-payers"; and the erosion of the old Nippur/Enlil-centered legitimation doctrine — kings no longer needed "election" by Enlil at Nippur, relying instead on local city-god legitimation by conquest (*razzia*).

The Hana or Hanaean tribal confederation occupied [[mari]] and much of Upper Mesopotamia after roughly 1920 BCE, when the trail of Mari's *shakkanakku* governors is lost. It split into two main branches: the Beni-Iamina/Iaminites ("sons of the right," south — largely semi-nomadic west of Mari, with some settled on the Euphrates and lower Khabur) and the Beni-Sima'al/Simalites ("sons of the left," north — settled in small kingdoms in the Idamaras region, the fertile Khabur-tributary triangle).

## Lifeways

As nomadic pastoralists in the steppe zone, Amorite tribes grazed flocks and moved seasonally between steppe and irrigated land, in regular contact with settled Mesopotamian populations — not isolated desert nomads (see Definition and Construct History above). Once installed as ruling dynasties (Isin, Larsa, Babylon, Mari under Amorite chiefs), Amorite elites rapidly took on settled Mesopotamian urban lifeways: temple building, Akkadian-language administration, and continuity of Sumerian religious and literary tradition.

## Identity Cautions

1. **The Sumerian literary caricature of the MAR.TU is a hostile period stereotype, not an ethnographic description.** It is preserved and quoted here because it is historiographically significant (evidence of settled-population prejudice against pastoralists), not because it accurately characterizes Amorite lifeways.
2. **"Amorite" denotes a lifestyle/language category (West Semitic-speaking, pastoral/steppe-dwelling), not a fixed biological population.** Roux's own model treats "Amorite" identity as fluid and largely a matter of political and cultural affiliation rather than descent — see, for instance, the Larsa dynasty of Kudur-Mabuk's sons Warad-Sin and Rim-Sin, who bore Semitic theophoric names despite an Elamite father and behaved, in Roux's words, "in every respect like genuine Mesopotamian monarchs." This is an explicit case, within the source itself, of ethnicity, language, and political role failing to align — reinforcing the four-way non-identity caution rather than contradicting it.
3. Do not conflate "Amorite" political dominance with a genetic replacement or migration event; the source presents no genetic evidence and Roux's own model favors continuous, small-scale movement within the Fertile Crescent over long-range mass migration.

## Historiography

Roux's account of the Amorites is explicitly framed as a corrective to an older "classical theory" of Semitic desert-nomad origins (see Definition and Construct History) — a genuine historiographic revision, not merely additional detail. His treatment of Amorite cultural assimilation ("the civilization created by the Sumerians outlived them") should be read as his own interpretive synthesis of the Isin-Larsa evidentiary record, most of it royal inscriptions and administrative archives, both source classes with their own self-presentational biases.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

**The nomad↔sedentary relationship, not an invasion/migration wave.** Consistent with Roux's rejection of the "wave of Semitic nomadic invasion" model above, Van De Mieroop frames Amorite political dominance in the early second millennium through the general nomad-sedentary relationship characteristic of the Fertile Crescent's steppe margins, rather than as a discrete migratory event. He notes cases of individuals moving fluidly between the two categories: discussing the later Kassites, he explicitly parallels them to "earlier semi-nomadic groups, Gutians and Amorites" — labels marking socially/linguistically distinct incoming groups whose members nonetheless appear as "fully integrated social members of the agricultural and urban economy from the moment we encounter them." His stated comparative generalization: "As was the case with the Amorites earlier, [incoming groups] acquired political power but did not have a cultural impact" — political dominance is not cultural or linguistic dominance, reinforcing this wiki's four-way non-identity principle. Zimri-Lim of Mari, who seized the throne from Shamshi-Adad's son Yasmah-Addu in 1775 BCE, is labeled by Van De Mieroop "an Amorite upstart" — Amorite here functioning as a political/dynastic-origin descriptor for a ruling lineage, not a racial or invading-horde category.

**"Who were the Amorites?" recorded as an open question (Debate 5.1, position set).** Van De Mieroop stages three positions on Amorite identity, none adjudicated in wiki voice: (1) the older "wave of Semitic nomadic invasion" model (Moscati 1960), now largely rejected; (2) Amorites as chronically-settling semi-nomadic pastoralists resisted by sedentary populations (Kupper 1957, still widely held — Charpin & Ziegler 2003; Jahn 2007; the "Amorite era" label per Charpin 2004); (3) a minimalist/skeptical position (Michalowski 2011; Briant 1982 on nomad-sedentary complementarity generally) holding that "Amorite" was not a coherent ethnic group, shows no clear role in Ur III's overthrow, and that the term's meaning was radically context-dependent — comparable to Gutian, Kassite, Aramean, and Sutean as similarly flexible political/social labels rather than bounded ethnicities.

**Amorite-dynasty territorial states.** Amorite lineages headed several of the early-second-millennium territorial states discussed on [[the-state]] and [[state-formation]] — Zimri-Lim's Mari (retaken from Shamshi-Adad's son Yasmah-Addu in 1775 BCE) and Hammurabi's Babylon among them — states Van De Mieroop treats as personalist and achievement-based rather than institutionally durable, structurally similar to their non-Amorite rivals "only more successful."
